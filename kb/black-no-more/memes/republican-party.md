# Republican Party

In [[Black No More]], the Republican Party represents the incumbent political establishment — the party of [[President Goosie]] — that becomes financially dependent on [[Black-No-More, Inc.]] and struggles to counter the [[Democratic Party]]'s explicitly white supremacist platform in the 1936 election.

## Political Entanglement with Black-No-More

### The Party's Racial Calculus

The Republicans' entanglement with the Black-No-More enterprise is literal and architectural: "[[Dr. Crookman|Crookman]] and [[Gorman Gay]], National Chairman of the Republican National Committee, sat in the physician's hotel suite conversing in low tones" (010-001). The party's finances are intertwined with Black-No-More's profits, creating a structural dependence that shapes its electoral strategy.

## 1936 Republican Convention

"The Republican convention opened in Chicago" (010-048). As the Democratic Party "adopted the slogan of White Supremacy and its platform dwelt largely on the necessity of genealogical investigation," the Republicans were forced to respond — adopting a counter-platform that avoided explicit racial appeals (010-048). The convention took place against the backdrop of [[Buggerie]]'s ancestry revelations, which had thrown the entire political landscape into chaos.

## Campaign Dynamics

"The Republicans certainly needed plenty of money to reelect President Goosie" (010-033). However, "people were not exactly for the Democrats but they were against the Republicans" (010-033). By May of the election year, "it did not seem possible for the Republicans to carry a single Southern state and many of the Northern and Eastern strongholds were imperiled" (010-033).

The Republican campaign attracted figures displaced by the Democratic Party's racial turn. [[Walter Williams]] and [[Joseph Bonds]], both former Negro leaders, "had been unable to stomach the Democratic crowd and so had fallen in with the Republicans, who were as different from them as one billiard ball is from another" (010-066). They busied themselves "rustling papers to appear busy" (010-066) while discussing the party's dilemma.

## Electoral Geography

The party's support base fractured along racial and regional lines. "There was another group which was Republican because it felt that a victory for the Democrats might cause another Civil War" (010-060). The Democrats had already made "inroads here and there in the 1934 Congressional elections" (008-007), and "if drastic steps were not immediately taken, the Republicans might carry the erstwhile Solid South" (008-007) — a paradoxical reversal in which the party of Lincoln might win the South precisely because the Democrats had embraced white supremacy so openly that moderate white Southerners defected.


### The Convention Maneuver

> "Next: We start a campaign of denunciation against the Republicans in The Warning, connecting them with the Pope, Black-No-More and anything else we can think of." (009-011)

> "Well, what I'm trying to do now is to bring these two organizations together. We've got numbers but not enough money to win an election; they have the money but not enough numbers." (009-046)

> The President kept his word. He named the commission, consisting of seven members, five Republicans and two Democrats. They were mostly politicians temporarily out of office, businessmen seeking prestige, and a sprinkling of professional reformers. (009-033)

The Republican Party's anti-Black-No-More stance was never about principle — it was a market calculation, an attempt to capture the white supremacist voting bloc before the Democrats could consolidate it. Schuyler, a lifelong socialist and fierce critic of both parties, presents this as the natural terminus of American electoral politics: any sufficiently profitable prejudice will be adopted by both parties, and the only variable is who gets there first.

> "Oh, he knows how to charm the yokels. He'll appeal to the American people to call upon the Republican administration to close up the sanitariums of Dr. Crookman." (009-007)

> The Grand Exalted Giraw had long known of Snobbcraft's obsession, the genealogical law. He also knew that there was no chance of ever getting such a law through Congress without first stirring up more anti-Negro sentiment. (009-056)

Schuyler's portrait of Republican politics is, like all his satirical targets, grounded in documentary reality. The 1920s Republican Party was the party of Coolidge prosperity and laissez-faire economics, increasingly reliant on the "Solid South" Democrats' disfranchisement of Black voters to maintain its Congressional majorities. The novel's suggestion that Republicans would embrace anti-Black-No-More politics was less a prediction than an extrapolation from the existing political logic: when both parties compete for the white supremacist vote, the only question is which one can offer the more persuasive performance of racial loyalty.

## Satirical Function

The Republican Party in [[Black No More]] represents the cynicism of machine politics: a party whose platform, finances, and electoral strategy are entirely determined by the racial anxieties unleashed by the Black-No-More treatment. Its dependence on Crookman's money and its inability to articulate a coherent alternative to Democratic white supremacy make it a vehicle for Schuyler's satire of American politics as fundamentally opportunistic rather than ideological.
