## Overview

Dr. Brocker is a physician who appears in Chapter 12, attending Helen Fisher during her confinement and the subsequent crisis when the truth about Matthew Fisher's identity is revealed. In one of the novel's most thematically significant exchanges, when old man Givens laments "I guess we're all niggers now," Brocker corrects him — "Negroes, Mr. Givens, Negroes" — and then reveals his own position: "I'm in the same boat with the rest of you, only my dark ancestors are not so far back. I sure hope the Republicans win" (012-053–012-054).

## Character Analysis

Dr. Brocker's function is to deliver the novel's punchline with professional detachment. He has known all along what the other characters are just discovering — that racial purity is a fiction, that everyone has mixed ancestry — and his response is neither outrage nor despair but a mild preference for the Republican Party. Schuyler positions Brocker as the closest thing to an authorial surrogate: the man who knows the truth, finds it unremarkable, and gets on with his work.

## The Revelation Scene

Brocker's entrance marks the exact moment the novel shifts from domestic crisis to cosmic joke. Matthew Fisher has just confessed his identity as the Black Max Disher to his wife, her parents, and the attending nurse. Helen, pregnant with Matthew's child, responds with acceptance rather than revulsion: "To hell with the world! To hell with society! Compared to what she possessed, thought Helen, all talk of race and color was damned foolishness" (012-051). Then Bunny grins: "it sure is good to be able to admit that you're a jigwalk once more" (012-052). And Givens groans: "I guess we're all niggers now" (012-053).

Into this charged scene walks Brocker, who corrects Givens's diction — "Negroes, Mr. Givens, Negroes" — before casually admitting his own mixed ancestry (012-054). The line "only my dark ancestors are not so far back" is the novel's thesis compressed into nine words: Black ancestry is universal among white Americans, differing only in recency, not in fact.

## The Flight to Mexico

Brocker accompanies the Fisher party on their escape to Mexico, "silently gazing out of the window at the twinkling lights of the Gulf Coast" (012-059). His silence here is significant: having spoken the novel's truth, he now recedes into the background — the physician's work is done, the diagnosis delivered, and the patient (American society) must decide what to do with it.

## Key Episodes

- Matthew Fisher recruits Brocker for the escape: "Doctor Brocker, will you go with us to look out for Helen and the baby? We've got to get out right now. I'll pay you your price." (012-041)
- Matthew confesses his identity: "Dr. Brocker smiled knowingly, Givens rose up indignantly, Bunny opened his mouth in surprise" (012-048)
- The "Negroes" correction and ancestry admission: "I'm in the same boat with the rest of you, only my dark ancestors are not so far back. I sure hope the Republicans win." (012-054)
- The flight: "Behind them sat the nurse and Dr. Brocker, silently gazing out of the window at the twinkling lights of the Gulf Coast" (012-059)
