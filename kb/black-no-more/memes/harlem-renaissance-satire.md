## Overview

*Black No More* is itself a document of the Harlem Renaissance — but one written against the grain of the movement's dominant tendencies. Where figures like Alain Locke and W.E.B. Du Bois promoted Black cultural achievement as a strategy for racial uplift, Schuyler argued that the very category of "Negro art" was a racist construct. His 1926 essay "The Negro-Art Hokum" had already declared that "the Aframerican is merely a lampblacked Anglo-Saxon," and the novel extends this argument into fiction: if Black culture is merely American culture performed by Black people, then eliminating Blackness does not eliminate culture — it only eliminates the racial frame through which culture is perceived.

## In the Narrative

The novel satirizes the Harlem Renaissance's cultural institutions through their collapse. Hank Johnson, the Numbers baron turned Black-No-More financier, had previously "given a couple of thousand dollars to advance Negro Art" (003-016) — a gesture that the novel presents as transparent self-promotion. The Negro newspapers, magazines, and artistic societies that had flourished during the Renaissance are shown to evaporate when their audience chooses whiteness, revealing that "Negro culture" was sustained not by authentic racial expression but by a captive market.

## Historical Context

The Harlem Renaissance (c. 1918–1937) produced an extraordinary flowering of Black literature, music, and visual art. Schuyler was a prominent but contrarian participant: he worked as a journalist for Black newspapers, published the novel with a mainstream press, and maintained friendships with many Renaissance figures while fiercely criticizing the movement's premises. His critique anticipated later debates about essentialism and representation: is "Black art" defined by the race of its creator, its subject matter, its aesthetic style, or its political function? The novel's answer — none of the above — was radical for 1931 and remains provocative.

## Satirical Treatment

The satire of the Harlem Renaissance operates through absence rather than direct parody. The novel's protagonist, Max Disher, is notably uninterested in art or culture — his concerns are money, status, and women. This is itself a satirical choice: Schuyler refuses to make his Black characters exemplars of cultural achievement, insisting instead on their ordinariness. The Renaissance's grand claims about "The New Negro" are implicitly mocked by characters who pursue individual self-interest rather than racial uplift, suggesting that ordinary human motivations are more powerful than cultural programs.

## Key Passages

> Hank Johnson "had given a couple of thousand dollars to advance Negro Art and been elected Grand Permanent Shogun of the Ancient and Honorable Order of Crocodiles" — patronage and self-promotion intertwined (003-016)

> Dr. Crookman "subscribed to six or seven Negro weekly newspapers and two of the magazines" — the Renaissance's infrastructure (003-020)
