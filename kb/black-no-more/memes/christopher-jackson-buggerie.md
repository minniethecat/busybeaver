## Overview

Dr. Samuel Buggerie is the novel's agent of catastrophic revelation — the statistician whose research, commissioned to prove white racial purity, instead destroys it. Hired by Arthur Snobbcraft and the Anglo-Saxon Association to produce genealogical data supporting the Democratic Party's white supremacy platform, Buggerie's investigation uncovers the opposite of what his patrons expect: that "over half the population has no record of its ancestry beyond five generations," that millions of Americans descend from Africans who passed for white generations ago, and — most devastatingly — that Snobbcraft, Givens, and Buggerie himself all have Negro ancestry. The leak of his findings during the election triggers the nationwide panic, lynchings, and martial law that constitute the novel's climax.

Schuyler names him "Buggerie" — the echo of "buggery" signals the obscene quality of racial-purity science — and makes him "a professional Anglo-Saxon" with his own hidden ancestry, a pompous author of absurd scholarly monographs, and the unwitting instrument of the racial order's self-destruction.

## Biography

### The Scholarly Charlatan

Buggerie is introduced as "the statistician of a great New York insurance company" who is "highly respected among members of his profession and well known by the reading public." His scholarly credentials are parodic: his well-known work, *The Fluctuation of the Sizes of Left Feet Among the Assyrians During the Ninth Century Before Christ*, has been "favorably commented upon by several reviewers, one of whom had actually read it." Another work, *Putting Wasted Energy to Work*, proposes "harnessing the power generated by the leaves of trees rubbing together on windy days" (009-062). His monographs proving that "rich people have smaller families than the poor" and "imprisonment does not stop crime" mark him as a purveyor of statistical banalities dressed as scholarship.

His most recent article, proving "statistically that unemployment and poverty are principally a state of mind," is "enthusiastically hailed by scholars and especially by business men as an outstanding contribution to contemporary thought" — a line that captures Schuyler's contempt for the alliance between academic authority and economic power.

### The Anglo-Saxon Association Commission

Buggerie, "like Mr. Snobbcraft, was a professional Anglo-Saxon as well as a descendant of one of the First Families of Virginia" (009-063). His proposal to the Association's directors is simple: a nationwide genealogical investigation to "disclose the various non-Nordic strains in the population," followed by laws "forbidding these strains from mixing or marrying with the pure strains that had produced such fine specimens of mankind as Mr. Snobbcraft and himself" (009-062). He estimates "as many as twenty million people" possess "some slight non-Nordic strain and were thus unfit for both citizenship and procreation."

The directors vote to fund the research on condition of secrecy — which pains Buggerie, who "agreed although it hurt him to the heart to forego any publicity" (009-064). His staff begins examining birth and marriage records nationwide, and by mid-September he presents a partial report (010-057).

### The Revelation

On election night, Buggerie arrives at Snobbcraft's celebration with urgent news. Alone in the study, he reveals the catastrophic implications of his findings:

> "I have found that over half the population has no record of its ancestry beyond five generations!" (011-042)

He explains that "a certain percentage of these Negroes in time lightened sufficiently to be able to pass for white. They then merged with the general population. Assuming that there were one thousand such cases fifteen generations ago — and we have proof that there were more — their descendants now number close to fifty million souls." His conclusion is stark: "Too many of our very first families are touched right here in Richmond!" (011-042)

The scene escalates when Buggerie, "seemingly enjoying his dissertation," turns to Snobbcraft's own family: "One of your maternal ancestors in the late seventeenth century was the offspring of an English serving maid and a black slave. This woman in turn had a daughter by the plantation owner." And of himself, with "almost gleeful" candor: "My great, great grandfather had his ears cropped for nonpayment of debts and was later jailed for thievery. His illegitimate daughter married a free Negro who fought in the Revolutionary War." He adds that Rev. Givens "is only four generations removed from a mulatto ancestor" (011-042).

The revelation — that the champions of white supremacy are themselves products of the racial mixing they seek to criminalize — triggers the novel's denouement: Snobbcraft and Buggerie flee, the findings leak, and the nation descends into genealogical panic.

## Satirical Function

Buggerie is the novel's most elaborate joke about the relationship between knowledge and power. The Anglo-Saxon Association commissions research to weaponize racial science; the research instead discovers that racial purity is a fiction and that the commissioners themselves are "tainted." The statistician who sets out to prove white supremacy ends up proving its impossibility — yet the knowledge, rather than liberating anyone, produces only chaos and violence. Buggerie's personal ancestry (criminal and Negro), his absurd scholarly career, and his role as unwitting truth-teller combine to satirize the entire apparatus of eugenic science: its methods are arbitrary, its practitioners are frauds, and its discoveries inevitably undermine its premises.
