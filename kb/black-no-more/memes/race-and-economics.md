## Overview

The economic basis of racial division is *Black No More*'s most enduring insight. Schuyler demonstrates that racism is not merely a psychological prejudice but a system of material interests: Black newspapers depend on advertising from skin-whitening companies, Black churches depend on congregations seeking solace from oppression, white politicians depend on racial fear to win elections, and the Knights of Nordica depend on membership dues from white men terrified of Black equality. When the Black-No-More treatment threatens to eliminate racial difference, all of these economic interests mobilize to preserve the distinction they claim to abhor.

## Historical Context

The novel's economic analysis of race was shaped by Schuyler's experience as a journalist covering Harlem's Black business district. He observed firsthand how the "Numbers" racket, real estate speculation, and the beauty industry created a parallel Black economy dependent on segregation. The 1929 stock market crash and the onset of the Great Depression (which deepened while Schuyler wrote) made economic analysis of social problems urgent. Schuyler's materialist approach to race — treating it as an economic institution rather than a natural fact — anticipates later work by scholars like W.E.B. Du Bois in *Black Reconstruction* (1935) and Oliver Cox in *Caste, Class, and Race* (1948).

## In the Narrative

The novel traces economic disruption across multiple sectors. The skin-whitening and hair-straightening industries are "bankrupted" (005-041). Black newspapers lose their advertising base and circulation. The Numbers racket — Harlem's underground lottery, run by Hank Johnson before he became Crookman's financier — loses its customer base as Black Harlem empties out (003-016). Meanwhile, Black-No-More, Incorporated generates staggering profits: "seventy thousand dollars" in two weeks (003-013), with the potential market of "twelve million Negroes at fifty dollars apiece" (003-016). The novel's economics are brutally clear: race is a commodity, and eliminating it is good business for some and bad business for others.

## Satirical Treatment

The satire of racial economics is most pointed in the character of Hank Johnson. A former chain-gang convict turned Numbers baron, Johnson sees the Black-No-More treatment purely as a business opportunity: "This here is the best and safest graft I've ever been in" (003-006). His cynicism mirrors the Reverend Givens's similar calculation on the white supremacist side, and the two men eventually form a political alliance that literalizes the economic basis of racial politics. Schuyler's point is that racial entrepreneurs on both sides profit from maintaining the very divisions they claim to oppose.
