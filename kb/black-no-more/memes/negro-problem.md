The "Negro Problem" is the satirical target at the center of [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]]'s plot architecture. In early-twentieth-century American discourse, the phrase framed Black people as a social difficulty to be managed — a framing that located the problem in Blackness itself. Schuyler's novel inverts this logic: Dr. [[dr-crookman|Crookman]]'s treatment "could possibly solve the American race problem" not by improving Black people but by eliminating Blackness, and the ensuing chaos reveals that the "problem" was never Black people but the investments that white America had made in racial hierarchy.

## Crookman's Logical Premise

The novel's entire plot rests on a syllogism articulated in Chapter 3. Crookman reasons that "if there were no Negroes, there could be no Negro problem. Without a Negro problem, Americans could concentrate their attention on something constructive" [003-019]. This logic is perfectly sound within its own terms — eliminating the category eliminates the supposed difficulty — but catastrophically naive about the economic and psychological functions that the "Negro problem" served. As the novel demonstrates, racial discourse was not a response to Blackness but a structure that required Blackness to sustain itself.

## The Racial Grievance Economy

The National Social Equality League, Schuyler's satire of the NAACP, exemplifies how the "Negro problem" sustained an entire professional class. League officials "began to envision the time when they would no longer be able for the sake of the Negro race to suffer the hardships of lunching on canvasback duck at the Urban Club" [005-046]. On salaries of five thousand dollars a year, they had "fought strenuously and tirelessly to obtain for the Negroes the constitutional rights which only a few thousand rich white folk possessed" — and the prospect of the problem's disappearance threatened their livelihood more than the problem itself ever had.

> On meager salaries of five thousand dollars a year they had fought strenuously and tirelessly to obtain for the Negroes the constitutional rights which only a few thousand rich white folk possessed. And now they saw the work of a lifetime being rapidly destroyed. [005-046]

## Political Weaponization

The "Negro problem" proves the most reliable political tool in the novel. When [[max-disher|Matthew Fisher]] and [[bunny-brown|Bunny Brown]] plan their campaign strategy, Bunny's advice is simple: "We'll try the old sure fire Negro problem stuff" [008-026]. This is not cynicism born of experience but the distillation of a political reality: racial anxiety is always available as a mobilization strategy because "these people have been raised on the Negro problem, they're used to it, they're trained to react to it" [008-028]. The problem is not real but its political utility is undeniable.

> "Bunny, I've learned something on this job, and that is that hatred and prejudice always go over big. These people have been raised on the Negro problem, they're used to it, they're trained to react to it. Why should I rack my brain to hunt up something else when I can use a dodge that's always delivered the goods?" [008-028]

## The Problem Eliminated

By Chapter 10, Crookman can declare: "We've about cleaned up the Negro problem in this country" [010-011]. But the novel's irony is that eliminating the problem's object — Black people — does not eliminate the problem. Instead, the logic of racial hierarchy simply shifts: the newly whitened population becomes too white, and whiteness itself becomes suspect. The final chapters reveal that the "Negro problem" was never about Negroes; it was about the need for a racial underclass that could be identified, managed, and exploited.

## The Satirical Payoff

The novel's final reference to the "Negro problem" appears in the Happy Hill episode, a grotesque satire of Southern lynching culture. Local boosters note that "the only Negro problem in Happy Hill was the difficulty of getting hold of a sufficient number of the Sons or Daughters of Ham to lighten the dullness of the place" [013-058]. This is Schuyler at his darkest: the "problem" reduces to supply-chain logistics for a lynching industry. The absurdity is the point — the entire discourse of the "Negro problem" was always a pretext for violence, whether economic, political, or physical.
