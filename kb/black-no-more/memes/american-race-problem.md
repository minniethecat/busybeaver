## Overview

The "American race problem" is the euphemism that Schuyler's novel systematically deconstructs. In 1930s discourse, "the Negro problem" referred to the supposed difficulty of integrating Black Americans into white society — a framing that located the problem in Blackness itself. Schuyler's satirical inversion is simple but devastating: what if the problem is not Black people but the category of race itself? Dr. Crookman's treatment "could possibly solve the American race problem" (001-075) not by improving Black people but by eliminating Blackness — and the ensuing chaos reveals that the "problem" was never Black people but the economic and psychological investments that white America had made in racial hierarchy.

## In the Narrative

The "race problem" is invoked repeatedly by characters who have a material stake in its continuation. Dr. Crookman, for all his scientific credentials, is "naively surprised that there should be opposition to his work" because he "fondly imagined people to be intelligent enough to accept a good thing when it was offered to them" (003-019). The Black leadership conference in Chapter 5 reveals that the "problem" is their livelihood: "the Negro leaders found themselves in a very predicament. Their jobs were at stake" (005-040). Meanwhile white politicians discover that without a visible Black population to demonize, their electoral strategies collapse.

## Historical Context

The phrase "the Negro problem" was ubiquitous in American public discourse from Reconstruction through the Civil Rights era. It appeared in academic treatises, political speeches, and newspaper editorials, always implying that Black people were a difficulty to be managed. Gunnar Myrdal's *An American Dilemma* (1944) would later reframe the issue as a moral problem of white America, but Schuyler's novel had already gone further in 1931: it suggested that the "problem" was entirely manufactured and that its disappearance would reveal the machinery of racial capitalism.

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler treats the "race problem" as a collective hallucination maintained by professionals. The Black "race leaders" are exposed as entrepreneurs of grievance; the white politicians as entrepreneurs of fear. The novel's most radical suggestion is that solving the "race problem" — eliminating racial difference — would be catastrophic not because of any inherent difficulty but because too many people profit from its continuation. When Max Disher reflects that "one never escape the plagued race problem" (002-028), the irony is that he cannot escape it because it is inside him, not outside.
