## Overview

Chromatic hierarchy — the internal ranking of African Americans by skin tone, with lighter skin conferring higher status — is one of Schuyler's most uncomfortable satirical targets. The novel exposes how the Black elite, who had built their social position on proximity to whiteness, react with ambivalence when actual whiteness becomes universally available. The "dicty Negroes" who had refused to fund Crookman's education (003-016) suddenly find that the color hierarchy they had navigated so carefully has been flattened overnight. The subversive implication is that intra-racial colorism, not just white racism, sustained the racial order.

## In the Narrative

The chromatics of *Black No More* are carefully calibrated. Mrs. Crookman is described as a "beautiful little octoroon" whose ability to "pass for white" is "something akin to a piece of anthracite coal passing for black" (003-025) — a description that locates her precisely on the color hierarchy. Chuck Foster, the cautious real estate speculator, is described as having worked his way into "Harlem society" through strategic philanthropy, suggesting that class and color were intertwined in Black social mobility (003-017). The treatment levels all of these distinctions, turning the careful calibration of skin tone into a meaningless memory.

## Historical Context

Colorism within Black communities has deep roots in American slavery, where lighter-skinned enslaved people (often the children of slave owners) were given preferential treatment including house work rather than field work. After Emancipation, light skin remained a marker of status, institutionalized through practices like the "brown paper bag test" at Black social clubs and the "blue vein societies" that admitted only those light enough to show blue veins through their skin. Schuyler's novel, by making actual whiteness available to everyone, renders this entire hierarchy absurd — but also forces the question of what Black identity means without the visual markers that sustained it.

## Satirical Treatment

The novel's treatment of chromatic hierarchy is characteristically double-edged. Schuyler mocks the pretensions of the "dicty" (high-toned) Negroes who had cultivated distinction through color, while also acknowledging that their social climbing was a rational response to a racist society. The treatment democratizes whiteness but also eliminates the subtle pleasures of chromatic distinction — and the novel does not pretend that this is an unmixed good. The disappearance of Black social hierarchy means the disappearance of Black social institutions, including those that had genuine cultural value.
