## Overview

Negro weekly newspapers are the novel's media infrastructure — the publications that first report Crookman's discovery, that editorialize on the treatment's consequences, and that ultimately collapse when their advertising base (predominantly skin-[[racial-passing|whitening]] and hair-straightening products) disappears. [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]], a journalist for Black newspapers himself, portrays the Black press with insider's ambivalence: it is simultaneously a vehicle for racial uplift and a business dependent on racial self-hatred.

## Role in the Narrative

The novel's plot is narrated partly through newspaper clippings. Crookman reads Southern editorials aloud in [[ch03|Chapter 3]] (003-009-010), and [[max-disher|Max]] first learns of the treatment from a newspaper account (001-072). The newspapers function as a Greek chorus, interpreting events for the reader — and Schuyler ensures that their interpretations are always suspect, always serving some institutional or commercial interest.

## Satirical Function

Exposes the economic contradiction at the heart of the Black press: the same newspapers that advocated [[racial-pride|racial pride]] depended on advertising from companies that promised to make Black people look less Black.
