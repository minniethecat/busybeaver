## Overview

Race science in *Black No More* is Schuyler's satirical target for the entire apparatus of scientific racism that flourished in the early 20th century. Dr. Junius Crookman's Black-No-More treatment — a three-day glandular and electrical procedure that transforms "the Negro into a white man" (001-080) — is presented with the sober authority of a medical breakthrough. The novel's joke is that this "science" is no more absurd than the racial theories it parodies: the only difference is that Crookman's treatment actually works, while eugenics, phrenology, and racial biology produced nothing but justification for oppression.

## Historical Context

The 1920s–1930s saw the high tide of American eugenics, with forced sterilizations, immigration restrictions, and "scientific" treatises on racial hierarchy. Madison Grant's *The Passing of the Great Race* (1916) and Lothrop Stoddard's *The Rising Tide of Color* (1920) were bestsellers. Schuyler's novel inverts this tradition: instead of warning that inferior races threaten white civilization, he imagines science giving Black people the power to become white — and white civilization promptly collapses into paranoid chaos.

## In the Narrative

Crookman announces his discovery to the Black press in Harlem, positioning himself as a "Race Man" who wants to "remove all obstacles in their path by depriving them of their racial characteristics" (003-020). The treatment's mechanism — glandular control and electrical nutrition — is deliberately vague, mimicking the impenetrable jargon of contemporary racial science. The novel traces the treatment's consequences: economic collapse of the skin-whitening industry (005-041), political panic in the South (005-062), and the eventual revelation that the treatment's effects are not hereditary (003-010), creating a permanent ambiguity about racial identity.

## Satirical Treatment

The novel's treatment of science is deeply ironic. Crookman is presented as a genuine researcher who studied in Germany and maintains professional standards — yet his "discovery" is transparently magical. The real satire targets the consumers of racial science: white Southerners who cannot argue against Crookman's results because "science" carries unquestioned authority, and Black customers who embrace the treatment without considering its eugenic implications (003-019). Schuyler suggests that science, in the hands of a racist society, becomes just another tool of social control.
