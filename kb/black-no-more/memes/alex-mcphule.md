## Overview

Rev. Alex McPhule is Schuyler's most concentrated satire of white Southern evangelicalism — a grotesque fusion of religious opportunism, sexual predation, and racial violence. Arriving in the mill town of Happy Hill with an invented theology, a hand-painted "Bible," and an altar topped with an old bureau's marble slab, McPhule builds a following among the desperate by staging revival orgies and making pastoral "comfort" calls on the wives and daughters of working men. When he reads *The Warning* and learns of the anti-Black-No-More fervor sweeping the South, McPhule prays for God to send him "a nigger for his congregation to lynch" — a sign that would prove his divine favor and grow his flock. He reappears in the climactic Chapter 13, where his mob leads the pursuit of the suspected "Negro" (actually the whitened Matthew Fisher) through the woods outside Happy Hill.

## Biography

### Arrival in Happy Hill

McPhule appears in Happy Hill several weeks before the main action of Chapter 11, claiming that an angel visited him during an illness in Meridian and commanded him to "preach the true faith of Christ's love" (013-061). His theology is deliberately simple: no music besides singing and tub-thumping, no chairs (everyone sits on the ground), and revival services that culminate in frenzied embracing and rolling while the pine torches conveniently burn out.

> One day, several weeks previously, there had come to the community one Rev. Alex McPhule who claimed to be the founder of a new faith, a true faith, that would save all from the machinations of the Evil One. (013-061)

His popularity grows rapidly — "every latchstring hung out for him" — and he makes a practice of visiting house after house "when the men were at work in the fields" to "comfort the womenfolk with his Christian message" (013-064). Schuyler's narrator notes drily that "Happy Hill was too good for Rev. Alex McPhule" (013-064).

McPhule holds private audiences in a room equipped with a painted eye on the wall and a crude altar. His visitors — "middle-aged wives and adenoidal and neurotic young girls" — confess their sins while gazing at the eye, and "none departed unsatisfied" (013-065). The narrator's deadpan delivery makes the sexual predation unmistakable without ever naming it directly.

> On the altar reposed a crudely-bound manuscript about three inches thick. This was the "Bible" of the Christ Lovers which the Rev. McPhule declared he had written at the command of Jesus Christ Himself. (013-066)

The turning point comes when someone brings McPhule a copy of *The Warning*. Reading the newspaper's anti-Black propaganda, he grows uneasy: "Rev. McPhule began to wonder what he had done that the Lord should so withhold His favor" (013-068). The idea crystallizes — if God would only send him a Black man to lynch, it would be "marked evidence of the power of Rev. Alex McPhule" (013-068).

> If the Lord would only send him a nigger for his congregation to lynch! That would, indeed, be marked evidence of the power of Rev. Alex McPhule. (013-068)

He prays with increased fervor. A bat flying through his window becomes, in his telling, an angel kissing his forehead. The red mark from striking his head on the marble altar is exhibited as proof of divine visitation — "Rev. McPhule exhibited a red spot on his forehead which, he explained, had been made by the angel's kiss" (013-070). The villagers are convinced that "the Rev. McPhule stood in well with the celestial powers" (013-071), and he schedules the great revival for Election Day, promising a Sign from God.

> An angel of the Lord, he told the gaping villagers, had flown through the window, alighted on his "Bible" and, kissing him on his forehead, had declared that the Lord would answer his prayer and send a sign. (013-070)

## The Lynching Climax (Ch. 13)

McPhule's long-awaited Sign arrives on Election Day when a stranger — the fugitive Matthew Fisher, whitened and on the run — stumbles into the Happy Hill revival. McPhule seizes the moment:

> Suddenly Rev. McPhule stopped, glared at the rows of people, and cried: "There's a nigger in this meeting! I can smell him!" (013-078)

When the terrified Fisher bolts, McPhule leads the chase on all fours, "running like a dog and barking vociferously" (013-082). This image — the Man of God reduced to a howling animal — is Schuyler's final satirical verdict: McPhule's religion is indistinguishable from mob violence. The pursuit continues through the woods and fields, McPhule "followed by his enthusiastic congregation" (013-095), until the posse from *The Scimitar* plane arrives.

When the outsiders question the mob's certainty about Fisher's racial identity, McPhule inserts himself as moral authority — "Mighty right it does! And it is you, too!" (013-111, 013-113) — before the crowd closes in and lynches the wrong man. McPhule's parting contribution is the post-lynching absolution: "the presence of mind of the Rev. McPhule, the True Faith Christ Lovers" (013-114) enables the mob to disperse with clean consciences.

## Satirical Function

McPhule embodies three layers of Schuyler's satire:

### Religious Authority as Sexual Exploitation McPhule's "faith" is a mechanism for accessing vulnerable women under the cover of pastoral care. The "private audiences," the painted eye, the "Bible" written at Christ's command — every trapping of his ministry serves his predation.

### Christianity Fused with Racial Violence McPhule sees a lynching as the ultimate proof of God's favor, a blasphemous conjunction that Schuyler presents without editorial comment. The prayer for "a nigger to lynch" and the beast-like pursuit on all fours collapse the distance between evangelical worship and mob murder.

### The Credulity of the Exploited The mill workers of Happy Hill accept the bat-kiss story and the hand-painted "Bible" because they are desperate for meaning in a world of economic exploitation and poverty. McPhule's success is an indictment not just of the con man but of the conditions that make his con irresistible.
