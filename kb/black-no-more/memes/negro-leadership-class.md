## Overview

The Negro leadership class is one of Schuyler's primary satirical targets in *Black No More*. Rather than attacking white supremacy directly, the novel devotes its most savage passages to the "professional Negro leaders" — the editors, college presidents, ministers, and organizational secretaries whose livelihoods depend on managing (not ending) racial inequality. When Dr. Crookman's Black-No-More treatment threatens to eliminate the Negro population — and with it, the entire economic basis of Negro leadership — these figures scramble not to advance racial justice but to preserve their institutional positions.

Schuyler's critique is fundamentally economic: the Negro leader, in this analysis, is a racial broker who profits from the gap between Black aspiration and white power. The satirical method is to expose this brokerage by showing what happens when the brokerage market collapses.

## Key Figures

### The N.S.E.L. Circle

- [[shakespeare-agamemnon-beard]] — Founder of the National Social Equality League; the archetype of the pompous, erudite leader whose radical rhetoric masks deep admiration for the whites he denounces. Re-emerges as "Karl von Beerde" when the racial market shifts.
- [[napoleon-wellington-jackson]] — Beard's secretary; the academic-as-race-man whose scholarly pretensions (plantation shouts > Beethoven) serve only to flatter his audience.
- [[herbert-gronne]] — President of Dunbar University; the institutional accommodationist who calibrates his statements to sound radical to Negroes but conservative to white trustees.
- [[colonel-mortimer-roberts]] — Director of the Dusky River Agricultural Institute; the educational bureaucrat whose institution's survival depends on the continued existence of segregated Negro education.

### The Rival Leaders

- [[santop-licorice]] — Rival leader excluded from Beard's Committee; delighted by Beard's setbacks, representing the factional infighting that plagues Negro organizations.
- [[joseph-bonds]] — Former head of the Negro Data League; associated with the N.S.E.L. circle but maintains his own institutional ambitions.

### The Economic Collaborators

- [[walter-williams]] — The intermediary who approaches Beard and other leaders on behalf of Matthew Fisher and the Knights of Nordica, offering money for lectures against Black-No-More.
- [[gorman-gay]] — Editor who publishes anti-Black-No-More propaganda; represents the media dimension of the Negro leadership economy.
- [[walter-brybe]] — Lesser figure in the leadership apparatus; represents the rank-and-file functionary whose job depends on racial segregation.

### The Religious Parallel

- [[rev-givens]] — While not part of the N.S.E.L. circle, Rev. Henry Givens operates in a parallel economy of religious racial brokerage. His career as a "race leader" in the pulpit mirrors the secular leaders' dependence on racial distinction.
- [[alex-mcphule]] — The white mirror-image: a Southern evangelical whose ministry fuses religious authority with racial violence, and whose prayer for "a nigger to lynch" exposes the same economic logic operating in the white religious sphere.

## Organizations

- [[national-social-equality-league]] — Beard's flagship organization; its name promises equality while its operations depend on inequality.
- [[negro-data-league]] — Research organization that produces racial data; its existence is predicated on the continued existence of a distinct Negro population to study.
- [[back-to-africa-society]] — The emigrationist alternative to the N.S.E.L. model; equally dependent on racial distinction for its relevance.
- [[anglo-saxon-association]] — The white counterpart to Negro organizations; the symmetry of organizational structure across the color line underscores the satire.
- [[down-with-white-prejudice-league]] — Beard's post-reversal organization; the ultimate proof that the leadership apparatus is color-transferable.

## Key Episodes

### The Committee Meeting (Ch. 5)

The novel's most concentrated satire of Negro leadership occurs in Chapter 5, when Beard convenes the Committee for the Preservation of Negro Racial Integrity. The scene is structured as a revival meeting — complete with spirituals, shouting, and amens — because Schuyler wants the reader to see that Negro leadership is a form of religious performance, not political action.

> They looked at Dr. Beard and each other in amazement. (005-072)

Beard's cynical proposal — to accept Knights of Nordica money to campaign against Black-No-More — is the scene's satirical climax. The logic is brutally clear: the Knights want to preserve white supremacy; the Negro leaders want to preserve Negro segregation; their interests are aligned. The only leader to object (Gronne) does so on procedural, not ethical, grounds.

### The Lecture Circuit (Ch. 10)

After Black-No-More devastates the Negro population, the leaders are recruited by Bunny Brown to lecture *for* the Knights of Nordica — the very organization they once denounced. The shift from opposition to collaboration is seamless because, Schuyler implies, it was always about the money. "Soon afterward Williams left Dr. Beard, took a stroll around the Anglo-Saxon Association headquarters" (010-100) — the spatial proximity of Negro and white supremacist organizations is a visual joke about their functional identity.

### The Reversal (Epilogue)

When the racial order inverts — whites becoming the persecuted minority — Beard resurfaces as "Karl von Beerde," proving that the leadership apparatus is transferable across any color line. The Down-With-White-Prejudice-League is the N.S.E.L. with the color adjectives swapped. Schuyler's point is that racial grievance is a commodity, and those who broker it will adapt to whatever market configuration prevails.

## Schuyler's Critique: Dimensions

### Economic

The Negro leader's material interest is in the *perpetuation* of racial distinction, not its elimination. Black-No-More threatens this interest not because it is morally wrong but because it eliminates the client base. The Committee meeting is, at bottom, a meeting of businessmen whose product (racial advocacy) has suddenly lost its market.

### Institutional

Organizations like the N.S.E.L., the Negro Data League, and Dunbar University are institutions before they are movements. They require budgets, staff, and student bodies. When Black-No-More reduces Dunbar's enrollment to sixty-five, Gronne's panic is institutional, not ideological.

### Rhetorical

Schuyler pays close attention to the *language* of Negro leadership — the windy resolutions, the padding-filled speeches, the "scholarly and biting editorials" that denounce whites while secretly admiring them. The satire targets not just what leaders do but how they talk: the verbal machinery of racial grievance is exposed as a professional skill, like knowing how to draft a resolution or polish eyeglasses during a speech.

### Personal

The leaders are personally compromised. Beard hires "comely yellow stenographers with weak resistance." Jackson's scholarship is motivated by professional advancement, not truth. Gronne's "perpetually earnest and sincere" expression is an asset he deploys strategically. The satire insists that the personal hypocrisy of these figures is inseparable from their political function.

## Historical Context

Schuyler's satire of Negro leadership reflects his own position as a fierce critic of what he saw as a self-serving "race industry." As a journalist at *The Pittsburgh Courier* and a contributor to H.L. Mencken's *American Mercury*, Schuyler was positioned at the intersection of Black journalism and white literary modernism — a position that made him skeptical of both white philanthropy and Black organizational politics. The N.S.E.L. is generally read as a composite satire of the NAACP (founded 1909), the National Urban League (1910), and Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association, all of which Schuyler criticized at various points in his career.
