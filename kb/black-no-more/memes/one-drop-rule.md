## Overview

The one-drop rule — the uniquely American principle that a single African ancestor makes a person Black regardless of appearance — is the legal fiction that *Black No More* explodes from within. Schuyler's narrative engine — a treatment that makes Black people indistinguishable from white people but does not alter their germ cells — weaponizes the one-drop rule against white supremacy: "the offspring of these whitened Negroes will be Negroes" (003-010). If anyone might secretly be Black, and if whiteness is invisible rather than self-evident, then the one-drop rule transforms from a tool of exclusion into a source of permanent existential terror for white America.

## Historical Context

The one-drop rule was codified in Southern states through anti-miscegenation laws and racial classification statutes. The 1924 Virginia Racial Integrity Act was a landmark example, requiring racial certification on birth certificates. The Supreme Court upheld such laws until *Loving v. Virginia* (1967). Schuyler's 1931 novel anticipates the absurdity of racial classification by dramatizing its collapse: once the physical markers of race disappear, the legal apparatus has nothing to enforce. The novel was published the same year as the infamous Scottsboro Boys case, when nine Black teenagers were falsely accused of rape in Alabama — a reminder that the one-drop rule had lethal consequences.

## In the Narrative

The one-drop rule's narrative function peaks in the novel's middle chapters. Southern newspapers warn that white women who marry whitened Negroes "may find herself with a black baby" (003-010). The Knights of Nordica scramble to hire "race statisticians" to verify whiteness, but their methods collapse into farce (004-010). By the novel's end, a new social order has emerged in which even the whitest-seeming person is suspected of hidden Black ancestry — an ironic reversal of the one-drop rule's original purpose of maintaining racial hierarchy.

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler treats the one-drop rule not as a tragedy but as a joke — and specifically as a joke on white people. The punchline is that white supremacy depends on visible Blackness to function; without it, the entire edifice of Jim Crow collapses into paranoia. The novel's most subversive move is to suggest that the fear of "black blood" is not about protecting white purity at all, but about protecting the economic and political privileges that whiteness confers.
