## Overview

Madeline Scranton appears only as the sender of a telegram to Bunny Brown in the novel's climax, but her message functions as a narrative hinge: its staccato, telegraphic sentences compress the resolution of multiple plot threads — Kretin lynched, Snobbcraft and Buggerie in flight, Goosie and Gump re-elected, martial law declared — into a single, breathless communication. The final question, "When can I come?", voiced by Bunny's lover, reintroduces the personal into the political chaos and closes the novel's action on a note of domestic uncertainty.

## Narrative Function

Schuyler uses Scranton's telegram as an economical closure device — rather than dramatizing the lynching of Senator Kretin, the flight of Snobbcraft and Buggerie, and the re-election results separately, he delivers all three via a single ninety-word message that Bunny reads aloud in the airplane. The telegram form itself — with its characteristic STOP markers and compressed syntax — mirrors the acceleration of events in the novel's final chapter, where revelations cascade faster than any single scene can contain (012-066–012-068).

The response to Scranton's telegram also provides the novel's final word on its racial themes. Matthew Fisher, now revealed as the Black Max Disher who conceived a brown baby with the formerly racist Helen Givens, asks "She must be the last black gal in the country. How come she didn't get white, too?" (012-073) — to which Bunny replies simply "She ain't no Caucasian!" (012-072). The exchange confirms that some Black Americans have chosen not to undergo the Black-No-More treatment, preserving the racial diversity the treatment was meant to erase.

## The Telegram's Aftermath

Matthew Fisher's exclamation that Scranton is "the last black gal in the country" (012-073) is bitterly ironic: he himself is Black, his newborn son is brown, and the airplane fleeing to Mexico carries a mixed party of the formerly Black and the formerly white who have all discovered their shared ancestry. The "last black gal" is in fact surrounded by Black people who simply look white.
