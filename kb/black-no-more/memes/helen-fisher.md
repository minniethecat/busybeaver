## Overview

Helen Fisher (née Givens) is the daughter of Dr. Junius Crookman and the wife of Matthew Fisher. As a white woman married to a man who was formerly Black — a fact she does not know — Helen embodies the novel's central dramatic irony. Her position at the intersection of the Crookman and Fisher families makes her the unwitting linchpin of the novel's climactic revelations, and her pregnancy — culminating in the birth of a "very, very dark" son (012-021) — triggers the cascade of events that exposes the treatment's impermanence.

## Biography

Helen is the daughter of Dr. Crookman — or rather, of Reverend Henry Givens, as Crookman's real name is revealed to be. She was raised in Harlem and sent away to school, returning with "newfangled foolishness" that her mother, Mrs. Givens, deplored (004-053). Helen represents the modern, educated generation — embarrassed by her mother's snuff-dipping and pipe-smoking, insisting she "act like civilized people" when company comes (004-055). She is described as confident and self-possessed at twenty: "Helen was twenty and quite confident that she possessed those attributes which make for success in a social way" (004-056). Her "long red-gold hair framing her face" (012-044) marks her as visibly, unmistakably white — a fact that heightens the shock when her child is born dark.

She meets Matthew Fisher through her father's connection to the Knights of Nordica. When Matthew first comes calling at the Givens home on West 139th Street, Helen is curious but guarded; her mother's heavy-handed matchmaking nearly drives her away (004-053). But she is drawn to Matthew's intensity and ambition, and their marriage solidifies the alliance between Crookman's scientific empire and Fisher's political ascension.

## The Pregnancy and Its Catastrophe

Helen's pregnancy is the novel's ticking clock. She had miscarried before — Matthew had sent her "to Palm Beach and the other resorts, thinking the travel and exercise might bring on another miscarriage" (011-004) — but this time the pregnancy goes to term. Worse, Helen miscalculates her due date, delivering just before Election Day when the political stakes are at their highest.

> The afternoon before election Matthew and Bunny sat in the latter's hotel suite sipping cocktails, smoking and awaiting the inevitable. Every time they heard a bell ring both jumped for the telephone, thinking it might be an announcement from Helen's bedside that an heir, and a dark one, had been born. (012-001)

Helen herself greets the birth with joy: "In the midst of her pain Helen was jubilant. What a present to give her Matthew on the eve of his greatest triumph! How good the Lord was to her; to doubly bless her in this way" (012-012). Her innocence — she has no idea of the racial secret she carries — makes the doctor's revelation all the more devastating.

> "I'm very sorry to have to tell you, Mr. Fisher, that something terrible has happened. Your son is very, very dark. Either you or Mrs. Fisher must possess some Negro blood." (012-021)

When the newspaper headline breaks — revealing that Givens, Snobbcraft, Buggerie, and Kretin are all of Negro ancestry — Helen sees it from her hospital bed. "Matthew rushed to her side as she slumped back on the pillow in a dead faint" (012-039).

## Character Analysis

Helen functions as both a realistic character and a symbolic device. As a character, she is warm, devoted, and genuinely distressed by the chaos that engulfs her family. Her plea to Matthew — "Oh, Matthew, Honey, please forgive me. I love you, my husband. Please don't leave me" (012-044) — is one of the novel's few moments of unironic emotion. Her instinct is to take responsibility for the "disgrace" she believes she has brought upon her husband, never suspecting that the "Negro blood" came from him.

> "You needn't worry about me, Helen. I'll stick by you as long as you'll have me and without you life wouldn't be worth a dime. You're not responsible for the color of our baby, my dear. I'm the guilty one." (012-047)

Matthew's confession — "I'm the guilty one" — passes as gallantry in the moment, but the reader knows its literal truth. Helen, who knows nothing of her husband's past, hears only devotion. This scene condenses the novel's central irony into a single domestic tableau: a white woman apologizing to her whitened husband for the darkness of their child, while he, the actual source, comforts her with a half-truth she cannot parse.

As a symbol, Helen represents the hidden truth that the entire social order depends on. Her unborn child is proof that the treatment is not permanent, and her father's monograph revealing this fact threatens to destroy everything. She is the embodied contradiction at the heart of the Black-No-More enterprise: the treatment can change skin color but not genes, and the "Negro problem" — redefined as a biological inheritance — reasserts itself in the nursery.

## Key Episodes

- Helen is introduced in Chapter 4 at the Givens home, embarrassed by her mother's country ways (004-053–004-062)
- Her marriage to Matthew Fisher cements the Crookman-Fisher alliance
- On the eve of Election Day, she gives birth to a "very, very dark" son (012-012–012-021)
- The newspaper headline reveals her father's Negro ancestry; she collapses upon reading it (012-039)
- She pleads with Matthew not to leave her, taking blame for the "disgrace" (012-044)
- Matthew's ambiguous confession — "I'm the guilty one" — closes the scene (012-047)
- In the epilogue, she reappears at the Fisher estate, calm and at peace while the outside world convulses (013-120)
