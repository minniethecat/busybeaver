## Overview

Racial identity — the question of what makes someone Black or white — is the philosophical core of *Black No More*. Schuyler's novel systematically demolishes every possible answer: biology (the treatment changes it), culture (characters change their behavior with their skin), law (the one-drop rule becomes unenforceable), and self-identification (no one knows who is "really" what). The novel's conclusion is radical for 1931: racial identity is a social construct maintained by economic interest, and when those interests shift, identity dissolves.

## In the Narrative

Max Disher's transformation into Matthew Fisher is the novel's identity laboratory. As a Black man, Max is proud, streetwise, and confined to Harlem. As a white man, "Matthew Fisher" joins the Knights of Nordica, courts a white Southern belle, and rises in Atlanta society (004-001). The question Schuyler poses is whether Max has changed his identity or merely his appearance — and the answer is deliberately ambiguous. Bunny Brown, reunited with Max after his own treatment, asks "Why it's me, Bunny Brown, you big sap!" (006-017), suggesting that personality persists beneath skin color.

## Historical Context

The 1920s–1930s saw intense debate about racial identity in Black intellectual circles. The NAACP's legal strategy, led by Charles Hamilton Houston and Thurgood Marshall, relied on the premise that race was a legally recognizable category. The Harlem Renaissance celebrated Black identity as a source of cultural pride. Schuyler's contrarian position — that race was a fiction that should be abandoned rather than celebrated — put him at odds with most of his contemporaries and prefigures later "post-racial" and "colorblind" arguments, though Schuyler's version is more satirical than utopian.

## Satirical Treatment

The novel's treatment of identity is darkly comic. Characters who have undergone the treatment develop an "exaggerated development of race pride" (008-003) as a telltale sign of their former Blackness — suggesting that racial identity, once lost, is performed rather than lived. The richest irony is reserved for the white characters who cannot tell who is "really" white and begin to suspect everyone, including themselves. Schuyler's implication is that racial identity has always been a performance; the treatment merely makes the performance visible.
