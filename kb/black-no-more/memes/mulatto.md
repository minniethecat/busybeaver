"Mulatto" — the term for a person of mixed Black and white ancestry — functions in [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]] as both a descriptive label and a satirical device. Schuyler uses the category to expose the fundamental impossibility of America's racial classification system. The novel's characters inhabit a world where "mulatto" is simultaneously a distinct social identity, a legal category, and an arbitrary distinction that reveals the absurdity of racial thinking. By the novel's end, when America has become "enthusiastically mulatto-minded" [013-120], the term has completed its arc from stigma to fashion — demonstrating that racial categories are entirely social constructs, available for reinvention when the incentives change.

## Mulatto Characters as Social Types

Schuyler populates his novel with mulatto characters who occupy specific social niches. Henry Johnson, "the beefy, sleek-jowled, mulatto 'Numbers' banker" [001-092], represents the criminal economy operating adjacent to respectable Harlem. The "wriggling, practically nude, mulatto chorines" [001-039] of the cabaret floor show embody the sexualized spectacle of mixed-race bodies in the entertainment industry. At the Black-No-More sanitarium, "a soft-voiced mulatto teller" [003-035] handles the financial transactions, while "mulatto clerks were hustling excitedly back and forth" [005-045] at the National Social Equality League. These figures are not individuated — they are social types, suggesting that "mulatto" itself was a role rather than an identity.

## The Anxiety of Reproduction

The novel's white supremacists are haunted by the prospect of mulatto reproduction. Charlie Foster voices the fear directly: "there'll be hell to pay when you whiten up a lot o' these darkies and them mulatto babies start appearing here and there" [001-093]. The "mulatto baby" is the ultimate anxiety — visible proof that the color line has been breached. This fear drives much of the political opposition to [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More]], culminating in the demand for genealogical examinations to distinguish "pure" whites from those with "a mulatto ancestor" [011-059].

> "Seems all right, Junius, but there'll be hell to pay when you whiten up a lot o' these darkies and them mulatto babies start appearing here and there. Watcha gonna do then?" [001-093]

## The Violence of Classification

The novel does not flinch from the violence that underlies racial classification. A "slender, anemic, ratty-looking mulatto Negro was almost beaten to death by a gigantic black laundress whose purse he had snatched" [002-064] — the violence is intra-racial, complicating any simple narrative of white-on-Black oppression. The mulatto body is vulnerable from all directions, belonging fully to neither category in a world that demands categorical clarity.

## The Great Inversion

The novel's most audacious satirical move is the final inversion of mulatto status. After Crookman's revelation that the newly whitened population is lighter than "real" whites, the entire society pivots: "America was definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded" [013-120]. Skin stains, artificial tans, and cosmetic darkening become fashionable. The same racial hybridity that was once the deepest social anxiety becomes the most desirable aesthetic. A society that had spent centuries enforcing the color line now competes to erase it from the other direction — proving Schuyler's thesis that racial value is entirely arbitrary and entirely malleable.

> America was definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded. [013-120]
