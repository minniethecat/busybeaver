## Overview

The Back-to-Africa Society is [[george-schuyler]]'s satirical version of Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association — a Black nationalist organization whose platform of African repatriation is portrayed as a commercial enterprise rather than a genuine political movement. Led by the stentorian [[santop-licorice]], the Society raises funds from followers, sells merchandise, and denounces competitors — until the [[black-no-more-treatment]] provides its membership with a faster, cheaper, and more individually convenient route out of their condition: becoming white.

The Society's central satirical irony is compressed into a single observation: a leader whose entire program is separation from America cannot tolerate his followers choosing their own form of separation. When Black-No-More begins depleting the Society's membership, Licorice's response is not to celebrate his constituents' escape from racial oppression but to denounce them for deserting his organization.

## Background

### The Garveyite Parallel

The Back-to-Africa Society directly satirizes Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), which in the 1920s had built one of the largest mass movements in Black American history on a platform of racial pride, economic self-sufficiency, and eventual repatriation to Africa. Garvey's Black Star Line — a shipping company intended to facilitate the return — was the subject of federal prosecution for mail fraud, and Schuyler, who had sparred with Garvey in the pages of *The Messenger*, channels that skepticism into the novel.

The key satirical move is economic. In Schuyler's rendering, the Society's platform is not a political commitment but a product, and its leader is not a prophet but a salesman whose "stentorian voice" [005-040] is his primary business asset.

### Organizational Rivalries

The Society exists within a competitive ecosystem of racial organizations, each with its own constituency and revenue model. Licorice is described as "always delighted when Dr. Beard met with any sort of rebuff or embarrassment" [005-099] — the rivalry between the Back-to-Africa Society and the [[national-social-equality-league]] is professional competition between two race men selling different products to the same market. Beard offers integration and social equality; Licorice offers separation and African destiny. Both depend on the continued existence of the racial grievances they claim to address.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Membership Crisis

When the Black-No-More treatment begins transforming Black Americans into white Americans, the Back-to-Africa Society faces an existential crisis faster and more brutally than any other organization in the novel. Its membership — people who had invested in the promise of African repatriation — discover that they can achieve separation from American racism instantly, privately, and without any collective action, simply by visiting a clinic.

Licorice is described as "daily raising his stentorian voice in denunciation of the race for deserting his organization" [005-040]. The formulation is precise and devastating: he denounces the race, not the racists. His anger is directed at the constituency that abandoned him, not at the system that produced the abandonment.

### The Attorney General Episode

In one of the novel's few scenes of cross-organizational cooperation — and its most explicit parallel to the real-world politics of the period — Licorice joins other Black leaders in petitioning the Attorney General to shut down Black-No-More, Incorporated. When the Attorney General's reply is unfavorable, Licorice "read the reply … with much malicious satisfaction" [005-099], pleased that his rival Beard has been rebuffed even though the reply harms Licorice's own interests. The scene crystallizes the novel's thesis: racial leadership is a zero-sum competition, not a solidarity movement.

### Post-Collapse Adaptation

Unlike the National Social Equality League, which ceases operations entirely, the Back-to-Africa Society's leader proves adaptable. Licorice appears in the novel's later chapters campaigning for the [[republican-party-harlem|Republican Party]], having pivoted from Back-to-Africa advocacy to electoral politics. The transformation reveals the portable nature of the racial leadership skill set: the constituency changes, the platform changes, but the technique — a "stentorian voice" and a capacity to monetize grievance — transfers intact.

## Satirical Function

### Garveyism as Commerce

Schuyler's satire of the Back-to-Africa movement is inseparable from his broader argument that all race-based politics is, at bottom, a business. The Society's specific target — Garveyism — was a live political fight when the novel was written in 1931. Schuyler, associated with the socialist magazine *The Messenger*, had been a prominent Garvey critic, and the novel extends that criticism into fiction.

The satirical charge is not that the Back-to-Africa idea is wrong on the merits but that its organizational form transforms political commitment into financial dependency. Members pay dues, buy merchandise, attend rallies — and the leader's prosperity depends on their continued participation, not on their liberation.

### The Inconvenience of Liberation

The novel's most original satirical observation about the Back-to-Africa Society is that the organization is threatened not by repression but by opportunity. The Black-No-More treatment gives its membership exactly what the Society had promised — an escape from the condition of Blackness in America — but through individual action rather than collective mobilization. The Society collapses not because it failed but because it was bypassed by a better product.
