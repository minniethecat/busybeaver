## Overview

The Harlem Republican Party machine appears briefly in Chuck Foster's backstory. Foster, before joining Black-No-More, Incorporated, had achieved an "important place in local Republican politics" (003-043) — a mark of his social climbing and political connections. In the 1930s, Black voters in Northern cities were still predominantly Republican, the party of Lincoln, and local Republican machines were important sources of patronage and power for Black politicians.

## Role in the Narrative

The Republican machine is part of the institutional landscape that the treatment dissolves. As Black voters become white, their political loyalties shift, and the patronage networks that sustained Black political organizations collapse. The novel's political satire extends to both parties: Republicans lose their Black base, Democrats lose their Southern white base, and both scramble to adapt to the new racial uncertainty.

## Satirical Function

Demonstrates that Black political organizations, like Black businesses and fraternal orders, depend on the continued existence of a distinct and identifiable Black constituency. When that constituency vanishes, so does the political machine.
