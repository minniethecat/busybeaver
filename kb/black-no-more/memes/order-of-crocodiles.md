## Overview

The Ancient and Honorable Order of Crocodiles is Schuyler's satirical name for a Black fraternal secret society — one of the many such organizations that constituted Harlem's institutional infrastructure. Hank Johnson's election as "Grand Permanent Shogun" (003-016) of the order is mentioned as one of his credentials for joining Crookman's venture: he is a man who understands the business of Black institutions. The order's absurdly grandiloquent name captures the mixture of aspiration and absurdity that Schuyler saw in Black organizational life.

## Background

Fraternal orders — Masons, Elks, Odd Fellows, and their Black counterparts — were among the most important Black institutions in the early 20th century, providing mutual aid, social networking, and burial insurance to members excluded from white organizations. Hank Johnson's rise through the Crocodiles is presented as evidence of his business acumen: the order, like everything else in the novel, is primarily an economic entity.

## Role in the Narrative

The Crocodiles appear only in Hank Johnson's backstory but represent an entire class of Black institutions whose membership base evaporates as the treatment spreads. They are the institutional infrastructure of Black social life — and their disappearance is part of the novel's depiction of social mortality.

## Satirical Function

Parodies the grandiosity of fraternal titles and rituals, suggesting that Black institutional life, for all its genuine value, was also a vehicle for individual ambition and social climbing.
