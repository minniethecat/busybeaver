## Overview

Walter Snickelfritz represents the white working-class men who form the rank-and-file of the Knights of Nordica. His comically Germanic name ("Snickelfritz") signals Schuyler's satirical treatment of "Nordic" racial pretensions — the same men who claim descent from Nordic warriors have names that reveal their mongrel European origins.

## Role in the Narrative

Snickelfritz appears in the Paradise, South Carolina labor conflict (Chapter 7), where white mill workers confront competition from whitened ex-Negroes. His economic grievances — low wages, company-town exploitation — are real, but they are channeled by organizations like the Knights of Nordica into racial rather than class consciousness.

## Satirical Function

Embodies the novel's argument that white working-class racism is a product of elite manipulation — that men like Snickelfritz have more in common economically with Black workers than with their white employers, but are prevented from recognizing this by the racial ideology that serves those employers' interests.

## The Name as Class Marker

"Snickelfritz" is not merely a comic name — it is a class signifier. Its Germanic origins mark Snickelfritz as descended from one of the large European immigrant groups (German, Irish, Eastern European) whose whiteness was, in the early twentieth century, still contested and conditional. The Knights of Nordica's "Nordic" ideology posited a racial hierarchy within whiteness itself, with Anglo-Saxons at the top and "lesser" European stocks below. Snickelfritz, whose name marks him as unambiguously non-Anglo-Saxon, is thus an ironic recruit to an organization whose racial ideology would, if applied consistently, place him beneath its leadership. This is the novel's recurring point about white supremacy: it demands allegiance from people it does not fully serve.

## Chapter 7 and the Labor Theme

The Paradise, South Carolina labor conflict (Chapter 7) is one of the novel's most direct engagements with class. White mill workers, facing wage pressure from the newly whitened workers entering the labor market, respond with racial violence rather than collective bargaining. Snickelfritz is the face of this misdirected anger — a worker who channels his legitimate economic grievance into a racial cause that benefits his employer, not his class. Schuyler, a lifelong critic of both racism and capital, uses this chapter to argue that the two are structurally linked: racism is not an irrational prejudice but a tool of labor discipline.

## Position in the Novel's Argument

Snickelfritz matters to the novel's argument because he disproves the premise that racism is an elite pathology. The Knights of Nordica's leadership — figures like [[rev-givens|Reverend Givens]] and Senator Bosh — are cynical operators who use race for power. Snickelfritz, by contrast, is a true believer. His racism is sincere, which makes it more dangerous, not less. The novel suggests that the sincerity of working-class racism is what makes it a durable political force — and what makes it so useful to elites who do not share it.
