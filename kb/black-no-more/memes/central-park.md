## Overview

Central Park is the novel's transitional space — neither the Black social world of Harlem's Seventh Avenue nor the white world Max will enter as Matthew Fisher, but the liminal ground where he walks, broods, and ultimately decides to become white. Schuyler uses the Park as a geographic metaphor for the threshold state Max inhabits between his humiliation at the Honky Tonk Club and his transformation: the act of walking through the Park is the act of walking away from his old identity (002-009, 002-033).

## Narrative Significance

The Park's function is structural rather than descriptive: it is the middle term in the novel's opening triad (Harlem nightclub → Central Park → Crookman's office). Max enters the Park as Max Disher and leaves it resolved to become someone else. The Park's public, anonymous, racially mixed character — a space where both Black and white New Yorkers circulate — makes it the only setting in the novel where the transition between racial identities can be privately contemplated in public.

## Three Passages Through the Park

Max passes through Central Park at three key moments, each marking a different stage in his transformation:

**First passage — Escape and decision.** Fleeing the reporters at the Black-No-More sanitarium, Max leaps into a taxi and yells "Central Park!" — "It was the only place he could think of at the moment. He wanted to have time to compose his mind, to plan the future in this great world of whiteness" (002-009). The Park is the first place the newly white Max goes: not to Harlem, not downtown, but to the green in-between.

**Second passage — Peace.** As the cab exits the Park at 110th Street, Max feels "curiously enough, a feeling of peace. There were all the old familiar sights: the all-night speakeasies, the frankfurter stands, the loiterers, the late pedestrians, the chop suey joints, the careening taxicabs, the bawdy laughter" (002-033). The passage out of the Park is a passage back into the familiar — but Max himself is no longer familiar to anyone who knew him.

**Third passage — Mastery.** An hour later, Max's taxi "bowled through Central Park" again, this time with luggage bound for the Pennsylvania Station and Atlanta. "He would just play around, enjoy life and laugh at the white folks up his sleeve. God! What an adventure! What a treat it would be to mingle with white people in places where as a youth he had never dared to enter. At last he felt like an American citizen" (002-081). The Park, once a refuge, is now simply a road through — the threshold has been crossed.
