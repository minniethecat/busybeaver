## Overview

Madame Sisseretta Blandish represents the Black beauty entrepreneurs whose businesses were built on the promise of racial transformation through cosmetics. Her name — combining the grandiosity of "Madame" with the ironic "Blandish" (to flatter or coax) — signals [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s satirical treatment of an industry that profited from racial self-hatred while claiming to serve racial uplift. Like the real Madame C.J. Walker, Blandish built an empire on products that promised Black women proximity to white beauty standards.

## Role in the Narrative

Blandish appears in the novel's economic background as part of the cosmetic industry ecosystem destroyed by the treatment. Her business — along with "the firms that made the [[racial-passing|whitening]] and straightening chemicals" — is "bankrupted" (005-041) when the treatment offers actual whiteness at a price competitive with her products.

## Satirical Function

Parodies the Black beauty industry's entrepreneurial grandiosity, suggesting that figures like Madame C.J. Walker were simultaneously racial heroines and profiteers from racial self-hatred.
