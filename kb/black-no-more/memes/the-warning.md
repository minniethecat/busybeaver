## Overview

*The Warning* was an eight-page propaganda newspaper published by the [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]] for over six months leading into the 1936 election cycle, edited by [[matthew-fisher|Matthew Fisher]]. Its lurid red headlines and poorly-drawn quarter-page cartoons served as the primary media organ through which the Knights mobilized white Southern workers against [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]] and the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]]. The paper exemplifies Schuyler's satire of how nativist media manufactured racial panic for political and financial gain.

## Historical Context

*The Warning* reflects the real-world proliferation of Klan and nativist newspapers in the 1920s–1930s, such as *The Fiery Cross* and *The Fellowship Forum*, which used sensational headlines and cartoons to spread anti-Black, anti-Catholic, and anti-immigrant propaganda. These publications were often financially supported by wealthy conservatives who saw them as bulwarks against labor organizing — a dynamic Schuyler directly captures through Rev. Givens' fundraising appeals.

## In the Narrative

*The Warning* appears at multiple critical junctures in the novel. It is first described as a six-month-old publication of the Knights of Nordica, carrying "lurid red headlines and poorly-drawn quarter-page cartoons" (PN 006-003). The paper's editorials proved devastatingly effective: white workers who had initially felt "a secret feeling akin to relief" at Black-No-More's economic disruption were transformed by the paper's rhetoric into a mob "yelling for the blood of Dr. Crookman and his associates" (PN 006-011).

During the campaign strategy sessions, Matthew Fisher outlines the paper's political function: it would launch "a campaign of denunciation against the Republicans," linking them to "the Pope, Black-No-More and anything else we can think of" (PN 009-011). The paper also printed form letters for readers to send to Congress, leveraging reader activism as a political weapon.

The paper's influence extended into the election season: the "vitriolic articles in The Warning" fed Democratic sentiment and anti-Republican feeling well before the party conventions (PN 010-033). Its reach even touched [[happy-hill|Happy Hill, Mississippi]], where Rev. [[alex-mcphule|Alex McPhule]] read a copy and was inspired to seek a lynching victim to boost his revival's attendance (PN 013-068).

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler uses *The Warning* to parody the real-world propaganda machinery of white supremacist organizations and the sensationalist journalism of the 1920s–1930s. The paper's format — eight pages, lurid cartoons, form letters — mirrors the publications of the Ku Klux Klan and other nativist groups. Matthew Fisher's cynical admission that "the people never remember anything" reveals the paper as pure political theater, not genuine journalism. The satire extends to the media literacy of its readers: white Southern workers abandon economic self-interest for racial grievance solely on the paper's say-so.
