## Overview

Dr. Napoleon Wellington Jackson is Schuyler's satire of the Negro academic-as-race-man — a figure whose scholarly pretensions are as elaborate as his name and as hollow as his arguments. Tall, lanky, "sooty black" with "long, apelike arms" and a "diminutive egg-shaped head," Jackson is physically caricatured in ways that mock both racist physiognomy and the self-importance of the Negro intellectual class. He serves as Dr. Beard's secretary and is appointed chairman of the Committee for the Preservation of Negro Racial Integrity, where he delivers a pompous, padding-filled address about the "bally turmoil" caused by Black-No-More.

## Biography

Jackson is introduced at the N.S.E.L. meeting in Chapter 5, "his tall, lanky secretary got slowly to his feet" (005-054). Beard appoints him chairman of the temporary committee, praising Jackson's "scholarship, his high sense of duty and his deep love of the suffering black race," crediting him with "many sorrow songs," translations of Latin poets, and "authoritative work on the Greek language" (005-052).

Jackson's first address to the committee is a masterpiece of windy evasion: "the Negro race is face to face with a grave crisis. I—ah—presume it is er—ah unnecessary for me to go into any details" before launching into a complaint that "our people are forgetting shamelessly their-ah duty to the-ah organizations" (005-055). During his leisure time, he writes articles "bristling with references" seeking to prove "that the plantation shouts of Southern Negro peons were superior to any of Beethoven's symphonies" and "that the city of Benin was the original site of the Garden of Eden" (005-055).

> "Hhmm! Hu‑umn! Now er—ah, gentlemen," began Dr. Jackson, rocking back on his heels, taking off his eye glasses and beginning to polish them with a silk kerchief, "as you know, the Negro race is face to face with a grave crisis." (005-055)

When the meeting descends into revival-like chaos — with Rev. Gronne marching and Colonel Roberts rocking — Jackson, "sensing his opportunity, began to sing a spiritual in his rich soprano voice. The others immediately joined him. The very air seemed charged with emotion" (005-069). The scene confirms that for all his scholarly pretension, Jackson reverts to the performance of authentic blackness when it serves him — his "rich soprano" more effective than his "learned articles" at commanding the room.

Jackson is later among the Negro leaders targeted by Bunny Brown to lecture for the Knights of Nordica (010-100), alongside Beard, Gronne, and Roberts — the entire N.S.E.L. circle now for sale. His economic desperation after Black-No-More renders Negro leadership obsolete makes him a willing collaborator with the white supremacist organization he once ostensibly opposed.

## Satirical Function

Jackson satirizes the intellectual wing of the Negro leadership class — the scholar who uses academia not to pursue truth but to manufacture racial prestige. His claim that plantation shouts surpass Beethoven is not presented as a legitimate argument about cultural value but as a self-serving inversion designed to flatter his audience and secure his position. His physical description — "a sooty black" with simian features — is uncomfortable to modern readers but functions within the novel's satiric economy to strip Jackson of the dignity his degrees are supposed to confer, exposing the gap between intellectual credential and embodied reality.

The name "Napoleon Wellington" — combining two conquerors who lost their final battles — encodes the satire before the character speaks a word.

## Key Episodes

> He was a sooty black, very broad shouldered, with long, apelike arms, a diminutive egg-shaped head that sat on his collar like a hen's egg on a demitasse cup and eyes that protruded so far from his head that they seemed about to fall out. (005-052)

> During his leisure time, which was naturally considerable, he wrote long and learned articles, bristling with references, for the more intellectual magazines, in which he sought to prove conclusively that the plantation shouts of Southern Negro peons were superior to any of Beethoven's symphonies and that the city of Benin was the original site of the Garden of Eden. (005-055)
