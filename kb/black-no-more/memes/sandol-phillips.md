## Overview

Sandol Phillips is Dr. Crookman's Senegalese associate and the living exhibit of Black-No-More's transformative power. At Crookman's press conference at the Phyllis Wheatley Hotel, Phillips serves a dual function: his Nordic-white appearance in the present ("the most Nordic looking person in the room") and the photograph of his former Black self ("a very black man... with bushy Negro hair, flat nose and full lips") together constitute the treatment's most compelling advertisement. A former aviator in the French Army, Phillips later appears as Crookman's pilot, muttering "Ah, zese Americains" while inspecting the engine — a brief moment of character that hints at a cosmopolitan life beyond his function as racial exhibit.

## Biography

### The Press Conference Demonstration

Phillips appears first at Crookman's New York press conference, summoned from the crowd: "'Come here, Sandol,' he called, turning to a pale white youth standing on the outskirts of the crowd, who was the most Nordic looking person in the room. 'This man is a Senegalese, a former aviator in the French Army. He is living proof that what I claim is true'" (001-085). Crookman then displays a photograph of "a very black man, somewhat resembling Sandol but with bushy Negro hair, flat nose and full lips" and announces: "This is Sandol as he looked before taking my treatment" (001-085).

The demonstration is the rhetorical climax of the press conference: the newspapermen are "properly awed," take photographs of Crookman, his associates, and Sandol, and depart — leaving only Max Disher, whose decision to undergo the treatment has just been sealed by what he has witnessed.

### The Denver Flight

Phillips reappears in a brief scene piloting Crookman's aircraft to Denver. After a period of silence, "Sandol, the erstwhile Senegalese, stepped from the cockpit grinning. 'Ah, zese Americains,' he muttered to himself as he went over the engine, examining everything minutely" (005-???). When asked their destination, he replies simply: "Den-vair." This scene, though brief, gives Phillips a rare moment of perspective — the Senegalese-French aviator observing Americans with bemusement — that distinguishes him from the other background functionaries in Crookman's enterprise.

## Satirical Function

Phillips embodies a specific irony within the novel's racial satire: the "most Nordic looking person in the room" is a Senegalese, and the treatment's effectiveness is demonstrated through a reversal that makes visible what the novel's world will soon experience at scale. His transformation from "very black" to ultra-Nordic literalizes the instability of racial categories: Sandol Phillips, the Senegalese, passes more perfectly as white than the actual white people in the room.
