## Overview

The Ku Klux Klan — never named directly but the obvious referent for the [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]] — was at the height of its 20th-century power when [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] wrote *[[black-no-more|Black No More]]*. With an estimated 4–5 million members in the mid-1920s, the Klan's influence extended far beyond [[the-southland|the South]], shaping national politics and culture. Schuyler's decision to parody rather than name the Klan is itself a satirical choice: [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]] of Nordica are funnier and more revealing precisely because they strip away the Klan's pretensions to reveal a profit-making enterprise.

## Role in the Narrative

The Klan's absence from the novel's explicit references is notable. Schuyler never uses the word "Klan," preferring "Knights of [[knights-of-nordica|Nordica]]" as a defamiliarizing device. This choice allows readers to see the organization's absurdity without the emotional charge of the real Klan's violence — making the satire sharper by making it more clinical.

## Satirical Function

By creating a fictional Klan parody that is more plausible than the real Klan, Schuyler invites readers to see the actual Klan as equally absurd — and equally motivated by economic self-interest rather than sincere ideology.
