## Overview

The Wheatley Trust Company was a financial institution in [[harlem|Harlem]], named for Phyllis Wheatley, the celebrated 18th-century Black poet. It appears in the novel alongside the [[douglass-bank|Douglass Bank]] as one of the Harlem institutions swept up in the financial panic following the mass adoption of the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]]. Schuyler uses the trust company, through its evocative name and its depiction as a site of scenes "similar" to the chaos at the Douglass Bank, to deepen the satire of how individual choices to change race cascade into institutional collapse.

## Background

The Wheatley Trust Company is introduced in Chapter 3 during the description of Harlem's post-treatment financial chaos. Like the [[douglass-bank|Douglass Bank]], it is a Black-owned or Black-oriented institution whose clientele is rapidly disappearing as Harlem residents take the Black-No-More treatment and pass into white society. Its name invokes Phyllis Wheatley (c. 1753–1784), the first published African American poet, connecting it to the tradition of Black cultural achievement and institutional pride that the novel simultaneously honors and satirizes.

## Role in the Narrative

The Wheatley Trust Company appears in a single scene, but its function is cumulative: "similar scenes were being enacted at the Wheatley Trust Company and at the local Post Office station" — meaning the same panicked withdrawals and understaffed counters seen at the Douglass Bank were happening across Harlem's financial infrastructure (PN 003-033). By naming the Wheatley Trust alongside the [[douglass-bank|Douglass Bank]], Schuyler makes the point that this is not a single-bank failure but a systemic crisis: the entire economic ecosystem of Black Harlem is collapsing. The juxtaposition of the two institutions — both named for towering figures of Black history (Frederick Douglass and Phyllis Wheatley) — underscores what is being lost.

## Satirical Function

The Wheatley Trust's brief mention is part of Schuyler's economic satire: institutions built on racial solidarity cannot survive when individuals can opt out of race. The trust company's name — honoring a figure of Black literary achievement — becomes ironic when the community that would value such heritage is literally dissolving. The "exodus from the locality" of moving vans that follows suggests not just a financial crisis but a population transfer, a re-segregation through market forces rather than law. The satire targets both the fragility of Black capitalism and the absurdity of an economic system where changing skin color changes everything.

## Key Passages

> Similar scenes were being enacted at the Wheatley Trust Company and at the local Post Office station. An observer passing up and down the streets would have noted a general exodus from the locality. Moving vans were everywhere. (PN 003-033)
