## Overview

President Harold Goosie serves as Schuyler's satirical portrait of Republican presidential mediocrity — a leader whose response to the Black-No-More crisis is to "give a great deal of study" and deploy a corps of clerks before passing the matter to Congress. He embodies the novel's contempt for political leadership that substitutes process for action and platitude for principle. Goosie is nominated for re-election, campaigns on meaningless slogans, and wins amid the national chaos — the system perpetuates itself regardless of merit.

## Biography

Goosie is introduced in Chapter 9, when Rev. Givens' radio campaign against Black-No-More prompts the President to announce he is "giving a great deal of study to the questions raised" (009-032). Six weeks after requesting Congressional action, the resolution is passed and he names the members of the investigative commission (009-032).

As the election approaches, the Republican Party finds itself in trouble — "people were not exactly for the Democrats but they were against the Republicans" (010-033). The Republicans deploy Goosie's frequent radio addresses and the Knights of Nordica's growing influence to rally support. At the Chicago convention, Goosie is nominated on the first ballot with Vice-President Gump as his running mate, on a platform "whose chief characteristic was vagueness" (010-048).

Goosie's campaign rhetoric consists of empty affirmations: "I intend to make my second term as honest and efficient as my first" (010-056) — which the narrator notes is "a dire threat" despite being "supposed to be a fine promise." His character is compared favorably to Roosevelt by one newspaper writer (010-054).

In the epilogue, a delegation of the new Caucasians — now whiter than the old whites and facing discrimination — "waited upon President Goosie firmly denounced the social trend and called upon the government to do something about it" (013-120). Goosie and Gump are "almost unanimously reelected" amid the national crisis triggered by Buggerie's ancestry revelations (013-125), a testament to how the political establishment absorbs even cataclysmic social upheaval without meaningful change.

## Satirical Function

Goosie functions as a composite satire of presidential inaction, Republican electoral machinery, and the emptiness of American political rhetoric. His name itself — "Goosie" — suggests foolishness, and his administration's defining characteristic is its ability to respond to crisis with procedure while avoiding substance. The novel's political satire reaches its logical endpoint when Goosie and Gump win "almost unanimously" even as the country descends into racial panic and martial law.

## Key Episodes
