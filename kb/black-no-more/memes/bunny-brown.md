## Overview

Bunny Brown is Max Disher's best friend and the novel's primary source of comic relief. One of the "two of the outstanding gay blades in black Harlem" (001-012), Bunny follows Max through the treatment and provides several of the novel's funniest moments — including his post-treatment reunion with Max, where he announces himself with "Why it's me, Bunny Brown, you big sap!" (006-017).

## Biography

Bunny and Max "had been pals ever since the war" (001-012), sharing a weakness for "yellow money, yellow women and yellow taxicabs." After Max disappears into the treatment, Bunny eventually follows, and the two reunite as white men in comically anti-climactic fashion. Bunny's easygoing personality — "Niggers didn't really have any race pride" (002-079) — contrasts with Max's more calculating approach to racial transformation.

## Character Analysis

Bunny functions as a foil to Max: where Max schemes, Bunny drifts; where Max infiltrates the power structure, Bunny just wants to have a good time. His casual dismissal of race pride suggests that for ordinary Black Americans, racial identity was less a matter of ideology than of circumstance — and that changing circumstance means changing identity without existential crisis.

## Bunny and Max: The Double Act

The Max-Bunny friendship is one of the novel's structural pillars. They are introduced together in the opening pages as Harlem's "gay blades," and their contrasting temperaments — Max's ambition and Bunny's contentment — drive much of the comic tension. When Max has transformed into Matthew Fisher and risen through the Knights of Nordica, Bunny's reappearance brings the narrative back to its comic roots. Their dynamic embodies Schuyler's satirical point: that underneath the machinery of race, people remain fundamentally themselves.[PN:001]

## Post-Treatment Life

After his treatment, Bunny adopts the identity of a white man with no more gravity than he applied to being Black. While Max/Matthew agonizes over exposure, Bunny treats the whole enterprise as an extended joke. This insouciance is both his charm and his limitation as a character — he is incapable of the moral reflection that Schuyler refuses to provide in the narrative itself.[PN:001]
