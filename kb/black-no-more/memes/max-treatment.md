## Overview

Max Disher's Black-No-More treatment is the inciting event of the novel. On New Year's Day, 1933 — the morning after being rebuffed by a white woman at an Atlanta nightclub with the words "I never dance with niggers" (001-051) — Max presents himself at Dr. Junius Crookman's newly opened Harlem sanitarium. He becomes the first person to undergo the experimental procedure that transforms Black skin to white, setting in motion the cascading social transformations that drive the entire narrative.

## Context

The treatment occurs against the backdrop of Max's humiliation at the Honky Tonk Club the previous evening. Max, a self-described "dapper" Harlem bon vivant, had approached a beautiful white woman from Atlanta named Helen, only to be publicly rejected on racial grounds (001-045). This rejection — combined with the news that Dr. Crookman, a former classmate, had reportedly perfected a skin-whitening procedure — drives Max to seek transformation (001-065).

Dr. Junius Crookman had spent years in Germany studying endocrinology, and upon his return to America announced that he had discovered a way to alter skin pigmentation through a combination of glandular treatments. The procedure had been sensationalized in the press, but no one had yet undergone it publicly (001-075).

## Narrative Account

On the morning after the nightclub incident, Max Disher makes his way to the Crookman Sanitarium in Harlem. The building, still under construction during Crookman's earlier press interviews, is now operational, its exterior emblazoned with the words "Black-No-More" running its full length in green letters (002-060).

### The Investigation Deepens

Inside, Max encounters Crookman — tall, ebony black, and dressed in a white coat — along with his business partners Hank Johnson and Chuck Foster. When Max announces himself, Crookman is at first surprised — the slender white man before him looks nothing like the round-faced, brown-skinned Max Disher he knew (001-087). But Crookman, ever the scientist, reassures him: the voice is unmistakably Max's (002-044).

The procedure itself involves an electrical apparatus that Crookman has designed. Max is strapped into the machine, and within three days the treatment is complete: "Slowly, haltingly, Max Disher dragged his way down the hall" of the sanitarium, emerging as a white man (002-001)(002-043).

## Aftermath

Max's transformation has immediate and far-reaching consequences. Freed from the social constraints of Black identity, he renames himself Matthew Fisher and reinvents himself as a white man — initially an anthropologist, later a white supremacist leader. His personal transformation prefigures the mass transformations that follow: within months, Black-No-More, Incorporated, processes millions of Black Americans, triggering widespread social panic and political upheaval.

Max's case also serves as Crookman's proof of concept. Once Max visibly demonstrates the treatment's efficacy, the sanitarium is overwhelmed with patients (002-004). The business model — fifty dollars per treatment — proves extraordinarily lucrative, and within a year Crookman opens sanitariums in twenty cities (005-024).

This episode illustrates the novel's broader satirical method: historical events are not merely referenced but structurally inverted, with the machinery of racial power consistently producing outcomes opposite to those intended by its architects.
