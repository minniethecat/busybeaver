## Overview

Gorman Gay, the National Chairman of the Republican Party, appears in two significant scenes: a telephone conversation with Attorney General Walter Brybe in which he reduces the Negro leaders' resolution to a matter of campaign cash flow, and a late-novel negotiation with Black-No-More executives where he attempts to extract a five-million-dollar campaign contribution. In both scenes, Gay embodies Schuyler's critique of American politics as a system in which race is never a moral question but always a financial one.

## First Appearance: The Brybe Call (Chapter 5)

Gay's first scene is a master class in political cynicism delivered via telephone. Attorney General Brybe, having received the Negro leaders' resolution condemning Black-No-More, calls Gay for instructions. The exchange — with its comic prelude of the valet's "Nossuh... this heah is Mistah Gay's valet" (005-080) — establishes Gay as a Southerner whose drawl and racial attitudes are inseparable from his political calculus.

Gay dismisses the Negro leaders with three devastating observations: they have no money ("They ain't got a thin dime"), the real money is with the Black-No-More corporation ("it's this other crowd that's holding the heavy jack"), and the party needs to pay its debts ("'course you know we gotta clean up our deficit") (005-083). When Brybe mentions white opposition to Black-No-More, Gay scoffs: "There's no money behind it much and besides it's in states we can't carry anyhow" (005-090). Race, for Gay, is not a civil rights issue or a moral issue or even an electoral issue in the broad sense — it is strictly a matter of which donors can be tapped and which constituencies can be ignored.

## Second Appearance: The Shakedown (Chapter 10)

Gay reappears late in the novel, meeting with Hank Johnson, Chuck Foster, and Dr. Crookman to solicit a campaign contribution. His opening line, "Unfortunately our friends are not contributing with their accustomed liberality" (010-002), is characteristic: every sentence is a negotiation.

The scene escalates from polite request to barely veiled extortion. Gay demands five million dollars from the Black-No-More executives — not for any policy concession, but simply because "if we don't get a contribution of about that size from you we're liable to lose this election" (010-024). When Crookman resists, Gay makes the implicit threat explicit: "you want to think of those poor women with their black babies. What will they do if you fellows leave the country or if the Democrats win and you have to close all of your places?" (010-024). The Republicans will protect the Lying-In hospitals — the very hospitals that treat the consequences of Black-No-More's treatment — but only if Black-No-More pays for that protection.

The arrangement is consummated with one instruction: "We cannot afford to let it be known that we are getting such a large sum from any one person or corporation" (010-030). Gay then "departed to carry the happy news to the National Executive Committee" (010-032). The party machine has been oiled; race has been reduced to a campaign finance line item.

## The Dwindling Campaign

As the novel nears its climax, the political calculus collapses. The revelation that the whitening process can produce visibly Black children devastates Republican prospects, and "Gorman Gay was at his wits' end. Nothing, he thought, could save them except a miracle" (010-065). The miracle arrives in the form of the ancestry investigation that will consume the Democratic ticket — but Gay's desperation reveals how completely the party's fortunes depend on forces (racial anxiety, scientific fraud) that no party chairman can control.
