# Egyptienne Stain

**Blandine's Egyptienne Stain** is a skin-darkening concoction
invented by [[sari-blandine]] (formerly Mme. Sisseretta Blandish of
Harlem) in the epilogue of *Black No More*. It emerges as the
commercial and cultural counterpoint to the [[black-no-more-treatment]],
completing the novel's satirical arc on the absurdity of racial
pigmentation as a social marker.

## Origin and Development

After Surgeon-General [[junius-crookman]] published his monograph
revealing that former Negroes made white by the Black-No-More process
were "from two to three shades lighter than the old Caucasians"
(013-122), American society underwent a rapid reversal in
its valuation of skin color. Pale skin — once the supreme marker of
social status — became suspect as possible evidence of Negro ancestry.

> The old Caucasians had never been really white but rather were a
> pale pink shading down to a sand color and a red.
> (013-122)

Sari Blandine, working on a steam table in a Broadway Automat,
recognized the commercial opportunity. She spent one week studying
skin stains at the Public Library and, within three to four weeks,
developed a formula that imparted "a long-wearing light-brown tinge to
the pigment" (013-128). The stain proved its efficacy when
her daughter received a marriage proposal from a young millionaire
within a month of application (013-129).

## Commercial Success

Free samples distributed to neighborhood women generated rapid
word-of-mouth demand. Blandine opened a shop in her front room, which
"soon had it crowded from morning till night" (013-129).
The formula was patented and, by the time President-Elect Hornbill was
inaugurated, "her Egyptienne Stain Shoppes dotted the country and she
had won three suits for infringement of patent" (013-130).

## Social Impact

The stain completed a full inversion of American racial aesthetics:

- A pale face became "startlingly rare" (013-130)
- "A girl without one was avoided by the young men; a young man
   without one was at a decided disadvantage, economically and
   socially" (013-130)
- "America was definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded"
   (013-130)

Within two years, fifteen companies manufactured competing stains and
artificial tans (013-131). The **Zulu Tan** became the
vogue among the smart set (013-131). Resort keepers in
Florida and California began hiring "naturally black bathing girls
from Africa" to attract the fashionable crowd (013-131).

## Satirical Significance

The Egyptienne Stain functions as Schuyler's ultimate satirical
inversion: after three centuries of whites defining blacks as inferior
by skin color, the discovery that "new Caucasians" were *whiter* than
"old Caucasians" triggers a mass cultural flight toward darkness. The
novel ends with all the major characters — including [[matthew-fisher]],
[[bunny-brown]], [[hank-johnson]], and the former [[henry-givens]] —
"quite as dusky as little Matthew Crookman Fisher" (013-132),
demonstrating that race is not biology but a market-driven social
performance.

### The Final Irony

The stain completes the novel's satirical arc: a treatment designed to make Black people white eventually makes white people want to be darker. The racial hierarchy, which seemed so immutable, is revealed as pure fashion — subject to the same whims and insecurities as any other consumer preference.

> Mrs. Blandine, however, was not downhearted. She had the information and in three or four weeks time she had a skin stain that would impart a long-wearing, smooth, even, dusky-brown complexion. (013-128)

> Free applications were given to all of the young women of the neighborhood. Mrs. Blandine's stain became most popular and her fame grew in her locality. (013-129)

> Those of the upper class began to look around for ways to get darker. It became the fashion for them to spend hours at the seashore basking naked in the sun. (013-126)

The Egyptienne Stain is thus the novel's last and most economical joke: the "Negro problem" was never about biology — it was always about who gets to decide what skin color is valuable, and that decision can be reversed overnight by an enterprising cosmetologist from Harlem.
