## Overview

The Anglo-Saxon Association of America is the legislative and political wing of the organized [[white-supremacy]] movement in [[george-schuyler]]'s *Black No More*. While the [[knights-of-nordica]] provide mass membership, dues collection, and paramilitary spectacle, the Anglo-Saxon Association provides legal respectability: its leader, [[arthur-snobbcraft]], is an F.F.V. (First Families of Virginia) aristocrat who drafts racial purity legislation, commissions pseudo-scientific research, and maneuvers through the corridors of power in Washington.

Matthew Fisher first proposes the Anglo-Saxon Association as a calculated business opportunity — "a graft" (009-040) — and the organization quickly becomes, alongside the Knights, one of "the two most powerful organizations in the country" (010-039). Its trajectory from a Richmond annual meeting to a national political force mirrors the novel's thesis that American racism is, at every level, a lucrative confidence game.

## Background

### Founding and Premise

The Anglo-Saxon Association enters the narrative through Matthew Fisher's entrepreneurial imagination. When he first mentions it to the president of the [[republican-party-harlem|Harlem Republican Party machine]], the man responds with blunt skepticism: "isn't a graft, you crook" (009-042). The reply — that it is indeed a graft, and a highly profitable one — sets the organization's moral coordinates.

Unlike the Knights of Nordica, which operates in storefront halls with oil-cloth signs (004-020), the Anglo-Saxon Association positions itself as the respectable face of the movement. Its president is not a revival preacher but a Virginia aristocrat who can credibly invoke lineage and tradition. Its work is not cross-burning but legislation, lobbying, and the commissioning of "scientific" racial research.

### The Richmond Annual Meeting

The Association's annual meeting in Richmond provides the novel's most detailed portrait of its internal operations. "The directors of the Anglo-Saxon Association gathered in their annual meeting" (009-057) at a hotel on Monument Avenue, where Matthew Fisher — appearing as Dr. Buggerie's assistant — presents "the results of some of his preliminary investigations" (009-064) on racial ancestry. The Richmond setting is precise: Virginia was the real-world epicenter of the racial integrity movement, having passed the Racial Integrity Act in 1924, and Richmond's Monument Avenue was the symbolic heart of the Lost Cause.

## Role in the Narrative

### Partnership with the Knights of Nordica

The Association's primary narrative function is as the Knights of Nordica's partner in a two-pronged strategy. The Knights mobilize mass white anxiety through rallies, radio broadcasts, and dues-paying membership drives; the Association translates that mobilized energy into political power. Simeon Dump, representing the Association at a Knights gathering, "swore he would not withdraw the Anglo-Saxon Association's support" (010-037) for the movement, and confirms that "the Anglo-Saxon Association is putting up half the money" (010-040) for the shared political operation.

The division of labor is clear: the Knights provide the numbers, the Association provides the money and the legal architecture. Together they dominate the Democratic National Convention, install Givens as a presidential nominee, and attempt to legislate racial purity into law.

### The Buggerie Research Scandal

A crucial plot point involves the Association's commissioning of "research work" from Dr. Buggerie, the same pseudo-scientific genealogist who later examines Matthew Fisher's ancestry. The Association employs Buggerie "under that Dr. Buggerie" (010-071) to produce data supporting the case that most prominent American families carry African ancestry — an investigation that eventually backfires catastrophically when Buggerie's findings are made public at the Democratic convention.

### Final Panic

When Buggerie's ancestry data is exposed at the convention, the Association's dignified facade collapses instantly. Its president "shouted … 'let's get away from here'" (013-009) as the delegates turn on one another. The organization that had promised to certify whiteness becomes the mechanism that destroys the pretense of whiteness — Schuyler's final satirical reversal.

## Satirical Function

### Respectability and Violence

The Anglo-Saxon Association is Schuyler's satire of how white supremacy launders itself through institutions. The Knights of Nordica are overtly thuggish — they burn crosses and terrorize Black workers. The Anglo-Saxon Association occupies the same ideological territory but presents it in legislative language and academic citation. The two organizations are, as the novel makes clear, "the two most powerful organizations in the country" (010-039) — not competitors but complementary divisions of the same enterprise.

### Virginia and the Cult of Lineage

The Association's Richmond headquarters and Snobbcraft's F.F.V. credentials target Virginia's self-image as the aristocratic cradle of white America. Schuyler, himself a Virginian (though not of the aristocracy), understood that Virginia's racial purity laws were advanced under the banner of heritage and science, not mob violence. The Anglo-Saxon Association perfects this formula: Snobbcraft "looked famous and well-dressed" while directing an organization whose work was functionally identical to the Knights'.

### The "Scientific" Pretext

The Association's employment of Dr. Buggerie to conduct "research work" (010-071) satirizes the real-world alliance between eugenicist science and legislative racism. The 1924 Virginia Racial Integrity Act — the direct real-world parallel to the Association's legislative program — was championed by the Anglo-Saxon Clubs of America and justified by eugenicist "science" of precisely the kind Buggerie is hired to produce. Schuyler's joke is that the science, when honestly applied, incriminates the very people who commissioned it.
