## Overview

White supremacy in *Black No More* is [[george-schuyler]]'s broadest satirical target: the ideology that assigns value, status, and humanity based on proximity to whiteness. The novel dismantles this ideology not through moral argument — Schuyler is too much the satirist for earnest persuasion — but through comic demonstration of its material basis.

When the [[black-no-more-treatment]] makes whiteness purchasable for fifty dollars, the ideology's true nature is exposed: white supremacy is not a belief system but a property system. Whiteness is a commodity, and the people who invest most heavily in its defense are, as the novel demonstrates, those who profit most from its enforcement. The [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]], founded by a man who privately "despised his followers" (004-041), are the novel's most complete embodiment of this thesis — white supremacy as a business model.

## Materialist Analysis

### Whiteness as Property

Schuyler's treatment of white supremacy is relentlessly materialist. The ideology is never presented as a sincere belief that characters hold because they have been persuaded of its truth. Instead, characters adopt or discard white supremacist rhetoric based on their economic interests.

[[rev-givens|Reverend Givens]], the Imperial Grand Wizard, "had no belief in the racial integrity nonsense nor any confidence in the racial purity nonsense" (004-041). He preaches white supremacy because preaching pays — fifty thousand dollars a year in membership dues (004-041). [[max-disher|Matthew Fisher]], a Black man passing as white, becomes the Knights' chief propagandist because it advances his career and protects his secret. The novel's white supremacists are uniformly either cynics or dupes, and cynics are the ones who get rich.

### The Economics of Fear

White supremacy in the novel operates on a simple economic logic: create fear, then sell the remedy. The [[black-no-more-treatment]] generates existential terror among white Americans who can no longer visually identify Black people. The Knights of Nordica step into this vacuum, offering "certainty" — the identification and suppression of the hidden Negro population — for five dollars in annual dues (004-075).

This cycle is self-reinforcing. More treated patients generate more white anxiety; more white anxiety generates more dues-paying Knights; more dues generate more political power for the Knights' leadership; more political power generates more demand for the treatment from Black people desperate to escape the backlash. White supremacy is not a problem that can be solved within this system; it is the system's operating logic.

## Narrative Moments

### The Inability to Identify Race

The novel's funniest and most politically devastating sequence involves the Knights' fundamental operational problem: they cannot tell who is "really" white. Matthew Fisher, their own Grand Exalted Giraw, is a former Negro — and the Knights never discover this because their methods of racial detection are pure fantasy.

> "C'ose yuh caint preach that white supremacy stuff ve'y effectively when the dark clouds are just as white as you are." — Bunny Brown to Matthew Fisher (008-019) (004-028)

Bunny's observation captures the comedy and the critique simultaneously. White supremacy requires a visible racial hierarchy; the treatment makes that hierarchy invisible; the organization dedicated to racial purity becomes an organization of racial guesswork, indistinguishable from a fraternal lodge with more dramatic rhetoric.

### The Genealogical Revelation

The novel's final satirical twist completes the demolition. [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]]'s genealogical research proves that all white Americans have Negro ancestry — and that former Negroes, post-treatment, are statistically whiter than the original Anglo-Saxon population (013-120). The revelation that "the United States of America was in fact a mulatto nation" (013-120) destroys the Knights politically and sends Givens fleeing to Mexico (012-056).

The punchline is devastating: the ideology that organized American life for three centuries was built on a fiction that genealogical science could have disproven at any time. White supremacy was never about truth — it was about power, and power that cannot identify its object is power that collapses.

## Historical Context

The Ku Klux Klan experienced a massive revival in the 1920s, reaching an estimated 4–5 million members by 1925. The Klan's ideology combined white supremacy with nativism, anti-Catholicism, and anti-Semitism. Schuyler's Knights of Nordica parody this revival with a crucial analytical twist: he emphasizes the economic incentives behind Klan membership — the dues, the salaries, the political careers — rather than treating the ideology as sincere belief.

This materialist analysis was radical for 1931 and remains the novel's sharpest critical insight. At a time when most anti-racist writing focused on moral condemnation of prejudice, Schuyler insisted on following the money. Who benefits from white supremacy? The answer the novel gives is precise: the people who sell it.

## Satirical Method

Schuyler's white supremacy satire works through three interlocking techniques:

| Technique | Example | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Economic reduction** | Givens founds the Knights for membership dues, not ideology | Exposes the profit motive beneath the moral rhetoric |
| **Reversal** | Matthew Fisher, a Black man, becomes the Knights' chief racial propagandist | Demonstrates that racial expertise is a performance, not a birthright |
| **Absurdist bureaucracy** | The Knights cannot identify who among their own members is "really" white | Reduces racial enforcement to comic impossibility |

The satire culminates not in a moral victory — racism is not defeated — but in an economic one. When Crookman's statistics prove that everyone is mixed-race, the market for white supremacy collapses not because people become morally enlightened but because the product becomes unsellable.
