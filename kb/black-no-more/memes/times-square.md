## Overview

Times Square appears in *Black No More* primarily as the site of the advertising campaign that propels Black-No-More, Incorporated to national prominence, and later as the headquarters of the Down-With-White-Prejudice-League in the epilogue. Schuyler, a Harlem-based journalist who knew New York intimately, uses Times Square to represent the commercial and media apparatus that transforms Crookman's medical procedure into a mass social movement. The Square's function in the novel is to literalize the connection between racial transformation and commodity culture: Black-No-More is advertised like any other consumer product, and the "offices established in the Times Square district" serve as the organizational center for the post-reversal racial politics of the epilogue.

## Narrative Significance

Times Square's appearances bracket the novel's action: early on, it is the media epicenter from which Black-No-More's fame radiates; in the epilogue, it houses Karl von Beerde's Down-With-White-Prejudice-League, a direct inversion of the N.S.E.L. that Beard once led. The geographic continuity — same location, same organizer, inverted racial valence — underscores the novel's thesis that racial politics is a transferable commodity.
