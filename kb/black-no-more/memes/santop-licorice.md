Santop Licorice is the founder and leader of the Back-to-Africa Society, one of the several racial organizations that [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]] satirizes as exploiting the very people they claim to serve. Described as having a "stentorian voice" [005-040], Licorice is a professional race man whose livelihood — like that of the National Social Equality League's leadership — depends on the continued existence of the racial grievances he purports to address. When the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]] threatens to eliminate his constituency, Licorice's response is not liberation but panic.

## The Back-to-Africa Platform

Licorice's organization promotes the return of African Americans to the African continent — a satirical reference to Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association and its Back-to-Africa movement. In the novel's economy of racial entrepreneurship, this platform is not a genuine political program but a fundraising mechanism. When Black-No-More begins depleting his membership, Licorice is "daily raising his stentorian voice in denunciation of the race for deserting his organization" [005-040]. The irony is sharp: a leader whose program is separation from America cannot tolerate his followers choosing their own form of separation — becoming white.

## The Defense Fund

Like every racial leader in the novel, Licorice maintains a perpetual crisis that requires financial support. The "Santop Licorice Defense Fund" [005-100] solicits five-dollar contributions, though the nature of the "defense" required is never specified. Schuyler's implication is clear: racial leadership is a business, and the "defense" is always against the disappearance of the leader's income. Licorice's "stentorian voice" — the tool of his trade — is deployed not against racism but against his own people's desertion of his organization.

## Rivalry with Dr. Beard

Licorice's relationship with [[shakespeare-agamemnon-beard|Dr. Shakespeare Agamemnon Beard]] of the National Social Equality League is one of competitive resentment. He is "always delighted when Dr. Beard met with any sort of rebuff or embarrassment" and is "doubly pleased" when he is overlooked in the formation of the Committee for the Preservation of Negro Racial Integrity [005-099]. The rivalry between these two leaders — one advocating social equality, the other African repatriation — is revealed as professional competition rather than ideological disagreement. Both depend on the same racial structure; both are threatened by the same technological disruption.

> Santop Licorice, founder and leader of the Back-to-Africa Society, read the reply of the Attorney General to the Negro leaders with much malicious satisfaction. ... He was always delighted when Dr. Beard met with any sort of rebuff or embarrassment. [005-099]

## Post-Treatment Trajectory

Licorice is one of the few racial leaders who survives the transition. He appears in the novel's later chapters alongside [[joseph-bonds|Joseph Bonds]], having shifted from Back-to-Africa advocacy to Republican Party campaigning. The transformation is revealing: when the racial basis of his original platform disappears, Licorice simply finds another institutional vehicle. The character thus illustrates Schuyler's thesis that racial leadership is a portable skill — not a commitment to a people but a technique for exploiting whatever constituency is available.

> Joseph Bonds and Santop Licorice, clean shaven and immaculate... [008-117]
