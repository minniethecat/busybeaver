# Simeon Dump

**Simeon Dump** is a director of the [[anglo-saxon-association]] in
*Black No More*. He appears in the political chapters surrounding the
national election, where he acts as a financier and power broker for
the white supremacist political alliance between the
[[knights-of-nordica]] and the Anglo-Saxon Association.

## Role in the Novel

### The Political Negotiations

Dump is introduced during the high-stakes negotiations between the two major white
supremacist organizations over the presidential ticket. The scene takes
place in a smoke-filled hotel room in the early hours of the morning,
with the various party factions locked in a bitter struggle over
nominations (010-037). Dump emerges as a determined advocate for his
faction's interests, insisting that [[arthur-snobbcraft]] remain on the
ticket:

> Matthew, wilted, worn but determined, fought for his chief. Simeon Dump of the Anglo-Saxon Association swore he would not withdraw the name of Arthur Snobbcraft. Rev. John Whiffle, a power in the party, gulped drink after drink, kept dabbing a damp handkerchief at the shining surface of his skull, and held out for one Bishop Belch. Moses Lejewski of New York argued obstinately for the nomination of Senator Bosh. (010-037)

The scene is characteristic of Schuyler's comic method: the grand
machinery of American presidential politics is reduced to a squalid
negotiation among men with absurd names, each fighting for personal or
factional advantage. Dump's stubborn loyalty to Snobbcraft is less a
matter of ideological commitment than of organizational interest — the
Anglo-Saxon Association had invested in Snobbcraft and expected a
return.

### The Financial Ultimatum

When [[matthew-fisher]] attempts to broker a compromise — Givens for
president, Snobbcraft for vice president, and cabinet positions for the
remaining contenders — the proposal "appealed to no one except Matthew"
(010-039). Dump's response is a blunt financial ultimatum:

> "You people forget," said Simeon Dump, "that the Anglo-Saxon
> Association is putting up half the money to finance this campaign."
> (010-040)

This line crystallizes the novel's central thesis about the relationship
between money and racial politics. Dump's power derives not from
ideological purity, popularity, or moral authority, but from the simple
fact that his organization controls the purse strings. White supremacy,
in Schuyler's satirical vision, is ultimately a business arrangement —
and the financier, not the ideologue, holds the real power.

## Political Context

Dump operates within the competitive landscape of white supremacist
organizational politics. The [[knights-of-nordica]] and the
Anglo-Saxon Association are described as "the two most powerful
organizations" of their kind (010-039), and Dump's role reflects the
internal negotiations and financial arrangements that sustain their
political machinery. The competition between the two groups mirrors
the broader factionalism that Schuyler depicts as endemic to racial
politics: organizations ostensibly united by shared ideology are in
reality divided by competing material interests.

His character exemplifies Schuyler's satirical depiction of
**white supremacy as a business enterprise** — maintained not by
ideological conviction alone but by financial interests, political
horse-trading, and organizational competition. The scene in which Dump
issues his financial ultimatum is set at three in the morning in a
smoke-filled room — a detail that underscores Schuyler's portrait of
political decision-making as fundamentally sordid and transactional
(010-037). The compromise that [[matthew-fisher]] proposed — giving
Givens the presidential nomination and Snobbcraft the vice presidency —
"appealed to no one except Matthew" (010-039), demonstrating that even
within the white supremacist coalition, organizational self-interest
trumped strategic rationality. Dump's insistence on Snobbcraft's
candidacy, backed by the Anglo-Saxon Association's financial leverage,
exemplifies the broader pattern in the novel of [[race-and-economics]]:
ideology follows money, not the reverse. The scene's setting — a
smoke-filled hotel room in the early hours of the morning — reinforces
the novel's insistence that the machinery of white supremacist politics
operates in the dark, far from public scrutiny, where men with names
like Dump and Snobbcraft divide the spoils of racial grievance among
themselves.

## Naming

The surname "Dump" is characteristic of Schuyler's satirical naming
conventions, joining other absurdist names in the novel such as
[[arthur-snobbcraft|Snobbcraft]], [[dr-buggerie|Buggerie]], and
[[rev-givens|Givens]]. The name suggests both the character's bulk and
the novel's implicit judgment of the political enterprise he represents:
a dump of money into a trash fire of racial grievance.
