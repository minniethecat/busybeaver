## Overview

*Black No More* is George S. Schuyler's 1931 satirical novel, subtitled "Being an Account of the Strange and Wonderful Workings of Science in the Land of the Free, A.D. 1933–1940." The novel imagines an invention — the Black-No-More treatment — that can transform Black people into white people, and traces the social, political, and economic consequences as millions of African Americans undergo the procedure. It is widely regarded as one of the earliest works of Afrofuturism and remains a uniquely caustic satire of American racial ideology.[PN:re-bnm-001]

## Plot Summary

Dr. Junius Crookman, a Harlem physician, develops a treatment — "three swift, painless and harmless treatments given on three successive days" (002-020) — that permanently removes melanin from skin, turning Black people indistinguishable from white people. The novel's protagonist, Max Disher, is among the first to undergo the procedure on New Year's Eve 1933, emerging as Matthew Fisher. He infiltrates the Knights of Nordica, a white supremacist organization, rises to become its chief strategist, and eventually marries Crookman's daughter Helen.[PN:re-bnm-001]

As millions of African Americans follow Max's path, the American racial order collapses. The Knights of Nordica, having lost their constituency, pivot to promoting racial purity within whiteness. The Democratic Party, deprived of the "Negro Problem" as a campaign issue, faces electoral chaos. Crookman becomes fabulously wealthy, and Fisher — now the organization's Grand Exalted Giraw — rides the wave of white anxiety to national prominence.[PN:re-bnm-001]

The crisis peaks when Crookman publishes a monograph revealing that babies born to treated parents are slightly darker than "real" white babies — an irreversible marker of the procedure. In the novel's final chapter, the newly elected President Snobbcraft and Vice President Fisher face the revelation that the entire white population has one or more "Black" ancestors, making everyone ineligible for the purity they have been fighting over.[PN:re-bnm-001]

## Satirical Targets

Schuyler's satire spares no one. The novel attacks white supremacists (the Knights of Nordica, thinly veiled Klan), Black race leaders (the National Social Equality League, mocking the NAACP), racial science (the chromatic hierarchy), the Democratic and Republican parties, the beauty industry, eugenics, and the very concept of racial identity. Schuyler's central argument — that race is a fiction maintained for economic and political convenience — is dramatized with a relentlessness unusual even in satire.[PN:re-bnm-001]
