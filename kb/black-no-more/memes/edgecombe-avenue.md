## Overview

Edgecombe Avenue, in Harlem's Sugar Hill district, was the address of the Black professional elite in the 1920s–1930s — the doctors, lawyers, real estate speculators, and "dicty" socialites who constituted Harlem's upper class. When [[chuck-foster|Chuck Foster]] purchases "the big private house on Edgecombe Avenue for fifteen thousand" (003-005) to expand [[black-no-more-inc|Black-No-More, Incorporated]], he literalizes the novel's central irony: Black elite achievement is being used to finance the elimination of the Black population that sustains that elite.

## In the Narrative

[[chuck-foster|Foster]]'s purchase of the Edgecombe Avenue property is presented as a straightforward business decision — "the workmen are getting it in shape now" (003-005) — but the address carries heavy symbolic weight. Edgecombe Avenue was where the people who had refused to fund Crookman's education lived; now their neighborhood is being repurposed to house the machine that will dissolve their social world.

## Significance

Edgecombe Avenue represents the trap of Black elite aspiration: the closer one gets to white standards of success, the more one's success depends on the continued existence of the Black masses one has left behind. The Black-No-More treatment collapses this distance, making whiteness available to everyone and rendering elite distinction meaningless.
