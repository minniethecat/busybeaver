# Jim Crow

## Overview

Jim Crow was the system of state and local laws, social customs, and economic practices that enforced racial segregation and subordination in the post-Reconstruction American South from the 1880s through the 1960s. Named for a nineteenth-century minstrel-show caricature, the regime rested on the legal fiction of "separate but equal" established by *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) and enforced through a combination of law, terror, and economic coercion. In *Black No More*, George Schuyler treats Jim Crow not as a tragedy to be lamented but as a confidence game to be exposed: a system whose entire functioning depends on the *visibility* of Blackness, and which collapses the moment that visual identification becomes unreliable.

## In the Narrative

Jim Crow is the omnipresent background condition of the novel's opening chapters. Max Disher's Harlem is a world shaped by segregation — separate neighborhoods, separate social institutions, separate economic opportunities — and his decision to undergo the Black-No-More treatment is presented as a rational response to Jim Crow's constraints (001-025). The novel's satirical genius is to show that Jim Crow is not primarily a system of racial *hatred* but of racial *identification*: it requires that Black people be recognizable as Black in order to function.

The treatment's consequences for Jim Crow infrastructure are explored most extensively in chapter 8, where Schuyler catalogs the economic dislocations that follow the disappearance of a segregated Black population: condemned railroad coaches that can no longer be used for "Jim Crow" cars, segregated waiting rooms that are too dingy for white patrons, dilapidated schools that must now meet white standards, and Black Belt streets without sewers that suddenly require municipal investment (008-004). The satire cuts both ways: it exposes the material degradation Jim Crow imposed on Black communities, while also mocking the white South's sudden concern for infrastructure standards once the occupants are white.

## Satirical Treatment

Schuyler's central satirical move is to treat Jim Crow as a *perceptual* rather than a moral problem. The novel never asks whether segregation is wrong; it simply demonstrates, through the machinery of plot, that segregation is *impractical* when race becomes invisible. This is a deeply cynical form of satire — one that refuses the conventions of the protest novel and instead deploys the logic of efficiency and market rationality against the Jim Crow order. The white South does not repent of its racism; it is simply forced, by economic and administrative necessity, to abandon the infrastructure of segregation when there are no longer any visibly Black people to segregate.

The novel's treatment of Jim Crow aesthetics — the visual cues of skin, hair, and dress through which racial identity was read — is explored in the companion page [[jim-crow-aesthetics]].

Jim Crow, in Schuyler's rendering, is not a cultural tradition or a regional custom but a profit-making enterprise — a system of racial classification that exists because it generates revenue for those who administer it. The novel's insistence on this economic reading was radical for its time and remains one of its most durable critical insights.
