Dr. Joseph Bonds is a minor but symptomatic figure in [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]]'s gallery of racial entrepreneurs. The former head of the Negro Data League — an organization whose name satirizes the progressive-era faith in social-scientific data as a solution to racial problems — Bonds exemplifies Schuyler's thesis that racial advocacy organizations are interchangeable with their leaders' career interests. After taking the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]] and becoming white, Bonds transitions seamlessly from Negro advocacy to Republican Party campaigning, his racial identity following his institutional affiliation rather than the reverse.

## The Negro Data League

Bonds first appears as a speaker at a gathering of Negro leaders, described by Schuyler with characteristic physical cruelty as "a little rat-faced Negro" [005-064]. The Negro Data League, which Bonds heads, is one of several organizations in the novel whose names parody actual race-advocacy institutions. The joke is in the word "Data": the League purports to solve racial problems through information, but its real function, like every other organization in the novel's racial economy, is to provide salaries for its leadership. When the Black-No-More treatment eliminates the League's reason for existence, Bonds does not defend the organization's principles — he simply finds new employment.

## The Post-Racial Career

By the novel's later chapters, Bonds has taken the treatment and is working as a Republican campaigner alongside [[walter-williams|Walter Williams]]. The two are "busily engaged in leading the other workers (who knew better) to believe that they were earning the ten dollars a day they were receiving" [010-066]. The detail is precise: Bonds was once paid to lead Negroes; now he is paid to pretend to work. The substance of his activities has not changed; only the racial identity from which he operates has shifted. Schuyler's point is that racial-advocacy skills — data collection, public speaking, constituency management — are transferable to any institutional context.

> Two flights below in a spacious office sat two of the Republican campaigners, Walter Williams and Joseph Bonds, busily engaged in leading the other workers (who knew better) to believe that they were earning the ten dollars a day they were receiving. [010-066]

## Appearances with Licorice

Bonds appears alongside [[santop-licorice|Santop Licorice]] in several scenes, the two former race men now "clean shaven and immaculate" [008-117] in their post-treatment white identities. The pairing is significant: Licorice advocated African repatriation, Bonds advocated data-driven racial advocacy, and both have pivoted to the same political consulting work. Schuyler suggests that the ideological differences between Black leaders were always superficial; what they shared was a class interest in the perpetuation of the racial structure that employed them.
