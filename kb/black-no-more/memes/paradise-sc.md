## Overview

Paradise, South Carolina is [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s satirical name for a company-owned mill town that is anything but paradisiacal. Introduced in Chapter 7, Paradise is where the novel's political economy becomes most explicit: "The owners of the Paradise Mill… own all of the houses in Paradise and operate all of the stores" (007-003). When whitened Negroes arrive looking for work and white workers resist, [[black-no-more-inc|the company]] exploits racial tension to break union organizing — a demonstration of how capital uses race to divide labor.

## In the Narrative

Chapter 7 shifts the novel's action from Harlem and Atlanta to Paradise, where the Black-No-More treatment's economic consequences are most visible. White mill workers, already exploited by company owners, find themselves competing with newly white ex-Negroes for jobs. The company's "secret operatives" (007-001) monitor the situation, manipulating racial anxiety to prevent labor solidarity. The episode demonstrates [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s thesis that racial antagonism is not natural but manufactured — a tool of economic control.

## Significance

Paradise is the novel's most direct engagement with class politics. By setting the labor conflict in a company town where "the owners… own all of the houses… and operate all of the stores" (007-003), [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] makes visible the economic structure that racial ideology obscures. Paradise's name is ironic in the extreme: the town is a paradise only for its owners, and race is the mechanism that keeps it that way.
