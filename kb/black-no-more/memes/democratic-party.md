The Democratic Party in [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]] functions as the political vehicle through which the novel's white supremacist coalition — the [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]] and the [[anglo-saxon|Anglo-Saxon]] Association — seeks national power. Schuyler's treatment of the party is characteristically cynical: both major parties are interchangeable instruments of power, distinguished only by which racial bloc they can currently mobilize. The Democrats' absorption of the white supremacist movement is not an ideological conversion but a marriage of convenience, and its platform is revealed to be as empty as the racial ideology that fuels it.

## The Political Calculus

The Democrats' opportunity arises from the chaos created by the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]]. With the South's Black population vanishing, "the political oligarchy that ruled the section was losing its old assurance and complacency" [009-034]. The Republicans had made inroads in the 1934 Congressional elections, and "if drastic steps were not immediately taken, the Republicans might carry the erstwhile Solid South, thus practically destroying the Democratic Party" [009-034]. The party's existential crisis is not about principle but arithmetic: without the Solid South's electoral votes, the Democrats cannot win nationally.

> The political oligarchy that ruled the section was losing its old assurance and complacency. The Republicans had made inroads here and there in the 1934 Congressional elections. [009-034]

## The White Supremacist Alliance

The Democratic Party becomes the umbrella under which the Knights of Nordica and the Anglo-Saxon Association unite. Their combined resources — the Knights' mass membership and the Anglo-Saxons' money — create "inexplicable affluence" for the party [010-034]. [[rev-givens|Rev. Givens]] becomes the party's presidential nominee, and the campaign's momentum builds on the same racial anxieties that the Knights have cultivated for years: "People were not exactly for the Democrats but they were against the Republicans" [010-034]. Schuyler's point is precise: racial politics does not require positive support, only negative mobilization.

> The frequent radio addresses of Rev. Givens, the growing numbers of the Knights of Nordica, the inexplicable affluence of the Democratic Party and the vitriolic articles in The Warning, had not failed to rouse much Democratic sentiment. [010-034]

## The Empty Platform

The novel's most devastating political joke comes when Givens announces his platform. Asked about his tariff policy, he tells reporters: "It is my intention, if elected, to carry out the traditional tariff policy of the Democratic Party" — and Schuyler adds the parenthetical aside "neither he or anyone else knew what that was" [010-045]. The party's substantive positions are literally unknown, even to its own candidate. What matters is not policy but the apparatus of power: the racial mobilization, the campaign funds, the radio addresses, the vitriolic articles. The Democratic Party, like every other institution in the novel, is revealed as a shell within which interests compete for advantage.

> "It is my intention, if elected, to carry out the traditional tariff policy of the Democratic Party" (neither he or anyone else knew what that was). [010-045]
