# New York Anthropological Society

## Overview

The New York Anthropological Society is a fictional organization in *Black No More* created by [[matthew-fisher|Matthew Fisher]] (formerly Max Disher) as a front for gaining entry to the [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]]. The Society exemplifies one of Schuyler's most persistent satirical targets: the performative deployment of scientific and academic credentials in the service of racial politics. Its very name — anthropological — invokes the era's entanglement of anthropology with scientific racism, while its actual function is pure political theater.

### The Credibility of Nomenclature

The Society's effectiveness as a fraud depends entirely on the power of its name. Schuyler understood that institutional authority in early twentieth-century America was largely a matter of nomenclature: the right combination of words — "New York," "Anthropological," "Society" — could open doors that would otherwise remain closed (004-028). The Society had no scholarly output, no membership beyond its founder, no physical address; it was a pure fiction. And yet it worked flawlessly, because no one in the novel's white supremacist hierarchy possessed either the inclination or the expertise to question academic credentials (004-034).

## In the Narrative

### The Approach

The Society appears in chapter 4, when Matthew Fisher, now white and seeking to infiltrate the white-supremacist power structure, approaches [[rev-givens|Reverend Henry Givens]] for an introduction to the Knights of Nordica. Fisher presents himself as a representative of the "New York Anthropological Society" who is "very anxious to have about a half-hour's conversation" with the Knights' leadership "relative to his new venture" (004-028). Matthew "spoke in an impressive, businesslike manner, rocked back on his heels and looked profound" (004-028), performing the role of the serious academic with theatrical precision.

### Givens's Reception

Givens's response is telling: after the receptionist delivers Fisher's message, Givens muses, "New York Anthropological Society, eh? This feller must know somethin'. Might be able to use him in this business" (004-034). The line captures the deference to scientific authority that Schuyler satirizes throughout the novel — the assumption that institutional affiliation, particularly one that sounds academic and white, confers legitimacy. Fisher then delivered his pitch, "now glorying in his newly-discovered eloquence," and Givens concluded that "this pale, dapper young fellow, with his ready tongue, his sincerity, his scientific training and knowledge of the situation ought to prove a valuable asset to the Knights of Nordica" (004-038).

That evening, Givens told his wife:

> "Oh, Honey," he gurgled, "I think this here Knights of Nordica is going over big; going over big! My fame is spreading. Only today I had a long talk with a famous anthropologist from New York and he's going to address our mass meeting tonight." (004-050)

Within hours, the fictional "New York Anthropological Society" had been elevated to the status of a credentialing institution for the Knights' propaganda — a transformation that perfectly encapsulates Schuyler's critique of how easily scientific authority could be manufactured and deployed.

## Satirical Significance

The New York Anthropological Society belongs to a class of satirical devices in the novel that expose the fragility of institutional authority. Like [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]]'s scientific credentials, which grant legitimacy to a procedure that destroys the racial order, the Society demonstrates that expertise in Schuyler's world is largely a matter of nomenclature. The right words — "New York," "Anthropological," "Society" — open doors that would otherwise remain closed.

The Society also prefigures the novel's broader treatment of anthropology and race science. Anthropology in the early twentieth century was deeply implicated in the project of racial classification, and Schuyler — a journalist who followed contemporary scientific debates — was aware of the discipline's complicity in constructing the racial categories his novel dismantles. The Society's complete lack of scholarly substance serves as Schuyler's implicit commentary on the intellectual vacuity of the racial science establishment itself.
