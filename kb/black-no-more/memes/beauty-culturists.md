## Overview

Beauty culturists — the Black women and men who operated beauty parlors, sold cosmetic products door-to-door, and built small businesses around the racial beauty market — are among the novel's most sympathetic economic victims. The [[preface|Preface]] notes that "temporarily effective preparations… have so far proved exceedingly profitable to manufacturers, advertising agencies, [[negro-weeklies|Negro newspapers]] and beauty culturists" (P04-002). When the treatment renders their products obsolete, these small entrepreneurs lose their livelihoods overnight.

## Role in the Narrative

The beauty culturists appear in the novel's economic background — part of the ecosystem that the treatment destroys. Unlike the corporate executives and politicians who can adapt to the new racial order, the small-scale beauty entrepreneurs have no fallback. Their disappearance is part of the novel's ambivalent treatment of racial economics: the same industry that exploited Black self-hatred also provided Black livelihoods.

## Satirical Function

Highlights the human cost of the treatment's economic disruption — the small entrepreneurs caught between the exploitative logic of the beauty industry and the destructive logic of the treatment that renders it obsolete.
