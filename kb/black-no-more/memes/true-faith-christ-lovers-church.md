## Overview

The True Faith Christ Lovers' Church is a fundamentalist Christian sect headquartered in [[happy-hill|Happy Hill, Mississippi]], which "made the prodigious boast of being the most truly Fundamentalist of all the Christian sects in the United States." Led by Rev. [[alex-mcphule|Alex McPhule]], the church serves as the novel's culminating satire of the intersection between Southern evangelical religion and racial violence — its adherents nearly lynch [[christopher-jackson-buggerie|Buggerie]] and [[arthur-snobbcraft|Snobbcraft]] on Election Day before McPhule restrains them for ritual purposes.

## Background

The True Faith Christ Lovers' Church was founded in Happy Hill, Mississippi, a community distinguished by "its inordinately high illiteracy rate and its lynching record." The church's defining characteristic is its claim to absolute fundamentalist purity — it is "the most truly Fundamentalist" denomination, a boast that Schuyler deploys to satirize the competitive factionalism of American Protestant sectarianism. The church's name itself is ironic: "Christ Lovers" who are eager to lynch.

## Role in the Narrative

The church appears in the novel's final chapter during the Election Day open-air revival. The faithful have gathered from miles around for what they hope will be an all-night service. When [[christopher-jackson-buggerie|Dr. Samuel Buggerie]] and [[arthur-snobbcraft|Arthur Snobbcraft]], fleeing the revelation of their partial African ancestry, crash their car near the revival, the congregation seizes them.

The crowd searches the two men, finds identification papers confirming their Black ancestry (according to the [[one-drop-rule]]), and prepares to lynch them. It is only "the calmness and presence of mind of the Rev. McPhule" that restrains the mob — not out of mercy, but because McPhule wants to incorporate the lynching into the scheduled religious ceremony, making it a ritual offering to attract divine attention. This conflation of religious observance with racial murder is the novel's most savage indictment of Southern Christianity.

## Satirical Function

The True Faith Christ Lovers' Church satirizes several interrelated targets: the fundamentalist-modernist controversy of the 1920s–1930s; the competitive claims to purity among Protestant denominations; the complicity of Southern white churches in lynching culture; and the theatrical, commercialized nature of revival meetings. McPhule's calculation — that a lynching would make an excellent draw for his revival — exposes the economic logic beneath the religious performance.

The church's location in Happy Hill is significant: the community has been "untouched by the troubles through which the rest of the country had been passing," meaning it has experienced none of the racial upheaval caused by Black-No-More. Its residents' eagerness for a lynching is thus not reactive but purely traditional — a ritual of community bonding.

## Key Passages

> Happy Hill, Mississippi, was all aflutter. For some days it had been preparing for the great, open-air revival of the True Faith Christ Lovers' Church. The faithful for miles around were expected to attend the services scheduled for the afternoon of Election Day and which all hoped would last well into the night. (PN 013-055)

> They were more proud, however, of the fact that Happy Hill was the home and birthplace of the True Faith Christ Lovers' Church, which made the prodigious boast of being the most truly Fundamentalist of all the Christian sects in the United States. Other things of which the community might have boasted were its inordinately high illiteracy rate and its lynching record. (PN 013-055)

> But for the calmness and presence of mind of the Rev. McPhule, the True Faith Christ Lovers would have torn the unfortunate men limb from limb. The evangelist restrained the more hotheaded individuals and insisted that the ceremonies proceed according to time-honored custom. (PN 013-114)
