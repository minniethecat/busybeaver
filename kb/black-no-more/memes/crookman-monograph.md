## Overview

Crookman's Monograph is the culminating event of *Black No More*'s epilogue, triggering a complete social inversion of American racial values. Dr. Junius Crookman, now Surgeon-General of the United States, published a scientific monograph demonstrating that the "new Caucasians" produced by his Black-No-More process were "from two to three shades lighter than the old Caucasians" (013-120). The finding — that the formerly Black were now literally whiter than whites — shattered the ideological foundation of a society built on the veneration of whiteness and set off a cascade of cultural reversals.

## Context

By the novel's final chapter, the Black-No-More process had been operating for several years, transforming millions of Black Americans into white citizens. Crookman himself had ascended from a controversial Harlem doctor to Surgeon-General of the United States. The social order that the treatment disrupted — with its elaborate apparatus of racial classification, segregation, and white supremacist politics — had already been destabilized by Buggerie's ancestry revelations. Crookman's monograph delivered the final blow.

The monograph was a scientific document, published "in the last days of the Goosie administration" (013-120). It examined "the differences in skin pigmentation of the real whites and those he had made white by the Black-No-More process" (013-120). The empirical finding was unambiguous: the artificial whites were whiter than the natural ones.

## Narrative Account

Crookman declared that "in practically every instance the new Caucasians were from two to three shades lighter than the old Caucasians, and that approximately one-sixth of the population were in the first group" (013-120). He further noted that "the old Caucasians had never been really white but rather were a pale pink shading down to a sand color and a red" (013-120). Even vitiligo, he pointed out, made old Caucasian skin lighter — so extreme paleness was, ironically, evidence of a skin condition, not racial purity.

The public reaction was immediate and transformative. "To a society that had been taught to venerate whiteness for over three hundred years, this announcement was rather staggering," the narrator observes. "What was the world coming to, if the blacks were whiter than the whites?" (013-120).

## Aftermath

The monograph's publication triggered a wholesale cultural reversal:

### Social Stigma: "Many people in the upper class began to look askance at their very pale complexions. If it were true that extreme whiteness was evidence of the possession of Negro blood, of having once been a member of a pariah class, then surely it were well not to be so white!" (013-120).

### Scientific Racism Inverted: Professor Handen Moutthe, an anthropologist, published findings that the palest citizens were "mentally inferior" and should be segregated in schools. Four state legislatures began considering "separate schools for pale children" (013-120).

### The Tanning Craze: The upper class adopted sunbathing as a fashion — "it became the fashion for them to spend hours at the seashore basking naked in the sunshine and then to dash back, heavily bronzed, to their homes, and, preening themselves in their dusky skins, lord it over their paler, and thus less fortunate, associates" (013-120).

### Commercialization: Beauty shops sold skin-darkening powders with names like *Poudre Nègre* and *Poudre le Egyptienne*. Mrs. Sari Blandine (formerly Mme. Sisseretta Blandish of Harlem) invented a commercially successful skin stain and built a national chain of "Egyptienne Stain Shoppes" (013-120).

### The Ultimate Verdict: "America was definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded" (013-120). A white face became "startlingly rare." The novel's closing image — Crookman observing his former associates, all "quite as dusky as little Matthew Crookman Fisher" on a beach at Cannes — completes the ironic reversal. The Black-No-More treatment, intended to eliminate the color line, instead eliminated whiteness itself as a valued category.

> "In practically every instance the new Caucasians were from two to three shades lighter than the old Caucasians, and approximately one-sixth of the population were in the first group." (013-120)

The monograph's significance lies not in its scientific accuracy — Schuyler treats Crookman's research with characteristic deadpan — but in its function as the final satirical mechanism. Crookman, the "Race Man" who set out to solve the Negro problem by eliminating Negroes, ends his career by publishing data that makes whiteness itself a liability. The novel's closing tableau — the Surgeon-General watching his dusky-skinned associates on a Cannes beach — is the final inversion: the man who made Black people white has, indirectly, made white people want to be Black. The racial hierarchy, Schuyler suggests, was never about biology; it was always about who controls the definition of desirability, and that control can shift with a single scientific publication.
