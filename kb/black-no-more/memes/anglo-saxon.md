"Anglo-Saxon" in [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]] is not merely an ethnic descriptor but a racial ideology — the claim that persons of English-descended "Nordic" stock constitute the supreme form of whiteness, entitled to social, political, and economic dominance. Schuyler satirizes this ideology through the Anglo-Saxon Association of America, a Virginia-based organization of "rich highbrows who can trace their ancestry back almost two hundred years" [009-042], and its intersection with the mass-movement [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]]. The collision between Anglo-Saxon genealogical elitism and the Knights' populist racism produces the novel's most sustained satire of white identity politics.

## Genealogical Supremacy as Class Performance

The Anglo-Saxon Association, headquartered in Richmond, represents the aristocratic wing of American white supremacy. Unlike the Knights of Nordica, whose appeal is populist and emotional, the Association's members "believe in white supremacy the same as our outfit but they claim that the Anglo-Saxons are the cream of the white race and should maintain the leadership in American social, economic and political life" [009-042]. This is racism with a pedigree — literally. The Association's pretensions are undercut by Schuyler's description of its president, Arthur Snobbcraft, as "a man suspiciously swarthy for an Anglo-Saxon" [009-053].

> "You see they believe in white supremacy the same as our outfit but they claim that the Anglo-Saxons are the cream of the white race and should maintain the leadership in American social, economic and political life." [009-042]

## Arthur Snobbcraft and the Professional Anglo-Saxon

[[arthur-snobbcraft|Mr. Arthur Snobbcraft]], President of the Anglo-Saxon Association, is an F.F.V. (First Family of Virginia) who "had devoted his entire life to fighting for two things: white racial integrity and Anglo-Saxon supremacy" [009-053]. It had been "very largely a losing fight" — the farther he got from his goal, the more desperate he became. Snobbcraft was "the genius that thought up the numerous racial integrity laws adopted in Virginia," was "strong for sterilization of the unfit: meaning Negroes, aliens, Jews and other riff raff," and "had an abiding hatred of democracy" [009-053]. The detail that Snobbcraft is himself "suspiciously swarthy" transforms this catalogue of racism into farce: the professional Anglo-Saxon cannot even meet his own phenotypic standard.

> Mr. Arthur Snobbcraft, President of the Anglo-Saxon Association, an F.F.V. and a man suspiciously swarthy for an Anglo-Saxon, had devoted his entire life to fighting for two things: white racial integrity and Anglo-Saxon supremacy. [009-053]

## The Ancestral Purity Project

Dr. Buggerie, the Association's statistician and "a professional Anglo-Saxon as well as a descendant of one of the First Families of Virginia," takes the ideology to its logical conclusion: a nationwide genealogical investigation to "disclose the various non-Nordic strains in the population" [009-063]. His preliminary research suggests "as many as twenty million people in the United States who possessed some slight non-Nordic strain and were thus unfit for both citizenship and procreation" [009-064]. The project is Schuyler's reductio ad absurdum of racial science: if you look hard enough at anyone's ancestry, you will find impurity — and the category "Anglo-Saxon" dissolves under its own scrutiny.

## Political Pragmatism Trumps Purity

The Association's ultimate fate illustrates the novel's thesis about the relationship between racial ideology and political power. When the opportunity arises to join forces with the Knights of Nordica for the [[democratic-party|Democratic]] presidential campaign, the Anglo-Saxon aristocrats swallow their pride: "By swallowing their pride for a season and joining with the riff raff of the Knights of Nordica, they saw an opportunity, for the first time in years to get into power; and they took it" [009-057]. Racial purity ideology is, in the end, subordinate to the will to power — the genealogical elitists will ally with the very "riff raff" they despise if it serves their political interests.

## The Economic Foundation

Schuyler never lets the reader forget the material basis of ideological commitments. Southern Chambers of Commerce, facing the economic disruption of Black-No-More, could still "point to their large reserves of docile, contented, Anglo-Saxon labor" [009-035] as a selling point for Northern investment — but "who knew how long that condition would last?" Anglo-Saxon identity is, at bottom, a labor-market brand: it signals a workforce disciplined by racial hierarchy rather than union organization.
