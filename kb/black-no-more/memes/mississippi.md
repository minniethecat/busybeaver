## Overview

Mississippi appears in *Black No More* not as a developed setting but as a political synecdoche — the state whose name signifies the deepest investments in white supremacy. Senator Bosh of Mississippi uses the *Congressional Record* to amplify racial panic about the new Caucasians, and the state epitomizes the "states we can't carry anyhow" that Gorman Gay dismisses, along with the militant Southern sentiment that Colonel Roberts threatens to ally with. Schuyler, a Southerner who understood the region's racial dynamics intimately, deploys Mississippi as shorthand for the unreconstructed white South that the novel's satire both targets and, in its own way, depends upon.

## Narrative Significance

Mississippi functions as the geographic pole of reaction against which the novel's progressive chaos — treatment, passing, racial reversal — unfolds. Senator Bosh's insertion of anti-Black-No-More rhetoric into the Congressional Record, "his remarks interspersed with 'Applauses'" (013-120), represents the performative dimension of Southern white supremacy, sustained less by genuine conviction than by political theater. When the ancestry revelations reveal that even Mississippi's senators are genealogically compromised, the state becomes one more site of the novel's leveling irony.
