# Times Square District

The **Times Square District** of Manhattan serves as the commercial
nerve center of the racial transformation industry in
*Black No More*. It is where [[black-no-more-inc]] establishes its
headquarters, where the [[national-social-equality-league]] maintains
its national office, and where multiple race-related enterprises,
organizations, and media operations are based (005-045).

## In the Novel

### Black-No-More Headquarters

After the success of the [[black-no-more-treatment]], Black-No-More,
Incorporated opens a prominent headquarters in the Times Square
district:

> Located in the Times Square district of Manhattan, it had...
> (005-045)

The location in New York's premier commercial district signals the
treatment's transition from a medical curiosity to a major corporate
enterprise (005-045). The choice of Times Square — the symbolic center of
American advertising, entertainment, and commerce — underscores
Schuyler's satirical framing of racial transformation as a consumer
product. By the time of the novel's epilogue, the whitening industry had
become so profitable that "Long articles appeared in the Sunday
newspapers extolling the simplicity, the freedom from danger, and the
marvelous results of the Black-No-More treatment" (010-054).

### The National Social Equality League

The National Social Equality League also maintained its national office
in the Times Square district, creating a spatial irony:

> The national office of the militant Negro organization, the National Social Equality League, was agog. Telephone bells were ringing, mulatto clerks were hustling excitedly back and forth, messenger boys rushed in and out. Located in the Times Square district of Manhattan, it had for forty years carried on the fight for full social equality for the Negro citizens and the immediate abolition of lynching as a national sport. (005-045)

The League's Times Square location placed it at the heart of the same
commercial district as the very enterprise that was destroying its
reason for existence (005-045). As Black-No-More's treatment reduced the
number of identifiable Negroes, the League's funding collapsed: "Ever
since the first sanitarium of Black-No-More, Incorporated, started
turning Negroes into Caucasians, the National Social Equality League's
income had been decreasing" (005-046). No dues had been collected in
months and subscriptions to the League's mouthpiece, *The Dilemma*, had
dwindled to almost nothing (005-046). Officials who had long been
"ensconced in palatial apartments" began to panic as pay days grew
farther apart (005-046).

The physical proximity of the League to Black-No-More headquarters
within Times Square created a spatial irony — two organizations with
opposed missions, sharing the same commercial address, one thriving
while the other withered.

### Down-With-White-Prejudice-League

In the novel's epilogue, the [[down-with-white-prejudice-league]],
founded by Karl von Beerde (an alias of [[doctor-beard]]), establishes
its offices in the same district:

> Offices were established in the Times Square district of New York
> and the mails were soon laden with releases...
> (013-120)

The recurrence of Times Square as the headquarters of choice for
race-based organizations — regardless of their political orientation —
reinforces Schuyler's thesis that racial politics is ultimately a form
of commercial enterprise (013-120).

### Max Disher's First Glimpse

Even before his transformation, [[max-disher]]'s narrative relationship
with Times Square is established during his pre-treatment wandering:

> He had strolled through the Times Square district before but never
> with such a feeling of absolute freedom and sureness. No one now looked
> at him curiously because he was with a white girl, as they had when he
> came down there with Minnie, his former octoroon lady friend. Gee, it
> was great! (002-030)

This early passage frames Times Square as a space of white commercial
power and spectacle — a world Max can only observe from the outside
until he becomes [[matthew-fisher]] (002-030). His post-treatment
experience of Times Square with a white woman marks his psychological
entry into white privilege, demonstrating that the district's
significance is not merely geographic but fundamentally racial.

## Symbolic Function

The Times Square district functions in the novel as a metonym for the
**commercialization of racial identity**. The clustering of
race-related businesses — from Black-No-More, Inc. to the National
Social Equality League to the Down-With-White-Prejudice-League — in
America's most famous commercial district reinforces Schuyler's thesis
that race is, ultimately, a market phenomenon subject to the same forces
of supply, demand, and advertising as any other commodity. The district
became the geographical expression of what [[social-equality]] advocates
feared most: the reduction of racial justice to a line item on a balance
sheet.

## Historical Context

Times Square in the 1930s was the epicenter of American commercial
culture — home to Broadway theaters, national advertising agencies,
radio networks, and corporate headquarters. By placing the race
industry's operations in this district, Schuyler positioned racial
transformation not as a marginal or underground activity but as an
integral part of mainstream American commerce. The district's
association with spectacle and entertainment also resonates with the
novel's depiction of racial identity as performance.

## Narrative Trajectory

The novel traces a progression through Times Square that mirrors Max
Disher's own transformation. Before treatment, Max was an outsider,
wandering the district "with Minnie, his former octoroon lady friend"
and drawing curious stares (002-030). After becoming Matthew Fisher, he
returned to the district as an insider — an executive whose
organization's headquarters defined the neighborhood's commercial
identity (005-045). By the epilogue, the district had become home to a
succession of race-based organizations, each packaging racial identity
as a product to be sold, a rhythm that showed no sign of ending
(013-120).
