## Overview

Social mortality — the death of Black social institutions when their constituency vanishes — is the novel's elegiac counterpoint to its comedic dismantling of race. As the Black-No-More treatment spreads, Schuyler depicts not liberation but dissolution: churches empty, newspapers fold, fraternal orders collapse, and the vibrant cultural ecosystem of Black Harlem disintegrates. The novel's implicit question is whether Black social institutions exist *because of* or *in spite of* racial oppression — and whether their disappearance is a tragedy or a liberation.

## Historical Context

The novel's depiction of institutional collapse reflected real anxieties in 1930s Black America. The Great Migration had already dispersed Black populations from the rural South to Northern cities, straining traditional institutions. The NAACP and Urban League competed with Garvey's UNIA for the loyalty of Black Americans. Schuyler's satire targets the professional class of "race leaders" whose livelihoods depended on the continued existence of a distinct and oppressed Black population. His argument — that race leadership is an industry like any other — was scandalous in 1931 and remains provocative.

## In the Narrative

The novel traces institutional collapse across several domains. Black newspapers, which had been "full of skin-whitening and hair-straightening advertisements" (005-101), lose their revenue base. Negro fraternal orders like the "Ancient and Honorable Order of Crocodiles" (003-016) lose their membership. Black churches face an existential crisis as their congregations seek salvation in whiteness rather than in God. The satirical high point is the Black leadership conference (Chapter 5), where prominent Negroes debate how to stop the exodus — revealing that their concern is not for the welfare of Black people but for the survival of their own institutions and incomes.

## Satirical Treatment

The satire of social mortality is the novel's most melancholic register. Beneath the comedy of Hank Johnson's get-rich schemes and Max Disher's identity games, there is genuine pathos in the image of empty churches and shuttered newspaper offices. Schuyler does not sentimentalize these losses — he consistently portrays Black institutions as exploitative of the very people they claim to serve — but the cumulative effect of their disappearance creates an unsettling vacuum. What replaces Black culture, the novel asks, once there are no Black people? The answer is ambiguous: a world without racial distinction, but also without the rich cultural forms that racial oppression paradoxically produced.
