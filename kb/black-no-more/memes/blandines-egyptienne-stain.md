## Overview

Blandine's Egyptienne Stain is the commercial product that drives the racial-economic reversal in the epilogue of [[george-schuyler|George Schuyler]]'s *Black No More*. Invented by [[sari-blandine|Mrs. Sari Blandine]] — formerly the Harlem beauty culturist Madame Sisseretta Blandish — the stain enables white Americans to darken their skin after [[crookman-monograph|Crookman's monograph]] makes extreme paleness socially suspect. It is the symmetrical inverse of the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]]: where Crookman's invention turned Black Americans white, Blandine's turns white Americans dark.

## Origin and Development

After Crookman's monograph reveals that new Caucasians are two to three shades lighter than old Caucasians — making paleness a marker of possible Negro ancestry — "those of the upper class began to look around for ways to get darker" [013-126]. Beauty shops began selling powders with names like *Poudre Nègre* and *Poudre le Egyptienne*.

Mrs. Blandine, then working a steam table at a Broadway Automat after the collapse of her hair-straightening business, spent a week at the Public Library researching skin stains. She developed "a skin stain that would impart a long-wearing light-brown tinge to the pigment" [013-128]. After successful testing on her daughter — who received a millionaire's proposal within a month — she gave free applications to neighborhood women. Demand exploded:

> Mrs. Blandine's stain became most popular and her fame grew in her locality. She opened a shop in her front room and soon had it crowded from morning till night. The concoction was patented as Blandine's Egyptienne Stain. [013-129]

## Commercial Empire

By the time [[president-hornbill|President-Elect Hornbill]] was inaugurated, "her Egyptienne Stain Shoppes dotted the country and she had won three suits for infringement of patent" [013-130]. The stain created a new social norm:

> Everybody that was anybody had a stained skin. A girl without one was avoided by the young men; a young man without one was at a decided disadvantage, economically and socially. A white face became startlingly rare. [013-130]

Within two years, fifteen companies manufactured competing stains and artificial tans, culminating in "even the Zulu Tan" becoming fashionable [013-131].

## Satirical Significance

The Egyptienne Stain completes the novel's economic argument about race. The same entrepreneurial logic that sustained the [[cosmetic-industry|cosmetic whitening industry]] — profiting from racial anxiety and the desire to approximate a socially privileged appearance — now sustains a darkening industry. The product name "Egyptienne" invokes an African civilization to sell darkness to white consumers, inverting the earlier chapters' association of whiteness with civilization and Blackness with primitiveness.

The stain's trajectory — from a woman's front-room shop to a national chain with patent litigation — also mirrors the [[black-no-more-inc|Black-No-More, Incorporated]] business model. Both enterprises monetize racial transformation; both make their founders wealthy; both are ultimately indifferent to the social consequences. The difference is only the direction of the transformation.
