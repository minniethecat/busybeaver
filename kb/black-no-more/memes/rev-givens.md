## Overview

Reverend Henry Givens is the novel's most cynical character — a man who founds a white supremacist organization despite privately having no belief in the racial ideology he peddles. Unlike the novel's other entrepreneurs, Givens understands that the product he sells is pure fabrication, and this clarity makes him the most dangerous figure in [[george-schuyler]]'s satirical universe.

His trajectory from marginal Atlanta preacher to Imperial Grand Wizard to President of the United States mirrors the arc of [[black-no-more-inc|Black-No-More, Incorporated]] — both rise on the same wave of racial anxiety that [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]]'s treatment releases. When the novel's final genealogical revelations expose him as having Negro ancestry, Givens flees the country, completing the satire of racial identity as a confidence game whose victims include its practitioners.

## Biography

### A "Short, Wizened" Man with Grand Ambitions

When Matthew Fisher ([[max-disher|Max Disher]]) first sees Givens, he is unimpressed: "Rev. Henry Givens, Imperial Grand Wizard of the Knights of Nordica, was a short, wizened, almost bald-headed old gentleman. He had the watery eyes of a man long accustomed to looking into the dark recesses of the human soul and not liking what he saw there" (004-030).

Matthew's first impression masks a shrewd assessment: "Givens must have some money, he thought" (004-021). The observation proves correct — Givens has already built a substantial organization from Southern white men terrified by the Black-No-More treatment.

### The Businessman of Fear

Givens's genius is entrepreneurial rather than ideological. "Unlike Givens, he had no belief in the racial integrity nonsense nor any confidence in the racial purity nonsense" (004-041) — this description of Matthew Fisher's private thoughts captures Givens's defining characteristic. He is a salesman whose product happens to be racial hatred.

At five dollars per head, the Knights of Nordica membership is priced for mass consumption. His meetings follow the grammar of religious revival — "Rev. Givens opened the meeting with a prayer" (004-066) — because Givens understands that white supremacy thrives on the same emotional dynamics as faith. Both promise salvation; both require a congregation; both generate a steady revenue stream.

### The Presidential Nomination

Givens's political ascent follows the logic of the novel's satirical universe: the most successful race-baiter becomes the most viable candidate. At the Democratic convention, "the sodden and nodding members of the Committee on Permanent Organization insisted on the selection of Rev. Givens" for the presidential nomination (010-046).

As a candidate, Givens receives the ultimate endorsement: "One writer suggested that Givens resembled Lincoln" (010-054). The comparison is grotesque but thematically precise — the party of Lincoln has become the party of the Imperial Grand Wizard, and the continuity is not rupture but fulfillment.

### The Genealogical Revelation and Flight

The novel's final satirical twist is Givens's exposure. When [[dr-crookman|Dr. Crookman]]'s genealogical research proves that all "white" Americans have Negro ancestry, Givens's own ancestry is among the first revealed. "Givens, Snobbcraft, Buggerie, Kretin" make the newspaper headlines (012-027).

The man who built his career on racial purity now faces the truth of his own mixed heritage. His response is pure survival instinct: "let's get out o' here before that mob gets here!" (012-056). Givens flees to Mexico, his presidential ambitions and his racial ideology collapsing simultaneously under the weight of the very genealogical science his movement had championed.

## Character Analysis

### The Cynic as Satirical Truth-Teller

Givens is Schuyler's most complete embodiment of the novel's materialist thesis about race. His cynicism is total — he "despised" his followers (004-041) — but Schuyler does not present this as villainy so much as clarity. Givens understands what the novel demonstrates: that racial ideology is always, at bottom, a mechanism for extracting money and power.

His previous career as a minister is not coincidental. Schuyler suggests that preaching and race-baiting draw on the same rhetorical skills and serve the same economic function. When Givens transitions from pulpit to political podium to radio broadcast, the medium changes but the message remains: fear of the Other, monetized.

### The Self-Devouring Logic of Racial Purity

The genealogical revelation that destroys Givens completes the novel's satire of racial essentialism. Givens spent his career insisting on racial purity; the science he invoked to justify his ideology now proves that purity was always a fiction. The fact that Givens flees rather than confronts the contradiction reveals the essential cowardice beneath the bombast — the Grand Wizard of white supremacy cannot face being "proved of Negro descent" (012-027).
