## Overview

Black churches were the cornerstone of African American institutional life in the early 20th century — places of worship, community organizing, mutual aid, and political mobilization. The novel traces their decline as the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]] empties their pews. Congregants who had sought salvation in God now seek salvation in whiteness, and the churches' spiritual authority proves no match for the treatment's material promise.

## Role in the Narrative

The churches' decline is part of the novel's broader depiction of institutional collapse. Ministers who had preached [[racial-pride|racial pride]] from the pulpit find their flocks choosing whiteness over worship. The novel treats the churches with less overt satire than other institutions, but the implication is clear: spiritual community, like every other form of Black social life, proves vulnerable to the economic logic of the treatment.

## Satirical Function

Questions whether Black religious institutions serve genuine spiritual needs or merely provide a captive market for Black preachers — the same materialist skepticism [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] applies to every other racial institution.
