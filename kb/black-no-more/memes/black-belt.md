# Black Belt

## Overview

The "Black Belt" originally referred to the dark, fertile soil of central Alabama and Mississippi — a geologic, not a racial, designation. By the late nineteenth century, however, the term had acquired a demographic meaning: the crescent of Southern counties where the Black population outnumbered the white, largely because the plantation economy had concentrated enslaved labor on the region's richest cotton land. After Reconstruction, the Black Belt became both the geographic heart of African American political power and the target of the most intense white-supremacist violence and disenfranchisement. In *Black No More*, Schuyler repurposes the term as an economic category, treating the Black Belt as the spatial infrastructure of segregation — and then showing what happens when the infrastructure loses its reason for being.

## In the Narrative

### Slumming and the Harlem Black Belt

The Black Belt enters the novel in two registers. In the early chapters, it is the social geography of segregated urban life: Max Disher and his friends move through a world bounded by the Black Belt's invisible but rigorously enforced borders. Schuyler depicts a white character from Atlanta "up here trying to get a thrill in the Black Belt but a thrill from observation instead of contact" (001-059), capturing the voyeuristic tourism — slumming — that the Black Belt attracted from white New Yorkers. Max's sardonic observation — "Gee, but white folks were funny. They didn't want black folks' game and yet they were always frequenting Negro resorts" (001-059) — exposes the hypocrisy at the heart of segregation-era racial tourism.

### The Southern Black Belts After the Treatment

The novel's most sustained treatment of the Black Belt comes in chapter 8, where Schuyler provides an economic audit of the treatment's consequences. The passage functions as a kind of revenge satire: everything white Southerners extracted from the Black Belt — cheap labor, political control, the profits of segregation — is now shown to have depended on the continued *existence* of an identifiable Black population. When the treatment eliminates that population, the Black Belt's economic logic collapses (008-004).

The railroad industry was among the first casualties: "Hundreds of wooden railroad coaches, long since condemned as death traps in all other parts of the country, had to be scrapped by the railroads when there were no longer any Negroes to Jim Crow" (008-004). Thousands of "Jim Crow" waiting rooms sat unused, while taxes rose to pay for infrastructure improvements that segregation had previously made unnecessary (008-005). The "mill barons viewed with distaste the prospect of having to do away with child labor" — another feature of the old racial-economic order now threatened by the disappearance of a visibly distinct Black workforce (008-006).

## Economic Analysis

### Infrastructure and Racial Visibility

Schuyler's chapter 8 catalog is remarkable for its specificity. Streets in the former Black Belts, "without sewers or pavement," suddenly require municipal investment because their occupants are now white — or at least, no longer visibly Black. Schools that paid Black teachers thirty and forty dollars a month must now pay standard salaries. Real estate owners who had never repaired their "tumbledown property" for Black tenants must now upgrade for white ones. The satire's edge lies in its implication: white Southerners were perfectly willing to tolerate squalor when the victims were Black; the same conditions become intolerable only when the occupants appear white.

### Political Class Collapse

The treatment also destroyed the political economy of the Black Belt's Negro leadership class:

> The Negro politicians in the various Black Belts, grown fat and sleek "protecting" vice with the aid of Negro votes which they were able to control by virtue of housing segregation, lectured in vain about black solidarity, race pride and political emancipation; but nothing stopped the exodus to the white race. (005-042)

Schuyler's portrait is characteristically unsentimental: the Black political class, in his view, was as invested in the perpetuation of the segregated system as the white elites who profited from it.

The Black Belt thus serves as the novel's master trope for the economic entanglement of race and infrastructure. Segregation was not merely a social arrangement; it was a system of resource extraction, maintained by the visible difference between those who paid and those who benefited. Without that visible difference, the economic incentives that sustained segregation evaporate.

## Key Passages

> "Thousands of miles of streets located in the former Black Belts, and thus without sewers or pavement, were having to be improved at the insistent behest of the rapidly increased white population, real and imitation." (008-004)

> "Real estate owners who had never dreamed of making repairs on their tumbledown property when it was occupied by the docile Negroes, were having to tear down, rebuild and alter to suit white tenants." (008-004)

> "Naturally taxes increased. Chambers of Commerce were now unable to send out attractive advertising to Northern business firms offering no or very low taxation as an inducement to them to move South." (008-005)

> "Hundreds of wooden railroad coaches, long since condemned as death traps in all other parts of the country, had to be scrapped by the railroads when there were no longer any Negroes to Jim Crow." (008-004)
