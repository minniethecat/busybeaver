Lynching — the extralegal murder of Black Americans by white mobs — appears in [[black-no-more-treatment|Black No More]] not as a central plot event but as a persistent background condition, a taken-for-granted feature of the racial landscape that Schuyler treats with the same savage satirical detachment he applies to every other institution. The novel's references to lynching reveal it as simultaneously a genuine horror, a political fundraising tool, a media commodity, and a form of civic entertainment — a layered critique that anticipates later scholarship on the social functions of racial violence.

## Lynching as Political Commodity

The National Social Equality League, Schuyler's satire of civil rights organizations, is revealed to depend on lynching for its institutional survival. League officials "were never so happy and excited as when a Negro was barred from a theater or fried to a crisp" [005-045]. The grotesque phrase "fried to a crisp" — applied to a human being burned alive — captures Schuyler's method: the horror is real, but so is the uncomfortable fact that atrocity generates donations. The League's fundraising depends on the continuation of the violence it claims to oppose, making it structurally invested in the very conditions it exists to combat.

> While the large staff of officials was eager to end all oppression and persecution of the Negro, they were never so happy and excited as when a Negro was barred from a theater or fried to a crisp. Then they would leap for telephones, grab telegraph pads and yell for stenographers; smiling through their simulated indignation at the spectacle of another reason for their continued existence and appeals for funds. [005-045]

## The Anti-Lynching Campaign as Industry

The novel situates lynching within a broader economy of racial advocacy. The League "had for forty years carried on the fight for full social equality for the Negro citizens and the immediate abolition of lynching as a national sport" [005-045]. The phrase "national sport" is a triple indictment: it names lynching as a form of popular entertainment, it implicates the entire nation in the practice (not just the South), and it suggests that abolition efforts have been spectacularly ineffective after four decades. The Black-No-More treatment, by contrast, threatens to succeed where agitation failed — not by ending lynching but by eliminating its targets, which is Schuyler's darkest joke about the limits of reform.

## The Declining Audience

As the Black-No-More treatment spreads, lynching loses its audience. [[bunny-brown|Bunny Brown]] observes that "Shines are too busy getting white to bother reading about lynching, crime and peonage" [006-103]. The observation is bleak: racial violence loses its political salience not because it stops happening but because the potential victims have found a way to escape the category of victimhood. The anti-lynching movement's fundraising base evaporates not because lynching has been defeated but because its constituency has chosen exit over voice.

> "Nope, they're a thing of the past. Shines are too busy getting white to bother reading about lynching, crime and peonage," said Bunny. [006-103]

## Happy Hill: Lynching as Civic Heritage

The novel's most sustained treatment of lynching appears in the Happy Hill episode, a chapter-length satire of Southern racial violence. Happy Hill boasts "its inordinately high illiteracy rate and its lynching record — but these things were seldom mentioned, although no one was ashamed of them. Certain things are taken for granted everywhere" [013-056]. The community's lynching post — "a large iron post about five feet high" on which "all blacks were burned" — is "one of the landmarks of the community and was pointed out to visitors with pardonable civic pride by local boosters" [013-058]. Each nick in the post "stood for a Negro dispatched." This is lynching as heritage tourism — the violence that the rest of the novel treats as political commodity is here literalized as a monument, as banal and unremarkable as a historical marker.

> This post was one of the landmarks of the community and was pointed out to visitors with pardonable civic pride by local boosters. [013-058]
