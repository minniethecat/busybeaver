## Overview

President-Elect Hornbill is a purely functional figure — his name appears only twice in the novel's epilogue, marking the transition from the Goosie to the Hornbill administration as the backdrop for the social inversion that closes the novel. "By the time President-Elect Hornbill was inaugurated," Mrs. Blandine's Egyptienne Stain Shoppes dotted the country, skin-darkening had become de rigueur, and America was "definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded." Hornbill's administration thus inherits a nation where the racial categories that structured the entire preceding plot have collapsed — though Schuyler gives no indication that Hornbill will govern any differently than Goosie.

## Satirical Function

Like President Goosie, President-Elect Hornbill is less a character than a fixed point in the novel's institutional landscape. Schuyler names the chief executive but gives him no policies, no speeches, no governing acts — only the calendar function of an inauguration date that marks how long it took the nation to flip its racial hierarchy. The choice of name — "Hornbill," a bird known for its oversized beak — continues Schuyler's satirical naming convention: a succession from Goosie (goose) to Hornbill suggests an avian menagerie of interchangeable political leadership. Neither president is portrayed as governing; they merely preside over the social currents that the real actors — Crookman, Hank Johnson, Mrs. Blandine — have set in motion.

## Key Episodes

> By the time President-Elect Hornbill was inaugurated, her Egyptienne Stain Shoppes dotted the country and she had won three suits for infringement of patent. (013-120)

> Everybody that was anybody had a stained skin. A girl without one was avoided by the young men; a young man without one was at a decided disadvantage, economically and socially. A white face became startlingly rare. America was definitely, enthusiastically mulatto-minded. (013-130)
