## Overview

The Black Leadership Conference of [[ch05|Chapter 5]] is the novel's most concentrated satire of the Black professional class. Summoned to address the crisis of mass [[racial-passing|whitening]], the "[[negro-leadership-class|Negro leaders]]" — newspaper editors, ministers, educators, politicians — debate strategies for preserving the Negro race. The conference quickly reveals that their concern is not for the welfare of Black people but for the survival of their own institutions, incomes, and status. "The Negro leaders found themselves in a very predicament. Their jobs were at stake" (005-040).

## Role in the Narrative

The conference occupies much of Chapter 5 and serves as the novel's most explicit statement of its materialist thesis: racial leadership is an industry, and its practitioners have the same self-interested motivations as any other professionals. The conference's failure to produce any effective strategy is the novel's answer to the question of whether racial solidarity can survive self-interest.

## Satirical Function

Lampoons the entire apparatus of Black "race leadership," suggesting that organizations like the NAACP and Urban League — while genuinely serving Black communities — also serve the careers of their leaders. [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s equal-opportunity cynicism extends to both white and Black racial institutions.
