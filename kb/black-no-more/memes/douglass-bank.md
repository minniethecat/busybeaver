## Overview

The Douglass Bank was a Black-owned financial institution in [[harlem|Harlem]], where [[bunny-brown|Bunny Brown]] worked as a teller before the [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More treatment]] upended the economic order of Black Harlem. Named, by implication, for Frederick Douglass, the bank appears briefly but significantly as one of the institutions caught in the financial chaos following the mass whitening of Harlem's population. Schuyler uses the bank to illustrate the economic dimensions of racial transformation — when Black people "vanish," so do their institutions.

## Background

The Douglass Bank is introduced early in the novel alongside [[bunny-brown|Bunny Brown]]'s biographical details: after serving in the 15th regiment in France during World War I, Bunny became a teller at the Douglass Bank, where he and [[max-disher|Max Disher]] earned a reputation as "gay blades in black Harlem" (PN 001-012). The bank's name evokes Frederick Douglass, the abolitionist and statesman, situating it within the tradition of Black institutional self-help that characterized early 20th-century Harlem.

## Role in the Narrative

The Douglass Bank appears at two critical narrative points. In the opening chapter, it serves as background characterization for Bunny Brown — the respectable job that anchors his otherwise pleasure-seeking life. In Chapter 3, after the Black-No-More treatment's popularity has begun emptying Harlem, the bank becomes a site of chaos: "the tellers were busier than bootleggers on Christmas Eve" as customers rush to withdraw funds, and the bank is "short-handed because of the mysterious absence of Bunny Brown" — who has, unbeknownst to his colleagues, taken the treatment himself and vanished into white society (PN 003-033).

## Satirical Function

The Douglass Bank is part of Schuyler's broader satire of Black economic aspiration and its vulnerability. The bank's respectable facade is undercut by the "feverish activity" of a bank run — a classic trope of financial panic. That the bank is named for Frederick Douglass adds ironic weight: the institution that symbolizes Black self-sufficiency is hollowed out not by white racism but by Black people's own choice to become white. The satire cuts both ways: it mocks the fragility of racial solidarity when individual economic incentives change, while also documenting the real economic disruption that the treatment causes in Black communities.

The absence of Bunny Brown, who has abandoned his post without notice, personalizes the institutional collapse — individual choices cascade into collective consequences.

## Key Passages

> Max Disher and Bunny Brown had been pals ever since the war when they soldiered together in the old 15th regiment in France. Max was one of the Aframerican Fire Insurance Company's crack agents, Bunny was a teller in the Douglass Bank and both bore the reputation of gay blades in black Harlem. (PN 001-012)

> Meantime there was feverish activity in Harlem's financial institutions. At the Douglass Bank the tellers were busier than bootleggers on Christmas Eve. Moreover, they were short-handed because of the mysterious absence of Bunny Brown. A long line of depositors wound from the tellers' cages out into Seventh Avenue. (PN 003-033)
