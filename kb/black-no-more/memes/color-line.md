## Overview

The "color line" — W.E.B. Du Bois's famous formulation from *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903): "the problem of the Twentieth Century is the problem of the color-line" — is the organizing concept of American racial segregation and the central target of Schuyler's satire. *Black No More* asks: what if the color line could be erased not by political struggle but by technology? And what if its erasure revealed that the line was never real to begin with — merely a social convention maintained for economic and political convenience?

## The Color Line in Black No More

Schuyler's novel treats the color line as both omnipresent and absurd. It is omnipresent because every character's life is structured by which side of it they occupy: Max cannot dance with the white woman at the Honky Tonk Club, Dr. Crookman cannot be acknowledged as a scientist outside of Harlem, the mill workers of Paradise are divided by a racial solidarity that serves only their employers. The line determines where you can live, who you can marry, what you can earn.

It is absurd because the Black-No-More treatment proves that the line has no material basis. When Black people can become white with "three swift, painless and harmless treatments given on three successive days" (002-020), the entire apparatus of segregation — the laws, the customs, the science, the theology — is revealed as a confidence trick. The color line was never about biology; it was always about power.

## The Novel's Resolution

The novel's ending — in which Crookman's monograph reveals that everyone has mixed ancestry, making the color line impossible to draw with any precision — completes the satire's argument. When whiteness is no longer a stable category, the color line dissolves into a continuum. The final image of Americans darkening their skin to match the treatment-altered babies suggests that Schuyler's target was never really Blackness or whiteness but the very idea of racial classification itself.
