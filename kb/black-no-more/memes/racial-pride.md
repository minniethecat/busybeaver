## Overview

Racial pride — the assertion of Black identity, achievement, and solidarity against white supremacy — is a central satirical target in *Black No More*. Schuyler, a provocative iconoclast within the Harlem Renaissance, uses the novel to question whether racial pride is a liberatory force or merely the mirror image of the white racism it opposes. When Black people can become white at fifty dollars a treatment, racial pride evaporates: "The Negroes… had forgotten all loyalties" (005-040). The novel's most acid observation is that racial pride, like racial prejudice, is sustained by material interest.

## In the Narrative

Dr. Crookman embodies the contradictions of racial pride. He "prided himself above all on being a great lover of his race" (003-020), subscribes to Negro newspapers, fills his office with African art, and is known in Harlem society as a "Race Man." Yet his solution to the race problem is to eliminate Blackness entirely — and his personal life undercuts his public posture: "he was wedded to everything black except the black woman — his wife was a white girl with remote Negro ancestry" (003-020). Meanwhile, Max Disher's friend Bunny Brown dismisses race pride entirely: "Niggers didn't really have any race pride" (002-079).

## Historical Context

The Harlem Renaissance (c. 1918–1937) was animated by debates over racial representation. Figures like Alain Locke promoted "The New Negro" — a vision of Black cultural achievement and dignity. Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association preached racial pride and pan-Africanism. Schuyler, a journalist and satirist, was a fierce critic of both camps, arguing in essays like "The Negro-Art Hokum" (1926) that racial categories themselves were the problem. *Black No More* extends this argument to its logical extreme: if racial pride depends on racial identity, eliminating race eliminates the need for pride.

## Satirical Treatment

The novel's satire of racial pride operates at every level. Black newspapers and organizations that preach solidarity are shown to collapse when their audience pursues whiteness. The "exaggerated development of race pride" (008-003) among whitened Negroes becomes a telltale sign that they are not "really" white. Schuyler's implicit argument is that racial pride is a reactive formation — a mirror of white supremacy rather than an authentic identity — and that both would dissolve if the physical markers of race were erased.
