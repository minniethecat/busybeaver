## Overview

Washington, DC, is the seat of federal political authority in *Black No More* — and, in the novel's satirical vision, the seat of political corruption. It is where legislation is shaped not by principle but by bribery, where the Attorney General coordinates with corporate interests, and where the White House's response to the Black-No-More phenomenon is driven by political calculation rather than justice.

Washington anchors the novel's critique of American governance. The mechanisms of federal power — legislation, investigation, regulation — are shown to be entirely for sale. [[hank-johnson|Hank Johnson]]'s summary is blunt and accurate: "he has spent nearly a million dollars in such endeavors and almost as much again in molding legislative sentiment in Washington and the various state capitals" [005-031].

## In the Narrative

### The Bribery Machine

Washington first appears in the novel as a target of [[hank-johnson|Hank Johnson]]'s political bribery operation. During Crookman's quarterly financial report, [[hank-johnson|Johnson]] explains his methods:

> "He has spent nearly a million dollars in such endeavors and almost as much again in molding legislative sentiment in Washington and the various state capitals. That accounts for the fact that every bill introduced in a legislature or municipal council to put us out of business has died in committee." [005-031]

The "secret operatives, who are mostly young women" [005-032] that [[hank-johnson|Johnson]] has deployed are never described in detail — the implication is clear. Washington's legislative machinery responds to money, and [[black-no-more-inc|Black-No-More, Incorporated]], has plenty of it.

### Sanitarium on the Border

Washington also serves as one of the border cities where [[crookman-sanitarium|Black-No-More sanitarium]]s operate, "at such places as Washington, DC, Baltimore, [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]], Louisville, Evansville, Cairo, St. Louis and Denver" [005-044]. These border locations are strategic: close enough to [[the-southland|the South]] to serve the masses of Black migrants but outside the reach of Southern hostility. Washington's position on the North-South border makes it a natural site for one of these facilities.

### The Attorney General's Desk

The most vivid Washington scene features the Honorable [[snickelfritz|Walter]] Brybe, Attorney General of the United States:

> "The Honorable Walter Brybe, who had won his exalted position as Attorney General of the United States because of his long and faithful service helping large corporations to circumvent the federal laws, sat at his desk in Washington, DC. Before him lay the wired resolution from the conference of Negro leaders." [005-078]

Brybe's biography is [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s sharpest single-sentence satire of American governance: a man elevated to the nation's highest law enforcement office specifically because of his skill at helping corporations evade the law. His response to the Negro leaders' resolution — a private telephone call — suggests the real decision-making happens offstage, beyond public scrutiny.

### The White House Responds

When [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]] of [[knights-of-nordica|Nordica]]'s radio campaign generates political pressure, "the agitation of [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]] of [[knights-of-nordica|Nordica]] soon brought action from the administration at Washington" [009-029]. President Goosie announces that "several truckloads of letters condemning the corporation had been received at the White House" and promises to "take some action within the next fortnight" [009-029]. The action, predictably, is the appointment of a commission — the classic Washington maneuver for appearing to respond while doing nothing.

### The Journey South

Washington serves as a transit point in the novel's geography. When [[walter-williams|Walter Williams]] is dispatched to investigate the [[anglo-saxon-association|Anglo-Saxon Association]]: "Williams left Washington immediately for Richmond" [010-086]. The journey from the political capital to the ideological capital of white supremacy traces the novel's geography of power: the two cities, north and south, are less than 110 miles apart, and the distance between federal politics and racial ideology is even shorter.

## Significance

Washington embodies the novel's thesis about American governance: that federal power is not a check on racial and economic injustice but an instrument of it. The Attorney General is a corporate lawyer in public office. Legislation is shaped by bribery. The White House's response to crisis is a commission. Every mechanism of democratic accountability is revealed to be hollow.

[[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s Washington is not a place where justice is done but a place where power is brokered. The federal government's eventual response to the Black-No-More crisis — a genealogical investigation of the entire American population — turns out to be more catastrophic than the phenomenon it was supposed to regulate.
