## Overview

"Race Man" is a term from early-20th-century African American discourse denoting a Black man who was publicly and performatively committed to the advancement of his race. In [[george-schuyler|George Schuyler]]'s *Black No More*, the figure of the Race Man is subjected to sustained satire — most pointedly through the character of [[dr-crookman|Dr. Junius Crookman]], whose public devotion to the Negro race coexists with a personal life and a professional project that both work to eliminate Blackness.

## Dr. Crookman as Race Man

Schuyler introduces Crookman's racial identity in explicitly ironic terms:

> Dr. Crookman prided himself above all on being a great lover of his race. He had studied its history, read of its struggles and kept up with its achievements. He subscribed to six or seven Negro weekly newspapers and two of the magazines. He was so interested in the continued progress of the American Negroes that he wanted to remove all obstacles in their path by depriving them of their racial characteristics. [003-020]

The sentence structure enacts the contradiction: Crookman loves his race so much he wants to eliminate it. His home and office are "filled with African masks and paintings of Negroes by Negroes," and "he was what was known in Negro society as a Race Man." Then Schuyler delivers the punchline:

> He was wedded to everything black except the black woman — his wife was a white girl with remote Negro ancestry, of the type that Negroes were wont to describe as being "able to pass for white." [003-020]

The Race Man's public performance of racial devotion is undercut by his private preferences, which align with the very white-supremacist values he publicly opposes.

## Broader Satirical Context

The Race Man is not unique to Crookman. The figure recurs across the novel's satirical landscape of Negro leadership. [[shakespeare-agamemnon-beard|Dr. Shakespeare Agamemnon Beard]], the Harvard–Yale–Copenhagen-educated president of the [[national-social-equality-league|National Social Equality League]], writes "scholarly and biting editorials denouncing the Caucasians whom he secretly admired" [005-049]. [[santop-licorice|Santop Licorice]] of the Back-to-Africa Society is described as "a professional race man whose livelihood — like that of the National Social Equality League's leadership — depends on the continued existence of the racial grievances he purports to address."

The novel's thesis, embodied in these figures, is that the Race Man is an economic role: a position whose occupant profits from racial inequality and therefore has a structural interest in its perpetuation. When [[black-no-more-treatment|Black-No-More]] threatens to eliminate the Negro population — and thus the Race Man's constituency — the response is not liberation but panic.

## The Term's Collapse

By the novel's epilogue, the category of "Race Man" has become unintelligible. After the mass whitening and subsequent re-darkening, the racial binary on which the Race Man's identity depended has dissolved. [[dr-crookman|Crookman]] himself, now Surgeon-General of the United States, gazes at a photograph of his former associates — all "quite as dusky" as his own son — and "smiled wearily" [013-131]. The Race Man has survived his race, but the race has not survived him.
