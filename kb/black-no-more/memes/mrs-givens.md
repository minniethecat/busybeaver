## Overview

Mrs. Givens is the wife of Reverend Henry Givens (a.k.a. Dr. Junius Crookman) and mother of Helen Givens. She is introduced in Chapter 4 as a deeply religious woman — "There was no doubt about it because she freely admitted it to everybody, with or without provocation" (004-054) — whose piety coexists with simmering racism, habitual dishonesty, and a past as head of "the ladies' auxiliary of the defunct Klan." Schuyler's portrait of Mrs. Givens is one of his most acid: a character whose self-image as a good Christian woman is belied by every detail he provides.[PN:re-mg-001]

## Biography

Mrs. Givens "had probably once been beautiful but the wear and tear of a long life as the better half of an itinerant evangelist was apparent" (004-055). Her "once flaming red hair was turning gray and roan-like," her "hatchet face was a crisscross of wrinkles and lines," and she "alternately dipped snuff and smoked an evil-smelling clay pipe" — except when company was present, at which point Helen would insist she "act like civilized people" (004-055).[PN:re-mg-001]

She is portrayed as a woman of profound contradictions: she "often took the name of the Creator in vain when she got to quarreling with Henry," she "had the reputation among her friends of not always stating the exact truth," she "hated Negroes," and "her spouse had made bitter and profane comment concerning her virginity on their wedding night" — yet she "believed the Bible from cover to cover, except what it said about people with money" (004-054).[PN:re-mg-001]

She suffers from rheumatism, which she uses as an excuse to avoid anything she does not wish to do, while simultaneously bemoaning that her daughter "ain't bin int'rested in nuthin' upliftin'" since going away to school (004-053).[PN:re-mg-001]

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Givens is a study in the banality of hypocrisy. Schuyler does not present her as a monster — she is too petty, too physically decrepit, too concerned with her rheumatism and her snuff to be truly threatening. But her casual racism, her stated Christianity that coexists with every violation of Christian ethics, and her former leadership in the Klan's ladies' auxiliary illustrate his larger argument: that American racism is not a grand ideology requiring true believers, but a set of habits and self-deceptions maintained by thoroughly ordinary people.[PN:re-mg-001]
