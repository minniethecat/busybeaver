## Overview

Max Disher is the protagonist of *Black No More*, a "dapper" Harlem socialite whose humiliation at the Honky Tonk Club — when a white woman from Atlanta calls him "nigger" — propels him to become Dr. Crookman's first patient. Emerging as "Matthew Fisher," he enters white society, rises through the Knights of Nordica, and marries the woman who rejected him. His dual identity as Max/Matthew embodies the novel's central questions: is racial identity skin-deep, and does changing one's race change one's self?

## Biography

Max begins the novel as one of Harlem's "gay blades" (001-012), noted for his style and his partnership with Bunny Brown. After his transformation, he moves to Atlanta, joins the Knights of Nordica under his new identity as Matthew Fisher, and uses his insider knowledge of Black life to fabricate the racial statistics that fuel the organization's fear-mongering. He marries Helen Givens — the "girl from Atlanta" who had rejected him at the Honky Tonk — in an act of personal and political revenge that is also, ambiguously, a genuine romance.

## Character Analysis

Max is an anti-hero rather than a hero — self-interested, opportunistic, and untroubled by the moral implications of his actions. Schuyler refuses to make him a sympathetic victim or a noble rebel. His transformation from Black victim of racism to white exploiter of racism is presented without moral judgment, as a natural consequence of the system Schuyler satirizes. Max's arc suggests that racial oppression produces not saints but survivors — and that survivors, given the chance, will reproduce the same systems that oppressed them.

## The Treatment Decision

Max's decision to undergo the Black-No-More treatment is the novel's founding act. It is motivated not by ideology but by personal humiliation: the rejection at the Honky Tonk Club crystallizes a lifetime of racial slights into a single, actionable grievance. Schuyler presents the decision as pragmatic rather than tragic — Max does not agonize over "losing his Blackness" because he has never invested in that identity as a source of meaning. His transformation from Max Disher to Matthew Fisher is, in this sense, less a metamorphosis than a costume change: the man underneath remains the same.[PN:001]

## Matthew Fisher: The White Self

As Matthew Fisher, Max discovers that whiteness is not an essence but a performance — one he is exceptionally good at. His success in the Knights of Nordica derives from his intimate knowledge of Black life, which he repackages as pseudo-scientific data to fuel white anxiety. The irony is doubled: the man providing "proof" of Black inferiority was himself Black, and the white audience consuming his statistics has no way to verify them. Schuyler's point is that the entire edifice of racial science rests on precisely such uncertainties.[PN:001]
