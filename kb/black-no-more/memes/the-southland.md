## Overview

The South in *Black No More* is not a realistically rendered region but an ideological space — the territory where white supremacy is most institutionally entrenched and where the novel's political satire achieves its sharpest focus. From Atlanta (where the Knights of Nordica are headquartered) to Paradise, South Carolina (the company town where labor and race intersect) to Mississippi (Senator Bosh's constituency), the South functions as both setting and symbol.

## Geography and Politics

Schuyler's South is defined by its institutions rather than its landscape. Atlanta — "that bustling, growing and enterprising city" (004-001) — is the site of Max/Matthew's re-entry into society and the organizational center of the Knights of Nordica. The city's boosterism, its newspapers (the *Atlanta Daily Claxon*), and its social hierarchy are rendered with the precision of a journalist who had covered the South extensively.

Paradise, South Carolina, by contrast, is a place of production — a mill town where the economic foundations of racial ideology are laid bare. And Mississippi, Senator Bosh's home state, represents the political power of white supremacy in its most concentrated form.

## The South as Satirical Target

Schuyler's South is not a place of moonlight and magnolias but of calculation and commerce. The racial order is not a matter of tradition or honor but of economic and political advantage. The Knights of Nordica are not noble defenders of a way of life but a racket operated by men like Reverend Givens, who has "copied his financial methods" from his wife's Klan auxiliary (004-054). This demystification of Southern white supremacy — stripping it of its ideological pretensions and revealing it as a confidence game — is one of the novel's most radical achievements.

## Narrative Significance

The South is the proving ground for Matthew Fisher's transformation. His success in Atlanta society demonstrates the completeness of his reinvention: a Black man from Harlem can not only pass as white but can become one of the South's most powerful racial ideologues. The region that defines itself by racial purity is, in Schuyler's telling, the region most vulnerable to its dissolution.
