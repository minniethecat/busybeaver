## Overview

Chicago appears in *Black No More* in four distinct roles, none of them central but each contributing to the novel's satirical texture: as the site of a hypothetical Italian gunman, as the home of the Artillery Café (sponsor of a [[knights-of-nordica|Knights of Nordica]] radio broadcast), as the workplace of a gag writer who is one of exactly nine people to read a government report, and as the host city of the 1936 Republican National Convention.

Chicago's scattered appearances mirror the city's real position in the novel's world: not a primary stage like [[atlanta]] or [[harlem]], but a connective node in the national network of media, politics, and violence that [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] satirizes.

## In the Narrative

### The Italian Gunman (Ch. 4)

Chicago's first mention is a simile about the impossibility of finding a specific person in a large city: "to seek a particular one whose name one did not know was somewhat akin to hunting for a Russian Jew in the Bronx or a particular Italian gunman in Chicago" [004-001]. The throwaway line, spoken of [[max-disher|Matthew Fisher]]'s search for the Atlanta girl, reveals the casual ethnic stereotyping that permeates the novel's world — and that [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] deploys satirically to expose the logic of racial classification.

### The Artillery Café (Ch. 9)

During [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]] of [[knights-of-nordica|Nordica]]'s radio broadcast, the emcee announces that the musical accompaniment is provided by "Sammy Snort's Bogalusa Babies through the courtesy of the Artillery Café, Chicago, Illinois" [009-021]. The Artillery Café is never described, never visited, and never mentioned again — a pure product of broadcast sponsorship, a name that exists only to be spoken into a microphone. Its presence in Chicago rather than New York or Atlanta suggests the national reach of [[knights-of-nordica|the Knights]]' media operation.

### The Gag Writer (Ch. 10)

In one of the novel's funniest passages, [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]] catalogs the nine people who read the government commission's 1,789-page report on Black-No-More. Among them: "a gag writer on the staff of a humorous weekly in Chicago" [009-036]. That a Chicago humor writer is among the nine readers of a government document about racial transformation is both a joke about the report's uselessness and a quiet acknowledgment of Chicago's place in the national media ecology.

### The Republican Convention (Ch. 11)

Chicago's most narratively significant appearance is as the host of the 1936 Republican National Convention. "A few days later the Republican convention opened in Chicago. Better disciplined, as usual, than the [[democratic-party|Democrats]], its business proceeded like clockwork" [010-048]. President Goosie is renominated, and the platform adopts the slogan "Personal Liberty and Ancestral Sanctity" — a phrase that, like the convention itself, sounds principled while meaning nothing at all.

The contrast between the two conventions is pointed: the [[democratic-party|Democrats]] meet in [[mississippi|Jackson, Mississippi]] under the banner of "[[white-supremacy|White Supremacy]]" and produce chaos; the Republicans meet in Chicago under the banner of vagueness and produce clockwork. Chicago, the Midwestern industrial capital, hosts the party of business as usual.

## Significance

Chicago's role in the novel is less about the city itself than about what it represents in the national imagination: the Midwest as the "normal" America against which the racial hysteria of [[the-southland|the South]] and the ethnic chaos of New York are measured. Chicago hosts the "better disciplined" Republican Convention; Chicago publishes a "humorous weekly"; Chicago sponsors a radio broadcast. It is, in [[george-schuyler|Schuyler]]'s geography, the sensible middle — the place where American life goes on, largely untroubled by the racial apocalypse unfolding elsewhere.

This very normalcy is part of the satire. The Republican Convention proceeds "like clockwork" while the genealogical crisis builds. The gag writer reads the government report while the genealogical investigation tears the country apart. Chicago's calm is not wisdom but indifference — the Midwestern detachment that allows racial catastrophe to unfold unchecked.
