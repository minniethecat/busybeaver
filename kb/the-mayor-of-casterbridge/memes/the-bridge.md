# The Bridge

The bridge at [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is a threshold space in the most literal sense — spanning [[the-river|the river]] that runs through [[casterbridge|the town]], connecting one bank to the other, life to death. It is here that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], at his lowest point, contemplates ending his life, making the bridge the novel's most charged symbolic location.

## Description

Hardy presents the bridge as a place of passage and peril — a structure that connects but also exposes. Its position over dark water makes it a natural site for the novel's darkest psychological moments, and Hardy's description emphasizes the drop, the darkness, and the solitude.

## Narrative Function

The bridge scene represents the nadir of [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s arc — the point where his [[regret-and-suffering|suffering]] becomes so absolute that annihilation seems preferable. That he does not jump is not [[guilt-and-redemption|redemption]] but simple persistence. The bridge is where the novel comes closest to the void that underlies all its tragedies.
