# Lucetta's Death

[[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta Templeman]]'s death is the novel's most brutal demonstration of the cost of [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]] in a society built on appearances. Already pregnant and fragile, she collapses after witnes[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]g the [[the-skimmity-ride|skimmity-ride]] pass beneath her windows at [[high-place-hall|High-Place Hall]]. Her death is direct murder by [[reputation-and-social-standing|public opinion]], and Hardy does not soften the indictment (045-149).

## Narrative Context

Lucetta's death is the logical endpoint of the novel's treatment of [[gender-and-power|gender]] and [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]]. She is vulnerable because she is a woman; she is exposed because she transgressed sexual norms; she is destroyed because the public sphere offers no protection to [[gender-and-power|women]] whose private lives become public spectacle.

## Consequences

Lucetta's death leaves [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] a widower, removes the romantic complication from the [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]-[[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] dynamic, and stands as the novel's most forceful statement about the violence of social judgment.
