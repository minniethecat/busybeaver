# Section 42

(041-001) “Tr-rue, tr-rue,” said [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]. “But why didn’t you marry her when your

(041-002) wife Susan died?” Farfrae asked this and the other questions in the

(041-003) comfortably indifferent tone of one whom the matter very remotely

(041-004) concerned.

(041-005) “Ah—well you may ask that!” said [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], the new-moon-shaped grin

(041-006) adumbrating itself again upon his mouth. “In spite of all her

(041-007) protestations, when I came forward to do so, as in generosity bound,

(041-008) she was not the woman for me.”

(041-009) “She had already married another—maybe?”

(041-010) Henchard seemed to think it would be sailing too near the wind to

(041-011) descend further into particulars, and he answered “Yes.”

(041-012) “The young lady must have had a heart that bore transplanting very

(041-013) readily!”

(041-014) “She had, she had,” said Henchard emphatically.

(041-015) He opened a third and fourth letter, and read. This time he approached

(041-016) the conclusion as if the signature were indeed coming with the rest.

(041-017) But again he stopped short. The [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] was that, as may be divined, he

(041-018) had quite intended to effect a grand catastrophe at the end of this

(041-019) drama by reading out the name, he had come to the house with no other

(041-020) thought. But sitting here in cold blood he could not do it.

(041-021) Such a wrecking of hearts appalled even him. His quality was such that

(041-022) he could have annihilated them both in the heat of action; but to

(041-023) accomplish the deed by oral poison was beyond the nerve of his enmity.

(041-024) XXXV.

(041-025) As Donald stated, [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] had retired early to her room because of

(041-026) fatigue. She had, however, not gone to rest, but sat in the bedside

(041-027) chair reading and thinking over the events of the day. At the ringing

(041-028) of the door-bell by Henchard she wondered who it should be that would

(041-029) call at that comparatively late hour. The dining-room was almost under

(041-030) her bed-room; she could hear that somebody was admitted there, and

(041-031) presently the indistinct murmur of a person reading became audible.

(041-032) The usual time for Donald’s arrival upstairs came and passed, yet still

(041-033) the reading and conversation went on. This was very singular. She could

(041-034) think of nothing but that some extraordinary crime had been committed,

(041-035) and that the visitor, whoever he might be, was reading an account of it

(041-036) from a special edition of the _[[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] Chronicle_. At last she

(041-037) left the room, and descended the stairs. The dining-room door was ajar,

(041-038) and in the silence of the resting household the voice and the words

(041-039) were recognizable before she reached the lower flight. She stood

(041-040) transfixed. Her own words greeted her in Henchard’s voice, like spirits

(041-041) from the grave.

(041-042) Lucetta leant upon the banister with her cheek against the smooth

(041-043) hand-rail, as if she would make a friend of it in her misery. Rigid in

(041-044) this position, more and more words fell successively upon her ear. But

(041-045) what amazed her most was the tone of her husband. He spoke merely in

(041-046) the accents of a man who made a present of his time.

(041-047) “One word,” he was saying, as the crackling of paper denoted that

(041-048) Henchard was unfolding yet another sheet. “Is it quite fair to this

(041-049) young woman’s memory to read at such length to a stranger what was

(041-050) intended for your eye alone?”

(041-051) “Well, yes,” said Henchard. “By not giving her name I make it an

(041-052) example of all womankind, and not a scandal to one.”

(041-053) “If I were you I would destroy them,” said Farfrae, giving more thought

(041-054) to the letters than he had hitherto done. “As another man’s wife it

(041-055) would injure the woman if it were known.”

(041-056) “No, I shall not destroy them,” murmured Henchard, putting the letters

(041-057) away. Then he arose, and Lucetta heard no more.

(041-058) She went back to her bedroom in a semi-paralyzed state. For very fear

(041-059) she could not undress, but sat on the edge of the bed, waiting. Would

(041-060) Henchard let out the secret in his parting words? Her suspense was

(041-061) terrible. Had she confessed all to Donald in their early acquaintance

(041-062) he might possibly have got over it, and married her just the

(041-063) same—unlikely as it had once seemed; but for her or any one else to

(041-064) tell him now would be fatal.

(041-065) The door slammed; she could hear her husband bolting it. After looking

(041-066) round in his customary way he came leisurely up the stairs. The spark

(041-067) in her eyes well-nigh went out when he appeared round the bedroom door.

(041-068) Her gaze hung doubtful for a moment, then to her joyous amazement she

(041-069) saw that he looked at her with the rallying smile of one who had just

(041-070) been relieved of a scene that was irksome. She could hold out no

(041-071) longer, and sobbed hysterically.

(041-072) When he had restored her Farfrae naturally enough spoke of Henchard.

(041-073) “Of all men he was the least desirable as a visitor,” he said; “but it

(041-074) is my belief that he’s just a bit crazed. He has been reading to me a

(041-075) long lot of letters relating to his past life; and I could do no less

(041-076) than indulge him by listening.”

(041-077) This was sufficient. Henchard, then, had not told. Henchard’s last

(041-078) words to Farfrae, in short, as he stood on the doorstep, had been

(041-079) these: “Well—I’m obliged to ’ee for listening. I may tell more about

(041-080) her some day.”

(041-082) opening the matter at all; for in such cases we attribute to an enemy a

(041-083) [[gender-and-power|power]] of consistent action which we never find in ourselves or in our

(041-084) friends; and forget that abortive efforts from want of heart are as

(041-085) possible to revenge as to generosity.

(041-088) conceived, was yet too bold; for she dreaded lest in doing so he, like

(041-094) “I overheard your interview with my husband last night, and saw the

(041-112) much from want of spirit as design—her poorest, plainest and longest

(041-113) discarded attire.

(041-114) To avoid the contingency of being recognized she veiled herself, and

(041-115) slipped out of the house quickly. The sun was resting on the hill like

(041-116) a drop of blood on an eyelid by the time she had got up the road

(041-118) shadowy, and emphatic of the absence of every living thing.
