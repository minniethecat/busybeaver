# Section 16

(015-001) [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]], as has been hinted, was a place deposited in the block

(015-002) upon a corn-field. There was no suburb in the modern sense, or

(015-003) transitional intermixture of town and down. It stood, with regard to

(015-004) the wide fertile land adjoining, clean-cut and distinct, like a

(015-005) chess-board on a green tablecloth. The farmer’s boy could sit under his

(015-006) barley-mow and pitch a stone into the office-window of the town-clerk;

(015-007) reapers at work among the sheaves nodded to acquaintances standing on

(015-008) the pavement-corner; the red-robed judge, when he condemned a

(015-009) sheep-stealer, pronounced sentence to the tune of Baa, that floated in

(015-010) at the window from the remainder of the flock browsing hard by; and at

(015-011) executions the waiting crowd stood in a meadow immediately before the

(015-012) drop, out of which the cows had been temporarily driven to give the

(015-013) spectators room.

(015-014) The corn grown on the upland side of the borough was garnered by

(015-015) farmers who lived in an eastern purlieu called [[durnover|Durnover]]. Here

(015-016) wheat-ricks overhung the old Roman street, and thrust their eaves

(015-017) against [[the-church|the church]] tower; green-thatched barns, with doorways as high

(015-018) as the gates of Solomon’s temple, opened directly upon the main

(015-019) thoroughfare. Barns indeed were so numerous as to alternate with every

(015-020) half-dozen houses along the way. Here lived burgesses who daily walked

(015-021) the fallow; shepherds in an intra-mural squeeze. A street of farmers’

(015-022) homesteads—a street ruled by a [[power-and-authority|mayor]] and corporation, yet echoing with

(015-023) the thump of the flail, the flutter of the winnowing-fan, and the purr

(015-024) of the milk into the pails—a street which had nothing urban in it

(015-025) whatever—this was the Durnover end of Casterbridge.

(015-026) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], as was natural, dealt largely with this nursery or bed of

(015-027) small farmers close at hand—and his waggons were often down that way.

(015-028) One day, when arrangements were in [[tradition-and-change|progress]] for getting home corn from

(015-029) one of the aforesaid farms, [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] received a note by hand,

(015-030) asking her to oblige the writer by coming at once to a granary on

(015-031) Durnover Hill. As this was the granary whose contents Henchard was

(015-032) removing, she thought the request had something to do with his

(015-033) business, and proceeded thither as soon as she had put on her bonnet.

(015-034) The granary was just within the farm-yard, and stood on stone staddles,

(015-035) high enough for persons to walk under. The gates were open, but nobody

(015-036) was within. However, she entered and waited. Presently she saw a figure

(015-037) approaching the gate—that of [[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]]. He looked up at the church

(015-038) clock, and came in. By some unaccountable shyness, some wish not to

(015-039) meet him there alone, she quickly ascended the step-ladder leading to

(015-040) the granary door, and entered it before he had seen her. Farfrae

(015-041) advanced, imagining himself in solitude, and a few drops of rain

(015-042) beginning to fall he moved and stood under the shelter where she had

(015-043) just been standing. Here he leant against one of the staddles, and gave

(015-044) himself up to patience. He, too, was plainly expecting some one; could

(015-045) it be herself? If so, why? In a few minutes he looked at his watch, and

(015-046) then pulled out a note, a duplicate of the one she had herself

(015-047) received.

(015-048) This situation began to be very awkward, and the longer she waited the

(015-049) more awkward it became. To emerge from a door just above his head and

(015-050) descend the ladder, and show she had been in hiding there, would look

(015-051) so very foolish that she still waited on. A winnowing machine stood

(015-052) close beside her, and to relieve her suspense she gently moved the

(015-053) handle; whereupon a cloud of wheat husks flew out into her face, and

(015-054) covered her clothes and bonnet, and stuck into the fur of her

(015-055) victorine. He must have heard the slight movement for he looked up, and

(015-056) then ascended the steps.

(015-057) “Ah—it’s Miss [[richard-newson|Newson]],” he said as soon as he could see into the

(015-058) granary. “I didn’t know you were there. I have kept the appointment,

(015-059) and am at your service.”

(015-060) “O Mr. Farfrae,” she faltered, “so have I. But I didn’t know it was you

(015-061) who wished to see me, otherwise I—”

(015-062) “I wished to see you? O no—at least, that is, I am afraid there may be

(015-063) a mistake.”

(015-064) “Didn’t you ask me to come here? Didn’t you write this?” Elizabeth held

(015-065) out her note.

(015-066) “No. Indeed, at no hand would I have thought of it! And for you—didn’t

(015-067) you ask me? This is not your writing?” And he held up his.

(015-068) “By no means.”

(015-069) “And is that really so! Then it’s somebody wanting to see us both.

(015-070) Perhaps we would do well to wait a little longer.”

(015-071) Acting on this consideration they lingered, Elizabeth-Jane’s face being

(015-072) arranged to an expression of preternatural composure, and the young

(015-073) Scot, at every footstep in the street without, looking from under the

(015-074) granary to see if the passer were about to enter and declare himself

(015-075) their summoner. They watched individual drops of rain creeping down the

(015-076) thatch of the opposite rick—straw after straw—till they reached the

(015-077) bottom; but nobody came, and the granary roof began to drip.

(015-078) “The person is not likely to be coming,” said Farfrae. “It’s a trick

(015-079) perhaps, and if so, it’s a great pity to waste our time like this, and

(015-080) so much to be done.”

(015-081) “’Tis a great liberty,” said Elizabeth.

(015-082) “It’s true, Miss Newson. We’ll hear news of this some day depend on’t,

(015-083) and who it was that did it. I wouldn’t stand for it hindering myself;

(015-084) but you, Miss Newson——”

(015-085) “I don’t mind—much,” she replied.

(015-086) “Neither do I.”

(015-087) They lapsed again into silence. “You are anxious to get back to

(015-088) Scotland, I suppose, Mr. Farfrae?” she inquired.

(015-089) “O no, Miss Newson. Why would I be?”

(015-090) “I only supposed you might be from the song you sang at the Three

(015-091) Mariners—about Scotland and home, I mean—which you seemed to feel so

(015-092) deep down in your heart; so that we all felt for you.”

(015-093) “Ay—and I did sing there—I did—— But, Miss Newson”—and Donald’s voice

(015-094) musically undulated between two semi-tones as it always did when he

(015-095) became earnest—“it’s well you feel a song for a few minutes, and your

(015-096) eyes they get quite tearful; but you finish it, and for all you felt

(015-097) you don’t mind it or think of it again for a long while. O no, I don’t

(015-098) want to go back! Yet I’ll sing the song to you wi’ pleasure whenever

(015-099) you like. I could sing it now, and not mind at all?”

(015-100) “Thank you, indeed. But I fear I must go—rain or no.”

(015-101) “Ay! Then, Miss Newson, ye had better say nothing about this hoax, and

(015-102) take no heed of it. And if the person should say anything to you, be

(015-103) civil to him or her, as if you did not mind it—so you’ll take the

(015-104) clever person’s laugh away.” In speaking his eyes became fixed upon her

(015-105) dress, still sown with wheat husks. “There’s husks and dust on you.

(015-106) Perhaps you don’t know it?” he said, in tones of extreme delicacy. “And

(015-107) it’s very bad to let rain come upon clothes when there’s chaff on them.

(015-108) It washes in and spoils them. Let me help you—blowing is the best.”

(015-109) As Elizabeth neither assented nor dissented Donald Farfrae began

(015-110) blowing her back hair, and her side hair, and her neck, and the crown

(015-111) of her bonnet, and the fur of her victorine, Elizabeth saying, “O,

(015-112) thank you,” at every puff. At last she was fairly clean, though

(015-113) Farfrae, having got over his first concern at the situation, seemed in

(015-114) no manner of hurry to be gone.

(015-115) “Ah—now I’ll go and get ye an umbrella,” he said.

(015-116) She declined the offer, stepped out and was gone. Farfrae walked slowly

(015-117) after, looking thoughtfully at her diminishing figure, and whistling in

(015-118) undertones, “As I came down through Cannobie.”

(015-119) XV.

(015-120) At first Miss Newson’s budding beauty was not regarded with much

(015-121) interest by anybody in Casterbridge. Donald Farfrae’s gaze, it is true,

(015-122) was now attracted by the Mayor’s so-called stepdaughter, but he was

(015-123) only one. The [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] is that she was but a poor illustrative instance of

(015-124) the prophet Baruch’s sly definition: “The virgin that loveth to go

(015-125) gay.”

(015-126) When she walked abroad she seemed to be occupied with an inner chamber

(015-127) of ideas, and to have slight need for visible objects. She formed

(015-128) curious resolves on checking gay fancies in the matter of clothes,

(015-129) because it was inconsistent with her past life to blossom gaudily the

(015-130) moment she had become possessed of money. But nothing is more insidious

(015-131) than the evolution of wishes from mere fancies, and of wants from mere

(015-132) wishes. Henchard gave Elizabeth-Jane a box of delicately-tinted gloves

(015-133) one spring day. She wanted to wear them to show her appreciation of his

(015-134) kindness, but she had no bonnet that would harmonize. As an artistic

(015-135) indulgence she thought she would have such a bonnet. When she had a

(015-136) bonnet that would go with the gloves she had no dress that would go

(015-137) with the bonnet. It was now absolutely necessary to finish; she ordered

(015-138) the requisite article, and found that she had no sunshade to go with

(015-139) the dress. In for a penny in for a pound; she bought the sunshade, and

(015-140) the whole structure was at last complete.

(015-141) Everybody was attracted, and some said that her bygone simplicity was

(015-142) the art that conceals art, the “delicate imposition” of Rochefoucauld;

(015-143) she had produced an effect, a contrast, and it had been done on

(015-144) purpose. As a matter of fact this was not true, but it had its result;

(015-145) for as soon as Casterbridge thought her artful it thought her worth

(015-146) notice. “It is the first time in my life that I have been so much

(015-147) admired,” she said to herself; “though perhaps it is by those whose

(015-148) admiration is not worth having.”

(015-149) But Donald Farfrae admired her, too; and altogether the time was an

(015-150) exciting one; sex had never before asserted itself in her so strongly,

(015-151) for in former days she had perhaps been too impersonally human to be

(015-152) distinctively feminine. After an unprecedented success one day she came

(015-153) indoors, went upstairs, and leant upon her bed face downwards quite

(015-154) forgetting the possible creasing and damage. “Good Heaven,” she

(015-155) whispered, “can it be? Here am I setting up as the town beauty!”

(015-156) When she had thought it over, her usual fear of exaggerating

(015-157) appearances engendered a deep sadness. “There is something wrong in all

(015-158) this,” she mused. “If they only knew what an unfinished girl I am—that

(015-159) I can’t talk Italian, or use globes, or show any of the accomplishments

(015-160) they learn at boarding schools, how they would despise me! Better sell

(015-161) all this finery and buy myself grammar-books and dictionaries and a

(015-162) history of all the philosophies!”

(015-163) She looked from the window and saw Henchard and Farfrae in the hay-yard

(015-164) talking, with that impetuous cordiality on the Mayor’s part, and genial

(015-165) modesty on the younger man’s, that was now so generally observable in

(015-166) their intercourse. Friendship between man and man; what a rugged

(015-167) strength there was in it, as evinced by these two. And yet the seed

(015-168) that was to lift the foundation of this friendship was at that moment

(015-169) taking root in a chink of its structure.

(015-170) It was about six o’clock; the men were dropping off homeward one by

(015-171) one. The last to leave was a round-shouldered, blinking young man of

(015-172) nineteen or twenty, whose mouth fell ajar on the slightest provocation,

(015-173) seemingly because there was no chin to support it. Henchard called

(015-174) aloud to him as he went out of the gate, “Here—[[abel-whittle|Abel Whittle]]!”

(015-175) Whittle turned, and ran back a few steps. “Yes, sir,” he said, in

(015-176) breathless deprecation, as if he knew what was coming next.

(015-177) “Once more—be in time to-morrow morning. You see what’s to be done, and

(015-178) you hear what I say, and you know I’m not going to be trifled with any

(015-179) longer.”

(015-180) “Yes, sir.” Then Abel Whittle left, and Henchard and Farfrae; and

(015-181) Elizabeth saw no more of them.
