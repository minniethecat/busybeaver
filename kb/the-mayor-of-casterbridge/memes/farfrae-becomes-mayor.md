# Farfrae Becomes Mayor

[[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]]'s election to the [[power-and-authority|mayor]]'s office that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] once occupied is the political dimension of their rivalry's resolution. The transfer is complete: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] has lost his bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess, his [[reputation-and-social-standing|social standing]], and now the office that defined his public identity. [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s ascent is both a personal triumph and a generational transition.

## Narrative Context

[[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s election represents the triumph of the modern over the [[tradition-and-change|tradition]]al. Where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] governed through personality and force of [[ambition-and-will|will]], [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] governs through competence and method. The town's choice is pragmatic — and for [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], humiliating precisely because it is pragmatic rather than personal.

## Consequences

The loss of the [[power-and-authority|mayor]]'s office strips [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] of his last public identity. He is no longer "[[michael-henchard|the Mayor]] of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]" — he is simply [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], a bankrupt labourer. The novel's title, having named him, now names an absence.
