# Section 50

(049-001) He hastily put on his hat, and went out in the direction that [[richard-newson|Newson]]

(049-002) had taken. Newson’s back was soon visible up the road, crossing

(049-003) Bull-stake. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] followed, and saw his visitor stop at the King’s

(049-004) Arms, where the morning coach which had brought him waited half-an-hour

(049-005) for another coach which crossed there. The coach Newson had come by was

(049-006) now about to move again. Newson mounted, his luggage was put in, and in

(049-007) a few minutes the vehicle disappeared with him.

(049-008) He had not so much as turned his head. It was an act of simple faith in

(049-009) Henchard’s words—faith so simple as to be almost sublime. The young

(049-010) sailor who had taken [[susan-henchard|Susan Henchard]] on the spur of the moment and on

(049-011) the faith of a glance at her face, more than twenty years before, was

(049-012) still living and acting under the form of the grizzled traveller who

(049-013) had taken Henchard’s words on trust so absolute as to shame him as he

(049-014) stood.

(049-015) Was [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] to remain his by virtue of this hardy invention of a

(049-016) moment? “Perhaps not for long,” said he. Newson might converse with his

(049-017) fellow-travellers, some of whom might be [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] people; and the

(049-018) trick would be discovered.

(049-019) This probability threw Henchard into a defensive attitude, and instead

(049-020) of considering how best to right the wrong, and acquaint Elizabeth’s

(049-021) father with the [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] at once, he bethought himself of ways to keep the

(049-022) position he had accidentally won. Towards the young woman herself his

(049-023) affection grew more jealously strong with each new hazard to which his

(049-024) claim to her was exposed.

(049-025) He watched the distant highway expecting to see Newson return on foot,

(049-026) enlightened and indignant, to claim his child. But no figure appeared.

(049-027) Possibly he had spoken to nobody on the coach, but buried his [[regret-and-suffering|grief]] in

(049-028) his own heart.

(049-029) His grief!—what was it, after all, to that which he, Henchard, would

(049-030) feel at the loss of her? Newson’s affection cooled by years, could not

(049-031) equal his who had been constantly in her presence. And thus his jealous

(049-032) soul speciously argued to excuse the separation of father and child.

(049-033) He returned to the house half expecting that she would have vanished.

(049-034) No; there she was—just coming out from the inner room, the marks of

(049-035) sleep upon her eyelids, and exhibiting a generally refreshed air.

(049-036) “O father!” she said smiling. “I had no sooner lain down than I napped,

(049-037) though I did not mean to. I wonder I did not dream about poor Mrs.

(049-038) [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], after thinking of her so; but I did not. How strange it is

(049-039) that we do not often dream of latest events, absorbing as they may be.”

(049-040) “I am glad you have been able to sleep,” he said, taking her hand with

(049-041) anxious proprietorship—an act which gave her a pleasant surprise.

(049-042) They sat down to breakfast, and Elizabeth-Jane’s thoughts reverted to

(049-043) [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]. Their sadness added charm to a countenance whose beauty had

(049-044) ever lain in its meditative soberness.

(049-045) “Father,” she said, as soon as she recalled herself to the outspread

(049-046) meal, “it is so kind of you to get this nice breakfast with your own

(049-047) hands, and I idly asleep the while.”

(049-048) “I do it every day,” he replied. “You have left me; everybody has left

(049-049) me; how should I live but by my own hands.”

(049-050) “You are very lonely, are you not?”

(049-051) “Ay, child—to a degree that you know nothing of! It is my own fault.

(049-052) You are the only one who has been near me for weeks. And you [[ambition-and-will|will]] come

(049-053) no more.”

(049-054) “Why do you say that? Indeed I will, if you would like to see me.”

(049-055) Henchard signified dubiousness. Though he had so lately hoped that

(049-056) Elizabeth-Jane might again live in his house as daughter, he would not

(049-057) ask her to do so now. Newson might return at any moment, and what

(049-058) Elizabeth would think of him for his deception it were best to bear

(049-059) apart from her.

(049-060) When they had breakfasted his stepdaughter still lingered, till the

(049-061) moment arrived at which Henchard was accustomed to go to his daily

(049-062) work. Then she arose, and with assurance of coming again soon went up

(049-063) the hill in the morning sunlight.

(049-064) “At this moment her heart is as warm towards me as mine is towards her,

(049-065) she would live with me here in this humble cottage for the asking! Yet

(049-066) before the evening probably he will have come, and then she will scorn

(049-067) me!”

(049-068) This reflection, constantly repeated by Henchard to himself,

(049-069) accompanied him everywhere through the day. His mood was no longer that

(049-070) of the rebellious, ironical, reckless misadventurer; but the leaden

(049-071) gloom of one who has lost all that can make life interesting, or even

(049-072) tolerable. There would remain nobody for him to be proud of, nobody to

(049-073) fortify him; for Elizabeth-Jane would soon be but as a stranger, and

(049-074) worse. Susan, Farfrae, Lucetta, Elizabeth—all had gone from him, one

(049-075) after one, either by his fault or by his misfortune.

(049-076) In place of them he had no interest, hobby, or desire. If he could have

(049-077) summoned music to his aid his existence might even now have been borne;

(049-078) for with Henchard music was of regal [[gender-and-power|power]]. The merest trumpet or organ

(049-079) tone was enough to move him, and high harmonies transubstantiated him.

(049-080) But [[fate-and-determinism|hard fate]] had ordained that he should be unable to call up this

(049-081) Divine spirit in his need.

(049-082) The whole land ahead of him was as darkness itself; there was nothing

(049-083) to come, nothing to wait for. Yet in the natural course of life he

(049-084) might possibly have to linger on earth another thirty or forty

(049-085) years—scoffed at; at best pitied.

(049-086) The thought of it was unendurable.

(049-087) To the east of Casterbridge lay moors and meadows through which much

(049-088) water flowed. The wanderer in this direction who should stand still for

(049-089) a few moments on a quiet night, might hear singular symphonies from

(049-090) these waters, as from a lampless orchestra, all playing in their sundry

(049-091) tones from near and far parts of the moor. At a hole in a rotten weir

(049-092) they executed a recitative; where a tributary brook fell over a stone

(049-093) breastwork they trilled cheerily; under an arch they performed a

(049-094) metallic cymballing, and at [[durnover|Durnover]] Hole they hissed. The spot at

(049-095) which their instrumentation rose loudest was a place called Ten

(049-096) Hatches, whence during high springs there proceeded a very fugue of

(049-097) sounds.

(049-098) The river here was deep and strong at all times, and the hatches on

(049-099) this account were raised and lowered by cogs and a winch. A path led

(049-100) from the second bridge over the highway (so often mentioned) to these

(049-101) Hatches, crossing the stream at their head by a narrow plank-bridge.

(049-102) But after night-fall human beings were seldom found going that way, the

(049-103) path leading only to a deep reach of the stream called Blackwater, and

(049-104) the passage being dangerous.

(049-105) Henchard, however, leaving the town by the east road, proceeded to the

(049-106) second, or stone bridge, and thence struck into this path of solitude,

(049-107) following its course beside the stream till the dark shapes of the Ten

(049-108) Hatches cut the sheen thrown upon [[the-river|the river]] by the weak lustre that

(049-109) still lingered in the west. In a second or two he stood beside the

(049-110) weir-hole where the water was at its deepest. He looked backwards and

(049-111) forwards, and no creature appeared in view. He then took off his coat

(049-112) and hat, and stood on the brink of the stream with his hands clasped in

(049-113) front of him.

(049-114) While his eyes were bent on the water beneath there slowly became

(049-115) visible a something floating in the circular pool formed by the wash of

(049-116) centuries; the pool he was intending to make his death-bed. At first it

(049-117) was indistinct by reason of the shadow from the bank; but it emerged

(049-118) thence and took shape, which was that of a human body, lying stiff and

(049-119) stark upon the surface of the stream.

(049-120) In the circular current imparted by the central flow the form was

(049-121) brought forward, till it passed under his eyes; and then he perceived

(049-122) with a sense of horror that it was _himself_. Not a man somewhat

(049-123) resembling him, but one in all respects his counterpart, his actual

(049-124) double, was floating as if dead in Ten Hatches Hole.

(049-125) The sense of the supernatural was strong in this unhappy man, and he

(049-126) turned away as one might have done in the actual presence of an

(049-127) appalling miracle. He covered his eyes and bowed his head. Without

(049-128) looking again into the stream he took his coat and hat, and went slowly

(049-129) away.

(049-130) Presently he found himself by the door of his own dwelling. To his

(049-131) surprise Elizabeth-Jane was standing there. She came forward, spoke,

(049-132) called him “father” just as before. Newson, then, had not even yet

(049-133) returned.

(049-134) “I thought you seemed very sad this morning,” she said, “so I have come

(049-135) again to see you. Not that I am anything but sad myself. But everybody

(049-136) and everything seem against you so, and I know you must be suffering.”

(049-137) How this woman divined things! Yet she had not divined their whole

(049-138) extremity.

(049-139) He said to her, “Are miracles still worked, do ye think, Elizabeth? I

(049-140) am not a read man. I don’t know so much as I could wish. I have tried

(049-141) to peruse and learn all my life; but the more I try to know the more

(049-142) ignorant I seem.”

(049-143) “I don’t quite think there are any miracles nowadays,” she said.

(049-144) “No interference in the case of desperate intentions, for instance?

(049-145) Well, perhaps not, in a direct way. Perhaps not. But will you come and

(049-146) walk with me, and I will show ’ee what I mean.”

(049-147) She agreed willingly, and he took her over the highway, and by the

(049-148) lonely path to Ten Hatches. He walked restlessly, as if some haunting

(049-150) would gladly have talked of Lucetta, but feared to disturb him. When

(049-151) they got near the weir he stood still, and asked her to go forward and

(049-152) look into the pool, and tell him what she saw.

(049-153) She went, and soon returned to him. “Nothing,” she said.

(049-154) “Go again,” said Henchard, “and look narrowly.”

(049-155) She proceeded to the river brink a second time. On her return, after

(049-156) some delay, she told him that she saw something floating round and

(049-159) “Are they like mine?” asked Henchard.

(049-160) “Well—they are. Dear me—I wonder if—Father, let us go away!”

(049-161) “Go and look once more; and then we will get home.”

(049-163) the margin of the pool. She started up, and hastened back to his side.

(049-165) “Let us go home.”

(049-167) “The effigy,” she answered hastily. “They must have thrown it into the

(049-171) “Ah—to be sure—the image o’ me! But where is the other? Why that one
