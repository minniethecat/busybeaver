# Elizabeth-Jane's Parentage

The question of [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]'s true father runs through the novel as a submerged thread that surfaces with devastating effect. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] believes her to be his daughter; Susan's letter after her death reveals that [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] is actually [[richard-newson|Newson]]'s child. The revelation strips [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] of his last remaining emotional connection and accelerates his final decline.

## Narrative Context

The parentage mystery is Hardy's mechanism for exploring identity, belonging, and the fragility of familial bonds. [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] herself is unaware of the question for much of the novel — she simply exists, uncertain of her origins, adapting to whatever household [[ambition-and-will|will]] have her. The revelation affects everyone around her more than it affects her.

## Consequences

For [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], the discovery that [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] is not his daughter severs the one relationship that had survived his other losses. His subsequent behaviour toward her — alternating between desperate possessiveness and bitter rejection — reflects his inability to accept a bond based on anything other than biology.
