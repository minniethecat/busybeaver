(P02-051) I.

(P02-052) One evening of late summer, before the nineteenth century had reached

(P02-053) one-third of its span, a young man and woman, the latter carrying a

(P02-054) child, were approaching the large village of [[weydon-priors|Weydon-Priors]], in Upper

(P02-055) Wessex, on foot. They were plainly but not ill clad, though the thick

(P02-056) hoar of dust which had accumulated on their shoes and garments from an

(P02-057) obviously long journey lent a disadvantageous shabbiness to their

(P02-058) [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]] just now.

(P02-059) The man was of fine figure, swarthy, and stern in aspect; and he showed

(P02-060) in profile a facial angle so slightly inclined as to be almost

(P02-061) perpendicular. He wore a short jacket of brown corduroy, newer than the

(P02-062) remainder of his suit, which was a fustian waistcoat with white horn

(P02-063) buttons, breeches of the same, tanned leggings, and a straw hat

(P02-064) overlaid with black glazed canvas. At his back he carried by a looped

(P02-065) strap a rush basket, from which protruded at one end the crutch of a

(P02-066) hay-knife, a wimble for hay-bonds being also visible in the aperture.

(P02-067) His measured, springless walk was the walk of the skilled countryman as

(P02-068) distinct from the desultory shamble of the general labourer; while in

(P02-069) the turn and plant of each foot there was, further, a dogged and

(P02-070) cynical indifference personal to himself, showing its presence even in

(P02-071) the regularly interchanging fustian folds, now in the left leg, now in

(P02-072) the right, as he paced along.

(P02-073) What was really peculiar, however, in this couple’s [[tradition-and-change|progress]], and would

(P02-074) have attracted the attention of any casual observer otherwise disposed

(P02-075) to overlook them, was the perfect silence they preserved. They walked

(P02-076) side by side in such a way as to suggest afar off the low, easy,

(P02-077) confidential chat of people full of reciprocity; but on closer view it

(P02-078) could be discerned that the man was reading, or pretending to read, a

(P02-079) ballad sheet which he kept before his eyes with some difficulty by the

(P02-080) hand that was passed through the basket strap. Whether this apparent

(P02-081) cause were the real cause, or whether it were an assumed one to escape

(P02-082) an intercourse that would have been irksome to him, nobody but himself

(P02-083) could have said precisely; but his taciturnity was unbroken, and the

(P02-084) woman enjoyed no society whatever from his presence. Virtually she

(P02-085) walked the highway alone, save for the child she bore. Sometimes the

(P02-086) man’s bent elbow almost touched her shoulder, for she kept as close to

(P02-087) his side as was possible without actual contact, but she seemed to have

(P02-088) no idea of taking his arm, nor he of offering it; and far from

(P02-089) exhibiting surprise at his ignoring silence she appeared to receive it

(P02-090) as a natural thing. If any word at all were uttered by the little

(P02-091) group, it was an occasional whisper of the woman to the child—a tiny

(P02-092) girl in short clothes and blue boots of knitted yarn—and the murmured

(P02-093) babble of the child in reply.

(P02-094) The chief—almost the only—attraction of the young woman’s face was its

(P02-095) mobility. When she looked down sideways to the girl she became pretty,

(P02-096) and even handsome, particularly that in the action her features caught

(P02-097) slantwise the rays of the strongly coloured sun, which made

(P02-098) transparencies of her eyelids and nostrils and set fire on her lips.

(P02-099) When she plodded on in the shade of the hedge, silently thinking, she

(P02-100) had the hard, half-apathetic expression of one who deems anything

(P02-101) possible at the hands of Time and Chance except, perhaps, fair play.

(P02-102) The first phase was the work of Nature, the second probably of

(P02-103) civilization.

(P02-104) That the man and woman were husband and wife, and the parents of the

(P02-105) girl in arms there could be little doubt. No other than such

(P02-106) relationship would have accounted for the atmosphere of stale

(P02-107) familiarity which the trio carried along with them like a nimbus as

(P02-108) they moved down the road.

(P02-109) The wife mostly kept her eyes fixed ahead, though with little

(P02-110) interest—the scene for that matter being one that might have been

(P02-111) matched at almost any spot in any county in England at this time of the

(P02-112) year; a road neither straight nor crooked, neither level nor hilly,

(P02-113) bordered by hedges, trees, and other vegetation, which had entered the

(P02-114) blackened-green stage of colour that the doomed leaves pass through on

(P02-115) their way to dingy, and yellow, and red. The grassy margin of the bank,

(P02-116) and the nearest hedgerow boughs, were powdered by the dust that had

(P02-117) been stirred over them by hasty vehicles, the same dust as it lay on

(P02-118) the road deadening their footfalls like a carpet; and this, with the

(P02-119) aforesaid total absence of conversation, allowed every extraneous sound

(P02-120) to be heard.

(P02-121) For a long time there was none, beyond the voice of a weak bird singing

(P02-122) a trite old evening song that might doubtless have been heard on the

(P02-123) hill at the same hour, and with the self-same trills, quavers, and

(P02-124) breves, at any sunset of that season for centuries untold. But as they

(P02-125) approached the village sundry distant shouts and rattles reached their

(P02-126) ears from some elevated spot in that direction, as yet screened from

(P02-127) view by foliage. When the outlying houses of Weydon-Priors could just

(P02-128) be described, the [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]] group was met by a turnip-hoer with his hoe on

(P02-129) his shoulder, and his dinner-bag suspended from it. The reader promptly

(P02-130) glanced up.

(P02-131) “Any trade doing here?” he asked phlegmatically, designating the

(P02-132) village in his van by a wave of the broadsheet. And thinking the

(P02-133) labourer did not understand him, he added, “Anything in the

(P02-134) hay-trussing line?”

(P02-135) The turnip-hoer had already begun shaking his head. “Why, save the man,

(P02-136) what wisdom’s in him that ’a should come to Weydon for a job of that

(P02-137) sort this time o’ year?”

(P02-138) “Then is there any house to let—a little small new cottage just a

(P02-139) builded, or such like?” asked the other.

(P02-140) The pessimist still maintained a negative. “Pulling down is more the

(P02-141) nater of Weydon. There were five houses cleared away last year, and

(P02-142) three this; and the volk nowhere to go—no, not so much as a thatched

(P02-143) hurdle; that’s the way o’ Weydon-Priors.”

(P02-144) The hay-trusser, which he obviously was, nodded with some

(P02-145) superciliousness. Looking towards the village, he continued, “There is

(P02-146) something going on here, however, is there not?”

(P02-147) “Ay. ’Tis Fair Day. Though what you hear now is little more than the

(P02-148) clatter and scurry of getting away the money o’ children and fools, for

(P02-149) the real business is done earlier than this. I’ve been working within

(P02-150) sound o’t all day, but I didn’t go up—not I. ’Twas no business of

(P02-151) mine.”

(P02-153) Fair-field, which showed standing-places and pens where many hundreds

(P02-154) of horses and sheep had been exhibited and sold in the forenoon, but

(P02-155) were now in great part taken away. At present, as their informant had

(P02-156) observed, but little real business remained on hand, the chief being

(P02-157) the sale by auction of a few inferior animals, that could not otherwise

(P02-158) be disposed of, and had been absolutely refused by the better [[class-and-social-order|class]] of

(P02-159) traders, who came and went early. Yet the crowd was denser now than

(P02-160) during the morning hours, the frivolous contingent of visitors,

(P02-161) including journeymen out for a holiday, a stray soldier or two come on

(P02-162) furlough, village shopkeepers, and the like, having latterly flocked

(P02-163) in; persons whose activities found a congenial field among the

(P02-164) peep-shows, toy-stands, waxworks, inspired monsters, disinterested

(P02-165) medical men who travelled for the public good, thimble-riggers,

(P02-166) nick-nack vendors, and readers of Fate.

(P02-167) Neither of our pedestrians had much heart for these things, and they

(P02-168) looked around for a refreshment tent among the many which dotted the

(P02-169) down. Two, which stood nearest to them in the ochreous haze of expiring

(P02-170) sunlight, seemed almost equally inviting. One was formed of new,

(P02-171) milk-hued canvas, and bore red flags on its summit; it announced “Good

(P02-172) Home-brewed Beer, Ale, and Cyder.” The other was less new; a little

(P02-173) iron stove-pipe came out of it at the back and in front appeared the

(P02-174) placard, “Good Furmity Sold Hear.” The man mentally weighed the two

(P02-175) inscriptions and inclined to the former tent.

(P02-176) “No—no—the other one,” said the woman. “I always like furmity; and so

(P02-177) does [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]; and so [[ambition-and-will|will]] you. It is nourishing after a long

(P02-178) hard day.”

(P02-179) “I’ve never tasted it,” said the man. However, he gave way to her

(P02-180) representations, and they entered the furmity booth forthwith.

(P02-181) A rather numerous company appeared within, seated at the long narrow

(P02-182) tables that ran down the tent on each side. At the upper end stood a

(P02-183) stove, containing a charcoal fire, over which hung a large three-legged

(P02-184) crock, sufficiently polished round the rim to show that it was made of

(P02-185) bell-metal. A haggish creature of about fifty presided, in a white

(P02-186) apron, which as it threw an air of [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]] over her as far as it

(P02-187) extended, was made so wide as to reach nearly round her waist. She

(P02-188) slowly stirred the contents of the pot. The dull scrape of her large

(P02-189) spoon was audible throughout the tent as she thus kept from burning the

(P02-190) mixture of corn in the grain, flour, milk, raisins, currants, and what

(P02-192) holding the separate ingredients stood on a white-clothed table of

(P02-193) boards and trestles close by.

(P02-194) The young man and woman ordered a basin each of the mixture, steaming

(P02-195) hot, and sat down to consume it at leisure. This was very well so far,

(P02-196) for furmity, as the woman had said, was nourishing, and as proper a

(P02-197) food as could be obtained within the four seas; though, to those not

(P02-198) accustomed to it, the grains of wheat swollen as large as lemon-pips,

(P02-199) which floated on its surface, might have a deterrent effect at first.

(P02-200) But there was more in that tent than met the cursory glance; and the

(P02-201) man, with the instinct of a perverse [[character-as-fate|character]], scented it quickly.

(P02-202) After a mincing attack on his bowl, he watched the hag’s proceedings

(P02-203) from the corner of his eye, and saw the game she played. He winked to

(P02-204) her, and passed up his basin in reply to her nod; when she took a

(P02-205) bottle from under the table, slily measured out a quantity of its

(P02-206) contents, and tipped the same into the man’s furmity. The liquor poured

(P02-207) in was rum. The man as slily sent back money in payment.

(P02-208) He found the concoction, thus strongly laced, much more to his

(P02-209) satisfaction than it had been in its natural state. His wife had

(P02-210) observed the proceeding with much uneasiness; but he persuaded her to

(P02-211) have hers laced also, and she agreed to a milder allowance after some

(P02-212) misgiving.

(P02-213) The man finished his basin, and called for another, the rum being

(P02-214) signalled for in yet stronger proportion. The effect of it was soon

(P02-215) apparent in his manner, and his wife but too sadly perceived that in

(P02-218) The child began to prattle impatiently, and [[susan-henchard|the wife]] more than once

(P02-219) said to her husband, “Michael, how about our lodging? You know we may

(P02-221) But he turned a deaf ear to those bird-like chirpings. He talked loud

(P02-223) gazes at the candles when they were lighted, fell together; then they

(P02-224) opened, then shut again, and she slept.

(P02-225) At the end of the first basin the man had risen to serenity; at the

(P02-226) second he was jovial; at the third, argumentative, at the fourth, the

(P02-229) conduct; he was overbearing—even brilliantly quarrelsome.
