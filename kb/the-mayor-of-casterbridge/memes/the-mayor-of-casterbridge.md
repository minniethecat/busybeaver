**The Mayor of Casterbridge: The Life and Death of a Man of Character** is a novel by Thomas Hardy, first published in 1886. Set in the fictional market-town of Casterbridge — Hardy's name for Dorchester — the novel traces the rise and fall of Michael Henchard, a hay-trusser who, in a moment of drunken despair, sells his wife and infant daughter at a country fair, then spends the rest of his life grappling with the consequences.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens at Weydon-Priors fair, where a young Michael Henchard, drunk on furmity laced with rum, auctions his wife Susan and daughter Elizabeth-Jane to a sailor named Richard Newson for five guineas (001-003). Eighteen years later, having sworn off alcohol and risen to become the prosperous Mayor of Casterbridge, Henchard finds his past returning when Susan and Elizabeth-Jane arrive in town seeking him (005-010). The narrative follows Henchard's subsequent financial ruin, his rivalry with the younger Donald Farfrae, the revelation of his past, and his ultimate isolation and death on Egdon Heath (056-001).

## Major Characters

- **Michael Henchard** — The protagonist, a man of powerful passions whose character flaws drive both his success and his destruction
- **Donald Farfrae** — A young Scotsman whose business acumen and personal charm make him Henchard's rival in commerce and love
- **Elizabeth-Jane Newson** — Henchard's stepdaughter, whose quiet strength and moral clarity provide the novel's emotional centre
- **Lucetta Templeman** — A woman from Jersey whose past relationship with Henchard complicates her marriage to Farfrae
- **Susan Henchard** — Henchard's wife, whose return sets the plot in motion

## Major Themes

The novel explores the tension between **character and fate** — Henchard's downfall is both self-inflicted and seemingly destined. Other major themes include the conflict between **tradition and change** (embodied in Henchard's old-world methods versus Farfrae's modern business practices), the role of **reputation and social standing** in a provincial community, and the moral weight of past actions.

## Structure and Style

The novel was originally published serially, and its tightly constructed plot reflects Hardy's mastery of narrative architecture. The prose combines naturalistic detail with classical tragic form — Henchard has been compared to figures from Greek tragedy and Shakespearean drama. Hardy's Wessex setting provides a richly textured backdrop, with Casterbridge itself functioning almost as a character.
