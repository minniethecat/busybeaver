Pride and its inevitable companion, humiliation, form one of the deepest thematic currents in *The Mayor of Casterbridge*. The novel traces a psychological arc in which Michael Henchard's fierce pride repeatedly collides with circumstances that expose and humiliate him — the wife-auction, his financial ruin, the public revelation of his past, and his final degradation as a labourer in his former employee's yard.

## Pride as a Tragic Flaw

Henchard's pride manifests from the novel's opening scene, where his wounded sense of self-worth at Weydon-Priors fair — he complains of being unable to find work and support his family — leads directly to the catastrophic wife-auction (001-003). This same pride, years later, prevents him from accepting Farfrae's help during his financial decline; he would rather risk ruin than appear dependent (009-032). Henchard's pride is not simple arrogance but a complex compound of self-reliance, sensitivity to social judgment, and an inability to bend — qualities that make him both admirable and self-destructive.

## The Spectacle of Public Humiliation

Hardy structures the novel around a series of public humiliations that strip Henchard of his standing. The skimmity-ride — a traditional rough-music ritual exposing Lucetta's past with Henchard — destroys not only Lucetta but also Henchard's last hope of social rehabilitation. The court-like scene in which the furmity-woman publicly reveals Henchard's past as a wife-seller before the very magistrates he once presided over (039-004) is perhaps the novel's most concentrated moment of dramatic irony and public shame.

## The Furmity-Woman as Agent of Fate

The furmity-woman who sold Henchard the spiked drink at Weydon-Priors reappears at the novel's midpoint as the instrument of his exposure. Her testimony before the magistrates — "I can prove it" — transforms Henchard from respected citizen to fallen man in a single scene. She functions as a living reminder that the past cannot be outrun in Hardy's moral universe.

## Pride Reformed: Henchard's Final Act

Henchard's last gesture — leaving a sealed will that demands no mourners, no bell, no flowers — represents a final, desperate assertion of pride in the face of total ruin (056-002). It is pride stripped of ambition, reduced to the bare wish to be forgotten. Elizabeth-Jane's discovery of the will after his death provides the novel's quiet moral close: the recognition that a man of fierce passions was, in the end, worthy of her tears.
