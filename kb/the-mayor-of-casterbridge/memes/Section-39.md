# Section 39

(038-001) “I have worked as a journeyman before now, ha’n’t I?” he would say in

(038-002) his defiant way; “and why shouldn’t I do it again?” But he looked a far

(038-003) different journeyman from the one he had been in his earlier days. Then

(038-004) he had worn clean, suitable clothes, light and cheerful in hue;

(038-005) leggings yellow as marigolds, corduroys immaculate as new flax, and a

(038-006) neckerchief like a flower-garden. Now he wore the remains of an old

(038-007) blue cloth suit of his gentlemanly times, a rusty silk hat, and a once

(038-008) black satin stock, soiled and shabby. Clad thus he went to and fro,

(038-009) still comparatively an active man—for he was not much over forty—and

(038-010) saw with the other men in the yard [[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]] going in and out the

(038-011) green door that led to the garden, and the big house, and [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]].

(038-012) At the beginning of the winter it was rumoured about [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] that

(038-013) Mr. Farfrae, already in the Town Council, was to be proposed for Mayor

(038-014) in a year or two.

(038-015) “Yes, she was wise, she was wise in her generation!” said [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to

(038-016) himself when he heard of this one day on his way to Farfrae’s hay-barn.

(038-017) He thought it over as he wimbled his bonds, and the piece of news acted

(038-018) as a reviviscent breath to that old view of his—of Donald Farfrae as

(038-019) his triumphant rival who rode rough-shod over him.

(038-020) “A fellow of his age going to be Mayor, indeed!” he murmured with a

(038-021) corner-drawn smile on his mouth. “But ’tis her money that floats en

(038-022) upward. Ha-ha—how cust odd it is! Here be I, his former master, working

(038-023) for him as man, and he the man standing as master, with my house and my

(038-024) furniture and my what-you-may-call wife all his own.”

(038-025) He repeated these things a hundred times a day. During the whole period

(038-026) of his acquaintance with Lucetta he had never wished to claim her as

(038-027) his own so desperately as he now regretted her loss. It was no

(038-028) mercenary hankering after her fortune that moved him, though that

(038-029) fortune had been the means of making her so much the more desired by

(038-030) giving her the air of independence and sauciness which attracts men of

(038-031) his composition. It had given her servants, house, and fine clothing—a

(038-032) setting that invested Lucetta with a startling novelty in the eyes of

(038-033) him who had known her in her narrow days.

(038-034) He accordingly lapsed into moodiness, and at every allusion to the

(038-035) possibility of Farfrae’s near election to the municipal chair his

(038-036) former hatred of the Scotchman returned. Concurrently with this he

(038-037) underwent a moral [[tradition-and-change|change]]. It resulted in his significantly saying every

(038-038) now and then, in tones of recklessness, “Only a fortnight more!”—“Only

(038-039) a dozen days!” and so forth, lessening his figures day by day.

(038-040) “Why d’ye say only a dozen days?” asked [[solomon-longways|Solomon Longways]] as he worked

(038-041) beside Henchard in the granary weighing oats.

(038-042) “Because in twelve days I shall be released from my oath.”

(038-043) “What oath?”

(038-044) “The oath to drink no spirituous liquid. In twelve days it [[ambition-and-will|will]] be

(038-045) twenty-one years since I swore it, and then I mean to enjoy myself,

(038-046) please God!”

(038-047) [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] sat at her window one Sunday, and while there she heard

(038-048) in the street below a conversation which introduced Henchard’s name.

(038-049) She was wondering what was the matter, when a third person who was

(038-050) passing by asked the question in her mind.

(038-051) “Michael Henchard have busted out drinking after taking nothing for

(038-052) twenty-one years!”

(038-053) Elizabeth-Jane jumped up, put on her things, and went out.

(038-055) At this date there prevailed in Casterbridge a convivial

(038-056) custom—scarcely recognized as such, yet none the less established. On

(038-057) the afternoon of every Sunday a large contingent of the Casterbridge

(038-058) journeymen—steady churchgoers and sedate characters—having attended

(038-059) service, filed from [[the-church|the church]] doors across the way to the Three

(038-060) Mariners Inn. The rear was usually brought up by the choir, with their

(038-061) bass-viols, fiddles, and flutes under their arms.

(038-062) The great point, the point of [[reputation-and-social-standing|honour]], on these sacred occasions was for

(038-063) each man to strictly limit himself to half-a-pint of liquor. This

(038-064) scrupulosity was so well understood by the landlord that the whole

(038-065) company was served in cups of that measure. They were all exactly

(038-066) alike—straight-sided, with two leafless lime-trees done in eel-brown on

(038-067) the sides—one towards the drinker’s lips, the other confronting his

(038-068) comrade. To wonder how many of these cups the landlord possessed

(038-069) altogether was a favourite exercise of children in the marvellous.

(038-070) Forty at least might have been seen at these times in the large room,

(038-071) forming a ring round the margin of the great sixteen-legged oak table,

(038-072) like the monolithic circle of Stonehenge in its pristine days. Outside

(038-073) and above the forty cups came a circle of forty smoke-jets from forty

(038-074) clay pipes; outside the pipes the countenances of the forty

(038-075) church-goers, supported at the back by a circle of forty chairs.

(038-076) The conversation was not the conversation of week-days, but a thing

(038-077) altogether finer in point and higher in tone. They invariably discussed

(038-078) the sermon, dissecting it, weighing it, as above or below the

(038-079) average—the general tendency being to regard it as a scientific feat or

(038-080) performance which had no relation to their own lives, except as between

(038-081) critics and the thing criticized. The bass-viol player and the clerk

(038-082) usually spoke with more [[power-and-authority|authority]] than the rest on account of their

(038-083) official connection with the preacher.

(038-084) Now the [[the-three-mariners|Three Mariners]] was the inn chosen by Henchard as the place for

(038-085) closing his long term of dramless years. He had so timed his entry as

(038-086) to be well established in the large room by the time the forty

(038-087) church-goers entered to their customary cups. The flush upon his face

(038-088) proclaimed at once that the vow of twenty-one years had lapsed, and the

(038-089) era of recklessness begun anew. He was seated on a small table, drawn

(038-090) up to the side of the massive oak board reserved for the churchmen, a

(038-091) few of whom nodded to him as they took their places and said, “How be

(038-092) ye, Mr. Henchard? Quite a stranger here.”

(038-093) Henchard did not take the trouble to reply for a few moments, and his

(038-094) eyes rested on his stretched-out legs and boots. “Yes,” he said at

(038-095) length; “that’s true. I’ve been down in spirit for weeks; some of ye

(038-096) know the cause. I am better now, but not quite serene. I want you

(038-097) fellows of the choir to strike up a tune; and what with that and this

(038-098) brew of Stannidge’s, I am in hopes of getting altogether out of my

(038-099) minor key.”

(038-100) “With all my heart,” said the first fiddle. “We’ve let back our

(038-101) strings, that’s true, but we can soon pull ’em up again. Sound A,

(038-102) neighbours, and give the man a stave.”

(038-103) “I don’t care a curse what the words be,” said Henchard. “Hymns,

(038-104) ballets, or rantipole rubbish; the Rogue’s March or the cherubim’s

(038-105) warble—’tis all the same to me if ’tis good harmony, and well put out.”

(038-106) “Well—heh, heh—it may be we can do that, and not a man among us that

(038-107) have sat in the gallery less than twenty year,” said the leader of the

(038-108) band. “As ’tis Sunday, neighbours, suppose we raise the Fourth Psa’am,

(038-109) to Samuel Wakely’s tune, as improved by me?”

(038-110) “Hang Samuel Wakely’s tune, as improved by thee!” said Henchard. “Chuck

(038-111) across one of your psalters—old Wiltshire is the only tune worth

(038-112) singing—the psalm-tune that would make my blood ebb and flow like the

(038-113) sea when I was a steady chap. I’ll find some words to fit en.” He took

(038-114) one of the psalters and began turning over the leaves.

(038-115) Chancing to look out of the window at that moment he saw a flock of

(038-116) people passing by, and perceived them to be the congregation of the

(038-117) upper church, now just dismissed, their sermon having been a longer one

(038-118) than that the lower parish was favoured with. Among the rest of the

(038-119) leading inhabitants walked Mr. Councillor Farfrae with Lucetta upon his

(038-120) arm, the observed and imitated of all the smaller tradesmen’s

(038-121) womankind. Henchard’s mouth changed a little, and he continued to turn

(038-122) over the leaves.

(038-123) “Now then,” he said, “Psalm the Hundred-and-Ninth, to the tune of

(038-124) Wiltshire: verses ten to fifteen. I gi’e ye the words:

(038-125) “His seed shall orphans be, his wife

(038-126) A widow plunged in [[regret-and-suffering|grief]];

(038-127) His vagrant children beg their bread

(038-128) Where none can give relief.

(038-129) His ill-got riches shall be made

(038-130) To usurers a prey;

(038-131) The fruit of all his toil shall be

(038-132) By strangers borne away.

(038-133) None shall be found that to his wants

(038-134) Their mercy will extend,

(038-135) Or to his helpless orphan seed

(038-136) The least assistance lend.

(038-137) A swift destruction soon shall seize

(038-138) On his unhappy race;

(038-139) And the next age his hated name

(038-140) Shall utterly deface.”

(038-141) “I know the Psa’am—I know the Psa’am!” said the leader hastily; “but I

(038-142) would as lief not sing it. ’Twasn’t made for singing. We chose it once

(038-143) when the gipsy stole the pa’son’s mare, thinking to please him, but

(038-144) pa’son were quite upset. Whatever Servant David were thinking about

(038-145) when he made a Psalm that nobody can sing without disgracing himself, I

(038-146) can’t fathom! Now then, the Fourth Psalm, to Samuel Wakely’s tune, as

(038-147) improved by me.”

(038-148) “’Od seize your sauce—I tell ye to sing the Hundred-and-Ninth to

(038-149) Wiltshire, and sing it you shall!” roared Henchard. “Not a single one

(038-150) of all the droning crew of ye goes out of this room till that Psalm is

(038-151) sung!” He slipped off the table, seized the poker, and going to the

(038-152) door placed his back against it. “Now then, go ahead, if you don’t wish

(038-153) to have your cust pates broke!”

(038-154) “Don’t ’ee, don’t ’ee take on so!—As ’tis the Sabbath-day, and ’tis

(038-155) Servant David’s words and not ours, perhaps we don’t mind for once,

(038-156) hey?” said one of the terrified choir, looking round upon the rest. So

(038-157) the instruments were tuned and the comminatory verses sung.

(038-158) “Thank ye, thank ye,” said Henchard in a softened voice, his eyes

(038-159) growing downcast, and his manner that of a man much moved by the

(038-160) strains. “Don’t you blame David,” he went on in low tones, shaking his

(038-161) head without raising his eyes. “He knew what he was about when he wrote

(038-162) that!... If I could afford it, be hanged if I wouldn’t keep a church

(038-163) choir at my own expense to play and sing to me at these low, dark times

(038-164) of my life. But the bitter thing is, that when I was rich I didn’t need

(038-165) what I could have, and now I be poor I can’t have what I need!”

(038-166) While they paused, Lucetta and Farfrae passed again, this time

(038-167) homeward, it being their custom to take, like others, a short walk out

(038-168) on the highway and back, between church and tea-time. “There’s the man

(038-169) we’ve been singing about,” said Henchard.

(038-170) The players and singers turned their heads and saw his meaning. “Heaven

(038-171) forbid!” said the bass-player.

(038-172) “’Tis the man,” repeated Henchard doggedly.

(038-173) “Then if I’d known,” said the performer on the clarionet solemnly,

(038-174) “that ’twas meant for a living man, nothing should have drawn out of my

(038-175) wynd-pipe the breath for that Psalm, so help me!”

(038-176) “Nor from mine,” said the first singer. “But, thought I, as it was made

(038-177) so long ago perhaps there isn’t much in it, so I’ll oblige a neighbour;

(038-178) for there’s nothing to be said against the tune.”
