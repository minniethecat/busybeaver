# The River

The river that runs through [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is more than a geographical feature — it is a constant symbolic presence. Water in Hardy is always ambivalent: it cleanses and it drowns, it sustains life and it takes it. The river is [[nature-and-landscape|nature]]'s indifference made visible, flowing through [[casterbridge|the town]] as though its human dramas were invisible.

## Description

Hardy's river is dark, deep, and patient. It flows beneath [[the-bridge|the bridge]] where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] contemplates suicide and past the weir where he nearly drowns. It is the novel's most consistent image of [[nature-and-landscape|nature]] as a force that neither judges nor intervenes — it simply is.

## Narrative Function

The river functions as a silent witness to the novel's events and as a symbol of the Naturalist vision. Characters come to its banks at moments of crisis, and the river offers neither comfort nor resistance — only the implacable continuation of its own existence.
