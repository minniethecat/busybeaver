# Michael Henchard

The protagonist of The Mayor of Casterbridge and one of Hardy's greatest tragic creations. Michael Henchard is a man of immense [[ambition-and-will|will]] and equally immense self-destructiveness — a figure whose rise from itinerant labourer to [[power-and-authority|mayor]] is as extraordinary as his fall is absolute. He is introduced at [[weydon-priors|Weydon-Priors]] fair, drunk and "out of [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] with the world," selling his wife and child for five guineas — an originating act that establishes the pattern of his life: [[temper-and-impulse|impulse]] followed by [[regret-and-suffering|remorse]], [[pride]] preventing repair, and the inexorable working-out of [[character-as-fate|character as destiny]].

## Character Overview

Henchard embodies what Hardy called "[[character-as-fate|character is fate]]." His virtues — [[ambition-and-will|will]], [[ambition-and-will|ambition]], generosity, physical strength — are inseparable from his flaws — [[pride]], [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], jealousy, [[temper-and-impulse|impulsiveness]]. Hardy presents him not as a villain but as a force of [[nature-and-landscape|nature]], a man whose "character knew no patience" and whose destruction is as inevitable as [[nature-and-landscape|weather]].

> "Henchard, and for him alone, she was fain to keep alive the flame which had so nearly gone out." [010-047]

His twenty-one-year [[henchards-abstinence-vow|abstinence vow]] — sworn in desperate [[regret-and-suffering|remorse]] after [[the-wife-auction|the wife-sale]] — transforms him from hay-trusser to prosperous corn-factor and [[mayor-of-casterbridge|Mayor of Casterbridge]]. But the same [[ambition-and-will|will]][[gender-and-power|power]] that [[ambition-and-will|drive]]s his rise cannot prevent his fall; it in fact accelerates it.

## Role in the Novel

Henchard's trajectory traces the full arc of Victorian social mobility in both directions. The opening at [[the-wife-auction|Weydon-Priors fair]] establishes him at his moral nadir; the twenty-one years that follow show a man attempting to rebuild himself through sheer force of [[ambition-and-will|will]]. His rivalry with [[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]] — the modern, methodical Scotchman whose "character was just the reverse of Henchard's" — is the novel's central conflict, mapping the displacement of old patriarchal [[power-and-authority|authority]] by modern commercial competence.

> "The face of Mr. Henchard beamed forth a satisfaction that was almost fierce in its strength." [010-091]

His fall is as systematic as his rise was improbable: the [[henchards-bankruptcy|bankruptcy]] that strips him of his bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess, the public revelation of his past by [[the-furmity-womans-revelation|the furmity-woman]], the death of [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]], and the final loss of [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] — each catastrophe follows the last with the logic of [[fate-and-determinism|tragic inevitability]].

## Character Analysis

Henchard is a study in the [[character-as-fate|tragedy of character]]. Unlike [[class-and-social-order|class]]ical tragic heroes brought down by [[fate-and-determinism|fate]] or the gods, Henchard is destroyed by himself — his own [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], his own [[pride]], his own inability to adapt to a changing world. Hardy's innovation is to locate tragedy not in external circumstance but in psychological necessity.

> "Henchard: 'Joshua Jopp, sir — by appointment — '" [010-125]
> " 'He's in his office,' said Henchard bluntly." [010-126]

The novel's design is symmetrical: Henchard rises from nothing through [[ambition-and-will|will]] and abstinence; he falls to nothing through the same [[ambition-and-will|will]], now turned to jealousy and self-destruction. His final [[ambition-and-will|will]] — demanding that no one remember him, that no stone mark his grave — is both an act of [[pride]] and an act of truth. He is, in the end, what he has made himself.
