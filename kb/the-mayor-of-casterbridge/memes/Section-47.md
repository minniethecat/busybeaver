# Section 47

(046-001) While leaning thus upon the parapet his listless attention was awakened

(046-002) by sounds of an unaccustomed kind from [[casterbridge|the town]] quarter. They were a

(046-003) confusion of rhythmical noises, to which the streets added yet more

(046-004) confusion by encumbering them with echoes. His first incurious thought

(046-005) that the clangour arose from the town band, engaged in an attempt to

(046-006) round off a memorable day in a burst of evening harmony, was

(046-007) contradicted by certain peculiarities of reverberation. But

(046-008) inexplicability did not rouse him to more than a cursory heed; his

(046-009) sense of degradation was too strong for the admission of foreign ideas;

(046-010) and he leant against the parapet as before.

(046-011) XXXIX.

(046-012) When [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] descended out of the loft breathless from his encounter

(046-013) with [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], he paused at the bottom to recover himself. He arrived

(046-014) at the yard with the intention of putting the horse into the gig

(046-015) himself (all the men having a holiday), and driving to a village on the

(046-016) [[budmouth|Budmouth]] Road. Despite the fearful struggle he decided still to

(046-017) persevere in his journey, so as to recover himself before going indoors

(046-018) and meeting the eyes of [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]. He wished to consider his course in a

(046-019) case so serious.

(046-020) When he was just on the point of driving off [[abel-whittle|Whittle]] arrived with a

(046-021) note badly addressed, and bearing the word “immediate” upon the

(046-022) outside. On opening it he was surprised to see that it was unsigned. It

(046-023) contained a brief request that he would go to [[weatherbury|Weatherbury]] that evening

(046-024) about some business which he was conducting there. Farfrae knew nothing

(046-025) that could make it pressing; but as he was bent upon going out he

(046-026) yielded to the anonymous request, particularly as he had a call to make

(046-027) at [[mellstock|Mellstock]] which could be included in the same tour. Thereupon he

(046-028) told Whittle of his [[tradition-and-change|change]] of direction, in words which Henchard had

(046-029) overheard, and set out on his way. Farfrae had not directed his man to

(046-030) take the message indoors, and Whittle had not been supposed to do so on

(046-031) his own responsibility.

(046-032) Now the anonymous letter was a well-intentioned but clumsy contrivance

(046-033) of [[solomon-longways|Longways]] and other of Farfrae’s men to get him out of the way for

(046-034) the evening, in order that the satirical mummery should fall flat, if

(046-035) it were attempted. By giving open information they would have brought

(046-036) down upon their heads the vengeance of those among their comrades who

(046-037) enjoyed these boisterous old games; and therefore the plan of sending a

(046-038) letter recommended itself by its indirectness.

(046-039) For poor Lucetta they took no protective measure, believing with the

(046-040) majority there was some [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] in the scandal, which she would have to

(046-041) bear as she best might.

(046-042) It was about eight o’clock, and Lucetta was sitting in the drawing-room

(046-043) alone. Night had set in for more than half an hour, but she had not had

(046-044) the candles lighted, for when Farfrae was away she preferred waiting

(046-045) for him by the firelight, and, if it were not too cold, keeping one of

(046-046) the window-sashes a little way open that the sound of his wheels might

(046-047) reach her ears early. She was leaning back in the chair, in a more

(046-048) hopeful mood than she had enjoyed since her [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]]. The day had been

(046-049) such a success, and the temporary uneasiness which Henchard’s show of

(046-050) effrontery had wrought in her disappeared with the quiet disappearance

(046-051) of Henchard himself under her husband’s reproof. The floating evidences

(046-052) of her absurd passion for him, and its consequences, had been

(046-053) destroyed, and she really seemed to have no cause for fear.

(046-054) The reverie in which these and other subjects mingled was disturbed by

(046-055) a hubbub in the distance, that increased moment by moment. It did not

(046-056) greatly surprise her, the afternoon having been given up to recreation

(046-057) by a majority of the populace since the passage of the Royal equipages.

(046-058) But her attention was at once riveted to the matter by the voice of a

(046-059) maid-servant next door, who spoke from an upper window across the

(046-060) street to some other maid even more elevated than she.

(046-061) “Which way be they going now?” inquired the first with interest.

(046-062) “I can’t be sure for a moment,” said the second, “because of the

(046-063) malter’s chimbley. O yes—I can see ’em. Well, I declare, I declare!”

(046-064) “What, what?” from the first, more enthusiastically.

(046-065) “They are coming up Corn Street after all! They sit back to back!”

(046-066) “What—two of ’em—are there two figures?”

(046-067) “Yes. Two images on a donkey, back to back, their elbows tied to one

(046-068) another’s! She’s facing the head, and he’s facing the tail.”

(046-069) “Is it meant for anybody in particular?”

(046-070) “Well—it mid be. The man has got on a blue coat and kerseymere

(046-071) leggings; he has black whiskers, and a reddish face. ’Tis a stuffed

(046-072) figure, with a falseface.”

(046-073) The din was increasing now—then it lessened a little.

(046-074) “There—I shan’t see, after all!” cried the disappointed first maid.

(046-075) “They have gone into a back street—that’s all,” said the one who

(046-076) occupied the enviable position in the attic. “There—now I have got ’em

(046-077) all endways nicely!”

(046-078) “What’s the woman like? Just say, and I can tell in a moment if ’tis

(046-079) meant for one I’ve in mind.”

(046-080) “My—why—’tis dressed just as _she_ was dressed when she sat in the

(046-081) front seat at the time the play-actors came to the Town Hall!”

(046-082) Lucetta started to her feet, and almost at the instant the door of the

(046-083) room was quickly and softly opened. [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] advanced into the

(046-084) firelight.

(046-085) “I have come to see you,” she said breathlessly. “I did not stop to

(046-086) knock—forgive me! I see you have not shut your shutters, and the window

(046-087) is open.”

(046-088) Without waiting for Lucetta’s reply she crossed quickly to the window

(046-089) and pulled out one of the shutters. Lucetta glided to her side. “Let it

(046-090) be—hush!” she said peremptorily, in a dry voice, while she seized

(046-091) Elizabeth-Jane by the hand, and held up her finger. Their intercourse

(046-092) had been so low and hurried that not a word had been lost of the

(046-093) conversation without, which had thus proceeded:—

(046-094) “Her neck is uncovered, and her hair in bands, and her back-comb in

(046-095) place; she’s got on a puce silk, and white stockings, and coloured

(046-096) shoes.”

(046-097) Again Elizabeth-Jane attempted to close the window, but Lucetta held

(046-098) her by main force.

(046-099) “’Tis me!” she said, with a face pale as death. “A procession—a

(046-100) scandal—an effigy of me, and him!”

(046-101) The look of Elizabeth betrayed that the latter knew it already.

(046-102) “Let us shut it out,” coaxed Elizabeth-Jane, noting that the rigid

(046-103) wildness of Lucetta’s features was growing yet more rigid and wild with

(046-104) the meaning of the noise and laughter. “Let us shut it out!”

(046-105) “It is of no use!” she shrieked. “He [[ambition-and-will|will]] see it, won’t he? Donald will

(046-106) see it! He is just coming home—and it will break his heart—he will

(046-107) never love me any more—and O, it will kill me—kill me!”

(046-108) Elizabeth-Jane was frantic now. “O, can’t something be done to stop

(046-109) it?” she cried. “Is there nobody to do it—not one?”

(046-110) She relinquished Lucetta’s hands, and ran to the door. Lucetta herself,

(046-111) saying recklessly “I will see it!” turned to the window, threw up the

(046-112) sash, and went out upon the balcony. Elizabeth immediately followed,

(046-113) and put her arm round her to pull her in. Lucetta’s eyes were straight

(046-114) upon the spectacle of the uncanny revel, now dancing rapidly. The

(046-115) numerous lights round the two effigies threw them up into lurid

(046-116) distinctness; it was impossible to mistake the pair for other than the

(046-117) intended victims.

(046-118) “Come in, come in,” implored Elizabeth; “and let me shut the window!”

(046-119) “She’s me—she’s me—even to the parasol—my green parasol!” cried Lucetta

(046-120) with a wild laugh as she stepped in. She stood motionless for one

(046-121) second—then fell heavily to the floor.

(046-122) Almost at the instant of her fall the rude music of the skimmington

(046-123) ceased. The roars of sarcastic laughter went off in ripples, and the

(046-124) trampling died out like the rustle of a spent wind. Elizabeth was only

(046-125) indirectly conscious of this; she had rung the bell, and was bending

(046-126) over Lucetta, who remained convulsed on the carpet in the paroxysms of

(046-127) an epileptic seizure. She rang again and again, in vain; the

(046-128) probability being that the servants had all run out of the house to see

(046-129) more of the Demoniac Sabbath than they could see within.

(046-130) At last Farfrae’s man, who had been agape on the doorstep, came up;

(046-131) then the cook. The shutters, hastily pushed to by Elizabeth, were quite

(046-132) closed, a light was obtained, Lucetta carried to her room, and the man

(046-133) sent off for a doctor. While Elizabeth was undressing her she recovered

(046-134) consciousness; but as soon as she remembered what had passed the fit

(046-135) returned.

(046-136) The doctor arrived with unhoped-for promptitude; he had been standing

(046-137) at his door, like others, wondering what the uproar meant. As soon as

(046-138) he saw the unhappy sufferer he said, in answer to Elizabeth’s mute

(046-139) appeal, “This is serious.”

(046-140) “It is a fit,” Elizabeth said.

(046-141) “Yes. But a fit in the present state of her health means mischief. You

(046-142) must send at once for Mr. Farfrae. Where is he?”

(046-143) “He has driven into the country, sir,” said the parlour-maid; “to some

(046-144) place on the Budmouth Road. He’s likely to be back soon.”

(046-145) “Never mind, he must be sent for, in case he should not hurry.” The

(046-146) doctor returned to the bedside again. The man was despatched, and they

(046-147) soon heard him clattering out of the yard at the back.
