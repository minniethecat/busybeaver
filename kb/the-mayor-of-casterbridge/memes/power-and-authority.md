# Power and Authority

Power in The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] operates at three levels: political (the mayor and town council), economic (control of the [[rural-economics|corn trade]]), and personal (the [[ambition-and-will|will]] to dominate those around you). [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] holds all three at his peak — and loses all three. Hardy's anatomy of [[gender-and-power|power]] suggests that authority, however absolute it appears, is always provisional.

## Overview

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[gender-and-power|power]] as mayor is the novel's central case study in authority — how it is acquired, exercised, and lost. His rise to the mayor's office represents the pinnacle of provincial [[gender-and-power|power]], but Hardy shows that political authority rests on economic success and personal [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], both of which [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] gambles away. Power in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is not a possession but a performance, and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s performance is ultimately unsustainable.

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **The mayor's authority**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s position gives him visible [[gender-and-power|power]] — he presides at public events, adjudicates disputes, and represents [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] to the wider world. But this authority is contingent on his commercial success and personal standing.
- **Economic [[gender-and-power|power]]**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s dominance of the [[rural-economics|corn trade]] is the material basis of his political [[gender-and-power|power]]. When [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] outcompetes him, political authority follows economic decline.
- **Personal domination**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[ambition-and-will|will]] to dominate — his employees, his [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]], his rival — is the psychological root of his approach to [[gender-and-power|power]]. But this personal style of authority generates resistance and resentment.
- **The [[farfrae-becomes-mayor|transfer of power]]**: [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s ascent to the mayor's office represents a generational transfer — from the [[temper-and-impulse|impulsive]], personal rule of [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to the methodical, modern governance of [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]].

## Literary Analysis

### Power as Performance

Hardy presents authority not as an inherent quality but as something that must be continually enacted. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s failure is partly a failure of performance — he cannot maintain the composed exterior that authority requires. Power is lost not only when it is challenged but when it ceases to be convincingly performed.

### The Naturalist View of Power

In keeping with Hardy's Naturalist vision, [[gender-and-power|power]] is not moral but mechanical. It operates according to predictable laws: economic [[gender-and-power|power]] begets political [[gender-and-power|power]]; personal authority requires social validation; all forms of [[gender-and-power|power]] are vulnerable to character flaws in those who wield them.
