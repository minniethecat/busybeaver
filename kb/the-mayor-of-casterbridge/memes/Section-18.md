# Section 18

(017-001) So [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] set about his preparations for a really brilliant

(017-002) thing—such as should be worthy of the venerable town. As for [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s

(017-003) little affair, Henchard nearly forgot it; except once now and then

(017-004) when, on it coming into his mind, he said to himself, “Charge admission

(017-005) at so much a head—just like a Scotchman!—who is going to pay anything a

(017-006) head?” The diversions which the Mayor intended to provide were to be

(017-007) entirely free.

(017-008) He had grown so dependent upon Donald that he could scarcely resist

(017-009) calling him in to consult. But by sheer self-coercion he refrained. No,

(017-010) he thought, Farfrae would be suggesting such improvements in his damned

(017-011) luminous way that in spite of himself he, Henchard, would sink to the

(017-012) position of second fiddle, and only scrape harmonies to his manager’s

(017-013) talents.

(017-014) Everybody applauded the Mayor’s proposed entertainment, especially when

(017-015) it became known that he meant to pay for it all himself.

(017-016) Close to [[casterbridge|the town]] was an elevated green spot surrounded by an ancient

(017-017) square earthwork—earthworks square and not square, were as common as

(017-018) blackberries hereabout—a spot whereon the Casterbridge people usually

(017-019) held any kind of merry-making, meeting, or sheep-fair that required

(017-020) more space than the streets would afford. On one side it sloped to the

(017-021) river Froom, and from any point a view was obtained of the country

(017-022) round for many miles. This pleasant upland was to be the scene of

(017-023) Henchard’s exploit.

(017-024) He advertised about the town, in long posters of a pink colour, that

(017-025) games of all sorts would take place here; and set to work a little

(017-026) battalion of men under his own eye. They erected greasy-poles for

(017-027) climbing, with smoked hams and local cheeses at the top. They placed

(017-028) hurdles in rows for jumping over; across [[the-river|the river]] they laid a slippery

(017-029) pole, with a live pig of the neighbourhood tied at the other end, to

(017-030) become the property of the man who could walk over and get it. There

(017-031) were also provided wheelbarrows for racing, donkeys for the same, a

(017-032) stage for boxing, wrestling, and drawing blood generally; sacks for

(017-033) jumping in. Moreover, not forgetting his principles, Henchard provided

(017-034) a mammoth tea, of which everybody who lived in the borough was invited

(017-035) to partake without payment. The tables were laid parallel with the

(017-036) inner slope of the rampart, and awnings were stretched overhead.

(017-037) Passing to and fro the Mayor beheld the unattractive exterior of

(017-038) Farfrae’s erection in the West Walk, rick-cloths of different sizes and

(017-039) colours being hung up to the arching trees without any regard to

(017-040) [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]]. He was easy in his mind now, for his own preparations far

(017-041) transcended these.

(017-042) The morning came. The sky, which had been remarkably clear down to

(017-043) within a day or two, was overcast, and the weather threatening, the

(017-044) wind having an unmistakable hint of water in it. Henchard wished he had

(017-045) not been quite so sure about the continuance of a fair season. But it

(017-046) was too late to modify or postpone, and the proceedings went on. At

(017-047) twelve o’clock the rain began to fall, small and steady, commencing and

(017-048) increasing so insensibly that it was difficult to state exactly when

(017-049) dry weather ended or wet established itself. In an hour the slight

(017-050) moisture resolved itself into a monotonous smiting of earth by heaven,

(017-051) in torrents to which no end could be prognosticated.

(017-052) A number of people had heroically gathered in the field but by three

(017-053) o’clock Henchard discerned that his project was doomed to end in

(017-054) failure. The hams at the top of the poles dripped watered smoke in the

(017-055) form of a brown liquor, the pig shivered in the wind, the grain of the

(017-056) deal tables showed through the sticking tablecloths, for the awning

(017-057) allowed the rain to drift under at its [[ambition-and-will|will]], and to enclose the sides

(017-058) at this hour seemed a useless undertaking. The landscape over the river

(017-059) disappeared; the wind played on the tent-cords in Æolian

(017-060) improvisations, and at length rose to such a pitch that the whole

(017-061) erection slanted to the ground those who had taken shelter within it

(017-062) having to crawl out on their hands and knees.

(017-063) But towards six the storm abated, and a drier breeze shook the moisture

(017-064) from the grass bents. It seemed possible to carry out the programme

(017-065) after all. The awning was set up again; the band was called out from

(017-066) its shelter, and ordered to begin, and where the tables had stood a

(017-067) place was cleared for dancing.

(017-068) “But where are the folk?” said Henchard, after the lapse of

(017-069) half-an-hour, during which time only two men and a woman had stood up

(017-070) to dance. “The shops are all shut. Why don’t they come?”

(017-071) “They are at Farfrae’s affair in the West Walk,” answered a Councilman

(017-072) who stood in the field with the Mayor.

(017-073) “A few, I suppose. But where are the body o’ ’em?”

(017-074) “All out of doors are there.”

(017-075) “Then the more fools they!”

(017-076) Henchard walked away moodily. One or two young fellows gallantly came

(017-077) to climb the poles, to save the hams from being wasted; but as there

(017-078) were no spectators, and the whole scene presented the most melancholy

(017-079) appearance Henchard gave orders that the proceedings were to be

(017-080) suspended, and the entertainment closed, the food to be distributed

(017-081) among the poor people of the town. In a short time nothing was left in

(017-082) the field but a few hurdles, the tents, and the poles.

(017-083) Henchard returned to his house, had tea with his wife and daughter, and

(017-084) then walked out. It was now dusk. He soon saw that the tendency of all

(017-085) promenaders was towards a particular spot in the Walks, and eventually

(017-086) proceeded thither himself. The notes of a stringed band came from the

(017-087) enclosure that Farfrae had erected—the pavilion as he called it—and

(017-088) when the Mayor reached it he perceived that a gigantic tent had been

(017-089) ingeniously constructed without poles or ropes. The densest point of

(017-090) the avenue of sycamores had been selected, where the boughs made a

(017-091) closely interlaced vault overhead; to these boughs the canvas had been

(017-092) hung, and a barrel roof was the result. The end towards the wind was

(017-093) enclosed, the other end was open. Henchard went round and saw the

(017-094) interior.

(017-095) In form it was like the nave of a cathedral with one gable removed, but

(017-096) the scene within was anything but devotional. A reel or fling of some

(017-097) sort was in [[tradition-and-change|progress]]; and the usually sedate Farfrae was in the midst

(017-098) of the other dancers in the costume of a wild Highlander, flinging

(017-099) himself about and spinning to the tune. For a moment Henchard could not

(017-100) help laughing. Then he perceived the immense admiration for the

(017-101) Scotchman that revealed itself in the [[gender-and-power|women]]’s faces; and when this

(017-102) exhibition was over, and a new dance proposed, and Donald had

(017-103) disappeared for a time to return in his natural garments, he had an

(017-104) unlimited choice of partners, every girl being in a coming-on

(017-105) disposition towards one who so thoroughly understood the poetry of

(017-106) motion as he.

(017-107) All the town crowded to the Walk, such a delightful idea of a ballroom

(017-108) never having occurred to the inhabitants before. Among the rest of the

(017-109) onlookers were Elizabeth and her mother—the former thoughtful yet much

(017-110) interested, her eyes beaming with a longing lingering light, as if

(017-111) Nature had been advised by Correggio in their creation. The dancing

(017-112) progressed with unabated spirit, and Henchard walked and waited till

(017-113) his wife should be disposed to go home. He did not care to keep in the

(017-114) light, and when he went into the dark it was worse, for there he heard

(017-115) remarks of a kind which were becoming too frequent:

(017-116) “Mr. Henchard’s rejoicings couldn’t say good morning to this,” said

(017-117) one. “A man must be a headstrong stunpoll to think folk would go up to

(017-118) that bleak place to-day.”

(017-119) The other answered that people said it was not only in such things as

(017-120) those that the Mayor was wanting. “Where would his business be if it

(017-121) were not for this young fellow? ’Twas verily Fortune sent him to

(017-122) Henchard. His accounts were like a bramblewood when Mr. Farfrae came.

(017-123) He used to reckon his sacks by chalk strokes all in a row like

(017-124) garden-palings, measure his ricks by stretching with his arms, weigh

(017-125) his trusses by a lift, judge his hay by a chaw, and settle the price

(017-126) with a curse. But now this accomplished young man does it all by

(017-127) ciphering and mensuration. Then the wheat—that sometimes used to taste

(017-128) so strong o’ mice when made into bread that people could fairly tell

(017-129) the breed—Farfrae has a plan for purifying, so that nobody would dream

(017-130) the smallest four-legged beast had walked over it once. O yes,

(017-131) everybody is full of him, and the care Mr. Henchard has to keep him, to

(017-132) be sure!” concluded this gentleman.

(017-133) “But he won’t do it for long, good-now,” said the other.

(017-134) “No!” said Henchard to himself behind the tree. “Or if he do, he’ll be

(017-135) honeycombed clean out of all the [[character-as-fate|character]] and standing that he’s built

(017-136) up in these eighteen year!”

(017-137) He went back to the dancing pavilion. Farfrae was footing a quaint

(017-138) little dance with [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]—an old country thing, the only one she

(017-139) knew, and though he considerately toned down his movements to suit her

(017-140) demurer gait, the pattern of the shining little nails in the soles of

(017-141) his boots became familiar to the eyes of every bystander. The tune had

(017-142) enticed her into it; being a tune of a busy, vaulting, leaping

(017-143) sort—some low notes on the silver string of each fiddle, then a

(017-144) skipping on the small, like running up and down ladders—“Miss M’Leod of

(017-145) Ayr” was its name, so Mr. Farfrae had said, and that it was very

(017-146) popular in his own country.

(017-147) It was soon over, and the girl looked at Henchard for approval; but he

(017-148) did not give it. He seemed not to see her. “Look here, Farfrae,” he

(017-149) said, like one whose mind was elsewhere, “I’ll go to Port-Bredy Great

(017-150) Market to-morrow myself. You can stay and put things right in your

(017-151) clothes-box, and recover strength to your knees after your vagaries.”

(017-152) He planted on Donald an antagonistic glare that had begun as a smile.

(017-153) Some other townsmen came up, and Donald drew aside. “What’s this,

(017-154) Henchard,” said Alderman Tubber, applying his thumb to the corn-factor

(017-155) like a cheese-taster. “An opposition randy to yours, eh? Jack’s as good

(017-156) as his master, eh? Cut ye out quite, hasn’t he?”

(017-157) “You see, Mr. Henchard,” said the lawyer, another goodnatured friend,

(017-158) “where you made the mistake was in going so far afield. You should have

(017-159) taken a leaf out of his book, and have had your sports in a sheltered

(017-160) place like this. But you didn’t think of it, you see; and he did, and

(017-161) that’s where he’s beat you.”

(017-162) “He’ll be top-sawyer soon of you two, and carry all afore him,” added

(017-163) jocular Mr. Tubber.

(017-164) “No,” said Henchard gloomily. “He won’t be that, because he’s shortly

(017-165) going to leave me.” He looked towards Donald, who had come near. “Mr.

(017-166) Farfrae’s time as my manager is drawing to a close—isn’t it, Farfrae?”

(017-167) The young man, who could now read the lines and folds of Henchard’s

(017-168) strongly-traced face as if they were clear verbal inscriptions, quietly

(017-169) assented; and when people deplored the fact, and asked why it was, he

(017-170) simply replied that Mr. Henchard no longer required his help.

(017-171) Henchard went home, apparently satisfied. But in the morning, when his

(017-172) jealous [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] had passed away, his heart sank within him at what he

(017-173) had said and done. He was the more disturbed when he found that this

(017-174) time Farfrae was determined to take him at his word.
