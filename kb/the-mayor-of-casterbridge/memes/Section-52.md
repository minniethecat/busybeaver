# Section 52

(051-001) There was nothing secret in [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]’s movements beyond what

(051-002) habitual reserve induced, and it may at once be owned on her account

(051-003) that she was guilty of occasional conversations with Donald when they

(051-004) chanced to meet. Whatever the origin of her walks on the [[budmouth|Budmouth]] Road,

(051-005) her return from those walks was often coincident with [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s

(051-006) emergence from Corn Street for a twenty minutes’ blow on that rather

(051-007) windy highway—just to winnow the seeds and chaff out of him before

(051-008) sitting down to tea, as he said. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] became aware of this by going

(051-009) to [[the-ring|the Ring]], and, screened by its enclosure, keeping his eye upon the

(051-010) road till he saw them meet. His face assumed an expression of extreme

(051-011) anguish.

(051-012) “Of her, too, he means to rob me!” he whispered. “But he has the right.

(051-013) I do not wish to interfere.”

(051-014) The meeting, in [[appearance-and-reality|truth]], was of a very innocent kind, and matters were by

(051-015) no means so far advanced between the young people as Henchard’s jealous

(051-016) [[regret-and-suffering|grief]] inferred. Could he have heard such conversation as passed he

(051-017) would have been enlightened thus much:—

(051-018) _He_.—“You like walking this way, Miss Henchard—and is it not so?”

(051-019) (uttered in his undulatory accents, and with an appraising, pondering

(051-020) gaze at her).

(051-021) _She_.—“O yes. I have chosen this road latterly. I have no great reason

(051-022) for it.”

(051-023) _He_.—“But that may make a reason for others.”

(051-024) _She_ (reddening).—“I don’t know that. My reason, however, such as it

(051-025) is, is that I wish to get a glimpse of the sea every day.”

(051-026) _He_.—“Is it a secret why?”

(051-027) _She_ ( reluctantly ).—“Yes.”

(051-028) _He_ (with the pathos of one of his native ballads).—“Ah, I doubt there

(051-029) [[ambition-and-will|will]] be any good in secrets! A secret cast a deep shadow over my life.

(051-030) And well you know what it was.”

(051-031) Elizabeth admitted that she did, but she refrained from confessing why

(051-032) the sea attracted her. She could not herself account for it fully, not

(051-033) knowing the secret possibly to be that, in addition to early marine

(051-034) associations, her blood was a sailor’s.

(051-035) “Thank you for those new books, Mr. Farfrae,” she added shyly. “I

(051-036) wonder if I ought to accept so many!”

(051-037) “Ay! why not? It gives me more pleasure to get them for you, than you

(051-038) to have them!”

(051-039) “It cannot.”

(051-040) They proceeded along the road together till they reached [[casterbridge|the town]], and

(051-041) their paths diverged.

(051-042) Henchard vowed that he would leave them to their own devices, put

(051-043) nothing in the way of their courses, whatever they might mean. If he

(051-044) were doomed to be bereft of her, so it must be. In the situation which

(051-045) their [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]] would create he could see no _locus standi_ for himself

(051-046) at all. Farfrae would never recognize him more than superciliously; his

(051-047) poverty ensured that, no less than his past conduct. And so Elizabeth

(051-048) would grow to be a stranger to him, and the end of his life would be

(051-049) friendless solitude.

(051-050) With such a possibility impending he could not help watchfulness.

(051-051) Indeed, within certain lines, he had the right to keep an eye upon her

(051-052) as his charge. The meetings seemed to become matters of course with

(051-053) them on special days of the week.

(051-054) At last full proof was given him. He was standing behind a wall close

(051-055) to the place at which Farfrae encountered her. He heard the young man

(051-056) address her as “Dearest Elizabeth-Jane,” and then kiss her, the girl

(051-057) looking quickly round to assure herself that nobody was near.

(051-058) When they were gone their way Henchard came out from the wall, and

(051-059) mournfully followed them to Casterbridge. The chief looming trouble in

(051-060) this engagement had not decreased. Both Farfrae and Elizabeth-Jane,

(051-061) unlike the rest of the people, must suppose Elizabeth to be his actual

(051-062) daughter, from his own assertion while he himself had the same belief;

(051-063) and though Farfrae must have so far forgiven him as to have no

(051-064) objection to own him as a father-in-law, intimate they could never be.

(051-065) Thus would the girl, who was his only friend, be withdrawn from him by

(051-066) degrees through her husband’s influence, and learn to despise him.

(051-067) Had she lost her heart to any other man in the world than the one he

(051-068) had rivalled, cursed, wrestled with for life in days before his spirit

(051-069) was broken, Henchard would have said, “I am content.” But content with

(051-070) the prospect as now depicted was hard to acquire.

(051-071) There is an outer chamber of the brain in which thoughts unowned,

(051-072) unsolicited, and of noxious kind, are sometimes allowed to wander for a

(051-073) moment prior to being sent off whence they came. One of these thoughts

(051-074) sailed into Henchard’s ken now.

(051-075) Suppose he were to communicate to Farfrae the fact that his betrothed

(051-076) was not the child of Michael Henchard at all—legally, nobody’s child;

(051-077) how would that correct and leading townsman receive the information? He

(051-078) might possibly forsake Elizabeth-Jane, and then she would be her

(051-079) step-sire’s own again.

(051-080) Henchard shuddered, and exclaimed, “God forbid such a thing! Why should

(051-081) I still be subject to these visitations of the devil, when I try so

(051-082) hard to keep him away?”

(051-083) XLIII.

(051-084) What Henchard saw thus early was, naturally enough, seen at a little

(051-085) later date by other people. That Mr. Farfrae “walked with that bankrupt

(051-086) Henchard’s stepdaughter, of all [[gender-and-power|women]],” became a common topic in the

(051-087) town, the simple perambulating term being used hereabout to signify a

(051-088) wooing; and the nineteen superior young ladies of Casterbridge, who had

(051-089) each looked upon herself as the only woman capable of making the

(051-090) merchant Councilman happy, indignantly left off going to [[the-church|the church]]

(051-091) Farfrae attended, left off conscious mannerisms, left off putting him

(051-092) in their prayers at night amongst their blood relations; in short,

(051-093) reverted to their normal courses.

(051-094) Perhaps the only inhabitants of the town to whom this looming choice of

(051-095) the Scotchman’s gave unmixed satisfaction were the members of the

(051-096) philosophic party, which included [[solomon-longways|Longways]], [[christopher-coney|Christopher Coney]], Billy

(051-097) Wills, Mr. Buzzford, and the like. [[the-three-mariners|The Three Mariners]] having been,

(051-098) years before, the house in which they had witnessed the young man and

(051-099) woman’s first and humble appearance on the Casterbridge stage, they

(051-100) took a kindly interest in their career, not unconnected, perhaps, with

(051-101) visions of festive treatment at their hands hereafter. Mrs. Stannidge,

(051-102) having rolled into the large parlour one evening and said that it was a

(051-103) wonder such a man as Mr. Farfrae, “a pillow of the town,” who might

(051-104) have chosen one of the daughters of the professional men or private

(051-105) residents, should stoop so low, Coney ventured to disagree with her.

(051-106) “No, ma’am, no wonder at all. ’Tis she that’s a stooping to he—that’s

(051-107) my opinion. A widow man—whose first wife was no credit to him—what is

(051-108) it for a young perusing woman that’s her own mistress and well liked?

(051-109) But as a neat patching up of things I see much good in it. When a man

(051-110) have put up a tomb of best marble-stone to the other one, as he’ve

(051-111) done, and weeped his fill, and thought it all over, and said to

(051-112) hisself, ‘T’other took me in, I knowed this one first; she’s a sensible

(051-113) piece for a partner, and there’s no faithful woman in high life

(051-114) now’;—well, he may do worse than not to take her, if she’s

(051-115) tender-inclined.”

(051-116) Thus they talked at the Mariners. But we must guard against a too

(051-117) liberal use of the conventional declaration that a great sensation was

(051-118) caused by the prospective event, that all the gossips’ tongues were set

(051-119) wagging thereby, and so-on, even though such a declaration might lend

(051-120) some eclat to the career of our poor only heroine. When all has been

(051-121) said about busy rumourers, a superficial and temporary thing is the

(051-122) interest of anybody in affairs which do not directly touch them. It

(051-123) would be a truer representation to say that Casterbridge (ever

(051-124) excepting the nineteen young ladies) looked up for a moment at the

(051-125) news, and withdrawing its attention, went on labouring and victualling,

(051-126) bringing up its children, and burying its dead, without caring a tittle

(051-127) for Farfrae’s domestic plans.

(051-128) Not a hint of the matter was thrown out to her stepfather by Elizabeth

(051-129) herself or by Farfrae either. Reasoning on the cause of their reticence

(051-130) he concluded that, estimating him by his past, the throbbing pair were

(051-131) afraid to broach the subject, and looked upon him as an irksome

(051-132) obstacle whom they would be heartily glad to get out of the way.

(051-133) Embittered as he was against society, this moody view of himself took

(051-134) deeper and deeper hold of Henchard, till the daily necessity of facing

(051-135) mankind, and of them particularly Elizabeth-Jane, became well-nigh more

(051-136) than he could endure. His health declined; he became morbidly

(051-137) sensitive. He wished he could escape those who did not want him, and

(051-138) hide his head for ever.

(051-139) But what if he were mistaken in his views, and there were no necessity

(051-140) that his own absolute separation from her should be involved in the

(051-141) incident of her marriage?

(051-142) He proceeded to draw a picture of the alternative—himself living like a

(051-143) fangless lion about the back rooms of a house in which his stepdaughter

(051-144) was mistress, an inoffensive old man, tenderly smiled on by Elizabeth,

(051-145) and good-naturedly tolerated by her husband. It was terrible to his

(051-146) [[pride]] to think of descending so low; and yet, for the girl’s sake he

(051-147) might put up with anything; even from Farfrae; even snubbings and

(051-148) masterful tongue-scourgings. The privilege of being in the house she

(051-149) occupied would almost outweigh the personal humiliation.

(051-150) Whether this were a dim possibility or the reverse, the courtship—which

(051-151) it evidently now was—had an absorbing interest for him.

(051-152) Elizabeth, as has been said, often took her walks on the Budmouth Road,

(051-153) and Farfrae as often made it convenient to create an accidental meeting

(051-154) with her there. Two miles out, a quarter of a mile from the highway,

(051-155) was the prehistoric fort called Mai Dun, of huge dimensions and many

(051-156) ramparts, within or upon whose enclosures a human being as seen from

(051-157) the road, was but an insignificant speck. Hitherward Henchard often

(051-158) resorted, glass in hand, and scanned the hedgeless _Via_—for it was the

(051-159) original track laid out by the legions of the Empire—to a distance of

(051-160) two or three miles, his object being to read the [[tradition-and-change|progress]] of affairs

(051-161) between Farfrae and his charmer.

(051-162) One day Henchard was at this spot when a masculine figure came along

(051-163) the road from Budmouth, and lingered. Applying his telescope to his eye

(051-164) Henchard expected that Farfrae’s features would be disclosed as usual.

(051-165) But the lenses revealed that today the man was not Elizabeth-Jane’s

(051-166) lover.

(051-167) It was one clothed as a merchant captain, and as he turned in the

(051-168) scrutiny of the road he revealed his face. Henchard lived a lifetime

(051-169) the moment he saw it. The face was [[richard-newson|Newson]]’s.

(051-170) Henchard dropped the glass, and for some seconds made no other

(051-171) movement. Newson waited, and Henchard waited—if that could be called a

(051-172) waiting which was a transfixture. But Elizabeth-Jane did not come.

(051-173) Something or other had caused her to neglect her customary walk that

(051-174) day. Perhaps Farfrae and she had chosen another road for variety’s

(051-175) sake. But what did that amount to? She might be here to-morrow, and in

(051-176) any case Newson, if bent on a private meeting and a revelation of the

(051-177) truth to her, would soon make his opportunity.

(051-178) Then he would tell her not only of his paternity, but of the ruse by

(051-179) which he had been once sent away. Elizabeth’s strict nature would cause

(051-180) her for the first time to despise her stepfather, would root out his

(051-181) image as that of an arch-deceiver, and Newson would reign in her heart

(051-182) in his stead.

(051-183) But Newson did not see anything of her that morning. Having stood still

(051-184) awhile he at last retraced his steps, and Henchard felt like a

(051-185) condemned man who has a few hours’ respite. When he reached his own

(051-186) house he found her there.
