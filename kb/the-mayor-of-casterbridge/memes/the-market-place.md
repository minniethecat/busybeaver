# The Market-Place

The beating heart of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] — a space where agricultural commerce, town [[power-and-authority|governance]], and social display intersect. The market-place is where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[power-and-authority|authority]] as [[power-and-authority|mayor]] is most visible and most vulnerable, where [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]]s are made in public and destroyed in public.

## Description

Hardy's market-place is a space of "the moneyed [[class-and-social-order|class]]" (028-045) conducting bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess alongside agricultural workers selling produce. It is democratic in its accessibility and hierarchical in its organization — a microcosm of [[casterbridge|the town]]'s [[class-and-social-order|social order]] rendered in spatial form.

## Narrative Function

The market-place is the novel's primary stage for public life. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[power-and-authority|authority]] is enacted here; [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s commercial ascent is visible here; [[casterbridge|the town]]'s judgment is formed here. It is the space where private character becomes public [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], with all the risk that entails.
