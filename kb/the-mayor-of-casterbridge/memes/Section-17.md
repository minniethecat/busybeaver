# Section 17

(016-001) Now there was good reason for this command on [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]’s part. Poor

(016-002) Abel, as he was called, had an inveterate habit of over-sleeping

(016-003) himself and coming late to his work. His anxious [[ambition-and-will|will]] was to be among

(016-004) the earliest; but if his comrades omitted to pull the string that he

(016-005) always tied round his great toe and left hanging out the window for

(016-006) that purpose, his will was as wind. He did not arrive in time.

(016-007) As he was often second hand at the hay-weighing, or at the crane which

(016-008) lifted the sacks, or was one of those who had to accompany the waggons

(016-009) into the country to fetch away stacks that had been purchased, this

(016-010) affliction of Abel’s was productive of much inconvenience. For two

(016-011) mornings in the present week he had kept the others waiting nearly an

(016-012) hour; hence Henchard’s threat. It now remained to be seen what would

(016-013) happen to-morrow.

(016-014) Six o’clock struck, and there was no [[abel-whittle|Whittle]]. At half-past six Henchard

(016-015) entered the yard; the waggon was horsed that Abel was to accompany; and

(016-016) the other man had been waiting twenty minutes. Then Henchard swore, and

(016-017) Whittle coming up breathless at that instant, the corn-factor turned on

(016-018) him, and declared with an oath that this was the last time; that if he

(016-019) were behind once more, by God, he would come and drag him out o’ bed.

(016-020) “There is sommit wrong in my make, your worshipful!” said Abel,

(016-021) “especially in the inside, whereas my poor dumb brain gets as dead as a

(016-022) clot afore I’ve said my few scrags of prayers. Yes—it came on as a

(016-023) stripling, just afore I’d got man’s wages, whereas I never enjoy my bed

(016-024) at all, for no sooner do I lie down than I be asleep, and afore I be

(016-025) awake I be up. I’ve fretted my gizzard green about it, maister, but

(016-026) what can I do? Now last night, afore I went to bed, I only had a

(016-027) scantling o’ cheese and—”

(016-028) “I don’t want to hear it!” roared Henchard. “To-morrow the waggons must

(016-029) start at four, and if you’re not here, stand clear. I’ll mortify thy

(016-030) flesh for thee!”

(016-031) “But let me clear up my points, your worshipful——”

(016-032) Henchard turned away.

(016-033) “He asked me and he questioned me, and then ’a wouldn’t hear my

(016-034) points!” said Abel, to the yard in general. “Now, I shall twitch like a

(016-035) moment-hand all night to-night for fear o’ him!”

(016-036) The journey to be taken by the waggons next day was a long one into

(016-037) Blackmoor Vale, and at four o’clock lanterns were moving about the

(016-038) yard. But Abel was missing. Before either of the other men could run to

(016-039) Abel’s and warn him Henchard appeared in the garden doorway. “Where’s

(016-040) Abel Whittle? Not come after all I’ve said? Now I’ll carry out my word,

(016-041) by my blessed fathers—nothing else will do him any good! I’m going up

(016-042) that way.”

(016-043) Henchard went off, entered Abel’s house, a little cottage in Back

(016-044) Street, the door of which was never locked because the inmates had

(016-045) nothing to lose. Reaching Whittle’s bedside the corn-factor shouted a

(016-046) bass note so vigorously that Abel started up instantly, and beholding

(016-047) Henchard standing over him, was galvanized into spasmodic movements

(016-048) which had not much relation to getting on his clothes.

(016-049) “Out of bed, sir, and off to the granary, or you leave my employ

(016-050) to-day! ’Tis to teach ye a lesson. March on; never mind your breeches!”

(016-051) The unhappy Whittle threw on his sleeve waistcoat, and managed to get

(016-052) into his boots at the bottom of the stairs, while Henchard thrust his

(016-053) hat over his head. Whittle then trotted on down Back Street, Henchard

(016-054) walking sternly behind.

(016-055) Just at this time [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], who had been to Henchard’s house to look for

(016-056) him, came out of the back gate, and saw something white fluttering in

(016-057) the morning gloom, which he soon perceived to be part of Abel’s shirt

(016-058) that showed below his waistcoat.

(016-059) “For maircy’s sake, what object’s this?” said Farfrae, following Abel

(016-060) into the yard, Henchard being some way in the rear by this time.

(016-061) “Ye see, Mr. Farfrae,” gibbered Abel with a resigned smile of terror,

(016-062) “he said he’d mortify my flesh if so be I didn’t get up sooner, and now

(016-063) he’s a-doing on’t! Ye see it can’t be helped, Mr. Farfrae; things do

(016-064) happen queer sometimes! Yes—I’ll go to Blackmoor Vale half naked as I

(016-065) be, since he do command; but I shall kill myself afterwards; I can’t

(016-066) outlive the disgrace, for the [[gender-and-power|women]]-folk will be looking out of their

(016-067) winders at my mortification all the way along, and laughing me to scorn

(016-068) as a man ’ithout breeches! You know how I feel such things, Maister

(016-069) Farfrae, and how forlorn thoughts get hold upon me. Yes—I shall do

(016-070) myself harm—I feel it coming on!”

(016-071) “Get back home, and slip on your breeches, and come to wark like a man!

(016-072) If ye go not, you’ll ha’e your death standing there!”

(016-073) “I’m afeard I mustn’t! Mr. Henchard said——”

(016-074) “I don’t care what Mr. Henchard said, nor anybody else! ’Tis simple

(016-075) foolishness to do this. Go and dress yourself instantly Whittle.”

(016-076) “Hullo, hullo!” said Henchard, coming up behind. “Who’s sending him

(016-077) back?”

(016-078) All the men looked towards Farfrae.

(016-079) “I am,” said Donald. “I say this joke has been carried far enough.”

(016-080) “And I say it hasn’t! Get up in the waggon, Whittle.”

(016-081) “Not if I am manager,” said Farfrae. “He either goes home, or I march

(016-082) out of this yard for good.”

(016-083) Henchard looked at him with a face stern and red. But he paused for a

(016-084) moment, and their eyes met. Donald went up to him, for he saw in

(016-085) Henchard’s look that he began to [[regret-and-suffering|regret]] this.

(016-086) “Come,” said Donald quietly, “a man o’ your position should ken better,

(016-087) sir! It is tyrannical and no worthy of you.”

(016-088) “’Tis not tyrannical!” murmured Henchard, like a sullen boy. “It is to

(016-089) make him remember!” He presently added, in a tone of one bitterly hurt:

(016-090) “Why did you speak to me before them like that, Farfrae? You might have

(016-091) stopped till we were alone. Ah—I know why! I’ve told ye the secret o’

(016-092) my life—fool that I was to do’t—and you take advantage of me!”

(016-093) “I had forgot it,” said Farfrae simply.

(016-094) Henchard looked on the ground, said nothing more, and turned away.

(016-095) During the day Farfrae learnt from the men that Henchard had kept

(016-096) Abel’s old mother in coals and snuff all the previous winter, which

(016-097) made him less antagonistic to the corn-factor. But Henchard continued

(016-098) moody and silent, and when one of the men inquired of him if some oats

(016-099) should be hoisted to an upper floor or not, he said shortly, “Ask Mr.

(016-100) Farfrae. He’s master here!”

(016-101) Morally he was; there could be no doubt of it. Henchard, who had

(016-102) hitherto been the most admired man in his circle, was the most admired

(016-103) no longer. One day the daughters of a deceased farmer in [[durnover|Durnover]]

(016-104) wanted an opinion of the value of their haystack, and sent a messenger

(016-105) to ask Mr. Farfrae to oblige them with one. The messenger, who was a

(016-106) child, met in the yard not Farfrae, but Henchard.

(016-107) “Very well,” he said. “I’ll come.”

(016-108) “But please will Mr. Farfrae come?” said the child.

(016-109) “I am going that way.... Why Mr. Farfrae?” said Henchard, with the

(016-110) fixed look of thought. “Why do people always want Mr. Farfrae?”

(016-111) “I suppose because they like him so—that’s what they say.”

(016-112) “Oh—I see—that’s what they say—hey? They like him because he’s cleverer

(016-113) than Mr. Henchard, and because he knows more; and, in short, Mr.

(016-114) Henchard can’t hold a candle to him—hey?”

(016-115) “Yes—that’s just it, sir—some of it.”

(016-116) “Oh, there’s more? Of course there’s more! What besides? Come, here’s a

(016-117) sixpence for a fairing.”

(016-118) “‘And he’s better tempered, and Henchard’s a fool to him,’ they say.

(016-119) And when some of the women were a-walking home they said, ‘He’s a

(016-120) diment—he’s a chap o’ wax—he’s the best—he’s the horse for my money,’

(016-121) says they. And they said, ‘He’s the most understanding man o’ them two

(016-122) by long chalks. I wish he was the master instead of Henchard,’ they

(016-123) said.”

(016-124) “They’ll talk any nonsense,” Henchard replied with covered gloom.

(016-125) “Well, you can go now. And _I_ am coming to value the hay, d’ye

(016-126) hear?—I.” The boy departed, and Henchard murmured, “Wish he were master

(016-127) here, do they?”

(016-128) He went towards Durnover. On his way he overtook Farfrae. They walked

(016-129) on together, Henchard looking mostly on the ground.

(016-130) “You’re no yoursel’ the day?” Donald inquired.

(016-131) “Yes, I am very well,” said Henchard.

(016-132) “But ye are a bit down—surely ye are down? Why, there’s nothing to be

(016-133) angry about! ’Tis splendid stuff that we’ve got from Blackmoor Vale. By

(016-134) the by, the people in Durnover want their hay valued.”

(016-135) “Yes. I am going there.”

(016-136) “I’ll go with ye.”

(016-137) As Henchard did not reply Donald practised a piece of music _sotto

(016-138) voce_, till, getting near the bereaved people’s door, he stopped

(016-139) himself with—

(016-140) “Ah, as their father is dead I won’t go on with such as that. How could

(016-141) I forget?”

(016-142) “Do you care so very much about hurting folks’ feelings?” observed

(016-143) Henchard with a half sneer. “You do, I know—especially mine!”

(016-144) “I am sorry if I have hurt yours, sir,” replied Donald, standing still,

(016-145) with a second expression of the same sentiment in the regretfulness of

(016-146) his face. “Why should you say it—think it?”

(016-147) The cloud lifted from Henchard’s brow, and as Donald finished the

(016-148) corn-merchant turned to him, regarding his breast rather than his face.

(016-149) “I have been hearing things that vexed me,” he said. “’Twas that made

(016-150) me short in my manner—made me overlook what you really are. Now, I

(016-151) don’t want to go in here about this hay—Farfrae, you can do it better

(016-152) than I. They sent for ’ee, too. I have to attend a meeting of the Town

(016-153) Council at eleven, and ’tis drawing on for’t.”

(016-154) They parted thus in renewed friendship, Donald forbearing to ask

(016-155) Henchard for meanings that were not very plain to him. On Henchard’s

(016-156) part there was now again repose; and yet, whenever he thought of

(016-157) Farfrae, it was with a dim dread; and he often regretted that he had

(016-158) told the young man his whole heart, and confided to him the secrets of

(016-159) his life.
