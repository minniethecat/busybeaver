# Tradition and Change

The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is set at a moment of historical transition. The old agricultural order — represented by [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s intuitive, personality-based bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess methods — is giving way to the new: [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s scientific [[rural-economics|agriculture]], modern machinery, and impersonal [[the-market-place|market]] logic. This economic transformation is the novel's deepest structure, beneath the personal drama of rivalry and revenge.

## Overview

Hardy wrote the novel in the 1880s but set it in the 1840s, looking back at a period when rural England was being transformed by mechanisation and [[the-market-place|market]] integration. [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is poised between two worlds: "the [[rural-economics|rural life]] around, not its urban opposite" (009-056). [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] represents the old world of personal [[reputation-and-social-standing|honour]] and intuitive judgment; [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] represents the new world of calculation and method. The old world loses.

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **Agricultural methods**: [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] introduces modern machinery and scientific grain testing — methods that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] dismisses but that prove superior. The "modern" disrupts traditional practice (030-104).
- **Bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess philosophy**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] does bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess through personality and risk; [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] through system and prudence. The novel's economic plot is a [[power-and-authority|control]]led experiment in which modern methods win consistently.
- **Generational change**: The transfer of the [[power-and-authority|mayor]]'s office from [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] represents a generational shift — not just of persons but of worldviews.
- **The cost of progress**: Hardy does not sentimentalise the old ways. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s methods are not noble but inefficient; his fall is tragic but not unjust. Yet the novel registers the human cost of the transition.

## Literary Analysis

### Hardy as Historian of the Present

Hardy's Dorset was undergoing the transformation he describes, and the novel is partly an act of historical preservation — a record of a world that was vanishing even as he wrote. The tension between nostalgia and realism gives the novel its distinctive tone.

### Naturalism and Historical Determinism

The economic transition from traditional to modern methods operates with the same impersonal force as [[nature-and-landscape|weather]] or [[fate-and-determinism|fate]]. Characters do not choose sides so much as they embody historical forces they cannot [[power-and-authority|control]]. This is Naturalism at the macro scale.
