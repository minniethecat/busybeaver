# Section 34

(033-001) “I would not—do anything against you at all!” stammered Elizabeth,

(033-002) keeping in all signs of emotion till she was ready to burst. “I cannot

(033-003) understand how my father can command you so; I don’t sympathize with

(033-004) him in it at all. I’ll go to him and ask him to release you.”

(033-005) “No, no,” said [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]. “Let it all be.”

(033-006) XXVIII.

(033-007) The next morning [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] went to the Town Hall below Lucetta’s house,

(033-008) to attend Petty Sessions, being still a magistrate for the year by

(033-009) virtue of his late position as Mayor. In passing he looked up at her

(033-010) windows, but nothing of her was to be seen.

(033-011) Henchard as a Justice of the Peace may at first seem to be an even

(033-012) greater incongruity than Shallow and Silence themselves. But his rough

(033-013) and ready perceptions, his sledge-hammer directness, had often served

(033-014) him better than nice legal knowledge in despatching such simple

(033-015) business as fell to his hands in this Court. To-day Dr. Chalkfield, the

(033-016) Mayor for the year, being absent, the corn-merchant took the big chair,

(033-017) his eyes still abstractedly stretching out of the window to the ashlar

(033-018) front of [[high-place-hall|High-Place Hall]].

(033-019) There was one case only, and the offender stood before him. She was an

(033-020) old woman of mottled countenance, attired in a shawl of that nameless

(033-021) tertiary hue which comes, but cannot be made—a hue neither tawny,

(033-022) russet, hazel, nor ash; a sticky black bonnet that seemed to have been

(033-023) worn in the country of the Psalmist where the clouds drop fatness; and

(033-024) an apron that had been white in time so comparatively recent as still

(033-025) to contrast visibly with the rest of her clothes. The steeped aspect of

(033-026) the woman as a whole showed her to be no native of the country-side or

(033-027) even of a country-town.

(033-028) She looked cursorily at Henchard and the second magistrate, and

(033-029) Henchard looked at her, with a momentary pause, as if she had reminded

(033-030) him indistinctly of somebody or something which passed from his mind as

(033-031) quickly as it had come. “Well, and what has she been doing?” he said,

(033-032) looking down at the charge sheet.

(033-033) “She is charged, sir, with the offence of disorderly female and

(033-034) nuisance,” whispered [[constable-stubberd|Stubberd]].

(033-035) “Where did she do that?” said the other magistrate.

(033-036) “By [[the-church|the church]], sir, of all the horrible places in the world!—I caught

(033-037) her in the act, your worship.”

(033-038) “Stand back then,” said Henchard, “and let’s hear what you’ve got to

(033-039) say.”

(033-040) Stubberd was sworn in, the magistrate’s clerk dipped his pen, Henchard

(033-041) being no note-taker himself, and the constable began—

(033-042) “Hearing a’ illegal noise I went down the street at twenty-five minutes

(033-043) past eleven P.M. on the night of the fifth instinct, Hannah Dominy.

(033-044) When I had—

(033-045) “Don’t go so fast, Stubberd,” said the clerk.

(033-046) The constable waited, with his eyes on the clerk’s pen, till the latter

(033-047) stopped scratching and said, “yes.” Stubberd continued: “When I had

(033-048) proceeded to the spot I saw defendant at another spot, namely, the

(033-049) gutter.” He paused, watching the point of the clerk’s pen again.

(033-050) “Gutter, yes, Stubberd.”

(033-051) “Spot measuring twelve feet nine inches or thereabouts from where I—”

(033-052) Still careful not to outrun the clerk’s penmanship Stubberd pulled up

(033-053) again; for having got his evidence by heart it was immaterial to him

(033-054) whereabouts he broke off.

(033-055) “I object to that,” spoke up the old woman, “‘spot measuring twelve

(033-056) feet nine or thereabouts from where I,’ is not sound testimony!”

(033-057) The magistrates consulted, and the second one said that the bench was

(033-058) of opinion that twelve feet nine inches from a man on his oath was

(033-059) admissible.

(033-060) Stubberd, with a suppressed gaze of victorious rectitude at the old

(033-061) woman, continued: “Was standing myself. She was wambling about quite

(033-062) dangerous to the thoroughfare and when I approached to draw near she

(033-063) committed the nuisance, and insulted me.”

(033-064) “‘Insulted me.’ ...Yes, what did she say?”

(033-065) “She said, ‘Put away that dee lantern,’ she says.”

(033-066) “Yes.”

(033-067) “Says she, ‘Dost hear, old turmit-head? Put away that dee lantern. I

(033-068) have floored fellows a dee sight finer-looking than a dee fool like

(033-069) thee, you son of a bee, dee me if I haint,’ she says.

(033-070) “I object to that conversation!” interposed the old woman. “I was not

(033-071) capable enough to hear what I said, and what is said out of my hearing

(033-072) is not evidence.”

(033-073) There was another stoppage for consultation, a book was referred to,

(033-074) and finally Stubberd was allowed to go on again. The [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] was that the

(033-075) old woman had appeared in court so many more times than the magistrates

(033-076) themselves, that they were obliged to keep a sharp look-out upon their

(033-077) procedure. However, when Stubberd had rambled on a little further

(033-078) Henchard broke out impatiently, “Come—we don’t want to hear any more of

(033-079) them cust dees and bees! Say the words out like a man, and don’t be so

(033-080) modest, Stubberd; or else leave it alone!” Turning to the woman, “Now

(033-081) then, have you any questions to ask him, or anything to say?”

(033-082) “Yes,” she replied with a twinkle in her eye; and the clerk dipped his

(033-083) pen.

(033-084) “Twenty years ago or thereabout I was selling of furmity in a tent at

(033-085) [[weydon-priors|Weydon Fair]]——”

(033-086) “‘Twenty years ago’—well, that’s beginning at the beginning; suppose

(033-087) you go back to the Creation!” said the clerk, not without satire.

(033-088) But Henchard stared, and quite forgot what was evidence and what was

(033-089) not.

(033-090) “A man and a woman with a little child came into my tent,” the woman

(033-091) continued. “They sat down and had a basin apiece. Ah, Lord’s my life! I

(033-092) was of a more respectable station in the world then than I am now,

(033-093) being a land smuggler in a large way of business; and I used to season

(033-094) my furmity with rum for them who asked for’t. I did it for the man; and

(033-095) then he had more and more; till at last he quarrelled with his wife,

(033-096) and offered to sell her to the highest bidder. A sailor came in and bid

(033-097) five guineas, and paid the money, and led her away. And the man who

(033-098) sold his wife in that fashion is the man sitting there in the great big

(033-099) chair.” The speaker concluded by nodding her head at Henchard and

(033-100) folding her arms.

(033-101) Everybody looked at Henchard. His face seemed strange, and in tint as

(033-102) if it had been powdered over with ashes. “We don’t want to hear your

(033-103) life and adventures,” said the second magistrate sharply, filling the

(033-104) pause which followed. “You’ve been asked if you’ve anything to say

(033-105) bearing on the case.”

(033-106) “That bears on the case. It proves that he’s no better than I, and has

(033-107) no right to sit there in judgment upon me.”

(033-108) “’Tis a concocted story,” said the clerk. “So hold your tongue!”

(033-109) “No—’tis true.” The words came from Henchard. “’Tis as true as the

(033-110) light,” he said slowly. “And upon my soul it does prove that I’m no

(033-111) better than she! And to keep out of any temptation to treat her hard

(033-112) for her revenge, I’ll leave her to you.”

(033-113) The sensation in the court was indescribably great. Henchard left the

(033-114) chair, and came out, passing through a group of people on the steps and

(033-115) outside that was much larger than usual; for it seemed that the old

(033-116) furmity dealer had mysteriously hinted to the denizens of [[mixen-lane|the lane]] in

(033-117) which she had been lodging since her arrival, that she knew a queer

(033-118) thing or two about their great local man Mr. Henchard, if she chose to

(033-119) tell it. This had brought them hither.

(033-120) “Why are there so many idlers round the Town Hall to-day?” said Lucetta

(033-121) to her servant when the case was over. She had risen late, and had just

(033-122) looked out of the window.

(033-123) “Oh, please, ma’am, ’tis this larry about Mr. Henchard. A woman has

(033-124) proved that before he became a gentleman he sold his wife for five

(033-125) guineas in a booth at a fair.”

(033-126) In all the accounts which Henchard had given her of the separation from

(033-127) his wife Susan for so many years, of his belief in her death, and so

(033-128) on, he had never clearly explained the actual and immediate cause of

(033-129) that separation. The story she now heard for the first time.

(033-130) A gradual misery overspread Lucetta’s face as she dwelt upon the

(033-131) promise wrung from her the night before. At bottom, then, Henchard was

(033-132) this. How terrible a contingency for a woman who should commit herself

(033-133) to his care.

(033-134) During the day she went out to [[the-ring|the Ring]] and to other places, not coming

(033-135) in till nearly dusk. As soon as she saw [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] after her return

(033-136) indoors she told her that she had resolved to go away from home to the

(033-137) [[budmouth|seaside]] for a few days—to Port-Bredy; [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] was so gloomy.

(033-138) Elizabeth, seeing that she looked wan and disturbed, encouraged her in

(033-139) the idea, thinking a [[tradition-and-change|change]] would afford her relief. She could not help

(033-140) suspecting that the gloom which seemed to have come over Casterbridge

(033-141) in Lucetta’s eyes might be partially owing to the fact that [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] was

(033-142) away from home.

(033-143) Elizabeth saw her friend depart for Port-Bredy, and took charge of

(033-144) High-Place Hall till her return. After two or three days of solitude

(033-145) and incessant rain Henchard called at the house. He seemed disappointed

(033-146) to hear of Lucetta’s absence and though he nodded with outward

(033-147) indifference he went away handling his beard with a nettled mien.

(033-148) The next day he called again. “Is she come now?” he asked.

(033-149) “Yes. She returned this morning,” replied his stepdaughter. “But she is

(033-150) not indoors. She has gone for a walk along the turnpike-road to

(033-151) Port-Bredy. She [[ambition-and-will|will]] be home by dusk.”

(033-152) After a few words, which only served to reveal his restless impatience,

(033-153) he left the house again.

(033-154) XXIX.

(033-155) At this hour Lucetta was bounding along the road to Port-Bredy just as

(033-156) Elizabeth had announced. That she had chosen for her afternoon walk the

(033-157) road along which she had returned to Casterbridge three hours earlier

(033-158) in a carriage was curious—if anything should be called curious in

(033-159) concatenations of phenomena wherein each is known to have its

(033-160) accounting cause. It was the day of the chief [[the-market-place|market]]—Saturday—and

(033-161) Farfrae for once had been missed from his corn-stand in the dealers’

(033-162) room. Nevertheless, it was known that he would be home that night—“for

(033-163) Sunday,” as Casterbridge expressed it.

(033-164) Lucetta, in continuing her walk, had at length reached the end of the

(033-165) ranked trees which bordered the highway in this and other directions

(033-166) out of the town. This end marked a mile; and here she stopped.

(033-167) The spot was a vale between two gentle acclivities, and the road, still

(033-168) adhering to its Roman foundation, stretched onward straight as a

(033-169) surveyor’s line till lost to sight on the most distant ridge. There was

(033-170) neither hedge nor tree in the prospect now, the road clinging to the

(033-171) stubby expanse of corn-land like a strip to an undulating garment. Near

(033-172) her was a barn—the single building of any kind within her horizon.

(033-173) She strained her eyes up the lessening road, but nothing appeared

(033-174) thereon—not so much as a speck. She sighed one word—“Donald!” and

(033-175) turned her face to the town for retreat.

(033-176) Here the case was different. A single figure was approaching

(033-177) her—Elizabeth-Jane’s.

(033-178) Lucetta, in spite of her loneliness, seemed a little vexed. Elizabeth’s

(033-179) face, as soon as she recognized her friend, shaped itself into

(033-180) affectionate lines while yet beyond speaking distance. “I suddenly

(033-181) thought I would come and meet you,” she said, smiling.

(033-182) Lucetta’s reply was taken from her lips by an unexpected diversion. A

(033-183) by-road on her right hand descended from the fields into the highway at

(033-184) the point where she stood, and down the track a bull was rambling

(033-185) uncertainly towards her and Elizabeth, who, facing the other way, did

(033-186) not observe him.

(033-187) In the latter quarter of each year cattle were at once the mainstay and

(033-188) the terror of families about Casterbridge and its neighbourhood, where

(033-189) breeding was carried on with Abrahamic success. The head of stock

(033-190) driven into and out of the town at this season to be sold by the local

(033-191) auctioneer was very large; and all these horned beasts, in travelling

(033-192) to and fro, sent [[gender-and-power|women]] and children to shelter as nothing else could

(033-193) do. In the main the animals would have walked along quietly enough; but

(033-194) the Casterbridge tradition was that to drive stock it was indispensable

(033-195) that hideous cries, coupled with Yahoo antics and gestures, should be

(033-196) used, large sticks flourished, stray dogs called in, and in general

(033-197) everything done that was likely to infuriate the viciously disposed and

(033-198) terrify the mild. Nothing was commoner than for a house-holder on going

(033-199) out of his parlour to find his hall or passage full of little children,

(033-200) nursemaids, aged women, or a ladies’ school, who apologized for their

(033-201) presence by saying, “A bull passing down street from the sale.”
