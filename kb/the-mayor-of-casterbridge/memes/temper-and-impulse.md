# Temper and Impulse

The defining psychological trait of [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]], and the immediate mechanism through which his character becomes his [[fate-and-determinism|fate]]. Where [[pride]] provides the architecture of his tragedy, temper provides the trigger — the impulsive action that sets each catastrophe in motion.

## Overview

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s temper is established in the novel's opening scene: "as a consequence, out of temper with the world" (001-143), he commits the act that [[ambition-and-will|will]] haunt him — selling his wife at a fair. This pattern — emotion erupting into irrevocable action — repeats throughout the novel. Hardy presents temper not as occasional irritability but as a constitutional force, a "hot-tempered" (022-137) [[nature-and-landscape|nature]] that overrides calculation and transforms momentary feeling into permanent consequence.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel provides a clinical catalogue of temper's destructive [[gender-and-power|power]]:

- **The wife-auction**: Drunk and "out of temper with the world" (001-143), [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] sells Susan — the original [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]] from which all subsequent tragedy flows.
- **Susan's assessment**: "Nothing but temper!" (001-175) is her bitter summary, a diagnosis that proves prophetic.
- **With [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "jealous temper" (017-172) transforms professional rivalry into personal vendetta. His employees note "he's better tempered" (016-118) than [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] — a comparison that wounds precisely because it is true.
- **Impulsive generosity**: Not all [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s impulses are destructive — his hiring of [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] is "impulsive" (018-032) and initially beneficial. Hardy's point is that impulse is morally neutral; it is the character behind it that determines outcome.
- **The final pattern**: Even in his last days, "the impulsive judgment of the moment" (039-168) governs [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s decisions, showing that temper is not a flaw he outgrows but a permanent condition.

## Literary Analysis

### Temper as Fate Mechanism

If "Character is Fate," temper is the transmission mechanism. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s hot temper converts his inner flaws — [[pride]], jealousy, insecurity — into external actions with irreversible consequences. This is Hardy's Naturalist insight: psychological traits function as physical laws.

### Victorian Theories of Character

Hardy wrote in an era fascinated by phrenology, physiognomy, and theories of inherited temperament. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "hot temper" participates in this discourse — it is presented as innate, almost biological, rather than chosen. This [[fate-and-determinism|determinism]] aligns with the novel's broader Naturalist vision.
