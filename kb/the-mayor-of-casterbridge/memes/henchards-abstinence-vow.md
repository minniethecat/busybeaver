# Henchard's Abstinence Vow

The morning after [[susan-henchard|the wife]]-auction, [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] wakes to the horror of what he has done. His response is characteristically extreme: he swears an oath upon the Bible to abstain from all alcohol for twenty-one years — one year for every year of his life so far. It is an act of monumental [[ambition-and-will|will]] that transforms his life and, ultimately, cannot be sustained (002-097).

## Narrative Context

The vow is the hinge between [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s two lives — the drunken hay-trusser who sold his wife and the abstinent [[power-and-authority|mayor]] of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]. It demonstrates his extraordinary [[ambition-and-will|will]][[gender-and-power|power]] and, at the same time, its fragility. The vow is not moral reform but mechanical substitution: he replaces alcohol with [[ambition-and-will|ambition]], and when the vow expires, the old pattern reasserts itself.

## Consequences

The vow fuels [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s rise: sober, he builds a bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess, wins the [[power-and-authority|mayor]]'s office, and becomes [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s leading citizen. When the twenty-one years expire and he begins drinking again, his decline is as rapid as his rise was. The vow's temporal limit — exactly twenty-one years — makes it a ticking clock that the reader watches with dread.
