# Henchard's Reconciliation Attempt

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s final attempt to repair his relationship with [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] — arriving at her wedding to [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] with a gift of a goldfinch in a cage, a gesture that is simultaneously touching, pathetic, and misguided. [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], who has endured his oscillations between desperate love and bitter rejection, turns him away (052-001).

## Narrative Context

The reconciliation attempt is the novel's last study in [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s tragic pattern: genuine feeling expressed in a form that guarantees its rejection. He loves [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], but his love has always been possessive rather than generous, demanding rather than giving. The wedding scene crystallizes this: he comes to give but cannot help asking to receive.

## Consequences

Rejected at the wedding, [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] leaves [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] and walks to his death. The goldfinch — still in its cage, still his gift, still unaccepted — becomes the novel's final image of him: a trapped creature offering what cannot be received.
