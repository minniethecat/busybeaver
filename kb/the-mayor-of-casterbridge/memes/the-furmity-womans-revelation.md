# The Furmity-Woman's Revelation

The furmity-seller from [[the-wife-auction|Weydon-Priors fair]] — the woman in whose tent [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] sold his wife — reappears in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] two decades later. Arrested for a minor offence and brought before the magistrates, she recognizes [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] and exposes his past to the court: [[susan-henchard|the wife]]-auction, the twenty-one-year secret, all of it.

## Narrative Context

The revelation is the novel's mechanism for collap[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]g past into present. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] has spent two decades building [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]] on the suppression of a [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]gle act. The [[the-furmity-woman|furmity-woman]]'s testimony, delivered from the dock of a courtroom, destroys that construction in minutes. This is Hardy's version of the return of the repressed — as brutal and as inexorable as Greek tragedy.

## Consequences

The public revelation of [[susan-henchard|the wife]]-auction completes [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s social destruction. His [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], already damaged by [[henchards-bankruptcy|bankruptcy]], is now destroyed at its foundation. The [[the-furmity-woman|furmity-woman]]'s reappearance is the novel's most direct statement that the past cannot be escaped.
