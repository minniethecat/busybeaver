# Gender and Power

Hardy's women — Susan, [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] — navigate a world where power is male by default and female by exception. The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] examines gender not as a separate theme but as the submerged architecture of all social relations: [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]], commerce, [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], and law all distribute power along gendered lines.

## Overview

The novel's gender dynamics are established in its opening scene: a man sells his wife, and the transaction is witnessed but not prevented. This radical act exposes the ordinary truth — that Victorian [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]] was an economic institution in which women were property in all but name. Throughout the novel, female characters exercise agency only through indirection, while male characters destroy themselves through its unfettered exercise.

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **Susan's silence**: [[susan-henchard|Susan Henchard]] endures her sale with "freedom from levity of character" (002-097) — a description that captures the era's ideal of female passivity while hinting at its cost.
- **[[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s vulnerability**: [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s social existence depends entirely on male protection — first [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s, then [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s. Her letters, her [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], her life are all hostage to male discretion, making her the novel's most exposed character.
- **[[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]'s pragmatism**: As the novel's survivor, [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] navigates gender constraints through observation and self-restraint — "the self-repression" (024-057) that the era demanded of women.
- **Masculine destruction**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s tragedy is specifically masculine — [[pride]], [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], and the refusal to appear weak are coded as male vices, and they destroy him as surely as patriarchal structures destroy the women.

## Literary Analysis

### Hardy and the Woman Question

Hardy was more sympathetic to women's position than many Victorian novelists, but his sympathy is tragic rather than programmatic. He exposes gender injustice without offering solutions — consistent with his Naturalist view that social structures, like natural laws, can be observed but not easily [[tradition-and-change|change]]d.
