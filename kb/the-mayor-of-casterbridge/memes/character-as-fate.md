# Character as Fate

The governing philosophical premise of The Mayor of Casterbridge — and perhaps Hardy's most distinctive contribution to the tragic [[tradition-and-change|tradition]]. The idea that a person's character, not external circumstance or divine [[ambition-and-will|will]], determines their [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]] runs through the novel like a spine, shaping both its plot and its moral vision.

## The Concept

In [[class-and-social-order|class]]ical tragedy, [[fate-and-determinism|fate]] is external — the gods, the stars, an inherited curse. In Hardy, [[fate-and-determinism|fate]] is internal — the inescapable logic of who a person is. [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] is not destroyed by gods or stars but by his own [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], his own [[pride]], his own inability to adapt. As Hardy writes, "character is fate" — and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s character ensures his destruction as surely as any Greek oracle.

> "His character knew no patience." (cf. 031-112)

This is not to say that circumstance is irrelevant. The novel is full of coincidences, missed connections, and bad timing — the return of [[susan-henchard|Susan]], the arrival of [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], the [[the-furmity-womans-revelation|furmity-woman]]'s revelation. But Hardy's point is that character determines how circumstance is met: the same coincidences that destroy [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] would not destroy [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], because [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s character is different.

## Expression in the Novel

| Character | Fate | Rooted In |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] | Total destruction | Pride, [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], jealousy, [[temper-and-impulse|impulsiveness]] |
| [[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]] | Steady success | Prudence, competence, emotional restraint |
| [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta Templeman]] | Public [[pride|humiliation]] and death | Vanity, social [[ambition-and-will|ambition]], romantic [[temper-and-impulse|impulsiveness]] |
| [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] | Qualified survival | Patience, self-effacement, emotional endurance |
| [[susan-henchard|Susan Henchard]] | Quiet endurance, then death | Passivity, acceptance of patriarchal [[power-and-authority|authority]] |

Each character's [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]] flows from their fundamental [[nature-and-landscape|nature]]. The novel is a laboratory in which Hardy tests the proposition that character is [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]] — and finds it holds across every social [[class-and-social-order|rank]] and moral disposition.

## Literary Context

Hardy's "character as [[fate-and-determinism|fate]]" is a key moment in the [[tradition-and-change|transition]] from [[class-and-social-order|class]]ical to modern tragedy. Where Sophocles and Shakespeare locate [[fate-and-determinism|fate]] in the gods or the stars, Hardy — writing in the post-Darwinian, post-religious world of the late nineteenth century — locates it in psychology. The tragic flaw (hamartia) is not a mistake but a character trait; the catastrophe is not punishment but consequence.

This shift aligns Hardy with the [[fate-and-determinism|deterministic philosophy]] of his time — the idea that human behaviour is governed by heredity, [[nature-and-landscape|environment]], and innate disposition. But Hardy's version is more nuanced than simple [[fate-and-determinism|determinism]]: his characters have [[ambition-and-will|will]], they make choices, but their choices are constrained and shaped by who they fundamentally are.
