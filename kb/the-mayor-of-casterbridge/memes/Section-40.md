# Section 40

(039-001) By this hour the volcanic fires of his nature had burnt down, and

(039-002) having drunk no great quantity as yet he was inclined to acquiesce. She

(039-003) took his arm, and together they went on. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] walked blankly, like

(039-004) a blind man, repeating to himself the last words of the singers—

(039-005) “And the next age his hated name

(039-006) Shall utterly deface.”

(039-007) At length he said to her, “I am a man to my word. I have kept my oath

(039-008) for twenty-one years; and now I can drink with a good conscience.... If

(039-009) I don’t do for him—well, I am a fearful practical joker when I choose!

(039-010) He has taken away everything from me, and by heavens, if I meet him I

(039-011) won’t answer for my deeds!”

(039-012) These half-uttered words alarmed Elizabeth—all the more by reason of

(039-013) the still [[ambition-and-will|determination]] of Henchard’s mien.

(039-014) “What will you do?” she asked cautiously, while trembling with

(039-015) disquietude, and guessing Henchard’s allusion only too well.

(039-016) Henchard did not answer, and they went on till they had reached his

(039-017) cottage. “May I come in?” she said.

(039-018) “No, no; not to-day,” said Henchard; and she went away; feeling that to

(039-019) caution [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] was almost her duty, as it was certainly her strong

(039-020) desire.

(039-021) As on the Sunday, so on the week-days, Farfrae and [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] might have

(039-022) been seen flitting about [[casterbridge|the town]] like two butterflies—or rather like a

(039-023) bee and a butterfly in league for life. She seemed to take no pleasure

(039-024) in going anywhere except in her husband’s company; and hence when

(039-025) business would not permit him to waste an afternoon she remained

(039-026) indoors waiting for the time to pass till his return, her face being

(039-027) visible to [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] from her window aloft. The latter, however,

(039-028) did not say to herself that Farfrae should be thankful for such

(039-029) devotion, but, full of her reading, she cited Rosalind’s exclamation:

(039-030) “Mistress, know yourself; down on your knees and thank Heaven fasting

(039-031) for a good man’s love.”

(039-032) She kept her eye upon Henchard also. One day he answered her inquiry

(039-033) for his health by saying that he could not endure [[abel-whittle|Abel Whittle]]’s

(039-034) pitying eyes upon him while they worked together in the yard. “He is

(039-035) such a fool,” said Henchard, “that he can never get out of his mind the

(039-036) time when I was master there.”

(039-037) “I’ll come and wimble for you instead of him, if you will allow me,”

(039-038) said she. Her motive on going to the yard was to get an opportunity of

(039-039) observing the general position of affairs on Farfrae’s premises now

(039-040) that her stepfather was a workman there. Henchard’s threats had alarmed

(039-041) her so much that she wished to see his behaviour when the two were face

(039-042) to face.

(039-043) For two or three days after her arrival Donald did not make any

(039-044) [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]]. Then one afternoon the green door opened, and through came,

(039-045) first Farfrae, and at his heels Lucetta. Donald brought his wife

(039-046) forward without hesitation, it being obvious that he had no suspicion

(039-047) whatever of any antecedents in common between her and the now

(039-048) journeyman hay-trusser.

(039-049) Henchard did not turn his eyes toward either of the pair, keeping them

(039-050) fixed on the bond he twisted, as if that alone absorbed him. A feeling

(039-051) of delicacy, which ever prompted Farfrae to avoid anything that might

(039-052) seem like triumphing over a fallen rival, led him to keep away from the

(039-053) hay-barn where Henchard and his daughter were working, and to go on to

(039-054) the corn department. Meanwhile Lucetta, never having been informed that

(039-055) Henchard had entered her husband’s service, rambled straight on to the

(039-056) barn, where she came suddenly upon Henchard, and gave vent to a little

(039-057) “Oh!” which the happy and busy Donald was too far off to hear.

(039-058) Henchard, with withering humility of demeanour, touched the brim of his

(039-059) hat to her as Whittle and the rest had done, to which she breathed a

(039-060) dead-alive “Good afternoon.”

(039-061) “I beg your pardon, ma’am?” said Henchard, as if he had not heard.

(039-062) “I said good afternoon,” she faltered.

(039-063) “O yes, good afternoon, ma’am,” he replied, touching his hat again. “I

(039-064) am glad to see you, ma’am.” Lucetta looked embarrassed, and Henchard

(039-065) continued: “For we humble workmen here feel it a great [[reputation-and-social-standing|honour]] that a

(039-066) lady should look in and take an interest in us.”

(039-067) She glanced at him entreatingly; the sarcasm was too bitter, too

(039-068) unendurable.

(039-069) “Can you tell me the time, ma’am?” he asked.

(039-070) “Yes,” she said hastily; “half-past four.”

(039-071) “Thank ’ee. An hour and a half longer before we are released from work.

(039-072) Ah, ma’am, we of the lower classes know nothing of the gay leisure that

(039-073) such as you enjoy!”

(039-074) As soon as she could do so Lucetta left him, nodded and smiled to

(039-075) Elizabeth-Jane, and joined her husband at the other end of the

(039-076) enclosure, where she could be seen leading him away by the outer gates,

(039-077) so as to avoid passing Henchard again. That she had been taken by

(039-078) surprise was obvious. The result of this casual rencounter was that the

(039-079) next morning a note was put into Henchard’s hand by the postman.

(039-080) “Will you,” said Lucetta, with as much bitterness as she could put into

(039-081) a small communication, “will you kindly undertake not to speak to me in

(039-082) the biting undertones you used to-day, if I walk through the yard at

(039-083) any time? I bear you no ill-will, and I am only too glad that you

(039-084) should have employment of my dear husband; but in common fairness treat

(039-085) me as his wife, and do not try to make me wretched by covert sneers. I

(039-086) have committed no crime, and done you no injury.”

(039-087) “Poor fool!” said Henchard with fond savagery, holding out the note.

(039-088) “To know no better than commit herself in writing like this! Why, if I

(039-089) were to show that to her dear husband—pooh!” He threw the letter into

(039-090) the fire.

(039-091) Lucetta took care not to come again among the hay and corn. She would

(039-092) rather have died than run the risk of encountering Henchard at such

(039-093) close quarters a second time. The gulf between them was growing wider

(039-094) every day. Farfrae was always considerate to his fallen acquaintance;

(039-095) but it was impossible that he should not, by degrees, cease to regard

(039-096) the ex-corn-merchant as more than one of his other workmen. Henchard

(039-097) saw this, and concealed his feelings under a cover of stolidity,

(039-098) fortifying his heart by drinking more freely at the [[the-three-mariners|Three Mariners]]

(039-099) every evening.

(039-100) Often did Elizabeth-Jane, in her endeavours to prevent his taking other

(039-101) liquor, carry tea to him in a little basket at five o’clock. Arriving

(039-102) one day on this errand she found her stepfather was measuring up

(039-103) clover-seed and rape-seed in the corn-stores on the top floor, and she

(039-104) ascended to him. Each floor had a door opening into the air under a

(039-105) cat-head, from which a chain dangled for hoisting the sacks.

(039-106) When Elizabeth’s head rose through the trap she perceived that the

(039-107) upper door was open, and that her stepfather and Farfrae stood just

(039-108) within it in conversation, Farfrae being nearest the dizzy edge, and

(039-109) Henchard a little way behind. Not to interrupt them she remained on the

(039-110) steps without raising her head any higher. While waiting thus she

(039-111) saw—or fancied she saw, for she had a terror of feeling certain—her

(039-112) stepfather slowly raise his hand to a level behind Farfrae’s shoulders,

(039-113) a curious expression taking possession of his face. The young man was

(039-114) quite unconscious of the action, which was so indirect that, if Farfrae

(039-115) had observed it, he might almost have regarded it as an idle

(039-116) outstretching of the arm. But it would have been possible, by a

(039-117) comparatively light touch, to push Farfrae off his balance, and send

(039-118) him head over heels into the air.

(039-119) Elizabeth felt quite sick at heart on thinking of what this _might_

(039-120) have meant. As soon as they turned she mechanically took the tea to

(039-121) Henchard, left it, and went away. Reflecting, she endeavoured to assure

(039-122) herself that the movement was an idle eccentricity, and no more. Yet,

(039-123) on the other hand, his subordinate position in an establishment where

(039-124) he once had been master might be acting on him like an irritant poison;

(039-125) and she finally resolved to caution Donald.

(039-127) Next morning, accordingly, she rose at five o’clock and went into the

(039-128) street. It was not yet light; a dense fog prevailed, and the town was

(039-129) as silent as it was dark, except that from the rectangular avenues

(039-130) which framed in the borough there came a chorus of tiny rappings,

(039-131) caused by the fall of water-drops condensed on the boughs; now it was

(039-132) wafted from the West Walk, now from the South Walk; and then from both

(039-133) quarters simultaneously. She moved on to the bottom of Corn Street,

(039-134) and, knowing his time well, waited only a few minutes before she heard

(039-135) the familiar bang of his door, and then his quick walk towards her. She

(039-136) met him at the point where the last tree of the engirding avenue

(039-137) flanked the last house in the street.

(039-138) He could hardly discern her till, glancing inquiringly, he said,

(039-139) “What—Miss Henchard—and are ye up so airly?”

(039-140) She asked him to pardon her for waylaying him at such an unseemly time.

(039-141) “But I am anxious to mention something,” she said. “And I wished not to

(039-142) alarm Mrs. Farfrae by calling.”

(039-143) “Yes?” said he, with the cheeriness of a superior. “And what may it be?

(039-144) It’s very kind of ye, I’m sure.”

(039-145) She now felt the difficulty of conveying to his mind the exact aspect

(039-146) of possibilities in her own. But she somehow began, and introduced

(039-147) Henchard’s name. “I sometimes fear,” she said with an effort, “that he

(039-148) may be betrayed into some attempt to—insult you, sir.”

(039-149) “But we are the best of friends?”

(039-150) “Or to play some practical joke upon you, sir. Remember that he has

(039-151) been hardly used.”

(039-152) “But we are quite friendly?”

(039-153) “Or to do something—that would injure you—hurt you—wound you.” Every

(039-154) word cost her twice its length of pain. And she could see that Farfrae

(039-155) was still incredulous. Henchard, a poor man in his employ, was not to

(039-156) Farfrae’s view the Henchard who had ruled him. Yet he was not only the

(039-157) same man, but that man with his sinister qualities, formerly latent,

(039-158) quickened into life by his buffetings.

(039-159) Farfrae, happy, and thinking no evil, persisted in making light of her

(039-160) fears. Thus they parted, and she went homeward, journeymen now being in

(039-161) the street, waggoners going to the harness-makers for articles left to

(039-162) be repaired, farm-horses going to the shoeing-smiths, and the sons of

(039-163) labour showing themselves generally on the move. Elizabeth entered her

(039-164) lodging unhappily, thinking she had done no good, and only made herself

(039-165) appear foolish by her weak note of warning.

(039-166) But Donald Farfrae was one of those men upon whom an incident is never

(039-167) absolutely lost. He revised impressions from a subsequent point of

(039-168) view, and the [[temper-and-impulse|impulsive]] judgment of the moment was not always his

(039-169) permanent one. The vision of Elizabeth’s earnest face in the rimy dawn

(039-170) came back to him several times during the day. Knowing the solidity of

(039-171) her [[character-as-fate|character]] he did not treat her hints altogether as idle sounds.

(039-172) But he did not desist from a kindly scheme on Henchard’s account that

(039-173) engaged him just then; and when he met Lawyer Joyce, the town-clerk,

(039-174) later in the day, he spoke of it as if nothing had occurred to damp it.

(039-175) “[[About]] that little seedsman’s shop,” he said, “the shop overlooking the

(039-176) churchyard, which is to let. It is not for myself I want it, but for

(039-177) our unlucky fellow-townsman Henchard. It would be a new beginning for

(039-178) him, if a small one; and I have told the Council that I would head a

(039-179) private subscription among them to set him up in it—that I would be

(039-180) fifty pounds, if they would make up the other fifty among them.”

(039-181) “Yes, yes; so I’ve heard; and there’s nothing to say against it for

(039-182) that matter,” the town-clerk replied, in his plain, frank way. “But,

(039-183) Farfrae, others see what you don’t. Henchard hates ’ee—ay, hates ’ee;

(039-184) and ’tis right that you should know it. To my knowledge he was at the
