# Section 23

(022-001) Thus she lived on, a dumb, deep-feeling, great-eyed creature, construed

(022-002) by not a single contiguous being; quenching with patient fortitude her

(022-003) incipient interest in [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], because it seemed to be one-sided,

(022-004) unmaidenly, and unwise. True, that for reasons best known to herself,

(022-005) she had, since Farfrae’s dismissal, shifted her quarters from the back

(022-006) room affording a view of the yard (which she had occupied with such

(022-007) zest) to a front chamber overlooking the street; but as for the young

(022-008) man, whenever he passed the house he seldom or never turned his head.

(022-009) Winter had almost come, and unsettled weather made her still more

(022-010) dependent upon indoor resources. But there were certain early winter

(022-011) days in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]—days of firmamental exhaustion which followed

(022-012) angry south-westerly tempests—when, if the sun shone, the air was like

(022-013) velvet. She seized on these days for her periodical visits to the spot

(022-014) where her mother lay buried—the still-used burial-ground of the old

(022-015) Roman-British city, whose curious feature was this, its continuity as a

(022-016) place of sepulture. [[susan-henchard|Mrs. Henchard]]’s dust mingled with the dust of [[gender-and-power|women]]

(022-017) who lay ornamented with glass hair-pins and amber necklaces, and men

(022-018) who held in their mouths coins of Hadrian, Posthumus, and the

(022-019) Constantines.

(022-020) Half-past ten in the morning was about her hour for seeking this spot—a

(022-021) time when the town avenues were deserted as the avenues of Karnac.

(022-022) Business had long since passed down them into its daily cells, and

(022-023) Leisure had not arrived there. So [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] walked and read, or

(022-024) looked over the edge of the book to think, and thus reached the

(022-025) churchyard.

(022-026) There, approaching her mother’s grave she saw a solitary dark figure in

(022-027) the middle of the gravel-walk. This figure, too, was reading; but not

(022-028) from a book: the words which engrossed it being the inscription on Mrs.

(022-029) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]’s tombstone. The personage was in mourning like herself, was

(022-030) about her age and size, and might have been her wraith or double, but

(022-031) for the fact that it was a lady much more beautifully dressed than she.

(022-032) Indeed, comparatively indifferent as Elizabeth-Jane was to dress,

(022-033) unless for some temporary whim or purpose, her eyes were arrested by

(022-034) the artistic perfection of the lady’s [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]]. Her gait, too, had a

(022-035) flexuousness about it, which seemed to avoid angularity. It was a

(022-036) revelation to Elizabeth that human beings could reach this stage of

(022-037) external development—she had never suspected it. She felt all the

(022-038) freshness and grace to be stolen from herself on the instant by the

(022-039) neighbourhood of such a stranger. And this was in face of the fact that

(022-040) Elizabeth could now have been writ handsome, while the young lady was

(022-041) simply pretty.

(022-042) Had she been envious she might have hated the woman; but she did not do

(022-043) that—she allowed herself the pleasure of feeling fascinated. She

(022-044) wondered where the lady had come from. The stumpy and practical walk of

(022-045) honest homeliness which mostly prevailed there, the two styles of dress

(022-046) thereabout, the simple and the mistaken, equally avouched that this

(022-047) figure was no Casterbridge woman’s, even if a book in her hand

(022-048) resembling a guide-book had not also suggested it.

(022-049) The stranger presently moved from the tombstone of Mrs. Henchard, and

(022-050) vanished behind the corner of the wall. Elizabeth went to the tomb

(022-051) herself; beside it were two footprints distinct in the soil, signifying

(022-052) that the lady had stood there a long time. She returned homeward,

(022-053) musing on what she had seen, as she might have mused on a rainbow or

(022-054) the Northern Lights, a rare butterfly or a cameo.

(022-055) Interesting as things had been out of doors, at home it turned out to

(022-056) be one of her bad days. Henchard, whose two years’ mayoralty was

(022-057) ending, had been made aware that he was not to be chosen to fill a

(022-058) vacancy in the list of aldermen; and that Farfrae was likely to become

(022-059) one of the Council. This caused the unfortunate discovery that she had

(022-060) played the waiting-maid in the town of which he was Mayor to rankle in

(022-061) his mind yet more poisonously. He had learnt by personal inquiry at the

(022-062) time that it was to Donald Farfrae—that treacherous upstart—that she

(022-063) had thus humiliated herself. And though Mrs. Stannidge seemed to attach

(022-064) no great importance to the incident—the cheerful souls at the Three

(022-065) Mariners having exhausted its aspects long ago—such was Henchard’s

(022-066) haughty spirit that the simple thrifty deed was regarded as little less

(022-067) than a social catastrophe by him.

(022-068) Ever since the evening of his wife’s arrival with her daughter there

(022-069) had been something in the air which had changed his luck. That dinner

(022-070) at the King’s Arms with his friends had been Henchard’s Austerlitz: he

(022-071) had had his successes since, but his course had not been upward. He was

(022-072) not to be numbered among the aldermen—that Peerage of burghers—as he

(022-073) had expected to be, and the consciousness of this soured him to-day.

(022-074) “Well, where have you been?” he said to her with offhand laconism.

(022-075) “I’ve been strolling in the Walks and churchyard, father, till I feel

(022-076) quite leery.” She clapped her hand to her mouth, but too late.

(022-077) This was just enough to incense Henchard after the other crosses of the

(022-078) day. “I _won’t_ have you talk like that!” he thundered. “‘Leery,’

(022-079) indeed. One would think you worked upon a farm! One day I learn that

(022-080) you lend a hand in public-houses. Then I hear you talk like a

(022-081) clodhopper. I’m burned, if it goes on, this house can’t hold us two.”

(022-082) The only way of getting a single pleasant thought to go to sleep upon

(022-083) after this was by recalling the lady she had seen that day, and hoping

(022-084) she might see her again.

(022-085) Meanwhile Henchard was sitting up, thinking over his jealous folly in

(022-086) forbidding Farfrae to pay his addresses to this girl who did not belong

(022-087) to him, when if he had allowed them to go on he might not have been

(022-088) encumbered with her. At last he said to himself with satisfaction as he

(022-089) jumped up and went to the writing-table: “Ah! he’ll think it means

(022-090) peace, and a [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]] portion—not that I don’t want my house to be

(022-091) troubled with her, and no portion at all!” He wrote as follows:—

(022-092) Sir,—On consideration, I don’t wish to interfere with your courtship of

(022-093) Elizabeth-Jane, if you care for her. I therefore withdraw my objection;

(022-094) excepting in this—that the business be not carried on in my

(022-095) house.—Yours,

(022-096) M. HENCHARD.

(022-097) Mr. Farfrae.

(022-098) The morrow, being fairly fine, found Elizabeth-Jane again in the

(022-099) churchyard, but while looking for the lady she was startled by the

(022-100) apparition of Farfrae, who passed outside the gate. He glanced up for a

(022-101) moment from a pocket-book in which he appeared to be making figures as

(022-102) he went; whether or not he saw her he took no notice, and disappeared.

(022-103) Unduly depressed by a sense of her own superfluity she thought he

(022-104) probably scorned her; and quite broken in spirit sat down on a bench.

(022-105) She fell into painful thought on her position, which ended with her

(022-106) saying quite loud, “O, I wish I was dead with dear mother!”

(022-107) Behind the bench was a little promenade under the wall where people

(022-108) sometimes walked instead of on the gravel. The bench seemed to be

(022-109) touched by something, she looked round, and a face was bending over

(022-110) her, veiled, but still distinct, the face of the young woman she had

(022-111) seen yesterday.

(022-112) Elizabeth-Jane looked confounded for a moment, knowing she had been

(022-113) overheard, though there was pleasure in her confusion. “Yes, I heard

(022-114) you,” said the lady, in a vivacious voice, answering her look. “What

(022-115) can have happened?”

(022-116) “I don’t—I can’t tell you,” said Elizabeth, putting her hand to her

(022-117) face to hide a quick flush that had come.

(022-118) There was no movement or word for a few seconds; then the girl felt

(022-119) that the young lady was sitting down beside her.

(022-120) “I guess how it is with you,” said the latter. “That was your mother.”

(022-121) She waved her hand towards the tombstone. Elizabeth looked up at her as

(022-122) if inquiring of herself whether there should be confidence. The lady’s

(022-123) manner was so desirous, so anxious, that the girl decided there should

(022-124) be confidence. “It was my mother,” she said, “my only friend.”

(022-125) “But your father, Mr. Henchard. He is living?”

(022-126) “Yes, he is living,” said Elizabeth-Jane.

(022-127) “Is he not kind to you?”

(022-128) “I’ve no wish to complain of him.”

(022-129) “There has been a disagreement?”

(022-130) “A little.”

(022-131) “Perhaps you were to blame,” suggested the stranger.

(022-132) “I was—in many ways,” sighed the meek Elizabeth. “I swept up the coals

(022-133) when the servants ought to have done it; and I said I was leery;—and he

(022-134) was angry with me.”

(022-135) The lady seemed to warm towards her for that reply. “Do you know the

(022-136) impression your words give me?” she said ingenuously. “That he is a

(022-137) hot-tempered man—a little proud—perhaps ambitious; but not a bad man.”

(022-138) Her anxiety not to condemn Henchard while siding with Elizabeth was

(022-139) curious.

(022-140) “O no; certainly not _bad_,” agreed the honest girl. “And he has not

(022-141) even been unkind to me till lately—since mother died. But it has been

(022-142) very much to bear while it has lasted. All is owing to my defects, I

(022-143) daresay; and my defects are owing to my history.”

(022-144) “What is your history?”

(022-145) Elizabeth-Jane looked wistfully at her questioner. She found that her

(022-146) questioner was looking at her, turned her eyes down; and then seemed

(022-147) compelled to look back again. “My history is not gay or attractive,”

(022-148) she said. “And yet I can tell it, if you really want to know.”

(022-149) The lady assured her that she did want to know; whereupon

(022-150) Elizabeth-Jane told the tale of her life as she understood it, which

(022-151) was in general the true one, except that the sale at [[weydon-priors|the fair]] had no

(022-152) part therein.

(022-153) Contrary to the girl’s expectation her new friend was not shocked. This

(022-154) cheered her; and it was not till she thought of returning to that home

(022-155) in which she had been treated so roughly of late that her spirits fell.

(022-156) “I don’t know how to return,” she murmured. “I think of going away. But

(022-157) what can I do? Where can I go?”

(022-158) “Perhaps it [[ambition-and-will|will]] be better soon,” said her friend gently. “So I would

(022-163) would do anything to be independent; for then perhaps my father might

(022-164) get to love me. But, ah!”

(022-165) “What?”

(022-166) “I am no accomplished person. And a companion to you must be that.”

(022-167) “O, not necessarily.”

(022-168) “Not? But I can’t help using rural words sometimes, when I don’t mean

(022-172) accidentally learned to write round hand instead of ladies’-hand. And,

(022-173) of course, you want some one who can write that?”

(022-174) “Well, no.”

(022-175) “What, not necessary to write ladies’-hand?” cried the joyous

(022-176) Elizabeth.

(022-177) “Not at all.”

(022-178) “But where do you live?”

(022-179) “In Casterbridge, or rather I shall be living here after twelve o’clock
