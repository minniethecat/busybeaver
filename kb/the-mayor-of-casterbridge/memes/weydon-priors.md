# Weydon-Priors

The village and its fairground where The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] opens — and where [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] commits the act that [[ambition-and-will|will]] define his life. Weydon-Priors is presented as a place of transience and moral looseness, where the normal rules of society are suspended by the licence of the fair.

## Description

Hardy presents [[the-wife-auction|Weydon-Priors fair]] as a place of temporary community — a gathering of itinerant traders, farmers, and labourers where normal social constraints are loosened by drink and anonymity. It is here, in a furmity-tent, that the drunken [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] sells his wife and child. The setting is morally significant: the fair's very transience makes it a place where permanent acts can be committed with the illusion that they are temporary.

## Narrative Function

Weydon-Priors functions as the novel's origin point — the place where the past is created that [[ambition-and-will|will]] haunt the entire subsequent narrative. Its distance from [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is both geographical and moral: [[the-wife-auction|the wife-sale]] happens elsewhere, in a space outside normal society, but its consequences pursue [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] into the heart of respectable [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]. The return of the [[the-furmity-woman|furmity-woman]] in a later chapter collapses the distance between the two places.
