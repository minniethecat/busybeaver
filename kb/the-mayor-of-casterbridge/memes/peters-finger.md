# Peter's Finger

The inn on [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]] that Hardy describes as "[[the-church|the church]] of [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]]" — a phrase that captures with bitter irony the tavern's role as the true seat of community, [[power-and-authority|authority]], and moral judgment in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s underworld.

## Description

Hardy introduces Peter's Finger with a phrase that is at once ironic and revelatory:

> The inn called Peter's Finger was the church of Mixen Lane. [042-121]

The metaphor is precise. If a church is where a community gathers to affirm its shared values, then Peter's Finger is indeed [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]]'s church — the place where its residents congregate, where its codes are enforced, and where its judgments are pronounced. The substitution of an inn for a church is not incidental: in the world of [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]], [[tradition-and-change|tradition]]al moral [[power-and-authority|authority]] has been replaced by a rougher, more direct form of communal justice.

The inn stands "going off [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]]" [044-121] — physically marginal even within the marginal quarter, at the very boundary of respectable [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]].

## Key Events

### Headquarters of the Skimmity-Ride

Peter's Finger is the operational centre of the [[the-skimmity-ride|skimmity-ride]] — the cruel public procession that exposes [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta Templeman]]'s past and [[ambition-and-will|drive]]s her to her death. The planning, the reading aloud of [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s letters, and the gathering of the participants all occur within its walls:

> The reading of Lucetta's letters at Peter's Finger had been the spark. [044-116]

The inn functions as a court of rough justice, where evidence ([[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s letters) is presented, judgment is rendered, and sentence is carried out — all without the sanction or knowledge of official [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]].

### Charl's Domain

Peter's Finger is the haunt of [[charl|Charl]], the deposed keeper who is questioned by [[mr-grower|Mr. Grower]] during the investigation:

> The other than Charl, of Peter's Finger. Mr. Grower repeated the words. [046-187]

[[charl|Charl]]'s identification with [[the-three-mariners|the inn]] is so complete that he is simply "[[charl|Charl]], of Peter's Finger" — the place defines the man. When [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] later seeks information, his path leads inevitably to "Peter's Finger" [048-099], acknowledging [[the-three-mariners|the inn]]'s [[class-and-social-order|status]] as the true information hub of the [[durnover|lower town]].

## Literary Significance

Peter's Finger is the dark twin of [[the-kings-arms|The King's Arms]]. Where the [[the-kings-arms|King's Arms]] represents official [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] — commerce, [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]], [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]] — Peter's Finger represents its underside: the world of the dispossessed, the criminal, and the vengeful. The two inns bracket the novel's social spectrum, and the tragedy unfolds in the space between them.

The inn's name itself carries a sardonic quality. "Peter's Finger" — a name that invokes Saint Peter, the gatekeeper of heaven — is the gatekeeper of a very different kind of judgment. In Hardy's irony, the saint's finger points not toward salvation but toward exposure and destruction.
