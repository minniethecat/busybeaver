# Henchard's Bankruptcy

The economic climax of [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s tragedy — the moment when his commercial empire, built over two decades, collapses in a [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]gle season. His bankruptcy is the direct consequence of his character: the risky speculation, the refusal to adapt to modern methods, and the obsessive [[the-rivalry|competition]] with [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] that blinds him to bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess logic (030-060).

## Narrative Context

Henchard's bankruptcy is the economic translation of his psychological flaws. His [[pride]] prevents him from seeking advice; his [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] alienates his bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess contacts; his rivalry with [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] leads him into reckless bets that fail. The bankruptcy is not bad luck — it is character made visible in a balance sheet.

## Event Details

- **Catalyst**: Failed grain speculation based on faulty [[nature-and-landscape|weather]] prediction
- **Key moment**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s public declaration of bankruptcy
- **Aftermath**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] loses his house, his bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess, and his social position; he becomes a day labourer working for [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]

## Consequences

The bankruptcy completes the [[farfrae-becomes-mayor|transfer of power]] from [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] and reduces [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to the social position from which he started: a manual labourer. It is the most visible demonstration of the novel's thesis that character determines [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]].
