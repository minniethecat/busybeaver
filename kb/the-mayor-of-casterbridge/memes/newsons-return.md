# Newson's Return

[[richard-newson|Richard Newson]], [[richard-newson|the sailor]] who bought Susan at [[the-wife-auction|Weydon-Priors fair]] and was presumed lost at sea, returns to [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] to claim his daughter [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]. His return completes the cycle begun by [[susan-henchard|the wife]]-auction: the past that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] thought he had escaped has finally caught up with him.

## Narrative Context

Newson's return is the novel's final structural irony. The man who took [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s wife returns to take his daughter — and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], who once sold his [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]], now loses the [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]] he has tried to rebuild. [[richard-newson|Newson]]'s "[[pride]] in what she had grown up to be" (052-117) contrasts painfully with [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s inability to sustain a loving relationship.

## Consequences

[[richard-newson|Newson]]'s arrival precipitates [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s final crisis. Faced with lo[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]g [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] to her biological father, [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] lies — telling [[richard-newson|Newson]] she is dead — and then flees. The lie is discovered, and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s final isolation is complete.
