# Section 38

(037-001) For to this pair of bridges gravitated all the failures of [[casterbridge|the town]];

(037-002) those who had failed in business, in love, in sobriety, in crime. Why

(037-003) the unhappy hereabout usually chose the bridges for their meditations

(037-004) in preference to a railing, a gate, or a stile, was not so clear.

(037-005) There was a marked difference of quality between the personages who

(037-006) haunted the near bridge of brick and the personages who haunted the far

(037-007) one of stone. Those of lowest [[character-as-fate|character]] preferred the former, adjoining

(037-008) the town; they did not mind the glare of the public eye. They had been

(037-009) of comparatively no account during their successes; and though they

(037-010) might feel dispirited, they had no particular sense of shame in their

(037-011) ruin. Their hands were mostly kept in their pockets; they wore a

(037-012) leather strap round their hips or knees, and boots that required a

(037-013) great deal of lacing, but seemed never to get any. Instead of sighing

(037-014) at their adversities they spat, and instead of saying the iron had

(037-015) entered into their souls they said they were down on their luck. [[joshua-jopp|Jopp]]

(037-016) in his time of distress had often stood here; so had [[mother-cuxsom|Mother Cuxsom]],

(037-017) [[christopher-coney|Christopher Coney]], and poor [[abel-whittle|Abel Whittle]].

(037-018) The _misérables_ who would pause on the remoter bridge were of a

(037-019) politer stamp. They included bankrupts, hypochondriacs, persons who

(037-020) were what is called “out of a situation” from fault or lucklessness,

(037-021) the inefficient of the professional [[class-and-social-order|class]]—shabby-genteel men, who did

(037-022) not know how to get rid of the weary time between breakfast and dinner,

(037-023) and the yet more weary time between dinner and dark. The eye of this

(037-024) species were mostly directed over the parapet upon the running water

(037-025) below. A man seen there looking thus fixedly into [[the-river|the river]] was pretty

(037-026) sure to be one whom the world did not treat kindly for some reason or

(037-027) other. While one in straits on the townward bridge did not mind who saw

(037-028) him so, and kept his back to the parapet to survey the passers-by, one

(037-029) in straits on this never faced the road, never turned his head at

(037-030) coming footsteps, but, sensitive to his own condition, watched the

(037-031) current whenever a stranger approached, as if some strange fish

(037-032) interested him, though every finned thing had been poached out of the

(037-033) river years before.

(037-034) There and thus they would muse; if their [[regret-and-suffering|grief]] were the grief of

(037-035) oppression they would wish themselves kings; if their grief were

(037-036) poverty, wish themselves millionaires; if [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]], they would wish they

(037-037) were saints or angels; if despised love, that they were some

(037-038) much-courted Adonis of county fame. Some had been known to stand and

(037-039) think so long with this fixed gaze downward that eventually they had

(037-040) allowed their poor carcases to follow that gaze; and they were

(037-041) discovered the next morning out of reach of their troubles, either here

(037-042) or in the deep pool called Blackwater, a little higher up the river.

(037-043) To this bridge came [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], as other unfortunates had come before

(037-044) him, his way thither being by the riverside path on the chilly edge of

(037-045) the town. Here he was standing one windy afternoon when [[durnover|Durnover]] church

(037-046) clock struck five. While the gusts were bringing the notes to his ears

(037-047) across the damp intervening flat a man passed behind him and greeted

(037-048) Henchard by name. Henchard turned slightly and saw that the comer was

(037-049) Jopp, his old foreman, now employed elsewhere, to whom, though he hated

(037-050) him, he had gone for lodgings because Jopp was the one man in

(037-051) Casterbridge whose observation and opinion the fallen corn-merchant

(037-052) despised to the point of indifference.

(037-053) Henchard returned him a scarcely perceptible nod, and Jopp stopped.

(037-054) “He and she are gone into their new house to-day,” said Jopp.

(037-055) “Oh,” said Henchard absently. “Which house is that?”

(037-056) “Your old one.”

(037-057) “Gone into my house?” And starting up Henchard added, “_My_ house of

(037-058) all others in the town!”

(037-059) “Well, as somebody was sure to live there, and you couldn’t, it can do

(037-060) ’ee no harm that he’s the man.”

(037-061) It was quite true: he felt that it was doing him no harm. [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]], who

(037-062) had already taken the yards and stores, had acquired possession of the

(037-063) house for the obvious convenience of its contiguity. And yet this act

(037-064) of his taking up residence within those roomy chambers while he, their

(037-065) former tenant, lived in a cottage, galled Henchard indescribably.

(037-066) Jopp continued: “And you heard of that fellow who bought all the best

(037-067) furniture at your sale? He was bidding for no other than Farfrae all

(037-068) the while! It has never been moved out of the house, as he’d already

(037-069) got the lease.”

(037-070) “My furniture too! Surely he’ll buy my body and soul likewise!”

(037-071) “There’s no saying he won’t, if you be willing to sell.” And having

(037-072) planted these wounds in the heart of his once imperious master Jopp

(037-073) went on his way; while Henchard stared and stared into the racing river

(037-074) till [[the-bridge|the bridge]] seemed moving backward with him.

(037-075) The low land grew blacker, and the sky a deeper grey, When the

(037-076) landscape looked like a picture blotted in with ink, another traveller

(037-077) approached the great stone bridge. He was driving a gig, his direction

(037-078) being also townwards. On the round of the middle of the arch the gig

(037-079) stopped. “Mr. Henchard?” came from it in the voice of Farfrae. Henchard

(037-080) turned his face.

(037-081) Finding that he had guessed rightly Farfrae told the man who

(037-082) accompanied him to [[ambition-and-will|drive]] home; while he alighted and went up to his

(037-083) former friend.

(037-084) “I have heard that you think of emigrating, Mr. Henchard?” he said. “Is

(037-085) it true? I have a real reason for asking.”

(037-086) Henchard withheld his answer for several instants, and then said, “Yes;

(037-087) it is true. I am going where you were going to a few years ago, when I

(037-088) prevented you and got you to bide here. ’Tis turn and turn about, isn’t

(037-089) it! Do ye mind how we stood like this in the Chalk Walk when I

(037-090) persuaded ’ee to stay? You then stood without a chattel to your name,

(037-091) and I was the master of the house in Corn Street. But now I stand

(037-092) without a stick or a rag, and the master of that house is you.”

(037-093) “Yes, yes; that’s so! It’s the way o’ the warrld,” said Farfrae.

(037-094) “Ha, ha, true!” cried Henchard, throwing himself into a mood of

(037-095) jocularity. “Up and down! I’m used to it. What’s the odds after all!”

(037-096) “Now listen to me, if it’s no taking up your time,” said Farfrae, “just

(037-097) as I listened to you. Don’t go. Stay at home.”

(037-098) “But I can do nothing else, man!” said Henchard scornfully. “The little

(037-099) money I have will just keep body and soul together for a few weeks, and

(037-100) no more. I have not felt inclined to go back to journey-work yet; but I

(037-101) can’t stay doing nothing, and my best chance is elsewhere.”

(037-102) “No; but what I propose is this—if ye will listen. Come and live in

(037-103) your old house. We can spare some rooms very well—I am sure my wife

(037-104) would not mind it at all—until there’s an opening for ye.”

(037-105) Henchard started. Probably the picture drawn by the unsuspecting Donald

(037-106) of himself under the same roof with [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] was too striking to be

(037-107) received with equanimity. “No, no,” he said gruffly; “we should

(037-108) quarrel.”

(037-109) “You should hae a part to yourself,” said Farfrae; “and nobody to

(037-110) interfere wi’ you. It will be a deal healthier than down there by the

(037-111) river where you live now.”

(037-112) Still Henchard refused. “You don’t know what you ask,” he said.

(037-113) “However, I can do no less than thank ’ee.”

(037-114) They walked into the town together side by side, as they had done when

(037-115) Henchard persuaded [[farfraes-arrival|the young Scotchman]] to remain. “Will you come in and

(037-116) have some supper?” said Farfrae when they reached the middle of the

(037-117) town, where their paths diverged right and left.

(037-118) “No, no.”

(037-119) “By-the-bye, I had nearly forgot. I bought a good deal of your

(037-120) furniture.

(037-121) “So I have heard.”

(037-122) “Well, it was no that I wanted it so very much for myself; but I wish

(037-123) ye to pick out all that you care to have—such things as may be endeared

(037-124) to ye by associations, or particularly suited to your use. And take

(037-125) them to your own house—it will not be depriving me, we can do with less

(037-126) very well, and I will have plenty of opportunities of getting more.”

(037-127) “What—give it to me for nothing?” said Henchard. “But you paid the

(037-128) creditors for it!”

(037-129) “Ah, yes; but maybe it’s worth more to you than it is to me.”

(037-130) Henchard was a little moved. “I—sometimes think I’ve wronged ’ee!” he

(037-131) said, in tones which showed the disquietude that the night shades hid

(037-132) in his face. He shook Farfrae abruptly by the hand, and hastened away

(037-133) as if unwilling to betray himself further. Farfrae saw him turn through

(037-134) the thoroughfare into Bull Stake and vanish down towards the Priory

(037-135) Mill.

(037-136) Meanwhile [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], in an upper room no larger than the Prophet’s

(037-137) chamber, and with the silk attire of her palmy days packed away in a

(037-138) box, was netting with great industry between the hours which she

(037-139) devoted to studying such books as she could get hold of.

(037-140) Her lodgings being nearly opposite her stepfather’s former residence,

(037-141) now Farfrae’s, she could see Donald and Lucetta speeding in and out of

(037-142) their door with all the bounding enthusiasm of their situation. She

(037-143) avoided looking that way as much as possible, but it was hardly in

(037-144) human nature to keep the eyes averted when the door slammed.

(037-145) While living on thus quietly she heard the news that Henchard had

(037-146) caught cold and was confined to his room—possibly a result of standing

(037-147) about the meads in damp weather. She went off to his house at once.

(037-148) This time she was determined not to be denied admittance, and made her

(037-149) way upstairs. He was sitting up in the bed with a greatcoat round him,

(037-150) and at first resented her intrusion. “Go away—go away,” he said. “I

(037-151) don’t like to see ’ee!”

(037-152) “But, father—”

(037-153) “I don’t like to see ’ee,” he repeated.

(037-154) However, the ice was broken, and she remained. She made the room more

(037-155) comfortable, gave directions to the people below, and by the time she

(037-156) went away had reconciled her stepfather to her visiting him.

(037-157) The effect, either of her ministrations or of her mere presence, was a

(037-158) rapid recovery. He soon was well enough to go out; and now things

(037-159) seemed to wear a new colour in his eyes. He no longer thought of

(037-160) emigration, and thought more of Elizabeth. The having nothing to do

(037-161) made him more dreary than any other circumstance; and one day, with

(037-162) better views of Farfrae than he had held for some time, and a sense

(037-163) that honest work was not a thing to be ashamed of, he stoically went

(037-164) down to Farfrae’s yard and asked to be taken on as a journeyman

(037-165) hay-trusser. He was engaged at once. This hiring of Henchard was done

(037-166) through a foreman, Farfrae feeling that it was undesirable to come

(037-167) personally in contact with the ex-corn-factor more than was absolutely

(037-168) necessary. While anxious to help him he was well aware by this time of

(037-169) his uncertain [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], and thought reserved relations best. For the same

(037-170) reason his orders to Henchard to proceed to this and that country farm

(037-171) trussing in the usual way were always given through a third person.

(037-172) For a time these arrangements worked well, it being the custom to truss

(037-173) in the respective stack-yards, before bringing it away, the hay bought

(037-174) at the different farms about the neighbourhood; so that Henchard was

(037-175) often absent at such places the whole week long. When this was all

(037-176) done, and Henchard had become in a measure broken in, he came to work

(037-177) daily on the home premises like the rest. And thus the once flourishing

(037-178) merchant and Mayor and what not stood as a day-labourer in the barns

(037-179) and granaries he formerly had owned.
