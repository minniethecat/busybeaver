# Marriage and Domestic Life

Marriage in The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is both a plot engine and a thematic laboratory. The novel opens with a wife-sale — the most extreme violation of the marital bond imaginable — and proceeds through remarriage, estrangement, and domestic rearrangement, u[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]g the institution to explore questions of obligation, identity, and [[class-and-social-order|social order]].

## Overview

Hardy uses marriage to map the tensions between individual desire and social constraint. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "early imprudent marriage" (001-004) sets the novel in motion; his strategic remarriage to Susan (019-055) represents an attempt to repair the past through legal form; and his relationship with [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] exposes the gulf between passion and [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]]. Throughout, marriage functions as a legal and economic institution that characters must navigate, not a romantic ideal.

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **The wife-auction**: The novel opens with [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], "out of [[temper-and-impulse|temper]]" and drink, selling his wife Susan at a fair — an act that inverts marriage into property transaction and generates the entire subsequent plot.
- **Remarriage as repair**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "re-marriage" to Susan (019-055) is an attempt to retroactively legitimize their relationship and protect [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]. Hardy treats this remarriage with irony — legal form cannot undo two decades of separation.
- **Susan's reluctance**: Susan "did not like the idea of repeating our marriage" (012-038), revealing marriage as an institution [[gender-and-power|women]] endure rather than choose.
- **[[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] and [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]]**: [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s predicament — a woman of ambiguous marital [[class-and-social-order|status]] in a provincial town — shows marriage functioning as social protection rather than emotional bond.
- **[[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]'s position**: As a product of uncertain parentage moving between households, [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] embodies the consequences of marital instability for the next generation.

## Literary Analysis

### Marriage as Social Microcosm

Hardy uses marriage to expose the economic and legal underpinnings of Victorian social life. The wife-auction literalizes what the novel suggests is always true: marriage is a transaction, and [[gender-and-power|women]] are vulnerable within it. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess failures and domestic failures mirror each other — both stem from the same character flaws.

### The Limits of Legal Form

The novel is skeptical about law's ability to govern intimate life. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s remarriage to Susan satisfies legal form but cannot restore emotional substance. Hardy, writing in an era when divorce required an Act of Parliament, understood marriage law as a blunt instrument applied to delicate human relations.
