# The Royal Visit

A royal personage's visit to [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] provides the stage for one of [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s most painful public [[pride|humiliation]]s. When [[casterbridge|the town]] council selects [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] rather than [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to organize the reception, the slight is both professional and personal — a public declaration that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[power-and-authority|authority]] has been superseded (027-001).

## Narrative Context

The royal visit is the novel's most concentrated scene of public ceremony and private agony. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], who once commanded such occasions as [[power-and-authority|mayor]], is reduced to watching from the crowd as [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] takes his place. The event crystallizes the novel's concern with [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]] and public performance: [[power-and-authority|authority]], once lost, cannot be reclaimed by force of [[ambition-and-will|will]].

## Consequences

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s exclusion from the royal reception is a turning point in his psychological decline. It is not the largest of his losses, but it is the most public, and therefore among the most painful. His subsequent behaviour — attempting to force his way into the proceedings — only deepens his [[pride|humiliation]].
