# Pride and Humiliation

Pride is the engine of [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]]'s tragedy — the force that propels his improbable rise from hay-trusser to [[power-and-authority|mayor]], and the flaw that ensures his destruction. In The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]], pride operates not as simple vanity but as a complex defensive response to shame and the terror of being seen as diminished.

## Overview

Hardy traces pride's dual [[nature-and-landscape|nature]] through [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s arc: it is both his strength and his undoing. The same pride that [[ambition-and-will|drive]]s him to abstain from alcohol for twenty-one years and build a successful bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess also makes him incapable of [[henchards-reconciliation-attempt|apology]], [[guilt-and-redemption|forgiveness]], or strategic retreat. When his position is threatened, pride transforms into "cursed pride and mortification" (053-066) — a self-lacerating compound that prevents the one action that might save him: humility.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Pride manifests in [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s relationships with every major character:

- **As Mayor**: His "natural pride as Mayor of" [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] (009-032) is wounded by any challenge to his [[power-and-authority|authority]], making him react with anger rather than calculation.
- **With [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s pride "fettered" him "sufficiently to accept the smallest" (050-041) kindness, yet the same pride prevents him from acknowledging [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s superior bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess acumen.
- **With [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]**: "Pride itself wore the garments of humility" (050-047) in his treatment of his stepdaughter — a passage revealing how pride can masquerade as other emotions.
- **With [[richard-newson|Newson]]**: [[richard-newson|Newson]]'s "pride in what she had grown up to be" (052-117) mirrors [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s own paternal pride, creating a triangulation of competing claims.
- **The final confession**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "cursed pride and mortification at being poor" (053-066) is revealed as the root of his inability to seek help — the terminal expression of his tragic flaw.

## Literary Analysis

### Pride as Hamartia

Classical tragedy requires a hamartia — a fatal flaw — and Hardy provides one in [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s pride. But Hardy's treatment is distinctively modern: rather than a [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]gle [[fate-and-determinism|fate]]ful error, pride expresses itself as a pattern of self-defeating behavior that compounds over time. Each proud refusal closes off a path of recovery.

### Victorian Masculinity

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s pride is [[gender-and-power|gender]]ed — it is specifically male pride, tied to Victorian expectations of masculine [[power-and-authority|authority]], economic independence, and public [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]]. His inability to be seen as diminished is inseparable from the era's construction of manhood.
