# The Ring

"The Ring at [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] was merely the local name of" an ancient Roman amphitheatre (011-063) — an overgrown ruin at [[casterbridge|the town]]'s edge that Hardy transforms into one of the novel's most resonant symbolic spaces. It is where secrets are told, where past and present collide, and where [[casterbridge|the town]]'s hidden life surfaces.

## Description

Hardy describes the Ring as a place where "the shades of the old Romans" still linger — a space outside ordinary time and social convention. Its overgrown condition makes it a marginal space, neither fully within [[casterbridge|the town]] nor fully outside it, which is precisely what makes it suitable for the clandestine meetings that occur there.

## Narrative Function

The Ring serves as the novel's threshold space — a zone where social rules are suspended and characters can say what they cannot say elsewhere. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] and [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] meet here to discuss their past relationship; later, it becomes the site of the [[the-skimmity-ride|skimmity-ride]] spectacle that destroys [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]. The Ring's trajectory from secret meeting-place to [[the-skimmity-ride|public shaming]]-stage mirrors the novel's arc from concealment to exposure.
