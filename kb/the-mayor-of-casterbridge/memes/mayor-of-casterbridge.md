# Mayor of Casterbridge

The office of Mayor in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is the novel's central symbol of civic [[power-and-authority|authority]] and social legitimacy. It is the position to which [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] rises through twenty-one years of disciplined [[ambition-and-will|ambition]], and from which he falls through the same force of [[character-as-fate|character]] that propelled his ascent. The mayoralty represents not merely political office but the summit of [[reputation-and-social-standing|social standing]] in a town where "reputation is currency" and [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]] is power.

## Overview

The mayoralty serves as the structural pivot of the novel's social architecture. In [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s hierarchical world, the Mayor occupies a position of quasi-patriarchal authority — presiding over the [[the-kings-arms|King's Arms]]'s grand dinners, adjudicating disputes at the [[the-market-place|market]], and embodying the town's [[tradition-and-change|traditional]] values. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s tenure as Mayor represents the old order: personal, impulsive, patriarchal. [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s succession represents the new: methodical, commercial, modern.

> "You see now that the Mayor of Casterbridge is not so" [013-036]

## The Two Mayors

| Mayor | Style | Era | Significance |
|-------|-------|-----|-------------|
| [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] | Impulsive, personal, patriarchal | Old Casterbridge | [[character-as-fate|Character]]-driven authority, tragic fall |
| [[donald-farfrae|Donald Farfrae]] | Methodical, commercial, modern | New Casterbridge | Competence-based authority, steady ascent |

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]] achieves the mayoralty through the same [[ambition-and-will|will]] that ultimately undoes him. His authority is personal rather than institutional — it depends on his physical presence, his [[temper-and-impulse|temper]], his capacity to dominate a room. When his [[henchards-bankruptcy|fortunes]] collapse, so does his claim to office. The position cannot be separated from the man.

> "He was years younger than the Mayor of Casterbridge; fair, fresh, and" [026-009]

[[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s accession to the mayoralty — documented in [[farfrae-becomes-mayor|Farfrae Becomes Mayor]] — completes the novel's generational transfer of [[power-and-authority|power]]. Where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] ruled through force of personality, [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] administers through competence. The office itself is unchanged; what has changed is the [[tradition-and-change|world]] around it.

## Symbolic Function

The mayoralty in Hardy's novel serves three interlocking symbolic functions:

- **Social summit**: The highest rung of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s [[class-and-social-order|social ladder]], representing everything [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] sought when he swore his [[henchards-abstinence-vow|vow of abstinence]].
- **Moral barometer**: The gap between the dignity of the office and the frailty of its holder measures the novel's central concern with [[appearance-and-reality|appearance versus reality]].
- **Historical marker**: The transition from [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] to [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] encodes the larger historical shift from [[tradition-and-change|traditional]] patriarchal authority to modern commercial [[power-and-authority|governance]].
