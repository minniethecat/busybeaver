# Charl

A deposed keeper associated with Peter's Finger — a tavern or locality on the margins of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] — who appears in the later chapters as a witness during the investigation of the skimmington-ride. With his comrade Joe, Charl represents the rough, semi-lawless world that exists at the edges of [[casterbridge|the town]]'s official life.

## Character Overview

Charl is introduced in conversation with another marginal figure, discus[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]g poaching and the [[tradition-and-change|old ways]] of the countryside:

> "Why don't ye ruffle the stream, Charl?" a deposed keeper was saying. [042-157]

The description "deposed keeper" immediately establishes Charl's position: he once held a legitimate role (gamekeeper) but has lost it, placing him among the dispossessed who populate the novel's margins. His world is that of Peter's Finger — a place outside the respectable geography of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] proper.

## Role in the Skimmington Investigation

Charl's most significant appearance comes during the investigation of the skimmington-ride that humiliates [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] and hastens her death. When [[mr-grower|Mr. Grower]] and [[constable-stubberd|the constable]]s come to Peter's Finger seeking the perpetrators, Charl is questioned:

> The other than Charl, of Peter's Finger. Mr. Grower repeated the words. [046-187]

Charl's responses are evasive — "Charl shook his head to the zero of nothing" [047-001] — embodying the lower-[[class-and-social-order|class]] code of silence in the face of official inquiry:

> "I haven't seen anything; have we, Joe? And you was here afore I." [047-002]

His comrade Joe echoes this refusal. Together they represent the uncooperative, self-protective instinct of the poor when confronted by the law — a theme that runs through Hardy's fiction.

## Character Analysis

### A Voice from the Margins

Charl speaks in dialect — "There's a river afore 'ee" [043-051] — and his speech patterns mark him as belonging to a different [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] than the one inhabited by [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] or [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]. He is part of what Hardy presents as an older, rougher rural world that persists alongside the commercial modernity the novel charts.

### Narrative Function

Charl functions in the novel as an obstacle to official truth-finding. The skimmington-ride is a communal act of cruelty, and the community — particularly its lower orders — closes [[class-and-social-order|rank]]s to protect its own. Charl's silence before [[mr-grower|Mr. Grower]] is not just personal evasiveness but a structural feature of the [[class-and-social-order|class]] divide: the law cannot penetrate Peter's Finger, and truth dissolves at the boundary between official and unofficial [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]].
