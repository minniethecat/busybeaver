# Regret and Suffering

If [[pride]] is the cause of [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s tragedy and [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] its mechanism, regret is its emotional substance. The Mayor of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] is a novel saturated with regret — for [[susan-henchard|the wife]] sold, the friend betrayed, the daughter lost. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s suffering is not redemptive; it simply accumulates, a weight that bends every relationship and darkens every subsequent action.

## Overview

Hardy's treatment of suffering is characteristically unsentimental. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s remorse is genuine — his "remorse" is described as "abandonment" (045-139) — but it does not lead to moral transformation. Instead, his regret feeds back into the same character flaws that caused it: [[pride]] prevents [[henchards-reconciliation-attempt|apology]], [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] triggers fresh injuries, and the cycle deepens. Suffering in Hardy is not educative; it is simply the condition of being human.

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **The wife-auction's aftermath**: Every relationship in the novel is shadowed by the opening crime. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s remorse is the novel's bass note, present even when unmentioned.
- **Regret and its limits**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s regret is real — his "remorse" is acknowledged by the narrator (045-139) — but it does not produce reformation. He regrets without changing, and this is his tragedy.
- **Suffering as atmosphere**: The novel's tone — its "tragic light" (030-018) — establishes suffering not as an event but as a condition. Characters suffer not because of what happens but because of who they are.
- **The [[the-furmity-woman|furmity-woman]]'s return**: The reappearance of the furmity-seller as witness to [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s past crime brings regret crashing into the present, showing that the past is never truly past.

## Literary Analysis

### Tragedy Without Catharsis

Classical tragedy promises catharsis — the purgation of pity and fear. Hardy's tragedy offers no such release. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s suffering produces neither wisdom nor [[guilt-and-redemption|redemption]]; it simply ends. This refusal of consolation is perhaps the novel's most radical feature.

### The Ethics of Regret

Hardy raises an uncomfortable question: what is regret worth if it does not lead to [[tradition-and-change|change]]? [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s remorse is [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]cere but sterile — a feeling that consumes without transforming. This is the Naturalist critique of moral sentiment: feeling is not action, and character is not reformed by suffering alone.
