# High-Place Hall

[[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta Templeman]]'s [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] residence — a house chosen for its commanding position but rendered dangerous by its very visibility. High-Place Hall embodies the double-edged [[nature-and-landscape|nature]] of social [[class-and-social-order|status]] in the novel: it elevates but also exposes.

## Description

High-Place Hall is described as one of [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s finest houses, chosen by [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] for its prestige. But its elevated position and large windows — emblems of [[class-and-social-order|status]] — become instruments of exposure when the [[the-skimmity-ride|skimmity-ride]] passes by, making [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s private shame visible to the entire town. The architecture of [[class-and-social-order|status]] becomes the architecture of destruction.

## Narrative Function

The house functions as a symbol of [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s social position and its fragility. She chooses it to establish herself in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] society, but its visibility — the very quality that makes it desirable — ensures that her exposure, when it comes, [[ambition-and-will|will]] be total. The scene where "the people can look in" (040-026) captures the house's transformation from sanctuary to stage.
