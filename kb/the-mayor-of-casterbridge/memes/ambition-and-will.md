# Ambition and Will

[[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]] is one of Hardy's most will-driven protagonists — a man whose ambition propels him from poverty to the [[power-and-authority|mayor]]'s office through sheer force of personality. But will, in Hardy's Naturalist universe, is not an unqualified virtue. The same determination that builds a fortune can destroy it when it becomes indistinguishable from stubbornness and [[pride]].

## Overview

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s ambition is introduced through negation: his "energies" were blunted "by an early imprudent [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]]" (001-004). The novel's opening implies that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s natural drive was always exceptional — it simply lacked the right channel. His twenty-one-year vow of abstinence, his rise to [[power-and-authority|mayor]], and his [[power-and-authority|dominance]] of the [[rural-economics|corn trade]] all testify to extraordinary will[[gender-and-power|power]]. But will without judgment is merely momentum, and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s "character knew no patience" (031-112).

## Manifestation in the Novel

- **The twenty-one-year vow**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s abstinence from alcohol after [[susan-henchard|the wife]]-auction is an act of monumental will — proof that his ambition can override even his [[temper-and-impulse|impulse]]s. But the vow is brittle; when it breaks, it breaks absolutely.
- **Bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess ambition**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s rise as "corn-factor and" merchant (009-109) reflects genuine commercial acumen married to relentless drive. His ambition to be [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]]'s leading citizen is achieved — then immediately threatened by [[farfraes-arrival|Farfrae's arrival]].
- **Competitive will**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s rivalry with [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] transforms from professional [[the-rivalry|competition]] into a test of will. He cannot tolerate being second, and this intolerance becomes self-destructive.
- **Will without wisdom**: The novel's tragedy is that [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s will, his greatest asset, operates without the [[temper-and-impulse|temper]]ing qualities — patience, foresight, humility — that would make it sustainable.

## Literary Analysis

### The Victorian Self-Made Man

[[michael-henchard|Henchard]] participates in the Victorian myth of the self-made man — the individual who rises through effort and character. But Hardy subverts the myth: the same qualities that enable the rise ensure the fall. There is no stable equilibrium for [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]; his trajectory is inherently unstable.

### Will as Natural Force

Hardy presents [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s will as something almost geological — a force of [[nature-and-landscape|nature]] that reshapes the social [[nature-and-landscape|landscape]] around it. But like all natural forces, it is subject to laws it cannot perceive. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s will can move [[the-market-place|market]]s but cannot [[power-and-authority|control]] [[nature-and-landscape|weather]]; it can win loyalty but cannot keep it.
