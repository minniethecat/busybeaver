# Section 45

(044-001) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] had determined to do no work that day. He primed himself in

(044-002) the morning with a glass of rum, and walking down the street met

(044-003) [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], whom he had not seen for a week. “It was lucky,” he

(044-004) said to her, “my twenty-one years had expired before this came on, or I

(044-005) should never have had the nerve to carry it out.”

(044-006) “Carry out what?” said she, alarmed.

(044-007) “This welcome I am going to give our Royal visitor.”

(044-008) She was perplexed. “Shall we go and see it together?” she said.

(044-009) “See it! I have other fish to fry. You see it. It [[ambition-and-will|will]] be worth

(044-010) seeing!”

(044-011) She could do nothing to elucidate this, and decked herself out with a

(044-012) heavy heart. As the appointed time drew near she got sight again of her

(044-013) stepfather. She thought he was going to the [[the-three-mariners|Three Mariners]]; but no, he

(044-014) elbowed his way through the gay throng to the shop of Woolfrey, the

(044-015) draper. She waited in the crowd without.

(044-016) In a few minutes he emerged, wearing, to her surprise, a brilliant

(044-017) rosette, while more surprising still, in his hand he carried a flag of

(044-018) somewhat homely construction, formed by tacking one of the small Union

(044-019) Jacks, which abounded in [[casterbridge|the town]] to-day, to the end of a deal

(044-020) wand—probably the roller from a piece of calico. Henchard rolled up his

(044-021) flag on the doorstep, put it under his arm, and went down the street.

(044-022) Suddenly the taller members of the crowd turned their heads, and the

(044-023) shorter stood on tiptoe. It was said that the Royal _cortège_

(044-024) approached. The railway had stretched out an arm towards Casterbridge

(044-025) at this time, but had not reached it by several miles as yet; so that

(044-026) the intervening distance, as well as the remainder of the journey, was

(044-027) to be traversed by road in the old fashion. People thus waited—the

(044-028) county families in their carriages, the masses on foot—and watched the

(044-029) far-stretching London highway to the ringing of bells and chatter of

(044-030) tongues.

(044-031) From the background Elizabeth-Jane watched the scene. Some seats had

(044-032) been arranged from which ladies could witness the spectacle, and the

(044-033) front seat was occupied by [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]], the Mayor’s wife, just at present.

(044-034) In the road under her eyes stood Henchard. She appeared so bright and

(044-035) pretty that, as it seemed, he was experiencing the momentary weakness

(044-036) of wishing for her notice. But he was far from attractive to a woman’s

(044-037) eye, ruled as that is so largely by the superficies of things. He was

(044-038) not only a journeyman, unable to appear as he formerly had appeared,

(044-039) but he disdained to appear as well as he might. Everybody else, from

(044-040) the Mayor to the washerwoman, shone in new vesture according to means;

(044-041) but Henchard had doggedly retained the fretted and weather-beaten

(044-042) garments of bygone years.

(044-043) Hence, alas, this occurred: Lucetta’s eyes slid over him to this side

(044-044) and to that without anchoring on his features—as gaily dressed [[gender-and-power|women]]’s

(044-045) eyes will too often do on such occasions. Her manner signified quite

(044-046) plainly that she meant to know him in public no more.

(044-047) But she was never tired of watching Donald, as he stood in animated

(044-048) converse with his friends a few yards off, wearing round his young neck

(044-049) the official gold chain with great square links, like that round the

(044-050) Royal unicorn. Every trifling emotion that her husband showed as he

(044-051) talked had its reflex on her face and lips, which moved in little

(044-052) duplicates to his. She was living his part rather than her own, and

(044-053) cared for no one’s situation but [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s that day.

(044-054) At length a man stationed at the furthest turn of the high road,

(044-055) namely, on the second bridge of which mention has been made, gave a

(044-056) signal, and the Corporation in their robes proceeded from the front of

(044-057) the Town Hall to the archway erected at the entrance to the town. The

(044-058) carriages containing the Royal visitor and his suite arrived at the

(044-059) spot in a cloud of dust, a procession was formed, and the whole came on

(044-060) to the Town Hall at a walking pace.

(044-061) This spot was the centre of interest. There were a few clear yards in

(044-062) front of the Royal carriage, sanded; and into this space a man stepped

(044-063) before any one could prevent him. It was Henchard. He had unrolled his

(044-064) private flag, and removing his hat he staggered to the side of the

(044-065) slowing vehicle, waving the Union Jack to and fro with his left hand

(044-066) while he blandly held out his right to the Illustrious Personage.

(044-067) All the ladies said with bated breath, “O, look there!” and Lucetta was

(044-068) ready to faint. Elizabeth-Jane peeped through the shoulders of those in

(044-069) front, saw what it was, and was terrified; and then her interest in the

(044-070) spectacle as a strange phenomenon got the better of her fear.

(044-071) Farfrae, with Mayoral [[power-and-authority|authority]], immediately rose to the occasion. He

(044-072) seized Henchard by the shoulder, dragged him back, and told him roughly

(044-073) to be off. Henchard’s eyes met his, and Farfrae observed the fierce

(044-074) light in them despite his excitement and irritation. For a moment

(044-075) Henchard stood his ground rigidly; then by an unaccountable [[temper-and-impulse|impulse]]

(044-076) gave way and retired. Farfrae glanced to the ladies’ gallery, and saw

(044-077) that his Calphurnia’s cheek was pale.

(044-078) “Why—it is your husband’s old patron!” said Mrs. Blowbody, a lady of

(044-079) the neighbourhood who sat beside Lucetta.

(044-080) “Patron!” said Donald’s wife with quick indignation.

(044-081) “Do you say the man is an acquaintance of Mr. Farfrae’s?” observed Mrs.

(044-082) Bath, the physician’s wife, a new-comer to the town through her recent

(044-083) [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]] with the doctor.

(044-084) “He works for my husband,” said Lucetta.

(044-085) “Oh—is that all? They have been saying to me that it was through him

(044-086) your husband first got a footing in Casterbridge. What stories people

(044-087) will tell!”

(044-088) “They will indeed. It was not so at all. Donald’s genius would have

(044-089) enabled him to get a footing anywhere, without anybody’s help! He would

(044-090) have been just the same if there had been no Henchard in the world!”

(044-091) It was partly Lucetta’s ignorance of the circumstances of Donald’s

(044-092) arrival which led her to speak thus, partly the sensation that

(044-093) everybody seemed bent on snubbing her at this triumphant time. The

(044-094) incident had occupied but a few moments, but it was necessarily

(044-095) witnessed by the Royal Personage, who, however, with practised tact

(044-096) affected not to have noticed anything unusual. He alighted, the Mayor

(044-097) advanced, the address was read; the Illustrious Personage replied, then

(044-098) said a few words to Farfrae, and shook hands with Lucetta as the

(044-099) Mayor’s wife. The ceremony occupied but a few minutes, and the

(044-100) carriages rattled heavily as Pharaoh’s chariots down Corn Street and

(044-101) out upon the [[budmouth|Budmouth]] Road, in continuation of the journey coastward.

(044-102) In the crowd stood [[christopher-coney|Coney]], Buzzford, and [[solomon-longways|Longways]]. “Some difference

(044-103) between him now and when he zung at the Dree Mariners,” said the first.

(044-104) “’Tis wonderful how he could get a lady of her quality to go snacks wi’

(044-105) en in such quick time.”

(044-106) “True. Yet how folk do worship fine clothes! Now there’s a

(044-107) better-looking woman than she that nobody notices at all, because she’s

(044-108) akin to that hontish fellow Henchard.”

(044-109) “I could worship ye, Buzz, for saying that,” remarked [[nance-mockridge|Nance Mockridge]].

(044-110) “I do like to see the trimming pulled off such Christmas candles. I am

(044-111) quite unequal to the part of villain myself, or I’d gi’e all my small

(044-112) silver to see that lady toppered.... And perhaps I shall soon,” she

(044-113) added significantly.

(044-114) “That’s not a noble passiont for a ’oman to keep up,” said Longways.

(044-115) Nance did not reply, but every one knew what she meant. The ideas

(044-116) diffused by the reading of Lucetta’s letters at Peter’s Finger had

(044-117) condensed into a scandal, which was spreading like a miasmatic fog

(044-118) through [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]], and thence up the back streets of Casterbridge.

(044-119) The mixed assemblage of idlers known to each other presently fell apart

(044-120) into two bands by a process of natural selection, the frequenters of

(044-121) Peter’s Finger going off Mixen Lanewards, where most of them lived,

(044-122) while Coney, Buzzford, Longways, and that connection remained in the

(044-123) street.

(044-124) “You know what’s brewing down there, I suppose?” said Buzzford

(044-125) mysteriously to the others.

(044-126) Coney looked at him. “Not the [[the-skimmity-ride|skimmity-ride]]?”

(044-127) Buzzford nodded.

(044-128) “I have my doubts if it will be carried out,” said Longways. “If they

(044-129) are getting it up they are keeping it mighty close.

(044-130) “I heard they were thinking of it a fortnight ago, at all events.”

(044-131) “If I were sure o’t I’d lay information,” said Longways emphatically.

(044-132) “’Tis too rough a joke, and apt to wake riots in towns. We know that

(044-133) the Scotchman is a right enough man, and that his lady has been a right

(044-134) enough ’oman since she came here, and if there was anything wrong about

(044-135) her afore, that’s their business, not ours.”

(044-136) Coney reflected. Farfrae was still liked in the community; but it must

(044-137) be owned that, as the Mayor and man of money, engrossed with affairs

(044-138) and ambitions, he had lost in the eyes of the poorer inhabitants

(044-139) something of that wondrous charm which he had had for them as a

(044-140) light-hearted penniless young man, who sang ditties as readily as the

(044-141) birds in the trees. Hence the anxiety to keep him from annoyance showed

(044-142) not quite the ardour that would have animated it in former days.

(044-143) “Suppose we make inquiration into it, Christopher,” continued Longways;

(044-144) “and if we find there’s really anything in it, drop a letter to them

(044-145) most concerned, and advise ’em to keep out of the way?”

(044-146) This course was decided on, and the group separated, Buzzford saying to

(044-147) Coney, “Come, my ancient friend; let’s move on. There’s nothing more to

(044-148) see here.”

(044-149) These well-intentioned ones would have been surprised had they known

(044-150) how ripe the great jocular plot really was. “Yes, to-night,” [[joshua-jopp|Jopp]] had

(044-151) said to the Peter’s party at the corner of Mixen Lane. “As a wind-up to

(044-152) the Royal visit the hit will be all the more pat by reason of their

(044-153) great elevation to-day.”

(044-154) To him, at least, it was not a joke, but a retaliation.

(044-156) The proceedings had been brief—too brief—to Lucetta whom an

(044-157) intoxicating _Weltlust_ had fairly mastered; but they had brought her a

(044-158) great triumph nevertheless. The shake of the Royal hand still lingered

(044-159) in her fingers; and the chit-chat she had overheard, that her husband

(044-160) might possibly receive the [[reputation-and-social-standing|honour]] of knighthood, though idle to a

(044-161) degree, seemed not the wildest vision; stranger things had occurred to

(044-162) men so good and captivating as her Scotchman was.

(044-163) After the collision with the Mayor, Henchard had withdrawn behind the

(044-164) ladies’ stand; and there he stood, regarding with a stare of

(044-165) abstraction the spot on the lapel of his coat where Farfrae’s hand had

(044-166) seized it. He put his own hand there, as if he could hardly realize

(044-167) such an outrage from one whom it had once been his wont to treat with

(044-168) ardent generosity. While pausing in this half-stupefied state the

(044-169) conversation of Lucetta with the other ladies reached his ears; and he

(044-170) distinctly heard her deny him—deny that he had assisted Donald, that he

(044-171) was anything more than a common journeyman.

(044-172) He moved on homeward, and met Jopp in the archway to the Bull Stake.

(044-173) “So you’ve had a snub,” said Jopp.

(044-174) “And what if I have?” answered Henchard sternly.

(044-175) “Why, I’ve had one too, so we are both under the same cold shade.” He

(044-176) briefly related his attempt to win Lucetta’s intercession.

(044-177) Henchard merely heard his story, without taking it deeply in. His own

(044-178) relation to Farfrae and Lucetta overshadowed all kindred ones. He went

(044-179) on saying brokenly to himself, “She has supplicated to me in her time;

(044-180) and now her tongue won’t own me nor her eyes see me!... And he—how

(044-181) angry he looked. He drove me back as if I were a bull breaking

(044-182) fence.... I took it like a lamb, for I saw it could not be settled

(044-183) there. He can rub brine on a green wound!... But he shall pay for it,

(044-184) and she shall be sorry. It must come to a tussle—face to face; and then

(044-185) we’ll see how a coxcomb can front a man!”
