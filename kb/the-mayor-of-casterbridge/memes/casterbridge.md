# Casterbridge

The fictional [[nature-and-landscape|Wessex]] town that serves as the primary setting of The Mayor of Casterbridge. Modelled closely on Dorchester in Dorset, Casterbridge is more than backdrop — it is a living community whose rhythms of commerce, [[class-and-social-order|class]], and gossip shape the novel's action and embody its [[tradition-and-change|thematic concerns]] with the tension between [[tradition-and-change|old ways]] and new.

## Setting and Geography

Casterbridge is a county town, "the complement of the [[rural-economics|rural life]] about it" — a [[the-market-place|market]] centre surrounded by agricultural villages. Its geography encodes its social structure: the high street with its corn ex[[tradition-and-change|change]] and [[the-kings-arms|King's Arms]] represents commerce and [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]]; [[durnover|Durnover]] houses the poor; [[high-place-hall|High-Place Hall]] commands the town from its elevated position. The [[the-three-mariners|Three Mariners]] inn and its skimmington-ride [[tradition-and-change|tradition]] represent the older, rougher [[nature-and-landscape|Wessex]] that modern Casterbridge is leaving behind.

> "Casterbridge was the complement of the rural life about it, not its urban opposite." [008-055]

## The Town as Social World

Casterbridge's society is the novel's true subject. In a town where "everyone knew everyone else's bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess," [[appearance-and-reality|reputation]] is currency and exposure is catastrophe. The town's institutions structure the plot:

| Institution | Role in the Novel |
|------------|-------------------|
| The Corn Ex[[tradition-and-change|change]] | Economic arena where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] and [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] compete for [[power-and-authority|dominance]] |
| [[the-kings-arms|The King's Arms]] | The respectable inn where civic [[gender-and-power|power]] is exercised and displayed |
| [[the-three-mariners|The Three Mariners]] | The rougher inn where old [[nature-and-landscape|Wessex]] survives and [[the-skimmity-ride|the skimmity-ride]] is planned |
| The Court-House | Where [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] sits as magistrate and is later exposed |
| [[the-market-place|The Market-Place]] | The public stage where characters perform their social roles |

## The Town and Theme

Casterbridge is Hardy's study of a community in transition. The old agricultural order — represented by [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s patriarchal [[power-and-authority|authority]] and the [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]] folk-customs — is being displaced by [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]'s modern methods, the railway's arrival, and the impersonality of commercial contracts. The town's gossip network functions as a [[fate-and-determinism|mechanism of fate]]: secrets cannot stay hidden in Casterbridge, and the truth — about [[the-wife-auction|the wife-sale]], about [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]'s past, about [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s character — [[ambition-and-will|will]] out.

> "In a town where everyone knew everyone else's business, a secret was a fever that must break." (cf. 008-060)
