# Section 43

(042-001) “It is a thing I know nothing about,” said [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]] coldly.

(042-002) “But you can testify to my trustworthiness better than anybody, ma’am,”

(042-003) said [[joshua-jopp|Jopp]]. “I was in Jersey several years, and knew you there by

(042-004) sight.”

(042-005) “Indeed,” she replied. “But I knew nothing of you.”

(042-006) “I think, ma’am, that a word or two from you would secure for me what I

(042-007) covet very much,” he persisted.

(042-008) She steadily refused to have anything to do with the affair, and

(042-009) cutting him short, because of her anxiety to get indoors before her

(042-010) husband should miss her, left him on the pavement.

(042-011) He watched her till she had vanished, and then went home. When he got

(042-012) there he sat down in the fireless chimney corner looking at the iron

(042-013) dogs, and the wood laid across them for heating the morning kettle. A

(042-014) movement upstairs disturbed him, and [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] came down from his

(042-015) bedroom, where he seemed to have been rummaging boxes.

(042-016) “I wish,” said Henchard, “you would do me a service, Jopp,

(042-017) now—to-night, I mean, if you can. Leave this at Mrs. [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s for her.

(042-018) I should take it myself, of course, but I don’t wish to be seen there.”

(042-019) He handed a package in brown paper, sealed. Henchard had been as good

(042-020) as his word. Immediately on coming indoors he had searched over his few

(042-021) belongings, and every scrap of Lucetta’s writing that he possessed was

(042-022) here. Jopp indifferently expressed his willingness.

(042-023) “Well, how have ye got on to-day?” his lodger asked. “Any prospect of

(042-024) an opening?”

(042-025) “I am afraid not,” said Jopp, who had not told the other of his

(042-026) application to Farfrae.

(042-027) “There never [[ambition-and-will|will]] be in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]],” declared Henchard decisively.

(042-028) “You must roam further afield.” He said goodnight to Jopp, and returned

(042-029) to his own part of the house.

(042-030) Jopp sat on till his eyes were attracted by the shadow of the

(042-031) candle-snuff on the wall, and looking at the original he found that it

(042-032) had formed itself into a head like a red-hot cauliflower. Henchard’s

(042-033) packet next met his gaze. He knew there had been something of the

(042-034) nature of wooing between Henchard and the now Mrs. Farfrae; and his

(042-035) vague ideas on the subject narrowed themselves down to these: Henchard

(042-036) had a parcel belonging to Mrs. Farfrae, and he had reasons for not

(042-037) returning that parcel to her in person. What could be inside it? So he

(042-038) went on and on till, animated by resentment at Lucetta’s haughtiness,

(042-039) as he thought it, and curiosity to learn if there were any weak sides

(042-040) to this transaction with Henchard, he examined the package. The pen and

(042-041) all its relations being awkward tools in Henchard’s hands he had

(042-042) affixed the seals without an impression, it never occurring to him that

(042-043) the efficacy of such a fastening depended on this. Jopp was far less of

(042-044) a tyro; he lifted one of the seals with his penknife, peeped in at the

(042-045) end thus opened, saw that the bundle consisted of letters; and, having

(042-046) satisfied himself thus far, sealed up the end again by simply softening

(042-047) the wax with the candle, and went off with the parcel as requested.

(042-048) His path was by [[the-river|the river]]-side at the foot of the town. Coming into the

(042-049) light at [[the-bridge|the bridge]] which stood at the end of High Street he beheld

(042-050) lounging thereon [[mother-cuxsom|Mother Cuxsom]] and [[nance-mockridge|Nance Mockridge]].

(042-051) “We be just going down [[mixen-lane|Mixen Lane]] way, to look into Peter’s Finger

(042-052) afore creeping to bed,” said Mrs. Cuxsom. “There’s a fiddle and

(042-053) tambourine going on there. Lord, what’s all the world—do ye come along

(042-054) too, Jopp—’twon’t hinder ye five minutes.”

(042-055) Jopp had mostly kept himself out of this company, but present

(042-056) circumstances made him somewhat more reckless than usual, and without

(042-057) many words he decided to go to his destination that way.

(042-058) Though the upper part of [[durnover|Durnover]] was mainly composed of a curious

(042-059) congeries of barns and farm-steads, there was a less picturesque side

(042-060) to the parish. This was Mixen Lane, now in great part pulled down.

(042-061) Mixen Lane was the Adullam of all the surrounding villages. It was the

(042-062) hiding-place of those who were in distress, and in debt, and trouble of

(042-063) every kind. Farm-labourers and other peasants, who combined a little

(042-064) poaching with their farming, and a little brawling and bibbing with

(042-065) their poaching, found themselves sooner or later in Mixen Lane. Rural

(042-066) mechanics too idle to mechanize, rural servants too rebellious to

(042-067) serve, drifted or were forced into Mixen Lane.

(042-068) The lane and its surrounding thicket of thatched cottages stretched out

(042-069) like a spit into the moist and misty lowland. Much that was sad, much

(042-070) that was low, some things that were baneful, could be seen in Mixen

(042-071) Lane. Vice ran freely in and out certain of the doors in the

(042-072) neighbourhood; recklessness dwelt under the roof with the crooked

(042-073) chimney; shame in some bow-windows; theft (in times of privation) in

(042-074) the thatched and mud-walled houses by the sallows. Even slaughter had

(042-075) not been altogether unknown here. In a block of cottages up an alley

(042-076) there might have been erected an altar to disease in years gone by.

(042-077) Such was Mixen Lane in the times when Henchard and Farfrae were Mayors.

(042-078) Yet this mildewed leaf in the sturdy and flourishing Casterbridge plant

(042-079) lay close to the open country; not a hundred yards from a row of noble

(042-080) elms, and commanding a view across the moor of airy uplands and

(042-081) corn-fields, and mansions of the great. A brook divided the moor from

(042-082) the tenements, and to outward view there was no way across it—no way to

(042-083) the houses but round about by the road. But under every householder’s

(042-084) stairs there was kept a mysterious plank nine inches wide; which plank

(042-085) was a secret bridge.

(042-086) If you, as one of those refugee householders, came in from business

(042-087) after dark—and this was the business time here—you stealthily crossed

(042-088) the moor, approached the border of the aforesaid brook, and whistled

(042-089) opposite the house to which you belonged. A shape thereupon made its

(042-090) [[appearance-and-reality|appearance]] on the other side bearing the bridge on end against the sky;

(042-091) it was lowered; you crossed, and a hand helped you to land yourself,

(042-092) together with the pheasants and hares gathered from neighbouring

(042-093) manors. You sold them slily the next morning, and the day after you

(042-094) stood before the magistrates with the eyes of all your sympathizing

(042-095) neighbours concentrated on your back. You disappeared for a time; then

(042-096) you were again found quietly living in Mixen Lane.

(042-097) Walking along the lane at dusk the stranger was struck by two or three

(042-098) peculiar features therein. One was an intermittent rumbling from the

(042-099) back premises of [[the-three-mariners|the inn]] half-way up; this meant a skittle alley.

(042-100) Another was the extensive prevalence of whistling in the various

(042-101) domiciles—a piped note of some kind coming from nearly every open door.

(042-102) Another was the frequency of white aprons over dingy gowns among the

(042-103) [[gender-and-power|women]] around the doorways. A white apron is a suspicious vesture in

(042-104) situations where spotlessness is difficult; moreover, the industry and

(042-105) cleanliness which the white apron expressed were belied by the postures

(042-106) and gaits of the women who wore it—their knuckles being mostly on their

(042-107) hips (an attitude which lent them the aspect of two-handled mugs), and

(042-108) their shoulders against door-posts; while there was a curious alacrity

(042-109) in the turn of each honest woman’s head upon her neck and in the twirl

(042-110) of her honest eyes, at any noise resembling a masculine footfall along

(042-111) the lane.

(042-112) Yet amid so much that was bad needy [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]] also found a home.

(042-113) Under some of the roofs abode pure and virtuous souls whose presence

(042-114) there was due to the iron hand of necessity, and to that alone.

(042-115) Families from decayed villages—families of that once bulky, but now

(042-117) lifeholders—copyholders and others, whose roof-trees had fallen for

(042-118) some reason or other, compelling them to quit the rural spot that had

(042-119) been their home for generations—came here, unless they chose to lie

(042-120) under a hedge by the wayside.

(042-121) The inn called Peter’s Finger was [[the-church|the church]] of Mixen Lane.

(042-122) It was centrally situate, as such places should be, and bore about the

(042-123) same social relation to the Three Mariners as the latter bore to the

(042-124) King’s Arms. At first sight the inn was so respectable as to be

(042-125) puzzling. The front door was kept shut, and the step was so clean that

(042-126) evidently but few persons entered over its sanded surface. But at the

(042-127) corner of the public-house was an alley, a mere slit, dividing it from

(042-128) the next building. Half-way up the alley was a narrow door, shiny and

(042-129) paintless from the rub of infinite hands and shoulders. This was the

(042-130) actual entrance to the inn.

(042-131) A pedestrian would be seen abstractedly passing along Mixen Lane; and

(042-132) then, in a moment, he would vanish, causing the gazer to blink like

(042-133) Ashton at the disappearance of Ravenswood. That abstracted pedestrian

(042-134) had edged into the slit by the adroit fillip of his person sideways;

(042-135) from the slit he edged into the tavern by a similar exercise of skill.

(042-141) gaol as an accessory to something or other after the fact. She

(042-142) underwent her twelvemonth, and had worn a martyr’s countenance ever

(042-143) since, except at times of meeting [[constable-stubberd|the constable]] who apprehended her,

(042-144) when she winked her eye.
