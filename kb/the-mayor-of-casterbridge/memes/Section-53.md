# Section 53

(052-001) “O father!” she said innocently. “I have had a letter—a strange one—not

(052-002) signed. Somebody has asked me to meet him, either on the [[budmouth|Budmouth]] Road

(052-003) at noon today, or in the evening at Mr. [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s. He says he came to

(052-004) see me some time ago, but a trick was played him, so that he did not

(052-005) see me. I don’t understand it; but between you and me I think Donald is

(052-006) at the bottom of the mystery, and that it is a relation of his who

(052-007) wants to pass an opinion on his choice. But I did not like to go till I

(052-008) had seen you. Shall I go?”

(052-009) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] replied heavily, “Yes; go.”

(052-010) The question of his remaining in [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] was for ever disposed of

(052-011) by this closing in of [[richard-newson|Newson]] on the scene. Henchard was not the man to

(052-012) stand the certainty of condemnation on a matter so near his heart. And

(052-013) being an old hand at bearing anguish in silence, and haughty withal, he

(052-014) resolved to make as light as he could of his intentions, while

(052-015) immediately taking his measures.

(052-016) He surprised the young woman whom he had looked upon as his all in this

(052-017) world by saying to her, as if he did not care about her more: “I am

(052-018) going to leave Casterbridge, [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]].”

(052-019) “Leave Casterbridge!” she cried, “and leave—me?”

(052-020) “Yes, this little shop can be managed by you alone as well as by us

(052-021) both; I don’t care about shops and streets and folk—I would rather get

(052-022) into the country by myself, out of sight, and follow my own ways, and

(052-023) leave you to yours.”

(052-024) She looked down and her tears fell silently. It seemed to her that this

(052-025) resolve of his had come on account of her attachment and its probable

(052-026) result. She showed her devotion to Farfrae, however, by mastering her

(052-027) emotion and speaking out.

(052-028) “I am sorry you have decided on this,” she said with difficult

(052-029) firmness. “For I thought it probable—possible—that I might marry Mr.

(052-030) Farfrae some little time hence, and I did not know that you disapproved

(052-031) of the step!”

(052-032) “I approve of anything you desire to do, Izzy,” said Henchard huskily.

(052-033) “If I did not approve it would be no matter! I wish to go away. My

(052-034) presence might make things awkward in the future, and, in short, it is

(052-035) best that I go.”

(052-036) Nothing that her affection could urge would induce him to reconsider

(052-037) his [[ambition-and-will|determination]]; for she could not urge what she did not know—that

(052-038) when she should learn he was not related to her other than as a

(052-039) step-parent she would refrain from despising him, and that when she

(052-040) knew what he had done to keep her in ignorance she would refrain from

(052-041) hating him. It was his conviction that she would not so refrain; and

(052-042) there existed as yet neither word nor event which could argue it away.

(052-043) “Then,” she said at last, “you will not be able to come to my wedding;

(052-044) and that is not as it ought to be.”

(052-045) “I don’t want to see it—I don’t want to see it!” he exclaimed; adding

(052-046) more softly, “but think of me sometimes in your future life—you’ll do

(052-047) that, Izzy?—think of me when you are living as [[susan-henchard|the wife]] of the richest,

(052-048) the foremost man in the town, and don’t let my sins, _when you know

(052-049) them all_, cause ’ee to quite forget that though I loved ’ee late I

(052-050) loved ’ee well.”

(052-051) “It is because of Donald!” she sobbed.

(052-052) “I don’t forbid you to marry him,” said Henchard. “Promise not to quite

(052-053) forget me when——” He meant when Newson should come.

(052-054) She promised mechanically, in her agitation; and the same evening at

(052-055) dusk Henchard left the town, to whose development he had been one of

(052-056) the chief stimulants for many years. During the day he had bought a new

(052-057) tool-basket, cleaned up his old hay-knife and wimble, set himself up in

(052-058) fresh leggings, kneenaps and corduroys, and in other ways gone back to

(052-059) the working clothes of his young manhood, discarding for ever the

(052-060) shabby-genteel suit of cloth and rusty silk hat that since his decline

(052-061) had characterized him in the Casterbridge street as a man who had seen

(052-062) better days.

(052-063) He went secretly and alone, not a soul of the many who had known him

(052-064) being aware of his departure. Elizabeth-Jane accompanied him as far as

(052-065) the second bridge on the highway—for the hour of her appointment with

(052-066) the unguessed visitor at Farfrae’s had not yet arrived—and parted from

(052-067) him with unfeigned wonder and [[regret-and-suffering|sorrow]], keeping him back a minute or two

(052-068) before finally letting him go. She watched his form diminish across the

(052-069) moor, the yellow rush-basket at his back moving up and down with each

(052-070) tread, and the creases behind his knees coming and going alternately

(052-071) till she could no longer see them. Though she did not know it Henchard

(052-072) formed at this moment much the same picture as he had presented when

(052-073) entering Casterbridge for the first time nearly a quarter of a century

(052-074) before; except, to be sure, that the serious addition to his years had

(052-075) considerably lessened the spring to his stride, that his state of

(052-076) hopelessness had weakened him, and imparted to his shoulders, as

(052-077) weighted by the basket, a perceptible bend.

(052-078) He went on till he came to the first milestone, which stood in the

(052-079) bank, half way up a steep hill. He rested his basket on the top of the

(052-080) stone, placed his elbows on it, and gave way to a convulsive twitch,

(052-081) which was worse than a sob, because it was so hard and so dry.

(052-082) “If I had only got her with me—if I only had!” he said. “Hard work

(052-083) would be nothing to me then! But that was not to be. I—Cain—go alone as

(052-084) I deserve—an outcast and a vagabond. But my punishment is _not_ greater

(052-085) than I can bear!”

(052-086) He sternly subdued his anguish, shouldered his basket, and went on.

(052-087) Elizabeth, in the meantime, had breathed him a sigh, recovered her

(052-088) equanimity, and turned her face to Casterbridge. Before she had reached

(052-089) the first house she was met in her walk by Donald Farfrae. This was

(052-090) evidently not their first meeting that day; they joined hands without

(052-091) ceremony, and Farfrae anxiously asked, “And is he gone—and did you tell

(052-092) him?—I mean of the other matter—not of ours.”

(052-093) “He is gone; and I told him all I knew of your friend. Donald, who is

(052-094) he?”

(052-095) “Well, well, dearie; you will know soon about that. And Mr. Henchard

(052-096) will hear of it if he does not go far.”

(052-097) “He will go far—he’s bent upon getting out of sight and sound!”

(052-098) She walked beside her lover, and when they reached the Crossways, or

(052-099) Bow, turned with him into Corn Street instead of going straight on to

(052-100) her own door. At Farfrae’s house they stopped and went in.

(052-104) memorable morning between one and two years before this time, and whom

(052-109) question of paternity. Henchard’s departure was in a moment explained.

(052-117) Newson’s [[pride]] in what she had grown up to be was more than he could

(052-118) express. He kissed her again and again.

(052-119) “I’ve saved you the trouble to come and meet me—ha-ha!” said Newson.

(052-120) “The fact is that Mr. Farfrae here, he said, ‘Come up and stop with me

(052-121) for a day or two, Captain Newson, and I’ll bring her round.’ ‘Faith,’

(052-122) says I, ‘so I will’; and here I am.”

(052-123) “Well, Henchard is gone,” said Farfrae, shutting the door. “He has done

(052-124) it all voluntarily, and, as I gather from Elizabeth, he has been very

(052-125) nice with her. I was got rather uneasy; but all is as it should be, and

(052-126) we will have no more deefficulties at all.”

(052-127) “Now, that’s very much as I thought,” said Newson, looking into the

(052-147) scrutinizing gaze at some small object in the street.
