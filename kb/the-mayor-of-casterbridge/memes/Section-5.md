# Section 5

(004-001) The sight of the girl made her mother sad—not vaguely but by logical

(004-002) inference. They both were still in that strait-waistcoat of poverty

(004-003) from which she had tried so many times to be delivered for the girl’s

(004-004) sake. The woman had long perceived how zealously and constantly the

(004-005) young mind of her companion was struggling for enlargement; and yet

(004-006) now, in her eighteenth year, it still remained but little unfolded. The

(004-007) desire—sober and repressed—of [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]’s heart was indeed to see,

(004-008) to hear, and to understand. How could she become a woman of wider

(004-009) knowledge, higher repute—“better,” as she termed it—this was her

(004-010) constant inquiry of her mother. She sought further into things than

(004-011) other girls in her position ever did, and her mother groaned as she

(004-012) felt she could not aid in the search.

(004-013) The sailor, drowned or no, was probably now lost to them; and Susan’s

(004-014) staunch, religious adherence to him as her husband in principle, till

(004-015) her views had been disturbed by enlightenment, was demanded no more.

(004-016) She asked herself whether the present moment, now that she was a free

(004-017) woman again, were not as opportune a one as she would find in a world

(004-018) where everything had been so inopportune, for making a desperate effort

(004-020) husband seemed, wisely or not, the best initiatory step. He had

(004-021) possibly drunk himself into his tomb. But he might, on the other hand,

(004-022) have had too much sense to do so; for in her time with him he had been

(004-032) In this attitude they proceeded on their journey, trusting solely to

(004-040) to quit a life she was growing thoroughly weary of.

(004-041) It was on a Friday evening, near the middle of September and just

(004-042) before dusk, that they reached the summit of a hill within a mile of

(004-043) the place they sought. There were high banked hedges to the coach-road

(004-044) here, and they mounted upon the green turf within, and sat down. The

(004-045) spot commanded a full view of the town and its environs.

(004-046) “What an old-fashioned place it seems to be!” said Elizabeth-Jane,

(004-047) while her silent mother mused on other things than topography. “It is

(004-048) huddled all together; and it is shut in by a square wall of trees, like

(004-049) a plot of garden ground by a box-edging.”

(004-050) Its squareness was, indeed, the characteristic which most struck the

(004-053) modernism. It was compact as a box of dominoes. It had no suburbs—in

(004-054) the ordinary sense. Country and town met at a mathematical line.

(004-062) glazings shining bleared and bloodshot with the coppery fire they

(004-067) pedestrians were about to enter. Before they had risen to proceed two

(004-068) men passed outside the hedge, engaged in argumentative conversation.

(004-069) “Why, surely,” said Elizabeth, as they receded, “those men mentioned

(004-071) “I thought so too,” said Mrs. [[richard-newson|Newson]].

(004-072) “That seems a hint to us that he is still here.”

(004-073) “Yes.”

(004-074) “Shall I run after them, and ask them about him——”

(004-075) “No, no, no! Not for the world just yet. He may be in the workhouse, or

(004-078) “’Twas just something to say—that’s all! But we must make private
