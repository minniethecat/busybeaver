# The Wife-Auction

The originating act of The Mayor of Casterbridge and one of the most shocking openings in Victorian fiction. At [[weydon-priors|Weydon-Priors]] fair, a young hay-trusser named [[michael-henchard|Michael Henchard]], drunk on [[the-furmity-womans-revelation|furmity]] laced with rum and "out of [[temper-and-impulse|temper]] with the world," auctions his wife [[susan-henchard|Susan]] and infant daughter [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] to the highest bidder. The sailor [[richard-newson|Richard Newson]] offers five guineas — and Susan, in fury and despair, goes with him.

## The Event

The wife-auction takes place in the furmity-tent at Weydon-Priors fair. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]], a twenty-one-year-old hay-trusser with a young [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]], is restless and resentful of his domestic bonds. The furmity, spiked with rum by the [[the-furmity-woman|furmity-woman]], loosens his inhibitions and his grievances pour out. What begins as a drunken boast — that he would sell his wife if he could — becomes, in the brutal logic of [[weydon-priors|the fair]]-ground, an actual transaction.

> "I'd sell my wife for five guineas, if any man would buy her." (cf. 001-143)

The auction is at once grotesque and banal: a [[marriage-and-domestic-life|family]] is destroyed over a bowl of furmity, in a tent, among strangers. Hardy's genius is to make the scene both [[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]gularly shocking and entirely believable — the convergence of character ([[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[temper-and-impulse|temper]]), circumstance ([[weydon-priors|the fair]]'s licence), and substance (the spiked furmity) producing catastrophe.

## Immediate Aftermath

| Consequence | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Susan's Departure | Susan takes [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] and leaves with [[richard-newson|Newson]], believing the transaction binding |
| [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s Remorse | The next morning, sober and horrified, [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] searches for them but cannot find them |
| The Abstinence Vow | [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] swears off alcohol for twenty-one years — the same span as his age — in a church, on his knees |
| [[richard-newson|Newson]]'s Guilt | [[richard-newson|Newson]] accepts the "sale" as real, setting up his later disappearance and [[susans-return|Susan's return]] |

## Long-Term Consequences

The wife-auction is the [[fate-and-determinism|novel's original sin]], and its consequences unfold over the entire narrative:

1. **[[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s Rise**: The twenty-one-year vow of abstinence transforms him from labourer to prosperous corn-factor and [[mayor-of-casterbridge|Mayor of Casterbridge]] — his [[regret-and-suffering|remorse]] [[gender-and-power|power]]s his [[ambition-and-will|ambition]].

2. **[[susans-return|Susan's Return]]**: After [[richard-newson|Newson]] is lost at sea, Susan, believing herself a widow, returns to [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] with [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], setting the plot in motion.

3. **The Revelation**: The [[the-furmity-womans-revelation|furmity-woman]]'s [[the-furmity-womans-revelation|public exposure]] of the wife-sale during the [[the-royal-visit|royal visit]] destroys [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s [[reputation-and-social-standing|social standing]].

4. **The Ultimate Irony**: [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]'s vow of abstinence ends on the very day of his final ruin — as if the twenty-one-year reprieve was all the [[fate-and-determinism|fate]] he was granted.

## Thematic Significance

The wife-auction establishes the novel's [[character-as-fate|governing philosophy]]: character is [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]]. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] is not a victim of circumstance — he creates his own catastrophe through [[temper-and-impulse|impulse]] and [[pride]]. The auction is not an aberration but a revelation: everything [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] [[ambition-and-will|will]] become is already present in the drunk young man at [[weydon-priors|the fair]].

The event also introduces the novel's structural irony. [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] spends twenty-one years trying to undo an act that cannot be undone, building [[reputation-and-social-standing|respectability]] on a foundation of shame. When the past finally catches up with him — as it must, in a [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] where "everyone knew everyone else's bu[[guilt-and-redemption|sin]]ess" — the collapse is total.
