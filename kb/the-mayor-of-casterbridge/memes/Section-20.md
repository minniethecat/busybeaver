# Section 20

(019-001) But most probably luck had little to do with it. Character is Fate,

(019-002) said Novalis, and [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]]’s [[character-as-fate|character]] was just the reverse of

(019-003) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]’s, who might not inaptly be described as Faust has been

(019-004) described—as a vehement gloomy being who had quitted the ways of vulgar

(019-005) men without light to guide him on a better way.

(019-006) Farfrae duly received the request to discontinue attentions to

(019-007) [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]]. His acts of that kind had been so slight that the

(019-008) request was almost superfluous. Yet he had felt a considerable interest

(019-009) in her, and after some cogitation he decided that it would be as well

(019-010) to enact no Romeo part just then—for the young girl’s sake no less than

(019-011) his own. Thus the incipient attachment was stifled down.

(019-012) A time came when, avoid collision with his former friend as he might,

(019-013) Farfrae was compelled, in sheer self-defence, to close with Henchard in

(019-014) mortal commercial combat. He could no longer parry the fierce attacks

(019-015) of the latter by simple avoidance. As soon as their war of prices began

(019-016) everybody was interested, and some few guessed the end. It was, in some

(019-017) degree, Northern insight matched against Southern doggedness—the dirk

(019-018) against the cudgel—and Henchard’s weapon was one which, if it did not

(019-019) deal ruin at the first or second stroke, left him afterwards well-nigh

(019-020) at his antagonist’s mercy.

(019-021) Almost every Saturday they encountered each other amid the crowd of

(019-022) farmers which thronged about the [[the-market-place|market-place]] in the weekly course of

(019-023) their business. Donald was always ready, and even anxious, to say a few

(019-024) friendly words, but the Mayor invariably gazed stormfully past him,

(019-025) like one who had endured and lost on his account, and could in no sense

(019-026) forgive the wrong; nor did Farfrae’s snubbed manner of perplexity at

(019-027) all appease him. The large farmers, corn-merchants, millers,

(019-028) auctioneers, and others had each an official stall in the corn-market

(019-029) room, with their names painted thereon; and when to the familiar series

(019-030) of “Henchard,” “Everdene,” “Shiner,” “Darton,” and so on, was added one

(019-031) inscribed “Farfrae,” in staring new letters, Henchard was stung into

(019-032) bitterness; like Bellerophon, he wandered away from the crowd, cankered

(019-033) in soul.

(019-034) From that day Donald Farfrae’s name was seldom mentioned in Henchard’s

(019-035) house. If at breakfast or dinner Elizabeth-Jane’s mother inadvertently

(019-036) alluded to her favourite’s movements, the girl would implore her by a

(019-037) look to be silent; and her husband would say, “What—are you, too, my

(019-038) enemy?”

(019-040) There came a shock which had been foreseen for some time by Elizabeth,

(019-041) as the box passenger foresees the approaching jerk from some channel

(019-042) across the highway.

(019-043) Her mother was ill—too unwell to leave her room. Henchard, who treated

(019-044) her kindly, except in moments of irritation, sent at once for the

(019-045) richest, busiest doctor, whom he supposed to be the best. Bedtime came,

(019-046) and they burnt a light all night. In a day or two she rallied.

(019-047) Elizabeth, who had been staying up, did not appear at breakfast on the

(019-048) second morning, and Henchard sat down alone. He was startled to see a

(019-049) letter for him from Jersey in a writing he knew too well, and had

(019-050) expected least to behold again. He took it up in his hands and looked

(019-051) at it as at a picture, a vision, a vista of past enactments; and then

(019-052) he read it as an unimportant finale to conjecture.

(019-053) The writer said that she at length perceived how impossible it would be

(019-054) for any further communications to proceed between them now that his

(019-055) re-[[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]] had taken place. That such reunion had been the only

(019-056) straightforward course open to him she was bound to admit.

(019-057) “On calm reflection, therefore,” she went on, “I quite forgive you for

(019-058) landing me in such a dilemma, remembering that you concealed nothing

(019-059) before our ill-advised acquaintance; and that you really did set before

(019-060) me in your grim way the fact of there being a certain risk in intimacy

(019-061) with you, slight as it seemed to be after fifteen or sixteen years of

(019-062) silence on your wife’s part. I thus look upon the whole as a misfortune

(019-063) of mine, and not a fault of yours.

(019-064) “So that, Michael, I must ask you to overlook those letters with which

(019-065) I pestered you day after day in the heat of my feelings. They were

(019-066) written whilst I thought your conduct to me cruel; but now I know more

(019-067) particulars of the position you were in I see how inconsiderate my

(019-068) reproaches were.

(019-069) “Now you [[ambition-and-will|will]], I am sure, perceive that the one condition which will

(019-070) make any future happiness possible for me is that the past connection

(019-071) between our lives be kept secret outside this isle. Speak of it I know

(019-072) you will not; and I can trust you not to write of it. One safe-guard

(019-073) more remains to be mentioned—that no writings of mine, or trifling

(019-074) articles belonging to me, should be left in your possession through

(019-075) neglect or forgetfulness. To this end may I request you to return to me

(019-076) any such you may have, particularly the letters written in the first

(019-077) abandonment of feeling.

(019-078) “For the handsome sum you forwarded to me as a plaster to the wound I

(019-079) heartily thank you.

(019-080) “I am now on my way to Bristol, to see my only relative. She is rich,

(019-081) and I hope will do something for me. I shall return through

(019-082) [[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] and [[budmouth|Budmouth]], where I shall take the packet-boat. Can you

(019-083) meet me with the letters and other trifles? I shall be in the coach

(019-084) which changes horses at the Antelope Hotel at half-past five Wednesday

(019-086) may easily be found. I should prefer this plan of receiving them to

(019-087) having them sent.—I remain still, yours; ever,

(019-088) “LUCETTA”

(019-089) Henchard breathed heavily. “Poor thing—better you had not known me!

(019-090) Upon my heart and soul, if ever I should be left in a position to carry

(019-091) out that marriage with thee, I _ought_ to do it—I ought to do it,

(019-092) indeed!”

(019-093) The contingency that he had in his mind was, of course, the death of

(019-094) [[susan-henchard|Mrs. Henchard]].

(019-095) As requested, he sealed up [[lucetta-templeman|Lucetta]]’s letters, and put the parcel aside

(019-096) till the day she had appointed; this plan of returning them by hand

(019-097) being apparently a little _ruse_ of the young lady for exchanging a

(019-098) word or two with him on past times. He would have preferred not to see

(019-099) her; but deeming that there could be no great harm in acquiescing thus

(019-100) far, he went at dusk and stood opposite the coach-office.

(019-101) The evening was chilly, and the coach was late. Henchard crossed over

(019-102) to it while the horses were being changed; but there was no Lucetta

(019-103) inside or out. Concluding that something had happened to modify her

(019-104) arrangements he gave the matter up and went home, not without a sense

(019-105) of relief. Meanwhile Mrs. Henchard was weakening visibly. She could not

(019-106) go out of doors any more. One day, after much thinking which seemed to

(019-107) distress her, she said she wanted to write something. A desk was put

(019-108) upon her bed with pen and paper, and at her request she was left alone.

(019-109) She remained writing for a short time, folded her paper carefully,

(019-110) called Elizabeth-Jane to bring a taper and wax, and then, still

(019-111) refusing assistance, sealed up the sheet, directed it, and locked it in

(019-112) her desk. She had directed it in these words:—

(019-113) “_Mr. Michael Henchard. Not to be opened till Elizabeth-Jane’s

(019-114) wedding-day._”

(019-115) The latter sat up with her mother to the utmost of her strength night

(019-116) after night. To learn to take the universe seriously there is no

(019-117) quicker way than to watch—to be a “waker,” as the country-people call

(019-118) it. Between the hours at which the last toss-pot went by and the first

(019-119) sparrow shook himself, the silence in Casterbridge—barring the rare

(019-120) sound of the watchman—was broken in Elizabeth’s ear only by the

(019-121) time-piece in the bedroom ticking frantically against the clock on the

(019-122) stairs; ticking harder and harder till it seemed to clang like a gong;

(019-123) and all this while the subtle-souled girl asking herself why she was

(019-124) born, why sitting in a room, and blinking at the candle; why things

(019-125) around her had taken the shape they wore in preference to every other

(019-126) possible shape. Why they stared at her so helplessly, as if waiting for

(019-127) the touch of some wand that should release them from terrestrial

(019-128) constraint; what that chaos called consciousness, which spun in her at

(019-129) this moment like a top, tended to, and began in. Her eyes fell

(019-130) together; she was awake, yet she was asleep.

(019-131) A word from her mother roused her. Without preface, and as the

(019-132) continuation of a scene already progressing in her mind, Mrs. Henchard

(019-133) said: “You remember the note sent to you and Mr. Farfrae—asking you to

(019-134) meet some one in [[durnover|Durnover]] Barton—and that you thought it was a trick to

(019-135) make fools of you?”

(019-136) “Yes.”

(019-137) “It was not to make fools of you—it was done to bring you together.

(019-138) ’Twas I did it.”

(019-139) “Why?” said Elizabeth, with a start.

(019-140) “I—wanted you to marry Mr. Farfrae.”

(019-141) “O mother!” Elizabeth-Jane bent down her head so much that she looked

(019-142) quite into her own lap. But as her mother did not go on, she said,

(019-143) “What reason?”

(019-144) “Well, I had a reason. ’Twill out one day. I wish it could have been in

(019-145) my time! But there—nothing is as you wish it! Henchard hates him.”

(019-146) “Perhaps they’ll be friends again,” murmured the girl.

(019-147) “I don’t know—I don’t know.” After this her mother was silent, and

(019-148) dozed; and she spoke on the subject no more.

(019-152) one; and then he was informed that Mrs. Henchard was dead—just

(019-153) dead—that very hour.
