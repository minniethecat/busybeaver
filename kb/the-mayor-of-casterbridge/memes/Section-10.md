# Section 10

(009-001) Though it was still early [[donald-farfrae|the young Scotchman]] expressed his wish to

(009-002) retire, whereupon the landlady whispered to Elizabeth to run upstairs

(009-003) and turn down his bed. She took a candlestick and proceeded on her

(009-004) mission, which was the act of a few moments only. When, candle in hand,

(009-005) she reached the top of the stairs on her way down again, Mr. Farfrae

(009-006) was at the foot coming up. She could not very well retreat; they met

(009-007) and passed in the turn of the staircase.

(009-010) girl characterized by earnestness and soberness of mien, with which

(009-014) that when confronting her he smiled; and then, with the manner of a

(009-015) temporarily light-hearted man, who has started himself on a flight of

(009-016) song whose momentum he cannot readily check, he softly tuned an old

(009-017) ditty that she seemed to suggest—

(009-018) “As I came in by my bower door,

(009-019) As day was waxin’ wearie,

(009-020) Oh wha came tripping down the stair

(009-021) But bonnie Peg my dearie.”

(009-022) [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]], rather disconcerted, hastened on; and the Scotchman’s

(009-023) voice died away, humming more of the same within the closed door of his

(009-024) room.

(009-025) Here the scene and sentiment ended for the present. When soon after,

(009-026) the girl rejoined her mother, the latter was still in thought—on quite

(009-027) another matter than a young man’s song.

(009-028) “We’ve made a mistake,” she whispered (that the Scotchman might not

(009-029) overhear). “On no account ought ye to have helped serve here to-night.

(009-030) Not because of ourselves, but for the sake of _him_. If he should

(009-031) befriend us, and take us up, and then find out what you did when

(009-032) staying here, ’twould grieve and wound his natural [[pride]] as Mayor of

(009-033) [[casterbridge|the town]].”

(009-034) Elizabeth, who would perhaps have been more alarmed at this than her

(009-036) about it as things stood. Her “he” was another man than her poor

(009-037) mother’s. “For myself,” she said, “I didn’t at all mind waiting a

(009-040) broad way of talking about themselves here. But of course he didn’t

(009-041) know—he was too refined in his mind to know such things!” Thus she

(009-043) Meanwhile, the “he” of her mother was not so far away as even they

(009-044) thought. After leaving the Three Mariners he had sauntered up and down

(009-045) the empty High Street, passing and repassing the inn in his promenade.

(009-046) When the Scotchman sang his voice had reached [[michael-henchard|Henchard]]’s ears through

(009-047) the heart-shaped holes in the window-shutters, and had led him to pause

(009-048) outside them a long while.

(009-049) “To be sure, to be sure, how that fellow does draw me!” he had said to

(009-050) himself. “I suppose ’tis because I’m so lonely. I’d have given him a

(009-051) third share in the business to have stayed!”

(009-053) When Elizabeth-Jane opened the hinged casement next morning the mellow

(009-054) air brought in the feel of imminent autumn almost as distinctly as if

(009-055) she had been in the remotest hamlet. Casterbridge was the complement of

(009-056) the [[rural-economics|rural life]] around, not its urban opposite. Bees and butterflies in

(009-057) the cornfields at the top of the town, who desired to get to the meads

(009-058) at the bottom, took no circuitous course, but flew straight down High

(009-059) Street without any apparent consciousness that they were traversing

(009-060) strange latitudes. And in autumn airy spheres of thistledown floated

(009-061) into the same street, lodged upon the shop fronts, blew into drains,

(009-062) and innumerable tawny and yellow leaves skimmed along the pavement, and

(009-063) stole through people’s doorways into their passages with a hesitating

(009-064) scratch on the floor, like the skirts of timid visitors.

(009-065) Hearing voices, one of which was close at hand, she withdrew her head

(009-067) no longer as a great personage, but as a thriving man of business—was

(009-069) looking from the window adjoining her own. Henchard it appeared, had

(009-070) gone a little way past the inn before he had noticed his acquaintance

(009-071) of the previous evening. He came back a few steps, Donald Farfrae

(009-072) opening the window further.

(009-073) “And you are off soon, I suppose?” said Henchard upwards.

(009-074) “Yes—almost this moment, sir,” said the other. “Maybe I’ll walk on till

(009-075) the coach makes up on me.”

(009-076) “Which way?”

(009-077) “The way ye are going.”

(009-078) “Then shall we walk together to the top o’ town?”

(009-079) “If ye’ll wait a minute,” said the Scotchman.

(009-080) In a few minutes the latter emerged, bag in hand. Henchard looked at

(009-081) the bag as at an enemy. It showed there was no mistake about the young

(009-082) man’s departure. “Ah, my lad,” he said, “you should have been a wise

(009-083) man, and have stayed with me.”

(009-084) “Yes, yes—it might have been wiser,” said Donald, looking

(009-085) microscopically at the houses that were furthest off. “It is only

(009-086) telling ye the [[appearance-and-reality|truth]] when I say my plans are vague.”

(009-087) They had by this time passed on from the precincts of the inn, and

(009-088) Elizabeth-Jane heard no more. She saw that they continued in

(009-093) corn; when they bent suddenly to the right into the Bristol Road, and

(009-095) “He was a good man—and he’s gone,” she said to herself. “I was nothing

(009-096) to him, and there was no reason why he should have wished me good-bye.”

(009-097) The simple thought, with its latent sense of slight, had moulded itself

(009-098) out of the following little fact: when the Scotchman came out at the

(009-099) door he had by accident glanced up at her; and then he had looked away

(009-100) again without nodding, or smiling, or saying a word.

(009-101) “You are still thinking, mother,” she said, when she turned inwards.

(009-102) “Yes; I am thinking of Mr. Henchard’s sudden liking for that young man.

(009-103) He was always so. Now, surely, if he takes so warmly to people who are

(009-104) not related to him at all, may he not take as warmly to his own kin?”

(009-128) “In that case,” answered Mrs. Henchard cautiously, “ask him to write me

(009-129) a note, saying when and how he will see us—or _me_.”
