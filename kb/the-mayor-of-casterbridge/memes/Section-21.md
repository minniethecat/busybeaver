# Section 21

(020-001) “Well, poor soul; she’s helpless to hinder that or anything now,”

(020-002) answered [[mother-cuxsom|Mother Cuxsom]]. “And all her shining keys [[ambition-and-will|will]] be took from

(020-003) her, and her cupboards opened; and little things a’ didn’t wish seen,

(020-004) anybody will see; and her wishes and ways will all be as nothing!”

(020-005) XIX.

(020-006) [[michael-henchard|Henchard]] and Elizabeth sat conversing by the fire. It was three weeks

(020-007) after [[susan-henchard|Mrs. Henchard]]’s funeral, the candles were not lighted, and a

(020-008) restless, acrobatic flame, poised on a coal, called from the shady

(020-009) walls the smiles of all shapes that could respond—the old pier-glass,

(020-010) with gilt columns and huge entablature, the picture-frames, sundry

(020-011) knobs and handles, and the brass rosette at the bottom of each riband

(020-012) bell-pull on either side of the chimney-piece.

(020-013) “Elizabeth, do you think much of old times?” said Henchard.

(020-014) “Yes, sir; often,” she said.

(020-015) “Who do you put in your pictures of ’em?”

(020-016) “Mother and father—nobody else hardly.”

(020-017) Henchard always looked like one bent on resisting pain when

(020-018) [[elizabeth-jane-newson|Elizabeth-Jane]] spoke of [[richard-newson|Richard Newson]] as “father.” “Ah! I am out of

(020-019) all that, am I not?” he said.... “Was Newson a kind father?”

(020-020) “Yes, sir; very.”

(020-021) Henchard’s face settled into an expression of stolid loneliness which

(020-022) gradually modulated into something softer. “Suppose I had been your

(020-023) real father?” he said. “Would you have cared for me as much as you

(020-024) cared for Richard Newson?”

(020-025) “I can’t think it,” she said quickly. “I can think of no other as my

(020-026) father, except my father.”

(020-027) Henchard’s wife was dissevered from him by death; his friend and helper

(020-028) [[donald-farfrae|Farfrae]] by estrangement; Elizabeth-Jane by ignorance. It seemed to him

(020-029) that only one of them could possibly be recalled, and that was the

(020-030) girl. His mind began vibrating between the wish to reveal himself to

(020-031) her and the policy of leaving well alone, till he could no longer sit

(020-032) still. He walked up and down, and then he came and stood behind her

(020-033) chair, looking down upon the top of her head. He could no longer

(020-034) restrain his [[temper-and-impulse|impulse]]. “What did your mother tell you about me—my

(020-035) history?” he asked.

(020-036) “That you were related by [[marriage-and-domestic-life|marriage]].”

(020-037) “She should have told more—before you knew me! Then my task would not

(020-038) have been such a hard one.... Elizabeth, it is I who am your father,

(020-039) and not Richard Newson. Shame alone prevented your wretched parents

(020-040) from owning this to you while both of ’em were alive.”

(020-041) The back of Elizabeth’s head remained still, and her shoulders did not

(020-042) denote even the movements of breathing. Henchard went on: “I’d rather

(020-043) have your scorn, your fear, anything than your ignorance; ’tis that I

(020-044) hate! Your mother and I were man and wife when we were young. What you

(020-045) saw was our second marriage. Your mother was too honest. We had thought

(020-046) each other dead—and—Newson became her husband.”

(020-047) This was the nearest approach Henchard could make to the full [[appearance-and-reality|truth]]. As

(020-048) far as he personally was concerned he would have screened nothing; but

(020-049) he showed a respect for the young girl’s sex and years worthy of a

(020-050) better man.

(020-051) When he had gone on to give details which a whole series of slight and

(020-052) unregarded incidents in her past life strangely corroborated; when, in

(020-053) short, she believed his story to be true, she became greatly agitated,

(020-054) and turning round to the table flung her face upon it weeping.

(020-055) “Don’t cry—don’t cry!” said Henchard, with vehement pathos, “I can’t

(020-056) bear it, I won’t bear it. I am your father; why should you cry? Am I so

(020-057) dreadful, so hateful to ’ee? Don’t take against me, Elizabeth-Jane!” he

(020-058) cried, grasping her wet hand. “Don’t take against me—though I was a

(020-059) drinking man once, and used your mother roughly—I’ll be kinder to you

(020-060) than _he_ was! I’ll do anything, if you will only look upon me as your

(020-061) father!”

(020-062) She tried to stand up and comfort him trustfully; but she could not;

(020-063) she was troubled at his presence, like the brethren at the avowal of

(020-064) Joseph.

(020-065) “I don’t want you to come to me all of a sudden,” said Henchard in

(020-066) jerks, and moving like a great tree in a wind. “No, Elizabeth, I don’t.

(020-067) I’ll go away and not see you till to-morrow, or when you like, and then

(020-068) I’ll show ’ee papers to prove my words. There, I am gone, and won’t

(020-069) disturb you any more.... ’Twas I that chose your name, my daughter;

(020-070) your mother wanted it Susan. There, don’t forget ’twas I gave you your

(020-071) name!” He went out at the door and shut her softly in, and she heard

(020-072) him go away into the garden. But he had not done. Before she had moved,

(020-073) or in any way recovered from the effect of his disclosure, he

(020-074) reappeared.

(020-075) “One word more, Elizabeth,” he said. “You’ll take my surname now—hey?

(020-076) Your mother was against it, but it will be much more pleasant to me.

(020-077) ’Tis legally yours, you know. But nobody need know that. You shall take

(020-078) it as if by choice. I’ll talk to my lawyer—I don’t know the law of it

(020-079) exactly; but will you do this—let me put a few lines into the newspaper

(020-080) that such is to be your name?”

(020-081) “If it is my name I must have it, mustn’t I?” she asked.

(020-082) “Well, well; usage is everything in these matters.”

(020-083) “I wonder why mother didn’t wish it?”

(020-084) “Oh, some whim of the poor soul’s. Now get a bit of paper and draw up a

(020-085) paragraph as I shall tell you. But let’s have a light.”

(020-086) “I can see by the firelight,” she answered. “Yes—I’d rather.”

(020-087) “Very well.”

(020-088) She got a piece of paper, and bending over the fender wrote at his

(020-089) dictation words which he had evidently got by heart from some

(020-090) advertisement or other—words to the effect that she, the writer,

(020-091) hitherto known as Elizabeth-Jane Newson, was going to call herself

(020-092) Elizabeth-Jane Henchard forthwith. It was done, and fastened up, and

(020-093) directed to the office of the _[[casterbridge|Casterbridge]] Chronicle_.

(020-094) “Now,” said Henchard, with the blaze of satisfaction that he always

(020-095) emitted when he had carried his point—though tenderness softened it

(020-096) this time—“I’ll go upstairs and hunt for some documents that will prove

(020-097) it all to you. But I won’t trouble you with them till to-morrow.

(020-098) Good-night, my Elizabeth-Jane!”

(020-099) He was gone before the bewildered girl could realize what it all meant,

(020-100) or adjust her filial sense to the new center of gravity. She was

(020-101) thankful that he had left her to herself for the evening, and sat down

(020-102) over the fire. Here she remained in silence, and wept—not for her

(020-103) mother now, but for the genial sailor Richard Newson, to whom she

(020-104) seemed doing a wrong.

(020-105) Henchard in the meantime had gone upstairs. Papers of a domestic nature

(020-106) he kept in a drawer in his bedroom, and this he unlocked. Before

(020-107) turning them over he leant back and indulged in reposeful thought.

(020-108) Elizabeth was his at last and she was a girl of such good sense and

(020-109) kind heart that she would be sure to like him. He was the kind of man

(020-110) to whom some human object for pouring out his heart upon—were it

(020-111) emotive or were it choleric—was almost a necessity. The craving for his

(020-112) heart for the re-establishment of this tenderest human tie had been

(020-113) great during his wife’s lifetime, and now he had submitted to its

(020-114) mastery without reluctance and without fear. He bent over the drawer

(020-115) again, and proceeded in his search.

(020-116) Among the other papers had been placed the contents of his wife’s

(020-117) little desk, the keys of which had been handed to him at her request.

(020-118) Here was the letter addressed to him with the restriction, “_Not to be

(020-119) opened till Elizabeth-Jane’s wedding-day_.”

(020-120) Mrs. Henchard, though more patient than her husband, had been no

(020-121) practical hand at anything. In sealing up the sheet, which was folded

(020-122) and tucked in without an envelope, in the old-fashioned way, she had

(020-123) overlaid the junction with a large mass of wax without the requisite

(020-124) under-touch of the same. The seal had cracked, and the letter was open.

(020-125) Henchard had no reason to suppose the restriction one of serious

(020-126) weight, and his feeling for his late wife had not been of the nature of

(020-127) deep respect. “Some trifling fancy or other of poor Susan’s, I

(020-128) suppose,” he said; and without curiosity he allowed his eyes to scan

(020-129) the letter:—

(020-130) MY DEAR MICHAEL,—For the good of all three of us I have kept one thing

(020-131) a secret from you till now. I hope you will understand why; I think you

(020-132) will; though perhaps you may not forgive me. But, dear Michael, I have

(020-133) done it for the best. I shall be in my grave when you read this, and

(020-134) Elizabeth-Jane will have a home. Don’t curse me Mike—think of how I was

(020-135) situated. I can hardly write it, but here it is. Elizabeth-Jane is not

(020-136) your Elizabeth-Jane—the child who was in my arms when you sold me. No;

(020-137) she died three months after that, and this living one is my other

(020-138) husband’s. I christened her by the same name we had given to the first,

(020-139) and she filled up the ache I felt at the other’s loss. Michael, I am

(020-140) dying, and I might have held my tongue; but I could not. Tell her

(020-141) husband of this or not, as you may judge; and forgive, if you can, a

(020-142) woman you once deeply wronged, as she forgives you.

(020-143) SUSAN HENCHARD

(020-144) Her husband regarded the paper as if it were a window-pane through

(020-145) which he saw for miles. His lips twitched, and he seemed to compress

(020-146) his frame, as if to bear better. His usual habit was not to consider

(020-147) whether [[fate-and-determinism|destiny]] were hard upon him or not—the shape of his ideals in

(020-148) cases of affliction being simply a moody “I am to suffer, I perceive.”

(020-149) “This much scourging, then, it is for me.” But now through his

(020-150) passionate head there stormed this thought—that the blasting disclosure

(020-151) was what he had deserved.

(020-152) His wife’s extreme reluctance to have the girl’s name altered from

(020-153) Newson to Henchard was now accounted for fully. It furnished another

(020-154) illustration of that honesty in dishonesty which had characterized her

(020-155) in other things.

(020-156) He remained unnerved and purposeless for near a couple of hours; till

(020-157) he suddenly said, “Ah—I wonder if it is true!”

(020-158) He jumped up in an impulse, kicked off his slippers, and went with a

(020-159) candle to the door of Elizabeth-Jane’s room, where he put his ear to

(020-160) the keyhole and listened. She was breathing profoundly. Henchard softly

(020-161) turned the handle, entered, and shading the light, approached the

(020-162) bedside. Gradually bringing the light from behind a screening curtain

(020-163) he held it in such a manner that it fell slantwise on her face without

(020-164) shining on her eyes. He steadfastly regarded her features.

(020-165) They were fair: his were dark. But this was an unimportant preliminary.

(020-166) In sleep there come to the surface buried genealogical facts, ancestral

(020-167) curves, dead men’s traits, which the mobility of daytime animation

(020-168) screens and overwhelms. In the present statuesque repose of the young

(020-169) girl’s countenance Richard Newson’s was unmistakably reflected. He

(020-170) could not endure the sight of her, and hastened away.

(020-171) Misery taught him nothing more than defiant endurance of it. His wife

(020-172) was dead, and the first impulse for revenge died with the thought that

(020-173) she was beyond him. He looked out at the night as at a fiend. Henchard,

(020-174) like all his kind, was superstitious, and he could not help thinking

(020-175) that the concatenation of events this evening had produced was the

(020-176) scheme of some sinister intelligence bent on punishing him. Yet they

(020-177) had developed naturally. If he had not revealed his past history to

(020-178) Elizabeth he would not have searched the drawer for papers, and so on.

(020-179) The mockery was, that he should have no sooner taught a girl to claim

(020-180) the shelter of his paternity than he discovered her to have no kinship

(020-181) with him.

(020-182) This ironical sequence of things angered him like an impish trick from

(020-183) a fellow-creature. Like Prester John’s, his table had been spread, and

(020-184) infernal harpies had snatched up the food. He went out of the house,

(020-185) and moved sullenly onward down the pavement till he came to [[the-bridge|the bridge]]

(020-186) at the bottom of the High Street. Here he turned in upon a bypath on

(020-187) [[the-river|the river]] bank, skirting the north-eastern limits of the town.
