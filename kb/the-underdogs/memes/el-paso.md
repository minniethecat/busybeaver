## Overview

El Paso, Texas, is not a setting within the novel's action but the place where the novel itself was born. Mariano Azuela, having served as a medical officer with revolutionary forces and then emigrated when the counterrevolutionary faction triumphed, sat down in El Paso in 1915 and wrote *Los de abajo* (*The Underdogs*). The city thus stands at the origin point of the entire narrative — Part III opens with the dateline "El Paso, Texas, May 16, 1915" (036-001), marking the physical and temporal distance between the author's exile and the revolution he had witnessed.

## Location & Geography

El Paso sits on the northern bank of the Rio Grande, directly across from Ciudad Juárez, Chihuahua. The international border that separates the two cities is itself a defining feature: El Paso was close enough to Mexico for the Revolution to be viscerally present — refugees, exiles, and news crossed the river continuously — yet distant enough to offer Azuela the stability to write.

## Role in the Narrative

The Preface records that "when the counterrevolutionary forces of Victoriano Huerta were temporarily triumphant, he emigrated to El Paso, Texas, where in 1915 he wrote *The Underdogs*" (P01-015). The novel's composition in exile is essential to its character — Azuela wrote from memory, without documents, preserving the raw immediacy of his experience rather than a historian's reconstruction.

Part III of the novel opens with the formal dateline: "El Paso, Texas, May 16, 1915" (036-001), followed by a letter from one character to another. This framing device draws an explicit line between the fiction and the circumstances of its creation — the reader is reminded that the story of Demetrio Macías was committed to paper in a room in El Paso, on a specific day, by a man who had lived through what he described.
