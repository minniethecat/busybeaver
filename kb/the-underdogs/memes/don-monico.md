## Overview

Don Monico is a cacique — a local political boss and landowner — in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. He never appears directly as a speaking character; instead, he exists in the novel as a name, a reputation, and a target. His house is burned by [[Demetrio Macías]]'s men in one of the novel's emblematic episodes of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary destruction, making him a symbol of the old rural order that the Revolution came to dismantle.

## Character

Don Monico is introduced through gossip and grievance. The men talk about "a little quarrel with Don Monico" (012-085) as the kind of local dispute that pushes peasants into rebellion. One soldier recounts Monico's history: "The fellow was a…" (013-026), trailing off in a way that suggests the cacique's reputation precedes any specific accusation. The men are explicit about their motivation: "You are under arms simply because of Señor Monico" (013-087) — the Revolution, at the local level, is personal.

In Chapter XXVI, [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band rides toward Monico's estate with deliberate intent. "We're going to bid Monico good morning" (026-032) is [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s dry announcement, and the men joke about having "breakfast with Don Monico, who's a particular friend of mine" (026-034). The tone is mocking — the false politeness of men about to commit violence.

When they arrive, the scene plays out as a ritual of inversion. Don Monico and his ladies are forced to kiss the hands of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s staff (026-099), a ceremonial humiliation of the cacique before his social inferiors. Then his house is burned: "tongues of fire rose from Monico's house, no one could account for how they started" (026-130). The passive construction is Azuela's irony — everyone knows how the fire started, but no one will say.

Don Monico is also remembered in later chapters through place-names — "the country of Monico the cacique" (026-004) — and through the recollections of characters who worked in establishments named after him. A former waiter from "El Monico" in Chihuahua (023-086) reminisces about the establishment (024-023), suggesting the reach of his patronage network.

## Key Passages

> "We're going to bid Monico good morning," Demetrio said with a strange smile. [PN=026-032]

[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s understated announcement of the attack on Monico's estate captures the casual, almost ritualistic nature of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary violence against the old elite.

> Don Monico, in consternation, throws himself at their feet. His staff obeys. Monico and the ladies kiss his hands. [PN=026-091, 026-099]

The humiliation of the cacique — forced to kiss the hands of men he would once have commanded — is the novel's most concentrated image of the Revolution's social inversion.

> Tongues of fire rose from Monico's house, no one could account for how they started. [PN=026-130]

Azuela's deadpan narration of the burning of Monico's house is a masterstroke of irony: the destruction is witnessed by all, claimed by none.

## Role in the Novel

Don Monico is less a character than a symbol — the embodiment of the Porfirian rural order that the Revolution targeted. His burning house, like the [[Burning of Demetrio's House]] in Chapter I, is one of the novel's bookend images of destruction: the poor man's house burns first, then the rich man's. But where [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s burning drives him into rebellion, Monico's burning signals that the rebellion has become something closer to vengeance. The fact that Monico is never given a voice — never speaks a line of dialogue — is itself a political choice: the old order, in Azuela's telling, does not get to explain itself.
