## Overview

Tepatitlán is a town in the Jalisco highlands that serves as a geographic waypoint early in Part III of *The Underdogs*. As [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio Macías]]'s band moves south toward [[aguascalientes|Aguascalientes]], Tepatitlán is the first named stop on their itinerary, and the scenes there establish the tone of the novel's final section — a mixture of movement, uncertainty, and the gathering darkness of the revolution's late phase.

## Location & Geography

Tepatitlán is located in the state of [[jalisco|Jalisco]], in the highland region east of [[guadalajara|Guadalajara]] and west of the [[aguascalientes|Aguascalientes]] state line. The narrative describes it as "nestling against the hillside" (032-053), suggesting a town built into the characteristic canyon-and-slope topography of the Jalisco highlands. From Tepatitlán, the route leads westward to [[cuquio|Cuquio]] and eventually to Guadalajara (032-006).

## Role in the Narrative

Tepatitlán appears at the opening of Part III, as Demetrio's band departs before dawn: "Their silhouettes wavered in the early morning mist" (032-001). The departure from Tepatitlán is rendered with an almost painterly quality — a moment of aesthetic distance before the narrative plunges into the political complications of the [[aguascalientes|Aguascalientes]] Convention.

The stay in Tepatitlán is brief, but the town provides one of the novel's few moments of visual tranquility: "the smiling row of houses in Tepatitlan nestling against the hillside" (032-053). This pastoral image is immediately undercut by the political and strategic anxieties that dominate the surrounding scenes, as [[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]] and [[venancio|Venancio]] debate their course.

Later, Tepatitlán recurs as a reference point for social grievances: a "peon from Tepatitlan who complained about his boss" (039-053) is cited as an example of the kind of local, personal complaints that the revolution has absorbed and transformed. The man from Tepatitlán stands for the thousands of peasants whose individual grievances against landlords and caciques became fuel for the revolutionary armies — even as the revolution itself failed to deliver systemic justice.
