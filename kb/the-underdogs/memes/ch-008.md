[008-001]On the morrow, Luis Cervantes was barely able to get up. His injured

[008-002]leg trailing behind him, he shuffled from hut to hut in search of a

[008-003]little alcohol, a kettle of boiled water and some rags. With unfailing

[008-004]kindness, Camilla provided him with all that he wanted.

[008-005]As he began washing his foot, she sat beside him, and, with typical

[008-006]mountaineer's curiosity, inquired:

[008-007]"Tell me, who learned you how to cure people? Why did you boil that

[008-008]water? Why did you boil the rags? Look, look, how careful you are about

[008-009]everything! And what did you put on your hands? Really.... And why did

[008-010]you pour on alcohol? I just knew alcohol was good to rub on when you

[008-011]had a bellyache, but ... Oh, I see! So you was going to be a doctor,

[008-012]huh? Ha, ha, that's a good one! Why don't you mix it with cold water?

[008-013]Well, there's a funny sort of a trick. Oh, stop fooling me ... the

[008-014]idea: little animals alive in the water unless you boil it! Ugh! Well,

[008-015]I can't see nothing in it myself."

[008-016]Camilla continued to cross-question him with such familiarity that she

[008-017]suddenly found herself addressing him intimately, in the singular tu.

[008-018]Absorbed in his own thoughts, Luis Cervantes had ceased listening to

[008-019]her. He thought:

[008-020]Where are those men on Pancho Villa's payroll, so admirably equipped

[008-021]and mounted, who only get paid in those pure silver pieces Villa coins

[008-022]at the Chihuahua mint? Bah! Barely two dozen half-naked mangy men, some

[008-023]of them riding decrepit mares with the coat nibbled off from neck to

[008-024]withers. Can the accounts given by the Government newspapers and by

[008-025]myself be really true and are these so-called revolutionists simply

[008-026]bandits grouped together, using the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] as a wonderful pretext

[008-027]to glut their thirst for gold and blood? Is it all a lie, then? Were

[008-028]their sympathizers talking a lot of exalted nonsense?

[008-029]If on one hand the Government newspapers vied with each other in noisy

[008-030]proclamation of Federal victory after victory, why then had a paymaster

[008-031]on his way from Guadalajara started the rumor that President Huerta's

[008-032]friends and relatives were abandoning the capital and scuttling away to

[008-033]the nearest port? Was Huerta's, "I shall have peace, at no matter what

[008-034]cost," a meaningless growl? Well, it looked as though the

[008-035]revolutionists or bandits, call them what you will, were going to

[008-036]depose the Government. Tomorrow would therefore belong wholly to them.

[008-037]A man must consequently be on their side, only on their side.

[008-038]"No," he said to himself almost aloud, "I don't think I've made a

[008-039]mistake this time."

[008-040]"What did you say?" Camilla asked. "I thought you'd lost your

[008-041]tongue.... I thought the mice had eaten it up!"

[008-042]Luis Cervantes frowned and cast a hostile glance at this little plump

[008-043]monkey with her bronzed complexion, her ivory teeth, and her thick

[008-044]square toes.

[008-045]"Look here, Tenderfoot, you know how to tell fairy stories, don't you?"

[008-046]For all answer, Luis made an impatient gesture and moved off, the

[008-047]girl's ecstatic glance following his retreating figure until it was

[008-048]lost on the river path. So profound was her absorption that she

[008-049]shuddered in nervous surprise as she heard the voice of her neighbor,

[008-050]one-eyed Maria Antonia, who had been spying from her hut, shouting:

[008-051]"Hey, you there: give him some love powder. Then he might fall for you."

[008-052]"That's what you'd do, all right!"
