[032-001]Before dawn, they left for Tepatitlan. Their silhouettes wavered

[032-002]indistinctly over the road and the fields that bordered it, rising and

[032-003]falling with the monotonous, rhythmical gait of their horses, then

[032-004]faded away in the nacreous light of the swooning moon that bathed the

[032-005]valley. Dogs barked in the distance.

[032-006]"By noon we'll reach Tepatitlan, Cuquio tomorrow, and then ... on to

[032-007]the sierra!" [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] said.

[032-008]"Don't you think it advisable to go to Aguascalientes first, General?"

[032-009]Luis Cervantes asked.

[032-010]"What for?"

[032-011]"Our funds are melting slowly."

[032-012]"Nonsense ... forty thousand pesos in eight days!"

[032-013]"Well, you see, just this week we recruited over five hundred new men;

[032-014]all the money's gone in advance loans and gratuities," Luis Cervantes

[032-015]answered in a low voice.

[032-016]"No! We'll go straight to the sierra. We'll see later on."

[032-017]"Yes, to the sierra!" many of the men shouted.

[032-018]"To the sierra! To the sierra! Hurrah for the mountains!"

[032-019]The plains seemed to torture them; they spoke with enthusiasm, almost

[032-020]with delirium, of the sierra. They thought of the mountains as of a

[032-021]most desirable mistress long since unvisited.

[032-022]Dawn broke behind a cloud of fine reddish dust; the sun rose an immense

[032-023]curtain of fiery purple. Luis Cervantes pulled his reins and waited for

[032-024]Quail. "What's the last word on our deal, Quail?"

[032-025]"I told you, Tenderfoot: two hundred for the watch alone."

[032-026]"No! I'll buy the lot: watches, rings, everything else. How much?"

[032-027]Quail hesitated, turned slightly pale; then he cried spiritedly:

[032-028]"Two thousand in bills, for the whole business!"

[032-029]Luis Cervantes gave himself away. His eyes shone with such an obvious

[032-030]greed that Quail recanted and said:

[032-031]"Oh, I was just fooling you. I won't sell nothing! Just the watch, see?

[032-032]And that's only because I owe Pancracio two hundred. He beat me at

[032-033]cards last night!"

[032-034]Luis Cervantes pulled out four crisp "double-face" bills of Villa's

[032-035]issue and placed them in Quail's hands.

[032-036]"I'd like to buy the lot.... Besides, nobody will offer you more than

[032-037]that!"

[032-038]As the sun began to beat down upon them, Manteca suddenly shouted:

[032-039]"Ho, Blondie, your orderly says he doesn't care to go on living. He

[032-040]says he's too damned tired to walk."

[032-041]The prisoner had fallen in the middle of the road, utterly exhausted.

[032-042]"Well, well!" Blondie shouted, retracing his steps. "So little mama's

[032-043]boy is tired, eh? Poor little fellow. I'll buy a glass case and keep

[032-044]you in a corner of my house just as if you were the Virgin Mary's own

[032-045]little son. You've got to reach home first, see? So I'll help you a

[032-046]little, sonny!"

[032-047]He drew his sword out and struck the prisoner several times.

[032-048]"Let's have a look at your rope, Pancracio," he said. There was a

[032-049]strange gleam in his eyes. Quail observed that the prisoner no longer

[032-050]moved arm or leg. Blondie burst into a loud guffaw: "The Goddamned

[032-051]fool. Just as I was learning him to do without food, too!"

[032-052]"Well, mate, we're almost to Guadalajara," Venancio said, glancing over

[032-053]the smiling row of houses in Tepatitlan nestling against the hillside.

[032-054]They entered joyously. From every window rosy cheeks, dark luminous

[032-055]eyes observed them. The schools were quickly converted into barracks;

[032-056]Demetrio found lodging in the chapel of an abandoned church.

[032-057]The soldiers scattered about as usual pretending to seek arms and

[032-058]horses, but in reality for the sole purpose of looting.

[032-059]In the afternoon some of Demetrio's men lay stretched out on the church

[032-060]steps, scratching their bellies. Venancio, his chest and shoulders

[032-061]bare, was gravely occupied in killing the fleas in his shirt. A man

[032-062]drew near the wall and sought permission to speak to the commander. The

[032-063]soldiers raised their heads; but no one answered.

[032-064]"I'm a widower, gentlemen. I've got nine children and I barely make a

[032-065]living with the sweat of my brow. Don't be hard on a poor widower!"

[032-066]"Don't you worry about women, Uncle," said Meco, who was rubbing his

[032-067]feet with tallow, "we've got War Paint here with us; you can have her

[032-068]for nothing."

[032-069]The man smiled bitterly.

[032-070]"She's only got one fault," Pancracio observed, stretched out on the

[032-071]ground, staring at the blue sky, "she goes mad over any man she sees."

[032-072]They laughed loudly; but Venancio with utmost gravity pointed to the

[032-073]chapel door. The stranger entered timidly and confided his troubles to

[032-074]Demetrio. The soldiers had cleaned him out; they had not left a single

[032-075]grain of corn.

[032-076]"Why did you let them?" Demetrio asked indolently.

[032-077]The man persisted, lamenting and weeping. Luis Cervantes was about to

[032-078]throw him out with an insult. But Camilla intervened.

[032-079]"Come on, Demetrio, don't be harsh, give him an order to get his corn

[032-080]back."

[032-081]Luis Cervantes was obliged to obey; he scrawled a few lines to which

[032-082]Demetrio appended an illegible scratch.

[032-083]"May God repay you, my child! God will lead you to heaven that you may

[032-084]enjoy his glory. Ten bushels of corn are barely enough for this year's

[032-085]food!" the man cried, weeping for gratitude. Then he took the paper,

[032-086]kissed everybody's hand, and withdrew.
