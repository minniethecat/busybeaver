## Overview

Torreón is a northern Mexican city that appears in the novel as a landmark on the map of revolutionary violence — a place Villa's forces captured, a place where men committed acts they remember with uneasy pride, and a place that marks a fighter's allegiance. Unlike some locations described for their physical character, Torreón enters the narrative as a name that carries weight: "When we took Torreón" functions as both a credential and a confession.

## Location & Geography

Torreón lies in the Laguna region of southwestern Coahuila, an arid basin where the Nazas and Aguanaval rivers converge. Its importance derived from its position as a railway junction connecting Mexico's northern mining and agricultural regions to the central plateau. In the novel it is listed alongside [[ciudad-juarez|Ciudad Juárez]], Tierra Blanca, and [[chihuahua|Chihuahua]] as one of the named battle sites that define Villa's legendary northern campaign (020-015).

## Role in the Narrative

Torreón first appears as part of the litany of Villa's victories that Natera's followers recount to Demetrio's men: "Villa! Villa's battles! Ciudad Juárez ... Tierra Blanca ... Chihuahua ... Torreón..." (020-015). The name is one of a string — each place a rung in Villa's ascent, each name a reason for the astonished silence of Demetrio's fighters.

The city recurs in a darker register when a man under Demetrio's command recounts: "When I was up at Torreón I killed an old lady who refused to sell me some enchiladas. She was angry, I can tell you; I got no enchiladas but I felt satisfied anyhow!" (022-108). The anecdote is told matter-of-factly, almost boastfully — Torreón here is the site of casual revolutionary brutality, the place where "feeling satisfied" substitutes for getting what one wanted.

Later, a persistent accuser confronts a fighter with the record of his shifting allegiances: "I know who you are. When we took Torreón you were with General Urbina. In Zacatecas you were with General Natera and then you shifted to the Jalisco troops. Am I lying?" (037-023). Torreón functions as a timestamp on a revolutionary's résumé — the name that fixes a moment in the chronology of loyalties.
