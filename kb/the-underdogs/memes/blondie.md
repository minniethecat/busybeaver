## Overview

Blondie is a soldier who enters [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band in the later chapters of [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. Introduced in a raucous cantina scene alongside [[War Paint]], Blondie is a "round little fellow" (023-084) whose irrepressible humor, sudden rages, and theatrical antics make him one of the novel's most vivid minor characters. He rises to the rank of major and remains with [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band through the final campaigns.

## Biography

Blondie first appears in Chapter XXII, introduced to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] at a cantina: "…e honor of introducing you to Blondie" (022-052). His first interaction with [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is marked by laughter and bluster: "[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] bowed assent, while Blondie, laughing, said…" (022-071). From the start, Blondie's personality is defined by excess—in drink, in speech, in emotion.

By Chapter XXIII, Blondie has become a fixture in the band. [[Mariano Azuela]] describes him physically: "Blondie was a round little fellow, wi…" (023-084). He delivers "eulogy after eulogy" (023-089), demonstrating his gift for extravagant speech.

Blondie is promoted to major alongside [[Luis Cervantes]] (026-013), marking his rise within the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary ranks despite his buffoonish exterior. He survives through the final chapters, appearing at the sacking of a town in Chapter XXIX where he and [[War Paint]] "had tied up" a prisoner (029-006) and where he calls out "To [[jalisco|Jalisco]], boys!" (029-031).

## Character

Blondie is defined by theatrical emotional swings. One moment he is laughing and telling stories; the next he "suddenly screamed, his shrill…" (024-069) in a jealous rage. He is possessive of women and quick to draw his gun: "'Keep your eye on me, boys,' Blondie went on, gun in hand. 'I'm go…'" (024-077). His relationship with [[War Paint]] is a mix of rivalry, camaraderie, and sexual tension—they bicker, drink together, and fight side by side.

Despite his comic excess, Blondie has a pragmatic streak. "Blondie's sound reasoning" (029-018) is noted in a moment of tactical discussion, suggesting that beneath the bluster lies a survivor's instinct.

## Key Passages

> "Here, waiter," Blondie cried, "I ordered ice water. …" [PN=022-093]

Blondie's comic demands at the cantina establish his character as a man who treats every setting as his personal stage.

> "You don't know this fellow Blondie yet," said War Paint, noticin… [PN=024-015]

[[War Paint]]'s amused warning to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] hints at Blondie's unpredictable nature—he is a man who cannot be taken at face value.

> "Boys," Blondie suddenly screamed, his shrill… [PN=024-069]

The sudden shift from laughter to fury is characteristic of Blondie's volatile temperament, a volatility that Azuela presents as symptomatic of a world in which violence is always simmering beneath the surface.

> "To Jalisco, boys!" cried Blondie, pounding on the counter. [PN=029-031]

In one of his final appearances, Blondie's irrepressible energy remains intact, calling the band forward to new campaigns even as the Revolution's meaning drains away.
