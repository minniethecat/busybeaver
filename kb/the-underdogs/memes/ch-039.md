[039-001]Juchipila rose in the distance, white, bathed in sunlight, shining in

[039-002]the midst of a thick forest at the foot of a proud, lofty mountain,

[039-003]pleated like a turban.

[039-004]Some of the soldiers, gazing at the spire of the church, sighed sadly.

[039-005]They marched forward through the canyon, uncertain, unsteady, as blind

[039-006]men walking without a hand to guide them. The bitterness of the exodus

[039-007]pervaded them.

[039-008]"Is that town Juchipila?" Valderrama asked.

[039-009]In the first stage of his drunkenness, Valderrama had been counting the

[039-010]crosses scattered along the road, along the trails, in the hollows near

[039-011]the rocks, in the tortuous paths, and along the riverbanks. Crosses of

[039-012]black timber newly varnished, makeshift crosses built out of two logs,

[039-013]crosses of stones piled up and plastered together, crosses whitewashed

[039-014]on crumbling walls, humble crosses drawn with charcoal on the surface

[039-015]of whitish rocks. The traces of the first blood shed by the

[039-016]revolutionists of 1910, murdered by the Government.

[039-017]Before Juchipila was lost from sight, Valderrama got off his horse,

[039-018]bent down, kneeled, and gravely kissed the ground.

[039-019]The soldiers passed by without stopping. Some laughed at the crazy man,

[039-020]others jested. Valderrama, deaf to all about him, breathed his unctuous

[039-021]prayer:

[039-022]"O Juchipila, cradle of the Revolution of 1910, O blessed land, land

[039-023]steeped in the blood of martyrs, blood of dreamers, the only true men..."

[039-024]"Because they had no time to be bad!" an ex-Federal officer interjected

[039-025]as he rode.

[039-026]Interrupting his prayer, Valderrama frowned, burst into stentorian

[039-027]laughter, reechoed by the rocks, and ran toward the officer begging for

[039-028]a swallow of tequila.

[039-029]Soldiers minus an arm or leg, cripples, rheumatics, and consumptives

[039-030]spoke bitterly of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]. Young whippersnappers were given officers'

[039-031]commissions and wore stripes on their hats without a day's service,

[039-032]even before they knew how to handle a rifle, while the veterans,

[039-033]exhausted in a hundred battles, now incapacitated for work, the

[039-034]veterans who had set out as simple privates, were still simple

[039-035]privates. The few remaining officers among Demetrio's friends also

[039-036]grumbled, because his staff was made up of wealthy, dapper young men

[039-037]who oiled their hair and used perfume.

[039-038]"The worst part of it," Venancio said, "is that we're gettin'

[039-039]overcrowded with Federals!"

[039-040]Anastasio himself, who invariably found only praise for Demetrio's

[039-041]conduct, now seemed to share the general discontent.

[039-042]"See here, brothers," he said, "I spits out the truth when I sees

[039-043]something. I always tell the boss that if these people stick to us very

[039-044]long we'll be in a hell of a fix. Certainly! How can anyone think

[039-045]otherwise? I've no hair on my tongue; and by the mother that bore me,

[039-046]I'm going to tell Demetrio so myself."

[039-047]Demetrio listened benevolently, and, when Anastasio had finished, he

[039-048]replied:

[039-049]"You're right, there's no gettin' around it, we're in a bad way. The

[039-050]soldiers grumble about the officers, the officers grumble about us,

[039-051]see? And we're damn well ready now to send both Villa and Carranza to

[039-052]hell to have a good time all by themselves.... I guess we're in the

[039-053]same fix as that peon from Tepatitlan who complained about his boss all

[039-054]day long but worked on just the same. That's us. We kick and kick, but

[039-055]we keep on killing and killing. But there's no use in saying anything

[039-056]to them!"

[039-057]"Why, Demetrio?"

[039-058]"Hm, I don't know.... Because ... because ... do you see? ... What

[039-059]we've got to do is to make the men toe the mark. I've got orders to

[039-060]stop a band of men coming through Cuquio, see? In a few days we'll have

[039-061]to fight the Carranzistas. It will be great to beat the hell out of

[039-062]them."

[039-063]Valderrama, the tramp, who had enlisted in Demetrio's army one day

[039-064]without anyone remembering the time or the place, overheard some of

[039-065]Demetrio's words. Fools do not eat fire. That very day Valderrama

[039-066]disappeared mysteriously as he had come.
