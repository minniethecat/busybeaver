## Overview

Remigia is a camp woman and healer attached to [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. She appears in two significant episodes — first as a nurse treating [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s fever wound in Chapter IV, then as the central figure in a Chapter IX scene of camp life in which her herbal knowledge and midwifery skills make her indispensable to the community of soldaderas. Unlike [[War Paint]], who fights alongside the men, Remigia represents the traditional but essential role of women as caregivers and keepers of folk medical knowledge.

## Biography

Remigia first appears when [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is suffering from fever after being wounded. She is the one who diagnoses the danger: "It's terrible for the fever" (004-075), recognizing that his condition could be fatal. She "offered them all she had, chilies and salt, and tortillas" (004-081), tending to the wounded man with the limited resources available to a camp follower.

Her most sustained appearance is in Chapter IX, which offers a rare close-up of the women's world within the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary camp. The chapter opens with soldiers calling for her help — "Ho there, Remigia, lend me some eggs" (009-001) — establishing her as a known resource for food and medicine. She sits "Indian-fashion" with another woman (009-010), operates as a de facto midwife or surgeon (009-069, 009-070), and dispenses folk blessings (009-074) alongside practical advice.

Remigia is portrayed as physically striking — "naked from the waist up, strong" (009-015) — and as a figure of calm authority among the women. When a neighbor asks for help, she lowers "her eyes to indicate her consent" (009-029), and when called upon to perform a medical procedure, she answers with modest deflection: "I've got no laurel leaves, honey" (009-037) before proceeding with evident competence. The procedure itself involves pulling "a long and curved knife" from a reed sheath (009-070), and her instructions are followed precisely: "Obeying Remigia's instructions, [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] lay still" (009-079).

## Key Passages

> "It's terrible for the fever," said Remigia, and she offered them all she had: chilies and salt, and tortillas. [PN=004-075, 004-081]

Remigia's first act is diagnosis and generosity — recognizing the danger of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s wound and giving what little she has to help.

> Remigia, naked from the waist up, strong and majestic… [PN=009-015]

Azuela's description of Remigia emphasizes her physical presence and dignity, portraying her not as a victim but as a figure of natural authority.

> "Come on, Remigia, you do it, you certainly know how." Out of a reed sheath, Remigia pulled a long and curved knife. [PN=009-069, 009-070]

The camp women's reliance on Remigia's medical skill — and her quiet competence with the knife — establish her as the community's unofficial surgeon.

> Obeying Remigia's instructions, Demetrio lay still. [PN=009-079]

Even the band's commander submits to Remigia's authority in matters of medicine, a rare inversion of the camp's usual power dynamics.

## Role in the Novel

Remigia represents a dimension of the Revolution often overlooked in battle-centered narratives: the community of women who sustained the armies through cooking, nursing, and folk medicine. Her Chapter IX episode is one of the novel's few sustained depictions of women's world within the camp, and her competence with knife and herbs underscores the skills that made survival possible for the thousands of soldaderas who followed the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary armies. Where [[War Paint]] fights, Remigia heals — and both roles, Azuela suggests, are essential to understanding the full human reality of the conflict.
