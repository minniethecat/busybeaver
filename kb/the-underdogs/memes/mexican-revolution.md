# Mexican Revolution

## Overview

The **Mexican Revolution** (1910–1920) is the historical backdrop and central thematic concern of [[mariano-azuela|Mariano Azuela]]'s *The Underdogs*. The novel explores the revolution not as a heroic struggle but as a complex, often contradictory human experience. Through the eyes of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio Macías]] and his band of followers, Azuela examines how revolutionary ideals collide with human nature, self-interest, and the brutal realities of war.

## Context in the Mexican Revolution

The revolution began in 1910 with [[francisco-i-madero|Francisco I. Madero]]'s uprising against the dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz. After [[francisco-i-madero|Madero]]'s assassination in 1913, the conflict widened into a multi-sided civil war involving Constitutionalist forces led by [[venustiano-carranza|Venustiano Carranza]], [[villistas|Villistas]] under [[pancho-villa|Pancho Villa]], Zapatistas under Emiliano Zapata, and the counterrevolutionary [[federal-army|Federal Army]] of [[victoriano-huerta|Victoriano Huerta]]. Azuela served as a doctor with [[villistas|Villa's forces]], giving him firsthand knowledge of the revolution's realities `(P01-014)`.

## Expression in the Novel

### Revolutionary Ideals and Their Corruption

[[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]], the educated medical student who joins [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band, articulates revolutionary ideals but is repeatedly shown to be self-serving. The novel questions whether the revolution truly benefits the common people, as Cervantes cynically observes that he stands to gain "whether the revolution wins or loses" `(007-054)`. At the same time, the narrator notes that "the revolution benefits the poor, the ignorant" `(007-057)`, suggesting a more ambivalent view.

### Revolutionaries or Bandits?

A central tension in the novel is whether [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s men are revolutionaries fighting for a cause or simply bandits using the revolution as a pretext for violence and plunder. The narrator observes men "grouped together, using the revolution as a wonderful pretext" `(008-026)` for their actions. The distinction between "revolutionists or bandits" `(008-035)` is deliberately blurred throughout the novel.

### The Revolution's Human Cost

Before the revolution, ordinary people had stability: "Before the revolution, I had my land all plowed" `(012-084)`. The war disrupted traditional life, and the novel shows how even those who fight for the revolution become disconnected from its original purpose. As Cervantes explains to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], "the revolution is..." `(013-065)` — but the sentence is left unfinished, as if the meaning of the revolution itself can never be fully articulated.
