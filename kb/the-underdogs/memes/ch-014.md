[014-001]"You ought to see how clear that fellow can make things, Compadre,"

[014-002][[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] said. All morning long he had been pondering as much of Luis

[014-003]Cervantes' speech as he had understood.

[014-004]"I heard him too," Anastasio answered. "People who can read and write

[014-005]get things clear, all right; nothing was ever truer. But what I can't

[014-006]make out is how you're going to go and meet Natera with as few men as

[014-007]we have."

[014-008]"That's nothing. We're going to do things different now. They tell me

[014-009]that as soon as Crispin Robles enters a town he gets hold of all the

[014-010]horses and guns in the place; then he goes to the jail and lets all the

[014-011]jailbirds out, and, before you know it, he's got plenty of men, all

[014-012]right. You'll see. You know I'm beginning to feel that we haven't done

[014-013]things right so far. It don't seem right somehow that this city guy

[014-014]should be able to tell us what to do."

[014-015]"Ain't it wonderful to be able to read and write!"

[014-016]They both sighed, sadly. Luis Cervantes came in with several others to

[014-017]find out the day of their departure.

[014-018]"We're leaving no later than tomorrow," said Demetrio without

[014-019]hesitation.

[014-020]Quail suggested that musicians be summoned from the neighboring hamlet

[014-021]and that a farewell dance be given. His idea met with enthusiasm on all

[014-022]sides.

[014-023]"We'll go, then," Pancracio shouted, "but I'm certainly going in good

[014-024]company this time. My sweetheart's coming along with me!"

[014-025]Demetrio replied that he too would willingly take along a girl he had

[014-026]set his eye on, but that he hoped none of his men would leave bitter

[014-027]memories behind them as the Federals did.

[014-028]"You won't have long to wait. Everything will be arranged when you

[014-029]return," Luis Cervantes whispered to him.

[014-030]"What do you mean?" Demetrio asked. "I thought that you and Camilla..."

[014-031]"There's not a word of truth in it, Chief. She likes you but she's

[014-032]afraid of you, that's all."

[014-033]"Really? Is that really true?"

[014-034]"Yes. But I think you're quite right in not wanting to leave any

[014-035]bitter feelings behind you as you go. When you come back as a

[014-036]conqueror, everything will be different. They'll all thank you for it

[014-037]even."

[014-038]"By God, you're certainly a shrewd one," Demetrio replied, patting him

[014-039]on the back.

[014-040]At sundown, Camilla went to the river to fetch water as usual. Luis

[014-041]Cervantes, walking down the same trail, met her. Camilla felt her

[014-042]heart leap to her mouth. But, without taking the slightest notice of

[014-043]her, Luis Cervantes hastily took one of the turns and disappeared among

[014-044]the rocks.

[014-045]At this hour, as usual, the calcinated rocks, the sun-burnt branches,

[014-046]and the dry weeds faded into the semi-obscurity of the shadows. The

[014-047]wind blew softly, the green lances of the young corn leaves rustling in

[014-048]the twilight. Nothing was changed; all nature was as she had found it

[014-049]before, evening upon evening; but in the stones and the dry weeds, amid

[014-050]the fragrance of the air and the light whir of falling leaves, Camilla

[014-051]sensed a new strangeness, a vast desolation in everything about her.

[014-052]Rounding a huge eroded rock, suddenly Camilla found herself face to

[014-053]face with Luis, who was seated on a stone, hatless, his legs dangling.

[014-054]"Listen, you might come down here to say good-bye."

[014-055]Luis Cervantes was obliging enough; he jumped down and joined her.

[014-056]"You're proud, ain't you? Have I been so mean that you don't even want

[014-057]to talk to me?"

[014-058]"Why do you say that, Camilla? You've been extremely kind to me; why,

[014-059]you've been more than a friend, you've taken care of me as if you were

[014-060]my sister. Now I'm about to leave, I'm very grateful to you; I'll

[014-061]always remember you."

[014-062]"Liar!" Camilla said, her face transfigured with joy. "Suppose I hadn't

[014-063]come after you?"

[014-064]"I intended to say good-bye to you at the dance this evening."

[014-065]"What dance? If there's a dance, I'll not go to it."

[014-066]"Why not?"

[014-067]"Because I can't stand that horrible man ... Demetrio!"

[014-068]"Don't be silly, child," said Luis. "He's really very fond of you.

[014-069]Don't go and throw away this opportunity. You'll never have one like it

[014-070]again in your life. Don't you know that Demetrio is on the point of

[014-071]becoming a general, you silly girl? He'll be a very wealthy man, with

[014-072]horses galore; and you'll have jewels and clothes and a fine house and

[014-073]a lot of money to spend. Just imagine what a life you would lead with

[014-074]him!"

[014-075]Camilla stared up at the blue sky so he should not read the expression

[014-076]in her eyes. A dead leaf shook slowly loose from the crest of a tree

[014-077]swinging slowly on the wind, fell like a small dead butterfly at her

[014-078]feet. She bent down and took it in her fingers. Then, without looking

[014-079]at him, she murmured:

[014-080]"It's horrible to hear you talk like that.... I like you ... no one

[014-081]else.... Ah, well, go then, go: I feel ashamed now. Please leave me!"

[014-082]She threw away the leaf she had crumpled in her hand and covered her

[014-083]face with a corner of her apron. When she opened her eyes, Luis

[014-084]Cervantes had disappeared.

[014-085]She followed the river trail. The river seemed to have been sprinkled

[014-086]with a fine red dust. On its surface drifted now a sky of variegated

[014-087]colors, now the dark crags, half light, half shadow. Myriads of

[014-088]luminous insects twinkled in a hollow. Camilla, standing on the beach

[014-089]of washed, round stones, caught a reflection of herself in the waters;

[014-090]she saw herself in her yellow blouse with the green ribbons, her white

[014-091]skirt, her carefully combed hair, her wide eyebrows and broad forehead,

[014-092]exactly as she had dressed to please Luis. She burst into tears.

[014-093]Among the reeds, the frogs chanted the implacable melancholy of the

[014-094]hour. Perched on a dry root, a dove wept also.
