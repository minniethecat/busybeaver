## Overview

Venustiano Carranza was the "First Chief" of the [[constitutionalist-army|Constitutionalist Army]] and a central political figure of the [[Mexican Revolution]]. In _The Underdogs_, Carranza appears primarily as a name on the soldiers' lips — a distant authority whose legitimacy they question and whose cause they ostensibly serve without fully understanding why. The novel portrays the gap between [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary leadership and the common fighters with bitter irony (027-036).

## Biography

### Rise to Leadership

Carranza emerged as a major [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary leader following [[Francisco I. Madero]]'s assassination. As governor of Coahuila, he denounced [[Victoriano Huerta]]'s coup and proclaimed the Constitutionalist movement, which sought to restore constitutional order. His faction attracted regional commanders like [[Panfilo Natera]], uniting them under a common banner against the [[Federal Army]].

### Carranza in The Underdogs

The novel's treatment of Carranza is notably sardonic. The soldiers who fight under his banner have only the vaguest notion of who he is. When one soldier asks "who the devil, then, is this man Carranza?" (034-026), the question captures the distance between political leadership and the men who do the fighting. Another soldier cynically remarks that men "rise up in arms to make some Carranza or Villa President of our Republic" (027-036), suggesting the interchangeability of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary leaders in the minds of the common fighters.

### The Villa–Carranza Split

The [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary coalition fractured after the defeat of [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s [[federal-army|Federal Army]]. By the novel's later chapters, "it's Villa fighting Carranza" (035-107), with the two former allies now locked in a civil war within the Revolution. The Convention government's refusal to "recognize Carranza" (035-110) deepens the political crisis. A soldier reports that "Obregon and Carranza is winning all along the line" (037-043), but another immediately asks: "Obregon? Carranza? What's the difference?" (037-045), a devastating comment on the meaninglessness of factional politics to the common people.

## Thematic Significance

Carranza embodies the novel's critique of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary politics. His name is invoked repeatedly but never with genuine reverence — unlike [[Pancho Villa]], whose name is "magic," Carranza's is merely a political label. The soldiers who fight and die for "Carranza" cannot explain who he is or what he stands for. This anonymity of the political leader stands in stark contrast to the visceral presence of Villa, highlighting Azuela's argument that the Revolution's political class was distant from — and ultimately betrayed — the *los de abajo* who fought its battles.
