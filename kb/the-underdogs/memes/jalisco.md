Jalisco, a state in central-western Mexico, serves as the geographic and
cultural heartland of [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. It is the home
region of [[Demetrio Macías]], the birthplace of Azuela himself, and the
setting for much of the novel's early action. The state's rugged sierras,
hacienda economy, and tradition of rural rebellion made it one of the key
theaters of the [[Mexican Revolution]]. In the novel, Jalisco represents
the *patria chica* — the local world that the peasant [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary fights
to defend and to which he ultimately returns.

## Geography and Economy

Jalisco stretches from the Pacific coast to the central highlands, encompassing
diverse terrain: coastal plains, the Sierra Madre Occidental, and the altiplano
around [[Lagos de Moreno]] — Azuela's own birthplace. In the 1910s, the state's
economy was dominated by haciendas producing corn, cattle, and tequila.
Landless peasants (*peones*) worked estates owned by a small oligarchy, a
system that the [[Mexican Revolution]] promised to overturn.

## Role in the Narrative

### Demetrio's Homeland

The novel opens in rural Jalisco, where [[Demetrio Macías]]'s house is burned
by [[Federal Army|Federales]] — the inciting event that drives him into armed
rebellion (001–005). The landscape of Jalisco — its rocky hills, maize fields,
and scattered *ranchos* — shapes [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s identity as a *ranchero* turned
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary. He fights not for abstract political ideals but for revenge and
for the right to return to his land.

### The Revolutionary Road

As [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band moves north toward [[Zacatecas]], the Jalisco landscape
recedes but never disappears from the characters' consciousness. References to
Jalisco punctuate the narrative, reminding readers that these peasant-soldiers
are fighting far from home for a cause that increasingly seems disconnected
from the local grievances that first mobilized them (012, 018).

> They had left Jalisco behind weeks ago. The hills here were different —
> drier, more barren. Demetrio sometimes thought of the green maize fields
> of his home and felt a sharp, unnameable ache. (018)

### Azuela's Double Vision

For Azuela, Jalisco was both home and object of critique. Born in [[Lagos de
Moreno]], he knew the state's rural society intimately — its hierarchies, its
pieties, its violence. His depiction of Jalisco's peasants is neither
romanticized nor condescending; it is the clear-eyed portrait of a native son
who loves his land too much to lie about it.

## Key Passages

> The sierra of Jalisco rose blue in the distance, tier upon tier of hills

> smoldering. (005)

> seemed to fill his mouth like a stone. (012)

## Historical Context

During the [[Mexican Revolution]], Jalisco was contested territory. While the
state's elite generally supported the status quo, its rural population provided
recruits for [[Pancho Villa]]'s [[villistas|División del Norte]] and, later, for the
Constitutionalist forces of [[Venustiano Carranza]]. The state's experience
mirrored the national pattern: [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary promises of [[[[agrarian-reform|Agrarian Reform]]|land
reform]] mobilized peasants, but the actual redistribution of hacienda land
remained incomplete long after the fighting ended.
