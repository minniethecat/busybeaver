[012-001][[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s wound had already healed. They began to discuss various

[012-002]projects to go northward where, according to rumor, the rebels had

[012-003]beaten the Federal troops all along the line.

[012-004]A certain incident came to precipitate their action. Seated on a crag

[012-005]of the sierra in the cool of the afternoon breeze, Luis Cervantes gazed

[012-006]away in the distance, dreaming and killing time. Below the narrow rock

[012-007]Pancracio and Manteca, lying like lizards between the jarales along one

[012-008]of the river margins, were playing cards. Anastasio Montanez, looking

[012-009]on indifferently, turned his black hairy face toward Luis Cervantes

[012-010]and, leveling his kindly gaze upon him, asked:

[012-011]"Why so sad, you from the city? What are you daydreaming about? Come on

[012-012]over here and let's have a chat!"

[012-013]Luis Cervantes did not move; Anastasio went over to him and sat down

[012-014]beside him like a friend.

[012-015]"What you need is the excitement of the city. I wager you shine your

[012-016]shoes every day and wear a necktie. Now, I may look dirty and my

[012-017]clothes may be torn to shreds, but I'm not really what I seem to be.

[012-018]I'm not here because I've got to be and don't you think so. Why, I own

[012-019]twenty oxen. Certainly I do; ask my friend Demetrio. I cleared ten

[012-020]bushels last harvest time. You see, if there's one thing I love, that's

[012-021]riling these Government fellows and making them furious. The last

[012-022]scrape I had--it'll be eight months gone now, ever since I've joined

[012-023]these men--I stuck my knife into some captain. He was just a nobody, a

[012-024]little Government squirt. I pinked him here, see, right under the

[012-025]navel. And that's why I'm here: that and because I wanted to give my

[012-026]mate Demetrio a hand." "Christ! The bloody little darling of my life!"

[012-027]Manteca shouted, waxing enthusiastic over a winning hand. He placed a

[012-028]twenty-cent silver coin on the jack of spades.

[012-029]"If you want my opinion, I'm not much on gambling. Do you want to bet?

[012-030]Well, come on then, I'm game. How do you like the sound of this leather

[012-031]snake jingling, eh?"

[012-032]Anastasio shook his belt; the silver coins rang as he shook them

[012-033]together.

[012-034]Meanwhile, Pancracio dealt the cards, the jack of spades turned up out

[012-035]of the deck and a quarrel ensued. Altercation, noise, then shouts, and,

[012-036]at last, insults. Pancracio brought his stony face close to Manteca,

[012-037]who looked at him with snake's eyes, convulsive, foaming at the mouth.

[012-038]Another moment and they would have been exchanging blows. Having

[012-039]completely exhausted their stock of direct insults, they now resorted

[012-040]to the most flowery and ornate insulting of each other's ancestors,

[012-041]male and female, paternal or maternal. Yet nothing untoward occurred.

[012-042]After their supply of words was exhausted, they gave over gambling and,

[012-043]their arms about each other's shoulders, marched off in search of a

[012-044]drink of alcohol.

[012-045]"I don't like to fight with my tongue either, it's not decent. I'm

[012-046]right, too, eh? I tell you no man living has ever breathed a word to me

[012-047]against my mother. I want to be respected, see? That's why you've never

[012-048]seen me fooling with anyone." There was a pause. Then, suddenly, "Look

[012-049]there, Tenderfoot," Anastasio said, changing his tone and standing up

[012-050]with one hand spread over his eyes. "What's that dust over there behind

[012-051]the hillock. By God, what if it's those damned Federals and we sitting

[012-052]here doing nothing. Come on, let's go and warn the rest of the boys."

[012-053]The news met with cries of joy.

[012-054]"Ah, we're going to meet them!" cried Pancracio jubilantly, first among

[012-055]them to rejoice.

[012-056]"Of course, we're going to meet them! We'll strip them clean of

[012-057]everything they brought with them."

[012-058]A few moments later, amid cries of joy and a bustle of arms, they began

[012-059]saddling their horses. But the enemy turned out to be a few burros and

[012-060]two Indians, driving them forward.

[012-061]"Stop them, anyhow. They must have come from somewhere and they've

[012-062]probably news for us," Demetrio said.

[012-063]Indeed, their news proved sensational. The Federal troops had fortified

[012-064]the hills in [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]]; this was said to be Huerta's last stronghold,

[012-065]but everybody predicted the fall of the city. Many families had hastily

[012-066]fled southward. Trains were overloaded with people; there was a

[012-067]scarcity of trucks and coaches; hundreds of people, panic-stricken,

[012-068]walked along the highroad with their belongings in a pack slung over

[012-069]their shoulders. General Panfilo Natera was assembling his men at

[012-070]Fresnillo; the Federals already felt it was all up with them.

[012-071]"The [[battle-of-zacatecas|fall of Zacatecas]] will be Huerta's requiescat in pace," Luis

[012-072]Cervantes cried with unusual excitement. "We've got to be there before

[012-073]the fight starts so that we can join Natera's army."

[012-074]Then, suddenly, he noted the surprise with which Demetrio and his men

[012-075]greeted his suggestion. Crestfallen, he realized they still considered

[012-076]him of no account.

[012-077]On the morrow, as the men set off in search of good mounts before

[012-078]taking to the road again, Demetrio called Luis Cervantes:

[012-079]"Do you really want to come with us? Of course you're cut from another

[012-080]timber, we all know that; God knows why you should like this sort of

[012-081]life. Do you imagine we're in this game because we like it? Now, I like

[012-082]the excitement all right, but that's not all. Sit down here; that's

[012-083]right. Do you want to know why I'm a rebel? Well, I'll tell you.

[012-084]"Before the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]], I had my land all plowed, see, and just right

[012-085]for sowing and if it hadn't been for a little quarrel with Don Monico,

[012-086]the boss of my town, Moyahua, I'd be there in a jiffy getting the oxen

[012-087]ready for the sowing, see?

[012-088]"Here, there, Pancracio, pull down two bottles of beer for me and this

[012-089]tenderfoot.... By the Holy Cross ... drinking won't hurt me, now, will

[012-090]it?"
