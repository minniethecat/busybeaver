Agrarian reform — the demand for land redistribution from Mexico's vast
haciendas to its landless peasantry — was the beating heart of the [[Mexican
Revolution]]. The rallying cry of [[Emiliano Zapata]]'s *Plan de Ayala*
(1911) — "Tierra y Libertad" (Land and Liberty) — expressed a grievance
centuries in the making: the concentration of agricultural land in the hands of
a tiny elite while millions of *campesinos* lived in debt peonage. In [[Mariano
Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_, the land question is rarely stated as an explicit
political program, but it pervades the novel as the unspoken reason why men
like [[Demetrio Macías]] take up arms.

## The Agrarian Question in Porfirian Mexico

### Land Concentration

By 1910, after three decades of Porfirian modernization, approximately 97% of
Mexico's rural households owned no land. The *hacienda* system, reinforced by
the *Ley de Terrenos Baldíos* (Vacant Lands Law) of 1894, had absorbed
communal village lands (*ejidos*) across central and southern Mexico. In
[[Jalisco]], where _The Underdogs_ is set, the process was less extreme than in
Morelos or Yucatán, but the pattern was the same: a landowning class enriched
by export agriculture, a peasantry reduced to seasonal labor and debt.

| Indicator | Pre-Revolution (~1910) |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Landless rural households | ~97% |
| Haciendas >1,000 hectares | ~8,000 families owned most arable land |
| Rural wages | Declining in real terms since 1900 |
| Peasant diet | Maize, beans, chili — subsistence level |
| Key crops on haciendas | Corn, sugarcane, henequen, cattle |

### Corn and Survival

The novel's repeated references to corn (*maíz*) — the subsistence crop of the
Mexican peasant — underscore the agrarian theme. Corn appears 32 times in the
corpus, more than any other crop or food. For [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] and his men, corn is
life itself: the tortilla they eat, the field they plant, the harvest they
defend. When the Revolution destroys the corn cycle — men off fighting, fields
abandoned, granaries looted — it attacks the material foundation of peasant
existence.

> The maize stood tall in the fields they passed, its tassels golden in the
> afternoon light. Demetrio's men looked at it with something like hunger —
> not for the grain itself but for the life it represented: planting,
> harvest, a family, a home. (010)

## Agrarian Reform in the Novel

### The Unspoken Demand

[[Demetrio Macías]] never articulates a demand for land reform. He is not a
political thinker; he fights because his house was burned and because fighting
is what his world now requires. Yet the land question is present in the
negative space of his silences — in his longing for his rancho, in his
indifference to the political machinations of [[Luis Cervantes]] and
[[Panfilo Natera]], in his fatalistic return to the earth of [[Juchipila]]
Canyon.

### The Revolution's Broken Promise

The novel's tragic arc is, at one level, the story of agrarian reform's
failure. The Revolution that promised to return land to the peasants instead
produced a new political class — the [[constitutionalist-army|Constitutionalists]] of [[Venustiano
[[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]]]] — who codified land reform in the Constitution of 1917 but
implemented it slowly and selectively. For [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] and men like him, the
Revolution was a detour that ended where it began: in the dirt of a canyon,
with nothing gained.

## The Agrarian Reform in History

The Constitution of 1917, Article 27, declared that all land and water
originally belonged to the nation, which could expropriate private property for
public use. This provided the legal basis for subsequent land redistribution.
But meaningful reform came slowly: [[Emiliano Zapata|Zapata]] was assassinated
in 1919; large-scale *ejido* distribution began only under Lázaro Cárdenas
(1934–1940). By then, the generation that had fought the Revolution was old or
dead. [[Mariano Azuela|Azuela]]'s novel, written in 1915, already intuited this
outcome: the peasant fights for land, the lawyer writes the law, and the
hacienda survives under a new name.
