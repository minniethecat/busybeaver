## Overview

Alcohol flows through *The Underdogs* from its opening pages to its final scenes. Mezcal and aguardiente are constants in camp life — drunk to celebrate victories, to mourn losses, to pass the idle hours between battles, and to drown the moral consequences of revolutionary violence. Azuela treats drunkenness not as a moral failing of individuals but as a collective condition: the revolution itself is a kind of intoxication, and alcohol is its material sacrament.

## Alcohol as Social Ritual

From the novel's first chapter, drinking is established as the currency of male camaraderie. Demetrio's men demand food from the woman at Limon, but what they really celebrate is the mezcal. A "quantity of mezcal was drunk" at gatherings, and the cry "I miss Camilla" is voiced "in a loud voice" — drink loosening the tongue and sentiment alike (015-002).

The ritual extends to the highest levels. Late in the novel, "Demetrio was engaged in getting drunk with his old comrades" (029-005), and the entire camp dissolves into a blur of "drunkards, the heavy breathing of the horses, the stamp of their hoofs" (029-012). Drinking together is the revolution's primary form of bonding — more reliable than ideology, more immediate than the cause.

## Alcohol and Violence

If drinking binds men together, it also loosens the restraints on violence. A soldier warns that "nor is alcohol a good counselor; quarrels naturally ensued" (018-083). The half-drunk soldiers whip their horses into a frenzy (024-058), and drunkenness becomes indistinguishable from the brutality of the campaign. When a soldier is badly wounded, alcohol serves a dual purpose — poured over the wound as disinfectant (034-058), and drunk to dull the horror of what has been done.

The novel's opening scene already establishes the link. Demetrio's first appearance is shadowed by drink — "I'm drunk, see" (001-073) — and his "alcoholic tenor trailed off into the night" (001-053). The revolution begins in a haze of mezcal, and it will end there too.

## Moral Decay and the Loss of Purpose

The accumulation of drinking scenes across the novel traces an arc of moral decay. In early chapters, drinking belongs to the swagger of confident men. By the later chapters, the laughter has turned hollow: "They laughed, they sang, they whistled, drunk with the sunlight" (015-059) — the drunkenness now desperate, a refusal to confront what the revolution has become.

The most damning exchange comes late, when a soldier deflects blame: "That alcohol went to our heads" (028-017). Another retorts: "What the hell do you mean: alcohol! It was all cooked up between" the men themselves (028-019). Alcohol is the alibi, not the cause — the revolution's violence has its own logic, and drink merely greases the wheels.

## Key Passages

> His alcoholic tenor trailed off into the night. [001-053]

*Demetrio's first appearance in the novel is marked by drink — the revolution's protagonist introduced through the haze of mezcal.*

> They laughed, they sang, they whistled, drunk with the sunlight, the air, the abundance of their lives. [015-059]

*The intoxication of survival — men drunk on more than alcohol, celebrating their own continued existence amid slaughter.*

> nor is alcohol a good counselor; quarrels naturally ensued. [018-083]

*A terse acknowledgment of the causal chain: drink → quarrel → violence — as predictable as the revolution's own trajectory.*

> "What the hell do you mean: alcohol! It was all cooked up between" [028-019]

*Alcohol as alibi — the men know that what they did was not the drink's fault, but their own.*
