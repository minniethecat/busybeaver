## Overview

War Paint (La Pintada) is one of the most vividly drawn soldaderas in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. Unlike the nameless camp women who cook and nurse, War Paint is a fighter in her own right — "of sterner stuff, tireless" (023-017) — who rides a black horse, carries weapons, and participates directly in the band's violence and looting. Her relationship with [[Demetrio Macías]]'s men, particularly [[Blondie]], is a volatile mix of desire, rivalry, and physical combat that erupts into one of the novel's most dramatic episodes.

## Character

War Paint is introduced looting a house with "convulsed with laughter" (023-001), immediately marking her as a transgressive figure who finds exhilaration in the chaos of war. She "plunged her hands into" the spoils (023-011) alongside [[Anastasio Montañés]] and [[Pancracio]], claiming an equal share. Azuela describes her as tougher than the men: "War Paint was of sterner stuff; tireless" (023-017), contrasting her with the exhausted soldiers around her.

Her physical prowess is repeatedly demonstrated. When a brawl erupts, she "dashed in after him" (025-012) and "put her leg between his, twisted him" (025-018), fighting with the skill of a trained combatant. In another confrontation, a bullet "grazed War Paint's hair" (024-082) — a near-miss she treats with contemptuous indifference.

War Paint's relationship with [[blondie|Blondie]] is the emotional core of her arc. She covers the sleeping girl with clothes (025-069) in a moment of unexpected tenderness, then later cries "God, I'm happy. I'm crazy about [[blondie|Blondie]]" (025-071). But this attachment curdles into violence: "that snake of a War Paint" (027-069) becomes a byword among the men after her jealousy turns deadly.

She is also fiercely articulate. When [[Luis Cervantes]] makes speeches, she interrupts: "Ho! This speech-making…" (024-033), cutting through rhetoric with the sharp skepticism of someone who has seen too much to be impressed by words.

## Key Passages

> "What damned fools," said War Paint convulsed with laughter! "Where did you ever see a general…" [PN=023-001]

Her first appearance establishes her defiant, mocking voice — a soldadera who laughs at the pretensions of rank.

> War Paint was of sterner stuff; tireless, she went from one room to the next… [PN=023-017]

Azuela's description contrasts War Paint's relentless energy with the exhausted men around her, marking her as exceptional among the camp followers.

> The bullet whizzed by, grazing War Paint's hair. The mirror broke into a thousand pieces. [PN=024-082]

A near-fatal moment that War Paint treats with characteristic bravado — the shattered mirror a symbol of the violence that follows her.

> "God, I'm happy," War Paint cried. "I'm crazy about Blondie." [PN=025-071]

Her declaration of love for [[blondie|Blondie]] reveals a vulnerability beneath her hardened exterior, making the eventual tragedy of their relationship all the more devastating.

## Role in the Novel

War Paint embodies the soldadera as active agent rather than passive camp follower. She fights, loots, gambles, and loves on equal terms with the men — and pays the price when that equality turns to violence. Her trajectory from laughing looter to "that snake" (027-069) mirrors the novel's larger arc from [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary optimism to bitter dissolution. She is Azuela's most fully realized female character, and her fate is a reminder that the Revolution's brutality was not exclusively male.
