[027-001]They established themselves in a large gloomy house, which likewise

[027-002]belonged to the cacique of Moyahua. The previous occupants had already

[027-003]left strong evidences in the patio, which had been converted into a

[027-004]manure pile. The walls, once whitewashed, were now faded and cracked,

[027-005]revealing the bare unbaked adobe; the floor had been torn up by the

[027-006]hoofs of animals; the orchard was littered with rotted branches and

[027-007]dead leaves. From the entrance one stumbled over broken bits of chairs

[027-008]and other furniture covered with dirt.

[027-009]By ten o'clock, Luis Cervantes yawned with boredom, said good night to

[027-010]Blondie and War Paint, who were downing endless drinks on a bench in

[027-011]the square, and made for the barracks. The drawing room was alone

[027-012]furnished. As he entered, [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], lying on the floor with his eyes

[027-013]wide open, trying to count the beams, gazed at him.

[027-014]"It's you, eh? What's new? Come on, sit down."

[027-015]Luis Cervantes first went over to trim the candle, then drew up a chair

[027-016]without a back, a coarse rag doing the duty of a wicker bottom. The

[027-017]legs of the chair squeaked. War Paint's black horse snorted and whirled

[027-018]its crupper in wide circles. Luis Cervantes sank into his seat.

[027-019]"General, I wish to make my report. Here you have ..."

[027-020]"Look here, man, I didn't really want this done, you know. Moyahua is

[027-021]almost like my native town. They'll say this is why we've been

[027-022]fighting!" Demetrio said, looking at the bulging sack of silver

[027-023]Cervantes was passing to him. Cervantes left his seat to squat down by

[027-024]Demetrio's side.

[027-025]He stretched a blanket over the floor and into it poured the ten-peso

[027-026]pieces, shining, burning gold.

[027-027]"First of all, General, only you and I know about this.... Secondly,

[027-028]you know well enough that if the sun shines, you should open the

[027-029]window. It's shining in our faces now but what about tomorrow? You

[027-030]should always look ahead. A bullet, a bolting horse, even a wretched

[027-031]cold in the head, and then there are a widow and orphans left in

[027-032]absolute want! ... The Government? Ha! Ha! ... Just go see Carranza or

[027-033]Villa or any of the big chiefs and try and tell them about your

[027-034]family.... If they answer with a kick you know where, they'll say

[027-035]they're giving you a handful of jewels. And they're right; we did not

[027-036]rise up in arms to make some Carranza or Villa President of our

[027-037]Republic. No--we fought to defend the sacred rights of the people

[027-038]against the tyranny of some vile cacique. And so, just as Villa or

[027-039]Carranza aren't going to ask our consent to the payment they're getting

[027-040]for the services they're rendering the country, we for our part don't

[027-041]have to ask anybody's permission about anything either."

[027-042]Demetrio half stood up, grasped a bottle that stood nearby, drained it,

[027-043]then spat out the liquor, swelling out his cheeks.

[027-044]"By God, my boy, you've certainly got the gift of gab!"

[027-045]Luis felt dizzy, faint. The spattered beer seemed to intensify the

[027-046]stench of the refuse on which they sat; a carpet of orange and banana

[027-047]peels, fleshlike slices of watermelon, moldy masses of mangoes and

[027-048]sugarcane, all mixed up with cornhusks from tamales and human offal.

[027-049]Demetrio's calloused hands shuffled through the brilliant coins,

[027-050]counting and counting. Recovering from his nausea, Luis Cervantes

[027-051]pulled out a small box of Fallieres phosphate and poured forth rings,

[027-052]brooches, pendants, and countless valuable jewels.

[027-053]"Look here, General, if this mess doesn't blow over (and it doesn't

[027-054]look as though it would), if the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] keeps on, there's enough

[027-055]here already for us to live on abroad quite comfortably."

[027-056]Demetrio shook his bead.

[027-057]"You wouldn't do that!"

[027-058]"Why not? What are we staying on for? ... What cause are we defending

[027-059]now?"

[027-060]"That's something I can't explain, Tenderfoot. But I'm thinking it

[027-061]wouldn't show much guts."

[027-062]"Take your choice, General," said Luis Cervantes, pointing to the

[027-063]jewels which he had set in a row.

[027-064]"Oh, you keep it all.... Certainly! ... You know, I don't really care

[027-065]for money at all. I'll tell you the truth! I'm the happiest man in the

[027-066]world, so long as there's always something to drink and a nice little

[027-067]wench that catches my eye...."

[027-068]"Ha! Ha! You make the funniest jokes, General. Why do you stand for

[027-069]that snake of a War Paint, then?"

[027-070]"I'll tell you, Tenderfoot, I'm fed up with her. But I'm like that: I

[027-071]just can't tell her so. I'm not brave enough to tell her to go plumb to

[027-072]hell. That's the way I am, see? When I like a woman, I get plain silly;

[027-073]and if she doesn't start something, I've not got the courage to do

[027-074]anything myself." He sighed. "There's Camilla at the ranch for

[027-075]instance.... Now, she's not much on looks, I know, but there's a woman

[027-076]I'd like to have......."

[027-077]"Well, General, we'll go and get her any day you like."

[027-078]Demetrio winked maliciously.

[027-079]"I promise you I'll do it."

[027-080]"Are you sure? Do you really mean it? Look here, if you pull that off

[027-081]for me, I'll give you the watch and chain you're hankering after."

[027-082]Luis Cervantes' eyes shone. He took the phosphate box, heavy with its

[027-083]contents, and stood up smiling.

[027-084]"I'll see you tomorrow," he said. "Good night, General! Sleep well."
