## Overview

Religion in *The Underdogs* appears not as faith but as form — robes, churches, confessions, and priests — that the revolution strips of sanctity and repurposes for its own ends. Azuela, who trained as a physician and wrote with clinical detachment, treats the Church not as a spiritual institution but as a material presence in the revolutionary landscape: stone towers that become sniper positions, robes that become costumes in a loyalty test, and priests who become corpses. Religion is everywhere in the novel, but almost never as an object of belief — only as an object of use.

## The Fake Confession: Loyalty Test as Sacrament

The novel's most sustained religious set piece is the fake confession scene in Chapter VII. Demetrio, suspecting that Luis Cervantes may be a spy sent to murder him, devises a test: "You go and find out where's the nearest church around here ... go and steal a priest's robe and bring it back" (007-033–034). Quail is to put on the robes, pretend to be a priest, and hear Cervantes's confession — if Cervantes has "anything up his sleeve, he'll come out with it, and then I'll shoot him" (007-039–041).

The scene works on multiple levels. It is a parody of the sacrament of confession — the priest is an impostor, the penitent is a suspected assassin, and absolution will be a bullet or a reprieve. But it is also a genuine test of character: Cervantes, terrified and sleepless, speaks "awkwardly, slowly" (007-046) and passes. The religious form is hollowed out — the robes are stolen, the "priest" is Quail — and yet the test produces a real result. Demetrio releases him.

When Quail appears "in priestly robes" (007-052) and the men laugh "uproariously," the scene completes its trajectory from sacred parody to pure farce. Religion has become costume, and confession has become comedy.

## The Hanged Priest

If the fake confession treats religion as farce, the hanged priest treats it as atrocity. Late in the novel, amid a rout of Federales, the narrative notes: "A poor devil of a priest left dangling from a" (030-010) — the sentence breaking off as if the image itself resists completion. The priest's body, stripped of "riches and rings stolen from the priest's" (030-016), is religion reduced to a target and a source of loot.

These two scenes — the comic fake confession and the hanged priest — bracket the novel's treatment of the Church: first as instrument, then as victim. The revolution that uses religion for its purposes also destroys it.

## The Church as Architecture

Beyond these set pieces, the Church appears repeatedly as physical structure — stone towers, cupolas, steeples, chapel walls — that the novel's military action incorporates into its geography. A "rectangular church cupola rose above the small" houses (016-043). Men fight from "the church tower" and along "the chapel wall" (017-028, 017-022). The church is battlefield architecture before it is sacred space — its height makes it tactically useful, and its stone makes it bulletproof. Azuela's descriptive eye registers what a soldier would register: not the cross but the line of fire.

## "Coreligionist": The Word as Badge

Early in the novel, Luis Cervantes attempts to win Demetrio's trust by declaring himself a "coreligionist" (005-080). Demetrio does not understand the word: "What's that? What did you say? Car ... what?" (005-082). Cervantes explains: "Coreligionist, sir, that is to say, a person ... who is inspired by the same religion" (005-083–084). The word choice is revealing — Cervantes reaches for religious vocabulary to describe political allegiance. For him, the revolution *is* a faith; for Demetrio, it is a fight. The gap between these understandings is the gap between the educated revolutionary and the man with a gun.

Later, Cervantes's experience of being betrayed by his own side is described in religious terms: "The first moment he was able to join his coreligionists, instead of welcoming him with open arms, they threw him into a pigsty with swine for company" (006-085–086). The religious language of fellowship meets the reality of betrayal — coreligionists who act like enemies.
