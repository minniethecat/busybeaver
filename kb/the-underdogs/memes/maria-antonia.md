## Overview

Maria Antonia, instantly recognizable by her single eye, is one of the most vividly drawn camp followers in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. A pockmarked woman with a loud voice and a taste for drink, she appears across Parts I and II as a fixture of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band. Her roles are multiple: she serves as a folk healer brewing potions for the sick, a sharp-tongued commentator on camp life, and a source of bawdy comic relief. Alongside [[War Paint]], she represents the indispensable yet marginalized presence of women in the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary camps.

## Character

Maria Antonia is introduced in Chapter VIII as a spy — "one-eyed Maria Antonia, who had been spying from her hut" (008-050) — establishing her as a watchful, knowing presence in the camp. Her physical description is unsparing: she is one-eyed and pockmarked, and the narrative does not romanticize her. Yet she is also a figure of resilience and earthy vitality.

In Chapter IX, her role as a folk healer emerges when the women talk of making "a potion for Maria Antonia who's not so well" (009-023), only for another to note that "Maria Antonia's got the curse today" (009-033), hinting at the practical, bodily realities of camp life that the novel does not shy from. Her drinking is legendary: by Chapter XV she is "utterly drunk" (015-012), calling out to the men as they depart. On the morrow, hungover but unbowed, she appears "though she was pockmarked, her face was swelling and her eyes were bloodshot" (015-013).

Maria Antonia's most memorable trait is her voice. She yells at the departing soldiers — "There they go! Why, they're running away!" (015-029) — and "burst into laughter" (015-038) at the chaos she has provoked. Her shout is so powerful that soldiers who had come out "could no longer contain themselves" (015-036), revealing her ability to shape the camp's emotional atmosphere. In Chapter XIX, [[Demetrio Macías]] himself calls for her by name: "[[pancracio|Pancracio]], bring one-eyed Maria Antonia. We're all going to have a good time tonight" (019-065), confirming her status as an essential part of the band's social life. She is last noted "early that evening" (028-004), a presence that persists into the band's later days.

## Role in the Novel

Maria Antonia embodies the unglamorous resilience of the camp follower — the women who cooked, healed, drank, fought, and died alongside the soldiers but whose stories are rarely told. Her physical imperfections (one eye, pockmarked face) and her raucous behavior mark her as a figure of earthy realism, a counterpoint to the more romanticized [[Camilla]]. She is both comic relief — her drunken shouting and laughter — and a reminder of the bodily realities of war: sickness, menstruation, hangovers, loneliness. That [[Demetrio Macías]] calls for her by name to "have a good time" suggests she is valued not despite her roughness but because of it; she is authentic in a world of shifting allegiances.
