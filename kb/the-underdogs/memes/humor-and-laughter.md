## Overview

In a novel saturated with violence — shootings, hangings, ambushes, slaughter — what is most striking is how much laughter there is. The verb "laugh" and its derivatives appear fifty times across *The Underdogs*. Soldiers exchange rifles "laughing and making wagers" (003-069); Maria Antonia "burst into laughter" at the sight of Demetrio's affection for Camilla (015-038); War Paint is "convulsed with laughter" at the violence around her (023-001); Blondie shouts "shaking with laughter" at the chaos of camp life (023-094); the crowd at Zacatecas "broke into peals of laughter" as Pancracio and Manteca compete for a woman (019-067). In a world of constant death, laughter is the body's refusal to be crushed.

This is gallows humor — the humor of men and women who have seen too much to cry. Azuela's peasant-soldiers laugh not because things are funny but because the alternative is silence or despair. The laughter of *The Underdogs* is not comic relief; it is existential strategy.

## Laughter as Social Bond

Laughter in the novel is rarely solitary. It happens in groups — among soldiers exchanging rifles (003-069), among men drinking mezcal (015-059), among women ransacking a house (023-042–058). Laughter is the sound of collective survival, the noise that says *we are still here, we are still together*.

The social function is clearest in the early chapters, where men who barely know each other use laughter to establish trust. When Demetrio's followers laugh at Luis Cervantes's pretensions — "They all laughed uproariously, when a few minutes later Luis Cervantes appeared" (007-075) — the laughter is a test. Cervantes passes it by laughing with them, not against them. Laughter marks the boundary between insider and outsider.

As the revolution wears on, the laughter grows darker but no less social. "The crowd broke into peals of laughter" at the Zacatecas restaurant (019-067); the men of Natera's army "laughed and joked as they despoiled the houses" (017-080). The content of the laughter has changed — looting, sexual competition, violence — but its function remains: laughter binds the group.

## War Paint's Laughter: The Woman Who Laughs at Violence

War Paint is the novel's most insistent laugher, and her laughter is the novel's most disturbing. She is "convulsed with laughter" (023-001) even as she participates in violence; she is "weeping with laughter" (033-027) as she tends to a prisoner. The compound "weeping with laughter" captures the fusion — grief and humor are not opposites but twins, born from the same unbearable reality.

Her laughter is gendered. In a world where women are expected to mourn, War Paint laughs. Her laughter is a refusal of the victim role, a claim to agency through ridicule. When she laughs at the men around her — and she is almost always laughing at men — she is asserting a power that the revolution's violence does not grant her but that laughter does.

## Demetrio's Smile and the Silence of Power

Demetrio Macías rarely laughs aloud, but he smiles — "Demetrio smiled" (005-086), "his eyes shining" (013-077), "Demetrio smiles" (026-068), "a sinister, malign smile" shared among the general's staff (026-035). The smile rather than the laugh is the expression of power. Laughter is democratic — anyone can laugh. The smile is controlled, withholding, hierarchical. Demetrio smiles because he does not need to laugh; his authority is secure enough to be expressed in the smallest possible gesture.

Luis Cervantes, by contrast, smiles with "scorn" (021-088) and "disdain" (001-089, 016-011). The educated man's smile is a weapon of class, a way of signaling that he sees more than the peasants around him. Where Demetrio's smile is an act of presence — he is here, he is in command — Cervantes's smile is an act of distance.

The novel's most revealing contrast comes in a single moment: "So scornfully Luis Cervantes smiled that Solis sat down on a rock" (021-088–089). Solis, the intellectual who understands the revolution's futility, is silenced by a smile. Laughter and its variants are communication before they are emotion.

## "Drunk with Sunlight": The Laughter of Survival

The novel's most lyrical description of laughter comes not at a moment of violence but at a moment of peace: "They laughed, they sang, they whistled, drunk with the sunlight, the air, the abundance of the fields" (015-059). The soldiers are not drunk on alcohol — though alcohol is present — but on *being alive*. The sunlight intoxicates; the air itself is a drug. This is laughter as pure biological affirmation, the body's "yes" to its own continued existence.

The passage is the novel's closest approach to joy, and it is telling that Azuela frames it as intoxication — "drunk with sunlight." Even at its most celebratory, laughter in *The Underdogs* is a loss of control, a surrender to forces larger than the self. The same verb — "drunk" — appears throughout the novel as a literal description of soldiers consuming mezcal. Laughter and alcohol are the twin mechanisms by which the revolution's participants escape the weight of what they have seen and done.

## Key Passages

> "All the fellows were exchanging rifles, laughing and making wagers on the shots." [003-069]

*The novel's first laughter — soldiers treating weapons as toys, gambling on marksmanship. The laughter of men accustomed to violence.*

> They all laughed uproariously, when a few minutes later Luis Cervantes appeared. [007-075]

*Laughter as social test — the group laughs at the newcomer, who must prove he can laugh with them.*

> They laughed, they sang, they whistled, drunk with the sunlight, the air, the abundance of the fields. [015-059]

*The novel's most joyful moment — laughter as intoxication, the body's affirmation of being alive.*

> The crowd broke into peals of laughter. Pancracio and Manteca vied with each other in boasting of their valor. [019-067]

*Laughter as competition — men performing for each other, violence transformed into entertainment.*

> "Lord, but I am happy!" said War Paint convulsed with laughter! [023-001]

*War Paint's defining laughter — joy and violence fused in a single exclamation.*

> Blondie shouted, shaking with laughter. [023-094]

*Blondie's laughter as physical force — the body possessed by mirth even amid chaos.*

> War Paint, weeping with laughter, pressed on with utmost skill. [033-027]

*The compound "weeping with laughter" — grief and humor as twins, born from the same unbearable reality.*
