## Overview

In the chaotic world of *The Underdogs*, where institutions have collapsed and ideology is barely articulate, personal loyalty is the only currency that holds value. Demetrio Macías extends his hand and says: "Here's my hand on it: friend to friend" (001-091). The gesture is primitive, physical, and absolute — more binding than any oath to nation or cause. Throughout the novel, friendship operates as the primary social structure: men join Demetrio's band because they are his friends, fight beside him because they are his friends, and die for him because they are his friends.

This is not modern comradeship — the abstract solidarity of citizens in arms. It is pre-modern, personal, face-to-face. The revolution provides the occasion; friendship provides the motive. When Luis Cervantes asks Demetrio why he fights, Demetrio's answer is not ideological — it is personal. He fights because federal soldiers wronged him, and his friends fight because he is their friend.

## The Pledge: "Friend to Friend"

The novel's foundational gesture of friendship comes in its first pages. Demetrio, a hunted man, meets a stranger and offers his hand: "happy to call them friends. Here's my hand on it: friend to friend" (001-091). This is not a casual greeting — it is a contract, sealed in flesh. In Demetrio's world, the handshake replaces the law.

The pledge establishes the pattern by which Demetrio's band grows. Men do not enlist; they join. The band is an extended friendship network, and its hierarchy is the hierarchy of personal closeness. Demetrio's closest friends — Anastasio Montañés, Venancio, Pancracio, Quail — are also his lieutenants. Military rank is the formalization of personal bond.

The same handshake recurs throughout the novel. "Demetrio, we're friends, aren't we? Well then, listen" (019-051) — the appeal to friendship is the prelude to every significant request. Friendship is not sentiment; it is the language in which power is negotiated.

## The Vulnerability of Friendship

But friendship in *The Underdogs* is also a site of vulnerability. Demetrio's enemies know that his friends are his weakness: "Somebody came and warned me: 'Look out, Demetrio, they're going to do something to your friends'" (013-036–039). The warning reveals the structure — to hurt Demetrio, you hurt his friends. The personal bond that makes his band strong also makes it brittle.

Luis Cervantes, the educated outsider, understands this vulnerability and exploits it. His friendship with Demetrio is instrumental — he cultivates it to advance his own position — and the novel is clear-eyed about what this means. "Cervantes writhed at his friend's words" (018-055) — the word "friend" here is already ironic, already corroded by self-interest. The revolution corrupts friendship as surely as it produces it.

## Brotherhood Beyond Blood

The novel's most intense expressions of friendship use the language of family: "you've been more than a friend, you've taken care of me as if you were my own" (014-059), "I love him like a brother" (022-053). In a world where biological families have been scattered by war, the revolutionary band becomes a family of choice. The language of brotherhood is not metaphorical — it is structural. Men who would otherwise be strangers bind themselves into kinship through shared risk.

This brotherhood has a dark side. The same men who "love each other like brothers" also participate in collective violence, looting, and rape. The bonds of friendship do not moralize the band; they simply hold it together. "A pair of old friends, met again. So warm was their embrace" (022-049) — the warmth is real, but it coexists with everything else the band does.

## The Drunken Comrade: Friendship Tested

In the novel's later chapters, comradeship is tested by alcohol and exhaustion. Demetrio is "engaged in getting drunk with his old comrades" (029-005), and the scene is ambiguous — is this friendship or its parody? The men drink together because they always have, but the drinking now feels compulsive, desperate, a ritual maintained after its meaning has drained away. "Pancracio assured his comrades he was hungrier than when he started" (035-081) — the joke is thin, the camaraderie strained. The revolution is ending, and the friendships it forged are ending with it.
