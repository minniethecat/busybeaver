## Overview

Class pervades *The Underdogs* as both grievance and horizon. The revolution is fought in the name of the poor, yet the novel tracks with clinical precision how the men who fight it begin to adopt the very material symbols — gold watches, embroidered clothes, heavy earrings — of the class they claim to oppose. Azuela, who practiced medicine among the urban poor in Mexico City (P01-027), writes about inequality not as a propagandist but as a diagnostician: he shows the disease and refuses to pretend the cure is working.

## The Poor Before the Revolution

The novel's poor are introduced through their vulnerability. A peasant voices the pre-revolutionary condition with blunt fatalism: "what in God's name can a man do when he's poor" (001-095). The question is rhetorical — the answer, before the revolution, is nothing. When Federales descend, "death on us, on all the poor" (004-049) follows, and the litany of theft — "they come and steal our pigs, our chickens and corn" — makes clear that the poor are prey for both armies.

The poor appear throughout the novel as a trembling presence: the "poor devil" who guides soldiers through the countryside, "trembling with fear" (016-035), the "poor day laborer" who pleads "I've got a wife and a lot of kids" (016-037). These are not revolutionary subjects — they are victims who happen to stand in the path of armies.

## Revolutionary Ideology and Its Contradictions

Luis Cervantes provides the novel's most articulate statement of revolutionary purpose: "The revolution benefits the poor, the ignorant, all those who have been slaves" (007-057). But in the same speech, his analysis sharpens into something more radical: the poor "are poor because the rich who stand above them, the rich who rule them, change their sweat and blood and tears into gold" (007-059, 007-060).

This is class analysis in its purest form — yet Cervantes delivers it as a seduction, trying to convince Demetrio to join the revolutionary cause. The irony is structural: the most lucid statement of revolutionary ideals comes from the novel's most cynical character.

The doubt is immediate. A listener asks whether the revolutionaries truly fight for ideals or "to glut their thirst for gold and blood" (008-027). The question hangs unanswered over the rest of the novel.

## The Material Seduction of Wealth

As the revolution advances, its fighters acquire the trappings of the class they overthrew. Demetrio "pulled out his jeweled gold watch" (022-121); his men wear "large heavy gold watch chains" and "rings on their fingers" (022-091). A woman displays "heavy gold earrings" (026-021). The revolution has not abolished wealth — it has redistributed its symbols.

The hunger for money becomes explicit and unapologetic. "Damn these rich people, they lock up everything they've got!" (023-008) — the complaint of a revolutionary who has become a looter, demanding what he believes the rich owe him. A prisoner is tortured for hidden money: "I know where they've buried their money but I won't tell" (026-102). The revolution that began with "to rob the rich" (020-023) as an ideal ends with robbery as a practice, the distinction between expropriation and theft dissolved.

## The Persistence of Hierarchy

By the novel's closing chapters, the class structure has reconstituted itself within the revolutionary camp. Officers "shower money" on entertainers (035-027), and a soldier speaks of "sending money back home to the old woman" (035-063) — the same old woman of the impoverished countryside. The revolution has not closed the gap between rich and poor; it has created new rich and left the same poor in place. Azuela's final judgment is not spoken but shown: the class pyramid endures, only the names at the top have changed.

## Key Passages

> "The revolution benefits the poor, the ignorant, all those who have been slaves." [007-057]

*Luis Cervantes' recruitment pitch — revolutionary ideology in its most seductive form, spoken by its most cynical mouthpiece.*

> they are poor because the rich who stand above them, the rich who rule them, change their sweat and blood and tears into gold. [007-059, 007-060]

*The novel's most condensed analysis of class exploitation — labor converted to wealth by those who do not labor.*

> "I'm just a poor day laborer, sir; I've got a wife and a lot of kids." [016-037]

*The voice of the civilian poor — not a revolutionary subject but a man begging to be left alone.*

> Damn these rich people, they lock up everything they've got! [023-008]

*The revolutionary as looter — the ideology of redistribution collapsing into the practice of plunder.*
