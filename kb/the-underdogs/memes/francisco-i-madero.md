## Overview

Francisco I. Madero was the wealthy landowner and idealistic reformer whose 1910 uprising overthrew the dictatorship of [[Porfirio Díaz]] and launched the [[Mexican Revolution]]. His brief presidency (1911–1913) ended with his assassination, an event that radicalized the Revolution and drew figures like [[Mariano Azuela]] into armed struggle. In _The Underdogs_, Madero exists as a memory — the martyred idealist whose death set loose forces he could not have foreseen (013-032).

## Biography

### The Uprising Against Díaz

Madero came from one of Mexico's wealthiest families but became convinced that Porfirio Díaz's thirty-year dictatorship must end. His 1910 call to arms — the Plan of San Luis Potosí — ignited a nationwide rebellion. [[Mariano Azuela]], like many young Liberals, "supported Francisco I. Madero's uprising" and was rewarded with the post of Director of Education of [[jalisco|Jalisco]] (P01-009, P02-008).

### Presidency and Assassination

Madero became President in 1911 but faced immediate challenges from both conservatives and radical [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries. A soldier in _The Underdogs_ recalls: "Madero was President" (015-065) — the past tense capturing his brief tenure. In February 1913, a counter[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary coup led by [[Victoriano Huerta]] resulted in Madero's arrest and murder. The novel references "the killing of Madero and some other fellow" with a bluntness that reflects how commonplace political violence had become (013-032).

### Madero's Words in The Novel

The most revealing passage about Madero in the novel comes from [[Luis Cervantes]], who recalls Madero's reported words: "Madero said: 'You men have made me President at the cost of ten thousand lives. What does it matter to you if then I demand some small sacrifice?'" (013-051, 013-055). This bitter quote — whether authentic or apocryphal — captures the novel's theme of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary betrayal: the leader who uses popular sacrifice for political ends.

### Legacy

After Madero's death, "the treacherous revolt of Feliz Diaz and [[Victoriano Huerta|Huerta]] against President Madero" became a rallying cry for the Constitutionalist forces (016-096). [[Mariano Azuela]] joined [[Pancho Villa]]'s army as a doctor following the assassination, acquiring firsthand knowledge of the war that would become _The Underdogs_ (P01-012, P02-011).

## Thematic Significance

Madero is the Revolution's lost innocent — the idealist whose death transformed a political movement into a vortex of violence. In Azuela's portrayal, Madero's reported words to the people reflect a patronizing attitude that prefigures the betrayals to come. The soldiers' casual reference to "the killing of Madero" (013-032) reveals how thoroughly the Revolution has normalized political murder. Madero's ghost haunts _The Underdogs_: the cause for which men fought and died had already been corrupted at its source.
