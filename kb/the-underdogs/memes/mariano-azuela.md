## Overview

Mariano Azuela was a Mexican physician and novelist, recognized as the first of the "novelists of the Revolution." His most celebrated work, *The Underdogs* (*Los de abajo*), written in 1915 during his exile in El Paso, Texas, became the defining novel of the [[mexican-revolution|Mexican Revolution]]. Azuela served as a field doctor in [[Pancho Villa]]'s army, giving him firsthand experience of the conflict that informed his unflinching literary portrayal of the Revolution's ideals and betrayals (P01-002, P02-001).

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Azuela was born in [[Lagos de Moreno]], [[Jalisco]], Mexico, in 1873. He studied medicine in Guadalajara and returned to Lagos in 1909 to begin his medical practice. His literary career began early: in 1896 he published *Impressions of a Student* in a Mexico City weekly, followed by numerous sketches and short stories. In 1911 he published his first novel, *Andres Perez, maderista* (P01-007, P02-006).

### Role in the Revolution

Like many young Liberals of his generation, Azuela supported [[Francisco I. Madero]]'s uprising that overthrew the dictatorship of [[Porfirio Díaz]]. In 1911 he was appointed Director of Education of the [[jalisco|State of Jalisco]]. After [[francisco-i-madero|Madero]]'s assassination, he joined the army of [[Pancho Villa]] as a doctor, acquiring his knowledge of the Revolution at firsthand (P01-015, P02-014).

When the counter[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary forces of [[Victoriano Huerta]] gained the upper hand, Azuela emigrated to El Paso, Texas. There, in 1915, he wrote *The Underdogs*, drawing directly on his experiences in Villa's army. The novel did not receive general recognition until 1924, when it was hailed as the novel of the Revolution (P01-020, P02-019).

### Later Years and Disillusionment

Azuela was fundamentally a moralist, and his disappointment with the Revolution's aftermath became a defining feature of his later work. He had fought for a better Mexico, but observed that while the Revolution had corrected certain injustices, it had given rise to others equally deplorable. The self-serving and unprincipled figures who turned the hopes of the underprivileged into a ladder for their own advancement left him deeply embittered (P01-025, P02-022).

During his later years, until his death in 1952, Azuela lived in Mexico City, continuing to write and practice medicine among the poor. His later novels are marked at times by a savage sarcasm born of disillusionment (P01-032, P02-029).

## Key Passages

> Mariano Azuela, the first of the "novelists of the Revolution," was born in Lagos de Moreno, Jalisco, Mexico, in 1873. He studied medicine in Guadalajara and returned to Lagos in 1909, where he began the practice of his profession. (P02-001)

> Like most of the young Liberals, he supported Francisco I. Madero's uprising, which overthrew the dictatorship of Porfirio Diaz, and in 1911 was made Director of Education of the State of Jalisco. After Madero's assassination, he joined the army of Pancho Villa as doctor, and his knowledge of the Revolution was acquired at firsthand. (P02-014)

> But Azuela was fundamentally a moralist, and his disappointment with the Revolution soon began to manifest itself. He had fought for a better Mexico; but he saw that while the Revolution had corrected certain injustices, it had given rise to others equally deplorable. (P02-022)

> When he saw the self-servers and the unprincipled turning his hopes for the redemption of the under-privileged of his country into a ladder to serve their own ends, his disillusionment was deep and often bitter. His later novels are marred at times by a savage sarcasm. (P01-030, P02-027)
