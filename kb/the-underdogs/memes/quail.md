## Overview

Quail (*La Codorniz* in the original Spanish) is one of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s most vividly drawn foot soldiers in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. Alongside [[Pancracio]] and [[Manteca]], Quail forms the novel's comic chorus—a hard-drinking, loud-mouthed peasant fighter whose antics provide the novel's earthy humor and whose loyalty to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is absolute, if entirely unexamined.

## Biography

Quail appears in the novel's earliest scenes, already among [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s original band before the [[Burning of Demetrio's House]]. In Chapter II, [[Anastasio Montañés]] calls out to him: "Montanez! Look here, Quail, you don't believe it, do you…" (002-063), establishing Quail as a longtime member of the group. During the band's flight from the [[Federal Army]], Quail is one of the unarmed men begging for a rifle: "[[manteca|Manteca]] and Quail, unarmed, begged for a gun" (003-041).

In one of the novel's most memorable comic scenes, Quail strips naked in the middle of a firefight: "Suddenly, Quail stood up, naked, holding his…" (003-046), a moment of absurd humor amid the violence that is characteristic of Azuela's unsentimental style.

Quail survives through the novel's campaigns and appears in the climactic scenes at the [[Juchipila]] Canyon, entering the final gathering alongside [[Anastasio Montañés]], [[Pancracio]], and [[Meco]] (022-045). He remains with [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] to the end.

## Character

Quail is the novel's principle comic-relief character, defined by his drunkenness, irreverence, and simple-mindedness. When [[Luis Cervantes]] delivers a political speech, Quail's response is: "Certainly talks his head off" (007-077). When [[Fortunata]] tells her tragic story, it "bored Quail and he…" (009-087). He is incapable of abstraction or empathy beyond his immediate circle.

Yet Quail is not merely comic. When [[Venancio]] reminds him why he joined the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]], Quail bristles: "I remember why you joined us, Quail," [[venancio|Venancio]] replied angrily (010-011), hinting at unspoken motivations. Like his comrades, Quail fights out of loyalty to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], not ideology.

## Key Passages

> "It's Pancracio," Quail cried joyfully. [PN=005-027]

Quail's joyful recognition of [[pancracio|Pancracio]] captures the tight bonds among [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s men—a brotherhood forged in shared danger.

> That night Quail returned with the priest's ro… [PN=007-044]

In a scheme to mock a prisoner before execution, Quail steals a priest's robe, demonstrating the band's casual blasphemy and their dark sense of humor.

> "Here, Quail, you son of a bitch, you stay…" [PN=003-052]

[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s rough address to Quail is typical of the novel's dialogue—insults that are marks of intimacy, not anger.

> Quail suggested that musicians be s… [PN=014-020]

Even amid military planning, Quail thinks of entertainment, underscoring the band's lack of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary discipline.
