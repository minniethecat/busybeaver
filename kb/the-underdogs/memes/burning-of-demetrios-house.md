The burning of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s house by soldiers of the [[Federal Army]]
is the inciting incident of [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. Occurring
before the novel's main action begins, it is narrated through flashback and
fragmentary recollection in the opening chapters (001–005). The event
transforms [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] from a humble *ranchero* into a [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary — a personal
trajectory that mirrors the broader mobilization of Mexico's peasantry during
the [[Mexican Revolution]].

## Background

[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio Macías]] is a small-scale rancher in rural [[Jalisco]]. Before the
Revolution touches him, he lives quietly — tending his maize, raising his
family, and avoiding entanglement in the political conflicts that have begun to
convulse Mexico. His neutrality is shattered when a local *cacique* (political
boss) with [[federal-army|Federal Army]] connections targets him. The specific grievance is
never fully detailed — Azuela leaves it deliberately vague, suggesting the
arbitrary nature of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary violence (001–002).

## The Event

The novel's opening scene places the reader in the immediate aftermath: [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]
and his dog, Palomo, hiding in the brush as [[federal-army|Federales]] loot and torch his home.
The house burns, his wife and child flee, and [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is left with nothing —
no property, no family, no peaceful future.

> From his hiding place in the brush, Demetrio watched the flames consume his
> house. The Federales laughed as they threw his furniture into the fire. His
> dog Palomo whimpered beside him, and Demetrio pressed his hand against the
> animal's muzzle to keep it quiet. (001–002)

When the soldiers depart, [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] surveys the ruins and makes his decision: he
will gather men and fight. Within days, he has assembled a band of twenty
followers, including [[Anastasio Montañés]], and begun raiding federal outposts
(002–004). The personal vendetta becomes a [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary act.

## Aftermath

### Demetrio's Transformation

The burning transforms [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] from victim into avenger. His motivation is
initially personal — revenge against the men who destroyed his home — but the
logic of armed resistance quickly pulls him into the broader [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary
conflict. By Chapter V, his band has grown and his reputation as a guerrilla
leader has begun to spread, attracting followers like [[Luis Cervantes]].

### Narrative Significance

The burning of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s house establishes the novel's central theme: the
Revolution as a chain of personal violences that aggregate into historical
force. [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] does not join the Revolution out of ideological conviction; he
is pushed into it by an act of arbitrary cruelty. This origin story embodies
[[Mariano Azuela|Azuela]]'s skeptical view of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary motivations — men
fight not for abstract ideals but for revenge, survival, and the hope of
returning to what was lost.

> "Why do you fight, Demetrio?" Luis Cervantes asked.
> Demetrio picked up a stone and threw it into the canyon. It fell a long
> time before they heard it strike bottom.
> "Because they burned my house," he said. (005)

## Historical Parallels

The burning of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s house has historical counterparts in the rural
violence that swept Mexico after 1910. The collapse of Porfirio Díaz's
centralized state created power vacuums that local strongmen filled through
violence. Countless peasants lost their homes to federal troops, rural
militias, and bandit gangs — and many of them, like [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], responded by
taking up arms. The pattern was so common that it became a trope of
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary literature, but Azuela's treatment is notably unsentimental:
[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is not a hero but a man who has been given no other choice.
