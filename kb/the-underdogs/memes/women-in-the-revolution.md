## Overview

Women in *The Underdogs* appear in three overlapping roles: as camp followers and caretakers who sustain the revolutionary bands, as objects of male desire and violence, and as voices of endurance and resistance in a world where men make the decisions and women bear the consequences. Azuela does not center women — he rarely even names them — but their presence is constant, and their brief appearances accumulate into a portrait of the revolution's gendered underside.

## Camp Followers and Caretakers

The most persistent female presence in the novel is the soldadera — the woman who follows the troops, cooking, nursing, and carrying children. When Demetrio's band first enters the sierra, an unnamed woman tends the fire and serves food under the sergeant's orders (001-029). Later, an old woman follows the stretcher to inspect a wounded man, and a girl brings water — "a jicara of bluish water" (004-064, 004-066). These women are not combatants, but the band cannot function without them.

The cost of this life is stark. An "old wrinkled barefooted woman, wearing a cloth rag for a blouse" (004-076) embodies the material deprivation that camp women endure. Azuela registers these figures without sentimentality — they are facts of the revolutionary landscape, as constant and as overlooked as the dust.

## Desire, Violence, and Victimhood

Women in the novel are persistently framed as objects of male attention, from casual harassment to outright violence. When Demetrio's men occupy a ranch, the sergeant calls to the woman of the house for food with a casual entitlement that shades into threat (001-036). A girl is told to "pour yourself a drink" for the soldiers' entertainment (001-078). The dynamic is consistent: women provide, men take.

The violence against women is most explicit when Demetrio's house is burned and his wife recounts how the Federales "carry our women off" (001-111). The woman of the house at Limon endures the occupation in silence, her senses "directed outside the hut" (001-007), waiting for the men to leave. When they finally go, she "drew close to Demetrio" (001-099) — an ambiguous gesture that might be gratitude, fear, or the survival instinct of someone who has learned to placate armed men.

The novel's most chilling female utterance comes from this same woman, who, when the soldiers discuss shooting a dog, croaks: "Shoot them!" (001-085). It is the voice of someone for whom violence has become the only language men understand.

## Motherhood and Endurance

Motherhood forms a separate register of female experience. Demetrio's soldier recalls his mother — "an old woman nailed to her chair for ten years" (006-052) — with a tenderness that stands in stark contrast to the casual cruelty of camp life. The figure of "a woman pushing forward painfully, bearing a child in her arms" (001-111) appears as a brief, iconic image of civilian suffering amid the chaos.

For some women, endurance hardens into defiance. Maria Antonia, drunk and challenging the men around her (015-012), and War Paint, who adopts male violence as her own, suggest that women's agency in the revolution, though constrained, was not absent. Camilla, Luis Cervantes' bride, represents yet another register — the woman who is courted, idealized, and ultimately left behind.

## Key Passages

> "Hey, there, woman: we want food! Give us eggs, milk, beans, anything you've got! We'll pay for it." [001-029]

*The transactional language of revolutionary requisition — "we'll pay" — barely masks the power imbalance between armed men and the women who serve them.*

> "Shoot them!" the woman croaked. [001-085]

*A woman's voice, hardened by occupation — the only command she gives in the entire chapter, and it is a call for death.*

> of a woman pushing forward painfully, bearing a child in her arms. [001-111]

*Azuela's brief, almost cinematic image of the civilian cost — a mother fleeing with her child as armies move through the land.*

> "God, what a bloody mess! Another unhappy woman!" [009-055]

*The exclamation collapses pity and exasperation — women's suffering is recognized as routine, even tiresome.*
