[040-001]They entered the streets of Juchipila as the church bells rang, loud

[040-002]and joyfully, with that peculiar tone that thrills every mountaineer.

[040-003]"It makes me think we are back in the days when the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] was just

[040-004]beginning, when the bells rang like mad in every town we entered and

[040-005]everybody came out with music, flags, cheers, and fireworks to welcome

[040-006]us," said Anastasio Montanez.

[040-007]"They don't like us no more," [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] returned.

[040-008]"Of course. We're crawling back like a dog with its tail between its

[040-009]legs," Quail remarked.

[040-010]"It ain't that, I guess. They don't give a whoop for the other side

[040-011]either."

[040-012]"But why should they like us?"

[040-013]They spoke no more.

[040-014]Presently they reached the city square and stopped in front of an

[040-015]octagonal, rough, massive church, reminiscent of the colonial period.

[040-016]At one time the square must have been a garden, judging from the bare

[040-017]stunted orange trees planted between iron and wooden benches. The

[040-018]sonorous, joyful bells rang again. From within the church, the honeyed

[040-019]voices of a female chorus rose melancholy and grave. To the strains of

[040-020]a guitar, the young girls of the town sang the "Mysteries."

[040-021]"What's the fiesta, lady?" Venancio asked of an old woman who was

[040-022]running toward the church.

[040-023]"The Sacred Heart of Jesus!" answered the pious woman, panting.

[040-024]They remembered that one year ago they had captured [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]]. They

[040-025]grew sadder still.

[040-026]Juchipila, like the other towns they had passed through on their way

[040-027]from Tepic, by way of Jalisco, Aguascalientes and Zacatecas, was in

[040-028]ruins. The black trail of the incendiaries showed in the roofless

[040-029]houses, in the burnt arcades. Almost all the houses were closed, yet,

[040-030]here and there, those still open offered, in ironic contrast, portals

[040-031]gaunt and bare as the white skeletons of horses scattered over the

[040-032]roads. The terrible pangs of hunger seemed to speak from every face;

[040-033]hunger on every dusty cheek, in their dusty countenances; in the hectic

[040-034]flame of their eyes, which, when they met a soldier, blazed with

[040-035]hatred. In vain the soldiers scoured the streets in search of food,

[040-036]biting their lips in anger. A single lunchroom was open; at once they

[040-037]filled it. No beans, no tortillas, only chili and tomato sauce. In vain

[040-038]the officers showed their pocketbooks stuffed with bills or used

[040-039]threats:

[040-040]"Yea, you've got papers all right! That's all you've brought! Try and

[040-041]eat them, will you?" said the owner, an insolent old shrew with an

[040-042]enormous scar on her cheek, who told them she had already lain with a

[040-043]dead man, "to cure her from ever feeling frightened again."

[040-044]Despite the melancholy and desolation of the town, while the women sang

[040-045]in the church, birds sang in the foliage, and the thrushes piped their

[040-046]lyrical strain on the withered branches of the orange trees.
