[021-001]The firing lessened, then slowly died out. Luis Cervantes, who had been

[021-002]hiding amid a heap of ruins at the fortification on the crest of the

[021-003]hill, made bold to show his face. How he had managed to hang on, he did

[021-004]not know. Nor did he know when [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] and his men had disappeared.

[021-005]Suddenly he had found himself alone; then, hurled back by an avalanche

[021-006]of infantry, he fell from his saddle; a host of men trampled over him

[021-007]until he rose from the ground and a man on horseback hoisted him up

[021-008]behind him. After a few moments, horse and riders fell. Left without

[021-009]rifle, revolver, or arms of any kind, Cervantes found himself lost in

[021-010]the midst of white smoke and whistling bullets. A hole amid a debris of

[021-011]crumbling stone offered a refuge of safety.

[021-012]"Hello, partner!"

[021-013]"Luis, how are you!"

[021-014]"The horse threw me. They fell upon me. Then they took my gun away. You

[021-015]see, they thought I was dead. There was nothing I could do!" Luis

[021-016]Cervantes explained apologetically. Then:

[021-017]"Nobody threw me down," Solis said. "I'm here because I like to play

[021-018]safe."

[021-019]The irony in Solis' voice brought a blush to Cervantes' cheek.

[021-020]"By God, that chief of yours is a man!" Solis said. "What daring, what

[021-021]assurance! He left me gasping--and a hell of a lot of other men with

[021-022]more experience than me, too!"

[021-023]Luis Cervantes vouchsafed no answer.

[021-024]"What! Weren't you there? Oh, I see! You found a nice place for

[021-025]yourself at the right time. Come here, Luis, I'll explain; let's go

[021-026]behind that rock. From this meadow to the foot of the hill, there's no

[021-027]road save this path below. To the right, the incline is too sharp; you

[021-028]can't do anything there. And it's worse to the left; the ascent is so

[021-029]dangerous that a second's hesitation means a fall down those rocks and

[021-030]a broken neck at the end of it. All right! A number of men from Moya's

[021-031]brigade who went down to the meadow decided to attack the enemy's

[021-032]trenches the first chance they got. The bullets whizzed about us, the

[021-033]battle raged on all sides. For a time they stopped firing, so we

[021-034]thought they were being attacked from behind. We stormed their

[021-035]trenches--look, partner, look at that meadow! It's thick with corpses!

[021-036]Their machine guns did that for us. They mowed us down like wheat; only

[021-037]a handful escaped. Those Goddamned officers went white as a sheet; even

[021-038]though we had reinforcements they were afraid to order a new charge.

[021-039]That was when Demetrio Macias plunged in. Did he wait for orders? Not

[021-040]he! He just shouted:

[021-041]"'Come on, boys! Let's go for them!'

[021-042]"'Damn fool!' I thought. 'What the hell does he think he's doing!'

[021-043]"The officers, surprised, said nothing. Demetrio's horse seemed to wear

[021-044]eagle's claws instead of hoofs, it soared so swiftly over the rocks.

[021-045]'Come on! Come on!' his men shouted, following him like wild deer,

[021-046]horses and men welded into a mad stampede. Only one young fellow

[021-047]stepped wild and fell headlong into the pit. In a few seconds the

[021-048]others appeared at the top of the hill, storming the trenches and

[021-049]killing the Federals by the thousand. With his rope, Demetrio lassoed

[021-050]the machine guns and carried them off, like a bull herd throwing a

[021-051]steer. Yet his success could not last much longer, for the Federals

[021-052]were far stronger in numbers and could easily have destroyed Demetrio

[021-053]and his men. But we took advantage of their confusion, we rushed upon

[021-054]them and they soon cleared out of their position. That chief of yours

[021-055]is a wonderful soldier!"

[021-056]Standing on the crest of the hill, they could easily sight one side of

[021-057]the Bufa peak. Its highest crag spread out like the feathered head of a

[021-058]proud Aztec king. The three-hundred-foot slope was literally covered

[021-059]with dead, their hair matted, their clothes clotted with grime and

[021-060]blood. A host of ragged women, vultures of prey, ranged over the tepid

[021-061]bodies of the dead, stripping one man bare, despoiling another, robbing

[021-062]from a third his dearest possessions.

[021-063]Amid clouds of white rifle smoke and the dense black vapors of flaming

[021-064]buildings, houses with wide doors and windows bolted shone in the

[021-065]sunlight. The streets seemed to be piled upon one another, or wound

[021-066]picturesquely about fantastic corners, or set to scale the hills

[021-067]nearby. Above the graceful cluster of houses, rose the lithe columns of

[021-068]a warehouse and the towers and cupola of the church.

[021-069]"How beautiful the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]! Even in its most barbarous aspect it is

[021-070]beautiful," Solis said with deep feeling. Then a vague melancholy

[021-071]seized him, and speaking low:

[021-072]"A pity what remains to do won't be as beautiful! We must wait a while,

[021-073]until there are no men left to fight on either side, until no sound of

[021-074]shot rings through the air save from the mob as carrion-like it falls

[021-075]upon the booty; we must wait until the psychology of our race,

[021-076]condensed into two words, shines clear and luminous as a drop of water:

[021-077]Robbery! Murder! What a colossal failure we would make of it, friend,

[021-078]if we, who offer our enthusiasm and lives to crush a wretched tyrant,

[021-079]became the builders of a monstrous edifice holding one hundred or two

[021-080]hundred thousand monsters of exactly the same sort. People without

[021-081]ideals! A tyrant folk! Vain bloodshed!"

[021-082]Large groups of Federals pushed up the hill, fleeing from the "high

[021-083]hats." A bullet whistled past them, singing as it sped. After his

[021-084]speech, Alberto Solis stood lost in thought, his arms crossed.

[021-085]Suddenly, he took fright.

[021-086]"I'll be damned if I like these plaguey mosquitoes!" he said. "Let's

[021-087]get away from here!"

[021-088]So scornfully Luis Cervantes smiled that Solis sat down on a rock quite

[021-089]calm, bewildered. He smiled. His gaze roved as he watched the spirals

[021-090]of smoke from the rifles, the dust of roofs crumbling from houses as

[021-091]they fell before the artillery. He believed he discerned the symbol of

[021-092]the revolution in these clouds of dust and smoke that climbed upward

[021-093]together, met at the crest of the hill and, a moment after, were

[021-094]lost....

[021-095]"By heaven, now I see what it all means!"

[021-096]He sketched a vast gesture, pointing to the station. Locomotives

[021-097]belched huge clouds of black dense smoke rising in columns; the trains

[021-098]were overloaded with fugitives who had barely managed to escape from

[021-099]the captured town.

[021-100]Suddenly he felt a sharp blow in the stomach. As though his legs were

[021-101]putty, he rolled off the rock. His ears buzzed... Then darkness ...

[021-102]silence ... eternity....
