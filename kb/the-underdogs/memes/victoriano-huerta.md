## Overview

Victoriano Huerta was the [[federal-army|Federal Army]] general who overthrew President [[Francisco I. Madero]] in a counter[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary coup in 1913 and briefly ruled Mexico as a military dictator. In _The Underdogs_, Huerta is the great antagonist — the embodiment of the old order that the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries fight to destroy. His name is spat as a curse, his soldiers are the enemy, and his defeat is the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries' consuming goal (020-007).

## Biography

### Rise and the Coup Against Madero

Huerta was a career military officer who served under the [[Porfirio Díaz]] regime and initially fought against the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary forces. In February 1913, he turned against [[Francisco I. Madero]], joining forces with Félix Díaz in a "treacherous revolt" that resulted in [[francisco-i-madero|Madero]]'s arrest and assassination (016-096). Huerta then declared himself President, establishing a military dictatorship.

### The Revolutionary Enemy

In _The Underdogs_, Huerta is the common enemy who unites the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary factions. The fall of [[Zacatecas]] — described as "Huerta's last stronghold" — would be "Huerta's requiescat in pace" (012-064, 012-071). His name serves as a curse: "Huerta the serpent!" cries one of [[Panfilo Natera]]'s officers (020-007). The [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries toast "Viva Presidente Huerta! Viva Mexico!" with savage irony (016-131).

### Huerta's Currency

Like [[Pancho Villa]], Huerta issued his own currency — but his "Huerta bills" become a symbol of the collapsing old order. A wounded man's wallet contains "two Huerta bills" (022-112), and another wallet holds "a few Huerta bills" (026-078). The contrast between Villa's silver coins and Huerta's paper money reflects the economic dimension of the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary struggle.

### Defeat and Legacy

By Part III of the novel, Huerta has been defeated. A soldier recalls: "Didn't we finish off this man Huerta and his Federals?" (036-037). But victory proves hollow — the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary coalition immediately fractures into [[Pancho Villa|Villa]] versus [[Venustiano Carranza|Carranza]] factions. Huerta's defeat does not bring the peace the soldiers fought for, reinforcing the novel's theme of cyclical violence.

## Thematic Significance

Huerta functions in _The Underdogs_ as the symbolic antagonist — the "serpent" whose defeat defines [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary victory. Yet his defeat does not resolve anything; the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries who "finished off" Huerta immediately begin fighting each other. The "Huerta bills" that circulate after his fall suggest the persistence of the old order's structures. Azuela implies that the Revolution's true enemy was never one man but a system of power that any strongman could occupy — whether named Huerta, Villa, or [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]].
