## Overview

Valderrama is a wandering tramp and self-styled poet who appears in Part III of [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. Unlike the peasant soldiers who fight out of loyalty or instinct, Valderrama is an outsider — called "the tramp, the crazy maker of verses" (039-063) — who drifts into [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band with a guitar and a sardonic detachment from the violence around him. His presence introduces an ironic, almost absurdist counterpoint to the novel's closing tragedy.

## Character

Valderrama is defined by incongruities. He is a tramp who speaks with philosophical gravity, a drunkard who composes prayers, a man "with his eternally complacent and enigmatic smile" (038-034) amid the chaos of war. His physical appearance is striking: a "close-cropped neck" (037-031) and a disdainful, theatrical "gesture that was disdainful and ironic" (037-044).

He is both inside and outside the band. [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] calls him by name — "Valderrama, come here" (038-031) — and the men tolerate his guitar at card games: "Valderrama finished tuning his guitar" (038-036). Yet he remains fundamentally detached, "deaf to all about him" (039-020) when absorbed in his own thoughts. His relationship to the Revolution is unsparing: he is "convinced that the prison was better than a hundred battles" (037-037), reducing the cause to a calculus of personal suffering.

His drinking is central to his character. When a bottle is passed around, "greedily the tramp seized it" (038-061). During the march past [[Juchipila]], he counts landmarks in the first stage of drunkenness (039-009), then dismounts in a fit of emotion when the town is lost from sight (039-017). His drunken prayer — "interrupting his prayer, Valderrama frowned, burst into stentorian laughter" (039-026) — blurs the line between blasphemy and genuine longing.

Valderrama also displays moments of unexpected warmth: he "fell upon him, embraced him with effusion" (038-058) in a rare moment of human connection, and the narrator notes he enlisted "of his own free will" (039-063), setting him apart from conscripts.

## Key Passages

> "What? What did you say?" Valderrama cried in surprise. "The men of Juchipila…" [PN=036-062]

Valderrama's first words establish him as a skeptic — the one man in the band willing to challenge received wisdom about [[Juchipila]].

> Valderrama, the tramp, the crazy maker of verses, who had enlisted of his own free will… [PN=039-063]

Azuela's summary description captures the paradox of Valderrama: a poet who volunteers for a war he does not believe in.

> Interrupting his prayer, Valderrama frowned, burst into stentorian laughter. [PN=039-026]

His mock-prayer is one of the novel's most surreal moments — a performance that could be sincere devotion, blasphemous parody, or both at once.

## Role in the Novel

If [[Luis Cervantes]] represents the Revolution's educated hypocrisy and [[Demetrio Macías]] its weary heart, Valderrama represents its absurdist fringe — the man who fights without believing, who sings without an audience, who prays without a god. He is Azuela's reminder that the Revolution gathered not only ideologues and peasants but also drifters, outcasts, and broken artists for whom the war was the only stage available.
