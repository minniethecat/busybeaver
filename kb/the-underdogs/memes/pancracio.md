## Overview

Pancracio is one of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s most loyal and colorful soldiers in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s novel _The Underdogs_. He serves as the band's bugler—blowing the horn to signal assembly and attack—and provides much of the novel's coarse, comic relief. Pancracio embodies the unreflective, instinct-driven peasant fighter whose loyalty to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is absolute even when he cannot articulate any political cause.

## Biography

Pancracio first appears in Chapter III as the man who blows the horn that summons [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s scattered band after the [[Burning of Demetrio's House]]. "…halloo of the horn, blown by Pancracio" (003-002) is the first sound of the band's regrouping. His physical description is memorably harsh: "Pancracio's harsh, gorillalike profile" with a "bearded face" (004-006) and later described as "pockmarked, blotchy, uns…" (006-092), marking him as a man hardened by rural poverty and violence.

Throughout the novel, Pancracio operates in close tandem with [[Manteca]] and [[Quail]], forming a trio of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s most trusted foot soldiers. He is present at nearly every major episode: the band's formation (Ch III–V), the card games and quarrels (Ch XII–XIII), and the advance toward [[Zacatecas]] (Ch XV–XVI).

## Character

Pancracio is defined by his loud voice, crude humor, and unquestioning loyalty. He roars with laughter, shouts impetuously, and treats violence with casual indifference. When a prisoner is to be executed, Pancracio "urged impatiently" to "shoot him" (005-090), displaying the brutalized sensibility of a man for whom killing has become routine. Yet his loyalty to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is never in question: he is "first among" the men to cry out in eagerness when battle approaches (012-054).

When [[Luis Cervantes]] begins lecturing the men, Pancracio's puzzled response captures the gulf between the educated intellectual and the instinctive peasant fighter. He cannot follow ideological arguments, only direct commands and immediate threats.

## Role in the Novel

Pancracio functions as one of the novel's *collective* protagonists—the nameless peasant masses whom Azuela portrays not as ideologues but as instinct-driven men. Pancracio never articulates why he fights; he fights because [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] fights, because his comrades fight, because fighting is what his world has always demanded. His comic moments—the card games, the drunken shouting, the crude jokes—are the novel's counterpoint to [[Luis Cervantes]]'s intellectual posturing and [[Demetrio Macías]]'s growing disillusionment. If Cervantes represents the Revolution's brain and [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] its weary heart, Pancracio represents its unthinking, irrepressible body.
