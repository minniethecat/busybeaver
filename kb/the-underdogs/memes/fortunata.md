## Overview

Fortunata is a minor but poignant figure among the camp followers of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. She appears solely in Chapter IX, where her brief moment of emotional vulnerability — giving "vent to her sorrows" (009-081) — offers a rare window into the interior lives of the women who followed the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary armies. Her story, though fleeting, exemplifies the novel's method of sketching complete human tragedies in a few lines.

## Character

Fortunata is first greeted casually: "Good morning to you, Fortunata. How are you?" (009-032). The everyday salutation places her among the familiar faces of the camp, a woman whose presence is known and unremarkable. The scene is set in the context of the women preparing a potion for the ailing [[Maria Antonia]] (009-023), and the mention of "the curse" (009-033) establishes the shared female space in which Fortunata's story unfolds.

Her moment comes later in the chapter, when "Fortunata gave vent to her sorrows" (009-081). The narrative does not specify the exact nature of her grief — lost family, lost home, the accumulated trauma of war — but her outpouring commands attention: the other women fall silent, "all ears to the story" (009-086). The scene ends with a striking image: "By the time Fortunata finished, the sun had already dried the clothes spread out in the sun" (009-088). The passage of time marked by the drying clothes mirrors the quiet absorption of her audience and the way sorrow, once spoken, becomes part of the camp's collective experience.

## Key Passages

> "Good morning to you, Fortunata. How are you?" (009-032)

The everyday greeting places Fortunata among the camp's familiar faces — a woman whose presence is known and accepted.

> Then Fortunata gave vent to her sorrows. She could keep silent no longer; all the bitter misery of her life came rushing to her lips. (009-081)

Fortunata's emotional outpouring is the defining moment of her brief appearance — the weight of accumulated grief finding release in shared female company.

> By the time Fortunata finished, the sun had already dried the clothes spread out on the rocks. (009-088)

The image of drying clothes marks the passage of time and suggests the slow, patient listening of the other women — sorrow absorbed into the rhythms of camp life.

## Role in the Novel

Fortunata's single-scene appearance is characteristic of Azuela's method in _The Underdogs_: minor characters flash into view with concentrated emotional force, then recede, leaving an impression of the countless invisible lives shaped and shattered by the [[Mexican Revolution]]. Her venting of sorrows — the specific content of which the narrative withholds — stands for the untold stories of every camp woman who followed the armies not for ideology but for survival. That the other women listen in silence, and that the sun moves across the sky as she speaks, elevates her confession from personal grievance to communal ritual. Fortunata is, in this sense, a representative figure: her sorrow is the sorrow of the underdogs themselves.
