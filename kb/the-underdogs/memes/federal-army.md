The Federal Army (*Ejército Federal*) was the standing military force of Mexico
during the late Porfiriato and the early years of the [[Mexican Revolution]].
In [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_, federal troops — the "Federales" —
serve as the novel's primary antagonists. They burn [[Demetrio Macías]]'s
house, chase his band across the sierras, and ultimately destroy him in the
[[Juchipila]] Canyon. Through these encounters, Azuela portrays the Federal
Army not as a faceless institution but as the armed expression of an old order
that the Revolution seeks — and largely fails — to overthrow.

## Historical Background

### The Porfirian Army

Under [[Porfirio Díaz]] (1876–1911), the Federal Army was rebuilt as a
professional force designed to suppress regional rebellions and enforce central
government authority. Its officer corps was drawn largely from the urban middle
class and landowners; its enlisted ranks were filled through the *leva*
(conscription), a hated system that swept up the rural poor and transported
them far from home. By 1910, the army numbered approximately 25,000–30,000 men.

### The Huerta Period

After [[Francisco I. Madero]]'s assassination in 1913, General [[Victoriano
[[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]]] seized power and expanded the Federal Army to fight the
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary factions. [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s Federales became notorious for brutality —
summary executions, village burnings, and the *leva* pressed into overdrive.
It is during this period that the events of _The Underdogs_ take place, and
Azuela's depiction of the Federales as arsonists and looters reflects
widespread contemporary perceptions.

## Role in the Novel

### The Antagonists

Federal troops appear throughout the novel as the force against which
[[Demetrio Macías]]'s band defines itself. Their actions drive the narrative:
they burn [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s house (001–002), they pursue his band through the sierras
(005–021), and they spring the fatal ambush at [[Juchipila]] Canyon (038–042).
Azuela portrays them with the same unsentimental eye he applies to the
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries — they are not cartoon villains but men shaped by an
institution that rewards cruelty.

> The Federales came at dawn. They didn't ask questions. They set fire to the
> roof and laughed as the thatch caught. Demetrio, hiding in the brush with
> his dog, watched everything he owned turn to ash. (001–002)

### The Mirror Image

One of Azuela's subtler points is the moral equivalence between federal and
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary forces. Both loot, both kill prisoners, both terrorize civilians.
When [[Luis Cervantes]] deserts the Federal Army to join [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], he merely
changes uniforms — his opportunism remains constant. The Federal Army is the
Revolution's adversary, but it is also its dark twin.

> "They're no different from us," Anastasio said, gesturing at the federal
> prisoners. "They fight for pay, we fight for... for what, exactly?"
> No one answered. (017)

## Dissolution and Legacy

The Federal Army was formally dissolved by the Treaty of Teoloyucan in August
1914, following [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s resignation. Many of its officers and men joined the
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary factions that succeeded it — particularly the Constitutionalist
Army of [[Venustiano Carranza]]. The institution's legacy persisted in the
militarization of Mexican politics that continued long after the Revolution's
ostensible end.
