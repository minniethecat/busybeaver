Anastasio Montañés is [[Demetrio Macías]]'s most devoted lieutenant and one
of the earliest members of his [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary band. Unlike the calculating
[[Luis Cervantes]], Anastasio acts on instinct — fierce in battle, fiercely loyal
to his chief, and untroubled by ideology. He represents the [[Mexican
Revolution]]'s grassroots soldier: illiterate, brutal, and bound by personal
loyalty rather than political conviction. His presence anchors the novel's
portrait of the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]'s human raw material.

## Character Profile

### Physical Presence

Anastasio is described as a man of formidable physical presence — dark,
hairy-faced, and imposing (012-009). His body bears the marks of a life of
labor and violence. He moves with the confidence of a man who has survived by
strength and instinct rather than calculation.

### Fierce Loyalty

Anastasio's defining trait is his unwavering devotion to [[Demetrio Macías]].
From the novel's opening, he stands at [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s side during the confrontation
with the [[federal-army|Federales]] who burned [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s house (002–004). When [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is
wounded, Anastasio carries him to safety. His loyalty is personal, not
ideological — he fights for [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]], not for any abstract [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary cause.

> Anastasio went up to Demetrio, his rifle slung across his back. He was a
> dark man with a huge black mustache and hairy cheeks. His eyes burned with
> a fierce, unwavering loyalty. (012-009, 012-013)

### Instinct Over Intellect

Anastasio embodies the instinctive warrior, in deliberate contrast to the
intellectual [[Luis Cervantes]]. While Cervantes calculates political advantage,
Anastasio acts on gut feeling. When Cervantes first joins the band, Anastasio
regards him with suspicion — the instinctive mistrust of the unlettered man for
the smooth-talking educated one (012-013). Their tense coexistence throughout
the novel embodies one of [[Mariano Azuela]]'s central themes: the uneasy
alliance between the Revolution's intellectual leadership and its peasant base.

### Violence Without Ideology

Anastasio fights with a natural ferocity untempered by moral reflection. He
kills [[federal-army|Federales]] without hesitation, loots without guilt, and follows [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]
without question. In this he represents both the Revolution's raw power and its
moral danger — the same qualities that make him an effective soldier also make
him capable of atrocities. [[Mariano Azuela|Azuela]] does not judge Anastasio
but presents him as a fact of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary life: the peasant turned soldier
for whom violence has become second nature.

## Thematic Significance

Anastasio represents the *ranchero* type — the rural Mexican peasant whose
participation in the [[Mexican Revolution|Revolution]] was driven not by
ideology but by personal grievance and loyalty. He is the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]'s muscle:
without men like him, no [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] could be fought; with men like him, no
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]] could remain pure. His illiteracy and instinctive violence make him
vulnerable to manipulation by more calculating figures like [[Luis Cervantes]],
yet his loyalty gives him a moral clarity that Cervantes lacks.

In the novel's moral economy, Anastasio occupies the middle ground between
[[Demetrio Macías]]'s noble simplicity and the cynical self-interest of the
educated class. He is neither hero nor villain — he is what the Revolution made
of the Mexican peasant.
