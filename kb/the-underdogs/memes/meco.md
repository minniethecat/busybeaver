## Overview

Meco is one of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s core soldiers in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_, appearing from the earliest chapters through the novel's final battle. He is distinguished physically by his dark skin — "completely black save for his teeth and eyes" (003-031) — and temperamentally by a grave, sardonic manner that sets him apart from the more boisterous members of the band. Alongside [[Pancracio]], [[Manteca]], and [[Quail]], he forms the backbone of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s fighting force.

## Character

Meco's defining characteristic is his gravity. When the men "laughed uproariously," Meco alone remained "grave and aloof, singing in a low voice" (015-068). Unlike [[Pancracio]]'s crude humor or [[Quail]]'s eager enthusiasm, Meco maintains a detached, almost philosophical distance from the chaos around him. He is one of the men who "filed in" to pay respects (022-045), a presence more often noted in collective scenes than in solo moments.

His wit is dry and pointed. When the band discusses politics, it is Meco who asks the blunt question: "That guy who killed [[francisco-i-madero|Madero]]?" (029-042), reducing complex [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary intrigue to its brutal essence. When conversation turns to women, Meco deflects with a joke: "Don't worry about women, Uncle" (032-066), revealing a soldier's pragmatic focus on survival over sentiment.

Meco participates in the full arc of the band's journey. He is present at the initial regrouping in Chapter III, where he calls out to [[Pancracio]] — "Look, [[pancracio|Pancracio]]" (003-031) — and lends his rifle (003-072). He fights at [[Zacatecas]], prances forward on his horse (015-061), and survives through the band's degeneration into banditry. In the novel's final chapter, he meets a violent end: "Meco hurtles over the precipice, bullets striking him" (042-037), falling in the same senseless slaughter that claims the rest of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s men.

## Key Passages

> "Look, Pancracio," said Meco, completely black save for his teeth and eyes. [PN=003-031]

Meco's introduction establishes his striking physical appearance — a visual shorthand for his indigenous heritage and the racial dimension of the peasant Revolution.

> Only Meco, grave and aloof, sang in a low voice. [PN=015-068]

While the others laugh and shout, Meco maintains his characteristic gravity — a man who keeps his own counsel even in moments of collective celebration.

> "That guy who killed Madero?" asked Meco. [PN=029-042]

Meco's blunt question cuts through political posturing, reducing the assassination of [[Francisco I. Madero]] to its starkest terms.

> Meco hurtles over the precipice, bullets striking him. [PN=042-037]

Meco's death in the final chapter is emblematic of the novel's conclusion: the loyal soldier dies not in heroic battle but in a meaningless rout, his body lost to the canyon.

## Role in the Novel

Meco represents the stoic, uncomplaining peasant fighter — the man who asks few questions, does what is asked of him, and dies without fanfare. His trajectory from the band's earliest days to its final destruction mirrors the arc of the Revolution itself: from purpose to drift, from solidarity to slaughter. His death at the precipice (042-037) is one of the novel's bleakest images, a man erased not by a named enemy but by the indiscriminate machinery of war.
