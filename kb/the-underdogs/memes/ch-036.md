[036-001]El Paso, Texas, May 16, 1915

[036-002]My Dear Venancio:

[036-003]Due to the pressure of professional duties I have been unable to answer

[036-004]your letter of January 4 before now. As you already know, I was

[036-005]graduated last December. I was sorry to hear of Pancracio's and

[036-006]Manteca's fate, though I am not surprised that they stabbed each other

[036-007]over the gambling table. It is a pity; they were both brave men. I am

[036-008]deeply grieved not to be able to tell Blondie how sincerely and

[036-009]heartily I congratulate him for the only noble and beautiful thing he

[036-010]ever did in his whole life: to have shot himself!

[036-011]Dear Venancio, although you may have enough money to purchase a degree,

[036-012]I am afraid you won't find it very easy to become a doctor in this

[036-013]country. You know I like you very much, Venancio; and I think you

[036-014]deserve a better fate. But I have an idea which may prove profitable to

[036-015]both of us and which may improve your social position, as you desire.

[036-016]We could do a fine business here if we were to go in as partners and

[036-017]set up a typical Mexican restaurant in this town. I have no reserve

[036-018]funds at the moment since I've spent all I had in getting my college

[036-019]degree, but I have something much more valuable than money; my perfect

[036-020]knowledge of this town and its needs. You can appear as the owner; we

[036-021]will make a monthly division of profits. Besides, concerning a question

[036-022]that interests us both very much, namely, your social improvement, it

[036-023]occurs to me that you play the guitar quite well. In view of the

[036-024]recommendations I could give you and in view of your training as well,

[036-025]you might easily be admitted as a member of some fraternal order; there

[036-026]are several here which would bring you no inconsiderable social

[036-027]prestige.

[036-028]Don't hesitate, Venancio, come at once and bring your funds. I promise

[036-029]you we'll get rich in no time. My best wishes to the General, to

[036-030]Anastasio, and the rest of the boys.

[036-031]Your affectionate friend,  

[036-032]Luis Cervantes

[036-033]Venancio finished reading the letter for the hundredth time and,

[036-034]sighing, repeated:

[036-035]"Tenderfoot certainly knows how to pull the strings all right!"

[036-036]"What I can't get into my head," observed Anastasio Montanez, "is why

[036-037]we keep on fighting. Didn't we finish off this man Huerta and his

[036-038]Federation?"

[036-039]Neither the General nor Venancio answered; but the same thought kept

[036-040]beating down on their dull brains like a hammer on an anvil.

[036-041]They ascended the steep hill, their heads bowed, pensive, their horses

[036-042]walking at a slow gait. Stubbornly restless, Anastasio made the same

[036-043]observation to other groups; the soldiers laughed at his candor. If a

[036-044]man has a rifle in his hands and a beltful of cartridges, surely he

[036-045]should use them. That means fighting. Against whom? For whom? That is

[036-046]scarcely a matter of importance.

[036-047]The endless wavering column of dust moved up the trail, a swirling ant

[036-048]heap of broad straw sombreros, dirty khaki, faded blankets, and black

[036-049]horses....

[036-050]Not a man but was dying of thirst; no pool or stream or well anywhere

[036-051]along the road. A wave of dust rose from the white, wild sides of a

[036-052]small canyon, swayed mistily on the hoary crest of huizache trees and

[036-053]the greenish stumps of cactus. Like a jest, the flowers in the cactus

[036-054]opened out, fresh, solid, aflame, some thorny, others diaphanous.

[036-055]At noon they reached a hut, clinging to the precipitous sierra, then

[036-056]three more huts strewn over the margin of a river of burnt sand.

[036-057]Everything was silent, desolate. As soon as they saw men on horseback,

[036-058]the people in the huts scurried into the hills to hide. [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] grew

[036-059]indignant.

[036-060]"Bring me anyone you find hiding or running away," he commanded in a

[036-061]loud voice.

[036-062]"What? What did you say?" Valderrama cried in surprise. "The men of the

[036-063]sierra? Those brave men who've not yet done what those chickens down in

[036-064]Aguascalientes and [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]] have done all the time? Our own brothers,

[036-065]who weather storms, who cling to the rocks like moss itself? I protest,

[036-066]sir; I protest!"

[036-067]He spurred his miserable horse forward and caught up with the General.

[036-068]"The mountaineers," he said solemnly and emphatically, "are flesh of

[036-069]our flesh, bone of our bone. Os ex osibus meis et caro de carne mea.

[036-070]Mountaineers are made from the same timber we're made of! Of the same

[036-071]sound timber from which heroes ..."

[036-072]With a confidence as sudden as it was courageous, he hit the General

[036-073]across the chest. The General smiled benevolently.

[036-074]Valderrama, the tramp, the crazy maker of verses, did he ever know what

[036-075]he said?

[036-076]When the soldiers reached a small ranch, despairingly, they searched

[036-077]the empty huts and small houses without finding a single stale

[036-078]tortilla, a solitary rotten pepper, or one pinch of salt with which to

[036-079]flavor the horrible taste of dry meat. The owners of the huts, their

[036-080]peaceful brethren, were impassive with the stonelike impassivity of

[036-081]Aztec idols; others, more human, with a slow smile on their colorless

[036-082]lips and beardless faces, watched these fierce men who less than a

[036-083]month ago had made the miserable huts of others tremble with fear, now

[036-084]in their turn fleeing their own huts where the ovens were cold and the

[036-085]water tanks dry, fleeing with their tails between their legs, cringing,

[036-086]like curs kicked out of their own houses.

[036-087]But the General did not countermand his order. Some soldiers brought

[036-088]back four fugitives, captive and bound.
