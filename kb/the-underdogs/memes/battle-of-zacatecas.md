# Battle of Zacatecas

## Overview

The **Battle of Zacatecas** (June 23, 1914), also known as the *Toma de Zacatecas*, was the bloodiest battle of the [[mexican-revolution|Mexican Revolution]] and the decisive engagement that ended [[victoriano-huerta|Victoriano Huerta]]'s dictatorship. [[pancho-villa|Pancho Villa]]'s [[villistas|División del Norte]], with artillery commanded by General Felipe Ángeles, defeated a Federal garrison of approximately 12,000 troops defending the city.

## Background & Context

By mid-1914, the Constitutionalist forces under [[venustiano-carranza|Venustiano Carranza]] had made significant gains against [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s [[federal-army|Federal Army]]. [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]], a mining city of strategic importance, became the key objective. [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]] believed holding [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]] would prevent the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries from advancing toward Mexico City. [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]], wary of Villa's growing power, initially ordered him not to attack the city — an order Villa disregarded.

## Description

### In the Novel

The battle is described indirectly in *The Underdogs* through the strategic discussions of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band. [[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]], the educated medical student, explains the battle's significance to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s men. He frames the engagement in almost apocalyptic terms, describing "the hills in [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]]" `(012-064)` where the fighting took place and confidently predicting that "the fall of Zacatecas will be [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s requiescat in pace" `(012-071)`.

### Military Outcome

Villa's artillery, positioned on the surrounding heights, bombarded the city for hours. The Federal defenders, many of whom were conscripts, broke under the assault. Approximately 6,000 Federal soldiers were killed, wounded, or captured. The victory opened the road to Mexico City and forced [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]] into exile within weeks.

## Aftermath & Impact

The battle's immediate consequence was [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s resignation on July 15, 1914. But its longer-term impact fractured the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary coalition. [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]], angered by Villa's insubordination in attacking [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]] against orders, refused to acknowledge Villa's role. This rift would eventually split the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries and lead to renewed civil war among the victors.
