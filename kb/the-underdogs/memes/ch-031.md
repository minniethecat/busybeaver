[031-001]The men camped in a meadow, near three small lone houses standing in a

[031-002]row, their white walls cutting the purple fringe of the horizon.

[031-003][[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] and Camilla rode toward them. Inside the corral a man, clad in

[031-004]shirt and trousers of cheap white cloth, sat greedily puffing at a

[031-005]cornhusk cigarette. Another man sitting beside him on a flat cut stone

[031-006]was shelling corn. Kicking the air with one dry, withered leg, the

[031-007]extremity of which was like a goat's hoof, he frightened the chickens

[031-008]away.

[031-009]"Hurry up, 'Pifanio," said the man who was smoking, "the sun has gone

[031-010]down already and you haven't taken the animals to water."

[031-011]A horse neighed outside the corral; both men glanced up in amazement.

[031-012]Demetrio and Camilla were looking over the corral wall at them.

[031-013]"I just want a place to sleep for my woman and me," Demetrio said

[031-014]reassuringly.

[031-015]As he explained that he was the chief of a small army which was to camp

[031-016]nearby that night, the man smoking, who owned the place, bid them enter

[031-017]with great deference. He ran to fetch a broom and a pail of water to

[031-018]dust and wash the best corner of the hut as decent lodging for his

[031-019]distinguished guests.

[031-020]"Here, 'Pifanio, go out there and unsaddle the horses."

[031-021]The man who was shelling corn stood up with an effort. He was clad in a

[031-022]tattered shirt and vest. His torn trousers, split at the seam, looked

[031-023]like the wings of a cold, stricken bird; two strings of cloth dangled

[031-024]from his waist. As he walked, he described grotesque circles.

[031-025]"Surely you're not fit to do any work!" Demetrio said, refusing to

[031-026]allow him to touch the saddles.

[031-027]"Poor man," the owner cried from within the hut, "he's lost all his

[031-028]strength.... But he surely works for his pay.... He starts working the

[031-029]minute God Almighty himself gets up, and it's after sundown now but

[031-030]he's working still!"

[031-031]Demetrio went out with Camilla for a stroll about the encampment. The

[031-032]meadow, golden, furrowed, stripped even of the smallest bushes,

[031-033]extended limitless in its immense desolation. The three tall ash trees

[031-034]which stood in front of the small house, with dark green crests, round

[031-035]and waving, with rich foliage and branches drooping to the very ground,

[031-036]seemed a veritable miracle.

[031-037]"I don't know why but I feel there's a lot of sadness around here,"

[031-038]said Demetrio.

[031-039]"Yes," Camilla answered, "I feel that way too."

[031-040]On the bank of a small stream, 'Pifanio was strenuously tugging at a

[031-041]rope with a large can tied to the end of it. He poured a stream of

[031-042]water over a heap of fresh, cool grass; in the twilight, the water

[031-043]glimmered like crystal. A thin cow, a scrawny nag, and a burro drank

[031-044]noisily together.

[031-045]Demetrio recognized the limping servant and asked him: "How much do you

[031-046]get a day?"

[031-047]"Eight cents a day, boss."

[031-048]He was an insignificant, scrofulous wraith of a man with green eyes and

[031-049]straight, fair hair. He whined complaint of his boss, the ranch, his

[031-050]bad luck, his dog's life.

[031-051]"You certainly earn your pay all right, my lad," Demetrio interrupted

[031-052]kindly. "You complain and complain, but you aren't no loafer, you work

[031-053]and work." Then, aside to Camilla: "There's always more damned fools in

[031-054]the valley than among us folk in the sierra, don't you think?"

[031-055]"Of course!" she replied.

[031-056]They went on. The valley was lost in darkness; stars came out. Demetrio

[031-057]put his arm around Camilla's waist amorously and whispered in her ear.

[031-058]"Yes," she answered in a faint voice.

[031-059]She was indeed beginning to "fall for him" as she had expressed it.

[031-060]Demetrio slept badly. He flung out of the house very early.

[031-061]"Something is going to happen to me," he thought.

[031-062]It was a silent dawn, with faint murmurs of joy. A thrush sang timidly

[031-063]in one of the ash trees. The animals in the corral trampled on the

[031-064]refuse. The pig grunted its somnolence. The orange tints of the sun

[031-065]streaked the sky; the last star flickered out.

[031-066]Demetrio walked slowly to the encampment.

[031-067]He was thinking of his plow, his two black oxen--young beasts they

[031-068]were, who had worked in the fields only two years--of his two acres of

[031-069]well-fertilized corn. The face of his young wife came to his mind,

[031-070]clear and true as life: he saw her strong, soft features, so gracious

[031-071]when she smiled on her husband, so proudly fierce toward strangers. But

[031-072]when he tried to conjure up the image of his son, his efforts were

[031-073]vain; he had forgotten....

[031-074]He reached the camp. Lying among the farrows, the soldiers slept with

[031-075]the horses, heads bowed, eyes closed.

[031-076]"Our horses are pretty tired, Anastasio. I think we ought to stay here

[031-077]at least another day."

[031-078]"Well, Compadre Demetrio, I'm hankering for the sierra.... If you only

[031-079]knew.... You may not believe me but nothing strikes me right here. I

[031-080]don't know what I miss but I know I miss something. I feel sad ...

[031-081]lost...."

[031-082]"How many hours' ride from here to Limon?"

[031-083]"It's no matter of hours; it's three days' hard riding, Demetrio."

[031-084]"You know," Demetrio said softly, "I feel as though I'd like to see my

[031-085]wife again!"

[031-086]Shortly after, War Paint sought out Camilla.

[031-087]"That's one on you, my dear.... Demetrio's going to leave you flat! He

[031-088]told me so himself; 'I'm going to get my real woman,' he says, and he

[031-089]says, 'Her skin is white and tender ... and her rosy cheeks.... How

[031-090]beautiful she is!' But you don't have to leave him, you know; if you're

[031-091]set on staying, well--they've got a child, you know, and I suppose you

[031-092]could drag it around...."

[031-093]When Demetrio returned, Camilla, weeping, told him everything.

[031-094]"Don't pay no attention to that crazy baggage. It's all lies, lies!"

[031-095]Since Demetrio did not go to Limon or remember his wife again, Camilla

[031-096]grew very happy. War Paint had merely stung herself, like a scorpion.
