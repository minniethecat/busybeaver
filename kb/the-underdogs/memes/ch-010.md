[010-001]"Why don't you call in the tenderfoot to treat you, Compadre [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]],"

[010-002]Anastasio Montanez asked his chief, who had been complaining daily of

[010-003]chills and fever. "You ought to see him; no one has laid a hand to him

[010-004]but himself, and now he's so fit that he doesn't limp a step."

[010-005]But Venancio, standing by with his tins of lard and his dirty string

[010-006]rags ready, protested:

[010-007]"All right, if anybody lays a hand on Demetrio, I won't be responsible."

[010-008]"Nonsense! Rot! What kind of doctor do you think you are? You're no

[010-009]doctor at all. I'll wager you've already forgotten why you ever joined

[010-010]us," said Quail.

[010-011]"Well, I remember why you joined us, Quail," Venancio replied angrily.

[010-012]"Perhaps you'll deny it was because you had stolen a watch and some

[010-013]diamond rings."

[010-014]"Ha, ha, ha! That's rich! But you're worse, my lad; you ran away from

[010-015]your hometown because you poisoned your sweetheart."

[010-016]"You're a Goddamned liar!"

[010-017]"Yes you did! And don't try and deny it! You fed her Spanish fly and..."

[010-018]Venancio's shout of protest was drowned out in the loud laughter of the

[010-019]others. Demetrio, looking pale and sallow, motioned for silence. Then,

[010-020]plaintively:

[010-021]"That'll do. Bring in the student."

[010-022]Luis Cervantes entered. He uncovered Demetrio's wound, examined it

[010-023]carefully, and shook his head. The ligaments had made a furrow in the

[010-024]skin. The leg, badly swollen, seemed about to burst. At every move he

[010-025]made, Demetrio stifled a moan. Luis Cervantes cut the ligaments, soaked

[010-026]the wound in water, covered the leg with large clean rags and bound it

[010-027]up. Demetrio was able to sleep all afternoon and all night. On the

[010-028]morrow he woke up happy.

[010-029]"That tenderfoot has the softest hand in the world!" he said.

[010-030]Quickly Venancio cut in:

[010-031]"All right; just as you say. But don't forget that tenderfoots are like

[010-032]moisture, they seep in everywhere. It's the tenderfoots who stopped us

[010-033]reaping the harvest of the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]."

[010-034]Since Demetrio believed in the barber's knowledge implicitly, when Luis

[010-035]Cervantes came to treat him on the next day he said:

[010-036]"Look here, do your best, see. I want to recover soon and then you can

[010-037]go home or anywhere else you damn well please."

[010-038]Discreetly, Luis Cervantes made no reply.

[010-039]A week, ten days, a fortnight elapsed. The Federal troops seemed to

[010-040]have vanished. There was an abundance of corn and beans, too, in the

[010-041]neighboring ranches. The people hated the Government so bitterly that

[010-042]they were overjoyed to furnish assistance to the rebels. Demetrio's

[010-043]men, therefore, were peacefully waiting for the complete recovery of

[010-044]their chief.

[010-045]Day after day, Luis Cervantes remained humble and silent.

[010-046]"By God, I actually believe you're in love," Demetrio said jokingly one

[010-047]morning after the daily treatment. He had begun to like this

[010-048]tenderfoot. From then on, Demetrio began gradually to show an

[010-049]increasing interest in Cervantes' comfort. One day he asked him if the

[010-050]soldiers gave him his daily ration of meat and milk; Luis Cervantes was

[010-051]forced to answer that his sole nourishment was whatever the old ranch

[010-052]women happened to give him and that everyone still considered him an

[010-053]intruder.

[010-054]"Look here, Tenderfoot, they're all good boys, really," Demetrio

[010-055]answered. "You've got to know how to handle them, that's all. You mark

[010-056]my words; from tomorrow on, there won't be a thing you'll lack."

[010-057]In effect, things began to change that very afternoon. Some of

[010-058]Demetrio's men lay in the quarry, glancing at the sunset that turned

[010-059]the clouds into huge clots of congealed blood and listening to

[010-060]Venancio's amusing stories culled from The Wandering Jew. Some of them,

[010-061]lulled by the narrator's mellifluous voice, began to snore. But Luis

[010-062]Cervantes listened avidly and as soon as Venancio topped off his talk

[010-063]with a storm of anticlerical denunciations he said emphatically:

[010-064]"Wonderful, wonderful! What intelligence! You're a most gifted man!"

[010-065]"Well, I reckon it's not so bad," Venancio answered, warming to the

[010-066]flattery, "but my parents died and I didn't have a chance to study for

[010-067]a profession."

[010-068]"That's easy to remedy, I'm sure. Once our cause is victorious, you can

[010-069]easily get a degree. A matter of two or three weeks' assistant's work

[010-070]at some hospital and a letter of recommendation from our chief and

[010-071]you'll be a full-fledged doctor, all right. The thing is child's play."

[010-072]From that night onward Venancio, unlike the others, ceased calling him

[010-073]Tenderfoot. He addressed him as Louie.

[010-074]It was Louie, this, and Louie, that, right and left, all the time.
