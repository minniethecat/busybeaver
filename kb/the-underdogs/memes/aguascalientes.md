## Overview

Aguascalientes is a city in central Mexico that played a pivotal political role in the later stages of the Mexican Revolution. In the narrative of *The Underdogs*, it appears primarily in Part III as the destination toward which [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio Macías]] and his men are moving, and as the site of the Convention of Aguascalientes — the revolutionary assembly that exposed the irreconcilable rifts between the [[villistas|Villistas]], [[venustiano-carranza|Carrancistas]], and Zapatistas.

## Location & Geography

Aguascalientes is located in the central Mexican state of the same name, situated between the revolutionary strongholds of [[zacatecas|Zacatecas]] (to the north) and [[jalisco|Jalisco]] (to the south). The city lies on the main rail line connecting Mexico's northern mining regions with the central plateau, making it a strategic transit point during the revolution.

## Role in the Narrative

Aguascalientes enters the narrative as a destination of strategic and political significance. [[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]] repeatedly urges [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio Macías]] to head there, believing the Convention will offer a path to political legitimacy beyond mere banditry (032-008). The men are skeptical — "Why in hell do we have to go to Aguascalientes?" (034-023) — revealing the growing tension between Cervantes's political ambitions and the soldiers' simpler motivations.

The Convention itself is presented as a gathering of revolutionary factions. [[panfilo-natera|Pánfilo Natera]] and his forces pass through on their way to the Convention, and the city becomes a meeting point for the various revolutionary armies crisscrossing the region. However, the narrative treats the Convention with characteristic irony: the political maneuvering in Aguascalientes does little to change the conditions of the common soldiers or the peasantry.

In the aftermath of the Convention's failure, Aguascalientes becomes associated with the general decay and disillusionment of the late revolutionary period. The streets are described as "refuse piles" (035-072), and the city — along with Zacatecas — becomes emblematic of the destruction wrought by the war (036-064, 038-001).

## Key Passages

> "Don't you think it advisable to go to Aguascalientes first, General?" [032-008]

*Luis Cervantes urges Demetrio Macías to head to Aguascalientes, seeing the Convention as an opportunity for political advancement.*

> "Why in hell do we have to go to Aguascalientes?" [034-023]

*A soldier voices the common men's confusion and resentment at being dragged into political machinations they do not understand or care about.*

> The streets in Aguascalientes were so many refuse piles. [035-072]

*The imagery of decay captures the novel's disillusioned view of the revolution's material consequences.*

> Aguascalientes and Zacatecas have done all they could... In Zacatecas and Aguascalientes, in the little country towns, the Federal soldiers had looted everything. [036-064, 038-001]

*The twin cities of Aguascalientes and Zacatecas are described as victims of systematic looting by the [[federal-army|Federal Army]], underscoring the war's devastation of civilian spaces.*
