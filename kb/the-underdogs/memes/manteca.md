## Overview

Manteca (*El Manteca*, literally "The Lard") is one of [[Demetrio Macías]]'s original soldiers in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. His nickname—which translates as "grease" or "fat"—carries both the affectionate mockery of peasant camaraderie and a hint of the dehumanization that war inflicts on all of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s men. Manteca appears throughout the novel alongside [[Pancracio]] and [[Quail]], forming the novel's trio of loyal, instinct-driven peasant soldiers.

## Biography

Manteca appears in the novel's earliest scenes, shouting [[Anastasio Montañés]]'s name during the band's flight (002-066). Like [[Quail]], he is among the unarmed men begging for weapons: "Manteca and [[quail|Quail]], unarmed, begged for a gun" (003-041), establishing him as one of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s original followers who joined before the band acquired its arsenal.

[[Mariano Azuela]]'s physical description of Manteca is deliberately harsh: "…k--a horrible man. The other, Manteca, was so much human refuse" (006-094). This unsparing portrait is characteristic of the novel's refusal to romanticize its peasant soldiers. Manteca is not a hero; he is a man brutalized by poverty and war, his humanity eroded to a crude survival instinct.

Manteca fights through all the band's campaigns. He and [[Pancracio]] are frequently paired: "[[pancracio|Pancracio]] and Manteca, lying like lizards between t…" (012-007), "[[pancracio|Pancracio]] and Manteca surpassed the others in the s…" (017-064), "[[pancracio|Pancracio]] and Manteca vied with…" (019-067). In the later chapters, Manteca "was boiling corn on the cob" (023-051) amid the smoke of battle—a small, domestic gesture that Azuela uses to remind the reader that these fighters remain peasants at heart.

## Character

Manteca is defined by crude physicality and the absence of reflection. He shouts, he fights, he eats, he loots. "Manteca shouted, waxing enthusiastic…" (012-027) captures his impulsive nature—he does not plan or calculate, he simply acts on whatever feeling the moment produces. When examining captured loot, someone who "had examined Manteca's treasure" (030-022) reveals the soldier's simple acquisitiveness, his war reduced to the accumulation of whatever can be carried.

Yet Manteca is not without loyalty. His presence through the novel's full arc—from the band's formation to its final dissolution—testifies to a steadfastness that requires no articulation. Like the other peasant soldiers, Manteca's loyalty to [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] is the one constant in a world where everything else has been stripped away.

## Key Passages

> Manteca, his smooth… [PN=003-078]

The fragmentary description hints at Manteca's peculiar physicality—a "smooth" quality that contrasts with the "pockmarked" [[Pancracio]] and the bearded [[Anastasio Montañés]].

> …k--a horrible man. The other, Manteca, was so much human refuse; [PN=006-094]

Azuela's most unsparing description of Manteca—language that refuses to sentimentalize the peasant soldier, presenting him as a product of degradation rather than nobility.

> "It's fun fighting this way," Manteca cried, spicing every other wo… [PN=030-018]

Manteca's crude enjoyment of combat reveals the extent to which violence has become normalized—even pleasurable—for men who have known nothing else.

> Manteca was boiling corn on the cob… [PN=023-051]

Amid the chaos of war, Manteca cooks. The image of a soldier boiling corn in "suffocating smoke" is quintessential Azuela: the domestic and the violent coexisting without comment.
