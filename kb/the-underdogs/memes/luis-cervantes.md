Luis Cervantes is one of the most complex characters in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s
novel _The Underdogs_ (*Los de abajo*), serving as [[Demetrio Macías]]'s
advisor, medical attendant, and intellectual foil. A [[[[mexican-revolution|Mexican Revolution]]|medical
student]] turned journalist, Cervantes joins [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band in
Chapter V under ambiguous circumstances — captured as a suspected [[federal-army|Federal Army]]
deserter, he persuades the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries to spare him. Throughout the novel,
he embodies the tension between [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary idealism and calculating
self-interest, making him a pivotal figure in Azuela's critique of the
Revolution's moral degeneration.

## Origins and Background

Cervantes introduces himself as a medical student and journalist who had been
conscripted into the [[Federal Army]] (005-072). His education sets him apart
from [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s largely peasant band: he speaks in measured, articulate tones,
and his knowledge of medicine immediately proves valuable when he treats
[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s wounds (010-022). Yet his background remains shadowy — the novel
never fully reveals his past before the Revolution, leaving readers to question
whether his [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary conversion is genuine or opportunistic.

## Role in Demetrio's Band

### Medical Service and Rising Influence

Cervantes' first contribution is medical: he operates on [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s wounded
thigh, cutting ligaments and dressing the wound with professional skill
(010-022, 010-025). This earns him [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s trust. Over the following weeks,
Cervantes remains "humble and silent," receiving a daily ration of meat and
milk that the soldiers grudgingly provide (010-045, 010-050).

His influence grows through counsel rather than combat. He advises [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] on
military strategy, urging him to join forces with [[Panfilo Natera]] (013-043,
013-065). When supplies run low, Cervantes proposes raiding a nearby hacienda
(017-046). His cleverness in identifying a spy among the prisoners solidifies
his reputation as a sharp observer (017-051, 017-056).

### The Opportunist

Cervantes' opportunism becomes increasingly apparent. He courts [[camilla|Camilla]],
[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s young companion, with calculated charm (011-028, 011-041). When the
band captures wealthy prisoners, Cervantes extorts money from them — pulling
out "four crisp double-dollar bills" and writing ransom letters (032-029,
032-034, 032-081). His eyes "shone" at the sight of money (027-082).

> "The triumph of our cause, gentlemen..." Luis Cervantes proposed, raising his
> glass. His toast was so elaborate and ornate that no one understood it, yet
> all applauded at the end. (024-030, 024-037, 024-049)

### The Deserter's Exit

When the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]aries face defeat in Part III, Cervantes abandons the band.
Unlike the fatalistic [[Demetrio Macías]], who returns to the [[Juchipila]]
canyon to meet his end, Cervantes crosses the border to the United States — a
survivor who recognizes when a cause is lost (036-032). His desertion is the
novel's final verdict on the intellectual who joined the Revolution for
personal advancement rather than conviction.

## Thematic Significance

Cervantes represents the educated *oportunista* — a recurring figure in
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary literature. Unlike the instinctive warrior [[Demetrio Macías]],
Cervantes calculates every move. His education gives him power over the
illiterate soldiers, but it also alienates him from their raw, unmediated
experience of the Revolution. His desertion at the novel's end mirrors
[[Mariano Azuela|Azuela]]'s own disillusionment: the intellectual who joined
the cause for ideals found only self-interest and corruption.

Critics have read Cervantes as a partial self-portrait of Azuela himself — both
were medical men who served with [[Pancho Villa]]'s forces and later grew
disillusioned. Yet Cervantes lacks Azuela's moral seriousness; he is the
portrait of what the author feared he might have become had he lacked
conviction.
