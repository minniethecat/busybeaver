The Constitutionalist Army (*Ejército Constitucionalista*) was the
[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary faction led by [[Venustiano Carranza]] that emerged after
[[Victoriano Huerta]]'s 1913 coup against President [[Francisco I. Madero]].
Unlike the more loosely organized popular armies of [[Pancho Villa]] and
[[Emiliano Zapata]], the Constitutionalists combined military force with a
coherent political program — the restoration of the 1857 Constitution and the
construction of a post-[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary state. In [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The
Underdogs_, the Constitutionalists represent the Revolution's second act: the
moment when political calculation replaces popular energy, and the lawyers and
generals push aside the peasant-soldiers.

## Historical Background

### The Plan of Guadalupe

[[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]], the governor of Coahuila, refused to recognize [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]]'s government
and issued the Plan of Guadalupe on March 26, 1913, calling for armed
resistance. The plan named [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]] "First Chief" (*Primer Jefe*) of the
Constitutionalist Army and promised to restore constitutional order. Unlike
[[Pancho Villa|Villa]]'s [[villistas|División del Norte]] — a more spontaneous popular
mobilization — the Constitutionalist Army was built around state militias and
professional officers.

### Victory and Its Costs

After defeating [[Pancho Villa|Villa]]'s forces at the Battles of Celaya
(April 1915), the Constitutionalist Army became the dominant military force in
Mexico. [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]]'s triumph was political as well as military: the Constitution
of 1917, drafted under his auspices, codified many [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary demands —
including [[Agrarian Reform|land reform]] and labor rights — while
consolidating power in a strong executive. But for the peasant-soldiers who had
fought and died under leaders like [[Demetrio Macías]], the Constitutionalist
victory brought few tangible benefits.

## Role in the Novel

### The Political Alternative

In _The Underdogs_, the Constitutionalists appear primarily through political
discussion rather than direct combat. [[Luis Cervantes]] urges [[[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]
Macías]] to align with [[Panfilo Natera]], a Villista general who is
negotiating with the Constitutionalist camp (013-043, 013-065). Cervantes
recognizes that the Constitutionalists represent the winning side — the faction
that will control the post-[[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary state — and his political instincts
pull him in their direction.

> "Natera is a genius!" Luis Cervantes said when they had returned to camp.
> "He sees which way the wind is blowing. Villa is finished, mark my words.
> Carranza is the future." (018-077)

### The Victory of the Lawyers

The Constitutionalist victory represents, in Azuela's skeptical vision, the
triumph of the Revolution's managerial class over its popular base. The
peasants who fought with [[Pancho Villa|Villa]] and [[Demetrio Macías]] are
discarded once they have served their military purpose. The novel's final
chapters — in which [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] dies in the [[Juchipila]] Canyon while [[Luis
Cervantes]] escapes to the United States — dramatize this betrayal: the
calculating survivors prosper while the true believers perish.

## Key Passages

> "When the revolution triumphs," Luis Cervantes resumed, "the

> not a politician. Carranza understands that revolutions are won in offices,
> not on battlefields." (013-065)

> Demetrio listened without understanding. Carranza, Villa, conventions,
> constitutions — these were words from another world, a world of cities and
> newspapers that had nothing to do with the sierra, the maize, the smell of
> rain on dry earth. (023)

## Legacy

The Constitutionalist Army's victory shaped Mexico's political trajectory for
the rest of the 20th century. The party that [[venustiano-carranza|Carranza]]'s successors founded —
eventually the Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) — ruled Mexico until
2000, embodying the paradox that Azuela diagnosed: a [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] that
institutionalized itself and forgot the underdogs who made it possible.
