## Overview

Pancho Villa (born Doroteo Arango) was the legendary commander of the Northern Division during the [[Mexican Revolution]]. A former bandit who became one of the Revolution's most effective military leaders, Villa commanded the loyalty of thousands of peasants and working-class Mexicans. [[Mariano Azuela]] served as a doctor in Villa's army, and Villa appears throughout _The Underdogs_ as an offstage presence — the magnetic, feared, and mythologized figure whose name electrifies the common soldiers (020-001).

## Biography

### From Bandit to Revolutionary

Before the Revolution, Villa was known as a bandit operating in northern Mexico. The novel captures his mythic transformation from outlaw to [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary hero. When the soldiers speak of Villa, they invoke both his bandit past and his providential role: "Villa as Providence, the bandit, that passes through the world with the luminous morning star in his forehead" (020-022). This duality — feared outlaw and folk savior — defines his legend.

### Commander of the Northern Division

Villa's Northern Division was among the most formidable forces of the Revolution. His troops, described as "all northerners, tough and rugged," inspired awe among [[Panfilo Natera]]'s men when they compared stories (020-030, 020-033). Villa's military innovations included the use of airplanes, a novelty that fascinated the peasant soldiers (020-040).

### The Magic of His Name

Perhaps no passage in the novel better captures Villa's mythic stature than the opening of Part II, Chapter I: "Villa is coming! The news spread like lightning. Villa — the magic word! The Great Man, the most esteemed man, the warrior with power and authority" (020-001, 020-013). The soldiers "retailed the wonders of Villa and his men" with an almost religious reverence (020-033).

### Villa's Currency and Economy

The novel provides material evidence of Villa's power through his currency. Villa minted his own silver coins and printed "double-face" paper bills that circulated throughout [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary territory. References to "those pure silver pieces Villa coins" and "crisp double-face bills of Villa's" indicate the economic infrastructure of his movement (008-021, 032-034). In one scene, a wounded man's wallet contains "two [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]] bills," contrasting rival currencies (022-112).

### Defeat at Celaya

By Part III of the novel, Villa's fortunes have turned. A deserter reports "the tremendous defeat of Villa at Celaya," a battle that shattered the myth of his invincibility (037-027). Deserters from "General Villa's troops this side of Celaya" signal the unraveling of the Northern Division (037-015). The soldiers who once spoke his name with awe now debate whether "anyone on earth who can beat General Villa" exists, their doubt answering the question (037-019).

## Thematic Significance

In _The Underdogs_, Villa functions less as a character than as a mythic presence — the embodiment of the Revolution's promise and its contradictions. He is simultaneously bandit and savior, warlord and folk hero. The soldiers' faith in Villa reflects the quasi-religious devotion that [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary leaders inspired among the *los de abajo* (those from below). Yet Villa's defeat at Celaya — and the subsequent fracturing of his forces — mirrors the novel's larger theme of [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary disillusionment. His name, once "magic," becomes a question mark.
