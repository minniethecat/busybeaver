[015-001]That evening, there was much merrymaking at the dance, and a great

[015-002]quantity of mezcal was drunk. "I miss Camilla," said [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] in a loud

[015-003]voice. Everybody looked about for Camilla.

[015-004]"She's sick, she's got a headache," said Agapita harshly, uneasy as she

[015-005]caught sight of the malicious glances leveled at her.

[015-006]When the dance was over, Demetrio, somewhat unsteady on his feet,

[015-007]thanked all the kind neighbors who had welcomed them and promised that

[015-008]when the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]] had triumphed he would remember them one and all,

[015-009]because "hospital or jail is a true test of friendship."

[015-010]"May God's hand lead you all," said an old woman.

[015-011]"God bless you all and keep you well," others added.

[015-012]Utterly drunk, Maria Antonia said: "Come back soon, damn soon!"

[015-013]On the morrow, Maria Antonia, who, though she was pockmarked and

[015-014]walleyed, nevertheless enjoyed a notorious reputation--indeed it was

[015-015]confidently proclaimed that no man had failed to go with her behind the

[015-016]river weeds at some time or other--shouted to Camilla:

[015-017]"Hey there, you! What's the matter? What are you doing there skulking

[015-018]in the corner with a shawl tied round your head! You're crying, I

[015-019]wager. Look at her eyes; they look like a witch's. There's no sorrow

[015-020]lasts more than three days!"

[015-021]Agapita knitted her eyebrows and muttered indistinctly to herself.

[015-022]The old crones felt uneasy and lonesome since Demetrio's men had left.

[015-023]The men, too, in spite of their gossip and insults, lamented their

[015-024]departure since now they would have no one to bring them fresh meat

[015-025]every day. It is pleasant indeed to spend your time eating and

[015-026]drinking, and sleeping all day long in the cool shade of the rocks,

[015-027]while clouds ravel and unravel their fleecy threads on the blue shuttle

[015-028]of the sky.

[015-029]"Look at them again. There they go!" Maria Antonia yelled. "Why, they

[015-030]look like toys."

[015-031]Demetrio's men, riding their thin nags, could still be descried in the

[015-032]distance against the sapphire translucence of the sky, where the broken

[015-033]rocks and the chaparral melted into a single bluish smooth surface.

[015-034]Across the air a gust of hot wind bore the broken, faltering strains of

[015-035]"La Adelita," the revolutionary song, to the settlement. Camilla, who

[015-036]had come out when Maria Antonia shouted, could no longer control

[015-037]herself; she dived back into her hut, unable to restrain her tears and

[015-038]moaning. Maria Antonia burst into laughter and moved off.

[015-039]"They've cast the evil eye on my daughter," Agapita said in perplexity.

[015-040]She pondered a while, then duly reached a decision. From a pole in the

[015-041]hut she took down a piece of strong leather which her husband used to

[015-042]hitch up the yoke. This pole stood between a picture of Christ and one

[015-043]of the Virgin. Agapita promptly twisted the leather and proceeded to

[015-044]administer a sound thrashing to Camilla in order to dispel the evil

[015-045]spirits.

[015-046]Riding proudly on his horse, Demetrio felt like a new man. His eyes

[015-047]recovered their peculiar metallic brilliance, and the blood flowed, red

[015-048]and warm, through his coppery, pure-blooded Aztec cheeks.

[015-049]The men threw out their chests as if to breathe the widening horizon,

[015-050]the immensity of the sky, the blue from the mountains and the fresh

[015-051]air, redolent with the various odors of the sierra. They spurred their

[015-052]horses to a gallop as if in that mad race they laid claims of

[015-053]possession to the earth. What man among them now remembered the stern

[015-054]chief of police, the growling policeman, or the conceited cacique? What

[015-055]man remembered his pitiful hut where he slaved away, always under the

[015-056]eyes of the owner or the ruthless and sullen foreman, always forced to

[015-057]rise before dawn, and to take up his shovel, basket, or goad, wearing

[015-058]himself out to earn a mere pitcher of atole and a handful of beans?

[015-059]They laughed, they sang, they whistled, drunk with the sunlight, the

[015-060]air of the open spaces, the wine of life.

[015-061]Meco, prancing forward on his horse, bared his white glistening teeth,

[015-062]joking and kicking up like a clown.

[015-063]"Hey, Pancracio," he asked with utmost seriousness, "my wife writes me

[015-064]I've got another kid. How in hell is that? I ain't seen her since

[015-065]Madero was President."

[015-066]"That's nothing," the other replied. "You just left her a lot of eggs

[015-067]to hatch for you!"

[015-068]They all laughed uproariously. Only Meco, grave and aloof, sang in a

[015-069]voice horribly shrill:

[015-070]"I gave her a penny  

[015-071]That wasn't enough.  

[015-072]I gave her a nickel  

[015-073]The wench wanted more.  

[015-074]We bargained. I asked  

[015-075]If a dime was enough  

[015-076]But she wanted a quarter.  

[015-077]By God! That was tough!  

[015-078]All wenches are fickle  

[015-079]And trumpery stuff!"

[015-080]The sun, beating down upon them, dulled their minds and bodies and

[015-081]presently they were silent. All day long they rode through the canyon,

[015-082]up and down the steep, round hills, dirty and bald as a man's head,

[015-083]hill after hill in endless succession. At last, late in the afternoon,

[015-084]they descried several stone church towers in the heart of a bluish

[015-085]ridge, and, beyond, the white road with its curling spirals of dust and

[015-086]its gray telegraph poles.

[015-087]They advanced toward the main road; in the distance they spied a figure

[015-088]of an Indian sitting on the embankment. They drew up to him. He proved

[015-089]to be an unfriendly looking old man, clad in rags; he was laboriously

[015-090]attempting to mend his leather sandals with the help of a dull knife. A

[015-091]burro loaded with fresh green grass stood by. Demetrio accosted him.

[015-092]"What are you doing, Grandpa?"

[015-093]"Gathering alfalfa for my cow."

[015-094]"How many Federals are there around here?"

[015-095]"Just a few: not more than a dozen, I reckon."

[015-096]The old man grew communicative. He told them of many important rumors:

[015-097]Obregon was besieging Guadalajara, Torres was in complete control of

[015-098]the Potosi region, Natera ruled over Fresnillo.

[015-099]"All right," said Demetrio, "you can go where you're headed for, see,

[015-100]but you be damn careful not to tell anyone you saw us, because if you

[015-101]do, I'll pump you full of lead. And I could track you down, even if you

[015-102]tried to hide in the pit of hell, see?"

[015-103]"What do you say, boys?" Demetrio asked them as soon as the old man had

[015-104]disappeared.

[015-105]"To hell with the mochos! We'll kill every blasted one of them!" they

[015-106]cried in unison.

[015-107]Then they set to counting their cartridges and the hand grenades the

[015-108]Owl had made out of fragments of iron tubing and metal bed handles.

[015-109]"Not much to brag about, but we'll soon trade them for rifles,"

[015-110]Anastasio observed.

[015-111]Anxiously they pressed forward, spurring the thin flanks of their nags

[015-112]to a gallop. Demetrio's brisk, imperious tones of order brought them

[015-113]abruptly to a halt.

[015-114]They dismounted by the side of a hill, protected by thick huizache

[015-115]trees. Without unsaddling their horses, each began to search for stones

[015-116]to serve as pillows.
