## Overview

Alberto Solís is General [[Panfilo Natera]]'s chief of staff in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_. He first appears in Chapter XVIII when [[Luis Cervantes]] and [[Demetrio Macías]] arrive at Natera's headquarters. Unlike the unreflective peasant soldiers of [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s band, Solís is an educated intellectual—articulate, ironic, and deeply disillusioned with the Revolution he serves. His conversations with Cervantes form some of the novel's most explicit philosophical meditations on the meaning (and meaninglessness) of the [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary cause.

## Biography

Solís enters the narrative at Natera's headquarters, where he recognizes Cervantes immediately: "The moment you entered I thought I recognized you. Well, well, even now I can hardly believe my eyes!" (018-022). The two men share a history from civilian life, and Solís is eager to reconnect over drinks.

He serves as Natera's chief of staff and speechwriter. When Cervantes praises Natera as "a genius," Solís remarks with characteristic irony that he himself described Natera as "The Aztec Eagle! He buried his beak of steel in the head of [[victoriano-huerta|Huerta]] the serpent!"—adding that this was how he "expressed it in a speech I made at Ciudad Juarez" (020-007). The self-aware theatricality of the remark reveals Solís's distance from his own [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary rhetoric.

Cervantes privately dismisses Solís as "a nobody ... a timeserver" (018-078)—a judgment that may reflect Cervantes's own opportunism more than Solís's character. In later chapters, Solís proves to be far more self-aware than Cervantes credits. When Cervantes asks why he stays in the Revolution, Solís replies with disarming honesty: "Nobody threw me down. I'm here because I like to play safe" (021-017).

At the battle in Chapter XXI, Solís delivers a speech and then "stood lost in thought, his arms crossed" (021-084). When bullets fly, he takes fright—"I'll be damned if I like these plaguey mosquitoes!" (021-084)—but then sits calmly on a rock, watching the battle unfold. His gaze "roved as he watched the spirals of smoke from the rifles, the dust of roofs crumbling from houses as they fell before the artillery" (021-088). It is in this moment that Solís utters his most famous line—an elegy for the Revolution that also serves as its epitaph.

## Character

Solís is the novel's most articulate spokesman for [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary disillusionment. Where [[Luis Cervantes]] is an opportunist who adapts his principles to his circumstances, Solís is a genuine intellectual who has thought his way into despair. He sees the Revolution clearly—its barbarism and its beauty—and can neither abandon it nor believe in it.

His defining trait is irony, directed both outward at [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary pretensions and inward at his own compromises. He flatters [[Demetrio Macías]] with stories so charming that [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]] "found himself repeating it as gospel truth" (018-074), yet Solís is never taken in by his own performance. When Cervantes's scorn makes him blush, Solís's retort—"By God, that chief of yours is a man! What daring, what assurance!" (021-020)—is admiration and self-indictment in equal measure.

## Key Passages

> "How beautiful the revolution! Even in its most barbarous aspect it is beautiful," Solis said with deep feeling. Then a vague melancholy seized him, and speaking low: "A pity what remains to do won't be as beautiful! We must wait a while, until there are no men left to fight—a day when we'll all be pacified and then every one of us, all of us, will be left alone, nursing our sorrow in the shadow of some wall or at the foot of some factory." [PN=021-070]

The novel's most quoted passage on the Revolution's tragic trajectory. Solís sees what the others cannot: that winning the war will not mean winning peace, and that the beautiful chaos of revolt will harden into something colder.

> "Nobody threw me down," Solis said. "I'm here because I like to play safe." The irony in Solis' voice brought a blush to Cervantes' cheek. [PN=021-017, 021-019]

Solís's admission of cowardice is also an accusation—against Cervantes, against himself, against every intellectual who serves the Revolution without quite believing in it.

> Unctuously, Solis congratulated Demetrio on the feats that had won him fame and the notice of Pancho Villa's northern division. Demetrio warmed to his praise. Gratefully, he heard his prowess vaunted, though at times he found it difficult to believe he was the hero of the exploits the other narrated. But Solis' story proved so charming, so convincing, that before long he found himself repeating it as gospel truth. [PN=018-070, 018-074]

The passage captures Solís's skill with words—and the novel's broader theme of how [[mexican-revolution|revolution]]ary legend is manufactured by literate men for illiterate heroes.

> After his speech, Alberto Solis stood lost in thought, his arms crossed. Suddenly, he took fright. "I'll be damned if I like these plaguey mosquitoes!" he said. [PN=021-084]

The only passage where his full name appears, and it juxtaposes the thinker and the frightened man in a single breath—Solís in miniature.
