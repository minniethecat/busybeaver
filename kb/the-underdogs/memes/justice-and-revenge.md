## Overview

The concept of justice runs through *The Underdogs* as both promise and accusation. The novel opens with an act of injustice — the burning of Demetrio Macías's house by Federal soldiers — that drives him into armed rebellion. But as the Preface acknowledges with unusual directness, the revolution that answered "certain injustices" soon "gave rise to others" (P01-021). Justice, in Azuela's telling, is not simply served by revolution; it is complicated by it, and ultimately betrayed.

## Justice as Revolutionary Motive

Demetrio's earliest articulation of his cause invokes justice in its simplest form: wrongs to be righted. When Luis Cervantes first encounters Demetrio's band, he finds men who "implore justice, and justice alone" (006-045–046). The repetition — "justice, and justice alone" — captures the purity of the initial revolutionary impulse. These men are not fighting for abstractions like "the Revolution"; they are fighting because specific injustices were done to them.

Cervantes, the educated outsider, recognizes this language and amplifies it. He becomes "intimate with" the men who speak of justice (006-046), translating their grievances into political vocabulary. But the translation itself is an act of distortion — the men's concrete injuries become abstract principles, and the gap between the two will widen as the novel progresses.

## The Preface's Verdict

The Preface delivers the novel's most direct assessment: "certain injustices, it had given rise to others" (P01-021). This is not a character speaking but the narrative frame itself, and the statement appears twice — once in each version of the preface (P01-021, P02-020) — as if to insist that the reader not miss it. The revolution that began by answering injustice ends by producing it. The cycle is structural, not accidental.

This framing distinguishes *The Underdogs* from revolutionary propaganda. Azuela does not argue that the revolution was pure and then corrupted; he argues that the mechanism of revolution — armed force answering armed force — contains injustice within itself. The means produce the ends.

## From Justice to Revenge

The novel traces the erosion of "justice" into something closer to revenge. Early Demetrio fights to recover what was taken from him. Late Demetrio fights because fighting has become what he does. The language of justice fades from the narrative as the revolution advances — not because justice has been achieved, but because the word has lost its meaning for men who have seen too much.

This is one of the ways [[banditry-and-revolution|the bandit-revolutionary distinction]] operates: the bandit seeks revenge; the revolutionary seeks justice. But when revolutionary justice and personal revenge become indistinguishable — when killing Federals satisfies both principles at once — the categories collapse.

## Key Passages

> Certain injustices, it had given rise to others. [P01-021]

*The Preface's verdict — spoken by the narrative frame itself, and repeated for emphasis (P02-020). The revolution's tragedy in a single sentence.*

> The subjugated, the beaten and baffled, who implore justice, and justice alone. [006-045–046]

*Demetrio's men as they see themselves — not revolutionaries in the abstract, but men demanding specific redress for specific wrongs.*
