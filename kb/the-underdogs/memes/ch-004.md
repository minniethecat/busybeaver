[004-001]Two men were missing, Serapio the candymaker, and Antonio, who

[004-002]played the cymbals in the Juchipila band. "Maybe they'll join us

[004-003]further on," said [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]].

[004-004]The return journey proved moody. Anastasio Montanez alone preserved his

[004-005]equanimity, a kindly expression playing in his sleepy eyes and on his

[004-006]bearded face. Pancracio's harsh, gorillalike profile retained its

[004-007]repulsive immutability.

[004-008]The soldiers had retreated; Demetrio began the search for the soldiers'

[004-009]horses which had been hidden in the sierra.

[004-010]Suddenly Quail, who had been walking ahead, shrieked. He had caught

[004-011]sight of his companions swinging from the branches of a mesquite. There

[004-012]could be no doubt of their identity; Serapio and Antonio they certainly

[004-013]were. Anastasio Montanez prayed brokenly.

[004-014]"Our Father Who art in heaven, hallowed be Thy name. Thy kingdom

[004-015]come..."

[004-016]"Amen," his men answered in low tones, their heads bowed, their hats

[004-017]upon their breasts....

[004-018]Then, hurriedly, they took the Juchipila canyon northward, without

[004-019]halting to rest until nightfall.

[004-020]Quail kept walking close to Anastasio unable to banish from his mind

[004-021]the two who were hanged, their dislocated limp necks, their dangling

[004-022]legs, their arms pendulous, and their bodies moving slowly in the wind.

[004-023]On the morrow, Demetrio complained bitterly of his wound; he could no

[004-024]longer ride on horseback. They were forced to carry him the rest of the

[004-025]way on a makeshift stretcher of leaves and branches.

[004-026]"He's bleeding frightfully," said Anastasio Montanez, tearing off one

[004-027]of his shirt-sleeves and tying it tightly about Demetrio's thigh, a

[004-028]little above the wound.

[004-029]"That's good," said Venancio. "It'll keep him from bleeding and stop

[004-030]the pain."

[004-031]Venancio was a barber. In his native town, he pulled teeth and

[004-032]fulfilled the office of medicine man. He was accorded an unimpeachable

[004-033]authority because he had read The Wandering Jew and one or two other

[004-034]books. They called him "Doctor"; and since he was conceited about his

[004-035]knowledge, he employed very few words.

[004-036]They took turns, carrying the stretcher in relays of four over the bare

[004-037]stony mesa and up the steep passes.

[004-038]At high noon, when the reflection of the sun on the calcareous soil

[004-039]burned their shoulders and made the landscape dimly waver before their

[004-040]eyes, the monotonous, rhythmical moan of the wounded rose in unison

[004-041]with the ceaseless cry of the locusts. They stopped to rest at every

[004-042]small hut they found hidden between the steep, jagged rocks.

[004-043]"Thank God, a kind soul and tortillas full of beans and chili are never

[004-044]lacking," Anastasio Montanez said with a triumphant belch.

[004-045]The mountaineers would shake calloused hands with the travelers, saying:

[004-046]"God's blessing on you! He will find a way to help you all, never fear.

[004-047]We're going ourselves, starting tomorrow morning. We're dodging the

[004-048]draft, with those damned Government people who've declared war to the

[004-049]death on us, on all the poor. They come and steal our pigs, our

[004-050]chickens and corn, they burn our homes and carry our women off, and if

[004-051]they ever get hold of us they'll kill us like mad dogs, and we die

[004-052]right there on the spot and that's the end of the story!"

[004-053]At sunset, amid the flames dyeing the sky with vivid, variegated

[004-054]colors, they descried a group of houses up in the heart of the blue

[004-055]mountains. Demetrio ordered them to carry him there.

[004-056]These proved to be a few wretched straw huts, dispersed all over the

[004-057]river slopes, between rows of young sprouting corn and beans. They

[004-058]lowered the stretcher and Demetrio, in a weak voice, asked for a glass

[004-059]of water.

[004-060]Groups of squalid Indians sat in the dark pits of the huts, men with

[004-061]bony chests, disheveled, matted hair, and ruddy cheeks; behind them,

[004-062]eyes shone up from floors of fresh reeds.

[004-063]A child with a large belly and glossy dark skin came close to the

[004-064]stretcher to inspect the wounded man. An old woman followed, and soon

[004-065]all of them drew about Demetrio in a circle.

[004-066]A girl sympathizing with him in his plight brought a jicara of bluish

[004-067]water. With hands shaking, Demetrio took it up and drank greedily.

[004-068]"Will you have some more?"

[004-069]He raised his eyes and glanced at the girl, whose features were common

[004-070]but whose voice had a note of kindness in it. Wiping his sweating brow

[004-071]with the back of his palm and turning on one side, he gasped: "May God

[004-072]reward you."

[004-073]Then his whole body shook, making the leaves of the stretcher rustle.

[004-074]Fever possessed him; he fainted.

[004-075]"It's a damp night and that's terrible for the fever," said Remigia, an

[004-076]old wrinkled barefooted woman, wearing a cloth rag for a blouse.

[004-077]She invited them to move Demetrio into her hut.

[004-078]Pancracio, Anastasio Montanez, and Quail lay down beside the stretcher

[004-079]like faithful dogs, watchful of their master's wishes. The rest

[004-080]scattered about in search of food.

[004-081]Remigia offered them all she had, chili and tortillas.

[004-082]"Imagine! I had eggs, chickens, even a goat and her kid, but those damn

[004-083]soldiers wiped me out clean."

[004-084]Then, making a trumpet of her hands, she drew near Anastasio and

[004-085]murmured in his ear:

[004-086]"Imagine, they even carried away Senora Nieves' little girl!"
