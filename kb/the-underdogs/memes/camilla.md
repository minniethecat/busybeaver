## Overview

Camilla is [[Luis Cervantes]]'s primary love interest in [[Mariano Azuela]]'s _The Underdogs_, a young girl with blue eyes and golden hair who becomes entangled with [[Demetrio Macías]]'s band. First introduced in Chapter VII, she serves as a domestic counterpoint to the violence of the Revolution — a figure of tenderness and hope whose relationship with the educated [[Luis Cervantes]] traces an emotional arc across Part I and into Part II. Her story also introduces sexual rivalry into the band's dynamics, as [[Demetrio Macías]] himself harbors feelings for her.

## Character

Camilla emerges as a sharply observed portrait of youthful romantic awakening amid war. When [[Luis Cervantes]] first encounters her, she is shy and easily flustered: "Camilla blushed" (007-018), and he soon discovers that through her "kindness, Camilla provided him with all that he wanted" (008-004). Her curiosity about the outsider — the educated "Tenderfoot" — leads her to cross-question him with growing "familiarity" (008-016), revealing a spirited intelligence beneath her initial reserve.

The courtship between Camilla and Luis unfolds across several chapters, marked by her oscillation between boldness and coy withdrawal. In Chapter XI, she calls to him — "Look here, Tenderfoot, I want to tell you something" (011-001) — but her tongue then "clove to her mouth, heavy and damp as a rag" (011-008). She vanishes and reappears "more coy and eager than ever" (011-020), her behavior revealing the intensity of her feelings: "Camilla had never seen [[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]] laugh so heartily" (011-041). The chapter climaxes with her confession that she has never known anyone quite like him, her adoring glances meeting his "radiant, clean" appearance (011-048).

Their relationship reaches a pivotal moment in Chapter XIV, when Luis confronts Camilla by the river at sundown (014-040). Walking the same trail, he meets her, and Camilla feels an overwhelming mixture of fear and attraction. When he declares his feelings, she cries "Liar!" but her face is "transfigured with joy" (014-062), a moment that captures the sincerity and vulnerability beneath her teasing exterior. However, complications arise: [[Demetrio Macías]], drunk on mezcal, loudly declares "I miss Camilla" (015-002), revealing his own desire for her and introducing a tension that echoes through the remaining Part I chapters.

## Key Passages

> "Look here, Tenderfoot, I want to tell you something," Camilla called to him in an impetuous and yet tender voice. (011-001)

Camilla's declaration initiates the central romantic plot of Part I, her boldness contrasting with her subsequent shyness.

> Camilla had never seen Luis Cervantes laugh so heartily. Utterly at a loss, Camilla could not answer. (011-041–011-043)

The growing intimacy between the educated outsider and the village girl is rendered through her disorientation — she is undone by feelings she cannot name.

> "Liar!" Camilla said, her face transfigured with joy. (014-062)

The accusation and the joy coexist in a single moment, revealing the sincerity of her love despite her defensive teasing.

> "I miss Camilla," said Demetrio in a loud voice. Everybody looked about for Camilla. (015-002–015-003)

[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s drunken confession introduces sexual rivalry into the plot and underscores Camilla's symbolic role as the object of desire that war cannot fulfill.

> "Do you remember Camilla?" Demetrio asked with a sigh. (019-038)

[[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s nostalgic question in Part II marks Camilla's absence from the narrative — she is remembered, not present, her fate unresolved.

## Role in the Novel

Camilla represents the domestic and romantic possibilities that the Revolution both creates and destroys. Her courtship with [[luis-cervantes|Luis Cervantes]] is one of the few sustained romantic subplots in the novel, offering a glimpse of tenderness and intimacy in a world of violence. Yet the relationship is shadowed by the war's disruptions — Luis's education and ambition separate him from her world, and [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s unspoken desire introduces a darker current. In Part II, Camilla is no longer present; she survives only in [[demetrio-macias|Demetrio]]'s nostalgic question, becoming an emblem of what the Revolution leaves behind. Her arc — from blushing introduction to vanished memory — mirrors the novel's broader theme of loss and disillusionment.
