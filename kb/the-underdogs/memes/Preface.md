[P02-001]Mariano Azuela, the first of the "novelists of the Revolution," was

[P02-002]born in Lagos de Moreno, Jalisco, Mexico, in 1873. He studied medicine

[P02-003]in Guadalajara and returned to Lagos in 1909, where he began the

[P02-004]practice of his profession. He began his writing career early; in 1896

[P02-005]he published Impressions of a Student in a weekly of Mexico City. This

[P02-006]was followed by numerous sketches and short stories, and in 1911 by his

[P02-007]first novel, Andres Perez, maderista.

[P02-008]Like most of the young Liberals, he supported Francisco I. Madero's

[P02-009]uprising, which overthrew the dictatorship of Porfirio Diaz, and in

[P02-010]1911 was made Director of Education of the State of Jalisco. After

[P02-011]Madero's assassination, he joined the army of Pancho Villa as doctor,

[P02-012]and his knowledge of the Revolution was acquired at firsthand. When the

[P02-013]counterrevolutionary forces of Victoriano Huerta were temporarily

[P02-014]triumphant, he emigrated to El Paso, Texas, where in 1915 he wrote The

[P02-015]Underdogs (Los de abajo), which did not receive general recognition

[P02-016]until 1924, when it was hailed as the novel of the Revolution.

[P02-017]But Azuela was fundamentally a moralist, and his disappointment with

[P02-018]the Revolution soon began to manifest itself. He had fought for a

[P02-019]better Mexico; but he saw that while the Revolution had corrected

[P02-020]certain injustices, it had given rise to others equally deplorable.

[P02-021]When he saw the self-servers and the unprincipled turning his hopes for

[P02-022]the redemption of the under-privileged of his country into a ladder to

[P02-023]serve their own ends, his disillusionment was deep and often bitter.

[P02-024]His later novels are marred at times by a savage sarcasm.

[P02-025]During his later years, and until his death in 1952, he lived in Mexico

[P02-026]City writing and practicing his profession among the poor.


#### Original Title: LOS DE ABAJO
