[006-001]Through the shadows of the starry night, Luis Cervantes had not yet

[006-002]managed to detect the exact shape of the objects about him. Seeking the

[006-003]most suitable resting-place, he laid his weary bones down on a fresh

[006-004]pile of manure under the blurred mass of a huizache tree. He lay down,

[006-005]more exhausted than resigned, and closed his eyes, resolutely

[006-006]determined to sleep until his fierce keepers or the morning sun,

[006-007]burning his ears, awakened him. Something vaguely like warmth at his

[006-008]side, then a tired hoarse breath, made him shudder. He opened his eyes

[006-009]and feeling about him with his hands, he sensed the coarse hairs of a

[006-010]large pig which, resenting the presence of a neighbor, began to grunt.

[006-011]All Luis' efforts to sleep proved quite useless, not only because the

[006-012]pain of his wound or the bruises on his flesh smarted, but because he

[006-013]suddenly realized the exact nature of his failure.

[006-014]Yes, failure! For he had never learned to appreciate exactly the

[006-015]difference between fulminating sentences of death upon bandits in the

[006-016]columns of a small country newspaper and actually setting out in search

[006-017]of them, and tracking them to their lairs, gun in hand. During his

[006-018]first day's march as volunteer lieutenant, he had begun to suspect the

[006-019]error of his ways--a brutal sixty miles' journey it was, that left his

[006-020]hips and legs one mass of raw soreness and soldered all his bones

[006-021]together. A week later, after his first skirmish against the rebels, he

[006-022]understood every rule of the game. Luis Cervantes would have taken up a

[006-023]crucifix and solemnly sworn that as soon as the soldiers, gun in hand,

[006-024]stood ready to shoot, some profoundly eloquent voice had spoken behind

[006-025]them, saying, "Run for your lives." It was all crystal clear. Even his

[006-026]noble-spirited horse, accustomed to battle, sought to sweep back on its

[006-027]hind legs and gallop furiously away, to stop only at a safe distance

[006-028]from the sound of firing. The sun was setting, the mountain became

[006-029]peopled with vague and restless shadows, darkness scaled the ramparts

[006-030]of the mountain hastily. What could be more logical then, than to seek

[006-031]refuge behind the rocks and attempt to sleep, granting mind and body a

[006-032]sorely needed rest?

[006-033]But the soldier's logic is the logic of absurdity. On the morrow, for

[006-034]example, his colonel awakened him rudely out of his sleep, cuffing and

[006-035]belaboring him unmercifully, and, after having bashed in his face,

[006-036]deprived him of his place of vantage. The rest of the officers,

[006-037]moreover, burst into hilarious mirth and holding their sides with

[006-038]laughter begged the colonel to pardon the deserter. The colonel,

[006-039]therefore, instead of sentencing him to be shot, kicked his buttocks

[006-040]roundly for him and assigned him to kitchen police.

[006-041]This signal insult was destined to bear poisonous fruit. Luis Cervantes

[006-042]determined to play turncoat; indeed, mentally, he had already changed

[006-043]sides. Did not the sufferings of the underdogs, of the disinherited

[006-044]masses, move him to the core? Henceforth he espoused the cause of

[006-045]Demos, of the subjugated, the beaten and baffled, who implore justice,

[006-046]and justice alone. He became intimate with the humblest private. More,

[006-047]even, he shed tears of compassion over a dead mule which fell, load and

[006-048]all, after a terribly long journey.

[006-049]From then on, Luis Cervantes' prestige with the soldiers increased.

[006-050]Some actually dared to make confessions. One among them, conspicuous

[006-051]for his sobriety and silence, told him: "I'm a carpenter by trade, you

[006-052]know. I had a mother, an old woman nailed to her chair for ten years by

[006-053]rheumatism. In the middle of the night, they pulled me out of my house;

[006-054]three damn policemen; I woke up a soldier twenty-five miles away from

[006-055]my hometown. A month ago our company passed by there again. My mother

[006-056]was already under the sod! ... So there's nothing left for me in this

[006-057]wide world; no one misses me now, you see. But, by God, I'm damned if

[006-058]I'll use these cartridges they make us carry, against the enemy. If a

[006-059]miracle happens (I pray for it every night, you know, and I guess our

[006-060]Lady of Guadalupe can do it all right), then I'll join Villa's men; and

[006-061]I swear by the holy soul of my old mother, that I'll make every one of

[006-062]these Government people pay, by God I will."

[006-063]Another soldier, a bright young fellow, but a charlatan, at heart, who

[006-064]drank habitually and smoked the narcotic marihuana weed, eyeing him

[006-065]with vague, glassy stare, whispered in his ear, "You know, partner ...

[006-066]the men on the other side ... you know, the other side ... you

[006-067]understand ... they ride the best horses up north there, and all over,

[006-068]see? And they harness their mounts with pure hammered silver. But us?

[006-069]Oh hell, we've got to ride plugs, that's all, and not one of them good

[006-070]enough to stagger round a water well. You see, don't you, partner? You

[006-071]see what I mean? You know, the men on the other side-they get shiny new

[006-072]silver coins while we get only lousy paper money printed in that

[006-073]murderer's factory, that's what we get, yes, that's ours, I tell you!"

[006-074]The majority of the soldiers spoke in much the same tenor. Even a top

[006-075]sergeant candidly confessed, "Yes, I enlisted all right. I wanted to.

[006-076]But, by God, I missed the right side by a long shot. What you can't

[006-077]make in a lifetime, sweating like a mule and breaking your back in

[006-078]peacetime, damn it all, you can make in a few months just running

[006-079]around the sierra with a gun on your back, but not with this crowd,

[006-080]dearie, not with this lousy outfit ...."

[006-081]Luis Cervantes, who already shared this hidden, implacably mortal

[006-082]hatred of the upper classes, of his officers, and of his superiors,

[006-083]felt that a veil had been removed from his eyes; clearly, now, he saw

[006-084]the final outcome of the struggle. And yet what had happened? The first

[006-085]moment he was able to join his coreligionists, instead of welcoming him

[006-086]with open arms, they threw him into a pigsty with swine for company.

[006-087]Day broke. The roosters crowed in the huts. The chickens perched in the

[006-088]huizache began to stretch their wings, shake their feathers, and fly

[006-089]down to the ground.

[006-090]Luis Cervantes saw his guards lying on top of a dung heap, snoring. In

[006-091]his imagination, he reviewed the features of last night's men. One,

[006-092]Pancracio, was pockmarked, blotchy, unshaven; his chin protruded, his

[006-093]forehead receded obliquely; his ears formed one solid piece with head

[006-094]and neck--a horrible man. The other, Manteca, was so much human refuse;

[006-095]his eyes were almost hidden, his look sullen; his wiry straight hair

[006-096]fen over his ears, forehead and neck; his scrofulous lips hung

[006-097]eternally agape. Once more, Luis Cervantes felt his flesh quiver.
