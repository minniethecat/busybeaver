## Overview

Beneath the institutional Catholicism that [[religion-in-the-revolution|Religion in the Revolution]] traces — stolen robes, fake confessions, a hanged priest — runs a deeper and more pervasive current of folk belief. This is the world of omens, curses, blessings, herbal remedies, and the devil as an everyday presence. It belongs not to the Church but to the peasantry: to Remigia's kitchen, to the card games on the riverbank, to the curses that fly in battle and the blessings murmured over wounds.

Where institutional religion appears in the novel as hollow form — robes without faith, churches without worshippers — folk belief appears as lived practice. Characters do not *believe* in curses and blessings; they *inhabit* a world where curses and blessings are as real as bullets and corn. Azuela's naturalism makes no distinction: a herbal remedy and a prayer are both technologies for surviving in a violent world.

## Curses and the Language of Misfortune

The language of cursing pervades the novel, but it is more than profanity — it is a folk taxonomy of misfortune. Maria Antonia "got the curse today and her belly's aching something fierce" (009-033–034) — the curse is not a metaphor but a diagnosis, as specific and material as a fever. When a soldier shouts "You cursed fool, you've maimed me for life" (005-019), the word "cursed" carries the weight of fate: the maiming is not accident but a visitation.

Demetrio himself, in the novel's final moments, breaks into cursing: "A curse broke from Demetrio's parched throat" (042-029). The curse is the last word the revolution offers — not a prayer, not an ideal, but a curse spat into indifferent stone.

## Blessings and Protection

Blessings form the counterpoint to curses. "God's blessing on you! He will find a way" (004-046), a woman tells Demetrio's men. When Remigia says "God and his Holy Mother, and Joseph" (009-074), she is not performing piety but deploying protection — the sacred names are a shield, spoken as automatically as one might raise a hand against a blow.

This pattern extends to departures and farewells. "God bless you all and keep you well" (015-011) is the ritual formula of parting — spoken not as theological conviction but as the only available insurance against a world in which men leave and do not return. The blessing is folk technology: it costs nothing and might work.

## The Devil as Everyday Presence

The devil appears throughout the novel not as theological adversary but as a familiar figure of peasant speech — an intensifier, an explanation, a name for forces beyond control. "What the devil are you waiting for, fool?" (011-053) is not demonology but idiom, yet the idiom reveals a worldview: the devil is always nearby, always a possible explanation for why things go wrong.

When a woman is described as "this she-devil" who "always beats" another (024-060), the term hovers between insult and diagnosis — she wins because she has something diabolic about her. The devil is not believed in; the devil is simply *there*, part of the furniture of the peasant cosmos, as real and as unremarkable as the sierra.

## Folk Medicine and Herbal Knowledge

Remigia's kitchen in Chapter IX is a site of folk medical practice. Neighbors come asking for "laurel leaves" to make "a potion for Maria Antonia who's not so well today" (009-022–024). When Remigia has none, she directs them to Dolores who "always got herbs" (009-041). The knowledge is collective and distributed — one woman knows this remedy, another knows that. The wounded Demetrio lies on his stretcher while around him the women deploy herbs, gossip, blessings, and curses — an entire parallel medical system operating beside (and in place of) the professional doctor.

This folk pharmacopeia is as much a part of the novel's naturalism as the clinical description of wounds. Azuela the physician observes the herbalist with the same eye he turns on the surgeon: both are practitioners, both work with what is at hand, both succeed or fail within limits set by material reality.
