## Overview

Mariano Azuela was a doctor. He "studied medicine" (P01-003) in Guadalajara and "joined the army of Pancho Villa as doctor" (P01-012). That biographical fact is not incidental to *The Underdogs* — it is structural. The novel's clinical gaze, its unsentimental registration of bodies in pain, its precise descriptions of wounds and their treatment, all belong to a writer trained to see flesh as material. Medicine in this novel is not a theme; it is a way of seeing.

The novel embeds medicine at every level: in its author's biography, in its central character (Luis Cervantes the medical student), in its folk healers (Venancio the "medicine man," Remigia the curandera), and in its most sustained plot thread (Demetrio's wounded leg and its slow healing across eight chapters). Medicine is the counter-force to violence — the attempt to repair what violence has broken — and the novel is unsparing about the gap between the two.

## The Doctor-Author: Azuela's Clinical Gaze

Azuela's medical training is legible in every chapter. His descriptions of wounds are anatomical: "a little above the wound" (004-028), "the wound in water, covered the leg with clean rags" (010-026), "it's like a red Castilian rose" (011-013). The simile "red Castilian rose" is both poetic and diagnostic — a doctor noting the color of healing tissue. When Azuela writes "the monotonous, rhythmical moan of the wounded rose in unison" (004-040), he is describing a sound he heard in field hospitals.

This clinical distance is the novel's ethical stance. Azuela does not sentimentalize the wounded; he describes them. The body in pain is a fact before it is a symbol. The doctor's detachment — the ability to look at a wound without flinching — becomes the novelist's detachment, the refusal to moralize what can only be observed.

## Luis Cervantes: The Doctor Who Never Practices

Luis Cervantes is introduced as a medical student — "So you was going to be a doctor, was you?" (008-011) — and his medical identity is central to his role in Demetrio's band. But Cervantes never practices medicine in any sustained way. He examines Demetrio's wound (010-022), he is called "Doctor" by the men (010-071), but his medical knowledge is more a social credential than a practical contribution.

The irony is deliberate. Cervantes, the educated man who could heal, instead becomes a political operator and eventually abandons the revolution for the United States. Medicine in his hands is wasted potential — like the revolution itself, it promises repair but delivers only further rupture. When Venancio accuses Cervantes of incompetence — "What kind of doctor do you think you are? You're no doctor at all" (010-008–009) — the charge is accurate. Cervantes's medical training is another thing the revolution has stripped of meaning.

## Venancio and Folk Medicine

Against Cervantes's failed professionalism stands Venancio, the self-taught healer who "fulfilled the office of medicine man" (004-032). Venancio has no formal training but he has experience: "I've a heap of experience already" (007-022). His knowledge is practical, acquired through treating wounds in the field, and the men trust him more than they trust Cervantes.

The tension between folk medicine and professional medicine runs through the novel. "They've got their own doctor, so I suppose—" (009-019) — the ellipsis captures the skepticism. Venancio's remedies — "I brought this medicine for the gentleman here" (009-059) — are met with equal skepticism by those who know professional medicine: "that's no medicine anyhow, it's just plain water" (009-061). The revolution is a space where both systems of knowledge are simultaneously present and neither is fully adequate.

Remigia extends the novel's medical spectrum into the supernatural. Her folk healing blends with religious ritual and exorcism, treating the body and the soul as a single field of affliction. The novel does not mock this; it registers it as part of the world it describes, as real to its practitioners as surgery is to the surgeon.

## Demetrio's Wound: The Body as Narrative Spine

Demetrio Macías's wounded leg is the novel's most sustained medical thread, running from Chapter IV — "Demetrio complained bitterly of his wound" (004-023) — to Chapter XII — "Demetrio's wound had already healed" (012-001). The wound is both physical and narrative: it immobilizes Demetrio, forces him into dependence on others, and provides the occasion for the medical subplot that introduces Cervantes's dual role as doctor and political aspirant.

The healing process is described with clinical precision. Luis Cervantes "uncovered Demetrio's wound, examined it carefully" (010-022), then "washed the wound in water, covered the leg with clean rags" (010-026). The care is competent but minimal — this is battlefield medicine, not hospital care. The wound heals because Demetrio is strong, not because the medicine is good.

The novel's final wound is mortal. "Of that mortal wound" (034-008) — the phrase signals the end of medicine's power. Some wounds cannot be healed, and the revolution itself is one of them.

## Key Passages

> He studied medicine in Guadalajara and received his degree in 1899. [P01-003]

*The biographical foundation — Azuela's medical training is the source of the novel's clinical gaze.*

> Venancio, who knew the plains of the earth, fulfilled the office of medicine man. He was accorded an unimpeachable authority. [004-032]

*Folk medicine as authority — Venancio's practical knowledge commands more respect than Cervantes's formal training.*

> "What kind of doctor do you think you are? You're no doctor at all. I'll wager you've already forgotten everything they taught you." [010-008–009]

*The accusation against Cervantes — professional medicine emptied of meaning by the revolution.*

> He uncovered Demetrio's wound, examined it carefully, and shook his head. Then he washed the wound in water, covered the leg with clean rags. [010-022–026]

*Clinical precision — Azuela's doctor-eye describing battlefield care with anatomical accuracy.*

> "Look, how nicely it's healing now: it's like a red Castilian rose." [011-013]

*The wound described as a flower — poetic and diagnostic at once, the doctor noting the color of healing tissue.*

> Demetrio's wound had already healed. They began to discuss various plans. [012-001]

*Healing as narrative turning-point — the body repaired, the revolution resumes.*

> Of that mortal wound. [034-008]

*The limit of medicine — some wounds cannot be healed, and the revolution itself is one of them.*
