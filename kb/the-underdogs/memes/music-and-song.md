## Overview

Music in *The Underdogs* is not entertainment — it is survival. Demetrio's men sing "monotonous, sad songs" as they march (002-085); Camilla begs Luis Cervantes to teach her the revolutionary ballad "Adelita" so she can remember him (011-023–027); eight musicians play wind instruments as the revolution celebrates its victories (029-023); and the novel ends with string musicians playing all day at a funeral — "the most solemn honors were paid" (038-014). Music threads through the novel from its first chapter to its last, not as a decorative motif but as the primary medium through which peasant-soldiers express what they cannot say in prose.

The corrido — the Mexican narrative ballad tradition — is the unstated model. Azuela's novel itself has been read as a prose corrido: episodic, built around a charismatic but doomed hero, narrating the revolution in the voice of those who lived it. The characters sing because singing is how their culture records and transmits experience.

## Demetrio's Opening Song

The novel's first musical moment comes early, and it establishes the pattern: Demetrio, speaking of a woman, breaks into song — "By God!" he said, breaking off into song" (001-048). The song interrupts speech at the moment when speech is insufficient. Whatever Demetrio feels about this woman, it cannot be stated; it must be sung. The move from words to music is the move from communication to expression — and for Azuela's peasants, expression is always musical.

## The March and the Sad Songs

As Demetrio's men move through the sierra, they sing: "They sang monotonous, sad songs, uttering a strident shout" (002-085). The adjective "monotonous" captures both the style and the function — these are not performance pieces but work-songs, march-songs, songs that regulate breathing and pace across long distances. The "strident shout" is the emotional release that the song's monotony contains. Singing is what men do when walking toward death.

The landscape itself sings back. "Birds sang lyrically from their hiding among the pitaya trees" while "the song of the locust in the brush" (002-017, 003-006) provides the continuous undersong of the natural world. Human music and natural music are continuous — both are sound raised against silence.

## "Adelita" and Love Across Distance

The novel's most extended treatment of song comes through Camilla and the revolutionary ballad "Adelita." Camilla, infatuated with Luis Cervantes, begs him: "Please go over the words of the revolutionary song 'Adelita' with me, will you? ... I want to sing it and sing it, over again often and often, see? Then when you're off and away and when you've forgotten all about Camilla, it'll remind me of you" (011-023–027).

The song here functions as memory-prosthesis. Camilla knows Cervantes will leave — all soldiers leave — and the song is what will remain. "Adelita" was the most famous song of the Mexican Revolution, a love ballad addressed to a soldadera (woman soldier), and Camilla's identification with it places her in a lineage of women who love across the distance that war creates. Cervantes, however, experiences her words as "the noise of a sharp steel knife" (011-028) — the educated man hears noise where the peasant woman hears melody.

## Music as Celebration and Mourning

In the novel's later chapters, music accompanies the revolution's social rituals. Quail suggests "that musicians be summoned from the neighborhood" (014-020) for a celebration. As the revolution achieves victories, "eight musicians, playing wind instruments" (029-023) provide the soundtrack. And in the novel's final scenes, "Demetrio awoke to the strains of music" (038-007) — music has become the background of his life, always present, whether he is celebrating or dying.

The guitar emerges as the novel's central instrument. A character is noted to "play the guitar quite well" (036-023); Demetrio himself "asked the musicians for a guitar" (038-035); and in town, young girls gather around "a guitar" (040-020). The guitar is portable, democratic, and intimate — the instrument of the campfire and the cantina, not the church or the concert hall. It belongs to the revolution's culture as surely as the rifle belongs to its violence.

The novel's final music is funeral music: "The string musicians played all day; the most solemn honors were paid" (038-014). Music that once accompanied marching and celebration now accompanies burial. The arc is complete: from Demetrio's opening song to the funeral strings, music has carried the revolution from beginning to end.

*The march-song — monotony as function, the shout as release.*

*Camilla and "Adelita" — song as memory-prosthesis, the ballad that outlasts the lover.*

*Music marks the revolution's victories — celebration as collective ritual.*

*Music as the constant background of revolutionary life — waking, celebrating, dying.*

> The string musicians played all day; the most solemn honors were paid. [038-014]

*Funeral music — the novel's last music, closing the arc from opening song to final rites.*
