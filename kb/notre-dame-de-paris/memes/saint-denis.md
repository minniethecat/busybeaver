# Porte Saint-Denis

The Porte Saint-Denis was one of the six principal gates in the wall
of Charles V that encircled [[paris|medieval Paris]] on the Right Bank. It stood
at the northern limit of the [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]], the great commercial
artery of the Right Bank, and controlled access to the road leading to
the Abbey of Saint-Denis, the royal necropolis. In Hugo's novel, the
gate and the street bearing its name are key coordinates in the
architectural geography of [[paris|Paris]].

## The Gate in the Novel

Hugo enumerates the Porte Saint-Denis among the principal gates of
[[paris|Paris]]'s Right Bank wall: "the [[porte-saint-antoine|Porte Saint-Antoine]], the Porte du Temple,
the Porte Saint-Martin, the Porte Saint-Denis, the Porte Montmartre,
the Porte Saint-Honoré. All these gates were strong, and also handsome,
which does not detract from strength" [020-192]. This passage
characterizes the gates as both military and aesthetic achievements —
a duality that runs through Hugo's architectural philosophy.

The gate also appears in passing in descriptions of [[paris|medieval Paris]]ian
topography. The wall of Charles V "culminated at the gates of
Saint-Denis and Saint-Martin, whose situation has not been changed"
[020-192]. This enduring geographical feature anchors Hugo's medieval
city to the [[paris|Paris]] his readers would recognize.

## Rue Saint-Denis

The [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]], leading from the Right Bank markets northward to
the gate, was one of [[paris|medieval Paris]]'s most important streets. Mentioned
alongside the Rue de la Harpe on the Left Bank and the Rue de la
Barillerié on the island, the [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]] was a commercial
lifeline: "Rue de la Harpe on the left bank, Rue de la Barillerié in
the island, [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]] on the right bank" [020-192]. These three
streets formed the spine of commercial [[paris|Paris]], connecting [[paris|the city]]'s
three divisions.

## The Abbey of Saint-Denis

The road from the Porte Saint-Denis led north to the royal Abbey of
Saint-Denis, the burial place of French kings. Hugo mentions
"Saint-Denis" in this ecclesiastical sense alongside [[saint-germain-des-pres|Saint-Germain]] des
Prés, praising its [[religion|church]]'s [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] and placing it within the
Romanesque zone of [[paris|Paris]]ian [[religion|church]] design: "its pillars in that Roman
zone, in which are plunged the portal of Saint-Denis, and the nave of
[[saint-germain-des-pres|Saint-Germain]] des Prés" [019-217].

The abbey also appears in the litany of ecclesiastical dignitaries at
the [[festival-of-fools|Feast of Fools]]: "Robert de Lespinasse, Abbé of [[saint-germain-des-pres|Saint-Germain]] des
Prés, that libertine brother of a mistress of [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]" — the listing
of abbots from Saint-Denis and [[saint-germain-des-pres|Saint-Germain]] des Prés emphasizes the
interweaving of sacred and profane that characterizes the novel's
portrait of medieval institutions [006-079].

## Hugo's Medieval Fortifications

The Porte Saint-Denis belongs to Hugo's larger treatment of [[paris|Paris]]ian
fortifications. The wall of Charles V, with its "large, deep moat, with
a brisk current during the high water of winter, bathed the base of the
wall round [[paris|Paris]]; the Seine furnished the water" [020-192]. These
fortifications represent the medieval city's physical boundaries and
its capacity for defense — themes that resonate with the novel's
emphasis on enclosure, [[sanctuary]], and the tension between protection
and imprisonment.
