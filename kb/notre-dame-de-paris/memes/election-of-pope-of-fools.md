# The Election of the Pope of Fools

The Election of the Pope of Fools is the first great set-piece of the
novel, a riot of popular energy that fills the great hall of the Palais
de Justice and establishes the carnivalesque spirit that will, in
various forms, haunt the entire narrative. On January 6, 1482 — the
double feast of Epiphany and the [[festival-of-fools|Feast of Fools]] — the crowd gathers
for a double spectacle: the performance of [[pierre-gringoire]]'s
mystery play and the traditional election of the ugliest man to serve
as mock-pontiff for the day. The mystery play fails; the election
succeeds, and [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]] is acclaimed [[quasimodo|Pope of Fools]].

## The Festive Context

The [[festival-of-fools|Feast of Fools]] was a medieval tradition in which the normal
hierarchies of Church and society were ritually inverted. A mock-pope
or mock-bishop was elected, often from among the lower clergy or even
the laity, and presided over a day of licensed disorder — drinking,
dancing, blasphemous parodies of the liturgy, and general mayhem.
Hugo's depiction draws on the historical practice while heightening
its grotesque energy to the level of symbolic drama.

## The Event

### The Contest

The election takes the form of a grimace contest: the candidates
thrust their faces through a broken window-pane in the great hall,
and the crowd judges which is the ugliest. The faces are described
by Hugo in a virtuoso passage of grotesque description — faces that
"blaze with an inhuman light," faces that "make the crowd recoil
in delighted horror." One by one they appear and are rejected, — until
a face of such transcendent [[grotesque|ugliness]] appears that the crowd
unanimously acclaims it the winner: "the grimace was his face" (009-004).

### Quasimodo's Coronation

The face is [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]]'s — Hugo writes that "his whole person was a grimace" (009-005), and the crowd acclaims him as the winner. He is "the pope whom the fools had just chosen" (009-014). The crowd
lifts him onto a litter, places a cardboard tiara on his head and a
mock-crozier in his hand, and parades him through the streets of [[paris|Paris]] — "the tiara, and the derisive robe of the Pope of the Fools" (009-094).
[[quasimodo|Quasimodo]], who has never known anything but rejection and fear,
experiences for the first and only time the intoxication of being
celebrated. His grotesque face, so long a source of pain, becomes
the instrument of a fleeting, ironic triumph.

### The Crowd

Hugo's crowd is not a passive audience but the protagonist of the
scene. Its energy, its cruelty, its gen[[love-and-desire|eros]]ity, its fickleness — all
are rendered with a novelist's precision and a historian's
detachment. The crowd that acclaims [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]] as [[quasimodo|Pope of Fools]] will,
in later scenes, jeer at him on [[quasimodos-pillory|the pillory]] and, later still, storm
Notre-Dame itself. The election scene introduces the crowd as a
character in its own right — collective, volatile, and unstoppable.

## Symbolism

### Carnival and Hierarchy

The Election of the Pope of Fools is Hugo's concentrated image of the
medieval carnival spirit — the ritualized inversion of hierarchy that,
paradoxically, reinforces hierarchy by providing a licensed outlet for
resentment. [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]], the lowest of the low, is made king for a day,
and the world is turned upside down. But the inversion is temporary:
tomorrow the bishop will be bishop again, and [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]] will return to
his bells.

### Beauty and the Beast

The election establishes the novel's central aesthetic opposition.
[[quasimodo|Quasimodo]]'s face, judged purely by the standard of [[grotesque|ugliness]], wins a
contest. Yet this very [[grotesque|ugliness]] is, in Hugo's rendering, a kind of
grandeur — a "sublime of the grotesque" that rivals conventional
[[beauty]] in its power. The scene prepares the reader for the novel's
great theme: that [[beauty|the beautiful]] may be hollow ([[phoebus-de-chateaupers|Phoebus]]) and the
grotesque may be noble ([[quasimodo|Quasimodo]]).
