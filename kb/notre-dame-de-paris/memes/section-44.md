---[044-001] The gypsy was dancing; she was twirling her tambourine on the tip

[044-002] of her finger, and tossing it into the air as she danced

[044-003] Provençal sarabands; agile, light, joyous, and unconscious of the

[044-004] formidable gaze which descended perpendicularly upon her head.

[044-005] The crowd was swarming around her; from time to time, a man

[044-006] accoutred in red and yellow made them form into a circle, and

[044-007] then returned, seated himself on a chair a few paces from the

[044-008] dancer, and took the goat’s head on his knees. This man seemed to

[044-009] be the gypsy’s companion. Claude Frollo could not distinguish his

[044-010] features from his elevated post.

[044-011] From the moment when the archdeacon caught sight of this

[044-012] stranger, his attention seemed divided between him and the

[044-013] dancer, and his face became more and more gloomy. All at once he

[044-014] rose upright, and a quiver ran through his whole body: “Who is

[044-015] that man?” he muttered between his teeth: “I have always seen her

[044-016] alone before!”

[044-017] Then he plunged down beneath the tortuous vault of the spiral

[044-018] staircase, and once more descended. As he passed the door of the

[044-019] bell chamber, which was ajar, he saw something which struck him;

[044-020] he beheld Quasimodo, who, leaning through an opening of one of

[044-021] those slate penthouses which resemble enormous blinds, appeared

[044-022] also to be gazing at the Place. He was engaged in so profound a

[044-023] contemplation, that he did not notice the passage of his adopted

[044-024] father. His savage eye had a singular expression; it was a

[044-025] charmed, tender look. “This is strange!” murmured Claude. “Is it

[044-026] the gypsy at whom he is thus gazing?” He continued his descent.

[044-027] At the end of a few minutes, the anxious archdeacon entered upon

[044-028] the Place from the door at the base of the tower.

[044-029] “What has become of the gypsy girl?” he said, mingling with the

[044-030] group of spectators which the sound of the tambourine had

[044-031] collected.

[044-032] “I know not,” replied one of his neighbors, “I think that she has

[044-033] gone to make some of her fandangoes in the house opposite,

[044-034] whither they have called her.”

[044-035] In the place of the gypsy, on the carpet, whose arabesques had

[044-036] seemed to vanish but a moment previously by the capricious

[044-037] figures of her dance, the archdeacon no longer beheld any one but

[044-038] the red and yellow man, who, in order to earn a few testers in

[044-039] his turn, was walking round the circle, with his elbows on his

[044-040] hips, his head thrown back, his face red, his neck outstretched,

[044-041] with a chair between his teeth. To the chair he had fastened a

[044-042] cat, which a neighbor had lent, and which was spitting in great

[044-043] affright.

[044-044] “Notre-Dame!” exclaimed the archdeacon, at the moment when the

[044-045] juggler, perspiring heavily, passed in front of him with his

[044-046] pyramid of chair and his cat, “What is Master Pierre Gringoire

[044-047] doing here?”

[044-048] The harsh voice of the archdeacon threw the poor fellow into such

[044-049] a commotion that he lost his equilibrium, together with his whole

[044-050] edifice, and the chair and the cat tumbled pell-mell upon the

[044-051] heads of the spectators, in the midst of inextinguishable

[044-052] hootings.

[044-053] It is probable that Master Pierre Gringoire (for it was indeed

[044-054] he) would have had a sorry account to settle with the neighbor

[044-055] who owned the cat, and all the bruised and scratched faces which

[044-056] surrounded him, if he had not hastened to profit by the tumult to

[044-057] take refuge in the church, whither Claude Frollo had made him a

[044-058] sign to follow him.

[044-059] The cathedral was already dark and deserted; the side-aisles were

[044-060] full of shadows, and the lamps of the chapels began to shine out

[044-061] like stars, so black had the vaulted ceiling become. Only the

[044-062] great rose window of the façade, whose thousand colors were

[044-063] steeped in a ray of horizontal sunlight, glittered in the gloom

[044-064] like a mass of diamonds, and threw its dazzling reflection to the

[044-065] other end of the nave.

[044-066] When they had advanced a few paces, Dom Claude placed his back

[044-067] against a pillar, and gazed intently at Gringoire. The gaze was

[044-068] not the one which Gringoire feared, ashamed as he was of having

[044-069] been caught by a grave and learned person in the costume of a

[044-070] buffoon. There was nothing mocking or ironical in the priest’s

[044-071] glance, it was serious, tranquil, piercing. The archdeacon was

[044-072] the first to break the silence.

[044-073] “Come now, Master Pierre. You are to explain many things to me.

[044-074] And first of all, how comes it that you have not been seen for

[044-075] two months, and that now one finds you in the public squares, in

[044-076] a fine equipment in truth! Motley red and yellow, like a Caudebec

[044-077] apple?”

[044-078] “Messire,” said Gringoire, piteously, “it is, in fact, an amazing

[044-079] accoutrement. You see me no more comfortable in it than a cat

[044-080] coiffed with a calabash. ’Tis very ill done, I am conscious, to

[044-081] expose messieurs the sergeants of the watch to the liability of

[044-082] cudgelling beneath this cassock the humerus of a Pythagorean

[044-083] philosopher. But what would you have, my reverend master? ’tis

[044-084] the fault of my ancient jerkin, which abandoned me in cowardly

[044-085] wise, at the beginning of the winter, under the pretext that it

[044-086] was falling into tatters, and that it required repose in the

[044-087] basket of a rag-picker. What is one to do? Civilization has not

[044-088] yet arrived at the point where one can go stark naked, as ancient

[044-089] Diogenes wished. Add that a very cold wind was blowing, and ’tis

[044-090] not in the month of January that one can successfully attempt to

[044-091] make humanity take this new step. This garment presented itself,

[044-092] I took it, and I left my ancient black smock, which, for a

[044-093] hermetic like myself, was far from being hermetically closed.

[044-094] Behold me then, in the garments of a stage-player, like Saint

[044-095] Genest. What would you have? ’tis an eclipse. Apollo himself

[044-096] tended the flocks of Admetus.”

[044-097] “’Tis a fine profession that you are engaged in!” replied the

[044-098] archdeacon.

[044-099] “I agree, my master, that ’tis better to philosophize and

[044-100] poetize, to blow the flame in the furnace, or to receive it from

[044-101] carry cats on a shield. So, when you addressed me, I was as

[044-102] foolish as an ass before a turnspit. But what would you have,

[044-103] messire? One must eat every day, and the finest Alexandrine

[044-104] verses are not worth a bit of Brie cheese. Now, I made for Madame

[044-105] Marguerite of Flanders, that famous epithalamium, as you know,

[044-106] and the city will not pay me, under the pretext that it was not

[044-107] excellent; as though one could give a tragedy of Sophocles for

[044-108] four crowns! Hence, I was on the point of dying with hunger.

[044-109] Happily, I found that I was rather strong in the jaw; so I said

[044-110] to this jaw,—perform some feats of strength and of equilibrium:

[044-111] nourish thyself. _Ale te ipsam_. A pack of beggars who have

[044-112] become my good friends, have taught me twenty sorts of herculean

[044-113] feats, and now I give to my teeth every evening the bread which

[044-114] they have earned during the day by the sweat of my brow. After

[044-115] all, _concedo_, I grant that it is a sad employment for my

[044-116] intellectual faculties, and that man is not made to pass his life

[044-117] in beating the tambourine and biting chairs. But, reverend

[044-118] master, it is not sufficient to pass one’s life, one must earn

[044-119] the means for life.”

[044-120] Dom Claude listened in silence. All at once his deep-set eye

[044-121] assumed so sagacious and penetrating an expression, that

[044-122] Gringoire felt himself, so to speak, searched to the bottom of

[044-123] the soul by that glance.

[044-124] “Very good, Master Pierre; but how comes it that you are now in

[044-125] company with that gypsy dancer?”

[044-126] “In faith!” said Gringoire, “’tis because she is my wife and I am

[044-127] her husband.”

[044-128] The priest’s gloomy eyes flashed into flame.

[044-129] “Have you done that, you wretch!” he cried, seizing Gringoire’s

[044-130] arm with fury; “have you been so abandoned by God as to raise

[044-131] your hand against that girl?”

[044-132] “On my chance of paradise, monseigneur,” replied Gringoire,

[044-133] trembling in every limb, “I swear to you that I have never

[044-134] touched her, if that is what disturbs you.”

[044-135] “Then why do you talk of husband and wife?” said the priest.

[044-136] Gringoire made haste to relate to him as succinctly as possible,

[044-137] all that the reader already knows, his adventure in the Court of

[044-138] Miracles and the broken-crock marriage. It appeared, moreover,

[044-139] that this marriage had led to no results whatever, and that each

[044-140] evening the gypsy girl cheated him of his nuptial right as on the

[044-141] first day. “’Tis a mortification,” he said in conclusion, “but

[044-142] that is because I have had the misfortune to wed a virgin.”

[044-143] “What do you mean?” demanded the archdeacon, who had been

[044-144] gradually appeased by this recital.

[044-145] “’Tis very difficult to explain,” replied the poet. “It is a

[044-146] superstition. My wife is, according to what an old thief, who is

[044-147] called among us the Duke of Egypt, has told me, a foundling or a

[044-148] lost child, which is the same thing. She wears on her neck an

[044-149] amulet which, it is affirmed, will cause her to meet her parents

[044-150] some day, but which will lose its virtue if the young girl loses

[044-151] hers. Hence it follows that both of us remain very virtuous.”

[044-152] “So,” resumed Claude, whose brow cleared more and more, “you

[044-153] believe, Master Pierre, that this creature has not been

[044-154] approached by any man?”

[044-155] “What would you have a man do, Dom Claude, as against a

[044-156] superstition? She has got that in her head. I assuredly esteem as

[044-157] a rarity this nunlike prudery which is preserved untamed amid

[044-158] those Bohemian girls who are so easily brought into subjection.

[044-159] But she has three things to protect her: the Duke of Egypt, who

[044-160] has taken her under his safeguard, reckoning, perchance, on

[044-161] selling her to some gay abbé; all his tribe, who hold her in

[044-162] singular veneration, like a Notre-Dame; and a certain tiny

[044-163] poignard, which the buxom dame always wears about her, in some

[044-164] nook, in spite of the ordinances of the provost, and which one

[044-165] causes to fly out into her hands by squeezing her waist. ’Tis a

[044-166] proud wasp, I can tell you!”

[044-167] The archdeacon pressed Gringoire with questions.

[044-168] La Esmeralda, in the judgment of Gringoire, was an inoffensive

[044-169] and charming creature, pretty, with the exception of a pout which

[044-170] was peculiar to her; a naïve and passionate damsel, ignorant of

[044-171] everything and enthusiastic about everything; not yet aware of

[044-172] the difference between a man and a woman, even in her dreams;

[044-173] made like that; wild especially over dancing, noise, the open

[044-174] air; a sort of woman bee, with invisible wings on her feet, and

[044-175] living in a whirlwind. She owed this nature to the wandering life

[044-176] which she had always led. Gringoire had succeeded in learning

[044-177] that, while a mere child, she had traversed Spain and Catalonia,

[044-178] even to Sicily; he believed that she had even been taken by the

[044-179] caravan of Zingari, of which she formed a part, to the kingdom of

[044-180] Algiers, a country situated in Achaia, which country adjoins, on

[044-181] one side Albania and Greece; on the other, the Sicilian Sea,

[044-182] which is the road to Constantinople. The Bohemians, said

[044-183] Gringoire, were vassals of the King of Algiers, in his quality of

[044-184] chief of the White Moors. One thing is certain, that la Esmeralda

[044-185] had come to France while still very young, by way of Hungary.

[044-186] From all these countries the young girl had brought back

[044-187] fragments of queer jargons, songs, and strange ideas, which made

[044-188] her language as motley as her costume, half Parisian, half

[044-189] African. However, the people of the quarters which she frequented

[044-190] loved her for her gayety, her daintiness, her lively manners, her

[044-191] dances, and her songs. She believed herself to be hated, in all

[044-192] the city, by but two persons, of whom she often spoke in terror:

[044-193] the sacked nun of the Tour-Roland, a villanous recluse who

[044-194] cherished some secret grudge against these gypsies, and who

[044-195] cursed the poor dancer every time that the latter passed before

[044-196] her window; and a priest, who never met her without casting at

[044-197] her looks and words which frightened her.
