# Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie

Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie was a medieval church in the butchers' quarter
on the Right Bank of Paris. Hugo mentions its tower briefly in Notre-Dame de
Paris, anchoring the geography of the novel's climactic scenes in the actual
architecture of 15th-century Paris.

## Historical Context (15th Century)

The church of Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie was built in the Gothic style and
served the wealthy butchers' guild of the Les Halles district. Its bell tower
— the Tour Saint-Jacques, which still stands today — was a prominent landmark
on the Right Bank, visible from the towers of Notre-Dame. The church was
demolished after the Revolution, but the tower remains as a Parisian monument.

## Role in the Novel

The tower of Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie appears in the final chapters of
the novel, when scaffolding visible on its tower marks the ongoing construction
that was a constant feature of medieval Paris. The reference situates the
novel's tragic ending within a living, changing city — Paris building itself
even as its inhabitants meet their fates ([089-164]).
