---[030-001] “I have studied hermetic science!” exclaimed Coictier, “and I

[030-002] affirm—”

[030-003] The fiery archdeacon did not allow him to finish: “And I have

[030-004] studied medicine, astrology, and hermetics. Here alone is the

[030-005] truth.” (As he spoke thus, he took from the top of the coffer a

[030-006] phial filled with the powder which we have mentioned above),

[030-007] “here alone is light! Hippocrates is a dream; Urania is a dream;

[030-008] Hermes, a thought. Gold is the sun; to make gold is to be God.

[030-009] Herein lies the one and only science. I have sounded the depths

[030-010] of medicine and astrology, I tell you! Naught, nothingness! The

[030-011] human body, shadows! the planets, shadows!”

[030-012] And he fell back in his armchair in a commanding and inspired

[030-013] attitude. Gossip Touraugeau watched him in silence. Coictier

[030-014] tried to grin, shrugged his shoulders imperceptibly, and repeated

[030-015] in a low voice,—

[030-016] “A madman!”

[030-017] “And,” said Tourangeau suddenly, “the wondrous result,—have you

[030-018] attained it, have you made gold?”

[030-019] “If I had made it,” replied the archdeacon, articulating his

[030-020] words slowly, like a man who is reflecting, “the king of France

[030-021] would be named Claude and not Louis.”

[030-022] The stranger frowned.

[030-023] “What am I saying?” resumed Dom Claude, with a smile of disdain.

[030-024] “What would the throne of France be to me when I could rebuild

[030-025] the empire of the Orient?”

[030-026] “Very good!” said the stranger.

[030-027] “Oh, the poor fool!” murmured Coictier.

[030-028] The archdeacon went on, appearing to reply now only to his

[030-029] thoughts,—

[030-030] “But no, I am still crawling; I am scratching my face and knees

[030-031] against the pebbles of the subterranean pathway. I catch a

[030-032] glimpse, I do not contemplate! I do not read, I spell out!”

[030-033] “And when you know how to read!” demanded the stranger, “will you

[030-034] make gold?”

[030-035] “Who doubts it?” said the archdeacon.

[030-036] “In that case Our Lady knows that I am greatly in need of money,

[030-037] and I should much desire to read in your books. Tell me, reverend

[030-038] master, is your science inimical or displeasing to Our Lady?”

[030-039] “Whose archdeacon I am?” Dom Claude contented himself with

[030-040] replying, with tranquil hauteur.

[030-041] “That is true, my master. Well! will it please you to initiate

[030-042] me? Let me spell with you.”

[030-043] Claude assumed the majestic and pontifical attitude of a Samuel.

[030-044] “Old man, it requires longer years than remain to you, to

[030-045] undertake this voyage across mysterious things. Your head is very

[030-046] gray! One comes forth from the cavern only with white hair, but

[030-047] only those with dark hair enter it. Science alone knows well how

[030-048] to hollow, wither, and dry up human faces; she needs not to have

[030-049] old age bring her faces already furrowed. Nevertheless, if the

[030-050] desire possesses you of putting yourself under discipline at your

[030-051] age, and of deciphering the formidable alphabet of the sages,

[030-052] come to me; ’tis well, I will make the effort. I will not tell

[030-053] you, poor old man, to go and visit the sepulchral chambers of the

[030-054] pyramids, of which ancient Herodotus speaks, nor the brick tower

[030-055] of Babylon, nor the immense white marble sanctuary of the Indian

[030-056] temple of Eklinga. I, no more than yourself, have seen the

[030-057] Chaldean masonry works constructed according to the sacred form

[030-058] of the Sikra, nor the temple of Solomon, which is destroyed, nor

[030-059] the stone doors of the sepulchre of the kings of Israel, which

[030-060] are broken. We will content ourselves with the fragments of the

[030-061] book of Hermes which we have here. I will explain to you the

[030-062] statue of Saint Christopher, the symbol of the sower, and that of

[030-063] the two angels which are on the front of the Sainte-Chapelle, and

[030-064] one of which holds in his hands a vase, the other, a cloud—”

[030-065] Here Jacques Coictier, who had been unhorsed by the archdeacon’s

[030-066] impetuous replies, regained his saddle, and interrupted him with

[030-067] the triumphant tone of one learned man correcting

[030-068] another,—“_Erras amice Claudi_. The symbol is not the number. You

[030-069] take Orpheus for Hermes.”

[030-070] “’Tis you who are in error,” replied the archdeacon, gravely.

[030-071] “Dædalus is the base; Orpheus is the wall; Hermes is the

[030-072] edifice,—that is all. You shall come when you will,” he

[030-073] continued, turning to Tourangeau, “I will show you the little

[030-074] parcels of gold which remained at the bottom of Nicholas Flamel’s

[030-075] alembic, and you shall compare them with the gold of Guillaume de

[030-076] Paris. I will teach you the secret virtues of the Greek word,

[030-077] _peristera_. But, first of all, I will make you read, one after

[030-078] the other, the marble letters of the alphabet, the granite pages

[030-079] of the book. We shall go to the portal of Bishop Guillaume and of

[030-080] Saint-Jean le Rond at the Sainte-Chapelle, then to the house of

[030-081] Nicholas Flamel, Rue Manvault, to his tomb, which is at the

[030-082] Saints-Innocents, to his two hospitals, Rue de Montmorency. I

[030-083] will make you read the hieroglyphics which cover the four great

[030-084] iron cramps on the portal of the hospital Saint-Gervais, and of

[030-085] the Rue de la Ferronnerie. We will spell out in company, also,

[030-086] the façade of Saint-Côme, of Sainte-Geneviève-des-Ardents, of

[030-087] Saint Martin, of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie—.”

[030-088] For a long time, Gossip Tourangeau, intelligent as was his

[030-089] glance, had appeared not to understand Dom Claude. He

[030-090] interrupted.

[030-091] “_Pasque-dieu_! what are your books, then?”

[030-092] “Here is one of them,” said the archdeacon.

[030-093] And opening the window of his cell he pointed out with his finger

[030-094] the immense church of Notre-Dame, which, outlining against the

[030-095] starry sky the black silhouette of its two towers, its stone

[030-096] flanks, its monstrous haunches, seemed an enormous two-headed

[030-097] sphinx, seated in the middle of the city.

[030-098] The archdeacon gazed at the gigantic edifice for some time in

[030-099] silence, then extending his right hand, with a sigh, towards the

[030-100] printed book which lay open on the table, and his left towards

[030-101] Notre-Dame, and turning a sad glance from the book to the

[030-102] church,—“Alas,” he said, “this will kill that.”

[030-103] Coictier, who had eagerly approached the book, could not repress

[030-104] an exclamation. “Hé, but now, what is there so formidable in

[030-105] this: ‘GLOSSA IN EPISTOLAS D. PAULI, _Norimbergæ, Antonius

[030-106] Koburger_, 1474.’ This is not new. ’Tis a book of Pierre Lombard,

[030-107] the Master of Sentences. Is it because it is printed?”

[030-108] “You have said it,” replied Claude, who seemed absorbed in a

[030-109] profound meditation, and stood resting, his forefinger bent

[030-110] backward on the folio which had come from the famous press of

[030-111] Nuremberg. Then he added these mysterious words: “Alas! alas!

[030-112] small things come at the end of great things; a tooth triumphs

[030-113] over a mass. The Nile rat kills the crocodile, the swordfish

[030-114] kills the whale, the book will kill the edifice.”

[030-115] The curfew of the cloister sounded at the moment when Master

[030-116] Jacques was repeating to his companion in low tones, his eternal

[030-117] refrain, “_He is mad!_” To which his companion this time replied,

[030-118] “I believe that he is.”

[030-119] It was the hour when no stranger could remain in the cloister.

[030-120] The two visitors withdrew. “Master,” said Gossip Tourangeau, as

[030-121] he took leave of the archdeacon, “I love wise men and great

[030-122] minds, and I hold you in singular esteem. Come to-morrow to the

[030-123] Palace des Tournelles, and inquire for the Abbé de Sainte-Martin,

[030-124] of Tours.”

[030-125] The archdeacon returned to his chamber dumbfounded, comprehending

[030-126] at last who Gossip Tourangeau was, and recalling that passage of

[030-127] the register of Sainte-Martin, of Tours:—_Abbas beati Martini,

[030-128] SCILICET REX FRANCIÆ, est canonicus de consuetudine et habet

[030-129] parvam præbendam quam habet sanctus Venantius, et debet sedere in

[030-130] sede thesaurarii_.

[030-131] It is asserted that after that epoch the archdeacon had frequent

[030-132] conferences with Louis XI., when his majesty came to Paris, and

[030-133] that Dom Claude’s influence quite overshadowed that of Olivier le

[030-134] Daim and Jacques Coictier, who, as was his habit, rudely took the

[030-135] king to task on that account.

[030-136] CHAPTER II. THIS WILL KILL THAT.

[030-137] Our lady readers will pardon us if we pause for a moment to seek

[030-138] what could have been the thought concealed beneath those

[030-139] enigmatic words of the archdeacon: “This will kill that. The book

[030-140] will kill the edifice.”

[030-141] To our mind, this thought had two faces. In the first place, it

[030-142] was a priestly thought. It was the affright of the priest in the

[030-143] presence of a new agent, the printing press. It was the terror

[030-144] and dazzled amazement of the men of the sanctuary, in the

[030-145] presence of the luminous press of Gutenberg. It was the pulpit

[030-146] and the manuscript taking the alarm at the printed word:

[030-147] something similar to the stupor of a sparrow which should behold

[030-148] the angel Legion unfold his six million wings. It was the cry of

[030-149] the prophet who already hears emancipated humanity roaring and

[030-150] swarming; who beholds in the future, intelligence sapping faith,

[030-151] opinion dethroning belief, the world shaking off Rome. It was the

[030-152] prognostication of the philosopher who sees human thought,

[030-153] volatilized by the press, evaporating from the theocratic

[030-154] recipient. It was the terror of the soldier who examines the

[030-155] brazen battering ram, and says:—“The tower will crumble.” It

[030-156] signified that one power was about to succeed another power. It

[030-157] meant, “The press will kill the church.”

[030-158] But underlying this thought, the first and most simple one, no

[030-159] doubt, there was in our opinion another, newer one, a corollary

[030-160] of the first, less easy to perceive and more easy to contest, a

[030-161] view as philosophical and belonging no longer to the priest alone

[030-162] but to the savant and the artist. It was a presentiment that

[030-163] human thought, in changing its form, was about to change its mode

[030-164] of expression; that the dominant idea of each generation would no

[030-165] longer be written with the same matter, and in the same manner;

[030-166] that the book of stone, so solid and so durable, was about to

[030-167] make way for the book of paper, more solid and still more

[030-168] durable. In this connection the archdeacon’s vague formula had a

[030-169] second sense. It meant, “Printing will kill architecture.”

[030-170] In fact, from the origin of things down to the fifteenth century

[030-171] of the Christian era, inclusive, architecture is the great book

[030-172] of humanity, the principal expression of man in his different

[030-173] stages of development, either as a force or as an intelligence.

[030-174] When the memory of the first races felt itself overloaded, when

[030-175] the mass of reminiscences of the human race became so heavy and

[030-176] so confused that speech naked and flying, ran the risk of losing

[030-177] them on the way, men transcribed them on the soil in a manner

[030-178] which was at once the most visible, most durable, and most

[030-179] natural. They sealed each tradition beneath a monument.

[030-180] The first monuments were simple masses of rock, “which the iron

[030-181] had not touched,” as Moses says. Architecture began like all

[030-182] writing. It was first an alphabet. Men planted a stone upright,

[030-183] it was a letter, and each letter was a hieroglyph, and upon each

[030-184] hieroglyph rested a group of ideas, like the capital on the

[030-185] column. This is what the earliest races did everywhere, at the

[030-186] same moment, on the surface of the entire world. We find the

[030-187] “standing stones” of the Celts in Asian Siberia; in the pampas of

[030-188] America.

[030-189] Later on, they made words; they placed stone upon stone, they

[030-190] coupled those syllables of granite, and attempted some

[030-191] combinations. The Celtic dolmen and cromlech, the Etruscan

[030-192] tumulus, the Hebrew galgal, are words. Some, especially the

[030-193] tumulus, are proper names. Sometimes even, when men had a great

[030-194] deal of stone, and a vast plain, they wrote a phrase. The immense

[030-195] pile of Karnac is a complete sentence.

[030-196] At last they made books. Traditions had brought forth symbols,

[030-197] beneath which they disappeared like the trunk of a tree beneath

[030-198] its foliage; all these symbols in which humanity placed faith

[030-199] continued to grow, to multiply, to intersect, to become more and

[030-200] more complicated; the first monuments no longer sufficed to

[030-201] contain them, they were overflowing in every part; these

[030-202] monuments hardly expressed now the primitive tradition, simple

[030-203] like themselves, naked and prone upon the earth. The symbol felt

[030-204] the need of expansion in the edifice. Then architecture was

[030-205] developed in proportion with human thought; it became a giant

[030-206] with a thousand heads and a thousand arms, and fixed all this

[030-207] floating symbolism in an eternal, visible, palpable form. While

[030-208] Dædalus, who is force, measured; while Orpheus, who is

[030-209] intelligence, sang;—the pillar, which is a letter; the arcade,

[030-210] which is a syllable; the pyramid, which is a word,—all set in

[030-211] movement at once by a law of geometry and by a law of poetry,

[030-212] grouped themselves, combined, amalgamated, descended, ascended,

[030-213] placed themselves side by side on the soil, ranged themselves in

[030-214] stories in the sky, until they had written under the dictation of

[030-215] the general idea of an epoch, those marvellous books which were

[030-216] also marvellous edifices: the Pagoda of Eklinga, the Rhamseion of

[030-217] Egypt, the Temple of Solomon.
