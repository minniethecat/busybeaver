[001-002] NOTRE-DAME DE PARIS

[001-003] Also known as:

[001-004] _THE HUNCHBACK OF NOTRE DAME_

[001-005] By Victor Hugo

[001-006] Translated by Isabel F. Hapgood

[001-007] PREFACE.

[001-008] A few years ago, while visiting or, rather, rummaging about

[001-009] Notre-Dame, the author of this book found, in an obscure nook of

[001-010] one of the towers, the following word, engraved by hand upon the

[001-011] wall:—

[001-012] ἈΝÁΓΚΗ.

[001-013] These Greek capitals, black with age, and quite deeply graven in

[001-014] the stone, with I know not what signs peculiar to Gothic

[001-015] caligraphy imprinted upon their forms and upon their attitudes,

[001-016] as though with the purpose of revealing that it had been a hand

[001-017] of the Middle Ages which had inscribed them there, and especially

[001-018] the fatal and melancholy meaning contained in them, struck the

[001-019] author deeply.

[001-020] He questioned himself; he sought to divine who could have been

[001-021] that soul in torment which had not been willing to quit this

[001-022] world without leaving this stigma of crime or unhappiness upon

[001-023] the brow of the ancient church.

[001-024] Afterwards, the wall was whitewashed or scraped down, I know not

[001-025] which, and the inscription disappeared. For it is thus that

[001-026] people have been in the habit of proceeding with the marvellous

[001-027] churches of the Middle Ages for the last two hundred years.

[001-028] Mutilations come to them from every quarter, from within as well

[001-029] as from without. The priest whitewashes them, the archdeacon

[001-030] scrapes them down; then the populace arrives and demolishes them.

[001-031] Thus, with the exception of the fragile memory which the author

[001-032] of this book here consecrates to it, there remains to-day nothing

[001-033] whatever of the mysterious word engraved within the gloomy tower

[001-034] of Notre-Dame,—nothing of the destiny which it so sadly summed

[001-035] up. The man who wrote that word upon the wall disappeared from

[001-036] the midst of the generations of man many centuries ago; the word,

[001-037] in its turn, has been effaced from the wall of the church; the

[001-038] church will, perhaps, itself soon disappear from the face of the

[001-039] earth.

[001-040] It is upon this word that this book is founded.

[001-041] March, 1831.

[001-127] BOOK FIRST.

[001-128] CHAPTER I. THE GRAND HALL.

[001-129] Three hundred and forty-eight years, six months, and nineteen

[001-130] days ago to-day, the Parisians awoke to the sound of all the

[001-131] bells in the triple circuit of the city, the university, and the

[001-132] town ringing a full peal.

[001-133] The sixth of January, 1482, is not, however, a day of which

[001-134] history has preserved the memory. There was nothing notable in

[001-135] the event which thus set the bells and the _bourgeois_ of Paris

[001-136] in a ferment from early morning. It was neither an assault by the

[001-137] Picards nor the Burgundians, nor a hunt led along in procession,

[001-138] nor a revolt of scholars in the town of Laas, nor an entry of

[001-139] “our much dread lord, monsieur the king,” nor even a pretty

[001-140] hanging of male and female thieves by the courts of Paris.

[001-141] Neither was it the arrival, so frequent in the fifteenth century,

[001-142] of some plumed and bedizened embassy. It was barely two days

[001-143] since the last cavalcade of that nature, that of the Flemish

[001-144] ambassadors charged with concluding the marriage between the

[001-145] dauphin and Marguerite of Flanders, had made its entry into

[001-146] Paris, to the great annoyance of M. le Cardinal de Bourbon, who,

[001-147] for the sake of pleasing the king, had been obliged to assume an

[001-148] amiable mien towards this whole rustic rabble of Flemish

[001-149] burgomasters, and to regale them at his Hôtel de Bourbon, with a

[001-150] very “pretty morality, allegorical satire, and farce,” while a

[001-151] driving rain drenched the magnificent tapestries at his door.

[001-152] What put the “whole population of Paris in commotion,” as Jehan

[001-153] de Troyes expresses it, on the sixth of January, was the double

[001-154] solemnity, united from time immemorial, of the Epiphany and the

[001-155] Feast of Fools.

[001-156] On that day, there was to be a bonfire on the Place de Grève, a

[001-157] maypole at the Chapelle de Braque, and a mystery at the Palais de

[001-158] Justice. It had been cried, to the sound of the trumpet, the

[001-159] preceding evening at all the cross roads, by the provost’s men,

[001-160] clad in handsome, short, sleeveless coats of violet camelot, with

[001-161] large white crosses upon their breasts.

[001-162] So the crowd of citizens, male and female, having closed their

[001-163] houses and shops, thronged from every direction, at early morn,

[001-164] towards some one of the three spots designated.

[001-165] Each had made his choice; one, the bonfire; another, the maypole;

[001-166] another, the mystery play. It must be stated, in honor of the

[001-167] good sense of the loungers of Paris, that the greater part of

[001-168] this crowd directed their steps towards the bonfire, which was

[001-169] quite in season, or towards the mystery play, which was to be

[001-170] presented in the grand hall of the Palais de Justice (the courts

[001-171] of law), which was well roofed and walled; and that the curious

[001-172] left the poor, scantily flowered maypole to shiver all alone

[001-173] beneath the sky of January, in the cemetery of the Chapel of

[001-174] Braque.

[001-175] The populace thronged the avenues of the law courts in

[001-176] particular, because they knew that the Flemish ambassadors, who

[001-177] had arrived two days previously, intended to be present at the

[001-178] representation of the mystery, and at the election of the Pope of

[001-179] the Fools, which was also to take place in the grand hall.

[001-180] It was no easy matter on that day, to force one’s way into that

[001-181] grand hall, although it was then reputed to be the largest

[001-182] covered enclosure in the world (it is true that Sauval had not

[001-183] yet measured the grand hall of the Château of Montargis). The

[001-184] palace place, encumbered with people, offered to the curious

[001-185] gazers at the windows the aspect of a sea; into which five or six

[001-186] streets, like so many mouths of rivers, discharged every moment

[001-187] fresh floods of heads. The waves of this crowd, augmented

[001-188] incessantly, dashed against the angles of the houses which

[001-189] projected here and there, like so many promontories, into the

[001-190] irregular basin of the place. In the centre of the lofty

[001-191] Gothic[1] façade of the palace, the grand staircase, incessantly

[001-192] ascended and descended by a double current, which, after parting

[001-193] on the intermediate landing-place, flowed in broad waves along

[001-194] its lateral slopes,—the grand staircase, I say, trickled

[001-195] incessantly into the place, like a cascade into a lake. The

[001-196] cries, the laughter, the trampling of those thousands of feet,

[001-197] produced a great noise and a great clamor. From time to time,

[001-198] this noise and clamor redoubled; the current which drove the

[001-199] crowd towards the grand staircase flowed backwards, became

[001-200] troubled, formed whirlpools. This was produced by the buffet of

[001-201] an archer, or the horse of one of the provost’s sergeants, which

[001-202] kicked to restore order; an admirable tradition which the

[001-203] provostship has bequeathed to the constablery, the constablery to

[001-204] the _maréchaussée_, the _maréchaussée_ to our _gendarmeri_ of

[001-205] Paris.

[001-206] Thousands of good, calm, _bourgeois_ faces thronged the windows,

[001-207] the doors, the dormer windows, the roofs, gazing at the palace,

[001-208] gazing at the populace, and asking nothing more; for many

[001-209] Parisians content themselves with the spectacle of the

[001-210] spectators, and a wall behind which something is going on becomes

[001-211] at once, for us, a very curious thing indeed.

[001-212] If it could be granted to us, the men of 1830, to mingle in

[001-213] thought with those Parisians of the fifteenth century, and to

[001-214] enter with them, jostled, elbowed, pulled about, into that

[001-215] immense hall of the palace, which was so cramped on that sixth of

[001-216] January, 1482, the spectacle would not be devoid of either

[001-217] interest or charm, and we should have about us only things that

[001-218] were so old that they would seem new.

[001-219] With the reader’s consent, we will endeavor to retrace in

[001-220] thought, the impression which he would have experienced in

[001-221] company with us on crossing the threshold of that grand hall, in

[001-222] the midst of that tumultuous crowd in surcoats, short, sleeveless

[001-223] jackets, and doublets.

[001-224] And, first of all, there is a buzzing in the ears, a dazzlement

[001-225] in the eyes. Above our heads is a double ogive vault, panelled

[001-226] with wood carving, painted azure, and sown with golden

[001-227] fleurs-de-lis; beneath our feet a pavement of black and white

[001-228] marble, alternating. A few paces distant, an enormous pillar,

[001-229] then another, then another; seven pillars in all, down the length

[001-230] of the hall, sustaining the spring of the arches of the double

[001-231] vault, in the centre of its width. Around four of the pillars,

[001-232] stalls of merchants, all sparkling with glass and tinsel; around

[001-233] the last three, benches of oak, worn and polished by the trunk

[001-234] hose of the litigants, and the robes of the attorneys. Around the

[001-235] hall, along the lofty wall, between the doors, between the

[001-236] windows, between the pillars, the interminable row of all the

[001-237] kings of France, from Pharamond down: the lazy kings, with

[001-238] pendent arms and downcast eyes; the valiant and combative kings,

[001-239] with heads and arms raised boldly heavenward. Then in the long,

[001-240] pointed windows, glass of a thousand hues; at the wide entrances

[001-241] to the hall, rich doors, finely sculptured; and all, the vaults,

[001-242] pillars, walls, jambs, panelling, doors, statues, covered from

[001-243] top to bottom with a splendid blue and gold illumination, which,

[001-244] a trifle tarnished at the epoch when we behold it, had almost

[001-245] entirely disappeared beneath dust and spiders in the year of

[001-246] grace, 1549, when du Breul still admired it from tradition.

[001-247] Let the reader picture to himself now, this immense, oblong hall,

[001-248] illuminated by the pallid light of a January day, invaded by a

[001-249] motley and noisy throng which drifts along the walls, and eddies

[001-250] round the seven pillars, and he will have a confused idea of the

[001-251] whole effect of the picture, whose curious details we shall make

[001-252] an effort to indicate with more precision.
