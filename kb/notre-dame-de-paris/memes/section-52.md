---[052-001] “Ah! do not weep for such a trifle, my graceful maid! ’tis a name

[052-002] to which one must get accustomed, that is all. When I once know

[052-003] it by heart, all will go smoothly. Listen then, my dear Similar;

[052-004] I adore you passionately. I love you so that ’tis simply

[052-005] miraculous. I know a girl who is bursting with rage over it—”

[052-006] The jealous girl interrupted him: “Who?”

[052-007] “What matters that to us?” said Phœbus; “do you love me?”

[052-008] “Oh!”—said she.

[052-009] “Well! that is all. You shall see how I love you also. May the

[052-010] great devil Neptunus spear me if I do not make you the happiest

[052-011] woman in the world. We will have a pretty little house somewhere.

[052-012] I will make my archers parade before your windows. They are all

[052-013] mounted, and set at defiance those of Captain Mignon. There are

[052-014] _voulgiers, cranequiniers_ and hand _couleveiniers_[44]. I will

[052-015] take you to the great sights of the Parisians at the storehouse

[052-016] of Rully. Eighty thousand armed men, thirty thousand white

[052-017] harnesses, short coats or coats of mail; the sixty-seven banners

[052-018] of the trades; the standards of the parliaments, of the chamber

[052-019] of accounts, of the treasury of the generals, of the aides of the

[052-020] mint; a devilish fine array, in short! I will conduct you to see

[052-021] the lions of the Hôtel du Roi, which are wild beasts. All women

[052-022] love that.”

[052-023] For several moments the young girl, absorbed in her charming

[052-024] thoughts, was dreaming to the sound of his voice, without

[052-025] listening to the sense of his words.

[052-026] “Oh! how happy you will be!” continued the captain, and at the

[052-027] same time he gently unbuckled the gypsy’s girdle.

[052-028] “What are you doing?” she said quickly. This “act of violence”

[052-029] had roused her from her revery.

[052-030] “Nothing,” replied Phœbus, “I was only saying that you must

[052-031] abandon all this garb of folly, and the street corner when you

[052-032] are with me.”

[052-033] “When I am with you, Phœbus!” said the young girl tenderly.

[052-034] She became pensive and silent once more.

[052-035] The captain, emboldened by her gentleness, clasped her waist

[052-036] without resistance; then began softly to unlace the poor child’s

[052-037] corsage, and disarranged her tucker to such an extent that the

[052-038] panting priest beheld the gypsy’s beautiful shoulder emerge from

[052-039] the gauze, as round and brown as the moon rising through the

[052-040] mists of the horizon.

[052-041] The young girl allowed Phœbus to have his way. She did not appear

[052-042] to perceive it. The eye of the bold captain flashed.

[052-043] Suddenly she turned towards him,—

[052-044] “Phœbus,” she said, with an expression of infinite love,

[052-045] “instruct me in thy religion.”

[052-046] “My religion!” exclaimed the captain, bursting with laughter, “I

[052-047] instruct you in my religion! _Corne et tonnerre!_ What do you

[052-048] want with my religion?”

[052-049] “In order that we may be married,” she replied.

[052-050] The captain’s face assumed an expression of mingled surprise and

[052-051] disdain, of carelessness and libertine passion.

[052-052] “Ah, bah!” said he, “do people marry?”

[052-053] The Bohemian turned pale, and her head drooped sadly on her

[052-054] breast.

[052-055] “My beautiful love,” resumed Phœbus, tenderly, “what nonsense is

[052-056] this? A great thing is marriage, truly! one is none the less

[052-057] loving for not having spit Latin into a priest’s shop!”

[052-058] While speaking thus in his softest voice, he approached extremely

[052-059] near the gypsy; his caressing hands resumed their place around

[052-060] her supple and delicate waist, his eye flashed more and more, and

[052-061] everything announced that Monsieur Phœbus was on the verge of one

[052-062] of those moments when Jupiter himself commits so many follies

[052-063] that Homer is obliged to summon a cloud to his rescue.

[052-064] But Dom Claude saw everything. The door was made of thoroughly

[052-065] rotten cask staves, which left large apertures for the passage of

[052-066] his hawklike gaze. This brown-skinned, broad-shouldered priest,

[052-067] hitherto condemned to the austere virginity of the cloister, was

[052-068] quivering and boiling in the presence of this night scene of love

[052-069] and voluptuousness. This young and beautiful girl given over in

[052-070] disarray to the ardent young man, made melted lead flow in

[052-071] his-veins; his eyes darted with sensual jealousy beneath all

[052-072] those loosened pins. Any one who could, at that moment, have seen

[052-073] the face of the unhappy man glued to the wormeaten bars, would

[052-074] have thought that he beheld the face of a tiger glaring from the

[052-075] depths of a cage at some jackal devouring a gazelle. His eye

[052-076] shone like a candle through the cracks of the door.

[052-077] All at once, Phœbus, with a rapid gesture, removed the gypsy’s

[052-078] gorgerette. The poor child, who had remained pale and dreamy,

[052-079] awoke with a start; she recoiled hastily from the enterprising

[052-080] officer, and, casting a glance at her bare neck and shoulders,

[052-081] red, confused, mute with shame, she crossed her two beautiful

[052-082] arms on her breast to conceal it. Had it not been for the flame

[052-083] which burned in her cheeks, at the sight of her so silent and

[052-084] motionless, one would have declared her a statue of Modesty. Her

[052-085] eyes were lowered.

[052-086] But the captain’s gesture had revealed the mysterious amulet

[052-087] which she wore about her neck.

[052-088] “What is that?” he said, seizing this pretext to approach once

[052-089] more the beautiful creature whom he had just alarmed.

[052-090] “Don’t touch it!” she replied, quickly, “’tis my guardian. It

[052-091] will make me find my family again, if I remain worthy to do so.

[052-092] Oh, leave me, monsieur le capitaine! My mother! My poor mother!

[052-093] My mother! Where art thou? Come to my rescue! Have pity, Monsieur

[052-094] Phœbus, give me back my gorgerette!”

[052-095] Phœbus retreated amid said in a cold tone,—

[052-096] “Oh, mademoiselle! I see plainly that you do not love me!”

[052-097] “I do not love him!” exclaimed the unhappy child, and at the same

[052-098] time she clung to the captain, whom she drew to a seat beside

[052-099] her. “I do not love thee, my Phœbus? What art thou saying, wicked

[052-100] man, to break my heart? Oh, take me! take all! do what you will

[052-101] with me, I am thine. What matters to me the amulet! What matters

[052-102] to me my mother! ’Tis thou who art my mother since I love thee!

[052-103] Phœbus, my beloved Phœbus, dost thou see me? ’Tis I. Look at me;

[052-104] ’tis the little one whom thou wilt surely not repulse, who comes,

[052-105] who comes herself to seek thee. My soul, my life, my body, my

[052-106] person, all is one thing—which is thine, my captain. Well, no! We

[052-107] will not marry, since that displeases thee; and then, what am I?

[052-108] a miserable girl of the gutters; whilst thou, my Phœbus, art a

[052-109] gentleman. A fine thing, truly! A dancer wed an officer! I was

[052-110] mad. No, Phœbus, no; I will be thy mistress, thy amusement, thy

[052-111] pleasure, when thou wilt; a girl who shall belong to thee. I was

[052-112] only made for that, soiled, despised, dishonored, but what

[052-113] matters it?—beloved. I shall be the proudest and the most joyous

[052-114] of women. And when I grow old or ugly, Phœbus, when I am no

[052-115] longer good to love you, you will suffer me to serve you still.

[052-116] Others will embroider scarfs for you; ’tis I, the servant, who

[052-117] will care for them. You will let me polish your spurs, brush your

[052-118] doublet, dust your riding-boots. You will have that pity, will

[052-119] you not, Phœbus? Meanwhile, take me! here, Phœbus, all this

[052-120] belongs to thee, only love me! We gypsies need only air and

[052-121] love.”

[052-122] So saying, she threw her arms round the officer’s neck; she

[052-123] looked up at him, supplicatingly, with a beautiful smile, and all

[052-124] in tears. Her delicate neck rubbed against his cloth doublet with

[052-125] its rough embroideries. She writhed on her knees, her beautiful

[052-126] body half naked. The intoxicated captain pressed his ardent lips

[052-127] to those lovely African shoulders. The young girl, her eyes bent

[052-128] on the ceiling, as she leaned backwards, quivered, all

[052-129] palpitating, beneath this kiss.

[052-130] All at once, above Phœbus’s head she beheld another head; a

[052-131] green, livid, convulsed face, with the look of a lost soul; near

[052-132] this face was a hand grasping a poniard. It was the face and hand

[052-133] of the priest; he had broken the door and he was there. Phœbus

[052-134] could not see him. The young girl remained motionless, frozen

[052-135] with terror, dumb, beneath that terrible apparition, like a dove

[052-136] which should raise its head at the moment when the hawk is gazing

[052-137] into her nest with its round eyes.

[052-138] She could not even utter a cry. She saw the poniard descend upon

[052-139] Phœbus, and rise again, reeking.

[052-140] “Maledictions!” said the captain, and fell.

[052-141] She fainted.

[052-142] At the moment when her eyes closed, when all feeling vanished in

[052-143] her, she thought that she felt a touch of fire imprinted upon her

[052-144] lips, a kiss more burning than the red-hot iron of the

[052-145] executioner.

[052-146] When she recovered her senses, she was surrounded by soldiers of

[052-147] the watch they were carrying away the captain, bathed in his

[052-148] blood the priest had disappeared; the window at the back of the

[052-149] room which opened on the river was wide open; they picked up a

[052-150] cloak which they supposed to belong to the officer and she heard

[052-151] them saying around her,

[052-152] “’Tis a sorceress who has stabbed a captain.”

[052-153] BOOK EIGHTH.

[052-154] CHAPTER I. THE CROWN CHANGED INTO A DRY LEAF.

[052-155] Gringoire and the entire Court of Miracles were suffering mortal

[052-156] anxiety. For a whole month they had not known what had become of

[052-157] la Esmeralda, which greatly pained the Duke of Egypt and his

[052-158] friends the vagabonds, nor what had become of the goat, which

[052-159] redoubled Gringoire’s grief. One evening the gypsy had

[052-160] disappeared, and since that time had given no signs of life. All

[052-161] search had proved fruitless. Some tormenting bootblacks had told

[052-162] Gringoire about meeting her that same evening near the Pont

[052-163] Saint-Michel, going off with an officer; but this husband, after

[052-164] the fashion of Bohemia, was an incredulous philosopher, and

[052-165] besides, he, better than any one else, knew to what a point his

[052-166] wife was virginal. He had been able to form a judgment as to the

[052-167] unconquerable modesty resulting from the combined virtues of the

[052-168] amulet and the gypsy, and he had mathematically calculated the

[052-169] resistance of that chastity to the second power. Accordingly, he

[052-170] was at ease on that score.

[052-171] Still he could not understand this disappearance. It was a

[052-172] profound sorrow. He would have grown thin over it, had that been

[052-173] possible. He had forgotten everything, even his literary tastes,

[052-174] even his great work, _De figuris regularibus et irregularibus_,

[052-175] which it was his intention to have printed with the first money

[052-176] which he should procure (for he had raved over printing, ever

[052-177] since he had seen the “Didascalon” of Hugues de Saint Victor,

[052-178] printed with the celebrated characters of Vindelin de Spire).

[052-179] One day, as he was passing sadly before the criminal Tournelle,

[052-180] he perceived a considerable crowd at one of the gates of the

[052-181] Palais de Justice.

[052-182] “What is this?” he inquired of a young man who was coming out.

[052-183] “I know not, sir,” replied the young man. “’Tis said that they

[052-184] are trying a woman who hath assassinated a gendarme. It appears

[052-185] that there is sorcery at the bottom of it, the archbishop and the

[052-186] official have intervened in the case, and my brother, who is the

[052-187] archdeacon of Josas, can think of nothing else. Now, I wished to

[052-188] speak with him, but I have not been able to reach him because of

[052-189] the throng, which vexes me greatly, as I stand in need of money.”

[052-190] “Alas! sir,” said Gringoire, “I would that I could lend you some,

[052-191] but, my breeches are worn to holes, and ’tis not crowns which

[052-192] have done it.”

[052-193] He dared not tell the young man that he was acquainted with his

[052-194] brother the archdeacon, to whom he had not returned after the

[052-195] scene in the church; a negligence which embarrassed him.

[052-196] The scholar went his way, and Gringoire set out to follow the

[052-197] crowd which was mounting the staircase of the great chamber. In

[052-198] his opinion, there was nothing like the spectacle of a criminal

[052-199] process for dissipating melancholy, so exhilaratingly stupid are

[052-200] judges as a rule. The populace which he had joined walked and

[052-201] elbowed in silence. After a slow and tiresome march through a

[052-202] long, gloomy corridor, which wound through the court-house like

[052-203] the intestinal canal of the ancient edifice, he arrived near a

[052-204] low door, opening upon a hall which his lofty stature permitted

[052-205] him to survey with a glance over the waving heads of the rabble.
