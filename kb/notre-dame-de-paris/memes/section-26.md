---[026-001] He baptized his adopted child, and gave him the name of

[026-002] Quasimodo, either because he desired thereby to mark the day,

[026-003] when he had found him, or because he wished to designate by that

[026-004] name to what a degree the poor little creature was incomplete,

[026-005] and hardly sketched out. In fact, Quasimodo, blind, hunchbacked,

[026-006] knock-kneed, was only an “almost.”

[026-007] CHAPTER III. _IMMANIS PECORIS CUSTOS, IMMANIOR IPSE_.

[026-008] Now, in 1482, Quasimodo had grown up. He had become a few years

[026-009] previously the bellringer of Notre-Dame, thanks to his father by

[026-010] adoption, Claude Frollo,—who had become archdeacon of Josas,

[026-011] thanks to his suzerain, Messire Louis de Beaumont,—who had become

[026-012] Bishop of Paris, at the death of Guillaume Chartier in 1472,

[026-013] thanks to his patron, Olivier Le Daim, barber to Louis XI., king

[026-014] by the grace of God.

[026-015] So Quasimodo was the ringer of the chimes of Notre-Dame.

[026-016] In the course of time there had been formed a certain peculiarly

[026-017] intimate bond which united the ringer to the church. Separated

[026-018] forever from the world, by the double fatality of his unknown

[026-019] birth and his natural deformity, imprisoned from his infancy in

[026-020] that impassable double circle, the poor wretch had grown used to

[026-021] seeing nothing in this world beyond the religious walls which had

[026-022] received him under their shadow. Notre-Dame had been to him

[026-023] successively, as he grew up and developed, the egg, the nest, the

[026-024] house, the country, the universe.

[026-025] There was certainly a sort of mysterious and pre-existing harmony

[026-026] between this creature and this church. When, still a little

[026-027] fellow, he had dragged himself tortuously and by jerks beneath

[026-028] the shadows of its vaults, he seemed, with his human face and his

[026-029] bestial limbs, the natural reptile of that humid and sombre

[026-030] pavement, upon which the shadow of the Romanesque capitals cast

[026-031] so many strange forms.

[026-032] Later on, the first time that he caught hold, mechanically, of

[026-033] the ropes to the towers, and hung suspended from them, and set

[026-034] the bell to clanging, it produced upon his adopted father,

[026-035] Claude, the effect of a child whose tongue is unloosed and who

[026-036] begins to speak.

[026-037] It is thus that, little by little, developing always in sympathy

[026-038] with the cathedral, living there, sleeping there, hardly ever

[026-039] leaving it, subject every hour to the mysterious impress, he came

[026-040] to resemble it, he incrusted himself in it, so to speak, and

[026-041] became an integral part of it. His salient angles fitted into the

[026-042] retreating angles of the cathedral (if we may be allowed this

[026-043] figure of speech), and he seemed not only its inhabitant but more

[026-044] than that, its natural tenant. One might almost say that he had

[026-045] assumed its form, as the snail takes on the form of its shell. It

[026-046] was his dwelling, his hole, his envelope. There existed between

[026-047] him and the old church so profound an instinctive sympathy, so

[026-048] many magnetic affinities, so many material affinities, that he

[026-049] adhered to it somewhat as a tortoise adheres to its shell. The

[026-050] rough and wrinkled cathedral was his shell.

[026-051] It is useless to warn the reader not to take literally all the

[026-052] similes which we are obliged to employ here to express the

[026-053] singular, symmetrical, direct, almost consubstantial union of a

[026-054] man and an edifice. It is equally unnecessary to state to what a

[026-055] degree that whole cathedral was familiar to him, after so long

[026-056] and so intimate a cohabitation. That dwelling was peculiar to

[026-057] him. It had no depths to which Quasimodo had not penetrated, no

[026-058] height which he had not scaled. He often climbed many stones up

[026-059] the front, aided solely by the uneven points of the carving. The

[026-060] towers, on whose exterior surface he was frequently seen

[026-061] clambering, like a lizard gliding along a perpendicular wall,

[026-062] those two gigantic twins, so lofty, so menacing, so formidable,

[026-063] possessed for him neither vertigo, nor terror, nor shocks of

[026-064] amazement.

[026-065] To see them so gentle under his hand, so easy to scale, one would

[026-066] have said that he had tamed them. By dint of leaping, climbing,

[026-067] gambolling amid the abysses of the gigantic cathedral he had

[026-068] become, in some sort, a monkey and a goat, like the Calabrian

[026-069] child who swims before he walks, and plays with the sea while

[026-070] still a babe.

[026-071] Moreover, it was not his body alone which seemed fashioned after

[026-072] the Cathedral, but his mind also. In what condition was that

[026-073] mind? What bent had it contracted, what form had it assumed

[026-074] beneath that knotted envelope, in that savage life? This it would

[026-075] be hard to determine. Quasimodo had been born one-eyed,

[026-076] hunchbacked, lame. It was with great difficulty, and by dint of

[026-077] great patience that Claude Frollo had succeeded in teaching him

[026-078] to talk. But a fatality was attached to the poor foundling.

[026-079] Bellringer of Notre-Dame at the age of fourteen, a new infirmity

[026-080] had come to complete his misfortunes: the bells had broken the

[026-081] drums of his ears; he had become deaf. The only gate which nature

[026-082] had left wide open for him had been abruptly closed, and forever.

[026-083] In closing, it had cut off the only ray of joy and of light which

[026-084] still made its way into the soul of Quasimodo. His soul fell into

[026-085] profound night. The wretched being’s misery became as incurable

[026-086] and as complete as his deformity. Let us add that his deafness

[026-087] rendered him to some extent dumb. For, in order not to make

[026-088] others laugh, the very moment that he found himself to be deaf,

[026-089] he resolved upon a silence which he only broke when he was alone.

[026-090] He voluntarily tied that tongue which Claude Frollo had taken so

[026-091] much pains to unloose. Hence, it came about, that when necessity

[026-092] constrained him to speak, his tongue was torpid, awkward, and

[026-093] like a door whose hinges have grown rusty.

[026-094] If now we were to try to penetrate to the soul of Quasimodo

[026-095] through that thick, hard rind; if we could sound the depths of

[026-096] that badly constructed organism; if it were granted to us to look

[026-097] with a torch behind those non-transparent organs to explore the

[026-098] shadowy interior of that opaque creature, to elucidate his

[026-099] obscure corners, his absurd no-thoroughfares, and suddenly to

[026-100] cast a vivid light upon the soul enchained at the extremity of

[026-101] that cave, we should, no doubt, find the unhappy Psyche in some

[026-102] poor, cramped, and ricketty attitude, like those prisoners

[026-103] beneath the Leads of Venice, who grew old bent double in a stone

[026-104] box which was both too low and too short for them.

[026-105] It is certain that the mind becomes atrophied in a defective

[026-106] body. Quasimodo was barely conscious of a soul cast in his own

[026-107] image, moving blindly within him. The impressions of objects

[026-108] underwent a considerable refraction before reaching his mind. His

[026-109] brain was a peculiar medium; the ideas which passed through it

[026-110] issued forth completely distorted. The reflection which resulted

[026-111] from this refraction was, necessarily, divergent and perverted.

[026-112] Hence a thousand optical illusions, a thousand aberrations of

[026-113] judgment, a thousand deviations, in which his thought strayed,

[026-114] now mad, now idiotic.

[026-115] The first effect of this fatal organization was to trouble the

[026-116] glance which he cast upon things. He received hardly any

[026-117] immediate perception of them. The external world seemed much

[026-118] farther away to him than it does to us.

[026-119] The second effect of his misfortune was to render him malicious.

[026-120] He was malicious, in fact, because he was savage; he was savage

[026-121] because he was ugly. There was logic in his nature, as there is

[026-122] in ours.

[026-123] His strength, so extraordinarily developed, was a cause of still

[026-124] greater malevolence: “_Malus puer robustus_,” says Hobbes.

[026-125] This justice must, however be rendered to him. Malevolence was

[026-126] not, perhaps, innate in him. From his very first steps among men,

[026-127] he had felt himself, later on he had seen himself, spewed out,

[026-128] blasted, rejected. Human words were, for him, always a raillery

[026-129] or a malediction. As he grew up, he had found nothing but hatred

[026-130] around him. He had caught the general malevolence. He had picked

[026-131] up the weapon with which he had been wounded.

[026-132] After all, he turned his face towards men only with reluctance;

[026-133] his cathedral was sufficient for him. It was peopled with marble

[026-134] figures,—kings, saints, bishops,—who at least did not burst out

[026-135] laughing in his face, and who gazed upon him only with

[026-136] tranquillity and kindliness. The other statues, those of the

[026-137] monsters and demons, cherished no hatred for him, Quasimodo. He

[026-138] resembled them too much for that. They seemed rather, to be

[026-139] scoffing at other men. The saints were his friends, and blessed

[026-140] him; the monsters were his friends and guarded him. So he held

[026-141] long communion with them. He sometimes passed whole hours

[026-142] crouching before one of these statues, in solitary conversation

[026-143] with it. If any one came, he fled like a lover surprised in his

[026-144] serenade.

[026-145] And the cathedral was not only society for him, but the universe,

[026-146] and all nature beside. He dreamed of no other hedgerows than the

[026-147] painted windows, always in flower; no other shade than that of

[026-148] the foliage of stone which spread out, loaded with birds, in the

[026-149] tufts of the Saxon capitals; of no other mountains than the

[026-150] colossal towers of the church; of no other ocean than Paris,

[026-151] roaring at their bases.

[026-152] What he loved above all else in the maternal edifice, that which

[026-153] aroused his soul, and made it open its poor wings, which it kept

[026-154] so miserably folded in its cavern, that which sometimes rendered

[026-155] him even happy, was the bells. He loved them, fondled them,

[026-156] talked to them, understood them. From the chime in the spire,

[026-157] over the intersection of the aisles and nave, to the great bell

[026-158] of the front, he cherished a tenderness for them all. The central

[026-159] spire and the two towers were to him as three great cages, whose

[026-160] birds, reared by himself, sang for him alone. Yet it was these

[026-161] very bells which had made him deaf; but mothers often love best

[026-162] that child which has caused them the most suffering.

[026-163] It is true that their voice was the only one which he could still

[026-164] hear. On this score, the big bell was his beloved. It was she

[026-165] whom he preferred out of all that family of noisy girls which

[026-166] bustled above him, on festival days. This bell was named Marie.

[026-167] She was alone in the southern tower, with her sister Jacqueline,

[026-168] a bell of lesser size, shut up in a smaller cage beside hers.

[026-169] This Jacqueline was so called from the name of the wife of Jean

[026-170] Montagu, who had given it to the church, which had not prevented

[026-171] his going and figuring without his head at Montfaucon. In the

[026-172] second tower there were six other bells, and, finally, six

[026-173] smaller ones inhabited the belfry over the crossing, with the

[026-174] wooden bell, which rang only between after dinner on Good Friday

[026-175] and the morning of the day before Easter. So Quasimodo had

[026-176] fifteen bells in his seraglio; but big Marie was his favorite.

[026-177] No idea can be formed of his delight on days when the grand peal

[026-178] was sounded. At the moment when the archdeacon dismissed him, and

[026-179] said, “Go!” he mounted the spiral staircase of the clock tower

[026-180] faster than any one else could have descended it. He entered

[026-181] perfectly breathless into the aerial chamber of the great bell;

[026-182] he gazed at her a moment, devoutly and lovingly; then he gently

[026-183] addressed her and patted her with his hand, like a good horse,

[026-184] which is about to set out on a long journey. He pitied her for

[026-185] the trouble that she was about to suffer. After these first

[026-186] caresses, he shouted to his assistants, placed in the lower story

[026-187] of the tower, to begin. They grasped the ropes, the wheel

[026-188] creaked, the enormous capsule of metal started slowly into

[026-189] motion. Quasimodo followed it with his glance and trembled. The

[026-190] first shock of the clapper and the brazen wall made the framework

[026-191] upon which it was mounted quiver. Quasimodo vibrated with the

[026-192] bell.
