---[036-001] Two of these women were dressed like good _bourgeoises_ of Paris.

[036-002] Their fine white ruffs; their petticoats of linsey-woolsey,

[036-003] striped red and blue; their white knitted stockings, with clocks

[036-004] embroidered in colors, well drawn upon their legs; the

[036-005] square-toed shoes of tawny leather with black soles, and, above

[036-006] all, their headgear, that sort of tinsel horn, loaded down with

[036-007] ribbons and laces, which the women of Champagne still wear, in

[036-008] company with the grenadiers of the imperial guard of Russia,

[036-009] announced that they belonged to that class wives which holds the

[036-010] middle ground between what the lackeys call a woman and what they

[036-011] term a lady. They wore neither rings nor gold crosses, and it was

[036-012] easy to see that, in their ease, this did not proceed from

[036-013] poverty, but simply from fear of being fined. Their companion was

[036-014] attired in very much the same manner; but there was that

[036-015] indescribable something about her dress and bearing which

[036-016] suggested the wife of a provincial notary. One could see, by the

[036-017] way in which her girdle rose above her hips, that she had not

[036-018] been long in Paris. Add to this a plaited tucker, knots of ribbon

[036-019] on her shoes—and that the stripes of her petticoat ran

[036-020] horizontally instead of vertically, and a thousand other

[036-021] enormities which shocked good taste.

[036-022] The two first walked with that step peculiar to Parisian ladies,

[036-023] showing Paris to women from the country. The provincial held by

[036-024] the hand a big boy, who held in his a large, flat cake.

[036-025] We regret to be obliged to add, that, owing to the rigor of the

[036-026] season, he was using his tongue as a handkerchief.

[036-027] The child was making them drag him along, _non passibus æquis_,

[036-028] as Virgil says, and stumbling at every moment, to the great

[036-029] indignation of his mother. It is true that he was looking at his

[036-030] cake more than at the pavement. Some serious motive, no doubt,

[036-031] prevented his biting it (the cake), for he contented himself with

[036-032] gazing tenderly at it. But the mother should have rather taken

[036-033] charge of the cake. It was cruel to make a Tantalus of the

[036-034] chubby-cheeked boy.

[036-035] Meanwhile, the three demoiselles (for the name of _dames_ was

[036-036] then reserved for noble women) were all talking at once.

[036-037] “Let us make haste, Demoiselle Mahiette,” said the youngest of

[036-038] the three, who was also the largest, to the provincial, “I

[036-039] greatly fear that we shall arrive too late; they told us at the

[036-040] Châtelet that they were going to take him directly to the

[036-041] pillory.”

[036-042] “Ah, bah! what are you saying, Demoiselle Oudarde Musnier?”

[036-043] interposed the other Parisienne. “There are two hours yet to the

[036-044] pillory. We have time enough. Have you ever seen any one

[036-045] pilloried, my dear Mahiette?”

[036-046] “Yes,” said the provincial, “at Reims.”

[036-047] “Ah, bah! What is your pillory at Reims? A miserable cage into

[036-048] which only peasants are turned. A great affair, truly!”

[036-049] “Only peasants!” said Mahiette, “at the cloth market in Reims! We

[036-050] have seen very fine criminals there, who have killed their father

[036-051] and mother! Peasants! For what do you take us, Gervaise?”

[036-052] It is certain that the provincial was on the point of taking

[036-053] offence, for the honor of her pillory. Fortunately, that discreet

[036-054] damoiselle, Oudarde Musnier, turned the conversation in time.

[036-055] “By the way, Damoiselle Mahiette, what say you to our Flemish

[036-056] Ambassadors? Have you as fine ones at Reims?”

[036-057] “I admit,” replied Mahiette, “that it is only in Paris that such

[036-058] Flemings can be seen.”

[036-059] “Did you see among the embassy, that big ambassador who is a

[036-060] hosier?” asked Oudarde.

[036-061] “Yes,” said Mahiette. “He has the eye of a Saturn.”

[036-062] “And the big fellow whose face resembles a bare belly?” resumed

[036-063] Gervaise. “And the little one, with small eyes framed in red

[036-064] eyelids, pared down and slashed up like a thistle head?”

[036-065] “’Tis their horses that are worth seeing,” said Oudarde,

[036-066] “caparisoned as they are after the fashion of their country!”

[036-067] “Ah my dear,” interrupted provincial Mahiette, assuming in her

[036-068] turn an air of superiority, “what would you say then, if you had

[036-069] seen in ’61, at the consecration at Reims, eighteen years ago,

[036-070] the horses of the princes and of the king’s company? Housings and

[036-071] caparisons of all sorts; some of damask cloth, of fine cloth of

[036-072] gold, furred with sables; others of velvet, furred with ermine;

[036-073] others all embellished with goldsmith’s work and large bells of

[036-074] gold and silver! And what money that had cost! And what handsome

[036-075] boy pages rode upon them!”

[036-076] “That,” replied Oudarde dryly, “does not prevent the Flemings

[036-077] having very fine horses, and having had a superb supper yesterday

[036-078] with monsieur, the provost of the merchants, at the

[036-079] Hôtel-de-Ville, where they were served with comfits and

[036-080] hippocras, and spices, and other singularities.”

[036-081] “What are you saying, neighbor!” exclaimed Gervaise. “It was with

[036-082] monsieur the cardinal, at the Petit Bourbon that they supped.”

[036-083] “Not at all. At the Hôtel-de-Ville.

[036-084] “Yes, indeed. At the Petit Bourbon!”

[036-085] “It was at the Hôtel-de-Ville,” retorted Oudarde sharply, “and

[036-086] Dr. Scourable addressed them a harangue in Latin, which pleased

[036-087] them greatly. My husband, who is sworn bookseller told me so.”

[036-088] “It was at the Petit Bourbon,” replied Gervaise, with no less

[036-089] spirit, “and this is what monsieur the cardinal’s procurator

[036-090] presented to them: twelve double quarts of hippocras, white,

[036-091] claret, and red; twenty-four boxes of double Lyons marchpane,

[036-092] gilded; as many torches, worth two livres a piece; and six

[036-093] demi-queues[29] of Beaune wine, white and claret, the best that

[036-094] could be found. I have it from my husband, who is a

[036-095] cinquantenier[30], at the Parloir-aux Bourgeois, and who was this

[036-096] morning comparing the Flemish ambassadors with those of Prester

[036-097] John and the Emperor of Trebizond, who came from Mesopotamia to

[036-098] Paris, under the last king, and who wore rings in their ears.”

[036-099] “So true is it that they supped at the Hôtel-de-Ville,” replied

[036-100] Oudarde but little affected by this catalogue, “that such a

[036-101] triumph of viands and comfits has never been seen.”

[036-102] “I tell you that they were served by Le Sec, sergeant of the

[036-103] city, at the Hôtel du Petit-Bourbon, and that that is where you

[036-104] are mistaken.”

[036-105] “At the Hôtel-de-Ville, I tell you!”

[036-106] “At the Petit-Bourbon, my dear! and they had illuminated with

[036-107] magic glasses the word _Hope_, which is written on the grand

[036-108] portal.”

[036-109] “At the Hôtel-de-Ville! At the Hôtel-de-Ville! And Husson-le-Voir

[036-110] played the flute!”

[036-111] “I tell you, no!”

[036-112] “I tell you, yes!”

[036-113] “I say, no!”

[036-114] Plump and worthy Oudarde was preparing to retort, and the quarrel

[036-115] might, perhaps, have proceeded to a pulling of caps, had not

[036-116] Mahiette suddenly exclaimed,—“Look at those people assembled

[036-117] yonder at the end of the bridge! There is something in their

[036-118] midst that they are looking at!”

[036-119] “In sooth,” said Gervaise, “I hear the sounds of a tambourine. I

[036-120] believe ’tis the little Esmeralda, who plays her mummeries with

[036-121] her goat. Eh, be quick, Mahiette! redouble your pace and drag

[036-122] along your boy. You are come hither to visit the curiosities of

[036-123] Paris. You saw the Flemings yesterday; you must see the gypsy

[036-124] to-day.”

[036-125] “The gypsy!” said Mahiette, suddenly retracing her steps, and

[036-126] clasping her son’s arm forcibly. “God preserve me from it! She

[036-127] would steal my child from me! Come, Eustache!”

[036-128] And she set out on a run along the quay towards the Grève, until

[036-129] she had left the bridge far behind her. In the meanwhile, the

[036-130] child whom she was dragging after her fell upon his knees; she

[036-131] halted breathless. Oudarde and Gervaise rejoined her.

[036-132] “That gypsy steal your child from you!” said Gervaise. “That’s a

[036-133] singular freak of yours!”

[036-134] Mahiette shook her head with a pensive air.

[036-135] “The singular point is,” observed Oudarde, “that _la sachette_

[036-136] has the same idea about the Egyptian woman.”

[036-137] “What is _la sachette_?” asked Mahiette.

[036-138] “Hé!” said Oudarde, “Sister Gudule.”

[036-139] “And who is Sister Gudule?” persisted Mahiette.

[036-140] “You are certainly ignorant of all but your Reims, not to know

[036-141] that!” replied Oudarde. “’Tis the recluse of the Rat-Hole.”

[036-142] “What!” demanded Mahiette, “that poor woman to whom we are

[036-143] carrying this cake?”

[036-144] Oudarde nodded affirmatively.

[036-145] “Precisely. You will see her presently at her window on the

[036-146] Grève. She has the same opinion as yourself of these vagabonds of

[036-147] Egypt, who play the tambourine and tell fortunes to the public.

[036-148] No one knows whence comes her horror of the gypsies and

[036-149] Egyptians. But you, Mahiette—why do you run so at the mere sight

[036-150] of them?”

[036-151] “Oh!” said Mahiette, seizing her child’s round head in both

[036-152] hands, “I don’t want that to happen to me which happened to

[036-153] Paquette la Chantefleurie.”

[036-154] “Oh! you must tell us that story, my good Mahiette,” said

[036-155] Gervaise, taking her arm.

[036-156] “Gladly,” replied Mahiette, “but you must be ignorant of all but

[036-157] your Paris not to know that! I will tell you then (but ’tis not

[036-158] necessary for us to halt that I may tell you the tale), that

[036-159] Paquette la Chantefleurie was a pretty maid of eighteen when I

[036-160] was one myself, that is to say, eighteen years ago, and ’tis her

[036-161] own fault if she is not to-day, like me, a good, plump, fresh

[036-162] mother of six and thirty, with a husband and a son. However,

[036-163] after the age of fourteen, it was too late! Well, she was the

[036-164] daughter of Guybertant, minstrel of the barges at Reims, the same

[036-165] who had played before King Charles VII., at his coronation, when

[036-166] he descended our river Vesle from Sillery to Muison, when Madame

[036-167] the Maid of Orleans was also in the boat. The old father died

[036-168] when Paquette was still a mere child; she had then no one but her

[036-169] mother, the sister of M. Pradon, master-brazier and coppersmith

[036-170] in Paris, Rue Parin-Garlin, who died last year. You see she was

[036-171] of good family. The mother was a good simple woman,

[036-172] unfortunately, and she taught Paquette nothing but a bit of

[036-173] embroidery and toy-making which did not prevent the little one

[036-174] from growing very large and remaining very poor. They both dwelt

[036-175] at Reims, on the river front, Rue de Folle-Peine. Mark this: For

[036-176] I believe it was this which brought misfortune to Paquette. In

[036-177] ’61, the year of the coronation of our King Louis XI. whom God

[036-178] preserve! Paquette was so gay and so pretty that she was called

[036-179] everywhere by no other name than _la Chantefleurie_—blossoming

[036-180] song. Poor girl! She had handsome teeth, she was fond of laughing

[036-181] and displaying them. Now, a maid who loves to laugh is on the

[036-182] road to weeping; handsome teeth ruin handsome eyes. So she was la

[036-183] Chantefleurie. She and her mother earned a precarious living;

[036-184] they had been very destitute since the death of the minstrel;

[036-185] their embroidery did not bring them in more than six farthings a

[036-186] week, which does not amount to quite two eagle liards. Where were

[036-187] the days when Father Guybertant had earned twelve sous parisian,

[036-188] in a single coronation, with a song? One winter (it was in that

[036-189] same year of ’61), when the two women had neither fagots nor

[036-190] firewood, it was very cold, which gave la Chantefleurie such a

[036-191] fine color that the men called her Paquette![31] and many called

[036-192] her Pâquerette![32] and she was ruined.—Eustache, just let me see

[036-193] you bite that cake if you dare!—We immediately perceived that she

[036-194] was ruined, one Sunday when she came to church with a gold cross

[036-195] about her neck. At fourteen years of age! do you see? First it

[036-196] was the young Vicomte de Cormontreuil, who has his bell tower

[036-197] three leagues distant from Reims; then Messire Henri de

[036-198] Triancourt, equerry to the King; then less than that, Chiart de

[036-199] Beaulion, sergeant-at-arms; then, still descending, Guery

[036-200] Aubergeon, carver to the King; then, Macé de Frépus, barber to

[036-201] monsieur the dauphin; then, Thévenin le Moine, King’s cook; then,

[036-202] the men growing continually younger and less noble, she fell to

[036-203] Guillaume Racine, minstrel of the hurdy-gurdy and to Thierry de

[036-204] Mer, lamplighter. Then, poor Chantefleurie, she belonged to every

[036-205] one: she had reached the last sou of her gold piece. What shall I

[036-206] say to you, my damoiselles? At the coronation, in the same year,

[036-207] ’61, ’twas she who made the bed of the king of the debauchees! In

[036-208] the same year!”
