---[027-001] “Vah!” he cried, with a senseless burst of laughter. However, the

[027-002] movement of the bass was accelerated, and, in proportion as it

[027-003] described a wider angle, Quasimodo’s eye opened also more and

[027-004] more widely, phosphoric and flaming. At length the grand peal

[027-005] began; the whole tower trembled; woodwork, leads, cut stones, all

[027-006] groaned at once, from the piles of the foundation to the trefoils

[027-007] of its summit. Then Quasimodo boiled and frothed; he went and

[027-008] came; he trembled from head to foot with the tower. The bell,

[027-009] furious, running riot, presented to the two walls of the tower

[027-010] alternately its brazen throat, whence escaped that tempestuous

[027-011] breath, which is audible leagues away. Quasimodo stationed

[027-012] himself in front of this open throat; he crouched and rose with

[027-013] the oscillations of the bell, breathed in this overwhelming

[027-014] breath, gazed by turns at the deep place, which swarmed with

[027-015] people, two hundred feet below him, and at that enormous, brazen

[027-016] tongue which came, second after second, to howl in his ear.

[027-017] It was the only speech which he understood, the only sound which

[027-018] broke for him the universal silence. He swelled out in it as a

[027-019] bird does in the sun. All of a sudden, the frenzy of the bell

[027-020] seized upon him; his look became extraordinary; he lay in wait

[027-021] for the great bell as it passed, as a spider lies in wait for a

[027-022] fly, and flung himself abruptly upon it, with might and main.

[027-023] Then, suspended above the abyss, borne to and fro by the

[027-024] formidable swinging of the bell, he seized the brazen monster by

[027-025] the ear-laps, pressed it between both knees, spurred it on with

[027-026] his heels, and redoubled the fury of the peal with the whole

[027-027] shock and weight of his body. Meanwhile, the tower trembled; he

[027-028] shrieked and gnashed his teeth, his red hair rose erect, his

[027-029] breast heaving like a bellows, his eye flashed flames, the

[027-030] monstrous bell neighed, panting, beneath him; and then it was no

[027-031] longer the great bell of Notre-Dame nor Quasimodo: it was a

[027-032] dream, a whirlwind, a tempest, dizziness mounted astride of

[027-033] noise; a spirit clinging to a flying crupper, a strange centaur,

[027-034] half man, half bell; a sort of horrible Astolphus, borne away

[027-035] upon a prodigious hippogriff of living bronze.

[027-036] The presence of this extraordinary being caused, as it were, a

[027-037] breath of life to circulate throughout the entire cathedral. It

[027-038] seemed as though there escaped from him, at least according to

[027-039] the growing superstitions of the crowd, a mysterious emanation

[027-040] which animated all the stones of Notre-Dame, and made the deep

[027-041] bowels of the ancient church to palpitate. It sufficed for people

[027-042] to know that he was there, to make them believe that they beheld

[027-043] the thousand statues of the galleries and the fronts in motion.

[027-044] And the cathedral did indeed seem a docile and obedient creature

[027-045] beneath his hand; it waited on his will to raise its great voice;

[027-046] it was possessed and filled with Quasimodo, as with a familiar

[027-047] spirit. One would have said that he made the immense edifice

[027-048] breathe. He was everywhere about it; in fact, he multiplied

[027-049] himself on all points of the structure. Now one perceived with

[027-050] affright at the very top of one of the towers, a fantastic dwarf

[027-051] climbing, writhing, crawling on all fours, descending outside

[027-052] above the abyss, leaping from projection to projection, and going

[027-053] to ransack the belly of some sculptured gorgon; it was Quasimodo

[027-054] dislodging the crows. Again, in some obscure corner of the church

[027-055] one came in contact with a sort of living chimera, crouching and

[027-056] scowling; it was Quasimodo engaged in thought. Sometimes one

[027-057] caught sight, upon a bell tower, of an enormous head and a bundle

[027-058] of disordered limbs swinging furiously at the end of a rope; it

[027-059] was Quasimodo ringing vespers or the Angelus. Often at night a

[027-060] hideous form was seen wandering along the frail balustrade of

[027-061] carved lacework, which crowns the towers and borders the

[027-062] circumference of the apse; again it was the hunchback of

[027-063] Notre-Dame. Then, said the women of the neighborhood, the whole

[027-064] church took on something fantastic, supernatural, horrible; eyes

[027-065] and mouths were opened, here and there; one heard the dogs, the

[027-066] monsters, and the gargoyles of stone, which keep watch night and

[027-067] day, with outstretched neck and open jaws, around the monstrous

[027-068] cathedral, barking. And, if it was a Christmas Eve, while the

[027-069] great bell, which seemed to emit the death rattle, summoned the

[027-070] faithful to the midnight mass, such an air was spread over the

[027-071] sombre façade that one would have declared that the grand portal

[027-072] was devouring the throng, and that the rose window was watching

[027-073] it. And all this came from Quasimodo. Egypt would have taken him

[027-074] for the god of this temple; the Middle Ages believed him to be

[027-075] its demon: he was in fact its soul.

[027-076] To such an extent was this disease that for those who know that

[027-077] Quasimodo has existed, Notre-Dame is to-day deserted, inanimate,

[027-078] dead. One feels that something has disappeared from it. That

[027-079] immense body is empty; it is a skeleton; the spirit has quitted

[027-080] it, one sees its place and that is all. It is like a skull which

[027-081] still has holes for the eyes, but no longer sight.

[027-082] CHAPTER IV. THE DOG AND HIS MASTER.

[027-083] Nevertheless, there was one human creature whom Quasimodo

[027-084] excepted from his malice and from his hatred for others, and whom

[027-085] he loved even more, perhaps, than his cathedral: this was Claude

[027-086] Frollo.

[027-087] The matter was simple; Claude Frollo had taken him in, had

[027-088] adopted him, had nourished him, had reared him. When a little

[027-089] lad, it was between Claude Frollo’s legs that he was accustomed

[027-090] to seek refuge, when the dogs and the children barked after him.

[027-091] Claude Frollo had taught him to talk, to read, to write. Claude

[027-092] Frollo had finally made him the bellringer. Now, to give the big

[027-093] bell in marriage to Quasimodo was to give Juliet to Romeo.

[027-094] Hence Quasimodo’s gratitude was profound, passionate, boundless;

[027-095] and although the visage of his adopted father was often clouded

[027-096] or severe, although his speech was habitually curt, harsh,

[027-097] imperious, that gratitude never wavered for a single moment. The

[027-098] archdeacon had in Quasimodo the most submissive slave, the most

[027-099] docile lackey, the most vigilant of dogs. When the poor

[027-100] bellringer became deaf, there had been established between him

[027-101] and Claude Frollo, a language of signs, mysterious and understood

[027-102] by themselves alone. In this manner the archdeacon was the sole

[027-103] human being with whom Quasimodo had preserved communication. He

[027-104] was in sympathy with but two things in this world: Notre-Dame and

[027-105] Claude Frollo.

[027-106] There is nothing which can be compared with the empire of the

[027-107] archdeacon over the bellringer; with the attachment of the

[027-108] bellringer for the archdeacon. A sign from Claude and the idea of

[027-109] giving him pleasure would have sufficed to make Quasimodo hurl

[027-110] himself headlong from the summit of Notre-Dame. It was a

[027-111] remarkable thing—all that physical strength which had reached in

[027-112] Quasimodo such an extraordinary development, and which was placed

[027-113] by him blindly at the disposition of another. There was in it, no

[027-114] doubt, filial devotion, domestic attachment; there was also the

[027-115] fascination of one spirit by another spirit. It was a poor,

[027-116] awkward, and clumsy organization, which stood with lowered head

[027-117] and supplicating eyes before a lofty and profound, a powerful and

[027-118] superior intellect. Lastly, and above all, it was gratitude.

[027-119] Gratitude so pushed to its extremest limit, that we do not know

[027-120] to what to compare it. This virtue is not one of those of which

[027-121] the finest examples are to be met with among men. We will say

[027-122] then, that Quasimodo loved the archdeacon as never a dog, never a

[027-123] horse, never an elephant loved his master.

[027-124] CHAPTER V. MORE ABOUT CLAUDE FROLLO.

[027-125] In 1482, Quasimodo was about twenty years of age; Claude Frollo,

[027-126] about thirty-six. One had grown up, the other had grown old.

[027-127] Claude Frollo was no longer the simple scholar of the college of

[027-128] Torchi, the tender protector of a little child, the young and

[027-129] dreamy philosopher who knew many things and was ignorant of many.

[027-130] He was a priest, austere, grave, morose; one charged with souls;

[027-131] monsieur the archdeacon of Josas, the bishop’s second acolyte,

[027-132] having charge of the two deaneries of Montlhéry, and Châteaufort,

[027-133] and one hundred and seventy-four country curacies. He was an

[027-134] imposing and sombre personage, before whom the choir boys in alb

[027-135] and in jacket trembled, as well as the machicots[25], and the

[027-136] brothers of Saint-Augustine and the matutinal clerks of

[027-137] Notre-Dame, when he passed slowly beneath the lofty arches of the

[027-138] choir, majestic, thoughtful, with arms folded and his head so

[027-139] bent upon his breast that all one saw of his face was his large,

[027-140] bald brow.

[027-141] Dom Claude Frollo had, however, abandoned neither science nor the

[027-142] education of his young brother, those two occupations of his

[027-143] life. But as time went on, some bitterness had been mingled with

[027-144] these things which were so sweet. In the long run, says Paul

[027-145] Diacre, the best lard turns rancid. Little Jehan Frollo, surnamed

[027-146] (_du Moulin_) “of the Mill” because of the place where he had

[027-147] been reared, had not grown up in the direction which Claude would

[027-148] have liked to impose upon him. The big brother counted upon a

[027-149] pious, docile, learned, and honorable pupil. But the little

[027-150] brother, like those young trees which deceive the gardener’s

[027-151] hopes and turn obstinately to the quarter whence they receive sun

[027-152] and air, the little brother did not grow and did not multiply,

[027-153] but only put forth fine bushy and luxuriant branches on the side

[027-154] of laziness, ignorance, and debauchery. He was a regular devil,

[027-155] and a very disorderly one, who made Dom Claude scowl; but very

[027-156] droll and very subtle, which made the big brother smile.

[027-157] Claude had confided him to that same college of Torchi where he

[027-158] had passed his early years in study and meditation; and it was a

[027-159] grief to him that this sanctuary, formerly edified by the name of

[027-160] Frollo, should to-day be scandalized by it. He sometimes preached

[027-161] Jehan very long and severe sermons, which the latter intrepidly

[027-162] endured. After all, the young scapegrace had a good heart, as can

[027-163] be seen in all comedies. But the sermon over, he none the less

[027-164] tranquilly resumed his course of seditions and enormities. Now it

[027-165] was a _béjaune_ or yellow beak (as they called the new arrivals

[027-166] at the university), whom he had been mauling by way of welcome; a

[027-167] precious tradition which has been carefully preserved to our own

[027-168] day. Again, he had set in movement a band of scholars, who had

[027-169] flung themselves upon a wine-shop in classic fashion, _quasi

[027-170] classico excitati_, had then beaten the tavern-keeper “with

[027-171] offensive cudgels,” and joyously pillaged the tavern, even to

[027-172] smashing in the hogsheads of wine in the cellar. And then it was

[027-173] a fine report in Latin, which the sub-monitor of Torchi carried

[027-174] piteously to Dom Claude with this dolorous marginal

[027-175] comment,—_Rixa; prima causa vinum optimum potatum_. Finally, it

[027-176] was said, a thing quite horrible in a boy of sixteen, that his

[027-177] debauchery often extended as far as the Rue de Glatigny.

[027-178] Claude, saddened and discouraged in his human affections, by all

[027-179] this, had flung himself eagerly into the arms of learning, that

[027-180] sister which, at least does not laugh in your face, and which

[027-181] always pays you, though in money that is sometimes a little

[027-182] hollow, for the attention which you have paid to her. Hence, he

[027-183] became more and more learned, and, at the same time, as a natural

[027-184] consequence, more and more rigid as a priest, more and more sad

[027-185] as a man. There are for each of us several parallelisms between

[027-186] our intelligence, our habits, and our character, which develop

[027-187] without a break, and break only in the great disturbances of

[027-188] life.

[027-189] As Claude Frollo had passed through nearly the entire circle of

[027-190] human learning—positive, exterior, and permissible—since his

[027-191] youth, he was obliged, unless he came to a halt, _ubi defuit

[027-192] orbis_, to proceed further and seek other aliments for the

[027-193] insatiable activity of his intelligence. The antique symbol of

[027-194] the serpent biting its tail is, above all, applicable to science.

[027-195] It would appear that Claude Frollo had experienced this. Many

[027-196] grave persons affirm that, after having exhausted the _fas_ of

[027-197] human learning, he had dared to penetrate into the _nefas_. He

[027-198] had, they said, tasted in succession all the apples of the tree

[027-199] of knowledge, and, whether from hunger or disgust, had ended by

[027-200] tasting the forbidden fruit. He had taken his place by turns, as

[027-201] the reader has seen, in the conferences of the theologians in

[027-202] Sorbonne,—in the assemblies of the doctors of art, after the

[027-203] manner of Saint-Hilaire,—in the disputes of the decretalists,

[027-204] after the manner of Saint-Martin,—in the congregations of

[027-205] physicians at the holy water font of Notre-Dame, _ad cupam

[027-206] Nostræ-Dominæ_. All the dishes permitted and approved, which

[027-207] those four great kitchens called the four faculties could

[027-208] elaborate and serve to the understanding, he had devoured, and

[027-209] had been satiated with them before his hunger was appeased. Then

[027-210] he had penetrated further, lower, beneath all that finished,

[027-211] material, limited knowledge; he had, perhaps, risked his soul,

[027-212] and had seated himself in the cavern at that mysterious table of

[027-213] the alchemists, of the astrologers, of the hermetics, of which

[027-214] Averroès, Guillaume de Paris, and Nicolas Flamel hold the end in

[027-215] the Middle Ages; and which extends in the East, by the light of

[027-216] the seven-branched candlestick, to Solomon, Pythagoras, and

[027-217] Zoroaster.
