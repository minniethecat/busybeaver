---[024-001] These are very superb structures. Let us add a quantity of fine,

[024-002] amusing, and varied streets, like the Rue de Rivoli, and I do not

[024-003] despair of Paris presenting to the eye, when viewed from a

[024-004] balloon, that richness of line, that opulence of detail, that

[024-005] diversity of aspect, that grandiose something in the simple, and

[024-006] unexpected in the beautiful, which characterizes a checker-board.

[024-007] However, admirable as the Paris of to-day may seem to you,

[024-008] reconstruct the Paris of the fifteenth century, call it up before

[024-009] you in thought; look at the sky athwart that surprising forest of

[024-010] spires, towers, and belfries; spread out in the centre of the

[024-011] city, tear away at the point of the islands, fold at the arches

[024-012] of the bridges, the Seine, with its broad green and yellow

[024-013] expanses, more variable than the skin of a serpent; project

[024-014] clearly against an azure horizon the Gothic profile of this

[024-015] ancient Paris. Make its contour float in a winter’s mist which

[024-016] clings to its numerous chimneys; drown it in profound night and

[024-017] watch the odd play of lights and shadows in that sombre labyrinth

[024-018] of edifices; cast upon it a ray of light which shall vaguely

[024-019] outline it and cause to emerge from the fog the great heads of

[024-020] the towers; or take that black silhouette again, enliven with

[024-021] shadow the thousand acute angles of the spires and gables, and

[024-022] make it start out more toothed than a shark’s jaw against a

[024-023] copper-colored western sky,—and then compare.

[024-024] And if you wish to receive of the ancient city an impression with

[024-025] which the modern one can no longer furnish you, climb—on the

[024-026] morning of some grand festival, beneath the rising sun of Easter

[024-027] or of Pentecost—climb upon some elevated point, whence you

[024-028] command the entire capital; and be present at the wakening of the

[024-029] chimes. Behold, at a signal given from heaven, for it is the sun

[024-030] which gives it, all those churches quiver simultaneously. First

[024-031] come scattered strokes, running from one church to another, as

[024-032] when musicians give warning that they are about to begin. Then,

[024-033] all at once, behold!—for it seems at times, as though the ear

[024-034] also possessed a sight of its own,—behold, rising from each bell

[024-035] tower, something like a column of sound, a cloud of harmony.

[024-036] First, the vibration of each bell mounts straight upwards, pure

[024-037] and, so to speak, isolated from the others, into the splendid

[024-038] morning sky; then, little by little, as they swell they melt

[024-039] together, mingle, are lost in each other, and amalgamate in a

[024-040] magnificent concert. It is no longer anything but a mass of

[024-041] sonorous vibrations incessantly sent forth from the numerous

[024-042] belfries; floats, undulates, bounds, whirls over the city, and

[024-043] prolongs far beyond the horizon the deafening circle of its

[024-044] oscillations.

[024-045] Nevertheless, this sea of harmony is not a chaos; great and

[024-046] profound as it is, it has not lost its transparency; you behold

[024-047] the windings of each group of notes which escapes from the

[024-048] belfries. You can follow the dialogue, by turns grave and shrill,

[024-049] of the treble and the bass; you can see the octaves leap from one

[024-050] tower to another; you watch them spring forth, winged, light, and

[024-051] whistling, from the silver bell, to fall, broken and limping from

[024-052] the bell of wood; you admire in their midst the rich gamut which

[024-053] incessantly ascends and re-ascends the seven bells of

[024-054] Saint-Eustache; you see light and rapid notes running across it,

[024-055] executing three or four luminous zigzags, and vanishing like

[024-056] flashes of lightning. Yonder is the Abbey of Saint-Martin, a

[024-057] shrill, cracked singer; here the gruff and gloomy voice of the

[024-058] Bastille; at the other end, the great tower of the Louvre, with

[024-059] its bass. The royal chime of the palace scatters on all sides,

[024-060] and without relaxation, resplendent trills, upon which fall, at

[024-061] regular intervals, the heavy strokes from the belfry of

[024-062] Notre-Dame, which makes them sparkle like the anvil under the

[024-063] hammer. At intervals you behold the passage of sounds of all

[024-064] forms which come from the triple peal of Saint-Germain-des-Prés.

[024-065] Then, again, from time to time, this mass of sublime noises opens

[024-066] and gives passage to the beats of the Ave Maria, which bursts

[024-067] forth and sparkles like an aigrette of stars. Below, in the very

[024-068] depths of the concert, you confusedly distinguish the interior

[024-069] chanting of the churches, which exhales through the vibrating

[024-070] pores of their vaulted roofs.

[024-071] Assuredly, this is an opera which it is worth the trouble of

[024-072] listening to. Ordinarily, the noise which escapes from Paris by

[024-073] day is the city speaking; by night, it is the city breathing; in

[024-074] this case, it is the city singing. Lend an ear, then, to this

[024-075] concert of bell towers; spread over all the murmur of half a

[024-076] million men, the eternal plaint of the river, the infinite

[024-077] breathings of the wind, the grave and distant quartette of the

[024-078] four forests arranged upon the hills, on the horizon, like

[024-079] immense stacks of organ pipes; extinguish, as in a half shade,

[024-080] all that is too hoarse and too shrill about the central chime,

[024-081] and say whether you know anything in the world more rich and

[024-082] joyful, more golden, more dazzling, than this tumult of bells and

[024-083] chimes;—than this furnace of music,—than these ten thousand

[024-084] brazen voices chanting simultaneously in the flutes of stone,

[024-085] three hundred feet high,—than this city which is no longer

[024-086] anything but an orchestra,—than this symphony which produces the

[024-087] noise of a tempest.

[024-088] BOOK FOURTH.

[024-089] CHAPTER I. GOOD SOULS.

[024-090] Sixteen years previous to the epoch when this story takes place,

[024-091] one fine morning, on Quasimodo Sunday, a living creature had been

[024-092] deposited, after mass, in the church of Notre-Dame, on the wooden

[024-093] bed securely fixed in the vestibule on the left, opposite that

[024-094] great image of Saint Christopher, which the figure of Messire

[024-095] Antoine des Essarts, chevalier, carved in stone, had been gazing

[024-096] at on his knees since 1413, when they took it into their heads to

[024-097] overthrow the saint and the faithful follower. Upon this bed of

[024-098] wood it was customary to expose foundlings for public charity.

[024-099] Whoever cared to take them did so. In front of the wooden bed was

[024-100] a copper basin for alms.

[024-101] The sort of living being which lay upon that plank on the morning

[024-102] of Quasimodo, in the year of the Lord, 1467, appeared to excite

[024-103] to a high degree, the curiosity of the numerous group which had

[024-104] congregated about the wooden bed. The group was formed for the

[024-105] most part of the fair sex. Hardly any one was there except old

[024-106] women.

[024-107] In the first row, and among those who were most bent over the

[024-108] bed, four were noticeable, who, from their gray _cagoule_, a sort

[024-109] of cassock, were recognizable as attached to some devout

[024-110] sisterhood. I do not see why history has not transmitted to

[024-111] posterity the names of these four discreet and venerable damsels.

[024-112] They were Agnès la Herme, Jehanne de la Tarme, Henriette la

[024-113] Gaultière, Gauchère la Violette, all four widows, all four dames

[024-114] of the Chapel Étienne Haudry, who had quitted their house with

[024-115] the permission of their mistress, and in conformity with the

[024-116] statutes of Pierre d’Ailly, in order to come and hear the sermon.

[024-117] However, if these good Haudriettes were, for the moment,

[024-118] complying with the statutes of Pierre d’Ailly, they certainly

[024-119] violated with joy those of Michel de Brache, and the Cardinal of

[024-120] Pisa, which so inhumanly enjoined silence upon them.

[024-121] “What is this, sister?” said Agnès to Gauchère, gazing at the

[024-122] little creature exposed, which was screaming and writhing on the

[024-123] wooden bed, terrified by so many glances.

[024-124] “What is to become of us,” said Jehanne, “if that is the way

[024-125] children are made now?”

[024-126] “I’m not learned in the matter of children,” resumed Agnès, “but

[024-127] it must be a sin to look at this one.”

[024-128] “’Tis not a child, Agnès.”

[024-129] “’Tis an abortion of a monkey,” remarked Gauchère.

[024-130] “’Tis a miracle,” interposed Henriette la Gaultière.

[024-131] “Then,” remarked Agnès, “it is the third since the Sunday of the

[024-132] _Lætare_: for, in less than a week, we had the miracle of the

[024-133] mocker of pilgrims divinely punished by Notre-Dame

[024-134] d’Aubervilliers, and that was the second miracle within a month.”

[024-135] “This pretended foundling is a real monster of abomination,”

[024-136] resumed Jehanne.

[024-137] “He yells loud enough to deafen a chanter,” continued Gauchère.

[024-138] “Hold your tongue, you little howler!”

[024-139] “To think that Monsieur of Reims sent this enormity to Monsieur

[024-140] of Paris,” added la Gaultière, clasping her hands.

[024-141] “I imagine,” said Agnès la Herme, “that it is a beast, an

[024-142] animal,—the fruit of a Jew and a sow; something not Christian, in

[024-143] short, which ought to be thrown into the fire or into the water.”

[024-144] “I really hope,” resumed la Gaultière, “that nobody will apply

[024-145] for it.”

[024-146] “Ah, good heavens!” exclaimed Agnès; “those poor nurses yonder in

[024-147] the foundling asylum, which forms the lower end of the lane as

[024-148] you go to the river, just beside Monseigneur the bishop! what if

[024-149] this little monster were to be carried to them to suckle? I’d

[024-150] rather give suck to a vampire.”

[024-151] “How innocent that poor la Herme is!” resumed Jehanne; “don’t you

[024-152] see, sister, that this little monster is at least four years old,

[024-153] and that he would have less appetite for your breast than for a

[024-154] turnspit.”

[024-155] The “little monster” we should find it difficult ourselves to

[024-156] describe him otherwise, was, in fact, not a new-born child. It

[024-157] was a very angular and very lively little mass, imprisoned in its

[024-158] linen sack, stamped with the cipher of Messire Guillaume

[024-159] Chartier, then bishop of Paris, with a head projecting. That head

[024-160] was deformed enough; one beheld only a forest of red hair, one

[024-161] eye, a mouth, and teeth. The eye wept, the mouth cried, and the

[024-162] teeth seemed to ask only to be allowed to bite. The whole

[024-163] struggled in the sack, to the great consternation of the crowd,

[024-164] which increased and was renewed incessantly around it.

[024-165] Dame Aloïse de Gondelaurier, a rich and noble woman, who held by

[024-166] the hand a pretty girl about five or six years of age, and

[024-167] dragged a long veil about, suspended to the golden horn of her

[024-168] headdress, halted as she passed the wooden bed, and gazed for a

[024-169] moment at the wretched creature, while her charming little

[024-170] daughter, Fleur-de-Lys de Gondelaurier, spelled out with her

[024-171] tiny, pretty finger, the permanent inscription attached to the

[024-172] wooden bed: “Foundlings.”

[024-173] “Really,” said the dame, turning away in disgust, “I thought that

[024-174] they only exposed children here.”

[024-175] She turned her back, throwing into the basin a silver florin,

[024-176] which rang among the liards, and made the poor goodwives of the

[024-177] chapel of Étienne Haudry open their eyes.

[024-178] A moment later, the grave and learned Robert Mistricolle, the

[024-179] king’s protonotary, passed, with an enormous missal under one arm

[024-180] and his wife on the other (Damoiselle Guillemette la Mairesse),

[024-181] having thus by his side his two regulators,—spiritual and

[024-182] temporal.

[024-183] “Foundling!” he said, after examining the object; “found,

[024-184] apparently, on the banks of the river Phlegethon.”

[024-185] “One can only see one eye,” observed Damoiselle Guillemette;

[024-186] “there is a wart on the other.”

[024-187] “It’s not a wart,” returned Master Robert Mistricolle, “it is an

[024-188] egg which contains another demon exactly similar, who bears

[024-189] another little egg which contains another devil, and so on.”

[024-190] “How do you know that?” asked Guillemette la Mairesse.

[024-191] “I know it pertinently,” replied the protonotary.

[024-192] “Monsieur le protonotare,” asked Gauchère, “what do you

[024-193] prognosticate of this pretended foundling?”

[024-194] “The greatest misfortunes,” replied Mistricolle.

[024-195] “Ah! good heavens!” said an old woman among the spectators, “and

[024-196] that besides our having had a considerable pestilence last year,

[024-197] and that they say that the English are going to disembark in a

[024-198] company at Harfleur.”

[024-199] “Perhaps that will prevent the queen from coming to Paris in the

[024-200] month of September,” interposed another; “trade is so bad

[024-201] already.”

[024-202] “My opinion is,” exclaimed Jehanne de la Tarme, “that it would be

[024-203] better for the louts of Paris, if this little magician were put

[024-204] to bed on a fagot than on a plank.”
