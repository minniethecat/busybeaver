---[032-001] In the fifteenth century everything changes.

[032-002] Human thought discovers a mode of perpetuating itself, not only

[032-003] more durable and more resisting than architecture, but still more

[032-004] simple and easy. Architecture is dethroned. Gutenberg’s letters

[032-005] of lead are about to supersede Orpheus’s letters of stone.

[032-006] _The book is about to kill the edifice_.

[032-007] The invention of printing is the greatest event in history. It is

[032-008] the mother of revolution. It is the mode of expression of

[032-009] humanity which is totally renewed; it is human thought stripping

[032-010] off one form and donning another; it is the complete and

[032-011] definitive change of skin of that symbolical serpent which since

[032-012] the days of Adam has represented intelligence.

[032-013] In its printed form, thought is more imperishable than ever; it

[032-014] is volatile, irresistible, indestructible. It is mingled with the

[032-015] air. In the days of architecture it made a mountain of itself,

[032-016] and took powerful possession of a century and a place. Now it

[032-017] converts itself into a flock of birds, scatters itself to the

[032-018] four winds, and occupies all points of air and space at once.

[032-019] We repeat, who does not perceive that in this form it is far more

[032-020] indelible? It was solid, it has become alive. It passes from

[032-021] duration in time to immortality. One can demolish a mass; how can

[032-022] one extirpate ubiquity? If a flood comes, the mountains will have

[032-023] long disappeared beneath the waves, while the birds will still be

[032-024] flying about; and if a single ark floats on the surface of the

[032-025] cataclysm, they will alight upon it, will float with it, will be

[032-026] present with it at the ebbing of the waters; and the new world

[032-027] which emerges from this chaos will behold, on its awakening, the

[032-028] thought of the world which has been submerged soaring above it,

[032-029] winged and living.

[032-030] And when one observes that this mode of expression is not only

[032-031] the most conservative, but also the most simple, the most

[032-032] convenient, the most practicable for all; when one reflects that

[032-033] it does not drag after it bulky baggage, and does not set in

[032-034] motion a heavy apparatus; when one compares thought forced, in

[032-035] order to transform itself into an edifice, to put in motion four

[032-036] or five other arts and tons of gold, a whole mountain of stones,

[032-037] a whole forest of timber-work, a whole nation of workmen; when

[032-038] one compares it to the thought which becomes a book, and for

[032-039] which a little paper, a little ink, and a pen suffice,—how can

[032-040] one be surprised that human intelligence should have quitted

[032-041] architecture for printing? Cut the primitive bed of a river

[032-042] abruptly with a canal hollowed out below its level, and the river

[032-043] will desert its bed.

[032-044] Behold how, beginning with the discovery of printing,

[032-045] architecture withers away little by little, becomes lifeless and

[032-046] bare. How one feels the water sinking, the sap departing, the

[032-047] thought of the times and of the people withdrawing from it! The

[032-048] chill is almost imperceptible in the fifteenth century; the press

[032-049] is, as yet, too weak, and, at the most, draws from powerful

[032-050] architecture a superabundance of life. But practically beginning

[032-051] with the sixteenth century, the malady of architecture is

[032-052] visible; it is no longer the expression of society; it becomes

[032-053] classic art in a miserable manner; from being Gallic, European,

[032-054] indigenous, it becomes Greek and Roman; from being true and

[032-055] modern, it becomes pseudo-classic. It is this decadence which is

[032-056] called the Renaissance. A magnificent decadence, however, for the

[032-057] ancient Gothic genius, that sun which sets behind the gigantic

[032-058] press of Mayence, still penetrates for a while longer with its

[032-059] rays that whole hybrid pile of Latin arcades and Corinthian

[032-060] columns.

[032-061] It is that setting sun which we mistake for the dawn.

[032-062] Nevertheless, from the moment when architecture is no longer

[032-063] anything but an art like any other; as soon as it is no longer

[032-064] the total art, the sovereign art, the tyrant art,—it has no

[032-065] longer the power to retain the other arts. So they emancipate

[032-066] themselves, break the yoke of the architect, and take themselves

[032-067] off, each one in its own direction. Each one of them gains by

[032-068] this divorce. Isolation aggrandizes everything. Sculpture becomes

[032-069] statuary, the image trade becomes painting, the canon becomes

[032-070] music. One would pronounce it an empire dismembered at the death

[032-071] of its Alexander, and whose provinces become kingdoms.

[032-072] Hence Raphael, Michael Angelo, Jean Goujon, Palestrina, those

[032-073] splendors of the dazzling sixteenth century.

[032-074] Thought emancipates itself in all directions at the same time as

[032-075] the arts. The arch-heretics of the Middle Ages had already made

[032-076] large incisions into Catholicism. The sixteenth century breaks

[032-077] religious unity. Before the invention of printing, reform would

[032-078] have been merely a schism; printing converted it into a

[032-079] revolution. Take away the press; heresy is enervated. Whether it

[032-080] be Providence or Fate, Gutenburg is the precursor of Luther.

[032-081] Nevertheless, when the sun of the Middle Ages is completely set,

[032-082] when the Gothic genius is forever extinct upon the horizon,

[032-083] architecture grows dim, loses its color, becomes more and more

[032-084] effaced. The printed book, the gnawing worm of the edifice, sucks

[032-085] and devours it. It becomes bare, denuded of its foliage, and

[032-086] grows visibly emaciated. It is petty, it is poor, it is nothing.

[032-087] It no longer expresses anything, not even the memory of the art

[032-088] of another time. Reduced to itself, abandoned by the other arts,

[032-089] because human thought is abandoning it, it summons bunglers in

[032-090] place of artists. Glass replaces the painted windows. The

[032-091] stone-cutter succeeds the sculptor. Farewell all sap, all

[032-092] originality, all life, all intelligence. It drags along, a

[032-093] lamentable workshop mendicant, from copy to copy. Michael Angelo,

[032-094] who, no doubt, felt even in the sixteenth century that it was

[032-095] dying, had a last idea, an idea of despair. That Titan of art

[032-096] piled the Pantheon on the Parthenon, and made Saint-Peter’s at

[032-097] Rome. A great work, which deserved to remain unique, the last

[032-098] originality of architecture, the signature of a giant artist at

[032-099] the bottom of the colossal register of stone which was closed

[032-100] forever. With Michael Angelo dead, what does this miserable

[032-101] architecture, which survived itself in the state of a spectre,

[032-102] do? It takes Saint-Peter in Rome, copies it and parodies it. It

[032-103] is a mania. It is a pity. Each century has its Saint-Peter’s of

[032-104] Rome; in the seventeenth century, the Val-de-Grâce; in the

[032-105] eighteenth, Sainte-Geneviève. Each country has its Saint-Peter’s

[032-106] of Rome. London has one; Petersburg has another; Paris has two or

[032-107] three. The insignificant testament, the last dotage of a decrepit

[032-108] grand art falling back into infancy before it dies.

[032-109] If, in place of the characteristic monuments which we have just

[032-110] described, we examine the general aspect of art from the

[032-111] sixteenth to the eighteenth century, we notice the same phenomena

[032-112] of decay and phthisis. Beginning with François II., the

[032-113] architectural form of the edifice effaces itself more and more,

[032-114] and allows the geometrical form, like the bony structure of an

[032-115] emaciated invalid, to become prominent. The fine lines of art

[032-116] give way to the cold and inexorable lines of geometry. An edifice

[032-117] is no longer an edifice; it is a polyhedron. Meanwhile,

[032-118] architecture is tormented in her struggles to conceal this

[032-119] nudity. Look at the Greek pediment inscribed upon the Roman

[032-120] pediment, and vice versâ. It is still the Pantheon on the

[032-121] Parthenon: Saint-Peter’s of Rome. Here are the brick houses of

[032-122] Henri IV., with their stone corners; the Place Royale, the Place

[032-123] Dauphine. Here are the churches of Louis XIII., heavy, squat,

[032-124] thickset, crowded together, loaded with a dome like a hump. Here

[032-125] is the Mazarin architecture, the wretched Italian pasticcio of

[032-126] the Four Nations. Here are the palaces of Louis XIV., long

[032-127] barracks for courtiers, stiff, cold, tiresome. Here, finally, is

[032-128] Louis XV., with chiccory leaves and vermicelli, and all the

[032-129] warts, and all the fungi, which disfigure that decrepit,

[032-130] toothless, and coquettish old architecture. From François II. to

[032-131] Louis XV., the evil has increased in geometrical progression. Art

[032-132] has no longer anything but skin upon its bones. It is miserably

[032-133] perishing.

[032-134] Meanwhile what becomes of printing? All the life which is leaving

[032-135] architecture comes to it. In proportion as architecture ebbs,

[032-136] printing swells and grows. That capital of forces which human

[032-137] thought had been expending in edifices, it henceforth expends in

[032-138] books. Thus, from the sixteenth century onward, the press, raised

[032-139] to the level of decaying architecture, contends with it and kills

[032-140] it. In the seventeenth century it is already sufficiently the

[032-141] sovereign, sufficiently triumphant, sufficiently established in

[032-142] its victory, to give to the world the feast of a great literary

[032-143] century. In the eighteenth, having reposed for a long time at the

[032-144] Court of Louis XIV., it seizes again the old sword of Luther,

[032-145] puts it into the hand of Voltaire, and rushes impetuously to the

[032-146] attack of that ancient Europe, whose architectural expression it

[032-147] has already killed. At the moment when the eighteenth century

[032-148] comes to an end, it has destroyed everything. In the nineteenth,

[032-149] it begins to reconstruct.

[032-150] Now, we ask, which of the three arts has really represented human

[032-151] thought for the last three centuries? which translates it? which

[032-152] expresses not only its literary and scholastic vagaries, but its

[032-153] vast, profound, universal movement? which constantly superposes

[032-154] itself, without a break, without a gap, upon the human race,

[032-155] which walks a monster with a thousand legs?—Architecture or

[032-156] printing?

[032-157] It is printing. Let the reader make no mistake; architecture is

[032-158] dead; irretrievably slain by the printed book,—slain because it

[032-159] endures for a shorter time,—slain because it costs more. Every

[032-160] cathedral represents millions. Let the reader now imagine what an

[032-161] investment of funds it would require to rewrite the architectural

[032-162] book; to cause thousands of edifices to swarm once more upon the

[032-163] soil; to return to those epochs when the throng of monuments was

[032-164] such, according to the statement of an eye witness, “that one

[032-165] would have said that the world in shaking itself, had cast off

[032-166] its old garments in order to cover itself with a white vesture of

[032-167] churches.” _Erat enim ut si mundus, ipse excutiendo semet,

[032-168] rejecta vetustate, candidam ecclesiarum vestem indueret_. (GLABER

[032-169] RADOLPHUS.)

[032-170] A book is so soon made, costs so little, and can go so far! How

[032-171] can it surprise us that all human thought flows in this channel?

[032-172] This does not mean that architecture will not still have a fine

[032-173] monument, an isolated masterpiece, here and there. We may still

[032-174] have from time to time, under the reign of printing, a column

[032-175] made I suppose, by a whole army from melted cannon, as we had

[032-176] under the reign of architecture, Iliads and Romanceros,

[032-177] Mahabâhrata, and Nibelungen Lieds, made by a whole people, with

[032-178] rhapsodies piled up and melted together. The great accident of an

[032-179] architect of genius may happen in the twentieth century, like

[032-180] that of Dante in the thirteenth. But architecture will no longer

[032-181] be the social art, the collective art, the dominating art. The

[032-182] grand poem, the grand edifice, the grand work of humanity will no

[032-183] longer be built: it will be printed.

[032-184] And henceforth, if architecture should arise again accidentally,

[032-185] it will no longer be mistress. It will be subservient to the law

[032-186] of literature, which formerly received the law from it. The

[032-187] respective positions of the two arts will be inverted. It is

[032-188] certain that in architectural epochs, the poems, rare it is true,

[032-189] resemble the monuments. In India, Vyasa is branching, strange,

[032-190] impenetrable as a pagoda. In Egyptian Orient, poetry has like the

[032-191] edifices, grandeur and tranquillity of line; in antique Greece,

[032-192] beauty, serenity, calm; in Christian Europe, the Catholic

[032-193] majesty, the popular naïvete, the rich and luxuriant vegetation

[032-194] of an epoch of renewal. The Bible resembles the Pyramids; the

[032-195] Iliad, the Parthenon; Homer, Phidias. Dante in the thirteenth

[032-196] century is the last Romanesque church; Shakespeare in the

[032-197] sixteenth, the last Gothic cathedral.

[032-198] Thus, to sum up what we have hitherto said, in a fashion which is

[032-199] necessarily incomplete and mutilated, the human race has two

[032-200] books, two registers, two testaments: masonry and printing; the

[032-201] Bible of stone and the Bible of paper. No doubt, when one

[032-202] contemplates these two Bibles, laid so broadly open in the

[032-203] centuries, it is permissible to regret the visible majesty of the

[032-204] writing of granite, those gigantic alphabets formulated in

[032-205] colonnades, in pylons, in obelisks, those sorts of human

[032-206] mountains which cover the world and the past, from the pyramid to

[032-207] the bell tower, from Cheops to Strasbourg. The past must be

[032-208] reread upon these pages of marble. This book, written by

[032-209] architecture, must be admired and perused incessantly; but the

[032-210] grandeur of the edifice which printing erects in its turn must

[032-211] not be denied.
