---[051-001] “Here is the wherewithal to pay.”

[051-002] Phœbus felt the stranger’s cold hand slip into his a large piece

[051-003] of money. He could not refrain from taking the money and pressing

[051-004] the hand.

[051-005] “_Vrai Dieu!_” he exclaimed, “you are a good fellow!”

[051-006] “One condition,” said the man. “Prove to me that I have been

[051-007] wrong and that you were speaking the truth. Hide me in some

[051-008] corner whence I can see whether this woman is really the one

[051-009] whose name you uttered.”

[051-010] “Oh!” replied Phœbus, “’tis all one to me. We will take, the

[051-011] Sainte-Marthe chamber; you can look at your ease from the kennel

[051-012] hard by.”

[051-013] “Come then,” said the shadow.

[051-014] “At your service,” said the captain, “I know not whether you are

[051-015] Messer Diavolus in person; but let us be good friends for this

[051-016] evening; to-morrow I will repay you all my debts, both of purse

[051-017] and sword.”

[051-018] They set out again at a rapid pace. At the expiration of a few

[051-019] minutes, the sound of the river announced to them that they were

[051-020] on the Pont Saint-Michel, then loaded with houses.

[051-021] “I will first show you the way,” said Phœbus to his companion, “I

[051-022] will then go in search of the fair one who is awaiting me near

[051-023] the Petit-Châtelet.”

[051-024] His companion made no reply; he had not uttered a word since they

[051-025] had been walking side by side. Phœbus halted before a low door,

[051-026] and knocked roughly; a light made its appearance through the

[051-027] cracks of the door.

[051-028] “Who is there?” cried a toothless voice.

[051-029] “_Corps-Dieu! Tête-Dieu! Ventre-Dieu!_” replied the captain.

[051-030] The door opened instantly, and allowed the new-comers to see an

[051-031] old woman and an old lamp, both of which trembled. The old woman

[051-032] was bent double, clad in tatters, with a shaking head, pierced

[051-033] with two small eyes, and coiffed with a dish clout; wrinkled

[051-034] everywhere, on hands and face and neck; her lips retreated under

[051-035] her gums, and about her mouth she had tufts of white hairs which

[051-036] gave her the whiskered look of a cat.

[051-037] The interior of the den was no less dilapitated than she; there

[051-038] were chalk walls, blackened beams in the ceiling, a dismantled

[051-039] chimney-piece, spiders’ webs in all the corners, in the middle a

[051-040] staggering herd of tables and lame stools, a dirty child among

[051-041] the ashes, and at the back a staircase, or rather, a wooden

[051-042] ladder, which ended in a trapdoor in the ceiling.

[051-043] On entering this lair, Phœbus’s mysterious companion raised his

[051-044] mantle to his very eyes. Meanwhile, the captain, swearing like a

[051-045] Saracen, hastened to “make the sun shine in a crown” as saith our

[051-046] admirable Régnier.

[051-047] “The Sainte-Marthe chamber,” said he.

[051-048] The old woman addressed him as monseigneur, and shut up the crown

[051-049] in a drawer. It was the coin which the man in the black mantle

[051-050] had given to Phœbus. While her back was turned, the bushy-headed

[051-051] and ragged little boy who was playing in the ashes, adroitly

[051-052] approached the drawer, abstracted the crown, and put in its place

[051-053] a dry leaf which he had plucked from a fagot.

[051-054] The old crone made a sign to the two gentlemen, as she called

[051-055] them, to follow her, and mounted the ladder in advance of them.

[051-056] On arriving at the upper story, she set her lamp on a coffer,

[051-057] and, Phœbus, like a frequent visitor of the house, opened a door

[051-058] which opened on a dark hole. “Enter here, my dear fellow,” he

[051-059] said to his companion. The man in the mantle obeyed without a

[051-060] word in reply, the door closed upon him; he heard Phœbus bolt it,

[051-061] and a moment later descend the stairs again with the aged hag.

[051-062] The light had disappeared.

[051-063] CHAPTER VIII. THE UTILITY OF WINDOWS WHICH OPEN ON THE RIVER.

[051-064] Claude Frollo (for we presume that the reader, more intelligent

[051-065] than Phœbus, has seen in this whole adventure no other surly monk

[051-066] than the archdeacon), Claude Frollo groped about for several

[051-067] moments in the dark lair into which the captain had bolted him.

[051-068] It was one of those nooks which architects sometimes reserve at

[051-069] the point of junction between the roof and the supporting wall. A

[051-070] vertical section of this kennel, as Phœbus had so justly styled

[051-071] it, would have made a triangle. Moreover, there was neither

[051-072] window nor air-hole, and the slope of the roof prevented one from

[051-073] standing upright. Accordingly, Claude crouched down in the dust,

[051-074] and the plaster which cracked beneath him; his head was on fire;

[051-075] rummaging around him with his hands, he found on the floor a bit

[051-076] of broken glass, which he pressed to his brow, and whose coolness

[051-077] afforded him some relief.

[051-078] What was taking place at that moment in the gloomy soul of the

[051-079] archdeacon? God and himself could alone know.

[051-080] In what order was he arranging in his mind la Esmeralda, Phœbus,

[051-081] Jacques Charmolue, his young brother so beloved, yet abandoned by

[051-082] him in the mire, his archdeacon’s cassock, his reputation perhaps

[051-083] dragged to la Falourdel’s, all these adventures, all these

[051-084] images? I cannot say. But it is certain that these ideas formed

[051-085] in his mind a horrible group.

[051-086] He had been waiting a quarter of an hour; it seemed to him that

[051-087] he had grown a century older. All at once he heard the creaking

[051-088] of the boards of the stairway; some one was ascending. The

[051-089] trapdoor opened once more; a light reappeared. There was a

[051-090] tolerably large crack in the worm-eaten door of his den; he put

[051-091] his face to it. In this manner he could see all that went on in

[051-092] the adjoining room. The cat-faced old crone was the first to

[051-093] emerge from the trap-door, lamp in hand; then Phœbus, twirling

[051-094] his moustache, then a third person, that beautiful and graceful

[051-095] figure, la Esmeralda. The priest beheld her rise from below like

[051-096] a dazzling apparition. Claude trembled, a cloud spread over his

[051-097] eyes, his pulses beat violently, everything rustled and whirled

[051-098] around him; he no longer saw nor heard anything.

[051-099] When he recovered himself, Phœbus and Esmeralda were alone seated

[051-100] on the wooden coffer beside the lamp which made these two

[051-101] youthful figures and a miserable pallet at the end of the attic

[051-102] stand out plainly before the archdeacon’s eyes.

[051-103] Beside the pallet was a window, whose panes broken like a

[051-104] spider’s web upon which rain has fallen, allowed a view, through

[051-105] its rent meshes, of a corner of the sky, and the moon lying far

[051-106] away on an eiderdown bed of soft clouds.

[051-107] The young girl was blushing, confused, palpitating. Her long,

[051-108] drooping lashes shaded her crimson cheeks. The officer, to whom

[051-109] she dared not lift her eyes, was radiant. Mechanically, and with

[051-110] a charmingly unconscious gesture, she traced with the tip of her

[051-111] finger incoherent lines on the bench, and watched her finger. Her

[051-112] foot was not visible. The little goat was nestling upon it.

[051-113] The captain was very gallantly clad; he had tufts of embroidery

[051-114] at his neck and wrists; a great elegance at that day.

[051-115] It was not without difficulty that Dom Claude managed to hear

[051-116] what they were saying, through the humming of the blood, which

[051-117] was boiling in his temples.

[051-118] (A conversation between lovers is a very commonplace affair. It

[051-119] is a perpetual “I love you.” A musical phrase which is very

[051-120] insipid and very bald for indifferent listeners, when it is not

[051-121] ornamented with some _fioriture;_ but Claude was not an

[051-122] indifferent listener.)

[051-123] “Oh!” said the young girl, without raising her eyes, “do not

[051-124] despise me, monseigneur Phœbus. I feel that what I am doing is

[051-125] not right.”

[051-126] “Despise you, my pretty child!” replied the officer with an air

[051-127] of superior and distinguished gallantry, “despise you,

[051-128] _tête-Dieu!_ and why?”

[051-129] “For having followed you!”

[051-130] “On that point, my beauty, we don’t agree. I ought not to despise

[051-131] you, but to hate you.”

[051-132] The young girl looked at him in affright: “Hate me! what have I

[051-133] done?”

[051-134] “For having required so much urging.”

[051-135] “Alas!” said she, “’tis because I am breaking a vow. I shall not

[051-136] find my parents! The amulet will lose its virtue. But what

[051-137] matters it? What need have I of father or mother now?”

[051-138] So saying, she fixed upon the captain her great black eyes, moist

[051-139] with joy and tenderness.

[051-140] “Devil take me if I understand you!” exclaimed Phœbus. La

[051-141] Esmeralda remained silent for a moment, then a tear dropped from

[051-142] her eyes, a sigh from her lips, and she said,—“Oh! monseigneur, I

[051-143] love you.”

[051-144] Such a perfume of chastity, such a charm of virtue surrounded the

[051-145] young girl, that Phœbus did not feel completely at his ease

[051-146] beside her. But this remark emboldened him: “You love me!” he

[051-147] said with rapture, and he threw his arm round the gypsy’s waist.

[051-148] He had only been waiting for this opportunity.

[051-149] The priest saw it, and tested with the tip of his finger the

[051-150] point of a poniard which he wore concealed in his breast.

[051-151] “Phœbus,” continued the Bohemian, gently releasing her waist from

[051-152] the captain’s tenacious hands, “You are good, you are generous,

[051-153] you are handsome; you saved me, me who am only a poor child lost

[051-154] in Bohemia. I had long been dreaming of an officer who should

[051-155] save my life. ’Twas of you that I was dreaming, before I knew

[051-156] you, my Phœbus; the officer of my dream had a beautiful uniform

[051-157] like yours, a grand look, a sword; your name is Phœbus; ’tis a

[051-158] beautiful name. I love your name; I love your sword. Draw your

[051-159] sword, Phœbus, that I may see it.”

[051-160] “Child!” said the captain, and he unsheathed his sword with a

[051-161] smile.

[051-162] The gypsy looked at the hilt, the blade; examined the cipher on

[051-163] the guard with adorable curiosity, and kissed the sword, saying,—

[051-164] “You are the sword of a brave man. I love my captain.” Phœbus

[051-165] again profited by the opportunity to impress upon her beautiful

[051-166] bent neck a kiss which made the young girl straighten herself up

[051-167] as scarlet as a poppy. The priest gnashed his teeth over it in

[051-168] the dark.

[051-169] “Phœbus,” resumed the gypsy, “let me talk to you. Pray walk a

[051-170] little, that I may see you at full height, and that I may hear

[051-171] your spurs jingle. How handsome you are!”

[051-172] The captain rose to please her, chiding her with a smile of

[051-173] satisfaction,—

[051-174] “What a child you are! By the way, my charmer, have you seen me

[051-175] in my archer’s ceremonial doublet?”

[051-176] “Alas! no,” she replied.

[051-177] “It is very handsome!”

[051-178] Phœbus returned and seated himself beside her, but much closer

[051-179] than before.

[051-180] “Listen, my dear—”

[051-181] The gypsy gave him several little taps with her pretty hand on

[051-182] his mouth, with a childish mirth and grace and gayety.

[051-183] “No, no, I will not listen to you. Do you love me? I want you to

[051-184] tell me whether you love me.”

[051-185] “Do I love thee, angel of my life!” exclaimed the captain, half

[051-186] kneeling. “My body, my blood, my soul, all are thine; all are for

[051-187] thee. I love thee, and I have never loved any one but thee.”

[051-188] The captain had repeated this phrase so many times, in many

[051-189] similar conjunctures, that he delivered it all in one breath,

[051-190] without committing a single mistake. At this passionate

[051-191] declaration, the gypsy raised to the dirty ceiling which served

[051-192] for the skies a glance full of angelic happiness.

[051-193] “Oh!” she murmured, “this is the moment when one should die!”

[051-194] Phœbus found “the moment” favorable for robbing her of another

[051-195] kiss, which went to torture the unhappy archdeacon in his nook.

[051-196] “Die!” exclaimed the amorous captain, “What are you saying, my

[051-197] lovely angel? ’Tis a time for living, or Jupiter is only a scamp!

[051-198] Die at the beginning of so sweet a thing! _Corne-de-bœuf_, what a

[051-199] jest! It is not that. Listen, my dear Similar, Esmenarda—Pardon!

[051-200] you have so prodigiously Saracen a name that I never can get it

[051-201] straight. ’Tis a thicket which stops me short.”

[051-202] “Good heavens!” said the poor girl, “and I thought my name pretty

[051-203] because of its singularity! But since it displeases you, I would

[051-204] that I were called Goton.”
