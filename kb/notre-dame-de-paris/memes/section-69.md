---[069-001] “And who the devil,” resumed the poet, “has amused himself with

[069-002] soliciting a decree of reintegration? Why couldn’t they leave

[069-003] parliament in peace? What harm does it do if a poor girl takes

[069-004] shelter under the flying buttresses of Notre-Dame, beside the

[069-005] swallows’ nests?”

[069-006] “There are satans in this world,” remarked the archdeacon.

[069-007] “’Tis devilish badly done,” observed Gringoire.

[069-008] The archdeacon resumed after a silence,—

[069-009] “So, she saved your life?”

[069-010] “Among my good friends the outcasts. A little more or a little

[069-011] less and I should have been hanged. They would have been sorry

[069-012] for it to-day.”

[069-013] “Would not you like to do something for her?”

[069-014] “I ask nothing better, Dom Claude; but what if I entangle myself

[069-015] in some villanous affair?”

[069-016] “What matters it?”

[069-017] “Bah! what matters it? You are good, master, that you are! I have

[069-018] two great works already begun.”

[069-019] The priest smote his brow. In spite of the calm which he

[069-020] affected, a violent gesture betrayed his internal convulsions

[069-021] from time to time.

[069-022] “How is she to be saved?”

[069-023] Gringoire said to him; “Master, I will reply to you; _Il padelt_,

[069-024] which means in Turkish, ‘God is our hope.’”

[069-025] “How is she to be saved?” repeated Claude dreamily.

[069-026] Gringoire smote his brow in his turn.

[069-027] “Listen, master. I have imagination; I will devise expedients for

[069-028] you. What if one were to ask her pardon from the king?”

[069-029] “Of Louis XI.! A pardon!”

[069-030] “Why not?”

[069-031] “To take the tiger’s bone from him!”

[069-032] Gringoire began to seek fresh expedients.

[069-033] “Well, stay! Shall I address to the midwives a request

[069-034] accompanied by the declaration that the girl is with child!”

[069-035] This made the priest’s hollow eye flash.

[069-036] “With child! knave! do you know anything of this?”

[069-037] Gringoire was alarmed by his air. He hastened to say, “Oh, no,

[069-038] not I! Our marriage was a real _forismaritagium_. I stayed

[069-039] outside. But one might obtain a respite, all the same.”

[069-040] “Madness! Infamy! Hold your tongue!”

[069-041] “You do wrong to get angry,” muttered Gringoire. “One obtains a

[069-042] respite; that does no harm to any one, and allows the midwives,

[069-043] who are poor women, to earn forty deniers parisis.”

[069-044] The priest was not listening to him!

[069-045] “But she must leave that place, nevertheless!” he murmured, “the

[069-046] decree is to be executed within three days. Moreover, there will

[069-047] be no decree; that Quasimodo! Women have very depraved tastes!”

[069-048] He raised his voice: “Master Pierre, I have reflected well; there

[069-049] is but one means of safety for her.”

[069-050] “What? I see none myself.”

[069-051] “Listen, Master Pierre, remember that you owe your life to her. I

[069-052] will tell you my idea frankly. The church is watched night and

[069-053] day; only those are allowed to come out, who have been seen to

[069-054] enter. Hence you can enter. You will come. I will lead you to

[069-055] her. You will change clothes with her. She will take your

[069-056] doublet; you will take her petticoat.”

[069-057] “So far, it goes well,” remarked the philosopher, “and then?”

[069-058] “And then? she will go forth in your garments; you will remain

[069-059] with hers. You will be hanged, perhaps, but she will be saved.”

[069-060] Gringoire scratched his ear, with a very serious air. “Stay!”

[069-061] said he, “that is an idea which would never have occurred to me

[069-062] unaided.”

[069-063] At Dom Claude’s proposition, the open and benign face of the poet

[069-064] had abruptly clouded over, like a smiling Italian landscape, when

[069-065] an unlucky squall comes up and dashes a cloud across the sun.

[069-066] “Well! Gringoire, what say you to the means?”

[069-067] “I say, master, that I shall not be hanged, perchance, but that I

[069-068] shall be hanged indubitably.

[069-069] “That concerns us not.”

[069-070] “The deuce!” said Gringoire.

[069-071] “She has saved your life. ’Tis a debt that you are discharging.”

[069-072] “There are a great many others which I do not discharge.”

[069-073] “Master Pierre, it is absolutely necessary.”

[069-074] The archdeacon spoke imperiously.

[069-075] “Listen, Dom Claude,” replied the poet in utter consternation.

[069-076] “You cling to that idea, and you are wrong. I do not see why I

[069-077] should get myself hanged in some one else’s place.”

[069-078] “What have you, then, which attaches you so strongly to life?”

[069-079] “Oh! a thousand reasons!”

[069-080] “What reasons, if you please?”

[069-081] “What? The air, the sky, the morning, the evening, the moonlight,

[069-082] my good friends the thieves, our jeers with the old hags of

[069-083] go-betweens, the fine architecture of Paris to study, three great

[069-084] books to make, one of them being against the bishops and his

[069-085] mills; and how can I tell all? Anaxagoras said that he was in the

[069-086] world to admire the sun. And then, from morning till night, I

[069-087] have the happiness of passing all my days with a man of genius,

[069-088] who is myself, which is very agreeable.”

[069-089] “A head fit for a mule bell!” muttered the archdeacon. “Oh! tell

[069-090] me who preserved for you that life which you render so charming

[069-091] to yourself? To whom do you owe it that you breathe that air,

[069-092] behold that sky, and can still amuse your lark’s mind with your

[069-093] whimsical nonsense and madness? Where would you be, had it not

[069-094] been for her? Do you then desire that she through whom you are

[069-095] alive, should die? that she should die, that beautiful, sweet,

[069-096] adorable creature, who is necessary to the light of the world and

[069-097] more divine than God, while you, half wise, and half fool, a vain

[069-098] sketch of something, a sort of vegetable, which thinks that it

[069-099] walks, and thinks that it thinks, you will continue to live with

[069-100] the life which you have stolen from her, as useless as a candle

[069-101] in broad daylight? Come, have a little pity, Gringoire; be

[069-102] generous in your turn; it was she who set the example.”

[069-103] The priest was vehement. Gringoire listened to him at first with

[069-104] an undecided air, then he became touched, and wound up with a

[069-105] grimace which made his pallid face resemble that of a new-born

[069-106] infant with an attack of the colic.

[069-107] “You are pathetic!” said he, wiping away a tear. “Well! I will

[069-108] think about it. That’s a queer idea of yours.—After all,” he

[069-109] continued after a pause, “who knows? perhaps they will not hang

[069-110] me. He who becomes betrothed does not always marry. When they

[069-111] find me in that little lodging so grotesquely muffled in

[069-112] petticoat and coif, perchance they will burst with laughter. And

[069-113] then, if they do hang me,—well! the halter is as good a death as

[069-114] any. ’Tis a death worthy of a sage who has wavered all his life;

[069-115] a death which is neither flesh nor fish, like the mind of a

[069-116] veritable sceptic; a death all stamped with Pyrrhonism and

[069-117] hesitation, which holds the middle station betwixt heaven and

[069-118] earth, which leaves you in suspense. ’Tis a philosopher’s death,

[069-119] and I was destined thereto, perchance. It is magnificent to die

[069-120] as one has lived.”

[069-121] The priest interrupted him: “Is it agreed.”

[069-122] “What is death, after all?” pursued Gringoire with exaltation. “A

[069-123] disagreeable moment, a toll-gate, the passage of little to

[069-124] nothingness. Some one having asked Cercidas, the Megalopolitan,

[069-125] if he were willing to die: ‘Why not?’ he replied; ‘for after my

[069-126] death I shall see those great men, Pythagoras among the

[069-127] philosophers, Hecatæus among historians, Homer among poets,

[069-128] Olympus among musicians.’”

[069-129] The archdeacon gave him his hand: “It is settled, then? You will

[069-130] come to-morrow?”

[069-131] This gesture recalled Gringoire to reality.

[069-132] “Ah! i’ faith no!” he said in the tone of a man just waking up.

[069-133] “Be hanged! ’tis too absurd. I will not.”

[069-134] “Farewell, then!” and the archdeacon added between his teeth:

[069-135] “I’ll find you again!”

[069-136] “I do not want that devil of a man to find me,” thought

[069-137] Gringoire; and he ran after Dom Claude. “Stay, monsieur the

[069-138] archdeacon, no ill-feeling between old friends! You take an

[069-139] interest in that girl, my wife, I mean, and ’tis well. You have

[069-140] devised a scheme to get her out of Notre-Dame, but your way is

[069-141] extremely disagreeable to me, Gringoire. If I had only another

[069-142] one myself! I beg to say that a luminous inspiration has just

[069-143] occurred to me. If I possessed an expedient for extricating her

[069-144] from a dilemma, without compromising my own neck to the extent of

[069-145] a single running knot, what would you say to it? Will not that

[069-146] suffice you? Is it absolutely necessary that I should be hanged,

[069-147] in order that you may be content?”

[069-148] The priest tore out the buttons of his cassock with impatience:

[069-149] “Stream of words! What is your plan?”

[069-150] “Yes,” resumed Gringoire, talking to himself and touching his

[069-151] nose with his forefinger in sign of meditation,—“that’s it!—The

[069-152] thieves are brave fellows!—The tribe of Egypt love her!—They will

[069-153] rise at the first word!—Nothing easier!—A sudden stroke.—Under

[069-154] cover of the disorder, they will easily carry her off!—Beginning

[069-155] to-morrow evening. They will ask nothing better.

[069-156] “The plan! speak,” cried the archdeacon shaking him.

[069-157] Gringoire turned majestically towards him: “Leave me! You see

[069-158] that I am composing.” He meditated for a few moments more, then

[069-159] began to clap his hands over his thought, crying: “Admirable!

[069-160] success is sure!”

[069-161] “The plan!” repeated Claude in wrath.

[069-162] Gringoire was radiant.

[069-163] “Come, that I may tell you that very softly. ’Tis a truly gallant

[069-164] counter-plot, which will extricate us all from the matter.

[069-165] Pardieu, it must be admitted that I am no fool.”

[069-166] He broke off.

[069-167] “Oh, by the way! is the little goat with the wench?”

[069-168] “Yes. The devil take you!”

[069-169] “They would have hanged it also, would they not?”

[069-170] “What is that to me?”

[069-171] “Yes, they would have hanged it. They hanged a sow last month.

[069-172] The headsman loveth that; he eats the beast afterwards. Take my

[069-173] pretty Djali! Poor little lamb!”

[069-174] “Malediction!” exclaimed Dom Claude. “You are the executioner.

[069-175] What means of safety have you found, knave? Must your idea be

[069-176] extracted with the forceps?”

[069-177] “Very fine, master, this is it.”

[069-178] Gringoire bent his head to the archdeacon’s head and spoke to him

[069-179] in a very low voice, casting an uneasy glance the while from one

[069-180] end to the other of the street, though no one was passing. When

[069-181] he had finished, Dom Claude took his hand and said coldly: “’Tis

[069-182] well. Farewell until to-morrow.”

[069-183] “Until to-morrow,” repeated Gringoire. And, while the archdeacon

[069-184] was disappearing in one direction, he set off in the other,

[069-185] saying to himself in a low voice: “Here’s a grand affair,

[069-186] Monsieur Pierre Gringoire. Never mind! ’Tis not written that

[069-187] because one is of small account one should take fright at a great

[069-188] enterprise. Bitou carried a great bull on his shoulders; the

[069-189] water-wagtails, the warblers, and the buntings traverse the

[069-190] ocean.”

[069-191] CHAPTER II. TURN VAGABOND.

[069-192] On re-entering the cloister, the archdeacon found at the door of

[069-193] his cell his brother Jehan du Moulin, who was waiting for him,

[069-194] and who had beguiled the tedium of waiting by drawing on the wall

[069-195] with a bit of charcoal, a profile of his elder brother, enriched

[069-196] with a monstrous nose.

[069-197] Dom Claude hardly looked at his brother; his thoughts were

[069-198] elsewhere. That merry scamp’s face whose beaming had so often

[069-199] restored serenity to the priest’s sombre physiognomy, was now

[069-200] powerless to melt the gloom which grew more dense every day over

[069-201] that corrupted, mephitic, and stagnant soul.

[069-202] “Brother,” said Jehan timidly, “I am come to see you.”

[069-203] The archdeacon did not even raise his eyes.

[069-204] “What then?”

[069-205] “Brother,” resumed the hypocrite, “you are so good to me, and you

[069-206] give me such wise counsels that I always return to you.”

[069-207] “What next?”

[069-208] “Alas! brother, you were perfectly right when you said to

[069-209] me,—“Jehan! Jehan! _cessat doctorum doctrina, discipulorum

[069-210] disciplina_. Jehan, be wise, Jehan, be learned, Jehan, pass not

[069-211] the night outside of the college without lawful occasion and due

[069-212] leave of the master. Cudgel not the Picards: _noli, Joannes,

[069-213] verberare Picardos_. Rot not like an unlettered ass, _quasi

[069-214] asinus illitteratus_, on the straw seats of the school. Jehan,

[069-215] allow yourself to be punished at the discretion of the master.

[069-216] Jehan go every evening to chapel, and sing there an anthem with

[069-217] verse and orison to Madame the glorious Virgin Mary.”—Alas! what

[069-218] excellent advice was that!”
