---[087-001] At that moment, the mother who was crouching on the pavement,

[087-002] opened her eyes wide. Without uttering a cry, she raised herself

[087-003] erect with a terrible expression; then she flung herself upon the

[087-004] hand of the executioner, like a beast on its prey, and bit it. It

[087-005] was done like a flash of lightning. The headsman howled with

[087-006] pain. Those near by rushed up. With difficulty they withdrew his

[087-007] bleeding hand from the mother’s teeth. She preserved a profound

[087-008] silence. They thrust her back with much brutality, and noticed

[087-009] that her head fell heavily on the pavement. They raised her, she

[087-010] fell back again. She was dead.

[087-011] The executioner, who had not loosed his hold on the young girl,

[087-012] began to ascend the ladder once more.

[087-013] CHAPTER II. THE BEAUTIFUL CREATURE CLAD IN WHITE. (Dante.)

[087-014] When Quasimodo saw that the cell was empty, that the gypsy was no

[087-015] longer there, that while he had been defending her she had been

[087-016] abducted, he grasped his hair with both hands and stamped with

[087-017] surprise and pain; then he set out to run through the entire

[087-018] church seeking his Bohemian, howling strange cries to all the

[087-019] corners of the walls, strewing his red hair on the pavement. It

[087-020] was just at the moment when the king’s archers were making their

[087-021] victorious entrance into Notre-Dame, also in search of the gypsy.

[087-022] Quasimodo, poor, deaf fellow, aided them in their fatal

[087-023] intentions, without suspecting it; he thought that the outcasts

[087-024] were the gypsy’s enemies. He himself conducted Tristan l’Hermite

[087-025] to all possible hiding-places, opened to him the secret doors,

[087-026] the double bottoms of the altars, the rear sacristries. If the

[087-027] unfortunate girl had still been there, it would have been he

[087-028] himself who would have delivered her up.

[087-029] When the fatigue of finding nothing had disheartened Tristan, who

[087-030] was not easily discouraged, Quasimodo continued the search alone.

[087-031] He made the tour of the church twenty times, length and breadth,

[087-032] up and down, ascending and descending, running, calling,

[087-033] shouting, peeping, rummaging, ransacking, thrusting his head into

[087-034] every hole, pushing a torch under every vault, despairing, mad. A

[087-035] male who has lost his female is no more roaring nor more haggard.

[087-036] At last when he was sure, perfectly sure that she was no longer

[087-037] there, that all was at an end, that she had been snatched from

[087-038] him, he slowly mounted the staircase to the towers, that

[087-039] staircase which he had ascended with so much eagerness and

[087-040] triumph on the day when he had saved her. He passed those same

[087-041] places once more with drooping head, voiceless, tearless, almost

[087-042] breathless. The church was again deserted, and had fallen back

[087-043] into its silence. The archers had quitted it to track the

[087-044] sorceress in the city. Quasimodo, left alone in that vast

[087-045] Notre-Dame, so besieged and tumultuous but a short time before,

[087-046] once more betook himself to the cell where the gypsy had slept

[087-047] for so many weeks under his guardianship.

[087-048] As he approached it, he fancied that he might, perhaps, find her

[087-049] there. When, at the turn of the gallery which opens on the roof

[087-050] of the side aisles, he perceived the tiny cell with its little

[087-051] window and its little door crouching beneath a great flying

[087-052] buttress like a bird’s nest under a branch, the poor man’s heart

[087-053] failed him, and he leaned against a pillar to keep from falling.

[087-054] He imagined that she might have returned thither, that some good

[087-055] genius had, no doubt, brought her back, that this chamber was too

[087-056] tranquil, too safe, too charming for her not to be there, and he

[087-057] dared not take another step for fear of destroying his illusion.

[087-058] “Yes,” he said to himself, “perchance she is sleeping, or

[087-059] praying. I must not disturb her.”

[087-060] At length he summoned up courage, advanced on tiptoe, looked,

[087-061] entered. Empty. The cell was still empty. The unhappy deaf man

[087-062] walked slowly round it, lifted the bed and looked beneath it, as

[087-063] though she might be concealed between the pavement and the

[087-064] mattress, then he shook his head and remained stupefied. All at

[087-065] once, he crushed his torch under his foot, and, without uttering

[087-066] a word, without giving vent to a sigh, he flung himself at full

[087-067] speed, head foremost against the wall, and fell fainting on the

[087-068] floor.

[087-069] When he recovered his senses, he threw himself on the bed and

[087-070] rolling about, he kissed frantically the place where the young

[087-071] girl had slept and which was still warm; he remained there for

[087-072] several moments as motionless as though he were about to expire;

[087-073] then he rose, dripping with perspiration, panting, mad, and began

[087-074] to beat his head against the wall with the frightful regularity

[087-075] of the clapper of his bells, and the resolution of a man

[087-076] determined to kill himself. At length he fell a second time,

[087-077] exhausted; he dragged himself on his knees outside the cell, and

[087-078] crouched down facing the door, in an attitude of astonishment.

[087-079] He remained thus for more than an hour without making a movement,

[087-080] with his eye fixed on the deserted cell, more gloomy, and more

[087-081] pensive than a mother seated between an empty cradle and a full

[087-082] coffin. He uttered not a word; only at long intervals, a sob

[087-083] heaved his body violently, but it was a tearless sob, like summer

[087-084] lightning which makes no noise.

[087-085] It appears to have been then, that, seeking at the bottom of his

[087-086] lonely thoughts for the unexpected abductor of the gypsy, he

[087-087] thought of the archdeacon. He remembered that Dom Claude alone

[087-088] possessed a key to the staircase leading to the cell; he recalled

[087-089] his nocturnal attempts on the young girl, in the first of which

[087-090] he, Quasimodo, had assisted, the second of which he had

[087-091] prevented. He recalled a thousand details, and soon he no longer

[087-092] doubted that the archdeacon had taken the gypsy. Nevertheless,

[087-093] such was his respect for the priest, such his gratitude, his

[087-094] devotion, his love for this man had taken such deep root in his

[087-095] heart, that they resisted, even at this moment, the talons of

[087-096] jealousy and despair.

[087-097] He reflected that the archdeacon had done this thing, and the

[087-098] wrath of blood and death which it would have evoked in him

[087-099] against any other person, turned in the poor deaf man, from the

[087-100] moment when Claude Frollo was in question, into an increase of

[087-101] grief and sorrow.

[087-102] At the moment when his thought was thus fixed upon the priest,

[087-103] while the daybreak was whitening the flying buttresses, he

[087-104] perceived on the highest story of Notre-Dame, at the angle formed

[087-105] by the external balustrade as it makes the turn of the chancel, a

[087-106] figure walking. This figure was coming towards him. He recognized

[087-107] it. It was the archdeacon.

[087-108] Claude was walking with a slow, grave step. He did not look

[087-109] before him as he walked, he was directing his course towards the

[087-110] northern tower, but his face was turned aside towards the right

[087-111] bank of the Seine, and he held his head high, as though trying to

[087-112] see something over the roofs. The owl often assumes this oblique

[087-113] attitude. It flies towards one point and looks towards another.

[087-114] In this manner the priest passed above Quasimodo without seeing

[087-115] him.

[087-116] The deaf man, who had been petrified by this sudden apparition,

[087-117] beheld him disappear through the door of the staircase to the

[087-118] north tower. The reader is aware that this is the tower from

[087-119] which the Hôtel-de-Ville is visible. Quasimodo rose and followed

[087-120] the archdeacon.

[087-121] Quasimodo ascended the tower staircase for the sake of ascending

[087-122] it, for the sake of seeing why the priest was ascending it.

[087-123] Moreover, the poor bellringer did not know what he (Quasimodo)

[087-124] should do, what he should say, what he wished. He was full of

[087-125] fury and full of fear. The archdeacon and the gypsy had come into

[087-126] conflict in his heart.

[087-127] When he reached the summit of the tower, before emerging from the

[087-128] shadow of the staircase and stepping upon the platform, he

[087-129] cautiously examined the position of the priest. The priest’s back

[087-130] was turned to him. There is an openwork balustrade which

[087-131] surrounds the platform of the bell tower. The priest, whose eyes

[087-132] looked down upon the town, was resting his breast on that one of

[087-133] the four sides of the balustrades which looks upon the Pont

[087-134] Notre-Dame.

[087-135] Quasimodo, advancing with the tread of a wolf behind him, went to

[087-136] see what he was gazing at thus.

[087-137] The priest’s attention was so absorbed elsewhere that he did not

[087-138] hear the deaf man walking behind him.

[087-139] Paris is a magnificent and charming spectacle, and especially at

[087-140] that day, viewed from the top of the towers of Notre-Dame, in the

[087-141] fresh light of a summer dawn. The day might have been in July.

[087-142] The sky was perfectly serene. Some tardy stars were fading away

[087-143] at various points, and there was a very brilliant one in the

[087-144] east, in the brightest part of the heavens. The sun was about to

[087-145] appear; Paris was beginning to move. A very white and very pure

[087-146] light brought out vividly to the eye all the outlines that its

[087-147] thousands of houses present to the east. The giant shadow of the

[087-148] towers leaped from roof to roof, from one end of the great city

[087-149] to the other. There were several quarters from which were already

[087-150] heard voices and noisy sounds. Here the stroke of a bell, there

[087-151] the stroke of a hammer, beyond, the complicated clatter of a cart

[087-152] in motion.

[087-153] Already several columns of smoke were being belched forth from

[087-154] the chimneys scattered over the whole surface of roofs, as

[087-155] through the fissures of an immense sulphurous crater. The river,

[087-156] which ruffles its waters against the arches of so many bridges,

[087-157] against the points of so many islands, was wavering with silvery

[087-158] folds. Around the city, outside the ramparts, sight was lost in a

[087-159] great circle of fleecy vapors through which one confusedly

[087-160] distinguished the indefinite line of the plains, and the graceful

[087-161] swell of the heights. All sorts of floating sounds were dispersed

[087-162] over this half-awakened city. Towards the east, the morning

[087-163] breeze chased a few soft white bits of wool torn from the misty

[087-164] fleece of the hills.

[087-165] In the Parvis, some good women, who had their milk jugs in their

[087-166] hands, were pointing out to each other, with astonishment, the

[087-167] singular dilapidation of the great door of Notre-Dame, and the

[087-168] two solidified streams of lead in the crevices of the stone. This

[087-169] was all that remained of the tempest of the night. The bonfire

[087-170] lighted between the towers by Quasimodo had died out. Tristan had

[087-171] already cleared up the Place, and had the dead thrown into the

[087-172] Seine. Kings like Louis XI. are careful to clean the pavement

[087-173] quickly after a massacre.

[087-174] Outside the balustrade of the tower, directly under the point

[087-175] where the priest had paused, there was one of those fantastically

[087-176] carved stone gutters with which Gothic edifices bristle, and, in

[087-177] a crevice of that gutter, two pretty wallflowers in blossom,

[087-178] shaken out and vivified, as it were, by the breath of air, made

[087-179] frolicsome salutations to each other. Above the towers, on high,

[087-180] far away in the depths of the sky, the cries of little birds were

[087-181] heard.

[087-182] But the priest was not listening to, was not looking at, anything

[087-183] of all this. He was one of the men for whom there are no

[087-184] mornings, no birds, no flowers. In that immense horizon, which

[087-185] assumed so many aspects about him, his contemplation was

[087-186] concentrated on a single point.

[087-187] Quasimodo was burning to ask him what he had done with the gypsy;

[087-188] but the archdeacon seemed to be out of the world at that moment.

[087-189] He was evidently in one of those violent moments of life when one

[087-190] would not feel the earth crumble. He remained motionless and

[087-191] silent, with his eyes steadily fixed on a certain point; and

[087-192] there was something so terrible about this silence and immobility

[087-193] that the savage bellringer shuddered before it and dared not come

[087-194] in contact with it. Only, and this was also one way of

[087-195] interrogating the archdeacon, he followed the direction of his

[087-196] vision, and in this way the glance of the unhappy deaf man fell

[087-197] upon the Place de Grève.
