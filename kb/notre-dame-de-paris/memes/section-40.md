---[040-001] He began by depositing on an angle of the pillory a black

[040-002] hour-glass, the upper lobe of which was filled with red sand,

[040-003] which it allowed to glide into the lower receptacle; then he

[040-004] removed his parti-colored surtout, and there became visible,

[040-005] suspended from his right hand, a thin and tapering whip of long,

[040-006] white, shining, knotted, plaited thongs, armed with metal nails.

[040-007] With his left hand, he negligently folded back his shirt around

[040-008] his right arm, to the very armpit.

[040-009] In the meantime, Jehan Frollo, elevating his curly blonde head

[040-010] above the crowd (he had mounted upon the shoulders of Robin

[040-011] Poussepain for the purpose), shouted: “Come and look, gentle

[040-012] ladies and men! they are going to peremptorily flagellate Master

[040-013] Quasimodo, the bellringer of my brother, monsieur the archdeacon

[040-014] of Josas, a knave of oriental architecture, who has a back like a

[040-015] dome, and legs like twisted columns!”

[040-016] And the crowd burst into a laugh, especially the boys and young

[040-017] girls.

[040-018] At length the torturer stamped his foot. The wheel began to turn.

[040-019] Quasimodo wavered beneath his bonds. The amazement which was

[040-020] suddenly depicted upon his deformed face caused the bursts of

[040-021] laughter to redouble around him.

[040-022] All at once, at the moment when the wheel in its revolution

[040-023] presented to Master Pierrat, the humped back of Quasimodo, Master

[040-024] Pierrat raised his arm; the fine thongs whistled sharply through

[040-025] the air, like a handful of adders, and fell with fury upon the

[040-026] wretch’s shoulders.

[040-027] Quasimodo leaped as though awakened with a start. He began to

[040-028] understand. He writhed in his bonds; a violent contraction of

[040-029] surprise and pain distorted the muscles of his face, but he

[040-030] uttered not a single sigh. He merely turned his head backward, to

[040-031] the right, then to the left, balancing it as a bull does who has

[040-032] been stung in the flanks by a gadfly.

[040-033] A second blow followed the first, then a third, and another and

[040-034] another, and still others. The wheel did not cease to turn, nor

[040-035] the blows to rain down.

[040-036] Soon the blood burst forth, and could be seen trickling in a

[040-037] thousand threads down the hunchback’s black shoulders; and the

[040-038] slender thongs, in their rotatory motion which rent the air,

[040-039] sprinkled drops of it upon the crowd.

[040-040] Quasimodo had resumed, to all appearance, his first

[040-041] imperturbability. He had at first tried, in a quiet way and

[040-042] without much outward movement, to break his bonds. His eye had

[040-043] been seen to light up, his muscles to stiffen, his members to

[040-044] concentrate their force, and the straps to stretch. The effort

[040-045] was powerful, prodigious, desperate; but the provost’s seasoned

[040-046] bonds resisted. They cracked, and that was all. Quasimodo fell

[040-047] back exhausted. Amazement gave way, on his features, to a

[040-048] sentiment of profound and bitter discouragement. He closed his

[040-049] single eye, allowed his head to droop upon his breast, and

[040-050] feigned death.

[040-051] From that moment forth, he stirred no more. Nothing could force a

[040-052] movement from him. Neither his blood, which did not cease to

[040-053] flow, nor the blows which redoubled in fury, nor the wrath of the

[040-054] torturer, who grew excited himself and intoxicated with the

[040-055] execution, nor the sound of the horrible thongs, more sharp and

[040-056] whistling than the claws of scorpions.

[040-057] At length a bailiff from the Châtelet clad in black, mounted on a

[040-058] black horse, who had been stationed beside the ladder since the

[040-059] beginning of the execution, extended his ebony wand towards the

[040-060] hour-glass. The torturer stopped. The wheel stopped. Quasimodo’s

[040-061] eye opened slowly.

[040-062] The scourging was finished. Two lackeys of the official torturer

[040-063] bathed the bleeding shoulders of the patient, anointed them with

[040-064] some unguent which immediately closed all the wounds, and threw

[040-065] upon his back a sort of yellow vestment, in cut like a chasuble.

[040-066] In the meanwhile, Pierrat Torterue allowed the thongs, red and

[040-067] gorged with blood, to drip upon the pavement.

[040-068] All was not over for Quasimodo. He had still to undergo that hour

[040-069] of pillory which Master Florian Barbedienne had so judiciously

[040-070] added to the sentence of Messire Robert d’Estouteville; all to

[040-071] the greater glory of the old physiological and psychological play

[040-072] upon words of Jean de Cumène, _Surdus absurdus_: a deaf man is

[040-073] absurd.

[040-074] So the hour-glass was turned over once more, and they left the

[040-075] hunchback fastened to the plank, in order that justice might be

[040-076] accomplished to the very end.

[040-077] The populace, especially in the Middle Ages, is in society what

[040-078] the child is in the family. As long as it remains in its state of

[040-079] primitive ignorance, of moral and intellectual minority, it can

[040-080] be said of it as of the child,—

[040-081] ’Tis the pitiless age.

[040-082] We have already shown that Quasimodo was generally hated, for

[040-083] more than one good reason, it is true. There was hardly a

[040-084] spectator in that crowd who had not or who did not believe that

[040-085] he had reason to complain of the malevolent hunchback of

[040-086] Notre-Dame. The joy at seeing him appear thus in the pillory had

[040-087] been universal; and the harsh punishment which he had just

[040-088] suffered, and the pitiful condition in which it had left him, far

[040-089] from softening the populace had rendered its hatred more

[040-090] malicious by arming it with a touch of mirth.

[040-091] Hence, the “public prosecution” satisfied, as the bigwigs of the

[040-092] law still express it in their jargon, the turn came of a thousand

[040-093] private vengeances. Here, as in the Grand Hall, the women

[040-094] rendered themselves particularly prominent. All cherished some

[040-095] rancor against him, some for his malice, others for his ugliness.

[040-096] The latter were the most furious.

[040-097] “Oh! mask of Antichrist!” said one.

[040-098] “Rider on a broom handle!” cried another.

[040-099] “What a fine tragic grimace,” howled a third, “and who would make

[040-100] him Pope of the Fools if to-day were yesterday?”

[040-101] “’Tis well,” struck in an old woman. “This is the grimace of the

[040-102] pillory. When shall we have that of the gibbet?”

[040-103] “When will you be coiffed with your big bell a hundred feet under

[040-104] ground, cursed bellringer?”

[040-105] “But ’tis the devil who rings the Angelus!”

[040-106] “Oh! the deaf man! the one-eyed creature! the hunch-back! the

[040-107] monster!”

[040-108] “A face to make a woman miscarry better than all the drugs and

[040-109] medicines!”

[040-110] And the two scholars, Jehan du Moulin, and Robin Poussepain, sang

[040-111] at the top of their lungs, the ancient refrain,—

[040-112] “Une hart

[040-113] Pour le pendard!

[040-114] Un fagot

[040-115] Pour le magot!”[33]

[040-116] A thousand other insults rained down upon him, and hoots and

[040-117] imprecations, and laughter, and now and then, stones.

[040-118] Quasimodo was deaf but his sight was clear, and the public fury

[040-119] was no less energetically depicted on their visages than in their

[040-120] words. Moreover, the blows from the stones explained the bursts

[040-121] of laughter.

[040-122] At first he held his ground. But little by little that patience

[040-123] which had borne up under the lash of the torturer, yielded and

[040-124] gave way before all these stings of insects. The bull of the

[040-125] Asturias who has been but little moved by the attacks of the

[040-126] picador grows irritated with the dogs and banderilleras.

[040-127] He first cast around a slow glance of hatred upon the crowd. But

[040-128] bound as he was, his glance was powerless to drive away those

[040-129] flies which were stinging his wound. Then he moved in his bonds,

[040-130] and his furious exertions made the ancient wheel of the pillory

[040-131] shriek on its axle. All this only increased the derision and

[040-132] hooting.

[040-133] Then the wretched man, unable to break his collar, like that of a

[040-134] chained wild beast, became tranquil once more; only at intervals

[040-135] a sigh of rage heaved the hollows of his chest. There was neither

[040-136] shame nor redness on his face. He was too far from the state of

[040-137] society, and too near the state of nature to know what shame was.

[040-138] Moreover, with such a degree of deformity, is infamy a thing that

[040-139] can be felt? But wrath, hatred, despair, slowly lowered over that

[040-140] hideous visage a cloud which grew ever more and more sombre, ever

[040-141] more and more charged with electricity, which burst forth in a

[040-142] thousand lightning flashes from the eye of the cyclops.

[040-143] Nevertheless, that cloud cleared away for a moment, at the

[040-144] passage of a mule which traversed the crowd, bearing a priest. As

[040-145] far away as he could see that mule and that priest, the poor

[040-146] victim’s visage grew gentler. The fury which had contracted it

[040-147] was followed by a strange smile full of ineffable sweetness,

[040-148] gentleness, and tenderness. In proportion as the priest

[040-149] approached, that smile became more clear, more distinct, more

[040-150] radiant. It was like the arrival of a Saviour, which the unhappy

[040-151] man was greeting. But as soon as the mule was near enough to the

[040-152] pillory to allow of its rider recognizing the victim, the priest

[040-153] dropped his eyes, beat a hasty retreat, spurred on rigorously, as

[040-154] though in haste to rid himself of humiliating appeals, and not at

[040-155] all desirous of being saluted and recognized by a poor fellow in

[040-156] such a predicament.

[040-157] This priest was Archdeacon Dom Claude Frollo.

[040-158] The cloud descended more blackly than ever upon Quasimodo’s brow.

[040-159] The smile was still mingled with it for a time, but was bitter,

[040-160] discouraged, profoundly sad.

[040-161] Time passed on. He had been there at least an hour and a half,

[040-162] lacerated, maltreated, mocked incessantly, and almost stoned.

[040-163] All at once he moved again in his chains with redoubled despair,

[040-164] which made the whole framework that bore him tremble, and,

[040-165] breaking the silence which he had obstinately preserved hitherto,

[040-166] he cried in a hoarse and furious voice, which resembled a bark

[040-167] rather than a human cry, and which was drowned in the noise of

[040-168] the hoots—“Drink!”

[040-169] This exclamation of distress, far from exciting compassion, only

[040-170] added amusement to the good Parisian populace who surrounded the

[040-171] ladder, and who, it must be confessed, taken in the mass and as a

[040-172] multitude, was then no less cruel and brutal than that horrible

[040-173] tribe of robbers among whom we have already conducted the reader,

[040-174] and which was simply the lower stratum of the populace. Not a

[040-175] voice was raised around the unhappy victim, except to jeer at his

[040-176] thirst. It is certain that at that moment he was more grotesque

[040-177] and repulsive than pitiable, with his face purple and dripping,

[040-178] his eye wild, his mouth foaming with rage and pain, and his

[040-179] tongue lolling half out. It must also be stated that if a

[040-180] charitable soul of a _bourgeois_ or _bourgeoise_, in the rabble,

[040-181] had attempted to carry a glass of water to that wretched creature

[040-182] in torment, there reigned around the infamous steps of the

[040-183] pillory such a prejudice of shame and ignominy, that it would

[040-184] have sufficed to repulse the good Samaritan.

[040-185] At the expiration of a few moments, Quasimodo cast a desperate

[040-186] glance upon the crowd, and repeated in a voice still more

[040-187] heartrending: “Drink!”

[040-188] And all began to laugh.

[040-189] “Drink this!” cried Robin Poussepain, throwing in his face a

[040-190] sponge which had been soaked in the gutter. “There, you deaf

[040-191] villain, I’m your debtor.”

[040-192] A woman hurled a stone at his head,—

[040-193] “That will teach you to wake us up at night with your peal of a

[040-194] dammed soul.”

[040-195] “He, good, my son!” howled a cripple, making an effort to reach

[040-196] him with his crutch, “will you cast any more spells on us from

[040-197] the top of the towers of Notre-Dame?”

[040-198] “Here’s a drinking cup!” chimed in a man, flinging a broken jug

[040-199] at his breast. “’Twas you that made my wife, simply because she

[040-200] passed near you, give birth to a child with two heads!”

[040-201] “And my cat bring forth a kitten with six paws!” yelped an old

[040-202] crone, launching a brick at him.

[040-203] “Drink!” repeated Quasimodo panting, and for the third time.

[040-204] At that moment he beheld the crowd give way. A young girl,

[040-205] fantastically dressed, emerged from the throng. She was

[040-206] accompanied by a little white goat with gilded horns, and carried

[040-207] a tambourine in her hand.

[040-208] Quasimodo’s eyes sparkled. It was the gypsy whom he had attempted

[040-209] to carry off on the preceding night, a misdeed for which he was

[040-210] dimly conscious that he was being punished at that very moment;

[040-211] which was not in the least the case, since he was being chastised

[040-212] only for the misfortune of being deaf, and of having been judged

[040-213] by a deaf man. He doubted not that she had come to wreak her

[040-214] vengeance also, and to deal her blow like the rest.
