---[057-001] The priest paused for a moment, overcome with emotion. Then he

[057-002] continued,—

[057-003] “Already half fascinated, I tried to cling fast to something and

[057-004] hold myself back from falling. I recalled the snares which Satan

[057-005] had already set for me. The creature before my eyes possessed

[057-006] that superhuman beauty which can come only from heaven or hell.

[057-007] It was no simple girl made with a little of our earth, and dimly

[057-008] lighted within by the vacillating ray of a woman’s soul. It was

[057-009] an angel! but of shadows and flame, and not of light. At the

[057-010] moment when I was meditating thus, I beheld beside you a goat, a

[057-011] beast of witches, which smiled as it gazed at me. The midday sun

[057-012] gave him golden horns. Then I perceived the snare of the demon,

[057-013] and I no longer doubted that you had come from hell and that you

[057-014] had come thence for my perdition. I believed it.”

[057-015] Here the priest looked the prisoner full in the face, and added,

[057-016] coldly,—

[057-017] “I believe it still. Nevertheless, the charm operated little by

[057-018] little; your dancing whirled through my brain; I felt the

[057-019] mysterious spell working within me. All that should have awakened

[057-020] was lulled to sleep; and like those who die in the snow, I felt

[057-021] pleasure in allowing this sleep to draw on. All at once, you

[057-022] began to sing. What could I do, unhappy wretch? Your song was

[057-023] still more charming than your dancing. I tried to flee.

[057-024] Impossible. I was nailed, rooted to the spot. It seemed to me

[057-025] that the marble of the pavement had risen to my knees. I was

[057-026] forced to remain until the end. My feet were like ice, my head

[057-027] was on fire. At last you took pity on me, you ceased to sing, you

[057-028] disappeared. The reflection of the dazzling vision, the

[057-029] reverberation of the enchanting music disappeared by degrees from

[057-030] my eyes and my ears. Then I fell back into the embrasure of the

[057-031] window, more rigid, more feeble than a statue torn from its base.

[057-032] The vesper bell roused me. I drew myself up; I fled; but alas!

[057-033] something within me had fallen never to rise again, something had

[057-034] come upon me from which I could not flee.”

[057-035] He made another pause and went on,—

[057-036] “Yes, dating from that day, there was within me a man whom I did

[057-037] not know. I tried to make use of all my remedies. The cloister,

[057-038] the altar, work, books,—follies! Oh, how hollow does science

[057-039] sound when one in despair dashes against it a head full of

[057-040] passions! Do you know, young girl, what I saw thenceforth between

[057-041] my book and me? You, your shade, the image of the luminous

[057-042] apparition which had one day crossed the space before me. But

[057-043] this image had no longer the same color; it was sombre, funereal,

[057-044] gloomy as the black circle which long pursues the vision of the

[057-045] imprudent man who has gazed intently at the sun.

[057-046] “Unable to rid myself of it, since I heard your song humming ever

[057-047] in my head, beheld your feet dancing always on my breviary, felt

[057-048] even at night, in my dreams, your form in contact with my own, I

[057-049] desired to see you again, to touch you, to know who you were, to

[057-050] see whether I should really find you like the ideal image which I

[057-051] had retained of you, to shatter my dream, perchance, with

[057-052] reality. At all events, I hoped that a new impression would

[057-053] efface the first, and the first had become insupportable. I

[057-054] sought you. I saw you once more. Calamity! When I had seen you

[057-055] twice, I wanted to see you a thousand times, I wanted to see you

[057-056] always. Then—how stop myself on that slope of hell?—then I no

[057-057] longer belonged to myself. The other end of the thread which the

[057-058] demon had attached to my wings he had fastened to his foot. I

[057-059] became vagrant and wandering like yourself. I waited for you

[057-060] under porches, I stood on the lookout for you at the street

[057-061] corners, I watched for you from the summit of my tower. Every

[057-062] evening I returned to myself more charmed, more despairing, more

[057-063] bewitched, more lost!

[057-064] “I had learned who you were; an Egyptian, Bohemian, gypsy,

[057-065] zingara. How could I doubt the magic? Listen. I hoped that a

[057-066] trial would free me from the charm. A witch enchanted Bruno

[057-067] d’Ast; he had her burned, and was cured. I knew it. I wanted to

[057-068] try the remedy. First I tried to have you forbidden the square in

[057-069] front of Notre-Dame, hoping to forget you if you returned no

[057-070] more. You paid no heed to it. You returned. Then the idea of

[057-071] abducting you occurred to me. One night I made the attempt. There

[057-072] were two of us. We already had you in our power, when that

[057-073] miserable officer came up. He delivered you. Thus did he begin

[057-074] your unhappiness, mine, and his own. Finally, no longer knowing

[057-075] what to do, and what was to become of me, I denounced you to the

[057-076] official.

[057-077] “I thought that I should be cured like Bruno d’Ast. I also had a

[057-078] confused idea that a trial would deliver you into my hands; that,

[057-079] as a prisoner I should hold you, I should have you; that there

[057-080] you could not escape from me; that you had already possessed me a

[057-081] sufficiently long time to give me the right to possess you in my

[057-082] turn. When one does wrong, one must do it thoroughly. ’Tis

[057-083] madness to halt midway in the monstrous! The extreme of crime has

[057-084] its deliriums of joy. A priest and a witch can mingle in delight

[057-085] upon the truss of straw in a dungeon!

[057-086] “Accordingly, I denounced you. It was then that I terrified you

[057-087] when we met. The plot which I was weaving against you, the storm

[057-088] which I was heaping up above your head, burst from me in threats

[057-089] and lightning glances. Still, I hesitated. My project had its

[057-090] terrible sides which made me shrink back.

[057-091] “Perhaps I might have renounced it; perhaps my hideous thought

[057-092] would have withered in my brain, without bearing fruit. I thought

[057-093] that it would always depend upon me to follow up or discontinue

[057-094] this prosecution. But every evil thought is inexorable, and

[057-095] insists on becoming a deed; but where I believed myself to be all

[057-096] powerful, fate was more powerful than I. Alas! ’tis fate which

[057-097] has seized you and delivered you to the terrible wheels of the

[057-098] machine which I had constructed doubly. Listen. I am nearing the

[057-099] end.

[057-100] “One day,—again the sun was shining brilliantly—I behold a man

[057-101] pass me uttering your name and laughing, who carries sensuality

[057-102] in his eyes. Damnation! I followed him; you know the rest.”

[057-103] He ceased.

[057-104] The young girl could find but one word:

[057-105] “Oh, my Phœbus!”

[057-106] “Not that name!” said the priest, grasping her arm violently.

[057-107] “Utter not that name! Oh! miserable wretches that we are, ’tis

[057-108] that name which has ruined us! or, rather we have ruined each

[057-109] other by the inexplicable play of fate! you are suffering, are

[057-110] you not? you are cold; the night makes you blind, the dungeon

[057-111] envelops you; but perhaps you still have some light in the bottom

[057-112] of your soul, were it only your childish love for that empty man

[057-113] who played with your heart, while I bear the dungeon within me;

[057-114] within me there is winter, ice, despair; I have night in my soul.

[057-115] “Do you know what I have suffered? I was present at your trial. I

[057-116] was seated on the official’s bench. Yes, under one of the

[057-117] priests’ cowls, there were the contortions of the damned. When

[057-118] you were brought in, I was there; when you were questioned, I was

[057-119] there.—Den of wolves!—It was my crime, it was my gallows that I

[057-120] beheld being slowly reared over your head. I was there for every

[057-121] witness, every proof, every plea; I could count each of your

[057-122] steps in the painful path; I was still there when that ferocious

[057-123] beast—oh! I had not foreseen torture! Listen. I followed you to

[057-124] that chamber of anguish. I beheld you stripped and handled, half

[057-125] naked, by the infamous hands of the tormentor. I beheld your

[057-126] foot, that foot which I would have given an empire to kiss and

[057-127] die, that foot, beneath which to have had my head crushed I

[057-128] should have felt such rapture,—I beheld it encased in that

[057-129] horrible boot, which converts the limbs of a living being into

[057-130] one bloody clod. Oh, wretch! while I looked on at that, I held

[057-131] beneath my shroud a dagger, with which I lacerated my breast.

[057-132] When you uttered that cry, I plunged it into my flesh; at a

[057-133] second cry, it would have entered my heart. Look! I believe that

[057-134] it still bleeds.”

[057-135] He opened his cassock. His breast was in fact, mangled as by the

[057-136] claw of a tiger, and on his side he had a large and badly healed

[057-137] wound.

[057-138] The prisoner recoiled with horror.

[057-139] “Oh!” said the priest, “young girl, have pity upon me! You think

[057-140] yourself unhappy; alas! alas! you know not what unhappiness is.

[057-141] Oh! to love a woman! to be a priest! to be hated! to love with

[057-142] all the fury of one’s soul; to feel that one would give for the

[057-143] least of her smiles, one’s blood, one’s vitals, one’s fame, one’s

[057-144] salvation, one’s immortality and eternity, this life and the

[057-145] other; to regret that one is not a king, emperor, archangel, God,

[057-146] in order that one might place a greater slave beneath her feet;

[057-147] to clasp her night and day in one’s dreams and one’s thoughts,

[057-148] and to behold her in love with the trappings of a soldier and to

[057-149] have nothing to offer her but a priest’s dirty cassock, which

[057-150] will inspire her with fear and disgust! To be present with one’s

[057-151] jealousy and one’s rage, while she lavishes on a miserable,

[057-152] blustering imbecile, treasures of love and beauty! To behold that

[057-153] body whose form burns you, that bosom which possesses so much

[057-154] sweetness, that flesh palpitate and blush beneath the kisses of

[057-155] another! Oh heaven! to love her foot, her arm, her shoulder, to

[057-156] think of her blue veins, of her brown skin, until one writhes for

[057-157] whole nights together on the pavement of one’s cell, and to

[057-158] behold all those caresses which one has dreamed of, end in

[057-159] torture! To have succeeded only in stretching her upon the

[057-160] leather bed! Oh! these are the veritable pincers, reddened in the

[057-161] fires of hell. Oh! blessed is he who is sawn between two planks,

[057-162] or torn in pieces by four horses! Do you know what that torture

[057-163] is, which is imposed upon you for long nights by your burning

[057-164] arteries, your bursting heart, your breaking head, your

[057-165] teeth-knawed hands; mad tormentors which turn you incessantly, as

[057-166] upon a red-hot gridiron, to a thought of love, of jealousy, and

[057-167] of despair! Young girl, mercy! a truce for a moment! a few ashes

[057-168] on these live coals! Wipe away, I beseech you, the perspiration

[057-169] which trickles in great drops from my brow! Child! torture me

[057-170] with one hand, but caress me with the other! Have pity, young

[057-171] girl! Have pity upon me!”

[057-172] The priest writhed on the wet pavement, beating his head against

[057-173] the corners of the stone steps. The young girl gazed at him, and

[057-174] listened to him.

[057-175] When he ceased, exhausted and panting, she repeated in a low

[057-176] voice,—

[057-177] “Oh my Phœbus!”

[057-178] The priest dragged himself towards her on his knees.

[057-179] “I beseech you,” he cried, “if you have any heart, do not repulse

[057-180] me! Oh! I love you! I am a wretch! When you utter that name,

[057-181] unhappy girl, it is as though you crushed all the fibres of my

[057-182] heart between your teeth. Mercy! If you come from hell I will go

[057-183] thither with you. I have done everything to that end. The hell

[057-184] where you are, shall be paradise; the sight of you is more

[057-185] charming than that of God! Oh! speak! you will have none of me? I

[057-186] should have thought the mountains would be shaken in their

[057-187] foundations on the day when a woman would repulse such a love.

[057-188] Oh! if you only would! Oh! how happy we might be. We would flee—I

[057-189] would help you to flee,—we would go somewhere, we would seek that

[057-190] spot on earth, where the sun is brightest, the sky the bluest,

[057-191] where the trees are most luxuriant. We would love each other, we

[057-192] would pour our two souls into each other, and we would have a

[057-193] thirst for ourselves which we would quench in common and

[057-194] incessantly at that fountain of inexhaustible love.”
