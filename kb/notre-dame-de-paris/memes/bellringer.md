# Bellringer

The bellringer (*sonneur de cloches*) is a central role in Notre-Dame de Paris, embodied by Quasimodo, whose physical connection to the cathedral bells defines both his identity and his relationship to the sacred space of Notre-Dame.

## Definition

The bellringer in Hugo's novel is not merely a church functionary but a figure whose entire existence is intertwined with the cathedral. Quasimodo becomes the bellringer through the intervention of Claude Frollo, and the position shapes his physical and emotional being. The role carries both practical duties — ringing the bells for services and celebrations — and symbolic weight as the voice of the cathedral itself [027-092] [027-100].

## Hugo's Treatment

### In the Narrative

Quasimodo is first identified publicly as "Quasimodo, the bellringer" during the Festival of Fools, where his role is integral to his identity in the eyes of the Parisian crowd [009-024]. His relationship with the bells is one of the few sources of joy in his life: Hugo describes how the bells were his only companions, and how the physical vibration of the great bell would restore a kind of hearing to the deaf bellringer [027-100] [027-107]. Frollo, who made Quasimodo bellringer, maintains a complex authority over him — the archdeacon is described as having power over the bellringer comparable to the bellringer's attachment to the archdeacon [027-107] [027-108].

When Quasimodo rescues Esmeralda and brings her to the cathedral, the role of bellringer becomes linked to the concept of sanctuary — he uses his knowledge of the cathedral's spaces, gained through years of bell-ringing, to protect her [028-086]. The crowd during the attack on Notre-Dame identifies him repeatedly as "the bellringer" [034-051] [043-002], cementing the role as his primary social identity.

### In the Author's Voice

Hugo uses the bellringer to explore the relationship between a human being and an architectural monument. He writes that Claude Frollo "had finally made him the bellringer," and that the great bell's peal was the only sound Quasimodo could still perceive [027-092] [027-100]. The bellringer's deafness is not presented as simple disability but as a transformation that deepens his bond with the bells — a kind of communion that replaces ordinary human communication.

The author also reflects on the social perception of the bellringer: Quasimodo is mocked as "the bellringer, our one-eyed man, our hunchback" [034-051], yet Hugo treats this mockery as revealing the crowd's cruelty rather than Quasimodo's deficiency. In a particularly pointed passage, Esmeralda's captors exclaim with indignation: "A bellringer carrying off a wench, like a viscount!" [043-002], exposing the rigid social hierarchy in which a bellringer's station is assumed to preclude romantic agency.
