---[081-001] In the meantime, windows had been opened again. The neighbors

[081-002] hearing the war cries of the king’s troops, had mingled in the

[081-003] affray, and bullets rained upon the outcasts from every story.

[081-004] The Parvis was filled with a thick smoke, which the musketry

[081-005] streaked with flame. Through it one could confusedly distinguish

[081-006] the front of Notre-Dame, and the decrepit Hôtel-Dieu with some

[081-007] wan invalids gazing down from the heights of its roof all

[081-008] checkered with dormer windows.

[081-009] At length the vagabonds gave way. Weariness, the lack of good

[081-010] weapons, the fright of this surprise, the musketry from the

[081-011] windows, the valiant attack of the king’s troops, all overwhelmed

[081-012] them. They forced the line of assailants, and fled in every

[081-013] direction, leaving the Parvis encumbered with dead.

[081-014] When Quasimodo, who had not ceased to fight for a moment, beheld

[081-015] this rout, he fell on his knees and raised his hands to heaven;

[081-016] then, intoxicated with joy, he ran, he ascended with the

[081-017] swiftness of a bird to that cell, the approaches to which he had

[081-018] so intrepidly defended. He had but one thought now; it was to

[081-019] kneel before her whom he had just saved for the second time.

[081-020] When he entered the cell, he found it empty.

[081-021] BOOK ELEVENTH.

[081-022] CHAPTER I. THE LITTLE SHOE.

[081-023] La Esmeralda was sleeping at the moment when the outcasts

[081-024] assailed the church.

[081-025] Soon the ever-increasing uproar around the edifice, and the

[081-026] uneasy bleating of her goat which had been awakened, had roused

[081-027] her from her slumbers. She had sat up, she had listened, she had

[081-028] looked; then, terrified by the light and noise, she had rushed

[081-029] from her cell to see. The aspect of the Place, the vision which

[081-030] was moving in it, the disorder of that nocturnal assault, that

[081-031] hideous crowd, leaping like a cloud of frogs, half seen in the

[081-032] gloom, the croaking of that hoarse multitude, those few red

[081-033] torches running and crossing each other in the darkness like the

[081-034] meteors which streak the misty surfaces of marshes, this whole

[081-035] scene produced upon her the effect of a mysterious battle between

[081-036] the phantoms of the witches’ sabbath and the stone monsters of

[081-037] the church. Imbued from her very infancy with the superstitions

[081-038] of the Bohemian tribe, her first thought was that she had caught

[081-039] the strange beings peculiar to the night, in their deeds of

[081-040] witchcraft. Then she ran in terror to cower in her cell, asking

[081-041] of her pallet some less terrible nightmare.

[081-042] But little by little the first vapors of terror had been

[081-043] dissipated; from the constantly increasing noise, and from many

[081-044] other signs of reality, she felt herself besieged not by

[081-045] spectres, but by human beings. Then her fear, though it did not

[081-046] increase, changed its character. She had dreamed of the

[081-047] possibility of a popular mutiny to tear her from her asylum. The

[081-048] idea of once more recovering life, hope, Phœbus, who was ever

[081-049] present in her future, the extreme helplessness of her condition,

[081-050] flight cut off, no support, her abandonment, her isolation,—these

[081-051] thoughts and a thousand others overwhelmed her. She fell upon her

[081-052] knees, with her head on her bed, her hands clasped over her head,

[081-053] full of anxiety and tremors, and, although a gypsy, an idolater,

[081-054] and a pagan, she began to entreat with sobs, mercy from the good

[081-055] Christian God, and to pray to our Lady, her hostess. For even if

[081-056] one believes in nothing, there are moments in life when one is

[081-057] always of the religion of the temple which is nearest at hand.

[081-058] She remained thus prostrate for a very long time, trembling in

[081-059] truth, more than praying, chilled by the ever-closer breath of

[081-060] that furious multitude, understanding nothing of this outburst,

[081-061] ignorant of what was being plotted, what was being done, what

[081-062] they wanted, but foreseeing a terrible issue.

[081-063] In the midst of this anguish, she heard some one walking near

[081-064] her. She turned round. Two men, one of whom carried a lantern,

[081-065] had just entered her cell. She uttered a feeble cry.

[081-066] “Fear nothing,” said a voice which was not unknown to her, “it is

[081-067] I.”

[081-068] “Who are you?” she asked.

[081-069] “Pierre Gringoire.”

[081-070] This name reassured her. She raised her eyes once more, and

[081-071] recognized the poet in very fact. But there stood beside him a

[081-072] black figure veiled from head to foot, which struck her by its

[081-073] silence.

[081-074] “Oh!” continued Gringoire in a tone of reproach, “Djali

[081-075] recognized me before you!”

[081-076] The little goat had not, in fact, waited for Gringoire to

[081-077] announce his name. No sooner had he entered than it rubbed itself

[081-078] gently against his knees, covering the poet with caresses and

[081-079] with white hairs, for it was shedding its hair. Gringoire

[081-080] returned the caresses.

[081-081] “Who is this with you?” said the gypsy, in a low voice.

[081-082] “Be at ease,” replied Gringoire. “’Tis one of my friends.” Then

[081-083] the philosopher setting his lantern on the ground, crouched upon

[081-084] the stones, and exclaimed enthusiastically, as he pressed Djali

[081-085] in his arms,—

[081-086] “Oh! ’tis a graceful beast, more considerable no doubt, for it’s

[081-087] neatness than for its size, but ingenious, subtle, and lettered

[081-088] as a grammarian! Let us see, my Djali, hast thou forgotten any of

[081-089] thy pretty tricks? How does Master Jacques Charmolue?...”

[081-090] The man in black did not allow him to finish. He approached

[081-091] Gringoire and shook him roughly by the shoulder.

[081-092] Gringoire rose.

[081-093] “’Tis true,” said he: “I forgot that we are in haste. But that is

[081-094] no reason master, for getting furious with people in this manner.

[081-095] My dear and lovely child, your life is in danger, and Djali’s

[081-096] also. They want to hang you again. We are your friends, and we

[081-097] have come to save you. Follow us.”

[081-098] “Is it true?” she exclaimed in dismay.

[081-099] “Yes, perfectly true. Come quickly!”

[081-100] “I am willing,” she stammered. “But why does not your friend

[081-101] speak?”

[081-102] “Ah!” said Gringoire, “’tis because his father and mother were

[081-103] fantastic people who made him of a taciturn temperament.”

[081-104] She was obliged to content herself with this explanation.

[081-105] Gringoire took her by the hand; his companion picked up the

[081-106] lantern and walked on in front. Fear stunned the young girl. She

[081-107] allowed herself to be led away. The goat followed them, frisking,

[081-108] so joyous at seeing Gringoire again that it made him stumble

[081-109] every moment by thrusting its horns between his legs.

[081-110] “Such is life,” said the philosopher, every time that he came

[081-111] near falling down; “’tis often our best friends who cause us to

[081-112] be overthrown.”

[081-113] They rapidly descended the staircase of the towers, crossed the

[081-114] church, full of shadows and solitude, and all reverberating with

[081-115] uproar, which formed a frightful contrast, and emerged into the

[081-116] courtyard of the cloister by the red door. The cloister was

[081-117] deserted; the canons had fled to the bishop’s palace in order to

[081-118] pray together; the courtyard was empty, a few frightened lackeys

[081-119] were crouching in dark corners. They directed their steps towards

[081-120] the door which opened from this court upon the Terrain. The man

[081-121] in black opened it with a key which he had about him. Our readers

[081-122] are aware that the Terrain was a tongue of land enclosed by walls

[081-123] on the side of the City and belonging to the chapter of

[081-124] Notre-Dame, which terminated the island on the east, behind the

[081-125] church. They found this enclosure perfectly deserted. There was

[081-126] here less tumult in the air. The roar of the outcasts’ assault

[081-127] reached them more confusedly and less clamorously. The fresh

[081-128] breeze which follows the current of a stream, rustled the leaves

[081-129] of the only tree planted on the point of the Terrain, with a

[081-130] noise that was already perceptible. But they were still very

[081-131] close to danger. The nearest edifices to them were the bishop’s

[081-132] palace and the church. It was plainly evident that there was

[081-133] great internal commotion in the bishop’s palace. Its shadowy mass

[081-134] was all furrowed with lights which flitted from window to window;

[081-135] as, when one has just burned paper, there remains a sombre

[081-136] edifice of ashes in which bright sparks run a thousand eccentric

[081-137] courses. Beside them, the enormous towers of Notre-Dame, thus

[081-138] viewed from behind, with the long nave above which they rise cut

[081-139] out in black against the red and vast light which filled the

[081-140] Parvis, resembled two gigantic andirons of some cyclopean

[081-141] fire-grate.

[081-142] What was to be seen of Paris on all sides wavered before the eye

[081-143] in a gloom mingled with light. Rembrandt has such backgrounds to

[081-144] his pictures.

[081-145] The man with the lantern walked straight to the point of the

[081-146] Terrain. There, at the very brink of the water, stood the

[081-147] wormeaten remains of a fence of posts latticed with laths,

[081-148] whereon a low vine spread out a few thin branches like the

[081-149] fingers of an outspread hand. Behind, in the shadow cast by this

[081-150] trellis, a little boat lay concealed. The man made a sign to

[081-151] Gringoire and his companion to enter. The goat followed them. The

[081-152] man was the last to step in. Then he cut the boat’s moorings,

[081-153] pushed it from the shore with a long boat-hook, and, seizing two

[081-154] oars, seated himself in the bow, rowing with all his might

[081-155] towards midstream. The Seine is very rapid at this point, and he

[081-156] had a good deal of trouble in leaving the point of the island.

[081-157] Gringoire’s first care on entering the boat was to place the goat

[081-158] on his knees. He took a position in the stern; and the young

[081-159] girl, whom the stranger inspired with an indefinable uneasiness,

[081-160] seated herself close to the poet.

[081-161] When our philosopher felt the boat sway, he clapped his hands and

[081-162] kissed Djali between the horns.

[081-163] “Oh!” said he, “now we are safe, all four of us.”

[081-164] He added with the air of a profound thinker, “One is indebted

[081-165] sometimes to fortune, sometimes to ruse, for the happy issue of

[081-166] great enterprises.”

[081-167] The boat made its way slowly towards the right shore. The young

[081-168] girl watched the unknown man with secret terror. He had carefully

[081-169] turned off the light of his dark lantern. A glimpse could be

[081-170] caught of him in the obscurity, in the bow of the boat, like a

[081-171] spectre. His cowl, which was still lowered, formed a sort of

[081-172] mask; and every time that he spread his arms, upon which hung

[081-173] large black sleeves, as he rowed, one would have said they were

[081-174] two huge bat’s wings. Moreover, he had not yet uttered a word or

[081-175] breathed a syllable. No other noise was heard in the boat than

[081-176] the splashing of the oars, mingled with the rippling of the water

[081-177] along her sides.

[081-178] “On my soul!” exclaimed Gringoire suddenly, “we are as cheerful

[081-179] and joyous as young owls! We preserve the silence of Pythagoreans

[081-180] or fishes! _Pasque-Dieu!_ my friends, I should greatly like to

[081-181] have some one speak to me. The human voice is music to the human

[081-182] ear. ’Tis not I who say that, but Didymus of Alexandria, and they

[081-183] are illustrious words. Assuredly, Didymus of Alexandria is no

[081-184] mediocre philosopher.—One word, my lovely child! say but one word

[081-185] to me, I entreat you. By the way, you had a droll and peculiar

[081-186] little pout; do you still make it? Do you know, my dear, that

[081-187] parliament hath full jurisdiction over all places of asylum, and

[081-188] that you were running a great risk in your little chamber at

[081-189] Notre-Dame? Alas! the little bird trochylus maketh its nest in

[081-190] the jaws of the crocodile.—Master, here is the moon re-appearing.

[081-191] If only they do not perceive us. We are doing a laudable thing in

[081-192] saving mademoiselle, and yet we should be hung by order of the

[081-193] king if we were caught. Alas! human actions are taken by two

[081-194] handles. That is branded with disgrace in one which is crowned in

[081-195] another. He admires Cicero who blames Catiline. Is it not so,

[081-196] master? What say you to this philosophy? I possess philosophy by

[081-197] instinct, by nature, _ut apes geometriam_.—Come! no one answers

[081-198] me. What unpleasant moods you two are in! I must do all the

[081-199] talking alone. That is what we call a monologue in

[081-200] tragedy.—_Pasque-Dieu!_ I must inform you that I have just seen

[081-201] the king, Louis XI., and that I have caught this oath from

[081-202] him,—_Pasque-Dieu!_ They are still making a hearty howl in the

[081-203] city.—’Tis a villanous, malicious old king. He is all swathed in

[081-204] furs. He still owes me the money for my epithalamium, and he came

[081-205] within a nick of hanging me this evening, which would have been

[081-206] very inconvenient to me.—He is niggardly towards men of merit. He

[081-207] ought to read the four books of Salvien of Cologne, _Adversus

[081-208] Avaritiam_. In truth! ’Tis a paltry king in his ways with men of

[081-209] letters, and one who commits very barbarous cruelties. He is a

[081-210] sponge, to soak money raised from the people. His saving is like

[081-211] the spleen which swelleth with the leanness of all the other

[081-212] members. Hence complaints against the hardness of the times

[081-213] become murmurs against the prince. Under this gentle and pious

[081-214] sire, the gallows crack with the hung, the blocks rot with blood,

[081-215] the prisons burst like over full bellies. This king hath one hand

[081-216] which grasps, and one which hangs. He is the procurator of Dame

[081-217] Tax and Monsieur Gibbet. The great are despoiled of their

[081-218] dignities, and the little incessantly overwhelmed with fresh

[081-219] oppressions. He is an exorbitant prince. I love not this monarch.

[081-220] And you, master?”
