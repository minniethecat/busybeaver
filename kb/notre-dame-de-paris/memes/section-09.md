---[009-001] The acclamation was unanimous; people rushed towards the chapel.

[009-002] They made the lucky Pope of the Fools come forth in triumph. But

[009-003] it was then that surprise and admiration attained their highest

[009-004] pitch; the grimace was his face.

[009-005] Or rather, his whole person was a grimace. A huge head, bristling

[009-006] with red hair; between his shoulders an enormous hump, a

[009-007] counterpart perceptible in front; a system of thighs and legs so

[009-008] strangely astray that they could touch each other only at the

[009-009] knees, and, viewed from the front, resembled the crescents of two

[009-010] scythes joined by the handles; large feet, monstrous hands; and,

[009-011] with all this deformity, an indescribable and redoubtable air of

[009-012] vigor, agility, and courage,—strange exception to the eternal

[009-013] rule which wills that force as well as beauty shall be the result

[009-014] of harmony. Such was the pope whom the fools had just chosen for

[009-015] themselves.

[009-016] One would have pronounced him a giant who had been broken and

[009-017] badly put together again.

[009-018] When this species of cyclops appeared on the threshold of the

[009-019] chapel, motionless, squat, and almost as broad as he was tall;

[009-020] _squared_ on the _base_, as a great man says; with his doublet

[009-021] half red, half violet, sown with silver bells, and, above all, in

[009-022] the perfection of his ugliness, the populace recognized him on

[009-023] the instant, and shouted with one voice,—

[009-024] “’Tis Quasimodo, the bellringer! ’tis Quasimodo, the hunchback of

[009-025] Notre-Dame! Quasimodo, the one-eyed! Quasimodo, the bandy-legged!

[009-026] Noël! Noël!”

[009-027] It will be seen that the poor fellow had a choice of surnames.

[009-028] “Let the women with child beware!” shouted the scholars.

[009-029] “Or those who wish to be,” resumed Joannes.

[009-030] The women did, in fact, hide their faces.

[009-031] “Oh! the horrible monkey!” said one of them.

[009-032] “As wicked as he is ugly,” retorted another.

[009-033] “He’s the devil,” added a third.

[009-034] “I have the misfortune to live near Notre-Dame; I hear him

[009-035] prowling round the eaves by night.”

[009-036] “With the cats.”

[009-037] “He’s always on our roofs.”

[009-038] “He throws spells down our chimneys.”

[009-039] “The other evening, he came and made a grimace at me through my

[009-040] attic window. I thought that it was a man. Such a fright as I

[009-041] had!”

[009-042] “I’m sure that he goes to the witches’ sabbath. Once he left a

[009-043] broom on my leads.”

[009-044] “Oh! what a displeasing hunchback’s face!”

[009-045] “Oh! what an ill-favored soul!”

[009-046] “Whew!”

[009-047] The men, on the contrary, were delighted and applauded.

[009-048] Quasimodo, the object of the tumult, still stood on the threshold

[009-049] of the chapel, sombre and grave, and allowed them to admire him.

[009-050] One scholar (Robin Poussepain, I think), came and laughed in his

[009-051] face, and too close. Quasimodo contented himself with taking him

[009-052] by the girdle, and hurling him ten paces off amid the crowd; all

[009-053] without uttering a word.

[009-054] Master Coppenole, in amazement, approached him.

[009-055] “Cross of God! Holy Father! you possess the handsomest ugliness

[009-056] that I have ever beheld in my life. You would deserve to be pope

[009-057] at Rome, as well as at Paris.”

[009-058] So saying, he placed his hand gayly on his shoulder. Quasimodo

[009-059] did not stir. Coppenole went on,—

[009-060] “You are a rogue with whom I have a fancy for carousing, were it

[009-061] to cost me a new dozen of twelve livres of Tours. How does it

[009-062] strike you?”

[009-063] Quasimodo made no reply.

[009-064] “Cross of God!” said the hosier, “are you deaf?”

[009-065] He was, in truth, deaf.

[009-066] Nevertheless, he began to grow impatient with Coppenole’s

[009-067] behavior, and suddenly turned towards him with so formidable a

[009-068] gnashing of teeth, that the Flemish giant recoiled, like a

[009-069] bull-dog before a cat.

[009-070] Then there was created around that strange personage, a circle of

[009-071] terror and respect, whose radius was at least fifteen geometrical

[009-072] feet. An old woman explained to Coppenole that Quasimodo was

[009-073] deaf.

[009-074] “Deaf!” said the hosier, with his great Flemish laugh. “Cross of

[009-075] God! He’s a perfect pope!”

[009-076] “Hé! I recognize him,” exclaimed Jehan, who had, at last,

[009-077] descended from his capital, in order to see Quasimodo at closer

[009-078] quarters, “he’s the bellringer of my brother, the archdeacon.

[009-079] Good-day, Quasimodo!”

[009-080] “What a devil of a man!” said Robin Poussepain still all bruised

[009-081] with his fall. “He shows himself; he’s a hunchback. He walks;

[009-082] he’s bandy-legged. He looks at you; he’s one-eyed. You speak to

[009-083] him; he’s deaf. And what does this Polyphemus do with his

[009-084] tongue?”

[009-085] “He speaks when he chooses,” said the old woman; “he became deaf

[009-086] through ringing the bells. He is not dumb.”

[009-087] “That he lacks,” remarks Jehan.

[009-088] “And he has one eye too many,” added Robin Poussepain.

[009-089] “Not at all,” said Jehan wisely. “A one-eyed man is far less

[009-090] complete than a blind man. He knows what he lacks.”

[009-091] In the meantime, all the beggars, all the lackeys, all the

[009-092] cutpurses, joined with the scholars, had gone in procession to

[009-093] seek, in the cupboard of the law clerks’ company, the cardboard

[009-094] tiara, and the derisive robe of the Pope of the Fools. Quasimodo

[009-095] allowed them to array him in them without wincing, and with a

[009-096] sort of proud docility. Then they made him seat himself on a

[009-097] motley litter. Twelve officers of the fraternity of fools raised

[009-098] him on their shoulders; and a sort of bitter and disdainful joy

[009-099] lighted up the morose face of the cyclops, when he beheld beneath

[009-100] his deformed feet all those heads of handsome, straight,

[009-101] well-made men. Then the ragged and howling procession set out on

[009-102] its march, according to custom, around the inner galleries of the

[009-103] Courts, before making the circuit of the streets and squares.

[009-104] CHAPTER VI. ESMERALDA.

[009-105] We are delighted to be able to inform the reader, that during the

[009-106] whole of this scene, Gringoire and his piece had stood firm. His

[009-107] actors, spurred on by him, had not ceased to spout his comedy,

[009-108] and he had not ceased to listen to it. He had made up his mind

[009-109] about the tumult, and was determined to proceed to the end, not

[009-110] giving up the hope of a return of attention on the part of the

[009-111] public. This gleam of hope acquired fresh life, when he saw

[009-112] Quasimodo, Coppenole, and the deafening escort of the pope of the

[009-113] procession of fools quit the hall amid great uproar. The throng

[009-114] rushed eagerly after them. “Good,” he said to himself, “there go

[009-115] all the mischief-makers.” Unfortunately, all the mischief-makers

[009-116] constituted the entire audience. In the twinkling of an eye, the

[009-117] grand hall was empty.

[009-118] To tell the truth, a few spectators still remained, some

[009-119] scattered, others in groups around the pillars, women, old men,

[009-120] or children, who had had enough of the uproar and tumult. Some

[009-121] scholars were still perched astride of the window-sills, engaged

[009-122] in gazing into the Place.

[009-123] “Well,” thought Gringoire, “here are still as many as are

[009-124] required to hear the end of my mystery. They are few in number,

[009-125] but it is a choice audience, a lettered audience.”

[009-126] An instant later, a symphony which had been intended to produce

[009-127] the greatest effect on the arrival of the Virgin, was lacking.

[009-128] Gringoire perceived that his music had been carried off by the

[009-129] procession of the Pope of the Fools. “Skip it,” said he,

[009-130] stoically.

[009-131] He approached a group of _bourgeois_, who seemed to him to be

[009-132] discussing his piece. This is the fragment of conversation which

[009-133] he caught,—

[009-134] “You know, Master Cheneteau, the Hôtel de Navarre, which belonged

[009-135] to Monsieur de Nemours?”

[009-136] “Yes, opposite the Chapelle de Braque.”

[009-137] “Well, the treasury has just let it to Guillaume Alixandre,

[009-138] historian, for six livres, eight sols, parisian, a year.”

[009-139] “How rents are going up!”

[009-140] “Come,” said Gringoire to himself, with a sigh, “the others are

[009-141] listening.”

[009-142] “Comrades,” suddenly shouted one of the young scamps from the

[009-143] window, “La Esmeralda! La Esmeralda in the Place!”

[009-144] This word produced a magical effect. Every one who was left in

[009-145] the hall flew to the windows, climbing the walls in order to see,

[009-146] and repeating, “La Esmeralda! La Esmeralda?” At the same time, a

[009-147] great sound of applause was heard from without.

[009-148] “What’s the meaning of this, of the Esmeralda?” said Gringoire,

[009-149] wringing his hands in despair. “Ah, good heavens! it seems to be

[009-150] the turn of the windows now.”

[009-151] He returned towards the marble table, and saw that the

[009-152] representation had been interrupted. It was precisely at the

[009-153] instant when Jupiter should have appeared with his thunder. But

[009-154] Jupiter was standing motionless at the foot of the stage.

[009-155] “Michel Giborne!” cried the irritated poet, “what are you doing

[009-156] there? Is that your part? Come up!”

[009-157] “Alas!” said Jupiter, “a scholar has just seized the ladder.”

[009-158] Gringoire looked. It was but too true. All communication between

[009-159] his plot and its solution was intercepted.

[009-160] “The rascal,” he murmured. “And why did he take that ladder?”

[009-161] “In order to go and see the Esmeralda,” replied Jupiter

[009-162] piteously. “He said, ‘Come, here’s a ladder that’s of no use!’

[009-163] and he took it.”

[009-164] This was the last blow. Gringoire received it with resignation.

[009-165] “May the devil fly away with you!” he said to the comedian, “and

[009-166] if I get my pay, you shall receive yours.”

[009-167] Then he beat a retreat, with drooping head, but the last in the

[009-168] field, like a general who has fought well.

[009-169] And as he descended the winding stairs of the courts: “A fine

[009-170] rabble of asses and dolts these Parisians!” he muttered between

[009-171] his teeth; “they come to hear a mystery and don’t listen to it at

[009-172] all! They are engrossed by every one, by Clopin Trouillefou, by

[009-173] the cardinal, by Coppenole, by Quasimodo, by the devil! but by

[009-174] Madame the Virgin Mary, not at all. If I had known, I’d have

[009-175] given you Virgin Mary; you ninnies! And I! to come to see faces

[009-176] and behold only backs! to be a poet, and to reap the success of

[009-177] an apothecary! It is true that Homerus begged through the Greek

[009-178] towns, and that Naso died in exile among the Muscovites. But may

[009-179] the devil flay me if I understand what they mean with their

[009-180] Esmeralda! What is that word, in the first place?—’tis Egyptian!”

[009-181] BOOK SECOND.

[009-182] CHAPTER I. FROM CHARYBDIS TO SCYLLA.

[009-183] Night comes on early in January. The streets were already dark

[009-184] when Gringoire issued forth from the Courts. This gloom pleased

[009-185] him; he was in haste to reach some obscure and deserted alley, in

[009-186] order there to meditate at his ease, and in order that the

[009-187] philosopher might place the first dressing upon the wound of the

[009-188] poet. Philosophy, moreover, was his sole refuge, for he did not

[009-189] know where he was to lodge for the night. After the brilliant

[009-190] failure of his first theatrical venture, he dared not return to

[009-191] the lodging which he occupied in the Rue Grenier-sur-l’Eau,

[009-192] opposite to the Port-au-Foin, having depended upon receiving from

[009-193] monsieur the provost for his epithalamium, the wherewithal to pay

[009-194] Master Guillaume Doulx-Sire, farmer of the taxes on cloven-footed

[009-195] animals in Paris, the rent which he owed him, that is to say,

[009-196] twelve sols parisian; twelve times the value of all that he

[009-197] possessed in the world, including his trunk-hose, his shirt, and

[009-198] his cap. After reflecting a moment, temporarily sheltered beneath

[009-199] the little wicket of the prison of the treasurer of the

[009-200] Sainte-Chappelle, as to the shelter which he would select for the

[009-201] night, having all the pavements of Paris to choose from, he

[009-202] remembered to have noticed the week previously in the Rue de la

[009-203] Savaterie, at the door of a councillor of the parliament, a

[009-204] stepping stone for mounting a mule, and to have said to himself

[009-205] that that stone would furnish, on occasion, a very excellent

[009-206] pillow for a mendicant or a poet. He thanked Providence for

[009-207] having sent this happy idea to him; but, as he was preparing to

[009-208] cross the Place, in order to reach the tortuous labyrinth of the

[009-209] city, where meander all those old sister streets, the Rues de la

[009-210] Barillerie, de la Vieille-Draperie, de la Savaterie, de la

[009-211] Juiverie, etc., still extant to-day, with their nine-story

[009-212] houses, he saw the procession of the Pope of the Fools, which was

[009-213] also emerging from the court house, and rushing across the

[009-214] courtyard, with great cries, a great flashing of torches, and the

[009-215] music which belonged to him, Gringoire. This sight revived the

[009-216] pain of his self-love; he fled. In the bitterness of his dramatic

[009-217] misadventure, everything which reminded him of the festival of

[009-218] that day irritated his wound and made it bleed.
