# Guillaume Rym

A Flemish counselor and diplomat attached to the embassy of Maximilian of Austria, whose intelligence, polish, and sardonic detachment make him one of the most penetrating observers of French society in Victor Hugo's novel.

## Role in the Novel

Guillaume Rym serves as a silent chorus and political commentator in the novel — an intelligent foreigner whose presence allows Hugo to hold a mirror up to French institutions.

He appears in two major set-pieces: the tumultuous Great Hall sequence in Book I, where he is introduced alongside the Flemish embassy, and the private audience with [[Louis XI]] at [[bastille|the Bastille]] in Book X, where he and [[Jacques Coppenole]] — though Coppenole is not yet a page — witness the king's mixture of piety, paranoia, and cynicism. ((006-128, 006-130, 006-193))

Rym is intelligent where Coppenole is bold, polished where Coppenole is blunt; together they form a complementary pair of outside perspectives on the French court and its decadence.

## Biography

### Origins and Background

Guillaume Rym is the "counsellor and pensioner of the City of Ghent" ((006-128)). Hugo identifies him as a historical figure, noting that few in the crowd at [[palais-de-justice|the Great Hall]] recognized him, though he was "a rare genius who in a time of revolution would have made a brilliant appearance on the surface of events, but who in the fifteenth century was reduced to cavernous intrigues" ((006-130)).

He was already familiar with [[Louis XI]], having "plotted familiarly" with the king and lent a hand to the king's secret jobs ((006-135)). The chronicler Philippe de Comines described him as a "sage and malicious man" ((006-193)).

### Key Appearances

**Book I: The Great Hall.** Rym first appears as part of the Flemish embassy attending the performance of [[Pierre Gringoire]]'s mystery play. He is introduced with a "subtle, intelligent, crafty-looking face, a sort of combined monkey and diplomat phiz" ((006-126)).

Throughout the chaos — [[pierre-gringoire|Gringoire]]'s failed play, the election of the [[quasimodo|Pope of Fools]], and the general disorder — Rym remains a calm, amused observer. He alone notices the Cardinal's discomfiture and retreat ((008-071)). When the Cardinal seeks his opinion, Rym replies with dry humor: "let us be content with having escaped half of the comedy" ((007-137)).

**Book X: The [[bastille|Bastille]].** Rym reappears much later in the novel, at a private audience with [[louis-xi|Louis XI]] in the king's retreat at [[bastille|the Bastille]]. Here he functions as a witness to the king's peculiar combination of miserliness and ruthlessness.

Rym quietly notes the king's character — observing his physician Coictier and his [[death|execution]]er Tristan l'Hermite, he murmurs to Coppenole: "Behold him between Coictier and Tristan. They are his whole court. A physician for himself, a headsman for others" ((078-194)).

When Louis orders the [[death|execution]] of the witch and the destruction of the mob in the same breath, Rym delivers the novel's most devastating political commentary: "punish the people for willing a thing, and then do what they wish" ((080-082)).

### Fate

Guillaume Rym's role in the novel concludes with his dismissal from [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]'s presence, at which point he and Coppenole retire to their apartments in [[bastille|the Bastille]]. As a historical figure who existed before and beyond the events of the novel, Rym simply exits the stage once his narrative function as a witness is complete. ((080-119))

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Rym is defined by his intelligence, restraint, and ironic detachment. Hugo gives him a "polished smile" ((006-164)) and a manner of watching events with "a smile of raillery and superiority" ((006-193)).

He is prudent where Coppenole is reckless — when Coppenole grumbles about having to stand in the king's presence, Rym cautions him with quiet humor: "Or on his knees" ((075-161)). His worldliness is captured in his remark after their audience with [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]: "Master Jacques, 'tis because wine renders kings less cruel than does barley water" ((080-123)).

### Relationships

- **[[Jacques Coppenole]]**: Rym's foil and companion throughout the novel. Where the hosier is blunt and willing to speak truth to power, Rym is subtle, watchful, and ironic. He frequently restrains Coppenole's bolder impulses while respecting his integrity. ((006-144, [077-125], 079-151))

- **[[Louis XI]]**: Rym's pre-existing familiarity with the king marks him as a player in European political intrigue. He observes Louis with the cold eye of a fellow diplomat, seeing through theatrical piety and calculated cruelty. ((006-135, 078-194))

- **[[Pierre Gringoire]]**: Rym witnesses [[pierre-gringoire|the poet]]'s humiliation in [[palais-de-justice|the Great Hall]] and later his desperate plea for clemency before the king. He remarks dryly: "He doth well to drag himself on the earth. Kings are like the Jupiter of Crete, they have ears only in their feet" ((078-147))

### Symbolism

Rym represents the tradition of European humanist diplomacy — the intelligent, literate counselor navigating the treacherous waters of fifteenth-century power politics. Along with Coppenole, he embodies the Flemish embassy as a mirror held up to the decadence and cruelty of the French court.

While Coppenole represents the raw voice of the common people, Rym represents their intellectual and political consciousness. His final observation about [[louis-xi|Louis XI]] distills Hugo's critique of monarchical hypocrisy into a devastating aphorism.

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo uses the historical figure of Guillaume Rym — the real "pensioner of Ghent" and Flemish diplomat — to anchor his novel in the political realities of the late fifteenth century. The author explicitly references Philippe de Comines's characterization of Rym as a "sage and malicious man" ((006-193)), lending the portrait historical credibility.

More importantly, Hugo deploys Rym as a device of authorial commentary: through Rym's quiet, intelligent observations, Hugo can pass judgment on [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]'s court without breaking the novel's narrative frame. Rym's presence — together with Coppenole's — allows the Flemish embassy to function as a structural critique of French institutions.
