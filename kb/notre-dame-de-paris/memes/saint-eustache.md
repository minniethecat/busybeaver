# Saint-Eustache

The Church of Saint-Eustache stands in the [[les-halles|Les Halles]] district of [[paris|Paris]],
one of [[paris|the city]]'s most significant parish [[religion|church]]es. In Victor Hugo's
*[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, it is not a scene of action but an essential
voice in the great symphony of [[paris|Paris]]ian bells that the author so
lovingly describes.

## The Bells of Saint-Eustache

Hugo's most vivid treatment of Saint-Eustache comes in his celebrated
passage on the bells of [[paris|Paris]]. He describes the auditory panorama of
[[paris|the city]] at dawn, when every [[religion|church]] tower sends its voice into the
air:

> "you admire in their midst the rich gamut which incessantly ascends
> and re-ascends the seven bells of Saint-Eustache; you see light and
> rapid notes running across it, executing three or four luminous
> zigzags, and vanishing like flashes of lightning" [024-054]

The seven bells of Saint-Eustache — a full musical gamut — distinguish
this [[religion|church]] from others in Hugo's soundscape. While the Abbey of
Saint-Martin is "a shrill, cracked singer" and [[bastille|the Bastille]] offers "the
gruff and gloomy voice," Saint-Eustache contributes "light and rapid
notes," a vivid, almost visual description of sound [024-054].

## In Hugo's Architectural Panorama

Saint-Eustache belongs to the dense network of [[paris|Paris]]ian [[religion|church]]es that
Hugo juxtaposes throughout the novel. It appears in the famous
bell-ringing chapter alongside Saint-Martin, [[bastille|the Bastille]], the Louvre
tower, and the royal chime of the palace — each contributing a distinct
voice to the urban symphony [024-054].

The [[religion|church]] also stands as an architectural witness to the [[les-halles|Les Halles]]
district, the commercial heart of [[paris|medieval Paris]]. Hugo's mention of
Saint-Eustache connects the sacred geography of the Right Bank to the
profane bustle of the market, a juxtaposition that runs throughout
the novel.

## Historical Significance

Although Hugo wrote in 1831, the Saint-Eustache of his novel's 1482
setting was a simpler structure than the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] [[religion|church]] visible
today. The original 13th-century [[religion|church]] dedicated to Saint Agnes was
replaced and renamed; the present building was largely constructed
between 1532 and 1637. Hugo's [[paris|medieval Paris]] thus features a
Saint-Eustache still in its earlier form — a neighborhood parish
[[religion|church]] with a remarkable set of bells.
