---[048-001] “Oh! good Brother Claude,” resumed Jehan, emboldened by this

[048-002] smile, “look at my worn out boots. Is there a cothurnus in the

[048-003] world more tragic than these boots, whose soles are hanging out

[048-004] their tongues?”

[048-005] The archdeacon promptly returned to his original severity.

[048-006] “I will send you some new boots, but no money.”

[048-007] “Only a poor little parisis, brother,” continued the suppliant

[048-008] Jehan. “I will learn Gratian by heart, I will believe firmly in

[048-009] God, I will be a regular Pythagoras of science and virtue. But

[048-010] one little parisis, in mercy! Would you have famine bite me with

[048-011] its jaws which are gaping in front of me, blacker, deeper, and

[048-012] more noisome than a Tartarus or the nose of a monk?”

[048-013] Dom Claude shook his wrinkled head: “_Qui non laborat_—”

[048-014] Jehan did not allow him to finish.

[048-015] “Well,” he exclaimed, “to the devil then! Long live joy! I will

[048-016] live in the tavern, I will fight, I will break pots and I will go

[048-017] and see the wenches.” And thereupon, he hurled his cap at the

[048-018] wall, and snapped his fingers like castanets.

[048-019] The archdeacon surveyed him with a gloomy air.

[048-020] “Jehan, you have no soul.”

[048-021] “In that case, according to Epicurius, I lack a something made of

[048-022] another something which has no name.”

[048-023] “Jehan, you must think seriously of amending your ways.”

[048-024] “Oh, come now,” cried the student, gazing in turn at his brother

[048-025] and the alembics on the furnace, “everything is preposterous

[048-026] here, both ideas and bottles!”

[048-027] “Jehan, you are on a very slippery downward road. Do you know

[048-028] whither you are going?”

[048-029] “To the wine-shop,” said Jehan.

[048-030] “The wine-shop leads to the pillory.”

[048-031] “’Tis as good a lantern as any other, and perchance with that

[048-032] one, Diogenes would have found his man.”

[048-033] “The pillory leads to the gallows.”

[048-034] “The gallows is a balance which has a man at one end and the

[048-035] whole earth at the other. ’Tis fine to be the man.”

[048-036] “The gallows leads to hell.”

[048-037] “’Tis a big fire.”

[048-038] “Jehan, Jehan, the end will be bad.”

[048-039] “The beginning will have been good.”

[048-040] At that moment, the sound of a footstep was heard on the

[048-041] staircase.

[048-042] “Silence!” said the archdeacon, laying his finger on his mouth,

[048-043] “here is Master Jacques. Listen, Jehan,” he added, in a low

[048-044] voice; “have a care never to speak of what you shall have seen or

[048-045] heard here. Hide yourself quickly under the furnace, and do not

[048-046] breathe.”

[048-047] The scholar concealed himself; just then a happy idea occurred to

[048-048] him.

[048-049] “By the way, Brother Claude, a form for not breathing.”

[048-050] “Silence! I promise.”

[048-051] “You must give it to me.”

[048-052] “Take it, then!” said the archdeacon angrily, flinging his purse

[048-053] at him.

[048-054] Jehan darted under the furnace again, and the door opened.

[048-055] CHAPTER V. THE TWO MEN CLOTHED IN BLACK.

[048-056] The personage who entered wore a black gown and a gloomy mien.

[048-057] The first point which struck the eye of our Jehan (who, as the

[048-058] reader will readily surmise, had ensconced himself in his nook in

[048-059] such a manner as to enable him to see and hear everything at his

[048-060] good pleasure) was the perfect sadness of the garments and the

[048-061] visage of this new-corner. There was, nevertheless, some

[048-062] sweetness diffused over that face, but it was the sweetness of a

[048-063] cat or a judge, an affected, treacherous sweetness. He was very

[048-064] gray and wrinkled, and not far from his sixtieth year, his eyes

[048-065] blinked, his eyebrows were white, his lip pendulous, and his

[048-066] hands large. When Jehan saw that it was only this, that is to

[048-067] say, no doubt a physician or a magistrate, and that this man had

[048-068] a nose very far from his mouth, a sign of stupidity, he nestled

[048-069] down in his hole, in despair at being obliged to pass an

[048-070] indefinite time in such an uncomfortable attitude, and in such

[048-071] bad company.

[048-072] The archdeacon, in the meantime, had not even risen to receive

[048-073] this personage. He had made the latter a sign to seat himself on

[048-074] a stool near the door, and, after several moments of a silence

[048-075] which appeared to be a continuation of a preceding meditation, he

[048-076] said to him in a rather patronizing way, “Good day, Master

[048-077] Jacques.”

[048-078] “Greeting, master,” replied the man in black.

[048-079] There was in the two ways in which “Master Jacques” was

[048-080] pronounced on the one hand, and the “master” by preeminence on

[048-081] the other, the difference between monseigneur and monsieur,

[048-082] between _domine_ and _domne_. It was evidently the meeting of a

[048-083] teacher and a disciple.

[048-084] “Well!” resumed the archdeacon, after a fresh silence which

[048-085] Master Jacques took good care not to disturb, “how are you

[048-086] succeeding?”

[048-087] “Alas! master,” said the other, with a sad smile, “I am still

[048-088] seeking the stone. Plenty of ashes. But not a spark of gold.”

[048-089] Dom Claude made a gesture of impatience. “I am not talking to you

[048-090] of that, Master Jacques Charmolue, but of the trial of your

[048-091] magician. Is it not Marc Cenaine that you call him? the butler of

[048-092] the Court of Accounts? Does he confess his witchcraft? Have you

[048-093] been successful with the torture?”

[048-094] “Alas! no,” replied Master Jacques, still with his sad smile; “we

[048-095] have not that consolation. That man is a stone. We might have him

[048-096] boiled in the Marché aux Pourceaux, before he would say anything.

[048-097] Nevertheless, we are sparing nothing for the sake of getting at

[048-098] the truth; he is already thoroughly dislocated, we are applying

[048-099] all the herbs of Saint John’s day; as saith the old comedian

[048-100] Plautus,—

[048-101] ‘Advorsum stimulos, laminas, crucesque, compedesque,

[048-102] Nervos, catenas, carceres, numellas, pedicas, boias.’

[048-103] Nothing answers; that man is terrible. I am at my wit’s end over

[048-104] him.”

[048-105] “You have found nothing new in his house?”

[048-106] “I’ faith, yes,” said Master Jacques, fumbling in his pouch;

[048-107] “this parchment. There are words in it which we cannot

[048-108] comprehend. The criminal advocate, Monsieur Philippe Lheulier,

[048-109] nevertheless, knows a little Hebrew, which he learned in that

[048-110] matter of the Jews of the Rue Kantersten, at Brussels.”

[048-111] So saying, Master Jacques unrolled a parchment. “Give it here,”

[048-112] said the archdeacon. And casting his eyes upon this writing:

[048-113] “Pure magic, Master Jacques!” he exclaimed. “‘Emen-Hétan!’ ’Tis

[048-114] the cry of the vampires when they arrive at the witches’ sabbath.

[048-115] _Per ipsum, et cum ipso, et in ipso!_ ’Tis the command which

[048-116] chains the devil in hell. _Hax, pax, max!_ that refers to

[048-117] medicine. A formula against the bite of mad dogs. Master Jacques!

[048-118] you are procurator to the king in the Ecclesiastical Courts: this

[048-119] parchment is abominable.”

[048-120] “We will put the man to the torture once more. Here again,” added

[048-121] Master Jacques, fumbling afresh in his pouch, “is something that

[048-122] we have found at Marc Cenaine’s house.”

[048-123] It was a vessel belonging to the same family as those which

[048-124] covered Dom Claude’s furnace.

[048-125] “Ah!” said the archdeacon, “a crucible for alchemy.”

[048-126] “I will confess to you,” continued Master Jacques, with his timid

[048-127] and awkward smile, “that I have tried it over the furnace, but I

[048-128] have succeeded no better than with my own.”

[048-129] The archdeacon began an examination of the vessel. “What has he

[048-130] engraved on his crucible? _Och! och!_ the word which expels

[048-131] fleas! That Marc Cenaine is an ignoramus! I verily believe that

[048-132] you will never make gold with this! ’Tis good to set in your

[048-133] bedroom in summer and that is all!”

[048-134] “Since we are talking about errors,” said the king’s procurator,

[048-135] “I have just been studying the figures on the portal below before

[048-136] ascending hither; is your reverence quite sure that the opening

[048-137] of the work of physics is there portrayed on the side towards the

[048-138] Hôtel-Dieu, and that among the seven nude figures which stand at

[048-139] the feet of Notre-Dame, that which has wings on his heels is

[048-140] Mercurius?”

[048-141] “Yes,” replied the priest; “’tis Augustin Nypho who writes it,

[048-142] that Italian doctor who had a bearded demon who acquainted him

[048-143] with all things. However, we will descend, and I will explain it

[048-144] to you with the text before us.”

[048-145] “Thanks, master,” said Charmolue, bowing to the earth. “By the

[048-146] way, I was on the point of forgetting. When doth it please you

[048-147] that I shall apprehend the little sorceress?”

[048-148] “What sorceress?”

[048-149] “That gypsy girl you know, who comes every day to dance on the

[048-150] church square, in spite of the official’s prohibition! She hath a

[048-151] demoniac goat with horns of the devil, which reads, which writes,

[048-152] which knows mathematics like Picatrix, and which would suffice to

[048-153] hang all Bohemia. The prosecution is all ready; ’twill soon be

[048-154] finished, I assure you! A pretty creature, on my soul, that

[048-155] dancer! The handsomest black eyes! Two Egyptian carbuncles! When

[048-156] shall we begin?”

[048-157] The archdeacon was excessively pale.

[048-158] “I will tell you that hereafter,” he stammered, in a voice that

[048-159] was barely articulate; then he resumed with an effort, “Busy

[048-160] yourself with Marc Cenaine.”

[048-161] “Be at ease,” said Charmolue with a smile; “I’ll buckle him down

[048-162] again for you on the leather bed when I get home. But ’tis a

[048-163] devil of a man; he wearies even Pierrat Torterue himself, who

[048-164] hath hands larger than my own. As that good Plautus saith,—

[048-165] ‘Nudus vinctus, centum pondo, es quando pendes per pedes.’

[048-166] The torture of the wheel and axle! ’Tis the most effectual! He

[048-167] shall taste it!”

[048-168] Dom Claude seemed absorbed in gloomy abstraction. He turned to

[048-169] Charmolue,—

[048-170] “Master Pierrat—Master Jacques, I mean, busy yourself with Marc

[048-171] Cenaine.”

[048-172] “Yes, yes, Dom Claude. Poor man! he will have suffered like

[048-173] Mummol. What an idea to go to the witches’ sabbath! a butler of

[048-174] the Court of Accounts, who ought to know Charlemagne’s text;

[048-175] _Stryga vel masca!_—In the matter of the little girl,—Smelarda,

[048-176] as they call her,—I will await your orders. Ah! as we pass

[048-177] through the portal, you will explain to me also the meaning of

[048-178] the gardener painted in relief, which one sees as one enters the

[048-179] church. Is it not the Sower? Hé! master, of what are you

[048-180] thinking, pray?”

[048-181] Dom Claude, buried in his own thoughts, no longer listened to

[048-182] him. Charmolue, following the direction of his glance, perceived

[048-183] that it was fixed mechanically on the great spider’s web which

[048-184] draped the window. At that moment, a bewildered fly which was

[048-185] seeking the March sun, flung itself through the net and became

[048-186] entangled there. On the agitation of his web, the enormous spider

[048-187] made an abrupt move from his central cell, then with one bound,

[048-188] rushed upon the fly, which he folded together with his fore

[048-189] antennæ, while his hideous proboscis dug into the victim’s head.

[048-190] “Poor fly!” said the king’s procurator in the ecclesiastical

[048-191] court; and he raised his hand to save it. The archdeacon, as

[048-192] though roused with a start, withheld his arm with convulsive

[048-193] violence.

[048-194] “Master Jacques,” he cried, “let fate take its course!” The

[048-195] procurator wheeled round in affright; it seemed to him that

[048-196] pincers of iron had clutched his arm. The priest’s eye was

[048-197] staring, wild, flaming, and remained riveted on the horrible

[048-198] little group of the spider and the fly.

[048-199] “Oh, yes!” continued the priest, in a voice which seemed to

[048-200] proceed from the depths of his being, “behold here a symbol of

[048-201] all. She flies, she is joyous, she is just born; she seeks the

[048-202] spring, the open air, liberty: oh, yes! but let her come in

[048-203] contact with the fatal network, and the spider issues from it,

[048-204] the hideous spider! Poor dancer! poor, predestined fly! Let

[048-205] things take their course, Master Jacques, ’tis fate! Alas!

[048-206] Claude, thou art the spider! Claude, thou art the fly also! Thou

[048-207] wert flying towards learning, light, the sun. Thou hadst no other

[048-208] care than to reach the open air, the full daylight of eternal

[048-209] truth; but in precipitating thyself towards the dazzling window

[048-210] which opens upon the other world,—upon the world of brightness,

[048-211] intelligence, and science—blind fly! senseless, learned man! thou

[048-212] hast not perceived that subtle spider’s web, stretched by destiny

[048-213] betwixt the light and thee—thou hast flung thyself headlong into

[048-214] it, and now thou art struggling with head broken and mangled

[048-215] wings between the iron antennæ of fate! Master Jacques! Master

[048-216] Jacques! let the spider work its will!”
