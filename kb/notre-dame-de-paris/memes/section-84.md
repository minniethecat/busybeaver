---[084-001] All at once, she stopped short, trembled in every limb, and cried

[084-002] in a voice which proceeded from the very depths of her being: “My

[084-003] daughter!”

[084-004] The gypsy had just drawn from the bag a little shoe absolutely

[084-005] similar to the other. To this little shoe was attached a

[084-006] parchment on which was inscribed this charm,—

[084-007] Quand le pareil retrouveras

[084-008] Ta mère te tendras les bras.[68]

[084-009] Quicker than a flash of lightning, the recluse had laid the two

[084-010] shoes together, had read the parchment and had put close to the

[084-011] bars of the window her face beaming with celestial joy as she

[084-012] cried,—

[084-013] “My daughter! my daughter!”

[084-014] “My mother!” said the gypsy.

[084-015] Here we are unequal to the task of depicting the scene. The wall

[084-016] and the iron bars were between them. “Oh! the wall!” cried the

[084-017] recluse. “Oh! to see her and not to embrace her! Your hand! your

[084-018] hand!”

[084-019] The young girl passed her arm through the opening; the recluse

[084-020] threw herself on that hand, pressed her lips to it and there

[084-021] remained, buried in that kiss, giving no other sign of life than

[084-022] a sob which heaved her breast from time to time. In the

[084-023] meanwhile, she wept in torrents, in silence, in the dark, like a

[084-024] rain at night. The poor mother poured out in floods upon that

[084-025] adored hand the dark and deep well of tears, which lay within

[084-026] her, and into which her grief had filtered, drop by drop, for

[084-027] fifteen years.

[084-028] All at once she rose, flung aside her long gray hair from her

[084-029] brow, and without uttering a word, began to shake the bars of her

[084-030] cage cell, with both hands, more furiously than a lioness. The

[084-031] bars held firm. Then she went to seek in the corner of her cell a

[084-032] huge paving stone, which served her as a pillow, and launched it

[084-033] against them with such violence that one of the bars broke,

[084-034] emitting thousands of sparks. A second blow completely shattered

[084-035] the old iron cross which barricaded the window. Then with her two

[084-036] hands, she finished breaking and removing the rusted stumps of

[084-037] the bars. There are moments when woman’s hands possess superhuman

[084-038] strength.

[084-039] A passage broken, less than a minute was required for her to

[084-040] seize her daughter by the middle of her body, and draw her into

[084-041] her cell. “Come let me draw you out of the abyss,” she murmured.

[084-042] When her daughter was inside the cell, she laid her gently on the

[084-043] ground, then raised her up again, and bearing her in her arms as

[084-044] though she were still only her little Agnès, she walked to and

[084-045] fro in her little room, intoxicated, frantic, joyous, crying out,

[084-046] singing, kissing her daughter, talking to her, bursting into

[084-047] laughter, melting into tears, all at once and with vehemence.

[084-048] “My daughter! my daughter!” she said. “I have my daughter! here

[084-049] she is! The good God has given her back to me! Ha you! come all

[084-050] of you! Is there any one there to see that I have my daughter?

[084-051] Lord Jesus, how beautiful she is! You have made me wait fifteen

[084-052] years, my good God, but it was in order to give her back to me

[084-053] beautiful.—Then the gypsies did not eat her! Who said so? My

[084-054] little daughter! my little daughter! Kiss me. Those good gypsies!

[084-055] I love the gypsies!—It is really you! That was what made my heart

[084-056] leap every time that you passed by. And I took that for hatred!

[084-057] Forgive me, my Agnès, forgive me. You thought me very malicious,

[084-058] did you not? I love you. Have you still the little mark on your

[084-059] neck? Let us see. She still has it. Oh! you are beautiful! It was

[084-060] I who gave you those big eyes, mademoiselle. Kiss me. I love you.

[084-061] It is nothing to me that other mothers have children; I scorn

[084-062] them now. They have only to come and see. Here is mine. See her

[084-063] neck, her eyes, her hair, her hands. Find me anything as

[084-064] beautiful as that! Oh! I promise you she will have lovers, that

[084-065] she will! I have wept for fifteen years. All my beauty has

[084-066] departed and has fallen to her. Kiss me.”

[084-067] She addressed to her a thousand other extravagant remarks, whose

[084-068] accent constituted their sole beauty, disarranged the poor girl’s

[084-069] garments even to the point of making her blush, smoothed her

[084-070] silky hair with her hand, kissed her foot, her knee, her brow,

[084-071] her eyes, was in raptures over everything. The young girl let her

[084-072] have her way, repeating at intervals and very low and with

[084-073] infinite tenderness, “My mother!”

[084-074] “Do you see, my little girl,” resumed the recluse, interspersing

[084-075] her words with kisses, “I shall love you dearly? We will go away

[084-076] from here. We are going to be very happy. I have inherited

[084-077] something in Reims, in our country. You know Reims? Ah! no, you

[084-078] do not know it; you were too small! If you only knew how pretty

[084-079] you were at the age of four months! Tiny feet that people came

[084-080] even from Epernay, which is seven leagues away, to see! We shall

[084-081] have a field, a house. I will put you to sleep in my bed. My God!

[084-082] my God! who would believe this? I have my daughter!”

[084-083] “Oh, my mother!” said the young girl, at length finding strength

[084-084] to speak in her emotion, “the gypsy woman told me so. There was a

[084-085] good gypsy of our band who died last year, and who always cared

[084-086] for me like a nurse. It was she who placed this little bag about

[084-087] my neck. She always said to me: ‘Little one, guard this jewel

[084-088] well! ’Tis a treasure. It will cause thee to find thy mother once

[084-089] again. Thou wearest thy mother about thy neck.’—The gypsy

[084-090] predicted it!”

[084-091] The sacked nun again pressed her daughter in her arms.

[084-092] “Come, let me kiss you! You say that prettily. When we are in the

[084-093] country, we will place these little shoes on an infant Jesus in

[084-094] the church. We certainly owe that to the good, holy Virgin. What

[084-095] a pretty voice you have! When you spoke to me just now, it was

[084-096] music! Ah! my Lord God! I have found my child again! But is this

[084-097] story credible? Nothing will kill one—or I should have died of

[084-098] joy.”

[084-099] And then she began to clap her hands again and to laugh and to

[084-100] cry out: “We are going to be so happy!”

[084-101] At that moment, the cell resounded with the clang of arms and a

[084-102] galloping of horses which seemed to be coming from the Pont

[084-103] Notre-Dame, amid advancing farther and farther along the quay.

[084-104] The gypsy threw herself with anguish into the arms of the sacked

[084-105] nun.

[084-106] “Save me! save me! mother! they are coming!”

[084-107] “Oh, heaven! what are you saying? I had forgotten! They are in

[084-108] pursuit of you! What have you done?”

[084-109] “I know not,” replied the unhappy child; “but I am condemned to

[084-110] die.”

[084-111] “To die!” said Gudule, staggering as though struck by lightning;

[084-112] “to die!” she repeated slowly, gazing at her daughter with

[084-113] staring eyes.

[084-114] “Yes, mother,” replied the frightened young girl, “they want to

[084-115] kill me. They are coming to seize me. That gallows is for me!

[084-116] Save me! save me! They are coming! Save me!”

[084-117] The recluse remained for several moments motionless and

[084-118] petrified, then she moved her head in sign of doubt, and suddenly

[084-119] giving vent to a burst of laughter, but with that terrible laugh

[084-120] which had come back to her,—

[084-121] “Ho! ho! no! ’tis a dream of which you are telling me. Ah, yes! I

[084-122] lost her, that lasted fifteen years, and then I found her again,

[084-123] and that lasted a minute! And they would take her from me again!

[084-124] And now, when she is beautiful, when she is grown up, when she

[084-125] speaks to me, when she loves me; it is now that they would come

[084-126] to devour her, before my very eyes, and I her mother! Oh! no!

[084-127] these things are not possible. The good God does not permit such

[084-128] things as that.”

[084-129] Here the cavalcade appeared to halt, and a voice was heard to say

[084-130] in the distance,—

[084-131] “This way, Messire Tristan! The priest says that we shall find

[084-132] her at the Rat-Hole.” The noise of the horses began again.

[084-133] The recluse sprang to her feet with a shriek of despair. “Fly!

[084-134] fly! my child! All comes back to me. You are right. It is your

[084-135] death! Horror! Maledictions! Fly!”

[084-136] She thrust her head through the window, and withdrew it again

[084-137] hastily.

[084-138] “Remain,” she said, in a low, curt, and lugubrious tone, as she

[084-139] pressed the hand of the gypsy, who was more dead than alive.

[084-140] “Remain! Do not breathe! There are soldiers everywhere. You

[084-141] cannot get out. It is too light.”

[084-142] Her eyes were dry and burning. She remained silent for a moment;

[084-143] but she paced the cell hurriedly, and halted now and then to

[084-144] pluck out handfuls of her gray hairs, which she afterwards tore

[084-145] with her teeth.

[084-146] Suddenly she said: “They draw near. I will speak with them. Hide

[084-147] yourself in this corner. They will not see you. I will tell them

[084-148] that you have made your escape. That I released you, i’ faith!”

[084-149] She set her daughter (down for she was still carrying her), in

[084-150] one corner of the cell which was not visible from without. She

[084-151] made her crouch down, arranged her carefully so that neither foot

[084-152] nor hand projected from the shadow, untied her black hair which

[084-153] she spread over her white robe to conceal it, placed in front of

[084-154] her her jug and her paving stone, the only articles of furniture

[084-155] which she possessed, imagining that this jug and stone would hide

[084-156] her. And when this was finished she became more tranquil, and

[084-157] knelt down to pray. The day, which was only dawning, still left

[084-158] many shadows in the Rat-Hole.

[084-159] At that moment, the voice of the priest, that infernal voice,

[084-160] passed very close to the cell, crying,—

[084-161] “This way, Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers.”

[084-162] At that name, at that voice, la Esmeralda, crouching in her

[084-163] corner, made a movement.

[084-164] “Do not stir!” said Gudule.

[084-165] She had barely finished when a tumult of men, swords, and horses

[084-166] halted around the cell. The mother rose quickly and went to post

[084-167] herself before her window, in order to stop it up. She beheld a

[084-168] large troop of armed men, both horse and foot, drawn up on the

[084-169] Grève.

[084-170] The commander dismounted, and came toward her.

[084-171] “Old woman!” said this man, who had an atrocious face, “we are in

[084-172] search of a witch to hang her; we were told that you had her.”

[084-173] The poor mother assumed as indifferent an air as she could, and

[084-174] replied,—

[084-175] “I know not what you mean.”

[084-176] The other resumed, “_Tête Dieu!_ What was it that frightened

[084-177] archdeacon said? Where is he?”

[084-178] “Monseigneur,” said a soldier, “he has disappeared.”

[084-179] “Come, now, old madwoman,” began the commander again, “do not

[084-180] lie. A sorceress was given in charge to you. What have you done

[084-181] with her?”

[084-182] The recluse did not wish to deny all, for fear of awakening

[084-183] suspicion, and replied in a sincere and surly tone,—

[084-184] “If you are speaking of a big young girl who was put into my

[084-185] hands a while ago, I will tell you that she bit me, and that I

[084-186] released her. There! Leave me in peace.”

[084-187] The commander made a grimace of disappointment. “Don’t lie to me,

[084-188] old spectre!” said he. “My name is Tristan l’Hermite, and I am

[084-189] the king’s gossip. Tristan the Hermit, do you hear?” He added, as

[084-190] he glanced at the Place de Grève around him, “’Tis a name which

[084-191] has an echo here.”

[084-192] “You might be Satan the Hermit,” replied Gudule, who was

[084-193] regaining hope, “but I should have nothing else to say to you,

[084-194] and I should never be afraid of you.”

[084-195] “_Tête-Dieu_,” said Tristan, “here is a crone! Ah! So the witch

[084-196] girl hath fled! And in which direction did she go?” Gudule
