[090-030] 20 (return) This is the same which is called, according to locality,

[090-031] climate, and races, Lombard, Saxon, or Byzantine. There are four sister

[090-032] and parallel architectures, each having its special character, but

[090-033] derived from the same origin, the round arch.

[090-034] _Facies non omnibus una,

[090-035] Non diversa tamen, qualem_, etc.

[090-036] Their faces not all alike, nor yet different, but such as the faces of

[090-037] sisters ought to be.

[090-038] 21 (return) This portion of the spire, which was of woodwork, is

[090-039] precisely that which was consumed by lightning, in 1823.

[090-040] 22 (return) The wall walling Paris makes Paris murmur.

[090-041] 23 (return) We have seen with sorrow mingled with indignation, that it

[090-042] is the intention to increase, to recast, to make over, that is to say,

[090-043] to destroy this admirable palace. The architects of our day have too

[090-044] heavy a hand to touch these delicate works of the Renaissance. We still

[090-045] cherish a hope that they will not dare. Moreover, this demolition of

[090-046] the Tuileries now, would be not only a brutal deed of violence, which

[090-047] would make a drunken vandal blush—it would be an act of treason. The

[090-048] Tuileries is not simply a masterpiece of the art of the sixteenth

[090-049] century, it is a page of the history of the nineteenth. This palace no

[090-050] longer belongs to the king, but to the people. Let us leave it as it

[090-051] is. Our revolution has twice set its seal upon its front. On one of its

[090-052] two façades, there are the cannon-balls of the 10th of August; on the

[090-053] other, the balls of the 29th of July. It is sacred. Paris, April 7,

[090-054] 1831. (_Note to the fifth edition._)

[090-096] 51 (return) Bark, Grève, grumble, Grève! Spin, spin, my distaff, spin

[090-097] her rope for the hangman, who is whistling in the meadow. What a

[090-098] beautiful hempen rope! Sow hemp, not wheat, from Issy to Vanvre. The

[090-099] thief hath not stolen the beautiful hempen rope. Grumble, Grève, bark,

[090-100] Grève! To see the dissolute wench hang on the blear-eyed gibbet,

[090-101] windows are eyes.

[090-102] 52 (return) Look not at the face, young girl, look at the heart. The

[090-103] heart of a handsome young man is often deformed. There are hearts in

[090-104] which love does not keep. Young girl, the pine is not beautiful; it is

[090-105] not beautiful like the poplar, but it keeps its foliage in winter.

[090-106] Alas! What is the use of saying that? That which is not beautiful has

[090-107] no right to exist; beauty loves only beauty; April turns her back on

[090-108] January. Beauty is perfect, beauty can do all things, beauty is the

[090-109] only thing which does not exist by halves. The raven flies only by day,

[090-110] the owl flies only by night, the swan flies by day and by night.
