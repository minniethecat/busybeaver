---[089-001] The mass of masonry which served as foundation to the odious

[089-002] edifice was hollow. A huge cellar had been constructed there,

[089-003] closed by an old iron grating, which was out of order, into which

[089-004] were cast not only the human remains, which were taken from the

[089-005] chains of Montfaucon, but also the bodies of all the unfortunates

[089-006] executed on the other permanent gibbets of Paris. To that deep

[089-007] charnel-house, where so many human remains and so many crimes

[089-008] have rotted in company, many great ones of this world, many

[089-009] innocent people, have contributed their bones, from Enguerrand de

[089-010] Marigni, the first victim, and a just man, to Admiral de Coligni,

[089-011] who was its last, and who was also a just man.

[089-012] As for the mysterious disappearance of Quasimodo, this is all

[089-013] that we have been able to discover.

[089-014] About eighteen months or two years after the events which

[089-015] terminate this story, when search was made in that cavern for the

[089-016] body of Olivier le Daim, who had been hanged two days previously,

[089-017] and to whom Charles VIII. had granted the favor of being buried

[089-018] in Saint Laurent, in better company, they found among all those

[089-019] hideous carcasses two skeletons, one of which held the other in

[089-020] its embrace. One of these skeletons, which was that of a woman,

[089-021] still had a few strips of a garment which had once been white,

[089-022] and around her neck was to be seen a string of adrézarach beads

[089-023] with a little silk bag ornamented with green glass, which was

[089-024] open and empty. These objects were of so little value that the

[089-025] executioner had probably not cared for them. The other, which

[089-026] held this one in a close embrace, was the skeleton of a man. It

[089-027] was noticed that his spinal column was crooked, his head seated

[089-028] on his shoulder blades, and that one leg was shorter than the

[089-029] other. Moreover, there was no fracture of the vertebrae at the

[089-030] nape of the neck, and it was evident that he had not been hanged.

[089-031] Hence, the man to whom it had belonged had come thither and had

[089-032] died there. When they tried to detach the skeleton which he held

[089-033] in his embrace, he fell to dust.

[089-034] NOTE

[089-035] ADDED TO THE DEFINITIVE EDITION.

[089-036] It is by mistake that this edition was announced as augmented by

[089-037] many _new_ chapters. The word should have been _unpublished_. In

[089-038] fact, if by new, _newly made_ is to be understood, the chapters

[089-039] added to this edition are not new. They were written at the same

[089-040] time as the rest of the work; they date from the same epoch, and

[089-041] sprang from the same thought, they have always formed a part of

[089-042] the manuscript of “Notre-Dame-de-Paris.” Moreover, the author

[089-043] cannot comprehend how fresh developments could be added to a work

[089-044] of this character after its completion. This is not to be done at

[089-045] will. According to his idea, a romance is born in a manner that

[089-046] is, in some sort, necessary, with all its chapters; a drama is

[089-047] born with all its scenes. Think not that there is anything

[089-048] arbitrary in the numbers of parts of which that whole, that

[089-049] mysterious microcosm which you call a drama or a romance, is

[089-050] composed. Grafting and soldering take badly on works of this

[089-051] nature, which should gush forth in a single stream and so remain.

[089-052] The thing once done, do not change your mind, do not touch it up.

[089-053] The book once published, the sex of the work, whether virile or

[089-054] not, has been recognized and proclaimed; when the child has once

[089-055] uttered his first cry he is born, there he is, he is made so,

[089-056] neither father nor mother can do anything, he belongs to the air

[089-057] and to the sun, let him live or die, such as he is. Has your book

[089-058] been a failure? So much the worse. Add no chapters to an

[089-059] unsuccessful book. Is it incomplete? You should have completed it

[089-060] when you conceived it. Is your tree crooked? You cannot

[089-061] straighten it up. Is your romance consumptive? Is your romance

[089-062] not capable of living? You cannot supply it with the breath which

[089-063] it lacks. Has your drama been born lame? Take my advice, and do

[089-064] not provide it with a wooden leg.

[089-065] Hence the author attaches particular importance to the public

[089-066] knowing for a certainty that the chapters here added have not

[089-067] been made expressly for this reprint. They were not published in

[089-068] the preceding editions of the book for a very simple reason. At

[089-069] the time when “Notre-Dame-de-Paris” was printed the first time,

[089-070] the manuscript of these three chapters had been mislaid. It was

[089-071] necessary to rewrite them or to dispense with them. The author

[089-072] considered that the only two of these chapters which were in the

[089-073] least important, owing to their extent, were chapters on art and

[089-074] history which in no way interfered with the groundwork of the

[089-075] drama and the romance, that the public would not notice their

[089-076] loss, and that he, the author, would alone be in possession of

[089-077] the secret. He decided to omit them, and then, if the whole truth

[089-078] must be confessed, his indolence shrunk from the task of

[089-079] rewriting the three lost chapters. He would have found it a

[089-080] shorter matter to make a new romance.

[089-081] Now the chapters have been found, and he avails himself of the

[089-082] first opportunity to restore them to their place.

[089-083] This now, is his entire work, such as he dreamed it, such as he

[089-084] made it, good or bad, durable or fragile, but such as he wishes

[089-085] it.

[089-086] These recovered chapters will possess no doubt, but little value

[089-087] in the eyes of persons, otherwise very judicious, who have sought

[089-088] in “Notre-Dame-de-Paris” only the drama, the romance. But there

[089-089] are perchance, other readers, who have not found it useless to

[089-090] study the æsthetic and philosophic thought concealed in this

[089-091] book, and who have taken pleasure, while reading

[089-092] “Notre-Dame-de-Paris,” in unravelling beneath the romance

[089-093] something else than the romance, and in following (may we be

[089-094] pardoned these rather ambitious expressions), the system of the

[089-095] historian and the aim of the artist through the creation of the

[089-096] poet.

[089-097] For such people especially, the chapters added to this edition

[089-098] will complete “Notre-Dame-de-Paris,” if we admit that

[089-099] “Notre-Dame-de-Paris” was worth the trouble of completing.

[089-100] In one of these chapters on the present decadence of

[089-101] architecture, and on the death (in his mind almost inevitable) of

[089-102] that king of arts, the author expresses and develops an opinion

[089-103] unfortunately well rooted in him, and well thought out. But he

[089-104] feels it necessary to say here that he earnestly desires that the

[089-105] future may, some day, put him in the wrong. He knows that art in

[089-106] all its forms has everything to hope from the new generations

[089-107] whose genius, still in the germ, can be heard gushing forth in

[089-108] our studios. The grain is in the furrow, the harvest will

[089-109] certainly be fine. He merely fears, and the reason may be seen in

[089-110] the second volume of this edition, that the sap may have been

[089-111] withdrawn from that ancient soil of architecture which has been

[089-112] for so many centuries the best field for art.

[089-113] Nevertheless, there are to-day in the artistic youth so much

[089-114] life, power, and, so to speak, predestination, that in our

[089-115] schools of architecture in particular, at the present time, the

[089-116] professors, who are detestable, produce, not only unconsciously

[089-117] but even in spite of themselves, excellent pupils; quite the

[089-118] reverse of that potter mentioned by Horace, who dreamed amphoræ

[089-119] and produced pots. _Currit rota, urcens exit_.

[089-120] But, in any case, whatever may be the future of architecture, in

[089-121] whatever manner our young architects may one day solve the

[089-122] question of their art, let us, while waiting for new monument,

[089-123] preserve the ancient monuments. Let us, if possible, inspire the

[089-124] nation with a love for national architecture. That, the author

[089-125] declares, is one of the principal aims of this book; it is one of

[089-126] the principal aims of his life.

[089-127] “Notre-Dame-de-Paris” has, perhaps opened some true perspectives

[089-128] on the art of the Middle Ages, on that marvellous art which up to

[089-129] the present time has been unknown to some, and, what is worse,

[089-130] misknown by others. But the author is far from regarding as

[089-131] accomplished, the task which he has voluntarily imposed on

[089-132] himself. He has already pleaded on more than one occasion, the

[089-133] cause of our ancient architecture, he has already loudly

[089-134] denounced many profanations, many demolitions, many impieties. He

[089-135] will not grow weary. He has promised himself to recur frequently

[089-136] to this subject. He will return to it. He will be as

[089-137] indefatigable in defending our historical edifices as our

[089-138] iconoclasts of the schools and academies are eager in attacking

[089-139] them; for it is a grievous thing to see into what hands the

[089-140] architecture of the Middle Ages has fallen, and in what a manner

[089-141] the botchers of plaster of the present day treat the ruin of this

[089-142] grand art, it is even a shame for us intelligent men who see them

[089-143] at work and content ourselves with hooting them. And we are not

[089-144] speaking here merely of what goes on in the provinces, but of

[089-145] what is done in Paris at our very doors, beneath our windows, in

[089-146] the great city, in the lettered city, in the city of the press,

[089-147] of word, of thought. We cannot resist the impulse to point out,

[089-148] in concluding this note, some of the acts of vandalism which are

[089-149] every day planned, debated, begun, continued, and successfully

[089-150] completed under the eyes of the artistic public of Paris, face to

[089-151] face with criticism, which is disconcerted by so much audacity.

[089-152] An archbishop’s palace has just been demolished, an edifice in

[089-153] poor taste, no great harm is done; but in a block with the

[089-154] archiepiscopal palace a bishop’s palace has been demolished, a

[089-155] rare fragment of the fourteenth century, which the demolishing

[089-156] architect could not distinguish from the rest. He has torn up the

[089-157] wheat with the tares; ’tis all the same. They are talking of

[089-158] razing the admirable chapel of Vincennes, in order to make, with

[089-159] its stones, some fortification, which Daumesnil did not need,

[089-160] however. While the Palais Bourbon, that wretched edifice, is

[089-161] being repaired at great expense, gusts of wind and equinoctial

[089-162] storms are allowed to destroy the magnificent painted windows of

[089-163] the Sainte-Chapelle. For the last few days there has been a

[089-164] scaffolding on the tower of Saint Jacques de la Boucherie; and

[089-165] one of these mornings the pick will be laid to it. A mason has

[089-166] been found to build a little white house between the venerable

[089-167] towers of the Palais de-Justice. Another has been found willing

[089-168] to prune away Saint-Germain-des-Prés, the feudal abbey with three

[089-169] bell towers. Another will be found, no doubt, capable of pulling

[089-170] down Saint-Germain l’Auxerrois. All these masons claim to be

[089-171] architects, are paid by the prefecture or from the petty budget,

[089-172] and wear green coats. All the harm which false taste can inflict

[089-173] on good taste, they accomplish. While we write, deplorable

[089-174] spectacle! one of them holds possession of the Tuileries, one of

[089-175] them is giving Philibert Delorme a scar across the middle of his

[089-176] face; and it is not, assuredly, one of the least of the scandals

[089-177] of our time to see with what effrontery the heavy architecture of

[089-178] this gentleman is being flattened over one of the most delicate

[089-179] façades of the Renaissance!

[089-180] PARIS, October 20, 1832.
