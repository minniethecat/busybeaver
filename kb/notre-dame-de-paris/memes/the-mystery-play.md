# The Mystery Play

The Mystery Play — officially titled *The Good Judgement of the Virgin Mary* — is Pierre Gringoire's ambitious theatrical production staged at the Palais de Justice on January 6, 1482, as part of the Festival of Fools celebrations. Intended to showcase the playwright's classical erudition and win royal patronage, the play instead becomes a study in artistic humiliation as it is repeatedly interrupted and ultimately abandoned by its audience.

## Context

The mystery play was to be one of three attractions offered to the Parisian crowd on the double feast of Epiphany and the Feast of Fools: a bonfire at the Place de Grève, a maypole at the Chapelle de Braque, and "a mystery at the Palais de Justice" [001-157]. Gringoire, a struggling poet and philosopher, had composed an allegorical piece in the medieval mystery tradition, filled with classical allusions and moral personifications.

The play's misfortune begins before it starts. The crowd, initially gathered to see the Flemish ambassadors who were expected at the performance, grows increasingly restless as both the ambassadors and the cardinal are late. "They knew that the Flemish ambassadors, who had arrived two days previously, proposed to be present at the representation of the mystery, and at the election of the Pope of Fools" [001-178].

## The Event

### Narrative Account

Gringoire's play opens with a prologue delivered by a personified figure of "the actor dressed as a woman." Before the prologue can establish momentum, it is interrupted by a beggar (actually [[clopin-trouillefou]] in disguise) calling from atop a pillar, demanding alms. [[jehan-frollo]], the student in the crowd, recognizes the beggar and engages in a loud, distracting exchange that halts the performance [001-048].

The play resumes only to be interrupted again — this time by the arrival of the Cardinal de Bourbon, whose entrance compels the actors to pause and the audience to turn toward the ecclesiastical procession. "As soon as his illustrious eminence, the cardinal, arrives, we will resume the play" [003-202]. The Flemish ambassadors follow shortly after, led by [[jacques-coppenole]], whose blunt speech proposing a grimace contest for the election of a Pope of Fools steals the audience entirely.

By the time [[quasimodo]] is crowned Pope of Fools in the grimace contest, Gringoire's play is forgotten. The playwright wanders into the night, his masterpiece abandoned — "no one paid any attention to him" [003-223].

### Key Participants

- **[[pierre-gringoire]]**: The playwright, whose philosophical ambition meets popular indifference. His insistence on the play's artistic merit ("the mystery play") becomes a running joke.
- **[[jehan-frollo]]**: The student whose heckling of the beggar provides the first interruption, embodying the irreverent student culture that Hugo satirizes.
- **[[clopin-trouillefou]]**: Disguised as a beggar, his disruption is the first blow to Gringoire's dignity.
- **[[cardinal-de-bourbon]]**: His late arrival — and the crowd's deference to ecclesiastical rank — demonstrates how institutional power trumps artistic endeavor.
- **[[jacques-coppenole]]**: The Flemish hosier whose democratic bluntness ("a hosier is as good as a cardinal") and suggestion of the grimace contest decisively ends the play's chance of recovery.

## Location

The play is staged on a marble table in the Grand Hall of the [[palais-de-justice]], whose massive dimensions and gothic architecture Hugo describes in exhaustive detail [002-088]. The hall's scale — meant to accommodate dignitaries, ambassadors, and a vast crowd — ironically dwarfs Gringoire's theatrical ambitions.

## Consequences

The failure of the mystery play sets Gringoire on a path that leads him through the night streets of Paris, into the Court of Miracles, and ultimately into his accidental marriage with Esmeralda and his role as a marginal observer of the novel's central tragedy. More broadly, the play's failure dramatizes Hugo's thesis about the cultural transition from medieval to modern: the mystery play — a form rooted in oral, communal, and sacred tradition — cannot compete with the visceral appeal of the grotesque spectacle (Quasimodo's grimace) or the democratic energy of the carnival crowd.

## Thematic Significance

The mystery play episode functions as a comic overture to the novel's exploration of doomed artistic ambition. Gringoire's experience mirrors the broader pattern Hugo traces: in a world turning from cathedral to printing press, from sacred ritual to popular entertainment, the artist who clings to old forms is left behind. The episode also establishes the crowd as a character in its own right — fickle, hungry for sensation, and ultimately indifferent to the pretensions of high culture.

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo treats Gringoire with affectionate mockery. The playwright is not talentless — his allegorical prologue, in its fragmentary form, suggests genuine learning — but he is spectacularly out of touch with his audience. Hugo, who himself struggled with the tension between artistic integrity and popular success, invests Gringoire's failure with both comedy and pathos. The mystery play's collapse is a parable about the artist in an age of transition: to cling to the forms of the past is to be abandoned by the present.
