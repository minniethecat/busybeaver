---[083-001] He hid his face in his hands. The young girl heard him weeping.

[083-002] It was for the first time. Thus erect and shaken by sobs, he was

[083-003] more miserable and more suppliant than when on his knees. He wept

[083-004] thus for a considerable time.

[083-005] “Come!” he said, these first tears passed, “I have no more words.

[083-006] I had, however, thought well as to what you would say. Now I

[083-007] tremble and shiver and break down at the decisive moment, I feel

[083-008] conscious of something supreme enveloping us, and I stammer. Oh!

[083-009] I shall fall upon the pavement if you do not take pity on me,

[083-010] pity on yourself. Do not condemn us both. If you only knew how

[083-011] much I love you! What a heart is mine! Oh! what desertion of all

[083-012] virtue! What desperate abandonment of myself! A doctor, I mock at

[083-013] science; a gentleman, I tarnish my own name; a priest, I make of

[083-014] the missal a pillow of sensuality, I spit in the face of my God!

[083-015] all this for thee, enchantress! to be more worthy of thy hell!

[083-016] And you will not have the apostate! Oh! let me tell you all! more

[083-017] still, something more horrible, oh! Yet more horrible!...”

[083-018] As he uttered these last words, his air became utterly

[083-019] distracted. He was silent for a moment, and resumed, as though

[083-020] speaking to himself, and in a strong voice,—

[083-021] “Cain, what hast thou done with thy brother?”

[083-022] There was another silence, and he went on—

[083-023] “What have I done with him, Lord? I received him, I reared him, I

[083-024] nourished him, I loved him, I idolized him, and I have slain him!

[083-025] Yes, Lord, they have just dashed his head before my eyes on the

[083-026] stone of thine house, and it is because of me, because of this

[083-027] woman, because of her.”

[083-028] His eye was wild. His voice grew ever weaker; he repeated many

[083-029] times, yet, mechanically, at tolerably long intervals, like a

[083-030] bell prolonging its last vibration: “Because of her.—Because of

[083-031] her.”

[083-032] Then his tongue no longer articulated any perceptible sound; but

[083-033] his lips still moved. All at once he sank together, like

[083-034] something crumbling, and lay motionless on the earth, with his

[083-035] head on his knees.

[083-036] A touch from the young girl, as she drew her foot from under him,

[083-037] brought him to himself. He passed his hand slowly over his hollow

[083-038] cheeks, and gazed for several moments at his fingers, which were

[083-039] wet, “What!” he murmured, “I have wept!”

[083-040] And turning suddenly to the gypsy with unspeakable anguish,—

[083-041] “Alas! you have looked coldly on at my tears! Child, do you know

[083-042] that those tears are of lava? Is it indeed true? Nothing touches

[083-043] when it comes from the man whom one does not love. If you were to

[083-044] see me die, you would laugh. Oh! I do not wish to see you die!

[083-045] One word! A single word of pardon! Say not that you love me, say

[083-046] only that you will do it; that will suffice; I will save you. If

[083-047] not—oh! the hour is passing. I entreat you by all that is sacred,

[083-048] do not wait until I shall have turned to stone again, like that

[083-049] gibbet which also claims you! Reflect that I hold the destinies

[083-050] of both of us in my hand, that I am mad,—it is terrible,—that I

[083-051] may let all go to destruction, and that there is beneath us a

[083-052] bottomless abyss, unhappy girl, whither my fall will follow yours

[083-053] to all eternity! One word of kindness! Say one word! only one

[083-054] word!”

[083-055] She opened her mouth to answer him. He flung himself on his knees

[083-056] to receive with adoration the word, possibly a tender one, which

[083-057] was on the point of issuing from her lips. She said to him, “You

[083-058] are an assassin!”

[083-059] The priest clasped her in his arms with fury, and began to laugh

[083-060] with an abominable laugh.

[083-061] “Well, yes, an assassin!” he said, “and I will have you. You will

[083-062] not have me for your slave, you shall have me for your master. I

[083-063] will have you! I have a den, whither I will drag you. You will

[083-064] follow me, you will be obliged to follow me, or I will deliver

[083-065] you up! You must die, my beauty, or be mine! belong to the

[083-066] priest! belong to the apostate! belong to the assassin! this very

[083-067] night, do you hear? Come! joy; kiss me, mad girl! The tomb or my

[083-068] bed!”

[083-069] His eyes sparkled with impurity and rage. His lewd lips reddened

[083-070] the young girl’s neck. She struggled in his arms. He covered her

[083-071] with furious kisses.

[083-072] “Do not bite me, monster!” she cried. “Oh! the foul, odious monk!

[083-073] leave me! I will tear out thy ugly gray hair and fling it in thy

[083-074] face by the handful!”

[083-075] He reddened, turned pale, then released her and gazed at her with

[083-076] a gloomy air. She thought herself victorious, and continued,—

[083-077] “I tell you that I belong to my Phœbus, that ’tis Phœbus whom I

[083-078] love, that ’tis Phœbus who is handsome! you are old, priest! you

[083-079] are ugly! Begone!”

[083-080] He gave vent to a horrible cry, like the wretch to whom a hot

[083-081] iron is applied. “Die, then!” he said, gnashing his teeth. She

[083-082] saw his terrible look and tried to fly. He caught her once more,

[083-083] he shook her, he flung her on the ground, and walked with rapid

[083-084] strides towards the corner of the Tour-Roland, dragging her after

[083-085] him along the pavement by her beautiful hands.

[083-086] On arriving there, he turned to her,—

[083-087] “For the last time, will you be mine?”

[083-088] She replied with emphasis,—

[083-089] “No!”

[083-090] Then he cried in a loud voice,—

[083-091] “Gudule! Gudule! here is the gypsy! take your vengeance!”

[083-092] The young girl felt herself seized suddenly by the elbow. She

[083-093] looked. A fleshless arm was stretched from an opening in the

[083-094] wall, and held her like a hand of iron.

[083-095] “Hold her well,” said the priest; “’tis the gypsy escaped.

[083-096] Release her not. I will go in search of the sergeants. You shall

[083-097] see her hanged.”

[083-098] A guttural laugh replied from the interior of the wall to these

[083-099] bloody words—“Hah! hah! hah!”—The gypsy watched the priest retire

[083-100] in the direction of the Pont Notre-Dame. A cavalcade was heard in

[083-101] that direction.

[083-102] The young girl had recognized the spiteful recluse. Panting with

[083-103] terror, she tried to disengage herself. She writhed, she made

[083-104] many starts of agony and despair, but the other held her with

[083-105] incredible strength. The lean and bony fingers which bruised her,

[083-106] clenched on her flesh and met around it. One would have said that

[083-107] this hand was riveted to her arm. It was more than a chain, more

[083-108] than a fetter, more than a ring of iron, it was a living pair of

[083-109] pincers endowed with intelligence, which emerged from the wall.

[083-110] She fell back against the wall exhausted, and then the fear of

[083-111] death took possession of her. She thought of the beauty of life,

[083-112] of youth, of the view of heaven, the aspects of nature, of her

[083-113] love for Phœbus, of all that was vanishing and all that was

[083-114] approaching, of the priest who was denouncing her, of the

[083-115] headsman who was to come, of the gallows which was there. Then

[083-116] she felt terror mount to the very roots of her hair and she heard

[083-117] the mocking laugh of the recluse, saying to her in a very low

[083-118] tone: “Hah! hah! hah! you are going to be hanged!”

[083-119] She turned a dying look towards the window, and she beheld the

[083-120] fierce face of the sacked nun through the bars.

[083-121] “What have I done to you?” she said, almost lifeless.

[083-122] The recluse did not reply, but began to mumble with a singsong

[083-123] irritated, mocking intonation: “Daughter of Egypt! daughter of

[083-124] Egypt! daughter of Egypt!”

[083-125] The unhappy Esmeralda dropped her head beneath her flowing hair,

[083-126] comprehending that it was no human being she had to deal with.

[083-127] All at once the recluse exclaimed, as though the gypsy’s question

[083-128] had taken all this time to reach her brain,—“‘What have you done

[083-129] to me?’ you say! Ah! what have you done to me, gypsy! Well!

[083-130] listen.—I had a child! you see! I had a child! a child, I tell

[083-131] you!—a pretty little girl!—my Agnès!” she went on wildly, kissing

[083-132] something in the dark.—“Well! do you see, daughter of Egypt? they

[083-133] took my child from me; they stole my child; they ate my child.

[083-134] That is what you have done to me.”

[083-135] The young girl replied like a lamb,—

[083-136] “Alas! perchance I was not born then!”

[083-137] “Oh! yes!” returned the recluse, “you must have been born. You

[083-138] were among them. She would be the same age as you! so!—I have

[083-139] been here fifteen years; fifteen years have I suffered; fifteen

[083-140] years have I prayed; fifteen years have I beat my head against

[083-141] these four walls—I tell you that ’twas the gypsies who stole her

[083-142] from me, do you hear that? and who ate her with their teeth.—Have

[083-143] you a heart? imagine a child playing, a child sucking; a child

[083-144] sleeping. It is so innocent a thing!—Well! that, that is what

[083-145] they took from me, what they killed. The good God knows it well!

[083-146] To-day, it is my turn; I am going to eat the gypsy.—Oh! I would

[083-147] bite you well, if the bars did not prevent me! My head is too

[083-148] large!—Poor little one! while she was asleep! And if they woke

[083-149] her up when they took her, in vain she might cry; I was not

[083-150] there!—Ah! gypsy mothers, you devoured my child! come see your

[083-151] own.”

[083-152] Then she began to laugh or to gnash her teeth, for the two things

[083-153] resembled each other in that furious face. The day was beginning

[083-154] to dawn. An ashy gleam dimly lighted this scene, and the gallows

[083-155] grew more and more distinct in the square. On the other side, in

[083-156] the direction of the bridge of Notre-Dame, the poor condemned

[083-157] girl fancied that she heard the sound of cavalry approaching.

[083-158] “Madam,” she cried, clasping her hands and falling on her knees,

[083-159] dishevelled, distracted, mad with fright; “madam! have pity! They

[083-160] are coming. I have done nothing to you. Would you wish to see me

[083-161] die in this horrible fashion before your very eyes? You are

[083-162] pitiful, I am sure. It is too frightful. Let me make my escape.

[083-163] Release me! Mercy. I do not wish to die like that!”

[083-164] “Give me back my child!” said the recluse.

[083-165] “Mercy! Mercy!”

[083-166] “Give me back my child!”

[083-167] “Release me, in the name of heaven!”

[083-168] “Give me back my child!”

[083-169] Again the young girl fell; exhausted, broken, and having already

[083-170] the glassy eye of a person in the grave.

[083-171] “Alas!” she faltered, “you seek your child, I seek my parents.”

[083-172] “Give me back my little Agnès!” pursued Gudule. “You do not know

[083-173] where she is? Then die!—I will tell you. I was a woman of the

[083-174] town, I had a child, they took my child. It was the gypsies. You

[083-175] see plainly that you must die. When your mother, the gypsy, comes

[083-176] to reclaim you, I shall say to her: ‘Mother, look at that

[083-177] gibbet!—Or, give me back my child. Do you know where she is, my

[083-178] little daughter? Stay! I will show you. Here is her shoe, all

[083-179] that is left me of her. Do you know where its mate is? If you

[083-180] know, tell me, and if it is only at the other end of the world, I

[083-181] will crawl to it on my knees.”

[083-182] As she spoke thus, with her other arm extended through the

[083-183] window, she showed the gypsy the little embroidered shoe. It was

[083-184] already light enough to distinguish its shape and its colors.

[083-185] “Let me see that shoe,” said the gypsy, quivering. “God! God!”

[083-186] And at the same time, with her hand which was at liberty, she

[083-187] quickly opened the little bag ornamented with green glass, which

[083-188] she wore about her neck.

[083-189] “Go on, go on!” grumbled Gudule, “search your demon’s amulet!”
