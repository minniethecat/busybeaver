# Gothic Architecture in the Novel

Hugo famously declared that the novel itself is a cathedral in prose. Gothic
architecture permeates the book — not only as setting, but as argument. The
novel's original preface, "Ceci tuera cela" (This Will Kill That), frames the
printed book as the successor to the cathedral as the vessel of human knowledge.

This page draws together every wiki entry that touches on Gothic architecture,
from the stones of Notre-Dame to the ideas carved into them.

## Notes

Hugo's architectural theory is most fully expressed in Book III, Chapter 1
("Notre-Dame") and Book V, Chapter 2 ("Ceci Tuera Cela"). Pages tagged `gothic`
or `architecture` are included; the distinction between Gothic architecture
proper and broader medieval construction is sometimes blurred in Hugo's own
usage.
