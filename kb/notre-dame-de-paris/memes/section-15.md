---[015-001] Near the fire was a hogshead, and on the hogshead a beggar. This

[015-002] was the king on his throne.

[015-003] The three who had Gringoire in their clutches led him in front of

[015-004] this hogshead, and the entire bacchanal rout fell silent for a

[015-005] moment, with the exception of the cauldron inhabited by the

[015-006] child.

[015-007] Gringoire dared neither breathe nor raise his eyes.

[015-008] “_Hombre, quita tu sombrero_!” said one of the three knaves, in

[015-009] whose grasp he was, and, before he had comprehended the meaning,

[015-010] the other had snatched his hat—a wretched headgear, it is true,

[015-011] but still good on a sunny day or when there was but little rain.

[015-012] Gringoire sighed.

[015-013] Meanwhile the king addressed him, from the summit of his cask,—

[015-014] “Who is this rogue?”

[015-015] Gringoire shuddered. That voice, although accentuated by menace,

[015-016] recalled to him another voice, which, that very morning, had

[015-017] dealt the deathblow to his mystery, by drawling, nasally, in the

[015-018] midst of the audience, “Charity, please!” He raised his head. It

[015-019] was indeed Clopin Trouillefou.

[015-020] Clopin Trouillefou, arrayed in his royal insignia, wore neither

[015-021] one rag more nor one rag less. The sore upon his arm had already

[015-022] disappeared. He held in his hand one of those whips made of

[015-023] thongs of white leather, which police sergeants then used to

[015-024] repress the crowd, and which were called _boullayes_. On his head

[015-025] he wore a sort of headgear, bound round and closed at the top.

[015-026] But it was difficult to make out whether it was a child’s cap or

[015-027] a king’s crown, the two things bore so strong a resemblance to

[015-028] each other.

[015-029] Meanwhile Gringoire, without knowing why, had regained some hope,

[015-030] on recognizing in the King of the Cour des Miracles his accursed

[015-031] mendicant of the Grand Hall.

[015-032] “Master,” stammered he; “monseigneur—sire—how ought I to address

[015-033] you?” he said at length, having reached the culminating point of

[015-034] his crescendo, and knowing neither how to mount higher, nor to

[015-035] descend again.

[015-036] “Monseigneur, his majesty, or comrade, call me what you please.

[015-037] But make haste. What have you to say in your own defence?”

[015-038] “_In your own defence?_” thought Gringoire, “that displeases me.”

[015-039] He resumed, stuttering, “I am he, who this morning—”

[015-040] “By the devil’s claws!” interrupted Clopin, “your name, knave,

[015-041] and nothing more. Listen. You are in the presence of three

[015-042] powerful sovereigns: myself, Clopin Trouillefou, King of Thunes,

[015-043] successor to the Grand Coësre, supreme suzerain of the Realm of

[015-044] Argot; Mathias Hunyadi Spicali, Duke of Egypt and of Bohemia, the

[015-045] old yellow fellow whom you see yonder, with a dish clout round

[015-046] his head; Guillaume Rousseau, Emperor of Galilee, that fat fellow

[015-047] who is not listening to us but caressing a wench. We are your

[015-048] judges. You have entered the Kingdom of Argot, without being an

[015-049] _argotier_; you have violated the privileges of our city. You

[015-050] must be punished unless you are a _capon_, a _franc-mitou_ or a

[015-051] _rifodé_; that is to say, in the slang of honest folks,—a thief,

[015-052] a beggar, or a vagabond. Are you anything of that sort? Justify

[015-053] yourself; announce your titles.”

[015-054] “Alas!” said Gringoire, “I have not that honor. I am the author—”

[015-055] “That is sufficient,” resumed Trouillefou, without permitting him

[015-056] to finish. “You are going to be hanged. ’Tis a very simple

[015-057] matter, gentlemen and honest _bourgeois_! as you treat our people

[015-058] in your abode, so we treat you in ours! The law which you apply

[015-059] to vagabonds, vagabonds apply to you. ’Tis your fault if it is

[015-060] harsh. One really must behold the grimace of an honest man above

[015-061] the hempen collar now and then; that renders the thing honorable.

[015-062] Come, friend, divide your rags gayly among these damsels. I am

[015-063] going to have you hanged to amuse the vagabonds, and you are to

[015-064] give them your purse to drink your health. If you have any

[015-065] mummery to go through with, there’s a very good God the Father in

[015-066] that mortar yonder, in stone, which we stole from Saint-Pierre

[015-067] aux Bœufs. You have four minutes in which to fling your soul at

[015-068] his head.”

[015-069] The harangue was formidable.

[015-070] “Well said, upon my soul! Clopin Trouillefou preaches like the

[015-071] Holy Father the Pope!” exclaimed the Emperor of Galilee, smashing

[015-072] his pot in order to prop up his table.

[015-073] “Messeigneurs, emperors, and kings,” said Gringoire coolly (for I

[015-074] know not how, firmness had returned to him, and he spoke with

[015-075] resolution), “don’t think of such a thing; my name is Pierre

[015-076] Gringoire. I am the poet whose morality was presented this

[015-077] morning in the grand hall of the Courts.”

[015-078] “Ah! so it was you, master!” said Clopin. “I was there, _par la

[015-079] tête Dieu_! Well! comrade, is that any reason, because you bored

[015-080] us to death this morning, that you should not be hung this

[015-081] evening?”

[015-082] “I shall find difficulty in getting out of it,” said Gringoire to

[015-083] himself. Nevertheless, he made one more effort: “I don’t see why

[015-084] poets are not classed with vagabonds,” said he. “Vagabond, Æsopus

[015-085] certainly was; Homerus was a beggar; Mercurius was a thief—”

[015-086] Clopin interrupted him: “I believe that you are trying to blarney

[015-087] us with your jargon. Zounds! let yourself be hung, and don’t kick

[015-088] up such a row over it!”

[015-089] “Pardon me, monseigneur, the King of Thunes,” replied Gringoire,

[015-090] disputing the ground foot by foot. “It is worth trouble—One

[015-091] moment!—Listen to me—You are not going to condemn me without

[015-092] having heard me”—

[015-093] His unlucky voice was, in fact, drowned in the uproar which rose

[015-094] around him. The little boy scraped away at his cauldron with more

[015-095] spirit than ever; and, to crown all, an old woman had just placed

[015-096] on the tripod a frying-pan of grease, which hissed away on the

[015-097] fire with a noise similar to the cry of a troop of children in

[015-098] pursuit of a masker.

[015-099] In the meantime, Clopin Trouillefou appeared to hold a momentary

[015-100] conference with the Duke of Egypt, and the Emperor of Galilee,

[015-101] who was completely drunk. Then he shouted shrilly: “Silence!”

[015-102] and, as the cauldron and the frying-pan did not heed him, and

[015-103] continued their duet, he jumped down from his hogshead, gave a

[015-104] kick to the boiler, which rolled ten paces away bearing the child

[015-105] with it, a kick to the frying-pan, which upset in the fire with

[015-106] all its grease, and gravely remounted his throne, without

[015-107] troubling himself about the stifled tears of the child, or the

[015-108] grumbling of the old woman, whose supper was wasting away in a

[015-109] fine white flame.

[015-110] Trouillefou made a sign, and the duke, the emperor, and the

[015-111] passed masters of pickpockets, and the isolated robbers, came and

[015-112] ranged themselves around him in a horseshoe, of which Gringoire,

[015-113] still roughly held by the body, formed the centre. It was a

[015-114] semicircle of rags, tatters, tinsel, pitchforks, axes, legs

[015-115] staggering with intoxication, huge, bare arms, faces sordid,

[015-116] dull, and stupid. In the midst of this Round Table of beggary,

[015-117] Clopin Trouillefou,—as the doge of this senate, as the king of

[015-118] this peerage, as the pope of this conclave,—dominated; first by

[015-119] virtue of the height of his hogshead, and next by virtue of an

[015-120] indescribable, haughty, fierce, and formidable air, which caused

[015-121] his eyes to flash, and corrected in his savage profile the

[015-122] bestial type of the race of vagabonds. One would have pronounced

[015-123] him a boar amid a herd of swine.

[015-124] “Listen,” said he to Gringoire, fondling his misshapen chin with

[015-125] his horny hand; “I don’t see why you should not be hung. It is

[015-126] true that it appears to be repugnant to you; and it is very

[015-127] natural, for you _bourgeois_ are not accustomed to it. You form

[015-128] for yourselves a great idea of the thing. After all, we don’t

[015-129] wish you any harm. Here is a means of extricating yourself from

[015-130] your predicament for the moment. Will you become one of us?”

[015-131] The reader can judge of the effect which this proposition

[015-132] produced upon Gringoire, who beheld life slipping away from him,

[015-133] and who was beginning to lose his hold upon it. He clutched at it

[015-134] again with energy.

[015-135] “Certainly I will, and right heartily,” said he.

[015-136] “Do you consent,” resumed Clopin, “to enroll yourself among the

[015-137] people of the knife?”

[015-138] “Of the knife, precisely,” responded Gringoire.

[015-139] “You recognize yourself as a member of the free

[015-140] _bourgeoisie?_”[12] added the King of Thunes.

[015-141] “Of the free _bourgeoisie_.”

[015-142] “Subject of the Kingdom of Argot?”

[015-143] “Of the Kingdom of Argot[13].”

[015-144] “A vagabond?”

[015-145] “A vagabond.”

[015-146] “In your soul?”

[015-147] “In my soul.”

[015-148] “I must call your attention to the fact,” continued the king,

[015-149] “that you will be hung all the same.”

[015-150] “The devil!” said the poet.

[015-151] “Only,” continued Clopin imperturbably, “you will be hung later

[015-152] on, with more ceremony, at the expense of the good city of Paris,

[015-153] on a handsome stone gibbet, and by honest men. That is a

[015-154] consolation.”

[015-155] “Just so,” responded Gringoire.

[015-156] “There are other advantages. In your quality of a high-toned

[015-157] sharper, you will not have to pay the taxes on mud, or the poor,

[015-158] or lanterns, to which the _bourgeois_ of Paris are subject.”

[015-159] “So be it,” said the poet. “I agree. I am a vagabond, a thief, a

[015-160] sharper, a man of the knife, anything you please; and I am all

[015-161] that already, monsieur, King of Thunes, for I am a philosopher;

[015-162] _et omnia in philosophia, omnes in philosopho continentur_,—all

[015-163] things are contained in philosophy, all men in the philosopher,

[015-164] as you know.”

[015-165] The King of Thunes scowled.

[015-166] “What do you take me for, my friend? What Hungarian Jew patter

[015-167] are you jabbering at us? I don’t know Hebrew. One isn’t a Jew

[015-168] because one is a bandit. I don’t even steal any longer. I’m above

[015-169] that; I kill. Cut-throat, yes; cutpurse, no.”

[015-170] Gringoire tried to slip in some excuse between these curt words,

[015-171] which wrath rendered more and more jerky.

[015-172] “I ask your pardon, monseigneur. It is not Hebrew; ’tis Latin.”

[015-173] “I tell you,” resumed Clopin angrily, “that I’m not a Jew, and

[015-174] that I’ll have you hung, belly of the synagogue, like that little

[015-175] shopkeeper of Judea, who is by your side, and whom I entertain

[015-176] strong hopes of seeing nailed to a counter one of these days,

[015-177] like the counterfeit coin that he is!”

[015-178] So saying, he pointed his finger at the little, bearded Hungarian

[015-179] Jew who had accosted Gringoire with his _facitote caritatem_, and

[015-180] who, understanding no other language beheld with surprise the

[015-181] King of Thunes’s ill-humor overflow upon him.

[015-182] At length Monsieur Clopin calmed down.

[015-183] “So you will be a vagabond, you knave?” he said to our poet.

[015-184] “Of course,” replied the poet.

[015-185] “Willing is not all,” said the surly Clopin; “good will doesn’t

[015-186] put one onion the more into the soup, and ’tis good for nothing

[015-187] except to go to Paradise with; now, Paradise and the thieves’

[015-188] band are two different things. In order to be received among the

[015-189] thieves,[14] you must prove that you are good for something, and

[015-190] for that purpose, you must search the manikin.”

[015-191] “I’ll search anything you like,” said Gringoire.

[015-192] Clopin made a sign. Several thieves detached themselves from the

[015-193] circle, and returned a moment later. They brought two thick

[015-194] posts, terminated at their lower extremities in spreading timber

[015-195] supports, which made them stand readily upon the ground; to the

[015-196] upper extremity of the two posts they fitted a cross-beam, and

[015-197] the whole constituted a very pretty portable gibbet, which

[015-198] Gringoire had the satisfaction of beholding rise before him, in a

[015-199] twinkling. Nothing was lacking, not even the rope, which swung

[015-200] gracefully over the cross-beam.

[015-201] “What are they going to do?” Gringoire asked himself with some

[015-202] uneasiness. A sound of bells, which he heard at that moment, put

[015-203] an end to his anxiety; it was a stuffed manikin, which the

[015-204] vagabonds were suspending by the neck from the rope, a sort of

[015-205] scarecrow dressed in red, and so hung with mule-bells and larger

[015-206] bells, that one might have tricked out thirty Castilian mules

[015-207] with them. These thousand tiny bells quivered for some time with

[015-208] the vibration of the rope, then gradually died away, and finally

[015-209] became silent when the manikin had been brought into a state of

[015-210] immobility by that law of the pendulum which has dethroned the

[015-211] water clock and the hour-glass. Then Clopin, pointing out to

[015-212] Gringoire a rickety old stool placed beneath the manikin,—“Climb

[015-213] up there.”
