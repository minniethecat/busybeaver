---[042-001] Fleur-de-Lys raised her beautiful eyes, full of reproach, “Is

[042-002] that all of which you can assure me?” she said, in a low voice.

[042-003] In the meantime, Dame Aloïse, delighted to see them thus bending

[042-004] towards each other and whispering, said as she toyed with the

[042-005] clasps of her prayer-book,—

[042-006] “Touching picture of love!”

[042-007] The captain, more and more embarrassed, fell back upon the

[042-008] subject of the tapestry,—“’Tis, in sooth, a charming work!” he

[042-009] exclaimed.

[042-010] Whereupon Colombe de Gaillefontaine, another beautiful blonde,

[042-011] with a white skin, dressed to the neck in blue damask, ventured a

[042-012] timid remark which she addressed to Fleur-de-Lys, in the hope

[042-013] that the handsome captain would reply to it, “My dear

[042-014] Gondelaurier, have you seen the tapestries of the Hôtel de la

[042-015] Roche-Guyon?”

[042-016] “Is not that the hôtel in which is enclosed the garden of the

[042-017] Lingère du Louvre?” asked Diane de Christeuil with a laugh; for

[042-018] she had handsome teeth, and consequently laughed on every

[042-019] occasion.

[042-020] “And where there is that big, old tower of the ancient wall of

[042-021] Paris,” added Amelotte de Montmichel, a pretty fresh and

[042-022] curly-headed brunette, who had a habit of sighing just as the

[042-023] other laughed, without knowing why.

[042-024] “My dear Colombe,” interpolated Dame Aloïse, “do you not mean the

[042-025] hôtel which belonged to Monsieur de Bacqueville, in the reign of

[042-026] King Charles VI.? there are indeed many superb high warp

[042-027] tapestries there.”

[042-028] “Charles VI.! Charles VI.!” muttered the young captain, twirling

[042-029] his moustache. “Good heavens! what old things the good dame does

[042-030] remember!”

[042-031] Madame de Gondelaurier continued, “Fine tapestries, in truth. A

[042-032] work so esteemed that it passes as unrivalled.”

[042-033] At that moment Bérangère de Champchevrier, a slender little maid

[042-034] of seven years, who was peering into the square through the

[042-035] trefoils of the balcony, exclaimed, “Oh! look, fair Godmother

[042-036] Fleur-de-Lys, at that pretty dancer who is dancing on the

[042-037] pavement and playing the tambourine in the midst of the loutish

[042-038] _bourgeois!_”

[042-039] The sonorous vibration of a tambourine was, in fact, audible.

[042-040] “Some gypsy from Bohemia,” said Fleur-de-Lys, turning carelessly

[042-041] toward the square.

[042-042] “Look! look!” exclaimed her lively companions; and they all ran

[042-043] to the edge of the balcony, while Fleur-de-Lys, rendered

[042-044] thoughtful by the coldness of her betrothed, followed them

[042-045] slowly, and the latter, relieved by this incident, which put an

[042-046] end to an embarrassing conversation, retreated to the farther end

[042-047] of the room, with the satisfied air of a soldier released from

[042-048] duty. Nevertheless, the fair Fleur-de-Lys’s was a charming and

[042-049] noble service, and such it had formerly appeared to him; but the

[042-050] captain had gradually become _blasé;_ the prospect of a speedy

[042-051] marriage cooled him more every day. Moreover, he was of a fickle

[042-052] disposition, and, must we say it, rather vulgar in taste.

[042-053] Although of very noble birth, he had contracted in his official

[042-054] harness more than one habit of the common trooper. The tavern and

[042-055] its accompaniments pleased him. He was only at his ease amid

[042-056] gross language, military gallantries, facile beauties, and

[042-057] successes yet more easy. He had, nevertheless, received from his

[042-058] family some education and some politeness of manner; but he had

[042-059] been thrown on the world too young, he had been in garrison at

[042-060] too early an age, and every day the polish of a gentleman became

[042-061] more and more effaced by the rough friction of his gendarme’s

[042-062] cross-belt. While still continuing to visit her from time to

[042-063] time, from a remnant of common respect, he felt doubly

[042-064] embarrassed with Fleur-de-Lys; in the first place, because, in

[042-065] consequence of having scattered his love in all sorts of places,

[042-066] he had reserved very little for her; in the next place, because,

[042-067] amid so many stiff, formal, and decent ladies, he was in constant

[042-068] fear lest his mouth, habituated to oaths, should suddenly take

[042-069] the bit in its teeth, and break out into the language of the

[042-070] tavern. The effect can be imagined!

[042-071] Moreover, all this was mingled in him, with great pretentions to

[042-072] elegance, toilet, and a fine appearance. Let the reader reconcile

[042-073] these things as best he can. I am simply the historian.

[042-074] He had remained, therefore, for several minutes, leaning in

[042-075] silence against the carved jamb of the chimney, and thinking or

[042-076] not thinking, when Fleur-de-Lys suddenly turned and addressed

[042-077] him. After all, the poor young girl was pouting against the

[042-078] dictates of her heart.

[042-079] “Fair cousin, did you not speak to us of a little Bohemian whom

[042-080] you saved a couple of months ago, while making the patrol with

[042-081] the watch at night, from the hands of a dozen robbers?”

[042-082] “I believe so, fair cousin,” said the captain.

[042-083] “Well,” she resumed, “perchance ’tis that same gypsy girl who is

[042-084] dancing yonder, on the church square. Come and see if you

[042-085] recognize her, fair Cousin Phœbus.”

[042-086] A secret desire for reconciliation was apparent in this gentle

[042-087] invitation which she gave him to approach her, and in the care

[042-088] which she took to call him by name. Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers

[042-089] (for it is he whom the reader has had before his eyes since the

[042-090] beginning of this chapter) slowly approached the balcony. “Stay,”

[042-091] said Fleur-de-Lys, laying her hand tenderly on Phœbus’s arm;

[042-092] “look at that little girl yonder, dancing in that circle. Is she

[042-093] your Bohemian?”

[042-094] Phœbus looked, and said,—

[042-095] “Yes, I recognize her by her goat.”

[042-096] “Oh! in fact, what a pretty little goat!” said Amelotte, clasping

[042-097] her hands in admiration.

[042-098] “Are his horns of real gold?” inquired Bérangère.

[042-099] Without moving from her arm-chair, Dame Aloïse interposed, “Is

[042-100] she not one of those gypsy girls who arrived last year by the

[042-101] Gibard gate?”

[042-102] “Madame my mother,” said Fleur-de-Lys gently, “that gate is now

[042-103] called the Porte d’Enfer.”

[042-104] Mademoiselle de Gondelaurier knew how her mother’s antiquated

[042-105] mode of speech shocked the captain. In fact, he began to sneer,

[042-106] and muttered between his teeth: “Porte Gibard! Porte Gibard! ’Tis

[042-107] enough to make King Charles VI. pass by.”

[042-108] “Godmother!” exclaimed Bérangère, whose eyes, incessantly in

[042-109] motion, had suddenly been raised to the summit of the towers of

[042-110] Notre-Dame, “who is that black man up yonder?”

[042-111] All the young girls raised their eyes. A man was, in truth,

[042-112] leaning on the balustrade which surmounted the northern tower,

[042-113] looking on the Grève. He was a priest. His costume could be

[042-114] plainly discerned, and his face resting on both his hands. But he

[042-115] stirred no more than if he had been a statue. His eyes, intently

[042-116] fixed, gazed into the Place.

[042-117] It was something like the immobility of a bird of prey, who has

[042-118] just discovered a nest of sparrows, and is gazing at it.

[042-119] “’Tis monsieur the archdeacon of Josas,” said Fleur-de-Lys.

[042-120] “You have good eyes if you can recognize him from here,” said the

[042-121] Gaillefontaine.

[042-122] “How he is staring at the little dancer!” went on Diane de

[042-123] Christeuil.

[042-124] “Let the gypsy beware!” said Fleur-de-Lys, “for he loves not

[042-125] Egypt.”

[042-126] “’Tis a great shame for that man to look upon her thus,” added

[042-127] Amelotte de Montmichel, “for she dances delightfully.”

[042-128] “Fair cousin Phœbus,” said Fleur-de-Lys suddenly, “Since you know

[042-129] this little gypsy, make her a sign to come up here. It will amuse

[042-130] us.”

[042-131] “Oh, yes!” exclaimed all the young girls, clapping their hands.

[042-132] “Why! ’tis not worth while,” replied Phœbus. “She has forgotten

[042-133] me, no doubt, and I know not so much as her name. Nevertheless,

[042-134] as you wish it, young ladies, I will make the trial.” And leaning

[042-135] over the balustrade of the balcony, he began to shout, “Little

[042-136] one!”

[042-137] The dancer was not beating her tambourine at the moment. She

[042-138] turned her head towards the point whence this call proceeded, her

[042-139] brilliant eyes rested on Phœbus, and she stopped short.

[042-140] “Little one!” repeated the captain; and he beckoned her to

[042-141] approach.

[042-142] The young girl looked at him again, then she blushed as though a

[042-143] flame had mounted into her cheeks, and, taking her tambourine

[042-144] under her arm, she made her way through the astonished spectators

[042-145] towards the door of the house where Phœbus was calling her, with

[042-146] slow, tottering steps, and with the troubled look of a bird which

[042-147] is yielding to the fascination of a serpent.

[042-148] A moment later, the tapestry portière was raised, and the gypsy

[042-149] appeared on the threshold of the chamber, blushing, confused,

[042-150] breathless, her large eyes drooping, and not daring to advance

[042-151] another step.

[042-152] Bérangère clapped her hands.

[042-153] Meanwhile, the dancer remained motionless upon the threshold. Her

[042-154] appearance had produced a singular effect upon these young girls.

[042-155] It is certain that a vague and indistinct desire to please the

[042-156] handsome officer animated them all, that his splendid uniform was

[042-157] the target of all their coquetries, and that from the moment he

[042-158] presented himself, there existed among them a secret, suppressed

[042-159] rivalry, which they hardly acknowledged even to themselves, but

[042-160] which broke forth, none the less, every instant, in their

[042-161] gestures and remarks. Nevertheless, as they were all very nearly

[042-162] equal in beauty, they contended with equal arms, and each could

[042-163] hope for the victory. The arrival of the gypsy suddenly destroyed

[042-164] this equilibrium. Her beauty was so rare, that, at the moment

[042-165] when she appeared at the entrance of the apartment, it seemed as

[042-166] though she diffused a sort of light which was peculiar to

[042-167] herself. In that narrow chamber, surrounded by that sombre frame

[042-168] of hangings and woodwork, she was incomparably more beautiful and

[042-169] more radiant than on the public square. She was like a torch

[042-170] which has suddenly been brought from broad daylight into the

[042-171] dark. The noble damsels were dazzled by her in spite of

[042-172] themselves. Each one felt herself, in some sort, wounded in her

[042-173] beauty. Hence, their battle front (may we be allowed the

[042-174] expression,) was immediately altered, although they exchanged not

[042-175] a single word. But they understood each other perfectly. Women’s

[042-176] instincts comprehend and respond to each other more quickly than

[042-177] the intelligences of men. An enemy had just arrived; all felt

[042-178] it—all rallied together. One drop of wine is sufficient to tinge

[042-179] a glass of water red; to diffuse a certain degree of ill temper

[042-180] throughout a whole assembly of pretty women, the arrival of a

[042-181] prettier woman suffices, especially when there is but one man

[042-182] present.

[042-183] Hence the welcome accorded to the gypsy was marvellously glacial.

[042-184] They surveyed her from head to foot, then exchanged glances, and

[042-185] all was said; they understood each other. Meanwhile, the young

[042-186] girl was waiting to be spoken to, in such emotion that she dared

[042-187] not raise her eyelids.

[042-188] The captain was the first to break the silence. “Upon my word,”

[042-189] said he, in his tone of intrepid fatuity, “here is a charming

[042-190] creature! What think you of her, fair cousin?”

[042-191] This remark, which a more delicate admirer would have uttered in

[042-192] a lower tone, at least was not of a nature to dissipate the

[042-193] feminine jealousies which were on the alert before the gypsy.

[042-194] Fleur-de-Lys replied to the captain with a bland affectation of

[042-195] disdain;—“Not bad.”

[042-196] The others whispered.

[042-197] At length, Madame Aloïse, who was not the less jealous because

[042-198] she was so for her daughter, addressed the dancer,—“Approach,

[042-199] little one.”

[042-200] “Approach, little one!” repeated, with comical dignity, little

[042-201] Bérangère, who would have reached about as high as her hips.

[042-202] The gypsy advanced towards the noble dame.

[042-203] “Fair child,” said Phœbus, with emphasis, taking several steps

[042-204] towards her, “I do not know whether I have the supreme honor of

[042-205] being recognized by you.”

[042-206] She interrupted him, with a smile and a look full of infinite

[042-207] sweetness,—

[042-208] “Oh! yes,” said she.

[042-209] “She has a good memory,” remarked Fleur-de-Lys.

[042-210] “Come, now,” resumed Phœbus, “you escaped nimbly the other

[042-211] evening. Did I frighten you!”

[042-212] “Oh! no,” said the gypsy.

[042-213] There was in the intonation of that “Oh! no,” uttered after that

[042-214] “Oh! yes,” an ineffable something which wounded Fleur-de-Lys.

[042-215] “You left me in your stead, my beauty,” pursued the captain,

[042-216] whose tongue was unloosed when speaking to a girl out of the

[042-217] street, “a crabbed knave, one-eyed and hunchbacked, the bishop’s

[042-218] bellringer, I believe. I have been told that by birth he is the

[042-219] bastard of an archdeacon and a devil. He has a pleasant name: he

[042-220] is called _Quatre-Temps_ (Ember Days), _Pâques-Fleuries_ (Palm

[042-221] Sunday), _Mardi-Gras_ (Shrove Tuesday), I know not what! The name

[042-222] of some festival when the bells are pealed! So he took the

[042-223] liberty of carrying you off, as though you were made for beadles!

[042-224] ’Tis too much. What the devil did that screech-owl want with you?

[042-225] Hey, tell me!”
