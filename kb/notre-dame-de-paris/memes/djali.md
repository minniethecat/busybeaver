# Djali

Djali is the trained goat belonging to Esmeralda, whose remarkable intelligence and performances captivate crowds in the Paris streets while also attracting suspicion and accusations of sorcery from the authorities.

## Role in the Novel

Djali functions as Esmeralda's constant companion and a source of both wonder and danger. The goat's trained tricks — spelling words with lettered blocks, telling the time, impersonating public figures — delight the populace but provide ammunition for those who accuse Esmeralda of witchcraft. Djali is first introduced during Esmeralda's performance in the Place de Grève, where the goat's cleverness draws the attention of the crowd [011-037] [011-043].

The goat's abilities become a legal liability during Esmeralda's trial, when Djali's tricks are presented as evidence of sorcery. The animal companion thus serves Hugo's broader theme of how ignorance and superstition transform the innocent into the criminal.

## Biography

### Origins and Background

Djali is a small, white goat with gilded horns and hooves, trained by Esmeralda to perform an array of tricks. The goat is always by Esmeralda's side, serving as both companion and livelihood in the street performances that sustain the young Romani dancer [011-046].

### Key Appearances

Djali appears prominently during the Festival of Fools, performing tricks at Esmeralda's command. The goat spells out "Phoebus" with lettered blocks, an early sign of Esmeralda's infatuation that Djali has been trained to facilitate. During the trial, the court interrogates the goat's abilities as evidence of demonic influence, turning a street performer's prop into an instrument of condemnation.

### Fate

Djali's fate parallels Esmeralda's — the goat is condemned alongside its mistress when the charges of witchcraft are brought. The animal's death is ordered by the same judicial system that sentences Esmeralda to the gallows.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Djali is described as intelligent, obedient, and remarkably skilled. The goat can count, tell time, spell words, and imitate human behavior with uncanny precision. Hugo treats this intelligence with a kind of wonder, but the novel's medieval characters interpret it through the lens of superstition — what seems like training to a modern reader reads as demonic possession to the fifteenth-century Parisian crowd [011-037] [011-043].

### Relationships

- **[[esmeralda]]**: Djali's owner and constant companion; the goat is an extension of Esmeralda's performance persona
- **[[claude-frollo]]**: The archdeacon views Djali's tricks as evidence of Esmeralda's supposed sorcery

### Symbolism

Djali represents the intersection of innocence and accusation in the novel. The goat's clever tricks are morally neutral, but they become weaponized by a society that sees the supernatural in everything it does not understand. Djali thus serves as a mirror to Esmeralda herself — beautiful, captivating, and fatally misunderstood.

## Notable Passages

> "Djali!" said the gypsy. "Djali!" said the dancer, "it is your turn" ([011-037] [011-043])

> "Djali," she continued, "what month is it in the year?" ([011-046])

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo uses Djali to satirize the credulity of the medieval justice system. The spectacle of a court seriously interrogating a goat's spelling abilities exposes the absurdity of witchcraft prosecutions. The narrator's tone when describing the legal proceedings against Djali is unmistakably ironic.
