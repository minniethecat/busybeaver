---[088-001] Thus he saw what the priest was looking at. The ladder was

[088-002] erected near the permanent gallows. There were some people and

[088-003] many soldiers in the Place. A man was dragging a white thing,

[088-004] from which hung something black, along the pavement. This man

[088-005] halted at the foot of the gallows.

[088-006] Here something took place which Quasimodo could not see very

[088-007] clearly. It was not because his only eye had not preserved its

[088-008] long range, but there was a group of soldiers which prevented his

[088-009] seeing everything. Moreover, at that moment the sun appeared, and

[088-010] such a flood of light overflowed the horizon that one would have

[088-011] said that all the points in Paris, spires, chimneys, gables, had

[088-012] simultaneously taken fire.

[088-013] Meanwhile, the man began to mount the ladder. Then Quasimodo saw

[088-014] him again distinctly. He was carrying a woman on his shoulder, a

[088-015] young girl dressed in white; that young girl had a noose about

[088-016] her neck. Quasimodo recognized her.

[088-017] It was she.

[088-018] The man reached the top of the ladder. There he arranged the

[088-019] noose. Here the priest, in order to see the better, knelt upon

[088-020] the balustrade.

[088-021] All at once the man kicked away the ladder abruptly, and

[088-022] Quasimodo, who had not breathed for several moments, beheld the

[088-023] unhappy child dangling at the end of the rope two fathoms above

[088-024] the pavement, with the man squatting on her shoulders. The rope

[088-025] made several gyrations on itself, and Quasimodo beheld horrible

[088-026] convulsions run along the gypsy’s body. The priest, on his side,

[088-027] with outstretched neck and eyes starting from his head,

[088-028] contemplated this horrible group of the man and the young

[088-029] girl,—the spider and the fly.

[088-030] At the moment when it was most horrible, the laugh of a demon, a

[088-031] laugh which one can only give vent to when one is no longer

[088-032] human, burst forth on the priest’s livid face.

[088-033] Quasimodo did not hear that laugh, but he saw it.

[088-034] The bellringer retreated several paces behind the archdeacon, and

[088-035] suddenly hurling himself upon him with fury, with his huge hands

[088-036] he pushed him by the back over into the abyss over which Dom

[088-037] Claude was leaning.

[088-038] The priest shrieked: “Damnation!” and fell.

[088-039] The spout, above which he had stood, arrested him in his fall. He

[088-040] clung to it with desperate hands, and, at the moment when he

[088-041] opened his mouth to utter a second cry, he beheld the formidable

[088-042] and avenging face of Quasimodo thrust over the edge of the

[088-043] balustrade above his head.

[088-044] Then he was silent.

[088-045] The abyss was there below him. A fall of more than two hundred

[088-046] feet and the pavement.

[088-047] In this terrible situation, the archdeacon said not a word,

[088-048] uttered not a groan. He merely writhed upon the spout, with

[088-049] incredible efforts to climb up again; but his hands had no hold

[088-050] on the granite, his feet slid along the blackened wall without

[088-051] catching fast. People who have ascended the towers of Notre-Dame

[088-052] know that there is a swell of the stone immediately beneath the

[088-053] balustrade. It was on this retreating angle that miserable

[088-054] archdeacon exhausted himself. He had not to deal with a

[088-055] perpendicular wall, but with one which sloped away beneath him.

[088-056] Quasimodo had but to stretch out his hand in order to draw him

[088-057] from the gulf; but he did not even look at him. He was looking at

[088-058] the Grève. He was looking at the gallows. He was looking at the

[088-059] gypsy.

[088-060] The deaf man was leaning, with his elbows on the balustrade, at

[088-061] the spot where the archdeacon had been a moment before, and

[088-062] there, never detaching his gaze from the only object which

[088-063] existed for him in the world at that moment, he remained

[088-064] motionless and mute, like a man struck by lightning, and a long

[088-065] stream of tears flowed in silence from that eye which, up to that

[088-066] time, had never shed but one tear.

[088-067] Meanwhile, the archdeacon was panting. His bald brow was dripping

[088-068] with perspiration, his nails were bleeding against the stones,

[088-069] his knees were flayed by the wall.

[088-070] He heard his cassock, which was caught on the spout, crack and

[088-071] rip at every jerk that he gave it. To complete his misfortune,

[088-072] this spout ended in a leaden pipe which bent under the weight of

[088-073] his body. The archdeacon felt this pipe slowly giving way. The

[088-074] miserable man said to himself that, when his hands should be worn

[088-075] out with fatigue, when his cassock should tear asunder, when the

[088-076] lead should give way, he would be obliged to fall, and terror

[088-077] seized upon his very vitals. Now and then he glanced wildly at a

[088-078] sort of narrow shelf formed, ten feet lower down, by projections

[088-079] of the sculpture, and he prayed heaven, from the depths of his

[088-080] distressed soul, that he might be allowed to finish his life,

[088-081] were it to last two centuries, on that space two feet square.

[088-082] Once, he glanced below him into the Place, into the abyss; the

[088-083] head which he raised again had its eyes closed and its hair

[088-084] standing erect.

[088-085] There was something frightful in the silence of these two men.

[088-086] While the archdeacon agonized in this terrible fashion a few feet

[088-087] below him, Quasimodo wept and gazed at the Grève.

[088-088] The archdeacon, seeing that all his exertions served only to

[088-089] weaken the fragile support which remained to him, decided to

[088-090] remain quiet. There he hung, embracing the gutter, hardly

[088-091] breathing, no longer stirring, making no longer any other

[088-092] movements than that mechanical convulsion of the stomach, which

[088-093] one experiences in dreams when one fancies himself falling. His

[088-094] fixed eyes were wide open with a stare. He lost ground little by

[088-095] little, nevertheless, his fingers slipped along the spout; he

[088-096] became more and more conscious of the feebleness of his arms and

[088-097] the weight of his body. The curve of the lead which sustained him

[088-098] inclined more and more each instant towards the abyss.

[088-099] He beheld below him, a frightful thing, the roof of Saint-Jean le

[088-100] Rond, as small as a card folded in two. He gazed at the

[088-101] impressive carvings, one by one, of the tower, suspended like

[088-102] himself over the precipice, but without terror for themselves or

[088-103] pity for him. All was stone around him; before his eyes, gaping

[088-104] monsters; below, quite at the bottom, in the Place, the pavement;

[088-105] above his head, Quasimodo weeping.

[088-106] In the Parvis there were several groups of curious good people,

[088-107] who were tranquilly seeking to divine who the madman could be who

[088-108] was amusing himself in so strange a manner. The priest heard them

[088-109] saying, for their voices reached him, clear and shrill: “Why, he

[088-110] will break his neck!”

[088-111] Quasimodo wept.

[088-112] At last the archdeacon, foaming with rage and despair, understood

[088-113] that all was in vain. Nevertheless, he collected all the strength

[088-114] which remained to him for a final effort. He stiffened himself

[088-115] upon the spout, pushed against the wall with both his knees,

[088-116] clung to a crevice in the stones with his hands, and succeeded in

[088-117] climbing back with one foot, perhaps; but this effort made the

[088-118] leaden beak on which he rested bend abruptly. His cassock burst

[088-119] open at the same time. Then, feeling everything give way beneath

[088-120] him, with nothing but his stiffened and failing hands to support

[088-121] him, the unfortunate man closed his eyes and let go of the spout.

[088-122] He fell.

[088-123] Quasimodo watched him fall.

[088-124] A fall from such a height is seldom perpendicular. The

[088-125] archdeacon, launched into space, fell at first head foremost,

[088-126] with outspread hands; then he whirled over and over many times;

[088-127] the wind blew him upon the roof of a house, where the unfortunate

[088-128] man began to break up. Nevertheless, he was not dead when he

[088-129] reached there. The bellringer saw him still endeavor to cling to

[088-130] a gable with his nails; but the surface sloped too much, and he

[088-131] had no more strength. He slid rapidly along the roof like a

[088-132] loosened tile, and dashed upon the pavement. There he no longer

[088-133] moved.

[088-134] Then Quasimodo raised his eyes to the gypsy, whose body he beheld

[088-135] hanging from the gibbet, quivering far away beneath her white

[088-136] robe with the last shudderings of anguish, then he dropped them

[088-137] on the archdeacon, stretched out at the base of the tower, and no

[088-138] longer retaining the human form, and he said, with a sob which

[088-139] heaved his deep chest,—“Oh! all that I have ever loved!”

[088-140] CHAPTER III. THE MARRIAGE OF PHOEBUS.

[088-141] Towards evening on that day, when the judiciary officers of the

[088-142] bishop came to pick up from the pavement of the Parvis the

[088-143] dislocated corpse of the archdeacon, Quasimodo had disappeared.

[088-144] A great many rumors were in circulation with regard to this

[088-145] adventure. No one doubted but that the day had come when, in

[088-146] accordance with their compact, Quasimodo, that is to say, the

[088-147] devil, was to carry off Claude Frollo, that is to say, the

[088-148] sorcerer. It was presumed that he had broken the body when taking

[088-149] the soul, like monkeys who break the shell to get at the nut.

[088-150] This is why the archdeacon was not interred in consecrated earth.

[088-151] Louis XI. died a year later, in the month of August, 1483.

[088-152] As for Pierre Gringoire, he succeeded in saving the goat, and he

[088-153] won success in tragedy. It appears that, after having tasted

[088-154] astrology, philosophy, architecture, hermetics,—all vanities, he

[088-155] returned to tragedy, vainest pursuit of all. This is what he

[088-156] called “coming to a tragic end.” This is what is to be read, on

[088-157] the subject of his dramatic triumphs, in 1483, in the accounts of

[088-158] the “Ordinary:” “To Jehan Marchand and Pierre Gringoire,

[088-159] carpenter and composer, who have made and composed the mystery

[088-160] made at the Châtelet of Paris, at the entry of Monsieur the

[088-161] Legate, and have ordered the personages, clothed and dressed the

[088-162] same, as in the said mystery was required; and likewise, for

[088-163] having made the scaffoldings thereto necessary; and for this

[088-164] deed,—one hundred livres.”

[088-166] CHAPTER IV. THE MARRIAGE OF QUASIMODO.

[088-167] We have just said that Quasimodo disappeared from Notre-Dame on

[088-168] the day of the gypsy’s and of the archdeacon’s death. He was not

[088-169] seen again, in fact; no one knew what had become of him.

[088-170] During the night which followed the execution of la Esmeralda,

[088-171] the night men had detached her body from the gibbet, and had

[088-172] carried it, according to custom, to the cellar of Montfaucon.

[088-173] Montfaucon was, as Sauval says, “the most ancient and the most

[088-174] superb gibbet in the kingdom.” Between the faubourgs of the

[088-175] Temple and Saint Martin, about a hundred and sixty toises from

[088-176] the walls of Paris, a few bow shots from La Courtille, there was

[088-177] to be seen on the crest of a gentle, almost imperceptible

[088-178] eminence, but sufficiently elevated to be seen for several

[088-179] leagues round about, an edifice of strange form, bearing

[088-180] considerable resemblance to a Celtic cromlech, and where also

[088-181] human sacrifices were offered.

[088-182] Let the reader picture to himself, crowning a limestone hillock,

[088-183] an oblong mass of masonry fifteen feet in height, thirty wide,

[088-184] forty long, with a gate, an external railing and a platform; on

[088-185] this platform sixteen enormous pillars of rough hewn stone,

[088-186] thirty feet in height, arranged in a colonnade round three of the

[088-187] four sides of the mass which support them, bound together at

[088-188] their summits by heavy beams, whence hung chains at intervals; on

[088-189] all these chains, skeletons; in the vicinity, on the plain, a

[088-190] stone cross and two gibbets of secondary importance, which seemed

[088-191] to have sprung up as shoots around the central gallows; above all

[088-192] this, in the sky, a perpetual flock of crows; that was

[088-193] Montfaucon.

[088-194] At the end of the fifteenth century, the formidable gibbet which

[088-195] dated from 1328, was already very much dilapidated; the beams

[088-196] were wormeaten, the chains rusted, the pillars green with mould;

[088-197] the layers of hewn stone were all cracked at their joints, and

[088-198] grass was growing on that platform which no feet touched. The

[088-199] monument made a horrible profile against the sky; especially at

[088-200] night when there was a little moonlight on those white skulls, or

[088-201] when the breeze of evening brushed the chains and the skeletons,

[088-202] and swayed all these in the darkness. The presence of this gibbet

[088-203] sufficed to render gloomy all the surrounding places.
