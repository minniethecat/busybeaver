# The Tour-Roland and the Rat-Hole

The Tour-Roland (Roland Tower) contains the Rat-Hole (Trou aux Rats), the
cell where [[sister-gudule|Paquette la Chantefleurie]] — known to [[paris|Paris]]ians as [[sister-gudule|Sister Gudule]]
— has walled herself up for over a decade in an act of perpetual penance
for the loss of her daughter. The tower and its cell are among Hugo's most
powerful architectural symbols: a place of utter desolation and
simultaneously of maternal [[love-and-desire|love]] so fierce that it has consumed the
woman who bears it.

## Location and Architecture

The Tour-Roland stands in the Rue Saint-Antoine area of [[paris|Paris]], one of many
ancient towers that dotted the medieval city. Hugo describes the Rat-Hole
as a small cell built into the base of the tower, with a single air-hole
facing the street — the recluse's only connection to the world. The cell
is modeled on the anchoritic tradition of the Middle Ages: a person,
usually a woman, would be ceremonially walled into a cell attached to a
[[religion|church]] or tower, where they would spend the remainder of their life in
prayer and penance [036-141].

## In the Novel

### The Anchoritic Tradition

Hugo provides a detailed account of the Rat-Hole's history: it was
established in honor of Madame Rolande, a woman of the nobility who,
grieving for her father killed in the Crusades, had herself walled up
in the cell. After her [[death]], the cell had never lacked recluses — a
succession of women who, for reasons of grief, penance, or madness,
chose to bury themselves alive in the tower's base [035-105] [035-161].

### The Recluse

[[sister-gudule]] — born [[sister-gudule|Paquette la Chantefleurie]] — is the current
occupant when the novel's action takes place. Her story, narrated by
[[mahiette]] to [[gervaise]] and [[oudarde-musnier]] as the three women
stand before the Tour-Roland, is one of the novel's most emotionally
devastating passages [036-153]. The recluse's infant daughter was stolen
by gypsies when the child was barely a year old. Paquette, after years of
searching, had herself walled into the Rat-Hole to mourn forever [038-196].

### The Recognition

The novel's most wrenching recognition scene unfolds at the Rat-Hole's
air-hole. When [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]] is brought past the Tour-Roland on her way to
[[death|execution]], [[sister-gudule|Sister Gudule]] — who has spent fifteen years cursing the
gypsies who stole her daughter — recognizes the amulet around
[[esmeralda|Esmeralda]]'s neck: the tiny embroidered shoe that matches the one she has
kept for fifteen years [058-176]. The recognition is mutual, devastating,
and comes too late to save either woman. The Rat-Hole, which has been a
tomb for the mother, becomes the site of the daughter's last embrace
before both are destroyed.

### Destruction

When [[sister-gudule|Sister Gudule]] attempts to save [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]] from the king's archers,
the soldiers drag her from the Rat-Hole. In the struggle, she strikes her
head against the pavement and dies. The cell, emptied of its occupant,
remains as a mute witness — one more piece of medieval stonework that
Hugo catalogs with the precision of an archaeologist of human suffering.

## Symbolism

### The Tomb of Maternal Love

The Tour-Roland is Hugo's most concentrated image of [[love-and-desire|love]] turned inward
until it becomes self-destruction. Paquette's [[love-and-desire|love]] for her lost daughter
is so absolute that it can express itself only as perpetual mourning —
the living [[death]] of the anchorite. The cell is both a tomb and a womb:
the mother who lost her child returns to the stone enclosure as if to
gestate her grief forever.

### The Architecture of Penance

Hugo presents the Tour-Roland as an example of the medieval genius for
turning [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] into penance. The cell is built — deliberately,
ceremonially — as a place where the occupant will die by inches, and
the society that authorizes this practice regards it as a form of
sanctity. The novel's critique of medieval [[religion]] is implicit in the
contrast between the institutional piety that consecrates the Rat-Hole
and the raw human suffering that it contains.

## Historical Context

Anchoritism was a recognized religious vocation in the Middle Ages.
Recluses were ceremonially walled into their cells by a bishop, often in
a rite that included the recitation of the Office of the Dead — the
anchorite was, symbolically, already deceased. Hugo's portrait of the
Tour-Roland and its succession of recluses draws on this historical
practice while heightening its emotional extremity.
