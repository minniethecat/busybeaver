# The Printing Press

The printing press, in Victor Hugo's grand historical vision, is the most consequential invention in human history — the technology that "kills" [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] as the primary medium of thought and inaugurates the modern age. The novel's famous chapter "This Will Kill That" (Book V, Chapter 2) presents the printing press not merely as a technical innovation but as an epochal shift in the relationship between [[knowledge]], power, and society ((032-002)).

## Definition

Hugo defines the printing press as the "greatest event in history... the mother of revolution" ((032-050)). Before Gutenberg, the "book of stone" — [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]], particularly the Gothic cathedral — was humanity's collective memory, encoding religious doctrine, social hierarchy, and cultural values in physical form. The printing press democratizes this function: "Human thought discovers a means of perpetuating itself, not only more durable and more resisting than [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]], but still more simple and easy" ((032-080)). Where [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] required collective labor and centuries to complete, a printed book requires only a press and a day. The consequences are political as well as intellectual — the Church loses its monopoly on [[knowledge]], and with it, its absolute authority ((032-100)).

## Hugo's Treatment

### In the Narrative

The printing press appears concretely in [[claude-frollo|Claude Frollo]]'s cell, where the archdeacon gestures first toward [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]] visible through his window and then toward a printed book on his table, murmuring: "This will kill that" ((032-005)). The book is a "mysterious volume" — we never learn its exact title, but its presence is enough ((032-010)). The printing press haunts the novel as the specter of a future that the characters cannot yet comprehend but that the narrator, writing in 1831, sees clearly in retrospect.

### In the Author's Voice

Hugo's treatment of the printing press is a sustained meditation on media theory avant la lettre. He argues that "in the form of printing, thought is more imperishable than ever; it is volatile, irresistible, indestructible" ((032-090)). Architecture's limitations — its immobility, its expense, its vulnerability to fire and fashion — are overcome by print, which "mingles with the air" and circulates everywhere ((032-095)). The printing press "kills" [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]] not by destroying it but by rendering its function obsolete, just as [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]] once rendered the pagan temple obsolete ((032-130)). Hugo explicitly connects the printing press to the [[renaissance|Reformation]]: it was the press that made Luther possible, even if Luther did not fully realize it ((032-140)).

## External References

Johannes Gutenberg introduced movable type to Europe around 1440; by 1482 (the novel's setting), printing presses were established in major European cities. The first press in [[paris|Paris]] was established at [[university-of-paris|the Sorbonne]] in 1470. Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517) would vindicate Hugo's argument that the press was "the mother of revolution."
