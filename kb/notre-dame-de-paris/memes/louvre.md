# The Louvre

The **Louvre** was the royal palace and fortress on the Right Bank of the Seine in [[paris|medieval Paris]]. In Hugo's *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, it is one of the three dominant structures that define [[paris|the city]]'s skyline, visible from the towers of Notre-Dame alongside the Hôtel de Ville (002-031).

## Location and Structure

The Louvre occupied the western edge of [[paris|medieval Paris]] on the Right Bank. From the vantage point of Notre-Dame's towers, the panoramic view of [[paris|the city]] included "the Town, the Louvre and the Hôtel de Ville" as its three principal landmarks (020-127). The structure was described as an elder palace, "already so old in the time of Philip the Fair" (002-031), with projecting stories and a massive tower at its river end (021-035, 024-058).

The building stood at the base of the university hill, with the Pré-aux-Clercs (scholars' meadow) stretching close about its foundations (023-010). Its great tower, which dominated [[seine-river|the river]]bank, was one of the twenty-one principal towers crowning the wall of [[paris|Paris]] on the Right Bank side (024-058).

## Historical Significance

By 1482, the Louvre was already centuries old. Hugo notes that the palace dated to an earlier era, contrasting it with the newer constructions of [[paris|the city]] (002-031). The structure originally served as a fortress before evolving into a royal residence. Its age and solidity made it a symbol of permanence in the evolving medieval cityscape.

The demolition of the old Louvre began in later centuries, with Hugo remarking that "the demolition of the old Louvre was begun" as part of the transformations that erased much of [[paris|medieval Paris]] (023-116). This reflects one of the novel's central themes: the destruction of the old [[paris|Paris]] to make way for the new.

## Role in the Novel

The Louvre appears primarily in the panoramic descriptions of [[paris|Paris]], particularly in the famous chapter "A Bird's-Eye View of [[paris|Paris]]" where Hugo describes [[paris|the city]] as seen from Notre-Dame's towers. It does not serve as a setting for direct narrative action but functions as a defining element of [[paris|the city]]'s geography and political [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]].

The Louvre's royal associations contrast with the ecclesiastical domain of Notre-Dame and the civic sphere of the Hôtel de Ville, together forming the three powers — [[religion|church]], crown, and city — that structured [[paris|medieval Paris]]ian life.

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo uses the Louvre as part of his architectural argument about the layers of history embedded in [[paris|Paris]]ian buildings. The palace represents royal authority but also the transience of power — the old Louvre, like Notre-Dame itself, was being eroded by time and by the "renovations" that Hugo saw as destructive to [[paris|the city]]'s medieval heritage (023-116).
