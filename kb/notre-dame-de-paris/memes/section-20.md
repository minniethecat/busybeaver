---[020-001] CHAPTER II. A BIRD’S-EYE VIEW OF PARIS.

[020-002] We have just attempted to restore, for the reader’s benefit, that

[020-003] admirable church of Notre-Dame de Paris. We have briefly pointed

[020-004] out the greater part of the beauties which it possessed in the

[020-005] fifteenth century, and which it lacks to-day; but we have omitted

[020-006] the principal thing,—the view of Paris which was then to be

[020-007] obtained from the summits of its towers.

[020-008] That was, in fact,—when, after having long groped one’s way up

[020-009] the dark spiral which perpendicularly pierces the thick wall of

[020-010] the belfries, one emerged, at last abruptly, upon one of the

[020-011] lofty platforms inundated with light and air,—that was, in fact,

[020-012] a fine picture which spread out, on all sides at once, before the

[020-013] eye; a spectacle _sui generis_, of which those of our readers who

[020-014] have had the good fortune to see a Gothic city entire, complete,

[020-015] homogeneous,—a few of which still remain, Nuremberg in Bavaria

[020-016] and Vittoria in Spain,—can readily form an idea; or even smaller

[020-017] specimens, provided that they are well preserved,—Vitré in

[020-018] Brittany, Nordhausen in Prussia.

[020-019] The Paris of three hundred and fifty years ago—the Paris of the

[020-020] fifteenth century—was already a gigantic city. We Parisians

[020-021] generally make a mistake as to the ground which we think that we

[020-022] have gained, since Paris has not increased much over one-third

[020-023] since the time of Louis XI. It has certainly lost more in beauty

[020-024] than it has gained in size.

[020-025] Paris had its birth, as the reader knows, in that old island of

[020-026] the City which has the form of a cradle. The strand of that

[020-027] island was its first boundary wall, the Seine its first moat.

[020-028] Paris remained for many centuries in its island state, with two

[020-029] bridges, one on the north, the other on the south; and two bridge

[020-030] heads, which were at the same time its gates and its

[020-031] fortresses,—the Grand-Châtelet on the right bank, the

[020-032] Petit-Châtelet on the left. Then, from the date of the kings of

[020-033] the first race, Paris, being too cribbed and confined in its

[020-034] island, and unable to return thither, crossed the water. Then,

[020-035] beyond the Grand, beyond the Petit-Châtelet, a first circle of

[020-036] walls and towers began to infringe upon the country on the two

[020-037] sides of the Seine. Some vestiges of this ancient enclosure still

[020-038] remained in the last century; to-day, only the memory of it is

[020-039] left, and here and there a tradition, the Baudets or Baudoyer

[020-040] gate, _Porta Bagauda_.

[020-041] Little by little, the tide of houses, always thrust from the

[020-042] heart of the city outwards, overflows, devours, wears away, and

[020-043] effaces this wall. Philip Augustus makes a new dike for it. He

[020-044] imprisons Paris in a circular chain of great towers, both lofty

[020-045] and solid. For the period of more than a century, the houses

[020-046] press upon each other, accumulate, and raise their level in this

[020-047] basin, like water in a reservoir. They begin to deepen; they pile

[020-048] story upon story; they mount upon each other; they gush forth at

[020-049] the top, like all laterally compressed growth, and there is a

[020-050] rivalry as to which shall thrust its head above its neighbors,

[020-051] for the sake of getting a little air. The street glows narrower

[020-052] and deeper, every space is overwhelmed and disappears. The houses

[020-053] finally leap the wall of Philip Augustus, and scatter joyfully

[020-054] over the plain, without order, and all askew, like runaways.

[020-055] There they plant themselves squarely, cut themselves gardens from

[020-056] the fields, and take their ease. Beginning with 1367, the city

[020-057] spreads to such an extent into the suburbs, that a new wall

[020-058] becomes necessary, particularly on the right bank; Charles V.

[020-059] builds it. But a city like Paris is perpetually growing. It is

[020-060] only such cities that become capitals. They are funnels, into

[020-061] which all the geographical, political, moral, and intellectual

[020-062] water-sheds of a country, all the natural slopes of a people,

[020-063] pour; wells of civilization, so to speak, and also sewers, where

[020-064] commerce, industry, intelligence, population,—all that is sap,

[020-065] all that is life, all that is the soul of a nation, filters and

[020-066] amasses unceasingly, drop by drop, century by century.

[020-067] So Charles V.’s wall suffered the fate of that of Philip

[020-068] Augustus. At the end of the fifteenth century, the Faubourg

[020-069] strides across it, passes beyond it, and runs farther. In the

[020-070] sixteenth, it seems to retreat visibly, and to bury itself deeper

[020-071] and deeper in the old city, so thick had the new city already

[020-072] become outside of it. Thus, beginning with the fifteenth century,

[020-073] where our story finds us, Paris had already outgrown the three

[020-074] concentric circles of walls which, from the time of Julian the

[020-075] Apostate, existed, so to speak, in germ in the Grand-Châtelet and

[020-076] the Petit-Châtelet. The mighty city had cracked, in succession,

[020-077] its four enclosures of walls, like a child grown too large for

[020-078] his garments of last year. Under Louis XI., this sea of houses

[020-079] was seen to be pierced at intervals by several groups of ruined

[020-080] towers, from the ancient wall, like the summits of hills in an

[020-081] inundation,—like archipelagos of the old Paris submerged beneath

[020-082] the new. Since that time Paris has undergone yet another

[020-083] transformation, unfortunately for our eyes; but it has passed

[020-084] only one more wall, that of Louis XV., that miserable wall of mud

[020-085] and spittle, worthy of the king who built it, worthy of the poet

[020-086] who sung it,—

[020-087] Le mur murant Paris rend Paris murmurant.[22]

[020-088] In the fifteenth century, Paris was still divided into three

[020-089] wholly distinct and separate towns, each having its own

[020-090] physiognomy, its own specialty, its manners, customs, privileges,

[020-091] and history: the City, the University, the Town. The City, which

[020-092] occupied the island, was the most ancient, the smallest, and the

[020-093] mother of the other two, crowded in between them like (may we be

[020-094] pardoned the comparison) a little old woman between two large and

[020-095] handsome maidens. The University covered the left bank of the

[020-096] Seine, from the Tournelle to the Tour de Nesle, points which

[020-097] correspond in the Paris of to-day, the one to the wine market,

[020-098] the other to the mint. Its wall included a large part of that

[020-099] plain where Julian had built his hot baths. The hill of

[020-100] Sainte-Geneviève was enclosed in it. The culminating point of

[020-101] this sweep of walls was the Papal gate, that is to say, near the

[020-102] present site of the Pantheon. The Town, which was the largest of

[020-103] the three fragments of Paris, held the right bank. Its quay,

[020-104] broken or interrupted in many places, ran along the Seine, from

[020-105] the Tour de Billy to the Tour du Bois; that is to say, from the

[020-106] place where the granary stands to-day, to the present site of the

[020-107] Tuileries. These four points, where the Seine intersected the

[020-108] wall of the capital, the Tournelle and the Tour de Nesle on the

[020-109] right, the Tour de Billy and the Tour du Bois on the left, were

[020-110] called pre-eminently, _the four towers of Paris_. The Town

[020-111] encroached still more extensively upon the fields than the

[020-112] University. The culminating point of the Town wall (that of

[020-113] Charles V.) was at the gates of Saint-Denis and Saint-Martin,

[020-114] whose situation has not been changed.

[020-115] As we have just said, each of these three great divisions of

[020-116] Paris was a town, but too special a town to be complete, a city

[020-117] which could not get along without the other two. Hence three

[020-118] entirely distinct aspects: churches abounded in the City;

[020-119] palaces, in the Town; and colleges, in the University. Neglecting

[020-120] here the originalities, of secondary importance in old Paris, and

[020-121] the capricious regulations regarding the public highways, we will

[020-122] say, from a general point of view, taking only masses and the

[020-123] whole group, in this chaos of communal jurisdictions, that the

[020-124] island belonged to the bishop, the right bank to the provost of

[020-125] the merchants, the left bank to the Rector; over all ruled the

[020-126] provost of Paris, a royal not a municipal official. The City had

[020-127] Notre-Dame; the Town, the Louvre and the Hôtel de Ville; the

[020-128] University, the Sorbonne. The Town had the markets (Halles); the

[020-129] city, the Hospital; the University, the Pré-aux-Clercs. Offences

[020-130] committed by the scholars on the left bank were tried in the law

[020-131] courts on the island, and were punished on the right bank at

[020-132] Montfaucon; unless the rector, feeling the university to be

[020-133] strong and the king weak, intervened; for it was the students’

[020-134] privilege to be hanged on their own grounds.

[020-135] The greater part of these privileges, it may be noted in passing,

[020-136] and there were some even better than the above, had been extorted

[020-137] from the kings by revolts and mutinies. It is the course of

[020-138] things from time immemorial; the king only lets go when the

[020-139] people tear away. There is an old charter which puts the matter

[020-140] naively: _àpropos_ of fidelity: _Civibus fidelitas in reges, quæ

[020-141] tamen aliquoties seditionibus interrupta, multa peperit

[020-142] privilegia_.

[020-143] In the fifteenth century, the Seine bathed five islands within

[020-144] the walls of Paris: Louviers island, where there were then trees,

[020-145] and where there is no longer anything but wood; l’île aux Vaches,

[020-146] and l’île Notre-Dame, both deserted, with the exception of one

[020-147] house, both fiefs of the bishop—in the seventeenth century, a

[020-148] single island was formed out of these two, which was built upon

[020-149] and named l’île Saint-Louis—, lastly the City, and at its point,

[020-150] the little islet of the cow tender, which was afterwards engulfed

[020-151] beneath the platform of the Pont-Neuf. The City then had five

[020-152] bridges: three on the right, the Pont Notre-Dame, and the Pont au

[020-153] Change, of stone, the Pont aux Meuniers, of wood; two on the

[020-154] left, the Petit Pont, of stone, the Pont Saint-Michel, of wood;

[020-155] all loaded with houses.

[020-156] The University had six gates, built by Philip Augustus; there

[020-157] were, beginning with la Tournelle, the Porte Saint-Victor, the

[020-158] Porte Bordelle, the Porte Papale, the Porte Saint-Jacques, the

[020-159] Porte Saint-Michel, the Porte Saint-Germain. The Town had six

[020-160] gates, built by Charles V.; beginning with the Tour de Billy they

[020-161] were: the Porte Saint-Antoine, the Porte du Temple, the Porte

[020-162] Saint-Martin, the Porte Saint-Denis, the Porte Montmartre, the

[020-163] Porte Saint-Honoré. All these gates were strong, and also

[020-164] handsome, which does not detract from strength. A large, deep

[020-165] moat, with a brisk current during the high water of winter,

[020-166] bathed the base of the wall round Paris; the Seine furnished the

[020-167] water. At night, the gates were shut, the river was barred at

[020-168] both ends of the city with huge iron chains, and Paris slept

[020-169] tranquilly.

[020-170] From a bird’s-eye view, these three burgs, the City, the Town,

[020-171] and the University, each presented to the eye an inextricable

[020-172] skein of eccentrically tangled streets. Nevertheless, at first

[020-173] sight, one recognized the fact that these three fragments formed

[020-174] but one body. One immediately perceived three long parallel

[020-175] streets, unbroken, undisturbed, traversing, almost in a straight

[020-176] line, all three cities, from one end to the other; from North to

[020-177] South, perpendicularly, to the Seine, which bound them together,

[020-178] mingled them, infused them in each other, poured and transfused

[020-179] the people incessantly, from one to the other, and made one out

[020-180] of the three. The first of these streets ran from the Porte

[020-181] Saint-Martin: it was called the Rue Saint-Jacques in the

[020-182] University, Rue de la Juiverie in the City, Rue Saint-Martin in

[020-183] the Town; it crossed the water twice, under the name of the Petit

[020-184] Pont and the Pont Notre-Dame. The second, which was called the

[020-185] Rue de la Harpe on the left bank, Rue de la Barillerié in the

[020-186] island, Rue Saint-Denis on the right bank, Pont Saint-Michel on

[020-187] one arm of the Seine, Pont au Change on the other, ran from the

[020-188] Porte Saint-Michel in the University, to the Porte Saint-Denis in

[020-189] the Town. However, under all these names, there were but two

[020-190] streets, parent streets, generating streets,—the two arteries of

[020-191] Paris. All the other veins of the triple city either derived

[020-192] their supply from them or emptied into them.
