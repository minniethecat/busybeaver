---[014-001] He was minded to augment his pace, but for the third time

[014-002] something barred his way. This something or, rather, some one was

[014-003] a blind man, a little blind fellow with a bearded, Jewish face,

[014-004] who, rowing away in the space about him with a stick, and towed

[014-005] by a large dog, droned through his nose with a Hungarian accent:

[014-006] “_Facitote caritatem_!”

[014-007] “Well, now,” said Gringoire, “here’s one at last who speaks a

[014-008] Christian tongue. I must have a very charitable aspect, since

[014-009] they ask alms of me in the present lean condition of my purse. My

[014-010] friend,” and he turned towards the blind man, “I sold my last

[014-011] shirt last week; that is to say, since you understand only the

[014-012] language of Cicero: _Vendidi hebdomade nuper transita meam

[014-013] ultimam chemisam_.”

[014-014] That said, he turned his back upon the blind man, and pursued his

[014-015] way. But the blind man began to increase his stride at the same

[014-016] time; and, behold! the cripple and the legless man, in his bowl,

[014-017] came up on their side in great haste, and with great clamor of

[014-018] bowl and crutches, upon the pavement. Then all three, jostling

[014-019] each other at poor Gringoire’s heels, began to sing their song to

[014-020] him,—

[014-021] “_Caritatem_!” chanted the blind man.

[014-022] “_La buona mancia_!” chanted the cripple in the bowl.

[014-023] And the lame man took up the musical phrase by repeating: “_Un

[014-024] pedaso de pan_!”

[014-025] Gringoire stopped up his ears. “Oh, tower of Babel!” he

[014-026] exclaimed.

[014-027] He set out to run. The blind man ran! The lame man ran! The

[014-028] cripple in the bowl ran!

[014-029] And then, in proportion as he plunged deeper into the street,

[014-030] cripples in bowls, blind men and lame men, swarmed about him, and

[014-031] men with one arm, and with one eye, and the leprous with their

[014-032] sores, some emerging from little streets adjacent, some from the

[014-033] air-holes of cellars, howling, bellowing, yelping, all limping

[014-034] and halting, all flinging themselves towards the light, and

[014-035] humped up in the mire, like snails after a shower.

[014-036] Gringoire, still followed by his three persecutors, and not

[014-037] knowing very well what was to become of him, marched along in

[014-038] terror among them, turning out for the lame, stepping over the

[014-039] cripples in bowls, with his feet imbedded in that ant-hill of

[014-040] lame men, like the English captain who got caught in the

[014-041] quicksand of a swarm of crabs.

[014-042] The idea occurred to him of making an effort to retrace his

[014-043] steps. But it was too late. This whole legion had closed in

[014-044] behind him, and his three beggars held him fast. So he proceeded,

[014-045] impelled both by this irresistible flood, by fear, and by a

[014-046] vertigo which converted all this into a sort of horrible dream.

[014-047] At last he reached the end of the street. It opened upon an

[014-048] immense place, where a thousand scattered lights flickered in the

[014-049] confused mists of night. Gringoire flew thither, hoping to

[014-050] escape, by the swiftness of his legs, from the three infirm

[014-051] spectres who had clutched him.

[014-052] “_Onde vas, hombre_?” (Where are you going, my man?) cried the

[014-053] cripple, flinging away his crutches, and running after him with

[014-054] the best legs that ever traced a geometrical step upon the

[014-055] pavements of Paris.

[014-056] In the meantime the legless man, erect upon his feet, crowned

[014-057] Gringoire with his heavy iron bowl, and the blind man glared in

[014-058] his face with flaming eyes!

[014-059] “Where am I?” said the terrified poet.

[014-060] “In the Court of Miracles,” replied a fourth spectre, who had

[014-061] accosted them.

[014-062] “Upon my soul,” resumed Gringoire, “I certainly do behold the

[014-063] blind who see, and the lame who walk, but where is the Saviour?”

[014-064] They replied by a burst of sinister laughter.

[014-065] The poor poet cast his eyes about him. It was, in truth, that

[014-066] redoubtable Cour des Miracles, whither an honest man had never

[014-067] penetrated at such an hour; the magic circle where the officers

[014-068] of the Châtelet and the sergeants of the provostship, who

[014-069] ventured thither, disappeared in morsels; a city of thieves, a

[014-070] hideous wart on the face of Paris; a sewer, from which escaped

[014-071] every morning, and whither returned every night to crouch, that

[014-072] stream of vices, of mendicancy and vagabondage which always

[014-073] overflows in the streets of capitals; a monstrous hive, to which

[014-074] returned at nightfall, with their booty, all the drones of the

[014-075] social order; a lying hospital where the bohemian, the disfrocked

[014-076] monk, the ruined scholar, the ne’er-do-wells of all nations,

[014-077] Spaniards, Italians, Germans,—of all religions, Jews, Christians,

[014-078] Mahometans, idolaters, covered with painted sores, beggars by

[014-079] day, were transformed by night into brigands; an immense

[014-080] dressing-room, in a word, where, at that epoch, the actors of

[014-081] that eternal comedy, which theft, prostitution, and murder play

[014-082] upon the pavements of Paris, dressed and undressed.

[014-083] It was a vast place, irregular and badly paved, like all the

[014-084] squares of Paris at that date. Fires, around which swarmed

[014-085] strange groups, blazed here and there. Every one was going,

[014-086] coming, and shouting. Shrill laughter was to be heard, the

[014-087] wailing of children, the voices of women. The hands and heads of

[014-088] this throng, black against the luminous background, outlined

[014-089] against it a thousand eccentric gestures. At times, upon the

[014-090] ground, where trembled the light of the fires, mingled with

[014-091] large, indefinite shadows, one could behold a dog passing, which

[014-092] resembled a man, a man who resembled a dog. The limits of races

[014-093] and species seemed effaced in this city, as in a pandemonium.

[014-094] Men, women, beasts, age, sex, health, maladies, all seemed to be

[014-095] in common among these people; all went together, they mingled,

[014-096] confounded, superposed; each one there participated in all.

[014-097] The poor and flickering flames of the fire permitted Gringoire to

[014-098] distinguish, amid his trouble, all around the immense place, a

[014-099] hideous frame of ancient houses, whose wormeaten, shrivelled,

[014-100] stunted façades, each pierced with one or two lighted attic

[014-101] windows, seemed to him, in the darkness, like enormous heads of

[014-102] old women, ranged in a circle, monstrous and crabbed, winking as

[014-103] they looked on at the Witches’ Sabbath.

[014-104] It was like a new world, unknown, unheard of, misshapen,

[014-105] creeping, swarming, fantastic.

[014-106] Gringoire, more and more terrified, clutched by the three beggars

[014-107] as by three pairs of tongs, dazed by a throng of other faces

[014-108] which frothed and yelped around him, unhappy Gringoire endeavored

[014-109] to summon his presence of mind, in order to recall whether it was

[014-110] a Saturday. But his efforts were vain; the thread of his memory

[014-111] and of his thought was broken; and, doubting everything, wavering

[014-112] between what he saw and what he felt, he put to himself this

[014-113] unanswerable question,—

[014-114] “If I exist, does this exist? if this exists, do I exist?”

[014-115] At that moment, a distinct cry arose in the buzzing throng which

[014-116] surrounded him, “Let’s take him to the king! let’s take him to

[014-117] the king!”

[014-118] “Holy Virgin!” murmured Gringoire, “the king here must be a ram.”

[014-119] “To the king! to the king!” repeated all voices.

[014-120] They dragged him off. Each vied with the other in laying his

[014-121] claws upon him. But the three beggars did not loose their hold

[014-122] and tore him from the rest, howling, “He belongs to us!”

[014-123] The poet’s already sickly doublet yielded its last sigh in this

[014-124] struggle.

[014-125] While traversing the horrible place, his vertigo vanished. After

[014-126] taking a few steps, the sentiment of reality returned to him. He

[014-127] began to become accustomed to the atmosphere of the place. At the

[014-128] first moment there had arisen from his poet’s head, or, simply

[014-129] and prosaically, from his empty stomach, a mist, a vapor, so to

[014-130] speak, which, spreading between objects and himself, permitted

[014-131] him to catch a glimpse of them only in the incoherent fog of

[014-132] nightmare,—in those shadows of dreams which distort every

[014-133] outline, agglomerating objects into unwieldy groups, dilating

[014-134] things into chimeras, and men into phantoms. Little by little,

[014-135] this hallucination was succeeded by a less bewildered and

[014-136] exaggerating view. Reality made its way to the light around him,

[014-137] struck his eyes, struck his feet, and demolished, bit by bit, all

[014-138] that frightful poetry with which he had, at first, believed

[014-139] himself to be surrounded. He was forced to perceive that he was

[014-140] not walking in the Styx, but in mud, that he was elbowed not by

[014-141] demons, but by thieves; that it was not his soul which was in

[014-142] question, but his life (since he lacked that precious

[014-143] conciliator, which places itself so effectually between the

[014-144] bandit and the honest man—a purse). In short, on examining the

[014-145] orgy more closely, and with more coolness, he fell from the

[014-146] witches’ sabbath to the dram-shop.

[014-147] The Cour des Miracles was, in fact, merely a dram-shop; but a

[014-148] brigand’s dram-shop, reddened quite as much with blood as with

[014-149] wine.

[014-150] The spectacle which presented itself to his eyes, when his ragged

[014-151] escort finally deposited him at the end of his trip, was not

[014-152] fitted to bear him back to poetry, even to the poetry of hell. It

[014-153] was more than ever the prosaic and brutal reality of the tavern.

[014-154] Were we not in the fifteenth century, we would say that Gringoire

[014-155] had descended from Michael Angelo to Callot.

[014-156] Around a great fire which burned on a large, circular flagstone,

[014-157] the flames of which had heated red-hot the legs of a tripod,

[014-158] which was empty for the moment, some wormeaten tables were

[014-159] placed, here and there, haphazard, no lackey of a geometrical

[014-160] turn having deigned to adjust their parallelism, or to see to it

[014-161] that they did not make too unusual angles. Upon these tables

[014-162] gleamed several dripping pots of wine and beer, and round these

[014-163] pots were grouped many bacchic visages, purple with the fire and

[014-164] the wine. There was a man with a huge belly and a jovial face,

[014-165] noisily kissing a woman of the town, thickset and brawny. There

[014-166] was a sort of sham soldier, a “naquois,” as the slang expression

[014-167] runs, who was whistling as he undid the bandages from his

[014-168] fictitious wound, and removing the numbness from his sound and

[014-169] vigorous knee, which had been swathed since morning in a thousand

[014-170] ligatures. On the other hand, there was a wretched fellow,

[014-171] preparing with celandine and beef’s blood, his “leg of God,” for

[014-172] the next day. Two tables further on, a palmer, with his pilgrim’s

[014-173] costume complete, was practising the lament of the Holy Queen,

[014-174] not forgetting the drone and the nasal drawl. Further on, a young

[014-175] scamp was taking a lesson in epilepsy from an old pretender, who

[014-176] was instructing him in the art of foaming at the mouth, by

[014-177] chewing a morsel of soap. Beside him, a man with the dropsy was

[014-178] getting rid of his swelling, and making four or five female

[014-179] thieves, who were disputing at the same table, over a child who

[014-180] had been stolen that evening, hold their noses. All circumstances

[014-181] which, two centuries later, “seemed so ridiculous to the court,”

[014-182] as Sauval says, “that they served as a pastime to the king, and

[014-183] as an introduction to the royal ballet of Night, divided into

[014-184] four parts and danced on the theatre of the Petit-Bourbon.”

[014-185] “Never,” adds an eye witness of 1653, “have the sudden

[014-186] metamorphoses of the Court of Miracles been more happily

[014-187] presented. Benserade prepared us for it by some very gallant

[014-188] verses.”

[014-189] Loud laughter everywhere, and obscene songs. Each one held his

[014-190] own course, carping and swearing, without listening to his

[014-191] neighbor. Pots clinked, and quarrels sprang up at the shock of

[014-192] the pots, and the broken pots made rents in the rags.

[014-193] A big dog, seated on his tail, gazed at the fire. Some children

[014-194] were mingled in this orgy. The stolen child wept and cried.

[014-195] Another, a big boy four years of age, seated with legs dangling,

[014-196] upon a bench that was too high for him, before a table that

[014-197] reached to his chin, and uttering not a word. A third, gravely

[014-198] spreading out upon the table with his finger, the melted tallow

[014-199] which dripped from a candle. Last of all, a little fellow

[014-200] crouching in the mud, almost lost in a cauldron, which he was

[014-201] scraping with a tile, and from which he was evoking a sound that

[014-202] would have made Stradivarius swoon.
