---[082-001] The man in black let the garrulous poet chatter on. He continued

[082-002] to struggle against the violent and narrow current, which

[082-003] separates the prow of the City and the stem of the island of

[082-004] Notre-Dame, which we call to-day the Isle St. Louis.

[082-005] “By the way, master!” continued Gringoire suddenly. “At the

[082-006] moment when we arrived on the Parvis, through the enraged

[082-007] outcasts, did your reverence observe that poor little devil whose

[082-008] skull your deaf man was just cracking on the railing of the

[082-009] gallery of the kings? I am near sighted and I could not recognize

[082-010] him. Do you know who he could be?”

[082-011] The stranger answered not a word. But he suddenly ceased rowing,

[082-012] his arms fell as though broken, his head sank on his breast, and

[082-013] la Esmeralda heard him sigh convulsively. She shuddered. She had

[082-014] heard such sighs before.

[082-015] The boat, abandoned to itself, floated for several minutes with

[082-016] the stream. But the man in black finally recovered himself,

[082-017] seized the oars once more and began to row against the current.

[082-018] He doubled the point of the Isle of Notre Dame, and made for the

[082-019] landing-place of the Port au Foin.

[082-020] “Ah!” said Gringoire, “yonder is the Barbeau mansion.—Stay,

[082-021] master, look: that group of black roofs which make such singular

[082-022] angles yonder, above that heap of black, fibrous grimy, dirty

[082-023] clouds, where the moon is completely crushed and spread out like

[082-024] the yolk of an egg whose shell is broken.—’Tis a fine mansion.

[082-025] There is a chapel crowned with a small vault full of very well

[082-026] carved enrichments. Above, you can see the bell tower, very

[082-027] delicately pierced. There is also a pleasant garden, which

[082-028] consists of a pond, an aviary, an echo, a mall, a labyrinth, a

[082-029] house for wild beasts, and a quantity of leafy alleys very

[082-030] agreeable to Venus. There is also a rascal of a tree which is

[082-031] called ‘the lewd,’ because it favored the pleasures of a famous

[082-032] princess and a constable of France, who was a gallant and a

[082-033] wit.—Alas! we poor philosophers are to a constable as a plot of

[082-034] cabbages or a radish bed to the garden of the Louvre. What

[082-035] matters it, after all? human life, for the great as well as for

[082-036] us, is a mixture of good and evil. Pain is always by the side of

[082-037] joy, the spondee by the dactyl.—Master, I must relate to you the

[082-038] history of the Barbeau mansion. It ends in tragic fashion. It was

[082-039] in 1319, in the reign of Philippe V., the longest reign of the

[082-040] kings of France. The moral of the story is that the temptations

[082-041] of the flesh are pernicious and malignant. Let us not rest our

[082-042] glance too long on our neighbor’s wife, however gratified our

[082-043] senses may be by her beauty. Fornication is a very libertine

[082-044] thought. Adultery is a prying into the pleasures of others—Ohé!

[082-045] the noise yonder is redoubling!”

[082-046] The tumult around Notre-Dame was, in fact, increasing. They

[082-047] listened. Cries of victory were heard with tolerable

[082-048] distinctness. All at once, a hundred torches, the light of which

[082-049] glittered upon the helmets of men at arms, spread over the church

[082-050] at all heights, on the towers, on the galleries, on the flying

[082-051] buttresses. These torches seemed to be in search of something;

[082-052] and soon distant clamors reached the fugitives distinctly:—“The

[082-053] gypsy! the sorceress! death to the gypsy!”

[082-054] The unhappy girl dropped her head upon her hands, and the unknown

[082-055] began to row furiously towards the shore. Meanwhile our

[082-056] philosopher reflected. He clasped the goat in his arms, and

[082-057] gently drew away from the gypsy, who pressed closer and closer to

[082-058] him, as though to the only asylum which remained to her.

[082-059] It is certain that Gringoire was enduring cruel perplexity. He

[082-060] was thinking that the goat also, “according to existing law,”

[082-061] would be hung if recaptured; which would be a great pity, poor

[082-062] Djali! that he had thus two condemned creatures attached to him;

[082-063] that his companion asked no better than to take charge of the

[082-064] gypsy. A violent combat began between his thoughts, in which,

[082-065] like the Jupiter of the Iliad, he weighed in turn the gypsy and

[082-066] the goat; and he looked at them alternately with eyes moist with

[082-067] tears, saying between his teeth:

[082-068] “But I cannot save you both!”

[082-069] A shock informed them that the boat had reached the land at last.

[082-070] The uproar still filled the city. The unknown rose, approached

[082-071] the gypsy, and endeavored to take her arm to assist her to

[082-072] alight. She repulsed him and clung to the sleeve of Gringoire,

[082-073] who, in his turn, absorbed in the goat, almost repulsed her. Then

[082-074] she sprang alone from the boat. She was so troubled that she did

[082-075] not know what she did or whither she was going. Thus she remained

[082-076] for a moment, stunned, watching the water flow past; when she

[082-077] gradually returned to her senses, she found herself alone on the

[082-078] wharf with the unknown. It appears that Gringoire had taken

[082-079] advantage of the moment of debarcation to slip away with the goat

[082-080] into the block of houses of the Rue Grenier-sur-l’Eau.

[082-081] The poor gypsy shivered when she beheld herself alone with this

[082-082] man. She tried to speak, to cry out, to call Gringoire; her

[082-083] tongue was dumb in her mouth, and no sound left her lips. All at

[082-084] once she felt the stranger’s hand on hers. It was a strong, cold

[082-085] hand. Her teeth chattered, she turned paler than the ray of

[082-086] moonlight which illuminated her. The man spoke not a word. He

[082-087] began to ascend towards the Place de Grève, holding her by the

[082-088] hand.

[082-089] At that moment, she had a vague feeling that destiny is an

[082-090] irresistible force. She had no more resistance left in her, she

[082-091] allowed herself to be dragged along, running while he walked. At

[082-092] this spot the quay ascended. But it seemed to her as though she

[082-093] were descending a slope.

[082-094] She gazed about her on all sides. Not a single passer-by. The

[082-095] quay was absolutely deserted. She heard no sound, she felt no

[082-096] people moving save in the tumultuous and glowing city, from which

[082-097] she was separated only by an arm of the Seine, and whence her

[082-098] name reached her, mingled with cries of “Death!” The rest of

[082-099] Paris was spread around her in great blocks of shadows.

[082-100] Meanwhile, the stranger continued to drag her along with the same

[082-101] silence and the same rapidity. She had no recollection of any of

[082-102] the places where she was walking. As she passed before a lighted

[082-103] window, she made an effort, drew up suddenly, and cried out,

[082-104] “Help!”

[082-105] The _bourgeois_ who was standing at the window opened it,

[082-106] appeared there in his shirt with his lamp, stared at the quay

[082-107] with a stupid air, uttered some words which she did not

[082-108] understand, and closed his shutter again. It was her last gleam

[082-109] of hope extinguished.

[082-110] The man in black did not utter a syllable; he held her firmly,

[082-111] and set out again at a quicker pace. She no longer resisted, but

[082-112] followed him, completely broken.

[082-113] From time to time she called together a little strength, and

[082-114] said, in a voice broken by the unevenness of the pavement and the

[082-115] breathlessness of their flight, “Who are you? Who are you?” He

[082-116] made no reply.

[082-117] They arrived thus, still keeping along the quay, at a tolerably

[082-118] spacious square. It was the Grève. In the middle, a sort of

[082-119] black, erect cross was visible; it was the gallows. She

[082-120] recognized all this, and saw where she was.

[082-121] The man halted, turned towards her and raised his cowl.

[082-122] “Oh!” she stammered, almost petrified, “I knew well that it was

[082-123] he again!”

[082-124] It was the priest. He looked like the ghost of himself; that is

[082-125] an effect of the moonlight, it seems as though one beheld only

[082-126] the spectres of things in that light.

[082-127] “Listen!” he said to her; and she shuddered at the sound of that

[082-128] fatal voice which she had not heard for a long time. He continued

[082-129] speaking with those brief and panting jerks, which betoken deep

[082-130] internal convulsions. “Listen! we are here. I am going to speak

[082-131] to you. This is the Grève. This is an extreme point. Destiny

[082-132] gives us to one another. I am going to decide as to your life;

[082-133] you will decide as to my soul. Here is a place, here is a night

[082-134] beyond which one sees nothing. Then listen to me. I am going to

[082-135] tell you.... In the first place, speak not to me of your Phœbus.

[082-136] (As he spoke thus he paced to and fro, like a man who cannot

[082-137] remain in one place, and dragged her after him.) Do not speak to

[082-138] me of him. Do you see? If you utter that name, I know not what I

[082-139] shall do, but it will be terrible.”

[082-140] Then, like a body which recovers its centre of gravity, he became

[082-141] motionless once more, but his words betrayed no less agitation.

[082-142] His voice grew lower and lower.

[082-143] “Do not turn your head aside thus. Listen to me. It is a serious

[082-144] matter. In the first place, here is what has happened.—All this

[082-145] will not be laughed at. I swear it to you.—What was I saying?

[082-146] Remind me! Oh!—There is a decree of Parliament which gives you

[082-147] back to the scaffold. I have just rescued you from their hands.

[082-148] But they are pursuing you. Look!”

[082-149] He extended his arm toward the City. The search seemed, in fact,

[082-150] to be still in progress there. The uproar drew nearer; the tower

[082-151] of the lieutenant’s house, situated opposite the Grève, was full

[082-152] of clamors and light, and soldiers could be seen running on the

[082-153] opposite quay with torches and these cries, “The gypsy! Where is

[082-154] the gypsy! Death! Death!”

[082-155] “You see that they are in pursuit of you, and that I am not lying

[082-156] to you. I love you.—Do not open your mouth; refrain from speaking

[082-157] to me rather, if it be only to tell me that you hate me. I have

[082-158] made up my mind not to hear that again.—I have just saved

[082-159] you.—Let me finish first. I can save you wholly. I have prepared

[082-160] everything. It is yours at will. If you wish, I can do it.”

[082-161] He broke off violently. “No, that is not what I should say!”

[082-162] As he went with hurried step and made her hurry also, for he did

[082-163] not release her, he walked straight to the gallows, and pointed

[082-164] to it with his finger,—

[082-165] “Choose between us two,” he said, coldly.

[082-166] She tore herself from his hands and fell at the foot of the

[082-167] gibbet, embracing that funereal support, then she half turned her

[082-168] beautiful head, and looked at the priest over her shoulder. One

[082-169] would have said that she was a Holy Virgin at the foot of the

[082-170] cross. The priest remained motionless, his finger still raised

[082-171] toward the gibbet, preserving his attitude like a statue. At

[082-172] length the gypsy said to him,—

[082-173] “It causes me less horror than you do.”

[082-174] Then he allowed his arm to sink slowly, and gazed at the pavement

[082-175] in profound dejection.

[082-176] “If these stones could speak,” he murmured, “yes, they would say

[082-177] that a very unhappy man stands here.”

[082-178] He went on. The young girl, kneeling before the gallows,

[082-179] enveloped in her long flowing hair, let him speak on without

[082-180] interruption. He now had a gentle and plaintive accent which

[082-181] contrasted sadly with the haughty harshness of his features.

[082-182] “I love you. Oh! how true that is! So nothing comes of that fire

[082-183] which burns my heart! Alas! young girl, night and day—yes, night

[082-184] and day I tell you,—it is torture. Oh! I suffer too much, my poor

[082-185] child. ’Tis a thing deserving of compassion, I assure you. You

[082-186] see that I speak gently to you. I really wish that you should no

[082-187] longer cherish this horror of me.—After all, if a man loves a

[082-188] woman, ’tis not his fault!—Oh, my God!—What! So you will never

[082-189] pardon me? You will always hate me? All is over then. It is that

[082-190] which renders me evil, do you see? and horrible to myself.—You

[082-191] will not even look at me! You are thinking of something else,

[082-192] perchance, while I stand here and talk to you, shuddering on the

[082-193] brink of eternity for both of us! Above all things, do not speak

[082-194] to me of the officer!—I would cast myself at your knees, I would

[082-195] kiss not your feet, but the earth which is under your feet; I

[082-196] would sob like a child, I would tear from my breast not words,

[082-197] but my very heart and vitals, to tell you that I love you;—all

[082-198] would be useless, all!—And yet you have nothing in your heart but

[082-199] what is tender and merciful. You are radiant with the most

[082-200] beautiful mildness; you are wholly sweet, good, pitiful, and

[082-201] charming. Alas! You cherish no ill will for any one but me alone!

[082-202] Oh! what a fatality!”
