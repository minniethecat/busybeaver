# Paquette la Chantefleurie

Paquette la Chantefleurie — born Paquette Guybertaut — is a figure of profound maternal tragedy in Victor Hugo's *Notre-Dame de Paris*. As a young woman, she was known throughout Reims for her beauty and her singing voice, hence the nickname "la Chantefleurie" (the singing flower). Her story, narrated in Book VI, traces a path from youthful joy through devastating loss to lifelong penitential seclusion — and finally, a brief and fatal reunion with her lost daughter.

## Role in the Novel

Paquette la Chantefleurie's story is told in flashback by the gossips of the Guele-de-Fer house, who recount her history to explain the identity of the recluse of the Tour-Roland. The narrative reveals that Paquette was once "a pretty maid of eighteen" [036-159] whose beauty and charm made her the delight of Reims. After the death of her father (a minstrel) left her orphaned, she sank gradually into poverty and prostitution — "poor Chantefleurie, she belonged to every one" [036-204].

Her redemption came through motherhood: the birth of her daughter Agnès (the infant who would become Esmeralda) gave her life meaning. When Egyptian beggars kidnapped the child and left a deformed foundling (Quasimodo) in her place, Paquette's grief drove her to become the penitential recluse known as Sister Gudule, who for fifteen years has lived in a cell in the Tour-Roland, cursing the Egyptian woman who stole her child — not knowing that Esmeralda, the dancer she now hates, is her own daughter.

The tragic irony culminates in Book XI, when Paquette recognizes Esmeralda by the matching infant shoe, only to lose her again moments later to the executioner.

## Biography

### Origins and Background

Paquette was born in Reims, the daughter of Guybertaut, a minstrel on the river-boats. Her father died when she was young, leaving her with only a poor mother "who had taught her nothing but a bit of embroidery and net-work" [036-172]. This meager education proved insufficient to support her, and after her mother's death, Paquette's beauty became both her only asset and her undoing.

### The Loss of Agnès

The central event of Paquette's life was the kidnapping of her infant daughter Agnès by a band of Egyptian fortune-tellers. "Chantefleurie had been a poor creature during the five years" after this loss [037-012], her grief transforming into obsessive devotion to a single object — one of the infant's tiny embroidered shoes. This shoe, preserved through years of madness and prayer, becomes the novel's most potent symbol of maternal love and its capacity to transcend time.

### Fate

In the novel's climactic recognition scene, Paquette — now the emaciated recluse Sister Gudule — produces the matching shoe that proves Esmeralda is her lost daughter. Their reunion lasts only moments before the king's archers arrive to take Esmeralda to the gallows. Paquette dies attempting to prevent her daughter's execution, her body falling lifeless to the pavement of the Tour-Roland.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Paquette's defining trait is the intensity of her maternal love — first as a young mother doting on her infant, then as a grief-maddened recluse whose hatred for "Egyptians" conceals a love that has never diminished. Hugo presents her not as a saint but as a woman whose entire being has been consumed by the single experience of loss.

### Relationships

- **[[esmeralda]]**: Her daughter, stolen as an infant and unrecognized until the novel's final act. The mother-daughter relationship is the emotional core of the Tour-Roland subplot.
- **[[quasimodo]]**: The deformed infant left in place of the stolen Agnès; Paquette was the one who consented to exchange the "monstrous" foundling for her child, inadvertently connecting the fates of Quasimodo and Esmeralda from infancy.
- **[[claude-frollo]]**: As archdeacon, Frollo was the one who took the deformed foundling from Paquette's bed and baptized him Quasimodo — a moment that links the recluse's tragedy to the novel's central characters.

### Symbolism

Paquette la Chantefleurie embodies the theme of maternal suffering that runs through the novel. Her transformation from "blossoming rose" to "withered recluse" mirrors the novel's broader meditation on how single moments of loss can define entire lives. The matching infant shoes — one kept by the mother, one worn by the daughter — function as a recognition token in the tradition of classical tragedy, but Hugo invests them with specifically Christian overtones of redemption through suffering.

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo uses Paquette's story to explore the novel's most painful theme: the destruction of innocence by social forces beyond individual control. Paquette is not a moral agent — she is acted upon by poverty, by the casual cruelty of the Egyptian fortune-tellers, by the institutional indifference of the Church that houses but does not heal her. Her fifteen years of penitential seclusion in the Tour-Roland literalize the imprisonment that Hugo sees as the fate of women in medieval society — whether in a convent cell, a prison tower, or the cage of poverty.

The irony of Paquette cursing Esmeralda — the very daughter she mourns — is Hugo's darkest illustration of how suffering can corrupt love itself, turning the mother's devotion into the persecutor's hatred.
