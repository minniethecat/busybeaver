---[047-001] “The deuce!” muttered Jehan in his beard.

[047-002] “Hence,” continued the priest, “one wretched thought is

[047-003] sufficient to render a man weak and beside himself! Oh! how

[047-004] Claude Pernelle would laugh at me. She who could not turn

[047-005] Nicholas Flamel aside, for one moment, from his pursuit of the

[047-006] great work! What! I hold in my hand the magic hammer of Zéchiélé!

[047-007] at every blow dealt by the formidable rabbi, from the depths of

[047-008] his cell, upon this nail, that one of his enemies whom he had

[047-009] condemned, were he a thousand leagues away, was buried a cubit

[047-010] deep in the earth which swallowed him. The King of France

[047-011] himself, in consequence of once having inconsiderately knocked at

[047-012] the door of the thermaturgist, sank to the knees through the

[047-013] pavement of his own Paris. This took place three centuries ago.

[047-014] Well! I possess the hammer and the nail, and in my hands they are

[047-015] utensils no more formidable than a club in the hands of a maker

[047-016] of edge tools. And yet all that is required is to find the magic

[047-017] word which Zéchiélé pronounced when he struck his nail.”

[047-018] “What nonsense!” thought Jehan.

[047-019] “Let us see, let us try!” resumed the archdeacon briskly. “Were I

[047-020] to succeed, I should behold the blue spark flash from the head of

[047-021] the nail. Emen-Hétan! Emen-Hétan! That’s not it. Sigéani!

[047-022] Sigéani! May this nail open the tomb to any one who bears the

[047-023] name of Phœbus! A curse upon it! Always and eternally the same

[047-024] idea!”

[047-025] And he flung away the hammer in a rage. Then he sank down so

[047-026] deeply on the arm-chair and the table, that Jehan lost him from

[047-027] view behind the great pile of manuscripts. For the space of

[047-028] several minutes, all that he saw was his fist convulsively

[047-029] clenched on a book. Suddenly, Dom Claude sprang up, seized a

[047-030] compass and engraved in silence upon the wall in capital letters,

[047-031] this Greek word

[047-032] ἈΝÁΓΚΗ.

[047-033] “My brother is mad,” said Jehan to himself; “it would have been

[047-034] far more simple to write _Fatum_, every one is not obliged to

[047-035] know Greek.”

[047-036] The archdeacon returned and seated himself in his armchair, and

[047-037] placed his head on both his hands, as a sick man does, whose head

[047-038] is heavy and burning.

[047-039] The student watched his brother with surprise. He did not know,

[047-040] he who wore his heart on his sleeve, he who observed only the

[047-041] good old law of Nature in the world, he who allowed his passions

[047-042] to follow their inclinations, and in whom the lake of great

[047-043] emotions was always dry, so freely did he let it off each day by

[047-044] fresh drains,—he did not know with what fury the sea of human

[047-045] passions ferments and boils when all egress is denied to it, how

[047-046] it accumulates, how it swells, how it overflows, how it hollows

[047-047] out the heart; how it breaks in inward sobs, and dull

[047-048] convulsions, until it has rent its dikes and burst its bed. The

[047-049] austere and glacial envelope of Claude Frollo, that cold surface

[047-050] of steep and inaccessible virtue, had always deceived Jehan. The

[047-051] merry scholar had never dreamed that there was boiling lava,

[047-052] furious and profound, beneath the snowy brow of Ætna.

[047-053] We do not know whether he suddenly became conscious of these

[047-054] things; but, giddy as he was, he understood that he had seen what

[047-055] he ought not to have seen, that he had just surprised the soul of

[047-056] his elder brother in one of its most secret altitudes, and that

[047-057] Claude must not be allowed to know it. Seeing that the archdeacon

[047-058] had fallen back into his former immobility, he withdrew his head

[047-059] very softly, and made some noise with his feet outside the door,

[047-060] like a person who has just arrived and is giving warning of his

[047-061] approach.

[047-062] “Enter!” cried the archdeacon, from the interior of his cell; “I

[047-063] was expecting you. I left the door unlocked expressly; enter

[047-064] Master Jacques!”

[047-065] The scholar entered boldly. The archdeacon, who was very much

[047-066] embarrassed by such a visit in such a place, trembled in his

[047-067] arm-chair. “What! ’tis you, Jehan?”

[047-068] “’Tis a J, all the same,” said the scholar, with his ruddy,

[047-069] merry, and audacious face.

[047-070] Dom Claude’s visage had resumed its severe expression.

[047-071] “What are you come for?”

[047-072] “Brother,” replied the scholar, making an effort to assume a

[047-073] decent, pitiful, and modest mien, and twirling his cap in his

[047-074] hands with an innocent air; “I am come to ask of you—”

[047-075] “What?”

[047-076] “A little lecture on morality, of which I stand greatly in need,”

[047-077] Jehan did not dare to add aloud,—“and a little money of which I

[047-078] am in still greater need.” This last member of his phrase

[047-079] remained unuttered.

[047-080] “Monsieur,” said the archdeacon, in a cold tone, “I am greatly

[047-081] displeased with you.”

[047-082] “Alas!” sighed the scholar.

[047-083] Dom Claude made his arm-chair describe a quarter circle, and

[047-084] gazed intently at Jehan.

[047-085] “I am very glad to see you.”

[047-086] This was a formidable exordium. Jehan braced himself for a rough

[047-087] encounter.

[047-088] “Jehan, complaints are brought me about you every day. What

[047-089] affray was that in which you bruised with a cudgel a little

[047-090] vicomte, Albert de Ramonchamp?”

[047-091] “Oh!” said Jehan, “a vast thing that! A malicious page amused

[047-092] himself by splashing the scholars, by making his horse gallop

[047-093] through the mire!”

[047-094] “Who,” pursued the archdeacon, “is that Mahiet Fargel, whose gown

[047-095] you have torn? _Tunicam dechiraverunt_, saith the complaint.”

[047-096] “Ah bah! a wretched cap of a Montaigu! Isn’t that it?”

[047-097] “The complaint says _tunicam_ and not _cappettam_. Do you know

[047-098] Latin?”

[047-099] Jehan did not reply.

[047-100] “Yes,” pursued the priest shaking his head, “that is the state of

[047-101] learning and letters at the present day. The Latin tongue is

[047-102] hardly understood, Syriac is unknown, Greek so odious that ’tis

[047-103] accounted no ignorance in the most learned to skip a Greek word

[047-104] without reading it, and to say, ‘_Græcum est non legitur_.’”

[047-105] The scholar raised his eyes boldly. “Monsieur my brother, doth it

[047-106] please you that I shall explain in good French vernacular that

[047-107] Greek word which is written yonder on the wall?”

[047-108] “What word?”

[047-109] “ἈΝÁΓΚΗ.”

[047-110] A slight flush spread over the cheeks of the priest with their

[047-111] high bones, like the puff of smoke which announces on the outside

[047-112] the secret commotions of a volcano. The student hardly noticed

[047-113] it.

[047-114] “Well, Jehan,” stammered the elder brother with an effort, “What

[047-115] is the meaning of yonder word?”

[047-116] “FATE.”

[047-117] Dom Claude turned pale again, and the scholar pursued carelessly.

[047-118] “And that word below it, graved by the same hand, _Ἀνάγνεία_,

[047-119] signifies ‘impurity.’ You see that people do know their Greek.”

[047-120] And the archdeacon remained silent. This Greek lesson had

[047-121] rendered him thoughtful.

[047-122] Master Jehan, who possessed all the artful ways of a spoiled

[047-123] child, judged that the moment was a favorable one in which to

[047-124] risk his request. Accordingly, he assumed an extremely soft tone

[047-125] and began,—

[047-126] “My good brother, do you hate me to such a degree as to look

[047-127] savagely upon me because of a few mischievous cuffs and blows

[047-128] distributed in a fair war to a pack of lads and brats, _quibusdam

[047-129] marmosetis_? You see, good Brother Claude, that people know their

[047-130] Latin.”

[047-131] But all this caressing hypocrisy did not have its usual effect on

[047-132] the severe elder brother. Cerberus did not bite at the honey

[047-133] cake. The archdeacon’s brow did not lose a single wrinkle.

[047-134] “What are you driving at?” he said dryly.

[047-135] “Well, in point of fact, this!” replied Jehan bravely, “I stand

[047-136] in need of money.”

[047-137] At this audacious declaration, the archdeacon’s visage assumed a

[047-138] thoroughly pedagogical and paternal expression.

[047-139] “You know, Monsieur Jehan, that our fief of Tirechappe, putting

[047-140] the direct taxes and the rents of the nine and twenty houses in a

[047-141] block, yields only nine and thirty livres, eleven sous, six

[047-142] deniers, Parisian. It is one half more than in the time of the

[047-143] brothers Paclet, but it is not much.”

[047-144] “I need money,” said Jehan stoically.

[047-145] “You know that the official has decided that our twenty-one

[047-146] houses should be moved full into the fief of the Bishopric, and

[047-147] that we could redeem this homage only by paying the reverend

[047-148] bishop two marks of silver gilt of the price of six livres

[047-149] parisis. Now, these two marks I have not yet been able to get

[047-150] together. You know it.”

[047-151] “I know that I stand in need of money,” repeated Jehan for the

[047-152] third time.

[047-153] “And what are you going to do with it?”

[047-154] This question caused a flash of hope to gleam before Jehan’s

[047-155] eyes. He resumed his dainty, caressing air.

[047-156] “Stay, dear Brother Claude, I should not come to you, with any

[047-157] evil motive. There is no intention of cutting a dash in the

[047-158] taverns with your unzains, and of strutting about the streets of

[047-159] Paris in a caparison of gold brocade, with a lackey, _cum meo

[047-160] laquasio_. No, brother, ’tis for a good work.”

[047-161] “What good work?” demanded Claude, somewhat surprised.

[047-162] “Two of my friends wish to purchase an outfit for the infant of a

[047-163] poor Haudriette widow. It is a charity. It will cost three

[047-164] florins, and I should like to contribute to it.”

[047-165] “What are names of your two friends?”

[047-166] “Pierre l’Assommeur and Baptiste Croque-Oison.”[37]

[047-167] “Hum,” said the archdeacon; “those are names as fit for a good

[047-168] work as a catapult for the chief altar.”

[047-169] It is certain that Jehan had made a very bad choice of names for

[047-170] his two friends. He realized it too late.

[047-171] “And then,” pursued the sagacious Claude, “what sort of an

[047-172] infant’s outfit is it that is to cost three florins, and that for

[047-173] the child of a Haudriette? Since when have the Haudriette widows

[047-174] taken to having babes in swaddling-clothes?”

[047-175] Jehan broke the ice once more.

[047-176] “Eh, well! yes! I need money in order to go and see Isabeau la

[047-177] Thierrye to-night; in the Val-d’ Amour!”

[047-178] “Impure wretch!” exclaimed the priest.

[047-179] “Ἀναγνεία!” said Jehan.

[047-180] This quotation, which the scholar borrowed with malice,

[047-181] perchance, from the wall of the cell, produced a singular effect

[047-182] on the archdeacon. He bit his lips and his wrath was drowned in a

[047-183] crimson flush.

[047-184] “Begone,” he said to Jehan. “I am expecting some one.”

[047-185] The scholar made one more effort.

[047-186] “Brother Claude, give me at least one little parisis to buy

[047-187] something to eat.”

[047-188] “How far have you gone in the Decretals of Gratian?” demanded Dom

[047-189] Claude.

[047-190] “I have lost my copy books.

[047-191] “Where are you in your Latin humanities?”

[047-192] “My copy of Horace has been stolen.”

[047-193] “Where are you in Aristotle?”

[047-194] “I’ faith! brother what father of the church is it, who says that

[047-195] the errors of heretics have always had for their lurking place

[047-196] the thickets of Aristotle’s metaphysics? A plague on Aristotle! I

[047-197] care not to tear my religion on his metaphysics.”

[047-198] “Young man,” resumed the archdeacon, “at the king’s last entry,

[047-199] there was a young gentleman, named Philippe de Comines, who wore

[047-200] embroidered on the housings of his horse this device, upon which

[047-201] I counsel you to meditate: _Qui non laborat, non manducet_.”

[047-202] The scholar remained silent for a moment, with his finger in his

[047-203] ear, his eyes on the ground, and a discomfited mien.

[047-204] All at once he turned round to Claude with the agile quickness of

[047-205] a wagtail.

[047-206] “So, my good brother, you refuse me a sou parisis, wherewith to

[047-207] buy a crust at a baker’s shop?”

[047-208] “_Qui non laborat, non manducet_.”

[047-209] At this response of the inflexible archdeacon, Jehan hid his head

[047-210] in his hands, like a woman sobbing, and exclaimed with an

[047-211] expression of despair: “Ὀτοτοτοτοτοῖ.”

[047-212] “What is the meaning of this, sir?” demanded Claude, surprised at

[047-213] this freak.

[047-214] “What indeed!” said the scholar; and he lifted to Claude his

[047-215] impudent eyes into which he had just thrust his fists in order to

[047-216] communicate to them the redness of tears; “’tis Greek! ’tis an

[047-217] anapæst of Æschylus which expresses grief perfectly.”

[047-218] And here he burst into a laugh so droll and violent that it made

[047-219] the archdeacon smile. It was Claude’s fault, in fact: why had he

[047-220] so spoiled that child?
