# Saint-Séverin

The Church of Saint-Séverin stands in the heart of the Latin Quarter on
the Left Bank of [[paris|Paris]], one of [[paris|the city]]'s most ancient parish [[religion|church]]es.
Dating from the 13th century and named after a 6th-century hermit, the
[[religion|church]] serves the neighborhood that in Victor Hugo's time — and in the
novel's 1482 setting — was the domain of [[university-of-paris|the University]] of [[paris|Paris]].

## In the Novel's Paris

Saint-Séverin does not appear by name in the primary narrative of
*[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*. However, the [[religion|church]]'s neighborhood — the Latin
Quarter — is vividly characterized. Hugo describes [[university-of-paris|the University]]
quarter as a warren of "tortuous streets" [021-182], a district defined
by its colleges, [[religion|church]]es, and the perpetual presence of scholars. The
Left Bank [[religion|church]]es, including Saint-Séverin, formed the ecclesiastical
infrastructure of this academic world.

The Church of Saint-Séverin, situated on the Rue Saint-Séverin (known
in Hugo's [[paris|medieval Paris]] as one of the quarter's principal
thoroughfares), stood within walking distance of [[university-of-paris|the Sorbonne]] college
and the broader University complex. Its parishioners would have
included scholars, tradespeople, and the varied population of the
Left Bank.

## Architectural Character

Saint-Séverin is a notable example of Flamboyant [[gothic-art|Gothic architecture]].
Its most distinctive feature — a twisted central pillar in the
ambulatory — dates from the late 15th century, contemporaneous with
Hugo's novel. The [[religion|church]]'s [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] exemplifies the "Gothic"
style that Hugo so [[love-and-desire|passion]]ately defended in the novel, contrasting
it with the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] innovations he viewed as a corruption of
France's architectural heritage.

## In the Latin Quarter

The Latin Quarter's character in the novel is one of intellectual and
physical density. Hugo presents [[university-of-paris|the University]] as one of the three
great divisions of [[paris|Paris]] (alongside the City and the Town), each a
"town" in its own right [020-]. Saint-Séverin, as a parish [[religion|church]]
serving this academic community, represents the integration of sacred
and scholarly life that defined the medieval Left Bank.
