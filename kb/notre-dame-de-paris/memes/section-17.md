---[017-001] You have been a child, reader, and you would, perhaps, be very

[017-002] happy to be one still. It is quite certain that you have not,

[017-003] more than once (and for my part, I have passed whole days, the

[017-004] best employed of my life, at it) followed from thicket to

[017-005] thicket, by the side of running water, on a sunny day, a

[017-006] beautiful green or blue dragon-fly, breaking its flight in abrupt

[017-007] angles, and kissing the tips of all the branches. You recollect

[017-008] with what amorous curiosity your thought and your gaze were

[017-009] riveted upon this little whirlwind, hissing and humming with

[017-010] wings of purple and azure, in the midst of which floated an

[017-011] imperceptible body, veiled by the very rapidity of its movement.

[017-012] The aerial being which was dimly outlined amid this quivering of

[017-013] wings, appeared to you chimerical, imaginary, impossible to

[017-014] touch, impossible to see. But when, at length, the dragon-fly

[017-015] alighted on the tip of a reed, and, holding your breath the

[017-016] while, you were able to examine the long, gauze wings, the long

[017-017] enamel robe, the two globes of crystal, what astonishment you

[017-018] felt, and what fear lest you should again behold the form

[017-019] disappear into a shade, and the creature into a chimera! Recall

[017-020] these impressions, and you will readily appreciate what Gringoire

[017-021] felt on contemplating, beneath her visible and palpable form,

[017-022] that Esmeralda of whom, up to that time, he had only caught a

[017-023] glimpse, amidst a whirlwind of dance, song, and tumult.

[017-024] Sinking deeper and deeper into his revery: “So this,” he said to

[017-025] himself, following her vaguely with his eyes, “is _la Esmeralda!_

[017-026] a celestial creature! a street dancer! so much, and so little!

[017-027] ’Twas she who dealt the death-blow to my mystery this morning,

[017-028] ’tis she who saves my life this evening! My evil genius! My good

[017-029] angel! A pretty woman, on my word! and who must needs love me

[017-030] madly to have taken me in that fashion. By the way,” said he,

[017-031] rising suddenly, with that sentiment of the true which formed the

[017-032] foundation of his character and his philosophy, “I don’t know

[017-033] very well how it happens, but I am her husband!”

[017-034] With this idea in his head and in his eyes, he stepped up to the

[017-035] young girl in a manner so military and so gallant that she drew

[017-036] back.

[017-037] “What do you want of me?” said she.

[017-038] “Can you ask me, adorable Esmeralda?” replied Gringoire, with so

[017-039] passionate an accent that he was himself astonished at it on

[017-040] hearing himself speak.

[017-041] The gypsy opened her great eyes. “I don’t know what you mean.”

[017-042] “What!” resumed Gringoire, growing warmer and warmer, and

[017-043] supposing that, after all, he had to deal merely with a virtue of

[017-044] the Cour des Miracles; “am I not thine, sweet friend, art thou

[017-045] not mine?”

[017-046] And, quite ingenuously, he clasped her waist.

[017-047] The gypsy’s corsage slipped through his hands like the skin of an

[017-048] eel. She bounded from one end of the tiny room to the other,

[017-049] stooped down, and raised herself again, with a little poniard in

[017-050] her hand, before Gringoire had even had time to see whence the

[017-051] poniard came; proud and angry, with swelling lips and inflated

[017-052] nostrils, her cheeks as red as an api apple,[15] and her eyes

[017-053] darting lightnings. At the same time, the white goat placed

[017-054] itself in front of her, and presented to Gringoire a hostile

[017-055] front, bristling with two pretty horns, gilded and very sharp.

[017-056] All this took place in the twinkling of an eye.

[017-057] The dragon-fly had turned into a wasp, and asked nothing better

[017-058] than to sting.

[017-059] Our philosopher was speechless, and turned his astonished eyes

[017-060] from the goat to the young girl. “Holy Virgin!” he said at last,

[017-061] when surprise permitted him to speak, “here are two hearty

[017-062] dames!”

[017-063] The gypsy broke the silence on her side.

[017-064] “You must be a very bold knave!”

[017-065] “Pardon, mademoiselle,” said Gringoire, with a smile. “But why

[017-066] did you take me for your husband?”

[017-067] “Should I have allowed you to be hanged?”

[017-068] “So,” said the poet, somewhat disappointed in his amorous hopes.

[017-069] “You had no other idea in marrying me than to save me from the

[017-070] gibbet?”

[017-071] “And what other idea did you suppose that I had?”

[017-072] Gringoire bit his lips. “Come,” said he, “I am not yet so

[017-073] triumphant in Cupido, as I thought. But then, what was the good

[017-074] of breaking that poor jug?”

[017-075] Meanwhile Esmeralda’s dagger and the goat’s horns were still upon

[017-076] the defensive.

[017-077] “Mademoiselle Esmeralda,” said the poet, “let us come to terms. I

[017-078] am not a clerk of the court, and I shall not go to law with you

[017-079] for thus carrying a dagger in Paris, in the teeth of the

[017-080] ordinances and prohibitions of M. the Provost. Nevertheless, you

[017-081] are not ignorant of the fact that Noël Lescrivain was condemned,

[017-082] a week ago, to pay ten Parisian sous, for having carried a

[017-083] cutlass. But this is no affair of mine, and I will come to the

[017-084] point. I swear to you, upon my share of Paradise, not to approach

[017-085] you without your leave and permission, but do give me some

[017-086] supper.”

[017-087] The truth is, Gringoire was, like M. Despreaux, “not very

[017-088] voluptuous.” He did not belong to that chevalier and musketeer

[017-089] species, who take young girls by assault. In the matter of love,

[017-090] as in all other affairs, he willingly assented to temporizing and

[017-091] adjusting terms; and a good supper, and an amiable tête-à-tête

[017-092] appeared to him, especially when he was hungry, an excellent

[017-093] interlude between the prologue and the catastrophe of a love

[017-094] adventure.

[017-095] The gypsy did not reply. She made her disdainful little grimace,

[017-096] drew up her head like a bird, then burst out laughing, and the

[017-097] tiny poniard disappeared as it had come, without Gringoire being

[017-098] able to see where the wasp concealed its sting.

[017-099] A moment later, there stood upon the table a loaf of rye bread, a

[017-100] slice of bacon, some wrinkled apples and a jug of beer. Gringoire

[017-101] began to eat eagerly. One would have said, to hear the furious

[017-102] clashing of his iron fork and his earthenware plate, that all his

[017-103] love had turned to appetite.

[017-104] The young girl seated opposite him, watched him in silence,

[017-105] visibly preoccupied with another thought, at which she smiled

[017-106] from time to time, while her soft hand caressed the intelligent

[017-107] head of the goat, gently pressed between her knees.

[017-108] A candle of yellow wax illuminated this scene of voracity and

[017-109] revery.

[017-110] Meanwhile, the first cravings of his stomach having been stilled,

[017-111] Gringoire felt some false shame at perceiving that nothing

[017-112] remained but one apple.

[017-113] “You do not eat, Mademoiselle Esmeralda?”

[017-114] She replied by a negative sign of the head, and her pensive

[017-115] glance fixed itself upon the vault of the ceiling.

[017-116] “What the deuce is she thinking of?” thought Gringoire, staring

[017-117] at what she was gazing at; “’tis impossible that it can be that

[017-118] stone dwarf carved in the keystone of that arch, which thus

[017-119] absorbs her attention. What the deuce! I can bear the

[017-120] comparison!”

[017-121] He raised his voice, “Mademoiselle!”

[017-122] She seemed not to hear him.

[017-123] He repeated, still more loudly, “Mademoiselle Esmeralda!”

[017-124] Trouble wasted. The young girl’s mind was elsewhere, and

[017-125] Gringoire’s voice had not the power to recall it. Fortunately,

[017-126] the goat interfered. She began to pull her mistress gently by the

[017-127] sleeve.

[017-128] “What dost thou want, Djali?” said the gypsy, hastily, as though

[017-129] suddenly awakened.

[017-130] “She is hungry,” said Gringoire, charmed to enter into

[017-131] conversation. Esmeralda began to crumble some bread, which Djali

[017-132] ate gracefully from the hollow of her hand.

[017-133] Moreover, Gringoire did not give her time to resume her revery.

[017-134] He hazarded a delicate question.

[017-135] “So you don’t want me for your husband?”

[017-136] The young girl looked at him intently, and said, “No.”

[017-137] “For your lover?” went on Gringoire.

[017-138] She pouted, and replied, “No.”

[017-139] “For your friend?” pursued Gringoire.

[017-140] She gazed fixedly at him again, and said, after a momentary

[017-141] reflection, “Perhaps.”

[017-142] This “perhaps,” so dear to philosophers, emboldened Gringoire.

[017-143] “Do you know what friendship is?” he asked.

[017-144] “Yes,” replied the gypsy; “it is to be brother and sister; two

[017-145] souls which touch without mingling, two fingers on one hand.”

[017-146] “And love?” pursued Gringoire.

[017-147] “Oh! love!” said she, and her voice trembled, and her eye beamed.

[017-148] “That is to be two and to be but one. A man and a woman mingled

[017-149] into one angel. It is heaven.”

[017-150] The street dancer had a beauty as she spoke thus, that struck

[017-151] Gringoire singularly, and seemed to him in perfect keeping with

[017-152] the almost oriental exaltation of her words. Her pure, red lips

[017-153] half smiled; her serene and candid brow became troubled, at

[017-154] intervals, under her thoughts, like a mirror under the breath;

[017-155] and from beneath her long, drooping, black eyelashes, there

[017-156] escaped a sort of ineffable light, which gave to her profile that

[017-157] ideal serenity which Raphael found at the mystic point of

[017-158] intersection of virginity, maternity, and divinity.

[017-159] Nevertheless, Gringoire continued,—

[017-160] “What must one be then, in order to please you?”

[017-161] “A man.”

[017-162] “And I—” said he, “what, then, am I?”

[017-163] “A man has a helmet on his head, a sword in his hand, and golden

[017-164] spurs on his heels.”

[017-165] “Good,” said Gringoire, “without a horse, no man. Do you love any

[017-166] one?”

[017-167] “As a lover?—”

[017-168] “Yes.”

[017-169] She remained thoughtful for a moment, then said with a peculiar

[017-170] expression: “That I shall know soon.”

[017-171] “Why not this evening?” resumed the poet tenderly. “Why not me?”

[017-172] She cast a grave glance upon him and said,—

[017-173] “I can never love a man who cannot protect me.”

[017-174] Gringoire colored, and took the hint. It was evident that the

[017-175] young girl was alluding to the slight assistance which he had

[017-176] rendered her in the critical situation in which she had found

[017-177] herself two hours previously. This memory, effaced by his own

[017-178] adventures of the evening, now recurred to him. He smote his

[017-179] brow.

[017-180] “By the way, mademoiselle, I ought to have begun there. Pardon my

[017-181] foolish absence of mind. How did you contrive to escape from the

[017-182] claws of Quasimodo?”

[017-183] This question made the gypsy shudder.

[017-184] “Oh! the horrible hunchback,” said she, hiding her face in her

[017-185] hands. And she shuddered as though with violent cold.

[017-186] “Horrible, in truth,” said Gringoire, who clung to his idea; “but

[017-187] how did you manage to escape him?”

[017-188] La Esmeralda smiled, sighed, and remained silent.

[017-189] “Do you know why he followed you?” began Gringoire again, seeking

[017-190] to return to his question by a circuitous route.

[017-191] “I don’t know,” said the young girl, and she added hastily, “but

[017-192] you were following me also, why were you following me?”

[017-193] “In good faith,” responded Gringoire, “I don’t know either.”

[017-194] Silence ensued. Gringoire slashed the table with his knife. The

[017-195] young girl smiled and seemed to be gazing through the wall at

[017-196] something. All at once she began to sing in a barely articulate

[017-197] voice,—

[017-198] Quando las pintadas aves,

[017-199] Mudas estan, y la tierra—[16]

[017-200] She broke off abruptly, and began to caress Djali.

[017-201] “That’s a pretty animal of yours,” said Gringoire.

[017-202] “She is my sister,” she answered.

[017-203] “Why are you called _la Esmeralda?_” asked the poet.

[017-204] “I do not know.”

[017-205] “But why?”

[017-206] She drew from her bosom a sort of little oblong bag, suspended

[017-207] from her neck by a string of adrézarach beads. This bag exhaled a

[017-208] strong odor of camphor. It was covered with green silk, and bore

[017-209] in its centre a large piece of green glass, in imitation of an

[017-210] emerald.

[017-211] “Perhaps it is because of this,” said she.

[017-212] Gringoire was on the point of taking the bag in his hand. She

[017-213] drew back.

[017-214] “Don’t touch it! It is an amulet. You would injure the charm or

[017-215] the charm would injure you.”

[017-216] The poet’s curiosity was more and more aroused.

[017-217] “Who gave it to you?”

[017-218] She laid one finger on her mouth and concealed the amulet in her

[017-219] bosom. He tried a few more questions, but she hardly replied.

[017-220] “What is the meaning of the words, _la Esmeralda?_”
