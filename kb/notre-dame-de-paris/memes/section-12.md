---[012-001] Each section of this grotesque procession had its own music. The

[012-002] Egyptians made their drums and African tambourines resound. The

[012-003] slang men, not a very musical race, still clung to the goat’s

[012-004] horn trumpet and the Gothic rubebbe of the twelfth century. The

[012-005] Empire of Galilee was not much more advanced; among its music one

[012-006] could hardly distinguish some miserable rebec, from the infancy

[012-007] of the art, still imprisoned in the _ré-la-mi_. But it was around

[012-008] the Pope of the Fools that all the musical riches of the epoch

[012-009] were displayed in a magnificent discord. It was nothing but

[012-010] soprano rebecs, counter-tenor rebecs, and tenor rebecs, not to

[012-011] reckon the flutes and brass instruments. Alas! our readers will

[012-012] remember that this was Gringoire’s orchestra.

[012-013] It is difficult to convey an idea of the degree of proud and

[012-014] blissful expansion to which the sad and hideous visage of

[012-015] Quasimodo had attained during the transit from the Palais de

[012-016] Justice, to the Place de Grève. It was the first enjoyment of

[012-017] self-love that he had ever experienced. Down to that day, he had

[012-018] known only humiliation, disdain for his condition, disgust for

[012-019] his person. Hence, deaf though he was, he enjoyed, like a

[012-020] veritable pope, the acclamations of that throng, which he hated

[012-021] because he felt that he was hated by it. What mattered it that

[012-022] his people consisted of a pack of fools, cripples, thieves, and

[012-023] beggars? it was still a people and he was its sovereign. And he

[012-024] accepted seriously all this ironical applause, all this derisive

[012-025] respect, with which the crowd mingled, it must be admitted, a

[012-026] good deal of very real fear. For the hunchback was robust; for

[012-027] the bandy-legged fellow was agile; for the deaf man was

[012-028] malicious: three qualities which temper ridicule.

[012-029] We are far from believing, however, that the new Pope of the

[012-030] Fools understood both the sentiments which he felt and the

[012-031] sentiments which he inspired. The spirit which was lodged in this

[012-032] failure of a body had, necessarily, something incomplete and deaf

[012-033] about it. Thus, what he felt at the moment was to him, absolutely

[012-034] vague, indistinct, and confused. Only joy made itself felt, only

[012-035] pride dominated. Around that sombre and unhappy face, there hung

[012-036] a radiance.

[012-037] It was, then, not without surprise and alarm, that at the very

[012-038] moment when Quasimodo was passing the Pillar House, in that

[012-039] semi-intoxicated state, a man was seen to dart from the crowd,

[012-040] and to tear from his hands, with a gesture of anger, his crosier

[012-041] of gilded wood, the emblem of his mock popeship.

[012-042] This man, this rash individual, was the man with the bald brow,

[012-043] who, a moment earlier, standing with the gypsy’s group had

[012-044] chilled the poor girl with his words of menace and of hatred. He

[012-045] was dressed in an ecclesiastical costume. At the moment when he

[012-046] stood forth from the crowd, Gringoire, who had not noticed him up

[012-047] to that time, recognized him: “Hold!” he said, with an

[012-048] exclamation of astonishment. “Eh! ’tis my master in Hermes, Dom

[012-049] Claude Frollo, the archdeacon! What the devil does he want of

[012-050] that old one-eyed fellow? He’ll get himself devoured!”

[012-051] A cry of terror arose, in fact. The formidable Quasimodo had

[012-052] hurled himself from the litter, and the women turned aside their

[012-053] eyes in order not to see him tear the archdeacon asunder.

[012-054] He made one bound as far as the priest, looked at him, and fell

[012-055] upon his knees.

[012-056] The priest tore off his tiara, broke his crozier, and rent his

[012-057] tinsel cope.

[012-058] Quasimodo remained on his knees, with head bent and hands

[012-059] clasped. Then there was established between them a strange

[012-060] dialogue of signs and gestures, for neither of them spoke. The

[012-061] priest, erect on his feet, irritated, threatening, imperious;

[012-062] Quasimodo, prostrate, humble, suppliant. And, nevertheless, it is

[012-063] certain that Quasimodo could have crushed the priest with his

[012-064] thumb.

[012-065] At length the archdeacon, giving Quasimodo’s powerful shoulder a

[012-066] rough shake, made him a sign to rise and follow him.

[012-067] Quasimodo rose.

[012-068] Then the Brotherhood of Fools, their first stupor having passed

[012-069] off, wished to defend their pope, so abruptly dethroned. The

[012-070] Egyptians, the men of slang, and all the fraternity of law

[012-071] clerks, gathered howling round the priest.

[012-072] Quasimodo placed himself in front of the priest, set in play the

[012-073] muscles of his athletic fists, and glared upon the assailants

[012-074] with the snarl of an angry tiger.

[012-075] The priest resumed his sombre gravity, made a sign to Quasimodo,

[012-076] and retired in silence.

[012-077] Quasimodo walked in front of him, scattering the crowd as he

[012-078] passed.

[012-079] When they had traversed the populace and the Place, the cloud of

[012-080] curious and idle were minded to follow them. Quasimodo then

[012-081] constituted himself the rearguard, and followed the archdeacon,

[012-082] walking backwards, squat, surly, monstrous, bristling, gathering

[012-083] up his limbs, licking his boar’s tusks, growling like a wild

[012-084] beast, and imparting to the crowd immense vibrations, with a look

[012-085] or a gesture.

[012-086] Both were allowed to plunge into a dark and narrow street, where

[012-087] no one dared to venture after them; so thoroughly did the mere

[012-088] chimera of Quasimodo gnashing his teeth bar the entrance.

[012-089] “Here’s a marvellous thing,” said Gringoire; “but where the deuce

[012-090] shall I find some supper?”

[012-091] CHAPTER IV. THE INCONVENIENCES OF FOLLOWING A PRETTY WOMAN

[012-092] THROUGH THE STREETS IN THE EVENING.

[012-093] Gringoire set out to follow the gypsy at all hazards. He had seen

[012-094] her, accompanied by her goat, take to the Rue de la Coutellerie;

[012-095] he took the Rue de la Coutellerie.

[012-096] “Why not?” he said to himself.

[012-097] Gringoire, a practical philosopher of the streets of Paris, had

[012-098] noticed that nothing is more propitious to revery than following

[012-099] a pretty woman without knowing whither she is going. There was in

[012-100] this voluntary abdication of his freewill, in this fancy

[012-101] submitting itself to another fancy, which suspects it not, a

[012-102] mixture of fantastic independence and blind obedience, something

[012-103] indescribable, intermediate between slavery and liberty, which

[012-104] pleased Gringoire,—a spirit essentially compound, undecided, and

[012-105] complex, holding the extremities of all extremes, incessantly

[012-106] suspended between all human propensities, and neutralizing one by

[012-107] the other. He was fond of comparing himself to Mahomet’s coffin,

[012-108] attracted in two different directions by two loadstones, and

[012-109] hesitating eternally between the heights and the depths, between

[012-110] the vault and the pavement, between fall and ascent, between

[012-111] zenith and nadir.

[012-112] If Gringoire had lived in our day, what a fine middle course he

[012-113] would hold between classicism and romanticism!

[012-114] But he was not sufficiently primitive to live three hundred

[012-115] years, and ’tis a pity. His absence is a void which is but too

[012-116] sensibly felt to-day.

[012-117] Moreover, for the purpose of thus following passers-by (and

[012-118] especially female passers-by) in the streets, which Gringoire was

[012-119] fond of doing, there is no better disposition than ignorance of

[012-120] where one is going to sleep.

[012-121] So he walked along, very thoughtfully, behind the young girl, who

[012-122] hastened her pace and made her goat trot as she saw the

[012-123] _bourgeois_ returning home and the taverns—the only shops which

[012-124] had been open that day—closing.

[012-125] “After all,” he half thought to himself, “she must lodge

[012-126] somewhere; gypsies have kindly hearts. Who knows?—”

[012-127] And in the points of suspense which he placed after this

[012-128] reticence in his mind, there lay I know not what flattering

[012-129] ideas.

[012-130] Meanwhile, from time to time, as he passed the last groups of

[012-131] _bourgeois_ closing their doors, he caught some scraps of their

[012-132] conversation, which broke the thread of his pleasant hypotheses.

[012-133] Now it was two old men accosting each other.

[012-134] “Do you know that it is cold, Master Thibaut Fernicle?”

[012-135] (Gringoire had been aware of this since the beginning of the

[012-136] winter.)

[012-137] “Yes, indeed, Master Boniface Disome! Are we going to have a

[012-138] winter such as we had three years ago, in ’80, when wood cost

[012-139] eight sous the measure?”

[012-140] “Bah! that’s nothing, Master Thibaut, compared with the winter of

[012-141] 1407, when it froze from St. Martin’s Day until Candlemas! and so

[012-142] cold that the pen of the registrar of the parliament froze every

[012-143] three words, in the Grand Chamber! which interrupted the

[012-144] registration of justice.”

[012-145] Further on there were two female neighbors at their windows,

[012-146] holding candles, which the fog caused to sputter.

[012-147] “Has your husband told you about the mishap, Mademoiselle la

[012-148] Boudraque?”

[012-149] “No. What is it, Mademoiselle Turquant?”

[012-150] “The horse of M. Gilles Godin, the notary at the Châtelet, took

[012-151] fright at the Flemings and their procession, and overturned

[012-152] Master Philippe Avrillot, lay monk of the Célestins.”

[012-153] “Really?”

[012-154] “Actually.”

[012-155] “A _bourgeois_ horse! ’tis rather too much! If it had been a

[012-156] cavalry horse, well and good!”

[012-157] And the windows were closed. But Gringoire had lost the thread of

[012-158] his ideas, nevertheless.

[012-159] Fortunately, he speedily found it again, and he knotted it

[012-160] together without difficulty, thanks to the gypsy, thanks to

[012-161] Djali, who still walked in front of him; two fine, delicate, and

[012-162] charming creatures, whose tiny feet, beautiful forms, and

[012-163] graceful manners he was engaged in admiring, almost confusing

[012-164] them in his contemplation; believing them to be both young girls,

[012-165] from their intelligence and good friendship; regarding them both

[012-166] as goats,—so far as the lightness, agility, and dexterity of

[012-167] their walk were concerned.

[012-168] But the streets were becoming blacker and more deserted every

[012-169] moment. The curfew had sounded long ago, and it was only at rare

[012-170] intervals now that they encountered a passer-by in the street, or

[012-171] a light in the windows. Gringoire had become involved, in his

[012-172] pursuit of the gypsy, in that inextricable labyrinth of alleys,

[012-173] squares, and closed courts which surround the ancient sepulchre

[012-174] of the Saints-Innocents, and which resembles a ball of thread

[012-175] tangled by a cat. “Here are streets which possess but little

[012-176] logic!” said Gringoire, lost in the thousands of circuits which

[012-177] returned upon themselves incessantly, but where the young girl

[012-178] pursued a road which seemed familiar to her, without hesitation

[012-179] and with a step which became ever more rapid. As for him, he

[012-180] would have been utterly ignorant of his situation had he not

[012-181] espied, in passing, at the turn of a street, the octagonal mass

[012-182] of the pillory of the fish markets, the open-work summit of which

[012-183] threw its black, fretted outlines clearly upon a window which was

[012-184] still lighted in the Rue Verdelet.

[012-185] The young girl’s attention had been attracted to him for the last

[012-186] few moments; she had repeatedly turned her head towards him with

[012-187] uneasiness; she had even once come to a standstill, and taking

[012-188] advantage of a ray of light which escaped from a half-open bakery

[012-189] to survey him intently, from head to foot, then, having cast this

[012-190] glance, Gringoire had seen her make that little pout which he had

[012-191] already noticed, after which she passed on.

[012-192] This little pout had furnished Gringoire with food for thought.

[012-193] There was certainly both disdain and mockery in that graceful

[012-194] grimace. So he dropped his head, began to count the

[012-195] paving-stones, and to follow the young girl at a little greater

[012-196] distance, when, at the turn of a street, which had caused him to

[012-197] lose sight of her, he heard her utter a piercing cry.

[012-198] He hastened his steps.

[012-199] The street was full of shadows. Nevertheless, a twist of tow

[012-200] soaked in oil, which burned in a cage at the feet of the Holy

[012-201] Virgin at the street corner, permitted Gringoire to make out the

[012-202] gypsy struggling in the arms of two men, who were endeavoring to

[012-203] stifle her cries. The poor little goat, in great alarm, lowered

[012-204] his horns and bleated.

[012-205] “Help! gentlemen of the watch!” shouted Gringoire, and advanced

[012-206] bravely. One of the men who held the young girl turned towards

[012-207] him. It was the formidable visage of Quasimodo.

[012-208] Gringoire did not take to flight, but neither did he advance

[012-209] another step.

[012-210] Quasimodo came up to him, tossed him four paces away on the

[012-211] pavement with a backward turn of the hand, and plunged rapidly

[012-212] into the gloom, bearing the young girl folded across one arm like

[012-213] a silken scarf. His companion followed him, and the poor goat ran

[012-214] after them all, bleating plaintively.
