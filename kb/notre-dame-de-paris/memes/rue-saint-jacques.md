# Rue Saint-Jacques

The Rue Saint-Jacques was the great arterial street of the left bank of [[paris|medieval Paris]], the spine of the university quarter, running south from the Petit Pont toward the southern gate of [[paris|the city]]. In *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, Hugo names it among the key streets that structured the medieval city and celebrates the [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] of the [[religion|church]]es that lined it.

## Overview

Hugo identifies the Rue Saint-Jacques as the left-bank counterpart to the [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]]: "it was called the Rue Saint-Jacques in the" ((020-181)) university quarter. Just as the [[rue-saint-denis|Rue Saint-Denis]] organized the commercial right bank, the Rue Saint-Jacques organized the scholarly left bank, connecting the bridges of the Seine to the colleges, [[religion|church]]es, and ultimately the southern countryside.

## In the Novel

### Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie

The most prominent landmark on the street was the [[religion|church]] of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie, whose bell tower Hugo celebrates: "of which the Church of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie was" ((019-127)). The [[religion|church]] appears among the great Romanesque architectural monuments of [[paris|Paris]]: "Saint-Jacques de la" ((019-132)) Boucherie, part of the architectural "triad" that included [[saint-germain-des-pres|Saint-Germain-des-Prés]].

Hugo devotes particular attention to the [[religion|church]]'s tower, describing "the rich square bell tower of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie, with its..." ((022-157)). This tower, which survives to the present day as the Tour Saint-Jacques, was one of the most admired structures in [[paris|medieval Paris]]. The [[religion|church]] itself was noted for its early adoption of pointed architectural features: "Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie had barely been..." ((023-115)) completed when Hugo's narrative unfolds.

### The Gate and the Faubourg

The Porte Saint-Jacques was one of the six gates of [[paris|medieval Paris]]: "the Porte Saint-Jacques, the..." ((020-158)) southern gate. Beyond it lay the Faubourg Saint-Jacques, described as having "beautiful carved crosses" ((021-209)). The [[religion|church]] of "Saint-Jacques du Haut-Pas, which was then..." ((021-210)) stood in this faubourg, a landmark on the southern approach to [[paris|the city]].

### The Pilgrimage Route

The street's name reflects its historical role as the [[paris|Paris]]ian starting point of the Via Turonensis, the pilgrimage route to Santiago de Compostela. Pilgrims departing [[paris|Paris]] for the shrine of Saint James in Spain would gather on this street, which took its name from the apostle.

## Historical Context

The Rue Saint-Jacques was the Roman cardo maximus of Lutetia, the main north-south axis of the Roman city. In the medieval period, it became the central artery of the Latin Quarter, lined with colleges of [[university-of-paris|the University]] of [[paris|Paris]]. [[sorbonne-college|The Sorbonne]] stood nearby, and the street was home to booksellers, scholars, and printers. Hugo's detailed [[knowledge]] of the street's [[religion|church]]es reflects his immersion in the architectural history of [[paris|medieval Paris]].
