---[028-001] That is, at least, what was supposed, whether rightly or not. It

[028-002] is certain that the archdeacon often visited the cemetery of the

[028-003] Saints-Innocents, where, it is true, his father and mother had

[028-004] been buried, with other victims of the plague of 1466; but that

[028-005] he appeared far less devout before the cross of their grave than

[028-006] before the strange figures with which the tomb of Nicolas Flamel

[028-007] and Claude Pernelle, erected just beside it, was loaded.

[028-008] It is certain that he had frequently been seen to pass along the

[028-009] Rue des Lombards, and furtively enter a little house which formed

[028-010] the corner of the Rue des Ecrivans and the Rue Marivault. It was

[028-011] the house which Nicolas Flamel had built, where he had died about

[028-012] 1417, and which, constantly deserted since that time, had already

[028-013] begun to fall in ruins,—so greatly had the hermetics and the

[028-014] alchemists of all countries wasted away the walls, merely by

[028-015] carving their names upon them. Some neighbors even affirm that

[028-016] they had once seen, through an air-hole, Archdeacon Claude

[028-017] excavating, turning over, digging up the earth in the two

[028-018] cellars, whose supports had been daubed with numberless couplets

[028-019] and hieroglyphics by Nicolas Flamel himself. It was supposed that

[028-020] Flamel had buried the philosopher’s stone in the cellar; and the

[028-021] alchemists, for the space of two centuries, from Magistri to

[028-022] Father Pacifique, never ceased to worry the soil until the house,

[028-023] so cruelly ransacked and turned over, ended by falling into dust

[028-024] beneath their feet.

[028-025] Again, it is certain that the archdeacon had been seized with a

[028-026] singular passion for the symbolical door of Notre-Dame, that page

[028-027] of a conjuring book written in stone, by Bishop Guillaume de

[028-028] Paris, who has, no doubt, been damned for having affixed so

[028-029] infernal a frontispiece to the sacred poem chanted by the rest of

[028-030] the edifice. Archdeacon Claude had the credit also of having

[028-031] fathomed the mystery of the colossus of Saint Christopher, and of

[028-032] that lofty, enigmatical statue which then stood at the entrance

[028-033] of the vestibule, and which the people, in derision, called

[028-034] “Monsieur Legris.” But, what every one might have noticed was the

[028-035] interminable hours which he often employed, seated upon the

[028-036] parapet of the area in front of the church, in contemplating the

[028-037] sculptures of the front; examining now the foolish virgins with

[028-038] their lamps reversed, now the wise virgins with their lamps

[028-039] upright; again, calculating the angle of vision of that raven

[028-040] which belongs to the left front, and which is looking at a

[028-041] mysterious point inside the church, where is concealed the

[028-042] philosopher’s stone, if it be not in the cellar of Nicolas

[028-043] Flamel.

[028-044] It was, let us remark in passing, a singular fate for the Church

[028-045] of Notre-Dame at that epoch to be so beloved, in two different

[028-046] degrees, and with so much devotion, by two beings so dissimilar

[028-047] as Claude and Quasimodo. Beloved by one, a sort of instinctive

[028-048] and savage half-man, for its beauty, for its stature, for the

[028-049] harmonies which emanated from its magnificent ensemble; beloved

[028-050] by the other, a learned and passionate imagination, for its myth,

[028-051] for the sense which it contains, for the symbolism scattered

[028-052] beneath the sculptures of its front,—like the first text

[028-053] underneath the second in a palimpsest,—in a word, for the enigma

[028-054] which it is eternally propounding to the understanding.

[028-055] Furthermore, it is certain that the archdeacon had established

[028-056] himself in that one of the two towers which looks upon the Grève,

[028-057] just beside the frame for the bells, a very secret little cell,

[028-058] into which no one, not even the bishop, entered without his

[028-059] leave, it was said. This tiny cell had formerly been made almost

[028-060] at the summit of the tower, among the ravens’ nests, by Bishop

[028-061] Hugo de Besançon[26] who had wrought sorcery there in his day.

[028-062] What that cell contained, no one knew; but from the strand of the

[028-063] Terrain, at night, there was often seen to appear, disappear, and

[028-064] reappear at brief and regular intervals, at a little dormer

[028-065] window opening upon the back of the tower, a certain red,

[028-066] intermittent, singular light which seemed to follow the panting

[028-067] breaths of a bellows, and to proceed from a flame, rather than

[028-068] from a light. In the darkness, at that height, it produced a

[028-069] singular effect; and the goodwives said: “There’s the archdeacon

[028-070] blowing! hell is sparkling up yonder!”

[028-071] There were no great proofs of sorcery in that, after all, but

[028-072] there was still enough smoke to warrant a surmise of fire, and

[028-073] the archdeacon bore a tolerably formidable reputation. We ought

[028-074] to mention however, that the sciences of Egypt, that necromancy

[028-075] and magic, even the whitest, even the most innocent, had no more

[028-076] envenomed enemy, no more pitiless denunciator before the

[028-077] gentlemen of the officialty of Notre-Dame. Whether this was

[028-078] sincere horror, or the game played by the thief who shouts, “stop

[028-079] thief!” at all events, it did not prevent the archdeacon from

[028-080] being considered by the learned heads of the chapter, as a soul

[028-081] who had ventured into the vestibule of hell, who was lost in the

[028-082] caves of the cabal, groping amid the shadows of the occult

[028-083] sciences. Neither were the people deceived thereby; with any one

[028-084] who possessed any sagacity, Quasimodo passed for the demon;

[028-085] Claude Frollo, for the sorcerer. It was evident that the

[028-086] bellringer was to serve the archdeacon for a given time, at the

[028-087] end of which he would carry away the latter’s soul, by way of

[028-088] payment. Thus the archdeacon, in spite of the excessive austerity

[028-089] of his life, was in bad odor among all pious souls; and there was

[028-090] no devout nose so inexperienced that it could not smell him out

[028-091] to be a magician.

[028-092] And if, as he grew older, abysses had formed in his science, they

[028-093] had also formed in his heart. That at least, is what one had

[028-094] grounds for believing on scrutinizing that face upon which the

[028-095] soul was only seen to shine through a sombre cloud. Whence that

[028-096] large, bald brow? that head forever bent? that breast always

[028-097] heaving with sighs? What secret thought caused his mouth to smile

[028-098] with so much bitterness, at the same moment that his scowling

[028-099] brows approached each other like two bulls on the point of

[028-100] fighting? Why was what hair he had left already gray? What was

[028-101] that internal fire which sometimes broke forth in his glance, to

[028-102] such a degree that his eye resembled a hole pierced in the wall

[028-103] of a furnace?

[028-104] These symptoms of a violent moral preoccupation, had acquired an

[028-105] especially high degree of intensity at the epoch when this story

[028-106] takes place. More than once a choir-boy had fled in terror at

[028-107] finding him alone in the church, so strange and dazzling was his

[028-108] look. More than once, in the choir, at the hour of the offices,

[028-109] his neighbor in the stalls had heard him mingle with the plain

[028-110] song, _ad omnem tonum_, unintelligible parentheses. More than

[028-111] once the laundress of the Terrain charged “with washing the

[028-112] chapter” had observed, not without affright, the marks of nails

[028-113] and clenched fingers on the surplice of monsieur the archdeacon

[028-114] of Josas.

[028-115] However, he redoubled his severity, and had never been more

[028-116] exemplary. By profession as well as by character, he had always

[028-117] held himself aloof from women; he seemed to hate them more than

[028-118] ever. The mere rustling of a silken petticoat caused his hood to

[028-119] fall over his eyes. Upon this score he was so jealous of

[028-120] austerity and reserve, that when the Dame de Beaujeu, the king’s

[028-121] daughter, came to visit the cloister of Notre-Dame, in the month

[028-122] of December, 1481, he gravely opposed her entrance, reminding the

[028-123] bishop of the statute of the Black Book, dating from the vigil of

[028-124] Saint-Barthélemy, 1334, which interdicts access to the cloister

[028-125] to “any woman whatever, old or young, mistress or maid.” Upon

[028-126] which the bishop had been constrained to recite to him the

[028-127] ordinance of Legate Odo, which excepts certain great dames,

[028-128] _aliquæ magnates mulieres, quæ sine scandalo vitari non possunt_.

[028-129] And again the archdeacon had protested, objecting that the

[028-130] ordinance of the legate, which dated back to 1207, was anterior

[028-131] by a hundred and twenty-seven years to the Black Book, and

[028-132] consequently was abrogated in fact by it. And he had refused to

[028-133] appear before the princess.

[028-134] It was also noticed that his horror for Bohemian women and

[028-135] gypsies had seemed to redouble for some time past. He had

[028-136] petitioned the bishop for an edict which expressly forbade the

[028-137] Bohemian women to come and dance and beat their tambourines on

[028-138] the place of the Parvis; and for about the same length of time,

[028-139] he had been ransacking the mouldy placards of the officialty, in

[028-140] order to collect the cases of sorcerers and witches condemned to

[028-141] fire or the rope, for complicity in crimes with rams, sows, or

[028-142] goats.

[028-143] CHAPTER VI. UNPOPULARITY.

[028-144] The archdeacon and the bellringer, as we have already said, were

[028-145] but little loved by the populace great and small, in the vicinity

[028-146] of the cathedral. When Claude and Quasimodo went out together,

[028-147] which frequently happened, and when they were seen traversing in

[028-148] company, the valet behind the master, the cold, narrow, and

[028-149] gloomy streets of the block of Notre-Dame, more than one evil

[028-150] word, more than one ironical quaver, more than one insulting jest

[028-151] greeted them on their way, unless Claude Frollo, which was rarely

[028-152] the case, walked with head upright and raised, showing his severe

[028-153] and almost august brow to the dumbfounded jeerers.

[028-154] Both were in their quarter like “the poets” of whom Régnier

[028-155] speaks,—

[028-156] “All sorts of persons run after poets,

[028-157] As warblers fly shrieking after owls.”

[028-158] Sometimes a mischievous child risked his skin and bones for the

[028-159] ineffable pleasure of driving a pin into Quasimodo’s hump. Again,

[028-160] a young girl, more bold and saucy than was fitting, brushed the

[028-161] priest’s black robe, singing in his face the sardonic ditty,

[028-162] “_niche, niche_, the devil is caught.” Sometimes a group of

[028-163] squalid old crones, squatting in a file under the shadow of the

[028-164] steps to a porch, scolded noisily as the archdeacon and the

[028-165] bellringer passed, and tossed them this encouraging welcome, with

[028-166] a curse: “Hum! there’s a fellow whose soul is made like the other

[028-167] one’s body!” Or a band of schoolboys and street urchins, playing

[028-168] hop-scotch, rose in a body and saluted him classically, with some

[028-169] cry in Latin: “_Eia! eia! Claudius cum claudo_!”

[028-170] But the insult generally passed unnoticed both by the priest and

[028-171] the bellringer. Quasimodo was too deaf to hear all these gracious

[028-172] things, and Claude was too dreamy.

[028-173] BOOK FIFTH.

[028-174] CHAPTER I. _ABBAS BEATI MARTINI_.

[028-175] Dom Claude’s fame had spread far and wide. It procured for him,

[028-176] at about the epoch when he refused to see Madame de Beaujeu, a

[028-177] visit which he long remembered.

[028-178] It was in the evening. He had just retired, after the office, to

[028-179] his canon’s cell in the cloister of Notre-Dame. This cell, with

[028-180] the exception, possibly, of some glass phials, relegated to a

[028-181] corner, and filled with a decidedly equivocal powder, which

[028-182] strongly resembled the alchemist’s “powder of projection,”

[028-183] presented nothing strange or mysterious. There were, indeed, here

[028-184] and there, some inscriptions on the walls, but they were pure

[028-185] sentences of learning and piety, extracted from good authors. The

[028-186] archdeacon had just seated himself, by the light of a

[028-187] three-jetted copper lamp, before a vast coffer crammed with

[028-188] manuscripts. He had rested his elbow upon the open volume of

[028-189] Honorius d’Autun, _De predestinatione et libero arbitrio_, and he

[028-190] was turning over, in deep meditation, the leaves of a printed

[028-191] folio which he had just brought, the sole product of the press

[028-192] which his cell contained. In the midst of his revery there came a

[028-193] knock at his door. “Who’s there?” cried the learned man, in the

[028-194] gracious tone of a famished dog, disturbed over his bone.

[028-195] A voice without replied, “Your friend, Jacques Coictier.” He went

[028-196] to open the door.
