---[074-001] Without doubt, this strange beacon light would awaken far away,

[074-002] the woodcutter of the hills of Bicêtre, terrified to behold the

[074-003] gigantic shadow of the towers of Notre-Dame quivering over his

[074-004] heaths.

[074-005] A terrified silence ensued among the outcasts, during which

[074-006] nothing was heard, but the cries of alarm of the canons shut up

[074-007] in their cloister, and more uneasy than horses in a burning

[074-008] stable, the furtive sound of windows hastily opened and still

[074-009] more hastily closed, the internal hurly-burly of the houses and

[074-010] of the Hôtel-Dieu, the wind in the flame, the last death-rattle

[074-011] of the dying, and the continued crackling of the rain of lead

[074-012] upon the pavement.

[074-013] In the meanwhile, the principal vagabonds had retired beneath the

[074-014] porch of the Gondelaurier mansion, and were holding a council of

[074-015] war.

[074-016] The Duke of Egypt, seated on a stone post, contemplated the

[074-017] phantasmagorical bonfire, glowing at a height of two hundred feet

[074-018] in the air, with religious terror. Clopin Trouillefou bit his

[074-019] huge fists with rage.

[074-020] “Impossible to get in!” he muttered between his teeth.

[074-021] “An old, enchanted church!” grumbled the aged Bohemian, Mathias

[074-022] Hungadi Spicali.

[074-023] “By the Pope’s whiskers!” went on a sham soldier, who had once

[074-024] been in service, “here are church gutters spitting melted lead at

[074-025] you better than the machicolations of Lectoure.”

[074-026] “Do you see that demon passing and repassing in front of the

[074-027] fire?” exclaimed the Duke of Egypt.

[074-028] “Pardieu, ’tis that damned bellringer, ’tis Quasimodo,” said

[074-029] Clopin.

[074-030] The Bohemian tossed his head. “I tell you, that ’tis the spirit

[074-031] Sabnac, the grand marquis, the demon of fortifications. He has

[074-032] the form of an armed soldier, the head of a lion. Sometimes he

[074-033] rides a hideous horse. He changes men into stones, of which he

[074-034] builds towers. He commands fifty legions ’Tis he indeed; I

[074-035] recognize him. Sometimes he is clad in a handsome golden robe,

[074-036] figured after the Turkish fashion.”

[074-037] “Where is Bellevigne de l’Étoile?” demanded Clopin.

[074-038] “He is dead.”

[074-039] Andry the Red laughed in an idiotic way: “Notre-Dame is making

[074-040] work for the hospital,” said he.

[074-041] “Is there, then, no way of forcing this door,” exclaimed the King

[074-042] of Thunes, stamping his foot.

[074-043] The Duke of Egypt pointed sadly to the two streams of boiling

[074-044] lead which did not cease to streak the black façade, like two

[074-045] long distaffs of phosphorus.

[074-046] “Churches have been known to defend themselves thus all by

[074-047] themselves,” he remarked with a sigh. “Saint-Sophia at

[074-048] Constantinople, forty years ago, hurled to the earth three times

[074-049] in succession, the crescent of Mahom, by shaking her domes, which

[074-050] are her heads. Guillaume de Paris, who built this one was a

[074-051] magician.”

[074-052] “Must we then retreat in pitiful fashion, like highwaymen?” said

[074-053] Clopin. “Must we leave our sister here, whom those hooded wolves

[074-054] will hang to-morrow.”

[074-055] “And the sacristy, where there are wagon-loads of gold!” added a

[074-056] vagabond, whose name, we regret to say, we do not know.

[074-057] “Beard of Mahom!” cried Trouillefou.

[074-058] “Let us make another trial,” resumed the vagabond.

[074-059] Mathias Hungadi shook his head.

[074-060] “We shall never get in by the door. We must find the defect in

[074-061] the armor of the old fairy; a hole, a false postern, some joint

[074-062] or other.”

[074-063] “Who will go with me?” said Clopin. “I shall go at it again. By

[074-064] the way, where is the little scholar Jehan, who is so encased in

[074-065] iron?”

[074-066] “He is dead, no doubt,” some one replied; “we no longer hear his

[074-067] laugh.”

[074-068] The King of Thunes frowned: “So much the worse. There was a brave

[074-069] heart under that ironmongery. And Master Pierre Gringoire?”

[074-070] “Captain Clopin,” said Andry the Red, “he slipped away before we

[074-071] reached the Pont-aux-Changeurs.”

[074-072] Clopin stamped his foot. “_Gueule-Dieu!_ ’twas he who pushed us

[074-073] on hither, and he has deserted us in the very middle of the job!

[074-074] Cowardly chatterer, with a slipper for a helmet!”

[074-075] “Captain Clopin,” said Andry the Red, who was gazing down Rue du

[074-076] Parvis, “yonder is the little scholar.”

[074-077] “Praised be Pluto!” said Clopin. “But what the devil is he

[074-078] dragging after him?”

[074-079] It was, in fact, Jehan, who was running as fast as his heavy

[074-080] outfit of a Paladin, and a long ladder which trailed on the

[074-081] pavement, would permit, more breathless than an ant harnessed to

[074-082] a blade of grass twenty times longer than itself.

[074-083] “Victory! _Te Deum!_” cried the scholar. “Here is the ladder of

[074-084] the longshoremen of Port Saint-Landry.”

[074-085] Clopin approached him.

[074-086] “Child, what do you mean to do, _corne-dieu!_ with this ladder?”

[074-087] “I have it,” replied Jehan, panting. “I knew where it was under

[074-088] the shed of the lieutenant’s house. There’s a wench there whom I

[074-089] know, who thinks me as handsome as Cupido. I made use of her to

[074-090] get the ladder, and I have the ladder, _Pasque-Mahom!_ The poor

[074-091] girl came to open the door to me in her shift.”

[074-092] “Yes,” said Clopin, “but what are you going to do with that

[074-093] ladder?”

[074-094] Jehan gazed at him with a malicious, knowing look, and cracked

[074-095] his fingers like castanets. At that moment he was sublime. On his

[074-096] head he wore one of those overloaded helmets of the fifteenth

[074-097] century, which frightened the enemy with their fanciful crests.

[074-098] His bristled with ten iron beaks, so that Jehan could have

[074-099] disputed with Nestor’s Homeric vessel the redoubtable title of

[074-100] δεκέμβολος.

[074-101] “What do I mean to do with it, august king of Thunes? Do you see

[074-102] that row of statues which have such idiotic expressions, yonder,

[074-103] above the three portals?”

[074-104] “Yes. Well?”

[074-105] “’Tis the gallery of the kings of France.”

[074-106] “What is that to me?” said Clopin.

[074-107] “Wait! At the end of that gallery there is a door which is never

[074-108] fastened otherwise than with a latch, and with this ladder I

[074-109] ascend, and I am in the church.”

[074-110] “Child let me be the first to ascend.”

[074-111] “No, comrade, the ladder is mine. Come, you shall be the second.”

[074-112] “May Beelzebub strangle you!” said surly Clopin, “I won’t be

[074-113] second to anybody.”

[074-114] “Then find a ladder, Clopin!”

[074-115] Jehan set out on a run across the Place, dragging his ladder and

[074-116] shouting: “Follow me, lads!”

[074-117] In an instant the ladder was raised, and propped against the

[074-118] balustrade of the lower gallery, above one of the lateral doors.

[074-119] The throng of vagabonds, uttering loud acclamations, crowded to

[074-120] its foot to ascend. But Jehan maintained his right, and was the

[074-121] first to set foot on the rungs. The passage was tolerably long.

[074-122] The gallery of the kings of France is to-day about sixty feet

[074-123] above the pavement. The eleven steps of the flight before the

[074-124] door, made it still higher. Jehan mounted slowly, a good deal

[074-125] incommoded by his heavy armor, holding his crossbow in one hand,

[074-126] and clinging to a rung with the other. When he reached the middle

[074-127] of the ladder, he cast a melancholy glance at the poor dead

[074-128] outcasts, with which the steps were strewn. “Alas!” said he,

[074-129] “here is a heap of bodies worthy of the fifth book of the Iliad!”

[074-130] Then he continued his ascent. The vagabonds followed him. There

[074-131] was one on every rung. At the sight of this line of cuirassed

[074-132] backs, undulating as they rose through the gloom, one would have

[074-133] pronounced it a serpent with steel scales, which was raising

[074-134] itself erect in front of the church. Jehan who formed the head,

[074-135] and who was whistling, completed the illusion.

[074-136] The scholar finally reached the balcony of the gallery, and

[074-137] climbed over it nimbly, to the applause of the whole vagabond

[074-138] tribe. Thus master of the citadel, he uttered a shout of joy, and

[074-139] suddenly halted, petrified. He had just caught sight of Quasimodo

[074-140] concealed in the dark, with flashing eye, behind one of the

[074-141] statues of the kings.

[074-142] Before a second assailant could gain a foothold on the gallery,

[074-143] the formidable hunchback leaped to the head of the ladder,

[074-144] without uttering a word, seized the ends of the two uprights with

[074-145] his powerful hands, raised them, pushed them out from the wall,

[074-146] balanced the long and pliant ladder, loaded with vagabonds from

[074-147] top to bottom for a moment, in the midst of shrieks of anguish,

[074-148] then suddenly, with superhuman force, hurled this cluster of men

[074-149] backward into the Place. There was a moment when even the most

[074-150] resolute trembled. The ladder, launched backwards, remained erect

[074-151] and standing for an instant, and seemed to hesitate, then

[074-152] wavered, then suddenly, describing a frightful arc of a circle

[074-153] eighty feet in radius, crashed upon the pavement with its load of

[074-154] ruffians, more rapidly than a drawbridge when its chains break.

[074-155] There arose an immense imprecation, then all was still, and a few

[074-156] mutilated wretches were seen, crawling over the heap of dead.

[074-157] A sound of wrath and grief followed the first cries of triumph

[074-158] among the besiegers. Quasimodo, impassive, with both elbows

[074-159] propped on the balustrade, looked on. He had the air of an old,

[074-160] bushy-headed king at his window.

[074-161] As for Jehan Frollo, he was in a critical position. He found

[074-162] himself in the gallery with the formidable bellringer, alone,

[074-163] separated from his companions by a vertical wall eighty feet

[074-164] high. While Quasimodo was dealing with the ladder, the scholar

[074-165] had run to the postern which he believed to be open. It was not.

[074-166] The deaf man had closed it behind him when he entered the

[074-167] gallery. Jehan had then concealed himself behind a stone king,

[074-168] not daring to breathe, and fixing upon the monstrous hunchback a

[074-169] frightened gaze, like the man, who, when courting the wife of the

[074-170] guardian of a menagerie, went one evening to a love rendezvous,

[074-171] mistook the wall which he was to climb, and suddenly found

[074-172] himself face to face with a white bear.

[074-173] For the first few moments, the deaf man paid no heed to him; but

[074-174] at last he turned his head, and suddenly straightened up. He had

[074-175] just caught sight of the scholar.

[074-176] Jehan prepared himself for a rough shock, but the deaf man

[074-177] remained motionless; only he had turned towards the scholar and

[074-178] was looking at him.

[074-179] “Ho ho!” said Jehan, “what do you mean by staring at me with that

[074-180] solitary and melancholy eye?”

[074-181] As he spoke thus, the young scamp stealthily adjusted his

[074-182] crossbow.

[074-183] “Quasimodo!” he cried, “I am going to change your surname: you

[074-184] shall be called the blind man.”

[074-185] The shot sped. The feathered vireton[62] whizzed and entered the

[074-186] hunchback’s left arm. Quasimodo appeared no more moved by it than

[074-187] by a scratch to King Pharamond. He laid his hand on the arrow,

[074-188] tore it from his arm, and tranquilly broke it across his big

[074-189] knee; then he let the two pieces drop on the floor, rather than

[074-190] threw them down. But Jehan had no opportunity to fire a second

[074-191] time. The arrow broken, Quasimodo breathing heavily, bounded like

[074-192] a grasshopper, and he fell upon the scholar, whose armor was

[074-193] flattened against the wall by the blow.

[074-194] Then in that gloom, wherein wavered the light of the torches, a

[074-195] terrible thing was seen.

[074-196] Quasimodo had grasped with his left hand the two arms of Jehan,

[074-197] who did not offer any resistance, so thoroughly did he feel that

[074-198] he was lost. With his right hand, the deaf man detached one by

[074-199] one, in silence, with sinister slowness, all the pieces of his

[074-200] armor, the sword, the daggers, the helmet, the cuirass, the leg

[074-201] pieces. One would have said that it was a monkey taking the shell

[074-202] from a nut. Quasimodo flung the scholar’s iron shell at his feet,

[074-203] piece by piece. When the scholar beheld himself disarmed,

[074-204] stripped, weak, and naked in those terrible hands, he made no

[074-205] attempt to speak to the deaf man, but began to laugh audaciously

[074-206] in his face, and to sing with his intrepid heedlessness of a

[074-207] child of sixteen, the then popular ditty:—

[074-208] “Elle est bien habillée,

[074-209] La ville de Cambrai;

[074-210] Marafin l’a pillée....”[63]

[074-211] He did not finish. Quasimodo was seen on the parapet of the

[074-212] gallery, holding the scholar by the feet with one hand and

[074-213] whirling him over the abyss like a sling; then a sound like that

[074-214] of a bony structure in contact with a wall was heard, and

[074-215] something was seen to fall which halted a third of the way down

[074-216] in its fall, on a projection in the architecture. It was a dead

[074-217] body which remained hanging there, bent double, its loins broken,

[074-218] its skull empty.
