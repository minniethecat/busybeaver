---[068-001] The gypsy, who had advanced to the threshold of her cell, beheld

[068-002] with surprise their roles abruptly changed. It was now the priest

[068-003] who menaced, Quasimodo who was the suppliant.

[068-004] The priest, who was overwhelming the deaf man with gestures of

[068-005] wrath and reproach, made the latter a violent sign to retire.

[068-006] The deaf man dropped his head, then he came and knelt at the

[068-007] gypsy’s door,—“Monseigneur,” he said, in a grave and resigned

[068-008] voice, “you shall do all that you please afterwards, but kill me

[068-009] first.”

[068-010] So saying, he presented his knife to the priest. The priest,

[068-011] beside himself, was about to seize it. But the young girl was

[068-012] quicker than he; she wrenched the knife from Quasimodo’s hands

[068-013] and burst into a frantic laugh,—“Approach,” she said to the

[068-014] priest.

[068-015] She held the blade high. The priest remained undecided.

[068-016] She would certainly have struck him.

[068-017] Then she added with a pitiless expression, well aware that she

[068-018] was about to pierce the priest’s heart with thousands of red-hot

[068-019] irons,—

[068-020] “Ah! I know that Phœbus is not dead!”

[068-021] The priest overturned Quasimodo on the floor with a kick, and,

[068-022] quivering with rage, darted back under the vault of the

[068-023] staircase.

[068-024] When he was gone, Quasimodo picked up the whistle which had just

[068-025] saved the gypsy.

[068-026] “It was getting rusty,” he said, as he handed it back to her;

[068-027] then he left her alone.

[068-028] The young girl, deeply agitated by this violent scene, fell back

[068-029] exhausted on her bed, and began to sob and weep. Her horizon was

[068-030] becoming gloomy once more.

[068-031] The priest had groped his way back to his cell.

[068-032] It was settled. Dom Claude was jealous of Quasimodo!

[068-033] He repeated with a thoughtful air his fatal words: “No one shall

[068-034] have her.”

[068-035] BOOK TENTH.

[068-036] CHAPTER I. GRINGOIRE HAS MANY GOOD IDEAS IN SUCCESSION.—RUE DES

[068-037] BERNARDINS.

[068-038] As soon as Pierre Gringoire had seen how this whole affair was

[068-039] turning, and that there would decidedly be the rope, hanging, and

[068-040] other disagreeable things for the principal personages in this

[068-041] comedy, he had not cared to identify himself with the matter

[068-042] further. The outcasts with whom he had remained, reflecting that,

[068-043] after all, it was the best company in Paris,—the outcasts had

[068-044] continued to interest themselves in behalf of the gypsy. He had

[068-045] thought it very simple on the part of people who had, like

[068-046] herself, nothing else in prospect but Charmolue and Torterue, and

[068-047] who, unlike himself, did not gallop through the regions of

[068-048] imagination between the wings of Pegasus. From their remarks, he

[068-049] had learned that his wife of the broken crock had taken refuge in

[068-050] Notre-Dame, and he was very glad of it. But he felt no temptation

[068-051] to go and see her there. He meditated occasionally on the little

[068-052] goat, and that was all. Moreover, he was busy executing feats of

[068-053] strength during the day for his living, and at night he was

[068-054] engaged in composing a memorial against the Bishop of Paris, for

[068-055] he remembered having been drenched by the wheels of his mills,

[068-056] and he cherished a grudge against him for it. He also occupied

[068-057] himself with annotating the fine work of Baudry-le-Rouge, Bishop

[068-058] of Noyon and Tournay, _De Cupa Petrarum_, which had given him a

[068-059] violent passion for architecture, an inclination which had

[068-060] replaced in his heart his passion for hermeticism, of which it

[068-061] was, moreover, only a natural corollary, since there is an

[068-062] intimate relation between hermeticism and masonry. Gringoire had

[068-063] passed from the love of an idea to the love of the form of that

[068-064] idea.

[068-065] One day he had halted near Saint Germain-l’Auxerrois, at the

[068-066] corner of a mansion called “For-l’Évêque” (the Bishop’s

[068-067] Tribunal), which stood opposite another called “For-le-Roi” (the

[068-068] King’s Tribunal). At this For-l’Évêque, there was a charming

[068-069] chapel of the fourteenth century, whose apse was on the street.

[068-070] Gringoire was devoutly examining its exterior sculptures. He was

[068-071] in one of those moments of egotistical, exclusive, supreme,

[068-072] enjoyment when the artist beholds nothing in the world but art,

[068-073] and the world in art. All at once he feels a hand laid gravely on

[068-074] his shoulder. He turns round. It was his old friend, his former

[068-075] master, monsieur the archdeacon.

[068-076] He was stupefied. It was a long time since he had seen the

[068-077] archdeacon, and Dom Claude was one of those solemn and

[068-078] impassioned men, a meeting with whom always upsets the

[068-079] equilibrium of a sceptical philosopher.

[068-080] The archdeacon maintained silence for several minutes, during

[068-081] which Gringoire had time to observe him. He found Dom Claude

[068-082] greatly changed; pale as a winter’s morning, with hollow eyes,

[068-083] and hair almost white. The priest broke the silence at length, by

[068-084] saying, in a tranquil but glacial tone,—

[068-085] “How do you do, Master Pierre?”

[068-086] “My health?” replied Gringoire. “Eh! eh! one can say both one

[068-087] thing and another on that score. Still, it is good, on the whole.

[068-088] I take not too much of anything. You know, master, that the

[068-089] secret of keeping well, according to Hippocrates; _id est: cibi,

[068-090] potus, somni, venus, omnia moderata sint_.”

[068-091] “So you have no care, Master Pierre?” resumed the archdeacon,

[068-092] gazing intently at Gringoire.

[068-093] “None, i’ faith!”

[068-094] “And what are you doing now?”

[068-095] “You see, master. I am examining the chiselling of these stones,

[068-096] and the manner in which yonder bas-relief is thrown out.”

[068-097] The priest began to smile with that bitter smile which raises

[068-098] only one corner of the mouth.

[068-099] “And that amuses you?”

[068-100] “’Tis paradise!” exclaimed Gringoire. And leaning over the

[068-101] sculptures with the fascinated air of a demonstrator of living

[068-102] phenomena: “Do you not think, for instance, that yon

[068-103] metamorphosis in bas-relief is executed with much adroitness,

[068-104] delicacy and patience? Observe that slender column. Around what

[068-105] capital have you seen foliage more tender and better caressed by

[068-106] the chisel. Here are three raised bosses of Jean Maillevin. They

[068-107] are not the finest works of this great master. Nevertheless, the

[068-108] naïvete, the sweetness of the faces, the gayety of the attitudes

[068-109] and draperies, and that inexplicable charm which is mingled with

[068-110] all the defects, render the little figures very diverting and

[068-111] delicate, perchance, even too much so. You think that it is not

[068-112] diverting?”

[068-113] “Yes, certainly!” said the priest.

[068-114] “And if you were to see the interior of the chapel!” resumed the

[068-115] poet, with his garrulous enthusiasm. “Carvings everywhere. ’Tis

[068-116] as thickly clustered as the head of a cabbage! The apse is of a

[068-117] very devout, and so peculiar a fashion that I have never beheld

[068-118] anything like it elsewhere!”

[068-119] Dom Claude interrupted him,—

[068-120] “You are happy, then?”

[068-121] Gringoire replied warmly;—

[068-122] “On my honor, yes! First I loved women, then animals. Now I love

[068-123] stones. They are quite as amusing as women and animals, and less

[068-124] treacherous.”

[068-125] The priest laid his hand on his brow. It was his habitual

[068-126] gesture.

[068-127] “Really?”

[068-128] “Stay!” said Gringoire, “one has one’s pleasures!” He took the

[068-129] arm of the priest, who let him have his way, and made him enter

[068-130] the staircase turret of For-l’Évêque. “Here is a staircase! every

[068-131] time that I see it I am happy. It is of the simplest and rarest

[068-132] manner of steps in Paris. All the steps are bevelled underneath.

[068-133] Its beauty and simplicity consist in the interspacing of both,

[068-134] being a foot or more wide, which are interlaced, interlocked,

[068-135] fitted together, enchained enchased, interlined one upon another,

[068-136] and bite into each other in a manner that is truly firm and

[068-137] graceful.”

[068-138] “And you desire nothing?”

[068-139] “No.”

[068-140] “And you regret nothing?”

[068-141] “Neither regret nor desire. I have arranged my mode of life.”

[068-142] “What men arrange,” said Claude, “things disarrange.”

[068-143] “I am a Pyrrhonian philosopher,” replied Gringoire, “and I hold

[068-144] all things in equilibrium.”

[068-145] “And how do you earn your living?”

[068-146] “I still make epics and tragedies now and then; but that which

[068-147] brings me in most is the industry with which you are acquainted,

[068-148] master; carrying pyramids of chairs in my teeth.”

[068-149] “The trade is but a rough one for a philosopher.”

[068-150] “’Tis still equilibrium,” said Gringoire. “When one has an idea,

[068-151] one encounters it in everything.”

[068-152] “I know that,” replied the archdeacon.

[068-153] After a silence, the priest resumed,—

[068-154] “You are, nevertheless, tolerably poor?”

[068-155] “Poor, yes; unhappy, no.”

[068-156] At that moment, a trampling of horses was heard, and our two

[068-157] interlocutors beheld defiling at the end of the street, a company

[068-158] of the king’s unattached archers, their lances borne high, an

[068-159] officer at their head. The cavalcade was brilliant, and its march

[068-160] resounded on the pavement.

[068-161] “How you gaze at that officer!” said Gringoire, to the

[068-162] archdeacon.

[068-163] “Because I think I recognize him.”

[068-164] “What do you call him?”

[068-165] “I think,” said Claude, “that his name is Phœbus de Châteaupers.”

[068-166] “Phœbus! A curious name! There is also a Phœbus, Comte de Foix. I

[068-167] remember having known a wench who swore only by the name of

[068-168] Phœbus.”

[068-169] “Come away from here,” said the priest. “I have something to say

[068-170] to you.”

[068-171] From the moment of that troop’s passing, some agitation had

[068-172] pierced through the archdeacon’s glacial envelope. He walked on.

[068-173] Gringoire followed him, being accustomed to obey him, like all

[068-174] who had once approached that man so full of ascendency. They

[068-175] reached in silence the Rue des Bernardins, which was nearly

[068-176] deserted. Here Dom Claude paused.

[068-177] “What have you to say to me, master?” Gringoire asked him.

[068-178] “Do you not think that the dress of those cavaliers whom we have

[068-179] just seen is far handsomer than yours and mine?”

[068-180] Gringoire tossed his head.

[068-181] “I’ faith! I love better my red and yellow jerkin, than those

[068-182] scales of iron and steel. A fine pleasure to produce, when you

[068-183] walk, the same noise as the Quay of Old Iron, in an earthquake!”

[068-184] “So, Gringoire, you have never cherished envy for those handsome

[068-185] fellows in their military doublets?”

[068-186] “Envy for what, monsieur the archdeacon? their strength, their

[068-187] armor, their discipline? Better philosophy and independence in

[068-188] rags. I prefer to be the head of a fly rather than the tail of a

[068-189] lion.”

[068-190] “That is singular,” said the priest dreamily. “Yet a handsome

[068-191] uniform is a beautiful thing.”

[068-192] Gringoire, perceiving that he was in a pensive mood, quitted him

[068-193] to go and admire the porch of a neighboring house. He came back

[068-194] clapping his hands.

[068-195] “If you were less engrossed with the fine clothes of men of war,

[068-196] monsieur the archdeacon, I would entreat you to come and see this

[068-197] door. I have always said that the house of the Sieur Aubry had

[068-198] the most superb entrance in the world.”

[068-199] “Pierre Gringoire,” said the archdeacon, “What have you done with

[068-200] that little gypsy dancer?”

[068-201] “La Esmeralda? You change the conversation very abruptly.”

[068-202] “Was she not your wife?”

[068-203] “Yes, by virtue of a broken crock. We were to have four years of

[068-204] it. By the way,” added Gringoire, looking at the archdeacon in a

[068-205] half bantering way, “are you still thinking of her?”

[068-206] “And you think of her no longer?”

[068-207] “Very little. I have so many things. Good heavens, how pretty

[068-208] that little goat was!”

[068-209] “Had she not saved your life?”

[068-210] “’Tis true, pardieu!”

[068-211] “Well, what has become of her? What have you done with her?”

[068-212] “I cannot tell you. I believe that they have hanged her.”

[068-213] “You believe so?”

[068-214] “I am not sure. When I saw that they wanted to hang people, I

[068-215] retired from the game.”

[068-216] “That is all you know of it?”

[068-217] “Wait a bit. I was told that she had taken refuge in Notre-Dame,

[068-218] and that she was safe there, and I am delighted to hear it, and I

[068-219] have not been able to discover whether the goat was saved with

[068-220] her, and that is all I know.”

[068-221] “I will tell you more,” cried Dom Claude; and his voice, hitherto

[068-222] low, slow, and almost indistinct, turned to thunder. “She has in

[068-223] fact, taken refuge in Notre-Dame. But in three days justice will

[068-224] reclaim her, and she will be hanged on the Grève. There is a

[068-225] decree of parliament.”

[068-226] “That’s annoying,” said Gringoire.

[068-227] The priest, in an instant, became cold and calm again.
