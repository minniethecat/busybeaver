# Gilles Lecornu

Gilles Lecornu, master furrier of the king's robes, is a figure of the
prosperous [[paris|Paris]]ian bourgeoisie in the opening tableau of the novel. He
appears in the crowded great hall of the [[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]], where his very
presence — respectable, well-dressed, self-satisfied — provokes the mockery of
the unruly students. Hugo uses Lecornu to sketch the social friction between
the scholar class and the merchant class, a minor thread in the vast tapestry
of the novel's opening [002-171].

## Role in the Novel

Lecornu is among the anonymous hundreds who pack the [[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] for the
mystery play and the election of the [[quasimodo|Pope of Fools]]. His role is entirely
circumstantial: he is jeered at by [[jehan-frollo|Jehan Frollo]] and his fellow scholars
because his name — literally "the horned" or "cuckold" — is an irresistible
target for wordplay [002-175]. Hugo uses him to populate the background of
a scene that establishes the novel's social universe: nobles, clergy,
scholars, merchants, and vagabonds all jostling in the same hall.

## Biography

### Social Position

As master furrier of the king's robes, Gilles Lecornu occupies a position of
considerable prosperity and respectability. The furrier's trade was among the
most lucrative in [[paris|medieval Paris]], and a royal appointment placed him near the
apex of the artisan-merchant hierarchy [002-171]. His title "Sire" marks him
as a man of substance, a recognized member of the bourgeois elite [003-131].

### Key Appearance

The scholars' mockery of Lecornu is one of many crowd episodes that Hugo uses
to paint the atmosphere of the great hall. [[jehan-frollo|Jehan Frollo]], spotting the furrier
in the crowd, leads a chorus of puns on his name: "Cornutus et hirsutus" —
horned and hairy — a Latin scholastic joke at the expense of bourgeois dignity
[002-175]. The episode is brief but telling: it establishes [[university-of-paris|the scholars]] as a
class of educated idlers whose wit is exercised at the expense of the
productive citizenry, and it places Lecornu among the crowd that will witness
the day's bizarre proceedings.

### Fate

Lecornu's [[fatalite|fate]] is not narrated; he disappears after the opening book. He is a
spear-carrier in Hugo's crowded cast, present to add texture to the social
canvas rather than to advance the plot.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Gilles Lecornu is drawn with the broad strokes of a caricature: prosperous,
dignified, and easily offended. His name is his defining characteristic — Hugo
delights in the Rabelaisian comedy of a man whose very appellation invites
mockery. Yet there is a certain pathos in his position: he has earned his
place through honest labor, only to be mocked by the leisured sons of the
bourgeoisie whom [[university-of-paris|the University]] has taught just enough Latin to be cruel
[002-178].

### Relationships

- **[[jehan-frollo]]**: The younger Frollo brother leads the mockery of
  Lecornu, weaponizing his education against a man of commerce. The episode
  establishes Jehan's character as a wastrel scholar whose [[knowledge|learning]] serves
  only to sharpen his insolence.

## Significance

Lecornu is a minor figure whose function is atmospheric and sociological. He
represents the class of prosperous merchants who, in the late fifteenth
century, were beginning to rival the nobility in wealth and influence —
a class that Hugo, the son of a Napoleonic general and the chronicler of
a world in transition, understood intimately. His brief appearance is a
reminder that the novel's [[paris|medieval Paris]] is not only a stage for its central
drama but a living city of ordinary people going about their business while
history happens around them.
