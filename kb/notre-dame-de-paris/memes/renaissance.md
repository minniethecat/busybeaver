# Renaissance and Humanism

The Renaissance, in Hugo's *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, is not yet fully arrived — the novel is set in 1482, on the threshold of the modern era. But the forces of transformation are already stirring: the [[printing-press|printing]] press is disseminating [[knowledge]] beyond the Church's control, and [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]]'s stone book is giving way to the [[printing-press|book of paper]] ((032-002)).

## Definition

Hugo defines the Renaissance not as a rebirth of classical antiquity but as the moment when [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] ceased to be humanity's primary medium of collective expression. The novel's famous chapter "This Will Kill That" presents the [[printing-press|printing]] press as the agent of this transformation — "the book of stone, so solid and so durable, was to give way to the [[printing-press|book of paper]], more solid and more durable still" ((032-002)). The Renaissance is thus not a stylistic period but a revolution in the means of transmitting thought, and with it, power ((032-010)).

## Hugo's Treatment

### In the Narrative

The Renaissance is personified in the conflict between [[claude-frollo|Claude Frollo]] and the [[printing-press|printed book]] on his table. Frollo, pointing first at [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]] and then at a printed volume, utters the novel's most pregnant phrase: "This will kill that" ((032-002)). The archdeacon is a Renaissance figure in spite of himself — his alchemical studies, his [[alchemy|hermetic]] texts, his rejection of conventional piety all mark him as a man of the new age. Yet he is also the old age's defender, and his tragedy is that he cannot embrace the transformation he perceives.

### In the Author's Voice

Hugo presents the Renaissance as an irreversible shift in human consciousness. The "zone of the Renaissance, which we would gladly call the Greco-Roman zone" appears architecturally in Notre-Dame, but its true significance is intellectual: it marks the end of the Church's monopoly on meaning ((019-217)). Hugo catalogs the architectural evidence of this transition — the Renaissance layer superimposed on the Gothic cathedral like a palimpsest of competing worldviews ((019-217)). The [[printing-press|printing]] press democratizes thought in a way that stone [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] never could, and this, for Hugo, is the Renaissance's true revolution.

## Key Passages

## External References

Hugo's "This Will Kill That" is one of the earliest literary treatments of the media revolution thesis — the idea that changes in communication technology transform social and intellectual structures. The actual Renaissance reached [[paris|Paris]] in the early 16th century (Francis I's reign), slightly after the novel's 1482 setting.
