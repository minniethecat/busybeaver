---[019-001] And who put the cold, white panes in the place of those windows,

[019-002] “high in color,” which caused the astonished eyes of our fathers

[019-003] to hesitate between the rose of the grand portal and the arches

[019-004] of the apse? And what would a sub-chanter of the sixteenth

[019-005] century say, on beholding the beautiful yellow wash, with which

[019-006] our archiepiscopal vandals have desmeared their cathedral? He

[019-007] would remember that it was the color with which the hangman

[019-008] smeared “accursed” edifices; he would recall the Hôtel du

[019-009] Petit-Bourbon, all smeared thus, on account of the constable’s

[019-010] treason. “Yellow, after all, of so good a quality,” said Sauval,

[019-011] “and so well recommended, that more than a century has not yet

[019-012] caused it to lose its color.” He would think that the sacred

[019-013] place had become infamous, and would flee.

[019-014] And if we ascend the cathedral, without mentioning a thousand

[019-015] barbarisms of every sort,—what has become of that charming little

[019-016] bell tower, which rested upon the point of intersection of the

[019-017] cross-roofs, and which, no less frail and no less bold than its

[019-018] neighbor (also destroyed), the spire of the Sainte-Chapelle,

[019-019] buried itself in the sky, farther forward than the towers,

[019-020] slender, pointed, sonorous, carved in open work. An architect of

[019-021] good taste amputated it (1787), and considered it sufficient to

[019-022] mask the wound with that large, leaden plaster, which resembles a

[019-023] pot cover.

[019-024] ’Tis thus that the marvellous art of the Middle Ages has been

[019-025] treated in nearly every country, especially in France. One can

[019-026] distinguish on its ruins three sorts of lesions, all three of

[019-027] which cut into it at different depths; first, time, which has

[019-028] insensibly notched its surface here and there, and gnawed it

[019-029] everywhere; next, political and religious revolution, which,

[019-030] blind and wrathful by nature, have flung themselves tumultuously

[019-031] upon it, torn its rich garment of carving and sculpture, burst

[019-032] its rose windows, broken its necklace of arabesques and tiny

[019-033] figures, torn out its statues, sometimes because of their mitres,

[019-034] sometimes because of their crowns; lastly, fashions, even more

[019-035] grotesque and foolish, which, since the anarchical and splendid

[019-036] deviations of the Renaissance, have followed each other in the

[019-037] necessary decadence of architecture. Fashions have wrought more

[019-038] harm than revolutions. They have cut to the quick; they have

[019-039] attacked the very bone and framework of art; they have cut,

[019-040] slashed, disorganized, killed the edifice, in form as in the

[019-041] symbol, in its consistency as well as in its beauty. And then

[019-042] they have made it over; a presumption of which neither time nor

[019-043] revolutions at least have been guilty. They have audaciously

[019-044] adjusted, in the name of “good taste,” upon the wounds of gothic

[019-045] architecture, their miserable gewgaws of a day, their ribbons of

[019-046] marble, their pompons of metal, a veritable leprosy of egg-shaped

[019-047] ornaments, volutes, whorls, draperies, garlands, fringes, stone

[019-048] flames, bronze clouds, pudgy cupids, chubby-cheeked cherubim,

[019-049] which begin to devour the face of art in the oratory of Catherine

[019-050] de Medicis, and cause it to expire, two centuries later, tortured

[019-051] and grimacing, in the boudoir of the Dubarry.

[019-052] Thus, to sum up the points which we have just indicated, three

[019-053] sorts of ravages to-day disfigure Gothic architecture. Wrinkles

[019-054] and warts on the epidermis; this is the work of time. Deeds of

[019-055] violence, brutalities, contusions, fractures; this is the work of

[019-056] the revolutions from Luther to Mirabeau. Mutilations,

[019-057] amputations, dislocation of the joints, _restorations_; this is

[019-058] the Greek, Roman, and barbarian work of professors according to

[019-059] Vitruvius and Vignole. This magnificent art produced by the

[019-060] Vandals has been slain by the academies. The centuries, the

[019-061] revolutions, which at least devastate with impartiality and

[019-062] grandeur, have been joined by a cloud of school architects,

[019-063] licensed, sworn, and bound by oath; defacing with the discernment

[019-064] and choice of bad taste, substituting the _chicorées_ of Louis

[019-065] XV. for the Gothic lace, for the greater glory of the Parthenon.

[019-066] It is the kick of the ass at the dying lion. It is the old oak

[019-067] crowning itself, and which, to heap the measure full, is stung,

[019-068] bitten, and gnawed by caterpillars.

[019-069] How far it is from the epoch when Robert Cenalis, comparing

[019-070] Notre-Dame de Paris to the famous temple of Diana at Ephesus, _so

[019-071] much lauded by the ancient pagans_, which Erostatus _has_

[019-072] immortalized, found the Gallic temple “more excellent in length,

[019-073] breadth, height, and structure.”[19]

[019-074] Notre-Dame is not, moreover, what can be called a complete,

[019-075] definite, classified monument. It is no longer a Romanesque

[019-076] church; nor is it a Gothic church. This edifice is not a type.

[019-077] Notre-Dame de Paris has not, like the Abbey of Tournus, the grave

[019-078] and massive frame, the large and round vault, the glacial

[019-079] bareness, the majestic simplicity of the edifices which have the

[019-080] rounded arch for their progenitor. It is not, like the Cathedral

[019-081] of Bourges, the magnificent, light, multiform, tufted, bristling

[019-082] efflorescent product of the pointed arch. Impossible to class it

[019-083] in that ancient family of sombre, mysterious churches, low and

[019-084] crushed as it were by the round arch, almost Egyptian, with the

[019-085] exception of the ceiling; all hieroglyphics, all sacerdotal, all

[019-086] symbolical, more loaded in their ornaments, with lozenges and

[019-087] zigzags, than with flowers, with flowers than with animals, with

[019-088] animals than with men; the work of the architect less than of the

[019-089] bishop; first transformation of art, all impressed with

[019-090] theocratic and military discipline, taking root in the Lower

[019-091] Empire, and stopping with the time of William the Conqueror.

[019-092] Impossible to place our Cathedral in that other family of lofty,

[019-093] aerial churches, rich in painted windows and sculpture; pointed

[019-094] in form, bold in attitude; communal and _bourgeois_ as political

[019-095] symbols; free, capricious, lawless, as a work of art; second

[019-096] transformation of architecture, no longer hieroglyphic, immovable

[019-097] and sacerdotal, but artistic, progressive, and popular, which

[019-098] begins at the return from the crusades, and ends with Louis IX.

[019-099] Notre-Dame de Paris is not of pure Romanesque, like the first;

[019-100] nor of pure Arabian race, like the second.

[019-101] It is an edifice of the transition period. The Saxon architect

[019-102] completed the erection of the first pillars of the nave, when the

[019-103] pointed arch, which dates from the Crusade, arrived and placed

[019-104] itself as a conqueror upon the large Romanesque capitals which

[019-105] should support only round arches. The pointed arch, mistress

[019-106] since that time, constructed the rest of the church.

[019-107] Nevertheless, timid and inexperienced at the start, it sweeps

[019-108] out, grows larger, restrains itself, and dares no longer dart

[019-109] upwards in spires and lancet windows, as it did later on, in so

[019-110] many marvellous cathedrals. One would say that it were conscious

[019-111] of the vicinity of the heavy Romanesque pillars.

[019-112] However, these edifices of the transition from the Romanesque to

[019-113] the Gothic, are no less precious for study than the pure types.

[019-114] They express a shade of the art which would be lost without them.

[019-115] It is the graft of the pointed upon the round arch.

[019-116] Notre-Dame de Paris is, in particular, a curious specimen of this

[019-117] variety. Each face, each stone of the venerable monument, is a

[019-118] page not only of the history of the country, but of the history

[019-119] of science and art as well. Thus, in order to indicate here only

[019-120] the principal details, while the little Red Door almost attains

[019-121] to the limits of the Gothic delicacy of the fifteenth century,

[019-122] the pillars of the nave, by their size and weight, go back to the

[019-123] Carlovingian Abbey of Saint-Germain-des-Prés. One would suppose

[019-124] that six centuries separated these pillars from that door. There

[019-125] is no one, not even the hermetics, who does not find in the

[019-126] symbols of the grand portal a satisfactory compendium of their

[019-127] science, of which the Church of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie was

[019-128] so complete a hieroglyph. Thus, the Roman abbey, the

[019-129] philosophers’ church, the Gothic art, Saxon art, the heavy, round

[019-130] pillar, which recalls Gregory VII., the hermetic symbolism, with

[019-131] which Nicolas Flamel played the prelude to Luther, papal unity,

[019-132] schism, Saint-Germain des Prés, Saint-Jacques de la

[019-133] Boucherie,—all are mingled, combined, amalgamated in Notre-Dame.

[019-134] This central mother church is, among the ancient churches of

[019-135] Paris, a sort of chimera; it has the head of one, the limbs of

[019-136] another, the haunches of another, something of all.

[019-137] We repeat it, these hybrid constructions are not the least

[019-138] interesting for the artist, for the antiquarian, for the

[019-139] historian. They make one feel to what a degree architecture is a

[019-140] primitive thing, by demonstrating (what is also demonstrated by

[019-141] the cyclopean vestiges, the pyramids of Egypt, the gigantic

[019-142] Hindoo pagodas) that the greatest products of architecture are

[019-143] less the works of individuals than of society; rather the

[019-144] offspring of a nation’s effort, than the inspired flash of a man

[019-145] of genius; the deposit left by a whole people; the heaps

[019-146] accumulated by centuries; the residue of successive evaporations

[019-147] of human society,—in a word, species of formations. Each wave of

[019-148] time contributes its alluvium, each race deposits its layer on

[019-149] the monument, each individual brings his stone. Thus do the

[019-150] beavers, thus do the bees, thus do men. The great symbol of

[019-151] architecture, Babel, is a hive.

[019-152] Great edifices, like great mountains, are the work of centuries.

[019-153] Art often undergoes a transformation while they are pending,

[019-154] _pendent opera interrupta_; they proceed quietly in accordance

[019-155] with the transformed art. The new art takes the monument where it

[019-156] finds it, incrusts itself there, assimilates it to itself,

[019-157] develops it according to its fancy, and finishes it if it can.

[019-158] The thing is accomplished without trouble, without effort,

[019-159] without reaction,—following a natural and tranquil law. It is a

[019-160] graft which shoots up, a sap which circulates, a vegetation which

[019-161] starts forth anew. Certainly there is matter here for many large

[019-162] volumes, and often the universal history of humanity in the

[019-163] successive engrafting of many arts at many levels, upon the same

[019-164] monument. The man, the artist, the individual, is effaced in

[019-165] these great masses, which lack the name of their author; human

[019-166] intelligence is there summed up and totalized. Time is the

[019-167] architect, the nation is the builder.

[019-168] Not to consider here anything except the Christian architecture

[019-169] of Europe, that younger sister of the great masonries of the

[019-170] Orient, it appears to the eyes as an immense formation divided

[019-171] into three well-defined zones, which are superposed, the one upon

[019-172] the other: the Romanesque zone[20], the Gothic zone, the zone of

[019-173] the Renaissance, which we would gladly call the Greco-Roman zone.

[019-174] The Roman layer, which is the most ancient and deepest, is

[019-175] occupied by the round arch, which reappears, supported by the

[019-176] Greek column, in the modern and upper layer of the Renaissance.

[019-177] The pointed arch is found between the two. The edifices which

[019-178] belong exclusively to any one of these three layers are perfectly

[019-179] distinct, uniform, and complete. There is the Abbey of Jumiéges,

[019-180] there is the Cathedral of Reims, there is the Sainte-Croix of

[019-181] Orléans. But the three zones mingle and amalgamate along the

[019-182] edges, like the colors in the solar spectrum. Hence, complex

[019-183] monuments, edifices of gradation and transition. One is Roman at

[019-184] the base, Gothic in the middle, Greco-Roman at the top. It is

[019-185] because it was six hundred years in building. This variety is

[019-186] rare. The donjon keep of d’Étampes is a specimen of it. But

[019-187] monuments of two formations are more frequent. There is

[019-188] Notre-Dame de Paris, a pointed-arch edifice, which is imbedded by

[019-189] its pillars in that Roman zone, in which are plunged the portal

[019-190] of Saint-Denis, and the nave of Saint-Germain des Prés. There is

[019-191] the charming, half-Gothic chapter-house of Bocherville, where the

[019-192] Roman layer extends half way up. There is the cathedral of Rouen,

[019-193] which would be entirely Gothic if it did not bathe the tip of its

[019-194] central spire in the zone of the Renaissance.[21]

[019-195] However, all these shades, all these differences, do not affect

[019-196] the surfaces of edifices only. It is art which has changed its

[019-197] skin. The very constitution of the Christian church is not

[019-198] attacked by it. There is always the same internal woodwork, the

[019-199] same logical arrangement of parts. Whatever may be the carved and

[019-200] embroidered envelope of a cathedral, one always finds beneath

[019-201] it—in the state of a germ, and of a rudiment at the least—the

[019-202] Roman basilica. It is eternally developed upon the soil according

[019-203] to the same law. There are, invariably, two naves, which

[019-204] intersect in a cross, and whose upper portion, rounded into an

[019-205] apse, forms the choir; there are always the side aisles, for

[019-206] interior processions, for chapels,—a sort of lateral walks or

[019-207] promenades where the principal nave discharges itself through the

[019-208] spaces between the pillars. That settled, the number of chapels,

[019-209] doors, bell towers, and pinnacles are modified to infinity,

[019-210] according to the fancy of the century, the people, and art. The

[019-211] service of religion once assured and provided for, architecture

[019-212] does what she pleases. Statues, stained glass, rose windows,

[019-213] arabesques, denticulations, capitals, bas-reliefs,—she combines

[019-214] all these imaginings according to the arrangement which best

[019-215] suits her. Hence, the prodigious exterior variety of these

[019-216] edifices, at whose foundation dwells so much order and unity. The

[019-217] trunk of a tree is immovable; the foliage is capricious.
