# Montfaucon

The Gibbet of Montfaucon was the great permanent gallows of [[paris|medieval Paris]], a monumental stone structure standing outside [[paris|the city]] walls where the bodies of executed criminals were suspended in chains for public display. In *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, Montfaucon appears as a recurring image of [[law-and-justice|justice]] and [[death]], culminating in the novel's devastating final scene.

## Physical Description

Montfaucon was, as the 18th-century historian Henri Sauval described it, "the most ancient and the most superb gibbet in the kingdom" ((088-173)). The structure consisted of a massive rectangular stone platform with sixteen pillars supporting three tiers of horizontal beams. Iron chains hung from these beams, each capable of holding a human body. The gibbet's cellar below served as a charnel house where [[death|corpse]]s were deposited after they had decayed sufficiently on the chains, "according to custom, to the cellar of Montfaucon" ((088-172)).

Hugo mentions the gibbet alongside other instruments of [[law-and-justice|medieval justice]]: "there was one at Montfaucon" ((035-149)), referring to the distribution of gibbets across [[paris|Paris]] alongside [[quasimodos-pillory|the pillory]] at the [[place-de-greve|Place de Grève]]. The structure stood as a permanent and visible reminder of royal and ecclesiastical authority.

## In the Novel

### Early Appearances

The gibbet is woven into the fabric of the novel from its earliest pages. Hugo describes the [[paris|medieval Paris]]ian landscape as dotted with such instruments of punishment: "a permanent gibbet and a pillory, 'a [[law-and-justice|justice]] and a ladder', as they said in those days" ((010-110)). The barrier "aux Pourceaux" is noted for its proximity to "that hideous Montfaucon" ((010-123)).

The gibbet serves as a constant threat throughout the narrative. Characters speak of it as their likely [[fatalite|fate]], acknowledging that for the criminal class of [[paris|medieval Paris]], there was "one escape, except it be for Montfaucon" ((080-069)). The gallows and pillory form a linked chain of punishment: "The pillory leads to the gallows" ((048-033)).

### The Final Scene

Montfaucon's most significant role comes in the novel's final chapter, set after the [[death]]s of the principal characters. The narrator describes how, years after the events of the main story, two skeletons are discovered in the charnel cellar of Montfaucon — one of a woman, one of a man. The male skeleton, deformed and twisted, is found embracing the female skeleton. When an attempt is made to separate them, the male skeleton crumbles to dust.

This final image — of [[quasimodo]] and [[esmeraldas-execution|Esmeralda]] united in [[death]] at Montfaucon — is one of the most powerful and enduring in all of nineteenth-century literature. The "c[[love-and-desire|lust]]er of skeletons at Montfaucon clashing" ((063-022)) foreshadows this conclusion.

## Historical Basis

The historical Gibbet of Montfaucon was constructed in the 13th century under the reign of Philip IV and stood until its demolition in 1760. It was located near the modern-day Parc des Buttes-Chaumont in the 19th arrondissement of [[paris|Paris]]. Its imposing structure — visible for miles across the plain — was intended to deter crime through the public humiliation of executed criminals. The gibbet could accommodate up to fifty [[death|corpse]]s simultaneously.

The structure was more than a gallows; it was a symbol of [[law-and-justice|medieval justice]], where the bodies of petty thieves, murderers, heretics, and political opponents alike were left to rot in public view, their remains eventually consigned to the charnel below.

## Significance

In Hugo's novel, Montfaucon operates on multiple symbolic levels:

- **Justice and Cruelty**: The gibbet represents the harsh, public nature of [[law-and-justice|medieval justice]], where [[death|execution]] was spectacle and the [[death|corpse]] was warning.
- **Social Inequality**: The threat of Montfaucon hangs heaviest over the novel's [[truands-of-paris|outcasts]] — the [[truands-of-paris|truands]], the vagabonds, the gypsies — while the powerful largely escape its chains.
- **Transcendence Through Love**: The final image of [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]]'s skeleton clinging to [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]]'s transforms this place of horror into a site of tragic [[beauty]]. In [[death]], the deformed bell-ringer achieves what he could never achieve in life — a union with the woman he [[love-and-desire|love]]d.
