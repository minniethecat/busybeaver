# Pont Notre-Dame

The Pont Notre-Dame was the busiest of the three great bridges of medieval
[[paris|Paris]], connecting the Île de la Cité — [[paris|the city]]'s historic and religious
heart — to the commercial Right Bank. Hugo catalogues it in his panoramic
survey of Paris: "three on the right, the Pont Notre-Dame, and the Pont au Change, both of them lined with houses" (020-152). Like the Pont au Change and the Pont
Saint-Michel, it was "loaded with houses" — a street suspended over water,
lined with the shops of merchants and the dwellings of tradesmen. Hugo's
novel invokes the bridge as part of its panoramic reconstruction of
fifteenth-century [[paris|Paris]].

## In the Novel

The Pont Notre-Dame appears in Hugo's architectural survey as an example
of the density that characterized medieval urbanism. The bridge was not a
passage but a destination — people lived, worked, traded, and died upon
it. Hugo's description of the bridge-houses captures the vertical
intensity of medieval construction: every inch of available space was
inhabited, and the distinction between land and water was almost entirely
effaced by human settlement.

> During the climactic events of the novel, a "cavalcade was heard in the direction of the Pont Notre-Dame" (083-100) — the bridge serving as a conduit for the forces that will determine Esmeralda's fate.

## Historical Context

The Pont Notre-Dame, the Pont au Change, and the [[pont-saint-michel|Pont Saint-Michel]] were
the arteries of [[paris|medieval Paris]]. The Pont Notre-Dame was the most important
commercially, connecting [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]]'s island to the mercantile Right
Bank. The houses that lined the bridge were among the most expensive
properties in [[paris|Paris]] — the combination of centrality and river views was
irresistible to the prosperous bourgeoisie. The bridge was repeatedly
destroyed by fire and flood and rebuilt, each reconstruction reflecting
the evolving architectural styles of [[paris|the city]].

## Significance

The Pont Notre-Dame, like the other bridges of the novel, is part of
Hugo's documentary project: the meticulous reconstruction of a lost world.
The bridge-houses would be demolished in the eighteenth century, and
the medieval bridges replaced by the broader, barer structures of
modern [[paris|Paris]]. Hugo's novel is, among many other things, an act of
preservation — a written [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] that survives the physical
destruction of [[paris|the city]] it describes.
