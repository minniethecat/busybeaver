---[002-001] It is certain, that if Ravaillac had not assassinated Henri IV.,

[002-002] there would have been no documents in the trial of Ravaillac

[002-003] deposited in the clerk’s office of the Palais de Justice, no

[002-004] accomplices interested in causing the said documents to

[002-005] disappear; hence, no incendiaries obliged, for lack of better

[002-006] means, to burn the clerk’s office in order to burn the documents,

[002-007] and to burn the Palais de Justice in order to burn the clerk’s

[002-008] office; consequently, in short, no conflagration in 1618. The old

[002-009] Palais would be standing still, with its ancient grand hall; I

[002-010] should be able to say to the reader, “Go and look at it,” and we

[002-011] should thus both escape the necessity,—I of making, and he of

[002-012] reading, a description of it, such as it is. Which demonstrates a

[002-013] new truth: that great events have incalculable results.

[002-014] It is true that it may be quite possible, in the first place,

[002-015] that Ravaillac had no accomplices; and in the second, that if he

[002-016] had any, they were in no way connected with the fire of 1618. Two

[002-017] other very plausible explanations exist: First, the great flaming

[002-018] star, a foot broad, and a cubit high, which fell from heaven, as

[002-019] every one knows, upon the law courts, after midnight on the

[002-020] seventh of March; second, Théophile’s quatrain,—

[002-021] “Sure, ’twas but a sorry game

[002-022] When at Paris, Dame Justice,

[002-023] Through having eaten too much spice,

[002-024] Set the palace all aflame.”

[002-025] Whatever may be thought of this triple explanation, political,

[002-026] physical, and poetical, of the burning of the law courts in 1618,

[002-027] the unfortunate fact of the fire is certain. Very little to-day

[002-028] remains, thanks to this catastrophe,—thanks, above all, to the

[002-029] successive restorations which have completed what it spared,—very

[002-030] little remains of that first dwelling of the kings of France,—of

[002-031] that elder palace of the Louvre, already so old in the time of

[002-032] Philip the Handsome, that they sought there for the traces of the

[002-033] magnificent buildings erected by King Robert and described by

[002-034] Helgaldus. Nearly everything has disappeared. What has become of

[002-035] the chamber of the chancellery, where Saint Louis consummated his

[002-036] marriage? the garden where he administered justice, “clad in a

[002-037] coat of camelot, a surcoat of linsey-woolsey, without sleeves,

[002-038] and a sur-mantle of black sandal, as he lay upon the carpet with

[002-039] Joinville?” Where is the chamber of the Emperor Sigismond? and

[002-040] that of Charles IV.? that of Jean the Landless? Where is the

[002-041] staircase, from which Charles VI. promulgated his edict of

[002-042] pardon? the slab where Marcel cut the throats of Robert de

[002-043] Clermont and the Marshal of Champagne, in the presence of the

[002-044] dauphin? the wicket where the bulls of Pope Benedict were torn,

[002-045] and whence those who had brought them departed decked out, in

[002-046] derision, in copes and mitres, and making an apology through all

[002-047] Paris? and the grand hall, with its gilding, its azure, its

[002-048] statues, its pointed arches, its pillars, its immense vault, all

[002-049] fretted with carvings? and the gilded chamber? and the stone

[002-050] lion, which stood at the door, with lowered head and tail between

[002-051] his legs, like the lions on the throne of Solomon, in the

[002-052] humiliated attitude which befits force in the presence of

[002-053] justice? and the beautiful doors? and the stained glass? and the

[002-054] chased ironwork, which drove Biscornette to despair? and the

[002-055] delicate woodwork of Hancy? What has time, what have men done

[002-056] with these marvels? What have they given us in return for all

[002-057] this Gallic history, for all this Gothic art? The heavy flattened

[002-058] arches of M. de Brosse, that awkward architect of the

[002-059] Saint-Gervais portal. So much for art; and, as for history, we

[002-060] have the gossiping reminiscences of the great pillar, still

[002-061] ringing with the tattle of the Patru.

[002-062] It is not much. Let us return to the veritable grand hall of the

[002-063] veritable old palace. The two extremities of this gigantic

[002-064] parallelogram were occupied, the one by the famous marble table,

[002-065] so long, so broad, and so thick that, as the ancient land

[002-066] rolls—in a style that would have given Gargantua an appetite—say,

[002-067] “such a slice of marble as was never beheld in the world”; the

[002-068] other by the chapel where Louis XI. had himself sculptured on his

[002-069] knees before the Virgin, and whither he caused to be brought,

[002-070] without heeding the two gaps thus made in the row of royal

[002-071] statues, the statues of Charlemagne and of Saint Louis, two

[002-072] saints whom he supposed to be great in favor in heaven, as kings

[002-073] of France. This chapel, quite new, having been built only six

[002-074] years, was entirely in that charming taste of delicate

[002-075] architecture, of marvellous sculpture, of fine and deep chasing,

[002-076] which marks with us the end of the Gothic era, and which is

[002-077] perpetuated to about the middle of the sixteenth century in the

[002-078] fairylike fancies of the Renaissance. The little open-work rose

[002-079] window, pierced above the portal, was, in particular, a

[002-080] masterpiece of lightness and grace; one would have pronounced it

[002-081] a star of lace.

[002-082] In the middle of the hall, opposite the great door, a platform of

[002-083] gold brocade, placed against the wall, a special entrance to

[002-084] which had been effected through a window in the corridor of the

[002-085] gold chamber, had been erected for the Flemish emissaries and the

[002-086] other great personages invited to the presentation of the mystery

[002-087] play.

[002-088] It was upon the marble table that the mystery was to be enacted,

[002-089] as usual. It had been arranged for the purpose, early in the

[002-090] morning; its rich slabs of marble, all scratched by the heels of

[002-091] law clerks, supported a cage of carpenter’s work of considerable

[002-092] height, the upper surface of which, within view of the whole

[002-093] hall, was to serve as the theatre, and whose interior, masked by

[002-094] tapestries, was to take the place of dressing-rooms for the

[002-095] personages of the piece. A ladder, naively placed on the outside,

[002-096] was to serve as means of communication between the dressing-room

[002-097] and the stage, and lend its rude rungs to entrances as well as to

[002-098] exits. There was no personage, however unexpected, no sudden

[002-099] change, no theatrical effect, which was not obliged to mount that

[002-100] ladder. Innocent and venerable infancy of art and contrivances!

[002-101] Four of the bailiff of the palace’s sergeants, perfunctory

[002-102] guardians of all the pleasures of the people, on days of festival

[002-103] as well as on days of execution, stood at the four corners of the

[002-104] marble table.

[002-105] The piece was only to begin with the twelfth stroke of the great

[002-106] palace clock sounding midday. It was very late, no doubt, for a

[002-107] theatrical representation, but they had been obliged to fix the

[002-108] hour to suit the convenience of the ambassadors.

[002-109] Now, this whole multitude had been waiting since morning. A

[002-110] goodly number of curious, good people had been shivering since

[002-111] daybreak before the grand staircase of the palace; some even

[002-112] affirmed that they had passed the night across the threshold of

[002-113] the great door, in order to make sure that they should be the

[002-114] first to pass in. The crowd grew more dense every moment, and,

[002-115] like water, which rises above its normal level, began to mount

[002-116] along the walls, to swell around the pillars, to spread out on

[002-117] the entablatures, on the cornices, on the window-sills, on all

[002-118] the salient points of the architecture, on all the reliefs of the

[002-119] sculpture. Hence, discomfort, impatience, weariness, the liberty

[002-120] of a day of cynicism and folly, the quarrels which break forth

[002-121] for all sorts of causes—a pointed elbow, an iron-shod shoe, the

[002-122] fatigue of long waiting—had already, long before the hour

[002-123] appointed for the arrival of the ambassadors, imparted a harsh

[002-124] and bitter accent to the clamor of these people who were shut in,

[002-125] fitted into each other, pressed, trampled upon, stifled. Nothing

[002-126] was to be heard but imprecations on the Flemish, the provost of

[002-127] the merchants, the Cardinal de Bourbon, the bailiff of the

[002-128] courts, Madame Marguerite of Austria, the sergeants with their

[002-129] rods, the cold, the heat, the bad weather, the Bishop of Paris,

[002-130] the Pope of the Fools, the pillars, the statues, that closed

[002-131] door, that open window; all to the vast amusement of a band of

[002-132] scholars and lackeys scattered through the mass, who mingled with

[002-133] all this discontent their teasing remarks, and their malicious

[002-134] suggestions, and pricked the general bad temper with a pin, so to

[002-135] speak.

[002-136] Among the rest there was a group of those merry imps, who, after

[002-137] smashing the glass in a window, had seated themselves hardily on

[002-138] the entablature, and from that point despatched their gaze and

[002-139] their railleries both within and without, upon the throng in the

[002-140] hall, and the throng upon the Place. It was easy to see, from

[002-141] their parodied gestures, their ringing laughter, the bantering

[002-142] appeals which they exchanged with their comrades, from one end of

[002-143] the hall to the other, that these young clerks did not share the

[002-144] weariness and fatigue of the rest of the spectators, and that

[002-145] they understood very well the art of extracting, for their own

[002-146] private diversion from that which they had under their eyes, a

[002-147] spectacle which made them await the other with patience.

[002-148] “Upon my soul, so it’s you, ‘Joannes Frollo de Molendino!’” cried

[002-149] one of them, to a sort of little, light-haired imp, with a

[002-150] well-favored and malign countenance, clinging to the acanthus

[002-151] leaves of a capital; “you are well named John of the Mill, for

[002-152] your two arms and your two legs have the air of four wings

[002-153] fluttering on the breeze. How long have you been here?”

[002-154] “By the mercy of the devil,” retorted Joannes Frollo, “these four

[002-155] hours and more; and I hope that they will be reckoned to my

[002-156] credit in purgatory. I heard the eight singers of the King of

[002-157] Sicily intone the first verse of seven o’clock mass in the

[002-158] Sainte-Chapelle.”

[002-159] “Fine singers!” replied the other, “with voices even more pointed

[002-160] than their caps! Before founding a mass for Monsieur Saint John,

[002-161] the king should have inquired whether Monsieur Saint John likes

[002-162] Latin droned out in a Provençal accent.”

[002-163] “He did it for the sake of employing those accursed singers of

[002-164] the King of Sicily!” cried an old woman sharply from among the

[002-165] crowd beneath the window. “I just put it to you! A thousand

[002-166] _livres parisi_ for a mass! and out of the tax on sea fish in the

[002-167] markets of Paris, to boot!”

[002-168] “Peace, old crone,” said a tall, grave person, stopping up his

[002-169] nose on the side towards the fishwife; “a mass had to be founded.

[002-170] Would you wish the king to fall ill again?”

[002-171] “Bravely spoken, Sire Gilles Lecornu, master furrier of king’s

[002-172] robes!” cried the little student, clinging to the capital.

[002-173] A shout of laughter from all the students greeted the unlucky

[002-174] name of the poor furrier of the king’s robes.

[002-175] “Lecornu! Gilles Lecornu!” said some.

[002-176] “_Cornutus et hirsutus_, horned and hairy,” another went on.

[002-177] “He! of course,” continued the small imp on the capital, “What

[002-178] are they laughing at? An honorable man is Gilles Lecornu, brother

[002-179] of Master Jehan Lecornu, provost of the king’s house, son of

[002-180] Master Mahiet Lecornu, first porter of the Bois de Vincennes,—all

[002-181] _bourgeois_ of Paris, all married, from father to son.”

[002-182] The gayety redoubled. The big furrier, without uttering a word in

[002-183] reply, tried to escape all the eyes riveted upon him from all

[002-184] sides; but he perspired and panted in vain; like a wedge entering

[002-185] the wood, his efforts served only to bury still more deeply in

[002-186] the shoulders of his neighbors, his large, apoplectic face,

[002-187] purple with spite and rage.

[002-188] At length one of these, as fat, short, and venerable as himself,

[002-189] came to his rescue.

[002-190] “Abomination! scholars addressing a _bourgeois_ in that fashion

[002-191] in my day would have been flogged with a fagot, which would have

[002-192] afterwards been used to burn them.”

[002-193] The whole band burst into laughter.

[002-194] “Holà hé! who is scolding so? Who is that screech owl of evil

[002-195] fortune?”
