---[080-001] “And against whom do you thus rebel?” inquired the king; “against

[080-002] your bailiffs? against your lords?”

[080-003] “Sometimes; that depends. Against the duke, also, sometimes.”

[080-004] Louis XI. returned and seated himself, saying, with a smile,—

[080-005] “Ah! here they have only got as far as the bailiffs.”

[080-006] At that instant Olivier le Daim returned. He was followed by two

[080-007] pages, who bore the king’s toilet articles; but what struck Louis

[080-008] XI. was that he was also accompanied by the provost of Paris and

[080-009] the chevalier of the watch, who appeared to be in consternation.

[080-010] The spiteful barber also wore an air of consternation, which was

[080-011] one of contentment beneath, however. It was he who spoke first.

[080-012] “Sire, I ask your majesty’s pardon for the calamitous news which

[080-013] I bring.”

[080-014] The king turned quickly and grazed the mat on the floor with the

[080-015] feet of his chair,—

[080-016] “What does this mean?”

[080-017] “Sire,” resumed Olivier le Daim, with the malicious air of a man

[080-018] who rejoices that he is about to deal a violent blow, “’tis not

[080-019] against the bailiff of the courts that this popular sedition is

[080-020] directed.”

[080-021] “Against whom, then?”

[080-022] “Against you, sire?’

[080-023] The aged king rose erect and straight as a young man,—

[080-024] “Explain yourself, Olivier! And guard your head well, gossip; for

[080-025] I swear to you by the cross of Saint-Lô that, if you lie to us at

[080-026] this hour, the sword which severed the head of Monsieur de

[080-027] Luxembourg is not so notched that it cannot yet sever yours!”

[080-028] The oath was formidable; Louis XI. had only sworn twice in the

[080-029] course of his life by the cross of Saint-Lô.

[080-030] Olivier opened his mouth to reply.

[080-031] “Sire—”

[080-032] “On your knees!” interrupted the king violently. “Tristan, have

[080-033] an eye to this man.”

[080-034] Olivier knelt down and said coldly,—

[080-035] “Sire, a sorceress was condemned to death by your court of

[080-036] parliament. She took refuge in Notre-Dame. The people are trying

[080-037] to take her from thence by main force. Monsieur the provost and

[080-038] monsieur the chevalier of the watch, who have just come from the

[080-039] riot, are here to give me the lie if this is not the truth. The

[080-040] populace is besieging Notre-Dame.”

[080-041] “Yes, indeed!” said the king in a low voice, all pale and

[080-042] trembling with wrath. “Notre-Dame! They lay siege to our Lady, my

[080-043] good mistress in her cathedral!—Rise, Olivier. You are right. I

[080-044] give you Simon Radin’s charge. You are right. ’Tis I whom they

[080-045] are attacking. The witch is under the protection of this church,

[080-046] the church is under my protection. And I thought that they were

[080-047] acting against the bailiff! ’Tis against myself!”

[080-048] Then, rendered young by fury, he began to walk up and down with

[080-049] long strides. He no longer laughed, he was terrible, he went and

[080-050] came; the fox was changed into a hyæna. He seemed suffocated to

[080-051] such a degree that he could not speak; his lips moved, and his

[080-052] fleshless fists were clenched. All at once he raised his head,

[080-053] his hollow eye appeared full of light, and his voice burst forth

[080-054] like a clarion: “Down with them, Tristan! A heavy hand for these

[080-055] rascals! Go, Tristan, my friend! slay! slay!”

[080-056] This eruption having passed, he returned to his seat, and said

[080-057] with cold and concentrated wrath,—

[080-058] “Here, Tristan! There are here with us in the Bastille the fifty

[080-059] lances of the Vicomte de Gif, which makes three hundred horse:

[080-060] you will take them. There is also the company of our unattached

[080-061] archers of Monsieur de Châteaupers: you will take it. You are

[080-062] provost of the marshals; you have the men of your provostship:

[080-063] you will take them. At the Hôtel Saint-Pol you will find forty

[080-064] archers of monsieur the dauphin’s new guard: you will take them.

[080-065] And, with all these, you will hasten to Notre-Dame. Ah!

[080-066] messieurs, louts of Paris, do you fling yourselves thus against

[080-067] the crown of France, the sanctity of Notre-Dame, and the peace of

[080-068] this commonwealth! Exterminate, Tristan! exterminate! and let not

[080-069] a single one escape, except it be for Montfaucon.”

[080-070] Tristan bowed. “’Tis well, sire.”

[080-071] He added, after a silence, “And what shall I do with the

[080-072] sorceress?”

[080-073] This question caused the king to meditate.

[080-074] “Ah!” said he, “the sorceress! Monsieur d’Estouteville, what did

[080-075] the people wish to do with her?”

[080-076] “Sire,” replied the provost of Paris, “I imagine that since the

[080-077] populace has come to tear her from her asylum in Notre-Dame, ’tis

[080-078] because that impunity wounds them, and they desire to hang her.”

[080-079] The king appeared to reflect deeply: then, addressing Tristan

[080-080] l’Hermite, “Well! gossip, exterminate the people and hang the

[080-081] sorceress.”

[080-082] “That’s it,” said Rym in a low tone to Coppenole, “punish the

[080-083] people for willing a thing, and then do what they wish.”

[080-084] “Enough, sire,” replied Tristan. “If the sorceress is still in

[080-085] Notre-Dame, must she be seized in spite of the sanctuary?”

[080-086] “_Pasque-Dieu!_ the sanctuary!” said the king, scratching his

[080-087] ear. “But the woman must be hung, nevertheless.”

[080-088] Here, as though seized with a sudden idea, he flung himself on

[080-089] his knees before his chair, took off his hat, placed it on the

[080-090] seat, and gazing devoutly at one of the leaden amulets which

[080-091] loaded it down, “Oh!” said he, with clasped hands, “our Lady of

[080-092] Paris, my gracious patroness, pardon me. I will only do it this

[080-093] once. This criminal must be punished. I assure you, madame the

[080-094] virgin, my good mistress, that she is a sorceress who is not

[080-095] worthy of your amiable protection. You know, madame, that many

[080-096] very pious princes have overstepped the privileges of the

[080-097] churches for the glory of God and the necessities of the State.

[080-098] Saint Hugues, bishop of England, permitted King Edward to hang a

[080-099] witch in his church. Saint-Louis of France, my master,

[080-100] transgressed, with the same object, the church of Monsieur

[080-101] Saint-Paul; and Monsieur Alphonse, son of the king of Jerusalem,

[080-102] the very church of the Holy Sepulchre. Pardon me, then, for this

[080-103] once. Our Lady of Paris, I will never do so again, and I will

[080-104] give you a fine statue of silver, like the one which I gave last

[080-105] year to Our Lady of Écouys. So be it.”

[080-106] He made the sign of the cross, rose, donned his hat once more,

[080-107] and said to Tristan,—

[080-108] “Be diligent, gossip. Take Monsieur Châteaupers with you. You

[080-109] will cause the tocsin to be sounded. You will crush the populace.

[080-110] You will seize the witch. ’Tis said. And I mean the business of

[080-111] the execution to be done by you. You will render me an account of

[080-112] it. Come, Olivier, I shall not go to bed this night. Shave me.”

[080-113] Tristan l’Hermite bowed and departed. Then the king, dismissing

[080-114] Rym and Coppenole with a gesture,—

[080-115] “God guard you, messieurs, my good friends the Flemings. Go, take

[080-116] a little repose. The night advances, and we are nearer the

[080-117] morning than the evening.”

[080-118] Both retired and gained their apartments under the guidance of

[080-119] the captain of the Bastille. Coppenole said to Guillaume Rym,—

[080-120] “Hum! I have had enough of that coughing king! I have seen

[080-121] Charles of Burgundy drunk, and he was less malignant than Louis

[080-122] XI. when ailing.”

[080-123] “Master Jacques,” replied Rym, “’tis because wine renders kings

[080-124] less cruel than does barley water.”

[080-125] CHAPTER VI. LITTLE SWORD IN POCKET.

[080-126] On emerging from the Bastille, Gringoire descended the Rue

[080-127] Saint-Antoine with the swiftness of a runaway horse. On arriving

[080-128] at the Baudoyer gate, he walked straight to the stone cross which

[080-129] rose in the middle of that place, as though he were able to

[080-130] distinguish in the darkness the figure of a man clad and cloaked

[080-131] in black, who was seated on the steps of the cross.

[080-132] “Is it you, master?” said Gringoire.

[080-133] The personage in black rose.

[080-134] “Death and passion! You make me boil, Gringoire. The man on the

[080-135] tower of Saint-Gervais has just cried half-past one o’clock in

[080-136] the morning.”

[080-137] “Oh,” retorted Gringoire, “’tis no fault of mine, but of the

[080-138] watch and the king. I have just had a narrow escape. I always

[080-139] just miss being hung. ’Tis my predestination.”

[080-140] “You lack everything,” said the other. “But come quickly. Have

[080-141] you the password?”

[080-142] “Fancy, master, I have seen the king. I come from him. He wears

[080-143] fustian breeches. ’Tis an adventure.”

[080-144] “Oh! distaff of words! what is your adventure to me! Have you the

[080-145] password of the outcasts?”

[080-146] “I have it. Be at ease. ‘Little sword in pocket.’”

[080-147] “Good. Otherwise, we could not make our way as far as the church.

[080-148] The outcasts bar the streets. Fortunately, it appears that they

[080-149] have encountered resistance. We may still arrive in time.”

[080-150] “Yes, master, but how are we to get into Notre-Dame?”

[080-151] “I have the key to the tower.”

[080-152] “And how are we to get out again?”

[080-153] “Behind the cloister there is a little door which opens on the

[080-154] Terrain and the water. I have taken the key to it, and I moored a

[080-155] boat there this morning.”

[080-156] “I have had a beautiful escape from being hung!” Gringoire

[080-157] repeated.

[080-158] “Eh, quick! come!” said the other.

[080-159] Both descended towards the city with long strides.

[080-160] CHAPTER VII. CHATEAUPERS TO THE RESCUE.

[080-161] The reader will, perhaps, recall the critical situation in which

[080-162] we left Quasimodo. The brave deaf man, assailed on all sides, had

[080-163] lost, if not all courage, at least all hope of saving, not

[080-164] himself (he was not thinking of himself), but the gypsy. He ran

[080-165] distractedly along the gallery. Notre-Dame was on the point of

[080-166] being taken by storm by the outcasts. All at once, a great

[080-167] galloping of horses filled the neighboring streets, and, with a

[080-168] long file of torches and a thick column of cavaliers, with free

[080-169] reins and lances in rest, these furious sounds debouched on the

[080-170] Place like a hurricane,—

[080-171] “France! France! cut down the louts! Châteaupers to the rescue!

[080-172] Provostship! Provostship!”

[080-173] The frightened vagabonds wheeled round.

[080-174] Quasimodo who did not hear, saw the naked swords, the torches,

[080-175] the irons of the pikes, all that cavalry, at the head of which he

[080-176] recognized Captain Phœbus; he beheld the confusion of the

[080-177] outcasts, the terror of some, the disturbance among the bravest

[080-178] of them, and from this unexpected succor he recovered so much

[080-179] strength, that he hurled from the church the first assailants who

[080-180] were already climbing into the gallery.

[080-181] It was, in fact, the king’s troops who had arrived. The vagabonds

[080-182] behaved bravely. They defended themselves like desperate men.

[080-183] Caught on the flank, by the Rue Saint-Pierre-aux-Bœufs, and in

[080-184] the rear through the Rue du Parvis, driven to bay against

[080-185] Notre-Dame, which they still assailed and Quasimodo defended, at

[080-186] the same time besiegers and besieged, they were in the singular

[080-187] situation in which Comte Henri Harcourt, _Taurinum obsessor idem

[080-188] et obsessus_, as his epitaph says, found himself later on, at the

[080-189] famous siege of Turin, in 1640, between Prince Thomas of Savoy,

[080-190] whom he was besieging, and the Marquis de Leganez, who was

[080-191] blockading him.

[080-192] The battle was frightful. There was a dog’s tooth for wolf’s

[080-193] flesh, as P. Mathieu says. The king’s cavaliers, in whose midst

[080-194] Phœbus de Châteaupers bore himself valiantly, gave no quarter,

[080-195] and the slash of the sword disposed of those who escaped the

[080-196] thrust of the lance. The outcasts, badly armed foamed and bit

[080-197] with rage. Men, women, children, hurled themselves on the

[080-198] cruppers and the breasts of the horses, and hung there like cats,

[080-199] with teeth, finger nails and toe nails. Others struck the

[080-200] archers’ in the face with their torches. Others thrust iron hooks

[080-201] into the necks of the cavaliers and dragged them down. They

[080-202] slashed in pieces those who fell.

[080-203] One was noticed who had a large, glittering scythe, and who, for

[080-204] a long time, mowed the legs of the horses. He was frightful. He

[080-205] was singing a ditty, with a nasal intonation, he swung and drew

[080-206] back his scythe incessantly. At every blow he traced around him a

[080-207] great circle of severed limbs. He advanced thus into the very

[080-208] thickest of the cavalry, with the tranquil slowness, the lolling

[080-209] of the head and the regular breathing of a harvester attacking a

[080-210] field of wheat. It was Clopin Trouillefou. A shot from an

[080-211] arquebus laid him low.
