---[038-001] “’Tis in truth, a frightful tale,” said Oudarde, “and one which

[038-002] would make even a Burgundian weep.”

[038-003] “I am no longer surprised,” added Gervaise, “that fear of the

[038-004] gypsies should spur you on so sharply.”

[038-005] “And you did all the better,” resumed Oudarde, “to flee with your

[038-006] Eustache just now, since these also are gypsies from Poland.”

[038-007] “No,” said Gervais, “’tis said that they come from Spain and

[038-008] Catalonia.”

[038-009] “Catalonia? ’tis possible,” replied Oudarde. “Pologne, Catalogne,

[038-010] Valogne, I always confound those three provinces, One thing is

[038-011] certain, that they are gypsies.”

[038-012] “Who certainly,” added Gervaise, “have teeth long enough to eat

[038-013] little children. I should not be surprised if la Smeralda ate a

[038-014] little of them also, though she pretends to be dainty. Her white

[038-015] goat knows tricks that are too malicious for there not to be some

[038-016] impiety underneath it all.”

[038-017] Mahiette walked on in silence. She was absorbed in that revery

[038-018] which is, in some sort, the continuation of a mournful tale, and

[038-019] which ends only after having communicated the emotion, from

[038-020] vibration to vibration, even to the very last fibres of the

[038-021] heart. Nevertheless, Gervaise addressed her, “And did they ever

[038-022] learn what became of la Chantefleurie?” Mahiette made no reply.

[038-023] Gervaise repeated her question, and shook her arm, calling her by

[038-024] name. Mahiette appeared to awaken from her thoughts.

[038-025] “What became of la Chantefleurie?” she said, repeating

[038-026] mechanically the words whose impression was still fresh in her

[038-027] ear; then, making an effort to recall her attention to the

[038-028] meaning of her words, “Ah!” she continued briskly, “no one ever

[038-029] found out.”

[038-030] She added, after a pause,—

[038-031] “Some said that she had been seen to quit Reims at nightfall by

[038-032] the Fléchembault gate; others, at daybreak, by the old Basée

[038-033] gate. A poor man found her gold cross hanging on the stone cross

[038-034] in the field where the fair is held. It was that ornament which

[038-035] had wrought her ruin, in ’61. It was a gift from the handsome

[038-036] Vicomte de Cormontreuil, her first lover. Paquette had never been

[038-037] willing to part with it, wretched as she had been. She had clung

[038-038] to it as to life itself. So, when we saw that cross abandoned, we

[038-039] all thought that she was dead. Nevertheless, there were people of

[038-040] the Cabaret les Vantes, who said that they had seen her pass

[038-041] along the road to Paris, walking on the pebbles with her bare

[038-042] feet. But, in that case, she must have gone out through the Porte

[038-043] de Vesle, and all this does not agree. Or, to speak more truly, I

[038-044] believe that she actually did depart by the Porte de Vesle, but

[038-045] departed from this world.”

[038-046] “I do not understand you,” said Gervaise.

[038-047] “La Vesle,” replied Mahiette, with a melancholy smile, “is the

[038-048] river.”

[038-049] “Poor Chantefleurie!” said Oudarde, with a shiver,—“drowned!”

[038-050] “Drowned!” resumed Mahiette, “who could have told good Father

[038-051] Guybertant, when he passed under the bridge of Tingueux with the

[038-052] current, singing in his barge, that one day his dear little

[038-053] Paquette would also pass beneath that bridge, but without song or

[038-054] boat.

[038-055] “And the little shoe?” asked Gervaise.

[038-056] “Disappeared with the mother,” replied Mahiette.

[038-057] “Poor little shoe!” said Oudarde.

[038-058] Oudarde, a big and tender woman, would have been well pleased to

[038-059] sigh in company with Mahiette. But Gervaise, more curious, had

[038-060] not finished her questions.

[038-061] “And the monster?” she said suddenly, to Mahiette.

[038-062] “What monster?” inquired the latter.

[038-063] “The little gypsy monster left by the sorceresses in

[038-064] Chantefleurie’s chamber, in exchange for her daughter. What did

[038-065] you do with it? I hope you drowned it also.”

[038-066] “No.” replied Mahiette.

[038-067] “What? You burned it then? In sooth, that is more just. A witch

[038-068] child!”

[038-069] “Neither the one nor the other, Gervaise. Monseigneur the

[038-070] archbishop interested himself in the child of Egypt, exorcised

[038-071] it, blessed it, removed the devil carefully from its body, and

[038-072] sent it to Paris, to be exposed on the wooden bed at Notre-Dame,

[038-073] as a foundling.”

[038-074] “Those bishops!” grumbled Gervaise, “because they are learned,

[038-075] they do nothing like anybody else. I just put it to you, Oudarde,

[038-076] the idea of placing the devil among the foundlings! For that

[038-077] little monster was assuredly the devil. Well, Mahiette, what did

[038-078] they do with it in Paris? I am quite sure that no charitable

[038-079] person wanted it.”

[038-080] “I do not know,” replied the Rémoise, “’twas just at that time

[038-081] that my husband bought the office of notary, at Beru, two leagues

[038-082] from the town, and we were no longer occupied with that story;

[038-083] besides, in front of Beru, stand the two hills of Cernay, which

[038-084] hide the towers of the cathedral in Reims from view.”

[038-085] While chatting thus, the three worthy _bourgeoises_ had arrived

[038-086] at the Place de Grève. In their absorption, they had passed the

[038-087] public breviary of the Tour-Roland without stopping, and took

[038-088] their way mechanically towards the pillory around which the

[038-089] throng was growing more dense with every moment. It is probable

[038-090] that the spectacle which at that moment attracted all looks in

[038-091] that direction, would have made them forget completely the

[038-092] Rat-Hole, and the halt which they intended to make there, if big

[038-093] Eustache, six years of age, whom Mahiette was dragging along by

[038-094] the hand, had not abruptly recalled the object to them: “Mother,”

[038-095] said he, as though some instinct warned him that the Rat-Hole was

[038-096] behind him, “can I eat the cake now?”

[038-097] If Eustache had been more adroit, that is to say, less greedy, he

[038-098] would have continued to wait, and would only have hazarded that

[038-099] simple question, “Mother, can I eat the cake, now?” on their

[038-100] return to the University, to Master Andry Musnier’s, Rue Madame

[038-101] la Valence, when he had the two arms of the Seine and the five

[038-102] bridges of the city between the Rat-Hole and the cake.

[038-103] This question, highly imprudent at the moment when Eustache put

[038-104] it, aroused Mahiette’s attention.

[038-105] “By the way,” she exclaimed, “we are forgetting the recluse! Show

[038-106] me the Rat-Hole, that I may carry her her cake.”

[038-107] “Immediately,” said Oudarde, “’tis a charity.”

[038-108] But this did not suit Eustache.

[038-109] “Stop! my cake!” said he, rubbing both ears alternatively with

[038-110] his shoulders, which, in such cases, is the supreme sign of

[038-111] discontent.

[038-112] The three women retraced their steps, and, on arriving in the

[038-113] vicinity of the Tour-Roland, Oudarde said to the other two,—

[038-114] “We must not all three gaze into the hole at once, for fear of

[038-115] alarming the recluse. Do you two pretend to read the _Dominus_ in

[038-116] the breviary, while I thrust my nose into the aperture; the

[038-117] recluse knows me a little. I will give you warning when you can

[038-118] approach.”

[038-119] She proceeded alone to the window. At the moment when she looked

[038-120] in, a profound pity was depicted on all her features, and her

[038-121] frank, gay visage altered its expression and color as abruptly as

[038-122] though it had passed from a ray of sunlight to a ray of

[038-123] moonlight; her eye became humid; her mouth contracted, like that

[038-124] of a person on the point of weeping. A moment later, she laid her

[038-125] finger on her lips, and made a sign to Mahiette to draw near and

[038-126] look.

[038-127] Mahiette, much touched, stepped up in silence, on tiptoe, as

[038-128] though approaching the bedside of a dying person.

[038-129] It was, in fact, a melancholy spectacle which presented itself to

[038-130] the eyes of the two women, as they gazed through the grating of

[038-131] the Rat-Hole, neither stirring nor breathing.

[038-132] The cell was small, broader than it was long, with an arched

[038-133] ceiling, and viewed from within, it bore a considerable

[038-134] resemblance to the interior of a huge bishop’s mitre. On the bare

[038-135] flagstones which formed the floor, in one corner, a woman was

[038-136] sitting, or rather, crouching. Her chin rested on her knees,

[038-137] which her crossed arms pressed forcibly to her breast. Thus

[038-138] doubled up, clad in a brown sack, which enveloped her entirely in

[038-139] large folds, her long, gray hair pulled over in front, falling

[038-140] over her face and along her legs nearly to her feet, she

[038-141] presented, at the first glance, only a strange form outlined

[038-142] against the dark background of the cell, a sort of dusky

[038-143] triangle, which the ray of daylight falling through the opening,

[038-144] cut roughly into two shades, the one sombre, the other

[038-145] illuminated. It was one of those spectres, half light, half

[038-146] shadow, such as one beholds in dreams and in the extraordinary

[038-147] work of Goya, pale, motionless, sinister, crouching over a tomb,

[038-148] or leaning against the grating of a prison cell.

[038-149] It was neither a woman, nor a man, nor a living being, nor a

[038-150] definite form; it was a figure, a sort of vision, in which the

[038-151] real and the fantastic intersected each other, like darkness and

[038-152] day. It was with difficulty that one distinguished, beneath her

[038-153] hair which spread to the ground, a gaunt and severe profile; her

[038-154] dress barely allowed the extremity of a bare foot to escape,

[038-155] which contracted on the hard, cold pavement. The little of human

[038-156] form of which one caught a sight beneath this envelope of

[038-157] mourning, caused a shudder.

[038-158] That figure, which one might have supposed to be riveted to the

[038-159] flagstones, appeared to possess neither movement, nor thought,

[038-160] nor breath. Lying, in January, in that thin, linen sack, lying on

[038-161] a granite floor, without fire, in the gloom of a cell whose

[038-162] oblique air-hole allowed only the cold breeze, but never the sun,

[038-163] to enter from without, she did not appear to suffer or even to

[038-164] think. One would have said that she had turned to stone with the

[038-165] cell, ice with the season. Her hands were clasped, her eyes

[038-166] fixed. At first sight one took her for a spectre; at the second,

[038-167] for a statue.

[038-168] Nevertheless, at intervals, her blue lips half opened to admit a

[038-169] breath, and trembled, but as dead and as mechanical as the leaves

[038-170] which the wind sweeps aside.

[038-171] Nevertheless, from her dull eyes there escaped a look, an

[038-172] ineffable look, a profound, lugubrious, imperturbable look,

[038-173] incessantly fixed upon a corner of the cell which could not be

[038-174] seen from without; a gaze which seemed to fix all the sombre

[038-175] thoughts of that soul in distress upon some mysterious object.

[038-176] Such was the creature who had received, from her habitation, the

[038-177] name of the “recluse”; and, from her garment, the name of “the

[038-178] sacked nun.”

[038-179] The three women, for Gervaise had rejoined Mahiette and Oudarde,

[038-180] gazed through the window. Their heads intercepted the feeble

[038-181] light in the cell, without the wretched being whom they thus

[038-182] deprived of it seeming to pay any attention to them. “Do not let

[038-183] us trouble her,” said Oudarde, in a low voice, “she is in her

[038-184] ecstasy; she is praying.”

[038-185] Meanwhile, Mahiette was gazing with ever-increasing anxiety at

[038-186] that wan, withered, dishevelled head, and her eyes filled with

[038-187] tears. “This is very singular,” she murmured.

[038-188] She thrust her head through the bars, and succeeded in casting a

[038-189] glance at the corner where the gaze of the unhappy woman was

[038-190] immovably riveted.

[038-191] When she withdrew her head from the window, her countenance was

[038-192] inundated with tears.

[038-193] “What do you call that woman?” she asked Oudarde.

[038-194] Oudarde replied,—

[038-195] “We call her Sister Gudule.”

[038-196] “And I,” returned Mahiette, “call her Paquette la Chantefleurie.”

[038-197] Then, laying her finger on her lips, she motioned to the

[038-198] astounded Oudarde to thrust her head through the window and look.

[038-199] Oudarde looked and beheld, in the corner where the eyes of the

[038-200] recluse were fixed in that sombre ecstasy, a tiny shoe of pink

[038-201] satin, embroidered with a thousand fanciful designs in gold and

[038-202] silver.

[038-203] Gervaise looked after Oudarde, and then the three women, gazing

[038-204] upon the unhappy mother, began to weep.

[038-205] But neither their looks nor their tears disturbed the recluse.

[038-206] Her hands remained clasped; her lips mute; her eyes fixed; and

[038-207] that little shoe, thus gazed at, broke the heart of any one who

[038-208] knew her history.

[038-209] The three women had not yet uttered a single word; they dared not

[038-210] speak, even in a low voice. This deep silence, this deep grief,

[038-211] this profound oblivion in which everything had disappeared except

[038-212] one thing, produced upon them the effect of the grand altar at

[038-213] Christmas or Easter. They remained silent, they meditated, they

[038-214] were ready to kneel. It seemed to them that they were ready to

[038-215] enter a church on the day of Tenebræ.
