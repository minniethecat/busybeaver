# Oudarde Musnier

Oudarde Musnier is introduced with a characteristic Hugo epithet: "that
fine, big Mademoiselle Oudarde" [003-012]. Plump, opinionated, and quick
of tongue, she is the most vivid of the three [[paris|Paris]]ian women who gather
at the Tour-Roland to hear [[mahiette|Mahiette]]'s story of [[sister-gudule|Paquette la Chantefleurie]].
Her surname — Musnier — links her to the merchant family that includes
[[andry-musnier]], placing her in the network of the [[paris|Paris]]ian bourgeoisie
that Hugo populates his novel with [036-042].

## Role in the Novel

Oudarde serves as the skeptical, practical voice in the Tour-Roland trio.
Where [[gervaise|Gervaise]] is curious and [[mahiette|Mahiette]] is the storyteller, Oudarde is
the foil: she questions, she objects, she requires evidence. Her sharpness
— "replied Oudarde dryly" [036-076], "retorted Oudarde sharply" [036-085]
— provides the tension that keeps the embedded narrative from becoming
mere sentiment. She is the reader's surrogate for skepticism, and her
gradual capitulation to the story's emotional power is the measure of its
force.

## Biography

### A Bourgeois Woman

Oudarde Musnier belongs to the prosperous [[paris|Paris]]ian bourgeoisie. Her
title — "Demoiselle" — and her surname place her in the merchant-
artisan class that constituted the economic backbone of [[paris|medieval Paris]].
The Musnier name appears in multiple contexts across the novel: Andry
Musnier in the [[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] crowd, an establishment on the Rue
Madame, and Oudarde herself among the citizen women — suggesting a
family of substance and wide connections [038-100].

### The Tour-Roland Scene

Oudarde is present when [[mahiette|Mahiette]], visiting from Reims, recognizes the
recluse of the Rat-Hole. While [[gervaise|Gervaise]] eagerly questions and [[mahiette|Mahiette]]
narrates, Oudarde provides a counterpoint of practical concern: she notes
the danger of alarming the recluse [038-115], she questions the logic of
the [[marginalization|gypsy]] abduction [037-026], she expresses the conventional piety and
prejudice of her class. Her "dry" and "sharp" responses [036-076] [036-085]
are the voice of bourgeois common sense encountering a story that defies
common sense.

### The Bourgeois Gaze

Oudarde's perspective is consistently that of the respectable citizen:
she is horrified by the gypsies, satisfied by conventional explanations,
and ultimately moved despite herself. When she and [[gervaise|Gervaise]] rejoin
[[mahiette|Mahiette]] after the latter's breathless flight [036-131], the moment
captures the collective response of ordinary women to extraordinary
events — a mixture of curiosity, fear, and the instinct to close ranks.

### Fate

Oudarde disappears from the narrative after the Tour-Roland scene. Her
world — the world of bourgeois domesticity and neighborhood gossip —
continues unchanged while the novel's central characters hurtle toward
tragedy.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Oudarde is defined by her physical presence ("fine, big") and her sharp
tongue. She is not cruel but is capable of sharpness; not incurious but
skeptical. Her "dry" responses [036-076] suggest a woman who has learned
to distrust sentiment, and her "sharp" retorts [036-085] a woman who
expects to be heard. In a novel of grand [[love-and-desire|passion]]s, she represents the
everyday: the citizen woman whose emotional range runs from curiosity
to disapproval, and whose world is bounded by neighborhood and [[religion|church]].

### Relationships

- **[[mahiette]]**: Oudarde listens to [[mahiette|Mahiette]]'s story with a mixture of
  skepticism and fascination, her resistance gradually worn down by the
  tale's accumulating horror.

- **[[gervaise]]**: The two [[paris|Paris]]ian women function as a pair, their
  contrasting responses — Oudarde sharp, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] curious — creating the
  dialogic texture of the scene [036-131].

- **[[sister-gudule]]**: Oudarde does not know that the recluse before her
  is [[sister-gudule|Paquette la Chantefleurie]], a fact that gives her every sharp remark
  an undertone of tragic irony.

- **[[andry-musnier]]**: The shared surname suggests a family connection —
  perhaps husband or brother — linking Oudarde's domestic world to the
  wider [[paris|Paris]]ian citizenry.

## Significance

Oudarde Musnier is part of the chorus of ordinary voices that Hugo uses
to ground the novel's more fantastic elements in the texture of everyday
life. Her skepticism, her bourgeois prejudices, and her ultimate emotional
surrender to the power of storytelling model the reader's own journey
through the Tour-Roland scene. She is, in Hugo's schema, the voice of the
city itself — practical, opinionated, and moved despite itself.
