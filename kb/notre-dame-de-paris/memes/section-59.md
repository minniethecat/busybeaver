---[059-001] “Well, sister, rejoice,” said the priest, icy as a sepulchral

[059-002] statue; “that is the one whom you are about to see die.”

[059-003] His head fell upon his bosom and he moved slowly away.

[059-004] The recluse writhed her arms with joy.

[059-005] “I predicted it for her, that she would ascend thither! Thanks,

[059-006] priest!” she cried.

[059-007] And she began to pace up and down with long strides before the

[059-008] grating of her window, her hair dishevelled, her eyes flashing,

[059-009] with her shoulder striking against the wall, with the wild air of

[059-010] a female wolf in a cage, who has long been famished, and who

[059-011] feels the hour for her repast drawing near.

[059-012] CHAPTER VI. THREE HUMAN HEARTS DIFFERENTLY CONSTRUCTED.

[059-013] Phœbus was not dead, however. Men of that stamp die hard. When

[059-014] Master Philippe Lheulier, advocate extraordinary of the king, had

[059-015] said to poor Esmeralda; “He is dying,” it was an error or a jest.

[059-016] When the archdeacon had repeated to the condemned girl; “He is

[059-017] dead,” the fact is that he knew nothing about it, but that he

[059-018] believed it, that he counted on it, that he did not doubt it,

[059-019] that he devoutly hoped it. It would have been too hard for him to

[059-020] give favorable news of his rival to the woman whom he loved. Any

[059-021] man would have done the same in his place.

[059-022] It was not that Phœbus’s wound had not been serious, but it had

[059-023] not been as much so as the archdeacon believed. The physician, to

[059-024] whom the soldiers of the watch had carried him at the first

[059-025] moment, had feared for his life during the space of a week, and

[059-026] had even told him so in Latin. But youth had gained the upper

[059-027] hand; and, as frequently happens, in spite of prognostications

[059-028] and diagnoses, nature had amused herself by saving the sick man

[059-029] under the physician’s very nose. It was while he was still lying

[059-030] on the leech’s pallet that he had submitted to the interrogations

[059-031] of Philippe Lheulier and the official inquisitors, which had

[059-032] annoyed him greatly. Hence, one fine morning, feeling himself

[059-033] better, he had left his golden spurs with the leech as payment,

[059-034] and had slipped away. This had not, however, interfered with the

[059-035] progress of the affair. Justice, at that epoch, troubled itself

[059-036] very little about the clearness and definiteness of a criminal

[059-037] suit. Provided that the accused was hung, that was all that was

[059-038] necessary. Now the judge had plenty of proofs against la

[059-039] Esmeralda. They had supposed Phœbus to be dead, and that was the

[059-040] end of the matter.

[059-041] Phœbus, on his side, had not fled far. He had simply rejoined his

[059-042] company in garrison at Queue-en-Brie, in the Isle-de-France, a

[059-043] few stages from Paris.

[059-044] After all, it did not please him in the least to appear in this

[059-045] suit. He had a vague feeling that he should play a ridiculous

[059-046] figure in it. On the whole, he did not know what to think of the

[059-047] whole affair. Superstitious, and not given to devoutness, like

[059-048] every soldier who is only a soldier, when he came to question

[059-049] himself about this adventure, he did not feel assured as to the

[059-050] goat, as to the singular fashion in which he had met La

[059-051] Esmeralda, as to the no less strange manner in which she had

[059-052] allowed him to divine her love, as to her character as a gypsy,

[059-053] and lastly, as to the surly monk. He perceived in all these

[059-054] incidents much more magic than love, probably a sorceress,

[059-055] perhaps the devil; a comedy, in short, or to speak in the

[059-056] language of that day, a very disagreeable mystery, in which he

[059-057] played a very awkward part, the role of blows and derision. The

[059-058] captain was quite put out of countenance about it; he experienced

[059-059] that sort of shame which our La Fontaine has so admirably

[059-060] defined,—

[059-061] Ashamed as a fox who has been caught by a fowl.

[059-062] Moreover, he hoped that the affair would not get noised abroad,

[059-063] that his name would hardly be pronounced in it, and that in any

[059-064] case it would not go beyond the courts of the Tournelle. In this

[059-065] he was not mistaken, there was then no _Gazette des Tribunaux;_

[059-066] and as not a week passed which had not its counterfeiter to boil,

[059-067] or its witch to hang, or its heretic to burn, at some one of the

[059-068] innumerable justices of Paris, people were so accustomed to

[059-069] seeing in all the squares the ancient feudal Themis, bare armed,

[059-070] with sleeves stripped up, performing her duty at the gibbets, the

[059-071] ladders, and the pillories, that they hardly paid any heed to it.

[059-072] Fashionable society of that day hardly knew the name of the

[059-073] victim who passed by at the corner of the street, and it was the

[059-074] populace at the most who regaled themselves with this coarse

[059-075] fare. An execution was an habitual incident of the public

[059-076] highways, like the braising-pan of the baker or the

[059-077] slaughter-house of the knacker. The executioner was only a sort

[059-078] of butcher of a little deeper dye than the rest.

[059-079] Hence Phœbus’s mind was soon at ease on the score of the

[059-080] enchantress Esmeralda, or Similar, as he called her, concerning

[059-081] the blow from the dagger of the Bohemian or of the surly monk (it

[059-082] mattered little which to him), and as to the issue of the trial.

[059-083] But as soon as his heart was vacant in that direction,

[059-084] Fleur-de-Lys returned to it. Captain Phœbus’s heart, like the

[059-085] physics of that day, abhorred a vacuum.

[059-086] Queue-en-Brie was a very insipid place to stay at then, a village

[059-087] of farriers, and cow-girls with chapped hands, a long line of

[059-088] poor dwellings and thatched cottages, which borders the grand

[059-089] road on both sides for half a league; a tail (queue), in short,

[059-090] as its name imports.

[059-091] Fleur-de-Lys was his last passion but one, a pretty girl, a

[059-092] charming dowry; accordingly, one fine morning, quite cured, and

[059-093] assuming that, after the lapse of two months, the Bohemian affair

[059-094] must be completely finished and forgotten, the amorous cavalier

[059-095] arrived on a prancing horse at the door of the Gondelaurier

[059-096] mansion.

[059-097] He paid no attention to a tolerably numerous rabble which had

[059-098] assembled in the Place du Parvis, before the portal of

[059-099] Notre-Dame; he remembered that it was the month of May; he

[059-100] supposed that it was some procession, some Pentecost, some

[059-101] festival, hitched his horse to the ring at the door, and gayly

[059-102] ascended the stairs to his beautiful betrothed.

[059-103] She was alone with her mother.

[059-104] The scene of the witch, her goat, her cursed alphabet, and

[059-105] Phœbus’s long absences, still weighed on Fleur-de-Lys’s heart.

[059-106] Nevertheless, when she beheld her captain enter, she thought him

[059-107] so handsome, his doublet so new, his baldrick so shining, and his

[059-108] air so impassioned, that she blushed with pleasure. The noble

[059-109] damsel herself was more charming than ever. Her magnificent blond

[059-110] hair was plaited in a ravishing manner, she was dressed entirely

[059-111] in that sky blue which becomes fair people so well, a bit of

[059-112] coquetry which she had learned from Colombe, and her eyes were

[059-113] swimming in that languor of love which becomes them still better.

[059-114] Phœbus, who had seen nothing in the line of beauty, since he left

[059-115] the village maids of Queue-en-Brie, was intoxicated with

[059-116] Fleur-de-Lys, which imparted to our officer so eager and gallant

[059-117] an air, that his peace was immediately made. Madame de

[059-118] Gondelaurier herself, still maternally seated in her big

[059-119] arm-chair, had not the heart to scold him. As for Fleur-de-Lys’s

[059-120] reproaches, they expired in tender cooings.

[059-121] The young girl was seated near the window still embroidering her

[059-122] grotto of Neptune. The captain was leaning over the back of her

[059-123] chair, and she was addressing her caressing reproaches to him in

[059-124] a low voice.

[059-125] “What has become of you these two long months, wicked man?”

[059-126] “I swear to you,” replied Phœbus, somewhat embarrassed by the

[059-127] question, “that you are beautiful enough to set an archbishop to

[059-128] dreaming.”

[059-129] She could not repress a smile.

[059-130] “Good, good, sir. Let my beauty alone and answer my question. A

[059-131] fine beauty, in sooth!”

[059-132] “Well, my dear cousin, I was recalled to the garrison.

[059-133] “And where is that, if you please? and why did not you come to

[059-134] say farewell?”

[059-135] “At Queue-en-Brie.”

[059-136] Phœbus was delighted with the first question, which helped him to

[059-137] avoid the second.

[059-138] “But that is quite close by, monsieur. Why did you not come to

[059-139] see me a single time?”

[059-140] Here Phœbus was rather seriously embarrassed.

[059-141] “Because—the service—and then, charming cousin, I have been ill.”

[059-142] “Ill!” she repeated in alarm.

[059-143] “Yes, wounded!”

[059-144] “Wounded!”

[059-145] The poor child was completely upset.

[059-146] “Oh! do not be frightened at that,” said Phœbus, carelessly, “it

[059-147] was nothing. A quarrel, a sword cut; what is that to you?”

[059-148] “What is that to me?” exclaimed Fleur-de-Lys, raising her

[059-149] beautiful eyes filled with tears. “Oh! you do not say what you

[059-150] think when you speak thus. What sword cut was that? I wish to

[059-151] know all.”

[059-152] “Well, my dear fair one, I had a falling out with Mahé Fédy, you

[059-153] know? the lieutenant of Saint-Germain-en-Laye, and we ripped open

[059-154] a few inches of skin for each other. That is all.”

[059-155] The mendacious captain was perfectly well aware that an affair of

[059-156] honor always makes a man stand well in the eyes of a woman. In

[059-157] fact, Fleur-de-Lys looked him full in the face, all agitated with

[059-158] fear, pleasure, and admiration. Still, she was not completely

[059-159] reassured.

[059-160] “Provided that you are wholly cured, my Phœbus!” said she. “I do

[059-161] not know your Mahé Fédy, but he is a villanous man. And whence

[059-162] arose this quarrel?”

[059-163] Here Phœbus, whose imagination was endowed with but mediocre

[059-164] power of creation, began to find himself in a quandary as to a

[059-165] means of extricating himself for his prowess.

[059-166] “Oh! how do I know?—a mere nothing, a horse, a remark! Fair

[059-167] cousin,” he exclaimed, for the sake of changing the conversation,

[059-168] “what noise is this in the Cathedral Square?”

[059-169] He approached the window.

[059-170] “Oh! _Mon Dieu_, fair cousin, how many people there are on the

[059-171] Place!”

[059-172] “I know not,” said Fleur-de-Lys; “it appears that a witch is to

[059-173] do penance this morning before the church, and thereafter to be

[059-174] hung.”

[059-175] The captain was so thoroughly persuaded that la Esmeralda’s

[059-176] affair was concluded, that he was but little disturbed by

[059-177] Fleur-de-Lys’s words. Still, he asked her one or two questions.

[059-178] “What is the name of this witch?”

[059-179] “I do not know,” she replied.

[059-180] “And what is she said to have done?”

[059-181] She shrugged her white shoulders.

[059-182] “I know not.”

[059-183] “Oh, _mon Dieu Jésus!_” said her mother; “there are so many

[059-184] witches nowadays that I dare say they burn them without knowing

[059-185] their names. One might as well seek the name of every cloud in

[059-186] the sky. After all, one may be tranquil. The good God keeps his

[059-187] register.” Here the venerable dame rose and came to the window.

[059-188] “Good Lord! you are right, Phœbus,” said she. “The rabble is

[059-189] indeed great. There are people on all the roofs, blessed be God!

[059-190] Do you know, Phœbus, this reminds me of my best days. The

[059-191] entrance of King Charles VII., when, also, there were many

[059-192] people. I no longer remember in what year that was. When I speak

[059-193] of this to you, it produces upon you the effect,—does it not?—the

[059-194] effect of something very old, and upon me of something very

[059-195] young. Oh! the crowd was far finer than at the present day. They

[059-196] even stood upon the machicolations of the Porte Sainte-Antoine.

[059-197] The king had the queen on a pillion, and after their highnesses

[059-198] came all the ladies mounted behind all the lords. I remember that

[059-199] they laughed loudly, because beside Amanyon de Garlande, who was

[059-200] very short of stature, there rode the Sire Matefelon, a chevalier

[059-201] of gigantic size, who had killed heaps of English. It was very

[059-202] fine. A procession of all the gentlemen of France, with their

[059-203] oriflammes waving red before the eye. There were some with

[059-204] pennons and some with banners. How can I tell? the Sire de Calan

[059-205] with a pennon; Jean de Châteaumorant with a banner; the Sire de

[059-206] Courcy with a banner, and a more ample one than any of the others

[059-207] except the Duc de Bourbon. Alas! ’tis a sad thing to think that

[059-208] all that has existed and exists no longer!”
