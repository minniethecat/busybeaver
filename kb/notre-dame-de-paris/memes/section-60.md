---[060-001] The two lovers were not listening to the venerable dowager.

[060-002] Phœbus had returned and was leaning on the back of his

[060-003] betrothed’s chair, a charming post whence his libertine glance

[060-004] plunged into all the openings of Fleur-de-Lys’s gorget. This

[060-005] gorget gaped so conveniently, and allowed him to see so many

[060-006] exquisite things and to divine so many more, that Phœbus, dazzled

[060-007] by this skin with its gleams of satin, said to himself, “How can

[060-008] any one love anything but a fair skin?”

[060-009] Both were silent. The young girl raised sweet, enraptured eyes to

[060-010] him from time to time, and their hair mingled in a ray of spring

[060-011] sunshine.

[060-012] “Phœbus,” said Fleur-de-Lys suddenly, in a low voice, “we are to

[060-013] be married three months hence; swear to me that you have never

[060-014] loved any other woman than myself.”

[060-015] “I swear it, fair angel!” replied Phœbus, and his passionate

[060-016] glances aided the sincere tone of his voice in convincing

[060-017] Fleur-de-Lys.

[060-018] Meanwhile, the good mother, charmed to see the betrothed pair on

[060-019] terms of such perfect understanding, had just quitted the

[060-020] apartment to attend to some domestic matter; Phœbus observed it,

[060-021] and this so emboldened the adventurous captain that very strange

[060-022] ideas mounted to his brain. Fleur-de-Lys loved him, he was her

[060-023] betrothed; she was alone with him; his former taste for her had

[060-024] re-awakened, not with all its freshness but with all its ardor;

[060-025] after all, there is no great harm in tasting one’s wheat while it

[060-026] is still in the blade; I do not know whether these ideas passed

[060-027] through his mind, but one thing is certain, that Fleur-de-Lys was

[060-028] suddenly alarmed by the expression of his glance. She looked

[060-029] round and saw that her mother was no longer there.

[060-030] “Good heavens!” said she, blushing and uneasy, “how very warm I

[060-031] am!”

[060-032] “I think, in fact,” replied Phœbus, “that it cannot be far from

[060-033] midday. The sun is troublesome. We need only lower the curtains.”

[060-034] “No, no,” exclaimed the poor little thing, “on the contrary, I

[060-035] need air.”

[060-036] And like a fawn who feels the breath of the pack of hounds, she

[060-037] rose, ran to the window, opened it, and rushed upon the balcony.

[060-038] Phœbus, much discomfited, followed her.

[060-039] The Place du Parvis Notre-Dame, upon which the balcony looked, as

[060-040] the reader knows, presented at that moment a singular and

[060-041] sinister spectacle which caused the fright of the timid

[060-042] Fleur-de-Lys to change its nature.

[060-043] An immense crowd, which overflowed into all the neighboring

[060-044] streets, encumbered the Place, properly speaking. The little

[060-045] wall, breast high, which surrounded the Place, would not have

[060-046] sufficed to keep it free had it not been lined with a thick hedge

[060-047] of sergeants and hackbuteers, culverines in hand. Thanks to this

[060-048] thicket of pikes and arquebuses, the Parvis was empty. Its

[060-049] entrance was guarded by a force of halberdiers with the armorial

[060-050] bearings of the bishop. The large doors of the church were

[060-051] closed, and formed a contrast with the innumerable windows on the

[060-052] Place, which, open to their very gables, allowed a view of

[060-053] thousands of heads heaped up almost like the piles of bullets in

[060-054] a park of artillery.

[060-055] The surface of this rabble was dingy, dirty, earthy. The

[060-056] spectacle which it was expecting was evidently one of the sort

[060-057] which possess the privilege of bringing out and calling together

[060-058] the vilest among the populace. Nothing is so hideous as the noise

[060-059] which was made by that swarm of yellow caps and dirty heads. In

[060-060] that throng there were more laughs than cries, more women than

[060-061] men.

[060-062] From time to time, a sharp and vibrating voice pierced the

[060-063] general clamor.

[060-064] “Ohé! Mahiet Baliffre! Is she to be hung yonder?”

[060-065] “Fool! ’tis here that she is to make her apology in her shift!

[060-066] the good God is going to cough Latin in her face! That is always

[060-067] done here, at midday. If ’tis the gallows that you wish, go to

[060-068] the Grève.”

[060-069] “I will go there, afterwards.”

[060-070] “Tell me, la Boucanbry? Is it true that she has refused a

[060-071] confessor?”

[060-072] “It appears so, La Bechaigne.”

[060-073] “You see what a pagan she is!”

[060-074] “’Tis the custom, monsieur. The bailiff of the courts is bound to

[060-075] deliver the malefactor ready judged for execution if he be a

[060-076] layman, to the provost of Paris; if a clerk, to the official of

[060-077] the bishopric.”

[060-078] “Thank you, sir.”

[060-079] “Oh, God!” said Fleur-de-Lys, “the poor creature!”

[060-080] This thought filled with sadness the glance which she cast upon

[060-081] the populace. The captain, much more occupied with her than with

[060-082] that pack of the rabble, was amorously rumpling her girdle

[060-083] behind. She turned round, entreating and smiling.

[060-084] “Please let me alone, Phœbus! If my mother were to return, she

[060-085] would see your hand!”

[060-086] At that moment, midday rang slowly out from the clock of

[060-087] Notre-Dame. A murmur of satisfaction broke out in the crowd. The

[060-088] last vibration of the twelfth stroke had hardly died away when

[060-089] all heads surged like the waves beneath a squall, and an immense

[060-090] shout went up from the pavement, the windows, and the roofs,

[060-091] “There she is!”

[060-092] Fleur-de-Lys pressed her hands to her eyes, that she might not

[060-093] see.

[060-094] “Charming girl,” said Phœbus, “do you wish to withdraw?”

[060-095] “No,” she replied; and she opened through curiosity, the eyes

[060-096] which she had closed through fear.

[060-097] A tumbrel drawn by a stout Norman horse, and all surrounded by

[060-098] cavalry in violet livery with white crosses, had just debouched

[060-099] upon the Place through the Rue Saint-Pierre-aux-Bœufs. The

[060-100] sergeants of the watch were clearing a passage for it through the

[060-101] crowd, by stout blows from their clubs. Beside the cart rode

[060-102] several officers of justice and police, recognizable by their

[060-103] black costume and their awkwardness in the saddle. Master Jacques

[060-104] Charmolue paraded at their head.

[060-105] In the fatal cart sat a young girl with her arms tied behind her

[060-106] back, and with no priest beside her. She was in her shift; her

[060-107] long black hair (the fashion then was to cut it off only at the

[060-108] foot of the gallows) fell in disorder upon her half-bared throat

[060-109] and shoulders.

[060-110] Athwart that waving hair, more glossy than the plumage of a

[060-111] raven, a thick, rough, gray rope was visible, twisted and

[060-112] knotted, chafing her delicate collar-bones and twining round the

[060-113] charming neck of the poor girl, like an earthworm round a flower.

[060-114] Beneath that rope glittered a tiny amulet ornamented with bits of

[060-115] green glass, which had been left to her no doubt, because nothing

[060-116] is refused to those who are about to die. The spectators in the

[060-117] windows could see in the bottom of the cart her naked legs which

[060-118] she strove to hide beneath her, as by a final feminine instinct.

[060-119] At her feet lay a little goat, bound. The condemned girl held

[060-120] together with her teeth her imperfectly fastened shift. One would

[060-121] have said that she suffered still more in her misery from being

[060-122] thus exposed almost naked to the eyes of all. Alas! modesty is

[060-123] not made for such shocks.

[060-124] “Jesus!” said Fleur-de-Lys hastily to the captain. “Look fair

[060-125] cousin, ’tis that wretched Bohemian with the goat.”

[060-126] So saying, she turned to Phœbus. His eyes were fixed on the

[060-127] tumbrel. He was very pale.

[060-128] “What Bohemian with the goat?” he stammered.

[060-129] “What!” resumed Fleur-de-Lys, “do you not remember?”

[060-130] Phœbus interrupted her.

[060-131] “I do not know what you mean.”

[060-132] He made a step to re-enter the room, but Fleur-de-Lys, whose

[060-133] jealousy, previously so vividly aroused by this same gypsy, had

[060-134] just been re-awakened, Fleur-de-Lys gave him a look full of

[060-135] penetration and distrust. She vaguely recalled at that moment

[060-136] having heard of a captain mixed up in the trial of that witch.

[060-137] “What is the matter with you?” she said to Phœbus, “one would

[060-138] say, that this woman had disturbed you.”

[060-139] Phœbus forced a sneer,—

[060-140] “Me! Not the least in the world! Ah! yes, certainly!”

[060-141] “Remain, then!” she continued imperiously, “and let us see the

[060-142] end.”

[060-143] The unlucky captain was obliged to remain. He was somewhat

[060-144] reassured by the fact that the condemned girl never removed her

[060-145] eyes from the bottom of the cart. It was but too surely la

[060-146] Esmeralda. In this last stage of opprobrium and misfortune, she

[060-147] was still beautiful; her great black eyes appeared still larger,

[060-148] because of the emaciation of her cheeks; her pale profile was

[060-149] pure and sublime. She resembled what she had been, in the same

[060-150] degree that a virgin by Masaccio, resembles a virgin of

[060-151] Raphael,—weaker, thinner, more delicate.

[060-152] Moreover, there was nothing in her which was not shaken in some

[060-153] sort, and which with the exception of her modesty, she did not

[060-154] let go at will, so profoundly had she been broken by stupor and

[060-155] despair. Her body bounded at every jolt of the tumbrel like a

[060-156] dead or broken thing; her gaze was dull and imbecile. A tear was

[060-157] still visible in her eyes, but motionless and frozen, so to

[060-158] speak.

[060-159] Meanwhile, the lugubrious cavalcade has traversed the crowd amid

[060-160] cries of joy and curious attitudes. But as a faithful historian,

[060-161] we must state that on beholding her so beautiful, so depressed,

[060-162] many were moved with pity, even among the hardest of them.

[060-163] The tumbrel had entered the Parvis.

[060-164] It halted before the central portal. The escort ranged themselves

[060-165] in line on both sides. The crowd became silent, and, in the midst

[060-166] of this silence full of anxiety and solemnity, the two leaves of

[060-167] the grand door swung back, as of themselves, on their hinges,

[060-168] which gave a creak like the sound of a fife. Then there became

[060-169] visible in all its length, the deep, gloomy church, hung in

[060-170] black, sparely lighted with a few candles gleaming afar off on

[060-171] the principal altar, opened in the midst of the Place which was

[060-172] dazzling with light, like the mouth of a cavern. At the very

[060-173] extremity, in the gloom of the apse, a gigantic silver cross was

[060-174] visible against a black drapery which hung from the vault to the

[060-175] pavement. The whole nave was deserted. But a few heads of priests

[060-176] could be seen moving confusedly in the distant choir stalls, and,

[060-177] at the moment when the great door opened, there escaped from the

[060-178] church a loud, solemn, and monotonous chanting, which cast over

[060-179] the head of the condemned girl, in gusts, fragments of melancholy

[060-180] psalms,—

[060-181] “_Non timebo millia populi circumdantis me: exsurge, Domine;

[060-182] salvum me fac, Deus!_”

[060-183] “_Salvum me fac, Deus, quoniam intraverunt aquæ usque ad animam

[060-184] meam_.

[060-185] “_Infixus sum in limo profundi; et non est substantia_.”

[060-186] At the same time, another voice, separate from the choir, intoned

[060-187] upon the steps of the chief altar, this melancholy offertory,—

[060-188] “_Qui verbum meum audit, et credit ei qui misit me, habet vitam

[060-189] æternam et in judicium non venit; sed transit a morte in

[060-190] vitam._”[46]

[060-191] This chant, which a few old men buried in the gloom sang from

[060-192] afar over that beautiful creature, full of youth and life,

[060-193] caressed by the warm air of spring, inundated with sunlight was

[060-194] the mass for the dead.

[060-195] The people listened devoutly.

[060-196] The unhappy girl seemed to lose her sight and her consciousness

[060-197] in the obscure interior of the church. Her white lips moved as

[060-198] though in prayer, and the headsman’s assistant who approached to

[060-199] assist her to alight from the cart, heard her repeating this word

[060-200] in a low tone,—“Phœbus.”

[060-201] They untied her hands, made her alight, accompanied by her goat,

[060-202] which had also been unbound, and which bleated with joy at

[060-203] finding itself free: and they made her walk barefoot on the hard

[060-204] pavement to the foot of the steps leading to the door. The rope

[060-205] about her neck trailed behind her. One would have said it was a

[060-206] serpent following her.

[060-207] Then the chanting in the church ceased. A great golden cross and

[060-208] a row of wax candles began to move through the gloom. The

[060-209] halberds of the motley beadles clanked; and, a few moments later,

[060-210] a long procession of priests in chasubles, and deacons in

[060-211] dalmatics, marched gravely towards the condemned girl, as they

[060-212] drawled their song, spread out before her view and that of the

[060-213] crowd. But her glance rested on the one who marched at the head,

[060-214] immediately after the cross-bearer.
