---[013-001] “Murder! murder!” shrieked the unhappy gypsy.

[013-002] “Halt, rascals, and yield me that wench!” suddenly shouted in a

[013-003] voice of thunder, a cavalier who appeared suddenly from a

[013-004] neighboring square.

[013-005] It was a captain of the king’s archers, armed from head to foot,

[013-006] with his sword in his hand.

[013-007] He tore the gypsy from the arms of the dazed Quasimodo, threw her

[013-008] across his saddle, and at the moment when the terrible hunchback,

[013-009] recovering from his surprise, rushed upon him to regain his prey,

[013-010] fifteen or sixteen archers, who followed their captain closely,

[013-011] made their appearance, with their two-edged swords in their

[013-012] fists. It was a squad of the king’s police, which was making the

[013-013] rounds, by order of Messire Robert d’Estouteville, guard of the

[013-014] provostship of Paris.

[013-015] Quasimodo was surrounded, seized, garroted; he roared, he foamed

[013-016] at the mouth, he bit; and had it been broad daylight, there is no

[013-017] doubt that his face alone, rendered more hideous by wrath, would

[013-018] have put the entire squad to flight. But by night he was deprived

[013-019] of his most formidable weapon, his ugliness.

[013-020] His companion had disappeared during the struggle.

[013-021] The gypsy gracefully raised herself upright upon the officer’s

[013-022] saddle, placed both hands upon the young man’s shoulders, and

[013-023] gazed fixedly at him for several seconds, as though enchanted

[013-024] with his good looks and with the aid which he had just rendered

[013-025] her. Then breaking silence first, she said to him, making her

[013-026] sweet voice still sweeter than usual,—

[013-027] “What is your name, monsieur le gendarme?”

[013-028] “Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers, at your service, my beauty!”

[013-029] replied the officer, drawing himself up.

[013-030] “Thanks,” said she.

[013-031] And while Captain Phœbus was turning up his moustache in

[013-032] Burgundian fashion, she slipped from the horse, like an arrow

[013-033] falling to earth, and fled.

[013-034] A flash of lightning would have vanished less quickly.

[013-035] “Nombrill of the Pope!” said the captain, causing Quasimodo’s

[013-036] straps to be drawn tighter, “I should have preferred to keep the

[013-037] wench.”

[013-038] “What would you have, captain?” said one gendarme. “The warbler

[013-039] has fled, and the bat remains.”

[013-040] CHAPTER V. RESULT OF THE DANGERS.

[013-041] Gringoire, thoroughly stunned by his fall, remained on the

[013-042] pavement in front of the Holy Virgin at the street corner. Little

[013-043] by little, he regained his senses; at first, for several minutes,

[013-044] he was floating in a sort of half-somnolent revery, which was not

[013-045] without its charm, in which æriel figures of the gypsy and her

[013-046] goat were coupled with Quasimodo’s heavy fist. This state lasted

[013-047] but a short time. A decidedly vivid sensation of cold in the part

[013-048] of his body which was in contact with the pavement, suddenly

[013-049] aroused him and caused his spirit to return to the surface.

[013-050] “Whence comes this chill?” he said abruptly, to himself. He then

[013-051] perceived that he was lying half in the middle of the gutter.

[013-052] “That devil of a hunchbacked cyclops!” he muttered between his

[013-053] teeth; and he tried to rise. But he was too much dazed and

[013-054] bruised; he was forced to remain where he was. Moreover, his hand

[013-055] was tolerably free; he stopped up his nose and resigned himself.

[013-056] “The mud of Paris,” he said to himself—for decidedly he thought

[013-057] that he was sure that the gutter would prove his refuge for the

[013-058] night; and what can one do in a refuge, except dream?—“the mud of

[013-059] Paris is particularly stinking; it must contain a great deal of

[013-060] volatile and nitric salts. That, moreover, is the opinion of

[013-061] Master Nicholas Flamel, and of the alchemists—”

[013-062] The word _alchemists_ suddenly suggested to his mind the idea of

[013-063] Archdeacon Claude Frollo. He recalled the violent scene which he

[013-064] had just witnessed in part; that the gypsy was struggling with

[013-065] two men, that Quasimodo had a companion; and the morose and

[013-066] haughty face of the archdeacon passed confusedly through his

[013-067] memory. “That would be strange!” he said to himself. And on that

[013-068] fact and that basis he began to construct a fantastic edifice of

[013-069] hypothesis, that card-castle of philosophers; then, suddenly

[013-070] returning once more to reality, “Come! I’m freezing!” he

[013-071] ejaculated.

[013-072] The place was, in fact, becoming less and less tenable. Each

[013-073] molecule of the gutter bore away a molecule of heat radiating

[013-074] from Gringoire’s loins, and the equilibrium between the

[013-075] temperature of his body and the temperature of the brook, began

[013-076] to be established in rough fashion.

[013-077] Quite a different annoyance suddenly assailed him. A group of

[013-078] children, those little bare-footed savages who have always roamed

[013-079] the pavements of Paris under the eternal name of _gamins_, and

[013-080] who, when we were also children ourselves, threw stones at all of

[013-081] us in the afternoon, when we came out of school, because our

[013-082] trousers were not torn—a swarm of these young scamps rushed

[013-083] towards the square where Gringoire lay, with shouts and laughter

[013-084] which seemed to pay but little heed to the sleep of the

[013-085] neighbors. They were dragging after them some sort of hideous

[013-086] sack; and the noise of their wooden shoes alone would have roused

[013-087] the dead. Gringoire who was not quite dead yet, half raised

[013-088] himself.

[013-089] “Ohé, Hennequin Dandèche! Ohé, Jehan Pincebourde!” they shouted

[013-090] in deafening tones, “old Eustache Moubon, the merchant at the

[013-091] corner, has just died. We’ve got his straw pallet, we’re going to

[013-092] have a bonfire out of it. It’s the turn of the Flemish to-day!”

[013-093] And behold, they flung the pallet directly upon Gringoire, beside

[013-094] whom they had arrived, without espying him. At the same time, one

[013-095] of them took a handful of straw and set off to light it at the

[013-096] wick of the good Virgin.

[013-097] “S’death!” growled Gringoire, “am I going to be too warm now?”

[013-098] It was a critical moment. He was caught between fire and water;

[013-099] he made a superhuman effort, the effort of a counterfeiter of

[013-100] money who is on the point of being boiled, and who seeks to

[013-101] escape. He rose to his feet, flung aside the straw pallet upon

[013-102] the street urchins, and fled.

[013-103] “Holy Virgin!” shrieked the children; “’tis the merchant’s

[013-104] ghost!”

[013-105] And they fled in their turn.

[013-106] The straw mattress remained master of the field. Belleforêt,

[013-107] Father Le Juge, and Corrozet affirm that it was picked up on the

[013-108] morrow, with great pomp, by the clergy of the quarter, and borne

[013-109] to the treasury of the church of Saint Opportune, where the

[013-110] sacristan, even as late as 1789, earned a tolerably handsome

[013-111] revenue out of the great miracle of the Statue of the Virgin at

[013-112] the corner of the Rue Mauconseil, which had, by its mere

[013-113] presence, on the memorable night between the sixth and seventh of

[013-114] January, 1482, exorcised the defunct Eustache Moubon, who, in

[013-115] order to play a trick on the devil, had at his death maliciously

[013-116] concealed his soul in his straw pallet.

[013-117] CHAPTER VI. THE BROKEN JUG.

[013-118] After having run for some time at the top of his speed, without

[013-119] knowing whither, knocking his head against many a street corner,

[013-120] leaping many a gutter, traversing many an alley, many a court,

[013-121] many a square, seeking flight and passage through all the

[013-122] meanderings of the ancient passages of the Halles, exploring in

[013-123] his panic terror what the fine Latin of the maps calls _tota via,

[013-124] cheminum et viaria_, our poet suddenly halted for lack of breath

[013-125] in the first place, and in the second, because he had been

[013-126] collared, after a fashion, by a dilemma which had just occurred

[013-127] to his mind. “It strikes me, Master Pierre Gringoire,” he said to

[013-128] himself, placing his finger to his brow, “that you are running

[013-129] like a madman. The little scamps are no less afraid of you than

[013-130] you are of them. It strikes me, I say, that you heard the clatter

[013-131] of their wooden shoes fleeing southward, while you were fleeing

[013-132] northward. Now, one of two things, either they have taken flight,

[013-133] and the pallet, which they must have forgotten in their terror,

[013-134] is precisely that hospitable bed in search of which you have been

[013-135] running ever since morning, and which madame the Virgin

[013-136] miraculously sends you, in order to recompense you for having

[013-137] made a morality in her honor, accompanied by triumphs and

[013-138] mummeries; or the children have not taken flight, and in that

[013-139] case they have put the brand to the pallet, and that is precisely

[013-140] the good fire which you need to cheer, dry, and warm you. In

[013-141] either case, good fire or good bed, that straw pallet is a gift

[013-142] from heaven. The blessed Virgin Marie who stands at the corner of

[013-143] the Rue Mauconseil, could only have made Eustache Moubon die for

[013-144] that express purpose; and it is folly on your part to flee thus

[013-145] zigzag, like a Picard before a Frenchman, leaving behind you what

[013-146] you seek before you; and you are a fool!”

[013-147] Then he retraced his steps, and feeling his way and searching,

[013-148] with his nose to the wind and his ears on the alert, he tried to

[013-149] find the blessed pallet again, but in vain. There was nothing to

[013-150] be found but intersections of houses, closed courts, and

[013-151] crossings of streets, in the midst of which he hesitated and

[013-152] doubted incessantly, being more perplexed and entangled in this

[013-153] medley of streets than he would have been even in the labyrinth

[013-154] of the Hôtel des Tournelles. At length he lost patience, and

[013-155] exclaimed solemnly: “Cursed be cross roads! ’tis the devil who

[013-156] has made them in the shape of his pitchfork!”

[013-157] This exclamation afforded him a little solace, and a sort of

[013-158] reddish reflection which he caught sight of at that moment, at

[013-159] the extremity of a long and narrow lane, completed the elevation

[013-160] of his moral tone. “God be praised!” said he, “There it is

[013-161] yonder! There is my pallet burning.” And comparing himself to the

[013-162] pilot who suffers shipwreck by night, “_Salve_,” he added

[013-163] piously, “_salve, maris stella_!”

[013-164] Did he address this fragment of litany to the Holy Virgin, or to

[013-165] the pallet? We are utterly unable to say.

[013-166] He had taken but a few steps in the long street, which sloped

[013-167] downwards, was unpaved, and more and more muddy and steep, when

[013-168] he noticed a very singular thing. It was not deserted; here and

[013-169] there along its extent crawled certain vague and formless masses,

[013-170] all directing their course towards the light which flickered at

[013-171] the end of the street, like those heavy insects which drag along

[013-172] by night, from blade to blade of grass, towards the shepherd’s

[013-173] fire.

[013-174] Nothing renders one so adventurous as not being able to feel the

[013-175] place where one’s pocket is situated. Gringoire continued to

[013-176] advance, and had soon joined that one of the forms which dragged

[013-177] along most indolently, behind the others. On drawing near, he

[013-178] perceived that it was nothing else than a wretched legless

[013-179] cripple in a bowl, who was hopping along on his two hands like a

[013-180] wounded field-spider which has but two legs left. At the moment

[013-181] when he passed close to this species of spider with a human

[013-182] countenance, it raised towards him a lamentable voice: “_La buona

[013-183] mancia, signor! la buona mancia_!”[10]

[013-184] “Deuce take you,” said Gringoire, “and me with you, if I know

[013-185] what you mean!”

[013-186] And he passed on.

[013-187] He overtook another of these itinerant masses, and examined it.

[013-188] It was an impotent man, both halt and crippled, and halt and

[013-189] crippled to such a degree that the complicated system of crutches

[013-190] and wooden legs which sustained him, gave him the air of a

[013-191] mason’s scaffolding on the march. Gringoire, who liked noble and

[013-192] classical comparisons, compared him in thought to the living

[013-193] tripod of Vulcan.

[013-194] This living tripod saluted him as he passed, but stopping his hat

[013-195] on a level with Gringoire’s chin, like a shaving dish, while he

[013-196] shouted in the latter’s ears: “_Señor cabellero, para comprar un

[013-197] pedaso de pan_!”[11]

[013-198] “It appears,” said Gringoire, “that this one can also talk; but

[013-199] ’tis a rude language, and he is more fortunate than I if he

[013-200] understands it.” Then, smiting his brow, in a sudden transition

[013-201] of ideas: “By the way, what the deuce did they mean this morning

[013-202] with their _Esmeralda?_”
