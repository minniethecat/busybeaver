# Les Halles

**Les Halles** was the central marketplace of [[paris|medieval Paris]], a teeming commercial district on the Right Bank. In Hugo's *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, it appears as one of the principal features of the Town district and as the location of a notorious pillory used for public punishments (010-122, 039-150).

## Location and Character

The Halles occupied a central position in the Town district on the Right Bank. Hugo describes it as part of the trio of key civic spaces: "[[place-de-greve|the Grève]], the Halles, the Place Dauphine" — each a distinct node in [[paris|the city]]'s public life (010-122). The marketplace was characterized by narrow, ancient passages that visitors would explore, navigating the dense medieval urban fabric of [[paris|Paris]] (013-122).

The Town district, of which the Halles was a central feature, was contrasted with [[university-of-paris|the University]] (Left Bank) and the City (Île de la Cité), forming the tripartite division of [[paris|Paris]] that structures much of Hugo's descriptive framework (020-128).

## The Pillory of the Halles

One of the most significant features of the Halles district was its pillory, where public punishments were carried out. The pillory served as a site of public humiliation and spectacle, drawing crowds to witness the punishment of criminals. The Halles pillory is mentioned as part of the "peregrinations of [[quasimodos-pillory|the pillory]] of the Halles," suggesting it could be moved or that its jurisdiction extended across the marketplace (039-150).

The proximity of commerce and punishment — the marketplace where goods were exchanged and [[quasimodos-pillory|the pillory]] where shame was dispensed — reflects the integration of public [[law-and-justice|justice]] into the daily life of [[paris|medieval Paris]]. This duality of function was characteristic of medieval urban spaces, where the practical and the punitive coexisted in close quarters.

## Role in the Novel

The Halles appears primarily as a geographical reference and as a destination. In the narrative, characters on the run navigate through or past the Halles district, using its crowded passages as cover (013-122). The marketplace represents the dense, labyrinthine quality of [[paris|medieval Paris]] that both shelters and entraps the novel's characters.

In one instance, a character is conducted "to the Halles" as part of a forced journey across [[paris|the city]], the marketplace serving as a waypoint in the geography of pursuit and punishment that structures much of the novel's action (033-128).

## Hugo's Commentary

The Halles exemplifies Hugo's interest in the organic, chaotic urban fabric of [[paris|medieval Paris]]. The narrow passages, the dense crowds, the mingling of commerce and [[law-and-justice|justice]] — all contribute to the vivid, sensory portrait of [[paris|the city]] that Hugo paints in the novel. The marketplace, like [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]] itself, is a space where all levels of society intersect.
