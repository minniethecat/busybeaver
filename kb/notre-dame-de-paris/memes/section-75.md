---[075-001] A cry of horror rose among the vagabonds.

[075-002] “Vengeance!” shouted Clopin. “To the sack!” replied the

[075-003] multitude. “Assault! assault!”

[075-004] There came a tremendous howl, in which were mingled all tongues,

[075-005] all dialects, all accents. The death of the poor scholar imparted

[075-006] a furious ardor to that crowd. It was seized with shame, and the

[075-007] wrath of having been held so long in check before a church by a

[075-008] hunchback. Rage found ladders, multiplied the torches, and, at

[075-009] the expiration of a few minutes, Quasimodo, in despair, beheld

[075-010] that terrible ant heap mount on all sides to the assault of

[075-011] Notre-Dame. Those who had no ladders had knotted ropes; those who

[075-012] had no ropes climbed by the projections of the carvings. They

[075-013] hung from each other’s rags. There were no means of resisting

[075-014] that rising tide of frightful faces; rage made these fierce

[075-015] countenances ruddy; their clayey brows were dripping with sweat;

[075-016] their eyes darted lightnings; all these grimaces, all these

[075-017] horrors laid siege to Quasimodo. One would have said that some

[075-018] other church had despatched to the assault of Notre-Dame its

[075-019] gorgons, its dogs, its drées, its demons, its most fantastic

[075-020] sculptures. It was like a layer of living monsters on the stone

[075-021] monsters of the façade.

[075-022] Meanwhile, the Place was studded with a thousand torches. This

[075-023] scene of confusion, till now hid in darkness, was suddenly

[075-024] flooded with light. The parvis was resplendent, and cast a

[075-025] radiance on the sky; the bonfire lighted on the lofty platform

[075-026] was still burning, and illuminated the city far away. The

[075-027] enormous silhouette of the two towers, projected afar on the

[075-028] roofs of Paris, and formed a large notch of black in this light.

[075-029] The city seemed to be aroused. Alarm bells wailed in the

[075-030] distance. The vagabonds howled, panted, swore, climbed; and

[075-031] Quasimodo, powerless against so many enemies, shuddering for the

[075-032] gypsy, beholding the furious faces approaching ever nearer and

[075-033] nearer to his gallery, entreated heaven for a miracle, and wrung

[075-034] his arms in despair.

[075-035] CHAPTER V. THE RETREAT IN WHICH MONSIEUR LOUIS OF FRANCE SAYS HIS

[075-036] PRAYERS.

[075-037] The reader has not, perhaps, forgotten that one moment before

[075-038] catching sight of the nocturnal band of vagabonds, Quasimodo, as

[075-039] he inspected Paris from the heights of his bell tower, perceived

[075-040] only one light burning, which gleamed like a star from a window

[075-041] on the topmost story of a lofty edifice beside the Porte

[075-042] Saint-Antoine. This edifice was the Bastille. That star was the

[075-043] candle of Louis XI. King Louis XI. had, in fact, been two days in

[075-044] Paris. He was to take his departure on the next day but one for

[075-045] his citadel of Montilz-les-Tours. He made but seldom and brief

[075-046] appearance in his good city of Paris, since there he did not feel

[075-047] about him enough pitfalls, gibbets, and Scotch archers.

[075-048] He had come, that day, to sleep at the Bastille. The great

[075-049] chamber five toises[64] square, which he had at the Louvre, with

[075-050] its huge chimney-piece loaded with twelve great beasts and

[075-051] thirteen great prophets, and his grand bed, eleven feet by

[075-052] twelve, pleased him but little. He felt himself lost amid all

[075-053] this grandeur. This good _bourgeois_ king preferred the Bastille

[075-054] with a tiny chamber and couch. And then, the Bastille was

[075-055] stronger than the Louvre.

[075-056] This little chamber, which the king reserved for himself in the

[075-057] famous state prison, was also tolerably spacious and occupied the

[075-058] topmost story of a turret rising from the donjon keep. It was

[075-059] circular in form, carpeted with mats of shining straw, ceiled

[075-060] with beams, enriched with fleurs-de-lis of gilded metal with

[075-061] interjoists in color; wainscoated with rich woods sown with

[075-062] rosettes of white metal, and with others painted a fine, bright

[075-063] green, made of orpiment and fine indigo.

[075-064] There was only one window, a long pointed casement, latticed with

[075-065] brass wire and bars of iron, further darkened by fine colored

[075-066] panes with the arms of the king and of the queen, each pane being

[075-067] worth two and twenty sols.

[075-068] There was but one entrance, a modern door, with a flat arch,

[075-069] garnished with a piece of tapestry on the inside, and on the

[075-070] outside by one of those porches of Irish wood, frail edifices of

[075-071] cabinet-work curiously wrought, numbers of which were still to be

[075-072] seen in old houses a hundred and fifty years ago. “Although they

[075-073] disfigure and embarrass the places,” says Sauvel in despair, “our

[075-074] old people are still unwilling to get rid of them, and keep them

[075-075] in spite of everybody.”

[075-076] In this chamber, nothing was to be found of what furnishes

[075-077] ordinary apartments, neither benches, nor trestles, nor forms,

[075-078] nor common stools in the form of a chest, nor fine stools

[075-079] sustained by pillars and counter-pillars, at four sols a piece.

[075-080] Only one easy arm-chair, very magnificent, was to be seen; the

[075-081] wood was painted with roses on a red ground, the seat was of ruby

[075-082] Cordovan leather, ornamented with long silken fringes, and

[075-083] studded with a thousand golden nails. The loneliness of this

[075-084] chair made it apparent that only one person had a right to sit

[075-085] down in this apartment. Beside the chair, and quite close to the

[075-086] window, there was a table covered with a cloth with a pattern of

[075-087] birds. On this table stood an inkhorn spotted with ink, some

[075-088] parchments, several pens, and a large goblet of chased silver. A

[075-089] little further on was a brazier, a praying stool in crimson

[075-090] velvet, relieved with small bosses of gold. Finally, at the

[075-091] extreme end of the room, a simple bed of scarlet and yellow

[075-092] damask, without either tinsel or lace; having only an ordinary

[075-093] fringe. This bed, famous for having borne the sleep or the

[075-094] sleeplessness of Louis XI., was still to be seen two hundred

[075-095] years ago, at the house of a councillor of state, where it was

[075-096] seen by old Madame Pilou, celebrated in _Cyrus_ under the name

[075-097] _Arricidie_ and of _la Morale Vivante_.

[075-098] Such was the chamber which was called “the retreat where Monsieur

[075-099] Louis de France says his prayers.”

[075-100] At the moment when we have introduced the reader into it, this

[075-101] retreat was very dark. The curfew bell had sounded an hour

[075-102] before; night was come, and there was only one flickering wax

[075-103] candle set on the table to light five persons variously grouped

[075-104] in the chamber.

[075-105] The first on which the light fell was a seigneur superbly clad in

[075-106] breeches and jerkin of scarlet striped with silver, and a loose

[075-107] coat with half sleeves of cloth of gold with black figures. This

[075-108] splendid costume, on which the light played, seemed glazed with

[075-109] flame on every fold. The man who wore it had his armorial

[075-110] bearings embroidered on his breast in vivid colors; a chevron

[075-111] accompanied by a deer passant. The shield was flanked, on the

[075-112] right by an olive branch, on the left by a deer’s antlers. This

[075-113] man wore in his girdle a rich dagger whose hilt, of silver gilt,

[075-114] was chased in the form of a helmet, and surmounted by a count’s

[075-115] coronet. He had a forbidding air, a proud mien, and a head held

[075-116] high. At the first glance one read arrogance on his visage; at

[075-117] the second, craft.

[075-118] He was standing bareheaded, a long roll of parchment in his hand,

[075-119] behind the arm-chair in which was seated, his body ungracefully

[075-120] doubled up, his knees crossed, his elbow on the table, a very

[075-121] badly accoutred personage. Let the reader imagine in fact, on the

[075-122] rich seat of Cordova leather, two crooked knees, two thin thighs,

[075-123] poorly clad in black worsted tricot, a body enveloped in a cloak

[075-124] of fustian, with fur trimming of which more leather than hair was

[075-125] visible; lastly, to crown all, a greasy old hat of the worst sort

[075-126] of black cloth, bordered with a circular string of leaden

[075-127] figures. This, in company with a dirty skull-cap, which hardly

[075-128] allowed a hair to escape, was all that distinguished the seated

[075-129] personage. He held his head so bent upon his breast, that nothing

[075-130] was to be seen of his face thus thrown into shadow, except the

[075-131] tip of his nose, upon which fell a ray of light, and which must

[075-132] have been long. From the thinness of his wrinkled hand, one

[075-133] divined that he was an old man. It was Louis XI. At some distance

[075-134] behind them, two men dressed in garments of Flemish style were

[075-135] conversing, who were not sufficiently lost in the shadow to

[075-136] prevent any one who had been present at the performance of

[075-137] Gringoire’s mystery from recognizing in them two of the principal

[075-138] Flemish envoys, Guillaume Rym, the sagacious pensioner of Ghent,

[075-139] and Jacques Coppenole, the popular hosier. The reader will

[075-140] remember that these men were mixed up in the secret politics of

[075-141] Louis XI. Finally, quite at the end of the room, near the door,

[075-142] in the dark, stood, motionless as a statue, a vigorous man with

[075-143] thickset limbs, a military harness, with a surcoat of armorial

[075-144] bearings, whose square face pierced with staring eyes, slit with

[075-145] an immense mouth, his ears concealed by two large screens of flat

[075-146] hair, had something about it both of the dog and the tiger.

[075-147] All were uncovered except the king.

[075-148] The gentleman who stood near the king was reading him a sort of

[075-149] long memorial to which his majesty seemed to be listening

[075-150] attentively. The two Flemings were whispering together.

[075-151] “Cross of God!” grumbled Coppenole, “I am tired of standing; is

[075-152] there no chair here?”

[075-153] Rym replied by a negative gesture, accompanied by a discreet

[075-154] smile.

[075-155] “Croix-Dieu!” resumed Coppenole, thoroughly unhappy at being

[075-156] obliged to lower his voice thus, “I should like to sit down on

[075-157] the floor, with my legs crossed, like a hosier, as I do in my

[075-158] shop.”

[075-159] “Take good care that you do not, Master Jacques.”

[075-160] “Ouais! Master Guillaume! can one only remain here on his feet?”

[075-161] “Or on his knees,” said Rym.

[075-162] At that moment the king’s voice was uplifted. They held their

[075-163] peace.

[075-164] “Fifty sols for the robes of our valets, and twelve livres for

[075-165] the mantles of the clerks of our crown! That’s it! Pour out gold

[075-166] by the ton! Are you mad, Olivier?”

[075-167] As he spoke thus, the old man raised his head. The golden shells

[075-168] of the collar of Saint-Michael could be seen gleaming on his

[075-169] neck. The candle fully illuminated his gaunt and morose profile.

[075-170] He tore the papers from the other’s hand.

[075-171] “You are ruining us!” he cried, casting his hollow eyes over the

[075-172] scroll. “What is all this? What need have we of so prodigious a

[075-173] household? Two chaplains at ten livres a month each, and, a

[075-174] chapel clerk at one hundred sols! A valet-de-chambre at ninety

[075-175] livres a year. Four head cooks at six score livres a year each! A

[075-176] spit-cook, an herb-cook, a sauce-cook, a butler, two

[075-177] sumpter-horse lackeys, at ten livres a month each! Two scullions

[075-178] at eight livres! A groom of the stables and his two aids at four

[075-179] and twenty livres a month! A porter, a pastry-cook, a baker, two

[075-180] carters, each sixty livres a year! And the farrier six score

[075-181] livres! And the master of the chamber of our funds, twelve

[075-182] hundred livres! And the comptroller five hundred. And how do I

[075-183] know what else? ’Tis ruinous. The wages of our servants are

[075-184] putting France to the pillage! All the ingots of the Louvre will

[075-185] melt before such a fire of expenses! We shall have to sell our

[075-186] plate! And next year, if God and our Lady (here he raised his

[075-187] hat) lend us life, we shall drink our potions from a pewter pot!”

[075-188] So saying, he cast a glance at the silver goblet which gleamed

[075-189] upon the table. He coughed and continued,—

[075-190] “Master Olivier, the princes who reign over great lordships, like

[075-191] kings and emperors, should not allow sumptuousness in their

[075-192] houses; for the fire spreads thence through the province. Hence,

[075-193] Master Olivier, consider this said once for all. Our expenditure

[075-194] increases every year. The thing displease us. How, _pasque-Dieu!_

[075-195] when in ’79 it did not exceed six and thirty thousand livres, did

[075-196] it attain in ’80, forty-three thousand six hundred and nineteen

[075-197] livres? I have the figures in my head. In ’81, sixty-six thousand

[075-198] six hundred and eighty livres, and this year, by the faith of my

[075-199] body, it will reach eighty thousand livres! Doubled in four

[075-200] years! Monstrous!”
