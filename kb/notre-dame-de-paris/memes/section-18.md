---[018-001] “I don’t know,” said she.

[018-002] “To what language do they belong?”

[018-003] “They are Egyptian, I think.”

[018-004] “I suspected as much,” said Gringoire, “you are not a native of

[018-005] France?”

[018-006] “I don’t know.”

[018-007] “Are your parents alive?”

[018-008] She began to sing, to an ancient air,—

[018-009] Mon père est oiseau,

[018-010] Ma mère est oiselle.

[018-011] Je passe l’eau sans nacelle,

[018-012] Je passe l’eau sans bateau,

[018-013] Ma mère est oiselle,

[018-014] Mon père est oiseau.[17]

[018-015] “Good,” said Gringoire. “At what age did you come to France?”

[018-016] “When I was very young.”

[018-017] “And when to Paris?”

[018-018] “Last year. At the moment when we were entering the papal gate I

[018-019] saw a reed warbler flit through the air, that was at the end of

[018-020] August; I said, it will be a hard winter.”

[018-021] “So it was,” said Gringoire, delighted at this beginning of a

[018-022] conversation. “I passed it in blowing my fingers. So you have the

[018-023] gift of prophecy?”

[018-024] She retired into her laconics again.

[018-025] “Is that man whom you call the Duke of Egypt, the chief of your

[018-026] tribe?”

[018-027] “Yes.”

[018-028] “But it was he who married us,” remarked the poet timidly.

[018-029] She made her customary pretty grimace.

[018-030] “I don’t even know your name.”

[018-031] “My name? If you want it, here it is,—Pierre Gringoire.”

[018-032] “I know a prettier one,” said she.

[018-033] “Naughty girl!” retorted the poet. “Never mind, you shall not

[018-034] provoke me. Wait, perhaps you will love me more when you know me

[018-035] better; and then, you have told me your story with so much

[018-036] confidence, that I owe you a little of mine. You must know, then,

[018-037] that my name is Pierre Gringoire, and that I am a son of the

[018-038] farmer of the notary’s office of Gonesse. My father was hung by

[018-039] the Burgundians, and my mother disembowelled by the Picards, at

[018-040] the siege of Paris, twenty years ago. At six years of age,

[018-041] therefore, I was an orphan, without a sole to my foot except the

[018-042] pavements of Paris. I do not know how I passed the interval from

[018-043] six to sixteen. A fruit dealer gave me a plum here, a baker flung

[018-044] me a crust there; in the evening I got myself taken up by the

[018-045] watch, who threw me into prison, and there I found a bundle of

[018-046] straw. All this did not prevent my growing up and growing thin,

[018-047] as you see. In the winter I warmed myself in the sun, under the

[018-048] porch of the Hôtel de Sens, and I thought it very ridiculous that

[018-049] the fire on Saint John’s Day was reserved for the dog days. At

[018-050] sixteen, I wished to choose a calling. I tried all in succession.

[018-051] I became a soldier; but I was not brave enough. I became a monk;

[018-052] but I was not sufficiently devout; and then I’m a bad hand at

[018-053] drinking. In despair, I became an apprentice of the woodcutters,

[018-054] but I was not strong enough; I had more of an inclination to

[018-055] become a schoolmaster; ’tis true that I did not know how to read,

[018-056] but that’s no reason. I perceived at the end of a certain time,

[018-057] that I lacked something in every direction; and seeing that I was

[018-058] good for nothing, of my own free will I became a poet and

[018-059] rhymester. That is a trade which one can always adopt when one is

[018-060] a vagabond, and it’s better than stealing, as some young brigands

[018-061] of my acquaintance advised me to do. One day I met by luck, Dom

[018-062] Claude Frollo, the reverend archdeacon of Notre-Dame. He took an

[018-063] interest in me, and it is to him that I to-day owe it that I am a

[018-064] veritable man of letters, who knows Latin from the _de Officiis_

[018-065] of Cicero to the mortuology of the Celestine Fathers, and a

[018-066] barbarian neither in scholastics, nor in politics, nor in

[018-067] rhythmics, that sophism of sophisms. I am the author of the

[018-068] Mystery which was presented to-day with great triumph and a great

[018-069] concourse of populace, in the grand hall of the Palais de

[018-070] Justice. I have also made a book which will contain six hundred

[018-071] pages, on the wonderful comet of 1465, which sent one man mad. I

[018-072] have enjoyed still other successes. Being somewhat of an

[018-073] artillery carpenter, I lent a hand to Jean Mangue’s great

[018-074] bombard, which burst, as you know, on the day when it was tested,

[018-075] on the Pont de Charenton, and killed four and twenty curious

[018-076] spectators. You see that I am not a bad match in marriage. I know

[018-077] a great many sorts of very engaging tricks, which I will teach

[018-078] your goat; for example, to mimic the Bishop of Paris, that cursed

[018-079] Pharisee whose mill wheels splash passers-by the whole length of

[018-080] the Pont aux Meuniers. And then my mystery will bring me in a

[018-081] great deal of coined money, if they will only pay me. And

[018-082] finally, I am at your orders, I and my wits, and my science and

[018-083] my letters, ready to live with you, damsel, as it shall please

[018-084] you, chastely or joyously; husband and wife, if you see fit;

[018-085] brother and sister, if you think that better.”

[018-086] Gringoire ceased, awaiting the effect of his harangue on the

[018-087] young girl. Her eyes were fixed on the ground.

[018-088] “_Phœbus,_” she said in a low voice. Then, turning towards the

[018-089] poet, “_Phœbus_,—what does that mean?”

[018-090] Gringoire, without exactly understanding what the connection

[018-091] could be between his address and this question, was not sorry to

[018-092] display his erudition. Assuming an air of importance, he

[018-093] replied,—

[018-094] “It is a Latin word which means _sun._”

[018-095] “Sun!” she repeated.

[018-096] “It is the name of a handsome archer, who was a god,” added

[018-097] Gringoire.

[018-098] “A god!” repeated the gypsy, and there was something pensive and

[018-099] passionate in her tone.

[018-100] At that moment, one of her bracelets became unfastened and fell.

[018-101] Gringoire stooped quickly to pick it up; when he straightened up,

[018-102] the young girl and the goat had disappeared. He heard the sound

[018-103] of a bolt. It was a little door, communicating, no doubt, with a

[018-104] neighboring cell, which was being fastened on the outside.

[018-105] “Has she left me a bed, at least?” said our philosopher.

[018-106] He made the tour of his cell. There was no piece of furniture

[018-107] adapted to sleeping purposes, except a tolerably long wooden

[018-108] coffer; and its cover was carved, to boot; which afforded

[018-109] Gringoire, when he stretched himself out upon it, a sensation

[018-110] somewhat similar to that which Micromégas would feel if he were

[018-111] to lie down on the Alps.

[018-112] “Come!” said he, adjusting himself as well as possible, “I must

[018-113] resign myself. But here’s a strange nuptial night. ’Tis a pity.

[018-114] There was something innocent and antediluvian about that broken

[018-115] crock, which quite pleased me.”

[018-116] BOOK THIRD.

[018-117] CHAPTER I. NOTRE-DAME.

[018-118] The church of Notre-Dame de Paris is still no doubt, a majestic

[018-119] and sublime edifice. But, beautiful as it has been preserved in

[018-120] growing old, it is difficult not to sigh, not to wax indignant,

[018-121] before the numberless degradations and mutilations which time and

[018-122] men have both caused the venerable monument to suffer, without

[018-123] respect for Charlemagne, who laid its first stone, or for Philip

[018-124] Augustus, who laid the last.

[018-125] On the face of this aged queen of our cathedrals, by the side of

[018-126] a wrinkle, one always finds a scar. _Tempus edax, homo

[018-127] edacior_[18]; which I should be glad to translate thus: time is

[018-128] blind, man is stupid.

[018-129] If we had leisure to examine with the reader, one by one, the

[018-130] diverse traces of destruction imprinted upon the old church,

[018-131] time’s share would be the least, the share of men the most,

[018-132] especially _the men of art_, since there have been individuals

[018-133] who assumed the title of architects during the last two

[018-134] centuries.

[018-135] And, in the first place, to cite only a few leading examples,

[018-136] there certainly are few finer architectural pages than this

[018-137] façade, where, successively and at once, the three portals

[018-138] hollowed out in an arch; the broidered and dentated cordon of the

[018-139] eight and twenty royal niches; the immense central rose window,

[018-140] flanked by its two lateral windows, like a priest by his deacon

[018-141] and subdeacon; the frail and lofty gallery of trefoil arcades,

[018-142] which supports a heavy platform above its fine, slender columns;

[018-143] and lastly, the two black and massive towers with their slate

[018-144] penthouses, harmonious parts of a magnificent whole, superposed

[018-145] in five gigantic stories;—develop themselves before the eye, in a

[018-146] mass and without confusion, with their innumerable details of

[018-147] statuary, carving, and sculpture, joined powerfully to the

[018-148] tranquil grandeur of the whole; a vast symphony in stone, so to

[018-149] speak; the colossal work of one man and one people, all together

[018-150] one and complex, like the Iliads and the Romanceros, whose sister

[018-151] it is; prodigious product of the grouping together of all the

[018-152] forces of an epoch, where, upon each stone, one sees the fancy of

[018-153] the workman disciplined by the genius of the artist start forth

[018-154] in a hundred fashions; a sort of human creation, in a word,

[018-155] powerful and fecund as the divine creation of which it seems to

[018-156] have stolen the double character,—variety, eternity.

[018-157] And what we here say of the façade must be said of the entire

[018-158] church; and what we say of the cathedral church of Paris, must be

[018-159] said of all the churches of Christendom in the Middle Ages. All

[018-160] things are in place in that art, self-created, logical, and well

[018-161] proportioned. To measure the great toe of the foot is to measure

[018-162] the giant.

[018-163] Let us return to the façade of Notre-Dame, as it still appears to

[018-164] us, when we go piously to admire the grave and puissant

[018-165] cathedral, which inspires terror, so its chronicles assert: _quæ

[018-166] mole sua terrorem incutit spectantibus_.

[018-167] Three important things are to-day lacking in that façade: in the

[018-168] first place, the staircase of eleven steps which formerly raised

[018-169] it above the soil; next, the lower series of statues which

[018-170] occupied the niches of the three portals; and lastly the upper

[018-171] series, of the twenty-eight most ancient kings of France, which

[018-172] garnished the gallery of the first story, beginning with

[018-173] Childebert, and ending with Phillip Augustus, holding in his hand

[018-174] “the imperial apple.”

[018-175] Time has caused the staircase to disappear, by raising the soil

[018-176] of the city with a slow and irresistible progress; but, while

[018-177] thus causing the eleven steps which added to the majestic height

[018-178] of the edifice, to be devoured, one by one, by the rising tide of

[018-179] the pavements of Paris,—time has bestowed upon the church perhaps

[018-180] more than it has taken away, for it is time which has spread over

[018-181] the façade that sombre hue of the centuries which makes the old

[018-182] age of monuments the period of their beauty.

[018-183] But who has thrown down the two rows of statues? who has left the

[018-184] niches empty? who has cut, in the very middle of the central

[018-185] portal, that new and bastard arch? who has dared to frame therein

[018-186] that commonplace and heavy door of carved wood, à la Louis XV.,

[018-187] beside the arabesques of Biscornette? The men, the architects,

[018-188] the artists of our day.

[018-189] And if we enter the interior of the edifice, who has overthrown

[018-190] that colossus of Saint Christopher, proverbial for magnitude

[018-191] among statues, as the grand hall of the Palais de Justice was

[018-192] among halls, as the spire of Strasbourg among spires? And those

[018-193] myriads of statues, which peopled all the spaces between the

[018-194] columns of the nave and the choir, kneeling, standing,

[018-195] equestrian, men, women, children, kings, bishops, gendarmes, in

[018-196] stone, in marble, in gold, in silver, in copper, in wax even,—who

[018-197] has brutally swept them away? It is not time.

[018-198] And who substituted for the ancient gothic altar, splendidly

[018-199] encumbered with shrines and reliquaries, that heavy marble

[018-200] sarcophagus, with angels’ heads and clouds, which seems a

[018-201] specimen pillaged from the Val-de-Grâce or the Invalides? Who

[018-202] stupidly sealed that heavy anachronism of stone in the

[018-203] Carlovingian pavement of Hercandus? Was it not Louis XIV.,

[018-204] fulfilling the request of Louis XIII.?
