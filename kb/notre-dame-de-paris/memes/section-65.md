---[065-001] She made an affirmative sign of the head, and said, “Yes.”

[065-002] He understood the motion of the head. “Alas!” he said, as though

[065-003] hesitating whether to finish, “I am—I am deaf.”

[065-004] “Poor man!” exclaimed the Bohemian, with an expression of kindly

[065-005] pity.

[065-006] He began to smile sadly.

[065-007] “You think that that was all that I lacked, do you not? Yes, I am

[065-008] deaf, that is the way I am made. ’Tis horrible, is it not? You

[065-009] are so beautiful!”

[065-010] There lay in the accents of the wretched man so profound a

[065-011] consciousness of his misery, that she had not the strength to say

[065-012] a word. Besides, he would not have heard her. He went on,—

[065-013] “Never have I seen my ugliness as at the present moment. When I

[065-014] compare myself to you, I feel a very great pity for myself, poor

[065-015] unhappy monster that I am! Tell me, I must look to you like a

[065-016] beast. You, you are a ray of sunshine, a drop of dew, the song of

[065-017] a bird! I am something frightful, neither man nor animal, I know

[065-018] not what, harder, more trampled under foot, and more unshapely

[065-019] than a pebble stone!”

[065-020] Then he began to laugh, and that laugh was the most heartbreaking

[065-021] thing in the world. He continued,—

[065-022] “Yes, I am deaf; but you shall talk to me by gestures, by signs.

[065-023] I have a master who talks with me in that way. And then, I shall

[065-024] very soon know your wish from the movement of your lips, from

[065-025] your look.”

[065-026] “Well!” she interposed with a smile, “tell me why you saved me.”

[065-027] He watched her attentively while she was speaking.

[065-028] “I understand,” he replied. “You ask me why I saved you. You have

[065-029] forgotten a wretch who tried to abduct you one night, a wretch to

[065-030] whom you rendered succor on the following day on their infamous

[065-031] pillory. A drop of water and a little pity,—that is more than I

[065-032] can repay with my life. You have forgotten that wretch; but he

[065-033] remembers it.”

[065-034] She listened to him with profound tenderness. A tear swam in the

[065-035] eye of the bellringer, but did not fall. He seemed to make it a

[065-036] sort of point of honor to retain it.

[065-037] “Listen,” he resumed, when he was no longer afraid that the tear

[065-038] would escape; “our towers here are very high, a man who should

[065-039] fall from them would be dead before touching the pavement; when

[065-040] it shall please you to have me fall, you will not have to utter

[065-041] even a word, a glance will suffice.”

[065-042] Then he rose. Unhappy as was the Bohemian, this eccentric being

[065-043] still aroused some compassion in her. She made him a sign to

[065-044] remain.

[065-045] “No, no,” said he; “I must not remain too long. I am not at my

[065-046] ease. It is out of pity that you do not turn away your eyes. I

[065-047] shall go to some place where I can see you without your seeing

[065-048] me: it will be better so.”

[065-049] He drew from his pocket a little metal whistle.

[065-050] “Here,” said he, “when you have need of me, when you wish me to

[065-051] come, when you will not feel too much horror at the sight of me,

[065-052] use this whistle. I can hear this sound.”

[065-053] He laid the whistle on the floor and fled.

[065-054] CHAPTER IV. EARTHENWARE AND CRYSTAL.

[065-055] Day followed day. Calm gradually returned to the soul of la

[065-056] Esmeralda. Excess of grief, like excess of joy is a violent thing

[065-057] which lasts but a short time. The heart of man cannot remain long

[065-058] in one extremity. The gypsy had suffered so much, that nothing

[065-059] was left her but astonishment. With security, hope had returned

[065-060] to her. She was outside the pale of society, outside the pale of

[065-061] life, but she had a vague feeling that it might not be impossible

[065-062] to return to it. She was like a dead person, who should hold in

[065-063] reserve the key to her tomb.

[065-064] She felt the terrible images which had so long persecuted her,

[065-065] gradually departing. All the hideous phantoms, Pierrat Torterue,

[065-066] Jacques Charmolue, were effaced from her mind, all, even the

[065-067] priest.

[065-068] And then, Phœbus was alive; she was sure of it, she had seen him.

[065-069] To her the fact of Phœbus being alive was everything. After the

[065-070] series of fatal shocks which had overturned everything within

[065-071] her, she had found but one thing intact in her soul, one

[065-072] sentiment,—her love for the captain. Love is like a tree; it

[065-073] sprouts forth of itself, sends its roots out deeply through our

[065-074] whole being, and often continues to flourish greenly over a heart

[065-075] in ruins.

[065-076] And the inexplicable point about it is that the more blind is

[065-077] this passion, the more tenacious it is. It is never more solid

[065-078] than when it has no reason in it.

[065-079] La Esmeralda did not think of the captain without bitterness, no

[065-080] doubt. No doubt it was terrible that he also should have been

[065-081] deceived; that he should have believed that impossible thing,

[065-082] that he could have conceived of a stab dealt by her who would

[065-083] have given a thousand lives for him. But, after all, she must not

[065-084] be too angry with him for it; had she not confessed her crime?

[065-085] had she not yielded, weak woman that she was, to torture? The

[065-086] fault was entirely hers. She should have allowed her finger nails

[065-087] to be torn out rather than such a word to be wrenched from her.

[065-088] In short, if she could but see Phœbus once more, for a single

[065-089] minute, only one word would be required, one look, in order to

[065-090] undeceive him, to bring him back. She did not doubt it. She was

[065-091] astonished also at many singular things, at the accident of

[065-092] Phœbus’s presence on the day of the penance, at the young girl

[065-093] with whom he had been. She was his sister, no doubt. An

[065-094] unreasonable explanation, but she contented herself with it,

[065-095] because she needed to believe that Phœbus still loved her, and

[065-096] loved her alone. Had he not sworn it to her? What more was

[065-097] needed, simple and credulous as she was? And then, in this

[065-098] matter, were not appearances much more against her than against

[065-099] him? Accordingly, she waited. She hoped.

[065-100] Let us add that the church, that vast church, which surrounded

[065-101] her on every side, which guarded her, which saved her, was itself

[065-102] a sovereign tranquillizer. The solemn lines of that architecture,

[065-103] the religious attitude of all the objects which surrounded the

[065-104] young girl, the serene and pious thoughts which emanated, so to

[065-105] speak, from all the pores of that stone, acted upon her without

[065-106] her being aware of it. The edifice had also sounds fraught with

[065-107] such benediction and such majesty, that they soothed this ailing

[065-108] soul. The monotonous chanting of the celebrants, the responses of

[065-109] the people to the priest, sometimes inarticulate, sometimes

[065-110] thunderous, the harmonious trembling of the painted windows, the

[065-111] organ, bursting forth like a hundred trumpets, the three

[065-112] belfries, humming like hives of huge bees, that whole orchestra

[065-113] on which bounded a gigantic scale, ascending, descending

[065-114] incessantly from the voice of a throng to that of one bell,

[065-115] dulled her memory, her imagination, her grief. The bells, in

[065-116] particular, lulled her. It was something like a powerful

[065-117] magnetism which those vast instruments shed over her in great

[065-118] waves.

[065-119] Thus every sunrise found her more calm, breathing better, less

[065-120] pale. In proportion as her inward wounds closed, her grace and

[065-121] beauty blossomed once more on her countenance, but more

[065-122] thoughtful, more reposeful. Her former character also returned to

[065-123] her, somewhat even of her gayety, her pretty pout, her love for

[065-124] her goat, her love for singing, her modesty. She took care to

[065-125] dress herself in the morning in the corner of her cell for fear

[065-126] some inhabitants of the neighboring attics might see her through

[065-127] the window.

[065-128] When the thought of Phœbus left her time, the gypsy sometimes

[065-129] thought of Quasimodo. He was the sole bond, the sole connection,

[065-130] the sole communication which remained to her with men, with the

[065-131] living. Unfortunate girl! she was more outside the world than

[065-132] Quasimodo. She understood not in the least the strange friend

[065-133] whom chance had given her. She often reproached herself for not

[065-134] feeling a gratitude which should close her eyes, but decidedly,

[065-135] she could not accustom herself to the poor bellringer. He was too

[065-136] ugly.

[065-137] She had left the whistle which he had given her lying on the

[065-138] ground. This did not prevent Quasimodo from making his appearance

[065-139] from time to time during the first few days. She did her best not

[065-140] to turn aside with too much repugnance when he came to bring her

[065-141] her basket of provisions or her jug of water, but he always

[065-142] perceived the slightest movement of this sort, and then he

[065-143] withdrew sadly.

[065-144] Once he came at the moment when she was caressing Djali. He stood

[065-145] pensively for several minutes before this graceful group of the

[065-146] goat and the gypsy; at last he said, shaking his heavy and

[065-147] ill-formed head,—

[065-148] “My misfortune is that I still resemble a man too much. I should

[065-149] like to be wholly a beast like that goat.”

[065-150] She gazed at him in amazement.

[065-151] He replied to the glance,—

[065-152] “Oh! I well know why,” and he went away.

[065-153] On another occasion he presented himself at the door of the cell

[065-154] (which he never entered) at the moment when la Esmeralda was

[065-155] singing an old Spanish ballad, the words of which she did not

[065-156] understand, but which had lingered in her ear because the gypsy

[065-157] women had lulled her to sleep with it when she was a little

[065-158] child. At the sight of that villanous form which made its

[065-159] appearance so abruptly in the middle of her song, the young girl

[065-160] paused with an involuntary gesture of alarm. The unhappy

[065-161] bellringer fell upon his knees on the threshold, and clasped his

[065-162] large, misshapen hands with a suppliant air. “Oh!” he said,

[065-163] sorrowfully, “continue, I implore you, and do not drive me away.”

[065-164] She did not wish to pain him, and resumed her lay, trembling all

[065-165] over. By degrees, however, her terror disappeared, and she

[065-166] yielded herself wholly to the slow and melancholy air which she

[065-167] was singing. He remained on his knees with hands clasped, as in

[065-168] prayer, attentive, hardly breathing, his gaze riveted upon the

[065-169] gypsy’s brilliant eyes.

[065-170] On another occasion, he came to her with an awkward and timid

[065-171] air. “Listen,” he said, with an effort; “I have something to say

[065-172] to you.” She made him a sign that she was listening. Then he

[065-173] began to sigh, half opened his lips, appeared for a moment to be

[065-174] on the point of speaking, then he looked at her again, shook his

[065-175] head, and withdrew slowly, with his brow in his hand, leaving the

[065-176] gypsy stupefied. Among the grotesque personages sculptured on the

[065-177] wall, there was one to whom he was particularly attached, and

[065-178] with which he often seemed to exchange fraternal glances. Once

[065-179] the gypsy heard him saying to it,—

[065-180] “Oh! why am not I of stone, like you!”

[065-181] At last, one morning, la Esmeralda had advanced to the edge of

[065-182] the roof, and was looking into the Place over the pointed roof of

[065-183] Saint-Jean le Rond. Quasimodo was standing behind her. He had

[065-184] placed himself in that position in order to spare the young girl,

[065-185] as far as possible, the displeasure of seeing him. All at once

[065-186] the gypsy started, a tear and a flash of joy gleamed

[065-187] simultaneously in her eyes, she knelt on the brink of the roof

[065-188] and extended her arms towards the Place with anguish, exclaiming:

[065-189] “Phœbus! come! come! a word, a single word in the name of heaven!

[065-190] Phœbus! Phœbus!” Her voice, her face, her gesture, her whole

[065-191] person bore the heartrending expression of a shipwrecked man who

[065-192] is making a signal of distress to the joyous vessel which is

[065-193] passing afar off in a ray of sunlight on the horizon.
