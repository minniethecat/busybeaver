---[010-001] He was on the point of turning to the Pont Saint-Michel; children

[010-002] were running about here and there with fire lances and rockets.

[010-003] “Pest on firework candles!” said Gringoire; and he fell back on

[010-004] the Pont au Change. To the house at the head of the bridge there

[010-005] had been affixed three small banners, representing the king, the

[010-006] dauphin, and Marguerite of Flanders, and six little pennons on

[010-007] which were portrayed the Duke of Austria, the Cardinal de

[010-008] Bourbon, M. de Beaujeu, and Madame Jeanne de France, and Monsieur

[010-009] the Bastard of Bourbon, and I know not whom else; all being

[010-010] illuminated with torches. The rabble were admiring.

[010-011] “Happy painter, Jehan Fourbault!” said Gringoire with a deep

[010-012] sigh; and he turned his back upon the bannerets and pennons. A

[010-013] street opened before him; he thought it so dark and deserted that

[010-014] he hoped to there escape from all the rumors as well as from all

[010-015] the gleams of the festival. At the end of a few moments his foot

[010-016] came in contact with an obstacle; he stumbled and fell. It was

[010-017] the May truss, which the clerks of the clerks’ law court had

[010-018] deposited that morning at the door of a president of the

[010-019] parliament, in honor of the solemnity of the day. Gringoire bore

[010-020] this new disaster heroically; he picked himself up, and reached

[010-021] the water’s edge. After leaving behind him the civic Tournelle[7]

[010-022] and the criminal tower, and skirted the great walls of the king’s

[010-023] garden, on that unpaved strand where the mud reached to his

[010-024] ankles, he reached the western point of the city, and considered

[010-025] for some time the islet of the Passeur-aux-Vaches, which has

[010-026] disappeared beneath the bronze horse of the Pont Neuf. The islet

[010-027] appeared to him in the shadow like a black mass, beyond the

[010-028] narrow strip of whitish water which separated him from it. One

[010-029] could divine by the ray of a tiny light the sort of hut in the

[010-030] form of a beehive where the ferryman of cows took refuge at

[010-031] night.

[010-032] “Happy ferryman!” thought Gringoire; “you do not dream of glory,

[010-033] and you do not make marriage songs! What matters it to you, if

[010-034] kings and Duchesses of Burgundy marry? You know no other daisies

[010-035] (_marguerites_) than those which your April greensward gives your

[010-036] cows to browse upon; while I, a poet, am hooted, and shiver, and

[010-037] owe twelve sous, and the soles of my shoes are so transparent,

[010-038] that they might serve as glasses for your lantern! Thanks,

[010-039] ferryman, your cabin rests my eyes, and makes me forget Paris!”

[010-040] He was roused from his almost lyric ecstacy, by a big double

[010-041] Saint-Jean cracker, which suddenly went off from the happy cabin.

[010-042] It was the cow ferryman, who was taking his part in the

[010-043] rejoicings of the day, and letting off fireworks.

[010-044] This cracker made Gringoire’s skin bristle up all over.

[010-045] “Accursed festival!” he exclaimed, “wilt thou pursue me

[010-046] everywhere? Oh! good God! even to the ferryman’s!”

[010-047] Then he looked at the Seine at his feet, and a horrible

[010-048] temptation took possession of him:

[010-049] “Oh!” said he, “I would gladly drown myself, were the water not

[010-050] so cold!”

[010-051] Then a desperate resolution occurred to him. It was, since he

[010-052] could not escape from the Pope of the Fools, from Jehan

[010-053] Fourbault’s bannerets, from May trusses, from squibs and

[010-054] crackers, to go to the Place de Grève.

[010-055] “At least,” he said to himself, “I shall there have a firebrand

[010-056] of joy wherewith to warm myself, and I can sup on some crumbs of

[010-057] the three great armorial bearings of royal sugar which have been

[010-058] erected on the public refreshment-stall of the city.”

[010-059] CHAPTER II. THE PLACE DE GRÈVE.

[010-060] There remains to-day but a very imperceptible vestige of the

[010-061] Place de Grève, such as it existed then; it consists in the

[010-062] charming little turret, which occupies the angle north of the

[010-063] Place, and which, already enshrouded in the ignoble plaster which

[010-064] fills with paste the delicate lines of its sculpture, would soon

[010-065] have disappeared, perhaps submerged by that flood of new houses

[010-066] which so rapidly devours all the ancient façades of Paris.

[010-067] The persons who, like ourselves, never cross the Place de Grève

[010-068] without casting a glance of pity and sympathy on that poor turret

[010-069] strangled between two hovels of the time of Louis XV., can easily

[010-070] reconstruct in their minds the aggregate of edifices to which it

[010-071] belonged, and find again entire in it the ancient Gothic place of

[010-072] the fifteenth century.

[010-073] It was then, as it is to-day, an irregular trapezoid, bordered on

[010-074] one side by the quay, and on the other three by a series of

[010-075] lofty, narrow, and gloomy houses. By day, one could admire the

[010-076] variety of its edifices, all sculptured in stone or wood, and

[010-077] already presenting complete specimens of the different domestic

[010-078] architectures of the Middle Ages, running back from the fifteenth

[010-079] to the eleventh century, from the casement which had begun to

[010-080] dethrone the arch, to the Roman semicircle, which had been

[010-081] supplanted by the ogive, and which still occupies, below it, the

[010-082] first story of that ancient house de la Tour Roland, at the

[010-083] corner of the Place upon the Seine, on the side of the street

[010-084] with the Tannerie. At night, one could distinguish nothing of all

[010-085] that mass of buildings, except the black indentation of the

[010-086] roofs, unrolling their chain of acute angles round the place; for

[010-087] one of the radical differences between the cities of that time,

[010-088] and the cities of the present day, lay in the façades which

[010-089] looked upon the places and streets, and which were then gables.

[010-090] For the last two centuries the houses have been turned round.

[010-091] In the centre of the eastern side of the Place, rose a heavy and

[010-092] hybrid construction, formed of three buildings placed in

[010-093] juxtaposition. It was called by three names which explain its

[010-094] history, its destination, and its architecture: “The House of the

[010-095] Dauphin,” because Charles V., when Dauphin, had inhabited it;

[010-096] “The Marchandise,” because it had served as town hall; and “The

[010-097] Pillared House” (_domus ad piloria_), because of a series of

[010-098] large pillars which sustained the three stories. The city found

[010-099] there all that is required for a city like Paris; a chapel in

[010-100] which to pray to God; a _plaidoyer_, or pleading room, in which

[010-101] to hold hearings, and to repel, at need, the King’s people; and

[010-102] under the roof, an _arsenac_ full of artillery. For the

[010-103] _bourgeois_ of Paris were aware that it is not sufficient to pray

[010-104] in every conjuncture, and to plead for the franchises of the

[010-105] city, and they had always in reserve, in the garret of the town

[010-106] hall, a few good rusty arquebuses. The Grève had then that

[010-107] sinister aspect which it preserves to-day from the execrable

[010-108] ideas which it awakens, and from the sombre town hall of

[010-109] Dominique Bocador, which has replaced the Pillared House. It must

[010-110] be admitted that a permanent gibbet and a pillory, “a justice and

[010-111] a ladder,” as they were called in that day, erected side by side

[010-112] in the centre of the pavement, contributed not a little to cause

[010-113] eyes to be turned away from that fatal place, where so many

[010-114] beings full of life and health have agonized; where, fifty years

[010-115] later, that fever of Saint Vallier was destined to have its

[010-116] birth, that terror of the scaffold, the most monstrous of all

[010-117] maladies because it comes not from God, but from man.

[010-118] It is a consoling idea (let us remark in passing), to think that

[010-119] the death penalty, which three hundred years ago still encumbered

[010-120] with its iron wheels, its stone gibbets, and all its

[010-121] paraphernalia of torture, permanent and riveted to the pavement,

[010-122] the Grève, the Halles, the Place Dauphine, the Cross du Trahoir,

[010-123] the Marché aux Pourceaux, that hideous Montfaucon, the barrier

[010-124] des Sergents, the Place aux Chats, the Porte Saint-Denis,

[010-125] Champeaux, the Porte Baudets, the Porte Saint Jacques, without

[010-126] reckoning the innumerable ladders of the provosts, the bishop of

[010-127] the chapters, of the abbots, of the priors, who had the decree of

[010-128] life and death,—without reckoning the judicial drownings in the

[010-129] river Seine; it is consoling to-day, after having lost

[010-130] successively all the pieces of its armor, its luxury of torment,

[010-131] its penalty of imagination and fancy, its torture for which it

[010-132] reconstructed every five years a leather bed at the Grand

[010-133] Châtelet, that ancient suzerain of feudal society almost expunged

[010-134] from our laws and our cities, hunted from code to code, chased

[010-135] from place to place, has no longer, in our immense Paris, any

[010-136] more than a dishonored corner of the Grève,—than a miserable

[010-137] guillotine, furtive, uneasy, shameful, which seems always afraid

[010-138] of being caught in the act, so quickly does it disappear after

[010-139] having dealt its blow.

[010-140] CHAPTER III. KISSES FOR BLOWS.

[010-141] When Pierre Gringoire arrived on the Place de Grève, he was

[010-142] paralyzed. He had directed his course across the Pont aux

[010-143] Meuniers, in order to avoid the rabble on the Pont au Change, and

[010-144] the pennons of Jehan Fourbault; but the wheels of all the

[010-145] bishop’s mills had splashed him as he passed, and his doublet was

[010-146] drenched; it seemed to him besides, that the failure of his piece

[010-147] had rendered him still more sensible to cold than usual. Hence he

[010-148] made haste to draw near the bonfire, which was burning

[010-149] magnificently in the middle of the Place. But a considerable

[010-150] crowd formed a circle around it.

[010-151] “Accursed Parisians!” he said to himself (for Gringoire, like a

[010-152] true dramatic poet, was subject to monologues) “there they are

[010-153] obstructing my fire! Nevertheless, I am greatly in need of a

[010-154] chimney corner; my shoes drink in the water, and all those cursed

[010-155] mills wept upon me! That devil of a Bishop of Paris, with his

[010-156] mills! I’d just like to know what use a bishop can make of a

[010-157] mill! Does he expect to become a miller instead of a bishop? If

[010-158] only my malediction is needed for that, I bestow it upon him! and

[010-159] his cathedral, and his mills! Just see if those boobies will put

[010-160] themselves out! Move aside! I’d like to know what they are doing

[010-161] there! They are warming themselves, much pleasure may it give

[010-162] them! They are watching a hundred fagots burn; a fine spectacle!”

[010-163] On looking more closely, he perceived that the circle was much

[010-164] larger than was required simply for the purpose of getting warm

[010-165] at the king’s fire, and that this concourse of people had not

[010-166] been attracted solely by the beauty of the hundred fagots which

[010-167] were burning.

[010-168] In a vast space left free between the crowd and the fire, a young

[010-169] girl was dancing.

[010-170] Whether this young girl was a human being, a fairy, or an angel,

[010-171] is what Gringoire, sceptical philosopher and ironical poet that

[010-172] he was, could not decide at the first moment, so fascinated was

[010-173] he by this dazzling vision.

[010-174] She was not tall, though she seemed so, so boldly did her slender

[010-175] form dart about. She was swarthy of complexion, but one divined

[010-176] that, by day, her skin must possess that beautiful golden tone of

[010-177] the Andalusians and the Roman women. Her little foot, too, was

[010-178] Andalusian, for it was both pinched and at ease in its graceful

[010-179] shoe. She danced, she turned, she whirled rapidly about on an old

[010-180] Persian rug, spread negligently under her feet; and each time

[010-181] that her radiant face passed before you, as she whirled, her

[010-182] great black eyes darted a flash of lightning at you.

[010-183] All around her, all glances were riveted, all mouths open; and,

[010-184] in fact, when she danced thus, to the humming of the Basque

[010-185] tambourine, which her two pure, rounded arms raised above her

[010-186] head, slender, frail and vivacious as a wasp, with her corsage of

[010-187] gold without a fold, her variegated gown puffing out, her bare

[010-188] shoulders, her delicate limbs, which her petticoat revealed at

[010-189] times, her black hair, her eyes of flame, she was a supernatural

[010-190] creature.
