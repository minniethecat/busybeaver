---[067-001] She no longer saw him, but she felt the presence of a good genius

[067-002] about her. Her provisions were replenished by an invisible hand

[067-003] during her slumbers. One morning she found a cage of birds on her

[067-004] window. There was a piece of sculpture above her window which

[067-005] frightened her. She had shown this more than once in Quasimodo’s

[067-006] presence. One morning, for all these things happened at night,

[067-007] she no longer saw it, it had been broken. The person who had

[067-008] climbed up to that carving must have risked his life.

[067-009] Sometimes, in the evening, she heard a voice, concealed beneath

[067-010] the wind screen of the bell tower, singing a sad, strange song,

[067-011] as though to lull her to sleep. The lines were unrhymed, such as

[067-012] a deaf person can make.

[067-013] Ne regarde pas la figure,

[067-014] Jeune fille, regarde le cœur.

[067-015] Le cœur d’un beau jeune homme est souvent difforme.

[067-016] Il y a des cœurs où l’amour ne se conserve pas.

[067-017] Jeune fille, le sapin n’est pas beau,

[067-018] N’est pas beau comme le peuplier,

[067-019] Mais il garde son feuillage l’hiver.

[067-020] Hélas! à quoi bon dire cela?

[067-021] Ce qui n’est pas beau a tort d’être;

[067-022] La beauté n’aime que la beauté,

[067-023] Avril tourne le dos à janvier.

[067-024] La beauté est parfaite,

[067-025] La beauté peut tout,

[067-026] La beauté est la seule chose qui n’existe pas à demi.

[067-027] Le corbeau ne vole que le jour,

[067-028] Le hibou ne vole que la nuit,

[067-029] Le cygne vole la nuit et le jour.[52]

[067-030] One morning, on awaking, she saw on her window two vases filled

[067-031] with flowers. One was a very beautiful and very brilliant but

[067-032] cracked vase of glass. It had allowed the water with which it had

[067-033] been filled to escape, and the flowers which it contained were

[067-034] withered. The other was an earthenware pot, coarse and common,

[067-035] but which had preserved all its water, and its flowers remained

[067-036] fresh and crimson.

[067-037] I know not whether it was done intentionally, but La Esmeralda

[067-038] took the faded nosegay and wore it all day long upon her breast.

[067-039] That day she did not hear the voice singing in the tower.

[067-040] She troubled herself very little about it. She passed her days in

[067-041] caressing Djali, in watching the door of the Gondelaurier house,

[067-042] in talking to herself about Phœbus, and in crumbling up her bread

[067-043] for the swallows.

[067-044] She had entirely ceased to see or hear Quasimodo. The poor

[067-045] bellringer seemed to have disappeared from the church. One night,

[067-046] nevertheless, when she was not asleep, but was thinking of her

[067-047] handsome captain, she heard something breathing near her cell.

[067-048] She rose in alarm, and saw by the light of the moon, a shapeless

[067-049] mass lying across her door on the outside. It was Quasimodo

[067-050] asleep there upon the stones.

[067-051] CHAPTER V. THE KEY TO THE RED DOOR.

[067-052] In the meantime, public rumor had informed the archdeacon of the

[067-053] miraculous manner in which the gypsy had been saved. When he

[067-054] learned it, he knew not what his sensations were. He had

[067-055] reconciled himself to la Esmeralda’s death. In that matter he was

[067-056] tranquil; he had reached the bottom of personal suffering. The

[067-057] human heart (Dom Claude had meditated upon these matters) can

[067-058] contain only a certain quantity of despair. When the sponge is

[067-059] saturated, the sea may pass over it without causing a single drop

[067-060] more to enter it.

[067-061] Now, with la Esmeralda dead, the sponge was soaked, all was at an

[067-062] end on this earth for Dom Claude. But to feel that she was alive,

[067-063] and Phœbus also, meant that tortures, shocks, alternatives, life,

[067-064] were beginning again. And Claude was weary of all this.

[067-065] When he heard this news, he shut himself in his cell in the

[067-066] cloister. He appeared neither at the meetings of the chapter nor

[067-067] at the services. He closed his door against all, even against the

[067-068] bishop. He remained thus immured for several weeks. He was

[067-069] believed to be ill. And so he was, in fact.

[067-070] What did he do while thus shut up? With what thoughts was the

[067-071] unfortunate man contending? Was he giving final battle to his

[067-072] formidable passion? Was he concocting a final plan of death for

[067-073] her and of perdition for himself?

[067-074] His Jehan, his cherished brother, his spoiled child, came once to

[067-075] his door, knocked, swore, entreated, gave his name half a score

[067-076] of times. Claude did not open.

[067-077] He passed whole days with his face close to the panes of his

[067-078] window. From that window, situated in the cloister, he could see

[067-079] la Esmeralda’s chamber. He often saw herself with her goat,

[067-080] sometimes with Quasimodo. He remarked the little attentions of

[067-081] the ugly deaf man, his obedience, his delicate and submissive

[067-082] ways with the gypsy. He recalled, for he had a good memory, and

[067-083] memory is the tormentor of the jealous, he recalled the singular

[067-084] look of the bellringer, bent on the dancer upon a certain

[067-085] evening. He asked himself what motive could have impelled

[067-086] Quasimodo to save her. He was the witness of a thousand little

[067-087] scenes between the gypsy and the deaf man, the pantomime of

[067-088] which, viewed from afar and commented on by his passion, appeared

[067-089] very tender to him. He distrusted the capriciousness of women.

[067-090] Then he felt a jealousy which he could never have believed

[067-091] possible awakening within him, a jealousy which made him redden

[067-092] with shame and indignation: “One might condone the captain, but

[067-093] this one!” This thought upset him.

[067-094] His nights were frightful. As soon as he learned that the gypsy

[067-095] was alive, the cold ideas of spectre and tomb which had

[067-096] persecuted him for a whole day vanished, and the flesh returned

[067-097] to goad him. He turned and twisted on his couch at the thought

[067-098] that the dark-skinned maiden was so near him.

[067-099] Every night his delirious imagination represented la Esmeralda to

[067-100] him in all the attitudes which had caused his blood to boil most.

[067-101] He beheld her outstretched upon the poniarded captain, her eyes

[067-102] closed, her beautiful bare throat covered with Phœbus’s blood, at

[067-103] that moment of bliss when the archdeacon had imprinted on her

[067-104] pale lips that kiss whose burn the unhappy girl, though half

[067-105] dead, had felt. He beheld her, again, stripped by the savage

[067-106] hands of the torturers, allowing them to bare and to enclose in

[067-107] the boot with its iron screw, her tiny foot, her delicate rounded

[067-108] leg, her white and supple knee. Again he beheld that ivory knee

[067-109] which alone remained outside of Torterue’s horrible apparatus.

[067-110] Lastly, he pictured the young girl in her shift, with the rope

[067-111] about her neck, shoulders bare, feet bare, almost nude, as he had

[067-112] seen her on that last day. These images of voluptuousness made

[067-113] him clench his fists, and a shiver run along his spine.

[067-114] One night, among others, they heated so cruelly his virgin and

[067-115] priestly blood, that he bit his pillow, leaped from his bed,

[067-116] flung on a surplice over his shirt, and left his cell, lamp in

[067-117] hand, half naked, wild, his eyes aflame.

[067-118] He knew where to find the key to the red door, which connected

[067-119] the cloister with the church, and he always had about him, as the

[067-120] reader knows, the key of the staircase leading to the towers.

[067-121] CHAPTER VI. CONTINUATION OF THE KEY TO THE RED DOOR.

[067-122] That night, la Esmeralda had fallen asleep in her cell, full of

[067-123] oblivion, of hope, and of sweet thoughts. She had already been

[067-124] asleep for some time, dreaming as always, of Phœbus, when it

[067-125] seemed to her that she heard a noise near her. She slept lightly

[067-126] and uneasily, the sleep of a bird; a mere nothing waked her. She

[067-127] opened her eyes. The night was very dark. Nevertheless, she saw a

[067-128] figure gazing at her through the window; a lamp lighted up this

[067-129] apparition. The moment that the figure saw that la Esmeralda had

[067-130] perceived it, it blew out the lamp. But the young girl had had

[067-131] time to catch a glimpse of it; her eyes closed again with terror.

[067-132] “Oh!” she said in a faint voice, “the priest!”

[067-133] All her past unhappiness came back to her like a flash of

[067-134] lightning. She fell back on her bed, chilled.

[067-135] A moment later she felt a touch along her body which made her

[067-136] shudder so that she straightened herself up in a sitting posture,

[067-137] wide awake and furious.

[067-138] The priest had just slipped in beside her. He encircled her with

[067-139] both arms.

[067-140] She tried to scream and could not.

[067-141] “Begone, monster! begone assassin!” she said, in a voice which

[067-142] was low and trembling with wrath and terror.

[067-143] “Mercy! mercy!” murmured the priest, pressing his lips to her

[067-144] shoulder.

[067-145] She seized his bald head by its remnant of hair and tried to

[067-146] thrust aside his kisses as though they had been bites.

[067-147] “Mercy!” repeated the unfortunate man. “If you but knew what my

[067-148] love for you is! ’Tis fire, melted lead, a thousand daggers in my

[067-149] heart.”

[067-150] She stopped his two arms with superhuman force.

[067-151] “Let me go,” she said, “or I will spit in your face!”

[067-152] He released her. “Vilify me, strike me, be malicious! Do what you

[067-153] will! But have mercy! love me!”

[067-154] Then she struck him with the fury of a child. She made her

[067-155] beautiful hands stiff to bruise his face. “Begone, demon!”

[067-156] “Love me! love me! pity!” cried the poor priest returning her

[067-157] blows with caresses.

[067-158] All at once she felt him stronger than herself.

[067-159] “There must be an end to this!” he said, gnashing his teeth.

[067-160] She was conquered, palpitating in his arms, and in his power. She

[067-161] felt a wanton hand straying over her. She made a last effort, and

[067-162] began to cry: “Help! Help! A vampire! a vampire!”

[067-163] Nothing came. Djali alone was awake and bleating with anguish.

[067-164] “Hush!” said the panting priest.

[067-165] All at once, as she struggled and crawled on the floor, the

[067-166] gypsy’s hand came in contact with something cold and metallic—it

[067-167] was Quasimodo’s whistle. She seized it with a convulsive hope,

[067-168] raised it to her lips and blew with all the strength that she had

[067-169] left. The whistle gave a clear, piercing sound.

[067-170] “What is that?” said the priest.

[067-171] Almost at the same instant he felt himself raised by a vigorous

[067-172] arm. The cell was dark; he could not distinguish clearly who it

[067-173] was that held him thus; but he heard teeth chattering with rage,

[067-174] and there was just sufficient light scattered among the gloom to

[067-175] allow him to see above his head the blade of a large knife.

[067-176] The priest fancied that he perceived the form of Quasimodo. He

[067-177] assumed that it could be no one but he. He remembered to have

[067-178] stumbled, as he entered, over a bundle which was stretched across

[067-179] the door on the outside. But, as the newcomer did not utter a

[067-180] word, he knew not what to think. He flung himself on the arm

[067-181] which held the knife, crying: “Quasimodo!” He forgot, at that

[067-182] moment of distress, that Quasimodo was deaf.

[067-183] In a twinkling, the priest was overthrown and a leaden knee

[067-184] rested on his breast.

[067-185] From the angular imprint of that knee he recognized Quasimodo;

[067-186] but what was to be done? how could he make the other recognize

[067-187] him? the darkness rendered the deaf man blind.

[067-188] He was lost. The young girl, pitiless as an enraged tigress, did

[067-189] not intervene to save him. The knife was approaching his head;

[067-190] the moment was critical. All at once, his adversary seemed

[067-191] stricken with hesitation.

[067-192] “No blood on her!” he said in a dull voice.

[067-193] It was, in fact, Quasimodo’s voice.

[067-194] Then the priest felt a large hand dragging him feet first out of

[067-195] the cell; it was there that he was to die. Fortunately for him,

[067-196] the moon had risen a few moments before.

[067-197] When they had passed through the door of the cell, its pale rays

[067-198] fell upon the priest’s countenance. Quasimodo looked him full in

[067-199] the face, a trembling seized him, and he released the priest and

[067-200] shrank back.
