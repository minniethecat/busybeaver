# Saint-Germain-des-Prés

The Abbey of Saint-Germain-des-Prés was one of the most venerable religious establishments of [[paris|medieval Paris]], a Benedictine monastery whose origins reached back to the Merovingian period. In *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, Hugo celebrates it as a masterpiece of Romanesque [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] and an essential feature of [[paris|the city]]'s ecclesiastical geography.

## Overview

Hugo describes the abbey with admiration, calling it a "Carlovingian Abbey of Saint-Germain-des-Prés" ((019-123)). He notes its architectural splendor, describing how "the nave of Saint-Germain des Prés" ((019-190)) compared favorably to other great [[religion|church]]es. Its Romanesque forms, with powerful round arches and massive stone piers, represented an architectural tradition older than the [[gothic-art|Gothic style]] of Notre-Dame.

## In the Novel

### The Abbey and Its Bourg

The abbey gave its name to one of the earliest suburbs of [[paris|Paris]]: "The Bourg Saint-Germain, already a large community" ((021-219)). This bourg grew around the monastery, forming an independent settlement on the left bank outside [[paris|the city]] walls. Hugo notes its commercial importance, observing that "Saint-Germain, where the market is situated" ((021-223)) was a center of trade.

The Porte Saint-Germain was one of [[paris|the city]]'s six principal gates: "the Porte Saint-Germain" ((020-159)). Hugo describes it as a "great thoroughfare" ((021-009)) connecting [[paris|the city]] to the southern roads.

### Architectural Comparisons

Hugo repeatedly invokes Saint-Germain-des-Prés in his surveys of [[paris|Paris]]ian [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]]. He notes that the [[religion|church]]'s triple bells could be heard across [[paris|the city]]: "come from the triple peal of Saint-Germain-des-Prés" ((024-064)). The abbey is described as having "splendor only to Saint-Germain des Prés" ((022-202)), positioning it as a benchmark for ecclesiastical magnificence.

The "new apse of Saint-Germain le Vieux" ((021-081)) is mentioned in the novel's panoramic description of the left bank, a detail that reveals Hugo's deep [[knowledge]] of the abbey's architectural evolution.

### A Libertine Reputation

An early reference in the novel associates the abbey with a certain worldly character: "Saint-Germain des Prés, that libertine broth" ((006-040)), suggesting that even the great monastery was not immune to the worldly currents of [[paris|Paris]]ian life.

## Architectural Significance

For Hugo, the Romanesque [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] of Saint-Germain-des-Prés represented an essential chapter in the history of French building, predating and in some ways rivaling the Gothic. The massive bell tower of the abbey — "the three Roman spires of Saint-Germain des Prés" ((021-218)) — punctuated the left bank skyline. Alongside Saint-Denis and Saint-Jacques, the abbey formed part of the "triad" ((019-132)) of great Romanesque monuments in and around [[paris|Paris]].
