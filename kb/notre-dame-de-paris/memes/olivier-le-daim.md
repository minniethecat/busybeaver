# Olivier le Daim

Olivier le Daim, known to the court as "Olivier the Devil" (Olivier le
Mauvais), is the barber, valet, and most trusted confidant of King Louis XI.
In a novel populated by nobles, clergy, and scholars, Olivier represents a
distinctively modern form of power: the low-born favorite whose influence
derives not from lineage or office but from the king's ear. Hugo devotes a
striking passage to the phenomenon: "Master Olivier, the princes who reign
over great men are the barbers" [075-190].

## Role in the Novel

Olivier appears during the key scenes at the Bastille, where Louis XI has
retreated to manage the crisis of the attack on Notre-Dame. He serves as
the king's sounding board, executing orders, conveying messages, and
providing the malicious commentary that Louis XI values — "Sire, resumed
Olivier le Daim, with the malicious air of a courtier who feels himself
necessary" [080-017]. His role is that of the indispensable functionary:
he does not make policy, but policy cannot be made without him.

## Biography

### Rise from Low Origins

Olivier le Daim's historical rise is one of the fairy tales of the
fifteenth century. Born Olivier Necker, the son of a Flemish barber, he
entered Louis XI's service through the most intimate of offices —
the royal barber. Louis XI, who detested the feudal nobility and preferred
the company of commoners he could trust (and dismiss at will), elevated
Olivier to positions of immense informal authority. Hugo alludes to this
trajectory when he notes that Olivier rose "thanks to his patron, Olivier
Le Daim, barber to Louis XI" — the recursive phrasing capturing the
incongruity of a barber as patron [026-013].

### Master Olivier at the Bastille

The Bastille scenes present Olivier at the height of his power. He stands
at the king's side, receiving orders, offering counsel, managing the
mechanics of royal governance. When Louis XI contemplates the expense of
maintaining his network of cages for prisoners, it is Olivier who produces
the account book [076-086]. When the king wants to inspect a cage, it is
Olivier who leads him there [076-104]. The relationship between king and
barber is rendered in casual, almost domestic terms: "Continue, Olivier"
[076-025], "Olivier, come to an understanding about this" [076-083].

### The King's Barber

Hugo explicitly theorizes the barber's power: "The princes who reign over
great men are the barbers" [075-190]. The aphorism captures something about
the nature of power: proximity matters more than rank, and the person who
holds the razor at the king's throat holds a kind of power that no duke can
claim. Olivier's influence is the dark side of Louis XI's anti-aristocratic
policy — he replaces hereditary nobles with personal dependents, but
personal dependence breeds its own kind of corruption.

### Historical Fate

Hugo notes that the historical Olivier le Daim was hanged two days after
Louis XI's death [089-016]. The detail is significant: the favorite who
owes everything to the king's favor is left with nothing when the king
dies. The barber's power, absolute as it seemed, was always contingent.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Olivier is characterized by a "malicious air" — the knowingness of the
functionary who understands the machinery of power better than those who
nominally command it [080-017]. He is efficient, cynical, and entirely
loyal to the king, not out of principle but out of self-interest: his
fate is tied to Louis XI's in a way that the great nobles' fates are not.

### Relationships

- **[[louis-xi]]**: The relationship between king and barber is the
  novel's most penetrating portrait of informal power. Louis XI trusts
  Olivier precisely because Olivier has no independent standing — his
  loyalty is guaranteed by his dependence.

- **[[claude-frollo]]**: Olivier and Frollo represent contrasting modes
  of service to the king: the barber through personal intimacy, the
  archdeacon through institutional authority.

## Significance

Olivier le Daim is Hugo's exemplar of a political truth that transcends
the medieval setting: in any court, the real power belongs not to those
with the grandest titles but to those with the closest access. The barber
who holds the king's ear is mightier than the duke who commands an army —
a fact that Louis XI understands perfectly, and that Hugo presents with
the cool clarity of a political analyst. Olivier's eventual fate — hanged
when his patron died — is the novel's quiet warning about the fragility of
borrowed power.
