---[086-001] She looked at him like one who does not understand.

[086-002] “_Tête Dieu!_” continued Tristan, “why do you try to prevent this

[086-003] sorceress being hung as it pleases the king?”

[086-004] The wretched woman began to laugh in her wild way.

[086-005] “Why? She is my daughter.”

[086-006] The tone in which she pronounced these words made even Henriet

[086-007] Cousin shudder.

[086-008] “I am sorry for that,” said the provost, “but it is the king’s

[086-009] good pleasure.”

[086-010] She cried, redoubling her terrible laugh,—

[086-011] “What is your king to me? I tell you that she is my daughter!”

[086-012] “Pierce the wall,” said Tristan.

[086-013] In order to make a sufficiently wide opening, it sufficed to

[086-014] dislodge one course of stone below the window. When the mother

[086-015] heard the picks and crowbars mining her fortress, she uttered a

[086-016] terrible cry; then she began to stride about her cell with

[086-017] frightful swiftness, a wild beasts’ habit which her cage had

[086-018] imparted to her. She no longer said anything, but her eyes

[086-019] flamed. The soldiers were chilled to the very soul.

[086-020] All at once she seized her paving stone, laughed, and hurled it

[086-021] with both fists upon the workmen. The stone, badly flung (for her

[086-022] hands trembled), touched no one, and fell short under the feet of

[086-023] Tristan’s horse. She gnashed her teeth.

[086-024] In the meantime, although the sun had not yet risen, it was broad

[086-025] daylight; a beautiful rose color enlivened the ancient, decayed

[086-026] chimneys of the Pillar-House. It was the hour when the earliest

[086-027] windows of the great city open joyously on the roofs. Some

[086-028] workmen, a few fruit-sellers on their way to the markets on their

[086-029] asses, began to traverse the Grève; they halted for a moment

[086-030] before this group of soldiers clustered round the Rat-Hole,

[086-031] stared at it with an air of astonishment and passed on.

[086-032] The recluse had gone and seated herself by her daughter, covering

[086-033] her with her body, in front of her, with staring eyes, listening

[086-034] to the poor child, who did not stir, but who kept murmuring in a

[086-035] low voice, these words only, “Phœbus! Phœbus!” In proportion as

[086-036] the work of the demolishers seemed to advance, the mother

[086-037] mechanically retreated, and pressed the young girl closer and

[086-038] closer to the wall. All at once, the recluse beheld the stone

[086-039] (for she was standing guard and never took her eyes from it),

[086-040] move, and she heard Tristan’s voice encouraging the workers. Then

[086-041] she aroused from the depression into which she had fallen during

[086-042] the last few moments, cried out, and as she spoke, her voice now

[086-043] rent the ear like a saw, then stammered as though all kind of

[086-044] maledictions were pressing to her lips to burst forth at once.

[086-045] “Ho! ho! ho! Why this is terrible! You are ruffians! Are you

[086-046] really going to take my daughter? Oh! the cowards! Oh! the

[086-047] hangman lackeys! the wretched, blackguard assassins! Help! help!

[086-048] fire! Will they take my child from me like this? Who is it then

[086-049] who is called the good God?”

[086-050] Then, addressing Tristan, foaming at the mouth, with wild eyes,

[086-051] all bristling and on all fours like a female panther,—

[086-052] “Draw near and take my daughter! Do not you understand that this

[086-053] woman tells you that she is my daughter? Do you know what it is

[086-054] to have a child? Eh! lynx, have you never lain with your female?

[086-055] have you never had a cub? and if you have little ones, when they

[086-056] howl have you nothing in your vitals that moves?”

[086-057] “Throw down the stone,” said Tristan; “it no longer holds.”

[086-058] The crowbars raised the heavy course. It was, as we have said,

[086-059] the mother’s last bulwark.

[086-060] She threw herself upon it, she tried to hold it back; she

[086-061] scratched the stone with her nails, but the massive block, set in

[086-062] movement by six men, escaped her and glided gently to the ground

[086-063] along the iron levers.

[086-064] The mother, perceiving an entrance effected, fell down in front

[086-065] of the opening, barricading the breach with her body, beating the

[086-066] pavement with her head, and shrieking with a voice rendered so

[086-067] hoarse by fatigue that it was hardly audible,—

[086-068] “Help! fire! fire!”

[086-069] “Now take the wench,” said Tristan, still impassive.

[086-070] The mother gazed at the soldiers in such formidable fashion that

[086-071] they were more inclined to retreat than to advance.

[086-072] “Come, now,” repeated the provost. “Here you, Rennet Cousin!”

[086-073] No one took a step.

[086-074] The provost swore,—

[086-075] “_Tête de Christ!_ my men of war! afraid of a woman!”

[086-076] “Monseigneur,” said Rennet, “do you call that a woman?”

[086-077] “She has the mane of a lion,” said another.

[086-078] “Come!” repeated the provost, “the gap is wide enough. Enter

[086-079] three abreast, as at the breach of Pontoise. Let us make an end

[086-080] of it, death of Mahom! I will make two pieces of the first man

[086-081] who draws back!”

[086-082] Placed between the provost and the mother, both threatening, the

[086-083] soldiers hesitated for a moment, then took their resolution, and

[086-084] advanced towards the Rat-Hole.

[086-085] When the recluse saw this, she rose abruptly on her knees, flung

[086-086] aside her hair from her face, then let her thin flayed hands fall

[086-087] by her side. Then great tears fell, one by one, from her eyes;

[086-088] they flowed down her cheeks through a furrow, like a torrent

[086-089] through a bed which it has hollowed for itself.

[086-090] At the same time she began to speak, but in a voice so

[086-091] supplicating, so gentle, so submissive, so heartrending, that

[086-092] more than one old convict-warder around Tristan who must have

[086-093] devoured human flesh wiped his eyes.

[086-094] “Messeigneurs! messieurs the sergeants, one word. There is one

[086-095] thing which I must say to you. She is my daughter, do you see? my

[086-096] dear little daughter whom I had lost! Listen. It is quite a

[086-097] history. Consider that I knew the sergeants very well. They were

[086-098] always good to me in the days when the little boys threw stones

[086-099] at me, because I led a life of pleasure. Do you see? You will

[086-100] leave me my child when you know! I was a poor woman of the town.

[086-101] It was the Bohemians who stole her from me. And I kept her shoe

[086-102] for fifteen years. Stay, here it is. That was the kind of foot

[086-103] which she had. At Reims! La Chantefleurie! Rue Folle-Peine!

[086-104] Perchance, you knew about that. It was I. In your youth, then,

[086-105] there was a merry time, when one passed good hours. You will take

[086-106] pity on me, will you not, gentlemen? The gypsies stole her from

[086-107] me; they hid her from me for fifteen years. I thought her dead.

[086-108] Fancy, my good friends, believed her to be dead. I have passed

[086-109] fifteen years here in this cellar, without a fire in winter. It

[086-110] is hard. The poor, dear little shoe! I have cried so much that

[086-111] the good God has heard me. This night he has given my daughter

[086-112] back to me. It is a miracle of the good God. She was not dead.

[086-113] You will not take her from me, I am sure. If it were myself, I

[086-114] would say nothing; but she, a child of sixteen! Leave her time to

[086-115] see the sun! What has she done to you? nothing at all. Nor have

[086-116] I. If you did but know that she is all I have, that I am old,

[086-117] that she is a blessing which the Holy Virgin has sent to me! And

[086-118] then, you are all so good! You did not know that she was my

[086-119] daughter; but now you do know it. Oh! I love her! Monsieur, the

[086-120] grand provost. I would prefer a stab in my own vitals to a

[086-121] scratch on her finger! You have the air of such a good lord! What

[086-122] I have told you explains the matter, does it not? Oh! if you have

[086-123] had a mother, monseigneur! you are the captain, leave me my

[086-124] child! Consider that I pray you on my knees, as one prays to

[086-125] Jesus Christ! I ask nothing of any one; I am from Reims,

[086-126] gentlemen; I own a little field inherited from my uncle, Mahiet

[086-127] Pradon. I am no beggar. I wish nothing, but I do want my child!

[086-128] oh! I want to keep my child! The good God, who is the master, has

[086-129] not given her back to me for nothing! The king! you say the king!

[086-130] It would not cause him much pleasure to have my little daughter

[086-131] killed! And then, the king is good! she is my daughter! she is my

[086-132] own daughter! She belongs not to the king! she is not yours! I

[086-133] want to go away! we want to go away! and when two women pass, one

[086-134] a mother and the other a daughter, one lets them go! Let us pass!

[086-135] we belong in Reims. Oh! you are very good, messieurs the

[086-136] sergeants, I love you all. You will not take my dear little one,

[086-137] it is impossible! It is utterly impossible, is it not? My child,

[086-138] my child!”

[086-139] We will not try to give an idea of her gestures, her tone, of the

[086-140] tears which she swallowed as she spoke, of the hands which she

[086-141] clasped and then wrung, of the heart-breaking smiles, of the

[086-142] swimming glances, of the groans, the sighs, the miserable and

[086-143] affecting cries which she mingled with her disordered, wild, and

[086-144] incoherent words. When she became silent Tristan l’Hermite

[086-145] frowned, but it was to conceal a tear which welled up in his

[086-146] tiger’s eye. He conquered this weakness, however, and said in a

[086-147] curt tone,—

[086-148] “The king wills it.”

[086-149] Then he bent down to the ear of Rennet Cousin, and said to him in

[086-150] a very low tone,—

[086-151] “Make an end of it quickly!” Possibly, the redoubtable provost

[086-152] felt his heart also failing him.

[086-153] The executioner and the sergeants entered the cell. The mother

[086-154] offered no resistance, only she dragged herself towards her

[086-155] daughter and threw herself bodily upon her. The gypsy beheld the

[086-156] soldiers approach. The horror of death reanimated her,—

[086-157] “Mother!” she shrieked, in a tone of indescribable distress,

[086-158] “Mother! they are coming! defend me!”

[086-159] “Yes, my love, I am defending you!” replied the mother, in a

[086-160] dying voice; and clasping her closely in her arms, she covered

[086-161] her with kisses. The two lying thus on the earth, the mother upon

[086-162] the daughter, presented a spectacle worthy of pity.

[086-163] Rennet Cousin grasped the young girl by the middle of her body,

[086-164] beneath her beautiful shoulders. When she felt that hand, she

[086-165] cried, “Heuh!” and fainted. The executioner who was shedding

[086-166] large tears upon her, drop by drop, was about to bear her away in

[086-167] his arms. He tried to detach the mother, who had, so to speak,

[086-168] knotted her hands around her daughter’s waist; but she clung so

[086-169] strongly to her child, that it was impossible to separate them.

[086-170] Then Rennet Cousin dragged the young girl outside the cell, and

[086-171] the mother after her. The mother’s eyes were also closed.

[086-172] At that moment, the sun rose, and there was already on the Place

[086-173] a fairly numerous assembly of people who looked on from a

[086-174] distance at what was being thus dragged along the pavement to the

[086-175] gibbet. For that was Provost Tristan’s way at executions. He had

[086-176] a passion for preventing the approach of the curious.

[086-177] There was no one at the windows. Only at a distance, at the

[086-178] summit of that one of the towers of Notre-Dame which commands the

[086-179] Grève, two men outlined in black against the light morning sky,

[086-180] and who seemed to be looking on, were visible.

[086-181] Rennet Cousin paused at the foot of the fatal ladder, with that

[086-182] which he was dragging, and, barely breathing, with so much pity

[086-183] did the thing inspire him, he passed the rope around the lovely

[086-184] neck of the young girl. The unfortunate child felt the horrible

[086-185] touch of the hemp. She raised her eyelids, and saw the fleshless

[086-186] arm of the stone gallows extended above her head. Then she shook

[086-187] herself and shrieked in a loud and heartrending voice: “No! no! I

[086-188] will not!” Her mother, whose head was buried and concealed in her

[086-189] daughter’s garments, said not a word; only her whole body could

[086-190] be seen to quiver, and she was heard to redouble her kisses on

[086-191] her child. The executioner took advantage of this moment to

[086-192] hastily loose the arms with which she clasped the condemned girl.

[086-193] Either through exhaustion or despair, she let him have his way.

[086-194] Then he took the young girl on his shoulder, from which the

[086-195] charming creature hung, gracefully bent over his large head. Then

[086-196] he set his foot on the ladder in order to ascend.
