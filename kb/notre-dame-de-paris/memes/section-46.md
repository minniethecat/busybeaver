---[046-001] Then he hastily donned his long jacket with furred half-sleeves,

[046-002] picked up his cap, and went out like a man driven to desperation.

[046-003] He descended the Rue de la Harpe toward the City. As he passed

[046-004] the Rue de la Huchette, the odor of those admirable spits, which

[046-005] were incessantly turning, tickled his olfactory apparatus, and he

[046-006] bestowed a loving glance toward the Cyclopean roast, which one

[046-007] day drew from the Franciscan friar, Calatagirone, this pathetic

[046-008] exclamation: _Veramente, queste rotisserie sono cosa

[046-009] stupenda!_[36] But Jehan had not the wherewithal to buy a

[046-010] breakfast, and he plunged, with a profound sigh, under the

[046-011] gateway of the Petit-Châtelet, that enormous double trefoil of

[046-012] massive towers which guarded the entrance to the City.

[046-013] He did not even take the trouble to cast a stone in passing, as

[046-014] was the usage, at the miserable statue of that Périnet Leclerc

[046-015] who had delivered up the Paris of Charles VI. to the English, a

[046-016] crime which his effigy, its face battered with stones and soiled

[046-017] with mud, expiated for three centuries at the corner of the Rue

[046-018] de la Harpe and the Rue de Buci, as in an eternal pillory.

[046-019] The Petit-Pont traversed, the Rue Neuve-Sainte-Geneviève crossed,

[046-020] Jehan de Molendino found himself in front of Notre-Dame. Then

[046-021] indecision seized upon him once more, and he paced for several

[046-022] minutes round the statue of M. Legris, repeating to himself with

[046-023] anguish: “The sermon is sure, the crown is doubtful.”

[046-024] He stopped a beadle who emerged from the cloister,—“Where is

[046-025] monsieur the archdeacon of Josas?”

[046-026] “I believe that he is in his secret cell in the tower,” said the

[046-027] beadle; “I should advise you not to disturb him there, unless you

[046-028] come from some one like the pope or monsieur the king.”

[046-029] Jehan clapped his hands.

[046-030] “_Bédiable!_ here’s a magnificent chance to see the famous

[046-031] sorcery cell!”

[046-032] This reflection having brought him to a decision, he plunged

[046-033] resolutely into the small black doorway, and began the ascent of

[046-034] the spiral of Saint-Gilles, which leads to the upper stories of

[046-035] the tower. “I am going to see,” he said to himself on the way.

[046-036] “By the ravens of the Holy Virgin! it must needs be a curious

[046-037] thing, that cell which my reverend brother hides so secretly!

[046-038] ’Tis said that he lights up the kitchens of hell there, and that

[046-039] he cooks the philosopher’s stone there over a hot fire. _Bédieu!_

[046-040] I care no more for the philosopher’s stone than for a pebble, and

[046-041] I would rather find over his furnace an omelette of Easter eggs

[046-042] and bacon, than the biggest philosopher’s stone in the world.”’

[046-043] On arriving at the gallery of slender columns, he took breath for

[046-044] a moment, and swore against the interminable staircase by I know

[046-045] not how many million cartloads of devils; then he resumed his

[046-046] ascent through the narrow door of the north tower, now closed to

[046-047] the public. Several moments after passing the bell chamber, he

[046-048] came upon a little landing-place, built in a lateral niche, and

[046-049] under the vault of a low, pointed door, whose enormous lock and

[046-050] strong iron bars he was enabled to see through a loophole pierced

[046-051] in the opposite circular wall of the staircase. Persons desirous

[046-052] of visiting this door at the present day will recognize it by

[046-053] this inscription engraved in white letters on the black wall:

[046-054] “J’ADORE CORALIE, 1823. SIGNÉ UGÈNE.” “Signé” stands in the text.

[046-055] “Ugh!” said the scholar; “’tis here, no doubt.”

[046-056] The key was in the lock, the door was very close to him; he gave

[046-057] it a gentle push and thrust his head through the opening.

[046-058] The reader cannot have failed to turn over the admirable works of

[046-059] Rembrandt, that Shakespeare of painting. Amid so many marvellous

[046-060] engravings, there is one etching in particular, which is supposed

[046-061] to represent Doctor Faust, and which it is impossible to

[046-062] contemplate without being dazzled. It represents a gloomy cell;

[046-063] in the centre is a table loaded with hideous objects; skulls,

[046-064] spheres, alembics, compasses, hieroglyphic parchments. The doctor

[046-065] is before this table clad in his large coat and covered to the

[046-066] very eyebrows with his furred cap. He is visible only to his

[046-067] waist. He has half risen from his immense arm-chair, his clenched

[046-068] fists rest on the table, and he is gazing with curiosity and

[046-069] terror at a large luminous circle, formed of magic letters, which

[046-070] gleams from the wall beyond, like the solar spectrum in a dark

[046-071] chamber. This cabalistic sun seems to tremble before the eye, and

[046-072] fills the wan cell with its mysterious radiance. It is horrible

[046-073] and it is beautiful.

[046-074] Something very similar to Faust’s cell presented itself to

[046-075] Jehan’s view, when he ventured his head through the half-open

[046-076] door. It also was a gloomy and sparsely lighted retreat. There

[046-077] also stood a large arm-chair and a large table, compasses,

[046-078] alembics, skeletons of animals suspended from the ceiling, a

[046-079] globe rolling on the floor, hippocephali mingled promiscuously

[046-080] with drinking cups, in which quivered leaves of gold, skulls

[046-081] placed upon vellum checkered with figures and characters, huge

[046-082] manuscripts piled up wide open, without mercy on the cracking

[046-083] corners of the parchment; in short, all the rubbish of science,

[046-084] and everywhere on this confusion dust and spiders’ webs; but

[046-085] there was no circle of luminous letters, no doctor in an ecstasy

[046-086] contemplating the flaming vision, as the eagle gazes upon the

[046-087] sun.

[046-088] Nevertheless, the cell was not deserted. A man was seated in the

[046-089] arm-chair, and bending over the table. Jehan, to whom his back

[046-090] was turned, could see only his shoulders and the back of his

[046-091] skull; but he had no difficulty in recognizing that bald head,

[046-092] which nature had provided with an eternal tonsure, as though

[046-093] desirous of marking, by this external symbol, the archdeacon’s

[046-094] irresistible clerical vocation.

[046-095] Jehan accordingly recognized his brother; but the door had been

[046-096] opened so softly, that nothing warned Dom Claude of his presence.

[046-097] The inquisitive scholar took advantage of this circumstance to

[046-098] examine the cell for a few moments at his leisure. A large

[046-099] furnace, which he had not at first observed, stood to the left of

[046-100] the arm-chair, beneath the window. The ray of light which

[046-101] penetrated through this aperture made its way through a spider’s

[046-102] circular web, which tastefully inscribed its delicate rose in the

[046-103] arch of the window, and in the centre of which the insect

[046-104] architect hung motionless, like the hub of this wheel of lace.

[046-105] Upon the furnace were accumulated in disorder, all sorts of

[046-106] vases, earthenware bottles, glass retorts, and mattresses of

[046-107] charcoal. Jehan observed, with a sigh, that there was no

[046-108] frying-pan. “How cold the kitchen utensils are!” he said to

[046-109] himself.

[046-110] In fact, there was no fire in the furnace, and it seemed as

[046-111] though none had been lighted for a long time. A glass mask, which

[046-112] Jehan noticed among the utensils of alchemy, and which served no

[046-113] doubt, to protect the archdeacon’s face when he was working over

[046-114] some substance to be dreaded, lay in one corner covered with dust

[046-115] and apparently forgotten. Beside it lay a pair of bellows no less

[046-116] dusty, the upper side of which bore this inscription incrusted in

[046-117] copper letters: SPIRA SPERA.

[046-118] Other inscriptions were written, in accordance with the fashion

[046-119] of the hermetics, in great numbers on the walls; some traced with

[046-120] ink, others engraved with a metal point. There were, moreover,

[046-121] Gothic letters, Hebrew letters, Greek letters, and Roman letters,

[046-122] pell-mell; the inscriptions overflowed at haphazard, on top of

[046-123] each other, the more recent effacing the more ancient, and all

[046-124] entangled with each other, like the branches in a thicket, like

[046-125] pikes in an affray. It was, in fact, a strangely confused

[046-126] mingling of all human philosophies, all reveries, all human

[046-127] wisdom. Here and there one shone out from among the rest like a

[046-128] banner among lance heads. Generally, it was a brief Greek or

[046-129] Roman device, such as the Middle Ages knew so well how to

[046-130] formulate.—_Unde? Inde?—Homo homini monstrum—Astra, castra,

[046-131] nomen, numen._—Μέγα βιβλίον, μέγα κακόν.—_Sapere aude. Fiat ubi

[046-132] vult_—etc.; sometimes a word devoid of all apparent sense,

[046-133] Ἀναγκοφαγία, which possibly contained a bitter allusion to the

[046-134] regime of the cloister; sometimes a simple maxim of clerical

[046-135] discipline formulated in a regular hexameter _Cœlestem dominum

[046-136] terrestrem dicite dominum_. There was also Hebrew jargon, of

[046-137] which Jehan, who as yet knew but little Greek, understood

[046-138] nothing; and all were traversed in every direction by stars, by

[046-139] figures of men or animals, and by intersecting triangles; and

[046-140] this contributed not a little to make the scrawled wall of the

[046-141] cell resemble a sheet of paper over which a monkey had drawn back

[046-142] and forth a pen filled with ink.

[046-143] The whole chamber, moreover, presented a general aspect of

[046-144] abandonment and dilapidation; and the bad state of the utensils

[046-145] induced the supposition that their owner had long been distracted

[046-146] from his labors by other preoccupations. Meanwhile, this master,

[046-147] bent over a vast manuscript, ornamented with fantastical

[046-148] illustrations, appeared to be tormented by an idea which

[046-149] incessantly mingled with his meditations. That at least was

[046-150] Jehan’s idea, when he heard him exclaim, with the thoughtful

[046-151] breaks of a dreamer thinking aloud,—

[046-152] “Yes, Manou said it, and Zoroaster taught it! the sun is born

[046-153] from fire, the moon from the sun; fire is the soul of the

[046-154] universe; its elementary atoms pour forth and flow incessantly

[046-155] upon the world through infinite channels! At the point where

[046-156] these currents intersect each other in the heavens, they produce

[046-157] light; at their points of intersection on earth, they produce

[046-158] gold. Light, gold; the same thing! From fire to the concrete

[046-159] state. The difference between the visible and the palpable,

[046-160] between the fluid and the solid in the same substance, between

[046-161] water and ice, nothing more. These are no dreams; it is the

[046-162] general law of nature. But what is one to do in order to extract

[046-163] from science the secret of this general law? What! this light

[046-164] which inundates my hand is gold! These same atoms dilated in

[046-165] accordance with a certain law need only be condensed in

[046-166] accordance with another law. How is it to be done? Some have

[046-167] fancied by burying a ray of sunlight, Averroës,—yes, ’tis

[046-168] Averroës,—Averroës buried one under the first pillar on the left

[046-169] of the sanctuary of the Koran, in the great Mahometan mosque of

[046-170] Cordova; but the vault cannot be opened for the purpose of

[046-171] ascertaining whether the operation has succeeded, until after the

[046-172] lapse of eight thousand years.

[046-173] “The devil!” said Jehan, to himself, “’tis a long while to wait

[046-174] for a crown!”

[046-175] “Others have thought,” continued the dreamy archdeacon, “that it

[046-176] would be better worth while to operate upon a ray of Sirius. But

[046-177] ’tis exceeding hard to obtain this ray pure, because of the

[046-178] simultaneous presence of other stars whose rays mingle with it.

[046-179] Flamel esteemed it more simple to operate upon terrestrial fire.

[046-180] Flamel! there’s predestination in the name! _Flamma!_ yes, fire.

[046-181] All lies there. The diamond is contained in the carbon, gold is

[046-182] in the fire. But how to extract it? Magistri affirms that there

[046-183] are certain feminine names, which possess a charm so sweet and

[046-184] mysterious, that it suffices to pronounce them during the

[046-185] operation. Let us read what Manon says on the matter: ‘Where

[046-186] women are honored, the divinities are rejoiced; where they are

[046-187] despised, it is useless to pray to God. The mouth of a woman is

[046-188] constantly pure; it is a running water, it is a ray of sunlight.

[046-189] The name of a woman should be agreeable, sweet, fanciful; it

[046-190] should end in long vowels, and resemble words of benediction.’

[046-191] Yes, the sage is right; in truth, Maria, Sophia, la

[046-192] Esmeral—Damnation! always that thought!”

[046-193] And he closed the book violently.

[046-194] He passed his hand over his brow, as though to brush away the

[046-195] idea which assailed him; then he took from the table a nail and a

[046-196] small hammer, whose handle was curiously painted with cabalistic

[046-197] letters.

[046-198] “For some time,” he said with a bitter smile, “I have failed in

[046-199] all my experiments! one fixed idea possesses me, and sears my

[046-200] brain like fire. I have not even been able to discover the secret

[046-201] of Cassiodorus, whose lamp burned without wick and without oil. A

[046-202] simple matter, nevertheless—”
