# Reims

Reims was the sacred city of French monarchy, where kings from Clovis to Charles X received their consecration at the Cathedral of Notre-Dame de Reims. In *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, Hugo references Reims as a symbol of royal authority and provincial France, contrasting its traditions with the life of [[paris|Paris]].

## Overview

Reims appears in the novel primarily through its cathedral and its association with royal coronations. Hugo mentions it alongside other great French cathedrals: "there is the Cathedral of Reims, there is the Sainte-Croix of..." ((019-180)), positioning it within the grand architectural heritage of medieval France. The city serves as a touchstone for the novel's exploration of monarchical power and ecclesiastical ceremony.

## In the Novel

### Royal Ceremony and Authority

The most significant reference to Reims concerns the coronation of [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]: "in '61, at the consecration at Reims, eighteen years ago" ((036-069)). This places the king's coronation within the living memory of the novel's characters, grounding the narrative's political context in historical fact. The novel's [[louis-xi|Louis XI]], who appears in the later chapters, is a king whose authority was sanctified at Reims.

### Provincial Comparison

Throughout the novel, characters from outside [[paris|Paris]] invoke Reims to establish their origins or to compare provincial customs with those of the capital. A provincial character declares "at Reims" ((036-046)), emphasizing [[paris|the city]]'s identity as a significant regional center distinct from [[paris|Paris]]. The pillory of Reims is unfavorably compared to that of [[paris|Paris]]: "What is your pillory at Reims? A miserable cage" ((036-047)), establishing [[paris|Paris]]'s superiority even in instruments of punishment.

### The Cloth Market

The cloth market of Reims is mentioned in a passage that celebrates the commercial vitality of [[paris|the city]]: "at the cloth market in Reims! We..." ((036-049)). This detail situates Reims within the economic geography of medieval France, alongside its ceremonial and architectural importance.

### Reims as Narrative Setting

A substantial portion of the novel's Reims references c[[love-and-desire|lust]]er in a single chapter where a character recounts tales from [[paris|the city]]: "One day there arrived in Reims a very..." ((037-095)). These stories, involving fortune-tellers who "came to Reims to tell fortunes in the name..." ((037-113)), weave [[paris|the city]] into the novel's broader tapestry of medieval life, superstition, and cross-European travel — the narrative even traces a journey to "Reims through Poland" ((037-106)).

## Architectural Significance

Hugo places Reims Cathedral within his broader meditation on [[gothic-art|Gothic architecture]]. The cathedral is listed among the great monuments of medieval France, a peer to [[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]] itself. In the author's architectural philosophy, expressed throughout the novel, Reims represents the high watermark of Gothic achievement.
