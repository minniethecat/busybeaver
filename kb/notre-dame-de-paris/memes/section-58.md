---[058-001] She interrupted with a terrible and thrilling laugh.

[058-002] “Look, father, you have blood on your fingers!”

[058-003] The priest remained for several moments as though petrified, with

[058-004] his eyes fixed upon his hand.

[058-005] “Well, yes!” he resumed at last, with strange gentleness, “insult

[058-006] me, scoff at me, overwhelm me with scorn! but come, come. Let us

[058-007] make haste. It is to be to-morrow, I tell you. The gibbet on the

[058-008] Grève, you know it? it stands always ready. It is horrible! to

[058-009] see you ride in that tumbrel! Oh mercy! Until now I have never

[058-010] felt the power of my love for you.—Oh! follow me. You shall take

[058-011] your time to love me after I have saved you. You shall hate me as

[058-012] long as you will. But come. To-morrow! to-morrow! the gallows!

[058-013] your execution! Oh! save yourself! spare me!”

[058-014] He seized her arm, he was beside himself, he tried to drag her

[058-015] away.

[058-016] She fixed her eye intently on him.

[058-017] “What has become of my Phœbus?”

[058-018] “Ah!” said the priest, releasing her arm, “you are pitiless.”

[058-019] “What has become of Phœbus?” she repeated coldly.

[058-020] “He is dead!” cried the priest.

[058-021] “Dead!” said she, still icy and motionless “then why do you talk

[058-022] to me of living?”

[058-023] He was not listening to her.

[058-024] “Oh! yes,” said he, as though speaking to himself, “he certainly

[058-025] must be dead. The blade pierced deeply. I believe I touched his

[058-026] heart with the point. Oh! my very soul was at the end of the

[058-027] dagger!”

[058-028] The young girl flung herself upon him like a raging tigress, and

[058-029] pushed him upon the steps of the staircase with supernatural

[058-030] force.

[058-031] “Begone, monster! Begone, assassin! Leave me to die! May the

[058-032] blood of both of us make an eternal stain upon your brow! Be

[058-033] thine, priest! Never! never! Nothing shall unite us! not hell

[058-034] itself! Go, accursed man! Never!”

[058-035] The priest had stumbled on the stairs. He silently disentangled

[058-036] his feet from the folds of his robe, picked up his lantern again,

[058-037] and slowly began the ascent of the steps which led to the door;

[058-038] he opened the door and passed through it.

[058-039] All at once, the young girl beheld his head reappear; it wore a

[058-040] frightful expression, and he cried, hoarse with rage and

[058-041] despair,—

[058-042] “I tell you he is dead!”

[058-043] She fell face downwards upon the floor, and there was no longer

[058-044] any sound audible in the cell than the sob of the drop of water

[058-045] which made the pool palpitate amid the darkness.

[058-046] CHAPTER V. THE MOTHER.

[058-047] I do not believe that there is anything sweeter in the world than

[058-048] the ideas which awake in a mother’s heart at the sight of her

[058-049] child’s tiny shoe; especially if it is a shoe for festivals, for

[058-050] Sunday, for baptism, the shoe embroidered to the very sole, a

[058-051] shoe in which the infant has not yet taken a step. That shoe has

[058-052] so much grace and daintiness, it is so impossible for it to walk,

[058-053] that it seems to the mother as though she saw her child. She

[058-054] smiles upon it, she kisses it, she talks to it; she asks herself

[058-055] whether there can actually be a foot so tiny; and if the child be

[058-056] absent, the pretty shoe suffices to place the sweet and fragile

[058-057] creature before her eyes. She thinks she sees it, she does see

[058-058] it, complete, living, joyous, with its delicate hands, its round

[058-059] head, its pure lips, its serene eyes whose white is blue. If it

[058-060] is in winter, it is yonder, crawling on the carpet, it is

[058-061] laboriously climbing upon an ottoman, and the mother trembles

[058-062] lest it should approach the fire. If it is summer time, it crawls

[058-063] about the yard, in the garden, plucks up the grass between the

[058-064] paving-stones, gazes innocently at the big dogs, the big horses,

[058-065] without fear, plays with the shells, with the flowers, and makes

[058-066] the gardener grumble because he finds sand in the flower-beds and

[058-067] earth in the paths. Everything laughs, and shines and plays

[058-068] around it, like it, even the breath of air and the ray of sun

[058-069] which vie with each other in disporting among the silky ringlets

[058-070] of its hair. The shoe shows all this to the mother, and makes her

[058-071] heart melt as fire melts wax.

[058-072] But when the child is lost, these thousand images of joy, of

[058-073] charms, of tenderness, which throng around the little shoe,

[058-074] become so many horrible things. The pretty broidered shoe is no

[058-075] longer anything but an instrument of torture which eternally

[058-076] crushes the heart of the mother. It is always the same fibre

[058-077] which vibrates, the tenderest and most sensitive; but instead of

[058-078] an angel caressing it, it is a demon who is wrenching at it.

[058-079] One May morning, when the sun was rising on one of those dark

[058-080] blue skies against which Garofolo loves to place his Descents

[058-081] from the Cross, the recluse of the Tour-Roland heard a sound of

[058-082] wheels, of horses and irons in the Place de Grève. She was

[058-083] somewhat aroused by it, knotted her hair upon her ears in order

[058-084] to deafen herself, and resumed her contemplation, on her knees,

[058-085] of the inanimate object which she had adored for fifteen years.

[058-086] This little shoe was the universe to her, as we have already

[058-087] said. Her thought was shut up in it, and was destined never more

[058-088] to quit it except at death. The sombre cave of the Tour-Roland

[058-089] alone knew how many bitter imprecations, touching complaints,

[058-090] prayers and sobs she had wafted to heaven in connection with that

[058-091] charming bauble of rose-colored satin. Never was more despair

[058-092] bestowed upon a prettier and more graceful thing.

[058-093] It seemed as though her grief were breaking forth more violently

[058-094] than usual; and she could be heard outside lamenting in a loud

[058-095] and monotonous voice which rent the heart.

[058-096] “Oh my daughter!” she said, “my daughter, my poor, dear little

[058-097] child, so I shall never see thee more! It is over! It always

[058-098] seems to me that it happened yesterday! My God! my God! it would

[058-099] have been better not to give her to me than to take her away so

[058-100] soon. Did you not know that our children are part of ourselves,

[058-101] and that a mother who has lost her child no longer believes in

[058-102] God? Ah! wretch that I am to have gone out that day! Lord! Lord!

[058-103] to have taken her from me thus; you could never have looked at me

[058-104] with her, when I was joyously warming her at my fire, when she

[058-105] laughed as she suckled, when I made her tiny feet creep up my

[058-106] breast to my lips? Oh! if you had looked at that, my God, you

[058-107] would have taken pity on my joy; you would not have taken from me

[058-108] the only love which lingered, in my heart! Was I then, Lord, so

[058-109] miserable a creature, that you could not look at me before

[058-110] condemning me?—Alas! Alas! here is the shoe; where is the foot?

[058-111] where is the rest? Where is the child? My daughter! my daughter!

[058-112] what did they do with thee? Lord, give her back to me. My knees

[058-113] have been worn for fifteen years in praying to thee, my God! Is

[058-114] not that enough? Give her back to me one day, one hour, one

[058-115] minute; one minute, Lord! and then cast me to the demon for all

[058-116] eternity! Oh! if I only knew where the skirt of your garment

[058-117] trails, I would cling to it with both hands, and you would be

[058-118] obliged to give me back my child! Have you no pity on her pretty

[058-119] little shoe? Could you condemn a poor mother to this torture for

[058-120] fifteen years? Good Virgin! good Virgin of heaven! my infant

[058-121] Jesus has been taken from me, has been stolen from me; they

[058-122] devoured her on a heath, they drank her blood, they cracked her

[058-123] bones! Good Virgin, have pity upon me. My daughter, I want my

[058-124] daughter! What is it to me that she is in paradise? I do not want

[058-125] your angel, I want my child! I am a lioness, I want my whelp. Oh!

[058-126] I will writhe on the earth, I will break the stones with my

[058-127] forehead, and I will damn myself, and I will curse you, Lord, if

[058-128] you keep my child from me! you see plainly that my arms are all

[058-129] bitten, Lord! Has the good God no mercy?—Oh! give me only salt

[058-130] and black bread, only let me have my daughter to warm me like a

[058-131] sun! Alas! Lord my God. Alas! Lord my God, I am only a vile

[058-132] sinner; but my daughter made me pious. I was full of religion for

[058-133] the love of her, and I beheld you through her smile as through an

[058-134] opening into heaven. Oh! if I could only once, just once more, a

[058-135] single time, put this shoe on her pretty little pink foot, I

[058-136] would die blessing you, good Virgin. Ah! fifteen years! she will

[058-137] be grown up now!—Unhappy child! what! it is really true then I

[058-138] shall never see her more, not even in heaven, for I shall not go

[058-139] there myself. Oh! what misery to think that here is her shoe, and

[058-140] that that is all!”

[058-141] The unhappy woman flung herself upon that shoe; her consolation

[058-142] and her despair for so many years, and her vitals were rent with

[058-143] sobs as on the first day; because, for a mother who has lost her

[058-144] child, it is always the first day. That grief never grows old.

[058-145] The mourning garments may grow white and threadbare, the heart

[058-146] remains dark.

[058-147] At that moment, the fresh and joyous cries of children passed in

[058-148] front of the cell. Every time that children crossed her vision or

[058-149] struck her ear, the poor mother flung herself into the darkest

[058-150] corner of her sepulchre, and one would have said, that she sought

[058-151] to plunge her head into the stone in order not to hear them. This

[058-152] time, on the contrary, she drew herself upright with a start, and

[058-153] listened eagerly. One of the little boys had just said,—

[058-154] “They are going to hang a gypsy to-day.”

[058-155] With the abrupt leap of that spider which we have seen fling

[058-156] itself upon a fly at the trembling of its web, she rushed to her

[058-157] air-hole, which opened as the reader knows, on the Place de

[058-158] Grève. A ladder had, in fact, been raised up against the

[058-159] permanent gibbet, and the hangman’s assistant was busying himself

[058-160] with adjusting the chains which had been rusted by the rain.

[058-161] There were some people standing about.

[058-162] The laughing group of children was already far away. The sacked

[058-163] nun sought with her eyes some passer-by whom she might question.

[058-164] All at once, beside her cell, she perceived a priest making a

[058-165] pretext of reading the public breviary, but who was much less

[058-166] occupied with the “lectern of latticed iron,” than with the

[058-167] gallows, toward which he cast a fierce and gloomy glance from

[058-168] time to time. She recognized monsieur the archdeacon of Josas, a

[058-169] holy man.

[058-170] “Father,” she inquired, “whom are they about to hang yonder?”

[058-171] The priest looked at her and made no reply; she repeated her

[058-172] question. Then he said,—

[058-173] “I know not.”

[058-174] “Some children said that it was a gypsy,” went on the recluse.

[058-175] “I believe so,” said the priest.

[058-176] Then Paquette la Chantefleurie burst into hyena-like laughter.

[058-177] “Sister,” said the archdeacon, “do you then hate the gypsies

[058-178] heartily?”

[058-179] “Do I hate them!” exclaimed the recluse, “they are vampires,

[058-180] stealers of children! They devoured my little daughter, my child,

[058-181] my only child! I have no longer any heart, they devoured it!”

[058-182] She was frightful. The priest looked at her coldly.

[058-183] “There is one in particular whom I hate, and whom I have cursed,”

[058-184] she resumed; “it is a young one, of the age which my daughter

[058-185] would be if her mother had not eaten my daughter. Every time that

[058-186] that young viper passes in front of my cell, she sets my blood in

[058-187] a ferment.”
