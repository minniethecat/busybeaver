---[005-001] It pains us to say it, but this first ecstasy was speedily

[005-002] disturbed. Hardly had Gringoire raised this intoxicating cup of

[005-003] joy and triumph to his lips, when a drop of bitterness was

[005-004] mingled with it.

[005-005] A tattered mendicant, who could not collect any coins, lost as he

[005-006] was in the midst of the crowd, and who had not probably found

[005-007] sufficient indemnity in the pockets of his neighbors, had hit

[005-008] upon the idea of perching himself upon some conspicuous point, in

[005-009] order to attract looks and alms. He had, accordingly, hoisted

[005-010] himself, during the first verses of the prologue, with the aid of

[005-011] the pillars of the reserve gallery, to the cornice which ran

[005-012] round the balustrade at its lower edge; and there he had seated

[005-013] himself, soliciting the attention and the pity of the multitude,

[005-014] with his rags and a hideous sore which covered his right arm.

[005-015] However, he uttered not a word.

[005-016] The silence which he preserved allowed the prologue to proceed

[005-017] without hindrance, and no perceptible disorder would have ensued,

[005-018] if ill-luck had not willed that the scholar Joannes should catch

[005-019] sight, from the heights of his pillar, of the mendicant and his

[005-020] grimaces. A wild fit of laughter took possession of the young

[005-021] scamp, who, without caring that he was interrupting the

[005-022] spectacle, and disturbing the universal composure, shouted

[005-023] boldly,—

[005-024] “Look! see that sickly creature asking alms!”

[005-025] Any one who has thrown a stone into a frog pond, or fired a shot

[005-026] into a covey of birds, can form an idea of the effect produced by

[005-027] these incongruous words, in the midst of the general attention.

[005-028] It made Gringoire shudder as though it had been an electric

[005-029] shock. The prologue stopped short, and all heads turned

[005-030] tumultuously towards the beggar, who, far from being disconcerted

[005-031] by this, saw, in this incident, a good opportunity for reaping

[005-032] his harvest, and who began to whine in a doleful way, half

[005-033] closing his eyes the while,—“Charity, please!”

[005-034] “Well—upon my soul,” resumed Joannes, “it’s Clopin Trouillefou!

[005-035] Holà hé, my friend, did your sore bother you on the leg, that you

[005-036] have transferred it to your arm?” So saying, with the dexterity

[005-037] of a monkey, he flung a bit of silver into the gray felt hat

[005-038] which the beggar held in his ailing arm. The mendicant received

[005-039] both the alms and the sarcasm without wincing, and continued, in

[005-040] lamentable tones,—

[005-041] “Charity, please!”

[005-042] This episode considerably distracted the attention of the

[005-043] audience; and a goodly number of spectators, among them Robin

[005-044] Poussepain, and all the clerks at their head, gayly applauded

[005-045] this eccentric duet, which the scholar, with his shrill voice,

[005-046] and the mendicant had just improvised in the middle of the

[005-047] prologue.

[005-048] Gringoire was highly displeased. On recovering from his first

[005-049] stupefaction, he bestirred himself to shout, to the four

[005-050] personages on the stage, “Go on! What the devil!—go on!”—without

[005-051] even deigning to cast a glance of disdain upon the two

[005-052] interrupters.

[005-053] At that moment, he felt some one pluck at the hem of his surtout;

[005-054] he turned round, and not without ill-humor, and found

[005-055] considerable difficulty in smiling; but he was obliged to do so,

[005-056] nevertheless. It was the pretty arm of Gisquette la Gencienne,

[005-057] which, passed through the railing, was soliciting his attention

[005-058] in this manner.

[005-059] “Monsieur,” said the young girl, “are they going to continue?”

[005-060] “Of course,” replied Gringoire, a good deal shocked by the

[005-061] question.

[005-062] “In that case, messire,” she resumed, “would you have the

[005-063] courtesy to explain to me—”

[005-064] “What they are about to say?” interrupted Gringoire. “Well,

[005-065] listen.”

[005-066] “No,” said Gisquette, “but what they have said so far.”

[005-067] Gringoire started, like a man whose wound has been probed to the

[005-068] quick.

[005-069] “A plague on the stupid and dull-witted little girl!” he

[005-070] muttered, between his teeth.

[005-071] From that moment forth, Gisquette was nothing to him.

[005-072] In the meantime, the actors had obeyed his injunction, and the

[005-073] public, seeing that they were beginning to speak again, began

[005-074] once more to listen, not without having lost many beauties in the

[005-075] sort of soldered joint which was formed between the two portions

[005-076] of the piece thus abruptly cut short. Gringoire commented on it

[005-077] bitterly to himself. Nevertheless, tranquillity was gradually

[005-078] restored, the scholar held his peace, the mendicant counted over

[005-079] some coins in his hat, and the piece resumed the upper hand.

[005-080] It was, in fact, a very fine work, and one which, as it seems to

[005-081] us, might be put to use to-day, by the aid of a little

[005-082] rearrangement. The exposition, rather long and rather empty, that

[005-083] is to say, according to the rules, was simple; and Gringoire, in

[005-084] the candid sanctuary of his own conscience, admired its

[005-085] clearness. As the reader may surmise, the four allegorical

[005-086] personages were somewhat weary with having traversed the three

[005-087] sections of the world, without having found suitable opportunity

[005-088] for getting rid of their golden dolphin. Thereupon a eulogy of

[005-089] the marvellous fish, with a thousand delicate allusions to the

[005-090] young betrothed of Marguerite of Flanders, then sadly cloistered

[005-091] in at Amboise, and without a suspicion that Labor and Clergy,

[005-092] Nobility and Merchandise had just made the circuit of the world

[005-093] in his behalf. The said dauphin was then young, was handsome, was

[005-094] stout, and, above all (magnificent origin of all royal virtues),

[005-095] he was the son of the Lion of France. I declare that this bold

[005-096] metaphor is admirable, and that the natural history of the

[005-097] theatre, on a day of allegory and royal marriage songs, is not in

[005-098] the least startled by a dolphin who is the son of a lion. It is

[005-099] precisely these rare and Pindaric mixtures which prove the poet’s

[005-100] enthusiasm. Nevertheless, in order to play the part of critic

[005-101] also, the poet might have developed this beautiful idea in

[005-102] something less than two hundred lines. It is true that the

[005-103] mystery was to last from noon until four o’clock, in accordance

[005-104] with the orders of monsieur the provost, and that it was

[005-105] necessary to say something. Besides, the people listened

[005-106] patiently.

[005-107] All at once, in the very middle of a quarrel between Mademoiselle

[005-108] Merchandise and Madame Nobility, at the moment when Monsieur

[005-109] Labor was giving utterance to this wonderful line,—

[005-110] In forest ne’er was seen a more triumphant beast;

[005-111] the door of the reserved gallery which had hitherto remained so

[005-112] inopportunely closed, opened still more inopportunely; and the

[005-113] ringing voice of the usher announced abruptly, “His eminence,

[005-114] Monseigneur the Cardinal de Bourbon.”

[005-115] CHAPTER III. MONSIEUR THE CARDINAL.

[005-116] Poor Gringoire! the din of all the great double petards of the

[005-117] Saint-Jean, the discharge of twenty arquebuses on supports, the

[005-118] detonation of that famous serpentine of the Tower of Billy,

[005-119] which, during the siege of Paris, on Sunday, the twenty-sixth of

[005-120] September, 1465, killed seven Burgundians at one blow, the

[005-121] explosion of all the powder stored at the gate of the Temple,

[005-122] would have rent his ears less rudely at that solemn and dramatic

[005-123] moment, than these few words, which fell from the lips of the

[005-124] usher, “His eminence, Monseigneur the Cardinal de Bourbon.”

[005-125] It is not that Pierre Gringoire either feared or disdained

[005-126] monsieur the cardinal. He had neither the weakness nor the

[005-127] audacity for that. A true eclectic, as it would be expressed

[005-128] nowadays, Gringoire was one of those firm and lofty, moderate and

[005-129] calm spirits, which always know how to bear themselves amid all

[005-130] circumstances (_stare in dimidio rerum_), and who are full of

[005-131] reason and of liberal philosophy, while still setting store by

[005-132] cardinals. A rare, precious, and never interrupted race of

[005-133] philosophers to whom wisdom, like another Ariadne, seems to have

[005-134] given a clew of thread which they have been walking along

[005-135] unwinding since the beginning of the world, through the labyrinth

[005-136] of human affairs. One finds them in all ages, ever the same; that

[005-137] is to say, always according to all times. And, without reckoning

[005-138] our Pierre Gringoire, who may represent them in the fifteenth

[005-139] century if we succeed in bestowing upon him the distinction which

[005-140] he deserves, it certainly was their spirit which animated Father

[005-141] du Breul, when he wrote, in the sixteenth, these naively sublime

[005-142] words, worthy of all centuries: “I am a Parisian by nation, and a

[005-143] Parrhisian in language, for _parrhisia_ in Greek signifies

[005-144] liberty of speech; of which I have made use even towards

[005-145] messeigneurs the cardinals, uncle and brother to Monsieur the

[005-146] Prince de Conty, always with respect to their greatness, and

[005-147] without offending any one of their suite, which is much to say.”

[005-148] There was then neither hatred for the cardinal, nor disdain for

[005-149] his presence, in the disagreeable impression produced upon Pierre

[005-150] Gringoire. Quite the contrary; our poet had too much good sense

[005-151] and too threadbare a coat, not to attach particular importance to

[005-152] having the numerous allusions in his prologue, and, in

[005-153] particular, the glorification of the dauphin, son of the Lion of

[005-154] France, fall upon the most eminent ear. But it is not interest

[005-155] which predominates in the noble nature of poets. I suppose that

[005-156] the entity of the poet may be represented by the number ten; it

[005-157] is certain that a chemist on analyzing and pharmacopolizing it,

[005-158] as Rabelais says, would find it composed of one part interest to

[005-159] nine parts of self-esteem.

[005-160] Now, at the moment when the door had opened to admit the

[005-161] cardinal, the nine parts of self-esteem in Gringoire, swollen and

[005-162] expanded by the breath of popular admiration, were in a state of

[005-163] prodigious augmentation, beneath which disappeared, as though

[005-164] stifled, that imperceptible molecule of which we have just

[005-165] remarked upon in the constitution of poets; a precious

[005-166] ingredient, by the way, a ballast of reality and humanity,

[005-167] without which they would not touch the earth. Gringoire enjoyed

[005-168] seeing, feeling, fingering, so to speak an entire assembly (of

[005-169] knaves, it is true, but what matters that?) stupefied, petrified,

[005-170] and as though asphyxiated in the presence of the incommensurable

[005-171] tirades which welled up every instant from all parts of his

[005-172] bridal song. I affirm that he shared the general beatitude, and

[005-173] that, quite the reverse of La Fontaine, who, at the presentation

[005-174] of his comedy of the “Florentine,” asked, “Who is the ill-bred

[005-175] lout who made that rhapsody?” Gringoire would gladly have

[005-176] inquired of his neighbor, “Whose masterpiece is this?”

[005-177] The reader can now judge of the effect produced upon him by the

[005-178] abrupt and unseasonable arrival of the cardinal.

[005-179] That which he had to fear was only too fully realized. The

[005-180] entrance of his eminence upset the audience. All heads turned

[005-181] towards the gallery. It was no longer possible to hear one’s

[005-182] self. “The cardinal! The cardinal!” repeated all mouths. The

[005-183] unhappy prologue stopped short for the second time.

[005-184] The cardinal halted for a moment on the threshold of the estrade.

[005-185] While he was sending a rather indifferent glance around the

[005-186] audience, the tumult redoubled. Each person wished to get a

[005-187] better view of him. Each man vied with the other in thrusting his

[005-188] head over his neighbor’s shoulder.

[005-189] He was, in fact, an exalted personage, the sight of whom was well

[005-190] worth any other comedy. Charles, Cardinal de Bourbon, Archbishop

[005-191] and Comte of Lyon, Primate of the Gauls, was allied both to Louis

[005-192] XI., through his brother, Pierre, Seigneur de Beaujeu, who had

[005-193] married the king’s eldest daughter, and to Charles the Bold

[005-194] through his mother, Agnes of Burgundy. Now, the dominating trait,

[005-195] the peculiar and distinctive trait of the character of the

[005-196] Primate of the Gauls, was the spirit of the courtier, and

[005-197] devotion to the powers that be. The reader can form an idea of

[005-198] the numberless embarrassments which this double relationship had

[005-199] caused him, and of all the temporal reefs among which his

[005-200] spiritual bark had been forced to tack, in order not to suffer

[005-201] shipwreck on either Louis or Charles, that Scylla and that

[005-202] Charybdis which had devoured the Duc de Nemours and the Constable

[005-203] de Saint-Pol. Thanks to Heaven’s mercy, he had made the voyage

[005-204] successfully, and had reached home without hindrance. But

[005-205] although he was in port, and precisely because he was in port, he

[005-206] never recalled without disquiet the varied haps of his political

[005-207] career, so long uneasy and laborious. Thus, he was in the habit

[005-208] of saying that the year 1476 had been “white and black” for

[005-209] him—meaning thereby, that in the course of that year he had lost

[005-210] his mother, the Duchesse de la Bourbonnais, and his cousin, the

[005-211] Duke of Burgundy, and that one grief had consoled him for the

[005-212] other.
