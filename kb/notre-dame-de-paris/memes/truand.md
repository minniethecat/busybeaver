# Truands and Vagabonds of Medieval Paris

The truands (vagabonds, beggars, thieves, and outcasts) form a substantial
shadow society in Victor Hugo's Notre-Dame de Paris. They inhabit the Court
of Miracles, operate under their own king (Clopin Trouillefou), and represent
the novel's most vivid depiction of marginal life beneath the official
institutions of church and state.

## Historical Basis

The term "truand" in medieval French denoted a vagabond or beggar, often
associated with criminality. Hugo draws on the historical reality of
marginalized populations in 15th-century Paris — the city's underworld of
thieves, counterfeiters, and the chronically poor who congregated in districts
like the Cour des Miracles. The novel's depiction blends historical observation
with literary imagination ([011-186], [011-199]).

## Role in the Novel

The truands first appear when Pierre Gringoire, lost in the streets of Paris
at night, stumbles into the Court of Miracles. He is captured, condemned to
death by Clopin Trouillefou, and saved only by Esmeralda's intervention —
marrying him by the truand custom of breaking a jug. This episode introduces
the parallel society that operates beneath Paris, with its own laws, hierarchies,
and codes ([015-052], [015-059], [015-144]).

The truands reappear in Book X, when Clopin leads the vagabonds in an attack
on Notre-Dame Cathedral to rescue Esmeralda — a spectacular set-piece that pits
the underworld against the combined forces of church and state ([016-018]).

## Social Structure

Hugo presents the truand world as a mirror of official society:
- **Clopin Trouillefou**, King of the Truands — the underworld's Louis XI
- **The Court of Miracles** — a parallel Paris where the blind see and the
  lame walk at nightfall
- **Argot** — the truands' secret language, a linguistic underworld
  paralleling the physical one

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo's portrait of the truands is ambivalent. They are brutal and grotesque
— capable of casual violence — yet they possess their own rough justice and
loyalty. When they rally to save Esmeralda, they display a solidarity absent
from the novel's official institutions. The truands represent Hugo's romantic
fascination with the marginal, the hidden, and the inverted.
