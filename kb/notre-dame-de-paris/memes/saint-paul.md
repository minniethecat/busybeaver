# Saint-Paul

The Saint-Paul district on the Right Bank of [[paris|Paris]] was a significant
residential and ecclesiastical area during the Middle Ages. Its most
notable feature for Victor Hugo's *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]* was the Hôtel
Saint-Paul, a royal residence where King [[louis-xi|Louis XI]] maintained a personal
menagerie of exotic animals, including lions [076-058].

## Role in the Novel

Saint-Paul appears indirectly in the novel through references to royal
expenditures and the king's peculiar interests. In a passage detailing
[[louis-xi|Louis XI]]'s accounts, Hugo mentions expenditures for "the safekeeping of
the lions at Saint-Paul, twenty-two livres" [076-058]. The king's
response to this expense — "These be dear beasts. It matters not; it is
a fine magnificence in a king" — reveals his character through his
attachment to these symbols of royal power [076-058].

The [[religion|church]] of Saint-Paul is also invoked as a precedent for royal
behavior. A character justifies an action by citing how "Saint-Louis of
France, my master, transgressed, with the same object, the [[religion|church]] of
Monsieur Saint-Paul; and Monsieur Alphonse, son of the king of
Jerusalem, the very [[religion|church]] of the Holy Sepulchre" [080-101]. This
passage positions Saint-Paul within the network of [[religion|church]]es that
monarchs could override in pursuit of their aims, il[[love-and-desire|lust]]rating the
tension between royal prerogative and ecclesiastical authority.

## The Royal Menagerie

The lions of Saint-Paul represent one of the novel's subtle details of
medieval royal life. [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]'s menagerie at the Hôtel Saint-Paul
housed "a great red lion whom I [[love-and-desire|love]] for his pleasant ways," as the
king himself notes [076-058]. Hugo uses this detail to characterize the
king: his affection for dangerous beasts mirrors his political
ruthlessness, while the expense of maintaining the menagerie reflects
the priorities of a monarch who could spare twenty-two livres for lions
but haggle over the price of public works.

## The Saint-Paul District

Beyond the royal residence, Saint-Paul was a thriving Right Bank
neighborhood in [[paris|medieval Paris]]. The district lay within the wall of
Charles V and formed part of the Marais, the fashionable residential
quarter of the period. In Hugo's panoramic vision of [[paris|the city]], areas
like Saint-Paul represented the layering of ecclesiastical, royal, and
civic identities that made [[paris|medieval Paris]] unique.
