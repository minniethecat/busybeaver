---[061-001] “Oh!” she said in a low voice, and with a shudder, “’tis he

[061-002] again! the priest!”

[061-003] It was in fact, the archdeacon. On his left he had the

[061-004] sub-chanter, on his right, the chanter, armed with his official

[061-005] wand. He advanced with head thrown back, his eyes fixed and wide

[061-006] open, intoning in a strong voice,—

[061-007] “_De ventre inferi clamavi, et exaudisti vocem meam_.

[061-008] “_Et projecisti me in profundum in corde maris, et flumem

[061-009] circumdedit me._”[47]

[061-010] At the moment when he made his appearance in the full daylight

[061-011] beneath the lofty arched portal, enveloped in an ample cope of

[061-012] silver barred with a black cross, he was so pale that more than

[061-013] one person in the crowd thought that one of the marble bishops

[061-014] who knelt on the sepulchral stones of the choir had risen and was

[061-015] come to receive upon the brink of the tomb, the woman who was

[061-016] about to die.

[061-017] She, no less pale, no less like a statue, had hardly noticed that

[061-018] they had placed in her hand a heavy, lighted candle of yellow

[061-019] wax; she had not heard the yelping voice of the clerk reading the

[061-020] fatal contents of the apology; when they told her to respond with

[061-021] _Amen_, she responded _Amen_. She only recovered life and force

[061-022] when she beheld the priest make a sign to her guards to withdraw,

[061-023] and himself advance alone towards her.

[061-024] Then she felt her blood boil in her head, and a remnant of

[061-025] indignation flashed up in that soul already benumbed and cold.

[061-026] The archdeacon approached her slowly; even in that extremity, she

[061-027] beheld him cast an eye sparkling with sensuality, jealousy, and

[061-028] desire, over her exposed form. Then he said aloud,—

[061-029] “Young girl, have you asked God’s pardon for your faults and

[061-030] shortcomings?”

[061-031] He bent down to her ear, and added (the spectators supposed that

[061-032] he was receiving her last confession): “Will you have me? I can

[061-033] still save you!”

[061-034] She looked intently at him: “Begone, demon, or I will denounce

[061-035] you!”

[061-036] He gave vent to a horrible smile: “You will not be believed. You

[061-037] will only add a scandal to a crime. Reply quickly! Will you have

[061-038] me?”

[061-039] “What have you done with my Phœbus?”

[061-040] “He is dead!” said the priest.

[061-041] At that moment the wretched archdeacon raised his head

[061-042] mechanically and beheld at the other end of the Place, in the

[061-043] balcony of the Gondelaurier mansion, the captain standing beside

[061-044] Fleur-de-Lys. He staggered, passed his hand across his eyes,

[061-045] looked again, muttered a curse, and all his features were

[061-046] violently contorted.

[061-047] “Well, die then!” he hissed between his teeth. “No one shall have

[061-048] you.” Then, raising his hand over the gypsy, he exclaimed in a

[061-049] funereal voice:—“_I nunc, anima anceps, et sit tibi Deus

[061-050] misericors!_”[48]

[061-051] This was the dread formula with which it was the custom to

[061-052] conclude these gloomy ceremonies. It was the signal agreed upon

[061-053] between the priest and the executioner.

[061-054] The crowd knelt.

[061-055] “_Kyrie eleison_,”[49] said the priests, who had remained beneath

[061-056] the arch of the portal.

[061-057] “_Kyrie eleison_,” repeated the throng in that murmur which runs

[061-058] over all heads, like the waves of a troubled sea.

[061-059] “_Amen_,” said the archdeacon.

[061-060] He turned his back on the condemned girl, his head sank upon his

[061-061] breast once more, he crossed his hands and rejoined his escort of

[061-062] priests, and a moment later he was seen to disappear, with the

[061-063] cross, the candles, and the copes, beneath the misty arches of

[061-064] the cathedral, and his sonorous voice was extinguished by degrees

[061-065] in the choir, as he chanted this verse of despair,—

[061-066] “_Omnes gurgites tui et fluctus tui super me transierunt_.”[50]

[061-067] At the same time, the intermittent clash of the iron butts of the

[061-068] beadles’ halberds, gradually dying away among the columns of the

[061-069] nave, produced the effect of a clock hammer striking the last

[061-070] hour of the condemned.

[061-071] The doors of Notre-Dame remained open, allowing a view of the

[061-072] empty desolate church, draped in mourning, without candles, and

[061-073] without voices.

[061-074] The condemned girl remained motionless in her place, waiting to

[061-075] be disposed of. One of the sergeants of police was obliged to

[061-076] notify Master Charmolue of the fact, as the latter, during this

[061-077] entire scene, had been engaged in studying the bas-relief of the

[061-078] grand portal which represents, according to some, the sacrifice

[061-079] of Abraham; according to others, the philosopher’s alchemical

[061-080] operation: the sun being figured forth by the angel; the fire, by

[061-081] the fagot; the artisan, by Abraham.

[061-082] There was considerable difficulty in drawing him away from that

[061-083] contemplation, but at length he turned round; and, at a signal

[061-084] which he gave, two men clad in yellow, the executioner’s

[061-085] assistants, approached the gypsy to bind her hands once more.

[061-086] The unhappy creature, at the moment of mounting once again the

[061-087] fatal cart, and proceeding to her last halting-place, was seized,

[061-088] possibly, with some poignant clinging to life. She raised her

[061-089] dry, red eyes to heaven, to the sun, to the silvery clouds, cut

[061-090] here and there by a blue trapezium or triangle; then she lowered

[061-091] them to objects around her, to the earth, the throng, the houses;

[061-092] all at once, while the yellow man was binding her elbows, she

[061-093] uttered a terrible cry, a cry of joy. Yonder, on that balcony, at

[061-094] the corner of the Place, she had just caught sight of him, of her

[061-095] friend, her lord, Phœbus, the other apparition of her life!

[061-096] The judge had lied! the priest had lied! it was certainly he, she

[061-097] could not doubt it; he was there, handsome, alive, dressed in his

[061-098] brilliant uniform, his plume on his head, his sword by his side!

[061-099] “Phœbus!” she cried, “my Phœbus!”

[061-100] And she tried to stretch towards him arms trembling with love and

[061-101] rapture, but they were bound.

[061-102] Then she saw the captain frown, a beautiful young girl who was

[061-103] leaning against him gazed at him with disdainful lips and

[061-104] irritated eyes; then Phœbus uttered some words which did not

[061-105] reach her, and both disappeared precipitately behind the window

[061-106] opening upon the balcony, which closed after them.

[061-107] “Phœbus!” she cried wildly, “can it be you believe it?” A

[061-108] monstrous thought had just presented itself to her. She

[061-109] remembered that she had been condemned to death for murder

[061-110] committed on the person of Phœbus de Châteaupers.

[061-111] She had borne up until that moment. But this last blow was too

[061-112] harsh. She fell lifeless on the pavement.

[061-113] “Come,” said Charmolue, “carry her to the cart, and make an end

[061-114] of it.”

[061-115] No one had yet observed in the gallery of the statues of the

[061-116] kings, carved directly above the arches of the portal, a strange

[061-117] spectator, who had, up to that time, observed everything with

[061-118] such impassiveness, with a neck so strained, a visage so hideous

[061-119] that, in his motley accoutrement of red and violet, he might have

[061-120] been taken for one of those stone monsters through whose mouths

[061-121] the long gutters of the cathedral have discharged their waters

[061-122] for six hundred years. This spectator had missed nothing that had

[061-123] taken place since midday in front of the portal of Notre-Dame.

[061-124] And at the very beginning he had securely fastened to one of the

[061-125] small columns a large knotted rope, one end of which trailed on

[061-126] the flight of steps below. This being done, he began to look on

[061-127] tranquilly, whistling from time to time when a blackbird flitted

[061-128] past. Suddenly, at the moment when the superintendent’s

[061-129] assistants were preparing to execute Charmolue’s phlegmatic

[061-130] order, he threw his leg over the balustrade of the gallery,

[061-131] seized the rope with his feet, his knees and his hands; then he

[061-132] was seen to glide down the façade, as a drop of rain slips down a

[061-133] window-pane, rush to the two executioners with the swiftness of a

[061-134] cat which has fallen from a roof, knock them down with two

[061-135] enormous fists, pick up the gypsy with one hand, as a child would

[061-136] her doll, and dash back into the church with a single bound,

[061-137] lifting the young girl above his head and crying in a formidable

[061-138] voice,—

[061-139] “Sanctuary!”

[061-140] This was done with such rapidity, that had it taken place at

[061-141] night, the whole of it could have been seen in the space of a

[061-142] single flash of lightning.

[061-143] “Sanctuary! Sanctuary!” repeated the crowd; and the clapping of

[061-144] ten thousand hands made Quasimodo’s single eye sparkle with joy

[061-145] and pride.

[061-146] This shock restored the condemned girl to her senses. She raised

[061-147] her eyelids, looked at Quasimodo, then closed them again

[061-148] suddenly, as though terrified by her deliverer.

[061-149] Charmolue was stupefied, as well as the executioners and the

[061-150] entire escort. In fact, within the bounds of Notre-Dame, the

[061-151] condemned girl could not be touched. The cathedral was a place of

[061-152] refuge. All temporal jurisdiction expired upon its threshold.

[061-153] Quasimodo had halted beneath the great portal, his huge feet

[061-154] seemed as solid on the pavement of the church as the heavy Roman

[061-155] pillars. His great, bushy head sat low between his shoulders,

[061-156] like the heads of lions, who also have a mane and no neck. He

[061-157] held the young girl, who was quivering all over, suspended from

[061-158] his horny hands like a white drapery; but he carried her with as

[061-159] much care as though he feared to break her or blight her. One

[061-160] would have said that he felt that she was a delicate, exquisite,

[061-161] precious thing, made for other hands than his. There were moments

[061-162] when he looked as if not daring to touch her, even with his

[061-163] breath. Then, all at once, he would press her forcibly in his

[061-164] arms, against his angular bosom, like his own possession, his

[061-165] treasure, as the mother of that child would have done. His

[061-166] gnome’s eye, fastened upon her, inundated her with tenderness,

[061-167] sadness, and pity, and was suddenly raised filled with

[061-168] lightnings. Then the women laughed and wept, the crowd stamped

[061-169] with enthusiasm, for, at that moment Quasimodo had a beauty of

[061-170] his own. He was handsome; he, that orphan, that foundling, that

[061-171] outcast, he felt himself august and strong, he gazed in the face

[061-172] of that society from which he was banished, and in which he had

[061-173] so powerfully intervened, of that human justice from which he had

[061-174] wrenched its prey, of all those tigers whose jaws were forced to

[061-175] remain empty, of those policemen, those judges, those

[061-176] executioners, of all that force of the king which he, the meanest

[061-177] of creatures, had just broken, with the force of God.

[061-178] And then, it was touching to behold this protection which had

[061-179] fallen from a being so hideous upon a being so unhappy, a

[061-180] creature condemned to death saved by Quasimodo. They were two

[061-181] extremes of natural and social wretchedness, coming into contact

[061-182] and aiding each other.

[061-183] Meanwhile, after several moments of triumph, Quasimodo had

[061-184] plunged abruptly into the church with his burden. The populace,

[061-185] fond of all prowess, sought him with their eyes, beneath the

[061-186] gloomy nave, regretting that he had so speedily disappeared from

[061-187] their acclamations. All at once, he was seen to re-appear at one

[061-188] of the extremities of the gallery of the kings of France; he

[061-189] traversed it, running like a madman, raising his conquest high in

[061-190] his arms and shouting: “Sanctuary!” The crowd broke forth into

[061-191] fresh applause. The gallery passed, he plunged once more into the

[061-192] interior of the church. A moment later, he re-appeared upon the

[061-193] upper platform, with the gypsy still in his arms, still running

[061-194] madly, still crying, “Sanctuary!” and the throng applauded.

[061-195] Finally, he made his appearance for the third time upon the

[061-196] summit of the tower where hung the great bell; from that point he

[061-197] seemed to be showing to the entire city the girl whom he had

[061-198] saved, and his voice of thunder, that voice which was so rarely

[061-199] heard, and which he never heard himself, repeated thrice with

[061-200] frenzy, even to the clouds: “Sanctuary! Sanctuary! Sanctuary!”

[061-201] “Noël! Noël!” shouted the populace in its turn; and that immense

[061-202] acclamation flew to astonish the crowd assembled at the Grève on

[061-203] the other bank, and the recluse who was still waiting with her

[061-204] eyes riveted on the gibbet.
