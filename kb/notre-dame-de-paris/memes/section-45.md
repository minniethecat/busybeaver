---[045-001] The mention of this last circumstance disturbed the archdeacon

[045-002] greatly, though Gringoire paid no attention to his perturbation;

[045-003] to such an extent had two months sufficed to cause the heedless

[045-004] poet to forget the singular details of the evening on which he

[045-005] had met the gypsy, and the presence of the archdeacon in it all.

[045-006] Otherwise, the little dancer feared nothing; she did not tell

[045-007] fortunes, which protected her against those trials for magic

[045-008] which were so frequently instituted against gypsy women. And

[045-009] then, Gringoire held the position of her brother, if not of her

[045-010] husband. After all, the philosopher endured this sort of platonic

[045-011] marriage very patiently. It meant a shelter and bread at least.

[045-012] Every morning, he set out from the lair of the thieves, generally

[045-013] with the gypsy; he helped her make her collections of targes[34]

[045-014] and little blanks[35] in the squares; each evening he returned to

[045-015] the same roof with her, allowed her to bolt herself into her

[045-016] little chamber, and slept the sleep of the just. A very sweet

[045-017] existence, taking it all in all, he said, and well adapted to

[045-018] revery. And then, on his soul and conscience, the philosopher was

[045-019] not very sure that he was madly in love with the gypsy. He loved

[045-020] her goat almost as dearly. It was a charming animal, gentle,

[045-021] intelligent, clever; a learned goat. Nothing was more common in

[045-022] the Middle Ages than these learned animals, which amazed people

[045-023] greatly, and often led their instructors to the stake. But the

[045-024] witchcraft of the goat with the golden hoofs was a very innocent

[045-025] species of magic. Gringoire explained them to the archdeacon,

[045-026] whom these details seemed to interest deeply. In the majority of

[045-027] cases, it was sufficient to present the tambourine to the goat in

[045-028] such or such a manner, in order to obtain from him the trick

[045-029] desired. He had been trained to this by the gypsy, who possessed,

[045-030] in these delicate arts, so rare a talent that two months had

[045-031] sufficed to teach the goat to write, with movable letters, the

[045-032] word “Phœbus.”

[045-033] “‘Phœbus!’” said the priest; “why ‘Phœbus’?”

[045-034] “I know not,” replied Gringoire. “Perhaps it is a word which she

[045-035] believes to be endowed with some magic and secret virtue. She

[045-036] often repeats it in a low tone when she thinks that she is

[045-037] alone.”

[045-038] “Are you sure,” persisted Claude, with his penetrating glance,

[045-039] “that it is only a word and not a name?”

[045-040] “The name of whom?” said the poet.

[045-041] “How should I know?” said the priest.

[045-042] “This is what I imagine, messire. These Bohemians are something

[045-043] like Guebrs, and adore the sun. Hence, Phœbus.”

[045-044] “That does not seem so clear to me as to you, Master Pierre.”

[045-045] “After all, that does not concern me. Let her mumble her Phœbus

[045-046] at her pleasure. One thing is certain, that Djali loves me almost

[045-047] as much as he does her.”

[045-048] “Who is Djali?”

[045-049] “The goat.”

[045-050] The archdeacon dropped his chin into his hand, and appeared to

[045-051] reflect for a moment. All at once he turned abruptly to Gringoire

[045-052] once more.

[045-053] “And do you swear to me that you have not touched her?”

[045-054] “Whom?” said Gringoire; “the goat?”

[045-055] “No, that woman.”

[045-056] “My wife? I swear to you that I have not.”

[045-057] “You are often alone with her?”

[045-058] “A good hour every evening.”

[045-059] Dom Claude frowned.

[045-060] “Oh! oh! _Solus cum sola non cogitabuntur orare Pater Noster_.”

[045-061] “Upon my soul, I could say the _Pater_, and the _Ave Maria_, and

[045-062] the _Credo in Deum patrem omnipotentem_ without her paying any

[045-063] more attention to me than a chicken to a church.”

[045-064] “Swear to me, by the body of your mother,” repeated the

[045-065] archdeacon violently, “that you have not touched that creature

[045-066] with even the tip of your finger.”

[045-067] “I will also swear it by the head of my father, for the two

[045-068] things have more affinity between them. But, my reverend master,

[045-069] permit me a question in my turn.”

[045-070] “Speak, sir.”

[045-071] “What concern is it of yours?”

[045-072] The archdeacon’s pale face became as crimson as the cheek of a

[045-073] young girl. He remained for a moment without answering; then,

[045-074] with visible embarrassment,—

[045-075] “Listen, Master Pierre Gringoire. You are not yet damned, so far

[045-076] as I know. I take an interest in you, and wish you well. Now the

[045-077] least contact with that Egyptian of the demon would make you the

[045-078] vassal of Satan. You know that ’tis always the body which ruins

[045-079] the soul. Woe to you if you approach that woman! That is all.”

[045-080] “I tried once,” said Gringoire, scratching his ear; “it was the

[045-081] first day: but I got stung.”

[045-082] “You were so audacious, Master Pierre?” and the priest’s brow

[045-083] clouded over again.

[045-084] “On another occasion,” continued the poet, with a smile, “I

[045-085] peeped through the keyhole, before going to bed, and I beheld the

[045-086] most delicious dame in her shift that ever made a bed creak under

[045-087] her bare foot.”

[045-088] “Go to the devil!” cried the priest, with a terrible look; and,

[045-089] giving the amazed Gringoire a push on the shoulders, he plunged,

[045-090] with long strides, under the gloomiest arcades of the cathedral.

[045-091] CHAPTER III. THE BELLS.

[045-092] After the morning in the pillory, the neighbors of Notre-Dame

[045-093] thought they noticed that Quasimodo’s ardor for ringing had grown

[045-094] cool. Formerly, there had been peals for every occasion, long

[045-095] morning serenades, which lasted from prime to compline; peals

[045-096] from the belfry for a high mass, rich scales drawn over the

[045-097] smaller bells for a wedding, for a christening, and mingling in

[045-098] the air like a rich embroidery of all sorts of charming sounds.

[045-099] The old church, all vibrating and sonorous, was in a perpetual

[045-100] joy of bells. One was constantly conscious of the presence of a

[045-101] spirit of noise and caprice, who sang through all those mouths of

[045-102] brass. Now that spirit seemed to have departed; the cathedral

[045-103] seemed gloomy, and gladly remained silent; festivals and funerals

[045-104] had the simple peal, dry and bare, demanded by the ritual,

[045-105] nothing more. Of the double noise which constitutes a church, the

[045-106] organ within, the bell without, the organ alone remained. One

[045-107] would have said that there was no longer a musician in the

[045-108] belfry. Quasimodo was always there, nevertheless; what, then, had

[045-109] happened to him? Was it that the shame and despair of the pillory

[045-110] still lingered in the bottom of his heart, that the lashes of his

[045-111] tormentor’s whip reverberated unendingly in his soul, and that

[045-112] the sadness of such treatment had wholly extinguished in him even

[045-113] his passion for the bells? or was it that Marie had a rival in

[045-114] the heart of the bellringer of Notre-Dame, and that the great

[045-115] bell and her fourteen sisters were neglected for something more

[045-116] amiable and more beautiful?

[045-117] It chanced that, in the year of grace 1482, Annunciation Day fell

[045-118] on Tuesday, the twenty-fifth of March. That day the air was so

[045-119] pure and light that Quasimodo felt some returning affection for

[045-120] his bells. He therefore ascended the northern tower while the

[045-121] beadle below was opening wide the doors of the church, which were

[045-122] then enormous panels of stout wood, covered with leather,

[045-123] bordered with nails of gilded iron, and framed in carvings “very

[045-124] artistically elaborated.”

[045-125] On arriving in the lofty bell chamber, Quasimodo gazed for some

[045-126] time at the six bells and shook his head sadly, as though

[045-127] groaning over some foreign element which had interposed itself in

[045-128] his heart between them and him. But when he had set them to

[045-129] swinging, when he felt that cluster of bells moving under his

[045-130] hand, when he saw, for he did not hear it, the palpitating octave

[045-131] ascend and descend that sonorous scale, like a bird hopping from

[045-132] branch to branch; when the demon Music, that demon who shakes a

[045-133] sparkling bundle of strette, trills and arpeggios, had taken

[045-134] possession of the poor deaf man, he became happy once more, he

[045-135] forgot everything, and his heart expanding, made his face beam.

[045-136] He went and came, he beat his hands together, he ran from rope to

[045-137] rope, he animated the six singers with voice and gesture, like

[045-138] the leader of an orchestra who is urging on intelligent

[045-139] musicians.

[045-140] “Go on,” said he, “go on, go on, Gabrielle, pour out all thy

[045-141] noise into the Place, ’tis a festival to-day. No laziness,

[045-142] Thibauld; thou art relaxing; go on, go on, then, art thou rusted,

[045-143] thou sluggard? That is well! quick! quick! let not thy clapper be

[045-144] seen! Make them all deaf like me. That’s it, Thibauld, bravely

[045-145] done! Guillaume! Guillaume! thou art the largest, and Pasquier is

[045-146] the smallest, and Pasquier does best. Let us wager that those who

[045-147] hear him will understand him better than they understand thee.

[045-148] Good! good! my Gabrielle, stoutly, more stoutly! Eli! what are

[045-149] you doing up aloft there, you two Moineaux (sparrows)? I do not

[045-150] see you making the least little shred of noise. What is the

[045-151] meaning of those beaks of copper which seem to be gaping when

[045-152] they should sing? Come, work now, ’tis the Feast of the

[045-153] Annunciation. The sun is fine, the chime must be fine also. Poor

[045-154] Guillaume! thou art all out of breath, my big fellow!”

[045-155] He was wholly absorbed in spurring on his bells, all six of which

[045-156] vied with each other in leaping and shaking their shining

[045-157] haunches, like a noisy team of Spanish mules, pricked on here and

[045-158] there by the apostrophes of the muleteer.

[045-159] All at once, on letting his glance fall between the large slate

[045-160] scales which cover the perpendicular wall of the bell tower at a

[045-161] certain height, he beheld on the square a young girl,

[045-162] fantastically dressed, stop, spread out on the ground a carpet,

[045-163] on which a small goat took up its post, and a group of spectators

[045-164] collect around her. This sight suddenly changed the course of his

[045-165] ideas, and congealed his enthusiasm as a breath of air congeals

[045-166] melted rosin. He halted, turned his back to the bells, and

[045-167] crouched down behind the projecting roof of slate, fixing upon

[045-168] the dancer that dreamy, sweet, and tender look which had already

[045-169] astonished the archdeacon on one occasion. Meanwhile, the

[045-170] forgotten bells died away abruptly and all together, to the great

[045-171] disappointment of the lovers of bell ringing, who were listening

[045-172] in good faith to the peal from above the Pont du Change, and who

[045-173] went away dumbfounded, like a dog who has been offered a bone and

[045-174] given a stone.

[045-175] CHAPTER IV. ἈΝÁΓΚΗ.

[045-176] It chanced that upon a fine morning in this same month of March,

[045-177] I think it was on Saturday the 29th, Saint Eustache’s day, our

[045-178] young friend the student, Jehan Frollo du Moulin, perceived, as

[045-179] he was dressing himself, that his breeches, which contained his

[045-180] purse, gave out no metallic ring. “Poor purse,” he said, drawing

[045-181] it from his fob, “what! not the smallest parisis! how cruelly the

[045-182] dice, beer-pots, and Venus have depleted thee! How empty,

[045-183] wrinkled, limp, thou art! Thou resemblest the throat of a fury! I

[045-184] ask you, Messer Cicero, and Messer Seneca, copies of whom, all

[045-185] dog’s-eared, I behold scattered on the floor, what profits it me

[045-186] to know, better than any governor of the mint, or any Jew on the

[045-187] Pont aux Changeurs, that a golden crown stamped with a crown is

[045-188] worth thirty-five unzains of twenty-five sous, and eight deniers

[045-189] parisis apiece, and that a crown stamped with a crescent is worth

[045-190] thirty-six unzains of twenty-six sous, six deniers tournois

[045-191] apiece, if I have not a single wretched black liard to risk on

[045-192] the double-six! Oh! Consul Cicero! this is no calamity from which

[045-193] one extricates one’s self with periphrases, _quemadmodum_, and

[045-194] _verum enim vero!_”

[045-195] He dressed himself sadly. An idea had occurred to him as he laced

[045-196] his boots, but he rejected it at first; nevertheless, it

[045-197] returned, and he put on his waistcoat wrong side out, an evident

[045-198] sign of violent internal combat. At last he dashed his cap

[045-199] roughly on the floor, and exclaimed: “So much the worse! Let come

[045-200] of it what may. I am going to my brother! I shall catch a sermon,

[045-201] but I shall catch a crown.”
