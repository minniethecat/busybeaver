[008-003] people had been upon the point of instituting a revolt against

[008-004] the bailiff through impatience to hear his work! now that they

[008-005] had it they did not care for it. This same representation which

[008-006] had been begun amid so unanimous an acclamation! Eternal flood

[008-007] and ebb of popular favor! To think that they had been on the

[008-008] point of hanging the bailiff’s sergeant! What would he not have

[008-009] given to be still at that hour of honey!

[008-010] But the usher’s brutal monologue came to an end; every one had

[008-011] arrived, and Gringoire breathed freely once more; the actors

[008-012] continued bravely. But Master Coppenole, the hosier, must needs

[008-013] rise of a sudden, and Gringoire was forced to listen to him

[008-014] deliver, amid universal attention, the following abominable

[008-015] harangue.

[008-016] “Messieurs the _bourgeois_ and squires of Paris, I don’t know,

[008-017] cross of God! what we are doing here. I certainly do see yonder

[008-018] in the corner on that stage, some people who appear to be

[008-019] fighting. I don’t know whether that is what you call a “mystery,”

[008-020] but it is not amusing; they quarrel with their tongues and

[008-021] nothing more. I have been waiting for the first blow this quarter

[008-022] of an hour; nothing comes; they are cowards who only scratch each

[008-023] other with insults. You ought to send for the fighters of London

[008-024] or Rotterdam; and, I can tell you! you would have had blows of

[008-025] the fist that could be heard in the Place; but these men excite

[008-026] our pity. They ought at least, to give us a moorish dance, or

[008-027] some other mummer! That is not what was told me; I was promised a

[008-028] feast of fools, with the election of a pope. We have our pope of

[008-029] fools at Ghent also; we’re not behindhand in that, cross of God!

[008-030] But this is the way we manage it; we collect a crowd like this

[008-031] one here, then each person in turn passes his head through a

[008-032] hole, and makes a grimace at the rest; time one who makes the

[008-033] ugliest, is elected pope by general acclamation; that’s the way

[008-034] it is. It is very diverting. Would you like to make your pope

[008-035] after the fashion of my country? At all events, it will be less

[008-036] wearisome than to listen to chatterers. If they wish to come and

[008-037] make their grimaces through the hole, they can join the game.

[008-038] What say you, Messieurs les _bourgeois_? You have here enough

[008-039] grotesque specimens of both sexes, to allow of laughing in

[008-040] Flemish fashion, and there are enough of us ugly in countenance

[008-041] to hope for a fine grinning match.”

[008-042] Gringoire would have liked to retort; stupefaction, rage,

[008-043] indignation, deprived him of words. Moreover, the suggestion of

[008-044] the popular hosier was received with such enthusiasm by these

[008-045] _bourgeois_ who were flattered at being called “squires,” that

[008-046] all resistance was useless. There was nothing to be done but to

[008-047] allow one’s self to drift with the torrent. Gringoire hid his

[008-048] face between his two hands, not being so fortunate as to have a

[008-049] mantle with which to veil his head, like Agamemnon of Timantis.

[008-050] CHAPTER V. QUASIMODO.

[008-051] In the twinkling of an eye, all was ready to execute Coppenole’s

[008-052] idea. _Bourgeois_, scholars and law clerks all set to work. The

[008-053] little chapel situated opposite the marble table was selected for

[008-054] the scene of the grinning match. A pane broken in the pretty rose

[008-055] window above the door, left free a circle of stone through which

[008-056] it was agreed that the competitors should thrust their heads. In

[008-057] order to reach it, it was only necessary to mount upon a couple

[008-058] of hogsheads, which had been produced from I know not where, and

[008-059] perched one upon the other, after a fashion. It was settled that

[008-060] each candidate, man or woman (for it was possible to choose a

[008-061] female pope), should, for the sake of leaving the impression of

[008-062] his grimace fresh and complete, cover his face and remain

[008-063] concealed in the chapel until the moment of his appearance. In

[008-064] less than an instant, the chapel was crowded with competitors,

[008-065] upon whom the door was then closed.

[008-066] Coppenole, from his post, ordered all, directed all, arranged

[008-067] all. During the uproar, the cardinal, no less abashed than

[008-068] Gringoire, had retired with all his suite, under the pretext of

[008-069] business and vespers, without the crowd which his arrival had so

[008-070] deeply stirred being in the least moved by his departure.

[008-071] Guillaume Rym was the only one who noticed his eminence’s

[008-072] discomfiture. The attention of the populace, like the sun,

[008-073] pursued its revolution; having set out from one end of the hall,

[008-074] and halted for a space in the middle, it had now reached the

[008-075] other end. The marble table, the brocaded gallery had each had

[008-076] their day; it was now the turn of the chapel of Louis XI.

[008-077] Henceforth, the field was open to all folly. There was no one

[008-078] there now, but the Flemings and the rabble.

[008-079] The grimaces began. The first face which appeared at the

[008-080] aperture, with eyelids turned up to the reds, a mouth open like a

[008-081] maw, and a brow wrinkled like our hussar boots of the Empire,

[008-082] evoked such an inextinguishable peal of laughter that Homer would

[008-083] have taken all these louts for gods. Nevertheless, the grand hall

[008-084] was anything but Olympus, and Gringoire’s poor Jupiter knew it

[008-085] better than any one else. A second and third grimace followed,

[008-086] then another and another; and the laughter and transports of

[008-087] delight went on increasing. There was in this spectacle, a

[008-088] peculiar power of intoxication and fascination, of which it would

[008-089] be difficult to convey to the reader of our day and our salons

[008-090] any idea.

[008-091] Let the reader picture to himself a series of visages presenting

[008-092] successively all geometrical forms, from the triangle to the

[008-093] trapezium, from the cone to the polyhedron; all human

[008-094] expressions, from wrath to lewdness; all ages, from the wrinkles

[008-095] of the new-born babe to the wrinkles of the aged and dying; all

[008-096] religious phantasmagories, from Faun to Beelzebub; all animal

[008-097] profiles, from the maw to the beak, from the jowl to the muzzle.

[008-098] Let the reader imagine all these grotesque figures of the Pont

[008-099] Neuf, those nightmares petrified beneath the hand of Germain

[008-100] Pilon, assuming life and breath, and coming in turn to stare you

[008-101] in the face with burning eyes; all the masks of the Carnival of

[008-102] Venice passing in succession before your glass,—in a word, a

[008-103] human kaleidoscope.

[008-104] The orgy grew more and more Flemish. Teniers could have given but

[008-105] a very imperfect idea of it. Let the reader picture to himself in

[008-106] bacchanal form, Salvator Rosa’s battle. There were no longer

[008-107] either scholars or ambassadors or _bourgeois_ or men or women;

[008-108] there was no longer any Clopin Trouillefou, nor Gilles Lecornu,

[008-109] nor Marie Quatrelivres, nor Robin Poussepain. All was universal

[008-110] license. The grand hall was no longer anything but a vast furnace

[008-111] of effrontry and joviality, where every mouth was a cry, every

[008-112] individual a posture; everything shouted and howled. The strange

[008-113] visages which came, in turn, to gnash their teeth in the rose

[008-114] window, were like so many brands cast into the brazier; and from

[008-115] the whole of this effervescing crowd, there escaped, as from a

[008-116] furnace, a sharp, piercing, stinging noise, hissing like the

[008-117] wings of a gnat.

[008-118] “Ho hé! curse it!”

[008-119] “Just look at that face!”

[008-120] “It’s not good for anything.”

[008-121] “Guillemette Maugerepuis, just look at that bull’s muzzle; it

[008-122] only lacks the horns. It can’t be your husband.”

[008-123] “Another!”

[008-124] “Belly of the pope! what sort of a grimace is that?”

[008-125] “Holà hé! that’s cheating. One must show only one’s face.”

[008-126] “That damned Perrette Callebotte! she’s capable of that!”

[008-127] “Good! Good!”

[008-128] “I’m stifling!”

[008-129] “There’s a fellow whose ears won’t go through!” Etc., etc.

[008-130] But we must do justice to our friend Jehan. In the midst of this

[008-131] witches’ sabbath, he was still to be seen on the top of his

[008-132] pillar, like the cabin-boy on the topmast. He floundered about

[008-133] with incredible fury. His mouth was wide open, and from it there

[008-134] escaped a cry which no one heard, not that it was covered by the

[008-135] general clamor, great as that was but because it attained, no

[008-136] doubt, the limit of perceptible sharp sounds, the thousand

[008-137] vibrations of Sauveur, or the eight thousand of Biot.

[008-138] As for Gringoire, the first moment of depression having passed,

[008-139] he had regained his composure. He had hardened himself against

[008-140] adversity.—“Continue!” he had said for the third time, to his

[008-141] comedians, speaking machines; then as he was marching with great

[008-142] strides in front of the marble table, a fancy seized him to go

[008-143] and appear in his turn at the aperture of the chapel, were it

[008-144] only for the pleasure of making a grimace at that ungrateful

[008-145] populace.—“But no, that would not be worthy of us; no, vengeance!

[008-146] let us combat until the end,” he repeated to himself; “the power

[008-147] of poetry over people is great; I will bring them back. We shall

[008-148] see which will carry the day, grimaces or polite literature.”

[008-149] Alas! he had been left the sole spectator of his piece. It was

[008-150] far worse than it had been a little while before. He no longer

[008-151] beheld anything but backs.

[008-152] I am mistaken. The big, patient man, whom he had already

[008-153] consulted in a critical moment, had remained with his face turned

[008-154] towards the stage. As for Gisquette and Liénarde, they had

[008-155] deserted him long ago.

[008-156] Gringoire was touched to the heart by the fidelity of his only

[008-157] spectator. He approached him and addressed him, shaking his arm

[008-158] slightly; for the good man was leaning on the balustrade and

[008-159] dozing a little.

[008-160] “Monsieur,” said Gringoire, “I thank you!”

[008-161] “Monsieur,” replied the big man with a yawn, “for what?”

[008-162] “I see what wearies you,” resumed the poet; “’tis all this noise

[008-163] which prevents your hearing comfortably. But be at ease! your

[008-164] name shall descend to posterity! Your name, if you please?”

[008-165] “Renauld Chateau, guardian of the seals of the Châtelet of Paris,

[008-166] at your service.”

[008-167] “Monsieur, you are the only representative of the muses here,”

[008-168] said Gringoire.

[008-169] “You are too kind, sir,” said the guardian of the seals at the

[008-170] Châtelet.

[008-171] “You are the only one,” resumed Gringoire, “who has listened to

[008-172] the piece decorously. What do you think of it?”

[008-173] “He! he!” replied the fat magistrate, half aroused, “it’s

[008-174] tolerably jolly, that’s a fact.”

[008-175] Gringoire was forced to content himself with this eulogy; for a

[008-176] thunder of applause, mingled with a prodigious acclamation, cut

[008-177] their conversation short. The Pope of the Fools had been elected.

[008-178] “Noël! Noël! Noël!”[6] shouted the people on all sides. That was,

[008-179] in fact, a marvellous grimace which was beaming at that moment

[008-180] through the aperture in the rose window. After all the

[008-181] pentagonal, hexagonal, and whimsical faces, which had succeeded

[008-182] each other at that hole without realizing the ideal of the

[008-183] grotesque which their imaginations, excited by the orgy, had

[008-184] constructed, nothing less was needed to win their suffrages than

[008-185] the sublime grimace which had just dazzled the assembly. Master

[008-186] Coppenole himself applauded, and Clopin Trouillefou, who had been

[008-187] among the competitors (and God knows what intensity of ugliness

[008-188] his visage could attain), confessed himself conquered: We will do

[008-189] the same. We shall not try to give the reader an idea of that

[008-190] tetrahedral nose, that horseshoe mouth; that little left eye

[008-191] obstructed with a red, bushy, bristling eyebrow, while the right

[008-192] eye disappeared entirely beneath an enormous wart; of those teeth

[008-193] in disarray, broken here and there, like the embattled parapet of

[008-194] a fortress; of that callous lip, upon which one of these teeth

[008-195] encroached, like the tusk of an elephant; of that forked chin;

[008-196] and above all, of the expression spread over the whole; of that

[008-197] mixture of malice, amazement, and sadness. Let the reader dream

[008-198] of this whole, if he can.
