---[007-001] Nevertheless, all was over for the poor cardinal, and he was

[007-002] obliged to quaff to the dregs the bitter cup of being in such bad

[007-003] company.

[007-004] The reader has, probably, not forgotten the impudent beggar who

[007-005] had been clinging fast to the fringes of the cardinal’s gallery

[007-006] ever since the beginning of the prologue. The arrival of the

[007-007] illustrious guests had by no means caused him to relax his hold,

[007-008] and, while the prelates and ambassadors were packing themselves

[007-009] into the stalls—like genuine Flemish herrings—he settled himself

[007-010] at his ease, and boldly crossed his legs on the architrave. The

[007-011] insolence of this proceeding was extraordinary, yet no one

[007-012] noticed it at first, the attention of all being directed

[007-013] elsewhere. He, on his side, perceived nothing that was going on

[007-014] in the hall; he wagged his head with the unconcern of a

[007-015] Neapolitan, repeating from time to time, amid the clamor, as from

[007-016] a mechanical habit, “Charity, please!” And, assuredly, he was,

[007-017] out of all those present, the only one who had not deigned to

[007-018] turn his head at the altercation between Coppenole and the usher.

[007-019] Now, chance ordained that the master hosier of Ghent, with whom

[007-020] the people were already in lively sympathy, and upon whom all

[007-021] eyes were riveted—should come and seat himself in the front row

[007-022] of the gallery, directly above the mendicant; and people were not

[007-023] a little amazed to see the Flemish ambassador, on concluding his

[007-024] inspection of the knave thus placed beneath his eyes, bestow a

[007-025] friendly tap on that ragged shoulder. The beggar turned round;

[007-026] there was surprise, recognition, a lighting up of the two

[007-027] countenances, and so forth; then, without paying the slightest

[007-028] heed in the world to the spectators, the hosier and the wretched

[007-029] being began to converse in a low tone, holding each other’s

[007-030] hands, in the meantime, while the rags of Clopin Trouillefou,

[007-031] spread out upon the cloth of gold of the dais, produced the

[007-032] effect of a caterpillar on an orange.

[007-033] The novelty of this singular scene excited such a murmur of mirth

[007-034] and gayety in the hall, that the cardinal was not slow to

[007-035] perceive it; he half bent forward, and, as from the point where

[007-036] he was placed he could catch only an imperfect view of

[007-037] Trouillerfou’s ignominious doublet, he very naturally imagined

[007-038] that the mendicant was asking alms, and, disgusted with his

[007-039] audacity, he exclaimed: “Bailiff of the Courts, toss me that

[007-040] knave into the river!”

[007-041] “Cross of God! monseigneur the cardinal,” said Coppenole, without

[007-042] quitting Clopin’s hand, “he’s a friend of mine.”

[007-043] “Good! good!” shouted the populace. From that moment, Master

[007-044] Coppenole enjoyed in Paris as in Ghent, “great favor with the

[007-045] people; for men of that sort do enjoy it,” says Philippe de

[007-046] Comines, “when they are thus disorderly.” The cardinal bit his

[007-047] lips. He bent towards his neighbor, the Abbé of Sainte-Geneviève,

[007-048] and said to him in a low tone,—“Fine ambassadors monsieur the

[007-049] archduke sends here, to announce to us Madame Marguerite!”

[007-050] “Your eminence,” replied the abbé, “wastes your politeness on

[007-051] these Flemish swine. _Margaritas ante porcos_, pearls before

[007-052] swine.”

[007-053] “Say rather,” retorted the cardinal, with a smile, “_Porcos ante

[007-054] Margaritam_, swine before the pearl.”

[007-055] The whole little court in cassocks went into ecstacies over this

[007-056] play upon words. The cardinal felt a little relieved; he was

[007-057] quits with Coppenole, he also had had his jest applauded.

[007-058] Now, will those of our readers who possess the power of

[007-059] generalizing an image or an idea, as the expression runs in the

[007-060] style of to-day, permit us to ask them if they have formed a very

[007-061] clear conception of the spectacle presented at this moment, upon

[007-062] which we have arrested their attention, by the vast parallelogram

[007-063] of the grand hall of the palace.

[007-064] In the middle of the hall, backed against the western wall, a

[007-065] large and magnificent gallery draped with cloth of gold, into

[007-066] which enter in procession, through a small, arched door, grave

[007-067] personages, announced successively by the shrill voice of an

[007-068] usher. On the front benches were already a number of venerable

[007-069] figures, muffled in ermine, velvet, and scarlet. Around the

[007-070] dais—which remains silent and dignified—below, opposite,

[007-071] everywhere, a great crowd and a great murmur. Thousands of

[007-072] glances directed by the people on each face upon the dais, a

[007-073] thousand whispers over each name. Certainly, the spectacle is

[007-074] curious, and well deserves the attention of the spectators. But

[007-075] yonder, quite at the end, what is that sort of trestle work with

[007-076] four motley puppets upon it, and more below? Who is that man

[007-077] beside the trestle, with a black doublet and a pale face? Alas!

[007-078] my dear reader, it is Pierre Gringoire and his prologue.

[007-079] We have all forgotten him completely.

[007-080] This is precisely what he feared.

[007-081] From the moment of the cardinal’s entrance, Gringoire had never

[007-082] ceased to tremble for the safety of his prologue. At first he had

[007-083] enjoined the actors, who had stopped in suspense, to continue,

[007-084] and to raise their voices; then, perceiving that no one was

[007-085] listening, he had stopped them; and, during the entire quarter of

[007-086] an hour that the interruption lasted, he had not ceased to stamp,

[007-087] to flounce about, to appeal to Gisquette and Liénarde, and to

[007-088] urge his neighbors to the continuance of the prologue; all in

[007-089] vain. No one quitted the cardinal, the embassy, and the

[007-090] gallery—sole centre of this vast circle of visual rays. We must

[007-091] also believe, and we say it with regret, that the prologue had

[007-092] begun slightly to weary the audience at the moment when his

[007-093] eminence had arrived, and created a diversion in so terrible a

[007-094] fashion. After all, on the gallery as well as on the marble

[007-095] table, the spectacle was the same: the conflict of Labor and

[007-096] Clergy, of Nobility and Merchandise. And many people preferred to

[007-097] see them alive, breathing, moving, elbowing each other in flesh

[007-098] and blood, in this Flemish embassy, in this Episcopal court,

[007-099] under the cardinal’s robe, under Coppenole’s jerkin, than

[007-100] painted, decked out, talking in verse, and, so to speak, stuffed

[007-101] beneath the yellow amid white tunics in which Gringoire had so

[007-102] ridiculously clothed them.

[007-103] Nevertheless, when our poet beheld quiet reestablished to some

[007-104] extent, he devised a stratagem which might have redeemed all.

[007-105] “Monsieur,” he said, turning towards one of his neighbors, a

[007-106] fine, big man, with a patient face, “suppose we begin again.”

[007-107] “What?” said his neighbor.

[007-108] “Hé! the Mystery,” said Gringoire.

[007-109] “As you like,” returned his neighbor.

[007-110] This semi-approbation sufficed for Gringoire, and, conducting his

[007-111] own affairs, he began to shout, confounding himself with the

[007-112] crowd as much as possible: “Begin the mystery again! begin

[007-113] again!”

[007-114] “The devil!” said Joannes de Molendino, “what are they jabbering

[007-115] down yonder, at the end of the hall?” (for Gringoire was making

[007-116] noise enough for four.) “Say, comrades, isn’t that mystery

[007-117] finished? They want to begin it all over again. That’s not fair!”

[007-118] “No, no!” shouted all the scholars. “Down with the mystery! Down

[007-119] with it!”

[007-120] But Gringoire had multiplied himself, and only shouted the more

[007-121] vigorously: “Begin again! begin again!”

[007-122] These clamors attracted the attention of the cardinal.

[007-123] “Monsieur Bailiff of the Courts,” said he to a tall, black man,

[007-124] placed a few paces from him, “are those knaves in a holy-water

[007-125] vessel, that they make such a hellish noise?”

[007-126] The bailiff of the courts was a sort of amphibious magistrate, a

[007-127] sort of bat of the judicial order, related to both the rat and

[007-128] the bird, the judge and the soldier.

[007-129] He approached his eminence, and not without a good deal of fear

[007-130] of the latter’s displeasure, he awkwardly explained to him the

[007-131] seeming disrespect of the audience: that noonday had arrived

[007-132] before his eminence, and that the comedians had been forced to

[007-133] begin without waiting for his eminence.

[007-134] The cardinal burst into a laugh.

[007-135] “On my faith, the rector of the university ought to have done the

[007-136] same. What say you, Master Guillaume Rym?”

[007-137] “Monseigneur,” replied Guillaume Rym, “let us be content with

[007-138] having escaped half of the comedy. There is at least that much

[007-139] gained.”

[007-140] “Can these rascals continue their farce?” asked the bailiff.

[007-141] “Continue, continue,” said the cardinal, “it’s all the same to

[007-142] me. I’ll read my breviary in the meantime.”

[007-143] The bailiff advanced to the edge of the estrade, and cried, after

[007-144] having invoked silence by a wave of the hand,—

[007-145] “_Bourgeois_, rustics, and citizens, in order to satisfy those

[007-146] who wish the play to begin again, and those who wish it to end,

[007-147] his eminence orders that it be continued.”

[007-148] Both parties were forced to resign themselves. But the public and

[007-149] the author long cherished a grudge against the cardinal.

[007-150] So the personages on the stage took up their parts, and Gringoire

[007-151] hoped that the rest of his work, at least, would be listened to.

[007-152] This hope was speedily dispelled like his other illusions;

[007-153] silence had indeed, been restored in the audience, after a

[007-154] fashion; but Gringoire had not observed that at the moment when

[007-155] the cardinal gave the order to continue, the gallery was far from

[007-156] full, and that after the Flemish envoys there had arrived new

[007-157] personages forming part of the _cortège_, whose names and ranks,

[007-158] shouted out in the midst of his dialogue by the intermittent cry

[007-159] of the usher, produced considerable ravages in it. Let the reader

[007-160] imagine the effect in the midst of a theatrical piece, of the

[007-161] yelping of an usher, flinging in between two rhymes, and often in

[007-162] the middle of a line, parentheses like the following,—

[007-163] “Master Jacques Charmolue, procurator to the king in the

[007-164] Ecclesiastical Courts!”

[007-165] “Jehan de Harlay, equerry guardian of the office of chevalier of

[007-166] the night watch of the city of Paris!”

[007-167] “Messire Galiot de Genoilhac, chevalier, seigneur de Brussac,

[007-168] master of the king’s artillery!”

[007-169] “Master Dreux-Raguier, surveyor of the woods and forests of the

[007-170] king our sovereign, in the land of France, Champagne and Brie!”

[007-171] “Messire Louis de Graville, chevalier, councillor, and

[007-172] chamberlain of the king, admiral of France, keeper of the Forest

[007-173] of Vincennes!”

[007-174] “Master Denis le Mercier, guardian of the house of the blind at

[007-175] Paris!” etc., etc., etc.

[007-176] This was becoming unbearable.

[007-177] This strange accompaniment, which rendered it difficult to follow

[007-178] the piece, made Gringoire all the more indignant because he could

[007-179] not conceal from himself the fact that the interest was

[007-180] continually increasing, and that all his work required was a

[007-181] chance of being heard.

[007-182] It was, in fact, difficult to imagine a more ingenious and more

[007-183] dramatic composition. The four personages of the prologue were

[007-184] bewailing themselves in their mortal embarrassment, when Venus in

[007-185] person, (_vera incessa patuit dea_) presented herself to them,

[007-186] clad in a fine robe bearing the heraldic device of the ship of

[007-187] the city of Paris. She had come herself to claim the dolphin

[007-188] promised to the most beautiful. Jupiter, whose thunder could be

[007-189] heard rumbling in the dressing-room, supported her claim, and

[007-190] Venus was on the point of carrying it off,—that is to say,

[007-191] without allegory, of marrying monsieur the dauphin, when a young

[007-192] child clad in white damask, and holding in her hand a daisy (a

[007-193] transparent personification of Mademoiselle Marguerite of

[007-194] Flanders) came to contest it with Venus.

[007-195] Theatrical effect and change.

[007-196] After a dispute, Venus, Marguerite, and the assistants agreed to

[007-197] submit to the good judgment of time holy Virgin. There was

[007-198] another good part, that of the king of Mesopotamia; but through

[007-199] so many interruptions, it was difficult to make out what end he

[007-200] served. All these persons had ascended by the ladder to the

[007-201] stage.

[007-202] But all was over; none of these beauties had been felt nor

[007-203] understood. On the entrance of the cardinal, one would have said

[007-204] that an invisible magic thread had suddenly drawn all glances

[007-205] from the marble table to the gallery, from the southern to the

[007-206] western extremity of the hall. Nothing could disenchant the

[007-207] audience; all eyes remained fixed there, and the new-comers and

[007-208] their accursed names, and their faces, and their costumes,

[007-209] afforded a continual diversion. This was very distressing. With

[007-210] the exception of Gisquette and Liénarde, who turned round from

[007-211] time to time when Gringoire plucked them by the sleeve; with the

[007-212] exception of the big, patient neighbor, no one listened, no one

[007-213] looked at the poor, deserted morality full face. Gringoire saw

[007-214] only profiles.
