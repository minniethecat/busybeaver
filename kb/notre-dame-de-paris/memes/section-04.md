---[004-001] “The morality this very instant!” repeated the crowd; “this very

[004-002] instant! the sack and the rope for the comedians, and the

[004-003] cardinal!”

[004-004] Poor Jupiter, haggard, frightened, pale beneath his rouge,

[004-005] dropped his thunderbolt, took his cap in his hand; then he bowed

[004-006] and trembled and stammered: “His eminence—the ambassadors—Madame

[004-007] Marguerite of Flanders—.” He did not know what to say. In truth,

[004-008] he was afraid of being hung.

[004-009] Hung by the populace for waiting, hung by the cardinal for not

[004-010] having waited, he saw between the two dilemmas only an abyss;

[004-011] that is to say, a gallows.

[004-012] Luckily, some one came to rescue him from his embarrassment, and

[004-013] assume the responsibility.

[004-014] An individual who was standing beyond the railing, in the free

[004-015] space around the marble table, and whom no one had yet caught

[004-016] sight of, since his long, thin body was completely sheltered from

[004-017] every visual ray by the diameter of the pillar against which he

[004-018] was leaning; this individual, we say, tall, gaunt, pallid, blond,

[004-019] still young, although already wrinkled about the brow and cheeks,

[004-020] with brilliant eyes and a smiling mouth, clad in garments of

[004-021] black serge, worn and shining with age, approached the marble

[004-022] table, and made a sign to the poor sufferer. But the other was so

[004-023] confused that he did not see him. The new comer advanced another

[004-024] step.

[004-025] “Jupiter,” said he, “my dear Jupiter!”

[004-026] The other did not hear.

[004-027] At last, the tall blond, driven out of patience, shrieked almost

[004-028] in his face,—

[004-029] “Michel Giborne!”

[004-030] “Who calls me?” said Jupiter, as though awakened with a start.

[004-031] “I,” replied the person clad in black.

[004-032] “Ah!” said Jupiter.

[004-033] “Begin at once,” went on the other. “Satisfy the populace; I

[004-034] undertake to appease the bailiff, who will appease monsieur the

[004-035] cardinal.”

[004-036] Jupiter breathed once more.

[004-037] “Messeigneurs the _bourgeois_,” he cried, at the top of his lungs

[004-038] to the crowd, which continued to hoot him, “we are going to begin

[004-039] at once.”

[004-040] “_Evoe Jupiter! Plaudite cives_! All hail, Jupiter! Applaud,

[004-041] citizens!” shouted the scholars.

[004-042] “Noël! Noël! good, good,” shouted the people.

[004-043] The hand clapping was deafening, and Jupiter had already

[004-044] withdrawn under his tapestry, while the hall still trembled with

[004-045] acclamations.

[004-046] In the meanwhile, the personage who had so magically turned the

[004-047] tempest into dead calm, as our old and dear Corneille puts it,

[004-048] had modestly retreated to the half-shadow of his pillar, and

[004-049] would, no doubt, have remained invisible there, motionless, and

[004-050] mute as before, had he not been plucked by the sleeve by two

[004-051] young women, who, standing in the front row of the spectators,

[004-052] had noticed his colloquy with Michel Giborne-Jupiter.

[004-053] “Master,” said one of them, making him a sign to approach.

[004-054] “Hold your tongue, my dear Liénarde,” said her neighbor, pretty,

[004-055] fresh, and very brave, in consequence of being dressed up in her

[004-056] best attire. “He is not a clerk, he is a layman; you must not say

[004-057] master to him, but messire.”

[004-058] “Messire,” said Liénarde.

[004-059] The stranger approached the railing.

[004-060] “What would you have of me, damsels?” he asked, with alacrity.

[004-061] “Oh! nothing,” replied Liénarde, in great confusion; “it is my

[004-062] neighbor, Gisquette la Gencienne, who wishes to speak with you.”

[004-063] “Not so,” replied Gisquette, blushing; “it was Liénarde who

[004-064] called you master; I only told her to say messire.”

[004-065] The two young girls dropped their eyes. The man, who asked

[004-066] nothing better than to enter into conversation, looked at them

[004-067] with a smile.

[004-068] “So you have nothing to say to me, damsels?”

[004-069] “Oh! nothing at all,” replied Gisquette.

[004-070] “Nothing,” said Liénarde.

[004-071] The tall, light-haired young man retreated a step; but the two

[004-072] curious maidens had no mind to let slip their prize.

[004-073] “Messire,” said Gisquette, with the impetuosity of an open

[004-074] sluice, or of a woman who has made up her mind, “do you know that

[004-075] soldier who is to play the part of Madame the Virgin in the

[004-076] mystery?”

[004-077] “You mean the part of Jupiter?” replied the stranger.

[004-078] “Hé! yes,” said Liénarde, “isn’t she stupid? So you know

[004-079] Jupiter?”

[004-080] “Michel Giborne?” replied the unknown; “yes, madam.”

[004-081] “He has a fine beard!” said Liénarde.

[004-082] “Will what they are about to say here be fine?” inquired

[004-083] Gisquette, timidly.

[004-084] “Very fine, mademoiselle,” replied the unknown, without the

[004-085] slightest hesitation.

[004-086] “What is it to be?” said Liénarde.

[004-087] “‘The Good Judgment of Madame the Virgin,’—a morality, if you

[004-088] please, damsel.”

[004-089] “Ah! that makes a difference,” responded Liénarde.

[004-090] A brief silence ensued—broken by the stranger.

[004-091] “It is a perfectly new morality, and one which has never yet been

[004-092] played.”

[004-093] “Then it is not the same one,” said Gisquette, “that was given

[004-094] two years ago, on the day of the entrance of monsieur the legate,

[004-095] and where three handsome maids played the parts—”

[004-096] “Of sirens,” said Liénarde.

[004-097] “And all naked,” added the young man.

[004-098] Liénarde lowered her eyes modestly. Gisquette glanced at her and

[004-099] did the same. He continued, with a smile,—

[004-100] “It was a very pleasant thing to see. To-day it is a morality

[004-101] made expressly for Madame the Demoiselle of Flanders.”

[004-102] “Will they sing shepherd songs?” inquired Gisquette.

[004-103] “Fie!” said the stranger, “in a morality? you must not confound

[004-104] styles. If it were a farce, well and good.”

[004-105] “That is a pity,” resumed Gisquette. “That day, at the Ponceau

[004-106] Fountain, there were wild men and women, who fought and assumed

[004-107] many aspects, as they sang little motets and bergerettes.”

[004-108] “That which is suitable for a legate,” returned the stranger,

[004-109] with a good deal of dryness, “is not suitable for a princess.”

[004-110] “And beside them,” resumed Liénarde, “played many brass

[004-111] instruments, making great melodies.”

[004-112] “And for the refreshment of the passers-by,” continued Gisquette,

[004-113] “the fountain spouted through three mouths, wine, milk, and

[004-114] hippocrass, of which every one drank who wished.”

[004-115] “And a little below the Ponceau, at the Trinity,” pursued

[004-116] Liénarde, “there was a passion performed, and without any

[004-117] speaking.”

[004-118] “How well I remember that!” exclaimed Gisquette; “God on the

[004-119] cross, and the two thieves on the right and the left.” Here the

[004-120] young gossips, growing warm at the memory of the entrance of

[004-121] monsieur the legate, both began to talk at once.

[004-122] “And, further on, at the Painters’ Gate, there were other

[004-123] personages, very richly clad.”

[004-124] “And at the fountain of Saint-Innocent, that huntsman, who was

[004-125] chasing a hind with great clamor of dogs and hunting-horns.”

[004-126] “And, at the Paris slaughter-houses, stages, representing the

[004-127] fortress of Dieppe!”

[004-128] “And when the legate passed, you remember, Gisquette? they made

[004-129] the assault, and the English all had their throats cut.”

[004-130] “And against the gate of the Châtelet, there were very fine

[004-131] personages!”

[004-132] “And on the Port au Change, which was all draped above!”

[004-133] “And when the legate passed, they let fly on the bridge more than

[004-134] two hundred sorts of birds; wasn’t it beautiful, Liénarde?”

[004-135] “It will be better to-day,” finally resumed their interlocutor,

[004-136] who seemed to listen to them with impatience.

[004-137] “Do you promise us that this mystery will be fine?” said

[004-138] Gisquette.

[004-139] “Without doubt,” he replied; then he added, with a certain

[004-140] emphasis,—“I am the author of it, damsels.”

[004-141] “Truly?” said the young girls, quite taken aback.

[004-142] “Truly!” replied the poet, bridling a little; “that is, to say,

[004-143] there are two of us; Jehan Marchand, who has sawed the planks and

[004-144] erected the framework of the theatre and the woodwork; and I, who

[004-145] have made the piece. My name is Pierre Gringoire.”

[004-146] The author of the “Cid” could not have said “Pierre Corneille”

[004-147] with more pride.

[004-148] Our readers have been able to observe, that a certain amount of

[004-149] time must have already elapsed from the moment when Jupiter had

[004-150] retired beneath the tapestry to the instant when the author of

[004-151] the new morality had thus abruptly revealed himself to the

[004-152] innocent admiration of Gisquette and Liénarde. Remarkable fact:

[004-153] that whole crowd, so tumultuous but a few moments before, now

[004-154] waited amiably on the word of the comedian; which proves the

[004-155] eternal truth, still experienced every day in our theatres, that

[004-156] the best means of making the public wait patiently is to assure

[004-157] them that one is about to begin instantly.

[004-158] However, scholar Johannes had not fallen asleep.

[004-159] “Holà hé!” he shouted suddenly, in the midst of the peaceable

[004-160] waiting which had followed the tumult. “Jupiter, Madame the

[004-161] Virgin, buffoons of the devil! are you jeering at us? The piece!

[004-162] the piece! commence or we will commence again!”

[004-163] This was all that was needed.

[004-164] The music of high and low instruments immediately became audible

[004-165] from the interior of the stage; the tapestry was raised; four

[004-166] personages, in motley attire and painted faces, emerged from it,

[004-167] climbed the steep ladder of the theatre, and, arrived upon the

[004-168] upper platform, arranged themselves in a line before the public,

[004-169] whom they saluted with profound reverences; then the symphony

[004-170] ceased.

[004-171] The mystery was about to begin.

[004-172] The four personages, after having reaped a rich reward of

[004-173] applause for their reverences, began, in the midst of profound

[004-174] silence, a prologue, which we gladly spare the reader. Moreover,

[004-175] as happens in our own day, the public was more occupied with the

[004-176] costumes that the actors wore than with the roles that they were

[004-177] enacting; and, in truth, they were right. All four were dressed

[004-178] in parti-colored robes of yellow and white, which were

[004-179] distinguished from each other only by the nature of the stuff;

[004-180] the first was of gold and silver brocade; the second, of silk;

[004-181] the third, of wool; the fourth, of linen. The first of these

[004-182] personages carried in his right hand a sword; the second, two

[004-183] golden keys; the third, a pair of scales; the fourth, a spade:

[004-184] and, in order to aid sluggish minds which would not have seen

[004-185] clearly through the transparency of these attributes, there was

[004-186] to be read, in large, black letters, on the hem of the robe of

[004-187] brocade, MY NAME IS NOBILITY; on the hem of the silken robe, MY

[004-188] NAME IS CLERGY; on the hem of the woolen robe, MY NAME IS

[004-189] MERCHANDISE; on the hem of the linen robe, MY NAME IS LABOR. The

[004-190] sex of the two male characters was briefly indicated to every

[004-191] judicious spectator, by their shorter robes, and by the cap which

[004-192] they wore on their heads; while the two female characters, less

[004-193] briefly clad, were covered with hoods.

[004-194] Much ill-will would also have been required, not to comprehend,

[004-195] through the medium of the poetry of the prologue, that Labor was

[004-196] wedded to Merchandise, and Clergy to Nobility, and that the two

[004-197] happy couples possessed in common a magnificent golden dolphin,

[004-198] which they desired to adjudge to the fairest only. So they were

[004-199] roaming about the world seeking and searching for this beauty,

[004-200] and, after having successively rejected the Queen of Golconda,

[004-201] the Princess of Trebizonde, the daughter of the Grand Khan of

[004-202] Tartary, etc., Labor and Clergy, Nobility and Merchandise, had

[004-203] come to rest upon the marble table of the Palais de Justice, and

[004-204] to utter, in the presence of the honest audience, as many

[004-205] sentences and maxims as could then be dispensed at the Faculty of

[004-206] Arts, at examinations, sophisms, determinances, figures, and

[004-207] acts, where the masters took their degrees.

[004-208] All this was, in fact, very fine.

[004-209] Nevertheless, in that throng, upon which the four allegories vied

[004-210] with each other in pouring out floods of metaphors, there was no

[004-211] ear more attentive, no heart that palpitated more, not an eye was

[004-212] more haggard, no neck more outstretched, than the eye, the ear,

[004-213] the neck, and the heart of the author, of the poet, of that brave

[004-214] Pierre Gringoire, who had not been able to resist, a moment

[004-215] before, the joy of telling his name to two pretty girls. He had

[004-216] retreated a few paces from them, behind his pillar, and there he

[004-217] listened, looked, enjoyed. The amiable applause which had greeted

[004-218] the beginning of his prologue was still echoing in his bosom, and

[004-219] he was completely absorbed in that species of ecstatic

[004-220] contemplation with which an author beholds his ideas fall, one by

[004-221] one, from the mouth of the actor into the vast silence of the

[004-222] audience. Worthy Pierre Gringoire!
