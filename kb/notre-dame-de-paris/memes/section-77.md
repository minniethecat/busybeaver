---[077-001] “Master Olivier,” said the king, “the total?”

[077-002] “Three hundred sixty-seven livres, eight sols, three deniers

[077-003] parisis.

[077-004] “Notre-Dame!” cried the king. “This is an outrageous cage!”

[077-005] He tore the book from Master Olivier’s hands, and set to

[077-006] reckoning it himself upon his fingers, examining the paper and

[077-007] the cage alternately. Meanwhile, the prisoner could be heard

[077-008] sobbing. This was lugubrious in the darkness, and their faces

[077-009] turned pale as they looked at each other.

[077-010] “Fourteen years, sire! Fourteen years now! since the month of

[077-011] April, 1469. In the name of the Holy Mother of God, sire, listen

[077-012] to me! During all this time you have enjoyed the heat of the sun.

[077-013] Shall I, frail creature, never more behold the day? Mercy, sire!

[077-014] Be pitiful! Clemency is a fine, royal virtue, which turns aside

[077-015] the currents of wrath. Does your majesty believe that in the hour

[077-016] of death it will be a great cause of content for a king never to

[077-017] have left any offence unpunished? Besides, sire, I did not betray

[077-018] your majesty, ’twas Monsieur d’Angers; and I have on my foot a

[077-019] very heavy chain, and a great ball of iron at the end, much

[077-020] heavier than it should be in reason. Eh! sire! Have pity on me!”

[077-021] “Olivier,” cried the king, throwing back his head, “I observe

[077-022] that they charge me twenty sols a hogshead for plaster, while it

[077-023] is worth but twelve. You will refer back this account.”

[077-024] He turned his back on the cage, and set out to leave the room.

[077-025] The miserable prisoner divined from the removal of the torches

[077-026] and the noise, that the king was taking his departure.

[077-027] “Sire! sire!” he cried in despair.

[077-028] The door closed again. He no longer saw anything, and heard only

[077-029] the hoarse voice of the turnkey, singing in his ears this ditty,—

[077-030] “Maître Jean Balue,

[077-031] A perdu la vue

[077-032] De ses évêchés.

[077-033] Monsieur de Verdun.

[077-034] N’en a plus pas un;

[077-035] Tous sont dépêchés.”[65]

[077-036] The king reascended in silence to his retreat, and his suite

[077-037] followed him, terrified by the last groans of the condemned man.

[077-038] All at once his majesty turned to the Governor of the Bastille,—

[077-039] “By the way,” said he, “was there not some one in that cage?”

[077-040] “Pardieu, yes sire!” replied the governor, astounded by the

[077-041] question.

[077-042] “And who was it?”

[077-043] “Monsieur the Bishop of Verdun.”

[077-044] The king knew this better than any one else. But it was a mania

[077-045] of his.

[077-046] “Ah!” said he, with the innocent air of thinking of it for the

[077-047] first time, “Guillaume de Harancourt, the friend of Monsieur the

[077-048] Cardinal Balue. A good devil of a bishop!”

[077-049] At the expiration of a few moments, the door of the retreat had

[077-050] opened again, then closed upon the five personages whom the

[077-051] reader has seen at the beginning of this chapter, and who resumed

[077-052] their places, their whispered conversations, and their attitudes.

[077-053] During the king’s absence, several despatches had been placed on

[077-054] his table, and he broke the seals himself. Then he began to read

[077-055] them promptly, one after the other, made a sign to Master Olivier

[077-056] who appeared to exercise the office of minister, to take a pen,

[077-057] and without communicating to him the contents of the despatches,

[077-058] he began to dictate in a low voice, the replies which the latter

[077-059] wrote, on his knees, in an inconvenient attitude before the

[077-060] table.

[077-061] Guillaume Rym was on the watch.

[077-062] The king spoke so low that the Flemings heard nothing of his

[077-063] dictation, except some isolated and rather unintelligible scraps,

[077-064] such as,—

[077-065] “To maintain the fertile places by commerce, and the sterile by

[077-066] manufactures....—To show the English lords our four bombards,

[077-067] London, Brabant, Bourg-en-Bresse, Saint-Omer....—Artillery is the

[077-068] cause of war being made more judiciously now....—To Monsieur de

[077-069] Bressuire, our friend....—Armies cannot be maintained without

[077-070] tribute, etc.”

[077-071] Once he raised his voice,—

[077-072] “_Pasque Dieu!_ Monsieur the King of Sicily seals his letters

[077-073] with yellow wax, like a king of France. Perhaps we are in the

[077-074] wrong to permit him so to do. My fair cousin of Burgundy granted

[077-075] no armorial bearings with a field of gules. The grandeur of

[077-076] houses is assured by the integrity of prerogatives. Note this,

[077-077] friend Olivier.”

[077-078] Again,—

[077-079] “Oh! oh!” said he, “What a long message! What doth our brother

[077-080] the emperor claim?” And running his eye over the missive and

[077-081] breaking his reading with interjection: “Surely! the Germans are

[077-082] so great and powerful, that it is hardly credible—But let us not

[077-083] forget the old proverb: ‘The finest county is Flanders; the

[077-084] finest duchy, Milan; the finest kingdom, France.’ Is it not so,

[077-085] Messieurs Flemings?”

[077-086] This time Coppenole bowed in company with Guillaume Rym. The

[077-087] hosier’s patriotism was tickled.

[077-088] The last despatch made Louis XI. frown.

[077-089] “What is this?” he said, “Complaints and fault finding against

[077-090] our garrisons in Picardy! Olivier, write with diligence to M. the

[077-091] Marshal de Rouault:—That discipline is relaxed. That the

[077-092] gendarmes of the unattached troops, the feudal nobles, the free

[077-093] archers, and the Swiss inflict infinite evils on the

[077-094] rustics.—That the military, not content with what they find in

[077-095] the houses of the rustics, constrain them with violent blows of

[077-096] cudgel or of lash to go and get wine, spices, and other

[077-097] unreasonable things in the town.—That monsieur the king knows

[077-098] this. That we undertake to guard our people against

[077-099] inconveniences, larcenies and pillage.—That such is our will, by

[077-100] our Lady!—That in addition, it suits us not that any fiddler,

[077-101] barber, or any soldier varlet should be clad like a prince, in

[077-102] velvet, cloth of silk, and rings of gold.—That these vanities are

[077-103] hateful to God.—That we, who are gentlemen, content ourselves

[077-104] with a doublet of cloth at sixteen sols the ell, of Paris.—That

[077-105] messieurs the camp-followers can very well come down to that,

[077-106] also.—Command and ordain.—To Monsieur de Rouault, our

[077-107] friend.—Good.”

[077-108] He dictated this letter aloud, in a firm tone, and in jerks. At

[077-109] the moment when he finished it, the door opened and gave passage

[077-110] to a new personage, who precipitated himself into the chamber,

[077-111] crying in affright,—

[077-112] “Sire! sire! there is a sedition of the populace in Paris!” Louis

[077-113] XI.’s grave face contracted; but all that was visible of his

[077-114] emotion passed away like a flash of lightning. He controlled

[077-115] himself and said with tranquil severity,—

[077-116] “Gossip Jacques, you enter very abruptly!”

[077-117] “Sire! sire! there is a revolt!” repeated Gossip Jacques

[077-118] breathlessly.

[077-119] The king, who had risen, grasped him roughly by the arm, and said

[077-120] in his ear, in such a manner as to be heard by him alone, with

[077-121] concentrated rage and a sidelong glance at the Flemings,—

[077-122] “Hold your tongue! or speak low!”

[077-123] The new comer understood, and began in a low tone to give a very

[077-124] terrified account, to which the king listened calmly, while

[077-125] Guillaume Rym called Coppenole’s attention to the face and dress

[077-126] of the new arrival, to his furred cowl, (_caputia fourrata_), his

[077-127] short cape, (_epitogia curta_), his robe of black velvet, which

[077-128] bespoke a president of the court of accounts.

[077-129] Hardly had this personage given the king some explanations, when

[077-130] Louis XI. exclaimed, bursting into a laugh,—

[077-131] “In truth? Speak aloud, Gossip Coictier! What call is there for

[077-132] you to talk so low? Our Lady knoweth that we conceal nothing from

[077-133] our good friends the Flemings.”

[077-134] “But sire...”

[077-135] “Speak loud!”

[077-136] Gossip Coictier was struck dumb with surprise.

[077-137] “So,” resumed the king,—“speak sir,—there is a commotion among

[077-138] the louts in our good city of Paris?”

[077-139] “Yes, sire.”

[077-140] “And which is moving you say, against monsieur the bailiff of the

[077-141] Palais-de-Justice?”

[077-142] “So it appears,” said the gossip, who still stammered, utterly

[077-143] astounded by the abrupt and inexplicable change which had just

[077-144] taken place in the king’s thoughts.

[077-145] Louis XI. continued: “Where did the watch meet the rabble?”

[077-146] “Marching from the Grand Truanderie, towards the

[077-147] Pont-aux-Changeurs. I met it myself as I was on my way hither to

[077-148] obey your majesty’s commands. I heard some of them shouting:

[077-149] ‘Down with the bailiff of the palace!’”

[077-150] “And what complaints have they against the bailiff?”

[077-151] “Ah!” said Gossip Jacques, “because he is their lord.”

[077-152] “Really?”

[077-153] “Yes, sire. They are knaves from the Cour-des-Miracles. They have

[077-154] been complaining this long while, of the bailiff, whose vassals

[077-155] they are. They do not wish to recognize him either as judge or as

[077-156] voyer?”[66]

[077-157] “Yes, certainly!” retorted the king with a smile of satisfaction

[077-158] which he strove in vain to disguise.

[077-159] “In all their petitions to the Parliament, they claim to have but

[077-160] two masters. Your majesty and their God, who is the devil, I

[077-161] believe.”

[077-162] “Eh! eh!” said the king.

[077-163] He rubbed his hands, he laughed with that inward mirth which

[077-164] makes the countenance beam; he was unable to dissimulate his joy,

[077-165] although he endeavored at moments to compose himself. No one

[077-166] understood it in the least, not even Master Olivier. He remained

[077-167] silent for a moment, with a thoughtful but contented air.

[077-168] “Are they in force?” he suddenly inquired.

[077-169] “Yes, assuredly, sire,” replied Gossip Jacques.

[077-170] “How many?”

[077-171] “Six thousand at the least.”

[077-172] The king could not refrain from saying: “Good!” he went on,—

[077-173] “Are they armed?”

[077-174] “With scythes, pikes, hackbuts, pickaxes. All sorts of very

[077-175] violent weapons.”

[077-176] The king did not appear in the least disturbed by this list.

[077-177] Jacques considered it his duty to add,—

[077-178] “If your majesty does not send prompt succor to the bailiff, he

[077-179] is lost.”

[077-180] “We will send,” said the king with an air of false seriousness.

[077-181] “It is well. Assuredly we will send. Monsieur the bailiff is our

[077-182] friend. Six thousand! They are desperate scamps! Their audacity

[077-183] is marvellous, and we are greatly enraged at it. But we have only

[077-184] a few people about us to-night. To-morrow morning will be time

[077-185] enough.”

[077-186] Gossip Jacques exclaimed, “Instantly, sire! there will be time to

[077-187] sack the bailiwick a score of times, to violate the seignory, to

[077-188] hang the bailiff. For God’s sake, sire! send before to-morrow

[077-189] morning.”

[077-190] The king looked him full in the face. “I have told you to-morrow

[077-191] morning.”

[077-192] It was one of those looks to which one does not reply. After a

[077-193] silence, Louis XI. raised his voice once more,—

[077-194] “You should know that, Gossip Jacques. What was—”

[077-195] He corrected himself. “What is the bailiff’s feudal

[077-196] jurisdiction?”

[077-197] “Sire, the bailiff of the palace has the Rue Calendre as far as

[077-198] the Rue de l’Herberie, the Place Saint-Michel, and the localities

[077-199] vulgarly known as the Mureaux, situated near the church of

[077-200] Notre-Dame des Champs (here Louis XI. raised the brim of his

[077-201] hat), which hotels number thirteen, plus the Cour des Miracles,

[077-202] plus the Maladerie, called the Banlieue, plus the whole highway

[077-203] which begins at that Maladerie and ends at the Porte

[077-204] Sainte-Jacques. Of these divers places he is voyer, high, middle,

[077-205] and low, justiciary, full seigneur.”

[077-206] “Bless me!” said the king, scratching his left ear with his right

[077-207] hand, “that makes a goodly bit of my city! Ah! monsieur the

[077-208] bailiff was king of all that.”

[077-209] This time he did not correct himself. He continued dreamily, and

[077-210] as though speaking to himself,—

[077-211] “Very fine, monsieur the bailiff! You had there between your

[077-212] teeth a pretty slice of our Paris.”

[077-213] All at once he broke out explosively, “_Pasque-Dieu!_ What people

[077-214] are those who claim to be voyers, justiciaries, lords and masters

[077-215] in our domains? who have their tollgates at the end of every

[077-216] field? their gallows and their hangman at every cross-road among

[077-217] our people? So that as the Greek believed that he had as many

[077-218] gods as there were fountains, and the Persian as many as he

[077-219] beheld stars, the Frenchman counts as many kings as he sees

[077-220] gibbets! Pardieu! ’tis an evil thing, and the confusion of it

[077-221] displeases me. I should greatly like to know whether it be the

[077-222] mercy of God that there should be in Paris any other lord than

[077-223] the king, any other judge than our parliament, any other emperor

[077-224] than ourselves in this empire! By the faith of my soul! the day

[077-225] must certainly come when there shall exist in France but one

[077-226] king, one lord, one judge, one headsman, as there is in paradise

[077-227] but one God!”
