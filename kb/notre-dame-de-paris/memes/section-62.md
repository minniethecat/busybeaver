[062-002] CHAPTER I. DELIRIUM.

[062-003] Claude Frollo was no longer in Notre-Dame when his adopted son so

[062-004] abruptly cut the fatal web in which the archdeacon and the gypsy

[062-005] were entangled. On returning to the sacristy he had torn off his

[062-006] alb, cope, and stole, had flung all into the hands of the

[062-007] stupefied beadle, had made his escape through the private door of

[062-008] the cloister, had ordered a boatman of the Terrain to transport

[062-009] him to the left bank of the Seine, and had plunged into the hilly

[062-010] streets of the University, not knowing whither he was going,

[062-011] encountering at every step groups of men and women who were

[062-012] hurrying joyously towards the Pont Saint-Michel, in the hope of

[062-013] still arriving in time to see the witch hung there,—pale, wild,

[062-014] more troubled, more blind and more fierce than a night bird let

[062-015] loose and pursued by a troop of children in broad daylight. He no

[062-016] longer knew where he was, what he thought, or whether he were

[062-017] dreaming. He went forward, walking, running, taking any street at

[062-018] haphazard, making no choice, only urged ever onward away from the

[062-019] Grève, the horrible Grève, which he felt confusedly, to be behind

[062-020] him.

[062-021] In this manner he skirted Mount Sainte-Geneviève, and finally

[062-022] emerged from the town by the Porte Saint-Victor. He continued his

[062-023] flight as long as he could see, when he turned round, the

[062-024] turreted enclosure of the University, and the rare houses of the

[062-025] suburb; but, when, at length, a rise of ground had completely

[062-026] concealed from him that odious Paris, when he could believe

[062-027] himself to be a hundred leagues distant from it, in the fields,

[062-028] in the desert, he halted, and it seemed to him that he breathed

[062-029] more freely.

[062-030] Then frightful ideas thronged his mind. Once more he could see

[062-031] clearly into his soul, and he shuddered. He thought of that

[062-032] unhappy girl who had destroyed him, and whom he had destroyed. He

[062-033] cast a haggard eye over the double, tortuous way which fate had

[062-034] caused their two destinies to pursue up to their point of

[062-035] intersection, where it had dashed them against each other without

[062-036] mercy. He meditated on the folly of eternal vows, on the vanity

[062-037] of chastity, of science, of religion, of virtue, on the

[062-038] uselessness of God. He plunged to his heart’s content in evil

[062-039] thoughts, and in proportion as he sank deeper, he felt a Satanic

[062-040] laugh burst forth within him.

[062-041] And as he thus sifted his soul to the bottom, when he perceived

[062-042] how large a space nature had prepared there for the passions, he

[062-043] sneered still more bitterly. He stirred up in the depths of his

[062-044] heart all his hatred, all his malevolence; and, with the cold

[062-045] glance of a physician who examines a patient, he recognized the

[062-046] fact that this malevolence was nothing but vitiated love; that

[062-047] love, that source of every virtue in man, turned to horrible

[062-048] things in the heart of a priest, and that a man constituted like

[062-049] himself, in making himself a priest, made himself a demon. Then

[062-050] he laughed frightfully, and suddenly became pale again, when he

[062-051] considered the most sinister side of his fatal passion, of that

[062-052] corrosive, venomous malignant, implacable love, which had ended

[062-053] only in the gibbet for one of them and in hell for the other;

[062-054] condemnation for her, damnation for him.

[062-055] And then his laughter came again, when he reflected that Phœbus

[062-056] was alive; that after all, the captain lived, was gay and happy,

[062-057] had handsomer doublets than ever, and a new mistress whom he was

[062-058] conducting to see the old one hanged. His sneer redoubled its

[062-059] bitterness when he reflected that out of the living beings whose

[062-060] death he had desired, the gypsy, the only creature whom he did

[062-061] not hate, was the only one who had not escaped him.

[062-062] Then from the captain, his thought passed to the people, and

[062-063] there came to him a jealousy of an unprecedented sort. He

[062-064] reflected that the people also, the entire populace, had had

[062-065] before their eyes the woman whom he loved exposed almost naked.

[062-066] He writhed his arms with agony as he thought that the woman whose

[062-067] form, caught by him alone in the darkness would have been supreme

[062-068] happiness, had been delivered up in broad daylight at full

[062-069] noonday, to a whole people, clad as for a night of

[062-070] voluptuousness. He wept with rage over all these mysteries of

[062-071] love, profaned, soiled, laid bare, withered forever. He wept with

[062-072] rage as he pictured to himself how many impure looks had been

[062-073] gratified at the sight of that badly fastened shift, and that

[062-074] this beautiful girl, this virgin lily, this cup of modesty and

[062-075] delight, to which he would have dared to place his lips only

[062-076] trembling, had just been transformed into a sort of public bowl,

[062-077] whereat the vilest populace of Paris, thieves, beggars, lackeys,

[062-078] had come to quaff in common an audacious, impure, and depraved

[062-079] pleasure.

[062-080] And when he sought to picture to himself the happiness which he

[062-081] might have found upon earth, if she had not been a gypsy, and if

[062-082] he had not been a priest, if Phœbus had not existed and if she

[062-083] had loved him; when he pictured to himself that a life of

[062-084] serenity and love would have been possible to him also, even to

[062-085] him; that there were at that very moment, here and there upon the

[062-086] earth, happy couples spending the hours in sweet converse beneath

[062-087] orange trees, on the banks of brooks, in the presence of a

[062-088] setting sun, of a starry night; and that if God had so willed, he

[062-089] might have formed with her one of those blessed couples,—his

[062-090] heart melted in tenderness and despair.

[062-091] Oh! she! still she! It was this fixed idea which returned

[062-092] incessantly, which tortured him, which ate into his brain, and

[062-093] rent his vitals. He did not regret, he did not repent; all that

[062-094] he had done he was ready to do again; he preferred to behold her

[062-095] in the hands of the executioner rather than in the arms of the

[062-096] captain. But he suffered; he suffered so that at intervals he

[062-097] tore out handfuls of his hair to see whether it were not turning

[062-098] white.

[062-099] Among other moments there came one, when it occurred to him that

[062-100] it was perhaps the very minute when the hideous chain which he

[062-101] had seen that morning, was pressing its iron noose closer about

[062-102] that frail and graceful neck. This thought caused the

[062-103] perspiration to start from every pore.

[062-104] There was another moment when, while laughing diabolically at

[062-105] himself, he represented to himself la Esmeralda as he had seen

[062-106] her on that first day, lively, careless, joyous, gayly attired,

[062-107] dancing, winged, harmonious, and la Esmeralda of the last day, in

[062-108] her scanty shift, with a rope about her neck, mounting slowly

[062-109] with her bare feet, the angular ladder of the gallows; he figured

[062-110] to himself this double picture in such a manner that he gave vent

[062-111] to a terrible cry.

[062-112] While this hurricane of despair overturned, broke, tore up, bent,

[062-113] uprooted everything in his soul, he gazed at nature around him.

[062-114] At his feet, some chickens were searching the thickets and

[062-115] pecking, enamelled beetles ran about in the sun; overhead, some

[062-116] groups of dappled gray clouds were floating across the blue sky;

[062-117] on the horizon, the spire of the Abbey Saint-Victor pierced the

[062-118] ridge of the hill with its slate obelisk; and the miller of the

[062-119] Copeaue hillock was whistling as he watched the laborious wings

[062-120] of his mill turning. All this active, organized, tranquil life,

[062-121] recurring around him under a thousand forms, hurt him. He resumed

[062-122] his flight.

[062-123] He sped thus across the fields until evening. This flight from

[062-124] nature, life, himself, man, God, everything, lasted all day long.

[062-125] Sometimes he flung himself face downward on the earth, and tore

[062-126] up the young blades of wheat with his nails. Sometimes he halted

[062-127] in the deserted street of a village, and his thoughts were so

[062-128] intolerable that he grasped his head in both hands and tried to

[062-129] tear it from his shoulders in order to dash it upon the pavement.

[062-130] Towards the hour of sunset, he examined himself again, and found

[062-131] himself nearly mad. The tempest which had raged within him ever

[062-132] since the instant when he had lost the hope and the will to save

[062-133] the gypsy,—that tempest had not left in his conscience a single

[062-134] healthy idea, a single thought which maintained its upright

[062-135] position. His reason lay there almost entirely destroyed. There

[062-136] remained but two distinct images in his mind, la Esmeralda and

[062-137] the gallows; all the rest was blank. Those two images united,

[062-138] presented to him a frightful group; and the more he concentrated

[062-139] what attention and thought was left to him, the more he beheld

[062-140] them grow, in accordance with a fantastic progression, the one in

[062-141] grace, in charm, in beauty, in light, the other in deformity and

[062-142] horror; so that at last la Esmeralda appeared to him like a star,

[062-143] the gibbet like an enormous, fleshless arm.

[062-144] One remarkable fact is, that during the whole of this torture,

[062-145] the idea of dying did not seriously occur to him. The wretch was

[062-146] made so. He clung to life. Perhaps he really saw hell beyond it.

[062-147] Meanwhile, the day continued to decline. The living being which

[062-148] still existed in him reflected vaguely on retracing its steps. He

[062-149] believed himself to be far away from Paris; on taking his

[062-150] bearings, he perceived that he had only circled the enclosure of

[062-151] the University. The spire of Saint-Sulpice, and the three lofty

[062-152] needles of Saint Germain-des-Prés, rose above the horizon on his

[062-153] right. He turned his steps in that direction. When he heard the

[062-154] brisk challenge of the men-at-arms of the abbey, around the

[062-155] crenelated, circumscribing wall of Saint-Germain, he turned

[062-156] aside, took a path which presented itself between the abbey and

[062-157] the lazar-house of the bourg, and at the expiration of a few

[062-158] minutes found himself on the verge of the Pré-aux-Clercs. This

[062-159] meadow was celebrated by reason of the brawls which went on there

[062-160] night and day; it was the hydra of the poor monks of

[062-161] Saint-Germain: _quod monachis Sancti-Germaini pratensis hydra

[062-162] fuit, clericis nova semper dissidiorum capita suscitantibus_. The

[062-163] archdeacon was afraid of meeting some one there; he feared every

[062-164] human countenance; he had just avoided the University and the

[062-165] Bourg Saint-Germain; he wished to re-enter the streets as late as

[062-166] possible. He skirted the Pré-aux-Clercs, took the deserted path

[062-167] which separated it from the Dieu-Neuf, and at last reached the

[062-168] water’s edge. There Dom Claude found a boatman, who, for a few

[062-169] farthings in Parisian coinage, rowed him up the Seine as far as

[062-170] the point of the city, and landed him on that tongue of abandoned

[062-171] land where the reader has already beheld Gringoire dreaming, and

[062-172] which was prolonged beyond the king’s gardens, parallel to the

[062-173] Ile du Passeur-aux-Vaches.

[062-174] The monotonous rocking of the boat and the ripple of the water

[062-175] had, in some sort, quieted the unhappy Claude. When the boatman

[062-176] had taken his departure, he remained standing stupidly on the

[062-177] strand, staring straight before him and perceiving objects only

[062-178] through magnifying oscillations which rendered everything a sort

[062-179] of phantasmagoria to him. The fatigue of a great grief not

[062-180] infrequently produces this effect on the mind.

[062-181] The sun had set behind the lofty Tour-de-Nesle. It was the

[062-182] twilight hour. The sky was white, the water of the river was

[062-183] white. Between these two white expanses, the left bank of the

[062-184] Seine, on which his eyes were fixed, projected its gloomy mass

[062-185] and, rendered ever thinner and thinner by perspective, it plunged

[062-186] into the gloom of the horizon like a black spire. It was loaded

[062-187] with houses, of which only the obscure outline could be

[062-188] distinguished, sharply brought out in shadows against the light

[062-189] background of the sky and the water. Here and there windows began

[062-190] to gleam, like the holes in a brazier. That immense black obelisk

[062-191] thus isolated between the two white expanses of the sky and the

[062-192] river, which was very broad at this point, produced upon Dom

[062-193] Claude a singular effect, comparable to that which would be

[062-194] experienced by a man who, reclining on his back at the foot of

[062-195] the tower of Strasbourg, should gaze at the enormous spire

[062-196] plunging into the shadows of the twilight above his head. Only,

[062-197] in this case, it was Claude who was erect and the obelisk which

[062-198] was lying down; but, as the river, reflecting the sky, prolonged

[062-199] the abyss below him, the immense promontory seemed to be as

[062-200] boldly launched into space as any cathedral spire; and the

[062-201] impression was the same. This impression had even one stronger

[062-202] and more profound point about it, that it was indeed the tower of

[062-203] Strasbourg, but the tower of Strasbourg two leagues in height;

[062-204] something unheard of, gigantic, immeasurable; an edifice such as

[062-205] no human eye has ever seen; a tower of Babel. The chimneys of the

[062-206] houses, the battlements of the walls, the faceted gables of the

[062-207] roofs, the spire of the Augustines, the tower of Nesle, all these

[062-208] projections which broke the profile of the colossal obelisk added

[062-209] to the illusion by displaying in eccentric fashion to the eye the

[062-210] indentations of a luxuriant and fantastic sculpture.
