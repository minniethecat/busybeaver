---[043-001] “I do not know,” she replied.

[043-002] “The inconceivable impudence! A bellringer carrying off a wench,

[043-003] like a vicomte! a lout poaching on the game of gentlemen! that is

[043-004] a rare piece of assurance. However, he paid dearly for it. Master

[043-005] Pierrat Torterue is the harshest groom that ever curried a knave;

[043-006] and I can tell you, if it will be agreeable to you, that your

[043-007] bellringer’s hide got a thorough dressing at his hands.”

[043-008] “Poor man!” said the gypsy, in whom these words revived the

[043-009] memory of the pillory.

[043-010] The captain burst out laughing.

[043-011] “Corne-de-bœuf! here’s pity as well placed as a feather in a

[043-012] pig’s tail! May I have as big a belly as a pope, if—”

[043-013] He stopped short. “Pardon me, ladies; I believe that I was on the

[043-014] point of saying something foolish.”

[043-015] “Fie, sir” said la Gaillefontaine.

[043-016] “He talks to that creature in her own tongue!” added

[043-017] Fleur-de-Lys, in a low tone, her irritation increasing every

[043-018] moment. This irritation was not diminished when she beheld the

[043-019] captain, enchanted with the gypsy, and, most of all, with

[043-020] himself, execute a pirouette on his heel, repeating with coarse,

[043-021] naïve, and soldierly gallantry,—

[043-022] “A handsome wench, upon my soul!”

[043-023] “Rather savagely dressed,” said Diane de Christeuil, laughing to

[043-024] show her fine teeth.

[043-025] This remark was a flash of light to the others. Not being able to

[043-026] impugn her beauty, they attacked her costume.

[043-027] “That is true,” said la Montmichel; “what makes you run about the

[043-028] streets thus, without guimpe or ruff?”

[043-029] “That petticoat is so short that it makes one tremble,” added la

[043-030] Gaillefontaine.

[043-031] “My dear,” continued Fleur-de-Lys, with decided sharpness, “You

[043-032] will get yourself taken up by the sumptuary police for your

[043-033] gilded girdle.”

[043-034] “Little one, little one;” resumed la Christeuil, with an

[043-035] implacable smile, “if you were to put respectable sleeves upon

[043-036] your arms they would get less sunburned.”

[043-037] It was, in truth, a spectacle worthy of a more intelligent

[043-038] spectator than Phœbus, to see how these beautiful maidens, with

[043-039] their envenomed and angry tongues, wound, serpent-like, and

[043-040] glided and writhed around the street dancer. They were cruel and

[043-041] graceful; they searched and rummaged maliciously in her poor and

[043-042] silly toilet of spangles and tinsel. There was no end to their

[043-043] laughter, irony, and humiliation. Sarcasms rained down upon the

[043-044] gypsy, and haughty condescension and malevolent looks. One would

[043-045] have thought they were young Roman dames thrusting golden pins

[043-046] into the breast of a beautiful slave. One would have pronounced

[043-047] them elegant grayhounds, circling, with inflated nostrils, round

[043-048] a poor woodland fawn, whom the glance of their master forbade

[043-049] them to devour.

[043-050] After all, what was a miserable dancer on the public squares in

[043-051] the presence of these high-born maidens? They seemed to take no

[043-052] heed of her presence, and talked of her aloud, to her face, as of

[043-053] something unclean, abject, and yet, at the same time, passably

[043-054] pretty.

[043-055] The gypsy was not insensible to these pin-pricks. From time to

[043-056] time a flush of shame, a flash of anger inflamed her eyes or her

[043-057] cheeks; with disdain she made that little grimace with which the

[043-058] reader is already familiar, but she remained motionless; she

[043-059] fixed on Phœbus a sad, sweet, resigned look. There was also

[043-060] happiness and tenderness in that gaze. One would have said that

[043-061] she endured for fear of being expelled.

[043-062] Phœbus laughed, and took the gypsy’s part with a mixture of

[043-063] impertinence and pity.

[043-064] “Let them talk, little one!” he repeated, jingling his golden

[043-065] spurs. “No doubt your toilet is a little extravagant and wild,

[043-066] but what difference does that make with such a charming damsel as

[043-067] yourself?”

[043-068] “Good gracious!” exclaimed the blonde Gaillefontaine, drawing up

[043-069] her swan-like throat, with a bitter smile. “I see that messieurs

[043-070] the archers of the king’s police easily take fire at the handsome

[043-071] eyes of gypsies!”

[043-072] “Why not?” said Phœbus.

[043-073] At this reply uttered carelessly by the captain, like a stray

[043-074] stone, whose fall one does not even watch, Colombe began to

[043-075] laugh, as well as Diane, Amelotte, and Fleur-de-Lys, into whose

[043-076] eyes at the same time a tear started.

[043-077] The gypsy, who had dropped her eyes on the floor at the words of

[043-078] Colombe de Gaillefontaine, raised them beaming with joy and pride

[043-079] and fixed them once more on Phœbus. She was very beautiful at

[043-080] that moment.

[043-081] The old dame, who was watching this scene, felt offended, without

[043-082] understanding why.

[043-083] “Holy Virgin!” she suddenly exclaimed, “what is it moving about

[043-084] my legs? Ah! the villanous beast!”

[043-085] It was the goat, who had just arrived, in search of his mistress,

[043-086] and who, in dashing towards the latter, had begun by entangling

[043-087] his horns in the pile of stuffs which the noble dame’s garments

[043-088] heaped up on her feet when she was seated.

[043-089] This created a diversion. The gypsy disentangled his horns

[043-090] without uttering a word.

[043-091] “Oh! here’s the little goat with golden hoofs!” exclaimed

[043-092] Bérangère, dancing with joy.

[043-093] The gypsy crouched down on her knees and leaned her cheek against

[043-094] the fondling head of the goat. One would have said that she was

[043-095] asking pardon for having quitted it thus.

[043-096] Meanwhile, Diane had bent down to Colombe’s ear.

[043-097] “Ah! good heavens! why did not I think of that sooner? ’Tis the

[043-098] gypsy with the goat. They say she is a sorceress, and that her

[043-099] goat executes very miraculous tricks.”

[043-100] “Well!” said Colombe, “the goat must now amuse us in its turn,

[043-101] and perform a miracle for us.”

[043-102] Diane and Colombe eagerly addressed the gypsy.

[043-103] “Little one, make your goat perform a miracle.”

[043-104] “I do not know what you mean,” replied the dancer.

[043-105] “A miracle, a piece of magic, a bit of sorcery, in short.”

[043-106] “I do not understand.” And she fell to caressing the pretty

[043-107] animal, repeating, “Djali! Djali!”

[043-108] At that moment Fleur-de-Lys noticed a little bag of embroidered

[043-109] leather suspended from the neck of the goat,—

[043-110] “What is that?” she asked of the gypsy.

[043-111] The gypsy raised her large eyes upon her and replied gravely,—

[043-112] “That is my secret.”

[043-113] “I should really like to know what your secret is,” thought

[043-114] Fleur-de-Lys.

[043-115] Meanwhile, the good dame had risen angrily,—“Come now, gypsy, if

[043-116] neither you nor your goat can dance for us, what are you doing

[043-117] here?”

[043-118] The gypsy walked slowly towards the door, without making any

[043-119] reply. But the nearer she approached it, the more her pace

[043-120] slackened. An irresistible magnet seemed to hold her. Suddenly

[043-121] she turned her eyes, wet with tears, towards Phœbus, and halted.

[043-122] “True God!” exclaimed the captain, “that’s not the way to depart.

[043-123] Come back and dance something for us. By the way, my sweet love,

[043-124] what is your name?”

[043-125] “La Esmeralda,” said the dancer, never taking her eyes from him.

[043-126] At this strange name, a burst of wild laughter broke from the

[043-127] young girls.

[043-128] “Here’s a terrible name for a young lady,” said Diane.

[043-129] “You see well enough,” retorted Amelotte, “that she is an

[043-130] enchantress.”

[043-131] “My dear,” exclaimed Dame Aloïse solemnly, “your parents did not

[043-132] commit the sin of giving you that name at the baptismal font.”

[043-133] In the meantime, several minutes previously, Bérangère had coaxed

[043-134] the goat into a corner of the room with a marchpane cake, without

[043-135] any one having noticed her. In an instant they had become good

[043-136] friends. The curious child had detached the bag from the goat’s

[043-137] neck, had opened it, and had emptied out its contents on the rush

[043-138] matting; it was an alphabet, each letter of which was separately

[043-139] inscribed on a tiny block of boxwood. Hardly had these playthings

[043-140] been spread out on the matting, when the child, with surprise,

[043-141] beheld the goat (one of whose “miracles” this was no doubt), draw

[043-142] out certain letters with its golden hoof, and arrange them, with

[043-143] gentle pushes, in a certain order. In a moment they constituted a

[043-144] word, which the goat seemed to have been trained to write, so

[043-145] little hesitation did it show in forming it, and Bérangère

[043-146] suddenly exclaimed, clasping her hands in admiration,—

[043-147] “Godmother Fleur-de-Lys, see what the goat has just done!”

[043-148] Fleur-de-Lys ran up and trembled. The letters arranged upon the

[043-149] floor formed this word,—

[043-150] PHŒBUS.

[043-151] “Was it the goat who wrote that?” she inquired in a changed

[043-152] voice.

[043-153] “Yes, godmother,” replied Bérangêre.

[043-154] It was impossible to doubt it; the child did not know how to

[043-155] write.

[043-156] “This is the secret!” thought Fleur-de-Lys.

[043-157] Meanwhile, at the child’s exclamation, all had hastened up, the

[043-158] mother, the young girls, the gypsy, and the officer.

[043-159] The gypsy beheld the piece of folly which the goat had committed.

[043-160] She turned red, then pale, and began to tremble like a culprit

[043-161] before the captain, who gazed at her with a smile of satisfaction

[043-162] and amazement.

[043-163] “Phœbus!” whispered the young girls, stupefied: “’tis the

[043-164] captain’s name!”

[043-165] “You have a marvellous memory!” said Fleur-de-Lys, to the

[043-166] petrified gypsy. Then, bursting into sobs: “Oh!” she stammered

[043-167] mournfully, hiding her face in both her beautiful hands, “she is

[043-168] a magician!” And she heard another and a still more bitter voice

[043-169] at the bottom of her heart, saying,—“She is a rival!”

[043-170] She fell fainting.

[043-171] “My daughter! my daughter!” cried the terrified mother. “Begone,

[043-172] you gypsy of hell!”

[043-173] In a twinkling, La Esmeralda gathered up the unlucky letters,

[043-174] made a sign to Djali, and went out through one door, while

[043-175] Fleur-de-Lys was being carried out through the other.

[043-176] Captain Phœbus, on being left alone, hesitated for a moment

[043-177] between the two doors, then he followed the gypsy.

[043-178] CHAPTER II. A PRIEST AND A PHILOSOPHER ARE TWO DIFFERENT THINGS.

[043-179] The priest whom the young girls had observed at the top of the

[043-180] North tower, leaning over the Place and so attentive to the dance

[043-181] of the gypsy, was, in fact, Archdeacon Claude Frollo.

[043-182] Our readers have not forgotten the mysterious cell which the

[043-183] archdeacon had reserved for himself in that tower. (I do not

[043-184] know, by the way be it said, whether it be not the same, the

[043-185] interior of which can be seen to-day through a little square

[043-186] window, opening to the east at the height of a man above the

[043-187] platform from which the towers spring; a bare and dilapidated

[043-188] den, whose badly plastered walls are ornamented here and there,

[043-189] at the present day, with some wretched yellow engravings

[043-190] representing the façades of cathedrals. I presume that this hole

[043-191] is jointly inhabited by bats and spiders, and that, consequently,

[043-192] it wages a double war of extermination on the flies).

[043-193] Every day, an hour before sunset, the archdeacon ascended the

[043-194] staircase to the tower, and shut himself up in this cell, where

[043-195] he sometimes passed whole nights. That day, at the moment when,

[043-196] standing before the low door of his retreat, he was fitting into

[043-197] the lock the complicated little key which he always carried about

[043-198] him in the purse suspended to his side, a sound of tambourine and

[043-199] castanets had reached his ear. These sounds came from the Place

[043-200] du Parvis. The cell, as we have already said, had only one window

[043-201] opening upon the rear of the church. Claude Frollo had hastily

[043-202] withdrawn the key, and an instant later, he was on the top of the

[043-203] tower, in the gloomy and pensive attitude in which the maidens

[043-204] had seen him.

[043-205] There he stood, grave, motionless, absorbed in one look and one

[043-206] thought. All Paris lay at his feet, with the thousand spires of

[043-207] its edifices and its circular horizon of gentle hills—with its

[043-208] river winding under its bridges, and its people moving to and fro

[043-209] through its streets,—with the clouds of its smoke,—with the

[043-210] mountainous chain of its roofs which presses Notre-Dame in its

[043-211] doubled folds; but out of all the city, the archdeacon gazed at

[043-212] one corner only of the pavement, the Place du Parvis; in all that

[043-213] throng at but one figure,—the gypsy.

[043-214] It would have been difficult to say what was the nature of this

[043-215] look, and whence proceeded the flame that flashed from it. It was

[043-216] a fixed gaze, which was, nevertheless, full of trouble and

[043-217] tumult. And, from the profound immobility of his whole body,

[043-218] barely agitated at intervals by an involuntary shiver, as a tree

[043-219] is moved by the wind; from the stiffness of his elbows, more

[043-220] marble than the balustrade on which they leaned; or the sight of

[043-221] the petrified smile which contracted his face,—one would have

[043-222] said that nothing living was left about Claude Frollo except his

[043-223] eyes.
