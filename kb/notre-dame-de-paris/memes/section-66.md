---[066-001] Quasimodo leaned over the Place, and saw that the object of this

[066-002] tender and agonizing prayer was a young man, a captain, a

[066-003] handsome cavalier all glittering with arms and decorations,

[066-004] prancing across the end of the Place, and saluting with his plume

[066-005] a beautiful lady who was smiling at him from her balcony.

[066-006] However, the officer did not hear the unhappy girl calling him;

[066-007] he was too far away.

[066-008] But the poor deaf man heard. A profound sigh heaved his breast;

[066-009] he turned round; his heart was swollen with all the tears which

[066-010] he was swallowing; his convulsively-clenched fists struck against

[066-011] his head, and when he withdrew them there was a bunch of red hair

[066-012] in each hand.

[066-013] The gypsy paid no heed to him. He said in a low voice as he

[066-014] gnashed his teeth,—

[066-015] “Damnation! That is what one should be like! ’Tis only necessary

[066-016] to be handsome on the outside!”

[066-017] Meanwhile, she remained kneeling, and cried with extraordinary

[066-018] agitation,—

[066-019] “Oh! there he is alighting from his horse! He is about to enter

[066-020] that house!—Phœbus!—He does not hear me! Phœbus!—How wicked that

[066-021] woman is to speak to him at the same time with me! Phœbus!

[066-022] Phœbus!”

[066-023] The deaf man gazed at her. He understood this pantomime. The poor

[066-024] bellringer’s eye filled with tears, but he let none fall. All at

[066-025] once he pulled her gently by the border of her sleeve. She turned

[066-026] round. He had assumed a tranquil air; he said to her,—

[066-027] “Would you like to have me bring him to you?”

[066-028] She uttered a cry of joy.

[066-029] “Oh! go! hasten! run! quick! that captain! that captain! bring

[066-030] him to me! I will love you for it!”

[066-031] She clasped his knees. He could not refrain from shaking his head

[066-032] sadly.

[066-033] “I will bring him to you,” he said, in a weak voice. Then he

[066-034] turned his head and plunged down the staircase with great

[066-035] strides, stifling with sobs.

[066-036] When he reached the Place, he no longer saw anything except the

[066-037] handsome horse hitched at the door of the Gondelaurier house; the

[066-038] captain had just entered there.

[066-039] He raised his eyes to the roof of the church. La Esmeralda was

[066-040] there in the same spot, in the same attitude. He made her a sad

[066-041] sign with his head; then he planted his back against one of the

[066-042] stone posts of the Gondelaurier porch, determined to wait until

[066-043] the captain should come forth.

[066-044] In the Gondelaurier house it was one of those gala days which

[066-045] precede a wedding. Quasimodo beheld many people enter, but no one

[066-046] come out. He cast a glance towards the roof from time to time;

[066-047] the gypsy did not stir any more than himself. A groom came and

[066-048] unhitched the horse and led it to the stable of the house.

[066-049] The entire day passed thus, Quasimodo at his post, la Esmeralda

[066-050] on the roof, Phœbus, no doubt, at the feet of Fleur-de-Lys.

[066-051] At length night came, a moonless night, a dark night. Quasimodo

[066-052] fixed his gaze in vain upon la Esmeralda; soon she was no more

[066-053] than a whiteness amid the twilight; then nothing. All was

[066-054] effaced, all was black.

[066-055] Quasimodo beheld the front windows from top to bottom of the

[066-056] Gondelaurier mansion illuminated; he saw the other casements in

[066-057] the Place lighted one by one, he also saw them extinguished to

[066-058] the very last, for he remained the whole evening at his post. The

[066-059] officer did not come forth. When the last passers-by had returned

[066-060] home, when the windows of all the other houses were extinguished,

[066-061] Quasimodo was left entirely alone, entirely in the dark. There

[066-062] were at that time no lamps in the square before Notre-Dame.

[066-063] Meanwhile, the windows of the Gondelaurier mansion remained

[066-064] lighted, even after midnight. Quasimodo, motionless and

[066-065] attentive, beheld a throng of lively, dancing shadows pass

[066-066] athwart the many-colored painted panes. Had he not been deaf, he

[066-067] would have heard more and more distinctly, in proportion as the

[066-068] noise of sleeping Paris died away, a sound of feasting, laughter,

[066-069] and music in the Gondelaurier mansion.

[066-070] Towards one o’clock in the morning, the guests began to take

[066-071] their leave. Quasimodo, shrouded in darkness watched them all

[066-072] pass out through the porch illuminated with torches. None of them

[066-073] was the captain.

[066-074] He was filled with sad thoughts; at times he looked upwards into

[066-075] the air, like a person who is weary of waiting. Great black

[066-076] clouds, heavy, torn, split, hung like crape hammocks beneath the

[066-077] starry dome of night. One would have pronounced them spiders’

[066-078] webs of the vault of heaven.

[066-079] In one of these moments he suddenly beheld the long window on the

[066-080] balcony, whose stone balustrade projected above his head, open

[066-081] mysteriously. The frail glass door gave passage to two persons,

[066-082] and closed noiselessly behind them; it was a man and a woman.

[066-083] It was not without difficulty that Quasimodo succeeded in

[066-084] recognizing in the man the handsome captain, in the woman the

[066-085] young lady whom he had seen welcome the officer in the morning

[066-086] from that very balcony. The place was perfectly dark, and a

[066-087] double crimson curtain which had fallen across the door the very

[066-088] moment it closed again, allowed no light to reach the balcony

[066-089] from the apartment.

[066-090] The young man and the young girl, so far as our deaf man could

[066-091] judge, without hearing a single one of their words, appeared to

[066-092] abandon themselves to a very tender tête-à-tête. The young girl

[066-093] seemed to have allowed the officer to make a girdle for her of

[066-094] his arm, and gently repulsed a kiss.

[066-095] Quasimodo looked on from below at this scene which was all the

[066-096] more pleasing to witness because it was not meant to be seen. He

[066-097] contemplated with bitterness that beauty, that happiness. After

[066-098] all, nature was not dumb in the poor fellow, and his human

[066-099] sensibility, all maliciously contorted as it was, quivered no

[066-100] less than any other. He thought of the miserable portion which

[066-101] Providence had allotted to him; that woman and the pleasure of

[066-102] love, would pass forever before his eyes, and that he should

[066-103] never do anything but behold the felicity of others. But that

[066-104] which rent his heart most in this sight, that which mingled

[066-105] indignation with his anger, was the thought of what the gypsy

[066-106] would suffer could she behold it. It is true that the night was

[066-107] very dark, that la Esmeralda, if she had remained at her post

[066-108] (and he had no doubt of this), was very far away, and that it was

[066-109] all that he himself could do to distinguish the lovers on the

[066-110] balcony. This consoled him.

[066-111] Meanwhile, their conversation grew more and more animated. The

[066-112] young lady appeared to be entreating the officer to ask nothing

[066-113] more of her. Of all this Quasimodo could distinguish only the

[066-114] beautiful clasped hands, the smiles mingled with tears, the young

[066-115] girl’s glances directed to the stars, the eyes of the captain

[066-116] lowered ardently upon her.

[066-117] Fortunately, for the young girl was beginning to resist but

[066-118] feebly, the door of the balcony suddenly opened once more and an

[066-119] old dame appeared; the beauty seemed confused, the officer

[066-120] assumed an air of displeasure, and all three withdrew.

[066-121] A moment later, a horse was champing his bit under the porch, and

[066-122] the brilliant officer, enveloped in his night cloak, passed

[066-123] rapidly before Quasimodo.

[066-124] The bellringer allowed him to turn the corner of the street, then

[066-125] he ran after him with his ape-like agility, shouting: “Hey there!

[066-126] captain!”

[066-127] The captain halted.

[066-128] “What wants this knave with me?” he said, catching sight through

[066-129] the gloom of that hipshot form which ran limping after him.

[066-130] Meanwhile, Quasimodo had caught up with him, and had boldly

[066-131] grasped his horse’s bridle: “Follow me, captain; there is one

[066-132] here who desires to speak with you!

[066-133] “_Cornemahom_!” grumbled Phœbus, “here’s a villanous; ruffled

[066-134] bird which I fancy I have seen somewhere. Holà master, will you

[066-135] let my horse’s bridle alone?”

[066-136] “Captain,” replied the deaf man, “do you not ask me who it is?”

[066-137] “I tell you to release my horse,” retorted Phœbus, impatiently.

[066-138] “What means the knave by clinging to the bridle of my steed? Do

[066-139] you take my horse for a gallows?”

[066-140] Quasimodo, far from releasing the bridle, prepared to force him

[066-141] to retrace his steps. Unable to comprehend the captain’s

[066-142] resistance, he hastened to say to him,—

[066-143] “Come, captain, ’tis a woman who is waiting for you.” He added

[066-144] with an effort: “A woman who loves you.”

[066-145] “A rare rascal!” said the captain, “who thinks me obliged to go

[066-146] to all the women who love me! or who say they do. And what if, by

[066-147] chance, she should resemble you, you face of a screech-owl? Tell

[066-148] the woman who has sent you that I am about to marry, and that she

[066-149] may go to the devil!”

[066-150] “Listen,” exclaimed Quasimodo, thinking to overcome his

[066-151] hesitation with a word, “come, monseigneur! ’tis the gypsy whom

[066-152] you know!”

[066-153] This word did, indeed, produce a great effect on Phœbus, but not

[066-154] of the kind which the deaf man expected. It will be remembered

[066-155] that our gallant officer had retired with Fleur-de-Lys several

[066-156] moments before Quasimodo had rescued the condemned girl from the

[066-157] hands of Charmolue. Afterwards, in all his visits to the

[066-158] Gondelaurier mansion he had taken care not to mention that woman,

[066-159] the memory of whom was, after all, painful to him; and on her

[066-160] side, Fleur-de-Lys had not deemed it politic to tell him that the

[066-161] gypsy was alive. Hence Phœbus believed poor “Similar” to be dead,

[066-162] and that a month or two had elapsed since her death. Let us add

[066-163] that for the last few moments the captain had been reflecting on

[066-164] the profound darkness of the night, the supernatural ugliness,

[066-165] the sepulchral voice of the strange messenger; that it was past

[066-166] midnight; that the street was deserted, as on the evening when

[066-167] the surly monk had accosted him; and that his horse snorted as it

[066-168] looked at Quasimodo.

[066-169] “The gypsy!” he exclaimed, almost frightened. “Look here, do you

[066-170] come from the other world?”

[066-171] And he laid his hand on the hilt of his dagger.

[066-172] “Quick, quick,” said the deaf man, endeavoring to drag the horse

[066-173] along; “this way!”

[066-174] Phœbus dealt him a vigorous kick in the breast.

[066-175] Quasimodo’s eye flashed. He made a motion to fling himself on the

[066-176] captain. Then he drew himself up stiffly and said,—

[066-177] “Oh! how happy you are to have some one who loves you!”

[066-178] He emphasized the words “some one,” and loosing the horse’s

[066-179] bridle,—

[066-180] “Begone!”

[066-181] Phœbus spurred on in all haste, swearing. Quasimodo watched him

[066-182] disappear in the shades of the street.

[066-183] “Oh!” said the poor deaf man, in a very low voice; “to refuse

[066-184] that!”

[066-185] He re-entered Notre-Dame, lighted his lamp and climbed to the

[066-186] tower again. The gypsy was still in the same place, as he had

[066-187] supposed.

[066-188] She flew to meet him as far off as she could see him. “Alone!”

[066-189] she cried, clasping her beautiful hands sorrowfully.

[066-190] “I could not find him,” said Quasimodo coldly.

[066-191] “You should have waited all night,” she said angrily.

[066-192] He saw her gesture of wrath, and understood the reproach.

[066-193] “I will lie in wait for him better another time,” he said,

[066-194] dropping his head.

[066-195] “Begone!” she said to him.

[066-196] He left her. She was displeased with him. He preferred to have

[066-197] her abuse him rather than to have afflicted her. He had kept all

[066-198] the pain to himself.

[066-199] From that day forth, the gypsy no longer saw him. He ceased to

[066-200] come to her cell. At the most she occasionally caught a glimpse

[066-201] at the summit of the towers, of the bellringer’s face turned

[066-202] sadly to her. But as soon as she perceived him, he disappeared.

[066-203] We must admit that she was not much grieved by this voluntary

[066-204] absence on the part of the poor hunchback. At the bottom of her

[066-205] heart she was grateful to him for it. Moreover, Quasimodo did not

[066-206] deceive himself on this point.
