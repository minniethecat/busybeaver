---[025-001] “A fine, flaming fagot,” added the old woman.

[025-002] “It would be more prudent,” said Mistricolle.

[025-003] For several minutes, a young priest had been listening to the

[025-004] reasoning of the Haudriettes and the sentences of the notary. He

[025-005] had a severe face, with a large brow, a profound glance. He

[025-006] thrust the crowd silently aside, scrutinized the “little

[025-007] magician,” and stretched out his hand upon him. It was high time,

[025-008] for all the devotees were already licking their chops over the

[025-009] “fine, flaming fagot.”

[025-010] “I adopt this child,” said the priest.

[025-011] He took it in his cassock and carried it off. The spectators

[025-012] followed him with frightened glances. A moment later, he had

[025-013] disappeared through the “Red Door,” which then led from the

[025-014] church to the cloister.

[025-015] When the first surprise was over, Jehanne de la Tarme bent down

[025-016] to the ear of la Gaultière,—

[025-017] “I told you so, sister,—that young clerk, Monsieur Claude Frollo,

[025-018] is a sorcerer.”

[025-019] CHAPTER II. CLAUDE FROLLO.

[025-020] In fact, Claude Frollo was no common person.

[025-021] He belonged to one of those middle-class families which were

[025-022] called indifferently, in the impertinent language of the last

[025-023] century, the high _bourgeoise_ or the petty nobility. This family

[025-024] had inherited from the brothers Paclet the fief of Tirechappe,

[025-025] which was dependent upon the Bishop of Paris, and whose

[025-026] twenty-one houses had been in the thirteenth century the object

[025-027] of so many suits before the official. As possessor of this fief,

[025-028] Claude Frollo was one of the twenty-seven seigneurs keeping claim

[025-029] to a manor in fee in Paris and its suburbs; and for a long time,

[025-030] his name was to be seen inscribed in this quality, between the

[025-031] Hôtel de Tancarville, belonging to Master François Le Rez, and

[025-032] the college of Tours, in the records deposited at Saint Martin

[025-033] des Champs.

[025-034] Claude Frollo had been destined from infancy, by his parents, to

[025-035] the ecclesiastical profession. He had been taught to read in

[025-036] Latin; he had been trained to keep his eyes on the ground and to

[025-037] speak low. While still a child, his father had cloistered him in

[025-038] the college of Torchi in the University. There it was that he had

[025-039] grown up, on the missal and the lexicon.

[025-040] Moreover, he was a sad, grave, serious child, who studied

[025-041] ardently, and learned quickly; he never uttered a loud cry in

[025-042] recreation hour, mixed but little in the bacchanals of the Rue du

[025-043] Fouarre, did not know what it was to _dare alapas et capillos

[025-044] laniare_, and had cut no figure in that revolt of 1463, which the

[025-045] annalists register gravely, under the title of “The sixth trouble

[025-046] of the University.” He seldom rallied the poor students of

[025-047] Montaigu on the _cappettes_ from which they derived their name,

[025-048] or the bursars of the college of Dormans on their shaved tonsure,

[025-049] and their surtout parti-colored of bluish-green, blue, and violet

[025-050] cloth, _azurini coloris et bruni_, as says the charter of the

[025-051] Cardinal des Quatre-Couronnes.

[025-052] On the other hand, he was assiduous at the great and the small

[025-053] schools of the Rue Saint Jean de Beauvais. The first pupil whom

[025-054] the Abbé de Saint Pierre de Val, at the moment of beginning his

[025-055] reading on canon law, always perceived, glued to a pillar of the

[025-056] school Saint-Vendregesile, opposite his rostrum, was Claude

[025-057] Frollo, armed with his horn ink-bottle, biting his pen,

[025-058] scribbling on his threadbare knee, and, in winter, blowing on his

[025-059] fingers. The first auditor whom Messire Miles d’Isliers, doctor

[025-060] in decretals, saw arrive every Monday morning, all breathless, at

[025-061] the opening of the gates of the school of the Chef-Saint-Denis,

[025-062] was Claude Frollo. Thus, at sixteen years of age, the young clerk

[025-063] might have held his own, in mystical theology, against a father

[025-064] of the church; in canonical theology, against a father of the

[025-065] councils; in scholastic theology, against a doctor of Sorbonne.

[025-066] Theology conquered, he had plunged into decretals. From the

[025-067] “Master of Sentences,” he had passed to the “Capitularies of

[025-068] Charlemagne;” and he had devoured in succession, in his appetite

[025-069] for science, decretals upon decretals, those of Theodore, Bishop

[025-070] of Hispalus; those of Bouchard, Bishop of Worms; those of Yves,

[025-071] Bishop of Chartres; next the decretal of Gratian, which succeeded

[025-072] the capitularies of Charlemagne; then the collection of Gregory

[025-073] IX.; then the Epistle of _Superspecula_, of Honorius III. He

[025-074] rendered clear and familiar to himself that vast and tumultuous

[025-075] period of civil law and canon law in conflict and at strife with

[025-076] each other, in the chaos of the Middle Ages,—a period which

[025-077] Bishop Theodore opens in 618, and which Pope Gregory closes in

[025-078] 1227.

[025-079] Decretals digested, he flung himself upon medicine, on the

[025-080] liberal arts. He studied the science of herbs, the science of

[025-081] unguents; he became an expert in fevers and in contusions, in

[025-082] sprains and abcesses. Jacques d’ Espars would have received him

[025-083] as a physician; Richard Hellain, as a surgeon. He also passed

[025-084] through all the degrees of licentiate, master, and doctor of

[025-085] arts. He studied the languages, Latin, Greek, Hebrew, a triple

[025-086] sanctuary then very little frequented. His was a veritable fever

[025-087] for acquiring and hoarding, in the matter of science. At the age

[025-088] of eighteen, he had made his way through the four faculties; it

[025-089] seemed to the young man that life had but one sole object:

[025-090] learning.

[025-091] It was towards this epoch, that the excessive heat of the summer

[025-092] of 1466 caused that grand outburst of the plague which carried

[025-093] off more than forty thousand souls in the vicomty of Paris, and

[025-094] among others, as Jean de Troyes states, “Master Arnoul,

[025-095] astrologer to the king, who was a very fine man, both wise and

[025-096] pleasant.” The rumor spread in the University that the Rue

[025-097] Tirechappe was especially devastated by the malady. It was there

[025-098] that Claude’s parents resided, in the midst of their fief. The

[025-099] young scholar rushed in great alarm to the paternal mansion. When

[025-100] he entered it, he found that both father and mother had died on

[025-101] the preceding day. A very young brother of his, who was in

[025-102] swaddling clothes, was still alive and crying abandoned in his

[025-103] cradle. This was all that remained to Claude of his family; the

[025-104] young man took the child under his arm and went off in a pensive

[025-105] mood. Up to that moment, he had lived only in science; he now

[025-106] began to live in life.

[025-107] This catastrophe was a crisis in Claude’s existence. Orphaned,

[025-108] the eldest, head of the family at the age of nineteen, he felt

[025-109] himself rudely recalled from the reveries of school to the

[025-110] realities of this world. Then, moved with pity, he was seized

[025-111] with passion and devotion towards that child, his brother; a

[025-112] sweet and strange thing was a human affection to him, who had

[025-113] hitherto loved his books alone.

[025-114] This affection developed to a singular point; in a soul so new,

[025-115] it was like a first love. Separated since infancy from his

[025-116] parents, whom he had hardly known; cloistered and immured, as it

[025-117] were, in his books; eager above all things to study and to learn;

[025-118] exclusively attentive up to that time, to his intelligence which

[025-119] broadened in science, to his imagination, which expanded in

[025-120] letters,—the poor scholar had not yet had time to feel the place

[025-121] of his heart.

[025-122] This young brother, without mother or father, this little child

[025-123] which had fallen abruptly from heaven into his arms, made a new

[025-124] man of him. He perceived that there was something else in the

[025-125] world besides the speculations of the Sorbonne, and the verses of

[025-126] Homer; that man needed affections; that life without tenderness

[025-127] and without love was only a set of dry, shrieking, and rending

[025-128] wheels. Only, he imagined, for he was at the age when illusions

[025-129] are as yet replaced only by illusions, that the affections of

[025-130] blood and family were the sole ones necessary, and that a little

[025-131] brother to love sufficed to fill an entire existence.

[025-132] He threw himself, therefore, into the love for his little Jehan

[025-133] with the passion of a character already profound, ardent,

[025-134] concentrated; that poor frail creature, pretty, fair-haired,

[025-135] rosy, and curly,—that orphan with another orphan for his only

[025-136] support, touched him to the bottom of his heart; and grave

[025-137] thinker as he was, he set to meditating upon Jehan with an

[025-138] infinite compassion. He kept watch and ward over him as over

[025-139] something very fragile, and very worthy of care. He was more than

[025-140] a brother to the child; he became a mother to him.

[025-141] Little Jehan had lost his mother while he was still at the

[025-142] breast; Claude gave him to a nurse. Besides the fief of

[025-143] Tirechappe, he had inherited from his father the fief of Moulin,

[025-144] which was a dependency of the square tower of Gentilly; it was a

[025-145] mill on a hill, near the château of Winchestre (Bicêtre). There

[025-146] was a miller’s wife there who was nursing a fine child; it was

[025-147] not far from the university, and Claude carried the little Jehan

[025-148] to her in his own arms.

[025-149] From that time forth, feeling that he had a burden to bear, he

[025-150] took life very seriously. The thought of his little brother

[025-151] became not only his recreation, but the object of his studies. He

[025-152] resolved to consecrate himself entirely to a future for which he

[025-153] was responsible in the sight of God, and never to have any other

[025-154] wife, any other child than the happiness and fortune of his

[025-155] brother. Therefore, he attached himself more closely than ever to

[025-156] the clerical profession. His merits, his learning, his quality of

[025-157] immediate vassal of the Bishop of Paris, threw the doors of the

[025-158] church wide open to him. At the age of twenty, by special

[025-159] dispensation of the Holy See, he was a priest, and served as the

[025-160] youngest of the chaplains of Notre-Dame the altar which is

[025-161] called, because of the late mass which is said there, _altare

[025-162] pigrorum_.

[025-163] There, plunged more deeply than ever in his dear books, which he

[025-164] quitted only to run for an hour to the fief of Moulin, this

[025-165] mixture of learning and austerity, so rare at his age, had

[025-166] promptly acquired for him the respect and admiration of the

[025-167] monastery. From the cloister, his reputation as a learned man had

[025-168] passed to the people, among whom it had changed a little, a

[025-169] frequent occurrence at that time, into reputation as a sorcerer.

[025-170] It was at the moment when he was returning, on Quasimodo day,

[025-171] from saying his mass at the Altar of the Lazy, which was by the

[025-172] side of the door leading to the nave on the right, near the image

[025-173] of the Virgin, that his attention had been attracted by the group

[025-174] of old women chattering around the bed for foundlings.

[025-175] Then it was that he approached the unhappy little creature, which

[025-176] was so hated and so menaced. That distress, that deformity, that

[025-177] abandonment, the thought of his young brother, the idea which

[025-178] suddenly occurred to him, that if he were to die, his dear little

[025-179] Jehan might also be flung miserably on the plank for

[025-180] foundlings,—all this had gone to his heart simultaneously; a

[025-181] great pity had moved in him, and he had carried off the child.

[025-182] When he removed the child from the sack, he found it greatly

[025-183] deformed, in very sooth. The poor little wretch had a wart on his

[025-184] left eye, his head placed directly on his shoulders, his spinal

[025-185] column was crooked, his breast bone prominent, and his legs

[025-186] bowed; but he appeared to be lively; and although it was

[025-187] impossible to say in what language he lisped, his cry indicated

[025-188] considerable force and health. Claude’s compassion increased at

[025-189] the sight of this ugliness; and he made a vow in his heart to

[025-190] rear the child for the love of his brother, in order that,

[025-191] whatever might be the future faults of the little Jehan, he

[025-192] should have beside him that charity done for his sake. It was a

[025-193] sort of investment of good works, which he was effecting in the

[025-194] name of his young brother; it was a stock of good works which he

[025-195] wished to amass in advance for him, in case the little rogue

[025-196] should some day find himself short of that coin, the only sort

[025-197] which is received at the toll-bar of paradise.
