---[085-001] “Through the Rue du Mouton, I believe.”

[085-002] Tristan turned his head and made a sign to his troop to prepare

[085-003] to set out on the march again. The recluse breathed freely once

[085-004] more.

[085-005] “Monseigneur,” suddenly said an archer, “ask the old elf why the

[085-006] bars of her window are broken in this manner.”

[085-007] This question brought anguish again to the heart of the miserable

[085-008] mother. Nevertheless, she did not lose all presence of mind.

[085-009] “They have always been thus,” she stammered.

[085-010] “Bah!” retorted the archer, “only yesterday they still formed a

[085-011] fine black cross, which inspired devotion.”

[085-012] Tristan cast a sidelong glance at the recluse.

[085-013] “I think the old dame is getting confused!”

[085-014] The unfortunate woman felt that all depended on her

[085-015] self-possession, and, although with death in her soul, she began

[085-016] to grin. Mothers possess such strength.

[085-017] “Bah!” said she, “the man is drunk. ’Tis more than a year since

[085-018] the tail of a stone cart dashed against my window and broke in

[085-019] the grating. And how I cursed the carter, too.”

[085-020] “’Tis true,” said another archer, “I was there.”

[085-021] Always and everywhere people are to be found who have seen

[085-022] everything. This unexpected testimony from the archer

[085-023] re-encouraged the recluse, whom this interrogatory was forcing to

[085-024] cross an abyss on the edge of a knife. But she was condemned to a

[085-025] perpetual alternative of hope and alarm.

[085-026] “If it was a cart which did it,” retorted the first soldier, “the

[085-027] stumps of the bars should be thrust inwards, while they actually

[085-028] are pushed outwards.”

[085-029] “Hé! hé!” said Tristan to the soldier, “you have the nose of an

[085-030] inquisitor of the Châtelet. Reply to what he says, old woman.”

[085-031] “Good heavens!” she exclaimed, driven to bay, and in a voice that

[085-032] was full of tears in despite of her efforts, “I swear to you,

[085-033] monseigneur, that ’twas a cart which broke those bars. You hear

[085-034] the man who saw it. And then, what has that to do with your

[085-035] gypsy?”

[085-036] “Hum!” growled Tristan.

[085-037] “The devil!” went on the soldier, flattered by the provost’s

[085-038] praise, “these fractures of the iron are perfectly fresh.”

[085-039] Tristan tossed his head. She turned pale.

[085-040] “How long ago, say you, did the cart do it?”

[085-041] “A month, a fortnight, perhaps, monseigneur, I know not.”

[085-042] “She first said more than a year,” observed the soldier.

[085-043] “That is suspicious,” said the provost.

[085-044] “Monseigneur!” she cried, still pressed against the opening, and

[085-045] trembling lest suspicion should lead them to thrust their heads

[085-046] through and look into her cell; “monseigneur, I swear to you that

[085-047] ’twas a cart which broke this grating. I swear it to you by the

[085-048] angels of paradise. If it was not a cart, may I be eternally

[085-049] damned, and I reject God!”

[085-050] “You put a great deal of heat into that oath;” said Tristan, with

[085-051] his inquisitorial glance.

[085-052] The poor woman felt her assurance vanishing more and more. She

[085-053] had reached the point of blundering, and she comprehended with

[085-054] terror that she was saying what she ought not to have said.

[085-055] Here another soldier came up, crying,—

[085-056] “Monsieur, the old hag lies. The sorceress did not flee through

[085-057] the Rue de Mouton. The street chain has remained stretched all

[085-058] night, and the chain guard has seen no one pass.”

[085-059] Tristan, whose face became more sinister with every moment,

[085-060] addressed the recluse,—

[085-061] “What have you to say to that?”

[085-062] She tried to make head against this new incident,

[085-063] “That I do not know, monseigneur; that I may have been mistaken.

[085-064] I believe, in fact, that she crossed the water.”

[085-065] “That is in the opposite direction,” said the provost, “and it is

[085-066] not very likely that she would wish to re-enter the city, where

[085-067] she was being pursued. You are lying, old woman.”

[085-068] “And then,” added the first soldier, “there is no boat either on

[085-069] this side of the stream or on the other.”

[085-070] “She swam across,” replied the recluse, defending her ground foot

[085-071] by foot.

[085-072] “Do women swim?” said the soldier.

[085-073] “_Tête Dieu!_ old woman! You are lying!” repeated Tristan

[085-074] angrily. “I have a good mind to abandon that sorceress and take

[085-075] you. A quarter of an hour of torture will, perchance, draw the

[085-076] truth from your throat. Come! You are to follow us.”

[085-077] She seized on these words with avidity.

[085-078] “As you please, monseigneur. Do it. Do it. Torture. I am willing.

[085-079] Take me away. Quick, quick! let us set out at once!—During that

[085-080] time,” she said to herself, “my daughter will make her escape.”

[085-081] “’S death!” said the provost, “what an appetite for the rack! I

[085-082] understand not this madwoman at all.”

[085-083] An old, gray-haired sergeant of the guard stepped out of the

[085-084] ranks, and addressing the provost,—

[085-085] “Mad in sooth, monseigneur. If she released the gypsy, it was not

[085-086] her fault, for she loves not the gypsies. I have been of the

[085-087] watch these fifteen years, and I hear her every evening cursing

[085-088] the Bohemian women with endless imprecations. If the one of whom

[085-089] we are in pursuit is, as I suppose, the little dancer with the

[085-090] goat, she detests that one above all the rest.”

[085-091] Gudule made an effort and said,—

[085-092] “That one above all.”

[085-093] The unanimous testimony of the men of the watch confirmed the old

[085-094] sergeant’s words to the provost. Tristan l’Hermite, in despair at

[085-095] extracting anything from the recluse, turned his back on her, and

[085-096] with unspeakable anxiety she beheld him direct his course slowly

[085-097] towards his horse.

[085-098] “Come!” he said, between his teeth, “March on! let us set out

[085-099] again on the quest. I shall not sleep until that gypsy is

[085-100] hanged.”

[085-101] But he still hesitated for some time before mounting his horse.

[085-102] Gudule palpitated between life and death, as she beheld him cast

[085-103] about the Place that uneasy look of a hunting dog which

[085-104] instinctively feels that the lair of the beast is close to him,

[085-105] and is loath to go away. At length he shook his head and leaped

[085-106] into his saddle. Gudule’s horribly compressed heart now dilated,

[085-107] and she said in a low voice, as she cast a glance at her

[085-108] daughter, whom she had not ventured to look at while they were

[085-109] there, “Saved!”

[085-110] The poor child had remained all this time in her corner, without

[085-111] breathing, without moving, with the idea of death before her. She

[085-112] had lost nothing of the scene between Gudule and Tristan, and the

[085-113] anguish of her mother had found its echo in her heart. She had

[085-114] heard all the successive snappings of the thread by which she

[085-115] hung suspended over the gulf; twenty times she had fancied that

[085-116] she saw it break, and at last she began to breathe again and to

[085-117] feel her foot on firm ground. At that moment she heard a voice

[085-118] saying to the provost: “_Corbœuf!_ Monsieur le Prevôt, ’tis no

[085-119] affair of mine, a man of arms, to hang witches. The rabble of the

[085-120] populace is suppressed. I leave you to attend to the matter

[085-121] alone. You will allow me to rejoin my company, who are waiting

[085-122] for their captain.”

[085-123] The voice was that of Phœbus de Châteaupers; that which took

[085-124] place within her was ineffable. He was there, her friend, her

[085-125] protector, her support, her refuge, her Phœbus. She rose, and

[085-126] before her mother could prevent her, she had rushed to the

[085-127] window, crying,—

[085-128] “Phœbus! aid me, my Phœbus!”

[085-129] Phœbus was no longer there. He had just turned the corner of the

[085-130] Rue de la Coutellerie at a gallop. But Tristan had not yet taken

[085-131] his departure.

[085-132] The recluse rushed upon her daughter with a roar of agony. She

[085-133] dragged her violently back, digging her nails into her neck. A

[085-134] tigress mother does not stand on trifles. But it was too late.

[085-135] Tristan had seen.

[085-136] “Hé! hé!” he exclaimed with a laugh which laid bare all his teeth

[085-137] and made his face resemble the muzzle of a wolf, “two mice in the

[085-138] trap!”

[085-139] “I suspected as much,” said the soldier.

[085-140] Tristan clapped him on the shoulder,—

[085-141] “You are a good cat! Come!” he added, “where is Henriet Cousin?”

[085-142] A man who had neither the garments nor the air of a soldier,

[085-143] stepped from the ranks. He wore a costume half gray, half brown,

[085-144] flat hair, leather sleeves, and carried a bundle of ropes in his

[085-145] huge hand. This man always attended Tristan, who always attended

[085-146] Louis XI. “Friend,” said Tristan l’Hermite, “I presume that this

[085-147] is the sorceress of whom we are in search. You will hang me this

[085-148] one. Have you your ladder?”

[085-149] “There is one yonder, under the shed of the Pillar-House,”

[085-150] replied the man. “Is it on this justice that the thing is to be

[085-151] done?” he added, pointing to the stone gibbet.

[085-152] “Yes.”

[085-153] “Ho, hé!” continued the man with a huge laugh, which was still

[085-154] more brutal than that of the provost, “we shall not have far to

[085-155] go.”

[085-156] “Make haste!” said Tristan, “you shall laugh afterwards.”

[085-157] In the meantime, the recluse had not uttered another word since

[085-158] Tristan had seen her daughter and all hope was lost. She had

[085-159] flung the poor gypsy, half dead, into the corner of the cellar,

[085-160] and had placed herself once more at the window with both hands

[085-161] resting on the angle of the sill like two claws. In this attitude

[085-162] she was seen to cast upon all those soldiers her glance which had

[085-163] become wild and frantic once more. At the moment when Rennet

[085-164] Cousin approached her cell, she showed him so savage a face that

[085-165] he shrank back.

[085-166] “Monseigneur,” he said, returning to the provost, “which am I to

[085-167] take?”

[085-168] “The young one.”

[085-169] “So much the better, for the old one seemeth difficult.”

[085-170] “Poor little dancer with the goat!” said the old sergeant of the

[085-171] watch.

[085-172] Rennet Cousin approached the window again. The mother’s eyes made

[085-173] his own droop. He said with a good deal of timidity,—

[085-174] “Madam”—

[085-175] She interrupted him in a very low but furious voice,—

[085-176] “What do you ask?”

[085-177] “It is not you,” he said, “it is the other.”

[085-178] “What other?”

[085-179] “The young one.”

[085-180] She began to shake her head, crying,—

[085-181] “There is no one! there is no one! there is no one!”

[085-182] “Yes, there is!” retorted the hangman, “and you know it well. Let

[085-183] me take the young one. I have no wish to harm you.”

[085-184] She said, with a strange sneer,—

[085-185] “Ah! so you have no wish to harm me!”

[085-186] “Let me have the other, madam; ’tis monsieur the provost who

[085-187] wills it.”

[085-188] She repeated with a look of madness,—

[085-189] “There is no one here.”

[085-190] “I tell you that there is!” replied the executioner. “We have all

[085-191] seen that there are two of you.”

[085-192] “Look then!” said the recluse, with a sneer. “Thrust your head

[085-193] through the window.”

[085-194] The executioner observed the mother’s finger-nails and dared not.

[085-195] “Make haste!” shouted Tristan, who had just ranged his troops in

[085-196] a circle round the Rat-Hole, and who sat on his horse beside the

[085-197] gallows.

[085-198] Rennet returned once more to the provost in great embarrassment.

[085-199] He had flung his rope on the ground, and was twisting his hat

[085-200] between his hands with an awkward air.

[085-201] “Monseigneur,” he asked, “where am I to enter?”

[085-202] “By the door.”

[085-203] “There is none.”

[085-204] “By the window.”

[085-205] “’Tis too small.”

[085-206] “Make it larger,” said Tristan angrily. “Have you not pickaxes?”

[085-207] The mother still looked on steadfastly from the depths of her

[085-208] cavern. She no longer hoped for anything, she no longer knew what

[085-209] she wished, except that she did not wish them to take her

[085-210] daughter.

[085-211] Rennet Cousin went in search of the chest of tools for the night

[085-212] man, under the shed of the Pillar-House. He drew from it also the

[085-213] double ladder, which he immediately set up against the gallows.

[085-214] Five or six of the provost’s men armed themselves with picks and

[085-215] crowbars, and Tristan betook himself, in company with them,

[085-216] towards the window.

[085-217] “Old woman,” said the provost, in a severe tone, “deliver up to

[085-218] us that girl quietly.”
