---[053-001] The hall was vast and gloomy, which latter fact made it appear

[053-002] still more spacious. The day was declining; the long, pointed

[053-003] windows permitted only a pale ray of light to enter, which was

[053-004] extinguished before it reached the vaulted ceiling, an enormous

[053-005] trellis-work of sculptured beams, whose thousand figures seemed

[053-006] to move confusedly in the shadows, many candles were already

[053-007] lighted here and there on tables, and beaming on the heads of

[053-008] clerks buried in masses of documents. The anterior portion of the

[053-009] hall was occupied by the crowd; on the right and left were

[053-010] magistrates and tables; at the end, upon a platform, a number of

[053-011] judges, whose rear rank sank into the shadows, sinister and

[053-012] motionless faces. The walls were sown with innumerable

[053-013] fleurs-de-lis. A large figure of Christ might be vaguely descried

[053-014] above the judges, and everywhere there were pikes and halberds,

[053-015] upon whose points the reflection of the candles placed tips of

[053-016] fire.

[053-017] “Monsieur,” Gringoire inquired of one of his neighbors, “who are

[053-018] all those persons ranged yonder, like prelates in council?”

[053-019] “Monsieur,” replied the neighbor, “those on the right are the

[053-020] counsellors of the grand chamber; those on the left, the

[053-021] councillors of inquiry; the masters in black gowns, the messires

[053-022] in red.”

[053-023] “Who is that big red fellow, yonder above them, who is sweating?”

[053-024] pursued Gringoire.

[053-025] “It is monsieur the president.”

[053-026] “And those sheep behind him?” continued Gringoire, who as we have

[053-027] seen, did not love the magistracy, which arose, possibly, from

[053-028] the grudge which he cherished against the Palais de Justice since

[053-029] his dramatic misadventure.

[053-030] “They are messieurs the masters of requests of the king’s

[053-031] household.”

[053-032] “And that boar in front of him?”

[053-033] “He is monsieur the clerk of the Court of Parliament.”

[053-034] “And that crocodile on the right?”

[053-035] “Master Philippe Lheulier, advocate extraordinary of the king.”

[053-036] “And that big, black tom-cat on the left?”

[053-037] “Master Jacques Charmolue, procurator of the king in the

[053-038] Ecclesiastical Court, with the gentlemen of the officialty.”

[053-039] “Come now, monsieur,” said Gringoire, “pray what are all those

[053-040] fine fellows doing yonder?”

[053-041] “They are judging.”

[053-042] “Judging whom? I do not see the accused.”

[053-043] “’Tis a woman, sir. You cannot see her. She has her back turned

[053-044] to us, and she is hidden from us by the crowd. Stay, yonder she

[053-045] is, where you see a group of partisans.”

[053-046] “Who is the woman?” asked Gringoire. “Do you know her name?”

[053-047] “No, monsieur, I have but just arrived. I merely assume that

[053-048] there is some sorcery about it, since the official is present at

[053-049] the trial.”

[053-050] “Come!” said our philosopher, “we are going to see all these

[053-051] magistrates devour human flesh. ’Tis as good a spectacle as any

[053-052] other.”

[053-053] “Monsieur,” remarked his neighbor, “think you not, that Master

[053-054] Jacques Charmolue has a very sweet air?”

[053-055] “Hum!” replied Gringoire. “I distrust a sweetness which hath

[053-056] pinched nostrils and thin lips.”

[053-057] Here the bystanders imposed silence upon the two chatterers. They

[053-058] were listening to an important deposition.

[053-059] “Messeigneurs,” said an old woman in the middle of the hall,

[053-060] whose form was so concealed beneath her garments that one would

[053-061] have pronounced her a walking heap of rags; “Messeigneurs, the

[053-062] thing is as true as that I am la Falourdel, established these

[053-063] forty years at the Pont Saint Michel, and paying regularly my

[053-064] rents, lord’s dues, and quit rents; at the gate opposite the

[053-065] house of Tassin-Caillart, the dyer, which is on the side up the

[053-066] river—a poor old woman now, but a pretty maid in former days, my

[053-067] lords. Some one said to me lately, ‘La Falourdel, don’t use your

[053-068] spinning-wheel too much in the evening; the devil is fond of

[053-069] combing the distaffs of old women with his horns. ’Tis certain

[053-070] that the surly monk who was round about the temple last year, now

[053-071] prowls in the City. Take care, La Falourdel, that he doth not

[053-072] knock at your door.’ One evening I was spinning on my wheel,

[053-073] there comes a knock at my door; I ask who it is. They swear. I

[053-074] open. Two men enter. A man in black and a handsome officer. Of

[053-075] the black man nothing could be seen but his eyes, two coals of

[053-076] fire. All the rest was hat and cloak. They say to me,—‘The

[053-077] Sainte-Marthe chamber.’—’Tis my upper chamber, my lords, my

[053-078] cleanest. They give me a crown. I put the crown in my drawer, and

[053-079] I say: ‘This shall go to buy tripe at the slaughter-house of la

[053-080] Gloriette to-morrow.’ We go up stairs. On arriving at the upper

[053-081] chamber, and while my back is turned, the black man disappears.

[053-082] That dazed me a bit. The officer, who was as handsome as a great

[053-083] lord, goes down stairs again with me. He goes out. In about the

[053-084] time it takes to spin a quarter of a handful of flax, he returns

[053-085] with a beautiful young girl, a doll who would have shone like the

[053-086] sun had she been coiffed. She had with her a goat; a big

[053-087] billy-goat, whether black or white, I no longer remember. That

[053-088] set me to thinking. The girl does not concern me, but the goat! I

[053-089] love not those beasts, they have a beard and horns. They are so

[053-090] like a man. And then, they smack of the witches, sabbath.

[053-091] However, I say nothing. I had the crown. That is right, is it

[053-092] not, Monsieur Judge? I show the captain and the wench to the

[053-093] upper chamber, and I leave them alone; that is to say, with the

[053-094] goat. I go down and set to spinning again—I must inform you that

[053-095] my house has a ground floor and story above. I know not why I

[053-096] fell to thinking of the surly monk whom the goat had put into my

[053-097] head again, and then the beautiful girl was rather strangely

[053-098] decked out. All at once, I hear a cry upstairs, and something

[053-099] falls on the floor and the window opens. I run to mine which is

[053-100] beneath it, and I behold a black mass pass before my eyes and

[053-101] fall into the water. It was a phantom clad like a priest. It was

[053-102] a moonlight night. I saw him quite plainly. He was swimming in

[053-103] the direction of the city. Then, all of a tremble, I call the

[053-104] watch. The gentlemen of the police enter, and not knowing just at

[053-105] the first moment what the matter was, and being merry, they beat

[053-106] me. I explain to them. We go up stairs, and what do we find? my

[053-107] poor chamber all blood, the captain stretched out at full length

[053-108] with a dagger in his neck, the girl pretending to be dead, and

[053-109] the goat all in a fright. ‘Pretty work!’ I say, ‘I shall have to

[053-110] wash that floor for more than a fortnight. It will have to be

[053-111] scraped; it will be a terrible job.’ They carried off the

[053-112] officer, poor young man, and the wench with her bosom all bare.

[053-113] But wait, the worst is that on the next day, when I wanted to

[053-114] take the crown to buy tripe, I found a dead leaf in its place.”

[053-115] The old woman ceased. A murmur of horror ran through the

[053-116] audience.

[053-117] “That phantom, that goat,—all smacks of magic,” said one of

[053-118] Gringoire’s neighbors.

[053-119] “And that dry leaf!” added another.

[053-120] “No doubt about it,” joined in a third, “she is a witch who has

[053-121] dealings with the surly monk, for the purpose of plundering

[053-122] officers.”

[053-123] Gringoire himself was not disinclined to regard this as

[053-124] altogether alarming and probable.

[053-125] “Goody Falourdel,” said the president majestically, “have you

[053-126] nothing more to communicate to the court?”

[053-127] “No, monseigneur,” replied the crone, “except that the report has

[053-128] described my house as a hovel and stinking; which is an

[053-129] outrageous fashion of speaking. The houses on the bridge are not

[053-130] imposing, because there are such multitudes of people; but,

[053-131] nevertheless, the butchers continue to dwell there, who are

[053-132] wealthy folk, and married to very proper and handsome women.”

[053-133] The magistrate who had reminded Gringoire of a crocodile rose,—

[053-134] “Silence!” said he. “I pray the gentlemen not to lose sight of

[053-135] the fact that a dagger was found on the person of the accused.

[053-136] Goody Falourdel, have you brought that leaf into which the crown

[053-137] which the demon gave you was transformed?

[053-138] “Yes, monseigneur,” she replied; “I found it again. Here it is.”

[053-139] A bailiff handed the dead leaf to the crocodile, who made a

[053-140] doleful shake of the head, and passed it on to the president, who

[053-141] gave it to the procurator of the king in the ecclesiastical

[053-142] court, and thus it made the circuit of the hall.

[053-143] “It is a birch leaf,” said Master Jacques Charmolue. “A fresh

[053-144] proof of magic.”

[053-145] A counsellor took up the word.

[053-146] “Witness, two men went upstairs together in your house: the black

[053-147] man, whom you first saw disappear and afterwards swimming in the

[053-148] Seine, with his priestly garments, and the officer. Which of the

[053-149] two handed you the crown?” The old woman pondered for a moment

[053-150] and then said,—

[053-151] “The officer.”

[053-152] A murmur ran through the crowd.

[053-153] “Ah!” thought Gringoire, “this makes some doubt in my mind.”

[053-154] But Master Philippe Lheulier, advocate extraordinary to the king,

[053-155] interposed once more.

[053-156] “I will recall to these gentlemen, that in the deposition taken

[053-157] at his bedside, the assassinated officer, while declaring that he

[053-158] had a vague idea when the black man accosted him that the latter

[053-159] might be the surly monk, added that the phantom had pressed him

[053-160] eagerly to go and make acquaintance with the accused; and upon

[053-161] his, the captain’s, remarking that he had no money, he had given

[053-162] him the crown which the said officer paid to la Falourdel. Hence,

[053-163] that crown is the money of hell.”

[053-164] This conclusive observation appeared to dissipate all the doubts

[053-165] of Gringoire and the other sceptics in the audience.

[053-166] “You have the documents, gentlemen,” added the king’s advocate,

[053-167] as he took his seat; “you can consult the testimony of Phœbus de

[053-168] Châteaupers.”

[053-169] At that name, the accused sprang up, her head rose above the

[053-170] throng. Gringoire with horror recognized la Esmeralda.

[053-171] She was pale; her tresses, formerly so gracefully braided and

[053-172] spangled with sequins, hung in disorder; her lips were blue, her

[053-173] hollow eyes were terrible. Alas!

[053-174] “Phœbus!” she said, in bewilderment; “where is he? O

[053-175] messeigneurs! before you kill me, tell me, for pity sake, whether

[053-176] he still lives?”

[053-177] “Hold your tongue, woman,” replied the president, “that is no

[053-178] affair of ours.”

[053-179] “Oh! for mercy’s sake, tell me if he is alive!” she repeated,

[053-180] clasping her beautiful emaciated hands; and the sound of her

[053-181] chains in contact with her dress, was heard.

[053-182] “Well!” said the king’s advocate roughly, “he is dying. Are you

[053-183] satisfied?”

[053-184] The unhappy girl fell back on her criminal’s seat, speechless,

[053-185] tearless, white as a wax figure.

[053-186] The president bent down to a man at his feet, who wore a gold cap

[053-187] and a black gown, a chain on his neck and a wand in his hand.

[053-188] “Bailiff, bring in the second accused.”

[053-189] All eyes turned towards a small door, which opened, and, to the

[053-190] great agitation of Gringoire, gave passage to a pretty goat with

[053-191] horns and hoofs of gold. The elegant beast halted for a moment on

[053-192] the threshold, stretching out its neck as though, perched on the

[053-193] summit of a rock, it had before its eyes an immense horizon.

[053-194] Suddenly it caught sight of the gypsy girl, and leaping over the

[053-195] table and the head of a clerk, in two bounds it was at her knees;

[053-196] then it rolled gracefully on its mistress’s feet, soliciting a

[053-197] word or a caress; but the accused remained motionless, and poor

[053-198] Djali himself obtained not a glance.

[053-199] “Eh, why—’tis my villanous beast,” said old Falourdel, “I

[053-200] recognize the two perfectly!”

[053-201] Jacques Charmolue interfered.

[053-202] “If the gentlemen please, we will proceed to the examination of

[053-203] the goat.” He was, in fact, the second criminal. Nothing more

[053-204] simple in those days than a suit of sorcery instituted against an

[053-205] animal. We find, among others in the accounts of the provost’s

[053-206] office for 1466, a curious detail concerning the expenses of the

[053-207] trial of Gillet-Soulart and his sow, “executed for their

[053-208] demerits,” at Corbeil. Everything is there, the cost of the pens

[053-209] in which to place the sow, the five hundred bundles of brushwood

[053-210] purchased at the port of Morsant, the three pints of wine and the

[053-211] bread, the last repast of the victim fraternally shared by the

[053-212] executioner, down to the eleven days of guard and food for the

[053-213] sow, at eight deniers parisis each. Sometimes, they went even

[053-214] further than animals. The capitularies of Charlemagne and of

[053-215] Louis le Débonnaire impose severe penalties on fiery phantoms

[053-216] which presume to appear in the air.
