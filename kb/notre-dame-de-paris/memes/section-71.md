---[071-001] Meanwhile, the rabble applauded with shouts of laughter; and

[071-002] seeing that the tumult was increasing around him, the scholar

[071-003] cried,—.

[071-004] “Oh! what a fine noise! _Populi debacchantis populosa

[071-005] debacchatio!_” Then he began to sing, his eye swimming in

[071-006] ecstasy, in the tone of a canon intoning vespers, _Quæ cantica!

[071-007] quæ organa! quæ cantilenæ! quæ melodiæ hic sine fine decantantur!

[071-008] Sonant melliflua hymnorum organa, suavissima angelorum melodia,

[071-009] cantica canticorum mira!_ He broke off: “Tavern-keeper of the

[071-010] devil, give me some supper!”

[071-011] There was a moment of partial silence, during which the sharp

[071-012] voice of the Duke of Egypt rose, as he gave instructions to his

[071-013] Bohemians.

[071-014] “The weasel is called Adrune; the fox, Blue-foot, or the Racer of

[071-015] the Woods; the wolf, Gray-foot, or Gold-foot; the bear the Old

[071-016] Man, or Grandfather. The cap of a gnome confers invisibility, and

[071-017] causes one to behold invisible things. Every toad that is

[071-018] baptized must be clad in red or black velvet, a bell on its neck,

[071-019] a bell on its feet. The godfather holds its head, the godmother

[071-020] its hinder parts. ’Tis the demon Sidragasum who hath the power to

[071-021] make wenches dance stark naked.”

[071-022] “By the mass!” interrupted Jehan, “I should like to be the demon

[071-023] Sidragasum.”

[071-024] Meanwhile, the vagabonds continued to arm themselves and whisper

[071-025] at the other end of the dram-shop.

[071-026] “That poor Esmeralda!” said a Bohemian. “She is our sister. She

[071-027] must be taken away from there.”

[071-028] “Is she still at Notre-Dame?” went on a merchant with the

[071-029] appearance of a Jew.

[071-030] “Yes, pardieu!”

[071-031] “Well! comrades!” exclaimed the merchant, “to Notre-Dame! So much

[071-032] the better, since there are in the chapel of Saints Féréol and

[071-033] Ferrution two statues, the one of John the Baptist, the other of

[071-034] Saint-Antoine, of solid gold, weighing together seven marks of

[071-035] gold and fifteen estellins; and the pedestals are of silver-gilt,

[071-036] of seventeen marks, five ounces. I know that; I am a goldsmith.”

[071-037] Here they served Jehan with his supper. As he threw himself back

[071-038] on the bosom of the wench beside him, he exclaimed,—

[071-039] “By Saint Voult-de-Lucques, whom people call Saint Goguelu, I am

[071-040] perfectly happy. I have before me a fool who gazes at me with the

[071-041] smooth face of an archduke. Here is one on my left whose teeth

[071-042] are so long that they hide his chin. And then, I am like the

[071-043] Marshal de Gié at the siege of Pontoise, I have my right resting

[071-044] on a hillock. _Ventre-Mahom!_ Comrade! you have the air of a

[071-045] merchant of tennis-balls; and you come and sit yourself beside

[071-046] me! I am a nobleman, my friend! Trade is incompatible with

[071-047] nobility. Get out of that! Holà hé! You others, don’t fight!

[071-048] What, Baptiste Croque-Oison, you who have such a fine nose are

[071-049] going to risk it against the big fists of that lout! Fool! _Non

[071-050] cuiquam datum est habere nasum_—not every one is favored with a

[071-051] nose. You are really divine, Jacqueline Ronge-Oreille! ’tis a

[071-052] pity that you have no hair! Holà! my name is Jehan Frollo, and my

[071-053] brother is an archdeacon. May the devil fly off with him! All

[071-054] that I tell you is the truth. In turning vagabond, I have gladly

[071-055] renounced the half of a house situated in paradise, which my

[071-056] brother had promised me. _Dimidiam domum in paradiso_. I quote

[071-057] the text. I have a fief in the Rue Tirechappe, and all the women

[071-058] are in love with me, as true as Saint Éloy was an excellent

[071-059] goldsmith, and that the five trades of the good city of Paris are

[071-060] the tanners, the tawers, the makers of cross-belts, the

[071-061] purse-makers, and the sweaters, and that Saint Laurent was burnt

[071-062] with eggshells. I swear to you, comrades.

[071-063] “Que je ne beuvrai de piment,

[071-064] Devant un an, si je cy ment![57]

[071-065] “’Tis moonlight, my charmer; see yonder through the window how

[071-066] the wind is tearing the clouds to tatters! Even thus will I do to

[071-067] your gorget.—Wenches, wipe the children’s noses and snuff the

[071-068] candles.—Christ and Mahom! What am I eating here, Jupiter? Ohé!

[071-069] innkeeper! the hair which is not on the heads of your hussies one

[071-070] finds in your omelettes. Old woman! I like bald omelettes. May

[071-071] the devil confound you!—A fine hostelry of Beelzebub, where the

[071-072] hussies comb their heads with the forks!

[071-073] “Et je n’ai moi,

[071-074] Par la sang-Dieu!

[071-075] Ni foi, ni loi,

[071-076] Ni feu, ni lieu,

[071-077] Ni roi,

[071-078] Ni Dieu.”[58]

[071-079] In the meantime, Clopin Trouillefou had finished the distribution

[071-080] of arms. He approached Gringoire, who appeared to be plunged in a

[071-081] profound revery, with his feet on an andiron.

[071-082] “Friend Pierre,” said the King of Thunes, “what the devil are you

[071-083] thinking about?”

[071-084] Gringoire turned to him with a melancholy smile.

[071-085] “I love the fire, my dear lord. Not for the trivial reason that

[071-086] fire warms the feet or cooks our soup, but because it has sparks.

[071-087] Sometimes I pass whole hours in watching the sparks. I discover a

[071-088] thousand things in those stars which are sprinkled over the black

[071-089] background of the hearth. Those stars are also worlds.”

[071-090] “Thunder, if I understand you!” said the outcast. “Do you know

[071-091] what o’clock it is?”

[071-092] “I do not know,” replied Gringoire.

[071-093] Clopin approached the Duke of Egypt.

[071-094] “Comrade Mathias, the time we have chosen is not a good one. King

[071-095] Louis XI. is said to be in Paris.”

[071-096] “Another reason for snatching our sister from his claws,” replied

[071-097] the old Bohemian.

[071-098] “You speak like a man, Mathias,” said the King of Thunes.

[071-099] “Moreover, we will act promptly. No resistance is to be feared in

[071-100] the church. The canons are hares, and we are in force. The people

[071-101] of the parliament will be well balked to-morrow when they come to

[071-102] seek her! Guts of the pope I don’t want them to hang the pretty

[071-103] girl!”

[071-104] Clopin quitted the dram-shop.

[071-105] Meanwhile, Jehan was shouting in a hoarse voice:

[071-106] “I eat, I drink, I am drunk, I am Jupiter! Eh! Pierre, the

[071-107] Slaughterer, if you look at me like that again, I’ll fillip the

[071-108] dust off your nose for you.”

[071-109] Gringoire, torn from his meditations, began to watch the wild and

[071-110] noisy scene which surrounded him, muttering between his teeth:

[071-111] “_Luxuriosa res vinum et tumultuosa ebrietas_. Alas! what good

[071-112] reason I have not to drink, and how excellently spoke

[071-113] Saint-Benoît: ‘_Vinum apostatare facit etiam sapientes!_’”

[071-114] At that moment, Clopin returned and shouted in a voice of

[071-115] thunder: “Midnight!”

[071-116] At this word, which produced the effect of the call to boot and

[071-117] saddle on a regiment at a halt, all the outcasts, men, women,

[071-118] children, rushed in a mass from the tavern, with great noise of

[071-119] arms and old iron implements.

[071-120] The moon was obscured.

[071-121] The Cour des Miracles was entirely dark. There was not a single

[071-122] light. One could make out there a throng of men and women

[071-123] conversing in low tones. They could be heard buzzing, and a gleam

[071-124] of all sorts of weapons was visible in the darkness. Clopin

[071-125] mounted a large stone.

[071-126] “To your ranks, Argot!”[59] he cried. “Fall into line, Egypt!

[071-127] Form ranks, Galilee!”

[071-128] A movement began in the darkness. The immense multitude appeared

[071-129] to form in a column. After a few minutes, the King of Thunes

[071-130] raised his voice once more,—

[071-131] “Now, silence to march through Paris! The password is, ‘Little

[071-132] sword in pocket!’ The torches will not be lighted till we reach

[071-133] Notre-Dame! Forward, march!”

[071-134] Ten minutes later, the cavaliers of the watch fled in terror

[071-135] before a long procession of black and silent men which was

[071-136] descending towards the Pont au Change, through the tortuous

[071-137] streets which pierce the close-built neighborhood of the markets

[071-138] in every direction.

[071-139] CHAPTER IV. AN AWKWARD FRIEND.

[071-140] That night, Quasimodo did not sleep. He had just made his last

[071-141] round of the church. He had not noticed, that at the moment when

[071-142] he was closing the doors, the archdeacon had passed close to him

[071-143] and betrayed some displeasure on seeing him bolting and barring

[071-144] with care the enormous iron locks which gave to their large

[071-145] leaves the solidity of a wall. Dom Claude’s air was even more

[071-146] preoccupied than usual. Moreover, since the nocturnal adventure

[071-147] in the cell, he had constantly abused Quasimodo, but in vain did

[071-148] he ill treat, and even beat him occasionally, nothing disturbed

[071-149] the submission, patience, the devoted resignation of the faithful

[071-150] bellringer. He endured everything on the part of the archdeacon,

[071-151] insults, threats, blows, without murmuring a complaint. At the

[071-152] most, he gazed uneasily after Dom Claude when the latter ascended

[071-153] the staircase of the tower; but the archdeacon had abstained from

[071-154] presenting himself again before the gypsy’s eyes.

[071-155] On that night, accordingly, Quasimodo, after having cast a glance

[071-156] at his poor bells which he so neglected now, Jacqueline, Marie,

[071-157] and Thibauld, mounted to the summit of the Northern tower, and

[071-158] there setting his dark lanturn, well closed, upon the leads, he

[071-159] began to gaze at Paris. The night, as we have already said, was

[071-160] very dark. Paris which, so to speak was not lighted at that

[071-161] epoch, presented to the eye a confused collection of black

[071-162] masses, cut here and there by the whitish curve of the Seine.

[071-163] Quasimodo no longer saw any light with the exception of one

[071-164] window in a distant edifice, whose vague and sombre profile was

[071-165] outlined well above the roofs, in the direction of the Porte

[071-166] Sainte-Antoine. There also, there was some one awake.

[071-167] As the only eye of the bellringer peered into that horizon of

[071-168] mist and night, he felt within him an inexpressible uneasiness.

[071-169] For several days he had been upon his guard. He had perceived men

[071-170] of sinister mien, who never took their eyes from the young girl’s

[071-171] asylum, prowling constantly about the church. He fancied that

[071-172] some plot might be in process of formation against the unhappy

[071-173] refugee. He imagined that there existed a popular hatred against

[071-174] her, as against himself, and that it was very possible that

[071-175] something might happen soon. Hence he remained upon his tower on

[071-176] the watch, “dreaming in his dream-place,” as Rabelais says, with

[071-177] his eye directed alternately on the cell and on Paris, keeping

[071-178] faithful guard, like a good dog, with a thousand suspicions in

[071-179] his mind.

[071-180] All at once, while he was scrutinizing the great city with that

[071-181] eye which nature, by a sort of compensation, had made so piercing

[071-182] that it could almost supply the other organs which Quasimodo

[071-183] lacked, it seemed to him that there was something singular about

[071-184] the Quay de la Vieille-Pelleterie, that there was a movement at

[071-185] that point, that the line of the parapet, standing out blackly

[071-186] against the whiteness of the water was not straight and tranquil,

[071-187] like that of the other quays, but that it undulated to the eye,

[071-188] like the waves of a river, or like the heads of a crowd in

[071-189] motion.

[071-190] This struck him as strange. He redoubled his attention. The

[071-191] movement seemed to be advancing towards the City. There was no

[071-192] light. It lasted for some time on the quay; then it gradually

[071-193] ceased, as though that which was passing were entering the

[071-194] interior of the island; then it stopped altogether, and the line

[071-195] of the quay became straight and motionless again.

[071-196] At the moment when Quasimodo was lost in conjectures, it seemed

[071-197] to him that the movement had re-appeared in the Rue du Parvis,

[071-198] which is prolonged into the city perpendicularly to the façade of

[071-199] Notre-Dame. At length, dense as was the darkness, he beheld the

[071-200] head of a column debouch from that street, and in an instant a

[071-201] crowd—of which nothing could be distinguished in the gloom except

[071-202] that it was a crowd—spread over the Place.

[071-203] This spectacle had a terror of its own. It is probable that this

[071-204] singular procession, which seemed so desirous of concealing

[071-205] itself under profound darkness, maintained a silence no less

[071-206] profound. Nevertheless, some noise must have escaped it, were it

[071-207] only a trampling. But this noise did not even reach our deaf man,

[071-208] and this great multitude, of which he saw hardly anything, and of

[071-209] which he heard nothing, though it was marching and moving so near

[071-210] him, produced upon him the effect of a rabble of dead men, mute,

[071-211] impalpable, lost in a smoke. It seemed to him, that he beheld

[071-212] advancing towards him a fog of men, and that he saw shadows

[071-213] moving in the shadow.
