---[006-001] Nevertheless, he was a fine man; he led a joyous cardinal’s life,

[006-002] liked to enliven himself with the royal vintage of Challuau, did

[006-003] not hate Richarde la Garmoise and Thomasse la Saillarde, bestowed

[006-004] alms on pretty girls rather than on old women,—and for all these

[006-005] reasons was very agreeable to the _populace_ of Paris. He never

[006-006] went about otherwise than surrounded by a small court of bishops

[006-007] and abbés of high lineage, gallant, jovial, and given to

[006-008] carousing on occasion; and more than once the good and devout

[006-009] women of Saint Germain d’ Auxerre, when passing at night beneath

[006-010] the brightly illuminated windows of Bourbon, had been scandalized

[006-011] to hear the same voices which had intoned vespers for them during

[006-012] the day carolling, to the clinking of glasses, the bacchic

[006-013] proverb of Benedict XII., that pope who had added a third crown

[006-014] to the Tiara—_Bibamus papaliter_.

[006-015] It was this justly acquired popularity, no doubt, which preserved

[006-016] him on his entrance from any bad reception at the hands of the

[006-017] mob, which had been so displeased but a moment before, and very

[006-018] little disposed to respect a cardinal on the very day when it was

[006-019] to elect a pope. But the Parisians cherish little rancor; and

[006-020] then, having forced the beginning of the play by their authority,

[006-021] the good _bourgeois_ had got the upper hand of the cardinal, and

[006-022] this triumph was sufficient for them. Moreover, the Cardinal de

[006-023] Bourbon was a handsome man,—he wore a fine scarlet robe, which he

[006-024] carried off very well,—that is to say, he had all the women on

[006-025] his side, and, consequently, the best half of the audience.

[006-026] Assuredly, it would be injustice and bad taste to hoot a cardinal

[006-027] for having come late to the spectacle, when he is a handsome man,

[006-028] and when he wears his scarlet robe well.

[006-029] He entered, then, bowed to those present with the hereditary

[006-030] smile of the great for the people, and directed his course slowly

[006-031] towards his scarlet velvet arm-chair, with the air of thinking of

[006-032] something quite different. His cortege—what we should nowadays

[006-033] call his staff—of bishops and abbés invaded the estrade in his

[006-034] train, not without causing redoubled tumult and curiosity among

[006-035] the audience. Each man vied with his neighbor in pointing them

[006-036] out and naming them, in seeing who should recognize at least one

[006-037] of them: this one, the Bishop of Marseilles (Alaudet, if my

[006-038] memory serves me right);—this one, the primicier of

[006-039] Saint-Denis;—this one, Robert de Lespinasse, Abbé of

[006-040] Saint-Germain des Prés, that libertine brother of a mistress of

[006-041] Louis XI.; all with many errors and absurdities. As for the

[006-042] scholars, they swore. This was their day, their feast of fools,

[006-043] their saturnalia, the annual orgy of the corporation of law

[006-044] clerks and of the school. There was no turpitude which was not

[006-045] sacred on that day. And then there were gay gossips in the

[006-046] crowd—Simone Quatrelivres, Agnès la Gadine, and Rabine Piédebou.

[006-047] Was it not the least that one could do to swear at one’s ease and

[006-048] revile the name of God a little, on so fine a day, in such good

[006-049] company as dignitaries of the church and loose women? So they did

[006-050] not abstain; and, in the midst of the uproar, there was a

[006-051] frightful concert of blasphemies and enormities of all the

[006-052] unbridled tongues, the tongues of clerks and students restrained

[006-053] during the rest of the year, by the fear of the hot iron of Saint

[006-054] Louis. Poor Saint Louis! how they set him at defiance in his own

[006-055] court of law! Each one of them selected from the new-comers on

[006-056] the platform, a black, gray, white, or violet cassock as his

[006-057] target. Joannes Frollo de Molendin, in his quality of brother to

[006-058] an archdeacon, boldly attacked the scarlet; he sang in deafening

[006-059] tones, with his impudent eyes fastened on the cardinal, “_Cappa

[006-060] repleta mero_!”

[006-061] All these details which we here lay bare for the edification of

[006-062] the reader, were so covered by the general uproar, that they were

[006-063] lost in it before reaching the reserved platforms; moreover, they

[006-064] would have moved the cardinal but little, so much a part of the

[006-065] customs were the liberties of that day. Moreover, he had another

[006-066] cause for solicitude, and his mien as wholly preoccupied with it,

[006-067] which entered the estrade the same time as himself; this was the

[006-068] embassy from Flanders.

[006-069] Not that he was a profound politician, nor was he borrowing

[006-070] trouble about the possible consequences of the marriage of his

[006-071] cousin Marguerite de Bourgoyne to his cousin Charles, Dauphin de

[006-072] Vienne; nor as to how long the good understanding which had been

[006-073] patched up between the Duke of Austria and the King of France

[006-074] would last; nor how the King of England would take this disdain

[006-075] of his daughter. All that troubled him but little; and he gave a

[006-076] warm reception every evening to the wine of the royal vintage of

[006-077] Chaillot, without a suspicion that several flasks of that same

[006-078] wine (somewhat revised and corrected, it is true, by Doctor

[006-079] Coictier), cordially offered to Edward IV. by Louis XI., would,

[006-080] some fine morning, rid Louis XI. of Edward IV. “The much honored

[006-081] embassy of Monsieur the Duke of Austria,” brought the cardinal

[006-082] none of these cares, but it troubled him in another direction. It

[006-083] was, in fact, somewhat hard, and we have already hinted at it on

[006-084] the second page of this book,—for him, Charles de Bourbon, to be

[006-085] obliged to feast and receive cordially no one knows what

[006-086] _bourgeois_;—for him, a cardinal, to receive aldermen;—for him, a

[006-087] Frenchman, and a jolly companion, to receive Flemish

[006-088] beer-drinkers,—and that in public! This was, certainly, one of

[006-089] the most irksome grimaces that he had ever executed for the good

[006-090] pleasure of the king.

[006-091] So he turned toward the door, and with the best grace in the

[006-092] world (so well had he trained himself to it), when the usher

[006-093] announced, in a sonorous voice, “Messieurs the Envoys of Monsieur

[006-094] the Duke of Austria.” It is useless to add that the whole hall

[006-095] did the same.

[006-096] Then arrived, two by two, with a gravity which made a contrast in

[006-097] the midst of the frisky ecclesiastical escort of Charles de

[006-098] Bourbon, the eight and forty ambassadors of Maximilian of

[006-099] Austria, having at their head the reverend Father in God, Jehan,

[006-100] Abbot of Saint-Bertin, Chancellor of the Golden Fleece, and

[006-101] Jacques de Goy, Sieur Dauby, Grand Bailiff of Ghent. A deep

[006-102] silence settled over the assembly, accompanied by stifled

[006-103] laughter at the preposterous names and all the _bourgeois_

[006-104] designations which each of these personages transmitted with

[006-105] imperturbable gravity to the usher, who then tossed names and

[006-106] titles pell-mell and mutilated to the crowd below. There were

[006-107] Master Loys Roelof, alderman of the city of Louvain; Messire

[006-108] Clays d’Etuelde, alderman of Brussels; Messire Paul de Baeust,

[006-109] Sieur de Voirmizelle, President of Flanders; Master Jehan

[006-110] Coleghens, burgomaster of the city of Antwerp; Master George de

[006-111] la Moere, first alderman of the kuere of the city of Ghent;

[006-112] Master Gheldolf van der Hage, first alderman of the _parchons_ of

[006-113] the said town; and the Sieur de Bierbecque, and Jehan Pinnock,

[006-114] and Jehan Dymaerzelle, etc., etc., etc.; bailiffs, aldermen,

[006-115] burgomasters; burgomasters, aldermen, bailiffs—all stiff,

[006-116] affectedly grave, formal, dressed out in velvet and damask,

[006-117] hooded with caps of black velvet, with great tufts of Cyprus gold

[006-118] thread; good Flemish heads, after all, severe and worthy faces,

[006-119] of the family which Rembrandt makes to stand out so strong and

[006-120] grave from the black background of his “Night Patrol”; personages

[006-121] all of whom bore, written on their brows, that Maximilian of

[006-122] Austria had done well in “trusting implicitly,” as the manifest

[006-123] ran, “in their sense, valor, experience, loyalty, and good

[006-124] wisdom.”

[006-125] There was one exception, however. It was a subtle, intelligent,

[006-126] crafty-looking face, a sort of combined monkey and diplomat phiz,

[006-127] before whom the cardinal made three steps and a profound bow, and

[006-128] whose name, nevertheless, was only, “Guillaume Rym, counsellor

[006-129] and pensioner of the City of Ghent.”

[006-130] Few persons were then aware who Guillaume Rym was. A rare genius

[006-131] who in a time of revolution would have made a brilliant

[006-132] appearance on the surface of events, but who in the fifteenth

[006-133] century was reduced to cavernous intrigues, and to “living in

[006-134] mines,” as the Duc de Saint-Simon expresses it. Nevertheless, he

[006-135] was appreciated by the “miner” of Europe; he plotted familiarly

[006-136] with Louis XI., and often lent a hand to the king’s secret jobs.

[006-137] All which things were quite unknown to that throng, who were

[006-138] amazed at the cardinal’s politeness to that frail figure of a

[006-139] Flemish bailiff.

[006-140] CHAPTER IV. MASTER JACQUES COPPENOLE.

[006-141] While the pensioner of Ghent and his eminence were exchanging

[006-142] very low bows and a few words in voices still lower, a man of

[006-143] lofty stature, with a large face and broad shoulders, presented

[006-144] himself, in order to enter abreast with Guillaume Rym; one would

[006-145] have pronounced him a bull-dog by the side of a fox. His felt

[006-146] doublet and leather jerkin made a spot on the velvet and silk

[006-147] which surrounded him. Presuming that he was some groom who had

[006-148] stolen in, the usher stopped him.

[006-149] “Hold, my friend, you cannot pass!”

[006-150] The man in the leather jerkin shouldered him aside.

[006-151] “What does this knave want with me?” said he, in stentorian

[006-152] tones, which rendered the entire hall attentive to this strange

[006-153] colloquy. “Don’t you see that I am one of them?”

[006-154] “Your name?” demanded the usher.

[006-155] “Jacques Coppenole.”

[006-156] “Your titles?”

[006-157] “Hosier at the sign of the ‘Three Little Chains,’ of Ghent.”

[006-158] The usher recoiled. One might bring one’s self to announce

[006-159] aldermen and burgomasters, but a hosier was too much. The

[006-160] cardinal was on thorns. All the people were staring and

[006-161] listening. For two days his eminence had been exerting his utmost

[006-162] efforts to lick these Flemish bears into shape, and to render

[006-163] them a little more presentable to the public, and this freak was

[006-164] startling. But Guillaume Rym, with his polished smile, approached

[006-165] the usher.

[006-166] “Announce Master Jacques Coppenole, clerk of the aldermen of the

[006-167] city of Ghent,” he whispered, very low.

[006-168] “Usher,” interposed the cardinal, aloud, “announce Master Jacques

[006-169] Coppenole, clerk of the aldermen of the illustrious city of

[006-170] Ghent.”

[006-171] This was a mistake. Guillaume Rym alone might have conjured away

[006-172] the difficulty, but Coppenole had heard the cardinal.

[006-173] “No, cross of God?” he exclaimed, in his voice of thunder,

[006-174] “Jacques Coppenole, hosier. Do you hear, usher? Nothing more,

[006-175] nothing less. Cross of God! hosier; that’s fine enough. Monsieur

[006-176] the Archduke has more than once sought his _gant_[5] in my hose.”

[006-177] Laughter and applause burst forth. A jest is always understood in

[006-178] Paris, and, consequently, always applauded.

[006-179] Let us add that Coppenole was of the people, and that the

[006-180] auditors which surrounded him were also of the people. Thus the

[006-181] communication between him and them had been prompt, electric,

[006-182] and, so to speak, on a level. The haughty air of the Flemish

[006-183] hosier, by humiliating the courtiers, had touched in all these

[006-184] plebeian souls that latent sentiment of dignity still vague and

[006-185] indistinct in the fifteenth century.

[006-186] This hosier was an equal, who had just held his own before

[006-187] monsieur the cardinal. A very sweet reflection to poor fellows

[006-188] habituated to respect and obedience towards the underlings of the

[006-189] sergeants of the bailiff of Sainte-Geneviève, the cardinal’s

[006-190] train-bearer.

[006-191] Coppenole proudly saluted his eminence, who returned the salute

[006-192] of the all-powerful _bourgeois_ feared by Louis XI. Then, while

[006-193] Guillaume Rym, a “sage and malicious man,” as Philippe de Comines

[006-194] puts it, watched them both with a smile of raillery and

[006-195] superiority, each sought his place, the cardinal quite abashed

[006-196] and troubled, Coppenole tranquil and haughty, and thinking, no

[006-197] doubt, that his title of hosier was as good as any other, after

[006-198] all, and that Marie of Burgundy, mother to that Marguerite whom

[006-199] Coppenole was to-day bestowing in marriage, would have been less

[006-200] afraid of the cardinal than of the hosier; for it is not a

[006-201] cardinal who would have stirred up a revolt among the men of

[006-202] Ghent against the favorites of the daughter of Charles the Bold;

[006-203] it is not a cardinal who could have fortified the populace with a

[006-204] word against her tears and prayers, when the Maid of Flanders

[006-205] came to supplicate her people in their behalf, even at the very

[006-206] foot of the scaffold; while the hosier had only to raise his

[006-207] leather elbow, in order to cause to fall your two heads, most

[006-208] illustrious seigneurs, Guy d’Hymbercourt and Chancellor Guillaume

[006-209] Hugonet.
