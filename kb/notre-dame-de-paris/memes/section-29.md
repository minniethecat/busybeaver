---[029-001] It was, in fact, the king’s physician; a person about fifty years

[029-002] of age, whose harsh physiognomy was modified only by a crafty

[029-003] eye. Another man accompanied him. Both wore long slate-colored

[029-004] robes, furred with minever, girded and closed, with caps of the

[029-005] same stuff and hue. Their hands were concealed by their sleeves,

[029-006] their feet by their robes, their eyes by their caps.

[029-007] “God help me, messieurs!” said the archdeacon, showing them in;

[029-008] “I was not expecting distinguished visitors at such an hour.” And

[029-009] while speaking in this courteous fashion he cast an uneasy and

[029-010] scrutinizing glance from the physician to his companion.

[029-011] “’Tis never too late to come and pay a visit to so considerable a

[029-012] learned man as Dom Claude Frollo de Tirechappe,” replied Doctor

[029-013] Coictier, whose Franche-Comté accent made all his phrases drag

[029-014] along with the majesty of a train-robe.

[029-015] There then ensued between the physician and the archdeacon one of

[029-016] those congratulatory prologues which, in accordance with custom,

[029-017] at that epoch preceded all conversations between learned men, and

[029-018] which did not prevent them from detesting each other in the most

[029-019] cordial manner in the world. However, it is the same nowadays;

[029-020] every wise man’s mouth complimenting another wise man is a vase

[029-021] of honeyed gall.

[029-022] Claude Frollo’s felicitations to Jacques Coictier bore reference

[029-023] principally to the temporal advantages which the worthy physician

[029-024] had found means to extract, in the course of his much envied

[029-025] career, from each malady of the king, an operation of alchemy

[029-026] much better and more certain than the pursuit of the

[029-027] philosopher’s stone.

[029-028] “In truth, Monsieur le Docteur Coictier, I felt great joy on

[029-029] learning of the bishopric given your nephew, my reverend seigneur

[029-030] Pierre Versé. Is he not Bishop of Amiens?”

[029-031] “Yes, monsieur Archdeacon; it is a grace and mercy of God.”

[029-032] “Do you know that you made a great figure on Christmas Day at the

[029-033] head of your company of the chamber of accounts, Monsieur

[029-034] President?”

[029-035] “Vice-President, Dom Claude. Alas! nothing more.”

[029-036] “How is your superb house in the Rue Saint-André des Arcs coming

[029-037] on? ’Tis a Louvre. I love greatly the apricot tree which is

[029-038] carved on the door, with this play of words: ‘A

[029-039] L’ABRI-COTIER—Sheltered from reefs.’”

[029-040] “Alas! Master Claude, all that masonry costeth me dear. In

[029-041] proportion as the house is erected, I am ruined.”

[029-042] “Ho! have you not your revenues from the jail, and the bailiwick

[029-043] of the Palais, and the rents of all the houses, sheds, stalls,

[029-044] and booths of the enclosure? ’Tis a fine breast to suck.”

[029-045] “My castellany of Poissy has brought me in nothing this year.”

[029-046] “But your tolls of Triel, of Saint-James, of

[029-047] Saint-Germain-en-Laye are always good.”

[029-048] “Six score livres, and not even Parisian livres at that.”

[029-049] “You have your office of counsellor to the king. That is fixed.”

[029-050] “Yes, brother Claude; but that accursed seigneury of Poligny,

[029-051] which people make so much noise about, is worth not sixty gold

[029-052] crowns, year out and year in.”

[029-053] In the compliments which Dom Claude addressed to Jacques

[029-054] Coictier, there was that sardonical, biting, and covertly mocking

[029-055] accent, and the sad cruel smile of a superior and unhappy man who

[029-056] toys for a moment, by way of distraction, with the dense

[029-057] prosperity of a vulgar man. The other did not perceive it.

[029-058] “Upon my soul,” said Claude at length, pressing his hand, “I am

[029-059] glad to see you and in such good health.”

[029-060] “Thanks, Master Claude.”

[029-061] “By the way,” exclaimed Dom Claude, “how is your royal patient?”

[029-062] “He payeth not sufficiently his physician,” replied the doctor,

[029-063] casting a side glance at his companion.

[029-064] “Think you so, Gossip Coictier,” said the latter.

[029-065] These words, uttered in a tone of surprise and reproach, drew

[029-066] upon this unknown personage the attention of the archdeacon

[029-067] which, to tell the truth, had not been diverted from him a single

[029-068] moment since the stranger had set foot across the threshold of

[029-069] his cell. It had even required all the thousand reasons which he

[029-070] had for handling tenderly Doctor Jacques Coictier, the

[029-071] all-powerful physician of King Louis XI., to induce him to

[029-072] receive the latter thus accompanied. Hence, there was nothing

[029-073] very cordial in his manner when Jacques Coictier said to him,—

[029-074] “By the way, Dom Claude, I bring you a colleague who has desired

[029-075] to see you on account of your reputation.”

[029-076] “Monsieur belongs to science?” asked the archdeacon, fixing his

[029-077] piercing eye upon Coictier’s companion. He found beneath the

[029-078] brows of the stranger a glance no less piercing or less

[029-079] distrustful than his own.

[029-080] He was, so far as the feeble light of the lamp permitted one to

[029-081] judge, an old man about sixty years of age and of medium stature,

[029-082] who appeared somewhat sickly and broken in health. His profile,

[029-083] although of a very ordinary outline, had something powerful and

[029-084] severe about it; his eyes sparkled beneath a very deep

[029-085] superciliary arch, like a light in the depths of a cave; and

[029-086] beneath his cap which was well drawn down and fell upon his nose,

[029-087] one recognized the broad expanse of a brow of genius.

[029-088] He took it upon himself to reply to the archdeacon’s question,—

[029-089] “Reverend master,” he said in a grave tone, “your renown has

[029-090] reached my ears, and I wish to consult you. I am but a poor

[029-091] provincial gentleman, who removeth his shoes before entering the

[029-092] dwellings of the learned. You must know my name. I am called

[029-093] Gossip Tourangeau.”

[029-094] “Strange name for a gentleman,” said the archdeacon to himself.

[029-095] Nevertheless, he had a feeling that he was in the presence of a

[029-096] strong and earnest character. The instinct of his own lofty

[029-097] intellect made him recognize an intellect no less lofty under

[029-098] Gossip Tourangeau’s furred cap, and as he gazed at the solemn

[029-099] face, the ironical smile which Jacques Coictier’s presence called

[029-100] forth on his gloomy face, gradually disappeared as twilight fades

[029-101] on the horizon of night. Stern and silent, he had resumed his

[029-102] seat in his great armchair; his elbow rested as usual, on the

[029-103] table, and his brow on his hand. After a few moments of

[029-104] reflection, he motioned his visitors to be seated, and, turning

[029-105] to Gossip Tourangeau he said,—

[029-106] “You come to consult me, master, and upon what science?”

[029-107] “Your reverence,” replied Tourangeau, “I am ill, very ill. You

[029-108] are said to be great Æsculapius, and I am come to ask your advice

[029-109] in medicine.”

[029-110] “Medicine!” said the archdeacon, tossing his head. He seemed to

[029-111] meditate for a moment, and then resumed: “Gossip Tourangeau,

[029-112] since that is your name, turn your head, you will find my reply

[029-113] already written on the wall.”

[029-114] Gossip Tourangeau obeyed, and read this inscription engraved

[029-115] above his head: “_Medicine is the daughter of

[029-116] dreams_.—JAMBLIQUE.”

[029-117] Meanwhile, Doctor Jacques Coictier had heard his companion’s

[029-118] question with a displeasure which Dom Claude’s response had but

[029-119] redoubled. He bent down to the ear of Gossip Tourangeau, and said

[029-120] to him, softly enough not to be heard by the archdeacon: “I

[029-121] warned you that he was mad. You insisted on seeing him.”

[029-122] “’Tis very possible that he is right, madman as he is, Doctor

[029-123] Jacques,” replied his comrade in the same low tone, and with a

[029-124] bitter smile.

[029-125] “As you please,” replied Coictier dryly. Then, addressing the

[029-126] archdeacon: “You are clever at your trade, Dom Claude, and you

[029-127] are no more at a loss over Hippocrates than a monkey is over a

[029-128] nut. Medicine a dream! I suspect that the pharmacopolists and the

[029-129] master physicians would insist upon stoning you if they were

[029-130] here. So you deny the influence of philtres upon the blood, and

[029-131] unguents on the skin! You deny that eternal pharmacy of flowers

[029-132] and metals, which is called the world, made expressly for that

[029-133] eternal invalid called man!”

[029-134] “I deny,” said Dom Claude coldly, “neither pharmacy nor the

[029-135] invalid. I reject the physician.”

[029-136] “Then it is not true,” resumed Coictier hotly, “that gout is an

[029-137] internal eruption; that a wound caused by artillery is to be

[029-138] cured by the application of a young mouse roasted; that young

[029-139] blood, properly injected, restores youth to aged veins; it is not

[029-140] true that two and two make four, and that emprostathonos follows

[029-141] opistathonos.”

[029-142] The archdeacon replied without perturbation: “There are certain

[029-143] things of which I think in a certain fashion.”

[029-144] Coictier became crimson with anger.

[029-145] “There, there, my good Coictier, let us not get angry,” said

[029-146] Gossip Tourangeau. “Monsieur the archdeacon is our friend.”

[029-147] Coictier calmed down, muttering in a low tone,—

[029-148] “After all, he’s mad.”

[029-149] “_Pasque-dieu_, Master Claude,” resumed Gossip Tourangeau, after

[029-150] a silence, “You embarrass me greatly. I had two things to consult

[029-151] you upon, one touching my health and the other touching my star.”

[029-152] “Monsieur,” returned the archdeacon, “if that be your motive, you

[029-153] would have done as well not to put yourself out of breath

[029-154] climbing my staircase. I do not believe in Medicine. I do not

[029-155] believe in Astrology.”

[029-156] “Indeed!” said the man, with surprise.

[029-157] Coictier gave a forced laugh.

[029-158] “You see that he is mad,” he said, in a low tone, to Gossip

[029-159] Tourangeau. “He does not believe in astrology.”

[029-160] “The idea of imagining,” pursued Dom Claude, “that every ray of a

[029-161] star is a thread which is fastened to the head of a man!”

[029-162] “And what then, do you believe in?” exclaimed Gossip Tourangeau.

[029-163] The archdeacon hesitated for a moment, then he allowed a gloomy

[029-164] smile to escape, which seemed to give the lie to his response:

[029-165] “_Credo in Deum_.”

[029-166] “_Dominum nostrum_,” added Gossip Tourangeau, making the sign of

[029-167] the cross.

[029-168] “Amen,” said Coictier.

[029-169] “Reverend master,” resumed Tourangeau, “I am charmed in soul to

[029-170] see you in such a religious frame of mind. But have you reached

[029-171] the point, great savant as you are, of no longer believing in

[029-172] science?”

[029-173] “No,” said the archdeacon, grasping the arm of Gossip Tourangeau,

[029-174] and a ray of enthusiasm lighted up his gloomy eyes, “no, I do not

[029-175] reject science. I have not crawled so long, flat on my belly,

[029-176] with my nails in the earth, through the innumerable ramifications

[029-177] of its caverns, without perceiving far in front of me, at the end

[029-178] of the obscure gallery, a light, a flame, a something, the

[029-179] reflection, no doubt, of the dazzling central laboratory where

[029-180] the patient and the wise have found out God.”

[029-181] “And in short,” interrupted Tourangeau, “what do you hold to be

[029-182] true and certain?”

[029-183] “Alchemy.”

[029-184] Coictier exclaimed, “Pardieu, Dom Claude, alchemy has its use, no

[029-185] doubt, but why blaspheme medicine and astrology?”

[029-186] “Naught is your science of man, naught is your science of the

[029-187] stars,” said the archdeacon, commandingly.

[029-188] “That’s driving Epidaurus and Chaldea very fast,” replied the

[029-189] physician with a grin.

[029-190] “Listen, Messire Jacques. This is said in good faith. I am not

[029-191] the king’s physician, and his majesty has not given me the Garden

[029-192] of Dædalus in which to observe the constellations. Don’t get

[029-193] angry, but listen to me. What truth have you deduced, I will not

[029-194] say from medicine, which is too foolish a thing, but from

[029-195] astrology? Cite to me the virtues of the vertical boustrophedon,

[029-196] the treasures of the number ziruph and those of the number

[029-197] zephirod!”

[029-198] “Will you deny,” said Coictier, “the sympathetic force of the

[029-199] collar bone, and the cabalistics which are derived from it?”

[029-200] “An error, Messire Jacques! None of your formulas end in reality.

[029-201] Alchemy on the other hand has its discoveries. Will you contest

[029-202] results like this? Ice confined beneath the earth for a thousand

[029-203] years is transformed into rock crystals. Lead is the ancestor of

[029-204] all metals. For gold is not a metal, gold is light. Lead requires

[029-205] only four periods of two hundred years each, to pass in

[029-206] succession from the state of lead, to the state of red arsenic,

[029-207] from red arsenic to tin, from tin to silver. Are not these facts?

[029-208] But to believe in the collar bone, in the full line and in the

[029-209] stars, is as ridiculous as to believe with the inhabitants of

[029-210] Grand-Cathay that the golden oriole turns into a mole, and that

[029-211] grains of wheat turn into fish of the carp species.”
