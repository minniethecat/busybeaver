# Foundling (Abandoned Children)

The foundling (*enfant trouvé*) is a recurring motif in Notre-Dame de Paris, most significantly as the origin of Quasimodo himself. Hugo uses the medieval institution of foundling care to examine themes of abandonment, social charity, and the precarious status of those without family in fifteenth-century Paris.

## Definition

In Hugo's novel, foundlings are children abandoned by their parents and left to public charity. The practice was institutionalized in medieval Paris through the foundling asylum (*l'hospice des enfants trouvés*), where abandoned infants were placed on a bed for public viewing and potential adoption [024-098] [024-147]. The system operated at the intersection of religious charity and civic obligation, reflecting the limited social safety net of the period.

## Hugo's Treatment

### In the Narrative

Quasimodo's origin as a foundling is revealed during the scene where he was abandoned as an infant at the cathedral. The child was left on the wooden bed reserved for foundlings at Notre-Dame, where he was discovered and eventually taken in by the young Claude Frollo [024-098] [024-193]. The reaction of the crowd is one of horror and superstition — they call him "a real monster of abomination" [024-135] and speculate about his demonic nature.

Hugo describes how the foundling Quasimodo grows up with the cathedral as his only home: "all this had gone to his heart" — the bells, the stones, the community of foundlings all shaped his soul [025-174] [025-180]. The foundling asylum itself is described as forming "the lower" part of the charitable institutions surrounding Notre-Dame [024-147].

Later in the novel, the foundling theme resurfaces when Esmeralda's own origin is questioned — she is described as "a foundling or a" child of uncertain parentage [044-147], creating a parallel between the two central characters. At the novel's climax, Quasimodo is lamented as "that orphan, that foundling" facing death [061-170] [064-124].

### In the Author's Voice

Hugo uses the foundling institution to critique medieval society's treatment of the vulnerable. The scene of Quasimodo's abandonment is rendered with careful attention to the institutional mechanisms — the wooden bed, the public display — that made foundlings simultaneously objects of charity and spectacle. The superstitious reactions of the onlookers ("this pretended foundling is a real monster") expose the gap between Christian charity's ideals and its practice [024-135].

A recurring authorial concern is the legal and social status of foundlings. The question of whether Quasimodo belongs anywhere — "a foundling or a" being without defined parentage — runs through the novel as a meditation on identity without lineage [044-147]. Hugo connects this to his broader theme of marginalization: the foundling, like the hunchback, the gypsy, and the outcast, exists outside the protected categories of medieval society.
