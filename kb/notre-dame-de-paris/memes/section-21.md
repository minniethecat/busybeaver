---[021-001] Independently of these two principal streets, piercing Paris

[021-002] diametrically in its whole breadth, from side to side, common to

[021-003] the entire capital, the City and the University had also each its

[021-004] own great special street, which ran lengthwise by them, parallel

[021-005] to the Seine, cutting, as it passed, at right angles, the two

[021-006] arterial thoroughfares. Thus, in the Town, one descended in a

[021-007] straight line from the Porte Saint-Antoine to the Porte

[021-008] Saint-Honoré; in the University from the Porte Saint-Victor to

[021-009] the Porte Saint-Germain. These two great thoroughfares

[021-010] intersected by the two first, formed the canvas upon which

[021-011] reposed, knotted and crowded together on every hand, the

[021-012] labyrinthine network of the streets of Paris. In the

[021-013] incomprehensible plan of these streets, one distinguished

[021-014] likewise, on looking attentively, two clusters of great streets,

[021-015] like magnified sheaves of grain, one in the University, the other

[021-016] in the Town, which spread out gradually from the bridges to the

[021-017] gates.

[021-018] Some traces of this geometrical plan still exist to-day.

[021-019] Now, what aspect did this whole present, when, as viewed from the

[021-020] summit of the towers of Notre-Dame, in 1482? That we shall try to

[021-021] describe.

[021-022] For the spectator who arrived, panting, upon that pinnacle, it

[021-023] was first a dazzling confusing view of roofs, chimneys, streets,

[021-024] bridges, places, spires, bell towers. Everything struck your eye

[021-025] at once: the carved gable, the pointed roof, the turrets

[021-026] suspended at the angles of the walls; the stone pyramids of the

[021-027] eleventh century, the slate obelisks of the fifteenth; the round,

[021-028] bare tower of the donjon keep; the square and fretted tower of

[021-029] the church; the great and the little, the massive and the aerial.

[021-030] The eye was, for a long time, wholly lost in this labyrinth,

[021-031] where there was nothing which did not possess its originality,

[021-032] its reason, its genius, its beauty,—nothing which did not proceed

[021-033] from art; beginning with the smallest house, with its painted and

[021-034] carved front, with external beams, elliptical door, with

[021-035] projecting stories, to the royal Louvre, which then had a

[021-036] colonnade of towers. But these are the principal masses which

[021-037] were then to be distinguished when the eye began to accustom

[021-038] itself to this tumult of edifices.

[021-039] In the first place, the City.—“The island of the City,” as Sauval

[021-040] says, who, in spite of his confused medley, sometimes has such

[021-041] happy turns of expression,—“the island of the city is made like a

[021-042] great ship, stuck in the mud and run aground in the current, near

[021-043] the centre of the Seine.”

[021-044] We have just explained that, in the fifteenth century, this ship

[021-045] was anchored to the two banks of the river by five bridges. This

[021-046] form of a ship had also struck the heraldic scribes; for it is

[021-047] from that, and not from the siege by the Normans, that the ship

[021-048] which blazons the old shield of Paris, comes, according to Favyn

[021-049] and Pasquier. For him who understands how to decipher them,

[021-050] armorial bearings are algebra, armorial bearings have a tongue.

[021-051] The whole history of the second half of the Middle Ages is

[021-052] written in armorial bearings,—the first half is in the symbolism

[021-053] of the Roman churches. They are the hieroglyphics of feudalism,

[021-054] succeeding those of theocracy.

[021-055] Thus the City first presented itself to the eye, with its stern

[021-056] to the east, and its prow to the west. Turning towards the prow,

[021-057] one had before one an innumerable flock of ancient roofs, over

[021-058] which arched broadly the lead-covered apse of the

[021-059] Sainte-Chapelle, like an elephant’s haunches loaded with its

[021-060] tower. Only here, this tower was the most audacious, the most

[021-061] open, the most ornamented spire of cabinet-maker’s work that ever

[021-062] let the sky peep through its cone of lace. In front of

[021-063] Notre-Dame, and very near at hand, three streets opened into the

[021-064] cathedral square,—a fine square, lined with ancient houses. Over

[021-065] the south side of this place bent the wrinkled and sullen façade

[021-066] of the Hôtel Dieu, and its roof, which seemed covered with warts

[021-067] and pustules. Then, on the right and the left, to east and west,

[021-068] within that wall of the City, which was yet so contracted, rose

[021-069] the bell towers of its one and twenty churches, of every date, of

[021-070] every form, of every size, from the low and wormeaten belfry of

[021-071] Saint-Denis du Pas (_Carcer Glaucini_) to the slender needles of

[021-072] Saint-Pierre aux Bœufs and Saint-Landry.

[021-073] Behind Notre-Dame, the cloister and its Gothic galleries spread

[021-074] out towards the north; on the south, the half-Roman palace of the

[021-075] bishop; on the east, the desert point of the Terrain. In this

[021-076] throng of houses the eye also distinguished, by the lofty

[021-077] open-work mitres of stone which then crowned the roof itself,

[021-078] even the most elevated windows of the palace, the hotel given by

[021-079] the city, under Charles VI., to Juvénal des Ursins; a little

[021-080] farther on, the pitch-covered sheds of the Palus Market; in still

[021-081] another quarter the new apse of Saint-Germain le Vieux,

[021-082] lengthened in 1458, with a bit of the Rue aux Febves; and then,

[021-083] in places, a square crowded with people; a pillory, erected at

[021-084] the corner of a street; a fine fragment of the pavement of Philip

[021-085] Augustus, a magnificent flagging, grooved for the horses’ feet,

[021-086] in the middle of the road, and so badly replaced in the sixteenth

[021-087] century by the miserable cobblestones, called the _pavement of

[021-088] the League;_ a deserted back courtyard, with one of those

[021-089] diaphanous staircase turrets, such as were erected in the

[021-090] fifteenth century, one of which is still to be seen in the Rue

[021-091] des Bourdonnais. Lastly, at the right of the Sainte-Chapelle,

[021-092] towards the west, the Palais de Justice rested its group of

[021-093] towers at the edge of the water. The thickets of the king’s

[021-094] gardens, which covered the western point of the City, masked the

[021-095] Island du Passeur. As for the water, from the summit of the

[021-096] towers of Notre-Dame one hardly saw it, on either side of the

[021-097] City; the Seine was hidden by bridges, the bridges by houses.

[021-098] And when the glance passed these bridges, whose roofs were

[021-099] visibly green, rendered mouldy before their time by the vapors

[021-100] from the water, if it was directed to the left, towards the

[021-101] University, the first edifice which struck it was a large, low

[021-102] sheaf of towers, the Petit-Châtelet, whose yawning gate devoured

[021-103] the end of the Petit-Pont. Then, if your view ran along the bank,

[021-104] from east to west, from the Tournelle to the Tour de Nesle, there

[021-105] was a long cordon of houses, with carved beams, stained-glass

[021-106] windows, each story projecting over that beneath it, an

[021-107] interminable zigzag of _bourgeois_ gables, frequently interrupted

[021-108] by the mouth of a street, and from time to time also by the front

[021-109] or angle of a huge stone mansion, planted at its ease, with

[021-110] courts and gardens, wings and detached buildings, amid this

[021-111] populace of crowded and narrow houses, like a grand gentleman

[021-112] among a throng of rustics. There were five or six of these

[021-113] mansions on the quay, from the house of Lorraine, which shared

[021-114] with the Bernardins the grand enclosure adjoining the Tournelle,

[021-115] to the Hôtel de Nesle, whose principal tower ended Paris, and

[021-116] whose pointed roofs were in a position, during three months of

[021-117] the year, to encroach, with their black triangles, upon the

[021-118] scarlet disk of the setting sun.

[021-119] This side of the Seine was, however, the least mercantile of the

[021-120] two. Students furnished more of a crowd and more noise there than

[021-121] artisans, and there was not, properly speaking, any quay, except

[021-122] from the Pont Saint-Michel to the Tour de Nesle. The rest of the

[021-123] bank of the Seine was now a naked strand, the same as beyond the

[021-124] Bernardins; again, a throng of houses, standing with their feet

[021-125] in the water, as between the two bridges.

[021-126] There was a great uproar of laundresses; they screamed, and

[021-127] talked, and sang from morning till night along the beach, and

[021-128] beat a great deal of linen there, just as in our day. This is not

[021-129] the least of the gayeties of Paris.

[021-130] The University presented a dense mass to the eye. From one end to

[021-131] the other, it was homogeneous and compact. The thousand roofs,

[021-132] dense, angular, clinging to each other, composed, nearly all, of

[021-133] the same geometrical element, offered, when viewed from above,

[021-134] the aspect of a crystallization of the same substance.

[021-135] The capricious ravine of streets did not cut this block of houses

[021-136] into too disproportionate slices. The forty-two colleges were

[021-137] scattered about in a fairly equal manner, and there were some

[021-138] everywhere. The amusingly varied crests of these beautiful

[021-139] edifices were the product of the same art as the simple roofs

[021-140] which they overshot, and were, actually, only a multiplication of

[021-141] the square or the cube of the same geometrical figure. Hence they

[021-142] complicated the whole effect, without disturbing it; completed,

[021-143] without overloading it. Geometry is harmony. Some fine mansions

[021-144] here and there made magnificent outlines against the picturesque

[021-145] attics of the left bank. The house of Nevers, the house of Rome,

[021-146] the house of Reims, which have disappeared; the Hôtel de Cluny,

[021-147] which still exists, for the consolation of the artist, and whose

[021-148] tower was so stupidly deprived of its crown a few years ago.

[021-149] Close to Cluny, that Roman palace, with fine round arches, were

[021-150] once the hot baths of Julian. There were a great many abbeys, of

[021-151] a beauty more devout, of a grandeur more solemn than the

[021-152] mansions, but not less beautiful, not less grand. Those which

[021-153] first caught the eye were the Bernardins, with their three bell

[021-154] towers; Sainte-Geneviève, whose square tower, which still exists,

[021-155] makes us regret the rest; the Sorbonne, half college, half

[021-156] monastery, of which so admirable a nave survives; the fine

[021-157] quadrilateral cloister of the Mathurins; its neighbor, the

[021-158] cloister of Saint-Benoît, within whose walls they have had time

[021-159] to cobble up a theatre, between the seventh and eighth editions

[021-160] of this book; the Cordeliers, with their three enormous adjacent

[021-161] gables; the Augustins, whose graceful spire formed, after the

[021-162] Tour de Nesle, the second denticulation on this side of Paris,

[021-163] starting from the west. The colleges, which are, in fact, the

[021-164] intermediate ring between the cloister and the world, hold the

[021-165] middle position in the monumental series between the hotels and

[021-166] the abbeys, with a severity full of elegance, sculpture less

[021-167] giddy than the palaces, an architecture less severe than the

[021-168] convents. Unfortunately, hardly anything remains of these

[021-169] monuments, where Gothic art combined with so just a balance,

[021-170] richness and economy. The churches (and they were numerous and

[021-171] splendid in the University, and they were graded there also in

[021-172] all the ages of architecture, from the round arches of

[021-173] Saint-Julian to the pointed arches of Saint-Séverin), the

[021-174] churches dominated the whole; and, like one harmony more in this

[021-175] mass of harmonies, they pierced in quick succession the multiple

[021-176] open work of the gables with slashed spires, with open-work bell

[021-177] towers, with slender pinnacles, whose line was also only a

[021-178] magnificent exaggeration of the acute angle of the roofs.

[021-179] The ground of the University was hilly; Mount Sainte-Geneviève

[021-180] formed an enormous mound to the south; and it was a sight to see

[021-181] from the summit of Notre-Dame how that throng of narrow and

[021-182] tortuous streets (to-day the Latin Quarter), those bunches of

[021-183] houses which, spread out in every direction from the top of this

[021-184] eminence, precipitated themselves in disorder, and almost

[021-185] perpendicularly down its flanks, nearly to the water’s edge,

[021-186] having the air, some of falling, others of clambering up again,

[021-187] and all of holding to one another. A continual flux of a thousand

[021-188] black points which passed each other on the pavements made

[021-189] everything move before the eyes; it was the populace seen thus

[021-190] from aloft and afar.

[021-191] Lastly, in the intervals of these roofs, of these spires, of

[021-192] these accidents of numberless edifices, which bent and writhed,

[021-193] and jagged in so eccentric a manner the extreme line of the

[021-194] University, one caught a glimpse, here and there, of a great

[021-195] expanse of moss-grown wall, a thick, round tower, a crenellated

[021-196] city gate, shadowing forth the fortress; it was the wall of

[021-197] Philip Augustus. Beyond, the fields gleamed green; beyond, fled

[021-198] the roads, along which were scattered a few more suburban houses,

[021-199] which became more infrequent as they became more distant. Some of

[021-200] these faubourgs were important: there were, first, starting from

[021-201] la Tournelle, the Bourg Saint-Victor, with its one arch bridge

[021-202] over the Bièvre, its abbey where one could read the epitaph of

[021-203] Louis le Gros, _epitaphium Ludovici Grossi_, and its church with

[021-204] an octagonal spire, flanked with four little bell towers of the

[021-205] eleventh century (a similar one can be seen at Étampes; it is not

[021-206] yet destroyed); next, the Bourg Saint-Marceau, which already had

[021-207] three churches and one convent; then, leaving the mill of the

[021-208] Gobelins and its four white walls on the left, there was the

[021-209] Faubourg Saint-Jacques with the beautiful carved cross in its

[021-210] square; the church of Saint-Jacques du Haut-Pas, which was then

[021-211] Gothic, pointed, charming; Saint-Magloire, a fine nave of the

[021-212] fourteenth century, which Napoleon turned into a hayloft;

[021-213] Notre-Dame des Champs, where there were Byzantine mosaics;

[021-214] lastly, after having left behind, full in the country, the

[021-215] Monastery des Chartreux, a rich edifice contemporary with the

[021-216] Palais de Justice, with its little garden divided into

[021-217] compartments, and the haunted ruins of Vauvert, the eye fell, to

[021-218] the west, upon the three Roman spires of Saint-Germain des Prés.

[021-219] The Bourg Saint-Germain, already a large community, formed

[021-220] fifteen or twenty streets in the rear; the pointed bell tower of

[021-221] Saint-Sulpice marked one corner of the town. Close beside it one

[021-222] descried the quadrilateral enclosure of the fair of

[021-223] Saint-Germain, where the market is situated to-day; then the

[021-224] abbot’s pillory, a pretty little round tower, well capped with a

[021-225] leaden cone; the brickyard was further on, and the Rue du Four,

[021-226] which led to the common bakehouse, and the mill on its hillock,

[021-227] and the lazar house, a tiny house, isolated and half seen.
