---[073-001] “To the sack!” repeated the thieves’ crew; but they dared not

[073-002] approach. They stared at the beam, they stared at the church. The

[073-003] beam did not stir, the edifice preserved its calm and deserted

[073-004] air; but something chilled the outcasts.

[073-005] “To work, locksmiths!” shouted Trouillefou. “Let the door be

[073-006] forced!”

[073-007] No one took a step.

[073-008] “Beard and belly!” said Clopin, “here be men afraid of a beam.”

[073-009] An old locksmith addressed him—

[073-010] “Captain, ’tis not the beam which bothers us, ’tis the door,

[073-011] which is all covered with iron bars. Our pincers are powerless

[073-012] against it.”

[073-013] “What more do you want to break it in?” demanded Clopin.

[073-014] “Ah! we ought to have a battering ram.”

[073-015] The King of Thunes ran boldly to the formidable beam, and placed

[073-016] his foot upon it: “Here is one!” he exclaimed; “’tis the canons

[073-017] who send it to you.” And, making a mocking salute in the

[073-018] direction of the church, “Thanks, canons!”

[073-019] This piece of bravado produced its effects,—the spell of the beam

[073-020] was broken. The vagabonds recovered their courage; soon the heavy

[073-021] joist, raised like a feather by two hundred vigorous arms, was

[073-022] flung with fury against the great door which they had tried to

[073-023] batter down. At the sight of that long beam, in the half-light

[073-024] which the infrequent torches of the brigands spread over the

[073-025] Place, thus borne by that crowd of men who dashed it at a run

[073-026] against the church, one would have thought that he beheld a

[073-027] monstrous beast with a thousand feet attacking with lowered head

[073-028] the giant of stone.

[073-029] At the shock of the beam, the half metallic door sounded like an

[073-030] immense drum; it was not burst in, but the whole cathedral

[073-031] trembled, and the deepest cavities of the edifice were heard to

[073-032] echo.

[073-033] At the same moment, a shower of large stones began to fall from

[073-034] the top of the façade on the assailants.

[073-035] “The devil!” cried Jehan, “are the towers shaking their

[073-036] balustrades down on our heads?”

[073-037] But the impulse had been given, the King of Thunes had set the

[073-038] example. Evidently, the bishop was defending himself, and they

[073-039] only battered the door with the more rage, in spite of the stones

[073-040] which cracked skulls right and left.

[073-041] It was remarkable that all these stones fell one by one; but they

[073-042] followed each other closely. The thieves always felt two at a

[073-043] time, one on their legs and one on their heads. There were few

[073-044] which did not deal their blow, and a large layer of dead and

[073-045] wounded lay bleeding and panting beneath the feet of the

[073-046] assailants who, now grown furious, replaced each other without

[073-047] intermission. The long beam continued to belabor the door, at

[073-048] regular intervals, like the clapper of a bell, the stones to rain

[073-049] down, the door to groan.

[073-050] The reader has no doubt divined that this unexpected resistance

[073-051] which had exasperated the outcasts came from Quasimodo.

[073-052] Chance had, unfortunately, favored the brave deaf man.

[073-053] When he had descended to the platform between the towers, his

[073-054] ideas were all in confusion. He had run up and down along the

[073-055] gallery for several minutes like a madman, surveying from above,

[073-056] the compact mass of vagabonds ready to hurl itself on the church,

[073-057] demanding the safety of the gypsy from the devil or from God. The

[073-058] thought had occurred to him of ascending to the southern belfry

[073-059] and sounding the alarm, but before he could have set the bell in

[073-060] motion, before Marie’s voice could have uttered a single clamor,

[073-061] was there not time to burst in the door of the church ten times

[073-062] over? It was precisely the moment when the locksmiths were

[073-063] advancing upon it with their tools. What was to be done?

[073-064] All at once, he remembered that some masons had been at work all

[073-065] day repairing the wall, the timber-work, and the roof of the

[073-066] south tower. This was a flash of light. The wall was of stone,

[073-067] the roof of lead, the timber-work of wood. (That prodigious

[073-068] timber-work, so dense that it was called “the forest.”)

[073-069] Quasimodo hastened to that tower. The lower chambers were, in

[073-070] fact, full of materials. There were piles of rough blocks of

[073-071] stone, sheets of lead in rolls, bundles of laths, heavy beams

[073-072] already notched with the saw, heaps of plaster.

[073-073] Time was pressing, The pikes and hammers were at work below. With

[073-074] a strength which the sense of danger increased tenfold, he seized

[073-075] one of the beams—the longest and heaviest; he pushed it out

[073-076] through a loophole, then, grasping it again outside of the tower,

[073-077] he made it slide along the angle of the balustrade which

[073-078] surrounds the platform, and let it fly into the abyss. The

[073-079] enormous timber, during that fall of a hundred and sixty feet,

[073-080] scraping the wall, breaking the carvings, turned many times on

[073-081] its centre, like the arm of a windmill flying off alone through

[073-082] space. At last it reached the ground, the horrible cry arose, and

[073-083] the black beam, as it rebounded from the pavement, resembled a

[073-084] serpent leaping.

[073-085] Quasimodo beheld the outcasts scatter at the fall of the beam,

[073-086] like ashes at the breath of a child. He took advantage of their

[073-087] fright, and while they were fixing a superstitious glance on the

[073-088] club which had fallen from heaven, and while they were putting

[073-089] out the eyes of the stone saints on the front with a discharge of

[073-090] arrows and buckshot, Quasimodo was silently piling up plaster,

[073-091] stones, and rough blocks of stone, even the sacks of tools

[073-092] belonging to the masons, on the edge of the balustrade from which

[073-093] the beam had already been hurled.

[073-094] Thus, as soon as they began to batter the grand door, the shower

[073-095] of rough blocks of stone began to fall, and it seemed to them

[073-096] that the church itself was being demolished over their heads.

[073-097] Any one who could have beheld Quasimodo at that moment would have

[073-098] been frightened. Independently of the projectiles which he had

[073-099] piled upon the balustrade, he had collected a heap of stones on

[073-100] the platform itself. As fast as the blocks on the exterior edge

[073-101] were exhausted, he drew on the heap. Then he stooped and rose,

[073-102] stooped and rose again with incredible activity. His huge gnome’s

[073-103] head bent over the balustrade, then an enormous stone fell, then

[073-104] another, then another. From time to time, he followed a fine

[073-105] stone with his eye, and when it did good execution, he said,

[073-106] “Hum!”

[073-107] Meanwhile, the beggars did not grow discouraged. The thick door

[073-108] on which they were venting their fury had already trembled more

[073-109] than twenty times beneath the weight of their oaken

[073-110] battering-ram, multiplied by the strength of a hundred men. The

[073-111] panels cracked, the carved work flew into splinters, the hinges,

[073-112] at every blow, leaped from their pins, the planks yawned, the

[073-113] wood crumbled to powder, ground between the iron sheathing.

[073-114] Fortunately for Quasimodo, there was more iron than wood.

[073-115] Nevertheless, he felt that the great door was yielding. Although

[073-116] he did not hear it, every blow of the ram reverberated

[073-117] simultaneously in the vaults of the church and within it. From

[073-118] above he beheld the vagabonds, filled with triumph and rage,

[073-119] shaking their fists at the gloomy façade; and both on the gypsy’s

[073-120] account and his own he envied the wings of the owls which flitted

[073-121] away above his head in flocks.

[073-122] His shower of stone blocks was not sufficient to repel the

[073-123] assailants.

[073-124] At this moment of anguish, he noticed, a little lower down than

[073-125] the balustrade whence he was crushing the thieves, two long stone

[073-126] gutters which discharged immediately over the great door; the

[073-127] internal orifice of these gutters terminated on the pavement of

[073-128] the platform. An idea occurred to him; he ran in search of a

[073-129] fagot in his bellringer’s den, placed on this fagot a great many

[073-130] bundles of laths, and many rolls of lead, munitions which he had

[073-131] not employed so far, and having arranged this pile in front of

[073-132] the hole to the two gutters, he set it on fire with his lantern.

[073-133] During this time, since the stones no longer fell, the outcasts

[073-134] ceased to gaze into the air. The bandits, panting like a pack of

[073-135] hounds who are forcing a boar into his lair, pressed tumultuously

[073-136] round the great door, all disfigured by the battering ram, but

[073-137] still standing. They were waiting with a quiver for the great

[073-138] blow which should split it open. They vied with each other in

[073-139] pressing as close as possible, in order to dash among the first,

[073-140] when it should open, into that opulent cathedral, a vast

[073-141] reservoir where the wealth of three centuries had been piled up.

[073-142] They reminded each other with roars of exultation and greedy

[073-143] lust, of the beautiful silver crosses, the fine copes of brocade,

[073-144] the beautiful tombs of silver gilt, the great magnificences of

[073-145] the choir, the dazzling festivals, the Christmasses sparkling

[073-146] with torches, the Easters sparkling with sunshine,—all those

[073-147] splendid solemneties wherein chandeliers, ciboriums, tabernacles,

[073-148] and reliquaries, studded the altars with a crust of gold and

[073-149] diamonds. Certainly, at that fine moment, thieves and pseudo

[073-150] sufferers, doctors in stealing, and vagabonds, were thinking much

[073-151] less of delivering the gypsy than of pillaging Notre-Dame. We

[073-152] could even easily believe that for a goodly number among them la

[073-153] Esmeralda was only a pretext, if thieves needed pretexts.

[073-154] All at once, at the moment when they were grouping themselves

[073-155] round the ram for a last effort, each one holding his breath and

[073-156] stiffening his muscles in order to communicate all his force to

[073-157] the decisive blow, a howl more frightful still than that which

[073-158] had burst forth and expired beneath the beam, rose among them.

[073-159] Those who did not cry out, those who were still alive, looked.

[073-160] Two streams of melted lead were falling from the summit of the

[073-161] edifice into the thickest of the rabble. That sea of men had just

[073-162] sunk down beneath the boiling metal, which had made, at the two

[073-163] points where it fell, two black and smoking holes in the crowd,

[073-164] such as hot water would make in snow. Dying men, half consumed

[073-165] and groaning with anguish, could be seen writhing there. Around

[073-166] these two principal streams there were drops of that horrible

[073-167] rain, which scattered over the assailants and entered their

[073-168] skulls like gimlets of fire. It was a heavy fire which

[073-169] overwhelmed these wretches with a thousand hailstones.

[073-170] The outcry was heartrending. They fled pell-mell, hurling the

[073-171] beam upon the bodies, the boldest as well as the most timid, and

[073-172] the parvis was cleared a second time.

[073-173] All eyes were raised to the top of the church. They beheld there

[073-174] an extraordinary sight. On the crest of the highest gallery,

[073-175] higher than the central rose window, there was a great flame

[073-176] rising between the two towers with whirlwinds of sparks, a vast,

[073-177] disordered, and furious flame, a tongue of which was borne into

[073-178] the smoke by the wind, from time to time. Below that fire, below

[073-179] the gloomy balustrade with its trefoils showing darkly against

[073-180] its glare, two spouts with monster throats were vomiting forth

[073-181] unceasingly that burning rain, whose silvery stream stood out

[073-182] against the shadows of the lower façade. As they approached the

[073-183] earth, these two jets of liquid lead spread out in sheaves, like

[073-184] water springing from the thousand holes of a watering-pot. Above

[073-185] the flame, the enormous towers, two sides of each of which were

[073-186] visible in sharp outline, the one wholly black, the other wholly

[073-187] red, seemed still more vast with all the immensity of the shadow

[073-188] which they cast even to the sky.

[073-189] Their innumerable sculptures of demons and dragons assumed a

[073-190] lugubrious aspect. The restless light of the flame made them move

[073-191] to the eye. There were griffins which had the air of laughing,

[073-192] gargoyles which one fancied one heard yelping, salamanders which

[073-193] puffed at the fire, tarasques[61] which sneezed in the smoke. And

[073-194] among the monsters thus roused from their sleep of stone by this

[073-195] flame, by this noise, there was one who walked about, and who was

[073-196] seen, from time to time, to pass across the glowing face of the

[073-197] pile, like a bat in front of a candle.
