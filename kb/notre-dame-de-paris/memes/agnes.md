# Agnes of Burgundy

Agnes of Burgundy is a historical figure briefly referenced in Victor Hugo's
Notre-Dame de Paris. She appears in the novel only as a genealogical footnote —
the mother of a minor character — yet her presence connects the story's
fictional world to the actual dynastic politics of 15th-century France.

## Role in the Novel

Agnes of Burgundy appears in a single passage, identified as the mother of a
character through whom certain privileges pass. Her mention anchors the novel's
social fabric in real French history, lending weight to Hugo's portrayal of
Louis XI's court ([005-194]).

## Biography

### Historical Origins

Agnes of Burgundy (1407–1476) was a historical personage — the daughter of
John the Fearless, Duke of Burgundy, and Margaret of Bavaria. She married
Charles I, Duke of Bourbon, and became the mother of several children who
played roles in French politics during the reign of Louis XI.

### In the Novel

In Notre-Dame de Paris, Agnes is only mentioned in passing, through the
genealogical connection of a character. Hugo uses such historical anchors
sparingly but deliberately, reminding the reader that the fictional events
of 1482 unfold against a backdrop of real dynastic struggles.

## Character Analysis

Agnes of Burgundy functions primarily as a historical reference point rather
than a developed character. Her mention serves Hugo's broader project of
weaving fiction into the documented fabric of medieval Paris, lending
verisimilitude to the novel's world.

## Notable Passages

> Through his mother, Agnes of Burgundy. Now, the dominant influence of the
> house of Burgundy at the court of Louis XI is well known. ([005-194])
