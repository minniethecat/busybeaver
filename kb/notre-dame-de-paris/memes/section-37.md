---[037-001] Mahiette sighed, and wiped away a tear which trickled from her

[037-002] eyes.

[037-003] “This is no very extraordinary history,” said Gervaise, “and in

[037-004] the whole of it I see nothing of any Egyptian women or children.”

[037-005] “Patience!” resumed Mahiette, “you will see one child.—In ’66,

[037-006] ’twill be sixteen years ago this month, at Sainte-Paule’s day,

[037-007] Paquette was brought to bed of a little girl. The unhappy

[037-008] creature! it was a great joy to her; she had long wished for a

[037-009] child. Her mother, good woman, who had never known what to do

[037-010] except to shut her eyes, her mother was dead. Paquette had no

[037-011] longer any one to love in the world or any one to love her. La

[037-012] Chantefleurie had been a poor creature during the five years

[037-013] since her fall. She was alone, alone in this life, fingers were

[037-014] pointed at her, she was hooted at in the streets, beaten by the

[037-015] sergeants, jeered at by the little boys in rags. And then, twenty

[037-016] had arrived: and twenty is an old age for amorous women. Folly

[037-017] began to bring her in no more than her trade of embroidery in

[037-018] former days; for every wrinkle that came, a crown fled; winter

[037-019] became hard to her once more, wood became rare again in her

[037-020] brazier, and bread in her cupboard. She could no longer work

[037-021] because, in becoming voluptuous, she had grown lazy; and she

[037-022] suffered much more because, in growing lazy, she had become

[037-023] voluptuous. At least, that is the way in which monsieur the curé

[037-024] of Saint-Remy explains why these women are colder and hungrier

[037-025] than other poor women, when they are old.”

[037-026] “Yes,” remarked Gervaise, “but the gypsies?”

[037-027] “One moment, Gervaise!” said Oudarde, whose attention was less

[037-028] impatient. “What would be left for the end if all were in the

[037-029] beginning? Continue, Mahiette, I entreat you. That poor

[037-030] Chantefleurie!”

[037-031] Mahiette went on.

[037-032] “So she was very sad, very miserable, and furrowed her cheeks

[037-033] with tears. But in the midst of her shame, her folly, her

[037-034] debauchery, it seemed to her that she should be less wild, less

[037-035] shameful, less dissipated, if there were something or some one in

[037-036] the world whom she could love, and who could love her. It was

[037-037] necessary that it should be a child, because only a child could

[037-038] be sufficiently innocent for that. She had recognized this fact

[037-039] after having tried to love a thief, the only man who wanted her;

[037-040] but after a short time, she perceived that the thief despised

[037-041] her. Those women of love require either a lover or a child to

[037-042] fill their hearts. Otherwise, they are very unhappy. As she could

[037-043] not have a lover, she turned wholly towards a desire for a child,

[037-044] and as she had not ceased to be pious, she made her constant

[037-045] prayer to the good God for it. So the good God took pity on her,

[037-046] and gave her a little daughter. I will not speak to you of her

[037-047] joy; it was a fury of tears, and caresses, and kisses. She nursed

[037-048] her child herself, made swaddling-bands for it out of her

[037-049] coverlet, the only one which she had on her bed, and no longer

[037-050] felt either cold or hunger. She became beautiful once more, in

[037-051] consequence of it. An old maid makes a young mother. Gallantry

[037-052] claimed her once more; men came to see la Chantefleurie; she

[037-053] found customers again for her merchandise, and out of all these

[037-054] horrors she made baby clothes, caps and bibs, bodices with

[037-055] shoulder-straps of lace, and tiny bonnets of satin, without even

[037-056] thinking of buying herself another coverlet.—Master Eustache, I

[037-057] have already told you not to eat that cake.—It is certain that

[037-058] little Agnès, that was the child’s name, a baptismal name, for it

[037-059] was a long time since la Chantefleurie had had any surname—it is

[037-060] certain that that little one was more swathed in ribbons and

[037-061] embroideries than a dauphiness of Dauphiny! Among other things,

[037-062] she had a pair of little shoes, the like of which King Louis XI.

[037-063] certainly never had! Her mother had stitched and embroidered them

[037-064] herself; she had lavished on them all the delicacies of her art

[037-065] of embroideress, and all the embellishments of a robe for the

[037-066] good Virgin. They certainly were the two prettiest little pink

[037-067] shoes that could be seen. They were no longer than my thumb, and

[037-068] one had to see the child’s little feet come out of them, in order

[037-069] to believe that they had been able to get into them. ’Tis true

[037-070] that those little feet were so small, so pretty, so rosy! rosier

[037-071] than the satin of the shoes! When you have children, Oudarde, you

[037-072] will find that there is nothing prettier than those little hands

[037-073] and feet.”

[037-074] “I ask no better,” said Oudarde with a sigh, “but I am waiting

[037-075] until it shall suit the good pleasure of M. Andry Musnier.”

[037-076] “However, Paquette’s child had more that was pretty about it

[037-077] besides its feet. I saw her when she was only four months old;

[037-078] she was a love! She had eyes larger than her mouth, and the most

[037-079] charming black hair, which already curled. She would have been a

[037-080] magnificent brunette at the age of sixteen! Her mother became

[037-081] more crazy over her every day. She kissed her, caressed her,

[037-082] tickled her, washed her, decked her out, devoured her! She lost

[037-083] her head over her, she thanked God for her. Her pretty, little

[037-084] rosy feet above all were an endless source of wonderment, they

[037-085] were a delirium of joy! She was always pressing her lips to them,

[037-086] and she could never recover from her amazement at their

[037-087] smallness. She put them into the tiny shoes, took them out,

[037-088] admired them, marvelled at them, looked at the light through

[037-089] them, was curious to see them try to walk on her bed, and would

[037-090] gladly have passed her life on her knees, putting on and taking

[037-091] off the shoes from those feet, as though they had been those of

[037-092] an Infant Jesus.”

[037-093] “The tale is fair and good,” said Gervaise in a low tone; “but

[037-094] where do gypsies come into all that?”

[037-095] “Here,” replied Mahiette. “One day there arrived in Reims a very

[037-096] queer sort of people. They were beggars and vagabonds who were

[037-097] roaming over the country, led by their duke and their counts.

[037-098] They were browned by exposure to the sun, they had closely

[037-099] curling hair, and silver rings in their ears. The women were

[037-100] still uglier than the men. They had blacker faces, which were

[037-101] always uncovered, a miserable frock on their bodies, an old cloth

[037-102] woven of cords bound upon their shoulder, and their hair hanging

[037-103] like the tail of a horse. The children who scrambled between

[037-104] their legs would have frightened as many monkeys. A band of

[037-105] excommunicates. All these persons came direct from lower Egypt to

[037-106] Reims through Poland. The Pope had confessed them, it was said,

[037-107] and had prescribed to them as penance to roam through the world

[037-108] for seven years, without sleeping in a bed; and so they were

[037-109] called penancers, and smelt horribly. It appears that they had

[037-110] formerly been Saracens, which was why they believed in Jupiter,

[037-111] and claimed ten livres of Tournay from all archbishops, bishops,

[037-112] and mitred abbots with croziers. A bull from the Pope empowered

[037-113] them to do that. They came to Reims to tell fortunes in the name

[037-114] of the King of Algiers, and the Emperor of Germany. You can

[037-115] readily imagine that no more was needed to cause the entrance to

[037-116] the town to be forbidden them. Then the whole band camped with

[037-117] good grace outside the gate of Braine, on that hill where stands

[037-118] a mill, beside the cavities of the ancient chalk pits. And

[037-119] everybody in Reims vied with his neighbor in going to see them.

[037-120] They looked at your hand, and told you marvellous prophecies;

[037-121] they were equal to predicting to Judas that he would become Pope.

[037-122] Nevertheless, ugly rumors were in circulation in regard to them;

[037-123] about children stolen, purses cut, and human flesh devoured. The

[037-124] wise people said to the foolish: “Don’t go there!” and then went

[037-125] themselves on the sly. It was an infatuation. The fact is, that

[037-126] they said things fit to astonish a cardinal. Mothers triumphed

[037-127] greatly over their little ones after the Egyptians had read in

[037-128] their hands all sorts of marvels written in pagan and in Turkish.

[037-129] One had an emperor; another, a pope; another, a captain. Poor

[037-130] Chantefleurie was seized with curiosity; she wished to know about

[037-131] herself, and whether her pretty little Agnès would not become

[037-132] some day Empress of Armenia, or something else. So she carried

[037-133] her to the Egyptians; and the Egyptian women fell to admiring the

[037-134] child, and to caressing it, and to kissing it with their black

[037-135] mouths, and to marvelling over its little band, alas! to the

[037-136] great joy of the mother. They were especially enthusiastic over

[037-137] her pretty feet and shoes. The child was not yet a year old. She

[037-138] already lisped a little, laughed at her mother like a little mad

[037-139] thing, was plump and quite round, and possessed a thousand

[037-140] charming little gestures of the angels of paradise.

[037-141] “She was very much frightened by the Egyptians, and wept. But her

[037-142] mother kissed her more warmly and went away enchanted with the

[037-143] good fortune which the soothsayers had foretold for her Agnès.

[037-144] She was to be a beauty, virtuous, a queen. So she returned to her

[037-145] attic in the Rue Folle-Peine, very proud of bearing with her a

[037-146] queen. The next day she took advantage of a moment when the child

[037-147] was asleep on her bed, (for they always slept together), gently

[037-148] left the door a little way open, and ran to tell a neighbor in

[037-149] the Rue de la Séchesserie, that the day would come when her

[037-150] daughter Agnès would be served at table by the King of England

[037-151] and the Archduke of Ethiopia, and a hundred other marvels. On her

[037-152] return, hearing no cries on the staircase, she said to herself:

[037-153] ‘Good! the child is still asleep!’ She found her door wider open

[037-154] than she had left it, but she entered, poor mother, and ran to

[037-155] the bed.—The child was no longer there, the place was empty.

[037-156] Nothing remained of the child, but one of her pretty little

[037-157] shoes. She flew out of the room, dashed down the stairs, and

[037-158] began to beat her head against the wall, crying: ‘My child! who

[037-159] has my child? Who has taken my child?’ The street was deserted,

[037-160] the house isolated; no one could tell her anything about it. She

[037-161] went about the town, searched all the streets, ran hither and

[037-162] thither the whole day long, wild, beside herself, terrible,

[037-163] snuffing at doors and windows like a wild beast which has lost

[037-164] its young. She was breathless, dishevelled, frightful to see, and

[037-165] there was a fire in her eyes which dried her tears. She stopped

[037-166] the passers-by and cried: ‘My daughter! my daughter! my pretty

[037-167] little daughter! If any one will give me back my daughter, I will

[037-168] be his servant, the servant of his dog, and he shall eat my heart

[037-169] if he will.’ She met M. le Curé of Saint-Remy, and said to him:

[037-170] ‘Monsieur, I will till the earth with my finger-nails, but give

[037-171] me back my child!’ It was heartrending, Oudarde; and I saw a very

[037-172] hard man, Master Ponce Lacabre, the procurator, weep. Ah! poor

[037-173] mother! In the evening she returned home. During her absence, a

[037-174] neighbor had seen two gypsies ascend up to it with a bundle in

[037-175] their arms, then descend again, after closing the door. After

[037-176] their departure, something like the cries of a child were heard

[037-177] in Paquette’s room. The mother, burst into shrieks of laughter,

[037-178] ascended the stairs as though on wings, and entered.—A frightful

[037-179] thing to tell, Oudarde! Instead of her pretty little Agnès, so

[037-180] rosy and so fresh, who was a gift of the good God, a sort of

[037-181] hideous little monster, lame, one-eyed, deformed, was crawling

[037-182] and squalling over the floor. She hid her eyes in horror. ‘Oh!’

[037-183] said she, ‘have the witches transformed my daughter into this

[037-184] horrible animal?’ They hastened to carry away the little

[037-185] club-foot; he would have driven her mad. It was the monstrous

[037-186] child of some gypsy woman, who had given herself to the devil. He

[037-187] appeared to be about four years old, and talked a language which

[037-188] was no human tongue; there were words in it which were

[037-189] impossible. La Chantefleurie flung herself upon the little shoe,

[037-190] all that remained to her of all that she loved. She remained so

[037-191] long motionless over it, mute, and without breath, that they

[037-192] thought she was dead. Suddenly she trembled all over, covered her

[037-193] relic with furious kisses, and burst out sobbing as though her

[037-194] heart were broken. I assure you that we were all weeping also.

[037-195] She said: ‘Oh, my little daughter! my pretty little daughter!

[037-196] where art thou?’—and it wrung your very heart. I weep still when

[037-197] I think of it. Our children are the marrow of our bones, you

[037-198] see.—My poor Eustache! thou art so fair!—If you only knew how

[037-199] nice he is! yesterday he said to me: ‘I want to be a gendarme,

[037-200] that I do.’ Oh! my Eustache! if I were to lose thee!—All at once

[037-201] la Chantefleurie rose, and set out to run through Reims,

[037-202] screaming: ‘To the gypsies’ camp! to the gypsies’ camp! Police,

[037-203] to burn the witches!’ The gypsies were gone. It was pitch dark.

[037-204] They could not be followed. On the morrow, two leagues from

[037-205] Reims, on a heath between Gueux and Tilloy, the remains of a

[037-206] large fire were found, some ribbons which had belonged to

[037-207] Paquette’s child, drops of blood, and the dung of a ram. The

[037-208] night just past had been a Saturday. There was no longer any

[037-209] doubt that the Egyptians had held their Sabbath on that heath,

[037-210] and that they had devoured the child in company with Beelzebub,

[037-211] as the practice is among the Mahometans. When La Chantefleurie

[037-212] learned these horrible things, she did not weep, she moved her

[037-213] lips as though to speak, but could not. On the morrow, her hair

[037-214] was gray. On the second day, she had disappeared.
