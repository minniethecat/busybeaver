---[041-001] He beheld her, in fact, mount the ladder rapidly. Wrath and spite

[041-002] suffocate him. He would have liked to make the pillory crumble

[041-003] into ruins, and if the lightning of his eye could have dealt

[041-004] death, the gypsy would have been reduced to powder before she

[041-005] reached the platform.

[041-006] She approached, without uttering a syllable, the victim who

[041-007] writhed in a vain effort to escape her, and detaching a gourd

[041-008] from her girdle, she raised it gently to the parched lips of the

[041-009] miserable man.

[041-010] Then, from that eye which had been, up to that moment, so dry and

[041-011] burning, a big tear was seen to fall, and roll slowly down that

[041-012] deformed visage so long contracted with despair. It was the

[041-013] first, in all probability, that the unfortunate man had ever

[041-014] shed.

[041-015] Meanwhile, he had forgotten to drink. The gypsy made her little

[041-016] pout, from impatience, and pressed the spout to the tusked month

[041-017] of Quasimodo, with a smile.

[041-018] He drank with deep draughts. His thirst was burning.

[041-019] When he had finished, the wretch protruded his black lips, no

[041-020] doubt, with the object of kissing the beautiful hand which had

[041-021] just succoured him. But the young girl, who was, perhaps,

[041-022] somewhat distrustful, and who remembered the violent attempt of

[041-023] the night, withdrew her hand with the frightened gesture of a

[041-024] child who is afraid of being bitten by a beast.

[041-025] Then the poor deaf man fixed on her a look full of reproach and

[041-026] inexpressible sadness.

[041-027] It would have been a touching spectacle anywhere,—this beautiful,

[041-028] fresh, pure, and charming girl, who was at the same time so weak,

[041-029] thus hastening to the relief of so much misery, deformity, and

[041-030] malevolence. On the pillory, the spectacle was sublime.

[041-031] The very populace were captivated by it, and began to clap their

[041-032] hands, crying,—

[041-033] “Noël! Noël!”

[041-034] It was at that moment that the recluse caught sight, from the

[041-035] window of her bole, of the gypsy on the pillory, and hurled at

[041-036] her her sinister imprecation,—

[041-037] “Accursed be thou, daughter of Egypt! Accursed! accursed!”

[041-038] CHAPTER V. END OF THE STORY OF THE CAKE.

[041-039] La Esmeralda turned pale and descended from the pillory,

[041-040] staggering as she went. The voice of the recluse still pursued

[041-041] her,—

[041-042] “Descend! descend! Thief of Egypt! thou shalt ascend it once

[041-043] more!”

[041-044] “The sacked nun is in one of her tantrums,” muttered the

[041-045] populace; and that was the end of it. For that sort of woman was

[041-046] feared; which rendered them sacred. People did not then willingly

[041-047] attack one who prayed day and night.

[041-048] The hour had arrived for removing Quasimodo. He was unbound, the

[041-049] crowd dispersed.

[041-050] Near the Grand Pont, Mahiette, who was returning with her two

[041-051] companions, suddenly halted,—

[041-052] “By the way, Eustache! what did you do with that cake?”

[041-053] “Mother,” said the child, “while you were talking with that lady

[041-054] in the bole, a big dog took a bite of my cake, and then I bit it

[041-055] also.”

[041-056] “What, sir, did you eat the whole of it?” she went on.

[041-057] “Mother, it was the dog. I told him, but he would not listen to

[041-058] me. Then I bit into it, also.”

[041-059] “’Tis a terrible child!” said the mother, smiling and scolding at

[041-060] one and the same time. “Do you see, Oudarde? He already eats all

[041-061] the fruit from the cherry-tree in our orchard of Charlerange. So

[041-062] his grandfather says that he will be a captain. Just let me catch

[041-063] you at it again, Master Eustache. Come along, you greedy fellow!”

[041-064] VOLUME II.

[041-065] BOOK SEVENTH.

[041-066] CHAPTER I. THE DANGER OF CONFIDING ONE’S SECRET TO A GOAT.

[041-067] Many weeks had elapsed.

[041-068] The first of March had arrived. The sun, which Dubartas, that

[041-069] classic ancestor of periphrase, had not yet dubbed the

[041-070] “Grand-duke of Candles,” was none the less radiant and joyous on

[041-071] that account. It was one of those spring days which possesses so

[041-072] much sweetness and beauty, that all Paris turns out into the

[041-073] squares and promenades and celebrates them as though they were

[041-074] Sundays. In those days of brilliancy, warmth, and serenity, there

[041-075] is a certain hour above all others, when the façade of Notre-Dame

[041-076] should be admired. It is the moment when the sun, already

[041-077] declining towards the west, looks the cathedral almost full in

[041-078] the face. Its rays, growing more and more horizontal, withdraw

[041-079] slowly from the pavement of the square, and mount up the

[041-080] perpendicular façade, whose thousand bosses in high relief they

[041-081] cause to start out from the shadows, while the great central rose

[041-082] window flames like the eye of a cyclops, inflamed with the

[041-083] reflections of the forge.

[041-084] This was the hour.

[041-085] Opposite the lofty cathedral, reddened by the setting sun, on the

[041-086] stone balcony built above the porch of a rich Gothic house, which

[041-087] formed the angle of the square and the Rue du Parvis, several

[041-088] young girls were laughing and chatting with every sort of grace

[041-089] and mirth. From the length of the veil which fell from their

[041-090] pointed coif, twined with pearls, to their heels, from the

[041-091] fineness of the embroidered chemisette which covered their

[041-092] shoulders and allowed a glimpse, according to the pleasing custom

[041-093] of the time, of the swell of their fair virgin bosoms, from the

[041-094] opulence of their under-petticoats still more precious than their

[041-095] overdress (marvellous refinement), from the gauze, the silk, the

[041-096] velvet, with which all this was composed, and, above all, from

[041-097] the whiteness of their hands, which certified to their leisure

[041-098] and idleness, it was easy to divine they were noble and wealthy

[041-099] heiresses. They were, in fact, Damoiselle Fleur-de-Lys de

[041-100] Gondelaurier and her companions, Diane de Christeuil, Amelotte de

[041-101] Montmichel, Colombe de Gaillefontaine, and the little de

[041-102] Champchevrier maiden; all damsels of good birth, assembled at

[041-103] that moment at the house of the dame widow de Gondelaurier, on

[041-104] account of Monseigneur de Beaujeu and Madame his wife, who were

[041-105] to come to Paris in the month of April, there to choose maids of

[041-106] honor for the Dauphiness Marguerite, who was to be received in

[041-107] Picardy from the hands of the Flemings. Now, all the squires for

[041-108] twenty leagues around were intriguing for this favor for their

[041-109] daughters, and a goodly number of the latter had been already

[041-110] brought or sent to Paris. These four maidens had been confided to

[041-111] the discreet and venerable charge of Madame Aloïse de

[041-112] Gondelaurier, widow of a former commander of the king’s

[041-113] cross-bowmen, who had retired with her only daughter to her house

[041-114] in the Place du Parvis, Notre-Dame, in Paris.

[041-115] The balcony on which these young girls stood opened from a

[041-116] chamber richly tapestried in fawn-colored Flanders leather,

[041-117] stamped with golden foliage. The beams, which cut the ceiling in

[041-118] parallel lines, diverted the eye with a thousand eccentric

[041-119] painted and gilded carvings. Splendid enamels gleamed here and

[041-120] there on carved chests; a boar’s head in faïence crowned a

[041-121] magnificent dresser, whose two shelves announced that the

[041-122] mistress of the house was the wife or widow of a knight banneret.

[041-123] At the end of the room, by the side of a lofty chimney blazoned

[041-124] with arms from top to bottom, in a rich red velvet arm-chair, sat

[041-125] Dame de Gondelaurier, whose five and fifty years were written

[041-126] upon her garments no less distinctly than upon her face.

[041-127] Beside her stood a young man of imposing mien, although partaking

[041-128] somewhat of vanity and bravado—one of those handsome fellows whom

[041-129] all women agree to admire, although grave men learned in

[041-130] physiognomy shrug their shoulders at them. This young man wore

[041-131] the garb of a captain of the king’s unattached archers, which

[041-132] bears far too much resemblance to the costume of Jupiter, which

[041-133] the reader has already been enabled to admire in the first book

[041-134] of this history, for us to inflict upon him a second description.

[041-135] The damoiselles were seated, a part in the chamber, a part in the

[041-136] balcony, some on square cushions of Utrecht velvet with golden

[041-137] corners, others on stools of oak carved in flowers and figures.

[041-138] Each of them held on her knee a section of a great needlework

[041-139] tapestry, on which they were working in company, while one end of

[041-140] it lay upon the rush mat which covered the floor.

[041-141] They were chatting together in that whispering tone and with the

[041-142] half-stifled laughs peculiar to an assembly of young girls in

[041-143] whose midst there is a young man. The young man whose presence

[041-144] served to set in play all these feminine self-conceits, appeared

[041-145] to pay very little heed to the matter, and, while these pretty

[041-146] damsels were vying with one another to attract his attention, he

[041-147] seemed to be chiefly absorbed in polishing the buckle of his

[041-148] sword belt with his doeskin glove. From time to time, the old

[041-149] lady addressed him in a very low tone, and he replied as well as

[041-150] he was able, with a sort of awkward and constrained politeness.

[041-151] From the smiles and significant gestures of Dame Aloïse, from the

[041-152] glances which she threw towards her daughter, Fleur-de-Lys, as

[041-153] she spoke low to the captain, it was easy to see that there was

[041-154] here a question of some betrothal concluded, some marriage near

[041-155] at hand no doubt, between the young man and Fleur-de-Lys. From

[041-156] the embarrassed coldness of the officer, it was easy to see that

[041-157] on his side, at least, love had no longer any part in the matter.

[041-158] His whole air was expressive of constraint and weariness, which

[041-159] our lieutenants of the garrison would to-day translate admirably

[041-160] as, “What a beastly bore!”

[041-161] The poor dame, very much infatuated with her daughter, like any

[041-162] other silly mother, did not perceive the officer’s lack of

[041-163] enthusiasm, and strove in low tones to call his attention to the

[041-164] infinite grace with which Fleur-de-Lys used her needle or wound

[041-165] her skein.

[041-166] “Come, little cousin,” she said to him, plucking him by the

[041-167] sleeve, in order to speak in his ear, “Look at her, do! see her

[041-168] stoop.”

[041-169] “Yes, truly,” replied the young man, and fell back into his

[041-170] glacial and absent-minded silence.

[041-171] A moment later, he was obliged to bend down again, and Dame

[041-172] Aloïse said to him,—

[041-173] “Have you ever beheld a more gay and charming face than that of

[041-174] your betrothed? Can one be more white and blonde? are not her

[041-175] hands perfect? and that neck—does it not assume all the curves of

[041-176] the swan in ravishing fashion? How I envy you at times! and how

[041-177] happy you are to be a man, naughty libertine that you are! Is not

[041-178] my Fleur-de-Lys adorably beautiful, and are you not desperately

[041-179] in love with her?”

[041-180] “Of course,” he replied, still thinking of something else.

[041-181] “But do say something,” said Madame Aloïse, suddenly giving his

[041-182] shoulder a push; “you have grown very timid.”

[041-183] We can assure our readers that timidity was neither the captain’s

[041-184] virtue nor his defect. But he made an effort to do what was

[041-185] demanded of him.

[041-186] “Fair cousin,” he said, approaching Fleur-de-Lys, “what is the

[041-187] subject of this tapestry work which you are fashioning?”

[041-188] “Fair cousin,” responded Fleur-de-Lys, in an offended tone, “I

[041-189] have already told you three times. ’Tis the grotto of Neptune.”

[041-190] It was evident that Fleur-de-Lys saw much more clearly than her

[041-191] mother through the captain’s cold and absent-minded manner. He

[041-192] felt the necessity of making some conversation.

[041-193] “And for whom is this Neptunerie destined?”

[041-194] “For the Abbey of Saint-Antoine des Champs,” answered

[041-195] Fleur-de-Lys, without raising her eyes.

[041-196] The captain took up a corner of the tapestry.

[041-197] “Who, my fair cousin, is this big gendarme, who is puffing out

[041-198] his cheeks to their full extent and blowing a trumpet?”

[041-199] “’Tis Triton,” she replied.

[041-200] There was a rather pettish intonation in Fleur-de-Lys’s laconic

[041-201] words. The young man understood that it was indispensable that he

[041-202] should whisper something in her ear, a commonplace, a gallant

[041-203] compliment, no matter what. Accordingly he bent down, but he

[041-204] could find nothing in his imagination more tender and personal

[041-205] than this,—

[041-206] “Why does your mother always wear that surcoat with armorial

[041-207] designs, like our grandmothers of the time of Charles VII.? Tell

[041-208] her, fair cousin, that ’tis no longer the fashion, and that the

[041-209] hinge (_gond_) and the laurel (_laurier_) embroidered on her robe

[041-210] give her the air of a walking mantlepiece. In truth, people no

[041-211] longer sit thus on their banners, I assure you.”
