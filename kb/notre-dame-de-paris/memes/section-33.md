---[033-001] That edifice is colossal. Some compiler of statistics has

[033-002] calculated, that if all the volumes which have issued from the

[033-003] press since Gutenberg’s day were to be piled one upon another,

[033-004] they would fill the space between the earth and the moon; but it

[033-005] is not that sort of grandeur of which we wished to speak.

[033-006] Nevertheless, when one tries to collect in one’s mind a

[033-007] comprehensive image of the total products of printing down to our

[033-008] own days, does not that total appear to us like an immense

[033-009] construction, resting upon the entire world, at which humanity

[033-010] toils without relaxation, and whose monstrous crest is lost in

[033-011] the profound mists of the future? It is the anthill of

[033-012] intelligence. It is the hive whither come all imaginations, those

[033-013] golden bees, with their honey.

[033-014] The edifice has a thousand stories. Here and there one beholds on

[033-015] its staircases the gloomy caverns of science which pierce its

[033-016] interior. Everywhere upon its surface, art causes its arabesques,

[033-017] rosettes, and laces to thrive luxuriantly before the eyes. There,

[033-018] every individual work, however capricious and isolated it may

[033-019] seem, has its place and its projection. Harmony results from the

[033-020] whole. From the cathedral of Shakespeare to the mosque of Byron,

[033-021] a thousand tiny bell towers are piled pell-mell above this

[033-022] metropolis of universal thought. At its base are written some

[033-023] ancient titles of humanity which architecture had not registered.

[033-024] To the left of the entrance has been fixed the ancient

[033-025] bas-relief, in white marble, of Homer; to the right, the polyglot

[033-026] Bible rears its seven heads. The hydra of the Romancero and some

[033-027] other hybrid forms, the Vedas and the Nibelungen bristle further

[033-028] on.

[033-029] Nevertheless, the prodigious edifice still remains incomplete.

[033-030] The press, that giant machine, which incessantly pumps all the

[033-031] intellectual sap of society, belches forth without pause fresh

[033-032] materials for its work. The whole human race is on the

[033-033] scaffoldings. Each mind is a mason. The humblest fills his hole,

[033-034] or places his stone. Rétif de La Bretonne brings his hod of

[033-035] plaster. Every day a new course rises. Independently of the

[033-036] original and individual contribution of each writer, there are

[033-037] collective contingents. The eighteenth century gives the

[033-038] _Encyclopedia_, the revolution gives the _Moniteur_. Assuredly,

[033-039] it is a construction which increases and piles up in endless

[033-040] spirals; there also are confusion of tongues, incessant activity,

[033-041] indefatigable labor, eager competition of all humanity, refuge

[033-042] promised to intelligence, a new Flood against an overflow of

[033-043] barbarians. It is the second tower of Babel of the human race.

[033-044] BOOK SIXTH.

[033-045] CHAPTER I. AN IMPARTIAL GLANCE AT THE ANCIENT MAGISTRACY.

[033-046] A very happy personage in the year of grace 1482, was the noble

[033-047] gentleman Robert d’Estouteville, chevalier, Sieur de Beyne, Baron

[033-048] d’Ivry and Saint Andry en la Marche, counsellor and chamberlain

[033-049] to the king, and guard of the provostship of Paris. It was

[033-050] already nearly seventeen years since he had received from the

[033-051] king, on November 7, 1465, the comet year,[27] that fine charge

[033-052] of the provostship of Paris, which was reputed rather a seigneury

[033-053] than an office. _Dignitas_, says Joannes Lœmnœus, _quæ cum non

[033-054] exigua potestate politiam concernente, atque prærogativis multis

[033-055] et juribus conjuncta est_. A marvellous thing in ’82 was a

[033-056] gentleman bearing the king’s commission, and whose letters of

[033-057] institution ran back to the epoch of the marriage of the natural

[033-058] daughter of Louis XI. with Monsieur the Bastard of Bourbon.

[033-059] The same day on which Robert d’Estouteville took the place of

[033-060] Jacques de Villiers in the provostship of Paris, Master Jehan

[033-061] Dauvet replaced Messire Helye de Thorrettes in the first

[033-062] presidency of the Court of Parliament, Jehan Jouvenel des Ursins

[033-063] supplanted Pierre de Morvilliers in the office of chancellor of

[033-064] France, Regnault des Dormans ousted Pierre Puy from the charge of

[033-065] master of requests in ordinary of the king’s household. Now, upon

[033-066] how many heads had the presidency, the chancellorship, the

[033-067] mastership passed since Robert d’Estouteville had held the

[033-068] provostship of Paris. It had been “granted to him for

[033-069] safekeeping,” as the letters patent said; and certainly he kept

[033-070] it well. He had clung to it, he had incorporated himself with it,

[033-071] he had so identified himself with it that he had escaped that

[033-072] fury for change which possessed Louis XI., a tormenting and

[033-073] industrious king, whose policy it was to maintain the elasticity

[033-074] of his power by frequent appointments and revocations. More than

[033-075] this; the brave chevalier had obtained the reversion of the

[033-076] office for his son, and for two years already, the name of the

[033-077] noble man Jacques d’Estouteville, equerry, had figured beside his

[033-078] at the head of the register of the salary list of the provostship

[033-079] of Paris. A rare and notable favor indeed! It is true that Robert

[033-080] d’Estouteville was a good soldier, that he had loyally raised his

[033-081] pennon against “the league of public good,” and that he had

[033-082] presented to the queen a very marvellous stag in confectionery on

[033-083] the day of her entrance to Paris in 14.... Moreover, he possessed

[033-084] the good friendship of Messire Tristan l’Hermite, provost of the

[033-085] marshals of the king’s household. Hence a very sweet and pleasant

[033-086] existence was that of Messire Robert. In the first place, very

[033-087] good wages, to which were attached, and from which hung, like

[033-088] extra bunches of grapes on his vine, the revenues of the civil

[033-089] and criminal registries of the provostship, plus the civil and

[033-090] criminal revenues of the tribunals of Embas of the Châtelet,

[033-091] without reckoning some little toll from the bridges of Mantes and

[033-092] of Corbeil, and the profits on the craft of Shagreen-makers of

[033-093] Paris, on the corders of firewood and the measurers of salt. Add

[033-094] to this the pleasure of displaying himself in rides about the

[033-095] city, and of making his fine military costume, which you may

[033-096] still admire sculptured on his tomb in the abbey of Valmont in

[033-097] Normandy, and his morion, all embossed at Montlhéry, stand out a

[033-098] contrast against the parti-colored red and tawny robes of the

[033-099] aldermen and police. And then, was it nothing to wield absolute

[033-100] supremacy over the sergeants of the police, the porter and watch

[033-101] of the Châtelet, the two auditors of the Châtelet, _auditores

[033-102] castelleti_, the sixteen commissioners of the sixteen quarters,

[033-103] the jailer of the Châtelet, the four enfeoffed sergeants, the

[033-104] hundred and twenty mounted sergeants, with maces, the chevalier

[033-105] of the watch with his watch, his sub-watch, his counter-watch and

[033-106] his rear-watch? Was it nothing to exercise high and low justice,

[033-107] the right to interrogate, to hang and to draw, without reckoning

[033-108] petty jurisdiction in the first resort (_in prima instantia_, as

[033-109] the charters say), on that viscomty of Paris, so nobly appanaged

[033-110] with seven noble bailiwicks? Can anything sweeter be imagined

[033-111] than rendering judgments and decisions, as Messire Robert

[033-112] d’Estouteville daily did in the Grand Châtelet, under the large

[033-113] and flattened arches of Philip Augustus? and going, as he was

[033-114] wont to do every evening, to that charming house situated in the

[033-115] Rue Galilée, in the enclosure of the royal palace, which he held

[033-116] in right of his wife, Madame Ambroise de Loré, to repose after

[033-117] the fatigue of having sent some poor wretch to pass the night in

[033-118] “that little cell of the Rue de Escorcherie, which the provosts

[033-119] and aldermen of Paris used to make their prison; the same being

[033-120] eleven feet long, seven feet and four inches wide, and eleven

[033-121] feet high?”[28]

[033-122] And not only had Messire Robert d’Estouteville his special court

[033-123] as provost and vicomte of Paris; but in addition he had a share,

[033-124] both for eye and tooth, in the grand court of the king. There was

[033-125] no head in the least elevated which had not passed through his

[033-126] hands before it came to the headsman. It was he who went to seek

[033-127] M. de Nemours at the Bastille Saint Antoine, in order to conduct

[033-128] him to the Halles; and to conduct to the Grève M. de Saint-Pol,

[033-129] who clamored and resisted, to the great joy of the provost, who

[033-130] did not love monsieur the constable.

[033-131] Here, assuredly, is more than sufficient to render a life happy

[033-132] and illustrious, and to deserve some day a notable page in that

[033-133] interesting history of the provosts of Paris, where one learns

[033-134] that Oudard de Villeneuve had a house in the Rue des Boucheries,

[033-135] that Guillaume de Hangest purchased the great and the little

[033-136] Savoy, that Guillaume Thiboust gave the nuns of Sainte-Geneviève

[033-137] his houses in the Rue Clopin, that Hugues Aubriot lived in the

[033-138] Hôtel du Porc-Épic, and other domestic facts.

[033-139] Nevertheless, with so many reasons for taking life patiently and

[033-140] joyously, Messire Robert d’Estouteville woke up on the morning of

[033-141] the seventh of January, 1482, in a very surly and peevish mood.

[033-142] Whence came this ill temper? He could not have told himself. Was

[033-143] it because the sky was gray? or was the buckle of his old belt of

[033-144] Montlhéry badly fastened, so that it confined his provostal

[033-145] portliness too closely? had he beheld ribald fellows, marching in

[033-146] bands of four, beneath his window, and setting him at defiance,

[033-147] in doublets but no shirts, hats without crowns, with wallet and

[033-148] bottle at their side? Was it a vague presentiment of the three

[033-149] hundred and seventy livres, sixteen sous, eight farthings, which

[033-150] the future King Charles VII. was to cut off from the provostship

[033-151] in the following year? The reader can take his choice; we, for

[033-152] our part, are much inclined to believe that he was in a bad

[033-153] humor, simply because he was in a bad humor.

[033-154] Moreover, it was the day after a festival, a tiresome day for

[033-155] every one, and above all for the magistrate who is charged with

[033-156] sweeping away all the filth, properly and figuratively speaking,

[033-157] which a festival day produces in Paris. And then he had to hold a

[033-158] sitting at the Grand Châtelet. Now, we have noticed that judges

[033-159] in general so arrange matters that their day of audience shall

[033-160] also be their day of bad humor, so that they may always have some

[033-161] one upon whom to vent it conveniently, in the name of the king,

[033-162] law, and justice.

[033-163] However, the audience had begun without him. His lieutenants,

[033-164] civil, criminal, and private, were doing his work, according to

[033-165] usage; and from eight o’clock in the morning, some scores of

[033-166] _bourgeois_ and _bourgeoises_, heaped and crowded into an obscure

[033-167] corner of the audience chamber of Embas du Châtelet, between a

[033-168] stout oaken barrier and the wall, had been gazing blissfully at

[033-169] the varied and cheerful spectacle of civil and criminal justice

[033-170] dispensed by Master Florian Barbedienne, auditor of the Châtelet,

[033-171] lieutenant of monsieur the provost, in a somewhat confused and

[033-172] utterly haphazard manner.

[033-173] The hall was small, low, vaulted. A table studded with

[033-174] fleurs-de-lis stood at one end, with a large arm-chair of carved

[033-175] oak, which belonged to the provost and was empty, and a stool on

[033-176] the left for the auditor, Master Florian. Below sat the clerk of

[033-177] the court, scribbling; opposite was the populace; and in front of

[033-178] the door, and in front of the table were many sergeants of the

[033-179] provostship in sleeveless jackets of violet camlet, with white

[033-180] crosses. Two sergeants of the Parloir-aux-Bourgeois, clothed in

[033-181] their jackets of Toussaint, half red, half blue, were posted as

[033-182] sentinels before a low, closed door, which was visible at the

[033-183] extremity of the hall, behind the table. A single pointed window,

[033-184] narrowly encased in the thick wall, illuminated with a pale ray

[033-185] of January sun two grotesque figures,—the capricious demon of

[033-186] stone carved as a tail-piece in the keystone of the vaulted

[033-187] ceiling, and the judge seated at the end of the hall on the

[033-188] fleurs-de-lis.

[033-189] Imagine, in fact, at the provost’s table, leaning upon his elbows

[033-190] between two bundles of documents of cases, with his foot on the

[033-191] train of his robe of plain brown cloth, his face buried in his

[033-192] hood of white lamb’s skin, of which his brows seemed to be of a

[033-193] piece, red, crabbed, winking, bearing majestically the load of

[033-194] fat on his cheeks which met under his chin, Master Florian

[033-195] Barbedienne, auditor of the Châtelet.
