---[076-001] He paused breathless, then resumed energetically,—

[076-002] “I behold around me only people who fatten on my leanness! you

[076-003] suck crowns from me at every pore.”

[076-004] All remained silent. This was one of those fits of wrath which

[076-005] are allowed to take their course. He continued,—

[076-006] “’Tis like that request in Latin from the gentlemen of France,

[076-007] that we should re-establish what they call the grand charges of

[076-008] the Crown! Charges in very deed! Charges which crush! Ah!

[076-009] gentlemen! you say that we are not a king to reign _dapifero

[076-010] nullo, buticulario nullo!_ We will let you see, _pasque-Dieu!_

[076-011] whether we are not a king!”

[076-012] Here he smiled, in the consciousness of his power; this softened

[076-013] his bad humor, and he turned towards the Flemings,—

[076-014] “Do you see, Gossip Guillaume? the grand warden of the keys, the

[076-015] grand butler, the grand chamberlain, the grand seneschal are not

[076-016] worth the smallest valet. Remember this, Gossip Coppenole. They

[076-017] serve no purpose, as they stand thus useless round the king; they

[076-018] produce upon me the effect of the four Evangelists who surround

[076-019] the face of the big clock of the palace, and which Philippe

[076-020] Brille has just set in order afresh. They are gilt, but they do

[076-021] not indicate the hour; and the hands can get on without them.”

[076-022] He remained in thought for a moment, then added, shaking his aged

[076-023] head,—

[076-024] “Ho! ho! by our Lady, I am not Philippe Brille, and I shall not

[076-025] gild the great vassals anew. Continue, Olivier.”

[076-026] The person whom he designated by this name, took the papers into

[076-027] his hands again, and began to read aloud,—

[076-028] “To Adam Tenon, clerk of the warden of the seals of the

[076-029] provostship of Paris; for the silver, making, and engraving of

[076-030] said seals, which have been made new because the others

[076-031] preceding, by reason of their antiquity and their worn condition,

[076-032] could no longer be successfully used, twelve livres parisis.

[076-033] “To Guillaume Frère, the sum of four livres, four sols parisis,

[076-034] for his trouble and salary, for having nourished and fed the

[076-035] doves in the two dove-cots of the Hôtel des Tournelles, during

[076-036] the months of January, February, and March of this year; and for

[076-037] this he hath given seven sextiers of barley.

[076-038] “To a gray friar for confessing a criminal, four sols parisis.”

[076-039] The king listened in silence. From time to time he coughed; then

[076-040] he raised the goblet to his lips and drank a draught with a

[076-041] grimace.

[076-042] “During this year there have been made by the ordinance of

[076-043] justice, to the sound of the trumpet, through the squares of

[076-044] Paris, fifty-six proclamations. Account to be regulated.

[076-045] “For having searched and ransacked in certain places, in Paris as

[076-046] well as elsewhere, for money said to be there concealed; but

[076-047] nothing hath been found: forty-five livres parisis.”

[076-048] “Bury a crown to unearth a sou!” said the king.

[076-049] “For having set in the Hôtel des Tournelles six panes of white

[076-050] glass in the place where the iron cage is, thirteen sols; for

[076-051] having made and delivered by command of the king, on the day of

[076-052] the musters, four shields with the escutcheons of the said

[076-053] seigneur, encircled with garlands of roses all about, six livres;

[076-054] for two new sleeves to the king’s old doublet, twenty sols; for a

[076-055] box of grease to grease the boots of the king, fifteen deniers; a

[076-056] stable newly made to lodge the king’s black pigs, thirty livres

[076-057] parisis; many partitions, planks, and trap-doors, for the

[076-058] safekeeping of the lions at Saint-Paul, twenty-two livres.”

[076-059] “These be dear beasts,” said Louis XI. “It matters not; it is a

[076-060] fine magnificence in a king. There is a great red lion whom I

[076-061] love for his pleasant ways. Have you seen him, Master Guillaume?

[076-062] Princes must have these terrific animals; for we kings must have

[076-063] lions for our dogs and tigers for our cats. The great befits a

[076-064] crown. In the days of the pagans of Jupiter, when the people

[076-065] offered the temples a hundred oxen and a hundred sheep, the

[076-066] emperors gave a hundred lions and a hundred eagles. This was wild

[076-067] and very fine. The kings of France have always had roarings round

[076-068] their throne. Nevertheless, people must do me this justice, that

[076-069] I spend still less money on it than they did, and that I possess

[076-070] a greater modesty of lions, bears, elephants, and leopards.—Go

[076-071] on, Master Olivier. We wished to say thus much to our Flemish

[076-072] friends.”

[076-073] Guillaume Rym bowed low, while Coppenole, with his surly mien,

[076-074] had the air of one of the bears of which his majesty was

[076-075] speaking. The king paid no heed. He had just dipped his lips into

[076-076] the goblet, and he spat out the beverage, saying: “Foh! what a

[076-077] disagreeable potion!” The man who was reading continued:—

[076-078] “For feeding a rascally footpad, locked up these six months in

[076-079] the little cell of the flayer, until it should be determined what

[076-080] to do with him, six livres, four sols.”

[076-081] “What’s that?” interrupted the king; “feed what ought to be

[076-082] hanged! _Pasque-Dieu!_ I will give not a sou more for that

[076-083] nourishment. Olivier, come to an understanding about the matter

[076-084] with Monsieur d’Estouteville, and prepare me this very evening

[076-085] the wedding of the gallant and the gallows. Resume.”

[076-086] Olivier made a mark with his thumb against the article of the

[076-087] “rascally foot soldier,” and passed on.

[076-088] “To Henriet Cousin, master executor of the high works of justice

[076-089] in Paris, the sum of sixty sols parisis, to him assessed and

[076-090] ordained by monseigneur the provost of Paris, for having bought,

[076-091] by order of the said sieur the provost, a great broad sword,

[076-092] serving to execute and decapitate persons who are by justice

[076-093] condemned for their demerits, and he hath caused the same to be

[076-094] garnished with a sheath and with all things thereto appertaining;

[076-095] and hath likewise caused to be repointed and set in order the old

[076-096] sword, which had become broken and notched in executing justice

[076-097] on Messire Louis de Luxembourg, as will more fully appear....”

[076-098] The king interrupted: “That suffices. I allow the sum with great

[076-099] good will. Those are expenses which I do not begrudge. I have

[076-100] never regretted that money. Continue.”

[076-101] “For having made over a great cage....”

[076-102] “Ah!” said the king, grasping the arms of his chair in both

[076-103] hands, “I knew well that I came hither to this Bastille for some

[076-104] purpose. Hold, Master Olivier; I desire to see that cage myself.

[076-105] You shall read me the cost while I am examining it. Messieurs

[076-106] Flemings, come and see this; ’tis curious.”

[076-107] Then he rose, leaned on the arm of his interlocutor, made a sign

[076-108] to the sort of mute who stood before the door to precede him, to

[076-109] the two Flemings to follow him, and quitted the room.

[076-110] The royal company was recruited, at the door of the retreat, by

[076-111] men of arms, all loaded down with iron, and by slender pages

[076-112] bearing flambeaux. It marched for some time through the interior

[076-113] of the gloomy donjon, pierced with staircases and corridors even

[076-114] in the very thickness of the walls. The captain of the Bastille

[076-115] marched at their head, and caused the wickets to be opened before

[076-116] the bent and aged king, who coughed as he walked.

[076-117] At each wicket, all heads were obliged to stoop, except that of

[076-118] the old man bent double with age. “Hum,” said he between his

[076-119] gums, for he had no longer any teeth, “we are already quite

[076-120] prepared for the door of the sepulchre. For a low door, a bent

[076-121] passer.”

[076-122] At length, after having passed a final wicket, so loaded with

[076-123] locks that a quarter of an hour was required to open it, they

[076-124] entered a vast and lofty vaulted hall, in the centre of which

[076-125] they could distinguish by the light of the torches, a huge cubic

[076-126] mass of masonry, iron, and wood. The interior was hollow. It was

[076-127] one of those famous cages of prisoners of state, which were

[076-128] called “the little daughters of the king.” In its walls there

[076-129] were two or three little windows so closely trellised with stout

[076-130] iron bars; that the glass was not visible. The door was a large

[076-131] flat slab of stone, as on tombs; the sort of door which serves

[076-132] for entrance only. Only here, the occupant was alive.

[076-133] The king began to walk slowly round the little edifice, examining

[076-134] it carefully, while Master Olivier, who followed him, read aloud

[076-135] the note.

[076-136] “For having made a great cage of wood of solid beams, timbers and

[076-137] wall-plates, measuring nine feet in length by eight in breadth,

[076-138] and of the height of seven feet between the partitions, smoothed

[076-139] and clamped with great bolts of iron, which has been placed in a

[076-140] chamber situated in one of the towers of the Bastille

[076-141] Saint-Antoine, in which cage is placed and detained, by command

[076-142] of the king our lord, a prisoner who formerly inhabited an old,

[076-143] decrepit, and ruined cage. There have been employed in making the

[076-144] said new cage, ninety-six horizontal beams, and fifty-two upright

[076-145] joists, ten wall plates three toises long; there have been

[076-146] occupied nineteen carpenters to hew, work, and fit all the said

[076-147] wood in the courtyard of the Bastille during twenty days.”

[076-148] “Very fine heart of oak,” said the king, striking the woodwork

[076-149] with his fist.

[076-150] “There have been used in this cage,” continued the other, “two

[076-151] hundred and twenty great bolts of iron, of nine feet, and of

[076-152] eight, the rest of medium length, with the rowels, caps and

[076-153] counterbands appertaining to the said bolts; weighing, the said

[076-154] iron in all, three thousand, seven hundred and thirty-five

[076-155] pounds; beside eight great squares of iron, serving to attach the

[076-156] said cage in place with clamps and nails weighing in all two

[076-157] hundred and eighteen pounds, not reckoning the iron of the

[076-158] trellises for the windows of the chamber wherein the cage hath

[076-159] been placed, the bars of iron for the door of the cage and other

[076-160] things.”

[076-161] “’Tis a great deal of iron,” said the king, “to contain the light

[076-162] of a spirit.”

[076-163] “The whole amounts to three hundred and seventeen livres, five

[076-164] sols, seven deniers.”

[076-165] “_Pasque-Dieu!_” exclaimed the king.

[076-166] At this oath, which was the favorite of Louis XI., some one

[076-167] seemed to awaken in the interior of the cage; the sound of chains

[076-168] was heard, grating on the floor, and a feeble voice, which seemed

[076-169] to issue from the tomb was uplifted. “Sire! sire! mercy!” The one

[076-170] who spoke thus could not be seen.

[076-171] “Three hundred and seventeen livres, five sols, seven deniers,”

[076-172] repeated Louis XI. The lamentable voice which had proceeded from

[076-173] the cage had frozen all present, even Master Olivier himself. The

[076-174] king alone wore the air of not having heard. At his order, Master

[076-175] Olivier resumed his reading, and his majesty coldly continued his

[076-176] inspection of the cage.

[076-177] “In addition to this there hath been paid to a mason who hath

[076-178] made the holes wherein to place the gratings of the windows, and

[076-179] the floor of the chamber where the cage is, because that floor

[076-180] could not support this cage by reason of its weight, twenty-seven

[076-181] livres fourteen sols parisis.”

[076-182] The voice began to moan again.

[076-183] “Mercy, sire! I swear to you that ’twas Monsieur the Cardinal

[076-184] d’Angers and not I, who was guilty of treason.”

[076-185] “The mason is bold!” said the king. “Continue, Olivier.”

[076-186] Olivier continued,—

[076-187] “To a joiner for window frames, bedstead, hollow stool, and other

[076-188] things, twenty livres, two sols parisis.”

[076-189] The voice also continued.

[076-190] “Alas, sire! will you not listen to me? I protest to you that

[076-191] ’twas not I who wrote the matter to Monseigneur de Guyenne, but

[076-192] Monsieur le Cardinal Balue.”

[076-193] “The joiner is dear,” quoth the king. “Is that all?”

[076-194] “No, sire. To a glazier, for the windows of the said chamber,

[076-195] forty-six sols, eight deniers parisis.”

[076-196] “Have mercy, sire! Is it not enough to have given all my goods to

[076-197] my judges, my plate to Monsieur de Torcy, my library to Master

[076-198] Pierre Doriolle, my tapestry to the governor of the Roussillon? I

[076-199] am innocent. I have been shivering in an iron cage for fourteen

[076-200] years. Have mercy, sire! You will find your reward in heaven.”
