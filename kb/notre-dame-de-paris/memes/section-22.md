---[022-001] But that which attracted the eye most of all, and fixed it for a

[022-002] long time on that point, was the abbey itself. It is certain that

[022-003] this monastery, which had a grand air, both as a church and as a

[022-004] seignory; that abbatial palace, where the bishops of Paris

[022-005] counted themselves happy if they could pass the night; that

[022-006] refectory, upon which the architect had bestowed the air, the

[022-007] beauty, and the rose window of a cathedral; that elegant chapel

[022-008] of the Virgin; that monumental dormitory; those vast gardens;

[022-009] that portcullis; that drawbridge; that envelope of battlements

[022-010] which notched to the eye the verdure of the surrounding meadows;

[022-011] those courtyards, where gleamed men at arms, intermingled with

[022-012] golden copes;—the whole grouped and clustered about three lofty

[022-013] spires, with round arches, well planted upon a Gothic apse, made

[022-014] a magnificent figure against the horizon.

[022-015] When, at length, after having contemplated the University for a

[022-016] long time, you turned towards the right bank, towards the Town,

[022-017] the character of the spectacle was abruptly altered. The Town, in

[022-018] fact much larger than the University, was also less of a unit. At

[022-019] the first glance, one saw that it was divided into many masses,

[022-020] singularly distinct. First, to the eastward, in that part of the

[022-021] town which still takes its name from the marsh where Camulogènes

[022-022] entangled Cæsar, was a pile of palaces. The block extended to the

[022-023] very water’s edge. Four almost contiguous hotels, Jouy, Sens,

[022-024] Barbeau, the house of the Queen, mirrored their slate peaks,

[022-025] broken with slender turrets, in the Seine.

[022-026] These four edifices filled the space from the Rue des

[022-027] Nonaindières, to the abbey of the Celestins, whose spire

[022-028] gracefully relieved their line of gables and battlements. A few

[022-029] miserable, greenish hovels, hanging over the water in front of

[022-030] these sumptuous hotels, did not prevent one from seeing the fine

[022-031] angles of their façades, their large, square windows with stone

[022-032] mullions, their pointed porches overloaded with statues, the

[022-033] vivid outlines of their walls, always clear cut, and all those

[022-034] charming accidents of architecture, which cause Gothic art to

[022-035] have the air of beginning its combinations afresh with every

[022-036] monument.

[022-037] Behind these palaces, extended in all directions, now broken,

[022-038] fenced in, battlemented like a citadel, now veiled by great trees

[022-039] like a Carthusian convent, the immense and multiform enclosure of

[022-040] that miraculous Hôtel de Saint-Pol, where the King of France

[022-041] possessed the means of lodging superbly two and twenty princes of

[022-042] the rank of the dauphin and the Duke of Burgundy, with their

[022-043] domestics and their suites, without counting the great lords, and

[022-044] the emperor when he came to view Paris, and the lions, who had

[022-045] their separate hotel at the royal hotel. Let us say here that a

[022-046] prince’s apartment was then composed of never less than eleven

[022-047] large rooms, from the chamber of state to the oratory, not to

[022-048] mention the galleries, baths, vapor-baths, and other “superfluous

[022-049] places,” with which each apartment was provided; not to mention

[022-050] the private gardens for each of the king’s guests; not to mention

[022-051] the kitchens, the cellars, the domestic offices, the general

[022-052] refectories of the house, the poultry-yards, where there were

[022-053] twenty-two general laboratories, from the bakehouses to the

[022-054] wine-cellars; games of a thousand sorts, malls, tennis, and

[022-055] riding at the ring; aviaries, fishponds, menageries, stables,

[022-056] barns, libraries, arsenals and foundries. This was what a king’s

[022-057] palace, a Louvre, a Hôtel de Saint-Pol was then. A city within a

[022-058] city.

[022-059] From the tower where we are placed, the Hôtel Saint-Pol, almost

[022-060] half hidden by the four great houses of which we have just

[022-061] spoken, was still very considerable and very marvellous to see.

[022-062] One could there distinguish, very well, though cleverly united

[022-063] with the principal building by long galleries, decked with

[022-064] painted glass and slender columns, the three hotels which Charles

[022-065] V. had amalgamated with his palace: the Hôtel du Petit-Muce, with

[022-066] the airy balustrade, which formed a graceful border to its roof;

[022-067] the Hôtel of the Abbé de Saint-Maur, having the vanity of a

[022-068] stronghold, a great tower, machicolations, loopholes, iron

[022-069] gratings, and over the large Saxon door, the armorial bearings of

[022-070] the abbé, between the two mortises of the drawbridge; the Hôtel

[022-071] of the Comte d’Étampes, whose donjon keep, ruined at its summit,

[022-072] was rounded and notched like a cock’s comb; here and there, three

[022-073] or four ancient oaks, forming a tuft together like enormous

[022-074] cauliflowers; gambols of swans, in the clear water of the

[022-075] fishponds, all in folds of light and shade; many courtyards of

[022-076] which one beheld picturesque bits; the Hôtel of the Lions, with

[022-077] its low, pointed arches on short, Saxon pillars, its iron

[022-078] gratings and its perpetual roar; shooting up above the whole, the

[022-079] scale-ornamented spire of the Ave-Maria; on the left, the house

[022-080] of the Provost of Paris, flanked by four small towers, delicately

[022-081] grooved, in the middle; at the extremity, the Hôtel Saint-Pol,

[022-082] properly speaking, with its multiplied façades, its successive

[022-083] enrichments from the time of Charles V., the hybrid excrescences,

[022-084] with which the fancy of the architects had loaded it during the

[022-085] last two centuries, with all the apses of its chapels, all the

[022-086] gables of its galleries, a thousand weathercocks for the four

[022-087] winds, and its two lofty contiguous towers, whose conical roof,

[022-088] surrounded by battlements at its base, looked like those pointed

[022-089] caps which have their edges turned up.

[022-090] Continuing to mount the stories of this amphitheatre of palaces

[022-091] spread out afar upon the ground, after crossing a deep ravine

[022-092] hollowed out of the roofs in the Town, which marked the passage

[022-093] of the Rue Saint-Antoine, the eye reached the house of Angoulême,

[022-094] a vast construction of many epochs, where there were perfectly

[022-095] new and very white parts, which melted no better into the whole

[022-096] than a red patch on a blue doublet. Nevertheless, the remarkably

[022-097] pointed and lofty roof of the modern palace, bristling with

[022-098] carved eaves, covered with sheets of lead, where coiled a

[022-099] thousand fantastic arabesques of sparkling incrustations of

[022-100] gilded bronze, that roof, so curiously damascened, darted upwards

[022-101] gracefully from the midst of the brown ruins of the ancient

[022-102] edifice; whose huge and ancient towers, rounded by age like

[022-103] casks, sinking together with old age, and rending themselves from

[022-104] top to bottom, resembled great bellies unbuttoned. Behind rose

[022-105] the forest of spires of the Palais des Tournelles. Not a view in

[022-106] the world, either at Chambord or at the Alhambra, is more magic,

[022-107] more aerial, more enchanting, than that thicket of spires, tiny

[022-108] bell towers, chimneys, weather-vanes, winding staircases,

[022-109] lanterns through which the daylight makes its way, which seem cut

[022-110] out at a blow, pavilions, spindle-shaped turrets, or, as they

[022-111] were then called, _tournelles_, all differing in form, in height,

[022-112] and attitude. One would have pronounced it a gigantic stone

[022-113] chess-board.

[022-114] To the right of the Tournelles, that truss of enormous towers,

[022-115] black as ink, running into each other and tied, as it were, by a

[022-116] circular moat; that donjon keep, much more pierced with loopholes

[022-117] than with windows; that drawbridge, always raised; that

[022-118] portcullis, always lowered,—is the Bastille. Those sorts of black

[022-119] beaks which project from between the battlements, and which you

[022-120] take from a distance to be cave spouts, are cannons.

[022-121] Beneath them, at the foot of the formidable edifice, behold the

[022-122] Porte Sainte-Antoine, buried between its two towers.

[022-123] Beyond the Tournelles, as far as the wall of Charles V., spread

[022-124] out, with rich compartments of verdure and of flowers, a velvet

[022-125] carpet of cultivated land and royal parks, in the midst of which

[022-126] one recognized, by its labyrinth of trees and alleys, the famous

[022-127] Dædalus garden which Louis XI. had given to Coictier. The

[022-128] doctor’s observatory rose above the labyrinth like a great

[022-129] isolated column, with a tiny house for a capital. Terrible

[022-130] astrologies took place in that laboratory.

[022-131] There to-day is the Place Royale.

[022-132] As we have just said, the quarter of the palace, of which we have

[022-133] just endeavored to give the reader some idea by indicating only

[022-134] the chief points, filled the angle which Charles V.’s wall made

[022-135] with the Seine on the east. The centre of the Town was occupied

[022-136] by a pile of houses for the populace. It was there, in fact, that

[022-137] the three bridges disgorged upon the right bank, and bridges lead

[022-138] to the building of houses rather than palaces. That congregation

[022-139] of _bourgeois_ habitations, pressed together like the cells in a

[022-140] hive, had a beauty of its own. It is with the roofs of a capital

[022-141] as with the waves of the sea,—they are grand. First the streets,

[022-142] crossed and entangled, forming a hundred amusing figures in the

[022-143] block; around the market-place, it was like a star with a

[022-144] thousand rays.

[022-145] The Rues Saint-Denis and Saint-Martin, with their innumerable

[022-146] ramifications, rose one after the other, like trees intertwining

[022-147] their branches; and then the tortuous lines, the Rues de la

[022-148] Plâtrerie, de la Verrerie, de la Tixeranderie, etc., meandered

[022-149] over all. There were also fine edifices which pierced the

[022-150] petrified undulations of that sea of gables. At the head of the

[022-151] Pont aux Changeurs, behind which one beheld the Seine foaming

[022-152] beneath the wheels of the Pont aux Meuniers, there was the

[022-153] Châlelet, no longer a Roman tower, as under Julian the Apostate,

[022-154] but a feudal tower of the thirteenth century, and of a stone so

[022-155] hard that the pickaxe could not break away so much as the

[022-156] thickness of the fist in a space of three hours; there was the

[022-157] rich square bell tower of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie, with its

[022-158] angles all frothing with carvings, already admirable, although it

[022-159] was not finished in the fifteenth century. (It lacked, in

[022-160] particular, the four monsters, which, still perched to-day on the

[022-161] corners of its roof, have the air of so many sphinxes who are

[022-162] propounding to new Paris the riddle of the ancient Paris. Rault,

[022-163] the sculptor, only placed them in position in 1526, and received

[022-164] twenty francs for his pains.) There was the Maison-aux-Piliers,

[022-165] the Pillar House, opening upon that Place de Grève of which we

[022-166] have given the reader some idea; there was Saint-Gervais, which a

[022-167] front “in good taste” has since spoiled; Saint-Méry, whose

[022-168] ancient pointed arches were still almost round arches;

[022-169] Saint-Jean, whose magnificent spire was proverbial; there were

[022-170] twenty other monuments, which did not disdain to bury their

[022-171] wonders in that chaos of black, deep, narrow streets. Add the

[022-172] crosses of carved stone, more lavishly scattered through the

[022-173] squares than even the gibbets; the cemetery of the Innocents,

[022-174] whose architectural wall could be seen in the distance above the

[022-175] roofs; the pillory of the Markets, whose top was visible between

[022-176] two chimneys of the Rue de la Cossonnerie; the ladder of the

[022-177] Croix-du-Trahoir, in its square always black with people; the

[022-178] circular buildings of the wheat mart; the fragments of Philip

[022-179] Augustus’s ancient wall, which could be made out here and there,

[022-180] drowned among the houses, its towers gnawed by ivy, its gates in

[022-181] ruins, with crumbling and deformed stretches of wall; the quay

[022-182] with its thousand shops, and its bloody knacker’s yards; the

[022-183] Seine encumbered with boats, from the Port au Foin to

[022-184] For-l’Évêque, and you will have a confused picture of what the

[022-185] central trapezium of the Town was like in 1482.

[022-186] With these two quarters, one of hotels, the other of houses, the

[022-187] third feature of aspect presented by the city was a long zone of

[022-188] abbeys, which bordered it in nearly the whole of its

[022-189] circumference, from the rising to the setting sun, and, behind

[022-190] the circle of fortifications which hemmed in Paris, formed a

[022-191] second interior enclosure of convents and chapels. Thus,

[022-192] immediately adjoining the park des Tournelles, between the Rue

[022-193] Saint-Antoine and the Vieille Rue du Temple, there stood

[022-194] Sainte-Catherine, with its immense cultivated lands, which were

[022-195] terminated only by the wall of Paris. Between the old and the new

[022-196] Rue du Temple, there was the Temple, a sinister group of towers,

[022-197] lofty, erect, and isolated in the middle of a vast, battlemented

[022-198] enclosure. Between the Rue Neuve-du-Temple and the Rue

[022-199] Saint-Martin, there was the Abbey of Saint-Martin, in the midst

[022-200] of its gardens, a superb fortified church, whose girdle of

[022-201] towers, whose diadem of bell towers, yielded in force and

[022-202] splendor only to Saint-Germain des Prés. Between the Rue

[022-203] Saint-Martin and the Rue Saint-Denis, spread the enclosure of the

[022-204] Trinité.
