# Andry Musnier

Master Andry Musnier is a minor figure in the crowded opening tableaux of
the novel. He appears in two brief references: as a grumbling citizen in the
[[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] crowd — "the devil take you! growled Master Andry Musnier"
[003-014] — and in a later reference that places him in the social network
of [[paris|Paris]]ian merchants and citizens [037-075]. His surname links him to
[[oudarde-musnier|Oudarde Musnier]], suggesting a family of some substance in the [[paris|Paris]]ian
bourgeoisie [038-100].

## Role in the Novel

Andry Musnier is part of the atmospheric crowd that Hugo assembles in the
[[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] for the novel's opening. His function is demographic, not
dramatic: he is one of the hundreds of faces that give the crowd its texture,
a representative of the respectable citizenry whose presence legitimates the
ceremonial proceedings — and whose grumbling underscores their tedium.

## Biography

### A Citizen of Paris

The name Andry Musnier appears twice in the novel, always in connection with
the commercial and professional life of [[paris|Paris]]. A "Master Andry Musnier's"
establishment is mentioned on the Rue Madame [038-100], suggesting a shop
or residence of some note. The title "Master" indicates membership in the
artisan or merchant elite, the class of citizens who possessed guild
privileges and municipal standing.

### Key Appearance

In the great hall, amid the chaos of the crowd waiting for the mystery play,
Andry Musnier growls his irritation [003-014]. The moment passes instantly
— Hugo does not linger on him — but the growl is part of the symphony of
noises that constitutes the crowd, a single note in the polyphony of the
novel's opening.

### The Musnier Family

[[oudarde-musnier|Oudarde Musnier]], who appears in the scene at the Tour-Roland and
participates in the story of [[sister-gudule|Paquette la Chantefleurie]] ([[sister-gudule]]),
shares the Musnier surname and likely represents a branch of the same
bourgeois family. The recurrence of the name across different social
contexts — the [[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] crowd, the women at the Tour-Roland,
a commercial establishment on the Rue Madame — suggests a family network
typical of the [[paris|Paris]]ian merchant class.

### Fate

Andry Musnier's [[fatalite|fate]] is not narrated. He dissolves back into the crowd from
which he briefly emerged.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Andry Musnier is given only one trait: irritability. His "growl" is his sole
individualizing detail. This is deliberate — Hugo uses him as a pixel in
a larger picture, not as a portrait. The citizen who grumbles about the
delay is a universal figure, instantly recognizable, requiring no further
development.

### Relationships

- **[[oudarde-musnier|Oudarde Musnier]]**: The shared surname suggests a family
  connection — perhaps wife and husband, or brother and sister — spanning
  the novel's different social spheres.

## Significance

Andry Musnier exemplifies Hugo's technique of populating his novel with
named figures who appear once and are gone, creating the impression of a
world too vast for any single narrative to contain. He is a technique of
realism: the novel's background characters have names and professions and
familial connections, just as they would in life, even if the story has no
use for them beyond a moment. The name "Musnier" — miller — is itself
emblematic of Hugo's method: ordinary [[paris|Paris]]ian trades, rendered with the
specificity of a city directory, building the texture of a lost world.
