# Sainte-Chapelle

The Sainte-Chapelle (Holy Chapel) is a royal Gothic chapel on the Île de la
Cité, built by Louis IX in the 13th century to house sacred relics including
the Crown of Thorns. In Notre-Dame de Paris, Hugo mentions it as a prominent
feature of the medieval Paris skyline, visible from the towers of Notre-Dame
and from the streets below.

## Historical Context (15th Century)

By 1482, the Sainte-Chapelle had stood for over two centuries. Built between
1242 and 1248, it was the private chapel of the French kings within the Palais
de la Cité complex. Its soaring stained-glass windows — 15 panels rising 15
meters — made it one of the most luminous spaces in medieval Christendom. Hugo
references its spire as a landmark in his panoramic descriptions of Paris
([002-158]).

## Role in the Novel

The Sainte-Chapelle appears primarily in Hugo's architectural descriptions,
notably in the famous chapter "A Bird's-Eye View of Paris" (Book III, Chapter 2),
where the narrator surveys the city from the towers of Notre-Dame. The chapel
is evoked as part of the dense fabric of ecclesiastical architecture that
defines the Île de la Cité ([019-018], [021-059]).

## Architectural Significance

The Sainte-Chapelle represents the Rayonnant Gothic style at its purest. Hugo
mentions it alongside Notre-Dame as evidence of the architectural genius of
the Middle Ages — the very genius he argues is threatened by the printing press
in "Ceci Tuera Cela." Its spire, described as "the spire of the Sainte-Chapelle,
like an elephant's haunches," contributes to the silhouette of medieval Paris
([021-091]).

## Notable Passages
