# Tour de Nesle

The Tour de Nesle was one of the four principal corner towers of the medieval wall of [[paris|Paris]] built under King Philip II Augustus. Standing on the left bank of the [[seine-river|Seine]] across from the Tour du Louvre on the right bank, the tower marked the western limit of [[paris|the city]]'s fortifications.

## Physical Description

The tower was a massive cylindrical stone structure, originally part of the Hôtel de Nesle, a fortified residence. Together with the Tour du Coin, the Tour de Bois, and the Tour du Louvre, it formed a quadrilateral of watchtowers that guarded [[paris|medieval Paris]]. The chain stretched across the Seine between the Tour de Nesle and the Tour du Louvre served as a river barrier in times of danger.

## In the Novel

The Tour de Nesle appears in Book III, Chapter 2, where Hugo presents his famous panoramic description of [[paris|Paris]] as seen from the towers of [[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame]]. He traces the course of the Seine and the landmarks along its banks, describing the university quarter stretching "from the Tournelle to the Tour de Nesle" ((020-096)), marking the southern boundary of [[paris|the city]]'s intellectual and scholastic world.

This geographical reference serves to establish the scale and layout of [[paris|medieval Paris]] in the reader's mind, situating the novel's action within a precisely imagined [[paris|urban landscape]]. The Tour de Nesle, along with other towers and gates, is part of Hugo's argument that medieval [[architecture-in-notre-dame|architecture]] was the "great book of humanity" — each stone a page in the record of civilization.

## Historical Basis

The historical Tour de Nesle was built around 1200 as part of Philip Augustus's wall and was associated with the infamous "Tour de Nesle Affair" of 1314, a scandal involving the daughters-in-[[law-and-justice|law]] of King Philip IV. The tower was eventually demolished in the 17th century to make way for the Collège des Quatre-Nations (now the Institut de France).
