---[023-001] Lastly, between the Rue Saint-Denis, and the Rue Montorgueil,

[023-002] stood the Filles-Dieu. On one side, the rotting roofs and unpaved

[023-003] enclosure of the Cour des Miracles could be descried. It was the

[023-004] sole profane ring which was linked to that devout chain of

[023-005] convents.

[023-006] Finally, the fourth compartment, which stretched itself out in

[023-007] the agglomeration of the roofs on the right bank, and which

[023-008] occupied the western angle of the enclosure, and the banks of the

[023-009] river down stream, was a fresh cluster of palaces and hôtels

[023-010] pressed close about the base of the Louvre. The old Louvre of

[023-011] Philip Augustus, that immense edifice whose great tower rallied

[023-012] about it three and twenty chief towers, not to reckon the lesser

[023-013] towers, seemed from a distance to be enshrined in the Gothic

[023-014] roofs of the Hôtel d’Alençon, and the Petit-Bourbon. This hydra

[023-015] of towers, giant guardian of Paris, with its four and twenty

[023-016] heads, always erect, with its monstrous haunches, loaded or

[023-017] scaled with slates, and all streaming with metallic reflections,

[023-018] terminated with wonderful effect the configuration of the Town

[023-019] towards the west.

[023-020] Thus an immense block, which the Romans called _insula_, or

[023-021] island, of _bourgeois_ houses, flanked on the right and the left

[023-022] by two blocks of palaces, crowned, the one by the Louvre, the

[023-023] other by the Tournelles, bordered on the north by a long girdle

[023-024] of abbeys and cultivated enclosures, all amalgamated and melted

[023-025] together in one view; upon these thousands of edifices, whose

[023-026] tiled and slated roofs outlined upon each other so many fantastic

[023-027] chains, the bell towers, tattooed, fluted, and ornamented with

[023-028] twisted bands, of the four and forty churches on the right bank;

[023-029] myriads of cross streets; for boundary on one side, an enclosure

[023-030] of lofty walls with square towers (that of the University had

[023-031] round towers); on the other, the Seine, cut by bridges, and

[023-032] bearing on its bosom a multitude of boats; behold the Town of

[023-033] Paris in the fifteenth century.

[023-034] Beyond the walls, several suburban villages pressed close about

[023-035] the gates, but less numerous and more scattered than those of the

[023-036] University. Behind the Bastille there were twenty hovels

[023-037] clustered round the curious sculptures of the Croix-Faubin and

[023-038] the flying buttresses of the Abbey of Saint-Antoine des Champs;

[023-039] then Popincourt, lost amid wheat fields; then la Courtille, a

[023-040] merry village of wine-shops; the hamlet of Saint-Laurent with its

[023-041] church whose bell tower, from afar, seemed to add itself to the

[023-042] pointed towers of the Porte Saint-Martin; the Faubourg

[023-043] Saint-Denis, with the vast enclosure of Saint-Ladre; beyond the

[023-044] Montmartre Gate, the Grange-Batelière, encircled with white

[023-045] walls; behind it, with its chalky slopes, Montmartre, which had

[023-046] then almost as many churches as windmills, and which has kept

[023-047] only the windmills, for society no longer demands anything but

[023-048] bread for the body. Lastly, beyond the Louvre, the Faubourg

[023-049] Saint-Honoré, already considerable at that time, could be seen

[023-050] stretching away into the fields, and Petit-Bretagne gleaming

[023-051] green, and the Marché aux Pourceaux spreading abroad, in whose

[023-052] centre swelled the horrible apparatus used for boiling

[023-053] counterfeiters. Between la Courtille and Saint-Laurent, your eye

[023-054] had already noticed, on the summit of an eminence crouching amid

[023-055] desert plains, a sort of edifice which resembled from a distance

[023-056] a ruined colonnade, mounted upon a basement with its foundation

[023-057] laid bare. This was neither a Parthenon, nor a temple of the

[023-058] Olympian Jupiter. It was Montfaucon.

[023-059] Now, if the enumeration of so many edifices, summary as we have

[023-060] endeavored to make it, has not shattered in the reader’s mind the

[023-061] general image of old Paris, as we have constructed it, we will

[023-062] recapitulate it in a few words. In the centre, the island of the

[023-063] City, resembling as to form an enormous tortoise, and throwing

[023-064] out its bridges with tiles for scales; like legs from beneath its

[023-065] gray shell of roofs. On the left, the monolithic trapezium, firm,

[023-066] dense, bristling, of the University; on the right, the vast

[023-067] semicircle of the Town, much more intermixed with gardens and

[023-068] monuments. The three blocks, city, university, and town, marbled

[023-069] with innumerable streets. Across all, the Seine, “foster-mother

[023-070] Seine,” as says Father Du Breul, blocked with islands, bridges,

[023-071] and boats. All about an immense plain, patched with a thousand

[023-072] sorts of cultivated plots, sown with fine villages. On the left,

[023-073] Issy, Vanvres, Vaugirarde, Montrouge, Gentilly, with its round

[023-074] tower and its square tower, etc.; on the right, twenty others,

[023-075] from Conflans to Ville-l’Évêque. On the horizon, a border of

[023-076] hills arranged in a circle like the rim of the basin. Finally,

[023-077] far away to the east, Vincennes, and its seven quadrangular

[023-078] towers to the south, Bicêtre and its pointed turrets; to the

[023-079] north, Saint-Denis and its spire; to the west, Saint Cloud and

[023-080] its donjon keep. Such was the Paris which the ravens, who lived

[023-081] in 1482, beheld from the summits of the towers of Notre-Dame.

[023-082] Nevertheless, Voltaire said of this city, that “before Louis

[023-083] XIV., it possessed but four fine monuments”: the dome of the

[023-084] Sorbonne, the Val-de-Grâce, the modern Louvre, and I know not

[023-085] what the fourth was—the Luxembourg, perhaps. Fortunately,

[023-086] Voltaire was the author of “Candide” in spite of this, and in

[023-087] spite of this, he is, among all the men who have followed each

[023-088] other in the long series of humanity, the one who has best

[023-089] possessed the diabolical laugh. Moreover, this proves that one

[023-090] can be a fine genius, and yet understand nothing of an art to

[023-091] which one does not belong. Did not Molière imagine that he was

[023-092] doing Raphael and Michael-Angelo a very great honor, by calling

[023-093] them “those Mignards of their age?”

[023-094] Let us return to Paris and to the fifteenth century.

[023-095] It was not then merely a handsome city; it was a homogeneous

[023-096] city, an architectural and historical product of the Middle Ages,

[023-097] a chronicle in stone. It was a city formed of two layers only;

[023-098] the Romanesque layer and the Gothic layer; for the Roman layer

[023-099] had disappeared long before, with the exception of the Hot Baths

[023-100] of Julian, where it still pierced through the thick crust of the

[023-101] Middle Ages. As for the Celtic layer, no specimens were any

[023-102] longer to be found, even when sinking wells.

[023-103] Fifty years later, when the Renaissance began to mingle with this

[023-104] unity which was so severe and yet so varied, the dazzling luxury

[023-105] of its fantasies and systems, its debasements of Roman round

[023-106] arches, Greek columns, and Gothic bases, its sculpture which was

[023-107] so tender and so ideal, its peculiar taste for arabesques and

[023-108] acanthus leaves, its architectural paganism, contemporary with

[023-109] Luther, Paris, was perhaps, still more beautiful, although less

[023-110] harmonious to the eye, and to the thought.

[023-111] But this splendid moment lasted only for a short time; the

[023-112] Renaissance was not impartial; it did not content itself with

[023-113] building, it wished to destroy; it is true that it required the

[023-114] room. Thus Gothic Paris was complete only for a moment.

[023-115] Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie had barely been completed when the

[023-116] demolition of the old Louvre was begun.

[023-117] After that, the great city became more disfigured every day.

[023-118] Gothic Paris, beneath which Roman Paris was effaced, was effaced

[023-119] in its turn; but can any one say what Paris has replaced it?

[023-120] There is the Paris of Catherine de Medicis at the

[023-121] Tuileries;[23]—the Paris of Henri II., at the Hôtel de Ville, two

[023-122] edifices still in fine taste;—the Paris of Henri IV., at the

[023-123] Place Royale: façades of brick with stone corners, and slated

[023-124] roofs, tri-colored houses;—the Paris of Louis XIII., at the

[023-125] Val-de-Grâce: a crushed and squat architecture, with vaults like

[023-126] basket-handles, and something indescribably pot-bellied in the

[023-127] column, and thickset in the dome;—the Paris of Louis XIV., in the

[023-128] Invalides: grand, rich, gilded, cold;—the Paris of Louis XV., in

[023-129] Saint-Sulpice: volutes, knots of ribbon, clouds, vermicelli and

[023-130] chiccory leaves, all in stone;—the Paris of Louis XVI., in the

[023-131] Pantheon: Saint Peter of Rome, badly copied (the edifice is

[023-132] awkwardly heaped together, which has not amended its lines);—the

[023-133] Paris of the Republic, in the School of Medicine: a poor Greek

[023-134] and Roman taste, which resembles the Coliseum or the Parthenon as

[023-135] the constitution of the year III., resembles the laws of

[023-136] Minos,—it is called in architecture, “the Messidor”[24]

[023-137] taste;—the Paris of Napoleon in the Place Vendôme: this one is

[023-138] sublime, a column of bronze made of cannons;—the Paris of the

[023-139] Restoration, at the Bourse: a very white colonnade supporting a

[023-140] very smooth frieze; the whole is square and cost twenty millions.

[023-141] To each of these characteristic monuments there is attached by a

[023-142] similarity of taste, fashion, and attitude, a certain number of

[023-143] houses scattered about in different quarters and which the eyes

[023-144] of the connoisseur easily distinguishes and furnishes with a

[023-145] date. When one knows how to look, one finds the spirit of a

[023-146] century, and the physiognomy of a king, even in the knocker on a

[023-147] door.

[023-148] The Paris of the present day has then, no general physiognomy. It

[023-149] is a collection of specimens of many centuries, and the finest

[023-150] have disappeared. The capital grows only in houses, and what

[023-151] houses! At the rate at which Paris is now proceeding, it will

[023-152] renew itself every fifty years.

[023-153] Thus the historical significance of its architecture is being

[023-154] effaced every day. Monuments are becoming rarer and rarer, and

[023-155] one seems to see them gradually engulfed, by the flood of houses.

[023-156] Our fathers had a Paris of stone; our sons will have one of

[023-157] plaster.

[023-158] So far as the modern monuments of new Paris are concerned, we

[023-159] would gladly be excused from mentioning them. It is not that we

[023-160] do not admire them as they deserve. The Sainte-Geneviève of M.

[023-161] Soufflot is certainly the finest Savoy cake that has ever been

[023-162] made in stone. The Palace of the Legion of Honor is also a very

[023-163] distinguished bit of pastry. The dome of the wheat market is an

[023-164] English jockey cap, on a grand scale. The towers of Saint-Sulpice

[023-165] are two huge clarinets, and the form is as good as any other; the

[023-166] telegraph, contorted and grimacing, forms an admirable accident

[023-167] upon their roofs. Saint-Roch has a door which, for magnificence,

[023-168] is comparable only to that of Saint-Thomas d’Aquin. It has, also,

[023-169] a crucifixion in high relief, in a cellar, with a sun of gilded

[023-170] wood. These things are fairly marvellous. The lantern of the

[023-171] labyrinth of the Jardin des Plantes is also very ingenious.

[023-172] As for the Palace of the Bourse, which is Greek as to its

[023-173] colonnade, Roman in the round arches of its doors and windows, of

[023-174] the Renaissance by virtue of its flattened vault, it is

[023-175] indubitably a very correct and very pure monument; the proof is

[023-176] that it is crowned with an attic, such as was never seen in

[023-177] Athens, a beautiful, straight line, gracefully broken here and

[023-178] there by stovepipes. Let us add that if it is according to rule

[023-179] that the architecture of a building should be adapted to its

[023-180] purpose in such a manner that this purpose shall be immediately

[023-181] apparent from the mere aspect of the building, one cannot be too

[023-182] much amazed at a structure which might be indifferently—the

[023-183] palace of a king, a chamber of communes, a town-hall, a college,

[023-184] a riding-school, an academy, a warehouse, a court-house, a

[023-185] museum, a barracks, a sepulchre, a temple, or a theatre. However,

[023-186] it is an Exchange. An edifice ought to be, moreover, suitable to

[023-187] the climate. This one is evidently constructed expressly for our

[023-188] cold and rainy skies. It has a roof almost as flat as roofs in

[023-189] the East, which involves sweeping the roof in winter, when it

[023-190] snows; and of course roofs are made to be swept. As for its

[023-191] purpose, of which we just spoke, it fulfils it to a marvel; it is

[023-192] a bourse in France as it would have been a temple in Greece. It

[023-193] is true that the architect was at a good deal of trouble to

[023-194] conceal the clock face, which would have destroyed the purity of

[023-195] the fine lines of the façade; but, on the other hand, we have

[023-196] that colonnade which circles round the edifice and under which,

[023-197] on days of high religious ceremony, the theories of the

[023-198] stock-brokers and the courtiers of commerce can be developed so

[023-199] majestically.
