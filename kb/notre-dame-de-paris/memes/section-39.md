---[039-001] At length Gervaise, the most curious of the three, and

[039-002] consequently the least sensitive, tried to make the recluse

[039-003] speak:

[039-004] “Sister! Sister Gudule!”

[039-005] She repeated this call three times, raising her voice each time.

[039-006] The recluse did not move; not a word, not a glance, not a sigh,

[039-007] not a sign of life.

[039-008] Oudarde, in her turn, in a sweeter, more caressing

[039-009] voice,—“Sister!” said she, “Sister Sainte-Gudule!”

[039-010] The same silence; the same immobility.

[039-011] “A singular woman!” exclaimed Gervaise, “and one not to be moved

[039-012] by a catapult!”

[039-013] “Perchance she is deaf,” said Oudarde.

[039-014] “Perhaps she is blind,” added Gervaise.

[039-015] “Dead, perchance,” returned Mahiette.

[039-016] It is certain that if the soul had not already quitted this

[039-017] inert, sluggish, lethargic body, it had at least retreated and

[039-018] concealed itself in depths whither the perceptions of the

[039-019] exterior organs no longer penetrated.

[039-020] “Then we must leave the cake on the window,” said Oudarde; “some

[039-021] scamp will take it. What shall we do to rouse her?”

[039-022] Eustache, who, up to that moment had been diverted by a little

[039-023] carriage drawn by a large dog, which had just passed, suddenly

[039-024] perceived that his three conductresses were gazing at something

[039-025] through the window, and, curiosity taking possession of him in

[039-026] his turn, he climbed upon a stone post, elevated himself on

[039-027] tiptoe, and applied his fat, red face to the opening, shouting,

[039-028] “Mother, let me see too!”

[039-029] At the sound of this clear, fresh, ringing child’s voice, the

[039-030] recluse trembled; she turned her head with the sharp, abrupt

[039-031] movement of a steel spring, her long, fleshless hands cast aside

[039-032] the hair from her brow, and she fixed upon the child, bitter,

[039-033] astonished, desperate eyes. This glance was but a lightning

[039-034] flash.

[039-035] “Oh my God!” she suddenly exclaimed, hiding her head on her

[039-036] knees, and it seemed as though her hoarse voice tore her chest as

[039-037] it passed from it, “do not show me those of others!”

[039-038] “Good day, madam,” said the child, gravely.

[039-039] Nevertheless, this shock had, so to speak, awakened the recluse.

[039-040] A long shiver traversed her frame from head to foot; her teeth

[039-041] chattered; she half raised her head and said, pressing her elbows

[039-042] against her hips, and clasping her feet in her hands as though to

[039-043] warm them,—

[039-044] “Oh, how cold it is!”

[039-045] “Poor woman!” said Oudarde, with great compassion, “would you

[039-046] like a little fire?”

[039-047] She shook her head in token of refusal.

[039-048] “Well,” resumed Oudarde, presenting her with a flagon; “here is

[039-049] some hippocras which will warm you; drink it.”

[039-050] Again she shook her head, looked at Oudarde fixedly and replied,

[039-051] “Water.”

[039-052] Oudarde persisted,—“No, sister, that is no beverage for January.

[039-053] You must drink a little hippocras and eat this leavened cake of

[039-054] maize, which we have baked for you.”

[039-055] She refused the cake which Mahiette offered to her, and said,

[039-056] “Black bread.”

[039-057] “Come,” said Gervaise, seized in her turn with an impulse of

[039-058] charity, and unfastening her woolen cloak, “here is a cloak which

[039-059] is a little warmer than yours.”

[039-060] She refused the cloak as she had refused the flagon and the cake,

[039-061] and replied, “A sack.”

[039-062] “But,” resumed the good Oudarde, “you must have perceived to some

[039-063] extent, that yesterday was a festival.”

[039-064] “I do perceive it,” said the recluse; “’tis two days now since I

[039-065] have had any water in my crock.”

[039-066] She added, after a silence, “’Tis a festival, I am forgotten.

[039-067] People do well. Why should the world think of me, when I do not

[039-068] think of it? Cold charcoal makes cold ashes.”

[039-069] And as though fatigued with having said so much, she dropped her

[039-070] head on her knees again. The simple and charitable Oudarde, who

[039-071] fancied that she understood from her last words that she was

[039-072] complaining of the cold, replied innocently, “Then you would like

[039-073] a little fire?”

[039-074] “Fire!” said the sacked nun, with a strange accent; “and will you

[039-075] also make a little for the poor little one who has been beneath

[039-076] the sod for these fifteen years?”

[039-077] Every limb was trembling, her voice quivered, her eyes flashed,

[039-078] she had raised herself upon her knees; suddenly she extended her

[039-079] thin, white hand towards the child, who was regarding her with a

[039-080] look of astonishment. “Take away that child!” she cried. “The

[039-081] Egyptian woman is about to pass by.”

[039-082] Then she fell face downward on the earth, and her forehead struck

[039-083] the stone, with the sound of one stone against another stone. The

[039-084] three women thought her dead. A moment later, however, she moved,

[039-085] and they beheld her drag herself, on her knees and elbows, to the

[039-086] corner where the little shoe was. Then they dared not look; they

[039-087] no longer saw her; but they heard a thousand kisses and a

[039-088] thousand sighs, mingled with heartrending cries, and dull blows

[039-089] like those of a head in contact with a wall. Then, after one of

[039-090] these blows, so violent that all three of them staggered, they

[039-091] heard no more.

[039-092] “Can she have killed herself?” said Gervaise, venturing to pass

[039-093] her head through the air-hole. “Sister! Sister Gudule!”

[039-094] “Sister Gudule!” repeated Oudarde.

[039-095] “Ah! good heavens! she no longer moves!” resumed Gervaise; “is

[039-096] she dead? Gudule! Gudule!”

[039-097] Mahiette, choked to such a point that she could not speak, made

[039-098] an effort. “Wait,” said she. Then bending towards the window,

[039-099] “Paquette!” she said, “Paquette le Chantefleurie!”

[039-100] A child who innocently blows upon the badly ignited fuse of a

[039-101] bomb, and makes it explode in his face, is no more terrified than

[039-102] was Mahiette at the effect of that name, abruptly launched into

[039-103] the cell of Sister Gudule.

[039-104] The recluse trembled all over, rose erect on her bare feet, and

[039-105] leaped at the window with eyes so glaring that Mahiette and

[039-106] Oudarde, and the other woman and the child recoiled even to the

[039-107] parapet of the quay.

[039-108] Meanwhile, the sinister face of the recluse appeared pressed to

[039-109] the grating of the air-hole. “Oh! oh!” she cried, with an

[039-110] appalling laugh; “’tis the Egyptian who is calling me!”

[039-111] At that moment, a scene which was passing at the pillory caught

[039-112] her wild eye. Her brow contracted with horror, she stretched her

[039-113] two skeleton arms from her cell, and shrieked in a voice which

[039-114] resembled a death-rattle, “So ’tis thou once more, daughter of

[039-115] Egypt! ’Tis thou who callest me, stealer of children! Well! Be

[039-116] thou accursed! accursed! accursed! accursed!”

[039-117] CHAPTER IV. A TEAR FOR A DROP OF WATER.

[039-118] These words were, so to speak, the point of union of two scenes,

[039-119] which had, up to that time, been developed in parallel lines at

[039-120] the same moment, each on its particular theatre; one, that which

[039-121] the reader has just perused, in the Rat-Hole; the other, which he

[039-122] is about to read, on the ladder of the pillory. The first had for

[039-123] witnesses only the three women with whom the reader has just made

[039-124] acquaintance; the second had for spectators all the public which

[039-125] we have seen above, collecting on the Place de Grève, around the

[039-126] pillory and the gibbet.

[039-127] That crowd which the four sergeants posted at nine o’clock in the

[039-128] morning at the four corners of the pillory had inspired with the

[039-129] hope of some sort of an execution, no doubt, not a hanging, but a

[039-130] whipping, a cropping of ears, something, in short,—that crowd had

[039-131] increased so rapidly that the four policemen, too closely

[039-132] besieged, had had occasion to “press” it, as the expression then

[039-133] ran, more than once, by sound blows of their whips, and the

[039-134] haunches of their horses.

[039-135] This populace, disciplined to waiting for public executions, did

[039-136] not manifest very much impatience. It amused itself with watching

[039-137] the pillory, a very simple sort of monument, composed of a cube

[039-138] of masonry about six feet high and hollow in the interior. A very

[039-139] steep staircase, of unhewn stone, which was called by distinction

[039-140] “the ladder,” led to the upper platform, upon which was visible a

[039-141] horizontal wheel of solid oak. The victim was bound upon this

[039-142] wheel, on his knees, with his hands behind his back. A wooden

[039-143] shaft, which set in motion a capstan concealed in the interior of

[039-144] the little edifice, imparted a rotatory motion to the wheel,

[039-145] which always maintained its horizontal position, and in this

[039-146] manner presented the face of the condemned man to all quarters of

[039-147] the square in succession. This was what was called “turning” a

[039-148] criminal.

[039-149] As the reader perceives, the pillory of the Grève was far from

[039-150] presenting all the recreations of the pillory of the Halles.

[039-151] Nothing architectural, nothing monumental. No roof to the iron

[039-152] cross, no octagonal lantern, no frail, slender columns spreading

[039-153] out on the edge of the roof into capitals of acanthus leaves and

[039-154] flowers, no waterspouts of chimeras and monsters, on carved

[039-155] woodwork, no fine sculpture, deeply sunk in the stone.

[039-156] They were forced to content themselves with those four stretches

[039-157] of rubble work, backed with sandstone, and a wretched stone

[039-158] gibbet, meagre and bare, on one side.

[039-159] The entertainment would have been but a poor one for lovers of

[039-160] Gothic architecture. It is true that nothing was ever less

[039-161] curious on the score of architecture than the worthy gapers of

[039-162] the Middle Ages, and that they cared very little for the beauty

[039-163] of a pillory.

[039-164] The victim finally arrived, bound to the tail of a cart, and when

[039-165] he had been hoisted upon the platform, where he could be seen

[039-166] from all points of the Place, bound with cords and straps upon

[039-167] the wheel of the pillory, a prodigious hoot, mingled with

[039-168] laughter and acclamations, burst forth upon the Place. They had

[039-169] recognized Quasimodo.

[039-170] It was he, in fact. The change was singular. Pilloried on the

[039-171] very place where, on the day before, he had been saluted,

[039-172] acclaimed, and proclaimed Pope and Prince of Fools, in the

[039-173] cortège of the Duke of Egypt, the King of Thunes, and the Emperor

[039-174] of Galilee! One thing is certain, and that is, that there was not

[039-175] a soul in the crowd, not even himself, though in turn triumphant

[039-176] and the sufferer, who set forth this combination clearly in his

[039-177] thought. Gringoire and his philosophy were missing at this

[039-178] spectacle.

[039-179] Soon Michel Noiret, sworn trumpeter to the king, our lord,

[039-180] imposed silence on the louts, and proclaimed the sentence, in

[039-181] accordance with the order and command of monsieur the provost.

[039-182] Then he withdrew behind the cart, with his men in livery

[039-183] surcoats.

[039-184] Quasimodo, impassible, did not wince. All resistance had been

[039-185] rendered impossible to him by what was then called, in the style

[039-186] of the criminal chancellery, “the vehemence and firmness of the

[039-187] bonds” which means that the thongs and chains probably cut into

[039-188] his flesh; moreover, it is a tradition of jail and wardens, which

[039-189] has not been lost, and which the handcuffs still preciously

[039-190] preserve among us, a civilized, gentle, humane people (the

[039-191] galleys and the guillotine in parentheses).

[039-192] He had allowed himself to be led, pushed, carried, lifted, bound,

[039-193] and bound again. Nothing was to be seen upon his countenance but

[039-194] the astonishment of a savage or an idiot. He was known to be

[039-195] deaf; one might have pronounced him to be blind.

[039-196] They placed him on his knees on the circular plank; he made no

[039-197] resistance. They removed his shirt and doublet as far as his

[039-198] girdle; he allowed them to have their way. They entangled him

[039-199] under a fresh system of thongs and buckles; he allowed them to

[039-200] bind and buckle him. Only from time to time he snorted noisily,

[039-201] like a calf whose head is hanging and bumping over the edge of a

[039-202] butcher’s cart.

[039-203] “The dolt,” said Jehan Frollo of the Mill, to his friend Robin

[039-204] Poussepain (for the two students had followed the culprit, as was

[039-205] to have been expected), “he understands no more than a cockchafer

[039-206] shut up in a box!”

[039-207] There was wild laughter among the crowd when they beheld

[039-208] Quasimodo’s hump, his camel’s breast, his callous and hairy

[039-209] shoulders laid bare. During this gayety, a man in the livery of

[039-210] the city, short of stature and robust of mien, mounted the

[039-211] platform and placed himself near the victim. His name speedily

[039-212] circulated among the spectators. It was Master Pierrat Torterue,

[039-213] official torturer to the Châtelet.
