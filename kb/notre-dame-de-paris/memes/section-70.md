---[070-001] “And then?”

[070-002] “Brother, you behold a culprit, a criminal, a wretch, a

[070-003] libertine, a man of enormities! My dear brother, Jehan hath made

[070-004] of your counsels straw and dung to trample under foot. I have

[070-005] been well chastised for it, and God is extraordinarily just. As

[070-006] long as I had money, I feasted, I lead a mad and joyous life. Oh!

[070-007] how ugly and crabbed behind is debauch which is so charming in

[070-008] front! Now I have no longer a blank; I have sold my napery, my

[070-009] shirt and my towels; no more merry life! The beautiful candle is

[070-010] extinguished and I have henceforth, only a wretched tallow dip

[070-011] which smokes in my nose. The wenches jeer at me. I drink water. I

[070-012] am overwhelmed with remorse and with creditors.”

[070-013] “The rest?” said the archdeacon.

[070-014] “Alas! my very dear brother, I should like to settle down to a

[070-015] better life. I come to you full of contrition, I am penitent. I

[070-016] make my confession. I beat my breast violently. You are quite

[070-017] right in wishing that I should some day become a licentiate and

[070-018] sub-monitor in the college of Torchi. At the present moment I

[070-019] feel a magnificent vocation for that profession. But I have no

[070-020] more ink and I must buy some; I have no more paper, I have no

[070-021] more books, and I must buy some. For this purpose, I am greatly

[070-022] in need of a little money, and I come to you, brother, with my

[070-023] heart full of contrition.”

[070-024] “Is that all?”

[070-025] “Yes,” said the scholar. “A little money.”

[070-026] “I have none.”

[070-027] Then the scholar said, with an air which was both grave and

[070-028] resolute: “Well, brother, I am sorry to be obliged to tell you

[070-029] that very fine offers and propositions are being made to me in

[070-030] another quarter. You will not give me any money? No. In that case

[070-031] I shall become a professional vagabond.”

[070-032] As he uttered these monstrous words, he assumed the mien of Ajax,

[070-033] expecting to see the lightnings descend upon his head.

[070-034] The archdeacon said coldly to him,—

[070-035] “Become a vagabond.”

[070-036] Jehan made him a deep bow, and descended the cloister stairs,

[070-037] whistling.

[070-038] At the moment when he was passing through the courtyard of the

[070-039] cloister, beneath his brother’s window, he heard that window

[070-040] open, raised his eyes and beheld the archdeacon’s severe head

[070-041] emerge.

[070-042] “Go to the devil!” said Dom Claude; “here is the last money which

[070-043] you will get from me?”

[070-044] At the same time, the priest flung Jehan a purse, which gave the

[070-045] scholar a big bump on the forehead, and with which Jehan

[070-046] retreated, both vexed and content, like a dog who had been stoned

[070-047] with marrow bones.

[070-048] CHAPTER III. LONG LIVE MIRTH.

[070-049] The reader has probably not forgotten that a part of the Cour de

[070-050] Miracles was enclosed by the ancient wall which surrounded the

[070-051] city, a goodly number of whose towers had begun, even at that

[070-052] epoch, to fall to ruin. One of these towers had been converted

[070-053] into a pleasure resort by the vagabonds. There was a dram-shop in

[070-054] the underground story, and the rest in the upper stories. This

[070-055] was the most lively, and consequently the most hideous, point of

[070-056] the whole outcast den. It was a sort of monstrous hive, which

[070-057] buzzed there night and day. At night, when the remainder of the

[070-058] beggar horde slept, when there was no longer a window lighted in

[070-059] the dingy façades of the Place, when not a cry was any longer to

[070-060] be heard proceeding from those innumerable families, those

[070-061] ant-hills of thieves, of wenches, and stolen or bastard children,

[070-062] the merry tower was still recognizable by the noise which it

[070-063] made, by the scarlet light which, flashing simultaneously from

[070-064] the air-holes, the windows, the fissures in the cracked walls,

[070-065] escaped, so to speak, from its every pore.

[070-066] The cellar then, was the dram-shop. The descent to it was through

[070-067] a low door and by a staircase as steep as a classic Alexandrine.

[070-068] Over the door, by way of a sign there hung a marvellous daub,

[070-069] representing new sous and dead chickens,[53] with this, pun

[070-070] below: _Aux sonneurs pour les trépassés_,—The ringers for the

[070-071] dead.

[070-072] One evening when the curfew was sounding from all the belfries in

[070-073] Paris, the sergeants of the watch might have observed, had it

[070-074] been granted to them to enter the formidable Court of Miracles,

[070-075] that more tumult than usual was in progress in the vagabonds’

[070-076] tavern, that more drinking was being done, and louder swearing.

[070-077] Outside in the Place, there, were many groups conversing in low

[070-078] tones, as when some great plan is being framed, and here and

[070-079] there a knave crouching down engaged in sharpening a villanous

[070-080] iron blade on a paving-stone.

[070-081] Meanwhile, in the tavern itself, wine and gaming offered such a

[070-082] powerful diversion to the ideas which occupied the vagabonds’

[070-083] lair that evening, that it would have been difficult to divine

[070-084] from the remarks of the drinkers, what was the matter in hand.

[070-085] They merely wore a gayer air than was their wont, and some weapon

[070-086] could be seen glittering between the legs of each of them,—a

[070-087] sickle, an axe, a big two-edged sword or the hook of an old

[070-088] hackbut.

[070-089] The room, circular in form, was very spacious; but the tables

[070-090] were so thickly set and the drinkers so numerous, that all that

[070-091] the tavern contained, men, women, benches, beer-jugs, all that

[070-092] were drinking, all that were sleeping, all that were playing, the

[070-093] well, the lame, seemed piled up pell-mell, with as much order and

[070-094] harmony as a heap of oyster shells. There were a few tallow dips

[070-095] lighted on the tables; but the real luminary of this tavern, that

[070-096] which played the part in this dram-shop of the chandelier of an

[070-097] opera house, was the fire. This cellar was so damp that the fire

[070-098] was never allowed to go out, even in midsummer; an immense

[070-099] chimney with a sculptured mantel, all bristling with heavy iron

[070-100] andirons and cooking utensils, with one of those huge fires of

[070-101] mixed wood and peat which at night, in village streets make the

[070-102] reflection of forge windows stand out so red on the opposite

[070-103] walls. A big dog gravely seated in the ashes was turning a spit

[070-104] loaded with meat before the coals.

[070-105] Great as was the confusion, after the first glance one could

[070-106] distinguish in that multitude, three principal groups which

[070-107] thronged around three personages already known to the reader. One

[070-108] of these personages, fantastically accoutred in many an oriental

[070-109] rag, was Mathias Hungadi Spicali, Duke of Egypt and Bohemia. The

[070-110] knave was seated on a table with his legs crossed, and in a loud

[070-111] voice was bestowing his knowledge of magic, both black and white,

[070-112] on many a gaping face which surrounded him. Another rabble

[070-113] pressed close around our old friend, the valiant King of Thunes,

[070-114] armed to the teeth. Clopin Trouillefou, with a very serious air

[070-115] and in a low voice, was regulating the distribution of an

[070-116] enormous cask of arms, which stood wide open in front of him and

[070-117] from whence poured out in profusion, axes, swords, bassinets,

[070-118] coats of mail, broadswords, lance-heads, arrows, and

[070-119] viretons,[54] like apples and grapes from a horn of plenty. Every

[070-120] one took something from the cask, one a morion, another a long,

[070-121] straight sword, another a dagger with a cross-shaped hilt. The

[070-122] very children were arming themselves, and there were even

[070-123] cripples in bowls who, in armor and cuirass, made their way

[070-124] between the legs of the drinkers, like great beetles.

[070-125] Finally, a third audience, the most noisy, the most jovial, and

[070-126] the most numerous, encumbered benches and tables, in the midst of

[070-127] which harangued and swore a flute-like voice, which escaped from

[070-128] beneath a heavy armor, complete from casque to spurs. The

[070-129] individual who had thus screwed a whole outfit upon his body, was

[070-130] so hidden by his warlike accoutrements that nothing was to be

[070-131] seen of his person save an impertinent, red, snub nose, a rosy

[070-132] mouth, and bold eyes. His belt was full of daggers and poniards,

[070-133] a huge sword on his hip, a rusted cross-bow at his left, and a

[070-134] vast jug of wine in front of him, without reckoning on his right,

[070-135] a fat wench with her bosom uncovered. All mouths around him were

[070-136] laughing, cursing, and drinking.

[070-137] Add twenty secondary groups, the waiters, male and female,

[070-138] running with jugs on their heads, gamblers squatting over taws,

[070-139] merelles,[55] dice, vachettes, the ardent game of tringlet,

[070-140] quarrels in one corner, kisses in another, and the reader will

[070-141] have some idea of this whole picture, over which flickered the

[070-142] light of a great, flaming fire, which made a thousand huge and

[070-143] grotesque shadows dance over the walls of the drinking shop.

[070-144] As for the noise, it was like the inside of a bell at full peal.

[070-145] The dripping-pan, where crackled a rain of grease, filled with

[070-146] its continual sputtering the intervals of these thousand

[070-147] dialogues, which intermingled from one end of the apartment to

[070-148] the other.

[070-149] In the midst of this uproar, at the extremity of the tavern, on

[070-150] the bench inside the chimney, sat a philosopher meditating with

[070-151] his feet in the ashes and his eyes on the brands. It was Pierre

[070-152] Gringoire.

[070-153] “Be quick! make haste, arm yourselves! we set out on the march in

[070-154] an hour!” said Clopin Trouillefou to his thieves.

[070-155] A wench was humming,—

[070-156] “Bonsoir mon père et ma mère,

[070-157] Les derniers couvrent le feu.”[56]

[070-158] Two card players were disputing,—

[070-159] “Knave!” cried the reddest faced of the two, shaking his fist at

[070-160] the other; “I’ll mark you with the club. You can take the place

[070-161] of Mistigri in the pack of cards of monseigneur the king.”

[070-162] “Ugh!” roared a Norman, recognizable by his nasal accent; “we are

[070-163] packed in here like the saints of Caillouville!”

[070-164] “My sons,” the Duke of Egypt was saying to his audience, in a

[070-165] falsetto voice, “sorceresses in France go to the witches’ sabbath

[070-166] without broomsticks, or grease, or steed, merely by means of some

[070-167] magic words. The witches of Italy always have a buck waiting for

[070-168] them at their door. All are bound to go out through the chimney.”

[070-169] The voice of the young scamp armed from head to foot, dominated

[070-170] the uproar.

[070-171] “Hurrah! hurrah!” he was shouting. “My first day in armor!

[070-172] Outcast! I am an outcast. Give me something to drink. My friends,

[070-173] my name is Jehan Frollo du Moulin, and I am a gentleman. My

[070-174] opinion is that if God were a _gendarme_, he would turn robber.

[070-175] Brothers, we are about to set out on a fine expedition. Lay siege

[070-176] to the church, burst in the doors, drag out the beautiful girl,

[070-177] save her from the judges, save her from the priests, dismantle

[070-178] the cloister, burn the bishop in his palace—all this we will do

[070-179] in less time than it takes for a burgomaster to eat a spoonful of

[070-180] soup. Our cause is just, we will plunder Notre-Dame and that will

[070-181] be the end of it. We will hang Quasimodo. Do you know Quasimodo,

[070-182] ladies? Have you seen him make himself breathless on the big bell

[070-183] on a grand Pentecost festival! _Corne du Père!_ ’tis very fine!

[070-184] One would say he was a devil mounted on a man. Listen to me, my

[070-185] friends; I am a vagabond to the bottom of my heart, I am a member

[070-186] of the slang thief gang in my soul, I was born an independent

[070-187] thief. I have been rich, and I have devoured all my property. My

[070-188] mother wanted to make an officer of me; my father, a sub-deacon;

[070-189] my aunt, a councillor of inquests; my grandmother, prothonotary

[070-190] to the king; my great aunt, a treasurer of the short robe,—and I

[070-191] have made myself an outcast. I said this to my father, who spit

[070-192] his curse in my face; to my mother, who set to weeping and

[070-193] chattering, poor old lady, like yonder fagot on the and-irons.

[070-194] Long live mirth! I am a real Bicêtre. Waitress, my dear, more

[070-195] wine. I have still the wherewithal to pay. I want no more Surène

[070-196] wine. It distresses my throat. I’d as lief, _corbœuf!_ gargle my

[070-197] throat with a basket.”
