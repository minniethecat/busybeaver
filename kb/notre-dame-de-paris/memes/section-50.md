---[050-001] Night was falling; the square was dark; the wine-shop, full of

[050-002] candles, flamed afar like a forge in the gloom; the noise of

[050-003] glasses and feasting, of oaths and quarrels, which escaped

[050-004] through the broken panes, was audible. Through the mist which the

[050-005] warmth of the room spread over the window in front, a hundred

[050-006] confused figures could be seen swarming, and from time to time a

[050-007] burst of noisy laughter broke forth from it. The passers-by who

[050-008] were going about their business, slipped past this tumultuous

[050-009] window without glancing at it. Only at intervals did some little

[050-010] ragged boy raise himself on tiptoe as far as the ledge, and hurl

[050-011] into the drinking-shop, that ancient, jeering hoot, with which

[050-012] drunken men were then pursued: “Aux Houls, saouls, saouls,

[050-013] saouls!”

[050-014] Nevertheless, one man paced imperturbably back and forth in front

[050-015] of the tavern, gazing at it incessantly, and going no further

[050-016] from it than a pikeman from his sentry-box. He was enveloped in a

[050-017] mantle to his very nose. This mantle he had just purchased of the

[050-018] old-clothes man, in the vicinity of the “Eve’s Apple,” no doubt

[050-019] to protect himself from the cold of the March evening, possibly

[050-020] also, to conceal his costume. From time to time he paused in

[050-021] front of the dim window with its leaden lattice, listened,

[050-022] looked, and stamped his foot.

[050-023] At length the door of the dram-shop opened. This was what he

[050-024] appeared to be waiting for. Two boon companions came forth. The

[050-025] ray of light which escaped from the door crimsoned for a moment

[050-026] their jovial faces.

[050-027] The man in the mantle went and stationed himself on the watch

[050-028] under a porch on the other side of the street.

[050-029] “_Corne et tonnerre!_” said one of the comrades. “Seven o’clock

[050-030] is on the point of striking. ’Tis the hour of my appointed

[050-031] meeting.”

[050-032] “I tell you,” repeated his companion, with a thick tongue, “that

[050-033] I don’t live in the Rue des Mauvaises Paroles, _indignus qui

[050-034] inter mala verba habitat_. I have a lodging in the Rue

[050-035] Jean-Pain-Mollet, _in vico Johannis Pain-Mollet_. You are more

[050-036] horned than a unicorn if you assert the contrary. Every one knows

[050-037] that he who once mounts astride a bear is never after afraid; but

[050-038] you have a nose turned to dainties like Saint-Jacques of the

[050-039] hospital.”

[050-040] “Jehan, my friend, you are drunk,” said the other.

[050-041] The other replied staggering, “It pleases you to say so, Phœbus;

[050-042] but it hath been proved that Plato had the profile of a hound.”

[050-043] The reader has, no doubt, already recognized our two brave

[050-044] friends, the captain and the scholar. It appears that the man who

[050-045] was lying in wait for them had also recognized them, for he

[050-046] slowly followed all the zigzags that the scholar caused the

[050-047] captain to make, who being a more hardened drinker had retained

[050-048] all his self-possession. By listening to them attentively, the

[050-049] man in the mantle could catch in its entirety the following

[050-050] interesting conversation,—

[050-051] “_Corbacque!_ Do try to walk straight, master bachelor; you know

[050-052] that I must leave you. Here it is seven o’clock. I have an

[050-053] appointment with a woman.”

[050-054] “Leave me then! I see stars and lances of fire. You are like the

[050-055] Château de Dampmartin, which is bursting with laughter.”

[050-056] “By the warts of my grandmother, Jehan, you are raving with too

[050-057] much rabidness. By the way, Jehan, have you any money left?”

[050-058] “Monsieur Rector, there is no mistake; the little butcher’s shop,

[050-059] _parva boucheria_.”

[050-060] “Jehan! my friend Jehan! You know that I made an appointment with

[050-061] that little girl at the end of the Pont Saint-Michel, and I can

[050-062] only take her to the Falourdel’s, the old crone of the bridge,

[050-063] and that I must pay for a chamber. The old witch with a white

[050-064] moustache would not trust me. Jehan! for pity’s sake! Have we

[050-065] drunk up the whole of the curé’s purse? Have you not a single

[050-066] parisis left?”

[050-067] “The consciousness of having spent the other hours well is a just

[050-068] and savory condiment for the table.”

[050-069] “Belly and guts! a truce to your whimsical nonsense! Tell me,

[050-070] Jehan of the devil! have you any money left? Give it to me,

[050-071] _bédieu!_ or I will search you, were you as leprous as Job, and

[050-072] as scabby as Cæsar!”

[050-073] “Monsieur, the Rue Galiache is a street which hath at one end the

[050-074] Rue de la Verrerie, and at the other the Rue de la Tixeranderie.”

[050-075] “Well, yes! my good friend Jehan, my poor comrade, the Rue

[050-076] Galiache is good, very good. But in the name of heaven collect

[050-077] your wits. I must have a sou parisis, and the appointment is for

[050-078] seven o’clock.”

[050-079] “Silence for the rondo, and attention to the refrain,—

[050-080] “Quand les rats mangeront les cas,

[050-081] Le roi sera seigneur d’Arras;

[050-082] Quand la mer, qui est grande et lée

[050-083] Sera à la Saint-Jean gelée,

[050-084] On verra, par-dessus la glace,

[050-085] Sortir ceux d’Arras de leur place.”[43]

[050-086] “Well, scholar of Antichrist, may you be strangled with the

[050-087] entrails of your mother!” exclaimed Phœbus, and he gave the

[050-088] drunken scholar a rough push; the latter slipped against the

[050-089] wall, and slid flabbily to the pavement of Philip Augustus. A

[050-090] remnant of fraternal pity, which never abandons the heart of a

[050-091] drinker, prompted Phœbus to roll Jehan with his foot upon one of

[050-092] those pillows of the poor, which Providence keeps in readiness at

[050-093] the corner of all the street posts of Paris, and which the rich

[050-094] blight with the name of “a rubbish-heap.” The captain adjusted

[050-095] Jehan’s head upon an inclined plane of cabbage-stumps, and on the

[050-096] very instant, the scholar fell to snoring in a magnificent bass.

[050-097] Meanwhile, all malice was not extinguished in the captain’s

[050-098] heart. “So much the worse if the devil’s cart picks you up on its

[050-099] passage!” he said to the poor, sleeping clerk; and he strode off.

[050-100] The man in the mantle, who had not ceased to follow him, halted

[050-101] for a moment before the prostrate scholar, as though agitated by

[050-102] indecision; then, uttering a profound sigh, he also strode off in

[050-103] pursuit of the captain.

[050-104] We, like them, will leave Jehan to slumber beneath the open sky,

[050-105] and will follow them also, if it pleases the reader.

[050-106] On emerging into the Rue Saint-André-des-Arcs, Captain Phœbus

[050-107] perceived that some one was following him. On glancing sideways

[050-108] by chance, he perceived a sort of shadow crawling after him along

[050-109] the walls. He halted, it halted; he resumed his march, it resumed

[050-110] its march. This disturbed him not overmuch. “Ah, bah!” he said to

[050-111] himself, “I have not a sou.”

[050-112] He paused in front of the College d’Autun. It was at this college

[050-113] that he had sketched out what he called his studies, and, through

[050-114] a scholar’s teasing habit which still lingered in him, he never

[050-115] passed the façade without inflicting on the statue of Cardinal

[050-116] Pierre Bertrand, sculptured to the right of the portal, the

[050-117] affront of which Priapus complains so bitterly in the satire of

[050-118] Horace, _Olim truncus eram ficulnus_. He had done this with so

[050-119] much unrelenting animosity that the inscription, _Eduensis

[050-120] episcopus_, had become almost effaced. Therefore, he halted

[050-121] before the statue according to his wont. The street was utterly

[050-122] deserted. At the moment when he was coolly retying his shoulder

[050-123] knots, with his nose in the air, he saw the shadow approaching

[050-124] him with slow steps, so slow that he had ample time to observe

[050-125] that this shadow wore a cloak and a hat. On arriving near him, it

[050-126] halted and remained more motionless than the statue of Cardinal

[050-127] Bertrand. Meanwhile, it riveted upon Phœbus two intent eyes, full

[050-128] of that vague light which issues in the night time from the

[050-129] pupils of a cat.

[050-130] The captain was brave, and would have cared very little for a

[050-131] highwayman, with a rapier in his hand. But this walking statue,

[050-132] this petrified man, froze his blood. There were then in

[050-133] circulation, strange stories of a surly monk, a nocturnal prowler

[050-134] about the streets of Paris, and they recurred confusedly to his

[050-135] memory. He remained for several minutes in stupefaction, and

[050-136] finally broke the silence with a forced laugh.

[050-137] “Monsieur, if you are a robber, as I hope you are, you produce

[050-138] upon me the effect of a heron attacking a nutshell. I am the son

[050-139] of a ruined family, my dear fellow. Try your hand near by here.

[050-140] In the chapel of this college there is some wood of the true

[050-141] cross set in silver.”

[050-142] The hand of the shadow emerged from beneath its mantle and

[050-143] descended upon the arm of Phœbus with the grip of an eagle’s

[050-144] talon; at the same time the shadow spoke,—

[050-145] “Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers!”

[050-146] “What, the devil!” said Phœbus, “you know my name!”

[050-147] “I know not your name alone,” continued the man in the mantle,

[050-148] with his sepulchral voice. “You have a _rendezvous_ this

[050-149] evening.”

[050-150] “Yes,” replied Phœbus in amazement.

[050-151] “At seven o’clock.”

[050-152] “In a quarter of an hour.”

[050-153] “At la Falourdel’s.”

[050-154] “Precisely.”

[050-155] “The lewd hag of the Pont Saint-Michel.”

[050-156] “Of Saint Michel the archangel, as the Pater Noster saith.”

[050-157] “Impious wretch!” muttered the spectre. “With a woman?”

[050-158] “_Confiteor_,—I confess—.”

[050-159] “Who is called—?”

[050-160] “La Smeralda,” said Phœbus, gayly. All his heedlessness had

[050-161] gradually returned.

[050-162] At this name, the shadow’s grasp shook the arm of Phœbus in a

[050-163] fury.

[050-164] “Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers, thou liest!”

[050-165] Any one who could have beheld at that moment the captain’s

[050-166] inflamed countenance, his leap backwards, so violent that he

[050-167] disengaged himself from the grip which held him, the proud air

[050-168] with which he clapped his hand on his swordhilt, and, in the

[050-169] presence of this wrath the gloomy immobility of the man in the

[050-170] cloak,—any one who could have beheld this would have been

[050-171] frightened. There was in it a touch of the combat of Don Juan and

[050-172] the statue.

[050-173] “Christ and Satan!” exclaimed the captain. “That is a word which

[050-174] rarely strikes the ear of a Châteaupers! Thou wilt not dare

[050-175] repeat it.”

[050-176] “Thou liest!” said the shadow coldly.

[050-177] The captain gnashed his teeth. Surly monk, phantom,

[050-178] superstitions,—he had forgotten all at that moment. He no longer

[050-179] beheld anything but a man, and an insult.

[050-180] “Ah! this is well!” he stammered, in a voice stifled with rage.

[050-181] He drew his sword, then stammering, for anger as well as fear

[050-182] makes a man tremble: “Here! On the spot! Come on! Swords! Swords!

[050-183] Blood on the pavement!”

[050-184] But the other never stirred. When he beheld his adversary on

[050-185] guard and ready to parry,—

[050-186] “Captain Phœbus,” he said, and his tone vibrated with bitterness,

[050-187] “you forget your appointment.”

[050-188] The rages of men like Phœbus are milk-soups, whose ebullition is

[050-189] calmed by a drop of cold water. This simple remark caused the

[050-190] sword which glittered in the captain’s hand to be lowered.

[050-191] “Captain,” pursued the man, “to-morrow, the day after to-morrow,

[050-192] a month hence, ten years hence, you will find me ready to cut

[050-193] your throat; but go first to your rendezvous.”

[050-194] “In sooth,” said Phœbus, as though seeking to capitulate with

[050-195] himself, “these are two charming things to be encountered in a

[050-196] rendezvous,—a sword and a wench; but I do not see why I should

[050-197] miss the one for the sake of the other, when I can have both.”

[050-198] He replaced his sword in its scabbard.

[050-199] “Go to your rendezvous,” said the man.

[050-200] “Monsieur,” replied Phœbus with some embarrassment, “many thanks

[050-201] for your courtesy. In fact, there will be ample time to-morrow

[050-202] for us to chop up father Adam’s doublet into slashes and

[050-203] buttonholes. I am obliged to you for allowing me to pass one more

[050-204] agreeable quarter of an hour. I certainly did hope to put you in

[050-205] the gutter, and still arrive in time for the fair one, especially

[050-206] as it has a better appearance to make the women wait a little in

[050-207] such cases. But you strike me as having the air of a gallant man,

[050-208] and it is safer to defer our affair until to-morrow. So I will

[050-209] betake myself to my rendezvous; it is for seven o’clock, as you

[050-210] know.” Here Phœbus scratched his ear. “Ah. _Corne Dieu!_ I had

[050-211] forgotten! I haven’t a sou to discharge the price of the garret,

[050-212] and the old crone will insist on being paid in advance. She

[050-213] distrusts me.”
