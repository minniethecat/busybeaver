---[054-001] Meanwhile the procurator had exclaimed: “If the demon which

[054-002] possesses this goat, and which has resisted all exorcisms,

[054-003] persists in its deeds of witchcraft, if it alarms the court with

[054-004] them, we warn it that we shall be forced to put in requisition

[054-005] against it the gallows or the stake. Gringoire broke out into a

[054-006] cold perspiration. Charmolue took from the table the gypsy’s

[054-007] tambourine, and presenting it to the goat, in a certain manner,

[054-008] asked the latter,—

[054-009] “What o’clock is it?”

[054-010] The goat looked at it with an intelligent eye, raised its gilded

[054-011] hoof, and struck seven blows.

[054-012] It was, in fact, seven o’clock. A movement of terror ran through

[054-013] the crowd.

[054-014] Gringoire could not endure it.

[054-015] “He is destroying himself!” he cried aloud; “You see well that he

[054-016] does not know what he is doing.”

[054-017] “Silence among the louts at the end of the hall!” said the

[054-018] bailiff sharply.

[054-019] Jacques Charmolue, by the aid of the same manœuvres of the

[054-020] tambourine, made the goat perform many other tricks connected

[054-021] with the date of the day, the month of the year, etc., which the

[054-022] reader has already witnessed. And, by virtue of an optical

[054-023] illusion peculiar to judicial proceedings, these same spectators

[054-024] who had, probably, more than once applauded in the public square

[054-025] Djali’s innocent magic were terrified by it beneath the roof of

[054-026] the Palais de Justice. The goat was undoubtedly the devil.

[054-027] It was far worse when the procurator of the king, having emptied

[054-028] upon a floor a certain bag filled with movable letters, which

[054-029] Djali wore round his neck, they beheld the goat extract with his

[054-030] hoof from the scattered alphabet the fatal name of _Phœbus_. The

[054-031] witchcraft of which the captain had been the victim appeared

[054-032] irresistibly demonstrated, and in the eyes of all, the gypsy,

[054-033] that ravishing dancer, who had so often dazzled the passers-by

[054-034] with her grace, was no longer anything but a frightful vampire.

[054-035] However, she betrayed no sign of life; neither Djali’s graceful

[054-036] evolutions, nor the menaces of the court, nor the suppressed

[054-037] imprecations of the spectators any longer reached her mind.

[054-038] In order to arouse her, a police officer was obliged to shake her

[054-039] unmercifully, and the president had to raise his voice,—

[054-040] “Girl, you are of the Bohemian race, addicted to deeds of

[054-041] witchcraft. You, in complicity with the bewitched goat implicated

[054-042] in this suit, during the night of the twenty-ninth of March last,

[054-043] murdered and stabbed, in concert with the powers of darkness, by

[054-044] the aid of charms and underhand practices, a captain of the

[054-045] king’s arches of the watch, Phœbus de Châteaupers. Do you persist

[054-046] in denying it?”

[054-047] “Horror!” exclaimed the young girl, hiding her face in her hands.

[054-048] “My Phœbus! Oh, this is hell!”

[054-049] “Do you persist in your denial?” demanded the president coldly.

[054-050] “Do I deny it?” she said with terrible accents; and she rose with

[054-051] flashing eyes.

[054-052] The president continued squarely,—

[054-053] “Then how do you explain the facts laid to your charge?”

[054-054] She replied in a broken voice,—

[054-055] “I have already told you. I do not know. ’Twas a priest, a priest

[054-056] whom I do not know; an infernal priest who pursues me!”

[054-057] “That is it,” retorted the judge; “the surly monk.”

[054-058] “Oh, gentlemen! have mercy! I am but a poor girl—”

[054-059] “Of Egypt,” said the judge.

[054-060] Master Jacques Charmolue interposed sweetly,—

[054-061] “In view of the sad obstinacy of the accused, I demand the

[054-062] application of the torture.”

[054-063] “Granted,” said the president.

[054-064] The unhappy girl quivered in every limb. But she rose at the

[054-065] command of the men with partisans, and walked with a tolerably

[054-066] firm step, preceded by Charmolue and the priests of the

[054-067] officiality, between two rows of halberds, towards a medium-sized

[054-068] door which suddenly opened and closed again behind her, and which

[054-069] produced upon the grief-stricken Gringoire the effect of a

[054-070] horrible mouth which had just devoured her.

[054-071] When she disappeared, they heard a plaintive bleating; it was the

[054-072] little goat mourning.

[054-073] The sitting of the court was suspended. A counsellor having

[054-074] remarked that the gentlemen were fatigued, and that it would be a

[054-075] long time to wait until the torture was at an end, the president

[054-076] replied that a magistrate must know how to sacrifice himself to

[054-077] his duty.

[054-078] “What an annoying and vexatious hussy,” said an aged judge, “to

[054-079] get herself put to the question when one has not supped!”

[054-080] CHAPTER II. CONTINUATION OF THE CROWN WHICH WAS CHANGED INTO A

[054-081] DRY LEAF.

[054-082] After ascending and descending several steps in the corridors,

[054-083] which were so dark that they were lighted by lamps at midday, La

[054-084] Esmeralda, still surrounded by her lugubrious escort, was thrust

[054-085] by the police into a gloomy chamber. This chamber, circular in

[054-086] form, occupied the ground floor of one of those great towers,

[054-087] which, even in our own century, still pierce through the layer of

[054-088] modern edifices with which modern Paris has covered ancient

[054-089] Paris. There were no windows to this cellar; no other opening

[054-090] than the entrance, which was low, and closed by an enormous iron

[054-091] door. Nevertheless, light was not lacking; a furnace had been

[054-092] constructed in the thickness of the wall; a large fire was

[054-093] lighted there, which filled the vault with its crimson

[054-094] reflections and deprived a miserable candle, which stood in one

[054-095] corner, of all radiance. The iron grating which served to close

[054-096] the oven, being raised at that moment, allowed only a view at the

[054-097] mouth of the flaming vent-hole in the dark wall, the lower

[054-098] extremity of its bars, like a row of black and pointed teeth, set

[054-099] flat apart; which made the furnace resemble one of those mouths

[054-100] of dragons which spout forth flames in ancient legends. By the

[054-101] light which escaped from it, the prisoner beheld, all about the

[054-102] room, frightful instruments whose use she did not understand. In

[054-103] the centre lay a leather mattress, placed almost flat upon the

[054-104] ground, over which hung a strap provided with a buckle, attached

[054-105] to a brass ring in the mouth of a flat-nosed monster carved in

[054-106] the keystone of the vault. Tongs, pincers, large ploughshares,

[054-107] filled the interior of the furnace, and glowed in a confused heap

[054-108] on the coals. The sanguine light of the furnace illuminated in

[054-109] the chamber only a confused mass of horrible things.

[054-110] This Tartarus was called simply, The Question Chamber.

[054-111] On the bed, in a negligent attitude, sat Pierrat Torterue, the

[054-112] official torturer. His underlings, two gnomes with square faces,

[054-113] leather aprons, and linen breeches, were moving the iron

[054-114] instruments on the coals.

[054-115] In vain did the poor girl summon up her courage; on entering this

[054-116] chamber she was stricken with horror.

[054-117] The sergeants of the bailiff of the courts drew up in line on one

[054-118] side, the priests of the officiality on the other. A clerk,

[054-119] inkhorn, and a table were in one corner.

[054-120] Master Jacques Charmolue approached the gypsy with a very sweet

[054-121] smile.

[054-122] “My dear child,” said he, “do you still persist in your denial?”

[054-123] “Yes,” she replied, in a dying voice.

[054-124] “In that case,” replied Charmolue, “it will be very painful for

[054-125] us to have to question you more urgently than we should like.

[054-126] Pray take the trouble to seat yourself on this bed. Master

[054-127] Pierrat, make room for mademoiselle, and close the door.”

[054-128] Pierrat rose with a growl.

[054-129] “If I shut the door,” he muttered, “my fire will go out.”

[054-130] “Well, my dear fellow,” replied Charmolue, “leave it open then.”

[054-131] Meanwhile, la Esmeralda had remained standing. That leather bed

[054-132] on which so many unhappy wretches had writhed, frightened her.

[054-133] Terror chilled the very marrow of her bones; she stood there

[054-134] bewildered and stupefied. At a sign from Charmolue, the two

[054-135] assistants took her and placed her in a sitting posture on the

[054-136] bed. They did her no harm; but when these men touched her, when

[054-137] that leather touched her, she felt all her blood retreat to her

[054-138] heart. She cast a frightened look around the chamber. It seemed

[054-139] to her as though she beheld advancing from all quarters towards

[054-140] her, with the intention of crawling up her body and biting and

[054-141] pinching her, all those hideous implements of torture, which as

[054-142] compared to the instruments of all sorts she had hitherto seen,

[054-143] were like what bats, centipedes, and spiders are among insects

[054-144] and birds.

[054-145] “Where is the physician?” asked Charmolue.

[054-146] “Here,” replied a black gown whom she had not before noticed.

[054-147] She shuddered.

[054-148] “Mademoiselle,” resumed the caressing voice of the procucrator of

[054-149] the Ecclesiastical court, “for the third time, do you persist in

[054-150] denying the deeds of which you are accused?”

[054-151] This time she could only make a sign with her head.

[054-152] “You persist?” said Jacques Charmolue. “Then it grieves me

[054-153] deeply, but I must fulfil my office.”

[054-154] “Monsieur le Procureur du Roi,” said Pierrat abruptly, “How shall

[054-155] we begin?”

[054-156] Charmolue hesitated for a moment with the ambiguous grimace of a

[054-157] poet in search of a rhyme.

[054-158] “With the boot,” he said at last.

[054-159] The unfortunate girl felt herself so utterly abandoned by God and

[054-160] men, that her head fell upon her breast like an inert thing which

[054-161] has no power in itself.

[054-162] The tormentor and the physician approached her simultaneously. At

[054-163] the same time, the two assistants began to fumble among their

[054-164] hideous arsenal.

[054-165] At the clanking of their frightful irons, the unhappy child

[054-166] quivered like a dead frog which is being galvanized. “Oh!” she

[054-167] murmured, so low that no one heard her; “Oh, my Phœbus!” Then she

[054-168] fell back once more into her immobility and her marble silence.

[054-169] This spectacle would have rent any other heart than those of her

[054-170] judges. One would have pronounced her a poor sinful soul, being

[054-171] tortured by Satan beneath the scarlet wicket of hell. The

[054-172] miserable body which that frightful swarm of saws, wheels, and

[054-173] racks were about to clasp in their clutches, the being who was

[054-174] about to be manipulated by the harsh hands of executioners and

[054-175] pincers, was that gentle, white, fragile creature, a poor grain

[054-176] of millet which human justice was handing over to the terrible

[054-177] mills of torture to grind. Meanwhile, the callous hands of

[054-178] Pierrat Torterue’s assistants had bared that charming leg, that

[054-179] tiny foot, which had so often amazed the passers-by with their

[054-180] delicacy and beauty, in the squares of Paris.

[054-181] “’Tis a shame!” muttered the tormentor, glancing at these

[054-182] graceful and delicate forms.

[054-183] Had the archdeacon been present, he certainly would have recalled

[054-184] at that moment his symbol of the spider and the fly. Soon the

[054-185] unfortunate girl, through a mist which spread before her eyes,

[054-186] beheld the boot approach; she soon beheld her foot encased

[054-187] between iron plates disappear in the frightful apparatus. Then

[054-188] terror restored her strength.

[054-189] “Take that off!” she cried angrily; and drawing herself up, with

[054-190] her hair all dishevelled: “Mercy!”

[054-191] She darted from the bed to fling herself at the feet of the

[054-192] king’s procurator, but her leg was fast in the heavy block of oak

[054-193] and iron, and she sank down upon the boot, more crushed than a

[054-194] bee with a lump of lead on its wing.

[054-195] At a sign from Charmolue, she was replaced on the bed, and two

[054-196] coarse hands adjusted to her delicate waist the strap which hung

[054-197] from the ceiling.

[054-198] “For the last time, do you confess the facts in the case?”

[054-199] demanded Charmolue, with his imperturbable benignity.

[054-200] “I am innocent.”

[054-201] “Then, mademoiselle, how do you explain the circumstance laid to

[054-202] your charge?”

[054-203] “Alas, monseigneur, I do not know.”

[054-204] “So you deny them?”

[054-205] “All!”

[054-206] “Proceed,” said Charmolue to Pierrat.

[054-207] Pierrat turned the handle of the screw-jack, the boot was

[054-208] contracted, and the unhappy girl uttered one of those horrible

[054-209] cries which have no orthography in any human language.

[054-210] “Stop!” said Charmolue to Pierrat. “Do you confess?” he said to

[054-211] the gypsy.

[054-212] “All!” cried the wretched girl. “I confess! I confess! Mercy!”

[054-213] She had not calculated her strength when she faced the torture.

[054-214] Poor child, whose life up to that time had been so joyous, so

[054-215] pleasant, so sweet, the first pain had conquered her!
