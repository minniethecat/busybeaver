# Medicine

Medicine in Notre-Dame de Paris appears primarily through the character of Claude Frollo, whose intellectual pursuits span medicine, alchemy, and astrology. Hugo depicts medieval medicine as a transitional discipline — caught between empirical observation and hermetic speculation — and uses it to characterize Frollo's restless, polymathic intelligence.

## Definition

In Hugo's novel, medicine is one facet of the broader medieval system of knowledge that Frollo embodies. It is presented not as a separate profession but as part of an integrated pursuit of understanding that merges the physical, the celestial, and the occult. The practice of medicine in fifteenth-century Paris drew on classical authorities, Arab scholarship, and Christian theology, making it a contested and evolving field.

## Hugo's Treatment

### In the Narrative

Claude Frollo's study of medicine is presented as one stage in his broader intellectual journey. After completing his theological studies, "he flung himself upon medicine" with characteristic intensity [025-079]. Hugo traces Frollo's progression through the sciences: having exhausted canon law, he turned to medicine; having exhausted medicine, he turned to alchemy and astrology [025-079] [030-004].

The character of Jacques Coictier, the king's physician, provides a counterpoint to Frollo's esoteric approach. When Coictier visits Frollo's cell, he scoffs at the archdeacon's hermetic studies, declaring that medicine is beneath such devotion [029-194]. The confrontation between Coictier and Frollo stages a broader debate about the nature of medical knowledge: Coictier represents practical, court-sanctioned medicine, while Frollo pursues a more speculative synthesis of medicine, astrology, and alchemy.

### In the Author's Voice

Hugo's narrator does not take sides in the debate between practical and speculative medicine. Instead, he presents both Coictier and Frollo as limited by their era's understanding. The archdeacon is challenged for asking "why blaspheme medicine and astrology?" — treating the two disciplines as coequal branches of knowledge [029-185]. The narrator's ironic distance suggests that both characters are prisoners of medieval epistemology.

The novel also mentions folk medicine in passing: a "formula against the bite of" some creature appears in the context of popular remedies, contrasting with the learned medicine of Frollo and Coictier [048-117]. This tripartite division — folk remedies, court medicine, and hermetic medicine — maps onto Hugo's broader interest in the social stratification of knowledge.
