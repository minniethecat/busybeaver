---[055-001] “Humanity forces me to tell you,” remarked the king’s procurator,

[055-002] “that in confessing, it is death that you must expect.”

[055-003] “I certainly hope so!” said she. And she fell back upon the

[055-004] leather bed, dying, doubled up, allowing herself to hang

[055-005] suspended from the strap buckled round her waist.

[055-006] “Come, fair one, hold up a little,” said Master Pierrat, raising

[055-007] her. “You have the air of the lamb of the Golden Fleece which

[055-008] hangs from Monsieur de Bourgogne’s neck.”

[055-009] Jacques Charmolue raised his voice,

[055-010] “Clerk, write. Young Bohemian maid, you confess your

[055-011] participation in the feasts, witches’ sabbaths, and witchcrafts

[055-012] of hell, with ghosts, hags, and vampires? Answer.”

[055-013] “Yes,” she said, so low that her words were lost in her

[055-014] breathing.

[055-015] “You confess to having seen the ram which Beelzebub causes to

[055-016] appear in the clouds to call together the witches’ sabbath, and

[055-017] which is beheld by socerers alone?”

[055-018] “Yes.”

[055-019] “You confess to having adored the heads of Bophomet, those

[055-020] abominable idols of the Templars?”

[055-021] “Yes.”

[055-022] “To having had habitual dealings with the devil under the form of

[055-023] a goat familiar, joined with you in the suit?”

[055-024] “Yes.”

[055-025] “Lastly, you avow and confess to having, with the aid of the

[055-026] demon, and of the phantom vulgarly known as the surly monk, on

[055-027] the night of the twenty-ninth of March last, murdered and

[055-028] assassinated a captain named Phœbus de Châteaupers?”

[055-029] She raised her large, staring eyes to the magistrate, and

[055-030] replied, as though mechanically, without convulsion or

[055-031] agitation,—

[055-032] “Yes.”

[055-033] It was evident that everything within her was broken.

[055-034] “Write, clerk,” said Charmolue. And, addressing the torturers,

[055-035] “Release the prisoner, and take her back to the court.”

[055-036] When the prisoner had been “unbooted,” the procurator of the

[055-037] ecclesiastical court examined her foot, which was still swollen

[055-038] with pain. “Come,” said he, “there’s no great harm done. You

[055-039] shrieked in good season. You could still dance, my beauty!”

[055-040] Then he turned to his acolytes of the officiality,—

[055-041] “Behold justice enlightened at last! This is a solace, gentlemen!

[055-042] Madamoiselle will bear us witness that we have acted with all

[055-043] possible gentleness.”

[055-044] CHAPTER III. END OF THE CROWN WHICH WAS TURNED INTO A DRY LEAF.

[055-045] When she re-entered the audience hall, pale and limping, she was

[055-046] received with a general murmur of pleasure. On the part of the

[055-047] audience there was the feeling of impatience gratified which one

[055-048] experiences at the theatre at the end of the last entr’acte of

[055-049] the comedy, when the curtain rises and the conclusion is about to

[055-050] begin. On the part of the judges, it was the hope of getting

[055-051] their suppers sooner.

[055-052] The little goat also bleated with joy. He tried to run towards

[055-053] his mistress, but they had tied him to the bench.

[055-054] Night was fully set in. The candles, whose number had not been

[055-055] increased, cast so little light, that the walls of the hall could

[055-056] not be seen. The shadows there enveloped all objects in a sort of

[055-057] mist. A few apathetic faces of judges alone could be dimly

[055-058] discerned. Opposite them, at the extremity of the long hall, they

[055-059] could see a vaguely white point standing out against the sombre

[055-060] background. This was the accused.

[055-061] She had dragged herself to her place. When Charmolue had

[055-062] installed himself in a magisterial manner in his own, he seated

[055-063] himself, then rose and said, without exhibiting too much

[055-064] self-complacency at his success,—“The accused has confessed all.”

[055-065] “Bohemian girl,” the president continued, “have you avowed all

[055-066] your deeds of magic, prostitution, and assassination on Phœbus de

[055-067] Châteaupers.”

[055-068] Her heart contracted. She was heard to sob amid the darkness.

[055-069] “Anything you like,” she replied feebly, “but kill me quickly!”

[055-070] “Monsieur, procurator of the king in the ecclesiastical courts,”

[055-071] said the president, “the chamber is ready to hear you in your

[055-072] charge.”

[055-073] Master Charmolue exhibited an alarming note book, and began to

[055-074] read, with many gestures and the exaggerated accentuation of the

[055-075] pleader, an oration in Latin, wherein all the proofs of the suit

[055-076] were piled up in Ciceronian periphrases, flanked with quotations

[055-077] from Plautus, his favorite comic author. We regret that we are

[055-078] not able to offer to our readers this remarkable piece. The

[055-079] orator pronounced it with marvellous action. Before he had

[055-080] finished the exordium, the perspiration was starting from his

[055-081] brow, and his eyes from his head.

[055-082] All at once, in the middle of a fine period, he interrupted

[055-083] himself, and his glance, ordinarily so gentle and even stupid,

[055-084] became menacing.

[055-085] “Gentlemen,” he exclaimed (this time in French, for it was not in

[055-086] his copy book), “Satan is so mixed up in this affair, that here

[055-087] he is present at our debates, and making sport of their majesty.

[055-088] Behold!”

[055-089] So saying, he pointed to the little goat, who, on seeing

[055-090] Charmolue gesticulating, had, in point of fact, thought it

[055-091] appropriate to do the same, and had seated himself on his

[055-092] haunches, reproducing to the best of his ability, with his

[055-093] forepaws and his bearded head the pathetic pantomine of the

[055-094] king’s procurator in the ecclesiastical court. This was, if the

[055-095] reader remembers, one of his prettiest accomplishments. This

[055-096] incident, this last proof, produced a great effect. The goat’s

[055-097] hoofs were tied, and the king’s procurator resumed the thread of

[055-098] his eloquence.

[055-099] It was very long, but the peroration was admirable. Here is the

[055-100] concluding phrase; let the reader add the hoarse voice and the

[055-101] breathless gestures of Master Charmolue,

[055-102] “_Ideo, domni, coram stryga demonstrata, crimine patente,

[055-103] intentione criminis existente, in nomine sanctæ ecclesiæ

[055-104] Nostræ-Dominæ Parisiensis quæ est in saisina habendi omnimodam

[055-105] altam et bassam justitiam in illa hac intemerata Civitatis

[055-106] insula, tenore præsentium declaremus nos requirere, primo,

[055-107] aliquamdam pecuniariam indemnitatem; secundo, amendationem

[055-108] honorabilem ante portalium maximum Nostræ-Dominæ, ecclesiæ

[055-109] cathedralis; tertio, sententiam in virtute cujus ista styrga cum

[055-110] sua capella, seu in trivio vulgariter dicto_ la Grève, _seu in

[055-111] insula exeunte in fluvio Secanæ, juxta pointam juardini regalis,

[055-112] executatæ sint!_”[45]

[055-113] He put on his cap again and seated himself.

[055-114] “_Eheu!_” sighed the broken-hearted Gringoire, “_bassa

[055-115] latinitas_—bastard latin!”

[055-116] Another man in a black gown rose near the accused; he was her

[055-117] lawyer. The judges, who were fasting, began to grumble.

[055-118] “Advocate, be brief,” said the president.

[055-119] “Monsieur the President,” replied the advocate, “since the

[055-120] defendant has confessed the crime, I have only one word to say to

[055-121] these gentlemen. Here is a text from the Salic law; ‘If a witch

[055-122] hath eaten a man, and if she be convicted of it, she shall pay a

[055-123] fine of eight thousand deniers, which amount to two hundred sous

[055-124] of gold.’ May it please the chamber to condemn my client to the

[055-125] fine?”

[055-126] “An abrogated text,” said the advocate extraordinary of the king.

[055-127] “_Nego_, I deny it,” replied the advocate.

[055-128] “Put it to the vote!” said one of the councillors; “the crime is

[055-129] manifest, and it is late.”

[055-130] They proceeded to take a vote without leaving the room. The

[055-131] judges signified their assent without giving their reasons, they

[055-132] were in a hurry. Their capped heads were seen uncovering one

[055-133] after the other, in the gloom, at the lugubrious question

[055-134] addressed to them by the president in a low voice. The poor

[055-135] accused had the appearance of looking at them, but her troubled

[055-136] eye no longer saw.

[055-137] Then the clerk began to write; then he handed a long parchment to

[055-138] the president.

[055-139] Then the unhappy girl heard the people moving, the pikes

[055-140] clashing, and a freezing voice saying to her,—

[055-141] “Bohemian wench, on the day when it shall seem good to our lord

[055-142] the king, at the hour of noon, you will be taken in a tumbrel, in

[055-143] your shift, with bare feet, and a rope about your neck, before

[055-144] the grand portal of Notre-Dame, and you will there make an

[055-145] apology with a wax torch of the weight of two pounds in your

[055-146] hand, and thence you will be conducted to the Place de Grève,

[055-147] where you will be hanged and strangled on the town gibbet; and

[055-148] likewise your goat; and you will pay to the official three lions

[055-149] of gold, in reparation of the crimes by you committed and by you

[055-150] confessed, of sorcery and magic, debauchery and murder, upon the

[055-151] person of the Sieur Phœbus de Châteaupers. May God have mercy on

[055-152] your soul!”

[055-153] “Oh! ’tis a dream!” she murmured; and she felt rough hands

[055-154] bearing her away.

[055-155] CHAPTER IV. _LASCIATE OGNI SPERANZA_—LEAVE ALL HOPE BEHIND, YE

[055-156] WHO ENTER HERE.

[055-157] In the Middle Ages, when an edifice was complete, there was

[055-158] almost as much of it in the earth as above it. Unless built upon

[055-159] piles, like Notre-Dame, a palace, a fortress, a church, had

[055-160] always a double bottom. In cathedrals, it was, in some sort,

[055-161] another subterranean cathedral, low, dark, mysterious, blind, and

[055-162] mute, under the upper nave which was overflowing with light and

[055-163] reverberating with organs and bells day and night. Sometimes it

[055-164] was a sepulchre. In palaces, in fortresses, it was a prison,

[055-165] sometimes a sepulchre also, sometimes both together. These mighty

[055-166] buildings, whose mode of formation and _vegetation_ we have

[055-167] elsewhere explained, had not simply _foundations_, but, so to

[055-168] speak, roots which ran branching through the soil in chambers,

[055-169] galleries, and staircases, like the construction above. Thus

[055-170] churches, palaces, fortresses, had the earth half way up their

[055-171] bodies. The cellars of an edifice formed another edifice, into

[055-172] which one descended instead of ascending, and which extended its

[055-173] subterranean grounds under the external piles of the monument,

[055-174] like those forests and mountains which are reversed in the

[055-175] mirror-like waters of a lake, beneath the forests and mountains

[055-176] of the banks.

[055-177] At the fortress of Saint-Antoine, at the Palais de Justice of

[055-178] Paris, at the Louvre, these subterranean edifices were prisons.

[055-179] The stories of these prisons, as they sank into the soil, grew

[055-180] constantly narrower and more gloomy. They were so many zones,

[055-181] where the shades of horror were graduated. Dante could never

[055-182] imagine anything better for his hell. These tunnels of cells

[055-183] usually terminated in a sack of a lowest dungeon, with a vat-like

[055-184] bottom, where Dante placed Satan, where society placed those

[055-185] condemned to death. A miserable human existence, once interred

[055-186] there; farewell light, air, life, _ogni speranza_—every hope; it

[055-187] only came forth to the scaffold or the stake. Sometimes it rotted

[055-188] there; human justice called this _forgetting_. Between men and

[055-189] himself, the condemned man felt a pile of stones and jailers

[055-190] weighing down upon his head; and the entire prison, the massive

[055-191] bastille was nothing more than an enormous, complicated lock,

[055-192] which barred him off from the rest of the world.

[055-193] It was in a sloping cavity of this description, in the

[055-194] _oubliettes_ excavated by Saint-Louis, in the _inpace_ of the

[055-195] Tournelle, that la Esmeralda had been placed on being condemned

[055-196] to death, through fear of her escape, no doubt, with the colossal

[055-197] court-house over her head. Poor fly, who could not have lifted

[055-198] even one of its blocks of stone!

[055-199] Assuredly, Providence and society had been equally unjust; such

[055-200] an excess of unhappiness and of torture was not necessary to

[055-201] break so frail a creature.

[055-202] There she lay, lost in the shadows, buried, hidden, immured. Any

[055-203] one who could have beheld her in this state, after having seen

[055-204] her laugh and dance in the sun, would have shuddered. Cold as

[055-205] night, cold as death, not a breath of air in her tresses, not a

[055-206] human sound in her ear, no longer a ray of light in her eyes;

[055-207] snapped in twain, crushed with chains, crouching beside a jug and

[055-208] a loaf, on a little straw, in a pool of water, which was formed

[055-209] under her by the sweating of the prison walls; without motion,

[055-210] almost without breath, she had no longer the power to suffer;

[055-211] Phœbus, the sun, midday, the open air, the streets of Paris, the

[055-212] dances with applause, the sweet babblings of love with the

[055-213] officer; then the priest, the old crone, the poignard, the blood,

[055-214] the torture, the gibbet; all this did, indeed, pass before her

[055-215] mind, sometimes as a charming and golden vision, sometimes as a

[055-216] hideous nightmare; but it was no longer anything but a vague and

[055-217] horrible struggle, lost in the gloom, or distant music played up

[055-218] above ground, and which was no longer audible at the depth where

[055-219] the unhappy girl had fallen.
