---[072-001] Then his fears returned to him, the idea of an attempt against

[072-002] the gypsy presented itself once more to his mind. He was

[072-003] conscious, in a confused way, that a violent crisis was

[072-004] approaching. At that critical moment he took counsel with

[072-005] himself, with better and prompter reasoning than one would have

[072-006] expected from so badly organized a brain. Ought he to awaken the

[072-007] gypsy? to make her escape? Whither? The streets were invested,

[072-008] the church backed on the river. No boat, no issue!—There was but

[072-009] one thing to be done; to allow himself to be killed on the

[072-010] threshold of Notre-Dame, to resist at least until succor arrived,

[072-011] if it should arrive, and not to trouble la Esmeralda’s sleep.

[072-012] This resolution once taken, he set to examining the enemy with

[072-013] more tranquillity.

[072-014] The throng seemed to increase every moment in the church square.

[072-015] Only, he presumed that it must be making very little noise, since

[072-016] the windows on the Place remained closed. All at once, a flame

[072-017] flashed up, and in an instant seven or eight lighted torches

[072-018] passed over the heads of the crowd, shaking their tufts of flame

[072-019] in the deep shade. Quasimodo then beheld distinctly surging in

[072-020] the Parvis a frightful herd of men and women in rags, armed with

[072-021] scythes, pikes, billhooks and partisans, whose thousand points

[072-022] glittered. Here and there black pitchforks formed horns to the

[072-023] hideous faces. He vaguely recalled this populace, and thought

[072-024] that he recognized all the heads who had saluted him as Pope of

[072-025] the Fools some months previously. One man who held a torch in one

[072-026] hand and a club in the other, mounted a stone post and seemed to

[072-027] be haranguing them. At the same time the strange army executed

[072-028] several evolutions, as though it were taking up its post around

[072-029] the church. Quasimodo picked up his lantern and descended to the

[072-030] platform between the towers, in order to get a nearer view, and

[072-031] to spy out a means of defence.

[072-032] Clopin Trouillefou, on arriving in front of the lofty portal of

[072-033] Notre-Dame had, in fact, ranged his troops in order of battle.

[072-034] Although he expected no resistance, he wished, like a prudent

[072-035] general, to preserve an order which would permit him to face, at

[072-036] need, a sudden attack of the watch or the police. He had

[072-037] accordingly stationed his brigade in such a manner that, viewed

[072-038] from above and from a distance, one would have pronounced it the

[072-039] Roman triangle of the battle of Ecnomus, the boar’s head of

[072-040] Alexander or the famous wedge of Gustavus Adolphus. The base of

[072-041] this triangle rested on the back of the Place in such a manner as

[072-042] to bar the entrance of the Rue du Parvis; one of its sides faced

[072-043] Hôtel-Dieu, the other the Rue Saint-Pierre-aux-Bœufs. Clopin

[072-044] Trouillefou had placed himself at the apex with the Duke of

[072-045] Egypt, our friend Jehan, and the most daring of the scavengers.

[072-046] An enterprise like that which the vagabonds were now undertaking

[072-047] against Notre-Dame was not a very rare thing in the cities of the

[072-048] Middle Ages. What we now call the “police” did not exist then. In

[072-049] populous cities, especially in capitals, there existed no single,

[072-050] central, regulating power. Feudalism had constructed these great

[072-051] communities in a singular manner. A city was an assembly of a

[072-052] thousand seigneuries, which divided it into compartments of all

[072-053] shapes and sizes. Hence, a thousand conflicting establishments of

[072-054] police; that is to say, no police at all. In Paris, for example,

[072-055] independently of the hundred and forty-one lords who laid claim

[072-056] to a manor, there were five and twenty who laid claim to a manor

[072-057] and to administering justice, from the Bishop of Paris, who had

[072-058] five hundred streets, to the Prior of Notre-Dame des Champs, who

[072-059] had four. All these feudal justices recognized the suzerain

[072-060] authority of the king only in name. All possessed the right of

[072-061] control over the roads. All were at home. Louis XI., that

[072-062] indefatigable worker, who so largely began the demolition of the

[072-063] feudal edifice, continued by Richelieu and Louis XIV. for the

[072-064] profit of royalty, and finished by Mirabeau for the benefit of

[072-065] the people,—Louis XI. had certainly made an effort to break this

[072-066] network of seignories which covered Paris, by throwing violently

[072-067] across them all two or three troops of general police. Thus, in

[072-068] 1465, an order to the inhabitants to light candles in their

[072-069] windows at nightfall, and to shut up their dogs under penalty of

[072-070] death; in the same year, an order to close the streets in the

[072-071] evening with iron chains, and a prohibition to wear daggers or

[072-072] weapons of offence in the streets at night. But in a very short

[072-073] time, all these efforts at communal legislation fell into

[072-074] abeyance. The _bourgeois_ permitted the wind to blow out their

[072-075] candles in the windows, and their dogs to stray; the iron chains

[072-076] were stretched only in a state of siege; the prohibition to wear

[072-077] daggers wrought no other changes than from the name of the Rue

[072-078] Coupe-Gueule to the name of the Rue-Coupe-Gorge[60] which is an

[072-079] evident progress. The old scaffolding of feudal jurisdictions

[072-080] remained standing; an immense aggregation of bailiwicks and

[072-081] seignories crossing each other all over the city, interfering

[072-082] with each other, entangled in one another, enmeshing each other,

[072-083] trespassing on each other; a useless thicket of watches,

[072-084] sub-watches and counter-watches, over which, with armed force,

[072-085] passed brigandage, rapine, and sedition. Hence, in this disorder,

[072-086] deeds of violence on the part of the populace directed against a

[072-087] palace, a hôtel, or house in the most thickly populated quarters,

[072-088] were not unheard-of occurrences. In the majority of such cases,

[072-089] the neighbors did not meddle with the matter unless the pillaging

[072-090] extended to themselves. They stopped up their ears to the musket

[072-091] shots, closed their shutters, barricaded their doors, allowed the

[072-092] matter to be concluded with or without the watch, and the next

[072-093] day it was said in Paris, “Étienne Barbette was broken open last

[072-094] night. The Marshal de Clermont was seized last night, etc.”

[072-095] Hence, not only the royal habitations, the Louvre, the Palace,

[072-096] the Bastille, the Tournelles, but simply seignorial residences,

[072-097] the Petit-Bourbon, the Hôtel de Sens, the Hôtel d’Angoulême,

[072-098] etc., had battlements on their walls, and machicolations over

[072-099] their doors. Churches were guarded by their sanctity. Some, among

[072-100] the number Notre-Dame, were fortified. The Abbey of

[072-101] Saint-Germain-des-Prés was castellated like a baronial mansion,

[072-102] and more brass expended about it in bombards than in bells. Its

[072-103] fortress was still to be seen in 1610. To-day, barely its church

[072-104] remains.

[072-105] Let us return to Notre-Dame.

[072-106] When the first arrangements were completed, and we must say, to

[072-107] the honor of vagabond discipline, that Clopin’s orders were

[072-108] executed in silence, and with admirable precision, the worthy

[072-109] chief of the band, mounted on the parapet of the church square,

[072-110] and raised his hoarse and surly voice, turning towards

[072-111] Notre-Dame, and brandishing his torch whose light, tossed by the

[072-112] wind, and veiled every moment by its own smoke, made the reddish

[072-113] façade of the church appear and disappear before the eye.

[072-114] “To you, Louis de Beaumont, bishop of Paris, counsellor in the

[072-115] Court of Parliament, I, Clopin Trouillefou, king of Thunes, grand

[072-116] Coësre, prince of Argot, bishop of fools, I say: Our sister,

[072-117] falsely condemned for magic, hath taken refuge in your church,

[072-118] you owe her asylum and safety. Now the Court of Parliament wishes

[072-119] to seize her once more there, and you consent to it; so that she

[072-120] would be hanged to-morrow in the Grève, if God and the outcasts

[072-121] were not here. If your church is sacred, so is our sister; if our

[072-122] sister is not sacred, neither is your church. That is why we call

[072-123] upon you to return the girl if you wish to save your church, or

[072-124] we will take possession of the girl again and pillage the church,

[072-125] which will be a good thing. In token of which I here plant my

[072-126] banner, and may God preserve you, bishop of Paris.”

[072-127] Quasimodo could not, unfortunately, hear these words uttered with

[072-128] a sort of sombre and savage majesty. A vagabond presented his

[072-129] banner to Clopin, who planted it solemnly between two

[072-130] paving-stones. It was a pitchfork from whose points hung a

[072-131] bleeding quarter of carrion meat.

[072-132] That done, the King of Thunes turned round and cast his eyes over

[072-133] his army, a fierce multitude whose glances flashed almost equally

[072-134] with their pikes. After a momentary pause,—“Forward, my Sons!” he

[072-135] cried; “to work, locksmiths!”

[072-136] Thirty bold men, square shouldered, and with pick-lock faces,

[072-137] stepped from the ranks, with hammers, pincers, and bars of iron

[072-138] on their shoulders. They betook themselves to the principal door

[072-139] of the church, ascended the steps, and were soon to be seen

[072-140] squatting under the arch, working at the door with pincers and

[072-141] levers; a throng of vagabonds followed them to help or look on.

[072-142] The eleven steps before the portal were covered with them.

[072-143] But the door stood firm. “The devil! ’tis hard and obstinate!”

[072-144] said one. “It is old, and its gristles have become bony,” said

[072-145] another. “Courage, comrades!” resumed Clopin. “I wager my head

[072-146] against a dipper that you will have opened the door, rescued the

[072-147] girl, and despoiled the chief altar before a single beadle is

[072-148] awake. Stay! I think I hear the lock breaking up.”

[072-149] Clopin was interrupted by a frightful uproar which re-sounded

[072-150] behind him at that moment. He wheeled round. An enormous beam had

[072-151] just fallen from above; it had crushed a dozen vagabonds on the

[072-152] pavement with the sound of a cannon, breaking in addition, legs

[072-153] here and there in the crowd of beggars, who sprang aside with

[072-154] cries of terror. In a twinkling, the narrow precincts of the

[072-155] church parvis were cleared. The locksmiths, although protected by

[072-156] the deep vaults of the portal, abandoned the door and Clopin

[072-157] himself retired to a respectful distance from the church.

[072-158] “I had a narrow escape!” cried Jehan. “I felt the wind, of it,

[072-159] _tête-de-bœuf!_ but Pierre the Slaughterer is slaughtered!”

[072-160] It is impossible to describe the astonishment mingled with fright

[072-161] which fell upon the ruffians in company with this beam.

[072-162] They remained for several minutes with their eyes in the air,

[072-163] more dismayed by that piece of wood than by the king’s twenty

[072-164] thousand archers.

[072-165] “Satan!” muttered the Duke of Egypt, “this smacks of magic!”

[072-166] “’Tis the moon which threw this log at us,” said Andry the Red.

[072-167] “Call the moon the friend of the Virgin, after that!” went on

[072-168] François Chanteprune.

[072-169] “A thousand popes!” exclaimed Clopin, “you are all fools!” But he

[072-170] did not know how to explain the fall of the beam.

[072-171] Meanwhile, nothing could be distinguished on the façade, to whose

[072-172] summit the light of the torches did not reach. The heavy beam lay

[072-173] in the middle of the enclosure, and groans were heard from the

[072-174] poor wretches who had received its first shock, and who had been

[072-175] almost cut in twain, on the angle of the stone steps.

[072-176] The King of Thunes, his first amazement passed, finally found an

[072-177] explanation which appeared plausible to his companions.

[072-178] “Throat of God! are the canons defending themselves? To the sack,

[072-179] then! to the sack!”

[072-180] “To the sack!” repeated the rabble, with a furious hurrah. A

[072-181] discharge of crossbows and hackbuts against the front of the

[072-182] church followed.

[072-183] At this detonation, the peaceable inhabitants of the surrounding

[072-184] houses woke up; many windows were seen to open, and nightcaps and

[072-185] hands holding candles appeared at the casements.

[072-186] “Fire at the windows,” shouted Clopin. The windows were

[072-187] immediately closed, and the poor _bourgeois_, who had hardly had

[072-188] time to cast a frightened glance on this scene of gleams and

[072-189] tumult, returned, perspiring with fear to their wives, asking

[072-190] themselves whether the witches’ sabbath was now being held in the

[072-191] parvis of Notre-Dame, or whether there was an assault of

[072-192] Burgundians, as in ’64. Then the husbands thought of theft; the

[072-193] wives, of rape; and all trembled.
