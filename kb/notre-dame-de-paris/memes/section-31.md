---[031-001] The generating idea, the word, was not only at the foundation of

[031-002] all these edifices, but also in the form. The temple of Solomon,

[031-003] for example, was not alone the binding of the holy book; it was

[031-004] the holy book itself. On each one of its concentric walls, the

[031-005] priests could read the word translated and manifested to the eye,

[031-006] and thus they followed its transformations from sanctuary to

[031-007] sanctuary, until they seized it in its last tabernacle, under its

[031-008] most concrete form, which still belonged to architecture: the

[031-009] arch. Thus the word was enclosed in an edifice, but its image was

[031-010] upon its envelope, like the human form on the coffin of a mummy.

[031-011] And not only the form of edifices, but the sites selected for

[031-012] them, revealed the thought which they represented, according as

[031-013] the symbol to be expressed was graceful or grave. Greece crowned

[031-014] her mountains with a temple harmonious to the eye; India

[031-015] disembowelled hers, to chisel therein those monstrous

[031-016] subterranean pagodas, borne up by gigantic rows of granite

[031-017] elephants.

[031-018] Thus, during the first six thousand years of the world, from the

[031-019] most immemorial pagoda of Hindustan, to the cathedral of Cologne,

[031-020] architecture was the great handwriting of the human race. And

[031-021] this is so true, that not only every religious symbol, but every

[031-022] human thought, has its page and its monument in that immense

[031-023] book.

[031-024] All civilization begins in theocracy and ends in democracy. This

[031-025] law of liberty following unity is written in architecture. For,

[031-026] let us insist upon this point, masonry must not be thought to be

[031-027] powerful only in erecting the temple and in expressing the myth

[031-028] and sacerdotal symbolism; in inscribing in hieroglyphs upon its

[031-029] pages of stone the mysterious tables of the law. If it were

[031-030] thus,—as there comes in all human society a moment when the

[031-031] sacred symbol is worn out and becomes obliterated under freedom

[031-032] of thought, when man escapes from the priest, when the

[031-033] excrescence of philosophies and systems devour the face of

[031-034] religion,—architecture could not reproduce this new state of

[031-035] human thought; its leaves, so crowded on the face, would be empty

[031-036] on the back; its work would be mutilated; its book would be

[031-037] incomplete. But no.

[031-038] Let us take as an example the Middle Ages, where we see more

[031-039] clearly because it is nearer to us. During its first period,

[031-040] while theocracy is organizing Europe, while the Vatican is

[031-041] rallying and reclassing about itself the elements of a Rome made

[031-042] from the Rome which lies in ruins around the Capitol, while

[031-043] Christianity is seeking all the stages of society amid the

[031-044] rubbish of anterior civilization, and rebuilding with its ruins a

[031-045] new hierarchic universe, the keystone to whose vault is the

[031-046] priest—one first hears a dull echo from that chaos, and then,

[031-047] little by little, one sees, arising from beneath the breath of

[031-048] Christianity, from beneath the hand of the barbarians, from the

[031-049] fragments of the dead Greek and Roman architectures, that

[031-050] mysterious Romanesque architecture, sister of the theocratic

[031-051] masonry of Egypt and of India, inalterable emblem of pure

[031-052] catholicism, unchangeable hieroglyph of the papal unity. All the

[031-053] thought of that day is written, in fact, in this sombre,

[031-054] Romanesque style. One feels everywhere in it authority, unity,

[031-055] the impenetrable, the absolute, Gregory VII.; always the priest,

[031-056] never the man; everywhere caste, never the people.

[031-057] But the Crusades arrive. They are a great popular movement, and

[031-058] every great popular movement, whatever may be its cause and

[031-059] object, always sets free the spirit of liberty from its final

[031-060] precipitate. New things spring into life every day. Here opens

[031-061] the stormy period of the Jacqueries, Pragueries, and Leagues.

[031-062] Authority wavers, unity is divided. Feudalism demands to share

[031-063] with theocracy, while awaiting the inevitable arrival of the

[031-064] people, who will assume the part of the lion: _Quia nominor leo_.

[031-065] Seignory pierces through sacerdotalism; the commonality, through

[031-066] seignory. The face of Europe is changed. Well! the face of

[031-067] architecture is changed also. Like civilization, it has turned a

[031-068] page, and the new spirit of the time finds her ready to write at

[031-069] its dictation. It returns from the crusades with the pointed

[031-070] arch, like the nations with liberty.

[031-071] Then, while Rome is undergoing gradual dismemberment, Romanesque

[031-072] architecture dies. The hieroglyph deserts the cathedral, and

[031-073] betakes itself to blazoning the donjon keep, in order to lend

[031-074] prestige to feudalism. The cathedral itself, that edifice

[031-075] formerly so dogmatic, invaded henceforth by the _bourgeoisie_, by

[031-076] the community, by liberty, escapes the priest and falls into the

[031-077] power of the artist. The artist builds it after his own fashion.

[031-078] Farewell to mystery, myth, law. Fancy and caprice, welcome.

[031-079] Provided the priest has his basilica and his altar, he has

[031-080] nothing to say. The four walls belong to the artist. The

[031-081] architectural book belongs no longer to the priest, to religion,

[031-082] to Rome; it is the property of poetry, of imagination, of the

[031-083] people. Hence the rapid and innumerable transformations of that

[031-084] architecture which owns but three centuries, so striking after

[031-085] the stagnant immobility of the Romanesque architecture, which

[031-086] owns six or seven. Nevertheless, art marches on with giant

[031-087] strides. Popular genius amid originality accomplish the task

[031-088] which the bishops formerly fulfilled. Each race writes its line

[031-089] upon the book, as it passes; it erases the ancient Romanesque

[031-090] hieroglyphs on the frontispieces of cathedrals, and at the most

[031-091] one only sees dogma cropping out here and there, beneath the new

[031-092] symbol which it has deposited. The popular drapery hardly permits

[031-093] the religious skeleton to be suspected. One cannot even form an

[031-094] idea of the liberties which the architects then take, even toward

[031-095] the Church. There are capitals knitted of nuns and monks,

[031-096] shamelessly coupled, as on the hall of chimney pieces in the

[031-097] Palais de Justice, in Paris. There is Noah’s adventure carved to

[031-098] the last detail, as under the great portal of Bourges. There is a

[031-099] bacchanalian monk, with ass’s ears and glass in hand, laughing in

[031-100] the face of a whole community, as on the lavatory of the Abbey of

[031-101] Bocherville. There exists at that epoch, for thought written in

[031-102] stone, a privilege exactly comparable to our present liberty of

[031-103] the press. It is the liberty of architecture.

[031-104] This liberty goes very far. Sometimes a portal, a façade, an

[031-105] entire church, presents a symbolical sense absolutely foreign to

[031-106] worship, or even hostile to the Church. In the thirteenth

[031-107] century, Guillaume de Paris, and Nicholas Flamel, in the

[031-108] fifteenth, wrote such seditious pages. Saint-Jacques de la

[031-109] Boucherie was a whole church of the opposition.

[031-110] Thought was then free only in this manner; hence it never wrote

[031-111] itself out completely except on the books called edifices.

[031-112] Thought, under the form of edifice, could have beheld itself

[031-113] burned in the public square by the hands of the executioner, in

[031-114] its manuscript form, if it had been sufficiently imprudent to

[031-115] risk itself thus; thought, as the door of a church, would have

[031-116] been a spectator of the punishment of thought as a book. Having

[031-117] thus only this resource, masonry, in order to make its way to the

[031-118] light, flung itself upon it from all quarters. Hence the immense

[031-119] quantity of cathedrals which have covered Europe—a number so

[031-120] prodigious that one can hardly believe it even after having

[031-121] verified it. All the material forces, all the intellectual forces

[031-122] of society converged towards the same point: architecture. In

[031-123] this manner, under the pretext of building churches to God, art

[031-124] was developed in its magnificent proportions.

[031-125] Then whoever was born a poet became an architect. Genius,

[031-126] scattered in the masses, repressed in every quarter under

[031-127] feudalism as under a _testudo_ of brazen bucklers, finding no

[031-128] issue except in the direction of architecture,—gushed forth

[031-129] through that art, and its Iliads assumed the form of cathedrals.

[031-130] All other arts obeyed, and placed themselves under the discipline

[031-131] of architecture. They were the workmen of the great work. The

[031-132] architect, the poet, the master, summed up in his person the

[031-133] sculpture which carved his façades, painting which illuminated

[031-134] his windows, music which set his bells to pealing, and breathed

[031-135] into his organs. There was nothing down to poor poetry,—properly

[031-136] speaking, that which persisted in vegetating in

[031-137] manuscripts,—which was not forced, in order to make something of

[031-138] itself, to come and frame itself in the edifice in the shape of a

[031-139] hymn or of prose; the same part, after all, which the tragedies

[031-140] of Æschylus had played in the sacerdotal festivals of Greece;

[031-141] Genesis, in the temple of Solomon.

[031-142] Thus, down to the time of Gutenberg, architecture is the

[031-143] principal writing, the universal writing. In that granite book,

[031-144] begun by the Orient, continued by Greek and Roman antiquity, the

[031-145] Middle Ages wrote the last page. Moreover, this phenomenon of an

[031-146] architecture of the people following an architecture of caste,

[031-147] which we have just been observing in the Middle Ages, is

[031-148] reproduced with every analogous movement in the human

[031-149] intelligence at the other great epochs of history. Thus, in order

[031-150] to enunciate here only summarily, a law which it would require

[031-151] volumes to develop: in the high Orient, the cradle of primitive

[031-152] times, after Hindoo architecture came Phœnician architecture,

[031-153] that opulent mother of Arabian architecture; in antiquity, after

[031-154] Egyptian architecture, of which Etruscan style and cyclopean

[031-155] monuments are but one variety, came Greek architecture (of which

[031-156] the Roman style is only a continuation), surcharged with the

[031-157] Carthaginian dome; in modern times, after Romanesque architecture

[031-158] came Gothic architecture. And by separating there three series

[031-159] into their component parts, we shall find in the three eldest

[031-160] sisters, Hindoo architecture, Egyptian architecture, Romanesque

[031-161] architecture, the same symbol; that is to say, theocracy, caste,

[031-162] unity, dogma, myth, God: and for the three younger sisters,

[031-163] Phœnician architecture, Greek architecture, Gothic architecture,

[031-164] whatever, nevertheless, may be the diversity of form inherent in

[031-165] their nature, the same signification also; that is to say,

[031-166] liberty, the people, man.

[031-167] In the Hindu, Egyptian, or Romanesque architecture, one feels the

[031-168] priest, nothing but the priest, whether he calls himself Brahmin,

[031-169] Magian, or Pope. It is not the same in the architectures of the

[031-170] people. They are richer and less sacred. In the Phœnician, one

[031-171] feels the merchant; in the Greek, the republican; in the Gothic,

[031-172] the citizen.

[031-173] The general characteristics of all theocratic architecture are

[031-174] immutability, horror of progress, the preservation of traditional

[031-175] lines, the consecration of the primitive types, the constant

[031-176] bending of all the forms of men and of nature to the

[031-177] incomprehensible caprices of the symbol. These are dark books,

[031-178] which the initiated alone understand how to decipher. Moreover,

[031-179] every form, every deformity even, has there a sense which renders

[031-180] it inviolable. Do not ask of Hindoo, Egyptian, Romanesque masonry

[031-181] to reform their design, or to improve their statuary. Every

[031-182] attempt at perfecting is an impiety to them. In these

[031-183] architectures it seems as though the rigidity of the dogma had

[031-184] spread over the stone like a sort of second petrifaction. The

[031-185] general characteristics of popular masonry, on the contrary, are

[031-186] progress, originality, opulence, perpetual movement. They are

[031-187] already sufficiently detached from religion to think of their

[031-188] beauty, to take care of it, to correct without relaxation their

[031-189] parure of statues or arabesques. They are of the age. They have

[031-190] something human, which they mingle incessantly with the divine

[031-191] symbol under which they still produce. Hence, edifices

[031-192] comprehensible to every soul, to every intelligence, to every

[031-193] imagination, symbolical still, but as easy to understand as

[031-194] nature. Between theocratic architecture and this there is the

[031-195] difference that lies between a sacred language and a vulgar

[031-196] language, between hieroglyphics and art, between Solomon and

[031-197] Phidias.

[031-198] If the reader will sum up what we have hitherto briefly, very

[031-199] briefly, indicated, neglecting a thousand proofs and also a

[031-200] thousand objections of detail, he will be led to this: that

[031-201] architecture was, down to the fifteenth century, the chief

[031-202] register of humanity; that in that interval not a thought which

[031-203] is in any degree complicated made its appearance in the world,

[031-204] which has not been worked into an edifice; that every popular

[031-205] idea, and every religious law, has had its monumental records;

[031-206] that the human race has, in short, had no important thought which

[031-207] it has not written in stone. And why? Because every thought,

[031-208] either philosophical or religious, is interested in perpetuating

[031-209] itself; because the idea which has moved one generation wishes to

[031-210] move others also, and leave a trace. Now, what a precarious

[031-211] immortality is that of the manuscript! How much more solid,

[031-212] durable, unyielding, is a book of stone! In order to destroy the

[031-213] written word, a torch and a Turk are sufficient. To demolish the

[031-214] constructed word, a social revolution, a terrestrial revolution

[031-215] are required. The barbarians passed over the Coliseum; the

[031-216] deluge, perhaps, passed over the Pyramids.
