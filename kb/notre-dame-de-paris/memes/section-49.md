---[049-001] “I assure you,” said Charmolue, who was gazing at him without

[049-002] comprehending him, “that I will not touch it. But release my arm,

[049-003] master, for pity’s sake! You have a hand like a pair of pincers.”

[049-004] The archdeacon did not hear him. “Oh, madman!” he went on,

[049-005] without removing his gaze from the window. “And even couldst thou

[049-006] have broken through that formidable web, with thy gnat’s wings,

[049-007] thou believest that thou couldst have reached the light? Alas!

[049-008] that pane of glass which is further on, that transparent

[049-009] obstacle, that wall of crystal, harder than brass, which

[049-010] separates all philosophies from the truth, how wouldst thou have

[049-011] overcome it? Oh, vanity of science! how many wise men come flying

[049-012] from afar, to dash their heads against thee! How many systems

[049-013] vainly fling themselves buzzing against that eternal pane!”

[049-014] He became silent. These last ideas, which had gradually led him

[049-015] back from himself to science, appeared to have calmed him.

[049-016] Jacques Charmolue recalled him wholly to a sense of reality by

[049-017] addressing to him this question: “Come, now, master, when will

[049-018] you come to aid me in making gold? I am impatient to succeed.”

[049-019] The archdeacon shook his head, with a bitter smile. “Master

[049-020] Jacques read Michel Psellus’ ‘_Dialogus de Energia et Operatione

[049-021] Dæmonum_.’ What we are doing is not wholly innocent.”

[049-022] “Speak lower, master! I have my suspicions of it,” said Jacques

[049-023] Charmolue. “But one must practise a bit of hermetic science when

[049-024] one is only procurator of the king in the ecclesiastical court,

[049-025] at thirty crowns tournois a year. Only speak low.”

[049-026] At that moment the sound of jaws in the act of mastication, which

[049-027] proceeded from beneath the furnace, struck Charmolue’s uneasy

[049-028] ear.

[049-029] “What’s that?” he inquired.

[049-030] It was the scholar, who, ill at ease, and greatly bored in his

[049-031] hiding-place, had succeeded in discovering there a stale crust

[049-032] and a triangle of mouldy cheese, and had set to devouring the

[049-033] whole without ceremony, by way of consolation and breakfast. As

[049-034] he was very hungry, he made a great deal of noise, and he

[049-035] accented each mouthful strongly, which startled and alarmed the

[049-036] procurator.

[049-037] “’Tis a cat of mine,” said the archdeacon, quickly, “who is

[049-038] regaling herself under there with a mouse.”

[049-039] This explanation satisfied Charmolue.

[049-040] “In fact, master,” he replied, with a respectful smile, “all

[049-041] great philosophers have their familiar animal. You know what

[049-042] Servius saith: ‘_Nullus enim locus sine genio est_,—for there is

[049-043] no place that hath not its spirit.’”

[049-044] But Dom Claude, who stood in terror of some new freak on the part

[049-045] of Jehan, reminded his worthy disciple that they had some figures

[049-046] on the façade to study together, and the two quitted the cell, to

[049-047] the accompaniment of a great “ouf!” from the scholar, who began

[049-048] to seriously fear that his knee would acquire the imprint of his

[049-049] chin.

[049-050] CHAPTER VI. THE EFFECT WHICH SEVEN OATHS IN THE OPEN AIR CAN

[049-051] PRODUCE.

[049-052] “_Te Deum laudamus_!” exclaimed Master Jehan, creeping out from

[049-053] his hole, “the screech-owls have departed. Och! och! Hax! pax!

[049-054] max! fleas! mad dogs! the devil! I have had enough of their

[049-055] conversation! My head is humming like a bell tower. And mouldy

[049-056] cheese to boot! Come on! Let us descend, take the big brother’s

[049-057] purse and convert all these coins into bottles!”

[049-058] He cast a glance of tenderness and admiration into the interior

[049-059] of the precious pouch, readjusted his toilet, rubbed up his

[049-060] boots, dusted his poor half sleeves, all gray with ashes,

[049-061] whistled an air, indulged in a sportive pirouette, looked about

[049-062] to see whether there were not something more in the cell to take,

[049-063] gathered up here and there on the furnace some amulet in glass

[049-064] which might serve to bestow, in the guise of a trinket, on

[049-065] Isabeau la Thierrye, finally pushed open the door which his

[049-066] brother had left unfastened, as a last indulgence, and which he,

[049-067] in his turn, left open as a last piece of malice, and descended

[049-068] the circular staircase, skipping like a bird.

[049-069] In the midst of the gloom of the spiral staircase, he elbowed

[049-070] something which drew aside with a growl; he took it for granted

[049-071] that it was Quasimodo, and it struck him as so droll that he

[049-072] descended the remainder of the staircase holding his sides with

[049-073] laughter. On emerging upon the Place, he laughed yet more

[049-074] heartily.

[049-075] He stamped his foot when he found himself on the ground once

[049-076] again. “Oh!” said he, “good and honorable pavement of Paris,

[049-077] cursed staircase, fit to put the angels of Jacob’s ladder out of

[049-078] breath! What was I thinking of to thrust myself into that stone

[049-079] gimlet which pierces the sky; all for the sake of eating bearded

[049-080] cheese, and looking at the bell-towers of Paris through a hole in

[049-081] the wall!”

[049-082] He advanced a few paces, and caught sight of the two screech

[049-083] owls, that is to say, Dom Claude and Master Jacques Charmolue,

[049-084] absorbed in contemplation before a carving on the façade. He

[049-085] approached them on tiptoe, and heard the archdeacon say in a low

[049-086] tone to Charmolue: “’Twas Guillaume de Paris who caused a Job to

[049-087] be carved upon this stone of the hue of lapis-lazuli, gilded on

[049-088] the edges. Job represents the philosopher’s stone, which must

[049-089] also be tried and martyrized in order to become perfect, as saith

[049-090] Raymond Lulle: _Sub conservatione formæ specificæ salva anima_.”

[049-091] “That makes no difference to me,” said Jehan, “’tis I who have

[049-092] the purse.”

[049-093] At that moment he heard a powerful and sonorous voice articulate

[049-094] behind him a formidable series of oaths. “_Sang Dieu!

[049-095] Ventre-Dieu! Bédieu! Corps de Dieu! Nombril de Belzébuth! Nom

[049-096] d’un pape! Corne et tonnerre_.”

[049-097] “Upon my soul!” exclaimed Jehan, “that can only be my friend,

[049-098] Captain Phœbus!”

[049-099] This name of Phœbus reached the ears of the archdeacon at the

[049-100] moment when he was explaining to the king’s procurator the dragon

[049-101] which is hiding its tail in a bath, from which issue smoke and

[049-102] the head of a king. Dom Claude started, interrupted himself and,

[049-103] to the great amazement of Charmolue, turned round and beheld his

[049-104] brother Jehan accosting a tall officer at the door of the

[049-105] Gondelaurier mansion.

[049-106] It was, in fact, Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers. He was backed up

[049-107] against a corner of the house of his betrothed and swearing like

[049-108] a heathen.

[049-109] “By my faith! Captain Phœbus,” said Jehan, taking him by the

[049-110] hand, “you are cursing with admirable vigor.”

[049-111] “Horns and thunder!” replied the captain.

[049-112] “Horns and thunder yourself!” replied the student. “Come now,

[049-113] fair captain, whence comes this overflow of fine words?”

[049-114] “Pardon me, good comrade Jehan,” exclaimed Phœbus, shaking his

[049-115] hand, “a horse going at a gallop cannot halt short. Now, I was

[049-116] swearing at a hard gallop. I have just been with those prudes,

[049-117] and when I come forth, I always find my throat full of curses, I

[049-118] must spit them out or strangle, _ventre et tonnerre!_”

[049-119] “Will you come and drink?” asked the scholar.

[049-120] This proposition calmed the captain.

[049-121] “I’m willing, but I have no money.”

[049-122] “But I have!”

[049-123] “Bah! let’s see it!”

[049-124] Jehan spread out the purse before the captain’s eyes, with

[049-125] dignity and simplicity. Meanwhile, the archdeacon, who had

[049-126] abandoned the dumbfounded Charmolue where he stood, had

[049-127] approached them and halted a few paces distant, watching them

[049-128] without their noticing him, so deeply were they absorbed in

[049-129] contemplation of the purse.

[049-130] Phœbus exclaimed: “A purse in your pocket, Jehan! ’tis the moon

[049-131] in a bucket of water, one sees it there but ’tis not there. There

[049-132] is nothing but its shadow. Pardieu! let us wager that these are

[049-133] pebbles!”

[049-134] Jehan replied coldly: “Here are the pebbles wherewith I pave my

[049-135] fob!”

[049-136] And without adding another word, he emptied the purse on a

[049-137] neighboring post, with the air of a Roman saving his country.

[049-138] “True God!” muttered Phœbus, “targes, big-blanks, little blanks,

[049-139] mailles,[38] every two worth one of Tournay, farthings of Paris,

[049-140] real eagle liards! ’Tis dazzling!”

[049-141] Jehan remained dignified and immovable. Several liards had rolled

[049-142] into the mud; the captain in his enthusiasm stooped to pick them

[049-143] up. Jehan restrained him.

[049-144] “Fye, Captain Phœbus de Châteaupers!”

[049-145] Phœbus counted the coins, and turning towards Jehan with

[049-146] solemnity, “Do you know, Jehan, that there are three and twenty

[049-147] sous parisis! whom have you plundered to-night, in the Street

[049-148] Cut-Weazand?”

[049-149] Jehan flung back his blonde and curly head, and said,

[049-150] half-closing his eyes disdainfully,—

[049-151] “We have a brother who is an archdeacon and a fool.”

[049-152] “_Corne de Dieu!_” exclaimed Phœbus, “the worthy man!”

[049-153] “Let us go and drink,” said Jehan.

[049-154] “Where shall we go?” said Phœbus; “‘To Eve’s Apple.’”

[049-155] “No, captain, to ‘Ancient Science.’ An old woman sawing a basket

[049-156] handle;[39] ’tis a rebus, and I like that.”

[049-157] “A plague on rebuses, Jehan! the wine is better at ‘Eve’s Apple’;

[049-158] and then, beside the door there is a vine in the sun which cheers

[049-159] me while I am drinking.”

[049-160] “Well! here goes for Eve and her apple,” said the student, and

[049-161] taking Phœbus’s arm. “By the way, my dear captain, you just

[049-162] mentioned the Rue Coupe-Gueule[40] That is a very bad form of

[049-163] speech; people are no longer so barbarous. They say,

[049-164] Coupe-Gorge[41].”

[049-165] The two friends set out towards “Eve’s Apple.” It is unnecessary

[049-166] to mention that they had first gathered up the money, and that

[049-167] the archdeacon followed them.

[049-168] The archdeacon followed them, gloomy and haggard. Was this the

[049-169] Phœbus whose accursed name had been mingled with all his thoughts

[049-170] ever since his interview with Gringoire? He did not know it, but

[049-171] it was at least a Phœbus, and that magic name sufficed to make

[049-172] the archdeacon follow the two heedless comrades with the stealthy

[049-173] tread of a wolf, listening to their words and observing their

[049-174] slightest gestures with anxious attention. Moreover, nothing was

[049-175] easier than to hear everything they said, as they talked loudly,

[049-176] not in the least concerned that the passers-by were taken into

[049-177] their confidence. They talked of duels, wenches, wine pots, and

[049-178] folly.

[049-179] At the turning of a street, the sound of a tambourine reached

[049-180] them from a neighboring square. Dom Claude heard the officer say

[049-181] to the scholar,—

[049-182] “Thunder! Let us hasten our steps!”

[049-183] “Why, Phœbus?”

[049-184] “I’m afraid lest the Bohemian should see me.”

[049-185] “What Bohemian?”

[049-186] “The little girl with the goat.”

[049-187] “La Smeralda?”

[049-188] “That’s it, Jehan. I always forget her devil of a name. Let us

[049-189] make haste, she will recognize me. I don’t want to have that girl

[049-190] accost me in the street.”

[049-191] “Do you know her, Phœbus?”

[049-192] Here the archdeacon saw Phœbus sneer, bend down to Jehan’s ear,

[049-193] and say a few words to him in a low voice; then Phœbus burst into

[049-194] a laugh, and shook his head with a triumphant air.

[049-195] “Truly?” said Jehan.

[049-196] “Upon my soul!” said Phœbus.

[049-197] “This evening?”

[049-198] “This evening.”

[049-199] “Are you sure that she will come?”

[049-200] “Are you a fool, Jehan? Does one doubt such things?”

[049-201] “Captain Phœbus, you are a happy gendarme!”

[049-202] The archdeacon heard the whole of this conversation. His teeth

[049-203] chattered; a visible shiver ran through his whole body. He halted

[049-204] for a moment, leaned against a post like a drunken man, then

[049-205] followed the two merry knaves.

[049-206] At the moment when he overtook them once more, they had changed

[049-207] their conversation. He heard them singing at the top of their

[049-208] lungs the ancient refrain,—

[049-209] Les enfants des Petits-Carreaux

[049-210] Se font pendre comme des veaux[42].

[049-211] CHAPTER VII. THE MYSTERIOUS MONK.

[049-212] The illustrious wine shop of “Eve’s Apple” was situated in the

[049-213] University, at the corner of the Rue de la Rondelle and the Rue

[049-214] de la Bâtonnier. It was a very spacious and very low hall on the

[049-215] ground floor, with a vaulted ceiling whose central spring rested

[049-216] upon a huge pillar of wood painted yellow; tables everywhere,

[049-217] shining pewter jugs hanging on the walls, always a large number

[049-218] of drinkers, a plenty of wenches, a window on the street, a vine

[049-219] at the door, and over the door a flaring piece of sheet-iron,

[049-220] painted with an apple and a woman, rusted by the rain and turning

[049-221] with the wind on an iron pin. This species of weather-vane which

[049-222] looked upon the pavement was the signboard.
