# Bastille

The Bastille is the grim fortress-prison of [[paris|medieval Paris]] — a massive stone presence at the [[porte-saint-antoine|Porte Saint-Antoine]] that embodies the oppressive machinery of royal [[law-and-justice|justice]]. Throughout *[[notre-dame-cathedral|Notre-Dame de Paris]]*, it appears as the destination of condemned prisoners, the seat of coercive state power, and a dark counterpoint to the [[Notre-Dame Cathedral|cathedral]]'s offer of [[sanctuary]].

> From Hugo's panoramic view of Paris, the Bastille is a "formidable edifice" — "those sorts of black crenellated masses which seem to foreigners like monstrous houses" (022-118). It stands at the eastern gate, the architectural embodiment of royal power.

## Overview

Under the reign of [[Louis XI]], the Bastille served as both a military fortress and a state prison. It represented the absolute authority of the crown — a place where prisoners could disappear without [[law-and-justice|trial]], where torture was routine, and from which few ever returned. Hugo presents it as the dark twin of the [[Palais de Justice]]: where the Palais judged, the Bastille consumed.

## Role in the Novel

### The Seat of Royal Power

[[louis-xi|Louis XI]] conducts state business from the Bastille, receiving his ministers and intelligence agents within its walls. From here he monitors the unrest in [[paris|Paris]], calculates political advantage, and ultimately decides the [[fatalite|fate]] of [[Esmeralda]] and the vagabonds:

> When news arrives that the truands are besieging Notre-Dame, the king is at the Bastille. He erupts in fury: "They lay siege to our Lady, my good mistress in her cathedral!" (080-042). From the Bastille, he orders Tristan l'Hermite to crush the assault (080-054, 080-058).

### The Destination of Prisoners

- **[[Quasimodo]]**: After his arrest for the attempted abduction of [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]] and his [[law-and-justice|trial]] at [[grand-chatelet|the Châtelet]], [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]] is taken to [[quasimodos-pillory|the pillory]] — but the threat of the Bastille looms over all interactions with royal [[law-and-justice|justice]].
- **[[Esmeralda]]**: Condemned by the ecclesiastical court, she faces the gallows at the [[Place de Grève]], but the Bastille overshadows all judicial proceedings in [[paris|Paris]].
- **The Vagabonds**: The [[Truands of Paris]] fear the Bastille as the ultimate sanction — the fortress where the poor and rebellious vanish without trace.

### Louis XI's Court

The Bastille chapters offer Hugo's most detailed portrait of royal power. [[louis-xi|Louis XI]] appears as a calculating, cynical monarch — more concerned with expense accounts and political advantage than with [[law-and-justice|justice]]. Hugo writes that the king "returned to his seat" after each eruption of fury, his policy decisions as cold as the fortress walls (080-056). The king's own words — "I am a good, easy king, but I don't like spending money" — encapsulate the novel's critique of a system where human life is valued less than the cost of a rope.

## Historical Significance

The Bastille in 1482 was a relatively recent construction, built as part of the fortifications of [[paris|Paris]] in the 14th century and converted to a state prison. Hugo presents it as the architectural expression of centralized royal authority — the physical counterpart to [[louis-xi|Louis XI]]'s political consolidation of power. It stands in stark contrast to [[Notre-Dame Cathedral]], which represents the older, more organic authority of the Church and which offers — via the right of [[sanctuary]] — a check on royal power.

## Hugo's Commentary

Hugo uses the Bastille to dramatize the tension between two competing systems of authority in [[paris|medieval Paris]]: the sacred (Notre-Dame, [[sanctuary]]) and the secular (the Bastille, the crown). When [[quasimodo|Quasimodo]] cries "Sanctuary!" and carries [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]] into [[notre-dame-cathedral|the cathedral]], he is invoking an older [[law-and-justice|law]] against the rising tide of absolutism that the Bastille represents. The novel's tragic conclusion — [[esmeralda|Esmeralda]] is ultimately dragged from [[sanctuary]] and hanged — marks the triumph of royal power over ecclesiastical immunity.

> The Bastille chapters end with Louis XI at prayer before his amulets — "gazing devoutly at one of the leaden amulets which encircled his hat" (080-090) — a portrait of royal piety that is indistinguishable from superstition, and of power that kneels only to itself.

## Notable Passages

> "Here, Tristan! There are here with us in the Bastille the fifty lances of the Vicomte de Gif" (080-058) — the king musters his forces from the fortress to crush the truands' assault.

> The king "returned to his seat, and said with cold and concentrated wrath" his orders (080-056) — the Bastille as the seat of a justice without mercy.
