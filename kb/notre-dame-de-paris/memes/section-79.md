---[079-001] “Oh! oh!” he murmured at length, “this is serious indeed.”

[079-002] “Is it not?” said the king, uneasily.

[079-003] “_Pulsus creber, anhelans, crepitans, irregularis_,” continued

[079-004] the leech.

[079-005] “_Pasque-Dieu!_”

[079-006] “This may carry off its man in less than three days.”

[079-007] “Our Lady!” exclaimed the king. “And the remedy, gossip?”

[079-008] “I am meditating upon that, sire.”

[079-009] He made Louis XI. put out his tongue, shook his head, made a

[079-010] grimace, and in the very midst of these affectations,—

[079-011] “Pardieu, sire,” he suddenly said, “I must tell you that there is

[079-012] a receivership of the royal prerogatives vacant, and that I have

[079-013] a nephew.”

[079-014] “I give the receivership to your nephew, Gossip Jacques,” replied

[079-015] the king; “but draw this fire from my breast.”

[079-016] “Since your majesty is so clement,” replied the leech, “you will

[079-017] not refuse to aid me a little in building my house, Rue

[079-018] Saint-André-des-Arcs.”

[079-019] “Heugh!” said the king.

[079-020] “I am at the end of my finances,” pursued the doctor; “and it

[079-021] would really be a pity that the house should not have a roof; not

[079-022] on account of the house, which is simple and thoroughly

[079-023] _bourgeois_, but because of the paintings of Jehan Fourbault,

[079-024] which adorn its wainscoating. There is a Diana flying in the air,

[079-025] but so excellent, so tender, so delicate, of so ingenuous an

[079-026] action, her hair so well coiffed and adorned with a crescent, her

[079-027] flesh so white, that she leads into temptation those who regard

[079-028] her too curiously. There is also a Ceres. She is another very

[079-029] fair divinity. She is seated on sheaves of wheat and crowned with

[079-030] a gallant garland of wheat ears interlaced with salsify and other

[079-031] flowers. Never were seen more amorous eyes, more rounded limbs, a

[079-032] nobler air, or a more gracefully flowing skirt. She is one of the

[079-033] most innocent and most perfect beauties whom the brush has ever

[079-034] produced.”

[079-035] “Executioner!” grumbled Louis XI., “what are you driving at?”

[079-036] “I must have a roof for these paintings, sire, and, although ’tis

[079-037] but a small matter, I have no more money.”

[079-038] “How much doth your roof cost?”

[079-039] “Why a roof of copper, embellished and gilt, two thousand livres

[079-040] at the most.”

[079-041] “Ah, assassin!” cried the king, “He never draws out one of my

[079-042] teeth which is not a diamond.”

[079-043] “Am I to have my roof?” said Coictier.

[079-044] “Yes; and go to the devil, but cure me.”

[079-045] Jacques Coictier bowed low and said,—

[079-046] “Sire, it is a repellent which will save you. We will apply to

[079-047] your loins the great defensive composed of cerate, Armenian bole,

[079-048] white of egg, oil, and vinegar. You will continue your ptisan and

[079-049] we will answer for your majesty.”

[079-050] A burning candle does not attract one gnat alone. Master Olivier,

[079-051] perceiving the king to be in a liberal mood, and judging the

[079-052] moment to be propitious, approached in his turn.

[079-053] “Sire—”

[079-054] “What is it now?” said Louis XI. “Sire, your majesty knoweth that

[079-055] Simon Radin is dead?”

[079-056] “Well?”

[079-057] “He was councillor to the king in the matter of the courts of the

[079-058] treasury.”

[079-059] “Well?”

[079-060] “Sire, his place is vacant.”

[079-061] As he spoke thus, Master Olivier’s haughty face quitted its

[079-062] arrogant expression for a lowly one. It is the only change which

[079-063] ever takes place in a courtier’s visage. The king looked him well

[079-064] in the face and said in a dry tone,—“I understand.”

[079-065] He resumed,—

[079-066] “Master Olivier, the Marshal de Boucicaut was wont to say,

[079-067] ‘There’s no master save the king, there are no fishes save in the

[079-068] sea.’ I see that you agree with Monsieur de Boucicaut. Now listen

[079-069] to this; we have a good memory. In ’68 we made you valet of our

[079-070] chamber: in ’69, guardian of the fortress of the bridge of

[079-071] Saint-Cloud, at a hundred livres of Tournay in wages (you wanted

[079-072] them of Paris). In November, ’73, by letters given to Gergeole,

[079-073] we instituted you keeper of the Wood of Vincennes, in the place

[079-074] of Gilbert Acle, equerry; in ’75, gruyer[67] of the forest of

[079-075] Rouvray-lez-Saint-Cloud, in the place of Jacques le Maire; in

[079-076] ’78, we graciously settled on you, by letters patent sealed

[079-077] doubly with green wax, an income of ten livres parisis, for you

[079-078] and your wife, on the Place of the Merchants, situated at the

[079-079] School Saint-Germain; in ’79, we made you gruyer of the forest of

[079-080] Senart, in place of that poor Jehan Daiz; then captain of the

[079-081] Château of Loches; then governor of Saint-Quentin; then captain

[079-082] of the bridge of Meulan, of which you cause yourself to be called

[079-083] comte. Out of the five sols fine paid by every barber who shaves

[079-084] on a festival day, there are three sols for you and we have the

[079-085] rest. We have been good enough to change your name of Le Mauvais

[079-086] (The Evil), which resembled your face too closely. In ’76, we

[079-087] granted you, to the great displeasure of our nobility, armorial

[079-088] bearings of a thousand colors, which give you the breast of a

[079-089] peacock. _Pasque-Dieu!_ Are not you surfeited? Is not the draught

[079-090] of fishes sufficiently fine and miraculous? Are you not afraid

[079-091] that one salmon more will make your boat sink? Pride will be your

[079-092] ruin, gossip. Ruin and disgrace always press hard on the heels of

[079-093] pride. Consider this and hold your tongue.”

[079-094] These words, uttered with severity, made Master Olivier’s face

[079-095] revert to its insolence.

[079-096] “Good!” he muttered, almost aloud, “’tis easy to see that the

[079-097] king is ill to-day; he giveth all to the leech.”

[079-098] Louis XI. far from being irritated by this petulant insult,

[079-099] resumed with some gentleness, “Stay, I was forgetting that I made

[079-100] you my ambassador to Madame Marie, at Ghent. Yes, gentlemen,”

[079-101] added the king turning to the Flemings, “this man hath been an

[079-102] ambassador. There, my gossip,” he pursued, addressing Master

[079-103] Olivier, “let us not get angry; we are old friends. ’Tis very

[079-104] late. We have terminated our labors. Shave me.”

[079-105] Our readers have not, without doubt, waited until the present

[079-106] moment to recognize in Master Olivier that terrible Figaro whom

[079-107] Providence, the great maker of dramas, mingled so artistically in

[079-108] the long and bloody comedy of the reign of Louis XI. We will not

[079-109] here undertake to develop that singular figure. This barber of

[079-110] the king had three names. At court he was politely called Olivier

[079-111] le Daim (the Deer); among the people Olivier the Devil. His real

[079-112] name was Olivier le Mauvais.

[079-113] Accordingly, Olivier le Mauvais remained motionless, sulking at

[079-114] the king, and glancing askance at Jacques Coictier.

[079-115] “Yes, yes, the physician!” he said between his teeth.

[079-116] “Ah, yes, the physician!” retorted Louis XI., with singular good

[079-117] humor; “the physician has more credit than you. ’Tis very simple;

[079-118] he has taken hold upon us by the whole body, and you hold us only

[079-119] by the chin. Come, my poor barber, all will come right. What

[079-120] would you say and what would become of your office if I were a

[079-121] king like Chilperic, whose gesture consisted in holding his beard

[079-122] in one hand? Come, gossip mine, fulfil your office, shave me. Go

[079-123] get what you need therefor.”

[079-124] Olivier perceiving that the king had made up his mind to laugh,

[079-125] and that there was no way of even annoying him, went off

[079-126] grumbling to execute his orders.

[079-127] The king rose, approached the window, and suddenly opening it

[079-128] with extraordinary agitation,—

[079-129] “Oh! yes!” he exclaimed, clapping his hands, “yonder is a redness

[079-130] in the sky over the City. ’Tis the bailiff burning. It can be

[079-131] nothing else but that. Ah! my good people! here you are aiding me

[079-132] at last in tearing down the rights of lordship!”

[079-133] Then turning towards the Flemings: “Come, look at this,

[079-134] gentlemen. Is it not a fire which gloweth yonder?”

[079-135] The two men of Ghent drew near.

[079-136] “A great fire,” said Guillaume Rym.

[079-137] “Oh!” exclaimed Coppenole, whose eyes suddenly flashed, “that

[079-138] reminds me of the burning of the house of the Seigneur

[079-139] d’Hymbercourt. There must be a goodly revolt yonder.”

[079-140] “You think so, Master Coppenole?” And Louis XI.’s glance was

[079-141] almost as joyous as that of the hosier. “Will it not be difficult

[079-142] to resist?”

[079-143] “Cross of God! Sire! Your majesty will damage many companies of

[079-144] men of war thereon.”

[079-145] “Ah! I! ’tis different,” returned the king. “If I willed.”

[079-146] The hosier replied hardily,—

[079-147] “If this revolt be what I suppose, sire, you might will in vain.”

[079-148] “Gossip,” said Louis XI., “with the two companies of my

[079-149] unattached troops and one discharge of a serpentine, short work

[079-150] is made of a populace of louts.”

[079-151] The hosier, in spite of the signs made to him by Guillaume Rym,

[079-152] appeared determined to hold his own against the king.

[079-153] “Sire, the Swiss were also louts. Monsieur the Duke of Burgundy

[079-154] was a great gentleman, and he turned up his nose at that rabble

[079-155] rout. At the battle of Grandson, sire, he cried: ‘Men of the

[079-156] cannon! Fire on the villains!’ and he swore by Saint-George. But

[079-157] Advoyer Scharnachtal hurled himself on the handsome duke with his

[079-158] battle-club and his people, and when the glittering Burgundian

[079-159] army came in contact with these peasants in bull hides, it flew

[079-160] in pieces like a pane of glass at the blow of a pebble. Many

[079-161] lords were then slain by low-born knaves; and Monsieur de

[079-162] Château-Guyon, the greatest seigneur in Burgundy, was found dead,

[079-163] with his gray horse, in a little marsh meadow.”

[079-164] “Friend,” returned the king, “you are speaking of a battle. The

[079-165] question here is of a mutiny. And I will gain the upper hand of

[079-166] it as soon as it shall please me to frown.”

[079-167] The other replied indifferently,—

[079-168] “That may be, sire; in that case, ’tis because the people’s hour

[079-169] hath not yet come.”

[079-170] Guillaume Rym considered it incumbent on him to intervene,—

[079-171] “Master Coppenole, you are speaking to a puissant king.”

[079-172] “I know it,” replied the hosier, gravely.

[079-173] “Let him speak, Monsieur Rym, my friend,” said the king; “I love

[079-174] this frankness of speech. My father, Charles the Seventh, was

[079-175] accustomed to say that the truth was ailing; I thought her dead,

[079-176] and that she had found no confessor. Master Coppenole undeceiveth

[079-177] me.”

[079-178] Then, laying his hand familiarly on Coppenole’s shoulder,—

[079-179] “You were saying, Master Jacques?”

[079-180] “I say, sire, that you may possibly be in the right, that the

[079-181] hour of the people may not yet have come with you.”

[079-182] Louis XI. gazed at him with his penetrating eye,—

[079-183] “And when will that hour come, master?”

[079-184] “You will hear it strike.”

[079-185] “On what clock, if you please?”

[079-186] Coppenole, with his tranquil and rustic countenance, made the

[079-187] king approach the window.

[079-188] “Listen, sire! There is here a donjon keep, a belfry, cannons,

[079-189] _bourgeois_, soldiers; when the belfry shall hum, when the

[079-190] cannons shall roar, when the donjon shall fall in ruins amid

[079-191] great noise, when _bourgeois_ and soldiers shall howl and slay

[079-192] each other, the hour will strike.”

[079-193] Louis’s face grew sombre and dreamy. He remained silent for a

[079-194] moment, then he gently patted with his hand the thick wall of the

[079-195] donjon, as one strokes the haunches of a steed.

[079-196] “Oh! no!” said he. “You will not crumble so easily, will you, my

[079-197] good Bastille?”

[079-198] And turning with an abrupt gesture towards the sturdy Fleming,—

[079-199] “Have you never seen a revolt, Master Jacques?”

[079-200] “I have made them,” said the hosier.

[079-201] “How do you set to work to make a revolt?” said the king.

[079-202] “Ah!” replied Coppenole, “’tis not very difficult. There are a

[079-203] hundred ways. In the first place, there must be discontent in the

[079-204] city. The thing is not uncommon. And then, the character of the

[079-205] inhabitants. Those of Ghent are easy to stir into revolt. They

[079-206] always love the prince’s son; the prince, never. Well! One

[079-207] morning, I will suppose, some one enters my shop, and says to me:

[079-208] ‘Father Coppenole, there is this and there is that, the

[079-209] Demoiselle of Flanders wishes to save her ministers, the grand

[079-210] bailiff is doubling the impost on shagreen, or something

[079-211] else,’—what you will. I leave my work as it stands, I come out of

[079-212] my hosier’s stall, and I shout: ‘To the sack?’ There is always

[079-213] some smashed cask at hand. I mount it, and I say aloud, in the

[079-214] first words that occur to me, what I have on my heart; and when

[079-215] one is of the people, sire, one always has something on the

[079-216] heart. Then people troop up, they shout, they ring the alarm

[079-217] bell, they arm the louts with what they take from the soldiers,

[079-218] the market people join in, and they set out. And it will always

[079-219] be thus, so long as there are lords in the seignories,

[079-220] _bourgeois_ in the bourgs, and peasants in the country.”
