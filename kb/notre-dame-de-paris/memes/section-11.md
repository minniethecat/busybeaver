---[011-001] “In truth,” said Gringoire to himself, “she is a salamander, she

[011-002] is a nymph, she is a goddess, she is a bacchante of the Menelean

[011-003] Mount!”

[011-004] At that moment, one of the salamander’s braids of hair became

[011-005] unfastened, and a piece of yellow copper which was attached to

[011-006] it, rolled to the ground.

[011-007] “Hé, no!” said he, “she is a gypsy!”

[011-008] All illusions had disappeared.

[011-009] She began her dance once more; she took from the ground two

[011-010] swords, whose points she rested against her brow, and which she

[011-011] made to turn in one direction, while she turned in the other; it

[011-012] was a purely gypsy effect. But, disenchanted though Gringoire

[011-013] was, the whole effect of this picture was not without its charm

[011-014] and its magic; the bonfire illuminated, with a red flaring light,

[011-015] which trembled, all alive, over the circle of faces in the crowd,

[011-016] on the brow of the young girl, and at the background of the Place

[011-017] cast a pallid reflection, on one side upon the ancient, black,

[011-018] and wrinkled façade of the House of Pillars, on the other, upon

[011-019] the old stone gibbet.

[011-020] Among the thousands of visages which that light tinged with

[011-021] scarlet, there was one which seemed, even more than all the

[011-022] others, absorbed in contemplation of the dancer. It was the face

[011-023] of a man, austere, calm, and sombre. This man, whose costume was

[011-024] concealed by the crowd which surrounded him, did not appear to be

[011-025] more than five and thirty years of age; nevertheless, he was

[011-026] bald; he had merely a few tufts of thin, gray hair on his

[011-027] temples; his broad, high forehead had begun to be furrowed with

[011-028] wrinkles, but his deep-set eyes sparkled with extraordinary

[011-029] youthfulness, an ardent life, a profound passion. He kept them

[011-030] fixed incessantly on the gypsy, and, while the giddy young girl

[011-031] of sixteen danced and whirled, for the pleasure of all, his

[011-032] revery seemed to become more and more sombre. From time to time,

[011-033] a smile and a sigh met upon his lips, but the smile was more

[011-034] melancholy than the sigh.

[011-035] The young girl, stopped at length, breathless, and the people

[011-036] applauded her lovingly.

[011-037] “Djali!” said the gypsy.

[011-038] Then Gringoire saw come up to her, a pretty little white goat,

[011-039] alert, wide-awake, glossy, with gilded horns, gilded hoofs, and

[011-040] gilded collar, which he had not hitherto perceived, and which had

[011-041] remained lying curled up on one corner of the carpet watching his

[011-042] mistress dance.

[011-043] “Djali!” said the dancer, “it is your turn.”

[011-044] And, seating herself, she gracefully presented her tambourine to

[011-045] the goat.

[011-046] “Djali,” she continued, “what month is this?”

[011-047] The goat lifted its fore foot, and struck one blow upon the

[011-048] tambourine. It was the first month in the year, in fact.

[011-049] “Djali,” pursued the young girl, turning her tambourine round,

[011-050] “what day of the month is this?”

[011-051] Djali raised his little gilt hoof, and struck six blows on the

[011-052] tambourine.

[011-053] “Djali,” pursued the Egyptian, with still another movement of the

[011-054] tambourine, “what hour of the day is it?”

[011-055] Djali struck seven blows. At that moment, the clock of the Pillar

[011-056] House rang out seven.

[011-057] The people were amazed.

[011-058] “There’s sorcery at the bottom of it,” said a sinister voice in

[011-059] the crowd. It was that of the bald man, who never removed his

[011-060] eyes from the gypsy.

[011-061] She shuddered and turned round; but applause broke forth and

[011-062] drowned the morose exclamation.

[011-063] It even effaced it so completely from her mind, that she

[011-064] continued to question her goat.

[011-065] “Djali, what does Master Guichard Grand-Remy, captain of the

[011-066] pistoliers of the town do, at the procession of Candlemas?”

[011-067] Djali reared himself on his hind legs, and began to bleat,

[011-068] marching along with so much dainty gravity, that the entire

[011-069] circle of spectators burst into a laugh at this parody of the

[011-070] interested devoutness of the captain of pistoliers.

[011-071] “Djali,” resumed the young girl, emboldened by her growing

[011-072] success, “how preaches Master Jacques Charmolue, procurator to

[011-073] the king in the ecclesiastical court?”

[011-074] The goat seated himself on his hind quarters, and began to bleat,

[011-075] waving his fore feet in so strange a manner, that, with the

[011-076] exception of the bad French, and worse Latin, Jacques Charmolue

[011-077] was there complete,—gesture, accent, and attitude.

[011-078] And the crowd applauded louder than ever.

[011-079] “Sacrilege! profanation!” resumed the voice of the bald man.

[011-080] The gypsy turned round once more.

[011-081] “Ah!” said she, “’tis that villanous man!” Then, thrusting her

[011-082] under lip out beyond the upper, she made a little pout, which

[011-083] appeared to be familiar to her, executed a pirouette on her heel,

[011-084] and set about collecting in her tambourine the gifts of the

[011-085] multitude.

[011-086] Big blanks, little blanks, targes[8] and eagle liards showered

[011-087] into it.

[011-088] All at once, she passed in front of Gringoire. Gringoire put his

[011-089] hand so recklessly into his pocket that she halted. “The devil!”

[011-090] said the poet, finding at the bottom of his pocket the reality,

[011-091] that is, to say, a void. In the meantime, the pretty girl stood

[011-092] there, gazing at him with her big eyes, and holding out her

[011-093] tambourine to him and waiting. Gringoire broke into a violent

[011-094] perspiration.

[011-095] If he had all Peru in his pocket, he would certainly have given

[011-096] it to the dancer; but Gringoire had not Peru, and, moreover,

[011-097] America had not yet been discovered.

[011-098] Happily, an unexpected incident came to his rescue.

[011-099] “Will you take yourself off, you Egyptian grasshopper?” cried a

[011-100] sharp voice, which proceeded from the darkest corner of the

[011-101] Place.

[011-102] The young girl turned round in affright. It was no longer the

[011-103] voice of the bald man; it was the voice of a woman, bigoted and

[011-104] malicious.

[011-105] However, this cry, which alarmed the gypsy, delighted a troop of

[011-106] children who were prowling about there.

[011-107] “It is the recluse of the Tour-Roland,” they exclaimed, with wild

[011-108] laughter, “it is the sacked nun who is scolding! Hasn’t she

[011-109] supped? Let’s carry her the remains of the city refreshments!”

[011-110] All rushed towards the Pillar House.

[011-111] In the meanwhile, Gringoire had taken advantage of the dancer’s

[011-112] embarrassment, to disappear. The children’s shouts had reminded

[011-113] him that he, also, had not supped, so he ran to the public

[011-114] buffet. But the little rascals had better legs than he; when he

[011-115] arrived, they had stripped the table. There remained not so much

[011-116] as a miserable _camichon_ at five sous the pound. Nothing

[011-117] remained upon the wall but slender fleurs-de-lis, mingled with

[011-118] rose bushes, painted in 1434 by Mathieu Biterne. It was a meagre

[011-119] supper.

[011-120] It is an unpleasant thing to go to bed without supper, it is a

[011-121] still less pleasant thing not to sup and not to know where one is

[011-122] to sleep. That was Gringoire’s condition. No supper, no shelter;

[011-123] he saw himself pressed on all sides by necessity, and he found

[011-124] necessity very crabbed. He had long ago discovered the truth,

[011-125] that Jupiter created men during a fit of misanthropy, and that

[011-126] during a wise man’s whole life, his destiny holds his philosophy

[011-127] in a state of siege. As for himself, he had never seen the

[011-128] blockade so complete; he heard his stomach sounding a parley, and

[011-129] he considered it very much out of place that evil destiny should

[011-130] capture his philosophy by famine.

[011-131] This melancholy revery was absorbing him more and more, when a

[011-132] song, quaint but full of sweetness, suddenly tore him from it. It

[011-133] was the young gypsy who was singing.

[011-134] Her voice was like her dancing, like her beauty. It was

[011-135] indefinable and charming; something pure and sonorous, aerial,

[011-136] winged, so to speak. There were continual outbursts, melodies,

[011-137] unexpected cadences, then simple phrases strewn with aerial and

[011-138] hissing notes; then floods of scales which would have put a

[011-139] nightingale to rout, but in which harmony was always present;

[011-140] then soft modulations of octaves which rose and fell, like the

[011-141] bosom of the young singer. Her beautiful face followed, with

[011-142] singular mobility, all the caprices of her song, from the wildest

[011-143] inspiration to the chastest dignity. One would have pronounced

[011-144] her now a mad creature, now a queen.

[011-145] The words which she sang were in a tongue unknown to Gringoire,

[011-146] and which seemed to him to be unknown to herself, so little

[011-147] relation did the expression which she imparted to her song bear

[011-148] to the sense of the words. Thus, these four lines, in her mouth,

[011-149] were madly gay,—

[011-150] Un cofre de gran riqueza

[011-151] Hallaron dentro un pilar,

[011-152] Dentro del, nuevas banderas

[011-153] Con figuras de espantar.[9]

[011-154] And an instant afterwards, at the accents which she imparted to

[011-155] this stanza,—

[011-156] Alarabes de cavallo

[011-157] Sin poderse menear,

[011-158] Con espadas, y los cuellos,

[011-159] Ballestas de buen echar,

[011-160] Gringoire felt the tears start to his eyes. Nevertheless, her

[011-161] song breathed joy, most of all, and she seemed to sing like a

[011-162] bird, from serenity and heedlessness.

[011-163] The gypsy’s song had disturbed Gringoire’s revery as the swan

[011-164] disturbs the water. He listened in a sort of rapture, and

[011-165] forgetfulness of everything. It was the first moment in the

[011-166] course of many hours when he did not feel that he suffered.

[011-167] The moment was brief.

[011-168] The same woman’s voice, which had interrupted the gypsy’s dance,

[011-169] interrupted her song.

[011-170] “Will you hold your tongue, you cricket of hell?” it cried, still

[011-171] from the same obscure corner of the place.

[011-172] The poor “cricket” stopped short. Gringoire covered up his ears.

[011-173] “Oh!” he exclaimed, “accursed saw with missing teeth, which comes

[011-174] to break the lyre!”

[011-175] Meanwhile, the other spectators murmured like himself; “To the

[011-176] devil with the sacked nun!” said some of them. And the old

[011-177] invisible kill-joy might have had occasion to repent of her

[011-178] aggressions against the gypsy had their attention not been

[011-179] diverted at this moment by the procession of the Pope of the

[011-180] Fools, which, after having traversed many streets and squares,

[011-181] debouched on the Place de Grève, with all its torches and all its

[011-182] uproar.

[011-183] This procession, which our readers have seen set out from the

[011-184] Palais de Justice, had organized on the way, and had been

[011-185] recruited by all the knaves, idle thieves, and unemployed

[011-186] vagabonds in Paris; so that it presented a very respectable

[011-187] aspect when it arrived at the Grève.

[011-188] First came Egypt. The Duke of Egypt headed it, on horseback, with

[011-189] his counts on foot holding his bridle and stirrups for him;

[011-190] behind them, the male and female Egyptians, pell-mell, with their

[011-191] little children crying on their shoulders; all—duke, counts, and

[011-192] populace—in rags and tatters. Then came the Kingdom of Argot;

[011-193] that is to say, all the thieves of France, arranged according to

[011-194] the order of their dignity; the minor people walking first. Thus

[011-195] defiled by fours, with the divers insignia of their grades, in

[011-196] that strange faculty, most of them lame, some cripples, others

[011-197] one-armed, shop clerks, pilgrims, _hubins_, bootblacks,

[011-198] thimble-riggers, street arabs, beggars, the blear-eyed beggars,

[011-199] thieves, the weakly, vagabonds, merchants, sham soldiers,

[011-200] goldsmiths, passed masters of pickpockets, isolated thieves. A

[011-201] catalogue that would weary Homer. In the centre of the conclave

[011-202] of the passed masters of pickpockets, one had some difficulty in

[011-203] distinguishing the King of Argot, the grand coësre, so called,

[011-204] crouching in a little cart drawn by two big dogs. After the

[011-205] kingdom of the Argotiers, came the Empire of Galilee. Guillaume

[011-206] Rousseau, Emperor of the Empire of Galilee, marched majestically

[011-207] in his robe of purple, spotted with wine, preceded by buffoons

[011-208] wrestling and executing military dances; surrounded by his

[011-209] macebearers, his pickpockets and clerks of the chamber of

[011-210] accounts. Last of all came the corporation of law clerks, with

[011-211] its maypoles crowned with flowers, its black robes, its music

[011-212] worthy of the orgy, and its large candles of yellow wax. In the

[011-213] centre of this crowd, the grand officers of the Brotherhood of

[011-214] Fools bore on their shoulders a litter more loaded down with

[011-215] candles than the reliquary of Sainte-Geneviève in time of pest;

[011-216] and on this litter shone resplendent, with crosier, cope, and

[011-217] mitre, the new Pope of the Fools, the bellringer of Notre-Dame,

[011-218] Quasimodo the hunchback.
