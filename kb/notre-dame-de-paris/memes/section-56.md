---[056-001] Since she had been there, she had neither waked nor slept. In

[056-002] that misfortune, in that cell, she could no longer distinguish

[056-003] her waking hours from slumber, dreams from reality, any more than

[056-004] day from night. All this was mixed, broken, floating,

[056-005] disseminated confusedly in her thought. She no longer felt, she

[056-006] no longer knew, she no longer thought; at the most, she only

[056-007] dreamed. Never had a living creature been thrust more deeply into

[056-008] nothingness.

[056-009] Thus benumbed, frozen, petrified, she had barely noticed on two

[056-010] or three occasions, the sound of a trapdoor opening somewhere

[056-011] above her, without even permitting the passage of a little light,

[056-012] and through which a hand had tossed her a bit of black bread.

[056-013] Nevertheless, this periodical visit of the jailer was the sole

[056-014] communication which was left her with mankind.

[056-015] A single thing still mechanically occupied her ear; above her

[056-016] head, the dampness was filtering through the mouldy stones of the

[056-017] vault, and a drop of water dropped from them at regular

[056-018] intervals. She listened stupidly to the noise made by this drop

[056-019] of water as it fell into the pool beside her.

[056-020] This drop of water falling from time to time into that pool, was

[056-021] the only movement which still went on around her, the only clock

[056-022] which marked the time, the only noise which reached her of all

[056-023] the noise made on the surface of the earth.

[056-024] To tell the whole, however, she also felt, from time to time, in

[056-025] that cesspool of mire and darkness, something cold passing over

[056-026] her foot or her arm, and she shuddered.

[056-027] How long had she been there? She did not know. She had a

[056-028] recollection of a sentence of death pronounced somewhere, against

[056-029] some one, then of having been herself carried away, and of waking

[056-030] up in darkness and silence, chilled to the heart. She had dragged

[056-031] herself along on her hands. Then iron rings that cut her ankles,

[056-032] and chains had rattled. She had recognized the fact that all

[056-033] around her was wall, that below her there was a pavement covered

[056-034] with moisture and a truss of straw; but neither lamp nor

[056-035] air-hole. Then she had seated herself on that straw and,

[056-036] sometimes, for the sake of changing her attitude, on the last

[056-037] stone step in her dungeon. For a while she had tried to count the

[056-038] black minutes measured off for her by the drop of water; but that

[056-039] melancholy labor of an ailing brain had broken off of itself in

[056-040] her head, and had left her in stupor.

[056-041] At length, one day, or one night, (for midnight and midday were

[056-042] of the same color in that sepulchre), she heard above her a

[056-043] louder noise than was usually made by the turnkey when he brought

[056-044] her bread and jug of water. She raised her head, and beheld a ray

[056-045] of reddish light passing through the crevices in the sort of

[056-046] trapdoor contrived in the roof of the _inpace_.

[056-047] At the same time, the heavy lock creaked, the trap grated on its

[056-048] rusty hinges, turned, and she beheld a lantern, a hand, and the

[056-049] lower portions of the bodies of two men, the door being too low

[056-050] to admit of her seeing their heads. The light pained her so

[056-051] acutely that she shut her eyes.

[056-052] When she opened them again the door was closed, the lantern was

[056-053] deposited on one of the steps of the staircase; a man alone stood

[056-054] before her. A monk’s black cloak fell to his feet, a cowl of the

[056-055] same color concealed his face. Nothing was visible of his person,

[056-056] neither face nor hands. It was a long, black shroud standing

[056-057] erect, and beneath which something could be felt moving. She

[056-058] gazed fixedly for several minutes at this sort of spectre. But

[056-059] neither he nor she spoke. One would have pronounced them two

[056-060] statues confronting each other. Two things only seemed alive in

[056-061] that cavern; the wick of the lantern, which sputtered on account

[056-062] of the dampness of the atmosphere, and the drop of water from the

[056-063] roof, which cut this irregular sputtering with its monotonous

[056-064] splash, and made the light of the lantern quiver in concentric

[056-065] waves on the oily water of the pool.

[056-066] At last the prisoner broke the silence.

[056-067] “Who are you?”

[056-068] “A priest.”

[056-069] The words, the accent, the sound of his voice made her tremble.

[056-070] The priest continued, in a hollow voice,—

[056-071] “Are you prepared?”

[056-072] “For what?”

[056-073] “To die.”

[056-074] “Oh!” said she, “will it be soon?”

[056-075] “To-morrow.”

[056-076] Her head, which had been raised with joy, fell back upon her

[056-077] breast.

[056-078] “’Tis very far away yet!” she murmured; “why could they not have

[056-079] done it to-day?”

[056-080] “Then you are very unhappy?” asked the priest, after a silence.

[056-081] “I am very cold,” she replied.

[056-082] She took her feet in her hands, a gesture habitual with unhappy

[056-083] wretches who are cold, as we have already seen in the case of the

[056-084] recluse of the Tour-Roland, and her teeth chattered.

[056-085] The priest appeared to cast his eyes around the dungeon from

[056-086] beneath his cowl.

[056-087] “Without light! without fire! in the water! it is horrible!”

[056-088] “Yes,” she replied, with the bewildered air which unhappiness had

[056-089] given her. “The day belongs to every one, why do they give me

[056-090] only night?”

[056-091] “Do you know,” resumed the priest, after a fresh silence, “why

[056-092] you are here?”

[056-093] “I thought I knew once,” she said, passing her thin fingers over

[056-094] her eyelids, as though to aid her memory, “but I know no longer.”

[056-095] All at once she began to weep like a child.

[056-096] “I should like to get away from here, sir. I am cold, I am

[056-097] afraid, and there are creatures which crawl over my body.”

[056-098] “Well, follow me.”

[056-099] So saying, the priest took her arm. The unhappy girl was frozen

[056-100] to her very soul. Yet that hand produced an impression of cold

[056-101] upon her.

[056-102] “Oh!” she murmured, “’tis the icy hand of death. Who are you?”

[056-103] The priest threw back his cowl; she looked. It was the sinister

[056-104] visage which had so long pursued her; that demon’s head which had

[056-105] appeared at la Falourdel’s, above the head of her adored Phœbus;

[056-106] that eye which she last had seen glittering beside a dagger.

[056-107] This apparition, always so fatal for her, and which had thus

[056-108] driven her on from misfortune to misfortune, even to torture,

[056-109] roused her from her stupor. It seemed to her that the sort of

[056-110] veil which had lain thick upon her memory was rent away. All the

[056-111] details of her melancholy adventure, from the nocturnal scene at

[056-112] la Falourdel’s to her condemnation to the Tournelle, recurred to

[056-113] her memory, no longer vague and confused as heretofore, but

[056-114] distinct, harsh, clear, palpitating, terrible. These souvenirs,

[056-115] half effaced and almost obliterated by excess of suffering, were

[056-116] revived by the sombre figure which stood before her, as the

[056-117] approach of fire causes letters traced upon white paper with

[056-118] invisible ink, to start out perfectly fresh. It seemed to her

[056-119] that all the wounds of her heart opened and bled simultaneously.

[056-120] “Hah!” she cried, with her hands on her eyes, and a convulsive

[056-121] trembling, “’tis the priest!”

[056-122] Then she dropped her arms in discouragement, and remained seated,

[056-123] with lowered head, eyes fixed on the ground, mute and still

[056-124] trembling.

[056-125] The priest gazed at her with the eye of a hawk which has long

[056-126] been soaring in a circle from the heights of heaven over a poor

[056-127] lark cowering in the wheat, and has long been silently

[056-128] contracting the formidable circles of his flight, and has

[056-129] suddenly swooped down upon his prey like a flash of lightning,

[056-130] and holds it panting in his talons.

[056-131] She began to murmur in a low voice,—

[056-132] “Finish! finish! the last blow!” and she drew her head down in

[056-133] terror between her shoulders, like the lamb awaiting the blow of

[056-134] the butcher’s axe.

[056-135] “So I inspire you with horror?” he said at length.

[056-136] She made no reply.

[056-137] “Do I inspire you with horror?” he repeated.

[056-138] Her lips contracted, as though with a smile.

[056-139] “Yes,” said she, “the headsman scoffs at the condemned. Here he

[056-140] has been pursuing me, threatening me, terrifying me for months!

[056-141] Had it not been for him, my God, how happy it should have been!

[056-142] It was he who cast me into this abyss! Oh heavens! it was he who

[056-143] killed him! my Phœbus!”

[056-144] Here, bursting into sobs, and raising her eyes to the priest,—

[056-145] “Oh! wretch, who are you? What have I done to you? Do you then,

[056-146] hate me so? Alas! what have you against me?”

[056-147] “I love thee!” cried the priest.

[056-148] Her tears suddenly ceased, she gazed at him with the look of an

[056-149] idiot. He had fallen on his knees and was devouring her with eyes

[056-150] of flame.

[056-151] “Dost thou understand? I love thee!” he cried again.

[056-152] “What love!” said the unhappy girl with a shudder.

[056-153] He resumed,—

[056-154] “The love of a damned soul.”

[056-155] Both remained silent for several minutes, crushed beneath the

[056-156] weight of their emotions; he maddened, she stupefied.

[056-157] “Listen,” said the priest at last, and a singular calm had come

[056-158] over him; “you shall know all I am about to tell you that which I

[056-159] have hitherto hardly dared to say to myself, when furtively

[056-160] interrogating my conscience at those deep hours of the night when

[056-161] it is so dark that it seems as though God no longer saw us.

[056-162] Listen. Before I knew you, young girl, I was happy.”

[056-163] “So was I!” she sighed feebly.

[056-164] “Do not interrupt me. Yes, I was happy, at least I believed

[056-165] myself to be so. I was pure, my soul was filled with limpid

[056-166] light. No head was raised more proudly and more radiantly than

[056-167] mine. Priests consulted me on chastity; doctors, on doctrines.

[056-168] Yes, science was all in all to me; it was a sister to me, and a

[056-169] sister sufficed. Not but that with age other ideas came to me.

[056-170] More than once my flesh had been moved as a woman’s form passed

[056-171] by. That force of sex and blood which, in the madness of youth, I

[056-172] had imagined that I had stifled forever had, more than once,

[056-173] convulsively raised the chain of iron vows which bind me, a

[056-174] miserable wretch, to the cold stones of the altar. But fasting,

[056-175] prayer, study, the mortifications of the cloister, rendered my

[056-176] soul mistress of my body once more, and then I avoided women.

[056-177] Moreover, I had but to open a book, and all the impure mists of

[056-178] my brain vanished before the splendors of science. In a few

[056-179] moments, I felt the gross things of earth flee far away, and I

[056-180] found myself once more calm, quieted, and serene, in the presence

[056-181] of the tranquil radiance of eternal truth. As long as the demon

[056-182] sent to attack me only vague shadows of women who passed

[056-183] occasionally before my eyes in church, in the streets, in the

[056-184] fields, and who hardly recurred to my dreams, I easily vanquished

[056-185] him. Alas! if the victory has not remained with me, it is the

[056-186] fault of God, who has not created man and the demon of equal

[056-187] force. Listen. One day—”

[056-188] Here the priest paused, and the prisoner heard sighs of anguish

[056-189] break from his breast with a sound of the death rattle.

[056-190] He resumed,—

[056-191] “One day I was leaning on the window of my cell. What book was I

[056-192] reading then? Oh! all that is a whirlwind in my head. I was

[056-193] reading. The window opened upon a Square. I heard a sound of

[056-194] tambourine and music. Annoyed at being thus disturbed in my

[056-195] revery, I glanced into the Square. What I beheld, others saw

[056-196] beside myself, and yet it was not a spectacle made for human

[056-197] eyes. There, in the middle of the pavement,—it was midday, the

[056-198] sun was shining brightly,—a creature was dancing. A creature so

[056-199] beautiful that God would have preferred her to the Virgin and

[056-200] have chosen her for his mother and have wished to be born of her

[056-201] if she had been in existence when he was made man! Her eyes were

[056-202] black and splendid; in the midst of her black locks, some hairs

[056-203] through which the sun shone glistened like threads of gold. Her

[056-204] feet disappeared in their movements like the spokes of a rapidly

[056-205] turning wheel. Around her head, in her black tresses, there were

[056-206] disks of metal, which glittered in the sun, and formed a coronet

[056-207] of stars on her brow. Her dress thick set with spangles, blue,

[056-208] and dotted with a thousand sparks, gleamed like a summer night.

[056-209] Her brown, supple arms twined and untwined around her waist, like

[056-210] two scarfs. The form of her body was surprisingly beautiful. Oh!

[056-211] what a resplendent figure stood out, like something luminous even

[056-212] in the sunlight! Alas, young girl, it was thou! Surprised,

[056-213] intoxicated, charmed, I allowed myself to gaze upon thee. I

[056-214] looked so long that I suddenly shuddered with terror; I felt that

[056-215] fate was seizing hold of me.”
