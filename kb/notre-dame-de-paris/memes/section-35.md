---[035-001] In a few minutes the sentence was drawn up. Its tenor was simple

[035-002] and brief. The customs of the provostship and the viscomty had

[035-003] not yet been worked over by President Thibaut Baillet, and by

[035-004] Roger Barmne, the king’s advocate; they had not been obstructed,

[035-005] at that time, by that lofty hedge of quibbles and procedures,

[035-006] which the two jurisconsults planted there at the beginning of the

[035-007] sixteenth century. All was clear, expeditious, explicit. One went

[035-008] straight to the point then, and at the end of every path there

[035-009] was immediately visible, without thickets and without turnings;

[035-010] the wheel, the gibbet, or the pillory. One at least knew whither

[035-011] one was going.

[035-012] The clerk presented the sentence to the provost, who affixed his

[035-013] seal to it, and departed to pursue his round of the audience

[035-014] hall, in a frame of mind which seemed destined to fill all the

[035-015] jails in Paris that day. Jehan Frollo and Robin Poussepain

[035-016] laughed in their sleeves. Quasimodo gazed on the whole with an

[035-017] indifferent and astonished air.

[035-018] However, at the moment when Master Florian Barbedienne was

[035-019] reading the sentence in his turn, before signing it, the clerk

[035-020] felt himself moved with pity for the poor wretch of a prisoner,

[035-021] and, in the hope of obtaining some mitigation of the penalty, he

[035-022] approached as near the auditor’s ear as possible, and said,

[035-023] pointing to Quasimodo, “That man is deaf.”

[035-024] He hoped that this community of infirmity would awaken Master

[035-025] Florian’s interest in behalf of the condemned man. But, in the

[035-026] first place, we have already observed that Master Florian did not

[035-027] care to have his deafness noticed. In the next place, he was so

[035-028] hard of hearing that he did not catch a single word of what the

[035-029] clerk said to him; nevertheless, he wished to have the appearance

[035-030] of hearing, and replied, “Ah! ah! that is different; I did not

[035-031] know that. An hour more of the pillory, in that case.”

[035-032] And he signed the sentence thus modified.

[035-033] “’Tis well done,” said Robin Poussepain, who cherished a grudge

[035-034] against Quasimodo. “That will teach him to handle people

[035-035] roughly.”

[035-036] CHAPTER II. THE RAT-HOLE.

[035-037] The reader must permit us to take him back to the Place de Grève,

[035-038] which we quitted yesterday with Gringoire, in order to follow la

[035-039] Esmeralda.

[035-040] It is ten o’clock in the morning; everything is indicative of the

[035-041] day after a festival. The pavement is covered with rubbish;

[035-042] ribbons, rags, feathers from tufts of plumes, drops of wax from

[035-043] the torches, crumbs of the public feast. A goodly number of

[035-044] _bourgeois_ are “sauntering,” as we say, here and there, turning

[035-045] over with their feet the extinct brands of the bonfire, going

[035-046] into raptures in front of the Pillar House, over the memory of

[035-047] the fine hangings of the day before, and to-day staring at the

[035-048] nails that secured them a last pleasure. The venders of cider and

[035-049] beer are rolling their barrels among the groups. Some busy

[035-050] passers-by come and go. The merchants converse and call to each

[035-051] other from the thresholds of their shops. The festival, the

[035-052] ambassadors, Coppenole, the Pope of the Fools, are in all mouths;

[035-053] they vie with each other, each trying to criticise it best and

[035-054] laugh the most. And, meanwhile, four mounted sergeants, who have

[035-055] just posted themselves at the four sides of the pillory, have

[035-056] already concentrated around themselves a goodly proportion of the

[035-057] populace scattered on the Place, who condemn themselves to

[035-058] immobility and fatigue in the hope of a small execution.

[035-059] If the reader, after having contemplated this lively and noisy

[035-060] scene which is being enacted in all parts of the Place, will now

[035-061] transfer his gaze towards that ancient demi-Gothic,

[035-062] demi-Romanesque house of the Tour-Roland, which forms the corner

[035-063] on the quay to the west, he will observe, at the angle of the

[035-064] façade, a large public breviary, with rich illuminations,

[035-065] protected from the rain by a little penthouse, and from thieves

[035-066] by a small grating, which, however, permits of the leaves being

[035-067] turned. Beside this breviary is a narrow, arched window, closed

[035-068] by two iron bars in the form of a cross, and looking on the

[035-069] square; the only opening which admits a small quantity of light

[035-070] and air to a little cell without a door, constructed on the

[035-071] ground-floor, in the thickness of the walls of the old house, and

[035-072] filled with a peace all the more profound, with a silence all the

[035-073] more gloomy, because a public place, the most populous and most

[035-074] noisy in Paris swarms and shrieks around it.

[035-075] This little cell had been celebrated in Paris for nearly three

[035-076] centuries, ever since Madame Rolande de la Tour-Roland, in

[035-077] mourning for her father who died in the Crusades, had caused it

[035-078] to be hollowed out in the wall of her own house, in order to

[035-079] immure herself there forever, keeping of all her palace only this

[035-080] lodging whose door was walled up, and whose window stood open,

[035-081] winter and summer, giving all the rest to the poor and to God.

[035-082] The afflicted damsel had, in fact, waited twenty years for death

[035-083] in this premature tomb, praying night and day for the soul of her

[035-084] father, sleeping in ashes, without even a stone for a pillow,

[035-085] clothed in a black sack, and subsisting on the bread and water

[035-086] which the compassion of the passers-by led them to deposit on the

[035-087] ledge of her window, thus receiving charity after having bestowed

[035-088] it. At her death, at the moment when she was passing to the other

[035-089] sepulchre, she had bequeathed this one in perpetuity to afflicted

[035-090] women, mothers, widows, or maidens, who should wish to pray much

[035-091] for others or for themselves, and who should desire to inter

[035-092] themselves alive in a great grief or a great penance. The poor of

[035-093] her day had made her a fine funeral, with tears and benedictions;

[035-094] but, to their great regret, the pious maid had not been

[035-095] canonized, for lack of influence. Those among them who were a

[035-096] little inclined to impiety, had hoped that the matter might be

[035-097] accomplished in Paradise more easily than at Rome, and had

[035-098] frankly besought God, instead of the pope, in behalf of the

[035-099] deceased. The majority had contented themselves with holding the

[035-100] memory of Rolande sacred, and converting her rags into relics.

[035-101] The city, on its side, had founded in honor of the damoiselle, a

[035-102] public breviary, which had been fastened near the window of the

[035-103] cell, in order that passers-by might halt there from time to

[035-104] time, were it only to pray; that prayer might remind them of

[035-105] alms, and that the poor recluses, heiresses of Madame Rolande’s

[035-106] vault, might not die outright of hunger and forgetfulness.

[035-107] Moreover, this sort of tomb was not so very rare a thing in the

[035-108] cities of the Middle Ages. One often encountered in the most

[035-109] frequented street, in the most crowded and noisy market, in the

[035-110] very middle, under the feet of the horses, under the wheels of

[035-111] the carts, as it were, a cellar, a well, a tiny walled and grated

[035-112] cabin, at the bottom of which a human being prayed night and day,

[035-113] voluntarily devoted to some eternal lamentation, to some great

[035-114] expiation. And all the reflections which that strange spectacle

[035-115] would awaken in us to-day; that horrible cell, a sort of

[035-116] intermediary link between a house and the tomb, the cemetery and

[035-117] the city; that living being cut off from the human community, and

[035-118] thenceforth reckoned among the dead; that lamp consuming its last

[035-119] drop of oil in the darkness; that remnant of life flickering in

[035-120] the grave; that breath, that voice, that eternal prayer in a box

[035-121] of stone; that face forever turned towards the other world; that

[035-122] eye already illuminated with another sun; that ear pressed to the

[035-123] walls of a tomb; that soul a prisoner in that body; that body a

[035-124] prisoner in that dungeon cell, and beneath that double envelope

[035-125] of flesh and granite, the murmur of that soul in pain;—nothing of

[035-126] all this was perceived by the crowd. The piety of that age, not

[035-127] very subtle nor much given to reasoning, did not see so many

[035-128] facets in an act of religion. It took the thing in the block,

[035-129] honored, venerated, hallowed the sacrifice at need, but did not

[035-130] analyze the sufferings, and felt but moderate pity for them. It

[035-131] brought some pittance to the miserable penitent from time to

[035-132] time, looked through the hole to see whether he were still

[035-133] living, forgot his name, hardly knew how many years ago he had

[035-134] begun to die, and to the stranger, who questioned them about the

[035-135] living skeleton who was perishing in that cellar, the neighbors

[035-136] replied simply, “It is the recluse.”

[035-137] Everything was then viewed without metaphysics, without

[035-138] exaggeration, without magnifying glass, with the naked eye. The

[035-139] microscope had not yet been invented, either for things of matter

[035-140] or for things of the mind.

[035-141] Moreover, although people were but little surprised by it, the

[035-142] examples of this sort of cloistration in the hearts of cities

[035-143] were in truth frequent, as we have just said. There were in Paris

[035-144] a considerable number of these cells, for praying to God and

[035-145] doing penance; they were nearly all occupied. It is true that the

[035-146] clergy did not like to have them empty, since that implied

[035-147] lukewarmness in believers, and that lepers were put into them

[035-148] when there were no penitents on hand. Besides the cell on the

[035-149] Grève, there was one at Montfaucon, one at the Charnier des

[035-150] Innocents, another I hardly know where,—at the Clichon House, I

[035-151] think; others still at many spots where traces of them are found

[035-152] in traditions, in default of memorials. The University had also

[035-153] its own. On Mount Sainte-Geneviève a sort of Job of the Middle

[035-154] Ages, for the space of thirty years, chanted the seven

[035-155] penitential psalms on a dunghill at the bottom of a cistern,

[035-156] beginning anew when he had finished, singing loudest at night,

[035-157] _magna voce per umbras_, and to-day, the antiquary fancies that

[035-158] he hears his voice as he enters the Rue du Puits-qui-parle—the

[035-159] street of the “Speaking Well.”

[035-160] To confine ourselves to the cell in the Tour-Roland, we must say

[035-161] that it had never lacked recluses. After the death of Madame

[035-162] Roland, it had stood vacant for a year or two, though rarely.

[035-163] Many women had come thither to mourn, until their death, for

[035-164] relatives, lovers, faults. Parisian malice, which thrusts its

[035-165] finger into everything, even into things which concern it the

[035-166] least, affirmed that it had beheld but few widows there.

[035-167] In accordance with the fashion of the epoch, a Latin inscription

[035-168] on the wall indicated to the learned passer-by the pious purpose

[035-169] of this cell. The custom was retained until the middle of the

[035-170] sixteenth century of explaining an edifice by a brief device

[035-171] inscribed above the door. Thus, one still reads in France, above

[035-172] the wicket of the prison in the seignorial mansion of Tourville,

[035-173] _Sileto et spera_; in Ireland, beneath the armorial bearings

[035-174] which surmount the grand door to Fortescue Castle, _Forte scutum,

[035-175] salus ducum_; in England, over the principal entrance to the

[035-176] hospitable mansion of the Earls Cowper: _Tuum est_. At that time

[035-177] every edifice was a thought.

[035-178] As there was no door to the walled cell of the Tour-Roland, these

[035-179] two words had been carved in large Roman capitals over the

[035-180] window,—

[035-181] TU, ORA.

[035-182] And this caused the people, whose good sense does not perceive so

[035-183] much refinement in things, and likes to translate _Ludovico

[035-184] Magno_ by _Porte Saint-Denis_, to give to this dark, gloomy, damp

[035-185] cavity, the name of “The Rat-Hole.” An explanation less sublime,

[035-186] perhaps, than the other; but, on the other hand, more

[035-187] picturesque.

[035-188] CHAPTER III. HISTORY OF A LEAVENED CAKE OF MAIZE.

[035-189] At the epoch of this history, the cell in the Tour-Roland was

[035-190] occupied. If the reader desires to know by whom, he has only to

[035-191] lend an ear to the conversation of three worthy gossips, who, at

[035-192] the moment when we have directed his attention to the Rat-Hole,

[035-193] were directing their steps towards the same spot, coming up along

[035-194] the water’s edge from the Châtelet, towards the Grève.
