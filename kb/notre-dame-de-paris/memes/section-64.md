[064-002] the hair of my flesh stood up.”

[064-003] CHAPTER II. HUNCHBACKED, ONE EYED, LAME.

[064-004] Every city during the Middle Ages, and every city in France down

[064-005] to the time of Louis XII. had its places of asylum. These

[064-006] sanctuaries, in the midst of the deluge of penal and barbarous

[064-007] jurisdictions which inundated the city, were a species of islands

[064-008] which rose above the level of human justice. Every criminal who

[064-009] landed there was safe. There were in every suburb almost as many

[064-010] places of asylum as gallows. It was the abuse of impunity by the

[064-011] side of the abuse of punishment; two bad things which strove to

[064-012] correct each other. The palaces of the king, the hôtels of the

[064-013] princes, and especially churches, possessed the right of asylum.

[064-014] Sometimes a whole city which stood in need of being repeopled was

[064-015] temporarily created a place of refuge. Louis XI. made all Paris a

[064-016] refuge in 1467.

[064-017] His foot once within the asylum, the criminal was sacred; but he

[064-018] must beware of leaving it; one step outside the sanctuary, and he

[064-019] fell back into the flood. The wheel, the gibbet, the strappado,

[064-020] kept good guard around the place of refuge, and lay in watch

[064-021] incessantly for their prey, like sharks around a vessel. Hence,

[064-022] condemned men were to be seen whose hair had grown white in a

[064-023] cloister, on the steps of a palace, in the enclosure of an abbey,

[064-024] beneath the porch of a church; in this manner the asylum was a

[064-025] prison as much as any other. It sometimes happened that a solemn

[064-026] decree of parliament violated the asylum and restored the

[064-027] condemned man to the executioner; but this was of rare

[064-028] occurrence. Parliaments were afraid of the bishops, and when

[064-029] there was friction between these two robes, the gown had but a

[064-030] poor chance against the cassock. Sometimes, however, as in the

[064-031] affair of the assassins of Petit-Jean, the headsman of Paris, and

[064-032] in that of Emery Rousseau, the murderer of Jean Valleret, justice

[064-033] overleaped the church and passed on to the execution of its

[064-034] sentences; but unless by virtue of a decree of Parliament, woe to

[064-035] him who violated a place of asylum with armed force! The reader

[064-036] knows the manner of death of Robert de Clermont, Marshal of

[064-037] France, and of Jean de Châlons, Marshal of Champagne; and yet the

[064-038] question was only of a certain Perrin Marc, the clerk of a

[064-039] money-changer, a miserable assassin; but the two marshals had

[064-040] broken the doors of St. Méry. Therein lay the enormity.

[064-041] Such respect was cherished for places of refuge that, according

[064-042] to tradition, animals even felt it at times. Aymoire relates that

[064-043] a stag, being chased by Dagobert, having taken refuge near the

[064-044] tomb of Saint-Denis, the pack of hounds stopped short and barked.

[064-045] Churches generally had a small apartment prepared for the

[064-046] reception of supplicants. In 1407, Nicolas Flamel caused to be

[064-047] built on the vaults of Saint-Jacques de la Boucherie, a chamber

[064-048] which cost him four livres six sous, sixteen farthings, parisis.

[064-049] At Notre-Dame it was a tiny cell situated on the roof of the side

[064-050] aisle, beneath the flying buttresses, precisely at the spot where

[064-051] the wife of the present janitor of the towers has made for

[064-052] herself a garden, which is to the hanging gardens of Babylon what

[064-053] a lettuce is to a palm-tree, what a porter’s wife is to a

[064-054] Semiramis.

[064-055] It was here that Quasimodo had deposited la Esmeralda, after his

[064-056] wild and triumphant course. As long as that course lasted, the

[064-057] young girl had been unable to recover her senses, half

[064-058] unconscious, half awake, no longer feeling anything, except that

[064-059] she was mounting through the air, floating in it, flying in it,

[064-060] that something was raising her above the earth. From time to time

[064-061] she heard the loud laughter, the noisy voice of Quasimodo in her

[064-062] ear; she half opened her eyes; then below her she confusedly

[064-063] beheld Paris checkered with its thousand roofs of slate and

[064-064] tiles, like a red and blue mosaic, above her head the frightful

[064-065] and joyous face of Quasimodo. Then her eyelids drooped again; she

[064-066] thought that all was over, that they had executed her during her

[064-067] swoon, and that the misshapen spirit which had presided over her

[064-068] destiny, had laid hold of her and was bearing her away. She dared

[064-069] not look at him, and she surrendered herself to her fate. But

[064-070] when the bellringer, dishevelled and panting, had deposited her

[064-071] in the cell of refuge, when she felt his huge hands gently

[064-072] detaching the cord which bruised her arms, she felt that sort of

[064-073] shock which awakens with a start the passengers of a vessel which

[064-074] runs aground in the middle of a dark night. Her thoughts awoke

[064-075] also, and returned to her one by one. She saw that she was in

[064-076] Notre-Dame; she remembered having been torn from the hands of the

[064-077] executioner; that Phœbus was alive, that Phœbus loved her no

[064-078] longer; and as these two ideas, one of which shed so much

[064-079] bitterness over the other, presented themselves simultaneously to

[064-080] the poor condemned girl; she turned to Quasimodo, who was

[064-081] standing in front of her, and who terrified her; she said to

[064-082] him,—

[064-083] “Why have you saved me?”

[064-084] He gazed at her with anxiety, as though seeking to divine what

[064-085] she was saying to him. She repeated her question. Then he gave

[064-086] her a profoundly sorrowful glance and fled. She was astonished.

[064-087] A few moments later he returned, bearing a package which he cast

[064-088] at her feet. It was clothing which some charitable women had left

[064-089] on the threshold of the church for her.

[064-090] Then she dropped her eyes upon herself and saw that she was

[064-091] almost naked, and blushed. Life had returned.

[064-092] Quasimodo appeared to experience something of this modesty. He

[064-093] covered his eyes with his large hand and retired once more, but

[064-094] slowly.

[064-095] She made haste to dress herself. The robe was a white one with a

[064-096] white veil,—the garb of a novice of the Hôtel-Dieu.

[064-097] She had barely finished when she beheld Quasimodo returning. He

[064-098] carried a basket under one arm and a mattress under the other. In

[064-099] the basket there was a bottle, bread, and some provisions. He set

[064-100] the basket on the floor and said, “Eat!” He spread the mattress

[064-101] on the flagging and said, “Sleep.”

[064-102] It was his own repast, it was his own bed, which the bellringer

[064-103] had gone in search of.

[064-104] The gypsy raised her eyes to thank him, but she could not

[064-105] articulate a word. She dropped her head with a quiver of terror.

[064-106] Then he said to her.—

[064-107] “I frighten you. I am very ugly, am I not? Do not look at me;

[064-108] only listen to me. During the day you will remain here; at night

[064-109] you can walk all over the church. But do not leave the church

[064-110] either by day or by night. You would be lost. They would kill

[064-111] you, and I should die.”

[064-112] She was touched and raised her head to answer him. He had

[064-113] disappeared. She found herself alone once more, meditating upon

[064-114] the singular words of this almost monstrous being, and struck by

[064-115] the sound of his voice, which was so hoarse yet so gentle.

[064-116] Then she examined her cell. It was a chamber about six feet

[064-117] square, with a small window and a door on the slightly sloping

[064-118] plane of the roof formed of flat stones. Many gutters with the

[064-119] figures of animals seemed to be bending down around her, and

[064-120] stretching their necks in order to stare at her through the

[064-121] window. Over the edge of her roof she perceived the tops of

[064-122] thousands of chimneys which caused the smoke of all the fires in

[064-123] Paris to rise beneath her eyes. A sad sight for the poor gypsy, a

[064-124] foundling, condemned to death, an unhappy creature, without

[064-125] country, without family, without a hearthstone.

[064-126] At the moment when the thought of her isolation thus appeared to

[064-127] her more poignant than ever, she felt a bearded and hairy head

[064-128] glide between her hands, upon her knees. She started (everything

[064-129] alarmed her now) and looked. It was the poor goat, the agile

[064-130] Djali, which had made its escape after her, at the moment when

[064-131] Quasimodo had put to flight Charmolue’s brigade, and which had

[064-132] been lavishing caresses on her feet for nearly an hour past,

[064-133] without being able to win a glance. The gypsy covered him with

[064-134] kisses.

[064-135] “Oh! Djali!” she said, “how I have forgotten thee! And so thou

[064-136] still thinkest of me! Oh! thou art not an ingrate!”

[064-137] At the same time, as though an invisible hand had lifted the

[064-138] weight which had repressed her tears in her heart for so long,

[064-139] she began to weep, and, in proportion as her tears flowed, she

[064-140] felt all that was most acrid and bitter in her grief depart with

[064-141] them.

[064-142] Evening came, she thought the night so beautiful that she made

[064-143] the circuit of the elevated gallery which surrounds the church.

[064-144] It afforded her some relief, so calm did the earth appear when

[064-145] viewed from that height.

[064-146] CHAPTER III. DEAF.

[064-147] On the following morning, she perceived on awaking, that she had

[064-148] been asleep. This singular thing astonished her. She had been so

[064-149] long unaccustomed to sleep! A joyous ray of the rising sun

[064-150] entered through her window and touched her face. At the same time

[064-151] with the sun, she beheld at that window an object which

[064-152] frightened her, the unfortunate face of Quasimodo. She

[064-153] involuntarily closed her eyes again, but in vain; she fancied

[064-154] that she still saw through the rosy lids that gnome’s mask,

[064-155] one-eyed and gap-toothed. Then, while she still kept her eyes

[064-156] closed, she heard a rough voice saying, very gently,—

[064-157] “Be not afraid. I am your friend. I came to watch you sleep. It

[064-158] does not hurt you if I come to see you sleep, does it? What

[064-159] difference does it make to you if I am here when your eyes are

[064-160] closed! Now I am going. Stay, I have placed myself behind the

[064-161] wall. You can open your eyes again.”

[064-162] There was something more plaintive than these words, and that was

[064-163] the accent in which they were uttered. The gypsy, much touched,

[064-164] opened her eyes. He was, in fact, no longer at the window. She

[064-165] approached the opening, and beheld the poor hunchback crouching

[064-166] in an angle of the wall, in a sad and resigned attitude. She made

[064-167] an effort to surmount the repugnance with which he inspired her.

[064-168] “Come,” she said to him gently. From the movement of the gypsy’s

[064-169] lips, Quasimodo thought that she was driving him away; then he

[064-170] rose and retired limping, slowly, with drooping head, without

[064-171] even daring to raise to the young girl his gaze full of despair.

[064-172] “Do come,” she cried, but he continued to retreat. Then she

[064-173] darted from her cell, ran to him, and grasped his arm. On feeling

[064-174] her touch him, Quasimodo trembled in every limb. He raised his

[064-175] suppliant eye, and seeing that she was leading him back to her

[064-176] quarters, his whole face beamed with joy and tenderness. She

[064-177] tried to make him enter the cell; but he persisted in remaining

[064-178] on the threshold. “No, no,” said he; “the owl enters not the nest

[064-179] of the lark.”

[064-180] Then she crouched down gracefully on her couch, with her goat

[064-181] asleep at her feet. Both remained motionless for several moments,

[064-182] considering in silence, she so much grace, he so much ugliness.

[064-183] Every moment she discovered some fresh deformity in Quasimodo.

[064-184] Her glance travelled from his knock knees to his humped back,

[064-185] from his humped back to his only eye. She could not comprehend

[064-186] the existence of a being so awkwardly fashioned. Yet there was so

[064-187] much sadness and so much gentleness spread over all this, that

[064-188] she began to become reconciled to it.

[064-189] He was the first to break the silence. “So you were telling me to

[064-190] return?”
