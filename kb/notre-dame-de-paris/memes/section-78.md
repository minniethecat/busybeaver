---[078-001] He lifted his cap again, and continued, still dreamily, with the

[078-002] air and accent of a hunter who is cheering on his pack of hounds:

[078-003] “Good, my people! bravely done! break these false lords! do your

[078-004] duty! at them! have at them! pillage them! take them! sack

[078-005] them!... Ah! you want to be kings, messeigneurs? On, my people

[078-006] on!”

[078-007] Here he interrupted himself abruptly, bit his lips as though to

[078-008] take back his thought which had already half escaped, bent his

[078-009] piercing eyes in turn on each of the five persons who surrounded

[078-010] him, and suddenly grasping his hat with both hands and staring

[078-011] full at it, he said to it: “Oh! I would burn you if you knew what

[078-012] there was in my head.”

[078-013] Then casting about him once more the cautious and uneasy glance

[078-014] of the fox re-entering his hole,—

[078-015] “No matter! we will succor monsieur the bailiff. Unfortunately,

[078-016] we have but few troops here at the present moment, against so

[078-017] great a populace. We must wait until to-morrow. The order will be

[078-018] transmitted to the City and every one who is caught will be

[078-019] immediately hung.”

[078-020] “By the way, sire,” said Gossip Coictier, “I had forgotten that

[078-021] in the first agitation, the watch have seized two laggards of the

[078-022] band. If your majesty desires to see these men, they are here.”

[078-023] “If I desire to see them!” cried the king. “What! _Pasque-Dieu!_

[078-024] You forget a thing like that! Run quick, you, Olivier! Go, seek

[078-025] them!”

[078-026] Master Olivier quitted the room and returned a moment later with

[078-027] the two prisoners, surrounded by archers of the guard. The first

[078-028] had a coarse, idiotic, drunken and astonished face. He was

[078-029] clothed in rags, and walked with one knee bent and dragging his

[078-030] leg. The second had a pallid and smiling countenance, with which

[078-031] the reader is already acquainted.

[078-032] The king surveyed them for a moment without uttering a word, then

[078-033] addressing the first one abruptly,—

[078-034] “What’s your name?”

[078-035] “Gieffroy Pincebourde.”

[078-036] “Your trade.”

[078-037] “Outcast.”

[078-038] “What were you going to do in this damnable sedition?”

[078-039] The outcast stared at the king, and swung his arms with a stupid

[078-040] air.

[078-041] He had one of those awkwardly shaped heads where intelligence is

[078-042] about as much at its ease as a light beneath an extinguisher.

[078-043] “I know not,” said he. “They went, I went.”

[078-044] “Were you not going to outrageously attack and pillage your lord,

[078-045] the bailiff of the palace?”

[078-046] “I know that they were going to take something from some one.

[078-047] That is all.”

[078-048] A soldier pointed out to the king a billhook which he had seized

[078-049] on the person of the vagabond.

[078-050] “Do you recognize this weapon?” demanded the king.

[078-051] “Yes; ’tis my billhook; I am a vine-dresser.”

[078-052] “And do you recognize this man as your companion?” added Louis

[078-053] XI., pointing to the other prisoner.

[078-054] “No, I do not know him.”

[078-055] “That will do,” said the king, making a sign with his finger to

[078-056] the silent personage who stood motionless beside the door, to

[078-057] whom we have already called the reader’s attention.

[078-058] “Gossip Tristan, here is a man for you.”

[078-059] Tristan l’Hermite bowed. He gave an order in a low voice to two

[078-060] archers, who led away the poor vagabond.

[078-061] In the meantime, the king had approached the second prisoner, who

[078-062] was perspiring in great drops: “Your name?”

[078-063] “Sire, Pierre Gringoire.”

[078-064] “Your trade?”

[078-065] “Philosopher, sire.”

[078-066] “How do you permit yourself, knave, to go and besiege our friend,

[078-067] monsieur the bailiff of the palace, and what have you to say

[078-068] concerning this popular agitation?”

[078-069] “Sire, I had nothing to do with it.”

[078-070] “Come, now! you wanton wretch, were not you apprehended by the

[078-071] watch in that bad company?”

[078-072] “No, sire, there is a mistake. ’Tis a fatality. I make tragedies.

[078-073] Sire, I entreat your majesty to listen to me. I am a poet. ’Tis

[078-074] the melancholy way of men of my profession to roam the streets by

[078-075] night. I was passing there. It was mere chance. I was unjustly

[078-076] arrested; I am innocent of this civil tempest. Your majesty sees

[078-077] that the vagabond did not recognize me. I conjure your majesty—”

[078-078] “Hold your tongue!” said the king, between two swallows of his

[078-079] ptisan. “You split our head!”

[078-080] Tristan l’Hermite advanced and pointing to Gringoire,—

[078-081] “Sire, can this one be hanged also?”

[078-082] This was the first word that he had uttered.

[078-083] “Phew!” replied the king, “I see no objection.”

[078-084] “I see a great many!” said Gringoire.

[078-085] At that moment, our philosopher was greener than an olive. He

[078-086] perceived from the king’s cold and indifferent mien that there

[078-087] was no other resource than something very pathetic, and he flung

[078-088] himself at the feet of Louis XI., exclaiming, with gestures of

[078-089] despair:—

[078-090] “Sire! will your majesty deign to hear me. Sire! break not in

[078-091] thunder over so small a thing as myself. God’s great lightning

[078-092] doth not bombard a lettuce. Sire, you are an august and, very

[078-093] puissant monarch; have pity on a poor man who is honest, and who

[078-094] would find it more difficult to stir up a revolt than a cake of

[078-095] ice would to give out a spark! Very gracious sire, kindness is

[078-096] the virtue of a lion and a king. Alas! rigor only frightens

[078-097] minds; the impetuous gusts of the north wind do not make the

[078-098] traveller lay aside his cloak; the sun, bestowing his rays little

[078-099] by little, warms him in such ways that it will make him strip to

[078-100] his shirt. Sire, you are the sun. I protest to you, my sovereign

[078-101] lord and master, that I am not an outcast, thief, and disorderly

[078-102] fellow. Revolt and brigandage belong not to the outfit of Apollo.

[078-103] I am not the man to fling myself into those clouds which break

[078-104] out into seditious clamor. I am your majesty’s faithful vassal.

[078-105] That same jealousy which a husband cherisheth for the honor of

[078-106] his wife, the resentment which the son hath for the love of his

[078-107] father, a good vassal should feel for the glory of his king; he

[078-108] should pine away for the zeal of this house, for the

[078-109] aggrandizement of his service. Every other passion which should

[078-110] transport him would be but madness. These, sire, are my maxims of

[078-111] state: then do not judge me to be a seditious and thieving rascal

[078-112] because my garment is worn at the elbows. If you will grant me

[078-113] mercy, sire, I will wear it out on the knees in praying to God

[078-114] for you night and morning! Alas! I am not extremely rich, ’tis

[078-115] true. I am even rather poor. But not vicious on that account. It

[078-116] is not my fault. Every one knoweth that great wealth is not to be

[078-117] drawn from literature, and that those who are best posted in good

[078-118] books do not always have a great fire in winter. The advocate’s

[078-119] trade taketh all the grain, and leaveth only straw to the other

[078-120] scientific professions. There are forty very excellent proverbs

[078-121] anent the hole-ridden cloak of the philosopher. Oh, sire!

[078-122] clemency is the only light which can enlighten the interior of so

[078-123] great a soul. Clemency beareth the torch before all the other

[078-124] virtues. Without it they are but blind men groping after God in

[078-125] the dark. Compassion, which is the same thing as clemency,

[078-126] causeth the love of subjects, which is the most powerful

[078-127] bodyguard to a prince. What matters it to your majesty, who

[078-128] dazzles all faces, if there is one poor man more on earth, a poor

[078-129] innocent philosopher spluttering amid the shadows of calamity,

[078-130] with an empty pocket which resounds against his hollow belly?

[078-131] Moreover, sire, I am a man of letters. Great kings make a pearl

[078-132] for their crowns by protecting letters. Hercules did not disdain

[078-133] the title of Musagetes. Mathias Corvin favored Jean de Monroyal,

[078-134] the ornament of mathematics. Now, ’tis an ill way to protect

[078-135] letters to hang men of letters. What a stain on Alexander if he

[078-136] had hung Aristoteles! This act would not be a little patch on the

[078-137] face of his reputation to embellish it, but a very malignant

[078-138] ulcer to disfigure it. Sire! I made a very proper epithalamium

[078-139] for Mademoiselle of Flanders and Monseigneur the very august

[078-140] Dauphin. That is not a firebrand of rebellion. Your majesty sees

[078-141] that I am not a scribbler of no reputation, that I have studied

[078-142] excellently well, and that I possess much natural eloquence. Have

[078-143] mercy upon me, sire! In so doing you will perform a gallant deed

[078-144] to our Lady, and I swear to you that I am greatly terrified at

[078-145] the idea of being hanged!”

[078-146] So saying, the unhappy Gringoire kissed the king’s slippers, and

[078-147] Guillaume Rym said to Coppenole in a low tone: “He doth well to

[078-148] drag himself on the earth. Kings are like the Jupiter of Crete,

[078-149] they have ears only in their feet.” And without troubling himself

[078-150] about the Jupiter of Crete, the hosier replied with a heavy

[078-151] smile, and his eyes fixed on Gringoire: “Oh! that’s it exactly! I

[078-152] seem to hear Chancellor Hugonet craving mercy of me.”

[078-153] When Gringoire paused at last, quite out of breath, he raised his

[078-154] head tremblingly towards the king, who was engaged in scratching

[078-155] a spot on the knee of his breeches with his finger-nail; then his

[078-156] majesty began to drink from the goblet of ptisan. But he uttered

[078-157] not a word, and this silence tortured Gringoire. At last the king

[078-158] looked at him. “Here is a terrible bawler!” said, he. Then,

[078-159] turning to Tristan l’Hermite, “Bah! let him go!”

[078-160] Gringoire fell backwards, quite thunderstruck with joy.

[078-161] “At liberty!” growled Tristan “Doth not your majesty wish to have

[078-162] him detained a little while in a cage?”

[078-163] “Gossip,” retorted Louis XI., “think you that ’tis for birds of

[078-164] this feather that we cause to be made cages at three hundred and

[078-165] sixty-seven livres, eight sous, three deniers apiece? Release him

[078-166] at once, the wanton (Louis XI. was fond of this word which

[078-167] formed, with _Pasque-Dieu_, the foundation of his joviality), and

[078-168] put him out with a buffet.”

[078-169] “Ugh!” cried Gringoire, “what a great king is here!”

[078-170] And for fear of a counter order, he rushed towards the door,

[078-171] which Tristan opened for him with a very bad grace. The soldiers

[078-172] left the room with him, pushing him before them with stout

[078-173] thwacks, which Gringoire bore like a true stoical philosopher.

[078-174] The king’s good humor since the revolt against the bailiff had

[078-175] been announced to him, made itself apparent in every way. This

[078-176] unwonted clemency was no small sign of it. Tristan l’Hermite in

[078-177] his corner wore the surly look of a dog who has had a bone

[078-178] snatched away from him.

[078-179] Meanwhile, the king thrummed gayly with his fingers on the arm of

[078-180] his chair, the March of Pont-Audemer. He was a dissembling

[078-181] prince, but one who understood far better how to hide his

[078-182] troubles than his joys. These external manifestations of joy at

[078-183] any good news sometimes proceeded to very great lengths thus, on

[078-184] the death, of Charles the Bold, to the point of vowing silver

[078-185] balustrades to Saint Martin of Tours; on his advent to the

[078-186] throne, so far as forgetting to order his father’s obsequies.

[078-187] “Hé! sire!” suddenly exclaimed Jacques Coictier, “what has become

[078-188] of the acute attack of illness for which your majesty had me

[078-189] summoned?”

[078-190] “Oh!” said the king, “I really suffer greatly, my gossip. There

[078-191] is a hissing in my ear and fiery rakes rack my chest.”

[078-192] Coictier took the king’s hand, and begun to feel of his pulse

[078-193] with a knowing air.

[078-194] “Look, Coppenole,” said Rym, in a low voice. “Behold him between

[078-195] Coictier and Tristan. They are his whole court. A physician for

[078-196] himself, a headsman for others.”

[078-197] As he felt the king’s pulse, Coictier assumed an air of greater

[078-198] and greater alarm. Louis XI. watched him with some anxiety.

[078-199] Coictier grew visibly more gloomy. The brave man had no other

[078-200] farm than the king’s bad health. He speculated on it to the best

[078-201] of his ability.
