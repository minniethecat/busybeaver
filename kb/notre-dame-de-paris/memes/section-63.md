---[063-001] Claude, in the state of hallucination in which he found himself,

[063-002] believed that he saw, that he saw with his actual eyes, the bell

[063-003] tower of hell; the thousand lights scattered over the whole

[063-004] height of the terrible tower seemed to him so many porches of the

[063-005] immense interior furnace; the voices and noises which escaped

[063-006] from it seemed so many shrieks, so many death groans. Then he

[063-007] became alarmed, he put his hands on his ears that he might no

[063-008] longer hear, turned his back that he might no longer see, and

[063-009] fled from the frightful vision with hasty strides.

[063-010] But the vision was in himself.

[063-011] When he re-entered the streets, the passers-by elbowing each

[063-012] other by the light of the shop-fronts, produced upon him the

[063-013] effect of a constant going and coming of spectres about him.

[063-014] There were strange noises in his ears; extraordinary fancies

[063-015] disturbed his brain. He saw neither houses, nor pavements, nor

[063-016] chariots, nor men and women, but a chaos of indeterminate objects

[063-017] whose edges melted into each other. At the corner of the Rue de

[063-018] la Barillerie, there was a grocer’s shop whose porch was

[063-019] garnished all about, according to immemorial custom, with hoops

[063-020] of tin from which hung a circle of wooden candles, which came in

[063-021] contact with each other in the wind, and rattled like castanets.

[063-022] He thought he heard a cluster of skeletons at Montfaucon clashing

[063-023] together in the gloom.

[063-024] “Oh!” he muttered, “the night breeze dashes them against each

[063-025] other, and mingles the noise of their chains with the rattle of

[063-026] their bones! Perhaps she is there among them!”

[063-027] In his state of frenzy, he knew not whither he was going. After a

[063-028] few strides he found himself on the Pont Saint-Michel. There was

[063-029] a light in the window of a ground-floor room; he approached.

[063-030] Through a cracked window he beheld a mean chamber which recalled

[063-031] some confused memory to his mind. In that room, badly lighted by

[063-032] a meagre lamp, there was a fresh, light-haired young man, with a

[063-033] merry face, who amid loud bursts of laughter was embracing a very

[063-034] audaciously attired young girl; and near the lamp sat an old

[063-035] crone spinning and singing in a quavering voice. As the young man

[063-036] did not laugh constantly, fragments of the old woman’s ditty

[063-037] reached the priest; it was something unintelligible yet

[063-038] frightful,—

[063-039] “Grève, aboie, Grève, grouille!

[063-040] File, file, ma quenouille,

[063-041] File sa corde au bourreau,

[063-042] Qui siffle dans le préau,

[063-043] Grève, aboie, Grève, grouille!

[063-044] “La belle corde de chanvre!

[063-045] Semez d’Issy jusqu’à Vanvre

[063-046] Du chanvre et non pas du blé.

[063-047] Le voleur n’a pas volé

[063-048] La belle corde de chanvre.

[063-049] “Grève, grouille, Grève, aboie!

[063-050] Pour voir la fille de joie,

[063-051] Prendre au gibet chassieux,

[063-052] Les fenêtres sont des yeux.

[063-053] Grève, grouille, Grève, aboie!”[51]

[063-054] Thereupon the young man laughed and caressed the wench. The crone

[063-055] was la Falourdel; the girl was a courtesan; the young man was his

[063-056] brother Jehan.

[063-057] He continued to gaze. That spectacle was as good as any other.

[063-058] He saw Jehan go to a window at the end of the room, open it, cast

[063-059] a glance on the quay, where in the distance blazed a thousand

[063-060] lighted casements, and he heard him say as he closed the sash,—

[063-061] “’Pon my soul! How dark it is; the people are lighting their

[063-062] candles, and the good God his stars.”

[063-063] Then Jehan came back to the hag, smashed a bottle standing on the

[063-064] table, exclaiming,—

[063-065] “Already empty, _cor-bœuf!_ and I have no more money! Isabeau, my

[063-066] dear, I shall not be satisfied with Jupiter until he has changed

[063-067] your two white nipples into two black bottles, where I may suck

[063-068] wine of Beaune day and night.”

[063-069] This fine pleasantry made the courtesan laugh, and Jehan left the

[063-070] room.

[063-071] Dom Claude had barely time to fling himself on the ground in

[063-072] order that he might not be met, stared in the face and recognized

[063-073] by his brother. Luckily, the street was dark, and the scholar was

[063-074] tipsy. Nevertheless, he caught sight of the archdeacon prone upon

[063-075] the earth in the mud.

[063-076] “Oh! oh!” said he; “here’s a fellow who has been leading a jolly

[063-077] life, to-day.”

[063-078] He stirred up Dom Claude with his foot, and the latter held his

[063-079] breath.

[063-080] “Dead drunk,” resumed Jehan. “Come, he’s full. A regular leech

[063-081] detached from a hogshead. He’s bald,” he added, bending down,

[063-082] “’tis an old man! _Fortunate senex!_”

[063-083] Then Dom Claude heard him retreat, saying,—

[063-084] “’Tis all the same, reason is a fine thing, and my brother the

[063-085] archdeacon is very happy in that he is wise and has money.”

[063-086] Then the archdeacon rose to his feet, and ran without halting,

[063-087] towards Notre-Dame, whose enormous towers he beheld rising above

[063-088] the houses through the gloom.

[063-089] At the instant when he arrived, panting, on the Place du Parvis,

[063-090] he shrank back and dared not raise his eyes to the fatal edifice.

[063-091] “Oh!” he said, in a low voice, “is it really true that such a

[063-092] thing took place here, to-day, this very morning?”

[063-093] Still, he ventured to glance at the church. The front was sombre;

[063-094] the sky behind was glittering with stars. The crescent of the

[063-095] moon, in her flight upward from the horizon, had paused at the

[063-096] moment, on the summit of the light hand tower, and seemed to have

[063-097] perched itself, like a luminous bird, on the edge of the

[063-098] balustrade, cut out in black trefoils.

[063-099] The cloister door was shut; but the archdeacon always carried

[063-100] with him the key of the tower in which his laboratory was

[063-101] situated. He made use of it to enter the church.

[063-102] In the church he found the gloom and silence of a cavern. By the

[063-103] deep shadows which fell in broad sheets from all directions, he

[063-104] recognized the fact that the hangings for the ceremony of the

[063-105] morning had not yet been removed. The great silver cross shone

[063-106] from the depths of the gloom, powdered with some sparkling

[063-107] points, like the milky way of that sepulchral night. The long

[063-108] windows of the choir showed the upper extremities of their arches

[063-109] above the black draperies, and their painted panes, traversed by

[063-110] a ray of moonlight had no longer any hues but the doubtful colors

[063-111] of night, a sort of violet, white and blue, whose tint is found

[063-112] only on the faces of the dead. The archdeacon, on perceiving

[063-113] these wan spots all around the choir, thought he beheld the

[063-114] mitres of damned bishops. He shut his eyes, and when he opened

[063-115] them again, he thought they were a circle of pale visages gazing

[063-116] at him.

[063-117] He started to flee across the church. Then it seemed to him that

[063-118] the church also was shaking, moving, becoming endued with

[063-119] animation, that it was alive; that each of the great columns was

[063-120] turning into an enormous paw, which was beating the earth with

[063-121] its big stone spatula, and that the gigantic cathedral was no

[063-122] longer anything but a sort of prodigious elephant, which was

[063-123] breathing and marching with its pillars for feet, its two towers

[063-124] for trunks and the immense black cloth for its housings.

[063-125] This fever or madness had reached such a degree of intensity that

[063-126] the external world was no longer anything more for the unhappy

[063-127] man than a sort of Apocalypse,—visible, palpable, terrible.

[063-128] For one moment, he was relieved. As he plunged into the side

[063-129] aisles, he perceived a reddish light behind a cluster of pillars.

[063-130] He ran towards it as to a star. It was the poor lamp which

[063-131] lighted the public breviary of Notre-Dame night and day, beneath

[063-132] its iron grating. He flung himself eagerly upon the holy book in

[063-133] the hope of finding some consolation, or some encouragement

[063-134] there. The hook lay open at this passage of Job, over which his

[063-135] staring eye glanced,—

[063-136] “And a spirit passed before my face, and I heard a small voice,

[063-137] and the hair of my flesh stood up.”

[063-138] On reading these gloomy words, he felt that which a blind man

[063-139] feels when he feels himself pricked by the staff which he has

[063-140] picked up. His knees gave way beneath him, and he sank upon the

[063-141] pavement, thinking of her who had died that day. He felt so many

[063-142] monstrous vapors pass and discharge themselves in his brain, that

[063-143] it seemed to him that his head had become one of the chimneys of

[063-144] hell.

[063-145] It would appear that he remained a long time in this attitude, no

[063-146] longer thinking, overwhelmed and passive beneath the hand of the

[063-147] demon. At length some strength returned to him; it occurred to

[063-148] him to take refuge in his tower beside his faithful Quasimodo. He

[063-149] rose; and, as he was afraid, he took the lamp from the breviary

[063-150] to light his way. It was a sacrilege; but he had got beyond

[063-151] heeding such a trifle now.

[063-152] He slowly climbed the stairs of the towers, filled with a secret

[063-153] fright which must have been communicated to the rare passers-by

[063-154] in the Place du Parvis by the mysterious light of his lamp,

[063-155] mounting so late from loophole to loophole of the bell tower.

[063-156] All at once, he felt a freshness on his face, and found himself

[063-157] at the door of the highest gallery. The air was cold; the sky was

[063-158] filled with hurrying clouds, whose large, white flakes drifted

[063-159] one upon another like the breaking up of river ice after the

[063-160] winter. The crescent of the moon, stranded in the midst of the

[063-161] clouds, seemed a celestial vessel caught in the ice-cakes of the

[063-162] air.

[063-163] He lowered his gaze, and contemplated for a moment, through the

[063-164] railing of slender columns which unites the two towers, far away,

[063-165] through a gauze of mists and smoke, the silent throng of the

[063-166] roofs of Paris, pointed, innumerable, crowded and small like the

[063-167] waves of a tranquil sea on a sum-mer night.

[063-168] The moon cast a feeble ray, which imparted to earth and heaven an

[063-169] ashy hue.

[063-170] At that moment the clock raised its shrill, cracked voice.

[063-171] Midnight rang out. The priest thought of midday; twelve o’clock

[063-172] had come back again.

[063-173] “Oh!” he said in a very low tone, “she must be cold now.”

[063-174] All at once, a gust of wind extinguished his lamp, and almost at

[063-175] the same instant, he beheld a shade, a whiteness, a form, a

[063-176] woman, appear from the opposite angle of the tower. He started.

[063-177] Beside this woman was a little goat, which mingled its bleat with

[063-178] the last bleat of the clock.

[063-179] He had strength enough to look. It was she.

[063-180] She was pale, she was gloomy. Her hair fell over her shoulders as

[063-181] in the morning; but there was no longer a rope on her neck, her

[063-182] hands were no longer bound; she was free, she was dead.

[063-183] She was dressed in white and had a white veil on her head.

[063-184] She came towards him, slowly, with her gaze fixed on the sky. The

[063-185] supernatural goat followed her. He felt as though made of stone

[063-186] and too heavy to flee. At every step which she took in advance,

[063-187] he took one backwards, and that was all. In this way he retreated

[063-188] once more beneath the gloomy arch of the stairway. He was chilled

[063-189] by the thought that she might enter there also; had she done so,

[063-190] he would have died of terror.

[063-191] She did arrive, in fact, in front of the door to the stairway,

[063-192] and paused there for several minutes, stared intently into the

[063-193] darkness, but without appearing to see the priest, and passed on.

[063-194] She seemed taller to him than when she had been alive; he saw the

[063-195] moon through her white robe; he heard her breath.

[063-196] When she had passed on, he began to descend the staircase again,

[063-197] with the slowness which he had observed in the spectre, believing

[063-198] himself to be a spectre too, haggard, with hair on end, his

[063-199] extinguished lamp still in his hand; and as he descended the

[063-200] spiral steps, he distinctly heard in his ear a voice laughing and

[063-201] repeating,—
