---[016-001] “Death of the devil!” objected Gringoire; “I shall break my neck.

[016-002] Your stool limps like one of Martial’s distiches; it has one

[016-003] hexameter leg and one pentameter leg.”

[016-004] “Climb!” repeated Clopin.

[016-005] Gringoire mounted the stool, and succeeded, not without some

[016-006] oscillations of head and arms, in regaining his centre of

[016-007] gravity.

[016-008] “Now,” went on the King of Thunes, “twist your right foot round

[016-009] your left leg, and rise on the tip of your left foot.”

[016-010] “Monseigneur,” said Gringoire, “so you absolutely insist on my

[016-011] breaking some one of my limbs?”

[016-012] Clopin tossed his head.

[016-013] “Hark ye, my friend, you talk too much. Here’s the gist of the

[016-014] matter in two words: you are to rise on tiptoe, as I tell you; in

[016-015] that way you will be able to reach the pocket of the manikin, you

[016-016] will rummage it, you will pull out the purse that is there,—and

[016-017] if you do all this without our hearing the sound of a bell, all

[016-018] is well: you shall be a vagabond. All we shall then have to do,

[016-019] will be to thrash you soundly for the space of a week.”

[016-020] “_Ventre-Dieu_! I will be careful,” said Gringoire. “And suppose

[016-021] I do make the bells sound?”

[016-022] “Then you will be hanged. Do you understand?”

[016-023] “I don’t understand at all,” replied Gringoire.

[016-024] “Listen, once more. You are to search the manikin, and take away

[016-025] its purse; if a single bell stirs during the operation, you will

[016-026] be hung. Do you understand that?”

[016-027] “Good,” said Gringoire; “I understand that. And then?”

[016-028] “If you succeed in removing the purse without our hearing the

[016-029] bells, you are a vagabond, and you will be thrashed for eight

[016-030] consecutive days. You understand now, no doubt?”

[016-031] “No, monseigneur; I no longer understand. Where is the advantage

[016-032] to me? hanged in one case, cudgelled in the other?”

[016-033] “And a vagabond,” resumed Clopin, “and a vagabond; is that

[016-034] nothing? It is for your interest that we should beat you, in

[016-035] order to harden you to blows.”

[016-036] “Many thanks,” replied the poet.

[016-037] “Come, make haste,” said the king, stamping upon his cask, which

[016-038] resounded like a huge drum! “Search the manikin, and let there be

[016-039] an end to this! I warn you for the last time, that if I hear a

[016-040] single bell, you will take the place of the manikin.”

[016-041] The band of thieves applauded Clopin’s words, and arranged

[016-042] themselves in a circle round the gibbet, with a laugh so pitiless

[016-043] that Gringoire perceived that he amused them too much not to have

[016-044] everything to fear from them. No hope was left for him,

[016-045] accordingly, unless it were the slight chance of succeeding in

[016-046] the formidable operation which was imposed upon him; he decided

[016-047] to risk it, but it was not without first having addressed a

[016-048] fervent prayer to the manikin he was about to plunder, and who

[016-049] would have been easier to move to pity than the vagabonds. These

[016-050] myriad bells, with their little copper tongues, seemed to him

[016-051] like the mouths of so many asps, open and ready to sting and to

[016-052] hiss.

[016-053] “Oh!” he said, in a very low voice, “is it possible that my life

[016-054] depends on the slightest vibration of the least of these bells?

[016-055] Oh!” he added, with clasped hands, “bells, do not ring,

[016-056] hand-bells do not clang, mule-bells do not quiver!”

[016-057] He made one more attempt upon Trouillefou.

[016-058] “And if there should come a gust of wind?”

[016-059] “You will be hanged,” replied the other, without hesitation.

[016-060] Perceiving that no respite, nor reprieve, nor subterfuge was

[016-061] possible, he bravely decided upon his course of action; he wound

[016-062] his right foot round his left leg, raised himself on his left

[016-063] foot, and stretched out his arm: but at the moment when his hand

[016-064] touched the manikin, his body, which was now supported upon one

[016-065] leg only, wavered on the stool which had but three; he made an

[016-066] involuntary effort to support himself by the manikin, lost his

[016-067] balance, and fell heavily to the ground, deafened by the fatal

[016-068] vibration of the thousand bells of the manikin, which, yielding

[016-069] to the impulse imparted by his hand, described first a rotary

[016-070] motion, and then swayed majestically between the two posts.

[016-071] “Malediction!” he cried as he fell, and remained as though dead,

[016-072] with his face to the earth.

[016-073] Meanwhile, he heard the dreadful peal above his head, the

[016-074] diabolical laughter of the vagabonds, and the voice of

[016-075] Trouillefou saying,—

[016-076] “Pick me up that knave, and hang him without ceremony.” He rose.

[016-077] They had already detached the manikin to make room for him.

[016-078] The thieves made him mount the stool, Clopin came to him, passed

[016-079] the rope about his neck, and, tapping him on the shoulder,—

[016-080] “Adieu, my friend. You can’t escape now, even if you digested

[016-081] with the pope’s guts.”

[016-082] The word “Mercy!” died away upon Gringoire’s lips. He cast his

[016-083] eyes about him; but there was no hope: all were laughing.

[016-084] “Bellevigne de l’Étoile,” said the King of Thunes to an enormous

[016-085] vagabond, who stepped out from the ranks, “climb upon the cross

[016-086] beam.”

[016-087] Bellevigne de l’Étoile nimbly mounted the transverse beam, and in

[016-088] another minute, Gringoire, on raising his eyes, beheld him, with

[016-089] terror, seated upon the beam above his head.

[016-090] “Now,” resumed Clopin Trouillefou, “as soon as I clap my hands,

[016-091] you, Andry the Red, will fling the stool to the ground with a

[016-092] blow of your knee; you, François Chanteprune, will cling to the

[016-093] feet of the rascal; and you, Bellevigne, will fling yourself on

[016-094] his shoulders; and all three at once, do you hear?”

[016-095] Gringoire shuddered.

[016-096] “Are you ready?” said Clopin Trouillefou to the three thieves,

[016-097] who held themselves in readiness to fall upon Gringoire. A moment

[016-098] of horrible suspense ensued for the poor victim, during which

[016-099] Clopin tranquilly thrust into the fire with the tip of his foot,

[016-100] some bits of vine shoots which the flame had not caught. “Are you

[016-101] ready?” he repeated, and opened his hands to clap. One second

[016-102] more and all would have been over.

[016-103] But he paused, as though struck by a sudden thought.

[016-104] “One moment!” said he; “I forgot! It is our custom not to hang a

[016-105] man without inquiring whether there is any woman who wants him.

[016-106] Comrade, this is your last resource. You must wed either a female

[016-107] vagabond or the noose.”

[016-108] This law of the vagabonds, singular as it may strike the reader,

[016-109] remains to-day written out at length, in ancient English

[016-110] legislation. (See _Burington’s Observations_.)

[016-111] Gringoire breathed again. This was the second time that he had

[016-112] returned to life within an hour. So he did not dare to trust to

[016-113] it too implicitly.

[016-114] “Holà!” cried Clopin, mounted once more upon his cask, “holà!

[016-115] women, females, is there among you, from the sorceress to her

[016-116] cat, a wench who wants this rascal? Holà, Colette la Charonne!

[016-117] Elisabeth Trouvain! Simone Jodouyne! Marie Piédebou! Thonne la

[016-118] Longue! Bérarde Fanouel! Michelle Genaille! Claude Ronge-oreille!

[016-119] Mathurine Girorou!—Holà! Isabeau-la-Thierrye! Come and see! A man

[016-120] for nothing! Who wants him?”

[016-121] Gringoire, no doubt, was not very appetizing in this miserable

[016-122] condition. The female vagabonds did not seem to be much affected

[016-123] by the proposition. The unhappy wretch heard them answer: “No!

[016-124] no! hang him; there’ll be the more fun for us all!”

[016-125] Nevertheless, three emerged from the throng and came to smell of

[016-126] him. The first was a big wench, with a square face. She examined

[016-127] the philosopher’s deplorable doublet attentively. His garment was

[016-128] worn, and more full of holes than a stove for roasting chestnuts.

[016-129] The girl made a wry face. “Old rag!” she muttered, and addressing

[016-130] Gringoire, “Let’s see your cloak!” “I have lost it,” replied

[016-131] Gringoire. “Your hat?” “They took it away from me.” “Your shoes?”

[016-132] “They have hardly any soles left.” “Your purse?” “Alas!”

[016-133] stammered Gringoire, “I have not even a sou.” “Let them hang you,

[016-134] then, and say ‘Thank you!’” retorted the vagabond wench, turning

[016-135] her back on him.

[016-136] The second,—old, black, wrinkled, hideous, with an ugliness

[016-137] conspicuous even in the Cour des Miracles, trotted round

[016-138] Gringoire. He almost trembled lest she should want him. But she

[016-139] mumbled between her teeth, “He’s too thin,” and went off.

[016-140] The third was a young girl, quite fresh, and not too ugly. “Save

[016-141] me!” said the poor fellow to her, in a low tone. She gazed at him

[016-142] for a moment with an air of pity, then dropped her eyes, made a

[016-143] plait in her petticoat, and remained in indecision. He followed

[016-144] all these movements with his eyes; it was the last gleam of hope.

[016-145] “No,” said the young girl, at length, “no! Guillaume Longuejoue

[016-146] would beat me.” She retreated into the crowd.

[016-147] “You are unlucky, comrade,” said Clopin.

[016-148] Then rising to his feet, upon his hogshead. “No one wants him,”

[016-149] he exclaimed, imitating the accent of an auctioneer, to the great

[016-150] delight of all; “no one wants him? once, twice, three times!”

[016-151] and, turning towards the gibbet with a sign of his hand, “Gone!”

[016-152] Bellevigne de l’Étoile, Andry the Red, François Chanteprune,

[016-153] stepped up to Gringoire.

[016-154] At that moment a cry arose among the thieves: “_La Esmeralda! La

[016-155] Esmeralda!_”

[016-156] Gringoire shuddered, and turned towards the side whence the

[016-157] clamor proceeded.

[016-158] The crowd opened, and gave passage to a pure and dazzling form.

[016-159] It was the gypsy.

[016-160] “La Esmeralda!” said Gringoire, stupefied in the midst of his

[016-161] emotions, by the abrupt manner in which that magic word knotted

[016-162] together all his reminiscences of the day.

[016-163] This rare creature seemed, even in the Cour des Miracles, to

[016-164] exercise her sway of charm and beauty. The vagabonds, male and

[016-165] female, ranged themselves gently along her path, and their brutal

[016-166] faces beamed beneath her glance.

[016-167] She approached the victim with her light step. Her pretty Djali

[016-168] followed her. Gringoire was more dead than alive. She examined

[016-169] him for a moment in silence.

[016-170] “You are going to hang this man?” she said gravely, to Clopin.

[016-171] “Yes, sister,” replied the King of Thunes, “unless you will take

[016-172] him for your husband.”

[016-173] She made her pretty little pout with her under lip. “I’ll take

[016-174] him,” said she.

[016-175] Gringoire firmly believed that he had been in a dream ever since

[016-176] morning, and that this was the continuation of it.

[016-177] The change was, in fact, violent, though a gratifying one. They

[016-178] undid the noose, and made the poet step down from the stool. His

[016-179] emotion was so lively that he was obliged to sit down.

[016-180] The Duke of Egypt brought an earthenware crock, without uttering

[016-181] a word. The gypsy offered it to Gringoire: “Fling it on the

[016-182] ground,” said she.

[016-183] The crock broke into four pieces.

[016-184] “Brother,” then said the Duke of Egypt, laying his hands upon

[016-185] their foreheads, “she is your wife; sister, he is your husband

[016-186] for four years. Go.”

[016-187] CHAPTER VII. A BRIDAL NIGHT.

[016-188] A few moments later our poet found himself in a tiny arched

[016-189] chamber, very cosy, very warm, seated at a table which appeared

[016-190] to ask nothing better than to make some loans from a larder

[016-191] hanging near by, having a good bed in prospect, and alone with a

[016-192] pretty girl. The adventure smacked of enchantment. He began

[016-193] seriously to take himself for a personage in a fairy tale; he

[016-194] cast his eyes about him from time to time to time, as though to

[016-195] see if the chariot of fire, harnessed to two-winged chimeras,

[016-196] which alone could have so rapidly transported him from Tartarus

[016-197] to Paradise, were still there. At times, also, he fixed his eyes

[016-198] obstinately upon the holes in his doublet, in order to cling to

[016-199] reality, and not lose the ground from under his feet completely.

[016-200] His reason, tossed about in imaginary space, now hung only by

[016-201] this thread.

[016-202] The young girl did not appear to pay any attention to him; she

[016-203] went and came, displaced a stool, talked to her goat, and

[016-204] indulged in a pout now and then. At last she came and seated

[016-205] herself near the table, and Gringoire was able to scrutinize her

[016-206] at his ease.
