# Im-Hanna

## Role in the Book

Im-Hanna (Arabic: "mother of Hanna") is a Syrian peddleress who shares a cellar dwelling with [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] in [[new-york-city|New York]]. She represents the domestic and communal dimension of immigrant life — the informal support networks that sustain individuals through poverty and dislocation. In a narrative dominated by male intellectual and sensual striving, Im-Hanna provides a grounding presence of maternal care and practical wisdom.

The narrator describes her with unusual tenderness: "her hair was as white as a dove's wing" (007-023). She is "affectionate, devoted as our mothers" (007-027) — a figure who transcends mere character to become emblematic of the nurturing immigrant woman who holds community together while men pursue their ambitions.

## Biographical Sketch

Im-Hanna is a peddleress — part of the network of Syrian immigrant women who sold goods door-to-door in [[new-york-city|America]]n cities. She occupies the "cellar" that [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] share, and her presence transforms a squalid space into something approaching a home. Her relationship to [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] is maternal rather than romantic — she represents the care and stability they have left behind in Lebanon.
