## Overview

The Committee of Union and Progress (CUP), commonly known in English as the Young Turks, was a constitutional reform movement in the late Ottoman Empire. Founded in 1889 and driven underground by Sultan Abd'ul Hamid II's secret police, the CUP advocated for the restoration of the 1876 constitution and the modernization of Ottoman governance. By the time Rihani wrote *The Book of Khalid* (1911), the Young Turks had forced the Sultan to restore the constitution in the 1908 Revolution — a seismic event in the Ottoman world that forms part of the book's political subtext.

## Role in the Book

The Young Turks appear in *The Book of Khalid* as a shadowy political force — referenced directly only twice [004-011, 004-021] but implied in the broader political awakening that shapes Khalid's trajectory. Khalid's early exposure to political meetings in New York's Syrian district [009-007, 009-008], his apprenticeship as a Tammany Hall political canvasser [014-006], and his eventual vision of delivering a reformist address before a "Turkish Areopagus at Constantinople" [034-006] all trace an arc that parallels the CUP's own reformist trajectory.

The direct references are characteristically satirical. When Khalid goes into hiding, a companion quips: "The Young Turks can not catch him now" [004-011]. The joke works on two levels — the CUP's secret police were feared, yet their reach is comically inadequate. Later, Rihani reflects that Khalid is caught "in the trap of the Young Turks" [004-021], suggesting that even in America, Ottoman political currents ensnare the emigrant.

## Key Passages

> "The Young Turks can not catch him now."
>
> — [004-011]

This throwaway line, spoken as Khalid disappears from Baalbek, registers the Young Turks as a known political menace — a force of surveillance that even remote Mount Lebanon feels.

> "And I would have stood in the Turkish Areopagus at Constantinople, defended himself somewhat Socratically, and delivered my message to the Ottoman nation."
>
> — [034-006]

Khalid imagines himself as a reformer addressing the Ottoman political class in its capital. The "Areopagus" metaphor — the Athenian council — casts Constantinople as both a seat of judgment and a potential site of enlightenment.

## Historical Context

The CUP originated among Ottoman military officers and civil servants educated in Western-style academies. After the 1908 Constitutional Revolution, they governed the Empire through its final decade — the Balkan Wars, World War I, and the Armenian genocide. Rihani, writing in 1911, captures them at their apex of reformist promise, before the darker chapters of CUP rule had unfolded. His ambivalent portrait — the Young Turks as both a trap and a natural destination for Khalid's message — reflects the uncertainty with which Arab intellectuals viewed the centralizing, Turkifying tendencies of post-1908 Ottoman governance.
