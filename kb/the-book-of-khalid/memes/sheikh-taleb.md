## Introduction

Sheikh Taleb of Damascus is a minor but symbolically potent character in Ameen Rihani's *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. Introduced to [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] by [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] in Book the Second, he represents an authentic, unmediated form of Eastern wisdom — a stark contrast to both the institutional orthodoxy of the ulema and the commercialized spirituality that [[khalid|Khalid]] encounters in [[new-york-city|New York]]. The Sheikh's ascetic lifestyle, consuming only oranges and preserving their peels for kindling, serves as Rihani's portrait of the philosopher who has renounced all attachment to worldly opinion.

## Character Portrait

[[shakib|Shakib]] introduces the Sheikh to readers in a passage of cumulative rhetorical force, piling attribute upon attribute to build a portrait of radical independence:

> He is a man who lives after his own mind and in his own robes; an Arab who prays after no Imam, but directly to Allah and his Apostle; a scholar who has more dryasdust knowledge on his finger ends than all the ulema of Cairo and Damascus; a philosopher who would not give an orange peel for the opinion of the world; an ascetic who flees celebrity as he would the plague; a sage who does not disdain to be a pedagogue; an eccentric withal to amuse even a Diogenes:—this is the noted Sheikh Taleb of Damascus, whom Mrs. Gotfry once met at Abbas Effendi's in Akka, and whom she was desirous of meeting again. (032-031)

The comparison to [[diogenes|Diogenes]] is striking: like the Cynic philosopher of ancient Greece, Sheikh Taleb lives in deliberate simplicity, uses his staff to strike those who would praise him publicly, and regards all social convention as dispensable. His "grey beard" and "sharp blue eyes" — animated when he speaks, "agitated like his spirit" (033-006) — suggest a man whose physical form barely contains his intellectual energy.

## The Orange-Peel Ascetic

Rihani uses the Sheikh's dietary habits as a metaphor for his philosophical stance. The narrator reports — with mock apology — that the Sheikh "lives on oranges, and preserves the peels for kindling the fire" (032-049). It is an existence stripped to the barest necessities: fruit for sustenance, rind for warmth. This is not poverty born of misfortune but austerity chosen as a philosophical position, a deliberate rejection of the consumer comforts that [[khalid|Khalid]] finds so seductive in [[new-york-city|America]].

The Sheikh cannot even tolerate the noise of running water: "the Sheikh can not tolerate the noise of the water; and so, suffering from thirst and neglect, the fountain goes to ruin" (032-032). This detail — the ruined fountain — serves as an emblem of how the Sheikh's radical simplicity transforms his environment, the water feature abandoned because its sound disturbs contemplation.

## Wisdom Without Imams

The Sheikh represents a mode of religious authority that operates entirely outside institutional structures. He "prays after no Imam, but directly to Allah and his Apostle" (032-031), bypassing the clerical hierarchy that the ulema embody. His knowledge surpasses "all the ulema of [[cairo|Cairo]] and [[damascus|Damascus]]" yet he holds no official position, teaches no formal students, and conducts no public rituals.

When [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] asks for his opinion of Wahhabism, the narrative pivots away from his answer to describe his character instead — implying that his specific doctrinal views matter less than his mode of being, which is itself a critique of all organized religious movements. The Sheikh's authority derives not from institutional affiliation but from lived authenticity.

## The Admonition to Khalid

Sheikh Taleb's narrative function crystallizes in Chapter 033, where he visits [[khalid|Khalid]] not to return a social call but to deliver a warning: "The Sheikh is come to admonish [[khalid|Khalid]], not to return his visit" (033-004). He counsels [[khalid|Khalid]] against speaking at the Mosque that day — "It's a dog's tail, Sheikh [[khalid|Khalid]]" (033-005) — expressing a deep skepticism toward public platforms and institutional religion.

[[khalid|Khalid]], as "Our Scribe" notes, is persuaded that "Sheikh Taleb spoke as a friend" (033-006). The Sheikh's authority is relational rather than hierarchical; he does not command but counsels, and his counsel is received precisely because it asks for nothing in return.

## The Final Invocation

The Sheikh's role in the novel is bookended by his appearance in the closing chapter, where the narrator — in the book's final words — invokes him as the presiding spirit of the entire enterprise:

> For our part, we repeat the farewell words of Sheikh Taleb of Damascus: "Judge us not severely." And if we did not study to entertain thee as other Scribes do, it is because we consider thee, dear good Reader, above such entertainment as our poor resources can furnish, Wassalmu aleik! (036-034)

By giving the novel's last word to Sheikh Taleb — or rather, to the narrator repeating the Sheikh's words — Rihani positions the [[damascus|Damascus]] sage as the ethical lodestar of the entire work. The appeal for merciful judgment, the refusal of conventional entertainment, the Arabic valediction: all distill the Sheikh's philosophy into a single gesture that simultaneously closes the book and opens it outward toward the reader.
