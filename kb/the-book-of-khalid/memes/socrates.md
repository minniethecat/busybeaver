## Role in the Book

Socrates functions in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as the Western archetype of the philosopher who teaches through being rather than through doctrine. He is mentioned alongside Eastern spiritual exemplars (al-Farid, Jalal ad-Din Rumi) as a model for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own mode of loafing-as-philosophy, and alongside Buddha and Christ as one of the rare souls who emerge "from the transparent, odoriferous bosom of a rose of light" (022-007).

Rihani deploys Socrates in two registers: as a positive exemplar of wisdom that requires no institutional validation, and — more ambivalently — as a figure whose frankness about bodily matters the narrator must nervously justify to a "chaste and demure age." This dual treatment reflects the novel's broader negotiation between [[oriental-spirituality|Eastern spirituality]] and Western rationalism.

## Key Passages

**The Philosophy of Loafing**

In the novel's opening chapter, [[shakib|Shakib]] defends [[khalid|Khalid]]'s apparent idleness by aligning it with the great contemplative traditions:

> "When he loafs, he does so in good earnest. Not like the camel-driver there or the camel, but after the manner of the great thinkers and mystics: like al-Farid and Jalal ad-Din Rumi, like Socrates and St. Francis of Assisi, Khalid loafs." (001-027)

Socrates is placed in a lineage that spans Muslim mystics and Christian saints — a characteristically Rihani-esque synthesis that treats the Athenian philosopher as part of a universal tradition of contemplative wisdom.

**The Rose of Light**

In one of the novel's most rhapsodic passages, [[khalid|Khalid]] distinguishes three categories of human souls based on the vessel from which the Me emerges:

> "In the very few among mortals, it emerges out of the iron calyx of a flower of red-hot steel, or flows from the transparent, odoriferous bosom of a rose of light. In the first we have a Caesar, an Alexander, a Napoleon; in the second, a Buddha, a Socrates, a Christ." (022-007)

Socrates here belongs to the highest category — the "rose of light" — alongside Buddha and Christ, figures whose spiritual radiance flows without the violence of the "red-hot steel" that produces conquerors. This is Socrates not as dialectician but as spiritual luminary.

**The Muslim Socrates**

[[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb of Damascus]] is introduced through a comparison that makes Socrates his explicit parallel:

> "A Muslim, like Socrates, who educates not by lesson, but by going about his business." (032-031)

The phrase "educates not by lesson, but by going about his business" is a remarkably precise encapsulation of the Socratic method — and by applying it to a Muslim sage, Rihani suggests that authentic philosophy transcends cultural and religious boundaries. [[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb]] is the Oriental Socrates, just as Socrates was the Athenian Taleb.

**The Apology for Frankness**

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s discussion of procreation draws him into Socratic territory, and the narrator must intervene:

> "Had he not quoted Socrates in that last paragraph, it would have been expunged. No, we are not utterly lost to the fine sense of propriety of this chaste and demure age. But no matter how etiolated and sickly the thought, it regains its colour and health when it breathes the literary air." (022-015)

The narrator's nervous apology — if Socrates said it, it's permissible — reveals the tension between Victorian propriety and the philosophical tradition [[khalid|Khalid]] claims.
