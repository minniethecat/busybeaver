## Overview

The *Diwan al-Mutanabbi* — the collected poems of Abu al-Tayyib Ahmad ibn al-Husayn al-Mutanabbi (915–965 CE) — is the single most-referenced literary work within *The Book of Khalid*. Al-Mutanabbi is widely regarded as the greatest classical Arabic poet, renowned for his soaring panegyrics, martial boast-poems, and philosophical aphorisms. In the novel, a "precious dilapidated copy" [017-006] of his Diwan travels everywhere with Shakib, functioning as a touchstone of Arabic literary heritage and a counterweight to the Western books that Khalid voraciously consumes and destroys.

## References in the Book

The Diwan appears at pivotal moments throughout the narrative. At sea during the emigration voyage, "Shakib takes out his favourite poet al-Mutanabbi, and Khalid, his favourite bottle" [006-004] — a pairing that encapsulates the two companions' temperaments: Shakib's literary devotion versus Khalid's sensual directness.

In New York's Battery Park, Shakib reflects: "the rolling waves of life, the tumbling waves of the sea, and the fiery waves of al-Mutanabbi's poetry have always been my delight" [008-004]. The Diwan here is a portable homeland — Arabic poetry serving as emotional ballast in the immigrant's dislocated life.

Shakib repeatedly tries to draw Khalid into the Diwan's world: "Get thee behind me on this dromedary," he calls, "and come from this desert of barren gold, if but for a day — come out with me to the oasis of poesy" [008-009]. Khalid resists; his motto is "One book at a time" [008-009], and he prefers to burn books after reading them — a practice that horrifies the bibliophilic Shakib.

When preparing to return to Syria, Shakib packs the Diwan with special care: "these he packs in the bottom of his trunk, and with them his precious dilapidated copy of al-Mutanabbi" [017-006], concealing it "to evade the Basilisk touch of the Port officials of Beirut" [017-010]. The Diwan thus becomes contraband — Arabic literary culture must be smuggled past Ottoman censorship.

The physical state of the book — "dilapidated" — signals its constant use and its talismanic role. It is the one book in the novel that is preserved rather than consumed, read rather than burned.

The Diwan and the bottle — poetry and wine — define the two companions. The passage establishes al-Mutanabbi as Shakib's answer to Khalid's materialism.

> "The rolling waves of life, the tumbling waves of the sea, and the fiery waves of al-Mutanabbi's poetry have always been my delight."
>
> — [008-004]

Shakib's tricolon elevates al-Mutanabbi's poetry to the elemental status of sea and life itself. "Fiery waves" captures the martial intensity of al-Mutanabbi's verse — his famous "scimitars and spears" [009-011].

> "These he packs in the bottom of his trunk, and with them his precious dilapidated copy of al-Mutanabbi."
>
> — [017-006]

The Diwan is packed alongside gold jewelry, a Winchester rifle, and diplomatic gifts — a hierarchy of valuables in which poetry holds its own. The adjective "dilapidated" is affectionate: the book is worn from love, not neglect.

> "Ay, in this MS. which Shakib is packing along with al-Mutanabbi in the bottom of his trunk to evade the Basilisk touch of the Port officials of Beirut, is packed all the hopes of the Modern School."
>
> — [017-010]

The Diwan travels alongside Khalid's own manuscript — classical Arabic poetry and modern Arabic reform literature, smuggled together into the Ottoman East.

## Thematic Connection

Al-Mutanabbi represents the classical Arabic tradition that Rihani both honors and seeks to transcend. Shakib's devotion to the Diwan embodies the *nahda* intellectual's relationship to the Arabic literary past: reverent, but also potentially limiting. Khalid's resistance to reading it — and his preference for experiencing life directly rather than through poetry — enacts the tension between tradition and experience that drives the novel.

The Diwan also functions as a material symbol of cultural transmission. It travels from Mount Lebanon to New York and back again, surviving Khalid's book-burning habits, Ottoman censorship, and the general upheaval of emigration. In a novel about translation and cultural mediation, the untranslated Arabic Diwan — "fiery" and untamed — represents what resists translation, what can only be carried, preserved, and loved in its original form.

## Historical Context

Al-Mutanabbi (915–965 CE) was born in Kufa (Iraq) and rose from humble origins to become the court poet of Sayf al-Dawla, the Hamdanid ruler of Aleppo. His poetry — renowned for its technical mastery, philosophical depth, and martial imagery — has been memorized, quoted, and debated across the Arabic-speaking world for over a millennium. His famous line "I am the one whose blind can see my literature / And whose deaf can hear my words" captures the soaring self-confidence that made him both revered and controversial. By the *nahda* period, al-Mutanabbi was a fixture of Arabic education, and every literate Arab — including Shakib — would have encountered his Diwan in childhood.
