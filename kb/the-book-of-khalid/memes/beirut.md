# Beirut

## Location & Geography

Beirut is the principal port city of Ottoman Syria, situated on the eastern Mediterranean coast at the foot of the Lebanon mountains. In Rihani's narrative it serves as the threshold between the old world and the new — the last Levantine soil [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] touch before their Atlantic crossing. The city is described not through its physical landmarks but through its function as a point of transit, a place where emigrants are "huddled like sheep on deck" before departure [006-003].

The port itself appears as a site of both logistical hardship and symbolic transition. The "verminous hostelries of Beirut" [006-007] and its boatmen represent the crude commercial machinery of emigration — indifferent to the dreams of those passing through. Yet Beirut also possesses a certain cosmopolitan craft tradition: the narrator notes that [[khalid|Khalid]]'s shoemaking "would compare well with that of the best craftsman in Beirut" [026-015], suggesting the city's reputation for skilled artisanship.

## Significance in the Book

Beirut functions in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as a liminal space — neither fully the Lebanon of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s childhood nor the [[new-york-city|America]] of his future. It is the site of departure, where the old identity is shed but the new one has not yet formed. The port embodies the economic realities driving the Levantine diaspora: "the Port of Beirut, the verminous hostelries of Beirut" [006-007] are part of the machinery that processes emigrants like cattle.

The city also serves as a narrative anchor for communication and return. After [[khalid|Khalid]] disappears into the Syrian interior, [[shakib|Shakib]] places notices in "one of the newspapers of Beirut, in the hope that [[khalid|Khalid]], if living, might chance to see it" [030-004]. Beirut is where [[najma|Najma]] waits, where letters are exchanged [030-006], and where the possibility of reunion lingers. In the novel's geography, Beirut is the hinge between departure and return, exile and homecoming.

## Key Passages

> "smuggled through the port of Beirut at night, and safely rowed to the steamer"
>
> — [006-002]

This passage captures the clandestine, precarious nature of emigration from Ottoman Syria. [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] do not depart openly but are "smuggled" — their journey begins in secrecy and uncertainty.

> "huddled like sheep on deck from Beirut to Marseilles; and like cattle exported to a foreign market"
>
> — [006-003]

Rihani's animal imagery underscores the dehumanization of the emigrant experience. The passengers are commodities processed through Beirut's port infrastructure, their individual stories subsumed into mass movement.

> "the Port of Beirut, the verminous hostelries of Beirut"
>
> — [006-007]

The repetition of "Beirut" in this litany of emigrant hardships positions the city as a site of suffering — one more station in the via dolorosa of departure, alongside the boatmen and the sharpers of [[marseilles|Marseilles]].

> "And whether in Beirut or in New York, even the morality of the people of the Book is become a commodity"
>
> — [017-004]

This moral observation draws a parallel between Beirut and [[new-york-city|New York]], suggesting that the commercial ethos [[khalid|Khalid]] critiques is not exclusively Western — it flourishes in Eastern ports as well, wherever money-seeking supplants spiritual values.

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, Beirut was the primary port of the Ottoman *vilayet* of Syria, experiencing rapid growth as a commercial and cultural centre. The city's population swelled with migrants from the [[mount-lebanon|Mount Lebanon]] region, particularly after the civil strife of 1860. Beirut's port handled the bulk of Syrian emigration to the [[new-york-city|America]]s — between 1880 and 1914, tens of thousands of Lebanese and Syrian emigrants passed through its docks.

The city was also an intellectual hub of the *Nahda* (Arab Renaissance), with printing presses, newspapers, and literary societies. Rihani's reference to "a booklet published in Beirut, setting forth the history of [[bahaullah|the Baha]]'i religion" [018-035] reflects the city's role as a centre for religious and political publishing, where new ideas circulated alongside traditional ones.
