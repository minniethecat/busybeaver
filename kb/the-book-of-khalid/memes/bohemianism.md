# Bohemianism

## Definition

Bohemianism in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* represents the sphere of artistic and intellectual freedom that [[khalid|Khalid]] enters after leaving conventional employment in [[new-york-city|New York]]. Derived from the 19th-century Western concept of Bohemian counterculture — artists and writers living outside bourgeois norms — Rihani's Bohemia is a space of simultaneous liberation and peril, where "the poison and the antidote are frequently offered together" (011-016).

Unlike [[khalid|the Dervish]] Philosophy, which is [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own Eastern-inflected creation, Bohemianism is a distinctly Western phenomenon that he encounters and navigates. It represents both the promise of [[new-york-city|America]]n freedom and its corruptions: creative fellowship, sensual indulgence, intellectual exchange — and the ever-present risk of losing oneself to pleasure.

## Development in the Book

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s Bohemian period begins in Book I, Chapter VIII, after he leaves the law office and the world of metaphysics. The narrative describes how he would "gallivant in the Parks, attend Bohemian dinners, and frequent the Do... studios of [[new-york-city|New York]]'s artistic underground" (011-005). At a Bohemian Club dinner, [[khalid|Khalid]] meets a circle of artists and free-thinkers — "they dine together at a Bohemian Club that evening, where [[khalid|Khalid]] is introduced to a new circle" (011-009).

It is in Bohemia that [[khalid|Khalid]]'s acquaintance with a certain woman deepens: "he alone accompanies her to Bohemia" (011-012). The Dervish, "who was always ready to explore the Unseen," is drawn into this world. But Rihani's narrator warns: "The aftermath, however, does not justify the venture" (011-015). Bohemia proves to be both inspiration and trap.

The most penetrating insight comes in the famous line: "Fortunately, [[khalid|Khalid]] finds himself in Bohemia where the poison and the antidote are frequently offered together. Here the spell of one sorceress can straightway be offset by that of her sister" (011-016). Bohemianism is a self-correcting ecosystem of temptation — each excess finds its counter. Yet this very quality makes it a place of perpetual instability, where [[khalid|Khalid]]'s "immanent morality and intellectualism suffered an interregnum" (011-017).

By Book II, the Bohemian period has ended in disillusionment. [[khalid|Khalid]] moves from "the Table of Bohemia to the Stump in [[tammany-hall|Tammany Land]]" (014-008) — from artistic freedom to political corruption — and the narrator asks: "Surely, from the Table of Bohemia to the Stump in [[tammany-hall|Tammany Land]], is a far cry. But believe us, O [[khalid|Khalid]], you will wish you were again in the gardens of Proserpine, when the silence and darkness extinguish the torch and the drum."
