## Role in the Book

Thomas Carlyle is the Western writer whose influence on [[khalid|Khalid]] runs deepest into the novel's plot. Two of Carlyle's works — *Hero-Worship* and the *Latter-Day Pamphlets* — function as structural devices in the narrative: the former as the book [[khalid|Khalid]] repeatedly reads and finally burns in his cellar, the latter as the incendiary text that the Jesuits misattribute to [[khalid|Khalid]], triggering his persecution.

Carlyle represents, for Rihani, both the prophetic strain in Western letters and its limitations. [[khalid|Khalid]] reveres him as a "Master," yet also notes that even Carlyle "can be longwinded and shortsighted on occasions" (025-028). The relationship is one of creative discipleship: [[khalid|Khalid]] learns from Carlyle's thunderous prose while rejecting his Eurocentric assumption that the French Revolution was humanity's final judgment on truth and falsehood.

## Key Passages

**The Cellar Discovery**

Jerry the book-vendor leads [[khalid|Khalid]] into the underground labyrinth of secondhand books where Carlyle's works molders among the literary detritus:

> Here, too, is Shakespeare in lamentable state; there is Carlyle in rags, still crying, as it were, against the filth and beastliness of this underworld. (004-018)

The image of Carlyle "in rags, still crying" against the degradation of his surroundings is both a literal description of a decaying book and a metaphor for the prophet's voice persisting amid the "filth and beastliness" of modern commerce.

**The Jesuitism Pamphlet**

The novel's most plot-significant engagement with Carlyle concerns his *Latter-Day Pamphlet* on Jesuitism. When a [[beirut|Beirut]] publisher circulates a mutilated Arabic translation, [[khalid|Khalid]] identifies the original source:

> Khalid having received a copy of this booklet from a friend in Beirut, reads it and writes back, saying that it is not a translation but a mutilation, rather, of one of Thomas Carlyle's Latter-Day Pamphlets entitled *Jesuitism*. (007-032)

[[the-jesuits|The Jesuits]] retaliate by attributing the pamphlet to [[khalid|Khalid]] himself, launching the campaign that leads to his imprisonment. Carlyle's text thus functions as the unwitting catalyst for the novel's central crisis.

**The Burning**

When [[khalid|Khalid]] resolves to leave his cellar and return to Lebanon, the last two books he owns — [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s *Emile* and Carlyle's *Hero-Worship* — become fuel for his stove:

> He finds Khalid sitting near the stove — we are now in the last month of Winter — warming his hands on the flames of the two last books he read. *Emile* and *Hero-Worship* go the way of all the rest. (005-017)

The burning of *Hero-Worship* is the novel's most ambiguous symbolic act: it is simultaneously an homage ([[khalid|Khalid]] has internalized Carlyle's hero-idea so thoroughly he no longer needs the physical book) and a rejection (the European prophet's fire must be consumed to make way for the Oriental prophet's emergence).

**The Critique**

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s mature engagement with Carlyle comes in Book the Third, where he respectfully challenges his master's Eurocentrism:

> "Even Carlyle can be longwinded and shortsighted on occasions... The world is not Europe, and the final decision on Who Is and What Is To Rule, was not delivered by the French Revolution. The Orient, the land of origination and prophecy, must yet solve for itself this eternal problem of the Old and New, the False and True." (025-028)

This is [[khalid|Khalid]] in his most characteristic mode: honoring his intellectual precursors while insisting that the East must generate its own solutions, not merely import European ones.
