# Oriental Spirituality

## Definition

Oriental Spirituality is the concept in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* that encapsulates the distinctive spiritual character of the East — a compound of "continence, purity of heart, fidelity, simplicity, a sense of true manhood, magnanimity of spirit, a healthiness of body and mind" (031-005). It stands as the antipode to Western materialism in Rihani's central dialectic: the grafting of "the strenuosity of Europe and [[new-york-city|America]] upon the ease of the Orient, the materialism of the West upon the spirituality of the East" (028-002).

Unlike organized religion, which Rihani critiques as encrusted with superstition, Oriental Spirituality represents an inherent, almost prelapsarian virtue — the "beautiful ancient virtues" that are "the supreme truths of the Books of Revelation" (031-005). Yet Rihani is not a simple nativist: he recognizes that this spirituality has become buried beneath "thick, obscene growths" and "cactus hedges and thistle-fields" of institutionalized religion and social decay.

## Development in the Book

The concept emerges gradually across the three Books. In the opening Al-Fatihah, the narrator establishes the Oriental lens: "Orientals seldom adventure into that region of fancy and fabrication so alluring to European and [[new-york-city|America]]n writers" (001-004). The book is framed not as a Western-style narrative but as "the chart and history of one little [[souls-journey|kingdom of the Soul]]."

In Book I, [[khalid|Khalid]]'s experience as an immigrant in [[new-york-city|New York]] reveals the Oriental instinct as something he both carries and struggles against. We are told that "he is gradually reverting to the Oriental instinct" (008-012) — loafing, dreaming, burrowing in bookshops — as if the Eastern disposition toward contemplation reasserts itself against the "strenuous spirit" of [[new-york-city|America]].

The concept reaches its fullest articulation in Book III, where [[khalid|Khalid]] returns to the East and becomes a prophet of reform. In [[damascus|Damascus]], he delivers his manifesto: the "lofty spirituality of the Orient" consists of ancient virtues now overgrown by institutional corruption (031-005). His famous metaphor crystallizes the theme: "to graft the strenuosity of Europe and [[new-york-city|America]] upon the ease of the Orient, the materialism of the West upon the spirituality of the East" (028-002). Yet Rihani adds the sober caveat: "no two opposing elements meet and fuse without both losing their original identity."

In the final chapters, the duality deepens. [[khalid|Khalid]] encounters a Western woman ([[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]) who embodies Bahaism — a religion of Eastern origin — and through her, the spirituality of the Orient is reflected back through Western eyes. The "lofty spirituality of the Bab" (030-010) is noted, but also its foreignness to the Syrian context.

## Key Passages

> "To graft the strenuosity of Europe and America upon the ease of the Orient, the materialism of the West upon the spirituality of the East — this to us seems to be the principal aim of Khalid. But often in his wanderings and divagations of thought does he give us fresh proof of the truism that no two opposing elements meet and fuse without both losing their original identity."
>
> — (028-002)

> "Continence, purity of heart, fidelity, simplicity, a sense of true manhood, magnanimity of spirit, a healthiness of body and mind — these are the beautiful ancient virtues. These are the supreme truths of the Books of Revelation: in these consists the lofty spirituality of the Orient. But through what thick, obscene growths we must pass today, through what cactus hedges and thistle-fields we must penetrate, before we rise again to those heights."
>
> — (031-005)

> "I am for a reformation by emigration. And quietly, peacefully, this can be done. Nor fire, nor sword bring I: only this I say: Will and do; resolve and act upon your resolution. The emigration of the mind before the revolution of the state, my Brothers."
>
> — (031-007)
