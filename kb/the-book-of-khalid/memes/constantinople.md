# Constantinople

## Location & Geography

Constantinople (present-day Istanbul) is the historic imperial capital straddling the Bosphorus strait between Europe and Asia. In [[the-book-of-khalid-work|Rihani's novel]] — set in the late Ottoman period under Sultan [[abdul-hamid|Abdul Hamid II]] — the city represents the distant centre of imperial authority: the seat of [[abdul-hamid|the Sultan]], the Sublime Porte, and the political machinery that governs the distant Lebanese provinces from which [[khalid|Khalid]] has fled. Unlike [[baalbek|Baalbek]], [[beirut|Beirut]], or [[new-york-city|New York]] — places [[khalid|Khalid]] physically inhabits — Constantinople exists in the narrative exclusively as a destination _not_ reached, a trial _not_ undergone, a fate _not_ fulfilled.

## Significance in the Book

Constantinople appears only in the third book, during the crisis of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s imprisonment and impending deportation. It functions as a shadow-capital of consequence: the place where political prisoners are sent, where imperial justice (or its parody) is dispensed, and where [[khalid|Khalid]] imagines himself making a Socratic stand. The city crystallises Rihani's critique of Hamidian autocracy — it is the nerve centre of the Ottoman system from which [[khalid|Khalid]] has twice fled (from [[baalbek|Baalbek]] and from [[beirut|Beirut]]) and which, in the novel's final movement, threatens to reclaim him.

Three distinct valences of Constantinople emerge from the text. First, as a **threat of deportation**: the Vali's undelivered message would send [[khalid|Khalid]] "on your way to Constantinople" [033-033], the imperial destination for political undesirables. Second, as a **missed martyrdom**: [[khalid|Khalid]] imagines himself standing "in the Turkish Areopagus at Constantinople, defended himself somewhat Socratic before his judges, and hung out his tung on a rickety gibbet in the neighborhood of St. Sophia" [034-006] — a fantasy of philosophical martyrdom that [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]], by persuading him to flee, "cheated him of." Third, as a **cosmopolitan comparator**: "angels of Equality, whether in Constantinople, in Berlin, in [[paris|Paris]], in London, or in [[new-york-city|New York]]" [035-003] — the city takes its place in Rihani's catalogue of world capitals where revolutionary idealism reveals its hypocrisies.

## Key Passages

> "Had I delivered this to the *Vali*, you would have been now in the hands of the police, and tomorrow on your way to Constantinople."
>
> — [033-033]

The Vali's undelivered message makes Constantinople the terminal point of imperial justice — a destination synonymous with punishment. The journey "to Constantinople" is a one-way deportation, the Empire's final word on [[khalid|Khalid]]'s seditious activities. That the message is withheld (by the speaker's own choice) makes Constantinople a fate suspended rather than suffered — a ghost-sentence that haunts the remainder of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s [[new-york-city|America]]n sojourn.

> "He would have stood in the Turkish Areopagus at Constantinople, defended himself somewhat Socratic before his judges, and hung out his tung on a rickety gibbet in the neighborhood of St. Sophia. But Mrs. Gotfry spoiled his great chance. She cheated him of the glory of dying for a noble cause."
>
> — [034-006]

This passage is the imaginative core of Constantinople in the novel — [[khalid|Khalid]]'s self-dramatisation as a Socratic martyr before Ottoman judges. The "Turkish Areopagus" is Rihani's ironic conflation of classical Athens and Hamidian Istanbul, casting [[khalid|Khalid]] as both philosopher-defendant and would-be hero. The "rickety gibbet in the neighborhood of St. Sophia" is a masterstroke of bathos: the sublime architecture of Byzantium juxtaposed with the shabby apparatus of Ottoman execution. [[shakib|Shakib]]'s narrative voice is sardonic — [[khalid|Khalid]]'s "great chance" for martyrdom is undercut by the comic reality that [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]], the practical [[new-york-city|America]]n woman, simply told him to run. The passage distills Rihani's ambivalence about heroism: is [[khalid|Khalid]]'s flight cowardice or wisdom? Is Constantinople a missed destiny or a bullet dodged?

> "For does not every one of these furious angels of Equality, whether in Constantinople, in Berlin, in Paris, in London, or in New York, sit on his wings and reveal his horns when he rises to power?"
>
> — [035-003]

In this late passage from the "Desert" chapter, Constantinople joins the roster of world capitals indicted for revolutionary hypocrisy. The city is no longer the specific destination of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s deportation but a metonym for imperial power as such — one node in a transnational network where "angels of Equality" transform into horned tyrants the moment they seize authority. The passage universalises the critique: Hamidian Constantinople is not uniquely corrupt but representative of a global pattern.

## Historical Context

At the time of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s narrative (c. 1890s–1900s), Constantinople was the capital of the Ottoman Empire under Sultan [[abdul-hamid|Abdul Hamid II]] (r. 1876–1909). The Hamidian era was characterised by authoritarian centralisation, widespread use of internal exile for political dissidents, and the *jurnal* (denunciation) system that Rihani satirises through the Vali's undelivered message. The "Turkish Areopagus" is a fictional construct but gestures toward the extraordinary tribunals that tried political offenders. Rihani, himself a Lebanese Christian who experienced Ottoman rule, would have been familiar with the machinery of Hamidian justice. St. Sophia (Hagia Sophia), referenced in the martyrdom fantasy, had been a mosque since 1453 and was a potent symbol of Ottoman-Islamic sovereignty over the former Byzantine Christian capital.
