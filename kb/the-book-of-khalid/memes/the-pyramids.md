# The Pyramids

## Location & Geography

The Pyramids of Giza stand on the Giza plateau, approximately 20 kilometres southwest of central [[cairo|Cairo]], on the west bank of the Nile. The three main pyramids — Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure — date from the Fourth Dynasty of the Old Kingdom (c. 2600–2500 BCE). In Rihani's narrative, the Pyramids are not described as archaeological sites but as cosmic symbols: they represent immensity, endurance, and the tension between material monumentality and spiritual aspiration.

The Pyramids appear in the novel's opening image alongside the modern skyscraper: "a Skyscraper in the shape of a Pyramid" [001-001] — a hybrid image that collapses the ancient and the modern into a single architectural gesture, suggesting that the skyscraper is the pyramid of the New World.

## Significance in the Book

The Pyramids and the Sphinx function in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as **symbols of the infinite**. In a key passage, [[khalid|Khalid]] invokes them alongside cosmic immensities: "the Sphinx and the Pyramid, the Origin and the End" [004-022]. This pairing positions the Pyramids not as tourist attractions but as metaphysical markers — the boundary stones of human comprehension.

The novel's opening image of "a Skyscraper in the shape of a Pyramid" [001-001] is Rihani's most compressed statement of the East–West duality that runs through the book. The skyscraper (modern [[new-york-city|America]], vertical ambition, commerce) merges with the pyramid (ancient Egypt, endurance, mystery) — a hybrid neither fully Eastern nor Western, suggesting that the book itself aspires to bridge both worlds.

Rihani's use of the Pyramids as symbols draws on a long Orientalist tradition in Western literature while subverting it: the pyramids are not exotic backdrop but active philosophical referents. When [[khalid|Khalid]] reflects that "a book is greater than a pyramid" — an implied comparison underlying the novel's self-conception — he is asserting the primacy of the word over stone, of ideas over monuments.

## Historical Context

By the late nineteenth century, the Pyramids of Giza were well-established tourist destinations, served by Thomas Cook & Son's Nile steamers and the [[cairo|Cairo]]–Giza tramway. European and [[new-york-city|America]]n travellers, from Mark Twain to Rudyard Kipling, had made the Pyramids a staple of Orientalist travel writing. Rihani's treatment is distinctive in treating them not as exotic spectacle but as philosophical symbols — part of a conceptual vocabulary that includes the Sphinx, the Origin, and the End.

The "Skyscraper in the shape of a Pyramid" image [001-001] may also reflect the Egyptomania that influenced [[new-york-city|America]]n architecture in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, from obelisk-shaped grave markers to the pyramidical design proposals for early skyscrapers.
