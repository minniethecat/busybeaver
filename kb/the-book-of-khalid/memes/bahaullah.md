## Role in the Book

Bahaullah (the "Splendour of God") appears in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* primarily through [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s devotion to the Baha'i cause. She is a "votary of [[abbas-effendi|Abbas Effendi]], [[abbas-effendi|the Pope of Babism]] at Haifa" [030-010] and makes annual pilgrimages to the Holy Land. Through her, Rihani introduces Bahaism as a modern religious movement that bridges [[east-west-duality|East and West]] — Persian in origin, universal in aspiration, and embodied in the charismatic figure of Bahaullah.

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s response to Bahaism is characteristically independent. When [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] urges him to become an apostle, he neither accepts nor rejects the faith outright. Instead, he appropriates its language for his own syncretic vision: "I am both a Camel-driver and Carpenter" — meaning he embraces both Muhammad and Christ, refusing the exclusivity that even the Baha'i reform, in [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s hands, seems to demand [031-020]. Bahaullah thus functions as a foil: his "splendour" illuminates both the appeal and the limits of institutionalized spirituality in [[khalid|Khalid]]'s quest.

## Biographical Sketch

Mirza Husayn-Ali Nuri (1817–1892), known as Bahaullah, was born in Tehran and became a leading figure in the Babi movement after the execution of the Bab in 1850. Imprisoned in the Black Pit of Tehran, he experienced a divine revelation and later proclaimed himself the messianic figure foretold by the Bab. Exiled to Baghdad, [[constantinople|Constantinople]], Adrianople, and finally Akka (Acre) in Ottoman Palestine, he spent forty years in exile and imprisonment, during which he authored the central texts of the Baha'i Faith.

At the time of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*'s setting (early 1900s), the Baha'i movement had recently established its center at Haifa under the leadership of [[abbas-effendi|Abbas Effendi]] (Abdul-Baha), Bahaullah's eldest son. [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s devotion reflects the real historical phenomenon of Western converts making pilgrimage to Haifa — a pattern that began in the 1890s and grew through the early twentieth century.

Rihani, writing in 1911, treats Bahaism with the same ambivalent fascination he brings to all organized religion: sympathetic to its universalist impulse, skeptical of its institutional form.

## Key Passages

> Mrs. Gotfry is surprised how a Syrian of Khalid's mind can not see the beauties of Babism, or Bahaism, as it is now called, and the lofty spirituality of the Bab.
>
> — [030-010]

This is [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s introduction to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s religious independence. She expects a "Syrian" — a man from the East — to be naturally receptive to a faith born in the East. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s refusal to be converted is her first lesson in his refusal to be categorized.

> "If you ask me the same question that was asked the Baha, I would not hesitate in saying that I am both a Camel-driver and Carpenter. I might also be a Bahaist in a certain sense. I renounce falsehood, whatsoever be the guise it assumes; and I embrace truth, wheresoever I find it."
>
> — [031-020]

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s response references Bahaullah's famous trial in Rhodes, where the Baha declared he was "neither a Camel-driver nor a Carpenter" (neither Muslim nor Christian). [[khalid|Khalid]] inverts Bahaullah's formula: he is *both*, refusing to discard either tradition. This is the novel's central theological move — syncretism over supersession.

> "Love, the splendour of God? Why, the Bab, the Baha, is the splendour of God. Baha mean splendour. The Baha, therefore, is love. Love is the new religion."
>
> — [031-020]

[[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s internal monologue shows her reinterpreting [[khalid|Khalid]]'s words through the lens of her Baha'i faith. The etymological chain — "Baha" means "splendour," [[khalid|Khalid]] equates splendour with [[love-sacred-and-profane|love]], therefore the Baha *is* [[love-sacred-and-profane|love]] — reveals how religious language can absorb even contrary ideas.

> "I am married to Bahaism. And you Khalid — remember my words — you will yet be an apostle — the apostle — of Bahaism."
>
> — [036-005]

[[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s declaration that she is "married to Bahaism" becomes the refrain of her relationship with [[khalid|Khalid]]. She cannot accept his refusal of her faith; she reframes it as a temporary resistance that destiny will overcome. The passage dramatizes the tension between institutional religion (embodied in Bahaullah's legacy) and [[khalid|Khalid]]'s insistence on personal, unmediated truth.

## Historical Context

The Baha'i Faith emerged from Twelver Shi'i Islam in nineteenth-century Persia, with the Bab (1819–1850) as the forerunner and Bahaullah as the founder-prophet. By 1911, when Rihani published *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*, the faith was attracting Western converts — particularly [[new-york-city|America]]n women like the fictional [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] — who were drawn to its progressive social teachings, its claim to fulfill all prior revelations, and its headquarters in the Holy Land.

Rihani's treatment of Bahaism reflects the broader Arab intellectual engagement with the movement in the early twentieth century. Some Arab nationalists saw in Bahaism a potential bridge between [[east-west-duality|East and West]]; others viewed it as a Persian sect competing with Islam. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s position — "truth, wheresoever I find it" — captures Rihani's own secular-universalist stance, which could admire Bahaullah's teachings without accepting their institutional authority.
