# Shakib

## Role in the Book

Shakib is the Scribe — the narrator, chronicler, and devoted friend who records [[khalid|Khalid]]'s life and preserves the "[[the-kl-manuscript|K.L. MS.]]" ([[the-kl-manuscript|Khalid's manuscript]]). He is simultaneously a character within the story and the voice that tells it. The book's narration frequently refers to "our Scribe" in the third person, creating a layered narrative structure where Shakib is both recorder and recorded.

Shakib's role is more than amanuensis. He is [[khalid|Khalid]]'s banker, advocate, and occasional conscience. When [[khalid|Khalid]] is imprisoned, it is Shakib who secures his release. When [[khalid|Khalid]] needs money, Shakib provides it and burns the promissory note (015-009). His devotion is absolute but not uncritical — his narration includes judgments of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s excesses.

## Biographical Sketch

Shakib is a Lebanese poet and intellectual who shares [[khalid|Khalid]]'s emigration to [[new-york-city|America]]. Unlike [[khalid|Khalid]], Shakib seems to have achieved some stability — he has resources to lend and connections to leverage. He is less restless than [[khalid|Khalid]], more grounded in practical affairs, yet equally committed to the life of the mind.

His relationship to the book's narration is complex. At times he writes in the first person as an eyewitness; at other times, the narration treats him as a character referred to in the third person. This ambiguity — is Shakib the author? is he transcribing [[the-kl-manuscript|Khalid's manuscript]]? is he embellishing? — is central to the book's literary effect.

Shakib is also a poet of the "Modern School of Arabic poetry," described as its choragus (leader). His Diwan is characterized as "a sort of rhymed inventory of all the inventions and discoveries of modern Science and all the wonders of America" — a fusion of Eastern poetic form with Western technological subject matter that mirrors the novel's own synthesis of traditions.

## Historical Context

Shakib functions as a framing device common in 19th and early 20th century fiction — the found-manuscript narrator. Like the narrators of Conrad's *Heart of Darkness* or James's *The Turn of the Screw*, Shakib mediates between the reader and the central figure, lending credibility through eyewitness testimony while also introducing the possibility of narrative distortion. Rihani's innovation is to make the frame narrator not a disinterested observer but an emotionally invested participant — Shakib's love for Khalid colors every page of his chronicle.

The name "Shakib" may derive from the Arabic root sh-k-b, associated with patience and endurance — fitting for one who devotes his life to chronicling another's.

## Relationships

- [[khalid|Khalid]] — Shakib's subject, friend, and philosophical companion. Their relationship — chronicler and subject, banker and borrower, friend and friend — is the emotional core of the book.
