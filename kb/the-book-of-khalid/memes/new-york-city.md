# New York City

## Location & Geography

New York City at the turn of the 20th century was the primary port of entry for immigrants to the United States, particularly through [[ellis-island|Ellis Island]]. In *The Book of Khalid*, the city is rendered as a vertical landscape — skyscrapers, cellars, and the Brooklyn Bridge — where the immigrant experience unfolds across multiple social strata. The financial district (Wall Street, the Stock Exchange), the immigrant tenements, the Bohemian cellars, and the Tammany Hall political machine all occupy the same compressed geography.

## Role in the Book

New York City is the primary American setting of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*, serving as both promised land and crucible. It is here that [[khalid|Khalid]] encounters the full force of Western modernity — its intellectual freedom and its political corruption, its [[bohemianism|Bohemia]]n artistic circles and its ruthless commercialism. The city is the laboratory in which [[khalid|Khalid]]'s philosophy is forged.

## Significance

New York represents the materialist pole of Rihani's East-West dialectic. The city's energy is both liberating and dehumanizing. [[khalid|Khalid]] finds free-thought literature in a cellar bookshop, attends [[bohemianism|Bohemia]]n dinners, and encounters the "mad crowds coming and going, running, as it were, between two fires" (010-013). [[battery-park|Battery Park]] becomes his refuge for philosophical contemplation amid urban chaos.

The city is also the site of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s deepest disillusionment. [[tammany-hall|Tammany Hall]] — the Democratic Party political machine — recruits him as an ethnic canvasser and then destroys him. "From the Table of [[bohemianism|Bohemia]] to the Stump in [[tammany-hall|Tammany Land]], is a far cry" (014-008).

The cellar bookshop run by "secondhand Jerry" serves as a counterpoint to the commercial city above — an underground temple of literature where Khalid discovers the works of Carlyle, Emerson, and Rousseau, and where "the Stock Exchange is as necessary to Society as the Church" (009-003).

## Historical Context

New York in the 1890s–1900s was undergoing rapid transformation: the consolidation of the five boroughs (1898), the construction of the first skyscrapers, and the peak of Ellis Island immigration. The city's population doubled between 1880 and 1910, driven largely by immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe and the Ottoman Empire. The "Syrian" (Lebanese/Syrian) community that Khalid joins was concentrated in lower Manhattan, near what is now the Financial District — the same neighborhood as the secondhand bookshops, Tammany Hall headquarters, and the Battery that feature prominently in the novel.

Rihani himself lived in New York from 1888 onward, working in his family's importing business before turning to literature. His portrayal of Tammany Hall politics, Bohemian intellectual circles, and the Syrian immigrant community draws directly from personal experience.
