## Introduction

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s departure from Lebanon is the structural pivot of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. Everything before it is preparation; everything after it, consequence. The event is narrated with a compression that befits its emotional weight: a single paragraph in which the two young men are smuggled through the Ottoman port of [[beirut|Beirut]], take one last look at the Lebanese mountains, and launch themselves toward [[new-york-city|America]]. In Rihani's hands, this departure is not merely a plot event but an existential threshold — [[the-atlantic-voyage|the crossing]] from a world governed by tradition and stasis into one defined by ambition and becoming.

## The Smuggling at Beirut

The departure is shadowed by the corruption of the Ottoman state. [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] cannot leave legally because they lack passports, but the Ottoman port officials can "better read a gold piece than a passport":

> In their baggy, lapping trousers and crimson caps, each carrying a bundle and a rug under his arm, Shakib and Khalid are smuggled through the port of Beirut at night, and safely rowed to the steamer. Indeed, we are in a country where one can not travel without a passport, or a password, or a little pass-money. And the boatmen and officials of the Ottoman Empire can better read a gold piece than a passport. So, Shakib and Khalid, not having the latter, slip in a few of the former, and are smuggled through.

The bribe is not dwelt on as moral failing; it is presented as the normal functioning of a decaying empire. The phrase "a country where one can not travel without a passport, or a password, or a little pass-money" collapses legal, social, and corrupt channels into a single syntax, suggesting they are three forms of the same thing. Rihani, writing from the perspective of the Arab immigrant, treats Ottoman corruption as background fact — the water his characters swim in, not an outrage to be protested.

## The Last Glance at the Lebanons

The emotional center of the departure is the visual image of the mountains receding:

> One more longing, lingering glance behind, and the dusky peaks of the Lebanons, beyond which their native City of Baal is sleeping in peace, recede from view. On the high sea of hope and joy they sail; "under the Favonian wind of enthusiasm, on the friendly billows of boyish dreams," they roll. Ay, and they sing for joy.

The alliteration ("longing, lingering glance") and the personification ("their native City of Baal is sleeping in peace") elevate the moment beyond mere travelogue. [[baalbek|Baalbek]] sleeps; the young men do not. The "high sea of hope and joy" and the classical "Favonian wind" (the west wind, associated with spring and renewal) suggest that emigration is not exile but liberation — a claim the novel will spend its remaining chapters testing and complicating.

## The Cedars and the Decision

The departure is framed by a prior moment at the [[mount-lebanon|Cedars of Lebanon]], where the decision to leave was made:

> One day, following some tourists to the Cedars, they behold from Dahr'ul-Qadhib the sun setting in the Mediterranean and make up their minds to follow it too. "For the sundown," writes Shakib, "was more appealing to us than the sunrise, ay, more beautiful. The one was so near, the other so far away."

The preference for sunset over sunrise is the preference for the West over the East, for the "near" possible future over the "far" ancestral past. The decision made at the Cedars is enacted at [[beirut|Beirut]]; the two locations — mountain summit and port — bracket the departure as a movement from contemplation to action, from the heights of symbol to the gritty mechanics of escape.

## Historical Context

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s departure occurs during the late Ottoman period, when emigration from Greater Syria to the [[new-york-city|America]]s was at its peak. Between 1880 and 1914, an estimated 300,000 people emigrated from the region of modern Lebanon and Syria to the United States and Brazil. Rihani himself arrived in [[new-york-city|New York]] in 1888 at the age of twelve. The detail about bribery at the port reflects historical reality: the Ottoman *mürur tezkeresi* (internal passport) system was riddled with corruption, and emigration was often quasi-legal, facilitated by a network of agents, boatmen, and bribable officials.

## Significance in the Novel's Structure

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s departure is the event that divides the novel into "before" and "after." The chapters that follow — the Atlantic voyage, the arrival in [[new-york-city|New York]], the years of struggle and ambition — all flow from this single moment of decision and escape. It is also the event that makes [[khalid|Khalid]] a representative figure: he is not merely a character but an emblem of the Arab immigrant experience, the "Syrian peddler" who carries the East within him as he navigates the West.
