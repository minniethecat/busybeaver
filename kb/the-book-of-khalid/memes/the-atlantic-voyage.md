# The Atlantic Voyage

## Role in the Book

The Atlantic voyage is the physical ordeal of emigration that [[khalid|Khalid]] endures between [[beirut|Beirut]] and [[new-york-city|New York]]. It is described in harrowing detail: emigrants are "huddled like sheep on deck from [[beirut|Beirut]] to [[marseilles|Marseilles]]; and like cattle transported under hatches across the Atlantic; and bullied and browbeaten by rough disdainful stewards" (006-003). The voyage is the passage between worlds — the suffering that must be endured to reach the "Western Paradise of the Oriental imagination."

## Significance

The voyage is the book's most visceral depiction of the immigrant experience. It strips away the romance of emigration and reveals the brutal economics of human movement. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s suffering on the ship is both personal and representative — the collective experience of millions of immigrants who crossed the Atlantic in steerage.

## Key Passages

> "We ourselves have known a little of the suffering and misery which emigrants must undergo, before they reach that Western Paradise of the Oriental imagination.
