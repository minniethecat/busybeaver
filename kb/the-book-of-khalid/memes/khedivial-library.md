# Khedivial Library

## Location & Geography

The Khedivial Library (Dar al-Kutub al-Misriyya) is located in [[cairo|Cairo]], Egypt. Founded in 1870 by Khedive Ismail, it served as the national repository of Arabic and Islamic manuscripts, printed books, and periodicals. In the novel's opening sentence, Rihani locates the library's "Scribbling Department" — his satirical name for the manuscript collection — as the site where "the Author of this Book was employed" [001-001], transforming a real institution into the fictional birthplace of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s text.

The library's collection of "Papyri" [001-001] reflects the historical breadth of Egyptian textual culture, from Pharaonic scrolls to medieval Arabic codices. Rihani's choice of the word "Scribbling" for the department name carries a note of self-deprecating humour — the grand manuscript tradition reduced, in the narrator's telling, to mere scribbling.

## Significance in the Book

The Khedivial Library is the **narrative ground zero** of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. The entire novel is presented as a found manuscript: [[shakib|Shakib]], working among the library's papers, discovers [[khalid|Khalid]]'s text, edits it, and presents it to the reader. Without the library and its "Scribbling Department," the book we are reading does not exist.

The library functions on multiple levels. **Structurally**, it is a framing device: the "Khedivial Library Manuscript" or "Khedivial Library MS." is repeatedly cited as a source [004-004], [007-024], [008-003], [008-013], [010-002], giving the narrative a documentary texture — as though we are reading a scholarly edition of a recovered text. When the narrator notes that "in the Khedivial Library MS. we find nothing which this [[battery-park|Battery Park]] might have inspired" [008-003], the manuscript becomes a character in its own right, with its own silences and omissions.

**Thematically**, the library embodies the novel's concern with **textuality and transmission**. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s ideas survive not through monuments or institutions but through the written word, preserved in a library where they wait to be discovered. The library is the medium of cultural memory — fragile (papyri can crumble) but persistent (the word outlasts the author).

**Satirically**, the "Scribbling Department" mocks bureaucratic taxonomy. By placing [[khalid|Khalid]]'s visionary manuscript among "Papyri" — a category that collapses millennia of Egyptian writing into one shelf — Rihani suggests that all writing, from Pharaonic tax records to modern philosophy, is ultimately just scribbling.

## Key Passages

> "In the Khedivial Library of Cairo, among the Papyri of the Scribbling Department, the Author of this Book was employed."
>
> — [001-001]

The novel's opening sentence establishes the library as generative space. The passive "was employed" distances the narrator ([[shakib|Shakib]]) from the Author ([[khalid|Khalid]]), setting up the found-manuscript frame.

> "the Author of the Khedivial Library Manuscript can make his Genius dance on the tip of his pen"
>
> — [004-004]

This passage distinguishes between [[khalid|Khalid]] the Author (whose Genius dances) and [[shakib|Shakib]] the editor (who must keep the reader in good humour), establishing the library manuscript as the source of the book's visionary quality.

> "In the Khedivial Library MS. we find nothing which this Battery Park might have inspired."
>
> — [008-003]

The manuscript's silence on [[battery-park|Battery Park]] becomes itself a piece of information — suggesting that [[khalid|Khalid]]'s [[new-york-city|New York]] observations were either too ephemeral or too painful to record in his polished text.

> "we are translating from the Khedivial Library MS."
>
> — [010-002]

This periodic reminder reinforces the novel's documentary fiction: we are not reading a free-standing narrative but a translation of a found manuscript, with all the gaps and editorial interventions that implies.

## Historical Context

The Khedivial Library (Dar al-Kutub al-Misriyya) was founded in 1870 and opened to the public in 1904 in a purpose-built building on Bab al-Khalq Square in [[cairo|Cairo]]. By the time Rihani was writing *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* (published 1911), the library had become the premier repository of Arabic manuscripts in the world, housing collections transferred from mosques, schools, and private libraries across Egypt.

Rihani's choice of the library as a framing device reflects the *Nahda*'s preoccupation with textual recovery and editorial scholarship. The nineteenth-century Arabic renaissance was driven in part by the rediscovery and republication of classical texts, and the Khedivial Library was a central institution in that project. [[shakib|Shakib]]'s role as editor-transmitter of [[the-kl-manuscript|Khalid's manuscript]] mirrors the *Nahda* intellectual who recovers a lost text and brings it to a modern readership.
