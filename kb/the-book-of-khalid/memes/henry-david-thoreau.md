## Role in the Book

Henry David Thoreau appears in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as a touchstone for the practice of solitude. Rihani invokes him not as a distant literary reference but as "my [[new-york-city|America]]n brother Thoreau" [007-016] — an intimate, familial address that collapses the distance between Lebanese mountains and Walden Woods. The [[baalbek|Baalbek]] terraces, with their stone walls full of gaps where cyclamen flowers bloom, "would have delighted the heart of the American naturalist Thoreau" precisely because those gaps let wildness flourish alongside cultivation [007-015].

[[khalid|Khalid]] uses Thoreau to think through the problem of solitude: how to withdraw without becoming a "spiritual larva in its cocoon," how to filter oneself through nature while maintaining "at least his practice of human speech" [015-026]. Thoreau models solitude *with* engagement — peeping into [[baalbek|the village]] every fortnight — against the hermits of [[mount-lebanon|Mount Lebanon]] who retreat into sterile isolation. This distinction is central to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own spiritual project: he seeks a solitude that enriches, not one that escapes.

## Biographical Sketch

Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862) was born in Concord, Massachusetts, and is best known for his experiment in simple living at Walden Pond (1845–1847), recorded in *Walden* (1854). His first book, *A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers* (1849), appears materially in the novel when [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] discover "a mutilated copy of the *Week*" in a mountain village — a "miracle of chance" that links the Lebanese highlands to the New England woods [012-012].

Thoreau's philosophy of civil disobedience, nature mysticism, and deliberate living resonated with Rihani's generation of Arab-[[new-york-city|America]]n writers, who saw in the Concord sage a secular prophet of individualism — one who, like the Arabic Sufi poets, sought divine truth in nature rather than in institutions.

## Key Passages

> "O, these terraces would have delighted the heart of the American naturalist Thoreau. He could not have desired stone walls with more gaps in them."
>
> — [007-015]

The speaker ([[shakib|Shakib]]) imagines Thoreau's pleasure at the [[baalbek|Baalbek]] terraces, where stone walls are imperfect by design — gaps allow cyclamens to grow wild. This is Thoreauvian aesthetics applied to Lebanese agriculture: the wall that lets nature through is superior to the wall that excludes it.

> "That is why, I believe, my American brother Thoreau liked walls with many gaps in them. The sweet wild daughters of Spring can live therein their natural life without being molested by the scythe or the plough."
>
> — [007-016]

The epithet "my [[new-york-city|America]]n brother" is striking — it places Thoreau in a kinship relation across continents and cultures. The walls-with-gaps become a metaphor for Rihani's own literary project: a permeable boundary between [[east-west-duality|East and West]], cultivated and wild.

> "Henry Thoreau's *Week*! What a miracle of chance. Whose this mutilated copy of the *Week*, we thought? Who in these mountains, having been in America, took more interest in the Dreamer of Walden Woods than in peddling and trading?"
>
> — [012-012]

The discovery of a battered copy of *A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers* in a Lebanese mountain village is one of the novel's most evocative moments. The book's presence testifies to invisible circuits of cultural exchange — some unnamed emigrant carried Thoreau home alongside trade goods, valuing the "Dreamer" over profit.

> "While that of a philosopher, a Thoreau, for instance, might be called Nature's filter; and one, issuing therefrom benefited in every sense, morally, physically, spiritually, can be said to have been filtered through Solitude."
>
> — [015-026]

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s taxonomy of solitude distinguishes the "cocoon larva" (the hermit who retreats permanently) from the Thoreauvian model: solitude as a filter that purifies rather than isolates. The metaphor is characteristic of Rihani's style — natural imagery pressed into philosophical service.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's reception in the Arab world was mediated through the *Mahjar* (émigré) writers of the early twentieth century, who encountered [[new-york-city|America]]n literature directly and selectively imported its figures into Arabic literary discourse. Rihani's invocation of Thoreau is unusually intimate — "my [[new-york-city|America]]n brother" — reflecting the Mahjar writers' project of cultural synthesis. Where European Orientalists had treated [[east-west-duality|East and West]] as incommensurable, Rihani's Thoreau is a companion, not an exhibit: his walls-with-gaps philosophy proves immediately applicable to Lebanese terraces, and his *Week* circulates in mountain villages as naturally as any Arabic text.
