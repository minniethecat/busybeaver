# Damascus

## Role in the Book

Damascus is the setting for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s prophetic mission in Book III. After returning from [[new-york-city|America]], he travels to this ancient city — "the stronghold of the ulema" (the learned religious authorities) — and delivers his message of "[[prophetic-reform|reformation by emigration]]." Damascus represents institutionalized religion and tradition, the force that [[khalid|Khalid]] must challenge to complete his spiritual journey.

## Significance

Damascus is presented as a city of contradictions: great beauty ("the City of Jasmine") and entrenched orthodoxy. The ulema — [[the-ulema|Islamic scholars]] and jurists — hold sway over the population through "enslaving superstitions" and "imbruting cants" (033-026). [[khalid|Khalid]]'s message that "the soul must be free, and the mind, before one has a right to be a member of a free Government" (031-007) is a direct challenge to their authority.

The city's response to [[khalid|Khalid]] is ambivalent. "On the following day Damascus was simmering with excitement" (031-008). Some are drawn to his message; the ulema see him as a threat. The tension between reform and tradition plays out in Damascus's streets and salons.
