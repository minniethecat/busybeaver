## Overview

Khalid's banishment from his father's house is the breaking point in his return to Baalbek after years in America. Having absorbed freethinking ideas abroad, Khalid refuses to attend church upon his return — a defiance that, in the tightly surveilled community of Baalbek, triggers a cascade of gossip, parental ultimatum, and Jesuit-orchestrated expulsion. The event is recorded by Shakib in the *Histoire Intime* and dramatizes the collision between individual conscience and communal religious conformity that is one of the novel's central themes.

## Narrative

### The Return and the Gossip

Khalid and Shakib arrive in Baalbek to a short-lived welcome. "A fortnight after our arrival in Baalbek — our civility towards newcomers seldom enjoys a longer lease — the town was alive with rumours and whim-whams about my friend" [018-006]. The gossip focuses on Khalid's foreign manners: his long hair, shaved mustaches, changed accent. But the real danger lies in the question that follows: "Why does he not come to Church like honest folks?" [018-006].

### The Ultimatum

The pressure converges on Khalid's family. His father, Abu-Khalid, delivers an ultimatum: "You will either go to Church like myself, or get out of this house" [018-006]. His mother, Im-Hanna, "prostrating herself before the Virgin, weeps, and prays, and beats her breast" [018-006] — but her intercession is powerless against the father's resolve and the community's judgment. Khalid chooses exile.

### The Mother's Plea

In one of the novel's most poignant scenes, Im-Hanna seeks Khalid in the ruins of the Temple of Venus at night — a pagan setting for a Christian family's tragedy. She begs him, "in the name of the Virgin, to return home" [018-009]:

> "She beats her breast, weeps, prostrates herself before him, beseeches, implores, cries out, '*dakhilak* (I am at your mercy), come home with me.' And Khalid, taking her up by the arm, embraces her and weeps, but says not a word."
>
> — [018-009]

The Arabic "*dakhilak*" — a plea for protection — inverts the parent-child relationship: the mother places herself at her son's mercy. Khalid's silent weeping is his answer: he cannot return, but he cannot explain why without hurting her further.

Khalid's counter-offer is heartbreaking in its practicality: "Let him move out of the house, and I will come. I will live with you, if he will keep at the Jesuits" [018-009]. In Khalid's analysis, his father is not independently zealous — his "religious ferocity is fuelled and fanned" by the Jesuits [018-010].

### Jesuit Involvement

Father Farouche, a Jesuit priest, visits Khalid shortly before the banishment and finds him "sitting cross-legged on a mat writing a letter" [018-010]. This seemingly innocuous visit masks the Jesuits' campaign against Khalid. Shakib's account makes the connection explicit: Khalid's banishment is not merely a family dispute but an institutional operation — the Jesuits, threatened by Khalid's freethinking, mobilize the community's religious conformity to expel him.

The banishment has literary consequences as well. "About a fortnight after Khalid's banishment from home," Shakib records, "a booklet was published in Beirut, setting forth the history of Ignatius Loyola and the purports and intents of Jesuitism" [018-035]. Khalid, in exile, turns to writing — the banishment becomes productive, generating the anti-Jesuit polemic that will further antagonize his enemies.

>

Abu-Khalid's ultimatum reduces religious practice to paternal obedience. The choice is false: Khalid can neither abandon his convictions nor defy his father without consequence.

> "She beats her breast, weeps, prostrates herself before him, beseeches, implores, cries out, '*dakhilak*, come home with me.' And Khalid, taking her up by the arm, embraces her and weeps, but says not a word."
>
> — [018-009]

The Temple of Venus scene is the emotional core of the banishment narrative. The mother's Arabic plea and Khalid's silent tears communicate what language cannot: the irreconcilable rift between filial love and intellectual integrity.

## Significance

The banishment marks Khalid's definitive break with the communal religious order of Baalbek. It is the domestic parallel to his larger critique of institutional religion: just as he refuses to pay the Church for his wedding dispensation, he refuses to attend church to satisfy his father. Both refusals cost him everything — his home, his beloved, his place in the community.

The event also demonstrates the Jesuits' role as enforcers of orthodoxy in late Ottoman Lebanon. They do not merely preach; they surveil, report, and orchestrate social consequences for dissent. Khalid's banishment is their victory, but it also liberates him to become the writer and reformer he is destined to be.
