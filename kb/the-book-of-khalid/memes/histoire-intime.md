## Overview

The *Histoire Intime* ("Intimate History") is a fictional Arabic manuscript written by Shakib, Khalid's loyal companion and scribe, within the narrative world of *The Book of Khalid*. It functions as an embedded biography — an intimate portrait of Khalid's life, character, and genius, compiled by the person who knew him best. The narrator of *The Book of Khalid* treats the *Histoire Intime* as a primary source, quoting from it, critiquing its excesses, and using it to construct the larger narrative of Khalid's life and ideas.

The *Histoire Intime* is distinct from the K.L. Manuscript (Khalid's own philosophical writings) and from *The Book of Khalid* itself (the novel we read). It occupies the middle layer of Rihani's triple-decker frame narrative: Shakib writes about Khalid; the unnamed narrator edits, translates, and comments on Shakib's text; and Rihani publishes the whole as a novel.

## References in the Book

The *Histoire Intime* is first mentioned in Book I, Chapter IV, when Shakib presents it to the narrator as essential reading: "You can not properly understand him, unless you read the Histoire Intime, which I have just finished. That will give you *les dessous de cartes* of his character" [004-027]. The French phrase ("the underside of the cards") signals the work's claim to reveal Khalid's hidden depths.

The narrator and his editorial team then take possession of the manuscript: "we split it into three parts and got our boys working on it. The result was most satisfying" [004-044]. This collaborative editing — "our boys" suggests a newspaper or publishing office — mirrors the actual process by which Arabic manuscripts of the *nahda* period were prepared for English-language publication.

In Book I, Chapter V, the narrator distances himself from Shakib's more extravagant claims: "Nor do we for a moment believe that the waywardness of a genius or a prophet in boyhood is always a significant adumbration… Not so far forth are we willing to piddle among the knicknacks of Shakib's Histoire Intime of his Master" [005-010]. This editorial irony — using the *Histoire Intime* while mocking its excesses — is central to Rihani's narrative method.

In Book I, Chapter VI, the narrator quotes directly: "And in justice to both, we give the following from the Histoire Intime" [006-004], introducing a scene of Khalid and Shakib at sea with wine, lute, and poetry.

## Key Passages

> "This is the Histoire Intime," said he, laying it gently on the table.
>
> — [004-031]

The physical gesture — laying the manuscript "gently on the table" — suggests the care Shakib has invested in his work and his reverence for Khalid's story.

> "You can not properly understand him, unless you read the Histoire Intime, which I have just finished. That will give you *les dessous de cartes* of his character."
>
> — [004-027]

Shakib claims the *Histoire Intime* as the indispensable key to Khalid — a claim the narrator both honors (by using it extensively) and subverts (by mocking its hagiographic tendencies).

> "As for the Histoire Intime, we split it into three parts and got our boys working on it. The result was most satisfying."
>
> — [004-044]

The editorial "we" and the casual reference to "our boys" locate the narrative in a modern, urban, English-language publishing milieu — likely Rihani's own New York circle — while the tripartite division echoes classical Arabic manuscript organization.

## Thematic Connection

The *Histoire Intime* embodies several of the novel's central concerns. As a frame narrative device, it dramatizes the act of translation and cultural mediation that is the book's deepest subject: an Arabic text (Shakib's) is filtered through an English-language narrator who simultaneously depends on it and condescends to it. This mirrors Rihani's own position as an Arab immigrant writing in English for an American audience.

The tension between Shakib's hagiographic impulse and the narrator's ironic distance also reflects the East–West duality theme: Shakib's devotion belongs to an older, Arab tradition of biographical reverence, while the narrator's skepticism is a modern, Western editorial stance. The *Histoire Intime* is thus not merely a plot device but a formal enactment of the cultural negotiation that *The Book of Khalid* performs on every page.
