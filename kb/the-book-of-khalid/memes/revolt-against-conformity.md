# Revolt Against Conformity

## Definition

Revolt Against Conformity is the existential and philosophical stance that defines [[khalid|Khalid]]'s life trajectory in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. It is the principled rejection of what the narrator calls "the dominating spirit of one's people and ancestors" (017-003) — the conformity to inherited values, ethnic expectations, and social norms that, in Rihani's diagnosis, afflicts both Eastern and Western societies with equal force.

The concept is distinguished from mere political revolution. [[khalid|Khalid]] advocates "[[khalids-departure-from-lebanon|the emigration]] of the mind before the revolution of the state" (031-007) — an inner transformation that must precede and ground any external change. The revolt is fundamentally individual: one person refusing to be determined by the "crushing influence of conformity" (017-003), even at the cost of personal destruction.

## Development in the Book

The concept crystallizes in Book II, Chapter IV, where the narrator provides the most explicit philosophical statement of the book on the subject of revolt. [[khalid|Khalid]], having been imprisoned by [[tammany-hall|Tammany Hall]] and now confronting the commercial world, asks himself: "To trade, or not to trade" — echoing Hamlet's existential dilemma. The narrator expands:

"Whether he opposes his [[immanent-morality|theory of immanent morality]] to the Cash Register, or to [[democracy-and-its-dowry|Democracy]], or to the ruling powers of Flunkeydom, we hope He will end well. Such is the penalty of revolt against the dominating spirit of one's people and ancestors, that only once in a generation is it attempted, and scarcely with much success" (017-003).

Rihani then articulates a biological law of social change: "In fact, the first who revolts must perish, the second, too, and the third, and the fourth, until, in the course of time and by dint of repetition and resistance, the new species of the race can overcome the forces of environment and the crushing influence of conformity" (017-003). This is a profoundly Darwinian view of social evolution — revolt is not a single dramatic act but a multi-generational process in which earlier rebels are sacrificed for later successes.

The narrator identifies the specific conformity [[khalid|Khalid]] revolts against: "the ethos of the Syrians... like that of the [[new-york-city|America]]ns, is essentially money-seeking. And whether in [[beirut|Beirut]] or in [[new-york-city|New York]], even the moralists and reformers, like the hammals and grocers, will ask themselves, before they undertake to do anything for you or for their country, 'What will this profit us? How much will it bring us?'" (017-004). The revolt is thus against a transnational disease — the reduction of all value to monetary value.

Crucially, Rihani adds a sober qualification: "when we revolt against a tendency inimical to our law of being, it is for our own sake, and not the race's, that we do so" (017-004). Revolt is not altruistic social engineering but personal self-preservation — the individual's refusal to be deformed by a hostile environment.

In Book III, the concept evolves from personal stance to public teaching. Before [[damascus|Damascus]], [[khalid|Khalid]] declares: "I am for a [[prophetic-reform|reformation by emigration]]. And quietly, peacefully, this can be done. Nor fire, nor sword bring I... The [[prophetic-reform|emigration of the mind]] before the revolution of the state, my Brothers. The soul must be free, and the mind, before one has a right to be a member of a free Government" (031-007). The internal revolt must precede the external one — a philosophy that distinguishes [[khalid|Khalid]] from both violent revolutionaries and passive conformists.
