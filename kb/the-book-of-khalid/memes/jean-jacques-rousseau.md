## Role in the Book

Jean-Jacques Rousseau occupies a distinctive place in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as both a figure of intimate self-revelation and a forbidden author. At the Jesuit school in [[baalbek|Baalbek]], students learned "enough of French to understand the authors tabooed by our Jesuit professors — the Voltaires, the Rousseaus, the Diderots" [006-005]. The censored status of these Enlightenment authors invests them with an aura of intellectual liberation: to read Rousseau is already to dissent from clerical authority.

Rousseau's presence intensifies in the [[new-york-city|New York]] cellar, where [[khalid|Khalid]]'s small library includes only two books: *Emile* and [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s *Hero-Worship*. These are "the only two books he had in the cellar" — a deliberate pairing of French Enlightenment pedagogy with Victorian hero-worship [029-013]. [[khalid|Khalid]] reads both twice and burns them, feeding their pages into the stove in a symbolic act that is at once destructive and consumptive: he has internalized their fire. The narrator places Rousseau alongside [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]], Gibbon, and Spencer in a meditation on biography's capacity for trivial revelation — Rousseau's confession of kissing Madame de Warens's linen becomes, in Rihani's hands, a question about the boundary between significant self-disclosure and mere "suppurative [[love-sacred-and-profane|passion]]" [009-010].

## Biographical Sketch

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was born in Geneva and became one of the most influential philosophers of the French Enlightenment, though his thought often set him at odds with his contemporaries. His major works include *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* (1750), *The Social Contract* (1762), *Emile, or On Education* (1762), and the posthumously published *Confessions* (1782–1789).

For Rihani's purposes, two Rousseaus matter most: the educational theorist of *Emile*, whose model of natural education resonates with [[khalid|Khalid]]'s autodidacticism, and the confessional autobiographer of the *Confessions*, whose willingness to reveal intimate detail — including his quasi-erotic attachment to his patroness Madame de Warens — provides a template for [[shakib|Shakib]]'s own biographical candor about [[khalid|Khalid]]. Rihani is skeptical of Rousseau's more self-indulgent disclosures ("suppurative [[love-sacred-and-profane|passion]]"), but the *Confessions* model of truth-telling through personal revelation underlies the novel's own narrative strategy.

## Key Passages

> "Enough of French to understand the authors tabooed by our Jesuit professors — the Voltaires, the Rousseaus, the Diderots."
>
> — [006-005]

[[shakib|Shakib]] lists Rousseau among the forbidden French authors whose work constituted the unofficial curriculum of the [[baalbek|Baalbek]] seminary. The Jesuit prohibition frames Rousseau as dangerous knowledge — precisely the kind [[khalid|Khalid]] pursues.

> "Rousseau, in a suppurative passion, whispers to us in his *Confessions* that he even kissed the linen of Madame de Warens' bed when he was alone in her room."
>
> — [009-010]

The narrator's critique of biographical triviality uses Rousseau as Exhibit A. "Suppurative [[love-sacred-and-profane|passion]]" is a devastating phrase — it suggests confession as a kind of wound that does not heal, a self-exposure that serves no higher purpose. Yet the passage acknowledges Rousseau's honesty even as it questions its value.

> "This I did, fetching with him Rousseau's *Emile* and Carlyle's *Hero-Worship*, the only two books he had in the cellar. And when he saw them, he exclaimed with joy, 'The very books I want! I read them twice already, and I shall read them again.'"
>
> — [029-013]

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s cellar library reduced to two books: one French (Rousseau's *Emile*, a treatise on natural education) and one English ([[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s *Hero-Worship*, a celebration of great men). The pairing is deliberate — the individual formed by nature (Rousseau) meets the individual formed by history ([[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]). [[khalid|Khalid]] has read both twice and wants them again, suggesting their ideas are being metabolized, not merely consumed.

> "*Emile* and *Hero-Worship* go the way of all the rest. And there he sits, meditating over Carlyle's crepitating fire and Rousseau's writhing, sibilating flame."
>
> — [029-014]

The burning of the two books is a ritual act. [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s fire is "crepitating" (crackling, vigorous), Rousseau's is "writhing, sibilating" (twisting, hissing) — the adjectives align each philosopher with his essential character. [[khalid|Khalid]] does not discard these thinkers; he absorbs them into his own intellectual fire before setting out for [[cairo|Cairo]] and his eventual return to Lebanon.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's reception in the Arab Nahda was mediated through French, the colonial language of much of the Levant. The Jesuit curriculum that [[shakib|Shakib]] describes — French literature taught selectively, with Enlightenment *philosophes* placed on the index of forbidden authors — reflects the historical reality of nineteenth-century missionary education in Syria and Lebanon. Students read enough French to access the banned books; the prohibition itself became a curriculum.

Rihani's pairing of Rousseau with [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] is a characteristic Mahjar gesture: the French and the English, the natural and the heroic, placed in dialogue through an Arab reader in a [[new-york-city|New York]] cellar. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s burning of both books marks neither rejection nor acceptance but *transformation* — the European sources are consumed as fuel for a new, synthetic Arab-[[new-york-city|America]]n consciousness.
