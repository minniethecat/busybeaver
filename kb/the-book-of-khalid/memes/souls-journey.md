# Soul's Journey

## Definition

The Soul's Journey is the book's governing metaphor, announced in its opening pages: "This then is a book of travels in an impalpable country, an enchanted country, from which we have all risen, and towards which we are still rising. It is, as it were, the chart and history of one little kingdom of the Soul — the Soul of a philosopher, poet and criminal" (001-004). The entire narrative of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s life — his emigration, his struggles in [[new-york-city|New York]], his [[bohemianism|Bohemia]]n period, his imprisonment, his return to Lebanon, and his prophetic mission — is framed not as biography but as spiritual cartography.

This concept distinguishes *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* from both conventional novels and conventional philosophy. The book is neither fiction nor treatise but "travels in an impalpable country" — a genre Rihani invents to convey that the real journey is inward. The "kingdom of the Soul" is both deeply personal ([[khalid|Khalid]]'s individual soul) and universal (the soul "from which we have all risen, and towards which we are still rising").

## Development in the Book

The soul's journey unfolds in three Books, corresponding to three phases of spiritual development. Book I (The City of the Sun) charts the soul's emergence from tradition: [[khalid|Khalid]] leaves Lebanon, endures the brutalities of emigration, and encounters the intellectual and sensual freedoms of [[new-york-city|New York]]. The soul here is awakening but undisciplined — "loafing" is described as "a necessary prelude to the travail" (008-012).

Book II (The Path of the Soul) represents the soul's trial by fire. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s imprisonment in [[khalids-imprisonment|the Tombs]] is the soul's dark night: "there, for ten days and nights... we leave [[khalid|Khalid]] to brood on [[democracy-and-its-dowry|Democracy]] and [[democracy-and-its-dowry|the Dowry of Democracy]]" (014-020). The soul is stripped of illusions about politics, about [[new-york-city|America]], about the efficacy of pure idealism. The narrator's comment — "So long as one has faith in the general course of things, one can not go far wrong" (014-020) — is the hard-won wisdom of the soul that has passed through disillusionment without surrendering to cynicism.

Book III (The Heights) is the soul's return and ascent. Back in Lebanon, [[khalid|Khalid]] climbs the mountain — literally and spiritually — and delivers his message of reform in [[damascus|Damascus]]. The geography mirrors the soul's trajectory: from the "cliffs 'neath which the lily blooms" to "the cliffs round which the eagles flit" (029-003). The soul's journey culminates not in arrival but in continued striving — the "[[prophetic-reform|emigration of the mind]]" that never ends.

>
> — (001-004)

> "Yes, the strenuous spirit is a long time dead in Khalid. He is gradually reverting to the Oriental instinct. And when he is not loafing in Battery Park, carving his name on the bench, he is burrowing in the shelves of some secondhand bookshop or dreaming in the dome of some Broadway skyscraper. Does not this seem inevitable, however, considering the palingenetic burden within him? And is not loafing a necessary prelude to the travail?"
>
> — (008-012)

> "So long as one has faith in the general experience of mankind, as the philosophy of reason assures us, one should not despair of the human race."
>
> — (014-020)

> "From the cliffs 'neath which the lily blooms to the cliffs round which the eagles flit — what a glorious promontory! What a contrast at this height, in this immensity, to the depths and the density of the City!"
>
> — (029-003)
