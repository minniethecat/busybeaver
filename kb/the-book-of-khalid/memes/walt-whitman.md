## Role in the Book

Walt Whitman enters *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* at a moment of self-revelation. [[khalid|Khalid]] has just confessed a moment of vulnerable nostalgia — the [[love-sacred-and-profane|desire]] to be a "burro-boy again," sleeping among the cyclamens of his homeland. The narrator immediately interrupts with a rebuke delivered through Whitman:

> Shades of Whitman! But Whitman, thou Donkey, never weeps. Whitman, if that goblin tried to silence him, would have wrung his neck, after he had ridden upon it. [007-018]

Whitman functions here as the anti-sentimental corrective — a poet of such self-possession that he refuses the tears [[khalid|Khalid]] indulges. The epithet "thou Donkey" is affectionate, even admiring: [[khalid|Khalid]] is the donkey-boy who weeps for what he has lost; Whitman is the poet who rides the goblin of nostalgia rather than submitting to it. The passage identifies Whitman with a specifically [[new-york-city|America]]n form of resilience — democratic, egalitarian, unwilling to perform vulnerability.

Later, in a more esoteric passage, [[khalid|Khalid]]'s philosophical synthesis yokes "Mrs. Eddy and the Prophet Dowie under the yoke of Whitman," marking *Leaves of Grass* as the democratic framework within which even religious innovators must operate [020-004]. Whitman is invoked not merely as a poet but as a structuring principle: a way of holding contradictions together without resolving them.

## Biographical Sketch

Walt Whitman (1819–1892) was born on Long Island, [[new-york-city|New York]], and worked as a journalist, teacher, and government clerk before publishing *Leaves of Grass* in 1855 — a book he would revise and expand across nine editions until his death. His free-verse style, his celebration of the body and the democratic self, and his homoerotic frankness made him a controversial and transformative figure in [[new-york-city|America]]n letters.

For the Arab-[[new-york-city|America]]n writers of the Mahjar, Whitman represented a distinctly [[new-york-city|America]]n mode of poetic utterance: expansive, egalitarian, unashamed. His refusal of European poetic forms paralleled their own project of creating an Arabic literature freed from classical constraints. Rihani, who read Whitman in English and corresponded with [[new-york-city|America]]n literary figures, was among the first to introduce Whitman's sensibility into Arabic prose.

## Key Passages

> "Shades of Whitman! But Whitman, thou Donkey, never weeps. Whitman, if that goblin tried to silence him, would have wrung his neck, after he had ridden upon it."
>
> — [007-018]

This passage follows [[khalid|Khalid]]'s nostalgic lament for his lost burro-boy days. The narrator's apostrophe to Whitman — "thou Donkey" — is generically unstable: it mocks [[khalid|Khalid]]'s sentimentality while simultaneously elevating the donkey ([[khalid|Khalid]]'s humble origins) to a figure of Whitman-like independence. The "goblin" is the spirit of nostalgic self-pity; Whitman wrings its neck.

> "He drags in Mrs. Eddy and the Prophet Dowie yoked under the yoke of Whitman. He marks the *Key to Scripture* with blades from *Leaves of Grass*, and such fuel as he gathers from both, he lights with an ember borrowed from the chariot to Elijah."
>
> — [020-004]

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s syncretic method: Christian Science (Mrs. Eddy), faith-healing (Dowie), and Whitman's democratic poetry are "yoked" together. *Leaves of Grass* becomes the common ground on which otherwise incompatible religious visions can meet. The image of "blades from *Leaves of Grass*" used to mark a "Key to Scripture" literalizes Whitman's title — grass as both text and tool.

> "And in my heart I sing / O, let me be a burro-boy again; / O, let me sleep among the cyclamens / Of my own land."
>
> — [007-017]

Though not directly about Whitman, this verse fragment — the one that provokes the "Shades of Whitman!" rebuke — is essential to understanding the Whitman-[[khalid|Khalid]] dynamic. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s lyric is nostalgic, yearning, pastoral. The Whitmanian response is to refuse nostalgia: the poet does not look backward with tears but forward with appetite. The dialectic between these two poetic modes — the elegiac and the expansive — structures [[khalid|Khalid]]'s entire spiritual journey.

## Historical Context

Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* reached a global audience in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and the Mahjar (Arab diaspora) writers in [[new-york-city|New York]] encountered him directly. Rihani's invocation of Whitman is part of a broader pattern: Arab-[[new-york-city|America]]n writers of this period used [[new-york-city|America]]n literary figures to authorize their own departures from classical Arabic poetic norms.

The image of Whitman as a poet who "never weeps" aligns with the Arabic *fahl* (virile poet) tradition — the poet whose strength lies in self-mastery. But Rihani complicates this: [[khalid|Khalid]] does weep, and the novel does not judge him for it. Whitman is a model, not a master; his influence is absorbed and transformed, like [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s *Emile* — consumed as fuel for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own intellectual fire.
