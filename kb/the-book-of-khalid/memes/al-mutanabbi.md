## Role in the Book

Al-Mutanabbi (915–965), the tenth-century Arab poet, serves as [[shakib|Shakib]]'s literary lodestar throughout *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. His *divan* — a precious, dilapidated copy — physically accompanies the two protagonists from [[baalbek|Baalbek]] to [[new-york-city|New York]] and back to Syria, functioning as a cultural talisman against the spiritual dislocation of emigration. Where [[khalid|Khalid]] has his lute and his bottle of Ksarah wine, [[shakib|Shakib]] has al-Mutanabbi: the three objects form a trinity of sensual, spiritual, and intellectual resistance to the "desert of barren gold" that is [[tammany-hall|Tammany]]'s [[new-york-city|America]] [008-004].

[[shakib|Shakib]]'s attachment to al-Mutanabbi is more than literary taste; it is an assertion of Arabic cultural identity in a foreign land. The poet becomes a character in his own right — invoked in argument, quoted in moments of heightened emotion, and packed carefully alongside [[shakib|Shakib]]'s own manuscript at the novel's end [017-006][017-010]. Al-Mutanabbi's presence marks the book's central tension between [[east-west-duality|East and West]]: the classical Arabic tradition, embodied in poetry, stands against the crass materialism and "atheistical bones" of the New World [009-011].

## Biographical Sketch

The historical Abu al-Tayyib Ahmad ibn Husayn al-Mutanabbi (915–965 AD) was born in Kufa and rose to become the most celebrated poet of the Abbasid era. His *nom de plume* means "the would-be prophet," reflecting the audacious self-regard that characterized both his verse and his life. He served as court poet to Sayf al-Dawla in Aleppo before falling out of favor and wandering through the courts of Egypt and Persia, eventually killed by bandits near Baghdad.

Rihani invokes al-Mutanabbi not as a biographical subject but as a living presence in [[shakib|Shakib]]'s consciousness. The novel never recounts the historical details of the poet's life; instead, it treats his *divan* as a portable homeland — a book that, when opened, restores the world [[shakib|Shakib]] has left behind. The [[the-jesuits|Jesuit education]] at [[baalbek|Baalbek]] taught the students "enough of Arabic to enable one to parse and analyse the verse of al-Mutanabbi" [006-005], positioning the poet as the pinnacle of the Arabic literary curriculum.

## Key Passages

> Shakib takes out his favourite poet al-Mutanabbi, and Khalid, his favourite bottle, the choicest of the Ksarah distillery of the Jesuits.
>
> — [006-004]

This passage, set at the Marseille docks as the two protagonists depart for [[new-york-city|America]], establishes the symbolic economy of the novel: [[khalid|Khalid]]'s wine and music against [[shakib|Shakib]]'s poetry. The pairing recurs throughout the narrative — al-Mutanabbi is always contrasted with sensory pleasure, never subordinated to it.

> "The lute for Khalid, al-Mutanabbi for me — neither of us could forego his hobby — and Im-Hanna, affectionate, devoted as our mothers — these were the joys of our Saturday nights in our underground diggings."
>
> — [007-027]

[[shakib|Shakib]]'s own words from his autobiography identify al-Mutanabbi as one of three essential ingredients of happiness in the [[new-york-city|New York]] cellar. The poet is not a luxury but a necessity — as vital as companionship and the lute's music.

> "The fiery waves of al-Mutanabbi's poetry have always been my delight. In Battery Park I took especial pleasure in reading him."
>
> — [008-004]

[[shakib|Shakib]] reads Arabic poetry in [[battery-park|Battery Park]], the iconic [[new-york-city|New York]] public space. The juxtaposition of al-Mutanabbi's "fiery waves" with the Atlantic Ocean visible from the park creates a layered geography: the sea physically connects [[new-york-city|America]] to the Levant, while poetry spiritually connects [[shakib|Shakib]] to his homeland.

> "Better the ring of al-Mutanabbi's scimitars and spears than the clatter of these atheistical bones!"
>
> — [009-011]

[[khalid|Khalid]], attending an atheistic lecture, invokes al-Mutanabbi's martial imagery as a rebuke to Western secularism. The poet's "scimitars and spears" — metaphors for the power of Arabic verse — are preferred to the dry rationalism of the lecture hall. [[khalid|Khalid]], who elsewhere mocks [[shakib|Shakib]]'s devotion to the poet, here appropriates al-Mutanabbi for his own rhetorical purpose.

> These he packs in the bottom of his trunk, and with them his precious dilapidated copy of al-Mutanabbi, and — what MS. be this? What, a Book of Verse spawned in the cellar?
>
> — [017-006]

At the novel's conclusion, [[shakib|Shakib]] packs al-Mutanabbi alongside [[the-kl-manuscript|Khalid's manuscript]] — the "Book of Verse spawned in the cellar." The physical juxtaposition of the classical Arabic *divan* with [[khalid|Khalid]]'s new Syrian-[[new-york-city|America]]n verse suggests a lineage: [[khalid|Khalid]]'s poetic aspirations, however modern, descend from al-Mutanabbi's tradition.

## Historical Context

Al-Mutanabbi's presence in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* reflects the late *Nahda* (Arab Renaissance) context in which Rihani wrote. The revival of classical Arabic poetry was central to Arab cultural nationalism, and al-Mutanabbi — with his themes of pride, exile, and self-assertion — held particular resonance for the Syrian-Lebanese diaspora in [[new-york-city|America]]. Rihani, himself a pioneer of Arab-[[new-york-city|America]]n literature, uses al-Mutanabbi as shorthand for the cultural inheritance that emigration threatens to erase, and that [[shakib|Shakib]]'s scribal labor is meant to preserve.
