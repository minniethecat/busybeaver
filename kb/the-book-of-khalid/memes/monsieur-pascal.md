## Role in the Book

Blaise Pascal occupies a unique position among the Western thinkers who shape [[khalid|Khalid]]'s intellectual development. In Chapter IV of Book the First ("On the Wharf of Enchantment"), [[shakib|Shakib]] records that "Monsieur Pascal, with whom he quarrels before he burns, had a particular influence upon him" (004-010). Pascal is the thinker [[khalid|Khalid]] wrestles with most intimately — not absorbing him passively, but engaging him in genuine intellectual combat.

The chapter preserves two remarkable excerpts from the [[khedivial-library|Khedivial Library]] manuscript in which [[khalid|Khalid]] addresses Pascal directly: letters written in the second person that function as a philosophical dialogue across centuries. In these, [[khalid|Khalid]] respectfully but firmly rejects Pascal's theology of human wretchedness, proposing instead a vision of evolution and self-transcendence that anticipates Rihani's own synthetic philosophy.

## Biographical Sketch

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, inventor, and Catholic theologian. His *Pensées* (Thoughts), published posthumously in 1670, is a fragmentary but enormously influential defense of Christianity that emphasizes the "misery of man without God" and the necessity of divine grace. Pascal's famous wager argument — that believing in God is a rational bet because the potential reward of eternal happiness outweighs the finite cost of earthly belief — makes faith a matter of prudential calculation rather than experiential conviction.

For Rihani's [[khalid|Khalid]], Pascal represents both the intellectual seriousness of Western Christianity and its fundamental error: the insistence that human beings must despise themselves in order to approach God. [[khalid|Khalid]] "could not rest after reading his *Thoughts* until he read the Bible" (004-010), suggesting that Pascal served as a gateway to scriptural engagement that ultimately transcended Pascal's own theological framework.

## Key Passages

**The Letter to Pascal**

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s first letter to Pascal is a masterpiece of respectful disagreement. He begins by acknowledging his attempt to follow Pascal's spiritual counsel:

> "O Monsieur Pascal, I tried hard to hate and detest myself, as you advise, and I found that I could not by so doing love God. 'Tis in loving the divine in Man, in me, in you, that we rise to the love of our Maker." (004-010)

This passage crystalizes the central theological disagreement between [[khalid|Khalid]] and Pascal. For Pascal, self-abasement is the prerequisite for grace; for [[khalid|Khalid]], self-[[love-sacred-and-profane|love]] — understood as [[love-sacred-and-profane|love]] of the divine element within each person — is the only path upward.

**The Theory of Evolution Foreshadowed**

[[khalid|Khalid]] finds in Pascal's description of the Christian paradox — man as simultaneously "vile, base, abominable" and called to "resemble his Maker" — an unexpected anticipation of evolutionary theory:

> "Now, I see in this a foreshadowing of the theory of evolution, nay a divine warrant for it. Nor is it the Christian religion alone which unfolds to man the twofold mystery of his nature; others are as dark and as bright on either side of the pole." (004-010)

This is a characteristically Rihani-esque move: taking a fragment of orthodox Christian theology and reinterpreting it through a lens that is simultaneously scientific, comparative, and spiritually affirming. The "twofold mystery" of human nature is not, for [[khalid|Khalid]], a cause for despair but the very engine of spiritual evolution.

**The Tail and the Apotheosis**

The letter's closing lines contain one of the novel's most memorable images:

> "Philosophy conspiring with Biology will not consent to the apotheosis of Man, unless he wear on his breast a symbol of his tail." (004-010)

The image of Man ascending to divinity while wearing "a symbol of his tail" is a perfect encapsulation of Rihani's philosophy: transcendence does not come through denial of our animal nature, but through its acknowledgment and integration. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s parting words — "Au-revoir, Monsieur Pascal, Remember me to St. Augustine" — are both affectionate and gently mocking, a student taking leave of a teacher he has outgrown.

**The Turn to the Prophets**

[[shakib|Shakib]] notes that Pascal's influence did not end with [[khalid|Khalid]]'s rejection of his theology. Rather, Pascal opened a door that led [[khalid|Khalid]] deeper into scriptural tradition:

> He could not rest after reading his *Thoughts* until he read the Bible. And of the Prophets of the Old Testament he had an especial liking for Jeremiah and Isaiah. (004-010)

This is Pascal's paradoxical gift to [[khalid|Khalid]]: a version of Christianity so intellectually demanding that it drives the reader beyond Christianity itself, into the Hebrew prophetic tradition that [[khalid|Khalid]] finds more congenial.

## Historical Context

Pascal's *Pensées* was widely read among Arab intellectuals of the *Nahda* (Arab Renaissance) period, often in French. The work's fragmentary, aphoristic style — so different from the systematic theology of Aquinas or the scholastics — appealed to a generation seeking to reconcile religious tradition with modern rationality. Rihani's choice of Pascal (rather than, say, Descartes or Voltaire) as [[khalid|Khalid]]'s principal French interlocutor is significant: Pascal is the Western thinker who most deeply feels the anguish of the human condition, and it is precisely this anguish that [[khalid|Khalid]] must work through and transcend.
