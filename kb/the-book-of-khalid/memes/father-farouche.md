## Role in the Book

Father Farouche is a minor antagonist in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*, representing the moral corruption Rihani attributes to institutional religion — specifically the [[the-jesuits|Jesuit Order]]. He appears in two distinct episodes: first as the priest who visits [[khalid|Khalid]]'s family and denounces him as an infidel, and later as the matchmaker who schemes to wed [[najma|Najma]] to a titled Ottoman functionary while [[khalid|Khalid]] languishes in prison. In both episodes, Farouche embodies the alliance of clerical authority with political power that [[khalid|Khalid]]'s entire philosophy rejects.

His name — "Farouche" means "wild" or "fierce" in French — is almost certainly chosen by Rihani for its ironic dissonance with the priest's oily, calculating manner. The "three-cornered hat" becomes a recurring symbol of his Jesuit identity, a visual marker that the narrator deploys with savage contempt.

## Biographical Sketch

Father Farouche is a Jesuit priest stationed in Zahleh, a town in the Bekaa Valley not far from [[baalbek|Baalbek]]. The novel provides no details of his origins or personal history — he exists entirely as a function of his institutional role. He speaks "miserable Arabic" (007-001), suggesting that he may be of European origin rather than a native Arabic speaker, which adds a colonial dimension to his interference in Lebanese family life.

His first appearance occurs when he visits the home of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s parents under the pretext of courtesy, discovering [[khalid|Khalid]] writing a letter. Finding [[khalid|Khalid]]'s bearing irreverent, he denounces him as "not only an infidel, but he is also crazy" (007-002) and declares that "for such wretches there is an asylum here and a Jahannam hereafter." His report to the Jesuit network contributes to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s banishment from home.

His second appearance occurs years later, when he is "commissioned to come all the way from Zahleh" (008-016) to arrange a marriage between [[najma|Najma]] and a "third-class Medjidi Bey" — a holder of an Ottoman imperial decoration. He presses [[najma|Najma]]'s father to accept the match, exploiting [[khalid|Khalid]]'s imprisonment in [[damascus|Damascus]] to further the intrigue.

## Key Passages

**The First Visit**

[[shakib|Shakib]] records the initial encounter between Farouche and [[khalid|Khalid]] with compressed dramatic irony. The priest arrives expecting deference and finds instead a young man absorbed in writing:

> One day, before Khalid was banished, Shakib tells us, one of them, Father Farouche by name, comes to pay a visit of courtesy, and finds Khalid sitting cross-legged on a mat writing a letter. (007-001)

The detail of [[khalid|Khalid]] sitting "cross-legged on a mat" — absorbed in his own intellectual world — contrasts with Farouche's formal, intrusive presence.

**The Anathema**

After [[khalid|Khalid]] walks out of the house rather than endure the priest's interrogation, Farouche condemns him to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s mother:

> "Your son," addressing the mother as he stands under the door-lintel, "is not only an infidel, but he is also crazy. And for such wretches there is an asylum here and a Jahannam hereafter." (007-002)

The judgment combines the temporal threat of the asylum with the eternal threat of hell — a fusion of worldly and spiritual power that epitomizes the institutional religion [[khalid|Khalid]] rejects.

**The Three-Cornered Hat**

Rihani transforms Farouche's clerical headgear into a symbol of Jesuit duplicity:

> That hat, which can outwit the monk's hood and the hundred fabled devils under it, that hat, with its many gargoyles, a visible symbol of the leaky conscience of the Jesuit, that hat, O Khalid, which you would have kicked out of your house, has eventually succeeded in ousting **you**, and will do its mighty best yet to send you to the Bosphorus. (007-031)

The passage builds rhetorical momentum through repetition of "that hat" — each iteration adding a new layer of accusation — until the hat itself becomes a character in the drama, more vivid and menacing than the priest who wears it.

**The Marriage Scheme**

In his second appearance, Farouche operates as a marriage broker for Ottoman officialdom:

> Now, Father Farouche is commissioned to come all the way from Zahleh to visit the brother of Abu-Khalid their porter, and bespeak him in the interest of his daughter. All their faculties of persuasion shall be exerted in behalf of Najma. She must be saved at any cost. (008-016)

The phrase "saved at any cost" drips with irony: the salvation Farouche offers is marriage to a decorated functionary of the same Ottoman system that [[khalid|Khalid]] fights against. [[najma|Najma]] flees to the terrace roof rather than receive the visitors.

**The Medjidi Dodo**

When the suitor arrives alone for a third visit, [[najma|Najma]]'s response is swift and physical:

> The third time the third-class Medjidi Dodo comes alone. And Najma, as soon as she catches a glimpse of him, takes up her earthen jar and hies her to the spring. (008-021)

The epithet "Medjidi Dodo" — conflating the Ottoman decoration with the extinct, ungainly bird — is one of Rihani's sharpest satirical touches. [[najma|Najma]]'s flight to the spring with her water jar is her only available form of refusal.

## Historical Context

[[the-jesuits|The Jesuits]] were active in Lebanon from the seventeenth century, founding the Université Saint-Joseph in [[beirut|Beirut]] in 1875. During the Hamidian era, the Jesuit network in the Ottoman Empire operated with considerable autonomy, and their relationship with [[abdul-hamid|the Sultan]]'s government was complex — sometimes adversarial, sometimes collaborative. Rihani's portrayal of Farouche as an informant for the Hamidian regime reflects a wider Arab intellectual suspicion of missionary orders as instruments of foreign intervention.

The "Medjidi" decoration referenced in the marriage-scheme episode was an Ottoman imperial order established by Sultan Abdul Mejid I in 1852, awarded to civil and military officials. A "third-class Medjidi Bey" would be a relatively minor functionary — the irony being that even this low-level Ottoman honor is deployed as leverage against [[najma|Najma]]'s family.
