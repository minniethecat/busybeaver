## Overview

The Maronite Church is an Eastern Catholic church in full communion with Rome, tracing its origins to the fourth-century hermit Saint Maron. It is the dominant Christian denomination in Mount Lebanon, and it governs the spiritual, communal, and — as Rihani dramatizes — the financial life of Khalid's homeland. In *The Book of Khalid*, the Maronite Church serves as the institutional face of organized religion, and Rihani subjects it to the same satirical scrutiny he applies to Tammany Hall and the Ottoman state.

## Role in the Book

The Maronite Church appears in the book as both a pastoral presence and a predatory institution. Its most extended treatment comes in Chapter 20, "The Howdaj of Falsehood," where Khalid seeks to marry his cousin Najma and collides with the Church's system of canonical prohibitions and dispensations. A Maronite priest informs Khalid that the marriage, being between cousins, requires the Bishop's sanction — obtainable "on payment of five hundred piasters" [020-003].

What follows is one of the book's most sustained anticlerical set-pieces. Khalid, indignant, interrogates the priest: if the marriage is truly unlawful by divine law, no amount of money can make it lawful; if it is only unlawful by canon law, then the Church is selling dispensations rather than protecting souls. His conclusion is devastating: "You bind for the sake of loosening, when a good bait is on the hook" [020-022].

Rihani extends the satire to the wider Maronite community. The "dear good Ancient of the Maronites must willy-nilly obey Rome" [020-029], and the phrase "marmot-like or Maronite-like" [022-007] reduces the proud identity to a near-homophone with hibernation — a sly dig at religious conformity. Even the Patriarch of the Maronites, paired with the Sultan of the Ottomans, represents institutional authority that is "certainly more certain" of its own importance than of anything else [029-017].

## Key Passages

> "An alm of a few gold pieces will remove the obstacle; the unlawfulness of your marriage resulting from consanguinity will cease on payment of five hundred piasters."
>
> — [020-003]

The priest's transactionalism, delivered with oily calm over coffee and cigarettes on Khalid's divan, sets the stage for the chapter's theological confrontation.

> "What is the use of binding, O Reverend Father, when a little sum of money can loosen anything you bind? It seems to me that these prohibitions of the Church are only made for the purpose of collecting alms. In other words, you bind for the sake of loosening, when a good bait is on the hook, do you not?"
>
> — [020-022]

Khalid's challenge to the "Bind-and-Loosen" economy is the moral core of the novel's critique of institutional religion. The passage echoes Rihani's own religious skepticism — he was raised Maronite but became a freethinker.

> "What is unlawful by virtue of the Divine Law the wealth of all the Trust-Kings of America can not make lawful. And what is so by virtue of your Canon Law concerns not me."
>
> — [020-022]

Khalid draws a sharp line between divine law and ecclesiastical regulation, a distinction that reflects the broader anti-clerical currents of the late Ottoman *nahda* (Arab Renaissance).

> "And the Patriarch of the Maronites and the Sultan of the Ottomans — 'tis curious how the Sultan of the Ottomans can serve the interests of Abd'ul Hamid."
>
> — [029-017]

Rihani pairs the Maronite Patriarch with the Ottoman Sultan as twin pillars of institutional authority — each serving its own interests under the guise of serving the people.

## Historical Context

The Maronite Church, centered in the Qadisha Valley of Mount Lebanon, maintained a delicate autonomy through centuries of Ottoman rule. By the late nineteenth century, Maronite clerics were among the leading figures of the Arab *nahda* — yet Rihani portrays them not as cultural luminaries but as bureaucratic gatekeepers. This dissonance reflects the tension within the *nahda* between clerical traditionalism and secular reformism. The novel's anticlericalism also mirrors a broader transatlantic phenomenon: Khalid's critique of the "Bind-and-Loosen" system echoes contemporaneous American and European critiques of Catholic indulgences, repositioned within an Eastern Christian context.
