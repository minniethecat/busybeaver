# Brooklyn Bridge

## Location & Geography

The Brooklyn Bridge, completed in 1883, spans the East River connecting [[new-york-city|Manhattan]] and Brooklyn in [[new-york-city|New York City]]. At the time of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s arrival in the 1890s, it was one of the engineering marvels of the world — the longest suspension bridge ever built, with its distinctive Gothic-towered stone pylons and web of steel cables. For immigrants approaching [[new-york-city|New York]] harbour, the bridge was, alongside the Statue of Liberty, an instantly recognisable symbol of [[new-york-city|America]]n industrial might. [[khalid|Khalid]] beholds it from the steamer as he enters [[new-york-city|New York]] harbour: "There is the Brooklyn Bridge, and here is the Statue of Liberty which people speak of, and which are as famous as the [[mount-lebanon|Cedars of Lebanon]]" [007-003].

## Significance in the Book

The Brooklyn Bridge appears at two distinct moments in [[khalid|Khalid]]'s [[new-york-city|America]]n sojourn, framing his relationship with the New World. At his **arrival**, the bridge is one of the "giant structures tickling heaven's sides" and "cable bridges, spanning rivers, uniting cities" [007-003] that [[shakib|Shakib]] enumerates to rouse [[khalid|Khalid]] from his reverie. It is a shorthand for [[new-york-city|America]]n technological sublimity — the industrial sublime that greets the Eastern emigrant. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s comparison to the [[mount-lebanon|Cedars of Lebanon]] is characteristically double-edged: the bridge is placed on equal footing with the ancient trees, but the "as famous as" formulation suggests a contest of values — engineered stone versus organic growth, modern fame versus ancient rootedness.

Later, during his **cellar-dwelling period** in [[new-york-city|New York]], [[khalid|Khalid]] composes a comic poem addressed to the bridge: "O Brooklyn Bridge, how oft upon thy back / I tramped, and once I crossed thee in a hack" [017-008]. The couplet, preserved in [[shakib|Shakib]]'s transcription of the K.L. Manuscript, is deliberately bad poetry — part of a series of parodic verses that includes odes to a soda-water fountain and the virtues of modern science. The bridge here is domesticated, reduced from sublime monument to quotidian footpath. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s "tramping" on its back drains it of grandeur; the hackney cab rhyme ("back" / "hack") completes the deflation. This is [[khalid|Khalid]] the ironist at work — the same man who once stood awestruck before the bridge now writes doggerel about it, having thoroughly absorbed and disenchanted the [[new-york-city|America]]n sublime.
