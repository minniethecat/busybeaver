## Overview

The Ottoman Empire is the centuries-old Islamic empire that controlled the Levant — including Mount Lebanon, Beirut, Damascus, and Baalbek — during the events of *The Book of Khalid*. By the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the Empire was widely referred to as "the sick man of Europe," its administrative machinery creaking under debt, bureaucratic corruption, and nationalist pressures. Rihani portrays this decay through satirical asides about Turkish officialdom, the Sultan's empty decorations, and the moral rot that drives young men like Khalid to emigrate.

## Role in the Book

The Ottoman Empire serves as the political backdrop against which Khalid's early life and eventual emigration unfold. The novel treats the Empire with Rihani's characteristic blend of satire and lament: it is both a once-great civilization now reduced to "scrub monuments" [005-007] and a system of bureaucratic predation that forces its subjects to flee. Khalid and Shakib are "smuggled through the port of Beirut at night" [006-002] to escape its reach. The Empire's decline is thematically tied to the East–West duality that pervades the book — the Ottoman East represents the stagnation Khalid must leave behind to seek knowledge and freedom in the West.

Later, when Khalid returns from America as a would-be reformer, the Ottoman order reappears as the object of his critique. His speeches denounce "Turkish blatherskite" [033-015] and he envisions standing before a "Turkish Areopagus at Constantinople" [034-006] to deliver his message. The Empire is thus both the thing fled and the thing he feels compelled to confront.

## Key Passages

> "In the City Square, as we issue from the congested windings of the Bazaar, we are greeted by one of those scrub monuments that are found in almost every city of the Ottoman Empire."
>
> — [005-007]

The "scrub monument" is Rihani's emblem for Ottoman decay: a once-grand imperial aesthetic reduced to shabby reproduction, as ubiquitous as it is uninspiring.

> "In their baggy, lapping trousers and crimson caps, each carrying a bundle and a rug under his arm, Shakib and Khalid are smuggled through the port of Beirut at night, and safely rowed to the steamer."
>
> — [006-002]

Khalid's emigration is an act of escape from Ottoman jurisdiction. The details of disguise and nocturnal flight underscore the Empire's role as a controlling, extractive force that a young man must evade rather than petition.

> "Now, the mighty troops of the Sultan of the Ottomans come forth like ants from the earth."
>
> — [020-038]

Rihani's use of "ants" to describe the Sultan's forces strips them of grandeur — a satirical inversion of the imperial sublime that recurs throughout the book.

> "And the Patriarch of the Maronites and the Sultan of the Ottomans — 'tis curious how the Sultan of the Ottomans can serve the interests of Abd'ul Hamid."
>
> — [029-017]

The Sultan's subservience to his own title — "the Sultan serving the Sultan" — is Rihani's concise satire of Ottoman institutional emptiness.

## Historical Context

The Ottoman Empire in Rihani's time (c. 1876–1911) was under the rule of Sultan Abd'ul Hamid II (r. 1876–1909), whose reign saw the rise of the Young Turk movement, the 1908 Constitutional Revolution, and the Empire's continuing loss of Balkan territories. Rihani, writing as a Lebanese Maronite emigrant in New York, had a complex relationship with Ottoman identity: he wrote in both Arabic and English, and his advocacy for Arab cultural revival positioned him ambiguously between Ottoman loyalism and Arab separatism. *The Book of Khalid* (1911) reflects this ambivalence — it mocks the Empire's decay but does not embrace nationalist alternatives uncritically.
