# Mrs. Gotfry

## Role in the Book

Mrs. Gotfry (also rendered "Goodfree") is an [[new-york-city|America]]n woman living in [[beirut|Beirut]] who has become a votary of [[abbas-effendi|Abbas Effendi]] — a leader of [[bahaullah|the Baha]]'i faith. She appears in Book III as an intellectual and spiritual interlocutor for [[khalid|Khalid]] after his return to the East. Their conversations on the terrace facing the Mediterranean represent one of the book's most sustained encounters between [[east-west-duality|East and West]], and between male and female perspectives on religion.

Mrs. Gotfry embodies a particular type: the Western spiritual seeker who turns to Eastern religions for what Western Christianity seems to lack. "She is surprised how a Syrian of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s mind can not see the beauties of Babism, or Bahaism, as it is now called" (030-010). Her genuine faith challenges [[khalid|Khalid]]'s skepticism, while his philosophical questioning tests her convictions.

## Biographical Sketch

Little is revealed of Mrs. Gotfry's background beyond her [[new-york-city|America]]n origin and her residence in [[beirut|Beirut]]. She is introduced through a Syrian friend of [[khalid|Khalid]]'s, [[ahmed-bey|Ahmed Bey]], and hosts a lunch on her terrace facing the sea. The narrator notes her attraction to "the lofty spirituality of the Bab" (030-010) — the founder of Babism, precursor to [[bahaullah|the Baha]]'i faith.

Her relationship with [[khalid|Khalid]] is intellectual and possibly romantic — the text is characteristically ambiguous. What is clear is that she represents a mode of cross-cultural spiritual seeking that mirrors and inverts [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own: she, the Westerner, seeking truth in the East; he, the Easterner, bringing Western critical thought back to his homeland.
