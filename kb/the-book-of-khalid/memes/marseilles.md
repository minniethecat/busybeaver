# Marseilles

## Location & Geography

Marseilles is the historic Mediterranean port city of southern France, situated on the Gulf of Lion. In Rihani's narrative, it functions as the pivotal European transit point between the Levant and the New World — the liminal space where the Orient's "baggy trousers and crimson caps" are stripped away and the emigrant is forcibly transformed for his [[new-york-city|America]]n destination [006-006]. For [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]], Marseilles is not a city of culture or commerce but a gauntlet of exploitation: "the verminous hostelries of Marseilles" [006-007] where the emigrant's purse — and with it his dignity — is systematically emptied.

## Significance in the Book

Marseilles occupies a critical position in [[khalid|Khalid]]'s journey from [[baalbek|Baalbek]] to [[new-york-city|America]]. It is the second station of the emigrant's Via Dolorosa, as [[shakib|Shakib]] names it: "the [[beirut|Port of Beirut]], the verminous hostelries of Marseilles, the [[ellis-island|Island of Ellis]] in [[new-york-city|New York]], are the three stations thereof" [006-007]. Here the emigrant undergoes what Rihani ironises as a three-act theatrical fleecing — the "Sorry Show" of the emigrant trade [006-009]. The sharpers of Marseilles strip [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] not only of their money but of their outward identity: the baggy trousers and crimson cap are replaced with linsey-woolsey and hispid felt [006-006]. Marseilles is where the Oriental self is unmade before the [[new-york-city|America]]n self can be born — a violent metamorphosis that prefigures the deeper cultural transformations [[khalid|Khalid]] will undergo in [[new-york-city|New York]].

The city also serves as Rihani's indictment of the predatory emigrant-trade infrastructure that linked Ottoman ports to [[new-york-city|America]]n immigration stations. The sharpers "hover around the emigrant" like "rapacious bats" [006-006], exploiting linguistic and cultural vulnerability. Marseilles thus becomes a synecdoche for European predation on Eastern hopefuls — the Old World's final betrayal before the New World's uncertain promise.

## Key Passages

> "But this happy humour is assailed at Marseilles. His placidity and stolid indifference are rudely shaken by the sharpers, who differ only from the boatmen of Beirut in that they wear pantaloons and intersperse their Arabic with a jargon of French."
>
> — [006-006]

Rihani draws a direct parallel between the boatmen of [[beirut|Beirut]] and the sharpers of Marseilles — both prey on the emigrant, differing only in costume and tongue. The shift from "baggy trousers" to "pantaloons" marks the emigrant's passage from one system of exploitation to another, more sophisticated one. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s celebrated "placidity and stolid indifference" — his philosophical armour — is "rudely shaken" by the encounter, signalling how European cunning punctures Oriental fatalism.

> "The voyage to America is the Via Dolorosa of the emigrant; and the Port of Beirut, the verminous hostelries of Marseilles, the Island of Ellis in New York, are the three stations thereof."
>
> — [006-007]

[[shakib|Shakib]]'s squib frames the emigrant journey as a Passion narrative, with Marseilles as the second station of suffering. The adjective "verminous" collapses physical squalor and moral corruption — the hostelries are both infested and infesting, breeding exploitation as surely as they breed lice. The Christological overtones (Via Dolorosa, three stations, potential "crucifixion" at [[ellis-island|Ellis Island]]) elevate the emigrant's ordeal from personal misfortune to archetypal trial.

> "Now, Khalid and Shakib, after passing a fortnight in Marseilles, and going through the Fourth Act of the Sorry Show, find their dignity as merchants rudely crushed beneath the hatches of the Atlantic steamer."
>
> — [006-009]

The "Sorry Show" — Rihani's bitterly ironic name for the emigrant-trade theatre — reaches its "Fourth Act" in Marseilles. The theatrical metaphor recasts exploitation as farce: the emigrant is both spectator and victim, paying admission to his own fleecing. The "fortnight" of detention is the sharpers' standard interval — long enough to drain the purse, short enough to maintain the fiction of service. What leaves Marseilles is not a merchant with capital but a refugee with "a little trachoma in his eyes" [006-006].

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, when [[khalid|Khalid]]'s voyage is set (c. 1890s), Marseilles was the principal embarkation port for Levantine emigrants bound for the [[new-york-city|America]]s. French shipping lines — particularly the Compagnie Générale Transatlantique and the Messageries Maritimes — dominated the Mediterranean passenger trade. The "sharpers" Rihani depicts were a documented phenomenon: commission agents, lodging-house keepers, and ticket brokers who extracted fees from emigrants through a system of kickbacks and enforced delays. Contemporary consular reports from the period record complaints about "emigrant runners" in Marseilles who detained travellers in overpriced hostelries under the pretence of arranging passage — precisely the "three-act" scheme Rihani describes. The fortnight's detention was a standard tactic: long enough to exhaust the emigrant's funds, short enough to evade police attention. Marseilles thus represents not merely a narrative device but a real node in the global infrastructure of mass migration that moved millions from the Ottoman Empire to the [[new-york-city|America]]s between 1880 and 1914.
