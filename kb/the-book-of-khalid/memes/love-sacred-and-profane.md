# Love Sacred and Profane

## Definition

Love Sacred and Profane names the fundamental duality of love in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* — a tension between spiritual devotion and carnal desire that mirrors the book's larger East-West, spirit-matter dialectic. [[khalid|Khalid]] experiences love not as a single emotion but as a battlefield where "the poison and the antidote are frequently offered together" (011-016).

Rihani draws on both Sufi traditions (where earthly love is a metaphor for divine love) and Western romanticism (where love is an end in itself). The result is a concept of love that is neither purely spiritual nor purely sensual but perpetually oscillating between the two poles — like [[khalid|Khalid]] himself, caught between civilizations.

## Development in the Book

The theme first appears in [[khalid|Khalid]]'s early life in Lebanon, where his inability to "learn to love" in the context of "the company of muleteers" (005-009) suggests a nature too refined for coarse expressions of desire. The "jasmine for your soul" (005-006) motif — the flower-seller's cry in the [[beirut|Beirut]] marketplace — establishes the connection between sensory beauty and spiritual longing.

In [[bohemianism|Bohemia]], the duality intensifies. [[khalid|Khalid]] plunges into sensual indulgence with the "houris," and his "immanent morality and intellectualism suffered an interregnum" (011-017). The narrator describes how "one woman complemented another. What the svelte brunette lacked, the steatopygous blonde amply supplied" (011-018). Yet even in dissolution, [[khalid|Khalid]] seeks something beyond pleasure — "Delicacy and intensity, effervescence and depth, these he would have in a woman, or a harem, as in anything else" (011-018). He is a sensualist who demands metaphysical satisfaction from physical experience.

In Book III, the sacred dimension reasserts itself through [[khalid|Khalid]]'s encounter with [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]], a Western woman drawn to Bahaism — "the lofty spirituality of the Bab" (030-010). She forgives [[khalid|Khalid]] his heresies, and their relationship suggests a possibility of love that transcends the sacred-profane binary. But the book does not resolve the tension — it deepens it.
