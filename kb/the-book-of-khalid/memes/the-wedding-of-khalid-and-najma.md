## Overview

The attempted wedding of Khalid to his cousin Najma is the central personal drama of Book II of *The Book of Khalid*. After returning from America, Khalid seeks to marry Najma — the "one star in my sky" [018-007] he has loved since childhood. What follows is a collision between love and institutional religion: the Maronite Church demands a dispensation fee for the marriage between cousins, Khalid refuses on principle, and the Jesuits intervene to prevent the union. The wedding becomes a tragedy of bureaucratic obstruction, ending with Najma married to another man while Khalid arrives too late.

## Narrative

### Preparations

The wedding preparations are detailed with Rihani's characteristic blend of tenderness and irony: "The sky-blue, many-ruffled wedding gown; the set-out for the wayfare; the camel and donkeys; the little stock of books; the coffee utensils; the lentils and sweet oil — all ready?" [020-003]. Khalid and Najma plan not merely a ceremony but a departure — the wedding is to launch their shared life in the desert, away from the corruptions of community and Church.

### The Church's Obstruction

The parish priest visits to explain that the marriage, being between cousins, requires a Bishop's dispensation — obtainable "on payment of five hundred piasters" [020-003]. For three successive Sundays, the intended marriage must be published in church, inviting any objections from the community.

Khalid's response is immediate and furious. He refuses to pay what he sees as a bribe: "What is the use of binding, O Reverend Father, when a little sum of money can loosen anything you bind? It seems to me that these prohibitions of the Church are only made for the purpose of collecting alms" [020-022]. His principled stand — that divine law cannot be bought and that canon law has no hold on him — alienates the ecclesiastical authorities.

### Jesuit Interference

The Jesuits, already hostile to Khalid for his freethinking views, work behind the scenes to prevent the marriage. "They would have succeeded, the mother and cousin of Khalid, in persuading the parish priest to accept from them the prescribed alms and perform the wedding ceremony, had not the Jesuits, in the interest of the Faith and the Church, been dogging Khalid still" [020-027]. The Jesuits' motive is explicit: if they cannot send Khalid to Constantinople, they will ensure he is exiled from Baalbek society.

### The Zeffah

The wedding's climax is Khalid's return from a brief imprisonment — itself perhaps arranged by his enemies — to find Najma's wedding procession already in the street:

> "We continued apace until we reached the byway through which we had to pass, and lo, we find it choked by the *zeffah* (wedding procession) of none but she and the third-class Medjidi."
>
> — [020-054]

The "third-class Medjidi" — an Ottoman decoration — indicates that Najma has been married to a minor Ottoman functionary. Khalid arrives at the very moment the procession passes, too late to intervene. The wedding he fought the Church to achieve has been given to another man, arranged by the same ecclesiastical authorities he defied.

## Key Passages

> "Every Sunday, for three such to come, the intended marriage of Khalid to Najma will be published in the Church, and whoso hath any objection to make can come forth and make it."
>
> — [020-003]

The public announcement of the banns transforms what should be a private union into a communal judgment, giving the village — and the Church — veto power over Khalid's heart.

> "They would have succeeded, the mother and cousin of Khalid, in persuading the parish priest to accept from them the prescribed alms and perform the wedding ceremony, had not the Jesuits, in the interest of the Faith and the Church, been dogging Khalid still."
>
> — [020-027]

The Jesuits' intervention reveals the wedding as a political and theological battlefield, not merely a personal milestone.

> "We find it choked by the *zeffah* (wedding procession) of none but she and the third-class Medjidi."
>
> — [020-054]

The wedding procession — the *zeffah*, a traditional Arabic celebration with drums and ululation — becomes a scene of loss rather than joy: Khalid's beloved is celebrating her marriage to another man.

## Significance

The wedding plot crystallizes the novel's critique of institutional religion. Khalid's refusal to pay the dispensation fee is not cheapness but principle: he distinguishes between divine law (which he might respect) and canon law (which he dismisses as a fundraising mechanism). His defeat — Najma married to a Medjidi-decorated Ottoman functionary — is one of the novel's darkest turns, demonstrating that the forces of institutional authority (Church, Empire, community) will crush individual conscience when it threatens their interests.
