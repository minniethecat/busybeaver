## Introduction

Mount Lebanon is never merely scenery in Rihani's *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. It is the novel's geographical anchor — the place [[khalid|Khalid]] departs from, dreams of returning to, and spiritually inhabits even when physically absent. The narrator describes how [[khalid|Khalid]]'s Genius, when not dancing or setting fires in [[new-york-city|New York]], is "flirting with the monks of the Lebanon hills." The mountains are the source of the novel's pastoral spirituality, the counterforce to [[new-york-city|New York]]'s industrial sublime.

## The Threshold of Departure

The mountains serve as the visual frame for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s departure to [[new-york-city|America]]. Smuggled out of [[beirut|Beirut]] at night, [[shakib|Shakib]] and [[khalid|Khalid]] take one last look at the landscape that shaped them:

> One more longing, lingering glance behind, and the dusky peaks of the Lebanons, beyond which their native City of Baal is sleeping in peace, recede from view. On the high sea of hope and joy they sail; "under the Favonian wind of enthusiasm, on the friendly billows of boyish dreams," they roll.

The "dusky peaks" receding from view is the novel's primal image of emigration — the homeland shrinking to a silhouette, then vanishing. The mountains are the last Eastern thing [[khalid|Khalid]] sees, and they will haunt his imagination throughout his [[new-york-city|America]]n years.

## The Cedars and the Call to the West

The Cedars of Lebanon, the ancient grove that was already a symbol of national identity in Rihani's time, play a pivotal role in the novel's plot. It is at the Cedars that [[khalid|Khalid]] and [[shakib|Shakib]] make the decision to emigrate:

> One day, following some tourists to the Cedars, they behold from Dahr'ul-Qadhib the sun setting in the Mediterranean and make up their minds to follow it too. "For the sundown," writes Shakib, "was more appealing to us than the sunrise, ay, more beautiful. The one was so near, the other so far away. Yes, we beheld the Hesperian light that day, and praised Allah. It was the New World's bonfire of hospitality: the sun called to us, and we obeyed."

The sunset is westward-facing, and the West here is literal — the direction of the Mediterranean, of Europe, and beyond it, [[new-york-city|America]]. [[shakib|Shakib]]'s logic inverts the conventional preference for sunrise (the East, origins, tradition): the sundown is "more beautiful" because it is "so near" — reachable, actionable, a decision that can be made now rather than a nostalgic return to origins. The Cedars become the platform from which the future is sighted.

## The Dream of Return

Even as [[khalid|Khalid]] sails toward [[new-york-city|America]], Mount Lebanon returns in his dream. On the steamer deck, after being "rudely jostled from their dreams by a spurt from the hose of the sailors," [[khalid|Khalid]] recounts a vision in which he rides in triumph "through the desert" to "[[new-york-city|the City]] on the Hills of the Cedar Groves":

> "I was riding through the desert. Whereto and wherefrom, I know not. But those who followed me seemed to know; for they cried, 'Long have we waited for thee, now we shall enter in peace.' ... until we reached the City on the Hills of the Cedar Groves. Outside the gate, we were met by the most beautiful of its tawny women ... And she, taking a necklace of cowries from a bag that hung on her breast, placed it on my head, saying, 'I crown thee King of—'"

The dream is interrupted by the sailors' hose — a blunt allegory for how the New World repeatedly cuts short the reveries of the old. The "City on the Hills of the Cedar Groves" is never named because it does not exist; it is a composite of [[baalbek|Baalbek]], [[damascus|Damascus]], and an idealized Lebanon that exists only in exile's imagination. The Cedar Groves crown the hills of the dream-city because they are the irreducible marker of home.

## Cedars as Symbol of Home

When [[khalid|Khalid]] arrives in [[new-york-city|New York]] harbor, the narrator explicitly connects the New World's monuments to the Cedars:

> "There is the Brooklyn Bridge, and here is the Statue of Liberty which people speak of, and which are as famous as the Cedars of Lebanon."

The comparison is telling: the Cedars are the standard of fame by which even the Statue of Liberty must be measured. In [[khalid|Khalid]]'s mental geography, the ancient trees outweigh the modern colossus. The [[brooklyn-bridge|Brooklyn Bridge]] and the Statue of Liberty are wonders of engineering, but the Cedars are wonders of being — older, deeper, organically rooted rather than constructed. This is the core of Rihani's argument: the West can build marvels, but the East has marvels that grow.
