## Role in the Book

Sultan Abdul Hamid II is the shadowy antagonist of Rihani's political imagination in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*. He never appears as a character, yet his presence — "Serene Majesty at Yildiz" — looms over the novel's political landscape as the personification of all that [[khalid|Khalid]] opposes: autocracy, censorship, religious hypocrisy, and the instrumental use of clerics as informants. The novel spans the final years of his reign (1876–1909), and his dethronement in the Young Turk Revolution serves as a crucial political backdrop to the later chapters.

Rihani deploys the Sultan as a rhetorical counterweight to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s democratic idealism. The "Government of Abdul Hamid" is described as "committing all its crimes in the interest of education," a bitter irony that [[khalid|Khalid]] exposes in his public writings. The Sultan's spies and censors — including the Jesuits who "act as spies to the criminal Government of Abdul Hamid" (007-031) — represent the alliance of religious institutional power with political despotism that [[khalid|Khalid]] fights against.

## Biographical Sketch

Abdul Hamid II (1842–1918) was the 34th Sultan of the Ottoman Empire, reigning from 1876 to 1909. He came to power promising constitutional reform — the First Constitutional Era — but within two years suspended the constitution and parliament, ruling as an absolute monarch for the next three decades. His reign was marked by the Hamidian massacres of Armenians (1894–1896), widespread censorship, and an extensive network of spies and informants.

In [[the-book-of-khalid-work|Rihani's novel]], Abdul Hamid functions as a symbol rather than a fully drawn historical figure. His name appears in contexts that highlight specific abuses: the pilfering of [[baalbek|Baalbek]]'s antiquities for the Berlin Museum ("when the German Emperor came, Abdul Hamid blinked, and the Berlin Museum is now the richer for it," 001-037), the censorship of the press, and the alliance between the Ottoman state and reactionary religious forces.

The novel's timeline is marked against his reign: [[khalid|Khalid]] gives his "banquet to the gods" at the River Adonis "in the thirty-fourth year of the reign of Abdul Hamid" (019-004), and the Bull is burned in the town-square of [[baalbek|Baalbek]] "in the last year of the reign of Abdul Hamid" (008-023) — the symbolic destruction of tyranny coinciding with the Sultan's fall.

## Key Passages

**The Theft of [[baalbek|Baalbek]]'s Antiquities**

Rihani's first mention of Abdul Hamid sets the tone for all subsequent references. [[baalbek|Baalbek]]'s Roman ruins — "the porphyry pillars, the statues, the tablets, the exquisite friezes" — have survived "Time and the Turks," but only just:

> The porphyry pillars, the statues, the tablets, the exquisite friezes, the palimpsests, the bas-reliefs — Time and the Turks have spared a few of these. And when the German Emperor came, Abdul Hamid blinked, and the Berlin Museum is now the richer for it. (001-037)

The verb "blinked" is devastating in its simplicity: the Sultan's willing blindness enables the imperial plunder of his own patrimony.

**The Jesuit-Sultan Alliance**

In one of the novel's most scathing passages, Rihani draws a direct line between Jesuit intrigue and Hamidian tyranny. After [[father-farouche|Father Farouche]]'s visit to [[khalid|Khalid]]'s home, the narrator indicts the Jesuit order for collaborating with the Sultan's spy network:

> Indeed, to serve their purpose, these honest servitors of Jesus will even act as spies to the criminal Government of Abdul Hamid. (007-031)

The phrase "criminal Government" leaves no room for diplomatic equivocation — it is the language of outright condemnation.

**The Ironic Defense of Tyranny**

When [[khalid|Khalid]] publishes an article criticizing the Hamidian regime, his opponents deploy the classic defense of autocracy — the sacredness of the Sultan's person:

> "Was the Government of Abdul Hamid, committing all its crimes in the interest of education, were we being trained by the Censorship and the Bosphorus Terror for the *Dustur*?" "But the person of Majesty, the sacredness of the Caliphate," cried the others. (025-004)

The phrase "committing all its crimes in the interest of education" is a masterwork of irony: the Sultan's apologists claim that censorship and terror are pedagogical tools, training the people for constitutional government by denying them every constitutional right.

**The Boatmen as Sultan-Metaphor**

After the Young Turk Revolution, Rihani notes that the tyranny has not disappeared — it has merely been redistributed. The boatmen of [[beirut|Beirut]] now wield the arbitrary power once held by the Sultan:

> Every one of them, in his swagger and bluster, is an Abdul Hamid. (025-006)

The Sultan's name has become a common noun, a metaphor for any petty tyrant who "rides the whirlwind and directs the storm."

**The Dethronement**

The novel's closing chapters register Abdul Hamid's fall as a turning point in the characters' lives. [[najma|Najma]]'s husband "after the dethronement of Abdul Hamid, being implicated in the reaction-movement, fled the country" (036-011), linking the Sultan's personal fate to the novel's domestic tragedy.

## Historical Context

Abdul Hamid II's reign coincided with the period of Rihani's own youth in Lebanon (then part of Ottoman Syria) and his early years in [[new-york-city|America]]. The Sultan's censorship apparatus directly affected Arab intellectuals of Rihani's generation. The Young Turk Revolution of 1908, which forced Abdul Hamid to restore the constitution, occurred just three years before *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* was published, making the novel's political commentary startlingly contemporary.

Rihani's treatment of Abdul Hamid reflects the broader Arab nationalist critique of Ottoman rule that was gaining force in this period. Yet Rihani's target is not the Ottoman Empire as such — he reserves respect for genuine [[the-ulema|Islamic scholars]]hip and spirituality — but specifically the alliance of autocratic power with institutional religion. The Sultan who "blinks" at the theft of antiquities and deploys Jesuits as spies represents a corruption that is political and spiritual at once.
