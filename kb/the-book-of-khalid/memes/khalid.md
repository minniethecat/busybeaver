# Khalid

## Role in the Book

Khalid is the protagonist and title character of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*, which is subtitled "the chart and history of one little [[souls-journey|kingdom of the Soul]] — the Soul of a philosopher, poet and criminal" (001-004). He is a young Lebanese immigrant who journeys to [[new-york-city|America]] at the turn of the 20th century, works as a lawyer's clerk in [[new-york-city|New York]], enters [[bohemianism|Bohemia]]n artistic circles, endures imprisonment at the hands of [[tammany-hall|Tammany Hall]], and ultimately returns to Lebanon as a prophetic voice of reform.

Khalid is not a conventional hero. He is simultaneously philosopher, poet, sensualist, and social critic — the "[[dervish-philosophy|dervish of science]]" and "ropedancer of metaphysics" (004-008). His life is a series of experiments in living: he tests ideas not in the study but in the street, the courtroom, the bedroom, and the jail cell. His failures are as instructive as his insights.

## Biographical Sketch

Khalid is born in Lebanon (then part of Ottoman Syria) and raised in [[baalbek|the village]] of [[baalbek|Baalbek]]. His early life is marked by intellectual precocity and religious skepticism — he is drawn to "free-thought literature" and "the higher religion" rather than orthodox practice. His [[love-sacred-and-profane|love]] for his cousin [[najma|Najma]] (005-012) is thwarted by family prohibition, and this early disappointment may contribute to his restless, questing nature.

He emigrates to [[new-york-city|America]] via [[beirut|Beirut]] and [[marseilles|Marseilles]], enduring the brutal conditions of immigrant passage — "huddled like sheep on deck... and like cattle transported under hatches across the Atlantic" (006-003). In [[new-york-city|New York]], he works as a clerk in a law office, where he develops his theory of "immanent morality" while observing the legal profession's entanglement with dishonesty (010-013).

After leaving the law, Khalid enters [[bohemianism|Bohemia]] — [[new-york-city|New York]]'s artistic underground — where his intellectual and sensual natures collide. He is recruited by [[tammany-hall|Tammany Hall]] as a political canvasser for the Syrian community, but his idealistic letter offends [[boss-odonohue|the Boss]] and lands him in prison for ten days (014-020).

Disillusioned with [[democracy-and-its-dowry|American democracy]], Khalid returns to Lebanon. In [[damascus|Damascus]], he delivers a message of "[[prophetic-reform|reformation by emigration]]" — "[[khalids-departure-from-lebanon|the emigration]] of the mind before the revolution of the state" (031-007). He challenges the ulema (religious authorities) and preaches a synthesis of [[oriental-spirituality|Eastern spirituality]] and Western intellectual freedom. The book ends with his continued striving rather than settled achievement.

## Historical Context

Khalid is a fictional character who embodies Ameen Rihani's own experience as a Lebanese-American intellectual at the turn of the 20th century. Like his creator, Khalid makes the passage from Lebanon to New York, navigates between East and West, and ultimately returns to the Arab world as a voice of reform. Rihani (1876–1940) was himself a pioneering figure — the first Arab American to publish an English-language novel — and Khalid's philosophy of synthesizing Eastern spirituality with Western rationalism mirrors Rihani's own intellectual project.

The novel was published in 1911, during a period of intense debate within the Arab world about modernization, Western influence, and Islamic reform. Khalid's call for "reformation by emigration" — changing the mind before changing the state — echoes the progressive reform movements of the late Ottoman era, including the Young Turk Revolution of 1908. His conflict with the ulema in Damascus reflects the real tensions between secular reformers and religious authorities that characterized the Nahda (Arab Renaissance) period.
