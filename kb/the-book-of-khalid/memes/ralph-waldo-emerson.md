## Role in the Book

Ralph Waldo Emerson enters *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* in a remarkable passage that stages the entire Western literary tradition as a jostling, unkempt crowd. Shakespeare is "in lamentable state," [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] is "in rags," Tennyson "shivering in his nakedness" — and Emerson is "wriggled and ruffled in this crushing crowd," still "sighing for a little solitude" [009-010]. The image captures both Emerson's cultural prestige and his discomfort within it: the philosopher who preached self-reliance is trapped in the marketplace of reputations.

Beyond this literary panorama, Emerson functions as [[khalid|Khalid]]'s authority on Nature. When [[khalid|Khalid]] rhapsodizes about the Lebanese mountain landscape, he twice invokes Emerson to validate his observations: "'A dilettantism in Nature is barren and unworthy,' says Emerson" [012-015] and "'The beauty of Nature must always seem unreal and mocking until the landscape has human figures, that are as good as itself'" [012-016]. [[khalid|Khalid]]'s Nature mysticism is explicitly Emersonian — but the Lebanese pines, he insists, dwarf the "scrubby and mean" pines he saw "in front of Emerson's house in Concord" [012-017]. The disciple surpasses the master's landscape, if not his thought.

## Biographical Sketch

Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882) was born in Boston and became the central figure of [[new-york-city|America]]n transcendentalism — a philosophical movement that emphasized individual intuition, the divinity of nature, and the rejection of institutional religious authority. His major works include *Nature* (1836), "The [[new-york-city|America]]n Scholar" (1837), and *Essays: First Series* (1841), which contains "Self-Reliance" and "The Over-Soul."

Emerson was also [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s mentor and [[walt-whitman|Whitman]]'s early champion — his 1855 letter greeting [[walt-whitman|Whitman]] "at the beginning of a great career" helped launch *Leaves of Grass*. For the Mahjar writers, Emerson represented a distinctly [[new-york-city|America]]n mode of spiritual inquiry: rooted in nature, independent of church authority, and compatible with the mystical traditions of the East. Rihani, who sought to synthesize Arabic and [[new-york-city|America]]n literary traditions, found in Emerson a natural ally.

## Key Passages

> "Observe how Emerson is wriggled and ruffled in this crushing crowd. Does he not seem to be still sighing for a little solitude?"
>
> — [009-010]

The narrator surveys a chaotic marketplace of Western literature, and Emerson — the philosopher of solitude — is conspicuously uncomfortable. "Wriggled and ruffled" is a deliberately undignified description; it humanizes the sage, showing him as a body suffering the press of lesser authors.

> "'A dilettantism in Nature is barren and unworthy,' says Emerson."
>
> — [012-015]

[[khalid|Khalid]] deploys Emerson's authority against the superficial nature-appreciation of city dwellers. In the Lebanese mountains, nature is not a hobby but a habitat; the children wading knee-deep in grass are "the least dilettanteish" [[love-sacred-and-profane|love]]rs of nature. Emerson provides the philosophical vocabulary for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s native experience.

> "'The beauty of Nature must always seem unreal and mocking until the landscape has human figures, that are as good as itself.'"
>
> — [012-016]

This Emerson quotation — modified by Rihani from "until the landscape has human figures" — becomes a justification for [[khalid|Khalid]]'s own project: to people the [[nature-as-temple|Lebanese landscape]] with figures worthy of its beauty. The passage bridges Emersonian nature-philosophy and [[khalid|Khalid]]'s social vision.

> "The only pines I have seen in the United States are those in front of Emerson's house in Concord; but compared with my native trees, they are scrubby and mean."
>
> — [012-017]

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s literary pilgrimage to Concord confirms both his respect for Emerson and his loyalty to Lebanon. The pines before Emerson's house are "scrubby and mean" — a judgment that is at once aesthetic (they are simply smaller) and symbolic (the New World cannot match the Old World's natural grandeur). [[khalid|Khalid]] honors Emerson by visiting his home; he honors Lebanon by preferring its trees.

## Historical Context

Emerson's reception in the Arab Nahda was limited compared to French Enlightenment figures, but his essays on nature and self-reliance resonated with Arab writers who sought to reconcile spiritual tradition with individual freedom. Rihani's direct experience of [[new-york-city|America]] — he lived in [[new-york-city|New York]], visited Boston, and read Emerson in English — gave him access to the transcendentalists that few Arab contemporaries possessed.

[[khalid|Khalid]]'s preference for Lebanese pines over Concord pines is not a rejection of Emerson but a re-grounding of Emersonian principles in native soil. The gesture is characteristically Mahjar: [[new-york-city|America]]n philosophy is absorbed, tested against local experience, and found both true and insufficient — true in principle, insufficient in detail. Emerson teaches [[khalid|Khalid]] *how* to see nature; Lebanon provides *what* he sees.
