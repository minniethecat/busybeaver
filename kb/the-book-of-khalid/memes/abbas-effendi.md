## Role in the Book

Abbas Effendi (1844–1921), later known as `Abdu'l-Bahá, appears in *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* as the spiritual center of a modern religious movement that draws the devotion of [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]], the [[new-york-city|America]]n patron who also supports [[khalid|Khalid]]. Rihani presents him with characteristic ambivalence: "the Pope of Babism at Haifa" who is "not impervious to the influence of other gods than his own" (025-076), receiving pilgrims' gold while dispensing divine revelation. He is neither condemned as a charlatan nor celebrated as a saint — rather, he occupies the same ambiguous space that all organized religious authority occupies in Rihani's worldview.

The Abbas Effendi episodes serve a structural function in the novel: they establish the spiritual landscape within which [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] operates, and by extension, the network of patronage that connects [[khalid|Khalid]] to [[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb of Damascus]], whom [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] "once met at Abbas Effendi's in Akka" (032-031).

## Biographical Sketch

`Abdu'l-Bahá Abbas (1844–1921) was the eldest son of Bahá'u'lláh, the founder of the Bahá'í Faith, and served as the religion's leader from 1892 until his death. Born in Tehran, he accompanied his father through decades of exile and imprisonment, eventually settling in Akka (Acre) and Haifa in Ottoman Palestine, where he established the faith's administrative and spiritual center.

During the period of *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]*'s composition, `Abdu'l-Bahá was becoming known in the West through the travels of early [[new-york-city|America]]n Bahá'ís. His message of religious unity, racial harmony, and the compatibility of science and religion attracted a network of Western followers — particularly [[new-york-city|America]]n women like the fictional [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]], who made pilgrimages to Haifa to receive his blessing.

Rihani's portrayal captures several authentic details: the pilgrimage circuit to Haifa, the practice of bringing "presents and gold," and the leader's willingness to receive Western visitors. Yet the narrator's tone — "who is not impervious to the influence of other gods than his own" — introduces a note of ironic distance characteristic of Rihani's treatment of all institutional religious figures.

## Key Passages

**[[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s Pilgrimage**

Rihani introduces Abbas Effendi through [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s devotional practice, establishing him as the object of her spiritual tourism:

> Mrs. Gotfry may not be a Babist in the strict sense of the word; but she is a votary and worshipper of the Bab. To her the personal element in a creed is of more importance than the ism. Hence, her pilgrimage every year to Haifa. She comes with presents and gold; and Abbas Effendi, who is not impervious to the influence of other gods than his own, permits her into the sanctuary, where she shares with him the light of divine revelation and returns to the States, as the Priestess of the Cult, to bless and console the Faithful. (025-076)

The passage is a masterwork of layered irony. [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] is not a "Babist in the strict sense" — her devotion is to the person, not the doctrine. The pilgrimage is an annual ritual of "presents and gold," and Abbas Effendi's receptivity to "other gods than his own" is noted with a raised eyebrow. Yet the narrator does not mock: [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s return to [[new-york-city|America]] "to bless and console the Faithful" is described with a certain respect for the sincerity of her belief, however unorthodox its expression.

**The Connection to [[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb]]**

It is through [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]]'s connection to Abbas Effendi that the novel's most significant spiritual figure enters the narrative:

> This is the noted Sheikh Taleb of Damascus, whom Mrs. Gotfry once met at Abbas Effendi's in Akka, and whom she was desirous of meeting again. (032-031)

The spiritual geography of the novel is thus triangulated: Haifa (Abbas Effendi), [[damascus|Damascus]] ([[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb]]), and [[new-york-city|New York]] ([[khalid|Khalid]]). Abbas Effendi is the midpoint in this network — more institutional than [[sheikh-taleb|Sheikh Taleb]], less worldly than [[khalid|Khalid]] — a figure whose existence establishes the coordinates within which the novel's spiritual drama unfolds.

**The Proposal to Spread Bahaism**

In a moment of visionary ambition, [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] contemplates recruiting [[khalid|Khalid]] to the Bahá'í cause:

> She will talk to Abbas Effendi about Khalid. He is young, eloquent, rising to power. And with her love, and influence superadded, what might he not do? what might he not accomplish? (027-016)

The proposal is never realized — [[khalid|Khalid]]'s path is too idiosyncratic to fit within any organized movement — but the passage reveals the networks of patronage and spiritual influence that connect the novel's characters across continents.

## Historical Context

The Bahá'í Faith emerged from Babism, a millenarian movement within Twelver Shi'a Islam founded by the Báb in 1844. Bahá'u'lláh, `Abdu'l-Bahá's father, proclaimed himself the messianic figure the Báb had foretold, and `Abdu'l-Bahá succeeded him as the faith's leader and authorized interpreter.

The early twentieth century — the period of [[the-book-of-khalid-work|Rihani's novel]] — was a time of intense Western interest in Eastern religions, from Theosophy to Vedanta to Bahá'í. [[new-york-city|America]]n pilgrims like the fictional [[mrs-gotfry|Mrs. Gotfry]] were a real phenomenon, and `Abdu'l-Bahá himself traveled to Europe and North [[new-york-city|America]] in 1911–1913, the very years *[[the-book-of-khalid-work|The Book of Khalid]]* was published. Rihani's portrayal is thus remarkably contemporary, capturing a religious movement at the moment of its emergence into Western consciousness.
