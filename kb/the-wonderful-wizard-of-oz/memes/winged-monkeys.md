# Winged Monkeys

## Overview

The Winged Monkeys are a band of intelligent, flying creatures who are bound by an ancient enchantment to obey whoever wears the Golden Cap. Though mischievous in nature, they are not inherently evil—they are compelled to serve both good and wicked masters alike, depending on who possesses the cap. They serve as both antagonists (when commanded by the Wicked Witch) and loyal allies (when commanded by Dorothy and Glinda).

## Properties

The Winged Monkeys possess several distinctive traits:

- **Flight**: They can fly across great distances, making them the fastest means of travel within the Land of Oz. However, they cannot cross the Deadly Desert that surrounds Oz (018-023).
- **Superhuman strength**: They can carry human-sized passengers through the air with ease, transporting Dorothy and her companions multiple times.
- **Intelligence and speech**: The Winged Monkeys are fully sentient, capable of conversation, storytelling, and reasoning. Their King speaks eloquently and remembers their centuries-long history.
- **Mischievous disposition**: The Queen of the Field Mice warns that the monkeys are "full of mischief and think it great fun to chase us" (014-017). They enjoy causing trouble for smaller creatures but show no true malice.
- **Enchantment-bound**: Their will is not their own when summoned by the Golden Cap. They must obey any command given, regardless of its nature or their personal feelings.

## Role in the Story

The Winged Monkeys first appear when the Wicked Witch of the West puts on the Golden Cap and summons them. She commands them to destroy the Scarecrow, the Tin Woodman, and the Cowardly Lion, and to capture Dorothy alive—but they may not harm Dorothy because of the protective mark of the Good Witch's kiss on her forehead (012-041). The monkeys carry out these orders with efficiency: they tear the Scarecrow apart, dent the Tin Woodman, bind the Lion, and carry Dorothy to the Witch's castle.

When Dorothy later summons them using the Golden Cap, the King of the Winged Monkeys tells her their history: they were once free creatures who lived happily, playing pranks and causing mischief. One day they played a trick on the intended bride of a powerful sorcerer named Quelala, dropping her into a river and ruining her beautiful silk dress and golden hair. The princess demanded their punishment. Quelala had a Golden Cap crafted as a wedding gift, and the enchantment was placed upon it binding the monkeys to obey its wearer three times (014-038).

Quelala used his first command to order the monkeys to stay away from him forever. The cap eventually passed into the hands of the Wicked Witch of the West, who used the monkeys to enslave the Winkies and drive the Wizard of Oz out of the Winkie Country (014-041). After Dorothy's commands, the cap passes to Glinda, who uses her three commands for benevolent purposes—returning Dorothy's companions to their new homes—and then gives the cap to the King of the Monkeys, freeing them from the enchantment forever (023-022).

## Notes

The Winged Monkeys represent one of Baum's more nuanced character groups—they are neither wholly good nor evil but are defined by the commands of those who possess power over them. Their liberation at the end of the story adds moral complexity, as Glinda uses the last of her power not for personal gain but to free an enslaved species. This theme of benevolent liberation runs throughout the Oz series.
