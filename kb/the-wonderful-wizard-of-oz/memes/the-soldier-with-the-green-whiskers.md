# Soldier with the Green Whiskers

## Overview

The Soldier with the Green Whiskers is a guard at the [[palace-of-oz|Palace of Oz]] in the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]]. His most striking feature — long green whiskers that reach to his knees — makes him one of the most visually memorable minor characters in Oz. He serves as the intermediary between the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] and visitors: he delivers messages to the Wizard, announces visitors, and relays the Wizard's decrees. Though his role is functional, his distinctive appearance and gentle efficiency make him a beloved figure in the Emerald City.

## Description

The soldier is described as having a green beard — or whiskers — so long they extend past his waist and nearly reach his knees (010-058). He wears a green uniform befitting the monochromatic scheme of the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]], where every citizen dresses in green. The soldier takes a "big golden key from a peg on the wall" to unlock the gates of the Palace (010-058), his movements careful and deliberate.

His personality is accommodating and efficient. When [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and her companions first arrive, he listens to their requests, conveys them to the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]], and returns with the Wizard's responses without embellishment or intimidation. He treats the companions with courtesy despite their strange appearances — a straw man, a tin man, and a lion.

## Role in the Story

### First Arrival

When [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and her companions first reach the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]], the [[guardian-of-the-gates|Guardian of the Gates]] escorts them to the [[palace-of-oz|Palace of Oz]], where the soldier greets them. Taking a "big golden key from a peg," he unlocks the door and admits them (010-058).

The soldier delivers their request to see the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] and returns with the Wizard's reply — an audience will be granted, but each companion must see the Wizard alone. He escorts [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] to the Throne Room first (011-003), then each of her companions in turn after they have cleaned and refreshed themselves.

### After the Witch's Death

When the companions return to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] after destroying the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]], the soldier is initially skeptical. He reports to the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]], who, as before, stalls. The soldier conveys the Wizard's reluctance to grant an audience, and the [[scarecrow]] threatens to summon the [[winged-monkey-band|Winged Monkeys]] (015-005).

After the [[discovery-of-oz|Wizard is exposed as a humbug]], the soldier remains at his post — he serves the Palace, not the man. When the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] prepares his balloon for departure, the soldier helps with the preparations (017-003).

### After the Wizard's Departure

After the [[wizards-departure|Wizard's departure]], the soldier continues to serve the new ruler of the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] — the [[scarecrow]]. His loyalty to the office rather than the individual reflects the stability of the Emerald City's institutions, which survive the revelation of the Wizard's fraud without crumbling.

## Relationships

### The Wizard of Oz

The soldier is the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard's]] most trusted attendant. He is one of the few Emerald City residents who has regular access to the Wizard — or at least, to the Wizard's Throne Room.

### Dorothy and Her Companions

The soldier treats [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]], the [[scarecrow]], the [[tin-woodman]], and the [[cowardly-lion]] with professional courtesy. He neither fears nor fawns over them, and his matter-of-fact delivery of the Wizard's refusals and demands makes him a neutral figure in the story — not an obstacle, but a conduit.

## Quotes

> Taking a big golden key from a peg on the wall, he opened a door and let them follow him through. (010-058)

## Notes

The Soldier with the Green Whiskers is typical of Baum's minor characters: vivid in one memorable detail (the green whiskers) but otherwise unremarkable. His function is to move the plot forward — opening doors and delivering messages — and he does so with quiet dignity. In the 1939 MGM film, the character is merged into the Guardian of the Gates (played by Frank Morgan in one of his multiple roles), but Baum's original gives the soldier his own moment with the golden key. The green whiskers are an example of Baum's playful literal-mindedness: in a city where everything is green, even facial hair obeys the color scheme.
