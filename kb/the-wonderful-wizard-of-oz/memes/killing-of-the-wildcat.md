## Overview

The **killing of the Wildcat** is a pivotal encounter in Chapter IX, in which the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]] saves the [[queen-of-the-field-mice|Queen of the Field Mice]] from a pursuing Wildcat. Despite claiming to have no heart, the Tin Woodman instinctively recognizes the wrongness of the Wildcat's attack and kills it with a single blow of his axe. The Queen's gratitude leads to the [[field-mice|field mice]] rescuing the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]] from the [[poppy-field|Deadly Poppy Field]], making this event a critical narrative turning point.

## The Encounter

As the Tin Woodman and the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] discuss their journey, a low growl interrupts them:

> The Tin Woodman was about to reply when he heard a low growl, and turning his head (which worked beautifully on hinges) he saw a strange beast come bounding over the grass toward them. It was, indeed, a great yellow Wildcat, and the Woodman thought it must be chasing something, for its ears were lying close to its head and its mouth was wide open, showing two rows of ugly teeth, while its red eyes glowed like balls of fire. [009-003]

Running before the Wildcat was a little gray field mouse—the Queen of the Field Mice. Though the Tin Woodman had no heart, "he knew it was wrong for the Wildcat to try to kill such a pretty, harmless creature" [009-003].

## The Killing

The Tin Woodman acted decisively:

> So the Woodman raised his axe, and as the Wildcat ran by he gave it a quick blow that cut the beast's head clean off from its body, and it rolled over at his feet in two pieces. [009-004]

The kill was instantaneous and clean—a single stroke of the axe that had once been enchanted by the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]], now turned to a compassionate purpose.

## The Queen's Gratitude

The field mouse, revealed to be their Queen, was surrounded by her subjects, who had feared she was dead:

> "Oh, your Majesty, we thought you would be killed! How did you manage to escape the great Wildcat?" They all bowed so low to the little Queen that they almost stood upon their heads. [009-012]

The Queen's response established a bond of eternal obligation:

> "This funny tin man," she answered, "killed the Wildcat and saved my life. So hereafter you must all serve him, and obey his slightest wish." [009-013]

## Consequences

When the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]] later fell unconscious in the Deadly Poppy Field and was too heavy for the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman to carry, the Tin Woodman called upon this debt. The Queen summoned thousands of her subjects, and the Tin Woodman built a truck from logs and branches. The mice—"each one had a piece of string in his mouth" [009-034]—pulled the great Lion out of the poppy-scented death zone to safety [009-037].

Had the Tin Woodman not killed the Wildcat, the Queen would have died, and there would have been no one to rescue the Lion. The killing thus forms the crucial causal link between the Tin Woodman's compassion and the survival of the entire party.

## Significance

The killing of the Wildcat is the clearest demonstration that the Tin Woodman possesses a heart in all but the literal sense. He acts not from calculation or self-interest, but from an innate moral recognition that the strong should not prey upon the weak. The Queen's decree—that all field mice must serve him—transforms a single act of kindness into a lasting alliance, illustrating the book's recurrent theme that goodness, even when performed by those who believe themselves lacking, generates far-reaching consequences.
