# The Old Crow

## Overview

The Old Crow is a minor but philosophically significant figure in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. When the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] is first mounted on his pole in the Munchkin cornfield, the crow flies down and taunts him — but in doing so, inadvertently delivers the most important insight the Scarecrow will ever receive. The crow tells the Scarecrow that if he only had brains, he would be "as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them" (004-024). This backhanded compliment plants the seed of ambition that will carry the Scarecrow from a cornfield to the throne of the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]]. The Old Crow is the unwitting catalyst for one of literature's most beloved character arcs.

## Description

The Old Crow is, as his name suggests, old — he speaks with the weary wisdom of a creature who has seen many seasons. He is not afraid of the Scarecrow (the straw man's very purpose is to frighten crows, and he is failing at it), but neither is he cruel. His taunting has a pedagogical quality — he is telling the Scarecrow the truth, not to wound him, but because it is simply what he observes.

The crow's insight is remarkable: he recognizes that the Scarecrow's sole deficiency is his empty head, and that even this deficiency is not inherent to his nature but contingent — something that could be fixed. In a world where most crows would simply mock the scarecrow and fly away, this crow does something extraordinary: he articulates the philosophical problem at the heart of the Scarecrow's existence.

## Role in the Story

### The Taunt

When the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] was first created and mounted on his pole, he discovered to his dismay that the crows were not afraid of him. Far from it — one old crow flew down, perched near him, and spoke:

> "If you only had brains in your head you would be as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them." (004-024)

This is a pivotal moment in the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow's]] self-understanding. Before the crow's words, the Scarecrow seems to have accepted his lot — standing in a field, scaring no one. After the crow's words, he understands that his problem is not his straw body but his empty head, and he conceives the desire for brains that will drive his entire quest.

### The Irony

The story's deepest irony is that the Old Crow is right and wrong simultaneously. He is right that the Scarecrow lacks brains — his head is literally stuffed with straw. But he is wrong in implying that brains are what make a man "good." The [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]], throughout the story, consistently proves to be the wisest of [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy's]] companions despite his empty head. The crow's taunt creates the desire for brains, but the story ultimately suggests that wisdom has nothing to do with what fills your head.

## Relationships

### The Scarecrow

The Old Crow and the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] have one of the story's most consequential brief encounters. The crow is the only character who directly tells the Scarecrow what he lacks — and in doing so, gives him the ambition that will lead him to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] and, eventually, to its throne.

## Quotes

> "If you only had brains in your head you would be as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them." (004-024)

## Notes

The Old Crow is a minor character with an outsized impact. He appears for a single paragraph in Chapter IV — a flashback narrated by the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] — but his words echo through the entire novel. In a story full of grand wizards and terrible witches, it is a common crow who delivers the most consequential insight: that intelligence is valuable, and that its absence is the only thing standing between the Scarecrow and greatness. The crow functions as the voice of folk wisdom — unlettered, unpretentious, and surprisingly profound. He is the first teacher in the Scarecrow's education, and though he only teaches one lesson, it is the right one.
