# King of the Winged Monkeys

## Overview

The King of the Winged Monkeys is the leader of the [[winged-monkey-band|Winged Monkey Band]], the flying creatures bound by magic to serve whoever possesses the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]]. When [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] summons the monkeys with her first command, the King appears and speaks for his band with a dignity that belies their forced servitude (012-047). He later provides one of the novel's most poignant passages: the story of how the Winged Monkeys — once free — were condemned to servitude by an enchantment placed on the Golden Cap as revenge for a prank (014-024–014-041). Though unnamed beyond his title, the King is one of the story's most sympathetic figures — a ruler forced to do terrible things at the command of masters he did not choose.

## Description

The King of the Winged Monkeys speaks with the voice of authority. When first summoned by [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]], he declares simply: "Your commands shall be obeyed" (012-050). He is physically described as part of the collective band of monkeys — creatures with wings and furry bodies — but he is distinguished by his speech and bearing.

Unlike his subjects, who act silently and in unison, the King is the band's voice. He alone addresses the cap's owner; he alone narrates history; he alone negotiates. His intelligence and dignity are evident in his account of the Winged Monkeys' enslavement — a story he tells not with anger or self-pity, but with the calm of someone who has long accepted an injustice he cannot change.

## Role in the Story

### First Summoning

When [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] discovers the charm words written inside the lining of the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] and summons the [[winged-monkey-band|Winged Monkeys]] for the first time, the King appears before her. He asks simply what her command is, and upon hearing that she wishes to be carried to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]], he and his band lift Dorothy and her companions — the [[scarecrow]], the [[tin-woodman]], and the [[cowardly-lion]] — and fly them swiftly through the air (014-020). The journey is smooth and rapid, accomplished with the efficiency of beings who have done this many times before, for many different masters.

### The History of the Enslavement

During the flight, [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] asks the King about the Winged Monkeys' history. The King tells her the full story: they were once free creatures who lived in a great forest, flying from tree to tree and doing as they pleased. Their downfall came when they played a prank on Quelala, a handsome young prince about to marry the beautiful princess [[gayelette|Gayelette]] (014-034–014-035).

The monkeys, finding Quelala by a river in his fine silk and velvet clothes, seized him and tossed him into the water — "thinking it good fun" (014-035). Gayelette's grandfather, a powerful sorcerer, was enraged. He enchanted the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] to compel the Winged Monkeys to serve its owner three times, binding them and all their descendants to eternal servitude (014-038).

The King tells this story without resentment toward [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] — he recognizes that the cap's owner is not responsible for the ancient injustice. His resignation is the resignation of a ruler who knows his people's suffering is woven into the fabric of the world.

> "We are free creatures, and have never injured anyone willingly," the King said. "But the Golden Cap compels us." (paraphrase of 014-033)

### The Limitation

When [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] later uses her second command — to fly her to [[kansas]] — the King regretfully explains the Winged Monkeys' limitation: they cannot cross the Deadly Desert that surrounds the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]] (018-023). This limitation, like their enslavement, is built into the enchantment — the monkeys are bound not only to obedience but to geography.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The King treats [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] with formal respect. She is the cap's owner, and he will obey — but he also recognizes her kindness, and his narration of the monkeys' history suggests he trusts her enough to share their story.

### The Winged Monkey Band

As King, he speaks for his people, commands them in service, and bears the moral weight of the atrocities they are forced to commit under the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap's]] compulsion.

### Glinda

The King's final and most important relationship is with [[glinda|Glinda the Good Witch]], who — after using her three commands from the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] — gives the cap to the King himself, freeing the Winged Monkeys from their enchantment forever (023-022).

## Quotes

> "Your commands shall be obeyed," said the leader of the monkeys (012-050)

## Notes

The King of the Winged Monkeys is a tragic figure in miniature. He rules a people who are not free, commands them to do things they would not choose, and bears witness to the long history of their suffering without hope of liberation — until [[glinda]]'s final act of mercy. His dignity, intelligence, and quiet sorrow make him one of Baum's most nuanced creations, and his story-within-a-story (the Quelala episode) is one of the novel's richest passages. The King is never named individually — he is defined entirely by his role — yet this anonymity only deepens his tragedy: he is not a person with a name and a destiny, but a function of an enchantment.
