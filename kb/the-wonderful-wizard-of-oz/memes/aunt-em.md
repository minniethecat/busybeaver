# Aunt Em

## Overview

Aunt Em is Dorothy Gale's aunt and guardian, appearing only in the opening and closing chapters of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. Yet she is the emotional center of the entire story: Dorothy's every action in Oz is motivated by the desire to return to her. When Dorothy cries "Take me home to Aunt Em!" (023-038), she crystallizes the story's deepest truth — that home is not a place but a person. Aunt Em, for all her brief page-time, is the reason the journey matters.

## Description

Aunt Em is introduced as a woman shaped by the harsh Kansas environment. "When Dorothy, who was an orphan, first came to her, Aunt Em had been so startled by the child's laughter that she would scream and press her hand upon her heart whenever Dorothy's merry voice reached her ears" (001-004). The years on the prairie have worn her down: "The sun and wind had taken the sparkle from her eyes and left them a sober gray; they had taken the red from her cheeks and lips, and they were gray also. She was thin and gaunt, and never smiled now" (001-004).

Baum's portrait is unsparing. Aunt Em is not warm, not affectionate, not even described as loving. Yet her importance to Dorothy is absolute. This is the story's quiet argument: love is not about physical beauty or demonstrative affection. Love is about belonging.

## Role in the Story

### Kansas Before the Cyclone

Aunt Em appears only briefly before the cyclone. She calls Dorothy to the storm cellar, then descends into it — and when she looks up, the house is gone, carried away with Dorothy inside (001-015). Her shock at losing her niece is unexpressed but implicit.

### Dorothy's Motivation

Throughout the story, Aunt Em is the person Dorothy names when explaining why she must return. "I am anxious to get back to my aunt and uncle, for I am sure they will worry about me" (004-013). The concern is mutual: Dorothy worries about Aunt Em's worry. This reciprocal care is what makes "home" more than a word.

### The Return

In the final chapter, Dorothy lands back on the Kansas prairie and runs to the farmhouse. "Aunt Em had just come out of the house to water the cabbages when she looked up and saw Dorothy running toward her. 'My darling child!' she cried, folding the little girl in her arms and covering her face with kisses. 'Where in the world did you come from?'" (023-042).

This moment — Aunt Em's sudden warmth, the embrace, the kisses — reveals what the gray opening chapter concealed: Aunt Em's love for Dorothy was always there, buried under the harshness of prairie life. "I'm so glad to be at home again!" Dorothy cries (023-044). The story ends where it began — with the gray Kansas prairie and Aunt Em, now revealed as the home Dorothy was seeking all along.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

Aunt Em took in Dorothy when she was orphaned (001-004). Though the Kansas years are described in gray terms, Dorothy's unwavering desire to return to her aunt is the most powerful evidence of their bond. Love does not require smiles.

### Uncle Henry

Aunt Em's husband and Dorothy's uncle. They share the gray life of Kansas farmers, and their home is the anchor Dorothy seeks.

### The Wicked Witches

Aunt Em has no connection to the magic of Oz — she is the opposite of everything fantastical in the story. Her ordinariness is her significance: she represents the real world, the mundane, the gray Kansas that Dorothy loves more than any emerald city.

## Notes

Aunt Em is a character of absences — absent from most of the story, absent in affection, physically grayed and diminished by the prairie. Yet her brief appearance at the story's close transforms everything. The woman who "never smiled" runs to Dorothy, folds her in her arms, and kisses her. This is Baum's most daring narrative move: he withholds warmth from Aunt Em for 23 chapters, only to reveal it in the final moments. The moral is quiet but profound: love does not always look like love from the outside, and the things we most wish to return to may be gray and ungainly — but they are home.
