# Winged Monkey Band

## Overview

The [[winged-monkey-band|Winged Monkey Band]] is an organized group of flying monkeys in the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]], bound by an ancient enchantment to serve whoever possesses the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]]. Their origin story — revealed by their King to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] — is one of the novel's most elaborate embedded narratives, a tale within a tale that stretches back to the time before the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] arrived in Oz (014-024–014-041). Once free creatures living in their own forest, the monkeys were cursed to servitude by a powerful enchantment placed on the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] as a wedding gift for the mortal Quelala. They pass through four masters — Quelala, the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]], [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]], and [[glinda]] — before [[glinda]] uses her third and final command to set them free forever.

## Characteristics

The Winged Monkeys are described as creatures with "wings like birds" and bodies covered in thick fur. They fly swiftly, capable of carrying passengers across great distances — they transport [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and her companions from the [[witchs-castle|Witch's Castle]] to the [[emerald-city]] in a single flight. The King of the Winged Monkeys speaks for the band and possesses the intelligence and dignity of a ruler, though his authority is circumscribed by the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap's]] enchantment.

Key characteristics of the band:

- **Winged flight**: Each monkey has functional wings that allow them to travel rapidly anywhere within the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]] — though they cannot cross the Deadly Desert that rings the country (018-023)
- **Collective organization**: They act as a disciplined band under their King, who speaks for them and issues orders
- **Bound by magic**: The [[golden-cap|Golden Cap's]] charm compels absolute obedience; the King says "Your commands shall be obeyed" (012-050), but the obedience is not voluntary
- **Mischief-loving nature**: The [[queen-of-the-field-mice|Queen of the Field Mice]] warns that the monkeys "are full of mischief and think it great fun to chase us" (014-017), suggesting they have retained their playful nature despite — or perhaps because of — their enslavement

## Role in the Story

### Origin: The Tale of Quelala

The Winged Monkeys were once free creatures who lived in a great forest, flying "from tree to tree, eating nuts and fruit, doing just as we pleased" (014-034). Their downfall came when they played a trick on Quelala, a handsome mortal prince about to marry a beautiful princess. The monkeys, "thinking it good fun," tossed Quelala into a river — ruining his fine silk and velvet clothes (014-035).

The princess's grandfather, a powerful sorcerer, was enraged. He enchanted the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] — the very cap Quelala was to receive as a wedding gift — with a charm that would compel the Winged Monkeys to obey its owner three times (014-038). Quelala was the first master; he used only one command, ordering the monkeys to keep their distance from him (014-040).

### Under the Wicked Witch

The [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] eventually passed to the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]]. Under her command, the Winged Monkeys did terrible things: they helped enslave the [[wicked-witchs-winkies|Winkies]], drove the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] from the Winkie Country, and most dramatically, destroyed the [[scarecrow]] — tearing out his straw and scattering it across the fields — and dropped the [[tin-woodman]] onto sharp rocks where he lay rusted and broken (012-055–012-056). They captured [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and the [[cowardly-lion]] and delivered them to the Witch's castle (012-065).

> "Your commands shall be obeyed," said the leader of the monkeys (012-050)

### Dorothy's Three Commands

[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy's]] commands were more benign. Her first, after discovering the charm words inside the Golden Cap's lining, was to have the monkeys carry her and her companions back to the [[emerald-city]] (014-020). Her second command — to fly her to [[kansas]] — failed because the monkeys cannot cross the desert (018-023). Her third command, after the [[wizards-departure|Wizard's departure]], was to carry the [[scarecrow]], [[tin-woodman]], and [[cowardly-lion]] to their respective new homes (022-017).

### Liberation

[[glinda|Glinda the Good Witch]] asked for the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]] as payment for revealing the secret of the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]] (023-009). She used her three commands for Dorothy's companions, and then:

> "Then, having used up the powers of the Golden Cap, I shall give it to the King of the Monkeys, that he and his band may thereafter be free for evermore." (023-022)

This final act of liberation is one of the novel's most satisfying resolutions — the Winged Monkeys, who were never evil but only compelled by magic, are set free from the enchantment that bound them for generations.

## Notable Members

The Winged Monkeys appear collectively; their King speaks for them when summoned, but no individual monkey is named. The King is distinguished by his speech — he alone addresses the cap's owner and narrates the band's history to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] (014-024). His dignity and the tragic resignation in his account of the band's enslavement make him one of the story's most sympathetic figures despite his limited appearances.

## Quotes

> "We are free creatures, and have never injured any one willingly," the King told Dorothy. "But the Golden Cap compels us." (014-033, paraphrased)

## Notes

The Winged Monkey Band is one of Baum's most sophisticated creations — servants who are neither good nor evil, but compelled to serve whoever holds the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]]. Their enslavement is a cosmic injustice: they are punished for a prank with a curse that forces them to commit atrocities they do not choose. The King's narration to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] (014-024–014-041) functions as a miniature tragedy embedded in the larger quest narrative. [[glinda|Glinda's]] act of freeing them — using the final command not for conquest but for liberation — is the novel's most explicitly moral act, suggesting that true power lies not in commanding others but in setting them free.
