# Cowardly Lion

## Overview

The Cowardly Lion is the last of Dorothy's three companions to join the journey on the Yellow Brick Road. He introduces himself by attacking the travelers — then immediately collapses into tears when Dorothy slaps his nose (006-024). His confession — "I know I am a coward" (006-025) — establishes the central paradox of his character: a lion who is afraid, and a coward who acts with consistent bravery. His quest for courage from the Wizard, fulfilled with a green liquid in a bottle (016-035), is the most transparent placebo in the story — and the most psychologically sophisticated.

## Description

The Lion is, by appearance, the most formidable of Dorothy's companions: a full-grown lion with "great, sharp claws and a mouth full of sharp teeth" (006-017). Yet his behavior is the opposite of his appearance. He whimpers, weeps, trembles, and confesses his fear with startling honesty: "Whenever there is danger, my heart begins to beat fast" (006-027).

His defining trait is self-awareness. Unlike the Scarecrow, who doesn't know he's intelligent, or the Tin Woodman, who doesn't know he's kind, the Lion knows exactly what he is — a coward — and hates it. His honesty about his fear is itself a form of courage. He weeps openly, and when the Tin Woodman diagnoses him with "heart disease" (006-028), the Lion misses the irony entirely.

## Role in the Story

### The Attack

The Lion's introduction is comically aggressive: he leaps from the forest, strikes the Tin Woodman to the ground, and swipes at the Scarecrow (006-018). When he goes for Toto, Dorothy slaps his nose (006-024). The Lion, this terrifying beast, immediately breaks down: "I know I am a coward" (006-025).

### Fear and Action

The Lion's cowardice is a running theme, but his courage is demonstrated in every crisis. When the travelers face the great ditch, the Lion leaps across — carrying each companion on his back, one by one (007-026). When Kalidahs attack, he stays behind as a rear guard (007-034). When Dorothy is threatened, he vows: "Whoever tried to hurt her would have me to reckon with" (009-014).

The poppy field — whose deadly scent puts all who breathe it to sleep — overcomes even the Lion, but not before he makes a desperate run toward the Emerald City. His fear never stops him from acting; it only makes him believe his actions don't count as courage.

### Receiving Courage

The Wizard pours liquid from a green bottle into a green-gold dish and tells the Lion to drink (016-035). When the Lion hesitates, Oz explains: "If it were inside of you, it would be courage. You know, of course, that courage is always inside one" (016-038). The Lion drinks and declares: "Full of courage!" (016-040).

### King of Beasts

Empowered by his new "courage," the Lion defeats a giant spider-like monster that has been terrorizing the forest animals (021-030). The beasts ask him to be their king. He agrees, fulfilling the dream he expressed at their first meeting: "I should have been the King of Beasts, as men call me" (006-039). He remains in the forest when Dorothy returns to Kansas.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The Lion is fiercely protective of Dorothy. Despite his fear, he threatens to destroy anything that would harm her. She, in turn, treats his fear with matter-of-fact kindness — never mocking, never shaming.

### The Scarecrow

The Lion responds to the Scarecrow's ingenuity with genuine admiration. Their relationship is one of mutual respect: the brainless thinker and the cowardly fighter.

### The Tin Woodman

The Woodman's gentle diagnosis of the Lion's "heart disease" (006-028) is one of the story's funniest exchanges. The two share an understanding of emotional insufficiency — one lacks a heart, the other lacks courage, and both discover they had what they sought all along.

### Toto

The Lion's relationship with Toto is the source of their introduction: it was snapping at Toto that earned the Lion Dorothy's slap (006-024). After that, Toto treats the Lion with wary respect.

## Notes

The Cowardly Lion is the most psychologically interesting of Baum's characters. His fear is genuine — he trembles and weeps — and his courage is equally genuine — he faces every danger and never abandons his friends. The green liquid is explicitly not courage: Oz admits it only becomes courage "inside of you." This is perhaps the most direct statement of the story's placebo thesis: the qualities we seek are already within us, and we need only the belief — or the green bottle — to access them. The Lion, who trembles while fighting monsters, is a more honest portrait of courage than any fearless hero could be.
