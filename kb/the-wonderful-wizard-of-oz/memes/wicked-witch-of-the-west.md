# Wicked Witch of the West

## Overview

The Wicked Witch of the West is the primary antagonist of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. Ruling the Winkie Country from her yellow castle, she is the last surviving Wicked Witch in the Land of Oz — her counterpart, the Wicked Witch of the East, having been killed by Dorothy's falling house (002-008). She is defined by three fears: the protective kiss of the Witch of the North on Dorothy's forehead, the powerful charm of the Silver Shoes, and — most fatally — water. Her destruction by a bucket of thrown water (012-083) is the story's most famous death, and the fulfillment of the Wizard's impossible demand.

## Description

The Wicked Witch of the West is never given a detailed physical description, but her psychological portrait is vivid. She is defined by cruelty — she rules the Winkies "night and day" (002-016) — and by covetousness: her desire for the Silver Shoes consumes her. She trembles with fear upon seeing them on Dorothy's feet, knowing "what a powerful charm belonged to them" (012-059).

She commands a fearsome arsenal: a pack of wolves, a flock of wild crows, a swarm of black bees, and an army of Winkie slaves (012-015–012-042). She also possesses the Golden Cap, which compels the Winged Monkeys to serve her three times (012-040). Despite all this power, she has one fatal vulnerability: "Water was the one thing that could destroy her" (012-070). This vulnerability is both literal and symbolic — water dissolves her as thoroughly as it would dissolve the Wicked Witch's wickedness.

## Role in the Story

### The Threat

The Witch is introduced as a looming threat. The Witch of the North warns Dorothy: "The Wicked Witch of the West would make you her slave if you passed her way" (002-042). Every character who speaks of her does so in whispers of fear.

### Dorothy's Capture

The Witch first attacks the travelers indirectly: wolves, crows, bees, and Winkie soldiers — all defeated by the companions (012-015–012-042). Frustrated, she uses the Golden Cap to summon the Winged Monkeys, who capture Dorothy and destroy the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman (012-055–012-060). The Lion is chained in the Witch's courtyard, but Dorothy — protected by the Witch of the North's kiss and the Silver Shoes' charm — cannot be harmed.

### The Silver Shoes

The Witch's obsession with the Silver Shoes is her defining narrative thread. "The Witch was too much afraid of the dark to dare go in Dorothy's room at night to take the shoes, and her dread of water was greater than her fear of the dark, so she never came near when Dorothy was bathing" (012-070). She finally succeeds in stealing one shoe by using an invisible iron bar to trip Dorothy (012-071). Dorothy, enraged, seizes a bucket of water and throws it over the Witch (012-083).

### The Melting

The Witch's death is swift and spectacular: "The Witch gave a loud cry and began to shrink and fall away. 'See what you have done!' she screamed. 'In a minute I shall melt away'" (012-083). Within moments, she is nothing but "a brown, melted, broad mass" on the kitchen floor (012-084). Dorothy, who killed her first witch by accident (the house landing on the Wicked Witch of the East), kills her second witch by the same principle — not through strength or skill, but through circumstance.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The Witch covets Dorothy's Silver Shoes and fears the protective kiss on her forehead. She enslaves Dorothy but cannot harm her physically — a frustration that defines their interactions.

### The Winged Monkeys

The Witch controls the Winged Monkeys through the Golden Cap. She has already used the Cap twice: to make the Winkies her slaves and to drive the Wizard from the West (012-040). Her third use — capturing Dorothy — exhausts her power over them.

### The Winkies

The Witch rules the Winkies as slaves: "She had made the Winkies her slaves, and set herself to rule over their country" (012-040). After her death, the Winkies gratefully ask the Tin Woodman to rule them instead.

### The Wizard of Oz

The Witch's relationship with the Wizard is one of mutual fear. The Wizard admits he was "just as much afraid of the Witches as you are" (015-074). He made his demand — kill the Wicked Witch of the West — hoping it was impossible.

## Notes

The Wicked Witch of the West is Baum's most iconic villain — though she appears in relatively few scenes. Her power is established through reputation and fear rather than direct action. Her death by water is one of the most famous moments in children's literature. It is significant that Dorothy kills her accidentally — as she killed the Wicked Witch of the East accidentally — because the pattern establishes that Dorothy's power is not martial but moral: she destroys evil not by fighting it but by refusing to tolerate it.
