# Scarecrow

## Overview

The [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] is one of [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy's]] three companions on the [[road-of-yellow-brick]]. Made of straw and mounted on a pole to frighten crows, he was created only two days before [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] finds him in a Munchkin cornfield (003-045). He believes himself stupid because his head is stuffed with straw, yet he consistently proves to be the group's most ingenious problem-solver. His quest is for [[brain|brains]] — the intelligence he believes he lacks — which the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] provides in the form of bran mixed with pins and needles (016-011). After the [[wizards-departure|Wizard's departure]], the Scarecrow becomes the ruler of the [[emerald-city]] (017-012).

## Description

The Scarecrow is a life-sized figure made of straw, dressed in old [[munchkin-country|Munchkin]] clothes — a blue suit and a pointed hat (003-037). His face is painted on a sack stuffed with straw, with eyes, nose, and mouth drawn in blue paint. His greatest physical advantage is his inability to feel pain: when the [[tin-woodman]] swings his axe, the Scarecrow can only be scattered, never injured.

His defining traits are humility and ingenuity. He humbly insists:

> "I have no brains at all." (003-045)

Yet in every crisis he devises the solution. His modesty is genuine — he is the last person to recognize his own intelligence. When the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] stuffs his head with bran and pins, the Scarecrow emerges convinced he is now wise, though he was wise all along.

## Role in the Story

### Meeting Dorothy

[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] finds the Scarecrow on a pole in a [[munchkin-country|Munchkin]] cornfield. He tells her his creation was only two days before, and his greatest sorrow is his empty head. When he learns of [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy's]] journey, he asks to join: "Do you think if I went to the Emerald City with you, that Oz would give me some brains?" (003-047). Dorothy's practical reply is both kind and accurate:

> "If Oz will not give you any brains you will be no worse off than you are now." (003-048)

### The Problem-Solver

Time and again, the Scarecrow's "empty" head produces the solution. The [[cowardly-lion|Lion]] observes:

> "One would almost suspect you had brains in your head, instead of straw." (009-008)

The Scarecrow's ingenuity is most dramatically displayed during the [[escape-from-the-kalidahs|escape from the Kalidahs]], when he shouts for the [[tin-woodman]] to cut the tree bridge — a split-second tactical decision that saves the entire group (007-034).

### Receiving Brains

The [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] fills the Scarecrow's head with bran, pins, and needles, declaring:

> "Hereafter you will be a great man, for I have given you a lot of bran-new brains." (016-012)

The pun — "bran-new" for "brand-new" — captures the comedy and pathos of the moment. The Scarecrow's head bulges at the top with brains (016-014), and he declares earnestly: "I feel wise indeed. When I get used to my brains I shall know everything" (016-016).

### Ruler of the Emerald City

Before departing in his balloon during the [[wizards-departure|disastrous attempted escape]], the Wizard names the Scarecrow as his successor: "I hereby appoint you to rule the Emerald City in my absence" (017-012). The Scarecrow proves a capable and beloved ruler of the [[emerald-city]].

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The Scarecrow is deeply grateful to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] for freeing him and joins her quest without hesitation. He is protective of her — when she despairs over the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard's]] demands, he comforts her with the steady optimism that comes from having nothing to lose.

### The Tin Woodman

The Scarecrow's philosophical rival. Their debate over whether [[brain|brains]] or [[heart]] is more valuable (005-050–005-052) is one of the story's most charming exchanges. The Scarecrow argues for brains:

> "I shall ask for brains instead of a heart; for a fool would not know what to do with a heart if he had one." (005-050)

Despite their intellectual disagreements, they are loyal friends.

### The Cowardly Lion

The Scarecrow treats the [[cowardly-lion|Lion]] with amused tolerance. He is the only character who can joke with the Lion about his [[courage|cowardice]] without cruelty, understanding that fear is not the same as weakness.

### The Old Crow

A crow who taunted the Scarecrow — but inadvertently gave him the ambition to seek [[brain|brains]]. The crow said: "If you only had brains in your head you would be as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them" (004-024).

## Notes

The Scarecrow is the most philosophical of Baum's characters. His debate with the [[tin-woodman]] about the relative value of [[brain|brains]] versus [[heart]] is a gentle introduction to the mind-body problem for children — and a surprisingly sophisticated one. The Scarecrow's position — that intelligence is the highest good — is undercut by the story itself, which shows that his supposed lack of intelligence never prevents his wisdom. The Scarecrow is the archetype of the person who doesn't know they're smart — a figure as common in adult life as in childhood. In becoming ruler of the [[emerald-city]], Baum makes his most pointed statement: wisdom has nothing to do with what fills your head, and everything to do with how you use it.
