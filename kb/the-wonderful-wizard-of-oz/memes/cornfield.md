## Overview

The Cornfield is the location in Munchkin Country where Dorothy first encounters the Scarecrow. Situated beyond the fence of the road of yellow brick, a tall pole rises from this field bearing the straw-stuffed figure that becomes Dorothy's first companion on her journey to the Emerald City. The cornfield serves as the Scarecrow's place of origin and the site of his liberation, and he refers back to it throughout the novel as his former home.

## Geography

The cornfield lies in Munchkin Country, visible from the road of yellow brick shortly after Dorothy leaves the Munchkin settlement [003-028]. It is described as "a great cornfield" situated beyond a fence [003-028]. A tall pole stands within it, upon which the Scarecrow was placed [003-028]. The field serves its agricultural purpose for the surrounding Munchkin farms, but its primary narrative function is as the vessel for the Scarecrow: a place of crops and crows, of work and loneliness.

## Inhabitants

The cornfield's sole notable inhabitant is the [[scarecrow]], who was made by a Munchkin farmer and "stuck on a pole" there to keep crows and blackbirds away from the corn [004-021]. Before his liberation, these birds were his daily antagonists — he recounts his frustration that "even the old crows, who have been in the cornfields all their lives, knew that I was no real man" [004-022]. After the Scarecrow is freed, the cornfield is abandoned by its primary figure, returning to its original state as farmland.

## Role in the Story

Dorothy discovers the cornfield shortly after beginning her journey. She stops to rest and sees the Scarecrow "placed high on a pole to keep the birds from the ripe corn" [003-028]. When he winks at her, she climbs the fence and lifts him from the pole [003-045]. This moment of liberation marks the first of the companions she will gather on the yellow brick road.

The Scarecrow repeatedly references his origins in the cornfield. He tells the Tin Woodman that he was "stuck on a pole in a cornfield" where he could "make-believe scare the crows" [008-013]. When he becomes the ruler of the Emerald City, he reflects on the distance he has traveled: he was lifted from a farmer's pole in a cornfield and now governs the most magnificent city in Oz [018-008, 018-046]. In Chapter XIX, he muses that if he were still in his cornfield, he "should have passed my whole life there without knowing anything of the wonderful Land of Oz" [023-026].

## Notes

The cornfield is one of the novel's humblest locations, yet it produces its most beloved character. The Scarecrow's journey from a pole in a farmer's field to the throne of the Emerald City is the novel's most complete rags-to-riches arc, and the cornfield serves as the zero point against which his ascent is measured. The image of the Scarecrow on his pole, unable to move or act, is echoed in his later reflections on the limitations of his straw body and his longing for brains — the cornfield is where his sense of incompleteness was born.
