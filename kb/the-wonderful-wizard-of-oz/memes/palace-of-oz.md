# Palace of Oz

## Overview

The Palace of Oz is the seat of the Wizard's power in the center of the Emerald City — a magnificent green structure where the Great and Terrible Oz receives visitors in a series of terrifying illusory forms. It is here that Dorothy and her companions present their wishes, receive their impossible quest, and ultimately expose the Wizard as an ordinary man from Omaha operating an elaborate deception. The Palace is both the most impressive building in Oz and the site of the novel's central revelation about power and belief.

## Geography

The Palace stands in the exact center of the Emerald City, behind a high wall and guarded by the soldier with the green whiskers. It is constructed entirely of green marble and set with emeralds (011-001, paraphrased). Inside, the Throne Room is "a big room with a green carpet and lovely green furniture set with emeralds" (015-026). Behind the Throne Room, a screen conceals a small chamber where the Wizard operates his illusions. The Palace also contains guest rooms for visitors, each decorated in green with comfortable furniture (011-005).

## Inhabitants

The Palace is staffed by green-clad attendants. The most prominent is "a beautiful green girl" who greets visitors, assigns them rooms, and carries messages to the Wizard (011-004). The soldier with the green whiskers guards the Palace door and also acts as the Wizard's messenger. The **[[wizard-of-oz|Wizard of Oz]]** himself occupies the inner chambers, appearing to visitors in different guises: as a giant head for Dorothy (011-009), a lovely lady for the Scarecrow (011-012), a terrible beast for the Tin Woodman (011-015), and a ball of fire for the Lion (011-018).

## Role in the Story

The Palace is the setting for the novel's major turning points. Dorothy's party first visits the Palace to present their requests, and each receives a private audience with the Wizard in a different form. The Wizard's demand — "destroy the Wicked Witch of the West" — sends them away from the Palace on their second quest.

When they return, the Wizard stalls for days before finally granting them an audience. It is in the Throne Room that **[[toto|Toto]]** tips over the screen, revealing "a little, old man, with a bald head and a wrinkled face" pulling levers and speaking into a microphone (015-029). The Wizard's confession — "I am a humbug" (015-035) — transforms the Palace from a seat of mystical power into a theater of illusion.

## Notable Features

- **The Throne Room**: The audience chamber where the Wizard's various forms appear — a stage set for magical theater.
- **The Screen**: Behind which the Wizard operates his apparatus of illusion — a microphone, levers, and painted backdrops.
- **The Guest Chambers**: Dorothy and her friends each have a luxurious green room, a mark of the Wizard's diplomatic hospitality.

## Quotes

> "I am Oz, the Great and Terrible. Who are you, and why do you seek me?" — the giant Head (011-009)

> "Pay no attention to that man behind the curtain." — popularized from the 1939 film; in the novel the Wizard says "I am a humbug" (015-035)

## Notes

The Palace of Oz literalizes the theme of theatrical power. Its architecture — a Throne Room with a hidden chamber, a screen that conceals the operator — mirrors the structure of a Victorian stage or a magic-lantern show. The Wizard's confession that he "grew tired of being a humbug" after meeting Dorothy suggests that the Palace's illusions are sustained not only by the Wizard's trickery but by the willing belief of those who enter it.
