# Kindness

## Overview

Kindness is the quiet moral thread running through *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. From the Witch of the North's protective kiss (002-054) to Glinda's patient guidance (023-027), from the Munchkins' hospitality (003-015) to the Stork's rescue of the Scarecrow (008-037), kindness appears again and again as the force that makes the journey possible. In a story ostensibly about magic, the real power is consistently human: the kindness of strangers, the care of companions, the mercy of the powerful toward the weak.

## Properties

Kindness in Baum's Oz has several distinct characteristics:

- **Unconditional**: The Witch of the North's kiss protects Dorothy without demanding anything in return (002-054)
- **Reciprocal**: The Stork rescues the Scarecrow from the river, and he promises to "find the Stork again and do her some kindness in return" (008-037)
- **Transformative**: The Tin Woodman, upon receiving his heart, is most concerned that it should be "a kind heart" (016-025)
- **The mark of goodness**: The good characters in Oz are distinguished by their kindness; the wicked characters by its absence

## Role in the Story

### The Witch of the North's Kiss

The first and most mysterious act of kindness is the Witch of the North's kiss. After explaining the dangers Dorothy faces, she "bent down and kissed Dorothy on the forehead, where her lips touched the girl they left a round, shining mark" (002-054). The kiss is a protection: "No one will dare injure a person who has been kissed by the Witch of the North" (002-055). This kindness — unearned, unexplained — shields Dorothy throughout her journey.

### Kindness Among the Companions

The travelers continually show kindness to one another. The Tin Woodman carefully avoids harming any living creature, "for his heart was kind and tender" (006-047). The Scarecrow comforts Dorothy when she despairs. Even the Cowardly Lion, terrified, threatens to defend her against all enemies (009-014). Their kindness to each other is the story's emotional center.

### The Stork's Rescue

When the Scarecrow is stranded on a pole in the middle of a river, a Stork flies to his rescue. "It is my good fortune to help you," the Stork says (008-036). The Scarecrow promises to "find the Stork again and do her some kindness in return" (008-037). This brief episode establishes kindness as a circulating currency in Oz — given freely, returned willingly.

### Glinda's Kindness

Glinda the Good Witch embodies kindness in its most powerful form. She does not command or threaten — she guides, explains, and helps. She reveals the Silver Shoes' power not with triumph but with gentle patience: "Because I knew she would not believe me. She had to learn it for herself" (023-027). Her kindness is wisdom in action.

### The Kind Heart

When the Wizard gives the Tin Woodman his silk-and-sawdust heart, the Woodman's only question is: "But is it a kind heart?" (016-025). Oz assures him it is "Oh, very!" kind (016-026). The Woodman's elation is not about the heart's magic but its kindness: "I am very grateful to you, and shall never forget your kindness" (016-028). Kindness, more than love or courage, is the quality the Woodman most values.

## Notes

Kindness is the most understated virtue in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* but arguably the most important. Baum's Oz is a dangerous place — witches, wolves, poppy fields, Kalidahs — and it is navigated not through strength or magic but through the kindness of strangers. This is a deeply democratic virtue: kindness does not require wealth, power, or intelligence — the Scarecrow has no brain but he is kind; the Lion is terrified but he is kind; the Tin Woodman has no heart but he is the kindest of all. The moral of Baum's story may be less "there's no place like home" than "there's no power like kindness."
