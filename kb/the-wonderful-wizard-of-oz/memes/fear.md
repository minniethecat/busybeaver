# Fear

## Overview

Fear is the emotion that gives the Cowardly Lion his name. Unlike the Scarecrow's intellectual insecurity or the Tin Woodman's emotional emptiness, the Lion's weakness is visceral: "Whenever there is danger, my heart begins to beat fast" (006-027). Fear, in Baum's story, is not something to be eliminated but something to be faced — and the Lion, paradoxically, faces it more often and more bravely than anyone else. His fear never prevents him from acting; it only prevents him from recognizing his own courage.

## Properties

Fear in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* has several key dimensions:

- **Physical manifestation**: The Lion's fear is bodily — a racing heart, trembling, whimpering — not intellectual
- **Self-awareness**: The Lion knows he is afraid, and this self-knowledge is itself a form of courage
- **Universal experience**: Every character experiences fear at some point; the Lion's distinction is his honesty about it
- **The prerequisite for courage**: As Oz explains, courage "is always inside one" (016-038) — without fear, courage would be meaningless

## Role in the Story

### The Cowardly Lion

The Lion enters the story by attacking the travelers — a surprising introduction for a coward. He strikes the Tin Woodman, who "fell over upon the ground, where he lay motionless" (006-018), and tries to bite the Scarecrow, who only scolds him. When Dorothy slaps his nose for snapping at Toto, the Lion dissolves entirely: "I know I am a coward" (006-025).

He reveals his interior life: "It is my great sorrow, and makes my life very unhappy. But whenever there is danger, my heart begins to beat fast" (006-027). This confession of fear is itself an act of vulnerability — and courage.

### Fear in Action

Throughout the journey, the Lion's fear coexists with action. When the Kalidahs attack, he is terrified — but he stays behind to guard the rear while his companions cross the tree-bridge (007-034). When the deadly poppy field puts Dorothy to sleep, the Lion "tried to get to the Emerald City, but the scent of the flowers was so strong that he too fell asleep" (008-020) — he ran into the danger while afraid. 

The Lion's fear is the most honest emotion in the book. Other characters hide their weaknesses; the Lion wears his openly. He whimpers, weeps, and trembles — but he never turns back.

### The Transformation

When Oz gives the Lion the green liquid courage, he explains: "If it were inside of you, it would be courage. You know, of course, that courage is always inside one" (016-038). The Lion drinks and declares himself "Full of courage!" (016-040). The placebo works — not because it removes fear, but because it changes the Lion's relationship to it.

## Notes

Fear in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is treated with surprising maturity for a children's book. The Cowardly Lion is never shamed into bravery; he is never told to stop being afraid. Instead, the story suggests that fear is natural, even universal, and that courage consists not in fearlessness but in acting despite fear. Oz's green bottle does not eliminate fear — it gives the Lion the belief that he can face it. This is perhaps the most psychologically sophisticated message in Baum's novel: courage is not the absence of fear but the decision to move forward while afraid.
