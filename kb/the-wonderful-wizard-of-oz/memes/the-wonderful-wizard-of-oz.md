## Overview

**The Wonderful Wizard of Oz** is a children's fantasy novel written by L. Frank Baum and illustrated by W. W. Denslow, first published in 1900 by the George M. Hill Company. It tells the story of a young Kansas farm girl named [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy Gale]], who is swept away by a cyclone to the magical [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]], where she embarks on a journey to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] to ask the [[wizard-of-oz|Great Wizard]] to send her home. Along the way, she befriends the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]], the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]], and the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]].

Baum wrote the book "solely to please children of today," aspiring to create "a modernized fairy tale, in which the wonderment and joy are retained and the heartaches and nightmares are left out" [P01-003].

## Publication History

The book was published in Chicago in April 1900, with Denslow's illustrations printed in color—an unusual and expensive production choice for the time. The first edition of 10,000 copies sold out quickly, and the book became the bestselling children's book of the 1900 Christmas season. By the time Baum's copyright expired in 1956, the book had sold over three million copies.

The book was the first in what became a series of fourteen Oz novels by Baum, followed by *The Marvelous Land of Oz* (1904), *Ozma of Oz* (1907), and eleven more. After Baum's death, the series was continued by other authors.

## Characters

### Protagonists
- **[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy Gale]]** — a young orphan girl from Kansas, practical and brave
- **[[scarecrow|Scarecrow]]** — a straw man who desires brains, though he proves clever throughout
- **[[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]** — a man made of tin who desires a heart, though he is deeply compassionate
- **[[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]]** — a lion who desires courage, though he acts bravely when needed
- **[[toto|Toto]]** — Dorothy's little black dog, whose actions drive the plot at key moments

### Antagonists
- **[[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]]** — the primary villain, who rules the Winkie Country through fear and enslaved labor
- **[[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]]** — killed by Dorothy's falling house, setting the story in motion

### Supporting
- **[[witch-of-the-north|Good Witch of the North]]** — Dorothy's first ally in Oz
- **[[glinda|Glinda the Good Witch]]** — ruler of the Quadlings who helps Dorothy return home
- **[[wizard-of-oz|The Wizard of Oz]]** — the humbug ruler of the Emerald City
- **[[guardian-of-the-gate|Guardian of the Gates]]**, **[[soldier-with-the-green-whiskers|Soldier with the Green Whiskers]]** — Emerald City functionaries
- **[[aunt-em|Aunt Em]]**, **[[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]]** — Dorothy's Kansas guardians

## Themes

**Home and belonging**: Dorothy's central quest is to return to Kansas. Despite Oz's beauty and wonder, she never wavers in her desire to go home. However, the book complicates this message—Kansas is described entirely in shades of gray, while Oz is brilliant with color. Dorothy's famous line at the end ("There is no place like home") is preceded by her observation that she loves her companions "just the same" as Aunt Em.

**Self-discovery through journey**: Each of Dorothy's companions already possesses the quality they seek. The Scarecrow devises clever strategies throughout; the Tin Woodman is moved to tears by others' suffering; the Lion faces mortal danger repeatedly. The Wizard's gifts—a bran-stuffed head, a silk heart, a green liquid "courage"—are merely tokens that confirm what each already has.

**The humbug and the nature of power**: The Wizard is revealed as an ordinary man from Omaha who rules through illusion and stagecraft. His confession ("I am a humbug") is one of the book's most subversive moments, suggesting that authority often rests on nothing more than managed perception.

## Illustrations

W. W. Denslow's illustrations were integral to the book's success. His bold, stylized drawings—with their distinctive use of color plates and Art Nouveau influence—defined the visual identity of Oz. Denslow's character designs, particularly the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman, became iconic and shaped subsequent adaptations.

## Legacy

The book has been continuously in print since its publication and has been translated into dozens of languages. It spawned one of the most successful series in children's literature and was adapted into the landmark 1939 MGM film starring Judy Garland, as well as numerous stage productions, television shows, and later film adaptations. The story's characters, settings, and phrases ("There's no place like home," "We're off to see the Wizard," "I'm melting!") have entered the popular lexicon.

The book was designated a "National Treasure" by the Library of Congress and frequently appears on lists of the greatest American novels.
