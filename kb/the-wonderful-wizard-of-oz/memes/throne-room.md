## Overview

The Throne Room is the central chamber of the Palace of Oz in the Emerald City, where the Wizard receives all visitors who seek an audience. It is the stage for the novel's most theatrical moments: Dorothy, the Scarecrow, the Tin Woodman, and the Cowardly Lion each enter separately and encounter a different terrifying apparition — a Great Head, a Lovely Lady, a Terrible Beast, and a Ball of Fire. The Throne Room embodies Oz's strategy of ruling through illusion and intimidation.

## Geography

The Throne Room is a "big, round room with a high arched roof" whose walls, ceiling, and floor are covered with "large emeralds set closely together" [011-054]. A great light in the center of the roof, "as bright as the sun," makes the emeralds sparkle brilliantly [011-054]. The room is accessed through "a little door" from a waiting area outside [011-054]. Each morning, petitioners assemble outside its doors, hoping to be admitted [011-020]. Only the green girl or the soldier with the green whiskers may approach the Throne Room door to announce visitors [011-009].

## Inhabitants

The Throne Room's sole occupant is the Wizard of Oz, though his appearance varies dramatically. To Dorothy, he manifests as an enormous Head without a body, floating in the air and speaking in a voice that seems to come from nowhere [011-055]. To the Scarecrow, he appears as a "most lovely Lady," seated upon a throne of emeralds, dressed in flowing silks [011-069]. To the Tin Woodman, he presents himself as a "most terrible Beast," nearly as large as an elephant [011-078]. To the Cowardly Lion, he is a "Ball of Fire," so fierce and glowing that the Lion's eyes cannot bear to look directly at it [011-087].

These transformations are later revealed to be illusions created by the Wizard using a variety of mechanical devices — masks, screens, costumes, and props — operated from behind a screen [015-082–092].

## Role in the Story

The Throne Room is the setting for four pivotal audience scenes in Chapter XI. The soldier with the green whiskers first brings Dorothy to the door of the Throne Room, where the green girl signals her to enter alone at the sound of a bell [011-024]. Dorothy encounters the Great Head, which interrogates her and demands that she kill the Wicked Witch of the West before it will grant her wish to return to Kansas [011-051–056].

The Scarecrow is admitted next and meets the Lovely Lady, who promises him brains in exchange for destroying the Wicked Witch [011-069]. The Tin Woodman sees the Terrible Beast and receives the same bargain for a heart [011-078]. The Cowardly Lion confronts the Ball of Fire and is promised courage [011-087]. In each case, the Wizard's promise is conditional on the death of the Wicked Witch of the West.

After the Witch's destruction, the travelers return to the Throne Room in Chapter XV, where the Wizard's deceptions are finally exposed. Toto tips over the screen behind which Oz operates his machinery, revealing "a little old man, with a bald head and a wrinkled face" [015-092].

## Notes

The Throne Room is the architectural centerpiece of Oz's system of rule by illusion. Each visitor sees a different form because Oz tailors his deceptions to exploit individual fears: Dorothy's awe of authority, the Scarecrow's sense of inadequacy, the Tin Woodman's fear of violence, and the Cowardly Lion's instinctual terror of fire. The revelation scene in Chapter XV, in which the Throne Room transforms from a chamber of magic to a workshop of props, is the novel's primary statement on the nature of power: it is not inherent but constructed.
