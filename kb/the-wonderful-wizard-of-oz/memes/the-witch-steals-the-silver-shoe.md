## Overview

The Witch Steals the Silver Shoe is the pivotal confrontation in Chapter XII where the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]] finally succeeds in taking one of Dorothy's [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]], provoking [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] to an act of furious defense that accidentally destroys the Witch and ends her reign of terror over the [[winkies|Winkies]].

## The Trick

For weeks, the Witch had been unable to take the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]] from Dorothy because "the girl never took them off, day or night" [012-045] and the Witch "did not know their true power" [012-045]. Desperate, the Witch devised a cunning trap: "She placed an iron bar across the floor of the kitchen, just hidden from sight" [012-046] so that when Dorothy walked past, "she stumbled over it and fell, but she was not hurt" [012-046]. In falling, one of the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]] slipped from her foot.

Before Dorothy could retrieve it, the Witch "grabbed up the shoe and put it on her own skinny foot" [012-047]. The Witch gloated over her prize, refusing to return it despite Dorothy's demands.

## Dorothy's Fury

The loss of the shoe — a gift from the [[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]] and her most precious possession — enraged Dorothy. "You are a wicked creature!" she cried. "You have no right to take my shoe from me!" [012-047]. In her anger, Dorothy seized a bucket of water that stood nearby and "dashed it over the Witch, wetting her from head to foot" [012-048].

## The Witch's Death

To Dorothy's astonishment, the Witch "gave a loud cry and began to shrink away" [012-048]. The Witch shrieked: "Didn't you know water would be the end of me?" and "in a moment she had melted away like brown sugar" [012-048]. All that remained was "a brown, melted, shapeless mass" on the kitchen floor [012-048] and the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoe]] that Dorothy recovered and put back on.

## Significance

This event is the climax of the Wicked Witch storyline. The Witch's greed for the Silver Shoes — items whose power she did not even understand — leads directly to her destruction. Dorothy's act, born of righteous anger rather than calculated violence, makes her an unwitting hero who liberates the [[winkies|Winkies]] from generations of slavery.
