# Queen of the Field Mice

## Overview

The Queen of the Field Mice is the dignified and capable ruler of the mouse community living near the deadly poppy field. When the **[[scarecrow|Scarecrow]]** appeals to her for help, she overcomes her natural fear of **[[toto|Toto]]** the dog and mobilizes her entire kingdom — thousands of mice, each carrying a piece of string — to pull the sleeping **[[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]]** out of the poisonous flowers (009-032). She is one of the most formally courteous characters in the novel, treating the rescue as a matter of royal obligation, and she leaves the travelers with a standing offer of future assistance (009-040).

## Description

The Queen is a "dignified little mouse" (009-032) who speaks in a "timid voice" (009-016) but acts with decisive authority. She wears no described regalia, but her status is unmistakable in her manner: when introduced to **[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]]**, she makes a formal curtsy (009-034). Her authority over her subjects is absolute — "as soon as they heard her orders they ran away in every direction as fast as possible" to gather the rest of the mice (009-029). Despite her fear of Toto, she approaches the situation with pragmatic courage, negotiating terms for her people's safety before committing them to the rescue.

## Role in the Story

The Queen first appears when the Scarecrow, seeking help for the unconscious Lion, spots a wildcat chasing a small gray mouse. The **[[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]** kills the wildcat, and the rescued mouse reveals herself to be the Queen (009-016). This act of kindness — saving the Queen from the wildcat — creates the debt of gratitude that she later repays by rescuing the Lion.

The Queen asks for reassurance that Toto will not harm her subjects: "Are you sure he will not bite us?" (009-016). Once Dorothy promises to hold Toto tightly, the Queen dispatches messengers to gather her people. Thousands of mice arrive, each carrying a piece of string (009-032). The Scarecrow and Tin Woodman harness the mice to a wooden truck they have built, and the tiny creatures pull the heavy Lion out of the poppy bed to safety (009-037).

Before departing, the Queen makes a formal pledge: "If ever you need us again, come out into the field and call, and we shall hear you and come to your assistance" (009-040). Dorothy holds Toto tightly lest he "run after her and frighten her" (009-041).

## Relationships

The Queen's relationship with the **[[scarecrow|Scarecrow]]** is the most developed in her brief appearance: he is the one who negotiates with her and introduces her to Dorothy as "her Majesty, the Queen" (009-033). Her gratitude to the **[[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]** for saving her from the wildcat (009-016) is the direct motivation for her help. She regards **[[toto|Toto]]** with well-founded fear — a dog's natural inclination to chase mice — and her entire participation in the rescue is contingent on Dorothy's promise to restrain him (009-016).

## Quotes

## Notes

The Queen of the Field Mice exemplifies the fairy-tale logic of reciprocity that governs much of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*: a kindness done (saving the Queen from the wildcat) is repaid in kind (the mice rescuing the Lion). Her character also introduces one of the few collective action sequences in the book — the image of thousands of mice harnessed to a truck is both visually striking and thematically significant, demonstrating that even the smallest creatures can accomplish great things when organized under capable leadership.
