# Heart

## Overview

The Heart is the central object of the Tin Woodman's quest in the Land of Oz. Unlike the Scarecrow's desire for brains or the Cowardly Lion's wish for courage, the Tin Woodman's longing for a heart represents a uniquely human capacity: the ability to love. He explicitly argues that "brains do not make one happy, and happiness is the best thing in the world" (005-051). The heart, in Baum's story, becomes a symbol of emotional wholeness — the quality that distinguishes a living being from a mere mechanical body.

## Properties

In the world of Oz, the heart has no literal magical properties — it is a symbolic organ. The Tin Woodman's original human heart was what enabled him to fall in love with a Munchkin maiden (005-045). After the Wicked Witch of the East enchanted his axe to cut off his limbs one by one, each replaced with tin by the tinsmith Ku-Klip, the Woodman gradually lost his human body — and with it, his heart. He reflects on this loss: "I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl, and did not care to marry her" (005-047).

The concept of the heart in the story carries several attributes:

- **Capacity for love**: The Woodman's defining loss — without a heart, he cannot feel romantic or compassionate attachment
- **Vulnerability to sorrow**: Having a heart means being susceptible to emotional pain; the Woodman is careful never to harm any living creature once he receives it
- **Kindness**: The Woodman associates the heart with the ability to be gentle and merciful (016-025)
- **Happiness**: The Woodman believes happiness comes from the heart, not the intellect (005-051)

## Role in the Story

### The Woodman's Origin

When Dorothy first meets the Tin Woodman in the forest, she hears his life story. He was once a flesh-and-blood woodman who fell deeply in love with a beautiful Munchkin maiden and promised to marry her (005-045). The Wicked Witch of the East, determined to prevent the marriage, enchanted his axe. Each time he swung it, it cut off a part of his body — legs, arms, head, and finally his torso. The tinsmith replaced each part with tin, but "there was one danger — that my joints would rust" (005-048). Left standing in the forest, unable to move, the Woodman realized his greatest loss: "I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love" (005-047). 

### The Quest

When Dorothy and the Scarecrow oil his joints and free him, the Tin Woodman joins their journey to the Emerald City with a single purpose: "Do you suppose Oz could give me a heart?" (005-029). He is emphatic about his choice, telling the Scarecrow: "Once I had brains, and a heart also; so, having tried them both, I should much rather have a heart" (005-040).

The debate between the Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman about which is more valuable — brains or heart — presents a central tension. The Scarecrow argues: "I shall ask for brains instead of a heart; for a fool would not know what to do with a heart if he had one" (005-050). The Woodman counters: "I shall take the heart, for brains do not make one happy, and happiness is the best thing in the world" (005-051). Dorothy, listening silently, can only think of her own heart's desire: getting back to Kansas (005-052).

### The Heart Without a Heart

Paradoxically, the Tin Woodman demonstrates the very qualities a heart is supposed to enable — kindness, compassion, care — long before receiving one. He weeps for a beetle he accidentally steps on, rusting his jaws shut (006-008); he pleads with the Cowardly Lion not to harm the tiny field mouse (009-015); he refuses to join in violence against the Wicked Witch of the West. This irony — that the Woodman acts with a heart before having one — is one of the story's philosophical depths.

### Receiving the Heart

When the Wizard is exposed as a humbug, the Tin Woodman angrily demands satisfaction (015-031). But even the humbug Wizard fulfills the quest: he cuts a square hole in the Woodman's breast and inserts a heart "made entirely of silk and stuffed with sawdust" (016-023). When the Woodman asks anxiously, "But is it a kind heart?" (016-025), Oz reassures him: "Oh, very!" (016-026). The Tin Woodman emerges beaming: "I am very grateful to you, and shall never forget your kindness" (016-028). He now has "a heart that any man might be proud of" (016-027).

### Aftermath

With his heart, the Tin Woodman rules the Winkies after the Wicked Witch of the West is destroyed. He remains with Dorothy only until she can return home, then takes up his new position as a kind ruler — fulfilling what a heart symbolizes: the capacity to care for others.

## Notes

The heart as a motif in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* participates in a long literary tradition of the heart-versus-mind dichotomy. Baum's treatment is gently ironic: the Tin Woodman, who complains of having "no heart," consistently acts with more compassion than anyone else in the story; the Wizard's "heart of silk and sawdust" is a placebo that works. The moral seems to be that the qualities we believe we lack may already be within us — or, alternately, that believing we have them is half the battle. The substitution of a physical token (the silk heart, the bran-and-pin brain, the green liquid courage) for internal qualities is a child's version of the philosophical problem of identity: what makes us who we are?
