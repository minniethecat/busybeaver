# Munchkin Country

## Overview

Munchkin Country is the eastern quadrant of the Land of Oz and the first Ozian region Dorothy encounters after the cyclone deposits her house there. It is a "country of marvelous beauty" (002-003) whose inhabitants — the Munchkins — wear blue, live in round blue-domed houses, and had been enslaved by the Wicked Witch of the East until Dorothy's arrival accidentally killed her. The region's liberation sets the story in motion, and its culture of gratitude and hospitality provides Dorothy with her first experience of Oz's strangeness and wonder.

## Geography

Munchkin Country occupies the eastern portion of Oz. The landscape Dorothy first sees is rich and cultivated: "There were lovely patches of greensward all about, with stately trees bearing rich and luscious fruits. Banks of gorgeous flowers were on every hand, and birds with rare and brilliant plumage sang and fluttered in the trees and bushes" (002-003). A small brook runs near where Dorothy's house lands (002-004). The region is heavily agricultural — the Munchkins "were not as big as the grown folk Dorothy had been used to, but they were well formed and pleasant faced" (002-003).

**[[boq|Boq]]** the rich Munchkin describes the journey from Munchkin Country to the Emerald City: "it is a long way to the Emerald City, and it will take you many days. The country here is rich and pleasant, but you must pass through rough and dangerous places before you reach the end of your journey" (003-026).

## Inhabitants

The Munchkins are a small-statured people who wear blue clothing and live in round houses with blue domes. They are agricultural and communal — they gather for feasts and dancing to celebrate Dorothy's arrival (003-016). **[[boq|Boq]]** the rich Munchkin is the only named individual among them. The Munchkins revere the **[[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]]** as their protector, though she lives in the North rather than the East. They were ruled by the **[[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]]**, who "held all the Munchkins in bondage for many years, making them slave for her night and day" (002-016).

## Role in the Story

Munchkin Country is where Dorothy's Oz journey begins. Her house lands on and kills the Wicked Witch of the East, and the Munchkins celebrate her as "a great sorceress" (003-017). The **[[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]]** arrives to greet Dorothy and gifts her the Witch of the East's **[[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]]** (002-035). She also bestows the protective **[[kiss-of-the-witch-of-the-north|Kiss of the Witch of the North]]** on Dorothy's forehead, which protects her throughout the journey.

After a night of feasting and dancing at Boq's house, Dorothy sets out from Munchkin Country on the **[[road-of-yellow-brick|road of yellow brick]]** toward the Emerald City (003-028). The Munchkins do not reappear in the narrative except through passing references — most notably when the Wizard says he will "go to the Land of the Munchkins and be their ruler" after leaving the Emerald City, though he never arrives (015-005, paraphrased).

## Notable Features

- **Blue Theme**: Everything in Munchkin Country — houses, clothing, sheets — is blue. "Blue is the color of the Munchkins" (003-021).
- **The Death Site**: The spot where Dorothy's house crushed the Wicked Witch of the East. Only her silver-shoed feet remained visible (002-012).
- **Round Houses**: The Munchkins' distinctive domed architecture.

## Quotes

> "The cyclone had set the house down, very gently — for a cyclone — in the midst of a country of marvelous beauty." (002-003)

> "We are so grateful to you for having killed the Wicked Witch of the East, and for setting our people free from bondage." — Witch of the North (002-008)

## Notes

Munchkin Country is the most fully realized of the four Oz quadrants in the novel. Baum develops it through sensory detail — the blue color scheme, the round houses, the fruit trees, the feasting — rather than through abstract geography. The 1939 film compresses the Munchkin celebration into an elaborate song-and-dance number, but the novel's Munchkin Country is quieter and more agrarian.
