## Overview

The China Clown, also known as Mr. Joker, is a living china figurine who inhabits the Dainty China Country encountered by Dorothy and her companions in Chapter XX. A comic figure dressed in bright colors, he is famous for his habit of standing on his head — a trick that has left him covered in cracks from repeated breakage and repair. He greets the travelers with an impudent rhyme before being scolded by the China Princess.

## Description

Mr. Joker is a "jolly little clown" dressed in a ruffled gown of red, yellow, and green, with round red spots painted on his cheeks and a tall, pointed cap [020-028]. Despite his colorful appearance, his china body is "completely covered with cracks, running every which way," the accumulated damage from his many accidents [020-070]. These cracks plainly show the mending in many places, evidence of his persistent recklessness [020-068]. He moves with a saucy, irreverent manner, puffing out his cheeks and nodding his head impudently at strangers [020-072].

## Role in the Story

When Dorothy and her companions enter the Dainty China Country, the China Princess points out Mr. Joker as an example of the foolishness of some of her subjects. She explains that he is "always trying to stand upon his head" and has broken himself so often that he has been mended in a hundred places [020-068].

The Clown then approaches the travelers and delivers a teasing little rhyme:

> "My lady fair, / Why do you stare / At poor old Mr. Joker? / You're quite as stiff / And prim as if / You'd eaten up a poker!" [020-074–076]

The China Princess rebukes him for his rudeness, saying strangers should be treated with respect [020-081]. Mr. Joker cheekily replies, "Well, that's respect, I expect," and immediately demonstrates his signature trick by standing on his head [020-083]. The Princess dismisses him with the observation that "he is considerably cracked in his head, and that makes him foolish" [020-085].

## Relationships

- **[[the-china-princess]]** — The China Princess tolerates Mr. Joker with exasperation, using him as an object lesson in foolish behavior for Dorothy. She scolds him for his impudence but seems accustomed to his antics [020-068, 020-081, 020-085].
- **[[dorothy-gale]]** — Dorothy is the audience for the Clown's teasing rhyme. She takes no offense, telling the Princess, "Oh, I don't mind him a bit" [020-087].

## Quotes

> "Now there is Mr. Joker, one of our clowns," continued the china lady, "who is always trying to stand upon his head. He has broken himself so often that he is mended in a hundred places, and doesn't look at all pretty." [020-068]

> The Clown put his hands in his pockets, and after puffing out his cheeks and nodding his head at them saucily, he said: "My lady fair, / Why do you stare / At poor old Mr. Joker? / You're quite as stiff / And prim as if / You'd eaten up a poker!" [020-072–076]

> "Well, that's respect, I expect," declared the Clown, and immediately stood upon his head. [020-083]

## Notes

Mr. Joker is one of several china figurines encountered in Chapter XX, but he is the only one given a name and a speaking role beyond the China Princess. His cracked body serves as visual evidence of the fragility of the china people — a fragility that motivates the China Princess to refuse Dorothy's offer to be taken to Kansas, where she fears being broken on a mantelpiece. Mr. Joker's reckless clowning, despite the damage it causes him, marks him as the comic foil of the Dainty China Country.
