# Brain

## Overview

The Brain is the central object of the Scarecrow's quest in the Land of Oz. Made of straw and convinced of his own stupidity, the Scarecrow believes that acquiring brains will transform him into a complete person — "as good a man as any of them" (004-024). The brain represents intellect, reason, and the capacity for knowledge. Yet throughout the story, the Scarecrow consistently demonstrates the very intelligence he believes he lacks: he devises solutions to every obstacle the group encounters, from crossing the great ditch (007-026) to escaping the poppy field (008-031).

## Properties

The brain in Baum's story is fundamentally a symbolic quality rather than a magical one. Its key attributes include:

- **The ability to think and reason**: What the Scarecrow believes he lacks — the capacity to process information and reach conclusions
- **Knowledge acquisition**: The Scarecrow associates brains with knowing things; he tells Dorothy: "I do not want people to call me a fool" (004-024)
- **Problem-solving**: Ironically, the Scarecrow is the group's primary problem-solver, using logic where others rely on instinct
- **Metaphorical placeholder**: The Wizard's "bran-new brains" (016-012) — a pun on "brand-new" — are literally bran mixed with pins and needles, yet they work as a placebo

## Role in the Story

### The Scarecrow's Origin

Dorothy discovers the Scarecrow mounted on a pole in a cornfield (003-035). He was made only two days before, and his greatest sorrow is his empty head: "You see, I am stuffed, so I have no brains at all" (003-045). When he learns of Dorothy's journey to the Emerald City, he asks: "Do you think if I went to the Emerald City with you, that Oz would give me some brains?" (003-047). Dorothy's practical reply — "If Oz will not give you any brains you will be no worse off than you are now" (003-048) — convinces him to join.

### Evidence of Intelligence

Despite his claims of stupidity, the Scarecrow repeatedly proves himself the most ingenious traveler. When faced with the great ditch, he plans how to cross it (007-026). When the Kalidahs pursue them, he has the Tin Woodman chop down the tree bridge (007-034). When Dorothy and the Lion fall asleep in the deadly poppy field, he devises a plan to rescue them using the field mice (009-004). The Lion observes: "One would almost suspect you had brains in your head, instead of straw" (009-008).

The old crow who first taunted the Scarecrow inadvertently planted the seed of his quest by saying: "If you only had brains in your head you would be as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them. Brains are the only things worth having in this world, no matter whether one is a crow or a man" (004-024).

### Receiving the Brain

When the Wizard is unmasked as a fraud, the Scarecrow still approaches him. Oz unfastens the Scarecrow's head, empties out the straw, and refills it with "a measure of bran, which he mixed with a great many pins and needles" (016-011). The Wizard declares: "Hereafter you will be a great man, for I have given you a lot of bran-new brains" (016-012). The pun is double-edged — "bran-new" sounds like "brand-new" while acknowledging the humble bran filling.

Dorothy observes that the Scarecrow's head is "quite bulged out at the top with brains" (016-014). Asked how he feels, the Scarecrow answers earnestly, "I feel wise indeed. When I get used to my brains I shall know everything" (016-016). The Tin Woodman notices the pins and needles sticking out, and the Lion wryly remarks, "That is proof that he is sharp" (016-018).

### Aftermath

With his new brains, the Scarecrow is chosen by the Wizard to rule the Emerald City after Oz departs in his balloon (017-012). He proves to be a wise and capable ruler, confirming that the intelligence was always there — he simply needed permission to believe in it.

## Notes

The brain in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* functions as a classic placebo narrative. The Scarecrow's supposed lack of intelligence is his defining insecurity, yet he is the most resourceful character in the group. Baum's message — that intelligence is more about confidence and application than innate endowment — is delivered through gentle comedy: the Scarecrow receives literal stuffing (bran and pins) and immediately declares himself wise. The pun on "bran-new brains" encapsulates the story's philosophical lightness: the transformation is real in its effects, however flimsy its material cause. This motif has resonated through American culture, with the Scarecrow's quest becoming shorthand for the pursuit of intellectual self-respect.
