## Overview

The **making of the Scarecrow** is the origin story recounted by the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] in Chapter IV. A Munchkin farmer constructs the Scarecrow from a sack of straw, paints his features, and mounts him on a pole to frighten crows from the corn. The Scarecrow's creation is narrated in intimate detail—from the painting of his first ear to his lonely vigil on the pole—and establishes his defining motivation: the desire for brains.

## The Making

The Scarecrow recounts his creation as having occurred "only day before yesterday" [004-014], making him a being of scarcely two days' existence at the time he meets Dorothy. The farmer first painted his ears, prompting a critique from a second Munchkin that "they aren't straight," to which the farmer replied pragmatically, "They are ears just the same" [004-015–004-016].

The farmer then painted the Scarecrow's right eye:

> As soon as it was finished I found myself looking at him and at everything around me with a great deal of curiosity, for this was my first glimpse of the world. [004-017]

The second eye was made "a little bigger" to improve the Scarecrow's vision [004-019]. Then came the nose, the mouth, the body, arms, and legs. When the head was fastened on, the Scarecrow felt "very proud, for I thought I was just as good a man as anyone" [004-019]. The farmer declared approvingly, "This fellow will scare the crows fast enough. He looks just like a man," to which the other Munchkin replied, "Why, he is a man" [004-020–004-021].

## Life on the Pole

The farmer carried the Scarecrow to the cornfield and set him on a tall pole. The Scarecrow, not yet understanding the limitations of his straw body, tried to walk after the departing Munchkins but found his feet would not touch the ground [004-022].

For a time, the Scarecrow took satisfaction in frightening the crows, which "pleased me and made me feel that I was quite an important person" [004-022]. But an old crow—wiser than the rest—saw through the illusion:

> "I wonder if that farmer thought to fool me in this clumsy manner. Any crow of sense could see that you are only stuffed with straw." [004-023]

The old crow then ate all the corn he wanted, and other birds followed, making the Scarecrow feel inadequate. But the crow offered a consoling thought that would become the Scarecrow's guiding philosophy:

> "If you only had brains in your head you would be as good a man as any of them, and a better man than some of them. Brains are the only things worth having in this world, no matter whether one is a crow or a man." [004-024]

## Consequences

This encounter with the old crow planted the seed of the Scarecrow's defining desire. After the crows departed, he resolved to "try hard to get some brains" [004-025]. When Dorothy arrived, pulled him off the pole, and told him about the [[wizard-of-oz|Great Oz]], the Scarecrow immediately saw his path forward and joined her quest to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]].

## Significance

The Scarecrow's creation story is the first of three origin narratives told by Dorothy's companions, followed by the [[enchantment-of-the-tin-woodman|Tin Woodman's enchantment]] and the [[cowardly-lion|Lion's]] confession of cowardice. Each establishes the companion's defining lack (brains, heart, courage) while subtly demonstrating that they already possess the quality they seek. The Scarecrow, who claims to have no brains, is the one who encounters the old crow's wisdom and draws the logical conclusion—a paradox that runs throughout the narrative.
