# Stork

## Overview

The Stork is a minor but pivotal helper character who appears in a single scene to rescue the Scarecrow from the river (008-025). When Dorothy, the Tin Woodman, and the Cowardly Lion are stranded on the riverbank after losing the Scarecrow to the current, the Stork flies by, stops to investigate, and — despite initial hesitation about the Scarecrow's weight — carries him back to safety (008-035). The episode exemplifies the story's recurring motif of unexpected kindness from strangers in the Land of Oz.

## Description

The Stork is a large bird with a long neck and "great claws" capable of grasping and carrying the Scarecrow (008-035). She speaks in a practical, somewhat skeptical tone — questioning whether the travelers are on the right road (008-028) and expressing doubt about her ability to lift the Scarecrow (008-032). She is also a mother, mentioning that her "babies are waiting in the nest" (008-038), which adds a domestic dimension to her brief appearance and explains her haste to depart after the rescue.

## Role in the Story

The Stork appears after the Scarecrow, stranded on his pole in the middle of a river, is separated from the group. Dorothy, the Tin Woodman, and the Lion sit on the bank, unable to help, until "a Stork flew by, who, upon seeing them, stopped to rest at the water's edge" (008-025). The Stork inquires about their situation and learns of the Scarecrow's predicament. When Dorothy assures her the Scarecrow is light — "he is stuffed with straw" (008-033) — the Stork agrees to attempt the rescue.

Flying over the water, the Stork "with her great claws grabbed the Scarecrow by the arm and carried him up into the air and back to the bank" (008-035). The rescue is swift and effective. The Stork then flies alongside the group briefly, offering well-wishes: "I hope you will find the Emerald City and that Oz will help you" (008-038), before departing to tend to her young. The Scarecrow vows to repay the kindness: "if I ever get any brains I shall find the Stork again and do her some kindness in return" (008-037).

## Relationships

The Stork has no ongoing relationships with any character. Her interaction is entirely with **[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]]**, who explains their predicament and persuades her to help (008-033). The **[[scarecrow|Scarecrow]]** is the direct beneficiary of her kindness, and he expresses the deepest gratitude (008-037). She briefly addresses the **[[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]** and **[[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]]** as part of "the queer party" (008-028) but has no meaningful exchange with them.

## Quotes

> "Who are you and where are you going?" (008-026)

> "This isn't the road," said the Stork, as she twisted her long neck and looked sharply at the queer party. (008-028)

> "Well, I'll try," said the Stork, "but if I find he is too heavy to carry I shall have to drop him in the river again." (008-034)

> "I always like to help anyone in trouble. But I must go now, for my babies are waiting in the nest for me." (008-038)

## Notes

The Stork is one of several animal helpers who assist Dorothy's party at critical moments, alongside the **[[queen-of-the-field-mice|Queen of the Field Mice]]** and the Winged Monkeys. This pattern — a stranger appearing at the moment of greatest need, helping, and departing — reinforces the fairy-tale structure of the narrative. The Stork's motherhood (008-038) is a rare domestic detail in a story otherwise populated by solitary figures and constructs.
