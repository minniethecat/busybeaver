# Uncle Henry's Farm

## Overview

Uncle Henry's Farm is a modest, weather-beaten homestead on the great Kansas prairie, home to Dorothy Gale before the cyclone carries her to the Land of Oz [001-001]. The farm is the narrative's anchor in the real world — a place of unrelenting grayness, hard work, and quiet love that Dorothy spends her entire journey trying to return to [001-001].

## Geography

The farm sits in the midst of the great Kansas prairies [001-001], an expanse so vast that from the doorway Dorothy can see "nothing but the great gray prairie on every side. Not a tree nor a house broke the broad sweep of flat country that reached to the edge of the sky in all directions" [001-002]. The land is flat, plowed, and sun-baked into cracked gray earth — even the grass blades are burned gray at the tips [001-002].

The isolation is profound: the nearest neighbor is miles away, the lumber for the house had to be carried by wagon many miles [001-001]. A cyclone cellar — a small hole dug into the ground, reached by a trap door in the middle of the floor — serves as the family's only refuge from the prairie twisters [001-001].

## Architecture

The farmhouse is strikingly humble: "four walls, a floor and a roof, which made one room" [001-001]. This single room contains a rusty cookstove, a cupboard for dishes, a table, three or four chairs, and two beds — a big bed in one corner for Uncle Henry and Aunt Em, and a small bed in another for Dorothy [001-001]. There is no garret, no cellar above ground — only the cyclone hole beneath the trap door [001-001].

The house was once painted, but "the sun blistered the paint and the rains washed it away" [001-002]. Now it is "as dull and gray as everything else" [001-002]. The outbuildings include sheds where cows and horses are kept [001-006].

After the first house is carried away by the cyclone to the Land of Oz, Uncle Henry builds a new farmhouse — this is the house Dorothy finds upon her return [024-001]. The new house is described only briefly: Dorothy sits up on the broad Kansas prairie and sees "just before her was the new farmhouse Uncle Henry built after the cyclone had carried away the old one" while Uncle Henry is milking cows in the barnyard [024-003].

## Inhabitants

### Uncle Henry

Uncle Henry is a farmer who "worked hard from morning till night and did not know what joy was" [001-003]. Gray from his long beard to his rough boots, he looks "stern and solemn, and rarely spoke" [001-003]. Yet his care for Dorothy is evident: when the cyclone approaches, his first thought is to "go look after the stock" [001-006]. In the end, he builds a new farmhouse to replace the one destroyed, and Dorothy finds him milking cows in the barnyard when she returns [024-003].

### Aunt Em

Aunt Em was once a young, pretty wife, but "the sun and wind had changed her" — her eyes, cheeks, and lips faded to sober gray, she grew thin and gaunt, and never smiled [001-002]. She is startled by Dorothy's laughter, pressing her hand to her heart whenever Dorothy's merry voice reaches her ears, and "still looked at the little girl with wonder that she could find anything to laugh at" [001-002]. Despite her severity, she acts decisively when danger comes — dropping her work, throwing open the trap door, and climbing into the cyclone cellar [001-008].

Dorothy's deepest motivation throughout her journey is returning to Aunt Em: "Aunt Em will surely think something dreadful has happened to me, and that will make her put on mourning; and unless the crops are better this year than they were last, I am sure Uncle Henry cannot afford it" [023-014].

### Toto

Toto is the little black dog who is Dorothy's constant companion — "the one thing that made Dorothy laugh, and saved her from growing as gray as her other surroundings" [001-004]. Toto is not gray: he has "long silky hair and small black eyes that twinkled merrily on either side of his funny, wee nose" [001-004]. He plays all day, Dorothy plays with him, and she loves him dearly — in a gray world of toil, Toto is the single splash of joy [001-004].

## The Cyclone

The farm is the site of the cyclone that changes everything. Uncle Henry first notices the danger: "There's a cyclone coming, Em" [001-006]. As he runs to the sheds, Aunt Em screams for Dorothy to run for the cellar [001-008]. Toto jumps from Dorothy's arms and hides under the bed; Dorothy chases him — and in that moment's delay, the trap door closes, the wind shrieks, and the house whirls up into the air [001-009].

The house rotates "two or three times and rose slowly through the air," Dorothy feeling "as if she were going up in a balloon" [001-009]. The farmhouse is carried miles across the prairie, over the Deadly Desert, and into the Land of Oz — where it lands squarely on the Wicked Witch of the East [001-010].

## Return and Reunion

After all her adventures — the yellow brick road, the Emerald City, the Wicked Witch of the West, Glinda's palace — Dorothy's silver shoes carry her home in three steps. She finds herself sitting on the broad Kansas prairie, the new farmhouse before her, Uncle Henry milking cows, and Toto running toward the barn barking furiously [024-003].

The shoes fall off in flight and are lost forever in the desert [024-005]. But Dorothy is home. The gray prairie, the simple farmhouse, the stern but loving aunt and uncle — these were worth crossing a magical land, defeating two wicked witches, and traversing the Deadly Desert to return to.

As Glinda observed, if Dorothy had known the power of the Silver Shoes from the start, she could have returned on her very first day in Oz — "but then I should not have had my wonderful brains!" cries the Scarecrow. "And I should not have had my lovely heart," says the Tin Woodman. "And I should have lived a coward forever," declares the Lion [023-037]. The farm, in its gray simplicity, is home — but the friends made along the way make the journey worth having taken.
