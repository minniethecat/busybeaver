# Quadlings

## Overview

The [[quadlings|Quadlings]] are the inhabitants of the southern quadrant of the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]], known as Quadling Country. Ruled by the benevolent [[glinda|Glinda the Good Witch]], the Quadlings are the only one of Oz's four peoples never subjected to a wicked witch's tyranny. The country of the Quadlings is described as "rich and happy" with "scarlet fields and bright red flowers" (022-023, 022-001), and the Quadlings themselves are a cheerful folk distinguished by their round heads and red clothing.

## Characteristics

The Quadlings are physically distinct from the other Oz peoples — they have "round heads that looked like pumpkins" and are noted for their good humor: "they were always laughing and joking, which Dorothy thought very good-natured" (022-026). Like the other quadrants of Oz, their preferred color reflects their region: red is the color of the Quadlings, just as blue is for the Munchkins and yellow for the Winkies (022-023). The country is agriculturally prosperous — the Quadlings maintain "fields of ripening grain" and their houses are painted a "bright red" (022-021).

## Role in the Story

### Dorothy's Final Destination

The Quadling Country is the travelers' last destination in Oz and represents the fulfillment of their quest. The [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard]] had promised to help Dorothy return to [[kansas|Kansas]] in exchange for killing the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]] — but after his departure in the balloon, the travelers are directed to [[glinda|Glinda the Good Witch]], who "rules over the Quadlings" (018-033). The journey south is the last leg of Dorothy's adventure, crossing through the [[fighting-trees|Fighting Trees]], the [[china-country|Dainty China Country]], and the land of the [[hammer-heads|Hammer-Heads]] before reaching the Quadling border (Chapter XIX–XXI).

### The Quadlings' Worldview

The Quadlings' attitude toward outsiders is shaped by their trust in [[glinda|Glinda]]. The [[soldier-with-the-green-whiskers|Guardian of the Gates]] remarks that "none of the Quadlings ever come to the Emerald City" (018-037) — they are content within their own prosperous country and feel no need to travel. Despite this isolationism, they greet [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and her companions warmly, providing food and shelter (022-025–022-026). A farmer's wife gives them "a good dinner, with three kinds of cake and four kinds of cookies" and her children "danced around the party and laughed with merry laughter" (022-026).

### Glinda's Palace

The travelers arrive at [[glinda]]'s castle, described as a grand building "of red brick, with great gates of polished silver," guarded by three young girls dressed in "handsome red uniforms trimmed with gold braid" — Quadling guards whose "round red cheeks and bright eyes showed they were perfectly healthy and happy" (023-002–023-004). It is here that Dorothy learns the secret of the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]] and achieves her long-awaited return to [[kansas|Kansas]].

## Quotes

## Notes

The Quadlings are one of the four color-coded peoples of Oz (red for the South). Unlike the Munchkins (freed at the story's opening) and the Winkies (freed mid-story), the Quadlings were never enslaved. This makes Quadling Country the narrative's gold standard for what Oz can be — a peaceful, prosperous land under wise and benevolent rule. The physical description of the Quadlings (round heads) is one of Baum's more whimsical characterizations and aligns with the book's overall tone of gentle absurdity.
