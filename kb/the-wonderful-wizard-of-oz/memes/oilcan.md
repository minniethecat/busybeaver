## Overview

The **oilcan** is a simple but essential tool belonging to the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]. Kept on a shelf in his forest cottage, it is used to lubricate his tin joints and prevent them from rusting. After Dorothy rescues the Woodman from his year-long rusted imprisonment, she carries the oilcan in her basket, making her the guardian of his mobility. The oilcan is the mundane object upon which the Woodman's very existence depends—a reminder that his magnificent tin body comes with the perpetual vulnerability of rust.

## Role in the Story

### The Rescue

When Dorothy and the Scarecrow first discover the Tin Woodman standing motionless in the forest, he tells them: "Get an oilcan and oil my joints. They are rusted so badly that I cannot move them at all; if I am well oiled I shall soon be all right again" [005-017]. Dorothy finds the oilcan in his cottage and, with the Scarecrow's help, oils his neck, arms, and legs until he can move freely [005-019–005-023].

The Woodman had stood rusted for more than a year because he had forgotten to oil himself before a rainstorm [005-048].

### On the Journey

After his rescue, the Tin Woodman asks Dorothy to carry the oilcan in her basket: "For, if I should get caught in the rain, and rust again, I would need the oilcan badly" [005-033]. This act of trust establishes Dorothy as the caretaker of the Woodman's physical integrity.

The oilcan proves its importance when the Woodman weeps after accidentally stepping on a beetle: his tears cause his jaws to rust shut, and the Scarecrow must fetch the oilcan from Dorothy's basket to free him [005-0??]. This moment illustrates the paradox of the Woodman's condition—he now has a heart (or at least the capacity for compassion), but expressing emotion literally threatens his ability to function.

## Significance

The oilcan is the humblest of all the magical or significant objects in the story. Unlike the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]], the [[golden-cap|Golden Cap]], or the [[silver-whistle|Silver Whistle]], it possesses no supernatural power. Yet it is the object upon which one of the four protagonists most depends for his survival. In a world of witches and wizards, the oilcan is a reminder that ordinary tools, ordinary preparedness, and ordinary kindness (Dorothy carrying it) can be as important as magic.
