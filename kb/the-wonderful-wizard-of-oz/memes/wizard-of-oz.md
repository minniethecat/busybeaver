# Wizard of Oz

## Overview

The Wizard of Oz is the titular figure of Baum's novel — and its greatest deception. Believed by all the inhabitants of Oz to be a mighty wizard capable of granting any wish, he is revealed near the story's end to be "a very good man — just a very bad wizard" (015-065): an ordinary human from Omaha, Nebraska, who arrived in Oz by balloon and built his authority on tricks, illusions, and the willingness of people to believe. His unmasking as "The Great and Terrible Humbug" (015-043) does not diminish his role in the story — if anything, it elevates him from a magical figure to a more human one, a man who rules not through power but through the careful management of perception.

## Description

The Wizard's physical reality is mundane: "a little old man, with a bald head and a wrinkled face" (015-042). But his projected forms are terrifying: a giant green Head without a body (011-008), a lovely Lady with flowing green hair (011-055), a terrible Beast with five eyes, five arms, and five legs (011-072), and a Ball of Fire that speaks in a roaring voice (011-096). Each of these is a mechanical illusion — "after practicing for many years, I thought I could still do the tricks fairly well" (015-048).

His greatest illusion, however, is sustained not by machinery but by policy: the green spectacles locked onto every citizen's eyes, making the Emerald City appear green when "as a matter of fact, the city is no greener than any other city" (015-073).

## Role in the Story

### The Legend

The Wizard is introduced by reputation. The Witch of the North whispers his name: "Oz himself is the Great Wizard" (002-032). Every character in Oz speaks of him with awe and terror. He is said to be more powerful than all the witches combined — a claim that, as it turns out, is entirely fabricated — and the sole authority capable of granting wishes.

### The Audiences

Each traveler's audience with Oz is a masterpiece of misdirection. To Dorothy, he appears as a gigantic Head: "I am Oz, the Great and Terrible. Who are you, and why do you seek me?" (011-008). To the Scarecrow, a lovely Lady (011-055). To the Tin Woodman, a terrible Beast (011-072). To the Lion, a Ball of Fire (011-096). In every case, he makes the same demand: kill the Wicked Witch of the West, and he will grant their wishes — a demand he makes knowing he cannot grant wishes at all, hoping the task is impossible.

### The Unmasking

The Wizard's exposure comes through Toto. When Oz refuses to see the travelers after the Witch's death, the Lion roars in frustration — and Toto, startled, knocks over a screen in the corner of the Throne Room (015-044). Behind it stands the real Oz: "a little old man, with a bald head and a wrinkled face, who seemed to be as much surprised as they were" (015-042).

His confession is immediate and complete: "I am a humbug" (015-043). He explains his history: a balloonist from Omaha who worked in a circus, drifted to Oz when his balloon's ropes broke, and was greeted as a wizard when he descended from the sky (015-068). "I was just as much afraid of the Witches as you are," he admits, "for while I had no magical powers at all I soon found out that the Witches were really able to do wonderful things" (015-074).

### The Humbug's Gifts

The Wizard's finest moment comes after his exposure. Though he cannot grant wishes by magic, he can grant them by psychology. He fills the Scarecrow's head with bran and pins, gives the Tin Woodman a silk-and-sawdust heart, and pours green liquid courage for the Lion — and in every case, the gift works because the recipient believes it will. The Wizard knows that "courage is always inside one" (016-038) — he is merely the catalyst that brings it out.

### The Departure

The Wizard plans to return Dorothy to Kansas by balloon — the same way he arrived. He constructs a silk balloon and announces his departure publicly (017-004). But at the moment of launch, Toto jumps from Dorothy's arms to chase a kitten, Dorothy follows, and the balloon rises without her (017-020). The Wizard floats away, calling back: "I do not know how it works, for you pull the ropes to make the balloon go, and the balloon goes wherever the wind takes it" (017-017). Even in departure, the Wizard's lack of control is the point.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The Wizard is genuinely moved by Dorothy's desire to go home. After his exposure, he works earnestly to help her — even if his balloon plan fails. His final words to her are a mixture of apology and affection.

### The Scarecrow

The Wizard appoints the Scarecrow as his successor, ruler of the Emerald City (017-012). This is perhaps his most genuinely wise act: recognizing that the person who already has the most intelligence is the right choice to lead.

### The Tin Woodman

The Wizard treats the Woodman with the same pragmatic deception he applies to everyone — but when exposed, he gives the Woodman his heart with surprising tenderness.

### The Cowardly Lion

The Wizard's gift to the Lion is the most transparent placebo — and the most philosophically explicit: "Courage is always inside one" (016-038).

### The Wicked Witches

The Wizard's deepest secret: he was terrified of the Witches. "While I had no magical powers at all I soon found out that the Witches were really able to do wonderful things" (015-074). His entire reign was built on the hope that the Witches would not challenge him.

## Notes

The Wizard of Oz is Baum's most subversive creation: a ruler whose authority rests entirely on belief, not power. He has no magic, no army, no special knowledge — only the ability to make people see what he wants them to see. The green spectacles, the mechanical illusions, the booming voice — these are the tools of a consummate showman who understood that perception is reality. The Wizard is both a satire of authority and a surprisingly sympathetic figure: a lost man from Omaha who made the best of an impossible situation, ruling through trickery because trickery was all he had. His final act — giving the travelers the gifts they already possessed — is either a cynic's final con or a wise man's recognition that the only real magic is self-belief. Baum leaves the ambiguity intact.
