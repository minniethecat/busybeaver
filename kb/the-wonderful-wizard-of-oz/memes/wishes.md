# Wishes

## Overview

Wishes are the engine of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. Every character who travels the Yellow Brick Road does so in service of a wish: Dorothy wishes to go home (002-040), the Scarecrow wishes for brains (003-047), the Tin Woodman wishes for a heart (005-029), and the Cowardly Lion wishes for courage (006-037). The Wizard of Oz — whose entire authority rests on the belief that he can grant wishes — is the object of all these desires. The story's resolution reveals that wishes, like Oz's magic, are self-fulfilling: each character already possesses what they wish for.

## Properties

Wishes in Baum's story have several defining characteristics:

- **Clarity of object**: Each wish is specific — brains, heart, courage, home — not vague or abstract
- **The Wizard as intermediary**: All wishes are directed at Oz, who is believed to have the power to grant them
- **Conditional fulfillment**: Oz demands payment: "If you wish me to use my magic power to send you home again you must do something for me first" (011-043)
- **Self-fulfillment**: Every wish is ultimately granted not by magic but by the wisher's own belief and action

## Role in the Story

### The Four Wishes

Each traveler articulates their wish clearly when asked by the Guardian of the Gate: "Why do you wish to see the terrible Oz?" (010-029). Dorothy wants to return to Kansas; the Scarecrow wants brains; the Tin Woodman wants a heart; the Lion wants courage. These four wishes structure the entire narrative.

### The Wizard's Terms

When the travelers finally meet Oz — in his various terrifying forms — he makes his demands: "If you wish me to use my magic power to send you home again you must do something for me first. Help me and I will help you" (011-043). The "something" is killing the Wicked Witch of the West, an apparently impossible task that the travelers nonetheless accomplish.

### The Resolution

After the Witch's death and the Wizard's exposure, the wishes are granted — not through magic but through belief. The Scarecrow receives bran and pins and believes himself wise (016-012). The Tin Woodman receives a silk-and-sawdust heart and believes himself capable of love (016-023). The Lion drinks green liquid and believes himself courageous (016-038). Dorothy's wish — the deepest and most genuine — is fulfilled by Glinda, who reveals that the Silver Shoes could have granted it at any time (023-025).

### The Nature of Wishing

The story's treatment of wishes is both playful and philosophical. The Wizard admits he cannot grant wishes: "I'm not much of a magician" (015-081). Yet the wishes are granted nonetheless — not by Oz's power but by the wisher's own journey. The Yellow Brick Road, the dangers faced, the friendships formed — these are what prepare each character to receive their wish. Dorothy could have gone home on day one; she needed the journey to understand what home meant.

## Notes

The wish motif in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* belongs to the oldest tradition of storytelling — the quest for a boon from a powerful figure. Baum's innovation is to democratize the granting: the Wizard is a fraud, the magic is placebo, and the real power lies in the wisher. This is a distinctively American fairy tale, in which self-reliance and self-belief replace supernatural intervention. The story's most radical claim is that wishes are not granted — they are realized, through the act of wishing itself.
