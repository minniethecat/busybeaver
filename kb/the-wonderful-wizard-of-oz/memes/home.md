# Home

## Overview

Home is the central emotional axis of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. Dorothy Gale's every action in the Land of Oz is motivated by a single, unchanging goal: to return to Kansas and Aunt Em. Though Kansas is described in the opening chapter as relentlessly gray and grim — "the sun had baked the plowed land into a gray mass" (001-001) — Dorothy never wavers in her desire to go back. Home, in Baum's story, is not a place of beauty or comfort but of belonging. As Dorothy explains to the Guardian of the Gate, "No matter how dreary and gray our homes are, we people of flesh and blood would rather live there than in any other country, be it ever so beautiful" (004-009).

## Properties

Home in Baum's narrative carries several key attributes:

- **Belonging over beauty**: Home is defined by who is there (Aunt Em, Uncle Henry) rather than by what it looks like
- **Irreplaceable attachment**: Dorothy refuses every alternative — ruling the Winkies, staying in the Emerald City, remaining in any beautiful land
- **Driving motivation**: Every step Dorothy takes in Oz — every danger faced, every enemy confronted — is toward the single goal of returning home
- **The power of simplicity**: The gray Kansas prairie is revealed, through Dorothy's unwavering love, to be more precious than the jeweled cities of Oz

## Role in the Story

### Kansas: Gray but Beloved

The story opens by establishing the bleakness of Dorothy's home: the Kansas prairie is gray — gray grass, gray house, even gray-faced Aunt Em and Uncle Henry (001-001). Only Toto brings color and laughter into Dorothy's life (001-006). When the cyclone carries Dorothy to the dazzling Land of Oz — "a country of marvelous beauty" with "stately trees bearing rich and luscious fruits" (002-004) — the contrast is total: she has been transported from the grayest place imaginable to the most vivid.

### The Unwavering Goal

From the moment Dorothy lands in Oz, she asks only one thing: "I want to get back to Kansas" (002-040). She explains her situation repeatedly to each new ally: "I am anxious to get back to my aunt and uncle, for I am sure they will worry about me" (004-013). Even when offered riches, magic, or rulership, Dorothy's answer never changes — she wants to go home.

When the Guardian of the Gate tells her Oz might send her home, she answers: "I don't know, but it is my home, and I'm sure it's somewhere" (010-038). This simple certainty — that Kansas is home regardless of its qualities — is the emotional core of the story.

### The Silver Shoes' Secret

The story's greatest irony is that Dorothy had the power to go home from her very first day in Oz. Glinda reveals: "Your Silver Shoes will carry you over the desert. If you had known their power you could have gone back to your Aunt Em the very first day you came to this country" (023-025). Everything — the journey, the Wizard's demands, the killing of the Wicked Witch of the West — was unnecessary. Dorothy could have gone home at any moment.

The Scarecrow asks why Glinda didn't tell her sooner, and Glinda explains: "Because I knew she would not believe me. She had to learn it for herself" (023-027). The path home required Dorothy's own growth and discovery.

### The Return

At the story's conclusion, Dorothy knocks the heels of the Silver Shoes together three times and commands: "Take me home to Aunt Em!" (023-038). The shoes carry her across the desert in three steps. She lands on the Kansas prairie, rushes to Aunt Em, and cries: "I'm so glad to be at home again!" (023-044). The shoes, lost forever in the Deadly Desert, have served their purpose.

## Notes

The "there's no place like home" theme of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is Baum's most enduring contribution to American culture. It is significant that home is not idealized — Kansas is grim and gray, and the people Dorothy loves (Aunt Em) are described as thin, gaunt, and unsmiling. Baum refuses to sentimentalize the actual conditions of home; what matters is belonging, not beauty. This honest depiction of home as imperfect yet irreplaceable gives the story its emotional weight. The revelation that Dorothy could have returned at any time — that the power was literally at her feet all along — is a gentle lesson in self-reliance and the discovery that what we seek is often closer than we think.
