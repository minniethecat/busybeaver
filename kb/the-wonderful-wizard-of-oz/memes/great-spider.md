## Overview

The **Great Spider** is a monstrous predator that terrorizes the animals of the great forest in the southern lands beyond the [[china-country|Dainty China Country]]. Described as a spider-like creature of enormous proportions, its killing by the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]] represents the Lion's culminating act of courage—the moment that earns him the title [[king-of-beasts|King of Beasts]] from the grateful forest animals.

## Description

The tiger who first tells the Lion of the threat describes the creature in terrifying detail:

> "It is a most tremendous monster, like a great spider, with a body as big as an elephant and legs as long as a tree trunk. It has eight of these long legs, and as the monster crawls through the forest he seizes an animal with a leg and drags it to his mouth, where he eats it as a spider does a fly." [021-013]

When the Lion finds the spider, it is asleep:

> Its legs were quite as long as the tiger had said, and its body covered with coarse black hair. It had a great mouth, with a row of sharp teeth a foot long; but its head was joined to the pudgy body by a neck as slender as a wasp's waist. [021-023]

## The Lion's Victory

The Lion, noting the spider's slender neck, recognizes it as a structural weakness. Choosing to strike while the creature sleeps—"as he knew it was easier to fight it asleep than awake"—the Lion springs onto the monster's back:

> Then, with one blow of his heavy paw, all armed with sharp claws, he knocked the spider's head from its body. Jumping down, he watched it until the long legs stopped wiggling, when he knew it was quite dead. [021-023]

The kill is swift and decisive, requiring only a single blow. The Lion then returns to the assembled animals and announces the spider's death, prompting them to immediately proclaim him their king [021-024].

## Significance

The Great Spider is the final external threat overcome by any of Dorothy's companions, and it serves as the Lion's definitive proof of courage. Throughout the story, the Lion has demonstrated bravery in action while denying it in self-conception. The spider, the most physically formidable monster in the book, is defeated by the Lion's combination of tactical intelligence (identifying the weak neck) and decisive action (striking while the spider sleeps). The forest animals' acclamation—"We will gladly serve you and do your bidding" [021-025]—mirrors the Winkies' gratitude to the Tin Woodman and the Emerald City citizens' acceptance of the Scarecrow as ruler, completing the arc in which each of Dorothy's companions finds a kingdom.
