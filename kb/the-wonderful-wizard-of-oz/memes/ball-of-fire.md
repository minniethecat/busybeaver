## Overview

The **Ball of Fire** is the third and most terrifying illusory form assumed by the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard of Oz]], reserved for the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion's]] audience. A blazing sphere of fire burning upon the throne, it is designed to test the courage of the one visitor who most fears his own fearfulness. The Lion's terrified response—fleeing from the Throne Room and diving headfirst through a window—is at once comic and revealing, complicating the Lion's claim that he lacks courage.

## The Lion's Audience

When the Lion enters the Throne Room for his audience, he is met with an overwhelming spectacle:

> To his great surprise, he saw no Head or Lady or Beast, but a great Ball of Fire, so fierce and glowing he could scarcely bear to gaze upon it. And from the Ball of Fire came a low, quiet voice, saying: "I am Oz, the Great and Terrible. Who are you, and why do you seek me?" [011-089]

The Lion replies that he is a coward who seeks courage. But when the Ball of Fire demands that he first help kill the Wicked Witch of the West, the Lion reacts with uncontrolled terror:

> The Lion was so frightened that he started to spring out of the window, forgetting the glass; and the glass shattered as he went through, and the Wizard was left with an empty pane. But the Lion ran down the corridor and out of the palace, and Dorothy and her friends found him outside, lying on his back with his four legs in the air, panting and trembling with fear. [011-090]

## Significance

The Ball of Fire is the Wizard's most elemental form—raw, undifferentiated power. Unlike the Head (intellect) or the Lady (beauty/nurture), the Fire represents primal terror, and it is fittingly deployed against the Lion, whose defining characteristic is fear. The Lion's reaction—smashing through a window to escape—is simultaneously the most cowardly and the most physically impressive response any of the companions give to the Wizard. He breaks through plate glass, an act requiring tremendous strength and the willingness to risk injury. The Lion's "cowardice" is, paradoxically, also a kind of courage.

The Ball of Fire is later revealed to be just another of the Wizard's theatrical illusions, like the [[great-head|Great Head]] and the [[beast-form|Beast]].
