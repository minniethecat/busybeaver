## Overview

The Wizard Is Exposed is the climactic revelation in Chapter XV when [[toto|Toto]] accidentally knocks over the screen concealing the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard of Oz]], exposing him as an ordinary old man operating mechanical contrivances rather than the terrifying magical being he had pretended to be. This event transforms the entire dynamic between the Wizard and the companions and is the pivotal moment that allows their wishes to be granted.

## The Revelation

After the companions had each met separately with the Wizard — who appeared to each in a different terrifying form (a [[great-head|Great Head]] to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]], a [[the-beast-form|Beast]] to the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]], and a [[ball-of-fire|Ball of Fire]] to the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]) — they returned together to the [[throne-room|Throne Room]] to demand that he fulfill his promises.

As the Wizard hesitated and made excuses, [[toto|Toto]] "jumped away from [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and, barking joyfully, ran behind the screen" [015-020]. Before anyone could stop him, Toto tipped over the screen, revealing "a little old man, with a bald head and a wrinkled face" [015-020] who was "working a bellows and pulling levers" to create his magical illusions.

## The Wizard's Confession

The companions were astonished and angry. The [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]] raised his axe and demanded: "Who are you?" The little old man answered in a trembling voice: "I am Oz, the Great and Terrible... but don't strike me — please don't — and I will do anything you wish" [015-021].

The Wizard then confessed: "I have been making believe. I am just a common man" [015-022]. He explained that he was a ventriloquist and balloonist from [[kansas|Omaha]] who had been carried to the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]] by a storm, and that the people, seeing him descend from the sky, "thought I was a great Wizard" [015-022]. He had maintained the deception ever since, ruling the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] through trickery and fear.

## Aftermath

Rather than punishing the exposed humbug, the companions demanded he fulfill his promises. The Wizard, relieved not to be killed, agreed to grant [[brain|brains]] to the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]], a [[heart]] to the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]], and [[courage|courage]] to the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion]]. He also promised to help [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] return to [[kansas|Kansas]].

## Significance

The exposure of the Wizard is the story's central statement on the theme of appearance versus reality. The terrifying, all-powerful Wizard was merely "a common man" who had built an elaborate deception. Yet paradoxically, the Wizard — despite being a fraud — actually delivers on his promises, suggesting that the companions' gifts were never truly external but were always within themselves.
