# Tin Woodman

## Overview

The Tin Woodman is one of Dorothy's three companions on the Yellow Brick Road. Once a flesh-and-blood woodman who loved a Munchkin maiden, he was cursed by the Wicked Witch of the East: his enchanted axe cut off his limbs one by one, each replaced with tin by the tinsmith Ku-Klip — until nothing human remained (005-045–005-048). His deepest loss is his heart: "I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl" (005-047). His quest for a heart from the Wizard is the most poignant of the four — because the Tin Woodman, more than anyone, already possesses the qualities a heart represents.

## Description

The Tin Woodman is a humanoid figure made entirely of tin. His body is perfectly jointed, oiled at every connection, and he carries an axe and an oil-can with him always — the latter being essential, since his joints rust if exposed to water (005-048). The one mark on his otherwise immaculate torso is a square patch soldered over his left breast, where the Wizard cut a hole to insert his silk-and-sawdust heart (016-023).

His character is defined by a paradox: he mourns his lost capacity for love, yet he is the most tender-hearted member of the group. He weeps when he accidentally steps on a beetle, rusting his jaws shut (006-008). He refuses to harm any living creature. He is gentle, protective, and unfailingly kind — all the qualities he believes he lost with his human heart.

## Role in the Story

### The Rusted Woodman

Dorothy and the Scarecrow find the Tin Woodman standing motionless in the forest, his axe raised mid-swing, his joints rusted solid from a year of exposure (005-012). He begs them to oil him — and as each joint is freed, he tells his story. He was born a human woodman who fell in love with a beautiful Munchkin maiden and promised to marry her (005-045). The Wicked Witch of the East, determined to prevent the marriage, enchanted his axe. Each swing cut off a body part; each part was replaced with tin by a tinsmith. His body became entirely mechanical, and his heart — the organ of love — was gone (005-047).

### The Quest for a Heart

"Once I had brains, and a heart also; so, having tried them both, I should much rather have a heart," the Woodman tells the Scarecrow (005-040). When the Scarecrow argues for brains, the Woodman counters: "I shall take the heart, for brains do not make one happy, and happiness is the best thing in the world" (005-051). This argument — that emotional fulfillment surpasses intellectual achievement — is the Woodman's defining philosophy.

### The Tender Heart

Despite his protestations of heartlessness, the Woodman is the group's moral compass. He accidentally kills a beetle and weeps so much that his jaws rust shut — the Scarecrow must oil them before he can speak (006-008). He pleads with the Lion not to kill a deer for food (007-005). He threatens to harm no creature unnecessarily. His "heartlessness" is, transparently, the story's most generous heart.

### Receiving the Heart

The Wizard cuts a square hole in the Woodman's chest and inserts "a pretty heart, made entirely of silk and stuffed with sawdust" (016-023). The Woodman's first question is not about the heart's magic but its morality: "But is it a kind heart?" (016-025). Oz assures him it is, and the Woodman beams: "I am very grateful to you, and shall never forget your kindness" (016-028).

### Ruler of the Winkies

After the Wicked Witch of the West is destroyed, the Winkies ask the Tin Woodman to be their ruler (013-029). He accepts and becomes a beloved leader — his kindness and fairness making him the ideal sovereign.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Gale

The Tin Woodman is Dorothy's most devoted protector. He weeps at the thought of her danger and vows to see her safely to Kansas.

### The Scarecrow

The Woodman's philosophical opposite and closest friend. Their debate over heart versus brains (005-050–005-052) is central to the story's intellectual tension.

### The Cowardly Lion

The Woodman treats the Lion with gentle sympathy — understanding, perhaps, the pain of feeling incomplete.

### The Munchkin Maiden

The woman the Woodman loved before his transformation. She is "still waiting for me to marry her" (005-048), though the Woodman — having lost his heart — no longer cares. This is the story's most tragic thread.

### The Wicked Witch of the East

The witch who cursed the Woodman's axe, causing his transformation. Her cruelty is the source of his suffering — and of his quest.

## Notes

The Tin Woodman is the most emotionally complex of Baum's characters. His tragedy — the loss of his human body and heart — is the darkest backstory in the book, and his response to it — gentle kindness, unfailing protectiveness — is the most redemptive. The Woodman is a walking paradox: the character who claims to have no heart acts with the most heart. This paradox is the story's deepest insight into human nature: we often believe we lack the very qualities we most clearly possess. The silk-and-sawdust heart is a placebo — but the Woodman's kindness was real all along.
