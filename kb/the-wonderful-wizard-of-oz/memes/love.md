# Love

## Overview

Love is the emotion at the core of the Tin Woodman's origin story. Before his enchantment, he was a flesh-and-blood woodman who "grew to love her with all my heart" (005-045) — a beautiful Munchkin maiden who promised to marry him. When the Wicked Witch of the East caused his axe to cut off his limbs one by one, each replaced with tin, the greatest loss was not his body but his heart: "I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl, and did not care to marry her" (005-047). Love, in Baum's story, is what makes a human being human — and its loss is the Tin Woodman's deepest wound.

## Properties

Love in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is characterized by:

- **The source of happiness**: The Tin Woodman declares that "happiness is the best thing in the world" (005-051) and associates it directly with the heart and love
- **Irreplaceable by intellect**: In the heart-vs-brain debate, love (heart) is argued to be superior to intelligence (brain)
- **Motivation for action**: The Woodman's love for the maiden drove him to work harder than ever to earn money for their home (005-047)
- **Vulnerability**: Love made the Woodman vulnerable to the Witch's cruelty; losing love left him hollow

## Role in the Story

### The Tin Woodman's Lost Love

The Tin Woodman tells his story to Dorothy and the Scarecrow in the forest. He was once a young woodman who fell in love with "one of the Munchkin girls who was so beautiful that I soon grew to love her with all my heart" (005-045). She promised to marry him, and he worked tirelessly to earn money for their future home.

The Wicked Witch of the East, determined to prevent their marriage, enchanted his axe. Each swing cut off a piece of his body until he was entirely made of tin. The worst consequence was emotional: "I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl, and did not care to marry her" (005-047).

The old woman he worked for — in league with the Witch — refused to pay him, and though the Munchkin girl "is still waiting for me to marry her" (005-048), the Woodman had lost the capacity to care. His quest for a heart is, at root, a quest to recover the ability to love.

### Love and the Heart

When the Wizard gives the Tin Woodman a silk-and-sawdust heart, the Woodman's first question reveals his values: "But is it a kind heart?" (016-025). He doesn't ask if it's strong, or brave, or wise — but kind. Love, for the Woodman, is inseparable from kindness.

### Dorothy's Love for Home

Love manifests differently in Dorothy: not as romantic love but as filial devotion to Aunt Em and Uncle Henry. Her love for Kansas — gray, grim, and unlovely — is the story's emotional anchor. She wants to go home because she loves the people there, not because the place is beautiful.

## Notes

Love in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is less a theme than a premise — the thing lost, the thing sought, the thing found. Baum treats it with simple sincerity rather than irony: the Tin Woodman's love story is genuinely tragic, and his loss of the capacity to love is treated as an existential catastrophe. The restoration of love through a silk-and-sawdust heart is either the story's most cynical joke or its most profound truth — that love is real because we believe it is. The Tin Woodman, who wept for a beetle he accidentally crushed (006-008), never lacked the capacity for love; he only lacked the belief that he possessed it.
