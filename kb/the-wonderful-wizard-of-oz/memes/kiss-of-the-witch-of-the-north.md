# Kiss of the Witch of the North

## Overview

The Kiss of the Witch of the North is a magical protective charm placed upon Dorothy's forehead before she begins her journey to the Emerald City. The Witch of the North bestows the kiss after giving Dorothy the Silver Shoes, explaining that "no one will dare injure a person who has been kissed by the Witch of the North" (002-036). This charm proves to be one of the most powerful protections in the story, respected even by the Wicked Witch of the West and the Winged Monkeys.

## Properties

The protective kiss possesses several distinctive properties:

- **Visible mark**: The kiss leaves a round, shining mark on Dorothy's forehead where the Witch's lips touched (002-036).
- **Universal protection**: No creature in the Land of Oz—neither witch, nor beast, nor Winged Monkey—dares to harm the bearer of the kiss.
- **Enduring charm**: The protection lasts throughout Dorothy's entire journey, from Munchkin Country all the way back to the Emerald City.
- **Passive power**: Dorothy does not need to activate or invoke the charm; it protects her without any action on her part.

## Role in the Story

The Witch of the North bestows the kiss immediately after giving Dorothy the Silver Shoes. Having already provided Dorothy with magical footwear, the Witch adds this additional protection out of genuine care for the child's safety. She tells Dorothy that the road to the Emerald City is paved with yellow brick so she cannot miss it, and that she should not be afraid (002-036).

The protective power of the kiss becomes evident at several critical moments. When the Winged Monkeys capture Dorothy on the orders of the Wicked Witch of the West, their King explains: "We dare not harm this little girl, for the Power of Good protects her, and greater than the Power of Evil" (012-041). The mark on Dorothy's forehead is visible to the Winged Monkeys, who carry her gently despite their mission to capture her.

The Wicked Witch of the West herself is "both surprised and worried" when she sees the mark on Dorothy's forehead, knowing well that "neither the Winged Monkeys nor she, herself, dare hurt the girl in any way" (012-059). The Witch is forced to resort to trickery—using the invisible iron bar to trip Dorothy and steal one of the Silver Shoes—rather than direct violence.

The protective kiss represents one of the few explicitly "good" magics in the novel, contrasting sharply with the coercive magic of the Golden Cap and the destructive spells of the Wicked Witches. It is a gift freely given, with no conditions, commands, or expiration date.

## Notes

The Witch's kiss is thematically significant as a representation of benevolent protection in a story filled with dangerous magic. Unlike the transactional nature of the Golden Cap or the hidden power of the Silver Shoes, the kiss is an unconditional gift. Literary scholars have noted that this protective charm, bestowed by a motherly figure (the Witch of the North is described as a kind old woman), positions Dorothy as a child under divine or fairy guardianship—a common motif in fairy tales and folk literature.
