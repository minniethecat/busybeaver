# Kansas

## Overview

[[kansas|Kansas]] is the frame setting of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* — the "real world" from which [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] is swept by the [[kansas-cyclone|cyclone]] and to which she spends the entire story trying to return. It is depicted as a vast, flat prairie dominated by a single color: gray. The landscape, the house, and even the emotional lives of its inhabitants are all described in terms of grayness and absence. Kansas represents the ordinary, the familiar, and the place of belonging that Dorothy must leave in order to recognize its value — a theme crystallized in her most famous declaration: "No matter how dreary and gray our homes are, we people of flesh and blood would rather live there than in any other country, be it ever so beautiful" (004-009).

## Geography

Baum's Kansas is defined by its stark minimalism. The Gale farm sits on "the great Kansas prairies" (001-001), a landscape so flat and featureless that "the sun had burned the plowed land into a gray mass, with little cracks running through it" (001-003). The house itself is small and wooden, with only one room (001-002). Beneath the house is a "cyclone cellar" — a small hole dug into the ground, reachable by a trapdoor, where the family takes shelter during storms (001-006). The surrounding land has "no trees" and "no houses" nearby (001-005); the isolation is absolute.

> "When Dorothy stood in the doorway and looked around, she could see nothing but the great gray prairie on every side. Not a tree nor a house broke the broad sweep of flat country that reached to the edge of the sky in all directions." (001-002)

The grayness is the defining feature: "Even the grass was not green, for the sun had burned the tops of the long blades until they were the same gray color to be seen everywhere" (001-003). This monochromatic desolation contrasts sharply with the polychromatic abundance of [[land-of-oz|Oz]], establishing the central visual opposition of the novel.

| Element | Kansas | Land of Oz |
|---------|--------|------------|
| **Dominant color** | Gray | Polychromatic (blue/purple/red/yellow/green by quadrant) |
| **Landscape** | Flat, treeless prairie | Forests, rivers, mountains, poppy fields |
| **Inhabitants** | Ordinary humans | Witches, constructs, talking animals |
| **Weather** | Cyclones and drought | Magical storms, controlled by witches |
| **Architecture** | One-room wooden farmhouse | Grand palaces, marble cities |
| **Emotional tone** | Solemnity, grayness of spirit | Wonder, danger, strangeness |

## Inhabitants

The Kansas sections feature only five characters. [[aunt-em|Aunt Em]] and [[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]] are [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy's]] guardians — a childless couple worn down by years of hard prairie life. [[aunt-em|Aunt Em]], "a thin, gaunt woman who never smiled," had been "startled" by marriage and child-rearing into permanent solemnity (001-004). [[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]] "never laughed" and "worked hard from morning till night" (001-004). [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] lives with them as an orphan, her only companion being [[toto|Toto]], a small black dog "who made Dorothy laugh, and saved her from growing as gray as her other surroundings" (001-004). Three farmhands — Hunk, Hickory, and Zeke — appear only briefly before the [[kansas-cyclone|cyclone]] strikes.

## Role in the Story

Kansas serves as the narrative frame. Chapter I opens on the Gale farm, establishing the setting, characters, and the [[kansas-cyclone|cyclone]] that transports [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] to [[land-of-oz|Oz]]. Dorothy's desire to return to Kansas is the primary motivation driving the entire quest: she repeatedly tells everyone she meets that she "want[s] to get back to Kansas" (002-030). The [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard's]] bargain — destroy the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]] in exchange for passage home — ties every subsequent adventure to this originating desire.

In the novel's final chapter, Dorothy returns to Kansas via the [[silver-shoes]], landing on the prairie to find the new farmhouse [[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]] has built. [[aunt-em|Aunt Em]] greets her with the simple words: "My darling child!" (024-033). The return is understated and domestic, affirming that Kansas is defined not by its grayness but by the love Dorothy finds there.

## Notable Features

- **The Cyclone Cellar**: A trapdoor-covered hole where the family shelters from storms. Dorothy cannot reach it in time because [[toto|Toto]] hides under the bed (001-007).
- **The One-Room House**: So small that it holds only a stove, a table, a bed, and Dorothy's cot. The [[kansas-cyclone|cyclone]] lifts it whole (001-008).
- **The Prairie**: Endless, flat, treeless grasslands stretching "in all directions" (001-002).

## Notes

Baum's Kansas draws on his own experience of the Dakota prairie during the drought years of the late 1880s. The novel was published in 1900, only a few years after Baum's time in Aberdeen, South Dakota. The grayness of the Kansas scenes reflects the psychological toll of prairie farming life — an environment that, in Baum's rendering, threatens to drain the color from both the land and its people. The contrast with [[land-of-oz|Oz's]] vibrant colors is one of the novel's most enduring aesthetic achievements. The frame structure — from gray Kansas to colorful Oz and back — suggests that adventure transforms perception: having seen wonder, one returns to the ordinary world with new eyes.
