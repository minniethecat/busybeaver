# The Cyclone

## Overview

The cyclone is the inciting event of the entire narrative—the natural force that transports Dorothy Gale and her house from the gray Kansas prairie to the vibrant Land of Oz. It serves as both a literal weather phenomenon and a narrative device that bridges the ordinary world and the magical realm. The cyclone is described in vivid detail, with Dorothy's house carried aloft "as easily as you could carry a feather" (001-003).

## Properties

As depicted in the story, the cyclone possesses notable characteristics:

- **Extraordinary power**: It lifts Dorothy's entire farmhouse into the air and carries it for miles across the prairie without destroying it.
- **Gentle transport**: Despite the violence outside, inside the house Dorothy experiences a relatively calm ride, with the house "whirling around two or three times" before beginning its journey (001-003).
- **Directional specificity**: The cyclone carries Dorothy from Kansas to the Land of Oz, landing the house precisely on top of the Wicked Witch of the East.
- **Cyclone cellar preparation**: Kansas farmhouses are equipped with cyclone cellars—holes dug in the ground with a trap door—because cyclones are common on the prairie (001-001).

## Role in the Story

The cyclone arrives suddenly one afternoon while Dorothy is playing with Toto. Uncle Henry spots the approaching funnel and calls for Aunt Em, who immediately orders Dorothy to the cyclone cellar. However, Dorothy hesitates to retrieve Toto, and before she can reach the trap door, "a strange thing happened"—the house was caught up by the cyclone (001-003).

Dorothy remains inside the house as it is carried through the air. Despite the terrifying situation, she eventually falls asleep on her bed, rocked by the motion of the whirling house (001-003). The journey lasts for hours—Dorothy wakes to find herself still in flight, with the house settling gradually toward the ground.

The cyclone deposits Dorothy's house in the Land of Oz, landing it directly on the Wicked Witch of the East and killing her instantly (002-008). This event sets the entire plot in motion: the Munchkins are freed from bondage, Dorothy acquires the Silver Shoes, and her quest to return to Kansas begins.

The cyclone is also discussed later in the story, when the Scarecrow wonders if Dorothy might find another cyclone to carry her back to Kansas. The idea never materializes, as the story instead finds resolution through the Silver Shoes and the revelation of their power (023-025).

## Notes

The cyclone as a narrative device was particularly resonant for Baum's American audience, as tornadoes were (and remain) a familiar danger on the Great Plains. The image of a house being transported by a tornado entered popular culture so deeply that it became one of the most iconic moments in children's literature, later immortalized in the 1939 film adaptation. Critics have noted that the cyclone represents both the capriciousness of nature and the transformative power of displacement—Dorothy is not merely moved geographically but transported into a world of moral clarity, where good and wicked witches are clearly identifiable and problems have definite solutions.
