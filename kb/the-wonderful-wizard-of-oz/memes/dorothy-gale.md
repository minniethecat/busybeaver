# Dorothy Gale

## Overview

Dorothy Gale is the protagonist of *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. An orphan girl living with her [[aunt-em|Aunt Em]] and [[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]] on the gray [[kansas]] prairies, she is swept to the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]] by the [[kansas-cyclone|Kansas Cyclone]] (001-008). Resourceful, kind-hearted, and possessed of unwavering determination to return home, Dorothy gathers companions — the [[scarecrow]], the [[tin-woodman]], and the [[cowardly-lion]] — each seeking something from the [[wizard-of-oz]]. Her journey on the [[road-of-yellow-brick]] and her encounters with the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witches]] frame one of the most enduring children's stories in American literature.

## Description

Dorothy is a young girl — young enough to be a child, old enough to undertake a perilous journey alone. She is described as an orphan who came to live with [[aunt-em|Aunt Em]] and [[uncle-henry|Uncle Henry]] in [[kansas]] (001-004). She wears a simple gingham dress and, upon arriving in Oz, acquires the [[silver-shoes]] of the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]] (002-012). The [[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]]'s kiss leaves "a round, shining mark" on her forehead that protects her throughout her journey (002-054).

Dorothy's defining trait is her combination of innocence and practicality. She is bewildered by the magical world she enters but never paralyzed — she asks direct questions, makes practical decisions, and treats every strange creature she meets with the same matter-of-fact kindness she would offer a Kansas neighbor. Her simplicity is her strength: when confronted with the terrifying [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard's]] demands, she simply weeps and then does what needs to be done (011-051).

## Role in the Story

### Arrival in Oz

The [[kansas-cyclone|cyclone]] carries Dorothy's house — with Dorothy and [[toto]] inside — from [[kansas]] to the [[land-of-oz|Land of Oz]], where it lands directly on the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]], killing her (002-008). The [[council-of-munchkins|Munchkins]] greet Dorothy as a liberator, and the [[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]] gives her the dead Witch's [[silver-shoes]] (002-012). From her very first moments in Oz, Dorothy has only one thought:

> "I want to get back to Kansas." (002-040)

### The Yellow Brick Road

Dorothy's journey to the [[emerald-city]] is a procession of rescues. She frees the [[scarecrow]] from his pole (003-036). She oils the rusted [[tin-woodman]] back to life (005-013). She faces down the [[cowardly-lion]] with a slap on the nose (006-024). Each companion joins her not because she recruits them but because they see in her quest a chance to pursue their own. Her leadership is accidental but effective — she leads by caring.

### The Wizard's Demands

When [[wizard-of-oz|Oz]] makes his demands, Dorothy is devastated:

> "There is no hope for me, for Oz will not send me home until I have killed the Wicked Witch of the West." (011-051)

Yet she does not turn back. She travels west, is captured by the [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch]] (012-009), endures captivity and forced labor, and finally destroys the Witch by accident — throwing a bucket of water that melts her (012-083). The [[melting-of-the-wicked-witch-of-the-west|melting of the Witch]] is one of the story's most dramatic moments, accomplished by a child who never set out to destroy anyone.

### The Return

After the [[wizards-departure|Wizard's departure]], Dorothy must seek help from [[glinda]] in the South. It is [[glinda]] who reveals the truth:

> "Your Silver Shoes will carry you over the desert. If you had known their power you could have gone back to your Aunt Em the very first day you came to this country." (023-025)

Dorothy knocks her heels together three times, speaks the command, and returns to [[kansas]] in three steps (023-037). The [[silver-shoes]] fall off in the desert, lost forever (023-041).

## Relationships

### Toto

[[toto|Toto]] is Dorothy's constant companion — the little black dog whose presence made [[kansas]] bearable (001-006) and whose courage emboldens Dorothy throughout Oz. It is [[toto]] who jumps from Dorothy's arms during the cyclone, causing her to remain in the house that is carried away (001-013). At the [[palace-of-oz|Wizard's palace]], it is [[toto]] who pulls aside the screen and exposes [[wizard-of-oz|Oz]] as a fraud (015-044).

### The Scarecrow

Dorothy's first companion. She frees him from the pole where he has been mounted (003-036) and welcomes him on the journey. The [[scarecrow]] becomes the group's problem-solver, and Dorothy treats his quest for [[brain|brains]] with earnest respect.

### The Tin Woodman

Dorothy oils the [[tin-woodman]]'s joints and restores him to movement (005-013). He becomes her most devoted protector — weeping at the thought of her danger and vowing to see her safely home.

### The Cowardly Lion

Dorothy slaps the [[cowardly-lion|Lion's]] nose when he threatens [[toto]] (006-024), an act of startling courage that establishes her authority. The Lion becomes her fierce guardian despite his fear.

### Aunt Em and Uncle Henry

The aunt and uncle who raised Dorothy after she was orphaned (001-004). Though the [[kansas]] chapters describe them as gray and unsmiling, Dorothy's love for them never wavers. Her entire journey is a quest to return to them.

### The Wicked Witch of the West

The [[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Witch]] covets Dorothy's [[silver-shoes]] and is terrified of the protective kiss on her forehead (012-059). She enslaves Dorothy but is ultimately destroyed by her, when a thrown bucket of water melts the Witch entirely in the [[melting-of-the-wicked-witch-of-the-west|most unexpected climax]] (012-083).

### Glinda the Good Witch

[[glinda]] appears only at the story's end but provides its resolution: she reveals the [[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes']] power and enables Dorothy's return to [[kansas]] (023-027).

## Quotes

## Notes

Dorothy Gale has become one of the most iconic characters in American children's literature. L. Frank Baum intended her as an ordinary American child — practical, brave, and above all, desiring to go home. Unlike fairy-tale princesses, Dorothy has no royal blood, no magical powers, and no grand destiny. Her heroism is accidental and democratic: she kills one witch by landing on her and another by splashing water on her, neither act intentional. This ordinariness is the point — Baum suggests that courage, kindness, and determination are available to anyone, even a little girl from [[kansas]].
