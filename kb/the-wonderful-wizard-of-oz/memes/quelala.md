## Overview

Quelala is the chosen husband of the sorceress-princess Gayelette, appearing in the Winged Monkey King's flashback narrative in Chapter XIV. A boy of extraordinary promise who was raised by Gayelette to be the "best and wisest man in all the land," Quelala became the first owner of the Golden Cap and the first to command the Winged Monkeys. His kindness in pleading for the Monkeys after their prank against him directly shaped the terms of the magical servitude that later affects Dorothy.

## Description

As a boy, Quelala was discovered by Gayelette and recognized as "handsome and manly and wise beyond his years" [014-035]. Under Gayelette's magical tutelage in her ruby palace, he grew into a man of exceptional qualities: "the best and wisest man in all the land," with a "manly beauty" so great that Gayelette loved him dearly [014-035]. He is also noted for his good nature — when the Winged Monkeys dropped him into a river as a prank, he "was much too wise not to swim" and bore the indignity with laughter, "not in the least spoiled by all his good fortune" [014-037].

## Role in the Story

Quelala's story is told by the King of the Winged Monkeys in Chapter XIV. His significance lies in his role in the origin of the Golden Cap's magic.

When Quelala reached manhood, Gayelette hastened to prepare their wedding [014-035]. Shortly before the ceremony, the old King of the Winged Monkeys (grandfather of the current King), who loved jokes "better than a good dinner," spotted Quelala walking beside the river dressed in "a rich costume of pink silk and purple velvet" [014-036]. On a whim, the old King ordered his band to seize Quelala, carry him over the river, and drop him into the water [014-036].

Quelala swam to shore and laughed at the incident, though his fine clothes were ruined [014-037]. When the furious Gayelette ordered the Winged Monkeys' wings tied so they would drown in the river as punishment, Quelala "said a kind word for them" [014-038]. His intercession, combined with the old King's pleading, moved Gayelette to commute the sentence to magical servitude: the Winged Monkeys would obey three commands from whoever held the Golden Cap.

As the first owner of the Golden Cap, Quelala was the first to command the Winged Monkeys. His first wish was that the Monkeys, whose appearance his bride could not bear, should always keep away from her sight [014-040].

## Relationships

- **[[gayelette]]** — Gayelette chose Quelala as a boy, raised him in her ruby palace, and shaped him with magic into her ideal husband. His love for her was reciprocated with her fierce protectiveness [014-035].
- **[[the-king-of-the-winged-monkeys]]** — Quelala's forgiving response to the Monkeys' prank — saying a kind word on their behalf — saved them from execution and led to the service compact [014-038].
- **[[wicked-witch-of-the-west]]** — Quelala's Golden Cap, inherited through unknown channels, eventually fell into the hands of the Wicked Witch of the West, who used it to command the Winged Monkeys against Dorothy's party [014-039].

## Notes

Quelala exists entirely within the frame of the Winged Monkey King's embedded narrative — a story within a story. He never appears in the main timeline of the novel, yet his legacy drives the plot through the Golden Cap. His first command to the Winged Monkeys — to stay away from Gayelette — is a quiet but telling gesture: even the "best and wisest man in all the land" could not bear to have his bride discomforted by the sight of the creatures he had spared. The Golden Cap, a wedding gift said to have cost half a kingdom, represents both Gayelette's love and her binding of supernatural forces to human will.
