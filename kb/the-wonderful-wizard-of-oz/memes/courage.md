# Courage

## Overview

Courage is the object of the Cowardly Lion's quest in the Land of Oz. Despite being a lion — traditionally the symbol of bravery — this Lion is paralyzed by fear. "Whenever there is danger, my heart begins to beat fast," he confesses (006-027). Yet, like the Scarecrow's intelligence and the Tin Woodman's compassion, the Lion's courage is present from the start: he faces every danger alongside his companions, and his fear never prevents him from acting. The courage he receives from the Wizard is a green liquid in a bottle (016-035), but the real courage, as Oz explains, "is always inside one" (016-038).

## Properties

Courage in Baum's story is defined by its paradoxical nature — it manifests most clearly in those who feel fear but act anyway:

- **Absence of fear** (perceived definition): What the Lion thinks courage means — a state of fearlessness
- **Action despite fear** (actual definition): What the Lion demonstrates throughout — pressing forward even while terrified
- **Moral courage**: The willingness to protect others — the Lion threatens to kill any enemy that threatens Dorothy (009-014)
- **Placebo effect**: The green liquid Oz gives the Lion is explicitly described as courage only "if it were inside of you" (016-038) — it works because the Lion believes it will

## Role in the Story

### The Cowardly Lion

The Lion bursts into the story by charging at the travelers — then immediately proves his cowardice by striking the Tin Woodman without effect and trying to bite the Scarecrow, who laughs at him (006-018). When Dorothy slaps his nose for attacking Toto, he dissolves into tears: "I know I am a coward" (006-025). He explains: "It is my great sorrow, and makes my life very unhappy. But whenever there is danger, my heart begins to beat fast" (006-027).

The Tin Woodman diagnoses him wryly: "Perhaps you have heart disease" (006-028). When the Lion protests that it's cowardice, the Woodman replies: "If you have, you ought to be glad, for it proves you have a heart. For my part, I have no heart; so I cannot have heart disease" (006-030). This exchange — the Lion afraid, the Woodman without a heart, the Scarecrow without brains — perfectly captures the story's central irony: each character possesses what they claim to lack.

### Acts of Courage

Throughout the journey, the Lion repeatedly acts bravely:
- He leaps across the great ditch carrying his companions on his back, one by one (007-026)
- He faces the Kalidahs, staying behind as the others cross the felled-tree bridge (007-034)
- He is ready to "tear the Witch to pieces" to protect Dorothy, and threatens: "Whoever tried to hurt her would have me to reckon with" (009-014)
- He fights through the poppy field's soporific fumes to try to reach the Emerald City, though the scent overcomes him (008-020)

### Receiving Courage

After the Wizard's exposure, the Lion approaches him and says, "I have come for my courage" (016-033). Oz pours liquid from "a square green bottle" into "a green-gold dish" and tells the Lion to drink (016-035). When the Lion asks what it is, Oz explains: "If it were inside of you, it would be courage. You know, of course, that courage is always inside one; so that this really cannot be called courage until you have swallowed it" (016-038).

The Lion drinks, and when Oz asks how he feels, he responds: "Full of courage!" (016-040). The green liquid is the most transparent placebo in the story — Oz essentially admits it isn't courage until the Lion believes it is.

### Aftermath

Empowered by his "courage," the Lion becomes the King of Beasts in the forest, defeating a giant spider-like monster that has been terrorizing the animals (021-030). The beasts ask him to be their king, and he agrees — fulfilling his original dream of becoming "the King of Beasts, as men call me" (006-039).

## Notes

Courage in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is the most explicitly deconstructed of the three quests. The Wizard openly admits the green liquid is not courage — it becomes courage only through the act of belief. This is Baum's most direct statement on the placebo nature of all three gifts: the silk-and-sawdust heart, the bran-and-pin brain, and the green liquid courage are all catalysts for qualities the characters already possess. The Lion's journey from self-proclaimed coward to King of Beasts is not a transformation but a revelation. His courage was always present — he simply needed the belief (and the green bottle) to recognize it.
