## Overview

The Munchkin Maiden is the young woman the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]] loved and intended to marry before the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]] enchanted his axe and caused his gradual transformation from flesh to tin. She serves as the emotional catalyst for the Tin Woodman's origin story and his enduring desire for a heart.

## Description

The Munchkin Maiden is described as "so beautiful" [005-045] that the young woodchopper (the future Tin Woodman) "grew to love her with all [his] heart" [005-045]. She is also called "the pretty Munchkin girl" [005-046] and "the beautiful Munchkin maiden" [005-047]. No physical details beyond her beauty are given in the text.

## Role in the Story

The Munchkin Maiden appears only within the Tin Woodman's recounted backstory in Chapter V, never in the present action of the narrative.

She lived with a lazy old woman who did not wish her to marry, as the old woman relied on the girl to "do the cooking and the housework" [005-045]. Despite this, the maiden promised to marry the young woodchopper "as soon as [he] could earn enough money to build a better house for her" [005-045].

The old woman, seeking to prevent the marriage, went to the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]] and "promised her two sheep and a cow if she would prevent the marriage" [005-045]. The Witch then enchanted the woodchopper's axe, leading to his gradual dismemberment and transformation into the Tin Woodman.

After losing his heart during the final transformation, the Tin Woodman states: "I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl, and did not care whether I married her or not" [005-047]. He muses: "I suppose she is still living with the old woman, waiting for me to come after her" [005-047].

At the end of his story, the Tin Woodman resolves to ask the [[wizard-of-oz|Wizard of Oz]] for a heart, declaring: "If he does, I will go back to the Munchkin maiden and marry her" [005-048].

## Relationships

- **[[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]]** — Her betrothed. She promised to marry him before his transformation. The Tin Woodman's quest for a heart is motivated by his desire to love her again.
- **The Old Woman (guardian)** — The lazy old woman she lived with, who opposed the marriage and enlisted the Wicked Witch of the East to prevent it.
- **[[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]]** — Responsible for enchanting the woodchopper's axe, ultimately separating the lovers.

## Notes

The Munchkin Maiden is never named in the text. In later Oz books by L. Frank Baum, the Tin Woodman's lost love is named Nimmie Amee, but this name does not appear in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*. The [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]] eventually finds and marries her in *The Tin Woodman of Oz* (1918), the twelfth book in the Oz series.
