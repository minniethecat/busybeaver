# Wicked Witch of the East

## Overview

The Wicked Witch of the East is the novel's first antagonist — yet she is dead before the story begins. Her death, caused when Dorothy's cyclone-borne house lands on her, frees the Munchkins from years of bondage and sets all subsequent events in motion (002-008). Though she never appears alive, her legacy permeates the narrative: the **[[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]]** taken from her feet become Dorothy's most powerful magical asset, and her kinship with the **[[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]]** creates the central conflict of the book's second half. She is a character defined entirely by her absence, her past crimes, and her powerful enchanted possessions.

## Description

The Witch of the East is known only through her remains and the testimony of others. When Dorothy's house lands, "two feet were sticking out, shod in silver shoes with pointed toes" from beneath the corner of the great beam (002-012). The Witch of the North explains that "she was so old that she dried up quickly in the sun" (002-035), leaving only the silver shoes behind. Before her death, she "held all the Munchkins in bondage for many years, making them slave for her night and day" (002-016). No description of her living appearance exists in the text beyond the silver shoes and the Munchkins' testimony about her cruelty.

## Role in the Story

The Witch of the East serves as the narrative's inciting incident in absentia. Her death is the first event reported to Dorothy upon arrival in Oz (002-008), and it immediately establishes Dorothy's unintended identity as a "sorceress" in the eyes of the Munchkins. The **[[silver-shoes|Silver Shoes]]** that Dorothy inherits from her corpse carry "some charm" (002-036) whose nature remains mysterious until much later in the story.

The Witch's relationship with her western counterpart drives the plot's second act. When the **[[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]]** sees Dorothy wearing the Silver Shoes, she becomes determined to possess them, recognizing them as rightfully hers by kinship: "I must have those shoes, for they belonged to my sister, the Wicked Witch of the East" (012-014). This claim triggers the Witch of the West's repeated attacks on Dorothy and her companions, culminating in the confrontation at the Witch's castle.

Even in death, the Witch of the East shapes the geography of power in Oz. Her destruction breaks the balance between the four witches — two good, two wicked — and her sister's subsequent death at Dorothy's hands leaves only the **[[glinda|Good Witch of the South]]** and the **[[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]]** as the remaining magical authorities.

## Relationships

The Witch of the East's most significant relationship is with her sister, the **[[wicked-witch-of-the-west|Wicked Witch of the West]]**, whose desire to claim the Silver Shoes as an inheritance drives the central conflict of Chapters XII–XV. Her relationship with the **[[munchkins|Munchkins]]** was purely exploitative: "she has held all the Munchkins in bondage for many years" (002-016). She has no direct relationship with **[[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]]**, yet Dorothy's accidental killing of her defines Dorothy's reputation throughout Oz. The **[[witch-of-the-north|Witch of the North]]** speaks of her as a known adversary, explaining her history to Dorothy with grim satisfaction at her death (002-035).

## Notes

The Wicked Witch of the East is a striking example of a character who never appears in person yet exerts enormous influence over the narrative. Her death, her shoes, and her sister together constitute the engine of the plot. In L. Frank Baum's original text, the Witch of the East's silver shoes were changed to ruby slippers for the 1939 MGM film adaptation. The silver shoes in the book are connected to the **[[golden-cap|Golden Cap]]** and the **[[winged-monkeys|Winged Monkeys]]** through the broader magical economy of Oz — a network of enchanted objects whose powers and ownership rules are gradually revealed.
