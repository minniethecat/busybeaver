## Overview

The **enchantment of the Tin Woodman** is the tragic origin story of the [[tin-woodman|Tin Woodman]], recounted to [[dorothy-gale|Dorothy]] and the [[scarecrow|Scarecrow]] in Chapter V. Once a flesh-and-blood Munchkin woodchopper in love with a beautiful maiden, he was cursed by the [[wicked-witch-of-the-east|Wicked Witch of the East]] at the behest of a lazy old woman. The Witch enchanted his axe to sever his limbs one by one, and each lost part was replaced with tin by a skilled tinsmith—until nothing remained of the original man but his tin body, and his heart was gone.

## The Woodchopper's Life

The Tin Woodman was born the son of a woodchopper and followed his father's trade. After his parents died, he resolved to marry rather than live alone:

> "There was one of the Munchkin girls who was so beautiful that I soon grew to love her with all my heart. She, on her part, promised to marry me as soon as I could earn enough money to build a better house for her." [005-045]

## The Curse

The maiden lived with a lazy old woman who did not wish to lose her servant. The old woman approached the Wicked Witch of the East and "promised her two sheep and a cow if she would prevent the marriage" [005-045]. The Witch enchanted the woodchopper's axe, and the systematic destruction of his body began.

Each time, the woodchopper went to a tinsmith who fashioned a replacement. The Witch, angered by his resilience, escalated the curse from limbs to head to body:

> "I thought I had beaten the Wicked Witch then, and I worked harder than ever; but I little knew how cruel my enemy could be. She thought of a new way to kill my love for the beautiful Munchkin maiden, and made my axe slip again, so that it cut right through my body, splitting me into two halves." [005-047]

## The Loss of the Heart

The final price of the enchantment was the woodchopper's heart. When his body was replaced with tin, his heart—the seat of his love—was lost:

> "But, alas! I had now no heart, so that I lost all my love for the Munchkin girl, and did not care whether I married her or not. I suppose she is still living with the old woman, waiting for me to come after her." [005-047]

The Tin Woodman's body, now wholly of tin, shone brilliantly in the sun, and the enchanted axe could no longer harm him. But without a heart, he could not love—and for him, this was the greatest loss.

## The Rusting

For a time, the Tin Woodman maintained himself with an oilcan, oiling his joints to prevent rust. But one day he was caught in a rainstorm and forgot to oil himself:

> "Before I thought of the danger my joints had rusted, and I was left to stand in the woods until you came to help me. It was a terrible thing to undergo, but during the year I stood there I had time to think that the greatest loss I had known was the loss of my heart." [005-048]

He stood motionless for more than a year, groaning for help that never came—until Dorothy and the Scarecrow heard him and oiled his joints [005-015][005-023].

## Consequences

Freed from his rusted prison, the Tin Woodman joins Dorothy's quest to the [[emerald-city|Emerald City]] to ask the [[wizard-of-oz|Great Oz]] for a heart. His story establishes the second of three companion origin narratives, following the [[making-of-the-scarecrow|making of the Scarecrow]] and preceding the [[cowardly-lion|Cowardly Lion's]] confession.

When the Wizard later gives him a silk heart stuffed with sawdust, the Tin Woodman accepts it gratefully—reinforcing the book's theme that the companions already possess what they seek. In the end, he becomes ruler of the [[winkies|Winkies]], who "were very kind to me, and wanted me to rule over them" [023-018].

## Significance

The Tin Woodman's enchantment is the most violent and psychologically complex of the three companion origin stories. It literalizes the idea of losing one's humanity piece by piece—each replaced limb a step toward becoming something other than human. The Witch's cruelty is methodical and personal, targeting not just his body but his capacity for love. And yet the Tin Woodman emerges from his ordeal not bitter but gentle: he weeps (though his tears rust his jaw) and is moved to save the [[queen-of-the-field-mice|Queen of the Field Mice]] from the [[wildcat|Wildcat]], demonstrating that perhaps his heart was never truly lost at all.
