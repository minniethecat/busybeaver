# Hope

## Overview

Hope is the quiet engine beneath the travelers' journey through Oz. It is not as dramatic as the Scarecrow's quest for brains or the Lion's fear, but it is more essential: without hope, none of them would take the next step on the Yellow Brick Road. Dorothy's hope of returning home sustains her through every danger; the Scarecrow hopes for intelligence he already possesses; the Tin Woodman hopes for a heart that already guides his actions; the Lion hopes for courage he already demonstrates. Hope, in Baum's story, is the bridge between wishing and receiving — the emotional state that makes transformation possible.

## Properties

Hope in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* has several defining qualities:

- **Forward-looking**: Hope always points toward the future — the Emerald City, the granting of wishes, the return home
- **Fragile but renewable**: Dorothy's hope is repeatedly dashed ("There is no hope for me" — 011-051) and repeatedly renewed ("Even Dorothy had hope" — 015-093)
- **Collective**: The travelers sustain each other's hope; when one despairs, the others rally
- **The prerequisite for action**: Without hope, the journey would end; hope is what transforms passive wishing into active questing

## Role in the Story

### Hope Deferred

The Wizard's demands seem designed to destroy hope. When Oz tells Dorothy she must kill the Wicked Witch of the West before he will help her, she is devastated: "There is no hope for me, for Oz will not send me home until I have killed the Wicked Witch of the West" (011-051). Her friends cannot comfort her — they too are trapped by the Wizard's impossible condition.

### Hope Renewed

After the Witch's death, hope returns with surprising speed. The travelers return to the Emerald City "in high spirits. Even Dorothy had hope that 'The Great and Terrible Humbug,' as she called him, would find a way to send her back to Kansas" (015-093). This hope survives even the discovery that Oz is a fraud — because the journey has taught them that solutions come from unexpected places.

### The Final Test

Dorothy's hope is most severely tested after Oz departs without her in his balloon. "Dorothy wept bitterly at the passing of her hope to get home to Kansas again" (018-002). But she rallies, deciding to seek Glinda in the South — and it is Glinda who finally reveals the simple solution that was always within reach.

### Hope and the Journey

The story suggests that hope is not merely an emotion but a discipline. Dorothy hopes for home even when it seems impossible; the Scarecrow hopes for brains even as he solves every problem; the Lion hopes for courage even as he faces every danger. Hope is what keeps them on the road — and the road is what prepares them to receive what they hope for.

## Notes

Hope is the most understated theme in *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*, but it is also the most structurally important. Without hope, the travelers would not leave Munchkin Country. Without hope, the journey would end at the first obstacle. Baum embeds hope in the novel's architecture — the Yellow Brick Road, the distant spires of the Emerald City, the promise of the Wizard's power — and then gradually reveals that the hope was never in the destination but in the journey itself. The story's final irony is that hope was justified, but not as expected: Oz cannot grant wishes, but the hope of Oz's power is what drove the travelers to discover their own.
