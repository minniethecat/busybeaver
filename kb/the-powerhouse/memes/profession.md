# Profession

The defining framework of identity in the novel. Leithen is not merely a man who happens to practice law; he *is* a barrister, and his professional identity shapes how he thinks, what he notices, and what tools he has available when the ordinary rules break down.

## Definition

Profession in *The Powerhouse* is more than occupation. It is a way of seeing the world. Leithen approaches the mystery of the Powerhouse as a lawyer would — gathering evidence, weighing testimony, looking for inconsistencies in the story. His professional habits are his greatest asset: they make him methodical, sceptical, and persistent.

But profession is also a limitation. The legal mind assumes that rules will be followed, that evidence will matter, that rational argument will prevail. The Powerhouse operates on entirely different principles. Leithen's professional competence is real but circumscribed — it works within the "compact" of civilisation and is nearly useless outside it.

## Origin in the Text

Leithen's professional identity is established early: he gravitated to the Bar because "we like feeling that, if we are not the builders, at any rate we are the cement of civilisation" (003-033). This self-description is revealing — it positions law as a conservative force, maintaining rather than creating, and it suggests a certain modesty of ambition that is characteristic of the gentleman-professional.

Macgillivray represents a different professional culture — the police, with its institutional knowledge and its procedures. His professional assessment of the Powerhouse — "the fellows have no names, no numbers even, which we can get hold of" (004-041) — defines the problem in institutional terms.

## Analysis

Buchan's treatment of profession reflects the Edwardian ideal of the "amateur professional" — the man whose competence derives not from narrow expertise but from broad intelligence, social judgment, and character. Leithen is not a legal specialist; he is a barrister, a role defined as much by social standing and judgment as by technical knowledge. This ideal would prove fragile in the twentieth century, as the "extra-social intelligences" Lumley describes — the genuine experts, the narrow specialists — proved more powerful than the gentleman-amateurs who opposed them.
