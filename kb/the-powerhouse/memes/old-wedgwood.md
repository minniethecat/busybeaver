# Old Wedgwood

## Overview

"Old Wedgwood" refers to antique Josiah Wedgwood pottery, particularly jasperware, which was a highly prized collectible in Edwardian England. In *The Powerhouse*, it functions as a narrative device connecting Leithen to Lumley's circle. Leithen describes himself as a "bit of a connoisseur" who has "collected some good pieces" (PN 199).

## In *The Powerhouse*

In Chapter II, Old Wedgwood provides the crucial link between Leithen, the art critic Jenkinson, and Andrew Lumley. Jenkinson, the "keenest" Wedgwoodite Leithen knows, tells him about a pair of "magnificent plaques, with a unique Flaxman design" from the Wentworth sale, bought by Lumley through an agent named Lutrin (PN 201-202). Jenkinson's invitation to inspect the plaques at a house in South London leads Leithen to the [[white-lodge|White Lodge]] in Blackheath, where he discovers the telegram sent to "Bokhare Saronov" that cements his suspicions (PN 239). The Wedgwood collecting community thus inadvertently unravels Lumley's secret operations.

## Historical Context

Josiah Wedgwood (1730-1795) founded the Wedgwood pottery company in 1759, famous for its jasperware -- a dense, unglazed stoneware in blue, sage-green, lilac, and other colours with white classical reliefs. John Flaxman (1755-1826) was among the celebrated artists who designed for Wedgwood. By the Edwardian era, "Old Wedgwood" (pieces made during Josiah Wedgwood's lifetime) commanded high prices at auction and was collected by a small but devoted community, as Buchan accurately portrays.
