# High Ashes

## Overview

High Ashes is Andrew Lumley's country house in Hampshire, a long whitewashed cottage covered with creepers and roses, where the pivotal conversation of Chapter III takes place. It serves as the stage for Lumley's seductive philosophical performance.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Leithen arrives at High Ashes by accident — or so he believes. After a motor breakdown near Wolmer Forest, he seeks shelter at "a long whitewashed cottage, rising to two storeys in the centre," plentifully "covered with creepers and roses," with "the odour of flowers mingled with the faintest savour of wood-smoke" [003-009]. Only later does he realise his host is the "maniac who dreamed of the Powerhouse" [004-003].

The house's library is described as "the most attractive I think I have ever seen" [003-017] — a long room lined with glass-fronted bookcases, dominated by a Raeburn portrait over the fireplace. Over a perfect dinner of soles, chicken, strawberries, and Perrier-Jouet champagne, Lumley — a "most benevolent and courteous old gentleman" [003-044] — turns the conversation from books and politics to his dark philosophy of civilisation's fragility.

High Ashes embodies the Edwardian country-house setting at its most deceptively comfortable. The beauty of the house and the graciousness of its host mask the radical threat Lumley represents. It is here, in this serene Hampshire setting, that Leithen first hears the doctrine of "the marriage of knowledge and will" [003-038] and begins to understand the nature of the enemy.
