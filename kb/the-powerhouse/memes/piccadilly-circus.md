# Piccadilly Circus

## Overview

Piccadilly Circus is one of London's most iconic public spaces, where Regent Street meets Piccadilly at the heart of the West End. In the Edwardian era, it was already a nexus of traffic, entertainment, and social life, famous for its illuminated advertising and as a meeting place. In *The Powerhouse*, it serves as a metaphor for the convergence of forces that will drive the plot.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Piccadilly Circus is mentioned in a single but significant sentence in Chapter III, at the moment Leithen transitions from passive observer to active participant: "They say that everybody turns up in time at the corner of Piccadilly Circus if you wait long enough. I was to find myself like a citizen of Bagdad in the days of the great Caliph" (PN 258). This evocative comparison sets the scene for the Arabian Nights quality of the story that follows -- a story in which Leithen, without ever leaving London, finds himself embroiled in an adventure as exotic as any Eastern tale. Piccadilly Circus becomes the symbolic centre of a London that is not merely the capital of an empire but the stage for a global conspiracy.

## Historical Context

Piccadilly Circus was created in 1819 at the junction of Piccadilly with the newly built Regent Street. It takes its name from "piccadilly," a type of stiff collar, and the "circus" form (Latin for circle) common to London roundabouts. By the Edwardian period, it was a bustling entertainment district, home to the Criterion Theatre and the Pavilion music hall. The famous statue of Eros (the Shaftesbury Memorial Fountain) was erected in 1893. The area's illuminated advertising displays were already a noted feature, with Perrier and Bovril among the early brands to advertise there.
