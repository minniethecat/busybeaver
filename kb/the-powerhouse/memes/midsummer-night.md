# Midsummer Night

"Tells of a Midsummer Night" is the title of [[chapter-iii|Chapter III]] of *The Powerhouse*, and the chapter in which the novel's antagonist, [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]], first reveals his philosophy to [[sir-edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]]. The chapter's title echoes Shakespeare's *A Midsummer Night's Dream* — a play of enchantment, confusion, and transformations — and Buchan exploits the echo to lend the dinner party an atmosphere of dark enchantment in which the boundaries between civilisation and its dissolution blur.

## The Chapter's Structure

The chapter opens with Leithen motoring down from the uplands to "the shallow vale of the Wey" on a midsummer morning, his thoughts preoccupied not with the landscape but with the discovery that "the friend of Pitt-Heron, who knew about his going to Bokhara, was the maniac who dreamed of the 'Powerhouse'" (004-001). The chapter then traces Leithen's day: from this anxious morning through the deepening suspense of the afternoon, culminating in the evening dinner party at Lumley's house.

The party itself is a set-piece. Lumley presides at the head of his table, "the centre of the talk at his end," wearing "some foreign orders, including the Legion of Honour" (005-018). Leithen watches him with a mounting sense of horror: "Here in this pleasant room, with servants moving softly about and a mellow light on the silver from the shaded candles, I felt the man was buttressed and defended beyond my reach" (005-021).

## The Thematic Power of Midsummer

Buchan's choice of season is deliberate. Midsummer in the English literary imagination is a time when the normal rules are suspended — when fairies walk, when lovers lose their way, when the daylight stretches into an ambiguous twilight that confuses the real and the imagined. Lumley exploits this atmosphere. His talk, which in the cold light of morning might sound like the ravings of a crank, acquires in the midsummer candlelight a hypnotic persuasiveness.

The seasonal setting also creates a contrast with the novel's other theatre: the "wastes of Turkestan" (004-055) where [[charles-pitt-heron|Pitt-Heron]] is being hunted. While Leithen sits in an English garden at the height of summer, men are pursuing each other across the Central Asian desert. The midsummer beauty of Surrey is a kind of blindness — it conceals the reach of the forces that Lumley has set in motion.

## Leithen's Sleepless Night

The chapter does not end with the dinner party. Leithen returns home and endures a sleepless night, his mind turning over Lumley's words: "But always that phrase of his, the 'Powerhouse,' kept recurring. You know how twisted your thoughts get during a wakeful night, and long before I fell asleep towards morning I had worked myself up into a very complete dislike of that bland and smiling gentleman, my host" (004-010). The midsummer night becomes a night of dark revelation — the moment when Leithen's suspicion crystallises into conviction.
