# British Government

## Overview

The British Government in the Edwardian era was a constitutional monarchy in which executive power was exercised by the Cabinet, headed by the Prime Minister and responsible to Parliament. It administered the world's largest empire and commanded immense global influence, though the novel suggests that its institutional machinery was complacent and slow-moving.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The British Government appears in multiple contexts throughout the novel. [[edward-leithen]] does occasional work for the Government: he "used to 'devil' a little for the Solicitor-General, and 'note' cases sent to him from the different Government offices" (PN 189). This connection provides him with the case file on a German spy at Plymouth, which in turn leads him to the name of [[andrew-lumley]] (PN 191-193). Later, Leithen holds "a junior brief for the British Government" in the Chilean Arbitration, which keeps him in London after the law term ends (PN 1194).

The Labour MP [[chapman]] has a combative relationship with the Government: "Wattles, him as used to be as good a socialist as me, replied for the Government and his blamed Board" (PN 594). Lumley, in his exposition of his philosophy, describes Governments and "their not very intelligent police forces" as forming a "worldwide league" of civilisation that conspirators must learn to counter (PN 390).

## Historical Context

At the time of the novel (published 1916, set before the First World War), the Liberal government under H. H. Asquith had been in power since 1908, implementing social reforms and grappling with the constitutional crisis over the House of Lords. The British Government's intelligence and police apparatus, as depicted in the novel, was still relatively primitive -- [[scotland-yard]]'s Criminal Investigation Department had only been established in 1878.
