# Wedgwood Plaques

## Overview

The Wedgwood Plaques are a set of ceramic jasperware plaques, manufactured by the renowned English pottery firm Wedgwood, that serve as an important plot device in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. Their purchase by [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] through agents provides the pretext for [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]]'s investigation of the White Lodge at Blackheath.

## In The Powerhouse

The plaques enter the narrative through the art critic Jenkinson, whom Leithen encounters at his club. Jenkinson, "an authority on the subject of Wedgwood" (PN Ch. II), informed Leithen that Lumley had recently purchased the plaques from a Piccadilly dealer named Lutrin.

Lumley's method of acquisition was characteristically indirect. He "always bought through agents," and Jenkinson had "never seen the man" (PN 209). Jenkinson lamented this mode of collecting: "He will fill half a dozen houses with priceless treasures, and then die, and the whole show will be sold at auction and the best things carried off to America" (PN 209).

The plaques had already been removed from Lutrin's shop and delivered to Lumley's Blackheath property, the White Lodge. Jenkinson had been informed that "Mr. Lumley apparently might be willing to resell the Wedgwood plaques if he got a fair offer" (PN 211). The supposed willingness to sell suggests Lumley's collecting was instrumental rather than genuinely connoisseurial -- the objects were props in his elaborate cover identity.

When Leithen and Jenkinson arrived at the White Lodge, the plaques were examined by Jenkinson while Leithen explored the room. It was among the packing materials for the plaques that Leithen discovered the crucial telegram reading "Suivez a Bokhare Saronov" (PN 239). The Wedgwood plaques thus served an unwitting role as the vehicle that delivered the key piece of evidence into Leithen's hands.

## Analysis

The Wedgwood plaques function as more than a simple plot device. They represent the intersection of culture, commerce, and conspiracy. Lumley used the civilized world of art collecting -- a world of connoisseurship, clubs, and respectable dealers -- as cover for his criminal network. The plaques are genuine art objects, beautiful in themselves, yet they are wrapped around a deadly secret.

Wedgwood jasperware, with its classical white cameo reliefs on colored grounds, was the quintessential product of eighteenth-century English refinement. Lumley's choice of such pieces to furnish his cover identity is deliberate: Wedgwood signals wealth, taste, and establishment credentials. That these emblems of civilization conceal a conspiracy to undermine civilization itself is one of the novel's central ironies.

The plaques also illustrate Buchan's use of material culture to drive plot. The physical objects -- their purchase, packing, and inspection -- create the chain of events that brings Leithen into Lumley's lair. The telegram hidden among the packing shavings is a classic detective-story device, but it gains additional resonance from its incongruity: a coded message from Vienna nestled amid the detritus of English art collecting.
