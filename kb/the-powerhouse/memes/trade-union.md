# Trade Union

Trade unions in Edwardian Britain were the institutional expression of organised labour — growing rapidly in membership and political influence in the years before the Great War. In *The Powerhouse*, the trade union movement provides the backstory for one of the novel's most significant minor characters: Josiah Routh, alias [[tuke|Tuke]], the "discredited genius" whom [[andrew-lumley|Lumley]] recruits into his criminal network.

## Role in the Novel

The trade union context emerges in [[ch04]] when [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] visits [[macgillivray|Macgillivray]] at [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] to investigate the identity of Tuke, the butler who has been following Tommy Deloraine to Central Asia. Macgillivray asks his records:

> "Routh! Routh! Why, yes, there was a Routh we had dealings with a dozen years ago, when I used to go the Northeastern circuit. He was a trade-union official who bagged the funds, and they couldn't bring him to justice because of the ridiculous extralegal status they possess. He knew it, and played their own privileges against them. Oh, yes, he was a very complete rogue." (004-041)

This reference captures a specific historical controversy. The Trade Disputes Act 1906 had granted unions significant legal immunities, and the Osborne Judgment of 1909 had further complicated their legal status. Routh's crime — embezzling union funds and escaping prosecution by exploiting these very immunities — reflects contemporary anxieties about labour law and its unintended consequences.

The fuller story emerges when Leithen recruits [[chapman|Chapman]], the Labour MP, in [[ch05]]. Chapman knew Routh personally from "conferences and on platforms" (005-008), and his account fills in the details. Routh had been "a young engineer of a superior type, with a job in a big shop at Sheffield" (005-008). He "had professed advanced political views" and had been treasurer of a major union branch, becoming "the leader of the left wing of the movement" (005-008) with "that gift of half-scientific, half-philosophic jargon which is dear at all times to the hearts of the half-baked" (005-008).

Routh's fall was spectacular. He "had speculated with the funds of his Union and had lost a large sum" (005-009), though Chapman suspects he may have hidden the money rather than lost it. Having been "one of the chief advocates of the Unions' claims" for legal privilege, Routh "had the cool effrontery to turn the tables on them and use those very privileges to justify his action and escape prosecution" (005-009). He disappeared from England — and resurfaced years later as Lumley's agent, his "diabolically clever" mind now directed toward ends far more dangerous than embezzlement.

## Historical Context

The novel was written in the "smooth days before the war" (per Buchan's dedication), a period of intense labour agitation in Britain. The years 1910-1914 saw the Great Labour Unrest — waves of strikes in mining, transport, and manufacturing that some contemporaries feared were revolutionary in character. Chapman's defensiveness about the Unions ("You hate the Unions, and you rake up any rotten old prejudice to discredit them," 005-007) reflects the political temperature of the moment.

## Thematic Significance

Routh's trajectory — from trade union official to Lumley's agent in Central Asia — illustrates one of the novel's central propositions: that exceptional intelligence, divorced from moral allegiance, is politically neutral and available to the highest bidder. The trade union movement gave Routh a platform; Lumley gave him a purpose. The novel suggests that institutions, whether of Capital or Labour, are vulnerable to exploitation by "extra-social" minds who recognise no allegiance beyond their own ambition.

Chapman's anger at Routh is not merely personal — it is institutional. Routh betrayed the union movement, and in doing so he demonstrated that the movement's legal protections could be turned against it. The novel's implicit argument is that any institution, however noble its aims, creates vulnerabilities that a sufficiently intelligent and unscrupulous operator can exploit.
