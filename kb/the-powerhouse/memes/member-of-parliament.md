# Member of Parliament

## Overview

The concept of a Member of Parliament (MP) is central to the social framework of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. Both the narrator [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] and his friend [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] serve as MPs, and their parliamentary status shapes their identities, obligations, and the way they are perceived by other characters.

## In The Powerhouse

Leithen's parliamentary identity is established early. When his car breaks down and he seeks shelter at Lumley's country house, he presents himself as "a Member of Parliament and of the Bar, who had suffered a motor accident" (PN 290). This self-description combines two pillars of Edwardian establishment identity: the law and the legislature.

Tommy Deloraine's relationship with Parliament is more ambivalent. He represents the constituency of Joggleberry and is deeply bored by his duties. His opening complaint sets the tone: "What a juggins I am to be mouldering here! Joggleberry is the celestial limit, what they call in happier lands the pink penultimate. And the frowst on those back benches! Was there ever such a moth-eaten old museum?" (PN 68). When Leithen calls the Commons "the Mother of Parliaments," Tommy retorts, "Damned monkey-house" (PN 71--72).

Later, when Chapman becomes involved in the investigation, his Parliamentary duties suffer: "His resolution led to a complete disregard of his Parliamentary duties. Deputations of constituents waited for him in vain" (PN 628). The novel thus presents Parliament as both a badge of respectability and a constraint that true men of action must occasionally escape.

The sitting of Parliament also affects the novel's timeline. Leithen notes that "the late sitting of Parliament kept me in London after the end of the term" (PN 1194), connecting the narrative to the rhythms of the political calendar. The Chilean Arbitration and the extended session together create the conditions for the story's summer-long crisis.

## Analysis

Parliament in *The Powerhouse* represents the tension between institutional respectability and genuine effectiveness. Tommy is an MP but finds the institution stifling; Chapman is a dedicated Labour member but finds himself distracted from his duties by the demands of real-life action. The novel suggests that Parliament, for all its dignity, is not equipped to deal with threats like the Powerhouse -- threats that require individual initiative, courage, and the willingness to operate outside institutional frameworks.

This tension is embodied in Lumley's critique of civilization. He argues that "civilisation wins because it is a worldwide league; its enemies fail because they are parochial. But supposing--" (PN 391). The ellipsis hangs over the entire novel: what if civilization's enemies learned to organize as effectively as civilization itself? Parliament, as the institutional embodiment of civilized order, is implicitly challenged by this question.

Buchan himself was a Conservative MP (from 1927), writing before his own parliamentary career. The novel reflects the Edwardian gentleman's ambivalence about democratic institutions: respect for their symbolic importance, frustration with their practical limitations, and a belief that the nation's real defense depends on the character of individual men rather than the machinery of the state.
