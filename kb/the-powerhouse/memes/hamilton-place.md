# Hamilton Place

## Overview

Hamilton Place is a short, prestigious street in Mayfair, connecting Park Lane to Piccadilly. Bounded by Hyde Park on one side and the grounds of some of London's grandest houses on the other, it was one of the most exclusive addresses in Edwardian London. In *The Powerhouse*, it marks a critical moment in Leithen's flight.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Hamilton Place appears in Chapter VII as Leithen leaves Grosvenor Gate and walks "down it" -- meaning down Park Lane towards Hamilton Place (PN 957). It is at this point, with "less than a quarter of a mile between me and Belgrave Square," that Leithen's brief respite ends. He has "the indefinable but unerring sensation of being watched" and catches "a man looking eagerly at me from the other side of the street ... making a sign to someone farther off" (PN 957). Hamilton Place thus marks the transition from apparent safety back to acute danger -- the threshold where Leithen realizes his pursuers have regrouped and the final dash to the Embassy will be the hardest part of the journey.

## Historical Context

Hamilton Place was named after the Duke of Hamilton and was developed in the early 19th century. It was part of the Grosvenor Estate and was known for its grand mansions. The proximity to Hyde Park, Piccadilly, and Buckingham Palace made it one of the most desirable addresses in London. Its short length -- barely a few hundred yards -- and its enclosed character, with the park wall on one side, made it a natural funnel through which pedestrians and traffic passed between the park and Piccadilly.
