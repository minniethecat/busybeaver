# Lord Morecambe

Lord Morecambe is a peer of the realm who appears as a dinner guest at the large political gathering in the House of Lords where [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] observes [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] at close range. His presence, and his evident fascination with Lumley, serve as markers of Lumley's extraordinary social penetration.

## Overview

Lord Morecambe is described only in passing, but his brief appearance is freighted with significance. He is seated near Lumley at the head of the table, alongside a famous General and an ex-Viceroy of India, and his posture and decorations tell a story of their own. The scene crystallises the paradox at the heart of Lumley's operation: a man whom Leithen knows to be a criminal mastermind is being treated as an honoured guest by the highest ranks of British society.

## Role in the Novel

Lord Morecambe appears in a single remarkable passage in [[0144-the-Powerhouse-30c98a18-ch010|Chapter V: I Take a Partner]] (though the dinner scene occurs chronologically later, during the period of Leithen's anxious waiting). The dinner, given by the chief of Leithen's party in the House of Lords, assembles "fifty or sixty guests, and a blaze of stars and decorations" (005-018). Lumley sits at the head, and Leithen watches him from a distance: "I could see the blue ribbon bulging out on Lord Morecambe's breast as he leaned forward to question him" (005-018).

The detail of the blue ribbon -- presumably the Garter -- signals Morecambe's status as one of the highest-ranking peers in the realm. That such a figure should lean forward eagerly to engage Lumley in conversation is a measure of Lumley's social achievements. Lumley, wearing foreign orders including the Legion of Honour, is the centre of attention, "beaming through his glasses on his neighbours" while Leithen watches with "despairing hatred."

## Significance

Lord Morecambe serves as a synecdoche for the entire British establishment that Lumley has successfully infiltrated. His blue ribbon represents the apex of the honours system; his eager attention to Lumley represents the establishment's willing embrace of a man it does not understand. Buchan uses Morecambe and the other distinguished guests to underscore the novel's central anxiety: that the institutions designed to protect society are themselves vulnerable to manipulation by those who understand how to work them.

The scene also heightens the dramatic irony that drives the novel's second half. The reader, like Leithen, knows what Lumley truly is; watching the great and good of Britain treat him as an honoured equal creates an almost unbearable tension.
