# The Wild Goose Chase

## Overview

The Wild Goose Chase is both the title of the opening chapter of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse* ("I: Beginning of the Wild-Goose Chase") and the organizing metaphor for the novel's initial action. It describes the search for the vanished [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]], which appears futile but proves essential to the novel's resolution.

## In The Powerhouse

The chapter "Beginning of the Wild-Goose Chase" (PN 57--62) opens with [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]]'s frustration with Parliament and his readiness for adventure. When [[ethel-pitt-heron|Ethel Pitt-Heron]] reveals her husband's mysterious disappearance, Tommy volunteers to find him, though he has only the vaguest idea where Pitt-Heron has gone.

The phrase captures the apparent hopelessness of the search. Pitt-Heron had fled without explanation, heading into Central Asia -- "the wild tangle of the Pamirs" (PN 443) -- where a man could vanish without trace. Tommy's initial journey through Moscow yielded nothing: "Nobody in Moscow had seen or heard of any wandering Englishman remotely like Charles" (PN 245). After three weeks, Tommy was "nearly at the end of his tether and spoke of returning home" (PN 245).

The chase transforms from wild goose to deadly serious pursuit when Leithen discovers the Vienna telegram. The discovery that Lumley's agent [[tuke|Tuke]] was also hunting Pitt-Heron gave the search new urgency and purpose. Leithen's wire to Tommy -- "Go on to Bokhara. Have information you will meet him there" (PN 247) -- converted the wild goose chase into a rescue mission.

The novel's final chapter is titled "IX: Return of the Wild Geese" (PN 1172), completing the metaphor. The wild geese return: Pitt-Heron, Tommy, and eventually Pitt-Heron's wife Ethel, all reunited after the destruction of the Powerhouse. What began as a quixotic errand ends in restoration.

## Analysis

The wild goose chase metaphor operates on multiple levels. On the surface, it denotes a futile search -- wild geese are proverbially elusive. But the chapter title's transformation into "Return of the Wild Geese" suggests that the chase was never truly futile. It was a necessary passage, a trial that tested and proved the friendships at the novel's core.

The metaphor also introduces the novel's geographical imagination. The wild geese fly across the map of empires -- from London to Moscow, across the Transcaspian, into the Pamirs, toward the Indian frontier. This geographical sweep contrasts with the claustrophobic interior spaces of Lumley's conspiracy, balancing the closed rooms of Piccadilly and Blackheath with the open wilderness of Central Asia.

Buchan's use of the phrase may also carry a faint echo of the "Wild Geese" -- the Irish soldiers who served in continental European armies after the Treaty of Limerick. Like those exiles, Pitt-Heron is a man fleeing his own country, though for very different reasons. The Irish Wild Geese served foreign masters; Pitt-Heron fled from one.
