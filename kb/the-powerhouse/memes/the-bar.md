# The Bar

## Overview

The Bar refers to the profession of barrister — a specialised type of lawyer in England who argues cases in court. Throughout *The Powerhouse*, Sir Edward Leithen's identity as a barrister shapes not only his social standing but his entire mode of investigation: slow, methodical, grounded in facts and logic.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Leithen is introduced as "just beginning to be busy at the Bar" [001-001], balancing his legal practice with a newly-won seat in Parliament. When asked why he chose the profession, he explains: "I am a dry creature, who loves facts and logic. I am not a flier, I have no new ideas, I don't want to lead men and I like work. I am the ordinary educated Englishman, and my sort gravitates to the Bar" [003-033].

This self-description proves crucial to the novel's structure. Unlike [[tommy-deloraine]], the impulsive adventurer who chases Pitt-Heron to Central Asia, Leithen approaches the mystery as a legal problem — gathering evidence, cross-examining witnesses, and building a case. His chambers at [[the-temple|the Temple]] serve as his command centre, and his professional contacts — including [[macgillivray]] at Scotland Yard, a former barrister himself — become his allies.

The novel repeatedly contrasts Leithen's legal cast of mind with the lawless world of [[the-powerhouse-org|the Powerhouse]]. Lumley derisively calls him "not the brightest jewel of the English Bar" [008-034], but it is precisely Leithen's lawyerly patience and respect for evidence that allows him to outmanoeuvre Lumley's conspiracy.

Leithen's famous self-characterisation as "the cement of civilisation" [003-033] is spoken in the context of explaining why he became a barrister — the law, in his view, is what holds society together against the forces of chaos represented by the Powerhouse.
