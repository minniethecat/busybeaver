# Portman Square

Portman Square is the London residence of [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]] and his wife [[ethel-pitt-heron|Ethel Pitt-Heron]]. It serves as a key location in the narrative, where [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] learns of the deepening mystery surrounding Charles's disappearance and later receives the news of his safe return.

## Role in the Narrative

Portman Square first enters the story through [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]]'s account of Charles Pitt-Heron's increasingly strange behaviour. Tommy remarks: "I go a good deal to Portman Square, and all last year he has been getting queerer" [001-018]. This casual reference establishes the house as the domestic centre of the Pitt-Heron household and the starting point of the mystery.

Following Tommy's departure for Russia, Leithen finds himself drawn to Portman Square. "Next afternoon I found my steps wending in the direction of Portman Square" [001-043]. His visit to Ethel marks the beginning of his personal involvement in the affair, transforming him from observer to participant.

## Leithen's Visits

Leithen returns to Portman Square before the Whitsuntide holiday: "But before I left London I went again to Portman Square" [003-004]. During this visit, Ethel shows him the draft letter she discovered, which reveals the true danger Charles faces and deepens Leithen's commitment to uncovering the conspiracy.

The house appears at the narrative's resolution: "Calling at Portman Square a few days later I learned from Ethel Pitt-Heron's glowing face that her troubles were over" [009-010]. With [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s death and the Powerhouse conspiracy broken, Charles returns safely, and Portman Square is restored as a place of domestic peace.
