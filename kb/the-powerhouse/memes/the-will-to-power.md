# The Will to Power

## Overview

The will to power, Friedrich Nietzsche's concept of a fundamental drive for mastery and self-overcoming, is the philosophical foundation of [[andrew-lumley]]'s worldview in *The Powerhouse*. The idea is introduced not by Lumley himself but through a memory Leithen recalls of a German professor he met in the Tyrol.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Leithen remembers the encounter while reflecting on Lumley's philosophy: "It was of a hot upland meadow in Tyrol, where among acres of flowers and beside a leaping stream I was breakfasting after a morning spent in climbing the white crags. I had picked up a German on the way, a small man of the Professor class, who did me the honour to share my sandwiches. He conversed fluently, but quaintly in English, and he was, I remember, a Nietzschean, and a hot rebel against the established order" [003-058].

The professor's key pronouncement — "The pity is that the reformers do not know, and those who know are too idle to reform. Some day there will come the marriage of knowledge and will, and then the world will march" [003-065] — serves as the philosophical key to the entire novel. Lumley embodies exactly this marriage: he possesses knowledge (of science, law, finance, human nature) and will (the ruthless drive to reshape the world). The novel casts Lumley as a Nietzschean superman — "that bland superman" [003-070] — who has transcended conventional morality and seeks to impose his will on civilisation itself. The Tyrol memory thus provides the intellectual framework for understanding the threat the Powerhouse represents.
