# Central Asia

Central Asia in *The Powerhouse* is the novel's great unseen stage — a vast, dangerous wilderness into which [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]] disappears and into which both allies and enemies pursue him. The region is never directly depicted; all information about it arrives through telegrams, cables, and the anxious consultations of maps in London. This narrative distance turns Central Asia into a theatre of the imagination, a blank space on which the characters project their hopes and fears.

## Bokhara as Destination

The ancient city of [[bokhara|Bokhara]] is the gravitational centre of the novel's Asian plot. Pitt-Heron's journey takes him there first, and it is from Bokhara that both [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] and the sinister [[felix-tuke|Tuke]] set out in pursuit. Leithen first learns of Bokhara through the telegram intercepted in the Blackheath house: the message "Go on to Bokhara. Have information you will meet him there" (002-020) is the piece of evidence that convinces him Pitt-Heron is in danger.

Bokhara functions as a threshold — the last outpost of communication before the true wilderness begins. From Bokhara, Pitt-Heron sets out "with a small caravan on June 2nd by the main road through the Hissar range" (004-050). Beyond Bokhara lies the Pamirs, an unmappable region where a man might vanish forever.

## The Geography of Pursuit

The novel maps Central Asia with surprising precision. Leithen's friend [[felix|Felix]] — who knows the region from his diplomatic service — spreads out a "big German atlas" (004-015) and traces the possible routes:

- **South, through the Hissar range by Pamirski Post to Gilgit and Kashmir** — this is identified as the "likeliest route, if a man wanted to travel fast" (004-015)
- **Up the Oxus into northern Afghanistan**
- **By Merv into northeastern Persia**

The pursuers split: Tuke and his companion [[saronov|Saronov]] take the shorter route through Samarkand, "evidently intending to strike into the hills by a shorter route than the main road from Bokhara" (005-022). The geography becomes a race — a contest of routes and speeds in which the fate of Pitt-Heron hangs on the relative pace of two caravans moving through unforgiving terrain.

## The Meaning of Distance

Central Asia serves a thematic function beyond plot mechanics. It represents the farthest reach of the Powerhouse's influence — proof that [[andrew-lumley|Lumley]]'s organisation can project force to the edges of the known world. Leithen's sense of helplessness is crystallised by the sheer distance: while he navigates the drawing rooms and streets of London, men are hunting each other across "the wastes of Turkestan" (004-055). The contrast between the two arenas — London society and Central Asian wilderness — is one of the novel's most powerful structural devices.

> He knew the Indian frontier as few men know it, and in the wild tangle of the Pamirs he hoped to baffle his enemy. (004-008)

> Pitt-Heron had left with a small caravan on June 2nd by the main road through the Hissar range. Tommy had arrived on June 10th and on the 12th had set off with two servants on the same trail. (004-050)

> The two had gone by Samarkand, evidently intending to strike into the hills by a shorter route than the main road from Bokhara. The frontier posts had been warned, and the stalkers had become the stalked. (005-022)
