# Leithen's Siege

Leithen's Siege is the climactic sequence in Chapter VII of the novel, during which [[edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]]'s London flat is effectively cut off from the outside world by Powerhouse operatives, forcing him to engineer a desperate escape through London to deliver his evidence before [[andrew-lumley|Lumley]] can silence him.

## Summary

The siege unfolds in a series of escalating isolations. Leithen, having stayed up "till the small hours, marshalling my evidence in a formal statement and making two copies of it" (007-005), sends [[chapman|Chapman]] to deliver one copy to [[macgillivray|Macgillivray]] at Scotland Yard (007-006). Chapman is to ring Leithen upon delivery, but the call never comes.

When half an hour passes, then three-quarters, Leithen rings Scotland Yard — they have no news of Chapman (007-008). A young doctor eventually telephones: Chapman has been "run down by a motorcar at the corner of Whitehall," with only minor injuries, but during the confusion his pocket was picked and the letter taken (007-010, 007-011). Leithen realizes with chilling clarity: "Lumley had got my statement, and realised precisely how much I knew" (007-012).

Leithen locks his flat door and telephones his chauffeur Stagg to collect him at two o'clock (007-013). Then he attempts to ring [[felix|Monsieur Felix]] — but "I found that my telephone was now broken and connection was impossible" (007-014). The isolation is now complete: no written word can leave, no spoken word can reach an ally.

Stagg arrives five minutes late — unprecedented for the reliable chauffeur (007-016). Leithen enters the car, gives instructions for a circuitous route through Harrow and Brentford (007-019), and sinks into a half-doze — only to realize with horror that the man driving is not Stagg at all. The driver's hair "grew regularly down to his coat-collar" where Stagg had a scar (007-020). Lumley has replaced his chauffeur.

At the Edgeware Road traffic block, Leithen makes his escape: he opens the car door quietly, steps out, and dives between a butcher's cart and a motorbus for the sidewalk (007-022). From there he makes his way to a friendly picture-shop, asks the proprietor "have you a back door?" (007-025), and begins the desperate flight toward [[the-russian-embassy|the Russian Embassy]] — the one place Lumley cannot reach him.

## Context

The siege represents the novel's climax, the moment when the conspiracy's abstract menace becomes concrete physical threat. Throughout the narrative, the Powerhouse has operated through intermediaries, financial manipulation, and psychological pressure. The siege is its final escalation: the decision to isolate and potentially eliminate the one man who has pieced together the full picture.

The sequence is masterful in its pacing. Each isolation — Chapman intercepted, telephone cut, chauffeur replaced — removes another layer of Leithen's normal protections. By the time he enters the car with the impostor chauffeur, he is completely alone in a city that has become hostile territory.

## Significance

The siege transforms Leithen from investigator into quarry. For the first time, he is not pursuing the conspiracy — it is pursuing him. The cutting of his telephone (007-014) and the interception of Chapman (007-010, 007-011) demonstrate the Powerhouse's ability to reach into the most intimate corners of daily life — a man's home, his telephone, his loyal servant, his trusted chauffeur. No space is safe.

Leithen's escape, accomplished through quick thinking and courage, represents the triumph of individual resourcefulness over organized conspiracy — a theme that runs through the novel's resolution. But the escape is narrow, contingent, and depends on luck (the traffic block at Edgeware Road) as much as skill. The siege leaves no doubt that Lumley's network is operationally formidable, and that Leithen survives only because he acts before the net can fully close.

The impostor chauffeur is a particularly effective device: the man most trusted with Leithen's physical safety — his driver — has been replaced without Leithen's knowledge. The betrayal of the familiar is the siege's deepest horror, suggesting that Lumley's reach extends into the very fabric of everyday trust.
