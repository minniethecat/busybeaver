# Macgillivray

Leithen's friend and contact at Scotland Yard, representing the institutional response to the Powerhouse. A professional policeman, Macgillivray provides Leithen with access to official knowledge — and illustrates the limits of that knowledge when confronting an enemy who operates entirely outside official frameworks.

## Role in the Story

Macgillivray serves as the institutional counterpoint to Leithen's amateur investigation. When Leithen first asks him about the Powerhouse in [[ch04]], Macgillivray laughs — "I should think we have several hundreds of such pet names on our records" (004-040). To the police, the Powerhouse is one name among many, indistinguishable from crackpot conspiracies and amateur anarchist circles.

But Macgillivray's professional dismissal is not the end of the matter. He agrees to look into it, and his subsequent findings confirm that the Powerhouse is real — a genuine threat that official channels have failed to recognise. His role is to validate Leithen's concerns while demonstrating why official institutions are poorly equipped to address them: "The fellows have no names, no numbers even, which we can get hold of" (004-041).

## Background

Macgillivray is a career policeman, a member of the professional class that serves the Edwardian state. He has experience with political crime — he mentions dealing with a trade-union official named Routh "a dozen years ago" (004-042) — and his institutional memory spans the various radical and revolutionary movements that have crossed Scotland Yard's radar.

## Key Appearances

- [[ch04]] — Leithen consults Macgillivray about the Powerhouse (004-040)
- Subsequent chapters — Macgillivray provides ongoing institutional support and intelligence

## Relationships

- **Sir Edward Leithen** — A friend and professional contact; their relationship is built on mutual respect between the law's two branches (barrister and policeman)
- **The Powerhouse** — Macgillivray's institutional knowledge confirms the threat but cannot neutralise it

## Significance

Macgillivray represents the institutional response to the Powerhouse — competent, methodical, but ultimately limited by its own procedures. His presence in the novel establishes that the threat is not merely Leithen's imagination: the police know about organisations like the Powerhouse, but they are organised to deal with criminals, not with "extra-social intelligences" who have learned to operate invisibly within the machine of civilisation.
