# Berkeley Square

## Overview

Berkeley Square is one of the principal garden squares of Mayfair, London W1, laid out in the mid-18th century. By the Edwardian period it was one of the most fashionable residential addresses in London, surrounded by townhouses occupied by the aristocracy and wealthy establishment figures.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Berkeley Square forms part of the Mayfair geography that Buchan maps in precise detail throughout the novel. It lies within walking distance of [[the-albany]] on Piccadilly, where [[andrew-lumley]] maintains his rooms, and [[down-street]], where [[tommy-deloraine]] lives. The square's establishment character — home to peers, politicians, and men of finance — represents the world of [[the-compact]] that Lumley's [[the-powerhouse-org]] seeks to undermine.

The Mayfair squares — Berkeley, [[grosvenor-place]], [[portman-square]], [[belgrave-square]] — form a network of aristocratic enclaves that Buchan's characters navigate as they move between the social world of dinner parties and the hidden world of conspiracy.

## Historical Context

In the early 20th century, Berkeley Square was the London seat of figures including the Dukes of Devonshire and other prominent families. Its proximity to the clubs of St. James's and the shops of [[bond-street]] made it central to the social geography of Edwardian high society.
