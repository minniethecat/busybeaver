# The Embankment

## Overview

The Thames Embankment — the riverside roadway and walkway along the north bank of the Thames — appears repeatedly in *The Powerhouse* as part of Sir Edward Leithen's daily route through London, marking moments of transition between danger and safety.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Leithen walks along the Embankment as part of his regular routine from his flat in Down Street to his chambers at the Temple. Early in the novel, he notes: "I was in the habit of walking down to the Temple by way of Pall Mall and the Embankment" [006-081]. These walks become freighted with tension as Leithen realises he is being watched and followed.

On the evening of his dinner with [[macgillivray]] at the restaurant in Antioch Street, Leithen takes the Embankment route: "It was a hot, still night, and I had had a heavy day in Court... I walked along the Embankment, and up Regent Street towards Oxford Circus" [006-134]. The walk marks a threshold — moving from his professional world into the dangerous territory of the Powerhouse's London operations.

The Embankment frames the novel's closure as well. After his final confrontation with [[andrew-lumley]], Leithen crosses [[westminster-bridge]] and notes the night express to the Continent rumbling over the river [008-058] — a moment of quiet resolution along the same Thames that the Embankment follows.

The Embankment is not a mere geographic reference but a narrative device: it represents the boundary between the safe, institutional London of Parliament and the Temple and the hidden London of the Powerhouse's surveillance network.
