# Regent Street

## Overview

Regent Street is one of London's most celebrated thoroughfares, planned by John Nash in the early 19th century as a grand route connecting the Prince Regent's Carlton House to Regent's Park. Lined with imposing neoclassical buildings, it was a major shopping and entertainment destination. In *The Powerhouse*, it serves as a backdrop for both futile surveillance and a fateful journey.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Regent Street appears twice in the novel. In Chapter V, Chapman "announces his intention ... of keeping watch on Mr. Andrew Lumley at the Albany" and his surveillance leads him to spend time in "the neighbourhood of Piccadilly and Regent Street," where he is "very nearly given in charge more than once for molesting peaceable citizens" (PN 628). Later, in Chapter VI, Leithen walks "up Regent Street towards Oxford Circus" on his way to the trap dinner at Rapaccini's in Antioch Street (PN 705). Both instances frame Regent Street as a threshold between the public world of Society and the hidden world of conspiracy -- a surface of respectability beneath which danger lurks.

## Historical Context

Regent Street was built between 1814 and 1825 to John Nash's designs, part of a broader scheme to create a ceremonial route through the West End. The original Nash buildings were largely replaced in the early 20th century by grander Edwardian structures. At the time of the novel, Regent Street was at the height of its fashion, home to the Cafe Royal, Liberty's department store, and numerous high-end retailers. Its sweeping curve from Piccadilly Circus to Oxford Circus made it one of the most recognizable streets in the Empire.
