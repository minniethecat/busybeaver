# Grosvenor Gate

## Overview

Grosvenor Gate is an entrance to Hyde Park on the southeastern side, located on Park Lane. It opens into one of the most fashionable sections of the park, near the eastern end of the Serpentine and close to the grand houses of Park Lane. In *The Powerhouse*, it provides a moment of apparent normality in Leithen's desperate flight.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Grosvenor Gate features in Chapter VII during Leithen's race across London to reach the Embassy. After crossing Hyde Park from the Bayswater side, Leithen reaches "Grosvenor Gate, where on the little green chairs well-dressed people were taking the air" (PN 956). He "recognised several acquaintances and stopped for a moment to talk to one of them" (PN 956). This surreal moment -- a hunted man pausing for casual conversation among Society -- illustrates the novel's central theme: the thin membrane between civilized London and the forces threatening to destroy it. The well-dressed loungers on their green chairs are utterly unaware that the man chatting with them is fleeing for his life.

## Historical Context

Grosvenor Gate was named for the Grosvenor family (later the Dukes of Westminster), whose vast London estate lay to the south and west of the park. The "little green chairs" Leithen mentions were a famous feature of London parks in the Edwardian era; park-keepers issued tickets for their use, and they were occupied by nannies, the elderly, and those seeking to see and be seen. The gate's proximity to Park Lane -- the most fashionable residential address in London -- made it a natural gathering place for Society.
