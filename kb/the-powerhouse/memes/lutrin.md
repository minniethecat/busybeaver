# Lutrin

Lutrin is an art dealer who serves as an intermediary for [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] in the purchase of fine art and antiques. He operates a shop in London and is known to [[jenkinson|Jenkinson]], the art critic, as a reliable agent for well-heeled private collectors.

## Overview

Lutrin operates in that shadowy borderland of the art world where dealers act as buying agents for wealthy collectors who prefer to remain anonymous. He purchased the celebrated Wentworth Wedgwood plaques at auction on behalf of Lumley, a transaction that provides [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] with one of his early concrete connections between the mysterious art collector and the wider world.

## Role in the Novel

Lutrin is mentioned but does not appear directly in the narrative. His role is described during a luncheon conversation in [[0144-the-Powerhouse-30c98a18-ch007|Chapter II: I First Hear of Mr. Andrew Lumley]], when [[jenkinson|Jenkinson]] tells Leithen about the Wentworth sale. Jenkinson explains that a pair of magnificent Wedgwood plaques with a unique Flaxman design had been "bought by Lutrin for a well-known private collector, by name Andrew Lumley" (002-005).

Later, it is Lutrin who informs Jenkinson that Lumley might be willing to resell the plaques for a fair offer, prompting the expedition to [[the-white-lodge|the White Lodge]] at Blackheath that proves so fruitful for Leithen's investigation. Jenkinson had expected to find the plaques in "Lutrin's shop," but discovered that Lumley had taken delivery of them at once -- a pattern consistent with Lumley's practice of removing valuable objects from public circulation.

## Significance

Lutrin represents one of the many intermediaries through whom Lumley operates, maintaining a layer of indirection between himself and his transactions. Like the agents who buy on his behalf, Lutrin is a figure who enables Lumley's elaborate system of concealment. The art dealer is a minor but necessary cog in the machinery of the Powerhouse, demonstrating how Lumley's network extended not only into politics and finance but into the very fabric of London's cultural and commercial life.

Lutrin also serves a narrative function: his purchase of the plaques creates the chain of events that brings Leithen to the White Lodge, where he finds the coded telegram that confirms his suspicions about [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]]'s journey.
