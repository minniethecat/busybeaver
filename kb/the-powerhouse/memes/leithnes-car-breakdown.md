# Leithen's Car Breakdown

## Overview

Leithen's Car Breakdown is a deceptively significant event in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. Occurring during the Whitsuntide holiday, what appears to be a routine motorcar malfunction in rural Surrey delivers [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] directly into the orbit of [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]].

## Context

Leithen had recently acquired "a car and a chauffeur called Stagg" and was looking forward to "trying it in a tour in the West country" (PN 262). The holiday followed a busy Parliamentary and legal term. However, before departing London, Leithen made a final visit to Portman Square to see [[ethel-pitt-heron|Ethel Pitt-Heron]], who was increasingly distressed by her husband's unexplained disappearance.

En route to Farnham, Leithen's car suffered a mechanical failure. As he describes it: the car had to be towed by horses to Farnham for repairs, "a job of some hours," and the nearest inn was eight miles distant (PN 282--283). His chauffeur Stagg borrowed a bicycle to seek help, leaving Leithen stranded in the Surrey countryside as evening approached.

Seeking shelter, Leithen happened upon "a long whitewashed cottage, rising to two storeys in the centre," covered with creepers and roses (PN 288). He presented himself as "a Member of Parliament and of the Bar, who had suffered a motor accident" and was invited to stay the night (PN 290).

## Significance

The breakdown is rich with irony. Leithen regarded the episode as a trivial misadventure, yet it placed him directly inside the home of the very man orchestrating the conspiracy against his friend [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]]. The ancient housekeeper, the absence of a car (which "had gone that day to London for repairs"), and the convenient hospitality all suggest that the breakdown was orchestrated by Lumley to study the man investigating Pitt-Heron's disappearance.

During the long evening conversation, Lumley expounded his philosophy. It was here that Leithen first heard the term "Powerhouse," and Lumley discoursed on "great extra-social intelligences" (PN 384), the fragility of civilization, and the secret powers operating behind world events. Leithen left at dawn, having learned Lumley's name and recognizing him as the "maniac who dreamed of the 'Powerhouse'" (PN 430).

The car breakdown thus functions as the moment Leithen transitions from passive observer to active participant in the conspiracy. What began as a frustrating mechanical failure became the catalyst for the entire adventure.
