# The Advertising Member

## Overview

The "advertising member" appears in the novel's opening paragraph as a satirical figure representing the mundane world of parliamentary politics from which [[edward-leithen]] is about to be drawn. Buchan uses this unnamed character to establish the contrast between ordinary political life and the extraordinary conspiracy that follows.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The novel opens with Leithen leaving the House of Commons: "Inside a dull debate was winding on, and an advertising member had been trying to get up a row with the Speaker" [001-001]. The phrase "advertising member" suggests a backbench MP seeking publicity rather than substance — precisely the kind of political theatre that Leithen finds tedious. The contrast between "the frowsy place and the cheerful world outside" sets up the novel's central tension between institutional routine and the vital world of action and danger.

The advertising member serves as a foil to [[andrew-lumley]]: where Lumley operates through secrecy and hidden power, the advertising member seeks the shallowest form of public attention. Both represent different corruptions of the political order that Leithen, as [[the-amateur]] gentleman, must navigate between.
