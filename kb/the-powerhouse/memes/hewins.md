# Hewins

Hewins is the butler at the Russian Embassy in London. He is described as an imperturbable man whose face registers no surprise even in the most extraordinary circumstances. His timely intervention during a critical scene in Chapter VII is likened by [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] to a *deus ex machina* -- the "god from the machine" who resolves an otherwise dangerous standoff.

## Overview

Hewins embodies the ideal of the diplomatic servant: discreet, unflappable, and governed entirely by protocol. He knows [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] from the latter's previous visits to the Embassy as a dinner guest and friend of [[felix|Monsieur Felix]]. This familiarity, combined with Hewins's steadfast refusal to be ruffled by anything, proves decisive when Leithen bursts into the Embassy kitchen pursued by [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s agents.

## Role in the Novel

Hewins appears in a single but pivotal moment in [[0144-the-Powerhouse-30c98a18-ch012|Chapter VII: I Find Sanctuary]]. Leithen has fled through the streets of London, pursued by Lumley's operatives, and has taken refuge in the Russian Embassy by entering through the kitchen of [[monsieur-alphonse|Monsieur Alphonse]], the Embassy's chef. A footman -- one of Lumley's agents -- attempts to seize Leithen with Alphonse's help, but the standoff is broken when Hewins enters the kitchen.

Leithen appeals to Hewins directly: "Hewins, you know me. I have often dined here, and you know that I am a friend of Monsieur Felix. I am on my way to see him on an urgent matter, and for various reasons I had to enter by Monsieur Alphonse's kitchen. Will you take me at once to Monsieur Felix?" (007-016).

Hewins's response is characteristic. He bows, his face shows no sign of surprise, and he says only, "This way, sir." He leads Leithen to [[felix|Felix]] without hesitation or further inquiry, effectively ending the threat from Lumley's agents and providing Leithen the sanctuary he needs to execute his plan against the Powerhouse.

## Significance

Hewins represents the quiet professionalism that Buchan often attributes to the anonymous servants who make the world of diplomacy and high society function. His role is small but essential: without his intervention, Leithen might have been captured or killed in the Embassy kitchen, and the conspiracy of [[the-powerhouse-org|the Powerhouse]] might never have been stopped. Buchan presents Hewins as a figure whose very imperturbability is a kind of moral force -- he does what is correct and proper regardless of the drama swirling around him.
