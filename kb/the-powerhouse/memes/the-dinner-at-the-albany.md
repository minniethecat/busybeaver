# The Dinner at the Albany

## Overview

The Dinner at the Albany is the climactic confrontation of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. After escaping the siege of his Down Street flat and finding sanctuary at the Embassy, [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] arranges a direct meeting with [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] at Lumley's chambers in the Albany, the prestigious Piccadilly residence.

## Context

Leithen, having secured his position at the Embassy and dispatched his evidence to Scotland Yard, sent a telegram to Lumley at the Albany "saying that I proposed to call upon him that evening at 8 sharp, and asking him to receive me" (PN 1030). Lumley replied promptly, aware of all Leithen's movements: "The telegram was directed to me at the Embassy, though I had put no address on the one I sent. Lumley of course knew all my movements" (PN 1037).

The Albany setting is richly described: "You know those rococo, late Georgian Albany rooms, large, square, clumsily corniced. Lumley's was lined with books, which I saw at a glance were of a different type from those in his working library at his country house. This was the collection of a bibliophile, and in the light of the summer evening the rows of tall volumes in vellum and morocco lined the walls like some rich tapestry" (PN 1050--1051).

## Significance

The dinner is the intellectual and psychological climax of the novel. Leithen opens with the declaration: "Let us be quite frank with each other, Mr. Lumley. There is really very little time to waste" (PN 1069). He reveals that Lumley had stolen and read the statement Chapman was carrying, that Pitt-Heron was on his way home under protection, and that Lumley's agent Saronov had confessed.

Leithen delivers his ultimatum with precision: "I am not in a position to reveal the full extent of your misdeeds. Of the Powerhouse and its doings I can only guess. But Pitt-Heron is on his way home, and he will be carefully safeguarded on that journey. Your creature, Saronov, has confessed. We shall know more very soon, and meantime I have clear evidence which implicates you in a conspiracy to murder" (PN 1072--1073).

Despite being cornered, Lumley maintains his composure. The scene is marked by its civilized veneer -- the fine books, the summer evening light, the polite exchange -- masking a deadly struggle. Lumley's reaction foreshadows his next move: flight. Leithen later wonders "if Lumley was on board or if he had taken one of the other ways of which he had spoken" (PN 1165), referring to the Continental express Lumley had mentioned as his possible escape routes.

The confrontation ends not with violence but with words, establishing Leithen's moral victory. Lumley's death from heart failure the following night makes this dinner their final encounter and the effective end of the Powerhouse.
