# Blackheath

Blackheath is a neighbourhood in South London that plays a significant role in [[edward-leithen|Leithen]]'s investigation into Charles Pitt-Heron's disappearance. Two key properties are located there: the villa of [[julius-pavia|Julius Pavia]], a retired East India merchant, and a secondary house belonging to [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]].

## Pavia's Villa

Leithen first encounters Blackheath in connection with a motor accident case. The vehicle involved belonged to "a Mr. Julius Pavia, a retired East India merchant, who lived in a large villa in the neighbourhood of Blackheath" [002-002]. The day after visiting the villa for the court case, Leithen observes that he seemed to keep encountering related names, deepening his suspicion of connections between the parties [002-007].

## Lumley's House

[[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] maintained a house at Blackheath in addition to his flat at [[the-albany|the Albany]]. The art critic Jenkinson describes it: "he has a house at Blackheath which is stuffed from cellar to garret" [002-027]. The house contained an extensive collection of artworks and treasures, far more like a dealer's shop than a dwelling.

## Investigation

During his inquiries, Leithen finds evidence on "the floor of the Blackheath house" [004-005] that connects the location to the wider conspiracy. The butler Tuke, who served at the Blackheath property, becomes a key figure in Leithen's investigation — described as "my super-butler of Blackheath, who passed under the name of Tuke" [004-035].
