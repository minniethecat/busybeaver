# The County Court

## Overview

The County Court was the lowest tier of the English civil justice system in the Edwardian era, handling small-value claims and minor disputes. Unlike the High Court or Court of Appeal, County Courts sat in towns and suburbs throughout the country and were designed to provide accessible justice for ordinary litigants.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The County Court is the setting for one of the novel's crucial early scenes. On "the 21st of May," [[edward-leithen]] takes a case in a South London County Court -- "an ordinary taxicab accident" involving a car belonging to a Mr. Julius Pavia and driven by his butler, Tuke (PN 165). Leithen normally would not take such a minor case, but he steps in when "the regular county-court junior was ill in bed" (PN 165). During the hearing, Leithen develops "a most intense and unreasoning dislike" for the "bland butler" Tuke, who answers all questions "with steady courtesy" (PN 171). This seemingly trivial encounter proves to be Leithen's first brush with the Powerhouse network -- Tuke is later revealed to be an agent of [[andrew-lumley]], sent to track [[charles-pitt-heron]] to Bokhara (PN 513).

Leithen later refers to the man he met "in the County Court" when deducing the identity of Lumley's operative pursuing Pitt-Heron in Central Asia (PN 445).

## Historical Context

County Courts were established in 1846 to provide a cheap and accessible forum for civil disputes. By the early 20th century there were some 500 County Courts across England and Wales. For a barrister of Leithen's standing, appearing in a County Court was unusual -- he normally practised in the High Court and Court of Appeal -- which is why his attendance required the special circumstance of the regular junior's illness.
