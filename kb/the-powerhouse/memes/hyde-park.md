# Hyde Park

Hyde Park appears at several points in the Powerhouse narrative, functioning as both a casual London landmark and a critical terrain in [[edward-leithen|Leithen]]'s flight from the conspiracy.

## A Walk Before Dinner

Early in the story, Leithen mentions Hyde Park in passing while describing his walk from [[down-street|Down Street]] to [[portman-square|Portman Square]]: "I told myself I would be none the worse of a walk in the Park before dinner" [001-043]. At this stage, the Park is merely a pleasant feature of a London evening, untainted by the danger to come.

## Chapman's Politics

The Park also features in [[chapman|Chapman]]'s political reminiscences. The Labour MP recalls his activist days: "Well I mind the day when I was hanging on to the Board's coattails in Hyde Park to keep it from talking treason" [005-005]. The reference locates Chapman's political origins in the tradition of open-air oratory for which Hyde Park was famous, and contrasts his radical past with his current Parliamentary role.

## The Crossing

Hyde Park's most significant appearance occurs during Leithen's desperate attempt to reach the [[belgrave-square|Embassy in Belgrave Square]]. Escaping from Bayswater with the Powerhouse network in pursuit, Leithen makes a calculated decision: "My reason was that the neighbourhood of Hyde Park Corner was at that time of day certain to be pretty well crowded, and I felt more security in a throng than in the empty streets of Kensington" [007-029].

He crosses the riding-track and the open space where Sunday demonstrations are held, passing nurses with perambulators, children at play, and exercised dogs. At Grosvenor Gate, he pauses to chat with acquaintances sitting on green chairs, attempting to maintain the appearance of normality even as the net closes around him. The crossing of Hyde Park marks the transition from the relative safety of west London to the deadly peril of [[grosvenor-place|Grosvenor Place]] and the final sprint to the Embassy.
