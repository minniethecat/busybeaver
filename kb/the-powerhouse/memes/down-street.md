# Down Street

Down Street is a short street in Mayfair, London, where Sir Edward Leithen maintains his bachelor flat. Throughout *The Powerhouse*, the flat serves as Leithen's base of operations — a place of planning, refuge, and ultimately siege. It is from Down Street that Leithen coordinates his investigation into Lumley's conspiracy.

## Description

Leithen's Down Street flat is a modest bachelor establishment appropriate to a rising barrister and Member of Parliament. It is positioned near Portman Square and within walking distance of the House of Commons (001-043). The flat has a telephone — a crucial detail in the pre-war setting — which Leithen uses to coordinate with his allies until the Powerhouse cuts the line (007-018). The flat's street-level entrance makes it accessible but also vulnerable, a fact that becomes apparent when Lumley's agents begin surveilling it.

## Role in the Novel

Down Street is Leithen's command post. He returns there after each development in the case — to study maps of Bokhara, to receive reports, and to plan his next moves. After Tommy Deloraine joins him, "we returned to the Down Street flat," and the sight of "his burly figure" in those familiar rooms provides a moment of reassurance amid the growing danger (005-017).

The flat's vulnerability becomes evident as the Powerhouse closes in. Leithen notices "a man at the corner of Down Street who seemed to peer curiously" at his doorway — the first sign that Lumley's agents have located his residence (007-018). His telephone is subsequently cut, isolating him from Macgillivray and Scotland Yard at a critical moment (007-018).

The Down Street flat represents the boundary between Leithen's ordinary life and the extraordinary danger he has stumbled into. It is a place of domestic routine — "I lived at the time in Down Street" — that becomes a forward operating base in a private war (001-043).

## Real-World Context

Down Street is a real street in Mayfair, London W1, running from Piccadilly to the southern edge of the Grosvenor Estate. It is a short, narrow street of predominantly Georgian and early Victorian terraced houses. At the time of the novel (c. 1913), the area was a fashionable residential district for wealthy professionals and minor aristocracy — exactly the kind of address a successful young barrister and MP like Leithen would maintain. Notably, Down Street has its own disused London Underground station (opened 1907, closed 1932), which would have been operational during the novel's timeframe.
