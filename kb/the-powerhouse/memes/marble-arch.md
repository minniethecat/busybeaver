# Marble Arch

## Overview

Marble Arch is a white Carrara marble monument at the junction of [[oxford-street]], [[park-lane]], and [[edgeware-road]], near the northeast corner of [[hyde-park]]. Designed by John Nash in 1827 as the ceremonial gateway to Buckingham Palace, it was relocated to its present site in 1851. In *The Powerhouse*, the Marble Arch Tube station is the setting for Leithen's ingenious counter-surveillance operation against [[andrew-lumley]]'s operatives.

## In the Novel

The Marble Arch sequence occurs in Chapter V, during the period when Leithen has become aware that he is being followed. Having noticed that "a certain carriage in the train which left Marble Arch about 9:30 stopped exactly opposite the exit at the Chancery Lane Station," Leithen devises a plan to catch his shadow:

> "I walked up Park Lane, for it seemed to me that by taking the Tube at the Marble Arch Station I could bring matters to the proof. ... I performed this manoeuvre with success, caught the early lift, reached the street and took cover behind a pillar-box from which I could watch the exit of passengers from the stairs." (Ch. V, PN 5)

The plan works perfectly. Leithen's pursuer, a "breathless gentleman," emerges from the station expecting to find his quarry on the lift, only to be observed from hiding by the very man he was tracking. This moment is a turning point in the novel: it is the first time Leithen, the passive object of surveillance, becomes the active agent of counter-surveillance. The psychological shift is significant -- having confirmed that "the espionage was no figment of my brain," Leithen can no longer dismiss his situation as paranoia.

The choice of Marble Arch as the Tube station for this operation is apt. The Central London Railway (today's Central line) station, opened in 1900, was a busy transport hub. Its layout -- with lifts serving different platforms and passages connecting to street level -- provided exactly the kind of architectural complexity a clever passenger could exploit.

Following this successful manoeuvre, Leithen walks to his chambers determined but uneasy: "I would have given a year's income to be honestly quit of it, but there seemed to be no way of escape. ... I was beginning to realize that I was very obstinate."

## Historical Context

Marble Arch was commissioned by George IV as the state entrance to the newly expanded Buckingham Palace. Completed in 1833, it was found to be too narrow for royal coaches and was moved to its present site at Cumberland Gate in 1851. By the Edwardian period, it had become a familiar London landmark, standing at the busy junction of Oxford Street and Park Lane, adjacent to the Speakers' Corner area of Hyde Park where Sunday demonstrations were held. The Marble Arch Tube station, on the Central London Railway, had opened in 1900 as part of London's expanding underground network.
