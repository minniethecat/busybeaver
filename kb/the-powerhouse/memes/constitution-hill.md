# Constitution Hill

## Overview

Constitution Hill is a road in central London, running along the southern boundary of Green Park and the northern edge of the gardens of Buckingham Palace. It connects Hyde Park Corner to The Mall and was a popular route for pedestrians and carriages. In *The Powerhouse*, it marks Leithen's return to the world of normality.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Constitution Hill appears in Chapter VIII as Leithen walks from the Embassy in Belgrave Square to his climactic meeting with Lumley at the Albany. After leaving the Embassy, he describes his route: "I retraced my steps along the route which for me that afternoon had been so full of tremors ... at the top of Constitution Hill I struck into the Green Park and kept to the grass till I emerged into Piccadilly, opposite Devonshire House" (PN 1047). Constitution Hill is the turning point -- the place where Leithen leaves the streets that had been "so full of tremors" and steps onto the grass of the park, walking openly and confidently. The contrast with his earlier flight could not be sharper: then he ran for his life; now he strolls, meeting acquaintances and exchanging greetings. Constitution Hill marks the boundary between hunted and hunter.

## Historical Context

Constitution Hill is named after the "constitutional" walks that George II was said to take along the route, though the name may be ironic -- it was also the site of three assassination attempts on Queen Victoria in the 1840s. The road forms part of the Royal Parks' grand landscape design, connecting Hyde Park Corner to Buckingham Palace. In the Edwardian period, it was a popular promenade for Londoners, offering views of the Palace gardens and Green Park.
