# The Wedgwood Plaques Discovery

## Overview

The Wedgwood Plaques Discovery is an important rising action event in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*, in which [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] learns through the art critic Jenkinson that [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] has recently purchased a set of [[wedgwood-plaques|Wedgwood plaques]], leading directly to the investigation at the White Lodge.

## Context

After Leithen's curiosity was aroused by the [[the-county-court-case|County Court case]] and the mention of Julius Pavia, he sought information about Lumley from everyone he knew. His breakthrough came when "lunching at the club, I ran against Jenkinson, the art critic" (PN 197). Leithen describes Jenkinson as an expert on Wedgwood who had "once or twice been allowed to look at his things for books he had been writing" (PN 209).

Jenkinson revealed that Lumley had recently purchased a set of Wedgwood plaques from Lutrin, a Piccadilly dealer. The discovery was layered: Jenkinson had never actually met Lumley, who "always bought through agents" (PN 209), adding to the mystery. The art critic lamented that Lumley collected treasures only to "fill half a dozen houses with priceless treasures, and then die, and the whole show will be sold at auction and the best things carried off to America" (PN 209).

The critical piece of information was that "Mr. Lumley apparently might be willing to resell the Wedgwood plaques if he got a fair offer," and Jenkinson was going that very afternoon to inspect them (PN 211). Leithen, "having nothing to do," accepted Jenkinson's invitation to accompany him (PN 211).

## Significance

This discovery is the narrative hinge that transforms the story from background inquiry to active investigation. The Wedgwood plaques provided a legitimate reason to enter Lumley's domain -- the White Lodge at Blackheath -- under the cover of an art appraisal. Jenkinson's presence lent credibility to the visit, while Leithen used the opportunity to observe Lumley's operation at close range.

The discovery also illuminates Lumley's methodology. His use of agents and intermediaries for purchases, the dispersal of his collections across multiple properties, and his operation under the alias of Julius Pavia all reveal the compartmentalized, intelligence-like structure of his activities. Leithen reflected that "less and less did I believe in the existence of the retired East Indian merchant" (PN 234).

Crucially, it was during this inspection visit that Leithen discovered the coded telegram from Vienna ("Suivez a Bokhare Saronov") concealed in the packing materials (PN 239). The Wedgwood plaques discovery thus provided both the venue and the opportunity for the most important piece of evidence in the investigation.
