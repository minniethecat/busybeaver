# Pitt-Heron's Disappearance

The inciting event of the novel, occurring off-stage before the narrative begins. Charles Pitt-Heron — wealthy, married, and increasingly strange in the months preceding — abruptly packs a kit-bag, draws a large sum in gold from his bank, and vanishes, leaving no word for his wife or friends. The discovery of his flight, as reported by [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] to [[edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]] in [[ch01]], sets in motion the investigation that will ultimately expose the [[the-powerhouse|Powerhouse]].

## The Disappearance

> "Charles Pitt-Heron," said Tommy, "is blowing up for a most thundering mess." (001-017)

On a spring afternoon in early May, Tommy Deloraine waylays Leithen outside the House of Commons with a startling report: their old Oxford friend Charles Pitt-Heron has vanished. The details, as Tommy relays them, are disturbing in their specificity:

> "Bolted without a word to a mortal soul. He told Ethel he would be home for luncheon yesterday, and never came. His man knew nothing about him, hadn't packed for him, or anything; but he found he had stuffed some things into a kit-bag and gone out by the back through the mews." (001-024)

The flight was not impulsive but planned: Pitt-Heron had drawn "a biggish sum in gold from the bank" (001-024) and obtained a high-powered passport from the Russian Embassy, arranging passage to Moscow via a clerk named Konalevsky (001-025). The destination — Moscow, then Bokhara — places him in Central Asia, as far from London and its circuits of civilisation as the geography of 1913 permits.

## Precipitating Factors

The disappearance did not come from nowhere. In the months before his flight, Pitt-Heron had been undergoing a marked transformation:

| Symptom | Observation | PN |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Social withdrawal | Stopped travelling, stopped writing, saw few friends | 001-016 |
| "Queerness" | "All last year he has been getting queerer" — Tommy | 001-018 |
| Scientific obsession | Built a laboratory in his billiard-room, worked half the night | 001-020 |
| Foreign associates | "Chinese and Turks, and long-haired chaps from Russia, and fat Germans" visiting his home | 001-020 |
| Secretiveness | "He won't answer a plain question or look you straight in the face" | 001-020 |

> "He has got a laboratory at the back of the house — used to be the billiard-room — where he works away half the night. And Lord! The crew you meet there! Every kind of heathen — Chinese and Turks, and long-haired chaps from Russia, and fat Germans." (001-020)

This constellation of symptoms — secrecy, foreign contacts, obsessive scientific work — forms the classic profile of a man being drawn into a conspiracy, whether as recruit, collaborator, or victim. Tommy's diagnosis is characteristically blunt: "He's got into a queer set, and there's no knowing what mischief he's up to" (001-029).

## Key Scene Sequence

1. **The Report** (001-014–001-033): Tommy Deloraine informs Leithen of Pitt-Heron's disappearance outside the House of Commons, detailing the circumstances and his intention to pursue him to Moscow
2. **The Russian Clue** (001-024–001-025): Tommy traces Pitt-Heron's movements to the Russian Embassy, discovering the passport arranged by Konalevsky
3. **Leithen's Reluctance** (001-030–001-033): Leithen, sceptical of Tommy's "heroics" and dismissive of Pitt-Heron's importance, resists involvement — a reluctance that will prove temporary
4. **The Financial Connection** (002-008–002-013): Leithen subsequently discovers Pitt-Heron's business entanglement with Julius Pavia, suggesting the disappearance has a financial dimension
5. **The Lumley Link** (004-040–004-041): Documents left by Pitt-Heron before his flight reveal his connection to [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] and the organisation known as the Powerhouse

## Consequences

The disappearance functions as the narrative's prime mover:

| Consequence | Development |
|-------------|------------|
| Leithen's investigation | The disappearance compels Leithen — initially reluctant — into the role of investigator |
| Exposure of the Powerhouse | Tracing Pitt-Heron's connections leads directly to Lumley's conspiracy |
| The conspiracy to murder | Pitt-Heron's flight reveals Lumley's willingness to eliminate those who threaten his organisation |
| Ethel's distress | Pitt-Heron's wife becomes a figure of pathos, anchoring the conspiracy's human cost |
| Tommy's involvement | The disappearance transforms Tommy from concerned friend to active participant in the investigation |

## Thematic Significance

Pitt-Heron's disappearance illustrates the novel's central anxiety: that the protections of civilisation are contingent and fragile. Pitt-Heron is not a criminal; he is a gentleman of wealth and standing who has somehow stepped outside the [[the-compact|compact]]. Whether by seduction or coercion, he has been removed from the network of obligations and relationships that constitute civilised life.

The destination — Bokhara — is not incidental. It represents the geographical correlative of Lumley's philosophical project: a place beyond the reach of English law, English custom, and English assumptions about how the world works. Pitt-Heron's flight to Bokhara is, in symbolic terms, a flight from civilisation itself.

Leithen's initial scepticism — "I could not see the reason for these heroics" (001-030) — is the novel's own starting position, the rational scepticism that the conspiracy will systematically dismantle. The disappearance, in other words, is not merely the plot's inciting incident; it is the novel's first lesson in the insufficiency of common-sense explanations.
