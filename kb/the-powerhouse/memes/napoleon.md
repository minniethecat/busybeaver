# Napoleon

The figure of Napoleon Bonaparte haunts the margins of *The Powerhouse* as the ultimate benchmark of worldly power. Andrew Lumley invokes Napoleon twice — first to deny that the Powerhouse needs one, then at the novel's climax to claim that he has surpassed one. This progression reveals the architecture of Lumley's megalomania: the man who disclaimed Napoleonic ambition has, in his own mind, equalled or exceeded it.

## Definition

In the novel's symbolic economy, Napoleon represents the apotheosis of the individual will — a single man who reshaped the political order of Europe through sheer force of personality and strategic genius. Lumley uses Napoleon as a measuring stick: he measures his own power against the Emperor's, measuring the scope of his hidden influence against the most visible conqueror in modern memory.

Lumley's initial argument is seductively modest: "you do not want a Napoleon. All that is needed is direction, which could be given by men of far lower gifts than a Bonaparte. In a word, you want a powerhouse" (003-058). Napoleon is invoked only to be dismissed as unnecessary — the Powerhouse allegedly needs no towering genius, only efficient organisation.

## Origin in the Text

The first reference occurs during the dinner conversation at [[andrew-lumley|Lumley]]'s house in [[chapter-iii]]. Leithen objects that "Mere destruction is too barren a creed to inspire a new Napoleon" (003-058). Lumley deftly sidesteps: destruction is not the aim, and a Napoleon is not required. This is the moment when the metaphor of the Powerhouse is first introduced — the organisation replaces the great man.

The second and decisive reference comes in [[chapter-viii]], during the climactic confrontation at [[the-albany|the Albany]]. Lumley, cornered by Leithen's evidence, makes the extraordinary claim: "No man since Napoleon has tasted such power. I may be willing to end it" (008-022). This is no longer a denial of Napoleonic ambition — it is a claim of Napoleonic achievement. Having spent the novel appearing to disclaim the role, Lumley now asserts that he has filled it.

## Key Passages

> "Mere destruction is too barren a creed to inspire a new Napoleon, and you can do with nothing short of one." (003-058)

> "It would scarcely be destruction," he replied gently. "Let us call it iconoclasm, the swallowing of formulas... And you do not want a Napoleon. All that is needed is direction, which could be given by men of far lower gifts than a Bonaparte. In a word, you want a powerhouse, and then the age of miracles will begin." (003-058)

> "No man since Napoleon has tasted such power. I may be willing to end it." (008-022)

## Epilogue

The novel's final paragraph returns to the Napoleon comparison with a crucial difference. After Lumley's death, Leithen reflects that "He was a Napoleon who left no Marshals behind him" (009-029). The image is exact: Lumley had Napoleonic ambition and Napoleonic reach, but his organisation depended entirely on himself. Without him, "the vast subterranean machine which he had controlled... was so much old iron" (009-029). The comparison is both flattering and devastating — Lumley equalled Napoleon in power but failed utterly in legacy.
