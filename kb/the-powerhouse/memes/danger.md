# Danger

The objective correlative of fear — the real, external threat that Leithen faces, as distinct from his subjective emotional response. Throughout the novel, the danger is genuine and escalating, grounded in the Powerhouse's demonstrated capacity for violence and its willingness to use it.

## Definition

Danger in *The Powerhouse* is not speculative but actual. By the middle of the novel, Leithen has accumulated concrete evidence that the Powerhouse will kill to protect itself: there is "clear evidence which implicates you in a conspiracy to murder" (008-015). The danger is not that something *might* happen but that something *has* happened and will happen again.

This distinguishes danger from the more abstract threat described in Lumley's philosophical monologue. Lumley speaks of civilisational collapse — a theoretical danger. Leithen faces the immediate danger of a specific organisation that has "declared war on me" (006-081). The novel's thriller structure depends on the progressive revelation that the philosophical danger and the physical danger are the same thing.

## Origin in the Text

Danger escalates through the novel. In [[ch03]], it is purely intellectual — unsettling ideas voiced over dinner. By [[ch05]], it has become practical — Leithen acquires a pistol. By [[ch07]], it is existential — he is hunted through London, aware that "an accident and a bogus ambulance" could make him disappear (007-033). The escalation is methodical, mirroring the Powerhouse's own methodical nature.

## Key Passages

> "Now I saw how thin is the protection of civilisation. An accident and a bogus ambulance — and a man might vanish as if the earth had swallowed him." (007-033)

> "I have clear evidence which implicates you in a conspiracy to murder." (008-015)

## Analysis

Buchan's treatment of danger is characteristically understated. Leithen never panics; he assesses. The danger is conveyed through precise observation rather than melodramatic description — the watcher on the street, the car that follows too closely, the door that should be locked but isn't. This restraint makes the danger more credible: it feels like something that could happen to anyone, not just to thriller heroes.
