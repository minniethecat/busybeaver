# The Confrontation at the Powerhouse

The climactic scene of *The Powerhouse*, occurring in [[ch08]] (008-001–008-025). Having survived the Powerhouse's campaign of surveillance and intimidation, [[edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]] arrives at [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s Albany chambers for a direct confrontation. What follows is not a physical battle but a duel of wits — a barrister's cross-examination deployed as weapon, in which Leithen's understanding of Lumley's psychology proves more decisive than his evidence.

## Setting

The confrontation takes place in Lumley's rooms at the Albany, the exclusive Piccadilly apartment complex whose residents included Byron, Macaulay, and Gladstone. The setting is deliberate: Lumley's chambers are "lined with books" of "a different type from those in his working library at his country house" — "the collection of a bibliophile," tall volumes in vellum and morocco that line the walls "like some rich tapestry" (008-004). This is Lumley's public face: the cultivated gentleman, the eminent diplomat, the friend of Viceroys and Cabinet Ministers.

> Again I had the old feeling of incredulity. It was the Lumley I had met two nights before at dinner, the friend of Viceroys and Cabinet Ministers. It was hard to connect him with Antioch Street or the red-haired footman with a pistol. Or with Tuke? Yes, I decided, Tuke fitted into the frame. Both were brains cut loose from the decencies that make life possible. (008-005)

The disjunction between setting and substance — between the elegant Albany rooms and the conspiracy they conceal — is the scene's dominant visual irony.

## The Duel

Leithen arrives at eight o'clock, declines Lumley's offer of dinner, and opens with a declaration of purpose:

> "I prefer to see the master rather than the subordinates who have been infesting my life during the past week." (008-009)

What follows is a three-movement verbal duel:

### Movement 1: The Evidence (008-013–008-017)

Leithen presents his case: [[charles-pitt-heron|Pitt-Heron]] is on his way home, Lumley's agent Saronov has confessed, and Leithen possesses "clear evidence which implicates you in a conspiracy to murder" (008-015). Lumley counters with the lawyer's objection — "what seems good evidence on paper is often feeble enough in Court" (008-017) — and the threat of unlimited legal resources.

### Movement 2: The Psychological Portrait (008-018–008-023)

This is the scene's decisive turn. Leithen does not rest on his evidence; he deploys his understanding of Lumley's character. The argument is not that Lumley will be convicted, but that he will be exposed:

> "From the day you leave the Court you will be a suspected man. The police of all Europe will be on your trail. You have been highly successful in the past, and why? Because you have been above suspicion, an honourable and distinguished gentleman, belonging to the best clubs, counting as your acquaintances the flower of our society. Now you will be a suspect, a man with a past, a centre of strange stories." (008-018)

The portrait that follows is the novel's psychological climax:

> "You are an artist in crime. You are not the common cutthroat who acts out of passion or greed. No, I think you are something subtler than that. You love power, hidden power. You flatter your vanity by despising mankind and making them your tools. You scorn the smattering of inaccuracies which passes for human knowledge, and I will not venture to say you are wrong. Therefore you use your brains to frustrate it. Unhappily the life of millions is built on that smattering, so you are a foe to society. But there would be no flavour in controlling subterranean things if you were yourself a mole working in the dark. To get the full flavour, the irony of it all, you must live in the light." (008-021)

This is Leithen's masterstroke. He has understood that Lumley's weakness is not his vulnerability to the law but his vanity — his need to operate *within* the society he despises, to taste the irony of his double life. Exposure would not merely threaten his freedom; it would destroy the very condition that makes his conspiracy satisfying.

### Movement 3: The Capitulation (008-024–008-025)

Lumley's response is physical, not verbal — "a sudden, odd, nervous movement, pushing his glasses close back upon his eyes" (008-024). His counter-threat — "Do you think you can play against me without suffering desperate penalties?" (008-025) — is the reflex of a man who knows he has already lost.

## Outcome

The confrontation does not end with Lumley's arrest or the Powerhouse's destruction. Leithen's victory is more limited and more realistic: Lumley flees, the immediate conspiracy is neutralised, and the threat of murder is lifted. The Powerhouse as an organisation survives — too large, too diffuse, too well-hidden to be destroyed by a single exposure. But its leader is neutralised, and the novel's immediate stakes are resolved.

## Thematic Significance

The confrontation scene is the novel's argument made flesh: that civilisation's weapons — law, evidence, rational argument, psychological insight — can be effective even against those who have rejected civilisation's premises. Leithen does not defeat Lumley with violence, cunning, or luck, but with the tools of his profession: meticulous investigation, logical reasoning, and the barrister's gift for constructing a devastating portrait from accumulated facts.

The scene also crystallises the novel's understanding of power. Lumley's power is hidden, organisational, and parasitic on the society he despises. Leithen's power is explicit, individual, and grounded in his profession and his character. The confrontation tests which kind of power is more durable — and Leithen wins not by matching Lumley's methods but by remaining, stubbornly, a barrister.

> "The end has come — the utter, final and absolute end." (008-023)
