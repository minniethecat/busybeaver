# Farnham

## Overview

Farnham is a historic market town in Surrey, approximately 35 miles southwest of London. In *The Powerhouse*, it serves as the destination toward which [[edward-leithen]] and his chauffeur [[stagg]] are driving when their automobile collides with a carrier's cart, leading to one of the novel's most pivotal encounters.

## In the Novel

The Farnham incident occurs in Chapter IV ("I Follow the Trail of the Super-Butler"). Leithen, eager to return to London by Monday afternoon, decides to push through a long day of driving on Sunday. He resolves to sleep at a friend's house near Farnham rather than stop earlier.

The accident takes place around half-past eight in the evening, on narrow roads in the neighbourhood of Wolmer Forest. Leithen's car rounds a sharp corner and runs "full into the tail of a heavy carrier's cart." Though no one is seriously hurt -- [[stagg]] receives a scratch on his cheek and Leithen bruises his shoulder -- the steering gear is damaged and the car cannot continue (Ch. IV, PN 2).

With Stagg dispatched to find horses capable of towing the car to Farnham for repairs, Leithen is left to find shelter for the night. He learns that "there was no inn where I could stay within eight miles" and resolves to seek help at a nearby gentleman's house. This decision leads him to [[high-ashes]], the country residence of [[andrew-lumley]], where he spends an evening in conversation with the very man who is the secret mastermind behind the [[the-powerhouse-org|Powerhouse]] -- though Leithen does not yet know it.

The Farnham car breakdown is thus a crucial plot device: an apparent accident of travel that places the hunter at the mercy of his quarry, heightening the dramatic irony of the story.

## Historical Context

Farnham in the Edwardian era was a prosperous market town serving the agricultural communities of western Surrey. Its location on the road from London to Winchester made it a natural stopping point for motorists. The surrounding landscape -- part of the Surrey Hills and bordering Wolmer Forest -- was known for its heathland, gorse, and winding country roads similar to those described by Buchan. The town's proximity to the army camps at Aldershot also made it familiar to many readers of the period.
