# Rat Lane

## Overview

Rat Lane is a London thoroughfare referenced in *The Powerhouse* as a byword for violence and criminality. It is invoked by [[bill-docker]], the reformed burglar, to convey the depth of his fear of the [[the-powerhouse-org|Powerhouse]]'s operatives.

## In the Novel

The reference to Rat Lane occurs in Chapter VIII, during Leithen's tense interview with Bill Docker at his lodgings. Leithen has sought out Docker, whom he once successfully defended in a burglary case, to recruit him as an ally against [[andrew-lumley]]'s organisation.

When Leithen suggests that Docker is afraid of Lumley's men, Docker responds with chilling candour:

> "Yuss. I'm afraid. Black afraid. So would you be if you knew the gents. I'd rather take on the whole Rat Lane crowd -- you know them as I mean -- on a Saturday night, when they're out for business, than go back to my gents and say as 'ow I had shirked the job." (Ch. VIII, PN 8)

Docker elaborates: "Good Lord, they'd freeze the 'eart out of a bull-pup."

This comparison is significant because it establishes a hierarchy of menace. Docker, a hardened criminal familiar with the worst of London's underworld, considers the "Rat Lane crowd" -- presumably a gang of violent street toughs -- to be less terrifying than the gentlemen of the Powerhouse. The specific mention of "Saturday night, when they're out for business" evokes the regular rhythms of working-class violence: payday drinking, brawling, and general disorder. Yet even this rough world is, in Docker's estimation, child's play compared to Lumley's refined operatives, whose menace is psychological and existential rather than merely physical.

## Historical Context

"Rat Lane" appears in London street directories of the period, typically in working-class districts of the East End or South London. The name itself evokes the squalor and overcrowding of Victorian and Edwardian slum London -- places where rats, both literal and figurative, thrived. Rat Lane was the kind of street that Buchan's middle-class readers would have regarded with fascinated horror, a place synonymous with the "dangerous classes" who inhabited the criminal underworld.

Docker's Cockney dialect in the passage ("Yuss," "gents," "as 'ow," "'eart") marks him as a figure from this world, and his invocation of Rat Lane establishes his credentials as someone who knows real danger when he sees it.
