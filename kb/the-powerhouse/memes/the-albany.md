# The Albany

The Albany is a real and prestigious apartment complex in London's Piccadilly, built in the late 18th century. In *The Powerhouse*, it serves as Andrew Lumley's residence and, by extension, the operational headquarters of his international criminal network. It is within these "rococo, late Georgian" rooms that Lumley conducts his manipulation of world events.

## Description

The Albany rooms are described in vivid detail during Leithen's climactic visit: "large, square, clumsily furnished" in the late Georgian style, yet with an atmosphere of cultivated menace (008-004). The apartment complex, originally a grand townhouse converted to bachelor chambers, had been home to prominent politicians, writers, and aristocrats. Lumley's tenancy there — the "decorous bachelor of the Albany" — provides him with a veneer of respectability that masks his true activities (004-008).

The rooms contain Lumley's celebrated library, left largely intact after his departure: "very few of his things in the Albany except his books" (002-027). The library functions both as a scholarly front and as a repository of the specialized knowledge that fuels the Powerhouse's schemes.

## Role in the Novel

The Albany is the fixed point in Lumley's otherwise shadowy existence. It is where Leithen first investigates the philanthropist's identity, discovering that he "lived in the Albany, belonged to half a dozen clubs" (002-017). The address confers social legitimacy — a man at the Albany cannot easily be dismissed as a crank or criminal.

It is from the Albany that Lumley orchestrates the pursuit of Pitt-Heron and the efforts to neutralize Leithen. Leithen sends a telegram to Lumley at the Albany proposing a final meeting (007-067), and later reflects that he would "only be permitted to reach the Albany unmolested" — a sign that Lumley's agents control access to their master (007-070).

The novel's climax occurs at the Albany. Leithen arrives "as the clock was striking eight" for his confrontation with Lumley (008-003). The ensuing conversation — a battle of wits between the barrister and the criminal mastermind — takes place entirely within those Georgian rooms, culminating in Lumley's accidental death.

## Real-World Context

The Albany is a genuine London landmark, located just off Piccadilly near Burlington House (now the Royal Academy). Built in 1771–76 as Melbourne House and converted to bachelor apartments in 1802, it has housed numerous notable residents including Lord Byron, William Gladstone, Aldous Huxley, and Edward Heath. Its reputation as a discreet and prestigious address for wealthy single gentlemen makes it the perfect fictional residence for Lumley — a man who requires social camouflage for his criminal enterprises.
