# Tommy's Pursuit to Bokhara

## Overview

Tommy's Pursuit to Bokhara is the critical parallel action in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*, in which [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]], redirected by Leithen's intelligence, races from Bokhara into the Pamir Mountains to protect [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]] from Lumley's pursuing agent.

## Context

After Leithen discovered the Vienna telegram reading "Suivez a Bokhare Saronov" (PN 239) and identified [[tuke|Tuke the super-butler]] as the agent sent to Bokhara, he realized the urgency of warning Tommy. With Ethel Pitt-Heron's help, a telegraphic message redirected Tommy: "Go on to Bokhara. Have information you will meet him there" (PN 247).

The pursuit became a three-sided race. Pitt-Heron had left Bokhara "with a small caravan on June 2nd by the main road through the Hissar range" (PN 535). Tommy arrived on June 10th and "on the 12th had set off with two servants on the same trail" (PN 535). Leithen calculated that "Travelling the lighter of the two, he should have overtaken Pitt-Heron by the 15th at latest" (PN 535).

Meanwhile, Tuke -- whose real name was Routh, a former trade union official turned Lumley agent -- was also in pursuit. Leithen had traced Tuke's identity through Macgillivray at Scotland Yard, who described him as "a discredited genius" and "the very man for Lumley's schemes -- one who asked for nothing better than to use his brains outside the ring-fence of convention" (PN 525). Leithen reflected grimly: "Somewhere in the wastes of Turkestan the ex-trade-union official was in search of Pitt-Heron. I did not fancy that Mr. Tuke would be very squeamish" (PN 525).

## Significance

The pursuit embodies the novel's central conflict: individual courage and friendship against organized conspiracy. Tommy, the bored parliamentarian, becomes a man of decisive action in the wild Pamirs. His success in overtaking Pitt-Heron before Tuke could reach him was the crucial field victory that complemented Leithen's intellectual victory in London.

Leithen enlisted the help of his friend Felix at the Embassy to track the pursuit through diplomatic channels. Felix cabled the Military Governor at Bokhara and received confirmation that both Tommy and Pitt-Heron were on the trail through the Hissar range toward Gilgit and Kashmir, which Felix identified as "the likeliest route, if a man wanted to travel fast" (PN 475).

Tommy's success meant that by the time of the climactic [[the-dinner-at-the-albany|Dinner at the Albany]], Leithen could truthfully tell Lumley that "Pitt-Heron is on his way home, and he will be carefully safeguarded on that journey" (PN 1072). Lumley's death soon after made the pursuit's success permanent.
