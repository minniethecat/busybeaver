# Iconoclasm

## Overview

Iconoclasm, literally "the breaking of icons," is the term [[andrew-lumley]] uses to describe the Powerhouse's true purpose. When [[sir-edward-leithen]] suggests the movement is about "destruction," Lumley corrects him with characteristic precision: the goal is not destruction but iconoclasm, "the swallowing of formulas."

## In *The Powerhouse*

During their dinner conversation, Leithen presses Lumley on what the Powerhouse ultimately seeks. When Leithen says "It would scarcely be destruction," Lumley replies gently: "Let us call it iconoclasm, the swallowing of formulas, which has always had its full retinue of idealists" [008-032]. The distinction matters: Lumley does not see himself as a destroyer but as a liberator from rigid formulas and conventions that constrain human potential.

Lumley's iconoclasm is philosophical rather than merely violent. He argues that every age has its iconoclasts — those who challenge received wisdom and inherited structures — and that these figures have always been accompanied by idealists who believe in a better order. By framing the Powerhouse's work as iconoclasm, Lumley positions himself in a tradition of intellectual rebellion rather than criminal conspiracy. The "formulas" he wishes to swallow are the settled assumptions of civilisation itself.
