# Fountain Court

## Overview

Fountain Court is one of the quadrangles within the Temple, the historic legal precinct between Fleet Street and the Thames. Home to barristers' chambers for centuries, it provided a quiet, cloistered working environment for London's legal profession. In *The Powerhouse*, it is the workplace of the narrator, Sir Edward Leithen.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Fountain Court is the fixed point of Leithen's professional life throughout the novel. In Chapter I, we see Tommy Deloraine visiting "the dusty window in Fountain Court" that "lit up the dust and squalor of my working chambers" (PN 138). The contrast is striking: while Tommy prepares to ride into the wilds of Central Asia, Leithen's domain is "stuffy rooms" where he can "hardly see the sun for the cobwebs on these windows" (PN 140). Yet it is from these very chambers that Leithen orchestrates the entire counter-conspiracy -- interviewing witnesses, telephoning Scotland Yard, and coordinating with the Embassy. Fountain Court becomes a kind of command centre: the still point from which the strings of international intrigue are pulled.

## Historical Context

Fountain Court is one of the principal courts of the Middle Temple, named after the fountain that stands at its centre. The Temple itself dates back to the Knights Templar in the 12th century and has been home to English lawyers since the 14th century. By Buchan's time, barristers' chambers in the Temple were the traditional professional home of the English Bar, though dusty and inconvenient by modern standards. Buchan himself was a barrister of the Middle Temple before turning to writing and politics, lending authenticity to Leithen's professional environment.
