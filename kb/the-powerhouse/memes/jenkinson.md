# Jenkinson

Jenkinson is an art critic and a passionate collector of Old Wedgwood. He is described by [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] as the keenest of all Wedgwood connoisseurs -- a man who could "discourse for hours on the 'feel' of good jasper and the respective merits of blue and sage-green grounds" (002-005). His monomaniacal devotion to his specialty inadvertently provides Leithen with one of the most important leads in his investigation of [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]].

## Overview

Jenkinson is a fellow clubman of Leithen's and shares his interest in Old Wedgwood, a specialised collecting field where, as Leithen notes, "the few who do are apt to be monomaniacs" and "Wedgwoodites all know each other" (002-005). He is characterised by an enthusiasm that borders on the compulsive -- he babbles through luncheon about auction prices, badgers museum authorities to acquire pieces for the nation, and purrs "like a contented cat" when examining fine specimens with a magnifying glass.

## Role in the Novel

Jenkinson appears in [[0144-the-Powerhouse-30c98a18-ch007|Chapter II: I First Hear of Mr. Andrew Lumley]], where Leithen encounters him at the club and learns of Lumley through his description of the Wentworth sale. Jenkinson had attended the auction and was furious that the nation had not acquired a pair of magnificent Wedgwood plaques with a unique Flaxman design. These plaques had been purchased by [[lutrin|Lutrin]], an art dealer, on behalf of a private collector named Andrew Lumley.

Jenkinson describes Lumley as "a rich old buffer who locked up his things in cupboards and never let the public get a look at them" (002-005). He suspects that many of the best pieces at recent sales have gone to Lumley, meaning "they were put in cold storage for good." He has never met Lumley in person -- Lumley always buys through agents -- but he has been permitted access to Lumley's collections for research.

When Lutrin informs Jenkinson that Lumley might be willing to resell the plaques, Jenkinson invites Leithen to accompany him to Lumley's property at Blackheath, [[the-white-lodge|the White Lodge]]. This visit proves momentous: while Jenkinson is absorbed in examining the plaques with his magnifying glass, Leithen searches the room and discovers a coded telegram from Vienna reading "Suivez a Bokhare Saronov" -- the clue that confirms his suspicions about the Powerhouse conspiracy.

## Significance

Jenkinson is a classic Buchan minor character: a man whose harmless obsession becomes an instrument of larger events. His single-minded focus on Wedgwood blinds him to everything else in the room, allowing Leithen to conduct his investigation unnoticed. He represents the world of connoisseurship and cultural patriotism that Lumley's conspiracy parasitically exploits -- Lumley uses the art market as both a cover for his operations and a means of laundering his identity.

Jenkinson is also significant as one of the many unwitting informants whose casual remarks help Leithen piece together the conspiracy. He never learns the truth about Lumley, the White Lodge, or the telegram; his role in the story is entirely accidental, a function of his enthusiasm for old jasper.
