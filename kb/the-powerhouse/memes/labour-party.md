# Labour Party

The Labour Party in Edwardian Britain was a rising political force — founded in 1900 as the Labour Representation Committee, it had grown rapidly and by 1913 held over forty seats in the House of Commons. In *The Powerhouse*, the party is represented by a single but vividly drawn character: [[chapman|Chapman]], the MP whom [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] calls "Chipmunk."

## Role in the Novel

Chapman's party affiliation is central to his character and to his role in the plot. When [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] needs an ally, "my mind turned at once to Tommy's friend Chapman. I thought with comfort of the bluff independence of the Labour member. So that night at the House I hunted him out in the smoking-room" (005-007).

Chapman's political instincts shape his interpretation of the conspiracy. He sees it as a class conflict — "another case of the strife between Capital and Labour" (006-002) — with Tommy and Leithen "standing for Labour" and Lumley for the worst kind of capitalist conspiracy. This framing is both earnest and comic: Chapman refers to Tommy in public as "a gilded popinjay" (006-002) and to Leithen as "a viperous lackey of Capitalism" (006-002), yet he fights beside them with total loyalty.

Chapman's Labour identity gives him a distinctive perspective on the Routh case. As a union man himself, he knew Routh "at conferences and on platforms" (005-008) and his anger at Routh's betrayal of the movement is personal and political. When Leithen first mentions Routh's name, Chapman explodes: "There you are, you Tories. You can't fight fair. You hate the Unions, and you rake up any rotten old prejudice to discredit them" (005-007). This defensiveness reflects the embattled position of Labour in Edwardian politics, perpetually accused of class warfare and revolutionary intent.

After the adventure, Leithen jests: "I think I'll join the Labour Party. You can tell your fellows to send me their whips" (007-012). The joke acknowledges the political gap between them while affirming their personal bond — a bond forged not in ideology but in shared danger.

Chapman's Parliamentary identity also creates complications. His "resolution" to spy on Lumley "led to a complete disregard of his Parliamentary duties. Deputations of constituents waited for him in vain" (006-004). This is played for comedy — the Labour MP neglecting his constituents to chase a capitalist conspirator — but it also makes a serious point: the novel's secret war cannot be fought through Parliamentary channels.

At the novel's close, Chapman wants a public reckoning: "He wanted a public exposure of Lumley, a big trial, a general ferreting out of secret agents, the whole winding up with a speech in Parliament by himself on this last outrage of Capitalism" (009-064). Leithen dissuades him, and Chapman's silence is an act of discipline that contradicts his party's instinct for public confrontation.

## Historical Context

In 1913, the Labour Party was on the cusp of transformation. The Great War would bring Labour into coalition government and accelerate its displacement of the Liberal Party as the main opposition to the Conservatives. Buchan, a Conservative (he would later serve as a Unionist MP), treats Chapman with affectionate condescension — the bluff Yorkshireman, the romantic revolutionary — but also with genuine respect. Chapman is no fool; his instincts are sound, even when his methods are impulsive.

## Thematic Significance

Chapman's Labour affiliation represents the novel's most explicit engagement with class politics. The Powerhouse conspiracy transcends class: Lumley is a wealthy gentleman, Routh is a disgraced union official, and their network draws on "every kind of heathen — Chinese and Turks, and long-haired chaps from Russia, and fat Germans" (002-010). The novel's answer to class conflict is not political resolution but personal loyalty — the bond between a Tory barrister, an aristocratic adventurer, and a Labour MP who find common cause against a threat that belongs to no class.
