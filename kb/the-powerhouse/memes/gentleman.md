# Gentleman

The unspoken code that defines Sir Edward Leithen and his world. He is not just a barrister or a narrator; he is a *gentleman* — a term that in Buchan's Edwardian context carries a weight of social obligation, behavioural expectation, and moral assumption that the novel both relies upon and quietly examines.

## Definition

The gentlemanly code in *The Powerhouse* is defined more by what it excludes than by what it prescribes. A gentleman does not panic. He does not complain. He does not impose his troubles on others. He acts with "restraint" — Leithen's defining quality — and he takes responsibility for situations even when they are not of his making.

Leithen embodies the type: "the ordinary educated Englishman" whose sort "gravitates to the Bar" (003-033). He is competent without being expert, brave without being reckless, moral without being priggish. His gentlemanliness is both his greatest strength (it keeps him steady under pressure) and his greatest vulnerability (the Powerhouse does not play by gentlemanly rules).

## Origin in the Text

The code is present from the first page, embedded in Leithen's narrative voice — his tone of moderate, reasonable, slightly ironic observation. He describes himself as "the ordinary educated Englishman" (003-033) with neither false modesty nor pride. His actions throughout the novel are those of a gentleman: he investigates Pitt-Heron's disappearance out of loyalty to a friend; he pursues the Powerhouse out of a sense of obligation; he confronts danger with composure.

## Key Passages

## Analysis

The gentlemanly code is both a period piece and a structural element of the thriller genre. Buchan's gentleman-protagonist — competent, restrained, slightly amateur — would become a template for a century of British thriller heroes, from Ian Fleming's James Bond (a gentleman pretending to be a cad) to John le Carré's George Smiley (a gentleman disillusioned with the code but unable to escape it).

In the context of *The Powerhouse*, the code serves a specific narrative function: it is the set of rules that Leithen follows and that the Powerhouse does not. The dramatic tension comes not from a clash of equals but from an asymmetry — one side fights by the rules, the other does not.
