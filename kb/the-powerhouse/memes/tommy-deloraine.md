# Tommy Deloraine

Tommy Deloraine is Leithen's oldest friend and most energetic ally — but also his greatest liability. An MP with a country seat in Yorkshire, Tommy possesses what Leithen calls "incurable romanticism" (005-023): a love of adventure for its own sake that makes him invaluable as a companion and dangerous as an operative. His presence in the novel provides the warmth and humour that balance Leithen's cool, lawyerly detachment.

## Role in the Story

Deloraine enters the narrative on its very first page, walking out of the House of Commons with Leithen on the May afternoon when the whole affair begins. It is Tommy who first breaks the news of Pitt-Heron's disappearance, and Tommy whose impulsive loyalty makes him determined to do something about it.

> It all started one afternoon, early in May, when I came out of the House of Commons with Tommy Deloraine. I had got in by an accident at a by-election, when I was supposed to be fighting a forlorn hope, and as I was just beginning to be busy at the Bar I found my hands pretty full. (001-002)

His role in the investigation is that of the enthusiastic amateur: willing, energetic, and almost entirely lacking in subtlety. Leithen quickly realises that he cannot stop Tommy from getting involved, so he channels his friend's energies into tasks suited to his temperament — the legwork, the social contacts, the physical presence that Leithen the barrister cannot provide.

> I knew it was no use explaining that for him to spy on the Powerhouse was like an elephant stalking a gazelle. The only way was to appeal to his incurable romanticism. (005-023)

## Background

Deloraine belongs to the same world as Leithen and Pitt-Heron: public school, Oxford, a seat in Parliament, and the easy assumption that the world is fundamentally ordered and decent. Before succeeding to the family seat in Yorkshire, he lived the life of the younger son — sociable, athletic, unreflective. His friendship with Pitt-Heron was particularly close: "for about two years Tommy and he had been inseparable" (001-015).

Leithen's description of himself standing in contrast to Tommy is revealing:

> I was a peaceful, sedentary man, a lover of a quiet life, with no appetite for perils and commotions. But I was beginning to realize that I was very obstinate. (004-053)

This self-characterisation — contrasting his own "peaceful, sedentary" nature with Tommy's athletic restlessness — is partly ironic. Leithen is far more formidable than he admits. But the contrast is real: Tommy acts, Leithen thinks; Tommy plunges, Leithen calculates. Together they form the quintessential Buchan partnership — brains and brawn, caution and courage.

## Relationships

- **[[edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]]** — Best friend and investigative partner; their contrasting temperaments — Leithen the thinker, Deloraine the doer — complement each other in the classic Buchan mould
- **[[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]]** — His closest friend from Oxford; Tommy's fierce loyalty to the missing Pitt-Heron is the emotional engine of his involvement

## Notable Quotes

> Tommy sniffed the spring breeze like a supercilious stag. (001-003)

This small, vivid image — comic and affectionate — captures Buchan's attitude toward Deloraine. He is a figure of fun, but a beloved one; his stag-like posturing is endearing rather than ridiculous.

> He got wildly excited before I was half through with it. No doubt of the correctness of my evidence ever entered his head, for, like most of his party, he hated anarchism worse than capitalism, and the notion of a highly capitalised, highly scientific, highly undemocratic anarchism fairly revolted his soul. (005-011)

## Significance

Tommy Deloraine represents a distinctly Buchan-esque ideal: the gentleman-amateur whose virtues — courage, loyalty, physical energy — are sufficient equipment for confronting evil, even if his methods are crude by professional standards. He is the romantic hero in a novel that is otherwise deeply suspicious of romance: his "incurable romanticism" is both his defining trait and his structural weakness.

In the broader context of Buchan's work, Deloraine prefigures the more developed comic-adventurer figures of the Richard Hannay novels — men like Sandy Arbuthnot and John S. Blenkiron, whose eccentricities are inseparable from their effectiveness. Tommy is a sketch rather than a full portrait, but the sketch is vivid and affectionate: the friend you want beside you not because he is the most competent, but because he is the most loyal.

His presence also serves a tonal function. *The Powerhouse* is a dark novel — conspiracy, anarchy, the threat of civilisation's collapse — and Tommy's irrepressible enthusiasm provides the leavening humour that keeps the darkness from becoming oppressive. When Leithen achieves his "first and last score at the expense of Tommy Deloraine" (009-020) in the novel's final chapter, it is a small, human moment of triumph after the grand, impersonal struggle against the Powerhouse.
