# Civilisation

The master theme of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse* — not civilisation as a triumph of human progress, but as a precarious construct, "a thread, a sheet of glass" separating order from barbarism. The novel uses its thriller framework to explore how thin the protections of civilised life truly are.

## Definition

In the novel, civilisation is defined not by its achievements but by its vulnerabilities. Lumley, the novel's philosophical voice, argues that civilisation is a "conspiracy" — a "silent compact of comfortable folk to keep up pretences" (003-048). It is not held together by law, police, or armies, but by the "goodwill of civilisation" itself — a shared, unspoken agreement not to question the foundations (003-036). This makes it simultaneously powerful (because it is ubiquitous) and fragile (because it rests on nothing more solid than consensus).

Leithen, the narrator and a barrister, initially defends a more conventional view — that civilisation's foundations "grow daily firmer" (003-038) — but his encounter with the Powerhouse forces him to reconsider. By the novel's end, he reflects on "how thin is the protection of civilisation" (007-033).

## Origin in the Text

The concept is most fully articulated in [[ch03]], in the extended after-dinner conversation between Leithen and Andrew Lumley. Lumley systematically dismantles Leithen's complacent assumption that civilisation is solid, arguing that the division between civilisation and barbarism is "a thread, a sheet of glass. A touch here, a push there, and you bring back the reign of Saturn" (003-039).

The argument is given concrete form through the Powerhouse itself — an organisation that has learned civilisation's methods (discipline, organisation, knowledge) while rejecting its moral constraints. Lumley's point is that civilisation's tools can be wielded by its enemies: "civilisation will win until its enemies learn from it the importance of the machine" (003-067).

## Analysis

Buchan wrote *The Powerhouse* on the eve of the First World War (published 1916), and the novel's meditation on civilisation's fragility is inseparable from that historical moment. Lumley's argument — that the tools of modernity can be turned against modernity itself — prefigures the twentieth century's experience of total war, ideological extremism, and technological destruction.

The novel's thriller form is itself part of the argument. By placing a respectable barrister in a situation where the normal protections of law and society fail, Buchan makes the abstract philosophical debate visceral. Leithen's experience — being hunted through familiar London streets, unable to rely on police or law — enacts Lumley's thesis that civilisation is only as strong as the consensus that supports it.

The concept resonates beyond its Edwardian context. Lumley's description of "extra-social intelligences" who "distrust the machine" and seek to build a "counter-compact" (003-064, 003-067) anticipates twentieth-century anxieties about technocratic elites, international conspiracies, and the vulnerability of open societies to those who reject their premises.
