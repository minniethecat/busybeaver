# The Chippendale Chairs

## Overview

The Chippendale Chairs are fine eighteenth-century furniture pieces by the celebrated English cabinetmaker Thomas Chippendale, forming part of [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s extensive collection at the White Lodge in Blackheath. They appear in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse* as elements of the chaotic, treasure-stuffed interior that shrouds Lumley's criminal activities.

## In The Powerhouse

The chairs are mentioned during [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]]'s visit to the White Lodge with the art critic Jenkinson for the [[the-wedgwood-examination|Wedgwood examination]]. As Jenkinson examined the Wedgwood plaques, Leithen surveyed the room: "It was an untidy little room, full of fine Chinese porcelain in dusty glass cabinets, and in a corner stood piles of old Persian rugs" (PN 231).

The Chippendale chairs were part of this accumulation. The White Lodge was "stuffed from cellar to garret" with treasures (PN 215). Leithen noted that Jenkinson acted "like a dog in a bone-yard," ecstatic among the objects, while Leithen used the distraction to investigate the packing materials that yielded the crucial telegram (PN Ch. II).

The chairs, like the Wedgwood plaques and the Chinese porcelain, represent the highest achievements of English and continental decorative arts. Chippendale (1718--1779) was the most famous English furniture maker of the eighteenth century, and his name is synonymous with Georgian elegance. That such objects should be heaped carelessly in a suburban villa, treated as props rather than appreciated as art, signals the instrumental nature of Lumley's collecting.

## Analysis

The Chippendale chairs function as a symbol of the theme of deceptive appearances that runs throughout *The Powerhouse*. On the surface, the White Lodge is the home of a wealthy connoisseur, a retired East India merchant who has filled his house with beautiful things. In reality, it is the operational base of a criminal conspiracy, and the treasures are camouflage -- the props of a false identity.

The carelessness with which the objects are treated is revealing. "Dusty glass cabinets," "piles of old Persian rugs" tossed in corners, and packing materials left on the floor -- this is not the behavior of a genuine collector. A true connoisseur would display Chippendale chairs with pride, not leave them jumbled among unrelated treasures. The disorder suggests that Lumley valued these objects not for their beauty or craftsmanship but for what they signaled to visitors: wealth, taste, and the leisure to acquire indiscriminately.

The chairs also connect to the broader theme of English cultural heritage. Lumley's hoarding and Jenkinson's fear that the collections would be "carried off to America" (PN 209) reflect Edwardian anxieties about the dispersal of British patrimony. Yet the novel's deeper irony is that the greatest threat to English civilization comes not from foreign buyers but from the Englishman who has made civilization itself his enemy.
