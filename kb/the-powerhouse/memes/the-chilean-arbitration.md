# The Chilean Arbitration

## Overview

The Chilean Arbitration is the professional legal case that frames the closing passages of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. It was an international boundary dispute between Chile and a neighboring South American state, in which [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] served as junior counsel for the British Government.

## Context

The arbitration is mentioned at the very end of the novel, after the resolution of the Powerhouse conspiracy. Leithen describes it in the opening of the final section: "It was the year of the Chilean Arbitration, in which I held a junior brief for the British Government, and that and the late sitting of Parliament kept me in London after the end of the term" (PN 1194).

The case required painstaking cartographic work. Leithen was "engaged in the beastly job of comparing half a dozen maps of a despicable little bit of South American frontier" (PN 1196) when [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]], lean and sunburnt from his Central Asian expedition, unexpectedly walked into his Temple chambers.

Leithen was feeling the after-effects of the summer's excitements: "I had had a bad reaction from the excitements of the summer, and in these days I was feeling pretty well hipped and overdone" (PN 1194). The arbitration work thus provides the mundane professional reality against which the extraordinary events of the Powerhouse conspiracy are contrasted.

## Significance

The Chilean Arbitration serves several important narrative functions. First, it grounds the story in the world of international law and diplomacy, reinforcing Leithen's credibility as a narrator. The fact that he holds a government brief demonstrates his professional standing and the trust placed in him by the British establishment -- the very establishment Lumley sought to undermine.

Second, the juxtaposition of the tedious arbitration work with Tommy's dramatic return from Central Asia highlights the contrast between ordinary legal life and the extraordinary adventure Leithen has just survived. The "despicable little bit of South American frontier" stands in stark contrast to the vast wilderness of the Pamirs through which Tommy has traveled.

Third, the mention of Chile and South American interests resonates with Lumley's earlier discussion of how fragile international order could be disrupted. Lumley had argued that "one or two minute changes might sink Britain to the level of Ecuador" (PN 361), making the South American arbitration an ironic echo of his warnings about the instability of nations.
