# Super-Anarchy

The political philosophy articulated by Andrew Lumley in [[ch03]] — anarchy transformed by the methods of civilisation into something far more dangerous than the "foolishness which today we call nihilism or anarchy" (003-067). Super-anarchy is what happens when the enemies of civilisation learn to use its own tools against it.

## Definition

Lumley distinguishes super-anarchy from ordinary anarchism by its sophistication. Traditional anarchists are "a few illiterate bandits in a Paris slum" or "half a dozen crazy Russian *intellectuels* in Geneva" — they are easily contained because they lack organisation, resources, and discipline (003-067). Super-anarchy, by contrast, is the product of "great extra-social intelligences" who have studied civilisation's methods and turned them to destructive ends (003-064).

The key innovation of super-anarchy is the "counter-compact" — a mirror image of civilisation's "silent compact of comfortable folk" (003-048). Where civilisation's compact is built on trust, consensus, and the "goodwill of civilisation" (003-036), the counter-compact is built on knowledge, secrecy, and the will to power. It is not a rejection of organisation but a rival form of it.

## Origin in the Text

The concept is introduced in the pivotal conversation of [[ch03]], when Leithen, struggling to comprehend the scope of what Lumley has described, names it: "What you describe is super-anarchy, and yet it makes no headway. What is the motive of those diabolical brains?" (003-063).

Lumley's response is characteristically evasive — he claims to be merely "a humble inquirer" who has stumbled upon "curious bits of fact" (003-064). But his detailed knowledge and philosophical sympathy for the concept suggest a deeper connection. He speculates that the "extra-social intelligences" behind super-anarchy may be "idealists" who "desire to make a new world," or "artists, loving for its own sake the pursuit of truth" (003-064).

The concept is given concrete form in the Powerhouse itself, which Leithen comes to understand as a real organisation embodying super-anarchy's principles: international in scope, disciplined in method, and utterly without moral constraint.

## Key Passages

> "What you describe is super-anarchy, and yet it makes no headway. What is the motive of those diabolical brains?" (003-063)

> "I only know of the existence of great extra-social intelligences. Let us say that they distrust the machine. They may be idealists and desire to make a new world, or they may simply be artists, loving for its own sake the pursuit of truth." (003-064)

> "The compact must endure until there is a counter-compact. Consider the ways of that form of foolishness which today we call nihilism or anarchy. A few illiterate bandits in a Paris slum defy the world, and in a week they are in jail." (003-067)

> "I said — supposing anarchy learned from civilisation and became international. Oh, I don't think the world would last very long." (003-072)

## Analysis

Super-anarchy is Buchan's prescient diagnosis of a twentieth-century phenomenon: the marriage of nihilistic intent with organisational sophistication. Lumley's distinction between the "illiterate bandits" of old-style anarchism and the "extra-social intelligences" of super-anarchy anticipates the rise of ideologically-driven, technically-competent revolutionary movements — from Bolshevik vanguardism to the international terrorist networks of the late twentieth century.

The concept also reflects Edwardian anxieties about the "managerial revolution" — the fear that the tools of modern administration (bureaucracy, communications, finance) could be captured by those who reject the values those tools were designed to serve. Lumley's super-anarchy is not a return to barbarism but a perversion of civilisation: it uses the machine to destroy the machine.
