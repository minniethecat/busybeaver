# Academy of Science

## Overview

The term "Academy of Science" in *The Powerhouse* does not refer to any single specific institution but serves as a stand-in for the entire edifice of institutional, public-facing science -- the world of published papers, academic honours, and public recognition. It represents, in Lumley's scheme, science that is safely contained within the "machine" of civilisation.

## In *The Powerhouse*

[[andrew-lumley]] invokes the Academy of Science during his lengthy night-time conversation with [[edward-leithen]] at his Hampshire country house. Contesting Leithen's optimistic view that new scientific discoveries are absorbed and neutralised by society, Lumley argues: "I read now and then in the papers that some eminent scientist has made a great discovery. He reads a paper before some Academy of Science, and there are leading articles on it, and his photograph adorns the magazines. That kind of man is not the danger. He is a bit of the machine, a party to the compact" (PN 368). Lumley distinguishes such public scientists from "the artists in discovery who will never use their knowledge till they can use it with full effect" -- the "extra-social intelligences" who form the hidden Powerhouse (PN 368, 384).

## Historical Context

In the Edwardian period, the most prominent English scientific institution was the Royal Society (founded 1660), while continental Europe had the French Academie des Sciences, the Prussian Academy of Sciences, and similar bodies. These institutions published journals, awarded prizes, and conferred prestige. Lumley's dismissal of them reflects the novel's thematic interest in the tension between public, accountable knowledge and secret, potentially catastrophic knowledge -- a tension that anticipates 20th-century anxieties about scientific secrecy and weapons research.
