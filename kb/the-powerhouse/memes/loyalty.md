# Loyalty

Loyalty in *The Powerhouse* functions as the human counterweight to Lumley's abstract, impersonal network of power. While the Powerhouse operates through fear, secrecy, and intellectual contempt, the alliance between Sir Edward Leithen, [[mr-chapman|Chapman]], and [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] is sustained by personal bonds that transcend ideology. The novel presents loyalty not as a political virtue but as a pre-political one — a loyalty of friends before a loyalty to causes.

## Definition

In Buchan's treatment, loyalty is distinguished from ideological allegiance. Chapman's "loyalty to Tommy" (009-020) is the operative phrase. It is personal, not doctrinal. Chapman's loyalty compels him to cooperate with men whose politics he despises — Leithen is a "viperous lackey of Capitalism" and Tommy is a "gilded popinjay" (005-015) — because the bond of friendship overrides the bond of ideology. This stands in direct contrast to Lumley, who explicitly claims allegiance only to "the intellect of man" (009-020), a commitment that requires no loyalty to any particular human being.

## Origin in the Text

The word surfaces most explicitly near the novel's end, when Leithen persuades Chapman to suppress his impulse toward public exposure. Chapman is a Labour MP who sees the Powerhouse as "this last outrage of Capitalism" (009-020) and wants a grand parliamentary reckoning. Leithen persuades him to silence, and Chapman "promised to hold his tongue out of loyalty to Tommy" (009-020). The loyalty here is not to a cause but to a person — Tommy Deloraine, whose dangerous friend [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]] must be protected from scandal.

Chapman's loyalty is earned through shared peril. The novel charts his transformation from a political opponent into a trusted ally. By the end of their adventures in [[the-restaurant-in-antioch-street|Antioch Street]], Chapman is not merely an ally of convenience but a friend whose loyalty is unquestioned.

## Key Passages

> I knew that Pitt-Heron's secret was safe with him. (009-020)

> "I am a sceptic about most things," he said, "but, believe me, I have my own worship. I venerate the intellect of man. I believe in its undreamed-of possibilities, when it grows free like an oak in the forest and is not dwarfed in a flowerpot. From that allegiance I have never wavered." (009-020)

## Related Characters

- **Mr. Chapman** — his personal loyalty to Tommy overrides his political convictions and his desire for a public reckoning
- **Tommy Deloraine** — the object of Chapman's loyalty, a friend whose trustworthiness earns the devotion of men from across the political spectrum
- **Sir Edward Leithen** — the protagonist who builds and relies upon loyalty among his allies to counter Lumley's impersonal network
- **Andrew Lumley** — the antagonist whose only loyalty is to an abstraction, "the intellect of man," making him incapable of the personal bonds that sustain Leithen's coalition
