# The Treasury

His Majesty's Treasury is the United Kingdom's economics and finance ministry, responsible for public spending, taxation, and the management of the national economy. In *The Powerhouse*, it appears as a single character — "old Milson of the Treasury" (002-007) — yet this brief reference carries considerable symbolic weight.

## Role in the Novel

The Treasury enters the novel at [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]]'s farewell dinner in [[ch01]], where the guest list maps the British Establishment:

> A Cabinet Minister was there, a gentle being for whom Tommy professed public scorn and private affection; a sailor; an Indian cavalry fellow; Chapman, the Labour member, whom Tommy called Chipmunk; myself, and old Milson of the Treasury. (002-007)

Tommy's assessment of Milson is characteristic: "he said that Milson should have been the Warden of a college full of port and prejudice" (002-008). The portrait is gently satirical — a comfortable, conservative, donnish civil servant, miscast in the machinery of government — but not hostile. Milson, like the Cabinet Minister Wytham, belongs to the world of decent, cultivated amateurs that Lumley despises.

The Treasury's presence at this dinner has a structural function. It establishes that the circle from which the conspiracy will emerge is not marginal or bohemian but central to the British state. The Treasury official, the Cabinet Minister, the Labour MP, and the Tory barrister all drink Tommy's wine together. The conspiracy will touch them all, but none of them will be told of it.

## The Treasury and Lumley's Critique

The Treasury is the institution most directly implicated in Lumley's arguments about the vulnerability of civilisation. In the Hampshire library, Lumley expounds his thesis: credit, currency, and the gold standard are the foundation of "a great system of credit" (004-014), and "once gold and silver lost their intrinsic value, the whole edifice of our commerce would collapse" (004-014). The Treasury is the guardian of this system — and Lumley's argument is that the guardians are incapable of imagining, let alone preventing, its collapse.

"When all is said, we are ruled by the amateurs and the second-rate," Lumley tells Leithen. "The methods of our departments would bring any private firm to bankruptcy" (004-016). The Treasury, for all its institutional weight, is in Lumley's view just another department — slow, conventional, staffed by men like Milson who should have been college wardens, not guardians of the national economy.

## Historical Context

In 1913, the Treasury was under the political leadership of David Lloyd George as Chancellor of the Exchequer, with a permanent civil service headed by Sir Robert Chalmers. The Treasury's responsibilities at this period extended well beyond finance into civil service management and imperial administration. Buchan's own career would later bring him into contact with Treasury mandarins, and his portrait of "old Milson" reflects the affectionate condescension with which the political class regarded senior civil servants: indispensable but faintly absurd.

## Thematic Significance

The Treasury represents the institutional architecture of economic order — precisely what Lumley believes can be destroyed by a single "new chemical combination" or a "little mechanical device" (004-016). The novel's conservative thesis is that this order is real but fragile, sustained by "our tacit agreement not to question" its premises (004-015). Milson, the Treasury man who should have been a college warden, embodies both the strength and the weakness of this arrangement: he is decent, competent, and utterly unprepared for the kind of threat that Lumley represents.
