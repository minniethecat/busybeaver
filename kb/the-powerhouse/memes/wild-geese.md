"Wild Geese" is the central metaphor of the novel's final chapter, "Return of the Wild Geese" (Chapter IX). It represents the instinctive, cyclical return of the story's scattered characters from their far-flung adventures back to home — a natural, migratory pattern that mirrors the resolution of the novel's conflicts.

## The Metaphor

Wild geese are migratory birds known for their long-distance flights and reliable seasonal returns. In the context of the novel, the metaphor operates on multiple levels:

- **Literal Return**: [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] returns from his pursuit across Central Asia, and [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]] is ultimately recovered from his flight.
- **Narrative Resolution**: After the chaos and dispersion of the Powerhouse conspiracy, the surviving characters return to their natural places — Leithen to his legal work, Tommy to his restless pursuits.
- **Cyclical Pattern**: The metaphor suggests that adventure and departure are natural, but so is the homeward turn.

## In the Novel

The phrase appears as the title of Chapter IX: "Return of the Wild Geese" [009-001]. The chapter brings the novel's action to a close, with Leithen reading the final resolution of events in the newspaper — Lumley's disappearance, the scattering of the conspiracy, and the safe return of the key players.

The metaphor is never explicitly explained within the text, functioning instead as a suggestive image that the reader is invited to interpret. It evokes the wildness of the chase, the exotic locales of the Central Asian pursuit, and the fundamental pull of home.
