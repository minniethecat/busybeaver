# The Restaurant in Antioch Street

The dinner at Rapaccini's restaurant in Antioch Street is a pivotal revelation scene in [[ch06|Chapter VI]], where [[macgillivray|Macgillivray]] — a figure from Scotland Yard — shares critical intelligence about the Powerhouse with [[edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]], transforming the investigation from a personal pursuit into an informed operation.

## Summary

Macgillivray telephones Leithen and proposes dinner at "a little place where I sometimes dine — Rapaccini's, in Antioch Street" (ch06), describing it as a discreet location for a confidential conversation. Leithen walks through a hot London evening to find the restaurant — "one of those discreet establishments, common enough in France, where no edibles are displayed in the British fashion, and muslin half-curtains deck the windows" (ch06). During the dinner, Macgillivray shares "very important news" (ch06) that advances Leithen's understanding of the Powerhouse's operations, membership, and objectives.

The restaurant is later revealed to have been a Powerhouse operational hub: "Routh's London headquarters had been at the restaurant in Antioch Street" (ch06), a fact that [[chapman|Chapman]] recalls from the Routh case, adding another layer of irony to the choice of meeting place.

## Context

The meeting at Antioch Street comes at a critical juncture. [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] is in Central Asia pursuing Pitt-Heron, Leithen has begun to grasp the scale of the conspiracy, and [[felix|Monsieur Felix]] has been mobilised through diplomatic channels. Macgillivray's intelligence provides the institutional perspective — Scotland Yard's awareness of the Powerhouse — that complements the personal and diplomatic angles Leithen has already developed.

## Significance

The Antioch Street dinner is a turning point in the novel's information architecture. Before this meeting, Leithen operates on fragments: Tommy's suspicions, Felix's diplomatic cables, the financial trail through Julius Pavia. Macgillivray's intelligence consolidates these fragments into a coherent picture of the Powerhouse as an organisation. The irony that the meeting takes place at a former Powerhouse base — known to Chapman but not to Macgillivray — reinforces the novel's central theme: the conspiracy is always closer than it appears, woven into the fabric of ordinary London life.
