# Connaught Mews

## Overview

Connaught Mews is a lane near the Edgware Road in London, part of the warren of mews, yards, and back streets that characterized the service infrastructure behind grand Georgian and Victorian terraces. In *The Powerhouse*, it forms part of Leithen's escape route.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Connaught Mews is mentioned in Chapter VII during Leithen's flight from the fake chauffeur. After slipping into Levison's picture-shop, Leithen asks if there is a back door. The proprietor replies: "Why, yes; there is the door into the lane which runs from Edgeley Street into Connaught Mews" (PN 949). Leithen uses this rear exit to slip away from his pursuers, emerging into a "narrow alley" and then making his way through "various cross streets" into Bayswater (PN 953-954). The Connaught Mews lane represents the hidden geography of London that saves Leithen's life -- the service routes and back passages invisible to the grand facades of the main streets.

## Historical Context

London mews were originally built as stables and coach-houses for the grand townhouses of the 18th and 19th centuries, with living accommodation for servants above. By the Edwardian era, many had been converted into garages for motorcars or had become modest residential and commercial premises. Connaught Mews was named after the Duke of Connaught, reflecting the association of the area with the nearby Connaught Square. The network of mews provided a hidden geography known to locals but invisible to pursuers from outside the neighbourhood.
