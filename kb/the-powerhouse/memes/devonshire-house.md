# Devonshire House

## Overview

Devonshire House was the London residence of the Dukes of Devonshire, situated on Piccadilly, roughly opposite the Ritz Hotel. A grand Palladian mansion set behind a high wall and courtyard, it was one of the last great aristocratic houses remaining in the West End. In *The Powerhouse*, it marks a point of transition from danger to safety.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Devonshire House appears in Chapter VIII, immediately after Leithen's climactic confrontation with Lumley at the Albany. Leaving the Albany and walking to his club, Leithen describes his route: "I struck into the Green Park and kept to the grass till I emerged into Piccadilly, opposite Devonshire House" (PN 1047). The passage is significant: Leithen has just delivered his ultimatum to Lumley -- exile or arrest -- and is now re-entering the normal world. Devonshire House, as a symbol of the aristocratic order, marks the threshold between Lumley's secretive chambers and the public life of the city. The light evening breeze, the men Leithen meets "going out to dinner on foot," and the very solidity of the great mansion declare that civilization has -- for the moment -- prevailed.

## Historical Context

Devonshire House was built in the 1730s for the third Duke of Devonshire on the site of the former Berkeley House. Its famous salon was the setting for grand political and social gatherings throughout the 18th and 19th centuries. By the Edwardian era, such great private houses on Piccadilly were increasingly rare, as commercial pressures and death duties forced many aristocratic families to sell. Devonshire House itself was demolished in 1924 and replaced by an office building. Its presence in the novel thus records a London that was already vanishing.
