# The Enemy

In the thriller architecture of *The Powerhouse*, the concept of "the enemy" operates at multiple scales: the personal adversary who stalks a man across Central Asia, the hidden organisation that regards all of civilisation as its opponent, and the duel of wits between two men who both move in the highest circles of London society. The novel explores what it means to have an enemy who cannot be seen, who may even sit at the same dinner table.

## Definition

Buchan distinguishes between the visible enemy of conventional combat and the invisible enemy of conspiracy. When [[charles-pitt-heron|Pitt-Heron]] flees, he hopes "in the wild tangle of the Pamirs he hoped to baffle his enemy" (004-008). The enemy here is personal, a pursuer — but Pitt-Heron's terror is described as "omnipotent" (004-008), suggesting a force so pervasive that it drives a brilliant, rich, successful man "to the fate of an absconding felon" (004-008).

For [[sir-edward-leithen|Leithen]], the enemy gradually shifts from abstract to personal. His watchers in Piccadilly, the footman-assassin at [[the-restaurant-in-antioch-street|Antioch Street]], the footpad on Constitution Hill — these are not faceless threats but agents of a single will. By the time Leithen confronts Lumley at [[the-albany|the Albany]], he can define his enemy with precision: "You love power, hidden power. You flatter your vanity by despising mankind and making them your tools... Unhappily the life of millions is built on that smattering, so you are a foe to society" (008-023).

## Origin in the Text

The language of enmity appears from the novel's earliest chapters. Leithen experiences the primal sensation of being watched — "a sort of sensation which the mind receives without actual evidence... a legacy from the days when the caveman had to look pretty sharp to keep from getting his enemy's knife between the ribs" (004-043). This ancient, embodied fear is the novel's baseline: the enemy is felt before it is understood.

[[mr-chapman|Chapman]]'s introduction reframes the enemy in ideological terms. He sees the struggle as "another case of the strife between Capital and Labour" (005-015), but relishes that this enemy can be opposed directly — not with "long-winded speeches but might any moment get a chance to pummel him with your fists" (005-015). The novel consistently tests this crude political model against a much stranger reality.

## Types of Enemy

### The Pursuer

[[felix-tuke|Tuke]] (alias Routh) functions as the novel's most literal enemy — a man dispatched to Central Asia to hunt [[charles-pitt-heron|Pitt-Heron]]. Tuke is "a discredited genius" (004-055), the perfect instrument for Lumley's purposes, a man who "asked for nothing better than to use his brains outside the ring-fence of convention" (004-055).

### The Hidden Adversary

Andrew Lumley operates as an enemy who is most dangerous precisely because he is unrecognised. At the dinner party, Leithen watches him with "a kind of despairing hatred" (005-021), aware that "here in this pleasant room, with servants moving softly about and a mellow light on the silver from the shaded candles... the man was buttressed and defended beyond my reach" (005-021).

### The Society as Enemy

Lumley inverts the framework: in his philosophy, society itself is the enemy of intelligence, conspiring to "dwarf in a flowerpot" (009-020) the free intellect. Lumley's enmity is directed not at individuals but at "the smattering of inaccuracies which passes for human knowledge" (008-023). He is, as Leithen diagnoses, "a foe to society" (008-023).
