# Yorkshire

Yorkshire is a historic county in northern England, significant in the narrative as the home region of two key characters: [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] and [[chapman|Chapman]], the Labour MP.

## Deloraine Family Seat

Tommy Deloraine's connection to Yorkshire is aristocratic. Early in the novel, Leithen notes that "it was before Tommy succeeded, in the days when he sat for the family seat in Yorkshire" [001-002]. Tommy's Yorkshire roots place him within the landed gentry, a contrast to his adventurous, unconventional lifestyle.

## Chapman's Home Ground

For Chapman, Yorkshire represents his political and personal identity. He and Deloraine "lived adjacent in Yorkshire, and on platforms abused each other like pickpockets" [001-008], a domestic proximity that makes their public political rivalry all the more theatrical. Chapman's speech and manner are repeatedly identified by their Yorkshire character: "He spoke rich Yorkshire with a touch of the drawl of the western dales" [005-005].

Leithen comes to appreciate the duality of Chapman's nature — "a piece of granite, with his Yorkshire bluntness and hard, downright, north-country sense" [005-018], yet also "as romantic as a boy." This Yorkshire bluntness becomes a marker of reliability in the crisis. When Chapman bursts into the restaurant in Antioch Street, "there could be no mistaking that broad Yorkshire tongue" [006-058], and his arrival turns the tide of the confrontation.
