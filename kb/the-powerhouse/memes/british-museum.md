# British Museum

The British Museum is the national museum of the United Kingdom, founded in 1753 and housed since the 1850s in its great neoclassical building on Great Russell Street, Bloomsbury. In *The Powerhouse*, it appears as both a literal institution — one that might have acquired treasures that instead went to Lumley — and a symbol of the public culture that Lumley's private collecting quietly subverts.

## Role in the Novel

The British Museum enters the narrative in [[ch02]], embedded in a conversation about Old Wedgwood. [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] lunches at his club with Jenkinson, the art critic, who is furious about a recent sale:

> He babbled through luncheon about the Wentworth sale, which he had attended the week before. There had been a pair of magnificent plaques, with a unique Flaxman design, which had roused his enthusiasm. Urns and medallions and whatnot had gone to this or that connoisseur, and Jenkinson could quote their prices, but the plaques dominated his fancy, and he was furious that the nation had not acquired them. It seemed that he had been to South Kensington and the British Museum and all sorts of dignitaries, and he thought he might yet persuade the authorities to offer for them if the purchaser would resell. They had been bought by Lutrin for a well-known private collector, by name Andrew Lumley. (003-004)

The British Museum here represents the public acquisition of culture — "the nation" acquiring treasures for the benefit of all. Jenkinson's frustration is that the Wedgwood plaques, with their "unique Flaxman design," have gone instead to Lumley, a private collector who "locked up his things in cupboards and never let the public get a look at them" (003-004).

This is Leithen's first substantive information about Lumley — and it comes through the filter of cultural politics. Lumley is presented as a hoarder who removes treasures from public circulation, "a rich old buffer" whose collecting habits Jenkinson condemns as "the old silly game" (003-005). The British Museum, by contrast, is the institution that ought to have secured the plaques — but failed to, because it cannot compete with private wealth.

## The Museum as Character Reference

The British Museum appears a second time in a more subtle context. When Leithen first meets Lumley at the Hampshire country house, he speculates about his host's profession: "He was not a lawyer, for he had not the inevitable lines on the cheek. I thought that he might be a retired Oxford don, or one of the higher civil servants, or perhaps some official of the British Museum" (004-022). This guess — that Lumley might be a Museum official — is deeply ironic. The man Leithen imagines as a custodian of national treasures is in fact the private collector who removes them from public view.

The guess also reveals something about the British Museum's social position. To be "some official of the British Museum" is to be a scholar and a gentleman — a figure of unquestionable respectability, exactly the kind of identity that Lumley's manner, voice, and library project.

## Historical Context

In the Edwardian period, the British Museum was undergoing significant expansion. The King's Library had been built in the 1820s; the Round Reading Room, completed in 1857, was the scholarly heart of the Empire. The Museum's collections were growing rapidly through bequest, purchase, and imperial acquisition. Its keepers were among the most respected scholars in Britain.

The competition between public institutions and private collectors that Jenkinson describes was a real feature of the period. American millionaires — J. P. Morgan, Henry Clay Frick, and others — were buying European art on an unprecedented scale, and the British Museum often found itself priced out of sales. Lumley, a domestic version of this phenomenon, represents the private wealth that public culture cannot match.

## Thematic Significance

The British Museum functions as Lumley's institutional antithesis. The Museum collects for the public — its treasures are visible, accessible, part of the shared culture. Lumley collects for himself — his treasures are hidden, hoarded, "put in cold storage for good" (003-004). This opposition mirrors the novel's larger theme: civilisation is a shared "compact" (004-015), while Lumley's "super-anarchy" (008-003) represents the private appropriation of power from the public domain.

Yet the irony cuts both ways. The British Museum is part of the institutional Establishment that Lumley despises and that is incapable of recognising him. Its keepers, like the Admiralty's officials and the Cabinet's ministers, are "amateurs" in Lumley's lexicon — decent men who cannot imagine the threat they face.
