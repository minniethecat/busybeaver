# The Wedgwood Examination

## Overview

The Wedgwood Examination is a critical rising action event in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*, in which [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] accompanies the art critic Jenkinson to examine [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s collection of [[wedgwood-plaques|Wedgwood plaques]] at the [[white-lodge|White Lodge]] in Blackheath. What begins as a casual art outing becomes a pivotal moment in Leithen's investigation.

## Context

After the [[the-county-court-case|County Court case]] piqued his curiosity about Lumley, Leithen encountered Jenkinson at his club. Jenkinson, an authority on Wedgwood, had been told that "Mr. Lumley apparently might be willing to resell the Wedgwood plaques if he got a fair offer," and invited Leithen to accompany him to inspect them (PN 211).

Jenkinson explained that Lumley "always bought through agents" and had already taken delivery of the plaques to a house at Blackheath rather than the Albany (PN 215). The house was "stuffed from cellar to garret" with treasures (PN 215). Leithen suspected that the fictional [[julius-pavia|Julius Pavia]], the supposed owner, did not exist: "The house was Lumley's, who chose to pass under another name during his occasional visits" (PN 234).

The White Lodge was "one of those small, square, late-Georgian mansions which you see all around London" (PN 227). Leithen looked for [[tuke|Tuke the super-butler]], but was met instead by a female servant. The interior was chaotic: "an untidy little room, full of fine Chinese porcelain in dusty glass cabinets, and in a corner stood piles of old Persian rugs" (PN 231).

## Significance

While Jenkinson was absorbed in examining the Wedgwood pieces, Leithen explored the room. By a packing case that had held the plaques, he spotted a crumpled telegraph form beneath cotton-wool and shavings. Feigning an accident with his cigarette case, he knelt to retrieve it and read the message.

The telegram was in French: "Suivez a Bokhare Saronov" (PN 239). It instructed a recipient named Saronov to follow someone to Bokhara. Leithen immediately connected this to [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]]'s flight to Central Asia. The telegram had been sent from Vienna in code, confirming that Lumley's network was international and that Pitt-Heron was being hunted.

This discovery transforms Leithen from a curious bystander into an active participant. He realizes that Lumley's butler Tuke is "too infernally intelligent" (PN 233) and that Lumley's organization is not a mere philanthropic eccentricity but a dangerous conspiracy. That night, Leithen telephoned [[ethel-pitt-heron|Ethel Pitt-Heron]] and instructed her to wire Tommy: "Go on to Bokhara. Have information you will meet him there" (PN 247).

The Wedgwood examination was thus the moment Leithen crossed the threshold from observation to action, and the telegram became the central piece of physical evidence that unraveled the Powerhouse plot.
