# Extra-social Intelligence

## Overview

Extra-social Intelligence is the central philosophical concept articulated by [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*. It describes the rare intellects that operate outside the moral and social conventions of civilization -- minds that possess deadly knowledge and the will to use it, forming what Lumley calls a "counter-compact" to the established order.

## In The Powerhouse

The concept emerges during Lumley's midnight discourse at his country house, following [[leithnes-car-breakdown|Leithen's car breakdown]]. After a series of unsettling observations about secret knowledge and hidden powers, Lumley declares: "I only know of the existence of great extra-social intelligences. Let us say that they distrust the machine. They may be idealists and desire to make a new world, or they may simply be artists, loving for its own sake the pursuit of truth. If I were to hazard a guess, I should say that it took both types to bring about results, for the second find the knowledge and the first the will to use it" (PN 384).

Leithen immediately labels this "super-anarchy" (PN 382) and challenges Lumley on its effectiveness: "if those extra-social brains are so potent, why after all do they effect so little? A dull police-officer, with the machine behind him, can afford to laugh at most experiments in anarchy" (PN 388).

Lumley's response is chilling. He concedes that isolated anarchists are easily crushed, but argues that the weakness of extra-social intelligence lies in its fragmentation: "Civilisation wins because it is a worldwide league; its enemies fail because they are parochial. But supposing--" (PN 391). The ellipsis contains the entire premise of the Powerhouse: what if the extra-social intelligences organized themselves into a worldwide league of their own?

The concept is further developed through a recollection Leithen has of a German Nietzschean he met in Tyrol: "The pity is that the reformers do not know, and those who know are too idle to reform. Some day there will come the marriage of knowledge and will, and then the world will march" (PN 387). Lumley represents precisely this marriage.

## Analysis

Extra-social intelligence is Buchan's diagnosis of a specific type of modern evil. It is not the evil of passion or greed but of detached, intellectual contempt for the social contract. Lumley does not hate civilization; he simply regards it as a fragile, contingent arrangement that more powerful minds have no obligation to honor.

The concept reflects early twentieth-century anxieties about the consequences of scientific and intellectual specialization. As knowledge advanced, it became increasingly possible for small groups of experts to wield disproportionate power. The "deadly knowledge" Lumley refers to -- "great engines of destruction" far beyond "the capacity of any defence" (PN 374) -- anticipates the role of scientists and technocrats in the total warfare of the coming century.

The Nietzschean echoes are deliberate. Lumley's vision of the marriage of knowledge and will, operating beyond conventional morality, is a precise rendering of the Nietzschean superman translated into the idiom of the Edwardian thriller. Buchan's achievement is to make this abstract philosophical threat concrete: Lumley is not a mad scientist in a laboratory but a charming, wealthy, socially adept gentleman whose "extra-social" nature is concealed by perfect manners.

The concept also illuminates the novel's resolution. Lumley's death from heart failure is not merely a convenient plot device but a demonstration of the fatal weakness of extra-social intelligence: it cannot institutionalize itself. Lumley leaves no successors; the "marriage of knowledge and will" dies with him.
