# Sir Edward Leithen

The narrator and protagonist of *The Powerhouse*. A successful barrister and Member of Parliament, Leithen is the "ordinary educated Englishman" whose quiet life is disrupted when his friend Charles Pitt-Heron disappears — drawing him into a confrontation with the shadowy organisation known as the Powerhouse.

## Role in the Story

Leithen serves as both protagonist and narrator, filtering the novel's events through his measured, lawyerly consciousness. His involvement begins casually — a dinner at his club, a friend's concern about Pitt-Heron — but escalates rapidly as he becomes the Powerhouse's target. His role is that of the amateur investigator: not a detective or spy, but an intelligent man of affairs who finds himself compelled to pursue a mystery that the ordinary institutions of law and police cannot resolve.

His narrative voice is characterised by restraint and ironic self-awareness. He describes himself as "the ordinary educated Englishman" whose sort "gravitates to the Bar" (003-033), a self-assessment that is both accurate and misleading — he is ordinary only by the standards of his class, and his ordinariness conceals considerable courage and resourcefulness.

## Background

Leithen is a barrister — a member of the English Bar — and a Member of Parliament. His professional identity is central to his character: he thinks like a lawyer, weighing evidence, testing hypotheses, and trusting in the institutions of law and order. He describes his profession with characteristic modesty: "We like feeling that, if we are not the builders, at any rate we are the cement of civilisation" (003-033).

His background places him firmly within the Edwardian establishment — public school, university, the Inns of Court, the House of Commons. He moves easily through London's clubs, drawing rooms, and government offices. This insider status is both a strength (it gives him access and credibility) and a limitation (it makes it difficult for him to imagine threats that operate entirely outside the establishment's framework).

## Relationships

- **Charles Pitt-Heron** — Leithen's old friend whose disappearance sets the plot in motion; their shared history goes back to university days
- **Andrew Lumley** — The enigmatic philosopher whose conversation opens Leithen's eyes to the Powerhouse; their relationship shifts from intellectual sparring to mortal antagonism
- **Macgillivray** — Leithen's contact at Scotland Yard; a professional relationship built on mutual respect
- **Tommy Deloraine** — A friend who assists Leithen in his investigation; represents the amateur, romantic spirit that complements Leithen's professional caution

## Significance

Leithen is the first of Buchan's recurring protagonists — he would appear in several subsequent novels, becoming one of the author's most enduring characters. In *The Powerhouse*, he embodies the Edwardian ideal of the gentleman-amateur: competent without being expert, brave without being reckless, moral without being sanctimonious.

His significance within the novel is structural as well as thematic. As narrator, he mediates between the reader and the extraordinary events he describes. His measured, rational voice makes the Powerhouse's threat credible: if a man this sensible is frightened, the danger must be real. His limitations — his difficulty imagining threats outside the framework of law and civilisation — are the novel's subject as much as its method.
