# Capital and Labour

## Overview

"Capital and Labour" in *The Powerhouse* represents the central ideological conflict of early 20th-century British politics rather than a formal organisation. It is the lens through which [[chapman]], the Yorkshire Labour MP, understands all social and political struggles -- including, comically, the secret conspiracy that [[edward-leithen]] is investigating.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Chapman sees everything through the prism of Capital versus Labour. When Leithen recruits him as an ally against the Powerhouse, Chapman is "quite clear that it was another case of the strife between Capital and Labour -- Tommy and I standing for Labour" (PN 622). This is despite the irony that [[tommy-deloraine]] is a Conservative country gentleman whom Chapman "used to refer to Tommy in public as a 'gilded popinjay,'" and whom he had described Leithen as a "'viperous lackey of Capitalism'" only a month earlier (PN 622). For Chapman, the conflict takes on an appealingly physical character: "It was the best kind of strife, in which you had not to meet your adversary with long-winded speeches but might any moment get a chance to pummel him with your fists" (PN 622).

After Chapman is attacked and his parliamentary report stolen, he demands "a public exposure of Lumley, a big trial, a general ferreting out of secret agents, the whole winding up with a speech in Parliament by himself on this last outrage of Capitalism" (PN 1163).

## Historical Context

The Capital-Labour divide was the dominant political cleavage of Edwardian Britain. The Labour Party, founded in 1900, was gaining parliamentary seats and challenging the Liberal-Conservative duopoly. Strikes, trade union disputes, and debates over workers' rights dominated the headlines. Chapman's character -- bluff, blunt, earnest, and sometimes comically wrong-headed -- reflects contemporary stereotypes of the Labour MP as an outsider to the gentlemanly culture of Westminster.
