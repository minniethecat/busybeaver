# Jermyn Street

## Overview

Jermyn Street is a fashionable street in St James's, central London, running parallel to Piccadilly. Historically known for its gentlemen's outfitters, shirt-makers, and luxury retail, it was a haunt of the well-heeled Edwardian gentleman. In *The Powerhouse*, it provides the setting for one of Chapman's more reckless escapades.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Jermyn Street appears in Chapter V when Chapman's amateur detective work goes awry. Convinced he has spotted Lumley, Chapman confronts a chauffeur outside "a shop in Jermyn Street" and demands his master's name. When the chauffeur replies abusively, Chapman "hauled him from the driver's seat and shaken him till his teeth rattled" (PN 631). The owner emerges, Chapman is arrested, and he is fined five pounds and costs. The incident demonstrates Chapman's belligerent approach to intrigue -- and how Lumley might exploit it to discredit Leithen's allies. The Jermyn Street fracas serves as a comic warning of the dangers of amateur detection.

## Historical Context

Developed in the late 17th century and named after Henry Jermyn, 1st Earl of St Albans, Jermyn Street has long been associated with high-end menswear. In the Edwardian era, it was the go-to destination for bespoke shirts, suits, and shoes. Floris, the perfumer established in 1730, and Paxton & Whitfield, the cheesemonger, were among its long-established businesses. The street's reputation for discretion and exclusivity made it a plausible place for a man like Lumley to conduct private business.
