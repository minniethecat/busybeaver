# Stratton Street

## Overview

Stratton Street is a short street in Mayfair, London, running between Piccadilly and Berkeley Street. In the Edwardian era, it was known for its discreet character, lined with elegant townhouses and located close to the gentlemen's clubs of St James's. In *The Powerhouse*, it is a pivot point in Leithen's detection of his shadowers.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Stratton Street appears in Chapter IV during Leithen's first experiments to confirm he is being watched. Walking home from his club on a "bright summer evening," he notices the feeling of being followed and takes action: "I halted twice, once in St. James's Street and once at the corner of Stratton Street, and retraced my steps for a bit, and each time I had the impression that someone a hundred yards or so off had done the same" (PN 533). The corner of Stratton Street becomes a test site: a place where Leithen's abrupt turn exposes the presence of a tail. The choice reflects the geography of the area -- Stratton Street's junction with Piccadilly offers a natural vantage point where a watcher must either follow or reveal himself.

## Historical Context

Stratton Street was developed in the 18th century and named after the Stratton family. It was part of the Berkeley Estate and was characterized by terraced townhouses. Located in the heart of London's clubland, it was close to the Devonshire Club, the Naval and Military Club, and other exclusive establishments. The street's proximity to Piccadilly and St James's Street made it a natural route for gentlemen moving between their clubs, their flats, and the West End.
