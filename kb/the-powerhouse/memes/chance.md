# Chance

Chance is the hidden engine of *The Powerhouse* — the wild card that even [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s meticulous conspiracy cannot fully control. For all the novel's philosophical apparatus of "counter-compacts" and "extra-social intelligences," Leithen's involvement begins through accident: a chance encounter, a friend's disappearance he happens to investigate, a dinner invitation accepted without suspicion. The novel's implicit argument is that chance is civilisation's secret ally — the unpredictable element that keeps any "counter-compact" from being perfectly realised.

## Definition

Chance in *The Powerhouse* operates in productive tension with the novel's philosophy of power. Lumley describes a world of systematic knowledge and organisation — the "counter-compact" that mirrors civilisation's own "silent compact" (003-048, 003-067). But the plot repeatedly turns on accidents: fortuitous recognitions, unplanned discoveries, overheard conversations. The novel suggests that no system, however perfectly organised, can eliminate contingency — and that contingency, paradoxically, is one of civilisation's most reliable defences.

## Origin in the Text

The word "chance" appears in the novel's opening paragraph — "I had got in by an accident at a by-election" (001-002) — establishing contingency as a narrative principle from the first sentence. Leithen is an MP by accident, drawn into the Powerhouse investigation by accident, and saved from the Antioch Street trap by Chapman's chance recollection.

The novel's most explicit reflection on chance comes in the form of its opposite: Lumley's vision of a world where knowledge eliminates contingency. If "knowledge is the only power" (003-053), then chance is the absence of knowledge — the gap through which the unexpected enters. Lumley's project is, in effect, the elimination of chance through systematic intelligence. Leithen's success depends on chance's persistence.

## Key Passages

> "Good evening, Mr. Leithen," a voice said. "It is a kindly mischance which gives a lonely old man the pleasure of your company." (003-021)

Lumley's greeting to Leithen at their first meeting is doubly ironic. He calls their encounter a "kindly mischance" — but we later learn that nothing involving Lumley is truly chance. The "mischance" is manufactured: Leithen's car has been disabled near Lumley's house, and the invitation to spend the night is a calculated opportunity for Lumley to deliver his philosophical seduction. The episode illustrates the novel's deeper point: that the powerful manipulate chance, while the ordinary are at its mercy.

> But always that phrase of his, the "Powerhouse," kept recurring. You know how twisted your thoughts get during a wakeful night, and long before I fell asleep towards morning I had worked myself up into a very complete dislike of that bland and smiling gentleman, my host. (003-079)

This passage — Leithen's sleepless night after Lumley's monologue — captures the psychological mechanism of the novel. Chance is not merely external events; it is also the unpredictable movement of the mind, the phrase that "keeps recurring" unbidden, the half-conscious process by which suspicion crystallises into conviction. Leithen's dislike of Lumley is not the product of rational analysis but of a chance mental process — the "twisted thoughts" of a wakeful night.

## Analysis

The role of chance in *The Powerhouse* is characteristic of Buchan's thriller technique. His protagonists are typically ordinary men — not detectives, not spies, not adventurers — who are drawn into extraordinary situations by accident. This is partly a realism effect (most people's lives are shaped by chance rather than design) and partly a narrative strategy (the ordinary-man-caught-up-in-events is more relatable than the master-detective who controls events).

But chance also serves a thematic function. It is the wild card that even the Powerhouse cannot fully control — the reminder that no system, however perfectly organised, can eliminate contingency. Lumley's conspiracy is an attempt to substitute design for chance, knowledge for accident, the "counter-compact" for the "silent compact." Leithen's success depends on the fact that this substitution can never be complete — that chance, like the "kindly mischance" of Lumley's own ironic phrase, always has the last word.

In this sense, chance is more than a plot device: it is the novel's quiet rebuttal to Lumley's philosophy. If civilisation is indeed a "conspiracy" and a "compact," as Lumley argues, then chance is what keeps the compact from ossifying into tyranny. Contingency is the price of freedom.
