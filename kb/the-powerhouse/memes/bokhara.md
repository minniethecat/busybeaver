# Bokhara

A distant Central Asian city that becomes the gravitational center of the conspiracy in *The Powerhouse*. Charles Pitt-Heron flees there under mysterious circumstances, pursued by Tommy Deloraine and later by the agents of Andrew Lumley's criminal network.

## Description

Bokhara (modern Bukhara, Uzbekistan) is presented as a remote and exotic destination on the fringes of the Russian Empire. The novel treats it as a place beyond the reach of English law and civilized order — precisely the kind of location where Lumley's extra-legal organization could operate without scrutiny. Its distance and inaccessibility are central to its narrative function: the journey there represents crossing into a world where conventional protections no longer apply (002-040).

## Role in the Novel

Bokhara serves as the destination of the "wild-goose chase" that drives the novel's middle chapters. Charles Pitt-Heron, having fallen under Lumley's influence, departs for Bokhara under false pretenses. His wife Ethel receives a telegram indicating the trip, and later sends word: "Go on to Bokhara. Have information you will meet" (002-043).

Tommy Deloraine follows Pitt-Heron to Bokhara, and Leithen receives word that Tommy has joined him there (004-017). The two men — Pitt-Heron and Tommy — are stranded in Bokhara, wanting to make "a long trek" but lacking funds and resources (004-019). Leithen pores over maps, calculating that from Bokhara the only routes lead deeper into Central Asia or back toward the Caspian (004-020).

It is eventually revealed that Tuke — a disgraced trade-union official turned Lumley operative — was the one sent to Bokhara to "reason with" Pitt-Heron, or if necessary, to silence him (004-038). Lumley receives confirmation from Bokhara that Pitt-Heron has left with a small caravan, heading further east (004-048). Meanwhile, Leithen discovers that the main road from Bokhara was being watched, and the frontier posts had been alerted to intercept the fugitives (005-037).

## Real-World Context

Bokhara is the early-20th-century English spelling of **Bukhara**, an ancient city in modern-day Uzbekistan. At the time of the novel (c. 1913), Bukhara was a vassal state of the Russian Empire, retaining nominal independence as the Emirate of Bukhara. The city lay on the old Silk Road and was known for its madrasas, trading domes, and Islamic architecture. Buchan's choice of Bokhara as the destination reflects the early-20th-century British fascination with Central Asia as a zone of imperial rivalry — the "Great Game" setting where English gentlemen could disappear beyond the reach of London's institutions.
