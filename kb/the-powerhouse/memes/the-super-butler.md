# The Super-Butler

## Overview

The Super-Butler is a concept in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse* describing a servant who possesses intelligence, capabilities, and bearing far exceeding those of an ordinary domestic servant. The term is explicitly coined by Leithen to describe [[tuke|Tuke]], Andrew Lumley's butler at the White Lodge, who is later revealed to be a former trade union leader and one of Lumley's most dangerous agents.

## In The Powerhouse

The concept emerges in two key encounters. First, in the [[the-county-court-case|County Court case]], Leithen describes Tuke as "very unlike the conventional butler, much liker one of those successful financiers whose portraits you see in the picture papers. His little eyes were quick with intelligence, and there were lines of ruthlessness around his mouth, like those of a man often called to decisive action" (PN 169).

When Leithen visits the White Lodge for the [[the-wedgwood-examination|Wedgwood examination]], he notes that the butler "had looked too infernally intelligent" (PN 233). Upon entering the house, he "looked to see my super-butler Tuke" (PN 227), cementing the phrase.

Leithen explicitly uses the term when tracking Tuke after the telegram discovery. The chapter title itself reads "IV: I Follow the Trail of the Super-Butler" (PN 432, 437). His investigation reveals that Tuke's real name is Routh, and that he was once a prominent figure in the trade union movement. Scotland Yard's Macgillivray describes him as "a discredited genius" -- "the very man for Lumley's schemes -- one who asked for nothing better than to use his brains outside the ring-fence of convention" (PN 525).

The concept is invoked when Leithen realizes Lumley has sent someone to Bokhara: "Someone must have gone to Bokhara, some creature of Lumley's, perhaps the super-butler I had met in the County Court" (PN 445). His intuition proves correct.

## Analysis

The super-butler concept serves as a vehicle for one of Buchan's recurring themes: the discrepancy between social appearance and actual capability. Tuke's butler disguise allows him to move through society invisibly while wielding the skills of a political organizer and the ruthlessness of a criminal agent.

The concept also illustrates Lumley's method of recruitment. Rather than hiring conventional criminals, Lumley recruited men of genuine ability whose careers had been derailed -- "discredited geniuses" who had no stake in conventional society and could be directed toward Lumley's "extra-social" purposes. Tuke's brother, a respectable clerk who unwittingly forwarded mail, represented the innocent, rule-abiding side of the family -- the life Tuke had abandoned.

The term "super-butler" captures the unsettling nature of Tuke's presence: a man of evident power and intelligence performing the most subservient of roles. This inversion of the natural order mirrors the larger threat of the Powerhouse itself, which operates beneath the surface of civilized society.
