# Marylebone Road

## Overview

Marylebone Road is a major east-west thoroughfare in northwest London, running from the Edgware Road junction to Euston Road. It forms part of the London Inner Ring Road and was a busy commercial and traffic artery in the Edwardian period. In *The Powerhouse*, its junction provides the crucial moment for Leithen's escape.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The Marylebone Road junction features in Chapter VII at one of the novel's most dramatic moments. Leithen, having discovered that his chauffeur is an impostor, waits for his opportunity: "Looking out I saw that we were some distance up the Edgeware Road, nearing the point where the Marylebone Road joins it" (PN 939). As the car slows for traffic at the junction, Leithen slips out: "The car slowed down in obedience to a policeman's uplifted hand, and very gently I opened the door on the left side" (PN 940). He vanishes into the crowds while the fake chauffeur drives on, oblivious. The Marylebone Road junction -- with its traffic, its policeman, and its crush of vehicles -- provides the anonymous cover Leithen needs to escape.

## Historical Context

Marylebone Road was laid out in the mid-18th century as the New Road, designed to bypass the congested routes through central London. It became an important east-west artery connecting Paddington to King's Cross and Euston. By the Edwardian period, it was a bustling thoroughfare lined with shops, offices, and the imposing facade of Madame Tussauds (which had moved there in 1884). The junction with Edgware Road was (and remains) one of London's busiest intersections, making it a plausible and effective location for a dramatic escape.
