# Parliament

Parliament is the supreme legislative body of the United Kingdom, consisting of the House of Commons and the House of Lords. In *The Powerhouse*, it functions as the institutional frame of reference for the novel's principal characters — a world of committee rooms and smoking-room conversations that stands in deliberate contrast to the hidden world of [[andrew-lumley|Lumley]]'s criminal conspiracy.

## Role in the Novel

Parliament is the novel's baseline for "normal" institutional life. The narrative opens with [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] emerging from the House of Commons with [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]], and Tommy's first complaint sets the tone: "This about finishes me," he groans. "What a juggins I am to be mouldering here! Joggleberry is the celestial limit, what they call in happier lands the pink penultimate. And the frowst on those back benches! Was there ever such a moth-eaten old museum?" (002-001). Leithen replies dryly: "It is the Mother of Parliaments," to which Tommy retorts: "Damned monkey-house" (002-001).

This opening exchange establishes Parliament as simultaneously august and ridiculous — an institution of enormous constitutional weight that its own members find stifling. Tommy promptly abandons it for adventure in Central Asia, while Leithen remains, conducting his secret investigation from within its precincts.

Parliamentary sitting patterns drive the novel's timeline. Leithen notes at the start that "Whitsuntide that year came very late, and I was glad of the fortnight's rest, for Parliament and the Law Courts had given me a busy time" (004-001). Later, "the late sitting of Parliament kept me in London after the end of the term" (009-066), extending the period during which the conspiracy can play out. Parliament's calendar, like its culture, is both framework and friction.

The House of Commons also provides the setting for several key conversations, particularly Leithen's recruitment of [[chapman|Chapman]], the Labour member, whom he finds "in the smoking-room" (005-007). Chapman's Parliamentary identity is central to his character: he is a "Labour Member" whose "bluff independence" Leithen values, and whose political instincts — suspicion of "this last outrage of Capitalism" (009-064) — colour his interpretation of the Powerhouse conspiracy.

At the novel's close, Chapman wants "a public exposure of Lumley, a big trial, a general ferreting out of secret agents, the whole winding up with a speech in Parliament by himself" (009-064). Leithen dissuades him, recognising that Parliament's public forum is precisely the wrong instrument for a truth that cannot be proved.

## Key Associates

- **[[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]]** — Barrister and MP; conducts his investigation while maintaining his Parliamentary duties
- **[[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]]** — MP for the family seat in Yorkshire; finds Parliament intolerably dull and escapes it for adventure
- **[[chapman|Chapman]]** — Labour MP; Leithen's ally, whose political instincts both help and complicate the investigation
- **[[member-of-parliament|Member of Parliament]]** — The role that provides Leithen his social and investigative cover

## Thematic Significance

Parliament in *The Powerhouse* represents the public, procedural face of power — exactly the kind of institutional machinery that Lumley dismisses with contempt. In the Hampshire library, Lumley tells Leithen: "The methods of Parliament — pardon me — would disgrace any board of directors. Our rulers pretend to buy expert knowledge, but they never pay the price for it that a business man would pay" (004-016). This is Lumley's indictment of democratic governance: slow, amateur, incapable of matching the ruthless efficiency of his private conspiracy.

Yet Parliament also produces the men who defeat Lumley. Leithen is a Member of Parliament; Chapman is a Labour MP; Tommy holds a family seat. The novel does not reject Parliament as an institution — it suggests rather that Parliament's strength lies in the character of its members, not in its procedures. When those members act outside Parliamentary channels, they succeed where institutional processes would fail.
