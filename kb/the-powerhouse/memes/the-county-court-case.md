# The County Court Case

## Overview

The County Court Case is the inciting event of John Buchan's *The Powerhouse*, occurring on the 21st of May. It is a routine taxicab accident lawsuit heard in South London, in which [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] serves as junior counsel. Though seemingly mundane, the case introduces Leithen to the butler Tuke and, indirectly, to the Lumley conspiracy.

## Context

Leithen, a barrister who rarely handled County Court work, took the case to oblige a client firm. As he recounts: "It was an ordinary taxicab accident, and, as the solicitors for the company were good clients of mine, and the regular county-court junior was ill in bed, I took the case to oblige them" (PN 165).

The facts were straightforward: "An empty taxicab, proceeding slowly on the right side of the road and hooting decorously at the corners, had been run into by a private motorcar, which had darted down a side street" (PN 165). The offending vehicle belonged to a Mr. [[julius-pavia|Julius Pavia]], a retired East India merchant living at the White Lodge in Blackheath. At the time of the accident, the car "had been occupied by his butler" (PN 166).

The butler, [[tuke|Tuke]], was the sole witness for the defense. Leithen describes him as "a tall man, with a very long, thin face, and a jaw the two parts of which seemed scarcely to fit" (PN 167). Tuke claimed he was delivering a message from his master to a passenger on the Continental express from Victoria -- the 11 a.m. train, which was the very train by which [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] had traveled.

## Significance

This case is crucial because it represents Leithen's first unwitting brush with the Powerhouse network. Tuke was not an ordinary butler; Leithen found him "very unlike the conventional butler, much liker one of those successful financiers whose portraits you see in the picture papers" (PN 169). Leithen felt "a most intense and unreasoning dislike to that bland butler" and admitted to losing his temper during cross-examination.

The timing is significant: the accident occurred on 8 May, "the very day that Tommy left London" (PN 171). Leithen later realizes that the "errand" to meet an "American gentleman, Mr. Wright Davies" at Victoria was likely connected to Lumley's surveillance of Pitt-Heron's associates. The case also introduced Leithen to the name Pavia, prompting him to investigate the White Lodge, which would later lead him to the crucial [[the-wedgwood-plaques-discovery|Wedgwood plaques discovery]].
