# Court of Appeal

## Overview

The Court of Appeal is the second-highest court in the English legal hierarchy, sitting below the House of Lords (later the Supreme Court) and above the High Court. In Edwardian London, it served as the primary appellate tribunal for both civil and criminal cases, hearing appeals from the High Court and the County Courts. It was staffed by the Master of the Rolls and several Lords Justices of Appeal.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The narrator, [[edward-leithen]], has a busy legal practice at the Temple and appears before the Court of Appeal in his professional capacity. During the tense weeks of June when he is tracking the Powerhouse conspiracy, Leithen notes that despite his preoccupation he "did his work well" and "was twice complimented by the Court of Appeal" (PN 664). This detail underscores Leithen's professional competence and the normal, prestigious legal career that forms the backdrop to his amateur detective work.

## Historical Context

The Court of Appeal was established by the Judicature Acts of 1873--1875, which restructured the English court system. By the Edwardian era it was a well-established institution, handling a heavy caseload of civil appeals. A barrister complimented by the Court of Appeal in this period had received a mark of judicial recognition that would significantly enhance his professional reputation -- fitting for Leithen, who later becomes Solicitor-General and eventually reaches the Woolsack.
