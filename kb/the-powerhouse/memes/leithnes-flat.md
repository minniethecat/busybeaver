# Leithen's Flat

## Overview

[[sir-edward-leithen]]'s flat, located in Down Street, Mayfair, serves as his London residence and as the setting for the novel's climactic siege in Chapter IX. It is here that Leithen makes his last stand against [[andrew-lumley]]'s network.

## In *The Powerhouse*

The flat becomes a fortress when Leithen realises he is being systematically isolated by Lumley's agents. His telephone line is cut, his valet Waters fails to return from an errand, and Leithen declares: "The state of siege had begun" [009-100]. He summons his ally [[chapman]] for reinforcement, and together they barricade themselves inside, preparing for the assault.

The climactic confrontation unfolds within the flat's rooms. Lumley's men force their way in while Chapman holds them off in the dining room. Leithen engages in his own desperate fight, knowing that capture means being taken away in Lumley's waiting motorcar. The arrival of two policemen — "good lads from Wensleydale" whom Chapman had alerted on his way — turns the tide, and the siege ends with Leithen's victory. The flat, a gentleman's private space transformed into a battleground, embodies the novel's central theme: the penetration of civilised spaces by hidden menace.
