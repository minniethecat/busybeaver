# Bond Street

## Overview

Bond Street is a prestigious shopping street in Mayfair, London, renowned since the 18th century for its luxury retailers, art dealers, antique shops, and jewellers. In *The Powerhouse*, it serves as a point of comparison for the extraordinary opulence of [[andrew-lumley]]'s private residence.

## In the Novel

Bond Street is referenced in Chapter II during Leithen's visit to [[andrew-lumley]]'s flat at [[the-albany]], accompanied by [[jenkinson]], the Wedgwood expert. The expedition is ostensibly to examine a pair of [[wedgwood-plaques|Wedgwood plaques]] from the Wentworth sale, but Leithen's real purpose is to investigate the man who provided a character reference for the accused spy in the [[the-county-court-case|County Court case]].

Upon entering Lumley's rooms, Leithen is struck by the sheer accumulation of treasure:

> "My companion had not exaggerated when he described the place as full of treasures. It was far more like the shop of a Bond Street art-dealer than a civilised dwelling. The hall was crowded with Japanese armour and lacquer cabinets. One room was lined from floor to ceiling with good pictures, mostly seventeenth-century Dutch, and had enough Chippendale chairs to accommodate a public meeting." (Ch. II, PN 2)

The Bond Street comparison is carefully chosen. A Bond Street art dealer's shop in the Edwardian period represented the pinnacle of the London art market -- a space designed to display objects of the highest quality and price. By likening Lumley's home to such a shop, Buchan signals both the scale of Lumley's wealth and something faintly unsettling about his taste. A home, the description implies, should not feel like a showroom. The aesthetic excess hints at Lumley's deeper character: a collector and manipulator of beautiful things, whether Wedgwood plaques, Japanese armour, or -- as the novel reveals -- human beings.

The detail about "enough Chippendale chairs to accommodate a public meeting" underscores the almost absurd abundance of Lumley's collection. Thomas Chippendale's furniture was, even in 1913, among the most coveted and expensive in the world.

## Historical Context

Bond Street in the early 20th century was already synonymous with London's luxury trade. Old Bond Street (the southern section, developed in the 1680s) and New Bond Street (the northern extension, developed from the 1720s) together formed a continuous thoroughfare of exclusive shops. Fine art dealers, such as Agnew's (established 1817), were a prominent feature, alongside jewellers, tailors, and perfumers. The street's clientele included aristocrats, industrial magnates, and wealthy collectors -- precisely the kind of person Andrew Lumley cultivated as his public persona.

The reference to "seventeenth-century Dutch" paintings also reflects contemporary taste: the Edwardian period saw a strong market for Dutch Golden Age masters, collected avidly by figures such as the American financier J.P. Morgan.
