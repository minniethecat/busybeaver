# Piccadilly

Piccadilly is one of London's principal thoroughfares, running from Piccadilly Circus to Hyde Park Corner. In the novel, it appears repeatedly as the setting for surveillance, pursuit, and the texture of Edwardian London life.

## A London Landmark

Leithen invokes Piccadilly as a symbol of London's ceaseless motion: "They say that everybody turns up in time at the corner of Piccadilly Circus if you wait long enough" [003-002]. This casual reference establishes Piccadilly as the city's central crossroads.

## Under Watch

As the Powerhouse conspiracy closes around Leithen, Piccadilly becomes the terrain of surveillance. Walking from his flat in [[down-street|Down Street]], he observes: "I saw a man enter from the Piccadilly end, while another moved across the Hertford Street opening" [004-050]. The watchers station themselves at the Piccadilly approaches, turning the familiar street into a gauntlet. Leithen notes that his flat lay "in the neighbourhood of Piccadilly and Regent Street" [005-021], ground zero for the network of spies.

## The Flight

Piccadilly features prominently in the novel's climactic chase sequence. Leithen's flight from the Powerhouse brings him through the street at multiple points. "Once in Piccadilly there could be no doubt about it" [007-032] — the pursuit is unmistakable. After his ordeal, "I found myself in Piccadilly in the wholesome company of my kind" [008-057], the familiar street now a symbol of safety regained.

The ordinary rhythms of the street — "the water-carts were sprinkling Piccadilly" [007-015] — provide a stark contrast to the mortal danger Leithen faces, a juxtaposition of everyday London life with the hidden war of the Powerhouse.
