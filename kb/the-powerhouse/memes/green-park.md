# Green Park

## Overview

Green Park is one of the eight Royal Parks of London, a 40-acre triangular expanse of grassland and mature trees situated between [[hyde-park]] and [[st-jamess-park|St. James's Park]], bounded by [[piccadilly]] to the north and Constitution Hill to the south. In *The Powerhouse*, Green Park serves as a critical waypoint in Leithen's flight from [[andrew-lumley]]'s agents, a seeming sanctuary in the heart of the city.

## In the Novel

Green Park appears in two tense passages during the novel's climactic pursuit sequence.

**The Piccadilly Chase (Ch. IX).** After crossing [[piccadilly]] and narrowly avoiding a car that swerves to strike him, Leithen reaches the park's boundary:

> "I jolly nearly shared the fate of Chapman. A car which seemed about to draw up at a club door suddenly swerved across the street, and I had to dash to an island to escape it. It was no occasion to hesitate, so, dodging a bus and missing a motor bicycle by a hair's breadth, I rushed across the remaining distance and reached the railings of the Green Park." (Ch. IX, PN 9)

But the park offers no real safety. A group of workmen with tools moves toward him from the kerb. A pavement artist, who had appeared to be a cripple, scrambles to his feet and approaches. A well-dressed man speaks to a policeman and nods in Leithen's direction. The park, supposedly a place of leisure and refuge, becomes a trap full of Lumley's disguised operatives.

Leithen does not wait: "I took to my heels and ran for my life down Grosvenor Place."

**The Evening Walk (Ch. X).** In a calmer moment, after the confrontation at [[belgrave-square]], Leithen again enters the park:

> "I left Belgrave Square about a quarter to eight and retraced my steps along the route which for me that afternoon had been so full of tremors. I was still being watched -- a little observation told me that -- but I would not be interfered with, provided my way lay in a certain direction. So completely without nervousness was I that at the top of Constitution Hill I struck into the Green Park and kept to the grass till I emerged into Piccadilly, opposite Devonshire House." (Ch. X, PN 10)

This second passage through Green Park shows Leithen's transformed state. No longer fleeing, he walks calmly across the grass, even stopping to greet acquaintances who assume from his clothes that he has "just returned from a day in the country." The park, which had earlier been a site of terror, has become merely a pleasant route home.

## Historical Context

Green Park, originally called Upper St. James's Park, was enclosed by Charles II in 1668 and opened to the public in the 18th century. Unlike its neighbours -- [[hyde-park]] with the Serpentine and St. James's Park with its lake -- Green Park has no water features. In the Edwardian period, its location between Buckingham Palace, the residences of Mayfair, and the clubs of Piccadilly made it a natural thoroughfare for the well-dressed Londoners Buchan describes. The railings mentioned in the novel were a prominent feature until their removal during the Second World War.
