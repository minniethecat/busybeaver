# The Times

*The Times* is London's newspaper of record, founded in 1785. By the Edwardian period in which *The Powerhouse* is set, it was the dominant voice of the British Establishment — widely read by the governing classes, respected for its foreign coverage, and known colloquially as "the Thunderer" for the weight of its editorial pronouncements.

## Role in the Novel

*The Times* appears at two crucial narrative moments, both serving as the official public record against which the novel's secret history unfolds.

The first reference is oblique but telling. When [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] first meets [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] at the Hampshire country house in [[ch03]], Lumley presents himself as a cultivated amateur with strong views on defence matters. Leithen observes: "I could fancy him inditing letters to *The Times* on national service" (004-021). This is the mask Lumley presents to the world — the concerned citizen, the letter-writer, the gentleman amateur whose opinions appear in the paper of record, indistinguishable from a dozen other retired colonels and public-spirited peers.

The second reference is far weightier. After Lumley's sudden death, Leithen reads the news in *The Times* next morning (009-062). The paper publishes a two-column obituary celebrating Lumley as "a man who might have done anything he pleased in public life, but had chosen to give to a small coterie of friends what was due to the country" (009-062). The obituary praises his "wit and learning, his amazing connoisseurship, his social gifts, his personal charm" and calls him "the finest type of cultivated amateur, a Beckford with more than Beckford's wealth and none of his folly" (009-063).

This encomium is deeply ironic. Leithen knows that Lumley was the mastermind of a vast criminal conspiracy — "a Napoleon who left no Marshals behind him" (009-064). Yet *The Times*, the voice of the Establishment, has been utterly fooled. Lumley's mask was so perfect that the official record of his life is a hagiography. The obituary becomes the novel's final, quiet indictment of institutional blindness: the paper of record records a fiction.

## Historical Context

In the Edwardian period, *The Times* was at the height of its influence. Under the editorship of George Earle Buckle (1884-1912) and then Geoffrey Dawson (1912-1919), it was the essential reading of the governing class. Its letters page carried the opinions of statesmen and scholars; its obituaries were a form of national canonisation. Buchan himself was a frequent contributor, and his familiarity with the paper's tone and readership gives the novel's references their satirical edge.

## Thematic Significance

*The Times* in the novel represents the public narrative — the version of reality that society agrees upon. Lumley understood this narrative perfectly and used it as camouflage. The fact that the paper's obituary so thoroughly misreads him is not a failure of journalism but a demonstration of his thesis: civilisation runs on a "silent compact" (004-015) of shared assumptions, and the institutions that uphold that compact — from newspapers to police forces — are intrinsically incapable of seeing what lies outside it.

The obituary also serves as Leithen's shield. He chooses not to expose Lumley, partly from "honest compunction" — Lumley "had had, as he expressed it, a good run for his money" (009-064) — and partly because "no one would have believed me unless I produced proofs, and these proofs were not to be made public" (009-064). The Establishment narrative, once set, is self-reinforcing. Leithen knows better than to fight it.
