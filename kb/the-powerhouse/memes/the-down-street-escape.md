# The Down Street Escape

## Overview

The Down Street Escape is a tense moment in John Buchan's *The Powerhouse* when [[edward-leithen|Edward Leithen]] departs his flat at Down Street under hostile surveillance, initiating the final phase of his confrontation with [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]] and the Powerhouse conspiracy.

## Context

Leithen lived at Down Street in Mayfair, and it served as his base of operations throughout the investigation (PN 146, 618). After Chapman was ambushed and Leithen's written statement stolen, the Powerhouse escalated to a siege of Leithen's residence. Leithen discovered his telephone line had been cut and his manservant Waters, sent on an errand, had never returned. He reflected: "The state of siege had begun" (PN 925).

Faced with the need to deliver his evidence to Scotland Yard and the Embassy, Leithen summoned his chauffeur Stagg and his car. He recognized the gravity of the moment: "It was a tremendous relief to go down the stairs and emerge into the hot daylight. I gave Stagg his instructions, and lay back in the closed car with a curious fluttering sense of anticipation. I had begun the last round in the wild game" (PN 932).

## Significance

The escape is significant for its revelation of how thoroughly Lumley had penetrated Leithen's world. At the corner of Down Street, Leithen spotted "a man at the corner of Down Street who seemed to peer curiously at the car. He was doubtless one of my watchers" (PN 932). Worse, Leithen soon realized the man driving was not his real chauffeur but an impostor who had replaced Stagg.

This substitution confirmed Leithen's fears: Lumley's agents were prepared to use lethal force. Leithen knew that if he approached the Embassy directly, "I should share the fate of Chapman. Worse, for from me they would not merely snatch the letter. What I had once written I could write again, and if they wished to ensure my silence it must be by more drastic methods" (PN 930). The escape thus marks the transition from investigation to physical survival.

The Down Street location grounds the story in the geography of Edwardian Mayfair, a fashionable quarter where the civilized veneer of London society masked the operations of Lumley's criminal intelligence network. Leithen's narrow escape from his own doorstep underscores the theme of danger lurking beneath the surface of respectable London life.
