# Konalevsky

Konalevsky is a clerk at the Russian Embassy in London. Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his testimony provides [[tommy-deloraine|Tommy Deloraine]] and [[edward-leithen|Leithen]] with their first solid lead about [[charles-pitt-heron|Charles Pitt-Heron]]'s disappearance -- specifically, that Pitt-Heron had obtained a high-powered diplomatic passport and was heading for Moscow.

## Overview

Konalevsky is one of the minor functionaries of the Russian Embassy, a name found on a calling card discovered by Pitt-Heron's valet, Tomlin, in the waistcoat pocket of Pitt-Heron's dress clothes. The discovery of this card sets in motion the investigation that ultimately leads Leithen and Tommy to uncover the full scope of [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s conspiracy.

## Role in the Novel

Konalevsky's role, though entirely offstage, is crucial to the plot of [[0144-the-Powerhouse-30c98a18-ch006|Chapter I: Beginning of the Wild-Goose Chase]]. Tommy Deloraine, searching for any clue to explain Pitt-Heron's sudden and unexplained disappearance, visits the Russian Embassy after Tomlin finds Konalevsky's card. There, he locates Konalevsky and questions him.

Konalevsky explains that he had visited Pitt-Heron two days before the disappearance to deliver a letter from a senior Embassy official -- a letter that was in fact one of the special diplomatic passports that Embassies issue to their friends, "a higher-powered sort than the ordinary make" (001-006). Crucially, Konalevsky adds that he "gathered from something he had heard that Charles was aiming for Moscow" (001-006). The senior Embassy official who had sent the letter had already departed for New York, leaving Konalevsky as the only available source of this information.

This testimony crystallises Tommy's determination to pursue Pitt-Heron. Armed with the knowledge that Pitt-Heron was heading for Moscow with a diplomatic passport, Tommy resolves to "hale him back from Moscow, even if I have to pretend he's an escaped lunatic" (001-006). This decision launches the Central Asian pursuit that occupies much of the novel's action.

## Significance

Konalevsky represents a pattern that recurs throughout *The Powerhouse*: the humble functionary who, without any awareness of the larger conspiracy unfolding around him, provides a piece of information that proves essential to unravelling it. He is the mirror image of [[hewins|Hewins]], the butler who later helps Leithen at the same Embassy -- both are servants of the diplomatic establishment whose professionalism and routine helpfulness become instruments of justice.

Konalevsky also illustrates the novel's reliance on the infrastructure of international diplomacy as a field of action. The Russian Embassy, with its clerks and passports and privileged status, is a node in the web of information that Lumley exploits and that Leithen and Tommy must navigate.
