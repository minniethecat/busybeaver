# My Bank

## Overview

"My Bank" is a colloquial reference to the Bank of England, used by [[andrew-lumley]] during his dinner-table exposition of the fragility of civilization in *The Powerhouse*. The phrase encapsulates Lumley's rhetorical style -- a tone of cultivated familiarity that masks his radical contempt for the institutions he claims to value.

## In the Novel

The reference appears during the pivotal dinner scene at [[high-ashes]] (Ch. IV, PN 4). After Leithen and his host have dined on soles, chicken, strawberries, and fine wine, their conversation turns to weighty matters. Lumley, in what Leithen interprets as learned dinner-table talk, begins to expound on the nature of civilization and its dependence on stable foundations.

Lumley takes the gold standard as his illustrative example:

> "Take one little instance," he said. "We are a commercial world, and have built up a great system of credit. Without our cheques and bills of exchange and currency the whole of our life would stop. But credit only exists because behind it we have a standard of value. My Bank of England notes are worthless paper unless I can get sovereigns for them if I choose." (Ch. IV)

The possessive "My" carries a deliberate irony. Lumley speaks as if he possesses the Bank, while in fact he seeks to destroy the entire system of which it is the keystone. His argument -- that civilization rests on fragile conventions which a sufficiently determined will could shatter -- foreshadows the revelation that he leads the [[the-powerhouse-org|Powerhouse]], an organisation dedicated to precisely such destruction.

Lumley continues by acknowledging that "there are economists who say that the world could be run on a pure credit basis, with no metal currency at the back of it," before dismissing this as practically impossible without converting "the whole of the world's stupidity to their economic faith." This casual dismissal of radical economic thought ironically mirrors his own secret project -- not reforming the existing order but annihilating it entirely.

## Historical Context

The Bank of England, founded in 1694 and located on Threadneedle Street in the City of London, was at the heart of the British and global financial system in the Edwardian period. The gold standard, to which Lumley refers, was the foundation of international trade and finance. Britain had adopted the gold standard de facto in 1717 and formally in 1816, and it remained in place until its suspension at the outbreak of the First World War in 1914 -- shortly before *The Powerhouse* was published in 1916.

Lumley's argument about the fragility of financial conventions would have resonated with contemporary readers who had witnessed the financial disruptions of the war's early years.
