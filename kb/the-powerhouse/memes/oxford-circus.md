# Oxford Circus

## Overview

Oxford Circus is one of London's most famous intersections, where Oxford Street crosses Regent Street in the heart of the West End. Designed by John Nash as part of the early 19th-century development of Regent Street, it was -- and remains -- a hub of commerce, traffic, and pedestrian movement. In *The Powerhouse*, it becomes a site of danger.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Oxford Circus features in Chapter VI during Leithen and Chapman's perilous walk home after the Antioch Street ambush. As they traverse the area, Leithen is nearly caught: "I was nearly caught before we reached Oxford Circus. There was a front of a big shop rebuilding, and the usual wooden barricade with a gate" (PN 865). In the press of the crowd, Leithen is pushed against the gate, which gives way, and he is pressed inward into darkness. Only Chapman's quick action -- his "burly shoulder" forcing the gap -- saves Leithen from being spirited away. The incident demonstrates how even London's most crowded public spaces can become dangerous when Lumley's agents are active.

## Historical Context

Oxford Circus was built between 1813 and 1820 to John Nash's designs, with the four quadrants originally featuring uniform stuccoed facades. By the Edwardian period, it was a thriving retail junction served by the Central London Railway's Oxford Circus Station (opened 1900). The rebuilding work described in the novel -- a "big shop rebuilding" with "wooden barricade" -- was typical of the ongoing development of the West End's commercial architecture during this period.
