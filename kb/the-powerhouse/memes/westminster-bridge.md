# Westminster Bridge

## Overview

Westminster Bridge is a major road and foot bridge crossing the River Thames in central London, connecting Westminster on the north bank with Lambeth on the south. The present bridge, built in 1862, is a seven-arch cast-iron structure designed by Thomas Page. It offers views of the Houses of Parliament and Big Ben that have become iconic symbols of London. In *The Powerhouse*, it provides the stage for the novel's quiet, resonant conclusion.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Westminster Bridge features in the closing moments of Chapter VIII, after Leithen has confronted Lumley and visited Chapman in hospital. Walking home, Leithen crosses the bridge and experiences a moment of reflection: "As I crossed Westminster Bridge on my way home the night express to the Continent rumbled over the river. I wondered if Lumley was on board or if he had taken one of the other ways of which he had spoken" (PN 1165). The image is powerful and ambiguous: the rumbling train, the dark river beneath, and Leithen's unresolved question about whether Lumley has fled to Paris or chosen death. The bridge -- connecting Westminster, seat of law and government, with the anonymous vastness of South London -- mirrors the novel's central tension between order and chaos, between the surface of civilization and the dark currents beneath.

## Historical Context

The present Westminster Bridge is the second on the site, replacing an earlier stone bridge built in 1750. The 1862 cast-iron structure was painted green -- a colour it retains today -- to match the leather seats in the House of Commons. At the time of the novel, the bridge was a major artery for trams, horse-drawn vehicles, and the growing number of motorcars. The "night express to the Continent" Leithen hears would have been crossing one of the river bridges further east, but the sound carried across the water, reaching Leithen on Westminster Bridge as a metonym for Lumley's departure from England.
