# The Romanovs

## Overview

The Romanovs were the ruling imperial dynasty of Russia from 1613 until the Russian Revolution of 1917. In *The Powerhouse*, [[andrew-lumley]] references them as an example of how the established order — "civilisation" — efficiently suppresses revolutionary threats through international cooperation among police forces.

## In *The Powerhouse*

Lumley invokes the Romanovs during his dinner-table exposition of the Powerhouse's philosophy. He argues that civilisation always wins against its enemies because it knows how to organise: "Half a dozen crazy Russian *intellectuels* in Geneva conspire to upset the Romanovs and are hunted down by the police of Europe" [008-041]. The passage is designed to show the hopelessness of amateur revolution — national governments and their police forces close ranks against any threat to the status quo.

But Lumley's point is not to celebrate this efficiency. He uses the Romanovs to illustrate how parochial his predecessors have been. The old revolutionaries failed because they were local where civilisation was global. The Powerhouse, he implies, will succeed precisely because it has learned from this failure: it will become a global counter-compact, an international conspiracy matching the international scope of the governments it opposes. Writing in 1913-1916, Buchan placed this argument in the mouth of Lumley just before the Romanov dynasty's actual collapse — a historical irony that would have been apparent to the novel's 1916 readers.
