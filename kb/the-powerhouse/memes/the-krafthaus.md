# The Krafthaus

*Krafthaus* — literally "powerhouse" in German — is the name by which European intelligence services knew [[andrew-lumley|Andrew Lumley]]'s secret organisation before [[sir-edward-leithen|Sir Edward Leithen]] ever heard the English equivalent. The word is a key to the novel's international dimension: Lumley's conspiracy is not merely a London affair but a threat recognised, however dimly, by police and intelligence services across the continent.

## The Name's Discovery

The word *Krafthaus* surfaces through [[mr-macgillivray|Macgillivray]], Leithen's contact at Scotland Yard, who shares the results of inquiries made by a German correspondent. Macgillivray's report is the pivot on which the novel turns from suspicion to confirmation: the Powerhouse is not Leithen's private obsession but a phenomenon that has left traces across Europe.

The report traces the word's appearances:

- In "some documents which were seized" relating to "a curious affair which a year before had excited Europe" — an incident involving "the Slav States of Austria and an Italian Students' Union" that "threatened at one time to be dangerous" (005-014)
- In "a sonnet of a poet-anarchist who shot himself in the slums of Antwerp" (005-014)
- In "the last ravings of more than one criminal" (005-014)
- In "the extraordinary testament of Professor M——, of Jena, who, at the age of thirty-seven, took his life after writing a strange, mystical message to his fellow citizens" (005-014)

## Significance

The discovery of *Krafthaus* transforms the novel's stakes. Before Macgillivray's report, Leithen has only Lumley's own word for the Powerhouse's existence — a word spoken at a dinner party in a tone that could be dismissed as eccentric philosophising. After the report, the Powerhouse has a history, a trail of documents and suicides, a European reach. The German name localises the concept: this is not a purely English phenomenon but an international one whose "motive power" is, tellingly, English (005-014).

Macgillivray's informant concludes with a judgment that becomes the novel's thesis: "if this *Krafthaus* could be found, the key would be discovered to the most dangerous secret organisation in the world" (005-014). The word itself thus functions as a kind of incantation — to name it is to take the first step toward finding and destroying it.

## Key Passages

> I had asked about the phrase, the "Powerhouse." Well, he had come across it in the letter of a German friend, a private letter, in which the writer gave the results of his inquiries into a curious affair which a year before had excited Europe. (005-014)

> Macgillivray's correspondent concluded by saying that, in his opinion, if this *Krafthaus* could be found, the key would be discovered to the most dangerous secret organisation in the world. He added that he had some reason to believe that the motive power of the concern was English. (005-014)
