# Law

The professional world of the narrator, Sir Edward Leithen, and the novel's test case for the fragility of civilisation. As a barrister, Leithen believes in law as a solid foundation; his encounter with the Powerhouse forces him to recognise how much of that solidity is an illusion.

## Definition

In the novel, law operates on two levels. On the surface, it is the familiar apparatus of courts, police, and statutes — the machinery by which civilisation protects itself. Leithen's professional identity is bound up in this apparatus: he is "the ordinary educated Englishman" whose sort "gravitate to the Bar" (003-033).

But Lumley exposes law's hidden dependency. Law is effective not because of its own force but because of the "silent compact" that underpins it: "What value would your police be if every criminal could find a sanctuary across the Channel, or your law courts if no other tribunal recognised their decisions?" (003-048). Law, in this view, is not a foundation but a convention — a set of rules that work only because everyone agrees they should.

## Origin in the Text

Law is present from the novel's opening, embedded in Leithen's narrative voice and professional habits of mind. He approaches the mystery of Pitt-Heron's disappearance as a lawyer would — gathering evidence, testing hypotheses, looking for motives. But the Powerhouse consistently operates outside the legal framework he trusts.

The critical moment comes in [[ch07]] and [[ch08]], when Leithen finds himself hunted through London — unable to call on the police, forced to operate outside the law he has spent his career serving. The experience enacts Lumley's thesis: law protects only those who are within the "ring which we call civilisation" (003-056).

## Analysis

Buchan's treatment of law reflects a distinctively Edwardian legal consciousness. Leithen is not a crusading lawyer or a courtroom dramatist; he is a barrister — a member of a profession defined as much by social status and unspoken codes as by legal expertise. His faith in law is not naive (he is too intelligent for that) but institutional: he believes in the system because he is part of it.

The novel's critique of law is not radical but conservative. It does not argue that law is unjust or oppressive; it argues that law is fragile — that it depends on conditions (consensus, goodwill, the absence of organised alternatives) that cannot be taken for granted. This is a lawyer's critique, not a revolutionary's: it asks not whether law is good but whether it is sufficient.
