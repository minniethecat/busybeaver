# The Island

A mysterious river island in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," covered with strange plants and [[servant|serving]] as the gateway to the magic country — a testing ground for those who seek the Hair Tree.

## Overview

The island appears in the middle of [[the-river|the great river]] as [[rupert|Rupert]] journeys toward the magic country. It is "covered with plants of various kinds" (003-112), and its fruit possesses protective magic. The island functions as a way-station between the ordinary world and the fully magical realm — not yet the Hair Tree itself, but the last point of relative safety before entering true danger. In an earlier episode, the [[the-brook|water]]-maiden was brought to this same island by a tortoise who [[hope|wish]]ed to marry her (003-079, 003-086).

> "The island was covered with plants of various kinds." (003-112)

## In the Narrative

### Rupert's Landing

When [[rupert|Rupert]] first sees the island, "he could not see it very plainly, for the river was wide" (003-112) — the island is veiled, uncertain, only partially visible. He "sprang onto the island, filled his pockets full of plants and fruit" (003-032), and his instinct to gather the protective fruit proves life-saving. The island rewards those who take its gifts seriously.

### The Water-Maiden's Story

The island is also the site of the [[the-brook|water]]-maiden's near-entrapment. A tortoise brought her "already halfway to the island" (003-079), intending to force marriage. "When we arrive at the island, where my armies are [[patience|waiting]]" (003-086), [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]] told her. The island thus carries a double valance: for [[rupert|Rupert]], it is refuge; for the [[the-brook|water]]-maiden, it was a prison. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s places are morally ambivalent — their meaning depends on who approaches them.

## Literary Context

The island as testing-ground echoes classical and medieval romance traditions (Calypso's island, Avalon), where islands are places of [[transformation|transform]]ation that can save or destroy. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] trims the tradition to its essentials: no elaborate description, no mythical backstory, just a river-island with plants — and the moral weight of the choices made there.
