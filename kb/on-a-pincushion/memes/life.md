# Life

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, life is not a passive state but an active achievement — characters must struggle to preserve, extend, or find meaning in their existence.

## Overview

The word "life" echoes through [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales with remarkable frequency — twenty times across the collection — and almost never casually. Life is something that can be *given* ("gives them life for another hundred years," 002-028), *saved* ("you saved my life," 005-250), *owed* ("you owe your life to the nuts," 003-078), or *lost* ("put an end to my life," 003-079). The cumulative effect is a world in which life is always at stake, always conditional, never to be taken for granted.

> "We will work for him all his life." (002-077)

## In the Narrative

### Life as Debt

The most striking pattern across the tales is the framing of life as a debt owed. In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," the protagonist is told "you owe your life to the nuts of the Zirbal-tree" (003-078) — life becomes something that must be repaid through continued courage. In "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," [[jack|Jack]]'s life is repeatedly saved by others, creating a chain of obligation that drives the plot forward. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] suggests that life is fundamentally social: we live because others help us live.

### Life Without Purpose

The sunbeam's complaint in "On a Pincushion" — "it had no object in life" (001-256) — introduces a different dimension: life without purpose is a kind of living death. The sunbeam "never spoke to the birds" and existed in isolation, suggesting that a life without connection and purpose is not fully alive. This is the philosophical core of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment: life requires both external preservation (not [[death|dying]]) and internal meaning (having purpose).

### The Hundred-Year Life

The [[fairy]]'s "[[fear|dread]]ful [[charm]], which gives them life for another hundred years" (002-028) in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" presents a dark variant: extended life as a curse rather than a blessing. The [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s' wearers are bound to labor for the shoemaker, and their extended lifespan is a sentence, not a gift. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] thus distinguishes between mere biological continuation and a life worth living.

## Literary Context

The Victorian period saw intense debate about the nature and value of life — from Darwinian evolution redefining life's origins to industrial labor raising questions about life's quality. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales engage these currents indirectly: the hundred-year laborers echo industrial workers bound to machines, while the sunbeam's purposelessness reflects the existential anxieties of the fin de siècle. Unlike the sanitized "happily ever after" of mainstream [[fairy]] tales, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] insists that life must be actively made meaningful.
