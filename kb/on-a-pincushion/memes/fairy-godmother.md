# Fairy Godmother

A benevolent magical guardian who appears in the tale "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," named [[taboret|Taboret]], who intervenes to protect a young princess from the emotional starvation of a rigid court education.

## Overview

The [[fairy|fairy godmother]] is a classic [[fairy]]-tale figure, but [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[taboret|Taboret]] is distinctive: she is neither a deus ex machina who solves all problems with a wave of her wand, nor a capricious tester of virtue. Instead, she is a careful observer who assesses the situation before acting — she makes herself invisible to watch [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]]'s daily life before deciding on her intervention. (004-011) Her approach combines magical power with maternal concern, reflecting the Victorian ideal of benevolent guardianship.

> Now she had a fairy godmother, named Taboret, whom she loved very dearly, and who was always kind to her.
>
> (004-005)

## In the Narrative

### Taboret's Intervention

[[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]]'s [[fairy|fairy godmother]], [[taboret|Taboret]], is deeply loved by the child. (004-005) Observing that [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] is "never taught any games or given any toys" and passes most of her time in solitary boredom (004-010), [[taboret|Taboret]] decides on a radical intervention. Rather than confronting the court directly, she employs magical craft: creating a "toy Princess" — a mechanical double — to take [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s place at court while [[princess-ursula|the real princess]] is raised in a loving, normal environment. (004-058)

### Moral Character

[[taboret|Taboret]]'s method reveals the story's moral framework. She does not punish the courtiers or expose their cruelty; she simply removes [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] from harm and provides her with genuine nurture. This parallels the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s approach in "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" — benevolent magical beings in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world prefer gentle correction and protection over vengeance or spectacle.

## Literary Context

The [[fairy|fairy godmother]] figure descends from Perrault's *Cendrillon* (Cinderella) and the broader European tradition. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s version is notably a godmother — implying a spiritual and familial bond beyond mere magical patronage. Unlike [[taboret|the fairy godmother]]s of pantomime tradition who appear in showers of sparks, [[taboret|Taboret]] operates through stealth, invisibility, and careful planning, making her more akin to a guardian angel than a stage magician.
