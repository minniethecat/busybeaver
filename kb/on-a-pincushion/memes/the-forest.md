# The Forest

The dark forest that borders the village and defines the threshold between safety and adventure in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. It is the archetypal [[fairy]]-tale forest: dense, dangerous, indifferent to human need, and [[transformation|transform]]ative for those who navigate it successfully. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] enters it to weep and finds a magical hare. The giant's workshop opens beneath it. The forest is the space where the ordinary world ends and the rules of magic begin.

## Geography

The forest is introduced at the very opening of "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]":

> On the border of a large forest there once stood a little village, containing but a few houses, which were small and poor.
>
> — 002-001

It is "large" — large enough that getting lost in it is a real danger, large enough that its interior conceals magical passages and hidden workshops. The forest is "full of birds and animals" (002-003), a [[life|living]] ecosystem that is neither hostile nor nurturing — merely present, vast, and indifferent to human purposes.

A "sheltering tree" within the forest becomes a site of [[transformation|transform]]ation: "the ugliest thing in the whole forest, became the most beautiful" (001-287), marking the forest as a space where appearances deceive and [[transformation|metamorphosis]] is natural law.

## The Forest as Liminal Space

The forest is the classic [[fairy]]-tale **threshold** — neither home nor destination but the passage between. Characters who enter it leave the domestic world behind:

- **[[siegfrid]]** enters the forest to cry alone, in despair over his family's poverty (002-022). What begins as retreat becomes adventure: the forest receives his grief and answers with magic.
- The **[[the-hare|Hare]]** who guides [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] is a forest creature — native to this liminal zone, able to navigate between the ordinary world of the village and the magical underground of the giant's workshop.
- The forest floor **opens**: "right into the middle of the forest. Then the ground opened, and he saw a long dark passage" (002-028). The forest is not merely a setting but an **active threshold** — the ground itself parts to admit the worthy traveler.

> Siegfrid wandered into the forest to cry by himself. Even that was better than crying at home; besides, it was so beautiful there that it seemed as if it must be easier to be brave.
>
> — 002-022

This passage captures the forest's dual nature: it is both escape (from the pain of home) and test (can you be brave?). The beauty of the forest — "so beautiful there" — is not decorative but **functional**: beauty enables courage.

## The Underground Passage

The forest's most significant feature is what lies **beneath** it. The giant's workshop — a vast underground complex of enchanted shoemaking — is accessed through the forest floor. The passage connects two worlds:

| Above Ground | Below Ground |
|-------------|--------------|
| Sunlight, birdsong, beauty | [[darkness|Darkness]], smoke, captivity |
| [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s tears | The children's chains |
| Freedom to wander | [[magic|Magic]]al imprisonment |

When [[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]] emerge from the passage after destroying the boots, "they saw that the sun was shining brightly" (002-053) — the forest above unchanged by the ordeal below, its indifference now reading as mercy.

## Characters Associated

- **[[siegfrid]]** — The forest is his proving ground. He enters as a weeping child, emerges as a hero who has destroyed the instruments of captivity.
- **[[the-hare|The Hare]]** — The forest's native guide, a creature who knows the paths between worlds. It "soon scampered away" (002-037) once its purpose is served — the forest's helper, not its prisoner.
- **[[the-giants-workshop|The Giant]]** — Hides his workshop beneath the forest, using the woods as camouflage for evil. The forest's indifference enables both heroism and villainy — it does not discriminate.

## Literary Significance

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s forest participates in [[the-sea|the deep]] tradition of the European [[fairy]]-tale forest — the Grimms' *Wald*, Perrault's *bois* — while adapting it to her distinctive moral [[dream|vision]]. Her forest is neither the punitive wilderness of Puritan allegory nor the sentimentalized nature of Romantic pastoral. It is a **functional liminal space**: a place where the rules are suspended, where the ground can open, where a crying child can become a hero.

The forest's indifference is its most modern quality. It does not test, judge, or reward — it simply **is**, and what characters make of it reveals their nature. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] finds courage; the giant finds concealment. The same trees shelter both.
