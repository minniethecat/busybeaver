# Kindness

Kindness in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is the virtue that operates beneath the surface of the tales — rarely named, always felt, and capable of crossing the boundary between the magical and mortal worlds.

## Overview

The word "kindness" appears only twice in the corpus, both in the same episode of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]": a character is "[[gratitude|grateful]] to my son for his kindness in offering to make you his wife" (003-079, 003-094). But this scarcity is deceptive. Kindness — the act of helping another without expectation of return — underlies the collection's moral architecture. The [[the-brook|water]]-people's rescue, the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song, the fire-people's aid: all are acts of kindness that ask nothing in return.

> "I have always been grateful to my son for his kindness in offering to make you his wife." (003-079)

## In the Narrative

### Kindness Across Worlds

The son's kindness in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" (003-079) is remarkable because it crosses the boundary between magical and mortal beings. A [[the-brook|water]]-creature offers marriage to a human — not from desire or obligation, but from kindness. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents this inter-species kindness as natural and unremarkable within the story-world, suggesting that kindness transcends the categories (magical/mortal, human/non-human) that otherwise structure the tales.

### The Kindness of Rescue

Beyond the named instances, kindness permeates the collection as the engine of rescue. The [[the-brook|water]]-people cure [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] not from duty but from a kind of fierce compassion. The fire-people save [[jack|Jack]] because they choose to, not because they must. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]] sings the story every night — an act of kindness toward the dead, who would otherwise be forgotten.

### Kindness Without Reward

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s kindness is distinguished by its lack of transactional logic. Characters are kind without expecting repayment, and the narrative rewards kindness indirectly — through plot resolution rather than explicit exchange. This makes kindness the collection's most quietly Christian virtue (compare the Gospel's "do good, expecting nothing in return") in tales otherwise free of religious content.

## Literary Context

Victorian children's literature often rewarded kindness explicitly (the kind sister gets the prince; the cruel sister gets punished). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s more subtle approach — kindness is its own reward, and its narrative benefits are indirect — reflects the [[power|influence]] of the aesthetic movement's distrust of overt moralizing. Kindness in these tales simply *is*, like the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song.
