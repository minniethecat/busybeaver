# Love

The central relational force animating every tale in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. Love in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral universe is never merely romantic — it is the active principle that opposes [[pride|vanity]], breaks [[magic|enchantment]]s, and restores what selfishness has broken. From [[erick|Erick]]'s steadfast devotion to scarred [[lamorna|Lamorna]], to [[jack|Jack]]'s [[kindness|compassion]]ate intervention for star-crossed elemental lovers, to the parental love that frames the collection's deepest [[transformation|transform]]ations, love is the force that makes magic redemptive rather than merely instrumental.

## Definition and Scope

Love in *On a Pincushion* operates across four distinct but interlinked registers:

| Register | Manifestation | Key Example |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Steadfast Love** | Love that persists through rejection and disfigurement | [[erick|Erick]]'s devotion to [[lamorna|Lamorna]] despite her [[pride|vanity]] and scar |
| **Liberating Love** | Love that breaks [[magic|enchantment]]s and restores true form | [[jack|Jack]]'s intervention reuniting [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] and [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] |
| **Transformative Love** | Love that changes the lover, not the beloved | The King's growing coldness toward [[zaire|Zaire]] versus his enduring love for [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] |
| **Selfless Love** | Love that sacrifices for another's good | The [[the-seeds-of-love|seeds of love]] that grow only when planted for another |

## Steadfast Love: Erick and Lamorna

The collection's most fully realized love story is embedded in its frame narrative. [[erick]], a poor fisherman, loves [[lamorna]] from childhood: "when we were children you said you loved me, and promised me to be my wife" (001-015). [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] — her obsessive love of her own reflection — makes her scorn his devotion: "if he spoke to her of his love, she would laugh and turn away" (001-033).

> Lamorna had plenty of other lovers, but as she grew cross and disagreeable they left her one by one, till at last the fisherman Erick alone remained.
>
> — 001-033

[[erick|Erick]]'s love is defined by what it **survives**: rejection, mockery, [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s physical scarring. When she fears "Now he will cease to love me, when he sees how ugly I am" (001-103), [[erick|Erick]] proves otherwise:

> Erick kissed the scar, and told her he loved her all the better for it.
>
> — 001-116

The scar — symbol of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s ordeal and moral growth — becomes the site of love's deepest affirmation. [[erick|Erick]] loves not despite the scar but **because** of what it represents: [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s cure from [[pride|vanity]], her becoming a person capable of receiving love.

## Liberating Love: Jack's Intervention

In "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," love is blocked by elemental incompatibility — [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] of the fire kingdom and [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] of the [[the-brook|water]] kingdom "can't go near each other, lest he should be dried up, or she should be put out" (005-087). Their love is real but impossible within the rules of their world.

[[jack]]'s role is to **mediate**: he carries the Princess's ring, summons the Prince, and journeys to the ice realm as their emissary. His intervention does not create their love but enables it — the mortal child who bridges what elemental nature has separated. This pattern — love requiring a third party's courage to be realized — recurs throughout the collection.

## Transformative Love: Blanchelys and the Rose-Tree

The embedded tale of [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and [[zaire|Zaire]] contrasts true and false love through the symbol of a [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]]. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] loves her husband, her baby, and "next to them, I love my [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]]" (001-157) — her loves are relational, outward-directed. [[zaire|Zaire]]'s "love" is possessive: "'Give me the love of the King's son'" (001-139) — she wants love as property, not as relationship.

The [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]] becomes the test: [[zaire|Zaire]] destroys it out of [[jealousy|envy]], and her husband's love withers as the tree did. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]], whose love is grounded in gratitude and care, finds that "your father will always love you well" (001-210) — love that is given rather than demanded is love that endures.

## The Seeds of Love

The [[the-seeds-of-love|Seeds of Love]] — magical seeds planted in a garden — literalize the collection's central argument about love: it must be planted, tended, and grown. It cannot be seized or demanded. The seeds' magic is conditional on the planter's intent — grown for another, they flourish; grown for oneself, they fail. This agricultural metaphor for love — patient, seasonal, requiring care — is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most original contribution to the [[fairy]]-tale treatment of the theme.

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] writes within a Victorian children's literature tradition that often moralized love into sentimental tropes. Her innovation is to treat love **structurally** rather than decoratively: love is not the reward at the story's end but the mechanism by which [[transformation|transform]]ation occurs. It is the force that:

- Cures [[pride|vanity]] ([[lamorna|Lamorna]])
- Breaks [[magic|enchantment]]s (the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] in [[rupert|Rupert]]'s tale)
- Crosses elemental boundaries ([[princess-pyra|Pyra]] and [[prince-fluvius|Fluvius]])
- Restores the stolen ([[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]], [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection)

In each case, love is not passive feeling but **active intervention** — someone must *do* something for love to work. [[erick|Erick]] must kiss the scar. [[jack|Jack]] must brave the ice realm. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] must seek Love itself when all seems lost.
