# Courage vs. Magic: Earned and Borrowed Heroism

In *On a Pincushion*, heroism takes two distinct forms: the earned courage of characters who act despite fear, and the borrowed power of those who rely on magical intervention. The collection's most interesting moral argument is that only the first kind counts.

## Siegfrid's Earned Courage

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], the protagonist of the collection's longest embedded tale, is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most fully developed hero precisely because he is not magical. A poor shoemaker's son, he walks barefoot into [[darkness|the dark]] passage beneath the forest to rescue [[handa|Handa]] and the other stolen children (002-028). He sacrifices his right eye to the [[owl|Owl]] as payment for the boots needed to burn (002-027). When the old man — the malevolent shoe-seller who has ensnared the village — is finally defeated, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] refuses vengeance: "his punishment will come of itself" (002-045).

Siegfrid's courage operates on a human scale. He is afraid — [[darkness|the dark]] passage is terrifying, the sacrifice of his eye agonizing — but he acts anyway. His motivation is not glory but love for [[handa|Handa]], and his journey through the enchanted forest, the [[spell]]-shop, and the underground vault is a pilgrimage of incremental courage. Each obstacle he overcomes prepares him for the next.

> He cried at the thought of losing one of his bright brown eyes. "But I would give both my eyes for Handa," he said to himself, "only then I should not be able to see her at all."
>
> (002-038)

> Siegfrid summoned all his courage, and sitting on the edge of the hole dropped gently into the passage, and ran down it.
>
> (002-049)

## Magical Shortcuts Elsewhere

In contrast, many of the collection's other tales resolve through external magical agency:

- In "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," the [[the-water-people|water people]] cure [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] by stealing her reflection — she is passive, the beneficiary of benevolent intervention (001-022)
- In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," [[taboret|Taboret]] rescues [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] by substitution — [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] does nothing to save herself (004-058)
- In "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]," the magical seeds grow without human effort
- In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," [[trevina|Trevina]]'s rescue depends entirely on [[rupert|Rupert]]'s arrival and the Hair Tree's rods — she waits for years as a tigress, unable to act (003-092)

These tales are not failures of imagination; they serve different moral purposes. The [[the-water-people|water people]]'s intervention in "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" models community care. [[taboret|Taboret]]'s substitution in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" is satire, not heroism. But the pattern is clear: in tale after tale, the resolution arrives from outside, through magic or [[wisdom|wise]] beings, not from the protagonist's own action.

## The Collection's Moral Hierarchy

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] seems to be building an implicit hierarchy of moral agency:

1. **Earned courage** ([[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]) — the highest form, combining fear, action, and sacrifice
2. **Benevolent intervention** ([[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]) — good but external, the beneficiary learns but does not earn
3. **Passive rescue** ([[trevina|Trevina]]) — [[patience|waiting]] to be saved, the lowest form of resolution still framed as happy

This hierarchy is never stated directly — [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] is too subtle a writer for that — but it emerges from the narrative structures themselves. Tales that end in marriage through magical rescue feel lighter, slighter, than "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," which earns its happy ending through accumulated acts of courage and loss.

## The Storytellers' Perspective

The frame narrative adds another layer. [[the-brooch|The Brooch]], Shawl-pin, and Pin are objects — they cannot act, only tell. Their choice of tales may reflect an awareness of their own passivity: by telling stories of agency (and its absence), they participate vicariously in the courage they lack. [[the-shawl-pin|The Shawl-pin]], having "seen better days" in aristocratic [[servant|service]], may value [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s earned status over inherited rank.

## Conclusion

"[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" stands as the collection's moral center because it insists that courage must be earned. In a [[fairy]]-tale world full of magical shortcuts, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] makes the case — quietly, through narrative rather than sermon — that sacrifice and action matter more than any [[magic|enchantment]].
