# The Kiss on the Scar

The emotional climax of "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" — the moment when [[erick|Erick]], the fisherman who has loved [[lamorna|Lamorna]] since childhood, kisses the scar on her face and tells her he loves her all the better for it. In a collection full of magic — [[magic-shoes|enchanted shoes]], fire-[[fairy|fairies]], talking birds, [[transformation|transform]]ative seeds — this is the most radical event: a purely human gesture that accomplishes what magic cannot. The kiss on the scar is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s argument that **love is [[the-sea|the deep]]est magic**, and that its power is proven not in beauty but in its opposite.

## Context

[[lamorna|Lamorna]] has been cured of her [[pride|vanity]] — the obsessive self-love that made her scorn [[erick|Erick]]'s [[love|devotion]]. Her [[the-theft-of-the-reflection|reflection has been stolen]] by the [[water-people|water people]] as punishment, then restored after her ordeal. But the cure has left a mark: a scar on her face, the physical remainder of her moral [[transformation|transform]]ation.

She stands before [[erick|Erick]], afraid. She fears that he loved only her beauty — the beauty that [[pride|vanity]] prized and that the scar has marred. She says "don't, dear [[erick|Erick]]" — a plea that is also a test, an invitation to prove her fear wrong.

## The Event

[[erick|Erick]]'s response is not words but action:

> Erick kissed the scar, and told her he loved her all the better for it.
>
> — 001-116

The scar — the visible sign of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s suffering, her cure, her moral growth — becomes the site of love's deepest affirmation. [[erick|Erick]] does not kiss [[lamorna|Lamorna]] *despite* the scar; he kisses **the scar itself**. The gesture is specific and deliberate: he loves not the beauty that was lost but the mark that remains, the evidence of the person she has become.

"I loved her all the better for it" is the collection's most important declaration about love. It inverts the [[fairy]]-tale logic in which love is rewarded with beauty. [[erick|Erick]]'s love is not diminished by the scar — it is **increased**. The scar, which [[lamorna|Lamorna]] feared would drive him away, is precisely what deepens his love, because the scar is proof of her moral reality.

## Consequences

### Immediate

[[lamorna|Lamorna]] is reconciled with [[erick|Erick]]. Her fear — that love was conditional on beauty — is disproven by the most direct possible evidence: the scar itself is kissed. She says "I will be your wife and love you dearly" (001-107), and the promise that began in childhood — "when we were children you said you loved me" (001-015) — is fulfilled.

### The Water People's Celebration

The [[water-people|water elves]] celebrate the happy ending (001-117). The magical beings who punished [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] now witness its cure's completion. Their celebration confirms that the moral arc of the tale is complete: [[pride|vanity]] has been punished, endured, cured, and finally replaced by the capacity to love and be loved.

### Symbolic

The kiss on the scar establishes a pattern that echoes through the collection: **love is proven not in pleasure but in the embrace of what is broken**. [[jack|Jack]]'s intervention for the star-crossed elemental lovers, [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s love for her [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]] even after [[zaire|Zaire]] destroys it, the Princess's [[patience|endurance]] of fire for love of her [[the-brook|water]]-Prince — all are variations on [[erick|Erick]]'s kiss. [[love|Love]] that touches the wound, rather than averting its eyes, is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s definition of the real thing.

## Thematic Significance

### Love Beyond Beauty

The event is the collection's most direct argument against the confusion of love with [[beauty]]. [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] was a category error: she loved her own beauty as if it were a person worthy of love. [[erick|Erick]]'s kiss corrects the error: he loves the **person**, and the scar — the anti-beauty — is the proof. The tale argues that beauty is not only unnecessary for love but that love proven in beauty's absence is the only love worth the name.

### The Scar as Transformation's Evidence

The scar is not a flaw to be magically erased — [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] pointedly does not have the [[the-brook|water]] people remove it. It is **evidence**: of the ordeal endured, the [[pride|vanity]] cured, the person [[lamorna|Lamorna]] has become. In a genre where [[transformation|transform]]ation is often instantaneous and cosmetic (the beast becomes a prince, the rags become a gown), [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] insists that [[transformation|transform]]ation leaves a mark. The scar is the price of becoming real.

### The Ordinary as Sacred

[[erick|Erick]] is a fisherman — the collection's most ordinary hero. His gesture — a kiss — is the most ordinary of human actions. No magic, no [[spells-and-charms|spell]], no [[charm|enchanted object]]. And yet it accomplishes what the [[the-brook|water]] people's magic could not: it completes [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[transformation|transform]]ation from object of [[pride|vanity]] to subject of love. The ordinary, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] insists, is sacred when it is true.
