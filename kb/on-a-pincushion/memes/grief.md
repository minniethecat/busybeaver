# Grief

Grief in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is the emotion that reveals what characters truly value — and, crucially, what they do not.

## Overview

Grief appears at pivotal moments across the corpus: the sunbeam grieves its purposelessness (001-256), the King sobs at the Queen's horrific [[transformation|transform]]ation (003-018), the King of "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" is "overpowered with grief" (004-072), and the court laments [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s departure with "great their grief" (004-086). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats grief not as weakness but as a moral barometer — those who grieve rightly are those who love rightly.

> "Great was their lamentations, and great their grief, when they heard she must leave." (004-086)

## In the Narrative

### Grief as Moral Indicator

The most revealing grief in the collection is [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s response to her grandmother's death — or rather, her lack of it. She "did not grieve for her grandmother's death, but sat brooding over what gifts would be left her" (001-129). The absence of grief signals [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s moral immaturity as clearly as any action could. Conversely, the sunbeam's grief — "it felt as if it would break with grief that it should be so stupid and so ugly" (001-256) — establishes the sunbeam as a sympathetic being despite its non-human form.

### Public and Private Grief

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] distinguishes between performative and genuine grief. The King of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" sobs aloud (003-018) — a public display that is nonetheless sincere. The [[fairy]] [[taboret|Taboret]] in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" steps forward when the King is "overpowered with grief" (004-072), using the moment of vulnerability to reveal the truth. Grief in these tales creates openings: it disarms defenses and makes [[truth|honesty]] possible.

### Grief That Moves Plot

Grief is never merely decorative in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales. The King's grief at the Queen's [[transformation|transform]]ation launches the search for the Hair Tree. The court's grief at [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s departure (004-086) confirms her value and closes her arc. Grief is the emotion that *acts* — it precipitates journeys, [[truth|revelation]]s, and endings.

## Literary Context

Victorian literature often treated grief as a private, feminine domain of tears and withdrawal. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s grief is strikingly active — it moves kings to action, [[fairy|fairies]] to intervention, and plots to resolution. This aligns her with the broader Victorian revaluation of emotion (from Romantic excess to moral sentiment) but adds a distinctive pragmatism: grief must *do* something to be meaningful.
