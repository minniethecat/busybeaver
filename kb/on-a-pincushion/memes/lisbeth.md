## Identity

Lisbeth is a young girl in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," identified as "the daughter of the baker" (002-010). She holds the distinction of being the first person in the village to purchase shoes from the disguised gnome, setting in motion the events that lead to the village's near-destruction.

## Role in the Story

For many weeks, the gnome sits by the roadside with his stall of colourful, cheap shoes, but "no customers came to the old man" because the villagers remained loyal to [[ralph|Ralph]] (002-009). Lisbeth is the one who breaks this resolve.

She approaches the stall with hesitation: she looks at a pair of red shoes with pretty buckles, turns away, feels the money in her pocket, returns the next morning to examine them, puts them down again, and finally, on her third visit, pays for them and "ran away, hiding the shoes under her apron, for she was rather afraid of being laughed at for having bought shoes of the old man" (002-010).

Immediately, she shows her purchase to her friend [[alys|Alys]]: "'Look at these pretty pink shoes,' she said. 'I have just bought them of the old man at the stall, and they only cost five silver pennies'" (002-010). This act of sharing her bargain triggers a cascade: [[alys|Alys]] buys a pair, then the other young girls, then the women, and finally the men — until [[ralph|Ralph]] is entirely abandoned.

> But one day a young girl named Lisbeth, the daughter of the baker, came near the stall, and looked at a pair of red shoes, with pretty buckles, and the old man looked at her, but did not say a word. Then she turned away, but she felt the money in her pocket as she walked, and she looked back at the shoes, and the old man nodded and chuckled to himself as he watched her.
>
> (002-010)

When [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] later burns all the gnome's shoes, among the heap are "the pretty pair of blue shoes that had first tempted Lisbeth, with the rosette torn off and worn quite white, and with great holes in the sides" (002-040) — visual proof of the shoddy quality that lay beneath the attractive appearance.

## Significance

Lisbeth functions as the unwitting catalyst of the village's moral and material collapse. Her individual choice — seemingly harmless, driven by the appeal of novelty and low price — becomes the first domino in a chain that leads to famine, fever, and the abduction of children. Her story illustrates how small acts of disloyalty, multiplied across a community, can have catastrophic consequences.
