The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] is a wind elemental being who serves as [[little-jack|Jack]]'s guide through the second half of his quest in "[[through-the-fire|Through the Fire]]." Taking over from the [[fire-fairy]] who brought [[jack|Jack]] to [[fireland|Fireland]], the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] carries the boy through the air to the [[north-pole|North Pole]] and back, protecting him from dangers and inadvertently teaching him a moral lesson about selflessness (005-153, 005-177).

## Nature and Voice

The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] speaks "in an odd gusty voice" befitting his elemental nature (005-153). As an air being, his domain is the sky and wind — he carries [[little-jack|Jack]] on his shoulders through rain and storm, flies at dizzying speeds, and [[power|control]]s the wind itself, blowing with enough force to turn ships and knock down attackers (005-153, 005-172, 005-186).

Like all wind-[[fairies]], he has a notably poor [[forget|memory]] and becomes cross when reminded of it: "How should I know? One really can't be expected to remember all these little things" (005-201). He is prone to falling asleep when idle — at the [[north-pole|North Pole]], [[jack|Jack]] finds him asleep on the ice while [[patience|waiting]] (005-207).

## Role in the Quest

### Taking Over from Fire

When [[little-jack|Jack]]'s time in [[fireland|Fireland]] concludes and he must travel to the [[north-pole|North Pole]], [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] directs him: "Go to the window, and you will see the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] who is to take you" (005-146). The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] becomes [[jack|Jack]]'s mount and protector for the remainder of the journey, carrying him on his shoulders as the [[fire-fairy]] had done before (005-153).

### The Mermaid and the Ship

While flying over the sea, the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] alerts [[jack|Jack]] to a mermaid singing to lure a ship into a whirlpool (005-168). He explains his duty with unexpected moral clarity: "I will go and blow the ship in another direction, whether it likes it or no, until it is out of the sound of her song, and then it will go on all right. Ah! men little think, when they complain of meeting gales of wind, that it is often for their own good, and that we are blowing them away from danger, not into it" (005-172).

The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] blows with immense force, turning the ship against [[rupert|the sailor]]s' will and saving them from destruction. When [[jack|Jack]] is later hypnotised by the mermaid's beauty and nearly throws himself into the sea, the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] seizes him and flies off before he can act (005-173).

The [[fairy|wind-fairy]]'s joy at saving the ship is profound and [[transformation|transform]]ative for [[jack|Jack]]:

> How pleased the wind-fairy was about the ship!
>
> (005-174)

Witnessing this selfless happiness, [[jack|Jack]] reflects: "Surely, if this poor silly little [[fairy|wind-fairy]] can be so glad when he has done a good deed, I ought to be glad to help other people and not to think of myself" (005-177). This moment becomes the moral turning point of [[jack|Jack]]'s journey.

### At the North Pole

At the [[north-pole|North Pole]], the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] demonstrates both his power and his limitations. When the old man remains asleep, "the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] had not blown a tremendous gust, which made the pink light in the lantern flicker, and the old man start up and open his eyes" (005-203). After [[jack|Jack]] asks his question — resisting the temptation to ask a second forbidden one — the old man flies into a violent rage. The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] "blew in his face till he was forced to shut his eyes, and turn his head away," then seizes [[jack|Jack]] and flies off to safety (005-217).

### The Return

The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] carries [[jack|Jack]] back home, where [[jack|Jack]] finds [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] still kneeling on the windowsill and [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] still sitting in the fire — everything exactly as he had left it (005-223). The [[fairy|wind-fairy]]'s role ends as [[jack|Jack]] completes his quest, having delivered the answer that will allow [[princess-pyra|the fire Princess]] and [[the-brook|water]] Prince to marry.

## Personality

The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] combines childlike simplicity with genuine moral instinct. He is easily pleased ("How pleased the [[fairy|wind-fairy]] was about the ship!"), [[forget]]ful and a little cross when reminded of it, and prone to napping. Yet he possesses an unshakeable commitment to protecting those in his charge — blowing ships from danger, snatching [[jack|Jack]] from the mermaid's lure, and facing down the raging old man at the [[north-pole|North Pole]]. It is his transparent, unself-conscious joy in doing good that teaches [[jack|Jack]] the story's central moral lesson.
