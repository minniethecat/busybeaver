# The Court

The royal court that appears in "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]" and "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" — a social space defined by protocol and performance, where authenticity is neither required nor noticed.

## Overview

The court is less a physical location than a social environment — the collection of courtiers, ladies-in-[[patience|waiting]], and officials who surround the throne. Its defining characteristic is blindness to authenticity: the courtiers "did not think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her place" (004-058). This failure of perception is the court's satirical function — it represents a world so devoted to form that it cannot see substance.

> In the morning, when the court ladies came to wake Princess Ursula, they found her sleeping as usual in her little bed, and little did they think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her stead. Indeed the ladies were much pleased; for when they said, “It is time for your Royal Highness to arise,” she only answered, “Certainly,” and let herself be dressed without another word. And as the time passed, and she was never naughty, and scarcely ever spoke, all said she was vastly improved, and she grew to be a great favourite.
>
> (004-058)

## Geography and Description

The court occupies the great halls and throne rooms of the castle. It features golden thrones, "fine ladies to wait on" the Queen (001-149), and "splendidly dressed" courtiers "in coloured velvets and gold" (001-131). The court's physical grandeur masks its emotional poverty.

## Narrative Significance

The court is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s primary satirical target. It is a place where a mechanical puppet can rule unnoticed, where a child can be "never taught any games or given any toys" (004-010) because protocol forbids it, and where the genuine love between [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and her Prince is the exception rather than the rule.
