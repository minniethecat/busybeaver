# Darkness

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, darkness is never merely the absence of light. It is a space of trial, concealment, and [[transformation|transform]]ation — something to be *entered* and *passed through*, never settled in.

## Overview

Darkness appears at crucial junctures in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales, always marking a threshold between states. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] cannot see the seeds in the darkness but grasps them firmly (001-143); characters nestle "in the darkness within the Stone" (001-282); [[jack|Jack]] flies "through the darkness so fast that he felt giddy" (005-056) before passing "from the darkness into the light through a kind of archway" (005-059). Darkness is consistently a passage, never a destination — a place of disorientation that leads, if endured, to [[light|illumination]].

> "They passed from the darkness into the light through a kind of archway." (005-059)

## In the Narrative

### Darkness as Trial

The most extended darkness passage occurs in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," where [[jack|Jack]]'s descent into the underground world is a journey through literal darkness that mirrors his inner trial. His guide "sprang into the hole, and began to fly through the darkness so fast that he felt giddy" (005-056). The speed and disorientation are essential to the experience — darkness is not a static condition but an active, almost violent passage. When they finally emerge "into the light through a kind of archway" (005-059), the transition is as physical as it is symbolic.

### Darkness as Concealment

In "On a Pincushion," darkness serves a protective function. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] "could not see the seeds in the darkness, but she grasped them firmly" (001-143) — the darkness hides the seeds' nature, requiring faith rather than sight. Later, two characters "passed into the hole, and nestled in the darkness within the Stone" (001-282), where the darkness becomes a cocoon, a space of temporary refuge before the next stage of the journey. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s darkness shelters as much as it threatens.

### From Darkness to Light

The recurring pattern of passing from darkness into light — most explicitly in 005-059 — establishes darkness as the necessary precondition for [[light|illumination]]. There is no light without first traversing darkness in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s symbolic economy. This pattern mirrors the tales' broader moral structure: hardship precedes reward, trial precedes [[transformation|transform]]ation.
