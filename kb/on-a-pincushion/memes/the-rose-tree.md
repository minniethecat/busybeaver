# The Rose Tree

A magical rose tree grown from jewel-like seeds given by [[love|Love]], whose health and blooming directly mirror the strength of [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s marriage — a symbiotic magical item from "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]."

## Overview

The rose tree is the most intimate of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s magical items: it grows from seeds planted by [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and blooms in exact proportion to her husband's love. "As it grows so his love for you will grow. While that tree lives he will love you more than all the world, but should it pine and die his love for you would wither too." (001-141) Unlike external magical aids (candles, shoes), the rose tree is a [[life|living]] barometer of relationship health — magic as emotional thermometer.

> After a time, Blanchelys had a little son, who was heir to the crown, and she was even happier than before, and her husband loved her better. The bells were rung at the birth of the prince, and all the people rejoiced. And the rose-tree grew so fast, that when Blanchelys came out into the garden, with her baby in her arms, it was quite a big tree, and she was able to stand under the shade of its branches.
>
> (001-151)

## In the Narrative

### Planting and Growth

[[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] plants the jewel-like seeds at sunrise, breathing the Prince's name over them. A tiny shoot appears the first night (001-145), and the plant grows steadily as the Prince's daily visits lengthen. A white rose blooms on the night he proposes (001-148), and by the time [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] has a child, the tree is large enough to stand under. (001-151)

### Zaire's Threat

The tree's vulnerability becomes a plot point when the jealous [[zaire|Zaire]] learns that [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] values it above almost everything: "if that tree were to die, would you be very unhappy, [[blanchelys|cousin Blanchelys]]?" (001-158) The tree's symbiotic nature — tied to [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s happiness — makes it both precious and dangerous, a magical vulnerability that an enemy can exploit.

## Literary Context

Symbiotic magical plants appear in folklore (the life-token tree, the external soul), but [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s rose tree is distinctive in being tied to relationship quality rather than life force. It is not that [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] dies if the tree dies, but that her marriage fails — a domestication of the life-token motif that reflects the tale's concern with emotional rather than physical survival.
