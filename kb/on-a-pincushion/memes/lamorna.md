# Lamorna

A pretty farmer's daughter whose [[pride|vanity]] drives the plot of "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," the first embedded tale in *On a Pincushion* — cured of self-obsession when the [[the-brook|water]] people steal her reflection.

## Overview

Lamorna is the protagonist of the first embedded tale told by [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]. She is described as having "bright brown hair and big brown eyes, and a mouth like a rosebud" (001-010), and everyone in the village tells her she is beautiful:

> She had bright brown hair and big brown eyes, and a mouth like a rosebud. (001-010)

Her defining trait is [[pride|vanity]]: she "does nothing but look at her own reflection, and loves nothing so much as her own pretty face." (001-017) Unlike [[fairy]]-tale villains who are [[vanity|vain]], Lamorna is not wicked — she is merely self-absorbed, and the narrative treats her [[pride|vanity]] as something to be cured rather than punished.

> "And if it had not been for my vanity," she sighed to herself, "I need not have been hurt at all. It was only that which made me want the bird's wing. Ah, what a little thing beauty is to be so vain of!" (001-094)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

Lamorna serves as a moral exemplar of [[pride|vanity]] corrected. Her arc — from self-absorption to self-knowledge — is the tale's central movement. She begins by scorning the love of the good fisherman [[erick|Erick]], preferring her own reflection to human connection. By the tale's end, she has learned that "a terrible thing is [[pride|vanity]]" (001-119) and accepts [[erick|Erick]]'s love.

## Character Interpretation

Lamorna represents a domesticated, feminized version of the Narcissus myth — where the self-absorbed figure is not destroyed but cured. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s innovation is therapeutic: Lamorna's [[pride|vanity]] is an illness to be healed, not a sin to be punished. This reflects the Victorian [[fairy]] tale's investment in moral education through gentle correction rather than harsh consequence.
