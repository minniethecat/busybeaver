# Siegfrid and Handa

The second tale in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* and the collection's most traditionally heroic narrative. Unlike the internal dramas of [[pride|vanity]] and redemption in the frame story's embedded tales, "Siegfrid and Handa" is an **action hero-tale** — a rescue, a villain, a fire, and an escape — grounded in the emotional reality of two foundlings raised as siblings who must become something more to survive.

## Synopsis

An old couple [[life|living]] "on the border of a large forest" in "a little village, containing but a few houses, which were small and poor" (002-001) find two abandoned infants — a boy and a girl — and raise them as their own. A [[fairy]] gives the old woman two [[magic-candles|magic candles]], one for each child: as long as the candles burn, the children live.

The boy, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], grows up as a shoemaker's son. The girl, [[handa|Handa]], becomes his inseparable companion. When a giant disguised as a shoemaker begins capturing village children — fitting them with [[magic-shoes|enchanted shoes]] that compel obedience — [[handa|Handa]] is among the taken. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], guided by a magical [[the-hare|Hare]], discovers the giant's underground workshop. He finds the captive children and [[handa|Handa]], wearing [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s that force her to dance.

The children cry out: "Let us set fire to them at once" (002-045). [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] sets fire to the giant's enchanted boots. They "soon caught fire, and burnt steadily" (002-048). With the boots destroyed and the [[magic|enchantment]] broken, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] remembers the [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s on [[handa|Handa]]'s feet — shoes that compel dancing — and uses their power to carry both himself and [[handa|Handa]] through the underground passage, out into the forest, and into the sunlight of freedom.

> On the border of a large forest there once stood a little village, where all the people were happy, for they were all good and industrious and honest. It was the pleasantest little village in the world. No fevers or illnesses ever came near it. The people died only of old age, and all the children were well grown and strong.

(002-001)

## Themes

### Courage as Action

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] embodies a specific kind of courage: not the [[patience|endurance]] of [[jack]] or the persistence of [[rupert]], but **decisive action in the critical moment**. He does not hesitate when the children cry for fire. He does not negotiate with the giant. His heroism is physical, immediate, and irreversible — the match struck, the boots [[fire-element|burning]], the shoes carrying them to safety. In a collection full of characters who must learn to act, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] is the one who already knows how.

### The Repurposing of Evil Magic

The tale's most structurally elegant move is [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s use of the giant's own magic against him. The [[magic-shoes|magic shoes]] — designed to compel obedience and trap wearers — become the vehicle of escape. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] does not destroy all magic; he **repurposes** what can serve good and destroys what cannot. This discernment — between magic that can be redeemed and magic that must be burned — is the tale's moral core.

### Found Family

Siegfrid and Handa are not siblings by blood but by circumstance — two foundlings raised together by strangers. Their bond is chosen rather than inherited, making [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s rescue not an act of familial obligation but of **elective love**. The old couple who raise them extend the same principle: family is what you build, not what you are born into.

## Structure and Narrative Features

"Siegfrid and Handa" follows a compressed **rescue narrative** structure:

1. **Establishment**: The village, the foundlings, the [[magic-candles|magic candle]]s
2. **Threat**: The giant-shoemaker, the disappearing children
3. **Descent**: [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] enters the forest, finds the underground passage
4. **Discovery**: The workshop, the captive children, [[handa|Handa]] in [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s
5. **Climax**: The [[fire-element|burning]] of the boots ([[the-burning-of-the-boots]])
6. **Escape**: The [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s carry Siegfrid and Handa to freedom
7. **Return**: Emerging into sunlight at the forest's edge

The tale is the collection's most **plot-driven** narrative. Where "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" is a moral fable and "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]" is a domestic allegory, "Siegfrid and Handa" is an adventure — and the collection is richer for its inclusion.

## Place in the Collection

"Siegfrid and Handa" occupies Chapter 002. It follows the frame narrative's embedded tales and precedes "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." Its active heroism provides a necessary contrast to the collection's more contemplative tales, establishing that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral universe includes not only those who endure and [[transformation|transform]] but also those who **act**.

The tale also introduces the collection's most significant recurring motif: **fire as liberation**. The [[fire-element|burning]] of the boots prefigures the fire-trial of "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" and establishes fire as the element of agency — the force that, when wielded with courage, can undo what evil has made.
