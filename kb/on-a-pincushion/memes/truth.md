# Truth

Truth in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is never cheap — it costs the teller something to speak it, and it costs the hearer something to receive it. But without truth, no happy ending is possible.

## Overview

The word "truth" appears in two of the five tales, but its thematic presence extends further. In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s foster parents "told them the whole truth about [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]" (004-085), shattering the court's illusion but enabling genuine relationship. In "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," truth has a magical dimension — "whatever he is asked he is bound to answer truthfully" (005-142) — making honesty not a virtue but a compulsion. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents truth as a force that must eventually break through, regardless of human preference.

> "They told them the whole truth about Ursula, and that they must take her back if they would." (004-085)

## In the Narrative

### Truth as Liberation

The revelation of [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s true identity is the emotional climax of "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]." The foster parents return to court and speak the truth (004-085), releasing both [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] from her false role and the court from its illusion. The truth is not gentle — it means [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] must leave the only life she has known — but it is necessary. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] suggests that [[life|living]] in falsehood, however comfortable, is unsustainable.

### Magically Compelled Truth

"[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" introduces a fascinating variant: the magical being who "is bound to answer truthfully" (005-142) when asked a direct question, but who may not volunteer information. This creates a legalistic relationship with truth — technically honest but potentially misleading. "No one else would tell the truth. He knew it was naughty" (005-167) — this line explicitly frames untruth as a moral failing, but one that the world tolerates until magic compels otherwise.

### Truth and Cost

Every truth-telling in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales carries a cost. The foster parents risk rejection when they reveal [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s origins. [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] herself must leave her home. The truth-teller in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" must answer even when the answer brings danger. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world does not reward truth-tellers with comfort; it rewards them with integrity, which is presented as more valuable but not easier.

## Literary Context

The Victorian period's complex relationship with truth — from the earnest moralism of the mid-century to the aestheticist embrace of artifice (Wilde's "The Decay of Lying," 1889) — provides the backdrop for [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment. She occupies a middle ground: truth is essential, but the cost of speaking it is never minimized. This realism about truth's difficulty distinguishes her from both the moralists (for whom truth is simple duty) and the aesthetes (for whom truth is irrelevant).
