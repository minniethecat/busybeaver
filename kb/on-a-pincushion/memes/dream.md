# Dream

Dreams in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* serve as narrative hinges — moments where the boundary between waking life and visionary experience dissolves, often carrying prophetic or [[transformation|transform]]ative weight.

## Overview

Dreams appear across four of the five tales, spanning the entire corpus: [[lamorna|Lamorna]] dreams of the King's son (001-132), the Queen of the Hair Tree dreams a vision that drives the plot of its entire tale (003-020), [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s former life becomes "like a dream she barely remembered" (004-061), and [[jack|Jack]]'s entire adventure in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" is framed as possibly being "a strange dream" (005-243). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] uses dreams not as escapist interludes but as structural devices that question the very boundary between real and unreal.

> "Dear boy, you have had a dream, and I am glad it was such a pleasant one." (005-259)

## In the Narrative

### The Prophetic Dream

The Queen's dream in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is the collection's most consequential vision. She "dreamt a dream" (003-020) of a wonderful tree, and the dream recurs with such vividness that a herald is sent to find it (003-024). The dream is treated as a form of knowledge — neither false nor fantastic — and the entire plot of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" unfolds from this nocturnal vision. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] accords dreams a cognitive status unusual in Victorian fiction: the dream is not a delusion but a map.

### Dream as Transformation Marker

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s dreaming of the King's son (001-132) marks her passage from self-absorbed child to young woman capable of desire. [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s former life becoming "like a dream" (004-061) marks her psychological distance from the false identity she has worn. In both cases, dreaming signals a change in consciousness — the inner self reorganizing around new realities.

### The Dream That Might Not Be

"[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" carries the dream motif to its logical extreme: the entire adventure is questioned as dream. "It might all have been a dream" (005-099), [[jack|Jack]] thinks, and the story's final line — "Dear boy, you have had a dream" (005-259) — leaves the ontological status of everything that happened deliberately ambiguous. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] refuses to resolve whether the magic was real or dreamed, making the point that the *effect* of the experience matters more than its reality status.

## Literary Context

Dreams in Victorian [[fairy]] tales often served as convenient framing devices (Alice's adventures are famously a dream). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] goes further: her dreams are not mere frames but active plot engines. [[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]'s multi-night recurring dream recalls biblical dream-visions (Joseph, Daniel) more than Carroll's whimsy, investing the dream with genuine epistemological weight.
