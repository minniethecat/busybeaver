# Cruelty

Cruelty in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is never accidental. It is a choice — made by communities, by authorities, and by magical beings — and the tales trace its consequences with unflinching moral clarity.

## Overview

The word "cruelty" appears at a pivotal moral moment in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]." The villagers' treatment of [[the-shoemaker|the old shoemaker]] is named for what it is: "you have brought these troubles on yourselves by your cruelty in leaving him to starve" (002-078). This directness is characteristic of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] — she does not soften moral language. Cruelty elsewhere in the collection takes more theatrical forms: [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's spiteful [[transformation|transform]]ation of [[trevina|Trevina]], the Queen's shaving-spectacle in the marketplace, the social cruelty of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]]-born isolation. But in every case, cruelty is presented not as a tragic inevitability but as a *decision*, and one that can be reversed through acknowledgment and change.

> "You have brought these troubles on yourselves by your cruelty in leaving him to starve." (002-078)

## In the Narrative

### Cruelty as Community Failure

The most pointed treatment of cruelty in the collection is not personal but communal. The village's abandonment of [[the-shoemaker|the old shoemaker]] is described as "cruelty" — a word usually reserved for individual villains applied to an entire community. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s point is radical for a Victorian [[fairy]] tale: cruelty is not the province of monsters but of ordinary people who fail in their obligations to one another. The villagers are not evil; they are convenient, and their convenience becomes cruelty.

### Cruelty as Spectacle

The tortoise-queen's cruelty takes the form of magical punishment: [[transformation|transform]]ing [[trevina|Trevina]] into a tigress with "eyes sparkling with spiteful pleasure" (003-095). The Queen's scaffold spectacle — shaving all the women of the kingdom publicly (003-167) — is cruelty as theater, performed for an audience of court and crowd. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] pairs these institutional and magical cruelties to suggest that the difference is only one of means, not of moral character.

### The Reversibility of Cruelty

What distinguishes [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of cruelty is her insistence that it can be undone. The villagers acknowledge their wrongdoing: "we behaved very wickedly and ungratefully" (002-079). Even [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's magic can be reversed — by being beaten with the right rods. Cruelty, in this moral universe, is a condition from which recovery is possible — but only if it is first named and acknowledged.
