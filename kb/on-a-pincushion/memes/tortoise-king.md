# The Tortoise King

The tortoise-king is the most fully realized antagonist in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* — a magic-wielding monarch of a tortoise realm who pursues [[trevina|Trevina]] with a combination of menace, absurdity, and genuine menace that makes him one of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most memorable creations.

## Overview

The tortoise-king first appears in disguise, following [[trevina|Trevina]] as "a large tortoise, who kept close to me" (003-079). He soon reveals his true identity: "Know that I am no common tortoise, but am [[the-tortoise-king|the king of the tortoises]], and only for your sake have assumed the shape of a common animal" (003-086). His kingdom is an island "covered with tortoises of all sizes" (003-088) where he presides with his mother, [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen. The character embodies a distinctively [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] blend of horror and comedy — his waddling approach, "as fast as his little short legs and heavy shell would let him" (003-092), is both ridiculous and threatening.

> "Know that I am no common tortoise, but am the king of the tortoises." (003-086)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The tortoise-king is the primary obstacle in [[trevina|Trevina]]'s embedded narrative within "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." He represents unwanted imposition — a suitor who will not be refused, whose power makes refusal dangerous. His magic allows him to shapeshift and to follow [[trevina|Trevina]] across distances, making escape nearly impossible. He is the [[fairy]]-tale abductor par excellence, rendered with a specificity — the waddling, the heavy shell, the "hideous" face — that elevates him beyond generic monster into a distinct character.

### Key Actions

- Follows [[trevina|Trevina]] in tortoise form, tracking her across the sea (003-079, 003-083, 003-085)
- Reveals his true identity as tortoise-king and declares his intention to marry [[trevina|Trevina]] (003-086)
- Rules over an island kingdom covered with tortoises of all sizes (003-088)
- Kidnaps [[trevina|Trevina]] and holds her on the island (003-090)
- Pursues [[the-seagull|the gull]] who carries [[trevina|Trevina]]'s message, "waddling as fast as his little short legs and heavy shell would let him" (003-092)

### Relationships

- [[tortoise-queen]] — his mother, who [[transformation|transform]]s [[trevina|Trevina]] into a tigress out of [[cruelty|spite]]
- [[the-hair-tree]] — the tale in which his story is embedded
- [[the-barber]] — another figure of menace in the same tale

## Character Interpretation

The tortoise-king is a study in the grotesque. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] gives him the trappings of monarchy — armies, an island kingdom, courtly language — and pairs them with the body of a tortoise, creating a dissonance that is simultaneously comic and unsettling. His waddling pursuit of [[the-seagull|the gull]] (003-092) is one of the collection's most vivid [[reflection|image]]s: a king, powerful enough to kidnap and [[transformation|transform]], reduced to a slow, absurd chase. This tension between power and physical limitation may reflect [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s broader interest in the gap between [[power|authority]] and capability.


The [[the-tortoise-king|Tortoise-King]] can also be read as a study in grotesque entitlement. His disguise as a harmless pet — something [[trevina|Trevina]] feeds and cares for — makes his eventual betrayal a violation of hospitality itself. His small stature (two feet high) and subservience to his mother suggest a figure of diminished masculinity: though he is a "king," he cannot act independently, and his power is entirely delegated from the more fearsome [[tortoise-queen]]. His demand that [[trevina|Trevina]] be "[[gratitude|grateful]]" for his proposal recasts abduction as [[kindness|generosity]] — a rhetorical move that exposes the logic of coercion beneath the language of romance.
