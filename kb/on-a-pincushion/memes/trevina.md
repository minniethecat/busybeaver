# Trevina

The protagonist of "The Story of Trevina," an embedded tale within "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" — a miller's daughter kidnapped by a monstrous suitor, [[transformation|transform]]ed into a tigress, and ultimately rescued by [[rupert|Rupert]].

## Overview

Trevina is the youngest of three sisters, "the most beautiful of the three" (003-081), who lives with her miller father by the sea. Her story is a dark [[fairy]] tale of unwanted courtship, [[transformation|transform]]ation, and [[patience|endurance]]: pursued by [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king who disguises himself as a common tortoise, she is abducted to an island and, when she resists his proposal, [[transformation|transform]]ed into a tigress by his jealous mother and exiled to an enchanted land (003-088-091). Her restoration depends on being beaten with the rods of the Hair Tree — a task that falls to [[rupert|Rupert]] in the frame narrative.

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

Trevina is the classic [[fairy]]-tale heroine beset by supernatural forces beyond her [[power|control]], but [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] gives her agency through [[patience|endurance]]. Unlike passive princesses who merely wait for rescue, Trevina actively resists: she tries to throw [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king into the sea (003-086-088), sends a message via the [[the-seagull|Seagull]] to her father (003-089), and sustains herself on grass and herbs for an unstated but clearly extended period rather than capitulate (003-098). Her [[transformation|transform]]ation into a tigress is a punishment for her defiance, but it is also, paradoxically, a form of power — the tigress is dangerous, and [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's curse contains the admission that Trevina could become "a real tigress" (003-091) if she eats flesh.

### Key Actions

- Takes in a stray tortoise as a pet, unaware of its true nature (003-081)
- Falls asleep in her father's boat and is abducted to [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]] island (003-086)
- Defies [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king's marriage proposal and tries to throw him into the sea (003-086–003-088)
- Enlists [[the-seagull]] to carry a message to her father (003-089)
- Is [[transformation|transform]]ed into a tigress by [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen as punishment (003-090–003-091)
- Survives on grass and herbs in [[the-magic-country|the enchanted land]], sustained by hope (003-096–003-098)
- Is found by [[rupert]], who eventually rescues her with the Hair Tree rods (003-122)

### Relationships

- [[the-tortoise-king]] — her abductor and unwanted suitor, who disguises himself as a pet
- [[the-tortoise-queen]] — her punisher, who [[transformation|transform]]s her into a tigress out of [[cruelty|spite]]
- [[the-seagull]] — her ally and messenger, the only creature who aids her (R21)
- [[rupert]] — her rescuer, [[rupert|the sailor]] who beats her with the Hair Tree rods to restore her form
- [[the-miller]] — her father, to whom she attempts to send word

## Character Interpretation

Trevina's ordeal follows the structure of Cupid and Psyche but inverts it: where Psyche's transgression is curiosity, Trevina's is resistance. She is punished not for looking but for refusing. The tortoise-king's demand that she be "[[gratitude|grateful]]" for his "kindness in offering to make you his wife" (003-090) exposes the entitlement at the heart of the tale — Trevina's "crime" is exercising choice. Her [[transformation|transform]]ation into a beast echoes [[beauty|Beauty]] and the Beast, but again [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] subverts the trope: Trevina becomes the beast not through a curse she brought on herself, but through another's [[cruelty|malice]]. Her [[patience|endurance]] — [[life|living]] on grass, maintaining hope — is the tale's true heroism, preceding and enabling [[rupert|Rupert]]'s eventual rescue.

## From the Text

> My name was Trevina. I was the youngest of three sisters, and my father was a miller. I was the most beautiful of the three. We lived in a mill on the bank of a river, close by the sea, and my greatest amusement was to search about on the rocks for the strange animals and seaweed I found there.
>
> (003-081)

> Drawing close to them, the sailor examined them carefully, to see where they could be gathered, for doubtless these were the rods of which Trevina spoke. They seemed to be fastened into the plants with a sort of hinge, and beneath this Rupert took hold of one to break it off, but no sooner had he begun to pull, than the rod, swinging on its joint, dealt him a smart blow on the face, which made him stagger back, and all the lip flowers laughed again.
>
> (003-122)
