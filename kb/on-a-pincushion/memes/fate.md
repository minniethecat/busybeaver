# Fate

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, fate is not a grand cosmic design but a moment of surrender — the instant when a character stops struggling and accepts what seems inevitable.

## Overview

The word "fate" crystallizes a dramatic turning point in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." The duchess, seated in "the [[fear|dread]]ful chair" and facing the barber's razor, "closing her eyes, resigned herself to her fate" (003-174). The scene is a tableau of impending doom: the barber sharpens his razor, the crowd holds its breath, and the duchess gives up fighting. But [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] immediately undercuts the finality of fate — a voice cries "Stop!" and a sailor rushes forward with something in his hand. Fate, in this pivotal moment, is the *belief* that the end has come, a belief that proves premature.

> "Closing her eyes, resigned herself to her fate." (003-174)

## In the Narrative

### The Architecture of the Scene

The fate scene in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is constructed with theatrical precision. The duchess takes her seat "with a last sob" — the adverb marking finality. She closes her eyes — shutting out the world. She resigns herself — the verb suggesting active surrender, not passive acceptance. The barber sharpens his razor, a small, terrible domestic detail that makes the threat real. "All held their breath from excitement" — the crowd as audience, the execution as spectacle. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] builds the tension to its peak, then breaks it with a single word: "Stop!"

### Fate Versus Rescue

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of fate is distinctive because she presents it as a *false ending*. The duchess's resignation to fate is genuine and complete — but fate itself is not what it appears. The sailor's intervention reframes "fate" as merely the worst moment before deliverance. This pattern recurs across the tales: characters believe themselves doomed, only to be saved. Fate, in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world, is what you believe in at [[darkness|the dark]]est moment — and what proves, time and again, to be less final than it seems.
