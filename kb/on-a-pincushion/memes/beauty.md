# Beauty

Physical beauty as a central preoccupation and moral testing ground in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, where it functions as both a gift to be enjoyed and a trap to be escaped.

## Overview

Beauty in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales is never neutral — it is always a force that shapes character and drives plot. For [[lamorna|Lamorna]] in "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," beauty becomes the object of obsessive [[pride|self-regard]], leading her to scorn genuine love. For the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] in "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]," beauty is something to be hidden or revealed, tied to vulnerability and trust. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats beauty not as inherently corrupting but as inherently dangerous: it must be managed, not worshipped. (001-094)

> "Ah, what a little thing beauty is to be so vain of!" (001-094)

## In the Narrative

### Lamorna's Beauty

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s physical beauty is established immediately — "bright brown hair and big brown eyes, and a mouth like a rosebud" (001-010) — and it defines her character arc. Her beauty is real, not an illusion, which makes her [[pride|vanity]] more subtle: the problem is not false self-assessment but excessive self-focus. When the [[the-water-people|water people]] steal her reflection, they do not make her ugly; they simply remove her ability to see herself, forcing her to look outward. (001-043)

### The Cost of Vanity

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s realization that [[pride|vanity]] cost her everything comes through the loss of her beauty. "She knew well that her beauty was gone forever" (001-095), and this knowledge — not any external punishment — is what completes her moral education. The narrative suggests beauty is transient, and those who build their identity on it build on sand.

### Blanchelys and Hidden Beauty

In the embedded tale "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]," the character [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] embodies a counterpoint to [[lamorna|Lamorna]]: her beauty is tied to a secret — she is the Sun's daughter — and must be guarded. Where [[lamorna|Lamorna]] flaunts her beauty, [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] must conceal hers. The story suggests beauty is safest when least displayed, completing a thematic arc from [[pride|vanity]] ([[lamorna|Lamorna]]) to hidden worth ([[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]).

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of beauty aligns with Victorian anxieties about female beauty and moral character. Unlike the Grimms' [[fairy]] tales where beauty = virtue (Cinderella, Snow White), [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] problematizes beauty as a moral hazard. This is closer to the aestheticist debates of the 1880s (Wilde, Pater) about beauty's relationship to ethics, transposed into the [[fairy]]-tale mode.
