# The Cottage

The small cottage where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and her [[zaire|cousin Zaire]] live, situated by a road where they open gates for travelers — a humble domestic setting that grounds the magical events of "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]."

## Overview

The cottage is the starting point of [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s [[transformation|transform]]ation from orphan to Queen. It is a gate-keeper's cottage — the two girls "went out as before to open the gates for the passersby" (001-131) — placing it at a literal threshold between the ordinary road and the grand castle across the river. The cottage contains a small garden with a great yew-tree "whose branches almost touched the ground" (001-136), the site where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] lights her [[magic-candles|magic candle]] and summons [[love|Love]].

## Geography and Description

The cottage stands near a bridge over a river. On "the other side of the river was a grand castle" (001-131). The garden contains a yew-tree, and the cottage has a cupboard where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] hides her candle, a window through which she watches the night, and small beds for the two girls. The flowerpot where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] plants the [[the-seeds-of-love|magic seeds]] sits in the garden.

## Narrative Significance

The cottage represents humility as virtue's nursery. It is from this small, ordinary place that [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] rises, and the contrast between cottage and castle structures the tale's Cinderella-like arc. The yew-tree in the garden — ancient, dark, associated in folklore with death and rebirth — is the tale's threshold to magic.

## Characters Associated

- [[blanchelys]] — the gentle orphan who lives here
- [[zaire]] — her jealous cousin and housemate
- [[the-rose-tree]] — the magical plant grown in a flowerpot in the cottage garden

## From the Text

> So she dressed herself quickly, but she left her hair hanging down her back, and trod noiselessly to the cupboard, and softly opened the door. She took out the candle, and hid it in her bosom. Then she crept from the room, down the passage, and into the little garden. In the middle of the garden stood a great yew-tree, whose branches almost touched the ground. It looked like a great black giant in the night, and Blanchelys trembled as she looked at it; but she summoned her courage, and going up to the tree crept under its branches, and knelt down, leaning against the trunk. It was black, black night, but not a breeze was blowing, and it was as hot as if the sun was shining. Blanchelys stuck her candle firmly in the ground, and then lit it. Directly it began to burn, there came a little rustling sound through the trees like the flapping of doves’ wings, and then in front of where Blanchelys knelt, in the light of the candle she saw a boy, who was not like anyone she had ever seen before, so beautiful was he. He had curly golden hair, which spread round his head like a halo, and he wore on his hair a wreath of pink roses, and he carried a branch of roses in his hand. His robe was white, but it did not hide his bare feet, on which were golden sandals; and a golden girdle was round his waist. From his shoulders grew soft pink wings, and his face was as beautiful as an angel’s.
>
> (001-136)
