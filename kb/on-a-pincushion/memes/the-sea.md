# The Sea

The sea that borders the village and receives the brook's [[the-brook|water]]s in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. Unlike the brook — a site of [[pride|vanity]], magic, and [[transformation|transform]]ation — the sea is the domain of honest labor: the place where [[erick|Erick]] rows to fish, where the working world meets the wild, and where the boundary between the known and the unknown runs like a horizon line. It appears across multiple tales as both setting and symbol, its character defined by the people who work it rather than the magic that occasionally visits it.

## Geography

The sea lies "about half a mile farther down" (001-010) from the village, where the brook empties into it. It is close enough for a fisherman to row out daily and return by evening; far enough to represent departure from the domestic world.

> The brook on the banks of which she stood ran into the sea about half a mile farther down.
>
> — 001-010

The sea is described as "smooth as glass" on [[the-moonbeam|moonlight]] nights (001-041, 001-108) — a recurring [[reflection|image]] of calm that belies its narrative function as a boundary between realms. In "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," the sea is the destination of the brook that runs past the cottage. In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," [[rupert|Rupert]] sails "to the north, and then to the south, and then to the east, and then to the west" (003-031) across unnamed seas that function as passage rather than place.

## The Sea as Working World

The sea is the domain of [[erick]], the fisherman who "pushing it off rowed out to sea and began to fish" (001-014). His relationship to the sea is practical, not romantic — he works it for a [[life|living]]. The [[water-people|water people]] observe him from the brook but do not inhabit the sea; they "often watched him, and knew that he was neither cruel nor wicked" (001-014). This observation from the magical realm into the working world establishes the sea as a neutral zone — watched by magic but not ruled by it.

> The sea was smooth as glass, and the moon shone upon it making it glisten.
>
> — 001-041

This [[reflection|image]] of calm recurs at key emotional moments — the sea is never stormy in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales, never threatening. Its danger is not violence but vastness: the sea is the unknown into which characters sail and from which strangers arrive.

## As Boundary and Threshold

The sea functions as the collection's most significant **geographic boundary**:

- **Between the known and unknown**: "a country far over the sea" (001-124) is the [[fairy]]-tale elsewhere — the place where magical things originate. The seeds of the [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s "were brought over the sea, from a strange land" (001-127).
- **Between freshwater and salt**: The brook (fresh, magical, inland) flows into the sea (salt, working, open). This hydrological boundary mirrors the narrative boundary between domestic magic and world-scale adventure.
- **Between life and death**: A churchyard stands "close by" where "the sea roared loud, and wild seabirds flew screaming around it" (001-188) — the sea as witness to human [[death|mortality]], its roar the soundtrack to grief.

## Characters Associated

- **[[erick]]** — The fisherman whose honest labor the sea sustains. He rows out daily, his boat a small vessel on a large [[the-brook|water]] — the [[reflection|image]] of the working man in right relation to nature.
- **[[jack]]** — Crosses the sea on the [[fairy|wind-fairy]]'s shoulders (005-166), passing from the urban world of London through the elemental realm toward the ice kingdom. For [[jack|Jack]], the sea is not destination but passage — the between-space on a journey between elements.
- **[[rupert]]** — The sailor whose four-directional voyage across the sea begins his quest for the Hair Tree. Unlike [[erick|Erick]], who fishes the local [[the-brook|water]]s, [[rupert|Rupert]] sails into unmapped ocean — the sea as departure into [[fairy]] tale.
- **[[water-people]]** — The brook-dwellers who watch the sea but do not enter it, respecting a boundary between fresh and salt, magical and mundane.

## Literary Significance

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s sea is unusual in Victorian children's fantasy for its **ordinariness**. It is not the sublime [[fear|terror]] of Coleridge's "Rime" or the moralized ocean of Kingsley's *Water-Babies*. It is a working sea, a fisherman's sea — the place where honest people earn honest [[life|living]]s. Its magic is not in monsters or mermaids (though a mermaid does appear to [[jack|Jack]] en route to the ice realm, 005-171) but in its capacity to connect: the brook to the ocean, the village to the wider world, the known to the unknown.

The sea also anchors the collection's **elemental geography**: brook (freshwater, [[pride|vanity]], [[transformation|transform]]ation), sea (saltwater, labor, boundary), fire (underground, passion, trial), ice (northern, stasis, [[patience|endurance]]). Each element has its own spatial logic, and the sea's logic is **connection** — it is the medium through which the other elements communicate.
