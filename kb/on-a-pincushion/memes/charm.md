# Charm

A magical object or [[magic|enchantment]] that confers supernatural properties — protection, power, or [[transformation|transform]]ation — upon its bearer or target. Charms in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales range from [[fear|dread]]ful necromantic objects to protective wards and enchanted garments.

## Overview

In [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s fictional world, a charm is a form of concentrated magic bound to a physical object or enacted through a spoken formula. Charms can be malevolent, as with the "[[fear|dread]]ful charm" crafted from bones that grants unnatural life (002-028), or protective, defending against the magic of "bad [[fairy|fairies]]" (003-184). Unlike the broader concept of [[magic]], which encompasses the entire supernatural framework of the tales, a charm is a specific, localized magical Working — something that can be made, broken, or worn.

## In the Narrative

### Appearances

Charms appear in three of the tales:

In *[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]*, charms are central to the antagonist's power. The old shoemaker creates a "[[fear|dread]]ful charm" from bones that gives life to his magical shoes (002-028). [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s victory is explicitly framed as the breaking of these charms — "he knew now that his wicked charms had been broken" (002-066) — and the narrative confirms that "the charm was broken, and that theirs was now the victory" (002-080).

In *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]*, charms appear both as verbs of [[magic|enchantment]] and as protective measures. The speaking bird "charmed away the Queen's hair" (003-047), using charm as a verb of magical theft. Later, the nut given for protection is said to "defend them from all charms of bad [[fairy|fairies]]" (003-184), positioning charms as hostile magic that requires counter-measures.

In *[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]*, charms are integrated into everyday magical commerce. The [[spell]] shop stocks dresses where "each had some special charm, and the hats were [[hope|wish]]ing-caps" (004-018), treating charms as purchasable [[magic|enchantment]]s woven into clothing.

> He belongs to a kind which only lives three hundred years, and then they begin to grow small, and gradually become less and less, till at last they vanish quite into air, unless they can steal seven little girls from whose bones they make a dreadful charm, which gives them life for another hundred years.
>
> (002-028)

### Narrative Function

Charms serve as tangible manifestations of magical power — objects and [[spell|spells]] that can be created, deployed, and crucially, broken. The breaking of a charm marks a turning point in *[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]*, signaling the restoration of moral order. The protective charm in *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]* establishes a magical economy of threat and defense: charms can harm, but also shield. In *[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]*, charms are commodified, available in a shop "where every sort of [[spell]] or charm was sold" (004-018), reflecting [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s lightly satirical treatment of magic as commerce.
