# The Hair Tree

The third tale in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, and the collection's most ambitious work of magical world-building. Unlike the pastoral domesticity of the frame story's embedded tales, "The Hair Tree" sends its hero — a poor sailor named [[rupert|Rupert]] — across oceans and into a fully realized enchanted country where [[spell|spells]] are bought in shops, animals speak in riddles, and guardians are themselves prisoners of older [[magic|enchantment]]s. It is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s fullest engagement with the quest narrative, and its magical ecology — of zirbal nuts, protective [[charm|charms]], and a tree whose seeds both grow hair and break curses — is the collection's most intricate construction.

## Synopsis

[[rupert|Rupert]], a poor sailor who has lost his hair, sees a royal proclamation: Princess [[trevina|Trevina]]'s hair will only regrow with seeds from the legendary Hair Tree. He sails "to the north, and then to the south, and then to the east, and then to the west" (003-031), landing on an island where a golden bird demands zirbal nuts in exchange for directions. The bird reveals that the Hair Tree grows in the country of the red-haired people, accessible only through a magic rhyme:

> "The lion's mane, the silver grain, the salmon's leap are in the deep; the North Wind's roar, the shingly shore, the waters flow; This makes the Hair Tree grow — I know, I know."
>
> — 003-064

[[rupert|Rupert]] follows the rhyme's directions to a magical country where he encounters a tigress — a [[transformation|transform]]ed woman bound by the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] — and learns her tragic history. He navigates enchanted rods that seize intruders, confronts the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] beneath the Hair Tree itself, and breaks her [[magic|enchantment]] with a gift of zirbal nuts. He returns with the seeds, restores Princess [[trevina|Trevina]]'s hair, and marries her — keeping one seed "to hang over his own door as a [[charm]]" (003-184).

## Themes

### The Economy of Magic

"The Hair Tree" is unique in the collection for treating magic as a **transactional system**. The golden bird trades information for zirbal nuts — one nut, one fact. Spell-shops sell protective [[charm|charms]]. The [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] demands all of [[rupert|Rupert]]'s seeds as passage-fee. [[magic|Magic]] here is not innate gift but **commerce** — learnable, purchasable, negotiable. This demystification of magic into a kind of [[fairy]]-tale market economy is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most original contribution to the genre's world-building.

### Transformation and Liberation

Every major non-human character in the tale is **[[transformation|transform]]ed**: the golden bird (whose nature is unexplained), the tigress (a woman enchanted), the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] (a princess cursed). The Hair Tree itself is a site of dual magic — its seeds restore hair (physical [[transformation|transform]]ation) but its roots entangle the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] (magical imprisonment). [[rupert|Rupert]]'s quest liberates not only himself (restored hair) and [[trevina|Trevina]] (restored hair) but also the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] (restored humanity). The tale argues that **liberation is contagious**: freeing one person creates the conditions for freeing others.

### Knowledge as Power

[[rupert|Rupert]]'s success depends not on strength but on **information**: the bird's directions, the rhyme's instructions, the tigress's warnings. His patience in bargaining — one nut per fact — and his willingness to listen to the tigress's story rather than flee are the skills that save him. The tale is a quiet argument for the [[fairy]]-tale hero as **scholar**: the one who asks questions, remembers warnings, and applies knowledge at the right moment.

## Structure and Narrative Features

The tale follows the full **hero's journey** structure more completely than any other in the collection:

1. **Call to Adventure**: The royal proclamation
2. **Crossing the Threshold**: Sailing to the four directions
3. **Supernatural Aid**: The golden bird, the magic rhyme
4. **Trials**: The tigress, the enchanted rods, the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]]
5. **The Boon**: The Hair Tree seeds
6. **The Return**: Sailing home; the six-month time dilation
7. **The Gift**: Marriage to [[trevina|Trevina]]; the seed kept as [[charm]]

The magical country is the collection's richest setting — a realm with its own geography (rivers, caves, islands), economy ([[spell]]-shops), ecology (zirbal nuts, talking animals), and politics (the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]]'s curse, the red-haired people's country). It anticipates the systematic fantasy worlds of twentieth-century literature.

## Place in the Collection

"The Hair Tree" occupies Chapter 003 of *On a Pincushion*. It stands between "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" (a hero-tale of rescue) and "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" (a tale of artificial beauty), bridging the collection's movement from active heroism toward more complex meditations on appearance and reality. Its magical commerce and [[transformation|transform]]ed guardians echo themes from the frame story's embedded tales ([[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]]-cure, the [[the-seeds-of-love|seeds of love]]'s conditional magic) while expanding their scope from domestic to epic.
