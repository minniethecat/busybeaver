# Wisdom

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, wisdom is a quality repeatedly singled out for public celebration. When the wise old [[water-people|water elf]] proposes his plan to steal [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection, "all the elves applauded his wisdom" (001-030) and the lady elves declare that "with such a mind as that, one could rule countries or take cities." Wisdom in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] is not a private virtue but a communal asset — something to be recognized, praised, and followed.

## Overview

Wisdom appears in several forms across the collection: the intellectual wisdom of the old elf, who solves practical problems through clear reasoning; the maternal wisdom of [[taboret|the fairy godmother]] [[taboret|Taboret]], who intervenes in [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]]'s life through careful observation and gentle correction; and the hard-won wisdom of [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], who learns through sacrifice rather than instruction. All three forms share a common thread: wisdom serves others, not the self.

> "He stopped, and all the elves applauded his wisdom again." (001-030)

## In the Narrative

### The Old Elf's Practical Wisdom

The old elf embodies wisdom as clear thinking applied to moral problems. When the [[the-water-people|water people]] debate how to cure [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]], he alone sees the practical solution: steal her reflection so she can no longer admire herself. His reasoning is methodical — first establish that each person has only one reflection, then explain how it moves — and he takes time to educate a young elf who has made an ignorant comment, because he "was never angry with ignorance in the young, but he [[hope|wish]]ed rather to correct it than blame it" (001-030).

His wisdom is democratic: anyone with a good idea is heard, and the best proposal wins applause regardless of its source. The [[the-water-people|water people]]'s miniature society models intellectual meritocracy — wisdom, not rank, confers [[power|authority]].

### The Wisdom of Sacrifice: Siegfrid

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s wisdom is not intellectual but experiential. He gives his right eye to the [[owl|Owl]] as payment for the stolen boots (002-027), and from that sacrifice he gains the knowledge needed to rescue [[handa|Handa]]. His wisdom is costly and irreversible — once given, the eye cannot be restored — but it is the price of effective action.

When the old shoe-seller is finally defeated and the villagers clamor for punishment, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] replies: "his punishment will come of itself. See! it is coming already" (002-045). This is the wisdom of restraint — knowing when not to act — and it marks [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s passage from boy to moral leader.

### Taboret's Maternal Wisdom

[[taboret|Taboret]], [[taboret|the fairy godmother]] in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," represents a different kind of wisdom — patient, invisible, and non-confrontational. She "made herself invisible" (004-011) to observe [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s daily life before deciding on her intervention. Rather than confronting the court directly, she creates a magical substitute so perfect that no one notices. Her wisdom lies in choosing the gentlest effective means, a hallmark of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral [[dream|vision]].

### Wisdom and the Magic Candles

The [[magic-candles|magic candle]]s in the frame narrative also invoke wisdom: they grant the [[hope|wish]] of one's heart, but their power is limited and precious — "requiring wisdom in their use" (001-127). This echoes the broader [[fairy]]-tale tradition in which magical gifts test the recipient's judgment rather than solving their problems outright.

### The Wise Seagull

Even minor characters model wisdom. The seagull in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" offers [[trevina|Trevina]] practical help — carrying a message to her father — rather than empty comfort. Its wisdom is instrumental: identify the problem, find the tool, act. The seagull's advice to "keep a good heart" and "be sure you eat nothing but herbs" (003-097) is survival wisdom, earned through knowledge of the enchanted island's rules.

## Wisdom and Age

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] links wisdom to age consistently: the old elf, [[taboret|Taboret]], the seagull, and even [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen (whose "wisdom" is inverted into cunning) are [[age|elders]]. The young — [[lamorna|Lamorna]], [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], [[trevina|Trevina]] — must acquire wisdom through experience, often painful. This pattern suggests a worldview in which wisdom is not innate but accumulated, and in which [[age|elders]] have a responsibility to use their wisdom for the young.
