# Magic

Magic in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* operates through [[charm|enchanted object]]s — candles, shoes, nuts, sugar, belts — each functioning as a moral test rather than a [[hope|wish]]-fulfillment device.

## Overview

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s magic is never capricious or inherited; it is always *earned through action*. Magic candles reveal what one most desires (001-127), [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s enable rescue missions (002-028), magic nuts guide the lost (003-061), magic sugar [[transformation|transform]]s identity (004-018), and a magic belt saves the imperilled (005-250). The common thread is that magical objects are inert without human courage. They do not solve problems — they enable characters to solve their own.

> "They are magic candles, and when you set them alight, whatever you wish will appear before you." (001-127)

## In the Narrative

### Magical Objects Across Tales

Magic manifests in distinct objects across the five tales:

| Object | Tale | Function | Moral Test |
|--------|------|----------|------------|
| [[magic-candles|Magic Candles]] | On a Pincushion (001) | Grant a [[hope|wish]] when lit | Whether one [[hope|wish]]es [[wisdom|wise]]ly |
| [[magic-shoes|Magic Shoes]] | [[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]] (002) | Enable the wearer to walk over [[the-brook|water]] | Whether one uses speed to help others |
| Magic Nuts / Fruit | [[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]] (003) | Protect against magical threats | Whether one trusts the gift |
| Magic Sugar | [[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]] (004) | Transform appearance | Whether identity is more than surface |
| Magic Belt | [[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]] (005) | Protection from ice | Whether one shares protection |

### The Moral Economy of Magic

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] establishes a consistent rule: magic rewards the brave and punishes the passive. The [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s work because [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] *runs* to save [[handa|Handa]] (002-050). The magic nuts work because the protagonist *continues forward* despite fear (003-063). Even the [[magic-candles|magic candle]]s require someone to *light* them — the [[hope|wish]] is not enough. (001-127) This distinguishes [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairy]]-tale magic from the Grimms' tradition, where magical objects often function automatically once possessed.

### Limits and Costs

Magic in these tales has clear limits. The magic nuts cannot save someone who refuses to act: "Why, if it were not for the magic fruit in your pocket, you would be powerless" (003-061). The magic belt, obtained only after great effort, is described as something "we have been a whole year weaving" (005-250), emphasizing that even magical objects carry a cost in time and labor. Magic is not free.

> "She is in a cavern underground," said the Hare. "She and all the other little girls are sitting there in a row, and they cannot move or speak, because on their feet are magic shoes that the old shoemaker made for them, which hold them as still as marble."
>
> (002-028)


## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s approach to magic reflects late-Victorian [[fairy]]-tale conventions, where the moral purpose of the tale often outweighs the spectacle of enchantment. Unlike Andrew Lang's color [[fairy]] books (published concurrently in the 1880s–1890s), where magic is often decorative, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s magical objects are structurally necessary — each advances a character's moral education. This aligns her more with George MacDonald's *The Princess and the Goblin* (1872), where magic is a test of character, than with the Arabian Nights tradition of magical [[hope|wish]]-fulfillment.
