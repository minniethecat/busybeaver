# The Brook

The stream that runs through [[the-village|Lamorna's village]] and into the sea — a liminal boundary between the human world and the magical realm of the [[the-water-people|water people]], and the stage for [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]], loss, and redemption.

## Overview

The brook is more than scenery in "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]." It functions as a threshold — a space where the human and magical worlds meet. It "ran into the sea about half a mile farther down, and it was full of [[the-water-people|water people]]" (001-010). [[lamorna|Lamorna]] bends over it to admire her reflection, the [[the-water-people|water people]] observe her through it, and her reflection is stolen from its surface. The brook is the site of [[pride|vanity]], magic, and eventually redemption — [[lamorna|Lamorna]] returns to it to find her reflection restored and her love reclaimed.

## In the Narrative

### Mirror and Gateway

The brook serves a dual function. As a mirror, it enables [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] — she can "see herself in her [[reflection|looking-glass]] or the brook" (001-020). As a gateway, it houses the [[the-water-people|water people]] who will cure her. The water's surface is thus both the problem and the solution: the medium of self-absorption is also the medium of magical intervention.

### Site of Transformation

Every major magical event in the tale occurs at or through the brook: [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s self-admiration, the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s observation, the theft of her reflection (001-043), the [[the-water-people|water elves]]' festival (001-117), and ultimately [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reunion with [[erick|Erick]] and her restored self. The brook is a classic [[fairy]]-tale threshold — a place where the ordinary world yields to the magical.

## Literary Context

Bodies of water as thresholds between worlds appear throughout folklore — from the River Styx to the wells of Celtic legend. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s brook is domestic in scale (a village stream, not a great river) but cosmic in function, embodying the literary [[fairy]] tale's characteristic move of finding the magical in the quotidian.

## From the Text

> The girl’s name was Lamorna, and she was the daughter of a farmer. Everyone told her she was very pretty, and so indeed she was. She had bright brown hair and big brown eyes, and a mouth like a rosebud. The brook by which she stood ran into the sea about half a mile farther down, and it was full of water people. Water people are a sort of elves, who live beneath the water, and never come to the surface, because if they were to breathe the air they would die.
>
> (001-010)

> “But you can never cure her of her vanity;” said the first, “as long as she can see herself in her looking-glass or the brook; while she can see her own face, she will continue to be vain and foolish.”
>
> (001-020)

> “Yes, it is lovely,” said she. But she did not lift her eyes from her own image. Then, at a given signal, the water elves threw up their sand ropes, and caught her reflection, and all pulled it together, and Lamorna started back with a shudder.
>
> (001-043)
