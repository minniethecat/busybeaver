# The Sunbeam

A celestial being in "On a Pincushion" whose loneliness — "the Sunbeams never mate" — leads him on a quest for beauty and love that becomes the collection's most tender love story.

## Overview

The Sunbeam is the protagonist of the collection's embedded love story — the tale-within-a-tale of the Sunbeam and the Moonbeam. Introduced as "a little Sunbeam" who "slid down his long golden ladder" (001-220), he is immediately characterized by his solitude: "the Sunbeams never mate. Yet he was the prettiest of all the sunbeams" (001-222). His loneliness drives him to seek beauty, which he finds first in the Moonbeam and then in the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s songs about her. His arc — from isolation to connection, from ignorance to love — is the collection's most emotionally complete character journey.

> "The Sunbeams never mate." (001-222)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The Sunbeam embodies the questing consciousness — the being who does not know what he lacks until beauty and art reveal it to him. His ignorance of the Moonbeam ("I have never seen a Moonbeam," 001-230) is not a flaw but a starting point. Through the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song, he learns to desire, and through desire, to love. His story is a parable of how art (the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s singing) creates the capacity for love.

### Key Actions

- Slides down to earth and begins "to feel very sad" at his solitude (001-220–001-222)
- Asks the [[bullfinch|Bullfinch]] and Wood-dove about beauty, leading to the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] (001-229–001-231)
- Waits faithfully for the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]: "I will wait till the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] comes" (001-235)
- Hears the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song of the Moonbeam and longs to know her (001-239–001-240)
- Returns every evening to talk with the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]], learning to love through stories (001-242)
- Finally sees the Moonbeam and finds her "the loveliest thing he had ever seen" (001-245)

## Character Interpretation

The Sunbeam is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most psychologically complex creation. His loneliness is existential, not circumstantial — it is built into his nature ("the Sunbeams never mate"). His [[transformation|transform]]ation comes not through action but through aesthetic experience: hearing the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song teaches him what he lacks, and wanting what he lacks makes him capable of love. This is a remarkably sophisticated model of emotional development for a Victorian [[fairy]] tale, anticipating the modernist insight that desire is learned, not innate.
