# Vanity

A recurrent moral theme in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s [[fairy]] tales, most prominently explored in "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" where a young woman's self-obsession is cured through the magical intervention of the [[the-water-people|water people]].

## Overview

Vanity in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales is not merely a character flaw but a narrative engine — it drives plot, motivates magical intervention, and provides the moral lesson. The treatment of [[pride|vanity]] is distinctive in its gentleness: rather than punishing the vain character, the narrative arranges for her to be cured through loss and recovery of [[reflection|self-image]]. This reflects a Victorian moral framework that values self-knowledge over self-admiration, but executes it with [[fairy]]-tale delicacy rather than sermonizing. (001-094)

> "And if it had not been for my vanity," she sighed to herself, "I need not have been hurt at all. It was only that which made me want the bird's wing. Ah, what a little thing beauty is to be so vain of!" (001-094)

## In the Narrative

### Lamorna's Vanity

The primary exploration of [[pride|vanity]] comes through [[lamorna|Lamorna]], a farmer's daughter who "does nothing but look at her own reflection, and loves nothing so much as her own pretty face." (001-017) Her [[pride|vanity]] makes her scorn the love of the good fisherman [[erick|Erick]], preferring her own [[reflection|image]] to genuine human connection. The [[the-water-people|water people]] recognize that her [[pride|vanity]] is inextricable from her ability to see herself — as long as mirrors and reflective [[the-brook|water]] show her own face, she will remain self-absorbed. (001-020)

### The Cure

The [[the-water-people|water people]]'s theft of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection is a literalization of [[pride|vanity]]'s cure: remove the object of self-admiration, and the self must look outward. When [[lamorna|Lamorna]] can no longer see her own beauty, she gradually turns her attention to others and recognizes the value of [[erick|Erick]]'s love. Her [[truth|confession]] — that it was [[pride|vanity]] that made her vulnerable to the bird's wing temptation (001-094) — marks her moral growth. The [[the-water-people|water elves]] release her reflection only when they confirm "she is quite cured of her [[pride|vanity]]." (001-098)

### Frame Story Moral

The theme is underlined in the frame narrative when [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] solemnly declares, "Ah, a terrible thing is [[pride|vanity]]!" (001-119) and claims personal experience with it:

> "Ah, a terrible thing is vanity!" said the Shawl-pin solemnly. "I have suffered from it. I myself make a point of pricking anyone who I think is getting too vain." (001-119)

This frames [[pride|vanity]] as a universal failing — even pins and brooches suffer from it — extending the moral beyond the embedded [[fairy]] tale to the storytelling objects themselves.

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of [[pride|vanity]] participates in a long [[fairy]]-tale tradition — from Snow White's stepmother to the Emperor's New Clothes — where [[pride|vanity]] is punished rather than cured. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s innovation is therapeutic: through magical agency, [[pride|vanity]] is healed rather than avenged. This aligns with the broader Victorian [[fairy]]-tale revival's interest in moral education through fantasy, but [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[the-water-people|water people]] are unusually benevolent agents of correction compared to the often cruel [[fairy|fairies]] of folklore.
