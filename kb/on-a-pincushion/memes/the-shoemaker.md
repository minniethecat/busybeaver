# The Shoemaker

The old village shoemaker in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," a master craftsman abandoned by his community whose magical shoes — made long ago — become the instruments of rescue and moral reckoning.

## Overview

The Shoemaker is the tale's moral center even in his absence. By the time the story begins, he has been discarded by the village in favor of a new shoemaker, and he "still sat by the roadside" (002-015), a figure of neglect. But the [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s he made years ago — "[[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s that the old shoemaker made for them, which hold them in a [[fear|dread]]ful [[spells-and-charms|charm]]" (002-028) — become the key to the plot. His son [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] inherits both his craft and his moral legacy, eventually becoming "the village shoemaker, and married [[handa|Handa]], and lived happily ever after" (002-080). The old shoemaker's arc is one of posthumous vindication: the work of his hands outlasts the ingratitude of his neighbors.

> "Magic shoes that the old shoemaker made for them." (002-028)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The Shoemaker functions as an absent presence — like the murdered father in Hamlet, his mistreatment haunts the tale. The community's abandonment of him is the original sin that the magical boots' torment punishes. His craft — shoemaking — is humble, but it carries magical significance: well-made things, made with care, contain power that shoddy replacements cannot match.

### Key Actions

- Made the [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s years before the story begins, binding the wearers with a "[[fear|dread]]ful [[spells-and-charms|charm]]" (002-028)
- Was abandoned by the village: "no one would have anything to do with the old shoemaker" (002-015)
- Sat by the roadside, a visible reminder of the village's ingratitude
- His legacy, through [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]], is restored: [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] becomes village shoemaker (002-080)
- The moral of the tale is spoken in his defense: "he had worked for you all his life" (002-078)

### Relationships

## Character Interpretation

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s Shoemaker echoes the [[fairy]]-tale archetype of the wronged craftsman (the Elves and the Shoemaker, the Sorcerer's Apprentice), but with a darker social critique. The village's abandonment of their old shoemaker for a newer, shinier one reads as a parable of capitalism's disregard for loyalty and craft. The [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s — products of his skilled hands — become the instruments of poetic justice, suggesting that true craft carries its own vindication.
