# Magic Shoes

Enchanted footwear appearing in the tale of [[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]], made by a wicked giant disguised as a shoemaker — instruments of [[power|control]] that ultimately enable escape and liberation.

## Overview

The magic shoes in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tale combine two [[fairy]]-tale motifs: the [[charm|enchanted object]] that compels its wearer, and the magical aid that enables flight from danger. Created by a giant who has disguised himself as a shoemaker in order to capture children (002-028), the shoes force their wearers to dance — but when [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] remembers he is wearing them and runs, they carry [[handa|Handa]] to safety. The shoes thus embody the dual nature of [[charm|enchanted object]]s in [[fairy]] tales: they can enslave or liberate, depending on the wearer's awareness and intent.

> "He is a giant, who is very cruel and wicked. He has disguised himself as a shoemaker, that he may steal little boys and girls, and he will not let us go until someone breaks the spell." (002-028)

## In the Narrative

### The Giant's Trap

The magic shoes are part of a larger [[magic|enchantment]]: the giant-shoemaker captures children by fitting them with shoes that bind them to his [[servant|service]]. The shoes represent captivity disguised as craftsmanship — beautiful footwear that is actually a chain. This reflects [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s recurring theme of appearances deceiving, paralleling [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s beautiful face hiding a [[vanity|vain]] heart.

### Siegfrid's Liberation

When [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] enters the giant's workshop and discovers the captive children, the breaking point comes when he remembers the magic shoes on [[handa|Handa]]'s feet. Rather than removing them, he uses their power — running with [[handa|Handa]], the shoes propel them beyond the giant's reach. (002-050) This inversion — using the captor's own magic against him — is a clever [[fairy]]-tale resolution that rewards quick thinking over brute force.

## Literary Context

Enchanted shoes appear widely in European folklore: the Seven-League Boots of French tales, the red dancing shoes of Andersen, and the glass slippers of Cinderella. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s version is distinctive in making the shoes a tool of the villain that the hero repurposes — a narrative choice that emphasizes agency and wit over passive receipt of magical aid.
