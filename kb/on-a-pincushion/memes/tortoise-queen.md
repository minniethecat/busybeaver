# The Tortoise Queen

The tortoise-queen is the more actively malicious half of [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]] royal family in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" — a creature whose "eyes sparkling with [[cruelty|spite]]ful pleasure" (003-095) embody the tale's understanding of cruelty as something *enjoyed*.

## Overview

Where [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king is a suitor — possessive, grotesque, but driven by desire — his mother [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen is driven by pure [[cruelty|spite]]. She appears at the climax of [[trevina|Trevina]]'s embedded narrative, delivering the curse that [[transformation|transform]]s the heroine: "you may be very thankful if you remain in your present condition; but if you only eat one mouthful of flesh, you will become a real tigress, and then you can never regain your proper form" (003-095). Her eyes sparkle "with [[cruelty|spite]]ful pleasure" — the adverb is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most damning characterization. The tortoise-queen does not merely do evil; she *enjoys* it.

> "And now," cried the tortoise-queen, her eyes sparkling with spiteful pleasure. (003-095)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The tortoise-queen is [[darkness|the dark]]er half of [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]] monarchy — [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king kidnaps, but [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen *punishes*. Her curse on [[trevina|Trevina]] is elaborate in its cruelty: [[transformation|transform]]ation into a tigress, the prohibition against eating flesh, and the cryptic condition for reversal — being "beaten till you bleed, by a man with the rods" (003-095). The complexity of the curse suggests a mind that has thought carefully about suffering. She is the tale's most purely malicious figure, lacking even [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king's misguided [[love|affection]].

### Key Actions

- Delivers the curse that [[transformation|transform]]s [[trevina|Trevina]] into a tigress (003-095)
- Sets the elaborate conditions for the curse's reversal — eating flesh triggers permanent [[transformation|transform]]ation, only a specific ritual can restore human form
- Speaks with "eyes sparkling with [[cruelty|spite]]ful pleasure," revealing her enjoyment of cruelty

## Character Interpretation

The tortoise-queen represents cruelty in its purest [[fairy]]-tale form: magical, irreversible-seeming, and motivated by nothing more than [[cruelty|spite]]. She is not a wronged figure seeking justice; she is a powerful figure who harms because she can, and who takes visible pleasure in the harm. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s addition of "her eyes sparkling with [[cruelty|spite]]ful pleasure" [[transformation|transform]]s what could be a generic wicked-queen figure into something more psychologically acute — a portrait of [[cruelty|malice]] as *enjoyment*, not merely action.


The [[the-tortoise-queen|Tortoise-Queen]] can also be understood as [[darkness|the dark]] mother archetype — maternal instinct perverted into [[jealousy|possessiveness]] and class-based cruelty. Her claim that [[tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king should have married the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] (another monster) rather than a "common human being" introduces a taxonomy of supernatural hierarchy that runs through the collection: even among monsters, there are ranks. Her punishment of [[trevina|Trevina]] — [[transformation|transform]]ation — is thematically precise: [[trevina|Trevina]] rejected a beast, so she shall become one. The Queen's power over her son (he kneels before her and cannot alter her decrees) suggests a matriarchal structure among these creatures, inverting the patriarchal dynamics of the human world [[trevina|Trevina]] comes from.
