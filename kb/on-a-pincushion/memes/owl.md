## Identity

The Owl is a bird character who appears in two tales: the frame story of "On a Pincushion" and the tale of "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]." He occupies a position of [[power|authority]] in the forest, described in the first tale as the "chief judge" who "always ate the little birds when they did not behave themselves" (001-249). He is characterised by his mercenary nature and his peculiar desire to collect eyes.

## Role in "On a Pincushion"

In the frame story, the [[bullfinch|Bullfinch]] threatens to report the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s disruptive nocturnal singing to the Owl: "she would speak to the Owl about it, and have it stopped. For the Owl was chief judge, and always ate the little birds when they did not behave themselves" (001-249). Although the Owl does not appear directly in this tale, his fearsome reputation as an enforcer of forest discipline is established.

## Role in "Siegfrid and Handa"

The Owl plays a more substantial role in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]." When [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] and the [[the-hare|Hare]] seek someone to steal all the gnome's boots and shoes from the sleeping villagers, the [[the-hare|Hare]] suggests: "Let us go and ask the Owl. He flies about at night when there is no one to see" (002-032).

The Owl agrees to help — but only for a price: "I will do it for you if you will pay me. If you will give me one of your eyes, I will bring you everyone's boots from the village tonight" (002-033). When [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] questions why the Owl wants his eye, the Owl replies bluntly: "That's not my business. I want your eye, and I will not get you the shoes without it. I am very blind, and I think I might see better with your eye than I do with my own" (002-035).

True to his word, the Owl delivers the pile of boots and collects his payment — [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s right eye. He "flew away with it, hooting in triumph" (002-043). This sacrifice becomes [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s permanent mark of [[love|devotion]] to [[handa|Handa]].

> "I will do it for you if you will pay me. If you will give me one of your eyes, I will bring you everyone's boots from the village tonight, after they are all in bed."
>
> (002-033)

## Significance

The Owl embodies a harsh, transactional justice — he will help, but only at a cost. Unlike the [[kindness|compassion]]ate [[the-hare|Hare]] who helps [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] out of gratitude, the Owl's assistance comes with a painful and irreversible price, underscoring the tale's theme that [[love|true love]] requires sacrifice.
