# Pride

Pride in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* spans a moral spectrum: at one end, the dignified self-respect of a craftsman; at the other, the vanity that precedes a fall.

## Overview

The word "pride" appears sparingly — only twice across the corpus — but the concept pervades the tales. A young elf "prided himself on his good manners" (001-026), and [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s foster parents regard Oliver as "their great pride" (004-065). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] distinguishes between pride as justified self-respect (the craftsman's pride in good work) and pride as vanity ([[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s self-admiration), treating the former as a virtue and the latter as a trap.

> "Oliver, who was their great pride." (004-065)

## In the Narrative

### Proper Pride

The young elf who "prided himself on his good manners" (001-026) represents pride in its healthy form: satisfaction in one's own virtues, grounded in actual accomplishment. This elf's pride is not arrogance but self-awareness — he knows he has cultivated good manners, and he values them. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] does not critique this pride; she presents it as natural and even admirable.

### Pride as Love

When [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s foster parents regard Oliver as "their great pride" (004-065), pride shifts from self-regard to regard for another. This is pride as love — the joy one takes in another's qualities. It is the collection's most generous form of the emotion, entirely outward-directed and free of the corruption that attends self-focused pride.

### The Spectrum's Dark End

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s vanity — though the word "pride" is never applied to her — represents pride's destructive form. Her obsession with her own beauty is pride in its most self-referential mode: the self admiring only itself. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s narrative structure implies the distinction: pride in what one *does* (manners, craftsmanship, raising a good child) is healthy; pride in what one *is* (beautiful, high-born) is corrupting.

## Literary Context

Victorian moralists often condemned pride as the deadliest of the seven deadly sins. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s more nuanced treatment — distinguishing productive from destructive pride — aligns her with the psychological realism emerging in late-Victorian fiction (George Eliot, Henry James) rather than with sermonizing tradition.
