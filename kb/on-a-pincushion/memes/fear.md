# Fear

Fear is the most pervasive emotional experience in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, afflicting characters in every tale and [[servant|serving]] as the primary obstacle that must be overcome for any happy resolution.

## Overview

Fear appears twenty times across the corpus, touching every major character and every tale. [[lamorna|Lamorna]] shivers with fear at the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s song (001-079); [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s companion shakes with fear in the forest (002-063); the Queen of the Hair Tree screams at a fearful sight (003-018). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] does not judge fear — her characters all feel it — but she judges the *response* to fear. Those who act despite fear are rewarded; those paralyzed by it are not.

> "Lamorna shivered with fear as she listened." (001-079)

## In the Narrative

### Fear as Universal Experience

No character in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales is fearless. Kings fear for their kingdoms, mothers fear for their children, and even the brave protagonist of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" enters only because he is one "who enters without fear" (003-063) — a phrase repeated twice (003-106), suggesting how rare and remarkable fearlessness is. The [[the-brook|water]]-people's victim confesses "I began to fear I must give myself up" (003-088). Fear is the default human condition in this story-world.

### Fear of Others' Judgment

A subtler fear runs alongside physical terror: the fear of being laughed at or misunderstood. [[lamorna|Lamorna]] "dare not tell anyone, for fear they should laugh or think I'm mad" (001-067). [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] "would not go home for fear he should be kept there" (002-037). This social fear — fear of shame, of being trapped, of losing autonomy — is treated as seriously as physical danger, if not more so.

### Overcoming Fear

The tales' moral architecture is clearest in their treatment of fear. Those who act *despite* fear succeed: the protagonist who enters the magic country despite the warning, [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] who runs across [[the-brook|water]] in [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s despite the danger. But fear must be *felt* first for courage to be meaningful. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world has no place for unearned [[courage|bravery]]; courage is defined by the fear it overcomes.

## Literary Context

Fear in Victorian children's literature was often sanitized or moralized away. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s approach is notably honest: fear is real, reasonable, and universal. This aligns her more with the psychological realism of George MacDonald (who also depicted children's genuine terror) than with the didactic tradition that treated fear as simply a moral failing. The repetition of "fear" — twenty times in a short collection — makes it almost a rhythmic element, a bass note beneath the brighter melodies of courage and love.
