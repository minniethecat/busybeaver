# Patience

Patience in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is not a passive virtue but an active discipline — one that magical beings explicitly demand and that determines whether a character's story will be heard.

## Overview

The word "patience" appears at critical narrative junctures in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." The tortoise-queen commands: "you shall hear me to the end with patience" (003-079) — patience is here a prerequisite for storytelling, a tribute the listener must pay. The demand is repeated: "I tried to seize my chance, but again the Queen bade me hear him to the end with patience" (003-087). And when patience fails, the consequences are immediate — the Queen, "unable to contain her impatience any longer, dashed through the crowd" (003-179), her loss of self-[[power|control]] marking a dramatic turning point. In [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world, patience is the quality that earns access to knowledge; impatience is the flaw that precipitates crisis.

> "You shall hear me to the end with patience." (003-079)

## In the Narrative

### Patience as Tribute

The tortoise-queen's demand for patience is not casual — it is a formal requirement. The listener must demonstrate the discipline to hear a story through to its end before acting. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents this as a kind of moral test: those who cannot wait, cannot understand. The Queen's repeated insistence on patience (003-079, 003-087) suggests that the magical beings of this world treat attention itself as a form of respect, and impatience as a form of disrespect.

### The Cost of Impatience

The Queen's loss of patience in the climactic scene is rendered with striking physicality — she "dashed through the crowd, and pushing the duchess on one side, sprang into the black chair herself" (003-179). Her impatience is not merely an emotional state but a violent action that disrupts the social order. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] draws a sharp line between the patient who earns their outcome and the impatient who seize it — and suggests that seizing, however dramatic, is not the same as deserving.
