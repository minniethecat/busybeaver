# The Pin

A common brass pin buried deep in [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] in the frame narrative of *On a Pincushion*, whose practicality and democratic sensibility make it the natural initiator of the storytelling that structures the entire collection. (001-001)

## Overview

The Pin is the most relatable of the three pincushion objects. Unlike the colorful Brooch and the aristocratic Shawl-pin, the Pin's complaint is practical: "I am always needed, and so many pins are taken every day that it seems hard I should be left here for nearly a week, and all because I am run so far into [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] that nothing but my head can be seen." (001-004) Its problem is not lost status but simple neglect — it is useful but invisible, a condition that gives it a different kind of sympathy from [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]'s [[pride|vanity]] or [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]]'s nostalgia.

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The Pin proposes the storytelling that generates the entire embedded narrative structure: "Let us do something to drown their noise. Let us tell stories." (001-006) This is a democratic gesture — anyone can tell a story, not just the colorful or the aristocratic. The Pin's very plainness makes it the ideal initiator: it has no story of its own to tell, but it creates the conditions for others to tell theirs.

### Key Actions

- Complains that it is needed but invisible, buried too deep in [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]]
- Proposes storytelling as the solution to the [[bracelets|Bracelets]]' noise
- Listens to [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]'s tale as audience

### Relationships

- [[the-brooch]] — the first storyteller to answer its proposal
- [[the-shawl-pin]] — the moral commentator on the tale
