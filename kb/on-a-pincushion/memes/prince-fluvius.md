# Prince Fluvius

The [[the-brook|water]] Prince in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" — son of the [[the-brook|water]] King, who falls in love with [[princess-pyra|the fire Princess]] [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] across the elemental boundary between their two realms. His name, from Latin *fluvius* (river), reflects his aqueous nature.

## Identity and Appearance

Prince Fluvius is the son of the [[the-brook|water]] King, whose realm is the mortal enemy of [[fire-element|Fire]]land — the [[the-brook|water]] King being the fire King's "bitterest enemy" (005-037). When he first appears to [[jack|Jack]], he arrives on a white cloud that floats up outside the window, stepping from it "gorgeously dressed in silver and green" (005-129). He has "long dark curls hanging down, and a sweet pale face, with eyes of a deep blue, just the colour of the sea" (005-129). [[jack|Jack]] thinks him, next to the Princess, "the most beautiful little creature he had ever seen" (005-129).

Before his [[transformation|transform]]ation, his appearance is distinctly [[the-brook|water]]y — his hair looks heavy and wet, his clothes are shiny with [[the-brook|water]]. After his union with [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]], his [[transformation|transform]]ation is striking: "his hair had lost its wet gloss, and was dry and curly; his clothes looked crisp and firm" and his eyes are "bright and clear" (005-238).

## The Forbidden Love

The Prince first sees [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] when she is at school in a [[fire-element|burning]] mountain — he "came and looked over the top of the mountain, and saw our Princess; and they fell in love with each other" (005-034). The romance is impossible by elemental law: if they approach, "he should be dried up, or she should be put out" (005-037). In addition to the physical barrier, their fathers are sworn enemies, making political union equally impossible.

When the fire King discovers [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] speaking with Prince Fluvius, he takes her home in a rage, and the lovers are separated.

## The Meeting at Jack's Window

The Prince is summoned by [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]]'s [[spell]]-song, which she sings through [[jack|Jack]]'s fire. A [[fairy|water-fairy]], compelled by the magical [[fire-element|heat]] of her song, fetches him. He arrives on a white cloud and kneels on the windowsill in the pouring rain, separated from his beloved by the few feet of mortal room between the grate and the window — an impassable distance for elemental beings.

His [[love|devotion]] is absolute. When he sees [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] after their separation he declares: "It is you, my darling. And I believed I should never see you again. Oh, let me only once take you in my arms!" (005-131). When the Princess warns this would be fatal, he answers: "At any rate, we should perish together" (005-133) — willing to face extinction rather than continued separation.

## The North Pole Quest

The Prince cannot travel to the [[north-pole|North Pole]] himself — "I should be frozen directly I got to the ice-people, and never return to you" (005-137). He must rely on [[jack|Jack]] to make the journey on their behalf. Before [[jack|Jack]] departs with the [[fairy|wind-fairy]], Prince Fluvius touches [[jack|Jack]] on the head, causing him to shrink to [[fairy]] size (005-152). His parting words show his gentle character: "You need not be afraid, little [[jack|Jack]]. You have nothing to do but to sit on his shoulders, and he will take you quite safely" (005-151).

## Transformation

When [[jack|Jack]] returns with the old man's answer — "What puts out fire but [[the-brook|water]]? what dries up [[the-brook|water]] but fire? Tell him to give her a kiss!" (005-210) — the Prince initially misunderstands, saying with a sigh: "It is as I thought. He means that there is no hope for us, and that we must perish together" (005-230). He declares himself willing, "as anything would be better than life without you, my [[princess-pyra|Pyra]]" (005-230).

The Princess understands the true meaning: they must embrace their opposite natures to be [[transformation|transform]]ed. The Prince "swept down from the window, and a flood of [[the-brook|water]] splashed on to the floor," and the two rush into each other's arms (005-233). The union produces "a great crash — a sound like a clap of thunder" (005-234).

After the smoke clears, Fluvius is [[transformation|transform]]ed — his [[the-brook|water]]y nature tempered by fire into something new, neither pure [[the-brook|water]] nor pure [[fire-element|flame]] but a synthesis. He stands beside [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] with "his arm about the Princess, and she leaned her head on his shoulder" (005-236). When real tears fall from [[princess-pyra|Pyra]]'s eyes for the first time, "the Prince stooped to kiss them away" (005-239).

## The Magic Belt and Farewell

A year later, Fluvius and [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] return together to [[jack|Jack]]'s bedside, carrying between them a silver hoop — a magic belt they have spent "a whole year making" (005-250). After placing it on the [[death|dying]] boy to cure his lameness, they depart forever to "live on the other side of the moon" (005-250).

## Significance

Prince Fluvius represents the complementary opposite in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s elemental allegory. Where [[princess-pyra|Pyra]] is fire — active, bright, singing [[spell|spells]] — Fluvius is [[the-brook|water]] — steady, mournful, [[patience|waiting]] faithfully in the rain. His willingness to face extinction for love mirrors [[princess-pyra|Pyra]]'s courage, making their union a tale of mutual risk and reward rather than one-sided rescue.
