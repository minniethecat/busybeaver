# Mercy

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, mercy is not freely given but must be begged for — and the response to that plea reveals the moral core of the powerful.

## Overview

The word "mercy" appears at the pivotal moment of [[trevina|Trevina]]'s story within "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." The plea is rendered with physical submission: "at the Queen's feet to beg for mercy" (003-079). The phrase is repeated — "at the Queen's feet to beg for mercy, but I was not allowed to remain" (003-096) — suggesting that the plea for mercy is a ritual gesture in this story-world, one whose outcome is never guaranteed. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats mercy not as an abstract virtue but as a transaction between the powerful and the powerless, with the supplicant's physical posture — at the feet — underscoring the asymmetry.

> "At the Queen's feet to beg for mercy, but I was not allowed to remain." (003-079)

## In the Narrative

### Mercy as Ritual

The act of begging for mercy in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" follows a precise choreography: the supplicant approaches, falls to their knees at the feet of power, and makes their plea. The repetition of the phrase across two passages (003-079, 003-096) suggests this is not a spontaneous cry but a recognized form — mercy-seeking has its own protocol in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairy]]-tale courts. The formality paradoxically heightens the emotional stakes: if even a properly performed plea can be refused, mercy is truly unpredictable.

### Mercy Denied

The second occurrence of the phrase carries a darker weight: "but I was not allowed to remain" (003-096). The plea for mercy has been made in proper form, yet the supplicant is dismissed — mercy withheld not through explicit refusal but through exclusion, a removal from the presence where mercy might be granted. This is a distinctively [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] touch: cruelty is often not active [[cruelty|malice]] but the refusal to listen, the denial of audience.
