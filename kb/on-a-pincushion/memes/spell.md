# Spell

A verbal or formulaic magical working — distinguished from a physical [[charm]] by its origin in speech or song rather than a material object. Spells in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales can be hostile curses, protective incantations, or commercial [[magic|enchantment]]s.

## Overview

Spells represent magic enacted through language. Unlike [[charm|charms]], which are bound to physical objects (bones, clothing, nuts), spells operate through the spoken or sung word. In *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]*, spells are hostile forces requiring magical protection (003-104). In *[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]*, spells are commodified alongside [[charm|charms]] in a shop "where every sort of spell or [[charm]] was sold" (004-018), suggesting a magical economy where verbal and material magic are distinct but parallel goods. In *[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]*, a spell takes the form of song — "a spell the Princess sang" to make fire endurable (005-126) — blending incantation with music.

## In the Narrative

### Appearances

Spells appear in three tales, each illustrating a different aspect of verbal magic:

**Spells as Threat.** In *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]*, spells are primarily hostile forces. The antagonist declares that a protective [[charm]] "has protected you from my spells" (003-061), positioning spells as weapons wielded by the powerful. The magical nut given for defense works "against every sort of spell" (003-104), and the request to "give me a nut to protect me from spells" (003-145) underscores the pervasiveness of spell-based danger in this tale's magical ecosystem.

**Spells as Commerce.** In *[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]*, the spell shop visited by [[taboret|Taboret]] sells "every sort of spell or [[charm]]" (004-018), placing spells alongside physical [[magic|magical objects]] as purchasable commodities. This commercialization of verbal magic reflects [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s gently ironic treatment of [[fairy]]-tale conventions.

**Spells as Song.** In *[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]*, the Princess's spell is sung — "it was a spell the Princess sang, and if she ceased to sing the [[fire-element|flames]] would scorch him" (005-126). This musical spell links magic to artistic expression, requiring continuous performance to maintain its effect.


> But, on hearing this, the Princess began to sing again in the same soft voice, growing louder and louder, till the water-fairy sprang up, promising to bring Prince Fluvius, or do anything else Jack wished, if only the song would cease, as he could not endure the heat, for it was a spell the Princess sang, and if she had gone on he would have been dried up altogether.
>
> (005-126)

### Narrative Function

Spells in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world are tools of power asymmetry: the powerful cast spells, the vulnerable seek protection from them. The recurring motif of protective nuts and [[charm|charms]] against spells (003-104, 003-145) establishes a defensive magical economy. The Princess's sung spell (005-126) introduces a benevolent counterpoint — magic used not to harm but to shield another from harm, sustained through the effort of continuous song.
