# Fire

The most physically present and narratively versatile elemental force in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. Fire is neither sacred nor satanic in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral universe — it is a **tool** whose meaning is determined entirely by its wielder and context. It destroys the giant's enchanted boots and liberates captive children. It tests a Princess's [[patience|endurance]] through sustained magical flame. It marks supernatural creatures with sparks from their bodies. Fire is the element of **agency**: unlike [[the-brook|water]] (passive reflection) or ice (stasis), fire requires someone to tend it, direct it, or endure it.

## Definition and Dual Nature

Fire in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales operates through a fundamental duality:

| Aspect | Manifestation | Key Example |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Fire as Weapon** | Destroys evil's instruments; liberates | [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] burning the giant's boots (002-048) |
| **Fire as Trial** | Tests [[patience|endurance]] and magical skill | [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]]'s sung fire-protection [[spell]] (005-126) |
| **Fire as Identity** | Marks magical beings; natural expression | Creature shooting "sparks of fire" (003-112) |
| **Fire as Home** | The elemental kingdom of [[fireland|Fireland]] | [[jack|Jack]]'s journey into the fireplace (005-069) |

This duality is not a contradiction but a **moral argument**: fire's goodness or danger depends entirely on the intention behind its use. The same flames that liberate children could consume them if wielded carelessly.

## Fire as Weapon: The Liberation of the Workshop

The collection's most dramatic use of fire occurs in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," when the captive children of the giant's workshop cry out:

> "Let us set fire to them at once."
>
> — 002-045

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] acts. The boots — [[charm|enchanted object]]s that bind children to the giant's servitude — "soon caught fire, and burnt steadily" (002-048). The fire's effect is immediate and total: the [[magic|enchantment]] breaks, the instruments of captivity are destroyed, and the path to freedom opens.

> The boots soon caught fire, and burnt steadily. When they were quite consumed, Siegfrid remembered the magic shoes.
>
> — 002-048

This is fire as **liberation** — the most primal magical weapon, requiring no [[spell]], no training, only the courage to strike the match. The giant's power, built on craft and [[magic|enchantment]], is undone by an element that predates both. Fire here is the democratizing force: any child can light a flame; not every child can break a [[spell]].

## Fire as Trial: The Princess's Song

In "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," fire takes its opposite form — not weapon but **ordeal**. [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] endures extreme heat through continuous magical song:

> If she ceased to sing for one moment she would have been burned to death.
>
> — 005-126

The sung [[spell]] is not a one-time [[magic|enchantment]] but a **sustained act of will** — every moment the Princess sings, she lives; the instant she stops, she dies. Fire-as-trial is the crucible that proves character: [[patience|endurance]] rather than strength, persistence rather than power.

The Princess's fire-trial also functions as a **narrative filter**: only someone willing to endure can reach her. [[jack]] witnesses her ordeal but is not subjected to it — his role is witness and messenger, not fellow sufferer. Fire distinguishes between the one who endures and the one who enables.

## Fireland: The Elemental Kingdom

[[jack|Jack]]'s journey into the fireplace reveals [[fireland|Fireland]] — a fully realized elemental realm:

> He stood in the middle of rich, red-glowing hills, from which sprouted jets of flame, like trees. Here and there was a black mountain, which smoked and hissed most alarmingly.
>
> — 005-069

[[fireland|Fireland]] is the most vividly described of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s elemental kingdoms. Its geography is active — hills [[light|glow]], jets sprout, mountains smoke — and its architecture is made of fire itself: "different-coloured fire, spouting out all over [[the-castle|the palace]]. There was blue fire, and red fire, and green fire, and yellow fire" (005-069). The garden's "flowers" are fireworks — catherine-wheels and rockets — in a brilliant inversion of the natural/artificial distinction.

[[fireland|Fireland]]'s society mirrors the human: a King who arranges his daughter's marriage, courtiers who gossip, a Princess who defies her father for love. Fire is not alien but **familiar** — a kingdom with politics and heartbreak, rendered in flame.

## Magical Fire: Sparks and Identity

Not all fire in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world is wielded — some is **innate**. A magical creature in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" "shot out sparks of fire" (003-112), fire emerging from its body as naturally as breath. This naturalized fire — not weapon, not trial, but **identity** — completes the element's range. Fire can be separate from its wielder (a tool), inflicted on a subject (a test), or inseparable from a being (a nature).

## Elemental Relationships

Fire's position in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s elemental cosmology is defined by its opposites and complements:

- **Fire and Water**: The central elemental conflict. [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]] (fire) and [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] ([[the-brook|water]]) cannot touch — "lest he should be dried up, or she should be put out" (005-087). Their love is the collection's most explicit statement that elemental boundaries are **tragic constraints**, not moral categories.
- **Fire and Ice**: [[jack|Jack]]'s journey moves from fire ([[fireland|Fireland]]) through [[the-brook|water]] (the sea) to ice (the northern realm). Fire is the starting point, the **energizing origin** from which all subsequent elemental travel proceeds.
- **Fire and Air**: The [[fairy|wind-fairy]] carries [[jack|Jack]]; fire needs air to burn. Their cooperation — wind carrying the fire-touched mortal — suggests that elemental cooperation, not conflict, is the natural order that love can restore.

## Literary Significance

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of fire participates in the long literary tradition of fire as dual symbol — Promethean gift and destructive conflagration — while grounding it in a **systematic elemental cosmology** unusual in Victorian children's fantasy. Her innovation is to make fire's moral valence entirely contextual: the same flames that liberate in one story test in another and identify in a third. Fire is not *good* or *evil* — it is **available**, and what matters is who reaches for it and why.

This treatment prefigures the systematic magic of later fantasy literature, where elements have rules, relationships, and political structures rather than merely symbolic meanings. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fireland|Fireland]] — a kingdom with a King, a Princess, court politics, and heartbreak — is a step toward the world-built fantasies of the twentieth century.
