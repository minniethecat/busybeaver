# Jealousy

Jealousy is the most destructive force in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, driving the collection's darkest acts: the killing of a [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]], the theft of love, and a mother's murderous [[hope|wish]] against her own child.

## Overview

Jealousy appears only three times across the corpus, but each appearance marks a critical narrative turn. The jealous [[fairy]] kills [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]] and steals the King's love (001-208). The [[the-brook|water]]-maiden fears "the jealousy of my mother, who [[hope|wish]]es me to put an end to my life" (003-079, 003-086). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents jealousy as uniquely corrosive — unlike fear (which can be overcome) or grief (which can motivate action), jealousy only destroys.

> "The wicked fairy had killed her rose-tree from jealousy, and had stolen the King's love." (001-208)

## In the Narrative

### Jealousy as Theft

The [[fairy]]'s jealousy in "On a Pincushion" manifests as a double crime: destruction of beauty (the [[the-rose-tree|rose-tree]]) and theft of love (the King's [[love|affection]]). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] does not present the [[fairy]]'s jealousy as pained [[hope|longing]]; it is active, malicious, and effective. The [[fairy]] *succeeds* in her destruction, which makes the moral point sharper — jealousy works, in the short term, and that is precisely what makes it so dangerous.

### Maternal Jealousy

The darkest iteration is in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," where a mother's jealousy threatens her own child. The [[the-brook|water]]-maiden twice states that she fears "the jealousy of my mother, who [[hope|wish]]es me to put an end to my life" (003-079, 003-086). This is jealousy turned inward on the family — a parent who cannot bear her child's [[life|existence]]. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s willingness to depict this makes "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" the collection's most psychologically complex tale.

### Jealousy's Aftermath

Notably, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] shows jealousy's consequences but never its interiority. We see what jealousy does but never what it feels like from within. This narrative choice — treating jealousy as pure external action — emphasizes its destructive effects while denying it the sympathy that grief or fear might earn. Jealousy in these tales has no voice, only victims.

## Literary Context

Victorian [[fairy]] tales frequently featured jealous antagonists (Snow White's stepmother is the archetype), but [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment is distinctive in two ways: first, jealousy crosses family boundaries (mother against daughter), and second, jealousy is never punished directly — the tales move on. This narrative indifference to poetic justice makes the moral point more chillingly: jealousy damages, and the world does not always repair the damage.
