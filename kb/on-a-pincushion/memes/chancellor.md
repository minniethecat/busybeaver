## Identity

The Chancellor is a court official in the kingdom of "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]." He appears alongside the [[prime-minister|Prime Minister]] as one of the King's chief advisors and participates in the council that decides the fate of the two Princesses [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] — the real one and the enchanted toy.

## Role in "A Toy Princess"

When the [[fairy]] [[taboret|Taboret]] reveals to the court that the Princess they have known is actually a toy substitute and that the real [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] has been raised by a farming family, the Chancellor is among the sceptics. He questions the [[fairy]]'s claim directly, asking the King: "Has your Majesty any reason to complain of her Royal Highness's conduct?" (004-074).

When the King admits he has none, the Chancellor dismisses [[the-substitution-reveal|Taboret's revelation]]: "Then I don't see what reason your Majesty can have for paying any attention to what this — this person says" (004-076). This prompts [[taboret|Taboret]] to demonstrate the truth by striking off the toy Princess's head, revealing the hollow shell beneath.

> "Then I don't see," said the Chancellor, "what reason your Majesty can have for paying any attention to what this—this person says."
>
> (004-076)

Later, when the council is asked to vote between the real [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] and the toy one, the Chancellor is firmly in favour of the sham. He agrees with the [[prime-minister|Prime Minister]] that the choice is obvious: "I should think not either" (004-110). The council votes unanimously for the toy Princess, and the real [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] is returned to the farming family where she happily marries Oliver.

## Significance

The Chancellor embodies the court's preference for artifice and decorum over authenticity and spontaneity. The real [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s natural behaviour — running to embrace the King, speaking freely, weeping openly — shocks the court, while the toy Princess's mechanical politeness and her perfect final words ("Just so") are deemed the model of royal conduct.
