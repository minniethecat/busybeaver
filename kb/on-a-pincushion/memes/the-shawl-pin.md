# The Shawl-pin

A jet shawl-pin on [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] in the frame narrative of *On a Pincushion*, who has "seen better days" and responds to [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s story with the tale's most memorable moral pronouncement.

## Overview

The Shawl-pin is the most class-conscious of the three pincushion objects. It "remember[s] the time when I was never used to pin up anything but the finest Indian and Cashmere shawls," marking it as an aristocrat fallen on hard times. Its jet-black color gives it a somber [[pride|dignity]] that contrasts with [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]'s colorful [[pride|vanity]]. After hearing [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]'s tale of [[lamorna|Lamorna]], the Shawl-pin delivers the story's moral with personal conviction: "Ah, a terrible thing is [[pride|vanity]]! I have suffered from it. I myself make a point of pricking anyone who I think is getting too [[vanity|vain]]." (001-119)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The Shawl-pin serves as moral commentator — not a storyteller itself, but the audience that draws the lesson. Its personal [[truth|confession]] ("I have suffered from it") makes the moral feel earned rather than preachy. The detail that it now actively pricks [[vanity|vain]] people gives it a continued purpose despite being relegated to [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]].

### Key Actions

- Complains about its fall from pinning cashmere shawls to [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]]
- Suggests the [[bracelets|Bracelets]]' chattering disturbs its nerves
- Delivers the tale's closing moral on [[pride|vanity]] with personal testimony (001-119)

### Relationships

- [[the-brooch]] — fellow complainer whose story it judges
- [[the-pin]] — the practical object who proposed storytelling
- [[vanity]] — the theme it claims personal experience with
