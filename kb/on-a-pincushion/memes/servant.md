# Servant

Service in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* spans the mundane and the magical: mortal servants in splendid procession, tiny sunbeam [[fairy|fairies]] serving the Sun, and a [[fairy]]-tale encounter where a character seeks a servant's position in an ice realm.

## Overview

The word "servant" and its variants thread through [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales at three distinct registers. There are the human servants of courts and processions — "behind were servants and pages leading hounds" (001-131) — part of the [[fairy]]-tale pageantry that establishes the world's social order. There are the magical servants of nature: the Sun "every morning tells his tiny servants, the beams, where they are to go" (001-220), recasting sunlight itself as willing service. And there is the [[fairy]]-tale encounter in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," where a character seeking work is told: "I am in want of a servant, if that's what you are come for" (005-184). Service is never degraded in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world — it is simply a relationship, capable of [[pride|dignity]] or tyranny depending on the master.

## In the Narrative

### Mortal Service as Spectacle

The courtly procession in "On a Pincushion" deploys servants as visual splendor: riders "splendidly dressed in coloured velvets and gold" with the King's son in "burnished gold, that sparkled and shone in the sunlight," and "behind were servants and pages leading hounds" (001-131). The servants are part of the aesthetic order, neither criticized nor sentimentalized — [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] simply registers their presence as part of a complete world.

### Elemental Servants

The most striking reimagining of service comes in the description of sunbeams: "All the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]]s are in reality tiny Sun-[[fairy|fairies]], run down to earth on golden ladders" (001-220). The Sun assigns them tasks each morning — service as a cosmic principle, woven into the fabric of nature itself. This reframes the servant-master relationship from a social hierarchy to a natural order: even light serves.

### The Servant's Contract

In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," the servant-seeking encounter (005-184) presents service as a contract — "I am very particular about my ice being bright" — with explicit expectations on both sides. The would-be master's demands are specific and the servant's acceptance is voluntary. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of service is notably free of the Victorian moralizing that typically accompanied the theme; she presents it as a practical relationship, capable of [[pride|dignity]].
