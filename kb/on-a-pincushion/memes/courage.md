# Courage

A central moral virtue in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, repeatedly shown as the quality that enables characters to face magical danger, confront their own failings, and earn their happy endings.

## Overview

Courage in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales is not the warrior's bravery of epic poetry but the quieter courage of ordinary people facing extraordinary circumstances. The phrase "summoned all her courage" recurs as a narrative beat (001-136, 001-138, 002-049), marking moments when characters must overcome fear to act. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats courage as a muscle that must be exercised — summoned, not possessed — making it a democratic virtue available to anyone willing to try.

## In the Narrative

### Blanchelys at the Yew-Tree

The most vivid depiction of courage comes when [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] faces [[the-yew-tree|the great yew-tree]] at midnight to light her [[magic-candles|magic candle]]. "She summoned her courage, and going up to the tree crept under the branches" (001-136). The darkness, the unknown, and the magnitude of what she seeks ([[love|Love]] itself) all demand courage. This is courage as vulnerability — [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] is not armed or protected, only determined.

> She took out the candle, and hid it in her bosom. Then she crept from the room, down the passage, and into the little garden. In the middle of the garden stood a great dark yew-tree. She summoned her courage, and going up to the tree crept under the branches.
>
> (001-136)

### Siegfrid Against the Giant

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] "summoned all his courage" (002-049) when confronting the giant-shoemaker who has captured children. His courage is practical and protective — he acts not for glory but to save [[handa|Handa]] and the captive children. This is courage as responsibility: the hero steps up because no one else will.

### Lamorna's Moral Courage

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s courage is retrospective and internal — the courage to admit fault. "If it had not been for my [[pride|vanity]], I need not have been hurt at all" (001-094). This is courage as self-knowledge, perhaps the hardest kind to summon.

## Literary Context

Victorian [[fairy]] tales frequently emphasized moral virtues — courage, kindness, [[truth|honesty]] — as the true source of heroic power. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment is distinctive in showing courage as effortful ("summoned") rather than innate, and in distributing it across female characters ([[blanchelys|Blanchelys]], [[lamorna|Lamorna]]) as much as male ones ([[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]).
