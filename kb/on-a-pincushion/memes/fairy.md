# Fairy

Supernatural beings who appear across multiple tales in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* collection, manifesting as [[fairy-godmother|fairy godmothers]], elemental spirits, and powerful enchantresses.

## Overview

[[fairies|Fairies]] in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales serve diverse narrative functions: as benefactors granting [[hope|wish]]es and protection, as antagonists wielding malevolent magic, and as elemental personifications of natural forces. Unlike the diminutive flower [[fairies]] of Victorian convention, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairies]] range from a mighty ruler of the Sun (001-220) to the domestic fairy godmother [[taboret|Taboret]] who oversees a princess's upbringing (004-005). The tales feature both traditional fairy-tale [[fairies]] operating within a framework of "fairy law" (004-039) and elemental [[fairies]] — fire, [[the-brook|water]], and wind — who guide the hero through supernatural trials (005-008).

## In the Narrative

### Types and Appearances

The fairy folk appear in several distinct forms across the collection:

**[[fairy-godmother|Fairy Godmother]]s.** [[taboret|Taboret]] is the most fully realized fairy character, [[servant|serving]] as fairy godmother to [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] in *[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]*. She watches over [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] invisibly (004-011), intervenes at court in a speaking role (004-082), and ultimately orchestrates the resolution of the tale (004-085). She operates within a fairy community with its own customs — she "flew back to her fairy home" to deliberate (004-016), and the narrative notes that "there's not one fairy in twenty who could tell it from a real one" regarding a magical disguise (004-039).

**Elemental [[fairies|Fairies]].** In *[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]*, [[jack|Jack]] encounters three elemental fairy beings who guide his journey: the fire-fairy who first reveals herself to him (005-008), the water-fairy summoned to bring [[the-brook|water]] across the [[fire-element|flames]] (005-125), and the wind-fairy who carries [[jack|Jack]] through the air and blows obstacles from his path (005-146, 005-153). These [[fairies]] are closely tied to their respective elements and speak in voices matching their nature — the wind-fairy speaks "in an odd gusty voice" (005-153).

**Ruling and Wicked [[fairies|Fairies]].** A "mighty fairy" rules the Sun and "every morning tells his [[servant|servants]] to fetch it and drive it away" (001-220). In *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]*, a "wicked fairy" is credited with [[transformation|transform]]ing a queen into a tigress (003-076), demonstrating [[darkness|the dark]]er aspect of fairy power in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world.


> The Sun is ruled by a mighty fairy, who every morning tells his tiny servants, the beams, where they are to shine, and every evening counts them on their return, to see he has the right number. No one knows why the Sun and Moon quarrelled. Once they were very good friends.
>
> (001-220)

### Narrative Function

[[fairies|Fairies]] serve as agents of both narrative progression and moral order. The benevolent [[fairies]] — [[taboret|Taboret]], the elemental guides — enable the protagonists' journeys by providing magical assistance, protection, and guidance. The [[hope|wish]]-granting fairy of Chapter 001 offers [[magic-candles|magic candle]]s that fulfill a heart's desire (001-127). In contrast, the wicked fairy's curse in *[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]* sets the central conflict in motion. This dual nature — fairy as helper and fairy as obstacle — reflects the broader fairy-tale tradition while allowing [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] to explore themes of earned versus unearned magical intervention.

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairies]] occupy a middle ground between the diminutive, ornamental [[fairies]] of Victorian children's literature and the more powerful, morally ambiguous [[fairies]] of folk tradition. [[taboret|Taboret]], as an [[age|elder]]ly, pragmatic fairy godmother who "frowned to herself" at the sight of a princess without toys (004-012), subverts the stereotype of the sweetly smiling fairy. The elemental [[fairies]] of *[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]* — fire, [[the-brook|water]], and wind — evoke the nature-spirit traditions that predate Victorian fairy conventions. [[jack|Jack]]'s reflection on the wind-fairy's selfless joy — "if this poor silly little wind-fairy can be so glad when he has done a kindness" (005-177) — draws an explicit moral from the fairy's behavior, integrating the supernatural into the tale's ethical framework.
