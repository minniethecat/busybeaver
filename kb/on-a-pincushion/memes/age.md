# Age

Age in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* operates on multiple scales — from the casual "I do not mean to marry for ages" (001-036) to the cosmic "two thousand years" that the zirbal-tree pod requires to ripen. It marks the distance between youth and wisdom, the slow accumulation of change, and the long arc of [[fairy]]-tale time.

## Overview

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] uses age to establish [[power|authority]] and to measure [[transformation|transform]]ation. The old [[water-people|water elf]] is defined by his elder status — his wisdom is inseparable from his age. The reflection stolen from [[lamorna|Lamorna]] is magical precisely because it will age with her: "if even a wrinkle comes on [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s face it will at once appear on the face of her [[reflection|image]] here, and should her hair become grey its hair would be grey also" (001-054). Age is the visible record of experience, and it cannot be escaped even by magical means.

## Age as Authority

### Elders as Guides

The old elf's age is central to his role. He is the "[[wisdom|wise]] old elf" — age and wisdom paired — and his [[power|authority]] within the [[the-brook|water]]-people community derives from his years. [[taboret|Taboret]] is the "old [[fairy]]" whose age signals accumulated knowledge and power. In [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral universe, age is not a diminishment but a qualification. The elders guide, the young learn.

### The Tortoise-Queen: Inverted Authority

The tortoise-queen subverts this pattern. She is an elder figure — [[the-tortoise-queen|the tortoise-king's mother]] — but her age brings not wisdom but [[cruelty|spite]]. She uses her power to curse [[trevina|Trevina]] rather than guide her. She is the collection's cautionary example: age without goodness is merely accumulated [[cruelty|malice]]. Her "mother's care" (003-092) is surveillance, not nurture.

## Age and Transformation

### The Reflection That Ages

The [[reflection|stolen reflection]]'s defining property is that it ages in parallel with [[lamorna|Lamorna]]. It is not a frozen [[reflection|image]] of youthful beauty but a [[life|living]] mirror that will record every change. This detail gives the theft moral weight: what the elves take is not merely a pretty picture but [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s future self, the record of a life she has not yet lived. The reflection is a prophecy in reverse — it shows what will be, not what was.

> "Then it will change too," said the old elf; "if even a wrinkle comes on Lamorna's face it will at once appear on the face of her image here, and should her hair become grey its hair would be grey also."
>
> (001-054)

### Growing Up

Child-to-adult [[transformation|transform]]ation runs throughout the collection. [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] is raised from infancy by the fisherman's family while her mechanical double rules at court (004-058). [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] moves from boy to hero through ordeal (002-027). [[trevina|Trevina]]'s years as a tigress are years of [[patience|waiting]] — age as [[patience|endurance]] rather than growth. Each tale traces a different arc of maturation, and [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s interest is in what is gained and lost at each stage.

### The Hair That Must Be Cut

The rods of the Hair Tree are the only cure for [[trevina|Trevina]]'s curse, but they must be obtained by a man who can beat her until she bleeds. The violence is disturbing, but the underlying logic is about age: only the mature — those who have grown strong enough to wield the rods — can undo [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's curse. Age brings the capacity for both harm and healing.

## Fairy-Tale Time

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales invoke vast spans of time characteristic of the [[fairy]]-tale genre. The zirbal-tree pod ripens only once every two thousand years; [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's curse operates across generations; the [[the-water-people|water people]] have lived beneath the brook "for as many years as there are stones in the brook." These long arcs place individual human lives in a larger perspective, suggesting that age — both personal and cosmic — is the medium through which change becomes visible.
