# The Yew Tree

A great ancient yew-tree in the garden of [[the-cottage|Blanchelys's cottage]], "whose branches almost touched the ground" (001-136) — [[darkness|the dark]], sacred site where she lights her [[magic-candles|magic candle]] and summons [[love|Love]].

## Overview

The yew-tree is the most potent single location in "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]." Ancient and dark, "it looked like a great black giant in the night" (001-136), and it is beneath its branches that [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] performs the tale's central magical act. Yew trees have ancient associations with death, rebirth, and sacred ground in European folklore, making this an apt setting for a [[transformation|transform]]ative encounter with [[love|Love]] himself.

## Geography and Description

The yew stands in the middle of the cottage garden. Its branches droop to the ground, creating an enclosed space beneath — a natural chamber. At midnight, when [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] creeps out to light her candle, the tree is dark and imposing. It is under this tree that [[love|Love]] appears: a boy with wings, a wreath of pink roses on his hair, carrying a branch of roses (001-136).

## Narrative Significance

The yew functions as a threshold between the ordinary garden and the magical encounter. Its ancient darkness contrasts with the light of the [[magic-candles|magic candle]], making this the tale's most vivid magical scene.

> So she dressed herself quickly, but she left her hair hanging down her back, and trod noiselessly to the cupboard, and softly opened the door. She took out the candle, and hid it in her bosom. Then she crept from the room, down the passage, and into the little garden. In the middle of the garden stood a great yew-tree, whose branches almost touched the ground. It looked like a great black giant in the night, and Blanchelys trembled as she looked at it; but she summoned her courage, and went close up to the tree, and pushed aside the branches, and crept in under them.
>
> (001-136)
