# Mary De Morgan: Author of *On a Pincushion*

Mary De Morgan (1850–1907) was a Victorian writer of literary [[fairy]] tales whose collection *On a Pincushion* (1877) stands as a distinctive contribution to the golden age of British [[fairy]]-tale writing. Though less widely read today than contemporaries such as George MacDonald or Oscar Wilde, her work displays a sharp satirical intelligence, a sophisticated use of embedded narrative, and a quiet feminism that rewards renewed attention.

## Life and Context

Mary De Morgan was born into an intellectually distinguished family — her father was the mathematician Augustus De Morgan and her mother Sophia Elizabeth (Frend) De Morgan was a spiritualist writer. Her brother William De Morgan became well-known as a ceramicist and novelist associated with the Arts and Crafts movement. This environment of mathematics, mysticism, and craftsmanship shaped her imaginative world.

She was part of the circle around William Morris and the Pre-Raphaelites, and her [[fairy]] tales share with that movement an interest in medieval romance, craft values, and the [[pride|dignity]] of the handmade. The shoemaker in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" — whose honest craftsmanship is contrasted with the cheap, shoddy goods of the magical shoe-seller — may be read as an Arts and Crafts parable in miniature.

## Narrative Technique

### The Framing Device

*On a Pincushion* uses a nested narrative structure: three pins on a pincushion tell stories to pass the time. This frame — a Brooch, a Shawl-pin, and a common Pin — gives the collection a distinctive object's-eye perspective. The pins have personalities, memories, and social hierarchies of their own. [[the-brooch|The Brooch]] claims to have witnessed the events of "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" before it was cut and polished, blurring the line between teller and tale.

This miniaturization of the storyteller convention (contrast Chaucer's pilgrims or Boccaccio's aristocrats) is characteristic of De Morgan's wit: the grand tradition of the frame narrative is domesticated to a sewing table, and the result is simultaneously [[charm]]ing and deflationary.

### Embedded Tales

Of the collection's five major narratives, three are embedded — told within other tales — rather than delivered directly by the pins. "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" is told by [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]. "The Story of [[trevina|Trevina]]" is told by a tigress within "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]." This Chinese-box structure creates layered perspectives: a story told by a creature who was once a woman, framed by a tale told by a character in a story told by a pin. De Morgan uses this complexity not as ornament but as thematic reinforcement — identity, in her world, is always layered and recoverable.

### Satire and Social Commentary

The collection's sharpest tale, "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," satirizes courtly life by having courtiers fail to notice the substitution of a mechanical doll for their princess. The satire operates on multiple levels: it critiques aristocratic child-rearing, the emptiness of court ritual, and — most daringly — the social construction of royalty itself. If a doll can rule as well as a princess, what is the divine right of kings worth?

## Place in Victorian Fairy-Tale Tradition

The Victorian period saw an extraordinary flourishing of literary [[fairy]] tales, driven by the Romantic rediscovery of folklore, the rise of children's literature as a commercial category, and the possibilities of the genre for social comment. De Morgan's work sits at the intersection of several traditions:

- **The Moral Tale**: Like George MacDonald's *The Princess and the Goblin* (1872), De Morgan's tales carry clear moral freight — [[pride|vanity]], greed, and selfishness are punished; courage, kindness, and [[truth|honesty]] are rewarded. But her moral framework is more pragmatic than MacDonald's Christian allegory.

- **The Satirical Fantasy**: "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" prefigures the social satire of Oscar Wilde's [[fairy]] tales (*The Happy Prince*, 1888), though De Morgan's tone is lighter and her targets more domestic.

- **The Arts and Crafts Parable**: As noted, the celebration of honest work in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" aligns with the values of the Morris circle.

## Legacy

De Morgan published two further collections — *The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde* (1880) and *The Windfairies* (1900) — but none achieved the cohesive [[charm]] of *On a Pincushion*. Her work was largely forgotten in the twentieth century, overshadowed by the male canonical figures of Victorian fantasy. Recent scholarship, particularly feminist reconsiderations of Victorian children's literature, has begun to reassess her contribution. The miniature society of [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]], with its talking objects and embedded stories, reads today as a sophisticated experiment in narrative voice and scale.
