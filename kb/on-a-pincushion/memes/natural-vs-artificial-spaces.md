# Natural and Artificial Spaces: De Morgan's Moral Geography

Across the tales of *On a Pincushion*, [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]] constructs a consistent moral geography. Natural settings — brooks, forests, the sea — are sites of [[transformation|transform]]ation, healing, and truth. Constructed spaces — castles, workshops, courts — are sites of deception, captivity, and false appearances. This pattern is not merely decorative; it is the spatial expression of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral [[dream|vision]], in which nature is aligned with authenticity and artifice with corruption.

## Natural Settings: Mirrors and Thresholds

The brook in "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" is the collection's most richly symbolic natural space. It functions as both mirror and portal: [[lamorna|Lamorna]] gazes into it to see her reflection (001-008), and the [[the-water-people|water people]] use it as the medium for their therapeutic intervention (001-022). The brook does not lie — it shows [[lamorna|Lamorna]] exactly what she is — but it also provides the means for her cure. It is a threshold between the human world and the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s miniature society, a liminal space where [[transformation|transform]]ation becomes possible.

The forest in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" serves a similar function. It is where [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] meets the talking [[the-hare|Hare]] who reveals the truth about the giant's workshop (002-028). The forest is wild but honest; its creatures speak truth, even when that truth is uncomfortable. The sea in "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" is [[erick|Erick]]'s domain — the fisherman's workplace and the site of his faithful, unrequited [[love|devotion]] (001-014).

What unites these [[natural-vs-artificial|natural settings]] is their function as spaces of *[[truth|revelation]]*. They do not conceal; they reveal. The brook reveals [[lamorna|Lamorna]] to herself. The forest reveals the giant's secret. The sea reveals [[erick|Erick]]'s constancy.

> A pretty young girl was standing by a brook, bending over it talking to her own reflection.
>
> (001-008)

## Constructed Spaces: Disguise and Captivity

In deliberate contrast, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[natural-vs-artificial|constructed spaces]] are sites of concealment and entrapment. The giant's workshop is the most explicit example: "the giant has disguised himself as a shoemaker, that he may steal little boys and girls" (002-028). The workshop looks like an honest place of trade but is actually a kidnapping apparatus. Children are immobilized by [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s and held in an underground cavern — the constructed space is literally a prison.

The castle and court in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" function similarly. The court is a space of pure performance, where appearances are everything and substance nothing. The substitution of the toy princess for the real one (004-058) works precisely because the court is a constructed space where no one looks beneath the surface. The [[spell]] shop in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" (003-031) is another constructed space of ambiguous morality — a place of commerce where magical bargains are struck, but always at a cost.

> "She is in a cavern underground," said the Hare. "She and all the other little girls are sitting there in a row, and they cannot move or speak, because on their feet are magic shoes that the old shoemaker made for them, which hold them as still as marble."
>
> (002-028)

## The Threshold Pattern

Several tales hinge on movement between natural and [[natural-vs-artificial|constructed spaces]]:

| Tale | Natural → Constructed | Consequence |
|------|----------------------|-------------|
| [[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]] | Forest → Workshop | Discovery of captivity, then liberation by fire (002-045) |
| [[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]] | Brook → Village | [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] exposed, then cured |
| [[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]] | (absent) → Court | Substitution goes undetected in the artificial space |

The pattern is clearest in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]": [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] learns the truth in the forest (natural), enters the workshop to liberate the captives (constructed), and destroys it with fire — an element that is both natural and [[transformation|transform]]ative.

## Conclusion

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral geography maps cleanly onto the Victorian tension between nature and industrialization. Natural spaces are aligned with [[truth|honesty]], growth, and moral clarity; [[natural-vs-artificial|constructed spaces]] with deception, exploitation, and moral confusion. This is not nostalgia for a pre-industrial past so much as a critique of spaces where appearances are manufactured — workshops that are really traps, courts that are really theaters. The brook tells the truth; the workshop lies. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairy]] tales are, among other things, an argument for the moral superiority of the un-built world.
