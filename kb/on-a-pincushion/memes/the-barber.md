# The Barber

The court barber in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is a figure of institutional menace — the state's instrument of punishment, rendered in the precise, chilling detail of a sharpened razor.

## Overview

The barber appears in the climactic scaffold scene of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," where the Queen has decreed that all women must have their heads shaved as punishment for the duchess's crime. "On it the court barber was to stand, whilst all the women, from the highest to the lowest, came before him in turn and had their heads shaved quite close" (003-167). The scene is a tableau of state-sanctioned [[shame|humiliation]], with the King and Queen watching from state seats. At its dramatic peak, the duchess takes her seat in "the [[fear|dread]]ful chair" and "the barber sharpened his razor" (003-174) — a small, terrible domestic detail that makes the threat viscerally real. When [[rupert|the sailor]] arrives with proof of the truth, "the barber flung away his razor" (003-176), and the Queen screams.

> "The barber sharpened his razor, and was just going to begin, whilst all held their breath from excitement." (003-174)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The barber is not a villain — he is an instrument. He does not judge, sentence, or accuse; he simply sharpens his razor and waits for the command. This makes him, in some ways, more chilling than the actively malicious characters. He represents the machinery of punishment, the ordinary man who performs extraordinary violence because it is his job. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s choice to make the executioner a *barber* — a figure associated with grooming, tidiness, and everyday civility — underscores the tale's critique of institutional cruelty dressed as justice.

### Key Actions

- Stands on the immense scaffold in the marketplace, prepared to shave all the women of the kingdom (003-167)
- Sharpens his razor as the duchess awaits her punishment, building unbearable tension (003-174)
- Flings away his razor upon hearing [[rupert|the sailor]]'s intervention (003-176)

## Character Interpretation

The barber embodies [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s critique of performative justice. The public scaffold, the state seats, the ceremonial shaving — it is punishment as theater, with the barber as its leading actor. His silent professionalism — sharpening, preparing, [[patience|waiting]] — makes the scene more horrifying than any ranting villain could. And his immediate compliance with the call to stop ("the barber flung away his razor") reveals the obedience that underlies institutional violence: he would have done it, and he would have stopped, with equal readiness. The moral weight rests not on the instrument but on the hands that wield it.
