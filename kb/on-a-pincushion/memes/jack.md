# Jack

The seven-year-old protagonist of "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," the final and most cosmologically ambitious tale in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. Jack is the collection's most unlikely hero: a crippled child, motherless by circumstance, who begins the story "so small and pale that he looked little more than five" (005-001) and ends it as the emissary between elemental kingdoms. His journey through fire and ice [[transformation|transform]]s the [[fairy]]-tale everyman into an agent of cosmic reconciliation.

## Origins and Introduction

Jack lives in a "dull old street in London" on the third floor of a small house, spending his days alone by the fireplace while his widowed mother teaches music and plays at children's parties (005-001). He has "no brothers or sisters" and is "nearly always alone" — the classic [[fairy]]-tale orphan in urban form. Unlike [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s other protagonists, who live in pastoral villages or magical countries, Jack dwells in the recognizable Victorian world of tenements and street lamps.

> Little Jack sat alone by the fire, watching it sadly. He was seven years old, but so small and pale that he looked little more than five, for he was a cripple. He had no brothers or sisters, and he was nearly always alone, for his mother, who was a widow, went out all day to teach music, and often in the evening also to play dance-music at children's parties.
>
> — 005-001

His sole preparation for the magical encounters to come is an oral tradition: his grandmother's "long, long stories of giants and [[fairy|fairies]]" (005-002) — narrative knowledge that proves to be his only weapon against elemental danger. The Christmas Eve setting, with his mother gone to a party with a Christmas-tree "with presents on it for all the little boys and girls," establishes Jack as the child excluded from joy, the one who watches other children receive while he receives nothing.

## Role and Significance

Jack serves as an **audience surrogate** — the ordinary human who enters the [[fairy]] tale without innate magical gifts, without lineage, without even physical wholeness. His grandmother's stories are his only preparation, making him a testament to the power of narrative knowledge: the child who heard tales becomes the child who *lives* one.

His role expands across the story from passive observer to active agent:

| Phase | Role | Key Actions |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Observer** | The lonely child | Meets the [[fairy|fire-fairy]]; witnesses [[fire-element|Fire]]land |
| **Advocate** | The [[kindness|compassion]]ate intruder | Confronts the Princess's ladies; speaks truth to power |
| **Messenger** | The bridge between realms | Carries the Princess's ring; summons [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] |
| **Emissary** | The ice-crosser | Travels to the ice realm; relays the Princess's message |
| **Hero** | The liberator | Enables the union of fire and [[the-brook|water]] through courage |

Unlike [[siegfrid]], whose heroism is physical ([[the-burning-of-the-boots|destroying the giant's boots]], freeing captives), or [[rupert]], whose quest is personal (recovering his lost hair), Jack's heroism is **relational**: he succeeds not by defeating an enemy but by connecting separated lovers across elemental boundaries.

## Key Events

### The Fire-Fairy's Visit

On Christmas Eve, Jack sees "a little figure, the strangest he had ever beheld, balancing itself skilfully on the top of a piece of [[fire-element|burning]] coal" — a [[fairy|fire-fairy]] "not more than three inches high, dressed from head to foot in orange-scarlet" (005-001). Jack's response — not fear but breathless curiosity — sets the tale in motion. He asks "Who are you?" and learns the [[fairy]]'s origin: the elemental kingdom of [[fire-element|Fire]]land.

### The Journey to Fireland

The [[fairy|fire-fairy]] equips Jack with enchanted garments and a "thin shining glass mask" (005-046) that protects him from [[fire-element|heat]]. Jack scrambles "over the fender" and into the fire itself — a vertiginous passage through a "long dark tunnel" to a subterranean realm where hills are "of every shade of red and orange" and "the sky was one mass of [[fire-element|flame]]" (005-069).

> On looking round, he thought he was in a new world. He stood in the middle of rich, red-glowing hills, from which sprouted jets of flame, like trees. Here and there was a black mountain, which smoked and hissed most alarmingly.
>
> — 005-069

### Meeting Princess Pyra

In [[fire-element|Fire]]land's palace garden — where "what he had at first supposed to be precious stones was nothing but different-coloured fire" (005-069) — Jack meets [[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]], daughter of the fire King. She is "the most beautiful lady he had ever seen" but "so very sad" (005-073). The [[fairy|fire-fairy]] explains her tragedy: she fell in love with [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] of the [[the-brook|water]] kingdom while at school in a [[fire-element|burning]] mountain, but their elemental natures make union impossible — "lest he should be dried up, or she should be put out" (005-087).

Jack's defining moment comes when, against the protests of the [[fire-fairy|fireman]], he steps forward and speaks:

> "I'm a little boy, and my name is Jack."
>
> — 005-089

This plain self-introduction — no title, no magic, no claim — is the collection's most radical heroic gesture. Jack's power is his ordinariness, his refusal to be cowed by elemental majesty.

### The Princess's Ring

On New Year's Eve, the Princess appears in Jack's [[death|dying]] fire, "paler than before, and looked quite transparent. Jack could see the coals plainly through her" (005-024). She gives him a ring and a mission: summon [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]]. Following her instructions, Jack opens the window to the rain and wind, and the Princess sings a summoning [[spells-and-charms|spell]]. The [[the-brook|water]]-[[fairy]] and [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] appear — a being of "such beauty that Jack could scarcely look at him" (005-129).

### The Journey to the Ice Realm

The Princess charges Jack with a final, most dangerous task: journey to the realm of the [[the-ice-people|ice people]], where "many people have caught and [[death|frozen to death]]" (005-195), to deliver her message to the ice King. [[prince-fluvius|Prince Fluvius]] touches Jack's head, and "Jack began to feel himself growing smaller and lighter" (005-152) — the mortal child [[transformation|transform]]ed for elemental travel. Carried by a [[fairy|wind-fairy]] across the sea, past a mermaid's song, Jack arrives in the frozen north where "blocks of ice were piled into huge fantastic towers" (005-190).

## Relationships

- **[[fire-element|Fire-Fairy]]** — Jack's guide and reluctant protector, whose initial gruffness gives way to genuine care. He throws Jack "with all his force" back to safety when the King's smoke-cloud threatens (005-107).
- **[[princess-pyra|Princess Pyra]]** — The fire Princess whose cause Jack adopts. She calls him "[[little-jack|Little Jack]]" with genuine [[love|affection]] and trusts him with her fate.
- **[[the-ice-people|Prince Fluvius]]** — The [[the-brook|water]] Prince who blesses Jack's journey and shrinks him for elemental travel. His touch is [[transformation|transform]]ative.
- **The [[wind-fairy|Wind-Fairy]]** — Jack's carrier across the sea, gruff but competent, who shields him from the dangers of ice and ocean.
- **[[the-ice-people|The Ice People]]** — The frozen beings Jack must negotiate with, representing the third elemental kingdom in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s cosmology.

## Literary Significance

Jack is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most subversive protagonist. In a collection where heroes are shoemakers' sons ([[siegfrid]]), questing youths ([[rupert]]), and kings in disguise ([[taboret]]), Jack alone is defined by his **limitations** — crippled, impoverished, isolated. Yet it is precisely these limitations that make him the ideal emissary: he has nothing to protect, no status to defend, no power to threaten. He can speak truth to a Princess because his ordinariness is invulnerable to court protocol.

The tale's elemental cosmology — fire, [[the-brook|water]], ice, wind — prefigures the systematic world-building of later fantasy literature. Jack's passage through all four elemental realms (fire in the opening, [[the-brook|water]] and wind at the window, ice in the climax) makes "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" the collection's most structurally ambitious narrative, and Jack its most versatile traveler.
