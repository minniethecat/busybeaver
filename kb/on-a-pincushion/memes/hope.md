# Hope

Hope in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is a quiet, persistent thread — never loud, never triumphant, but essential for survival in a world where happy endings are far from guaranteed.

## Overview

The word "hope" appears sparingly in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales — only six times across the collection — and this scarcity is itself meaningful. Hope is not a dominant emotion in these stories; it flickers at the edges, voiced by characters facing uncertainty. "I hope, dear [[zaire|cousin Zaire]], that you will be very happy" (001-153) is spoken at a wedding, a conventional wish that carries the weight of the entire tale's themes. "I hope [[handa|Handa]] will know me" (002-044) is the anxious thought of a rescuer approaching the climax. "There is no hope for us, and we must perish" (005-230) is [[darkness|the dark]]est moment in the collection — the explicit statement that hope has died.

> "I hope Handa will know me," he thought. (002-044)

## In the Narrative

### Hope as Social Grace

In "On a Pincushion," hope appears at a wedding — "I hope, dear [[zaire|cousin Zaire]], that you will be very happy" (001-153). This conventional expression is the most socially embedded use of the word, and [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] may be subtly questioning its sincerity. [[lamorna|Lamorna]], the speaker, has just learned that her beauty is gone; her expression of hope for another's happiness carries the weight of her own loss.

### Hope in Action

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s hope that [[handa|Handa]] will recognize him (002-044) is hope's most functional form: not passive wishing but active faith that past love persists. He is running toward danger on [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s, betting his life on the hope that recognition and love survive [[transformation|transform]]ation. This is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s model of hope — not optimism but *commitment to action despite uncertainty*.

### The Death of Hope

The darkest passage in the collection belongs to "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]": "He means that there is no hope for us, and that we must perish" (005-230). Spoken as the Ice People close in, this is hope explicitly negated. But [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s narrative logic is that hope's negation is always premature: [[jack|Jack]] survives, the Ice People are defeated, and the story ends not in perishing but in rescue. The passage functions almost as a test — can the reader continue to hope even when the characters have given up?

## Literary Context

Victorian [[fairy]] tales often treated hope as a given — the default emotional register of "once upon a time." [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s more restrained treatment, where hope is scarce and must be earned, reflects [[darkness|the dark]]er current in late-Victorian children's literature (compare Christina Rossetti's *Goblin Market*, where hope is similarly hard-won). The six instances of "hope" are placed with care, each marking a moment of genuine emotional risk.
