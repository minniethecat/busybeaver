# The Miller

Handa's father in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," the village miller whose initial injustice toward [[the-shoemaker|the old shoemaker]] becomes the catalyst for the tale's moral education.

## Overview

The Miller is the tale's [[power|authority]] figure — a man of standing in the village whose word carries weight. When the people abandon [[the-shoemaker|the old shoemaker]] for a new one, the Miller is complicit in the injustice. But unlike a [[fairy]]-tale villain, he possesses the capacity for self-correction. After [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s magical rescue of [[handa|Handa]], the Miller is the first to speak: "Let us all be thankful" (002-074), and later acknowledges, "we behaved very wickedly and ungratefully" (002-079). His arc is from complicity to [[truth|confession]] — a moral trajectory rare in [[fairy]]-tale [[power|authority]] figures.

> “You are talking of what you don’t understand, Handa,” said her father, the miller, angrily. “Of course it is right for us to buy everything as cheaply as we can. Be quiet, child!” And everyone was angry with Handa for speaking; but she thought just the same, and she cried when she saw Ralph, or his wife, or Siegfrid.
>
> (002-018)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

The Miller represents the moral weight of community. When he speaks, the village listens. His initial anger at [[handa|Handa]]'s defense of the shoemaker — "Of course it is right that the new shoemaker should have the best seat" (002-018) — shows how easily [[power|authority]] aligns with convenience over justice. His later repentance demonstrates that [[power|authority]], properly exercised, can lead a community toward repair rather than destruction.

### Key Actions

- Scolds [[handa|Handa]] for defending [[the-shoemaker|the old shoemaker]], enforcing the village's unjust consensus (002-018)
- Grows "so weary with want of sleep" from the magical boots' torment (002-055), experiencing poetic justice
- Examines the boots and witnesses their supernatural behavior (002-062–002-067)
- Hears [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s account and is the first to call for [[gratitude|thankfulness]] (002-074)
- Publicly admits the village's wrongdoing: "we behaved very wickedly" (002-079)

### Relationships

- [[handa]] — his daughter, whose moral clarity exceeds his own
- [[siegfrid]] — the boy who rescues [[handa|Handa]] and shames the village into repentance
- [[the-shoemaker]] — the man he and the village wronged

## Character Interpretation

The Miller is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most nuanced [[power|authority]] figure. Unlike the [[fairy]]-tale convention of the wicked [[age|elder]] who must be deposed, the Miller is redeemable. His arc — from complicity to [[truth|confession]] — suggests that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] believes even flawed authorities can be restored to moral legitimacy through honest acknowledgment of wrong. This is a remarkably democratic [[dream|vision]] for a Victorian [[fairy]] tale.
