# Vanity Across Tales: Cure vs. Exposure

[[vanity|Vanity]] is the most prominent moral theme in *On a Pincushion*, appearing as the central concern of two embedded tales. Yet [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats it very differently in each: in "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," [[pride|vanity]] is a curable illness healed through benevolent magical intervention; in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," [[pride|vanity]] (of the court, not the princess) is exposed through substitution and social reversal. This article compares the two treatments and argues that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] uses [[pride|vanity]] as a lens for two distinct moral projects — one therapeutic, one satirical.

## Vanity as Curable Illness: Vain Lamorna

[[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]] is presented as an affliction rather than a sin. She is "the daughter of a farmer" whose beauty is real and whose self-admiration is framed as excessive but not malicious (001-010). The [[the-water-people|water people]], upon hearing her talk to her reflection, "shook their heads and sighed" (001-012) — they are concerned, not outraged. Crucially, when one elf proposes punishment, another immediately redirects: "what good will it do to [[erick|Erick]] for us to punish her? Rather let us think how we can cure her of her [[pride|vanity]]" (001-019).

The cure itself is ingenious and gentle: steal her reflection from the [[the-brook|water]] so she can no longer see herself (001-022). The old elf's plan is greeted with cheers — "Ah, what a fine thing it is to have a mind like that!" (001-023) — and the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s miniature society celebrates intellectual brilliance rather than moral condemnation. The theft of the reflection forces [[lamorna|Lamorna]] to look outward; without her [[reflection|image]] to admire, she finally sees [[erick|Erick]]'s [[love|devotion]] and her own fault. The tale ends with restoration and marriage, not punishment.

> "Nay," said a third; "what good will it do to Erick for us to punish her? Rather let us think how we can cure her of her vanity and win him her love."
>
> (001-019)

## Vanity as Social Corruption: A Toy Princess

In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," [[pride|vanity]] operates at the level of the court rather than the individual. The courtiers are so obsessed with royal appearances that they accept a mechanical doll as their princess without question. When [[taboret|Taboret]] substitutes the toy princess for the real [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]], "they did not think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her place" (004-058). The satire is sharp: the court's [[pride|vanity]] is so complete that they cannot distinguish a [[life|living]] person from an automaton.

Unlike [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s [[pride|vanity]], which is cured, the court's [[pride|vanity]] is simply exposed. There is no therapeutic intervention — only the reveal that their adoration was directed at a doll. The moral is bleaker: institutional [[pride|vanity]] cannot be cured, only unmasked.

> "Ah, a terrible thing is vanity!" said the Shawl-pin solemnly. "I have suffered from it. I myself make a point of pricking anyone who I think is getting too vain."
>
> (001-119)

## Structural Parallels and Divergences

| Dimension | [[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]] | [[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]] |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Subject | Individual ([[lamorna|Lamorna]]) | Collective (the court) |
| Method | [[magic|Magic]]al cure (reflection theft) | Substitution (toy replacement) |
| Outcome | Restoration, marriage | Exposure, [[shame|humiliation]] |
| Tone | Gentle, benevolent | Satirical, sharp |
| Moral agents | Water people ([[wisdom|wise]], kind) | [[taboret|Taboret]] (trickster-figure) |

## Conclusion

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s two [[pride|vanity]] tales form a diptych. "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" offers the optimistic thesis that [[pride|vanity]] can be cured through love and clever intervention — the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s miniature democracy of kindness in action. "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" offers the pessimistic corollary: when [[pride|vanity]] is systemic, the only remedy is exposure through absurdity. Together they show [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] working both sides of the Victorian moral imagination: the belief in improvement and the recognition that some follies can only be laughed at.
