# The Opal

A magical gem in [[on-a-pincushion|"On a Pincushion"]], born from the mingled [[death|dying]] light of [[the-sunbeam|the Sunbeam]] and [[the-moonbeam|the Moonbeam]]. The story of its origin is told by [[the-pin|the Pin]] as [[the-story-of-the-opal|the third embedded tale]] in [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] storytelling session, learned from an Opal Ring who knew how it came by its wonderful colours (001-220).

## Overview

The Opal represents one of [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s most inventive origin myths — a [[transformation]] narrative in which beauty arises from sacrifice. Unlike the collection's other magical gems ([[the-brooch|the Brooch]], [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]]), the Opal is not a character but a [[transformation|transform]]ed substance: a once-ugly stone that became the most beautiful object in the forest when [[the-sunbeam|the Sunbeam]] and [[the-moonbeam|the Moonbeam]] died together within it, suffusing the stone with their mingled colours and light (001-288).

## Origin

The Opal began as an ordinary, even ugly stone in the forest (001-288). [[the-sunbeam|The Sunbeam]] — a tiny Sun-[[fairy]] who had fallen in love with [[the-moonbeam|the Moonbeam]] — and his beloved had sheltered together within this stone. When they died, unable to live apart from the Sun and Moon, their combined silver-and-gold light shot up through the stone in "bright beautiful rays" of every colour of the rainbow (001-288):

> But through the Stone wherein the beams had sheltered, shot up bright beautiful rays of light, silver and gold. They coloured it all over with every colour of the rainbow, and when the Sun or Moon warmed it with their light it became quite brilliant. So that the Stone, from being the ugliest thing in the whole forest, became the most beautiful.
>
> (001-288)

Men later discovered the stone and named it the Opal (001-289).

## Properties

The Opal's defining characteristic is its changing, iridescent colours — explained in the tale as the mingled light of the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] (gold) and Moonbeam (silver) still alive within the gem (001-289, 001-291). When warmed by sunlight or [[the-moonbeam|moonlight]], the Opal becomes especially brilliant (001-288). [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]], who witnessed the tragedy of the lovers, knows the stone's true origin and continues to sing their story every night — which, the tale explains, is why the nightingale's song is so sad (001-289).

## In the Narrative

The Opal's story is told by [[the-pin|the Pin]] during the storytelling session on [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]], after [[the-brooch|the Brooch]] has told "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" and the "[[the-seeds-of-love|Seeds of Love]]" narrative has followed. [[the-pin|The Pin]] had learned the tale from an Opal Ring who explained how it came by its wonderful colours (001-220). The story provokes mixed reactions: [[the-brooch|the Brooch]] finds it "instructive as well as amusing" and declares "Now we know why the Opal has changing colours" (001-291), while [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] scornfully dismisses it, claiming to have known a great number of Opals who "never told me any such thing" (001-292). The disagreement nearly sparks a quarrel before a hand descends on [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] and removes both [[the-brooch|the Brooch]] and [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] (001-293).

## Literary Context

The Opal origin myth follows a pattern common in Victorian [[fairy]] literature — explaining a natural phenomenon (the opal's iridescence) through a story of love and loss. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s version is notable for treating the gem not as a [[life|living]] character (unlike the talking Brooch and Shawl-pin) but as a memorial: a physical record of the love between the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] and Moonbeam, preserved in mineral form. This distinguishes the Opal from the collection's other [[magic|magical objects]] and aligns it with the theme of [[transformation]] through sacrifice.
