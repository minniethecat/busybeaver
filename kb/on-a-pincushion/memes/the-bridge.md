# The Bridge

The bridge spanning [[the-river|the great river]] in "On a Pincushion" — a literal and symbolic crossing point where the humble village world meets the realm of royalty, love, and [[transformation|transform]]ation.

## Overview

The bridge is the collection's most freighted threshold. It connects the village (where [[lamorna|Lamorna]] grew up, where the cottage stands, where ordinary life happens) to the castle (where the King's son lives, where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and [[zaire|Zaire]] find their happy endings). "Over the river was a bridge, with gates" (001-124) — the gates suggest that crossing is not automatic; the bridge is guarded, permitting passage only to those with purpose. [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s grandmother's cottage stands "close beside the bridge" (001-124), positioning [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s childhood at the very threshold between worlds.

> "A little white cottage stood close beside the bridge." (001-124)

## In the Narrative

### The Bridge as Stage

The bridge is where the tale's romantic climax unfolds. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and [[zaire|Zaire]] "dressed themselves in their best" and positioned themselves so that the King's son would see them as he crossed (001-130). "Every day he came across the bridge on his snow-white horse" (001-132), and it is on the bridge — or in its immediate vicinity — that love is recognized. The bridge [[transformation|transform]]s from architecture to theater: a stage for the recognition scene that [[fairy]] tales require.

### The Bridge as Return Point

When [[lamorna|Lamorna]], [[transformation|transform]]ed by her ordeal, returns home, she comes "to the little house by the bridge where she had lived" (001-186). The bridge marks the point of return — crossing it means coming home, but as a changed person. The bridge that once led outward (to the castle, to romance) now leads inward (to [[forget|memory]], to the self one was before). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s bridge thus serves both departure and return.

## Literary Context

Bridges in [[fairy]] tales are classic threshold symbols — from the bridge in "The Three Billy Goats Gruff" (where a troll must be defeated) to the bridge to [[death|Death]]'s kingdom in Andersen. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s bridge is quieter: no monster guards it, no toll is demanded. But its narrative function is the same — crossing the bridge changes things.
