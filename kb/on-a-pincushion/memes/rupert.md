# Rupert

The questing sailor-hero of "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," the third tale in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*. Rupert's adventure begins with a trivial personal misfortune — the loss of his hair — and expands into a full [[fairy]]-tale quest through a magical country populated by talking animals, enchanted guardians, and a [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] whose curse and liberation form the tale's emotional core. More than any other [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] protagonist, Rupert exemplifies the [[fairy]]-tale pattern of the unpromising hero whose persistence [[transformation|transform]]s a [[pride|vanity]]-driven errand into genuine heroic action.

## Origins and Introduction

Rupert is introduced as "a poor sailor" (003-025) who sees a royal proclamation offering a reward to anyone who can bring seeds from the legendary Hair Tree. The King's daughter, Princess [[trevina|Trevina]], has lost her hair, and "nothing would ever make it grow" except seeds from this magical tree (003-023). Unlike [[siegfrid]], whose quest is compelled by injustice, or [[jack]], who is drawn into adventure by loneliness, Rupert is motivated by a sailor's pragmatic hope for reward — but his journey [[transformation|transform]]s him from [[fate|fortune]]-seeker to genuine hero.

> It happened that a poor sailor named Rupert saw one of the placards, and thought he would try to find the tree; for, of course, he would be well paid for his trouble if he succeeded, and he liked a roving life.
>
> — 003-025

Rupert "sailed and sailed to the north, and then to the south, and then to the east, and then to the west" (003-031) — the four-directional journey marking his passage out of ordinary geography and into the [[fairy]]-tale realm where compass points lead to magic rather than mapped coastlines.

## Role and Significance

Rupert represents the **[[fairy]]-tale quester** whose initial motivation is self-interested (reward, restored hair) but who proves himself through encounters that demand courage, cleverness, and [[kindness|compassion]]. His story expands the collection's geographic and imaginative range: where "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" is confined to a brook and cottage, and "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]" to a domestic garden, Rupert's quest spans oceans, jungles, and the hidden valley of the Hair Tree — a fully realized magical ecosystem.

## Key Events

### The Bargain with the Golden Bird

Landing on an island where "there were great woods, with beautiful flowers" (003-032), Rupert encounters a golden bird who demands the zirbal nuts he carries. Through patient bargaining — giving one nut per piece of information — Rupert learns that the Hair Tree grows in "the country of the red-haired people" (003-057) and that a magic rhyme opens the path:

> "The lion's mane, the silver grain, the salmon's leap are in the deep; the North Wind's roar, the shingly shore, the waters flow; This makes the Hair Tree grow — I know, I know."
>
> — 003-064

### The Tigress's Tale

In [[the-magic-country|the magical country]], Rupert finds a tigress "crouched in one corner of what seemed a large natural cave" (003-044) who guards the approach to the Hair Tree. She was once a woman — [[transformation|transform]]ed by the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]] for refusing to obey. Her story mirrors the collection's recurring theme of **[[transformation|transform]]ation as punishment**: like [[lamorna]]'s [[reflection|stolen reflection]] or the children in [[magic-shoes|enchanted shoes]], the tigress is a person trapped in a wrong form. Rupert's response — listening to her story rather than fleeing — marks his growth from [[fate|fortune]]-hunter to empathetic hero.

### Confronting the Snake-Princess

Beneath the Hair Tree, Rupert faces the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]], who demands all his seeds in exchange for passage. When Rupert refuses — "No, I can't do that; you must be content with what I give you" (003-159) — he demonstrates the moral discernment that separates him from mere adventurers. His refusal is not greed but principle: the seeds are needed to heal Princess [[trevina|Trevina]], and the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]]'s demand is unjust.

> Rupert at once gave the pieces of nut to the snake, who ate them greedily; and in a moment her skin fell from her, and she stood before him, a beautiful woman.
>
> — 003-126

The [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]]'s [[transformation|transform]]ation — freed by Rupert's gift of the zirbal nut — reveals the story's deeper pattern: the Hair Tree's magic is not merely restorative but **liberating**. Seeds that grow hair also break [[magic|enchantment]]s. Rupert's success is not in force but in the right gift given at the right moment.

### The Return and Restoration

Rupert returns with the Hair Tree seeds to find that six months have passed — "and it has seemed to me like six days" (003-138) — a classic [[fairy]]-tale time dilation. He restores Princess [[trevina|Trevina]]'s hair, wins her hand in marriage, and keeps one seed "to hang over his own door as a [[spells-and-charms|charm]]" (003-184), ensuring the magic's permanence.

## Relationships

- **The Golden Bird** — Rupert's first magical informant, whose greed for zirbal nuts makes him a reliable, if transactional, ally. Rupert's patience in bargaining — one nut, one fact — establishes him as a shrewd negotiator.
- **The Tigress** — A [[transformation|transform]]ed woman whose story awakens Rupert's [[kindness|compassion]]. Their exchange is the tale's moral turning point.
- **The Snake-Princess** — Guardian and prisoner of the Hair Tree, whose [[magic|enchantment]] Rupert breaks. Her liberation parallels the hair-restoration: both are freed from wrong forms.
- **Princess [[trevina|Trevina]]** — The hairless princess whose plight launches Rupert's quest and whose hand he wins. Their marriage is the collection's most conventionally [[fairy]]-tale ending.
- **[[hair-tree-seeds]]** — The quest object, whose magic enables both restoration ([[trevina|Trevina]]'s hair) and liberation (the [[the-snake-princess|snake-princess]]'s form).

## Literary Significance

Rupert's tale is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most thorough engagement with the **quest narrative** — the full [[fairy]]-tale structure of departure, trials, helpers, and return. Unlike the frame story's domestic embedded tales, "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is a journey outward into unmapped magical geography. Rupert's growth from reward-seeker to genuine hero models the [[fairy]] tale's core argument: that the journey [[transformation|transform]]s the traveler more than the destination.

[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]] itself — a tree whose seeds grow hair but whose roots entangle [[transformation|transform]]ed prisoners — is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most complex magical object, combining [[hair-tree-seeds|restoration]], [[spells-and-charms|enchantment]], and liberation in a single botanical symbol of nature's dual power to bind and free.
