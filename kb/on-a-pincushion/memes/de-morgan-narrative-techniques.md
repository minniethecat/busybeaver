# Mary De Morgan's Narrative Techniques: The Art of Gentle Correction

[[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]] (1850–1907) was a Victorian writer whose [[fairy]] tales have often been overshadowed by those of her contemporaries — George MacDonald, Juliana Horatia Ewing, and Andrew Lang. Yet *On a Pincushion* (1877) displays a distinctive set of narrative techniques that mark [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] as an original voice in the Victorian [[fairy]]-tale tradition. This article identifies four signature techniques: frame-narrative embedding, miniature moral communities, correction without punishment, and the domestication of heroism.

## Frame Narrative and Embedded Tales

*On a Pincushion* is structured as a frame narrative: objects on a pincushion — [[the-brooch|the Brooch]], the Pin, [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] — take turns telling stories. This device is not merely decorative. It creates a domestic, intimate storytelling situation — objects on a dressing-table, speaking to one another — that domesticates the [[fairy]]-tale mode. The frame also allows [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] to layer perspectives: [[the-brooch|the Brooch]] claims to have known [[lamorna|Lamorna]] "before I was cut and polished," giving the embedded tale a pseudo-testimonial [[power|authority]].

The frame narrative is itself a tale, with the objects' own personalities and social dynamics. [[the-brooch|The Brooch]] is worldly and a bit [[vanity|vain]]; the Pin is sharp-tongued; [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] is quiet and observant. This miniature society of storytellers mirrors the miniature society of the [[the-water-people|water people]] within the tales — a recursive structure in which small communities pass judgment on larger ones.

## Miniature Societies as Moral Commentators

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most distinctive technique is her use of miniature communities as moral observers. The [[the-water-people|water people]] in "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]" are "never more than two or three inches high" (001-011) yet they function as a complete moral community — debating, proposing solutions, applauding intelligence, and gently mocking ignorance. When the old elf proposes [[the-theft-of-the-reflection|stealing Lamorna's reflection]], the community cheers: "Ah, what a fine thing it is to have a mind like that!" (001-023). A lady elf adds: "With such a mind as that, one could rule countries or take cities" (001-025). The old elf "bowed all round and smiled pleasantly, for he prided himself on his good manners" (001-026).

This is comedy, but it is also moral philosophy in miniature. The [[the-water-people|water people]] model the very values [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] [[hope|wish]]es to teach: collective deliberation, respect for intelligence, gentle correction of ignorance (the old elf corrects the young elf's mistake about reflections "blandly," without anger — 001-029, 001-030). By making the moral community tiny, [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] makes its lessons both [[charm]]ing and inescapable.

> On hearing this all the elves gave a loud cheer. "You have got it," they cried. "Ah, what a fine thing it is to have a mind like that!"
>
> (001-023)

## Correction Without Punishment

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales consistently reject punishment in favor of cure. When the [[the-water-people|water people]] debate what to do about [[lamorna|Lamorna]], one proposes punishment — and is immediately overruled: "what good will it do to [[erick|Erick]] for us to punish her? Rather let us think how we can cure her of her [[pride|vanity]]" (001-019). This is the collection's moral thesis statement. The [[the-water-people|water people]] are not avengers; they are therapists.

This pattern recurs across the collection. [[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]'s magic is not punitive — it offers [[rupert|Rupert]] a quest. The toy princess substitution in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" exposes folly but does not harm anyone. Even the destruction of the giant's workshop by fire (002-045, 002-048) is an act of liberation, not revenge — the giant is not killed, only his apparatus of captivity is destroyed.

This rejection of punitive morality distinguishes [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] from the Grimms' often brutal justice and aligns her with the Victorian progressive belief in reform over retribution. Her tales argue that wrongdoing is more often error than evil, and that the appropriate response is education, not punishment.

## The Domestication of Heroism

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s heroes are notable for their ordinariness. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] is not a prince but a foundling. [[erick|Erick]] is a fisherman. [[jack|Jack]] is a traveler. [[rupert|Rupert]] is a quest-hero, but his quest is for a cure for his mother's hair loss — a domestic, almost comically un-epic motivation. When [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] bargains with the [[owl|Owl]] for the villagers' boots, he cries "at the thought of losing one of his bright brown eyes" (002-038) — he is brave, but he is also afraid, and [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] does not conceal his fear.

This domestication of heroism is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s quietest but most modern technique. Her heroes succeed not through supernatural strength or [[fate|destiny]] but through ordinary virtues: perseverance, kindness, the willingness to sacrifice. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s reflection — "I would give both my eyes for [[handa|Handa]], only then I should not be able to see her at all" (002-038) — captures the [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] hero perfectly: brave, fearful, practical, and motivated by love rather than glory.

> He cried at the thought of losing one of his bright brown eyes. "But I would give both my eyes for Handa," he said to himself, "only then I should not be able to see her at all."
>
> (002-038)

## Conclusion

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s narrative techniques form a coherent artistic [[dream|vision]]. The frame narrative domesticates the [[fairy]]-tale mode. Miniature societies model moral reasoning in [[charm]]ing miniature. Correction without punishment expresses a progressive Victorian ethics. And domesticated heroism insists that ordinary people — foundlings, fishermen, travelers — are capable of extraordinary acts. Together, these techniques make *On a Pincushion* more than a collection of [[fairy]] tales; they make it a quiet argument for a gentler way of being in the world.
