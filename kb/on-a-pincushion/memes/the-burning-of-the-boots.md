# The Burning of the Boots

The climactic liberating event of "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" and the collection's most dramatic single action. In the giant's underground workshop, surrounded by captive children fitted with [[magic-shoes|enchanted shoes]], [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] — a shoemaker's son — uses fire to destroy the instruments of magical captivity. The [[fire-element|burning]] of the boots is the moment where the collection's recurring argument — that **ordinary courage applied at the right moment can undo extraordinary evil** — finds its purest expression. (002-045) (002-048)

## Context

The giant, disguised as a shoemaker, has been capturing village children and fitting them with [[magic-shoes|enchanted shoes]] that compel obedience. His workshop is an underground cavern accessed through a passage in the [[the-forest|forest]] floor. (002-028) The children are prisoners, bound by magic they cannot break.

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] has discovered the truth. Guided by a magical [[the-hare|Hare]], he has descended into the workshop and seen the children — including [[handa|Handa]], wearing shoes that force her to dance without rest. (002-028) The giant is absent. The moment for action has arrived.

## The Event

The children themselves propose the solution:

> "Let us set fire to them at once."
>
> (002-045)

This is a crucial narrative choice: the **captives** name their liberation. [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] is not a savior imposing rescue from above but an ally who acts on the imprisoned children's own plan. His heroism is in execution, not conception — he does what they cannot do themselves.

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] acts. The boots — the giant's enchanted instruments — catch [[fire-element|flame]]:

> The boots soon caught fire, and burnt steadily. When they were quite consumed, Siegfrid remembered the magic shoes.
>
> (002-048)

The fire burns "steadily" — not explosively, not chaotically, but with the calm purpose of something that knows its work. The adverb is deliberate: this is not destruction as rage but destruction as **method**. [[fire-element|Fire]] that burns steadily is fire under [[power|control]] — wielded, not unleashed.

## Consequences

### Immediate

The boots are consumed. The [[magic|enchantment]] binding the children to the giant's workshop breaks. The instruments of captivity are destroyed beyond repair — fire's gift is that what it takes cannot be given back. (002-048)

### The Escape

[[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] remembers the [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s on [[handa|Handa]]'s feet — shoes that compel dancing, designed to exhaust and [[power|control]]. In a brilliant inversion, he uses their compulsive magic as propulsion: the shoes carry both himself and [[handa|Handa]] through the underground passage, out into the [[the-forest|forest]], and into sunlight:

> When they got to the opening in the forest, they saw that the sun was shining brightly.
>
> (002-053)

The transition from underground darkness to forest sunlight is the tale's structural marker of liberation: the natural world, indifferent but beautiful, receives the freed captives.

### Long-Term

The giant's power is broken — not by defeating the giant in combat but by destroying the **means** of his power. The tale argues that evil's instruments are more vulnerable than evil itself: you cannot always defeat the giant, but you can burn his boots. The workshop's economy of captivity collapses when its tools are ash.

## Thematic Significance

### Fire as Liberation

This event establishes the collection's central argument about [[fire-element|fire]]: it is the element of **agency**. Unlike [[the-brook|water]] (passive, reflective), ice (static, preserving), or wind (invisible, carrying), fire is the element that **does something**. The [[fire-element|burning]] of the boots is the collection's paradigm case of fire-as-liberation — a pattern that echoes through [[jack|Jack]]'s fire-journey and the Princess's fire-trial in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]."

### The Ordinary Hero

Siegfried is a shoemaker's son — not a prince, not a wizard, not a prophesied hero. His weapon is not a magic sword but a match. The tale insists that evil's instruments are vulnerable to the most ordinary of tools, wielded by the most ordinary of hands. The [[fire-element|burning]] of the boots is a democratization of heroism: anyone can strike a [[fire-element|flame]].

### Repurposing vs. Destroying

Siegfried's genius — and the tale's moral sophistication — lies in his discernment between what must be destroyed and what can be repurposed. The boots **must** burn: they are instruments of pure captivity. The [[magic-shoes|magic shoe]]s can be **used**: their compulsive power, redirected, becomes the vehicle of escape. This is not a tale that rejects magic wholesale but one that distinguishes between magic that binds and magic that can be turned to liberation.
