# Water People

Tiny benevolent elves who live beneath the [[the-brook|water]] in the brook that runs through the village, central to the embedded tale "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]" in *On a Pincushion*.

## Overview

Water people are a race of diminutive elves who inhabit the brook near [[the-village|Lamorna's village]]. They are shaped like humans but stand no more than two or three inches tall, and they cannot survive exposure to air. Despite their size, they are powerful agents of moral intervention — able to steal a person's reflection and, in doing so, cure [[pride|vanity]]. They are kindly disposed towards humans who are neither cruel nor wicked, and they take an active interest in the lives of those who live near their [[the-brook|water]]s. ((001-011))

> "They are not mermaids, but are shaped exactly like men, only they are never more than two or three inches high. They are very kindly and well-disposed towards human beings, and never hurt anyone who does not hurt them." ((001-011))

## In the Narrative

### Appearances

Water people appear throughout "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," the first embedded tale told by [[the-brooch|the Brooch]]. They first observe [[lamorna|Lamorna]] as she admires her reflection in the brook, shaking their heads and sighing at her [[pride|vanity]]. ((001-012)) They then gather around the fisherman [[erick|Erick]]'s boat, having long watched and approved of him because he was careful not to torture his catch but killed fish at once. ((001-014)) When they hear [[erick|Erick]] lamenting that [[lamorna|Lamorna]] scorns his love, they decide to intervene. ((001-016))

Their most dramatic action is the theft of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection. On a moonlit night, they throw sand ropes over her [[reflection|image]] in the [[the-brook|water]] and pull it down into the brook. ((001-043)) They keep the captured reflection tied down in a special grotto, delighted with their prize. ((001-049)) When [[lamorna|Lamorna]] later repents of her [[pride|vanity]] and returns to the brook to search for [[erick|Erick]], the [[the-water-people|water elves]] release her reflection:

> When the water elves heard her words, they flew to the wise old elf, and said—"See how hardly she has been punished. She is quite cured of her vanity. Let us cut the ropes of sand, and let her image free." (001-097)

They then hold a great festival to celebrate the happy resolution. ((001-117))

### Function

The [[the-water-people|water people]] serve as a narrative device for moral correction. They cannot physically force [[lamorna|Lamorna]] to change, but they can remove the source of her [[pride|vanity]] — her reflection — creating the conditions for her character growth. They function as a form of benevolent [[fairy]]-tale justice: invisible observers who reward the virtuous ([[erick|Erick]]) and gently correct the flawed ([[lamorna|Lamorna]]), using cleverness rather than force. Their intervention [[transformation|transform]]s what could be a simple [[pride|vanity]] fable into a story about the relationship between [[reflection|self-image]] and genuine love.


### Moral Community

The Water People also function as a moral community within [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]]'s miniature ecosystem. They represent a natural order that recognizes and values goodness — their fondness for [[erick|Erick]] is not arbitrary but earned through his virtuous conduct (001-014). Their presence establishes that [[the-pincushion|the pincushion]] world is not merely an inanimate landscape but a populated microcosm with its own society and moral sensibilities. In the broader thematic structure of "On a Pincushion," the Water People serve as a chorus of sorts — witnessing and responding to the central love story between [[erick|Erick]] and [[lamorna|Lamorna]] from their aquatic vantage point, their quiet approval functioning as a form of narrative validation.

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s [[the-water-people|water people]] belong to the rich Victorian [[fairy]] tradition of miniature supernatural beings — akin to the flower [[fairy|fairies]] of contemporary illustration, but with more agency and moral purpose. Unlike the capricious [[fairy|fairies]] of traditional folklore who might kidnap or trick humans for sport, these [[the-water-people|water elves]] are morally engaged. Their stated principle — "never hurt anyone who does not hurt them" ((001-011)) — establishes a reciprocal ethic that aligns them with the story's moral framework. Their communal decision-making (the council of elves, [[the-old-elf|the wise old elf]] who proposes the plan, the lady elves who admire his wisdom) also mirrors human village governance, making them a miniature parallel society rather than alien beings.
