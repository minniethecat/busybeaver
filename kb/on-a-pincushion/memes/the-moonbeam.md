# The Moonbeam

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," the moonbeam is no ordinary shaft of light — it is a portal, a stage, and a path of departure, the medium through which the supernatural Princess makes her appearances and her final exit from the mortal world.

## Overview

The moonbeam appears twice in the climactic scenes of "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," both times as the setting for numinous encounter. First, [[jack|Jack]] looks up and sees the Princess "standing in a moonbeam" (005-249), looking "far more beautiful even than before," with the Prince beside her — a [[dream|vision]] made possible by the moonbeam's light. Later, in the tale's final supernatural moment, "both Prince and Princess floated up the moonbeam" (005-257), and the Princess "looked back and kissed her hand as before, and they flew out of the window, and [[jack|Jack]] never saw them again." The moonbeam is the threshold between worlds — a bridge of light that connects the mortal realm to whatever place the Prince and Princess belong.

> "Both Prince and Princess floated up the moonbeam, and the Princess looked back and kissed her hand as before." (005-257)

## In the Narrative

### The Moonbeam as Stage

The first moonbeam appearance (005-249) functions as a theatrical reveal. [[jack|Jack]] looks up at the window and sees the Princess standing *in* the light — the preposition is crucial. She is not merely illuminated by the moonbeam; she is *within* it, as if the light itself is a space she can occupy. The moonbeam makes her visible, but it also seems to make her *present* — a visitation enabled by light.

### The Moonbeam as Passage

The second moonbeam (005-257) [[transformation|transform]]s the light from a stage into a road. The Prince and Princess "floated up the moonbeam" — the verb suggesting weightlessness, ease, and supernatural grace. They "flew out of the window," ascending along the light's path. The moonbeam here is a one-way passage out of the mortal world, carrying the tale's magical beings back to wherever they came from. [[jack|Jack]] "never saw them again" — the moonbeam closes as definitively as it opened.

### Light as Transition

Across [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales, light — especially moonlight and sunbeams — marks moments of transition between worlds. The moonbeam in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" is the purest expression of this motif: a vertical shaft of light that is simultaneously a location, a [[truth|revelation]], and a departure. It is the visual equivalent of a [[fairy]]-tale ending — a lifting up and away into [[light|radiance]].
