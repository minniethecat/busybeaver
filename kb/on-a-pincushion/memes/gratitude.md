# Gratitude

Gratitude in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s tales is the hinge between receiving kindness and deserving it. The tortoise-queen's curse on [[trevina|Trevina]] — "you will [[hope|wish]] you had been grateful to my son for his kindness" (003-079) — makes the stakes explicit: ingratitude is punished, and gratitude is the foundation of right relationship.

## Overview

Though the word "gratitude" itself appears sparingly in the corpus, the concept runs through the collection's moral architecture. The [[the-brook|water]]-people's intervention for [[erick|Erick]] is an act of kindness that expects no return, but [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s eventual cure requires her to recognize what has been done for her. [[trevina|Trevina]]'s story in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is structured around gratitude and its absence. The seagull's help is motivated by remembered kindness, and [[trevina|Trevina]]'s own role as storyteller is an act of gratitude — she tells her tale to [[rupert|Rupert]] as thanks for his presence on her island.

## In the Narrative

### Trevina and the Tortoise-Queen: Gratitude Weaponized

The tortoise-queen's accusation — ingratitude — is the most explicit moral charge in the collection. When [[trevina|Trevina]] rejects [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king and plots her escape, the queen [[transformation|transform]]s her into a tigress: "you will [[hope|wish]] you had been grateful to my son for his kindness in offering to make you his wife" (003-079). The irony is layered: [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king's "kindness" is coercive, and yet the narrative treats gratitude as a genuine virtue, not merely a weapon of the powerful. [[trevina|Trevina]] cannot be grateful for a forced marriage, but the structure of the world demands some form of reciprocity.

> "Yes! you shall be well punished, for you shall become a tigress; and, left by yourself in the enchanted land, you will wish you had been grateful to my son for his kindness in offering to make you his wife." Then she waved her black hands in the air, and I felt a dreadful change coming upon me. Hair was growing all over me. My arms became forelegs like those of a tiger. I felt that a tail was beginning to sprout out behind.
>
> (003-094)

### Communal Ingratitude: The Shoemaker

The old shoemaker in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" is a study in communal ingratitude. Having made shoes for the village for years, he is abandoned the moment a cheaper alternative appears. The villagers' [[forget]]ting is ingratitude made structural — a failure to honor the relationship that sustained them. The tale's resolution — [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]'s heroic rescue earning the village's renewed loyalty — is a restoration of the gratitude economy. The moral is clear: a community without gratitude destroys itself.

### Gratitude as Love's Foundation

[[blanchelys|Blanchelys]], in the framing story of "On a Pincushion," embodies gratitude as the ground of love. Her love for her father is described as "grounded in gratitude and care" — love that is received and returned, not demanded. This contrasts with [[zaire|Zaire]]'s envious love, which destroys what it cannot possess. For [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]], gratitude is what distinguishes healthy love from its corrosive alternatives.

### The Seagull's Repaid Debt

The seagull in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" helps [[trevina|Trevina]] not from abstract [[kindness|benevolence]] but from specific gratitude: "I hate [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king and his mother as much as you do" (003-080). The seagull owes [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen a grudge, and helping [[trevina|Trevina]] is its repayment. Gratitude and its inverse — the gratitude of revenge — are two sides of the same coin. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s moral universe tracks debts of both kindness and cruelty, and settlement comes for both.

## Gratitude and the Gift Economy

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales operate within a gift economy in which every kindness creates an obligation. The [[magic-candles|magic candle]]s grant [[hope|wish]]es "requiring wisdom in their use" (001-127) — they are gifts, but gifts that demand responsible receiving. The hair-tree rods cure [[trevina|Trevina]] but only after the ordeal of being beaten. Even magical gifts in this world are not free; they require the recipient to become worthy. Gratitude is the emotional register of this worthiness.
