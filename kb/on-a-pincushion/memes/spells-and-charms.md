# Spells and Charms

The system of magical [[spell|incantation]]s, protective [[charm|charms]], and [[charm|enchanted object]]s that characters in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales use to defend themselves, bind enemies, and navigate magical dangers.

## Overview

Spells in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales form a surprisingly coherent magical system. Characters obtain [[spell|spells]] from specialized shops, carry protective tokens (nuts that guard against [[magic|enchantment]]s), and sing [[spell|spells]] aloud to maintain their power. Unlike the one-off magical items (candles, seeds), [[spell|spells]] are a reusable technology that characters learn, buy, and deploy strategically.

> It was, in fact, a shop where every sort of spell or charm was sold. (004-018)

## In the Narrative

### Protective Magic

The most prominent use of [[spell|spells]] is protective. A character in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is given "a nut to protect me from [[spell|spells]]" (003-145), establishing a rock-paper-scissors logic where specific [[charm|charms]] counter specific [[magic|enchantment]]s. The hero is told "you can have against every sort of [[spell]]" (003-104), suggesting a comprehensive magical armory available to those who know where to look.

### The Spell Shop

The mention of a commercial [[spell]] shop (004-018) is a distinctively modern touch — magic as commodity, available for purchase:

> It was a queer sort of shop. It was neither a grocer's, nor a draper's, nor a hatter's. Yet it contained sugar, and dresses, and hats. But the sugar was magic sugar, which transformed any liquid into which it was put; the dresses each had some special charm, and the hats were wishing-caps. (004-018)

This demystifies [[spell]]-casting and makes it accessible to ordinary characters, not just [[fairy]]-born beings. It also introduces an economic dimension: magical protection can be bought, suggesting social commentary on access to safety and power.

### Sung Spells

The Princess in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" sings a [[spell]] continuously to exert magical pressure — "it was a [[spell]] the Princess sang, and if she had gone on he would have been dried up altogether." (005-126) This introduces duration and stamina as magical constraints: [[spell|spells]] require sustained performance, making them tests of will as much as knowledge.

## Literary Context

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[spell]] system reflects the Victorian fascination with systematizing magic — from the Golden Dawn's ritual magic to the cataloguing impulse of Frazer's *The Golden Bough*. The [[spell]] shop is particularly modern, anticipating the commercialization of magic that would become standard in 20th-century fantasy from Tolkien's [[magic|magical objects]] to Harry Potter's Diagon Alley.
