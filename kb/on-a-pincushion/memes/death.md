# Death

Death in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is not an ending but a narrative threshold: it tests the [[life|living]], reveals character, and in several tales serves as the precondition for [[transformation|transform]]ation.

## Overview

Death appears across four of the five tales, but never as mere tragedy. The grandmother's death in "On a Pincushion" sets the plot in motion — [[lamorna|Lamorna]] does not grieve but broods over what gifts she might receive (001-129), revealing her self-absorption. The Queen's death in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" creates the vacuum that the false princess must fill (004-006). The threat of being "frozen to death" (005-144) by the Ice People hangs over [[jack|Jack]]'s entire journey in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]." [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats death as a moral revealer: how characters respond to death — their own or others' — shows who they truly are.

> "She did not grieve for her grandmother's death, but sat brooding over what gifts would be left her." (001-129)

## In the Narrative

### Death as Plot Catalyst

In "On a Pincushion," the grandmother's death is the first major event after the opening description. [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s failure to grieve properly — "she did not grieve" (001-129) — immediately signals her moral deficiency. The death is not dwelt upon; its narrative function is to free [[lamorna|Lamorna]] for the adventures that will educate her.

### Death as Threat

"[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]" treats death most explicitly as a physical threat. [[the-ice-people|The Ice People]] "have caught and frozen to death" (005-195) unfortunates, and [[jack|Jack]] himself falls "sick unto death" (005-244) before the climax. In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," the protagonist faces death from "arms" that "would have crushed him to death, for they were fast tightening round him" (003-067). Death in these tales is embodied, physical, and requires active resistance — it cannot be reasoned with or [[charm]]ed away.

### Death and Rebirth

Several tales hint at a death-and-rebirth pattern. The Queen's death in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" enables [[princess-ursula|Ursula]]'s eventual liberation from her false identity. The "frozen to death" fate of the Ice People's victims implies a kind of suspended animation rather than annihilation — bodies preserved in ice, awaiting the fire that might release them. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s death is rarely absolute; it opens doors as often as it closes them.

## Literary Context

Victorian [[fairy]] tales frequently used death as a moral instrument — consider the Little Match Girl's death as social critique (Andersen) or the various deaths in Oscar Wilde's *The Happy Prince* that serve as spiritual commentary. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] differs in making death a *plot mechanism* rather than a *moral conclusion*. Her characters' responses to death matter more than death's finality. This is closer to the folk-tale tradition where death is a trial to be survived than to the Victorian sentimental deathbed scene.
