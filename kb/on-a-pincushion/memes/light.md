# Light

Light in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is never neutral. It is magical, directional, and transient — a candle that dies out at the departure of [[love|Love]], a sunbeam that is secretly a [[fairy]], a [[the-moonbeam|moonbeam]] that serves as a road between worlds.

## Overview

Light suffuses [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales, appearing in multiple forms: candlelight, [[the-moonbeam|moonlight]], sunlight, and the mysterious inner light of [[magic|magical objects]]. When [[love|Love]] departs, "the light from the candle died out" (001-143), leaving [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] in sudden darkness. When the sun shines, "all the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]]s are in reality tiny Sun-[[fairy|fairies]]" (001-220). When the magical candles are lit, "there will appear to you a [[fairy]]" (001-127). Light consistently marks moments of supernatural presence — its arrival signals magic; its departure signals loss.

> "The light from the candle died out, and Blanchelys was left alone." (001-143)

## In the Narrative

### Light as Magical Presence

The candle that [[love|Love]] carries is the purest symbol of light-as-magic in the collection. Its [[fire-element|flame]] illuminates [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s garden encounter, and its extinction coincides with [[love|Love]]'s disappearance (001-143). The magical candles given to the sisters operate by the same logic: "when you set them alight there will appear to you a [[fairy]] who will grant you the [[hope|wish]] of your heart" (001-127). Light here is not metaphor but mechanism — the physical medium through which magic enters the mortal world.

### Light as Revelation

[[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] kneels "in the light of the candle" and sees a boy (001-136) — the light reveals what darkness conceals. [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection-theft depends on [[the-moonbeam|moonlight]]: "the elves sat down on the edge of the brook in the [[the-moonbeam|moonlight]]" (001-041). And the Princess in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" can only be seen "standing in a [[the-moonbeam|moonbeam]]" (005-249). Light, in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s visual imagination, is what makes the invisible visible — it is the condition of [[truth|revelation]].

### The Death of Light

Just as light's arrival signals magic, its departure signals loss. When the candle dies out, [[love|Love]] vanishes. When the [[the-moonbeam|moonbeam]] ascends, the Prince and Princess "flew out of the window, and [[jack|Jack]] never saw them again" (005-257). Light in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales is inherently transient — it comes, it illuminates, it departs. The tales are structured around this rhythm of illumination and loss.

> She wore a shining flame-coloured dress, with a long train, and one pale blue and silver catherine-wheel fixed in her bosom, and another in her hair.
