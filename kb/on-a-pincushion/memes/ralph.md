## Identity

Ralph is the shoemaker of the little village in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]," the father of the protagonist [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]]. He is "the only shoemaker in the village" and makes "shoes and boots for all the people" (002-001). He is characterised by his craftsmanship, [[truth|honesty]], and pride in his work — he "took a long time making each pair, for he always wanted them to be well made, and would have been [[shame|ashamed]] if he had been told that they had worn out before their time or did not fit well" (002-001).

## Role in the Story

Ralph's prosperity is destroyed when a strange old man — actually a wicked gnome in disguise — arrives in the village selling cheap but shoddy boots and shoes. Despite Ralph's goods being far superior ("The boots and shoes Ralph made cost three times as much" [002-005]), the villagers are seduced by the low prices. One by one, they abandon Ralph for the gnome's wares.

> The boots and shoes Ralph made cost three times as much, but they were very different! They were good, well-made shoes; and they were not enchanted, and bought with every penny the maker's honest earnings.
>
> (002-005)

When the village is struck by a series of calamities — frost killing the corn, blight destroying the fruit, drought, and fever — [[handa|Handa]] speaks out: "You are very cruel, you are letting poor Ralph starve, because you will all buy your boots and shoes of the new old man. Ralph has worked well for you all his life, and now you all leave him" (002-017). But her father silences her, and the villagers continue to buy from the gnome.

Ralph himself falls victim to the fever: "Ralph caught it, and was very ill, and had to lie in bed all day" (002-019). He and his wife become destitute, forced to sell all their furniture and clothes, and [[siegfrid|Siegfrid]] and his mother "had to go about barefoot" (002-019).

## Significance

Ralph represents the honest craftsman undone by the community's greed and disloyalty. His fate is directly tied to the moral decay of the village — as the old proverb warned, misfortune would come "directly they forgot to behave well" (002-001). His suffering embodies the cost of the villagers' choice of cheap novelty over proven quality and loyalty.
