# Memory

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, [[forget|memory]] is never mere recall — it is a moral obligation. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]]'s final promise, "the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] will never [[forget]] them, for every night he sings their story" (001-288), elevates [[forget|memory]] to the collection's highest purpose: the artist who remembers ensures that love outlasts its moment.

## Overview

Memory weaves through [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales as the thread that connects past to present. The pincushion objects — [[the-brooch|the Brooch]], the Pin, [[the-shawl-pin|the Shawl-pin]] — are themselves artifacts of [[forget|memory]], each carrying a story from its former life. [[jack|Jack]] holds onto the [[forget|memory]] of the fire-people even when he cannot see them. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]]'s nightly song is an act of deliberate remembering, a refusal to let the story of the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] and Moonbeam fade. Memory, in this moral economy, is what separates the [[life|living]] from the merely existing.

## In the Narrative

### The Nightingale as Keeper of Memory

[[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]] is the collection's most explicit embodiment of [[forget|memory]]. His role as storyteller — singing the tale of the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] and Moonbeam — is also a role as preserver. The closing line of the frame narrative — "the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] will never [[forget]] them, for every night he sings their story" (001-288) — makes clear that [[forget|memory]] is not passive but active, sustained through art. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]]'s song is the collection's own metaphor for itself: storytelling as [[forget|memory]]-work.

> Men found it and called it the Opal. But the Nightingale knew that it was the Sunbeam and Moonbeam who, in dying, had suffused the Stone with their mingled colours and light; and the Nightingale will never forget them, for every night he sings their story, and that is why his song is so sad.
>
> (001-288)

### Jack's Remembering

In "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," [[jack|Jack]]'s [[forget|memory]] of the fire-people sustains him through the story's trials. After his first journey, "he could not [[forget]] the fire-people and the pale pretty Princess" (005-101). His return to the fireplace is driven by [[forget|memory]] — the refusal to let an extraordinary experience become merely a dream. [[jack|Jack]]'s [[forget|memory]] is tested: he must hold onto the impossible knowledge of the fire-people's world while [[life|living]] in his own. The reward for faithful [[forget|memory]] is reunion.

### Objects with Memory

[[the-brooch|The Brooch]], Pin, and Shawl-pin are objects with pasts. [[the-shawl-pin|The Shawl-pin]] "remember[s] the time when I was never used to pin up anything but the finest Indian and Cashmere shawls" — its [[forget|memory]] of former status gives it personality and pathos. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s talking objects suggest that [[forget|memory]] is what makes any being — human, animal, or pin — into a self with a story.

### Memory Across Generations

The tales themselves function as intergenerational [[forget|memory]]. [[trevina|Trevina]]'s story, told to [[rupert|Rupert]] within "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," becomes the [[forget|memory]] he carries forward. The embedded tale structure mirrors [[forget|memory]]'s nested quality — stories within stories, each remembering the one before. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s formal choice of the frame narrative is itself an argument for [[forget|memory]] as the architecture of meaning.

## Memory and Forgetting

Memory's moral weight is sharpened by its opposite: [[forget]]ting. When the villagers in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" [[forget]] [[ralph|Ralph]]'s years of honest [[servant|service]] in favor of the old man's cheap shoes (002-020), they bring disaster on themselves. [[forget|Forgetting]] is ingratitude made structural — the erosion of loyalty through the convenience of not remembering. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats [[forget]]ting not as a cognitive failure but as a moral one.

## The Collection as Memory

The meta-narrative implication is powerful: *On a Pincushion* itself is [[forget|memory]]. [[the-brooch|The Brooch]], Shawl-pin, and Pin tell stories to pass the time, but the act of telling preserves what would otherwise be lost. When the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] sings his final song, he is doing what the collection itself does — ensuring that the story survives its own ending.
