# Power

Power in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* is exercised by magical beings over mortals, by parents over children, and by enchanters over the enchanted — but it is never absolute, and resistance is always possible.

## Overview

The word "power" appears only twice in the corpus, but the concept pervades every tale. The zirbal-tree's warning in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" — "he will have as much power over you as he had over the previous victims" (003-152) — establishes power as transferable, dangerous, and something to be resisted. The King in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" is "overpowered with grief" (004-072), showing that even sovereigns are subject to forces beyond their control. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s power dynamics are always relational: power is something one being holds over another, never an abstract capacity.

> "He will have as much power over you as he had over the previous victims." (003-152)

## In the Narrative

### Magical Power as Threat

The explicit warning about the zirbal-tree (003-152) frames magical power as something that accumulates through victims. Each person defeated adds to the power of the victor, creating an escalating threat. The protagonist must break this cycle not by being more powerful but by being more clever — [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales consistently privilege intelligence over raw magical strength.

### Power and Resistance

The collection's moral architecture suggests that power is never perfectly secure. The wicked [[fairy]]'s power over [[lamorna|Lamorna]] is broken by truth. [[the-ice-people|The Ice People]]'s power is broken by fire. The mother's jealous power is evaded. In each case, power appears absolute until the moment it fails — a narrative pattern that teaches readers that domination, however complete it seems, contains the seeds of its own undoing.

### The Limits of Sovereignty

Even legitimate authority is limited in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world. The King is "overpowered with grief" (004-072) — his royal power irrelevant against emotional pain. The Queen's authority cannot prevent her [[dream|nightmare]]. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] systematically shows that political and magical power are equally bounded by forces — love, grief, truth — that obey no hierarchy.

## Literary Context

Victorian [[fairy]] tales often reinforced existing power structures (the rightful prince regains his throne, the good king rules [[wisdom|wise]]ly). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] is notably skeptical of power in all its forms. Her tales suggest that power corrupts whether it is magical or political, legitimate or usurped. This aligns her with the more critical strand of Victorian fantasy (George MacDonald's *Lilith*, 1895) rather than the conservative tradition.
