# Toy Princess

A magically animated mechanical double of [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]], created by [[taboret|the fairy godmother]] [[taboret|Taboret]] in the tale "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]" to replace [[princess-ursula|the real princess]] at court.

## Overview

[[a-toy-princess-tale|The Toy Princess]] is one of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s most striking magical creations: not a [[life|living]] being but an enchanted automaton, crafted to mimic a human princess so perfectly that the courtiers never suspect the substitution. "They did not think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her place." (004-058) The construct raises questions about identity, performance, and what makes a "real" princess — questions that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] explores with characteristic lightness and moral clarity.

> "They did not think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her place." (004-058)

## In the Narrative

### Creation

[[taboret|Taboret]], seeing that [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] lives an emotionally starved [[life|existence]] — "never taught any games or given any toys" (004-010) — crafts the Toy Princess as a magical substitute. The construct is placed at court where it performs royal duties, while the real [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] is raised in a warm, loving household where she can develop naturally. (004-063)

### The Reveal

[[a-toy-princess-tale|The Toy Princess]] functions flawlessly until [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] comes of age, at which point [[taboret|Taboret]] reveals the substitution. (004-072) The court's shock at having been deceived by a mechanical double is a pointed comment on the artificiality of courtly life — the "toy" princess was indistinguishable from the "real" one because court behavior itself is a kind of performance.

## Literary Context

[[magic|Magic]]al doubles and automata appear throughout 19th-century literature — from E.T.A. Hoffmann's *The Sandman* (Olympia the automaton) to Carlo Collodi's *Pinocchio*. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s Toy Princess is unusual in being benevolent: where most literary doppelgangers are uncanny or threatening (Freud's *unheimlich*), [[taboret|Taboret]]'s creation is protective, designed to preserve rather than usurp the original's identity.
