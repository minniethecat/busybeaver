## Identity

The Bullfinch is a bird character who appears in the frame narrative of "On a Pincushion," the opening tale of the collection. Her nest in a large tree becomes the central gathering place where the birds discuss the Moonbeam and where the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] first learns of her beauty (001-221). The Bullfinch is characterised by her grumpy, scornful temperament and her impatience with romantic sentiment.

## Role in the Frame Story

The Bullfinch's nest is where the young Bullfinch first mentions wanting to see the Moonbeam, prompting a conversation among the birds about her loveliness (001-223). When the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] praises the Moonbeam, the old Bullfinch dismisses him scornfully: "[[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]]! Everyone knows that the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] was moonstruck long ago. Who can trust a word he says?" (001-226).

As the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] begins visiting the tree daily to talk to the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] about the Moonbeam, the Bullfinch grows increasingly annoyed: "the Bullfinch was quite angry" (001-242). When the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] sings his loud and clear song about the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] to the Moonbeam at night, it "awoke the Bullfinch, who flew into a rage, and declared that if it went on any longer she would speak to the [[owl|Owl]] about it, and have it stopped. For the [[owl|Owl]] was chief judge, and always ate the little birds when they did not behave themselves" (001-249).

After the [[the-sunbeam|Sunbeam]] and Moonbeam die together within the Stone, the Bullfinch's reaction is notably unsympathetic: "When the Bullfinch heard of it she was quite pleased. 'Now, at last,' she said, 'we shall hear the end of the Moonbeam. I am heartily glad, for I was sick of her'" (001-285). This response contrasts sharply with the Wood-dove's [[kindness|compassion]]ate reaction and underscores the Bullfinch's character as practical and unsentimental.

## Significance

The Bullfinch serves as a foil to the romantic [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]] and gentle Wood-dove, representing a pragmatic, earthbound perspective that rejects the transcendent love story unfolding in her own tree. Her grumbling provides comic relief while also highlighting the indifference of the ordinary world to extraordinary beauty and sacrifice.
