# The Palace Garden

The garden at the royal palace where [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]]'s rose tree flourishes and where [[princess-ursula|Princess Ursula]] later walks — a space that bridges the natural world and the artificial court.

## Overview

The palace garden appears across tales as a space of relative freedom within the royal enclosure. [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] "came out into the garden, with her baby in her arms" and "stood under the shade" of the rose tree (001-151). For [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] in "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," the garden is one of the few places she can escape the barren formality of her rooms — she "ran into [[the-castle|the palace]] garden, in [[darkness|the dark]] night" (001-163).

## Geography and Description

The garden contains a rose tree that grows to significant size, with branches wide enough to provide shade. It is enclosed within [[the-castle|the palace]] grounds, making it a semi-private space — not as exposed as the village brook, not as confined as the castle interior.

## Narrative Significance

The garden represents cultivated nature — nature tamed and shaped by human hands, much as the characters' wilder impulses ([[pride|vanity]], jealousy) must be tamed. The rose tree that grows here is the [[life|living]] symbol of love's health, making the garden a space of emotional barometry.

> After a time, Blanchelys had a little son, who was heir to the crown, and she was even happier than before, and her husband loved her better. The bells were rung at the birth of the prince, and all the people rejoiced. And the rose-tree grew so fast, that when Blanchelys came out into the garden, with her baby in her arms, it was quite a big tree, and she was able to stand under the shade of its branches.
>
> (001-151)
