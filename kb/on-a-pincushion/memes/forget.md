# Forgetting

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion*, the act of forgetting — and its counterpoint, [[memory|remembering]] — carries moral weight: those who remember are blessed, and those who forget betray.

## Overview

The language of forgetting threads through the corpus with quiet insistence. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]] "will never forget them, for every night he sings their story" (001-288). A journey requires that "you do not forget to bring with you some of the fruit" (003-104). The fire-people promise "we shall not forget to help you" (005-154). And at parting, "we shall never forget you" (005-240) is [[the-sea|the deep]]est blessing one character can give another. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents forgetting as a form of moral failure and [[memory|remembering]] as an act of love.

> "The Nightingale will never forget them, for every night he sings their story." (001-288)

## In the Narrative

### The Nightingale's Vow

The collection's most poignant statement about memory comes at its close: the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s promise never to forget (001-288). This is not casual reassurance but a sacred commitment — to sing the story every night is to make forgetting impossible. [[the-nightingale|The Nightingale]] becomes the keeper of collective memory, a role that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] implicitly assigns to storytellers themselves.

### The Practical Imperative

"Forget not" appears as a practical command throughout the tales: "do not forget to bring with you some of the fruit" (003-104), "Do not forget all we have told you" (005-155). These are survival instructions, but their repetition elevates them beyond the practical. To forget the fruit is to lose protection; to forget the instructions is to face danger unprepared. [[memory|Memory]], in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world, is literally life-saving.

### The Promise Never to Forget

The fire-people's parting words — "we shall never forget you" (005-240) — [[transformation|transform]] forgetting/[[memory|remembering]] into the ultimate measure of relationship. To be remembered is to matter; to be forgotten is to cease to exist in any meaningful sense. The Princess's promise is the tale's emotional climax, more significant than any material gift.

## Literary Context

Victorian culture was deeply invested in memory and commemoration — from the cult of [[grief|mourning]] to the invention of photography. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment of forgetting as moral failure reflects this broader cultural anxiety about impermanence. Her tales, like the [[the-nightingale|Nightingale]]'s song, are themselves acts of remembrance: stories told so that characters and their lessons will not be forgotten.
