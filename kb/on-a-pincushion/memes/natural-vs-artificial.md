# Natural vs. Artificial: Setting as Moral Commentary

[[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]] employs setting in *On a Pincushion* with remarkable consistency: natural spaces — forests, brooks, rivers, the sea — are sites of [[transformation|transform]]ation, healing, and authentic feeling, while artificial spaces — courts, castles, shops — are arenas of performance, deception, and constraint. This spatial binary functions as a submerged moral vocabulary throughout the collection.

## Natural Spaces: Sites of Truth

### The Brook

The brook is the collection's central threshold space. In "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," it is where [[lamorna|Lamorna]] bends to admire her reflection (001-010), and where the [[the-water-people|water people]] observe her [[pride|vanity]] with concern. The brook is both a mirror — showing [[lamorna|Lamorna]] her own [[reflection|image]] — and a gateway to an alternative moral community. The [[the-water-people|water people]] live "beneath the [[the-brook|water]], and never come to the surface, because if they were to breathe the air they would die" (001-011). Their underwater realm is a space of wisdom inaccessible from the surface, a depth that literalizes moral depth.

> They are not mermaids, but are shaped exactly like men, only they are never more than two or three inches high. They are very kindly and well-disposed towards human beings, and never hurt anyone who does not hurt them.
>
> (001-011)

### The Forest

Forests in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] function as sites of ordeal and truth. In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," the enchanted forest where [[trevina|Trevina]] is imprisoned as a tigress is wild and dangerous but also honest: there is no pretense about its dangers. [[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]] itself, growing the rods that alone can reverse [[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-queen's curse, is a natural object with magical properties — nature as the source of both curse and cure.

### The River and the Sea

The sea is a domain of kidnapping and [[transformation|transform]]ation ([[the-tortoise-king|the tortoise]]-king's island, 003-088) but also of rescue. The river that connects inland to sea is a conduit of narrative movement — [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s brook "ran into the sea about half a mile farther down" (001-011), linking private [[pride|vanity]] to the larger world.

## Artificial Spaces: Sites of Deception

### The Court

The court is the collection's primary negative space. In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," the court is so absorbed in its own rituals of performance that it accepts an automaton as its princess (004-058). The courtiers' inability to distinguish [[life|living]] from mechanical is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s sharpest critique of institutional life: the court trains its members to value appearance so completely that reality becomes invisible.

> In the morning, when the court ladies came to wake Princess Ursula, they found her sleeping as usual in her little bed, and little did they think it was not she, but a toy Princess placed there in her stead.
>
> (004-058)

### The Spell-Shop

The [[spell]]-shop in "[[siegfrid-and-handa-tale|Siegfrid and Handa]]" is a site of commerce in magic — a constructed, transactional space where [[magic|enchantment]] is bought and sold. It represents the commodification of wonder, a theme that [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] develops with satirical edge. Unlike natural magic (the [[the-water-people|water people]]'s benevolent intervention, the Hair Tree's curative rods), purchased magic carries moral ambiguity.

### The Palace Garden

Gardens occupy an ambiguous middle ground: cultivated nature, neither fully wild nor fully artificial. In "[[the-seeds-of-love|The Seeds of Love]]," the garden is a space of possibility where natural growth and human cultivation meet. It is the collection's most hopeful space precisely because it refuses the binary.

## The Pattern

| Natural Space | Function | Artificial Space | Function |
|--------------|----------|-----------------|----------|
| Brook | Moral reflection, communion with [[the-water-people|water people]] | Court | Performance, deception |
| Forest | Ordeal, truth, [[transformation|transform]]ation | Spell-shop | Commodified magic |
| River/Sea | Movement, danger, rescue | Castle | Constraint, hierarchy |
| Hair Tree | Natural magic, cure | Palace | Appearance over substance |

## Conclusion

[[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s spatial imagination is not merely decorative but structurally moral. By consistently associating natural settings with authenticity and constructed ones with performance, she builds a worldview in which the environment itself judges its inhabitants. This is [[fairy]]-tale logic at its most elegant: the forest tells the truth that the court conceals.
