# Shame

Shame in [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s *On a Pincushion* functions as an internalized social conscience — characters learn not through punishment but through the painful awareness of having been seen doing wrong.

## Overview

Shame appears at key moments across the collection: the young elf feels "quite ashamed of his mistake" (001-030), a character is "[[fear|dread]]fully ashamed of its tears being seen" (001-218), a craftsman "would have been ashamed" of poor workmanship (002-001), and [[jack|Jack]] declares "It's a shame, a [[fear|dread]]ful shame" (005-004) at an injustice. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] distinguishes between productive shame (which corrects behavior) and unproductive shame (which merely hides), making shame a nuanced moral instrument rather than a simple negative.

> "It's a shame," he said, "a dreadful shame." (005-004)

## In the Narrative

### Shame as Self-Correction

The young elf's shame at his mistake (001-030) is the healthiest form of the emotion: immediate recognition of error followed by self-correction. No one punishes the elf; he punishes himself through the internal discomfort of shame. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] presents this as the ideal — shame as an internal moral compass that requires no external enforcer.

### The Shame of Being Seen

A more complex form appears when a character is "[[fear|dread]]fully ashamed of its tears being seen" (001-218). Here, shame attaches not to wrongdoing but to vulnerability. The character has done nothing wrong, yet feels shame at being witnessed in a moment of weakness. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats this sympathetically, suggesting that the shame of vulnerability, while painful, is part of the human condition, not a moral failing.

### Righteous Shame

[[jack|Jack]]'s cry — "It's a shame, a [[fear|dread]]ful shame" (005-004) — [[transformation|transform]]s shame from an internal emotion to an external moral judgment. He is not ashamed of himself; he is declaring a situation shameful. This outward-facing shame is the emotion of moral awakening: the recognition that the world contains injustice that should not be tolerated.

## Literary Context

Victorian literature often used shame as a disciplinary tool — the fallen woman's shame, the child's shame at disobedience. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s treatment is more psychological and less punitive. Her characters' shame leads to growth rather than destruction, making it closer to the classical Greek concept of *aidos* (productive shame) than to Victorian moralizing.
