# The River

A great river that flows through two of [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s tales, [[servant|serving]] as a boundary between the ordinary village world and the realms of castles, islands, and [[the-brook|water]]-spirits.

## Overview

The river appears in the geography of "On a Pincushion" and "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," functioning as a narrative boundary that characters must cross. In "On a Pincushion," it separates the village from [[the-castle|the King's castle]]: "over the river was a bridge, with gates" (001-124), and "on the other side of the river was a grand castle which belonged to the King" (001-130). In "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]," it is the domain of the [[the-brook|water]]-maiden, whose "father owned a boat on the river" (003-079), and the barrier that must be crossed to reach [[the-island|the magic island]] (003-118). Rivers in [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s world are not obstacles but thresholds — crossing them marks a transition in the story.

> "Over the river was a bridge, with gates." (001-124)

## In the Narrative

### The River in "On a Pincushion"

The river defines the geography of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s world. The village stands "by a great river" (001-124), and the bridge across it connects the humble village to the grand castle. When [[blanchelys|Blanchelys]] and [[zaire|Zaire]] dress in their finest to watch the King's son ride across the bridge (001-130–001-131), the river becomes the stage for the tale's romantic climax. The river is both a physical feature and a social boundary — crossing it means moving between worlds.

### The River in "The Hair Tree"

The river in "[[the-hair-tree-tale|The Hair Tree]]" is deeper and stranger. The [[the-brook|water]]-maiden's father "owned a boat on the river" (003-079), and the river leads to the island where mysterious plants grow (003-112). When [[rupert|Rupert]] asks to be carried "on your back across the river" (003-118), the crossing is fraught with magical danger. Here the river is not a social boundary but a magical one — beyond it lie the realms of the non-human.

## Literary Context

Rivers in Victorian [[fairy]] tales often function as boundaries between the mundane and the magical (the river in *The Water-Babies*, the Thames in *The Wind in the Willows*). [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s river shares this boundary-function but is more economically rendered — it is never described in lush detail, but its narrative function is precise and consistent.
