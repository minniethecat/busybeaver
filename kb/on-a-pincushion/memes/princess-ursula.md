# Princess Ursula

The true princess of "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," rescued from a stifling court upbringing by her [[fairy|fairy godmother]] [[taboret|Taboret]], who creates a magical double to rule in her place while Ursula is raised with love and freedom.

## Overview

Princess Ursula begins the tale as a victim of well-meaning neglect. Her mother the Queen has died, and the court raises her as a proper princess — which means she is "never taught any games or given any toys" (004-010). She "passed most of her time in solitary boredom" (004-010). Her [[fairy|fairy godmother]] [[taboret|Taboret]], whom Ursula loves dearly (004-005), intervenes by creating a magical [[toy-princess|Toy Princess]] to take Ursula's place, allowing the real princess to be raised in a warm, ordinary household.

> She was a pretty little girl, with a round baby face and big merry blue eyes; but as she grew older, her eyes grew less and less merry and bright, and her fat little face grew thin and pale. She was not allowed to play with any other children, lest she might learn bad manners; and she was not taught any games or given any toys. So she passed most of her time, when she was not at her lessons, looking out of the window at the birds flying against the clear blue sky; and sometimes she would give a sad little sigh when her ladies were not listening.
>
> (004-010)

## In the Story

### Role and Significance

Ursula is the tale's moral center — not because she does anything remarkable, but because her natural goodness flourishes when removed from the artificial court environment. The story argues that "princess" is a role, not an identity: the [[toy-princess|Toy Princess]] performs the role perfectly, while Ursula develops real character in obscurity. The substitution reveal at the tale's end (004-072) affirms that authentic personhood matters more than performed royalty.

### Key Actions

- Endures a childhood of courtly isolation with no play or [[love|affection]]
- Is loved by her [[fairy|fairy godmother]] [[taboret|Taboret]], who watches over her invisibly (004-005, 004-011)
- Is secretly removed from court and raised in a warm, loving home
- Returns as an adult, her true identity revealed, having been formed by love rather than protocol

### Relationships

- [[fairy-godmother]] — [[taboret|Taboret]], her magical protector and surrogate mother
- [[toy-princess]] — the magical double who takes her place at court

## Character Interpretation

Ursula embodies the Victorian critique of aristocratic child-rearing — children raised by protocol rather than parents become emotionally stunted. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s [[fairy]]-tale solution is radical: remove the child entirely and replace her with a puppet. The courtiers' inability to tell the difference is the tale's sharpest satirical point.
