# Reflection

In [[de-morgan-author-page|Mary De Morgan]]'s "[[the-story-of-vain-lamorna|The Story of Vain Lamorna]]," the reflection is more than an optical phenomenon. It is the visible self — the image one presents to the world and, crucially, to oneself. When the [[the-water-people|water elves]] steal [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection, they take not merely her ability to see her face but her relationship with her own identity.

## Overview

The reflection is the story's central magical object. [[lamorna|Lamorna]] "does nothing but look at her own reflection, and loves nothing so much as her own pretty face" (001-017). The [[the-water-people|water elves]] conclude that "while she can see her own face, she will continue to be [[vanity|vain]] and foolish" (001-020), and so they steal it. The reflection's absence forces [[lamorna|Lamorna]] to look outward — at [[erick|Erick]], at others, at the world beyond her own image — and its restoration marks her moral cure.

The reflection motif extends beyond "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]." In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," the court's acceptance of a mechanical doll as their princess is a kind of reflection-failure — they see only surface, never substance. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] treats the gap between image and reality as one of her deepest themes, and the reflection is its most concentrated symbol.

## Metaphysics of Reflection

The old elf provides the tale's theory of reflection: "people have more than one reflection. It is a common mistake to suppose so, but in reality there is only one reflection to each object; only, as the object moves before a glass, the reflection moves too, so that all sides of it are shown" (001-030). This is both folk physics and moral philosophy — the self is singular, and what you see in the mirror is the whole of it. The elf's patient correction of a younger elf's ignorance ("he was never angry with ignorance in the young") models the pedagogy that [[lamorna|Lamorna]] herself will later receive.

> "Do not suppose, my young friend," he said, blandly, "that people have more than one reflection. It is a common mistake to suppose so, but in reality there is only one reflection to each object; only, as the object moves before a glass, the reflection moves too, so that all sides of it are shown."
>
> (001-030)

## In the Narrative

### The Theft

On a moonlit night, the [[the-water-people|water elves]] throw sand ropes over [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s reflection as she leans over the brook. "At a given signal, the [[the-water-people|water elves]] threw up their sand ropes, and caught her reflection, and all pulled it together, and [[lamorna|Lamorna]] started back with a shudder" (001-043). The reflection is thirty times the size of any elf, requiring communal effort. They store it in a special grotto where all may look but none may touch (001-049).

### The Absence

Without her reflection, [[lamorna|Lamorna]] cannot see herself in any glass or [[the-brook|water]]. She stares into the mirror and "saw the reflection of the room — only her own image was wanting" (001-064). The loss is terrifying — a kind of self-erasure — but it is also the necessary condition for her growth. She can no longer be the center of her own world.

### The Stolen Reflection That Ages

A crucial detail: the stolen reflection ages with [[lamorna|Lamorna]]. The old elf explains that "if even a wrinkle comes on [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s face it will at once appear on the face of her image here, and should her hair become grey its hair would be grey also" (001-054). The reflection is not a frozen portrait but a [[life|living]] record. The [[the-water-people|water elves]]' custody of it is a kind of trusteeship of [[lamorna|Lamorna]]'s future self — they hold her image in trust until she is worthy of it.

### The Restoration

When [[lamorna|Lamorna]] admits her fault and accepts [[erick|Erick]]'s love, the old elf orders the reflection's release. It floats back to the surface and reunites with her — but she is no longer its prisoner. The reflection is now a true mirror: it shows her as she is, not as she [[hope|wish]]es to be.

## Beyond the Brook: Reflections Across the Collection

### The Toy Princess as False Reflection

In "[[a-toy-princess-tale|A Toy Princess]]," [[taboret|Taboret]]'s automaton is a reflection that has displaced its original. The doll is physically perfect — indistinguishable from the real [[princess-ursula|Ursula]] — but it has no interior life. The court's failure to notice the substitution is [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s sharpest comment on the emptiness of mere appearance. [[a-toy-princess-tale|The Toy Princess]] is what [[lamorna|Lamorna]] might have become: an image with no self behind it.

### The Frame Narrative's Mirror

[[the-brooch|The Brooch]], Pin, and Shawl-pin themselves reflect the reader — they are miniature versions of human [[pride|vanity]], status anxiety, and storytelling impulse. The pincushion is a mirror held up to society, shrunk to the scale of a sewing table.
