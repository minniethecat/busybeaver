# The Ice People

A race of cold-dwelling magical beings appearing in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]," who inhabit a frozen realm and pose mortal danger to travelers — counterparts to the benevolent [[the-water-people|water people]] of "[[lamorna|Vain Lamorna]]."

## Overview

The ice people represent [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s capacity for magical world-building beyond the pastoral brook-and-village setting. Where the [[the-water-people|water people]] are "kindly and well-disposed towards human beings" (001-011), the ice people are lethal: they catch travelers and freeze them to death. "Many people have caught and [[death|frozen to death]]" (005-195) in their realm. This duality — [[the-water-people|water people]] below, ice people beyond — creates a moral geography where elemental forces can be benevolent or hostile depending on their nature.

## In the Narrative

### The Frozen Realm

The ice people inhabit a realm of extreme cold beyond the normal world. Travelers must navigate through their territory, and the danger is not [[cruelty|malice]] but environment: "I should be frozen directly I got to the ice-people" (005-137). Their threat is existential rather than personal — they do not hunt humans but their realm itself is lethal.

### Narrative Function

The ice people serve as an environmental antagonist — less characters than a sentient climate. Their function is to establish the stakes of the journey in "[[through-the-fire-tale|Through the Fire]]": the Princess must cross their realm, and the danger is absolute. This contrasts with the [[the-water-people|water people]] who are individualized, named, and morally engaged — suggesting [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]] distinguishes between magical beings who are people ([[the-water-people|water elves]], with councils and festivals) and magical forces that are embodied nature (ice people, more phenomenon than personality).

> Looking about him, Jack saw that the blocks of ice were growing larger and larger as they went on, and the spaces of water less and less, until at last they disappeared altogether, and nothing could be seen but an immense plain of solid ice. The moon shone upon it brightly, and moving noiselessly over the surface were a number of almost transparent forms of men and women, with deadly white faces and cold glittering eyes. They never spoke, but moved about swiftly and silently. They fled at the sight of the fireball, but when they saw Jack some of them stopped and motioned to him to stop too.

(005-190)


## Literary Context

The ice people belong to a tradition of elemental beings in folklore — frost giants of Norse mythology, the Snow Queen's realm in Andersen, the Northern spirits of Arctic legend. [[de-morgan-author-page|De Morgan]]'s version is notably under-described compared to the vividly characterized [[the-water-people|water people]], suggesting the ice realm represents the unknown and unknowable — nature at its most inhuman.
