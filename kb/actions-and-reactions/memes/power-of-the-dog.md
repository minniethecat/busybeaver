## Definition

The **Power of the Dog** is a thematic motif in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909). It examines the relationship between humans and canines as a lens through which loyalty, power dynamics, and moral [[duty|responsibility]] are explored.

The phrase originates from Chapter 4's poem of the same name, but the motif runs throughout the collection, appearing prominently in Chapters 1, 3, and 4.

## Context

In Kipling's Edwardian [[earth|world]], the dog serves as more than a domestic animal. The recurring image of dogs functions as a moral barometer throughout the collection.

> "Nonsense! That dog's worth more than most men, Strickland." (003-025)

[[strickland|Strickland]]'s observation encapsulates Kipling's critique of human society through canine virtue. The motif operates on three levels: **loyalty** ([[garm|Garm]]'s unwavering [[love|attachment]]), **power** (the dog's submission mirroring colonial structures), and **mortality** (Chapter 4's poem confronting the grief of loss).

> "I had Vixen, who was all my dog to me; and I felt what my friend said about not touching Garm on any account." (003-064)

## Analysis

The dog motif serves as Kipling's vehicle for examining the ethics of care and [[order|authority]].

The relationship between the narrator and [[garm|Garm]] in Chapter 3 parallels the broader imperial theme of [[duty|obligation]]. When the narrator states "I give you the dog because of what you got me out of" (003-047), Kipling establishes a transactional moral economy that resonates throughout the collection.

The dog-boy's subordinate role (003-143–149) further reinforces the hierarchical structure of the colonial setting. Even among those who serve, there are gradations of status, and the dog occupies a liminal space between human society and animal nature.
