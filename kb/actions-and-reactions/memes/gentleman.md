## Definition

The **gentleman** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) is a social identity that transcends nationality and class. Kipling's gentlemen include a Brazilian planter, a Mohammedan official, and Englishmen of various stations — the common thread being not birth but conduct: restraint under pressure, loyalty to one's word, and the capacity to act without resentment.

## Context

In Edwardian [[england|England]], "gentleman" was a term in transition. Birth and [[earth|land]] remained its traditional foundations, but professional competence and personal conduct were increasingly recognised as alternative qualifications. Kipling, who came from professional rather than [[earth|land]]ed stock, was attuned to this shift.

> "He's a Brazilian gentleman — very sunburnt like." (001-578)

The description of [[sangres|Sangres]] as a "Brazilian gentleman" in Chapter 1 is striking: the category expands to include a foreign planter, suggesting that gentlemanliness is portable — a matter of bearing rather than breeding.

> "The best type of a Mohammedan gentleman can be, pater?" (009-232)

Chapter 9 goes further, locating gentlemanliness within Islamic culture. The question is asked without irony — the Mohammedan gentleman is a recognised type, and his conduct under risk ("rather risky, and it might have been...") confirms the category's validity across cultural boundaries.

## Analysis

Kipling's gentleman is defined by conduct under pressure. In Chapter 3, the dog [[garm|Garm]] is said to kill "like a gentleman" (003-274) — the phrase collapses the distinction between animal instinct and human code, suggesting that gentlemanly conduct is not a social veneer but something closer to nature. In Chapter 7, the gentleman appears in the professional context of the night mail service — competent, collegial, capable of irony ("Methinks the gentleman protests too much," 007-1250).

The Brazilian gentleman (ch01) and the Mohammedan gentleman (ch09) together make a Kiplingesque argument: the gentleman is not an English monopoly. The form varies by culture, but the substance — restraint, honour, the keeping of one's word — is recognisable across borders. This is consistent with Kipling's imperial vision, in which English values are universal values, discoverable in other cultures by those with eyes to see.
