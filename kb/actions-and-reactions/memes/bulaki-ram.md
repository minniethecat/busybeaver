## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9]]: [[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]] (009-200 through 009-414)

## Role

Bulaki Ram serves as the narrator's native clerk during [[deal-in-cotton|the cotton negotiation]]s in Chapter 9. He is an intermediary figure — working up "to a ferocious pitch" of enthusiasm for his employer's interests (009-200), yet also subject to the fear and deference that the local power dynamics demand. His knowledge of English [[law|Law]] makes him a valuable asset in the commercial dealings, and his presence at Dupé during the tense waiting period underscores his reliability.

## Characteristics

Bulaki Ram is industrious, knowledgeable, and loyal to his employer. The narrator describes working him up "to a ferocious pitch" (009-200), suggesting both his capacity for intensity and his responsiveness to direction. He understands English [[law|Law]] (009-399), marking him as educated and professionally valuable. However, he is not fearless — he "feared the Hajji" (009-347), indicating his place within the complex social [[order|hierarchy]]. Despite this fear, he remains at his post, waiting among the Angari at Dupé (009-414), demonstrating a steadfastness that complements his professional skills.

## Key actions

- Is worked up "to a ferocious pitch" by the narrator to support [[deal-in-cotton|the cotton negotiation]]s (009-200)
- Provides the narrator with information about the business dealings (009-290)
- Is instructed by the Hajji to occupy the compartment at Dupé until their return (009-346 through 009-347)
- Feared the Hajji, highlighting the social tensions in the narrative (009-347)
- Discusses matters with the narrator using his knowledge of English [[law|Law]] (009-399)
- Waits faithfully among the Angari at Dupé during the narrator's absence (009-414)

## Relationships

- **The Narrator**: Bulaki Ram is a trusted subordinate whose legal knowledge and diligence make him valuable to the cotton enterprise
- **[[the-hajji|The Hajji]]**: Bulaki Ram fears the Hajji (009-347), a relationship that illustrates the power dynamics at play. [[the-hajji|The Hajji]] gives him direct orders, which he follows
- **[[ibn-makarrah|Ibn Makarrah]]**: As an employee of the narrator, Bulaki Ram is implicitly involved in the [[war|conflict]] with [[ibn-makarrah|Ibn Makarrah]]'s men

## Significance

Bulaki Ram represents the educated native intermediary — the clerk who bridges British commercial interests and local realities. His knowledge of English [[law|Law]] (009-399) marks him as a product of colonial education, while his fear of the Hajji (009-347) places him within indigenous social hierarchies. Kipling uses him to illustrate the layered nature of colonial society, where professional competence coexists with personal vulnerability. His steadfast waiting at Dupé is a quiet testament to the loyalty that underpins the narrator's enterprises.
