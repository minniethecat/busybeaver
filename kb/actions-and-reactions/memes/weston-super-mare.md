## Location

Weston-super-Mare is a real seaside town on the Bristol Channel in Somerset, [[england|England]]. A popular Victorian and Edwardian resort, it attracted retired colonial officials and military officers. Kipling places [[strickland|Strickland]] here deliberately — it is neither [[london|London]] nor the countryside, but a respectable, slightly peripheral place suited to a man whose active life is behind him.

## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9|Chapter 9: A Deal in Cotton]] — mentioned as [[strickland|Strickland]]'s retirement home (009-004)

## Description

Kipling's Weston-super-Mare is sketched in a single sentence: [[strickland|Strickland]] "lives now at a place in [[england|England]] called Weston-super-Mare, where his wife plays the organ in one of the churches" (009-004). The detail of the church organ situates the [[strickland|Strickland]]s in respectable, quiet retirement — a life of golf, harriers, and occasional trips to [[london|London]] (009-007) that contrasts sharply with the action and [[order|authority]] of his [[punjab-police|Punjab Police]] career.

## Significance

Weston-super-Mare represents the terminus of imperial service — the English town where careers end and memories are stored. Its ordinariness underscores the distance between the drama of colonial life and the quiet of retirement. The contrast between [[strickland|Strickland]]'s Indian past and his English present is mirrored in the contrast between his son Adam's active African career and his own settled domesticity.
