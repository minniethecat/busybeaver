## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9|Chapter 9: A Deal in Cotton]] — recurring presence, assisting [[strickland|Strickland]] and the narrator throughout the cotton scheme (009-055 through 009-484)

## Role

Imam Din is the personal servant of [[strickland|Strickland]] in "[[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]]." He functions as more than a domestic aide — he is an active participant in [[strickland|Strickland]]'s cotton speculation scheme, serving as intermediary, intelligence gatherer, and caretaker. When the narrator falls gravely ill with fever, Imam Din nurses him through the crisis alongside [[strickland|Strickland]] (009-220).

## Characteristics

Imam Din is quiet, capable, and deeply loyal. He speaks sparingly but with [[order|authority]] when he does — his voice emerges from the shadows at key moments (009-115). He possesses a dry wit, describing the sale of man-eaters with deadpan humor (009-274). His competence extends from domestic duties to the delicate work of massaging a paralyzed patient back to health (009-224).

## Key actions

- Attends to [[stalky|Stalky]] upon his arrival and inquires about his uniform (009-055)
- Retires into the shadows and re-emerges when needed, demonstrating unobtrusive vigilance (009-088)
- Assists in emptying a corpse from a log for the cotton scheme's staging (009-220)
- Massages the narrator's paralysis for a week during his fever recovery (009-224)
- Makes talk with the Makalali police as part of the cotton scheme (009-265)
- Confirms the arrival of Hajji on the night before the scheme's climax (009-484)

## Relationships

- **[[Strickland]]**: His master, whom he serves with absolute loyalty
- **[[Stalky]]**: Treats him with the respect due to [[strickland|Strickland]]'s associates
- **The narrator**: Nurses him through a life-threatening fever

## Significance

Imam Din exemplifies Kipling's portrayal of the loyal native servant — a figure who appears throughout his Indian fiction. Yet Imam Din is not merely passive: he is an active agent in the cotton scheme, trusted with critical tasks. His quiet competence and unwavering loyalty form the operational backbone that allows the British characters to execute their plans. The narrator describes him as a wonderful man, equal in marvel to Hajji himself (009-245).
