[007-001]A STORY OF 2000 A. D.

[007-002](\_Together with extracts from the magazine in which it appeared\_)

[007-003]A nine o'clock of a gusty winter night I stood on the lower stages of

[007-004]one of the G.P.O. outward mail towers. My purpose was a run to Quebec

[007-005]in “Postal Packet 162 or such other as may be appointed”; and the

[007-006]Postmaster-General himself countersigned the order. This talisman opened

[007-007]all doors, even those in the despatching-caisson at the foot of the

[007-008]tower, where they were delivering the sorted Continental mail. The bags

[007-009]lay packed close as herrings in the long grey underbodies which our

[007-010]G.P.O. still calls “coaches.” Five such coaches were filled as I

[007-011]watched, and were shot up the guides to be locked on to their waiting

[007-012]packets three hundred feet nearer the stars.

[007-013]From the despatching-caisson I was conducted by a courteous and

[007-014]wonderfully learned official Mr. L.L. Geary, Second Despatcher of

[007-015]the Western Route--to the Captains' Room (this wakes an echo of old

[007-016]romance), where the mail captains come on for their turn of duty. He

[007-017]introduces me to the captain of “162”--Captain Purnall, and his relief,

[007-018]Captain Hodgson. The one is small and dark; the other large and red;

[007-019]but each has the brooding sheathed glance characteristic of eagles and

[007-020]aeronauts. You can see it in the pictures of our racing professionals,

[007-021]from L.V. Rautsch to little Ada Warrleigh--that fathomless abstraction

[007-022]of eyes habitually turned through naked space.

[007-023]On the notice-board in the Captains' Room, the pulsing arrows of some

[007-024]twenty indicators register, degree by geographical degree, the progress

[007-025]of as many homeward-bound packets. The word “Cape” rises across the face

[007-026]of a dial; a gong strikes: the South African mid-weekly mail is in at

[007-027]the Highgate Receiving Towers. That is all. It reminds one comically of

[007-028]the traitorous little bell which in pigeon-fanciers', lofts notifies the

[007-029]return of a homer.

[007-030]“Time for us to be on the move,” says Captain Purnall, and we are shot

[007-031]up by the passenger-lift to the top of the despatch-towers. “Our coach

[007-032]will lock on when it is filled and the clerks are aboard.”

[007-033]“No. 162” waits for us in Slip E of the topmost stage. The great curve

[007-034]of her back shines frostily under the lights, and some minute alteration

[007-035]of trim makes her rock a little in her holding-down slips.

[007-036]Captain Purnall frowns and dives inside. Hissing softly, “162” comes to

[007-037]rest as level as a rule. From her North Atlantic Winter nose-cap (worn

[007-038]bright as diamond with boring through uncounted leagues of hail, snow,

[007-039]and ice) to the inset of her three built out propeller-shafts is some

[007-040]two hundred and forty feet. Her extreme diameter, carried well forward,

[007-041]is thirty-seven. Contrast this with the nine hundred by ninety-five of

[007-042]any crack liner, and you will realize the power that must drive a hull

[007-043]through all weathers at more than the emergency speed of the Cyclonic!

[007-044]The eye detects no joint in her skin plating save the sweeping

[007-045]hair-crack of the bow-rudder--Magniac's rudder that assured us the

[007-046]dominion of the unstable air and left its inventor penniless and

[007-047]half-blind. It is calculated to Castelli's “gullwing” curve. Raise a few

[007-048]feet of that all but invisible plate three-eighths of an inch and she

[007-049]will yaw five miles to port or starboard ere she is under control again.

[007-050]Give her full helm and she returns on her track like a whip-lash. Cant

[007-051]the whole forward--a touch on the wheel will suffice--and she sweeps

[007-052]at your good direction up or down. Open the complete circle and she

[007-053]presents to the air a mushroom-head that will bring her up all standing

[007-054]within a half mile.

[007-055]“Yes,” says Captain Hodgson, answering my thought, “Castelli thought

[007-056]he'd discovered the secret of controlling aeroplanes when he'd only

[007-057]found out how to steer dirigible balloons. Magniac invented his rudder

[007-058]to help war-boats ram each other; and war went out of fashion and

[007-059]Magniac he went out of his mind because he said he couldn't serve his

[007-060]country any more. I wonder if any of us ever know what we're really

[007-061]doing.”

[007-062]“If you want to see the coach locked you'd better go aboard. It's due

[007-063]now,” says Mr. Geary. I enter through the door amidships. There is

[007-064]nothing here for display. The inner skin of the gas-tanks comes down to

[007-065]within a foot or two of my head and turns over just short of the turn of

[007-066]the bilges. Liners and yachts disguise their tanks with decoration,

[007-067]but the G.P.O. serves them raw under a lick of grey official paint. The

[007-068]inner skin shuts off fifty feet of the bow and as much of the stern,

[007-069]but the bow-bulkhead is recessed for the lift-shunting apparatus as

[007-070]the stern is pierced for the shaft-tunnels. The engine-room lies almost

[007-071]amidships. Forward of it, extending to the turn of the bow tanks, is an

[007-072]aperture--a bottomless hatch at present--into which our coach will

[007-073]be locked. One looks down over the coamings three hundred feet to

[007-074]the despatching-caisson whence voices boom upward. The light below is

[007-075]obscured to a sound of thunder, as our coach rises on its guides. It

[007-076]enlarges rapidly from a postage-stamp to a playing-card; to a punt and

[007-077]last a pontoon. The two clerks, its crew, do not even look up as it

[007-078]comes into place. The Quebec letters fly under their fingers and leap

[007-079]into the docketed racks, while both captains and Mr. Geary satisfy them

[007-080]selves that the coach is locked home. A clerk passes the way-bill over

[007-081]the hatch coaming. Captain Purnall thumb-marks and passes it to Mr.

[007-082]Geary. Receipt has been given and taken. “Pleasant run,” says Mr. Geary,

[007-083]and disappears through the door which a foot high pneumatic compressor

[007-084]locks after him.

[007-085]“A-ah!” sighs the compressor released. Our holding-down clips part with

[007-086]a tang. We are clear.

[007-087]Captain Hodgson opens the great colloid underbody porthole through which

[007-088]I watch over-lighted London slide eastward as the gale gets hold of

[007-089]us. The first of the low winter clouds cuts off the well-known view and

[007-090]darkens Middlesex. On the south edge of it I can see a postal packet's

[007-091]light ploughing through the white fleece. For an instant she gleams like

[007-092]a star ere she drops toward the Highgate Receiving Towers. “The Bombay

[007-093]Mail,” says Captain Hodgson, and looks at his watch. “She's forty

[007-094]minutes late.”

[007-095]“What's our level?” I ask.

[007-096]“Four thousand. Aren't you coming up on the bridge?”

[007-097]The bridge (let us ever praise the G.P.O. as a repository of ancientest

[007-098]tradition!) is represented by a view of Captain Hodgson's legs where he

[007-099]stands on the Control Platform that runs thwart-ships overhead. The bow

[007-100]colloid is unshuttered and Captain Purnall, one hand on the wheel,

[007-101]is feeling for a fair slant. The dial shows 4300 feet. “It's steep

[007-102]to-night,” he mutters, as tier on tier of cloud drops under. “We

[007-103]generally pick up an easterly draught below three thousand at this time

[007-104]o' the year. I hate slathering through fluff.”

[007-105]“So does Van Cutsem. Look at him huntin' for a slant!” says Captain

[007-106]Hodgson. A foglight breaks cloud a hundred fathoms below. The Antwerp

[007-107]Night Mail makes her signal and rises between two racing clouds far to

[007-108]port, her flanks blood-red in the glare of Sheerness Double Light.

[007-109]The gale will have us over the North Sea in half-an-hour, but Captain

[007-110]Purnall lets her go composedly--nosing to every point of the compass as

[007-111]she rises.

[007-112]“Five thousand-six, six thousand eight hundred”--the dip-dial reads ere

[007-113]we find the easterly drift, heralded by a flurry of snow at the thousand

[007-114]fathom level. Captain Purnall rings up the engines and keys down the

[007-115]governor on the switch before him. There is no sense in urging machinery

[007-116]when Eolus himself gives you good knots for nothing. We are away in

[007-117]earnest now--our nose notched home on our chosen star. At this level the

[007-118]lower clouds are laid out, all neatly combed by the dry fingers of the

[007-119]East. Below that again is the strong westerly blow through which we

[007-120]rose. Overhead, a film of southerly drifting mist draws a theatrical

[007-121]gauze across the firmament. The moonlight turns the lower strata to

[007-122]silver without a stain except where our shadow underruns us. Bristol and

[007-123]Cardiff Double Lights (those statelily inclined beams over Severnmouth)

[007-124]are dead ahead of us; for we keep the Southern Winter Route. Coventry

[007-125]Central, the pivot of the English system, stabs upward once in ten

[007-126]seconds its spear of diamond light to the north; and a point or two off

[007-127]our starboard bow The Leek, the great cloud-breaker of Saint David's

[007-128]Head, swings its unmistakable green beam twenty-five degrees each way.

[007-129]There must be half a mile of fluff over it in this weather, but it does

[007-130]not affect The Leek.

[007-131]“Our planet's over-lighted if anything,” says Captain Purnall at the

[007-132]wheel, as Cardiff-Bristol slides under. “I remember the old days of

[007-133]common white verticals that 'ud show two or three hundred feet up in a

[007-134]mist, if you knew where to look for 'em. In really fluffy weather they

[007-135]might as well have been under your hat. One could get lost coming home

[007-136]then, an' have some fun. Now, it's like driving down Piccadilly.”

[007-137]He points to the pillars of light where the cloud-breakers bore through

[007-138]the cloud-floor. We see nothing of England's outlines: only a white

[007-139]pavement pierced in all directions by these manholes of variously

[007-140]coloured fire--Holy Island's white and red--St. Bee's interrupted white,

[007-141]and so on as far as the eye can reach. Blessed be Sargent, Ahrens,

[007-142]and the Dubois brothers, who invented the cloud-breakers of the world

[007-143]whereby we travel in security!

[007-144]“Are you going to lift for The Shamrock?” asks Captain Hodgson. Cork

[007-145]Light (green, fixed) enlarges as we rush to it. Captain Purnall

[007-146]nods. There is heavy traffic hereabouts--the cloud-bank beneath us is

[007-147]streaked with running fissures of flame where the Atlantic boats are

[007-148]hurrying Londonward just clear of the fluff. Mail-packets are supposed,

[007-149]under the Conference rules, to have the five-thousand-foot lanes to

[007-150]themselves, but the foreigner in a hurry is apt to take liberties with

[007-151]English air. “No. 162” lifts to a long-drawn wail of the breeze in the

[007-152]fore-flange of the rudder and we make Valencia (white, green, white) at

[007-153]a safe 7000 feet, dipping our beam to an incoming Washington packet.

[007-154]There is no cloud on the Atlantic, and faint streaks of cream round

[007-155]Dingle Bay show where the driven seas hammer the coast. A big S.A.T.A.

[007-156]liner (Société Anonyme des Transports Aëriens) is diving and lifting

[007-157]half a mile below us in search of some break in the solid west wind.

[007-158]Lower still lies a disabled Dane she is telling the liner all about it

[007-159]in International. Our General Communication dial has caught her talk and

[007-160]begins to eavesdrop. Captain Hodgson makes a motion to shut it off but

[007-161]checks himself. “Perhaps you'd like to listen,” he says.

[007-162]“Argol of St. Thomas,” the Dane whimpers. “Report owners three starboard

[007-163]shaft collar-bearings fused. Can make Flores as we are, but impossible

[007-164]further. Shall we buy spares at Fayal?”

[007-165]The liner acknowledges and recommends inverting the bearings. The Argol

[007-166]answers that she has already done so without effect, and begins to

[007-167]relieve her mind about cheap German enamels for collar-bearings. The

[007-168]Frenchman assents cordially, cries “Courage, mon ami,” and switches off.

[007-169]Then lights sink under the curve of the ocean.

[007-170]“That's one of Lundt & Bleamers' boats,” says Captain Hodgson. “Serves

[007-171]'em right for putting German compos in their thrust-blocks. She won't

[007-172]be in Fayal to-night! By the way, wouldn't you like to look round the

[007-173]engine-room?”

[007-174]I have been waiting eagerly for this invitation and I follow Captain

[007-175]Hodgson from the control-platform, stooping low to avoid the bulge

[007-176]of the tanks. We know that Fleury's gas can lift anything, as the

[007-177]world-famous trials of '89 showed, but its almost indefinite powers

[007-178]of expansion necessitate vast tank room. Even in this thin air the

[007-179]lift-shunts are busy taking out one-third of its normal lift, and still

[007-180]“162” must be checked by an occasional downdraw of the rudder or our

[007-181]flight would become a climb to the stars. Captain Purnall prefers an

[007-182]overlifted to an underlifted ship; but no two captains trim ship alike.

[007-183]“When I take the bridge,” says Captain Hodgson, “you'll see me shunt

[007-184]forty per cent of the lift out of the gas and run her on the upper

[007-185]rudder. With a swoop upward instead of a swoop downward, as you say.

[007-186]Either way will do. It's only habit. Watch our dip-dial! Tim fetches her

[007-187]down once every thirty knots as regularly as breathing.”

[007-188]So is it shown on the dip-dial. For five or six minutes the arrow creeps

[007-189]from 6700 to 7300. There is the faint “szgee” of the rudder, and back

[007-190]slides the arrow to 6000 on a falling slant of ten or fifteen knots.

[007-191]“In heavy weather you jockey her with the screws as well,” says Captain

[007-192]Hodgson, and, unclipping the jointed bar which divides the engine-room

[007-193]from the bare deck, he leads me on to the floor. Here we find Fleury's

[007-194]Paradox of the Bulk-headed Vacuum--which we accept now without

[007-195]thought--literally in full blast. The three engines are H.T.&T.

[007-196]assisted-vacuo Fleury turbines running from 3000 to the Limit--that is

[007-197]to say, up to the point when the blades make the air “bell”--cut out a

[007-198]vacuum for themselves precisely as over-driven marine propellers used

[007-199]to do. “162's” Limit is low on account of the small size of her nine

[007-200]screws, which, though handier than the old colloid Thelussons, “bell”

[007-201]sooner. The midships engine, generally used as a reinforce, is not

[007-202]running; so the port and starboard turbine vacuum-chambers draw direct

[007-203]into the return-mains.

[007-204]The turbines whistle reflectively. From the low-arched expansion-tanks

[007-205]on either side the valves descend pillarwise to the turbine-chests,

[007-206]and thence the obedient gas whirls through the spirals of blades with a

[007-207]force that would whip the teeth out of a power saw. Behind, is its own

[007-208]pressure held in leash of spurred on by the lift-shunts; before it,

[007-209]the vacuum where Fleury's Ray dances in violet-green bands and whirled

[007-210]turbillons of flame. The jointed U-tubes of the vacuum-chamber are

[007-211]pressure-tempered colloid (no glass would endure the strain for an

[007-212]instant) and a junior engineer with tinted spectacles watches the Ray

[007-213]intently. It is the very heart of the machine--a mystery to this day.

[007-214]Even Fleury who begat it and, unlike Magniac, died a multi-millionaire,

[007-215]could not explain how the restless little imp shuddering in the U-tube

[007-216]can, in the fractional fraction of a second, strike the furious blast

[007-217]of gas into a chill greyish-green liquid that drains (you can hear it

[007-218]trickle) from the far end of the vacuum through the eduction-pipes and

[007-219]the mains back to the bilges. Here it returns to its gaseous, one had

[007-220]almost written sagacious, state and climbs to work afresh. Bilge-tank,

[007-221]upper tank, dorsal-tank, expansion-chamber, vacuum, main-return (as a

[007-222]liquid), and bilge-tank once more is the ordained cycle. Fleury's

[007-223]Ray sees to that; and the engineer with the tinted spectacles sees to

[007-224]Fleury's Ray. If a speck of oil, if even the natural grease of the human

[007-225]finger touch the hooded terminals, Fleury's Ray will wink and disappear

[007-226]and must be laboriously built up again. This means half a day's work for

[007-227]all hands and an expense of, one hundred and seventy-odd pounds to the

[007-228]G.P.O. for radium-salts and such trifles.

[007-229]“Now look at our thrust-collars. You won't find much German compo there.

[007-230]Full-jewelled, you see,” says Captain Hodgson as the engineer shunts

[007-231]open the top of a cap. Our shaft-bearings are C.M.C. (Commercial

[007-232]Minerals Company) stones, ground with as much care as the lens of a

[007-233]telescope. They cost £37 apiece. So far we have not arrived at their

[007-234]term of life. These bearings came from “No. 97,” which took them over

[007-235]from the old Dominion of Light which had them out of the wreck of the

[007-236]Persew aeroplane in the years when men still flew wooden kites over oil

[007-237]engines!

[007-238]They are a shining reproof to all low-grade German “ruby” enamels,

[007-239]so-called “boort” facings, and the dangerous and unsatisfactory alumina

[007-240]compounds which please dividend-hunting owners and turn skippers crazy.

[007-241]The rudder-gear and the gas lift-shunt, seated side by side under the

[007-242]engine-room dials, are the only machines in visible motion. The former

[007-243]sighs from time to time as the oil plunger rises and falls half an inch.

[007-244]The latter, cased and guarded like the U-tube aft, exhibits another

[007-245]Fleury Ray, but inverted and more green than violet. Its function is

[007-246]to shunt the lift out of the gas, and this it will do without watching.

[007-247]That is all! A tiny pump-rod wheezing and whining to itself beside a

[007-248]sputtering green lamp. A hundred and fifty feet aft down the flat-topped

[007-249]tunnel of the tanks a violet light, restless and irresolute. Between the

[007-250]two, three white-painted turbine-trunks, like eel-baskets laid on their

[007-251]side, accentuate the empty perspectives. You can hear the trickle of the

[007-252]liquefied gas flowing from the vacuum into the bilge-tanks and the soft

[007-253]gluck-glock of gaslocks closing as Captain Purnall brings “162” down by

[007-254]the head. The hum of the turbines and the boom of the air on our skin is

[007-255]no more than a cotton-wool wrapping to the universal stillness. And we

[007-256]are running an eighteen-second mile.

[007-257]I peer from the fore end of the engine-room over the hatch-coamings

[007-258]into the coach. The mail-clerks are sorting the Winnipeg, Calgary, and

[007-259]Medicine Hat bags; but there is a pack of cards ready on the table.

[007-260]Suddenly a bell thrills; the engineers run to the turbine-valves and

[007-261]stand by; but the spectacled slave of the Ray in the U-tube never

[007-262]lifts his head. He must watch where he is. We are hard-braked and going

[007-263]astern; there is language from the Control Platform.

[007-264]“Tim's sparking badly about something,” says the unruffled Captain

[007-265]Hodgson. “Let's look.”

[007-266]Captain Purnall is not the suave man we left half an hour since, but

[007-267]the embodied authority of the G.P.O. Ahead of us floats an ancient,

[007-268]aluminum-patched, twin-screw tramp of the dingiest, with no more right

[007-269]to the 5000-foot lane than has a horse-cart to a modern road. She

[007-270]carries an obsolete “barbette” conning tower--a six-foot affair with

[007-271]railed platform forward--and our warning beam plays on the top of it

[007-272]as a policeman's lantern flashes on the area sneak. Like a sneak-thief,

[007-273]too, emerges a shock-headed navigator in his shirt-sleeves. Captain

[007-274]Purnall wrenches open the colloid to talk with him man to man. There are

[007-275]times when Science does not satisfy.

[007-276]“What under the stars are you doing here, you sky-scraping

[007-277]chimney-sweep?” he shouts as we two drift side by side. “Do you know

[007-278]this is a Mail-lane? You call yourself a sailor, sir? You ain't fit to

[007-279]peddle toy balloons to an Esquimaux. Your name and number! Report and

[007-280]get down, and be--!”

[007-281]“I've been blown up once,” the shock-headed man cries, hoarsely, as a

[007-282]dog barking. “I don't care two flips of a contact for anything you can

[007-283]do, Postey.”

[007-284]“Don't you, sir? But I'll make you care. I'll have you towed stern first

[007-285]to Disko and broke up. You can't recover insurance if you're broke for

[007-286]obstruction. Do you understand that?”

[007-287]Then the stranger bellows: “Look at my propellers! There's been a

[007-288]wulli-wa down below that has knocked us into umbrella-frames! We've

[007-289]been blown up about forty thousand feet! We're all one conjuror's watch

[007-290]inside! My mate's arm's broke; my engineer's head's cut open; my Ray

[007-291]went out when the engines smashed; and... and... for pity's sake give

[007-292]me my height, Captain! We doubt we're dropping.”

[007-293]“Six thousand eight hundred. Can you hold it?” Captain Purnall overlooks

[007-294]all insults, and leans half out of the colloid, staring and snuffing.

[007-295]The stranger leaks pungently.

[007-296]“We ought to blow into St. John's with luck. We're trying to plug the

[007-297]fore-tank now, but she's simply whistling it away,” her captain wails.

[007-298]“She's sinking like a log,” says Captain Purnall in an undertone. “Call

[007-299]up the Banks Mark Boat, George.” Our dip-dial shows that we, keeping

[007-300]abreast the tramp, have dropped five hundred feet the last few minutes.

[007-301]Captain Purnall presses a switch and our signal beam begins to swing

[007-302]through the night, twizzling spokes of light across infinity.

[007-303]“That'll fetch something,” he says, while Captain Hodgson watches the

[007-304]General Communicator. He has called up the North Banks Mark Boat, a few

[007-305]hundred miles west, and is reporting the case.

[007-306]“I'll stand by you,” Captain Purnall roars to the lone figure on the

[007-307]conning-tower.

[007-308]“Is it as bad as that?” comes the answer. “She isn't insured. She's

[007-309]mine.”

[007-310]“Might have guessed as much,” mutters Hodgson. “Owner's risk is the

[007-311]worst risk of all!”

[007-312]“Can't I fetch St. John's--not even with this breeze?” the voice

[007-313]quavers.

[007-314]“Stand by to abandon ship. Haven't you any lift in you, fore or aft?”

[007-315]“Nothing but the midship tanks, and they're none too tight. You see, my

[007-316]Ray gave out and--” he coughs in the reek of the escaping gas.

[007-317]“You poor devil!” This does not reach our friend. “What does the Mark

[007-318]Boat say, George?”

[007-319]“Wants to know if there's any danger to traffic. Says she's in a bit of

[007-320]weather herself, and can't quit station. I've turned in a General Call,

[007-321]so even if they don't see our beam some one's bound to help--or else we

[007-322]must. Shall I clear our slings? Hold on! Here we are! A Planet liner,

[007-323]too! She'll be up in a tick!”

[007-324]“Tell her to have her slings ready,” cries his brother captain. “There

[007-325]won't be much time to spare... Tie up your mate,” he roars to the

[007-326]tramp.

[007-327]“My mate's all right. It's my engineer. He's gone crazy.”

[007-328]“Shunt the lift out of him with a spanner. Hurry!”

[007-329]“But I can make St. John's if you'll stand by.”

[007-330]“You'll make the deep, wet Atlantic in twenty minutes. You're less than

[007-331]fifty-eight hundred now. Get your papers.”

[007-332]A Planet liner, east bound, heaves up in a superb spiral and takes

[007-333]the air of us humming. Her underbody colloid is open land her

[007-334]transporter-slings hang down like tentacles. We shut off our beam as she

[007-335]adjusts herself--steering to a hair--over the tramp's conning-tower.

[007-336]The mate comes up, his arm strapped to his side, and stumbles into the

[007-337]cradle. A man with a ghastly scarlet head follows, shouting that he must

[007-338]go back and build up his Ray. The mate assures him that he will find

[007-339]a nice new Ray all ready in the liner's engine-room. The bandaged head

[007-340]goes up wagging excitedly. A youth and a woman follow. The liner cheers

[007-341]hollowly above us, and we see the passengers' faces at the saloon

[007-342]colloid.

[007-343]“That's a pretty girl. What's the fool waiting for now?” says Captain

[007-344]Purnall.

[007-345]The skipper comes up, still appealing to us to stand by and see him

[007-346]fetch St. John's. He dives below and returns--at which we little human

[007-347]beings in the void cheer louder than ever--with the ship's kitten.

[007-967]The Life of Xavier Lavalle

[007-968](Reviewed by Rene Talland. École Aëronautique, Paris)

[007-969]Ten years ago Lavalle, “that imperturbable dreamer of the heavens,”

[007-970]as Lazareff hailed him, gathered together the fruits of a lifetime's

[007-971]labour, and gave it, with well-justified contempt, to a world bound

[007-972]hand and foot to Barald's Theory of Vertices and “compensating electric

[007-973]nodes.” “They shall see,” he wrote--in that immortal postscript to The

[007-974]Heart of the Cyclone--“the Laws whose existence they derided written in

[007-975]fire beneath them.”

[007-976]“But even here,” he continues, “there is no finality. Better a thousand

[007-977]times my conclusions should be discredited than that my dead name should

[007-978]lie across the threshold of the temple of Science--a bar to further

[007-979]inquiry.”

[007-980]So died Lavalle--a prince of the Powers of the Air, and even at his

[007-981]funeral Cellier jested at “him who had gone to discover the secrets of

[007-982]the Aurora Borealis.”

[007-983]If I choose thus to be banal, it is only to remind you that Céllier's

[007-984]theories are today as exploded as the ludicrous deductions of the

[007-985]Spanish school. In the place of their fugitive and warring dreams we

[007-986]have, definitely, Lavalle's Law of the Cyclone which he surprised in

[007-987]darkness and cold at the foot of the overarching throne of the Aurora

[007-988]Borealis. It is there that I, intent on my own investigations, have

[007-989]passed and re-passed a hundred times the worn leonine face, white as the

[007-990]snow beneath him, furrowed with wrinkles like the seams and gashes upon

[007-991]the North Cape; the nervous hand, integrally a part of the mechanism of

[007-992]his flighter; and above all, the wonderful lambent eyes turned to the

[007-993]zenith.

[007-994]“Master,” I would cry as I moved respectfully beneath him, “what is it

[007-995]you seek today?” and always the answer, clear and without doubt, from

[007-996]above: “The old secret, my son!”

[007-997]The immense egotism of youth forced me on my own path, but (cry of the

[007-998]human always!) had I known--if I had known--I would many times have

[007-999]bartered my poor laurels for the privilege, such as Tinsley and Herrera

[007-1000]possess, of having aided him in his monumental researches.

[007-1001]It is to the filial piety of Victor Lavalle that we owe the two volumes

[007-1002]consecrated to the ground-life of his father, so full of the holy

[007-1003]intimacies of the domestic hearth. Once returned from the abysms of the

[007-1004]utter North to that little house upon the outskirts of Meudon, it was

[007-1005]not the philosopher, the daring observer, the man of iron energy that

[007-1006]imposed himself on his family, but a fat and even plaintive jester, a

[007-1007]farceur incarnate and kindly, the co-equal of his children, and, it must

[007-1008]be written, not seldom the comic despair of Madame Lavalle, who, as she

[007-1011]very untidy boy.” Here is her letter:

[007-1014]whom I begin to hate. Instead of anchoring,--I had set out the

[007-1015]guide-light above our roof, so he had but to descend and fasten the

[007-1016]plane--he wandered, profoundly distracted, above the town with his

[007-1022]to the window, enormously amused at the parabolic flight without reason,

[007-1023]for it is too dark to see the grapnel, of my prized tree. The Mayor

[007-1024]of Meudon, thunders at our door in the name of the Law, demanding,

[007-1025]I suppose, my husband's head. Here is the conversation through the

[007-1026]megaphone--Xavier is two hundred feet above us:

[007-1027]“'Mons. Lavalle, descend and make reparation for outrage of domicile.

[007-1028]Descend, Mons. Lavalle!'

[007-1029]“No one answers.

[007-1030]“'Xavier Lavalle, in the name of the Law, descend and submit to process

[007-1054]whom they adjudged to the last a pedant and a theorist.
