## Appears in

- [[Chapter-1|Chapter 1: An Habitation Enforced]] — mentioned as [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]]'s husband and a major local [[earth|land]]owner; the Conant family is a recurring reference point throughout the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s' settlement (001-479 through 001-1046)

## Role

Sir Walter Conant, Baronet, is a large [[earth|land]]holder in the neighbourhood of Friars Pardon. Although he never appears directly in the story's fore[[earth|ground]] action, his presence is felt throughout as the head of the local aristocratic order. His wife, [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]], serves as the couple's public face in the narrative, but it is Sir Walter's social position — Baronet, major [[earth|land]]owner — that anchors the couple's [[order|authority]]. The Conant family tree, "faded gilt and alabaster" (001-623), is a physical emblem of the hereditary order the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s must navigate.

## Characteristics

Sir Walter is presented entirely through his position and his wife's agency. He is "a large [[earth|land]]holder in the neighbourhood" (001-479), a Baronet whose name carries weight in county society. The narrative shows him indirectly — through the Conant family tree displayed in the church (001-623), through the deference of tenants like [[mrs-cloke|Mrs. Cloke]], and through [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]]'s confident exercise of social [[order|authority]]. The fact that the Conants "bought" the old Lashmar place (001-794) marks them as relatively recent arrivals themselves within the deeper time scale of the countryside.

## Key actions

- The Conants purchased the old Lashmar property before the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s arrived; this earlier transfer of [[earth|land]] established the precedent for outsider settlement (001-794)
- His wife [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]] acts as the family's social emissary, visiting the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s and assessing their fitness for the community
- The Conant name serves as a seal of social approval — [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]]'s gift to the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s at Friars Pardon signals the community's acceptance (001-1046)

## Relationships

- **[[Lady Conant]]**: His wife, who serves as the family's visible social agent
- **[[George Chapin]]**: The new American [[earth|land]]holder whose acceptance depends partly on Conant approval
- **[[Sophie Chapin]]**: [[lady-conant|Lady Conant]] takes a particular interest in her development as lady of Friars Pardon
- **The Lashmar family**: The Conants bought the Lashmar property, literally taking the place of [[sophie-chapin|Sophie]]'s ancestors in the [[earth|land]]holding order

## Significance

Sir Walter Conant represents the established aristocracy — present more through position and name than through direct action. His significance lies precisely in this back[[earth|ground]]ed [[order|authority]]: the Conants are "His visible Providence" (001-480), the taken-for-granted framework of rural [[order|hierarchy]]. That they themselves bought [[earth|land]] from the declining Lashmars adds historical irony — the Conants are simultaneously the established order and themselves relative newcomers, a fact that foreshadows the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s' own possible integration.
