## Definition

The **ghost** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) appears in both literal and figurative forms. Chapter 15, "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]," is built around a ghost story — or rather an anti-ghost story, in which the haunting is explicitly denied to be a ghost. But the language of ghostliness pervades the collection: the "ghost of a patch of lucerne" (ch01), the "ghost" of a wax-moth (ch05), the "ghost of a little smile" (ch09). Kipling uses the word to describe things that persist past their proper end — remnants, survivals, the not-quite-dead.

## Context

The Edwardian ghost story was a flourishing genre — M.R. James published *Ghost Stories of an Antiquary* in 1904, and Kipling himself had written several classic ghost stories before *Actions and Reactions*. "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]" subverts the genre: the haunting is real, but it is explicitly not a ghost. It is depression — a psychological condition that behaves like a haunting, occupying a house as a ghost would, affecting all who enter.

> "It isn't a ghost. Our trouble is more depression than anything else." (015-015)

This denial is the story's key move. By refusing the ghost explanation while retaining all the ghost-story furniture — the affected house, the sensitive witness, the clinical investigation — Kipling relocates the supernatural into the psychological. The house is haunted by a feeling, not a [[soul|spirit]].

> "No whispering ghost remain." (016-010)

[[rabbis-song|The Rabbi's Song]], by contrast, uses "ghost" in the traditional religious sense — the unclean [[soul|spirit]] that must be cleansed. This is the ghost as sin, as residue, as that which resists divine purification.

## Analysis

Kipling's ghosts are things that should have died but haven't. The lucerne patch (001-125) that persists as a "ghost" against agricultural neglect; the wax-moth (005-015) that enters [[mother-hive|the hive]] "like a ghost" because it is already half-invisible; the smile (009-063) that is only the "ghost" of a fuller expression — in each case, the ghost is a diminished continuation, a presence at the threshold of absence.

This pattern illuminates "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]." The house is haunted not by a dead person but by a living person's depression — something that should have been contained within a single [[soul|consciousness]] but instead persists in a physical space, ghostlike. Kipling's innovation is to treat depression as a ghost: not as metaphor but as causal mechanism. The feeling occupies the house exactly as a ghost would, and the cure is not exorcism but empathy.
