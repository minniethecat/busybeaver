## Role

Strickland is a recurring Kipling character — "Strickland of the Punjab Police (who married Miss Youghal)" (009-002). He has finished his Indian Service and retired to [[england|England]], living at [[weston-super-mare|Weston-super-Mare]] where his wife plays the church organ (009-004). He represents the generation of Anglo-Indians who served the Raj and then returned to [[england|England]]. In "[[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]]," he visits friends accompanied by his son Adam.

## Characteristics

Strickland is depicted as a man of action who struggles with retirement. He "plays golf and follows the harriers for his figure's sake" (009-007) — pursuits that substitute for the active life he led in India. His occasional visits to [[london|London]] and to friends suggest a man maintaining connections to his former life. He is reserved but respected, a figure from Kipling's earlier Indian tales now seen in later life.

## Key actions

- Completes his Indian Service and retires to [[weston-super-mare|Weston-super-Mare]] (009-003)
- Visits the Infant's estate with his son Adam (009-021)
- Arranges for Adam's career in the [[centro-euro-africa-protectorate|Centro-Euro-Africa Protectorate]] (009-025)

## Relationships

- **Adam Strickland**: His son, now an Assistant-Commissioner in Africa (009-026)
- **Miss Youghal**: His wife (referenced at 009-003)
- **The Infant**: A friend and baronet who hosts him
- **Colonel [[stalky|Corkran]] ([[stalky|Stalky]])**: Fellow Anglo-Indian, friend

## Significance

Strickland bridges Kipling's Indian fiction with the English settings of *Actions and Reactions*. His retirement in [[england|England]] and his son's colonial service in Africa illustrate the generational arc of imperial service — from India to the newer African protectorates.
