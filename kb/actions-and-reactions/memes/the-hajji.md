## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9]]: [[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]] (009-225 through 009-364)

## Role

The Hajji is a key figure in the cotton-trading expedition of Chapter 9. A man who has made the pilgrimage to Mecca three times (009-225), he [[order|command]]s deep respect among the local Sheshaheli people. He serves as guide, interpreter, and mediator, helping the Sahib navigate the complex negotiations over cotton.

## Characteristics

The Hajji is portrayed as shrewd, authoritative, and deeply loyal. He "loved our Sahib with the love of a father for his son" (009-351), and his [[love|devotion]] drives much of the chapter's action. He is physically imposing — [[bulaki-ram|Bulaki Ram]] the clerk "feared the Hajji, because the Hajji had often gloatingly appraised his physical points" (009-348). His combination of [[soul|spirit]]ual [[order|authority]] (as a three-time pilgrim) and practical cunning makes him the indispensable intermediary in the [[deal-in-cotton|cotton deal]].

## Key actions

- Reveals his status as a three-time Hajji, establishing his [[soul|spirit]]ual [[order|authority]] (009-225)
- Leads the narrator to interview the Sheshaheli elders (009-294)
- Listens intently and mediates between the Sahib and the local leaders (009-319)
- Breathes on the Sahib's forehead to signal conviction and blessing (009-322)
- Produces his money-belt and the old woman who guards his [[money|wealth]] (009-329–330)
- Shows the old woman the knife as a symbolic gesture of [[order|authority]] (009-342)
- Stands watch at the gate as the [[deal-in-cotton|cotton deal]] concludes, silhouetted against the whitening breeze (009-359, 009-364)

## Relationships

- **The Sahib (narrator)**: The Hajji loves the Sahib with paternal [[love|devotion]] (009-351), serving as his most trusted local ally
- **[[Imam Din]]**: Another trusted servant who marvels at the Hajji's abilities — "was not our Hajji marvellous?" (009-245)
- **[[bulaki-ram|Bulaki Ram]]**: The native clerk who fears the Hajji's physical appraisal (009-347–348)
- **The Sheshaheli elders**: The local leaders whom the Hajji helps persuade to accept the [[deal-in-cotton|cotton deal]]

## Significance

The Hajji embodies the theme of cross-cultural trust and mediation in Kipling's colonial fiction. His triple pilgrimage grants him [[soul|spirit]]ual [[money|capital]] that translates into practical negotiating power. His paternal love for the Sahib — reciprocated with deep trust — represents an idealized form of the colonial relationship, where local wisdom and European enterprise combine to achieve what neither could alone. Among the native characters in *Actions and Reactions*, the Hajji is portrayed with notable depth and narrative sympathy.
