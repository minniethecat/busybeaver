## Definition

**Madness** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) appears in two distinct registers: as a literal affliction sent by God (in "[[little-foxes|Little Foxes]]"), and as a metaphor for the mind's vulnerability and society's reflection (in "[[rabbis-song|The Rabbi's Song]]"). It is treated not as romantic lunacy but as a concrete condition — a disease with symptoms, causes, and consequences.

## Context

The densest concentration of the word "madness" occurs in Chapter 13, "[[little-foxes|Little Foxes]]," where it refers to a specific disease affecting dogs — a canine madness that kills in the ninth month (013-495). The speaker's theological explanation — "God knows. He sent the madness" (013-499) — frames mental affliction within a providential [[earth|world]]view: madness comes from God, beyond human fault or understanding.

> "God knows. He sent the madness. It was no fault of mine." (013-499)

Earlier in the same chapter, Farag uses "madness" pejoratively to dismiss the legal titles and procedures of civil [[order|authority]] (013-476), contrasting the blood feud's own logic with what he sees as the irrationality of formal law. This usage inverts the expected relation: the law is madness, the feud is sense.

The collection closes with Chapter 16's poem "[[rabbis-song|The Rabbi's Song]]," where the rabbi contemplates a God "Whose brain reflect our madness" (016-019) — the most philosophically charged use of the term. Here madness is not merely affliction but a property of the human condition, mirrored in the divine.

## Analysis

Kipling's handling of madness in this collection is notable for its pre-Freudian directness. Madness is not psychoanalyzed but described — as divine visitation (013-499), as biological fact (013-495), and as metaphysical mirror (016-019). The progression from the dog's madness to the rabbi's cosmic reflection traces an arc from the concrete to the contemplative, without ever leaving the plain language of affliction.

The theological dimension is striking: madness is consistently attributed to God, not to personal failing or social pressure. This framing — "He sent the madness" — suggests a [[earth|world]]view in which mental affliction, like physical disease, belongs to the domain of providence rather than psychology.
