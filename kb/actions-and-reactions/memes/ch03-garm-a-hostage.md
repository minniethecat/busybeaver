[003-001]One night, a very long time ago, I drove to an Indian military

[003-002]cantonment called Mian Mir to see amateur theatricals. At the back of

[003-003]the Infantry barracks a soldier, his cap over one eye, rushed in front

[003-004]of the horses and shouted that he was a dangerous highway robber. As a

[003-005]matter of fact, he was a friend of mine, so I told him to go home before

[003-006]any one caught him; but he fell under the pole, and I heard voices of a

[003-007]military guard in search of some one.

[003-008]The driver and I coaxed him into the carriage, drove home swiftly,

[003-009]undressed him and put him to bed, where he waked next morning with

[003-010]a sore headache, very much ashamed. When his uniform was cleaned and

[003-011]dried, and he had been shaved and washed and made neat, I drove him back

[003-012]to barracks with his arm in a fine white sling, and reported that I

[003-013]had accidentally run over him. I did not tell this story to my

[003-014]friend's sergeant, who was a hostile and unbelieving person, but to his

[003-015]lieutenant, who did not know us quite so well.

[003-016]Three days later my friend came to call, and at his heels slobbered and

[003-017]fawned one of the finest bull-terriers--of the old-fashioned breed, two

[003-018]parts bull and one terrier--that I had ever set eyes on. He was pure

[003-019]white, with a fawn-coloured saddle just behind his neck, and a fawn

[003-020]diamond at the root of his thin whippy tail. I had admired him distantly

[003-021]for more than a year; and Vixen, my own fox-terrier, knew him too, but

[003-022]did not approve.

[003-023]“'E's for you,” said my friend; but he did not look as though he liked

[003-024]parting with him.

[003-025]“Nonsense! That dog's worth more than most men, Stanley,” I said.

[003-026]“'E's that and more. 'Tention!”

[003-027]The dog rose on his hind legs, and stood upright for a full minute.

[003-028]“Eyes right!”

[003-029]He sat on his haunches and turned his head sharp to the right. At a sign

[003-030]he rose and barked thrice. Then he shook hands with his right paw and

[003-031]bounded lightly to my shoulder. Here he made himself into a necktie,

[003-032]limp and lifeless, hanging down on either side of my neck. I was told to

[003-033]pick him up and throw him in the air. He fell with a howl, and held up

[003-034]one leg.

[003-035]“Part o' the trick,” said his owner. “You're going to die now. Dig

[003-036]yourself your little grave an' shut your little eye.”

[003-037]Still limping, the dog hobbled to the garden-edge, dug a hole and lay

[003-038]down in it. When told that he was cured, he jumped out, wagging his

[003-039]tail, and whining for applause. He was put through half-a-dozen other

[003-040]tricks, such as showing how he would hold a man safe (I was that man,

[003-041]and he sat down before me, his teeth bared, ready to spring), and how

[003-042]he would stop eating at the word of command. I had no more than finished

[003-043]praising him when my friend made a gesture that stopped the dog as

[003-044]though he had been shot, took a piece of blue-ruled canteen-paper from

[003-045]his helmet, handed it to me and ran away, while the dog looked after him

[003-046]and howled. I read:

[003-047]SIR--I give you the dog because of what you got me out of. He is the

[003-048]best I know, for I made him myself, and he is as good as a man. Please

[003-049]do not give him too much to eat, and please do not give him back to me,

[003-050]for I'm not going to take him, if you will keep him. So please do not

[003-051]try to give him back any more. I have kept his name back, so you can

[003-052]call him anything and he will answer, but please do not give him back.

[003-053]He can kill a man as easy as anything, but please do not give him too

[003-054]much meat. He knows more than a man.

[003-055]Vixen sympathetically joined her shrill little yap to the bull-terrier's

[003-056]despairing cry, and I was annoyed, for I knew that a man who cares for

[003-057]dogs is one thing, but a man who loves one dog is quite another. Dogs

[003-058]are at the best no more than verminous vagrants, self-scratchers, foul

[003-059]feeders, and unclean by the law of Moses and Mohammed; but a dog with

[003-060]whom one lives alone for at least six months in the year; a free thing,

[003-061]tied to you so strictly by love that without you he will not stir or

[003-062]exercise; a patient, temperate, humorous, wise soul, who knows your

[003-063]moods before you know them yourself, is not a dog under any ruling.

[003-064]I had Vixen, who was all my dog to me; and I felt what my friend must

[003-065]have felt, at tearing out his heart in this style and leaving it in

[003-066]my garden. However, the dog understood clearly enough that I was his

[003-067]master, and did not follow the soldier. As soon as he drew breath I made

[003-068]much of him, and Vixen, yelling with jealousy, flew at him. Had she been

[003-069]of his own sex, he might have cheered himself with a fight, but he only

[003-070]looked worriedly when she nipped his deep iron sides, laid his heavy

[003-071]head on my knee, and howled anew. I meant to dine at the Club that

[003-072]night; but as darkness drew in, and the dog snuffed through the empty

[003-073]house like a child trying to recover from a fit of sobbing, I felt that

[003-074]I could not leave him to suffer his first evening alone. So we fed at

[003-075]home, Vixen on one side, and the stranger-dog on the other; she watching

[003-076]his every mouthful, and saying explicitly what she thought of his table

[003-077]manners, which were much better than hers.

[003-078]It was Vixen's custom, till the weather grew hot, to sleep in my bed,

[003-079]her head on the pillow like a Christian; and when morning came I would

[003-080]always find that the little thing had braced her feet against the wall

[003-081]and pushed me to the very edge of the cot. This night she hurried to bed

[003-082]purposefully, every hair up, one eye on the stranger, who had dropped

[003-083]on a mat in a helpless, hopeless sort of way, all four feet spread out,

[003-084]sighing heavily. She settled her head on the pillow several times,

[003-085]to show her little airs and graces, and struck up her usual whiney

[003-086]sing-song before slumber. The stranger-dog softly edged toward me. I

[003-087]put out my hand and he licked it. Instantly my wrist was between Vixen's

[003-088]teeth, and her warning aaarh! said as plainly as speech, that if I took

[003-089]any further notice of the stranger she would bite.

[003-090]I caught her behind her fat neck with my left hand, shook her severely,

[003-091]and said:

[003-092]“Vixen, if you do that again you'll be put into the verandah. Now,

[003-093]remember!”

[003-094]She understood perfectly, but the minute I released her she mouthed my

[003-095]right wrist once more, and waited with her ears back and all her body

[003-096]flattened, ready to bite. The big dog's tail thumped the floor in a

[003-097]humble and peace-making way.

[003-098]I grabbed Vixen a second time, lifted her out of bed like a rabbit

[003-099](she hated that and yelled), and, as I had promised, set her out in the

[003-100]verandah with the bats and the moonlight. At this she howled. Then

[003-101]she used coarse language--not to me, but to the bull-terrier--till she

[003-102]coughed with exhaustion. Then she ran round the house trying every door.

[003-103]Then she went off to the stables and barked as though some one were

[003-104]stealing the horses, which was an old trick of hers. Last she returned,

[003-105]and her snuffing yelp said, “I'll be good! Let me in and I'll be good!”

[003-106]She was admitted and flew to her pillow. When she was quieted I

[003-107]whispered to the other dog, “You can lie on the foot of the bed.” The

[003-108]bull jumped up at once, and though I felt Vixen quiver with rage, she

[003-109]knew better than to protest. So we slept till the morning, and they had

[003-110]early breakfast with me, bite for bite, till the horse came round and

[003-111]we went for a ride. I don't think the bull had ever followed a horse

[003-112]before. He was wild with excitement, and Vixen, as usual, squealed and

[003-113]scuttered and scooted, and took charge of the procession.

[003-114]There was one corner of a village near by, which we generally passed

[003-115]with caution, because all the yellow pariah-dogs of the place gathered

[003-116]about it.

[003-117]They were half-wild, starving beasts, and though utter cowards, yet

[003-118]where nine or ten of them get together they will mob and kill and eat an

[003-119]English dog. I kept a whip with a long lash for them.

[003-120]That morning they attacked Vixen, who, perhaps of design, had moved from

[003-121]beyond my horse's shadow.

[003-122]The bull was ploughing along in the dust, fifty yards behind, rolling in

[003-123]his run, and smiling as bull-terriers will. I heard Vixen squeal; half a

[003-124]dozen of the curs closed in on her; a white streak came up behind me;

[003-125]a cloud of dust rose near Vixen, and, when it cleared, I saw one tall

[003-126]pariah with his back broken, and the bull wrenching another to earth.

[003-127]Vixen retreated to the protection of my whip, and the bull paddled back

[003-128]smiling more than ever, covered with the blood of his enemies. That

[003-129]decided me to call him “Garm of the Bloody Breast,” who was a great

[003-130]person in his time, or “Garm” for short; so, leaning forward, I told him

[003-131]what his temporary name would be. He looked up while I repeated it, and

[003-132]then raced away. I shouted “Garm!” He stopped, raced back, and came up

[003-133]to ask my will.

[003-134]Then I saw that my soldier friend was right, and that that dog knew and

[003-135]was worth more than a man. At the end of the ride I gave an order which

[003-136]Vixen knew and hated: “Go away and get washed!” I said. Garm understood

[003-137]some part of it, and Vixen interpreted the rest, and the two trotted off

[003-138]together soberly. When I went to the back verandah Vixen had been washed

[003-139]snowy-white, and was very proud of herself, but the dog-boy would not

[003-140]touch Garm on any account unless I stood by. So I waited while he was

[003-141]being scrubbed, and Garm, with the soap creaming on the top of his broad

[003-142]head, looked at me to make sure that this was what I expected him to

[003-143]endure. He knew perfectly that the dog-boy was only obeying orders.

[003-144]“Another time,” I said to the dog-boy, “you will wash the great dog with

[003-145]Vixen when I send them home.”

[003-146]“Does he know?” said the dog-boy, who understood the ways of dogs.

[003-147]“Garm,” I said, “another time you will be washed with Vixen.”

[003-148]I knew that Garm understood. Indeed, next washing-day, when Vixen as

[003-149]usual fled under my bed, Garm stared at the doubtful dog-boy in the

[003-150]verandah, stalked to the place where he had been washed last time, and

[003-151]stood rigid in the tub.

[003-152]But the long days in my office tried him sorely. We three would drive

[003-153]off in the morning at half-past eight and come home at six or later.

[003-154]Vixen knowing the routine of it, went to sleep under my table; but

[003-155]the confinement ate into Garm's soul. He generally sat on the verandah

[003-156]looking out on the Mall; and well I knew what he expected.

[003-157]Sometimes a company of soldiers would move along on their way to the

[003-158]Fort, and Garm rolled forth to inspect them; or an officer in uniform

[003-159]entered into the office, and it was pitiful to see poor Garm's welcome

[003-160]to the cloth--not the man. He would leap at him, and sniff and bark

[003-161]joyously, then run to the door and back again. One afternoon I heard

[003-162]him bay with a full throat--a thing I had never heard before--and he

[003-163]disappeared. When I drove into my garden at the end of the day a soldier

[003-164]in white uniform scrambled over the wall at the far end, and the Garm

[003-165]that met me was a joyous dog. This happened twice or thrice a week for a

[003-166]month.

[003-167]I pretended not to notice, but Garm knew and Vixen knew. He would glide

[003-168]homewards from the office about four o'clock, as though he were only

[003-169]going to look at the scenery, and this he did so quietly that but for

[003-170]Vixen I should not have noticed him. The jealous little dog under

[003-171]the table would give a sniff and a snort, just loud enough to call my

[003-172]attention to the flight. Garm might go out forty times in the day and

[003-173]Vixen would never stir, but when he slunk off to see his true master in

[003-174]my garden she told me in her own tongue. That was the one sign she made

[003-175]to prove that Garm did not altogether belong to the family. They were

[003-176]the best of friends at all times, but, Vixen explained that I was never

[003-177]to forget Garm did not love me as she loved me.

[003-178]I never expected it. The dog was not my dog—could never be my dog--and I

[003-179]knew he was as miserable as his master who tramped eight miles a day to

[003-180]see him. So it seemed to me that the sooner the two were reunited the

[003-181]better for all. One afternoon I sent Vixen home alone in the dog-cart

[003-182](Garm had gone before), and rode over to cantonments to find another

[003-183]friend of mine, who was an Irish soldier and a great friend of the dog's

[003-184]master.

[003-185]I explained the whole case, and wound up with:

[003-186]“And now Stanley's in my garden crying over his dog. Why doesn't he take

[003-187]him back? They're both unhappy.”

[003-188]“Unhappy! There's no sense in the little man any more. But 'tis his

[003-189]fit.”

[003-190]“What is his fit? He travels fifty miles a week to see the brute, and

[003-191]he pretends not to notice me when he sees me on the road; and I'm as

[003-192]unhappy as he is. Make him take the dog back.”

[003-193]“It's his penance he's set himself. I told him by way of a joke, afther

[003-194]you'd run over him so convenient that night, whin he was drunk--I said

[003-195]if he was a Catholic he'd do penance. Off he went wid that fit in his

[003-196]little head an' a dose of fever, an nothin' would suit but givin' you

[003-197]the dog as a hostage.”

[003-198]“Hostage for what? I don't want hostages from Stanley.”

[003-199]“For his good behaviour. He's keepin' straight now, the way it's no

[003-200]pleasure to associate wid him.”

[003-201]“Has he taken the pledge?”

[003-202]“If 'twas only that I need not care. Ye can take the pledge for three

[003-203]months on an' off. He sez he'll never see the dog again, an' so mark

[003-204]you, he'll keep straight for evermore. Ye know his fits? Well, this is

[003-205]wan of them. How's the dog takin' it?”

[003-206]“Like a man. He's the best dog in India. Can't you make Stanley take him

[003-207]back?”

[003-208]“I can do no more than I have done. But ye know his fits. He's just

[003-209]doin' his penance. What will he do when he goes to the Hills? The

[003-210]doctor's put him on the list.”

[003-211]It is the custom in India to send a certain number of invalids from each

[003-212]regiment up to stations in the Himalayas for the hot weather; and though

[003-213]the men ought to enjoy the cool and the comfort, they miss the society

[003-214]of the barracks down below, and do their best to come back or to avoid

[003-215]going. I felt that this move would bring matters to a head, so I left

[003-216]Terrence hopefully, though he called after me “He won't take the dog,

[003-217]sorr. You can lay your month's pay on that. Ye know his fits.”

[003-218]I never pretended to understand Private Ortheris; and so I did the next

[003-219]best thing—I left him alone.

[003-220]That summer the invalids of the regiment to which my friend belonged

[003-221]were ordered off to the Hills early, because the doctors thought

[003-222]marching in the cool of the day would do them good. Their route lay

[003-223]south to a place called Umballa, a hundred and twenty miles or more.

[003-224]Then they would turn east and march up into the hills to Kasauli or

[003-225]Dugshai or Subathoo. I dined with the officers the night before they

[003-226]left--they were marching at five in the morning. It was midnight when I

[003-227]drove into my garden, and surprised a white figure flying over the wall.

[003-228]“That man,” said my butler, “has been here since nine, making talk to

[003-229]that dog. He is quite mad.”

[003-230]“I did not tell him to go away because he has been here many times

[003-231]before, and because the dog-boy told me that if I told him to go away,

[003-232]that great dog would immediately slay me. He did not wish to speak to

[003-233]the Protector of the Poor, and he did not ask for anything to eat or

[003-234]drink.”

[003-235]“Kadir Buksh,” said I, “that was well done, for the dog would surely

[003-236]have killed thee. But I do not think the white soldier will come any

[003-237]more.”

[003-238]Garm slept ill that night and whimpered in his dreams. Once he sprang up

[003-239]with a clear, ringing bark, and I heard him wag his tail till it waked

[003-240]him and the bark died out in a howl. He had dreamed he was with his

[003-241]master again, and I nearly cried. It was all Stanley's silly fault.

[003-242]The first halt which the detachment of invalids made was some miles

[003-243]from their barracks, on the Amritsar road, and ten miles distant from my

[003-244]house. By a mere chance one of the officers drove back for another good

[003-245]dinner at the Club (cooking on the line of march is always bad), and

[003-246]there I met him. He was a particular friend of mine, and I knew that he

[003-247]knew how to love a dog properly. His pet was a big fat retriever who was

[003-248]going up to the Hills for his health, and, though it was still April,

[003-249]the round, brown brute puffed and panted in the Club verandah as though

[003-250]he would burst.

[003-251]“It's amazing,” said the officer, “what excuses these invalids of mine

[003-252]make to get back to barracks. There's a man in my company now asked me

[003-253]for leave to go back to cantonments to pay a debt he'd forgotten. I

[003-254]was so taken by the idea I let him go, and he jingled off in an ekka as

[003-255]pleased as Punch. Ten miles to pay a debt! Wonder what it was really?”

[003-256]“If you'll drive me home I think I can show you,” I said.

[003-257]So he went over to my house in his dog-cart with the retriever; and on

[003-258]the way I told him the story of Garm.

[003-259]“I was wondering where that brute had gone to. He's the best dog in the

[003-260]regiment,” said my friend. “I offered the little fellow twenty rupees

[003-261]for him a month ago. But he's a hostage, you say, for Stanley's good

[003-262]conduct. Stanley's one of the best men I have when he chooses.”

[003-263]“That's the reason why,” I said. “A second-rate man wouldn't have taken

[003-264]things to heart as he has done.”

[003-265]We drove in quietly at the far end of the garden, and crept round the

[003-266]house. There was a place close to the wall all grown about with tamarisk

[003-267]trees, where I knew Garm kept his bones. Even Vixen was not allowed to

[003-273]your friends. You takes your bones an' you eats your biscuit, an' you

[003-276]I could hear him holding Garm's nose as the dog threw it up to the

[003-277]stars.

[003-278]“You'll stay here an' be'ave, an'--an' I'll go away an' try to be'ave,

[003-279]an' I don't know 'ow to leave you. I don't know--”

[003-280]“I think this is damn silly,” said the officer, patting his foolish

[003-281]fubsy old retriever. He called to the private, who leaped to his feet,

[003-282]marched forward, and saluted.

[003-283]“You here?” said the officer, turning away his head.

[003-284]“Yes, sir, but I'm just goin' back.”

[003-285]“I shall be leaving here at eleven in my cart. You come with me. I can't

[003-288]We did not say much when we went indoors, but the officer muttered and

[003-289]pulled his retriever's ears.

[003-290]He was a disgraceful, overfed doormat of a dog; and when he waddled off

[003-291]to my cookhouse to be fed, I had a brilliant idea.

[003-292]At eleven o'clock that officer's dog was nowhere to be found, and you

[003-293]never heard such a fuss as his owner made. He called and shouted and

[003-294]grew angry, and hunted through my garden for half an hour.

[003-295]Then I said:

[003-296]“He's sure to turn up in the morning. Send a man in by rail, and I'll

[003-297]find the beast and return him.”

[003-298]“Beast?” said the officer. “I value that dog considerably more than I

[003-299]value any man I know. It's all very fine for you to talk--your dog's

[003-300]here.”

[003-301]So she was--under my feet--and, had she been missing, food and wages

[003-302]would have stopped in my house till her return. But some people grow

[003-303]fond of dogs not worth a cut of the whip. My friend had to drive away at

[003-305]“What kind of animal is Bullen Sahib's dog? Look at him!”

[003-306]I went to the boy's hut, and the fat old reprobate was lying on a mat

[003-314]Next evening who should turn up but Stanley. The officer had sent

[003-315]him back fourteen miles by rail with a note begging me to return the

[003-322]followed Stanley like a shadow. The retriever said nothing, but licked

[003-325]So that last meeting was over, and I felt as wretched as Garm, who

[003-326]moaned in his sleep all night. When we went to the office he found a

[003-327]place under the table close to Vixen, and dropped flat till it was

[003-333]Here Vixen was ever in great form. She had to attend to all the moving

[003-335]ponies; as well as to keep up her dignity when she passed low friends

[003-340]But Garm cared for none of these things. His big eyes were on the

[003-345]well-meaning idiot, but Garm did not encourage him. He would slide his

[003-346]head round the door panting, “Rats! Come along Garm!” and Garm would

[003-347]shift one forepaw over the other, and curl himself round, leaving Bob to

[003-348]whine at a most uninterested back. The office was nearly as cheerful as

[003-350]Once, and only once, did I see Garm at all contented with his

[003-351]surroundings. He had gone for an unauthorised walk with Vixen early one

[003-352]Sunday morning, and a very young and foolish artilleryman (his battery
