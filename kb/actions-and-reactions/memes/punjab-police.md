## Type

A colonial police force — a law enforcement organization operating in the Punjab province of British India, staffed by British officers and Indian personnel. The Punjab Police was one of the more prominent provincial police forces in the Raj, responsible for maintaining order in a strategically important region.

## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9|Chapter 9: A Deal in Cotton]] — referenced as [[strickland|Strickland]]'s former service (009-002)

## Description

The Punjab Police in Kipling's fiction is associated with [[strickland|Strickland]], one of his recurring characters who represents the sharp, knowledgeable Anglo-Indian police officer. Kipling wrote "some tales concerning [[strickland|Strickland of the Punjab Police]] (who married Miss Youghal)" (009-002) in his earlier Indian fiction. The force epitomizes the combination of local knowledge, cunning, and [[order|authority]] that Kipling admired in colonial administrators.

## Members

- **[[Strickland]]** — the most notable Punjab Police officer in Kipling's fiction, now retired

## Role in story

The Punjab Police functions as back[[earth|ground]] — the institution that shaped [[strickland|Strickland]]'s character and career. Its mention in "[[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]]" serves to anchor [[strickland|Strickland]]'s identity and to establish the generational arc: from [[strickland|Strickland]]'s Punjab Police service to his son Adam's career in the newer [[centro-euro-africa-protectorate|Centro-Euro-Africa Protectorate]] (009-025). The contrast between the established Punjab Police and the newer African protectorate reflects the shifting geography of British imperial administration.
