## Appears in

- [[Chapter-15]]: The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]] (015-511 through 015-780, referenced throughout)

## Role

Agnes Moultrie — "poor Aggie" — is the youngest of the three Moultrie sisters, and her death at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] is the emotional centre around which the entire mystery of Chapter 15 revolves. She fell from the window of her bedroom at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]], and for years her surviving sisters believed she had committed suicide — a belief that haunted them with religious terror, since suicide meant eternal damnation in their Evangelical faith (015-642). The narrator's investigation ultimately proves that Agnes suffered from the same "family throat" [[madness|affliction]] (015-603) that nearly killed her sister [[mary-cloke|Mary]]: in a choking spasm, she rushed to open the dangerously low window and fell accidentally. She never appears alive in the story, yet her presence — and the need to clear her name — drives every scene.

## Characteristics

Agnes is reconstructed through the memories of those who loved her. She "used to arrange the flowers" at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] (015-687), suggesting a domestic, gentle nature. She had "the family throat" (015-560) — the same physical vulnerability to choking spasms that afflicted [[mary-cloke|Mary]]. The window of her bedroom had a sill that "doesn't come much above your knees" (015-566), making an accidental fall tragically plausible. The label of suicide hung over her memory for years, a fiction born of Evangelical severity and the house's depressive influence. The truth, when it emerges, releases her family from guilt and horror.

## Key actions

- Dies falling from her bedroom window at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]], under the laburnum tree (015-586)
- Is believed by her sisters to have committed suicide — a belief they "brooded on day and night" (015-598)
- The family throat [[madness|affliction]] that caused her to rush to the window for air is revealed as the true cause of her fall (015-603)
- [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s near-identical crisis at the Hydro window provides the proof that Agnes's death was accidental (015-569)
- The narrator and Baxter lead [[mary-cloke|Mary]] to [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]], where the house's now-lifted curse confirms Agnes's innocence (015-704)

## Relationships

- **[[miss-mary|Miss Mary Moultrie]]**: Her eldest sister, whose fierce belief that Agnes committed suicide is shattered when [[mary-cloke|Mary]] herself nearly suffers the same fate. [[mary-cloke|Mary]] ultimately declares: "Aggie didn't do it!" (015-708)
- **[[miss-bessie|Miss Elizabeth Moultrie]]**: Her middle sister, who absorbed the suicide narrative from [[mary-cloke|Mary]] and wept for Agnes for years (015-648)
- **[[john-baxter|John Baxter]]**: Their cousin who "never have" believed Agnes committed suicide (015-602) and whose faith in her is vindicated
- **The Narrator**: The outsider whose investigation at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] and insistence on the physical evidence of the low window ultimately proves Agnes's innocence
- **[[mrs-mcleod|Mrs. M'Leod]]**: Who mourns "the poor Aggie" (015-780) and understands the house's tragic influence

## Significance

Agnes Moultrie is the absent presence that gives Chapter 15 its moral gravity. Her story is Kipling's vehicle for exploring how grief can curdle into guilt, how religious severity can poison the memory of the dead, and how a single misinterpreted event can imprison a family for years. The revelation that Agnes died not by her own hand but by an accident — the same accident that nearly killed [[mary-cloke|Mary]] — is the key that unlocks the house's evil: once the truth is spoken aloud, [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]]'s "dumb Thing" is exorcised, and the house returns to its natural state of "well-being and peace" (015-690). Agnes's exoneration is also [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s absolution: "I can think henceforward of Aggie without sorrow" (015-719).
