## Definition

**Spirit** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) operates in two registers that the collection deliberately blurs: the psychological ([[soul|spirit]] as attitude, morale, the "proper [[soul|spirit]]" with which one faces adversity) and the supernatural ([[soul|spirit]] as [[ghost|haunting]] presence, the discarnate [[soul|consciousness]] that pervades the house in "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]"). The collection's movement between these registers suggests that Kipling saw psychological and supernatural phenomena as adjacent, perhaps continuous.

## Context

Kipling wrote "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]" (ch15) at a time when [[soul|spirit]]ualism and psychical research were active Edwardian preoccupations. The Society for Psychical Research had been founded in 1882, and the idea that houses could retain the emotional imprint of their former inhabitants was a recognised hypothesis. Kipling treats the [[ghost|haunting]] in Chapter 15 with clinical seriousness — the house is diagnosed, not exorcised.

> "I am always there in spirit — waking or sleeping. I couldn't face it in the body." (015-626)

The speaker in Chapter 15 describes a form of psychic attendance — being present "in the [[soul|spirit]]" at the house she cannot physically enter. This is not metaphor but reported experience, and Kipling presents it without irony.

> "If you go there in the spirit the greater need." (015-628)

The response — "the greater need" — confirms the reality of [[soul|spirit]]-travel within the story's [[earth|world]]. The house's depression is a [[soul|spirit]]ual phenomenon requiring [[soul|spirit]]ual intervention.

## Analysis

Kipling's treatment of [[soul|spirit]] reveals a mind unwilling to separate the psychological from the supernatural. The "proper [[soul|spirit]]" of Chapter 1 (001-556) is an attitude — the right way to receive unwelcome news. By Chapter 15, [[soul|spirit]] has become a mode of presence — a way of being in a place without being in a body. The dog's [[soul|spirit]] in Chapter 4 (004-021) occupies the intermediate position: animal responsiveness that feels [[soul|spirit]]ual without being supernatural.

The theological register appears in Chapter 16's "Cleanse and call home thy [[soul|spirit]]" (016-013), where [[soul|spirit]] is the essential self that returns to its divine source. This completes the collection's spectrum: [[soul|spirit]] as social attitude, as animal rapport, as [[ghost|haunting]] presence, and as immortal soul.
