Iggulden appears exclusively in the first chapter, but his presence — and absence — anchors the story's central theme of rural continuity. He is both a specific character and a representative type: the English agricultural labourer bound to the [[earth|land]] across generations.

## Role

Old Iggulden is a tenant farmer on the [[rocketts-farm|Rocketts]] estate, part of the Friars Pardon [[earth|land]]holding. He is one of the first local characters the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s encounter upon arriving from America, and his practical wisdom about the [[earth|land]] makes him a touchstone of authentic English country life.

His son — referred to as "young Iggulden" — takes over the tenancy after the old man's death and continues the line. Between them, father and son embody the generational continuity that the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s are drawn into: the [[earth|land]] does not change, only the hands that work it.

> "Iggulden sat in his chair by the fire, a thistle-spud between his knees, his head nodding with age. Sophie watched the last of Iggulden's last lighted fire gradually thin and cease." (001-365–375)

## Characteristics

Old Iggulden is characterised by quiet dignity, rootedness, and taciturn practicality. His "blue smock" (001-267) marks him as a man of the old country, and his "cackled and chuckled" manner conveys warmth without sentimentality.

Kipling draws him sparingly but precisely: the thistle-spud, the fire, the nodding head. These details establish a life measured by small, steady acts of maintenance — cutting [[earth|ground]]-ash for [[sophie-chapin|Sophie]] (001-359), tending his [[earth|land]], dying as he lived, without fuss.

> "She had been less fortunate in her inquiries than Iggulden, for her Aunt Sydney's letter, among a mass of family gossip, hardly gave her a clue." (001-813)

[[sophie-chapin|Sophie]] comes to measure herself against Iggulden's standard of knowing the [[earth|land]]. His knowledge is empirical, not academic; it is the knowledge of hands and seasons, precisely what the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s lack and slowly acquire.

## Significance

Iggulden matters to the collection because he crystallises the theme that gives Chapter 1 its title — "An Habitation Enforced." The [[earth|land]] imposes [[duty|obligation]]s on those who occupy it, and Iggulden, who has always known this, serves as the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s' unwitting teacher.

His death, early in the chapter, is not a plot twist but a structural necessity: the old order passes so the new can begin, but the new is judged by how well it honours the old. When young Iggulden continues at [[rocketts-farm|Rocketts]] under the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s' ownership, the cycle is preserved.

The vicar's eulogy — "Iggulden he had had his day in my time" (001-397) — captures the man's modest completeness. He lived his day; the [[earth|land]] remembers.
