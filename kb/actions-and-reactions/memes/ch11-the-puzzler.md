[011-001]I had not seen Penfentenyou since the Middle Nineties, when he was

[011-002]Minister of Ways and Woodsides in De Thouar's first Administration. Last

[011-003]summer, though he nominally held the same portfolio, he was his Colony's

[011-004]Premier in all but name, and the idol of his own province, which is two

[011-005]and a half times the size of England. Politically, his creed was his

[011-006]growing country; and he came over to England to develop a Great Idea in

[011-007]her behalf.

[011-008]Believing that he had put it in train, I made haste to welcome him to my

[011-009]house for a week.

[011-010]That he was chased to my door by his own Agent-General in a motor; that

[011-011]they turned my study into a Cabinet Meeting which I was not invited to

[011-012]attend; that the local telegraph all but broke down beneath the strain

[011-013]of hundred word coded cables; and that I practically broke into the

[011-014]house of a stranger to get him telephonic facilities on a Sunday, are

[011-015]things I overlook. What I objected to was his ingratitude, while I thus

[011-016]tore up England to help him. So I said: “Why on earth didn't you see

[011-017]your Opposite Number in Town instead of bringing your office work here?”

[011-018]“Eh? Who?” said he, looking up from his fourth cable since lunch.

[011-019]“See the English Minister for Ways and Woodsides.”

[011-020]“I saw him,” said Penfentenyou, without enthusiasm.

[011-021]It seemed that he had called twice on the gentleman, but without an

[011-022]appointment--(“I thought if I wasn't big enough, my business was”)--and

[011-023]each time had found him engaged. A third party intervening, suggested

[011-024]that a meeting might be arranged if due notice were given.

[011-025]“Then,” said Penfentenyou, “I called at the office at ten o'clock.”

[011-026]“But they'd be in bed,” I cried.

[011-027]“One of the babies was awake. He told me that--that 'my sort of

[011-028]questions'”--he slapped the pile of cables--“were only taken between 11

[011-029]and 2 P.M. So I waited.”

[011-030]“And when you got to business?” I asked.

[011-031]He made a gesture of despair. “It was like talking to children. They'd

[011-032]never heard of it.”

[011-033]“And your Opposite Number?”

[011-034]Penfentenyou described him.

[011-035]“Hush! You mustn't talk like that!” I shuddered. “He's one of the best

[011-036]of good fellows. You should meet him socially.”

[011-037]“I've done that too,” he said. “Have you?”

[011-038]“Heaven forbid!” I cried; “but that's the proper thing to say.”

[011-039]“Oh, he said all the proper things. Only I thought as this was England

[011-040]that they'd more or less have the hang of all the--general hang-together

[011-041]of my Idea. But I had to explain it from the beginning.”

[011-042]“Ah! They'd probably mislaid the papers,” I said, and I told him

[011-043]the story of a three-million pound insurrection caused by a deputy

[011-044]Under-Secretary sitting upon a mass of green-labelled correspondence

[011-045]instead of reading it.

[011-046]“I wonder it doesn't happen every week,” he answered. “D'you mind my

[011-047]having the Agent-General to dinner again tonight? I'll wire, and he can

[011-048]motor down.”

[011-049]The Agent-General arrived two hours later, a patient and expostulating

[011-050]person, visibly torn between the pulling Devil of a rampant Colony,

[011-051]and the placid Baker of a largely uninterested England. But with

[011-052]Penfentenyou behind him he had worked; for he told us that Lord

[011-053]Lundie--the Law Lord was--the final authority on the legal and

[011-054]constitutional aspects of the Great Idea, and to him it must be

[011-055]referred.

[011-056]“Good Heavens alive!” thundered Penfentenyou. “I told you to get that

[011-057]settled last Christmas.”

[011-058]“It was the middle of the house-party season,” said the Agent-General

[011-059]mildly. “Lord Lundie's at Credence Green now--he spends his holidays

[011-060]there. It's only forty miles off.”

[011-061]“Shan't I disturb his Holiness?” said Penfentenyou heavily. “Perhaps

[011-062]'my sort of questions,”' he snorted, “mayn't be discussed except at

[011-063]midnight.”

[011-064]“Oh, don't be a child,” I said.

[011-065]“What this country needs,” said Penfentenyou, “is--” and for ten minutes

[011-066]he trumpeted rebellion.

[011-067]“What you need is to pay for your own protection,” I cut in when he

[011-068]drew breath, and I showed him a yellowish paper, supplied gratis by

[011-069]Government, which is called Schedule D. To my merciless delight he had

[011-070]never seen the thing before, and I completed my victory over him and

[011-071]all the Colonies with a Brassey's “Naval Annual” and a “Statesman's Year

[011-072]Book.”

[011-073]The Agent-General interposed with agent-generalities (but they were

[011-074]merely provocateurs) about Ties of Sentiment.

[011-075]“They be blowed!” said Penfentenyou. “What's the good of sentiment

[011-076]towards a Kindergarten?”

[011-077]“Quite so. Ties of common funk are the things that bind us together; and

[011-078]the sooner you new nations realize it the better. What you need is an

[011-079]annual invasion. Then you'd grow up.”

[011-080]“Thank you! Thank you!” said the Agent-General. “That's what I am always

[011-081]trying to tell my people.”

[011-082]“But, my dear fool,” Penfentenyou almost wept, “do you pretend that

[011-083]these banana-fingered amateurs at home are grown up?”

[011-084]“You poor, serious, pagan man,” I retorted, “if you take 'em that way,

[011-085]you'll wreck your Great Idea.”

[011-086]“Will you take him to Lord Lundie's to-morrow?” said the Agent-General

[011-087]promptly.

[011-088]“I suppose I must,” I said, “if you won't.”

[011-089]“Not me! I'm going home,” said the Agent-General, and departed. I am

[011-090]glad that I am no colony's Agent-General.

[011-091]Penfentenyou continued to argue about naval contributions till 1.15

[011-092]A.M., though I was victor from the first.

[011-093]At ten o'clock I got him and his correspondence into the motor, and

[011-094]he had the decency to ask whether he had been unpolished over-night.

[011-095]I replied that I waited an apology. This he made excuse for renewed

[011-096]arguments, and used wayside shows as illustrations of the decadence of

[011-097]England.

[011-098]For example we burst a tyre within a mile of Credence Green, and, to

[011-099]save time, walked into the beautifully kept little village. His eye was

[011-100]caught by a building of pale-blue tin, stencilled “Calvinist Chapel,”

[011-101]before whose shuttered windows an Italian organ-grinder with a

[011-102]petticoated monkey was playing “Dolly Grey.”

[011-103]“Yes. That's it!” snapped the egoist. “That's a parable of the general

[011-104]situation in England. And look at those brutes!” A huge household

[011-105]removals van was halted at a public-house. The men in charge were

[011-106]drinking beer from blue and white mugs. It seemed to me a pretty sight,

[011-107]but Penfentenyou said it represented Our National Attitude.

[011-108]Lord Lundie's summer resting-place we learned was a farm, a little out

[011-109]of the village, up a hill round which curled a high hedged road. Only

[011-110]an initiated few spend their holidays at Credence Green, and they have

[011-111]trained the householders to keep the place select. Penfentenyou made a

[011-112]grievance of this as we walked up the lane, followed at a distance by

[011-113]the organ-grinder.

[011-114]“Suppose he is having a house-party,” he said: “Anything's possible in

[011-115]this insane land.”

[011-116]Just at that minute we found ourselves opposite an empty villa. Its roof

[011-117]was of black slate, with bright unweathered ridge-tiling; its walls were

[011-118]of blood-coloured brick, cornered and banded with vermiculated stucco

[011-119]work, and there was cobalt, magenta, and purest apple-green window-glass

[011-120]on either side of the front door. The whole was fenced from the road by

[011-121]a low, brick-pillared, flint wall, topped with a cast-iron Gothic rail,

[011-122]picked out in blue and gold.

[011-123]Tight beds of geranium, calceolaria, and lobelia speckled the

[011-124]glass-plat, from whose centre rose one of the finest araucarias (its

[011-125]other name by the way is “monkey-puzzler”) that it has ever been my

[011-126]lot to see. It must have been full thirty feet high, and its foliage

[011-127]exquisitely answered the iron railings. Such bijou ne plus ultras,

[011-128]replete with all the amenities, do not, as I pointed out to

[011-129]Penfentenyou, transpire outside of England.

[011-130]A hedge, swinging sharp right, flanked the garden, and above it on

[011-131]a slope of daisy-dotted meadows we could see Lord Lundie's tiled and

[011-132]half-timbered summer farmhouse. Of a sudden we heard voices behind the

[011-133]tree--the fine full tones of the unembarrassed English, speaking to

[011-134]their equals--that tore through the hedge like sleet through rafters.

[011-135]“That it is not called 'monkey-puzzler' for nothing, I willingly

[011-136]concede”--this was a rich and rolling note--“but on the other hand--”

[011-137]“I submit, me lud, that the name implies that it might, could, would,

[011-138]or should be ascended by a monkey, and not that the ascent is a physical

[011-139]impossibility. I believe one of our South American spider monkeys

[011-140]wouldn't hesitate... By Jove, it might be worth trying, if--”

[011-141]This was a crisper voice than the first. A third, higher-pitched, and

[011-142]full of pleasant affectations, broke in.

[011-143]“Oh, practical men, there is no ape here. Why do you waste one of God's

[011-144]own days on unprofitable discussion? Give me a match!”

[011-145]“I've a good mind to make you demonstrate in your own person. Come on,

[011-146]Bubbles! We'll make Jimmy climb!”

[011-147]There was a sound of scuffling, broken by squeaks from Jimmy of the high

[011-148]voice. I turned back and drew Penfentenyou into the side of the flanking

[011-149]hedge. I remembered to have read in a society paper that Lord Lundie's

[011-150]lesser name was “Bubbles.”

[011-151]“What are they doing?” Penfentenyou said sharply. “Drunk?”

[011-152]“Just playing! Superabundant vitality of the Race, you know. We'll watch

[011-153]'em,” I answered. The noise ceased.

[011-154]“My deliver,” Jimmy gasped. “The ram caught in the thicket, and--I'm the

[011-155]only one who can talk Neapolitan! Leggo my collar!” He cried aloud in a

[011-156]foreign tongue, and was answered from the gate.

[011-157]“It's the Calvinistic organ-grinder,” I whispered. I had already found

[011-158]a practicable break at the bottom of the hedge. “They're going to try to

[011-159]make the monkey climb, I believe.”

[011-160]“Here--let me look!” Penfentenyou flung himself down, and rooted till he

[011-161]too broke a peep-hole. We lay side by side commanding the entire garden

[011-162]at ten yards' range.

[011-163]“You know 'em?” said Penfentenyou, as I made some noise or other.

[011-164]“By sight only. The big fellow in flannels is Lord Lundie; the

[011-165]light-built one with the yellow beard painted his picture at the last

[011-166]Academy: He's a swell R.A., James Loman.”

[011-167]“And the brown chap with the hands?”

[011-168]“Tomling, Sir Christopher Tomling, the South American engineer who built

[011-169]the--”

[011-171]with us.... Do you think a monkey would climb the tree?”

[011-172]The organ-grinder at the gate fenced his beast with one arm as

[011-173]Jimmy talked.

[011-174]“Don't show off your futile accomplishments,” said Lord Lundie. “Tell

[011-175]him it's an experiment. Interest him!”

[011-176]“Shut up, Bubbles. You aren't in court,” Jimmy replied. “This needs

[011-177]delicacy. Giuseppe says--”

[011-178]“Interest the monkey,” the brown engineer interrupted. “He won't climb

[011-180]and an orange or two. No need to tell our womenfolk.”

[011-181]The huge white figure lobbed off at a trot which would not have

[011-182]disgraced a boy of seventeen. I gathered from something Jimmy let fall

[011-184]“That Tomling has a head on his Shoulders,” muttered Penfentenyou. “Pity

[011-185]we didn't get him for the Colony. But the question is, will the monkey

[011-187]“Be quick, Jimmy. Tell the man we'll give him five bob for the loan of

[011-188]the beast. Now run the organ under the tree, and we'll dress it when

[011-189]Bubbles comes back,” Sir Christopher cried.

[011-190]“I've often wondered,” said Penfentenyou, “whether it would puzzle

[011-191]a monkey?” He had forgotten the needs of his Growing Nation, and was

[011-192]earnestly parting the white-thorn stems with his fingers.

[011-195]a wary and malignant eye.

[011-197]off the wheels.” He spoke volubly to the proprietor. “Oh, it's so as

[011-200]machine for love. For love, Chris! And Michael Angelo was one of 'em!”

[011-201]“Don't jaw! Tell him to take the beast's petticoat off,” said Sir

[011-202]Christopher Tomling.

[011-205]“They're all out, thank goodness!” he cried, “but I've raided what I
