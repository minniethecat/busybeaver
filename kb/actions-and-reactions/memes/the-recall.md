## Definition

The Recall is a thematic concept running through *Actions and Reactions*, most explicitly stated in the poem that opens Chapter 2: "I am the [[earth|land]] of their fathers, / In me the virtue stays; / I will bring back my children, / After certain days" (002-001). It describes [[england|England]]'s almost supernatural power to draw back those of English descent — whether from America, India, or elsewhere — and root them in ancestral [[earth|soil]]. The Recall operates not through force but through an accumulation of sensory and emotional experiences that reawaken dormant connections.

## Appears in

- [[Chapter-2|Chapter 2: The Recall]] — the poem that names and defines the concept (002-001 through 002-020)
- [[Chapter-1|Chapter 1: An Habitation Enforced]] — [[george-chapin|George Chapin]]'s experience of being drawn into English rural life

## Context

The concept reflects Edwardian anxieties about emigration, imperial diaspora, and the weakening of ties between Britain and its overseas populations. Kipling, himself born in India and returned to [[england|England]], understood the complex psychology of belonging across continents. The Recall offers a vision of organic, almost mystical [[love|attachment]] — "Under their feet in the grasses / My clinging magic runs" (002-005) — that transcends rational allegiance.

## Analysis

The Recall works gradually and through the senses: "Scent of smoke in the evening, / Smell of rain in the night" (002-013). It is not ideological but experiential, operating below conscious awareness. Those it claims "shall return as strangers, / They shall remain as sons" (002-007) — the transformation from outsider to inheritor is the core promise. In "An Habitation Enforced," this plays out literally as the American [[george-chapin|George Chapin]] finds himself unable to leave the English farm where he has recuperated.
