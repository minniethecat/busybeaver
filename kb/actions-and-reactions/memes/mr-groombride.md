## Appears in

- [[Chapter-13]]: [[little-foxes|Little Foxes]] (013-417 through 013-704)

## Role

Mr. Lethabie Groombride is a Member of Parliament who forms a Committee to investigate alleged colonial abuses in Ethiopia. Having heard a distorted tale of the "Mudir's Cranes" from the Inspector in [[england|England]], he disseminates pamphlets charging the provincial administration with cruelty (013-417). Backed by the [[government|Imperial Government]], he travels upriver with his interpreter [[abdul|Abdul]] to conduct a personal inquiry — only to become the unwitting butt of an elaborate trap laid by the Governor, the Inspector, and the kennel-huntsman Farag (013-436 through 013-704).

## Characteristics

Groombride is pompous, self-important, and utterly ignorant of the people he claims to champion. He has "large, loose lips" (013-548) and a habit of sputtering in people's faces (013-420), which the Inspector had noted years earlier in [[england|England]]. He imagines himself a righteous crusader — "If they only knew how I felt towards them in my heart!" (013-643) — yet understands nothing of the language, culture, or political realities of the Province. His vanity makes him easy to manipulate: [[abdul|Abdul]], his own interpreter, mocks his undergarments and steals his perfumes (013-582), while Farag orchestrates the entire village to treat him as an "afflicted of God."

## Key actions

- Forms a Committee in the House of Commons and disseminates pamphlets charging colonial abuse (013-417)
- Travels to Ethiopia backed by [[government|Imperial Government]] orders to conduct an inquiry (013-436)
- Demands to address the "victims" in the village, refusing official escort (013-544, 013-554)
- Delivers a carefully learned vernacular speech asking to meet a "Mudir's Crane" — unwittingly using an obscene local word provided by the Inspector (013-648 through 013-659)
- Stands bewildered as the entire village dissolves into uncontrollable laughter at his speech (013-660 through 013-676)
- Beats his interpreter [[abdul|Abdul]] with an umbrella, crying betrayal (013-677 through 013-680)
- Pleads with the Governor to hush up the affair to save his political career (013-690 through 013-697)

## Relationships

- **The Inspector (Baker)**: Having met Groombride years earlier in [[england|England]], the Inspector plants the fatal vulgar word and masterminds the scheme to discredit him. He describes Groombride as resembling "Bigglebai who ate unclean matters" (013-479)
- **[[abdul|Abdul]]**: Groombride's interpreter, a "yellowish boy" who publicly mocks his employer, steals his perfume, and ultimately collapses laughing with the villagers (013-552, 013-582, 013-675)
- **The Governor (Peter)**: Hosts Groombride under Imperial duress but orchestrates his public humiliation while ensuring his physical safety with hidden soldiers (013-527)
- **Farag**: The kennel-huntsman who prepares the village by spreading the tale of the "afflicted of God," framing Groombride's entire visit as entertainment (013-560)

## Significance

Groombride embodies Kipling's savage critique of meddling metropolitan reformers — the "[[little-foxes|Little Foxes]]" who spoil the vines from afar. He arrives armed with pamphlets and moral certainty, yet his ignorance of local realities makes him not merely ineffectual but actively destructive: his literature nearly triggers a [[earth|land]]-title crisis that would have required military intervention (013-462). Kipling uses Groombride to demonstrate that righteous ignorance backed by Imperial [[order|authority]] is more dangerous than malice, and that the provincial administrators — for all their rough methods — understand the delicate fabric they govern. The narrator's closing observation that Groombride "should not be judged too harshly" (013-677) is Kipling's characteristic blend of mercy and contempt.
