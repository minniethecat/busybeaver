## Appears in

- [[Chapter-3]]: [[garm|Garm]] — a Hostage (003-025 through 003-476)

## Role

Stanley is a soldier and the original owner of [[Garm]], [[garm|the bull-terrier]]. He serves as an off-stage presence whose relationship with his dog drives the central plot of "[[garm|Garm]] — a Hostage." The narrator describes him as "one of the best men I have" (003-262), and the entire hostage arrangement hinges on Stanley's deep [[love|attachment]] to [[garm|Garm]].

## Characteristics

Stanley is portrayed as emotionally attached to his dog — the narrator finds him "crying over his dog" (003-186) in the garden. He is a man of strong loyalties, both to his dog and to his duty. His address is at Kasauli in the Hills (003-427), and the phrase "Stanley—Kasauli; Kasauli—Stanley" (003-437) becomes a powerful signal that [[garm|Garm]] understands.

## Key actions

- Entrusts [[garm|Garm]] to the narrator's care, setting the hostage arrangement in motion
- Visits the narrator periodically, during which [[garm|Garm]] follows him "like a shadow" (003-322)
- Returns at the end of the story to reclaim [[garm|Garm]], reuniting with the dog at the narrator's home

## Relationships

- **[[Garm]]**: Stanley's beloved bull-terrier, for whom he serves as the emotional anchor. [[garm|Garm]] responds to the mere mention of Stanley's name combined with Kasauli (003-449)
- **The Narrator**: Holds [[garm|Garm]] as a hostage to ensure Stanley's good conduct. The two men maintain a cordial, gentlemanly understanding throughout the arrangement

## Significance

Stanley represents the theme of loyalty and the bond between a man and his dog in Kipling's story. Though he appears only briefly in person, his off-stage presence — conveyed through [[garm|Garm]]'s [[love|devotion]] and the narrator's repeated invocations of "Stanley—Kasauli" — drives the emotional core of "[[garm|Garm]] — a Hostage."
