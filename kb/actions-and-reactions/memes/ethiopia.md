## Location

Ethiopia is the setting for the events of "Little Foxes" (Chapter 13), a tale of the Gihon Hunt. The [[gihon|Great River Gihon]] "waters Ethiopia" (013-003), flowing through a dry landscape where British colonial officers administer a remote province.

## Description

Ethiopia in Kipling's telling is a land on the edge of imperial administration — remote, sparsely governed, and subject to the whims of both nature and distant [[london|London]] politics. The province along the [[gihon|Gihon River]] is managed by a small cadre of British officers who understand the local realities, in contrast to the metropolitan reformers who arrive with theories and pamphlets.

The land is described through the lens of the hunt: a fox on the banks of the Great River, a white man riding "through the dry" (013-003). The administration's documents are "new in Ethiopia in those days" (013-430), and the meaning of [[earth|land]]ownership there is "the letting in of waters" (013-463) — a fundamental difference from [[england|English]] property law.

## Significance

Ethiopia serves as Kipling's stage for satirising imperial reform movements. The contrast between the knowing provincial administrators and the meddling [[mr-groombride|Mr. Groombride]] — who "took him on for the honour of Ethiopia" (013-333) — exposes the gap between metropolitan moralising and colonial reality. Ethiopia is "unblushing" (013-363), indifferent to the sensibilities of visiting MPs.
