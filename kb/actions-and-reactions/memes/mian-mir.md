## Location

Mian Mir was a British Indian military cantonment near Lahore, in present-day Pakistan. It served as a station for British and Indian troops during the colonial period.

In Kipling's time, it was a typical cantonment — a self-contained military settlement with barracks, officers' quarters, and a club, set apart from the surrounding Indian city.

## Appears in

- [[Chapter-3|Chapter 3: Garm — a Hostage]] — the opening setting (003-001 through 003-007)

## Description

Kipling's Mian Mir is a place of military routine and minor transgressions. The narrator drives there "to see amateur theatricals" (003-001), and encounters a drunken soldier friend outside the Infantry barracks (003-002).

The scene conveys the informal social [[earth|world]] of the cantonment: officers and civilians mixing, theatrical entertainments, the ever-present military guard, and the web of friendships binding the Anglo-Indian community.

## Significance

Mian Mir serves as more than backdrop. It is the setting for the event that triggers the story's central transaction — the narrator's rescue of [[stanley|Stanley]], which leads to the gift of [[garm|Garm]].

The cantonment represents the structured [[earth|world]] of British India, where personal relationships and military discipline intersect in ways that generate both [[duty|obligation]] and narrative.
