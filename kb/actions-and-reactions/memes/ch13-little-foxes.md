[013-001]A TALE OF THE GIHON HUNT

[013-002]A fox came out of his earth on the banks of the Great River Gihon,

[013-003]which waters Ethiopia. He saw a white man riding through the dry

[013-004]dhurra-stalks, and, that his destiny might be fulfilled, barked at him.

[013-005]The rider drew rein among the villagers round his stirrup.

[013-006]“What,” said he, “is that?”

[013-007]“That,” said the Sheikh of the village, “is a fox, O Excellency Our

[013-008]Governor.”

[013-009]“It is not, then, a jackal?”

[013-010]“No jackal, but Abu Hussein the father of cunning.”

[013-011]“Also,” the white man spoke half aloud, “I am Mudir of this Province.”

[013-012]“It is true,” they cried. “Ya, Saart el Mudir” (O Excellency Our

[013-013]Governor).

[013-014]The Great River Gihon, well used to the moods of kings, slid between his

[013-015]mile-wide banks toward the sea, while the Governor praised God in a loud

[013-016]and searching cry never before heard by the river.

[013-017]When he had lowered his right forefinger from behind his right ear, the

[013-018]villagers talked to him of their crops--barley, dhurrah, millet, onions,

[013-019]and the like. The Governor stood in his stirrups. North he looked up

[013-020]a strip of green cultivation a few hundred yards wide that lay like a

[013-021]carpet between the river and the tawny line of the desert. Sixty miles

[013-022]that strip stretched before him, and as many behind. At every half-mile

[013-023]a groaning water-wheel lifted the soft water from the river to the crops

[013-024]by way of a mud-built aqueduct. A foot or so wide was the water-channel;

[013-025]five foot or more high was the bank on which it ran, and its base was

[013-026]broad in proportion. Abu Hussein, misnamed the Father of Cunning, drank

[013-027]from the river below his earth, and his shadow was long in the low sun.

[013-028]He could not understand the loud cry which the Governor had cried.

[013-029]The Sheikh of the village spoke of the crops from which the rulers of

[013-030]all lands draw revenue; but the Governor's eyes were fixed, between his

[013-031]horse's ears, on the nearest water-channel.

[013-032]“Very like a ditch in Ireland,” he murmured, and smiled, dreaming of a

[013-033]razor-topped bank in distant Kildare.

[013-034]Encouraged by that smile, the Sheikh continued. “When crops fail it is

[013-035]necessary to remit taxation. Then it is a good thing, O Excellency

[013-036]Our Governor, that you come and see the crops which have failed, and

[013-037]discover that we have not lied.”

[013-038]“Assuredly.” The Governor shortened his reins. The horse cantered on,

[013-039]rose at the embankment of the water-channel, changed leg cleverly on

[013-040]top, and hopped down in a cloud of golden dust.

[013-041]Abu Hussein from his earth watched with interest. He had never before

[013-042]seen such things.

[013-043]“Assuredly,” the Governor repeated, and came back by the way he had

[013-044]gone. “It is always best to see for one's self.”

[013-045]An ancient and still bullet-speckled stern-wheel steamer, with a barge

[013-046]lashed to her side, came round the river bend. She whistled to tell the

[013-047]Governor his dinner was ready, and the horse, seeing his fodder piled on

[013-048]the barge, whinnied back.

[013-049]“Moreover,” the Sheikh added, “in the days of the Oppression the Emirs

[013-050]and their creatures dispossessed many people of their lands. All up and

[013-051]down the river our people are waiting to return to their lawful fields.”

[013-052]“Judges have been appointed to settle that matter,” said the Governor.

[013-053]“They will presently come in steamers and hear the witnesses.”

[013-054]“Wherefore? Did the Judges kill the Emirs? We would rather be judged by

[013-055]the men who executed God's judgment on the Emirs. We would rather abide

[013-056]by your decision, O Excellency Our Governor.”

[013-057]The Governor nodded. It was a year since he had seen the Emirs stretched

[013-058]close and still round the reddened sheepskin where lay El Mahdi, the

[013-059]Prophet of God. Now there remained no trace of their dominion except the

[013-060]old steamer, once part of a Dervish flotilla, which was his house and

[013-061]office. She sidled into the shore, lowered a plank, and the Governor

[013-062]followed his horse aboard.

[013-063]Lights burned on her till late, dully reflected in the river that tugged

[013-064]at her mooring-ropes. The Governor read, not for the first time, the

[013-065]administration reports of one John Jorrocks, M.F.H.

[013-066]“We shall need,” he said suddenly to his Inspector, “about ten couple.

[013-067]I'll get 'em when I go home. You'll be Whip, Baker?”

[013-068]The Inspector, who was not yet twenty-five, signified his assent in the

[013-069]usual manner, while Abu Hussein barked at the vast desert moon.

[013-070]“Ha!” said the Governor, coming out in his pyjamas, “we'll be giving you

[013-071]capivi in another three months, my friend.”

[013-072]\* \* \* \* \*

[013-073]It was four, as a matter of fact, ere a steamer with a melodious

[013-074]bargeful of hounds anchored at that landing. The Inspector leaped down

[013-075]among them, and the homesick wanderers received him as a brother.

[013-076]“Everybody fed 'em everything on board ship, but they're real dainty

[013-077]hounds at bottom,” the Governor explained. “That's Royal you've got hold

[013-078]of--the pick of the bunch--and the bitch that's got hold of you--she's

[013-079]a little excited--is May Queen. Merriman, out of Cottesmore Maudlin, you

[013-080]know.”

[013-081]“I know. 'Grand old betch with the tan eyebrows,'” the Inspector cooed.

[013-082]“Oh, Ben! I shall take an interest in life now. Hark to 'em! O hark!”

[013-083]Abu Hussein, under the high bank, went about his night's work. An eddy

[013-084]carried his scent to the barge, and three villages heard the crash of

[013-085]music that followed. Even then Abu Hussein did not know better than to

[013-086]bark in reply.

[013-087]“Well, what about my Province?” the Governor asked.

[013-088]“Not so bad,” the Inspector answered, with Royal's head between his

[013-089]knees. “Of course, all the villages want remission of taxes, but, as far

[013-090]as I can see, the whole country's stinkin' with foxes. Our trouble will

[013-091]be choppin' 'em in cover. I've got a list of the only villages entitled

[013-092]to any remission. What d'you call this flat-sided, blue-mottled beast

[013-093]with the jowl?”

[013-094]“Beagle-boy. I have my doubts about him. Do you think we can get two

[013-095]days a week?”

[013-096]“Easy; and as many byes as you please. The Sheikh of this village here

[013-097]tells me that his barley has failed, and he wants a fifty per cent

[013-098]remission.”

[013-099]“We'll begin with him to-morrow, and look at his crops as we go. Nothing

[013-100]like personal supervision,” said the Governor.

[013-101]They began at sunrise. The pack flew off the barge in every direction,

[013-102]and, after gambols, dug like terriers at Abu Hussein's many earths. Then

[013-103]they drank themselves pot-bellied on Gihon water while the Governor and

[013-104]the Inspector chastised them with whips. Scorpions were added; for May

[013-105]Queen nosed one, and was removed to the barge lamenting. Mystery (a

[013-106]puppy, alas!) met a snake, and the blue-mottled Beagle-boy (never a

[013-107]dainty hound) ate that which he should have passed by. Only Royal, of

[013-108]the Belvoir tan head and the sad, discerning eyes, made any attempt to

[013-109]uphold the honour of England before the watching village.

[013-110]“You can't expect everything,” said the Governor after breakfast.

[013-111]“We got it, though--everything except foxes. Have you seen May Queen's

[013-112]nose?” said the Inspector.

[013-113]“And Mystery's dead. We'll keep 'em coupled next time till we get well

[013-114]in among the crops. I say, what a babbling body-snatcher that Beagle-boy

[013-115]is! Ought to be drowned!”

[013-116]“They bury people so damn casual hereabouts. Give him another chance,”

[013-117]the Inspector pleaded, not knowing that he should live to repent most

[013-118]bitterly.

[013-119]“Talkin' of chances,” said the Governor, “this Sheikh lies about his

[013-120]barley bein' a failure. If it's high enough to hide a hound at this time

[013-121]of year, it's all right. And he wants a fifty per cent remission, you

[013-122]said?”

[013-123]“You didn't go on past the melon patch where I tried to turn Wanderer.

[013-124]It's all burned up from there on to the desert. His other water-wheel

[013-125]has broken down, too,” the Inspector replied.

[013-126]“Very good. We'll split the difference and allow him twenty-five per

[013-127]cent off. Where'll we meet to-morrow?”

[013-128]“There's some trouble among the villages down the river about their

[013-129]land-titles. It's good goin' ground there, too,” the Inspector said.

[013-130]The next meet, then, was some twenty miles down the river, and the pack

[013-131]were not enlarged till they were fairly among the fields. Abu Hussein

[013-132]was there in force--four of him. Four delirious hunts of four minutes

[013-133]each--four hounds per fox--ended in four earths just above the river.

[013-134]All the village looked on.

[013-135]“We forgot about the earths. The banks are riddled with 'em. This'll

[013-136]defeat us,” said the Inspector.

[013-137]“Wait a moment!” The Governor drew forth a sneezing hound. “I've just

[013-138]remembered I'm Governor of these parts.”

[013-139]“Then turn out a black battalion to stop for us. We'll need 'em, old

[013-140]man.”

[013-141]The Governor straightened his back. “Give ear, O people!” he cried. “I

[013-142]make a new Law!”

[013-143]The villagers closed in. He called:--

[013-144]“Henceforward I will give one dollar to the man on whose land Abu

[013-145]Hussein is found. And another dollar”--he held up the coin--“to the man

[013-146]on whose land these dogs shall kill him. But to the man on whose land

[013-147]Abu Hussein shall run into a hole such as is this hole, I will give not

[013-148]dollars, but a most unmeasurable beating. Is it understood?”

[013-149]“Our Excellency,” a man stepped forth, “on my land Abu Hussein was found

[013-150]this morning. Is it not so, brothers?”

[013-151]None denied. The Governor tossed him over four dollars without a word.

[013-152]“On my land they all went into their holes,” cried another. “Therefore I

[013-153]must be beaten.”

[013-154]“Not so. The land is mine, and mine are the beatings.”

[013-155]This second speaker thrust forward his shoulders already bared, and the

[013-156]villagers shouted.

[013-157]“Hullo! Two men anxious to be licked? There must be some swindle about

[013-158]the land,” said the Governor. Then in the local vernacular: “What are

[013-159]your rights to the beating?”

[013-160]As a river-reach changes beneath a slant of the sun, that which had been

[013-161]a scattered mob changed to a court of most ancient justice. The hounds

[013-162]tore and sobbed at Abu Hussein's hearthstone, all unnoticed among

[013-163]the legs of the witnesses, and Gihon, also accustomed to laws, purred

[013-164]approval.

[013-165]“You will not wait till the Judges come up the river to settle the

[013-166]dispute?” said the Governor at last.

[013-167]“No!” shouted all the village save the man who had first asked to be

[013-168]beaten. “We will abide by Our Excellency's decision. Let Our Excellency

[013-169]turn out the creatures of the Emirs who stole our land in the days of

[013-170]the Oppression.”

[013-171]“And thou sayest?” the Governor turned to the man who had first asked to

[013-172]be beaten.

[013-173]“I say \_I\_ will wait till the wise Judges come down in the steamer.

[013-174]Then I will bring my many witnesses,” he replied.

[013-175]“He is rich. He will bring many witnesses,” the village Sheikh muttered.

[013-176]“No need. Thy own mouth condemns thee!” the Governor cried. “No man

[013-177]lawfully entitled to his land would wait one hour before entering upon

[013-178]it. Stand aside!” The man, fell back, and the village jeered him.

[013-179]The second claimant stooped quickly beneath the lifted hunting-crop. The

[013-180]village rejoiced.

[013-181]“Oh, Such an one; Son of such an one,” said the Governor, prompted by

[013-182]the Sheikh, “learn, from the day when I send the order, to block up all

[013-183]the holes where Abu Hussein may hide--on--thy--land!”

[013-184]The light flicks ended. The man stood up triumphant. By that accolade

[013-185]had the Supreme Government acknowledged his title before all men.

[013-186]While the village praised the perspicacity of the Governor, a naked,

[013-187]pock-marked child strode forward to the earth, and stood on one leg,

[013-188]unconcerned as a young stork.

[013-189]“Hal” he said, hands behind his back. “This should be blocked up with

[013-190]bundles of dhurra stalks--or, better, bundles of thorns.”

[013-191]“Better thorns,” said the Governor. “Thick ends innermost.”

[013-192]The child nodded gravely and squatted on the sand.

[013-193]“An evil day for thee, Abu Hussein,” he shrilled into the mouth of the

[013-194]earth. “A day of obstacles to thy flagitious returns in the morning.”

[013-195]“Who is it?” the Governor asked the Sheikh. “It thinks.”

[013-196]“Farag the Fatherless. His people were slain in the days of the

[013-197]Oppression. The man to whom Our Excellency has awarded the land is, as

[013-198]it were, his maternal uncle.”

[013-199]“Will it come with me and feed the big dogs?” said the Governor.

[013-200]The other peering children drew back. “Run!” they cried. “Our Excellency

[013-201]will feed Farag to the big dogs.”

[013-202]“I will come,” said Farag. “And I will never go.” He threw his arm round

[013-203]Royal's neck, and the wise beast licked his face.

[013-204]“Binjamin, by Jove!” the Inspector cried.

[013-205]“No!” said the Governor. “I believe he has the makings of a James Pigg!”

[013-206]Farag waved his hand to his uncle, and led Royal on to the barge. The

[013-207]rest of the pack followed.

[013-208]\* \* \* \* \*

[013-209]Gihon, that had seen many sports, learned to know the Hunt barge well.

[013-210]He met her rounding his bends on grey December dawns to music wild and

[013-211]lamentable as the almost forgotten throb of Dervish drums, when, high

[013-212]above Royal's tenor bell, sharper even than lying Beagle-boy's falsetto

[013-213]break, Farag chanted deathless war against Abu Hussein and all his seed.

[013-214]At sunrise the river would shoulder her carefully into her place, and

[013-215]listen to the rush and scutter of the pack fleeing up the gang-plank,

[013-216]and the tramp of the Governor's Arab behind them. They would pass over

[013-217]the brow into the dewless crops where Gihon, low and shrunken, could

[013-218]only guess what they were about when Abu Hussein flew down the bank

[013-219]to scratch at a stopped earth, and flew back into the barley again. As

[013-220]Farag had foretold, it was evil days for Abu Hussein ere he learned to

[013-221]take the necessary steps and to get away crisply. Sometimes Gihon saw

[013-222]the whole procession of the Hunt silhouetted against the morning-blue,

[013-223]bearing him company for many merry miles. At every half mile the horses

[013-224]and the donkeys jumped the water-channels--up, on, change your leg, and

[013-225]off again like figures in a zoetrope, till they grew small along the

[013-226]line of waterwheels. Then Gibon waited their rustling return through

[013-227]the crops, and took them to rest on his bosom at ten o'clock. While the

[013-228]horses ate, and Farag slept with his head on Royal's flank, the Governor

[013-229]and his Inspector worked for the good of the Hunt and his Province.

[013-230]After a little time there was no need to beat any man for neglecting

[013-231]his earths. The steamer's destination was telegraphed from waterwheel

[013-232]to waterwheel, and the villagers stopped out and put to according. If an

[013-233]earth were overlooked, it meant some dispute as to the ownership of the

[013-234]land, and then and there the Hunt checked and settled it in this wise:

[013-235]The Governor and the Inspector side by side, but the latter half a

[013-236]horse's length to the rear; both bare-shouldered claimants well in

[013-237]front; the villagers half-mooned behind them, and Farag with the pack,

[013-238]who quite understood the performance, sitting down on the left. Twenty

[013-239]minutes were enough to settle the most complicated case, for, as the

[013-240]Governor said to a judge on the steamer, “One gets at the truth in a

[013-241]hunting-field a heap quicker than in your lawcourts.”

[013-242]“But when the evidence is conflicting?” the Judge suggested.

[013-243]“Watch the field. They'll throw tongue fast enough if you're running a

[013-244]wrong scent. You've never had an appeal from one of my decisions yet.”

[013-245]The Sheikhs on horseback--the lesser folk on clever donkeys--the

[013-246]children so despised by Farag soon understood that villages which

[013-247]repaired their waterwheels and channels stood highest in the Governor's

[013-248]favour. He bought their barley for his horses.

[013-249]“Channels,” he said, “are necessary that we may all jump them. They are

[013-250]necessary, moreover, for the crops. Let there be many wheels and sound

[013-251]channels--and much good barley.”

[013-252]“Without money,” replied an aged Sheikh, “there are no waterwheels.”

[013-253]“I will lend the money,” said the Governor.

[013-254]“At what interest, O Our Excellency?”

[013-255]“Take you two of May Queen's puppies to bring up in your village in

[013-256]such a manner that they do not eat filth, nor lose their hair, nor catch

[013-257]fever from lying in the sun, but become wise hounds.”

[013-258]“Like Ray-yal--not like Bigglebai?” (Already it was an insult along

[013-259]the River to compare a man to the shifty anthropophagous blue-mottled

[013-260]harrier.)

[013-261]“Certainly, like Ray-yal--not in the least like Bigglebai. That shall be

[013-262]the interest on the loan. Let the puppies thrive and the waterwheel be

[013-263]built, and I shall be content,” said the Governor.

[013-264]“The wheel shall be built, but, O Our Excellency, if by God's favour

[013-265]the pups grow to be well-smellers, not filth-eaters, not unaccustomed to

[013-266]their names, not lawless, who will do them and me justice at the time of

[013-267]judging the young dogs?”

[013-268]“Hounds, man, hounds! Ha-wands, O Sheikh, we call them in their

[013-269]manhood.”

[013-270]“The ha-wands when they are judged at the Sha-ho. I have unfriends down

[013-271]the river to whom Our Excellency has also entrusted ha-wands to bring

[013-272]up.”

[013-273]“Puppies, man! Pah-peaz we call them, O Sheikh, in their childhood.”

[013-274]“Pah-peat. My enemies may judge my pah-peaz unjustly at the Sha-ho. This

[013-275]must be thought of.”

[013-276]“I see the obstacle. Hear now! If the new waterwheel is built in a month

[013-277]without oppression, thou, O Sheikh, shalt be named one of the judges to

[013-278]judge the pah-peaz at the Sha-ho. Is it understood?”

[013-279]“Understood. We will build the wheel. I and my seed are responsible for

[013-280]the repayment of the loan. Where are my pah-peaz? If they eat fowls,

[013-281]must they on any account eat the feathers?”

[013-282]“On no account must they eat the feathers. Farag in the barge will tell

[013-283]thee how they are to live.”

[013-284]There is no instance of any default on the Governor's personal and

[013-285]unauthorized loans, for which they called him the Father of Waterwheels.

[013-286]But the first puppy-show at the capital needed enormous tact and the

[013-287]presence of a black battalion ostentatiously drilling in the barrack

[013-288]square to prevent trouble after the prize-giving.

[013-289]But who can chronicle the glories of the Gihon Hunt--or their shames?

[013-290]Who remembers the kill in the market-place, when the Governor bade the

[013-291]assembled sheikhs and warriors observe how the hounds would instantly

[013-292]devour the body of Abu Hussein; but how, when he had scientifically

[013-293]broken it up, the weary pack turned from it in loathing, and Farag wept

[013-294]because he said the world's face had been blackened? What men who have

[013-295]not yet ridden beyond the sound of any horn recall the midnight run

[013-296]which ended--Beagleboy leading--among tombs; the hasty whip-off, and the

[013-297]oath, taken above bones, to forget the worry? The desert run, when Abu

[013-298]Hussein forsook the cultivation, and made a six-mile point to earth in

[013-299]a desolate khor--when strange armed riders on camels swooped out of a

[013-300]ravine, and instead of giving battle, offered to take the tired hounds

[013-301]home on their beasts. Which they did, and vanished.

[013-302]Above all, who remembers the death of Royal, when a certain Sheikh

[013-303]wept above the body of the stainless hound as it might have been his

[013-304]son's--and that day the Hunt rode no more? The badly-kept log-book says

[013-305]little of this, but at the end of their second season (forty-nine brace)

[013-306]appears the dark entry: “New blood badly wanted. They are beginning to

[013-307]listen to beagle-boy.”

[013-308]\* \* \* \* \*

[013-309]The Inspector attended to the matter when his leave fell due.

[013-310]“Remember,” said the Governor, “you must get us the best blood in

[013-311]England--real, dainty hounds--expense no object, but don't trust your

[013-312]own judgment. Present my letters of introduction, and take what they

[013-313]give you.”

[013-314]The Inspector presented his letters in a society where they make much

[013-315]of horses, more of hounds, and are tolerably civil to men who can

[013-316]ride. They passed him from house to house, mounted him according to his

[013-317]merits, and fed him, after five years of goat chop and Worcester sauce,

[013-318]perhaps a thought too richly.

[013-319]The seat or castle where he made his great coup does not much matter.

[013-320]Four Masters of Foxhounds were at table, and in a mellow hour the

[013-321]Inspector told them stories of the Gihon Hunt. He ended: “Ben said I

[013-322]wasn't to trust my own judgment about hounds, but I think there ought to

[013-323]be a special tariff for Empire-makers.”

[013-324]As soon as his hosts could speak, they reassured him on this point.

[013-325]“And now tell us about your first puppy-show all over again,” said one.

[013-326]“And about the earth-stoppin'. Was that all Ben's own invention?” said

[013-327]another.

[013-328]“Wait a moment,” said a large, clean-shaven man--not an M.F.H.--at the

[013-329]end of the table. “Are your villagers habitually beaten by your Governor

[013-330]when they fail to stop foxes' holes?”

[013-331]The tone and the phrase were enough even if, as the Inspector confessed

[013-332]afterwards, the big, blue double-chinned man had not looked so like

[013-333]Beagle-boy. He took him on for the honour of Ethiopia.

[013-334]“We only hunt twice a week--sometimes three times. I've never known a

[013-335]man chastised more than four times a week unless there's a bye.”

[013-336]The large loose-lipped man flung his napkin down, came round the table,

[013-337]cast himself into the chair next the Inspector, and leaned forward

[013-338]earnestly, so that he breathed in the Inspector's face.

[013-339]“Chastised with what?” he said.

[013-340]“With the kourbash--on the feet. A kourbash is a strip of old hippo-hide

[013-341]with a sort of keel on it, like the cutting edge of a boar's tusk. But

[013-342]we use the rounded side for a first offender.”

[013-343]“And do any consequences follow this sort of thing? For the victim, I

[013-344]mean--not for you?”

[013-345]“Ve-ry rarely. Let me be fair. I've never seen a man die under the lash,

[013-346]but gangrene may set up if the kourbash has been pickled.”

[013-347]“Pickled in what?” All the table was still and interested.

[013-348]“In copperas, of course. Didn't you know that” said the Inspector.

[013-349]“Thank God I didn't.” The large man sputtered visibly.

[013-350]The Inspector wiped his face and grew bolder.

[013-351]“You mustn't think we're careless about our earthstoppers. We've a Hunt

[013-352]fund for hot tar. Tar's a splendid dressing if the toe-nails aren't

[013-353]beaten off. But huntin' as large a country as we do, we mayn't be back

[013-354]at that village for a month, and if the dressings ain't renewed, and

[013-355]gangrene sets in, often as not you find your man pegging about on his

[013-356]stumps. We've a well-known local name for 'em down the river. We call

[013-357]'em the Mudir's Cranes. You see, I persuaded the Governor only to

[013-358]bastinado on one foot.”

[013-359]“On one foot? The Mudir's Cranes!” The large man turned purple to the

[013-360]top of his bald head. “Would you mind giving me the local word for

[013-361]Mudir's Cranes?”

[013-362]From a too well-stocked memory the Inspector drew one short adhesive

[013-363]word which surprises by itself even unblushing Ethiopia. He spelt it

[013-364]out, saw the large man write it down on his cuff and withdraw. Then the

[013-365]Inspector translated a few of its significations and implications to the

[013-366]four Masters of Foxhounds. He left three days later with eight couple of
