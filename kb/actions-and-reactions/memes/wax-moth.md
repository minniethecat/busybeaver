## Definition

The Wax-moth is an allegorical figure in Kipling's "The [[mother-hive|Mother Hive]]" (Chapter 5), representing a parasite that enters a weakened beehive and exploits internal divisions to destroy it. More than a literal pest, she embodies the insidious spread of destructive ideologies that prey on community discontent, using flattery and false solidarity to undermine institutions from within.

## Appears in

- [[Chapter-5|Chapter 5: The Mother Hive]] — primary antagonist throughout the chapter (005-013 through 005-999)

## Context

Written in 1909, "The [[mother-hive|Mother Hive]]" uses [[mother-hive|the beehive]] as an allegory for British society. The Wax-moth enters when "the stock had not been old and overcrowded" (005-001) — conditions that mirror contemporary anxieties about imperial overextension and social fragmentation. The moth's tactics — infiltrating youth quarters, laying "principles" not eggs (005-033), and pitting bees against each other — reflect Kipling's critique of ideological movements that exploit genuine grievances for destructive ends.

## Analysis

The Wax-moth's method is deliberately subversive: she scuttles "in like a ghost" (005-014) and avoids senior bees who would recognize the threat, instead targeting "youngsters who had not yet seen the winds blow or the flowers nod" (005-016). Her key tactic is to present her eggs as "principles" she is "ready to die for" (005-033), disguising her destructive reproductive purpose as ideological commitment. This mirrors the way parasitic ideas can exploit the idealism of youth.
