## Definition

A **house surgeon** is a metaphorical figure who diagnoses and treats the non-physical ailments of a [[home|dwelling]] — the oppressive atmospheres, inexplicable depressions, and lingering griefs that affect a house's inhabitants. The term originates from Kipling's short story "The House Surgeon" (ch15 of *Actions and Reactions*, 1909), in which the narrator acts as a diagnostician for Holmescroft, a house suffering from a mysterious "depression" (015-015).

The concept is specific to ch15, where the narrator investigates the source of an oppressive atmosphere at Holmescroft, a country house owned by the M'Leod family. The house is afflicted by waves of "aching, helpless grief" (015-182) that overwhelm all who enter.

## Context

The story reflects late-Victorian and Edwardian interests in [[soul|spirit]]ualism, psychology, and the idea that places can absorb human emotion. The narrator's investigation proceeds through interviews with the M'Leod family, legal inquiries with the solicitor Baxter, and ultimately a confrontation with the former owner [[miss-mary|Miss Mary Moultrie]].

The "ailment" of Holmescroft is traced not to drains or ghosts, but to the projected guilt and grief of [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]], who believed her sister [[miss-aggie|Agnes]] had committed suicide at the house (015-555). In reality, [[miss-aggie|Agnes]] — like [[mary-cloke|Mary]] herself — suffered from a family throat condition and accidentally fell from a dangerously low window while gasping for air (015-532). Once [[mary-cloke|Mary]] visits the house and accepts the truth, the oppressive "Shadow" lifts immediately (015-742).

## Analysis

The story operates on multiple levels:

1. **Psychological realism**: The "depression" is a genuine psychological phenomenon — the M'Leod family's experience of it is real and debilitating, even though its cause is entirely mental (015-165).

2. **The power of brooding**: [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]]'s decades of concentrated guilt and grief act "like a burning glass" (015-775), projecting her emotional state onto the physical space of Holmescroft.

3. **The house surgeon's method**: The narrator's approach — systematic interviews, legal investigation, medical analogy — mirrors both detective work (015-237: "I am less calculated to make a Sherlock Holmes than any man I know") and medical diagnosis, earning him the title "Doctor" from [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]] (015-545).
