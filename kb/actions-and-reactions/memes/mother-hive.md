## Definition

The Mother Hive is the central allegorical framework of Chapter 5, in which a beehive serves as a microcosm of British society circa 1909. The hive represents an established community or institution — productive, ordered, but vulnerable. Its overcrowded, overheated condition at the story's opening signals systemic stress: "the heat of the hive had risen with the June honey-flow" (005-003). The Mother Hive concept unites themes of social cohesion, generational tension, and institutional resilience against parasitic forces.

## Context

Kipling wrote during a period of intense debate about Britain's imperial and social future. The hive's problems — overcrowding, exhausted workers, harsh gate-keeping — mirror contemporary concerns about industrial society, generational alienation, and vulnerability to ideological infection. The Guard bee snaps at a young worker returning from her first honey-flight (005-009), illustrating how institutional harshness creates the grievances that [[sacharissa|the Wax-moth]] exploits.

## Analysis

The hive operates on several allegorical levels. As a political allegory, it represents the British Empire — productive but overextended, its defenders weary, its youth alienated. As a social allegory, it depicts any community where intergenerational tension and institutional rigidity create openings for destructive forces. The story's resolution — the hive purging itself through recognition and collective action — suggests that healthy communities can resist parasitic ideologies if they recognize the threat in time.
