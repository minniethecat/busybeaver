## Background

The Puzzler is the central episode of Chapter 11 in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909). The story turns on the visit of [[penfentenyou|Penfentenyou]] — a [[penfentenyou|Colonial Premier]] "in all but name" (011-004) — to [[england|England]], where he intends to advance a "Great Idea" (011-006) for his colony's benefit.

[[penfentenyou|Penfentenyou]] is introduced as a figure of formidable political stature: his province is "two and a half times the size of [[england|England]]" (011-005), and he is "the idol of his own province" (011-004). Yet his entry into [[england|England]] is absurdly compromised — he is "chased to my door by his own Agent-General in a motor" (011-010), setting the tone for a comedy of imperial miscommunication.

> "Last summer, though he nominally held the same portfolio, he was his Colony's Premier in all but name, and the idol of his own province, which is two and a half times the size of England." (011-003–005)

## The Episode

The narrative unfolds as a puzzle in two senses. First, literally: the monkey-puzzler tree, with its forbidding geometry of spiked branches, provides a physical metaphor for the diplomatic thicket [[penfentenyou|Penfentenyou]] must navigate. Second, politically: the colonial premier's attempt to advance his colony's interests through [[london|London]]'s channels proves to be a puzzle with no obvious solution — every door opens onto another door, every official refers him to another official.

> "That it is not called 'monkey-puzzler' for nothing, I willingly concede." (011-135)

The Agent-General's pursuit by motor car is the story's comic centrepiece: the man supposed to represent [[penfentenyou|Penfentenyou]]'s interests in [[london|London]] is instead chasing him through the [[england|English countryside]], a perfect inversion of colonial-metropolitan relations.

## Consequences

The Puzzler episode illuminates the structural comedy of imperial governance. [[penfentenyou|Penfentenyou]], a man of genuine power in his own domain, becomes a supplicant in [[london|London]] — reduced to navigating a bureaucratic maze whose architecture was designed without reference to the colonies it ostensibly serves.

Kipling's satire here is gentle but precise. The puzzle is not that colonial premiers cannot get what they want from [[london|London]]; the puzzle is that [[london|London]] does not know what it wants from the colonies. The monkey-puzzler tree stands as nature's emblem of a political system that has grown spiky, impenetrable, and "not called monkey-puzzler for nothing."
