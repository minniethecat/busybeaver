## Definition

In the context of Kipling's *Actions and Reactions*, **cotton** represents both a literal agricultural commodity and a symbol of colonial economic ambition. The concept is explored most fully in ch09 "[[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]]," where the protagonist Adam [[strickland|Strickland]] recounts his venture into cotton-growing in Africa (009-093) and the complex moral and financial dealings that surround it.

The overwhelming majority of cotton references cluster in ch09, where Adam [[strickland|Strickland]] describes his cotton-planting scheme in Africa — from the selection of "cotton-[[earth|soil]]" (009-143) to the financial machinations of "the cotton game" (009-191) and the resolution involving [[imam-din|Imam Din]] and the Hajji.

## Context

Cotton in the Edwardian era was a cornerstone of the British Empire's global trade network. Kipling's treatment of the subject in ch09 reflects the real-[[earth|world]] dynamics of colonial agriculture: European [[money|capital]] and expertise applied to African [[earth|land]], managed through local intermediaries (009-227). The story interweaves cotton-growing with themes of fever (009-044), loyalty, and the complex moral economy of empire.

Adam's cotton venture succeeds not merely through agricultural skill but through a web of personal [[duty|obligation]]s and debts — most notably the loyalty of [[imam-din|Imam Din]], who nurses Adam through fever (009-293) and later facilitates the deal that secures the cotton scheme's future.

## Analysis

Kipling uses cotton as a narrative device to explore several interlocking themes:

1. **Colonial economics**: The cotton scheme is presented as a legitimate business venture, yet its success depends on pre-existing structures of loyalty and [[duty|obligation]] that transcend pure [[money|commerce]].

2. **[[fever|Fever]] and sacrifice**: The cotton venture is intertwined with Adam's bout of [[fever|Peshawur fever]] (009-044), during which his fevered ravings reveal his true desires (009-335). The cotton scheme is, in part, a fever-dream made real.

3. **The "deal" as moral transaction**: The phrase "cotton-play" (009-247, 009-288, 009-314) suggests that cotton-growing is as much a game of human relationships as an agricultural pursuit.
