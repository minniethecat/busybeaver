## Definition

The **Bees and Flies** motif is a central allegorical framework in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909). Drawing on classical pastoral imagery — specifically Virgil's *Georgics* — Kipling deploys bees as emblems of industry, communal order, and productive labour, while flies represent corruption, parasitism, and the decay that follows moral or institutional failure.

The motif operates across two chapters: the prose allegory of Chapter 5 ("The [[mother-hive|Mother Hive]]") and the verse satire of Chapter 6 ("The Bees and the Flies"), forming a diptych that examines, from complementary angles, what happens when a well-ordered community admits corrupting influences.

## Context

Kipling's choice of beekeeping as an allegorical vehicle draws on a long tradition stretching from Virgil's fourth *Georgic* to the English pastoral. But his treatment is distinctively Edwardian: [[mother-hive|the hive]] is an imperial institution, and the wax-moth is not merely a pest but a social parasite who exploits the community's liberal tolerance.

> "If the stock had not been old and overcrowded, the Wax-moth would never have entered; but where bees are too thick on the comb there must be sickness or parasites." (005-001–003)

The poem in Chapter 6 sharpens the satire. The farmer, "an egregious rustic" (006-009), follows Virgil's recipe for spontaneous generation from a dead bull — and is rewarded not with bees but with "worms of the riper grave" (006-032) and "the hissing blow-fly" (006-037). Kipling's target here is the credulous application of ancient [[order|authority]] without empirical scrutiny, a theme that resonates with his broader scepticism toward ideological schemes.

> "Where are the honest toilers? Where / The gravid mistress of their care? / A busy scene, indeed, he sees, / But not a sign or sound of bees." (006-028–031)

The young bees' response to the wax-moth in Chapter 5 parallels this foolishness: they dismiss warnings about the Death's Head Moth as "nonsense" (005-059) and trust in abstract principles — "In the third, if you trust a Death's Head, he will trust you" (005-062–063) — while the parasite lays eggs in their midst.

## Analysis

Three thematic dimensions structure the bees-and-flies allegory:

**1. The economy of labour.** Bees in Kipling's hive are defined by work — "When a bee cannot fly, a bee must not live; and a bee knows it" (005-096). This brutal efficiency is presented not as cruelty but as the price of communal survival. The fly, by contrast, produces nothing; it consumes "corruption twice corrupt" (006-039).

**2. The vulnerability of tolerance.** The young bees' willingness to "tolerate any sort of stranger" (005-018) is not celebrated but shown as the mechanism of [[mother-hive|the hive]]'s undoing. Kipling's conservatism here is explicit: open gates, unaccompanied by vigilance, admit destruction.

**3. Classical [[order|authority]] versus empirical truth.** The Virgilian farmer in Chapter 6 trusts the book and gets filth. The parallel with the bees who trust abstract principle over the Guard's practical warning is exact. In both narratives, deference to [[order|authority]] — textual or ideological — blinds the deferent to observable reality.
