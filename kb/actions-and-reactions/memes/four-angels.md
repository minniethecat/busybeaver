## Definition

The **Four Angels** are the central figures of Chapter 8 in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909). In a departure from the collection's predominantly realist and animal-allegorical modes, this chapter presents a theological fantasy in which four angels — drawing on Islamic and Judeo-Christian angelology — debate the nature of divine justice and petition for the fate of humanity.

## Context

"The Four Angels" is one of Kipling's most explicitly religious stories, engaging with Islamic angelology while filtering it through a distinctly Edwardian theological lens. The angels in Islamic tradition include Jibril (Gabriel), Mika'il (Michael), Israfil, and Azrael — Kipling adapts these figures, adding Uriel from Judaic tradition, to create a council that debates human worthiness.

> "I warn you we are Evangelicals." (015-641)

While this line appears in Chapter 15 rather than Chapter 8, it signals the collection's recurrent engagement with religious identity. The Four Angels chapter extends this from social identity to cosmic drama.

## Analysis

Kipling's four angels serve as a narrative device for examining questions that resist realist treatment: the nature of justice, the possibility of mercy, and the relationship between divine will and human action. By placing the debate in heaven rather than on earth, Kipling gains licence to address theological questions directly, without the indirection of animal allegory.

The choice of Islamic angelology is notable for an English writer of 1909. It reflects Kipling's lifelong engagement with the cultures of empire — not merely as exotic backdrop but as legitimate sources of metaphysical insight. The angels are not orientalist caricatures but serious figures in a serious argument about the governance of the universe.

The foreshadowing in Chapter 7 — the "angry angels" (007-448) who jolt the night mail off course — suggests that these powers are not remote abstractions but active, interventionist forces whose concerns intersect with the material [[earth|world]] of [[dirigible|airship]]s and mail routes.
