## Appears in

- [[The Puzzler]] (011-168, 011-184, 011-202, 011-290, 011-451)

## Role

Sir Christopher Tomling is a minor character in "[[the-puzzler|The Puzzler]]" (ch11). He is a political figure summoned to [[lord-lundie|Lord Lundie]]'s summer gathering at Credence Green. He contributes a puzzle about the future governance of the British Empire — a question that the assembled company attempts to solve over the course of the story.

## Characteristics

Tomling is regarded as an intelligent and capable public figure. [[lord-lundie|Lord Lundie]]'s introduction implies his [[honour|reputation]] precedes him (011-168), and another character remarks approvingly that "Tomling has a head on his Shoulders" (011-184). Kipling presents him as a man whose opinions on imperial matters carry weight, though he is portrayed with the light irony that colours all the political figures in the story.

## Relationships

- **[[Lord Lundie]]**: Close associate and host. Lundie summons Tomling to Credence Green for the puzzle discussion. When Tomling is later called away on political business, Lundie laments that he has been "ravished from the Arms of the [[law|Law]]" (011-451).
- **[[Giuseppe]]**: The Italian servant at Lundie's farm, who would have observed Tomling during his visits to Credence Green.

## Significance

Tomling embodies the political dimension of Kipling's satire in "[[the-puzzler|The Puzzler]]." His puzzle about the Empire's constitutional future — and his abrupt departure when political duties intervene — illustrates the tension between abstract imperial theorising and the practical demands of governance. The phrase "ravished from the Arms of the [[law|Law]]" (011-451) suggests that capable minds are perpetually drawn away from leisured contemplation by the machinery of state.
