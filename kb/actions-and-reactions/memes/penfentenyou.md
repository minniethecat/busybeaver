Penfentenyou drives the action of "[[the-puzzler|The Puzzler]]" from its opening sentence. He is introduced as a figure of political weight — "the idol of his own province, which is two and a half times the size of [[england|England]]" (011-001) — and his presence in [[england|England]] sets the story's machinery in motion.

## Role

Penfentenyou is the Colonial Premier who comes to [[england|England]] ostensibly to develop a Great Idea, but who is pursued across the Atlantic by the unsolved puzzle of the [[the-puzzler|monkey-puzzler]] tree. His political creed is "his growing country" (011-001), and his visit is shadowed by the colonial anxieties that Kipling's story satirises.

> "What this country needs," said Penfentenyou, "is--" and for ten minutes he trumpeted rebellion. (011-065)

His function in the story is dual: he is at once the earnest colonial statesman and the comic straight man whose political gravity is continually undermined by the absurdity of the diplomatic puzzle. When he is drawn behind the hedge to observe the monkey-climbing experiment, his statesman's perspective is replaced by the schoolboy curiosity of the Harrovian.

## Characteristics

Penfentenyou is characterised by political intensity and a certain colonial impatience with metropolitan indirection. He thunders at his Agent-General — "Good Heavens alive! I told you to get that settled last Christmas" (011-056) — and snorts at the suggestion of consulting [[lord-lundie|Lord Lundie]]: "Shan't I disturb his Holiness? Perhaps 'my sort of questions' mayn't be discussed except at midnight" (011-061).

Yet beneath the bluster is a man capable of quick observation and genuine curiosity. Peering through the hedge at the [[the-puzzler|monkey-puzzler]] experiment, he is reduced to schoolboy eagerness: "Here — let me look!" (011-160).

## Significance

Penfentenyou represents a Kipling type: the colonial administrator whose practical intelligence is both admirable and slightly comic when transplanted to English [[earth|soil]]. He is the man of action entangled in a puzzle that action alone cannot solve. His journey from political bluster to bemused spectatorship mirrors the story's broader comic design — the deflation of imperial gravity by the absurd.

In the collection's larger architecture, Penfentenyou is one of several figures (alongside [[Lord Lundie]] and [[Sir Christopher Tomling]]) who embody distinct facets of the Anglo-imperial character: the politician, the judge, the engineer. Together they form the composite Kiplingesque ideal — not one man but a network of complementary competences.
