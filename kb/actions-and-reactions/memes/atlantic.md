## Location

The Atlantic Ocean is the body of water between [[england|Europe]] and [[america|America]], crossed by the [[aerial-board-of-control|Aerial Board of Control]]'s [[dirigible|airships]] on the night-mail run. In "With the Night Mail," the Atlantic is traversed not by ship but by air — the postal packet "162" flies from [[london|London]] across the ocean at altitude.

## Description

Kipling's Atlantic is seen from above — a futuristic vision where the ocean is no barrier but a landscape. The dirigible wears a "North Atlantic Winter nose-cap" (007-037) and passes over "Atlantic boats" whose lights streak the water below (007-147). From altitude, "there is no cloud on the Atlantic" (007-154), and the crossing is measured in minutes: "You'll make the deep, wet Atlantic in twenty minutes" (007-330).

The ocean's character changes with altitude and weather. Below the cloud-belts, it appears "dappled" (007-637), while in the final descent "that pitiless ladder of light, and the Atlantic takes her" (007-367) — the sea reclaiming the ship at journey's end.

## Significance

The Atlantic in "With the Night Mail" represents the technological triumph of the [[aerial-board-of-control|A.B.C.]] system. Where the ocean once separated continents by weeks of sea travel, it is now crossed in hours by air. Kipling uses the Atlantic crossing to dramatise the scale of the [[aerial-board-of-control|A.B.C.]]'s achievement — the ocean becomes not a barrier but a routine transit corridor, patrolled by "Transatlantic and Mediterranean Traffic Lines" (007-1158).
