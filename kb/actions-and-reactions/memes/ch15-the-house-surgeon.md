[015-001]On an evening after Easter Day, I sat at a table in a homeward bound

[015-002]steamer's smoking-room, where half a dozen of us told ghost stories. As

[015-003]our party broke up a man, playing Patience in the next alcove, said to

[015-004]me: “I didn't quite catch the end of that last story about the Curse on

[015-005]the family's first-born.”

[015-006]“It turned out to be drains,” I explained. “As soon as new ones were put

[015-007]into the house the Curse was lifted, I believe. I never knew the people

[015-008]myself.”

[015-009]“Ah! I've had my drains up twice; I'm on gravel too.”

[015-010]“You don't mean to say you've a ghost in your house? Why didn't you join

[015-011]our party?”

[015-012]“Any more orders, gentlemen, before the bar closes?” the steward

[015-013]interrupted.

[015-014]“Sit down again, and have one with me,” said the Patience player. “No,

[015-015]it isn't a ghost. Our trouble is more depression than anything else.”

[015-016]“How interesting? Then it's nothing any one can see?”

[015-017]“It's--it's nothing worse than a little depression. And the odd part is

[015-018]that there hasn't been a death in the house since it was built--in 1863.

[015-019]The lawyer said so. That decided me--my good lady, rather--and he made me

[015-020]pay an extra thousand for it.”

[015-021]“How curious. Unusual, too!” I said.

[015-022]“Yes; ain't it? It was built for three sisters--Moultrie was the

[015-023]name--three old maids. They all lived together; the eldest owned it. I

[015-024]bought it from her lawyer a few years ago, and if I've spent a pound

[015-025]on the place first and last, I must have spent five thousand. Electric

[015-026]light, new servants' wing, garden--all that sort of thing. A man and his

[015-027]family ought to be happy after so much expense, ain't it?” He looked at

[015-028]me through the bottom of his glass.

[015-029]“Does it affect your family much?”

[015-030]“My good lady--she's a Greek, by the way--and myself are middle-aged. We

[015-031]can bear up against depression; but it's hard on my little girl. I say

[015-032]little; but she's twenty. We send her visiting to escape it. She almost

[015-033]lived at hotels and hydros, last year, but that isn't pleasant for her.

[015-034]She used to be a canary--a perfect canary--always singing. You ought to

[015-035]hear her. She doesn't sing now. That sort of thing's unwholesome for the

[015-036]young, ain't it?”

[015-037]“Can't you get rid of the place?” I suggested.

[015-038]“Not except at a sacrifice, and we are fond of it. Just suits us three.

[015-039]We'd love it if we were allowed.”

[015-040]“What do you mean by not being allowed?”

[015-041]“I mean because of the depression. It spoils everything.”

[015-042]“What's it like exactly?”

[015-043]“I couldn't very well explain. It must be seen to be appreciated, as the

[015-044]auctioneers say. Now, I was much impressed by the story you were telling

[015-045]just now.”

[015-046]“It wasn't true,” I said.

[015-047]“My tale is true. If you would do me the pleasure to come down and spend

[015-048]a night at my little place, you'd learn more than you would if I talked

[015-049]till morning. Very likely 'twouldn't touch your good self at all.

[015-050]You might be--immune, ain't it? On the other hand, if this

[015-051]influenza,--influence does happen to affect you, why, I think it will be

[015-055]corner.

[015-057]furs--I've given thirty years of my life to 'em.”

[015-058]“You're very kind,” I murmured.

[015-059]“Far from it, I assure you. I can meet you next Saturday afternoon

[015-062]rhododendrons will be out. I mean it. You don't know how truly I mean

[015-065]skins and horns have to go through London, and L. Maxwell M'Leod, he
