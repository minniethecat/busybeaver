## Appears in

- [[Chapter-15]]: The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]] (015-445 through 015-715)

## Role

Miss Elizabeth is the youngest of the three surviving Moultrie sisters and the most visibly fragile. Described as "a small and" (015-445) delicate figure, she suffers intensely from the depressive influence emanating from [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]]. Throughout the crisis, she alternates between physical collapse and moments of crucial insight, ultimately providing key testimony that helps resolve the mystery of her sister [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]'s death.

## Characteristics

Miss Elizabeth is physically slight and emotionally susceptible, making her the most visibly affected by the house's malevolent atmosphere. She pants breathlessly (015-485) during her struggles with [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]] and leaps back in fright (015-496). Yet she possesses a sharp observational faculty — she notices that a character "had the same throat" (015-560) as another, a subtle clue to the house's modus operandi. Her vulnerability conceals a capacity for decisive action and accurate witness.

## Key actions

- Wrestles with [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]] near the open window (015-482)
- Pants "She's done it! She's done it too!" as she struggles (015-485)
- Leaps back to shelter when the crisis intensifies (015-496)
- Lies in "the far bed" where Baxter turns to check on her (015-503)
- States "I saw her do it" as a witness (015-512)
- Notices the similarity of throats — a crucial observation (015-560)
- Sits on one side of [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]] in the park during recovery (015-580)
- Confirms "I felt it too" regarding the shared oppressive experience (015-715)

## Relationships

- **[[miss-mary|Miss Mary]]**: Her elder sister and frequent antagonist. Their physical wrestling (015-482) dramatizes the friction the house generates between them, yet they are bound by shared suffering and grief
- **[[miss-aggie|Agnes Moultrie]]**: The deceased youngest sister, whose loss haunts Miss Elizabeth. Her observation about the throat (015-560) connects [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]'s fate to the house's pattern
- **[[john-baxter|John Baxter]]**: The solicitor who tends to Miss Elizabeth when she faints, finding a shawl for her and pushing her under the wash-basin (015-489, 015-507)
- **The Narrator**: Miss Elizabeth sits beside him in the park (015-580), forming a quiet alliance

## Significance

Miss Elizabeth represents the innocent collateral damage of psychological evil. Smaller and more fragile than her elder sister, she has fewer defenses against the house's depressive force. Yet Kipling grants her the most perceptive observation in the story — the recognition of a pattern that connects the living and the dead. Her physical weakness masks a [[soul|spirit]]ual sensitivity that makes her an unlikely but essential truth-teller in the [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] mystery.
