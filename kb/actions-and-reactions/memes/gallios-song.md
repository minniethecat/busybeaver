## Definition

**Gallio's Song** is a poem by Rudyard Kipling, appearing as ch14 of *Actions and Reactions* (1909). It dramatises the biblical episode from Acts 18:12–17, in which Gallio, the Roman proconsul of Achaia, refuses to judge the theological dispute between the Apostle Paul and the Jewish community. The poem explores the boundary between secular governance and religious controversy, encapsulated in Gallio's repeated refrain: "I care for none of these things" (014-016, 014-024).

The poem is one of two verse-only chapters in the collection (alongside ch12 [[the-puzzler|The Puzzler]]), and serves as a thematic complement to the collection's broader exploration of [[order|authority]], duty, and the limits of human judgment.

## Context

The historical Gallio (Lucius Junius Gallio Annaeanus) was the elder brother of the philosopher Seneca and served as proconsul of Achaia circa AD 51–52. The biblical account in Acts portrays him as dismissive of Jewish religious disputes — an attitude Kipling amplifies and gives dramatic voice to.

Kipling wrote the poem during a period when questions of imperial governance and religious pluralism were intensely debated. The Roman Empire served as a frequent analogy for the British Empire in Edwardian literature, and Gallio's stance — enforcing civil law while refusing to adjudicate matters of faith — reflects a particular vision of secular imperial administration.

## Analysis

The poem's structure alternates between the chaotic demands of the "crazed Provincials" (014-002) and Gallio's measured refusals. Gallio draws a sharp distinction between civil matters — "a question of lawful due / Or a labourer's hire denied" (014-017–018) — which he is willing to adjudicate, and religious disputes — "a question of words and names" (014-021) — which he dismisses.

His governing principle is clear: "Claudius Caesar hath set me here / Rome's Deputy to be" (014-027–028). His duty is to maintain Roman peace (*Pax Romana*), not to resolve theological controversies.
