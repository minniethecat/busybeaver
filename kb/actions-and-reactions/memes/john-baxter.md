## Appears in

- [[Chapter-15]]: The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]] (015-250 through 015-507)

## Role

John Baxter is the solicitor who sold [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] to the M'Leod family. A "large, greyish, throaty-voiced" man (015-252), he becomes deeply involved when the house proves to have a mysterious depressive [[madness|affliction]]. As the narrator cultivates him for information, Baxter transitions from a purely professional figure to a central participant in diagnosing and addressing the house's supernatural malaise.

## Characteristics

Baxter is professional, methodical, and deeply conscientious. He "entered every letter, telegram, and official note of the case" (015-324) in his diary, demonstrating the meticulousness of a good solicitor. Despite his imposing physical presence, he shows genuine emotional vulnerability — his face is "beautiful at that moment" (015-716) when speaking of deeper truths. He is also practical in a crisis, keeping his composure when Miss Elizabeth faints.

## Key actions

- Sits up in his chair when the narrator first discusses [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]]'s atmosphere (015-257)
- Admits ruefully that M'Leod had been right about the house (015-320)
- Takes the narrator to his home, where the ice of their acquaintance breaks (015-334)
- Tends to Miss Elizabeth when she faints, pushing her under the wash-basin and finding a shawl (015-489, 015-507)
- Reveals the full truth about the house's history and the previous occupant's suicide

## Relationships

- **The Narrator**: Baxter is initially guarded but gradually opens up to the narrator, who "cultivates" him for information about [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]]. Their relationship evolves from professional to confiding
- **Miss Elizabeth**: A woman suffering from the house's influence, whom Baxter assists during her fainting spells
- **The M'Leod family**: Baxter's clients, for whom he handled the [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] purchase. He feels a professional and personal [[duty|responsibility]] for their suffering

## Significance

John Baxter represents the rational, professional Victorian gentleman confronting the irrational. As a solicitor, he deals in facts, documents, and legal precision — yet the [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] case forces him to acknowledge forces beyond professional explanation. His meticulous diary-keeping (015-324) provides the documentary evidence that ultimately helps solve the mystery. Kipling uses Baxter to explore the tension between professional skepticism and the undeniable reality of psychological and supernatural phenomena.
