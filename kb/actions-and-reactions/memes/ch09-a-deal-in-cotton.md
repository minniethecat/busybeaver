[009-001]Long and long ago, when Devadatta was King of Benares, I wrote some

[009-002]tales concerning Strickland of the Punjab Police (who married Miss

[009-003]Youghal), and Adam, his son. Strickland has finished his Indian Service,

[009-004]and lives now at a place in England called Weston-super-Mare, where his

[009-005]wife plays the organ in one of the churches. Semi-occasionally he comes

[009-006]up to London, and occasionally his wife makes him visit his friends.

[009-007]Otherwise he plays golf and follows the harriers for his figure's sake.

[009-008]If you remember that Infant who told a tale to Eustace Cleever the

[009-009]novelist, you will remember that he became a baronet with a vast estate.

[009-010]He has, owing to cookery, a little lost his figure, but he never loses

[009-011]his friends. I have found a wing of his house turned into a hospital

[009-012]for sick men, and there I once spent a week in the company of two dismal

[009-013]nurses and a specialist in “Sprue.” Another time the place was full of

[009-014]schoolboys--sons of Anglo-Indians whom the Infant had collected for the

[009-015]holidays, and they nearly broke his keeper's heart.

[009-016]But my last visit was better. The Infant called me up by wire, and I

[009-017]fell into the arms of a friend of mine, Colonel A.L. Corkran, so that

[009-018]the years departed from us, and we praised Allah, who had not yet

[009-019]terminated the Delights, nor separated the Companions.

[009-020]Said Corkran, when he had explained how it felt to command a

[009-021]native Infantry regiment on the border: “The Stricks are coming for

[009-022]to-night-with their boy.”

[009-023]“I remember him. The little fellow I wrote a story about,” I said. “Is

[009-024]he in the Service?”

[009-025]“No. Strick got him into the Centro-Euro-Africa Protectorate. He's

[009-026]Assistant-Commissioner at Dupé--wherever that is. Somaliland, ain't it,

[009-027]Stalky?” asked the Infant.

[009-028]Stalky puffed out his nostrils scornfully. “You're only three thousand

[009-029]miles out. Look at the atlas.”

[009-030]“Anyhow, he's as rotten full of fever as the rest of you,” said the

[009-031]Infant, at length on the big divan. “And he's bringing a native servant

[009-032]with him. Stalky be an athlete, and tell Ipps to put him in the stable

[009-033]room.”

[009-034]“Why? Is he a Yao--like the fellow Wade brought here--when your

[009-035]housekeeper had fits?” Stalky often visits the Infant, and has seen some

[009-036]odd things.

[009-037]“No. He's one of old Strickland's Punjabi policemen--and quite

[009-038]European--I believe.”

[009-039]“Hooray! Haven't talked Punjabi for three months--and a Punjabi from

[009-040]Central Africa ought to be amusin'.”

[009-041]We heard the chuff of the motor in the porch, and the first to enter was

[009-042]Agnes Strickland, whom the Infant makes no secret of adoring.

[009-043]He is devoted, in a fat man's placid way, to at least eight designing

[009-044]women; but she nursed him once through a bad bout of Peshawur fever, and

[009-045]when she is in the house, it is more than all hers.

[009-046]“You didn't send rugs enough,” she began. “Adam might have taken a

[009-047]chill.”

[009-048]“It's quite warm in the tonneau. Why did you let him ride in front?”

[009-049]“Because he wanted to,” she replied, with the mother's smile, and we

[009-050]were introduced to the shadow of a young man leaning heavily on the

[009-051]shoulder of a bearded Punjabi Mohammedan.

[009-052]“That is all that came home of him,” said his father to me. “There

[009-053]was nothing in it of the child with whom I had journeyed to Dalhousie

[009-054]centuries since.”

[009-055]“And what is this uniform?” Stalky asked of Imam Din, the servant, who

[009-056]came to attention on the marble floor.

[009-057]“The uniform of the Protectorate troops, Sahib. Though I am the Little

[009-058]Sahib's body-servant, it is not seemly for us white men to be attended

[009-059]by folk dressed altogether as servants.”

[009-060]“And--and you white men wait at table on horseback?” Stalky pointed to

[009-061]the man's spurs.

[009-062]“These I added for the sake of honour when I came to England,” said Imam

[009-063]Din Adam smiled the ghost of a little smile that I began to remember,

[009-064]and we put him on the big couch for refreshments. Stalky asked him how

[009-065]much leave he had, and he said “Six months.”

[009-066]“But he'll take another six on medical certificate,” said Agnes

[009-067]anxiously. Adam knit his brows.

[009-068]“You don't want to--eh? I know. Wonder what my second in command is

[009-069]doing.” Stalky tugged his moustache, and fell to thinking of his Sikhs.

[009-070]“Ah!” said the Infant. “I've only a few thousand pheasants to look

[009-071]after. Come along and dress for dinner. We're just ourselves. What

[009-072]flower is your honour's ladyship commanding for the table?”

[009-073]“Just ourselves?” she said, looking at the crotons in the great hall.

[009-074]“Then let's have marigolds--the little cemetery ones.”

[009-075]So it was ordered.

[009-076]Now, marigolds to us mean hot weather, discomfort, parting, and death.

[009-077]That smell in our nostrils, and Adam's servant in waiting, we naturally

[009-078]fell back more and more on the old slang, recalling at each glass those

[009-079]who had gone before. We did not sit at the big table, but in the bay

[009-080]window overlooking the park, where they were carting the last of the

[009-081]hay. When twilight fell we would not have candles, but waited for the

[009-082]moon, and continued our talk in the dusk that makes one remember.

[009-083]Young Adam was not interested in our past except where it had touched

[009-084]his future. I think his mother held his hand beneath the table. Imam

[009-085]Din--shoeless, out of respect to the floors--brought him his medicine,

[009-086]poured it drop by drop, and asked for orders.

[009-087]“Wait to take him to his cot when he grows weary,” said his mother, and

[009-088]Imam Din retired into the shadow by the ancestral portraits.

[009-089]“Now what d'you expect to get out of your country?” the Infant asked,

[009-090]when--our India laid aside we talked Adam's Africa. It roused him at

[009-091]once.

[009-092]“Rubber--nuts--gums--and so on,” he said. “But our real future is

[009-093]cotton. I grew fifty acres of it last year in my District.”

[009-094]“My District!” said his father. “Hear him, Mummy!”

[009-095]“I did though! I wish I could show you the sample. Some Manchester chaps

[009-096]said it was as good as any Sea Island cotton on the market.”

[009-097]“But what made you a cotton-planter, my son?” she asked.

[009-098]“My Chief said every man ought to have a shouk (a hobby) of sorts, and

[009-099]he took the trouble to ride a day out of his way to show me a belt of

[009-100]black soil that was just the thing for cotton.”

[009-101]“Ah! What was your Chief like?” Stalky asked, in his silkiest tones.

[009-102]“The best man alive--absolutely. He lets you blow your own nose

[009-103]yourself. The people call him”--Adam jerked out some heathen

[009-104]phrase--“that means the Man with the Stone Eyes, you know.”

[009-105]“I'm glad of that. Because I've heard--from other quarters”--Stalky's

[009-106]sentence burned like a slow match, but the explosion was not long

[009-107]delayed. “Other quarters!” Adam threw out a thin hand. “Every dog has

[009-108]his fleas. If you listen to them, of course!” The shake of his head was

[009-109]as I remembered it among his father's policemen twenty years before, and

[009-110]his mother's eyes shining through the dusk called on me to adore it. I

[009-111]kicked Stalky on the shin. One must not mock a young man's first love or

[009-112]loyalty.

[009-113]A lump of raw cotton appeared on the table.

[009-114]“I thought there might be a need. Therefore I packed it between our

[009-115]shirts,” said the voice of Imam Din.

[009-116]“Does he know as much English as that?” cried the Infant, who had

[009-117]forgotten his East.

[009-118]We all admired the cotton for Adam's sake, and, indeed, it was very long

[009-119]and glossy.

[009-120]“It's--it's only an experiment,” he said. “We're such awful paupers we

[009-121]can't even pay for a mailcart in my District. We use a biscuit-box

[009-122]on two bicycle wheels. I only got the money for that”--he patted the

[009-123]stuff--“by a pure fluke.”

[009-124]“How much did it cost?” asked Strickland.

[009-125]“With seed and machinery--about two hundred pounds. I had the labour

[009-126]done by cannibals.”

[009-127]“That sounds promising.” Stalky reached for a fresh cigarette.

[009-128]“No, thank you,” said Agnes. “I've been at Weston-super-Mare a little

[009-129]too long for cannibals. I'll go to the music-room and try over next

[009-130]Sunday's hymns.”

[009-131]She lifted the boy's hand lightly to her lips, and tripped across the

[009-132]acres of glimmering floor to the music-room that had been the Infant's

[009-133]ancestors' banqueting hall. Her grey and silver dress disappeared under

[009-134]the musicians' gallery; two electrics broke out, and she stood backed

[009-135]against the lines of gilded pipes.

[009-136]“There's an abominable self-playing attachment here!” she called.

[009-137]“Me!” the Infant answered, his napkin on his shoulder. “That's how I

[009-138]play Parsifal.”

[009-139]“I prefer the direct expression. Take it away, Ipps.”

[009-140]We heard old Ipps skating obediently all over the floor.

[009-141]“Now for the direct expression,” said Stalky, and moved on the Burgundy

[009-142]recommended by the faculty to enrich fever-thinned blood.

[009-143]“It's nothing much. Only the belt of cotton-soil my chief showed me

[009-144]ran right into the Sheshaheli country. We haven't been able to prove

[009-145]cannibalism against that tribe in the courts; but when a Sheshaheli

[009-146]offers you four pounds of woman's breast, tattoo marks and all, skewered

[009-147]up in a plantain leaf before breakfast, you--”

[009-148]“Naturally burn the villages before lunch,” said Stalky.

[009-149]Adam shook his head. “No troops,” he sighed. “I told my Chief about it,

[009-150]and he said we must wait till they chopped a white man. He advised me if

[009-151]ever I felt like it not to commit a--a barren felo de se, but to let the

[009-152]Sheshaheli do it. Then he could report, and then we could mop 'em up!”

[009-153]“Most immoral! That's how we got--” Stalky quoted the name of a province

[009-154]won by just such a sacrifice.

[009-155]“Yes, but the beasts dominated one end of my cotton-belt like anything.

[009-156]They chivied me out of it when I went to take soil for analysis--me and

[009-157]Imam Din.”

[009-158]“Sahib! Is there a need?” The voice came out of the darkness, and the

[009-159]eyes shone over Adam's shoulder ere it ceased.

[009-160]“None. The name was taken in talk.” Adam abolished him with a turn of

[009-161]the finger. “I couldn't make a casus belli of it just then, because my

[009-162]Chief had taken all the troops to hammer a gang of slave kings up north.

[009-163]Did you ever hear of our war against Ibn Makarrah? He precious nearly

[009-164]lost us the Protectorate at one time, though he's an ally of ours now.”

[009-165]“Wasn't he rather a pernicious brute, even as they go?” said Stalky.

[009-166]“Wade told me about him last year.”

[009-167]“Well, his nickname all through the country was 'The Merciful,' and he

[009-168]didn't get that for nothing. None of our people ever breathed his

[009-169]proper name. They said 'He' or 'That One,' and they didn't say it aloud,

[009-170]either. He fought us for eight months.”

[009-171]“I remember. There was a paragraph about it in one of the papers,” I

[009-172]said.

[009-173]“We broke him, though. No--the slavers don't come our way, because our

[009-174]men have the reputation of dying too much, the first month after they're

[009-175]captured. That knocks down profits, you see.”

[009-176]“What about your charming friends, the Sheshahelis?” said the Infant.

[009-177]“There's no market for Sheshaheli. People would as soon buy crocodiles.

[009-178]I believe, before we annexed the country, Ibn Makarrah dropped down on

[009-179]'em once--to train his young men--and simply hewed 'em in pieces.

[009-180]The bulk of my people are agriculturists--just the right stamp for

[009-181]cotton-growers. What's Mother playing?--'\_Once in royal\_'?”

[009-182]The organ that had been crooning as happily as a woman over her babe

[009-183]restored, steadied to a tune.

[009-184]“Magnificent! Oh, magnificent!” said the Infant loyally. I had never

[009-185]heard him sing but once, and then, though it was early in the tolerant

[009-186]morning, his mess had rolled him into a lotus pond.

[009-187]“How did you get your cannibals to work for you?” asked Strickland.

[009-188]“They got converted to civilization after my Chief smashed Ibn

[009-189]Makarrah--just at the time I wanted 'em. You see my Chief had promised

[009-190]me in writing that if I could scrape up a surplus he would not bag it

[009-191]for his roads this time, but I might have it for my cotton game. I only

[009-192]needed two hundred pounds. Our revenues didn't run to it.”

[009-193]“What is your revenue?” Stalky asked in the vernacular.

[009-194]“With hut-tax, traders' game and mining licenses, not more than fourteen

[009-195]thousand rupees; every penny of it ear-marked months ahead.” Adam

[009-196]sighed.

[009-197]“Also there is a fine for dogs straying in the Sahib's camp. Last year

[009-198]it exceeded three rupees,” Imam Din said quietly.

[009-199]“Well, I thought that was fair. They howled so. We were rather strict on

[009-200]fines. I worked up my native clerk--Bulaki Ram--to a ferocious pitch

[009-201]of enthusiasm. He used to calculate the profits of our cotton-scheme

[009-202]to three points of decimals, after office. I tell you I envied your

[009-203]magistrates here hauling money out of motorists every week I had managed

[009-204]to make our ordinary revenue and expenditure just about meet, and I

[009-205]was crazy to get the odd two hundred pounds for my cotton. That sort of

[009-206]thing grows on a chap when he's alone--and talks aloud!”

[009-207]“Hul-lo! Have you been there already?” the father said, and Adam nodded.

[009-208]“Yes. Used to spout what I could remember of 'Marmion' to a tree, sir.

[009-209]Well then my luck turned. One evening an English-speaking nigger came in

[009-210]towing a corpse by the feet. (You get used to little things like that.)

[009-211]He said he'd found it, and please would I identify, because if it

[009-212]was one of Ibn Makarrah's men there might be a reward. It was an old

[009-213]Mohammedan, with a strong dash of Arab--a smallboned, bald-headed chap,

[009-214]and I was just wondering how it had kept so well in our climate when it

[009-215]sneezed. You ought to have seen the nigger! He fetched a howl and bolted

[009-216]like--like the dog in 'Tom Sawyer,' when he sat on the what's-its-name

[009-217]beetle. He yelped as he ran, and the corpse went on sneezing. I could

[009-218]see it had been sarkied. (That's a sort of gum-poison, pater, which

[009-219]attacks the nerve centres. Our chief medical officer is writing a

[009-220]monograph about it.) So Imam Din and I emptied out the corpse one time,

[009-221]with my shaving soap and trade gunpowder, and hot water.

[009-222]“I'd seen a case of sarkie before; so when the skin peeled off his feet,

[009-223]and he stopped sneezing, I knew he'd live. He was bad, though; lay like

[009-224]a log for a week while Imam Din and I massaged the paralysis out of him.

[009-225]Then he told us he was a Hajji--had been three times to Mecca--come in

[009-226]from French Africa, and that he'd met the nigger by the wayside--just

[009-227]like a case of thuggee, in India--and the nigger had poisoned him. That

[009-228]seemed reasonable enough by what I knew of Coast niggers.”

[009-229]“You believed him?” said his father keenly.

[009-230]“There was no reason I shouldn't. The nigger never came back, and the

[009-231]old man stayed with me for two months,” Adam returned. “You know what

[009-232]the best type of a Mohammedan gentleman can be, pater? He was that.”

[009-233]“None finer, none finer,” was the answer.

[009-234]“Except a Sikh,” Stalky grunted.

[009-235]“He'd been to Bombay; he knew French Africa inside out; he could quote

[009-236]poetry and the Koran all day long. He played chess--you don't know what

[009-237]that meant to me--like a master. We used to talk about the regeneration

[009-238]of Turkey and the Sheik-ul-Islam between moves. Oh, everything under the

[009-239]sun we talked about! He was awfully open-minded. He believed in slavery,

[009-240]of course, but he quite saw that it would have to die out. That's why

[009-241]he agreed with me about developing the resources of the district by

[009-242]cotton-growing, you know.”

[009-243]“You talked of that too?” said Strickland.

[009-244]“Rather. We discussed it for hours. You don't know what it meant to me.

[009-245]A wonderful man. Imam Din, was not our Hajji marvellous?”

[009-246]“Most marvellous! It was all through the Hajji that we found the money

[009-247]for our cotton-play.” Imam Din had moved, I fancy, behind Strickland's

[009-248]chair.

[009-249]“Yes. It must have been dead against his convictions too. He brought me

[009-250]news when I was down with fever at Dupé that one of Ibn Makarrah's men

[009-251]was parading through my District with a bunch of slaves--in the Fork!”

[009-252]“What's the matter with the Fork, that you can't abide it?” said Stalky.

[009-253]Adam's voice had risen at the last word.

[009-254]“Local etiquette, sir,” he replied, too earnest to notice Stalky's

[009-255]atrocious pun. “If a slaver runs slaves through British territory he

[009-256]ought to pretend that they're his servants. Hawkin' 'em about in the

[009-257]Fork--the forked stick that you put round their necks, you know--is

[009-258]insolence--same as not backing your topsails in the old days. Besides,

[009-259]it unsettles the District.”

[009-260]“I thought you said slavers didn't come your way,” I put in.

[009-261]“They don't. But my Chief was smoking 'em out of the North all that

[009-262]season, and they were bolting into French territory any road they could

[009-263]find. My orders were to take no notice so long as they circulated, but

[009-264]open slave-dealing in--the--Fork, was too much. I couldn't go myself, so I

[009-265]told a couple of our Makalali police and Imam Din to make talk with

[009-266]the gentleman one time. It was rather risky, and it might have been

[009-267]expensive, but it turned up trumps. They were back in a few days with

[009-268]the slaver (he didn't show fight) and a whole crowd of witnesses, and

[009-269]we tried him in my bedroom, and fined him properly. Just to show you

[009-270]how demoralized the brute must have been (Arabs often go dotty after a

[009-271]defeat), he'd snapped up four or five utterly useless Sheshaheli, and

[009-272]was offering 'em to all and sundry along the road. Why, he offered 'em

[009-273]to you, didn't he, Imam Din?”

[009-274]“I was witness that he offered man-eaters for sale,” said Imam Din.

[009-275]“Luckily for my cotton-scheme, that landed him both ways. You see, he

[009-276]had slaved and exposed slaves for sale in British territory. That meant

[009-277]the double fine if I could get it out of him.”

[009-278]“What was his defence?” said Strickland, late of the Punjab Police.

[009-279]“As far as I remember--but I had a temperature of 104 degrees at the

[009-280]time--he'd mistaken the meridians of longitude. Thought he was in French

[009-281]territory. Said he'd never do it again, if we'd let him off with a fine.

[009-282]I could have shaken hands with the brute for that. He paid up cash like

[009-283]a motorist and went off one time.”

[009-284]“Did you see him?”

[009-285]“Ye-es. Didn't I, Imam Din?”

[009-286]“Assuredly the Sahib both saw and spoke to the slaver. And the Sahib

[009-287]also made a speech to the man-eaters when he freed them, and they swore

[009-288]to supply him with labour for all his cotton-play. The Sahib leaned on

[009-289]his own servant's shoulder the while.”

[009-290]“I remember something of that. I remember Bulaki Ram giving me the

[009-291]papers to sign, and I distinctly remember him locking up the money in

[009-292]the safe--two hundred and ten beautiful English sovereigns. You don't

[009-293]know what that meant to me! I believe it cured my fever; and as soon

[009-294]as I could, I staggered off with the Hajji to interview the Sheshaheli

[009-295]about labour. Then I found out why they had been so keen to work! It

[009-296]wasn't gratitude. Their big village had been hit by lightning and burned

[009-297]out a week or two before, and they lay flat in rows around me asking me

[009-298]for a job. I gave it 'em.”

[009-299]“And so you were very happy?” His mother had stolen up behind us. “You

[009-300]liked your cotton, dear?” She tidied the lump away.

[009-301]“By Jove, I was happy!” Adam yawned. “Now if any one,” he looked at

[009-302]the Infant, “cares to put a little money into the scheme, it'll be the

[009-303]making of my District. I can't give you figures, sir, but I assure--”

[009-304]“You'll take your arsenic, and Imam Din'll take you up to bed, and I'll

[009-305]come and tuck you in.”

[009-306]Agnes leaned forward, her rounded elbows on his shoulders, hands joined

[009-307]across his dark hair, and “Isn't he a darling?” she said to us, with

[009-308]just the same heart-rending lift to the left eyebrow and the same break

[009-309]of her voice as sent Strickland mad among the horses in the year '84. We

[009-310]were quiet when they were gone. We waited till Imam Din returned to us

[009-311]from above and coughed at the door, as only dark-hearted Asia can.

[009-312]“Now,” said Strickland, “tell us what truly befell, son of my servant.”

[009-313]“All befell as our Sahib has said. Only--only there was an

[009-314]arrangement--a little arrangement on account of his cotton-play.”

[009-315]“Tell! Sit! I beg your pardon, Infant,” said Strickland.

[009-316]But the Infant had already made the sign, and we heard Imam Din hunker

[009-317]down on the floor: One gets little out of the East at attention.

[009-318]“When the fever came on our Sahib in our roofed house at Dupé,” he

[009-319]began, “the Hajji listened intently to his talk. He expected the names

[009-320]of women; though I had already told him that Our virtue was beyond

[009-321]belief or compare, and that Our sole desire was this cotton-play. Being

[009-324]made a mock of the law. Sahib,” Imam Din turned to Strickland, “our

[009-325]Sahib answered to those false words as a horse of blood answers to

[009-327]slavedealer. Then he fell back. Then we left him.”

[009-328]“Alone--servant of my son, and son of my servant?” said his father.

[009-329]“There was an old woman which belonged to the Hajji. She had come in

[009-330]with the Hajji's money-belt. The Hajji told her that if our Sahib died,

[009-331]she would die with him. And truly our Sahib had given me orders to

[009-332]depart.”

[009-333]“Being mad with fever--eh?”

[009-334]“What could we do, Sahib? This cotton-play was his heart's desire. He

[009-335]talked of it in his fever. Therefore it was his heart's desire that

[009-336]the Hajji went to fetch. Doubtless the Hajji could have given him money

[009-337]enough out of hand for ten cotton-plays; but in this respect also our

[009-338]Sahib's virtue was beyond belief or compare. Great Ones do not exchange

[009-339]moneys. Therefore the Hajji said--and I helped with my counsel--that we

[009-340]must make arrangements to get the money in all respects conformable with

[009-342]And the Hajji showed the old woman the knife by which she would die if

[009-343]our Sahib died. So I accompanied the Hajji.”

[009-344]“Knowing who he was?” said Strickland.

[009-345]“No! Fearing the man. A virtue went out from him overbearing the virtue

[009-346]of lesser persons. The Hajji told Bulaki Ram the clerk to occupy the

[009-347]seat of government at Dupé till our return. Bulaki Ram feared the Hajji,

[009-348]because the Hajji had often gloatingly appraised his skill in figures

[009-349]at five thousand rupees upon any slave-block. The Hajji then said to

[009-350]me: 'Come, and we will make the man-eaters play the cotton-game for my

[009-351]delight's delight.' The Hajji loved our Sahib with the love of a father

[009-352]for his son, of a saved for his saviour, of a Great One for a Great One.
