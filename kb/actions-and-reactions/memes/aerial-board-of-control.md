## Overview

The **Aerial Board of Control** (A.B.C.) is the governing body of the futuristic [[earth|world]] depicted in Chapter 7 of Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909), "With the Night Mail: A Story of 2000 A.D." It represents Kipling's speculative answer to the question of what comes after national government: a technocratic administration run by engineers, not politicians.

> "She is responsible only to the Aerial Board of Control — the A. B. C. of which reports talk — and that body, the assembly of a few score of persons of both sexes, controls this planet." (007-567–570)

## Appears in

| Chapter | Role |
|---------|------|
| Chapter 7: With the Night Mail | The A.B.C. is the unseen but omnipresent governing [[order|authority]]; its certificates, [[law|regulation]]s, and standards appear throughout the chapter's apparatus, from the narrative text to the mock advertisements appended to the story |

The A.B.C. is not depicted in session; it is encountered entirely through its effects — the [[law|regulation]]s it enforces, the certificates it issues, the order it maintains. This is Kipling's point: real government, in this vision, is infrastructural rather than theatrical.

## Structure

Based on the story's details and appended materials, the A.B.C.'s remit includes:

| Domain | Function |
|--------|---------|
| Air Traffic Control | Regulation of all planetary air traffic; the "lanes" and "levels" system that organises the sky |
| Certification | A.B.C. certificates required for equipment, pilots, and vessels — from "A.B.C. certificate L3 10s" (007-1183) to wireless installations "syntonised to A.B.C. requirements" (007-1379) |
| Rescue Coordination | Oversight of mid-air emergencies; the Halma freighter rescue proceeds under A.B.C. protocols |
| Dispute Resolution | The A.B.C. has absorbed functions previously belonging to nations, including the Convention of [[london|London]]'s provisions |

The A.B.C. is composed of "a few score of persons of both sexes" (007-570) — a detail that signals both efficiency (small government) and modernity (gender-inclusive, for 1909). Kipling imagines governance as a technical profession, not a political one.

## Contrast with Political Government

The story explicitly contrasts the A.B.C. with the "archaic" institutions it has superseded:

> "The planet knows was the sole surviving European repository of 'autonomous institutions,' 'local self-government,' and the rest of the archaic lumber." (007-838–839)

| Aspect | A.B.C. | Old Government ([[government]]) |
|--------|--------|-------------------------------|
| Basis of [[order|authority]] | Technical competence | Political legitimacy |
| Mode of operation | Regulation, certification, standardisation | Legislation, diplomacy, [[war|warfare]] |
| Personnel | Engineers and administrators | Politicians and civil servants |
| Relationship to nations | Has absorbed them | Consists of them |

## Kipling's Technocratic Vision

The A.B.C. is one of Kipling's most striking speculative creations. It anticipates themes that would become central to 20th-century political thought: the tension between expertise and democracy, the possibility of governance without politics, and the replacement of national sovereignty by functional administration.

Kipling's attitude toward his creation is characteristically ambiguous. The A.B.C. works — the planetary traffic flows, rescues proceed efficiently, the mail arrives on time. But the vision is also chilling: "this tolerant, humorous, lazy little planet" (007-575) has "shifted the whole burden of public administration" (007-576) onto a few score technocrats, and no one seems to mind. Whether this is utopia or dystopia Kipling leaves for the reader to decide.
