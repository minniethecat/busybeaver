## Definition

**Love** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) is less a romantic emotion than a **binding force** — the tie that attaches a person to a place, a dog to a master, a bee to her hive. It is tested not by intensity of feeling but by the actions it compels: the sacrifices made, the distances crossed, the work undertaken. Kipling's treatment consistently subordinates love-as-passion to love-as-[[duty|obligation]].

## Context

[[sophie-chapin|Sophie Chapin]]'s relationship to Friars Pardon begins not as love but as duty. When George asks "When did you begin to fall in love with the house?" (001-532), [[sophie-chapin|Sophie]] deflects: "I'm not in love with it. One must do something." Her love grows through action — the work of restoration, the learning of the [[earth|land]] — rather than preceding it. Kipling reverses the romantic sequence: feeling follows doing, not the reverse.

> "I just want to be loved, and there..." (001-892)

[[sophie-chapin|Sophie]]'s quiet admission — that she wants to be loved — is one of the collection's rare moments of emotional candour. It comes not in a love scene but in the context of her absorption into the life of the estate, suggesting that love of place and love of person are not separate categories but aspects of the same belonging.

> "A man who loves one dog is quite another." (003-057)

Chapter 3 presents the most intense love relationship in the collection — not between humans but between [[strickland|Strickland]] and his dog [[garm|Garm]]. The narrator observes that a man who loves one dog is fundamentally different from a man who keeps many: love concentrated is love transformed. [[garm|Garm]], "tied to you so strictly by love that without you he will not live" (003-061), embodies love as absolute fidelity.

> "Our loves are not given, but only lent." (004-027)

The poem "The [[power-of-the-dog|Power of the Dog]]" introduces a darker note. Love is lent, not given — conditional, revocable, shadowed by loss. The poem's famous opening line frames all the collection's attachments as provisional. Even [[garm|Garm]]'s absolute fidelity exists within a contract whose terms include death.

> "One that loved her fellow-worker." (005-040)

In Chapter 5, love is subordinated to work. [[melissa|Melissa]], the wise bee, is introduced as one who "loved her fellow-worker" — love is not the antithesis of work but its companion. The hive's discipline does not exclude affection; it channels it into productive forms. The wax-moth, by contrast, offers a parody of love: flattery and seduction that serve only consumption.

## Analysis

Kipling's treatment of love is notable for what it **excludes**. There is no romantic courtship in *Actions and Reactions* — the [[george-chapin|Chapin]]s are already married when the collection begins. Passion is present only in displaced forms: the love of [[earth|land]], the love of dog, the love of work. This is partly Kipling's Edwardian reticence, but it also serves a structural purpose: by removing romantic love from the centre, the collection can examine the other bonds that hold a society together.

The love between [[strickland|Strickland]] and [[garm|Garm]] (Chapter 3) is the collection's emotional high-water mark precisely because it is uncomplicated by social convention. The dog's love is pure [[duty|obligation]] — he cannot choose not to love — and this purity makes it the standard against which human attachments are measured.

[[sophie-chapin|Sophie]]'s love for Friars Pardon, by contrast, is achieved rather than given. She must learn to love the house, the [[earth|land]], the life. Her love is a discipline, not a passion — closer to the bee's love of [[mother-hive|the hive]] than to romantic attachment. This is Kipling's deepest claim about love: that its truest form is not the thunderbolt but the slow, daily labour of belonging.
