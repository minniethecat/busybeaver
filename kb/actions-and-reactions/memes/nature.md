## Definition

**Nature** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) appears primarily in its philosophical sense: the inherent character of persons and the given order of things, rather than the Romantic sense of [[garden|landscape]] or wilderness. Kipling uses "nature" to denote what is fixed, given, and must be accepted — the unalterable foundation upon which human choice must build.

## Context

All three occurrences of "nature" in the collection cluster in Chapter 15, "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]," a story organized around the question of what can be known and what must be accepted. The haunted house affects each visitor differently — "according to his or her nature" (015-183) — making disposition, not the house itself, the determining variable in the psychological drama.

> "According to his or her nature." (015-183)

The narrator's investigation into the house's oppressive atmosphere repeatedly encounters the limits of evidence: "in the nature of things, they can't be sure" (015-351). Here "nature" names the boundary between the provable and the merely felt — the epistemological constraint that makes the house's effect real yet resistant to demonstration.

The third occurrence (015-353) reinforces this theme: judgment depends on "the nature of the evidence," and the nature of the evidence in matters of psychological disturbance is inherently uncertain.

## Analysis

The concentration of "nature" in a single chapter — the collection's most psychologically sophisticated story — is telling. Kipling uses the concept not to celebrate the natural [[earth|world]] but to mark the boundary of human knowledge. "The nature of things" (015-351) and "the nature of the evidence" (015-353) bracket what can be proven; "his or her nature" (015-183) determines individual response to the unprovable.

This usage is thoroughly Kiplingesque: nature is not an object of Romantic contemplation but a functional term for the given — the way things are, the way people are, the limits of knowing. It is the concept that names the back[[earth|ground]] against which action, reaction, and judgment occur.
