## Definition

In Kipling's *Actions and Reactions*, **fever** appears as a recurring motif that serves both as a literal medical condition (tropical fevers endemic to colonial India and Africa) and as a narrative device that reveals character under duress. Fever episodes mark moments of crisis, transformation, and revelation across multiple stories in the collection.

The most significant treatment occurs in ch09, where Adam [[strickland|Strickland]] contracts Peshawur fever (009-044) and in his delirious state reveals his true desires about the cotton scheme (009-335). His fever becomes the catalyst for [[imam-din|Imam Din]]'s loyalty and the eventual resolution of the "deal."

## Context

Fever was an inescapable reality of colonial life in India and Africa during the British Empire. Kipling himself experienced tropical illnesses, and his fiction frequently uses fever as both a physical trial and a symbolic threshold. In ch03, [[garm|Garm]]'s fevered state reveals the bond between the dog and his master [[strickland|Strickland]] (003-516). In ch09, fever acts as a truth-serum: "Being mad with fever" (009-333) strips away social pretense and exposes genuine motives.

## Analysis

Kipling employs fever in three distinct registers:

1. **Medical realism**: The detailed descriptions of fever symptoms — "rotten full of fever" (009-030), "fever-thinned blood" (009-142) — reflect the genuine medical concerns of colonial administrators and soldiers.

2. **Narrative catalyst**: Fever forces characters into vulnerable states where hidden loyalties and desires surface. Adam's fevered ravings about cotton (009-335) drive the plot of ch09.

3. **Symbolic threshold**: Crossing through fever often marks a character's passage from one state of being to another — from sickness to health, from concealment to revelation, from individual ambition to communal [[duty|obligation]].
