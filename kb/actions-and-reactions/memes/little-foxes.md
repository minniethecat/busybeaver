## Definition

**Little Foxes** is the titular concept of Chapter 13 of Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909). Drawing on the biblical reference from the Song of Solomon ("the little foxes that spoil the vines"), Kipling uses fox-hunting in a colonial setting as an allegory for the small corruptions, bureaucratic pettiness, and incremental abuses that erode the moral foundations of imperial administration.

## Context

The chapter "Little Foxes" is set in a colonial territory where a British administrator organizes a fox-hunt. The hunt serves as a microcosm of imperial governance — the rules, the [[order|hierarchy]], the relationship between ruler and ruled, all played out in the ritualized pursuit of foxes.

> "A fox came out of his earth on the hillside." (013-002)

The narrative opens with a naturalistic image that quickly gives way to moral allegory. The "little foxes" are both literal animals and metaphorical pests — the minor officials, petty graft, and procedural corruption that, left unchecked, do disproportionate damage to the colonial enterprise.

> "The whole country's stinkin' with foxes. Our trouble will be to stop the earths." (013-090)

## Analysis

Kipling's choice of the fox as allegorical vehicle is significant. The fox in English tradition is the quarry of the hunt — worthy, cunning, and destructive. In the colonial context, the "little foxes" are the small-scale malefactors whose cumulative effect is far greater than any single grand corruption.

The hunt itself becomes a ritual of order-imposition. The administrator's attempt to organize the hunt mirrors the larger project of imperial administration: imposing English forms on foreign terrain, with mixed results. The fox escapes, as foxes do — and with it, Kipling suggests, the neat moral categories of the colonizer.
