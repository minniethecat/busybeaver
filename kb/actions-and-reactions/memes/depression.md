## Definition

**Depression** in Rudyard Kipling's *Actions and Reactions* (1909) appears almost exclusively in Chapter 15, "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]," where it names a mysterious psychological [[madness|affliction]] that settles over a house and all who enter it. Unlike the modern clinical concept, Kipling's depression is an *atmospheric* condition — a "blasting gust" (015-211) that descends without warning, affecting everyone according to their nature.

## Context

"The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]" is the collection's most psychologically sophisticated story, and "depression" is its central diagnostic term. The word occurs eight times — the highest concentration of any psychological term in the collection — always in reference to the oppressive atmosphere of a particular house.

> "Our trouble is more depression than anything else." (015-015)

The hosts describe their [[madness|affliction]] with remarkable clinical precision for 1909: the depression is not a ghost but an atmosphere; it affects the young more severely than the old (015-031); it descends in sudden gusts (015-211); and it dilates subjective time so that "one can live through ages of mental depression" in moments (015-264). This is a strikingly modern description of what would later be called clinical depression, though Kipling frames it as an external, environmental phenomenon rather than an internal pathology.

> "The blasting gust of depression would return — the surest way." (015-211)

The story's resolution — in which the source of the depression is located and removed — suggests a therapeutic model: depression has a cause, and removing the cause removes the symptom. This is not psychoanalysis (Freud was Kipling's contemporary, but there is no evidence of influence here), but a practical, almost mechanical approach to mental [[madness|affliction]] characteristic of Kipling's empiricism.

## Analysis

Kipling's treatment of depression in "The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]]" occupies an interesting historical position. Writing in 1909, he anticipates several features of modern depression discourse: the recognition that depression is "more depression than anything else" (015-015) — that is, not a moral failing or a ghost but a condition; the observation that it "spoils everything" (015-041) — the anhedonic core of depressive experience; and the insight that time dilates under depression (015-264) — a phenomenological observation confirmed by later clinical research.

Yet Kipling's depression is externalized. It belongs to the *house*, not the *person*. The sufferers are not depressed people but people experiencing a depressing place. This externalization is both a limitation (it avoids the question of endogenous depression) and a strength (it captures the helplessness of being subject to an overwhelming atmosphere beyond one's control).
