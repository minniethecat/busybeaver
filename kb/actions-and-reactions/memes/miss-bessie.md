## Appears in

- [[Chapter-15]]: The [[house-surgeon|House Surgeon]] (015-512 through 015-725)

## Role

[[miss-elizabeth|Miss Elizabeth Moultrie]] — called Bessie — is the middle of the three Moultrie sisters. She serves as a foil to her formidable elder sister [[miss-mary|Miss Mary]]: where [[mary-cloke|Mary]] [[order|command]]s, Bessie falters; where [[mary-cloke|Mary]] rages, Bessie weeps. She lives with [[mary-cloke|Mary]] at the Hydro near [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] (015-439) and is deeply entangled in the family's brooding over the death of their youngest sister [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]. Baxter dismisses her as having the "brain of a hen" (015-610), yet her timid nature makes her a crucial witness to the truth about both [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]'s death and [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s own crisis.

## Characteristics

Bessie is anxious, suggestible, and easily overcome by emotion. She is "such an old maid about draughts" (015-530), fussing over open windows while her sister chokes for air. When [[mary-cloke|Mary]] suffers her crisis at the Hydro window, Bessie immediately assumes suicide — "I saw her do it, and she's cut her throat too!" (015-512) — revealing how deeply the family narrative of [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]'s suicide has shaped her perceptions. She weeps behind her veil during the journey to [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] (015-664) and is terrified to enter the house (015-678). Baxter's assessment is harsh but not wholly inaccurate: she follows rather than leads, absorbing the darkest interpretations from those around her.

## Key actions

- Witnesses [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s crisis at the open window and leaps to the conclusion of suicide (015-512)
- Has her throat (the "family throat") mentioned as a shared [[madness|affliction]] with [[mary-cloke|Mary]] and [[miss-aggie|Agnes]] (015-560)
- Receives a savage tongue-lashing from [[mary-cloke|Mary]] for her assumption (015-548)
- Weeps behind her veil during the carriage journey to [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] (015-664)
- Is too frightened to enter [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] until [[mary-cloke|Mary]] [[order|command]]s her: "Come, Bessie" (015-677, 015-678)

## Relationships

- **[[miss-mary|Miss Mary Moultrie]]**: Bessie's elder sister, who dominates and tongue-lashes her but also protects her. Bessie is "pressed in a corner" (015-664) beside [[mary-cloke|Mary]], physically and psychologically subordinate
- **[[miss-aggie|Agnes Moultrie]]**: The deceased youngest sister. Bessie took her belief that [[miss-aggie|Agnes]] committed suicide from [[mary-cloke|Mary]] (015-648), demonstrating her derivative thinking
- **[[john-baxter|John Baxter]]**: Their cousin and solicitor, who dismisses Bessie's intellect but protects her through the crisis
- **The Narrator**: Bessie shows him fearful deference, and he observes her fragility with clinical interest

## Significance

Bessie Moultrie represents the psychological cost of living in the shadow of a dominant personality and a family tragedy. Her immediate assumption of suicide — for both [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s crisis and, by extension, [[miss-aggie|Agnes]]'s death — shows how a single dark interpretation can colonise a family's understanding for years. Kipling uses Bessie's suggestibility to dramatise how the "dumb Thing" at [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]] spreads its poison: by turning family members against one another, by making them believe the worst of themselves and each other. Her eventual entry into [[house-surgeon|Holmescroft]], following [[mary-cloke|Mary]]'s [[order|command]], marks her quiet participation in the house's final exorcism of its depressive power.
