## Appears in

- [[Chapter-9]]: [[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]] (009-163 through 009-488)

## Role

Ibn Makarrah is the off-stage antagonist of "[[deal-in-cotton|A Deal in Cotton]]" — a tribal leader who "precious nearly" wiped out the British presence before the country was annexed (009-163). Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his shadow looms over the entire chapter: his men still operate in the region, any one of them potentially worth a reward (009-212), and the fever-ridden atmosphere at Dupé is connected to his ongoing threat. He represents the violent resistance to colonial [[order|authority]] that forms the backdrop to the narrator's commercial dealings.

## Characteristics

Ibn Makarrah is portrayed as formidable, dangerous, and persistent. The narrator's casual reference to "our war against Ibn Makarrah" (009-163) suggests a significant military campaign. That he "precious nearly" succeeded before annexation indicates both his military capability and the narrow margin of British victory. Even after his defeat, his men remain active — "one of Ibn Makarrah's men there might be a reward" (009-212) — demonstrating that his influence survives his military defeat. The mention of "Ibn Makarrah yonder" (009-488) at the story's end confirms his continued presence as a threat on the periphery.

## Key actions

- Wages war against the British, nearly succeeding before the country was annexed (009-163)
- Drops down on the region before annexation, establishing his military [[honour|reputation]] (009-178)
- His men continue to operate in the area, with rewards offered for their capture (009-212)
- One of his men is reported to be suffering from fever at Dupé (009-250)
- Remains a looming presence referenced at the chapter's conclusion: "Ibn Makarrah yonder" (009-488)

## Relationships

- **The British (the narrator's side)**: Ibn Makarrah is a defeated but still-threatening enemy whose guerrilla presence shapes the security calculations of everyone in the region
- **His men**: Though defeated as an organized force, Ibn Makarrah's followers continue to operate, creating an atmosphere of low-level threat that complicates the narrator's commercial mission
- **[[the-hajji|The Hajji]]**: As a local power broker, the Hajji navigates the space between British [[order|authority]] and Ibn Makarrah's lingering influence

## Significance

Ibn Makarrah embodies the unresolved tensions of colonial conquest in Kipling's fiction. He is defeated but not eliminated — a reminder that military victory does not erase resistance. His off-stage presence creates what modern readers would recognize as an insurgency narrative: a defeated leader whose men continue to threaten the occupying power through irregular [[war|warfare]]. Kipling's casual treatment of him — mentioned almost in passing, yet repeatedly — reflects the colonial administrator's matter-of-fact awareness of constant, low-level threat. Ibn Makarrah functions as the darkness at the edge of the commercial story, reminding readers that deals in cotton occur against a backdrop of violence barely suppressed.
