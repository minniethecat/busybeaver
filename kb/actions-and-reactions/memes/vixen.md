## Appears in

- [[Chapter-3]]: [[garm|Garm]] — a Hostage (003-021 through 003-147)

## Role

Vixen is the narrator's own fox-terrier and "all my dog to me" (003-064). She serves as a foil to [[Garm]], [[garm|the bull-terrier]] hostage. Her fierce loyalty to the narrator and her jealousy of the newcomer [[garm|Garm]] create much of the chapter's domestic tension and comedy.

## Characteristics

Vixen is described as fiercely territorial and jealous. When [[garm|Garm]] first arrives, Vixen greets him with "yelling with jealousy" (003-055) and flies at him. She is protective of her place in the [[home|household]] — it is her "[[tradition|custom]], till the weather grew hot, to sleep on my bed" (003-078). Despite her small size, she shows considerable courage, standing up to the much larger bull-terrier. The narrator clearly loves her deeply, describing her as "all my dog."

## Key actions

- Initially attacks [[garm|Garm]] out of jealousy when he first arrives (003-055, 003-064)
- Is grabbed and restrained by the narrator to prevent fights (003-092, 003-098)
- Is attacked by two bull-terriers while out walking, squealing in distress (003-120, 003-123)
- Retreats to the narrator's protection after [[garm|the bull-terrier]] attack (003-127)
- Interprets the narrator's [[order|command]]s to [[garm|Garm]], acting as a mediator between them (003-137)
- Is washed after the fight on the back verandah (003-138)

## Relationships

- **The Narrator**: Vixen's beloved owner, who protects her and describes her as "all my dog to me" (003-064). Their bond is deep and [[love|affection]]ate
- **[[Garm]]**: The bull-terrier newcomer who becomes Vixen's rival. Their relationship evolves from violent jealousy to a grudging co-existence mediated by the narrator
- **[[Strickland]]**: Another character associated with dogs in the Kipling collection

## Significance

Vixen represents the theme of loyalty and the special bond between a narrator and his personal dog. Her jealousy of [[garm|Garm]] highlights the emotional complexity of canine relationships in Kipling's animal stories. As a fox-terrier — smaller but [[soul|spirit]]ed — she demonstrates that courage and [[love|devotion]] are not matters of size. Her role in the story also provides moments of domestic warmth and comedy that balance the more serious hostage plot.
