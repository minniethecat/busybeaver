## Quotation

> "I like persons better than principles, and I like persons with no principles better than anything else in the world." (001-053)

## Context

This aphorism concludes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s long defense of intellectual [[multiply-our-personalities|insincerity]] in Chapter I. Having argued that "the [[the-value-of-an-idea|value of an idea]] has nothing whatsoever to do with the sincerity of the man who expresses it" (001-053), [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] abruptly pivots from epistemology to personality: "However, I don't propose to discuss politics, sociology, or metaphysics with you." The pivot is itself a performance — having made his philosophical point, he dismisses the entire domain of abstract discussion in favor of the concrete and personal. "Tell me more about Mr. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]," he adds immediately, turning from principle to person in the same breath.

The remark occurs in the same paragraph as the "[[the-value-of-an-idea|value of an idea]]" argument, making them a matched pair: first the theory (ideas are independent of their speakers), then the application (persons are preferable to principles). Together they form [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s intellectual manifesto.

## Analysis

The aphorism's structure is a rhetorical staircase: three clauses descending from the general ("persons") to the specific ("persons with no principles") to the superlative ("better than anything else in the world"). The descent mirrors the moral descent the aphorism celebrates — from principle-bound to principle-free, from good to interesting.

The preference for persons over principles is not, on its face, immoral. It can be read as a defense of human particularity against abstract moral systems — a position with a respectable lineage in Romanticism and pragmatism. But [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s escalator clause — that persons with *no* principles are the best of all — transforms the aphorism from a humane preference into a provocation. Principles, in this framing, are not merely less important than people; they are obstacles to the fullest [[pleasure|enjoyment]] of people.

This aphorism is one of the novel's most consequential. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], by the end of the story, has become precisely the kind of person [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] claims to prefer: a person with no principles. The novel tests whether such a person is actually "better than anything else in the world." The answer, written in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s blood and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own self-destruction, is unambiguous — and yet the aphorism retains its glittering power even after the plot has exposed its danger.

## Themes

- [[morality]]: The aphorism is a direct assault on the primacy of moral principle, substituting personal preference as the highest value.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s preference shapes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s development; the [[youth|young]] man becomes, in effect, a person with no principles.
- [[individualism]]: The aphorism articulates a radical individualism — the individual liberated from all abstract moral constraint — that the novel both glamorizes and condemns.
