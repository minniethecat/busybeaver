## Summary

[[james-vane|James Vane]] — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s brother, a sailor returned from Australia — tracks down "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" ([[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]) to avenge his sister's death. After a confrontation in a Limehouse opium den (Ch16) and a terrifying appearance at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]] (Ch17), James is accidentally shot by Sir Geoffrey Clouston during a hare hunt. His death in the thicket (Ch18) frees [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from the last external threat to his safety — but not from the internal decay of his soul.

## Context

Eighteen years after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide, her brother [[james-vane|James Vane]] returns to London. He has never forgotten his vow of vengeance. By chance, he hears a woman in an opium den call [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" — the same name [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] used for her lover — and realizes the [[beauty|beautiful]] [[youth|young]] man before him is the same person who destroyed his sister, inexplicably unaged.

## Detailed Account

### The Opium Den Confrontation (Ch16)

James follows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from the opium den, seizes him in a dark archway, and presses a revolver to his head:

> "You had better confess your sin, for as sure as I am James Vane, you are going to die." (016-052)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s desperate defense is his youth itself: he claims he cannot be the man who wronged [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] because that man would be middle-aged by now, while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] still looks barely twenty. James, confronted with the impossible evidence of his eyes, releases him:

> "Forgive me, sir," muttered James Vane. "I was deceived. A chance word I heard in that damned den set me on the wrong track." (016-060)

After [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] flees, one of the women from the den tells James she has known "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" for eighteen years: "He hasn't [[transformation|change]]d a bit." James realizes he has been tricked, but it is too late — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is gone.

### The Face at the Window (Ch17)

Days later, at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is entertaining guests in the conservatory. He glances at the window and sees the face of [[james-vane|James Vane]] pressed against the glass, "like a white handkerchief." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] collapses in terror. When servants search the grounds, they find nothing — but a man has been seen lurking about.

### The Fatal Hunt (Ch18)

The next day, still shaken, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] joins Sir Geoffrey Clouston's hunting party. A hare bolts from the grass; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] calls out "Don't shoot it, Geoffrey. Let it live." Sir Geoffrey fires anyway:

> As the hare bounded into the thicket, he fired. There were two cries heard, the cry of a hare in pain, which is dreadful, the cry of a man in agony, which is worse. (018-085)

The beater hiding in the thicket has taken the full charge of shot in his chest. He dies almost instantly. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] remarks on the inconvenience: "It makes people think that one is a wild shot."

Later, word comes that the dead man is a stranger, a sailor. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], seized by desperate hope, rides to the farm where the body lies. He opens the door:

> A cry of joy broke from his lips. The man who had been shot in the thicket was James Vane. (018-093)

## Aftermath / Consequences

1. **[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s Safety**: The last external threat to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is eliminated. [[james-vane|James Vane]] is buried in a "nameless grave in Selby churchyard" (020-006).
2. **False Security**: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] believes he is now perfectly safe — but it is this very safety that leads him to his final act of violence against the [[the-portrait|portrait]].
3. **Ironic Justice**: James dies not as an avenger but as an anonymous accident, mirroring the erasure of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s story.
