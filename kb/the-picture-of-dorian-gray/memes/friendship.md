## Definition

In *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]*, friendship is the primary relational framework through which the novel's central conflicts unfold. More than a neutral social bond, friendship in Wilde's treatment is a morally charged force — it can inspire great art, as [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s devotion to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] inspires his finest painting, and it can also become the conduit for [[influence|corruption]], as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]'s friendship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] becomes the vehicle for a philosophy that destroys its object. The novel examines friendship across a spectrum from disinterested worship to cynical manipulation, refusing to settle on a single definition of what friendship is or should be.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Friendship in the novel takes three distinct forms, embodied in three central relationships. The first is **Basil's devoted friendship** — an artist's worship of beauty that borders on idolatry. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "your friendship is dearer to me than any fame or reputation" (009-039), and his confession that he "worshipped you too much" (013-028) reveals that this friendship has crossed from affection into something closer to religion. The second is **Lord Henry's aesthetic friendship** — a relationship built on intellectual stimulation and the pleasure of exercising influence. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] declares that "I choose my friends for their good looks" (001-046), reducing friendship to an aesthetic preference, and tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "to project one's soul into some gracious form ... that is the true joy of life." His friendship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is a kind of experiment, a work of art in its own right. The third is **Dorian's instrumental friendships** — relationships he cultivates for pleasure, novelty, and ultimately abandons when they no longer serve him. By the novel's end, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has destroyed or discarded every significant friendship he possessed.

## Key Characters

- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: The novel's most sincere friend, whose love for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] produces great art but proves fatal. He confesses that he "put too much of [him]self" into the [[the-portrait|portrait]] (001-012), and his friendship is ultimately undone by the very devotion that defined it.
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The friend as corrupter. His witty companionship remakes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in his own image while insuring him against responsibility — "I am not responsible for the effect of my words" — making friendship a one-way transmission of influence without accountability.
- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: The friend as consumer. He moves from being the beloved friend of [[basil-hallward|Basil]] to the fascinated protégé of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] to a man whose "friendship[s] [are] fatal to young men" (012-019), treating friendship as another pleasure to be exhausted and discarded.

## Key Passages

> "Laughter is not at all a bad beginning for a friendship, and it is far the best ending for one," said the young lord, plucking another daisy.
>
> (001-043)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram, spoken in the garden at [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio, frames friendship as an aesthetic experience — something that begins and ends in pleasure. The remark is characteristically double-edged: it makes friendship sound delightful while also suggesting its inconsequence.

> Hallward shook his head. "You don't understand what friendship is, Harry," he murmured — "or what enmity is, for that matter. You like every one; that is to say, you are indifferent to every one."
>
> (001-044)

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s rejoinder exposes the hollowness beneath [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s charm. For [[basil-hallward|Basil]], friendship demands depth and commitment; for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], universal affability is indistinguishable from universal indifference.

> He had known Basil Hallward for months, but the friendship between them had never altered him. Suddenly there had come some one across his life who seemed to have disclosed to him life's mystery.
>
> (002-041)

This passage marks the asymmetry that will define the novel: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s steady friendship leaves [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] unchanged, while [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s intoxicating presence transforms him in a single afternoon. The novel asks whether a friendship that alters you is a liberation or a violation.

> "In that case, let our friendship be a caprice," he murmured, flushing at his own boldness.
>
> (002-056)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — accepting friendship on terms of whim and impermanence — is his first act of self-assertion under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence. It is also an early sign of his willingness to treat relationships as aesthetic experiments rather than moral commitments.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats friendship with the same ambiguity he brings to [[beauty]] and [[morality]]. The novel does not offer a single model of virtuous friendship but instead dramatizes the dangers inherent in each approach. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s devotion, however sincere, is possessive; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s companionship, however stimulating, is irresponsible; and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sociability, however charming, is predatory. By the novel's end, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has destroyed [[basil-hallward|Basil]], absorbed [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s worst qualities, and left a trail of ruined young men whose "friendship[s]" with him proved "fatal" (012-019).

This dark vision of friendship reflects the novel's broader critique of a society in which social bonds are governed by [[beauty|aesthetics]], [[influence|power]], and [[pleasure|self-interest]] rather than by genuine care. Friendship in Dorian Gray's world is not a refuge from the self but an extension of it — another arena in which the will to dominate and possess holds sway.

## Related Concepts

- **[[influence]]** — The force that flows through friendship, transforming it from mutual regard into something more dangerous
- **[[beauty]]** — The quality that initially attracts friends to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and that friendship, in turn, seeks to possess or exploit
- **[[conscience]]** — The foundation of friendship that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] repeatedly betrays, from [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s misplaced trust to [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]]'s coerced loyalty

- **[[morality]]** — The framework against which the novel's friendships are measured and found wanting
