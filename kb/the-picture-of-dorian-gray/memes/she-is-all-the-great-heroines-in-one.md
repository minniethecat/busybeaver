## Quotation

> "She is all the great heroines of the world in one. She is more than an individual." (004-084)

## Context

A month after their first meeting in [[basil-hallward]]'s studio, [[dorian-gray]] visits [[lord-henry-wotton]] at his house in Mayfair to announce that he is in love. The object of his passion is [[sibyl-vane]], a seventeen-year-old actress he has discovered performing Shakespeare at a shabby theatre in the Euston Road.

Dorian's description of Sibyl is notable for its complete absence of concrete detail about her as a person. He does not describe her appearance, her character, or her history. Instead, he describes the roles she plays: "To-night she is Imogen," he says, "and to-morrow night she will be Juliet." When Lord Henry asks, "When is she Sibyl Vane?", Dorian answers, "Never" (004-083). The declaration that she is "all the great heroines of the world in one" follows immediately — and it is the most revealing thing Dorian could say about his love.

## Analysis

Dorian's exclamation is a diagnosis as much as a celebration. To say that Sibyl is "all the great heroines" and "more than an individual" is to say that he does not love Sibyl Vane at all — he loves the art she embodies. She is a vessel for Shakespeare's women: Rosalind, Juliet, Imogen, Desdemona, Ophelia. Her individual self, whatever it may be, is invisible to him.

This idealization sets the terms for the catastrophe that follows. When Sibyl falls in love with Dorian and loses her ability to act — because she can no longer simulate passions she now feels genuinely — Dorian rejects her with a cruelty that leads to her suicide. "You have killed my love," he tells her. "Without your art you are nothing" (007-054). The logical endpoint of loving someone for being "all the great heroines" is that the real woman becomes intolerable.

Wilde uses this dynamic to explore the relationship between art and love. Dorian loves as an aesthete: he loves Sibyl as one might love a painting or a poem, for what she represents rather than what she is. This is the same tendency that Lord Henry has cultivated in him — to see life as material for art, people as occasions for aesthetic experience. Sibyl's tragedy is that she is loved for being a work of art and destroyed for being a person.

## Themes

- **[[art-and-life]]**: Dorian's love for Sibyl is an aesthetic love — he loves her as art, not as life. When her art fails, his love fails with it. The episode dramatizes the danger of conflating the two categories.
- **[[love-and-idealization]]**: The quote is the purest expression of idealizing love in the novel. Sibyl is not a person to Dorian but an idea — "more than an individual" — and this idealization is inherently dehumanizing.
- **[[reality-vs-illusion]]**: Dorian prefers the illusion of the stage to the reality of the woman. His love is for a fiction, and when the fiction collapses, he has nothing left.
