## Quotation

> "But it appeared to Dorian Gray that the true nature of the senses had never been understood, and that they had remained savage and animal merely because the world had sought to starve them into submission or to kill them by pain, instead of aiming at making them elements of a new spirituality, of which a fine instinct for beauty was to be the dominant characteristic." (011-009)

## Context

Chapter 11 describes the intellectual architecture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s [[duality|double life]]. Having declared his ambition to "find in the [[soul|spirit]]ualizing of the senses" his life's "highest realization," [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] now offers — through the narrator's free indirect discourse — a revisionist history of the senses themselves. Why, he asks, have the senses been regarded as "savage and animal"? Not because they *are* savage and animal, but because civilization has treated them that way.

The passage that follows this sentence is one of the most densely philosophical in the novel. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] looks back upon "man moving through history" and sees only "loss" — "mad wilful rejections, monstrous forms of self-torture and self-denial." Asceticism, in this view, is not [[morality|virtue]] but violence against the body. The senses have been starved, beaten, and degraded — and their degradation is the degradation of humanity itself.

## Analysis

The sentence encodes an entire philosophy of history in a single period. The "world" — meaning civilization, religion, morality — is the villain: it has "sought to starve" the senses "into submission or to kill them by pain." The language is deliberately violent, casting traditional morality as a form of torture. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s project is not merely to *indulge* the senses but to *rescue* them — to liberate them from their long imprisonment.

The word "appeared" does subtle work. The narrator does not say "[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] believed" or "[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] argued"; he says "it appeared to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]" — a formulation that simultaneously presents the thought and distances the narrator from it. The reader is invited to consider the possibility that what "appeared" to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] may not be what actually *is*. The free indirect discourse allows the narrator to articulate [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s philosophy vividly while maintaining an ironic gap between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-understanding and the novel's judgment of it.

The phrase "a fine instinct for beauty" names the quality that is to replace religious asceticism as the organizing principle of life. This is the novel's central (and most dangerous) idea: that beauty, not [[morality|virtue]], should be the measure of human action. The novel's plot tests this proposition and finds it fatal.

## Themes

- [[aestheticism]]: The sentence is aestheticism's critique of civilization — the claim that traditional morality has maimed the human sensorium.
- [[civilization]]: Civilization is cast as the enemy of the senses, a vast apparatus of suppression and denial.
- [[new-hedonism]]: The "new [[soul|spirit]]uality" announced here is the [[pleasure|new Hedonism]] — a religion of beauty to replace the religion of denial.
