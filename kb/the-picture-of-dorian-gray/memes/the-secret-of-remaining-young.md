## Quotation

> "The secret of remaining young is never to have an emotion that is unbecoming." (007-036)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism in Chapter 7, immediately after his taxonomy of "[[there-are-only-two-kinds-of-fascinating-people|two kinds of fascinating people]]." [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has just watched [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] fail catastrophically on stage and is visibly distraught. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s full remark is: "Good heavens, my dear boy, don't look so tragic! The secret of remaining young is never to have an emotion that is unbecoming. Come to the club with [[basil-hallward|Basil]] and myself. We will smoke cigarettes and drink to the beauty of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. She is [[beauty|beautiful]]. What more can you want?"

The context is crucial. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is experiencing genuine anguish — the first serious emotional crisis of his adult life — and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response is to treat that anguish as an aesthetic error. Not "your pain is understandable" but "your expression of pain is unbecoming." The epigram reduces emotional life to a matter of style, replacing the moral category of right and wrong with the aesthetic category of becoming and unbecoming.

This is the same paragraph in which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismissed both love and art as "simply forms of imitation." Having evacuated both categories of their seriousness, he now offers youth as the alternative value — but youth redefined not as biological fact but as a discipline of emotional self-presentation. The secret is not diet or cosmetics or even the supernatural intervention [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will later enjoy; it is the refusal to feel anything that might disturb the smooth surface of one's beauty.

## Analysis

The epigram is among the most chilling in the novel because it is, in a limited sense, true. Strong emotion *does* age the face — grief carves lines, rage reddens the complexion, despair dulls the eyes. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is offering a genuine technique for preserving [[beauty|physical beauty]]: suppress all feeling that might leave a trace. The method works, provided one accepts its premise that beauty is more valuable than emotional authenticity.

The word "unbecoming" does the epigram's heaviest work. In its primary sense it means "unattractive" or "unflattering" — an emotion that makes one look worse. But it also carries the older sense of "unsuitable" or "inappropriate" — behavior that does not befit one's station. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] fuses the two meanings: an emotion is unsuitable precisely because it is unattractive; [[morality|moral judgment]] collapses into aesthetic judgment. To be "becoming" is to become oneself — to achieve the self one presents. An "unbecoming" emotion threatens the coherence of the performed identity that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] calls youth.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will take this ad[[sin|vice]] to heart, and it will destroy him. He remains physically [[youth|young]] — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] absorbs the evidence of his "unbecoming" emotions — but the suppression of feeling does not extinguish the feelings themselves. They fester, finding expression in increasingly cruel and destructive acts. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s formula for youth is also a formula for dissociation: separate the appearance from the reality, the face from the soul, and preserve the former at the expense of the latter. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the attic is the novel's visual proof that "unbecoming" emotions cannot be suppressed without consequence; they merely relocate.

The epigram also contains an implicit class critique. The ability to "never have an emotion that is unbecoming" is a luxury of the idle — people who do not have to grieve for lost children, toil for bread, or face the ordinary brutalities of life. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s "secret" is available only to those whose lives are sufficiently insulated from reality that emotion itself can be treated as a lifestyle choice.

## Themes

- [[youth]]: The epigram redefines youth as an achievement of emotional style rather than a biological given — a redefinition that proves lethal when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] takes it literally.
- [[emotion]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats emotion not as a human inevitability but as an aesthetic performance that can and should be curated for maximum visual appeal.
- [[detachment]]: The secret of youth is the secret of dissociation — separate what one feels from what one shows, and value only the latter.
- [[aestheticism]]: The epigram represents aestheticism taken to its ethical extreme: the moral life is replaced entirely by the [[beauty|beautiful]] life.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: The remark exposes the dark side of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s charm — his philosophy is not merely witty but, for those who live by it, annihilating.
