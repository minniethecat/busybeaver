## Summary

The morning after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] heartlessly rejects [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], he notices that [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]] of him has [[transformation|change]]d: there is now "a touch of cruelty in the mouth" (008-004). This is the first visible manifestation of the supernatural bargain struck in Chapter 2 — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] will bear the moral consequences of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life while his physical body remains [[youth|young]] and [[beauty|beautiful]]. The event confirms the novel's central supernatural premise and marks the point of no return for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral trajectory.

## Context

Two events precede the transformation. First, in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio (Ch2), [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] made his fateful wish: "If it were I who was to be always [[youth|young]], and the picture that was to grow old! ... I would give my soul for that!" (002-032). Second, the night before the [[transformation|change]] (Ch7), [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cruelly rejected [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] after her poor performance at the theatre, telling her "You have killed my love" (007-070) and leaving her sobbing on the floor.

## Detailed Account

### The Morning After (Ch8)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wakes late the morning after his cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. Sunlight streams into the room. As he rises, his eye [[transformation|fall]]s on the [[the-portrait|portrait]] [[basil-hallward|Basil]] painted of him, which hangs on the wall. Something is different:

> The expression looked different. One would have said that there was a touch of cruelty in the mouth. It was certainly strange. (008-004)

He approaches, examining the [[the-portrait|painting]] more closely. The [[transformation|change]] is subtle but unmistakable — the lips have acquired a slight cruelty that was never in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s original. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] recalls his wish in [[basils-studio|the studio]]:

> He had uttered a mad wish that he himself might remain young, and the portrait grow old; that his own beauty might be untarnished, and the face on the canvas bear the burden of his passions and his sins. (008-010)

### The Realization

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s memory sharpens: his cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] was the sin that triggered the [[transformation|change]]. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] has become a moral mirror:

> For every sin that he committed, a stain would fleck and wreck its fairness. ... Here was a visible symbol of the degradation of sin. Here was an ever-present sign of the ruin men brought upon their souls. (008-014)

He resolves to reform — to marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], to live virtuously, to undo the damage. He writes her a passionate letter. But [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives before he can deliver it, bearing the news that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is dead.

### Lord Henry's Influence

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reframes the suicide as an artistic tragedy. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], swayed, accepts this aestheticized view. Rather than grief and repentance, he chooses to embrace the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s burden:

> The portrait was to bear the burden of his shame: that was all. (008-086)

He covers the [[the-portrait|painting]] with a screen and leaves to attend the opera with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — the first of many choices to hide the evidence rather than confront his soul.

## Aftermath / Consequences

1. **Confirmation of the Supernatural**: The wish is real — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] will record every sin.
2. **[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s Moral Fork**: He chooses to hide the [[the-portrait|portrait]] rather than reform, establishing the pattern for the rest of the novel.
3. **[[the-portrait|The Portrait]]'s Fate**: It will be hidden away in the locked schoolroom, growing more hideous with each passing year.
