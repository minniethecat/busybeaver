## Quotation

> "I am not worthy of him. And yet — why, I cannot tell — though I feel so much beneath him, I don't feel humble. I feel proud, terribly proud." (005-012)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] speaks these words to her mother in Chapter 5, in the shabby sitting-room of their lodging-house in the Euston Road. She has just confessed her love for [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]], the mysterious gentleman who comes nightly to the theatre and sends her flowers. She knows she loves him because "he is like [[i-love-him-because-he-is-like-what-love-himself-should-be|what love himself should be]]." But she cannot understand what he sees in her.

The line captures a paradox that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] herself recognizes but cannot resolve. She feels unworthy — "so much beneath him" — and yet she does not feel humble. Love, which should teach humility, has taught her [[vanity|pride]]. She is "terribly proud" — terribly, because the [[vanity|pride]] frightens her even as it exhilarates her.

The line is one of the novel's most psychologically acute moments. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is describing something real: the way love can elevate self-regard without erasing self-doubt, the way being chosen by someone we admire can make us admire ourselves.

## Analysis

### The Paradox of Love

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s line articulates a genuine psychological paradox. Humility and [[vanity|pride]] are supposed to be opposites, but in love they coexist. She feels "so much beneath him" — a statement of comparative worth — and simultaneously feels "proud, terribly proud." The [[vanity|pride]] is not despite the unworthiness but *because* of it: to be loved by someone so far above her is proof that she must be worth something after all.

Wilde, who understood the psychology of admiration better than almost any writer, gives [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] a moment of genuine self-knowledge. She does not resolve the paradox; she simply reports it. The stammering syntax — "And yet — why, I cannot tell — though I feel so much beneath him, I don't feel humble" — is the sound of a mind discovering something it did not expect to find.

### Class and Pride

The line is also about class. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s sense of unworthiness is not purely romantic; it is social. [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] is a gentleman; she is an actress living in the Euston Road. Her mother, who has been ruined by just such a gentleman, hears the line and grows pale. Sibyl's pride, her mother knows, is dangerous — it is the same [[vanity|pride]] she herself felt twenty years ago, and it led to the same place.

But Sibyl's pride is also a form of resistance. She refuses to be humiliated by her own love. The line is a quiet assertion of dignity: I may be beneath him, but I am not *less* than I was. Love has not diminished me; it has enlarged me.

### The Foreshadowing

The line's darker implication emerges only in retrospect. Sibyl's pride is founded on being loved — and when that love is withdrawn, the [[vanity|pride]] will collapse into its opposite. The girl who felt "terribly proud" will, within days, feel nothing at all. The [[vanity|pride]] was real, but it was also fragile, dependent on a glance from a man who barely knew her name.
