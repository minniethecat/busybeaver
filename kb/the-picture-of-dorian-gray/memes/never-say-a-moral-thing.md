## Quotation

> "I hate the way you talk about your married life, Harry. I believe that you are really a very good husband, but that you are thoroughly ashamed of your own virtues. You are an extraordinary fellow. You never say a moral thing, and you never do a wrong thing. Your cynicism is simply a pose." (001-018)
>
> — Basil Hallward, to Lord Henry Wotton, Chapter 1

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] delivers this assessment during the opening chapter's long garden conversation. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just delivered a string of epigrams about marriage — "the one charm of marriage is that it makes a life of deception absolutely necessary for both parties" (001-017) — and [[basil-hallward|Basil]], strolling toward the garden door, offers this rejoinder.

The remark is notable because it is one of the few moments in the novel when someone sees through [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s performance. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] identifies the central paradox of his friend: the man who talks of deception, selfishness, and immoral pleasure is, in practice, harmless. The cynicism is a costume, not a creed.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not deny the charge. He merely deflects it with another epigram: "Being natural is simply a pose, and the most irritating pose I know" (001-019).

## Analysis

The epigram is a masterful piece of character portraiture — of both men. For [[basil-hallward|Basil]], it reveals his perceptiveness and his affection. He sees through [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s cynicism not because he is cleverer but because he cares enough to look. The phrase "thoroughly ashamed of your own virtues" is psychologically acute: it captures the late-Victorian dandy's fear of sincerity, his need to disguise goodness as irony.

For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the remark exposes the gap between his words and his deeds that the novel never closes. Throughout the story, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will preach a gospel of self-indulgence and immoral experiment, yet he himself remains a dinner-party spectator, "summing up the world in a phrase" (001-067) while never risking anything. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s early diagnosis — "your cynicism is simply a pose" — proves prophetic.

The line also establishes a structural irony that defines the novel's moral architecture: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] talks sin but does not commit it; [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] commits sin but does not talk of it; [[basil-hallward|Basil]] alone strives for moral coherence — and he is murdered for it.

## Themes

- [[morality]]: The epigram questions whether morality resides in words or actions, and whether the two can be genuinely separated.
- [[cynicism]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] suggests that cynicism can be a form of performance rather than conviction.
- [[hypocrisy]]: The remark exposes the paradox of the man who preaches one thing and lives another — though in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s case, the hypocrisy is almost admirable.
