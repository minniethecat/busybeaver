## Overview

The yellow book is one of the novel's most potent symbols: a French novel sent by Lord Henry to Dorian Gray that becomes, in Wilde's phrase, "a poisonous book" whose influence shapes the course of Dorian's life. Though never named in the text, the book is universally identified by scholars as Joris-Karl Huysmans's *À Rebours* (1884), the so-called "Bible of the Decadence."

## Role in the Novel

Lord Henry sends the book to Dorian shortly after Sibyl Vane's death. Dorian's eye falls upon it and he cannot stop reading: "It was a poisonous book. The heavy odour of incense seemed to cling about its pages and to trouble the brain" (010-042). The novel's hero — "the wonderful young Parisian in whom the romantic and the scientific temperaments were so strangely blended" — becomes to Dorian "a kind of prefiguring type of himself" (011-001). Dorian procures nine large-paper copies bound in different colours to suit his varying moods, and the book becomes the script for the life of sensation and sin that he pursues over the following decades.


> His eye fell on the yellow book that Lord Henry had sent him. What was it, he wondered. He went towards the little, pearl-coloured octagonal stand that had always looked to him like the work of some strange Egyptian bees that wrought in silver, and taking up the volume, flung himself into an arm-chair and began to turn over the leaves. After a few minutes he became absorbed. It was the strangest book that he had ever read. It seemed to him that in exquisite raiment, and to the delicate sound of flutes, the sins of the world were passing in dumb show before him.
>
> (010-042)

## Symbolism

The yellow book functions as a secular scripture of decadence, a mirror in which Dorian finds his own darkest impulses reflected and justified. Its yellow binding — the colour associated with fin-de-siècle decadent literature — marks it as a transgressive object from the moment of its appearance. The book represents the dangerous power of art and literature to shape a receptive sensibility, a theme that runs throughout Wilde's novel and his broader critical work.
