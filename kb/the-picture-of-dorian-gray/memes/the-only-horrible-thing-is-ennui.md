## Quotation

> "The only horrible thing in the world is _ennui_ , Dorian. That is the one sin for which there is no forgiveness. But we are not likely to suffer from it unless these fellows keep chattering about this thing at dinner." (018-025)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at a dinner party in Chapter 18, during a conversation about omens and fate. Earlier in the evening, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] — who is being hunted by [[james-vane|James Vane]] — has fainted at the sight of [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s face pressed against the conservatory window. The dinner guests have been discussing the unsettling event, and the topic of omens has made the atmosphere uncomfortable.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram performs its usual function: it defuses tension by replacing moral seriousness with aesthetic flippancy. The other guests are disturbed by the talk of fate and portents; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redirects the conversation toward the one thing he genuinely fears — boredom. In doing so, he implicitly dismisses everything else as unworthy of concern.

The final clause — "unless these fellows keep chattering about this thing at dinner" — is a characteristic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] touch: the pivot from philosophical pronouncement to social complaint, collapsing the cosmic into the conversational. The real sin, it turns out, is not ennui itself but the tedious guests who might cause it.

## Analysis

The epigram is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most revealing statements because it exposes the value system beneath his wit. He has spent the novel dismissing conventional morality, but this epigram reveals what he has put in its place: a morality of sensation. The only sin is boredom. The only [[morality|virtue]] is being interesting.

The word "ennui" carries a specific nineteenth-century weight. It is not mere boredom but existential weariness — the _tædium vitæ_ that Wilde's decadent contemporaries diagnosed as the disease of modern life. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s invocation of ennui as the unforgivable sin is both a joke and a confession. He is a man who has made a life out of avoiding seriousness, and ennui is the one enemy his wit cannot defeat — because ennui is what happens when wit runs out of things to say.

The theological language — "sin," "forgiveness" — is deliberately applied to a trivial category. This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method in miniature: borrow the vocabulary of morality, empty it of moral content, and fill it with aesthetic content. The result is a value system in which being boring is worse than being evil.

## Themes

- [[ennui]]: The existential boredom that haunts the decadent temperament — the fear that there is nothing left worth experiencing.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy|Lord Henry's philosophy]]: The epigram distills [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s worldview into a single sentence: the only real crime is to be uninteresting.
- [[sin]]: The deliberate misapplication of theological categories to aesthetic ones — a signature [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] move.
- [[amorality]]: The epigram's implication that conventional sins (cruelty, betrayal, destruction) are not "horrible" at all — only boredom is.
