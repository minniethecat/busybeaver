## Quotation

> "You have gone from corruption to corruption, and now you have culminated in crime." (014-081)
>
> — Alan Campbell, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 14

## Context

Alan Campbell, a [[youth|young]] chemist and former intimate of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], has been summoned to [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] the morning after [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s murder. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has asked — and then blackmailed — Campbell into using his scientific knowledge to dispose of the body.

Campbell initially refuses. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] writes something on a piece of paper and sends it to Campbell; whatever it says, it is sufficient to compel obedience. As the clock strikes one, Campbell turns and sees [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face "filled with tears." The sight enrages him: "[[you-are-infamous|You are infamous, absolutely infamous!]]" (014-079)

When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] thanks him — "Hush, Alan. You have saved my life" — Campbell delivers the quoted judgment. It is the most explicit moral condemnation of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] spoken by any character in the novel, and it comes from a man who has known [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] intimately enough to trace the full arc of his degradation.

Campbell clarifies: "In doing what I am going to do — what you force me to do — it is not of your life that I am thinking." He is not saving [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; he is acting under coercion. The distinction strips [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s gratitude of any redemptive content.

## Analysis

The sentence's rhetorical force comes from its tripartite structure and the climactic placement of "crime." "From [[sin|corruption]] to [[sin|corruption]]" uses repetition to suggest duration — this is not a single [[transformation|fall]] but a sequence of degradations, each leading to the next. "Culminated" is the key word: it frames murder not as aberration but as logical endpoint, the inevitable terminus of a progressive [[sin|moral decay]].

Campbell's judgment is significant partly because of who he is. Unlike [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], whose condemnation of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] was always tempered by worship; unlike [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who treats morality as a category error; unlike the narrative voice, which maintains aesthetic distance — Campbell is a man of science, precise and unsentimental. His diagnosis carries diagnostic authority. "Corruption to [[sin|corruption]]" reads like a clinical progression: symptom, syndrome, terminal stage.

The line also functions as a structural summary of the novel's moral trajectory. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s story *is* the movement from [[sin|corruption]] (the first whispers of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, the wish, the cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]) to crime (the murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]). Campbell names what the novel has shown — and what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has refused to name.

The character of Alan Campbell appears only in this chapter, but his function is essential: he is the novel's moral witness, the one character who sees [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] clearly and speaks the truth without love or fascination to soften it.

## Themes

- [[corruption]]: Campbell's diagnosis charts [[sin|corruption]] as a progressive condition with an identifiable endpoint.
- [[crime]]: Murder is framed not as a discrete act but as the culmination of a longer moral trajectory.
- [[moral-judgment]]: The line represents the novel's clearest instance of explicit moral condemnation from one character to another.
- [[alan-campbell]]: Campbell's brief but crucial role as moral diagnostician.
