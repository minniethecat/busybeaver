## Quotation

> "I love scandals about other people, but scandals about myself don't interest me. They have not got the charm of novelty." (012-018)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s library on the night of November 9th (Chapter 12). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to confront [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about the "most [[mind-of-the-well-informed-man|dreadful thing]]s" being said against him in London (012-017). He warns that "every gentleman is interested in his good name" (012-019). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response is flippant and evasive — he refuses to engage with the substance of the accusations by treating them as a matter of aesthetic boredom rather than moral concern (012-018).

## Analysis

This line reveals the depth of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|moral decay]] through its very tone of amused detachment. By reducing scandal to a question of "novelty" and "charm," [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] recasts ethical judgment as aesthetic fatigue. The epigram is classic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] in style — indeed, it sounds like something [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] might say — suggesting how completely [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has internalized his mentor's philosophy. Yet there is also an edge of genuine self-knowledge here: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does have scandals, and they are far worse than any gossip [[basil-hallward|Basil]] could repeat. His dismissal is a defense mechanism, protecting the secret that he will, moments later, reveal to [[basil-hallward|Basil]] by showing him the [[the-portrait|portrait]] (012-026).
