## Overview

Dorian Gray's transformation is the central narrative arc of the novel: the journey of a beautiful, innocent young man into a figure of hidden corruption and secret vice.

The transformation is driven by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]'s philosophy and enabled by the magical [[the-portrait|portrait]] painted by [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]].

The arc spans nearly two decades — from Dorian's first meeting with Lord Henry (Chapter 2) to his final, fatal attempt at reformation (Chapter 20).

Key events include his rejection of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], his murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil]], and the destruction of the portrait.

## The Wish

The transformation begins with a wish.

In [[basils-studio|Basil's studio]], under the spell of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words about the brevity of [[youth]], Dorian looks at his portrait and makes a fateful declaration (002-093):

> If the picture could change, and I could be always what I am now! Why did you paint it? It will mock me some day — mock me horribly!
>
> (002-093)

This wish — that the portrait should age in his place — is granted by a mechanism the novel never explains. It is the supernatural premise on which the entire moral drama depends.

## First Evidence of Change

The first visible sign of the transformation appears after Dorian's cruel rejection of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]].

Returning home, he notices something different in the portrait (007-061):

> The expression looked different. One would have said that there was a touch of cruelty in the mouth.
>
> (007-061)

The cruelty he has shown Sibyl has marked the painted face. Dorian understands the true nature of the bargain he has struck.

He resolves to reform — to return to Sibyl, to resist Lord Henry's influence — but [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl's suicide]] forecloses that path.

## The Hidden Years

After Sibyl's death, Dorian embraces the implications of his wish. He will remain outwardly beautiful while the portrait bears the evidence of his life.

The narrative compresses nearly two decades into a catalogue of pursuits: [[jewels]], embroideries, [[perfumes]], [[music]], and the study of exotic philosophies.

He moves through London society untouched by scandal, his face a perpetual argument for his innocence.

Meanwhile, the portrait grows increasingly hideous — a record of every cruelty and every act of secret degradation.

The portrait becomes both a source of [[horror]] and fascination; Dorian returns to it repeatedly, comparing its ruin with his own unmarked reflection.

## Key Stages

1. **The wish** (Chapter 2): Lord Henry's influence prompts Dorian to wish the portrait would age in his place.

2. **First evidence** (Chapter 7): After Sibyl's suicide, a touch of cruelty appears in the portrait's mouth.

3. **The hidden years** (Chapters 11–12): Dorian pursues sensation and vice while the portrait grows hideous.

4. **Murder** (Chapter 13): Dorian kills Basil Hallward, the creator of the portrait and the one man who confronts him.

5. **Attempted redemption** (Chapter 20): Dorian stabs the portrait in a final attempt at reform — and dies instead.

## The Final Reversal

Dorian's final attempt at reformation — a "good" act meant to reverse the portrait's corruption — fails. The portrait only grows worse, adding hypocrisy to its sins.

In a rage, Dorian seizes the knife he used to kill [[basil-hallward|Basil]] and stabs the portrait.

The servants find a hideous, aged corpse and a portrait restored to its original beauty.

The transformation is complete: the portrait was never a separate thing from Dorian. It was his [[soul]].
