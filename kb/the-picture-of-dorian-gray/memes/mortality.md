## Overview

Mortality is the shadow that falls across every page of *The Picture of Dorian Gray*. The novel opens with Basil declaring that he will not exhibit Dorian's portrait because "I have put too much of myself into it" — art, like life, is subject to decay.

Lord Henry's hymn to youth makes mortality explicit: beauty fades, the body ages, and the only sane response is to live intensely while one can. Dorian's wish to trade places with the portrait is, at bottom, a wish to escape mortality itself (001-038).

## In the Novel

Dorian's bargain — that the portrait will age while he remains young — attempts to decouple his body from decay. The portrait becomes a memento mori in reverse: rather than reminding Dorian that he must die, it allows him to pretend that he will not.

The irony is that escaping mortality makes Dorian more obsessed with death than any other character. He causes Sibyl Vane's suicide, murders Basil Hallward, drives Alan Campbell to self-destruction, and contemplates his own end with increasing frequency.

Physical death in the novel is often indirect and ambiguous. Sibyl Vane dies by poison — accident or suicide? Lord Henry describes it theatrically: "She had often mimicked death on the stage. Then Death himself touched her, and took her with him" (008-073).

Basil dies violently at Dorian's hands, his body dissolved in acid. James Vane is killed accidentally during a hunt — "the mother snatched away by death, the boy left to solitude" (003-033). Only Dorian's own death achieves grim visibility — the portrait restored to beauty, the man "withered, wrinkled, and loathsome of visage."

Dorian studies "the corruption of death itself" in the portrait's transformations (010-011). By the final chapters, "his soul, certainly, was sick to death" (016-004).

## Thematic Significance

Wilde treats mortality as the engine of all human urgency. Lord Henry's New Hedonism is a direct response: because we die, we must live exquisitely.

Dorian takes this logic to its extreme — he tries so hard to escape death that he forgets to live. The restored portrait suggests that art, unlike life, can survive its maker. But the cost is everything that makes life worth living.
