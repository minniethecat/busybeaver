## Quotation

> "The only difference between a caprice and a lifelong passion is that the caprice lasts a little longer." (002-055)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] utters this aphorism near the end of his long conversation with [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], overwhelmed by the ideas [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been pouring into him, has just said, "I am glad now. I wonder shall I always be glad?" — to which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] seizes on the word "always":

> "Always! That is a dreadful word. It makes me shudder when I hear it. Women are so fond of using it. They spoil every romance by trying to make it last for ever. It is a meaningless word, too. The only difference between a caprice and a lifelong passion is that the caprice lasts a little longer." (002-055)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response is immediate and performative: "In that case, let our friendship be a caprice," he murmurs, "flushing at his own boldness." The moment marks [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s first attempt to speak in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s idiom — he has already begun to absorb the epigrammatic style.

## Analysis

The aphorism collapses the distinction between two opposed categories — the fleeting impulse (caprice) and the enduring commitment (lifelong passion) — by claiming that the supposedly durable one lasts *less* long. The logic is absurdist: if a lifelong passion ends, then it was never lifelong, and all passions are therefore caprices that have not yet ended. The word "only" does the work of reduction, suggesting that duration is the sole variable, when in fact the entire meaning of "passion" depends on qualitative distinctions that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is determined to erase.

The epigram extends the argument of [[all-influence-is-immoral]]. That speech promoted "self-development" through the yielding to temptation; this one dismantles the vocabulary of permanence that would give self-development any direction or purpose. If nothing lasts, then every impulse is equally valid — and equally meaningless.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s reply — "let our friendship be a caprice" — reveals the dangerous seductiveness of this logic. By accepting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s terms, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has already ceded the ground on which resistance would be possible. If love is just a caprice that happens to endure, then there is no moral difference between a marriage and a flirtation. The novel will spend the next eighteen chapters demonstrating the consequences of this collapse.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s framing of the aphorism — his attack on the word "always" — is itself revealing. "Always" is a word associated with women in his speech ("Women are so fond of using it"), and his shudder at it suggests not philosophical disagreement but aesthetic distaste. Permanence is vulgar. It lacks style. The aphorism is thus a defense of style against substance, elegance against earnestness.

## Themes

- [[love]]: The epigram reduces love to a question of duration, draining it of qualitative meaning.
- [[time]]: Time is the hidden subject — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s horror of "always" reveals a deeper horror of finitude, masked as sophistication.
- [[passion]]: Passion is presented as inherently theatrical, a performance whose value lies in its intensity, not its truth.
- [[human-nature]]: The aphorism implies a psychology in which all commitments are provisional and all emotions are performances.
