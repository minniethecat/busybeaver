## Summary

In Chapter 10, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] moves [[the-portrait|the portrait]] — which has already begun to show the first traces of cruelty — to the disused schoolroom on the top floor of his house. He wraps it in a purple-and-gold pall, dismisses the frame-makers who carried it, and locks the door. This is his first active step to hide the evidence of his [[sin|corruption]] from the world, establishing the pattern of concealment that defines the next eighteen years of his life.

## Context

The morning after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's suicide]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] noticed the first [[transformation|change]] in the [[the-portrait|portrait]]: a touch of cruelty in the mouth (008-004). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] visited, reframing [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death as a [[beauty|beautiful]] artistic tragedy. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] accepted this interpretation and agreed to go to the opera that evening.

By the time he returned home, his resolve to marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] had dissolved under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] had [[transformation|change]]d — and would continue to [[transformation|change]] with every sin. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] realized it must be hidden. He summoned frame-makers to move it, and called for the housekeeper, [[mrs-leaf|Mrs. Leaf]].

## Detailed Account

### Securing the Key

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sent his servant to fetch Mrs. Leaf, the elderly housekeeper. When she arrived, he asked for the key to the old schoolroom — a room that had been shut since his grandfather's death:

> "The old schoolroom, Mr. Dorian?" she exclaimed. "Why, it is full of dust. I must get it arranged and put straight before you go into it. It is not fit for you to see, sir." (010-004)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] insisted he only wanted the key, not the room cleaned: "I don't want it put straight, Leaf. I only want the key" (010-005). She warned him about cobwebs — the room hadn't been opened for nearly five years (010-006) — but handed over the key.

### The Purple Pall

With the key in his pocket, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] surveyed the library. His eye fell on a large purple satin coverlet heavily embroidered with gold — a seventeenth-century Venetian piece his grandfather had found in a convent near Bologna:

> It had perhaps served often as a pall for the dead. Now it was to hide something that had a corruption of its own, worse than the corruption of death itself — something that would breed horrors and yet would never die. (010-011)

He took the purple-and-gold fabric, passed behind the screen where the [[the-portrait|portrait]] stood, and examined the painted face. Though un[[transformation|change]]d in its features, the expression now carried "horrible cruelty." He flung the rich pall over the picture (010-013).

A knock came at the door — the frame-makers had arrived.

### The Old Schoolroom

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] led the frame-makers up the elaborate staircase to the top floor. At the landing, he unlocked the door:

> "I am afraid it is rather heavy," murmured Dorian as he unlocked the door that opened into the room that was to keep for him the curious secret of his life and hide his soul from the eyes of men. (010-026)

He had not entered the room for more than four years — not since using it as a play-room in childhood and later as a study. The room was un[[transformation|change]]d: the Italian *cassone* where he had hidden as a boy, the satinwood bookcase with his schoolbooks, the ragged Flemish tapestry of a faded king and queen playing chess (010-027).

> Every moment of his lonely childhood came back to him as he looked round. He recalled the stainless purity of his boyish life, and it seemed horrible to him that it was here the fatal portrait was to be hidden away. (010-027)

The irony was sharp: the room of his innocent childhood would now house the record of his [[sin|corruption]].

### The Concealment

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] directed the frame-makers to lean the wrapped [[the-portrait|portrait]] against the wall. He refused to let them look at it:

> But there was no other place in the house so secure from prying eyes as this. He had the key, and no one else could enter it. Beneath its purple pall, the face painted on the canvas could grow bestial, sodden, and unclean. What did it matter? No one could see it. (010-028)

He briefly entertained the hope that his nature might grow finer — that the cruel look might pass and he could one day show the world [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s masterpiece — but rejected it:

> No; that was impossible. ... The picture had to be concealed. There was no help for it. (010-029)

After the frame-makers tramped back down the stairs, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] locked the door and put the key in his pocket. He felt safe now. No one could enter the room. He would keep his youth while the [[the-portrait|portrait]] bore the burden.

## Aftermath / Consequences

With the [[the-portrait|portrait]] hidden, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] established a ritual: he would creep upstairs to the locked room, use a mirror to compare his un[[transformation|change]]d face with the decaying [[the-portrait|portrait]], and take pleasure in the contrast (011-032). The locked room became the repository of his secret life.

For eighteen years, no one saw the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. It grew hideous — recording every sin [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] committed — while he remained youthful and [[beauty|beautiful]]. The concealment enabled his [[duality|double life]]: by day a society figure, by night a seeker of obscure pleasures.

When [[basil-confronts-dorian|Basil confronted Dorian]] about the rumors years later, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — emboldened by years of successful concealment — showed him the [[the-portrait|portrait]], then murdered him. The concealment that began in Chapter 10 led directly to the murder in Chapter 13.

## Thematic Significance

The concealment of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] represents the central theme of the novel: the separation of appearance from reality. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty remains public while his [[sin|corruption]] is hidden — a physical enactment of Victorian society's hypocritical division between respectable exteriors and private [[sin|vice]].

The event also marks [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s transition from passive recipient of supernatural consequences to active agent of concealment. He moves from "what is happening to me?" to "how do I hide what I am becoming?" — a moral threshold he crosses alone, without counsel.

## Quotations

> It had perhaps served often as a pall for the dead. Now it was to hide something that had a corruption of its own, worse than the corruption of death itself. (010-011)

The purple pall — a funeral cloth — becomes the shroud for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul, not his body.

> Every moment of his lonely childhood came back to him as he looked round. He recalled the stainless purity of his boyish life, and it seemed horrible to him that it was here the fatal portrait was to be hidden away. (010-027)

The contrast between innocent childhood and adult [[sin|corruption]] is made physically manifest in the same room.

> He had the key, and no one else could enter it. Beneath its purple pall, the face painted on the canvas could grow bestial, sodden, and unclean. What did it matter? No one could see it. (010-028)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral logic: sin does not matter if no one witnesses it — a thesis the novel's tragic ending refutes.
