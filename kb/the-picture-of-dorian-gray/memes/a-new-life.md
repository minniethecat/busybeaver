## Quotation

> "A new life! That was what he wanted. That was what he was waiting for. Surely he had begun it already. He had spared one innocent thing, at any rate. He would never again tempt innocence. He would be good." (020-007)
>
> — Dorian Gray, reflecting on his resolution, Chapter 20

## Context

This passage occurs in the penultimate section of Chapter 20, as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] sits alone in his library after walking home through the warm London night. He has just been recognized on the street by two [[youth|young]] men — a reminder of his notoriety that now wearies him. His thoughts turn to the quiet village where no one knows his name, and to Hetty Merton, the country girl whom he has chosen to spare.

The paragraph preceding this exclamation traces [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s memory of having told Hetty he was poor, and wicked — and of her laughter in response. The narrator notes: "[[she-knew-nothing|She knew nothing, but she had everything that he had lost]]." (020-001) From this ache of loss, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind turns urgently toward self-reform: "Was it really true that one could never [[transformation|change]]? He felt a wild longing for the [[rose-white-boyhood|unstained purity of his boyhood]]." (020-003)

The "new life" resolution is the climax of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s final internal drama — a determination to be good, made in the shadow of the still-hidden [[the-portrait|portrait]]. The chapter will end, moments later, with his knife in the canvas.

## Analysis

The exclamation "A new life!" is the hinge upon which the novel's final irony turns. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s resolution to be good sounds like redemption; it reads like the turning point of a moral narrative. But Wilde has structured the chapter so that the reader already knows — and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will soon discover — that this resolution is fatally compromised.

The language of the passage is notable for its accumulation of certainty: "That was what he wanted. That was what he was waiting for. Surely he had begun it already." The word "surely" betrays the wish behind the thought — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is persuading himself, and the narrator's later revelation that "through vanity he had spared her" (020-010) confirms that the "new life" was motivated not by genuine moral transformation but by the desire for a new sensation.

The passage also carries structural echoes of the novel's beginning. Just as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speech in Chapter 2 awakened [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to the transient beauty of his youth, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own internal monologue here attempts to awaken himself to goodness. But where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s seduction succeeded, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-reformation fails — the [[the-portrait|portrait]], the novel's in[[transformation|fall]]ible moral barometer, shows only "the curved wrinkle of the hypocrite" (020-010) after his "good deed."

## Themes

- [[redemption]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit treatment of the possibility — and ultimate failure — of moral reformation.
- [[self-deception]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s certainty that he has "begun it already" is revealed, pages later, to be self-deception. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] sees what he cannot.
- [[conscience]]: The paragraph immediately preceding this passage describes the [[the-portrait|portrait]] as having "been like conscience to him" (020-011) — the "new life" resolution is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s attempt to silence that conscience without confession.
- [[vanity]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy taught [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that actions have no moral weight; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "good deed" is merely a new costume, a fresh role to play.
