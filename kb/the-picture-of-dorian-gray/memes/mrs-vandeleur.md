## Overview

Mrs. Vandeleur is a minor character who appears at Lady Agatha's luncheon in Chapter 3.

She is a neighbour of Dorian Gray at the table and one of his aunt's oldest friends, embodying the older generation's conventional morality.

## Biography

Mrs. Vandeleur is introduced as Dorian's dining companion at Lady Agatha's table:

> His own neighbour was Mrs. Vandeleur, one of his aunt's oldest friends, a perfect saint amongst women...
>
> (003-036)

During Lord Henry's discourse dismissing philanthropy and social reform, Mrs. Vandeleur timidly ventures a mild counterpoint:

> "...responsibilities," ventured Mrs. Vandeleur timidly.
>
> (003-068)

Her single-word intervention — "responsibilities" — is the voice of conventional Victorian morality, instantly dismissed by Lord Henry's rhetoric.

She functions as a dramatic foil between old and new values in the novel.

## Relationships

Mrs. Vandeleur is a friend of [[lady-agatha]] and briefly interacts with [[dorian-gray]] and [[lord-henry-wotton]] at the luncheon.

Her timid defence of conventional morality contrasts with Lord Henry's amoral philosophy.
