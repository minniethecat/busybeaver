## Quotation

> "Conscience and cowardice are really the same things, Basil. Conscience is the trade-name of the firm. That is all." (001-035)

## Context

Early in Chapter 1, [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] refuses to exhibit the [[the-portrait|portrait]] of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], confessing that he has "put too much of myself in it." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s reluctance as vanity disguised as principle.

When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] insists he cannot expose his soul to the public, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism. He reduces conscience — the [[conscience|inner voice]] of [[morality|moral judgment]] — to mere cowardice: fear of social consequences rebranded as [[morality|virtue]].

The line sets the philosophical tone for the entire novel. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will spend the rest of the story encouraging [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to discard conscience entirely.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram operates by inversion: it takes a [[morality|virtue]] (conscience) and renames it as a weakness (cowardice). The phrase "trade-name of the firm" is commercial language — morality reduced to branding. By this logic, the only difference between a good man and a bad one is the label they put on their self-restraint.

The aphorism is rhetorically dazzling but philosophically hollow. It rests on the suppressed premise that all moral action is motivated by fear of punishment or social disapproval. It denies the possibility of genuine moral conviction.

Yet the novel does not simply refute [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s story can be read as a test of this aphorism — and the results are ambiguous. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] without conscience becomes monstrous, but the conscience-keeping characters ([[basil-hallward|Basil]], [[james-vane|James Vane]]) meet violent ends.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats morality as an aesthetic category — something to be styled rather than obeyed.
- [[sin]]: The aphorism provides [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] with intellectual permission to ignore his conscience. The [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s visible decay is the counter-argument: conscience is real, whether or not it is "cowardice."
