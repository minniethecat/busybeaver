## Definition

Jewels in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* function as the supreme symbol of aesthetic materialism — physical objects of flawless, amoral beauty that Dorian Gray collects and studies as a substitute for the soul he has lost. Unlike art, which can accuse (the portrait), jewels are silent: they reflect light but not conscience. In Chapter 11, Wilde devotes extended passages to Dorian's study of jewels, presenting the collection as both a genuine scholarly passion and a desperate diversion from moral reckoning.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The motif of jewels runs throughout the novel, evolving in meaning. In the early chapters, jewelled accessories appear as markers of affectation: Lord Henry's "fat jewelled hands" (007-001) and the theatre audience's "false-jewelled fingers" (005-008) suggest a world of performance and inauthenticity.

The motif reaches its fullest expression in Chapter 11, where Dorian "took up the study of jewels" (011-017). Wilde catalogs Dorian's fascination with historical gem lore — from Alphonso's *Clericalis Disciplina* to the jewelled gloves of Henry II — in prose that is itself "that curious jewelled style, vivid and obscure at once" (010-043). The description of jewels becomes an exercise in aestheticism for its own sake: language as ornament, prose as gemstone.

The final iteration appears in the image of the woman "who must wear purple and jewels and fine linen that she may hide the pallor of her soul" (011-024) — jewels as concealment, beauty laid over corruption.

## Key Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]** — His study of jewels in Chapter 11 represents the aesthetic obsession that fills the void left by conscience. Each gem is catalogued with scholarly precision, yet the accumulation is precisely the evasion of moral thought.
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]** — His "fat jewelled hands" (007-001) and "curious jewelled style" (010-043) associate jewels with artificiality and performance. Lord Henry's prose itself is jewel-like — brilliant, hard, and morally empty.

## Key Passages

> …he took up the study of jewels, and appeared at a costume ball as Anne de Joyeuse, Admiral of France, in a dress covered with five hundred and sixty pearls.
>
> (011-017)

This passage marks the beginning of Dorian's systematic study of jewels, presented as part of his aesthetic self-education in Chapter 11.

> …wonderful stories, also, about jewels. In Alphonso's *Clericalis Disciplina* a serpent was mentioned with eyes of real jacinth…
>
> (011-018)

Wilde draws on medieval and Renaissance gem lore to lend historical weight to Dorian's obsession, while also parodying the pedantry of aesthetic scholarship.

> …who must wear purple and jewels and fine linen that she may hide the pallor of her soul.
>
> (011-024)

The novel's most explicit statement of jewels as concealment — external radiance masking internal decay.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats jewels with the ambivalence that characterizes his entire aesthetic philosophy. On one hand, the Chapter 11 jewel catalogues are among the most densely beautiful passages in the novel — prose that enacts the very aestheticism it describes. On the other hand, the accumulation is pathological: Dorian studies jewels not from genuine curiosity but to escape the portrait in the locked room upstairs.

This doubleness is Wilde's critique. Jewels are genuinely beautiful, and the erudition Dorian brings to them is real. But when beauty becomes a mechanism for avoiding conscience, it ceases to be art and becomes mere opium — a narcotic for the soul. The "jewelled style" that Lord Henry cultivates and Dorian emulates is, ultimately, a way of making language as morally inert as a diamond.
