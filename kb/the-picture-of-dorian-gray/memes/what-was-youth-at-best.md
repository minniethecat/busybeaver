## Quotation

> "What was youth at best? A green, an unripe time, a time of shallow moods, and sickly thoughts." (020-005)

## Context

In the novel's penultimate scene, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] stands in his attic schoolroom, alone with the [[the-portrait|fatal portrait]]. He has just resolved to "be good" — to leave [[hetty-merton|Hetty Merton]] unharmed, to confess his sins, to begin again.

He takes up the mirror that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] gave him years ago. He looks into it as he did on that first night when the portrait changed. Once, someone who "terribly loved him" wrote: "The world is changed because you are made of ivory and gold. The curves of your lips rewrite history."

He repeats the words to himself. Then he loathes his own beauty. He flings the mirror to the floor and crushes it "into silver splinters beneath his heel."

The narrator delivers the verdict: "It was his beauty that had ruined him, his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for. But for those two things, his life might have been free from stain. His beauty had been to him but a mask, his youth but a mockery."

Then the final assessment: youth was never what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] said it was. It was not "the one thing worth having." It was a green, unripe season of shallow feeling and sickly thought. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wore its livery — and it spoiled him.

## Analysis

This passage is the novel's great reversal. For twenty chapters, youth has been worshipped as the supreme value. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] launched the plot with his hymn to youth in Chapter 2: "Youth is the one thing worth having." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] traded his soul for it. The entire narrative has seemed to confirm that beauty and youth are the only currencies that matter.

Now Wilde pulls the rug. Youth was never the treasure; it was the trap. The adjectives are chosen to dismantle the earlier rhetoric: "green" (inexperienced, not verdant), "unripe" (immature, not fresh), "shallow" (vacant, not charming), "sickly" (diseased, not innocent). Every word is a negation of the [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] creed.

The crushing of the mirror is the symbolic counterpoint to the wish in [[dorian-makes-his-wish|Chapter 2]]. The wish was a prayer for eternal youth; the mirror-shattering is a curse upon everything that youth has meant. But it is too late. Destruction of the mirror does not restore the soul.

The passage also completes the arc of the novel's aesthetic theology. [[art-and-life|Art]] — the portrait — told the truth all along. The mirror — nature — lied. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s final act before killing himself is to smash the lie, not the truth.

## Themes

- [[youth]]: The novel's terminal judgment on youth — it was never worth having; it was the source of corruption.
- [[beauty]]: Beauty is recast as a mask and a mockery. It concealed rather than revealed.
- [[regret]]: The passage is saturated with retrospective clarity. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] finally sees what youth really was.
- [[sin]]: The "stain" that youth and beauty enabled. Without them, his life "might have been free from stain."
- [[art-and-life]]: The mirror (life/nature) lied; the portrait (art) told the truth. Wilde's aesthetic reversal is complete.
