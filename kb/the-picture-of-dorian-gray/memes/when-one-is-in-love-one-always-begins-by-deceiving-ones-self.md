## Quotation

> "When one is in love, one always begins by deceiving one's self, and one always ends by deceiving others. That is what the world calls a romance." (004-057)

## Context

Chapter 4 takes place one month after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has met [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is visiting his uncle [[lord-fermor|Lord Fermor]] when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] arrives, glowing with the news of his engagement to the young actress. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] expresses amusement rather than alarm, and the conversation turns to the nature of love and marriage.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just announced that he will take [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] to see [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] act, and describes her with the fervour of a young lover (004-056). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds with the aphorism, delivered "with a strange touch of pathos in his voice" (004-057). The remark frames [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s romance as a predictable social ritual, draining it of the uniqueness that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] believes it possesses.

## Analysis

The aphorism performs a complete inversion of the romantic ideal. Romance, which is conventionally understood as the discovery of another person, becomes in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s formulation a two-stage process of deception: first of oneself, then of others. The progression is inevitable — "always begins... always ends" — leaving no room for the genuine article.

The final sentence — "That is what the world calls a romance" — is the key. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is not making a psychological observation; he is offering a definition of a social convention. The "world" colludes in this double deception by giving it a respectable name. By exposing the name, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] claims to expose the thing itself.

The pathos in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s voice complicates the aphorism. If romance is merely self-deception leading to mutual deception, why is there sadness in the saying of it? The novel never resolves this ambiguity in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s character — whether his cynicism is a defence against disappointment or the genuine conclusion of a man who has seen too much.

## Themes

- [[deception]]: The aphorism structures deception as a narrative with a beginning, middle, and end — it is not an aberration but the plot of romance itself.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: The remark is a pure example of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method — the reduction of an emotional experience to a social mechanism (showcase).
- [[love]]: Love, in this formulation, is not a relation between two people but between each person and their own illusions.
- [[romance]]: The "world" is the silent third party that names the deception and thereby legitimises it.
