## Quotation

> "American girls are as clever at concealing their parents, as English women are at concealing their past." (003-021)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at the close of his luncheon with his uncle, Lord Fermor, in Chapter 3. The conversation has ranged across family, money, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s mysterious parentage [003-003–005]. When the talk turns to American heiresses — Lord Fermor mentions Dartmoor's desire to "marry an American" (003-014) — Henry and his uncle ex[[transformation|change]] playful jabs about English versus American women. Lord Fermor declares he will "back English women against the world" (003-016); Henry counters that "the betting is on the Americans" (003-017).

The epigram is Henry's exit line: he delivers it while "rising to go," terminating the argument with an aphorism that admits no reply. It is a social gesture as much as a statement — the epigram as farewell. Lord Fermor, unable to match Henry's wit, can only mutter a question about pork-packers (003-022).

The line participates in the chapter's recurrent theme of concealment and revelation. Henry has come to uncover [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s family history; the epigram about concealment mirrors the larger narrative of secrets and origins that the novel is about to unfold.

## Analysis

The epigram works through a structural parallelism: "American girls are as clever at concealing their parents" mirrors "English women are at concealing their past." The parallel is formally perfect but semantically asymmetrical. Concealing one's parents (a matter of social embarrassment about commercial origins) is not really equivalent to concealing one's past (a matter of sexual reputation). The wit lies in the false equivalence — the claim that these two acts of concealment are comparable, when the second carries far graver moral weight.

Henry's choice of "clever" as the operative adjective is characteristic. For him, concealment is an intellectual achievement, not a moral failing. The epigram praises deception as an art form. This is the same logic that will later enable [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to treat his hidden [[the-portrait|portrait]] as a kind of cleverness rather than a confession of [[sin|corruption]].

The aphorism also reveals Henry's own position as an outsider to the world he describes. He is neither American nor a woman; his wit depends on a stance of amused detachment. Throughout the novel, Henry's epigrams treat human behaviour as a spectacle to be observed and formulated, never as experience to be participated in. This line is a perfect example: it turns the anxieties of class, gender, and reputation into a neat syntactic equation, and the equation's elegance is meant to substitute for its truth.

## Themes

- [[society]]: The epigram anatomises the social performance of concealment — what different groups hide and how.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: Henry's method of converting social observation into paradoxical formula.
- [[paradox]]: The false parallelism is a structural feature of Wildean wit.
- [[class]]: The American "parent" problem is really a class problem — the parents are concealed because their commercial origins embarrass their daughters.
