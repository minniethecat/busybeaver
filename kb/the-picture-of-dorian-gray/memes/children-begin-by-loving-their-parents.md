## Quotation

> "Children begin by loving their parents; as they grow older they judge them; sometimes they forgive them." (005-060)

## Context

This observation occurs in Chapter 5, during the scenes introducing [[james-vane|James Vane]] and his family. James, about to sail for Australia, is brooding on the danger [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] poses to his sister [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. The narrator notes that James is conscious of "the shallowness and vanity of his mother's nature, and in that saw infinite peril for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s happiness." The aphorism about children and parents follows immediately — a psychological generalization that illuminates James's complex feelings toward his mother.

James is at the "judge" stage: he sees his mother clearly, recognizes her failings, and feels the weight of that knowledge. The progression — from love to judgment to possible forgiveness — sketches the arc of maturation that James, at sixteen, is living through with unusual intensity.

## Analysis

The aphorism moves in three stages, each marking a developmental shift. The first — "children begin by loving" — describes an unconditional, pre-critical attachment. The second — "as they grow older they judge them" — introduces moral discrimination; the child becomes a critic of the parent. The third — "sometimes they forgive them" — is the most ambiguous: forgiveness is presented as possible but not guaranteed. The "sometimes" carries the weight of all the relationships in which it never arrives.

In the novel's moral universe, this progression resonates beyond the Vane family. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s relationship with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] follows a parallel arc — from uncritical admiration to something more complex — though the novel never allows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] the third stage. Judgment, in his case, remains unsoftened by forgiveness.

The observation also belongs to the novel's broader pattern of treating psychological truths as natural laws — like [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams, it presents a contingent observation as a universal necessity.

## Themes

- [[family]]: The Vane family is the novel's primary domestic unit, and this aphorism captures its tragic dynamics — love strained by knowledge.
- [[love]]: The passage traces love's evolution from instinct to moral awareness — a trajectory the novel explores across multiple relationships.
- [[judgment]]: The shift from love to judgment mirrors the novel's larger concern with moral evaluation and its consequences.
