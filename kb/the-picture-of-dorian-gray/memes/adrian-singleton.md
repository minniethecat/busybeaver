## Role in the Novel

Adrian Singleton is a young man of good family who becomes one of Dorian Gray's companions in debauchery and is subsequently destroyed by the association. His name first surfaces in Chapter 12, when Basil Hallward confronts Dorian about the scandals attached to his name, citing Singleton as an example: "What about Adrian Singleton and his dreadful end?" The question implies that Singleton's fate—ruin, disgrace, or worse—is already widely known in London society. (012-019) (012-020)

Singleton later appears in person in Chapter 16, now a degraded figure haunting an opium den. His physical and moral deterioration provides the most concrete, visible evidence of the corrupting influence Dorian exerts on those who fall into his orbit. (016-020) (016-030)

## The Opium Den Encounter (Chapter 16)

The opium den scene is Singleton's most significant appearance and one of the novel's most powerful moments. Dorian, seeking oblivion after the murder of Basil Hallward, visits a squalid opium den near the docks and encounters Singleton, who recognizes him. Singleton is described as a young man who "rose up wearily"—his movements heavy with the lethargy of the opium addict. He addresses Dorian with a mixture of familiarity and reproach, calling him by a nickname from their former life. (016-020) (016-030)

The encounter disturbs Dorian profoundly. The narrator notes that "his meeting with Adrian Singleton had strangely moved him." For Dorian, Singleton's degraded state serves as an unwelcome mirror—a vision of what his own soul, unshielded by his miraculously preserved beauty, might look like. The fact that Singleton was given a copy of the "yellow book" by Dorian himself underscores the chain of transmission: Dorian corrupts, and the corrupted spread corruption further. (016-034) (016-043) (014-010)

## Symbolic Function

Adrian Singleton functions in the novel as a cautionary figure—a visible manifestation of the ruin that Dorian's influence brings. While Dorian's own corruption remains hidden behind his beautiful face, Singleton's is written plainly on his body and circumstances. This contrast between hidden and visible corruption is central to the novel's exploration of appearances and moral reality. (012-019) (016-034)

The name "Singleton" itself is suggestive: he is a single instance, one example among many, of the human wreckage left in Dorian's wake. Basil's question—"What about Adrian Singleton?"—implies there are others, a long list of young men whose lives have been similarly blighted. (012-019)

## Relationship to Dorian

The relationship between Adrian Singleton and Dorian Gray is one of the novel's implicit homoeretic bonds. They were "inseparable" at one point, and the yellow book—the novel's symbol of decadent aestheticism—passed from Dorian's hands to Singleton's. The exact nature of their intimacy is left deliberately vague, but the intensity of Singleton's degradation and Dorian's disturbed reaction to seeing him suggest a bond that was more than casual friendship. (012-019) (014-010) (016-043)

When Basil confronts Dorian about the trail of ruined young men left in his wake, Dorian dismisses responsibility with characteristic coldness, reducing Singleton's destruction to a matter of financial indiscretion:

> "If Kent's silly son takes his wife from the streets, what is that to me? If Adrian Singleton writes his friend's name across a bill, am I his keeper?"
>
> (012-020)
