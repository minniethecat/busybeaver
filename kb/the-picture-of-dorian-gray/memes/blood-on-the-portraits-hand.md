## Quotation

> "What was that loathsome red dew that gleamed, wet and glistening, on one of the hands, as though the canvas had sweated blood?" (014-097)
>
> — Narrator, Chapter 14

## Context

The morning after murdering [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in the locked room upstairs, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has summoned Alan Campbell — a former friend and skilled chemist — to dispose of the body. While Campbell works, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] half-opens the door to the room and sees the [[the-portrait|portrait]] "leering in the sunlight" (014-096). He had forgotten to cover it the night before.

The quoted passage is the narrator's rendering of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s horrified perception. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] has [[transformation|change]]d again: fresh blood now gleams on one of the painted hands, "as though the canvas had sweated blood." Below the [[the-portrait|portrait]] lies the real body of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], its "grotesque misshapen shadow on the spotted carpet" confirming it has not stirred (014-097).

The scene is one of the novel's most gothic moments — the supernatural [[the-portrait|portrait]] and the material corpse in the same room, the painted blood more horrible to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] than the real body.

## Analysis

Wilde's descriptive choices are carefully calibrated for maximum horror. "Loathsome" attaches moral revulsion to physical disgust. "Red dew" is an oxymoronic euphemism — dew is innocent, morning-fresh, natural; red dew is blood that refuses to name itself. The alliteration of "dew that gleamed, wet and glistening" creates a viscous, sensuous texture that delays and intensifies the revelation.

The central metaphor — "as though the canvas had sweated blood" — draws on the Christian imagery of the agony in Gethsemane (Luke 22:44: "his sweat was as it were great drops of blood [[transformation|fall]]ing down to the ground"). The [[the-portrait|portrait]] does not merely depict [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sin; it suffers it. It sweats, it bleeds, it lives. The metaphor elevates the [[the-portrait|portrait]] from symbol to quasi-sacramental object — it bears the physical marks of sin, like a stigmatic.

The scene also inverts the novel's earlier treatment of art and life. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] had argued that "an artist should create [[beauty|beautiful]] things, but should put nothing of his own life into them" (001-014). Here, the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has absorbed exactly what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has put into it — murder — and displays it with a corporeal immediacy that exceeds mere representation.

## Themes

- [[the-portrait]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s supernatural agency reaches its horrifying peak — it does not merely age but bleeds.
- [[murder]]: The painted blood is the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s record of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s murder, the crime that marks the point of no return.
- [[conscience]]: The bleeding hand is an externalised conscience more visceral than any [[conscience|inner voice]] could be.
- [[horror]]: The gothic register of the passage signals the novel's shift from social comedy and philosophical dialogue into the terrain of horror fiction.
