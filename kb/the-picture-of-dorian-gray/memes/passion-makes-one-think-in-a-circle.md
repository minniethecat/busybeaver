## Quotation

> "It is said that passion makes one think in a circle. Certainly with hideous iteration the bitten lips of Dorian Gray shaped and reshaped those subtle words that dealt with soul and sense, till he had found in them the full expression, as it were, of his mood, and justified, by intellectual approval, passions that without such justification would still have dominated his temper." (016-009)

## Context

The narrator offers this psychological observation in Chapter 16, as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] rides through the rain-swept London streets toward the [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]] of the waterfront. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is fleeing the memory of [[murder-of-basil-hallward|Basil Hallward's murder]], repeating to himself [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words: "To cure the soul by means of the senses, and the senses by means of the soul."

The line introduces the chapter's governing image: the circle. Passion traps thought in a loop. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind circles the same words, the same justifications, the same desperate logic, unable to escape. The "hideous iteration" is both the symptom and the cause of his condition — the more he repeats [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aphorism, the more trapped he becomes.

The narrator diagnoses the mechanism with clinical precision. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] uses intellect not to discover truth but to rationalize appetite. The words "justified, by intellectual approval, passions that without such justification would still have dominated his temper" are a devastating account of self-deception: the mind serving the desires it pretends to govern.

## Analysis

### The Circular Psychology of Passion

The image of circular thought is Wilde's contribution to the psychology of obsession. Passion does not progress; it revolves. It returns to the same points, rehearses the same arguments, finds the same dead ends and starts again. This is why passion is so exhausting and so unproductive: it is motion without progress, energy without direction.

The circle image also suggests a trap. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not thinking — he is circling a fixed point, and the fixed point is his own [[conscience|guilt]]. He cannot think about anything else, but he also cannot think *through* it. The circle is the shape of evasion.

### Intellectual Justification as Self-Deception

The passage's most acute insight is its account of how the intellect serves the passions. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not simply feel desire; he *justifies* it, finding in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words an intellectual warrant for what he would do anyway. The justification is retrospective — the desires "would still have dominated his temper" without it — but it is still necessary. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] needs to believe he is acting on principle, not appetite.

Wilde, who understood the seductions of intellectualized decadence better than anyone, here diagnoses its central fraud: the mind is not the master of the passions but their servant, providing elegant reasons for inelegant desires.

### The Hideous Iteration

The phrase "hideous iteration" is strikingly physical. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "bitten lips" shape and reshape the words — the body is already participating in the obsession, the mouth already performing the repetition. The iteration is hideous because it reveals a mind no longer free, a will no longer sovereign. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not choosing to think about soul and sense; he cannot stop.

This is the state in which he enters the opium den — already trapped, already circling, seeking not escape but deeper immersion in the circle.
