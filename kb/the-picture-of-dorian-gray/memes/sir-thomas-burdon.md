## Role in the Novel

Sir Thomas Burdon is a Radical Member of Parliament who appears exclusively in Chapter 3 at Lady Agatha's dinner party. He occupies the seat of honor to Lady Agatha's right, a placement that signals his social and political standing. Throughout the evening's conversation, Sir Thomas provides a foil to Lord Henry—where Lord Henry is paradoxical, ironic, and deliberately provocative, Sir Thomas is earnest, literal-minded, and easily affronted. (003-036)

His function in the dinner-party scene is to represent the established political and social order that Lord Henry's philosophy implicitly challenges. As a Radical MP, he might be expected to represent progressive ideas; yet in conversation he emerges as thoroughly conventional, shocked by Lord Henry's views and unable to counter them with anything more than bluster. (003-040) (003-041)

## Character Portrayal

Wilde draws Sir Thomas with satirical precision. He is described as looking "supercilious" when pronouncing on matters of which he understands little, as having "a large wardrobe of Humor's cast-off clothes," and as growing "rather red" when he fails to understand Lord Henry's paradoxes. His physical reactions—frowning, waving his hand dismissively, shaking his head gravely—register his discomfort with ideas that cannot be accommodated within his conventional worldview. (003-040) (003-049) (003-052) (003-054) (003-056)

The detail that he has "a large wardrobe of Humor's cast-off clothes" is particularly Wildean: Sir Thomas deals in second-hand wit, the worn-out jokes of a previous generation, and is utterly outclassed when confronted with the fresh, dangerous sparkle of Lord Henry's epigrams. (003-049)

## Political and Social Significance

Sir Thomas Burdon's presence at the dinner table raises the question of what "radicalism" means in the context of Wilde's satire. As a Radical MP, Sir Thomas is nominally a progressive; yet he is among the most conventionally-minded guests at the table. The implication is that political radicalism, as practiced by the Sir Thomas Burdons of the world, is itself a form of conformity—a set of approved opinions that do nothing to disturb the real foundations of society. (003-036) (003-064)

When Sir Thomas gravely pronounces on "the most important problem" of the day, his earnestness is punctured by the surrounding wit. The novel suggests that earnestness itself may be the real problem—a failure of imagination that is more dangerous than any paradox. (003-064) (003-078)
