## Quotation

> "Oh! anything becomes a pleasure if one does it too often," cried Lord Henry, laughing. "That is one of the most important secrets of life. I should fancy, however, that murder is always a mistake. One should never do anything that one cannot talk about after dinner." (019-027)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just asked [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] a hypothetical question: "What would you say, [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]], if I told you that I had murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil]]?" (019-024). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses the suggestion as a pose — "you were posing for a character that doesn't suit you. [[all-crime-is-vulgar|All crime is vulgar]], just as all [[all-crime-is-vulgar|vulgarity is crime]]" (019-025) — and launches into a disquisition on crime as a method of procuring "extraordinary sensations."

When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] presses the question — could a murderer repeat the act? — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds with this epigram. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the moral problem of murder is not its evil but its *vulgarity*: it is not something one can discuss at dinner. The line encapsulates his entire ethical system, which replaces good and evil with the aesthetic categories of charm and coarseness.

## Analysis

The epigram is a devastatingly honest description of the psychology of habituation. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s insight is that repetition does not dull pleasure — it creates it. What begins as [[sin|transgression]] becomes, through repetition, a familiar comfort. This is the decadent version of Aristotle's [[morality|ethics]]: [[morality|virtue]] is a habit, and so is [[sin|vice]]. The difference is that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] finds the habit of [[sin|vice]] more interesting.

The line also operates as dramatic irony. The reader knows that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] *has* murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil]] — and that the act has, in some terrible way, become part of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s repertoire of sensation. The murder is both a one-time horror and, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s logic, a potential pleasure if repeated often enough. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s silent knowledge of his own [[conscience|guilt]] hangs over [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s light words.

The final clause — "One should never do anything that one cannot talk about after dinner" — reduces all morality to the standard of dinner-table conversation. This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most extreme statement of the aesthetic life: the ultimate sin is not cruelty but social awkwardness.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The epigram describes the psychological mechanism by which [[sin|transgression]] becomes habitual pleasure.
- [[decadence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] articulates a decadent [[morality|ethics]] in which the only measure of an act is its effect on sensation and conversation.
- [[sin]]: The line indirectly illuminates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s relationship to his own sins, which have become, through repetition, a dark kind of pleasure.
