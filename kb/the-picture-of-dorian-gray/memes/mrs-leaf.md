## Overview

Mrs. Leaf is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s housekeeper, an elderly woman first encountered in Chapter 10 when she bustles into the library "in old-fashioned silk and thread mittens on her wrinkled hands" to enquire about the key to the old schoolroom (010-003). Her final appearance, in Chapter 20, when "old Mrs. Leaf was crying and wringing her hands" (020-017) after the discovery of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s body, closes the domestic frame of the novel: the servant who opened the door to the secret now witnesses its catastrophic resolution.

## Biography

Mrs. Leaf appears in two symmetrically placed chapters—the first at the novel's midpoint, the last at its conclusion.

In Chapter 10, she is the keeper of the key to the old schoolroom, the disused upper room where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] intends to hide the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. Her dialogue reveals her long service to the family: she remembers the room from the time of [[lord-kelso|Dorian's grandfather]], and she speaks of "the day the late lord was carried out in his coffin" (010-004). Her recollections establish the house's layered history and connect [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to his ancestral past—a past he is determined to escape but cannot.

> After a few moments, in her black silk dress, with old-fashioned thread mittens on her wrinkled hands, Mrs. Leaf bustled into the library. He asked her for the key of the schoolroom.
>
> (010-003)

When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] asks for the key, Mrs. Leaf's curiosity is palpable: "This here room, sir, is dusty and damp. You don't want to go in there, sir. You will only catch cold" (010-006). Her concern, maternal in tone, is the last voice of ordinary domestic care that speaks to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] before he undertakes the systematic concealment of the portrait.

In Chapter 20, after the servants have broken down the schoolroom door and discovered the body of an old, withered man beside the miraculously restored portrait, Mrs. Leaf is found weeping. Her tears—simple, genuine, untheorised—are the novel's sole expression of unmediated grief for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. In a world of epigrams and poses, Mrs. Leaf's crying is the authentic emotional response that no other character can offer.

> Old Mrs. Leaf was crying and wringing her hands. Francis was as pale as death.
>
> (020-017)

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Leaf belongs to the novel's small cast of servant characters—alongside [[victor|Victor]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s valet—who provide a ground-bass of ordinary domestic reality beneath the aestheticised world of their master. She is devout, superstitious (she crosses herself at the mention of the schoolroom), and genuinely attached to the household she has served for decades. Her wrinkled hands and old-fashioned silk mark her as a survivor from an earlier era, a living link to the world of [[lord-kelso|Dorian's grandfather]] that the novel's relentless modernity has left behind.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Leaf embodies the theme of faithful service and domestic witness. She is the guardian of the threshold between the public and private spaces of the house, and her tears at the novel's close remind the reader that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy, for all its Gothic extravagance, takes place within a real household populated by real people who loved him in their uncomprehending way.
