## Definition

In *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]*, temptation is not merely the lure of forbidden pleasures but a philosophical principle. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] redefines temptation from something to be resisted into something to be embraced, arguing that "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" (002-026). This inversion of conventional [[morality]] transforms temptation from a moral danger into a positive duty — the failure to yield becomes the true sin, a "mutilation" of the self that breeds sickness and regret. The novel thus presents temptation as a battlefield between two incompatible ethics: the Victorian morality of self-denial and the [[pleasure|aesthetic]] creed of self-fulfillment.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Temptation operates in the novel through two contrasting frameworks. **Lord Henry's philosophy of temptation** treats yielding as a form of liberation. In his garden monologue at [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio, he tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "we are punished for our refusals" and that resisting temptation causes the soul to "grow sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself" (002-026). Later in the same speech, he warns of the regret that comes from "the exquisite temptations that we had not the courage to yield to" (002-048). For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], temptation is an opportunity to be seized, not a test to be passed.

Against this stands **the traditional morality of resistance**. After [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] momentarily resolves to "resist temptation" and to "not see Lord Henry any more" (007-069), recognizing the [[the-portrait|portrait]] as "the visible emblem of [[conscience]]" (007-069). This resolution proves short-lived, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s inability to sustain resistance against temptation becomes the engine of his moral descent. The novel's darkest hour comes when [[basil-hallward|Basil]] kneels and prays: "Lead us not into temptation. Forgive us our sins" (013-028) — the Lord's Prayer deployed as a desperate last appeal that arrives too late.

## Key Characters

- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The tempter as philosopher. He provides the intellectual justification for yielding to temptation and, through his influence, becomes the living embodiment of temptation for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].
- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: The tempted subject whose entire life becomes an experiment in yielding. His trajectory — from innocent resistance to total surrender — is the novel's case study in what happens when temptation is elevated from temptation to principle.
- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: The voice of resistance whose prayer against temptation (013-028) represents the moral framework that the novel's dominant philosophy has rendered powerless.

## Key Passages

> The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it. Resist it, and your soul grows sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself, with desire for what its monstrous laws have made monstrous and unlawful.
>
> (002-026)

This is the novel's most consequential aphorism. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not merely excuse temptation — he mandates it, recasting resistance as pathology and surrender as health. The logic is seductive because it inverts the moral order while sounding like liberation.

> We degenerate into hideous puppets, haunted by the memory of the passions of which we were too much afraid, and the exquisite temptations that we had not the courage to yield to.
>
> (002-048)

Here temptation is aestheticized — the "exquisite temptations" are not base urges but refined experiences that require "courage" to accept. The framing makes resistance seem not virtuous but cowardly.

> He would resist temptation. He would not see Lord Henry any more — would not, at any rate, listen to those subtle poisonous theories that in Basil Hallward's garden had first stirred within him the passion for impossible things.
>
> (007-069)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s resolution to resist marks the novel's moral hinge-point. The passage is freighted with dramatic irony — the reader knows this resistance will fail, and the language of the resolution itself ("would not, at any rate") betrays its fragility.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats temptation with characteristic ambivalence. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy of yielding is given the novel's most memorable and persuasive lines — the reader is tempted, alongside [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], to find the argument compelling. Yet the narrative itself functions as a rebuttal: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life of serial surrender leads not to liberation but to moral ruin, murder, and self-destruction.

This tension reflects the novel's broader strategy of entertaining dangerous ideas while dramatizing their consequences. Wilde gives temptation its best possible advocate in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and then shows, through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fate, what happens when advocacy becomes a way of life. The result is neither a simple moral fable nor an endorsement of transgression, but a work that holds both possibilities in unresolved suspension.
