## Quotation

> "The past could always be annihilated. Regret, denial, or forgetfulness could do that. But the future was inevitable." (010-012)

## Context

This aphorism occurs in Chapter 10, the morning after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has hidden the [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the attic schoolroom (010-011). Having instructed his servant to lock the room permanently, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sits alone and reflects. He has just received [[it-was-a-poisonous-book|the yellow book]] from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — the "[[it-was-a-poisonous-book|poisonous book]]" that will shape his worldview for years to come (010-013) — but has not yet opened it. In this interval, the narrator gives us access to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind as he rehearses his strategy for living with his secret.

He thinks of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], who painted the [[the-portrait|portrait]] and who might have "saved him" (010-012). He recognises that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s love was "noble and intellectual," not mere physical admiration. But it is too late: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has already seen [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s momentary cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], and the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has already begun to [[transformation|change]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s conclusion — that the past can be erased — is the sophistry by which he justifies continuing on his path rather than turning back.

This is one of the novel's most concentrated passages of free indirect discourse. The aphorism is voiced by the narrator, but it channels [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own reasoning — the elegant, self-deceiving logic by which he converts moral failure into philosophical detachment.

## Analysis

The aphorism is structured as a two-part proposition, with the second clause fatally undermining the first. "The past could always be annihilated" — the word "always" suggests omnipotence, mastery. The three instruments — regret, denial, forgetfulness — are all internal, psychological mechanisms. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is claiming that the past has no objective reality; it exists only in memory, and memory can be manipulated.

But the second clause is unambiguous: "the future was inevitable." The past tense of both clauses ("could be," "was") creates a curious effect. The aphorism is presented as a general truth, but the grammar implies it has already been disproven. By the novel's end, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] discovers that the past cannot be annihilated — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] remembers everything, and his attempt to destroy it destroys him instead.

The aphorism also marks a philosophical shift in the novel. In the early chapters, Wilde's epigrams celebrate the triumph of style over substance, surface over depth. Here, in the novel's middle section, the epigrammatic mode begins to curdle. The elegant formulation conceals a dangerous falsehood. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not magisterially detached from his past; he is fleeing from it, and the aphorism is his rationalisation.

Wilde's choice of "annihilated" is striking. It is a violent word for a psychological process — to annihilate is to reduce to nothing, to obliterate entirely. The word choice hints at the violence — psychological and eventually physical — that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s strategy of denial will require. [[james-vane|James Vane]], Alan Campbell, [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]: each must be annihilated in turn to preserve the fiction that the past does not exist.

## Themes

- [[time]]: The aphorism is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s attempt to manipulate time itself — to live in an eternal present untroubled by past or future.
- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s proposed annihilation of the past is, in effect, the annihilation of conscience — the faculty that remembers and judges.
- [[memory-and-forgetting]]: The three instruments (regret, denial, forgetfulness) map onto different strategies of memory management, all of which the novel ultimately judges inadequate.
- [[fate]]: The inevitability of the future — of consequences — is the novel's answer to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s attempted escape.
