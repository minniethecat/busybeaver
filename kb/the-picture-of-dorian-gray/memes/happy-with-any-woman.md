## Quotation

> "What nonsense people talk about happy marriages!" exclaimed Lord Henry. "A man can be happy with any woman, as long as he does not love her." (015-045)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at [[lady-narborough|Lady Narborough]]'s dinner party, in response to her announcement that she intends to find [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] a suitable wife. Lady Narborough has just declared her intention to "go through Debrett carefully to-night and draw out a list of all the eligible [[youth|young]] ladies," with their ages "slightly edited." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], playing along, asks for the ages to be included, and Lady Narborough insists she wants them both to be happy.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply dismantles the very category she is invoking. "Happy marriages" is nonsense, he declares, because happiness in marriage has nothing to do with the person one marries and everything to do with the absence of love. The epigram is delivered with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s characteristic timing — the dinner-party setting, the audience of fashionable women, the casual brutality of the claim disguised as wit.

The scene is the same dinner at which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], fresh from murdering [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], has just sighed that "Life is a great disappointment." The juxtaposition is sharp: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaks from the depths of genuine [[soul|spirit]]ual exhaustion; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks from the surface of perpetual performance. Both are statements about the failure of human connection, but one is a confession and the other is a pose.

## Analysis

The epigram is constructed on [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s signature rhetorical move: take a conventional value (love, marriage, happiness), assert its opposite, and make the assertion sound like wisdom by delivering it with perfect timing. The structure — "[[man-can-be-happy-with-any-woman|A man can be happy with any woman]], as long as he does not love her" — is a conditional that inverts the expected relationship between love and happiness. Normally, love is understood as the *condition* of happiness in marriage. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] makes it the *obstacle*.

The word "any" does the heavy lifting. If a man can be happy with *any* woman, then the particular woman is irrelevant — marriage is not about the other person at all but about one's own emotional state. This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s consistent philosophy: other people are occasions for one's own sensations, not beings with whom one might form genuine relationships. The epigram is thus not really about marriage; it is about [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s inability — or refusal — to conceive of love as anything other than self-regarding pleasure.

Lady Narborough calls him a cynic, and she is right. But Wilde gives the cynic the best lines, and the reader is invited to enjoy the epigram even while recognizing the human emptiness it encodes.

## Themes

- [[marriage]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] consistently treats marriage as a social arrangement that succeeds only when it avoids genuine emotional investment.
- [[love]]: The epigram reduces love to an obstacle to happiness, inverting the conventional romantic formula.
- [[cynicism]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s defining mode — the cool demolition of sentiment that is both amusing and, in the novel's larger arc, destructive.
