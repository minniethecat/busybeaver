## Quotation

> "My dear boy, the people who love only once in their lives are really the shallow people. What they call their loyalty, and their fidelity, I call either the lethargy of custom or their lack of imagination. Faithfulness is to the emotional life what consistency is to the life of the intellect — simply a confession of failure. Faithfulness! I must analyse it some day. The passion for property is in it. There are many things that we would throw away if we were not afraid that others might pick them up." (004-043)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks these words to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] in Chapter 4, moments after his epigram on being "[[you-will-always-be-in-love-with-love|in love with love]]" ((004-039)). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has reacted with anger: "Do you think my nature so shallow?" [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply — "No; I think your nature so deep" — is the bridge into this second, more elaborate epigram.

The rhetorical situation is a reversal: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has accused [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of shallowness, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds by redefining depth and shallowness entirely. Those who love only once — conventionally understood as deep, loyal, faithful — are in fact shallow, because their "fidelity" is merely passivity. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] substitutes a psychological vocabulary (lethargy, lack of imagination) for a moral vocabulary (loyalty, fidelity), and the substitution itself is the argument.

The epigram is the most concentrated expression of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s anti-bourgeois philosophy of love. It attacks fidelity not as a practice but as a concept — a word that disguises inertia as [[morality|virtue]]. The final turn, linking faithfulness to property ("The passion for property is in it"), completes the deconstruction: monogamous love is not a [[soul|spirit]]ual commitment but a proprietary claim, and fidelity is not a gift to the beloved but a reluctance to see the beloved possessed by another.

## Analysis

The epigram builds through four movements:

**1. Redefinition of depth** ("the people who love only once in their lives are really the shallow people"). The opening sentence inverts the conventional hierarchy: constancy, usually the mark of depth, is recoded as shallowness. The word "only" does the work — it reframes singularity as limitation.

**2. Reclassification of motive** ("What they call their loyalty, and their fidelity, I call either the lethargy of custom or their lack of imagination"). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replaces the language of [[morality|virtue]] (loyalty, fidelity) with the language of pathology (lethargy). "Lack of imagination" is the key charge: in his aesthetic framework, imagination is the highest faculty, so to lack it is to be not merely dull but deficient.

**3. The central analogy** ("Faithfulness is to the emotional life what consistency is to the life of the intellect — simply a confession of failure"). This is the structural core. The form is that of a logical proposition, but the content is pure paradox. Consistency, in intellectual life, is ordinarily a [[morality|virtue]]; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] revalues it as an admission of failure — the failure to think again, to revise, to surprise oneself. By analogy, faithfulness in emotional life is the failure to feel again. The word "confession" is the knife: faithfulness is not a choice but an admission of incapacity.

**4. Property and possession** ("The passion for property is in it. There are many things that we would throw away if we were not afraid that others might pick them up."). The epigram descends from paradox to a surprisingly direct insight: fidelity is a form of ownership. The beloved is property, and constancy is the refusal to relinquish a possession. This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] at his most corrosive — not merely witty but genuinely unsettling, because the observation is hard to refute.

## Themes

- [[fidelity]]: The central target of the epigram — fidelity is dismantled as a moral category and reconstructed as a psychological symptom.
- [[paradox]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method at its purest: take a [[morality|virtue]], invert its value, and defend the inversion with an elegance that forestalls objection.
- [[marriage]]: The implied context — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own marriage is a marriage of mutual indifference, and the epigram is its philosophical justification.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: The linking of emotional life to property reveals the materialist underpinning of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aestheticism.
- [[property]]: The final reveal — faithfulness as proprietary instinct — connects romantic love to the economic sphere.
