## Quotation

> "Harry spends his days in saying what is incredible and his evenings in doing what is improbable. Just the sort of life I would like to lead. But still I don't think I would go to Harry if I were in trouble. I would sooner go to you, Basil." (009-058)

## Context

The epigram is spoken by [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in Chapter 9, the morning after [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has confronted [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about the rumours circulating through [[high-society|London society]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] in a state of distress, having heard terrible stories about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s influence on those around him. He demands that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deny the rumours and show him the soul that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] supposedly reveals.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deflects [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s moral seriousness with charm and deflection. When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] presses him, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] laughs and delivers this epigram about [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — a perfect miniature of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method, delivered in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own style, but spoken about him rather than by him. The epigram is both an affectionate tribute and a subtle distancing: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] admires [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s way of life but also understands its limits. He would not go to [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]] for help — he would go to [[basil-hallward|Basil]].

The line marks a turning point in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s relationship with both men. His admiration for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is undiminished, but his moral instinct still recognises [[basil-hallward|Basil]] as the one who genuinely cares.

## Analysis

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s epigram is a brilliant piece of characterisation that works on multiple levels. First, it is an accurate description of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method: the "incredible" things he says are his paradoxes, which sound outrageous but contain an unsettling grain of truth; the "improbable" things he does are his social performances, which blur the line between cultivated persona and genuine action. The chiasmus — saying/incredible, doing/improbable — is itself a [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]-like formal touch, evidence that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has absorbed his mentor's style.

Second, the epigram reveals [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own sophistication. He can describe [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] with the same epigrammatic precision that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] uses to describe others. He has become not merely [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s pupil but his equal — or near-equal — as a stylist. The phrase "just the sort of life I would like to lead" suggests that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has internalised [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s values to the point of aspiration.

But the third and most important level is the qualification that follows. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] would not go to [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]] if he were in trouble — he would go to [[basil-hallward|Basil]]. This is a moment of startling moral clarity. Despite everything, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] still recognises that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s charm is not the same as goodness, and that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s genuine affection is worth more than [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s brilliant conversation. The tragedy of the novel is that this recognition does not save him.

## Themes

- [[friendship]]: The epigram maps the novel's central triangular relationship, with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] as the fascinating but unreliable friend and [[basil-hallward|Basil]] as the true one.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s description captures [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method — the division of life into witty speech and improbable action — with affectionate precision.
- [[epigram]]: The epigram is itself an epigram about epigrams, demonstrating how thoroughly [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has absorbed [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s mode of speech.
- [[appearance-and-reality]]: The distinction between "saying" and "doing" mirrors the novel's broader concern with the gap between surface and substance.
