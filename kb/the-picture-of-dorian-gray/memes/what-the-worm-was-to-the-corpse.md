## Quotation

> "What the worm was to the corpse, his sins would be to the painted image on the canvas. They would mar its beauty and eat away its grace. They would defile it and make it shameful. And yet the thing would still live on. It would be always alive." (010-011)

## Context

Dorian has just obtained the key to the old schoolroom and is preparing to conceal [[the-portrait|the portrait]] there. As he looks around the library for something to wrap the painting in, his eye falls on a purple satin coverlet — a Venetian piece his grandfather had found in a convent. The coverlet, which "had perhaps served often as a pall for the dead," prompts this dark meditation on what his sins will do to the painted image. The passage occurs early in Chapter 10, immediately after Dorian has decided to hide the portrait from all eyes, including his own.

## Analysis

The worm-corpse simile is one of the novel's most concentrated images of moral decay made visible. Wilde inverts the natural order: in nature, worms consume the dead; here, sins consume the living image, which paradoxically "would never die." The portrait is both more vulnerable than a corpse — it can be marred, defiled, made shameful — and more enduring, forever alive to bear witness.

The passage's rhetorical structure moves from the organic (worm, corpse) to the aesthetic (beauty, grace) to the moral (defile, shameful), tracing the path by which sin becomes visible. The three verbs — *mar*, *eat away*, *defile* — escalate from surface damage to consumption to moral contamination. Yet the final sentence reverses the trajectory: "the thing would still live on. It would be always alive." The portrait achieves a grotesque immortality precisely through its corruption.

This is also the moment when Dorian fully understands the nature of his bargain. The "painted image" is not merely a record of his sins but their victim — and their eternal accuser. The worm metaphor casts Dorian's sins as parasites feeding on the portrait's beauty, with the horrifying implication that the portrait will never find the release of death.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: The passage defines sin as a corrosive, visible force that consumes beauty from within.
- [[corruption]]: The worm metaphor makes corruption tangible — a process of eating away, defiling, and shaming.
- [[the-portrait]]: The portrait is established as both victim and accuser, the permanent record of sins that "would never die."
