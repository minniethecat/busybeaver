## Quotation

> "The dead linger sometimes. The man upstairs will not go away. He is sitting at the table with bowed head and outstretched arms. If you don't come to my assistance, I am ruined. Why, they will hang me, Alan! Don't you understand? They will hang me for what I have done." (014-057)
>
> — Dorian Gray, pleading with Alan Campbell, Chapter 14

## Context

This passage comes from the long, desperate scene in Chapter 14 in which [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] attempts to persuade Alan Campbell — a chemist and former friend — to dispose of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s body. Campbell has arrived at [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] and has just refused, emphatically, to have anything to do with the matter: "I will have absolutely nothing to do with it. I don't care what shame comes on you." (014-050)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has already admitted to Campbell that the death was "murder" (014-051), and has tried and failed to persuade him on scientific grounds — arguing that Campbell routinely dissects bodies in hospitals and that destroying this one is merely "a certain scientific experiment" (014-053). Campbell remains unmoved.

It is at this point that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] shifts rhetorical registers. Campbell has just said "Don't speak about those days, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — they are dead" (014-056), referring to their former friendship. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] seizes on the word "dead" and transforms it: the dead are not dead; they linger. The line functions both as a literal description — [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s body is still sitting upstairs — and as a metaphysical truth that the novel has been building toward since Chapter 1.

## Analysis

"The dead linger sometimes" is one of the novel's most concentrated thematic statements. On its surface, it is a desperate man's plea for practical help — the body will be discovered; he will hang. But the sentence radiates outward into the novel's deepest concerns.

First, it literalizes the metaphor that the novel has been developing since Chapter 1: the past does not pass. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s body is the physical embodiment of a truth the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has been illustrating symbolically for the entire novel — what is done cannot be undone; what is hidden will eventually surface.

Second, the line reverses the power dynamic of the scene. Until this moment, Campbell has held the moral high ground, refusing to be complicit. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s phrase "the dead linger sometimes" is a threat wrapped in a metaphysical observation — it implies that Campbell, too, is implicated in the world of consequences, that his refusal to act will not insulate him from what is to come.

The shift from the philosophical "the dead linger sometimes" to the panicked "They will hang me, Alan!" — from aphorism to desperation — captures [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s characteristic oscillation between [[to-become-the-spectator-of-ones-own-life|aesthetic detachment]] and raw terror. Even in extremis, he cannot resist the well-turned phrase.

## Themes

- [[murder]]: The passage is the most direct confrontation in the novel with the practical consequences of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s crime.
- [[guilt]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s terror of hanging is terror not of [[morality|moral judgment]] but of social consequences — a distinction the novel insists upon.
- [[the-past]]: "The dead linger sometimes" is the novel's thesis statement about the inescapability of the past.
- [[consequences]]: The scene dramatizes what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy has denied: that actions have inescapable consequences.
