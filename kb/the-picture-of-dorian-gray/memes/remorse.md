## Definition

Remorse is the emotional suffering that accompanies the recognition of one's own wrongdoing.

In the novel, it appears in two opposing lights: as the natural consequence of sin that drives the conscience (018-002), and as a painful state that characters seek to numb or escape (008-017).

## Lord Henry's View

Lord Henry dismisses remorse as the price of self-denial, suggesting it can be medicated away:

> There were opiates for remorse, drugs that could lull the moral sense to sleep.
>
> (008-017)

Basil Hallward, in contrast, believes remorse is the inevitable cost of wrongdoing:

> "One has to pay in other ways but money." — "What sort of ways, Basil?" — "Oh! I should fancy in remorse, in suffering, in ... well, in the consciousness of degradation."
>
> (006-044—045)

## Dorian's Experience

Dorian's relationship to remorse is complex. Lord Henry imagines finding him "plunged in remorse and tearing that nice curly hair" (008-027), yet Dorian consistently chooses to suppress rather than act on his guilt.

The novel ultimately frames remorse as a function of imagination and conscience:

> It was the imagination that set remorse to dog the feet of sin.
>
> (018-002)
