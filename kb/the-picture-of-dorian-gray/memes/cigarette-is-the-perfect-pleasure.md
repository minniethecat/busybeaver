## Quotation

> "A cigarette is the perfect type of a perfect pleasure. It is exquisite, and it leaves one unsatisfied. What more can one want?" (006-055)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton]] delivers this miniature aestheticist manifesto at a lunch with [[dorian-gray]] and [[basil-hallward]] in Chapter 6. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just announced his engagement to [[sibyl-vane]], and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been holding forth on the nature of pleasure and the philosophy of the new [[pleasure|hedonism]].

The immediate occasion is mundane — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is offering cigarettes after coffee. But the remark encapsulates an entire philosophy. The cigarette is the perfect pleasure precisely because it is self-consuming, transient, never fully satisfying. Its perfection lies in what it lacks: permanence, fulfillment, satiety.

The line that follows in the same paragraph is equally significant: "I represent to you all the sins you have never had the courage to commit." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aestheticism is not merely about pleasure — it is about [[sin|transgression]], about the pleasures that convention forbids.

## Analysis

### The Aesthetics of Unsatisfied Desire

The paradox at the heart of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram is an inversion of conventional wisdom. [[pleasure|Pleasure]] is normally understood as satisfaction — the fulfillment of desire, the end of wanting. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines pleasure as the state of being unsatisfied. A pleasure that leaves one content is, in his terms, imperfect because it terminates the process of desiring. The cigarette, which must be lit again and again, models a [[pleasure|hedonism]] of eternal incompletion.

### The New Hedonism in Miniature

The epigram distills the philosophy [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been expounding to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the "[[pleasure|new Hedonism]]" that would "re-create life" and "save it from that harsh, uncomely puritanism." In this philosophy, pleasure is not a sequence of satisfactions but an art of cultivated dissatisfaction. One should never be done wanting, because wanting is the only genuine pleasure. Fulfillment is death.

### The Sins You Have Never Had the Courage to Commit

The cigarette epigram is followed immediately by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s self-description: "I represent to you all the sins you have never had the courage to commit." This coupling is revealing. The "perfect pleasure" of the cigarette is not innocent — it is the model for sin itself. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] offers [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a [[pleasure|hedonism]] of [[sin|transgression]] dressed in the language of aesthetics. The cigarette's "exquisite" quality is precisely the quality he attributes to forbidden experience.
