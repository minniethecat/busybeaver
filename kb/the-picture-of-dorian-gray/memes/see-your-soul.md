## Quotation

> "Know you? I wonder do I know you? Before I could answer that, I should have to see your soul." (012-023)

And its reply:

> "Yes," answered Hallward gravely, and with deep-toned sorrow in his voice, "to see your soul. But only God can do that." (012-025)

## Context

In Chapter 12, [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has been enumerating the scandals attached to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s name — the ruined reputations, the destroyed careers, the "dreadful houses" (012-022). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deflects each charge with contempt for [[native-land-of-the-hypocrite|English hypocrisy]]. But when [[basil-hallward|Basil]] mentions a letter from Lord Gloucester's dying wife — "Your name was implicated in the most terrible confession I ever read" (012-022) — he reaches a personal breaking point.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] says he defended [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], insisting he knew him too well to believe such things. But then he stops, and the doubt surfaces: "Know you? I wonder do I know you?" The question is both rhetorical and genuine — an admission that the face [[basil-hallward|Basil]] painted and the man [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has become may no longer be the same person.

The phrase "see your soul" is the pivot of the entire chapter. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], hearing it, "start[s] up from the sofa and turn[s] almost white from fear" (012-024). He recognizes that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has named the one thing he can never allow: authentic moral scrutiny. When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] adds that "only God can do that" (012-025), [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fear transforms into a "bitter laugh of mockery" (012-026) and he declares: "You shall see it yourself, to-night!" (012-026). The invitation to view the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is the prelude to murder.

## Analysis

The line operates as a moment of tragic dramatic irony. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] speaks of seeing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul as a metaphor — an impossibility, something reserved for divine judgment. But the reader knows, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] knows, that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] *is* his soul made visible. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] offers to show it, the literal meaning collides with the metaphorical, and the collision is fatal.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s theological reference — "only God can do that" — is the one genuinely religious sentiment in a novel largely indifferent to orthodox belief. It frames the [[the-portrait|portrait]] as a blasphemous object: something that does what only God should be able to do. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] not only records sin but makes it visible, collapsing the distance between inner [[sin|corruption]] and outer appearance that conventional morality depends on.

The repetition of "see your soul" across two paragraphs (PN 012-023 and 012-025) gives the phrase ritual weight. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] echoes it — "To see my soul!" (012-024) — and the triple iteration turns what began as a figure of speech into an irreversible commitment.

## Themes

- [[the-portrait]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]] is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul rendered visible — [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s metaphor becomes literal fact, with fatal consequences.
- [[sin-and-conscience]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s desire to "see" [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul represents the return of moral scrutiny that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[duality|double life]] was designed to evade.
- [[appearance-and-reality]]: The line crystallizes the novel's central paradox: that appearance and reality, normally separate, have been forcibly fused in the [[the-portrait|portrait]].
