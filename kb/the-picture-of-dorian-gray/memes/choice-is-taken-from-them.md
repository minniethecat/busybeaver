## Quotation

> "Choice is taken from them, and conscience is either killed, or, if it lives at all, lives but to give rebellion its fascination and disobedience its charm." (016-044)

## Context

This line is not spoken by any character — it is the narrator's psychological commentary, delivered as [[dorian-gray]] hastens through the dark streets toward an opium den in Chapter 16. The paragraph it introduces is one of the novel's most explicit statements about the psychology of sin.

The narrator invokes "psychologists" as authority: at certain moments, the passion for sin so dominates a nature that men and women "lose the freedom of their will" and "move to their terrible end as automatons move." The quoted sentence is the climax of this diagnosis — choice is removed, and conscience, instead of guiding, becomes an accomplice to rebellion.

Immediately after this reflection, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is seized from behind by [[james-vane]], who has been tracking him for months. The narrator's meditation on automatism and lost free will thus frames the novel's most direct moment of physical consequence for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins.

## Analysis

### The Death of Conscience

The sentence imagines two possible fates for conscience under sin's dominion. The first is simple: conscience is "killed" — extinguished, silenced, no longer operative. The second is subtler and more dangerous: conscience survives but is perverted, living only "to give rebellion its fascination and disobedience its charm." In this second state, the sinner knows the wrongness of his acts but experiences that knowledge as a heightening of pleasure. [[dorian-gray]] belongs to this second category — his conscience is not dead but corrupted, making his sins more exquisite by their very [[sin|transgression]].

### Sin as Automatism

The framing of sin as automatic — men and women "move to their terrible end as automatons move" — is a radical psychological claim. It denies the sinner agency precisely at the moment of greatest moral culpability. This paradox runs through the novel: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is both fully responsible for his crimes and, in some sense, unable to stop. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the attic is the mechanism Wilde uses to externalize this inner automatism — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] watches himself deteriorate without being able to arrest the process.

### Theological Echo

The narrator reinforces the psychological claim with a theological one: "all sins, as theologians weary not of reminding us, are sins of disobedience." The reference to Lucifer's [[transformation|fall]] — "when that high [[soul|spirit]], that morning star of evil, fell from heaven, it was as a rebel that he fell" — elevates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s opium-den errand into a cosmic pattern. [[sin|Sin]] is not merely [[sin|transgression]]; it is rebellion against an order, and rebellion carries its own dark charisma.
