## Quotation

> "The basis of optimism is sheer terror. We think that we are generous because we credit our neighbour with the possession of those virtues that are likely to be a benefit to us." (006-021)

## Context

The epigram is part of a long speech [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers at a restaurant to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], who has just accused him of not meaning anything he says. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has objected to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s apparent approval of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], calling it "some silly infatuation." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds with a cascade of paradoxes about marriage, individuality, and the value of experience.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] interrupts: "You don't mean a single word of all that, [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]]; you know you don't. If [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s life were spoiled, no one would be sorrier than yourself. You are much better than you pretend to be." It is this — the suggestion that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is secretly kind — that triggers the passage. He denies it not by asserting his cruelty but by redescribing kindness as a disguised form of self-interest. The epigram about optimism is the hinge of that argument: we praise the banker to overdraw our account, find [[morality|virtue]] in the highwayman to spare our pockets. Optimism is not warmth of character; it is a survival strategy dressed in moral clothing.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] arrives moments later, flushed with the news of his engagement, and the philosophical argument is interrupted by life.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram is a work of moral alchemy that turns the base metal of self-interest into the gold of kindness — and then reverses the formula to show the base metal was there all along. His argument is that [[morality|virtue]]-words like "generosity" and "optimism" are not descriptions of character but tactical self-descriptions. We call ourselves generous when we are actually making a down payment on future favours from others. We call ourselves optimists when we are actually afraid of what would happen if we admitted our fears.

The structure of the passage follows a pattern typical of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speeches: a provocative thesis ("the basis of optimism is sheer terror"), a psychological explanation ("we [[credit-is-the-capital-of-a-younger-son|credit]] our neighbour with [[morality|virtue]]s likely to be a benefit to us"), and a series of concrete illustrations (the banker, the highwayman) that make the abstract claim feel like a matter of common observation. The progression from "terror" to "generosity" to "self-interest" systematically dismantles the moral vocabulary [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just deployed.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s accusation — "You are much better than you pretend to be" — is itself an example of the very psychology [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is describing. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] *wants* [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] to be better than he appears, because [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s own worldview depends on the existence of genuine goodness. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply says, in effect: your faith in me is just another form of self-protection. The circle is closed.

## Themes

- [[optimism]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats optimism not as a [[morality|virtue]] but as a symptom — a psychological defence against the fear that other people are not on your side.
- [[self-interest]]: The passage models a reductive psychology in which all apparent altruism is decoded as disguised egoism, a mode of analysis characteristic of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s worldview.
- [[morality]]: The epigram destabilizes moral language itself, treating terms like "generous" and "good" as strategic fictions rather than descriptions of reality.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] uses this kind of psychological unmasking as a form of seduction — by dissolving [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s categories, he clears space for his own.
