## Definition

Innocence in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* is the quality of being untouched by sin, corruption, or the knowledge of evil. It is presented not as a virtue to be cultivated but as a fragile state inevitably lost through experience. The novel traces innocence's destruction — in Sibyl Vane, in Dorian himself — and suggests that its loss is irreversible.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The narrator describes how certain pleasures "recall to them the memory of the innocence that they had tarnished" (011-003), suggesting that the memory of innocence persists as a form of torment. Even those who have abandoned virtue cannot forget what they have lost.

The theme reaches its fullest expression in the final chapter. After a lifetime of sin, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] resolves: "He would never again tempt innocence. He would be good" (020-007). This resolution — to spare others the corruption he has undergone — is his final, futile gesture toward redemption. The novel leaves ambiguous whether innocence, once lost, can ever be regained.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: Begins the novel as innocence personified — "a young man of extraordinary personal beauty" untouched by the world. Lord Henry's philosophy and his own choices destroy this innocence systematically.
- **[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]**: Represents innocence in its artistic form. Her love for Dorian is pure, and her death is the novel's first sacrifice of innocence to experience.
- **[[hetty-merton|Hetty Merton]]**: The village girl Dorian spares in Chapter 20. She represents innocence that Dorian, for once, chooses not to destroy — though the novel questions whether this choice is truly moral or merely another form of vanity.

## Key Passages

> He felt a wild longing for the unstained purity of his boyhood — his rose-white boyhood, as Lord Henry had once called it. (020-003)

Dorian's final, desperate nostalgia for innocence. The passage acknowledges that innocence is not merely a moral condition but an aesthetic one: it was beautiful, and its loss is ugly.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats innocence as a fundamentally aesthetic category. It is valuable not because it is morally good but because it is beautiful — and its loss is tragic not because it is sinful but because it is ugly. This is consistent with the novel's broader aesthetic philosophy: moral categories are less real, less urgent, than aesthetic ones. The pathos of lost innocence is the pathos of beauty destroyed.
