## Definition

The artist-model relationship in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* transcends professional arrangement to become the novel's foundational human dynamic. Basil Hallward's relationship with Dorian Gray as his model and sitter is one of artistic worship, emotional dependency, and ultimately fatal vulnerability. Wilde uses this relationship to explore questions of creative possession, the power imbalance between creator and subject, and the dangerous intimacy of portraiture.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Basil's confession to Lord Henry establishes the extraordinary nature of his attachment to Dorian. He tells Lord Henry that "he is much more to me than a model or a sitter" (001-056), declaring that "my life as an artist depends on him" (001-080). This dependency inverts the expected power relationship: the artist, who should control the representation, is instead controlled by his subject.

The dynamics of the sitting itself reveal the intimacy of the artist-model relationship. Basil tells Dorian that he likes "his sitters to have some one to chat to" (002-014), and apologizes for being "horribly tedious for my unfortunate sitters" (002-018). These domestic details ground the relationship in the practical reality of long hours in the studio, during which Basil's emotional investment deepened beyond professional bounds.

Wilde's most famous statement on the artist-model relationship appears through Basil: "Every portrait that is painted with feeling is a portrait of the artist, not of the sitter. The sitter is merely the accident, the occasion" (001-027). This aesthetic credo contains the novel's central irony—Basil's portrait of Dorian is indeed a portrait of the artist's soul, which is precisely why it becomes the instrument of his destruction.

Lord Henry recognizes the danger immediately, observing that Dorian is "a capital sitter" (002-004) and warning Basil that his worship of his subject is "horribly unjust" (001-045). As an outsider to the artist-model bond, Lord Henry sees what Basil cannot: that the artist has surrendered too much of himself to his creation.

## Key Characters

- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: The archetypal artist whose devotion to his model transcends the professional and becomes worship. His confession that he has shown "the secret of my own soul" (001-027) in the portrait makes explicit what the artist-model relationship has cost him.
- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: The model who, once conscious of his power over the artist, becomes the dominant partner. His beauty makes him the ideal sitter at the novel's opening; his corruption makes him the impossible subject by its close.
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The spectator to the artist-model relationship who recognizes its danger and intervenes to claim the model for himself—redirecting Dorian from Basil's worship to his own philosophy.

## Key Passages

The artist's confession that the portrait reveals himself, not his subject:

> Every portrait that is painted with feeling is a portrait of the artist, not of the sitter. The sitter is merely the accident, the occasion.
>
> (001-027)

Basil's declaration of the model's importance beyond the professional:

> He is much more to me than a model or a sitter. I won't tell you that I am devoted to him. I won't tell you that I adore him.
>
> (001-056)

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde inverts the conventional power dynamic of the artist-model relationship. The artist, who should hold creative and social power, is instead spiritually dependent on the model. The model, who should be passive, acquires the power to destroy the artist—first through emotional withdrawal, then through literal murder. This inversion reflects Wilde's broader interest in the relationship between art and life: when the model becomes more powerful than the artist, life has triumphed over art, with tragic consequences. The Preface's pronouncement that "to reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim" (P02-001) is precisely what Basil fails to achieve—he has revealed the artist, and the result is catastrophe.
