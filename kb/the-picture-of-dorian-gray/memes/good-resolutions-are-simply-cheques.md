## Quotation

> "Good resolutions are useless attempts to interfere with scientific laws. Their origin is pure vanity. Their result is absolutely _nil_. They give us, now and then, some of those luxurious sterile emotions that have a certain charm for the weak. That is all that can be said for them. They are simply cheques that men draw on a bank where they have no account." (008-047)

## Context

Lord Henry delivers this aphorism near the end of the long conversation in Chapter 8, the morning after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane]]'s death. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been wracked by guilt — he has looked at [[the-portrait|the portrait]], seen its first change, resolved to marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], then learned she is dead. He has written a passionate letter of self-reproach and for a moment felt "forgiven" (008-018).

But [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has spent the chapter methodically dismantling Dorian's guilt. He has reframed Sibyl's death as a "wonderful ending" to a "wonderful play" (008-031). He has assured Dorian that women "never have anything to say, but they say it charmingly" (008-043). When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] protests that he thought it was his "duty" to marry Sibyl, Lord Henry delivers this verdict on good resolutions in their entirety.

## Analysis

The metaphor is financial: good resolutions are "cheques" — promises of future payment. But the bank (character, will, moral capacity) holds no funds. The resolution is therefore not merely futile but fraudulent — a promise the issuer knows cannot be honoured, made to a payee (oneself, God, society) who will never check the account.

Lord Henry's argument proceeds through three stages. First, he declares good resolutions "useless attempts to interfere with scientific laws" — casting morality as a kind of physics, immune to human intention. Second, he diagnoses their origin as "pure vanity" — the desire to think well of oneself. Third, he acknowledges their emotional function: they produce "luxurious sterile emotions," feelings that feel meaningful but produce nothing. The word "sterile" links resolutions to the aesthetic realm — they are art without creation, sensation without consequence.

The aphorism is seductive because it contains a grain of uncomfortable truth. Dorian's resolution to marry Sibyl was indeed too late, indeed self-serving, indeed a "cheque" on an empty account. But Lord Henry's cynicism is itself a kind of resolution — a resolution never to make resolutions, which is just as sterile as the thing it mocks.

## Themes

- [[good-resolutions]]: The passage is the novel's most concentrated critique of moral resolve, reducing it to a psychological self-indulgence.
- [[vanity]]: Lord Henry traces good intentions to vanity — they exist to make us feel virtuous, not to make us act virtuously.
- [[morality]]: Conventional morality is presented as a set of "scientific laws" that human will cannot alter — a deterministic worldview that excuses inaction.
- [[self-deception]]: The "cheque" metaphor exposes moral language as a system of self-deception, where promises are made precisely because they will never be called in.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: This aphorism crystallises Lord Henry's role as the novel's anti-moralist — his philosophy is not an alternative morality but a rejection of morality as a category.
