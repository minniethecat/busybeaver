## Quotation

> "Courage has passed from men to women. It is a new experience for us." (017-093)
>
> — The Duchess of Monmouth, to Lord Henry Wotton, Chapter 17

## Context

This remark occurs during the extended verbal duel between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and the Duchess of Monmouth at Lady Narborough's tea-table in Chapter 17. The ex[[transformation|change]] has been escalating in intimacy and wit. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just delivered his epigram "[[a-burnt-child-loves-the-fire|That a burnt child loves the fire]]" (017-090), and the Duchess has parried: "I am not even singed. My wings are untouched." (017-091)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] counters that she uses her wings "for everything, except flight" (017-092) — a clever but patronising suggestion that she talks boldly but never actually commits. The Duchess's response here is a masterstroke: rather than defend herself, she shifts the terms of debate entirely. Courage is no longer a personal quality but a historical force, and its migration from men to women is presented as a fait accompli. The closing clause — "It is a new experience for us" — is both self-deprecating (women are new to courage) and triumphant (they now possess it).

## Analysis

This is one of the novel's few explicitly feminist moments, delivered not by a narrator but by a female character who consistently holds her own against the most formidable conversationalist in the book. The Duchess reframes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s personal barb as evidence of a broader cultural shift: men have lost the courage they once monopolised, and women have inherited it.

The word "passed" is significant — it suggests a natural, almost evolutionary transfer, not a violent seizure. Courage is not stolen; it migrates. This framing deprives men of the heroic narrative of loss and women of the accusatory narrative of theft. The Duchess's tone is observational rather than polemical.

The line also subtly critiques [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] himself. His epigrams are acts of verbal courage — daring, paradoxical, provocative — but they are precisely that: *verbal*. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] never acts. He talks. The Duchess, by naming courage as a feminine possession, implies that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s wit is a substitute for, not an expression of, genuine courage.

Structurally, the line participates in the chapter's gender dynamics. Lady Narborough's tea-table is a female-dominated space in which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is a guest, and the Duchess's remark reclaims the conversational high ground from the man who normally dominates every room he enters.

## Themes

- [[gender]]: The Duchess articulates a progressive vision of gender relations, rare in the novel's otherwise male-dominated philosophical landscape.
- [[paradox]]: The remark inverts the conventional association of courage with masculinity, Wilde's characteristic rhetorical strategy applied to gender.
- [[society]]: The line reflects the late-Victorian discourse on the "New Woman" and shifting gender roles in fin-de-siècle England.
