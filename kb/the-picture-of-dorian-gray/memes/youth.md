## Definition

Youth in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is more than a biological stage — it is an aesthetic ideal, a form of power, and ultimately a curse. For [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], youth is the source of his extraordinary beauty and the quality that captivates everyone who encounters him. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] elevates youth to an almost sacred status, telling [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "youth is the one thing worth having" (002-046). The novel treats youth as a finite resource whose inevitable loss becomes the central anxiety driving the plot.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Youth appears first as an attribute of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s character — the "candour of youth" (002-009) that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] captures in his [[the-portrait|portrait]] and that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] immediately recognizes as valuable. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speech in Chapter 2 transforms youth from a natural state into a commodity to be hoarded: "You have [[youth-is-the-one-thing-worth-having|the most marvellous youth]], and youth is the one thing worth having" (002-046). He warns [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "when your youth goes, your beauty will go with it" (002-048), planting the seed of terror that will lead to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s wish in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio.

Throughout the novel, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s preserved youth becomes increasingly unnatural. While his face remains young, his soul bears the marks of age and sin. The "pulse and passion of youth" (004-102) that once animated him gives way to a hollow, desperate seeking after sensation. By the end, his eternal youth has become not a blessing but a prison — a permanent mask that hides the truth of what he has become.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The embodiment of youth and its prisoner. His supernatural preservation of youth enables his life of secret [[sin|corruption]] — and ultimately destroys him.
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The philosopher of youth who convinces [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that aging is a tragedy. His famous speech in Chapter 2 sets the entire plot in motion.

## Key Passages

> You have the most marvellous youth, and youth is the one thing worth having. (002-046)

The opening of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s pivotal speech. It reframes youth not as a natural phase but as the supreme value — a scarcity that must be protected at all costs.

> When your youth goes, your beauty will go with it, and then you will suddenly discover that there are no triumphs left for you. (002-048)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s prophecy of what awaits [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] if he allows himself to age. The terror this induces leads directly to the Faustian wish.

> The sense of his own beauty came on him like a revelation. He had never felt it before. Basil Hallward's compliments had seemed to him to be merely the charming exaggerations of friendship. (002-065)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s awakening to his own beauty — and by extension, the value of his youth. This self-consciousness is the first step toward his [[sin|corruption]].

> Eternal youth, infinite passion, pleasures subtle and secret, wild joys and wilder sins — he was to have all these things. The portrait was to bear the burden of his shame. (008-074)

The terms of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s bargain crystallized: eternal youth in ex[[transformation|change]] for the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s deterioration. The passage captures both the desire and the horror of the arrangement.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde's treatment of youth reflects the fin-de-siècle cult of youth that emerged alongside the Aesthetic and Decadent movements. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy channels Walter Pater's injunction to "burn always with this hard, gemlike flame" — to extract maximum intensity from every moment of life. Yet the novel ultimately critiques this position: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s pursuit of eternal youth leads not to fulfillment but to emptiness, not to freedom but to a different kind of bondage.

The [[the-portrait|portrait]] de[[sin|vice]] literalizes the separation between appearance and reality that the cult of youth produces. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] looks young but his soul is ancient and corrupt — a visual metaphor for a society that values surface over substance.
