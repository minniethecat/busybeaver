## Quotation

> "Actual life was chaos, but there was something terribly logical in the imagination. It was the imagination that set remorse to dog the feet of sin. It was the imagination that made each crime bear its misshapen brood." (018-002)
>
> — Narrator, Chapter 18

## Context

The passage opens the second paragraph of Chapter 18, the morning after [[james-vane|James Vane]] has appeared at Selby Royal and been accidentally shot by Sir Geoffrey Clouston's hunting party. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has spent the previous day in terror, convinced that the sailor he glimpsed through the conservatory window was [[james-vane|Sibyl Vane's brother]] come to avenge her death.

Now, in the aftermath, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] tries to persuade himself that the threat was imaginary: "But perhaps it had been only his fancy that had called vengeance out of the night and set the hideous shapes of punishment before him" (018-002). The narrator's reflection that follows — that "actual life was chaos" but imagination has "something terribly logical" in it — both confirms and subverts this self-reassurance. The terror may have been a product of imagination, but imagination, the narrator insists, is not therefore unreal in its effects.

The paragraph's imagery is relentlessly punitive: remorse "dogs" sin like a hound; each crime bears its "misshapen brood" of consequences. Even if no human agent pursues [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], his own mind will do the pursuing.

## Analysis

The passage is the novel's most sustained philosophical reflection on the psychology of guilt. It presents a three-term structure: sin → imagination → remorse. Sin is the act; imagination is the faculty that converts the act into guilt; remorse is the suffering that results. The chain is internal — no external authority (God, law, society) is required.

The word "dog" is used as a verb — to pursue relentlessly, as a hound tracks its prey. The image recalls the hunting party of this very chapter, in which a beater was shot "like a wild animal" (018-070). The metaphor collapses the distance between hunter and hunted: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is both the gentleman who shoots and the animal that is tracked.

The phrase "terribly logical" is the passage's most striking formulation. Logic is normally a neutral or positive quality; "terribly" twists it. The imagination is logical in the way a nightmare is logical — events follow with an inexorability that feels inescapable even while it is internally generated. This is the novel's answer to those who would dismiss conscience as mere "fancy": fancy, when given logical form by imagination, becomes indistinguishable from fate.

The final clause — "made each crime bear its misshapen brood" — uses the language of monstrous birth. Crimes are not sterile; they reproduce, generating offspring that are distorted ("misshapen") versions of the original act. This is an apt description of the novel's own plot, in which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s original sin (the wish) has generated a "brood" of further sins: cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil]], blackmail of [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]].

## Themes

- [[conscience]]: The passage is the novel's most direct anatomy of conscience — understood not as a divine voice but as the imagination's punitive logic.
- [[sin]]: Sin is presented as generative: each crime "bears" further crimes, in a cycle that imagination makes visible.
- [[morality]]: The paragraph argues that morality is enforced not from without but from within — by the mind's own implacable processes.
- [[soul]]: The "misshapen brood" is the visible record that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] keeps — the imagination's logic made material.
