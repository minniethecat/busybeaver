## Quotation

> "It was his beauty that had ruined him, his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for." (020-005)
>
> — The Narrator, Chapter 20

## Context

This sentence appears in the novel's final paragraphs, after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has stabbed his portrait and fallen dead. The servants discover the body: a withered, loathsome old man lying beneath the masterpieces of his collection. Only the rings on his fingers identify him.

The narrator delivers this verdict in a single, devastating sentence that distills the entire novel into one causal claim. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s wish — that the portrait should age instead of him — was granted. But what seemed a gift was in fact the engine of his ruin.

## Analysis

The sentence is structured as a chiasmus: "his beauty that had ruined him, his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for." The repetition of "his beauty" builds a hammer-fall effect — the first clause names the crime, the second names the co-conspirator.

The word "ruined" is carefully chosen. It carries both moral and physical weight: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been ruined in soul (by sins the portrait records) and now ruined in body (aged beyond recognition). The verb also echoes the novel's persistent metaphor of art as both preservation and destruction. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] remained beautiful while the portrait decayed, but that beauty was not a shelter — it was the very instrument of his undoing.

The phrase "the youth that he had prayed for" explicitly recalls the Faustian bargain struck in Chapter 2: "If it were I who was to be always young, and the picture that was to grow old! ... I would give my soul for that!" (002-034). The narrator's closing judgment makes clear that the prayer was answered — and that the answer was a curse.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: The novel's central paradox — beauty as both gift and fatal liability.
- [[youth]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s eternal youth isolates him from human consequence, enabling his moral descent.
- [[self-destruction]]: The final act of stabbing the portrait is an act of self-destruction, unwittingly fulfilling the narrator's verdict.
- [[dorian-grays-transformation]]: The arc from innocent youth to ruined man is completed in this sentence.
