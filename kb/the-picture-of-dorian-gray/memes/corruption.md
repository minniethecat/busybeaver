## Definition

In *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, corruption is the progressive degradation of the soul, linked inextricably to Dorian's hidden life and the supernatural portrait that bears the visible marks of his sins. Unlike conventional moral decline, Wilde presents corruption as an aesthetic phenomenon — something that fascinates as much as it repels, and that Dorian actively cultivates even as he fears its exposure. The portrait becomes "the visible emblem of conscience" (010-028), showing what Dorian's face refuses to register.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Dorian Gray's corruption begins with Lord Henry's influence and accelerates after Sibyl Vane's suicide. He hides the portrait in the locked schoolroom, where it festers as a "corruption of its own, worse than the corruption of death" (010-011). Over the years chronicled in Chapter 11, Dorian grows "more and more enamoured of his own beauty, more and more interested in the corruption of his own soul" (011-004). When Basil confronts him, Dorian reveals the portrait in a moment of terrible honesty: "You have chattered enough about corruption. Now you shall look on it face to face" (012-026). By the novel's final chapters, Dorian reflects bitterly on his trajectory: "You have gone from corruption to corruption, and now you have culminated in crime" (014-081). Lord Henry frames corruption as the dark twin of culture, an insight Dorian echoes near the end: "Culture and corruption — I have known something of both" (019-006).

> It was to hide something that had a corruption of its own, worse than the corruption of death itself — something that would breed horrors and yet would never die. What the worm was to the corpse, his sins would be to the painted image on the canvas. They would mar its beauty and eat away its grace. They would defile it and make it shameful. And yet the thing would still live on. It would be always alive.
>
> (010-011)

## Key Characters

**Dorian Gray** is the primary subject of corruption. His initial innocence is undone by Lord Henry's philosophy, and his fear of exposure drives him deeper into moral decay. The novel's final assessment is that his mind was "filled with corruption" (020-003), leading to his destruction.

**Lord Henry Wotton** provides the intellectual justification for corruption, though he never acts on his own theories. He frames corruption as a form of experience and culture, insulating it from conventional moral judgment.

**Basil Hallward** serves as the voice of conscience, confronting Dorian about his corruption and ultimately paying with his life for that confrontation.

## Key Passages

- (010-011) — The portrait's first concealment: Dorian hides "something that had a corruption of its own."
- (011-004) — Dorian's deepening fascination: "more and more interested in the corruption of his own soul."
- (012-026) — Basil's confrontation: Dorian reveals the portrait, daring Basil to "look on it face to face."
- (014-081) — Dorian's self-reflection: "You have gone from corruption to corruption."
- (019-006) — The "Culture and corruption" exchange between Lord Henry and Dorian.
- (020-003) — The final judgment: Dorian's mind was "filled with corruption."

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats corruption not as a simple moral failure but as a complex aesthetic and psychological phenomenon. The portrait literalizes the metaphor of the soul's decay, allowing Wilde to explore corruption visually while the protagonist remains physically beautiful. Crucially, Wilde refuses to specify most of Dorian's sins, preserving their suggestive power. The novel's treatment of corruption is characteristically paradoxical: it is both condemned (through the portrait's horrifying transformation) and rendered seductive (through the lush prose of Chapter 11).
