## Quotation

> As the chime struck one, Campbell turned round, and looking at Dorian Gray, saw that his eyes were filled with tears. There was something in the purity and refinement of that sad face that seemed to enrage him. "You are infamous, absolutely infamous!" he muttered. (014-079)
>
> — Alan Campbell, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 14

## Context

This moment comes near the end of the long, excruciating scene in Chapter 14 in which [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] blackmails Alan Campbell into disposing of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s body. Campbell, a chemist and former friend of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s, has just written a note summoning the chemicals and equipment he will need. The valet has been dispatched. The two men wait in silence.

The clock strikes one. Campbell looks at [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and sees tears in his eyes — and the sight enrages him. The narrator explains: "There was something in the purity and refinement of that sad face that seemed to enrage him." Campbell is furious precisely because [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face remains [[beauty|beautiful]], innocent-looking, refined — the external appearance is a lie that continues to function even as the two men sit in the shadow of a murder that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] committed and Campbell is being forced to cover up.

The accusation — "You are infamous, absolutely infamous!" — is the novel's most direct moral condemnation of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], spoken not by a moralist or a clergyman but by a man who has just been broken by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s will.

## Analysis

Alan Campbell's "You are infamous, absolutely infamous!" is remarkable in the novel for being one of the few moments of unambiguous moral speech. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s paradoxes have trained the reader to expect moral categories to be inverted or dissolved; here, Campbell restores them with brute force. "Infamous" is not a paradox — it is a plain statement of fact, and the intensifier "absolutely" rejects any possibility of qualification or aesthetic distance.

The narrator's gloss on Campbell's rage is crucial: "There was something in the purity and refinement of that sad face that seemed to enrage him." Campbell is not enraged by the crime itself — he already knew about it. He is enraged by the *discrepancy* between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s actions. The [[beauty|beautiful]] surface continues to do its work even in the presence of the worst evidence. Campbell's fury is the fury of someone who sees the mask and knows what lies behind it, but is powerless to tear the mask off.

The structural position of the line is also significant. Campbell speaks it *after* he has already agreed, under blackmail, to do what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] asks. The condemnation comes too late to [[transformation|change]] anything; it is purely expressive, a cry of moral outrage from a man who has already been morally compromised. This makes the line both more powerful — it is disinterested, not strategic — and more tragic.

## Themes

- [[condemnation]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit act of [[morality|moral judgment]] upon its protagonist.
- [[appearance-vs-reality]]: Campbell's rage is triggered by the discrepancy between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[beauty|beautiful]] face and his infamous deeds — the novel's central thematic tension.
- [[crime]]: The line names [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s state — "infamous" — without euphemism or aesthetic transformation.
- [[hypocrisy]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tearful, sad face is a performance of innocence that Campbell sees through but cannot expose.
