## Quotation

> "And, certainly, to him life itself was the first, the greatest, of the arts, and for it all the other arts seemed to be but a preparation." (011-007)

## Context

Chapter 11 narrates the years of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s [[duality|double life]] — his public career as an aesthete and arbiter of taste, his private descent into [[sin|corruption]]. The narrator has just described how [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "little dinners" and exquisite taste made him, in the eyes of [[youth|young]] [[high-society|London society]], "the true realization of a type of which they had often dreamed in Eton or Oxford days." To these admirers, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] seemed to belong to "the company of those whom Dante describes as having sought to 'make themselves perfect by the worship of beauty.'"

The sentence about life as the first of arts is the narrator's distillation of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s philosophy — a creed that transforms the aestheticism of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s drawing-room epigrams into a principle of living. The sentence is followed by a catalogue of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s interests: fashion, dandyism, dress — all treated as branches of the one great art, which is life itself.

## Analysis

The sentence enacts a reversal of the traditional relationship between art and life. In conventional aesthetics, art imitates life; life is the original, art the copy. Here, life *is* the art — the primary creative act — and all other arts ([[the-portrait|painting]], music, literature) are merely "preparation," exercises that train the sensibility for the real work of self-creation.

The word "certainly" is the sentence's most interesting choice. It is an asseveration that pretends to be a concession — as if the narrator is acknowledging something obvious, when in fact the claim is radical. The effect is to normalize [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s inverted values, to present the aestheticization of life as a matter of course rather than a philosophical position.

The phrase "the first, the greatest" uses a rhetorical pattern characteristic of Wilde's narrator: the double superlative that escalates within itself. Life is not merely "the greatest" of the arts; it is "the first" — the origin, the model, the thing that makes all other arts meaningful. The hierarchy this establishes is the novel's own: the [[the-portrait|portrait]] (a secondary art) matters only insofar as it illuminates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life (the primary art).

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The sentence is the novel's most concentrated statement of the art-life relationship — life as art, art as preparation for life.
- [[aestheticism]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s creed is a practical application of the aestheticist doctrine that life should be lived as a work of art.
- [[dorian-gray]]: The sentence crystallizes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-conception during the years of his [[duality|double life]].
