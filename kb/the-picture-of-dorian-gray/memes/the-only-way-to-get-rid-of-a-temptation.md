## Quotation

> "The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it. Resist it, and your soul grows sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself, with desire for what its monstrous laws have made monstrous and unlawful." (002-028)

## Context

This is the climactic sentence of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s great seduction speech in Chapter 2. Standing in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio, while [[basil-hallward|Basil]] paints and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] poses, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been delivering an extended improvisation on self-development, influence, and the tragedy of unlived life [002-021–002-028].

The immediate lead-in is a declaration about the cost of repression: "The mutilation of the savage has its tragic survival in the self-denial that mars our lives. We are punished for our refusals. Every impulse that we strive to strangle broods in the mind and poisons us. The body sins once, and has done with its sin, for action is a mode of purification" (002-028).

Then comes the famous sentence. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is so overwhelmed that he cries "Stop!" — "you bewilder me. I don't know what to say" (002-029). The narrator records that "the few words that Basil's friend had said to him... had touched some secret chord that had never been touched before" (002-030).

## Analysis

The sentence is the novel's most compact statement of its central moral heresy — and the nearest thing to a creed that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] ever offers.

Its rhetorical power comes from the reversal of the expected remedy. We ordinarily think of temptation as a problem to be solved by resistance; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] claims that resistance *is* the problem, and that yielding is the solution. The word "get rid of" is crucial — it frames the temptation itself, not the sin, as the thing to be eliminated. Yielding is not indulgence; it is hygiene.

The second sentence explains the mechanism: resistance does not extinguish desire but intensifies it. The "monstrous laws" — a phrase that covers religion, convention, morality, law — create the very desires they prohibit by making them forbidden. The soul sickens not from sin but from the *longing* for sin. This is a psychological theory dressed as an epigram: prohibition is the engine of desire.

The phrase "action is a mode of purification" that precedes this sentence borrows the language of ritual — catharsis, cleansing — and applies it to the satisfaction of impulse. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is offering [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a counter-theology in which the body's sins are absolved by the very act of committing them.

- [[desire]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] offers a theory of desire in which prohibition creates the very longing it seeks to prevent.
