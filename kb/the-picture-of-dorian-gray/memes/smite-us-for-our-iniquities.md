## Quotation

> "Not 'Forgive us our sins' but 'Smite us for our iniquities' should be the prayer of man to a most just God." (020-004)

## Context

In the final chapter, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] walks alone through the streets after midnight, reflecting on his life. He has just destroyed [[the-portrait|the picture]] of [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]]'s death and the evidence of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s murder. He remembers [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s death, which seemed to free him, and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide, which began his descent.

He thinks of [[hetty-merton|Hetty Merton]] and the one "good action" he performed — sparing her from himself. But was it truly good? Or merely another form of vanity? He is no longer sure.

The narrator gives us [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s reflection: "Ah! in what a monstrous moment of pride and passion he had prayed that the portrait should bear the burden of his days, and he keep the unsullied splendour of eternal youth! All his failure had been due to that."

Then comes the theological inversion. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not ask for mercy. He reverses the Lord's Prayer itself. "Forgive us our sins" becomes "Smite us for our iniquities." Punishment, not pardon, is the sign of a just God. Impunity is the real curse.

## Analysis

This is the novel's most compressed theological statement. It inverts Christian doctrine at its core — the plea for forgiveness is recast as a coward's prayer. A just God would not forgive; He would strike. The logic is that punishment is purification: "There was purification in punishment."

The passage completes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral arc. At the novel's midpoint, he envied those who could confess and be absolved. Now he sees that absolution itself is the cheat. To be forgiven without being punished is to remain unchanged. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] has been his false forgiveness — bearing the visible signs of sin while leaving his body untouched. It spared him punishment and therefore denied him purification.

The language is unmistakably biblical: "iniquities," "a most just God," the echo of the Lord's Prayer. But Wilde's God is an aesthetician: He judges by the visible, not the invisible. The portrait is a truer theology than the Church's, because it shows the face sin actually wears.

That [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaks this insight — not the narrator, not [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — is crucial. This is not detached philosophy; it is the cry of a man who has lived the alternative and found it empty.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: The novel's final word on sin is that it demands punishment. Without consequence, sin is meaningless — and meaninglessness is the worst punishment of all.
- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s conscience is not dead; it expresses itself as theology. He knows what he deserves.
- [[religion]]: Wilde engages with Christian theology directly, inverting the central prayer of Christianity.
- [[punishment]]: The passage argues that punishment is a form of grace. To be struck is to be taken seriously by God.
