## Quotation

> "It had been like conscience to him. Yes, it had been conscience. He would destroy it." (020-011)
>
> — Dorian Gray, to himself, Chapter 20

## Context

This passage occurs in the final chapter, as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] returns home after [[dorians-attempted-reform|sparing Hetty Merton]] — his one act of apparent goodness. He has just examined the [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the locked room and found it un[[transformation|change]]d, or worse: the eyes show cunning, the mouth a hypocrite's wrinkle, the red stain on the hand has spread (020-010). His renunciation of Hetty has been exposed as vanity, not [[morality|virtue]].

Standing before the [[the-portrait|portrait]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] confronts the question of whether he must confess to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s murder. He quickly rationalises against it — no one would believe him, the evidence has been destroyed — but the [[the-portrait|portrait]] remains: "only one bit of evidence left against him." (020-011) It is in this moment that he names the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s function: it has been his conscience.

He resolves to destroy it with the same knife that killed [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] — an act that kills himself instead.

## Analysis

The sentence is, in its first clause, tentative — "It had been like conscience to him." The simile signals that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has never before admitted what the [[the-portrait|portrait]] truly is. The second clause corrects the hesitation: "Yes, it had been conscience." The "yes" is self-persuasion, the moment of recognition after a lifetime of evasion.

Wilde's conception of conscience here is radically externalised. [[conscience|Conscience]] is not an [[conscience|inner voice]] but a visible object — a [[the-portrait|painting]] one can lock away, uncover, and ultimately stab. This externalisation is the novel's central conceit made explicit in its final pages. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] *is* conscience made material, and conscience made material can be destroyed — or so [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] believes.

The irony is fatal: in destroying the [[the-portrait|portrait]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] destroys himself. The conscience he sought to kill turns out to be inseparable from his life. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] has not merely recorded his sins; it has borne them, and without that bearer, the weight [[transformation|fall]]s back on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] all at once. The knife that killed the painter kills the painter's work — and the painter's subject.

## Themes

- [[the-portrait]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s function as externalised conscience is stated explicitly here, the thematic climax of the novel's central symbol.
- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s delayed recognition that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] was his conscience dramatises the novel's argument about the impossibility of escaping moral self-awareness.
- [[sin]]: The passage marks the endpoint of sin's trajectory — not repentance, but the attempt to annihilate the evidence of sin, which proves self-destructive.
- [[confession]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] considers and rejects confession, choosing instead to destroy the evidence — an act that functions as an involuntary confession.
