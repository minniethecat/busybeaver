## Quotation

> "Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing." (004-014)
>
> — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]], Chapter 4

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives late to [[lady-agatha|Lady Agatha]]'s luncheon party and offers this epigram by way of apology: "I went to look after a piece of old brocade in Wardour Street and had to bargain for hours for it. Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing" (004-014).

The remark is aimed at the dealer with whom he has been haggling — a man who knew the market price of the brocade but not, in Lord Henry's view, its aesthetic worth. But the aphorism radiates outward to characterize the entire social world of the luncheon: Lady Henry's chatter about pianists and parties, the guests' preoccupation with status and money.

The line appears in the same scene as "Never marry at all, Dorian" (004-021), and both belong to Lord Henry's performance at the luncheon — a series of epigrams that establish him as the most interesting person in the room and seduce [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] further into his influence.

## Analysis

The aphorism rests on a distinction between "price" and "value" that is at once economic and philosophical. Price is what the market assigns — a number, a sum of money. Value is intrinsic worth — beauty, rarity, meaning. Lord Henry's complaint is that modernity has collapsed the second category into the first, so that nothing can be appreciated except in monetary terms.

The word "nowadays" is the aphorism's historical claim: this confusion of price and value is a condition of modern life, not a universal human failing. The implication is that there was a time — classical Athens, the Renaissance — when value and price were properly distinguished. Lord Henry positions himself as a survivor from that earlier age, a connoisseur in a world of shopkeepers.

But the aphorism contains its own irony. Lord Henry has just spent hours bargaining — that is, negotiating price — for a piece of brocade. His distinction between price and value is made possible by his wealth, which allows him to treat the price as incidental to the value. The aphorism is, in this sense, an aristocratic luxury: only someone who can afford to ignore price can afford to value value alone.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The distinction between price and value is an aesthetic distinction — the brocade is valuable because it is beautiful, not because it is expensive.
- [[beauty]]: Beauty is the paradigm of value without price — it cannot be quantified, only appreciated.
- [[morality]]: The aphorism implies a moral critique of materialism, but Lord Henry's alternative is aesthetic, not ethical.
