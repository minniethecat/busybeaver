## Quotation

> "Believe me, no civilized man ever regrets a pleasure, and no uncivilized man ever knows what a pleasure is." (006-046)

## Context

The epigram arrives near the end of a long dinner-table oration [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], who has just objected to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s apparent approval of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] insists that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not mean the things he says. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replies with a torrent of paradoxes about individualism, morality, and the value of experience. The immediate trigger for this particular epigram is [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s mention of medieval art — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses "medieval emotions" as out of date, claiming that the only things one can use in fiction are things one has ceased to use in fact.

The pleasure epigram is the culminating point of this argument: civilization does not consist in renouncing pleasure but in the capacity to enjoy it without retroactive [[conscience|guilt]]. The "uncivilized man" is doubly excluded — he cannot enjoy pleasure fully, and he cannot even know what pleasure is. The statement flatters its hearer by placing him on the right side of the line while making the line itself imperiously clear.

The speech is cut short by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s arrival, flushed with the news of his engagement.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram works through a double exclusion. The "civilized man" is defined by a negative capacity — he does not regret — while the "uncivilized man" is defined by a cognitive deficit — he does not know. The asymmetry is deliberate: civilization is a mode of being (one *is* civilized), while savagery is a mode of ignorance (one does not *know*). To regret a pleasure is to place oneself outside the circle of civilization; to be uncertain about what pleasure is places one outside the circle of humanity altogether.

The statement is also a meta-commentary on [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own method. He has just spent several minutes delivering epigrams that dissolve conventional moral categories, and this epigram explains why: the regret that follows pleasure is not a sign of conscience but a sign of incomplete civilization. The implication is that [[basil-hallward|Basil]], who worries about the moral consequences of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement, is not more virtuous than [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — he is simply less civilized.

Structurally, the epigram follows the pattern of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most effective paradoxes: a simple antithesis ("civilized" vs "uncivilized"), a reversal of expectation (it is the civilized who are free, not the moral), and a finality of tone that discourages counter-argument. The word "believe me" at the start is not a request but a command — it assumes agreement before the proposition has even been stated.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The epigram elevates pleasure from an indulgence to a criterion of civilization, making the capacity for unregretted [[pleasure|enjoyment]] a mark of cultural advancement.
- [[civilization]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines civilization not as moral refinement but as psychological freedom — the ability to act without retroactive self-punishment.
- [[morality]]: The statement implicitly attacks conventional morality as a form of incomplete development, a failure to achieve the civilized state of [[conscience|guilt]]-free [[pleasure|enjoyment]].
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] uses the epigram as a tool of seduction, inviting his hearer to identify with the "civilized" and to view moral scruple as a form of backwardness.
