## Quotation

> "Conscience makes egotists of us all." (008-053)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton]] delivers this verdict on conscience during a long, cynical monologue to [[dorian-gray]] in Chapter 8, the morning after [[sibyl-vane]]'s suicide. Dorian has just read the newspaper account of the inquest and is struggling with guilt. Lord Henry's response is to systematically dismantle every possible source of consolation — and then to argue that none is needed, because the tragedy is best understood as a beautiful aesthetic event.

The immediate context is Lord Henry's catalogue of the consolations available to women: sentimental colours, rediscovering their husbands' good qualities, and religion. "Religion consoles some," he says. "Its mysteries have all the charm of a flirtation, a woman once told me, and I can quite understand it. Besides, nothing makes one so vain as being told that one is a sinner" (008-053). The aphorism that follows — "Conscience makes egotists of us all" — is the logical endpoint of this chain: conscience is not a moral faculty but a form of vanity.

## Analysis

This aphorism is one of Lord Henry's most subversive pronouncements because it inverts the traditional moral valuation of conscience. In Christian ethics, conscience is the voice of God within the soul, the internal witness to moral law. Lord Henry reinterprets it as a variety of self-absorption: to be conscious of one's sins is to be conscious of oneself, and to be conscious of oneself is to be an egotist.

The brilliance of the aphorism lies in its psychological plausibility. There is indeed a kind of moral self-regard that can accompany guilt — a fascination with one's own wickedness that is the opposite of genuine repentance. Dorian himself will later exemplify this: he becomes increasingly obsessed with his own corruption, studying the changes in [[the-portrait]] with "a purely scientific interest." The portrait functions as his conscience made visible, and his relationship to it is profoundly egotistical.

Lord Henry's dismissal of conscience serves a strategic purpose in the conversation. By convincing Dorian that feeling guilty is merely a form of vanity, he absolves him of responsibility for Sibyl's death — and simultaneously deepens his own influence over the younger man. The aphorism is both a philosophical claim and a tool of manipulation.

## Themes

- **[[conscience]]**: The quote directly addresses the novel's treatment of conscience, which Wilde consistently ironizes. Whether conscience is a genuine moral faculty or merely a psychological phenomenon is left deliberately ambiguous.
- **[[sin]]**: Lord Henry's aphorism implies that the awareness of sin is more problematic than sin itself. This inverts conventional morality and prepares the ground for Dorian's eventual descent into ever-greater crimes without apparent remorse.
- **[[morality-and-art]]**: The quote belongs to the novel's broader project of aestheticizing morality — treating ethical questions as if they were matters of taste or style.
