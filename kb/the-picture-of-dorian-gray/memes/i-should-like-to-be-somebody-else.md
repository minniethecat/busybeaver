## Quotation

> "I hope it is not about myself. I am tired of myself to-night. I should like to be somebody else." (012-014)

## Context

It is the night before [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s thirty-eighth birthday — the eve of the fateful confrontation in which he will show [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] the transformed [[the-portrait|portrait]] and then murder him. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has arrived unexpectedly at [[mayfair|Dorian's house]], carrying a Gladstone bag and an ulster, on his way to catch the midnight train to Paris. He has come, he says, because he must speak to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] seriously before he leaves: there are "most [[mind-of-the-well-informed-man|dreadful thing]]s" being said about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in London, and [[basil-hallward|Basil]] feels he has a right to know the truth (012-012).

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] receives him with practiced insouciance, offering brandy-and-soda and making small talk about servants and French fashions. But when [[basil-hallward|Basil]] insists that the subject is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself, the mask slips. "I hope it is not about myself," [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cries, flinging himself down on the sofa "in his petulant way" (012-014). "I am tired of myself to-night. I should like to be somebody else."

The line is devastating precisely because it is, for once, true. After eighteen years of multiplying his personality, of being one man in the drawing room and another in the opium den, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is exhausted by the self he has become. His wish to be "somebody else" is not a Wildean paradox but a cry of genuine despair — a confession that the [[beauty|beautiful]] surface has become unbearable to its owner.

## Analysis

The line marks a crucial turn in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s psychological trajectory. Throughout the novel's middle chapters, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been the supreme practitioner of what the narrator calls the "multiplication of personalities" (011-031). He is the charming host and the secret sinner, the patron of the arts and the corrupter of youth, the man about town and the visitor to "[[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|dens of horror]] where the memory of old sins could be destroyed by the madness of sins that were new" (016-036). The multiplication of selves has been his survival mechanism — and, as Chapter 11 argues, his aesthetic project. To "be somebody else" was the whole point.

Now, on the eve of his thirty-eighth birthday, that project has collapsed into exhaustion. The word "tired" is the key: not [[conscience|guilt]]y, not afraid, but *tired*. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins have not produced [[conscience|remorse]]; they have produced fatigue. The self has become a burden not because it is evil but because it is boring — the ultimate Wildean sin. His wish to be "somebody else" is the dark underside of the aestheticist program. If the self is a work of art, then the artist can grow tired of his creation, just as [[basil-hallward|Basil]] grew tired of [[the-portrait|painting]] [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the attic is the frozen record of a self that its owner can no longer bear to inhabit.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s reply — "It is about yourself... and I must say it to you" (012-014) — seals [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fate. He cannot escape himself because [[basil-hallward|Basil]] will not let him. The confrontation that follows, in which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reveals the [[the-portrait|portrait]] and murders [[basil-hallward|Basil]], is the violent climax of a self that has become inescapable.

## Themes

- [[identity]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s confession that he is "tired of myself" is the collapse of the performative self constructed across eighteen years of [[duality|double life]].
- [[double-life]]: The multiplication of selves, once a source of pleasure, has become a source of exhaustion.
- [[insincerity]]: The line is one of the novel's rare moments of unguarded sincerity — and it leads directly to murder.
