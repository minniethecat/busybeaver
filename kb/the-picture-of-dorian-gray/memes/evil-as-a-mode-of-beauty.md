## Quotation

> "There were moments when he looked on evil simply as a mode through which he could realize his conception of the beautiful." (011-036)

## Context

This sentence concludes a vertiginous passage in Chapter 11 — the novel's chronicle of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s years of [[sin|corruption]]. The preceding paragraphs have catalogued the Renaissance criminals and decadents whose lives [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] imaginatively inhabits: Filippo, Duke of Milan, who slew his wife; Pietro Barbi, the Venetian Pope; Gian Maria Visconti, who hunted men with hounds; the Borgia; Sigismondo Malatesta — "the awful and [[beauty|beautiful]] forms of those whom [[sin|vice]] and blood and weariness had made monstrous or mad" (011-035).

The narrator then pivots from historical catalogue to diagnosis: "There was a horrible fascination in them all. He saw them at night, and they troubled his imagination in the day" (011-036). The passage traces a genealogy of influence — the Renaissance knew "strange manners of poisoning," but in the modern world, poisoning has been sublimated into literature: "[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] had been [[poisoned-by-a-book|poisoned by a book]]" (011-036). It is immediately after this diagnosis that the sentence about evil as a mode of beauty appears, functioning as the passage's dark thesis statement.

## Analysis

The sentence is the novel's most compressed statement of its central moral-aesthetic paradox. Evil is not embraced as evil — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not pursue wickedness for its own sake. Instead, evil is instrumentalized: it becomes a "mode," a technique, a medium through which beauty can be realized. The language is that of the artist ("realize his conception"), as if sin were a pigment and [[sin|corruption]] a compositional principle.

The qualifying phrase "there were moments when" is crucial. It acknowledges that this is not [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s permanent mental state but a recurring temptation — a particular angle of vision that he occasionally adopts. The sentence thus captures the psychology of self-deception with precision: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not consistently believe that evil serves beauty, but he can, at moments, make himself believe it, and those moments are enough to license his actions.

The sentence also rewrites the relationship between aesthetics and [[morality|ethics]] that the Preface had established. The Preface had declared that "there is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book" (P02-001) — art exists in a realm apart from [[morality|ethics]]. But this sentence describes a far more dangerous position: not the separation of art from morality, but the subordination of morality to art. Evil becomes the servant of the [[beauty|beautiful]], and beauty becomes the justification for evil.
