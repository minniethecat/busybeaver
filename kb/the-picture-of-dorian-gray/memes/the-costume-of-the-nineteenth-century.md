## Quotation

> "Yes," answered Lord Henry dreamily, "the costume of the nineteenth century is detestable. It is so sombre, so depressing. Sin is the only real colour-element left in modern life." (002-102)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Basil Hallward, Chapter 2 (002-102)

## Context

The line comes early in Lord Henry's first sustained conversation with [[basil-hallward|Basil]] at his studio, before [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has even entered the room.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just commented that he will not exhibit the portrait because he has "put too much of [himself] into it" (002-101).

From this confession of artistic self-exposure, Lord Henry pivots outward to aesthetic judgment of the age itself.

The remark about costume appears casual — a throwaway observation about fashion.

But it is Lord Henry's characteristic method: he uses a surface-level aesthetic judgment to lever open a deeper philosophical claim.

The segue from "the costume of the nineteenth century is detestable" to "Sin is the only real colour-element left in modern life" is the rhetorical move that defines his character.

Aesthetic disgust at the mundane becomes a warrant for moral transgression.

## Analysis

Lord Henry's critique of nineteenth-century dress is not really about clothing. "Sombre" and "depressing" are aesthetic judgments, not moral ones.

He is diagnosing a civilization that has traded colour for respectability, intensity for decorum.

The criticism echoes Wilde's own aesthetic commitments — the Preface declares art's independence from morality, and Lord Henry extends this logic to life itself.

The pivot to sin is audacious.

If modern life has drained itself of colour, then sin — a category of experience that remains vivid, charged, transgressive — is the only remaining source of aesthetic intensity.

Lord Henry is not advocating sin in any crude sense; he is making an aesthetic argument for the value of intensity over propriety.

The logic will echo through the novel: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s descent is framed not as moral collapse but as a pursuit of sensation.

The novel itself treats his sins as occasions for the portrait's aesthetic transformation.

The line also reveals Lord Henry's fundamental project: to aestheticize morality, to treat right and wrong as categories of taste rather than ethics.

This is the seed of everything he will say to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] over the next eighteen chapters.
