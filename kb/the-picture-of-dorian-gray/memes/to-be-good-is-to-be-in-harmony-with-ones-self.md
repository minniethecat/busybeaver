## Quotation

> "To be good is to be in harmony with one's self. Discord is to be forced to be in harmony with others. One's own life — that is the important thing. As for the lives of one's neighbours, if one wishes to be a prig or a Puritan, one can flaunt one's moral views about them, but they are not one's concern. Besides, individualism has really the higher aim. Modern morality consists in accepting the standard of one's age. I consider that for any man of culture to accept the standard of his age is a form of the grossest immorality." (006-040)

## Context

The speech occurs during a dinner conversation between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in Chapter 6. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has accused [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of corrupting [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and of not meaning any of the things he says. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replies with an extended defence of individualism that touches on marriage, the value of experience, and the nature of goodness. This epigram — with its gesture toward the wine glass and its carefully staged physical composure — is the philosophical centre of the speech.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] touches the "thin stem of his glass with his pale, fine-pointed fingers" as he speaks, a characteristic detail that links his philosophy to his aesthetic self-presentation. The gesture is as much a part of the argument as the words: it signals detachment, refinement, and the aristocratic privilege of treating morality as an object of contemplation rather than a code of conduct.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] has no effective reply. He [[transformation|fall]]s back on personal affection — "You are much better than you pretend to be" — which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] immediately dismantles as a form of psychological self-interest.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram performs a radical redefinition of the word "good." In conventional morality, goodness is a matter of conformity to an external standard — the law, the community, God. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reverses this: goodness is harmony with *oneself*, and conformity to others is not [[morality|virtue]] but "discord" — a violent wrenching of the self out of its natural shape. The word "forced" is crucial: it recasts social morality as coercion and individualism as liberation.

The argument then escalates. Having redefined goodness as self-consistency, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses the lives of one's neighbours as irrelevant — concern for them is merely the mark of a "prig or a Puritan." The word "flaunt" is particularly sharp: it suggests that moral concern for others is not genuine care but a form of exhibitionism, a way of displaying one's own superiority.

The final turn — that accepting the standard of one's age is "the grossest immorality" — completes the reversal. Modern morality, which presents itself as enlightened and progressive, is actually a form of cowardice: it consists in letting one's age do one's thinking. The "man of culture" is the one who refuses this surrender. The epigram thus offers a complete moral programme in miniature: be true to yourself, ignore your neighbours, and never let your era tell you what to think.

## Themes

- [[individualism]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] elevates the individual self to the sole source of moral authority, dismissing social conformity as a form of self-betrayal.
- [[morality]]: The epigram systematically inverts conventional morality, recasting goodness as internal consistency and social obligation as a form of violence.
- [[self]]: The self is presented as the only legitimate object of moral concern; attention to others is redescribed as vanity or Puritanism.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this philosophy to [[basil-hallward|Basil]] — and, implicitly, to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — as a form of seduction, offering a vision of moral freedom that is also an invitation to self-absorption.
