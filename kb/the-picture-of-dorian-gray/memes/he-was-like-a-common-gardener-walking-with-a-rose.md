## Quotation

> "He was like a common gardener walking with a rose." (005-046)

## Context

The line appears in Chapter 5 as [[james-vane]] and his sister [[sibyl-vane]] walk together through the dreary Euston Road. James, about to depart for Australia as a sailor, has asked [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] to walk with him — a final moment of intimacy before his long absence.

The narrator first describes the passersby who "glanced in wonder at the sullen heavy youth who, in coarse, ill-fitting clothes, was in the company of such a graceful, refined-looking girl" (005-046). Then comes the simile: "He was like a common gardener walking with a rose."

The image is compressed and devastating. James is "common" — coarse, heavy, ill-dressed, sullen. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is a rose — delicate, [[beauty|beautiful]], something to be protected. The gardener cultivates the rose, tends it, but does not *own* it. He is its servant, not its possessor.

## Analysis

### The Simile's Economy

In a single sentence, the narrator captures three relationships: James's protective love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], the class gulf that separates them from the world [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] inhabits, and the fragility of what James is about to lose.

A gardener does not keep the rose; the rose is for someone else — someone who will pluck it. James is walking [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] toward her fate but does not know it.

### Class and Beauty

The simile depends on class contrast. The "common gardener" is earth-bound, working-class, coarse. The "rose" is rare, cultivated, [[beauty|beautiful]]. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s beauty isolates her from her own family — she belongs to a different order of being than her brother.

This class tension pervades the Vane subplot. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s acting career is the family's precarious route to [[reputation|respectability]]. Her relationship with "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" — [[dorian-gray]] — is a crossing of class lines that Mrs. Vane encourages and James instinctively mistrusts.

### Foreshadowing

The simile is elegiac. James will soon leave for Australia, and when he returns, [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] will be dead. The rose has been plucked and discarded. James's protective walk through the Euston Road is the last time he will see his sister alive — and the simile already mourns her.
