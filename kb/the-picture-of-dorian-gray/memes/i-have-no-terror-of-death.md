## Quotation

> "I have no terror of death. It is the coming of death that terrifies me. Its monstrous wings seem to wheel in the leaden air around me." (018-026)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]], walking back from a shooting-party at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]] (Chapter 18). A beater has just been accidentally shot and killed by Sir Geoffrey Clouston. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], already agitated after seeing [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s face at the window days before, interprets the death as a "bad omen" (018-024). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses omens, but [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] confesses his terror — not of death itself, but of its approach, the suffocating anticipation of doom that now haunts him (018-026). The dead beater is later revealed to be [[james-vane|James Vane]], making [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s premonition tragically accurate.

## Analysis

This confession is one of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s most honest moments in the novel. Stripped of epigrammatic wit, he admits to a visceral, animal terror. The distinction between death and "the coming of death" is psychologically acute: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has already experienced a kind of [[soul|spirit]]ual death through his [[the-portrait|portrait]], and what he now fears is the closing in of consequence — the feeling of being "hunted, snared, tracked down" (018-001). The image of "monstrous wings" wheeling in "leaden air" is Gothic in its intensity, recalling the novel's debts to the supernatural and to Poe. It also foreshadows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s eventual death: when he finally does face the [[the-portrait|portrait]], it is the waiting — the cumulative weight of his sins — that proves unbearable (020-XXX).
