## Description

The Church of England (Anglican Church) was the established national church of Victorian Britain, woven into every layer of social, political, and moral life. Church attendance was a social obligation for the upper classes, and the Church served as the guardian of public morality. Its rituals — baptism, marriage, burial — framed the lives of all English subjects, from the highest aristocrat to the humblest cottager.

## Role in the Novel

The Church appears at three distinct points in the novel, each marking a stage in Dorian Gray's moral trajectory. Early on, Lord Henry describes the Duchess of Harley as having "a perfect mania for going to church" (004-008) — a throwaway line that captures the superficial religiosity of London high society, where church-going is a social performance rather than spiritual conviction.

In a darker register, the narrator later reflects on how "shameful passion built a pagan church for Christian worship" (011-035), linking Charles II's court to the theme of appearance masking corruption — a clear parallel to Dorian's own hidden life. The final church reference is the most damning: Alan Campbell's suicide is discovered when his body is found "in a nameless grave in Selby churchyard" (020-006), placing the Church in the orbit of Dorian's destructive influence. The Church, like every institution in the novel, proves powerless to stop moral decay — it can only bury the dead.

## Social / Historical Context

In Wilde's Victorian England, the Church of England was under pressure from multiple directions: Darwinian science, German biblical criticism, and the Oxford Movement's internal reforms. Wilde himself, raised Protestant but drawn to Catholicism (he converted on his deathbed), viewed the established Church with a mixture of aesthetic appreciation and moral skepticism. The novel's treatment of the Church — as a social institution hollowed of spiritual power — reflects the broader Victorian crisis of faith, where religious observance increasingly became a matter of form rather than belief.
