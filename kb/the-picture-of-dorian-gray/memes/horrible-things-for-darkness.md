## Quotation

> "Gradually the events of the preceding night crept with silent, blood-stained feet into his brain and reconstructed themselves there with terrible distinctness. He winced at the memory of all that he had suffered, and for a moment the same curious feeling of loathing for Basil Hallward that had made him kill him passed across him. The dead man was still sitting there, too, and in the sunlight now. How horrible that was! Such hideous things were for the darkness, not for the day." (014-004)
>
> — Narrator, describing Dorian Gray's thoughts, Chapter 14

## Context

This passage opens the second paragraph of Chapter 14, the morning after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. The preceding paragraph describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s peaceful sleep — "He looked like a boy who had been tired out with play, or study" (014-001) — and the servant opening the shutters to a bright November morning. The contrast between the serenity of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sleeping face and the horror of the previous night is the paragraph's governing tension.

The phrase "crept with silent, blood-stained feet" personifies memory as a stealthy, violent intruder — something that enters the mind unbidden and leaves traces. The "curious feeling of loathing for [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]" that returns to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is significant: his motive for the murder was not premeditated hatred but a sudden revulsion, and that revulsion is still active in him the morning after.

The final sentence — "Such hideous things were for the darkness, not for the day" — is both a cry of revulsion and a statement of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s aesthetic worldview. Horror belongs to the night; daylight is the province of beauty and pleasure. The corpse in the sun violates this categorical distinction, forcing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to confront the fact that his crimes cannot be contained within a separate sphere of existence.

## Analysis

The sentence "Such hideous things were for the darkness, not for the day" is a key to understanding how [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s aestheticism functions as a mechanism of denial. Throughout the novel, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has attempted to compartmentalize his life — the [[beauty|beautiful]] surface and the corrupted interior, the public charm and the private degradation. The corpse in the sunlight represents the collapse of that compartmentalization.

The narrator's language enforces the distinction even while recording its violation. "Hideous things" are assigned to "darkness"; the daylight is implicitly the realm of beauty. But the sentence is structured as a lament — "Such hideous things were for the darkness" — the past tense "were" acknowledging that reality has already violated the expected order. The corpse *is* in the sunlight; the horror has crossed the boundary.

The passage also marks a turning point in the novel's treatment of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s psychology. The murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is the act that makes the division between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s two lives unsustainable. From this point forward, the novel traces the progressive collapse of the compartment walls — the [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]], the blackmail of Alan Campbell, [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s pursuit, and finally the destruction of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] itself.

## Themes

- [[murder]]: The passage is the first description of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s state of mind after the murder, tracing the return of awareness and the first stirrings of consequence.
- [[concealment]]: "Hideous things were for the darkness" expresses [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s desire to keep his crimes hidden — a desire the novel punishes.
- [[horror]]: The invasion of daylight by horror is a Gothic motif that Wilde deploys with characteristic restraint.
- [[duality]]: The darkness/daylight opposition mirrors the novel's larger structure of doubled existence.
