## Quotation

> "I worshipped you too much. I am punished for it. You worshipped yourself too much. We are both punished." (013-028)

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has just seen the corrupted [[the-portrait|portrait]] — the "hideous face on the canvas" that was once his masterpiece. The shock is physical: his hand shakes so violently that the candle [[transformation|fall]]s and sputters out on the floor. He stumbles into a chair and buries his face in his hands.

Then he speaks. "Good God, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], what a lesson! What an awful lesson!" There is no answer from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], only sobbing at the window. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] begins to pray aloud, reciting fragments of the Lord's Prayer: "Lead us not into temptation. Forgive us our sins. Wash away our iniquities."

Then, in a moment of terrible clarity, he arrives at this four-part statement. It is the moral summary of the entire novel, spoken by the one character who genuinely loved [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — and who will be dead within minutes.

The structure is a mirror, and a judgment. "I worshipped you too much. I am punished for it." [[basil-hallward|Basil]] acknowledges his own sin: [[worship|idolatry]]. He made [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] into an ideal, poured his "whole soul" into the [[the-portrait|portrait]], and worshipped the creation more than the Creator. "You worshipped yourself too much. You are punished for it." He names [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sin: [[vanity|narcissism]], the worship of his own beauty. "We are both punished." The final line is not accusation but solidarity. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] does not exempt himself. He and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] are bound together in mutual destruction — the worshipper and the worshipped, both damned.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s reply is three words: "It is too late, [[basil-hallward|Basil]]." Then, looking at the [[the-portrait|portrait]], an "uncontrollable feeling of hatred for [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]" surges through him.

## Analysis

This is the novel's most theologically precise passage, and it comes from its most morally serious character. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s four sentences trace a complete theology of sin, punishment, and shared [[conscience|guilt]]. They also answer the question the novel has been asking since Chapter 2: who is responsible for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|corruption]]?

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s answer is: both of them. He does not say "You have done this to yourself." He says "We are both punished." The pronoun is the most important word in the sentence. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] refuses the comfort of blaming [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] alone. His own worship — his aesthetic [[worship|idolatry]], his substitution of the [[beauty|beautiful]] boy for God — was the enabling condition of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[transformation|fall]]. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] was [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s creation before it was [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s prison.

The word "worshipped" is chosen with precision. It is a religious term applied to an aesthetic relationship, and the novel has been documenting that confusion from the start. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] worships [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as an ideal of beauty; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] worships himself as that ideal; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] worships the pleasure of influence. All three commit the same sin in different forms: putting the creature in the place of the Creator.

The tragedy is that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s recognition comes too late to save either of them. His prayer is genuine, but [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has passed beyond the reach of prayer — "Those words mean nothing to me now." The scene is a deathbed confession without the deathbed: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] speaks his moral insight, and then [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] stabs him in the neck.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] names two sins precisely — [[worship|idolatry]] (his own) and [[vanity|narcissism]] ([[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s) — and declares them equally damning.
- [[conscience]]: This is the novel's last moment of genuine moral consciousness. After [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s death, no character capable of this clarity remains.
- [[worship]]: The confusion of aesthetic worship with religious worship is the novel's deepest theological concern. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s confession makes the confusion explicit.
