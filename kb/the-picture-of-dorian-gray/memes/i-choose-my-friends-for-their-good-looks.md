## Quotation

> "I choose my friends for their good looks, my acquaintances for their good characters, and my enemies for their good intellects. A man cannot be too careful in the choice of his enemies. I have not got one who is a fool. They are all men of some intellectual power, and consequently they all appreciate me. Is that very vain of me? I think it is rather vain." (001-045)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s garden, early in the afternoon when he first meets [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just accused him of indifference dressed as universal liking — "You like every one; that is to say, you are indifferent to every one" (001-044). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is not a denial but a refinement: he does not like everyone indiscriminately; he likes everyone according to a precise and inverted hierarchy.

The taxonomy is characteristically Wildean in its reversals. Friends — the most intimate category — are chosen for the most superficial reason (looks). Enemies — the most hostile category — are chosen for the most admirable reason (intellect). Acquaintances occupy the middle ground and are selected for the most conventionally respectable reason (character). The hierarchy inverts traditional values at every level.

The self-conscious vanity of the closing question — "Is that very vain of me? I think it is rather vain" — is essential to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rhetorical method. By preemptively naming his own fault before anyone else can, he disarms criticism and converts a character flaw into a performance. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], standing nearby on the [[the-portrait|portrait]] dais, is hearing ideas of this kind for the first time; the taxonomy of friends, acquaintances, and enemies is the first taste of a worldview that will intoxicate him.

## Analysis

The epigram works by smuggling a serious point inside a frivolous packaging. The surface is pure dandyism — a man who treats human relations as an aesthetic exercise. But the underlying claim is that conventional morality's hierarchy of values is arbitrary. Why *should* we choose friends for their [[morality|virtue]]? Why *shouldn't* we admire our enemies? [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not argue these positions; he simply states them as if they were obvious, and the effect is to make the conventional position seem arbitrary rather than his own.

The sentence about enemies is the sharpest point in the epigram. "A man cannot be too careful in the choice of his enemies" sounds like a parody of prudential ad[[sin|vice]] ("a man cannot be too careful in the choice of his friends"), but the substitution reveals a genuine insight. Enemies, unlike friends, engage with you intellectually; they read you with attention and seek out your weaknesses. In an ironic sense, enemies may appreciate you more accurately than friends do.

The taxonomy also maps onto the novel's central triangle. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is chosen for his looks (friend). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is chosen for his character (acquaintance — and indeed [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s relationship with [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is one of genial condescension). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s enemies — "all men of some intellectual power" — are offstage, but the fact that he values them above his friends says something about the emptiness of his relationship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], which is built entirely on surfaces.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: Physical beauty is the highest value in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s inverted hierarchy, outweighing both character and intellect.
- [[influence]]: The epigram is part of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s seduction of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind — it demonstrates a worldview in which conventional morality is merely one aesthetic choice among others.
- [[friendship]]: The novel's central friendship is founded on this taxonomy — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as the [[beauty|beautiful]] friend, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] as the good acquaintance — and the taxonomy's shallowness predicts the relationship's collapse.
- [[vanity]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s self-diagnosed vanity is both genuine and performative; the performance of self-awareness masks the refusal to [[transformation|change]].
