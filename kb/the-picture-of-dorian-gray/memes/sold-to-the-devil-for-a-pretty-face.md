## Quotation

> "He is the worst one that comes here. They say he has sold himself to the devil for a pretty face. It's nigh on eighteen years since I met him. He hasn't changed much since then. I have, though." (016-069)

## Context

These words are spoken by an unnamed, degraded woman in a squalid opium den in the London docklands (Ch16). She speaks to [[james-vane|James Vane]], who has tracked [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] to this den, seeking revenge for his sister [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death. James has been pursuing the man known as "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" for eighteen years.

The scene is one of the novel's darkest. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has come to the opium den seeking "oblivion" and "forgetfulness" after murdering [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] (016-002). The woman who speaks these lines had earlier taunted [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as he left, calling "There goes the devil's bargain!" (016-039), to which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] replied, "Curse you! Don't call me that" (016-040).

When [[james-vane|James Vane]] interrogates her, the woman swears "before God" (016-068) that her account is true. Her testimony—that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has not aged in eighteen years—both confirms the supernatural element of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] and provides James with the final proof he needs to identify his prey.

## Analysis

This quote is significant because it provides an external, low-society perspective on [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s supernatural condition. Unlike the novel's educated characters who attribute [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s youth to genetics or lifestyle, this woman of the London underworld reaches instinctively for a theological explanation: a Faustian bargain.

The phrase "[[he-has-sold-himself-to-the-devil-for-a-pretty-face|sold himself to the devil]] for a pretty face" is the most direct statement in the novel of what has actually occurred. The wish [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] made in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio (002-029)—that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] would age instead of him—is functionally a pact. What was phrased as an idle wish in a sunlit studio is recognized, in the darkness of the opium den, as a demonic transaction.

The woman's final words—"I have, though"—carry a grim irony. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s unnatural preservation is contrasted with the natural decay of age. The full horror of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s condition has an external witness for the first time, and notably, the witness comes from the lowest stratum of society, where such supernatural explanations are taken seriously rather than dismissed as superstition.
