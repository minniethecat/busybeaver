## Quotation

> "Harry spends his days in saying what is incredible and his evenings in doing what is improbable." (009-058)
>
> — Dorian Gray, to Basil Hallward, Chapter 9

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] delivers this wry characterization of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] during his conversation with [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] on the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has been pressing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to show more feeling, to acknowledge the tragedy. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deflects — and in deflecting, he echoes the very man whose philosophy has reshaped him.

The line is delivered "with a ripple of laughter," a detail that captures [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s new lightness of tone — a direct inheritance from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]. Where [[basil-hallward|Basil]] speaks in earnest, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] now speaks in epigrams.

## Analysis

The sentence is a miniature portrait of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], built on a chiasmus: "saying what is incredible" balances against "doing what is improbable." The structure suggests that speech and action are equally unreal for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — he neither believes what he says nor commits to what he does. His life is an aesthetic performance, not a moral project.

But the line also reveals [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own transformation. In Chapter 1, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] was nearly silent — a beautiful object observed by [[basil-hallward|Basil]] and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]. By Chapter 9, he has absorbed [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s idiom so completely that he can produce a polished epigram on demand. The student has learned to speak like the master.

The "ripple of laughter" that accompanies the line is equally revealing. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] laughs at what should be a moment of sobriety — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is dead — and his laughter is the most damning evidence of his transformation. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has taught him to aestheticize suffering, and this epigram is the proof.
