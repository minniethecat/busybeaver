## Quotation

> "Every impulse that we strive to strangle broods in the mind and poisons us. The body sins once, and has done with its sin, for action is a mode of purification. Nothing remains then but the recollection of a pleasure, or the luxury of a regret. The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it. Resist it, and your soul grows sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself, with desire for what its monstrous laws have made monstrous and unlawful." (002-026)

## Context

In Chapter 2, in the second half of his great speech on self-realization, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] pivots from diagnosis to prescription. Having argued that society and religion govern through terror (002-024), he now offers an alternative: a [[a-new-hedonism|new Hedonism]] built on the principle that repression is the true sin.

This passage comes immediately after [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s claim that "the bravest man amongst us is afraid of himself" (002-026) and his invocation of the Hellenic ideal. The argument moves from the collective ("we are punished for our refusals") to the individual ("your soul grows sick") — and the pronoun "you" is aimed directly at [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]].

## Analysis

The passage constructs a psychological theory in miniature. The mechanism: strangling an impulse does not kill it — it drives it underground, where it "broods" and "poisons." The body, by contrast, is healthier than the mind: it "sins once, and has done with its sin." Action is catharsis; inaction is infection.

The word "purification" is crucial — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] borrows religious language and repurposes it. [[sin|Sin]] is not the act; sin is the refusal to act. The body's "mode of purification" is physical expression; the mind's equivalent is the embrace of temptation. This is a complete inversion of conventional Victorian morality, where self-denial was the highest [[morality|virtue]] and bodily indulgence the deepest shame.

The final sentence introduces "monstrous laws" — a direct attack on the social and religious codes that forbid desire. The laws themselves are what make desire "monstrous"; desire, in its natural state, is innocent. This anticipates the novel's later exploration of [[sin]] as a socially constructed category.

This passage is arguably the most consequential paragraph in the novel. It gives [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a philosophical license for everything that follows. When he pursues pleasure without restraint, when he descends into the [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]], when he murders [[basil-hallward|Basil]] — each act can be traced back to this moment: "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it."

## Themes

- [[temptation]]: The passage reframes temptation not as a test of [[morality|virtue]] but as an invitation to health. Yielding is therapeutic.
- [[repression]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s psychological model: repression is the true pathology. What you resist persists; what you express dissipates.
- [[pleasure]]: [[pleasure|Pleasure]] becomes not merely permissible but obligatory — a form of psychological hygiene.
- [[morality]]: Conventional morality is here redefined as a "monstrous law" that creates the very sins it claims to prevent.
