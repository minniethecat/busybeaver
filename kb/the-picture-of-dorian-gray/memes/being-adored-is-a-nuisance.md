## Quotation

> "Being adored is a nuisance. Women treat us just as humanity treats its gods. They worship us, and are always bothering us to do something for them." (006-048)

## Context

The epigram is one of the final turns in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s long dinner-table speech to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in Chapter 6. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just challenged [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s approval of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] — first by accusing [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of not meaning what he says, then by insisting that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is secretly better than he pretends to be. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has dismantled both claims with a series of paradoxes about optimism, self-interest, and morality.

This epigram comes in response to [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s specific observation about the difference between [[worship|adoration]] and love. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats the distinction as self-evidently in favour of love: to be adored is to be burdened with responsibilities one never asked for, while to love is to act freely. The theological metaphor — "as humanity treats its gods" — is characteristic: it elevates a social observation into a cosmic principle while simultaneously diminishing the sacred by comparing it to a drawing-room annoyance.

The gesture that accompanies the speech — toying with fruit — reinforces the tone of amused detachment. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is performing the very indifference he recommends.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram inverts the conventional valuation of [[worship|adoration]]. In the standard romantic script, being adored is the highest form of being loved — it implies [[worship|devotion]], worship, the surrender of the self to the beloved. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats this as a burden. The key word is "bothering": [[worship|adoration]], in his account, is not a gift but a demand. The worshipper gives [[worship|devotion]] and expects favours in return; the god is pestered with prayers.

The theological metaphor works through bathos — the sacred brought down to the level of the drawing-room. "Humanity treats its gods" by making endless requests, and women treat men the same way. The comparison is irreverent in both directions: it mocks women for being demanding and mocks religion for being a model of demandingness. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s wit depends on this double irreverence.

Structurally, the epigram follows the pattern of many of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most memorable lines: it begins with a provocative thesis ("Being adored is a nuisance"), supports it with an unexpected analogy, and ends with a concrete illustration that makes the abstract claim feel like common sense. The progression is from assertion to analogy to anecdote — a miniature rhetorical arc that makes arguments feel like observations.

## Themes

- [[love]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] distinguishes sharply between [[worship|adoration]] — a passive state of being worshipped that imposes obligations — and love, which he treats as an active, freely chosen pursuit.
- [[women]]: The epigram reflects the novel's period-specific generalisations about gender, using women as the vehicle for a broader argument about the asymmetry of romantic relationships.
- [[relationships]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s model of relationships is fundamentally transactional; even [[worship|adoration]] is redescribed as a form of demand.
- [[influence]]: In the context of the larger speech, the epigram contributes to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s project of reshaping [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s understanding of love and relationships — away from [[worship|devotion]] and toward detachment.
