## Quotation

> "That a burnt child loves the fire." (017-090)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to the Duchess of Monmouth, Chapter 17

## Context

This epigram occurs during a rapid-fire ex[[transformation|change]] of witticisms between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and the Duchess of Monmouth at Lady Narborough's tea-table soirée. The Duchess has been teasing [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] with increasingly personal barbs. When she declares "I shall write it in my diary to-night" (017-088) — threatening to memorialise his defeat in their verbal duel — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds with this remarkable inversion of the familiar proverb.

The Duchess immediately counters: "I am not even singed. My wings are untouched." (017-091) [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] parries: "You use them for everything, except flight." (017-092) The ex[[transformation|change]] marks the Duchess as one of the few characters who can match [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrammatic style, and her response — "[[courage-has-passed-from-men-to-women|Courage has passed from men to women]]" (017-093) — shifts the gendered dynamics of the conversation.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] takes the proverbial wisdom — "a burnt child dreads the fire" — and reverses its moral logic. The original proverb teaches caution through painful experience; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s version teaches that the very experience of pain intensifies desire. The burnt child does not dread the fire but *loves* it — because it has *known* it.

This is one of the purest expressions of the novel's decadent epistemology: knowledge through sensation, wisdom through excess. The proverb becomes a celebration of the forbidden. To have been burnt is to have tasted something worth burning for. The epigram condenses [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s entire philosophy: experience, especially dangerous experience, is its own justification.

The line also functions as a psychological observation about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], who is not present in this scene but whose life exemplifies the maxim. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been "burnt" by his sins — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] grows more hideous, his conscience occasionally stirs — yet he returns again and again to the "fire" of sensation. The burnt child who loves the fire is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]].

## Themes

- [[temptation]]: The epigram articulates the core mechanism of temptation — the allure of the forbidden, the magnetising power of known danger.
- [[paradox]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most characteristic rhetorical move is the inversion of received wisdom; this line is a paradigmatic example.
- [[sin]]: The proverb describes a moral psychology in which sin is not repented but repeated, not feared but courted.
