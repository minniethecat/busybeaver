## Quotation

> "Ugliness is one of the seven deadly virtues, Gladys. You, as a good Tory, must not underrate them. Beer, the Bible, and the seven deadly virtues have made our England what she is." (017-020)

## Context

The epigram occurs during an extended verbal duel at [[lady-narborough|Lady Narborough]]'s in Chapter 17. The Duchess of Monmouth — "Gladys" to her friends — has been pressing [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] on his aesthetic philosophy. Moments earlier, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has admitted that "it is better to be [[beauty|beautiful]] than to be good" but quickly added that "no one is more ready than I am to acknowledge that it is better to be good than to be ugly" (017-018). The duchess, hearing the inversion, asks: "Ugliness is one of the seven deadly sins, then?" (017-019).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply does not merely correct her word choice; it rewrites the entire moral vocabulary. Where the duchess hears "sins," [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] substitutes "[[morality|virtue]]s." The substitution is not a slip — it is the point. In his inverted value system, what conventional morality calls [[morality|virtue]]s are in fact the forces that produce the England he disdains: a nation of beer, the Bible, and ugliness — three things he regards with equal contempt.

The phrase "as a good Tory" is a characteristic piece of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s social wit. Gladys is a duchess, presumably Conservative; to call her a "good Tory" while attacking the foundations of Tory England is to compliment her while insulting her world. The technique — insinuation through flattery — is one [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has perfected over years of dinner-party warfare.

## Analysis

The epigram operates on three levels of inversion. The first is the surface paradox: "seven deadly virtues" rewrites the most famous list in Christian moral theology — [[vanity|pride]], greed, wrath, envy, lust, gluttony, sloth — by relabeling the category. The second is the cultural critique: "Beer, the Bible, and the seven deadly virtues" is a devastating triad that reduces English civilization to its least glamorous components: a working-class drink, a middle-class book, and a set of values that prize the unglamorous. The third is the political jab: the "good Tory" duchess is enlisted, against her interests, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s demolition of the world her class has built.

The rhetorical structure is worth noting. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not argue that ugliness is good; he argues that it is a [[morality|virtue]] — a word that carries both moral approval and a suggestion of practical efficacy. Virtues, in this scheme, are not what is morally admirable but what is socially effective. Beer, the Bible, and ugliness have "made our England what she is" — a statement that sounds patriotic but is, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s mouth, an indictment.

The epigram also reveals the limits of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy. He can invert moral categories with brilliant ease, but the inversion is parasitic on the categories it inverts. "Seven deadly [[morality|virtue]]s" only works as a phrase because "seven deadly sins" is in the listener's ear. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is not proposing a new morality; he is performing the inadequacy of the old one, and the performance depends on the audience knowing the original. This is the paradox of the Wildean epigram: it is radical in effect but conservative in method — it can only subvert what already exists.

Within the novel, the epigram marks a stage in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s escalating campaign against English [[reputation|respectability]]. By Chapter 17, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has lived [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy for nearly two decades, and the consequences — visible only to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], on the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — are catastrophic. The epigram's breezy [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] is the sound of a philosophy untested by its own consequences.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: The epigram is built on the opposition between beauty and ugliness that structures the entire novel — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] consistently revalues the former at the expense of the latter.
- [[morality]]: The central move — "sins" becomes "[[morality|virtue]]s" — is a miniature of the novel's project of questioning whether conventional moral categories have any meaning.
- [[civilization]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s sardonic account of what "made England" is a critique of the civilization he inhabits, delivered from within its most privileged precincts.
