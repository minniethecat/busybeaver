## Summary

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] [[dorian-gray]] wanders into a grimy theatre and discovers [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] [[sibyl-vane]], an actress of extraordinary talent.

Captivated by her Shakespeare performances, he [[transformation|fall]]s in love and proposes within weeks.

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] knows him only as "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]." The engagement sets the stage for catastrophe.

## Context

Some weeks after the afternoon in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio [[dorian-makes-his-wish]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wanders London's streets.

He finds a hideous little theatre in the Euston Road (004-038)—gaudy playbills, a dreadful orchestra.

But when [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] steps onto the stage, all the squalor [[transformation|fall]]s away.

## Detailed Account

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is the daughter of Mrs. Vane and has a protective brother, James [[james-vane]], sailing for Australia.

On stage, she transforms into Shakespeare's women with genius that transcends her surroundings.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] returns night after night. He worships from a distance before arranging a meeting.

> "Harry! Sibyl Vane is sacred!" Dorian cried, his cheeks flushed and his eyes burning. (004-056)

He tells [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] that he is engaged "to be married to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]" (004-107).

> "His sudden mad love for Sibyl Vane was a psychological phenomenon of no small interest." (004-105)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is amused, calling it a "psychological phenomenon." He agrees to see [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] perform.

> "If he ever does you any wrong, I shall kill him," James Vane swears before departing. (005-075)

James warns his mother to watch over [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. He suspects "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" of base intentions.

His vow will echo through the novel's final chapters [[in-the-opium-den]], [[death-of-james-vane]].

## Aftermath

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], [[basil-hallward|Basil]], and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] attend [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s next performance.

She gives [[sibyls-bad-performance|a disastrously bad performance]]. Experiencing real love, she can no longer simulate passion.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] rejects her. By morning, she is dead: [[sibyl-vanes-suicide]].

## Thematic Significance

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement embodies the collision between life and art.

He loves [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] not as a person but as a living work of art—"all the heroines of Shakespeare in one" (004-064).

When she becomes real, she ceases to be art, and his love evaporates.

This pattern defines [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s entire trajectory, making this engagement a microcosm of [[art-and-life]].
