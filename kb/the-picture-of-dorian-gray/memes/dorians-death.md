## Summary

In the novel's final scene, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] — returned from the country where he spared the innocent Hetty Merton — confronts his [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the locked schoolroom. The [[the-portrait|painting]] now shows not only the accumulated [[sin|corruption]] of his life but a new hypocrisy in the mouth and fresh blood on the hands. In a desperate act, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] seizes the knife with which he murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] and stabs the [[the-portrait|portrait]], crying to "kill the past." The servants hear an agonized cry; when they break into the room, they find on the wall a splendid [[the-portrait|portrait]] of their master in perfect youth, and on the floor a dead man — withered, wrinkled, loathsome — recognizable only by his rings.

## Context

The final chapter opens with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] returning from a walk on a "lovely night." He has just left Hetty Merton, a village girl he courted but chose to spare from ruin — an act he considers virtuous. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s dinner party follows, where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is questioned about his eternal youth and the disappearance of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. Afterward, alone in his house, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reflects on his one "good deed" and hopes it may have reversed the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s decay.

## Detailed Account

### The Mirror and Self-Loathing (Ch20)

Alone after the party, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] picks up the mirror [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] gave him years ago. Looking at his own perfect reflection, he is suddenly revolted:

> Then he loathed his own beauty, and flinging the mirror on the floor, crushed it into silver splinters beneath his heel. It was his beauty that had ruined him, his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for. (020-002)

### The Portrait Revealed

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] goes upstairs to the locked schoolroom. Hoping his "good deed" with Hetty has cleansed the [[the-portrait|portrait]], he pulls away the purple hanging:

> A cry of pain and indignation broke from him. He could see no change, save that in the eyes there was a look of cunning and in the mouth the curved wrinkle of the hypocrite. The thing was still loathsome — more loathsome, if possible, than before — and the scarlet dew that spotted the hand seemed brighter, and more like blood newly spilled. (020-004)

He realizes his so-called [[morality|virtue]] was merely vanity and hypocrisy — yet another mask. The [[the-portrait|portrait]], which once bore only his sins, now reflects his self-deception with even greater horror.

### The Fatal Stab

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sees the knife — the same one that killed [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] — lying nearby, cleaned of blood. He resolves to destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]], the record of his soul:

> He looked round and saw the knife that had stabbed Basil Hallward. ... As it had killed the painter, so it would kill the painter's work, and all that that meant. It would kill the past, and when that was dead, he would be free. He seized the thing, and stabbed the picture with it. (020-006)

### The Transformation

A terrible cry rings through the house. Servants wake and eventually break into the room:

> When they entered, they found hanging upon the wall a splendid portrait of their master as they had last seen him, in all the wonder of his exquisite youth and beauty. Lying on the floor was a dead man, in evening dress, with a knife in his heart. He was withered, wrinkled, and loathsome of visage. It was not till they had examined the rings that they recognized who it was. (020-007)

## Aftermath / Consequences

1. **The Supernatural Reversal**: The [[the-portrait|portrait]] returns to its original beauty; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s body takes on all the [[sin|corruption]] the [[the-portrait|portrait]] had accumulated.
2. **Moral Resolution**: The novel closes on its central metaphor — art outlasts the sinner, and the soul cannot be killed without killing the self.
3. **The Rings**: The servants can only identify the corpse by its rings — the ultimate erasure of identity through sin.
