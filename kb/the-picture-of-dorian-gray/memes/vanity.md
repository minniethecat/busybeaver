## Definition

Vanity in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is the fatal flaw that sets the tragedy in motion. It is not merely superficial self-regard but a deeper moral vulnerability — the susceptibility to being defined by one's own beauty. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speech in Chapter 2 exploits this vulnerability, transforming [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s innocent self-consciousness into an obsessive terror of aging. Vanity becomes the engine of the plot: it is because [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] values his appearance above all else that he makes his Faustian wish, and it is vanity that blinds him to the moral cost of his preserved youth.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Vanity appears first as a passive quality — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is [[beauty|beautiful]] but unaware of it. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]] and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words awaken his self-consciousness, and the "sense of his own beauty came on him like a revelation" (002-065). From this moment, vanity becomes active: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] begins to treasure his appearance as his most valuable possession.

The [[the-portrait|portrait]] externalizes vanity's consequences. Each time [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sins, the [[the-portrait|portrait]] grows uglier, while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself remains [[beauty|beautiful]]. His vanity is thus both preserved and punished — he keeps his beauty but must live with the knowledge of what it conceals. The novel traces the arc of vanity from self-love to self-loathing: by the end, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] hates the [[the-portrait|portrait]] not because it is ugly but because it is true.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The embodiment of vanity and its victim. His beauty makes vanity almost inevitable, and his surrender to it makes tragedy certain.
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The exploiter of vanity who recognizes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s vulnerability and cultivates it through flattery and warning.

## Key Passages

> The sense of his own beauty came on him like a revelation. He had never felt it before. (002-065)

The moment of awakening — vanity entering [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s consciousness. Before this, he was [[beauty|beautiful]] without self-awareness; after, beauty becomes the center of his identity.

> He grew more and more enamoured of his own beauty, more and more interested in the corruption of his own soul. (011-008)

Wilde's summary of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral trajectory. Vanity and [[sin|corruption]] are linked — self-love becomes a form of self-destruction.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats vanity with more sympathy than conventional morality would dictate. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s vanity is not merely a personal failing but a response to genuine beauty — beauty so extraordinary that self-love seems almost rational. The novel suggests that vanity is dangerous not because it is irrational but because it is insufficient: a life organized around admiration of one's own appearance is ultimately empty. This is a more nuanced critique than simple moral condemnation.
