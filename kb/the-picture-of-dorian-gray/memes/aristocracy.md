## Description

The British aristocracy in the late Victorian period was a hereditary ruling class built on land, title, and social exclusivity. It comprised the peerage (dukes, marquesses, earls, viscounts, barons) and the landed gentry, together controlling vast agricultural estates and dominating the House of Lords. By the 1890s, however, its political power was waning in the face of industrial wealth, democratic reform, and agricultural depression — though its social prestige remained largely intact.

## Role in the Novel

The aristocracy is the social medium in which nearly all of the novel's action unfolds. Dorian Gray is born into it — his mother, Lady Margaret Devereux, was an aristocrat who "made all the men frantic by running away with a penniless young fellow" (005-035). The scandal of his birth haunts him; when James Vane learns Dorian's background, he reflects, "He is probably a member of the aristocracy" (005-035), his suspicion confirmed by the "appearance of it" (005-037) that Dorian carries.

Lord Henry, too, is an aristocrat — his uncle Lord Fermor provides the genealogical intelligence about the Devereux family — and his worldview is entirely the product of a class that has never needed to work, to account, or to repent. The dinner parties at Selby Royal, the season in London, the gossip at clubs: these are the rituals of an aristocracy that Wilde portrays as beautiful, witty, and morally hollow. The novel's central critique is not that the aristocracy is singularly evil, but that its insulation from consequence — "civilized society, at least, is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating" (011-031) — enables Dorian's corruption to go unchecked for decades.

## Social / Historical Context

Wilde was both an insider and a critic of the British aristocracy. He moved in its circles, dined at its tables, and entertained it with his wit — but he also saw its hypocrisies with the clarity of an outsider (as an Irishman in London). The novel reflects the late-Victorian aristocracy at a moment of transition: still socially dominant but increasingly anachronistic, its traditional values eroded by aestheticism, decadence, and the slow advance of democratic reform. Dorian's fate — beautiful, corrupt, and ultimately destroyed by his own excess — can be read as an allegory for the aristocracy itself.
