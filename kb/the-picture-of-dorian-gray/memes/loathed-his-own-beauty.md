## Quotation

> "Then he loathed his own beauty, and flinging the mirror on the floor, crushed it into silver splinters beneath his heel. It was his beauty that had ruined him — his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for." (020-005)

## Context

The final chapter. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is alone in his London house on a night of reckoning. He has tried to do a good deed — sparing a country girl named Hetty Merton from seduction — but [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has mocked his attempt at reformation. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has come to believe that even his "good" action was motivated by vanity: the desire for a new sensation, the pleasure of self-congratulation.

Standing before a mirror, he sees his own beauty and is suddenly revolted by it. The beauty he prayed to preserve in [[dorian-makes-his-wish|Chapter 2]] is now the object of his loathing. He recognizes beauty not as a gift but as the instrument of his damnation: "It was his beauty that had ruined him."

The mirror-smashing is a gesture of self-destruction that prefigures the actual suicide-by-[[the-portrait|portrait]] moments later. He cannot destroy his beauty by smashing a mirror — the mirror only reflects — so he goes upstairs and stabs the [[the-portrait|portrait]], the true mirror of his soul.

## Analysis

The mirror-smashing is one of the novel's most compressed symbolic actions. The mirror is a false [[the-portrait|portrait]] — it reflects only the body, not the soul. Smashing it is a misdirected act of self-hatred: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] attacks the reflection, not the reality. But the gesture announces that the enchantment has broken. For the first time, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sees his preserved youth as a curse rather than a blessing.

"His beauty and the youth that he had prayed for" — the phrase is a retrospective judgment on the entire novel. The wish in Chapter 2, which seemed like a fantasy of eternal youth, is now named as the origin of his ruin. The novel's structure is circular: it ends by reinterpreting its beginning.

Wilde inverts the Victorian moral trajectory. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not repent and find peace; he recognizes his error too late to undo it. The loathing is genuine but sterile — it leads not to redemption but to a final, futile act of violence against the self.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The mirror is art without truth — surface without depth. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] smashes it but keeps the [[the-portrait|portrait]], which is art with truth, until he tries to destroy that too.
- [[sin]]: The passage marks sin's endpoint: not pleasure but self-hatred. The arc from temptation to loathing is the novel's moral geometry.
- [[beauty]]: [[beauty|Beauty]] is revealed as the agent of destruction, not salvation. The novel's aestheticism contains its own critique.
