## Definition

In *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, paradox is a statement that appears self-contradictory yet carries a provocative truth. More than a rhetorical flourish, paradox serves as Wilde's primary vehicle for overturning conventional morality and exposing the hypocrisies of Victorian society. Lord Henry Wotton explicitly declares that "the way of paradoxes is the way of truth. To test reality we must see it on the tight rope. When the verities become acrobats, we can judge them" (003-059).

## Manifestation in the Novel

Paradox permeates the novel through Lord Henry's epigrams. From his first meeting with Dorian Gray, Lord Henry's "wilful paradox" (002-028) works upon the young man like a spell, awakening desires and ambitions he had never confronted. At Lady Agatha's luncheon, Lord Henry's dinner-table performance is described as one that "winged it with paradox" (003-081), transforming the praise of folly into a seductive philosophy. Dorian himself becomes a living paradox — eternally young in appearance while his portrait bears the corruption of his soul.

## Key Characters

**Lord Henry Wotton** is the primary vehicle of paradox in the novel. He uses paradoxical statements to unsettle conventional thinking, declaring that "there is no such thing as a good influence. All influence is immoral" (002-022) and that "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it." His paradoxes are both entertainment and poison — they delight his listeners while undermining their moral foundations.

**Dorian Gray** absorbs Lord Henry's paradoxes and, in time, becomes one himself: a man whose outward beauty masks inner corruption.

## Key Passages

- (002-028) — Lord Henry's words, "spoken by chance, no doubt, and with wilful paradox in them," touch a "secret chord" in Dorian, initiating his transformation under influence.
- (003-059) — Lord Henry's meta-commentary on paradox: "the way of paradoxes is the way of truth. To test reality we must see it on the tight rope."
- (003-081) — At the duchess's luncheon, Lord Henry "winged it with paradox" in an extraordinary improvisation that captivates Dorian entirely.


> For nearly ten minutes he stood there, motionless, with parted lips and eyes strangely bright. He was dimly conscious that entirely fresh influences were at work within him. ## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde uses paradox as more than stylistic decoration. In the aestheticist tradition, paradox is a tool for exposing the arbitrariness of conventional truths. By inverting received wisdom, Wilde forces the reader to question what they accept as moral certainty. Lord Henry's paradoxes, while seductive and brilliant, are shown to have destructive consequences — the novel thus contains a paradox of its own: it celebrates the beauty of paradox while dramatizing its corrosive effects on those who live by it.
