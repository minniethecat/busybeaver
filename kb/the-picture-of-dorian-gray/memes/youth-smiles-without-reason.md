## Quotation

> "But youth smiles without any reason. It is one of its chiefest charms." (014-002)

## Context

The morning after [[murder-of-basil-hallward|murdering Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] sleeps "quite peacefully, lying on his right side, with one hand underneath his cheek. He looked like a boy who had been tired out with play, or study."

His servant brings chocolate and opens the shutters. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wakes with "a faint smile" on his lips. The narrator adds: "His night had been untroubled by any images of pleasure or of pain."

Then comes this reflection: youth smiles without reason. It is among its principal charms.

The timing is everything. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just stabbed his oldest friend to death. He blackmailed [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]] into disposing of the body. Yet he sleeps like a child and wakes smiling. The absence of [[conscience|guilt]] is not moral triumph — it is the vacancy of a soul too shallow for [[conscience|remorse]].

## Analysis

The sentence operates as a pivot between innocence and amorality. A baby smiles without reason — that is charm. A murderer smiles without reason the morning after — that is horror. Wilde's phrasing deliberately blurs the line.

The word "chiefest" is arch, almost Wilde-as-narrator intruding. It signals that this is not merely a psychological observation but a philosophical one. [[youth|Youth]], for Wilde, is not a moral category. It is an aesthetic condition that happens to confer advantages: beauty, charm, the appearance of innocence. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] mechanism makes this explicit — youth persists while conscience decays.

The passage also echoes classical pastoral: the sleeping boy, the peaceful face. But Wilde inverts the pastoral. The sleep is not innocent — it is the sleep of a man who has outsourced his conscience to canvas. The smile on waking is not joy but vacancy.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: [[youth|Youth]]'s smile is [[beauty|beautiful]], and beauty masks everything — including murder.
- [[sin]]: The absence of [[conscience|guilt]] after the novel's most violent act demonstrates sin's hollowing effect. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not overcome his conscience; he has eliminated it.
