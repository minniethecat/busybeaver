## Quotation

> "Good resolutions are useless attempts to interfere with scientific laws. Their origin is pure vanity. Their result is absolutely _nil_. They give us, now and then, some of those luxurious sterile emotions that have a certain charm for the weak. That is all that can be said for them. They are simply cheques that men draw on a bank where they have no account." (008-047)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks these words in Chapter 8, the morning after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's death]]. He has come to [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] expecting to console a grief-stricken [[youth|young]] man — only to find [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] already rationalizing his way out of [[conscience|remorse]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just quoted [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own aphorism back to him: that "there is a [[good-resolutions-always-made-too-late|fatality about good resolutions]] — that they are always made too late" (008-046).

Rather than contradicting his protégé, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] doubles down, expanding the offhand remark into a full philosophical position. The passage is one of the novel's most concentrated expressions of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s moral nihilism — and one of its most seductive.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s argument proceeds through a series of dismantling moves. First, he reclassifies good resolutions from "moral" to "scientific" — they are not virtuous acts of will but "useless attempts to interfere with scientific laws." The language of science grants his [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] an air of objectivity.

Second, he attacks the motive: "Their origin is pure vanity." The person who resolves to be better is not noble but self-regarding. Third, he attacks the outcome: their "result is absolutely _nil_." The italicized _nil_ is Wilde at his most epigrammatic — the finality of the word is the argument.

The passage's rhetorical climax is the metaphor of the cheque: good resolutions are "cheques that men draw on a bank where they have no account." The image is of fraud — of writing promises one cannot [[reputation|honor]]. [[morality|Morality]], in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s account, is a form of financial self-deception.

Tellingly, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] grants that good resolutions offer "luxurious sterile emotions" — pleasure without issue — and that these "have a certain charm for the weak." He classifies moral effort as a kind of aesthetic experience for the feeble, maintaining his own position of strength by contrast.

## Themes

- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s position is a radical moral skepticism — not that morality is wrong, but that it is literally impossible. Good resolutions cannot succeed because they violate the "scientific laws" of human nature.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words here complete [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s education in [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]]. The passage demonstrates how influence works in the novel — not by command, but by providing elegant justifications for what the listener already wishes to believe.
- [[conscience]]: The passage anatomizes how conscience is neutralized: not by denying it exists, but by reclassifying its promptings as vanity and weakness.
