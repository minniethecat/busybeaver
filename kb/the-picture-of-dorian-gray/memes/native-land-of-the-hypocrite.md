## Quotation

> "I know how people chatter in England. The middle classes air their moral prejudices over their gross dinner-tables, and whisper about what they call the profligacies of their betters in order to try and pretend that they are in smart society and on intimate terms with the people they slander. In this country, it is enough for a man to have distinction and brains for every common tongue to wag against him. And what sort of lives do these people, who pose as being moral, lead themselves? My dear fellow, you forget that we are in the native land of the hypocrite." (012-020)

## Context

It is the eve of [[dorian-gray|Dorian's]] thirty-eighth birthday. He is walking home from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry's]] when he encounters [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], who is waiting outside [[mayfair|Dorian's house]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to confront [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] before leaving for Paris — he has heard terrible rumours about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life, about the ruined reputations of those who have been close to him, and he wants to hear from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself that the rumours are untrue.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not deny the rumours. Instead, he attacks the society that spreads them. His speech is a masterclass in deflection: he transforms a moral accusation into a sociological critique. If England is a nation of hypocrites, then the rumours about him are not evidence of his [[conscience|guilt]] but evidence of the nation's prurience. The technique is effective — [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is momentarily thrown off balance — but it is also a confession. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not say the rumours are false; he says those who spread them are hypocrites.

## Analysis

The speech is one of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s most Wildean moments — it could have come from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s lips. The class analysis is sharp ("the middle classes air their moral prejudices over their gross dinner-tables"), the aphoristic structure is elegant, and the final sentence lands with the force of a well-timed epigram. Wilde gives [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] the best lines in this ex[[transformation|change]], even though [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is in the wrong.

But the speech also reveals [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral condition. He does not defend himself by asserting his innocence; he defends himself by attacking the very concept of [[morality|moral judgment]]. If everyone is a hypocrite, then no one has the standing to judge. It is the logical endpoint of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy: the destruction of the moral framework within which [[conscience|guilt]] and innocence have meaning.

The phrase "native land of the hypocrite" is a bitter inversion of patriotic language. England is not the land of liberty or justice but of hypocrisy — a place where the private [[sin|vice]] and public [[morality|virtue]] are not opposed but symbiotic. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], who lives the [[duality|double life]] more completely than anyone, is its truest son.

## Themes

- [[morality]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] dismantles the very possibility of [[morality|moral judgment]] by attacking the character of those who judge.
- [[hypocrisy]]: The speech is itself hypocritical — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] condemns hypocrisy while living the most hypocritical life of all.
- [[reputation]]: The rumours about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] have become impossible to ignore; this speech is his attempt to manage them.
- [[double-life|the double life]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s public defence mirrors his private [[sin|corruption]] — both depend on the gap between appearance and reality.
