## Definition

Individuality, in the context of the novel, is the quality of being distinct and self-defined — the opposite of the conformity that Lord Henry Wotton derides.

Lord Henry articulates this view when defending Dorian's engagement to Sibyl Vane against Basil Hallward's objection:

> Unselfish people are colourless. They lack individuality.
>
> (006-019)

For Lord Henry, individuality is not merely a personal trait but an aesthetic value: the interesting personality is one that refuses to be absorbed into the common morality of the age.

## Lord Henry's Philosophy

Lord Henry extends the theme in a later conversation, elevating individualism to a philosophical principle:

> Individualism has really the higher aim. Modern morality consists in accepting the standard of one's age. I consider that for any man of culture to accept the standard of his age is a form of the grossest immorality.
>
> (006-040)

This passage reveals the radical core of Lord Henry's philosophy: true morality is self-defined, and conformity — even to benevolent social norms — is a kind of cowardice. The individual who thinks for himself is, in Lord Henry's view, the only genuinely moral person.

## Dorian's Individualism

What begins as Lord Henry's abstract principle becomes, in Dorian Gray's life, a darker practice. The narrator describes the pride that Dorian takes in his own singularity:

> At other times, with that pride of individualism that is half the fascination of sin, he watched the portrait with a feeling of pleasure.
>
> (011-025)

Here individualism has shifted from a philosophical ideal to a rationalization of self-indulgence. The "pride of individualism" becomes indistinguishable from the pride that resists repentance. Wilde traces the arc from Lord Henry's elegant paradox to Dorian's moral solitude — a trajectory in which individuality, untethered from conscience, becomes isolation.
