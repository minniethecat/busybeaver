## Quotation

> "I like men who have a future and women who have a past," he answered. "Or do you think that would make it a petticoat party?" (015-047)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at a dinner party hosted by Lady Narborough, an elderly society hostess who has just called him a cynic and asked him to suggest a guest list for her next gathering. The request — "You must tell me what people you would like to meet" (015-046) — is an invitation to social discrimination, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is a miniature sociology of desirability.

The epigram's symmetry is its wit: men are valued for what lies ahead (prospects, ambition, untapped potential), women for what lies behind (experience, mystery, scandal). The chiasmus — future/past, men/women — makes the double standard visible by compressing it into a single balanced sentence. The structure is so neat that it almost disguises the asymmetry it describes: men gain value through time, women through having survived it.

The follow-up question — "Or do you think that would make it a petticoat party?" — is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] at his most performative. The term "petticoat party" suggests a gathering dominated by women, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s feigned concern exposes the epigram's real subject: not who is invited to dinner, but who holds social power and how it is distributed. The joke depends on the assumption that a roomful of women is inherently undesirable — an assumption [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] expects his audience to share.

## Analysis

The epigram is a study in compression. In fifteen words, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] encodes a theory of gender, a sociology of desirability, and a commentary on the double standard. The compression is the argument: social truths that would be unpalatable if spelled out at length become charming when crystallized into an aphorism.

The double standard the epigram articulates is genuinely descriptive of Victorian society. Men were judged by their careers and prospects; women were judged by their reputations and histories. A man with a "past" was interesting; a woman with a "past" was ruined. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram does not challenge this asymmetry — it merely observes it with a stylized detachment that passes for critique.

But the epigram also reveals [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s limitations as a social observer. Both halves of the sentence depend on reducing people to their social utility: men are valuable insofar as they might become something, women insofar as they have already been something. Neither category allows for a person who simply *is*. The same reductiveness that makes the epigram witty also makes it cruel — and this is the recurring pattern of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s discourse throughout the novel.

By Chapter 15, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams have become a known quantity. Lady Narborough calls him a cynic before he speaks, and her laughter at his reply suggests she enjoys the performance without taking it seriously. The dinner-party setting is important: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy flourishes in social spaces where nothing is at stake. The novel contrasts these spaces with the private world of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[conscience|guilt]], where wit provides no protection.

## Themes

- [[high-society]]: The epigram belongs to the dinner-party world of Lady Narborough's salon, where social performance is its own end.
- [[gender]]: The chiasmus encodes a Victorian double standard in miniature — men and women are valued on opposite temporal axes.
- [[pleasure]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s social philosophy reduces human relations to a source of aesthetic pleasure; the epigram is a tool for making conversation [[beauty|beautiful]], not for understanding people.
- [[reputation]]: The word "past" in the context of a woman carries the weight of sexual scandal — the entire history of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s hidden life is compressed into this single euphemism.
