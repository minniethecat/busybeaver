## Quotation

> "The man who could call a spade a spade should be compelled to use one. It is the only thing he is fit for." (017-004)

## Context

The line opens the conversational hostilities of Chapter 17. The scene is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country house, [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], where a dozen guests have gathered for tea. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] strolls over to the table where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and the Duchess of Monmouth are talking and announces his "plan for rechristening everything" (017-002). He has just been thinking about flowers: an orchid he cut for his buttonhole turned out to bear the dreadful name *Robinsoniana*. "It is a sad truth," he declares, "but we have lost the faculty of giving lovely names to things. Names are everything. I never quarrel with actions. My one quarrel is with words" (017-004).

From this aestheticist credo, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] pivots to the attack on "vulgar realism in literature" that produces the spade epigram. The Duchess, playing along, asks what they should call him instead, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] supplies the answer: "His name is Prince Paradox" (017-004). The entire ex[[transformation|change]] sets the tone for the chapter's extended tournament of wit.

## Analysis

The epigram attacks realism not on the grounds of accuracy but on the grounds of taste. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not dispute that a spade is a spade; he disputes that saying so is a [[morality|virtue]]. His quarrel is with the moral claims of realism — the assumption, pervasive in the nineteenth-century novel, that calling things by their plain names is somehow more honest, more virtuous, more true than ornamenting them. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], this is not honesty but poverty of imagination.

The punishment he prescribes — that the plain-speaker should be "compelled to use one" — is perfectly Wildean in its logic. The realist who insists on the literal is sentenced to the literal: if all you can see in a spade is a spade, then manual labor is all you are fit for. The epigram links verbal sophistication to social and intellectual hierarchy. To rename the world is to master it; to accept its given names is to be mastered by it.

The line also performs the very thing it advocates. It does not say "I dislike realism." It invents a character — "the man who could call a spade a spade" — and then destroys him with his own logic. The epigram is an argument conducted entirely through wit, demonstrating by its form that form is substance. Naming, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s world, is a creative act, and those who refuse that creativity deserve the coarsest of fates.
