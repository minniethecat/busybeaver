## Quotation

> "Oh! anything becomes a pleasure if one does it too often," cried Lord Henry, laughing. "That is one of the most important secrets of life. I should fancy, however, that murder is always a mistake. One should never do anything that one cannot talk about after dinner." (019-027)

## Context

This is the second of two devastatingly ironic aphorisms that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers in Chapter 19 at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has just asked him, hypothetically, whether a man who had committed murder could possibly do it again. The question is not hypothetical: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has killed [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] and is probing whether [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] might, inadvertently, offer absolution.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] laughs and treats the question as an occasion for wit. His reply has three movements, each worsening the irony. First, the generalisation that "anything becomes a pleasure if one does it too often" — a hedonist's version of habituation that, applied to murder, is grotesque. Second, the concession that "murder is always a mistake" — not because it is wrong but because it is conversationally inconvenient. Third, the epigram itself: "One should never do anything that one cannot talk about after dinner."

The criterion is not moral but social. An action's wrongness is measured by its suitability as after-dinner conversation. Murder is a mistake not because it violates the sixth commandment but because it violates the rules of good talk. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reduces [[morality|ethics]] to etiquette, and the reduction is delivered to a man who has just confessed — and been disbelieved — to the very crime being dismissed.

## Analysis

The epigram is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy reduced to its purest and most terrible form. The measure of an action is not its moral content but its conversational potential. If you cannot discuss it elegantly over coffee and Chopin, it was a mistake. This is aestheticism as [[morality|ethics]], and the novel has spent twenty chapters demonstrating its inadequacy.

The line also functions as inadvertent judgment. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cannot talk about what he has done — not really. His confession to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is made in hypothetical terms and is immediately disbelieved. His earlier attempt to confess to [[alan-campbell-disposal|Alan Campbell]] was blackmail, not confession. The murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] is, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own terms, the worst thing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has ever done — because it is the one thing he can never, truly, talk about after dinner.

Wilde's dramatic irony is at its most concentrated here. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the master of conversation, spends the entire chapter talking about murder without knowing that he is talking to a murderer. His epigram, meant as wit, becomes unwitting truth: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has done something he cannot talk about, and it is destroying him.

The phrase "after dinner" is precise. It places conversation in its proper Wildean context: the dining room, the coffee, the Chopin, the performance of wit. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s world is bounded by the dinner party. What cannot be discussed there does not, in his philosophy, exist. But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] knows otherwise. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the attic, the body in [[basils-studio|the studio]], the [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]] in the East End — these exist, and they cannot be talked about.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The epigram is the endpoint of treating [[life-the-first-of-arts|life as art]]: actions are judged by their aesthetic and conversational qualities, not their moral ones (showcase).
- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reduction of murder to a "mistake" rather than a sin is the novel's most pointed critique of aestheticised [[morality|ethics]].
- [[murder]]: The epigram cuts to the heart of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s situation: he has done the one thing his world cannot process.
- [[conversation]]: The entire Chapter 19 dialogue is a study in what can and cannot be said — the limits of wit as a mode of truth-telling.
