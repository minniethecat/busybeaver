## Quotation

> "I wish I had, for as sure as there is a God in heaven, if he ever does you any wrong, I shall kill him." (005-075)

## Context

[[james-vane]] speaks these words to his sister [[sibyl-vane]] during their walk through the Euston Road in Chapter 5, on the eve of James's departure for Australia.

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] has been speaking of "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" — the name she gives to [[dorian-gray]], whose real name she does not know. James's reaction is immediate and hostile: he distrusts the stranger, resents [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s absorption in him, and fears for her vulnerability.

When [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] asks whether James would "like to kill" anyone who wronged her, she is half-teasing. James's answer is not teasing at all. He swears by God — the only genuinely religious oath in the novel — and means every word.

The scene is the novel's most concentrated moment of working-class directness. Where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks in epigrams, James speaks in oaths. His language has the force of conviction, not performance.

## Analysis

### The Prophecy

James's vow is one of the novel's most explicit instances of foreshadowing. He *will* return from Australia. He *will* learn of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death. He *will* hunt [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] through the streets of London. And he will come very close to killing him.

When James finally confronts [[in-the-opium-den|Dorian in the opium den]] in Chapter 16, the vow of Chapter 5 is fulfilled — almost. The "almost" is the novel's cruelest twist. James has [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] at gunpoint but is deceived by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s youthful appearance and releases him. The vow, however sincere, fails at the moment of execution.

### Love and Violence

The line binds love and violence together. James's love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] expresses itself as a threat of murder. This is not a paradox but a truth about the Vane family: their love is desperate, protective, and edged with the awareness that the world will hurt them.

Contrast this with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s elegant detachment from all feeling. James *feels* — too much, too violently — and his feeling will destroy him as surely as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s detachment destroys [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].

### The Unheeded Warning

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] does not take the threat seriously. She laughs, [[transformation|change]]s the subject, refuses to believe that her "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" could ever do her wrong. Her innocence is not ignorance but willed blindness — and it costs her life.

James, for all his roughness, sees what [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] cannot: that the world outside the theatre is not a stage, and that "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" is not a character in a play.

## Themes

- **[[violence]]** — The line introduces the threat of physical violence into a novel dominated by psychological cruelty.
- **[[revenge]]** — James's vow establishes the revenge plot that will surface in Chapters 16–18.
- **[[fate]]** — The line functions as prophecy; James speaks his own destiny without knowing it.
- **[[family]]** — The Vane family's love is fierce, protective, and ultimately impotent against the forces arrayed against them.
- **[[foreshadowing]]** — One of the novel's most explicit instances of the technique.
