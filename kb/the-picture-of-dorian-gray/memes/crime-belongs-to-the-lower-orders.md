## Quotation

> "All crime is vulgar, just as all vulgarity is crime. It is not in you, Dorian, to commit a murder. I am sorry if I hurt your vanity by saying so, but I assure you it is true. Crime belongs exclusively to the lower orders. I don't blame them in the smallest degree. I should fancy that crime was to them what art is to us, simply a method of procuring extraordinary sensations." (019-025)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This epigram occurs during [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s final extended conversation with [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], on the evening before Dorian's fateful encounter with [[james-vane|James Vane]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been attempting — obliquely — to confess something of his true nature. He asks [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] what he would say if Dorian told him he had murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]].

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] refuses to take the hypothetical seriously. His refusal is devastatingly ironic: the reader knows that Dorian *has* murdered Basil, making Lord Henry's confidence — "It is not in you, Dorian, to commit a murder" — a perfect inversion of the truth.

## Analysis

Lord Henry's epigram operates on three levels simultaneously. First, it is a class prejudice dressed as an aesthetic principle: crime is "vulgar," and vulgarity belongs to "the lower orders." The aesthetic vocabulary (vulgar, extraordinary sensations) masks a simple social snobbery.

Second, it is a moment of profound dramatic irony. The reader knows Dorian has murdered Basil; Lord Henry does not. His smooth confidence — "I assure you it is true" — exposes the limits of his own philosophy. Aestheticism can explain everything except the actual moral facts it refuses to see.

Third, the epigram articulates Lord Henry's most dangerous idea: that crime and art are interchangeable categories, both valued only for the sensations they produce. If "crime was to them what art is to us," then the only difference between a murderer and an artist is social class — a view that implicitly licenses Dorian's behavior.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: Lord Henry redefines sin as an aesthetic category — crime is "vulgar," not wrong, collapsing moral judgment into taste.
- [[morality]]: The epigram is a case study in how aestheticism systematically evades moral responsibility.
- [[art-and-life]]: The equivalence of crime and art ("simply a method of procuring extraordinary sensations") is the logical endpoint of the novel's central theme.
- [[reputation]]: Lord Henry's refusal to believe ill of the rich and fascinating echoes the novel's earlier observation that society "is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating" (011-030).
