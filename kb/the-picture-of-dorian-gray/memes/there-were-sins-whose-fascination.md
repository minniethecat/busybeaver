## Quotation

> There were sins whose fascination was more in the memory than in the doing of them, strange triumphs that gratified the pride more than the passions, and gave to the intellect a quickened sense of joy, greater than any joy they brought, or could ever bring, to the senses. But this was not one of them. (014-005)

## Context

The morning after murdering [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] wakes in his own bed looking "like a boy who had been tired out with play, or study." He sips his chocolate in the mellow November sun. Then, gradually, "the events of the preceding night crept with silent, blood-stained feet into his brain."

The narrator offers this reflection as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wrestles with the horror of what he has done. The generalisation — "there were sins whose fascination was more in the memory than in the doing of them" — is a piece of psychological analysis that the novel has been building toward. It distinguishes between categories of [[sin|transgression]]: the aesthetic sins that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has pursued for eighteen years (art, sensation, cultivated decadence) and the sin of murder, which belongs to an entirely different order.

The key phrase is "but this was not one of them." Murder, unlike the refined sins of the previous chapters, offers no retrospective pleasure. It is "a thing to be driven out of the mind, to be drugged with poppies, to be strangled lest it might strangle one itself."

## Analysis

The passage is one of the novel's most compressed pieces of moral psychology. It distinguishes three categories of sin:

First, sins whose pleasure is in the memory — the cultivated [[sin|transgression]]s of the aesthete, which become richer in recollection than they were in experience. This is the sin that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy implicitly endorses: [[life-the-first-of-arts|life as art]], experience as material for retrospective appreciation.

Second, sins that gratify the [[vanity|pride]] rather than the passions — "strange triumphs" that feed the intellect's sense of its own power. This describes much of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s eighteen-year career of [[sin|corruption]]: the seductions, the ruined reputations, the "subtle pleasure in the thought that he had really been to the minds of many a fatal presence" ((011-006)).

Third, sins that offer no retrospective pleasure at all — the sin of murder, which must be "driven out of the mind." This is the category that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has now entered, and the narrator's language ("strangled," "drugged," "poppies") already looks forward to the [[the-opium-den|opium den]] of Chapter 16.

The passage is remarkable for what it does not say. It does not say that murder is worse than the aesthetic sins. It says that murder is different in kind — it cannot be aestheticised. The whole architecture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life — the philosophy that turned experience into art and sin into sensation — collapses when confronted with a sin that resists being recollected in tranquillity.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit taxonomy of sin, distinguishing aesthetic [[sin|transgression]]s from acts of violence that cannot be aestheticised (showcase).
- [[memory-and-forgetting]]: The sentence "it was a thing to be driven out of the mind" introduces the theme of forced forgetting that drives the rest of the novel — from opium to the destruction of the [[the-portrait|portrait]].
- [[corruption]]: The "strange triumphs" that gratify [[vanity|pride]] more than passion describe the psychology of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s gradual [[sin|corruption]] over eighteen years.
- [[conscience]]: The passage registers conscience not as [[morality|moral judgment]] but as psychological torment — something that "might strangle one itself."
