## Quotation

> "Each time that one loves is the only time one has ever loved. Difference of object does not alter singleness of passion. It merely intensifies it. We can have in life but one great experience at best, and the secret of life is to reproduce that experience as often as possible." (017-067)

## Context

Lord Henry speaks these words at a dinner gathering at Selby Royal, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country estate in Chapter 17. The company includes the [[the-duchess-of-monmouth|Duchess of Monmouth]], whom Lord Henry addresses directly, calling her "My dear Gladys."

The Duchess has just declared that she does not believe in romantic mediocrity: "We women... can't bear mediocrities" (017-066). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has murmured the provocative suggestion that society people "never do anything else" but love — implying that all social interaction is a form of erotic play. The Duchess challenges this: "Ah! then, you never really love, Mr. Gray" (017-066).

Lord Henry intervenes with a speech that begins as a defence of romantic repetition — "Romance lives by repetition, and repetition converts an appetite into an art" — and then deepens into a meditation on the nature of passion itself. The aphorism that gives this page its title is the philosophical core of that speech.

## Analysis

The sentence "Each time that one loves is the only time one has ever loved" performs a conceptual sleight of hand characteristic of Lord Henry's wit. It takes the language of uniqueness — "the only time" — and applies it not to a single love across a lifetime, but to each love in succession. The paradox dissolves when we understand that for Lord Henry, love is not defined by its object but by its quality of experience.

"Difference of object does not alter singleness of passion." This is the most radical claim in the passage. It severs love from the beloved. Passion is not about who you love but about the intensity with which you love. The object is interchangeable; the passion is constant. This is an aesthetic theory of emotion: the form (passion) is everything, the content (the person) is incidental.

The claim "It merely intensifies it" is counter-intuitive. Conventionally, changing partners dilutes passion — infidelity diminishes love. Lord Henry inverts this: the variety of objects intensifies the singleness of passion, because each new object forces the passion to re-create itself at full intensity.

The closing sentence — "the secret of life is to reproduce that experience as often as possible" — reveals the hedonistic program beneath the philosophy. If passion is object-independent and self-renewing, then the optimal life strategy is not fidelity but serial intensity. Each love is total; each love is the only one; and the goal is to have as many "only ones" as possible.

In the larger arc of the novel, this aphorism is both seductive and ominous. It is spoken at Selby Royal, where [[james-vane|James Vane]] will soon appear at the window — a reminder that objects of past passion do not always consent to being replaced.

## Themes

- [[love]]: Lord Henry dissolves the connection between love and its object, redefining love as a self-contained experience whose value is independent of the beloved.
- [[passion]]: Passion is "single" not in the sense of fidelity but in the sense of indivisible intensity — each instance of passion is complete and self-sufficient.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: The aphorism represents the logical endpoint of Lord Henry's aestheticism: if life is to be lived as art, then love is to be lived as a recurring aesthetic experience.
- [[experience]]: The passage articulates a theory of experience as repetition — life has "but one great experience at best," and wisdom consists in reproducing it.
- [[object]]: The object (the beloved, the artwork, the experience) is demoted to a mere occasion for passion — a radical devaluation of the particular in favour of the general.
