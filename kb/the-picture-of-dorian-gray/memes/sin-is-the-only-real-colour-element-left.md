## Quotation

> "Yes," answered Lord Henry dreamily, "the costume of the nineteenth century is detestable. It is so sombre, so depressing. Sin is the only real colour-element left in modern life." (002-043)

## Context

The scene is still Chapter 2, in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio. Tea has been brought in, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] proposes going to the theatre that evening (002-042). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] grumbles about the nuisance of dressing for the occasion: "It is such a bore putting on one's dress-clothes... And, when one has them on, they are so horrid" (002-042).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] seizes on the complaint and elevates it into a cultural diagnosis. What began as a practical grumble about stiff collars becomes an indictment of an entire century's visual poverty — and, by implication, its moral poverty as well.

## Analysis

The word "dreamily" is the key to the sentence's tone. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is not making a philosophical argument here; he is musing, letting an aesthetic impression find language. The remark has the feel of an improvised performance — a throwaway that, because it is so elegantly phrased, lands with the weight of a thesis.

The sentence works by yoking three registers that Victorian culture kept separate. "Costume" belongs to fashion and the theatrical. "Detestable" and "depressing" are moral-emotional judgments. "Sin" belongs to religion. By threading them together, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] collapses the distinction between dress, mood, and morality — as if the black of a gentleman's evening clothes and the darkness of the soul were symptoms of the same malady.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] immediately protests: "You really must not say things like that before Dorian, Harry" (002-044). The protest is not a rebuttal but a recognition of danger. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] understands that for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — young, impressionable, already stirred by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s earlier words — the equation of sin with aesthetic interest is a permission slip. If sin is the only colour left, then a life without sin is a life in grey.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: The aphorism aestheticizes sin, transforming it from a moral category into a visual one — the only vividness in a drab age (showcase).
- [[modernity]]: The indictment of the nineteenth century's "sombre" costume is part of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s broader critique of modern life as timid and self-denying.
- [[aesthetics]]: The sentence enacts the novel's central transvaluation — beauty is the highest good, and ugliness (even moral ugliness) is the real sin.
