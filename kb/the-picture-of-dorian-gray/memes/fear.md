## Definition

Fear in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* operates as a Gothic atmosphere, a psychological motive, and an existential condition.

Basil describes Dorian as appearing like "a curious dream from which he feared he might awake" (001-004). This establishes dread as a narrative keynote from the opening pages.

Wilde presents fear as both warning signal and corrupting force. The fear of exposure drives Dorian to murder, while the fear of death makes him cling desperately to life.

The novel's Gothic machinery amplifies these fears into tangible horrors. Psychological dread transforms into the physical decay of the portrait.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Fear enters the novel through Basil's premonitory anxiety about exhibiting the portrait.

"There was a look of fear in his eyes, such as people have when they are suddenly awakened" (002-039). This early fear proves prophetic.

After the portrait's first transformation, Dorian feels "a strange sense of fear, for which he could not account" (012-002). This develops into systematic dread of discovery.

He hides the portrait in the old schoolroom. The silent house moves "as though it feared to wake the sleepers" (011-011). He lives in perpetual anxiety that someone will see the truth.

The fear of exposure culminates in the murder of Basil Hallward. Afterwards, Dorian is haunted by the fear of retribution, embodied in James Vane.

When James Vane is killed, Dorian feels "the wild desire to live, most terrible of all man's appetites" (016-009). Beneath his cultivated indifference lies a primal terror of death.

The novel's final act shows Dorian trying to escape "the fear that seemed to him at times to be almost too great to be borne" (011-025). He discovers the fear he has been fleeing is himself.

## Key Characters

**Dorian Gray** is the novel's central figure of fear. Dread of exposure, aging, and death drives his most destructive actions.

**Basil Hallward** fears for Dorian's soul, and for what the portrait might reveal about his own desire. This leads him to confront Dorian and meet his death.

**James Vane** embodies retributive fear. His relentless pursuit transforms Dorian from detached aesthete to terrified man.

**Lord Henry Wotton** is notably free from fear. He observes Dorian's terror with detached amusement and never experiences the consequences he inspires.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde weaves fear into the novel's Gothic texture while maintaining psychological realism. The portrait is a supernatural device and a metaphor for the fear of being truly seen.

He connects fear to the aestheticist critique of conventional morality. Just as Lord Henry dismisses conscience as cowardice, fear becomes the cost of caring about society's judgments.

Yet the novel suggests that living without fear leads not to freedom but to deeper terror. The fear Dorian cannot escape is not of society or death, but of the self he has become.

Wilde thus treats fear as an inescapable consequence of moral awareness. To have a conscience is to be capable of fear.
