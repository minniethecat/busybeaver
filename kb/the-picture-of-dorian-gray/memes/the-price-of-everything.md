## Quotation

> "Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing." (004-014)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks this epigram in Chapter 4, at his own house in [[mayfair|Mayfair]], in the presence of his wife, Lady Henry, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. The conversation has turned — with Lady Henry's characteristic nervous energy — to music, Wagner, and the nature of pleasure [004-010–012]. Lady Henry, who speaks in "a curious breaking voice" and punctuates her sentences with "a nervous staccato laugh" (004-011), remarks on the charm of hearing _Lohengrin_ — "the music produces that effect on me. It seems to reproduce the emotions one has lived through" (004-010).

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] politely demurs: "I am afraid I don't think so, Lady Henry" (004-012). Lady Henry deflects by attributing the view to her husband: "that is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]]'s views, isn't it, [[dorian-gray|Mr. Gray]]?" [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], entering the room late and catching the tail of this ex[[transformation|change]], responds with the epigram as a kind of conversational rescue — turning his wife's awkwardness into an occasion for wit.

The scene is notable for the glimpse it gives of the Wottons' marriage: Lady Henry is nervous, eager to please, and slightly ridiculous; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats her with amused condescension. The epigram, for all its brilliance, is also an act of social domination — Henry uses it to reclaim control of a conversation his wife was mishandling.

## Analysis

The epigram is one of Wilde's most quoted lines, and its structure deserves close attention. The opposition between "price" and "value" is not merely rhetorical — it maps onto a fundamental distinction in classical economics, where "price" is the market ex[[transformation|change]] value and "value" is the intrinsic worth. Wilde's innovation is to claim that modern people have lost the ability to perceive the latter. They can calculate cost but not assess significance.

"Nowadays" is the epigram's most important word — it historicises the claim, framing it as a condition of modernity rather than a permanent feature of human nature. The Victorian era, with its industrial capitalism, its worship of commerce, and its reduction of all things to market terms, is the target. Henry, who never works and never pays his bills (003-005), positions himself as a critic of this commercial culture — but he is also its product, a leisured aristocrat whose leisure is funded by the very system he disdains.

The epigram becomes bitterly ironic when read against [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s trajectory. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will come to "know [[nowadays-people-know-the-price|the price of everything]]" in the most literal sense — he will spend decades acquiring [[beauty|beautiful]] objects, indulging every appetite, treating experience as a commodity. And he will lose entirely the sense of "value" — moral, [[soul|spirit]]ual, human — that might have saved him. The epigram is a prophecy that its speaker does not recognise as such: it describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s future.

Wilde's genius here is to make the epigram work on both a social and a psychological level. On the surface, it is a piece of dinner-party cynicism. Deeper, it is a diagnosis of the [[soul|spirit]]ual condition of the age — and of the novel's protagonist.

## Themes

- [[society]]: The epigram is a critique of Victorian commercial culture and its reduction of all values to market terms.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: Henry's epigrams consistently position him as an ironic observer of a society he [[transformation|decline]]s to take seriously.
- [[materialism]]: The distinction between price and value is the novel's recurring meditation on what is truly worth having.
- [[morality]]: The loss of moral value is the inevitable consequence of reducing everything to price — a process [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] enacts across the novel.
