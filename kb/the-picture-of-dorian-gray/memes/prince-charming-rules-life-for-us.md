## Quotation

> Sibyl Vane tossed her head and laughed. "We don't want him any more, Mother. Prince Charming rules life for us now." Then she paused. A rose shook in her blood and shadowed her cheeks. Quick breath parted the petals of her lips. They trembled. Some southern wind of passion swept over her and stirred the dainty folds of her dress. "I love him," she said simply. (005-007)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] speaks these words to her mother, Mrs. Vane, in their shabby theatrical lodgings. Mrs. Vane has been pressing [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] about [[james-vane|Jim]] — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s brother, who is about to sail for Australia and wants to see his sister before he leaves. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], radiant with new love, dismisses the concern: [[james-vane|Jim]] is no longer the center of her world. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] — whom she knows only as "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]," having met him at the theatre but never learned his real name — has taken his place.

The scene is dense with dramatic irony. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s declaration of love is entirely sincere, but the reader knows what she does not: that "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], that his love for her is rooted in her art not her self, and that the romance will end in her destruction. The effusive physical description — "a rose shook in her blood," "southern wind of passion" — is Wilde at his most lushly romantic, and also at his most ominous: the language of nature overwhelming the language of caution.

The line "We don't want him any more" is casual, almost cruel in its dismissal of [[james-vane|Jim]], who loves [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] with a protective ferocity that will later drive the revenge plot. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] does not mean to be cruel — she is simply absorbed, transformed by love into someone for whom the past (her brother, her mother's anxieties, the practical world) has ceased to exist.

## Analysis

The epigram's structure is a miniature drama of love's arrival. The first sentence dismisses the old order ([[james-vane|Jim]], the family's dependence on him). The second sentence announces the new order ([[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] as the governing principle of existence). Between them, Wilde inserts a paragraph of pure physical description — the rose in the blood, the quickened breath, the trembling lips — that represents love as a force of nature, beyond language and beyond will.

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] does not say "I love [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" or "I have met a man who will support us." She says "Prince Charming rules life for us now" — the verb is political ("rules"), the scope is total ("life"), and the beneficiary is collective ("us"). Love has restructured her entire universe, and she assumes — with the confidence of youth — that it has restructured her mother's universe as well.

The name "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" is itself significant. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] lives in the world of fairy tales — onstage she is [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]], Rosalind, Imogen — and she has cast [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in the role of the prince. The fairy-tale frame is both romantic and fatal: in fairy tales, the prince saves the heroine; in this novel, the prince destroys her. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s inability to distinguish between theatrical romance and real love is precisely what makes her vulnerable.

## Themes

- [[love]]: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s declaration is the novel's purest expression of romantic love — and also its most ironic, given what follows.
- [[infatuation]]: The suddenness and totality of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s passion marks it as infatuation, a state in which the beloved is a projection rather than a person.
- [[illusion]]: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s love is built on illusion — she does not know [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s name, his nature, or his intentions — and the novel will punish illusion with death.
- [[sibyl-vane]]: The epigram encapsulates [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s character: passionate, theatrical, innocent, doomed.
