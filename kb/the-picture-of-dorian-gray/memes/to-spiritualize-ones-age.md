## Quotation

> "To spiritualize one's age — that is something worth doing." (007-005)

## Context

[[basil-hallward]] speaks this line to [[dorian-gray]] in Chapter 7, after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has announced his engagement to [[sibyl-vane]] and described her effect on him. [[lord-henry-wotton]] has been cynical about the match, but [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s response is entirely different.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] elaborates: "If this girl can give a soul to those who have lived without one, if she can create the sense of beauty in people whose lives have been sordid and ugly, if she can strip them of their selfishness and lend them tears for sorrows that are not their own, she is worthy of all your [[worship|adoration]], worthy of the [[worship|adoration]] of the world."

The statement is one of the novel's purest expressions of idealism. Where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] sees [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] as raw material for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s aesthetic education, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] sees her as a transformative force — an artist whose medium is not paint or words but human souls. "The gods made [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] for you," he tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. "Without her you would have been incomplete."

The tragic irony is total. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] will, within pages, lose her art through the very love [[basil-hallward|Basil]] celebrates, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will destroy her.

## Analysis

### Art as Moral Transformation

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s vision of art is diametrically opposed to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], art is sensation — something to be consumed, experienced, and discarded. For [[basil-hallward|Basil]], art is transformation — something that [[transformation|change]]s the soul of the beholder. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s acting, in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s account, does not merely entertain; it "gives a soul," "creates the sense of beauty," "strips them of their selfishness." Art is a moral force.

This is the idealist position the novel is most skeptical of. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s faith in [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s transformative power is [[beauty|beautiful]] and wrong. Art, in this novel, does not save — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] himself painted becomes the instrument of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|corruption]]. But [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s faith is also what makes him the novel's most sympathetic character.

### "Spiritualize" — The Word

The verb "[[soul|spirit]]ualize" is carefully chosen. It means to give [[soul|spirit]]ual character to something material — to raise matter toward [[soul|spirit]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s claim is that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], through her acting, performs this alchemy on her age. She takes the sordid and ugly lives of her audience and gives them soul, beauty, tears. This is the highest possible praise in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s vocabulary.

The word also echoes the novel's broader preoccupation with the relationship between surface and depth, body and soul. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy is that his body remains [[beauty|beautiful]] while his soul rots. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s vision, does the opposite: she works on souls through surfaces, making the [[soul|spirit]]ual visible.

### The Wreck of Idealism

Every idealist claim [[basil-hallward|Basil]] makes in this speech is immediately contradicted by events. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] loses her art because she [[transformation|fall]]s in love with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — real emotion destroys theatrical emotion. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] abandons her because she can no longer act — he loved her art, not her. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s vision of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] as [[soul|spirit]]ual savior is exposed as naive. But the novel does not mock [[basil-hallward|Basil]] for his naivety; it mourns the world in which such idealism is impossible.
