## Overview

Mr. Hubbard is the frame-maker of South Audley Street, "the celebrated frame-maker" (010-017) whose professional reputation makes him the natural choice when [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] decides to move the [[the-portrait|portrait]] from his main rooms to the old schoolroom at the top of the house. His appearance in Chapter 10 is brief but narratively pivotal: it is Mr. Hubbard and his assistant who physically transport the painting, and it is during this process that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] covers the portrait with a purple satin coverlet—an act that inaugurates the systematic concealment of the portrait's moral testimony.

## Biography

Mr. Hubbard appears only in Chapter 10, when [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] summons him to move the [[the-portrait|portrait]] from the ground-floor room to the disused schoolroom upstairs. The narrator establishes his professional standing immediately: "there was another knock, and Mr. Hubbard himself, the celebrated frame-maker of South Audley Street, came in with a somewhat rough-looking young assistant" (010-017). The fact that "Mr. Hubbard himself" attends—rather than sending only an assistant—suggests both the value of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s patronage and the tradesman's awareness of his client's social importance.

> Mr. Hubbard himself, the celebrated frame-maker of South Audley Street, came in with a somewhat rough-looking young assistant. Mr. Hubbard was a florid, red-whiskered little man, whose admiration for art was considerably tempered by the inveterate impecuniosity of most of the artists who dealt with him.
>
> (010-017)

Throughout the scene, Mr. Hubbard exhibits "the true tradesman's bustling, obsequious protests" (010-024), a class marker that [[oscar-wilde|Wilde]] renders with satirical precision. He offers to send someone to polish the frame, inquires whether the painting has been varnished recently, and generally performs the rituals of deference expected of a tradesman in the presence of a gentleman. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] asks him and his assistant to carry the heavy painting up four flights of stairs, Mr. Hubbard complies without objection. After the task is complete, he "tramped downstairs, followed by the assistant" (010-035), his departure as unceremonious as his entrance.

> The elaborate character of the frame had made the picture extremely bulky, and now and then, in spite of the obsequious protests of Mr. Hubbard, who had the true tradesman's spirited dislike of seeing a gentleman doing anything useful, Dorian put his hand to it so as to help them.
>
> (010-024)

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Hubbard's function in the novel is primarily structural: he is the mechanism by which the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is physically relocated from a public space to a private one. Without his labour—and without the covering that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] arranges during the move—the portrait's transformation would remain visible to any servant or visitor who entered the ground-floor room. The scene at the top of the stairs, where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] covers the portrait with the "large purple satin coverlet heavily embroidered with gold" that had originally served as a pall for his grandfather's coffin (010-032), links the frame-maker's practical service to the novel's Gothic symbolism: the portrait is being buried alive.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Hubbard is one of several minor tradesman characters in the novel—alongside [[mr-isaacs|Mr. Isaacs]] the theatre manager and the chemist who supplies [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]]—whose professional competence is rendered faintly comic by [[oscar-wilde|Wilde]]'s satirical eye. His obsequiousness is the authentic behaviour of a Victorian tradesman who depends on aristocratic patronage, and his unquestioning execution of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s odd request (moving a valuable painting to a disused room) reflects the social dynamics that insulate the wealthy from scrutiny. He is too deferential, too eager to please, to ask why a celebrated portrait should be hidden away in a dusty schoolroom.

## Thematic Significance

Mr. Hubbard embodies the theme of [[class|class]] and social deference: the tradesman's obedience, his reluctance to question a gentleman's eccentric wishes, is precisely what allows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to construct the architecture of concealment that sustains his double life. In this sense, Mr. Hubbard is an unwitting enabler—a small but necessary cog in the machinery of secrecy that drives the novel's plot.
