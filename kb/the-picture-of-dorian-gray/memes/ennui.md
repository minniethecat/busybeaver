## Definition

Ennui, in Wilde's novel, is more than ordinary boredom. It is a spiritual exhaustion — the *taedium vitae* of classical tradition — that afflicts those who have exhausted the available pleasures of life. It is the shadow-side of the decadent pursuit of sensation: having tasted everything, the decadent finds that nothing satisfies. As [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] warns, "The only horrible thing in the world is *ennui*, Dorian. That is the one sin for which there is no forgiveness" (018-025).

## Manifestation in the Novel

The concept first appears in Chapter 9, in the narrator's account of Dorian's grandfather: "the old lord had nothing to do, almost died of *ennui*, and became a confirmed misanthrope" (009-017). This passing reference establishes ennui as an aristocratic ailment, the disease of those with too much leisure and too little purpose.

By Chapter 11, ennui has become the constant companion of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s pursuits. The narrator describes him as "sick with that ennui, that terrible *taedium vitae* that comes upon those to whom life denies nothing" (011-034). This is the paradox at the heart of Dorian's hedonism: the pursuit of pleasure leads not to fulfillment but to a weariness so profound that life itself becomes burdensome.

The theme culminates in Chapter 18, where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers his definitive pronouncement on ennui as the unforgivable sin — unforgivable precisely because it represents an exhaustion not of the body but of the will to desire.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The novel's supreme example of ennui's grip. Having pursued every sensation and exhausted every pleasure, he is left hollow and haunted.
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The theorist of ennui, who diagnoses it in others while affecting immunity to it himself. His detachment is itself a defense against the weariness he describes.

## Key Passages

> He grew more and more enamoured of his own beauty, more and more interested in the corruption of his own soul. He would examine with minute care, and sometimes with a monstrous and terrible delight, the hideous lines that seared the wrinkling forehead or crawled around the heavy sensual mouth. (011-004)

Dorian's self-examination is driven by ennui: having exhausted external pleasures, he turns inward, studying his own corruption as if it were a new form of entertainment.

## Wilde's Treatment

Ennui functions in the novel as the inevitable endpoint of aestheticism taken to its extreme. Wilde, who in his own life moved through the decadent circles of fin-de-siècle London and Paris, portrays ennui not as a moral failing but as an aesthetic dead-end. The novel's implicit critique is that a life devoted solely to sensation and beauty is subject to the law of diminishing returns: each pleasure is less intense than the last, until even sin becomes tedious.
