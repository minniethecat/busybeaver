## Role in the Novel

Margaret Devereux, Lady Margaret Devereux, is Dorian Gray's mother, whose story is revealed in Chapter 3 through the conversation between Lord Henry and his uncle Lord Fermor. She was, by all accounts, an "extraordinarily beautiful girl" who "made all the men frantic" when she came out in London society. Her beauty was such that she might have married anyone; instead, she eloped with a penniless young man of no family. (003-010) (003-014)

Her father, the last Lord Kelso, responded with implacable cruelty. He arranged to have her husband killed in a duel—"a sort of duel, or something of that kind"—and then disowned his daughter entirely. Margaret died within a year of giving birth to Dorian, the victim, as Lord Fermor puts it, of her father's vengeance. (003-009) (003-012)

Lord Fermor, recalling her debut into society decades earlier, paints a vivid portrait of her beauty and the scandal of her elopement:

> "She was an extraordinarily beautiful girl, Margaret Devereux, and made all the men frantic by running away with a penniless young fellow—a mere nobody, sir, a subaltern in a foot regiment, or something of that kind."
>
> (003-010)

## A Figure of Romantic Tragedy

Margaret Devereux is the novel's central figure of romantic tragedy—a woman destroyed by the collision between love and the rigid social codes of the aristocracy. Lord Fermor describes her as "one of the loveliest creatures I ever saw," and the lingering admiration in his voice, decades after her death, testifies to the impression she made. Her story is a cautionary tale embedded within the novel's larger narrative: beauty, when it defies the social order, can be fatal. (003-010) (003-014)

The parallel between Margaret Devereux and Sibyl Vane is significant. Both women are exceptionally beautiful; both are destroyed by their love for men who cannot or will not protect them. Margaret's tragedy foreshadows Sibyl's, and the echo between their fates suggests that the world of the novel is one in which beautiful women who love unwisely are systematically destroyed. (003-014)

## Legacy in Dorian

Margaret's beauty lives on in her son, and it is through Dorian that her genetic and symbolic legacy is transmitted to the novel's present. Dorian inherits not only Margaret's extraordinary looks but also her position as an object of fascination and desire. The novel implicitly asks whether Dorian, like his mother, is fated to be destroyed by the beauty that makes him exceptional. (003-009) (003-014)

The absence of maternal influence in Dorian's life—Margaret died when he was an infant—is one of the unspoken absences that shape his character. Raised without a mother, inheriting wealth from the grandfather who destroyed her, Dorian enters adulthood with a legacy of beauty and cruelty intertwined. (003-012)
