## Definition

The portrait is the supernatural object at the heart of the novel. Painted by [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] as a masterpiece of aesthetic [[worship|devotion]], it becomes enchanted after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wishes that it might age in his place. Throughout the novel, the portrait functions as a visual conscience — its changing appearance records every sin [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] commits, growing increasingly hideous while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself retains his youthful beauty. It is both a Gothic de[[sin|vice]] and a psychological symbol, externalizing the hidden [[sin|corruption]] that polite society conceals.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The portrait's role evolves across the novel. In Chapter 1, it is [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s greatest artistic achievement — a painting so perfect that it seems to capture its subject's soul. After [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s wish in Chapter 2, it becomes a magical object: the "sense of his own beauty came on him like a revelation" (002-065) as he realizes the portrait's true nature. Through the middle chapters, [[dorian-hides-the-portrait|the portrait is hidden]] away in the old schoolroom, visited secretly by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to measure his [[sin|moral decay]]. In the final chapter, the portrait becomes the instrument of his destruction: stabbing the painting kills [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself, while the portrait returns to its original beauty.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The portrait's subject and prisoner. His relationship with it moves from admiration to fear to hatred.
- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: The portrait's creator, who puts too much of himself into his work — and is killed by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] for seeing what the portrait has become.

## Key Passages

> If it were I who was to be always young, and the picture that was to grow old! For that — for that — I would give everything! Yes, there is nothing in the whole world I would not give! I would give my soul for that! (002-080)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s Faustian wish — the moment the portrait's supernatural transformation begins. The passage captures the desperate, almost childish intensity of his desire.

> The portrait was to bear the burden of his shame: that was all. (008-074)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s rationalization of his bargain. The passage reveals his growing comfort with the arrangement — and his moral detachment from its implications.

## Wilde's Treatment

The portrait is Wilde's most ingenious narrative de[[sin|vice]]. It allows him to literalize the Gothic theme of the double — the [[duality|hidden self]] that respectable society conceals. At the same time, it functions as a commentary on art: the portrait is, after all, a painting, and its deterioration can be read as a metaphor for what happens when art is forced to bear moral weight. The portrait's final restoration — [[beauty|beautiful]] again after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s death — suggests that art ultimately transcends the moral uses to which it is put, returning to pure aesthetic form.
