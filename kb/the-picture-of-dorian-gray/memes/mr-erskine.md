## Role in the Novel

Mr. Erskine of Treadley is an elderly gentleman who appears exclusively in Chapter 3, at Lady Agatha's dinner party. He occupies the seat to Lady Agatha's left—a position of honor that signals his status as a respected figure in her social circle. Throughout the dinner conversation, he functions as a gentle, slightly bewildered interlocutor to Lord Henry, whose paradoxical pronouncements he receives with a mixture of curiosity, mild protest, and self-deprecating humor. (003-036)

His role is to provide a contrast to Lord Henry's dazzling but unsettling wit. Where Lord Henry is young, sharp, and subversive, Mr. Erskine is old, mild, and conventional. Yet he is not simply a target of ridicule; Wilde grants him a certain dignity, and his polite scepticism toward Lord Henry's epigrams provides a counterweight that prevents the scene from becoming a mere showcase for Lord Henry's brilliance. (003-047) (003-053)

## Character and Temperament

Mr. Erskine is presented as a gentleman of the old school—courteous, a little bewildered by modern cleverness, but not without a gentle wisdom of his own. When Lord Henry declares that "to be educated" is a terrible burden, Mr. Erskine responds plaintively, "I don't feel up to being educated," a reply that is both self-deprecating and quietly deflating. His manner throughout the dinner is one of mild, smiling engagement; he listens more than he speaks, and when he does speak, it is with a diffidence that contrasts sharply with Lord Henry's theatrical self-assurance. (003-047) (003-053) (003-078)

Lord Henry looks over at Mr. Erskine at one point and remarks that "Humanity takes itself too seriously. It is the world's original sin." Mr. Erskine, characteristically, does not argue; he listens. His silence in the face of Lord Henry's provocations can be read either as assent, polite disagreement, or simply the disengagement of an old man who has heard clever young men before and knows that their cleverness passes. (003-070)

## The Exchange on Paradox

The most revealing exchange involving Mr. Erskine occurs when Lord Henry makes one of his characteristic paradoxical statements, and Mr. Erskine asks, gently, "Was that a paradox?" When Lord Henry replies that he did not think so, Mr. Erskine murmurs "Perhaps it was" with a smile—a response that manages to be both agreeable and subtly critical. The moment encapsulates Mr. Erskine's role in the dinner-party dynamic: he is the gentle sceptic, never openly challenging but never quite convinced either. (003-059)

## On Writing and Literature

In a brief but significant exchange near the end of the dinner scene, Lord Henry tells Mr. Erskine that he would like to write a novel—"a novel as lovely as a Persian carpet, and as unreal." Mr. Erskine responds that he "used to have literary ambitions" himself but gave them up—a confession that hints at a life of quiet disappointments and abandoned dreams, the kind of life that Lord Henry, with his relentless pursuit of sensation, would find intolerable. (003-087) (003-088) (003-093)

This exchange provides one of the few moments in the chapter where a character other than Lord Henry reveals something personal and vulnerable, and it deepens Mr. Erskine beyond the mere type of the "elderly gentleman."

## Narrative Function

Mr. Erskine represents the older generation in Wilde's social comedy—courteous, a little bewildered by the new aestheticism, but not without his own gentle wisdom. His presence at Lady Agatha's dinner anchors the scene in a recognizable social reality, preventing it from floating entirely into the realm of Lord Henry's brilliant but weightless paradoxes. (003-036) (003-070) (003-078)
