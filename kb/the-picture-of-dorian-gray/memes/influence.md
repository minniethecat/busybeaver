## Definition

In *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]*, influence is the mechanism by which one character reshapes another's beliefs, values, and ultimately their destiny. It is not a neutral force — Wilde presents it as inherently dangerous, a violation of the self. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] articulates this most directly: "There is no such thing as a good influence, [[dorian-gray|Mr. Gray]]. All influence is immoral — immoral from the scientific point of view" (002-022). Influence in the novel operates through conversation, aphorism, and example, spreading like a contagion from one mind to another.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Influence manifests primarily through [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s relationship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. From their first meeting in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words begin to reshape [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-conception. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] himself recognizes the danger, warning [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]: "Don't try to influence him. Your influence would be bad" (001-080). Yet the influence is already at work — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reflects that "some subtle influence passed from him to me" (001-056), and soon after, "entirely fresh influences were at work within him" (002-028).

The novel traces a chain of influence: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] influences [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in turn influences others ([[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], Alan Campbell, and the [[youth|young]] men of [[high-society|London society]] whose lives he is said to have ruined). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] represents the counter-force — an influence grounded in genuine affection and artistic [[worship|devotion]], but one that proves powerless against [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s seductive philosophy.

## Key Characters

- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The primary agent of influence, who treats the shaping of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul as an artistic experiment. His epigrams and paradoxes provide the intellectual framework for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s descent.
- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The subject of influence who becomes an agent of it. Once corrupted, he exerts a fatal fascination over others, drawing them into his orbit and leaving ruin in his wake.
- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: The failed counter-influence, whose love and artistic [[worship|devotion]] to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cannot compete with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy of pleasure.

## Key Passages

> There is no such thing as a good influence, Mr. Gray. All influence is immoral — immoral from the scientific point of view. Because to influence a person is to give him one's own soul. (002-022)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most famous declaration on the subject. It defines influence as a form of [[soul|spirit]]ual colonization — the erasure of another's authentic self.

> Some subtle influence passed from him to me, and for the first time in my life I saw in the woodland the grace and comeliness that we had always been taught to look for in pictures and sculpture. (001-056)

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] describes how [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s presence transformed his artistic vision — a positive instance of influence, but one that foreshadows the more dangerous influence to come.

> Don't spoil him. Don't try to influence him. Your influence would be bad. (001-080)

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s prescient warning to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], uttered before the [[sin|corruption]] has begun. It establishes influence as a central dramatic tension from the novel's opening chapters.

> He was conscious that entirely fresh influences were at work within him. Yet they seemed to him to have come really from himself. (002-028)

Wilde captures the insidious nature of influence — it feels like self-discovery even as it is externally imposed. This passage marks [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s first internal transformation after meeting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]].

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde explores influence with characteristic duality. As an artist and critic, he was deeply interested in the relationship between influence and originality — the idea that all art is influenced, yet great art transcends its sources. In the novel, influence is both creative ([[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s art is inspired by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]) and destructive ([[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy destroys [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral compass). This mirrors the Aesthetic movement's ambivalence about the relationship between art and morality — can an influence be judged by its effects, or only by its aesthetic quality?

The Preface's declaration that "there is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book" stands in tension with the novel's clear depiction of influence as a morally charged force. Wilde seems to suggest that while art may be amoral, the influence of one person upon another is inescapably ethical.
