## Quotation

> "Love is more than money." (005-005)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane]] speaks this line to her mother in Chapter 5, during a conversation about "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s name for [[dorian-gray]], whose real identity she does not yet know.

Mrs. Vane has expressed concern about money — the family is in debt to Mr. Isaacs, the theatre manager. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s response is immediate and absolute: love transcends such material considerations. She is in the first flush of romantic feeling, and to her, love is not merely more important than money — it is a different order of value entirely.

The line is short — five words — but it is [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s credo. She lives by it, and she will die by it.

## Analysis

### Romantic Idealism

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s declaration belongs to the world of Shakespearean comedy — the world she inhabits on stage as Rosalind, Imogen, and [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]]. In that world, love does conquer all: class barriers dissolve, fortunes are irrelevant, and the lovers marry in the final act.

But *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is not a comedy. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s belief that love transcends money is [[beauty|beautiful]] and fatal. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] abandons her, she has no material resources to [[transformation|fall]] back on — no money, no family wealth, no social position. Love was everything, and when love fails, everything fails.

### Class Irony

The line's irony is sharpened by class. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] speaks from poverty; her faith that love is more than money is the faith of someone who has no money. Mrs. Vane, who has lived longer and harder, knows better — she has "owed money" her whole life and knows that [[credit-is-the-capital-of-a-younger-son|credit]]ors do not accept love as payment.

But [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s idealism is also what makes her the novel's moral center. She is the one character who genuinely believes in something beyond self-interest. Her tragedy is that the novel's world has no place for such belief.

### Dorian's Opposite

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has money — and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. He could give her both. Instead he destroys her love and keeps his money. When [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] dies, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] goes to the opera with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]. The contrast between [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s credo and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s actions is the novel's most damning indictment of its protagonist.

- **[[innocence]]** — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s innocence expresses itself as idealism; the novel punishes it.
- **[[idealism]]** — The line is [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s philosophy; the novel tests it to destruction.
