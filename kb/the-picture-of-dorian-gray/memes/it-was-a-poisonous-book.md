## Quotation

> The style in which it was written was that curious jewelled style, vivid and obscure at once, full of _argot_ and of archaisms, of technical expressions and of elaborate paraphrases, that characterizes the work of some of the finest artists of the French school of _Symbolistes_. There were in it metaphors as monstrous as orchids and as subtle in colour. The life of the senses was described in the terms of mystical philosophy. One hardly knew at times whether one was reading the spiritual ecstasies of some mediæval saint or the morbid confessions of a modern sinner. It was a poisonous book. The heavy odour of incense seemed to cling about its pages and to trouble the brain. (010-043)

## Context

In Chapter 10, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] sends [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a book — the notorious "yellow book" — a thinly veiled reference to Joris-Karl Huysmans' *À rebours* (1884), the bible of the Decadent movement. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] receives it shortly after learning of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane's]] suicide, and it becomes the single most influential text in his life after [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words.

Wilde's narrator describes the book in prose that mimics the book's own style: jewelled, synaesthetic, blurring the boundary between the [[soul|spirit]]ual and the sensual. The description functions as a warning embedded in seduction — the narrator tells us the book is poison, even as the prose itself intoxicates. The final image of incense clinging to the pages suggests a kind of liturgical evil: reading as an act of profane worship.

## Analysis

This passage is the novel's most direct statement about the power — and danger — of aesthetic experience. The "poisonous book" is not poisonous because it advocates evil; it is poisonous because it dissolves the boundary between morality and aesthetics, presenting sin as a mode of beauty. The word "poisonous" is carefully chosen: poison works slowly, invisibly, from within — exactly as the book works on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] over the eighteen years that follow.

The incense image is doubly significant. Incense belongs to religious ritual; its presence here suggests that the book has become a kind of anti-scripture, its reading a form of [[worship|devotion]] to a new and darker faith. The "troubled brain" anticipates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s later psychological disintegration: the book does not merely entertain or even corrupt — it destabilizes the very faculty of judgment.

Wilde's own relationship to this passage is characteristically ambiguous. He wrote the very kind of jewelled, Decadent prose he describes, and the passage is as much self-[[the-portrait|portrait]] as warning. The reader is left uncertain whether Wilde is condemning the book's influence or advertising it.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: The book is the second great influence on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], after [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — and the two work in concert.
- [[art-and-life]]: The passage dramatizes the central Wildean problem: can art be morally dangerous? If so, what responsibility does the artist bear?
- [[sin]]: The book presents sin "in the terms of mystical philosophy" — aestheticized, [[soul|spirit]]ualized, made [[beauty|beautiful]].
- [[beauty]]: The book's beauty is precisely what makes it poisonous — beauty and danger are inseparable in Wilde's aesthetic.
