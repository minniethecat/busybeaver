## Quotation

> "You wrecked the life of Sibyl Vane, and Sibyl Vane was my sister." (016-055)

## Context

[[james-vane]] speaks these words to [[dorian-gray]] in a dark archway off the opium-den district in Chapter 16. James has tracked [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from the den, where a woman called him "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" — the name [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] used for her mysterious lover.

The line is James's accusation, his indictment, and his justification all in one sentence. It is also his revenge — the moment the hunter confronts the hunted.

The structure is precise: first the crime ("You wrecked the life of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]"), then the connection that gives James standing to prosecute ("and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] was my sister"). The word "wrecked" is chosen with care — not "killed," not "murdered," but "wrecked." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] did not put a knife in [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s heart; he broke her heart, and she did the rest herself. But James — and the novel — holds [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] responsible for the wreckage as surely as if he had wielded the knife.

## Analysis

### The Indictment

The line transforms James from a rough, inarticulate sailor into something like a prosecutor. The sentence is simple, declarative, and devastating. There is no rhetoric, no ornament, no [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] wit. There is only fact.

"Wrecked" is the crucial word. It carries the weight of the novel's [[morality|moral judgment]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] *wrecked* [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] — destroyed her as a person, not as a body. The verb implies a process rather than a single act. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s cruelty was gradual, and its consequence was gradual, and the wreckage was total.

### Brother as Judge

James speaks as [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s brother, not as an agent of the law. This is personal justice, not legal justice — and the novel, by placing this scene in an opium den rather than a courtroom, makes its preference clear. The law cannot reach [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; only a brother's vengeance can.

But James is also destiny's instrument (see [[destiny-never-closed-her-accounts]]). The personal and the cosmic merge: James's private grief is destiny's public accounting.

### Dorian's Response

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response is terror and evasion. He does not deny the charge. He cannot. His defense is not innocence but appearance — his youthful face, which convinces James that he cannot be the man who wronged [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] eighteen years ago.

The irony is that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face *is* the evidence of his [[conscience|guilt]] — it conceals the [[the-portrait|portrait]], which is the true record of his crimes. James sees the mask and releases the monster.
