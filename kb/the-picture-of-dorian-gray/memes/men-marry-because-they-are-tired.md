## Quotation

> "Never marry at all, Dorian. Men marry because they are tired; women, because they are curious: both are disappointed." (004-021)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just returned to his [[mayfair|Mayfair]] library after his wife [[victoria-wotton|Victoria]] — "looking like a bird of paradise that had been out all night in the rain" — flutters out of the room. He lights a cigarette, flings himself onto the sofa, and offers [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] his first piece of ad[[sin|vice]]: "Never marry a woman with straw-coloured hair, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]... Because they are so sentimental."

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] protests that he likes sentimental people. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] escalates: don't marry at all. His reasoning is characteristically symmetrical — men and women are both disappointed, just for different reasons. Men marry from exhaustion, women from curiosity. Neither motive survives the institution.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deflects with a revealing reply: "I am too much in love. That is one of your aphorisms. I am putting it into practice, as I do everything that you say." The irony is sharp: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] claims he won't marry because he is in love — a position that should refute [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] — yet frames it as obedience to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own teachings. The pupil has learned to use the master's tools, unaware he is being hollowed out in the process.

The conversation pivots immediately. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] asks whom [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] loves. "With an actress," [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] says, blushing. The name [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] enters the novel.

## Analysis

The epigram is a miniature of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rhetorical method: take a universal institution, diagnose it with reductive symmetry, and conclude with shared misery. The structure — men do X for reason A, women for reason B, both get C — is a Wildean signature. It sounds wise because it is balanced; it is devastating because it forecloses hope.

"Tired" is the operative word for men. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] means it literally — men marry when the chase exhausts them — but the word also gestures at the [[soul|spirit]]ual fatigue that haunts the novel's male characters. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will spend his life fleeing this very tiredness, pursuing sensation to outrun the weariness that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] takes for granted.

"Curious" for women is equally cruel. It reduces female desire to a tourist's impulse: women marry to see what it's like, then discover the answer is disappointment. The word denies women depth — they are not driven by passion or love but by a shallow inquisitiveness, consistent with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s later pronouncement that "women are a decorative sex" ((004-031)).

The epigram's real power lies not in its truth but in its effect on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just dismissed marriage; moments later, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will pour out the story of his love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] does not prevent [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s passion — it frames it as a doomed experiment from the start.

## Themes

- [[marriage]]: The epigram reduces marriage to a miscalculation both sexes make for different reasons. The novel offers no counterexample: the Wottons' marriage is a farce, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] never marries.
- [[love]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s dismissiveness toward marriage is of a piece with his treatment of love as an aesthetic experience, not a moral commitment.
