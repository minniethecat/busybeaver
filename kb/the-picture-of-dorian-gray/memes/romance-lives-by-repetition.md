## Quotation

> "Romance lives by repetition, and repetition converts an appetite into an art. Besides, each time that one loves is the only time one has ever loved. Difference of object does not alter singleness of passion. It merely intensifies it. We can have in life but one great experience at best, and the secret of life is to reproduce that experience as often as possible." (017-067)

## Context

The scene is a dinner party at Selby Royal, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country house. The guests include [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the [[the-duchess-of-monmouth|Duchess of Monmouth]] (Gladys), her husband the Duke, and several other society figures. The conversation has turned — as conversations at Dorian's table always do — to love.

The Duchess has just declared that she and her husband "never love" and that "women can't bear mediocrities" (017-066). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] murmurs that "we never do anything else" — suggesting that all of society life is a form of love-play. The Duchess challenges him: "Ah! then, you never really love, Mr. Gray" (017-066).

Lord Henry, as always, cannot resist an aphorism. He springs to Dorian's defence by attacking the very idea that love should be unique. His speech — addressing Gladys directly — is one of the novel's most concentrated statements of his philosophy of repetition.

## Analysis

Lord Henry's aphorism overturns the conventional assumption that love is defined by novelty. For him, romance does not require new objects; it requires renewed experience. The word "repetition" is rescued from its negative connotations — boredom, staleness, routine — and revalued as the essence of art. An "appetite" (base, physical, involuntary) is elevated by repetition into an "art" (cultivated, refined, intentional). The aesthetic transformation is the point.

The second sentence pushes the logic further. "Each time that one loves is the only time one has ever loved." This is a paradox: how can each time be the only time? The answer is that the quality of the experience — its intensity, its totality — makes the past irrelevant. Love is not cumulative; it is absolute in the moment. Previous loves do not diminish the present one because "difference of object does not alter singleness of passion."

The final turn — "the secret of life is to reproduce that experience as often as possible" — converts a philosophy of love into a philosophy of life. The goal is not variety but intensity, and intensity is achieved through repetition, not novelty. This is Lord Henry's hedonism in its most refined form: not the pursuit of new pleasures, but the cultivation of a single, endlessly repeated ecstasy.

Within the novel's moral architecture, the aphorism is another step in Dorian's education in aestheticism. If romance is repetition, then there is no betrayal, no infidelity, no consequence — only the next iteration of the same beautiful moment.

## Themes

- [[romance]]: The passage redefines romance as a structure of repetition rather than a unique encounter — a radical departure from Victorian ideals of love.
- [[repetition]]: Repetition is elevated from a sign of exhaustion to an aesthetic principle — the mechanism by which raw desire becomes cultivated art.
- [[art]]: Lord Henry applies the category of "art" to emotional life, collapsing the distinction between aesthetic and erotic experience.
- [[love]]: Love is not a relationship between persons but a quality of experience — object-independent, self-referential, aesthetic.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: This aphorism is a late, mature expression of Lord Henry's hedonism — no longer the provocative paradoxes of the early chapters, but a sustained philosophy of experience.
