## Core Tenets

Lord Henry Wotton's philosophy is a distinctive blend of aestheticism, hedonism, and amoral individualism, delivered through a stream of epigrams and paradoxes that exert a fatal influence on Dorian Gray. Its fundamental premises can be distilled into several interlocking doctrines:

**The supremacy of youth and beauty.** Lord Henry teaches Dorian that youth is the one thing worth having, and that beauty is a form of genius — higher, indeed, than genius, because it needs no explanation. When Dorian sees his finished portrait, Lord Henry's words echo in his mind: "You have the most marvellous youth, and youth is the one thing worth having... When your youth goes, your beauty will go with it" (002-023). This terror of decay becomes the engine of Dorian's fateful wish.

**The new Hedonism.** Lord Henry proclaims that the aim of life is self-development through the perfect realization of one's own nature, and that the only way to rid oneself of a temptation is to yield to it. He calls for "a new Hedonism — that is what our century wants" (002-048), a philosophy that would recreate life and save it from the "harsh, uncomely puritanism" of the age. This is later recast as the "new Hedonism that was to recreate life and to save it from that harsh, uncomely puritanism that is having, in our own day, its curious revival" (011-010).

**The rejection of conventional morality.** Lord Henry systematically undermines conventional moral categories. He declares that "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" (002-015), that "to be good is to be in harmony with oneself" (008-010), and that sin is merely a provincial and outdated concept. His epigrams invert the pieties of Victorian society, recasting virtue as hypocrisy and pleasure as the only genuine good.

**The theory of influence.** Lord Henry articulates a philosophy of influence as the most satisfying form of power: "to project one's soul into some gracious form, and let it tarry there for a moment; to hear one's own intellectual views echoed back to one with all the added music of passion and youth" (003-033). This theory becomes the blueprint for his relationship with Dorian, whom he seeks to dominate and mould as Basil had once done through art.

> Because to influence a person is to give him one's own soul. He does not think his natural thoughts, or burn with his natural passions. His virtues are not real to him. His sins, if there are such things as sins, are borrowed. He becomes an echo of some one else's music, an actor of a part that has not been written for him. The aim of life is self-development. To realize one's nature perfectly — that is what each of us is here for.
>
> (002-024)

**The spectator theory of life.** Lord Henry advocates detachment from emotion and the cultivation of the aesthetic standpoint: "To become the spectator of one's own life is to escape the suffering of life" (009-017). He counsels against sympathy and remorse, urging instead a dispassionate appreciation of life's unfolding drama — a doctrine Dorian disastrously internalizes after Sibyl Vane's death.

## Influence on Dorian Gray

Lord Henry's philosophy is not merely spoken; it is enacted through Dorian's life. The portrait itself becomes the material embodiment of the philosophy's consequences — allowing Dorian to live the life of sensation Lord Henry preaches while the painted image bears the moral cost. Critics have long debated whether Lord Henry is a tempter figure (a Mephistopheles to Dorian's Faust) or merely the articulate voice of Dorian's own latent desires. Basil Hallward identifies the danger early: "He has a very bad influence over all his friends" (002-020), yet Dorian himself acknowledges that Lord Henry merely revealed to him what he already contained: "You filled me with a wild desire to know everything about life" (004-020).

## Narrative Function

Lord Henry's philosophy serves as the intellectual engine of the plot. His epigrams — witty, seductive, and morally destabilizing — create the atmosphere in which Dorian's corruption unfolds. Wilde uses Lord Henry to voice many of the aesthetic doctrines he himself had espoused in the Preface and in his critical writings, yet the novel's tragic arc implicitly critiques the amoral application of these ideas. The philosophy is simultaneously seductive and destructive, beautiful and corrupting — a paradox that mirrors the novel's own ambivalent relationship to aestheticism.
