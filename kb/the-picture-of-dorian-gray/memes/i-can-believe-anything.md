## Quotation

> "I can believe anything, provided that it is quite incredible." (001-032)

## Context

In Chapter 1, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is about to tell [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] why he cannot exhibit [[the-portrait|Dorian Gray's portrait]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] hesitates, warning that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] "will hardly understand it" and "will hardly believe it" (001-031). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], lounging on the divan amid the heavy scent of lilac and the murmur of bees (001-001), plucks a pink-petalled daisy and replies with this sentence (001-032).

The line is a study in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rhetorical method: taking a conversational cue — "you won't believe this" — and inverting it into a paradox. He does not say "I will believe you." He says belief itself is only possible when the object is incredible. The ordinary is unworthy of credence; only the absurd commands it.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s story follows immediately: the "curious sensation of terror" upon first meeting [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] (001-034), the feeling that this stranger's personality "would absorb my whole nature, my whole soul, my very art itself" (001-034).

## Analysis

The sentence is a self-consuming paradox. If one believes only the incredible, then one cannot consistently believe this very statement — because the statement itself must be incredible to be believed, yet is too obviously a performance of wit to qualify. The paradox voids itself.

This is not a flaw but the point. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams do not describe the world; they perform a stance toward it. The statement announces that he is not interested in truth-claims at all. He is interested in the pleasure of inversion — the delight of saying the opposite of what is expected and watching ordinary logic collapse.

The sentence also prefigures the novel's treatment of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will become a living version of this paradox: a man whose youth is preserved while his soul decays, a "fact" so incredible that everyone who meets him believes it — until the evidence of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] becomes undeniable. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s throwaway line about belief turns out to be prophecy.

Structurally, the sentence serves as a hinge. Before it, Chapter 1 is aesthetic description ([[basils-studio|the studio]], the garden, the [[the-portrait|portrait]]). After it, the novel's first long confession begins. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s paradox opens the door to [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s secret, which opens the door to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].

## Themes

- [[belief]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines belief as an aesthetic category — not a commitment to truth but an appetite for the impossible.
- [[paradox]]: The sentence is a crystalline example of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrammatic technique: reversal of expectation, performed as wit.
- [[influence]]: The line marks the moment [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] first exerts his conversational power over the story's direction, drawing out [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s confession.
