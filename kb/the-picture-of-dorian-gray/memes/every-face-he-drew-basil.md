## Quotation

> "Suddenly he remarked that every face that he drew seemed to have a fantastic likeness to Basil Hallward." (014-009)

## Context

The morning after murdering [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has breakfast, writes two letters — one summoning [[alan-campbell]] to dispose of the body — and then, alone, lights a cigarette and begins to sketch. He draws flowers, bits of architecture, then human faces.

And every face he draws looks like [[basil-hallward|Basil]].

The narrator reports this with clinical detachment: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] "frowned," got up, and took a book from the shelf, "determined that he would not think about what had happened." The sketching is an attempt at normalcy — the morning routine of a gentleman of leisure — but his hand betrays him. The subconscious, which the [[the-portrait|portrait]] absorbs and displays, leaks onto the paper.

This is one of the novel's most psychologically acute moments: the return of the repressed through the very act meant to suppress it.

## Analysis

### The Unconscious Hand

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sketches are the mind's equivalent of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — an image that reveals what the conscious self would conceal. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] hides [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins in the attic; the sketches broadcast his [[conscience|guilt]] onto the breakfast table. He cannot control either.

The line is a masterful piece of psychological observation. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not thinking about [[basil-hallward|Basil]]; he is actively *not* thinking about [[basil-hallward|Basil]]. But repression, as Wilde understood, is not erasure. The hand that held the knife now holds the pencil, and it remembers.

### The Portrait's Shadow

The sketches form a symbolic triangle with the [[the-portrait|portrait]] and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face shows innocence; the [[the-portrait|portrait]] shows [[conscience|guilt]]; the sketches show something in between — the [[conscience|guilt]] leaking through, not yet fully visible but no longer fully hidden.

This anticipates the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s behavior later in the chapter, when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] checks it after the murder and finds blood on its hands. The sketches are the first sign that the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s separation from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is beginning to fail — that the boundary between hidden [[conscience|guilt]] and visible self is porous.

### The Aesthetic Escape

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response to the haunting sketches is to reach for a book — Gautier's *Émaux et Camées*, a volume of French Parnassian poetry. He reads about Venice, about lacquered surfaces and "the cold yellow hand," seeking refuge in aestheticism's surface pleasures.

But the poetry he chooses is itself about hands — about Lacenaire's hand "still ill-washed" of its crime. Even his escape reading circles back to murder. The aesthetic cannot save him from the moral.

- **[[soul]]** — [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s face haunts [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s drawings because [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s soul haunts [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s.
