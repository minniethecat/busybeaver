## Quotation

> "It is not much to ask of you, Dorian, and it is entirely for your own sake that I am speaking. I think it right that you should know that the most dreadful things are being said against you in London." (012-017)

## Context

It is the eve of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s thirty-eighth birthday — the day before the events of Chapter 12. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has come to [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] in the small hours before departing for a six-month stay in Paris. He has resolved to confront [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about the rumors circulating through [[high-society|London society]].

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] frames his intervention carefully: he prefaces the revelation by minimizing his request ("it is not much to ask"), asserting benevolent intent ("for your own sake"), and then delivering the accusation in the passive voice — "[rumors] are being said," as though the scandal exists independently of any particular speaker. This careful rhetorical framing reveals [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s discomfort with confrontation; he is a man who would rather paint than preach.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response — "[[i-love-scandals-about-other-people|I love scandals about other people]], but scandals about myself don't interest me" (012-018) — attempts to defuse the warning with the same epigrammatic detachment [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] favors. But the conversation that follows, the longest sustained dialogue in the novel, will end with [[basil-hallward|Basil]] seeing the corrupted [[the-portrait|portrait]] and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] murdering him.

## Analysis

The sentence is a masterpiece of diplomatic understatement. "The most [[mind-of-the-well-informed-man|dreadful thing]]s" is deliberately vague — [[basil-hallward|Basil]] does not yet specify the charges, allowing the gravity to accumulate before he enumerates the scandals: Lady Gwendolen's ruined reputation (012-022), the country-house orgies, the [[youth|young]] men whose careers have been destroyed by association with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] (012-020).

The phrase "against you" is telling. The rumors are framed as an adversarial force, a collective judgment pronounced by [[high-society|London society]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] positions himself not as a participant in this judgment but as a concerned friend who merely reports it. This stance — sympathetic but reporting hostile intelligence — is the last moment of friendship between the two men before the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s revelation destroys everything.

The sentence also marks a structural turning point in the novel. Before Chapter 12, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins are private — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] bears them alone. After this chapter, the public world begins to close in: the rumors, [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s pursuit, Alan Campbell's forced complicity, and finally [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own destruction of the [[the-portrait|portrait]].

## Themes

- [[sin-and-reputation]]: The gap between private [[sin|corruption]] and public rumor is the chapter's central concern — and this sentence opens it.
- [[london-society]]: "Against you in London" invokes the social world of clubs, dinner parties, and whispered scandal that enforces collective judgment.
- [[morality-and-ethics]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s moral seriousness contrasts with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s amoral wit; this sentence marks the return of conscience to the novel's discursive field.
