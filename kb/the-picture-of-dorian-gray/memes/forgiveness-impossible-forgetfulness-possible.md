## Quotation

> "...though forgiveness was impossible, forgetfulness was possible still, and he was determined to forget, to stamp the thing out, to crush it as one would crush the adder that had stung one." (016-004)

## Context

The passage occurs early in Chapter 16, as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] rides through the rainy London streets toward the [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]]. He has just repeated to himself [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words from their first meeting: "To cure the soul by means of the senses, and the senses by means of the soul" (016-002). He is seeking an opium cure for the sickness of having murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]].

The narrator enters [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s consciousness through free indirect discourse. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind cycles through a series of questions: Was it true the senses could cure the soul? "Innocent blood had been spilled. What could atone for that?" The answer is devastating in its honesty: "for that there was no atonement."

The key sentence then arrives. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] recognizes that forgiveness is impossible because atonement is impossible — murder cannot be undone. But he immediately replaces the impossible goal (forgiveness) with a possible one (forgetfulness). The verb "determined" carries immense weight: this is a willed act, a deliberate choice to suppress rather than confront.

## Analysis

The passage's structure enacts its psychological content. The short, staccato sentences that precede it — "Innocent blood had been spilled. What could atone for that?" — have the rhythm of a mind trying and failing to find a way out. The long, complex sentence that follows — "though forgiveness was impossible, forgetfulness was possible still..." — has the momentum of resolution. The syntax itself moves from paralysis to decision.

The "adder" simile is crucial. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] imagines his [[conscience|guilt]] as a venomous snake that has bitten him — an external threat, not an internal condition. By "crushing" the memory, he frames his project of forgetting as self-defense rather than self-deception. The language of violence ("stamp the thing out, crush it") also echoes the violence of [[murder-of-basil-hallward|Basil's murder]] — as though [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is now killing the memory as he killed the man.

But the passage contains its own refutation. The word "still" in "forgetfulness was possible still" carries a submerged temporality: forgetfulness is possible _for now_. The rest of the chapter, and the novel, will prove that forgetfulness is temporary. Memory returns — as [[memory-like-a-horrible-malady|a horrible malady]] that eats the soul away (016-020).

## Themes

- [[sin]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] recognizes the irreversibility of mortal sin — "for that there was no atonement" — but then attempts to bypass atonement entirely by substituting forgetting for forgiving.
- [[conscience]]: The passage is an anatomy of conscience in action: the question "What could atone for that?" is conscience speaking; the decision to "crush" the thought is conscience being silenced.
- [[morality]]: The distinction between forgiveness and forgetfulness maps onto the novel's larger concern with moral transformation versus cosmetic self-improvement. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] chooses the easier path — not to be forgiven, but to cease to care.
