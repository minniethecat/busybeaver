## Quotation

> "His beauty had been to him but a mask, his youth but a mockery." (020-005)
>
> — Narrator, describing Dorian Gray's thoughts, Chapter 20

## Context

This passage occurs in Chapter 20 as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], alone in his library after returning from his encounter with Hetty Merton, takes up the carved mirror [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] had given him years before. He recalls a mad love letter he once received — "The world is [[transformation|change]]d because you are made of [[the-world-is-changed-because-you-are-made-of-ivory-and-gold|ivory and gold]]. The [[the-world-is-changed-because-you-are-made-of-ivory-and-gold|curves of your lips rewrite history]]" (020-005) — and repeats the phrases obsessively.

Then, in a paroxysm of self-loathing, he flings the mirror to the floor and crushes it beneath his heel: "It was his beauty that had ruined him, his beauty and the youth that he had prayed for." The quoted sentence is the narrative distillation of this moment — a verdict delivered not in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s voice but in the narrator's, lending it the weight of authorial judgment.

The sentence is immediately followed by a series of rhetorical questions: "What was youth at best? A green, an unripe time, a time of shallow moods, and sickly thoughts. Why had he worn its livery? [[youth|Youth]] had spoiled him." (020-005)

## Analysis

The sentence pairs two metaphors for beauty and youth — mask and mockery — that systematically dismantle the novel's earlier idealisation of both.

"Mask" concedes the central truth [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has evaded: his beauty has been a concealment, not a revelation. It has hidden the [[sin|corruption]] the [[the-portrait|portrait]] displays. Where earlier chapters treat beauty as the visible form of goodness ([[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s "sin is a thing that writes itself across a man's face" (012-023)), the mask metaphor admits the opposite: beauty conceals, it does not express.

"Mockery" goes further. [[youth|Youth]] has not merely concealed — it has *deceived*. It promised [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] exemption from consequence while delivering him into consequence of the worst kind. The eternal youth he prayed for has become the permanent mask behind which his soul has rotted unseen. The word "mockery" carries the bitterness of someone who has been tricked by his own wish.

The passage also echoes the Preface: "It is the spectator, and not life, that art really mirrors." (P02-006) [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been the spectator of his own surface, and the surface has lied.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit denunciation of beauty as deceptive surface, reversing the aestheticist celebration of beauty found earlier in the text.
- [[youth]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s eternal youth, once his greatest gift, is here recast as the instrument of his destruction.
- [[the-portrait]]: The mirror scene parallels the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — both are reflective surfaces, but the mirror shows only the mask, while the [[the-portrait|portrait]] shows what the mask conceals.
- [[ruin]]: The language of ruination — "had ruined him" — frames beauty and youth as active agents of destruction, not passive gifts.
