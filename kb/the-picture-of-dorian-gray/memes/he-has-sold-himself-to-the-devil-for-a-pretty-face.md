## Quotation

> "They say he has sold himself to the devil for a pretty face. It's nigh on eighteen years since I met him. He hasn't changed much since then. I have, though." (016-069)

## Context

The unnamed opium woman speaks these words in Chapter 16, moments after [[james-vane|James Vane]] has released [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], deceived by his youthful face. James, now uncertain, asks the woman about the man he just let go. Her reply — crude, superstitious, and absolutely true — reveals what polite society only whispers.

The woman is a figure from the London underworld: broken, aged by opium, speaking with a "sickly leer." She has known [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] for eighteen years and watched him remain un[[transformation|change]]d while she decayed. Her explanation — that he sold his soul to the devil — is folkloric, but it is also the novel's supernatural premise stated in its plainest form.

James, hearing this, realizes his mistake and rushes back into the street — but [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has vanished. The woman's gossip undoes the escape that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s youth had just secured.

## Analysis

### Folk Wisdom and Supernatural Truth

The opium woman's language is the language of legend and superstition — "sold himself to the devil" — but in Wilde's novel, the supernatural is real. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] does age; the soul is transferred. The woman's crude formulation is closer to the truth than any of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s polished epigrams.

Wilde draws a pointed contrast between high-society euphemism and low-world directness. In [[the-club|the club]] and the drawing room, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s unchanging face is a marvel, a topic for admiration. In the opium den, it is evidence of diabolism. The same fact, interpreted through different social lenses, yields opposite verdicts.

### The Testimony of Time

"*I* have, though" — the woman's parenthetical is devastating. She has aged; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has not. Her body is the evidence that his is unnatural. The line carries the weight of eighteen years of decay, a life consumed by opium while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s consumption remains invisible.

This is the novel's double standard made visible: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins leave no mark on his face, but they mark everyone around him. The opium woman, [[sibyl-vane]], [[basil-hallward]], [[alan-campbell]] — all are destroyed, aged, or killed, while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s surface remains pristine.

### The Moment of Undoing

The woman's revelation is the pivot on which the chapter turns. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s escape — so brilliantly achieved moments before — is immediately undone by a chance encounter and a piece of gossip. The novel's machinery of fate, which the narrator calls "[[destiny-never-closed-her-accounts|destiny's accounts]]" in the same chapter, works through the most unlikely instruments: an addict's loose tongue.
