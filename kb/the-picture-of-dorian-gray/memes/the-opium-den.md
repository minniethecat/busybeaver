## Description

The opium den is located in the East End of London, near the docks (016-002) — a world away from the elegance of [[mayfair|Mayfair]]. Wilde describes its inhabitants in vivid, grotesque detail: sailors, thieves, and the broken-down remnants of humanity. The air is thick with opium smoke; figures huddle in corners; a Malay sailor offers [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a pipe. It is the novel's most sustained depiction of London's underbelly and the visual antithesis of [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s aestheticized studio.


> Lying back in the hansom, with his hat pulled over his forehead, Dorian Gray watched with listless eyes the sordid shame of the great city, and now and then he repeated to himself the words that Lord Henry had said to him on the first day they had met, "To cure the soul by means of the senses, and the senses by means of the soul." Yes, that was the secret. He had often tried it, and would try it again now. There were opium dens where one could buy oblivion, dens of horror where the memory of old sins could be destroyed by the madness of sins that were new.
>
> (016-002)

## Significance in the Novel

The opium den represents the hidden world that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s public life conceals. His visits here are part of his "[[duality|double life]]" — the secret pursuit of sensation that the novel alludes to but rarely depicts directly. It is also the site of the novel's most suspenseful scene: [[james-vane|James Vane]], now a sailor, overhears [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] being called "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" and realizes he has found his sister's destroyer. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] escapes only because his youthful appearance convinces James that he cannot be the man he seeks. (016-052)
