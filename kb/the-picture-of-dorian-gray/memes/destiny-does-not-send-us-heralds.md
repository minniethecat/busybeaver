## Quotation

> "As for omens, there is no such thing as an omen. Destiny does not send us heralds. She is too wise or too cruel for that." (018-025)

## Context

The day after the shooting-party accident at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]] — in which Sir Geoffrey Clouston accidentally shot and killed a beater — [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is unnerved. He and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] walk back toward the house, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], "with a heavy sigh," declares the accident "a bad omen, [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]], a very bad omen" (018-022). He adds, "I feel as if something horrible were going to happen to some of us. To myself, perhaps" (018-024).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response is characteristically urbane and dismissive. He first declares that "the [[the-only-horrible-thing-is-ennui|only horrible thing in the world is ennui]]" (018-025) — the boredom that comes when dinner conversation grows tedious. Then he delivers this epigrammatic dismissal of omens and destiny, reducing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s genuine terror to a failure of style.

The dramatic irony is devastating. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s premonition is accurate: [[james-vane|James Vane]] is hunting him, and the beater's death is indeed an omen of the violence to come. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s urbane dismissal of omens is, unknowingly, a dismissal of the truth.

## Analysis

The epigram works through a rhetorical strategy of personification and paradox. Destiny is personified as female ("she"), in the classical tradition of Fortuna or the Fates. But this Destiny does not behave as the tradition would predict. The traditional Destiny sends signs, warnings, prophecies — heralds that announce her approach. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s Destiny does the opposite: she arrives unannounced, either because she is "too wise" (knowing that heralds would allow her victims to prepare) or "too cruel" (preferring the shock of sudden arrival).

The "too wise or too cruel" alternative is a false choice. Both options describe the same phenomenon — the absence of warning — but assign it opposite moral valences. Wisdom implies purpose; cruelty implies malice. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not commit to either interpretation because the epigram's purpose is not to describe reality but to close down the conversation. The sentence is a rhetorical performance, not a statement of belief.

The epigram also reveals the limit of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy. He treats destiny as an aesthetic category — something to be discussed over dinner, a subject for epigrams. But destiny in this novel is real and violent: [[james-vane|James Vane]] is outside the window, and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] is already dead. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s wit is exquisite, but it is also irrelevant — a [[beauty|beautiful]] surface that cannot touch the depths of what is actually happening.

## Themes

- [[destiny]]: The epigram redefines destiny as an absence of warning rather than a presence of meaning.
- [[omen]]: The passage stages a collision between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s belief in omens and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aesthetic dismissal of them.
- [[death]]: The beater's death is the immediate context, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own death is the distant subject.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: The epigram is a pure expression of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method — replacing genuine fear with formal elegance.
