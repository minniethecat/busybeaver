## Description

South Audley Street is a Mayfair street running north–south, intersecting Grosvenor Square at its southeast corner. In the geography of *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, it is a minor but carefully placed coordinate — a street that connects the novel's world of art (the frame-maker's shop) with its world of moral crisis (the foggy encounter between Dorian and Basil).

Wilde mentions South Audley Street twice. The first reference is to a commercial establishment — the celebrated frame-maker who supplies the elaborate frame for the fatal portrait. The second is to the street corner where [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] passes [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] unrecognized in the fog.

## Significance in the Novel

South Audley Street appears first in Chapter 10, in a passage that links art and material luxury:

> The frame-maker of South Audley Street, came in with a somewhat rough-looking young assistant. (010-017)

The frame — elaborate, expensive, designed to display the portrait as an object of beauty — comes from South Audley Street. The street is thus, from its first mention, associated with the presentation and concealment of art: the frame that will hide the portrait in the locked attic room originates here.

The street's second appearance is more dramatic. In Chapter 12, Basil Hallward passes Dorian at the corner of [[grosvenor-square|Grosvenor Square]] and South Audley Street (012-002). Basil is on his way to Paris, hoping to confront Dorian about the rumors that surround him. Dorian, walking home through the fog, recognizes Basil but pretends not to see him. The corner of Grosvenor Square and South Audley Street is the last place Basil is seen alive in the novel.

## Associated Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: Passes through the South Audley Street corner on the night he evades Basil — a small, geographical evasion that prefigures the larger moral one to come.
- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: Last seen at the corner of Grosvenor Square and South Audley Street, walking toward his death.

## Atmosphere / Symbolism

South Audley Street exemplifies the novel's technique of embedding moral significance in geographical coordinates. The street itself is neutral — a fashionable Mayfair address with fine shops — but Wilde endows it with narrative weight by making it the site of two linked events: the framing of the portrait (the attempt to contain and beautify the evidence of sin) and Dorian's evasion of Basil (the refusal to confront the artist who made the portrait).

That both events involve concealment — first of the painting, then of recognition between two men — makes South Audley Street a quiet but insistent presence in the novel's geography of secrecy.
