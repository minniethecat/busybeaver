## Description

The East End is the impoverished district of Victorian London, known in the late nineteenth century for its overcrowded slums and social deprivation.

In the novel, it represents the sphere of philanthropic activity that stands in sharp contrast to the luxurious West End.

## Key Appearances

The East End is first introduced when Lord Henry learns of Dorian Gray's charitable activities there, as Basil Hallward recounts:

> I believe he was going to help her in the East End, and that his name was Dorian Gray.
>
> (001-071)

Lord Henry later expresses his characteristic disdain for Dorian's philanthropic pursuits:

> "Still, the East End is a very important problem," remarked Sir Thomas Burdon. "Quite so," answered the young lord. "It is the problem of slavery, and we try to solve it by amusing the slaves."
>
> (003-064)

After the luncheon, Lord Henry resolves to detach Dorian from his charitable work entirely, declaring internally that he would take no interest at all in the East End for the future (003-071).

## Significance in the Novel

Lord Henry actively works to persuade Dorian to abandon his East End philanthropy, regarding it as an unworthy distraction from the pursuit of pleasure (003-060).

The East End represents the moral dimension of Victorian society — the claim of conscience and social responsibility that Lord Henry's philosophy seeks to extinguish in Dorian.

## Atmosphere / Symbolism

The East End functions as the moral and social antithesis of the aristocratic world of Mayfair.

Dorian's movement between the two districts mirrors his internal struggle between conscience and hedonism.

His eventual abandonment of East End charity marks a decisive turn toward the amoral aestheticism that Lord Henry advocates.
