## Quotation

> "I didn't say I liked it, Harry. I said it fascinated me. There is a great difference." (010-048)

## Context

It is almost nine o'clock when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] arrives at the club, where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] sits alone in the morning-room, looking bored. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] apologises for his lateness: "That book you sent me so fascinated me that I forgot how the time was going." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], pleased, replies, "Yes, I thought you would like it." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s correction is swift and precise — he did not say he *liked* it; he said it *fascinated* him. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] murmurs, "Ah, you have discovered that?" — and they pass into the dining-room.

The ex[[transformation|change]] happens the evening after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] receives the "yellow book" ([[it-was-a-poisonous-book]]), and only hours after he has learned of the inquest on [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. The book has already begun its work.

## Analysis

The distinction [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] draws — between liking and being fascinated — is one of the novel's most important philosophical moments. To "like" something implies moral approval, a judgment that the thing is good. To be "fascinated" by something is to be held by it regardless of judgment, to be compelled by a force that operates beneath or beyond the moral.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has absorbed enough of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy to make this distinction instinctively, and his precision pleases his mentor. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s murmured "Ah, you have discovered that?" is the sound of a teacher recognising that his pupil has internalised the lesson. The distinction is the foundation of the aesthetic attitude: the suspension of [[morality|moral judgment]] in favour of pure absorption in the object.

But the ex[[transformation|change]] is also ominous. Fascination without liking is the posture of the addict, not the connoisseur. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s relationship with the book — and, by extension, with his own life of sensation — is already one of compulsion rather than choice. The "great difference" he names is real, but it is a difference that leads not to freedom but to enslavement.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] recognises in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s correction the successful transmission of his own worldview.
- [[art-and-life]]: The distinction between aesthetic fascination and moral approval is the core of the Wildean paradox.
- [[morality]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s separation of "like" from "fascinated" enacts the suspension of [[morality|moral judgment]] that will define his life.
