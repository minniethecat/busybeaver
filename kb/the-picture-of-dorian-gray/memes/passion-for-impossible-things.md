## Quotation

> "There are few of us who have not wakened at dawn to the sense of some impossible passion — some longing for a beauty that has no shape, or a love that returns no kiss. It was such a morning's awakening that came to Dorian Gray as he sat in the library at Lord Henry's house in Mayfair." (008-011)

## Context

Chapter 8, the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has received the news from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who has reframed the tragedy as an aesthetic experience: "It would have been an imitation. But let's not talk about it." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reads [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death as a artistic event rather than a human catastrophe — the girl who ceased to act has now performed her final, most perfect role.

The narrator's meditation on "impossible passion" arrives at the hinge between grief and self-absorption. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is on the verge of deciding to treat his [[life-the-first-of-arts|life as art]]. The phrase captures the nature of the desire that will consume him: it is desire for something that has no object, longing without aim, appetite that feeds on itself.

## Analysis

The phrase "passion for impossible things" is one of the narrator's rare direct interventions in the story's moral landscape. It is neither [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s cynical epigram nor [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-deceived rationalization — it is the narrator naming a psychological condition that the novel takes seriously.

"Impossible things" are desires whose fulfillment would destroy the desirer. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] itself is an impossible thing made real — a wish granted that should not have been. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy is not that his desires were thwarted but that they were *fulfilled*, and fulfillment proved more destructive than frustration.

The passage also signals the shift from active to passive: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] no longer pursues objects of desire but is pursued by desire itself. He becomes, in the chapters that follow, a collector of experiences — sensations sought for their own sake, detached from any particular object or person. The "passion for impossible things" is the engine of his [[sin|corruption]].

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The "impossible passion" is the aesthetic attitude applied to life — wanting from experience what can only be found in art.
- [[desire]]: The novel distinguishes between desire-with-an-object (love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], friendship for [[basil-hallward|Basil]]) and desire-without-an-object (the "passion for impossible things"). The latter is lethal.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence is the catalyst that transforms ordinary longing into impossible passion.
