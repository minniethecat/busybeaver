## Quotation

> "Life is not governed by will or intention. Life is a question of nerves, and fibres, and slowly built-up cells in which thought hides itself and passion has its dreams. You may fancy yourself safe and think yourself strong. But a chance tone of colour in a room or a morning sky, a particular perfume that you had once loved and that brings subtle memories with it, a line from a forgotten poem that you had come across again, a cadence from a piece of music that you had ceased to play — I tell you, Dorian, that it is on things like these that our lives depend." (019-044)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This passage is the climax of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s final monologue in the novel, delivered to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] on the evening before the narrative's catastrophe. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has repeatedly tried to suggest that he has changed, that he is not the "flawless" creature [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] insists he is. Each attempt has been deflected by an epigram.

Now [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] moves from epistemology — "[[things-one-feels-certain-about-are-never-true|The things one feels absolutely certain about are never true]]" (019-042) — to psychology. If certainty about truth is impossible, then certainty about choice is equally illusory. The will is not a sovereign faculty but a passive register of sensory impressions.

## Analysis

Lord Henry's epigram is a précis of late-nineteenth-century materialist psychology. The mind is "nerves, and fibres, and slowly built-up cells" — physiology, not soul. Thought "hides itself" in these cells; passion "has its dreams" there. Both verbs are passive and intransitive. The self does not think or feel; thoughts and feelings happen to the self.

The catalogue of sensory triggers — colour, sky, perfume, poem, music — is quintessentially Decadent. Each item is an *impression*, not an *action*. Lord Henry's list abolishes moral agency: if "our lives depend" on a "chance tone of colour," then Dorian cannot be held responsible for what he has done. The epigram is an accidental absolution — accidental because Lord Henry, who does not know about the murder, does not realize he is offering it.

The phrase "I tell you, Dorian" is unusual for Lord Henry — a moment of genuine intensity breaking through the polished surface of epigram. For once, he seems to mean what he says. And what he says is that meaning itself is an illusion.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: This is the capstone of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophical influence — a doctrine that makes moral judgment structurally impossible.
- [[soul]]: The epigram denies the soul's existence, replacing it with nerves and cells — the materialist counter to the novel's supernatural elements.
- [[pleasure]]: The sensory catalogue (colour, perfume, music) frames life as a sequence of aesthetic impressions rather than moral choices.
- [[morality]]: By denying the reality of will and intention, the epigram eliminates the preconditions of moral responsibility.
