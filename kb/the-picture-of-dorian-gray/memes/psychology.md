## Definition

Psychology in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* is not a formal discipline but a mode of inquiry that runs through the novel's narrative voice and its characters' reflections. The novel asks: what drives human behavior? Can the self be known? Is the mind a unity or a multiplicity? These questions are posed not abstractly but through the lived experience of characters who discover that they do not fully understand their own motives.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel's psychological inquiry is most explicit in Chapter 4, where the narrator asks "whether we could ever make psychology so absolute a science that each little spring of life would be revealed to us" (004-104). The question is left open, but the implication is clear: human motivation is too complex, too contradictory, to be reduced to scientific clarity.

This skepticism about self-knowledge pervades the novel. In Chapter 11, the narrator critiques "the shallow psychology of those who conceive the Ego in man as a thing simple, permanent, reliable, and of one essence" (011-032). Against this "shallow" view, the novel proposes a psychology of multiplicity — the self as a "complex multiform creature" containing contradictory impulses and "strange legacies of thought and passion."

The novel also gestures toward specialized psychological domains. The narrator describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s efforts "to elaborate a real psychology of perfumes" (011-015), treating sensory experience as a window into the mind. This is psychology as aesthetic practice — the study of how sensation shapes consciousness.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The novel's primary psychological subject. His transformation from innocent youth to corrupted aesthete is a case study in the malleability of the self.
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The novel's amateur psychologist, whose epigrams are miniature psychological theories. He claims that "to be good is to be in harmony with one's self" (006-040) — a definition that raises more questions than it answers.

## Key Passages

> He used to wonder at the shallow psychology of those who conceive the Ego in man as a thing simple, permanent, reliable, and of one essence. To him, man was a being with myriad lives and myriad sensations, a complex multiform creature that bore within itself strange legacies of thought and passion. (011-032)

The novel's clearest statement of its psychological philosophy. The self is not a unity but a multiplicity; not stable but fluid; not simple but unfathomably complex.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde's psychological insights anticipate modernist preoccupations with the fragmented self. His insistence on the multiplicity of the ego — that "man was a being with myriad lives and myriad sensations" — resonates with later psychological theories of the unconscious and the divided self. Yet Wilde's psychology remains distinctively aesthetic: the self is interesting not because it can be cured but because it can be contemplated, like a work of art, in all its contradiction and complexity.
