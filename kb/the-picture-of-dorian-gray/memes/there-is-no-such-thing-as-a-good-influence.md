## Quotation

> "There is no such thing as a good influence, Mr. Gray. All influence is immoral — immoral from the scientific point of view." (002-020)

## Context

In Chapter 2, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] steps onto the dais in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio as the painter works on the portrait. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has warned [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] not to pay attention to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], remarking that "he has a very bad influence over all his friends, with the single exception of myself" (002-019).

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — intrigued, already drawn to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s manner and voice — asks directly: "Have you really a very bad influence, Lord Henry? As bad as Basil says?" (002-019).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is characteristically paradoxical: he does not deny having influence but instead denies that influence can *ever* be good. The sentence is the opening move in the long seduction of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind that occupies the rest of the chapter.

## Analysis

The aphorism is a rhetorical trap. To argue against it — to insist that some influence *is* good — is already to accept [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s premise that influence matters. And by accepting that premise, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has already granted [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] the very influence he is questioning.

The claim "immoral from the scientific point of view" is a deliberate category confusion: science describes, it does not prescribe. But the gesture toward "science" — that talisman of nineteenth-century authority — lends the paradox an air of inevitability that mere opinion would lack. What follows extends the argument: influence makes a person "an echo of some one else's music, an actor of a part that has not been written for him" (002-021). The self, for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], is not something given but something made — and any external shaping is a theft.

The paradox is self-consuming. If all influence is immoral, then [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own words — which are themselves an attempt to influence — are also immoral. He is, in effect, warning [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] against himself while simultaneously applying the very pressure he condemns.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: The aphorism launches the novel's central interrogation of whether one person can — or should — shape another.
- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines morality as a question of autonomy rather than virtue — what is "immoral" is to live by borrowed thoughts.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: The sentence is a self-portrait in miniature — brilliant, seductive, and logically unstable (showcase).
- [[self-development]]: The aim of life, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will say, is "to realize one's nature perfectly" (002-021) — influence frustrates this by substituting another's nature for one's own.
