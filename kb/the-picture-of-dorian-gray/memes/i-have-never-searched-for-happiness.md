## Quotation

> "I have never searched for happiness. Who wants happiness? I have searched for pleasure." (017-075)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaks these words at Lady Narborough's in Chapter 17, in response to a question from the Duchess of Monmouth. The duchess has been pressing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence. "And does his philosophy make you happy?" she asks (017-074). It is a simple question — the kind a sensible person might ask about a friend's influence — but it lands on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] with unexpected weight.

His reply is one of the most revealing things he says in the entire novel. The Duchess has asked about happiness; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] answers about pleasure. The distinction is not a quibble — it is the architecture of his life. He has spent eighteen years pursuing pleasure — sensations, experiences, beauties, [[sin|corruption]]s — and the question of whether any of it made him happy has simply never occurred to him. The word "searched" is carefully chosen: he has been an active agent in his [[pleasure|hedonism]], not a passive recipient. And the rhetorical question — "Who wants happiness?" — is not a joke. It is a genuine expression of a worldview in which happiness is beside the point.

The duchess's follow-up — "And found it, [[dorian-gray|Mr. Gray]]?" (017-076) — goes unanswered. The narrative cuts away to the duchess's own reflection on peace before [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] can reply. The silence is significant. The novel refuses to let [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] say whether he found pleasure, because the answer — visible on the rotting [[the-portrait|portrait]] — is that he found it, and it destroyed him.

## Analysis

The epigram is a hinge point in the novel's moral argument. For the first time, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] states his creed explicitly, not as something he has absorbed from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] but as something he has lived. The first-person voice — "I have never searched," "I have searched" — claims ownership. This is not [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy repeated by a disciple; it is a disciple's report on what the philosophy has meant in practice.

The distinction between happiness and pleasure is one the novel takes seriously. Happiness, in the world of this novel, is a Victorian concept — domestic, settled, compatible with [[reputation|respectability]]. [[pleasure|Pleasure]] is something else: intense, transient, indifferent to consequence. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has chosen the latter, and the epigram makes clear that this was a choice, not an accident. The verb "searched" implies effort, direction, intention. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not a victim of his desires; he has organized his life around them.

The rhetorical question — "Who wants happiness?" — is the most chilling part. It is not a paradox in the style of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]; it is something closer to a confession. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] genuinely does not understand the appeal of happiness. He has substituted pleasure so completely that the original term has lost meaning. This is what separates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] can still recognize happiness as a category, even if he prefers to mock it; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] can no longer recognize it at all.

The epigram's placement in Chapter 17 is deliberate. By this point, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has committed murder, blackmailed a former friend into disposing of the body, and descended into [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]]. The charming [[youth|young]] man of Chapter 2, who wished to be [[youth|young]] forever, has become someone who cannot remember what happiness felt like. The epigram is the sound of a soul that has substituted aesthetics for [[morality|ethics]] so thoroughly that it no longer knows the difference.

Within the larger structure of the novel, the epigram prepares for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s final crisis. In Chapter 20, he will try to do a good deed — spare Hetty Merton — and discover that goodness, like happiness, is a category he has lost the ability to inhabit. The epigram is the early warning: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[pleasure|hedonism]] has not failed to make him happy; it has made it impossible for him to want happiness.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The central theme of the epigram — the replacement of happiness by pleasure as the organizing goal of a life, and the consequences of that substitution.
- [[good-and-evil]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s inability to distinguish happiness from pleasure is part of a larger inability to make moral distinctions, dramatized most vividly by the [[the-portrait|portrait]].
- [[dorian-gray]]: The epigram is a key to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s character — a statement of philosophy that is also a confession of [[soul|spirit]]ual exhaustion.
