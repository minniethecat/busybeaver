## Quotation

> "One felt that he had kept himself unspotted from the world." (002-009)

> "He had always the look of one who had kept himself unspotted from the world." (011-003)

## Context

The phrase appears twice, functioning as an ironic bookend to the novel's moral arc.

**First occurrence (Chapter 2)** : [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] studies [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] in [[basils-studio|Basil's studio]]. The narrator describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "crisp gold hair," his "frank blue eyes," his "passionate purity." The observation that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has "kept himself unspotted from the world" is sincere — this is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] before [[sin|corruption]], genuinely innocent.

**Second occurrence (Chapter 11)** : Years later, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has lived a [[duality|double life]] of secret degradation. Rumors circulate through [[high-society|London society]]. Yet his face remains un[[transformation|change]]d — [[beauty|beautiful]], pure, rebuking. The narrator notes that "men who talked grossly became silent when [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] entered the room." The same phrase now carries heavy irony: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has kept himself *looking* unspotted. The reality is the opposite.

## Analysis

Wilde uses the identical phrase to measure the distance [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has traveled — or rather, has not traveled, at least on the surface. In Chapter 2, "unspotted" describes genuine innocence. By Chapter 11, it describes a weaponized appearance: the pure look that silences accusers and conceals [[sin|corruption]].

The phrase is carefully chosen. "Unspotted" suggests a stain that has not yet touched the fabric — a visual metaphor that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] literalizes. Where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s body remains unspotted, the [[the-portrait|painting]] accumulates every stain.

The second occurrence also reveals the social dimension of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s deception. His appearance does not merely protect him; it *judges* others. Men "wondered how one so charming and graceful as he was could have escaped the stain of an age that was at once sordid and sensual." The purity of his face becomes a rebuke to those who lack it — a form of moral power wielded by the most immoral character.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: The passage equates [[beauty|physical beauty]] with moral purity in the public imagination — an equation the novel both exploits and demolishes.
- [[sin]]: The gap between "look of one who had kept himself unspotted" and the reality of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life is the central irony of the novel.
- [[art-and-life]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]] absorbs the spots that life inflicts, leaving the living man an unchanging work of art.
