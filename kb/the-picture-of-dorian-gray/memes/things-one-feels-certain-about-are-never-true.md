## Quotation

> "The things one feels absolutely certain about are never true. That is the fatality of faith, and the lesson of romance." (019-042)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This epigram is delivered during [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s final conversation with [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], late in Chapter 19. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just challenged [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s claim that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is "flawless" and "the same," insisting: "I am changed" (019-041).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses the protest with this characteristically paradoxical aphorism. If Dorian *feels certain* that he has changed, then — by Lord Henry's logic — he must be wrong. The epigram is a rhetorical trap: any conviction Dorian expresses can be refuted simply by the fact that he expresses it with conviction.

The remark segues into Lord Henry's extended monologue on youth, aging, and the nature of life, which culminates in his declaration that "[[life-is-not-governed-by-will|Life is not governed by will or intention]]" (019-044).

## Analysis

The epigram encapsulates the negative epistemology of Decadence: truth is not something one discovers but something one suspects. Certainty is not evidence of knowledge but of error — "the fatality of faith." Lord Henry inverts the traditional relationship between belief and truth. In orthodox epistemology, one believes something *because* it is true; for Lord Henry, the fact that one believes something proves it is *false*.

"The lesson of romance" is a sly literary reference. Romantic narratives — including, implicitly, the novel the reader is holding — teach that what characters believe with absolute conviction (in fidelity, in innocence, in the permanence of beauty) is precisely what will be overturned. Lord Henry speaks as both philosopher and literary critic, collapsing the distinction.

The epigram also functions as a preemptive defense against moral judgment. If certainty is always wrong, then moral certainty — the kind [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] possessed, the kind that condemns [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s actions — is merely another form of illusion. Lord Henry's skepticism, for all its charm, is a strategy for making moral reflection impossible.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: This epigram is a pure distillation of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophical influence on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the doctrine that nothing can be known, therefore nothing can be judged.
- [[morality]]: By undermining the possibility of certainty, the epigram undermines the possibility of moral conviction.
- [[art-and-life]]: The reference to "romance" frames life as a literary genre, subject to the ironic reversals of plot — art provides the template for understanding (or refusing to understand) experience.
