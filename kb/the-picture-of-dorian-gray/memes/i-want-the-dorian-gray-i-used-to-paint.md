## Quotation

> "I want the Dorian Gray I used to paint," said the artist sadly. (009-012)
>
> — Basil Hallward, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 9

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] visits [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide. He has come to offer comfort, expecting to find [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] devastated. Instead he finds [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] calm, detached, already speaking of the tragedy as though it were a distant theatrical event.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] is shocked by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s coldness. He searches [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face for some sign of the innocent young man whose portrait he painted, and finds none. This simple, sorrowful sentence is his lament: the model he once captured on canvas no longer exists.

## Analysis

The line gains its power from its understatement. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] does not accuse, does not moralize. He simply names what he has lost: not a person but a version of a person — "the Dorian Gray I used to paint."

The choice of the verb "paint" rather than "know" or "love" is significant. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s relationship to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] was always mediated by art. He worshipped [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as a model, an ideal of beauty, an incarnation of the "new manner in art" (001-018) he sought. The [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] before him now — unfeeling, superficial, already hardening into the creature [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is shaping — is no longer paintable in that original sense.

The word "sadly" does more work than a longer speech could. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] does not rage — he mourns. And his mourning is for something that died without a funeral: the innocent [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] of Chapter 1, whose beauty and purity moved [[basil-hallward|Basil]] to an art he could not exhibit.

## Themes

- [[art]]: The artist's relationship to his subject — what happens when the subject changes, but the portrait stays the same.
- [[friendship]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s love for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is the novel's most sincere — and most betrayed — affection.
- [[innocence-and-experience]]: The loss of innocence is named here not as a moral fall but as a personal loss — the disappearance of a person [[basil-hallward|Basil]] loved.
- [[dorian-grays-transformation]]: This is the first explicit acknowledgment that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has changed, setting up the long arc of deterioration the portrait will record.
