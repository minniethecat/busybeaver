## Quotation

> "As for a spoiled life, no life is spoiled but one whose growth is arrested. If you want to mar a nature, you have merely to reform it." (006-022)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism during the extended conversation at his aunt Lady Agatha's luncheon in Chapter 6. The conversation has turned to marriage, optimism, and the nature of moral improvement. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], holding forth, dismisses optimism as "sheer terror" and proceeds to dismantle the very concept of reform.

The aphorism about "spoiled life" and "arrested growth" is followed immediately by the claim that "if you want to mar a nature, you have merely to reform it" — a direct assault on the Victorian morality of self-improvement. In [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s inverted value system, growth is everything and reform is its enemy.

## Analysis

The aphorism reframes moral failure as developmental arrest. A "spoiled life" is conventionally understood as one corrupted by [[sin|vice]] or sin. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines it: spoilage is not excess but *stasis*. The metaphor shifts from morality to biology — growth is a natural, organic process; to arrest it is to kill the organism.

This doctrine of growth-without-limits is the philosophical engine of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy. Under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] pursues experience without restraint — sensation, art, beauty, [[sin|vice]] — because to stop would be to "spoil" his life. The logic makes any limit, any moral boundary, into an act of self-destruction.

The second sentence radicalizes the first: "If you want to mar a nature, you have merely to reform it." Reform — the Victorian [[morality|virtue]] — becomes the real [[sin|corruption]]. Nature, left alone, grows; morality, imposing limits, damages it. This is the novel's most concentrated statement of its anti-moralist philosophy.

## Themes

- [[growth]]: The aphorism makes growth the supreme value, redefining spoilage as stasis rather than excess — a philosophy [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] embodies literally through his ageless [[the-portrait|portrait]].
- [[human-nature]]: [[temptation-yield|Lord Henry's doctrine]] assumes a natural self that flourishes best when unrestrained — a direct challenge to the Victorian belief in moral discipline.
- [[morality]]: Reform is recast as damage; moral limits are the true corrupters. This inversion structures much of the novel's ethical inquiry.
- [[influence]]: The aphorism is a cornerstone of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence over [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], providing the intellectual justification for a life without restraint.
