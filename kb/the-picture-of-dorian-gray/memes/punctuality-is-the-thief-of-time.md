## Quotation

> "He was always late on principle, his principle being that punctuality is the thief of time." (004-002)

## Context

It is a summer afternoon, one month after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] first dined with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sits alone in the "little library of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s house in [[mayfair|Mayfair]]," a room lavishly decorated with olive-stained oak, Persian rugs, a Clodion statuette, blue china jars, and parrot-tulips. Apricot-coloured light streams through leaded windowpanes.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has not yet arrived. The narrator explains: he is always late, and not from carelessness but from conviction. His principle is that punctuality steals time — an inversion of the bourgeois maxim that punctuality is a [[morality|virtue]].

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]], "looking rather sulky," leafs listlessly through an illustrated *Manon Lescaut*. The Louis Quatorze clock ticks with "formal monotonous" regularity. He thinks once or twice of leaving.

The sentence works on two levels. It characterizes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] — the dandy for whom even lateness is an aesthetic position — and it frames the afternoon that follows: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams on marriage, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s confession of love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], and the philosophical seduction that sends [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] into the "labyrinth of grimy streets" searching for adventure.

## Analysis

The aphorism is pure [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], though it comes through the narrator's voice. Punctuality, in the Victorian moral economy, signals reliability, [[reputation|respectability]], industry. To call it "the thief of time" inverts the entire framework. Time, for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], is not to be managed — it is to be spent lavishly, aesthetically, on pleasure. Being on time would mean surrendering to a schedule, and schedules are for the middle class.

There is a second, subtler inversion. "Thief" normally describes the thing stolen from: "Procrastination is the thief of time" says the proverb. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reverses subject and object — it is not delay that steals but punctuality itself. By arriving on time, one *gives away* time that could have been used for something better. The principle echoes Wilde's own contempt for Victorian utility: "All art is quite useless" ((P02-007)), and by extension, all useful things are anti-art, anti-life.

The phrase also foreshadows the novel's deeper engagement with time. [[dorian-gray|Dorian's wish]] — to remain [[youth|young]] while the [[the-portrait|portrait]] ages — is the ultimate refusal to let time steal anything. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s small rebellion against punctuality is a light overture to the tragedy that follows when the same principle is taken to its extreme.

## Themes

- [[time]]: The epigram frames time as something to be hoarded for pleasure, not surrendered to schedule. The novel will test whether time can be cheated.
- [[hedonism]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s lateness is a miniature of his philosophy — pleasure before obligation, the aesthetic before the moral.
