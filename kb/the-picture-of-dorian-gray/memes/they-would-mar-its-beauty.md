## Quotation

> "They would mar its beauty and eat away its grace. They would defile it and make it shameful." (010-011)

## Context

This line is the second half of Dorian's meditation in the library, as he prepares to move [[the-portrait|the portrait]] to the old schoolroom. Having compared his sins to a worm feeding on a corpse — "[[what-the-worm-was-to-the-corpse|What the worm was to the corpse]], his sins would be to the painted image on the canvas" — Dorian now enumerates the specific forms the damage will take. The purple coverlet he selects to wrap the portrait implicitly becomes a funeral pall, and this passage is the eulogy for the portrait's doomed beauty.

## Analysis

The epigram distills sin into an aesthetic crime: the verbs *mar*, *eat away*, *defile* are drawn from the vocabulary of art conservation and desecration. Sin does not simply violate a moral code; it ruins a beautiful object. This is Wilde's aesthetic morality at its most concentrated — the bad is what is ugly, the evil is what destroys beauty.

The phrase "eat away its grace" carries a double meaning. In the aesthetic sense, *grace* is the elegance and harmony of Basil Hallward's masterpiece. In the theological sense, *grace* is divine favour, the state of the soul free from sin. By conflating the two, Wilde suggests that the portrait's beauty *is* its state of grace — and that sin, by marring that beauty, is a kind of excommunication from the aesthetic ideal.

The passage also foreshadows the novel's end. When Dorian finally stabs the portrait, it is restored to its original beauty while his own body bears the accumulated defilement. The verbs *mar* and *defile* are thus prophetic: they describe not only what sins *will* do to the portrait but what the portrait, in the final reversal, will do to Dorian.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: The passage frames beauty as a moral category — to mar beauty is to commit a sin against the aesthetic order.
- [[sin]]: Sin is defined by its visible effects, its power to "defile" and make "shameful."
- [[the-portrait]]: The portrait is the canvas on which sin becomes visible, the aesthetic record of moral decay.
