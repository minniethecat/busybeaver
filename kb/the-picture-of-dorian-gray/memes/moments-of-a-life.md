## Quotation

> "But it was to teach man to concentrate himself upon the moments of a life that is itself but a moment." (011-010)

## Context

In Chapter 11, the narrator describes the "[[pleasure|new Hedonism]]" that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] had prophesied and that [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] now attempts to practice. This sentence is the practical conclusion of a long passage that has moved from theoretical ambition ("elaborate some [[spiritualizing-of-the-senses|new scheme of life]]") through historical diagnosis ("the [[nature-of-the-senses|true nature of the senses]] had never been understood") to a concrete prescription: concentrate on the moment.

The full passage runs: "Its aim, indeed, was to be experience itself, and not the fruits of experience, sweet or bitter as they might be. Of the asceticism that deadens the senses, as of the vulgar profligacy that dulls them, it was to know nothing. But it was to teach man to concentrate himself upon the moments of a life that is itself but a moment." The [[pleasure|new Hedonism]] is a middle way between self-denial and self-indulgence — a discipline of attention rather than a discipline of abstinence.

## Analysis

The sentence is built around a pun on "moment" — the word means both "an instant of time" and "importance, significance." A life that is "itself but a moment" is both brief (a mere instant in geological time) and consequential (a moment of great significance). The [[pleasure|new Hedonism]] asks us to treat each instant as a moment in both senses: fleeting, precious, and unrepeatable.

The phrase "concentrate himself" is the sentence's ethical core. It is not "abandon himself" or "indulge himself" — it is *concentrate*. The [[pleasure|new Hedonism]] is not a philosophy of self-indulgence but a philosophy of attention. The aesthetic life, in this formulation, is not about doing whatever you want; it is about paying full attention to whatever you do. This is the nearest the novel comes to a positive moral vision, and it is placed, with characteristic irony, in the mouth of a character who is about to commit murder.

The sentence also functions as a structural echo of an earlier moment in the novel. In Chapter 2, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]: "The aim of life is self-development. To realize one's nature perfectly — that is what each of us is here for." The [[pleasure|new Hedonism]]'s injunction to "concentrate himself upon the moments" is the practical method for that self-development — the *how* that complements the *what*.

## Themes

- [[new-hedonism]]: This sentence is the [[pleasure|new Hedonism]]'s practical rule of life — a discipline of attention rather than abstinence.
- [[time]]: The pun on "moment" encodes a theory of time: life is brief yet infinitely significant.
- [[experience]]: The [[pleasure|new Hedonism]] values "experience itself, and not the fruits of experience" — process over outcome.
