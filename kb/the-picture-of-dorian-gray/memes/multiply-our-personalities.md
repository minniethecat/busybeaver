## Quotation

> "Is insincerity such a terrible thing? It is merely a method by which we can multiply our personalities." (011-031)

## Context

Chapter 11 is the novel's compressed chronicle of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s [[duality|double life]] in the years between his coming of age and the murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. The narrator has just described how whispered scandals, far from destroying [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[reputation|social standing]], "only increased in the eyes of many his strange and dangerous charm" (011-031). Society, the narrator observes, is "never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating" (011-031). It is in this context — analyzing the logic by which [[high-society|London society]] excuses [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — that the narrator articulates this epigrammatic defense of insincerity.

The passage draws an explicit parallel between society and art: "the canons of [[high-society|good society]] are, or should be, the same as the canons of art. Form is absolutely essential to it. It should have the dignity of a ceremony, as well as its unreality, and should combine the insincere character of a romantic play with the wit and beauty that make such plays delightful to us" (011-031). The rhetorical question about insincerity is the culmination of this chain of reasoning.

## Analysis

The sentence performs an elegant inversion of conventional morality. [[sin|Sin]]cerity — normally the cardinal [[morality|virtue]] of the Victorian [[morality|moral code]] — is redefined as a limitation, a poverty of the self. Insincerity, by contrast, becomes a form of abundance: the capacity to hold multiple selves, to multiply one's personality beyond the confines of a single authentic identity.

The word "merely" does heavy rhetorical work. It dismisses the entire moral weight of the term "insincerity" by reframing it as a technique — a "method" — no more morally charged than a painter multiplying perspectives or an actor multiplying roles. This is the logic of aestheticism taken to its social conclusion: if life is to be lived as art, then the self is a performance, and performance requires the capacity to be more than one thing.

The sentence also functions as a structural diagnosis of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s condition. Throughout Chapter 11, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s identity fragments — he is the charming dinner guest and the visitor to [[opium-dens-where-one-could-buy-oblivion|opium dens]], the patron of the arts and the corrupter of [[youth|young]] men. The ability to "multiply our personalities" is both [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s social survival mechanism and the engine of his moral disintegration.

## Themes

- [[insincerity]]: The passage reframes insincerity from a moral failing to an aesthetic technique.
- [[identity]]: The "multiplication of personalities" is the chapter's central psychological subject — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as a being of "myriad lives and myriad sensations" (011-032).
- [[society]]: The epigram diagnoses the logic by which high society protects its own.
- [[art-and-life]]: The explicit comparison of social canons to artistic canons is the passage's governing conceit.
