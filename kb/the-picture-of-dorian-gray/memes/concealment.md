## Definition

Concealment is established in the novel's very first sentence: "To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim" (P02-001). In *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, concealment operates as the governing principle of Dorian's existence—the portrait is hidden away to conceal the truth of his soul, his crimes are concealed from society, and his youth remains the public mask that conceals his private corruption. The novel explores what happens when concealment becomes not a temporary expedient but a way of life.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Basil Hallward initially embodies the principle of concealment in its artistic form, refusing to exhibit the portrait because "I have put too much of myself into it" (001-027). His fear that the painting reveals his "artistic idolatry" (001-060) of Dorian leads him to keep it hidden—an act of artistic concealment that paradoxically enables the novel's plot.

Dorian inherits this logic of concealment and perverts it. After discovering the portrait's magical property, his first instinct is to hide it: "The picture had to be concealed. There was no help for it" (010-029). He moves it to the locked schoolroom, covering it with a "gorgeous hanging" (010-034), transforming concealment from an artistic principle into a mechanism for denying moral accountability.

The narrator observes that concealment is ultimately impossible: "A man's face... It cannot be concealed. People talk sometimes of secret vices. There are no such things" (012-019). This aphorism articulates the novel's central irony: Dorian's face remains beautiful precisely because the portrait bears the burden of his concealed sins, yet the very act of concealment makes the truth inescapably present in the hidden canvas.

## Key Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: His entire adult life is an act of concealment. The locked room, the covered portrait, the carefully maintained social persona—all serve to hide what the portrait reveals. His murder of [[basil-hallward]] is the ultimate act of concealment, eliminating the one person who might expose the connection between the beautiful face and the corrupted soul.
- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: Practices concealment as artistic principle before Dorian perverts it. His initial refusal to exhibit the portrait stems from a legitimate artistic scruple, but his concealment of his feelings for Dorian renders him vulnerable.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde deploys concealment as both a plot mechanism and a philosophical problem. The locked room that hides the portrait is the novel's central spatial symbol, a Gothic device that literalizes the Victorian culture of secrecy and the closet. Yet Wilde also ironizes concealment through the Preface, which declares that art conceals the artist while the narrative itself demonstrates that concealment always fails—the truth will out, whether in a painted canvas or a murdered body. This tension between aesthetic concealment and moral revelation is the engine of the novel's tragedy.
