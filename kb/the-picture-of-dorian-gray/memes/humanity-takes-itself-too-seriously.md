## Quotation

> Lord Henry looked over at Mr. Erskine. "Humanity takes itself too seriously. It is the world's original sin. If the caveman had known how to laugh, history would have been different." (003-020)

## Context

Chapter 3 takes place at a lunch party at [[lady-agatha|Lady Agatha]]'s house in [[mayfair|Mayfair]]. The conversation has turned to serious matters — the East End, philanthropy, political responsibility — and the assembled company has been performing their expected gravity. Mrs. Vandeleur ventures timidly that "we have such grave responsibilities" (003-019); Lady Agatha echoes her (003-019).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who has already dismissed the problem of slavery by suggesting "we try to solve it by amusing the slaves" (003-018), now turns to Mr. Erskine and delivers his verdict on the whole solemn proceeding. The remark is directed at a fellow diner, but its real audience is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], who is watching [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] "with the amused expression of one who had determined to be a spectator" (003-018).

## Analysis

The aphorism rewrites Genesis. The "original sin" in the biblical account is disobedience — the eating of the fruit. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replaces it with seriousness. The implication is radical: what humanity needs is not redemption from sin but redemption *into* laughter. The caveman, not Adam, is the lost ideal — a creature for whom history had not yet become a burden.

The sentence is also a meta-comment on the scene itself. The lunch guests are performing exactly the seriousness that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] diagnoses: they worry about the East End, about slavery, about their responsibilities. By calling the whole enterprise a species of original sin, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] exempts himself from it. He is the only person at the table who takes nothing seriously — and the performance is itself a form of power.

The duchess's response — "You are really very comforting" (003-021) — is instructive. She does not argue with the claim; she is grateful for the permission it grants. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s function in the novel is often to absolve his listeners of their scruples, and this absolution is precisely what makes him dangerous.

## Themes

- [[humanity]]: The aphorism is an anthropology in miniature — a theory of what is wrong with the species and what it has forgotten.
- [[laughter]]: Laughter, for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], is not mere amusement but a philosophical position — the refusal to grant the world the gravity it claims.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: The remark exemplifies [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method of substituting aesthetic categories for moral ones (showcase).
- [[sin]]: The theological register is borrowed and inverted — "original sin" is not disobedience against God but solemnity before the world (showcase).
