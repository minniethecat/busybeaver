## Quotation

> "As it had killed the painter, so it would kill the painter's work, and all that that meant. It would kill the past, and when that was dead, he would be free." (020-012)
>
> — Narrator, representing Dorian Gray's thoughts, Chapter 20

## Context

This passage occurs in the novel's penultimate paragraph of action, immediately before the climax. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], having resolved to destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — "the only bit of evidence left against him" (020-011) — looks round the locked schoolroom and sees "the knife that had stabbed [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]" (020-012).

The knife has been cleaned "many times, till there was no stain left upon it." It is "bright, and glistened" (020-012). The physical cleanliness of the knife contrasts with the moral stain it represents — a stain that cannot be cleaned away. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] seizes the knife and reasons through a chain of associations: the knife killed the painter → it will kill the painter's work (the [[the-portrait|portrait]]) → it will kill the past → he will be free.

The logic is perverse but psychologically coherent. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] is not merely a [[the-portrait|painting]]; it is a record, a witness, a "conscience" (020-011). To destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is, in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mind, to destroy the past it records — and with the past dead, freedom becomes possible.

The sentence that follows — "It would kill this monstrous soul-life, and without its hideous warnings, he would be at peace" (020-012) — completes the fantasy. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] stabs the picture. The result is the opposite of what he intended: he kills himself, and the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is restored to its original beauty.

## Analysis

The sentence is structured as a syllogism that the novel's finale proves false: the knife killed the painter → it will kill the painter's work → it will kill the past → freedom. Every link in the chain is mistaken. The knife cannot kill "all that that meant" — the moral reality of murder. The past, once lived, cannot be killed by destroying its record. And freedom achieved through destruction is not freedom.

The word "free" resonates with the novel's persistent concern with freedom and its illusions. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has promised [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a life of freedom — freedom from conventional morality, from ageing, from consequence. But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has never been free; he has been enslaved to his own beauty, his own fear, his own [[the-portrait|portrait]]. The promise that killing the past will bring freedom is the last in a long series of false promises [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has believed.

The irony is structural: the very act [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] believes will liberate him is the act that kills him. The knife that "killed the painter" now kills the painter's subject. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] — the record of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins — survives, restored to its original beauty, while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself becomes the withered, loathsome corpse discovered by the servants. The past cannot be killed; it can only be unconfessed.

## Themes

- [[the-past]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit statement of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fantasy that the past can be annihilated — and the novel's most decisive refutation of that fantasy.
- [[freedom]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s understanding of freedom as destruction is the tragic culmination of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy.
- [[the-portrait]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]] is both record and conscience; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s attempt to destroy it is an attempt to destroy the evidence of his own soul.
- [[irony]]: The structural irony of the climax — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] kills himself while trying to kill his past — is the novel's definitive moral statement.
