## Quotation

> "Pleasure is the only thing worth having a theory about. But I am afraid I cannot claim my theory as my own. It belongs to Nature, not to me. Pleasure is Nature's test, her sign of approval. When we are happy, we are always good, but when we are good, we are not always happy." (006-037)
>
> — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]], Chapter 6

## Context

The aphorism is delivered at a dinner at the Bristol, where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] have gathered before going to the theatre to see [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] perform. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just announced his engagement, and [[basil-hallward|Basil]] — horrified — has challenged [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence.

When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] asks sarcastically what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has learned from Lord Henry — "your theories about life, your theories about love, your theories about pleasure" (006-036) — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replies with this single, concentrated defense. He does not deny having theories; he asserts that pleasure is the only subject that merits theorizing.

The final sentence — "When we are happy, we are always good, but when we are good, we are not always happy" — inverts conventional morality. Happiness (pleasure) guarantees goodness, but goodness does not guarantee happiness. The priority is clear: pleasure is the foundation, morality the uncertain superstructure.

## Analysis

The aphorism is Lord Henry's most explicit philosophical statement, and it operates on several levels. "Pleasure is the only thing worth having a theory about" is first a claim about intellectual priorities — of all possible objects of thought, pleasure alone repays systematic reflection. It is also a claim about what a "theory" is for: not to discover truth but to maximize satisfaction.

The second movement — "I cannot claim my theory as my own. It belongs to Nature" — is a characteristic Lord Henry move. He disclaims originality (the theory is Nature's, not his) while simultaneously claiming authority (he is Nature's spokesman). The appeal to Nature as the source of the pleasure principle is itself a theoretical claim — and a deeply nineteenth-century one, echoing the evolutionary ethics that were transforming Victorian thought.

The final clause — "When we are happy, we are always good, but when we are good, we are not always happy" — is the aphorism's most subversive element. It does not reject the category of "goodness"; it merely makes it dependent on happiness. This is hedonism not as the rejection of morality but as its redefinition: the moral is the pleasurable.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The aphorism is the novel's central statement of the hedonistic philosophy, making pleasure the measure of all value.
- [[art-and-life]]: Lord Henry's "theory" of pleasure is an aesthetic theory — pleasure is to be cultivated, refined, theorized, like art.
- [[morality]]: The aphorism inverts the conventional priority of goodness over happiness, making morality subordinate to sensation.
