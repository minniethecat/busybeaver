## Quotation

> "The tragedy of old age is not that one is old, but that one is young." (019-042)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], in the music-room after dinner (Chapter 19). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been urging [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to play Chopin for him, and musing on his own lost youth. He reflects that he is "only ten years older" than [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] yet "wrinkled, and worn, and yellow," while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] remains "really wonderful" and has "never looked more charming" (019-042). The aphorism emerges from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s elegy for his own youth: the true pain of aging is not physical [[transformation|decline]] but the memory of having once been [[youth|young]] — the consciousness of what has been lost (019-042).

## Analysis

This epigram inverts the expected sentiment: old age is tragic not because one faces death but because one remembers youth. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who prizes beauty and sensation above all, the greatest suffering is the awareness of loss. The line also carries a hidden sting for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], whose supernatural youth is preserved at the cost of his soul — he will never experience the "tragedy" [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] describes, but his escape from it is itself the greater tragedy (019-042). The passage continues with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s extended meditation on youth as "the one thing worth having" and his famous declaration: "[[youth|Youth]]! There is nothing like it" (019-042).
