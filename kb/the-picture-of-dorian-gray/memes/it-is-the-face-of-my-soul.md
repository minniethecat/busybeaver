## Quotation

> "It is the face of my soul." (013-023)

## Context

Hours after [[murder-of-basil-hallward|murdering Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] leads his old friend upstairs to see the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to confront [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about the rumours that have destroyed his reputation — and to urge him to pray. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], in a surge of bitter recklessness, decides to show [[basil-hallward|Basil]] the truth.

In the attic, he tears the curtain from the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is horrified. He sees not the [[beauty|beautiful]] youth he painted but a hideous, aging figure with a cruel mouth and blood-stained hands. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] cries out that it is "the face of a satyr" (013-022) and "has the eyes of a devil" (013-024). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s five-word reply — "It is the face of my soul" — is the novel's most compressed utterance, and its philosophical center.

## Analysis

The line operates through devastating simplicity. Five monosyllabic words. No metaphor, no qualification, no epigrammatic flourish. For the first time in the novel, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaks without ornament — and in doing so, speaks the truth.

The statement reverses the novel's founding logic. [[sin|Sin]]ce Chapter 2, the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has absorbed the physical consequences of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins while his body remained [[beauty|beautiful]]. Now [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] collapses that separation: the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is not merely a magical receptacle for his [[sin|corruption]]; it _is_ his soul made visible. The distinction between appearance and reality — the very distinction [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy depends upon — dissolves in five words.

The line also transforms the [[the-portrait|portrait]] from a Gothic plot de[[sin|vice]] into a theological one. "The face of my soul" implies that souls have faces — that inward states are, in some register, visible. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] literalizes the medieval belief that sin marks the body. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy is not that his soul is hideous; it is that only the [[the-portrait|portrait]] shows it.

## Themes

- [[the-portrait]]: The line is the definitive statement of the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s meaning — it is not a [[the-portrait|painting]] of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] but a [[the-portrait|painting]] of what [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has become inside.
- [[soul]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s identification of the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s face with his soul gives the novel's most concrete definition of what the soul is: the moral self, made visible.
- [[conscience]]: The line is an act of confession — the one moment in the novel where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] tells someone the truth about himself without evasion or self-justification.
