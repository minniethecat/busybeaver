## Quotation

> "There are few of us who have not sometimes wakened before dawn, either after one of those dreamless nights that make us almost enamoured of death, or one of those nights of horror and misshapen joy, when through the chambers of the brain sweep phantoms more terrible than reality itself... Gradually white fingers creep through the curtains, and they appear to tremble. Black fantastic shadows crawl into the corners of the room... Duty, Memory, and the remembrance even of joy having its bitterness." (011-011)
>
> — Narrator, Chapter 11

## Context

This passage occurs early in Chapter 11, immediately after the narrator's account of the "new Hedonism" (011-010). It is one of the chapter's most lyrical and philosophical digressions — a meditation on the strange half-light between night and morning, when the mind is most vulnerable to memory and regret.

The passage describes a universal experience: waking before dawn, when the defenses of daytime consciousness are down, and the mind is invaded by "phantoms more terrible than reality itself." The narrator names these phantoms: Duty, Memory — and, most strikingly, "the remembrance even of joy having its bitterness."

This is not a character's speech but the narrator's own voice — Wilde writing at his most Pateresque, blending Gothic imagery with philosophical reflection.

## Analysis

The key phrase — "the remembrance even of joy having its bitterness" — captures a paradox at the heart of the novel's treatment of pleasure. If even joy, remembered, becomes bitter, then the pursuit of experience for its own sake is doomed. The new Hedonism promises that experience is its own justification; this passage suggests that experience, once past, becomes a source of pain.

The Gothic imagery ("black fantastic shadows," "phantoms more terrible than reality") places this meditation in the tradition of the supernatural. But the true horror here is psychological: the mind's inability to escape its own past. This prefigures [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s later torment, when the portrait becomes an inescapable record of his sins.

The passage also enacts the very aestheticism it describes. The prose is highly wrought, consciously beautiful, turning the painful experience of pre-dawn waking into a piece of exquisite prose. The style itself is a demonstration of the "new Hedonism" — extracting aesthetic pleasure from even the most melancholy material.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The passage complicates the novel's celebration of pleasure by showing that pleasure's afterlife is pain.
- [[beauty]]: The beautiful prose style transforms painful experience into aesthetic object — a miniature demonstration of the novel's central method.
- [[memory-and-forgetting]]: Memory appears as one of the "phantoms" that torment the waking mind.
