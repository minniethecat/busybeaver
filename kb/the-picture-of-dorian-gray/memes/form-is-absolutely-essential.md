## Quotation

> "For the canons of good society are, or should be, the same as the canons of art. Form is absolutely essential to it. It should have the dignity of a ceremony, as well as its unreality, and should combine the insincere character of a romantic play with the wit and beauty that make such plays delightful to us." (011-031)
>
> — Narrator, Chapter 11

## Context

This passage appears early in Chapter 11, during the narrative's extended account of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s social ascendancy. The paragraph preceding this declaration observes that "Society — civilized society, at least — is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating" (011-030). The narrator then pivots to a general pronouncement on the nature of society itself.

The phrase "the canons of good society are, or should be, the same as the canons of art" directly echoes the aesthetic doctrine of the Preface, where [[oscar-wilde|Wilde]] declared that "the artist is the creator of beautiful things" and that art exists for its own sake. Here the narrator applies that doctrine to social life — form, not substance, is what matters.

The passage reflects [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]'s pervasive influence: his dinner-table epigrams have been recast as narrative truth.

## Analysis

The epigram is remarkable for its radical aestheticization of social morality. The narrator does not merely observe that society values appearances — he *endorses* this as it *should* be. The auxiliary verb "should" transforms description into prescription.

"Form is absolutely essential to it" is the thesis sentence. The word "absolutely" tolerates no exception. The qualities that follow — dignity, unreality, insincerity, wit, beauty — are all drawn from the vocabulary of literary criticism, not ethics. A society is to be judged as one judges a play: by its style, not its truthfulness.

Most provocatively, the narrator praises "the insincere character of a romantic play." Insincerity, in conventional morality a vice, is here elevated to an aesthetic virtue — "a method by which we can multiply our personalities" (011-032). The passage marks the point at which [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s worldview has fully absorbed [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s teaching.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The passage is a direct application of aesthetic doctrine to social existence — art provides the model for living.
- [[beauty]]: Wit and beauty are named as the criteria by which a society (and by extension a life) should be evaluated.
- [[morality]]: The epigram explicitly replaces moral judgment with aesthetic judgment as the standard of value.
