## Quotation

> "You have been the one person in my life who has really influenced my art. Whatever I have done that is good, I owe to you. Ah! you don't know what it cost me to tell you all that I have told you." (009-051)

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] speaks these words to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] during a tense conversation the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide (Ch9). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to console [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] but finds him unnervingly indifferent to the tragedy. The conversation turns to the [[the-portrait|portrait]], which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] refuses to let [[basil-hallward|Basil]] see, and then to the reason [[basil-hallward|Basil]] had earlier refused to exhibit it.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just made an extraordinary confession: that from the moment he met [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], "your personality had the most extraordinary influence over me. I was dominated, soul, brain, and power, by you" (009-043). He described worshipping [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as "the visible incarnation of that unseen ideal whose memory haunts us artists like an exquisite dream" (009-043).

The confession leaves [[basil-hallward|Basil]] feeling exposed. He tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "something seems to have gone out of me" and adds, in a related line that has become one of the novel's most quoted passages, "Perhaps [[never-put-ones-worship-into-words|one should never put one's worship into words]]" (009-053).

## Analysis

This quote captures the asymmetrical nature of influence in the novel's central relationships. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has been artistically transformed by [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty, yet [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] receives the confession with detachment—"That is not even a compliment" (009-052). The artist has poured his soul into his creation, but the subject is unmoved.

The phrase "whatever I have done that is good, I owe to you" echoes the Preface's declaration about the artist and his work. Wilde wrote that "to reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim" (P02-001), yet [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has done the opposite—he has revealed himself in the [[the-portrait|portrait]] and now in this confession. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] contains [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s "[[worship|idolatry]]" (009-043), just as it will come to contain [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins.

The line also foreshadows [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s fate. Having confessed his worship, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has made himself vulnerable. When he later confronts [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] about the rumors surrounding his life [013-005–016], and finally sees the hideously transformed [[the-portrait|portrait]] (013-017), his earlier emotional exposure becomes literal, physical vulnerability—[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] murders him (013-025).

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response to [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s confession reveals his growing emotional coldness. Instead of being moved by his friend's vulnerability, he feels relief: "instead of having been forced to reveal his own secret, he had succeeded, almost by chance, in wresting a secret from his friend" (009-064). The asymmetry of their relationship—[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s [[worship|devotion]] versus [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s detachment—prefigures the violence to come.
