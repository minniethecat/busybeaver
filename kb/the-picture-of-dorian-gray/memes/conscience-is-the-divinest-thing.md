## Quotation

> "I know what conscience is, to begin with. It is not what you told me it was. It is the divinest thing in us. Don't sneer at it, Harry, any more—at least not before me. I want to be good. I can't bear the idea of my soul being hideous." (008-028)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] speaks these words to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] on the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide (Ch8). The previous evening, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] had witnessed the first visible [[transformation|change]] in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]]—the "mask of his shame" (008-015)—and had spent the night in [[conscience|remorse]], writing a passionate letter to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] begging forgiveness (008-018). When [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives to console him, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] declares his moral awakening.

The speech is a direct repudiation of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy. Throughout their acquaintance, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has dismissed conscience as a form of cowardice, telling [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "the terror of society, which is the basis of morals" is one of the two forces governing humanity (002-024). Here [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] rejects that view outright.

## Analysis

This passage represents the most explicit moral statement [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] ever makes in the novel. It occurs at a pivotal moment: the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has just revealed its supernatural property, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] feels the weight of his cruelty toward [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. He experiences what the narrator describes as "the luxury in self-reproach" (008-018).

The phrase "the divinest thing in us" is striking because it frames conscience in [[soul|spirit]]ual terms—a direct counter to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aesthetic-materialist worldview. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s plea—"I want to be good"—is his last sincere moral aspiration before his descent into [[sin|corruption]].

The irony is profound: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moment of moral clarity arrives too late. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is already dead, and his resolution to "marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]" (008-030) cannot undo the past. Within hours, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will arrive with news of the inquest, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fragile conscience will be seduced away by the aesthetic appeal of tragedy (008-051). The [[the-portrait|portrait]], which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] hoped would be "a guide to him through life" (008-017), will become instead a record of his sins.
