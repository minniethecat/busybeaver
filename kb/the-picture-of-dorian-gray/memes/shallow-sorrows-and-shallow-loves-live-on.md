## Quotation

> "Shallow sorrows and shallow loves live on. The loves and sorrows that are great are destroyed by their own plenitude." (018-004)

## Context

Three days after [[murder-of-basil-hallward|murdering Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] finally ventures outdoors at his country estate. The morning is clear and pine-scented; something in the winter air seems to restore his "joyousness and his ardour for life."

The narrator pauses to explain why. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s nature has "revolted against the excess of anguish that had sought to maim and mar the perfection of its calm." With "subtle and finely wrought temperaments it is always so. Their strong passions must either bruise or bend. They either slay the man, or themselves die."

Then comes the aphorism: shallow emotions endure; great ones self-destruct. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has convinced himself he was merely "the victim of a terror-stricken imagination." He looks back on his fears "with something of pity and not a little of contempt."

The irony is total. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s survival is not a sign of depth — it is the proof of his shallowness.

## Analysis

This passage is Wilde's most explicit statement of the novel's psychological thesis. The mechanism is paradoxical: intensity of feeling is self-consuming. The guilty man who truly feels remorse may be destroyed by it; the shallow man who shrugs off his crimes lives on. [[conscience|Moral depth]] becomes a liability; superficiality becomes a survival trait.

The word "plenitude" is key. It is not simply that great emotions are painful — it is that they are *full*, complete, leaving no room for anything else. They have nowhere to go but inward. A shallow sorrow, by contrast, is porous; it dissipates. A shallow love is fungible; it can be replaced.

This idea connects directly to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy in [[only-two-kinds-of-people-who-are-really-fascinating|Chapter 7]] and [[it-is-only-shallow-people-who-require-years|Chapter 9]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] advocates emotional distance as a strategy; the narrator here presents it as a psychological law. The two converge: the novel's most dangerous idea is that depth of feeling is maladaptive.

The passage also echoes the [[preface-moral-immoral|Preface]]: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book." Wilde is describing a world without moral ordering — where sorrow and love are forces of nature, indifferent to desert.

## Themes

- [[psychology]]: The claim that "strong passions must either bruise or bend" is a theory of mind — temperament determines whether feeling destroys or dissipates.
- [[sin]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s survival after murder is not innocence but the ultimate indictment — his soul is too shallow for genuine remorse.
- [[conscience]]: By implication, [[conscience|guilt]] that does not destroy is not real guilt. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s calm is the calm of vacancy.
- [[double-life]]: The split between inner corruption and outer composure is sustained by this emotional shallowness. Depth would shatter the mask.
