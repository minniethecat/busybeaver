## Quotation

> "What has the actual lapse of time got to do with it? It is only shallow people who require years to get rid of an emotion. A man who is master of himself can end a sorrow as easily as he can invent a pleasure. I don't want to be at the mercy of my emotions. I want to use them, to enjoy them, and to dominate them." (009-007)
>
> — Dorian Gray, to Basil Hallward, Chapter 9

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has come to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s house the morning after learning of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s death. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is devastated — he had come to offer condolences, expecting [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to be prostrate with grief. Instead, he finds [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] composed, detached, already dressed for the day. When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] expresses shock that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] went to the opera the previous evening — the very day [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] died — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] delivers this response.

The exchange is a turning point. [[basil-hallward|Basil]], who has known [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] since before [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, cannot reconcile the innocent young man he once painted with this cold philosopher of self-mastery. "What has the actual lapse of time got to do with it?" — the question itself is a dismissal of ordinary human grief, which requires time, which cannot be hurried, which resists being mastered.

## Analysis

This passage is the most explicit statement of the philosophy [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has absorbed from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]. Every clause bears [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s imprint: the contempt for "shallow people" who need time to heal; the aesthetic ideal of the man who is "master of himself"; the language of invention and domination applied to emotional life. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is no longer quoting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]; he has become him.

The progression of verbs is revealing: "use them, enjoy them, dominate them." Emotions are not to be felt but to be instrumentalised. "Use" suggests utility; "enjoy" suggests aesthetic consumption; "dominate" suggests power. The sequence moves from pragmatism to pleasure to tyranny. This is the logic that will justify [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s entire subsequent life — a life in which other people's feelings are raw material for his own experience.

Yet the passage also contains a hidden fragility. The insistence on mastery — "I don't want to be at the mercy of my emotions" — betrays a fear of exactly that. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not describing a achieved state of self-command; he is arguing for it, performing it, willing it into being. The very vehemence of the declaration suggests that his composure is precarious. Later events — his panic at [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s murder, his terror of [[james-vane|James Vane]], his final desperate attack on [[the-portrait|the portrait]] — will prove that the emotions he claimed to dominate have been dominating him all along.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: The passage is a document of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaking in a voice not his own, so thoroughly colonised that he believes it is authentic.
- [[transformation]]: The passage marks the completion of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s first moral transformation — from innocent to aesthetician of the self.
- [[pleasure]]: The reduction of emotion to pleasure ("enjoy them") is the novel's central moral problem.
