## Quotation

> "I won't hear of it," laughed Lord Henry, sinking into a chair. "From a label there is no escape! I refuse the title." (017-008)

## Context

The scene is the conservatory at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country house. A house party is in progress: the Duchess of Monmouth presides over tea; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been expounding his plan for "rechristening everything," lamenting that "we have lost the faculty of giving lovely names to things." The Duchess, amused, asks what they should call [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] himself.

"His name is Prince Paradox," says [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].

"I recognize him in a flash," exclaims the Duchess.

"I won't hear of it," laughs [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], sinking into a chair. "From a label there is no escape! I refuse the title."

"Royalties may not abdicate," the Duchess warns.

"You wish me to defend my throne, then?"

"Yes."

The ex[[transformation|change]] is playful, but it encodes one of the novel's deepest anxieties: the relationship between names and identity. Throughout the novel, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] attempts to escape the consequences of his actions by separating his appearance from his reality — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] from the man. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s jest that "from a label there is no escape" speaks directly to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s predicament, though neither character acknowledges it.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram is a paradox about paradox. The man who is being called "Prince Paradox" protests that a label is inescapable — which is to say, naming something fixes it, and once fixed, it cannot be unfixed. But [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s entire career as a wit consists of *escaping* labels: he is a husband who speaks against marriage, a man of society who despises society, a moralist who preaches immorality. His protest — "I refuse the title" — is itself a performance of the paradox he is being named for.

The Duchess's reply — "Royalties may not abdicate" — extends the metaphor into political theory. A prince cannot stop being a prince; a paradox cannot stop being paradoxical. The ex[[transformation|change]] is a comedy of manners that is also, in miniature, a theory of identity: we are what we are called, whether we like it or not.

For [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], listening to this banter, the stakes are mortal. His entire life since Ch2 has been an attempt to escape the label of his own sins — to keep the [[the-portrait|portrait]] hidden, to maintain the mask of youth and beauty. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s quip, spoken in jest, is the truth [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cannot face: the label *will* catch up. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] *is* his label, and from it there is no escape.

## Themes

- [[identity]]: The epigram raises the question of whether identity is chosen or assigned — and whether labels can be refused.
- [[language]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s concern with naming reflects Wilde's broader interest in the power of words to create reality.
- [[paradox]]: The epigram is self-referential — a paradox about being called a paradox — performing what it describes.
