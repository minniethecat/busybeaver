## Quotation

> "My dear fellow, you forget that we are in the native land of the hypocrite." (012-020)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks this line to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] on the eve of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s thirty-eighth birthday. They are discussing the rumours that swirl around [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the ruined reputations, the "fatal" friendships, the whispers in London society.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just asked whether people say anything against him. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses the question. In this country, he implies, everyone is a hypocrite — so what does it matter what people say? Hypocrisy is not a deviation from the norm; it is the norm. The charge of immorality loses its force when the accusers are themselves living lies.

The aphorism functions as comfort and indictment simultaneously. It comforts [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] by normalising his double life. It indicts the society that judges him — a society whose morality is a performance, whose respectability is a costume.

The setting is rich with irony. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is about to encounter [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] for the last time. The man who knows his secret is waiting in the library. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s glib dismissal of hypocrisy is, unwittingly, the last intellectual defence [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will hear before he commits murder.

## Analysis

The aphorism is classic Wilde: a truth delivered as a joke, a critique disguised as a quip. On its face, it is a flippant remark about English society. Beneath, it is a devastating indictment. To call a nation "the native land of the hypocrite" is to say that hypocrisy is structural, not incidental — baked into the national character.

The line also functions as social critique of Victorian England. The period's famous "respectability" was, in Wilde's view, a vast apparatus of pretence. People did not live moral lives; they performed them. The true sin was not immorality but detection. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not more immoral than his accusers — he is merely less careful.

For [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], the aphorism is deeply enabling. If everyone is a hypocrite, then his own double life is not exceptional but representative. He is not a monster; he is a native. This reframing allows him to continue his course without confronting its moral implications.

The irony is that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] himself is the novel's supreme hypocrite. He preaches a philosophy of aesthetic individualism while living a life of conventional respectability. He is married, attends society functions, and never acts on his own doctrines. He is at home in the native land of the hypocrite because he is one of its principal citizens.

Wilde himself, of course, would soon be destroyed by the very hypocrisy this line mocks. The line reads differently after 1895.

## Themes

- [[hypocrisy]]: The aphorism defines hypocrisy as the national condition — not a vice but an environment.
- [[society]]: A critique of Victorian society's performative morality and the gap between public face and private conduct.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: Lord Henry's characteristic move — transform a moral problem into a sociological observation, defusing its force.
- [[double-life]]: Dorian's double life is reframed as normal — he is not exceptional, merely adapted to his environment.
- [[morality]]: The sceptical claim that public morality is always a disguise, never a reality.
