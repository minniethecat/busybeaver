## Quotation

> "Each of us has heaven and hell in him, Basil," cried Dorian with a wild gesture of despair. (013-025)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] cries out these words in Chapter 13, moments after [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has seen the hideous [[the-portrait|portrait]] and recognized it as a representation of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul. The revelation has passed through several stages: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s initial horror ("It has the eyes of a devil"), [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s cold admission ("It is the [[it-is-the-face-of-my-soul|face of my soul]]"), and [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s anguished cry: "Christ! what a thing I must have worshipped!"

The line is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s only moment of theological self-defence in the chapter. After the cold theatricality of "Draw that curtain back" ((013-006)) and the flat declaration "It is the [[it-is-the-face-of-my-soul|face of my soul]]," his voice suddenly breaks. The "wild gesture of despair" marks a shift from performance to something closer to genuine anguish. For an instant, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not the aesthete displaying his [[sin|corruption]] but a man horrified by what he has become — and reaching for a universal claim to mitigate the horror.

The claim itself is not original to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; it is a variation on the Christian doctrine of original sin, democratized and psychologized. By saying "each of us," [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] attempts to implicate [[basil-hallward|Basil]] — and all humanity — in his condition. If everyone contains both heaven and hell, then [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s visible hell is merely an extreme case of a universal condition. The line is both a confession and an evasion: it admits the horror while refusing full responsibility for it.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s response is to reject the evasion: "My God! If it is true, and this is what you have done with your life, why, you must be worse even than those who talk against you fancy you to be!" He does not dispute the premise that each person contains both good and evil; he disputes the implication that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s evil is therefore ordinary.

## Analysis

The line's power lies in its emotional register. Throughout the chapter, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been in control — leading [[basil-hallward|Basil]] up the stairs, unlocking the door, delivering his lines with theatrical precision. The "wild gesture of despair" is the first crack in that control. The word "cried" — not "said" or "remarked" or "answered" — signals a loss of composure.

The theological content of the line is less important than its rhetorical function. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is attempting to universalize his condition: if "each of us" contains hell, then his own hell is not exceptional. This is a strategy of evasion through generalisation — a move [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] would recognise and admire. But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], unlike [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], cannot sustain the ironic distance. The despair is real; the gesture is wild; the performance is crumbling.

The line also functions as a counterweight to the novel's aestheticist premises. The doctrine that life should be treated as art, that beauty is the highest value, has led [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to this room — a dusty attic, a hideous [[the-portrait|portrait]], a knife on a painted chest. "Each of us has heaven and hell in him" is not an aesthetic statement; it is a moral one. It acknowledges categories — good and evil, heaven and hell — that the novel's aesthetic surface has systematically avoided. In this moment, Wilde allows the moral vocabulary back into the text, and the effect is devastating.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s response is the correct theological one: the universality of the condition does not excuse the particularity of the sin. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s hell is visible not because he is human but because of "what you have done with your life."

## Themes

- [[duality]]: The line is the novel's most direct articulation of the double nature of the self — the inner division that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] literalises.
- [[good-and-evil]]: The categories, long submerged beneath the novel's aesthetic surface, erupt into explicit statement.
- [[soul]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul is uniquely visible, but the line insists that all souls contain the same darkness.
- [[human-nature]]: The claim that "each of us" shares this duality is both a theological truth and a strategy of evasion.
- [[corruption]]: The line marks the moment when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|corruption]] is named — by himself — in moral rather than aesthetic terms.
