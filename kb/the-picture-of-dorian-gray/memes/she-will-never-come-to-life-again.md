## Quotation

> "She will never come to life again now." (008-060)
>
> — Dorian Gray, to Lord Henry Wotton, Chapter 8

## Context

Lord Henry has just reminded [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] of his own words about [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]: "You said to me that Sibyl Vane represented to you all the heroines of romance — that she was Desdemona one night, and Ophelia the other; that if she died as Juliet, she came to life as Imogen." (008-059) In [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s imagination, [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] was endlessly renewable — each night a different heroine, each death followed by a resurrection.

Now, with the news of her actual suicide, the metaphor collapses. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] mutters the line "burying his face in his hands" (008-060). It is the closest he comes to genuine grief in the entire chapter, and it lasts only a moment. Lord Henry immediately overrides it with aesthetic consolation: "you must think of that lonely death in the tawdry dressing-room simply as a strange lurid fragment from some Jacobean tragedy" (008-061).

## Analysis

The line is remarkable for what it reveals about Dorian's relationship to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. He had never loved _her_ — he loved what she represented: an art that was more vivid than life. The phrase "come to life again" is telling: in Dorian's mind, Sibyl existed most fully when she was on stage, performing other identities. Her offstage self was, for him, a disappointment — the "real" Sibyl who could not act was the Sibyl he rejected the night before her death.

The line also marks the dawning of a terrible knowledge: that real death is final in a way that stage death is not. Ophelia drowns every night and walks offstage; Sibyl Vane drinks prussic acid once and does not return. The aesthetic frame that Dorian had used to contain his experience of Sibyl has now broken, and for one brief moment he sees clearly what has happened.

Lord Henry's immediate response — reconverting the real death back into "a strange lurid fragment from some Jacobean tragedy" — is the novel's most chilling demonstration of his philosophy in action. He will not let Dorian feel. The moment of clarity is smothered under a layer of aesthetic rhetoric, and by the next paragraph Dorian is relieved: "You have explained me to myself, Harry" (008-063).

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The line marks the violent collision of Dorian's aesthetic worldview with the brute fact of death.
- [[death]]: Sibyl's suicide is the novel's first death, and Dorian's response sets the pattern for his later encounters with mortality.
- [[illusion]]: Dorian realizes that his image of Sibyl as an endlessly renewing theatrical figure was an illusion — and that the real woman has paid for it with her life.
