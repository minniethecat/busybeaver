## Quotation

> "Never! Scepticism is the beginning of faith." (017-048)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to the Duchess of Monmouth, Chapter 17

## Context

This epigram arises in the midst of the tea-table battle of wits at Lady Narborough's. The conversation has moved through several topics — the "race," decay, and the nature of belief. When the Duchess says "The fashionable substitute for belief" (017-046) — dismissing something (likely religion or conventional morality) as a mere stand-in for genuine conviction — and then accuses [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of being "a sceptic" (017-047), he counters with this paradox.

The emphatic "Never!" at the opening gives the line dramatic force, as though [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been waiting for precisely this accusation. He does not deny being a sceptic; instead, he redefines what scepticism means. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], doubt is not the terminus of belief but its starting point.

## Analysis

The epigram echoes a theological tradition that runs from Augustine ("I believe in order to understand") through Pascal to Kierkegaard, but [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] inverts it. In the traditional formulation, faith precedes understanding; here, scepticism — doubt, questioning, refusal to accept — is the door through which one must pass to reach genuine faith. A faith that has never been doubted is not faith at all but habit, inheritance, or social conformity.

In the context of the novel, the line illuminates [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own relationship to belief. He is the great sceptic of the novel, questioning every received idea about morality, art, love, and society. Yet his scepticism is not nihilistic; it is generative. It produces the epigrams, the paradoxes, the new valuations that so entrance [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s scepticism is creative, not destructive — or rather, it destroys only to create.

The epigram also serves as a subtle defence of Wilde's own intellectual method. The Preface to *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is a catalogue of sceptical aphorisms about art, and yet the novel that follows is deeply interested in moral and [[soul|spirit]]ual questions. Scepticism — the refusal of easy answers — is the beginning of the novel's own engagement with faith, conscience, and the soul.

## Themes

- [[scepticism]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] elevates scepticism from a negative position (lack of belief) to a positive one (the precondition for authentic belief).
- [[paradox]]: The rhetorical structure — denying one thing to affirm its apparent opposite — is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s signature move.
