## Quotation

> "I have put too much of myself into it." (001-008)

## Context

In the opening chapter of the novel, [[basil-hallward]] has just completed the full-length portrait of [[dorian-gray]] — a work that [[lord-henry-wotton]] immediately declares to be "your best work, Basil, the best thing you have ever done" (001-007). When Lord Henry urges him to send it to the Grosvenor for exhibition, Basil refuses with an intensity that surprises his friend.

"I know you will laugh at me," Basil says, "but I really can't exhibit it. I have put too much of myself into it" (001-008). Lord Henry does indeed laugh, interpreting the remark as vanity — "I really can't see any resemblance between you, with your rugged strong face and your coal-black hair, and this young Adonis" (001-009). But Basil is serious. The confession reveals a conception of art in which the artist's soul is present in the work, not merely as style or technique but as a quasi-physical essence.

Basil elaborates later in the conversation: "every portrait that is painted with feeling is a portrait of the artist, not of the sitter" (001-011). The painting of Dorian is, in some sense, a self-portrait — and this is precisely why Basil cannot bear to expose it to public view.

## Analysis

Basil's confession is the seed from which the entire supernatural plot of the novel grows. By putting "too much of himself" into the portrait, Basil creates something more than a representation: he creates an object charged with emotional and perhaps even spiritual force. The portrait's later transformation — aging and corrupting in Dorian's place — can be read as the logical extension of this initial investment of self.

The phrase also introduces Wilde's complex theory of art. In the [[preface-moral-immoral|Preface]], Wilde will declare that "to reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim" — a principle that Basil's confession directly violates. Basil has failed to conceal himself; the artwork betrays its maker. This is, in Wilde's terms, an artistic failure — but it is also what gives the portrait its uncanny power.

There is a homoerotic dimension to Basil's confession that would not have been lost on Wilde's contemporary readers. The "too much of myself" that Basil has put into the painting is inseparable from his feelings for Dorian. His later confession in Chapter 9 — "I worshipped you" (009-043) — makes explicit what Chapter 1 can only suggest. The portrait is a vessel for desires that cannot be spoken, and Basil's refusal to exhibit it is a form of self-protection.

## Themes

- **[[art-and-life]]**: The quote establishes the central premise that art and life can interpenetrate — that a painting can contain not just the image of its subject but the soul of its creator.
- **[[the-portrait]]**: The portrait itself is born from this confession. Its supernatural properties are a consequence of the excessive investment of self that Basil describes.
- **[[identity]]**: Basil's remark raises the question of where one self ends and another begins — a question the novel explores through the triangular relationship of Basil, Dorian, and the portrait.
