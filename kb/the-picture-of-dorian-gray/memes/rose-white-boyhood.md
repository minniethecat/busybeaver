## Quotation

> "He felt a wild longing for the unstained purity of his boyhood—his rose-white boyhood, as Lord Henry had once called it." (020-003)
>
> — Narrative voice, Chapter 20

## Context

This passage opens the final chapter of the novel. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is alone, reflecting on his life after the events of the preceding evening — the confrontation with [[james-vane|James Vane]], the death of [[james-vane|James Vane]] at the shooting party, and his resolve to reform. The phrase "[[youth|rose-white boyhood]]" deliberately echoes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]'s speech in Chapter 2, where Henry first seduced [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] with the gospel of youth: "You, [[dorian-gray|Mr. Gray]], you yourself, with your [[youth|rose-red youth]] and your [[youth|rose-white boyhood]], you have had passions that have made you afraid" (002-026). By Chapter 20, that boyhood is irretrievably lost, and the echo of Henry's words underscores the tragedy: the very man who warned [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that youth would fade is the one who set him on the path that destroyed it.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s longing for "unstained purity" is the novel's most explicit statement of his desire to undo the past. It comes immediately before his rationalisation that he will "be good" and that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] will show signs of his reformation — a hope the novel's final pages will cruelly dash.

## Analysis

The adjective "wild" is key: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s longing is not temperate or reasoned but desperate, animal. It is the longing of a man who knows, at some level, that what he wants is impossible. The word "unstained" carries the novel's central metaphor of [[sin|corruption]] as stain — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has been taking on stains that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s body has been spared. To long for "unstained purity" is to long for a self that predates the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s existence.

"Rose-white boyhood" is a phrase of extraordinary sensory precision. "Rose-white" suggests both the colour of youthful skin (the blush of health and innocence) and the fragility of a flower. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] coined the phrase as an aesthetic observation; the narrative voice now reprises it as an elegy. The distance between Chapter 2's "[[youth|rose-white boyhood]]" (something to be feared losing) and Chapter 20's (something already lost) measures the arc of the entire novel.

## Themes

- [[youth]] — The novel's central preoccupation: youth as a fleeting state whose loss is inevitable and whose worship is corrupting.
- [[innocence]] — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s longing for lost innocence is the final, futile gesture of a conscience that has been suppressed for eighteen years.
- [[beauty]] — The aestheticisation of childhood — "[[youth|rose-white boyhood]]" — reveals how even innocence is framed in terms of beauty in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s world.
- [[time]] — The temporal structure of the novel collapses here: Chapter 2's warning and Chapter 20's lamentation form a closed loop of desire and regret.
