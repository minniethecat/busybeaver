## Quotation

> "Yes: there was to be, as Lord Henry had prophesied, a new Hedonism that was to recreate life and to save it from that harsh uncomely puritanism that is having, in our own day, its curious revival. It was to have its service of the intellect, certainly, yet it was never to accept any theory or system that would involve the sacrifice of any mode of passionate experience. Its aim, indeed, was to be experience itself, and not the fruits of experience, sweet or bitter as they might be." (011-010)
>
> — Narrator, Chapter 11

## Context

This passage appears in Chapter 11, the long catalogue chapter that chronicles Dorian Gray's years of decadent exploration. The narrator describes the philosophy that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has adopted under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence — a "new Hedonism" that treats experience as an end in itself.

The passage explicitly credits [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] as the prophet of this philosophy ("as Lord Henry had prophesied"), connecting it back to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s earlier pronouncements in the novel's opening chapters. But the narrator's voice here is not [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s — it is Wilde's own, describing with evident sympathy a philosophy that elevates sensation and intellect above moral systems.

## Analysis

The passage defines the new Hedonism through three core commitments. First, it opposes "puritanism" — not Christianity as such, but the "harsh uncomely" strain that denies the body and the senses. Second, it insists on intellectual seriousness ("its service of the intellect"), distinguishing itself from mere debauchery. Third, and most radically, it values experience over outcome: "experience itself, and not the fruits of experience."

This third point is the philosophical heart of the passage. Traditional morality judges actions by their consequences — sweet or bitter. The new Hedonism refuses this calculus. Experience is its own justification. This is a dangerous but seductive doctrine, and the novel will spend the rest of its pages testing whether it can be lived.

The phrase "it was never to accept any theory or system that would involve the sacrifice of any mode of passionate experience" is particularly telling. It makes the new Hedonism not just a philosophy of pleasure but a philosophy of refusal — a rejection of any limit on experience. This absolutism is what distinguishes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s decadence from ordinary self-indulgence.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The new Hedonism is the novel's most explicit philosophical treatment of pleasure as a way of life.
- [[art-and-life]]: The philosophy aestheticizes life itself, turning every experience into a potential work of art.
- [[beauty]]: The new Hedonism is inextricably tied to the worship of beauty that drives the novel's plot.
