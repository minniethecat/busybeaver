## Quotation

> "The thoroughly well-informed man — that is the modern ideal. And the mind of the thoroughly well-informed man is a dreadful thing. It is like a bric-à-brac shop, all monsters and dust, with everything priced above its proper value." (001-065)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s garden, in the same paragraph as "genius lasts longer than beauty." (001-065) Having diagnosed the anxiety that drives [[genius-lasts-longer-than-beauty|over-education]] — the fear that beauty fades and only intellect endures — he now turns his fire on the product of that anxiety: the modern ideal of the "thoroughly well-informed man."

The setting is telling: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks these words while reclining in a sunlit garden, having just plucked and dismembered a daisy. The casual destructiveness of the gesture mirrors the casual destructiveness of the aphorism. He is not arguing against knowledge; he is aestheticizing the argument against it, turning intellectualism into an image of Gothic absurdity.

## Analysis

The "bric-à-brac shop" is one of Wilde's most memorable images, and its power lies in its specificity. A bric-à-brac shop is not a library or a museum — it is a jumble, a collection without curation, a space where objects accumulate without order or purpose. The mind of the well-informed man, in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s metaphor, is not organized knowledge but miscellaneous clutter: facts without synthesis, information without wisdom.

The catalogue of the shop's contents — "all monsters and dust" — sharpens the critique. "Monsters" suggests that the ideas collected are not merely useless but grotesque, misshapen things that ought not to be displayed. "Dust" suggests neglect and obsolescence: the knowledge has been acquired and then abandoned, never integrated into a living intelligence. And "everything priced above its proper value" is the clinching indictment: the well-informed man does not merely hoard useless facts; he overvalues them, mistaking accumulation for understanding.

This aphorism is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] at his most paradoxically educational. Even as he denounces education, he is demonstrating its alternative: the epigram as the unit of real intelligence. The well-informed man accumulates facts; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] distills them into images. The bric-à-brac shop is a critique by counter-example: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own mind, the aphorism implies, is curated, selective, and proportioned — everything the well-informed man's is not.

For the novel's larger argument, the aphorism aligns with the Preface's declaration that "all art is quite useless." [P02] Useful knowledge — the kind the well-informed man hoards — is, in Wilde's aesthetic, the enemy of art. Art, like [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s wit, is useless and therefore free. The well-informed man, burdened by his facts, is the opposite of free.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The aphorism contrasts the curated, aesthetic mind ([[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s) with the accumulative, undiscriminating mind (the modern ideal), mapping the novel's larger opposition between art and information.
- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s critique of the "modern ideal" is also a critique of moral earnestness — the well-informed man is the Victorian improvement-industry's ideal consumer.
- [[influence]]: The aphorism models the kind of thinking [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will teach [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]: not the accumulation of knowledge but the elegant dismissal of it.
