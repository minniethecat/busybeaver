## Quotation

> "You will always be fond of me. I represent to you all the sins you have never had the courage to commit." (006-040)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks these words to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] at [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s home, as they prepare to leave for the theatre to see [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] perform. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] is also present. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just said to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], "I don't know why I like you so much" — a statement that acknowledges the irrational power of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s attraction.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is startling in its frankness. He does not claim to be liked for his wit, charm, or intelligence. Instead, he names the dark transaction at the heart of their relationship: he embodies the [[sin|transgression]]s [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] desires but dares not attempt. Their friendship is not mutual admiration but vicarious sin.

## Analysis

The statement is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most psychologically acute moments. It recognizes that influence is not about persuasion but about *representation* — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not tell [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to sin; he simply *is* the possibility of sin, making it imaginable. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "fondness" is really gratitude: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has given him permission to desire what he already wanted.

The phrase "sins you have never had the courage to commit" is double-edged. It flatters [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] (suggesting he has the desire, only lacking the nerve) while also positioning [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] as braver, more authentic. The dynamic creates a permanent asymmetry: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will always be in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s debt for enlarging his imaginative horizon.

The word "always" is also significant. In a novel where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty is literally preserved while everything else decays, the claim of permanence — "you will *always* be fond of me" — is both a prediction and a curse. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] imagines a static relationship in a novel where stasis proves monstrous.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: The passage is the novel's most explicit statement of how influence works — not through commands but through embodying forbidden possibilities.
- [[sin]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s "sins" are unspecified, which makes them more powerful — they are whatever [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] most desires and fears.
- [[temptation]]: The dynamic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] describes is the structure of temptation itself: the tempter does not force, he represents; the tempted chooses freely.
