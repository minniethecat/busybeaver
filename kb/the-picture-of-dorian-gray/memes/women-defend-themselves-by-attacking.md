## Quotation

> "Women defend themselves by attacking, just as they attack by sudden and strange surrenders." (005-029)
>
> — Narrator, describing Mrs Vane's behaviour, Chapter 5

## Context

This sentence occurs in Chapter 5 during the domestic scene at the Vane household. [[james-vane|James Vane]], about to depart for Australia as a sailor, has asked his mother whether his things are ready. Mrs Vane, "keeping her eyes on her work," has been feeling "ill at ease" alone with her "rough stern son" (005-029).

The narrator explains that "her shallow secret nature was troubled when their eyes met" and that she "used to wonder if he suspected anything" (005-029) — presumably about the irregularity of her own past and the circumstances of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s birth. When James's silence becomes "intolerable," Mrs Vane "began to complain" — and the narrator inserts this aphoristic gloss on her behaviour.

The observation is immediately followed by Mrs Vane's own words: "I hope you will be contented, James, with your sea-faring life... You might have entered a solicitor's office. Solicitors are a very respectable class" (005-029). The complaint is, characteristically, an attack disguised as maternal concern — exactly the behaviour the aphorism describes.

## Analysis

The sentence is a classic Wildean epigram in miniature: a chiasmus that makes a psychological observation symmetrical. "Defend / attack" and "attack / surrender" form a closed loop — women, on this account, are in a perpetual state of tactical reversal where aggression and submission are indistinguishable.

But the sentence is more than a decorative paradox. Placed in Mrs Vane's specific context, it acquires a particular sting. Mrs Vane cannot defend herself directly against James's unspoken suspicions — her secret history makes direct defence impossible. So she attacks instead, veiling her attack in maternal solicitude. The aphorism describes a real psychological dynamic: the weaponisation of weakness, the attack that cloaks itself as concern.

The sentence also illuminates a pattern that recurs throughout the novel. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s surrender to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s love is, in its own way, a form of attack — it demands from him a response he cannot give, and his inability to give it destroys her. The Duchess of Monmouth's conversational parries with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] are attacks disguised as flirtation. Even [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s lifelong performance of beauty and charm is a defence that attacks those who love him.

The narrator's generalisation about "women" reflects the novel's broader tendency to treat gender as a source of epigrammatic material — a habit that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] perfects ("Women are a decorative sex" (004-031), "No woman is a genius. Women are a decorative sex" (004-031)). But here the epigram is grounded in a specific character moment, giving it more dramatic weight than [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s free-floating aphorisms.
