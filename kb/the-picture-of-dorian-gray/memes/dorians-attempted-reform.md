## Summary

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] [[dorian-gray]] attempts to reform by sparing Hetty Merton, a village girl, from seduction.

Expecting the [[the-portrait|portrait]] to show improvement, he instead finds it more hideous than ever.

Now marked with the stain of hypocrisy, the [[the-portrait|portrait]] drives [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to his fatal attack [[dorians-death]].

## Context

[[sin|Sin]]ce [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s pursuit [[in-the-opium-den]] and death [[death-of-james-vane]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been haunted.

> "I can't bear the idea of my soul being hideous." (008-028)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] [[lord-henry-wotton]] remains cynically detached from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s anguish.

The [[the-portrait|portrait]] in the locked schoolroom has become an obsession—a visible emblem of [[conscience]] that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] can neither ignore nor escape.

## Detailed Account

At [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] notices Hetty Merton, a simple and [[beauty|beautiful]] village girl.

The old pattern begins: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] charms her, separates her from her rustic suitor, and prepares to add another conquest to his long list.

But something is different this time. Perhaps it is the lingering terror from [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s pursuit.

Perhaps it is genuine exhaustion with a life of evil. At the decisive moment, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] releases her.

He tells her to go back to the [[youth|young]] man who loves her. He writes no letter, makes no confession—he simply lets her go.

> "It had been like conscience to him. Yes, it had been conscience." (020-011)

He hurries to the schoolroom to examine the [[the-portrait|portrait]], expecting some sign of redemption.

Instead, he finds it "more loathsome, if possible, than before" (020-013), now marked with hypocrisy in the eyes.

> "Choice is taken from them, and conscience is either killed, or, if it lives at all, lives but to give rebellion its fascination." (016-044)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "good deed" was not genuine reform but vanity—the desire to admire his own goodness reflected in the [[the-portrait|portrait]].

The [[the-portrait|portrait]], being an absolutely truthful record of his soul, registers this self-deception as yet another sin.

## Aftermath

The failure of this reform is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s final blow.

Realizing the [[the-portrait|portrait]] will never show mercy, he seizes the knife he killed [[basil-hallward|Basil]] with [[basil-confronts-dorian]].

He stabs the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. The servants find a dead old man and the restored masterpiece: [[dorians-death]].

## Thematic Significance

This is the novel's most complex moral episode. Sparing Hetty is genuinely different from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s past [[sin|corruption]]s.

Yet the [[the-portrait|portrait]] reveals a deeper truth: an act done to admire one's own goodness is vanity, not [[morality|virtue]].

Wilde critiques conventional morality at its sharpest here. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] cannot be fooled by a "good deed" done for wrong reasons.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has externalized his [[conscience]] into the [[the-portrait|portrait]]. That external conscience judges him without mercy or nuance.

There is no forgiveness in a painted record—only an unblinking record of what the soul truly is.
