## Quotation

> "That awful thing, a woman's memory!" (014-006)

## Context

The epigram is reported indirectly by the narrator in Chapter 14. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is at breakfast the morning after [[murder-of-basil-hallward|murdering Basil Hallward]], and he has just read through his correspondence. Among the letters is one that he "read several times over and then tore up with a slight look of annoyance in his face." The narrator then supplies [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aphorism as gloss: "That awful thing, a woman's memory!" as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] had once said.

The letter is from a woman — presumably a former lover or social acquaintance — whose persistence in recollection [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] finds irritating. The context is fraught with irony: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just committed murder, yet it is a woman's letter, not his own conscience, that provokes visible annoyance. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram, deployed here as a throwaway witticism, takes on darker resonance when juxtaposed with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s absolute refusal to remember his own crime.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s original context for the remark is not supplied, but the epigram is entirely in character. Throughout the novel, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reduces women to a series of witty dismissals, treating them as aesthetic objects whose value diminishes with familiarity. The "awful thing" about a woman's memory is, in his view, that she remembers what a man would prefer to forget — promises, declarations, slights, the accumulation of emotional history that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy of perpetual novelty requires one to transcend.

## Analysis

The epigram operates through the collision of its two key terms. "Awful" carries the weight of genuine dread — the original sense of inspiring awe or terror — while "a woman's memory" domesticates that dread into a social complaint. The juxtaposition is comically disproportionate, and the comedy is the point: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] converts real fear into urbane wit, aestheticizing anxiety into epigram.

The remark belongs to a larger pattern in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s discourse about women. He has earlier declared that "women are a decorative sex" who "never have anything to say, but they say it charmingly," and that "one should never trust a woman who tells one her real age." The common thread is a desire to deny women interiority, agency, and the capacity for judgment. A woman who remembers is a woman who can hold a man accountable — hence the "awfulness" of the faculty.

But the epigram cuts in more than one direction. In the immediate context of Chapter 14, the woman's letter is a minor annoyance next to the murder [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just committed, yet it is the letter, not the murder, that elicits a visible reaction. The irony suggests that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], like [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], has learned to manage [[conscience|guilt]] through trivialization — to be more bothered by social inconvenience than by moral catastrophe. The "awful thing" is not really a woman's memory at all; it is the conscience that memory enables, the persistence of the past that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s aesthetic existence is designed to escape.

The line also foreshadows a key plot development: it is a woman — [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s mother — whose memory of a past slight sets in motion the revenge plot that will eventually kill [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. Women's memories, in this novel, are not merely socially awkward; they are lethal.

## Themes

- [[memory]]: The epigram trivializes memory as a social failing, but the novel consistently demonstrates that memory — and the conscience it feeds — is inescapable.
- [[sex-and-gender]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s casual misogyny is part of his larger project of treating all human relationships as occasions for aesthetic performance.
- [[past-and-conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s irritation at the letter mirrors his larger project of escaping the past, a project the novel ultimately judges impossible.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: The epigram is a perfect specimen of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s method — convert a genuine human concern (accountability) into a polished dismissal.
