## Role in the Novel

Lady Agatha is Lord Henry Wotton's aunt and a prominent figure in London society. Her dinner parties at her London residence serve as the backdrop for several important early conversations, including the gathering in Chapter 3 where Lord Henry expounds his paradoxical views before a mixed company of aristocrats and gentry. (001-071) (003-029)

She is described as a woman who takes her social duties seriously—arranging dinners, managing guests, and attempting to maintain the decorum of polite society even when Lord Henry deliberately unsettles it. She represents the conventional aristocratic world against which Lord Henry's subversive wit plays. (003-039) (003-045)

## Character and Temperament

Lady Agatha is portrayed as a well-meaning but somewhat conventional society woman. She is easily shocked by Lord Henry's pronouncements and attempts, ineffectually, to rein him in. When Lord Henry makes his deliberately provocative remarks, Lady Agatha responds with the expected moral propriety—"How dreadful!" and "Really, some one should interfere"—yet her protests only serve to heighten the effect of his wit. (003-039) (003-045) (003-060)

She serves as a foil to Lord Henry: where he is subversive and ironic, she is earnest and conventional. Her inability to counter his arguments with anything more than conventional exclamations demonstrates the shallow nature of the society morality that Lord Henry delights in puncturing. (003-078) (003-084)

## Key Appearances

### Chapter 1: The Introduction

Lady Agatha is first mentioned by Lord Henry, who reveals that he learned of Dorian Gray's existence at her house. She told him she had discovered a "wonderful young man" — Basil Hallward's subject — setting in motion the chain of events that would bring Dorian into Lord Henry's orbit. (001-071)

> "Don't look so angry, Basil. It was at my aunt, Lady Agatha's. She told me she had discovered a wonderful young man who was going to help her in the East End, and that his name was Dorian Gray. I am bound to state that she never told me he was good-looking. Women have no appreciation of good looks; at least, good women have not. She said that he was very earnest and had a beautiful nature. I at once pictured to myself a creature with spectacles and lank hair, horribly freckled, and tramping about on huge feet. I wish I had known it was your friend."
>
> (001-071)

### Chapter 3: The Dinner Party

Lady Agatha's most substantial appearance occurs at her own dinner party, where Lord Henry holds court before an audience that includes Mr. Erskine of Treadley, Sir Thomas Burdon, the Duchess of Harley, and others. Throughout the evening, she attempts to manage the conversation, deflecting Lord Henry's more outrageous statements and trying to maintain a civil tone. Her efforts are undermined by Lord Henry's relentless paradoxes and the general fascination his ideas provoke among her guests. (003-029) (003-036) (003-052)

At the end of the evening, Lady Agatha leads the ladies out of the room, leaving the gentlemen to their port—a ritual of Victorian dining that underscores her role as the guardian of social convention. (003-084)

## Narrative Function

Lady Agatha serves a structural function in the novel's early chapters. Her dinner party in Chapter 3 provides Wilde with a theatrical setting in which to stage Lord Henry's philosophy as performance. The assembled guests—each representing a different type of society figure—function as an audience, reacting to Lord Henry's epigrams with the mixture of shock, amusement, and moral unease that Wilde's own readers might have felt. (003-036) (003-060) (003-070)

Her presence also establishes the social world into which Dorian is being drawn—a world of dinner parties, titled guests, and witty conversation, far removed from the artistic solitude of Basil's studio.
