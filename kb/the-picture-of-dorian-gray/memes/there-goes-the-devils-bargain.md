## Quotation

> "There goes the devil's bargain!" she hiccoughed, in a hoarse voice. (016-039)

## Context

The opium woman — known in the den as "the mad-cat" — speaks these words in Chapter 16 as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] walks out of a squalid opium den on the London waterfront. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just tossed coins at her to stop her talking to him. She has taken the money with greedy fingers, and now, as he leaves, she laughs and names him for what he is.

The line is a revelation from the underworld. The opium woman — degraded, cynical, knowing — recognizes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] not as a gentleman slumming among the poor but as a man who has made a supernatural transaction. "Devil's bargain" is not a metaphor in her mouth; it is a description. She has seen many things in the den, and what she sees in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face is something she can only explain in the language of damnation.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response — "Curse you! Don't call me that" — confirms that she has struck truth. He does not deny the bargain; he only objects to being named.

## Analysis

### The Oracle of the Underworld

The opium woman is the novel's unlikeliest truth-teller. She is coarse, greedy, and physically repulsive — everything the novel's aesthetic values reject. And yet she sees what no one else can see. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the master of aphorism, never guesses [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s secret. But a woman in an opium den, with "sodden eyes" and "painted lips," speaks the truth in a single hiccoughed line.

Wilde inverts the Romantic convention of the wise beggar. The opium woman is not wise — she is ruined, cynical, and self-interested. But her ruin has given her a kind of second sight. She has been in the underworld long enough to recognize its prince.

### The Devil's Bargain

The phrase "devil's bargain" is the novel's own diagnosis of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s situation, spoken by a character within the fiction. Until this moment, the supernatural nature of the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has been presented through the narrator's descriptions and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s private horror. The opium woman makes it public — or at least, makes it speakable.

Her line also introduces the Faust legend explicitly into the novel's symbolic vocabulary. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has made a pact: eternal youth in ex[[transformation|change]] for his soul. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] is the contract, the evidence of the transaction. And the opium woman, who has never seen the [[the-portrait|portrait]], knows the transaction has occurred by looking at [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face — the very face the bargain was made to preserve.

### Recognition and Danger

The line is dangerous for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in two ways. First, it reveals that his secret is legible — that someone, somewhere, can read the truth in his appearance. Second, it reaches the ears of [[james-vane|James Vane]], who hears the woman's words and understands them as confirmation that he has the right man. The opium woman's revelation undoes the alibi of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s youthful face.

The line is the hinge on which the chapter's plot turns. Without it, James releases [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and walks away. With it, James knows he was right — and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s escape is only temporary.
