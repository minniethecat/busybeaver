## Quotation

> "You would sacrifice anybody, Harry, for the sake of an epigram." (018-035)
>
> — Dorian Gray, to Lord Henry Wotton, Chapter 18

## Context

This remark occurs during a conversation at [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s country house, [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the Duchess of Monmouth, and other guests have gathered. The ex[[transformation|change]] begins with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s characteristically cynical observation about the Duchess: "The basis of every scandal is an [[the-basis-of-every-scandal-is-an-immoral-certainty|immoral certainty]]" (018-034). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] protests that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is "talking scandal" about the Duchess, a woman they both claim to like.

When [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses the distinction between scandal and truth, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] delivers this rebuke. It is one of the novel's most direct critiques of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophical method — the relentless conversion of human experience into polished, amoral epigrams. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response ("[[the-world-goes-to-the-altar-of-its-own-accord|The world goes to the altar of its own accord]]" (018-036)) only proves [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s point: even when accused of moral vacancy, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] cannot resist a clever reply.

## Analysis

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s accusation cuts to the heart of the novel's critique of aestheticism. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams are acts of intellectual brilliance, but they are also acts of reduction. To "sacrifice anybody for the sake of an epigram" is to treat human beings as raw material for wit — to value the sentence over the person it describes. The word "sacrifice" has quasi-religious connotations: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams are a form of ritual offering, and the offering is always someone else.

The line is also significant because it comes from [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], not from a narrator or a moralising character. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has lived [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy more thoroughly than anyone — he has made his life into a work of art, and the cost has been enormous. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] accuses [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of sacrificing people for epigrams, he is speaking from experience: he has been both sacrifice (his soul) and sacrificer ([[sibyl-vane|Sybil Vane]], [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], Alan Campbell, and others).

There is a painful irony here. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own life is a kind of epigram — a compressed, polished, witty inversion of conventional morality. He accuses [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of doing with words what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has done with his life. The accusation is also a confession.

## Themes

- [[friendship]]: The remark exposes the hollowness of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s friendship — his wit takes precedence over loyalty, his epigrams over the people they concern.
- [[language]]: The novel is deeply concerned with the power and danger of language; this line names epigrammatic speech as a form of violence.
- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats life as material for art (epigrams); [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life has become a demonstration of how catastrophic that aestheticisation can be.
