## Summary

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], a seventeen-year-old actress performing Shakespearean heroines at a shabby Holborn theatre, takes her own life by poison after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] — to whom she is engaged — coldly rejects her following a single poor performance. Her death marks the novel's first irreversible moral catastrophe: it severs [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from his conscience, reveals [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s amoral influence, and triggers the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s first visible transformation from beauty to hideousness.

## Context

Before the event, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has [[transformation|fall]]en in love with [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] not as a person but as an artistic ideal — she embodies all the heroines of Shakespeare for him. He proposes marriage impulsively, bringing [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] to witness her perform. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], having experienced real love through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], finds she can no longer feign passion on stage:

> "Before I knew you, acting was the one reality of my life. ... You came — oh, my beautiful love! — and you freed my soul from prison. You taught me what reality really is." (007-043)

## Detailed Account

### The Failed Performance (Ch7)

On the night [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] brings [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil]] to the theatre, [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] gives a lifeless performance. Having experienced genuine emotion through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], she can no longer simulate passion for fictional characters. The audience grows restless; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil]] leave early. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], humiliated before his friends, goes backstage afterward:

> "You have killed my love. You used to stir my imagination. Now you don't even stir my curiosity. You simply produce no effect. I loved you because you were marvellous... You have thrown it all away." (007-070)

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] begs him to stay, declaring her love, but [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] leaves her weeping on the floor. He wanders the streets through the night, returning home at dawn.

### The Portrait Changes (Ch8)

The next morning, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] notices the [[the-portrait|portrait]] [[basil-hallward|Basil]] painted of him has [[transformation|change]]d — there is a touch of cruelty in the mouth:

> The expression looked different. One would have said that there was a touch of cruelty in the mouth. It was certainly strange. (008-004)

He recalls his cruelty to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] and his wish made in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio — that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] should bear the burden of his sins while he remains [[youth|young]]. He resolves to marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] after all and writes her a passionate letter. But before he can deliver it, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives with news.

### The Suicide (Ch8)

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] informs [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] is dead. She swallowed prussic acid at the theatre the previous night:

> "She was quite quiet, and went back to her dressing-room. ... About half-past six the next morning the girl was found dead. She had swallowed something by mistake, something of a very dangerous character, no doubt." (008-037)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] at first blames himself — "So I have murdered [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]" (008-044) — but [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] persuades him to view the death as a [[beauty|beautiful]], artistic tragedy rather than a moral crime. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] accepts this framing and agrees to go to the opera that evening.

## Aftermath / Consequences

1. **[[the-portrait|The Portrait]]'s [[transformation|Transformation]]**: The [[the-portrait|painting]] acquires its first mark of cruelty, confirming the supernatural bargain struck in Chapter 2.
2. **[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s Moral Descent**: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aesthetic reframing of suicide as art permanently alters [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral compass — he attends the opera the same evening.
3. **[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s Brother's Vow**: [[james-vane|James Vane]] swears revenge on "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]," setting up the revenge plot that culminates in Chapter 16–18.
4. **Coroner's Inquest**: A verdict of "death by misadventure" is returned (010-039); [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is never implicated.
