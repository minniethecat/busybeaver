## Quotation

> "I won't hear of it," laughed Lord Henry, sinking into a chair. "From a label there is no escape! I refuse the title." (017-008)

## Context

This epigram occurs during an afternoon tea at Selby Royal, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s country house. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just proposed his "plan for rechristening everything" (017-002) — a characteristically perverse project of renaming flowers, people, and ideas to restore the "faculty of giving lovely names to things" (017-004). When the Duchess of Monmouth asks what they should call [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] answers: "His name is Prince Paradox" (017-006).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response is an elegant paradox in itself: he "refuses the title" in a phrase that perfectly exemplifies the wit for which he has just been labelled. The "Prince Paradox" cannot escape his own nature — every attempt to refuse the label confirms it.

## Analysis

The epigram is a miniature demonstration of the performative contradiction that defines [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s character. He protests being called Prince Paradox by uttering a paradox: to say "I refuse the title" is to perform the very quality the title names. The label cannot be escaped because the act of escaping it is itself paradoxical.

The theatricality of the moment — "sinking into a chair" — is characteristic of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who treats conversation as performance. The chair becomes a throne he is abdicating even as he sits in it. The Duchess immediately recognizes the irony, responding: "Royalties may not abdicate" (017-009), drawing out the royal metaphor that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] initiated with "Prince."

The larger context of the chapter amplifies the epigram's resonance. The conversation that follows is a cascading exchange of paradoxes between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and the Duchess — on beauty and goodness, ugliness and virtue, England and hypocrisy — that constitutes the novel's most sustained exercise in the conversational art Wilde called "the wonderful and divine art of the paradox." The epigram thus serves as the chapter's thesis statement: in the world of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], language is both cage and performance, and the self is a series of epigrams from which there is no escape.

## Themes

- [[identity]]: The epigram raises the question of whether identity can be refused or whether labels — especially accurate ones — are inescapable.
- [[paradox]]: The statement is self-demonstrating: refusing a label by performing it.
- [[language]]: Lord Henry's world is built of words, and words, once spoken, become identities from which "there is no escape."
