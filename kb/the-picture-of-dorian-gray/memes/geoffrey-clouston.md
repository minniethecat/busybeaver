## Role in the Novel

Geoffrey Clouston, later Sir Geoffrey Clouston, is a country gentleman who appears in the narrative under two quite different circumstances. In Chapter 15, he is mentioned as one of the guests at a shooting party hosted by Lord Henry's wife—"the usual set," as Lord Henry dismissively describes them. In Chapter 18, he reappears in a far more consequential role: it is Clouston's shot that accidentally kills James Vane during a hunting expedition at Dorian's country estate. (015-064) (018-006)

This accidental killing provides the novel with its most stark example of deus ex machina—the hand of fate, or chance, intervening to remove the threat that James Vane poses to Dorian. Clouston is the instrument of this intervention, but he is entirely unconscious of his role; he never learns that the man he shot was Dorian's would-be avenger. (018-006)

## The Shooting

The scene of James Vane's death is narrated with grim economy. Dorian, who has been living in terror of James Vane's revenge, is at his country estate when a man is accidentally shot during a hunt. The victim is found to be James Vane—"the brother of the actress who had committed suicide." Clouston, the shooter, is described as "the duchess's brother," a detail that connects the fatal shot to the aristocratic world of which Dorian is a part. (018-006)

> Sir Geoffrey put his gun to his shoulder, but there was something in the animal's grace of movement that strangely charmed Dorian Gray, and he cried out at once, "Don't shoot it, Geoffrey. Let it live."... "Good heavens! I have hit a beater!" exclaimed Sir Geoffrey. "What an ass the man was to get in front of the guns!"
>
> (018-006)

The accidental nature of the killing is crucial to its thematic meaning. Dorian is not guilty of James Vane's murder in any legal sense, yet the death serves his interests perfectly. The novel thus raises the question of moral responsibility for consequences that one benefits from but does not directly cause. (018-006)

## Symbolic Significance

Geoffrey Clouston's shooting of James Vane symbolizes the casual, almost accidental way in which the aristocratic world destroys those who threaten its pleasures. Clouston fires a shot at a fleeing animal and kills a man instead; the aristocracy's recreation results, with perfect indifference, in a working-class death. The novel offers no commentary on Clouston's feelings about the incident—he simply disappears from the narrative, his role as fate's instrument complete.

> He heard Sir Geoffrey ask if the man was really dead, and the affirmative answer of the keeper. The wood seemed to him to have become suddenly alive with faces.
>
> (018-006)
