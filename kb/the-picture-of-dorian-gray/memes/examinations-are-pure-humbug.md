## Quotation

> "Examinations, sir, are pure humbug from beginning to end. If a man is a gentleman, he knows quite enough, and if he is not a gentleman, whatever he knows is bad for him." (003-006)

## Context

In Chapter 3, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is visiting his uncle [[lord-fermor|Lord Fermor]] to gather information about [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s family history (003-003). When [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] specifies that he wants "not useful information, of course; useless information," Lord Fermor replies that he can provide anything found in "an English Blue Book" — the bound volumes of government reports (003-006).

But he immediately qualifies his offer. The quality of Blue Books has [[transformation|decline]]d because "they let them in now by examination." This triggers his pronouncement on examinations — a defence of the old system of patronage and birth against the new Victorian meritocracy of competitive examination.

The passage is a small comic set-piece. Lord Fermor, like many of Wilde's aristocratic characters, believes competence is a function of class rather than training. Knowledge acquired by examination is suspect by definition; knowledge absorbed through breeding is sufficient by definition.

## Analysis

The sentence is the novel's purest expression of aristocratic anti-intellectualism — and, paradoxically, one of its most intellectually precise moments. Lord Fermor's logic is circular but internally consistent: the gentleman's knowledge is adequate because gentility is defined by adequate knowledge; the non-gentleman's knowledge is harmful because knowledge in the wrong hands corrupts.

The word "humbug" was a Victorian favourite, most famously deployed by Scrooge in Dickens's _A Christmas Carol_. Lord Fermor uses it in its precise contemporary sense: sham, pretence, fraud. Examinations pretend to measure merit but in fact measure only the ability to pass examinations — a skill Lord Fermor considers unrelated to the qualities that actually matter.

The sentence also reveals the ideological foundation of the world [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] inhabits. The novel's aestheticism — the belief that beauty is its own justification, that surface is depth — rests on an unexamined class privilege. Lord Fermor's blunt statement makes explicit what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams imply: this world of leisure, beauty, and wit is sustained by the exclusion of those who must earn their place through examination.

## Themes

- [[education]]: Lord Fermor dismisses formal education as a fraud that mistakes testable knowledge for actual worth.
- [[class]]: The sentence draws a bright line between gentleman and non-gentleman, asserting that knowledge cannot cross it.
- [[aristocracy]]: Lord Fermor embodies an older aristocratic order that predates and resists Victorian meritocracy.
