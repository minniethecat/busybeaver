## Quotation

> "Women love us for our defects. If we have enough of them, they will forgive us everything, even our intellects." (015-035)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Lady Narborough, Chapter 15

## Context

Lord Henry delivers this epigram during a dinner party at Lady Narborough's house. The conversation has turned to marriage and remarriage: Lady Narborough, a widow, has just been told by Lord Henry that she will "never marry again" because she was "far too happy" in her first marriage. When Lady Narborough protests that her late husband "wasn't perfect," Lord Henry seizes the opening with this rejoinder.

Lady Narborough, far from being offended, agrees wholeheartedly: "If we women did not love you for your defects, where would you all be? Not one of you would ever be married." (015-036) The exchange is a polished piece of society badinage — half provocation, half truth — typical of Lord Henry's dinner-table manner. He murmurs "_Fin de siècle_" under his breath as the conversation moves on (015-037).

## Analysis

The epigram is a miniature of Lord Henry's rhetorical method: take a conventional assumption, invert it, and deliver the result with maximum elegance. The conventional assumption is that women love men _despite_ their flaws. Lord Henry flips this: women love men _because_ of their flaws. The parenthetical "even our intellects" is a self-deprecating jab — Lord Henry's own intellect is his most conspicuous feature, and he knows it can be insufferable.

The deeper structure of the remark is the same paradox that runs through all of Lord Henry's thinking: what society calls virtue is really disguised vice, and what it calls vice may be the truest thing about us. Defects are authentic; perfection is suspect. This is why Lady Narborough can agree so readily — she knows that the "perfect" man would be unlovable precisely because he would be unreal.

The dinner-party setting is significant. Chapter 15 is the novel's most sustained piece of society satire, and Lord Henry's epigrams here function as both entertainment and social critique. The remark about defects is a mirror held up to the company: they are all performing, all hiding their defects behind manners, and Lord Henry is the one person willing to say so — in the most charming possible way.
