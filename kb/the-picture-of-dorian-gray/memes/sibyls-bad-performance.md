## Summary

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s bad performance is the pivotal turning point in [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s relationship with the [[youth|young]] actress.

When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] brings [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] to witness [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s genius, she delivers a shockingly lifeless performance.

Experiencing real love has made stage-acting meaningless to her. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], humiliated, coldly rejects her backstage.

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] meets [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] at a shabby theatre in the Euston Road [[the-theatre]].

Captivated by her performances as Shakespeare's heroines, he [[transformation|fall]]s deeply in love and proposes engagement within weeks.

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], who has known only the make-believe world of the stage, experiences real love for the first time.

Her brother [[james-vane]] warns her to be cautious of the mysterious "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]."

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] [[lord-henry-wotton]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] [[basil-hallward]] agree to attend a performance.

## Detailed Account

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] arrives at the theatre with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil]], full of anticipation.

The theatre is crowded, filled with gaudy playbills and a noisy audience (004-038).

When [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] appears on stage as [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]], something is immediately wrong.

> "She spoke the words as though they conveyed no meaning to her. It was not nervousness. Indeed, so far from being nervous, she was absolutely self-contained. It was simply bad art. She was a complete failure." (007-008)

The audience grows restless. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is amused. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is embarrassed for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sake.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] sits "white with pain" as the performance deteriorates scene by scene.

> "You have killed my love," he muttered, turning away his face. (007-049)

After the curtain, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] goes backstage. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] greets him joyfully.

She explains that before real love, acting was her entire reality: "the painted scenes were my world" (007-027).

> "Before I knew you, acting was the one reality of my life. I thought that it was all true. I knew nothing but shadows, and thought them real." (007-027)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is unmoved. She has "killed his love." He walks out, leaving her distraught.

## Aftermath

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] notices the first [[transformation|change]] in [[the-portrait-transforms|the portrait]]—cruelty around the mouth (007-061).

His wish from Chapter 2 [[dorian-makes-his-wish]] begins to materialize. He resolves to return to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] and make amends.

But it is too late: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] brings news of [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl's suicide]].

## Thematic Significance

The performance embodies the central Wildean paradox of [[art-and-life]].

When [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] experiences real love, she loses the ability to represent it artistically. Art and life are incompatible.

This marks [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s first significant act of cruelty—the moral turning point of the novel.

His rejection of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] for becoming "real" foreshadows his pursuit of sensation over substance.
