## Quotation

> "Sphinxes without secrets." (017-102)

## Context

A week after the shooting-party accident at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is hosting a house-party. In the conservatory, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and the Duchess of Monmouth (Gladys) engage in an extended battle of epigrams — a verbal duel of wit that runs from PN=017-016 through PN=017-109. The duchess challenges [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] to "describe us as a sex" (017-101). His reply — "Sphinxes without secrets" (017-102) — is the epigrammatic climax of their ex[[transformation|change]].

The duchess deflects the answer with a [[transformation|change]] of subject ("How long [[dorian-gray|Mr. Gray]] is!"), and moments later [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] faints at the sight of [[james-vane|James Vane]]'s face pressed against the conservatory window (017-110). The placement is deliberate: the most elegant epigram in the scene is immediately undercut by the intrusion of real danger.

## Analysis

The epigram is a masterclass in Wildean compression. In three words, it dismantles a cultural archetype. The Sphinx is the classical figure of feminine mystery — the creature who poses riddles, who guards secrets, whose inscrutability is the source of her power. To call women "Sphinxes without secrets" is to suggest that feminine mystery is a performance without content — that the riddle has no answer, the secret no substance.

The epigram works through a double movement. First, it grants women the status of Sphinxes — mysterious, powerful, fascinating. Then, with the qualifier "without secrets," it withdraws everything that made the Sphinx significant. The result is a compliment that is also an insult, a description that dignifies and diminishes in the same breath.

The context of the duchess's challenge is important. She has been matching [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] epigram for epigram throughout the scene. Her request — "Describe us as a sex" — is itself a test of his wit. His reply is both a triumph of epigrammatic form and a confession of imaginative limitation: the most brilliant man in the novel can only describe women as a negation, an absence.
