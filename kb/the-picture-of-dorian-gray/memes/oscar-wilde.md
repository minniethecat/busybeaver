## Overview

Oscar Wilde is the author of *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, his only novel, first published in *Lippincott's Monthly Magazine* in 1890 and revised for book publication in 1891.

The novel's Preface — a series of aphorisms about art, beauty, and morality — is one of the most famous statements of the aestheticist creed.

Lord Henry Wotton's epigrams throughout the novel echo Wilde's own conversational style and critical writings.

The novel's exploration of the relationship between [[art-and-life|art and life]], [[beauty]] and [[morality]], reflects Wilde's central artistic preoccupations.

## The Preface as Aesthetic Manifesto

Wilde added the Preface to the 1891 edition in response to the moral outrage that greeted the novel's serial publication.

It consists of epigrams that refuse to resolve into a single argument, challenging the reader at every turn.

The most famous of these declares (P02-004):

> There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all.
>
> (P02-004)

The Preface concludes with an assertion that has become Wilde's most quoted line on art (P02-007):

> All art is quite useless.
>
> (P02-007)

These aphorisms defend the novel against its moralistic critics while asserting that art exists for its own sake.

## Wilde and Lord Henry

Critics have long recognized elements of Wilde himself in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]].

Lord Henry's epigrammatic style — witty, paradoxical, morally unsettling — was Wilde's own conversational mode, honed in London drawing-rooms.

But the identification is not simple. Wilde remarked that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is what he thought he was.

Lord Henry is what the world thought he was, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is what he would like to be — in his own age, in other ages.

This triangulation distributes Wilde's self across multiple figures, complicating any easy equation of author and character.

## The Novel and Wilde's Life

*The Picture of Dorian Gray* occupies a pivotal position in Wilde's career. Published at the height of his fame, it prefigures the scandals that would destroy him.

The novel's themes — the [[double-life]], the concealment of vice beneath respectability, the tension between art and morality — became literal during Wilde's trials in 1895.

Prosecutors introduced the novel as evidence of Wilde's character, collapsing the distinction between artist and creation.

Wilde was sentenced to two years of hard labour and died in Paris in 1900, aged forty-six. The novel survived to become one of the most widely read works of the nineteenth century.
