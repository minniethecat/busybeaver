## Quotation

> "My God! my God!" he cried, "and I would have murdered you!" (016-058)

## Context

[[james-vane|James Vane]] cries these words in Chapter 16, in a dark archway near the London docks. He has dragged [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] under a lamp, prepared to execute the vengeance he swore eighteen years earlier — "if he ever does you any wrong, [[if-he-ever-does-you-any-wrong-i-shall-kill-him|I shall kill him]]." He has accused [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] of wrecking [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s life. He has told [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to make his peace with God.

And then he sees [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face.

The lamp reveals "all the bloom of boyhood, all the unstained purity of youth." The man [[james-vane|Jim]] was about to kill looks barely twenty — [[youth|young]]er, perhaps, than [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] was when they parted. [[james-vane|Jim]] loosens his grip, reels back, and cries out in horror at what he almost did. He has been deceived, he thinks, by a chance word in a "damned den." The revenger becomes the penitent.

The line is the novel's most concentrated expression of the power of appearances. A face — and nothing more — has transformed murder into apology, hatred into self-reproach.

## Analysis

### The Horror of Near-Murder

[[james-vane|Jim]]'s cry is not relief but horror. "My God! my God!" is the language of prayer twisted into exclamation. He is not thanking God that he stopped in time; he is recoiling from the abyss he nearly stepped into. The repetition of "my God" suggests a man grasping for a moral anchor in a world that has just shifted under his feet.

The line "and I would have murdered you" is self-accusation. [[james-vane|Jim]] does not say "I almost killed you" — he says "I would have murdered you," emphasizing the intention, the near-inevitability of the act. The conditional "would have" marks the distance between what nearly happened and what did happen, but it also confesses that the distance was terrifyingly small.

### The Face That Deceives

The dramatic irony is devastating. [[james-vane|Jim]]'s horror is based on a false premise — that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is innocent. The reader knows that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] *did* wreck [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s life, that he *is* the man [[james-vane|Jim]] seeks, that his youthful face is a supernatural lie. [[james-vane|Jim]]'s moral scruple — his refusal to kill an innocent man — is correct in principle and catastrophic in application.

Wilde is making a point about the limits of [[morality|moral judgment]]. [[james-vane|Jim]] judges by what he can see, and what he can see is false. His morality is sound; his epistemology is broken. The novel does not mock [[james-vane|Jim]] for his mistake; it mourns it.

### Vengeance Undone

The line marks the collapse of the novel's revenge plot. [[james-vane|Jim]] has spent eighteen years nursing his vow, tracking his man, preparing for this moment. And the moment evaporates — not because [[james-vane|Jim]] lacks courage, but because he has a conscience. The avenger who would not kill an innocent man is a better man than the innocent man he spares.

The line also prepares the final irony: [[james-vane|Jim]] will be killed by a hunting accident shortly after, having failed to avenge his sister. The man who recoiled from murder is himself murdered by chance. Destiny's accounts remain open.
