## Quotation

> "When poverty creeps in at the door, love flies in through the window. Our proverbs want rewriting. They were made in winter, and it is summer now." (005-059)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] delivers these words in the cramped parlour of her mother's house on the evening before her brother [[james-vane|James Vane]] departs for Australia. James, uneasy about her new suitor — the mysterious "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]" she refuses to name — has been pressing her for information. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], drunk on love, dismisses his concerns with an effusion of romantic language.

The speech in which the quote appears is a remarkable piece of character-writing. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] moves from genuine feeling — "I ... love him" — to theatrical rapture — "Fancy, [[james-vane|Jim]], to be in love and play [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]]!" — to a kind of folk-wisdom revisionism. The progression is seamless: life, theatre, and language all blur into one medium of romantic expression. The fact that she is speaking to her brother, who genuinely fears for her safety, makes the speech not merely charming but unsettling. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] lives so entirely in the world of the stage and of romance that she cannot hear the real anxiety in James's voice.

The proverb she revises is the familiar "When poverty comes in at the door, love flies out of the window." [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] inverts it: for her, poverty is immaterial because love transcends it. The claim that proverbs "were made in winter, and it is summer now" is Wilde at his most compressed — an entire philosophy of youth, art, and the rewriting of received wisdom in a single sentence.

## Analysis

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s revision of the proverb performs the central operation of her character: the transformation of the ordinary into the theatrical. The traditional proverb is a piece of worldly [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] — love cannot survive the material pressures of life. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], who has never experienced those pressures and who lives almost entirely through the roles she plays, reverses the polarity. For her, poverty is not a threat to love but a condition that makes love more dramatic: it must enter through the window, like Romeo climbing to [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]]'s balcony.

The second sentence — "They were made in winter, and it is summer now" — elevates the remark from mere romantic effusion to a philosophical claim. The old proverbs belong to a cold season, a world of scarcity and caution. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] inhabits a perpetual summer, a "very dance of blossoms in blue skies." Her conviction is that the conditions of life have [[transformation|change]]d, that the old rules no longer apply.

The novel, of course, destroys this conviction. Winter comes for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. Her revision of the proverb is not wisdom but dramatic irony: the audience knows that love *will* fly out of the window, that poverty — of [[soul|spirit]], of experience, of worldly knowledge — *will* crush her. But Wilde makes the moment of her confidence so vivid, so *summer-like*, that the irony does not feel cheap. It feels tragic.

## Themes

- [[love]]: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s love for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is absolute and unguarded — she rewrites proverbs to accommodate it rather than questioning the love itself.
- [[poverty]]: The material poverty of the Vane household is the unspoken reality beneath [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s romantic language; her revision of the proverb is an act of denial as much as hope.
- [[illusion]]: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s entire worldview is a theatrical illusion — the proverb rewritten as a love script. The novel's tragedy begins here.
- [[innocence]]: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s innocence is both her charm and her doom. She cannot imagine that love might be anything other than what she has played on stage.
