## Overview

Civilization in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* is less a state of enlightened progress than a thin membrane stretched over darker human impulses.

Wilde's London society performs civilization while harbouring the very appetites it purports to transcend. Lord Henry Wotton is the novel's chief anatomist of this paradox.

## In the Novel

Lord Henry observes that Dorian's unlined face "survived contact with Western civilizations, and loved to touch and try them all" — civilization as sensory experience, to be tasted rather than respected (011-016).

The novel juxtaposes high society's rituals with brutal acts committed by its members. Dorian attends the opera after disposing of Basil's body. He hosts gatherings near the site of James Vane's death. The machinery of civilization operates uninterrupted while participants commit murder and self-destruction.

Wilde's narrator notes that "civilized society, at least, is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating" (011-031). Basil Hallward articulates the social performance demanded: "artists have to show ourselves in society from time to time, just to remind people that we are not savages" (001-034). The irony is that the savagery is inside the drawing room.

## Thematic Significance

"The terror of society, which is the basis of morals, the terror of God, which is the secret of religion — these are the two things that govern us" (002-024).

Civilization, in Wilde's reading, is a system of terror dressed in evening clothes — a collective agreement to look away from what is happening beneath the surface. Dorian maintains his position for eighteen years not despite his crimes but because civilization protects those who wear its mask convincingly.

> Society — civilized society, at least — is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating.
>
> (011-031)
