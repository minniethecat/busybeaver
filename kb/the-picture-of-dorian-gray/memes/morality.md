## Definition

Morality in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is both denied and affirmed. The Preface declares art's independence from moral judgment — "there is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book" (P02-003) — yet the narrative arc is unmistakably moral: a [[beauty|beautiful]] [[youth|young]] man is corrupted, commits terrible sins, and is destroyed. This paradox is not inconsistent but programmatic. Wilde argues for the separation of aesthetic and moral value while demonstrating that in life, the two are inseparable.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Morality appears through its violation. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s progressive abandonment of moral constraint is charted through the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s decay. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] represents conventional morality — the friend who urges repentance and is killed for it. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] represents aesthetic amorality — the philosopher who treats moral categories as illusions and is never held accountable. The novel's moral structure is retributive for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] but curiously exempts [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], suggesting that the true target of Wilde's critique may be not immorality itself but the hypocrisy of those who preach what they do not practice.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The subject of moral judgment, whose sins are punished with death
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The amoral philosopher who escapes judgment — the novel's most unsettling character
- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: The moral center whose death represents the novel's darkest turn

## Key Passages

> There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all. (P02-003)

The Preface's most famous declaration. It separates aesthetic judgment from moral judgment — a principle the novel's plot seems to challenge.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde's treatment of morality is the novel's most complex achievement. Rather than simply rejecting morality (as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does) or simply affirming it (as conventional Victorian fiction would), Wilde holds both positions in tension. Art is amoral; life is not. The novel thus enacts a double movement: it frees art from moral obligation while insisting that human beings cannot live as if they were artworks.
