## Quotation

> "To define is to limit." (017-050)

## Context

In Chapter 17, a week after [[murder-of-basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s disappearance, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] hosts a small gathering at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], his country estate. The company includes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the [[duchess-of-monmouth|Duchess of Monmouth]], and her husband.

The conversation turns to the nature of beauty and the inadequacy of language to capture it. The Duchess challenges [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s habitual paradoxes, accusing him of valuing phrases over truth. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] responds with this five-word aphorism — the shortest and one of the most philosophically resonant lines he speaks in the entire novel.

Coming in the penultimate chapter of the main narrative (before the final hunting-party and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s confrontation with [[james-vane|James Vane]]), the aphorism reads as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s mature philosophical position. After hundreds of pages of dazzling talk, he reduces his worldview to its barest essence: all definition is diminishment.

## Analysis

"To define is to limit" operates simultaneously as epistemology, [[morality|ethics]], and self-defense. Epistemologically, it claims that naming something — assigning it to a category — necessarily strips it of whatever exceeds that category. Ethically, it provides a rationale for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s lifelong refusal to commit to any fixed position: why be "good" or "wicked" when those definitions merely limit? As self-defense, it justifies a life spent in conversation rather than action — talking about things is safer than being them.

The aphorism echoes Wilde's own aesthetic positions. In "The Critic as Artist," Wilde argues that "to define is to limit" applies to art itself: the moment a work is interpreted, it is reduced. The novel's Preface makes a similar move: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all." Both statements refuse the categories that conventional morality would impose.

Yet the novel also exposes the limits of this position. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has lived by "to define is to limit" — refusing to be defined by any act, any relationship, any consequence. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] shows what this costs. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who never acts on his philosophy, survives; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], who does, is destroyed. The aphorism is true and fatal in equal measure.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats life as an aesthetic object that loses value when categorized — beauty exists only in the undefined.
- [[sin]]: The aphorism provides intellectual cover for avoiding [[morality|moral judgment]]: if defining is limiting, then calling an act "sinful" is merely a failure of imagination.
