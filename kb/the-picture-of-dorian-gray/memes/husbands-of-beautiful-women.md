## Quotation

> "The husbands of very beautiful women belong to the criminal classes." (015-026)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] delivers this epigram at Lady Narborough's dinner party (Ch15). The guests are discussing Madame de Ferrol, a woman who has been married four times. Lady Narborough has asked what Ferrol's current (fourth) husband is like, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has described him as audacious (015-024). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] seizes the moment to deliver his characteristic paradox.

The dinner party takes place shortly after [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is maintaining his social composure while inwardly tormented—"His forehead was throbbing with maddened nerves" (015-001). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s effortless epigrams provide a facade of normalcy, though [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] can barely eat (015-008).

Lady Narborough's response—hitting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] with her fan and declaring him "extremely wicked" (015-027)—is the typical reaction his paradoxes provoke: amusement mixed with mock outrage. The epigram exemplifies [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s role as social entertainer and philosophical provocateur.

## Analysis

This epigram exemplifies [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s signature rhetorical technique: the inversion of conventional morality into an aesthetic paradox. The statement reframes jealousy and possessiveness—traits typically associated with criminals—as the natural condition of being married to beauty. [[beauty|Beauty]], in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s formulation, creates victims of those who possess it.

The line belongs to a sustained sequence of epigrams about marriage that punctuate the dinner party. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] follows it with "[[when-a-woman-marries-again|When a woman marries again]], it is because she detested her first husband. When a man marries again, it is because he adored his first wife" (015-033), and "[[man-can-be-happy-with-any-woman|A man can be happy with any woman]], as long as he does not love her" (015-045). Together they form a comprehensive, cynical philosophy of marriage.

The epigram operates on multiple levels. Superficially, it is dinner-party wit—a clever reversal designed to amuse. Philosophically, it extends [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s amoral aestheticism into the domestic sphere. Dramatically, the reference to "criminal classes" carries an irony [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] cannot appreciate: the man sitting beside him, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], is both exceptionally [[beauty|beautiful]] and a murderer. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has literally joined the criminal classes while retaining his [[beauty|beautiful]] face.

The line also subtly comments on Wilde's own social world, where beauty, marriage, and hidden criminality coexist beneath the surface of polite society—a theme the novel explores through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[duality|double life]].
