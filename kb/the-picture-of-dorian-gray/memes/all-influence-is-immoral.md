## Quotation

> "There is no such thing as a good influence, Mr. Gray. All influence is immoral — immoral from the scientific point of view." (002-022)

## Context

This declaration opens [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s famous seduction of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], standing on the dais for his [[the-portrait|portrait]], has just asked [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] whether he really has "a very [[influence|bad influence]], as [[basil-hallward|Basil]] says." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply — beginning with this sentence — is neither denial nor confession but a philosophical argument that dissolves the very category of "good influence."

The speech that follows is one of the longest in the novel and functions as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s manifesto. He argues that influencing another person is "to give him one's own soul," that the aim of life is "self-development," and that "[[every-impulse-strangled-broods-in-the-mind|the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it]]." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], who has never encountered ideas like these, is visibly transformed: "He was dimly conscious that entirely fresh influences were at work within him. Yet they seemed to him to have come really from himself." The paradox — influence that feels like self-discovery — is the mechanism by which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reshapes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s consciousness.

## Analysis

The epigram is an argument performed in the act of refuting itself. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] says influence is immoral while actively influencing [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the speech *is* the demonstration. The paradox is not accidental but essential: if influence is immoral, then the most honest thing [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] can do is admit the immorality while continuing to practice it.

The phrase "from the scientific point of view" is a characteristic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] touch. It borrows the authority of science without adopting any of its methods, using the language of objective observation to license a wholly subjective claim. The gesture recurs throughout [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speeches — he consistently frames his most extravagant paradoxes as dispassionate observations of fact.

The word "immoral" does double work. In its surface meaning, it condemns influence. But in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s inverted value system, immorality is not necessarily undesirable — it may simply be inevitable, like the processes of nature. The speech continues: "Because to influence a person is to give him one's own soul. He does not think his natural thoughts, or burn with his natural passions." The real crime, in this framework, is not immorality but inauthenticity — living someone else's life instead of one's own.

The irony of the novel is that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy of self-development produces, in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], the opposite of a developed self. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] becomes an echo chamber of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s ideas even as he believes he is discovering his own nature. The epigram is thus doubly prophetic: influence *is* immoral, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is its most effective practitioner.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: The central theme — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] argues that all influence is a form of soul-theft, yet his entire relationship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is an exercise in influence.
- [[morality]]: The epigram destabilizes conventional moral categories, insisting that what we call "good influence" is merely influence we approve of.
- [[self-development]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s stated ideal, which the novel tests and ultimately undermines — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "self-development" becomes self-destruction.
- [[individuality]]: The tension between authentic selfhood and borrowed identity runs through the entire novel.
