## Definition

Marriage is the legally recognized union of two people, which in the Victorian society of the novel functions as a central social institution.

The novel treats marriage almost exclusively through the lens of Lord Henry Wotton's epigrams, which subvert conventional Victorian pieties about the institution.

## Lord Henry's Aphorisms

Lord Henry opens the novel with a characteristic dismissal of marriage's claims to virtue:

> The one charm of marriage is that it makes a life of deception absolutely necessary for both parties.
>
> (001-017)

He later elaborates his philosophy, declaring himself "not a champion of marriage" because:

> The real drawback to marriage is that it makes one unselfish. And unselfish people are colourless. They lack individuality.
>
> (006-019)

Lord Henry dismisses marriage as "silly" (006-021) and prefers "other and more interesting bonds between men and women."

## Dorian and Sibyl

Dorian's engagement to Sibyl Vane brings marriage into the plot. He announces it with youthful enthusiasm, declaring he has "never been so happy" (006-022). But Lord Henry's influence corrupts Dorian's view:

> "What is marriage? An irrevocable vow. You mock at it for that."
>
> (006-034)

After Sibyl's death, Lord Henry advises Dorian not to "squander the gold of your days" by being "remorseful about silly marriages" (009-016).

## Mrs. Vane's Pragmatism

Sibyl's mother views marriage through a purely practical lens, calculating that a wealthy match would be "a most brilliant marriage for Sibyl" (005-037). Her pragmatic view contrasts with and underscores Lord Henry's cynical one.
