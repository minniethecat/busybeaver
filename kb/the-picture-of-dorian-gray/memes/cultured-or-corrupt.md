## Quotation

> "My dear boy," said Lord Henry, smiling, "anybody can be good in the country. There are no temptations there. That is the reason why people who live out of town are so absolutely uncivilized. Civilization is not by any means an easy thing to attain to. There are only two ways by which man can reach it. One is by being cultured, the other by being corrupt. Country people have no opportunity of being either, so they stagnate." (019-005)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This exchange occurs in Chapter 19, late in the novel, when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] encounters [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] at [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s home the morning after a chance meeting in the country. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just told [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] that he spent the previous night in "a little inn by myself" (019-004), prompting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s characteristic dismissal of rural life.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram with the smile that is his signature gesture — a mark of his detachment and pleasure in his own wit. The remark is classic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]: it inverts conventional morality (goodness as uncivilized), elevates corruption to the status of culture, and dismisses an entire way of life with a single contemptuous verb: "stagnate."

The exchange is particularly charged because [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is at this moment attempting to reform — he has spared a young village girl, Hetty Merton, from seduction. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy, which celebrates corruption as a form of sophistication, directly undermines [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fragile moral resolve.

## Analysis

The epigram works through a series of provocative equations. "Good" = "uncivilized" — a direct inversion of Victorian morality. "Civilization" = difficulty — civilization is not a natural state but an achievement. And then the decisive claim: the two paths to civilization are "culture" and "corruption," paired as if they were equivalent routes to the same destination.

The pairing is audacious. Culture — art, learning, refinement — is traditionally opposed to corruption. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] collapses the distinction, suggesting that both are forms of sophistication, both lift one above the natural innocence (or ignorance) of country life. The remark is a miniature of the novel's larger argument: that aesthetic cultivation and moral decay are not opposites but neighbors.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response — "Culture and corruption... I have known something of both" (019-006) — shows that he has internalized this worldview. He does not dispute [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s framing; he accepts it and adds his own experience as evidence.

## Themes

- [[civilization]]: The epigram offers a deliberately perverse definition of civilization as the product of temptation, not its absence.
- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] systematically inverts conventional morality, making "good" the mark of the uncivilized.
- [[sin]]: Corruption is elevated from a moral failing to a cultural achievement.
