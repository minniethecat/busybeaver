## Quotation

> Lord Henry laughed. "If a man treats life artistically, his brain is his heart," he answered, sinking into an arm-chair. (019-034)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This ex[[transformation|change]] occurs in Chapter 19, during one of the novel's final conversations between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. The immediate subject is [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s [[transformation|decline]] as a painter. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just observed that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s later work was "that curious mixture of bad [[the-portrait|painting]] and good intentions that always entitles a man to be called a representative British artist" (019-029) — noting that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] "ceased to be a great artist" when he and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] ceased to be friends.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]], visibly uncomfortable with the subject of [[basil-hallward|Basil]], deflects by quoting two lines of verse: "'Like the [[the-portrait|painting]] of a sorrow, a face without a heart.' Yes: that is what it was like." [019-031–033]

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response — "If a man treats life artistically, his brain is his heart" — is a rejoinder to the very distinction [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s quotation draws. In the quoted verse, a "face without a heart" is a negative: appearance without feeling. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dissolves the binary: for the man who treats [[life-the-first-of-arts|life as art]], thought *is* feeling; intellect *is* passion.

## Analysis

The aphorism "If a man treats life artistically, his brain is his heart" is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most condensed expressions of the aestheticist worldview. It collapses two traditional oppositions — brain versus heart, intellect versus emotion — into a single identity, but only on the condition that life itself be approached as an aesthetic project.

The key word is "artistically." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not claim that the brain is the heart for everyone; only for the man who "treats life artistically" — that is, who approaches existence as a work of art to be shaped, not a moral problem to be solved. The aphorism is both a description and a prescription: it describes the aesthetic temperament while simultaneously holding it up as an ideal.

The physical gesture that accompanies the line — "sinking into an arm-chair" — is characteristic. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers his most radical ideas from a position of maximum physical ease. The arm-chair is his pulpit; the aphorism, his sermon. The contrast between the intellectual audacity of the claim and the languid comfort of the posture is itself a performance of the aesthetic life.

The line also functions as an epitaph for [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], who is the unspoken third presence in this conversation. [[basil-hallward|Basil]], in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s implicit judgment, failed precisely because he could not treat life artistically — he invested too much of his heart in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], and his art suffered for it. The brain-is-heart formula is a standard that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] could not meet.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The aphorism is a direct formulation of the relationship between art and life that the novel explores throughout.
- [[aesthetics]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s claim that the aesthetic approach to life unifies intellect and emotion is the novel's most succinct statement of the aestheticist creed.
- [[basil-hallward]]: The line serves as an oblique judgment on [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s artistic and personal failure.
- [[intellect]]: The elevation of intellect (brain) to the status of emotion (heart) inverts the Romantic privileging of feeling over thought.
