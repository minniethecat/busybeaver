## Quotation

> "You will never marry again, Lady Narborough. You were far too happy. When a woman marries again, it is because she detested her first husband. When a man marries again, it is because he adored his first wife. Women try their luck; men risk theirs." (015-033)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at Lady Narborough's dinner party. She has jested that she might "marry again so as to be in the fashion" (015-032).

His reply begins with a compliment — she was "far too happy" to remarry. Then he launches into the aphorism: gallant opening, then the generalisation.

Lady Narborough protests: "Narborough wasn't perfect" (015-034). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] deepens the paradox: "Women love us for our defects" (015-035). She calls him "an admirable tonic" (015-046).

## Analysis

The epigram uses parallel structure: woman/man, detested/adored, luck/risk. The form suggests inevitability, as if remarriage could be reduced to a formula.

The gendered logic is misogynistic but self-aware. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] presents a double standard as witty observation. He is "analysing," not judging.

The final line inverts convention. Women remarry from dissatisfaction; men remarry from nostalgia. Both are gamblers.
