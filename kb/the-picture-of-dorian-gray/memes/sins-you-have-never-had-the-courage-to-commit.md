## Quotation

> "You will always like me, Dorian. I represent to you all the sins you have never had the courage to commit." (006-055)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 6

## Context

This epigram closes an extraordinary passage of self-revelation — or self-display — at the Bristol dinner. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] are dining before going to the theatre to see [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] perform. The conversation has ranged over pleasure, morality, individualism, and marriage. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just declared, "Harry, you are dreadful! I don't know why I like you so much" (006-054).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is at once a piece of flattery, a confession, and a diagnosis. He understands his role in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life perfectly: he is the tempter who does not tempt directly but who makes temptation thinkable. He represents "all the sins" — not because he commits them, but because he talks about them with such charm that they cease to seem sinful.

The word "courage" is the epigram's sharpest point. It reframes cowardice as the only barrier to sin, and sin itself as a form of bravery. This inversion of conventional morality is the essence of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence.

## Analysis

The epigram crystallises the mechanism of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence over [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. He does not command or instruct; he "represents." The word is chosen with precision — it suggests performance, embodiment, symbol. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] becomes a kind of living portrait of temptation, just as [[the-portrait|the painted portrait]] will become a record of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sins.

The epigram also contains a crucial irony that the novel will unfold at length. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] represents sins he has never committed — [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has already observed that he "never does a wrong thing" (001-018). But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], by actually committing those sins, will move beyond representation into reality. The vicarious relationship breaks down; the experiment escapes the laboratory.

The word "courage" does particularly insidious work. By casting sin as courage, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] makes virtue seem timid. The rhetorical move is characteristic: he never argues against morality; he simply makes it sound boring.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: The epigram redefines sin as a form of experience available to the courageous.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s power over [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is founded on this exact dynamic — the vicarious enjoyment of transgression.
- [[temptation]]: The remark shows temptation operating not through command but through charm.
- [[morality]]: The epigram inverts the conventional moral order, making courage — rather than conscience — the arbiter of action.
