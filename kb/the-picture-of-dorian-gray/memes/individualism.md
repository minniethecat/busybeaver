## Definition

Individualism in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is the ethical and aesthetic doctrine that self-realization takes priority over social obligation. Articulated primarily by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], it holds that "individualism has really the higher aim" (006-040) — higher, that is, than the altruistic morality that subordinates individual flourishing to collective good. This is not mere selfishness but a philosophical position: the cultivation of personality as the highest human achievement.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Individualism is the ideological engine of the novel. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] preaches it as a liberation from conventional morality; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] practices it as license for self-indulgence. The gap between theory and practice is the novel's great cautionary structure. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks of individualism with "that pride of individualism that is half the fascination of sin" (011-025) — treating it as an aesthetic stance rather than a lived ethic. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], by contrast, lives individualism to its logical extreme, pursuing every impulse and desire until his [[the-portrait|portrait]] registers the accumulated cost. The novel thus interrogates whether individualism can function as anything other than self-destruction when severed from moral constraint.

- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The philosopher of individualism who never pays its price

## Key Passages

> Individualism has really the higher aim. Modern morality consists in accepting the standard of one's age. I consider that for any man of culture to accept the standard of his age is a form of the grossest immorality.

(006-040)

Lord Henry's manifesto: individualism as moral independence. The irony is that he uses this argument to gain influence over Dorian — substituting his own standard for society's rather than liberating Dorian to find his own.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde's treatment of individualism is characteristically double. The novel's Preface and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams make the case for individual self-cultivation against the tyranny of conventional morality — a position Wilde himself advanced in his essays. Yet the narrative punishes individualism's logical endpoint. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s complete self-absorption leads not to self-realization but to self-annihilation. Wilde thus distinguishes between a noble individualism (the artist's independence from public taste) and a degraded one (the pursuit of sensation without conscience).
