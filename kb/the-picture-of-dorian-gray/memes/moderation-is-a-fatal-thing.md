## Quotation

> "Pray don't, Lady Ruxton," said Lord Henry. "Moderation is a fatal thing. Enough is as bad as a meal. More than enough is as good as a feast." (015-050)

## Context

The epigram is delivered at [[lady-narborough|Lady Narborough]]'s dinner party in Chapter 15, one of the novel's great set-piece scenes of aristocratic conversation. The guests have just finished dinner, and Lady Ruxton — responding to Lady Narborough's apology for not noticing her unfinished cigarette — declares her intention to "limit myself." This small resolution to moderate her smoking draws from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] an immediate, theatrical objection.

The ex[[transformation|change]] unfolds within a rapid sequence of epigrams. Moments earlier, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has declared that he likes "men who have a future and women who have a past" (015-047), provoking Lady Narborough's laughing reply about the risk of a "[[i-like-men-who-have-a-future-and-women-who-have-a-past|petticoat party]]." The conversation then pivots on Lady Ruxton's vow of self-restraint — the very kind of middle-class [[morality|virtue]] [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] cannot resist dismantling.

The setting is significant. Lady Narborough's table is the novel's most sustained [[the-portrait|portrait]] of the world [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] inhabits and dominates — a world where conversation is competitive performance, where every statement must be witty or be forgotten. The epigram's success is measured by Lady Ruxton's response: she does not argue but invites him to "explain that to me some afternoon" (015-051), acknowledging that his paradox has done its work — it has made the conventional position (moderation is good) suddenly feel conventional.

## Analysis

The epigram is structured as a classic Wildean escalation. The first sentence is the provocation — "Moderation is a fatal thing" — which inverts the entire edifice of Victorian moral common sense, where moderation was the cardinal [[morality|virtue]]. The second sentence intensifies: not only is moderation not good, but its opposite ("enough") is actively bad — "as bad as a meal." The third sentence completes the arc by elevating excess — "More than enough is as good as a feast" — into a positive good.

The rhetorical movement matters: the epigram does not merely deny the value of moderation; it builds an alternative hierarchy in which abundance, excess, and overflow are the true [[morality|virtue]]s. "Enough" is pejorative — it suggests mere sufficiency, the absence of desire. "A feast," by contrast, suggests celebration, abundance, the aesthetic life. The structural parallel between "a meal" and "a feast" makes the argument through connotation rather than logic: we feel that "feast" is better than "meal" before we think about whether the analogy is valid.

The epigram also dramatizes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s relationship to authority. Lady Ruxton is not a friend or disciple; she is a social equal, and her resolution to limit her smoking is the kind of thing a sensible person says. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response treats her resolution as though it were a philosophical position requiring refutation. He grants her remark the dignity of intellectual engagement precisely so he can demolish it — a technique that turns social courtesy into a weapon of aesthetic warfare.

Within the novel's larger architecture, the epigram belongs to a category of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s sayings that aestheticize appetite. It looks back to his earlier declaration that "[[every-impulse-strangled-broods-in-the-mind|the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it]]" (002-029) and forward to his insistence throughout Ch17–19 that pleasure, not [[morality|virtue]], is the object of life. The word "fatal" carries an undertone that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] himself does not hear: in a novel where excess does prove literally fatal — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] dies pursuing it — the epigram reads as both truth and prophecy.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: The epigram enacts the novel's central hedonistic argument — that pleasure is its own justification and that the attempt to limit it is self-defeating.
- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats conversation as an art form in which the epigram is the unit of composition; life, in his scheme, should imitate art by valuing the [[beauty|beautiful]] over the prudent.
- [[temptation]]: The epigram's structure — deny moderation, affirm excess — is the rhetorical form of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s broader philosophy of temptation that shapes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s entire trajectory.
