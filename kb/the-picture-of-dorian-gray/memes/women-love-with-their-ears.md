## Quotation

> "We women, as some one says, love with our ears, just as you men love with your eyes, if you ever love at all." (017-064)

## Context

Chapter 17 takes place at [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country house, where a house-party of twelve has gathered for tea in the conservatory. The Duchess of Monmouth — called Gladys by her intimates — presides over the tea ser[[sin|vice]], her "white hands moving daintily among the cups" while she ex[[transformation|change]]s whispered remarks with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] (017-001). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] lounges nearby in "a silk-draped wicker chair," and the scene is saturated with the languid elegance of aristocratic sociability.

The line arrives in the middle of a cascade of Wildean epigrams. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just declared, "Every effect that one produces gives one an enemy. To be popular one must [[to-be-popular-one-must-be-a-mediocrity|be a mediocrity]]" (017-063). The Duchess counters by reversing the terms: mediocrity may be the price of male popularity, but "women rule the world" (017-064), and women — she insists — "can't bear mediocrities" (017-064). Her epigram about love arriving through the ears is both a diagnosis of female psychology and a playful jab at male vanity: the closing clause, "if you ever love at all," undermines the whole proposition by suggesting that men may be incapable of the very feeling under discussion.

## Analysis

The Duchess's line belongs to the novel's running investigation of the relationship between the senses and the passions. Where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy often treats aesthetic experience as primarily visual — the [[the-portrait|portrait]], the beauty of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face, the "visible symbol" of the soul's degradation — the Duchess proposes an alternative sensorium: women, she claims, are moved by the ear, by language, by what is said. This is more than a drawing-room pleasantry. It encodes a theory of gendered desire in which men are seduced by appearance and women by discourse — by the "charming things" that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] himself specializes in saying.

The conditional "if you ever love at all" does the real work of the sentence. By casting doubt on the very existence of male love, the Duchess turns what sounds like a confession of feminine weakness — women are susceptible to flattery, to sweet words — into an indictment of masculine emotional poverty. The rhetorical structure is an elegant example of Wilde's technique: an apparent generalization about women becomes, on its second beat, a sharper observation about men. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s murmured response — "It seems to me that we never do anything else" (017-064) — only deepens the ambiguity, since the "it" could refer to loving, or to something far more superficial.

The episode also dramatizes the social function of the epigram in Wilde's world. Serious topics — love, power, gender — are handled not through earnest debate but through the competitive ex[[transformation|change]] of polished sentences. The Duchess and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] are engaged in a kind of conversational duel, each epigram parrying the last. The content matters less than the form; the pleasure lies in the inversion itself.

## Themes

- [[love]]: The Duchess proposes a gendered phenomenology of love — women love through the ear, men through the eye — and simultaneously questions whether men love at all.
- [[society]]: The epigram is performed for a drawing-room audience; its wit is inseparable from its social context.
- [[gender]]: The line diagnoses a fundamental asymmetry between masculine and feminine desire, one that runs throughout the novel.
- [[art-and-life]]: The epigram's form — polished, inverted, theatrical — is itself an argument for the aestheticization of conversation.
