## Quotation

> "To see him is to worship him; to know him is to trust him." (005-065)
>
> — Sibyl Vane, to her brother James Vane, Chapter 5

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] delivers this line to James immediately after her declaration that she "shudders at the thought of being free" (005-063). James has insisted "I want you to beware of him" (005-064), and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s response is this perfectly balanced aphorism, which unites two senses — sight and knowledge — with two absolute responses — worship and trust.

The line's formal perfection (parallel clauses, ascending intensity) reveals [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s theatrical sensibility: she speaks in the cadences of the stage even in private conversation. But the content is deadly serious. She offers James a theory of knowledge in which beauty guarantees [[morality|virtue]]: to see [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty is to worship it, and to know him is therefore to trust him. The novel will dismantle every term of this equation.

## Analysis

The aphorism's structure is chiastic: sight → worship, knowledge → trust. The first pair (sight/worship) belongs to the aesthetic realm — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty compels [[worship|adoration]]. The second pair (knowledge/trust) belongs to the moral realm — familiarity with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] should inspire confidence in his character. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] assumes that the two realms are continuous, that beauty implies goodness and familiarity implies trustworthiness. The novel's entire plot exists to prove this assumption catastrophically wrong.

"[[worship|Worship]]" is a strong word, drawn from religious vocabulary. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] frames her love for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as a form of [[worship|devotion]], with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as the object of a cult. This anticipates [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s own language of worship — "[[we-are-both-punished|I worshipped you too much]]" (013-028) — and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s secularised gospel of beauty. The novel repeatedly draws love into the sphere of the sacred, only to expose the dangers of worshipping a human being.

The word "trust" is the most painful in the aphorism. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] trusts [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] absolutely, and he will betray that trust within days. The gap between "to know him" (which suggests intimacy, familiarity, understanding) and what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] actually is (a man whose surface conceals moral rot) is the gap the entire novel exists to explore.

## Themes

- [[love]] — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s aphorism defines love as a fusion of aesthetic admiration (worship) and moral faith (trust).
- [[worship]] — The language of religious [[worship|devotion]] applied to human love recurs throughout the novel, always with tragic consequences.
- [[beauty]] — The assumption that visible beauty implies invisible [[morality|virtue]] is the novel's founding [[transformation|fall]]acy.
- [[idealism]] — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s idealism about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] prefigures and mirrors [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own idealism about youth and beauty.
