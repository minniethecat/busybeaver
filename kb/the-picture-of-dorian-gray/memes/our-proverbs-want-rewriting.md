## Quotation

> "When poverty creeps in at the door, love flies in through the window. Our proverbs want rewriting. They were made in winter, and it is summer now; spring-time for me, I think, a very dance of blossoms in blue skies." (005-059)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] speaks these words in Chapter 5, during her farewell scene with her brother [[james-vane|James Vane]] before his departure for Australia. James has been warning her about [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] — the mysterious gentleman who has been courting her — but [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is incandescent with love and will hear none of it.

She is to play [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]] that night at [[the-theatre]], and her lover will be in the audience. The speech is a cascade of romantic conviction: love transforms everything, overturns every piece of received wisdom, makes the world new. She revises the proverb — "When poverty creeps in at the door, [[when-poverty-creeps-in-at-the-door|love flies in through the window]]" — and then dismisses proverbs entirely as obsolete, made in a colder season.

The passage is the fullest expression of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s worldview: art, love, and youth fused into a single ecstatic vision. It is also — and the reader senses this even on first reading — a premonition of tragedy. Summer does not last.

## Analysis

### The Revision of Proverbs

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s revision of the proverb is audacious. The original saying — "When poverty comes in at the door, love flies out the window" — is a piece of worldly [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]], a warning that love cannot survive material hardship. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] reverses it: poverty lets love *in*. Her version insists that love is not diminished by adverse circumstances but intensified by them — that love is the compensation for poverty, not its victim.

This is romanticism in its purest form: the belief that feeling transforms reality. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is, in this moment, the most articulate spokesperson for the novel's central argument about [[art-and-life|art and life]] — that art (and love, which is art's emotional twin) is "[[i-love-acting-more-real-than-life|more real than life]]."

### Winter and Summer

The seasonal metaphor — proverbs were made in winter, but it is summer now — is [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s theory of everything. Received wisdom belongs to a colder, older world; love creates a new climate. The "dance of blossoms in blue skies" is the language of pastoral romance, of Shakespearean comedy. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is speaking in the register of the part she is about to play.

But the novel's irony is that winter does come. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s summer lasts exactly as long as her belief in [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] — which is to say, less than twenty-four hours after this speech. By the end of Chapter 7, she is dead, and the "blue skies" have become the gray London morning in which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reads of her suicide.

### The Price of Rewriting

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s desire to rewrite proverbs is a desire to rewrite the rules of the world — and the novel punishes that desire. The proverbs [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] dismisses contain the wisdom she needs: that gentlemen do not marry actresses, that love built on illusion cannot survive disillusionment, that summer turns to winter.

Her tragedy is not that she is wrong about love's power, but that the world does not agree to be rewritten. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigrams survive because they are cynical; [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s because they are [[beauty|beautiful]]. But beauty, in Wilde's novel, does not guarantee truth.

## Themes

- **[[youth]]** — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s speech is youth incarnate: the conviction that one's own experience invalidates all previous wisdom.
- **[[love]]** — The chapter's central theme; [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s love is absolute, transformative, and fatally vulnerable.
- **[[beauty]]** — The aesthetic of the speech — blossoms, blue skies, summer — is beauty as [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] understands it: something that remakes the world.
- **[[transformation]]** — Love as the agent of transformation; the belief that emotion can rewrite reality.
- **[[art-and-life]]** — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s fusion of art and life: she speaks like a character in a play because, in her mind, life *is* a play.
