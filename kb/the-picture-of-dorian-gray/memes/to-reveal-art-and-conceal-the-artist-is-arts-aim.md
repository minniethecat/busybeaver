## Quotation

> "The artist is the creator of beautiful things. To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim. The critic is he who can translate into another manner or a new material his impression of beautiful things." (P02-001)
>
> — Oscar Wilde, The Preface (P02-001)

## Context

This is the opening sentence of the Preface to *The Picture of Dorian Gray*.

Wilde added the Preface in 1891 in response to the moral outrage that greeted the novel's first publication in *Lippincott's Monthly Magazine* (1890).

The Preface is a series of aphorisms designed to pre-empt and disarm moralistic criticism by declaring art's absolute independence from ethics.

The declaration is the aestheticist credo in miniature. The artist's business is making beautiful things — nothing more.

What the artist feels, believes, or intends is irrelevant to the work's value.

The critic's role is not to judge but to translate: to take one's impression of beauty and render it in a new form.

Both artist and critic are engaged in the same project — the production and circulation of beauty — and neither has any obligation to morality.

In the context of the novel, the line also functions as a warning. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s tragedy is precisely that he fails to conceal himself.

He puts "too much of [himself] into" the portrait (002-101), and that self-exposure leads to his death.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] becomes the living refutation of the aestheticist ideal: his life becomes a work of art that reveals everything about the artist (in this case, his own soul).

## Analysis

The aphorism operates on multiple levels. Most straightforwardly, it is a statement of aesthetic independence: art should be judged by its own standards, not by the life of its maker.

Wilde, who faced constant moral scrutiny of his work (and his life), is drawing a bright line between the two.

But the line is also a provocation. By declaring that art's aim is to *conceal* the artist, Wilde is asserting that sincerity, confession, and self-expression are irrelevant to artistic merit.

The reigning values of nineteenth-century literature — sincerity and self-expression — are dismissed as beside the point.

Art is artifice; the artist who reveals himself in his work has failed as an artist.

The third clause — "The critic is he who can translate into another manner or a new material his impression of beautiful things" — elevates the critic to the status of co-creator.

This is a radical gesture: criticism is not secondary or parasitic; it is another form of art-making.

The novel itself enacts this idea, as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] functions as a kind of critic-artist, "translating" [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] into a new material — his own corrupted soul.
