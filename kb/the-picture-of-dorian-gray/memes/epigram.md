## Overview

The epigram is the characteristic rhetorical form of *The Picture of Dorian Gray*.

Lord Henry Wotton speaks almost exclusively in epigrams — pithy, paradoxical pronouncements that invert conventional wisdom.

These aphorisms unsettle moral certainties through their wit and formal elegance.

Examples include "The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" (002-015) and "To be good is to be in harmony with oneself" (008-010).

Another instance: "There is only one thing in the world worse than being talked about, and that is not being talked about" (001-006).

The novel's Preface consists entirely of epigrams about art, embodying the aestheticist privileging of style over substance.

## Lord Henry's Epigrams

Lord Henry's epigrams are the engine of the novel's philosophical drama. They constitute his mode of thought and his instrument of [[influence]].

His epigrams share a characteristic structure: they take a received moral truth and invert it, replacing the expected second term with a destabilizing alternative.

"The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" reverses the Victorian maxim of self-restraint.

"There is no such thing as a good influence. All influence is immoral" (002-022) transforms influence from virtue to vice.

Lord Henry's listeners — [[basil-hallward|Basil]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — recognize the logical trickery but cannot resist its charm.

The novel describes Lord Henry's rhetorical method in vivid terms (003-081):

> He played with the idea, and grew wilful; tossed it into the air and transformed it; let it escape and recaptured it; made it iridescent with fancy and winged it with paradox.
>
> (003-081)

## The Preface as Epigram Collection

Oscar Wilde prefaced the novel with a series of epigrams about art that function as both aesthetic manifesto and defensive provocation.

These aphorisms — "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book," "All art is quite useless" — have become among the most quoted lines in Wilde's corpus.

The Preface epigrams share the same paradoxical structure as Lord Henry's: they challenge the reader's assumptions about art and morality.

They refuse to resolve the contradictions they expose, forcing the reader to sit with the tension between surface and depth.

By placing his own voice in epigrammatic form, Wilde aligns himself with Lord Henry even as the narrative complicates the epigrammatic worldview.

> There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all.
>
> (P02-003)

This creates a productive tension: the form invites delighted assent, while the story questions what that assent costs.

## Epigram as Moral Subversion

The epigram serves a subversive function in the novel. By compressing a complex moral reversal into a memorable phrase, it makes the unacceptable sound inevitable.

Lord Henry's epigrams short-circuit the reader's moral reasoning, substituting wit for argument and charm for conviction.

This is precisely how [[influence]] operates in the novel: not through reasoned persuasion but through aesthetic seduction.

The epigram is thus the formal counterpart to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]]: a beautiful surface that conceals — or reveals — the corruption beneath.
