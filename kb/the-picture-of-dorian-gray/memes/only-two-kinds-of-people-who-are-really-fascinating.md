## Quotation

> "There are only two kinds of people who are really fascinating—people who know absolutely everything, and people who know absolutely nothing." (007-036)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 7

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at the theatre, moments after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s disastrous performance as Juliet. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is devastated — the woman he loved as an artist has revealed herself to be a mediocre actress, and his infatuation has collapsed. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], ever the detached observer, dismisses the significance of the evening: "It is not good for one's morals to see bad acting. Besides, I don't suppose you will want your wife to act, so what does it matter if she plays Juliet like a wooden doll?" (007-035).

The epigram about "two kinds of people" is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s attempt to console [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] by reframing his disappointment as a trivial matter. It is preceded by an equally characteristic remark: "She is very lovely, and if she knows as little about life as she does about acting, she will be a delightful experience" (007-036). The paragraph concludes with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] urging [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to come to the club, smoke cigarettes, and "drink to the beauty of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]" — an invitation to treat the crisis as a social occasion.

## Analysis

This epigram is quintessential [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]: elegant, symmetrical, and intellectually hollow. The binary of "people who know absolutely everything" and "people who know absolutely nothing" is a deliberate absurdity — no one knows "absolutely everything" — but its rhetorical force masks its emptiness. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is not describing reality; he is performing wit.

The epigram functions as a sedative. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is in genuine emotional distress, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] offers him a polished sentence in place of sympathy. This is the method [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will deploy throughout the novel: convert pain into paradox, suffering into epigram, so that neither speaker nor listener has to feel anything. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will learn this lesson too well.

There is also a submerged irony. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who prides himself on knowing "everything" worth knowing about society and human nature, is himself the kind of person who "knows absolutely nothing" about love, sacrifice, or moral consequence. His epigrams are a defence against the very emotions he claims to analyse. By reducing the world to wit, he ensures he never has to live in it.

## Themes

- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is actively shaping [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sensibility — teaching him to respond to emotional crisis with aesthetic detachment rather than feeling.
- [[youth]]: The epigram is embedded in a passage where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] also tells [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], "The secret of remaining young is never to have an emotion that is unbecoming" (007-036) — linking the cult of youth to the suppression of genuine feeling.
- [[pleasure]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] urges [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to exchange grief for pleasure — the club, cigarettes, wine — a pattern that will define [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s entire subsequent [[transformation]].
