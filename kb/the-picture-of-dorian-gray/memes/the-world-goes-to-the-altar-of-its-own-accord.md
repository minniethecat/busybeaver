## Quotation

> "The world goes to the altar of its own accord." (018-036)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], walking through the grounds of [[selby-royal|Selby Royal]] after the shooting accident (Chapter 18). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just accused [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] of being willing to "sacrifice anybody, [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]], for the sake of an epigram" (018-035). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is this lapidary aphorism, meaning: the world eagerly rushes to its own sacrifice — scandal and ruin are self-inflicted, and no one is truly an innocent victim (018-036). The line is delivered with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s characteristic detachment from human suffering.

## Analysis

This is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most concentrated epigrams — six words that encapsulate his entire philosophy. The metaphor of "the altar" transforms scandal into a form of ritual sacrifice, a quasi-religious ceremony in which society willingly participates. The phrase "of its own accord" is the key: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] absolves himself (and by extension all gossips and cynics) of responsibility. If the world chooses sacrifice, the observer merely watches. The remark is also an elegant deflection of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s criticism — rather than dispute the charge that he would "sacrifice anybody," [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] aestheticizes sacrifice itself. The epigram echoes the novel's broader concern with the relationship between art and morality: for [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the world is a spectacle to be observed and epigrammatized, not a field of moral action (018-025).
