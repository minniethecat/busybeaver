## Quotation

> "But what world says that? It can only be the next world. This world and I are on excellent terms." (015-028)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at Lady Narborough's dinner party, during a lively ex[[transformation|change]] about his reputation. Lady Narborough has just struck him with her fan and declared, "[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], I am not at all surprised that the world says that you are extremely wicked" (015-027).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is a two-step manoeuvre. First, he questions the premise: "But what world says that?" The question implies that "the world" is not a monolith but a set of competing social circles. Second, he draws a distinction between "this world" — the glittering dinner parties, witty conversation, and cultivated pleasures of [[high-society|London society]] — and "the next world" — the realm of divine judgment. He is on excellent terms with the former; the latter alone finds him wicked.

Lady Narborough protests, "Everybody I know says you are very wicked" (015-029), to which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replies with another paradox: "It is perfectly monstrous the way people go about nowadays saying things against one behind one's back that are absolutely and entirely true" (015-030).

## Analysis

The epigram is a masterpiece of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rhetorical method: refuse the moral framework, replace it with an aesthetic one, and deliver the substitution as wit. By reducing "wickedness" to a matter of which "world" is doing the judging, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] sidesteps the question of morality entirely. The word "excellent" — a term of social approval — replaces "good" — a term of moral approval.

The distinction between this world and the next runs throughout the novel. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s tragedy is precisely that he attempts to live entirely in "this world" — the world of sensation, beauty, and pleasure — while pretending the next does not exist. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], who never takes his own epigrams as instructions for living, survives. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], who enacts them, does not.

The follow-up line — "things against one behind one's back that are absolutely and entirely true" — deepens the paradox. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not deny the charge of wickedness; he merely objects to the venue in which it is discussed. Truth, for him, is not a defence; wit is.
