## Quotation

> "The aim of life is self-development. To realize one's nature perfectly — that is what each of us is here for. People are afraid of themselves, nowadays. They have forgotten the highest of all duties, the duty that one owes to one's self." (002-024)

## Context

Chapter 2 takes place in [[basils-studio|Basil Hallward's studio]] and its adjoining garden. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just finished painting the portrait of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been introduced to the young man. While [[basil-hallward|Basil]] works inside, the two sit in the garden, and [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers an extended monologue — a seduction by ideas — that will change the course of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s life.

The speech from which this passage is taken is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s initial resistance. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been arguing against the very idea of influence — "to influence a person is to give him one's own soul" (002-023) — but paradoxically, this argument against influence is itself the most influential thing anyone has ever said to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].

## Analysis

The aphorism recasts the purpose of life from moral obligation to aesthetic self-realization. "Self-development" is not virtue, not service, not salvation — it is the cultivation of one's own nature to its fullest expression. The phrase "to realize one's nature perfectly" borrows the language of artistic creation: one's life is the work, and the self is the material.

The charge that "people are afraid of themselves, nowadays" is the pivot. It transforms what might sound like a call to self-indulgence into a call to courage. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] frames the failure to pursue self-development not as a moral failing but as cowardice — a failure of nerve. The "highest of all duties" is not to God or to others but to oneself, and the neglect of this duty is the source of the soul's starvation: "their own souls starve, and are naked" (002-025).

The passage is the philosophical core of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s seduction of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. What sounds like liberation is also a trap: once [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] accepts that self-development is the highest aim, he has no moral vocabulary with which to judge any action that serves that aim. The portrait will record the cost.

## Themes

- [[self-development]]: The aphorism is the novel's most direct statement of its hedonistic philosophy.
- [[individuality]]: The self is not to be tamed or transcended but realised — a radical departure from Victorian morality.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: This speech is the purest expression of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s gospel, and its effect on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is immediate and irreversible (showcase).
- [[influence]]: The paradox of the speech is that it is an argument against influence that exerts the maximum possible influence on its listener.
