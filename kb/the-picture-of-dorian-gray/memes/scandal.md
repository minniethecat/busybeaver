## Definition

Scandal in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is the mechanism by which society enforces its moral code while pretending to be above it. It is simultaneously condemned and consumed: characters profess disdain for scandal while eagerly trading in its currency. Lord Henry articulates the paradox most precisely — "the basis of every scandal is an immoral certainty" (018-034) — capturing how scandal depends on the gap between public propriety and private truth.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Scandal surrounds [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] from the moment his reputation begins to decay. After his rupture with society, "whispered scandals only increased in the eyes of many his strange and dangerous charm" (011-031). The novel charts scandal's double nature: it alienates [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] from respectable company while perversely enhancing his mystique. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats scandal as an aesthetic pleasure — "I love scandals about other people" (012-018) — while [[lady-narborough|Lady Narborough]] uses it as dinner-table entertainment, chiding her guests for lingering "so long over your politics and scandal" (015-052). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s advice to "never make one's début with a scandal. One should reserve that to give an interest to one's old age" (008-039) proves tragically ironic: scandal does not wait for old age but consumes him entirely.

## Key Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: The novel's central object of scandal, whose mysterious life generates endless speculation
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The connoisseur of scandal who treats moral transgression as intellectual entertainment
- **[[lady-narborough|Lady Narborough]]**: The society hostess whose dinner-table conversation revolves around scandal

Lord Henry's aphorism reveals scandal's structural logic: it requires actual transgression to function. Mere rumour is insufficient.

> I love scandals about other people, but scandals about myself don't interest me. They have not got the charm of novelty.
>
> (012-018)

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats scandal as both theme and method. The novel itself was calculated to provoke scandal — and did, being cited at Wilde's own trial. Within the narrative, scandal functions as a secular version of divine judgment: a social mechanism that punishes transgression without requiring belief in transcendent morality. Yet Wilde subverts this by showing that scandal, unlike conscience, can be survived — and even enjoyed — by those sufficiently detached from conventional morality, like [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]].
