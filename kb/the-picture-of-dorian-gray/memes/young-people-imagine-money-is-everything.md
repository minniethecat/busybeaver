## Quotation

> "Young people, nowadays, imagine that money is everything." (003-004)

## Context

In Chapter 3, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] visits his uncle [[lord-fermor|Lord Fermor]] at the Albany to inquire about [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s parentage (003-003). The old gentleman, "a genial if somewhat rough-mannered old bachelor," is smoking a cheroot and grumbling over _The Times_ when [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives (003-002).

When [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] says he wants to "get something out of you," Lord Fermor assumes — with the reflex of a wealthy old man accustomed to being petitioned by [[youth|young]]er relatives — that the request is for money (003-004). His reply is a sentence of avuncular [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]]: [[youth|young]] people think money is everything.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s murmured reply completes the thought: "and when they grow older they know it" (003-005). The two men — uncle and nephew, old cynic and [[youth|young]] dandy — share a moment of complicity in worldly wisdom. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] deflects the request away from money ("I don't want money... I never pay mine") and toward information: he wants to know about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mother.

## Analysis

The sentence is a study in generational [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]]. Lord Fermor speaks as one who has passed through the illusion and emerged on the other side — but the implication in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rejoinder is that the disillusionment is the truth: the [[youth|young]] are wrong not because money is unimportant but because they only *imagine* it, rather than *knowing* it as the old do.

Lord Fermor's voice is a minor key in the novel's orchestration. He is the only character who speaks from the perspective of old age and established wealth, and his observations — blunt, unparadoxical, concerned with practical advantage — provide a counterpoint to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aesthetic paradoxes. Where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] transforms everyday truths into brilliant falsehoods, Lord Fermor states everyday truths without transformation.

The sentence also introduces the economic subtext that runs beneath [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s story. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty and charm are enabled by his inherited wealth — the Selby property, his mother's fortune, the house on [[mayfair|Grosvenor Square]]. Lord Fermor's [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] about money is the realist's reply to the novel's aestheticism: beauty may be its own justification, but it cannot function without capital.

## Themes

- [[money]]: Lord Fermor grounds the novel's aesthetic concerns in economic reality — money is the unglamorous precondition of glamour.
- [[youth]]: The sentence contrasts youthful illusion with aged knowledge, a contrast that the ageless [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will overturn.
- [[lord-henry-wotton]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply — "and when they grow older they know it" — reveals his own [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]] as inherited, part of a family tradition.
