## Quotation

> "The books that the world calls immoral are books that show the world its own shame." (019-052)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 19

## Context

This epigram occurs near the end of the novel, in the final conversation between [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just declared his intention to reform — to "be good" — and reproaches [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] for having "poisoned" him with a book (the "yellow book" given to him years earlier, in Chapter 10). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] demands that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] promise never to lend that book to anyone else, insisting "it does harm."

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], characteristically, refuses to accept the premise. He dismisses [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral turn as a temporary affectation ("you are really beginning to moralize"), then launches into a brief defense of [[preface-moral-immoral|art's autonomy]] that culminates in this line. The remark is directed at [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] but serves as one of the novel's clearest statements of its aestheticist creed: art exists apart from morality, and those who find immorality in art are merely recognising their own failings.

## Analysis

This sentence condenses the Wildean paradox at the heart of the novel's Preface: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all." (P02-004) [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s formulation goes further — it does not merely deny that books can be immoral; it argues that the very accusation of immorality is itself a confession. The word "shame" is crucial: the world, not the book, is the [[conscience|guilt]]y party.

The rhetorical structure is one of reversal. The accuser becomes the accused. A book called "immoral" is in fact a mirror held up to the society that condemns it. This is a deeply aestheticist position: art (the book) is a revelation of truth, and society's discomfort with that truth is projected back onto the art as a charge of immorality.

The line also participates in the novel's larger interest in surfaces and depths. To call a book "immoral" is to mistake the surface (the representation) for the depth (the represented). The book shows the world "its own shame" — the book is not the shame itself, merely its reflection. This logic extends the novel's treatment of the [[the-portrait|portrait]]: the [[the-portrait|painting]] shows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] his shame, but it is not itself the source of his [[sin|corruption]].

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s epigram reasserts the Preface's doctrine that art is autonomous — it "has no influence upon action" (019-052) and is "[[art-has-no-influence-upon-action|superbly sterile]]" (019-052). The moral responsibility lies with the viewer, not the artwork.
- [[influence]]: The remark is ironic in context: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is denying the power of art to influence action, even as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] insists that [[it-was-a-poisonous-book|the yellow book]] *did* influence him. The novel leaves this tension unresolved.
- [[sin]]: The line connects to the novel's treatment of sin as something society projects outward rather than confronts inwardly.
