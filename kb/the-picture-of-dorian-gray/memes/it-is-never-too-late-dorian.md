## Quotation

> "It is never too late, Dorian. Let us kneel down and try if we cannot remember a prayer. Isn't there a verse somewhere, 'Though your sins be as scarlet, yet I will make them as white as snow'?" (013-030)

## Context

Chapter 13 is the novel's turning point. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] has come to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s house before leaving for Paris, hoping to confront the rumours that have gathered around [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s name. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] decides to show him the portrait — the hidden record of his soul's degradation.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]] recoils in horror at what he sees. The beautiful young man he painted has become, on the canvas, a leering thing "bestial, sodden, and unclean" (013-022). After his initial shock, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] recovers his deepest impulse — not to judge, but to save. He urges [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to pray. The verse he quotes, though he cannot place it, is Isaiah 1:18 — the promise of divine forgiveness.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response is immediate and final: "Those words mean nothing to me now" (013-031). The rejection of prayer is the moment at which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s damnation becomes irrevocable, and the murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil]] follows within minutes.

## Analysis

The scene is structured as a sacrament inverted. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] kneels — the posture of prayer — but [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not join him. The "scarlet" and "white as snow" of the biblical verse map directly onto the novel's central visual opposition: the portrait's rotting flesh against the living [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s unspoiled beauty. The theological vocabulary that the novel has largely avoided now arrives, and it arrives too late.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s "It is never too late" is the novel's most direct moral claim. It asserts that redemption is always possible, that no degradation is final. The claim is tested and — in the narrative — apparently refuted. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will murder the man who offers him redemption, and the portrait will record the murder.

The biblical quotation is significant in its incompleteness. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] cannot remember where the verse comes from, and his uncertainty — "Isn't there a verse somewhere..." — weakens the authority of the appeal. The word of God comes to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] through a man who has lost faith in his own medium: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] can no longer paint, and now he cannot even quote scripture with confidence.

## Themes

- [[redemption]]: The scene is the novel's central treatment of the possibility and refusal of redemption.
- [[basil-hallward]]: The plea is [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s final act of love — and it costs him his life (standard).
- [[repentance]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s inability to repent is the spiritual climax of the novel, and the murder follows inexorably.
- [[prayer]]: The word "prayer" appears here almost uniquely in the novel — the language of orthodox religion has been absent, and its arrival signals the extremity of the situation.
