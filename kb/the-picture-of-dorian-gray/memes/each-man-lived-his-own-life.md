## Quotation

> "Each man lived his own life and paid his own price for living it. The only pity was one had to pay so often for a single fault. One had to pay over and over again, indeed. In her dealings with man, destiny never closed her accounts." (016-043)
>
> — Narrator, representing Dorian Gray's thoughts, Chapter 16

## Context

This passage occurs in Chapter 16 immediately after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] leaves the opium den where he has encountered Adrian [[sin|Sin]]gleton — a [[youth|young]] man whose life [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has helped to ruin. Adrian, once a gentleman, is now a degraded opium addict whom "none of the chaps will speak to" (016-017).

Hurrying along the quay through drizzling rain, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] briefly wonders "if the ruin of that [[youth|young]] life was really to be laid at his door, as [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] had said to him with such infamy of insult" (016-043). His eyes grow sad "for a few seconds." But the flicker of conscience is immediately extinguished by a philosophy of radical individualism: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] convinces himself that each person is a self-contained moral unit, bearing responsibility only for his own life.

The passage is remarkable for its rhetorical structure: what begins as a cold principle ("each man...paid his own price") modulates into self-pity ("one had to pay so often for a single fault") and finally into cosmic fatalism ("destiny never closed her accounts"). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] moves in three sentences from refusing [[conscience|guilt]] to claiming victimhood.

## Analysis

The passage performs the psychological manoeuvre that defines [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral life throughout the second half of the novel: the rapid conversion of a flicker of conscience into a philosophy that absolves him. The sequence is always the same — discomfort → rationalisation → self-pity — and it appears here in compressed form.

"Each man lived his own life and paid his own price for living it" is, on its face, a statement of radical individual responsibility. But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s application of the principle is perverse: he uses it not to accept responsibility for his own actions but to *disclaim* responsibility for their effects on others. Adrian [[sin|Sin]]gleton's ruin is not [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s concern, because Adrian "pays his own price." The philosophy that sounds like moral seriousness is, in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s hands, a philosophy of moral evasion.

The final sentence — "destiny never closed her accounts" — introduces a note of cosmic grievance. Even as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] denies his responsibility for others, he frames himself as the victim of a merciless destiny that demands payment "over and over again." The reader, who knows what the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has recorded, understands that the repeated payments are not destiny's cruelty but the natural return of unrepented sin.

The passage echoes the novel's persistent financial metaphor for moral accounting. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has earlier said to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]: "[[corruption-to-crime|You have gone from corruption to corruption, and now you have culminated in crime]]" (014-081). But [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s internal metaphor replaces moral accounting with economic accounting — and then complains about the terms.

## Themes

- [[responsibility]]: The passage is a case study in the evasion of moral responsibility through philosophical rationalisation.
- [[individualism]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s radical individualism serves not as an ethical commitment but as a shield against the claims of others.
- [[fate]]: The invocation of "destiny" allows [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to recast his own choices as cosmic inevitabilities.
- [[guilt]]: The flicker of [[conscience|guilt]] at Adrian's ruin is swiftly converted into self-pity and rationalisation.
