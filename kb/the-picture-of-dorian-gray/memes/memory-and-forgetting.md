## Definition

In *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, memory and forgetting form a thematic dyad that underpins the novel's treatment of moral responsibility. Memory functions as an internal conscience—the mind's equivalent of the portrait—while forgetting represents the seductive possibility of escaping consequence. Lord Henry's philosophy consistently advocates for forgetting as liberation; the novel's plot demonstrates that genuine forgetting is impossible when the portrait remembers everything.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Lord Henry introduces the theme early, warning Dorian that "the memory of your past will make more bitter the mean triumphs" (002-048), urging him to live without the burden of recollection. This philosophy of deliberate forgetting proves intoxicating to Dorian, who adopts it as a survival strategy after Sibyl Vane's suicide.

Sibyl's mother, Mrs. Vane, is described as having "called on memory to remake him" (005-010), attempting to recall a lost lover through the power of reminiscence. This passage establishes memory as a creative and potentially deceptive force.

After murdering Basil, Dorian seeks to "cure the soul by means of the senses" (002-040), a formula for forgetting through sensation that Lord Henry has supplied. Yet the portrait refuses to forget: "He winced at the memory of all that he had suffered" (014-004). Even as Dorian pursues opium-induced oblivion, "the hideous memory of what he had done" (012-027) pursues him.

The narrator's reflection that "the memory of the innocence that they had lost" haunts the novel's characters (011-003) suggests that forgetting is never complete—what is repressed returns with greater force. This is the novel's psychological realism beneath its Gothic surface.

## Key Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: His entire project of aesthetic living depends on forgetting. Each sin must be forgotten for the next to be possible. His failure to forget—the portrait's inexorable memory—drives him to murder and ultimately suicide.
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: Provides the philosophical warrant for forgetting, telling Dorian that memory of past pleasures is the "secret of being young" (007-036). His own practice of forgetting is less thorough than he claims, as his conversation returns obsessively to the same epigrams.
- **[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]**: Her tragedy is that Dorian forgets her almost instantly, while her brother [[james-vane|James Vane]] is defined by his inability to forget—his memory of Sibyl drives his quest for revenge.

## Key Passages

Lord Henry's philosophy urging the cultivation of forgetting:

> There were opiates for remorse, drugs that could lull the moral sense to sleep. But here was a visible symbol of the degradation of sin. Here was an ever-present sign of the ruin men brought upon their souls.
>
> (008-017)

The portrait as indestructible memory:

> He winced at the memory of all that he had suffered, and for a moment the same curious feeling of loathing for Basil Hallward came back to him.
>
> (014-004)

The recovery of lost innocence through memory:

> It was a lovely night, and the perfume of the heliotrope seemed to recall to them the memory of the innocence that they had lost.
>
> (011-003)

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde transforms the classical opposition of memory and forgetting into a Gothic mechanism. The portrait literalizes memory—it is an object that never forgets. This is the novel's profoundest moral insight: you can live as if the past does not exist, but somewhere a record is being kept. The portrait is memory made material, and Dorian's destruction of it is his final, futile attempt to forget. In this sense, the novel anticipates Freudian concepts of repression and the return of the repressed by a generation.
