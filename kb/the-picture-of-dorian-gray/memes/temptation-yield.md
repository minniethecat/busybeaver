## Overview

"The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" (002-043).

This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most dangerous epigram — and the most consequential sentence spoken in the novel.

Delivered in [[basils-studio|Basil's studio]] during the fateful afternoon of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s awakening, it provides the philosophical license for everything that

follows.

If resistance strengthens desire, then yielding extinguishes it. This is [[pleasure|hedonism]] as hygiene — a doctrine of purification through indulgence.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] absorbs this logic and lives by
it for eighteen years. The novel asks: what happens when you take an epigram as a philosophy of life?

## Context

### The Scene

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers the line in Chapter 2, in the garden of [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio, while [[basil-hallward|Basil]] paints [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]] inside. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just met [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] for the first time.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been speaking about [[influence]], [[youth]], and the brevity of beauty. His speech crescendos:

> "The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it.
Resist it, and your soul grows sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself, with desire for what its monstrous laws have made monstrous and unlawful." (002-043)

The line comes near the climax of a monologue that transforms [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s understanding of himself. Moments later, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will see the finished [[the-portrait|portrait]] and make his fateful wish.

### Immediate Effect

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is "dimly conscious that entirely fresh influences were at work within him" (002-045).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words have "touched some secret chord that had never been touched before"
(002-045).

The epigram does its work. From this moment, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s creation.

## Analysis

### The Logic

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s argument is psychologically acute. Asceticism — the denial of desire — often intensifies what it forbids. The Puritan who forbids pleasure makes pleasure obsessive.

By
this logic, yielding is liberation: satisfy the desire and it ceases to dominate you.

### The Trap

The epigram's elegance conceals its danger. Some temptations, once yielded to, do not disappear — they grow.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s first cruelty (rejecting [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]) does not satisfy his appetite
for cruelty; it inaugurates it. The [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s first [[transformation|change]] is only the beginning.

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s formula works for trivial temptations (a cigar, an evening at the opera). It fails catastrophically for sins of consequence.

But the epigram does not distinguish
between the two.

### Dramatic Irony

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] never follows his own ad[[sin|vice]]. He talks about yielding to temptation but lives a life of comfortable [[reputation|respectability]]. He is married, attends his club, dines in society.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is the one who actually *lives* the doctrine — and is destroyed by it.

## Consequences in the Novel

The epigram's consequences ripple through the entire plot:

1. **[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s wish** (002-073): Fresh from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s speech, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wishes the [[the-portrait|portrait]] would age in his place — his first "yielding" to a supernatural temptation

2. **[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s abandonment** (007-065): [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] yields to the temptation of cruelty when [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] fails as an artist

3. **Eighteen years of [[pleasure|hedonism]]** (011-008): The "[[pleasure|new Hedonism]]" is the epigram systematized into a way of life

4. **[[murder-of-basil-hallward|Basil's murder]]** (013-045): The temptation to silence the voice of conscience — yielded to with fatal finality

5. **Self-destruction** (020-030): [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] yields to the temptation to destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — and destroys himself
