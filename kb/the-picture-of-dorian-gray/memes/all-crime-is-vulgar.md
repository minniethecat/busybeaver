## Quotation

> "All crime is vulgar, just as all vulgarity is crime." (019-025)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]] to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], during a private dinner at [[mayfair|Dorian's house]] (Chapter 19). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just hypothetically asked what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] would say if told that he had murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], dismissing the confession as a pose, responds that crime "belongs exclusively to the lower orders" (019-025), suggesting it is beneath [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. The remark reveals [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s amoral aestheticism: for him, crime is distasteful not because it is wrong but because it is crude (019-025).

## Analysis

This aphorism is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most revealing epigrams. It inverts conventional morality by equating crime not with evil but with a lack of refinement. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s world-view reduces [[morality|ethics]] to aesthetics: just as "vulgarity is crime," crime itself is merely the ultimate vulgarity. The statement also functions as dramatic irony — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has in fact committed murder, yet [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] refuses to see it, blinded by his own philosophy and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s unchanging beauty (019-025). The word "vulgar" recurs throughout the novel as the ultimate condemnation in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s vocabulary, applied to everything from realism in art (001-056) to emotional sincerity (003-033).
