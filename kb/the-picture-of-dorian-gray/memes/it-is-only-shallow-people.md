## Quotation

> "What has the actual lapse of time got to do with it? It is only shallow people who require years to get rid of an emotion. A man who is master of himself can end a sorrow as easily as he can invent a pleasure. I don't want to be at the mercy of my emotions. I want to use them, to enjoy them, and to dominate them." (009-007)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] speaks these words to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] on the morning after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s suicide. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to offer condolences, expecting to find [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] prostrate with grief. Instead he finds [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] dressed for the opera, calm and untroubled. When [[basil-hallward|Basil]] expresses shock, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] delivers this epigram — a manifesto of emotional self-mastery that he has absorbed from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]].

The scene is the first clear demonstration of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s transformation. Before [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wept at [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s cruelty onstage. Now, after her death, he speaks of grief as something to be "used," "enjoyed," and "dominated" — the vocabulary of a collector, not a mourner. The epigram is not [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own invention; it is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy refracted through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s voice, marking the moment when influence becomes identity.

[[basil-hallward|Basil]], the moral witness, is horrified. He has come to console a grieving boy and finds instead a [[youth|young]] dandy who treats emotion as an aesthetic commodity. The ex[[transformation|change]] dramatizes the novel's central question: is it possible to master one's emotions without losing one's soul?

## Analysis

The epigram operates through a series of escalating claims. The first sentence — "It is only shallow people who require years to get rid of an emotion" — reverses the conventional valuation of grief. To mourn deeply and long is not a sign of depth but of shallowness. The truly profound person, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] implies, processes emotion quickly and moves on.

The second sentence extends the claim: "A man who is master of himself can end a sorrow as easily as he can invent a pleasure." Here [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] equates emotional control with artistic creation — sorrow and pleasure are both things to be "invented" at will. This is the aesthetic attitude taken to its logical extreme: if life is an art form, then emotions are materials, not experiences.

The final sentence — "I want to use them, to enjoy them, and to dominate them" — makes the will to power explicit. The verbs ascend in intensity: use → enjoy → dominate. Emotion is not something that happens to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; it is something he does. The grammar of mastery replaces the grammar of suffering.

But the epigram contains its own refutation. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s insistence on his emotional mastery is itself a form of defensiveness — the protest-too-much of a man who has, in fact, been deeply affected by [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death but cannot admit it without undermining his new identity. The [[the-portrait|portrait]], after all, has already begun to [[transformation|change]].

## Themes

- [[emotion]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s epigram is a radical revaluation of emotional experience — grief as shallowness, detachment as depth.
- [[self-mastery]]: The ideal of the self as something to be commanded, not something to be endured.
- [[transformation|Dorian Gray's transformation]]: This speech marks the moment when [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence fully takes hold, converting a boy who felt too much into a man who refuses to feel at all.
- [[shallowness]]: The paradox at the heart of the epigram — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] accuses others of shallowness while demonstrating the deepest shallowness of all.
