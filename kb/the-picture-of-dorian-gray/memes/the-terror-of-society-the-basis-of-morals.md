## Quotation

> "The terror of society, which is the basis of morals, the terror of God, which is the secret of religion — these are the two things that govern us." (002-024)

## Context

In Chapter 2, during his first extended speech to [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] in [[basils-studio|Basil's garden]], [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers a long monologue on influence and self-development (002-024). He argues that "to influence a person is to give him one's own soul" — that every external influence is a form of colonization of the self. The speech builds toward the claim that people are afraid to be themselves, and that this fear is institutionalized in society and religion.

This sentence is the diagnostic climax of that argument. It names the two governing forces of human life — and identifies both as forms of terror.

## Analysis

The sentence is built on a chiasmus: "terror of society" pairs with "basis of morals," and "terror of God" pairs with "secret of religion." The parallel structure suggests a symmetry between the two forces, but the second term of each pair undercuts the first: morals are not grounded in [[morality|virtue]] but in fear; religion is not grounded in faith but in a "secret" — something hidden, perhaps shameful.

The word "terror" does the philosophical work. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not say "respect for society" or "fear of God" (the traditional phrase). He chooses "terror" — a word that connotes not reverence but dread, not awe but paralysis. Society and God are not guides; they are jailers.

This is a radical claim in the context of Victorian England, where both social [[reputation|respectability]] and religious observance were public [[morality|virtue]]s. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reframes them as pathologies: symptoms of cowardice rather than expressions of character. The "aim of life is self-development" (002-024), he has already said, and these twin terrors are the obstacles.

The sentence also sets up the novel's central tension: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will spend his life evading both terrors — society's judgment (through his un[[transformation|change]]d appearance) and God's judgment (through the hidden [[the-portrait|portrait]]). He will discover that escaping terror does not produce freedom.

## Themes

- [[morality]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] exposes morality not as an internal compass but as an external threat — a system of social control backed by terror.
- [[society]]: "The terror of society" is the novel's most succinct definition of Victorian [[reputation|respectability]]: a regime of fear disguised as propriety.
- [[influence]]: The sentence concludes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s argument that all external influence is corrupting — and that self-realization requires resisting both society and religion.
