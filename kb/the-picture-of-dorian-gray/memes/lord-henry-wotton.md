## Overview

Lord Henry Wotton, called "Harry," is the novel's most dangerous character.

He corrupts through charm and intelligence, not malice. With [[basil-hallward]], he forms one pole of the [[duality|dialectic]] that defines the novel.

His epigrams supply the philosophy that governs [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s descent into [[pleasure|hedonism]].

## Biography

Lord Henry first appears in [[basils-studio|Basil Hallward's studio]]. Son of a diplomat, educated at Eton and Oxford.

His marriage to Victoria is a social arrangement. She is "never in the way, and never out of the way" (004-022).

He serves as chorus and catalyst — commenting with wit while driving [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s transformation through planted ideas.

Unlike [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], Lord Henry never [[transformation|change]]s. He ends as he began: charming, amoral, untouched by consequence.

## Epigrams

Lord Henry's influence operates through paradoxical aphorisms:

- "There is no such thing as a good influence. All influence is immoral." (002-022)

- "The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it." (002-043)

- "[[youth|Youth]] is the one thing worth having." (002-032)

- "I can sympathize with everything except suffering." (003-073)

- "To be popular one must [[to-be-popular-one-must-be-a-mediocrity|be a mediocrity]]." (003-095)

- "Nothing can cure the soul but the senses." (002-041)

## Relationships

- [[dorian-gray]]: His "creation" and philosophical experiment.

- [[basil-hallward]]: Friend and foil — sincerity vs. [[to-become-the-spectator-of-ones-own-life|aesthetic detachment]].

- Victoria Wotton: Wife; their union is a hollow social form.

## Character Analysis

Lord Henry is seductive and genuinely witty. He is not a conventional villain — he commits no crimes.

Yet his influence is destructive. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] hints at murder, Lord Henry dismisses it as a "mistake" (019-019).

Wilde put himself into Lord Henry's wit and paradoxes. But the character also demonstrates the limits of aesthetic philosophy applied to human lives.

## Key Scenes

- **The Studio (Ch 1–2)**: Meets [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], speech on youth and beauty.

- **Luncheon (Ch 3)**: Performs wit for [[high-society|aristocratic society]].

- **[[the-theatre|The Theatre]] (Ch 7)**: Detached response to [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's death]].

- **After [[basil-hallward|Basil]] (Ch 19)**: Deflects confession with epigrams.

## Quotations

> There is no such thing as a good influence, Mr. Gray. All influence is immoral. (002-022)

The paradox that sets the novel's tragedy in motion.

> The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it. (002-043)

The principle licensing all of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|transgression]]s.

> I can sympathize with everything except suffering. (003-073)

Always a spectator, never a participant in human pain.

## Thematic Significance

Lord Henry embodies **[[influence]]** — the novel's central mechanism of [[sin|corruption]].

He articulates the philosophy of **[[pleasure]]** and **[[beauty]]**. His detachment critiques aestheticism as [[morality|ethics]].

His survival past [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s destruction suggests the amoral spectator escapes judgment — a darker fate than death.
