## Overview

The relationship between beauty and morality is the novel's deepest philosophical question. The Preface declares that "there is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book" (P02-003) and that "the artist is the creator of beautiful things," asserting the separation of art from ethics. Yet the narrative itself seems to contradict this — Dorian's pursuit of beauty leads to moral catastrophe, and the portrait becomes a visible register of sin. The tension between the novel's aestheticist doctrines and its moralistic plot has been a central subject of critical debate since publication. Lord Henry represents the amoral position (beauty justifies all), Basil Hallward the moral one (beauty and goodness are intertwined), and Dorian Gray is the experiment that tests both.
