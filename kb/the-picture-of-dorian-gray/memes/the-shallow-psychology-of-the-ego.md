## Quotation

> "He used to wonder at the shallow psychology of those who conceive the ego in man as a thing simple, permanent, reliable, and of one essence. To him, man was a being with myriad lives and myriad sensations, a complex multiform creature that bore within itself strange legacies of thought and passion, and whose very flesh was tainted with the monstrous maladies of the dead." (011-032)
>
> — Narrator, describing Dorian Gray's belief, Chapter 11

## Context

This passage directly follows the narrator's pronouncement that "Form is absolutely essential" to both art and society (011-031). Having established that insincerity is "merely a method by which we can multiply our personalities," the narrator moves into [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s interior world to show how completely he has internalized this philosophy.

The passage is situated within Chapter 11's catalogue of Dorian's pursuits — his study of perfumes, music, jewels, embroideries, and ecclesiastical vestments. But before detailing these, Wilde pauses to establish the psychological rationale: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] no longer believes in a unified self. This disbelief is the philosophical precondition for everything that follows — the double life, the hidden portrait, the unpunished crimes.

## Analysis

The epigram is a miniature manifesto of Decadent psychology. Against the classical and Christian tradition that the soul is one, simple, and eternal, it posits a self that is "complex," "multiform," and historically layered. The adjectives "myriad lives and myriad sensations" evoke the Decadent ideal of the *dandy* who experiences everything, exhausts every sensation, and refuses to be defined by any single identity.

The final clause — "whose very flesh was tainted with the monstrous maladies of the dead" — introduces a Gothic element. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s multiplicity is not liberating; it is hereditary, compulsive, and contaminated. The passage connects his psychology to the portraits of his ancestors, described in the paragraph that follows [011-033 onward], and foreshadows the revelation that his corruption is inscribed in his blood as well as in his choices.

The word "shallow" is strategically placed: those who believe in a simple self are psychologically naive. The Decadent, by contrast, knows the self is a fiction — and exploits that knowledge.

## Themes

- [[duality]]: The passage provides the intellectual foundation for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s double life — if the self is multiple, one persona need not answer for another.
- [[soul]]: By reconceiving the soul as a "complex multiform creature," the passage challenges the religious framework that will ultimately condemn Dorian.
- [[the-portrait]]: The ancestral portraits described after this passage literalize the idea that the self bears "strange legacies" from the dead.
- [[transformation]]: The belief in a fluid, non-essential self enables Dorian's moral transformation without apparent consequence — at least until the novel's end.
