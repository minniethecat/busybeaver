## Quotation

> "I have known everything," said Lord Henry, with a tired look in his eyes, "but I am always ready for a new emotion. I am afraid, however, that, for me at any rate, there is no such thing." (006-057)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Dorian Gray and Basil Hallward, Chapter 6

## Context

The Bristol dinner is ending. [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], flushed with excitement about his engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], has promised that seeing her perform will give [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] "a new ideal of life" — that "she will represent something to you that you have never known" (006-056).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply is delivered with "a tired look in his eyes," a rare physical description that Wilde uses to puncture the epigram's bravado. The statement is structured as a three-part movement: assertion ("I have known everything"), concession to appetite ("I am always ready for a new emotion"), and self-deflating qualification ("I am afraid there is no such thing").

The men rise from the table and prepare to leave for the theatre, where [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s performance — and her catastrophic failure — await.

## Analysis

This is one of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s most self-revealing — and self-contradicting — epigrams. The claim "I have known everything" is grandiose, the pose of a man who mistakes a well-stocked mind for a lived life. But the phrase "with a tired look in his eyes" immediately undercuts it. Wilde gives us the performance and the exhaustion behind it in the same sentence.

The second clause — "always ready for a new emotion" — is the dandy's creed. Experience is valued for its novelty, not its depth; the goal is sensation, not understanding. This aesthetic of perpetual appetite is what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been preaching to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] since Chapter 2: "The aim of life is self-development. To realise one's nature perfectly — that is what each of us is here for" (002-013).

But the third clause admits defeat: there may be no new emotions left. This is the terminal condition of the decadent — the palate so jaded that nothing tastes. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has talked himself into a corner where real experience can never match his epigrams about it.

The irony is that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s performance that night will indeed produce a new emotion — not in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], but in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]: disillusionment, and cruelty.

## Themes

- [[pleasure]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] frames emotion-seeking as the highest human activity, yet confesses its futility.
- [[experience]]: The epigram captures the paradox of the experiential ideal — the more one seeks new sensations, the fewer remain.
- [[ennui]]: The "tired look" reveals the exhaustion beneath the dandy's appetite.
- [[decadence]]: The remark is a textbook expression of fin-de-siècle decadence: world-weary, sensation-hungry, self-aware.
