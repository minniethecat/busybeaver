## Definition

Crime represents the endpoint of Dorian Gray's corruption — the passage from hidden sin to violent transgression. While his earlier sins are left deliberately vague, the novel renders two crimes in sharp focus: the murder of Basil Hallward (Chapter 13) and the moral coercion that drives Alan Campbell to suicide (Chapter 14). These crimes mark the irreversibility of Dorian's fall, transforming him from a hedonistic aesthete into a figure of genuine evil.

## Dorian's Crimes

Dorian's first and central crime is the murder of Basil Hallward. After revealing the horrifying portrait, Dorian stabs Basil in a surge of "mad passions of a hunted animal" (013-033). The murder is not premeditated but impulsive — a convulsion of hatred for the man whose painting and conscience have become unbearable to him.

His second crime is the blackmail of Alan Campbell. Leveraging a past secret, Dorian forces the chemist to destroy Basil's body with nitric acid. Campbell, unable to refuse, later takes his own life — a death for which Dorian bears clear moral responsibility (015-001).

These crimes set Dorian apart from the mere corruption of his earlier years. As he himself acknowledges: "You have gone from corruption to corruption, and now you have culminated in crime" (014-081).

> All crime is vulgar, just as all vulgarity is crime. It is not in you, Dorian, to commit a murder. I am sorry if I hurt your vanity by saying so, but I assure you it is true. Crime belongs exclusively to the lower orders. I don't blame them in the smallest degree. I should fancy that crime was to them what art is to us, simply a method of procuring extraordinary sensations.
>
> (019-025)

## Lord Henry's View

Lord Henry treats crime with characteristic detachment, dismissing it as a "method of procuring extraordinary sensations" (019-025). He insists that Dorian is constitutionally incapable of murder, his ironic blindness to the truth underscoring the gulf between his theoretical amorality and the brutal reality of Dorian's acts.

## Consequences

The crimes seal Dorian's fate. After Basil's murder, the portrait acquires a new mark — "a stain of blood on the hand" (013-040) — and Dorian's peace of mind is permanently shattered. His attempt at reformation with Hetty Merton is undermined by the portrait's unchanged testimony, leading to his desperate and fatal attack on the painting itself.
