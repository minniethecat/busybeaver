## Quotation

> "He had mad hungers that grew more ravenous as he fed them." (011-005)

## Context

Chapter 11 is the novel's great compressed chronicle of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s [[duality|double life]] — the years between his coming of age and the murder of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]. The narrator describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s nocturnal rituals: returning from "mysterious and prolonged absences," creeping upstairs to the locked room, standing before the [[the-portrait|portrait]] with a mirror, comparing the "evil and aging face on the canvas" with "the fair [[youth|young]] face that laughed back at him from the polished glass." The contrast sharpens his pleasure; he grows "more and more [[enamoured-of-his-own-beauty|enamoured of his own beauty]], more and more interested in the [[enamoured-of-his-own-beauty|corruption of his own soul]]."

The sentence about "mad hungers" follows a description of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sleepless nights, when he would think of "the ruin he had brought upon his soul with a pity that was all the more poignant because it was purely selfish." But such moments of self-awareness are rare. The dominant logic of his life is appetite, not reflection: "That curiosity about life which [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] had first stirred in him... seemed to increase with gratification. The more he knew, the more he desired to know."

## Analysis

The sentence is a model of Wildean compression. In eleven words, it captures the entire dynamic of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral life: desire that does not diminish with satisfaction but intensifies. The metaphor of "hungers" replaces the language of morality with the language of appetite — sin is not a [[sin|transgression]] but a meal. And the qualifying "mad" does the work of [[morality|moral judgment]] without breaking the narrator's urbane surface.

The paradox at the sentence's core is that feeding a hunger should satisfy it, not make it "more ravenous." This is the logic of addiction, not of appetite — and it is precisely addiction that the sentence diagnoses. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not a libertine taking discrete pleasures; he is an addict whose pleasures have become compulsions, each satisfaction increasing rather than decreasing the need.

The sentence also functions as a hinge in the chapter's structure. It sits at the passage's turn from the description of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s private [[sin|corruption]] (the locked room, the mirror, the [[the-portrait|portrait]]) to the description of his public life (the dinners, the concerts, the "exquisite taste"). [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s mad hungers are the hidden engine of his visible refinement.

## Themes

- [[desire]]: The sentence captures desire as a self-perpetuating machine — each fulfillment creates new need.
- [[corruption]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|corruption]] is described not as [[sin|moral decay]] but as the intensification of appetite.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s "curiosity about life" is the seed from which [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "mad hungers" grow.
- [[the-yellow-book]]: The unnamed book that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] gives [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], which accelerates the transformation this sentence describes.
