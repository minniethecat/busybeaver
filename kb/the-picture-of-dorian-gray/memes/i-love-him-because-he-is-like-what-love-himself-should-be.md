## Quotation

> "I know why I love him. I love him because he is like what love himself should be." (005-012)
>
> — Sibyl Vane, to her brother James Vane, Chapter 5

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] speaks these words to her brother James during their conversation in a London park before his departure for Australia. James has been pressing her about her mysterious new suitor, "[[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]]," whom he distrusts. When James asks why she loves him, [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] offers this remarkable definition: she loves [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] not for his qualities, his character, or even his beauty, but because he embodies the Platonic form of Love itself.

The line immediately follows her admission: "But what does he see in me? I am not worthy of him" (005-013). This self-effacement, combined with the idealisation of the beloved, sets up the fatal dynamic of their relationship: [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] loves an ideal, and when that ideal proves false, she has nothing left.

## Analysis

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s formulation is strikingly philosophical for a seventeen-year-old actress. She does not say "I love him because he is kind" or "because he is [[beauty|beautiful]]" but because he incarnates an abstraction — Love with a capital L. This is the language of Neoplatonism, in which earthly beauty is a shadow of a divine Form. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] has, in effect, [[transformation|fall]]en in love with the Idea of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] rather than with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] himself.

The tragedy is that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has similarly [[transformation|fall]]en in love not with [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] but with her art — with [[sibyl-vane|Juliet]], Rosalind, Imogen — and when she can no longer perform those roles, his love evaporates. Both lovers are [[conscience|guilt]]y of the same error: loving the symbol rather than the person. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s line is thus both [[beauty|beautiful]] and ominous, a statement of romantic idealism that the novel's plot will punish mercilessly.

The phrase "what love himself should be" personifies Love as a masculine entity — the classical Eros or Cupid. This adds a layer of complexity: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] (the beloved) is being compared to Love (the god), which elevates him to divine status. When a mortal is worshipped as a god, tragedy follows.

## Themes

- [[love]] — [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s Neoplatonic conception of love as the worship of an ideal contrasts with [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s cynical epigrams and [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s [[worship|devotion]]al aestheticism.
- [[beauty]] — The conflation of beauty with moral worth drives the novel's tragic engine; [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s line perfectly captures this conflation.
- [[idealization]] — The discrepancy between the ideal and the real is the novel's central tragic structure, and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s words initiate that structure for her own story arc.
