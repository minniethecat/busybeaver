## Quotation

> "Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing." (004-014)

## Context

In Chapter 4, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives late to a luncheon at Lord Agatha's, explaining that he was bargaining for a piece of old brocade in Wardour Street. When asked about it, he delivers this epigram.

The remark is made in passing — a casual piece of table-talk — but it encapsulates a key theme of the novel: the reduction of all experience to market terms. The brocade has a price (what someone will pay for it), but its value (beauty, history, craftsmanship) is invisible to the "nowadays people" who reduce everything to commerce.

## Analysis

The epigram's power lies in its compression: twelve words that distinguish between two ways of knowing. "Price" is public, objective, and measurable — the market decides it. "Value" is private, subjective, and incommensurable — it requires taste, sensitivity, and time to perceive.

The line also contains an implicit social critique. The "nowadays people" are the Victorian middle class — practical, commercial, and aesthetically blind. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] positions himself (and by extension, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] and the novel's implied reader) above this crowd: we know value; they know only price.

Wilde uses this epigram in his own voice elsewhere (it appears in his letters and in *Lady Windermere's Fan*), making it one of the most portable and quotable lines in his oeuvre.
