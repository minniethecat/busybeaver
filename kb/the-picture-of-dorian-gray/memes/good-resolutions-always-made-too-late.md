## Quotation

> "I remember your saying once that there is a fatality about good resolutions — that they are always made too late. Mine certainly were." (008-046)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] speaks these words to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] the morning after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's suicide]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has come to offer consolation, but instead finds [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] already adopting his mentor's detached language. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just confessed that he thought it was his "duty" to marry [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] — a resolution that her death has rendered impossible. He then quotes [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s own aphorism back to him: that good resolutions are fatalistically doomed to arrive too late.

The moment is pivotal: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not merely parroting [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]; he is using [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy to absolve himself. If good resolutions are inherently futile, then [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] bears no [[conscience|guilt]] for failing to act on his. The philosophy becomes a mechanism of denial.

## Analysis

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s speech operates on two levels. On the surface, it is self-exculpation: the "terrible tragedy" of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death "prevented" him from doing what was right. But the deeper logic is more disturbing — he recasts his own moral failure as a law of nature. The "fatality about good resolutions" transforms [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s inaction into inevitability.

The word "fatality" is carefully chosen. It suggests destiny — something cosmic and inescapable — rather than personal weakness. By framing his failure in these terms, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] externalizes his conscience. He is not morally lazy; he is subject to a fatal law.

This moment also marks the beginning of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s strategy for living with [[conscience|guilt]]. He will spend the rest of the novel oscillating between attempted reform (good resolutions) and immediate sensory distraction — always arriving, as here, too late to matter.

## Themes

- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s conscience stirs — he recognizes that marrying [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] was "his duty" — but the recognition is immediately neutralized by the philosophy of fatalism.
- [[influence]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence is dramatized as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] literally quotes his mentor's words back to him. The student has learned the master's lesson too well.
- [[morality]]: The passage exposes the novel's moral psychology: the gap between knowing what is right and doing it, and the rationalizations that fill that gap.
