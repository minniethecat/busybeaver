## Quotation

> "Because you have the most marvellous youth, and youth is the one thing worth having." (002-046)

## Context

In Chapter 2, in [[basils-studio|Basil's garden]], [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just warned [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] that sunburn would spoil his [[the-portrait|portrait]] — that his appearance "should matter everything" (002-044) to him. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] asks "Why?" (002-045), [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this single, compressed sentence. It is the pivot: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s casual indifference meets [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s absolute conviction.

The sentence stands as the thesis statement for the much longer "youth" speech that follows at (002-048), where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] expands it into a full meditation on beauty, mortality, and the tragic brevity of youth. But this short line does all the work — it names youth as the supreme value, and in doing so plants the seed of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s eventual wish: to trade his soul for eternal youth.

## Analysis

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s rhetoric operates through two moves. First, he flatters: "the most marvellous youth" positions [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as exceptional, the possessor of something rare. Second, he universalizes: "youth is the one thing worth having" elevates a personal compliment into a metaphysical claim. The definite article "the one thing" excludes all other values — morality, art, love, knowledge — in a single stroke.

Structurally, the line functions as a hinge. Before it, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is naive and indifferent to his beauty; after it, he becomes conscious of what he possesses and terrified of losing it. The moment [[dorian-makes-his-wish|Dorian makes his fatal wish]] at (002-075) — "If it were I who was to be always [[youth|young]], and the picture that was to grow old!" — is a direct echo of this sentence.

The brevity is strategic. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not argue; he declares. There is no syllogism, no evidence — just an assertion delivered with the confidence of an oracle. This is how [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] operates throughout the novel: not by persuasion but by pronouncement.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats beauty as a self-justifying value — it "needs no explanation" (002-048). This sentence is the novel's first unambiguous statement of that aesthetic creed.
- [[youth-and-mortality]]: By naming youth "the one thing worth having," [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] frames aging as absolute loss. This dread of decay becomes the engine of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s entire trajectory.
