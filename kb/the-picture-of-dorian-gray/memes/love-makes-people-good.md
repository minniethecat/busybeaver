## Quotation

> "Ah! I wish you would fall in love. Love makes people good, and what you said was wicked." (005-090)

## Context

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] speaks these words to her brother [[james-vane|James Vane]] in Chapter 5, after he has sworn to kill [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] if he ever wrongs her. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] has just dismissed [[james-vane|Jim]]'s threat as the foolishness of a "bad-tempered boy," and now she softens her tone. Her rebuke is gentle but absolute: his words were wicked, and only love — the love he has not yet experienced — could have prevented them.

The line is [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s credo. She believes in love as a moral force, a transformative power that makes people better than they would otherwise be. This belief is what she offers [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]; it is also what will destroy her when she discovers that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s love does not make *him* good.

The dramatic irony is double. [[james-vane|Jim]] has not [[transformation|fall]]en in love, and his protective rage is untouched by the softening [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] describes. But neither has [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — not truly — and the novel will demonstrate that love, in Wilde's world, does not always make people good. Sometimes it makes them worse.

## Analysis

### Love as Moral Philosophy

[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s line is the novel's simplest and most direct statement of a moral philosophy. It belongs to the world of romantic idealism that [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] inhabits — a world where love is the answer to every question, the cure for every fault. When she says "love makes people good," she is not theorizing; she is testifying. She knows it because she feels it. Her love for [[dorian-gray|Prince Charming]] has made *her* good — generous, humble, radiant with happiness.

The line is Wilde's homage to the Romantic tradition that runs from Shakespeare's comedies to the Victorian melodramas [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] performs nightly. In that tradition, love really does transform: the villain reforms, the cynic softens, the lost are found. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] believes she is living in that story.

### The Limits of the Philosophy

The novel, of course, does not share [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s faith. Love does not make [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] good; it makes him bored. It does not make [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] good; it never touches him at all. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s philosophy is [[beauty|beautiful]] and wrong — which is, in Wilde's view, the condition of all philosophies.

But the line is not merely ironic. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] is not wrong about *herself*. Her love does make her good — selfless, devoted, capable of the ultimate sacrifice. The tragedy is that her goodness, born of love, is precisely what destroys her. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] rejects her, she cannot hate him; she can only cease to exist.

### The Sibling Dynamic

The line is also a moment of intimacy between siblings. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] speaks to [[james-vane|Jim]] as only a sister can: with authority, affection, and a touch of condescension. She is a year older and "knew so much more of life." Her wish — "I wish you would [[transformation|fall]] in love" — is both generous and patronizing. She wants [[james-vane|Jim]] to experience what she experiences, but she also assumes that her experience is the universal one.

[[james-vane|Jim]], who "hated" the gentleman who courted his sister "through some curious race-instinct," cannot answer her. Love, for him, is not a softening force but a hardening one — his love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] drives him to vengeance, not gentleness.
