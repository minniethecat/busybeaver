## Definition

Worship in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* describes the intense devotion that art and beauty can inspire — a devotion that crosses from admiration into something more consuming. [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s feelings for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] exemplify this: he speaks of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in the language of idolatry, [[credit-is-the-capital-of-a-younger-son|credit]]ing him with transforming his artistic vision. This worship is both creative (it produces [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s greatest work) and destructive (it blinds [[basil-hallward|Basil]] to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[sin|corruption]] and ultimately leads to his death).

## Manifestation in the Novel

[[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s worship of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is established in the opening chapter. He speaks of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as a revelation: "Some subtle influence passed from him to me, and for the first time in my life I saw in the woodland the grace and comeliness that we had always been taught to look for in pictures and sculpture" (001-056). This language of religious conversion frames [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as a divine figure rather than a human subject — a framing that the novel's Gothic logic literalizes.

## Key Characters

- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: The worshipper whose devotion to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] produces great art and fatal blindness
- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The object of worship, who becomes corrupted by being treated as an ideal rather than a person

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde explores worship as an intensified form of aesthetic appreciation — a natural response to extraordinary beauty, but one that carries moral risks. The worshipper loses objectivity; the worshipped loses humanity. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s tragedy is that his devotion to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] prevents him from seeing what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is becoming. The novel suggests that worship, however sincere, is not love — and that treating a person as an ideal is a form of violence.
