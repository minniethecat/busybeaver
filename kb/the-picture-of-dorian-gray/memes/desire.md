## Overview

Desire is the motor of action in *The Picture of Dorian Gray*. It propels [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s pursuit of sensation, animates [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy, and haunts [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s devotion to beauty.

The novel treats desire as a double force: it is the source of art and vitality, yet also the gateway to corruption when pursued without restraint.

Central to the novel's moral scheme is the question of what happens when desire is severed from conscience.

## Lord Henry's Philosophy of Desire

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] preaches a doctrine of unbridled desire. His celebrated speech to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] elevates wanting above refusing.

He argues that "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" and that resistance breeds sickness (002-026):

> Every impulse that we strive to strangle broods in the mind and poisons us. The body sins once, and has done with its sin, for action is a mode of purification.
>
> (002-026)

For Lord Henry, desire is not a problem to be managed but a force to be obeyed. His philosophy treats self-denial as the true immorality.

This inversion of conventional Victorian morality is the intellectual seduction that captures [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].

## Dorian's Education in Desire

Under Lord Henry's influence, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] learns to treat his desires not as temptations to resist but as imperatives to fulfill.

His pursuit of sensation becomes increasingly elaborate, moving from the simple pleasures of youth to the exotic and the forbidden.

The [[the-yellow-book|yellow book]] serves as a catalogue of desires, introducing Dorian to modes of experience he had never imagined.

The novel charts this education without moralizing, allowing the reader to feel both the intoxication of Dorian's freedom and the accumulating horror of its cost.

## Desire and Art

Desire in the novel is bound up with art. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s desire for beauty — his [[worship]] of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s physical perfection — produces his greatest work, the [[the-portrait|portrait]].

Yet this same desire, redirected by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy, becomes the source of corruption.

Wilde's Preface declares that "no artist desires to prove anything" (P02-006), separating artistic desire from moral purpose. The novel tests this separation in life rather than art — with devastating results.
