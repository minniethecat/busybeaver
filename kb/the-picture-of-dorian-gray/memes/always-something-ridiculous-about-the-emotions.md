## Quotation

> "There is always something ridiculous about the emotions of people whom one has ceased to love." (007-055)
>
> — The Narrator, Chapter 7

## Context

This sentence appears in the aftermath of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s disastrous performance as Juliet. Having been humiliated in front of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] goes backstage to confront [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]. She throws herself at his feet, sobbing, begging him not to leave her: "I am so sorry I didn't act well. I was thinking of you all the time. But I will try—indeed, I will try" (007-054). She crouches on the floor "like a wounded thing."

The narrator then turns to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s reaction, and the sentence is chilling: "[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], with his beautiful eyes, looked down at her, and his chiselled lips curled in exquisite disdain. There is always something ridiculous about the emotions of people whom one has ceased to love. [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] seemed to him to be absurdly melodramatic. Her tears and sobs annoyed him" (007-055).

## Analysis

This is one of the novel's most important narrative sentences, because it records the exact moment when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral sensibility cracks. Up to this point, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has been an innocent — impressionable, vain, but not cruel. Here, for the first time, he looks at another human being's suffering and feels not compassion but contempt. The word "ridiculous" is the key: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not angry at [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] for disappointing him; he finds her *embarrassing*. Her genuine emotion has become aesthetically offensive to him.

The narrator's choice of free indirect discourse — filtering the observation through [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s consciousness — is a formal masterstroke. The sentence reads like a universal truth, a piece of worldly wisdom, but it is in fact a symptom of moral decay. What sounds like sophistication is actually cruelty in evening dress. Wilde is showing us that aestheticism, taken to its extreme, transforms other people's pain into a matter of taste.

The image of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "chiselled lips curled in exquisite disdain" is deliberately aestheticised — even his cruelty is beautiful. The word "exquisite" belongs to the vocabulary of art criticism, not ethics. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is beginning to treat life as a spectator sport, and [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s anguish is merely a bad performance.

## Themes

- [[transformation]]: This is the pivotal moment in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moral transformation — the first time he exercises the emotional cruelty that will define his later life.
- [[vanity]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s disdain is rooted in wounded vanity; he loved [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] not as a person but as an artist, and when her art fails, his love evaporates instantly.
- [[pleasure]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]] was always a form of aesthetic pleasure; when she ceases to provide that pleasure, she becomes worthless to him.
