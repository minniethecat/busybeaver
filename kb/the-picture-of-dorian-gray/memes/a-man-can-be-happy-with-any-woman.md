## Quotation

> "What nonsense people talk about happy marriages! A man can be happy with any woman, as long as he does not love her." (015-045)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism at his aunt Lady Agatha's lunch party, where [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is also present. The conversation has turned to marriage and society. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], as always, plays the provocateur.

The remark is a characteristic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] inversion. The conventional wisdom holds that love is the foundation of a happy marriage; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] claims it is the obstacle. His logic is that love brings expectation, jealousy, and disappointment. Without love, a man can enjoy the companionship and social convenience of marriage without emotional entanglement.

The extreme brevity of the passage — a single sentence embedded in dialogue — is itself significant. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not argue; he pronounces. His epigrams are designed to preempt debate. They are wit as seduction and wit as armour.

## Analysis

The aphorism belongs to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s broader philosophy of detachment. Love, like [[sin|morality]], like [[conscience|guilt]], like [[art-and-life|art]] — all are to be experienced aesthetically rather than morally. To love a woman is to invest emotionally; to be happy with any woman is to treat her as an aesthetic object, interchangeable and undemanding.

The implied view of women is, of course, misogynistic. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats women throughout the novel as decorative, emotional, and intellectually inferior. "Women are a decorative sex," he says elsewhere. "They never have anything to say, but they say it charmingly." This aphorism extends that logic: if all women are interchangeable objects, then attachment to any particular one is irrational.

But the aphorism also contains a darker truth about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own trajectory. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] loved [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]] — and that love destroyed her. His indifference to women thereafter — the ruined reputations, the "fatal" friendships — is precisely what [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] recommends here. The maxim functions as both description and prophecy.

The passage also contrasts with the more romantic [[romance-lives-by-repetition|Chapter 17]] speech, where [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] seems to celebrate love's singularity. The inconsistency is deliberate: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy is a performance, not a system.

## Themes

- [[love]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] redefines love as an obstacle to happiness, inverting the conventional romantic ideal.
- [[marriage]]: A minor but persistent theme in the novel — marriage as a social institution to be managed, not a romantic ideal.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: The aphorism is a pure expression of the [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] doctrine: detachment, aesthetic distance, emotional self-interest.
- [[gender]]: The implicit view of women as interchangeable objects of male convenience.
- [[cynicism]]: The aphorism's strategic cynicism — wit deployed to preempt sincerity.
