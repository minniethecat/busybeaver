## Description

Selby Royal is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s country estate, the setting for a hunting party in the novel's penultimate chapters. (003-012) It is depicted as a place of cultivated rural beauty — gardens, parkland, and woods — where the English aristocracy retreats from London. The hunting party that gathers there includes the Duchess of Monmouth and other society figures, continuing the novel's interest in the rituals of the upper class.

## Significance in the Novel

Selby Royal is the setting for [[death-of-james-vane|James Vane's death]]. After tracking [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to the estate, James is accidentally shot by a hunter who mistakes him for game. (018-006) His death removes the last external threat to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the last person who might hold him accountable. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] experiences this as a providential deliverance, but the novel frames it more darkly: the removal of consequence is itself the deepest form of damnation.

## Associated Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: His country estate, where he briefly feels safe from James's pursuit
- **[[james-vane|James Vane]]**: Dies here, ending the novel's revenge plot

## Atmosphere / Symbolism

Selby Royal's pastoral beauty is undercut by violent death — a pattern that repeats the novel's central theme of beauty concealing horror.
