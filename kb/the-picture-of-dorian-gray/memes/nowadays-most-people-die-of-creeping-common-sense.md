## Quotation

> "Yes," he continued, "that is one of the great secrets of life. Nowadays most people die of a sort of creeping common sense, and discover when it is too late that the only things one never regrets are one's mistakes." (003-022)

## Context

The setting is the Chapter 3 lunch party at [[lady-agatha|Lady Agatha]]'s. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has just advised the duchess that "to get back one's youth, one has merely to repeat one's follies" (003-021) — a remark she calls "a delightful theory" (003-021) and Sir Thomas calls "a dangerous theory" (003-022).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] then expands the thought into a philosophy of error. The sentence that follows is one of his most compact paradoxes: common sense, which Victorian culture prized as the supreme intellectual virtue, is reframed as a fatal illness — something that "creeps" and kills. And mistakes, which the same culture taught one to avoid, become the only experiences one looks back on without regret.

The narrator notes that as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] continued, "the praise of folly... soared into a philosophy" (003-023), and the passage that follows is one of the novel's most extravagant set pieces — a description of his wit as a Bacchante dancing over the hills of life.

## Analysis

The sentence is a double reversal. First, common sense — the quality that keeps one safe, solvent, and respectable — is redefined as a pathology: it "creeps," it is fatal, and its victim discovers the truth only "when it is too late." Second, mistakes — the things one is taught to avoid, the evidence of poor judgment — are redefined as the only experiences immune to regret.

The logic is psychologically acute despite its deliberate perversity. Regret attaches to caution, not to abandon. The risk one did *not* take haunts more persistently than the risk one took and lost. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is offering a justification for imprudence — and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], listening, is absorbing a philosophy that will, in time, license every imprudence he commits.

The word "creeping" does important work. It is the opposite of the dashing, the brilliant, the spontaneous — all the qualities [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] embodies. Common sense does not strike; it infiltrates. It is not a decision but a habit. To die of it is to die without having noticed one was dying.
