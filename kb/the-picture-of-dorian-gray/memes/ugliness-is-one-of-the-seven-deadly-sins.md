## Quotation

> "Ugliness is one of the seven deadly sins, then?" cried the duchess. "What becomes of your simile about the orchid?" (017-019)

## Context

In Chapter 17, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] is hosting a party at Selby Royal, his country house. Among the guests are [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and the [[duchess-of-monmouth|Duchess of Monmouth]], a clever woman who enjoys matching wits with Lord Henry. The conversation has turned to names — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is lamenting that "we have lost the faculty of giving lovely names to things" (017-004).

To illustrate, he recounts how he asked a gardener the name of "a marvellous spotted thing, as effective as the seven deadly sins" (017-004) that he had cut for his button-hole. The gardener gave him a tedious Latin botanical name. For [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], the crime is not the flower itself but the name — an ugly name for a beautiful thing.

The duchess seizes on his simile. If the orchid is "as effective as the seven deadly sins," then ugliness — like the flower's tedious name — must itself be sinful. Her question is playful but sharp: she is testing whether [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aestheticism really does collapse ethics into aesthetics. His reply — "Ugliness is one of the seven deadly virtues" (017-020) — confirms it, while wittily reversing her reversal.

## Analysis

The duchess's question does what the best dialogue in the novel does: it exposes the logic of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s position by taking it more seriously than he does. If beauty is the supreme value — if a flower's visual splendor outweighs its botanical name — then ugliness must be the supreme failing.

The "seven deadly sins" reference is not accidental. By slotting ugliness into the traditional Christian moral framework (pride, greed, wrath, envy, lust, gluttony, sloth), the duchess suggests that [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aestheticism is itself a moral system — one in which aesthetic categories have replaced ethical ones. A sin, in this new dispensation, is whatever is ugly.

Her follow-up — "What becomes of your simile about the orchid?" — restores the original comparison and shows its instability. The orchid was "as effective as the seven deadly sins" (017-004) — that is, as alluring, as seductive, as dangerous. The sins themselves are beautiful in [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s framing. The duchess has caught him in a contradiction: if sins are like beautiful orchids, then ugliness cannot be a sin.

## Themes

- [[ugliness]]: The duchess's question crystallizes the novel's treatment of ugliness as a moral category.
- [[beauty]]: The exchange reveals beauty as the unspoken premise beneath all of Lord Henry's paradoxes.
- [[morality]]: The aesthetic moral code is shown to be logically unsustainable even by its own adherents.
- [[duchess-of-monmouth]]: A rare interlocutor who can match Lord Henry's wit without being seduced by it.
