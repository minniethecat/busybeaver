## Overview

Dorian Gray is the protagonist of Wilde's novel. A [[youth|young]] man of "extraordinary personal beauty" (001-003), he becomes the subject of [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s greatest [[the-portrait|portrait]].

Under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, Dorian wishes that the [[the-portrait|portrait]] might age in his place. The wish comes true.

He pursues pleasure while his painted image grows hideous. His arc from innocent beauty to corrupt hedonist forms the novel's central tragedy.

## Biography

Dorian is introduced as a [[beauty|beautiful]], naive youth sitting for [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]]. He is the grandson of Lord Kelso, heir to a fortune.

His mother, Lady Margaret Devereux, married beneath her station and died after his birth. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] finds this background romantically tragic.

Under [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s influence, Dorian transforms. He [[transformation|fall]]s in love with actress [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], then cruelly rejects her. Her suicide marks his first visible sin on the [[the-portrait|portrait]] (008-001).

Over eighteen years, Dorian pursues darker pleasures. He murders [[basil-hallward|Basil]] (013-045) and blackmails [[alan-campbell|Alan Campbell]] into disposing of the body.

In the final chapter, he stabs the [[the-portrait|portrait]], killing himself. His body is found "withered, wrinkled, and loathsome" while the [[the-portrait|portrait]] returns to its original beauty (020-030).

- [[alan-campbell]]: The scientist blackmailed into disposing of [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s body.

## Character Analysis

Dorian is both a character and a symbol — beauty corrupted by influence and indulgence.

His defining traits are extraordinary beauty, susceptibility to influence, and progressive moral blindness. Wilde traces his development with psychological precision.

He is never simply evil. He retains moments of genuine feeling — his love for [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]], horror at [[murder-of-basil-hallward|Basil's murder]], brief resolve to [[transformation|change]].

But each moment of conscience collapses into rationalization. His tragedy is not lacking a [[conscience|moral sense]], but consistently ignoring it.

## Quotations

The awakening that sets his transformation in motion.

The Faustian wish that determines the entire novel's course.

> Each of us has Heaven and Hell in him. (019-029)

Dorian's late insight into his own duality — too late to save him.

## Thematic Significance

Dorian embodies nearly all the novel's major themes. He is the living demonstration of **[[influence]]** — a soul reshaped by [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy.

His life pursues **[[beauty]]** and **[[pleasure]]** above all else. His preserved youth and decaying [[the-portrait|portrait]] literalize **[[duality]]**.

His inability to escape **[[conscience]]** affirms the inescapability of [[morality|moral judgment]]. **[[transformation]]**, once set in motion, cannot be reversed.
