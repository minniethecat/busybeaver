## Definition

Passion in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* operates across multiple registers: artistic passion (Basil's devotion to Dorian as muse), romantic passion (Dorian's infatuation with Sibyl Vane), and the philosophical passion for experience that Lord Henry preaches. Wilde draws a crucial distinction between passion and caprice: "the difference between a caprice and a lifelong passion is that the caprice lasts a little longer" (002-055), framing genuine passion as rare and the pursuit of sensation as a poor substitute.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Basil Hallward embodies artistic passion, confessing that his portrait of Dorian contains "all the passion of the romantic spirit, all the perfection of the spirit that is Greek" (001-056). His worship of Dorian's beauty leads him to reveal "the secret of my own soul" (001-027) in the painting—a confession of passion that proves fatal.

Lord Henry articulates a philosophy that treats passion as material for aesthetic consumption. He tells Dorian that "one's own soul, and the passions of one's friends—those were the fascinating things in life" (001-067), encouraging a detached, spectatorial relationship to emotion. His influence awakens in Dorian "passions that have made you afraid" (002-026), setting in motion the transformation that the portrait records.

Dorian's grandfather, Lord Kelso, is described as having "an inordinate passion for pleasure" (003-001), foreshadowing Dorian's inherited tendency toward hedonism. Dorian's own passion for Sibyl Vane burns intensely but briefly—a caprice rather than the lifelong passion it briefly appeared to be.

## Key Characters

- **[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]**: Experiences passion as successively awakening, consuming, and destroying. His passion for Sibyl Vane transforms into cruelty when she fails to perform on stage; his later passions are catalogued in Chapter XI as an encyclopaedia of sensation-seeking.
- **[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]**: Represents the noble passion of the artist for his subject. His confession that "my life as an artist depends on him" (001-080) reveals the fatal intensity of his devotion.
- **[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]]**: Treats passion as intellectual sport. His distinction between passion and caprice (002-055) provides the novel's most precise definition.
- **[[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]**: Embodies romantic passion at its most innocent and destructive. Her willingness to sacrifice her art for love is both the height of passion and the cause of her abandonment.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats passion as a double-edged force: it is the wellspring of art and beauty, yet ungoverned passion leads to moral ruin. This tension reflects fin-de-siècle anxieties about the relationship between aestheticism and ethics. Dorian's tragedy is not that he feels passion but that he treats it as a consumer good, exhausting one object after another without ever being transformed. Basil's passion, by contrast, produces art that lasts—the portrait that becomes conscience made visible.
