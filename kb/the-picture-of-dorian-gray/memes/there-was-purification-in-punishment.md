## Quotation

> "There was purification in punishment. Not 'Forgive us our sins' but 'Smite us for our iniquities' should be the prayer of man to a most just God." (020-004)

## Context

These words appear in the final chapter, as [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] walks home through the cold dawn streets after destroying the last evidence of his crimes — the letter that could have exposed him. He has also, he believes, committed an act of mercy by sparing [[hetty-merton|Hetty Merton]], the country girl he might have ruined. He thinks this single act of renunciation might undo the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s accumulated [[sin|corruption]].

But when he examines the [[the-portrait|portrait]], it is worse than before — "through the crimson of the lips crept a look of cunning, and in the bleared eyes was a look of cruelty." His "mercy" was really vanity — a desire to experience a new sensation, the pleasure of [[morality|virtue]]. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] has seen through him, as it always does.

The epigram that follows is the novel's final theological statement. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has spent his life avoiding consequences — the [[the-portrait|portrait]] absorbed them all. Now, in his last hour, he arrives at a vision of justice in which punishment is not a horror to be escaped but a purification to be welcomed. It is the most orthodox thought [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has ever had, and it comes too late.

## Analysis

The epigram rewrites the Lord's Prayer. "Forgive us our sins" — the petition for mercy that stands at the center of Christian piety — is replaced by "Smite us for our iniquities," a petition for punishment. The theological inversion is radical: mercy is rejected as insufficient, even dishonest; only punishment can cleanse.

The word "purification" is precise. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has spent the novel accumulating [[sin|corruption]], and the [[the-portrait|portrait]] has been the ledger of that accumulation. His final insight is that [[sin|corruption]] can only be undone by its opposite — not forgiveness, which leaves the sin intact, but punishment, which burns it away. This is not masochism; it is a desperate grasping for moral order after a lifetime of moral chaos.

But the epigram is also deeply ironic. The "most just God" [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] invokes is the God he has spent his whole life ignoring. And his "prayer" is not a prayer at all but a thought — a private reflection delivered to no one. The theological language is sincere but the theological posture is absent. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] wants the effects of religion (purification, justice, absolution) without the substance (faith, repentance, community). Even in his moment of seeming moral clarity, he is still aestheticizing — still treating ideas as things to be tried on rather than truths to be lived.

## Themes

- [[punishment]]: The epigram is the novel's most direct statement on punishment as a form of grace — the thing that makes moral repair possible.
- [[purification]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s longing for purification is genuine; the question the novel leaves open is whether it is achievable.
- [[sin]]: The epigram acknowledges sin as a real category — a striking departure from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s amoral aestheticism.
- [[redemption]]: The possibility — or impossibility — of redemption hangs over the entire final chapter. The epigram gestures toward it; the ending refuses it.
- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s final thoughts are the return of the conscience he has spent a lifetime suppressing.
