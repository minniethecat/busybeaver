## Quotation

> "We can have in life but one great experience at best, and the secret of life is to reproduce that experience as often as possible." (017-067)

## Context

At [[the-duchess-of-monmouth|Lady Monmouth]]'s party, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] converses with [[the-duchess-of-monmouth|Gladys, Duchess of Monmouth]]. She has just praised constancy in love. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] objects.

He has already delivered two other aphorisms in the same exchange: "Romance lives by repetition, and repetition converts an appetite into an art" and "Each time that one loves is the only time one has ever loved. Difference of object does not alter singleness of passion. It merely intensifies it."

This third statement completes the triptych. The idea of "one great experience" suggests that all of life's affective intensity is concentrated in a single pattern. We are not capable of multiple great loves — only multiple repetitions of the first one. The "secret" is not to seek new experiences but to master the art of recurrence.

The setting is significant: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] speaks in high society, performing for an audience. His philosophy is inseparable from its social context. The aphorism is designed to provoke and charm simultaneously.

## Analysis

This aphorism is the culmination of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s theory of love. It brings together three ideas: (1) the singularity of true experience, (2) the deliberate repetition of that experience, and (3) the aesthetic nature of this project. Love is recast as a form of art-making — one does not "fall" in love; one composes it.

The word "reproduce" carries multiple meanings. In aesthetics, to reproduce is to copy, to make a facsimile. In biology, it suggests propagation, continuation. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] collapses the distinction: to repeat an experience is to give it new life. Each repetition is both copy and original.

The statement also echoes the [[preface-moral-immoral|Preface]]: "To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim." By this logic, the lover should conceal the fact of repetition — each iteration should feel like the first. The art is in the concealment.

For [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]], this philosophy has disastrous consequences. His repetitions are not of love but of sensation: each new pleasure is an attempt to reproduce the thrill of the forbidden, and each one fails. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s elegant theory collapses in practice.

## Themes

- [[love]]: The theory that love is singular and repeatable — a radical departure from romantic ideals of uniqueness.
- [[art-and-life]]: Love as an aesthetic practice, governed by the logic of art rather than emotion.
- [[lord-henrys-philosophy]]: The aphorism is a pure expression of the [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] doctrine: life is to be composed, not lived.
- [[beauty]]: The "great experience" is implicitly beautiful — one would not wish to repeat an ugly one.
