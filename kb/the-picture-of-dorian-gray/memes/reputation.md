## Definition

Reputation in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is the public face of morality — not goodness itself but the appearance of goodness. In the Victorian London Wilde depicts, reputation is the supreme social currency: it determines one's place in society, one's relationships, one's very identity. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s ability to maintain an impeccable reputation while leading a corrupt private life is both his greatest achievement and the clearest evidence of society's superficiality. The novel argues that reputation is a performance, not a reflection of character.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Reputation is what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] preserves and what his victims lose. The [[youth|young]] men whose lives he is said to have ruined lose their reputations — and with them, their social existence — while [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s own reputation remains intact. The novel repeatedly shows how reputation operates as a form of power: those with spotless reputations can destroy others with impunity, while a single scandal can obliterate a lifetime's standing. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] cynically observes this mechanism; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] exploits it.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The master of reputation management, whose spotless public image conceals—and enables—his private [[sin|corruption]]
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The cynic who understands that reputation is a game and teaches [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] how to play it

## Key Passages

> Society — civilized society, at least — is never very ready to believe anything to the detriment of those who are both rich and fascinating. (011-008)

The narrator's acid commentary on how wealth and charm insulate the privileged from the consequences of their actions.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde knew the power and fragility of reputation firsthand — his own public down[[transformation|fall]] for "gross indecency" would come just five years after the novel's publication. The novel reads as both a critique of reputation as a social mechanism and a prophecy of its author's fate. Wilde exposes reputation as a fiction maintained by hypocrisy, yet the novel's plot demonstrates that losing one's reputation is devastating — a tension that gives the theme its particular poignancy.
