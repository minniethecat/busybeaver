## Quotation

> "Oh, she is better than good—she is beautiful," murmured Lord Henry, sipping a glass of vermouth and orange-bitters. "Dorian says she is beautiful, and he is not often wrong about things of that kind. Your portrait of him has quickened his appreciation of the personal appearance of other people. It has had that excellent effect, amongst others. We are to see her to-night, if that boy doesn't forget his appointment." (006-015)

## Context

In Chapter 6, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] and [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] are discussing [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]'s sudden engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]], an actress [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] encountered at [[the-theatre|a shabby little theatre]] (005-022). [[basil-hallward|Basil]], concerned for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s welfare, expresses the hope that "the girl is good, [[lord-henry-wotton|Harry]]" (006-014) — he fears [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] being "tied to some vile creature, who might degrade his nature and ruin his intellect" (006-014).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s reply — "she is better than good—she is [[beauty|beautiful]]" — dismisses moral categories entirely. The statement is delivered not as a reasoned argument but as a murmured aside over a glass of vermouth, the very casualness of its delivery heightening its subversive effect. The claim that beauty outranks goodness as a criterion of value is a concise expression of the aesthetic doctrine [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] has been preaching since Chapter 2.

## Analysis

The sentence operates through a simple but devastating rhetorical move: it takes a conventional hierarchy (moral goodness as the highest value) and inverts it without argument. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not say "she is good *and* [[beauty|beautiful]]" or even "beauty is a kind of goodness." He says beauty is *better* than goodness — a direct, unqualified substitution of aesthetic value for moral value.

The structure of the remark is an epigram: short, balanced, and paradoxical. The expectation set up by "better than good" is that the second term will be some elevated moral state — virtuous, saintly, pure. Instead, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] supplies "[[beauty|beautiful]]," collapsing the moral vocabulary into the aesthetic. This is how [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] operates throughout the novel: not by persuasion through evidence, but by pronouncement through wit.

The remark also reveals something about [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s effect on [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. He notes that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s [[the-portrait|portrait]] "has quickened [[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s] appreciation of the personal appearance of other people" (006-015) — an observation that links [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s aesthetic awakening directly to the [[the-portrait|portrait]], the very object that will later become the instrument of his moral degradation.

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats beauty as a self-justifying value that supersedes moral categories — a core tenet of the novel's aesthetic philosophy.
- [[art-and-aesthetics]]: The remark equates personal attractiveness with artistic merit, extending aesthetic judgment into the moral domain — just as the novel itself does.
- [[morality-and-ethics]]: By positioning beauty *above* goodness, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] undermines the very framework of moral evaluation that characters like [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] represent.
