## Quotation

> "I wish it were *fin du globe*," said Dorian with a sigh. "Life is a great disappointment." (015-039)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] speaks these words at Lady Narborough's dinner party, the evening after he has murdered [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in the attic room of his house, and blackmailed [[alan-campbell]] into destroying the body with chemicals. The dinner is a small, rather tedious affair — Lady Narborough's daughter has arrived unexpectedly — but [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives late and provides [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] with the relief of familiar company.

The conversation turns to the *fin de siècle* mood, with Lady Narborough quipping "*Fin du globe*." [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response — "I wish it were *fin du globe*. Life is a great disappointment" — is the one moment of raw truth he speaks all evening. The other guests hear it as fashionable Weltschmerz, the kind of cultivated ennui expected of a [[youth|young]] man-about-town. But the reader, who knows what [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has done, hears something far darker: a confession of [[soul|spirit]]ual exhaustion from a man who has passed beyond moral horror into something like emptiness.

Lady Narborough's reply — "When a man says that one knows that life has exhausted him" — is shrewder than she knows. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is not tired in the way of a jaded aristocrat; he is exhausted by the weight of his own crimes, which he must carry in secret while maintaining the mask of charm and ease. The epigram is the novel's most economical statement of its central tragedy: the man who has everything has discovered that everything is not enough.

## Analysis

The phrase functions on three levels. On the surface, it is drawing-room wit — the fashionable pessimism of the 1890s. One level down, it is the private despair of a murderer sitting among people who do not know what he is. At the deepest level, it is a theological statement: a life lived entirely for sensation, beauty, and self-gratification turns out to be a life of profound disappointment.

The word "disappointment" is precise. It is not terror or [[conscience|guilt]] that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] names, though both are present in the chapter. It is disappointment — the gap between what was promised and what was delivered. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s philosophy had promised that a life devoted to beauty and sensation would be a life of perpetual ecstasy. Instead it has produced murder, blackmail, paranoia, and a dinner party where the only interesting person is the man who corrupted you in the first place.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s sigh — the stage direction Wilde includes — is telling. It is not a cry or a confession but a sigh, the smallest possible unit of despair, the kind that can pass unnoticed in polite company. It is exactly the gesture of a man who has learned that the most terrible things must be hidden behind the most ordinary ones.

## Themes

- [[disillusionment]]: The central emotion — the collapse of the hedonistic promise that pleasure alone can sustain a life.
- [[morality]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s despair is the indirect consequence of his crimes, which have not brought satisfaction but emptiness.
- [[double-life|the double life]]: The gap between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s public charm and private horror is compressed into a single, ambiguous sentence at a dinner table.
