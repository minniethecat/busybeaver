## Quotation

> "Poets are not so scrupulous as you are. They know how useful passion is for publication. Nowadays a broken heart will run to many editions." (001-061)
>
> — Lord Henry Wotton, to Basil Hallward, Chapter 1

## Context

Lord Henry delivers this epigram during his first extended conversation with [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] in the painter's studio-garden. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just explained why he refuses to exhibit his portrait of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]: "I have put into it some expression of all this curious artistic idolatry... My heart shall never be put under their microscope. There is too much of myself in the thing, Harry — too much of myself!" (001-060).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] counters with this characteristically cynical observation. For him, art and emotion are not sacred — they are material. The poet's "broken heart" is not a private wound but a public asset, something to be packaged and sold. The metaphor of "editions" reduces the deepest human feeling to a commercial print run.

The epigram marks an early articulation of the philosophy [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will practice throughout the novel: detachment, wit, and the reduction of the serious to the aesthetic.

## Analysis

The epigram operates on several levels. Most immediately, it deflates [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s romantic self-seriousness. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] speaks of his soul, his heart, his idolatry; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] replies with "editions" — a word from the counting-house. The technique is characteristic: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] consistently punctures high sentiment with low practicality.

More subtly, the epigram introduces a theme that will haunt the novel: the relationship between suffering and art. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] believes the artist's suffering sanctifies the work; [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] suggests that suffering is merely useful — a raw material no different from paint or marble. This debate — is art confession or craft? — runs through the entire narrative.

The phrase "run to many editions" is also a wry self-portrait. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is himself a kind of poet of conversation, packaging his own cynicism into portable epigrams designed for maximum circulation. He knows exactly what he is doing.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The epigram collapses the boundary between genuine feeling and artistic production, suggesting that emotion exists to be exploited.
- [[artists-and-models]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s view of art as commerce stands in direct opposition to [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s view of art as revelation.
- [[cynicism]]: The remark is a pure specimen of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s worldview — worldly, reductive, and irresistibly quotable.
