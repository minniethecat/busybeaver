## Overview

"A new Hedonism" is the phrase Lord Henry Wotton uses in Chapter 2 to describe the philosophical creed he offers to the young Dorian Gray. He declares that "a new Hedonism — that is what our century wants" (002-048), framing the pursuit of pleasure and sensation as a progressive, even revolutionary, project that would liberate the individual from the dead weight of conventional morality.

## Origins and Articulation

Lord Henry first expounds the doctrine during his fateful conversation with Dorian in Basil Hallward's garden. His speech moves from a meditation on youth and beauty to a full-throated call for a revaluation of values: "Be always searching for new sensations. Be afraid of nothing" (002-048). He presents this new Hedonism as the answer to the spiritual exhaustion of the late Victorian age, a way to restore intensity and meaning to a life rendered dull by propriety and convention.

## Dorian's Embodiment

The novel chronicles the working-out of this philosophy in Dorian's life. Chapter 11 notes that "Lord Henry had prophesied a new Hedonism that was to recreate life and to save it from that harsh, uncomely puritanism that is having, in our own day, its curious revival" (011-010), and describes how Dorian pursued this creed through the study of perfumes, music, jewels, embroideries, and, ultimately, darker sensations. The new Hedonism becomes the intellectual justification for Dorian's double life, transforming what might otherwise appear as mere dissipation into a kind of aesthetic experiment.

## Critical Significance

The concept of a "new Hedonism" is central to the novel's engagement with the aesthetic and decadent movements of the 1890s. It echoes Walter Pater's call to "burn always with a hard, gemlike flame" and the broader fin-de-siècle reaction against Victorian moralism. Yet Wilde's treatment is deeply ambivalent: the philosophy is seductive in its articulation but catastrophic in its consequences, raising the question of whether the novel endorses or indicts the creed it dramatizes.
