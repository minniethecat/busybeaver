## Quotation

> "Laughter is not at all a bad beginning for a friendship, and it is far the best ending for one." (001-043)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this aphorism while plucking a daisy in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s garden, after [[basil-hallward|Basil]] recounts how he first met [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] had described Lady Brandon's disastrous attempt at a salon — a story so absurd that [[basil-hallward|Basil]] and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] "could not help laughing, and became friends at once." (001-042) [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]], ever the aphorist, distills the anecdote into a maxim.

The physical setting is significant: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is literally pulling apart a flower as he speaks, a gesture of casual destructiveness that prefigures his treatment of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]. The daisy, traditional symbol of innocence and simplicity, is dismembered by the very hands that are constructing a philosophy of friendship as elegant detachment.

## Analysis

The aphorism's structure moves from understatement ("not at all a bad beginning") to superlative ("far the best ending"), a rhetorical arc that mirrors friendship's ideal trajectory from ease to final grace. The word "ending" carries a double weight in the context of the novel: it can mean the natural conclusion of a friendship, but it also glances at death — the ultimate ending. In this light, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] is saying that laughter is the best way to conclude anything, including a life.

The remark is also, characteristically, a moral evasion. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has just earnestly described his friendship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] as something transformative and profound. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response domesticates that intensity into an elegant formula, reducing friendship to a matter of style and timing rather than loyalty, sacrifice, or love. This is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s consistent rhetorical strategy: to translate moral categories into aesthetic ones, making everything a question of taste.

For the novel's plot, the aphorism is darkly ironic. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s friendship with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — which begins with laughter in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s studio — ends with ruin and death. The laughter that began it does not return to close it. The aphorism describes an ideal that the novel's action systematically refutes.

## Themes

- [[friendship]]: The aphorism offers [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s aestheticized theory of friendship — a relationship governed by wit, not obligation.
- [[art-and-life]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] treats friendship as an art form, subject to criteria of style and timing rather than moral substance.
- [[influence]]: The daisy-plucking gesture, simultaneous with the aphorism's delivery, foreshadows [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s destructive influence over [[dorian-gray|Dorian]].
