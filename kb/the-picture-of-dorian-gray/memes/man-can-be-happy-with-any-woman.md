## Quotation

> "What nonsense people talk about happy marriages! A man can be happy with any woman, as long as he does not love her." (015-045)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram at Lady Narborough's dinner party in Chapter 15. The conversation has turned to the Duchess of Monmouth and her marital plans. Lady Narborough, playing matchmaker, declares that the duchess and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] must "[[transformation|fall]] in love" and that theirs should be "a suitable alliance" where "both shall be happy" (015-044).

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response is immediate and dismissive. "What nonsense people talk about happy marriages!" The aphorism that follows — "A man can be happy with any woman, as long as he does not love her" — reduces the entire institution of marriage to a paradox: happiness in marriage is possible only in the absence of love. Love brings expectation, jealousy, and disappointment; indifference brings peace.

Lady Narborough's reply — "Ah! what a cynic you are!" (015-046) — captures the dinner-party reception of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s wit: it is consumed as entertainment, not taken as counsel. The novel's structure repeatedly tests whether such epigrams can be safely enjoyed without being enacted.

## Analysis

The aphorism inverts the conventional wisdom that love is the foundation of a happy marriage. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] argues the opposite: love is the obstacle to marital contentment. His logic is that love introduces emotional investment, and investment leads to suffering. A marriage without love is a contract without liability.

The epigram belongs to [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s broader philosophy of detachment. Earlier in the novel he advised [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] that "the aim of life is self-development" (002-016) and that influence is "immoral" because "to influence a person is to give him one's own soul" (002-017). The happy marriage, in this framework, is one in which neither party attempts to influence the other — a state achievable only through indifference.

Like all of [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s best epigrams, the statement is at once absurd and uncomfortably plausible. The novel's own marriages — Lady Narborough's, the Vanes', the various unions mentioned in passing — offer no counterexample.
