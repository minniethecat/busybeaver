## Quotation

> "Sin is a thing that writes itself across a man's face. It cannot be concealed. People talk sometimes of secret vices. There are no such things. If a wretched man has a vice, it shows itself in the lines of his mouth, the droop of his eyelids, the moulding of his hands even." (012-019)
>
> — Basil Hallward, to Dorian Gray, Chapter 12

## Context

[[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] visits [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] on the eve of his departure for Paris. He has heard the rumours circulating about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in London society — that "so many gentlemen in London will neither go to your house or invite you to theirs," that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s friendship is "fatal to young men" (012-019). [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has come to confront [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] directly, to demand to know the truth.

But [[basil-hallward|Basil]] cannot sustain his accusation. He looks at [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face — still "pure, bright, innocent" — and his faith in physiognomy overpowers the evidence of rumour. "I can't believe them when I see you," he says (012-019). The irony is devastating: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s belief that sin is visible on the face is the very belief that makes him unable to see the sin standing directly in front of him. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face has not changed — [[the-portrait|the portrait]] has changed instead. The entire supernatural premise of the novel is a refutation of [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s theory.

## Analysis

This passage is the novel's most sustained examination of the relationship between appearance and moral reality. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is an artist; he believes that surfaces tell the truth. His profession has trained him to read faces, to trust what he sees. The idea that sin writes itself "across a man's face" — in "the lines of his mouth, the droop of his eyelids, the moulding of his hands even" — is essentially an aesthetic theory of morality. Goodness is beautiful; wickedness is ugly; and you can tell which is which by looking.

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]] is the living refutation of this theory. He is beautiful and he is corrupt, and the fact that these two properties can coexist in the same person is the novel's central horror. [[the-portrait|The portrait]] is the device Wilde uses to make the contradiction visible: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face remains innocent because [[the-portrait|the portrait]] absorbs the visible evidence of his sin. The painting is [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s theory made literal — sin *does* write itself across a face; it just happens to be the face in the attic, not the face in the drawing room.

There is also a professional tragedy here. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] is the creator of [[the-portrait|the portrait]], and he believes his creation captures [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "real" self. He is right, but not in the way he thinks. The portrait does register moral truth — just not on the surface where society can see it. [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s faith in appearances is both his greatness as an artist and his blindness as a man. When he finally sees the degraded portrait in Chapter 13, he sees the truth his own theory should have prepared him for — and it destroys him.

## Themes

- [[sin]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s theory of visible sin is the moral framework the novel exists to complicate.
- [[soul]]: The implicit claim is that sin marks the soul, and the soul marks the face; [[the-portrait|the portrait]] externalises this process.
- [[the-portrait]]: The entire passage functions as dramatic irony — the reader knows about [[the-portrait|the portrait]], and knows that [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s trust in appearances is tragically misplaced.
- [[morality]]: [[basil-hallward|Basil]] represents a conventional morality in which virtue and vice are publicly legible categories.
