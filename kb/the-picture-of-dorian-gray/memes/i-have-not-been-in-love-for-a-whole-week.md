## Quotation

> "Dear Lady Narborough, I have not been in love for a whole week — not, in fact, since Madame de Ferrol left town." (015-011)

## Context

[[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] speaks these words at Lady Narborough's dinner party in Chapter 15, in response to Lady Narborough's teasing remark that she believes he is "in love" and "afraid to tell me for fear I should be jealous" (015-010).

The dinner party occurs late in the novel, long after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's death]], [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s murder, and [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s descent into the [[duality|double life]]. Yet here [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] appears in his public persona: charming, witty, entirely at ease among the aristocracy. His remark about Madame de Ferrol — a sophisticated Frenchwoman with a reputation — signals his immersion in the worldly society [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] inhabits.

Lady Narborough exclaims, "How you men can [[transformation|fall]] in love with that woman! I really cannot understand it" (015-012). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] supplies the explanation: "She is the one link between us and your short frocks" (015-013) — Madame de Ferrol remembers Lady Narborough as a child.

The entire ex[[transformation|change]] is light, performative, and utterly detached from the darkness [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] carries. The gap between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s dinner-party wit and the hideous [[the-portrait|portrait]] upstairs in the locked schoolroom is the structural irony of the chapter.

## Analysis

The line is [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] doing his best [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] impression. The temporal precision — "a whole week" — treats love as a measurable commodity, something consumed and replenished on a schedule. The casual qualifier "not, in fact, since Madame de Ferrol left town" places the affair in the recent past with the nonchalance of a weather report.

The remark reveals how far [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has travelled from the earnest [[youth|young]] man who trembled at [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]'s name. In Chapter 4, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] blushed at the word "actress." Here, he discusses his romantic history as dinner-party entertainment. The transformation is complete: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has become the creature [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] described — a man for whom experience is material for epigrams.

Yet the line also hints at the emptiness beneath. A week without love is, for [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], a notable duration. The relentless pursuit of new sensation — the life [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] promised — has become a treadmill. Madame de Ferrol is already in the past; the next affair is already being planned. The epigram conceals but does not heal.
