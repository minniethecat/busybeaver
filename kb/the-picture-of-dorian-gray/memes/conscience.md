## Definition

Conscience in *The Picture of [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]]* is the internal moral sense that registers the difference between right and wrong. Wilde portrays it not as a guide to virtuous action but as a source of torment — an unwanted guest that disturbs the pursuit of pleasure. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s tragedy lies not in lacking a conscience but in systematically ignoring it, until its accumulated weight becomes unbearable. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] serves as an externalized conscience, making visible the [[sin|moral decay]] that [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s face conceals.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Conscience operates on two levels in the novel. On the psychological level, it appears as [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moments of doubt and self-reproach — his reflection after [[sibyl-vanes-suicide|Sibyl Vane's death]], his fear of discovery, his periodic resolutions to reform. On the symbolic level, it is the [[the-portrait|portrait]] itself: a visible record of every sin, growing more hideous with each [[sin|transgression]].

After killing [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]], [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s conscience becomes inescapable. He cannot look at the [[the-portrait|portrait]] without being reminded of his guilt. His attempt to destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]] — and thereby silence his conscience — results in his own death. The novel suggests that conscience, however suppressed, cannot be permanently evaded.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: The protagonist who wages a lifelong war against his own conscience, seeking to live beyond good and evil, and who is ultimately destroyed by the guilt he tried to escape.
- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: The voice of conscience external to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — the friend who urges him to reform, to pray, to repent. His murder represents [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s most decisive act of conscience-suppression.
- **[[James Vane]]**: The agent of retribution whose pursuit of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] reawakens his dormant fear and guilt.

## Key Passages

> He felt a wild longing for the unstained purity of his boyhood — his rose-white boyhood, as Lord Henry had once called it. (010-028)

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s moment of moral clarity — an awareness of what he has lost. The passage reveals that conscience has never fully left him, even at his most corrupt.

> It was conscience, and not cowardice, that made him stay. He knew that he had tarnished himself, filled his mind with corruption, and given horror its stupendous shape; but was it all irretrievable? (010-030)

A rare moment of explicit moral reflection. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] confronts the possibility that his [[sin|corruption]] may be permanent — and briefly considers whether it is not.

> There was nothing to be done. There was nothing to be hoped for. The portrait itself — that was the evidence. He would destroy it. (020-010)

The novel's climax: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s decision to destroy the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is simultaneously an act of conscience-destruction. He seeks to kill the only witness to his guilt — but the [[the-portrait|portrait]] is inextricably linked to his own life.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde treats conscience with the same ambivalence he brings to all moral concepts. The novel can be read as a critique of conscience as mere social conditioning — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] dismisses it as "the trade-name of the firm." Yet the narrative itself demonstrates that conscience is real and consequential. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] cannot simply decide to live without it; the [[the-portrait|portrait]] ensures that his sins are recorded and that the reckoning, when it comes, is absolute.

This dual treatment reflects the tension in Wilde's own work between aestheticism (which rejects [[morality|moral judgment]] of art) and the undeniable moral force of the traditional fable form. The novel is simultaneously an immoral book (as the Preface claims) and a deeply moral one.
