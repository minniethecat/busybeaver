## Quotation

> "The prayer of your pride has been answered. The prayer of your repentance will be answered also. I worshipped you too much. I am punished for it. You worshipped yourself too much. We are both punished." (013-028)

## Context

This is [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s last attempt to save [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] — and his last words before Dorian murders him.

Basil has come to Dorian's house before his planned departure for Paris, wanting to confront the rumours that have circulated through London society for years. He has demanded to see [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s soul, and Dorian — in a mood of "mad hungered pride" (013-013) — has taken him upstairs to the locked schoolroom and shown him [[the-portrait|the portrait]].

The sight is horrific. The portrait that Basil himself painted, the "fairest thing that I had ever done in my life" (013-020), has become a "foul parody" — "a leprosies of sin" consuming the canvas (013-021). Basil has recoiled, wept, begged Dorian to deny what he sees. But Dorian has only watched him "with a feeling of infinite contempt" (013-024).

Now Basil turns from the portrait and kneels. He tries to remember a prayer, fumbling for the words of his boyhood faith. What emerges is not a conventional prayer but a theological diagnosis of the relationship between the two men.

## Analysis

The passage is structured as three parallel clauses, each identifying a symmetrical relationship between Basil and Dorian. The "prayer of your pride" — Dorian's wish, uttered in Basil's studio years ago, that the portrait should age instead of him — has been answered. The portrait has borne the burden of his sins; his beauty remains untouched. The "prayer of your repentance" — Basil's hope that Dorian might yet turn back — will be answered also, though not in the way Basil imagines. Dorian's repentance, when it comes in Chapter 20, will take the form of destruction, not redemption.

The second and third sentences name the sin: idolatry. "I worshipped you too much" — Basil confesses his own transgression, the aesthetic worship that made the portrait possible in the first place. "You worshipped yourself too much" — Dorian's sin is the mirror image of Basil's. Both men have broken the first commandment, but in different directions: Basil outward toward beauty, Dorian inward toward his own image.

"We are both punished." The sentence is devastating in its finality. Basil offers no escape, no forgiveness — only the shared acknowledgment of mutual destruction. He has come to save Dorian and ends by condemning them both. The pronoun "we" is the last word of community between them; moments later, Dorian will seize the knife.

## Themes

- [[pride]]: Dorian's "prayer of pride" — his wish to remain forever beautiful — is the original sin from which all others follow.
- [[repentance]]: Basil's faith in repentance is genuine but impotent; the novel raises the possibility of repentance only to demonstrate its impossibility for Dorian.
- [[worship]]: The passage recasts the Basil–Dorian relationship as mutual idolatry — the artist worshipping his creation, the creation worshipping itself.
- [[punishment]]: "We are both punished" — punishment is not deferred to an afterlife but is already present, inscribed on the portrait and in the corrupted bond between the two men.
- [[basil-hallward]]: Basil's final act is to name the spiritual truth of their relationship; it is his most lucid moment and his last.
