## Quotation

> "To see my soul!" muttered Dorian Gray, starting up from the sofa and turning almost white from fear. (012-024)

## Context

This exclamation occurs during Basil Hallward's midnight confrontation with [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in Chapter 12. [[basil-hallward|Basil]] has been cataloguing the terrible rumours circulating about [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] in London society — stories of ruined reputations, corrupted young men, and houses where [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s presence brings shame. After listing these charges, [[basil-hallward|Basil]] declares: "I don't know whether it is so or not. How should I know? But it is said of you. ... Know you? I wonder do I know you? Before I could answer that, I should have to see your soul" (012-023).

[[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s response — starting up from the sofa, turning white — is the first crack in his facade. His terror is not at the accusations (which he deflects with contemptuous ease) but at the word *soul*, which touches the one thing he cannot let anyone see: [[the-portrait|the portrait]] hidden in the schoolroom.

## Analysis

The exclamation is remarkable for what it does not say. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] does not deny that his soul is hideous; he reacts with pure, uncalculated terror at the thought of its being seen. The word *soul* is the one word that can penetrate his elaborate defences — the wit, the indifference, the "curl of contempt" (012-020) with which he dismisses [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s accusations. In the economy of the novel, *soul* is the word that bridges the moral and the visible: the portrait is the soul made manifest.

The physical description — "starting up from the sofa," "turning almost white from fear" — registers the shock in bodily terms. This is the only moment in the chapter when [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s body betrays him. For the rest of the confrontation, he controls his expression and his posture. But the word *soul* triggers an involuntary response, a visceral reminder that his preserved youth is a lie.

The exclamation also functions as a narrative hinge. It transforms [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s moral appeal into a fatal dare. Within moments, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] will seize a lamp and lead [[basil-hallward|Basil]] upstairs to "[[i-shall-show-you-my-soul|show you my soul]]" (012-028) — a decision that will end in [[basil-hallward|Basil]]'s murder.

## Themes

- [[soul]]: The word *soul* is the trigger that shatters Dorian's composure, linking the moral and the visible.
- [[secrecy]]: Dorian's terror is the terror of exposure — the one secret that must never be revealed.
- [[the-portrait]]: The portrait is the soul made visible, the thing Basil unknowingly asks to see.
