## Quotation

> "Never marry at all, Dorian. Men marry because they are tired; women, because they are curious: both are disappointed." (004-021)
>
> — [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry Wotton]], Chapter 4

## Context

The aphorism is delivered at [[lady-agatha|Lady Agatha]]'s luncheon party, a month after [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] has announced his engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] arrives late — "So sorry I am late, Dorian. I went to look after a piece of old brocade in Wardour Street and had to bargain for hours for it. Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing" (004-014) — and immediately begins to dominate the conversation.

When the topic turns to marriage, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] directs the remark specifically to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] — "Never marry at all, Dorian" — making the general aphorism personal. The structure of the sentence is a chiasmus (men/tired :: women/curious, both/disappointed) that presents gendered folly as symmetrical. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] deflects: "I don't think I am likely to marry, Harry. I am too much in love. That is one of your aphorisms. I am putting it into practice, as I do everything that you say" (004-022).

The irony is layered: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] has just become engaged, and his protestation that he is "too much in love" to marry is itself a Lord Henry paradox — a self-contradiction that reveals the aphorism's influence.

## Analysis

The aphorism's structure is deceptively simple. The imperative "Never marry at all" is followed by a sociological diagnosis disguised as wit. "Men marry because they are tired" suggests exhaustion with the pursuit of pleasure; "women, because they are curious" implies a desire for novelty that marriage cannot satisfy. The colon introduces the shared fate: "both are disappointed."

The disappointment is universal and symmetrical — Lord Henry offers no alternative, no model of a happy marriage. The aphorism belongs to his broader program of aesthetic individualism, in which any permanent attachment is a compromise of freedom. Marriage is the institution that most visibly binds one person to another, and for that reason it cannot survive Lord Henry's critique.

The line also functions dramatically. Spoken to [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] a month into his engagement, it is a seed of doubt planted in fertile ground. When [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] replies that he is "putting into practice" Lord Henry's aphorisms, he reveals how completely he has internalized a worldview that should make his own engagement impossible.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The aphorism treats marriage not as a moral or religious institution but as an aesthetic experience — and finds it aesthetically unsatisfactory.
- [[morality]]: Lord Henry's wit is a solvent of conventional morality; marriage, the most conventional of institutions, dissolves under it.
