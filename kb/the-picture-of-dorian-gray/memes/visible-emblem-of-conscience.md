## Quotation

> "This portrait would be to him the most magical of mirrors. As it had revealed to him his own body, so it would reveal to him his own soul. And when winter came upon it, he would still be standing where spring trembles on the verge of summer. When the blood crept from its face, and left behind a pallid mask of chalk with leaden eyes, he would keep the glamour of boyhood. Not one blossom of his loveliness would ever fade. Not one pulse of his life would ever weaken. Like the gods of the Greeks, he would be strong, and fleet, and joyous. What did it matter what happened to the coloured image on the canvas? He would be safe. That was everything. … For every sin that he committed, a stain would fleck and wreck its fairness. But he would not sin. The picture, changed or unchanged, would be to him the visible emblem of conscience. He would resist temptation." (007-069)

## Context

In Chapter 7, [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] stands before his finished [[the-portrait|portrait]] in [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]]'s studio, the day after his cruel rejection of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. He has just noticed the first trace of cruelty around the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s mouth — the [[the-portrait|painting]] has [[transformation|change]]d. Instead of horror, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] feels fascination.

This passage is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s internal monologue (rendered through free indirect discourse by the narrator). He reasons that the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s ability to register [[sin|moral decay]] is actually a gift: it will bear the burden of aging and sin while he remains physically perfect. The line "the visible emblem of conscience" is the climax of this reasoning — [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] recognizes the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s moral function and resolves to use it as a guide *against* sinning.

The resolution "He would resist temptation" is heavy with dramatic irony. Within pages, [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] will arrive with news of [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death and persuade [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to view the [[the-portrait|portrait]] not as a call to [[morality|virtue]] but as an "enduring pleasure."

## Analysis

The phrase "visible emblem of conscience" crystallizes the novel's central conceit: the [[the-portrait|portrait]] as externalized [[conscience|moral sense]]. In Wilde's inversion, conscience is not an [[conscience|inner voice]] but an image one can look at — or lock away. The word "emblem" is carefully chosen: it suggests a symbolic representation rather than conscience itself, implying a distance between the sign and what it signifies.

The passage also reveals [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s fundamental misunderstanding of his own nature. He believes the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s warning function will be sufficient to keep him virtuous: "But he would not sin." This is wishful thinking dressed as rational planning. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] shows consequences but does not prevent them — it is a mirror, not a teacher.

The religious undertone is unmistakable. The [[the-portrait|portrait]] functions as a secularized soul-book: a visible record of moral state previously accessible only to God. By making the soul visible, Wilde asks whether seeing our sins would make us sin less — a question the novel answers with a grim negative.

## Themes

- [[art-and-life]]: The [[the-portrait|portrait]] blurs the boundary between representation and reality — art literally registers moral truth that the living body conceals.
- [[sin]]: This passage sets up the novel's theory of sin as something that *marks* — visible, cumulative, irreversible.
- [[conscience]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] identifies conscience as something external and visible, foreshadowing his attempt to destroy it by slashing the [[the-portrait|portrait]] in Chapter 20.
