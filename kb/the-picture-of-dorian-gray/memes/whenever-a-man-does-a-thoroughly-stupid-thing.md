## Quotation

> "Whenever a man does a thoroughly stupid thing, it is always from the noblest motives." (006-014)

## Context

[[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] delivers this epigram to [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] during their discussion of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane]]. [[basil-hallward|Basil]], anxious about the match, points out [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s "birth, and position, and wealth" — arguing it would be "absurd for him to marry so much beneath him." [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s response is characteristically perverse: if [[basil-hallward|Basil]] wants [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] to *marry* the girl, he should simply tell [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] *not* to — because "he is sure to do it, then."

The aphorism that follows generalizes this perversity into a law of human nature. Noble motives — duty, [[reputation|honor]], love — are the engines of folly. Self-interest, by implication, is the safer guide.

## Analysis

The aphorism inverts the conventional relationship between motive and outcome. Conventionally, good motives produce good actions; bad motives produce bad ones. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] collapses this distinction: the *best* motives produce the *worst* results. The inversion is comic on the surface but corrosive underneath — it suggests that moral reasoning itself is a trap.

The structure of the sentence reinforces its [[the-price-of-everything|cynicism]]. "Thoroughly stupid" is intensified by "always" and placed against "noblest motives" — the superlatives work against each other, producing the paradoxical frisson that is [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s signature. The aphorism works as a miniature argument: premise (men do stupid things), apparent cause (noble motives), conclusion (therefore moral reasoning is self-defeating).

In the novel's plot, the aphorism proves prophetic. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s noblest impulses — his resolution to reform after [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]'s death, his determination to spare Hetty Merton — arrive too late or prove hollow. The novel seems to confirm [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s cynical logic even as it dramatizes its destructive consequences.

## Themes

- [[human-nature]]: The aphorism makes a universal claim about human psychology — that noble motives are inherently dangerous.
- [[marriage]]: The immediate context is [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s engagement to [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl]]; the aphorism serves as [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s commentary on marriage as a "thoroughly stupid thing."
- [[morality]]: The epigram undermines moral reasoning itself, suggesting that ethical deliberation leads to folly.
- [[influence]]: This is one of the key aphorisms that shape [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s worldview, contributing to his eventual moral paralysis.
