## Quotation

> "Always! That is a dreadful word. It makes me shudder when I hear it. Women are so fond of using it. They spoil every romance by trying to make it last for ever. It is a meaningless word, too. The only difference between a caprice and a lifelong passion is that the caprice lasts a little longer." (002-055)

## Context

In Chapter 2, after [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s great speech on youth (002-048), he and [[dorian-gray|Dorian Gray]] walk back toward [[basils-studio|the studio]]. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], still reeling from [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]]'s words, says: "Yes, I am glad now. I wonder shall I always be glad?" (002-054). [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] seizes on the word "always" and responds with this aphoristic dismissal.

This is a characteristic [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] move: take a casual word from his interlocutor, extract it, and spin a philosophy around it. [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s innocent "always" becomes an occasion for a miniature treatise on the superiority of the temporary over the permanent.

## Analysis

The passage enacts its own argument. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] does not refute permanence with logic — he dismisses it with style. "It makes me shudder" is a visceral reaction, not a reasoned position. The shudder is the argument.

The attack on "women" is a characteristic Wildean epigram: the gendered generalization is the vehicle, the philosophical point is the payload. The real target is not women but the concept of eternal love — the idea that a romance gains value by lasting. [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] reverses this: duration does not deepen a feeling, it merely stretches it thin.

The final aphorism — "the only difference between a caprice and a lifelong passion is that the caprice lasts a little longer" — is a masterpiece of inversion. It takes the conventional hierarchy (passion > caprice) and reverses it by collapsing the distinction entirely. If the difference is only one of duration, then a lifelong passion is simply a caprice that hasn't ended yet.

This ethic of impermanence directly enables [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s later life: if nothing needs to last, if "always" is a dreadful word, then no commitment binds, no promise constrains, and pleasure is the only remaining metric.

## Themes

- [[transience]]: [[lord-henry-wotton|Lord Henry]] valorizes the temporary — the caprice over the passion, the moment over the lifetime. This is the temporal dimension of his aestheticism.
- [[pleasure]]: If nothing is permanent, pleasure becomes the only defensible pursuit. The rejection of "always" is the philosophical precondition for [[a-new-hedonism]].
- [[love]]: The passage dismisses romantic love as a category error — an attempt to impose permanence on something that should be fleeting.
