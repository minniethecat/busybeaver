## Quotation

> He grew more and more enamoured of his own beauty, more and more interested in the corruption of his own soul. He would examine with minute care, and sometimes with a monstrous and terrible delight, the hideous lines that seared the wrinkling forehead or crawled around the heavy sensual mouth, wondering sometimes which were the more horrible, the signs of sin or the signs of age. (011-004)

## Context

Chapter 11 covers eighteen years in a single extended passage — one of the most remarkable structural choices in the novel. After [[sibyl-vane|Sibyl Vane's]] death and his discovery of the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s magical property, [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] embarks on a [[duality|double life]]: outwardly the polished [[youth|young]] gentleman of [[high-society|London society]], inwardly a man pursuing every sensation and sin the world offers.

The narrator describes [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s secret ritual: returning home from one of his "mysterious and prolonged absences," he would creep upstairs to the locked attic room, open the door with the key that never leaves him, and stand before [[the-portrait|the portrait]] — comparing the fair [[youth|young]] face in his mirror with the increasingly hideous face on the canvas. The contrast does not horrify him; it *quickens his sense of pleasure*.

## Analysis

The sentence is structured as a chiasmus: enamoured of beauty → interested in [[sin|corruption]]. The two clauses are grammatically parallel but morally opposed, and their juxtaposition captures the novel's central paradox: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s [[vanity|self-love]] and self-destruction are the same impulse.

The phrase "enamoured of his own beauty" echoes the myth of Narcissus, but with a crucial Wildean twist. Narcissus fell in love with his reflection not knowing it was himself; [[dorian-gray|Dorian]] [[transformation|fall]]s in love with his own beauty precisely *because* he knows it is himself, and because he can compare it to the [[the-portrait|portrait]]'s decay. The mirror-[[the-portrait|portrait]] ritual is not passive [[vanity|self-admiration]] but an active, almost scientific study of his own [[sin|corruption]] — "with minute care." The language of investigation ("examine," "wondering," "which were the more horrible") turns [[sin|moral decay]] into an aesthetic puzzle.

The final question — "the signs of sin or the signs of age" — is devastating. For [[dorian-gray|Dorian]], sin and age are now indistinguishable categories of ugliness. He fears both equally, or rather, he fears neither but studies both with the same "monstrous and terrible delight."

## Themes

- [[beauty]]: [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s beauty is the constant against which he measures his [[sin|corruption]]; it enables and intensifies his [[vanity|narcissism]].
- [[sin]]: [[sin|Sin]] has become an aesthetic category — something to be studied, measured, compared — rather than a moral one.
- [[double-life|the double life]]: The mirror-and-[[the-portrait|portrait]] ritual is the hinge between [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s two lives — the one seen by society, the one seen only by himself.
- [[narcissism]]: The passage is the novel's fullest expression of [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s self-absorption, which has passed beyond vanity into something like religious [[worship|devotion]].
