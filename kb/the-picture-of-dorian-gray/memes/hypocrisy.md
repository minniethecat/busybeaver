## Definition

Hypocrisy in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* is the structural condition of a society that demands moral appearances while tolerating — even enabling — private vice. It is not merely an individual failing but a social system, captured in [[dorian-gray|Dorian]]'s acerbic observation: "My dear fellow, you forget that we are in the native land of the hypocrite" (012-020). The novel suggests that hypocrisy is the price of civilization itself — the mask that makes social life possible.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Dorian Gray is the novel's supreme hypocrite. While the portrait records every sin in grotesque detail, Dorian himself remains "made of ivory and gold" (020-005), his beauty untouched by his degradation. He attends dinners, frequents clubs, and moves through London society as a figure of admiration, all while the hidden portrait tells a different story. The narrator notes that "in hypocrisy he had worn the mask of goodness" (020-010) — a mask that, in the novel's final irony, he almost comes to believe himself.

The theme extends beyond Dorian. Society at large is portrayed as a system of organized hypocrisy. Lord Henry observes in Chapter 17 that "stupidity [is mistaken] for simplicity, and hypocrisy for virtue" (017-032). The aristocracy's elaborate codes of respectability serve not to prevent vice but to conceal it. Even [[basil-hallward|Basil Hallward]] is complicit: he refuses to exhibit the portrait because it reveals too much of his own devotion — a different kind of hypocrisy, in which the artist hides behind his art.

## Key Characters

- **[[Dorian Gray]]**: Hypocrisy personified. The gap between his appearance and his reality is the novel's central image.
- **[[Lord Henry Wotton]]**: The theorist of hypocrisy. He understands that society runs on the gap between what people are and what they pretend to be — and finds the whole arrangement amusing.
- **[[Basil Hallward]]**: A quieter hypocrite, hiding his worship of Dorian behind the language of artistic devotion.

## Key Passages

> He used to wonder at the shallow psychology of those who conceive the Ego in man as a thing simple, permanent, reliable, and of one essence. To him, man was a being with myriad lives and myriad sensations, a complex multiform creature that bore within itself strange legacies of thought and passion. (011-032)

Dorian's rationalization of his double life: hypocrisy reframed not as moral failure but as psychological complexity. The passage reveals how thoroughly he has internalized a worldview that makes hypocrisy not a sin but a form of sophistication.

## Wilde's Treatment

Wilde's treatment of hypocrisy is characteristically ambivalent. The novel condemns hypocrisy in its moral dimension — Dorian is destroyed by the gap between what he is and what he appears to be — but it also suggests that hypocrisy is inescapable, even necessary. The Preface declares that "to reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim," extending the logic of concealment from social life to aesthetic practice. For Wilde, the mask is not simply a lie; it is a form of protection, a necessary fiction. The tragedy of the novel is not that Dorian wears a mask, but that he confuses the mask with his soul.
