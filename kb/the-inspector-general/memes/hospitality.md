# Hospitality

## Definition

Hospitality (*gostepriimstvo*) in Russian provincial culture was both a genuine virtue and a strategic tool. The provision of food, drink, and lodging to travelers — particularly those of higher rank — was a social obligation, but one that shaded easily into the exchange of favors. In Gogol's world, the boundary between hospitality and bribery is almost invisible: a good lunch can purchase what money alone cannot, and the refusal of hospitality is a declaration of hostility.

## In the Play

Hospitality is the medium through which the town's corruption operates. From the moment Khlestakov is identified as the supposed inspector, the officials' strategy is to entertain him into complacency. The Governor conducts him on a tour of institutions capped by an elaborate lunch at the hospital — "a dandy lunch" (037-002) — and each official subsequently visits him privately, offering not just money but food, drink, and obsequious attention.

Khlestakov, initially unable to pay his hotel bill and reduced to begging the innkeeper for soup, is transformed by the town's hospitality into a gourmand and connoisseur: "I like this little town" (035-002), he declares, having been plied with food, money, and deference. His famous line "I like hospitality, and I must say I like it all the more when it is offered with a pure heart" (033-002) is Gogol's most elegant fusion of genuine sentiment and self-serving rationalization — Khlestakov genuinely believes he is being treated well because he deserves it, not because he is being bribed.

## Historical Background

Russian hospitality was legendary — and legendarily expensive. Provincial officials were expected to entertain traveling superiors in a manner commensurate with their rank, and the cost of such entertainment was often borne by the local population through increased "contributions." The tradition of *kormlenie* ("feeding") — the practice by which officials supported themselves from the populations they administered — had medieval roots and persisted, in mutated form, into Gogol's era. Lunch, in this context, was never just lunch.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's satire of hospitality exposes the impossibility of pure generosity in a corrupt system. Every meal served to Khlestakov is a bribe; every compliment he returns is self-deception. The play's great comic set pieces — the hospital lunch, the officials' private audiences — are all structured around the giving and receiving of hospitality. Gogol's point is that in a world where everything is transactional, the most basic human acts of sharing food and shelter become indistinguishable from corruption.
