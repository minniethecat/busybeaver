# The Governor's Dream

## Summary

The Governor's dream of two monstrous black rats occurs in the opening scene of *The Inspector General*, immediately after Anton Antonovich announces the impending arrival of an incognito inspector from St. Petersburg. The dream serves as a superstitious premonition — the Governor interprets it as a "presentiment" of the disaster to come — and establishes the play's central themes of irrational fear, self-deception, and the gap between official authority and private terror.

## In the Play

The dream is recounted in the play's first extended speech. Having summoned the town's principal officials to his residence, the Governor breaks the news of the inspector's arrival, then adds:

> I had a sort of presentiment of it. Last night I kept dreaming of two rats — regular monsters! Upon my word, I never saw the likes of them — black and supernaturally big. They came in, sniffed, and then went away. ([002-002])

The imagery is carefully crafted. The rats are "black" — the color of ill omen in Russian folklore — and "supernaturally big," suggesting forces beyond the Governor's control. Their action — coming in, sniffing, and departing — mirrors the inspector himself: arriving incognito, investigating, and leaving devastation in his wake. The Governor's immediate association of the dream with the news from St. Petersburg reveals a mind governed by superstition rather than reason.

## Dramatic Function

The dream serves three distinct dramatic purposes:

**Establishing character.** The Governor, despite his bluster and authority, is revealed as a man who interprets his world through folk superstition. His opening line to the assembled officials — "I have called you together, gentlemen, to tell you an unpleasant piece of news" ([002-002]) — is immediately undercut by the dream, which betrays his private anxiety beneath the official manner.

**Foreshadowing the plot.** The two rats prefigure the dual threat that will destroy the Governor: Khlestakov (the false inspector who exploits the town's fear) and the real inspector (announced in the final scene). Gogol uses the dream as a structural device — what the Governor fears in Act I is precisely what destroys him in Act V.

**Establishing the play's symbolic economy.** Throughout the comedy, characters mistake appearance for reality, and the rats — "monsters" that are merely rats — inaugurate this pattern of perceptual inflation. The Governor sees omens everywhere because his conscience knows what his official persona denies: that the town is a machine of systematic corruption awaiting exposure.

## Interpretations

The dream has attracted attention from literary critics as a quintessential Gogolian device. Vladimir Nabokov, in his study of Gogol, noted the author's gift for investing the mundane with grotesque significance — two rats become "regular monsters" through the alchemy of the Governor's guilt. The dream also echoes a broader Russian folk tradition in which dreams of rodents signify impending betrayal or exposure.

In the context of the play's satire, the dream underscores the intellectual poverty of provincial officialdom. The Governor's "presentiment" is no rational analysis of his vulnerable position but a piece of peasant superstition dressed in the language of bureaucratic seriousness. Gogol's target is not merely individual corruption but the epistemological void at the center of the tsarist administration — a system whose leaders govern by interpreting dreams.
