# Piotr Bobchinsky

## Identity

Piotr Ivanovich Bobchinsky is one of the two country squires who serve as the town's informal news network. The Directions for Actors describe both Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky as:

> "Short little fellows, strikingly like each other. Both have small paunches, and talk rapidly, with emphatic gestures of their hands, features and bodies. Dobchinsky is slightly the taller and more subdued in manner. Bobchinsky is freer, easier and livelier. They are both exceedingly inquisitive." [P03-005]

Their indistinguishability is a running joke — both have identical first names (Piotr Ivanovich), identical mannerisms, and an identical compulsion to be first with any news. The difference Gogol specifies — Bobchinsky is "freer, easier and livelier" [P03-005] — matters only in performance; within the play's world, no one can tell them apart, and more importantly, no one cares to.

## Role in the Play

Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky are the play's comic messengers and, crucially, the mechanism that converts a rumor into a catastrophe. They first identify Khlestakov as the supposed inspector, having spotted him at the inn observing travelers and inquiring about the quality of meals:

> "What an extraordinary occurrence!" [004-002]

Their breathless entrance in Act I [004-001] sets the entire plot in motion. The two men interrupt each other, compete to deliver the news, and in their competitive overlapping produce a narrative that is simultaneously precise in detail and catastrophically wrong in conclusion.

Khlestakov's behavior — staying at the inn for two weeks without paying, looking carefully at everyone's plates, refusing to travel further — is interpreted through the lens of the inspector rumor. Every fact is accurate; the inference is entirely false.

In Act II, Bobchinsky peeps in at Khlestakov's door [016-003, 017-001, 017-002], spying on the supposed inspector with a mixture of terror and fascination. His physical comedy — the small man pressing himself against the doorframe, trying to be invisible while consumed by the need to see — is a visual metaphor for the town's relationship to authority: simultaneous terror and desperate proximity.

Both squires participate in the bribery in Act IV. Their scene [038-002] is the most pathetically comic of the bribery sequence: between them, after much fumbling and pocket-searching, they can produce only sixty-five rubles. Dobchinsky requests legitimation for his illegitimate son; Bobchinsky makes the most modest and magnificent request in the entire play.

## Key Scenes

- **(004-001 to 004-002)**: Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky rush in out of breath to announce they have found the inspector at the inn. Their competitive storytelling — each interrupting the other, finishing each other's sentences, correcting each other's details — creates the comic rhythm that defines their function.

- **(005-001 to 005-002)**: Bobchinsky begs to accompany the Governor to the inn: "And me — me, too. Let me come" [005-002]. His eagerness to be part of the official response reveals his aspiration to be more than a mere news-bearer.

- **(016-003, 017-001 to 017-002)**: Bobchinsky repeatedly peeps in at Khlestakov's door at the inn, spying on the supposed inspector. His fall at the inn door [017-001] — a moment of physical comedy that combines terror, curiosity, and total loss of dignity — is one of the play's signature comic images.

- **(038-002)**: The bribery scene with Khlestakov. After a comic inventory of pocket contents yields only forty rubles (Dobchinsky contributes twenty-five), Bobchinsky makes his famous request.

- **(056-002)**: When the letter is read and the blame begins circulating, Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky are the scapegoats. Their mutual finger-pointing — "It wasn't I. It was Piotr Ivanovich" / "No, Piotr Ivanovich, you were the first" — recapitulates their earlier competitive dynamic, now turned to self-preservation.

## Relationships

- **Piotr Dobchinsky**: His mirror-image companion. The two are virtually identical in name, appearance, and function [P03-005]. Their relationship is the play's most sustained comic double act — two men who are indistinguishable to everyone but themselves, forever competing over who speaks first.

- **Anton Antonovich (Governor)**: Bobchinsky is eager to ingratiate himself with the Governor by being the bearer of important news. The Governor tolerates the squires with barely concealed contempt, treating them as useful idiots whose information network he exploits but whose company he despises.

- **Ivan Khlestakov**: The supposed inspector. Bobchinsky's fear and fascination with Khlestakov produce some of the play's finest physical comedy. His desperate request to Khlestakov reveals the pathos beneath the buffoonery.

## Character Analysis

Bobchinsky is the play's study in the desire for significance. He has no official position, no authority, no evident function in the town beyond the circulation of information he has no business possessing. His entire identity is bound up in being first with news.

When he arrives breathless at the Governor's house [004-001], it is his moment of maximum importance — the one moment when everyone in the room, including the Governor, needs to hear what he has to say. The rest of the play traces his descent from this peak of relevance back to insignificance, culminating in his status as designated scapegoat.

## The Famous Request

In a moment of absurd pathos that is simultaneously the play's funniest and most heartbreaking scene, Bobchinsky asks Khlestakov for a single favor:

> "I beg your Highness or your Excellency most worshipfully, when you get back to St. Petersburg, please tell all the high personages there, the senators and the admirals, that Piotr Ivanovich Bobchinsky lives in this town. Say this: 'Piotr Ivanovich lives there.' And if you should happen to speak to the Czar, then tell him, too: 'Your Majesty, Piotr Ivanovich Bobchinsky lives in this town.'" [038-002]

The request is magnificent in its modesty. He does not ask for a promotion, a decoration, or money. He asks only for acknowledgment — for someone, anyone, in the distant capital to know that he exists. The escalation from "senators and admirals" to "the Czar" is the comic structure of provincial aspiration: having secured the attention of high personages, why not go directly to the top?

The request encapsulates the provincial longing for metropolitan notice that is one of the play's central themes. Bobchinsky does not want to go to Petersburg; he does not want to become important. He wants exactly what he has — his life in this town — to be *known* in Petersburg. The desire is not for transformation but for recognition, for the center to acknowledge the periphery's existence.

## Notable Lines

> "What an extraordinary occurrence!" [004-002]

Bobchinsky's first line, delivered out of breath, announces both the news and the character. Everything Bobchinsky encounters is "extraordinary" — the word is his signature, the verbal tic of a man for whom provincial life is one long sequence of unprecedented events, because he has no standard of comparison.

> "And me — me, too. Let me come." [005-002]

Begging the Governor for inclusion in the official party to the inn. The repetition of "me" is the grammar of Bobchinsky's character: the desperate assertion of selfhood by a man no one remembers.

> "Piotr Ivanovich Bobchinsky lives in this town." [038-002]

The line he wants spoken in St. Petersburg. Its perfect simplicity — subject, verb, location — is the entire content of his existence. He does not need to be described or praised; he merely needs to be acknowledged.
