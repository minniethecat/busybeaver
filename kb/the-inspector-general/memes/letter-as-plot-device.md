# Letter as Plot Device

## Definition

The letter as a plot device is a narrative mechanism in which a written communication — whether intercepted, misdelivered, read aloud, or forged — drives the action forward or precipitates a reversal. In comedy, the intercepted letter is a particularly potent device: it exposes secrets, reverses fortunes, and delivers the climactic revelation in a form that feels at once surprising and inevitable.

Gogol, whose literary career was built on the narrative power of documents (the stolen overcoat in "The Overcoat," the bureaucratic paperwork in "Diary of a Madman"), uses the letter as the structural linchpin of *The Inspector General*.

The device has a distinguished theatrical pedigree, from the misdirected letter in Shakespeare's *Twelfth Night* to the fatal correspondence in Racine's *Phedre*. Gogol's innovation was to double the device: the play is framed by two letters that mirror and invert each other.

The first letter (from Andrey Ivanovich to the Governor) creates the crisis; the second letter (from Khlestakov to Triapichkin) resolves it — but in typical Gogolian fashion, the resolution is also a catastrophe.

## In the Play

Letters bookend the play's action and drive its climactic reversal.

In Act I, Scene I, the Governor reads aloud a letter from Andrey Ivanovich Chmykhov, a friend in the capital, warning of the inspector general's incognito arrival: "I hasten to let you know, among other things, that an official has arrived here with instructions to inspect the whole government, and your district especially" (002-002).

This letter — and the Governor's decision to share it — sets the entire panic in motion. The letter's language is notably vague: the official "pretends to be a private person" and may have "already arrived." This ambiguity, combined with the officials' guilty consciences, is sufficient to transform the letter into a prophecy.

In Act V, the structural bookend arrives. The Postmaster — whose professional habit of opening private correspondence is an open secret, described by the Governor as "not so much a precaution as curiosity" (003-002) — intercepts Khlestakov's letter to his journalist friend Triapichkin in St. Petersburg.

Reading it aloud to the assembled officials, the Postmaster reveals the truth: "The official whom we took to be an inspector general is not an inspector general. I found it out from the letter. His own letter" (056-002).

The letter is Khlestakov's casual confession of imposture, written for the amusement of a friend — "They seem to be taking me for a government functionary" (039-001) — and never intended to be read by its subjects.

The intercepted letter serves three functions simultaneously.

First, it delivers the climactic recognition (*anagnorisis*). Second, it exposes every official's individual foolishness (Khlestakov has mocked each of them by name).

Third, it arrives moments too late — Khlestakov has already departed, having extracted money, proposed marriage to the Governor's daughter, and vanished into the postal relay system that was supposed to facilitate his detection.

The irony is structural: the postal system that enables the letter's interception is the same system that carries Khlestakov away.

The reading of the letter unfolds as a cruel comic rhythm. Each official, hearing his colleague mocked, laughs — until his own name appears. The Superintendent of Charities eagerly reads aloud the Postmaster's description as "just like the porter Mikheyev — and the scoundrel must drink just as hard" — before balking at his own entry: "a regular pig in a cap" (056-002).

This chain of Schadenfreude is the play's most precise structural joke, and it could only be delivered through the mechanism of a shared document read aloud.

A secondary letter appears within Khlestakov's correspondence system: his plan to "write all about it to Triapichkin in St. Petersburg. He'll write them up in the papers. Let him give them a nice walloping" (039-001).

This threat — the fear of appearing in the satirical press — was a real anxiety in Gogol's Russia, and it extends the letter as plot device beyond the play's action into the real world of literary journalism that Gogol himself inhabited.

## Historical Background

In Gogol's Russia, the postal service was both an instrument of state surveillance and a sieve of privacy. Postmasters were notoriously corrupt and curious — the "black cabinet" (*cherny kabinet*) system of officially sanctioned mail opening operated alongside the unofficial habit of private curiosity.

Letters were the primary means of long-distance communication, and their physical journey — by relay horses, across vast distances, through multiple hands — created opportunities for interception, delay, and discovery that Gogol exploited for maximum comic effect.

The letter from Khlestakov to Triapichkin also reflects the real culture of literary journalism in 1830s St. Petersburg. Writers like Gogol himself, Nikolai Polevoy, and Osip Senkovsky (the "Baron Brambeus" whom Khlestakov claims to be) published satirical sketches of provincial life in journals.

Khlestakov's threat to "write them up in the papers" was not idle — it was a recognized genre, and the fear of appearing in print as a provincial grotesque was a genuine anxiety among the officials Gogol depicted.

The device of the intercepted letter had classical precedents that Gogol's educated audience would have recognized. In Plautus's *Pseudolus*, a stolen letter drives the comic intrigue. In Moliere's *Les Femmes Savantes*, an intercepted love letter precipitates crisis.

Gogol's contribution was to make the letter not merely a plot mechanism but a thematic statement: in a society built on performances of rank, only the private document — itself a violation of privacy — can reveal the truth.

## Satirical Function

The letter reveals what no amount of face-to-face interaction could: the truth. In a play built entirely on mistaken impressions and performative deception, writing possesses an authority that speech does not. Khlestakov's boasts are believed when spoken; his casual confession of imposture is believed only when read.

Gogol's implicit argument is both comic and profound: in a society where everyone lies to each other's faces, only private correspondence — itself a violation of privacy — can deliver the truth.

The double letter structure creates a perfect comic symmetry. The first letter, from an official source, is incomplete and ambiguous — it warns but does not identify, creating a gap that fear fills with fantasy. The second letter, from an unofficial source (the counterfeit inspector himself), is complete and unambiguous — it names names and catalogues absurdities.

The trajectory from the first letter to the second is the trajectory of the play itself: from anxiety to exposure, from mystery to humiliation.

The fact that the Postmaster — the man who opens everyone's letters — is the one who intercepts the truth is Gogol's final satirical twist. The official whose entire job is the violation of privacy becomes the agent of revelation. The system that was designed to protect authority (by enabling surveillance) becomes the instrument that destroys it.
