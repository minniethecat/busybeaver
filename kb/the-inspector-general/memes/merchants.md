# Merchants

The **merchants** (купцы) form a significant social class in the provincial society depicted in *The Inspector General*. They function primarily as a counterweight to the corrupt municipal administration — both as its victims and as a conduit exposing its abuses.

## Role in the Municipal Economy

The merchants are the economic backbone of the town, but the Governor's relationship with them is adversarial. He complains that "the merchants and townspeople bother me" [003-002], revealing the constant friction between commerce and governance. Abdulin, specifically identified as a merchant in the *Dramatis Personae* [P02-025], becomes a particular target of the Governor's resentment — the Governor calls him "that dog of a merchant" when discussing the missing sword [005-002].

The merchants' economic power is felt through their control of goods. The Governor notes that "every damned merchant" provides raw materials like wax [006-002], and specific trade connections are mentioned — the Kholmogory merchants bring goods to the market [016-003].

## As Petitioners

In Act IV, the merchants emerge as key petitioners seeking Khlestakov's intervention. They gather outside his window [040-002], and later the Governor is forced to acknowledge their complaints: "The merchants have complained to your Excellency" [046-002]. The Governor attempts to manage the situation by having the merchants summoned [049-002].

Khlestakov's anecdote about merchants reveals the power dynamics: "I'm a merchant, don't touch me," he mimics [050-002], capturing the merchantry's emerging sense of independence from the old administrative order.

## Administrative Abuse

The Governor's final confession reveals the depth of exploitation: in thirty years of service, "not one merchant, not one contractor has been cheated" — a deeply ironic boast that underscores the systematic nature of municipal corruption [056-002].
