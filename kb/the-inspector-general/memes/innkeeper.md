# The Innkeeper

The **innkeeper**, named Vlas, runs the establishment where Ivan Khlestakov stays upon arriving in the provincial town. He appears only indirectly — through the reports and fears of other characters — yet his commercial persistence is a key driver of the play's early tension.

## The Unpaid Bill

The innkeeper's most significant role is as an offstage creditor. Bobchinsky reports that Khlestakov has been staying with "the landlord, Vlas, the innkeeper" for three weeks without paying [004-002], establishing the material predicament that traps Khlestakov in the town. Osip later confirms the gravity of the situation: "Here's the innkeeper says he won't let you have anything more to eat" [009-001].

## A Source of Anxiety

The innkeeper's demands create a ripple of anxiety that extends from Khlestakov to the Governor himself. When Khlestakov boasts about his St. Petersburg connections, the Governor panics, imagining that the innkeeper "has gone and made a complaint" about him [015-002] — revealing the Governor's guilty conscience about his treatment of local tradespeople.

Khlestakov later embellishes his predicament for sympathy, claiming that "the innkeeper here wanted to send me to jail" [056-002] when he couldn't pay his bill — an exaggeration that serves his self-dramatizing narrative.

## Dramatic Function

The innkeeper, though never appearing on stage, functions as a commercial counterweight to the bureaucratic world. His insistence on payment — a simple, legitimate demand — stands in stark contrast to the elaborate corruption and mutual deception that characterizes the town's administration.
