## Description

The Imperial Council (Государственный Совет) was the supreme state advisory body of the Russian Empire, established by Alexander I in 1810. It advised the Tsar on legislation and administration, composed of the highest-ranking officials appointed directly by the sovereign. In Gogol's time (the play was written in 1836), the Imperial Council represented the pinnacle of the bureaucratic hierarchy — a body whose members were among the most powerful men in the empire.

## In the Play

The Imperial Council appears only in Khlestakov's fantastical boast during Act III, when he claims: "Even the Imperial Council is afraid of me" ([025-002]). This declaration, delivered with escalating drunken bravado, is the climax of Khlestakov's self-aggrandizing monologue. The Governor and assembled officials, already trembling at Khlestakov's earlier claims, are struck with terror at this invocation of the empire's highest council.

The line is a masterstroke of Gogol's satire. No genuine high official would need to claim such influence — the very act of boasting exposes Khlestakov as a fraud, yet the provincial officials' paranoia prevents them from seeing through it. Their fear of authority is so ingrained that they cannot imagine anyone would dare lie about the Imperial Council.

Bobchinsky later reports breathlessly: "Did you hear how he bullies the Imperial Council?" ([026-002]), confirming that Khlestakov's absurd claims have been accepted as terrifying truth by the townspeople.
