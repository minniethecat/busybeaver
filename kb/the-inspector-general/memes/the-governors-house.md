## Overview

The Governor's House is the primary setting for Acts III, IV, and V of Gogol's *The Inspector General*.
It serves as both the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s family residence and the center of [[provincial-town-place|the town]]'s social and political activity
once [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] is installed as an honored guest. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s wife [[anna-andreyevna|Anna Andreyevna]] and daughter Marya
Antonovna live here permanently.

[[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] moves from the inn to the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House after the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] mistakes him for the government
inspector traveling incognito [008-001]. From this point forward, the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House becomes the stage
for the play's central comic action: the bribery scenes, the [[merchant-class|merchants]]' petition, and the final revelation.

## Description and Layout

The house reflects the provincial governor's aspirations to appear sophisticated. According to Gogol's
stage directions in the Dramatis Personae, the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] is "not a stupid man" who "loves to put on airs"
[P02-001]. His house would reflect this character — furnished with whatever provincial taste could afford.

The residence includes:
- A **drawing-room** where [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] tells his fantastical [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] stories, "stalking straight
  into the drawing-room like a bear" [013-002]
- A **dining-room** where the family entertains the im[[post-office|post]]er guest [014-002]
- A **kitchen** connected to the dining area, through which people pass [014-002]
- Private rooms for the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]], his wife, and daughter
- A room assigned to [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] as the honored guest

## Role in the Plot

### Installation of Khlestakov

After the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] discovers [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] at the inn — and mistakenly believes him to be [[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]] —
he insists [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] move to his house. [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] readily agrees, delighted by the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s
[[charitable-institutions|hospital]]ity and unaware of the misunderstanding [016-003].

### The Bribery Sequence

In Act IV, the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House becomes the venue where town officials visit [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] one by one,
each offering a "loan" — in reality, a bribe. The [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]], [[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster]], Superintendent of [[school-administration|Schools]],
[[artemy-zemlianika|Superintendent of Charities]], and the landowners Dob[[social-rank|chin]]sky and Bob[[social-rank|chin]]sky all come to the house
to secure [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]]'s favor [024-002].

### The Merchants' Petition

Merchants and townspeople gather outside the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House to petition [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] against
the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s abuses. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] attempts to keep them away [030-002].

### The Letter and Revelation

In Act V, [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]]'s [[letter-as-plot-device|letter]] to Tryapichkin — written from the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House — is read aloud
by the [[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster]] in the same house, revealing the [[deception-and-self-deception|imposture]]. The final scene, with the [[the-gendarme|Gendarme]]'s
announcement of the real inspector's arrival, also takes place here [056-002].

## Significance

The Governor's House functions as more than a backdrop. It represents the false security and
self-congratulation of provincial officialdom. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] believes he has successfully managed
[[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]] by bringing him under his own roof, when in fact he has merely given the im[[post-office|post]]er
a more comfortable stage for his deception. The house thus embodies the play's central irony.
