# District Courthouse

## Location

The district courthouse is one of the provincial town's official buildings, where [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Ammos Fiodorovich Liapkin-Tiapkin]] has served as elected judge since 1816. It is never seen on stage — its absurdities are reported rather than witnessed — but its reported condition is one of the play's funniest institutional portraits.

## In the Play

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]], preparing for [[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]]'s visit, catalogues the courthouse's deficiencies. Geese have taken up residence in the waiting room. The assessor reeks of alcohol. The [[district-court|court]] attendants keep their boots in the filing cabinets.

As the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] describes it, "the attendants have turned the entrance hall where the petitioners usually wait into a poultry yard, and the geese and goslings go poking their beaks between people's legs" (002-002). The image is both absurd and precise: justice is served amid livestock.

The [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] defends himself with Gogolian logic. The geese were a gift from a litigant — they cannot count as [[bribery|corruption]]. The hunting-crop on his desk is sport, not evidence.

Liapkin-Tiapkin openly admits he takes [[bribery|bribes]], but only in the form of "white greyhound puppies" (002-002). The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] objects that "[[bribery|bribes]] are [[bribery|bribes]], whether puppies or anything else" (002-002), but [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]]'s casuistry remains unshaken.

Zemlianika, the [[artemy-zemlianika|Superintendent of Charities]], paints an even more damning picture. He reports [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] "does nothing but hunt hares, and he keeps his dogs in the [[district-court|court]] rooms" (037-002).

He further claims "the moment Dob[[social-rank|chin]]sky leaves the house, [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] is there with Dob[[social-rank|chin]]sky's wife" (037-002).

He adds that the children are "the very image of [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]]" (037-002). This report — one corrupt official exposing another — epitomizes the mutual surveillance of [[provincial-town-place|the town]]'s officialdom.

## Physical State

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s inventory covers three distinct defects. The geese in the entrance hall signal the collapse of private-public boundaries (002-002). The hunting-crop amid the papers (002-002) signals the abdication of judicial seriousness for rural amusement.

The assessor "reeks of spirits, as if he had just emerged from a distillery" (002-002). The [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] protests it is congenital — "his nurse struck him when he was a child" (002-002) — which only makes the place sound more absurd, not less.

## Significance

The courthouse is Gogol's metaphor for the state of Russian justice. The geese represent the intrusion of private interest into public space. The vodka-soaked assessor represents the degradation of professional standards. The boots in the filing cabinets represent the collapse of documentary order. The hunting-crop on the desk represents the usurpation of judicial duty by leisure.

The greyhound puppies, accepted as [[bribery|bribes]] but distinguished from money, represent the sophistry by which [[bribery|corruption]] rationalizes itself. None of these details is individually catastrophic, but together they paint a picture of institutional decay that is comic because it is recognizable.

After Act I, the courthouse does not reappear, but its imagery persists in the play's portrait of institutional failure. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s wry observation — "I dare say nobody will ever look at it. It's an enviable place. God Almighty Himself seems to watch over it" (002-002) — is an ironic prophecy: the courthouse escapes inspection only because the "inspector" is himself a fraud.
