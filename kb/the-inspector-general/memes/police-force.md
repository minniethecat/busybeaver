# Police Force

## Definition

The police force in provincial Russia was responsible for maintaining public order, but in practice it functioned as the executive arm of the Governor's personal authority. Sergeants and constables were poorly paid, ill-disciplined, and deployed as much for the Governor's private errands as for any public function. The police, in Gogol's portrait, are enforcers of a corrupt status quo.

## In the Play

Captain Stepan Ukhovertov commands the town's police force, assisted by sergeants Svistunov, Derzhimorda, and Pugovitzyn. From the moment the Governor learns of the inspector general's arrival, the police are mobilized not to maintain order but to conceal disorder. The Governor orders Ukhovertov to deploy policemen with brooms to sweep the street leading to the inn — but he also issues a warning: "I know you, I know your tricks. You insinuate yourselves into the inn and walk off with silver spoons in your boots" (005-002). The enforcers are themselves thieves.

The police force is characterized by incompetence and brutality in equal measure. Prokhorov, one of the sergeants, is found dead drunk and cannot be roused even with two buckets of water (005-002). Derzhimorda earns his name — which suggests violent restraint — through his habit of beating suspects indiscriminately: "Guilty or innocent, he makes them all see stars in the cause of public order" (006-002). The Governor's rebuke is not directed at the violence itself but at its visibility: such behavior might draw the inspector's attention.

In Act IV, when petitioners attempt to reach Khlestakov, Derzhimorda blocks the door shouting "Where are you going, whiskers? You've been told that nobody is allowed to come in" (040-002). The police serve as a barrier between the governor's victims and any potential source of justice — a literal embodiment of the system's refusal to hear complaints.

## Satirical Function

The police force in *The Inspector General* is the physical manifestation of the administration's corruption. They sweep streets not to clean but to create an illusion of order. They block petitioners rather than hear complaints. The Governor's simultaneous reliance on and contempt for his police — he knows they steal, he knows they drink, he knows they brutalize — captures the paradox of a system that depends on instruments it cannot control.
