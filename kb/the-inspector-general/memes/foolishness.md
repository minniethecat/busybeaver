# Foolishness

## Definition

Foolishness (*glupost*) in Gogol's comic universe is not simple stupidity but a systemic failure of perception — an inability to see what is plainly before one's eyes. Gogol's fools are not unintelligent (the Governor is "by no means a fool in his own way," P03-001); rather, they are intelligent people whose intelligence has been entirely consumed by the demands of a corrupt social order. Their foolishness is the cognitive cost of living in a society organized around rank, bribery, and fear.

## In the Play

The play's central dramatic action — the collective misidentification of Khlestakov as an inspector general — is a study in systemic foolishness. Not one official questions the imposture; not one demands credentials. The Governor, who prides himself on having "cheated three governors general," is fooled by a penniless clerk whose servant has to tell him to leave town before he's caught. The officials line up to offer "loans" to a man who has given no indication of understanding — let alone inspecting — anything.

Khlestakov himself is the play's purest fool: "rather stupid, being, as they say, 'without a czar in his head'" (P03-003). Yet it is precisely his foolishness that makes him so effective an impostor. A clever man would overplay the role; a stupid man simply enjoys the attention. Gogol's insight — that in a foolish world, the biggest fool wins — is the dark core of his comedy.

## Historical Background

The figure of the fool has deep roots in Russian culture, from the *yurodivy* (holy fool) of Orthodox tradition to the folk-tale Ivanushka-durachok (Ivan the Little Fool), whose apparent stupidity conceals deeper wisdom. Gogol drew on both traditions while adding a distinctly modern, bureaucratic twist: his fools are not holy or folkloric but institutional — products of a system that rewards conformity to rank over independent judgment.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's satire of foolishness is democratic: no character escapes. The officials are fools for being deceived; Khlestakov is a fool for not understanding his own deception; the audience — laughing at the spectacle — is invited to recognize its own complicity in the foolishness of the human condition. The play's epigraph, traditionally attributed to a Russian proverb — "Don't blame the mirror if your mug is crooked" — makes this universalizing move explicit.
