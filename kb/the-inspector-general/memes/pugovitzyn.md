# Pugovitzyn

## Identity

Pugovitzyn (also rendered as Pugovichyn) is one of the police sergeants serving under Captain [[stepan-ukhovertov|Stepan Ukhovertov]] in the [[provincial-town-place|provincial town]]. He is listed in the Dramatis Personae alongside his fellow sergeants [[svistunov|Svistunov]] and [[derzhimorda|Derzhimorda]] [P02-022], forming the trio of "police sergeants" who constitute the lower ranks of the town's [[police-force|law enforcement]] [P02-024].

## Role in the Play

Pugovitzyn exemplifies the Governor's approach to governance: appearances over substance. His sole named appearance occurs in Act I, Scene V, during the Governor's frantic preparations for the inspector's visit. When the Governor berates Captain Ukhovertov for the state of the town, Ukhovertov defensively reports: "What you told me to. I sent Sergeant Pugovichyn with policemen to clean the street" [006-002]. The Governor then issues specific instructions concerning Pugovitzyn's deployment:

> "Sergeant Pugovichyn — he is tall. So he is to stand on duty on the bridge for appearance' sake." [006-002]

This brief exchange encapsulates the play's satirical portrait of provincial administration. Pugovitzyn is selected not for competence but for height — he looks imposing, and that is what matters when the inspector arrives. The police are deployed to create the illusion of order rather than to enforce it. Pugovitzyn himself never speaks on stage; he is a functionary, an instrument of the Governor's panic, mentioned but never seen.

The Governor's instruction that Pugovitzyn should stand on the bridge "for appearance' sake" is one of the play's most economical satirical moments. In four words, Gogol condenses the entire philosophy of the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s administration: governance as theater, authority as costume, and public order as a spectacle staged for the benefit of a single observer.
