## In the Play

Saratov is the government (*gubernia*) to which Ivan Khlestakov claims to be traveling when he is stranded at the provincial inn. Bobchinsky first identifies him by this destination: "And his postchaise is ordered for Saratov" [002-014]. The Governor, hearing that Khlestakov is supposedly bound for "the Government of Saratov" [011-001], notes his boldness — a real inspector would not travel so far from the capital.

Khlestakov confirms his destination when first meeting the Governor: "I'm going to my own village in the Government of Saratov" [011-001]. The Governor's aside reveals his suspicion: "The Government of Saratov! H'm, h'm! And doesn't even blush!" [011-001].

The Postmaster mentions reading letters about officials in Saratov and Kostroma: "No, nothing about a St. Petersburg official, but plenty about Kostroma and Saratov ones" [002-005].

In Act V, Khlestakov's intercepted letter to Triapichkin reveals his actual address: "Village of Podkatilovka in the Government of Saratov" [055-002]. This detail — a real address embedded in a fictitious persona — is one of the few verifiable facts in Khlestakov's web of lies.

## Dramatic Function

Saratov serves as a geographical anchor in the play's comedy of misidentification. Its remoteness from St. Petersburg is part of the joke: a genuine government inspector would have no reason to travel to such a distant province, yet the officials' panic overrides this obvious inconsistency.

The repeated invocation of Saratov by multiple characters (Bobchinsky, Dobchinsky, Khlestakov, the Governor, and Korobkin) weaves it through the play's three-act structure, making it one of the few specific geographical references in Gogol's deliberately vague provincial setting.
