# Piotr Dobchinsky

## Identity

Piotr Ivanovich Dobchinsky is a country squire in the nameless [[provincial-town]] of Gogol's *The Inspector General*. Listed in the [[dramatis-personae]] alongside his inseparable counterpart [[piotr-bobchinsky]] [P02-009], Dobchinsky is described in the [[directions-for-actors]] as "slightly the taller and more subdued in manner" of the two — yet, like Bobchinsky, he is "exceedingly inquisitive," speaks rapidly, gesticulates frantically, and has a small protruding belly [P03-005]. The pair are so physically and temperamentally alike that they function as a single comic unit, a two-headed embodiment of provincial gossip and small-town busybody-ism.

## Role in the Play

Dobchinsky, together with Bobchinsky, is the catalyst of the play's central misunderstanding. While "entering the inn" [004-002], the two squires spot a young man who refuses to pay his bill and whose postchaise is ostensibly ordered for Saratov — and they leap to the conclusion that he is the dreaded inspector general traveling [[incognito]]. It is Dobchinsky who delivers the decisive line of reasoning:

> "Why, he doesn't pay his bills and he doesn't leave. Who else can it be? And his postchaise is ordered for Saratov." [004-002]

This moment of credulous deduction sets the entire machinery of the plot in motion: the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s panic, the cascade of [[bribery]], and the collective self-deception that eventually humiliates every official in the town. Gogol uses Dobchinsky (and Bobchinsky) to satirize the petty provincial psychology that cannot distinguish between a genuine threat and a hungry young clerk from St. Petersburg — a satire in which the gossips who "discover" the news are ultimately revealed as the most ridiculous figures of all.

Dobchinsky is also distinguished from Bobchinsky by a private subplot: his illegitimate eldest son. In a private audience with [[ivan-khlestakov]], Dobchinsky begs the supposed high official to have his son legitimized — revealing an endearingly human anxiety beneath the comic surface [038-001].

## Key Scenes

**Act I, Scene 3 — The Announcement (004-001 through 004-002)**

Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky burst in "out of breath" [004-001] and, in a feverish, overlapping duologue, report their discovery of [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] at [[the-inn]]. Dobchinsky provides key factual details: the stranger is in "Room number 5 near the stairway" [004-002] and has been there "two weeks" since "St. Vasili's day" [004-002]. He also insists on correcting Bobchinsky's narrative — their competitive, interruptive exchange is a perfect theatrical set-piece.

**Act I, Scene 5 — The Governor's Scout (005-001)**

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] selects Dobchinsky (not Bobchinsky) as his companion for the unofficial reconnaissance mission to [[the-inn]], telling him "Now, Piotr Ivanovich, come" [005-001] while Bobchinsky is left pleading "let me come, too" [005-001]. This preference suggests Dobchinsky is perceived as marginally more reliable than his counterpart.

**Act II, Scene 8 — Witness to the Governor's Bribe (016-003)**

Dobchinsky accompanies [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] and the Governor at the inn, silently observing the Governor's first [[bribery|bribe]]. Khlestakov repeatedly urges him to sit — "Please take a seat, I beg of you" [016-001] — treating the flustered squire with exaggerated courtesy that Dobchinsky mistakes for the gravity of high rank.

**Act II, Scene 10 — The Messenger (016-003)**

The Governor sends Dobchinsky racing back to town with two notes: one to [[artemy-zemlianika|Zemlianika]] at the hospital, the other to his wife [[anna-andreyevna]]. Dobchinsky willingly walks back on foot when the cab is too full — "It doesn't matter. I'll walk" [018-001] — his eagerness to serve underscoring his complete absorption in the inspector delusion.

**Act III, Scene 2 — The Report to Anna Andreyevna (021-001)**

Dobchinsky arrives breathless at the Governor's house and delivers a colorful report to [[anna-andreyevna]], describing Khlestakov's appearance with unintentionally absurd precision:

> "His eyes dart about like little animals. They make you nervous." [021-001]

He boasts that "It was Piotr Ivanovich and I who first discovered him" [021-001] — a claim that will return to haunt him at the [[letter-reading-finale]].

**Act III, Scene 7 — Rank Speculation (026-002)**

After witnessing [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] hold court, Dobchinsky confides to Bobchinsky his assessment: "I think he's almost a general" [026-002]. Bobchinsky one-ups him by declaring the man a "generalissimo," and the two rush off to spread the news — feeding the town's inflation of Khlestakov's importance in the [[table-of-ranks|rank-obsessed]] provincial imagination.

**Act IV, Scene 1 — Full Dress (032-001)**

Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky enter "cautiously, almost on tiptoe" in full dress-uniform [032-001] to pay court to [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]], part of the growing procession of officials seeking individual audiences in the [[bribery]]-fueled scramble of Act IV.

**Act IV, Scene 7 — The Delicate Matter (038-001)**

In his private audience with [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]], Dobchinsky hands over twenty-five rubles (while Bobchinsky contributes forty) [038-001] and then makes his most personal appeal — the legitimization of his eldest son, born before wedlock. "I made up everything afterwards, set everything right, as it should be, with the bonds of matrimony," he explains, before adding the touching detail that the boy "can recite different poems by heart; and whenever he gets hold of a penknife, he makes little carriages as skilfully as a conjurer" [038-001] — a request that echoes the play's broader theme of [[social-status]] anxiety. This moment gives Dobchinsky a pathos absent from his mirror-image counterpart.

**Act V, Scene 5 — The Congratulations (053-002)**

Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky enter "jostling each other" [053-002] to congratulate the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s family on Khlestakov's supposed engagement to Marya. They bump foreheads while bending to kiss [[anna-andreyevna]]'s hand [053-002], and Dobchinsky delivers an effusive speech to Marya Antonovna: "You will wear garments of gold and eat the most delicate soups" [053-002] — a display of the [[provincial-town|provincial]] sycophancy that Gogol returns to throughout the play.

**Act V, Scene 8 — The Reckoning (056-002)**

When Khlestakov's letter is read aloud and the truth is revealed, [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin]] immediately points at "these two here, this noble pair" [056-002]. Dobchinsky denies everything — "I didn't say a thing, not a thing" [056-002] — but the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] unleashes a torrent of abuse: "Town gossips, damned liars! ... confounded chatterboxes, short-tailed magpies!" [056-002]. In the ensuing recrimination, Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky turn on each other: "No, [[piotr-bobchinsky|Piotr Ivanovich]], you were the first" [056-002] — their symbiotic partnership dissolving into mutual betrayal.

**Act V, Silent Scene (058-001)**

In the final tableau, Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky are frozen at the edge of the stage, "gesticulating at each other, open-mouthed and wide-eyed" [058-001] — a last, wordless image of their perpetual agitation and their inability to escape the chaos they have created, and a fitting coda to the [[letter-reading-finale|devastating letter]] that precedes it.

## Relationships

- **[[piotr-bobchinsky]]** — Dobchinsky's doppelganger and constant companion. The two are so indistinguishable that Gogol gives them identical patronymics (Piotr Ivanovich) and nearly identical surnames. They function as a comic double-act, interrupting, correcting, and jostling each other throughout the play — until the moment of reckoning at the [[letter-reading-finale]], when each tries to blame the other.

- **[[anton-antonovich]]** (the Governor) — Dobchinsky is both subordinate to and instrumental in the Governor's downfall, illustrating the [[social-status|rigid hierarchy]] of provincial society. The Governor specifically chooses Dobchinsky as his escort to the inn [005-001] and as his trusted messenger [018-001], but after the letter is read, he denounces Dobchinsky with unrestrained fury [056-002].

- **[[anna-andreyevna]]** — Dobchinsky's "godmother," to whom he hurriedly reports the news and delivers the Governor's note. Their relationship reveals the web of family connections and gossip networks that suffuse the town [021-001].

- **[[ivan-khlestakov]]** — Dobchinsky both "discovers" Khlestakov and is duped by him, a victim of the same [[incognito]] delusion that he himself helped propagate. He contributes his savings to the charade [038-001] and, in the play's most vulnerable moment, begs Khlestakov to legitimize his son [038-001].

- **The Other Officials** — Alongside the [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]], [[artemy-zemlianika|Superintendent of Charities]], [[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster]], and [[luka-khlopov|School Inspector]], Dobchinsky participates in the collective bribery and subsequent humiliation. He is singled out as the instigator of the misidentification when the truth emerges [056-002].
