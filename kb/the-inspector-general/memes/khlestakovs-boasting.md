# Khlestakov's Boasting Scene

## Summary

Khlestakov's boasting scene in Act III, Scene VI is the play's most celebrated comic set piece. Gathered in the Governor's drawing room with the town's officials and the Governor's wife and daughter, Khlestakov — emboldened by the deference shown him and the wine at lunch — constructs an escalating fantasy of his own importance. Within minutes he claims intimacy with Pushkin, authorship of canonical European works, a St. Petersburg mansion receiving counts and princes, and command of a government department with thirty-five thousand couriers. The scene is a masterclass in Gogolian inflation: every lie generates its own momentum, and the officials' terror prevents them from recognizing the absurdity.

## In the Play

The scene opens with elaborate courtesies between Khlestakov and Anna Andreyevna, the Governor's wife, who is determined to charm the supposed dignitary. Khlestakov, "showing off" as the stage direction notes, quickly escalates from flirtation to fantasy:

> Maybe you think I am only a copying clerk. No, I am on a friendly footing with the chief of our department. He slaps me on the back. "Come, brother," he says, "and have dinner with me." ([025-002])

The lie contains a grain of truth — Khlestakov is a copying clerk — but the grain is immediately buried under layers of invention. The doorkeeper shines his boots; he was once mistaken for the commander-in-chief; soldiers rushed from the guardhouse to salute him.

### Literary Pretensions

The boasting reaches its comic peak when Khlestakov claims authorship:

> I am on an intimate footing with Pushkin. I often say to him: "Well, Pushkin, old boy, how goes it?" "So, so, partner," he'd reply, "as usual." ([025-002])

He then claims to have written *The Marriage of Figaro*, *Robert le Diable*, and *Norma* — all actual works by other authors. When Anna Andreyevna's daughter Marya points out that *Yury Miroslavsky* is by Zagoskin, Khlestakov pivots without missing a beat: "Oh, yes, it's so. That was by Zagoskin. But there is another Yury Miroslavsky which was written by me" ([025-002]).

### The Escalation

The speech builds to delirium. Khlestakov describes his St. Petersburg mansion where a watermelon costs seven hundred rubles and soup arrives by steamer from Paris. "Counts and princes jostle each other and humming like bees" in his foyer. His mail is addressed to "Your Excellency." He once directed an entire department, summoned by thirty-five thousand couriers. "Even the Imperial Council is afraid of me" ([025-002]).

The scene climaxes with Khlestakov, carried away by his own rhetoric, about to claim a field-marshalship — before he slips, nearly falls, and is caught by the trembling officials.

## Dramatic Function

The boasting scene fulfills three structural purposes:

**Revealing Khlestakov's character.** The lies are not calculated; they are improvised, self-propelling, unconscious. Gogol's stage direction is crucial: Khlestakov "works himself up more and more as he speaks." The character is not a schemer but a vessel for fantasy — he believes his own lies as he utters them.

**Demonstrating the officials' complicity.** The Governor and his subordinates, "trembling from head to toe," accept every absurdity because their fear demands it. Gogol's satire targets not the liar but the listeners — a system so corrupt that its functionaries cannot distinguish between a department director and a starving clerk.

**Structuring the comedy's arc.** The boasting scene is the pivot between the misunderstanding of Act II and the bribery of Act IV. Having established Khlestakov's imagined grandeur, the officials' subsequent offerings of "loans" become not bribes but tributes to a fantasy they have collectively authored.

## Critical Reception

The scene has been recognized as one of the purest expressions of Gogol's comic method. The critic Boris Eikhenbaum noted that the speech is structured not by logic but by rhythm — each lie generates the next through associative leaps rather than rational connection. The result is a verbal crescendo that mirrors the play's larger movement from provincial anxiety to shared delusion.
