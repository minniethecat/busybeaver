# Vanity

## Introduction

Vanity is a pervasive theme in Gogol's *The Inspector General*, manifesting in nearly every character's behavior.
From Anna Andreyevna's provincial coquetry to the Governor's self-satisfaction with his town's appearance,
vanity blinds the characters to reality and renders them vulnerable to Khlestakov's imposture.

## Anna Andreyevna's Coquetry

Anna Andreyevna, the Governor's wife, is explicitly characterized as "a provincial coquette" with "streaks of vanity" [P03-002].
Her self-regard is cultivated through novels and domestic fussing rather than any genuine accomplishment.
This vanity drives her to believe Khlestakov's flattering attentions are directed at her personally,
leading to her fantasy of a Petersburg marriage.

## The Governor's Civic Pride

Governor Antonovich's vanity takes the form of civic boosterism.
When Khlestakov compliments the town's institutions, the Governor responds by disparaging other towns:
"In other towns, I venture to observe, the authorities and officials look out for themselves more.
Here, I may say, we have no other thought than to earn the government's good opinion through order and vigilance" [024-002].
This self-congratulatory speech is delivered to a man he believes to be an incognito government inspector — the irony being that his vaunted "order and vigilance" are entirely illusory.

## Khlestakov's Boastful Self-Fashioning

Khlestakov's vanity is the engine of the play's central deception.
His self-importance, flattery of officials, and inflated boasts about his Petersburg life are all expressions of a shallow character
"without a czar in his head" who nevertheless craves the deference shown to an important personage [P03-003].

## Dramatic Function

Vanity serves as the structural weakness that Gogol exploits in every character.
The officials' vanity makes them susceptible to flattery;
Khlestakov's vanity makes him unable to stop exaggerating;
and Anna Andreyevna's vanity makes her interpret every gesture as romantic interest.
Collectively, vanity ensures that no character in the play sees the truth until it is too late.
