# The Reading of Khlestakov's Letter

## Summary

The letter-reading finale is the climactic sequence of Act V that unfolds across three rapid movements: the Postmaster's explosive entrance with Khlestakov's intercepted letter, the public reading that humiliates every official by name, and the silent scene — a frozen tableau held for nearly two minutes — that follows the gendarme's announcement of the *real* inspector general. It is one of the most famous endings in world theatre.

## Dramatic Function

The letter reading is the play's recognition scene (*anagnorisis*) — the moment shared delusion is shattered by documentary evidence (056-001). Gogol structures the exposure as a chain of self-interest: each official enjoys his colleagues' humiliation until his own name appears. The [[artemy-zemlianika|Superintendent of Charities]] eagerly reads the passage about the Postmaster but balks at his own description ("a regular pig in a cap," 056-002). The Governor's incredulity — "That can't be in the letter" (056-002) — gives way to fury as he realizes every official has been systematically mocked. This comic rhythm mirrors the structure of [[deception-and-self-deception]] that governs the entire play.

The arrival of a gendarme announcing the real inspector (057-002) precipitates the famous silent scene — a frozen tableau in which every character is petrified in a revealing posture: the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] rigid as a post, the [[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster]] "metamorphosed into a question mark," the [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] crouching "as if to whistle" (058-001). Gogol's stage direction — holding the pose for "almost a minute and a half" — transforms comedy into something approaching existential dread.

## Key Moments

**The Postmaster's entrance** (056-001). [[ivan-shpekin|The Postmaster]] rushes in "with an unsealed letter in his hand" and announces: "The official whom we took to be an inspector general is not an inspector general." His confession of how he opened the letter — "A supernatural power moved me. I was overcome by a greater curiosity than I have ever felt in my life" (056-001) — is a comic masterpiece of self-exculpation.

**The Governor's denial** (056-002). [[anton-antonovich|The Governor]] refuses to believe the letter is genuine: "Impossible! That can't be in the letter!" He accuses the Postmaster of inserting the insult himself — a final act of denial before the collapse of his world.

**The Postmaster's exposure** (056-002). [[artemy-zemlianika|Zemlianika]], having been spared so far, seizes the letter and reads aloud: "The Postmaster is just like the porter Mikheyev in our office, and the scoundrel must drink just as hard." The Postmaster's aside to the audience — "A bad boy! He ought to be given a licking" — is the moment the Schadenfreude begins to curdle.

**The chain of humiliation** (056-002). The letter passes from hand to hand. The [[luka-khlopov|School Inspector]] is told he "reeks of onions." The [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] — who had been "thanking God" there was nothing about him — is branded "extremely *mauvais ton*." The Governor, described as "stupid as an old horse," beats his forehead and delivers the play's most famous line: "What are you laughing at? You are laughing at yourselves!"

**The recrimination** (056-002). The officials turn on [[piotr-bobchinsky|Bobchinsky]] and [[piotr-dobchinsky|Dobchinsky]], the town gossips who first identified Khlestakov as the inspector. "You run about the city, bother everybody, confounded chatterboxes. You spread gossip, you short-tailed magpies, you!" (056-002). The scapegoating of the two buffoons replays the [[announcement-of-inspector|catalytic moment]] — this time as farce instead of panic.

**The gendarme's announcement** (057-002). An official sent "by imperial order" has arrived and wants to see everyone at once. The line strikes the company "as by a thunderbolt" — the real inspector has come, but only after the town has been stripped bare of its defenses, its money, and its dignity. The [[letter-as-plot-device|letter]] has done its work: exposure has occurred, but it has come too late to prevent anything.

**The silent scene** (058-001). Gogol freezes every character in a posture that epitomizes their nature. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] stands rigid at center, his wife and daughter straining toward him. The [[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster]] is "metamorphosed into a question mark." The Judge crouches as if to whistle. Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky gesticulate at each other, mouths agape. The tableau is held for ninety seconds — a durational demand that forces the audience to contemplate the ruin before them.

## Satirical Significance

The letter-reading finale is Gogol's most sophisticated structural joke. The entire play has been a [[deception-and-self-deception|collective delusion]] maintained through face-to-face performance. Now the delusion is broken not by confrontation but by the written word: a private letter, opened illegally by a postmaster who opens all letters as a matter of habit, read aloud while its author is already galloping away on the best relay horses the town could provide (056-002).

The satire cuts two ways. On the surface, the officials are exposed as fools — each man's vanity catalogued in Khlestakov's casual prose. But the deeper joke is at the audience's expense. The Governor's line to the house — "What are you laughing at? You are laughing at yourselves!" (056-002) — was, in the original 1836 production, delivered directly to the first row of St. Petersburg notables, including Tsar Nicholas I himself. Gogol's fourth-wall breach implicates everyone in the room.

The silent scene radicalizes this implication. By freezing the action and holding it, Gogol denies the audience the relief of a conventional comic resolution — there is only the image and the knowledge that a real inspector has arrived when there is nothing left to inspect. The [[bribery-parade|bribes have been given]], the [[letter-as-plot-device|letter has been mailed]], and the town has consumed itself. The ending is comedy at the precipice of tragedy.
