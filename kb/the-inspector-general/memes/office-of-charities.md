# Office of Charities

## Overview

The Office of Charities (formally the Board of Public Welfare, *Prikaz obshchestvennogo prizreniya*) was established by Catherine the Great's provincial reforms of 1775 to oversee hospitals, almshouses, orphanages, and insane asylums at the local level. In theory, it represented the state's commitment to the welfare of its most vulnerable subjects. In Gogol's portrayal, it represents the gap between institutional mandate and institutional reality.

## In the Play

Under Artemy Zemlianika's administration, the Office of Charities has become a theater of neglect. The hospital patients "look as if they worked in a brewery" (037-002); the church that was to be built adjacent to the hospital was never started despite funds being appropriated five years earlier; and the Superintendent's primary administrative strategy is to discharge patients before the inspector arrives, on the principle that "the fewer the patients the better" (006-002).

## Significance

Gogol's satire of the charitable institutions is particularly pointed because they were explicitly designed to demonstrate the state's benevolence. The gap between the Catherine-era ideal of enlightened welfare and the Nikolaevan reality of systematic neglect is the measure of institutional decay that *The Inspector General* documents.
