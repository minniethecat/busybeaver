# Osip

## Identity

Osip is the middle-aged servant of [[ivan-khlestakov|Ivan Khlestakov]], described as "a typical middle-aged servant, grave in his address, with eyes always a bit lowered. He is argumentative and loves to read sermons directed at his master. He is the cleverer of the two and grasps a situation more quickly. But he does not like to talk. He is a silent, uncommunicative rascal" (P03-004). His shabby coat marks his place in the [[table-of-ranks|social hierarchy]].

Unlike every other character, Osip sees clearly: his master is a wastrel, the [[provincial-town|provincial town]] is a trap, and the longer they stay the more likely they are to be caught. His lowered eyes suggest not servility but protective invisibility — he sees because no one sees him.

## Role in the Play

Osip is the play's moral compass — the sole character whose judgment is consistently reliable. His Act II soliloquy at [[the-inn|the inn]] opens with raw physical complaint and expands into devastating social commentary:

> "The devil take it! I'm so hungry. There's a racket in my belly, as if a whole regiment were blowing trumpets. We'll never reach home."
>
> — (011-001)

Where officials project authority onto Khlestakov, Osip knows his master is a [[civil-service|common copying clerk]] who gambles, demands the best rooms, and sells his clothes when broke (011-001). This knowledge — acquired through [[domestic-service|domestic service]] — gives him insight no official can match.

When the [[bribery|bribes]] begin, Osip is first to recognize the danger:

> "Leave this place. Upon my word, it's time. You've had a good time here for two days. It's enough."
>
> — (040-002)

He states the [[deception-and-self-deception|deception]] plainly: "You see, they've taken you for somebody else, honest" (040-002).

Osip participates in the bribery economy without delusion. As the Governor's family crowds around him, he calculates: "What shall I say? Seeing as they've given me such good feed now, I s'pose they'll do even better later" (029-002). He takes the Governor's rubles with theatrical gratitude — "Thank you, much obliged to you, sir" (029-002) — knowing it is farce. He coaches merchant supplicants: "Why don't you take the sugar, your Highness? Take it. Everything will come in handy on the road" (041-002).

## Relationships

- **[[ivan-khlestakov|Ivan Khlestakov]]** (master): Lectures him "for his own good," combining loyalty with contempt. He serves not from affection but because survival demands it.
- **The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] and his family**: Exploits their sycophancy for food, drink, and rubles.
- **[[piotr-bobchinsky|Bobchinsky]] and Dobchinsky**: Silently witnesses Bobchinsky tumble through the inn door (017-001).

## Character Analysis

Osip extends the tradition of the clever servant in comedy, but Gogol gives the type a Russian inflection: grave, watchful, and decent rather than witty or scheming. His silence — "he does not like to talk" (P03-004) — is a form of power: he hoards information while everyone else spills it. His description of Khlestakov as "a general, only the other way round" (023-001) is the play's most economical social analysis: the [[table-of-ranks|Table of Ranks]] inverted.

Osip is not morally superior — he pockets bribes, lies, and facilitates escape without remorse. In Gogol's universe, where [[deception-and-self-deception|self-deception]] is universal, epistemological honesty alone makes him a kind of hero.

## Historical Context

As a serf-servant, Osip reflects [[domestic-service|domestic service]] under Nicholas I. The household serf, legally property yet living intimately with his master, occupied a liminal position that gave servants unique knowledge of private life. Gogol exploits this: Osip's soliloquy transforms the minor genre of the "servant's complaint" into a mirror held up to the entire [[social-status|social order]].
