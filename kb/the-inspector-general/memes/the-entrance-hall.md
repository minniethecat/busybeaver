## Overview

The entrance hall (or anteroom) is mentioned in Act I of Gogol's *The Inspector General* during
the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s inspection of municipal facilities before the supposed government inspector's
visit. It appears in connection with the [[district-court|court]]room, where it serves as the waiting area for
petitioners — a space that should represent the accessibility and dignity of public institutions
but instead embodies their decay.

## Degradation of Public Space

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] rebukes [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] for the condition of the entrance hall, noting with exasperation:
"The attendants have turned the entrance hall where the petitioners usually wait into a regular
poultry yard with all those goslings running about under foot" [002-002].

This transformation — from a waiting area for citizens seeking justice to an improvised farmyard —
is one of the play's most vivid images of institutional dysfunction. The geese in the entrance
hall are not merely untidy; they signal that the apparatus of government has ceased to function
as a public institution and has become something closer to a private estate.

## Role in the Play

The entrance hall is not a stage setting but belongs to the world described by the characters —
an "off-stage" space that reveals the on-stage characters' attitudes toward their official
responsibilities. The [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]]'s indifference to geese in the petitioners' waiting room parallels
his casual approach to justice itself.

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s concern is purely instrumental: he worries not about the principle of a dignified
public space but about what [[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]] might think. "If [[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]] so much as casts a
glance, he'll see the kind of order you keep here" [002-002].

## Significance

The entrance hall, with its geese and disorder, is a microcosm of Gogol's satirical vision
of provincial Russia. The space where citizens should encounter the [[justice|law]] has become a barnyard.
This visual absurdity — [[justice|law]] books in one room, goslings in the next — captures the central
comic tension of *The Inspector General*: the gap between the forms of imperial administration
and the reality of provincial life.
