# Khlestakov's Departure

## Summary

Khlestakov's departure at the end of Act IV is the pivot on which the entire denouement turns. Having spent two days as the town's honored guest — accepting bribes, proposing to the Governor's daughter, and entertaining the officials' wives — Khlestakov heeds Osip's warning and flees before his imposture is discovered. The departure scene is a masterpiece of comic compression: in a few rapid exchanges, Khlestakov extracts another four hundred rubles, secures a Persian rug for his seat, and rides away, leaving the Governor's family celebrating an engagement that will evaporate within hours.

## In the Play

### Osip's Warning

The departure is stage-managed by Osip, who — alone among the characters — understands the danger of their situation. Osip has ordered the horses and packed their belongings. His entrance with the announcement "The horses are ready" ([047-002]) punctures the Governor's triumphant mood. The audience understands what the Governor does not: Osip is not facilitating a brief visit to a rich uncle but engineering an escape.

### The Final Extraction

Khlestakov's departure demonstrates the full refinement of his parasitic instincts. When the Governor asks whether he needs money for the road, Khlestakov initially demurs — "Oh, no, what for?" — then, after a calculated pause, adds: "However, if you like" ([047-002]). He then performs an audacious bit of arithmetic:

> You gave me two hundred then, that is, not two hundred, but four hundred — I don't want to take advantage of your mistake — you might let me have the same now so that it should be an even eight hundred. ([047-002])

The Governor, still giddy from the engagement, produces brand-new banknotes "as though on purpose." Khlestakov's observation that "new money means good luck" ([047-002]) is one of Gogol's sharpest ironic touches — the luck is entirely Khlestakov's, and it will not hold.

### The Persian Rug

The scene's final comic detail is the Governor's insistence on providing a rug for the post-chaise. Khlestakov, consistent to the last, first declines ("No, what for? It's not necessary"), then immediately reverses himself: "However, let them bring a rug if you please" ([047-002]). The Governor orders "the very best rug from there, the Persian rug with the blue ground" — a final, absurd gift to the man who has systematically plundered the town.

### The Exit

The departure is rendered in rapid fragments — "Goodbye," "Goodbye, your Excellency," "Get up, my boys!" — and the act concludes with the ringing of the post-chaise bell and the dropping of the curtain. Gogol's stagecraft is precise: the speed of the exit conveys the precariousness of Khlestakov's escape, while the bell's echo lingers as a promise of the reckoning to come.

## Dramatic Significance

Khlestakov's departure is structurally crucial to the play's design. Gogol places it at the end of Act IV rather than Act V, creating a full act of dramatic irony in which the audience watches the Governor's family celebrate a phantom engagement while knowing that Khlestakov is already on the road. The departure also completes Khlestakov's character arc: having arrived as a hungry clerk and been elevated to an "Excellency," he exits as a thief — charming, impulsive, and utterly unreformed.

The scene is also Gogol's final word on the Governor's complicity. Even as Khlestakov rides away in a post-chaise, the Governor is offering rugs and banknotes with the eagerness of a man who believes he is buying a future. The audience knows he is buying nothing — except perhaps the right to look foolish for an additional act.
