# Svistunov

## Identity

Svistunov is one of the police sergeants serving under Captain Stepan Ukhovertov in the provincial town. Listed in the Dramatis Personae alongside Pugovitzyn and Derzhimorda, Svistunov functions as an instrument of the Governor's administrative machinery, carrying out orders without question (P02-021).

## Role in the Play

As a police sergeant, Svistunov appears in Act I and Act IV, representing the lowest tier of municipal authority that the Governor deploys for crowd control and public-order operations. The character embodies the physical enforcement arm of Gogol's satirized bureaucracy: obedient, unreflective, and complicit in the Governor's schemes.

## Key Scenes

- **(004-002)**: The Governor dispatches Svistunov to ensure that visitors to town are properly accommodated — part of the preparations to receive the supposed inspector general.
- **(005-001)**: Svistunov reports back on the readiness of the cab, and informs the Governor that Prokhorov, another officer, is incapacitated after being found dead drunk and doused with two buckets of water without effect (005-002).
- **(028-002)**: In Act IV, the Governor summons Svistunov and Derzhimorda to guard the entrance and prevent petitioners — especially merchants — from reaching Khlestakov.
- **(030-001)**: Svistunov and Derzhimorda appear together, receiving orders to stand on the steps and forcibly eject anyone attempting to present a petition.

## Relationships

- **Stepan Ukhovertov** (police captain): Svistunov's direct superior in the police hierarchy.
- **Derzhimorda**: Fellow police sergeant; the two are frequently deployed together as a pair.
- **Anton Antonovich** (Governor): The ultimate authority Svistunov serves, who issues direct orders without intermediary.
- **Prokhorov**: A fellow officer whose drunken incapacitation Svistunov reports to the Governor.

## Character Analysis

Svistunov illustrates Gogol's technique of using minor characters to expose systemic dysfunction. The sergeant's unquestioning execution of orders — from arranging transport to physically blocking petitioners — mirrors the blind obedience that enables corruption at higher levels. The report of Prokhorov's drunkenness (005-002) underscores the broader incompetence of the town's police force, where even the enforcers are incapable of basic discipline.
