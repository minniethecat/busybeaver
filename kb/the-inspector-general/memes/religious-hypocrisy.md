# Religious Hypocrisy

## Definition

Religious hypocrisy in Gogol's Russia was the performative display of Orthodox piety — church attendance, fasting, the veneration of icons — while conducting business with moral indifference. Gogol, himself a deeply religious man whose later work took a mystical turn, was particularly attuned to the gap between public profession and private practice. His satire of religious hypocrisy is never an attack on religion itself but on its instrumentalization as social currency.

## In the Play

The Governor's self-description sets the tone: unlike the freethinking Judge, "I at least am a firm believer and go to church every Sunday" (002-002). This profession of faith is immediately undercut by his admission that he takes bribes and has "sins to answer for" (003-002). The church at the hospital — for which government funds were appropriated five years earlier — was never built (006-002), a fact that literalizes the gap between spiritual profession and material reality.

The play's most theologically pointed joke is the Governor's exclamation when he learns of the inspector's arrival: "Great God! What's that you're saying?" (004-002) — a genuine cry of terror that exposes the hollowness of his Sunday Christianity. When real judgment arrives (even in the form of a mistaken inspector), the Governor's religious observance provides no comfort.

## Historical Background

The Russian Orthodox Church in Gogol's era was a state institution, its bishops appointed by the Tsar and its local clergy dependent on the nobility for material support. Provincial priests were often poorly educated and poorly paid, and the gap between the Church's spiritual authority and its social reality was a subject of widespread commentary. Gogol's own religious development moved from the comic skepticism of his early works to the intense, almost messianic Orthodox piety of his later years — *Selected Passages from Correspondence with Friends* (1847) was condemned by his former allies for its religious conservatism.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's treatment of religion in *The Inspector General* is distinctive for its restraint. He does not mock faith itself — unlike the Voltairians the Governor despises — but exposes the self-serving uses to which faith is put. The Governor goes to church and takes bribes; the Judge never goes to church and takes bribes. The difference, Gogol suggests, is merely cosmetic. The unbuilt church at the hospital is the play's most devastating symbol: a sacred space that exists only on paper, its funds diverted into the same channels as everything else.
