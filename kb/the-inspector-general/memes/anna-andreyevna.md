# Anna Andreyevna

## Identity

Anna Andreyevna is the wife of Governor Anton Antonovich Skvoznik-Dmukhanovsky. Gogol describes her in the Dramatis Personae as "a provincial coquette, still this side of middle age, educated on novels and albums and on fussing with household affairs and servants. She is highly inquisitive and has streaks of vanity" (P03-002).

She lives in the Governor's House with her husband and daughter Marya Antonovna. Though not an office-holder herself, she is a significant figure in the play's social dynamics — the gatekeeper of provincial gentility, the arbiter of fashion, and the character whose ambitions most nakedly reveal the emptiness at the heart of the society Gogol anatomizes.

## Role in the Play

Anna Andreyevna's role centers on her social vanity and her competition with her daughter Marya Antonovna for Khlestakov's romantic attention. While the male officials panic about the inspector's investigation, Anna Andreyevna sees an entirely different opportunity: the supposed inspector is a man from St.

Petersburg, and his presence in her house is a chance for social elevation that may never come again.

Her entrance in Act I sets the tone for her character. She and Marya "rush in on the stage" (007-001) demanding to know everything about the visitor: "Where are they? Where are they? Oh, my God!...Is he a colonel or what? Eh?" (007-002).

The questions she shouts from the window — is he young, does he have a moustache, what color are his eyes — are not the questions of a governor's wife concerned about her husband's political survival but of a coquette assessing a potential romantic prospect. The Governor's dismissive response — "Wait, dear.

Later" (007-002) — establishes the dynamic of their marriage: she is an irritation to be managed, not a partner to be consulted.

In Act III, Anna Andreyevna and Marya prepare for the inspector's visit with comic intensity. Anna insists Marya wear her blue dress; Marya protests it makes her look like Liapkin-Tiapkin's wife. Anna declares she will wear her dun-colored dress; Marya tells her it does not suit her.

The argument over dresses is at once trivial and revealing: for these women, the inspector's visit is not a political crisis but a social occasion, and the primary weapon in their arsenal is appearance. Gogol's stage direction — "She changes her dress four times in the course of the play" (P03-002) — reduces her entire character to a costume change, a performance of identity through wardrobe.

When Anna finally meets Khlestakov, she is dazzled. She hangs on his every boastful word, believes his claims of intimacy with Pushkin and of directing a department, and afterward exclaims to her daughter: "Oh, how charming he is!...Such refined manners. You can recognize the big city article at once. How he carries himself, and all that sort of thing! Exquisite!

I'm just crazy for young men like him" (027-002). Her romantic enthusiasm — the middle-aged wife swooning over a twenty-three-year-old clerk — is both comic and pathetic. She has been "educated on novels and albums" (P03-002), and her response to the situation is entirely conditioned by the sentimental literature she has consumed.

She cannot see Khlestakov for what he is because she is seeing him through the lens of a romantic hero.

The competition with Marya for Khlestakov's attention is the play's most sustained domestic comedy. Khlestakov, characteristically, exploits the situation: he flatters the mother and flirts with the daughter, keeping both suspended in hope.

When he finally proposes to Marya — on his knees, having just been caught in that position by Anna (044-002) — Anna's response is not maternal concern but social calculation: a marriage to the supposed inspector would make her the mother-in-law of a high-ranking Petersburg official.

Her immediate pivot from romantic rival to future mother-in-law is the play's fastest character transformation — and a devastating demonstration of how social ambition overrides every other consideration.

In Act V, as the town's notables arrive to offer congratulations on the engagement, Anna Andreyevna is in her element — receiving guests, accepting compliments, and radiating the social triumph she has achieved. The guests kiss her hand, calling her "Anna Andreyevna," and then Marya's, calling her "Marya Antonovna" (053-001).

The ritual of congratulations is the social reward for which Anna has been striving throughout the play — and it is, of course, based entirely on an imposture. When the Postmaster arrives with Khlestakov's letter and the truth is revealed, Anna's collapse is as complete as the Governor's.

In the silent scene, she stands "straining toward" her husband (058-001) — the social climber frozen at the moment her ladder has been kicked away.

## Key Scenes

**Act I, Scene V (007-001)**. Anna and Marya rush in upon hearing of Khlestakov's arrival at the Governor's House. Anna shouts questions from the window and berates Marya for dawdling, revealing her impatient curiosity and her tendency to blame others.

**Act III, Scene I (020-001)**. Anna and Marya stand at the window, competing to identify the approaching figure in the street. The scene is a miniature of their relationship: competition disguised as collaboration.

**Act III, Scene V (025-001)**. Anna is present during Khlestakov's fantastical boasting session. She hangs on his every word, asking breathlessly whether he writes for the papers and declaring that she "guessed at once" that *Yury Miroslavsky* was his.

**Act III, Scene VIII (027-001)**. After Khlestakov's boasting, Anna and Marya discuss him, each convinced he favors her — the competition made explicit.

**Act IV, Scene XIII (044-002)**. Anna discovers Khlestakov on his knees before Marya. Her response — "Oh, what a situation!" — is outrage at the impropriety, not the proposal. The scene rapidly pivots to her managing the engagement.

**Act V, Scene V (052-001)**. The congratulations scene: Anna receives the town's notables, basking in her social triumph — unaware that the letter exposing the imposture is already in the Postmaster's hands.

**Act V, Silent Scene (058-001)**. Anna is frozen "straining toward" the Governor — the posture of a woman whose social world has collapsed and who instinctively reaches for the husband she has spent the entire play ignoring.

## Relationships

- **Anton Antonovich (Governor)**: Her husband. Gogol notes that "sometimes she gets the upper hand over her husband, and he gives in simply because at the moment he cannot find the right thing to say. Her ascendency, however, is confined to mere trifles and takes the form of lecturing and twitting" (P03-002). Their marriage is a power struggle conducted entirely through domestic trivia.

- **Marya Antonovna**: Her daughter and rival for Khlestakov's attention. Their mother-daughter competition — each convinced Khlestakov favors her — is a running comic motif that Gogol uses to expose the vanity of both women. Anna is willing to sacrifice her daughter's romantic prospects for her own ambitions, then equally willing to promote them when a better social outcome appears.

- **Ivan Khlestakov**: The supposed inspector. Anna sees him as a romantic and social opportunity, entirely misreading his character.

Her "education on novels and albums" (P03-002) has prepared her to see a romantic hero where there is only an empty vessel — making her the female counterpart to her husband, whose bureaucratic experience has prepared him to see a powerful official where there is only a copying clerk.

- **Dobchinsky**: The town gossip who brings Anna news of the inspector. She upbraids him as a godmother would — "I stood godmother to your Vanichka and Lizanko" (021-002) — revealing the network of provincial social obligations that bind the town's elite families.

## Character Analysis

Anna Andreyevna is Gogol's most sustained portrait of provincial feminine vanity, and her function in the play is more significant than her relatively few scenes might suggest.

She is the female counterpart to her husband: where the Governor's vanity is professional and official, measured in ranks and service records, Anna's is social and personal, measured in dresses, compliments, and the attentions of fashionable men.

Both are forms of status anxiety — *chinopochitanie* translated from the masculine world of the Table of Ranks to the feminine world of parlors and wardrobes.

Her "education on novels and albums" (P03-002) is crucial to understanding her role. Gogol suggests that sentimental literature has deformed her perception of reality as thoroughly as bureaucratic culture has deformed her husband's.

She interprets Khlestakov's empty boasts as evidence of refinement; she reads his casual flirtation as romantic interest; she transforms his accidental presence into a providential opportunity. Like her husband, she is not merely deceived — she actively deceives herself, constructing a narrative that flatters her vanity at every turn.

The contrast between Anna's social ambitions and the reality of provincial life is the source of her pathos. She longs for St. Petersburg society — for balls, literary salons, fashionable company — but she is trapped in a town "from which you might ride three years and not reach any other country" (002-002).

Khlestakov, the counterfeit Petersburger, represents everything she desires, and her desperate belief in him is a measure of her desperation to escape.

When the imposture is exposed, her devastation is not moral (she has not been corrupt in the way her husband has been) but social: she has been made ridiculous, and for a woman whose identity is entirely constituted by social performance, ridicule is annihilation.

## Historical Context

The figure of the provincial governor's wife had a recognizable social reality in Gogol's Russia. The wives of civil officials were expected to maintain a household appropriate to their husband's rank, to entertain visiting dignitaries, and to cultivate the social connections that could advance a career.

Anna Andreyevna's obsession with fashion — "she changes her dress four times in the course of the play" (P03-002) — reflects not merely personal vanity but the real social pressure on official wives to maintain appearances in a system where rank was signaled through clothing.

The education "on novels and albums" that Gogol attributes to Anna was a recognized cultural phenomenon in 1830s Russia. Sentimental novels — particularly those of French and English origin in translation, and the domestic imitations they inspired — were the primary reading material of provincial gentlewomen.

These novels cultivated romantic sensibilities and social ambitions that were often in painful contrast with the realities of provincial life. Gogol, whose own mother was an enthusiastic consumer of such literature, understood both its appeal and its dangers: it filled women's heads with fantasies that made them vulnerable to exploitation and eager for escape.
