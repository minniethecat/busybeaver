# Charitable Hospital

## Overview

The charitable [[charitable-institutions|hospital]] (*bogougodnoye zavedeniye*) is one of the principal municipal institutions in the provincial town, administered by [[artemy-zemlianika|Superintendent of Charities]] [[artemy-zemlianika|Artemy Filippovich Zemlianika]]. It serves as the play's most vivid example of institutional neglect disguised as public benevolence.

## Preparing for Inspection

When the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] briefs Zemlianika before [[ivan-khlestakov|the inspector]]'s supposed visit, his instructions are purely cosmetic. The patients should "not go about as they usually do, looking as grimy as blacksmiths" (002-002). He advises clean nightcaps and Latin placards over each bed stating "the name of the disease, when the patient fell ill, the day of the week and the month" (002-002).

He also suggests reducing the patient count: "It would be better, too, if there weren't so many of them. If there are a large number, it will instantly be ascribed to bad supervision or incompetent medical treatment" (002-002). The concern is not with curing the sick but with managing [[appearance|appearances]] — a smaller census implies fewer failures.

Zemlianika is unfazed: "Oh, that's a small matter. We can get nightcaps easily enough" (002-002). His treatment philosophy is nihilistic: "Our rule is: the nearer to nature the better. We use no expensive medicines. A man is a simple affair. If he dies, he'd die anyway. If he gets well, he'd get well anyway" (002-002).

The German doctor, Christian Ivanovich, adds another layer of absurdity: "he doesn't know a word of Russian" (002-002). Patients cannot communicate with the man supposed to cure them — a detail that passes without comment.

## The Inspection as Banquet

The [[charitable-institutions|hospital]] also functions as a site of [[charitable-institutions|hospital]]ity and bribery. The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] suggests entertaining [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] there: "We'll have something at the [[charitable-institutions|hospital]] with Artemy Filippovich" (021-002). The charitable institution doubles as a venue for [[bribery|corruption]].

After the meal, [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] remarks: "The lunch was excellent. I've positively overeaten" (024-002). When he asks where they dined, he's told it was the [[charitable-institutions|hospital]]. His response — "There were beds there. The patients must have gotten well. There don't seem to have been many of them" (024-002) — [[appearance|show]]s he registered the scene without questioning its absurdity.

Zemlianika seizes the opportunity. "[[About]] ten are left. The rest recovered. The place is so well run, there is such perfect order." He adds, incredibly: "ever since I've taken over the management, they all recover like flies" (024-002). He explains: this result comes "not so much by medicaments as by honesty and orderliness" (024-002).

## Significance

The [[charitable-institutions|hospital]] is the play's most damning portrait of failure. The [[district-courthouse|courthouse]] is comically disorderly; the school timidly dysfunctional; but the [[charitable-institutions|hospital]] is actively fatal. Zemlianika's philosophy is to let nature take its course — which, given no treatment, means letting patients die. The placards and nightcaps are not reforms but disguises, a performance of medicine without medical substance.
