# Education

## Introduction

Education in Gogol's *The Inspector General* is represented through the character of Luka Lukich Khlopov,
the Inspector of Schools, and through passing references to the town's educational institutions.
As with all civic functions in the play, education is portrayed as neglected, underfunded,
and staffed by incompetents whose primary concern is self-preservation rather than pedagogy.

## The Inspector of Schools

Luka Lukich Khlopov holds the title of Inspector of Schools [P02-004] and appears among the officials summoned by the Governor
at the play's opening [002-001]. Despite his title, Khlopov is characterized by extreme timidity:
when presenting himself to the supposed Inspector General, he is described as "trembling, with his hand on his sword" [036-002].
His rank of Titular Councilor (the ninth grade in the Table of Ranks) places him near the bottom of the civil-service hierarchy.

## The Schools as Civic Institutions

The schools are among the institutions the Governor offers to show Khlestakov during his tour of the town [018-002].
The Governor's eagerness to display these institutions suggests he believes they will withstand scrutiny,
yet his earlier admission that the schoolteachers exhibit "very queer behavior" calls this confidence into question.

## Gogol's Satirical Treatment

Gogol himself served briefly as a professor of history, and his portrait of the educational system
reflects a broader critique of the Tsarist civil service.
The Inspector of Schools is not a reformer or educator but a bureaucrat whose highest concern
is avoiding the displeasure of his superiors.
Education is reduced to another theater of appearances — institutions that must look functional
during an inspection but serve no genuine pedagogical purpose.
