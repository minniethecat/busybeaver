# Servants

The **servants** in *The Inspector General* occupy a distinctive position in the social fabric — they are subordinate to all other characters yet possess unique access to private spaces and information, making them unwitting witnesses to the folly of their social superiors.

## Named Servants

### Osip

Osip, Khlestakov's servant, is the most developed servant character. Listed in the *Dramatis Personae* as "his servant" [P02-013], he receives an unusually detailed character description: "A typical middle-aged servant, grave in his address" [P03-004]. Unlike his master, Osip is pragmatic and clear-sighted — he immediately perceives the precariousness of their situation at the inn [016-003].

Osip serves as Khlestakov's confidant and enabler, arranging practical matters such as luggage transport [018-002] while privately mocking his master's pretensions.

### Mishka

Mishka is the Governor's servant [P02-028], representing the domestic staff of the provincial administration's household. His presence underscores the Governor's social standing — even a corrupt provincial official maintains domestic servants.

## Social Position

Anna Andreyevna, the Governor's wife, is described in the character notes as being "deeply concerned with household affairs and servants" [P03-002], suggesting that the management of domestic staff is a significant preoccupation for women of her social class. Servants are simultaneously intimate members of the household and thoroughly subordinate — a tension that Gogol exploits for both comic and satirical effect.

## The Servant at the Inn

The servant at the inn where Khlestakov stays ([servant-at-the-inn](servant-at-the-inn)) represents another layer of the servant hierarchy — a commercial domestic worker who mediates between the travelling public and the innkeeper's establishment.
