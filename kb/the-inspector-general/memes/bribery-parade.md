# The Bribery Parade

## Summary

In Act IV, each of the town's officials visits Khlestakov privately in the Governor's residence to offer money under the transparent pretense of a "loan." The sequence forms the play's most extended comic set piece and its most systematic satirical argument.

The Judge enters trembling, drops money from his burning hand, and calls it a "loan." The Postmaster follows with practiced obsequiousness. The School Inspector is so terrified he tries to light the wrong end of a cigar. The Superintendent of Charities denounces every colleague before offering his money. Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky, having little to give, are mocked for their poverty.

After the visitors depart, Khlestakov counts the proceeds — "more than a thousand" (039-003) — and writes the letter that will expose the entire enterprise.

## Dramatic Function

The bribery parade is the structural heart of the play's satire. Each official's method of offering the bribe reveals his character: the Judge's legalistic distinction between bribery and "loans," the Postmaster's eagerness to please a superior, the School Inspector's paralyzing terror, Zemlianika's weaponization of denunciation.

The cumulative effect is a complete taxonomy of bureaucratic corruption — each official corrupt in his own way, all united in their dependence on a system they cannot imagine challenging.

The sequence also demonstrates the accelerating logic of the play's larger mechanism. The initial panic of Act I has become ritualized: the officials line up outside Khlestakov's door, the Judge organizes them "in military line, strictly in military line" (032-002), and each visitor performs the same comic pattern of fear, obsequiousness, bribe, and relief.

The repetition is the point — corruption is not exceptional but procedural, not hidden but performed according to unwritten rules that everyone understands.

Khlestakov's role in the sequence evolves from passive recipient to active exploiter. Initially surprised that the officials are bringing him money, he quickly learns to demand it. By the time Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky arrive, Khlestakov has transformed from a hungry clerk into a confident extortionist: "Have you any money?...A loan...a thousand rubles" (038-018).

The speed of this transformation — from victim of the innkeeper to predator of the bureaucracy — is Gogol's most compressed demonstration of how power corrupts.

## Key Moments

### The Assembly (Act IV, Scene I)

Before the individual visits begin, the officials gather in the corridor. They enter "cautiously, almost on tiptoe...in full dress-uniform" (032-001). The Judge — who has discoursed grandly about "the cause being a deep one and rather political in character" (002-002) — is now reduced to organizing a reception line: "For God's sake, gentlemen, quick, form your line, and let's have more order.

Why, man alive, he goes to Court and rages at the Imperial Council" (032-002). The officials debate whether each should present himself individually or collectively, ultimately deciding on individual audiences — a decision that ensures each will be fleeced separately.

### The Judge (Act IV, Scene III)

[[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge Liapkin-Tiapkin]] enters trembling, his knees knocking together. He draws himself up with his sword and formally presents himself. His internal monologue reveals the psychological mechanism of the bribe: "I have the money in my hand and my hand is on fire" (034-014).

He drops the bills on the floor in his agitation, and Khlestakov — the supposed inspector — casually picks them up and asks to "borrow" them. The Judge's distinction between a bribe and a loan — "I don't take bribes. Oh, if, for example, you would offer me a loan, then it would be quite another matter.

A loan I could take" (034-002) — is the play's definitive statement on bureaucratic self-deception. He exits in relief, concluding: "Well, the die is cast. The matter's settled now" (034-025).

### The Postmaster (Act IV, Scene IV)

[[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster Shpekin]] enters more composed than the Judge. Khlestakov engages him in small talk about the capital versus provincial life before asking for a loan of three hundred rubles. Shpekin immediately obliges, noting aside: "He isn't a bit proud — he inquires about everything" (035-018).

The Postmaster's practiced ease — he has been currying favor with superiors his entire career — is contrasted with the Judge's panic. Where the Judge rationalizes, the Postmaster simply conforms.

### The School Inspector (Act IV, Scene V)

[[luka-khlopov|Luka Khlopov]] is practically pushed into the room, a voice behind him urging "Don't be chickenhearted" (036-001). His terror is so extreme that when Khlestakov offers him a cigar, he attempts to light the wrong end and drops it from fright (036-018).

This physical comedy renders bureaucratic fear visible on stage — the trembling schoolmaster, a man whose job is to command respect from children, reduced to infantile terror before a supposed superior. Khlestakov gets three hundred rubles from him as well.

### The Superintendent of Charities (Act IV, Scene VI)

[[artemy-zemlianika|Artemy Zemlianika]] is the most calculating official. After offering his bribe, he draws his chair closer and begins denouncing every colleague in a lowered tone (037-013): the Postmaster does absolutely nothing and holds up the mail; the Judge hunts hares and keeps his dogs in the courtrooms; Dobchinsky's children all resemble the Judge rather than their supposed father.

Zemlianika's weaponization of denunciation reveals him as the most dangerous of the officials — a man who understands that in a corrupt system, the best defense is to destroy others before they can destroy you.

### Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky (Act IV, Scene VII)

[[piotr-dobchinsky|Dobchinsky]] and [[piotr-bobchinsky|Bobchinsky]] enter together, the two country squires who first identified Khlestakov as the inspector. Unlike the officials who preceded them, they have little money.

Khlestakov abruptly demands a thousand rubles (038-018); after negotiation — and Bobchinsky's improbable request that Khlestakov tell the Tsar "there is a Piotr Ivanovich Bobchinsky living in this town" (038-023) — they produce only sixty-five rubles between them (038-025).

Their appearance as the final supplicants completes the social spectrum, from the highest-ranking official to the lowliest squire — all equally convinced that greasing the inspector's palm is the only way to survive.

### Khlestakov Alone (Act IV, Scene VIII)

After the visitors depart, Khlestakov counts his money: "Here's three hundred from the Judge and three hundred from the Postmaster — six hundred, seven hundred, eight hundred...Rolls up to more than a thousand!" (039-003). His amusement at the officials' gullibility leads him to write the letter to Tryapichkin that will ultimately expose the entire deception (039-001).

The letter is the bribery parade's fatal coda: having fleeced the officials, Khlestakov now mocks them — and the mockery, when intercepted, will destroy them.

## Satirical Significance

The bribery parade achieves what no amount of moralizing could: it demonstrates the *mechanism* of systemic corruption. Each official's bribe follows the same pattern — fear, obsequiousness, money, relief — not because each official is the same kind of person, but because the system has taught them all the same script.

The Judge rationalizes ("a loan is quite another matter"); the Postmaster conforms; the School Inspector trembles; the Charity Superintendent denounces — but the result is identical. Gogol's argument is structural: in a system where rank is everything and accountability is nothing, bribery is not a crime but a survival strategy.

The sequence is also a brilliant piece of comic construction. The repetition-with-variation pattern — each visitor a variation on the same theme — builds momentum across six scenes. The speed of Khlestakov's transformation from victim to predator is the sequence's sharpest satirical point: corruption is learned behavior, and it is learned quickly.

By the time Khlestakov demands a thousand rubles from the penniless Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky, he has internalized the logic of the system he is exploiting.

The letter Khlestakov writes after the parade — "I'll write all about it to Triapichkin in St. Petersburg. He'll write them up in the papers" (039-001) — transforms the bribery parade from a local comic set piece into a national scandal. The officials have not merely been fooled; they have been documented. The money they gave to protect themselves will become the evidence that destroys them.
