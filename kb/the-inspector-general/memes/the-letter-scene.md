## Overview

The Letter Scene (Act V, Scene VIII) is the **dramatic climax** of *The Inspector General*, in which the Postmaster [[ivan-shpekin|Ivan Shpekin]] bursts into the Governor's house with Khlestakov's intercepted letter and reads it aloud to the assembled company. The scene systematically dismantles every character's self-deception as the letter mocks each official in turn [056-032, 056-035, 056-037, 056-039].

## Dramatic Context

The scene occurs immediately after the Governor's triumphant celebration (Act V, Scene VII), where the officials congratulate him on his daughter's engagement to the supposed "government inspector." The guests are still present, and the Governor has just declared his ambition to become a general [055-077]. Into this atmosphere of self-congratulation, the Postmaster arrives with devastating news [056-001].

## Scene Summary

### The Postmaster's Entrance

Shpekin rushes in holding an unsealed letter addressed to "Pochtamtskaya Street" in St. Petersburg. Overcome by "a supernatural power" of curiosity, he confesses to having opened it despite hearing an inner voice warning him not to [056-026–056-029]. The Governor, still believing Khlestakov to be a powerful official, is outraged that Shpekin would dare open such a personage's correspondence [056-030].

### The Revelation

Shpekin announces the truth: the supposed inspector general is "neither one thing nor another" — a complete impostor [056-038]. When the Governor threatens arrest, Shpekin retorts that the Governor no longer has the authority, and proceeds to read the letter aloud [056-048–056-054].

### The Letter's Contents

Khlestakov's letter to his friend Tryapichkin systematically insults every official in town in colorful terms [056-054]:

|-----------|--------------------------|----|
| The Governor | "as stupid as an old horse" | [056-054] |
| The Postmaster | "just like the porter Mikheyev…must drink just as hard" | [056-080] |
| Superintendent Zemlianika | "a regular pig in a cap" | [056-106] |
| School Inspector Khlopov | "reeks of onions" | [056-110] |
| Judge Liapkin-Tiapkin | "extremely *mauvais ton*" | [056-126] |

### Aftermath

The Governor is devastated by the public humiliation. He delivers a famous monologue of self-accusation: "I have been in the service thirty years…I have overreached one swindler after another" — yet he was fooled by "an icicle, a rag" [056-150–056-154]. He then turns on Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky, blaming them for first spreading the rumor that Khlestakov was the inspector [056-158–056-169].

## Thematic Significance

The Letter Scene functions as the **denouement** of the play, exposing the central irony: the officials' own guilty consciences led them to mistake a penniless clerk for a government inspector. The letter itself is a literary device — a confession written in private that, once made public, destroys the illusions upon which the entire plot has rested. Gogol uses it to drive home his satirical point that vanity, corruption, and self-deception are their own punishment.

## Key Quotes

> "A most astonishing thing, ladies and gentlemen! The official whom we took to be an inspector general is not an inspector general." [056-018]

> "I have been in the service thirty years. Not one merchant, not one contractor has been able to impose on me…And all of a sudden…to take an icicle, a rag for a personage of rank!" [056-150]

> "What are you laughing at? You are laughing at yourself!" [056-154]
