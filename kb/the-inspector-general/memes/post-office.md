# Post Office

## Overview

The post office was a vital institution in provincial Russia, connecting the vast empire through a relay system of stations, horses, and postmasters. It was simultaneously a public service, an instrument of state surveillance, and — as Gogol portrays it — a sieve through which private correspondence passed with minimal protection.

## In the Play

The post office provides the play's climactic revelation [053-001]. Postmaster Shpekin, who admits to opening letters "more out of curiosity than precaution" [011-002], intercepts Khlestakov's letter to his journalist friend Triapichkin and reads it aloud to the assembled officials [053-001]. The letter — in which Khlestakov mocks each official by name [054-001] — shatters the collective delusion and precipitates the play's famous silent-scene finale [055-002].

## Significance

Gogol's choice of the post office as the mechanism of revelation is thematically perfect: the truth arrives through the very institution designed to facilitate communication, but only because its administrator routinely violates the privacy it is supposed to protect [011-002]. The comedy of the intercepted letter depends on the audience's recognition that in Russia, privacy has never been more than a polite fiction.
