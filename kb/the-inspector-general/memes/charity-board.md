# Board of Charities

## Overview

The Board of Charities (*bogougodnye zavedeniya*, literally "God-pleasing institutions") administers the town's charitable hospital and related welfare functions. Under Superintendent Artemy Filippovich Zemlianika, the board exemplifies the gap between institutional mission and institutional performance — a charitable organization distinguished primarily by its lack of charity.

## In the Play

Zemlianika describes his management philosophy with unnerving candor: "I take care of the charitable institutions as though they were my own children" (024-002). The paternal metaphor is revealing — these are institutions to be possessed and managed for personal benefit, not impartial services to be administered for public good.

The Governor's instructions to Zemlianika before the inspector's visit focus entirely on cosmetic improvements: clean caps for patients, Latin inscriptions above beds, and a cover story for the church that was funded five years ago but never built. "Some blamed fool might forget and let out that the building was never even begun," the Governor warns (006-002). The board's primary activity is not charity but the management of its own reputation.

The board's corruption extends beyond neglect to active malfeasance. Zemlianika later denounces his colleagues to Khlestakov — the Judge, the Postmaster, the School Inspector — in a display of institutional backstabbing that reveals the board's internal dynamics as purely self-interested. Charity, in Gogol's presentation, is a theater of benevolence staged for the benefit of auditors.
