# The Petition Scene

## Overview

The petition scene in Act IV brings the town's abused citizens — merchants, a locksmith's wife, the widow of a noncommissioned officer — to Khlestakov's door, seeking redress against the Governor. It is the play's most explicit confrontation between the governed and their governors, and it captures the impossibility of justice in a system where the petitioners must appeal to a phantom authority.

## The Event

The scene unfolds in stages. First, Derzhimorda's voice is heard shouting "Where are you going, whiskers? You've been told that nobody is allowed to come in" (040-002) — the police physically block petitioners from reaching the supposed inspector. Khlestakov, curious and bored, orders them admitted.

The merchants enter and present their complaint: the Governor "puts his hand into everything" (041-002), extorting goods without payment, imprisoning those who refuse, and expecting gratitude (in the form of expensive gifts) for release. Their petition, addressed to "his most honorable, illustrious financial Excellency," uses absurdly inflated titles — Khlestakov himself observes "there's no such title" (040-002).

Fevronya Poshliopkina follows: her husband, a locksmith, was unlawfully conscripted because the Governor's son needed a tradesman for some work. She begins stating her case — "My father—" — but is cut off by the arrival of more supplicants and the Governor himself. Her story remains unfinished.

## The Governor's Counterattack

When the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] learns that the merchants have complained, he bursts in to defend himself. He assures Khlestakov that "not one half of what they said is so" and that the merchants themselves are cheats who "give short measure and short weight" [046-002]. The officer's widow, he claims, "flogged herself" [046-002].

This counterattack reveals the Governor's dual strategy: denial combined with counter-accusation. By painting the petitioners as liars and swindlers, he attempts to neutralize their testimony. The irony is that the Governor's accusations against the merchants — that they cheat and lie — are the very crimes of which he himself is guilty, projected onto his accusers.

## Dramatic Significance

The petition scene is the play's moment of potential accountability. The governed speak, the governor shrinks — but the audience to whom they appeal is a fraud. The scene's pathos lies in this misdirection: genuine grievances are presented to a person who cannot address them, while the person who could (the Governor) works frantically to prevent the petitioners from being heard. Justice is structurally impossible.

The scene also functions as a counterweight to the officials' self-serving narratives. Throughout the play, the townspeople have been dismissed as nuisances; here, for the first time, the audience hears their voices directly. The specificity of their complaints — illegal conscription, extortion of goods, public flogging — gives the satire a hard edge of social realism that distinguishes Gogol's comedy from mere farce [041-016, 042-018].
