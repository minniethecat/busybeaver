# The Mistaken Identity

## Overview

The mistaken identity is the foundational event of *The Inspector General*: two country squires, Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky, observe a well-dressed stranger at the inn who has been there two weeks without paying, and conclude — on no evidence whatsoever — that he must be the government inspector traveling incognito. This misidentification sets in motion every subsequent event in the play.

## The Event

The misidentification unfolds in three stages. First, Bobchinsky and Dobchinsky spot Khlestakov at the inn and note his fashionable Petersburg attire and his reluctance to pay his bill. Second, they leap to the conclusion that a man who does not pay must be an official traveling anonymously — a logic that reveals more about their expectations of official behavior than about Khlestakov himself. Third, they rush to the Governor with their discovery: "An extraordinary occurrence! An unexpected piece of news!" (004-002).

The Governor accepts their conclusion without question. His immediate mobilization of the town's officials — summoning them, issuing orders, preparing the town's appearance — transforms a groundless rumor into an institutional reality. The misidentification is not merely an error; it is an error that the entire administrative apparatus is structurally predisposed to believe.

## Dramatic Significance

The mistaken identity functions as the play's engine. Because Khlestakov never claims to be an inspector — he simply accepts the role thrust upon him — the event exposes the officials' psychology: they see an inspector because they fear an inspector. The event also establishes the play's fundamental comic mechanism: the gap between what characters believe and what the audience knows to be true.

## Consequences

The misidentification triggers: the Governor's panic-driven preparations (005-002), the officials' bribery parade (Act IV), Khlestakov's courtship of Marya Antonovna, his departure with funds extracted from the town, and ultimately the final revelation and catastrophe (Act V).
