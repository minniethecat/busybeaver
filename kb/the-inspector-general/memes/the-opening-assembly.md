# The Opening Assembly

## Summary

The play opens with the Governor, Anton Antonovich, addressing the assembled officials of the provincial town. "I have called you together, gentlemen, to tell you an unpleasant piece of news. An Inspector General is coming" (001-001). The announcement — that the inspector is from St. Petersburg, traveling incognito, and bearing secret instructions — sets the play's engine in motion.

## The Governor's Premonition

Before reading the letter, the Governor recounts a dream that he interprets as an omen: "Last night I kept dreaming of two rats — regular monsters! Upon my word, I never saw the likes of them — black and supernaturally big. They came in, sniffed, and then went away" (001-001). The dream establishes the Governor's superstitious character and the atmosphere of dread that pervades the opening scene.

## The Fateful Letter

The Governor reads aloud a letter from Andrey Ivanovich, a reliable contact, warning: "An official has arrived here with instructions to inspect the whole government, and your district especially. I have learned of his being here from highly trustworthy sources, though he pretends to be a private person" (002-001). The Governor skips over the letter's flattering preamble ("my dear friend, godfather and benefactor" — 002-001) and cuts straight to the threat.

## Reaction and Speculation

The officials react with alarm. Judge Ammos Fiodorovich proposes a political theory: "Russia intends to go to war, and the Government has secretly commissioned an official to find out if there is any treasonable activity anywhere" (003-001). The Governor dismisses this — "Treason in this little country town! As if it were on the frontier! Why, you might gallop three years away from here and reach nowhere" (003-001) — but the impulse to find hidden meaning in the inspector's arrival, however absurd, characterizes the officials' collective panic.

## Individual Instructions

The Governor proceeds to issue corrective instructions to each department head. He tells Artemy Filippovich to clean up the hospital — the patients "looking as grimy as blacksmiths" (004-001) — and to post Latin placards above each bed. He reprimands Ammos Fiodorovich for allowing geese to roam the courthouse entrance hall (005-001) and for the assessor who "reeks of spirits, as if he had just emerged from a distillery" (005-001). He warns Luka Lukich about the history teacher who, when lecturing on Alexander of Macedon, "jumped down from the platform, picked up a chair and dashed it to the floor" (006-001).

## The Postmaster's Entrance

In Scene II, the Postmaster Ivan Kuzmich arrives and immediately guesses, "There'll be a war with the Turks" (007-001) — echoing Ammos's earlier theory. The Governor corrects him: "It's we who are going to get it, not the Turks" (007-001). The Governor then makes a fateful request: that the Postmaster open and read every letter passing through the post office "for the common benefit of us all" (007-001), to check for denunciations. The Postmaster readily agrees — he already does so "not so much as a precaution as out of curiosity" (007-001).
