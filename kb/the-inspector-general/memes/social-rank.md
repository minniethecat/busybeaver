# Social Rank

## Definition

*Chin* (rank) in Imperial Russia was more than a bureaucratic classification — it was the organizing principle of social identity. The Table of Ranks, introduced by Peter the Great in 1722, divided the nobility and civil service into fourteen grades, each carrying specific privileges, forms of address, and sumptuary entitlements. But *chin* as a social phenomenon transcended the formal table: it became an obsession, a lens through which every human interaction was refracted.

## In the Play

Rank consciousness pervades every scene of *The Inspector General*. When Khlestakov arrives at the inn, the Governor and his officials do not ask who he is — they ask what his rank is. Dobchinsky speculates anxiously: "What do you think is his rank, Piotr Ivanovich?" (026-002). The question is not incidental but diagnostic: in a society where rank determines everything about how a person must be treated, misidentifying rank is the most dangerous possible error.

The Governor's psychological profile, as sketched in the Directions for Actors, is built around rank: "He has the typical hard stern features of the official who has worked his way up from the lowest rank in the arduous government service" (P03-001). His entire personality — the oscillation between arrogance and servility, the carefully calibrated dignity — is a product of the rank system.

Khlestakov's imposture succeeds precisely because he does not claim any specific rank. He allows the officials to project their own rank-anxieties onto him. When Osip is asked about his master's rank, he replies evasively: "The usual rank" (029-002). This ambiguity is the imposture's genius — each official fills in the blank with whatever authority he most fears.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's treatment of rank exposes its absurdity without proposing an alternative. The officials are simultaneously terrified of rank — because a high-ranking inspector can destroy them — and obsessed with it — because their own status depends entirely on its recognition. The play's final irony is that Khlestakov, the lowest-ranking person in the drama (a mere collegiate registrar, the fourteenth and lowest civil rank), is taken for a general because he wears a fashionable coat and speaks with St. Petersburg confidence. Rank, Gogol suggests, is not a quality of the person but a performance that the audience — the provincial officials — is all too eager to believe.
