## Overview

The dining room of the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s House is briefly but memorably referenced in Act II of Gogol's
*The Inspector General*. Connected to the kitchen, it serves as the backdrop for the chaotic
household preparations when the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s family learns of [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]]'s impending arrival.

## The Scene

In Act II, the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s wife [[anna-andreyevna|Anna Andreyevna]] and daughter [[marya-antonovna|Marya Antonovna]] prepare for the
visit of the supposed government inspector. The scene is marked by domestic bustle and farce.

[[anna-andreyevna|Anna Andreyevna]] describes the commotion: "I nearly dropped dead when I passed through the kitchen.
And in the dining-room this morning two short little men were eating salmon and sturgeon and
something else" [014-002]. This description — of Dob[[social-rank|chin]]sky and Bob[[social-rank|chin]]sky helping themselves
to the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s food — captures the informality and social ambition of provincial life.

## The Kitchen Connection

The dining room's proximity to the kitchen is significant. Gogol establishes a spatial geography
where food preparation and genteel entertaining are in uncomfortably close quarters. The fact
that [[anna-andreyevna|Anna Andreyevna]] must pass through the kitchen to reach the dining room [014-002] suggests
a house layout that is more provincial aspiration than [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] refinement.

## Significance

The dining room represents the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s social pretensions. Through it, Gogol satirizes the
provincial [[nobility|gentry]]'s attempts to imitate metropolitan manners. The image of the two landowners
(Dob[[social-rank|chin]]sky and Bob[[social-rank|chin]]sky) gorging themselves on the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s food before reporting the
important news creates a comic dissonance between the gravity of the situation — an inspector
is in town — and the petty domestic comedy unfolding in the dining room.
