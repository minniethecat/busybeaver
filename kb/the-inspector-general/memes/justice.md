# Justice

## Definition

The judicial system in Gogol's provincial Russia was administered by elected judges who, like other officials, supplemented their meager salaries through informal payments. The court served not as an impartial arbiter of disputes but as another arena in which power, status, and money were transacted. Justice, in this context, was a commodity — distributed according to rank and connection rather than law.

## In the Play

Judge Ammos Fiodorovich Liapkin-Tiapkin is the play's embodiment of judicial failure. His courtroom has become a hunting ground — literally: geese have taken up residence in the anteroom, and the assessor reeks of vodka. The Governor's warning to him before the supposed inspector's visit is telling: "You don't believe in God. You never go to church. I at least am a God-fearing man. But you — oh, I know you. You're a freethinker" (002-002). The critique is not that the Judge is unjust — it is that he is unorthodox, a deviation from the expected pattern of hypocritical piety.

When Liapkin-Tiapkin offers Khlestakov money, he constructs an elaborate distinction: "I don't take bribes. Oh, if, for example, you would offer me a loan, then it would be quite another matter. A loan I could take" (034-002). This piece of legal sophistry — redefining a bribe as a loan — is the play's most precise satire of how language is manipulated to disguise corruption within the judicial system.

The merchants' complaint to the supposed inspector reveals the court's true function: "he'll put you in prison if he wants. And if you do pay him, then he'll let you out" (041-002). Justice is reduced to a simple transaction, with no pretense of legal principle.

## Satirical Function

The figure of the Judge allows Gogol to attack the judicial system from within. Liapkin-Tiapkin is not a mere criminal but a "freethinker" — he has read "four or five books" and considers himself intellectually above his colleagues. His corruption is not brutish but philosophical: he has constructed a worldview in which bribery is not bribery if properly relabeled. Gogol's satire suggests that the most dangerous corruption is not the ignorant kind but the self-justifying, intellectually elaborate variety — the kind that can look at a bribe and call it a loan without blinking.

## Historical Background

Elected judges in provincial Russia served limited terms and were paid by the state. The position carried prestige but insufficient income, creating structural pressure toward informal payments. The "loan" euphemism employed by Liapkin-Tiapkin was not Gogol's invention but a documented practice: officials routinely accepted money as "temporary accommodation," with the understanding — understood by all parties — that repayment would never be demanded.
