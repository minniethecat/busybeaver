# The Engagement Announcement

## Summary

The engagement of Khlestakov to Marya Antonovna spans the transition from Act IV to Act V and represents the peak of the Governor's family's delusion. In Act IV, Khlestakov, carried away by his own momentum after proposing to both mother and daughter in quick succession, secures the Governor's blessing. In Act V, the Governor and his wife celebrate their imagined elevation — dreaming of St. Petersburg mansions, generalships, and the humiliation of their enemies — moments before the Postmaster's intercepted letter destroys their fantasy forever.

## In the Play

### The Double Proposal (Act IV, Scenes XIII–XIV)

Khlestakov's proposal unfolds with characteristic absurdity. Caught on his knees before Marya, he is discovered by Anna Andreyevna, who demands an explanation. Khlestakov, "aside: She's very appetizing, too," promptly shifts his declaration to the mother: "No, I am in love with you. My life hangs by a thread" ([044-002]). When Anna reminds him she is married, Khlestakov invokes Karamzin: "The laws condemn. We will fly in the shadow of a brook" ([044-002]).

Marya's sudden return complicates matters. Anna berates her daughter as "a child of three" lacking "good manners and a proper demeanor" — then is stunned when Khlestakov seizes Marya's hand and declares: "Anna Andreyevna, don't oppose our happiness. Give your blessing to our constant love" ([045-002]). The Governor's wife pivots instantly: "Well, there, you fool, you see? Our guest is pleased to go down on his knees for such trash as you" ([045-002]).

### The Triumph (Act V, Scene I)

The engagement announcement transforms the Governor into a figure of grotesque triumph. He enters Act V crowing:

> From just a simple governor's wife suddenly — whew! — I'll be hanged! — to marry into the family of such a big gun. ([049-002])

The Governor immediately plans vengeance against the merchants and petitioners who complained about him: "I used to dose you down to your ears. Now I'll dose you down to your beards" ([049-002]). His imagination inflates as rapidly as Khlestakov's lies: he envisions a generalship, debates the merits of the red St. Anne ribbon versus the blue St. Andrew, and relishes the image of keeping lesser officials waiting at his door.

### The Merchants' Humiliation (Act V, Scene II)

The merchants who had petitioned Khlestakov against the Governor are summoned and subjected to a gloating tirade. The Governor reminds them of his past protection: "And who helped you with that grafting when you built a bridge and charged twenty thousand for wood when there wasn't even a hundred rubles' worth used? I did" ([050-002]). The merchants prostrate themselves, and the Governor — magnanimous in victory — forgives them, warning darkly: "Don't try to get away with a dried sturgeon or a loaf of sugar" as a wedding gift ([050-002]).

## Dramatic Irony

The engagement announcement is the play's most concentrated instance of dramatic irony. The audience knows what the Governor does not: Khlestakov is a penniless clerk who has already fled the town in a carriage ordered by Osip. Every boast about the family's elevation, every fantasy of St. Petersburg grandeur, is built on a foundation of air. Gogol structures the scene so that the higher the Governor ascends in his imagination, the harder the fall will be — a fall that arrives within minutes when the Postmaster enters with Khlestakov's intercepted letter.

The scene also deepens the play's satire of provincial ambition. Anna Andreyevna's insistence that "our life will have to be completely changed" and that her room must have "so much amber in it that when you come in you have to shut your eyes" ([049-002]) reveals that she and her husband are not merely corrupt but absurd — their fantasies are as disconnected from reality as Khlestakov's lies.
