# Servant at the Inn

## Identity

The Servant at the Inn is an unnamed minor character listed in the Dramatis Personae as "Servant at the inn" [P02-029]. He waits tables at the provincial inn where [[ivan-khlestakov|Ivan Khlestakov]] and his servant [[osip|Osip]] have been lodging without paying for two weeks. He appears in Act II, Scenes IV and VI, serving as the intermediary between Khlestakov and the innkeeper.

## Role in the Play

The Servant's function in the play is both practical and thematic. Practically, he delivers the innkeeper's ultimatum: Khlestakov will receive no more food until his existing bill is settled [012-002]. Thematically, his presence underscores Khlestakov's desperate circumstances before his mistaken elevation to "inspector." Through the Servant's dialogue, Gogol paints a vivid picture of Khlestakov's degradation:

> "The landlord said he won't let you have anything any more. He was all for going to the Governor today and making a complaint against you." [012-002]

Khlestakov's wheedling response — "Judge for yourself, friend. Why, I've got to eat. If I go on like this I'll turn into a skeleton" [012-002] — reveals his essential character: feckless, manipulative, and utterly dependent on the goodwill of others. The Servant, a working man who has seen plenty of deadbeat guests, is unimpressed.

In Scene VI, the Servant returns with a meager dinner of soup and roast beef, pointedly informing Khlestakov that "this is the last time the landlord will let you have dinner." When Khlestakov complains about the poor quality of the food — "there is some [salmon] and there isn't" — the Servant delivers the play's most succinct class commentary: the better food is reserved "only for the better kind of folk" [012-002]. Unlike the officials who later lavish Khlestakov with bribes and deference, the Servant treats him with the casual indifference accorded to a penniless nobody. This makes him one of the few characters in the play who sees Khlestakov for what he actually is.

The Servant's role also illuminates the play's concern with [[social-status|social hierarchy]] and the gap between appearance and reality. To the Servant, Khlestakov is simply a deadbeat — a judgment that turns out to be more accurate than the elaborate fantasies projected onto him by the town's terrified officialdom. This ironic clarity links him thematically to [[osip|Osip]], the other servant figure who sees through pretension with working-class common sense.

## Key Scenes

- **(012-002, Act II Scene IV)**: Delivers the landlord's ultimatum — no more food until Khlestakov pays his bill.
- **(012-002, Act II Scene VI)**: Serves Khlestakov his final meal and makes the play's sharpest class-based observation: better food is reserved "only for the better kind of folk."

## Relationships

- **[[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]]**: A deadbeat guest whom he treats with professional indifference — one of the few characters who sees Khlestakov clearly.
- **[[osip|Osip]]**: Khlestakov's servant, fellow occupant of the inn; together they represent the world of servants who quietly observe their masters' follies.
