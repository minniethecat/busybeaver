# Appearance vs. Reality

## Introduction

The tension between appearance and reality is the structural foundation of Gogol's *The Inspector General*.
Every character in the play maintains a public facade that conceals private corruption, incompetence, or vacuity.
The central comic premise — that Khlestakov is mistaken for an incognito inspector — is itself an elaborate
confusion of appearance for reality, and the play's enduring power lies in its demonstration that
the two are indistinguishable in a society built on performance.

## The Town as Stage Set

When the Governor offers to show Khlestakov the town's institutions, he frames the tour as an exhibition:
"In other towns, I venture to observe, the authorities and officials look out for themselves more.
Here, I may say, we have no other thought than to earn the government's good opinion through order and vigilance" [024-002].
The town has been hastily prepared to present an appearance of good governance — streets swept,
policemen stationed, patients in clean caps — while the reality of corruption and neglect remains hidden.

## The Officials' Public Faces

Each official presents himself to Khlestakov in full uniform, sword in hand, with formal military bearing [035-002][036-002][037-002].
The repeated choreography of "drawing himself up" and declaring "I have the honor to present myself" creates a ritual of
self-presentation that is simultaneously comic and grotesque.
The officials' anxiety about proper appearances is captured in the Judge's desperate plea:
"quick, form your line, and let's have more order" [032-002].

## Khlestakov's Performance

Khlestakov does not intend to impersonate an inspector general; the role is thrust upon him by the officials'
readiness to believe appearances. His subsequent boasting — about his Petersburg connections,
his literary achievements, his extravagant lifestyle — is simply a more elaborate version of
the same performance every character in the play is already engaged in.
The difference between Khlestakov and the officials is not that he is a fraud and they are genuine,
but that he is a more successful performer.

## Gogol's Epigraph

The play's epigraph — "Don't blame the mirror if your mug is crooked" — distills the appearance-reality theme
to its essence. The play is a mirror held up to the audience, and if what they see is ugly,
the fault lies not in the reflection but in the reality it reveals.
