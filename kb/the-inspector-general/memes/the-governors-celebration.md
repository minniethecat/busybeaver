# The Governor's Celebration

## Summary

Act V opens in triumph. The Governor and his family, believing Khlestakov's imposture and his engagement to Marya Antonovna, exult in their sudden elevation. The first seven scenes of Act V form an extended set piece of hubris and social comedy — the Governor dreams of a generalship, Anna of St. Petersburg society, and the town's officials file through in a parade of congratulation more absurd than the bribery parade that preceded it. The entire sequence is shadowed by dramatic irony: the audience knows the exposure is imminent.

## Domestic Triumph

The Governor is euphoric: "What gay birds we have turned into now, you and I. Eh, Anna Andreyevna? High fliers, by Jove!" (053-001). He immediately pivots to revenge: "I'll give those fellows who were so eager to present their petitions and denunciations a peppering" (053-001). The Governor's first instinct on achieving power is to abuse it — he threatens to make the merchants "smart," and relishes his new status: "Make a list of all who came to protest against me" (054-001).

## The Generalship Dream

The Governor and Anna debate their future. Anna insists on St. Petersburg — "How could we remain here?" (049-002). The Governor fantasizes about military rank: "Ah, the devil take it, it's nice to be a general. They hang a ribbon across your shoulders" (049-002). He asks Anna whether the red St. Anne or blue St. Andrew ribbon is better, and when Anna chooses blue, he exclaims: "My, you're aiming high!" (049-002). His fantasy of power is entirely external: when a general travels, "there are always couriers and aides on ahead with 'Horses'!" and lesser officials wait while he dines with the governor-general (049-002).

Anna's ambitions are equally materialistic: "I shall insist upon our house being the first in the capital and my room having so much amber in it that when you come in you have to shut your eyes" (049-002). The Governor, characteristically, thinks of food: "They say there are two kinds of fish there, the sea-eel and the smelt" (049-002).

## The Merchants' Humiliation

The merchants who petitioned against the Governor now crawl before him: "We are guilty, Anton Antonovich. The evil spirit tempted us" (058-001). The Governor, magnanimous in victory, demands tribute for his daughter's wedding: "Don't try to get away with a dried sturgeon or a loaf of sugar" (059-001).

## The Congratulation Parade

Scenes III through VII present a parade of officials offering congratulations. Zemlianika kisses hands effusively; the Police Captain wishes "long years of prosperity" (055-002). Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky bump foreheads competing to kiss the ladies' hands (053-003). Luka Lukich's wife delivers a comic monologue of sycophancy: "Really, it made me so glad that I couldn't say a word. I cried and cried. I simply screamed" (054-002).

Throughout the celebration, Artemy Zemlianika's asides puncture the surface: "Good luck always does crawl into the mouths of swine like him" (055-002). These muttered resentments are a reminder that the social order is held together by fear, not loyalty — a fact that will be devastatingly confirmed in Scene VIII.
