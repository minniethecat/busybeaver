# Domestic Service

## Definition

Domestic service in Imperial Russia encompassed a range of positions from the household serf (*dvornya*) — who was legally the property of his or her master — to the hired urban servant who received wages. In provincial noble households, servants performed all domestic labor: cooking, cleaning, waiting at table, driving carriages, running errands, and attending to the personal needs of the family. The servant class occupied the lowest rung of the social hierarchy but, uniquely, lived in intimate proximity to the highest — a position that gave servants a distinctive perspective on their masters' private lives.

## In the Play

Servants in *The Inspector General* are peripheral figures who occasionally illuminate the action from below. Osip, Khlestakov's middle-aged servant, is the play's most acute social commentator. His soliloquy in Act II, scene 1, offers a devastating assessment of his master: a wastrel who gambles away his traveling money and then demands the best room at the inn. Osip's perspective — "grave in his address, with eyes always cast down" (P03-004) — provides the audience with the one authentic moral compass in the play, even as he remains powerless to change anything.

Mishka, the Governor's servant, appears only briefly — he is sent to fetch the police and return with the news of a fight outside the town, a minor comic errand that reveals the Governor's reliance on his household staff. The **servant at the inn**, who first describes Khlestakov to the alarmed officials, similarly occupies a functional role: he delivers exposition from the lower-class perspective, confirming that the mysterious visitor is "a young man of good appearance in civilian dress" (004-002).

## Historical Background

In Gogol's Russia, the boundary between serfdom and service was blurry. Wealthy nobles might maintain dozens of household serfs who performed specialized roles — cooks, valets, maids, coachmen, and footmen. These serfs were legally chattel but often possessed skills and literacy that exceeded those of the provincial merchants they served. The urban servant who worked for wages represented a transitional figure: still socially inferior, but not legally bound to a single master.

Gogol's treatment of servants reflects both the conventions of Russian comedy (the clever servant is a stock figure going back to Plautus) and the specific conditions of the 1830s, when the institution of serfdom was increasingly subject to moral critique. Osip's combination of loyalty to Khlestakov and clear-eyed contempt for his master's behavior captures the psychological complexity of the servant-master relationship.

## Satirical Function

The servants in *The Inspector General* function as a silent Greek chorus, observing the folly of their social superiors with a clarity the superiors themselves lack. Osip is the only character who recognizes that Khlestakov is an impostor — not through any detective work, but through the intimate knowledge of his master's habits that only a servant possesses. Gogol's satirical point is subtle but devastating: in a world where everyone is deceived by appearances, only those at the very bottom can see clearly — and they have no power to act on what they see.
