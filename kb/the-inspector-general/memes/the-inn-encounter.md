# The Inn Encounter

## Summary

Act II Scene VIII brings the Governor and Khlestakov face to face for the first time at the squalid inn. It is the play's foundational comic scene: every word each man utters is interpreted by the other through the wrong frame of reference. The Governor, convinced he is facing the inspector, hears threats and accusations; Khlestakov, convinced he is about to be arrested for non-payment, protests his innocence. The scene demonstrates the complete absence of communication that drives the plot forward.

## The Entrance

The Governor advances a few steps and stops. "They stare at each other a few moments wide-eyed and frightened" (016-002). The stage direction is critical: the extended mutual stare establishes the shared terror that underlies the comedy. The Governor, "recovering himself a little and saluting military fashion," announces: "I have come to present my compliments, sir" (016-002). His military salute is both absurd and revealing — he is treating a twenty-three-year-old copying clerk as a personage requiring military deference.

## The Misunderstanding Unfolds

Khlestakov, interpreting the visit as a threat of arrest, immediately launches into a defensive rant about the inn's conditions: "He gives me beef as hard as a board and the soup — the devil knows what he put into it. I ought to have pitched it out of the window" (024-001). The Governor, convinced the inspector is complaining about the town's conditions, apologizes profusely: "I assure you, it's not my fault. I always have good beef in the market here" (025-001). The two men are having entirely separate conversations.

Khlestakov escalates: "I won't go even if you come here with your whole force. I'll go straight to the minister" (026-001). This threat, which Khlestakov intends as resistance against arrest, the Governor hears as confirmation of his worst fear — the inspector has already found him out and will denounce him to the highest authorities.

## The Governor's Confession

Terrified, the Governor confesses to petty corruption: "If I have taken bribes, they were mere trifles — something for the table, or a coat or two. As for the officer's widow to whom they say I gave a beating, she's in business now, and it's a slander" (027-001). Khlestakov, who has no idea what the Governor is talking about, replies with magnificent irrelevance: "I don't see, though, why you should talk to me about your scoundrels or officer's widow. An officer's widow is quite a different matter" (028-001).

## The Bribe as Loan

The misunderstanding resolves when the Governor offers money — "It is my duty to assist strangers in town" (016-003). Khlestakov immediately asks for a loan of two hundred rubles. The Governor gives four hundred, but says two hundred, and notes aside: "I slipped four hundred instead of two into his hand" (016-003). This moment transforms the dynamic: Khlestakov now sees the Governor as "openhearted and generous" (016-003), while the Governor believes he has successfully bribed the inspector.

## The Invitation

The Governor invites Khlestakov to stay at his own residence: "I have a fine room for you at home, light and cosy" (016-003). Khlestakov accepts with pleasure, preferring a "private house" to the "disreputable tavern" (016-003). The Governor sends Dobchinsky ahead with notes to Zemlianika at the hospital and to his wife, preparing the house for the distinguished guest.
