# Luka Lukich Khlopov

## Identity

Luka Lukich Khlopov serves as Inspector of Schools of the provincial town [P02-004]. He is the town's education official, responsible for the district school and its teachers — a role that fills him with the very terror he is supposed to inspire in others.

His rank, Titular Councilor (grade 9 in the Table of Ranks), places him near the bottom of the bureaucratic hierarchy among the assembled officials. This low rank, combined with his position of nominal authority over teachers, creates a structural irony: he is simultaneously an authority figure (to his subordinates) and a subordinate (to everyone else in the room).

His perpetual terror is the emotional expression of this structural position — caught between those he must command and those he must obey, he commands no one effectively and fears everyone.

## Role in the Play

### The Opening Announcement

Khlopov is defined by fear from his first moment on stage. When the Governor announces the inspector general's imminent arrival, Khlopov's immediate reaction is:

> "Good Lord! With secret instructions!" [002-002]

The line is his signature. Where the other officials respond with speculation (the Judge) or strategic calculation (Zemlianika), Khlopov's response is pure, undiluted terror. The "secret instructions" terrify him because they multiply his vulnerability: not only is an inspector coming, but his mandate is unknown, which means everything he does could be grounds for condemnation.

The Governor specifically warns Khlopov about his teachers:

> "That queer fellow who makes faces" when he lectures, and the history teacher who "gets so worked up that he loses control of himself" and once "smashed a chair on the floor" [002-002].

These details reveal that the school system is as dysfunctional as every other institution in the town. Khlopov's teachers are not incompetent in the ordinary bureaucratic sense — they are literally out of control, making faces and destroying furniture. The school is less an educational institution than a containment facility for eccentrics.

### The Bribery Scene

In Act IV, Khlopov is "practically pushed in" to Khlestakov's room during the bribery sequence [036-001]. A voice behind him is heard saying "Don't be chickenhearted" — a command that simultaneously acknowledges and exacerbates his terror. His entrance is the most reluctant of all the officials; he must be physically propelled into the presence of the man he fears.

His audience with Khlestakov [036-002] is a masterpiece of physical comedy:

> "Drawing himself up, trembling, with his hand on his sword. I have the honor to present myself — School Inspector, Titular Councilor Khlopov."

The formal military posture ("drawing himself up," "hand on his sword") is undermined by the verb "trembling" — his body betrays what his words attempt to conceal. When Khlestakov offers him a cigar, he cannot decide whether to accept: "There now! That's something I hadn't anticipated. To take or not to take?" [036-002].

When he tries to light it, his hands shake so violently that he cannot manage the simple physical operation.

His terror is so extreme and so visible that Khlestakov finds him pitiable. Instead of the elaborate bribery choreography the other officials perform, Khlestakov simply asks Khlopov directly for money — and Khlopov, relieved to have a clear instruction, complies without the semantic gymnastics of the Judge's "loan."

### Final Scenes

In the play's closing moments, Khlopov and his wife appear among the assembled guests [054-001]. He attempts to maintain dignity, offering "I have the honor" [054-002], but the impending revelation reduces him — like every other official — to terrified paralysis.

His wife's gushing congratulations to Anna Andreyevna on the betrothal provide a comic counterpoint: the domestic rituals of provincial society continue even as the foundations crumble.

## Key Scenes

- **(002-001 to 002-002)**: Khlopov reacts with terror to the inspector general news: "Good Lord! With secret instructions!" The Governor gives him specific instructions about controlling his teachers — the face-maker and the chair-smasher.

- **(036-001 to 036-002)**: Khlopov is pushed into Khlestakov's room for the bribe. He shakes so badly he cannot light a cigar. Khlestakov directly asks for money, and Khlopov is relieved to comply with a simple demand.

- **(054-001 to 054-002)**: Khlopov and his wife attend the gathering where Khlestakov's letter is read, leading to universal humiliation. His presence in this scene, attempting dignity while knowing exposure is imminent, distills his character to its essence.

- **(056-002)**: His humiliation in the letter: "The School Inspector reeks of onions." His protest — "Upon my word, I never put an onion to my mouth" — is the weak denial of a man whose defense mechanisms have been exhausted.

- **(058-001)**: Present in the final Silent Scene, petrified with the rest of the company at the Gendarme's announcement [057-002].

## Relationships

- **Anton Antonovich (Governor)**: Khlopov's superior, who bullies him about the quality of the town's teachers. The Governor's instructions to Khlopov are delivered in the same tone he uses for all his subordinates — a mixture of contempt and urgency that reveals he considers Khlopov's domain as significant only insofar as it could embarrass the administration.

- **The District School Teachers**: Khlopov's subordinates, described as even more eccentric than their inspector.

The history teacher who "smashes a chair on the floor" [002-002] when lecturing about Alexander the Great is simultaneously an indictment of Khlopov's management (he cannot control his staff) and a suggestion that madness is the natural product of an educational system designed to produce obedient functionaries rather than independent thinkers.

The teachers are what happens when the pursuit of knowledge meets the bureaucratic demand for compliance.

- **Ivan Khlestakov**: The supposed inspector. Khlopov's terror before him is so extreme it becomes physically comedic. His shaking hands [036-002] are the exact physical manifestation of bureaucratic fear: the body refusing to perform the composure the mind commands.

## Character Analysis

"Good Lord! With secret instructions!" [002-002] — Khlopov's first line — encapsulates his function in the play. He embodies bureaucratic terror in its purest form: a man whose job title makes him an object of fear to his subordinates, yet who lives in perpetual terror of being inspected himself. The irony is exquisite: the Inspector of Schools is terrified of an inspector.

His shaking hands in Act IV [036-002] are the physical manifestation of a system in which everyone fears the person above them, and no one is secure.

Khlopov is the play's study in the psychology of the middle manager. He occupies the most precarious position in the bureaucratic hierarchy: high enough to be held accountable for the failures of his subordinates, low enough to have no power to remedy those failures.

His terror is not personal weakness but structural necessity — the emotional adaptation required of anyone who must simultaneously command and obey.

The description of his teachers — the face-maker, the chair-smasher — suggests that Khlopov's school is less an institution of learning than a theater of neurosis. The teachers' eccentricities are the price of their employment: in a system that rewards conformity, only eccentrics survive, and only by becoming more eccentric.

Khlopov's inability to control them is not individual incompetence but systemic impossibility — the system produces the very disorder it then punishes.

Khlopov's hands shaking over the cigar [036-002] is one of the play's most precise images. The cigar — a Petersburg luxury, offered by a man he believes to be a Petersburg grandee — is a test he cannot pass. He cannot refuse a superior's offer; he cannot accept it without demonstrating his own incapacity. The shaking hands are the body's confession: I am not equal to this situation, and I know it.

## Notable Lines

> "Good Lord! With secret instructions!" [002-002]

His first and defining line. The exclamation is prayer and panic simultaneously — "Good Lord" invokes divine protection while "secret instructions" names the threat against which no protection exists. The "secret" in "secret instructions" is the multiplier of terror: an inspector whose mandate is known can be managed; an inspector with unknown authority cannot.

> "There now! That's something I hadn't anticipated. To take or not to take?" [036-002]

Hesitating over Khlestakov's offered cigar. The soliloquy reveals the paralysis that is fear's consequence: Khlopov cannot make even the smallest decision without calculating its potential danger. Every gesture, every acceptance or refusal, becomes a potential trap.

> "Upon my word, I never put an onion to my mouth." [056-002]

His protest when the letter describes him as reeking of onions. The denial is pathetic not because it is false but because it is irrelevant — whether or not he eats onions, the image has stuck, and his reputation is now defined by a smell rather than a record.

## Historical Context

The position of School Inspector was created as part of Alexander I's educational reforms of 1802-1804, which established a hierarchical system of schools under the Ministry of Education. District schools, the lowest tier, were supervised by inspectors who reported to provincial directors.

The position was poorly paid and low in prestige — reflected in Khlopov's modest rank of Titular Councilor — and attracted functionaries of limited competence.

The teachers Khlopov describes — the face-maker, the history teacher who smashes chairs — reflect the chronic underfunding and low standards of provincial education under Nicholas I. Teachers were poorly trained, inadequately supervised, and operated in an environment where intellectual independence was viewed with suspicion.

The "freethinking" that the Governor attributes to one teacher is precisely the quality the system was designed to suppress: education under Nicholas I was tasked with producing obedient subjects, not critical minds.
