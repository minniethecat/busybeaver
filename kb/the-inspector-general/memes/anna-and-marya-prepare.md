# Anna and Marya Prepare

## Summary

Act III opens with Anna Andreyevna and her daughter Marya Antonovna standing at the window, having waited "a whole hour" (020-002) for news of the inspector. Their scene of anticipation, Dobchinsky's breathless arrival with a note, and their ensuing preparations constitute a comic portrait of provincial vanity and social climbing.

## The Wait

Anna is immediately irritable, blaming her daughter: "All on account of your silly prinking. You were completely dressed, but no, you have to keep on dawdling" (020-002). When Marya spots someone approaching, Anna contradicts her — "It's not Dobchinsky at all" — only to concede moments later: "Yes, it is, I see now. Why do you argue about it?" (020-002). This brief exchange establishes Anna's character: vain, contradictory, and incapable of acknowledging fault.

## Dobchinsky's Report

Dobchinsky arrives breathless, bearing a note from the Governor. Anna's first question — "But who is he? A general?" (021-002) — reveals her priorities. Dobchinsky describes the guest: "Young — a young man of about twenty-three. But he talks as if he were older. He does it all with such distinction" (033-001). Asked about his appearance, Dobchinsky offers a peculiarly nervous description: "His eyes dart about like little animals. They make you nervous" (033-001).

The Governor's note, scribbled on a scrap of bill-paper, contains an absurd mix of domestic instruction and bureaucratic anxiety: "Get a room ready quickly for the distinguished guest — the one with the gold wall paper. Don't bother to get any extras for dinner because we'll have something at the hospital with Artemy Filippovich. Order a little more wine, and tell Abdulin to send the best, or I'll wreck his whole cellar" (034-001).

## The Dressing Scene

With the guest confirmed as a "metropolitan swell," mother and daughter quarrel over their attire. Anna insists on the blue dress for Marya, but Marya protests — "Liapkin-Tiapkin's wife wears blue and so does Zemlianika's daughter" (035-001) — preferring her flowered dress. Anna declares she will wear her dun-colored dress. When Marya says dun-color doesn't suit her, Anna retorts: "Aren't my eyes dark? They are as dark as can be. What nonsense you talk! How can they be anything but dark when I always draw the queen of clubs" (035-001). Marya suggests she is more the queen of hearts, and Anna, flustered, hurries out protesting: "I never was a queen of hearts!" (035-001).

## Comic Significance

The scene establishes the female characters' absorption in trivialities while the men face what they believe is an existential threat. The Governor's note — mixing bribery, fine wine, and domestic arrangements — captures the play's tonal blend of terror and farce.
