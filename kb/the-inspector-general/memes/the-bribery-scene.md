## Overview

The Bribery Scene encompasses Act IV, Scenes I through VIII, in which the town officials — believing [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] to be a powerful government inspector — visit him individually to offer bribes. Each official attempts to disguise the bribe as a loan or a gift, and Khlestakov, gradually realizing the situation, exploits their fear with increasing boldness [032-001–039-009].

## Dramatic Context

The scene follows the Governor's decision that the only way to handle the inspector is to offer him money. The officials gather nervously in the Governor's house, plotting their approach. Judge Liapkin-Tiapkin takes the lead in organizing the "military line" formation [032-001]. Artemy Zemlianika explicitly suggests "greasing" the inspector's palm [032-022].

## The Bribery Sequence

### The Judge (Scene III)

[[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge Liapkin-Tiapkin]] enters trembling, his "knees knocking together." He intends to offer three hundred rubles but is so nervous he drops the money on the floor. Khlestakov casually picks it up and asks to "borrow" it, to the Judge's immense relief [034-002–034-025].

### The Postmaster (Scene IV)

[[ivan-shpekin|Postmaster Shpekin]] is more composed but equally eager to please. When Khlestakov asks for a loan of three hundred rubles, Shpekin immediately agrees, noting to himself that the inspector "isn't a bit proud — he inquires about everything" [035-002–035-018].

### The School Inspector (Scene V)

[[luka-khlopov|Luka Khlopov]], the most timid of the officials, is literally pushed into the room. He is so frightened that when Khlestakov offers him a cigar, he attempts to light the wrong end. Khlestakov asks for three hundred rubles, and the trembling Khlopov hands it over [036-002–036-016].

### The Superintendent of Charities (Scene VI)

[[artemy-zemlianika|Artemy Zemlianika]] is the most calculating of the visitors. After offering his bribe, he proceeds to denounce every other official: the Postmaster does nothing, the Judge hunts hares in the courtroom, and Dobchinsky's children all resemble the Judge [037-013–037-019]. This scene exemplifies the theme of mutual betrayal among the corrupt.

### Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky (Scene VII)

The two country squires enter together. Unlike the officials, they have little money to offer. Khlestakov abruptly demands a thousand rubles, but they can only scrape together sixty-five between them [038-018–038-025].

### Khlestakov's Realization (Scene VIII)

Alone after the visits, Khlestakov counts his haul: over a thousand rubles in total. He laughs at the officials' gullibility and decides to write to his friend Tryapichkin in St. Petersburg, setting in motion the letter that will ultimately expose him [039-001–039-006].

## Thematic Significance

The Bribery Scene is Gogol's most concentrated satire of bureaucratic corruption. Each official's approach reveals his character: the Judge's cowardice, the Postmaster's obsequiousness, the School Inspector's terror, Zemlianika's treachery, and the squires' absurdity. The scene's comic structure — five nearly identical encounters in rapid succession — creates a cumulative farcical effect that underscores the **systemic** nature of the corruption [032-001–039-009].
