# Bribery

## Definition

Bribery (*vzyatochnichestvo*) in Imperial Russia was the unofficial but universally acknowledged practice by which government officials supplemented their meager state salaries through payments from private citizens seeking favors, exemptions, or simply the performance of legally required duties. Unlike modern conceptions of bribery as an exceptional crime, bribery in Gogol's Russia was a embedded feature of the administrative system — so routine that officials openly ranked themselves by the scale of their graft, not by whether they took it.

## In the Play

Bribery is the hidden engine that drives every relationship in *The Inspector General*. The Governor "takes bribes" but "carries himself with dignity" (P03-001), treating corruption not as a moral failing but as a professional perquisite. When he hears of the inspector general's arrival, his first instinct is not to reform but to manage appearances: the hospital patients should be given clean caps, the streets should be swept, and the geese kept out of the courthouse. The comedy escalates when Khlestakov — who initially asks for a loan to pay his hotel bill — discovers that the officials are lining up to give him money under the transparent pretense of "loans." Each official enters trembling, hands over bills, and exits with palpable relief, having purchased what he believes is the inspector's goodwill.

The merchants, who have suffered most from the Governor's extortion, provide the play's most direct accusation: "such a governor there never was before in the world. No matter what he sees, he's got to have it" (041-002). Yet the Governor turns the accusation back on them: the merchants sell rotten cloth to the government and "donate twenty rubles expecting a medal" (050-002) — suggesting bribery is not a top-down imposition but a system in which all parties are complicit.

## Historical Background

The Table of Ranks system unintentionally institutionalized bribery. Civil servants in the lower and middle ranks received salaries that were often insufficient to maintain the lifestyle expected of their station — particularly the requirement to dress fashionably and entertain appropriately. The gap between official salary and expected expenditure was filled by *vzyatki* (bribes), *blagodarnosti* (gratuities), and various forms of "feeding" (*kormlenie*) — a practice with roots in medieval Muscovy, where officials were not paid by the state at all but expected to live off the populations they administered.

By the 1830s, when Gogol wrote *The Inspector General*, bribery had become the subject of official concern and literary satire simultaneously. Nicholas I himself was aware of the problem but lacked the administrative capacity to reform it. Gogol's play, by making bribery not merely a crime but a *system* — a closed loop of fear, extortion, and mutual complicity — offered the most devastating critique yet of Russia's administrative culture.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's treatment of bribery is notable for its refusal to moralize. No character in the play condemns bribery in principle; they only condemn being caught, or being out-bribed. The Judge's distinction between a "bribe" and a "loan" (034-002) is the play's most brilliant encapsulation of bureaucratic self-deception. Gogol's satire suggests that in a system where rank and appearance are everything, the line between official compensation and unofficial extraction becomes impossible to draw — and the real corruption is not monetary but moral: the destruction of any shared understanding of public service.
