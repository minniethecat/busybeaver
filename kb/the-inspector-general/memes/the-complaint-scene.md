## Overview

The Complaint Scene (Act IV, Scenes X–XI) depicts the townspeople of the provincial capital — merchants, a locksmith's wife, and a noncommissioned officer's widow — petitioning [[ivan-khlestakov|Khlestakov]] with grievances against the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]. The scene provides the only direct testimony from ordinary citizens about the Governor's abuses, making it a crucial counterpoint to the officials' self-serving perspective [041-001–042-026].

## Dramatic Context

The scene follows the bribery sequence (Act IV, Scenes I–VIII), in which the town officials have each paid off Khlestakov individually. As the officials leave, ordinary townspeople — who have heard that a powerful government inspector is in town — seize the opportunity to present their grievances [041-001].

## Scene Summary

### The Merchants' Petition (Scene X)

A group of merchants arrives bearing gifts: a basket of wine and sugar loaves. They beg Khlestakov not to "ruin" them and present a litany of complaints against the Governor [041-010–041-018]:

| Complaint | Detail | PN |
|-----------|--------|----|
| Quartering of soldiers | The Governor forces soldiers to be quartered in merchants' homes, ruining their businesses | [041-016] |
| Physical abuse | The Governor "catches hold of your beard and says, 'Oh, you Tartar!'" | [041-016] |
| Theft from shops | The Governor enters shops, takes whatever he wants — including forty yards of cloth — without payment | [041-018] |
| Extortion for family | The merchants must provide goods for the Governor's wife and daughter's clothing | [041-016] |
| Name-day extortion | The Governor claims to have two name-days a year (his own and Anton's) to extract gifts on the pretext of celebrations | [041-016] |

The merchants offer Khlestakov money and gifts if he will intervene. Khlestakov initially declines the money but accepts the wine and sugar, and promises to "exert his efforts" [041-022–041-028].

### The Citizens' Complaints (Scene XI)

Two women arrive: Fevronya Poshliopkina, a locksmith's wife, and Ivanova, the widow of a noncommissioned officer. Each has a specific grievance [042-001–042-012]:

**The Locksmith's Wife** complains that the Governor had her husband conscripted into the army illegally. Her husband was due for exemption because he was married, but the Governor took a bribe from the son of a merchant's wife to send the locksmith instead, and then had the merchant's son released as "unfit" [042-018–042-022].

**The Widow Ivanova** complains that the Governor had her flogged. The Governor claims she "flogged herself," but she insists this is a lie — she was publicly beaten by the police on the Governor's orders [042-006–042-010].

### The Governor's Intervention (Scenes XV–XVI)

When the Governor learns of the complaints, he rushes in to defend himself, claiming the merchants are "rank liars" and "swindlers" [046-009–046-012]. Khlestakov, however, is indifferent — "The devil take the officer's widow" — and is already planning his departure with Osip's help [046-013, 047-003].

## Thematic Significance

The Complaint Scene serves several dramatic functions. First, it provides **objective evidence** of the Governor's corruption, confirming what the audience has already inferred. Second, it demonstrates the **powerlessness of ordinary people** in the tsarist bureaucracy — their only recourse is to petition a man who is himself a fraud. Third, the scene's placement immediately before Khlestakov's romantic entanglement with Marya and Anna (Scenes XII–XIV) heightens the **absurdity** of the situation: the townspeople's genuine suffering is dismissed while the farce of mistaken identity continues [042-001–044-010].

## Key Quotes
