# The Kitchen

The kitchen appears in two distinct locations in the play — the courthouse and the inn — and in both cases it is mentioned only in passing. Yet these brief references serve Gogol's method of revealing character through mundane domestic detail.

## Description

The kitchen is never shown on stage; it exists as an offstage space that characters refer to in dialogue. In the courthouse, it is where the Judge, Ammos Fiodorovich Liapkin-Tiapkin, directs hunting spoils to be taken. At the inn, it is where Khlestakov glimpses meal preparations that underscore his impoverished status relative to the "better kind of folk."

## Scenes Set Here

No scenes are set in the kitchen — it is purely an offstage reference point:

- **Act I, Scene I** — During the initial conference of officials, the Judge casually remarks: "Well, I'll have them all taken into the kitchen today. Will you come and dine with me?" (002-002). The "them" refers to hunting spoils — gifts from litigants — and the line reveals the Judge's conflation of his judicial chambers with his personal larder, a small window into the casual corruption of the town's administration.

- **Act II, Scene VII** — Hungry and frustrated at the inn, Khlestakov tells Osip that he saw food being prepared "when I passed through the kitchen. And in the dining-room this morning two short little men were eating salmon and lots of other things" (014-002). The inn servant's reply — "Only for the better kind of folk" — underscores Khlestakov's diminished standing and the social hierarchy that operates even in a provincial coaching inn.

## Significance

Gogol uses the kitchen as an index of character. For the Judge, it is a space of privilege — a place to stash the informal economy of hunting gifts that lubricate the town's justice system. For Khlestakov, the kitchen is a space of exclusion — he can see the food but cannot access it, a miniature of his larger predicament as a low-ranking clerk who longs to belong to the "better kind of folk."
