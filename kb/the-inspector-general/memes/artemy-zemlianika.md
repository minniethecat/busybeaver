# Artemy Zemlianika

## Identity

Artemy Filippovich Zemlianika is the Superintendent of Charities (*popechitel bogougodnykh zavedeny*), responsible for the town's hospital, almshouse, and welfare institutions. His name means "wild strawberry" — a Gogolian comic incongruity for a man who is anything but sweet. The Directions for Actors describe him as "a fat man, but a sly scoundrel in spite of his bulk.

Very obliging and bustling" [P03-006].

His corpulence is not incidental but thematic: in a town where patients are systematically underfed, the hospital administrator is conspicuously well-nourished. His physical bulk mirrors his moral weightlessness — he is substantial in presence but utterly insubstantial in character.

## Role in the Play

Zemlianika is the play's supreme opportunist and its most cynical operator. Where the other officials are reactive in their corruption, Zemlianika is strategic. When the inspector's arrival is announced, he is the first to propose the "grease" strategy — a euphemism for bribery that the Judge immediately recognizes: "Grease? ... It's risky" [032-002].

His management of the hospital is a masterpiece of bureaucratic theater. The Governor enumerates the catalogue of neglect: patients wear dirty nightcaps "as if they worked in a brewery," no one receives proper medicine, and the hospital lacks even the most basic sanitation.

Yet when Khlestakov visits, Zemlianika stages a performance of institutional competence: patients are hidden or discharged, the remaining ones are cleaned up, and a lavish lunch is prepared as the centerpiece of the hospitality campaign. The institution exists not to heal but to be seen to heal — and seeing is believing if no one looks too closely.

But Zemlianika's defining trait is his willingness to denounce his colleagues. In his private audience with Khlestakov [037-002], he systematically destroys the reputations of everyone in the room:

> "The Judge is a regular Mazepa. He never goes to church ... The Postmaster does absolutely nothing ... The School Inspector is worse than a Jacobin ... I don't know how the government could have entrusted the Governor with such an office."

His strategy is pure self-preservation through the destruction of others. Every denunciation creates a contrast: *I* am the one telling you this, therefore *I* am the trustworthy one. It perfectly captures the ethos of Gogol's bureaucratic world, where loyalty is a liability and betrayal is the only reliable currency.

## Key Scenes

- **(006-002)**: Reports to the Governor that the church at the hospital was never built — the money was diverted ("appropriated for other purposes"). This is the first concrete evidence of institutional corruption in the play, and Zemlianika reports it not as a scandal but as an administrative footnote.

- **(032-002)**: Proposes the bribery strategy to the assembled officials. His "Do as you please, Ammos Fiodorovich, I think we ought to try" is couched as a suggestion but delivered as a command. He correctly reads the room's anxiety and channels it toward collective corruption.

- **(037-002)**: Private audience with Khlestakov — the denunciation scene. Zemlianika's monologue is the play's most concentrated burst of bureaucratic backstabbing. His transition from formal courtesy ("I have the honor to present myself") to ruthless character assassination is seamless.

- **(056-002)**: His own humiliation arrives when the letter is read: "The Superintendent of Charities, Zemlianika, is a regular pig in a cap." His response — "Have you ever seen a pig wear a cap?" [056-002] — is a desperate attempt at deflection that only underscores the accuracy of the description.

- **(058-001)**: Present in the final Silent Scene, described as standing "his head to one side as if listening" [058-001] — the denouncer reduced to silent, frozen attention, his only remaining skill rendered useless.

## Relationships

- **Anton Antonovich (Governor)**: His superior, whom he privately denounces as incompetent ("I don't know how the government could have entrusted him with such an office" [037-002]) while publicly performing absolute loyalty. The denunciation of the Governor is the climax of his betrayal sequence — having destroyed every colleague, he ascends to destroying his chief.

- **Ammos Liapkin-Tiapkin (Judge)**: His primary rival, whom he accuses of being "a regular Mazepa" — a reference to the Ukrainian hetman who betrayed Peter the Great, implying both treason and exotic otherness. The two men represent competing styles of corruption: the Judge's passive, self-deceiving variety versus Zemlianika's active, strategic variety.

- **Christian Hübner (District Doctor)**: The German doctor who serves under Zemlianika and speaks no Russian. This detail — the hospital's chief medical officer cannot communicate with his patients — is both a practical explanation for the hospital's failures and a metaphor for an administration that is structurally incapable of understanding those it is meant to serve.

- **Ivan Khlestakov**: The supposed inspector. Zemlianika's denunciation is calibrated precisely to Khlestakov's vanity: by presenting himself as the one honest official surrounded by corrupt colleagues, Zemlianika mirrors Khlestakov's own self-image as the sole competent person in a world of fools.

## Character Analysis

Zemlianika is the play's most chilling character because he is the most deliberate. The Governor blusters, the Judge rationalizes, the Postmaster is driven by curiosity — but Zemlianika calculates. His denunciations in Act IV [037-002] are not impulsive confessions but strategic investments. Each accusation purchases a moment of apparent honesty that masks the accuser's own guilt.

His physical description — "a fat man, but a sly scoundrel in spite of his bulk" [P03-006] — reverses the expected correlation between physical and moral weight. His bulk, which in another character might suggest joviality or slowness, masks an operational intelligence that moves faster than anyone else's.

He is "very obliging and bustling" [P03-006] — the bureaucratic virtue of appearing busy while accomplishing nothing.

The hospital itself is the play's most devastating institutional satire. Patients "recover like flies" — that is, they die. The church was never built. The doctor speaks no Russian. Medicine is not administered. And yet this institution is the one Zemlianika proudly conducts Khlestakov through, the one he cites as evidence of his service to the state.

The discrepancy between the institution's nominal purpose (charity, healing) and its actual function (depletion, death) is the play's sharpest indictment of Russian bureaucracy: institutions exist to be administered, not to serve.

## Notable Lines

> "I don't know how the government could have entrusted him with such an office. Why, the stores are positively empty, nothing but dirt, and the patients go about looking as if they worked in a brewery." [037-002]

The supreme irony of this denunciation is that Zemlianika describes his own hospital while pretending to describe the courthouse. The patients who "look as if they worked in a brewery" are under his care, not the Judge's. This slippage — attacking others for one's own failures — is the psychological signature of the denouncer.

> "For God's sake, gentlemen, form your line, and let's have more order." [032-002]

The bureaucratic mind organizing corruption as if it were a military parade. Zemlianika's insistence on "order" and "military line" in the bribery queue is the perfect image of a system in which form has entirely displaced function.

> "I am glad to do all I can in behalf of my country." [037-002]

The phrase that precedes his cascade of denunciations. Patriotism, in Zemlianika's usage, is the rhetorical garment in which betrayal is dressed.

## Historical Context

The position of Superintendent of Charities (*popechitel bogougodnykh zavedeny*) was created as part of Catherine the Great's provincial reforms. Charitable institutions — hospitals, almshouses, orphanages — were administered by local boards under the nominal supervision of the Ministry of the Interior.

In practice, these institutions were chronically underfunded and understaffed, creating the conditions for the kind of neglect Zemlianika exemplifies.

The "appropriation" of funds for the hospital church — diverted to other purposes [006-002] — was a standard bureaucratic practice: institutional budgets were treated as fungible resources to be redirected toward personal or political priorities.

The fact that no one is surprised by this diversion, and that the Governor's only concern is that the inspector might notice, reveals how thoroughly normalized such practices were.
