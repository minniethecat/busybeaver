## Overview

The [[district-court|court]]room is the workspace of [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Ammos Fiodorovich Liapkin-Tiapkin]], mentioned in Act I of Gogol's
*The Inspector General* during the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s review of municipal institutions before the supposed
inspector's arrival. Though not [[appearance|show]]n on stage directly, it is described as being in a state of
neglect that reflects the broader dysfunction of the [[provincial-civil-service|provincial administration]].

## State of the Courtroom

In Act I, the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] confronts [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] about the disorderly condition of the [[district-court|court]]room.
The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] complains that "it isn't right to have the [[district-court|court]]room littered up with all sorts of
rubbish" [002-002], noting that the anteroom has been taken over by attendants who keep geese there.

The specific complaints include:
- Geese wandering in the entrance hall where petitioners wait [002-002]
- Hunting equipment — "a hunting crop hanging right over the cupboard of [[justice|law]] books" [002-002]
- General untidiness suggesting [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] treats the [[district-court|court]]room more as a personal domain than
  a place of public administration

## The Judge's Defense

[[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|Judge]] Liapkin-Tiapkin defends himself by claiming he has "taken nothing but borzoi puppies" as
[[bribery|bribes]] [002-002], distinguishing his form of [[bribery|corruption]] from monetary [[bribery|graft]]. His relaxed attitude
toward the [[district-court|court]]room's condition mirrors his casual approach to justice — he has presided for
fifteen years without apparently taking his duties seriously.

The [[anton-antonovich|Governor]]'s rebuke that [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]] "never go[es] to church" while the [[anton-antonovich|Governor]] is "at least a
firm believer" [002-002] adds a layer of moral critique to the physical neglect. The [[district-court|court]]room's
state is presented as symptomatic of [[ammos-liapkin-tiapkin|the Judge]]'s broader indifference to civic duty.

## Significance

The [[district-court|court]]room represents institutional decay in Gogol's provincial Russia. Rather than a place
of justice, it has become a personal fiefdom where geese roam and hunting gear hangs among [[justice|law]]
books. The contrast between the [[district-court|court]]room's ostensible purpose — the administration of [[justice|law]] — and
its actual condition is one of the play's earliest satirical images.
