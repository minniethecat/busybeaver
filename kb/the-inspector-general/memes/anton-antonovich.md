# Anton Antonovich

## Identity

Anton Antonovich Skvoznik Dmukhanovsky is the Governor (*gorodnichy*) of the unnamed provincial town where *The Inspector General* takes place. A man "grown old in the service" who has "worked his way up from the lowest rank in the arduous government service" (P03-001), he embodies the career *chinovnik*: shrewd, unprincipled, and acutely sensitive to the demands of rank. His surname Skvoznik-Dmukhanovsky derives from *skvoznyak* (draft, through-wind) and *dmukhat* (to blow), suggesting someone who is blown about by external forces — an apt name for a man whose entire life is reactive to the demands of superiors.

## Role in the Play

The Governor is both protagonist and primary target of the play's satire. He opens the action by summoning the town's officials to announce the terrifying news: "An Inspector General is coming" (002-002) — "incognito, and with secret instructions." His panic is both self-serving (he fears exposure of his corruption) and professionally rational (an unfavorable inspection could end his career).

Having "fooled three governor-generals" in his career (056-002), the Governor initially approaches the supposed inspector with his habitual mixture of bribery and bluster. His tragedy — or comedy — is that his strategy works perfectly on a real inspector, and he has no way of knowing that the man before him is not one. By the play's end, his humiliation is total: exposed by Khlestakov's intercepted letter, frozen in the famous "silent scene" as a *real* inspector is announced.

## Key Scenes

- **(002-002)**: Convenes officials to announce the inspector's arrival — the play's catalytic moment.
- **(016-003)**: First meeting with Khlestakov at the inn, oscillating between terror and attempted bribery.
- **(024-002)**: Conducts Khlestakov on a tour of the town's institutions, lying fluently about their excellence.
- **(029-002)**: Envisions his glorious future as father-in-law to a high-ranking official — his delusion at its peak.
- **(046-002)**: Confronts the merchants who complained about him, threatening vengeance.
- **(056-002)**: The letter is read — his world collapses. "What are you laughing at? You are laughing at yourselves!"

## Relationships

- **Anna Andreyevna** (wife): A provincial coquette whom he tolerates and occasionally consults.
- **Marya Antonovna** (daughter): His route to social advancement through marriage to a high-ranking official.
- **The Officials** (Ammos, Artemy, Luka, Shpekin): Subordinates whom he manages through a mixture of threat and collusion.
- **The Merchants**: His victims and accusers — the town's economic lifeblood whom he systematically extorts.

## Character Analysis

The Governor is not a simple villain. Gogol's stage directions describe him as "by no means a fool in his own way" — a man of "rather serious turn" who "passes rapidly from fear to joy, from servility to arrogance" (P03-001). This emotional volatility is his defining trait: he is a man whose entire personality is determined by his perception of the other person's rank. Before a superior, he is obsequious; before a subordinate, he is tyrannical. He has no self apart from his place in the hierarchy.

Gogol's satirical genius is to make the Governor sympathetic even as he exposes his corruption. The Governor's famous line to the audience — "What are you laughing at? You are laughing at yourselves!" — breaks the fourth wall to implicate the audience in the moral universe the play has created.
