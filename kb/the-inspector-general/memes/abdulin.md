# Abdulin

## Identity

Abdulin is a merchant in the provincial town, listed in the Dramatis Personae as "Abdulin, a merchant" (P02-025). He occupies a liminal position in the town's economy: necessary to the Governor for the goods he supplies, yet vulnerable to official predation.

## Role in the Play

Abdulin appears across three acts as a representative of the town's commercial class. His role illustrates the extractive relationship between the provincial administration and local business — the Governor both depends on and exploits the merchants who supply his household and the municipal institutions.

## Key Scenes

- **(005-002)**: The Governor, inspecting his sword before going to meet the supposed inspector, complains: "It's that dog of a merchant, Abdulin. He sees the Governor's sword is old and doesn't provide a new one. Oh, the sharpers! I'll bet they've got their petitions against me ready in their coattail pockets" (005-002). This reveals both the Governor's expectation of tribute from merchants and his awareness that they resent his exactions.
- **(021-002)**: In a letter to his wife, the Governor instructs Anna Andreyevna: "Order a little more wine, and tell Abdulin to send the best, or I'll wreck his whole cellar" (021-002) — demonstrating the coercive nature of the Governor's commercial relationships.
- **(040-002)**: In Act IV, a petition "from the merchant Abdulin" is among those thrust through Khlestakov's window, addressed to "his most honorable, illustrious financial Excellency" — an absurdly inflated title that Khlestakov himself finds puzzling (040-002).

## Relationships

- **Anton Antonovich** (Governor): The Governor is Abdulin's chief exploiter, demanding goods under threat and resenting any failure of tribute.
- **Anna Andreyevna**: The Governor's wife acts as intermediary, relaying demands for wine to Abdulin's establishment.
- **The Petitioners**: Abdulin is part of the community of merchants and townspeople who seek redress from the supposed inspector against the Governor.

## Character Analysis

Abdulin illustrates the economic dimension of Gogol's satire. The merchant class is caught between the Governor's demands for tribute and the hope of appeal to a higher authority. The Governor's complaint about his old sword (005-002) reveals the circular logic of official corruption: the merchants are expected to provide goods gratis, and their failure to do so is treated as a personal affront rather than a consequence of their exploitation. Abdulin's petition to Khlestakov (040-002) represents the merchants' only recourse — an appeal to a phantom authority that can offer no real remedy.
