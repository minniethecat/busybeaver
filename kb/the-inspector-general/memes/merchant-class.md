# Merchant Class

## Definition

The merchant estate (*kupechestvo*) was one of the formal social estates (*sosloviya*) of Imperial Russia, ranking below the nobility and clergy but above the urban lower classes (*meshchane*) and peasantry.

Organized into three guilds based on declared capital, merchants enjoyed certain privileges — exemption from the poll tax, freedom from military conscription, the right to trade — in exchange for conducting commerce and paying guild fees.

In provincial towns like the one in *The Inspector General*, merchants formed the economic backbone but were socially subordinate to the noble-born officials who governed and, as Gogol shows, systematically exploited them.

Gogol's treatment of the merchant class is unusually nuanced for a satirist. The merchants are victims of the Governor's extortion, but they are not innocent. They cheat the government on supply contracts, sell rotten goods, and — when given the opportunity — attempt to buy off officials with the same bribery mechanisms they denounce.

Gogol refuses the easy moral binary of oppressor and victim, presenting instead a closed system of mutual exploitation in which every party is implicated.

## In the Play

The merchants appear in two significant scenes, and the contrast between them reveals the complexity of Gogol's satirical vision.

In Act IV, scene X, the merchants arrive at Khlestakov's lodgings as collective supplicants, bearing "a basket of wine and sugar loaves" (041-001) — gifts that are transparently intended to purchase the supposed inspector's favor. Their grievances against the Governor are specific and material: "Such a governor there never was before in the world.

No matter what he sees, he's got to have it" (041-002).

They itemize his offenses: he seizes goods for his household, demands "presents" on his name day, stations soldiers in their shops to extort provisions, and treats them with open contempt — calling them "tea tanks" and "scurvy hucksters." Their petition is at once a genuine cry for relief and a performance of victimhood calibrated to win the inspector's sympathy.

Khlestakov, characteristically, accepts their gifts without committing to anything — he pockets the money and the sugar loaves while offering vague reassurances. His servant Osip, more pragmatic, advises him: "If you let a single one in, he'll fill your ears with all kinds of nonsense" (040-002).

Osip, as usual, sees the transaction more clearly than his master: the merchants are not innocent petitioners but players in the same game of influence and extraction.

In Act V, the Governor confronts the merchants who have complained about him. The scene is a masterpiece of inversion and accusation.

The Governor does not deny extorting the merchants; he turns their complaint inside out by accusing them of cheating the government: "You do the government out of a hundred thousand with the rotten cloth you sell them, and then you donate twenty rubles and you expect a medal for it" (050-002).

The merchants, caught between their own dishonest practices and the Governor's retaliation, can only bow and promise to "be more careful in the future." The scene establishes a kind of corrupt equilibrium: the Governor extorts the merchants, the merchants cheat the government, and the government — in the person of the inspector — is supposed to police a system in which it is itself complicit.

The merchant Abdulin appears more obliquely but significantly. The Governor mentions that "that dog of a merchant, Abdulin — he sees the Governor's sword is old and doesn't provide a new one" (005-002).

This aside reveals the social dynamics in miniature: the merchant is expected to supply the Governor with equipment; the Governor treats non-compliance as a personal insult; and the entire transaction occurs outside any official channel, as an unspoken obligation.

## Historical Background

The merchant guild system, formalized by Catherine the Great's *Charter to the Towns* (1785), divided merchants into three guilds based on declared capital: first guild (over 10,000 rubles), second guild (5,000–10,000), and third guild (1,000–5,000).

Guild membership conferred status and legal privileges — most crucially, exemption from the soul tax and from military conscription — but also imposed obligations, including the payment of a percentage of declared capital as guild fees. This created a perverse incentive to under-declare wealth, which in turn made merchants vulnerable to officials who could threaten to investigate their books.

Merchants occupied an ambiguous position in Russian society. Economically indispensable — they provisioned the army, supplied the cities, and generated the tax revenue that funded the state — they were nonetheless looked down upon by the nobility, who valued service rank over commercial wealth.

A merchant might be wealthier than the governor who ruled his town, but he was still required to bow to that governor and to suffer the casual contempt of noble-born officials. This status contradiction — economic power without social standing — is the structural tension that Gogol dramatizes in the merchants' scenes.

The practice of quartering soldiers in private homes, which the merchants cite as a particular grievance, was a real burden on urban populations in Imperial Russia. Military units on the move were billeted in private residences at the householder's expense — a system that local officials could manipulate to reward allies and punish enemies.

The merchants' complaint that the Governor stations soldiers in their shops specifically on market days (041-002) reflects a well-documented form of official harassment.

## Satirical Function

The merchants in *The Inspector General* serve a dual satirical function that is more sophisticated than it first appears. On one level, they expose the predatory nature of provincial officialdom: the Governor is a parasite on commerce, taking what he wants under color of authority and using the mechanisms of the state — police, courts, quartering regulations — to enforce his personal extortion.

The merchants' grievances are the most vivid indictment of the Governor's corruption in the play.

On another level, however, the merchants expose the universality of corruption. The Governor's counter-accusation — that the merchants sell the government "rotten cloth" and expect medals for their "charity" — is never refuted. The merchants cheat the government on contracts; the government cheats the merchants through extortion.

There is no victim without complicity, no oppressor who is not in turn oppressed. Gogol's refusal to sentimentalize the merchants — to make them purely sympathetic — is what gives the satire its uncomfortable, enduring power.

This moral symmetry is characteristic of Gogol's larger vision. In a society organized around rank and appearance rather than substance and law, every relationship becomes transactional. The merchants are not reformers seeking justice; they are competitors in the same corrupt market, hoping to exchange one protector (the Governor) for another (the supposed inspector).

The sugar loaves they bring Khlestakov are bribes, not gifts — and their petition is as performative as the Governor's hospitality.
