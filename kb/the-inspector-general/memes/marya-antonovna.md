# Marya Antonovna

## Identity

Marya Antonovna is the daughter of Governor Anton Antonovich and Anna Andreyevna. She lives in the Governor's House and becomes the unwitting center of Khlestakov's romantic imposture. Gogol describes her simply as "his daughter" in the Dramatis Personae [P02-003], leaving her character to emerge through her competition with her mother rather than through explicit characterization.

Her diminutive, "Mashenka," used by her parents, reinforces her status as a dependent — a marriageable object whose value is defined by her social connections rather than her individual qualities. She is defined almost entirely through her relationship to others: daughter of the Governor, rival of her mother, fiancee of the supposed inspector.

## Role in the Play

Marya Antonovna is the object of Khlestakov's most audacious lie: a spontaneous marriage proposal that converts her from provincial obscurity into — what she and her parents believe — a Petersburg match of staggering social consequence.

She participates in the farcical competition with her mother for Khlestakov's attention, each woman convinced the Petersburg visitor favors her. In Act III, she and Anna Andreyevna rush onto the stage upon hearing of Khlestakov's arrival [007-001].

Throughout the remaining acts, she appears in scenes where the two women play audience to Khlestakov's boasts about his Petersburg life — the soup arriving by steamer, the thirty-five thousand couriers, the seven-hundred-ruble watermelon [022-001, 025-001, 027-001].

The mother-daughter competition reaches its comic apex in Act III, when the two women stand at the window [020-001], each claiming to be the first to identify the approaching figure.

This domestic rivalry — trivial in its object, intense in its execution — mirrors the larger competition among the town's officials for Khlestakov's favor, and suggests that the Governor's own household is a miniature of the competitive, backbiting bureaucracy he presides over.

The climactic scene occurs when Khlestakov, alone with Marya Antonovna [043-001], drops to his knees and proposes. When she protests that he already made advances to her mother, he dismisses it: "It was just a little fun. Marya Antonovna, don't be angry" [043-002].

She accepts, and the betrothal is announced in Act V before the assembled town notables — only to collapse when the imposture is revealed in the letter-reading scene that follows [056-002].

## Key Scenes

- **(007-001)**: Marya and her mother rush in upon hearing of Khlestakov's arrival, establishing the competitive dynamic that will define their shared scenes.

- **(020-001)**: Standing at the window with Anna Andreyevna, competing to identify the approaching figure. The rivalry over who saw him first prefigures the romantic rivalry to come — both women jockeying for the attention of a man they have never spoken to, based entirely on his perceived social status.

- **(022-001, 025-001, 027-001)**: Present during Khlestakov's boasting scenes in the Governor's house. She and her mother function as an audience: gasping at the right moments, expressing the appropriate awe, and thereby enabling Khlestakov's escalating fabrications.

- **(043-001 to 043-002)**: Khlestakov proposes marriage.

The scene is a masterpiece of manipulative courtship: Khlestakov declares his love, drops to his knees, and when Marya protests about his earlier attentions to her mother, dismisses them as "just a little fun" [043-002] — three words that simultaneously nullify his flirtation with the mother and characterize his entire approach to relationships.

- **(045-001)**: Present as the betrothal is announced. Her parents' ecstatic reaction — "From just a simple governor's wife suddenly — whew! — to marry into the family of such a big gun" [049-002] — reveals that her value to them is entirely instrumental: she is elevated not as a person but as a vehicle for family ambition.

- **(058-001)**: In the final Silent Scene, Marya stands on the Governor's right, "straining toward him" — frozen in the same tableau that paralyzes the entire company, her romantic future collapsed in an instant.

## Relationships

- **Anna Andreyevna**: Her mother and rival. Their competition for Khlestakov's attention is a running comic motif throughout Acts III-V. The mother's assertion that the Petersburg visitor "couldn't take his eyes off me" and Marya's counter-claim that "he kept looking at me" transform a domestic interior into a romantic battlefield.

Their rivalry exposes the shallowness of provincial social aspiration: Khlestakov's value is not personal but positional, and both women compete for the position, not the person.

- **Ivan Khlestakov**: The supposed inspector who proposes marriage. For Marya, Khlestakov represents escape from provincial obscurity; for Khlestakov, Marya is a momentary amusement. The asymmetry of investment between them — she stakes her future on his sincerity; he stakes nothing — is the tragic dimension of the comic subplot.

- **Anton Antonovich**: Her father, the Governor, who eagerly supports the betrothal as a strategic coup for the family's social standing. His reaction — "I'll be a high official and will have the power to exile to Siberia" [056-002] — reveals that Marya's marriage is, for him, primarily a career opportunity.

## Character Analysis

Marya Antonovna represents the provincial young woman's vulnerability to metropolitan deception. Her acceptance of Khlestakov's impulsive proposal reflects not personal weakness but structural position: in provincial society, a woman's future depends on marriage, and marriage to a Petersburg official represents the highest conceivable social mobility.

Her willingness to believe Khlestakov's absurd fabrications is of a piece with her parents' willingness — everyone in the play believes what they need to believe.

She is also the play's most silent significant character. She has fewer lines than any other named character with a major plot function. Her silence is structural: in the Governor's household, women are seen and not heard, valued for their marriageability rather than their opinions.

When she does speak, it is often in protest — against her mother's claims, against Khlestakov's abruptness — and her protests are uniformly overridden.

The betrothal is the play's most extravagant comic invention: a marriage promise extracted under false pretenses by a man who is not what he seems, to a girl whose parents are too blinded by ambition to see the absurdity.

The scene in which the Governor, having just learned of the engagement, threatens the merchants — "Do you know that the very official to whom you complained is going to marry my daughter?" [050-002] — perfectly captures the instrumental logic that governs Marya's world. She is not a person making a choice; she is a prize being deployed.

## Notable Lines

> "I really wasn't going anywhere." [043-002]

Marya's response when Khlestakov asks where she was going. The line's defensive vagueness — she was looking for her mother, but will not admit it — suggests the self-consciousness of a young woman who knows she is being watched and judged. Her inability to articulate a purpose mirrors her position in the play: she is always in motion but never going anywhere of her own volition.

> "It was just a little fun." [043-002]

Khlestakov's dismissal of his attentions to her mother, which Marya accepts. The line's casual brutality — reducing an entire flirtation to "a little fun" — passes without protest because Marya has been trained not to question the statements of powerful men. Her silence in response to this dismissal is more revealing than any protest would be.

## Historical Context

The position of a provincial governor's daughter in Nicholas I's Russia was defined by social expectation rather than legal status. Women of the provincial gentry had limited educational opportunities and no independent career paths; marriage was the sole mechanism of social advancement.

The Governor's calculation — that his daughter's marriage to a high-ranking official would elevate the entire family — reflects the genuinely instrumental function of marriage in the provincial elite's social strategy.

The mother-daughter rivalry for Khlestakov's attention, while comic in Gogol's treatment, also reflects the economic reality of provincial society: eligible men of high rank were scarce resources, and competition for them was fierce.

Anna Andreyevna's attempt to attract Khlestakov's attention is not merely vanity but a displaced expression of the limited opportunities available to women — if she cannot advance through her daughter's marriage, perhaps she can advance through her own allure.
