# Nobility

## Definition

The nobility (*dvoryanstvo*) was the dominant social estate of Imperial Russia, enjoying exclusive rights to own serfs, hold officer commissions, and participate in provincial noble assemblies. Peter the Great's Table of Ranks complicated this picture by creating a parallel path to nobility through state service — the *chinovnik* who attained the eighth civil grade received hereditary nobility regardless of birth. This created a tension between the old hereditary nobility and the new service nobility that pervades Gogol's bureaucratic world.

## In the Play

The nobility in *The Inspector General* is both present and pointedly absent. The Governor, the Judge, and the other officials hold noble rank — not by birth, but by having ascended through the Table of Ranks. The Judge notes proudly that he "was elected by the nobility in 1816" (034-002), referring to the noble assembly's right to elect local judicial officers. Yet the hereditary aristocracy — the great landowning families with ancient lineage — is conspicuously absent from the provincial town. Gogol's focus is squarely on the *service* nobility: people whose status depends entirely on their rank and their ability to maintain appearances.

The comedy of status anxiety reaches its peak when the officials attempt to treat Khlestakov as a superior nobleman, addressing him as "Your Excellency" — a form reserved for the highest civil and military grades. The gap between Khlestakov's actual rank (the lowest) and the deference he receives is the measure of the town's provincial desperation.

## Historical Background

By Gogol's era, the Russian nobility was a diverse estate ranging from fabulously wealthy serf-owners with thousands of "souls" to impoverished gentry who owned fewer serfs than prosperous peasants. The provincial noble assembly, which met every three years to elect local officials, was both a pillar of local self-government and a theater of petty social competition. The right to elect the district judge and other officials gave the nobility a stake in local administration — and, as Gogol suggests, ensured that the elected officials would be beholden to the interests that elected them.

## Satirical Function

Gogol's treatment of nobility exposes the gap between the ideal of noble service and the reality of provincial self-interest. The officials who claim noble status do so not as an obligation (*noblesse oblige*) but as a privilege that exempts them from accountability. The Judge's pride in having been "elected by the nobility" is set against his manifest incompetence — he takes bribes in the form of hunting dogs and has never read a legal brief. The nobility, in Gogol's portrayal, has become a costume rather than a calling.
