# Charitable Institutions

## Definition

Charitable institutions (*bogougodnye zavedeniya*, literally "God-pleasing establishments") in Imperial Russia were state-funded facilities providing medical care, poor relief, and social welfare at the local level.

Established by Catherine the Great's provincial reforms of 1775, these institutions were administered at the district level by a Superintendent of Charities — an elected position that, in Gogol's portrayal, became a byword for corruption, neglect, and the inversion of institutional purpose.

The system was theoretically comprehensive, covering hospitals, almshouses, orphanages, and lunatic asylums, but in practice the gap between mandate and reality was vast — and Gogol makes that gap the site of his most bitter comedy.

## In the Play

Artemy Filippovich Zemlianika, the Superintendent of Charities, is one of the play's most memorably cynical characters. Gogol's dramatis personae describes him as "very fat, slow and awkward; but for all that a sly, cunning scoundrel. He is very obliging and officious" (P03-007).

This description is a miniature of the institutional paradox: the man responsible for caring for the sick is himself morally and physically bloated.

When the Governor warns of the inspector's arrival in Act I, Zemlianika's first response betrays his administrative philosophy: concern is not for the patients' welfare but for the appearance of welfare.

The Governor instructs him: "You had better see to it that everything is in order, that the nightcaps are clean, and the patients don't go about as they usually do, looking as grimy as blacksmiths" (006-002). The concern is entirely cosmetic — clean nightcaps, not medicine; fewer patients, not healthier ones. Zemlianika's response is chillingly casual: "Oh, that's a small matter.

We can get nightcaps easily enough."

The Governor's instructions continue with bureaucratic absurdity: "over each bed you might hang up a placard stating in Latin or some other language — that's your end of it, Christian Ivanovich — the name of the disease, when the patient fell ill, the day of the week and the month."

The demand for Latin labels — which neither the patients nor the German doctor who "doesn't know a word of Russian" can read — is the purest expression of the play's governing principle: appearances over substance, documentation over care.

Zemlianika then delivers the play's most devastating line on institutional medicine: "As to treatment, Christian Ivanovich and I have worked out our own system. Our rule is: the nearer to nature the better. We use no expensive medicines. A man is a simple affair. If he dies, he'd die anyway. If he gets well, he'd get well anyway" (006-002).

This is at once a confession of fatalism, a rationalization of neglect, and a parody of Enlightenment naturalism. The patients are left to nature not because nature heals but because treatment costs money — and the money, as we later learn, has been diverted to Zemlianika's table.

The hospital visit itself becomes a set piece of bureaucratic theater. Zemlianika proudly conducts Khlestakov through the wards, serving an elaborate lunch at the hospital while — as he later admits while denouncing the Judge — "the stores are positively empty, nothing but dirt, and the patients go about looking as if they worked in a brewery" (037-002).

The irony is double: Zemlianika's denunciation of the Judge is a confession of his own crimes, and his weaponization of truth (telling Khlestakov what is actually true about the hospital, but attributing it to someone else) is the play's most cynical act.

The church at the hospital — for which government funds were appropriated five years before the play's action — was never built. The Governor reminds Zemlianika to say "that the building was begun but was destroyed by fire. I sent in a report about it, you know" (006-002).

The burned-church alibi is a detail of pure Gogolian genius: even the story devised to conceal the theft is a story of destruction. Where there should be a building dedicated to God and charity, there is not even a ruin — only a lie about a ruin.

## Historical Background

Catherine the Great's *Statute on the Provinces* (1775) created a network of *Prikazy obshchestvennogo prizreniya* (Boards of Public Welfare) in each province, charged with establishing and overseeing hospitals, almshouses, workhouses, and insane asylums.

These were among the first state-funded social services in Russian history — a genuine Enlightenment reform intended to bring medical care and poor relief to the provinces.

However, funding was chronically inadequate. The initial capital provided to each board was small, and the interest generated was insufficient to maintain even minimal services. Boards were expected to supplement their resources through charitable donations and income-generating activities (lending money at interest, operating pharmacies), but these sources rarely met the need.

By Gogol's time, the charitable institutions had become notorious for mismanagement — the subject of periodic government investigations and perennial literary mockery.

The position of Superintendent of Charities was, like the Judge's post, filled by election from among the local nobility. It was an unpaid position that conferred status and influence — and, as Gogol shows, opportunities for enrichment.

Zemlianika represents a recognizable type: the petty official who treats his post as a sinecure, skims from the budget, and deflects scrutiny by denouncing his colleagues.

The German doctor Hübner, who "doesn't know a word of Russian" and can only make "a sound intermediate between M and A" (006-002), is another recognizable type: the foreign professional imported to lend prestige to an institution that functions without professional standards.

## Satirical Function

The charitable institutions embody Gogol's darkest satirical insight: that institutions designed to serve the most vulnerable become, in a corrupt system, the sites of the worst abuse. The hospital is nominally a place of healing; in practice, it is a death trap where patients "recover like flies" — the Russian idiom meaning they die in droves.

The church — symbol of charity, spiritual care, and the institution's very name ("God-pleasing") — is never built, its funds diverted into the same channels as the medicine budget. The "philanthropic" lunch that Zemlianika serves Khlestakov at the hospital is the play's most nauseating joke: while patients rot, the administrators feast.

Zemlianika's denunciation of his colleagues in Act IV is the scene that most fully reveals the institutional satire.

He tells Khlestakov, in a lowered tone: "the Postmaster does absolutely nothing; business is in great disorder and mail is held up...the Judge does nothing but hunt hares and keeps his dogs in the courtrooms...and Dobchinsky's children — all five of them — don't resemble Dobchinsky in the least, but rather the Judge" (037-002).

Every word of this is true — and every word is spoken by a man whose own institution is a scandal of neglect. Gogol's point is not that one official is worse than another but that the system selects for denunciation and deflection, creating an ecology of mutual surveillance in which no one is innocent and everyone is vulnerable.

The charitable institutions also serve as the physical correlative of the town's moral condition. The hospital without medicine, the church that was never built, the patients "looking as if they worked in a brewery" — these are not merely details of local color but emblems of a society in which the gap between official language ("philanthropic," "God-pleasing") and lived reality is unbridgeable.
