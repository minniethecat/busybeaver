# Khlestakov at the Inn

## Summary

Khlestakov's stay at the provincial inn forms the entirety of Act II and establishes the comedy's central misunderstanding. Penniless after gambling away his travel funds in Penza, Khlestakov has been living on credit at the inn for two weeks while his servant Osip fumes with hunger. The arrival of the Governor — who mistakes Khlestakov for the expected incognito inspector — transforms the starving clerk's fortunes in an instant, setting the entire plot in motion.

## In the Play

### Osip's Soliloquy (Scene I)

The act opens with Osip lying on his master's bed, delivering a long monologue that establishes Khlestakov's true character before the audience ever sees him. Osip reveals that they have been on the road for two months since leaving St. Petersburg, and that Khlestakov has squandered his travel allowance:

> He's gone through all his cash, the precious buck, so now he sticks here with his tail between his legs and takes it easy. ([009-001])

Osip's account — Khlestakov demanding the best rooms and finest dinners in every town, playing cards with fellow travelers until penniless, selling his English cloth dress coat for a fraction of its value — paints a portrait of feckless vanity. The servant's hunger ("There's a racket in my belly, as if a whole regiment were blowing trumpets") grounds the comedy in physical need.

### Khlestakov's Hunger (Scenes II–IV)

Khlestakov enters "with his hand to his forehead, looking very downcast" ([010-001]). He has tried walking off his hunger but "it clings" ([011-001]). His encounter with the servant at the inn reveals the precariousness of his position. When informed that the landlord will serve no more meals on credit, Khlestakov blusters — "I spit on your landlord" — but his bravado is hollow ([014-002]).

The meal that finally arrives is meager: soup and roast beef, two courses that Khlestakov declares insufficient. Yet he eats ravenously, and Osip's aside — "they'll both be gobbled up in a trice" — confirms that this is the first proper food either has seen in days.

### The Mistaken Identity (Scenes V–VIII)

The pivotal comic turn occurs when Dobchinsky and Bobchinsky arrive at the inn and identify Khlestakov as the inspector. The Governor's entrance transforms the scene. Khlestakov, who moments earlier was terrified of being arrested for unpaid bills, suddenly finds himself being offered money and better accommodations by the town's highest official.

The Governor offers to move Khlestakov to his own residence, and Khlestakov — still not understanding why he is being treated with such deference — accepts. The scene concludes with Khlestakov writing a letter to his friend Tryapichkin in St. Petersburg, delighting in the absurd situation but failing to grasp its full implications.

## Dramatic Significance

The inn scenes establish the play's fundamental dramatic irony. The audience knows Khlestakov is not an inspector; they watch the Governor and officials construct an elaborate fantasy around a hungry clerk. Gogol structures the act so that Khlestakov's genuine distress — his hunger, his fear of the landlord, his social humiliation — is misread by the officials as the cunning disguise of a government agent.

This misreading is the engine of the entire comedy. Every detail that should expose Khlestakov — his cheap traveling bag, his patched clothes, his petty complaints about the food — is reinterpreted by the terrified officials as evidence of his authority. The inn, as a liminal space between the road and the town, between St. Petersburg and the provinces, is the perfect setting for this confusion of identities.
