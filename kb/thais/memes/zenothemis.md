## Overview

Zénothémis is a philosopher in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), appearing primarily in the banquet scenes of Part II. He represents the Platonic-idealist strain of late-antique philosophy — speculative, mystical, and inclined toward elaborate cosmological theories. His debates with [[dorion|Dorion]], [[nicias|Nicias]], and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] structure much of the banquet's intellectual drama. France portrays him with characteristic irony: the philosopher of transcendent forms ends the evening pressing the courtesan [[philina|Philina]] in his arms (002-334).

## Biography

Zénothémis is first listed among the philosophers at [[cotta|Cotta]]'s banquet alongside [[dorion|Dorion]], [[nicias|Nicias]], the poet [[callicrate|Callicrate]], and the young Chéréas (002-230). Throughout the evening, he engages in vigorous philosophical debate. He turns toward the monk [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] and initiates a dialogue on divine matters (002-271), to which [[nicias|Nicias]] responds "Dis mieux, Zénothémis" — speak better, Zénothémis (002-272). [[dorion|Dorion]] challenges his positions (002-274: "Souffre, Zénothémis, que je t'arrête ici"), and a multi-party debate unfolds on the nature of Christ, the soul, and knowledge.

Zénothémis is described as "avide de discours" — greedy for discourse (002-288) — and possesses, along with [[hermodore|Hermodore]], "les principes de la connaissance" — the principles of knowledge (002-291). When the flute falls silent, he begins a formal discourse (002-308: "Zénothémis parla de la sorte"). The poet [[callicrate|Callicrate]] rebukes him as impious for offending the gods (002-317: "impie Zénothémis").

As the banquet descends into sensual chaos, Zénothémis abandons philosophy for pleasure. He takes [[philina|Philina]]'s vacated place (002-328) and is last seen pressing her in his arms (002-334). In the novel's visionary epilogue, Zénothémis reappears in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s dream, picking golden apples in a garden — a final ironic image of the philosopher as eternal sensualist (003-186).

## Character

Zénothémis embodies the speculative excess of late-antique Platonism. His discourse is elaborate, cosmological, and ultimately disconnected from the moral life — France's critique of philosophy that constructs beautiful systems while the philosopher's hands are occupied with courtesans. He is "avide de discours" — hungry for words — but his verbosity leads nowhere.

The contrast between Zénothémis and [[dorion|Dorion]] is instructive: [[dorion|Dorion]] is a melancholy skeptic who doubts even his own philosophy; Zénothémis is a confident systematizer whose confidence evaporates at the first touch of [[philina|Philina]]. Together they dramatize the novel's thesis that no philosophical system survives contact with human desire.

## Relationships

- **[[Dorion]]** — Zénothémis's primary debating partner at the banquet. Their exchanges on the soul, knowledge, and virtue structure the philosophical drama of Part II.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Zénothémis engages the ascetic in theological debate (002-271), attempting to fit Christianity into his Platonic framework.
- **[[Nicias]]** — Fellow philosopher at the banquet who engages Zénothémis with urbane wit.
- **[[Callicrate]]** — The poet who rebukes Zénothémis for impiety (002-317).
- **[[Philina]]** — The courtesan with whom Zénothémis ends the banquet entangled (002-334).
- **[[Cotta]]** — The [[rome|Roma]]n prefect who hosts the banquet at which Zénothémis debates.
