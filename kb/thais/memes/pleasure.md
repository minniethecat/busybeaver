## Definition

Pleasure (*le plaisir*) in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* operates as a philosophical and theological fault line: for the ascetics of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] it is the enemy of salvation, while for the Epicureans of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] it is the measured goal of a wise life. The novel declines to resolve this tension, instead letting each character's relationship to pleasure expose their inner architecture.

## In the Novel

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] identifies pleasure as the defining danger of woman and of the world. At [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet, he declares: "la femme est notre pire ennemie. Elle donne le plaisir et c'est en cela qu'elle est redoutable" (001-177). Woman gives pleasure, and that is precisely what makes her terrible. This logic — that the problem is not suffering but enjoyment — is the ascetic's core thesis, and the novel subjects it to sustained ironic pressure.

The Epicurean [[dorion|Dorion]] immediately counters: "la femme apporte aux hommes non le plaisir, mais la tristesse, le trouble et les noirs soucis" (001-178). For [[dorion|Dorion]], love brings not pleasure but sorrow, and his own remedy is a leaf from Phaedra's myrtle kept at his bedside as a permanent warning against desire. The ironic effect is clear: the ascetic and the Epicurean, supposedly opposites, agree that pleasure is dangerous — they differ only in whether they blame women or love itself.

[[thais|Thaïs]] embodies the pleasure that both philosophies reject. Her life in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] is described as immersion in "tous les plaisirs qu'étalait la ville élégante et monstrueuse" (002-134) — all the pleasures spread out by the elegant and monstrous city. Yet her question to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] cuts through both ascetic condemnation and Epicurean caution: "si je renonce à mes plaisirs et si je fais pénitence, est-il vrai que je renaîtrai au ciel avec mon corps intact et dans toute sa beauté?" (002-196). She is willing to exchange earthly pleasure for heavenly pleasure — a transaction that neither [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s severity nor [[dorion|Dorion]]'s detachment has the language to address.

The novel's deepest irony appears in the final book, when [[nicias|Nicias]] bids farewell to [[thais|Thaïs]]: "je te proclame digne d'envie... tu auras goûté dans la vie, chère [[thais|Thaïs]], des voluptés contraires" (003-015). She has tasted opposite pleasures — the pleasures of the flesh and the pleasures of renunciation — a completeness of experience that neither the ascetic nor the Epicurean can claim. Pleasure, the thing everyone in the novel tries to define, evade, or transcend, turns out to be the measure of a life fully lived.

## Philosophical Context

The debate at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet (001-177 through 001-184) stages a compressed history of ancient ethics. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] represents the radical dualist position: pleasure belongs to the body, the body belongs to sin, therefore pleasure must be extirpated. [[dorion|Dorion]] represents Epicurean *ataraxia* — the avoidance of disturbance rather than the pursuit of pleasure, properly understood. The absent position is Aristippean hedonism, the active pursuit of sensory pleasure as the highest good, which none of the characters defend. France's irony is that all three positions — ascetic rejection, Epicurean caution, and the hedonism no one admits to — prove inadequate to the complexity of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s actual experience.
