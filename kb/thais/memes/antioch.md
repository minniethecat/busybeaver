## Overview

Antioch (Antioche) was one of the great cities of the [[rome|Roman Empire]], situated on the Orontes River in the province of [[syria|Syria]]. In the fourth century, it was both a thriving commercial center and a major seat of early Christian authority — the Patriarchate of Antioch was one of the five ancient patriarchates of the Church. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, the city serves as the backdrop for the heroine's early career: it is here that the slave-girl [[thais|Thaïs]], trained in music and dance, first captivated the public and rose from obscurity to notoriety.

## In the Novel

Antioch appears in Part II (*Le Papyrus*) as the city where [[thais|Thaïs]]'s beauty first blazed into public consciousness. After being trained by the old procuress Moeroé, [[thais|Thaïs]] was brought to Antioch and rented out as a dancer and flute player to the city's wealthy merchants (002-118). Her debut was modest — she pleased only moderately at first, lacking experience. But within a few months, the power of her beauty erupted on the stage with such force that "all Antioch crowded the theatre" (002-136). Imperial magistrates, leading citizens, dock workers, and porters all paid to see her. Poets composed epigrams in her honor; bearded philosophers declaimed against her in the baths and gymnasia; Christian priests turned away their heads as her litter passed. Her doorstep was crowned with flowers and watered with blood, and she received gold measured by the *medimne* from the city's wealthiest men (002-136).

The city also appears in [[thais|Thaïs]]'s memories when, during the burning of her possessions, she recalls a young man she knew in Antioch — a lover whose memory was stirred by the sight of a small [[love|Eros]] statue given to her by [[nicias|Nicias]] (002-397). Later, in Part III (*L'Euphorbe*), the Patriarch of Antioch himself, then visiting [[egypt|Egypt]], comes with his entire clergy to witness [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] on his pillar and approves the stylite's extraordinary conduct (003-113).

## Geography

Antioch lay on the eastern bank of the Orontes River in what was then the [[rome|Roma]]n province of [[syria|Syria]] (modern-day Antakya, Turkey). The city was situated in a fertile valley surrounded by mountains, with access to the Mediterranean Sea via the port of Seleucia Pieria. The "groves of the Orontes" (002-118) were a noted pleasure-ground where the city's wealthy citizens entertained themselves — and where [[thais|Thaïs]], after performing at banquets, was taken by the richest bankers.

## Historical Background

In the fourth century, Antioch ranked alongside [[rome|Rome]], [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], and Constantinople as one of the greatest cities of the Empire. It was a major intellectual center, home to the renowned rhetorician Libanius, and a crucial seat of early Christian theology. The Patriarchate of Antioch traced its foundation to Saint Peter and exercised ecclesiastical authority over the Christians of the East. The novel's reference to the Patriarch visiting [[egypt|Egypt]] (003-113) reflects the city's genuine ecclesiastical prestige during this period, when the Antiochene school of theology competed with the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n school in doctrinal influence.
