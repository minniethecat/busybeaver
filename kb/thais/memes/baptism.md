## Definition

Baptism (*le baptême*) is the Christian sacrament of initiation, in which water symbolizes spiritual cleansing, death to sin, and rebirth into the community of believers. In the 4th-century Church, baptism was typically administered to adult converts after a period of catechetical instruction.

It was understood as indispensable for salvation: as the novel states, "Ceux-là seuls qui sont baptisés en Jésus, goûteront les félicités à venir" (002-058) — only those baptized in Jesus will taste future felicities.

## In the Novel

Baptism occupies a pivotal structural position in *[[thais|Thaïs]]*. The entire second half of Part II is organized around [[thais|Thaïs]]'s baptismal preparation and the ceremony itself. Her demand is direct: "[[thais|Thaïs]] demandait à être baptisée" (002-059) — [[thais|Thaïs]] asked to be baptized.

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], seeing her spiritual readiness, intones the invitation: "viens revêtir les aubes du baptême" (002-063) — come put on the albs of baptism.

The ceremony itself is described with liturgical precision. [[thais|Thaïs]] is immersed "trois fois dans la cuve baptismale" (002-074) — three times in the baptismal font — a Trinitarian immersion. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] delivers a homily "pour expliquer le baptême" (002-081), contrasting the baptism of water received by Christians with the fate of demons who receive no such cleansing (002-082).

The chapter closes with the definitive statement: "Ainsi que [[thais|Thaïs]] reçut le saint baptême" (002-084) — thus [[thais|Thaïs]] received holy baptism.

Earlier in the novel, baptism appears in a biographical register: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] himself "après son baptême, il resta un an encore parmi les gentils" (001-015) — after his baptism he remained a year among the gentiles. [[nicias|Nicias]], too, "avait reçu le baptême, et on le nommait [[ahmes|Théodore]]" (002-043) — had received baptism and was named Theodore — though his Christianity is nominal at best.

## Theological Background

In the 4th-century [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n tradition, baptism carried rich symbolic weight. Cyril of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] taught that baptism effected not merely symbolic cleansing but ontological transformation — the convert literally died and was reborn.

The *catecheses* of Cyril of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] (delivered around 350 CE) describe baptism as illumination (*photismos*), a term that resonates with the novel's imagery of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s spiritual awakening. The threefold immersion represented the three days of Christ's entombment, while the white baptismal garment signified the newly cleansed soul.
