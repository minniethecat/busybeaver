## Overview

Paul le Simple — Paul the Simple — is one of the most distinctive minor figures in *[[thais|Thaïs]]*. Originally a young peasant "surnommé le Simple à cause de sa naïveté" (001-010) — nicknamed the Simple because of his naivety — he possesses a childlike faith that paradoxically grants him clearer spiritual vision than the learned and zealous [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]. In the novel's climactic scene, it is Paul, not the abbot, who sees the heavens open and the angels receiving [[thais|Thaïs]]'s soul, while [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sees only emptiness. This reversal — the simpleton as true visionary — is one of France's most pointed theological ironies.

## Biography

Paul enters the narrative as a young peasant who has attached himself to the desert community. The text introduces him as a disciple of [[antoine|Antoine]] — "les enseignements de Paul et d'[[antoine|Antoine]], nos pères" (003-092) — placing him, remarkably, alongside the great saint as a source of spiritual teaching. His simplicity is not a defect but a vocation: his very name, "le Simple," marks him as one of those whom Christ called blessed.

During the gathering around the dying [[thais|Thaïs]], Paul remains standing while others kneel or weep (003-064). When [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], in his anguish, challenges the monks to look up and describe what they see, it is Paul whom he singles out: "Lève les yeux, mon fils Paul, et dis ce que tu vois dans le ciel" (003-253). Paul looks up, and what he describes — angels, prophets, the heavenly city — contrasts sharply with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s vision of emptiness and despair (003-254).

After this moment of spiritual clarity, Paul's vision fades — he "promena vainement ses regards" (003-260), wandering his gaze in vain — and he falls silent, his mouth open and his eyes haggard (003-262). The gift of vision is transient, but its authenticity is beyond question: for one moment, the simple peasant saw what the learned abbot could not.

## Character

Paul is defined entirely by his simplicity. France does not give him a biography, a personality, or a psychology in the modern sense — he is, as his name suggests, a figure of pure spiritual transparency. His few lines of dialogue are direct and unadorned, and his actions — standing while others kneel, looking up when bidden — have the quality of a child following instructions without self-consciousness.

This simplicity is the source of his spiritual authority. Unlike [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], whose learning and zeal become obstacles to genuine vision, Paul has nothing to unlearn. His faith is not the product of theological reasoning but of an unmediated openness to the divine. In the novel's implicit hierarchy of spiritual perception, Paul occupies the highest place — higher than [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], higher even than [[palemon|Palémon]] — because he does not try to see; he simply sees.

## Relationships

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] singles out Paul at the critical moment, perhaps unconsciously recognizing that the simple monk sees what he himself cannot. Their relationship is brief but decisive: Paul's vision confirms what [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s spiritual pride has obscured.
- **[[Antoine]]** — Paul is associated with Anthony as a co-source of spiritual teaching — a striking juxtaposition that elevates the simple peasant to the level of the great patriarch.
- **[[Palémon]]** — [[palemon|Palémon]] cares for Paul as part of the desert community and instructs [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] to "prenez soin de notre frère Paul" (003-097).

## Role in the Narrative

Paul's narrative function is to enact the Gospel paradox that the last shall be first and the simple shall confound the wise. In a novel deeply concerned with the relationship between knowledge and salvation, Paul represents the alternative to both [[nicias|Nicias]]'s philosophical sophistication and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s theological rigor: a faith so pure that it requires no concepts at all.

His moment of vision at [[thais|Thaïs]]'s deathbed is the novel's theological climax. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], the learned abbot who set out to save [[thais|Thaïs]]'s soul, sees nothing but darkness and his own thwarted desire. Paul, the simple peasant, sees paradise. The irony is devastating: the entire apparatus of ascetic discipline, theological study, and spiritual ambition has not brought [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] closer to God — it has pushed him further away, while Paul's simplicity has kept the door open.

## Themes

Paul embodies the theme of holy folly — the Christian tradition, stretching back to Saint Paul's letters, that God's wisdom appears as foolishness to the world. His nickname, "le Simple," carries both its literal meaning and its theological weight: he is simple-minded in the world's terms, but in spiritual terms he possesses the *simplicitas* that the desert fathers prized as the highest form of wisdom.

His character also develops the novel's critique of spiritual ambition. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] wants to be a saint; Paul simply is one, without knowing it. The difference is not one of effort or knowledge but of disposition — Paul does not strive to see God; he merely looks up when asked.

## Historical Context

The figure of Paul the Simple appears in the historical records of desert monasticism. The *Historia Monachorum in Aegypto* and Palladius's *Lausiac History* mention a Paul the Simple, a disciple of Anthony who was known for his extreme simplicity and his gift of spiritual discernment. The historical Paul was said to have been a farmer who, discovering his wife's infidelity, left his home and went to the desert. Anthony initially rejected him as too old to become a monk, but Paul's persistence and humility eventually won the saint's approval, and he became known for miracles of healing and exorcism.

France's portrait draws on this tradition but focuses on the theological meaning of Paul's simplicity rather than his miraculous powers. The novel's Paul is not a wonder-worker but a witness — his single moment of vision is enough.
