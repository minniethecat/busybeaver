## Overview

Racôtis (Racotis, from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian *Ra-qedet*) was the original fishing village on the Mediterranean strand that predated [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. When Alexander the Great founded his city in 331 BCE, Rhakotis became its indigenous [[egypt|Egypt]]ian quarter — the district where native traditions, temples, and populations persisted within the grid of the [[hell|Hell]]enistic polis. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, the quarter appears twice: first as the site of the actress's luxurious villa, "près du tombeau d'Alexandre," and later as the source of the toast raised at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet: "À la perle de Racotis!" The pearl is [[thais|Thaïs]] herself, and the epithet condenses the novel's central paradox — the courtesan is simultaneously the most cosmopolitan product of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] and the most rooted in its pre-Greek soil.

## In the Novel

Racôtis enters the narrative at the pivotal moment when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], having escaped the theater where [[thais|Thaïs]] has just performed, arrives at her door. The narrator situates her residence with topographic precision: "La comédienne alors, dans le riche quartier de Racotis, près du tombeau d'Alexandre, habitait une maison entourée de jardins ombreux, dans lesquels s'élevaient des rochers artificiels et coulait un ruisseau bordé de peupliers" (002-027). The rich quarter of Racotis, near the tomb of Alexander — the detail is carefully chosen. Rhakotis was traditionally the native [[egypt|Egypt]]ian district, but Anatole France places [[thais|Thaïs]]'s villa in its "riche quartier," implicitly on the borderland between the indigenous city and the [[hell|Hell]]enistic Neapolis. The artificial rocks and poplar-lined stream in her garden are a miniature pastoral — a constructed nature no less artificial than the grotto of the Nymphs where she was said to dwell.

Later, at the banquet in [[nicias|Nicias]]'s house, the feasters raise their cups in a litany of praise for [[thais|Thaïs]]: "À la perle de Racotis! … À la rose d'[[alexandrie|Alexandrie]]!" (002-223–224). The two epithets are paired but not identical: "the pearl of Racotis" anchors [[thais|Thaïs]] in the native quarter, while "the rose of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]" claims her for the cosmopolitan city. She is, the toasts imply, the single object on which both constituencies can agree — the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian quarter and the Greek city, the old cults and the new philosophies, all find their common denominator in her beauty.

> "La comédienne alors, dans le riche quartier de Racotis, près du tombeau d'Alexandre, habitait une maison entourée de jardins ombreux, dans lesquels s'élevaient des rochers artificiels et coulait un ruisseau bordé de peupliers." (002-027)
