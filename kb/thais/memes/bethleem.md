## Overview

Bethléem (Bethlehem) — the town of Judah where, according to the Gospels, Jesus was born — appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* not as geographic reality but as devotional landscape, the setting for the Nativity story retold twice: first in the artless, folk-inflected gospel of Paul-le-Simple, and then at the novel's spiritual climax, when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] falls into ecstatic speech before the kneeling [[thais|Thaïs]]. In both passages, Bethléem is a place where heaven touches earth with disarming closeness — where stars come within reach of children's hands and the divine consents to be a nursing infant.

## In the Novel

Bethléem first appears in the comic-tender episode where the simple monk Paul relates his version of the Gospel to the monks of [[antinoe|Antinoé]]. In his retelling, the infant Jesus "vint sur la terre en un lieu nommé Bethléem de Juda" (002-048) and walked through anemone-filled meadows, promising the hungry a seat at his father's table. Paul's Bethléem is a verdant, almost fairy-tale place — the "Bethléem de Juda" of the French nativity tradition, remote from [[rome|Roma]]n Palestine and close to the illuminated manuscripts of medieval Provence.

The second appearance is anything but naive. In the conversion scene of Part II, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], transported by religious ecstasy, speaks to the invisible Christ standing beside him: "N'est-ce pas, ô mon Jésus! que tu m'apparais en ce moment, comme tu apparus aux hommes de Galilée en ces jours merveilleux où les étoiles, descendues avec toi du ciel, étaient si près de la terre, que les saints Innocents pouvaient les saisir dans leurs mains, quand ils jouaient aux bras de leurs mères, sur les terrasses de Bethléem?" (002-202). The image of the Holy Innocents reaching up from the rooftops of Bethlehem to catch the stars is derived from the Gospel of Pseudo-Matthew and the Protevangelium of James — apocryphal Nativity traditions that Anatole France knew well. In [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s mouth, the image serves a precise rhetorical purpose: if the stars descended so close to Bethlehem that infants could grasp them, then surely Christ can descend into the house of a courtesan in the [[racotis|Racotis]] quarter.

> "N'est-ce pas, ô mon Jésus! que tu m'apparais en ce moment, comme tu apparus aux hommes de Galilée en ces jours merveilleux où les étoiles, descendues avec toi du ciel, étaient si près de la terre, que les saints Innocents pouvaient les saisir dans leurs mains, quand ils jouaient aux bras de leurs mères, sur les terrasses de Bethléem?" (002-202)
