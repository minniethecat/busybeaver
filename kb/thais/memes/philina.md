## Overview

Philina is a courtesan who appears in the banquet scene of Part II of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). Alongside her companion [[drose|Drosé]], she represents the world of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n pleasure that forms the backdrop to [[thais|Thaïs]]'s story. Her brief but vivid appearances — admiring [[thais|Thaïs]]'s beauty, whispering warnings, and finally collapsing into the arms of [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] — trace an arc from admiration to dissolution that mirrors the banquet's own descent from philosophy to sensuality.

## Biography

Philina appears at the banquet alongside [[drose|Drosé]], "qu'il faut louer pour sa beauté" (002-230) — who must be praised for her beauty. The two courtesans fix their gaze on [[thais|Thaïs]] with an intensity that borders on consumption: "Philina et [[drose|Drosé]] dévoraient [[thais|Thaïs]] des yeux" (002-235) — Philina and [[drose|Drosé]] devoured [[thais|Thaïs]] with their eyes. Philina then addresses [[thais|Thaïs]] directly: "Que tu es belle! lui dit Philina. Tu ne pouvais l'être plus qu'au moment où tu es apparue..." (002-236) — How beautiful you are! Philina said to her. You could not be more beautiful than at the moment you appeared...

But Philina's admiration carries an edge of warning. She places a finger on her lips (002-238), a gesture that suggests she knows something about the danger [[thais|Thaïs]] faces — perhaps the possessiveness of the men who surround her, perhaps the fragility of beauty itself. The gesture is ambiguous but charged with meaning in a novel where beauty and danger are inseparable.

Philina's final appearance is in the tableau of the banquet's collapse. As philosophy gives way to intoxication, "[[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] pressait dans ses bras Philina défaite. [[dorion|Dorion]] versait sur la gorge nue de [[drose|Drosé]]..." (002-334) — [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] pressed the undone Philina in his arms. [[dorion|Dorion]] poured wine on the bare throat of [[drose|Drosé]]... The word "défaite" — undone, defeated, unraveled — is crucial. Philina, who earlier admired [[thais|Thaïs]] with such intensity, ends the night a passive object of male desire, her agency dissolved in wine and flesh.

## Character

Philina is a minor character but a significant one. She and [[drose|Drosé]] form a pair that mirrors the [[thais|Thaïs]]-[[nicias|Nicias]] relationship in miniature — the courtesan and the philosopher-intellectual, the beauty and the beholder. But where [[thais|Thaïs]] transcends this dynamic through her conversion, Philina remains trapped within it. Her whispered warning to [[thais|Thaïs]] (002-238) suggests an awareness of her own condition — the knowledge that beauty attracts not only admiration but predation.

France's portrayal is characteristically double. Philina is both agent and victim, both admirer and competitor. Her "défaite" at the banquet's end is not presented as comic or deserved but as the logical terminus of a world where women's beauty is everyone's property but their own.

## Relationships

- **[[Thaïs]]** — Philina admires [[thais|Thaïs]]'s beauty with an intensity that combines genuine awe with potential rivalry. Her whispered warning suggests a complex mix of protectiveness and self-interest.
- **[[Zenothemis]]** — The philosopher who ends the banquet with Philina in his arms (002-334), embodying the collapse of philosophical discourse into physical appetite.
- **[[Dorion]]** — Present in the final tableau, pouring wine on [[drose|Drosé]] while Philina lies in [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]]'s embrace.
- **[[drose|Drosé]]** — Philina's companion, praised for her beauty, who shares Philina's fate at the banquet's end.

## Role in the Narrative

Philina's role is structurally minor but thematically significant. She embodies the fate of beauty in a world governed by male desire — admired, consumed, and discarded in a single evening. Her trajectory from gazing at [[thais|Thaïs]] to lying "défaite" in [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]]'s arms is a compressed version of the danger [[thais|Thaïs]] herself faces, and from which only her conversion — and death — ultimately free her.
