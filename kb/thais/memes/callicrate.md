## Overview

Callicrate is a poet who appears in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), primarily at the banquet scene in Part II and briefly in the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n streets of Part III. He represents the artistic temperament amid the philosophical disputations of [[nicias|Nicias]]'s circle — a figure more concerned with beauty and verse than with systematic argument. His presence at the banquet alongside philosophers and a priest of Serapis illustrates the eclectic cultural milieu of 4th-century [[alexandrie|Alexandria]].

## Biography

Callicrate first appears at the banquet in Part II, listed among "les philosophes avides de disputes, le poète Callicrate, le grand prêtre de Sérapis" (002-212) — the philosophers avid for disputes, the poet Callicrate, the high priest of Serapis. He is named again among the banquet guests alongside [[nicias|Nicias]], [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]], [[dorion|Dorion]], and the young Chéréas (002-230).

During the philosophical debates, Callicrate is addressed when the topic turns to [[thais|Thaïs]]. A guest asks: "Que dis-tu, Callicrate? Notre chère [[thais|Thaïs]] aurait connu..." (002-318) — What do you say, Callicrate? Our dear [[thais|Thaïs]] would have known... — suggesting that Callicrate is associated with the courtesan's world and may have firsthand knowledge of her. The question is followed by a dramatic collapse: the speaker rolls under the table, and Callicrate raises his cup (002-320), a gesture that aligns him with the banquet's descent from philosophical debate into intoxicated revelry.

In Part III, Callicrate appears briefly in a different context. "Sous un portique Callicrate déclamait des vers" (003-186) — Under a portico, Callicrate was declaiming verses. The image is striking: while [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] descends into spiritual torment, Callicrate continues his artistic life in the city, unchanged and untroubled by the drama that has consumed the novel's central characters.

## Character

Callicrate is a minor figure, but his presence serves an important function in France's anatomy of late-antique [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. He represents the aesthetic life — the pursuit of beauty through art — as yet another option among the competing worldviews on display at the banquet. Unlike the philosophers, Callicrate does not argue; he recites. His intelligence is performative rather than analytical, and his raising of the cup when the philosophical debate collapses into drunken chaos suggests a temperament more at home in pleasure than in disputation.

France's treatment of Callicrate is characteristically gentle. The poet is not satirized as a fool but presented as one more figure in a world of irreducible diversity — a world that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s totalizing ascetic vision cannot comprehend or contain.

## Relationships

- **[[Nicias]]** — Host of the banquet where Callicrate recites and drinks.
- **[[Thaïs]]** — The courtesan whose name provokes the question directed at Callicrate (002-318), implying some connection or knowledge.
- **[[Dorion]]** — Fellow guest at the banquet; both participate in its philosophical and sensual dimensions.
- **[[Cotta]]** — The [[rome|Roma]]n prefect who presides over the gathering where Callicrate appears.

## Role in the Narrative

Callicrate's role is emblematic rather than functional. He does not drive the plot or develop as a character, but his presence expands the novel's portrait of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n society. The contrast between his serene verse-declaiming under the portico (003-186) and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s spiritual anguish in the desert is one of the novel's quiet ironies: art goes on, beauty goes on, the city goes on — indifferent to the drama of salvation unfolding elsewhere.
