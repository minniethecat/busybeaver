## Overview

Aristée (Aristaeus) is a physician in fourth-century [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], serving as the personal doctor to the wealthy Epicurean philosopher [[nicias|Nicias]]. He appears primarily in Part III (*L'Euphorbe*) of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), where he attends the banquet hosted by [[nicias|Nicias]] and participates in the philosophical debates surrounding the stylite ascetic. As a man of medicine and reason, Aristée represents the skeptical, empirical worldview that contrasts with both Christian asceticism and pagan superstition.

## Biography

Aristée first enters the narrative when [[nicias|Nicias]], examining a wound, calls for his physician: "Qu'on appelle mon médecin Aristée!" (002-356). He later appears at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s side during the grand procession that greets the arrival of the philosophers and courtesans in Part III, described as part of [[nicias|Nicias]]'s retinue — a secretary carrying tablets, and "Aristée, son médecin, avec qui il aimait à converser" (003-128), indicating the intellectual companionship between doctor and patron.

At the banquet, Aristée engages in the debate about the stylite who lives atop a column. When the guests marvel that the monk has not descended in a year, [[cotta|Cotta]] asks whether such a feat is possible. Aristée responds with clinical precision: "Cela est possible à un fou et à un malade… et ce serait impossible à un homme sain de corps et d'esprit" (003-135). His diagnosis frames the ascetic's endurance as pathology rather than sanctity.

[[cotta|Cotta]] repeatedly invokes Aristée's medical authority — "tu l'entends, Aristée" (003-143) — seeking the physician's validation in debate. Aristée, however, maintains a measured skepticism, shaking his head and ultimately diagnosing the stylite's condition with two words: "L'ignorance et la folie" (003-147) — ignorance and madness.

## Character

Aristée embodies the rational, medical worldview of late antiquity. Unlike the philosophers at the banquet who argue from abstract principles, Aristée reasons from clinical observation. His judgment of the stylite as "a madman and a sick man" is not mockery but diagnosis — the voice of a physician who has seen pathology and recognizes it even in religious guise.

France uses Aristée sparingly but precisely. He is neither a central character nor a mere name; he occupies the functional role of the *rational observer* whose presence anchors the banquet's speculative flights in empirical ground. His quiet head-shaking at [[cotta|Cotta]]'s enthusiasm suggests a man accustomed to humoring the passions of the wealthy while privately reserving judgment.

## Relationships

- **[[Nicias]]** — Aristée serves as [[nicias|Nicias]]'s personal physician and intellectual companion. [[nicias|Nicias]] "aimait à converser" with him (003-128), suggesting a relationship that transcends mere medical attendance.
- **[[Cotta]]** — The [[rome|Roma]]n noble repeatedly seeks Aristée's validation during the banquet debates, invoking his medical authority as a rhetorical weapon. Aristée obliges with clinical observations but does not share [[cotta|Cotta]]'s exuberance.

## Role in the Narrative

Aristée functions as the voice of medical empiricism in the banquet's philosophical polyphony. His diagnosis of the stylite — madness and ignorance — provides a naturalistic counterpoint to both Christian veneration of the ascetic and pagan superstition. By framing extreme asceticism as pathology, Aristée implicitly questions the spiritual value that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] and the desert tradition invest in bodily mortification.
