## Definition

A miracle (*miracle*, from the Latin *miraculum* meaning "object of wonder") is an event that appears to transcend natural laws and is attributed to divine agency. In Christian tradition, miracles serve as signs that authenticate divine revelation and sanctity. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, miracle narratives surround [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s later career — but the novel's framing quietly destabilizes any simple equation between miraculous power and genuine holiness.

## In the Novel

Miracles are invoked from the novel's opening as the foundation of Christian belief. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] declares to [[thais|Thaïs]]: "Dieu, qui est la vérité, s'est révélé aux hommes par des miracles. Et il s'est fait chair et il a habité parmi nous" (001-135) — God, who is truth, revealed himself to men through miracles. And he became flesh and dwelt among us. The Incarnation itself is presented as the miracle that validates all others: the pattern of divine self-disclosure through extraordinary signs.

The novel's treatment of miracles shifts markedly in its second half. Before the [[syria|Syria]]n prefect [[cotta|Cotta]], [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] thunders that all his audience are "de vivantes immondices et c'est par un miracle de la bonté céleste que vous ne vous répandez pas soudain en ruisseaux de boue" (002-435) — living filth, and it is by a miracle of celestial goodness that you do not suddenly dissolve into streams of mud. Here miracle is rhetorically weaponized: the mere fact of continued existence is recast as an unmerited divine intervention, a rhetorical move that magnifies the preacher's authority by minimizing his audience's desert.

The novel's most extensive miracle narrative concerns [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s career atop his column. The people "publiait partout les miracles opérés par le saint" (003-117) — published everywhere the miracles operated by the saint — and epileptics flocked to him in "légions innombrables" (003-117). The scene at the foot of the column dissolves into collective hysteria: "Moines et pèlerins, hommes, femmes, se vautraient, se débattaient pêle-mêle, les membres tordus, la bouche écumeuse" (003-117) — Monks and pilgrims, men, women, wallowed and struggled pell-mell, limbs twisted, mouths foaming. The narrator's clinical description refuses to endorse the supernatural interpretation, leaving the reader to decide whether these are miracles or mass psychogenic illness.

The Easter gathering at "cette cité du miracle" (003-122) — this city of miracle — is described with a densely catalogued materialism that crowds out the supernatural. Merchants sell amulets, acrobats perform, snake charmers display their serpents. The "miracle" of the place name is swallowed by the sheer noise and commerce of the scene.

The conversion of the prefect [[cotta|Cotta]] is reported as a miracle whose details were embellished in transmission: "De pieuses imaginations les ornèrent et les transformèrent" (003-149) — Pious imaginations ornamented and transformed them. The narrator adds, with detectable irony, that "le miracle étant notoire, les diacres des principales églises de Lybie en rédigèrent les actes authentiques" (003-149) — the miracle being notorious, the deacons of the principal churches of Libya drew up its authentic acts. "Notoire" carries a double sense — both "widely known" and "notorious" — and the phrase "actes authentiques" is undercut by the acknowledgment that the story had already been embellished.

The culminating miracle is [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s own disappearance from the column. His disciple [[flavien|Flavien]] "publia le miracle et prit à ta place le gouvernement des moines" (003-217) — published the miracle and took in your place the government of the monks — a formulation that invites the reader to consider whether [[flavien|Flavien]]'s eagerness to publish the miracle might have had something to do with his eagerness to assume [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s authority. The sole dissenter, a simple monk named Paul, "assurait qu'il t'avait vu en rêve emporté par des diables" (003-217) — assured everyone that he had seen you in a dream carried off by devils — and "c'est merveille qu'il ait pu échapper à la mort" (003-217) — it is a wonder he escaped death. The novel's final word on miracles may be this: the community's need for miraculous narrative is so powerful that it will kill anyone who offers a competing account.

## Theological Background

Miracles in early Christianity served an apologetic function — they validated the truth of the faith against pagan competitors. The Gospels present Christ's miracles as signs of the Kingdom's arrival, and the Acts of the Apostles extends this pattern to the apostolic church. By the fourth century, the cult of saints and their miracles had become central to popular piety, with shrines and relics generating pilgrimage economies.

The Desert Fathers were deeply ambivalent about miracles. The *Apophthegmata* record numerous warnings against seeking or boasting of miraculous powers, which were seen as distractions from the true work of repentance. A famous saying attributed to Anthony holds that the greatest miracle is not raising the dead but forgiving one's enemy. *[[thais|Thaïs]]* operates within this tradition of ambivalence, presenting miracle as a phenomenon that may say more about the audience's needs than about the saint's holiness.
