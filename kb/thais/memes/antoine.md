## Overview

Antoine — Saint Anthony the Great — is the founding figure of Christian desert monasticism, whose legendary life hovers over the world of *[[thais|Thaïs]]* as the ultimate model of ascetic perfection. Though Anthony never appears directly in the novel, he is invoked repeatedly by the desert monks as "notre père Antoine" (001-009), the standard against whom all ascetic striving is measured. His reported age — over a hundred years — and his heroic resistance to demonic temptation established the template that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] and his fellow monks seek to follow.

## Biography

By the time of the novel's events, Antoine has already withdrawn from the world. The text notes that "Antoine, âgé de plus de cent ans, s'était retiré" (001-009) — Anthony, aged more than a hundred years, had withdrawn to his mountain retreat. From this remote fastness, his reputation radiates across the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian desert, and monks travel to receive his blessing.

Anthony's life is recalled through the teachings and sayings that the younger monks transmit. [[palemon|Palémon]] recalls Anthony's wisdom: that a sinner should not despair, for even the fishes die when taken from the water (001-038). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] himself once journeyed to Anthony's mountain for guidance (001-096), and the saint's example is cited in moments of spiritual crisis — when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] wrestles with temptation, he remembers that Anthony on his mountain suffered even greater trials (003-167).

The monks speak of Anthony's visions, his battles with demons, and his miracles with a mixture of awe and emulation. His hundred-and-five-year lifespan (003-214) and his enduring spiritual vigor even in extreme old age make him a living legend, the patriarch of the desert whose blessing every ascetic seeks before his death (003-221).

## Character

Anthony appears in the novel only through reported speech and memory, but France's treatment captures the historical Anthony's paradoxical combination of extreme solitude and magnetic influence. Anthony fled the world, yet the world came to him; he sought silence, yet his sayings were repeated across the desert. The monks' invocations of "notre père Antoine" reveal a man whose authority derived not from institutional position but from the sheer intensity of his ascetic witness.

The novel's Anthony is not the tormented figure of Flaubert's *La [[temptation|Tentation]] de saint Antoine* but a more serene presence — a man who has fought his battles and emerged into a kind of luminous old age. His advice, as reported by [[palemon|Palémon]], is humane rather than harsh: he counsels patience with sinners and moderation in judgment.

## Relationships

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] venerates Anthony as the supreme model of ascetic life. He traveled to Anthony's mountain for guidance and later, in his own spiritual crisis, measures his sufferings against Anthony's legendary trials.
- **[[Palémon]]** — [[palemon|Palémon]] transmits Anthony's teachings to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], serving as a living link to the great saint. His knowledge of Anthony's sayings establishes his own authority as a spiritual elder.
- **[[Paul Le Simple]]** — Paul is directly associated with Anthony in the text: "les enseignements de Paul et d'Antoine, nos pères" (003-092), marking both as founding figures of the tradition.

## Role in the Narrative

Anthony functions as the novel's spiritual horizon — the asymptote of ascetic perfection that the desert monks strive toward but never reach. By keeping Anthony offstage and at a distance (on his remote mountain, aged beyond a century), France preserves his mystique while allowing the reader to focus on the more human and flawed ascetics who occupy the foreground.

The repeated invocations of Anthony also establish the historical depth of the desert tradition. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is not an isolated eccentric but the inheritor of a movement already two generations old by the time of the novel's events. Anthony's sayings and example provide the theological and practical framework within which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s more extreme actions can be understood — and, implicitly, critiqued.

## Themes

Anthony embodies the theme of spiritual fatherhood — the *abba* tradition of the desert, in which wisdom is transmitted through personal relationship rather than institutional hierarchy. The monks' repeated phrase "notre père Antoine" is not mere rhetoric; it expresses a genuine sense of filiation.

The contrast between Anthony's serene old age and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s anguished middle age is thematically significant. Anthony has passed through temptation into peace; [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is still in the fire. This difference suggests a developmental model of the spiritual life — one in which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s torment may, if endured, eventually yield to the tranquility Anthony represents.

## Historical Context

The historical Anthony the Great (c. 251–356 AD) was a Coptic Christian from Lower [[egypt|Egypt]] who, inspired by the Gospel command to sell all possessions, withdrew into the desert around 270–275 AD. His biography, written by Athanasius of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] around 360 AD, became one of the most influential texts of late antiquity — translated into Latin, [[syria|Syria]]c, and many other languages, it created the literary genre of the saint's life and established the desert father as a Christian ideal.

Anthony's reported lifespan of 105 years made him a living bridge between the age of the martyrs and the age of the monks. By the time of the novel's setting (late 4th century), Anthony would have been near the end of his life, his legend already well established. France's treatment is historically plausible: Anthony would indeed have been the patriarch of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian monasticism, his mountain (modern Deir Mar Antonios near the Red Sea) a place of pilgrimage.
