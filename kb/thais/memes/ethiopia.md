## Overview

Ethiopia (Éthiopie, Aethiopia) appears in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890) as a peripheral but meaningful presence — the African kingdom beyond [[egypt|Egypt]]'s southern frontier whose people are woven into the fabric of late antique [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n society. Ethiopian figures appear at key moments in the narrative, serving as markers of the cosmopolitan reach of the [[rome|Roma]]n world and, in one striking image, of the sensuous luxury that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has renounced.

## In the Novel

Ethiopia enters the novel first through demonic imagery. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], recalling the temptations that afflict the desert monk, describes how demons "having taken the figures of Ethiopians or of animals, wandered about" (001-005). The association of Ethiopians with the demonic — a troubling convention of early Christian literature — reflects the racial imagination of late antiquity, in which the dark-skinned inhabitants of Africa's interior were sometimes figured as embodiments of evil. France records this convention without endorsing it; the demons that haunt [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] assume many forms, and the Ethiopian figure is but one among many masks of temptation.

The second, more vivid appearance occurs in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], in the palace of [[thais|Thaïs]]. As [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] waits in her chambers, "three robust Ethiopians rushed in holding a woman embraced in their arms" (002-394). The woman, her feet trailing on the carpet, is the dancer [[philina|Philina]], and the Ethiopians are her bearers — enslaved attendants whose physical strength serves the culture of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n luxury. The image distills the social reality of fourth-century [[egypt|Egypt]]: a world where the wealth of the Mediterranean elite was sustained by the labor and bodies of those from beyond its frontiers.

## Historical Background

In the fourth century, the Kingdom of Aksum (in modern Ethiopia and Eritrea) was a major power in northeast Africa, controlling trade routes between the [[rome|Roman Empire]] and [[india|India]]. Aksum had adopted Christianity as its state religion in the early fourth century under King Ezana, making it one of the earliest Christian kingdoms in the world. The "Ethiopians" referenced in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* likely reflect the presence of Aksumite traders, envoys, and enslaved people in [[rome|Roma]]n [[egypt|Egypt]], where the kingdom's growing commercial and diplomatic influence was increasingly felt.
