## Overview

Lucius Aurélius Cotta is a [[rome|Roma]]n official in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890) — prefect of the fleet (*préfet de la flotte*) and host of the great banquet in Part II. An elderly, serene pagan, Cotta provides the social frame for the novel's central philosophical debates. His [[rome|Roma]]n dignity and detached curiosity toward religious matters embody the late-antique pagan establishment that Christianity was in the process of displacing. In Part III, Cotta reappears as the bearer of news that reconnects [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] with the world he has fled.

## Biography

Cotta first appears in Part II as the host of the banquet at which [[thais|Thaïs]], [[nicias|Nicias]], [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], and the novel's entire philosophical cast gather. He is introduced as "le vieux Cotta, préfet de la flotte" — the old Cotta, prefect of the fleet (002-212). When [[thais|Thaïs]] enters the banquet hall she addresses him warmly as her host (002-225). He speaks with serenity throughout the evening, his voice described as "impérieuse et bienveillante" — commanding and benevolent (002-241).

During the banquet, Cotta presides with [[rome|Roma]]n calm over the escalating chaos of philosophical debate and sensual indulgence. He signals to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] to take his place (002-262), and later dozes off, his bald head nodding among the cushions (002-322). Waking from his light sleep, he strikes his forehead and joins the discussion once more (002-355, 002-359).

In Part III, Cotta reappears in a different register. He has gone to inspect the canals and waterways of the [[nile|Nile]] delta (003-128). When news reaches [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] that "Lucius Aurélius Cotta va venir" — Cotta is coming — the information takes on portentous weight, reconnecting the anchorite to the [[rome|Roma]]n world he thought he had left behind (003-127). When he does arrive, Cotta exclaims "Par Jupiter! voilà une grande aberration!" (003-136), voicing the pagan perspective on Christian asceticism. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], considering Cotta an idolater, does not deign to respond (003-141), yet Cotta persists in his questions and observations (003-143, 003-146, 003-148).

## Character

Cotta represents [[rome|Roma]]n *gravitas* — the calm, administrative temperament of a man who has governed fleets and provinces. France draws him with affection and irony: he is dignified even while dozing, curious about Christianity without being converted by it, and ultimately a representative of the passing order. His paganism is not fervent but habitual — he swears by Jupiter (003-136) as a matter of course, not conviction.

Cotta's serenity contrasts sharply with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s spiritual torment. Where [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] wrestles with demons, Cotta simply goes about his [[rome|Roma]]n business — inspecting canals, hosting banquets, asking polite questions about philosophy. His benevolent curiosity toward the Christian ascetic ("je souhaite qu'un jour..." 003-148) is the civility of a dying world toward the one that will replace it.

## Relationships

- **[[Thaïs]]** — Cotta is her host at the banquet; she addresses him with warmth. Their relationship frames the social world [[thais|Thaïs]] occupies before her conversion.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Cotta treats [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] with courteous interest; [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] dismisses Cotta as an idolater. Their interactions dramatize the gulf between pagan tolerance and Christian exclusivism.
- **[[Nicias]]** — Fellow guest at the banquet, though Cotta as host occupies a position above the philosophical fray.
- **[[Zenothemis]]** — One of the philosophers Cotta hosts and whose debates he observes with detached interest.

## Role in the Narrative

Cotta serves as a structural pillar of the novel's social world. As host of the banquet, he provides the physical and social space within which the philosophical debates of Part II unfold. His reappearance in Part III — as the [[rome|Roma]]n official whose approach signals the return of the world — creates a narrative bridge between [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s desert isolation and the civilization he abandoned.
