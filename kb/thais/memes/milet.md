## Overview

Milet (Miletus) was an ancient Greek city on the western coast of [[asia|Asia Minor]], at the mouth of the Maeander River. Once the wealthiest of all Greek cities in the sixth century BCE, it remained a prosperous trading center into the [[rome|Roma]]n period, celebrated for its fine wool, linen, and textiles. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, Miletus appears as the source of luxury fabric and as the native city of Aspasia, the celebrated courtesan of classical [[athens|Athens]] — a figure whose name evokes the association of beauty, intellect, and feminine influence that [[thais|Thaïs]] herself embodies.

## In the Novel

Miletus appears in three passages, each tied to the world of luxury and refinement that surrounds [[thais|Thaïs]]. In the opening pages, as the narrator paints the portrait of fourth-century [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], Aspasia of Miletus is invoked as a paragon of courtly elegance: "Near him, Aspasia of Miletus, elegantly wrapped in her mantle" (001-162). Aspasia, the famous companion of Pericles in classical [[athens|Athens]], is placed among the legendary beauties of the ancient world, her Milesian origin an integral part of her identity.

Later, in [[nicias|Nicias]]'s letter to an imagined correspondent, he describes the domesticity of a well-appointed [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n household where a beautiful housekeeper "unwinds the wool and the thread of Miletus" (002-086) — the fine woolens of Miletus being a mark of luxury and good taste. The city's reputation for textiles is again underscored when reference is made to a merchant "who sold the wool of Miletus and the linen of Tarentum" (002-422). Together, these references build a consistent picture: Miletus represents the eastern Mediterranean luxury trade that furnishes the sensuous material world [[thais|Thaïs]] ultimately renounces.

## Historical Background

Founded by Ionian Greeks, Miletus flourished as a commercial and intellectual hub from the seventh to the fifth centuries BCE, founding dozens of colonies throughout the Black Sea and Mediterranean. The Milesian school of philosophy, inaugurated by Thales, was among the earliest in the Greek tradition. Though the city declined after its destruction by the Persians in 494 BCE and never fully recovered its preeminence, it remained an important regional center throughout the [[hell|Hell]]enistic and [[rome|Roma]]n periods. Its woolen industry sustained its commercial reputation well into late antiquity.
