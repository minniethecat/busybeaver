## Overview

Arabia (Arabie) — in the fourth-century [[rome|Roma]]n understanding, the arid regions east of [[egypt|Egypt]] and south of [[syria|Syria]], encompassing both the [[rome|Roma]]n province of Arabia Petraea and the vast Arabian Peninsula beyond — appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* in two distinct registers. It is a land of persecution, named alongside [[syria|Syria]] and Mesopotamia as a theater of Christian suffering, and simultaneously a land of aromatic luxury, its gums and incense lending sensuous perfume to the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n scenes.

## In the Novel

Arabia first enters the novel in the context of the great persecution. The Christians of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], listening in terror to reports of the tortures afflicting the faithful, hear that "in [[syria|Syria]], in Arabia, in Mesopotamia, in Cappadocia, throughout the whole empire, whips, racks, iron claws, the cross, and wild beasts were tearing apart pontiffs and virgins" (002-044). The litany of provinces, moving eastward from [[syria|Syria]] through Arabia, registers the geographic sweep of the persecution under Diocletian and Galerius.

In a wholly different register, Arabia is the source of the precious resins that perfume the banquet scenes. At the feast in [[nicias|Nicias]]'s villa, the narrator describes how "the smoke of Arabian gums seemed like the folds of veils" (002-143) — an image that suggests the opulence and seductive beauty that surrounds the dinner guests. Arabian incense, frankincense and myrrh, had been the luxury commodity of the eastern trade since antiquity, and its presence signals the cosmopolitan wealth of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n society.

## Historical Background

The [[rome|Roma]]n province of Arabia Petraea, established by Trajan in 106 CE after the annexation of the Nabatean kingdom, covered the [[sin|Sin]]ai, the Negev, and parts of modern Jordan. In the fourth century, Arabia was both a frontier province — with military roads and garrisons defending against desert incursions — and a vital commercial corridor for the spice and incense trade from southern Arabia (Arabia Felix, modern Yemen) to the Mediterranean. Christianity reached the province early; by the time of the Council of [[nicee|Nicaea]], Arabian bishops were in attendance. The persecution referenced in 002-044 reflects the historical reality that the Diocletianic persecution (303–313) was particularly severe in the eastern provinces, including Arabia.
