## Overview

Cos (Kos), one of the Dodecanese islands of the southeastern Aegean, enters the world of *[[thais|Thaïs]]* through a single character: [[timocles|Timoclès]], the sceptical philosopher who declares himself "né à Cos de parents enrichis dans le négoce" (001-085). In the fourth century, Cos was a prosperous trading center known for its silk, wine, and the Asklepieion — the great healing sanctuary of the Hippocratic school. The novel uses Cos as a marker of Greek philosophical culture: [[timocles|Timoclès]] carries his island's rationalist, mercantile spirit into the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n banquet debates, where his scepticism collides with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s desert-bred asceticism.

## In the Novel

Cos is introduced when [[timocles|Timoclès]], challenged at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet to explain his philosophical position, identifies himself by name and origin. His self-presentation is emphatically genealogical: he is [[timocles|Timoclès]], "born in Cos of parents enriched in commerce," adding that his father "armed ships" and that his two brothers followed the same profession of ship-owning (001-085). The island thus stands for a specific intellectual lineage: the practical, seafaring, commercially-minded Greek rationalism that France pointedly contrasts with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s otherworldly asceticism.

The identification is formalized when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] addresses him as "[[timocles|Timoclès de Cos]]" (001-087) — an epithet that echoes Homeric convention, binding a thinker to his place of origin. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s response to [[timocles|Timoclès]]'s scepticism is a confession of Christian truth, framed as an address precisely to the man *of Cos*.

> "Je me nomme Timoclès et que je suis né à Cos de parents enrichis dans le négoce. Mon père armait des navires." (001-085)

## Geography

Cos lies in the southeastern Aegean Sea, off the coast of [[asia|Asia Minor]] near the Gulf of Ceramus and the ancient city of Halicarnassus. The island is approximately 40 km long, characterized by fertile plains, thermal springs, and a mild Mediterranean climate. In antiquity, its strategic position on maritime trade routes connecting [[greece|Greece]], [[asia|Asia Minor]], and [[egypt|Egypt]] made it a prosperous commercial center.
