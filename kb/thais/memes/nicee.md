## Overview

Nicée (Nicaea) was an ancient city in Bithynia, [[asia|Asia Minor]] (modern İznik, Turkey), best known as the site of the First Ecumenical Council convened by the Emperor Constantine in 325 CE. The council produced the Nicene Creed, which defined the orthodox doctrine of the Trinity against Arianism. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, Nicaea is invoked not as a physical location but as a theological symbol — the "faith of Nicaea," the "fathers of Nicaea," and the "symbol of Nicaea" are all contested points in the doctrinal conflicts that form the intellectual background of the novel's fourth-century setting.

## In the Novel

Nicaea appears in the novel exclusively through its theological legacy. During the philosophical banquet hosted by [[nicias|Nicias]] in Part II (*Le Papyrus*), the guests debate the nature of Christ, and the authority of the Nicene Creed becomes a flashpoint. The Arian [[marcus|Marcus]] attacks the creed directly: "it is clear that the inept symbol of Nicaea attacks the majesty of the one God" (002-296). His reference to "the mules of Nicaea and the stubborn ass who governed them" (002-293) is a crude Arian insult directed at the bishops who formulated the creed, implying they were stubborn beasts incapable of grasping the subtler theology of Arian subordinationism.

Earlier in the same discussion, the broader conflict is framed as "the persecution of the faith of Nicaea" (002-245), situating the Nicene party as the embattled orthodox minority during a period when Arianism enjoyed imperial favor. Later, in Part III (*L'Euphorbe*), the narrator invokes the "apostles and the fathers of Nicaea" (003-149) as the authoritative sources of apostolic tradition to which the faithful appealed — placing the council fathers alongside the apostles themselves in the chain of doctrinal authority.

## Historical Background

The First Council of Nicaea was convened in 325 CE at Constantine's summer palace in Nicaea, chosen for its accessibility to the western bishops and its pleasant climate. Between 250 and 318 bishops attended, though the exact number is disputed in antiquity. The council addressed the Arian controversy — whether Christ was of the same substance (*homoousios*) as the Father or merely of similar substance (*homoiousios*) — and produced the original Nicene Creed, condemning [[arius|Arius]] and his teachings. Twenty canons were also issued, covering church discipline, clerical conduct, and the date of Easter. The novel's setting in the mid-to-late fourth century places it during the long aftermath of Nicaea, when its creed was repeatedly challenged, revised, and reasserted before final victory at the Council of Constantinople in 381.
