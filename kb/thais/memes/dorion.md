## Overview

Dorion is an Epicurean philosopher in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), appearing primarily in the banquet scenes of Parts I and II. He represents the skeptical wing of the Epicurean tradition — not the cheerful hedonism of [[nicias|Nicias]] but a more melancholy philosophy that regards desire with suspicion and women as a source of trouble rather than pleasure. His interventions at the banquet drive much of the philosophical debate, and his interactions with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], [[nicias|Nicias]], and [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] illuminate the novel's central conflict between competing visions of the good life.

## Biography

Dorion first appears in Part I, during the philosophical gathering at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s house. When [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] arrives and begins to denounce the actors of [[antioch|Antioch]], Dorion challenges his moral authority, prompting [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] to retreat from argument to assertion: "Entends la vérité, Dorion, et reçois la lumière" (001-184). This exchange establishes the pattern of Dorion's role — the skeptic who questions, forcing the zealot to reveal the limits of his reasoning.

In Part II, Dorion reappears at the great banquet hosted by [[cotta|Cotta]], where he is listed among the philosophers with [[nicias|Nicias]], [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]], the poet [[callicrate|Callicrate]], and the young Chéréas (002-230). Throughout the banquet, Dorion debates the nature of virtue, the soul, and knowledge. He asks pointed questions — "Qu'est-ce que..." (002-250) — that expose the fragility of his opponents' positions. When told that philosophy of virtue is not dead in the world, he is corrected: "Tu te trompes, Dorion. La philosophie de la vertu n'est pas morte en ce monde" (002-267). As the banquet descends into drunkenness, Dorion becomes increasingly agitated, fidgeting in his philosopher's cloak (002-323), and finally refusing to hear more (002-328). The scene concludes with a vivid tableau: "[[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] pressait dans ses bras [[philina|Philina]] défaite. Dorion versait sur la gorge nue de [[drose|Drosé]]..." (002-334), showing the philosopher abandoning his detachment for sensual abandon.

## Character

Dorion embodies a specific strain of Epicureanism that is more cautious than celebratory. His master taught him that "le désir est redoutable" (001-178) — desire is formidable, fearsome, something to be managed rather than indulged. He keeps a leaf from Phaedra's myrtle at his bedside as a permanent warning against desire. This makes him an unlikely ally of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] on the surface — both agree that pleasure is dangerous — but they differ fundamentally in their response: Dorion manages desire through philosophical detachment, while [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] attempts to extirpate it through ascetic discipline.

France portrays Dorion with characteristic irony. For all his philosophical sophistication, Dorion ends the banquet pouring wine on a courtesan's bare throat. The gap between his doctrine and his behavior is precisely the point — philosophy, in France's depiction, is a beautiful mirror that reflects everything and changes nothing.

## Relationships

- **[[Nicias]]** — Dorion's fellow Epicurean, though [[nicias|Nicias]] represents the more serene, cheerful face of the school while Dorion inclines toward melancholy skepticism. The two debate together at the banquet.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Dorion challenges [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s moral certainty and exposes the authoritarian impulse beneath the abbot's rhetoric. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s "Entends la vérité, Dorion, et reçois la lumière" (001-184) is the novel's most compressed example of how religious zeal substitutes imperative for argument.
- **[[Zenothemis]]** — A fellow philosopher at the banquet who debates Dorion on the nature of spirit, virtue, and knowledge. Their exchanges structure much of the banquet's intellectual drama.
- **[[Philina]]** — The courtesan with whom Dorion is entangled in the final tableau of the banquet scene (002-334).

## Role in the Narrative

Dorion functions as the skeptical conscience of the novel's philosophical debates. His questions — about the nature of virtue, the reliability of knowledge, the status of women — are the questions the novel itself asks but refuses to answer definitively. His trajectory from philosophical detachment to drunken sensuality enacts France's central ironic proposition: that no philosophical system survives contact with human desire intact.
