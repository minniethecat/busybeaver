## Definition

Holiness (*la sainteté*) is the attribute of being sacred — set apart from the profane world and consecrated to God. In Christian theology, holiness is both a divine attribute (God alone is essentially holy) and a human vocation (the call to "be holy as I am holy"). In the world of the Desert Fathers, holiness was understood as the visible fruit of ascetic labor: the monk who had purified himself through fasting, prayer, and solitude was believed to radiate holiness that others could perceive and benefit from.

## In the Novel

Holiness enters the novel as a palpable force. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s cell is described as a place protected "par la vertu de sa sainteté" (001-011) — by the virtue of his holiness — suggesting that holiness operates almost as a physical barrier against evil. The same phrase recurs when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] encounters [[timocles|Timocles]]: "par la vertu de sa sainteté, il comprenait aisément le sens des choses cachées" (001-044) — by the virtue of his holiness, he easily understood the meaning of hidden things. Holiness is presented here as conferring spiritual discernment, the ability to read souls.

The novel's most critical treatment of holiness comes in Part III, when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] visits the community of holy women where [[thais|Thaïs]] has been placed. The narrator describes how "la sainteté de l'abbé embaume toutes les cellules" (003-055) — the holiness of the abbot perfumes all the cells. The olfactory metaphor is telling: holiness is presented as something one can smell, an atmosphere rather than an inward state. The suggestion — characteristically French — is that what passes for holiness may be partly theatrical, a fragrance that can be manufactured.

The novel ultimately distinguishes between two kinds of holiness. There is [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s holiness: achieved through visible rigor, admired by disciples, effective at casting out demons, yet hollow at its core — a holiness that cannot survive contact with genuine human love. And there is the holiness that [[thais|Thaïs]] stumbles into: unperformed, unwilled, arriving only after she has abandoned all pretension to it. The novel's quiet scandal is that the courtesan may be holier than the saint.

## Theological Background

In 4th-century [[egypt|Egypt]]ian Christianity, holiness was understood in strongly charismatic terms. The holy man or woman was believed to possess *parrhesia* (bold speech before God), the power of intercession, and often the gift of healing or exorcism. The *Life of Antony* by Athanasius established the template: the holy ascetic as spiritual athlete whose external discipline produces internal purity and supernatural power. But the tradition also preserved warnings against vainglory — the desert literature is full of stories of monks who achieved visible holiness only to fall into pride and delusion. France's portrayal of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] draws deeply on this cautionary tradition.
