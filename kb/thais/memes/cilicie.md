## Overview

Cilicie (Cilicia) — the coastal province of southeastern [[asia|Asia Minor]], birthplace of the Apostle Paul, a crossroads of trade between Anatolia and the Levant — enters *[[thais|Thaïs]]* through the humblest of portals: its beer. Twice in the novel, "la meilleure bière de Cilicie" and "la vraie bière de Cilicie" are invoked, not as objects of description but as items in a commercial catalogue. The recurrence of this single commodity — in the childhood tavern of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s father and, much later, on the shop signs of the burgeoning city around [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s pillar — is a quiet structural rhyme. Cilician beer links the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s origins to the ascetic metropolis that rises, paradoxically, around the most world-denying of monks.

## In the Novel

Cilicie first appears in the extended flashback of Part II, where the narrator recounts [[thais|Thaïs]]'s impoverished childhood. Her father kept a sailors' tavern near the Gate of the Moon in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]; there, amid the stench of fermented drinks and resinous skins, the sailors "demandant, en blasphémant les dieux, la meilleure bière de Cilicie" (002-031). Cilician beer was evidently a recognized brand in the eastern Mediterranean — a premium import that rough sailors called for by name. The detail is ethnographically precise: Cilicia Pedias, the fertile coastal plain around Tarsus, was known for its grain production, and Cilician beer was exported throughout the [[rome|Roma]]n world.

The beer reappears at the opposite end of the narrative arc. In Part III, after [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has ascended his pillar near the ruins of an old temple, a commercial city springs up around him — pilgrim hostels, fishmongers, barbers, and taverns whose signs, nailed to ancient pillars, advertise: "On vend ici du vin de grenades, du vin de figues et de la vraie bière de Cilicie" (003-112). The phrase "la vraie bière de Cilicie" — the *real* beer of Cilicia — implies a market with counterfeit goods and discerning customers. The commercial energy that surrounds the stylite's column is the same energy that animated the tavern of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s father; the world that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] fled has followed him into the desert.

> "Les cabaretiers transformaient en caves les salles souterraines et clouaient aux antiques piliers des enseignes surmontées de l'image du saint homme Paphnuce, et portant cette inscription en grec et en égyptien: On vend ici du vin de grenades, du vin de figues et de la vraie bière de Cilicie." (003-112)
