## Definition

Faith (*la foi*) in the Christian tradition is the virtue of belief in God and trust in divine revelation — traditionally counted as the first of the three theological virtues alongside hope and charity. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, Anatole France treats faith not as a settled possession but as a dynamic and contested territory: different characters understand faith differently, and the novel's dramatic arc is driven by the conflict between these understandings.

## In the Novel

Faith appears in the novel in three distinct registers. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] embodies militant, ascetic faith — the faith that "embrassa la foi du Calvaire et il adora Jésus crucifié" (001-015), embracing the faith of Calvary and adoring the crucified Christ. His faith is outwardly confident, marked by visible signs: "un bâton noueux, image de la foi chrétienne" (001-046), a knotted staff as the image of Christian faith with which he departs for [[alexandrie|Alexandria]].

[[nicias|Nicias]] represents a second register: the cultured skepticism of late antique philosophy. At the banquet, the guests debate the nature of virtue and belief with an ironic detachment that treats faith as one opinion among many. Their world is one where philosophical discourse has rendered certainty impossible.

[[thais|Thaïs]] embodies the third register: faith as genuine transformation. Her conversion scene shows a woman who, unlike [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], does not perform faith but is overtaken by it. The novel's structural irony is that the courtesan's faith proves more authentic than the ascetic's — [[thais|Thaïs]] progresses toward genuine belief while [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s faith unravels into obsession.

The narrator also employs *fois* in its quotidian sense ("parfois" — sometimes, "chaque fois" — each time), creating a subtle counterpoint between ordinary occurrence and theological conviction. Time and repetition, not dramatic conversion, mark most human experience — a quiet skepticism woven into the prose itself.

## Theological Background

In 4th-century Christianity, faith was understood primarily as *fides quae creditur* (the content of belief — right doctrine) as much as *fides qua creditur* (the act of believing). The Nicene Creed (325 CE) had recently established orthodoxy, and the Arian controversy made right belief a matter of imperial as well as ecclesiastical concern. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s world is one where faith must be defended against heresy — he is "instruit dans les choses de la foi" (001-090), instructed in the things of faith. France's 19th-century perspective introduces a historical irony: the faith [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] fights to defend will not survive the century unchanged.

## Key Passages

> "Il embrassa la foi du Calvaire et il adora Jésus crucifié." [001-015]

> *"He embraced the faith of Calvary and adored the crucified Jesus."*

> "Paphnuce, ayant pris un bâton noueux, image de la foi chrétienne, sortit de sa cellule." [001-046]

> *"Paphnuce, having taken a knotted staff, image of the Christian faith, left his cell."*

> "Par la connaissance qu'il avait des choses de la foi, il comprit que la grâce de Dieu n'était pas sur le visage de Timoclès." [001-090]

> *"Through the knowledge he had of the things of faith, he understood that the grace of God was not on the face of Timocles."*
