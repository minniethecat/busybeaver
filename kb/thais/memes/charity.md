## Definition

Charity (*caritas*, *charité*, *agape*) is the theological virtue of supernatural love — love of God for God's own sake and love of neighbor for the sake of God. It is traditionally counted as the greatest of the three theological virtues.

Paul's First Letter to the Corinthians declares charity superior to all spiritual gifts. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, charity functions less as an explicit theme than as a diagnostic absence — the novel's most powerful critique of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is not that he sins but that he does not love.

## In the Novel

Charity is present in the novel primarily through its absence. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s mission to save [[thais|Thaïs]] is framed as an act of spiritual rescue, but the narrator reveals that it contains remarkably little warmth. He approaches [[thais|Thaïs]] not with compassion but with horror at a soul in peril.

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] actively avoids human connection, "craignant que son coeur ne s'amollit à la vue des hommes" (001-046). He has trained himself to see softness of heart as a danger rather than a virtue. He possesses every ascetic achievement yet lacks the one virtue that renders all others valid.

By contrast, the word *charité* appears in the context of [[thais|Thaïs]] herself. The people of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] bless her "comme d'une charité céleste" (002-160) — as a heavenly charity. The description credits the courtesan with the very virtue the ascetic lacks.

[[nicias|Nicias]] speaks of "véritable et divine charité" (002-171) and declares "Je brûle du feu de la charité" (002-192). These declarations complicate any simple assignment of virtue to the religious and vice to the worldly.

The novel's climactic demonstration of charity's absence comes when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] stands over the dying [[thais|Thaïs]] and cannot summon a word of comfort. In the final movement, he experiences: "Une tendresse inconnue amollissait son âme" (003-273). The verb *amollir*, which he had spent a lifetime avoiding, returns as the novel's last word on his spiritual state.

The minor characters who show genuine kindness — [[albine|Albina]], who shelters [[thais|Thaïs]]; Palemon, who counsels moderation — embody charity without claiming spiritual heroism. The novel suggests that charity flourishes in ordinary kindnesses, not dramatic ascetic gestures.

## Theological Background

The primacy of charity was established in the New Testament — Paul's hymn to charity (1 Corinthians 13), John's declaration that "God is love" (1 John 4:8), and Christ's summary of the Law as love of God and neighbor (Matthew 22:37–40).

Augustine would later make charity the hermeneutic key to all of scripture. The Desert Father tradition preserved this emphasis: the *Apophthegmata Patrum* contain numerous sayings that privilege charity over fasting. Palemon's advice to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] — moderation rather than extremes — echoes this tradition.
