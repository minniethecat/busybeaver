## Overview

Jerusalem (Jérusalem) appears in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890) not as a geographical location but as a spiritual ideal — "the heavenly Jerusalem," the celestial city of Christian eschatology. It is invoked twice in the novel, both times as a figure of transcendent beauty and divine fulfillment, standing in contrast to the desert of earthly existence.

## In the Novel

The first mention of Jerusalem occurs early in Part I (*Le Lotus*), during a lyrical vision of paradise. An anonymous voice — whether angelic or human is left ambiguous — exclaims: "A balsam rose-bush in your heavenly Jerusalem!" (001-094). The phrase evokes the lush, perfumed garden of the celestial city, a counter-image to the arid wastes of the [[thebaide|Thébaïde]] in which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has spent his years of mortification.

The second appearance deepens the personal significance of the image. [[antoine|Saint Anthony]], the aged patriarch of the desert, speaks of a vision: "An image appeared to me in my Jerusalem of the sands, and I knew that..." (002-188). Here, Jerusalem is deliberately relocated to the desert — "my Jerusalem of the sands" — reflecting the desert father's conviction that the holy city can be found not in Palestine but in the purified heart. The phrase captures the spiritual geography of desert monasticism: the true Jerusalem is wherever the saint stands.

## Symbolic Significance

In the theology of the fourth-century Church, the heavenly Jerusalem represented the ultimate destination of the faithful — the city of God described in the Book of Revelation, where there is no temple because God himself is its temple. France's use of the image in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* draws on this tradition while adapting it to the novel's distinctive spiritual landscape. The "Jerusalem of the sands" that Anthony speaks of is a monastic reinterpretation: the desert is not a place of exile from the holy city but its antechamber, and the purified soul carries Jerusalem within it.

The contrast between the "heavenly Jerusalem" of 001-094 and the "Jerusalem of the sands" of 002-188 maps onto the novel's central tension between the transcendent ideal and the earthly reality. [[thais|Thaïs]], at the novel's end, achieves the first — the celestial city of salvation. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], for all his ascetic striving, remains trapped in the second — the Jerusalem of sand, the city of his own pride.
