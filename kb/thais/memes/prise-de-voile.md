## Overview

The Prise de Voile — the Taking of the Veil — marks [[thais|Thaïs]]'s formal entry into the monastic life, the ritual culmination of her conversion by [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]. After their journey from [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] to the desert, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] delivers [[thais|Thaïs]] to the convent governed by [[albine|Albina]], a woman of imperial birth who exchanged the purple of the Caesars for the rough wool of a nun. The ceremony transforms [[thais|Thaïs]] from courtesan to consecrated virgin, and it represents the moment when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] believes his mission is complete — the last moment of certainty in his spiritual life before obsession begins its slow work.

## Narrative

**The Journey to the Convent**: Following her conversion in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] escorts [[thais|Thaïs]] to the desert convent "at twelve hours from [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], toward the Occident, not far from the sea" (002-371). He describes it as a place where women live in poverty and prayer, a house of salvation where [[albine|Albina]] will receive her.

**[[albine|Albina]]'s Introduction**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] presents [[albine|Albina]] to [[thais|Thaïs]] in exalted terms:

> "Albine qui, née dans la pourpre, revêtit la bure et, fille des maîtres du monde, s'éleva au rang de servante de Jésus-Christ. Elle sera ta mère." (002-374)

The abbess's biography is itself a conversion narrative in miniature: she was the niece of the emperor Carus, born to imperial power, yet chose the coarse wool of monastic poverty over the purple of royalty. Her authority over [[thais|Thaïs]] derives not from office but from this radical renunciation.

**The Ritual**: The ceremony involves the symbolic stripping of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s former identity. The novel does not provide a blow-by-blow liturgical description; rather, it focuses on the emotional and symbolic weight of the transition. [[thais|Thaïs]] exchanges her jewels and silks for the rough woolen veil — the *bure* — and is enclosed in a narrow cell with only bread, water, and a cross. The veiling is both a death (to the world, to her former self) and a birth (into the monastic community, into her new identity as a bride of Christ).

**The Community**: When [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] and [[thais|Thaïs]] arrive at the convent, they see women engaged in the ordinary labor of monastic life — baking bread, preparing vegetables, spinning wool — illuminated by a light that descends "like a smile from heaven" (003-039). The image suggests the peace and order of the regulated religious life into which [[thais|Thaïs]] is being initiated.

## Participants

- **[[Thais]]** — The convert who receives the veil. Her consent is marked by exhaustion rather than enthusiasm — she is weary of the world she has conquered.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — The agent of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s conversion who delivers her to the convent. He believes his work is complete; it is, in fact, only beginning.
- **[[Albina]]** — The abbess, former imperial princess, who receives [[thais|Thaïs]] into the monastic community. Her own life story is the template for [[thais|Thaïs]]'s transformation.

## Consequences

The Prise de Voile is the hinge between the novel's two major arcs. For [[thais|Thaïs]], it begins a three-year process of genuine spiritual transformation that culminates in her saintly death. For [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], it is the beginning of his undoing: having removed [[thais|Thaïs]] from the world, he finds he cannot remove her from his thoughts. The enclosed woman in the desert cell becomes an obsession more powerful than the visible courtesan in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] ever was. The veil he helped place upon her becomes, in his imagination, the most alluring of garments.

## Themes

The veiling ceremony dramatizes the paradox of Christian renunciation: the world is rejected only to return with greater power in the imagination. The veil simultaneously conceals and eroticizes — it makes [[thais|Thaïs]] inaccessible, which makes her irresistible to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]. France's irony is that the ritual of chastity becomes the catalyst of desire.
