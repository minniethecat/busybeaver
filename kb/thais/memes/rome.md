## Overview

Rome (Rome) — the ancient capital of the Roman Empire, situated on the Tiber River — appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* as the distant center of imperial and ecclesiastical authority. In the fourth century, Rome was no longer the administrative capital of the Empire (the emperors resided in Constantinople, Milan, and other cities), but it retained immense symbolic prestige as the seat of the Roman Church and the traditional heart of Roman civilization. The novel references Rome in both capacities: as the source of the Caesar's authority and as one of the great Christian sees where disciples of Christ had taken root.

## In the Novel

Rome appears twice in the narrative, each time in connection with the spread of Christianity and the exercise of imperial power. During the philosophical banquet, the guests discuss the rapid expansion of the Christian faith in the Empire's great cities: "disciples in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], in Rome, and in Constantinople" (002-267). Rome is named among the three principal centers of the early Church — alongside [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], the theological powerhouse of the East, and Constantinople, the new imperial capital — confirming its status as the apostolic see of the West.

The second reference belongs to Part III, when the narrator describes the immense crowd that gathered to witness [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] on his pillar: "ambassadors, and the Caesar of Rome, the divine Constant, who supported the orthodox with all the force of his piety and power" (003-149). The emperor Constantius II (r. 337–361), a supporter of Arianism rather than orthodoxy, is here positioned as the ultimate earthly power, his authority radiating from Rome outward. Even on a stylite's pillar in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian desert, the political reach of Rome is felt.

## Historical Background

By the mid-fourth century, Rome had ceased to be the regular residence of the emperors, who preferred strategically located capitals closer to the frontiers. However, the city remained the symbolic center of the Roman world, its Senate still the pinnacle of aristocratic prestige and its bishop — increasingly recognized as the pope — exercising growing authority over the western Church. The "Caesar of Rome" referenced in 003-149 is Constantius II, the son of Constantine the Great, who ruled the eastern half of the Empire from Constantinople and supported Arian bishops against the adherents of the Nicene Creed. His long reign (337–361) spanned most of the period in which the novel's events are set, though Anatole France's novel compresses and fictionalizes the chronology of political and ecclesiastical history.
