## Overview

Eucrite is the representative of Stoic philosophy in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). Introduced at the banquet scene as "le dernier des stoïciens" — the last of the Stoics (002-266) — he embodies the austere dignity of a dying philosophical tradition. Unlike the skeptical [[dorion|Dorion]] or the serene [[nicias|Nicias]], Eucrite defends a coherent ethical system grounded in effort and conformity to cosmic reason. His death, briefly noted in Part III, is presented as the Stoic ideal realized: detachment from life achieved with quiet grace.

## Biography

Eucrite first appears at the banquet in Part II, where he is welcomed by the guests with respectful salutations (002-263). He responds while stroking his beard (002-264), a gesture that signals his philosophical gravity. The prefect [[cotta|Cotta]] introduces him with ceremonial praise, and Eucrite immediately engages in the philosophical disputation that forms the core of the banquet scene.

Eucrite's contributions to the debate center on the nature of virtue. He argues that virtue "consiste seulement dans l'effort, et dans cette tension par laquelle..." (002-268) — virtue consists only in effort, in that tension by which the soul strives toward the good. This is orthodox Stoicism: virtue is not a state but an activity, not a possession but a striving. When challenged on the subject of freedom and the body, Eucrite maintains his composure: "Comment peut-on être libre, Eucrite, quand on a un corps?" (002-342). His answer, not directly quoted but implied by his conduct, is that freedom consists in the right use of impressions — the classic Stoic doctrine.

As the banquet dissolves into disorder, Eucrite rises and places his arm on the shoulder of [[nicias|Nicias]] (002-334), a gesture of philosophical solidarity amid the chaos. He expresses himself with such wisdom that the other guests fall silent (002-344). His final words before departing urge detachment: "Détache-toi de la vie, Eucrite, comme l'olive mûre qui tombe..." (002-353). The narrator notes that these words penetrated the heart of the sage, and Eucrite entered into repose (002-355). In Part III, his death is mentioned briefly: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] encounters the monk who closed the philosopher Eucrite's eyes (003-005), confirming that Eucrite achieved the tranquil end his philosophy prescribed.

## Character

Eucrite is portrayed with genuine respect — perhaps more than any other philosopher in the novel. Where [[dorion|Dorion]]'s skepticism collapses into sensuality and [[nicias|Nicias]]'s Epicureanism is presented as charming but shallow, Eucrite's Stoicism proves capable of sustaining him through the banquet's chaos and into a dignified death. His key argument — that virtue is effort, not achievement — is one France seems to endorse, or at least refuses to mock.

France's irony is gentler with Eucrite than with the other philosophers. The epithet "the last of the Stoics" carries a genuine pathos: Eucrite represents a tradition that, by the 4th century, was being eclipsed by Christianity. His death, overseen by a Christian monk, marks the historical transition from classical philosophy to Christian asceticism — but France stages it as a peaceful passing, not a defeat.

## Relationships

- **[[Nicias]]** — The host of the banquet. Eucrite places his arm on [[nicias|Nicias]]'s shoulder in a gesture of philosophical kinship (002-334), suggesting mutual respect between the Stoic and the Epicurean.
- **[[Dorion]]** — Fellow philosopher at the banquet. Their debates on virtue, freedom, and the body structure much of the intellectual drama of Part II.
- **[[Cotta]]** — The [[rome|Roma]]n prefect who introduces Eucrite with ceremonial praise and engages him in philosophical dialogue.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Though they do not interact directly at length, Eucrite's Stoic detachment offers a pointed contrast to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s tormented asceticism. The monk who closes Eucrite's eyes (003-005) symbolizes the passing of the classical philosophical tradition into Christian hands.

## Role in the Narrative

Eucrite serves as the novel's most sympathetic portrait of classical philosophy. His Stoicism — with its emphasis on effort, detachment, and acceptance of natural limits — offers a genuine alternative to both Christian asceticism and Epicurean hedonism. His death scene, described with quiet solemnity, suggests that France saw something worth preserving in the Stoic tradition even as he chronicled its historical eclipse.
