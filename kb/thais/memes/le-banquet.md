## Overview

Le Banquet is the central set-piece of Part II of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, a philosophical dinner party hosted by the wealthy Epicurean [[nicias|Nicias]] in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. Modeled loosely on Plato's *Symposium* but filtered through fin-de-siècle irony, the banquet brings together representatives of the major philosophical schools of late antiquity — Stoicism, Epicureanism, Skepticism, and Christianity — for a debate that exposes the inadequacy of all systems before the mystery of human desire. [[thais|Thaïs]]'s late entrance transforms the intellectual contest into something more primal.

## Narrative

The banquet takes place in the dining hall of [[nicias|Nicias]]'s [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n villa. The guests arrive in sequence: [[eucrite|Eucrite]] the Stoic, [[dorion|Dorion]] the Skeptic, [[cotta|Cotta]] the [[rome|Roma]]n magistrate, [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] the Platonist, and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] the Christian abbot. [[nicias|Nicias]] presides with the amused detachment of a man who has sampled every philosophy and committed to none.

The conversation ranges over the nature of virtue, the reality of hell, the legitimacy of pleasure, and the possibility of knowledge. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] denounces the philosophers' speculations as demonic illusions. [[dorion|Dorion]] responds with skeptical calm. [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]] expounds a mystic Neoplatonism in which Greek myths become allegories of spiritual truth. [[nicias|Nicias]], the host, offers the banquet's implicit thesis: "la volupté est le bien suprême" — pleasure is the supreme good.

[[thais|Thaïs]]'s entrance transforms the scene. The abstract debate collapses before her physical presence: "[[thais|Thaïs]] entra dans la salle du banquet" (002-217). The philosophers, who moments before had discoursed on the good with such confidence, fall silent. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] alone remains fixed on his mission, seeing in her beauty not pleasure but a soul to be captured for God.

The banquet ends in chaos. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] denounces the gathering as "ce banquet des démons" (002-286) — this banquet of demons — and departs, leaving the philosophers to their wine and their perplexity.

## Consequences

The banquet marks the point of no return for [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]. His confrontation with [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n intellectual culture — urbane, self-confident, indifferent to his certainties — hardens his resolve while simultaneously exposing its fragility. The philosophers' arguments do not convince him, but they lodge in his mind like splinters, resurfacing in his later torments.

For [[thais|Thaïs]], the banquet is her last appearance as a public figure before her conversion. Her entrance at its climax — the courtesan as philosophical event — crystallizes the novel's central irony: that beauty, not argument, is what moves the soul.

## Themes

The banquet dramatizes the conflict between reason and revelation, philosophy and faith. Each philosophical system offers a coherent account of the good life, yet none can account for the actual mess of human desire. France's irony is that the most dogmatic character — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] — is the one most consumed by the very passions he condemns.

The Platonic form of the banquet is itself parodic. Where Plato's *Symposium* ascends from physical beauty to the Form of the Good, France's banquet descends from intellectual discourse to erotic chaos. The truth, France suggests, is not at the top of the ladder but in the muddle at its base.
