## Overview

Drosé is one of the courtesans present at [[cotta|Cotta]]'s banquet in Part II of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). Paired with her companion [[philina|Philina]], she provides a sensual counterpoint to the evening's philosophical debates. Unlike the silent, observant [[thais|Thaïs]], Drosé is vocal, spirited, and unafraid to interrupt the philosophers with cries of indignation. Her outburst at the banquet — "Ne dis-tu pas que les dieux t'aiment?" (002-257) — marks the moment when the body's claims erupt into the philosophers' abstractions.

## Biography

Drosé enters the narrative at the banquet alongside [[philina|Philina]]: "et près d'eux [[philina|Philina]] avec Drosé, qu'il faut louer grandement" (002-230). Together, the two women devour [[thais|Thaïs]] with their eyes, fascinated by her presence (002-235), and Drosé immediately demands to know the identity of [[thais|Thaïs]]'s new lover (002-237). [[philina|Philina]] silences her by pressing a finger to Drosé's lips (002-238).

The philosophical disputations continue, but Drosé interrupts them with a piercing cry (002-255). Provoked by the debates, she demands to know whether the gods love her — a question that cuts through the philosophers' abstractions with raw personal urgency (002-257). When answered, she flies into a great rage (002-258), though her companion urges her to continue speaking regardless: "Quoi que tu dises, il faut t'écouter" (002-261).

As the banquet dissolves into sensual chaos in its final hours, the narrator notes a shift in the seating: an Epicurean moves closer to Drosé, who summons him with her gaze (002-328). In the bacchanalian climax, wine is poured over Drosé's bare throat — droplets rolling down in a scene that merges consumption, eroticism, and ritual (002-334).

## Character

Drosé represents the irrepressible claims of the body and the emotions in a world of philosophical abstraction. While the men debate the nature of the soul, truth, and God, Drosé wants to know one thing: do the gods love *her*? Her question is profoundly personal, and her rage when it is not satisfactorily answered is the rage of a woman who refuses to be an abstraction.

France treats Drosé with a mixture of comedy and sympathy. Her outbursts are disruptive but honest; her sensuality is neither condemned nor idealized. She is what she is — a courtesan who has come to a banquet and finds the philosophers tiresome — and the novel lets her be that without moralizing.

## Relationships

- **[[Philina]]** — Drosé's constant companion at the banquet. [[philina|Philina]] alternately restrains and encourages Drosé, pressing a finger to her lips when she is too forward, then urging her to speak freely when the philosophers provoke her.
- **[[Thaïs]]** — Together with [[philina|Philina]], Drosé stares at [[thais|Thaïs]] with fascination. Drosé's curiosity about [[thais|Thaïs]]'s new lover suggests the competitive attention of the courtesan world.
- **[[Cotta]]** — As host, [[cotta|Cotta]] presides over the banquet where Drosé's outbursts occur. His [[rome|Roma]]n dignity contrasts with her unbridled expressiveness.

## Role in the Narrative

Drosé has a small but structurally important role: she punctures the philosophical pretensions of the banquet. When the men debate abstractions, Drosé introduces the body — her body, her desires, her anger. Her wine-soaked throat at the banquet's end is the novel's emblem of pagan sensuality at its most uninhibited, the world against which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s asceticism defines itself.
