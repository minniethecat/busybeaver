## Definition

Love (*l'amour*) in Christian thought is not one thing but (at least) two: *agape* — self-giving, divine love that seeks the good of the other — and *eros* — desiring love that seeks union with the beloved. The Desert Fathers attempted to sublimate eros into agape through ascetic discipline. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s tragedy is that he succeeds only in deceiving himself about which kind of love drives him.

## In the Novel

Love permeates *[[thais|Thaïs]]* from its opening — the old men who visit [[thais|Thaïs]] "pleins d'amour" (001-017) — to the novel's devastating conclusion. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] frames his mission to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] as an act of divine love: he will rescue [[thais|Thaïs]]'s soul "pour l'amour de Dieu" (001-046). Every decision he makes is justified by love — love of God, love of souls, love of the truth.

The novel relentlessly exposes the self-deception in this framing. [[nicias|Nicias]], with characteristic lucidity, observes that love and philosophy are never far apart in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s temperament: his old friend speaks "tout ensemble d'amour et de philosophie" — all at once of love and philosophy (001-162). The implication is clear: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s intellectual passion and his erotic passion are manifestations of the same drive.

The reversal is complete when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], having locked [[thais|Thaïs]] in a tomb to die to the world, finds himself tormented by desire. The ascetic who renounced "les immondes amours de la terre" — the foul loves of the earth (001-146) — discovers that renunciation did not eliminate love but concentrated it. His final confession to [[thais|Thaïs]] is not theological but erotic: he loves her, not God, and never did.

The novel's bitterest irony is that love does save [[thais|Thaïs]] — but not [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s love. The love that transforms her is the divine love she glimpses in her cell, a love [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] preached but could not embody. "L'amour est la cause de nos maux les plus cuisants" — love is the cause of our most searing pains (001-178).

## Theological Background

The *agape*/eros distinction was central to patristic thought. Augustine's *Confessions* traces the redirection of eros toward God; the desert tradition sought to eliminate eros entirely through *apatheia*. France's novel, written after Freud but set before Augustine, exposes the psychological impossibility of this project: eros cannot be killed, only disguised.
