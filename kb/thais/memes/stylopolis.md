## Overview

Stylopolis — literally "City of the Column" or "City of the Stylites" — is the name given to the settlement that spontaneously arises around the pillar where [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] practices his stylite asceticism. In a striking episode of the novel's third part, what begins as one man's extreme renunciation of the world becomes, paradoxically, the foundation of a new community. The name Stylopolis itself, bestowed by "les Stylopolitains" (003-128), captures the logic of anchorite fame: the desert saint who flees society ends up attracting it.

## In the Novel

Stylopolis appears in Part III ("L'Euphorbe"), after [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], tormented by his earthly desires, ascends a column to live as a stylite. The narrator reports that "the news was true" (003-128): the settlement has grown to the point that it attracts the attention of the [[rome|Roma]]n authorities. The elderly [[cotta|Cotta]], on an official mission to inspect the [[nile|Nile]]'s canals and navigation, requests to see "the stylite and the new city, to which they gave the name of Stylopolis."

> "La nouvelle était vraie. Le vieux Cotta, parti pour inspecter les canaux et la navigation du Nil, avait témoigné à plusieurs reprises le désir de voir le stylite et la nouvelle ville, à laquelle on donnait le nom de Stylopolis." (003-128)

[[cotta|Cotta]] arrives in a gilded galley with purple sails, accompanied by a flotilla and his retinue — a secretary with tablets and [[aristee|Aristée]], his physician (003-128). The scene is rich with ironic juxtaposition: the [[rome|Roma]]n official's worldly pomp confronts the stylite's radical asceticism. [[cotta|Cotta]] is intellectually curious — "d'un esprit naturellement curieux" — and, having observed many things in his long travels, he "meditated on writing, after the Punic history, a book of the singular things he had seen" (003-129).

After his visit, [[cotta|Cotta]] remarks that he has "rarely seen anything more curious" and expresses the wish that "one day a skilled writer would recount the foundation of Stylopolis" (003-148). He then departs to continue his canal inspection, inviting [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] to supper in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] should he ever descend — an invitation heavy with dramatic irony, given [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s spiritual trajectory.

> "J'ai rarement vu quelque chose de plus curieux que ce que je vois en ce moment, reprit Cotta, et je souhaite qu'un jour un écrivain habile raconte la fondation de Stylopolis." (003-148)

## Historical Background

The historical phenomenon of stylite asceticism — living atop a pillar (Greek: *stylos*) — originated with Simeon Stylites in fifth-century [[syria|Syria]], several decades after the novel's fourth-century setting. Anatole France's inclusion of a stylite episode is thus an anachronism, consciously or not. The settlement pattern, however, is historically attested: stylite saints attracted pilgrims, disciples, and merchants, creating de facto communities around their pillars. The name "Stylopolis" is France's invention, a literary condensation of this historical pattern into a single, vividly named place.
