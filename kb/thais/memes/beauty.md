## Definition

Beauty (*la beauté*) in the world of late antiquity carried a double valence: it was, in the Platonic tradition, the visible radiance of the Good — yet in the ascetic tradition of the Desert Fathers, it was the primary instrument of temptation. *[[thais|Thaïs]]* exploits this ambiguity to its fullest, making beauty the novel's most contested value.

## In the Novel

Beauty is introduced as a theological problem before it becomes a narrative one. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] knows from his desert training that demons "se revêtent parfois d'une beauté apparente qui empêche de discerner leur nature véritable" — sometimes clothe themselves in an apparent beauty that prevents discernment of their true nature (001-006). Beauty deceives; it is the signature of the demonic.

[[thais|Thaïs]] embodies this paradox. She is introduced as "une comédienne d'une grande beauté" — an actress of great beauty (001-017), and her beauty is simultaneously her power, her profession, and her spiritual peril. The old men who come "pleins d'amour" to hang flowers at her door are captives of beauty's spell. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s own undoing begins when he cannot stop seeing her — when beauty, which he trained himself to despise, becomes the object of his obsession.

[[nicias|Nicias]] provides the counter-argument: "la beauté est ce qu'il y a de plus puissant au monde" — beauty is the most powerful thing in the world (001-144). For [[nicias|Nicias]], beauty justifies itself; it needs no theological permission. The novel refuses to resolve this debate. Beauty destroys [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], redeems [[thais|Thaïs]], and leaves the reader suspended between the ascetic's horror and the philosopher's admiration.

France deepens the theme through aesthetic beauty: the novel repeatedly frames scenes with painterly attention to light, colour, and form. "Sous le frisson tragique de la beauté" — under the tragic thrill of beauty (002-009) — the narrator describes the spectacle of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], aligning the reader's gaze with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s reluctant fascination.

## Philosophical Context

The Platonic tradition held that earthly beauty was a shadow of the Form of Beauty, capable of leading the soul upward toward the divine. Plotinus developed this into a full-scale mystical aesthetics. The desert ascetics inverted this: beauty was the enemy of the soul because it chained desire to the body. France stages this conflict without resolving it, making beauty the novel's central ambiguity.
