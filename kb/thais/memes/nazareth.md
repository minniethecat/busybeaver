## Overview

Nazareth, the modest Galilean town of Jesus's childhood, enters *[[thais|Thaïs]]* not as a geographic setting but as a christological epithet: "Jésus de Nazareth." The formula appears twice, each time in a passage of striking visual description, and each time connecting the figure of Christ to the novel's broader tension between worldly beauty and spiritual renunciation. Anatole France, known for his sceptical treatment of Christianity, nevertheless draws on the name of Nazareth with the evocative power it held for his late-nineteenth-century readers.

## In the Novel

Nazareth first appears in an ekphrastic passage describing the mural [[thais|Thaïs]] contemplates in the Christian oratory. Among the painted figures, "[[thais|Thaïs]], among these figures, recognized Jesus of Nazareth by the anemones that flowered at his feet" (002-065). The detail is precise and poetic: the anemones serve as an iconographic marker, a visual signature distinguishing Christ from the other painted figures. The setting is the oratory where Bishop [[vivantius|Vivantius]] presides, a scene rich with the material culture of early Christian art.

The second occurrence is in the dialogue at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet, where the speaker argues for the primacy of sentiment over reason:

> "Ce n'est pas sans raison qu'on représente quelquefois Apollon Citharède, et Jésus de Nazareth, vêtus comme des femmes, d'une robe flottante." (002-275)

Here Nazareth appears in a startling comparison: Apollo the Citharode and Jesus of Nazareth are depicted similarly, "dressed like women, in a flowing robe." The observation reflects a late-antique aesthetic sensibility — the androgynous representation of divine figures — and folds Christ into the syncretic visual culture of the [[hell|Hell]]enistic Mediterranean. The juxtaposition of Apollo and Jesus, both robed, both beautiful, both objects of devotion, is characteristic of France's ironic, comparatist gaze.

> "Thaïs, parmi ces figures, reconnut Jésus de Nazareth à ce que des anémones fleurissaient à ses pieds." (002-065)

## Geography

Nazareth was a small Jewish village in Lower Galilee, unmentioned in the Hebrew Bible or Josephus, its obscurity in the first century making the epithet "Jesus of Nazareth" a mark of humble origins. Located in the hills southeast of Sepphoris, it lay away from the major trade routes, its population likely numbering only a few hundred in the first century CE.
