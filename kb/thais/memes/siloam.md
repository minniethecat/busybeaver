## Overview

The Fountain of Siloé (Siloam) — the freshwater pool in ancient [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] fed by the Gihon Spring through Hezekiah's Tunnel — appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* as a potent metaphor for spiritual purification. Invoked twice, both times in connection with [[thais|Thaïs]]'s conversion, the Siloam image bridges the novel's ascetic theology and its biblical substrate. The fountain of Siloé becomes, in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s rhetoric, a figure for the cleansing of the soul: what the pool's waters do for the body, divine grace does for the sinner.

## In the Novel

### Paphnuce's Plea (002-179)

The primary evocation of Siloé occurs in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s impassioned address to [[thais|Thaïs]] during his attempt to convert her. After [[thais|Thaïs]] speaks of the glory she finds in her life of pleasure, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] counters with a rhetorical crescendo:

> "Qui m'inspirera des discours embrasés pour que tu fondes comme la cire à mon souffle, ô femme, et que les doigts de mes désirs puissent te modeler à leur gré? Quelle vertu te livrera à moi, ô la plus chère des âmes, pour que l'esprit qui m'anime, m'animant une seconde fois, crée de toi une nouvelle créature et que du fond de mon coeur jaillisse une fontaine de Siloé, dans laquelle tu retrouves, en te baignant, ta pureté première?" (002-179)

The image is carefully constructed: from the depth of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s heart, a "fountain of Siloé" would spring forth, and [[thais|Thaïs]], bathing in it, would recover her original purity. The metaphor draws on the biblical Siloam's association with healing (John 9:7, where Jesus sends the blind man to wash in Siloam) and transforms it into an image of baptismal regeneration — a spiritual cleansing that restores an Edenic innocence.

### Echo (003-120)

The image returns, in abbreviated form, near the novel's end. A character remarks simply: "We have just seen another fountain of Siloé" (003-120). The brevity of the echo underlines how the metaphor has become part of the community's shared vocabulary — a shorthand for transformative spiritual experience.

## Biblical Background

The Pool of Siloam (Hebrew: *Shiloah*, Greek: *Silōam*) was a rock-cut reservoir in the southern part of ancient [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], fed by the Siloam Tunnel constructed under King Hezekiah (2 Kings 20:20). In the New Testament, it is the site where Jesus heals a man born blind, instructing him: "Go, wash in the pool of Siloam" (John 9:7). This association with healing and illumination made Siloam a natural metaphor for baptism and spiritual cleansing in early Christian discourse — the tradition France draws on in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s speech.
