## Overview

Nicias is the principal representative of Epicurean philosophy in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). A wealthy [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n who emerges from the bath attended by slave-women, he embodies the cultivated hedonism that the ascetic [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has renounced. Nicias serves as both [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s oldest friend and the former lover of [[thais|Thaïs]], positioning him at the intersection of the novel's central relationships. His amused, tolerant worldview — "je ne soupçonne point le mal" (001-141) — provides the philosophical counterweight to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s zeal, and his refusal to judge [[thais|Thaïs]] marks one of the novel's most humane moments.

## Biography

Nicias lives in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] as a man of considerable wealth and refined taste. When [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] arrives in the city after years of desert solitude, he seeks out Nicias as his first contact with the world he left behind. Nicias receives him with genuine warmth, emerging from his bath attended by two beautiful slave-women (001-123), and offers [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] hospitality in the form of cushions, fruit, and wine.

The conversation that follows between the two old friends reveals Nicias's philosophical commitments. He lives by pleasure, but not crudely — his Epicureanism is informed by the Greek tradition, and he speaks of Venus as a "puissante déesse" (001-147), a powerful goddess whose domain includes the beauty of [[thais|Thaïs]]. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s demand that Nicias lead him to [[thais|Thaïs]] so he might save her soul provokes Nicias's gentle skepticism. He does not believe in the sin [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sees everywhere; for Nicias, [[thais|Thaïs]] is simply beautiful, and the world is simply what it is.

After this encounter, Nicias remains largely in the background, but his presence haunts the narrative. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] later sees a vision of Nicias's shadow smiling (001-162), and in Part II, [[thais|Thaïs]] reflects that Nicias was among those who desired her (002-138). A marble goat — a gift from Nicias — stands in [[thais|Thaïs]]'s house (002-161), a silent reminder of the world she has left. Nicias also appears in the banquet scene among the philosophers debating the nature of the soul (002-212).

## Character

Nicias is characterized by a quality rare in the novel: serenity. Unlike [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], who is tormented by visions of sin, or [[thais|Thaïs]], who wearies of her own pleasures, Nicias seems at peace with himself and his philosophy. He does not preach; he lives. His response to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s apocalyptic warnings is neither hostile nor dismissive, but affectionate — he accompanies his friend to the threshold and embraces him before they part (001-149).

France uses Nicias to articulate a genuinely attractive alternative to Christian asceticism. Nicias is not a villain or a fool; he is intelligent, generous, and perceptive. He warns [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] against offending Venus not out of superstition but from a sense that beauty deserves reverence. His final appearance — among the philosophers at the banquet — shows him as part of a broader intellectual world that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has rejected and cannot understand.

## Relationships

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Nicias's oldest friend from before [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s retreat to the desert. Their reunion in Part I is the novel's first sustained philosophical dialogue, in which Nicias's Epicureanism and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s ascetic Christianity confront each other with mutual respect but irreconcilable premises.
- **[[Thaïs]]** — Nicias was once [[thais|Thaïs]]'s lover (001-144), and he speaks of her beauty without possessiveness or regret. His gift of a marble goat remains in her house as an emblem of their past intimacy. Unlike [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], Nicias does not seek to change [[thais|Thaïs]] — he accepts her as she is.
- **[[Dorion]]** — A fellow philosopher at the banquet, with whom Nicias debates the nature of virtue and knowledge.
- **[[Zenothemis]]** — Another participant in the banquet's philosophical disputations.
- **[[Cotta]]** — The [[rome|Roma]]n prefect who hosts the gathering where Nicias and other philosophers debate.

## Role in the Narrative

Nicias functions as the novel's moral touchstone. In a world divided between [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s fanatical renunciation and [[thais|Thaïs]]'s exhausted sensuality, Nicias offers a third way: the moderate, intelligent enjoyment of life without metaphysical anguish. His refusal to condemn [[thais|Thaïs]] — "elle est belle" (001-144) — stands as the novel's quiet rebuke to the cruelty that sometimes accompanies religious zeal.

His role is structurally concentrated in Part I, where his dialogue with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sets the philosophical stakes of the entire narrative. His subsequent appearances are brief but thematically significant, reminding the reader of the world of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n refinement that [[thais|Thaïs]] has abandoned and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] can never reclaim.

## Themes

Nicias embodies the Epicurean ideal of *ataraxia* — tranquility achieved through the wise management of desire rather than its extirpation. Where [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sees sin, Nicias sees nature; where [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] demands conversion, Nicias offers acceptance. France's portrayal is notably sympathetic: Nicias is not caricatured as a debauchee but depicted as a man of genuine cultivation and warmth.

The contrast between Nicias and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] enacts a broader conflict between [[hell|Hell]]enistic philosophy and early Christian asceticism. Nicias quotes no scripture but lives by a coherent ethic of moderation. The novel does not resolve this conflict — it lets Nicias smile and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] tremble, and leaves the reader to weigh the two ways of being in the world.

## Historical Context

Nicias represents the survival of classical Greek philosophy in late-antique [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. The historical Epicurean school, founded by Epicurus (341–270 BC), taught that pleasure — understood as the absence of pain — was the highest good, and that the gods, if they existed, were indifferent to human affairs. By the 4th century AD, Epicureanism was in decline, eclipsed by Neoplatonism and Christianity, but it retained adherents among the educated elite of the eastern Mediterranean. France's Nicias captures this moment of cultural transition: a philosopher whose worldview is about to be extinguished by the very ascetic movement [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] represents.
