## Overview

Marcus, called Marcus l'Arien (Marcus the Arian), is a Christian theologian in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). He appears at [[cotta|Cotta]]'s banquet in Part II, where he engages in theological debate with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] and other guests. Described by [[nicias|Nicias]] as "le Platon des chrétiens" — the Plato of the Christians (002-234) — Marcus represents the intellectual wing of Arian Christianity, the dominant theological movement in 4th-century [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], an opponent of Arianism, recognizes him with fear ("craignant de voir..." 002-286), and the encounter dramatizes the theological fractures within late-antique Christianity.

## Biography

Marcus is introduced at the banquet when [[nicias|Nicias]] warns [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]: "Si Marcus est le Platon des chrétiens, son éloquence est terrible" (002-234) — if Marcus is the Plato of the Christians, his eloquence is terrible. When Marcus appears, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] recognizes him immediately and fears the encounter (002-286).

During the banquet, Marcus delivers a theological discourse on the nature of Christ and creation. The guests address him as "Très illustre Marcus" — most illustrious Marcus (002-288) — and engage him on the relationship between God and the created world (002-290, 002-292). Marcus affirms the Arian doctrine that the Son was created by the Father (002-292) and persists in his exposition (002-295: "Marcus poursuivit"). [[dorion|Dorion]] later apologizes for having spoken to him as though he believed in divine law (002-307), exposing the epistemological gulf between philosophical skepticism and theological certainty.

In Part III, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] in his visions recalls "l'erreur d'[[arius|Arius]] et de Marcus" (003-154) — the error of [[arius|Arius]] and Marcus. The novel's epilogue refers to "le bréviaire de Marcus l'hérétique" (003-206) — the breviary of Marcus the heretic — marking him as a lasting figure of theological error in the orthodox imagination.

## Character

Marcus embodies the intellectual prestige and theological danger of Arian Christianity as seen through orthodox eyes. France presents him not as a villain but as a formidable intellect — "le Platon des chrétiens" — whose error is all the more dangerous for its eloquence. The epithet captures the paradox of Arianism in the late-antique world: it was the most intellectually sophisticated form of Christianity, yet destined for defeat by the Nicene orthodoxy [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] represents.

## Relationships

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Marcus represents the theological opponent [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] fears and opposes. Their encounter at the banquet is charged with doctrinal danger.
- **[[Nicias]]** — [[nicias|Nicias]] introduces Marcus with wry admiration, treating him as another interesting specimen in the philosophical menagerie.
- **[[Dorion]]** — [[dorion|Dorion]] debates Marcus on epistemology and divine law (002-307).
- **[[Cotta]]** — Host of the banquet where Marcus debates.
- **[[Arius]]** — The founder of Arianism, with whom Marcus is paired ("l'erreur d'[[arius|Arius]] et de Marcus" 003-154).
