## Overview

The Stylite Episode — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s ascent of a ruined temple column and his year-long life as a pillar saint — is the central dramatic sequence of Part III ("L'Euphorbe"). Returning from [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] after converting [[thais|Thaïs]], [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] finds himself tormented by visions and desires he cannot subdue through conventional asceticism. A dream commands him to climb a column, and he obeys, founding by this act both a new form of penance and an unintended city. The episode is France's most sustained piece of ironic world-building: the saint who fled the world creates, by the force of his reputation, a bustling commercial center at the foot of his column, complete with markets, inns, and tourists. The stylite episode dramatizes the paradox at the heart of Christian asceticism — that the rejection of the world invariably attracts the world's attention, and that no physical height can outrun an interior obsession.

## Narrative

**The Crisis**: After returning [[thais|Thaïs]] to the desert convent, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is besieged by hallucinations of her. He visits the aged gardener [[palemon|Palémon]], who advises moderation — visit neighboring monasteries, study their rules, take up copying manuscripts — rather than more extreme penance. [[palemon|Palémon]] quotes the desert father Anthony: "L'excès du jeûne produit la faiblesse et la faiblesse engendre l'inertie" (003-092). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] thanks him but cannot accept the counsel of moderation.

**The [[vision|Dream]]**: Returning to his cell, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] finds it swarming with phantom jackals. That night he dreams:

> "Cette nuit-là, il vit en songe une haute colonne de pierre, surmontée d'une figure humaine et il entendit une voix qui disait: —Monte sur cette colonne!" (003-094–095)

Convinced the dream is divine, he assembles his disciples, places them under [[flavien|Flavien]]'s authority, and departs.

**The Ascent**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] walks day and night to the ruins of a pagan temple he had visited during his earlier journey. Among the temple's thirty gigantic columns, one stands free — its capital carved as a woman's head with cow horns, smiling ominously (003-099). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] recognizes it as the column from his dream, estimates it at thirty-two cubits high, and has a ladder made in the neighboring village. He climbs, kneels on the capital, and prays: "Voici donc, mon Dieu, la demeure que tu m'as choisie" (003-101).

**The Growth of [[stylopolis|Stylopolis]]**: What begins as a solitary penance rapidly becomes a social phenomenon. The carpenter who built the ladder, moved by piety, constructs a roof and balustrade on the column (003-104). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s disciples learn of his retreat and build huts at the column's base. Farmers come on Sundays with their families "contempler le stylite" (003-105). Pilgrims arrive from ever-greater distances, and commerce follows:

> "Il y eut bientôt devant la colonne un marché où les pêcheurs du Nil apportaient leurs poissons et les jardiniers leurs légumes" (003-112).

Within six months, a city is founded — [[stylopolis|Stylopolis]] — with a guard post, a tribunal, a prison, and a school. The ruined temple fills with the "mouvements et des rumeurs innombrables de la vie" (003-112). Innkeepers convert subterranean chambers into taverns and hang signs bearing [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s image.

**Ecclesiastical Reception**: The abbots [[ephrem|Ephrem]] and [[serapion|Serapion]], hearing of this innovation, sail to investigate. They initially condemn so extraordinary a penance as "contraire à l'usage" and "hors de toute règle" (003-108). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] replies that the monastic life itself is a prodigious life, and that the same sign of God that raised him up will bring him down (003-110). Later, the Patriarch of [[antioch|Antioch]] visits and "approuva hautement la conduite si extraordinaire du stylite" (003-113), after which [[ephrem|Ephrem]] and [[serapion|Serapion]] return to apologize.

**[[miracle|Miracles]] and [[temptation|Temptation]]s**: The sick come in great numbers — the blind, the paralyzed, the cancerous, the epileptic. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] blesses them, and some depart healed. Crutches hang from the miraculous column; votive images and crowns are suspended by grateful women (003-121). But from his height, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] feels the crowd's frenzy enter his own body: "Et [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], du haut de sa colonne, sentait un frisson lui secouer les membres" (003-117). He prays to become "l'égout de l'univers" (003-126), the sewer of the world, absorbing all its impurities. Yet the desire for [[thais|Thaïs]] continues to burn within him:

> "Seule, au-dessus de ces formes agitées, s'élevait l'immuable colonne… et Paphnuce croit voir la chair de Thaïs étinceler dans les lueurs des eaux, parmi les saphirs de la nuit" (003-124).

**[[cotta|Cotta]]'s Visit**: The prefect of the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n fleet, Lucius [[cotta|Cotta]] — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s former dinner host — arrives with his physician [[aristee|Aristée]] to inspect the stylite. [[cotta|Cotta]] is amused and curious, dictating notes to his secretary: "Note cela, enfant, sur mes tablettes; ainsi que les dimensions de la colonne, sans oublier la forme du chapiteau" (003-132). [[aristee|Aristée]] diagnoses the stylite's condition as a disease of hidden organs, comparing him to the [[india|India]]n gymnosophists who maintain immobility for decades. [[cotta|Cotta]], ever the administrator, decides to leave the stylite in peace — good governance, in his view, means tolerating rather than abolishing popular customs (003-136). When he asks [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] directly whether the column has a phallic significance (003-140), [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], considering him an idolater, does not deign to reply.

**The Descent**: After more than a year on the column — "depuis un an qu'il est monté sur cette colonne, notre moine ne l'a pas quittée un moment" (003-134) — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s torment becomes unbearable. The column has not cured him; it has only concentrated his obsession. He descends, fleeing the shadow of what he now calls "la colonne maudite" (003-168), and plunges into a deeper form of eremitic isolation — the tomb of a pharaoh — where the visions of [[thais|Thaïs]] only intensify.

## Participants

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — The stylite himself, who transforms a pagan ruin into a Christian pilgrimage site while remaining internally consumed by desire for [[thais|Thaïs]].
- **[[Palemon]]** — The aged gardener-monk whose counsel of moderation [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] cannot accept; his advice to study neighboring monasteries ironically foreshadows the community that will form around the column.
- **[[Flavien]]** — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s disciple, left in charge of the desert community; later serves as spokesman to [[cotta|Cotta]].
- **[[ephrem|Ephrem]] and [[serapion|Sérapion]]** — The abbots who initially condemn the stylite's practice as irregular, then reverse their judgment after the Patriarch of [[antioch|Antioch]] endorses it.
- **[[Cotta|Lucius Cotta]]** — The prefect of the fleet who visits [[stylopolis|Stylopolis]] as a curious tourist, dictating anthropological observations to his secretary and debating with his physician.
- **[[Aristee]]** — [[cotta|Cotta]]'s physician, who interprets the stylite's behavior through a medical lens, comparing it to [[india|India]]n ascetic practices.

## Consequences

The stylite episode is the pivot of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s spiritual trajectory. His year on the column fails to extinguish his desire for [[thais|Thaïs]] — if anything, the isolation and the adulation of the crowd intensify it. The episode illustrates a structural pattern in the novel: each attempt at radical renunciation generates its opposite. The column, intended as a ladder toward heaven, becomes a vantage point from which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sees only [[thais|Thaïs]]'s flesh in the moonlight. After descending, he enters the darkness of a pharaoh's tomb, a descent that completes the arc from aerial saint to subterranean madman. The stylite episode also stands as France's most elaborate satire of religious celebrity — the saint who flees the world inadvertently founds a city, complete with souvenir merchants and a tourist economy.
