## Overview

Hermodore is the grand priest of Serapis — the syncretic Greco-[[egypt|Egypt]]ian deity whose cult was one of the last bastions of paganism in late-antique [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. In Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), he appears as a commanding presence at the banquet hosted by [[cotta|Cotta]] in Part II, where he debates philosophy and theology with Christians and Epicureans alike. Dignified, articulate, and ultimately tragic, Hermodore embodies the intellectual weight of the pagan establishment in the moment of its eclipse by Christianity. A final vision of him meditating beneath a sacred persea tree (003-186) closes his arc with solemn grandeur.

## Biography

Hermodore first enters the narrative at [[cotta|Cotta]]'s banquet as one of the principal guests. He is introduced alongside other figures worthy of admiration: "Hermodore, grand prêtre de Sérapis" (002-230). When the young Aristobule interrupts him, the text underscores Hermodore's authority: he does not need to assert himself; his status as grand priest commands respect (002-251).

As the banquet's philosophical debates intensify, Hermodore engages directly with Christian thinkers. He questions the difference between Christian and pagan conceptions of the divine, arguing that Christians, like pagans, possess principles of truth (002-291). In a striking physical gesture, he extends his arm across the tablecloth and rests his head upon it, absorbed in thought (002-298). When [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] — disguised as a philosopher — speaks of the corruption of the flesh, Hermodore is among those who acknowledge that men have indeed been granted an awareness of justice (002-314).

As dawn approaches and the banquet disintegrates into chaos, Hermodore and [[marcus|Marcus]] approach the sleeping [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] together (002-344). Later, Hermodore and [[nicias|Nicias]] carry the unconscious [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] from the hall (002-355). In Part III, a brief but vivid vision shows Hermodore meditating beneath a sacred persea tree, his head crowned with a glittering mitre (003-186) — a final image of pagan dignity before the ascendant Christian order.

## Character

Hermodore represents the intellectual seriousness of late-antique paganism. He is no idol-worshipping primitive but a philosopher-priest capable of engaging Christian theology on equal terms. His debate with Christians at the banquet is respectful rather than hostile; he seeks common ground rather than confrontation.

France draws Hermodore with a mixture of respect and elegy. The image of the grand priest meditating beneath the persea tree — solitary, crowned with a mitre, absorbed in contemplation — suggests a spirituality that is genuine even if it belongs to a dying world. Hermodore's paganism is not the sensual excess of the banquet but a quieter, more philosophical devotion that Christianity has not so much refuted as displaced.

## Relationships

- **[[Cotta]]** — As host of the banquet, [[cotta|Cotta]] provides the setting for Hermodore's philosophical interventions. Both are representatives of the pagan [[rome|Roma]]n order.
- **[[Marcus]]** — The Arian theologian with whom Hermodore debates Christian doctrine and with whom he approaches the sleeping [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] (002-344).
- **[[Zenothemis]]** — A fellow philosopher at the banquet with whom Hermodore shares principles of truth (002-291).
- **[[Nicias]]** — Together with [[nicias|Nicias]], Hermodore carries [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] from the banquet hall at the chaotic conclusion (002-355).
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Hermodore observes and engages the ascetic [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] during the banquet, acknowledging the justice of some of his pronouncements while remaining firmly pagan.

## Role in the Narrative

Hermodore serves as the novel's principal voice for philosophical paganism. Where other pagan characters — [[philina|Philina]], [[drose|Drosé]], even [[cotta|Cotta]] — represent paganism as sensuality or administrative habit, Hermodore alone gives it intellectual and spiritual dignity. His presence at the banquet ensures that the debate is genuinely polyphonic: Christian, Epicurean, and traditional pagan voices all receive a hearing.

His final image in Part III — meditating beneath the sacred persea — is one of France's most elegiac touches. The grand priest of a doomed cult, still practicing his devotion as the world around him converts, Hermodore becomes a figure of pathos rather than villainy.
