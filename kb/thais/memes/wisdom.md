## Definition

Wisdom (*sagesse*, from the Latin *sapientia*) is the quality of having experience, knowledge, and good judgment — but in *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, the term is contested ground. Greek philosophy claims it as the fruit of reason; Christian theology locates it in submission to divine revelation; the novel itself seems to suggest that true wisdom lies in neither camp, but in the humble recognition of one's own limits.

## In the Novel

The question of wisdom surfaces early, when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] consults an aged solitary about his plan to convert [[thais|Thaïs]]. "Ce solitaire, se disait-il, est de bon conseil; l'esprit de prudence est en lui. Et il doute de la sagesse de mon dessein" (001-043) — This solitary, he told himself, is of good counsel; the spirit of prudence is in him. And he doubts the wisdom of my design. The hermit's doubt is the novel's first hint that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s mission may not be as wise as his zeal suggests — but [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] proceeds anyway, mistaking intensity for discernment.

At [[nicias|Nicias]]'s banquet, wisdom becomes the explicit subject of debate. [[timocles|Timoclès]], the Cynic philosopher, relates how a lifetime of observing human suffering led him to a radical conclusion: he resolved "de professer l'entier détachement des biens de ce monde et de vivre dans la solitude et dans l'immobilité, à l'exemple de l'Indien" (001-085) — to profess entire detachment from the goods of this world and to live in solitude and immobility, after the example of the [[india|India]]n. His wisdom is the wisdom of renunciation — but it was purchased at the price of seeing his entire family destroyed by passion, and the novel leaves open whether such wisdom is enlightenment or merely scar tissue.

The prefect [[cotta|Cotta]] invokes "la sagesse latine" (002-228) — Latin wisdom — in welcoming [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] to his table, framing Christianity as compatible with the imperial philosophical tradition. But this is political wisdom, the wisdom of accommodation rather than truth. [[marcus|Marcus]], the Stoic, offers a more demanding version: "En toutes choses, ma sagesse copiera la sagesse divine, et la copie sera plus précieuse que le modèle" (002-267) — In all things, my wisdom will copy divine wisdom, and the copy will be more precious than the model. The claim is breathtaking in its pride — human wisdom surpassing its divine original — and the novel lets it stand as self-indictment.

The most searching treatment of wisdom comes through the discussion of Judas. One dinner guest argues that "si Judas n'avait pas reçu la bourse de trente sicles, la sagesse divine était démentie" (002-305) — if Judas had not received the purse of thirty shekels, divine wisdom was belied. The claim is that divine wisdom encompasses even treachery, using it as a stone in the edifice of redemption. "La sagesse divine avait prévu que Judas, libre de ne pas donner le baiser du traître, le donnerait pourtant" (002-306) — Divine wisdom had foreseen that Judas, free not to give the traitor's kiss, would give it nonetheless. This is wisdom as cosmic irony: the worst human act becomes the instrument of salvation.

Yet the novel's final word on wisdom may belong to the narrator, who describes [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] near the end as holding forth "des propos d'une infaillible sagesse" (003-246) — discourse of infallible wisdom — to the elders, even as his interior life collapses. The juxtaposition is devastating: wisdom spoken publicly, folly lived privately.

## Philosophical Context

The novel engages three competing wisdom traditions. **Greek philosophy** (represented by [[timocles|Timoclès]], [[marcus|Marcus]], and the banquet debaters) claims wisdom is accessible through reason and detachment. **[[rome|Roma]]n political wisdom** ([[cotta|Cotta]]) treats wisdom as practical accommodation to power. **Christian wisdom** ([[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s stated ideal) locates wisdom in submission to God — but the novel shows [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] substituting his own judgment for divine guidance at every critical turn. The unstated fourth position, which the novel seems to endorse, is the wisdom of humility — the wisdom that knows it does not know, exemplified by Palemon the gentle gardener rather than by any philosopher or theologian.

> *"This solitary, he told himself, is of good counsel; the spirit of prudence is in him. And he doubts the wisdom of my design."*

> *"If Judas had not received the purse of thirty shekels, divine wisdom was belied, Providence deceived, its designs overturned, and the world returned to evil, ignorance, and death."*
