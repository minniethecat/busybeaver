## Overview

Chaldée (Chaldea) — the marshy lowlands of southern Babylonia, the land of Ur from which, according to Genesis, God called Abraham to depart for Canaan — appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* in two sharply contrasting rhetorical moments. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] invokes it as the place of origins for the Abrahamic vocation that he claims to reenact; [[thais|Thaïs]] invokes it as a repository of esoteric wisdom whose practitioners — like all the pagan priests she has consulted — have failed to save her. Between these two invocations, the novel sketches a map of competing claims to supernatural power: the God of Abraham versus the mages of Babylon.

## In the Novel

The first mention of Chaldée occurs in the climactic recognition scene of Part II. Standing before [[thais|Thaïs]] in her house, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] tears open his borrowed tunic to reveal the cilice beneath and declares: "Je suis [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], abbé d'[[antinoe|Antinoé]], et je viens du saint désert. La main qui retira Abraham de Chaldée et Loth de Sodome m'a séparé du siècle" (002-188). By framing his own departure from the world as a recapitulation of Abraham's call out of Chaldea, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] casts his eremitic vocation in biblical typology — the desert monk is the new Abraham, and the hand of God is the same hand that reached into Mesopotamia two millennia earlier.

[[thais|Thaïs]]'s response, however, repurposes Chaldée as a site of failed magic. She asks [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] directly: "pourras-tu pour moi ce que n'ont pu ni les prêtres d'Isis, ni ceux d'Hermès, ni ceux de la Junon Céleste, ni les devins de Chaldée, ni les mages babyloniens?" (002-193). Her catalogue of pagan priesthoods — [[egypt|Egypt]]ian, Greek, [[rome|Roma]]n, Chaldean, Babylonian — is at once a confession of spiritual desperation and a taxonomy of the late-antique religious marketplace. The diviners of Chaldée occupy the penultimate position, just before the Babylonian mages; in the Greco-[[rome|Roma]]n imagination, "Chaldean" had become synonymous with astrologer and soothsayer. That they could not prevent death is, for [[thais|Thaïs]], the definitive proof of their impotence.

> "Je suis Paphnuce, abbé d'Antinoé, et je viens du saint désert. La main qui retira Abraham de Chaldée et Loth de Sodome m'a séparé du siècle. Je n'existais déjà plus pour les hommes." (002-188)
