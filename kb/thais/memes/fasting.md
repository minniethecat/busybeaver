## Definition

Fasting (*jeûne*) is the practice of abstaining from food, partially or completely, for a defined period as an act of religious devotion. In Christian monasticism, fasting serves multiple purposes: it disciplines the body, sharpens spiritual perception, expresses repentance, and prepares the soul for deeper union with God. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, fasting is presented as both the anchorite's indispensable tool and, potentially, his most dangerous instrument of self-deception.

## In the Novel

Fasting appears in the very first description of ascetic practice in the novel. The anchorites of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] defend themselves against demonic assault "au moyen du jeûne, de la pénitence et des macérations" (001-006) — by means of fasting, penance, and mortifications. Fasting is grouped with penance and bodily discipline as the triad of ascetic defense, the monk's weaponry against the devil's attacks.

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is introduced as an extreme practitioner: "[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] observait les jeûnes les plus rigoureux et demeurait parfois trois jours entiers sans prendre de nourriture" (001-009) — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] observed the most rigorous fasts and sometimes remained three full days without taking nourishment. His three-day fasts place him among the most severe ascetics of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]], and the narrator presents this not as pathology but as evidence of his spiritual stature — at least initially.

The novel complicates this picture through the figure of Saint John of [[egypt|Egypt]]. The devil, having taken a human face, tells John: "Jean, tu prolongeras ton jeûne jusqu'à demain soir" (001-170) — John, you will prolong your fast until tomorrow evening. John obeys, believing he hears an angel. The narrator dryly notes: "C'est la seule victoire que le prince des Ténèbres ait jamais remportée sur saint Jean l'Égyptien, et cette victoire est petite" (001-170) — This is the only victory the Prince of Darkness ever won over Saint John the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian, and this victory is small. The episode reveals a danger inherent in fasting itself: the discipline that sharpens discernment can also, when pushed too far, erode it.

The novel's most sustained critique of extreme fasting comes through the gentle anchorite Palemon, who quotes [[antoine|Saint Anthony]] himself: "L'excès du jeûne produit la faiblesse et la faiblesse engendre l'inertie. Il est des moines qui ruinent leur corps par des abstinences indiscrètement prolongées. On peut dire de ceux-ci qu'ils se plongent le poignard dans le sein et qu'ils se livrent inanimés au pouvoir du démon" (003-092) — The excess of fasting produces weakness and weakness engenders inertia. There are monks who ruin their bodies by indiscreetly prolonged abstinences. Of these one may say that they plunge the dagger into their own breast and deliver themselves, inanimate, into the power of the demon. This passage, placed near the novel's conclusion, recasts [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s fasting: what he took for spiritual heroism may have been, all along, a form of self-destruction that left him *more* vulnerable to the devil, not less.

## Theological Background

Fasting has deep biblical roots — Moses on [[sin|Sin]]ai, Elijah's forty-day journey, and Christ's own forty days in the wilderness all establish the practice as a means of preparation for divine encounter. The early Church institutionalized fasting in the seasons of Lent and Advent and in the weekly Wednesday and Friday observances. The Desert Fathers elevated fasting from a communal rhythm to an individual athleticism, with some — like [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] — competing in severity.

The theological rationale for fasting rests on the body-soul dualism pervasive in late antique Christianity: by weakening the body, the monk strengthens the soul. But the tradition also contains warnings — from Cassian, from Anthony, from the *Apophthegmata* — against excessive fasting that produces not holiness but irritability, pride, and spiritual delusion. Palemon's counsel in the novel echoes this cautionary strand precisely.
