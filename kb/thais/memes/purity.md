## Definition

Purity (*pureté*) in Christian theology denotes the absence of sin, particularly sins of the flesh, and the positive condition of being consecrated to God. In the ascetic tradition of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]], purity was the explicit goal of every discipline — fasting, vigils, flagellation, and solitude were all means to this single end. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, purity functions as the novel's central spiritual problem: can it be achieved through technique, or does it require something that technique cannot produce?

## In the Novel

Purity is introduced as the visible fruit of monastic life. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s disciples, including former brigands, embrace the monastic state and "la pureté de leur vie édifiait leurs compagnons" (001-010) — the purity of their life edified their companions. The implication is that monastic discipline produces genuine transformation: men who were once criminals now live lives of visible purity that inspire others.

But the novel distinguishes between purity that can be seen and purity that is real. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s address to [[thais|Thaïs]] in her cell is saturated with the language of purity and its opposite. He longs for her spiritual cleansing: "Qui fera jaillir de mon coeur une fontaine de [[siloam|Siloé]], dans laquelle tu retrouves, en te baignant, ta pureté première?" (002-179) — Who will make flow from my heart a fountain of [[siloam|Siloé]], in which you may recover, in bathing, your first purity? The image is baptismal — washing that restores an original state — but the language betrays [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s deeper motivation: he wants to be the *source* of her transformation, the fountain from which her purity flows.

The novel's most concentrated treatment of purity comes through [[zenothemis|Zénothémis]], who describes the soul's ascent: "dans cet état, qui participe de la pureté divine, il possède à la fois la joie infinie et la science absolue" (002-278) — in this state, which partakes of divine purity, it possesses at once infinite joy and absolute knowledge. This is Neoplatonic purity — achieved through philosophical contemplation rather than ascetic discipline — and its presence in the novel creates an implicit critique of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s bodily approach: perhaps purity is a matter of the mind's orientation, not the body's mortification.

The sharpest language of purity appears in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s condemnation of the converted [[thais|Thaïs]]: "tes impuretés sont telles que, maintenant que tu sais la vérité, tu ne peux plus unir tes lèvres ou joindre les mains sans que le dégoût de toi-même ne te soulève le coeur" (003-026) — your impurities are such that, now that you know the truth, you can no longer bring your lips together or join your hands without the disgust of yourself rising in your heart. The cruelty of this speech — delivered to a woman who has already converted — exposes the impurity in [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s own heart: his obsession with her past contamination masks his own spiritual pride. In the end, it is [[thais|Thaïs]] whose blood is prayed over: "qu'une anémone miraculeuse fleurisse sur le sable arrosé par le sang de [[thais|Thaïs]], afin que tous ceux qui verront cette fleur recouvrent la pureté du coeur et des sens" (003-031) — may a miraculous anemone flower on the sand watered by the blood of [[thais|Thaïs]], so that all who see this flower may recover purity of heart and senses. The one who was called impure becomes the source of purity for others.

## Theological Background

Purity in early Christian thought is inseparable from the Levitical codes of the Hebrew Bible, where ritual purity governed access to the Temple. Christianity spiritualized these categories: purity became a matter of the heart rather than of external observance. But the desert tradition reintroduced a quasi-physical dimension — the monk's body, through fasting and continence, became a site where purity was visibly demonstrated.

The danger, which *[[thais|Thaïs]]* explores with precision, is that visible purity can become an end in itself, a marker of status rather than a disposition of the soul. The novel's critique aligns with the prophetic tradition — "these people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me" — and with the Desert Fathers' own warnings against the pride that often accompanies ascetic achievement.
