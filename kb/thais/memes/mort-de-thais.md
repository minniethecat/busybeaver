## Overview

The Death of Thaïs is the climactic event of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, occupying the final pages of Part III ("L'Euphorbe"). After three years enclosed in the desert convent under the abbess [[albine|Albina]], [[thais|Thaïs]] lies dying. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], driven by the obsession that has consumed him since her conversion, rushes to her deathbed. The scene is the novel's supreme ironic reversal: [[thais|Thaïs]] dies in a state of beatific vision, her soul ascending to heaven, while [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], kneeling beside her, confesses the carnal love that has undone his asceticism — and is left howling in despair at the corpse of the woman he saved and lost.

## Narrative

**The Summons**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is informed that [[thais|Thaïs]] is dying. Having descended from his pillar at [[stylopolis|Stylopolis]], consumed by visions of her, he races to the convent. The journey reverses his earlier trek — where he once walked to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] to save her soul, he now runs to the desert to possess her body.

**The Deathbed**: [[thais|Thaïs]] lies on a bed beneath a fig tree in the convent courtyard, "toute blanche, les bras en croix" (003-278). Veiled women stand beside her reciting the prayers of the dying. The scene is bathed in morning light — an image of peace that contrasts sharply with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s inner torment. A dove's plaintive voice breaks the silence, then the monk's sobs mingle with the nuns' psalms:

> "La mort était sur elle et la sueur de l'agonie couronnait son front. Rompant le silence auguste, une tourterelle éleva sa voix plaintive. Puis les sanglots du moine se mêlèrent à la psalmodie des vierges." (003-288)

**The [[vision|Vision]]**: As she dies, [[thais|Thaïs]] sees the heavens open. Her final words describe a vision of angels and light — a mystical experience that France renders with striking beauty. Her death is authentic sanctity: the sinner has become a saint.

**The Confession**: At the moment of her death, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] — who came to save her soul and instead fell in love with her body — confesses his passion. He cries out that he was a fool to have believed anything mattered besides her, that he should have possessed her when he had the chance. But [[thais|Thaïs]] has already transcended him; she sees only heaven, not the broken man at her feet.

**The Final Image**: The novel ends with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], transformed by despair into a hideous creature, feeling his own ugliness with his hand — "il sentit sa laideur" (003-305). The saint rises to heaven; the would-be saint falls into damnation.

## Participants

- **[[Thais]]** — The dying saint. Her death is a spiritual triumph, the culmination of her three-year enclosure.
- **[[Paphnuce]]** — The failed ascetic. His confession of love at [[thais|Thaïs]]'s deathbed is the novel's tragic climax.
- **[[Albina]]** — The abbess who governs the convent and oversees [[thais|Thaïs]]'s final hours.
- **The Veiled Women** — The community of nuns who chant the prayers of the dying.

## Consequences

The death of [[thais|Thaïs]] is the novel's definitive resolution. For [[thais|Thaïs]], it is salvation: three years of genuine penitence have transformed her, and she dies in a state of grace. For [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], it is damnation: having sublimated his desire into religious zeal, he discovers at the final moment that it was always carnal. The structural irony is complete — the sinner is saved and the saint is damned, a reversal that embodies France's sceptical humanism.

## Themes

The death scene crystallizes the novel's central themes: the instability of ascetic motivation, the paradoxical efficacy of grace ([[thais|Thaïs]], who never sought sanctity, achieves it), and the tragic gap between self-knowledge and spiritual action. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s tragedy is that he acted for God but loved for himself; he could not see the difference until it was too late.
