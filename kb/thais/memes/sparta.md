## Overview

Sparta (Sparte), the austere city-state of the Peloponnese renowned for its martial discipline, appears in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* in two distinct literary registers. It is invoked first in a theological-fabulistic context — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], wrestling with temptation atop his column, recalls hearing "a certain forger of impostures" claim that "the bitch of Sparta took upon herself the sins of the world" (003-126). Later, Sparta resurfaces in the mythological register: the Helen of Troy narrative is reanimated when a vision declares that "[[thais|Thaïs]] lived once in Sparta under the name of Helen" (003-178). Between these two allusions, Sparta serves as a classical touchstone — a city whose name evokes both sacrificial expiation and legendary beauty.

## In the Novel

### The Bitch of Sparta (003-126)

In the grip of spiritual torment, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] cries out to God: "Lord, make pass through me all the lust of men, so that I may expiate it all!" He then reflects on a fable he has heard:

> "S'il est faux que la chienne de Sparte ait pris sur elle les péchés du monde, comme je l'ai entendu dire à certain forgeron d'impostures, cette fable contient pourtant un sens caché dont je reconnais aujourd'hui l'exactitude." (003-126)

The "bitch of Sparta" — a garbled or invented classical reference — becomes for [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] a figure for substitutionary suffering, a pagan prefiguration of the Christian doctrine of expiation. He recognizes its "hidden sense": that "the filth of peoples enters the soul of the saints" (003-126). The passage reveals both [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s theological imagination and France's ironic treatment of it — the saint finds Christian truth in what he himself dismisses as a forgery.

### Thaïs as Helen of Sparta (003-178)

In a vision near the novel's climax, a beautiful figure addresses [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]: "I am the beauty of woman. Where do you think to flee me, fool? … It is a thousand years that the man whose name you bear has sworn to destroy me." She reveals a metempsychotic genealogy:

> "C'est pourtant une vérité, dans l'ordre des vérités universellement connues, que Thaïs a vécu jadis dans Sparte sous le nom d'Hélène." (003-178)

Through this identification — [[thais|Thaïs]] as the reincarnation of Helen of Sparta — France links the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n courtesan to the most famous beauty of classical antiquity. Sparta thus becomes a node in the novel's chain of reincarnations, where the eternal feminine reappears across history, each time confronting the ascetic who would renounce her.

## Historical Background

In the classical period, Sparta was the dominant military power of the Peloponnese, known for its rigorous *agoge* training system, its dual kingship, and its victory over [[athens|Athens]] in the Peloponnesian War. By the fourth century CE, [[rome|Roma]]n Sparta was a diminished provincial city, but its legendary associations — with Helen, with Lycurgus's discipline, with the heroic self-sacrifice of the Three Hundred — persisted in the Greco-[[rome|Roma]]n literary imagination that Anatole France draws upon.
