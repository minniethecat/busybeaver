## Definition

A hermit (*ermite*, from the Greek *erēmitēs* meaning "of the desert") is a Christian solitary who withdraws from human society to pursue a life of prayer, asceticism, and contemplation in the wilderness. In fourth-century [[egypt|Egypt]], the hermits of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] — above all [[antoine|Saint Anthony]] — established the archetype of desert spirituality that would shape monasticism for centuries.

## In the Novel

The hermit tradition envelops [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] from the novel's opening. When he first encounters signs of habitation in the desert, he immediately identifies the occupant's way of life: "Voilà, se dit-il, le mobilier d'un ascète. Communément les ermites s'éloignent peu de leur cabane" (001-052) — Here is the furniture of an ascetic. Commonly, hermits do not stray far from their hut. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s expectation that he will receive the kiss of peace "à l'exemple du saint solitaire [[antoine|Antoine]] qui, s'étant rendu auprès de l'ermite Paul, l'embrassa par trois fois" (001-052) — after the example of the holy solitary Anthony who, having visited the hermit Paul, embraced him three times — places his own journey within the hermit lineage explicitly.

The hermit life, however, is not the simple idyll [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] imagines. The narrator notes that "les déserts sont peuplés de fantômes" (001-169) — the deserts are populated with phantoms. When pilgrims approached the ruined castle where "s'était retiré le saint ermite [[antoine|Antoine]], ils entendaient des clameurs comme il s'en élève aux carrefours des villes, dans les nuits de fête" (001-169) — the holy hermit Anthony had withdrawn, they heard clamors like those rising from city crossroads on festival nights. The solitude the hermit seeks is crowded with spiritual presences, many of them hostile.

The novel's most unsettling meditation on the hermit life arrives late, when the dead [[thais|Thaïs]] returns to taunt [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] as "Mon bel ermite" (003-180) — My beautiful hermit — mocking both his self-denial and his pretensions to spiritual heroism. Her speech exposes the hermit's vulnerability: in fleeing the world, he has cut himself off from the very relationships through which grace might operate, leaving him alone with forces he underestimated.

## Theological Background

The hermit tradition draws on biblical precedents — Elijah on Mount Carmel, John the Baptist in the wilderness, Christ's own forty days in the desert — but received its definitive Christian form in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian desert of the third and fourth centuries. [[antoine|Saint Anthony]] (c. 251–356), repeatedly invoked in *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, became the model: his withdrawal into the deep desert was understood not as escape but as combat, a direct confrontation with demons in their own territory.

The theology of the hermit life rests on a paradox: the solitary abandons human society to seek God, yet discovers that solitude intensifies rather than eliminates spiritual struggle. The *Apophthegmata Patrum* (Sayings of the Desert Fathers) record this tension repeatedly — hermits who found that fleeing the city only brought the city's temptations into the cell with them.

## Key Passages

> "Voilà, se dit-il, le mobilier d'un ascète. Communément les ermites s'éloignent peu de leur cabane. Je ne manquerai pas de rencontrer bientôt celui-ci. Je veux lui donner le baiser de paix, à l'exemple du saint solitaire Antoine qui, s'étant rendu auprès de l'ermite Paul, l'embrassa par trois fois." [001-052]

> *"Here, he said to himself, is the furniture of an ascetic. Commonly, hermits do not stray far from their hut. I shall not fail to meet this one soon. I wish to give him the kiss of peace, after the example of the holy solitary Anthony who, having visited the hermit Paul, embraced him three times."*

> "Les déserts sont peuplés de fantômes. Quand les pèlerins approchaient du château en ruines où s'était retiré le saint ermite Antoine, ils entendaient des clameurs comme il s'en élève aux carrefours des villes, dans les nuits de fête." [001-169]

> *"My beautiful hermit, give me a kiss."*
