## Overview

In the Greco-[[rome|Roma]]n geography of late antiquity, "Asia" (Asie) typically referred not to the continent but to Asia Minor — the peninsula of Anatolia corresponding to modern Turkey. This was one of the wealthiest and most urbanized regions of the [[rome|Roman Empire]], a land of ancient Greek colonies, Persian imperial heritage, and a distinctive blend of [[hell|Hell]]enic and indigenous cultures. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, Asia appears as a recurring cultural signifier: the source of luxury textiles, exotic fashions, mysterious religious cults, and the mythic landscape of Homer's Troy.

## In the Novel

Asia is woven into the sensory texture of the novel through its material culture. When [[nicias|Nicias]] dresses the reluctant [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] in fine clothing, his servants bring forth a tunic "embroidered in the Asiatic style, with flowers and animals" (001-139) — the epitome of luxurious ornament. [[timocles|Timoclès]]'s father, a wealthy shipowner, was rich enough "to buy all the meats and fruits of the markets of Asia" (001-085), a mark of his vast fortune. At the banquet, [[marcus|Marcus]] the Arian makes his startling entrance "dressed in the Asiatic fashion, in an azure tunic and wearing around his legs, like the barbarians, red breeches sprinkled with gold stars" (002-286) — his Asiatic garb marking him as exotic and, to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], dangerous.

The novel's theatrical prologue reaches back to Homeric myth: "Cassandra, once queen of flourishing Asia, now a slave, soiled her unfortunate head with dust" (002-008). Here Asia is the realm of King Priam and the doomed splendor of Troy — a mythic landscape of tragic grandeur. Later, the poet in the banquet scene invokes the same mythic register, calling [[thais|Thaïs]] "charming as in the days of Priam and Asia in bloom" (002-317), identifying the courtesan with an eternal feminine beauty that transcends epochs.

In Part III, the former governor [[cotta|Cotta]] attributes the practice of pillar-sitting to "certain Asiatic cults" (003-136), tracing [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s extraordinary penance to Eastern religious traditions. For [[cotta|Cotta]], this is grounds for tolerance rather than condemnation — a view that sets him apart from the novel's more absolutist characters.

## Geography

[[rome|Roma]]n Asia (the province of Asia Proconsularis) occupied the western third of the Anatolian peninsula, including the wealthy cities of Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, and Sardis. It was bounded by the Aegean Sea to the west, the provinces of Bithynia and Galatia to the north and east, and Lycia to the south. The region's fertile river valleys and strategic ports had made it prosperous since before the Persian Empire.

## Historical Background

Asia Minor was one of the earliest and most thoroughly Christianized regions of the [[rome|Roman Empire]]. The Apostle Paul had traveled extensively through the province, and the Book of Revelation addressed its seven churches. By the fourth century, Asia was a stronghold of orthodox (Nicene) Christianity, though heterodox movements — including Arianism, which the character [[marcus|Marcus]] represents — also flourished there. The reference to "Asiatic cults" (003-136) reflects the historical reality that older pagan traditions, including the ecstatic cults of Cybele and Attis, persisted alongside Christianity well into late antiquity.
