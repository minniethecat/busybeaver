## Overview

Syria (Syrie) was a prosperous and strategically vital province of the [[rome|Roman Empire]], stretching from the Mediterranean coast to the Euphrates River. In the fourth century, it was a crossroads of cultures, religions, and trade routes — Phoenician, Greek, [[rome|Roma]]n, and Semitic traditions mingled in its cities. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, Syria appears both as a geographic reference and as a cultural marker: it is the province where Christians were persecuted, where the "good goddess of the Syrians" was worshipped, and where the former governor [[cotta|Cotta]] observed the religious customs of the local population.

## In the Novel

Syria is invoked at several key moments across the narrative. During the great persecution of Christians under the Emperor, the faithful in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] heard with terror reports that "in Syria, in [[arabia|Arabia]], in Mesopotamia, in Cappadocia, throughout the whole empire, whips, racks, iron claws, the cross, and wild beasts were tearing apart pontiffs and virgins" (002-044). This litany of provinces, with Syria named first, establishes the geographic scope of the suffering.

In [[ahmes|Ahmès]]'s simplified retelling of the Gospel story, "the princes of Syria erected a cross on a high mountain and put Jesus to death on that cross" (002-050) — a folkloric version that places the crucifixion within a Syrian landscape familiar to the novel's [[egypt|Egypt]]ian audience.

[[thais|Thaïs]] herself, in her eclectic religiosity, "believed in Jesus Christ and in the good goddess of the Syrians" (002-138), alongside a host of other beliefs — an indication of the syncretic religious landscape of the [[rome|Roma]]n East.

The most substantial treatment of Syria comes in Part III, when the [[rome|Roma]]n nobleman [[cotta|Cotta]] recounts his tenure as governor of the province. He describes a Syrian religious custom: phalluses erected on the propylaea of Hera's city, where a man would ascend twice a year and remain for seven days, believed by the people to converse with the gods and secure the prosperity of Syria (003-136). [[cotta|Cotta]] reflects that a good administrator should understand and tolerate local customs rather than abolish them — a philosophy of governance that stands in ironic contrast to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s absolutist asceticism.

## Geography

Roman Syria encompassed a diverse landscape: the narrow Mediterranean coastal plain, the Lebanon and Anti-Lebanon mountain ranges, the fertile Orontes River valley (where [[antioch|Antioch]] lay), and the vast Syrian Desert stretching toward the Euphrates. The province's principal cities — [[antioch|Antioch]], Damascus, Emesa, and Palmyra — were connected by an extensive [[rome|Roma]]n road network that facilitated trade from Persia to the Mediterranean.

## Historical Background

By the early fourth century, Syria had been under [[rome|Roma]]n rule for over three centuries. It was one of the Empire's wealthiest provinces, contributing grain, olive oil, wine, and textiles. The province held particular importance for early Christianity: [[antioch|Antioch]], its capital, was where the followers of Jesus were first called "Christians" and where the Apostle Peter was traditionally held to have established his first episcopal see. The [[antioch|Antioche]]ne school of theology, with its emphasis on the literal and historical interpretation of Scripture, was a major intellectual force. The references in the novel to Syrian persecution (002-044) draw on the historical memory of Diocletian's Great Persecution (303–313 CE), which was particularly severe in the eastern provinces.
