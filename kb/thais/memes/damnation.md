## Definition

Damnation (*la damnation*) is the state of eternal condemnation and punishment after death — the irrevocable separation of the soul from God. In Christian theology, it is the consequence of unrepented mortal sin and the antithesis of salvation. In *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, damnation functions less as a doctrinal abstraction than as an existential horizon that shapes every character's actions: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] fears it for [[thais|Thaïs]], for himself, and ultimately cannot tell which of them stands closer to the abyss.

## In the Novel

Damnation enters the novel in the opening description of ascetic combat. The hermits of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] are "furieusement assaillis par des légions de damnés" (001-006) — furiously assailed by legions of the damned — establishing damnation not as a distant threat but as an immediate, swarming presence. The damned are not safely locked away in hell; they are actively engaged in warfare against the living.

The novel's most pointed theological discussion of damnation occurs at the banquet in Part II, where the guests debate the eternal fate of Judas Iscariot. [[nicias|Nicias]], with characteristic irony, observes: "Il faut de ceux qui croient à la damnation éternelle de Judas" (002-307) — There must be those who believe in the eternal damnation of Judas. The remark exposes damnation-belief as a psychological necessity for certain temperaments rather than a rationally defended doctrine — a need for cosmic justice that damns the betrayer to confirm the order of the universe.

The novel's climactic treatment of damnation comes in Part III, as [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] recognizes his own spiritual peril. The narrator describes "l'horreur mélancolique de son trouble et de sa damnation" (003-168) — the melancholy horror of his trouble and his damnation. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] descends the stairs in haste, and the scene marks the moment when the ascetic who came to save another realizes he may himself be lost. The novel's structural irony reaches its peak: [[thais|Thaïs]] dies in a state of grace, while her would-be savior is consumed by despair.

The novel also deploys the related term *condamné* in its juridical sense. Early Christians are described as those "condamnés à mort et livrés aux bêtes" (002-127) — condemned to death and thrown to the beasts — drawing a parallel between earthly condemnation and eternal damnation that recurs throughout the text.

## Theological Background

In 4th-century Christianity, the doctrine of eternal damnation was contested. Origen's universalism — the hope that all souls, even Satan's, would ultimately be restored — had not yet been formally condemned, and Augustine's later hardening of the doctrine of eternal punishment was still a century away. The [[egypt|Egypt]] of the Desert Fathers was a theological landscape where damnation was feared but its precise boundaries remained unsettled. France, writing in 1890, brings to this ambiguity a post-Enlightenment skepticism: the novel quietly asks whether a God of love would damn anyone eternally, and whether the fear of damnation serves faith or merely feeds despair.

## Key Passages

> "Les ascètes, furieusement assaillis par des légions de damnés, se défendaient avec l'aide de Dieu et des anges, au moyen du jeûne, de la pénitence et des macérations." [001-006]

> *"The ascetics, furiously assailed by legions of the damned, defended themselves with the help of God and the angels, by means of fasting, penance, and mortifications."*

> "Il faut de ceux qui croient à la damnation éternelle de Judas. Mais en réalité, qui sait?" [002-307]

> *"There must be those who believe in the eternal damnation of Judas. But in reality, who knows?"*

> "L'horreur mélancolique de son trouble et de sa damnation. Il descendit à la hâte tous les degrés." [003-168]

> *"The melancholy horror of his trouble and his damnation. He descended all the steps in haste."*
