## Overview

Timoclès de Cos is an elderly philosopher in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), a representative of the Academic-Platonic tradition who appears in Part I. As "le vieillard Timoclès" — the old man Timoclès — he engages [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] in a charged dialogue on salvation and the possibility of conversion. His skepticism toward Christian exclusivism provides an early philosophical counterpoint to [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s zeal.

## Biography

Timoclès first appears in Part I during the philosophical gathering at [[nicias|Nicias]]'s house. He introduces himself simply: "Je me nomme Timoclès et je suis né à [[cos|Cos]]" (001-085) — I am named Timoclès and was born at [[cos|Cos]]. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] responds with a declaration of the Christian faith (001-087), but Timoclès interrupts him (001-088: "Mais Timoclès l'interrompant"), challenging the Christian claim to exclusive truth.

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is frustrated by Timoclès's resistance: "la vérité n'était pas sur le vieillard Timoclès et que le jour du salut n'était pas encore venu pour ce Céen" (001-090) — the truth was not upon the old man Timoclès and the day of salvation had not yet come for this man of [[cos|Cos]]. He bids farewell with the harsh judgment: "Adieu donc! malheureux Timoclès" (001-091) — Farewell then, unhappy Timoclès.

Despite [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s dismissal, [[nicias|Nicias]] later observes that "pour un gentil, le vieillard Timoclès n'est pas dénué de sens" (001-107) — for a gentile, the old man Timoclès is not devoid of sense. By the end of the gathering, Timoclès stands apart: "Seul, le vieillard Timoclès se tenait à l'écart et secouait la tête" (001-157) — alone, the old man Timoclès stood apart shaking his head. This final image of the philosopher — isolated, skeptical, unconvinced — is France's emblem for the limits of rational discourse in the face of religious certainty.

## Character

Timoclès embodies the dignity and limitation of pagan philosophy confronted by Christian revelation. He is old, learned, from a distinguished philosophical lineage — but his wisdom is of this world, and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s message demands a leap he cannot make. France treats him with sympathy: his head-shaking at the end is not obstinacy but the honest response of a mind that cannot embrace what it does not understand.

## Relationships

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — Timoclès is the first philosopher to challenge [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s certainty directly, and [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s dismissive response ("malheureux Timoclès") reveals the authoritarian core of his zeal.
- **[[Nicias]]** — [[nicias|Nicias]] defends Timoclès's intelligence ("pas dénué de sens"), positioning himself as a mediator between pagan philosophy and Christian fervor.
- **[[Dorion]]** — Fellow philosopher at the gathering who shares Timoclès's skeptical disposition.
