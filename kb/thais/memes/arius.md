## Overview

Arius (c. 256–336 AD) is a presbyter of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] whose teaching that Christ was a created being, subordinate to God the Father, ignited the Arian controversy that convulsed the fourth-century Church. In Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), Arius appears as a historical reference — his "error" is invoked alongside that of [[marcus|Marcus]] as a heresy that the Emperor Constantius favors, against which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is called to contend (003-154).

## Biography

Arius does not appear as a character in *[[thais|Thaïs]]* but is invoked once in a crucial passage. A celestial voice commands [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]: "Lève-toi, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], et va trouver dans son palais l'impie Constance, qui, loin d'imiter la sagesse de son frère Constant, favorise l'erreur d'Arius et de [[marcus|Marcus]]" (003-154). The "error of Arius" — Arianism — is framed as a doctrinal crisis threatening the Church, one that demands [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s intervention.

Historically, Arius's teaching was condemned at the Council of [[nicee|Nicaea]] (325 AD), but Arianism remained influential throughout the fourth century, especially under Emperor Constantius II (r. 337–361), who actively supported Arian bishops. France's anachronistic fiction — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] being called to confront Constantius — weaves the historical Arius into the novel's theological landscape.

## Character

In the novel, Arius functions not as a character but as a symbol of theological error. His name, paired with that of [[marcus|Marcus]] ([[marcus|Marcus]] the Arian, who *does* appear at the banquet), anchors the novel's ecclesial conflicts in real fourth-century doctrinal disputes. France uses the Arian reference to situate [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s ascetic rigor within a Church besieged by heresy — a Church whose purity the desert fathers are called to defend.
