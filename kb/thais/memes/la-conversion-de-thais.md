## Overview

The Conversion of Thaïs is the inciting event of Anatole France's novel: the moment when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], an ascetic abbot of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]], resolves to leave his desert community, travel to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], and save the soul of the courtesan he glimpsed in his youth. The conversion is not a single dramatic moment but a sequence spanning Parts I and II — from [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s initial vision to [[thais|Thaïs]]'s entrance into the desert convent. It is the hinge on which the novel's tragic irony turns: the sinner is saved, and the savior is damned.

## Narrative

**The [[vision|Vision]]**: The conversion begins not with [[thais|Thaïs]] but with [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]. Years after retreating to the desert, he is suddenly seized by the memory of a beautiful [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n actress he saw in his youth. The memory arrives unbidden, mingled with spiritual urgency: "il songea de nouveau à [[thais|Thaïs]]" (001-022). [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] interprets this as a divine calling — God has chosen him to pluck this soul from the demon.

**The Journey**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] walks from the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]], a journey of several days. Along the way, he encounters omens — a black jackal that vanishes at the sign of the cross (001-022), a colossal Sphinx whose pagan silence rebukes his certainties (001-047) — but he presses on, armored in his righteousness.

**The Confrontation**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] finds [[thais|Thaïs]] at her house in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. He preaches hellfire and judgment, describing the torments awaiting the unrepentant. [[thais|Thaïs]], weary of the life she has mastered, does not resist. Her conversion is less a theological persuasion than an exhaustion with vanity — France's secularized version of the medieval legend.

**The Enclosure**: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] escorts [[thais|Thaïs]] to the desert convent governed by the abbess [[albine|Albina]]. There, [[thais|Thaïs]] is enclosed in a cell with only bread, water, and a cross. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] departs, believing his work complete. The irony — that her salvation will undo him — has already begun its slow work.

## Participants

- **[[Paphnuce]]** — The agent of conversion. His motive is contaminated from the start by pride and repressed desire.
- **[[Thaïs]]** — The object of conversion. She consents not out of terror but out of exhaustion with the world she has conquered.
- **[[Nicias]]** — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s childhood friend, who facilitates the confrontation and watches with Epicurean amusement.
- **[[Albina]]** — The abbess who receives [[thais|Thaïs]] into the convent, providing the institutional framework for her transformation.

## Consequences

The conversion's consequences form the entire arc of the novel. For [[thais|Thaïs]], it leads to genuine sanctity — her death in the convent is a scene of mystical peace. For [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], it leads to psychological disintegration. Having exposed himself to the world to save [[thais|Thaïs]], he can no longer seal himself off from desire. His repressed passion for her erupts in the novel's final scene, where he confesses his love at her deathbed only to find she has transcended him entirely.

The structural irony is that the conversion succeeds as a spiritual act ([[thais|Thaïs]] is saved) while destroying its human agent ([[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is damned). France's point is not that conversion is false but that the motives for conversion are never pure.

## Themes

The conversion dramatizes the novel's central themes: the ambiguity of religious motivation, the paradox of salvation through human agency, and the instability of the ascetic ideal. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] believes he is God's instrument; France suggests he is desire's puppet. [[thais|Thaïs]], who seems the passive object of others' actions, achieves genuine transformation precisely because she does not seek it.
