## Overview

India (Inde) appears in Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890) not as a setting but as a distant cultural horizon — a land whose ascetic traditions and exotic luxuries serve as reference points for the novel's characters. For [[timocles|Timoclès]], it is the destination of a philosophical pilgrimage. For [[nicias|Nicias]], it is the source of the precious perfumes and fabrics that adorn his banquet. For [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], the Gymnosophists of India become a measure against which [[thais|Thaïs's]] spiritual merit is weighed. In a novel whose geography spans from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian desert to the hills of [[greece|Greece]], India represents the farthest reach of the known world — and of the imagination.

## In the Novel

India is mentioned three times in the novel, each in a distinct register. [[timocles|Timoclès]] of [[cos|Cos]], recounting his intellectual journey, tells the banquet guests: "Having travelled as far as India, I saw on the banks of the Ganges a man who, entirely naked, had held his feet together for thirty years" (001-085). For [[timocles|Timoclès]], the Indian ascetic — who wrapped his legs with vines and trained dry branches around his body — represents a spiritual discipline that rivals anything Christian [[egypt|Egypt]] can offer.

[[nicias|Nicias]], in his role as the wealthy Epicurean host, boasts that the perfumes and aromatics of his banquet "have been brought at great expense from India and Persia" (002-161). India here is the source of material luxury, the perfumed East that sustains the [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n culture of pleasure that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has come to condemn.

The novel's third and most pointed reference comes in its closing pages. As [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] wrestles with jealousy and spiritual despair, he recalls "the Gymnosophists of India, who can maintain complete impassivity" (003-135). The phrase is charged with bitter irony: the pagan philosophers of India have achieved the detachment that the Christian saint, consumed by earthly desire, cannot. India, in this final appearance, is not a place but a reproach — a mirror in which [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] sees his own spiritual failure.

## Cultural Significance

In the late antique world of the fourth century, India was known to the Greco-[[rome|Roma]]n Mediterranean primarily through trade contacts and the accounts of travelers. The Gymnosophists — "naked philosophers" — had been described by Greek writers since the time of Alexander the Great, and represented a tradition of bodily asceticism that both paralleled and rivaled Christian monastic practices. France's use of India in the novel draws on this classical tradition: India is the land where the wisdom of the body's mastery predates, and in some respects surpasses, the Christian desert.
