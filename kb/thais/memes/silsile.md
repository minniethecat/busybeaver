## Overview

Silsilé is a narrow granite defile on the [[nile|Nile]] in Upper [[egypt|Egypt]], where the river contracts between steep rock walls. In Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890), it serves as a crucial waypoint on [[paphnuce|Paphnuce's]] journey from the [[thebaide|Thébaïde]] to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] — a place of dream, vision, and spiritual trial where the ascetic encounters the enigmatic figure of the Sphinx.

## In the Novel

Silsilé appears in Part I (*Le Lotus*) when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], having resolved to travel to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] and convert the courtesan [[thais|Thaïs]], sets out on foot along the [[nile|Nile]]. He arrives at Silsilé — "le fleuve y coule dans une étroite vallée bordée d'une double chaîne de montagnes de granit" — the river flowing through a narrow valley bordered by twin chains of granite mountains (001-047). There he beholds an ancient [[egypt|Egypt]]ian temple carved from the living rock, and a colossal Sphinx that seems to guard the sanctuary.

The Sphinx of Silsilé becomes the focus of a powerful visionary experience. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], observing the monument's immutable features — headless body, serene smile — reflects on the ancient [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cult of idols. The Sphinx speaks to him of the pagan kings "whose image it is" and who set up their glory in stone, contrasting their earthly immortality with the living God of Israel (001-049, 001-059). The encounter is ambivalent: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] blesses the Sphinx in the name of Christ, yet the monument returns to haunt him. Later in the novel, during his stylite penance, he recognizes the Sphinx of Silsilé among his demonic visions — "la bête le saisit entre les crocs" — the beast seizing him in its jaws (001-155).

## Geography

Silsilé (modern Gebel Silsila) lies on the [[nile|Nile]] approximately 65 km north of Aswan, where the river narrows dramatically between sandstone and granite cliffs. In antiquity, it was a major quarrying site — the sandstone for many of [[egypt|Egypt]]'s great temples was cut here. The presence of rock-cut shrines and a Sphinx described by France reflects the site's genuine archaeological character.

## Symbolic Significance

The Sphinx of Silsilé is one of the novel's chief symbols of the pagan past that Christianity cannot fully exorcise. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] blesses it with the sign of the cross, yet the gesture proves inadequate — the Sphinx returns in dream and vision, its enigmatic smile unchanged. The gorge of Silsilé, with its monumental sculpture carved from living rock, embodies the persistence of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]] beneath the surface of the Christian present. It is no accident that [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] passes through Silsilé on his way to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]: the conversion of the pagan world begins with a reckoning with its monuments.
