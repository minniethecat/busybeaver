## Overview

Lollius is a young [[rome|Roma]]n nobleman of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]] who appears in the retrospective flashback of Part II of Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). He is [[thais|Thaïs]]'s lover in the immediate aftermath of her separation from the Christian community of [[ahmes|Ahmès]] and [[vivantius|Vivantius]]. Noble, beautiful, and wealthy — "Lollius est noble; il est beau, il est puissant" (002-121) — he represents [[thais|Thaïs]]'s first entry into the world of [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n high society. Their affair is brief but transformative, ending when Lollius's mother, Moeroé, intervenes to reclaim her son. Lollius's departure marks the moment [[thais|Thaïs]]'s career as a courtesan truly begins.

## Biography

Lollius enters [[thais|Thaïs]]'s life shortly after her baptism and the execution of [[ahmes|Ahmès]]. She has left the Christian community and entered the cabaret world when Lollius, a young [[rome|Roma]]n of noble birth, takes notice of her. The banquet guests of Part I recall him with a mixture of admiration and pity: "Lollius est noble; il est beau, il est puissant. Quelle folie!" (002-121) — what madness, they exclaim, that Lollius should have thrown himself away on a cabaret girl.

[[thais|Thaïs]] loved Lollius with all the fury of first passion — "avec toutes les fureurs de l'amour" (002-128). But their affair was short-lived. Lollius's mother, Moeroé, came to [[thais|Thaïs]]'s dwelling to reclaim her son with loud cries (002-125). Lollius returned alone to his family house (002-122), and when [[thais|Thaïs]] sought him out, his mother sent her away with a heavy purse (002-127).

For [[thais|Thaïs]], the loss of Lollius was devastating. She no longer recognized the man who had left her; she wondered whether he had been transformed or whether she had never known him at all (002-132). In the aftermath, she sought Lollius in other men — a secret desire to find him in another lover, since she could not reclaim him as himself (002-134). She despaired, finally, of ever recovering what she had felt with him (002-137).

## Character

Lollius is drawn by France in the briefest of strokes, but his effect on [[thais|Thaïs]] is profound. He is the first man [[thais|Thaïs]] loves as an adult — as distinct from the spiritual love she bore [[ahmes|Ahmès]] or the transactional relationships that would follow. His nobility, beauty, and wealth make him the ideal object of romantic desire, but his weakness — his submission to his mother's authority — makes him a figure of pathos rather than villainy.

The dinner guests who recall Lollius's story frame it as folly, but the narrative voice does not judge. Lollius is simply the boy who loved [[thais|Thaïs]] and could not keep her — the first in a series of men who wanted her without being able to hold her.

## Relationships

- **[[Thaïs]]** — Lollius was [[thais|Thaïs]]'s first adult love. Their brief affair marked her transition from Christian neophyte to [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n courtesan. She loved him with consuming intensity and never fully recovered from his loss.
- **Moeroé** — Lollius's mother, who intervened decisively to separate her son from [[thais|Thaïs]], reclaiming him for his family and his class.
- **[[Nicias]]** — Lollius belongs to the same [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]n noble milieu as [[nicias|Nicias]], though they are not depicted together. Both represent the world of privilege that [[thais|Thaïs]] would later inhabit.

## Role in the Narrative

Lollius serves as a narrative hinge in [[thais|Thaïs]]'s autobiography. Before Lollius, she was the baptized child of [[ahmes|Ahmès]]'s tutelage; after Lollius, she is a courtesan who has learned that love — at least the love of [[rome|Roma]]n noblemen — cannot be trusted to last. His departure is the wound that opens her adult life. The sequence of men who follow — [[nicias|Nicias]], [[cotta|Cotta]], the philosophers at the banquet — are all, in a sense, attempts to fill the space Lollius left.
