## Overview

Timaessa is a Carian woman mentioned in an embedded narrative within Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]* (1890). She appears in a parable told by [[antoine|Antoine]] ([[antoine|Saint Anthony]]), relating the story of two brothers — one forced to marry Timaessa, the other consumed by illicit passion for her. The tale serves as an exemplum on the dangers of desire and the path to renunciation.

## Biography

The story of Timaessa unfolds within [[antoine|Antoine]]'s discourse. The elder brother was compelled by their father to marry "une femme carienne nommée Timaessa" — a Carian woman named Timaessa (001-085). The marriage proved disastrous: "elle lui déplaisait si fort qu'il ne put vivre à son côté sans tomber dans une noire mélancolie" — she displeased him so greatly that he could not live beside her without falling into black melancholy.

Compounding the tragedy, Timaessa inspired in the younger brother "un amour criminel" — a criminal love — and this illicit passion quickly transformed into a furious hatred. The parable traces how Timaessa, an unwitting catalyst, set in motion the destruction of an entire household.

The elder brother, fleeing his miserable marriage, withdrew to the desert and became an anchorite. Years later, seeing Timaessa's image in his memory, the narrator understood that the anchorite's emaciated body and wild appearance were the fruits of a life consumed by a single, unresolved domestic catastrophe.

## Character

Timaessa is not a developed character but a narrative device — the object of conflicting male desires whose very presence unravels a family. France presents her through the lens of the parable's teller: she is "une femme carienne" whose defining trait is the misery she inadvertently causes. The story frames her not as an agent but as a catalyst, a figure whose erotic charge sets male passions on a collision course from which only the desert offers escape.
