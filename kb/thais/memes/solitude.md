## Definition

Solitude (*la solitude*) in the Christian monastic tradition is the deliberate withdrawal from human society into the wilderness, practiced by the Desert Fathers of the [[thebaide|Thebaid]] as a means of focusing the soul entirely on God. In Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, solitude is the defining condition of the ascetic life — the desert is chosen "afin de mieux goûter la solitude" (001-001) — but the novel complicates this ideal by revealing solitude as the very medium through which demons operate.

## In the Novel

The novel opens with the desert itself, framing solitude as the default state of the ascetic heroes. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] has lived for decades in isolation among the tombs and ruins of the ancient necropolis. The anchorite Palemon praises this life: "comme toi l'abstinence dans la solitude" (001-079) — abstinence practiced in solitude. For the monks, solitude is not mere isolation but a spiritual instrument: by removing the distractions of the world, the soul becomes a vessel for divine encounter.

Yet the novel systematically subverts this ideal. Solitude, far from being a place of peace, is where [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] is tormented by visions and desires. "La solitude de ce tombeau était plus peuplée que les rues" (003-190) — the solitude of the tomb was more crowded than the streets. The demons that assault him "procèdent de la solitude, en portent les profonds caractères" (003-079): they proceed from solitude and bear its deep marks. Solitude amplifies rather than extinguishes the inner life; what surfaces in the silence is not always divine.

The novel's irony reaches its peak when [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], having returned to the desert after [[thais|Thaïs]]'s death, discovers that solitude has become unbearable. The same silence he once sought as a blessing now terrifies him. The "sources bénies de la solitude" (002-194) — blessed springs of solitude — to which he invited [[thais|Thaïs]] have become poisoned wells for him.
