## Definition

Prayer (*la prière*) in the world of early Christian monasticism was the primary work of the ascetic — the *opus Dei* that replaced manual labour, intellectual inquiry, and social engagement. In Anatole France's *[[thais|Thaïs]]*, prayer functions less as a theological practice than as a narrative barometer: the changing quality of [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s prayers tracks his spiritual trajectory from confident intercessor to desperate supplicant.

## In the Novel

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s first prayer in the novel is a model of ascetic discipline. Disturbed by the memory of [[thais|Thaïs]], he prostrates himself: "S'étant prosterné, il fit cette prière" (001-020). The gesture is formal, controlled, the act of a man who has spent decades mastering the technology of supplication. When he seeks out the stylite at the temple of Serapis, he waits in prayer from noon until sunset: "il se mit à genoux, les mains jointes, à côté de l'inconnu et resta ainsi en prières jusqu'au coucher du soleil" (001-055). Prayer here is endurance, a physical discipline as much as a spiritual one.

The turning point comes in [[alexandrie|Alexandria]]. Standing before [[thais|Thaïs]], [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s prayer shifts from public performance to inward eruption: "[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] se tenait immobile et, voyant combien elle était belle, il faisait du fond du coeur cette prière" (002-164). The phrase "du fond du coeur" — from the depths of his heart — signals a transformation. This is no longer the practiced prayer of the abbot but something involuntary and destabilizing. The prayer that follows is not reported; the narrator only tells us that it happened, that it came from somewhere deeper than discipline.

The arc completes in the novel's final pages. After [[thais|Thaïs]]'s death, [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s prayer becomes indistinguishable from a cry of anguish: "quand vint l'aube, il exhala une prière plus douce qu'une plainte" (003-203) — a prayer sweeter than a lament. The distinction between prayer and complaint, between devotion and despair, has collapsed. The novel's final image of prayer is communal rather than individual: "des femmes voilées récitaient les prières de l'agonie" (003-278) around [[thais|Thaïs]]'s deathbed. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], who began as the confident intercessor, is now outside the frame of prayer entirely — the one who cannot pray, watching others perform the rites he once commanded.

## Monastic Context

In the 4th-century [[egypt|Egypt]]ian desert, prayer was understood as both *opus* (work) and *munus* (gift). The Apophthegmata Patrum record dozens of sayings on prayer, emphasizing two principles: *perseverance* (pray without ceasing) and *purity of intention* (pray without distraction or self-regard). John Cassian's *Conferences* systematized these teachings for Western monasticism. [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] embodies the first principle perfectly — he prays constantly, in every situation — but the novel quietly documents his failure at the second. His prayers for [[thais|Thaïs]] are never free of self-regard; they are always, at some level, prayers for his own sanctity. France's irony is that the most practiced pray-er in the novel ends as the one least able to pray.
