## Definition

Temptation (*la tentation*) in Christian ascetic theology is the spiritual trial orchestrated by demonic forces to induce the faithful to sin. Unlike mere desire, temptation is understood as an external assault — something that happens *to* the ascetic, not something that originates within.

The Desert Fathers developed an elaborate demonology of temptation: each demon specialized in a particular vice, and the anchorite's cell was a battlefield where invisible warfare was waged nightly.

## In the Novel

Temptation structures the novel's psychological arc. It is introduced early as a constant of desert life: demons take on forms "afin de les induire en tentation" (001-005) — to lead the monks into temptation. The monks' resistance is a mark of sanctity: "ils ne succombaient pas à la tentation" (001-006) — they did not succumb to temptation.

But the novel's treatment of temptation deepens dramatically in Part III, where it becomes [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]]'s defining experience. Isolated after [[thais|Thaïs]]'s confinement, he finds himself besieged.

He cries out: "Encore des tentations! Encore des pensées immondes!" (003-126) — More temptations! More foul thoughts! The temptations take the specific form of visions of [[thais|Thaïs]]: "les tentations qu'elle lui donnait" (003-179) — the temptations she gave him.

[[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] attempts to contextualize his suffering: "la pénitence que j'endure est à peine égale aux tentations qui me sont envoyées" (003-114) — the penance I endure is barely equal to the temptations sent to me. Yet unlike Antony, who "évita les pièges du démon" (001-030) — avoided the demon's snares — [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]] does not overcome.

The novel's climax is not a victory over temptation but a surrender to it: "Dieu m'a éprouvé par d'effroyables tentations. Je n'ai point été ravi..." (003-218) — God has tested me with frightful temptations. I was not carried away...

The irony is structural: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], who warned others against temptation, proves the most susceptible. The teacher becomes the cautionary example.

## Theological Background

The 4th-century literature of temptation is dominated by Athanasius's *Life of Antony*, which Anatole France knew well. In that work, Antony's temptations are externalized as demonic assaults — physical beatings, terrifying visions, seductive apparitions — all of which Antony withstands through prayer and ascetic discipline.

France deliberately inverts this template: [[paphnuce|Paphnuce]], who models himself on Antony, encounters temptations that grow stronger the more he resists. The novel's psychological realism (a 19th-century sensibility) transforms the hagiographic demonology of the 4th century into a study of repressed desire.
