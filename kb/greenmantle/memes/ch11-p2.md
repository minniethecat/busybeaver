# Ch. 11: The Companions of the Rosy Hours (cont'd, part 2)

[011-001] It was very clear to me that, unless we could find sanctuary and shed

[011-002] all our various pursuers during this day, we should be done in for good

[011-003] and all. But where on earth were we to find sanctuary? We had neither

[011-004] of us a word of the language, and there was no way I could see of

[011-005] taking on new characters. For that we wanted friends and help, and I

[011-006] could think of none anywhere. Somewhere, to be sure, there was

[011-007] [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]], but how could we get in touch with him? As for [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]], I had

[011-008] pretty well given him up. I always thought his enterprise the craziest

[011-009] of the lot and bound to fail. He was probably somewhere in [[anatolia|Asia Minor]],

[011-010] and a month or two later would get to Constantinople and hear in some

[011-011] pot-house the yarn of the two wretched Dutchmen who had disappeared so

[011-012] soon from men’s sight.

[011-013] That rendezvous at Kuprasso’s was no good. It would have been all right

[011-014] if we had got here unsuspected, and could have gone on quietly

[011-015] frequenting the place till Blenkiron picked us up. But to do that we

[011-016] wanted leisure and secrecy, and here we were with a pack of hounds at

[011-017] our heels. The place was horribly dangerous already. If we showed

[011-018] ourselves there we should be gathered in by [[rasta-bey|Rasta]], or by the German

[011-019] military police, or by the madman in the skin cap. It was a stark

[011-020] impossibility to hang about on the off-chance of meeting Blenkiron.

[011-021] I reflected with some bitterness that this was the 17th day of January,

[011-022] the day of our assignation. I had had high hopes all the way down the

[011-023] [[danube-river|Danube]] of meeting with Blenkiron—for I knew he would be in time—of

[011-024] giving him the information I had had the good fortune to collect, of

[011-025] piecing it together with what he had found out, and of getting the

[011-026] whole story which [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]] hungered for. After that, I thought it

[011-027] wouldn’t be hard to get away by [[rumania|Rumania]], and to get home through

[011-028] [[russia|Russia]]. I had hoped to be back with my battalion in February, having

[011-029] done as good a bit of work as anybody in the war. As it was, it looked

[011-030] as if my information would die with me, unless I could find Blenkiron

[011-031] before the evening.

[011-032] I talked the thing over with [[peter-pienaar|Peter]], and he agreed that we were fairly

[011-033] up against it. We decided to go to Kuprasso’s that afternoon, and to

[011-034] trust to [[fate-and-providence|luck]] for the rest. It wouldn’t do to wander about the streets,

[011-035] so we sat tight in our room all morning, and swopped old hunting yarns

[011-036] to keep our minds from the beastly present. We got some food at

[011-037] midday—cold mutton and the same cheese, and finished our whisky. Then I

[011-038] paid the bill, for I didn’t dare to stay there another night. [[About]]

[011-039] half-past three we went into the street, without the foggiest notion

[011-040] where we would find our next quarters.

[011-041] It was snowing heavily, which was a piece of luck for us. Poor old

[011-042] Peter had no greatcoat, so we went into a Jew’s shop and bought a

[011-043] ready-made abomination, which looked as if it might have been meant for

[011-044] a dissenting parson. It was no good saving my money when the future was

[011-045] so black. The snow made the streets deserted, and we turned down the

[011-046] long lane which led to [[ratchik|Ratchik]] ferry, and found it perfectly quiet. I

[011-047] do not think we met a soul till we got to Kuprasso’s shop.

[011-048] We walked straight through the cafe, which was empty, and down the dark

[011-049] passage, till we were stopped by the garden door. I knocked and it

[011-050] swung open. There was the bleak yard, now puddled with snow, and a

[011-051] blaze of light from the pavilion at the other end. There was a scraping

[011-052] of fiddles, too, and the sound of human talk. We paid the negro at the

[011-053] door, and passed from the bitter afternoon into a garish saloon.

[011-054] There were forty or fifty people there, drinking coffee and sirops and

[011-055] filling the air with the fumes of latakia. Most of them were Turks in

[011-056] European clothes and the fez, but there were some German officers and

[011-057] what looked like German civilians—Army Service Corps clerks, probably,

[011-058] and mechanics from the Arsenal. A woman in cheap finery was tinkling at

[011-059] the piano, and there were several shrill females with the officers.

[011-060] Peter and I sat down modestly in the nearest corner, where old Kuprasso

[011-061] saw us and sent us coffee. A girl who looked like a Jewess came over to

[011-062] us and talked French, but I shook my head and she went off again.

[011-063] Presently a girl came on the stage and danced, a silly affair, all a

[011-064] clashing of tambourines and wriggling. I have seen native women do the

[011-065] same thing better in a Mozambique kraal. Another sang a German song, a

[011-066] simple, sentimental thing about golden hair and rainbows, and the

[011-067] Germans present applauded. The place was so tinselly and common that,

[011-068] coming to it from weeks of rough travelling, it made me impatient. I

[011-069] forgot that, while for the others it might be a vulgar little

[011-070] dancing-hall, for us it was as perilous as a brigands’ den.

[011-071] Peter did not share my mood. He was quite interested in it, as he was

[011-072] interested in everything new. He had a genius for living in the moment.

[011-073] I remember there was a drop-scene on which was daubed a blue lake with

[011-074] very green hills in the distance. As the tobacco smoke grew thicker and

[011-075] the fiddles went on squealing, this tawdry picture began to mesmerize

[011-076] me. I seemed to be looking out of a window at a lovely summer landscape

[011-077] where there were no wars or danger. I seemed to feel the warm sun and

[011-078] to smell the fragrance of blossom from the islands. And then I became

[011-079] aware that a queer scent had stolen into the atmosphere.

[011-080] There were braziers burning at both ends to warm the room, and the thin

[011-081] smoke from these smelt like incense. Somebody had been putting a powder

[011-082] in the flames, for suddenly the place became very quiet. The fiddles

[011-083] still sounded, but far away like an echo. The lights went down, all but

[011-084] a circle on the stage, and into that circle stepped my enemy of the

[011-085] skin cap.

[011-086] He had three others with him. I heard a whisper behind me, and the

[011-087] words were those which Kuprasso had used the day before. These

[011-088] bedlamites were called the Companions of the Rosy Hours, and Kuprasso

[011-089] had promised great dancing.

[011-090] I hoped to goodness they would not see us, for they had fairly given me

[011-091] the horrors. Peter felt the same, and we both made ourselves very small

[011-092] in that dark corner. But the newcomers had no eyes for us.

[011-093] In a twinkling the pavilion changed from a common saloon, which might

[011-094] have been in Chicago or Paris, to a place of mystery—yes, and of

[011-095] beauty. It became the Garden-House of Suliman the Red, whoever that

[011-096] sportsman may have been. Sandy had said that the ends of the earth

[011-097] converged there, and he had been right. I lost all consciousness of my

[011-098] neighbours—stout German, frock-coated Turk, frowsy Jewess—and saw only

[011-099] strange figures leaping in a circle of light, figures that came out of

[011-100] the deepest darkness to make a big magic.

[011-101] The leader flung some stuff into the brazier, and a great fan of blue

[011-102] light flared up. He was weaving circles, and he was singing something

[011-103] shrill and high, whilst his companions made a chorus with their deep

[011-104] monotone. I can’t tell you what the dance was. I had seen the Russian

[011-105] ballet just before the war, and one of the men in it reminded me of

[011-106] this man. But the dancing was the least part of it. It was neither

[011-107] sound nor movement nor scent that wrought the spell, but something far

[011-108] more potent. In an instant I found myself reft away from the present

[011-109] with its dull dangers, and looking at a world all young and fresh and

[011-110] beautiful. The gaudy drop-scene had vanished. It was a window I was

[011-111] looking from, and I was gazing at the finest landscape on earth, lit by

[011-112] the pure clean light of morning.

[011-113] It seemed to be part of the veld, but like no veld I had ever seen. It

[011-114] was wider and wilder and more gracious. Indeed, I was looking at my

[011-115] first youth. I was feeling the kind of immortal light-heartedness which

[011-116] only a boy knows in the dawning of his days. I had no longer any fear

[011-117] of these magic-makers. They were kindly wizards, who had brought me

[011-118] into fairyland.

[011-119] Then slowly from the silence there distilled drops of music. They came

[011-120] like water falling a long way into a cup, each the essential quality of

[011-121] pure sound. We, with our elaborate harmonies, have forgotten the charm

[011-122] of single notes. The African natives know it, and I remember a learned

[011-123] man once telling me that the Greeks had the same art. Those silver

[011-124] bells broke out of infinite space, so exquisite and perfect that no

[011-125] mortal words could have been fitted to them. That was the music, I

[011-126] expect, that the morning stars made when they sang together.

[011-127] Slowly, very slowly, it changed. The glow passed from blue to purple,

[011-128] and then to an angry red. Bit by bit the notes spun together till they

[011-129] had made a harmony—a fierce, restless harmony. And I was conscious

[011-130] again of the skin-clad dancers beckoning out of their circle.

[011-131] There was no mistake about the meaning now. All the daintiness and

[011-132] youth had fled, and passion was beating the air—terrible, savage

[011-133] passion, which belonged neither to day nor night, life nor death, but

[011-134] to the half-world between them. I suddenly felt the dancers as

[011-135] monstrous, inhuman, devilish. The thick scents that floated from the

[011-136] brazier seemed to have a tang of new-shed blood. Cries broke from the

[011-137] hearers—cries of anger and lust and terror. I heard a woman sob, and

[011-138] Peter, who is as tough as any mortal, took tight hold of my arm.

[011-139] I now realized that these Companions of the Rosy Hours were the only

[011-140] thing in the world to fear. Rasta and Stumm seemed feeble simpletons by

[011-141] contrast. The window I had been looking out of was changed to a prison

[011-142] wall—I could see the mortar between the massive blocks. In a second

[011-143] these devils would be smelling out their enemies like some foul

[011-144] witch-doctors. I felt the burning eyes of their leader looking for me

[011-145] in the gloom. Peter was praying audibly beside me, and I could have

[011-146] choked him. His infernal chatter would reveal us, for it seemed to me

[011-147] that there was no one in the place except us and the magic-workers.

[011-148] Then suddenly the spell was broken. The door was flung open and a great

[011-149] gust of icy wind swirled through the hall, driving clouds of ashes from

[011-150] the braziers. I heard loud voices without, and a hubbub began inside.

[011-151] For a moment it was quite dark, and then someone lit one of the flare

[011-152] lamps by the stage. It revealed nothing but the common squalor of a low

[011-153] saloon—white faces, sleepy eyes, and frowsy heads. The drop-piece was

[011-154] there in all its tawdriness.

[011-155] The Companions of the Rosy Hours had gone. But at the door stood men in

[011-156] uniform, I heard a German a long way off murmur, “[[ennver-pasha|Enver]]’s bodyguards,”

[011-157] and I heard him distinctly; for, though I could not see clearly, my

[011-158] hearing was desperately acute. That is often the way when you suddenly

[011-159] come out of a swoon.

[011-160] The place emptied like magic. Turk and German tumbled over each other,

[011-161] while Kuprasso wailed and wept. No one seemed to stop them, and then I

[011-162] saw the reason. Those Guards had come for us. This must be Stumm at

[011-163] last. The authorities had tracked us down, and it was all up with Peter

[011-164] and me.
