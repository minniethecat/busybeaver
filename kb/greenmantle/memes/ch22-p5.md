# Ch. 22: The Guns of the North (cont'd, part 5)

[022-001] But, Great God! what an hour it was! There was loose shooting on our

[022-002] flank, but nothing to trouble us, though the gun team of some Austrian

[022-003] howitzer, struggling madly at a bridge, gave us a bit of a tussle.

[022-004] Everything flitted past me like smoke, or like the mad finale of a

[022-005] dream just before waking. I knew the living movement under me, and the

[022-006] companionship of men, but all dimly, for at heart I was alone,

[022-007] grappling with the realization of a new world. I felt the shadows of

[022-008] the Palantuken glen fading, and the great burst of light as we emerged

[022-009] on the wider valley. Somewhere before us was a pall of smoke seamed

[022-010] with red flames, and beyond the darkness of still higher hills. All

[022-011] that time I was dreaming, crooning daft catches of song to myself, so

[022-012] happy, so deliriously happy that I dared not try to think. I kept

[022-013] muttering a kind of prayer made up of Bible words to Him who had shown

[022-014] me His goodness in the land of the living.

[022-015] But as we drew out from the skirts of the hills and began the long

[022-016] slope to the city, I woke to clear consciousness. I felt the smell of

[022-017] sheepskin and lathered horses, and above all the bitter smell of fire.

[022-018] Down in the trough lay Erzerum, now burning in many places, and from

[022-019] the east, past the silent forts, horsemen were closing in on it. I

[022-020] yelled to my comrades that we were nearest, that we would be first in

[022-021] the city, and they nodded happily and shouted their strange war-cries.

[022-022] As we topped the last ridge I saw below me the van of our charge—a dark

[022-023] mass on the snow—while the broken enemy on both sides were flinging

[022-024] away their arms and scattering in the fields.

[022-025] In the very front, now nearing the city ramparts, was one man. He was

[022-026] like the point of the steel spear soon to be driven home. In the clear

[022-027] morning air I could see that he did not wear the uniform of the

[022-028] invaders. He was turbaned and rode like one possessed, and against the

[022-029] snow I caught the dark sheen of emerald. As he rode it seemed that the

[022-030] fleeing Turks were stricken still, and sank by the roadside with eyes

[022-031] strained after his unheeding figure ...

[022-032] Then I knew that the prophecy had been true, and that their prophet had

[022-033] not failed them. The long-looked for revelation had come. Greenmantle

[022-034] had appeared at last to an awaiting people.
