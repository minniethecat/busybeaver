# Ch. 17: Trouble By the Waters of Babylon (cont'd, part 2)

[017-001] “Do you remember what you said about the Germans being open to bluff?

[017-002] Well, I’m going to put up the steepest sort of bluff. Of course they’ll

[017-003] stop us. [[rasta-bey|Rasta]] will do his damnedest. But remember that he and his

[017-004] friends are not very popular with the Germans, and Madame von Einem is.

[017-005] We’re her proteges, and the bigger the German swell I get before the

[017-006] safer I’ll feel. We’ve got our passports and our orders, and he’ll be a

[017-007] bold man that will stop us once we get into the German zone. Therefore

[017-008] I’m going to hurry as fast as God will let me.”

[017-009] It was a ride that deserved to have an epic written about it. The car

[017-010] was good, and I handled her well, though I say it who shouldn’t. The

[017-011] road in that big central plain was fair, and often I knocked fifty

[017-012] miles an hour out of her. We passed troops by a circuit over the veld,

[017-013] where we took some awful risks, and once we skidded by some transport

[017-014] with our off wheels almost over the lip of a ravine. We went through

[017-015] the narrow streets of Siwas like a fire-engine, while I shouted out in

[017-016] German that we carried despatches for headquarters. We shot out of

[017-017] drizzling rain into brief spells of winter sunshine, and then into a

[017-018] snow blizzard which all but whipped the skin from our faces. And always

[017-019] before us the long road unrolled, with somewhere at the end of it two

[017-020] armies clinched in a death-grapple.

[017-021] That night we looked for no lodging. We ate a sort of meal in the car

[017-022] with the hood up, and felt our way on in the darkness, for the

[017-023] headlights were in perfect order. Then we turned off the road for four

[017-024] hours’ sleep, and I had a go at the map. Before dawn we started again,

[017-025] and came over a pass into the vale of a big river. The winter dawn

[017-026] showed its gleaming stretches, ice-bound among the sprinkled meadows. I

[017-027] called to [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]:

[017-028] “I believe that river is the [[euphrates-river|Euphrates]],” I said.

[017-029] “So,” he said, acutely interested. “Then that’s the waters of Babylon.

[017-030] Great snakes, that I should have lived to see the fields where King

[017-031] Nebuchadnezzar grazed! Do you know the name of that big hill, Major?”

[017-032] “Ararat, as like as not,” I cried, and he believed me.

[017-033] We were among the hills now, great, rocky, black slopes, and, seen

[017-034] through side glens, a hinterland of snowy peaks. I remember I kept

[017-035] looking for the _castrol_ I had seen in my dream. The thing had never

[017-036] left off haunting me, and I was pretty clear now that it did not belong

[017-037] to my South African memories. I am not a superstitious man, but the way

[017-038] that little _kranz_ clung to my mind made me think it was a warning

[017-039] sent by Providence. I was pretty certain that when I clapped eyes on it

[017-040] I would be in for bad trouble.

[017-041] All morning we travelled up that broad vale, and just before noon it

[017-042] spread out wider, the road dipped to the water’s edge, and I saw before

[017-043] me the white roofs of a town. The snow was deep now, and lay down to

[017-044] the riverside, but the sky had cleared, and against a space of blue

[017-045] heaven some peaks to the south rose glittering like jewels. The arches

[017-046] of a bridge, spanning two forks of the stream, showed in front, and as

[017-047] I slowed down at the bend a sentry’s challenge rang out from a

[017-048] block-house. We had reached the fortress of Erzingjan, the headquarters

[017-049] of a Turkish corps and the gate of Armenia.

[017-050] I showed the man our passports, but he did not salute and let us move

[017-051] on. He called another fellow from the guardhouse, who motioned us to

[017-052] keep pace with him as he stumped down a side lane. At the other end was

[017-053] a big barracks with sentries outside. The man spoke to us in Turkish,

[017-054] which Hussin interpreted. There was somebody in that barracks who

[017-055] wanted badly to see us.

[017-056] “By the waters of Babylon we sat down and wept,” quoted Blenkiron

[017-057] softly. “I fear, Major, we’ll soon be remembering Zion.”

[017-058] I tried to persuade myself that this was merely the red tape of a

[017-059] frontier fortress, but I had an instinct that difficulties were in

[017-060] store for us. If Rasta had started wiring I was prepared to put up the

[017-061] brazenest bluff, for we were still eighty miles from Erzerum, and at

[017-062] all costs we were going to be landed there before night.

[017-063] A fussy staff-officer met us at the door. At the sight of us he cried

[017-064] to a friend to come and look.

[017-065] “Here are the birds safe. A fat man and two lean ones and a savage who

[017-066] looks like a Kurd. Call the guard and march them off. There’s no doubt

[017-067] about their identity.”

[017-068] “Pardon me, Sir,” I said, “but we have no time to spare and we’d like

[017-069] to be in Erzerum before the dark. I would beg you to get through any

[017-070] formalities as soon as possible. This man,” and I pointed to the

[017-071] sentry, “has our passports.”

[017-072] “Compose yourself,” he said impudently; “you’re not going on just yet,

[017-073] and when you do it won’t be in a stolen car.” He took the passports and

[017-074] fingered them casually. Then something he saw there made him cock his

[017-075] eyebrows.

[017-076] “Where did you steal these?” he asked, but with less assurance in his

[017-077] tone.

[017-078] I spoke very gently. “You seem to be the victim of a mistake, sir.

[017-079] These are our papers. We are under orders to report ourselves at

[017-080] Erzerum without an hour’s delay. Whoever hinders us will have to answer

[017-081] to [[liman|General von Liman]]. We will be obliged if you will conduct us at once

[017-082] to the Governor.”

[017-083] “You can’t see [[posselt|General Posselt]],” he said; “this is my business. I have

[017-084] a wire from Siwas that four men stole a car belonging to one of [[ennver-pasha|Enver]]

[017-085] Damad’s staff. It describes you all, and says that two of you are

[017-086] notorious spies wanted by the Imperial Government. What have you to say

[017-087] to that?”

[017-088] “Only that it is rubbish. My good Sir, you have seen our passes. Our

[017-089] errand is not to be cried on the housetops, but five minutes with

[017-090] General Posselt will make things clear. You will be exceedingly sorry

[017-091] for it if you delay another minute.”

[017-092] He was impressed in spite of himself, and after pulling his moustache

[017-093] turned on his heel and left us. Presently he came back and said very

[017-094] gruffly that the Governor would see us. We followed him along a

[017-095] corridor into a big room looking out on the river, where an oldish

[017-096] fellow sat in an arm-chair by a stove, writing letters with a fountain

[017-097] pen.

[017-098] This was Posselt, who had been Governor of Erzerum till he fell sick

[017-099] and Ahmed Fevzi took his place. He had a peevish mouth and big blue

[017-100] pouches below his eyes. He was supposed to be a good engineer and to

[017-101] have made Erzerum impregnable, but the look on his face gave me the

[017-102] impression that his reputation at the moment was a bit unstable.

[017-103] The staff-officer spoke to him in an undertone.

[017-104] “Yes, yes, I know,” he said testily. “Are these the men? They look a

[017-105] pretty lot of scoundrels. What’s that you say? They deny it. But

[017-106] they’ve got the car. They can’t deny that. Here, you,” and he fixed on

[017-107] Blenkiron, “who the devil are you?”

[017-108] Blenkiron smiled sleepily at him, not understanding one word, and I

[017-109] took up the parable.

[017-110] “Our passports, Sir, give our credentials,” I said. He glanced through

[017-111] them, and his face lengthened.

[017-112] “They’re right enough. But what about this story of stealing a car?”

[017-113] “It is quite true,” I said, “but I would prefer to use a pleasanter

[017-114] word. You will see from our papers that every authority on the road is

[017-115] directed to give us the best transport. Our own car broke down, and

[017-116] after a long delay we got some wretched horses. It is vitally important

[017-117] that we should be in Erzerum without delay, so I took the liberty of

[017-118] appropriating an empty car we found outside an inn. I am sorry for the

[017-119] discomfort of the owners, but our business was too grave to wait.”

[017-120] “But the telegram says you are notorious spies!”

[017-121] I smiled. “Who sent the telegram?”

[017-122] “I see no reason why I shouldn’t give you his name. It was Rasta Bey.

[017-123] You’ve picked an awkward fellow to make an enemy of.”

[017-124] I did not smile but laughed. “Rasta!” I cried. “He’s one of Enver’s

[017-125] satellites. That explains many things. I should like a word with you

[017-126] alone, Sir.”

[017-127] He nodded to the staff-officer, and when he had gone I put on my most

[017-128] Bible face and looked as important as a provincial mayor at a royal

[017-129] visit.

[017-130] “I can speak freely,” I said, “for I am speaking to a soldier of

[017-131] Germany. There is no love lost between Enver and those I serve. I need

[017-132] not tell you that. This Rasta thought he had found a chance of delaying

[017-133] us, so he invents this trash about spies. Those Comitadjis have spies

[017-134] on the brain ... Especially he hates Frau von Einem.”

[017-135] He jumped at the name.

[017-136] “You have orders from her?” he asked, in a respectful tone.

[017-137] “Why, yes,” I answered, “and those orders will not wait.”

[017-138] He got up and walked to a table, whence he turned a puzzled face on me.

[017-139] “I’m torn in two between the Turks and my own countrymen. If I please

[017-140] one I offend the other, and the result is a damnable confusion. You can

[017-141] go on to Erzerum, but I shall send a man with you to see that you

[017-142] report to headquarters there. I’m sorry, gentlemen, but I’m obliged to

[017-143] take no chances in this business. Rasta’s got a grievance against you,

[017-144] but you can easily hide behind the lady’s skirts. She passed through

[017-145] this town two days ago.”

[017-146] Ten minutes later we were coasting through the slush of the narrow

[017-147] streets with a stolid German lieutenant sitting beside me.

[017-148] The afternoon was one of those rare days when in the pauses of snow you

[017-149] have a spell of weather as mild as May. I remembered several like it

[017-150] during our winter’s training in Hampshire. The road was a fine one,

[017-151] well engineered, and well kept too, considering the amount of traffic.

[017-152] We were little delayed, for it was sufficiently broad to let us pass

[017-153] troops and transport without slackening pace. The fellow at my side was

[017-154] good-humoured enough, but his presence naturally put the lid on our

[017-155] conversation. I didn’t want to talk, however. I was trying to piece

[017-156] together a plan, and making very little of it, for I had nothing to go

[017-157] upon. We must find [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem and [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]], and between us we must

[017-158] wreck the Greenmantle business. That done, it didn’t matter so much

[017-159] what happened to us. As I reasoned it out, the Turks must be in a bad

[017-160] way, and, unless they got a fillip from Greenmantle, would crumple up

[017-161] before the Russians. In the rout I hoped we might get a chance to

[017-162] change our sides. But it was no good looking so far forward; the first

[017-163] thing was to get to Sandy.

[017-164] Now I was still in the mood of reckless bravado which I had got from

[017-165] bagging the car. I did not realize how thin our story was, and how

[017-166] easily Rasta might have a big graft at headquarters. If I had, I would

[017-167] have shot out the German lieutenant long before we got to Erzerum, and

[017-168] found some way of getting mixed up in the ruck of the population.

[017-169] Hussin could have helped me to that. I was getting so confident since

[017-170] our interview with Posselt that I thought I could bluff the whole

[017-171] outfit.
