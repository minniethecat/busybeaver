# Ch. 19: Greenmantle (cont'd, part 2)

[019-001] [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] must have found something uncommon interesting, for he stayed on

[019-002] the roof the better part of the day. It was a dull job for us, since

[019-003] there was no light, and [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] had not even the consolation of a

[019-004] game of Patience. But for all that he was in good spirits, for he had

[019-005] had no dyspepsia since we left Constantinople, and announced that he

[019-006] believed he was at last getting even with his darned duodenum. As for

[019-007] me I was pretty restless, for I could not imagine what was detaining

[019-008] [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]. It was clear that our presence must have been kept secret from

[019-009] [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem, for she was a pal of Stumm’s, and he must by now have

[019-010] blown the gaff on Peter and me. How long could this secrecy last, I

[019-011] asked myself. We had now no sort of protection in the whole outfit.

[019-012] [[rasta-bey|Rasta]] and the Turks wanted our blood: so did Stumm and the Germans; and

[019-013] once the lady found we were deceiving her she would want it most of

[019-014] all. Our only hope was Sandy, and he gave no sign of his existence. I

[019-015] began to fear that with him, too, things had miscarried.

[019-016] And yet I wasn’t really depressed, only impatient. I could never again

[019-017] get back to the beastly stagnation of that Constantinople week. The

[019-018] guns kept me cheerful. There was the devil of a bombardment all day,

[019-019] and the thought that our Allies were thundering there half a dozen

[019-020] miles off gave me a perfectly groundless hope. If they burst through

[019-021] the defence Hilda von Einem and her prophet and all our enemies would

[019-022] be overwhelmed in the deluge. And that blessed chance depended very

[019-023] much on old Peter, now brooding like a pigeon on the house-tops.

[019-024] It was not till the late afternoon that Hussin appeared again. He took

[019-025] no notice of Peter’s absence, but lit a lantern and set it on the

[019-026] table. Then he went to the door and waited. Presently a light step fell

[019-027] on the stairs, and Hussin drew back to let someone enter. He promptly

[019-028] departed and I heard the key turn in the lock behind him.

[019-029] Sandy stood there, but a new Sandy who made Blenkiron and me jump to

[019-030] our feet. The pelts and skin-cap had gone, and he wore instead a long

[019-031] linen tunic clasped at the waist by a broad girdle. A strange green

[019-032] turban adorned his head, and as he pushed it back I saw that his hair

[019-033] had been shaved. He looked like some acolyte—a weary acolyte, for there

[019-034] was no spring in his walk or [[courage-and-honour|nerve]] in his carriage. He dropped numbly

[019-035] on the divan and laid his head in his hands. The lantern showed his

[019-036] haggard eyes with dark lines beneath them.

[019-037] “Good God, old man, have you been sick?” I cried.

[019-038] “Not sick,” he said hoarsely. “My body is right enough, but the last

[019-039] few days I have been living in hell.”

[019-040] Blenkiron nodded sympathetically. That was how he himself would have

[019-041] described the company of the lady.

[019-042] I marched across to him and gripped both his wrists.

[019-043] “Look at me,” I said, “straight in the eyes.”

[019-044] His eyes were like a sleep-walker’s, unwinking, unseeing. “Great

[019-045] heavens, man, you’ve been drugged!” I said.

[019-046] “Drugged,” he cried, with a weary laugh. “Yes, I have been drugged, but

[019-047] not by any physic. No one has been doctoring my food. But you can’t go

[019-048] through hell without getting your eyes red-hot.”

[019-049] I kept my grip on his wrists. “Take your time, old chap, and tell us

[019-050] about it. Blenkiron and I are here, and old Peter’s on the roof not far

[019-051] off. We’ll look after you.”

[019-052] “It does me good to hear your voice, Dick,” he said. “It reminds me of

[019-053] clean, honest things.”

[019-054] “They’ll come back, never fear. We’re at the last lap now. One more

[019-055] spurt and it’s over. You’ve got to tell me what the new snag is. Is it

[019-056] that woman?”

[019-057] He shivered like a frightened colt. “Woman!” he cried. “Does a woman

[019-058] drag a man through the nether-pit? She’s a she-devil. Oh, it isn’t

[019-059] madness that’s wrong with her. She’s as sane as you and as cool as

[019-060] Blenkiron. Her life is an infernal game of chess, and she plays with

[019-061] souls for pawns. She is evil—evil—evil.” And once more he buried his

[019-062] head in his hands.

[019-063] It was Blenkiron who brought sense into this hectic atmosphere. His

[019-064] slow, beloved drawl was an antiseptic against nerves.

[019-065] “Say, boy,” he said, “I feel just like you about the lady. But our job

[019-066] is not to investigate her character. Her Maker will do that good and

[019-067] sure some day. We’ve got to figure how to circumvent her, and for that

[019-068] you’ve got to tell us what exactly’s been occurring since we parted

[019-069] company.”

[019-070] Sandy pulled himself together with a great effort.

[019-071] “Greenmantle died that night I saw you. We buried him secretly by her

[019-072] order in the garden of the villa. Then came the trouble about his

[019-073] successor ... The four Ministers would be no party to a swindle. They

[019-074] were honest men, and vowed that their task now was to make a tomb for

[019-075] their master and pray for the rest of their days at his shrine. They

[019-076] were as immovable as a granite hill and she knew it.... Then they, too,

[019-077] died.”

[019-078] “Murdered?” I gasped.

[019-079] “Murdered ... all four in one morning. I do not know how, but I helped

[019-080] to bury them. Oh, she had Germans and Kurds to do her foul work, but

[019-081] their hands were clean compared to hers. Pity me, Dick, for I have seen

[019-082] honesty and virtue put to the shambles and have abetted the deed when

[019-083] it was done. It will haunt me to my dying day.”

[019-084] I did not stop to console him, for my mind was on fire with his news.

[019-085] “Then the prophet is gone, and the humbug is over,” I cried.

[019-086] “The prophet still lives. She has found a successor.”

[019-087] He stood up in his linen tunic.

[019-088] “Why do I wear these clothes? Because I am Greenmantle. I am the

[019-089] _Kaába-i-hurriyeh_ for all Islam. In three days’ time I will reveal

[019-090] myself to my people and wear on my breast the green ephod of the

[019-091] prophet.”

[019-092] He broke off with an hysterical laugh. “Only you see, I won’t. I will

[019-093] cut my throat first.”

[019-094] “Cheer up!” said Blenkiron soothingly. “We’ll find some prettier way

[019-095] than that.”

[019-096] “There is no way,” he said; “no way but death. We’re done for, all of

[019-097] us. Hussin got you out of Stumm’s clutches, but you’re in danger every

[019-098] moment. At the best you have three days, and then you, too, will be

[019-099] dead.”

[019-100] I had no words to reply. This change in the bold and unshakeable Sandy

[019-101] took my breath away.

[019-102] “She made me her accomplice,” he went on. “I should have killed her on

[019-103] the graves of those innocent men. But instead I did all she asked and

[019-104] joined in her game ... She was very candid, you know ... She cares no

[019-105] more than [[ennver-pasha|Enver]] for the faith of Islam. She can laugh at it. But she

[019-106] has her own dreams, and they consume her as a saint is consumed by his

[019-107] devotion. She has told me them, and if the day in the garden was hell,

[019-108] the days since have been the innermost fires of Tophet. I think—it is

[019-109] horrible to say it—that she has got some kind of crazy liking for me.

[019-110] When we have reclaimed [[eastern-front|the East]] I am to be by her side when she rides

[019-111] on her milk-white horse into Jerusalem ... And there have been

[019-112] moments—only moments, I swear to God—when I have been fired myself by

[019-113] her madness ...”

[019-114] Sandy’s figure seemed to shrink and his voice grew shrill and wild. It

[019-115] was too much for Blenkiron. He indulged in a torrent of blasphemy such

[019-116] as I believe had never before passed his lips.

[019-117] “I’m blessed if I’ll listen to this God-darned stuff. It isn’t

[019-118] delicate. You get busy, Major, and pump some sense into your afflicted

[019-119] friend.”

[019-120] I was beginning to see what had happened. Sandy was a man of genius—as

[019-121] much as anybody I ever struck—but he had the defects of such

[019-122] high-strung, fanciful souls. He would take more than mortal risks, and

[019-123] you couldn’t scare him by any ordinary terror. But let his old

[019-124] conscience get cross-eyed, let him find himself in some situation which

[019-125] in his eyes involved his honour, and he might go stark crazy. The

[019-126] woman, who roused in me and Blenkiron only hatred, could catch his

[019-127] imagination and stir in him—for the moment only—an unwilling response.

[019-128] And then came bitter and morbid repentance, and the last desperation.

[019-129] It was no time to mince matters. “Sandy, you old fool,” I cried, “be

[019-130] thankful you have friends to keep you from playing the fool. You saved

[019-131] my life at [[loos|Loos]], and I’m jolly well going to get you through this show.

[019-132] I’m bossing the outfit now, and for all your confounded prophetic

[019-133] manners, you’ve got to take your orders from me. You aren’t going to

[019-134] reveal yourself to your people, and still less are you going to cut

[019-135] your throat. Greenmantle will avenge the murder of his ministers, and

[019-136] make that bedlamite woman sorry she was born. We’re going to get clear

[019-137] away, and inside of a week we’ll be having tea with [[grand-duke-nicholas|the Grand Duke]]

[019-138] Nicholas.”

[019-139] I wasn’t bluffing. Puzzled as I was about ways and means I had still

[019-140] the blind belief that we should win out. And as I spoke two legs

[019-141] dangled through the trap and a dusty and blinking Peter descended in

[019-142] our midst.

[019-143] I took the maps from him and spread them on the table.

[019-144] “First, you must know that we’ve had an almighty piece of [[fate-and-providence|luck]]. Last

[019-145] night Hussin took us for a walk over the roofs of Erzerum, and by the

[019-146] blessing of Providence I got into Stumm’s room, and bagged his staff

[019-147] map ... Look there ... d’you see his notes? That’s the danger-point of

[019-148] the whole defence. Once the Russians get that fort, [[kara-gubek|Kara Gubek]], they’ve

[019-149] turned the main position. And it can be got; Stumm knows it can; for

[019-150] these two adjacent hills are not held ... It looks a mad enterprise on

[019-151] paper, but Stumm knows that it is possible enough. The question is:

[019-152] Will the Russians guess that? I say no, not unless someone tells them.

[019-153] Therefore, by hook or by crook, we’ve got to get that information

[019-154] through to them.”

[019-155] Sandy’s interest in ordinary things was beginning to flicker up again.

[019-156] He studied the map and began to measure distances.

[019-157] “Peter’s going to have a try for it. He thinks there’s a sporting

[019-158] chance of his getting through the lines. If he does—if he gets this map

[019-159] to the Grand Duke’s staff—then Stumm’s goose is cooked. In three days

[019-160] the Cossacks will be in the streets of Erzerum.”

[019-161] “What are the chances?” Sandy asked.

[019-162] I glanced at Peter. “We’re hard-bitten fellows and can face the truth.

[019-163] I think the chances against success are about five to one.”

[019-164] “Two to one,” said Peter modestly. “Not worse than that. I don’t think

[019-165] you’re fair to me, Dick, my old friend.”

[019-166] I looked at that lean, tight figure and the gentle, resolute face, and

[019-167] I changed my mind. “I’m hanged if I think there are any odds,” I said.

[019-168] “With anybody else it would want a miracle, but with Peter I believe

[019-169] the chances are level.”

[019-170] “Two to one,” Peter persisted. “If it was evens I wouldn’t be

[019-171] interested.”

[019-172] “Let me go,” Sandy cried. “I talk the lingo, and can pass as a Turk,

[019-173] and I’m a million times likelier to get through. For God’s sake, Dick,

[019-174] let me go.”

[019-175] “Not you. You’re wanted here. If you disappear the whole show’s busted

[019-176] too soon, and the three of us left behind will be strung up before

[019-177] morning ... No, my son. You’re going to escape, but it will be in

[019-178] company with Blenkiron and me. We’ve got to blow the whole Greenmantle

[019-179] business so high that the bits of it will never come to earth again ...

[019-180] First, tell me how many of your fellows will stick by you? I mean the

[019-181] Companions.”
