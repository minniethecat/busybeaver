# Kuprasso

> "You are Mr Kuprasso," I said. "I wanted to show that I believed that what I have heard about you is true. They tell me you are the greatest judge of dancing girls in all Constantinople." [010-124]

## Overview

Angelo Kuprasso is the proprietor of a dilapidated café in Constantinople and a minor but memorable figure in the novel's middle section. He runs the "Pavilion" — formerly the "Garden of the Rosy Hours" — a venue that serves as Hannay's entry point into the Constantinople underworld. A figure of faded grandeur and comic anxiety, Kuprasso provides both local intelligence and a rare moment of levity in an otherwise tense narrative.

## Role in the Story

### The Café Owner

Kuprasso first appears when Hannay seeks out his establishment: "a tumble-down coffee house, with A. Kuprasso above the door in a flourishing hand" [010-113]. The description is immediately evocative of decline — a once-grand establishment now reduced to shabbiness. The name "A. Kuprasso" written in "a flourishing hand" [010-113] hints at pretensions to elegance that circumstances no longer support.

Hannay's approach is characteristically direct: "I tried to speak to Mr Kuprasso. He paid no attention, so I sat down" [010-118]. The indignity of being ignored in his own establishment sets Kuprasso's tone — a man perpetually overmatched by the forces swirling around him.

### The Pavilion and Its History

Kuprasso's establishment has a history that predates and exceeds its present owner. "Behold the Pavilion," Kuprasso says proudly [010-149], but Hannay's question cuts through the bravado: "Tell me, Mr Kuprasso, do you ever open it now?" [010-151] — the implication being that the Pavilion, like its owner, is past its prime.

The café's true significance emerges when the narrative reveals its connection to the "Companions of the Rosy Hours" — a secret society or underground network that used Kuprasso's establishment as a meeting point. The words Hannay hears are "those which Kuprasso had used the day before. These were the words of the Companions of the Rosy Hours, and Kuprasso was a member" [011-087]. This revelation transforms Kuprasso from comic relief to covert operative — or at least, covertly adjacent.

### The Raid and Its Aftermath

When Hannay and Peter Pienaar's meeting at Kuprasso's is compromised, the café owner's response is characteristic: "Kuprasso wailed and wept" [011-161]. His fear is genuine, and his position as a marginal figure caught between competing powers makes his panic understandable. The rendezvous plan at his establishment is abandoned: "That rendezvous at Kuprasso's was no good. It would have been asking for trouble" [011-013].

Blenkiron and Hannay decide to try again: "We decided to go to Kuprasso's that afternoon" [011-033], and they manage to reach "Kuprasso's shop" [011-047], but the earlier raid has clearly compromised the location's utility.

## Character Traits

### Comic Relief in a Spy Thriller

Kuprasso's function is partly tonal. In a novel dominated by mortal stakes, his self-importance, his faded establishment, and his theatrical panic provide a moment of almost comic relief. The gap between his self-presentation — "the greatest judge of dancing girls in all Constantinople" [010-124] — and his actual circumstances is the stuff of gentle satire rather than condemnation.

### The Marginal Man

Kuprasso also represents the position of the ethnic Greek in late Ottoman Constantinople — a figure who operates in the interstices of empire, serving whichever power holds the immediate advantage. His Greek name and Ottoman location place him between worlds, a position that makes him useful to Hannay's intelligence work but also leaves him perpetually vulnerable.

### Knowledge and Fear

Kuprasso knows things — the passwords of the Rosy Hours, the rhythms of Constantinople's underground — but his knowledge is accompanied by terror. Unlike the novel's operatives, who move with practiced confidence through hostile environments, Kuprasso "wailed and wept" [011-161] when danger arrives. He is an informant by circumstance rather than conviction, and his fear humanizes the intelligence-gathering that the novel otherwise presents as glamorous.

## Relationships

- **Richard Hannay**: Hannay flatters Kuprasso to gain access [010-124], and the café owner responds to flattery. The relationship is instrumental on both sides.

- **Peter Pienaar**: Pienaar uses Kuprasso's café as a rendezvous point [011-033], and Kuprasso presumably recognizes him as part of the Rosy Hours network.

- **The Companions of the Rosy Hours**: Kuprasso is revealed as a member [011-087], though the extent of his involvement remains ambiguous. His use of their recognition phrases suggests at least peripheral participation.

## Significance

Kuprasso is a minor character who illuminates a major theme: the vulnerability of civilians caught in the machinery of espionage and war. He is neither hero nor villain, neither patriot nor traitor — he is a businessman trying to survive in a collapsing empire, and his café, like his person, bears the marks of that precarity. In a novel otherwise populated by larger-than-life figures, Kuprasso's smallness is itself a kind of truth.
