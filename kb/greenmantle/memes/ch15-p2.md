# Ch. 15: An Embarrassed Toilet (cont'd, part 2)

[015-001] “He was the man who brought you this?” she asked, pointing to the

[015-002] envelope on the table which bore the big blue stamp of the Ministry of

[015-003] War.

[015-004] “The same,” I said. “I’m not perfectly sure of his name, but I think

[015-005] they call him [[rasta-bey|Rasta]].”

[015-006] Not a flicker of a smile crossed her face, but I had a feeling that the

[015-007] news pleased her.

[015-008] “Did he thwart you?” she asked.

[015-009] “Why, yes. He thwarted me some. His head is a bit swelled, and an hour

[015-010] or two on the shelf will do him good.”

[015-011] “He is a powerful man,” she said, “a jackal of [[ennver-pasha|Enver]]’s. You have made a

[015-012] dangerous enemy.”

[015-013] “I don’t value him at two cents,” said I, though I thought grimly that

[015-014] as far as I could see the value of him was likely to be about the price

[015-015] of my neck.

[015-016] “Perhaps you are right,” she said with serious eyes. “In these days no

[015-017] enemy is dangerous to a bold man. I have come tonight, Mr Hanau, to

[015-018] talk business with you, as they say in your country. I have heard well

[015-019] of you, and today I have seen you. I may have need of you, and you

[015-020] assuredly will have need of me....”

[015-021] She broke off, and again her strange potent eyes fell on my face. They

[015-022] were like a burning searchlight which showed up every cranny and crack

[015-023] of the soul. I felt it was going to be horribly difficult to act a part

[015-024] under that compelling gaze. She could not mesmerize me, but she could

[015-025] strip me of my fancy dress and set me naked in the masquerade.

[015-026] “What came you forth to seek?” she asked. “You are not like the stout

[015-027] American [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]], a lover of shoddy power and a devotee of a feeble

[015-028] science. There is something more than that in your face. You are on our

[015-029] side, but you are not of the Germans with their hankerings for a rococo

[015-030] Empire. You come from America, the land of pious follies, where men

[015-031] worship gold and words. I ask, what came you forth to seek?”

[015-032] As she spoke I seemed to get a vision of a figure, like one of the old

[015-033] gods looking down on human nature from a great height, a figure

[015-034] disdainful and passionless, but with its own magnificence. It kindled

[015-035] my imagination, and I answered with the stuff I had often cogitated

[015-036] when I had tried to explain to myself just how a case could be made out

[015-037] against the Allied cause.

[015-038] “I will tell you, Madam,” I said. “I am a man who has followed a

[015-039] science, but I have followed it in wild places, and I have gone through

[015-040] it and come out at the other side. The world, as I see it, had become

[015-041] too easy and cushioned. Men had forgotten their manhood in soft speech,

[015-042] and imagined that the rules of their smug civilization were the laws of

[015-043] the universe. But that is not the teaching of science, and it is not

[015-044] the teaching of life. We have forgotten the greater virtues, and we

[015-045] were becoming emasculated humbugs whose gods were our own weaknesses.

[015-046] Then came war, and the air was cleared. Germany, in spite of her

[015-047] blunders and her grossness, stood forth as the scourge of cant. She had

[015-048] the [[courage-and-honour|courage]] to cut through the bonds of humbug and to laugh at the

[015-049] fetishes of the herd. Therefore I am on Germany’s side. But I came here

[015-050] for another reason. I know nothing of [[eastern-front|the East]], but as I read history

[015-051] it is from the desert that the purification comes. When mankind is

[015-052] smothered with shams and phrases and painted idols a wind blows out of

[015-053] the wild to cleanse and simplify life. The world needs space and fresh

[015-054] air. The civilization we have boasted of is a toy-shop and a blind

[015-055] alley, and I hanker for the open country.”

[015-056] This confounded nonsense was well received. Her pale eyes had the cold

[015-057] light of the fanatic. With her bright hair and the long exquisite oval

[015-058] of her face she looked like some destroying fury of a Norse legend. At

[015-059] that moment I think I first really feared her; before I had half-hated

[015-060] and half-admired. Thank Heaven, in her absorption she did not notice

[015-061] that I had forgotten the speech of Cleveland, Ohio.

[015-062] “You are of the Household of Faith,” she said. “You will presently

[015-063] learn many things, for the Faith marches to victory. Meantime I have

[015-064] one word for you. You and your companion travel eastward.”

[015-065] “We go to [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]],” I said. “I reckon these are our passports,” and

[015-066] I pointed to the envelope.

[015-067] She picked it up, opened it, and then tore it in pieces and tossed it

[015-068] in the fire.

[015-069] “The orders are countermanded,” she said. “I have need of you and you

[015-070] go with me. Not to the flats of the Tigris, but to the great hills.

[015-071] Tomorrow you will receive new passports.”

[015-072] She gave me her hand and turned to go. At the threshold she paused, and

[015-073] looked towards the oak cupboard. “Tomorrow I will relieve you of your

[015-074] prisoner. He will be safer in my hands.”

[015-075] She left me in a condition of pretty blank bewilderment. We were to be

[015-076] tied to the chariot-wheels of this fury, and started on an enterprise

[015-077] compared to which fighting against our friends at [[kut|Kut]] seemed tame and

[015-078] reasonable. On the other hand, I had been spotted by Rasta, and had got

[015-079] the envoy of the most powerful man in Constantinople locked in a

[015-080] cupboard. At all costs we had to keep Rasta safe, but I was very

[015-081] determined that he should not be handed over to the lady. I was going

[015-082] to be no party to cold-blooded murder, which I judged to be her

[015-083] expedient. It was a pretty kettle of fish, but in the meantime I must

[015-084] have food, for I had eaten nothing for nine hours. So I went in search

[015-085] of [[peter-pienaar|Peter]].

[015-086] I had scarcely begun my long deferred meal when [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] entered. He was

[015-087] before his time, and he looked as solemn as a sick owl. I seized on him

[015-088] as a drowning man clutches a spar.

[015-089] He heard my story of Rasta with a lengthening face.

[015-090] “That’s bad,” he said. “You say he spotted you, and your subsequent

[015-091] doings of course would not disillusion him. It’s an infernal nuisance,

[015-092] but there’s only one way out of it. I must put him in charge of my own

[015-093] people. They will keep him safe and sound till he’s wanted. Only he

[015-094] mustn’t see me.” And he went out in a hurry.

[015-095] I fetched Rasta from his prison. He had come to his senses by this

[015-096] time, and lay regarding me with stony, malevolent eyes.

[015-097] “I’m very sorry, Sir,” I said, “for what has happened. But you left me

[015-098] no alternative. I’ve got a big job on hand and I can’t have it

[015-099] interfered with by you or anyone. You’re paying the price of a

[015-100] suspicious nature. When you know a little more you’ll want to apologize

[015-101] to me. I’m going to see that you are kept quiet and comfortable for a

[015-102] day or two. You’ve no cause to worry, for you’ll suffer no harm. I give

[015-103] you my word of honour as an American citizen.”

[015-104] Two of Sandy’s miscreants came in and bore him off, and presently Sandy

[015-105] himself returned. When I asked him where he was being taken, Sandy said

[015-106] he didn’t know. “They’ve got their orders, and they’ll carry them out

[015-107] to the letter. There’s a big unknown area in Constantinople to hide a

[015-108] man, into which the _Khafiyeh_ never enter.”

[015-109] Then he flung himself in a chair and lit his old pipe.

[015-110] “Dick,” he said, “this job is getting very difficult and very dark. But

[015-111] my knowledge has grown in the last few days. I’ve found out the meaning

[015-112] of the second word that [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] [[sir-walter-bullivant|Bullivant]] scribbled.”

[015-113] “_Cancer_?” I asked.

[015-114] “Yes. It means just what it reads and no more. Greenmantle is dying—has

[015-115] been dying for months. This afternoon they brought a German doctor to

[015-116] see him, and the man gave him a few hours of life. By now he may be

[015-117] dead.”

[015-118] The news was a staggerer. For a moment I thought it cleared up things.

[015-119] “Then that busts the show,” I said. “You can’t have a crusade without a

[015-120] prophet.”

[015-121] “I wish I thought it did. It’s the end of one stage, but the start of a

[015-122] new and blacker one. Do you think that woman will be beaten by such a

[015-123] small thing as the death of her prophet? She’ll find a substitute—one

[015-124] of the four Ministers, or someone else. She’s a devil incarnate, but

[015-125] she has the soul of a Napoleon. The big danger is only beginning.”

[015-126] Then he told me the story of his recent doings. He had found out the

[015-127] house of Frau von Einem without much trouble, and had performed with

[015-128] his ragamuffins in the servants’ quarters. The prophet had a large

[015-129] retinue, and the fame of his minstrels—for the Companions were known

[015-130] far and wide in the land of Islam—came speedily to the ears of the Holy

[015-131] Ones. Sandy, a leader in this most orthodox coterie, was taken into

[015-132] favour and brought to the notice of the four Ministers. He and his

[015-133] half-dozen retainers became inmates of the villa, and Sandy, from his

[015-134] knowledge of Islamic lore and his ostentatious piety, was admitted to

[015-135] the confidence of the household. Frau von Einem welcomed him as an

[015-136] ally, for the Companions had been the most devoted propagandists of the

[015-137] new revelation.

[015-138] As he described it, it was a strange business. Greenmantle was dying

[015-139] and often in great pain, but he struggled to meet the demands of his

[015-140] protectress. The four Ministers, as Sandy saw them, were unworldly

[015-141] ascetics; the prophet himself was a saint, though a practical saint

[015-142] with some notions of policy; but the controlling brain and will were

[015-143] those of the lady. Sandy seemed to have won his favour, even his

[015-144] affection. He spoke of him with a kind of desperate pity.

[015-145] “I never saw such a man. He is the greatest gentleman you can picture,

[015-146] with a dignity like a high mountain. He is a dreamer and a poet, too—a

[015-147] genius if I can judge these things. I think I can assess him rightly,

[015-148] for I know something of the soul of the East, but it would be too long

[015-149] a story to tell now. The West knows nothing of the true Oriental. It

[015-150] pictures him as lapped in colour and idleness and luxury and gorgeous

[015-151] dreams. But it is all wrong. The _Kaf_ he yearns for is an austere

[015-152] thing. It is the austerity of the East that is its beauty and its

[015-153] terror ... It always wants the same things at the back of its head. The

[015-154] Turk and the Arab came out of big spaces, and they have the desire of

[015-155] them in their bones. They settle down and stagnate, and by the by they

[015-156] degenerate into that appalling subtlety which is their ruling passion

[015-157] gone crooked. And then comes a new revelation and a great simplifying.

[015-158] They want to live face to face with God without a screen of ritual and

[015-159] images and priestcraft. They want to prune life of its foolish fringes

[015-160] and get back to the noble bareness of the desert. Remember, it is

[015-161] always the empty desert and the empty sky that cast their spell over

[015-162] them—these, and the hot, strong, antiseptic sunlight which burns up all

[015-163] rot and decay. It isn’t inhuman. It’s the humanity of one part of the

[015-164] human race. It isn’t ours, it isn’t as good as ours, but it’s jolly

[015-165] good all the same. There are times when it grips me so hard that I’m

[015-166] inclined to forswear the gods of my fathers!

[015-167] “Well, Greenmantle is the prophet of this great simplicity. He speaks

[015-168] straight to the heart of Islam, and it’s an honourable message. But for

[015-169] our sins it’s been twisted into part of that damned German propaganda.

[015-170] His unworldliness has been used for a cunning political move, and his

[015-171] creed of space and simplicity for the furtherance of the last word in

[015-172] human degeneracy. My God, Dick, it’s like seeing St Francis run by

[015-173] Messalina.”
