# Ch. 21: The Little Hill

[021-001] The thought of a chapel and a bell gave him the notion of some human

[021-002] agency. And then suddenly the notion was confirmed. The sound was

[021-003] regular and concerted—dot, dash, dot—dash, dot, dot. The branch of a

[021-004] tree and the wind may play strange pranks, but they do not produce the

[021-005] longs and shorts of the Morse Code.

[021-006] This was where [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]’s [[espionage|intelligence work]] in the Boer War helped him. He

[021-007] knew the Morse, he could read it, but he could make nothing of the

[021-008] signalling. It was either in some special code or in a strange

[021-009] language.

[021-010] He lay still and did some calm thinking. There was a man in front of

[021-011] him, a Turkish soldier, who was in the enemy’s pay. Therefore he could

[021-012] fraternize with him, for they were on the same side. But how was he to

[021-013] approach him without getting shot in the process? Again, how could a

[021-014] man send signals to the enemy from a firing-line without being

[021-015] detected? Peter found an answer in the strange configuration of the

[021-016] ground. He had not heard a sound until he was a few yards from the

[021-017] place, and they would be inaudible to men in the reserve trenches and

[021-018] even in the communication trenches. If somebody moving up the latter

[021-019] caught the noise, it would be easy to explain it naturally. But the

[021-020] wind blowing down the cup would carry it far in the enemy’s direction.

[021-021] There remained the risk of being heard by those parallel with the bell

[021-022] in the firing trenches. Peter concluded that that trench must be very

[021-023] thinly held, probably only by a few observers, and the nearest might be

[021-024] a dozen yards off. He had read about that being the French fashion

[021-025] under a big bombardment.

[021-026] The next thing was to find out how to make himself known to this ally.

[021-027] He decided that the only way was to surprise him. He might get shot,

[021-028] but he trusted to his strength and agility against a man who was almost

[021-029] certainly wearied. When he had got him safe, explanations might follow.

[021-030] Peter was now enjoying himself hugely. If only those infernal guns kept

[021-031] silent he would play out the game in the sober, decorous way he loved.

[021-032] So very delicately he began to wriggle forward to where the sound was.

[021-033] The night was now as black as ink around him, and very quiet, too,

[021-034] except for soughings of the dying gale. The snow had drifted a little

[021-035] in the lee of the ruined walls, and Peter’s progress was naturally very

[021-036] slow. He could not afford to dislodge one ounce of snow. Still the

[021-037] tinkling went on, now in greater volume. Peter was in terror lest it

[021-038] should cease before he got his man.

[021-039] Presently his hand clutched at empty space. He was on the lip of the

[021-040] front trench. The sound was now a yard to his right, and with infinite

[021-041] care he shifted his position. Now the bell was just below him, and he

[021-042] felt the big rafter of the woodwork from which it had fallen. He felt

[021-043] something else—a stretch of wire fixed in the ground with the far end

[021-044] hanging in the void. That would be the spy’s explanation if anyone

[021-045] heard the sound and came seeking the cause.

[021-046] Somewhere in the darkness before him and below was the man, not a yard

[021-047] off. Peter remained very still, studying the situation. He could not

[021-048] see, but he could feel the presence, and he was trying to decide the

[021-049] relative position of the man and bell and their exact distance from

[021-050] him. The thing was not so easy as it looked, for if he jumped for where

[021-051] he believed the figure was, he might miss it and get a bullet in the

[021-052] stomach. A man who played so risky a game was probably handy with his

[021-053] firearms. Besides, if he should hit the bell, he would make a hideous

[021-054] row and alarm the whole front.

[021-055] Fate suddenly gave him the right chance. The unseen figure stood up and

[021-056] moved a step, till his back was against the parados. He actually

[021-057] brushed against Peter’s elbow, who held his breath.

[021-058] There is a catch that the Kaffirs have which would need several

[021-059] diagrams to explain. It is partly a neck hold, and partly a paralysing

[021-060] backward twist of the right arm, but if it is practised on a man from

[021-061] behind, it locks him as sure as if he were handcuffed. Peter slowly got

[021-062] his body raised and his knees drawn under him, and reached for his

[021-063] prey.

[021-064] He got him. A head was pulled backward over the edge of the trench, and

[021-065] he felt in the air the motion of the left arm pawing feebly but unable

[021-066] to reach behind.

[021-067] “Be still,” whispered Peter in German; “I mean you no harm. We are

[021-068] friends of the same purpose. Do you speak German?”

[021-069] “_Nein_,” said a muffled voice.

[021-070] “English?”

[021-071] “Yes,” said the voice.

[021-072] “Thank God,” said Peter. “Then we can understand each other. I’ve

[021-073] watched your notion of signalling, and a very good one it is. I’ve got

[021-074] to get through to the Russian lines somehow before morning, and I want

[021-075] you to help me. I’m English—a kind of English, so we’re on the same

[021-076] side. If I let go your neck, will you be good and talk reasonably?”

[021-077] The voice assented. Peter let go, and in the same instant slipped to

[021-078] the side. The man wheeled round and flung out an arm but gripped

[021-079] vacancy.

[021-080] “Steady, friend,” said Peter; “you mustn’t play tricks with me or I’ll

[021-081] be angry.”

[021-082] “Who are you? Who sent you?” asked the puzzled voice.

[021-083] Peter had a happy thought. “The Companions of the Rosy Hours,” he said.

[021-084] “Then are we friends indeed,” said the voice. “Come out of the

[021-085] darkness, friend, and I will do you no harm. I am a good Turk, and I

[021-086] fought beside the English in Kordofan and learned their tongue. I live

[021-087] only to see the ruin of [[ennver-pasha|Enver]], who has beggared my family and slain my

[021-088] twin brother. Therefore I serve the _Muscov ghiaours_.”

[021-089] “I don’t know what the Musky Jaws are, but if you mean the Russians I’m

[021-090] with you. I’ve got news for them which will make Enver green. The

[021-091] question is, how I’m to get to them, and that is where you shall help

[021-092] me, my friend.”

[021-093] “How?”

[021-094] “By playing that little tune of yours again. Tell them to expect within

[021-095] the next half-hour a deserter with an important message. Tell them, for

[021-096] God’s sake, not to fire at anybody till they’ve made certain it isn’t

[021-097] me.”

[021-098] The man took the blunt end of his bayonet and squatted beside the bell.

[021-099] The first stroke brought out a clear, searching note which floated down

[021-100] the valley. He struck three notes at slow intervals. For all the world,

[021-101] Peter said, he was like a telegraph operator calling up a station.

[021-102] “Send the message in English,” said Peter.

[021-103] “They may not understand it,” said the man.

[021-104] “Then send it any way you like. I trust you, for we are brothers.”

[021-105] After ten minutes the man ceased and listened. From far away came the

[021-106] sound of a trench-gong, the kind of thing they used on the Western

[021-107] Front to give the gas-alarm.

[021-108] “They say they will be ready,” he said. “I cannot take down messages in

[021-109] the darkness, but they have given me the signal which means ‘Consent’.”

[021-110] “Come, that is pretty good,” said Peter. “And now I must be moving. You

[021-111] take a hint from me. When you hear big firing up to the north get ready

[021-112] to beat a quick retreat, for it will be all up with that city of yours.

[021-113] And tell your folk, too, that they’re making a bad mistake letting

[021-114] those fool Germans rule their land. Let them hang Enver and his little

[021-115] friends, and we’ll be happy once more.”

[021-116] “May Satan receive his soul!” said the Turk. “There is wire before us,

[021-117] but I will show you a way through. The guns this evening made many

[021-118] rents in it. But haste, for a working party may be here presently to

[021-119] repair it. Remember there is much wire before the other lines.”

[021-120] Peter, with certain directions, found it pretty easy to make his way

[021-121] through the entanglement. There was one bit which scraped a hole in his

[021-122] back, but very soon he had come to the last posts and found himself in

[021-123] open country. The place, he said, was a graveyard of the unburied dead

[021-124] that smelt horribly as he crawled among them. He had no inducements to

[021-125] delay, for he thought he could hear behind him the movement of the

[021-126] Turkish working party, and was in terror that a flare might reveal him

[021-127] and a volley accompany his retreat.

[021-128] From one shell-hole to another he wormed his way, till he struck an old

[021-129] ruinous communication trench which led in the right direction. The

[021-130] Turks must have been forced back in the past week, and the Russians

[021-131] were now in the evacuated trenches. The thing was half full of water,

[021-132] but it gave Peter a feeling of safety, for it enabled him to get his

[021-133] head below the level of the ground. Then it came to an end and he found

[021-134] before him a forest of wire.

[021-135] The Turk in his signal had mentioned half an hour, but Peter thought it

[021-136] was nearer two hours before he got through that noxious entanglement.

[021-137] Shelling had made little difference to it. The uprights were all there,

[021-138] and the barbed strands seemed to touch the ground. Remember, he had no

[021-139] wire-cutter; nothing but his bare hands. Once again fear got hold of

[021-140] him. He felt caught in a net, with monstrous vultures waiting to pounce

[021-141] on him from above. At any moment a flare might go up and a dozen rifles

[021-142] find their mark. He had altogether forgotten about the message which

[021-143] had been sent, for no message could dissuade the ever-present death he

[021-144] felt around him. It was, he said, like following an old lion into bush

[021-145] when there was but one narrow way in, and no road out.

[021-146] The guns began again—the Turkish guns from behind the ridge—and a shell

[021-147] tore up the wire a short way before him. Under cover of the burst he

[021-148] made good a few yards, leaving large portions of his clothing in the

[021-149] strands. Then, quite suddenly, when hope had almost died in his heart,

[021-150] he felt the ground rise steeply. He lay very still, a star-rocket from

[021-151] the Turkish side lit up the place, and there in front was a rampart

[021-152] with the points of bayonets showing beyond it. It was the Russian hour

[021-153] for stand-to.

[021-154] He raised his cramped limbs from the ground and shouted “Friend!

[021-155] English!”

[021-156] A face looked down at him, and then the darkness again descended.

[021-157] “Friend,” he said hoarsely. “English.”

[021-158] He heard speech behind the parapet. An electric torch was flashed on

[021-159] him for a second. A voice spoke, a friendly voice, and the sound of it

[021-160] seemed to be telling him to come over.

[021-161] He was now standing up, and as he got his hands on the parapet he

[021-162] seemed to feel bayonets very near him. But the voice that spoke was

[021-163] kindly, so with a heave he scrambled over and flopped into the trench.

[021-164] Once more the electric torch was flashed, and revealed to the eyes of

[021-165] the onlookers an indescribably dirty, lean, middle-aged man with a

[021-166] bloody head, and scarcely a rag of shirt on his back. The said man,

[021-167] seeing friendly faces around him, grinned cheerfully.

[021-168] “That was a rough trek, friends,” he said; “I want to see your general

[021-169] pretty quick, for I’ve got a present for him.”

[021-170] He was taken to an officer in a dug-out, who addressed him in French,

[021-171] which he did not understand. But the sight of Stumm’s plan worked

[021-172] wonders. After that he was fairly bundled down communication trenches

[021-173] and then over swampy fields to a farm among trees. There he found staff

[021-174] officers, who looked at him and looked at his map, and then put him on

[021-175] a horse and hurried him eastwards. At last he came to a big ruined

[021-176] house, and was taken into a room which seemed to be full of maps and

[021-177] generals.
