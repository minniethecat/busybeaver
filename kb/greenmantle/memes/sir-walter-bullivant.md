# Sir Walter Bullivant

## Overview

Sir Walter Bullivant is the senior Foreign Office official who recruits Richard Hannay for the Greenmantle mission. A spymaster worn down by war, he carries personal as well as professional stakes in the operation — his son Harry Bullivant died pursuing the same conspiracy [001-053].

## Role in the Story

Sir Walter summons Hannay from convalescence to the Foreign Office, where he reveals the intelligence about a brewing Islamic holy war orchestrated by Germany [001-023]. Unlike the robust man Hannay knew before the war, Sir Walter now shows the physical toll of his work — gaunt, grey, and red-eyed from overwork [001-055].

> Hannay barely recognises him: "I would not have recognized the man I had known eighteen months before. His big frame seemed to have dropped flesh and there was a stoop in the square shoulders. His face had lost its rosiness and was red in patches, like that of a man who gets too little fresh air. His hair was much greyer and very thin about the temples, and there were lines of overwork below the eyes." [P01-150]

> "But the eyes were the same as before, keen and kindly and shrewd, and there was no change in the firm set of the jaw." [P01-155]

Sir Walter provides the mission's strategic framework, connecting the dots between Enver Pasha, German ambitions, and the mysterious "Greenmantle" prophecy.

> Sir Walter opens the briefing with characteristic clarity: "Sir Walter lay back in an arm-chair and spoke to the ceiling. It was the best story, the clearest and the fullest, I had ever got of any bit of the war." [P01-234]

He begins by framing the stakes in terms of national service, probing whether Hannay — a battalion commander awaiting his own promotion — can be diverted to a more dangerous and solitary task [P01-188]. His personal loss — the death of his son Harry pursuing the same thread — gives the mission its emotional gravity [001-793].

> Sir Walter lays bare the personal cost: "I take it you are in this business to serve your country, Hannay?" [P01-188]

> "My heart was beginning to thump uncomfortably. Sir Walter was not the man to pitch a case too high." [P01-210]

## Key Appearances

Sir Walter's primary scene is the extended briefing in his Foreign Office office, where he lays out the entirety of the threat [001-053]. He later provides logistical support and coordination for the scattered team members. His presence looms over the entire narrative even when off-page — Hannay frequently measures his decisions against what Sir Walter would expect.

> Hannay reflects on his handler's fairness: "I was very angry with Bullivant, till I remembered how fair he had been. My fate was my own choosing." [001-035]

> In Sir Walter's dossier on Blenkiron, his judgment is characteristically terse and superlative: "Sir Walter had written at the end: 'The best man we ever had. Better than Scudder. He would go through hell with a box of bismuth tablets and a pack of Patience cards.'" [001-159]

## Relationships

Sir Walter's relationship with Hannay dates to the pre-war Black Stone affair (recounted in *The Thirty-Nine Steps*), giving them a history of trust forged in crisis [001-046]. He is also Blenkiron's handler, having recruited the American for intelligence work [001-158]. His most profound relationship is with his dead son Harry — the mission is, for Sir Walter, as much about completing Harry's work as about serving the state [001-793].

> Even Hannay's fellow agents measure their understanding against Bullivant's judgment: "Sir Walter was right, as Blenkiron has told us. There's a great stirring in Islam, something moving on the face of the waters." [012-066]

## Character Traits

Sir Walter is shrewd, patient, and burdened. The war has physically diminished him — Hannay notes the stoop, the grey hair, the red patches on his face — but his eyes remain "keen and kindly and shrewd" [001-056]. He combines the bureaucratic authority of the Foreign Office with a willingness to use unorthodox methods and unconventional agents.

## Significance

Sir Walter represents the hidden cost of intelligence work — the physical and emotional erosion of those who direct the shadow war. His personal loss makes him more than a bureaucratic functionary; he is a father seeking to finish what his son started. In the novel's moral economy, Sir Walter is the conscience that justifies Hannay's dangerous mission.
