# Kut

**Kut-al-Amara** appears in *Greenmantle* as a touchstone of military disaster — the siege that had recently ended in British surrender when the novel's action takes place. It serves as a point of comparison by which other desperate situations are measured.

## In the Novel

Kut is referenced three times, each time as a benchmark of military extremity. Peter Pienaar's arrival is dated in relation to the siege:

> Three months ago, just before Kut, he staggered into Delamain's camp with ten bullet holes in him and a knife slash on his... [001-141]

Other characters are marked by the siege too: a name appears "in the casualty list before Kut," and in Constantinople the dangers of secret warfare make fighting "at Kut seemed tame and reasonable" — a measure of how far beyond conventional combat the characters have travelled.

## Historical Context

The Siege of Kut (December 1915–April 1916) was one of the British Army's most severe defeats of the First World War. A British-Indian force under General Townshend was surrounded at Kut on the Tigris River and, after a 147-day siege, surrendered to Ottoman forces. Over 13,000 British and Indian soldiers were taken prisoner. The disaster occurred just before the main action of *Greenmantle* and would have been fresh in the minds of Buchan's readers.
