# Kaiser Wilhelm II

Kaiser Wilhelm II (1859–1941) was the German Emperor and King of Prussia during the First World War. In John Buchan's 1916 novel *Greenmantle*, he is referenced throughout the narrative as the supreme figure of German authority and as a key actor in Germany's strategic exploitation of Islam.

## Role in the Novel

The Kaiser never appears as a character in *Greenmantle*, but his presence is felt throughout the story. His "suite" represents the highest level of German diplomatic protection — Hannay reflects that "if I travelled to Turkey, in the Kaiser's suite, I would be as safe as the mail; but if I went on my own I was done" [^1]. This observation crystallises the novel's central problem: how to operate behind enemy lines without the official cover that only supreme authority can provide.

## The Holy War Proclamation

One of Buchan's most pointed references concerns the Kaiser's role in proclaiming a _jihad_ against the Allies. The narrator notes that "though the Kaiser proclaims a Holy War and calls himself Hadji Mohammed Guilliamo, and says the Hohenzollerns are descended from the Prophet" [^1], the Sheikh-ul-Islam is neglected. Buchan uses the Kaiser's opportunistic embrace of Islamic symbolism to critique the shallowness of Germany's religious strategy — a theme central to the Greenmantle conspiracy.

## Strategic Analysis

Sir Walter Bullivant provides the novel's most incisive assessment of the Kaiser's strategic position. He predicts that "if the worst happens, Kaiser will fling overboard a lot of ballast in Europe, and it will look like a big victory for the Allies, but he won't be beaten" [^1]. This analysis — that Germany would sacrifice European positions to preserve its core power — reflects British intelligence thinking of 1915–1916 and frames the broader strategic stakes of Hannay's mission.

## Significance

The Kaiser serves as the symbolic apex of the German power structure that Hannay must penetrate. His proclamation of Holy War provides the ideological cover for the Greenmantle plot, while his political calculations shape the strategic environment. By keeping the Kaiser at a narrative distance, Buchan maintains the focus on the mid-level operators — Stumm, Hilda von Einem, and the Companions of the Rosy Hours — who do the actual work of the conspiracy.
