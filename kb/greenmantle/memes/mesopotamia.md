# Mesopotamia

**Mesopotamia** (modern-day Iraq) in *Greenmantle* represents the easternmost theatre of the Great War and the climactic destination of Richard Hannay's mission. While other fronts are mired in stalemate or disaster, Mesopotamia is presented as the one theatre where Allied prospects appear hopeful.

## Strategic Significance

### The War Context

At the time of the novel's setting, the Mesopotamian campaign was one of Britain's key efforts against the Ottoman Empire. The narrative presents it as a rare bright spot:

> "Only in Mesopotamia did things look fairly cheerful, owing to the blunders of British strategy."

This stands in stark contrast to the grim news from Gallipoli and the Western Front.

### Germany's Eastern Ambitions

Sandy Arbuthnot identifies Mesopotamia as the ultimate prize that Germany will never voluntarily surrender:

> "She'll give up Belgium and Alsace-Lorraine and Poland, but by God! she'll never give up the road to Mesopotamia till you have her by the throat and make her drop it." [012-100]

### British Intelligence in the Region

Sir Walter Bullivant reveals that British agents have been operating covertly in the Mesopotamian theatre throughout the war:

> "We have had our agents working in Persia and Mesopotamia for years -- mostly young officers of the Indian Army. They carry their lives in their hands, and now and then one disappears, and the sewers of Baghdad might tell a tale."

### Hannay's Mission Objective

Hannay's mission, as conceived by Sir Walter Bullivant and Sandy Arbuthnot, is to prevent the Germans from raising a religious revolt in the Islamic world -- a revolt that could threaten British positions in Mesopotamia. The region is thus central to the novel's geopolitical stakes.

## Key References

### The Map

In Chapter 5, Hannay examines a map that shows the through route to Mesopotamia [005-367], highlighting the strategic importance of the overland corridors through Anatolia.

### The Opposing Commander

The German general commanding against the British in Mesopotamia is identified as Nasr-ed-din, whom Sandy remembers from pre-war days:

> "He's the General believed to be commanding against us in Mesopotamia. I remember him years ago in Aleppo. He talked bad French and drank the sweetest of sweet champagne."

### Flooding and Terrain

The Mesopotamian landscape is described in practical military terms. Sandy Arbuthnot sketches the flooding patterns [013-332] and discusses what might be accomplished in Mesopotamia [013-161], while Blenkiron's hydraulic expertise serves as their cover story [013-161]. Sandy demonstrates intimate knowledge of the terrain.

### The Final Push

The decision to "nose our way down into Mesopotamia" [013-175] represents the culmination of Hannay's journey. The phrase captures the improvisational, audacious character of the mission:

> "We go to Mesopotamia," I said. "I reckon these are the orders."

### Cover Story and Revelation

The trio's cover as American engineers becomes a point of dramatic irony when their true identities are uncovered:

> "Two eminent American engineers and their servant bound for Mesopotamia on business of high Government importance! It was a good lie."

Later, when Hilda von Einem tears up their passports, Hannay abandons the Mesopotamia destination:

> "We're not going to Mesopotamia," I said. "Our orders have been cancelled."
