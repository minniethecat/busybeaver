# Ch. 21: The Little Hill (cont'd, part 2)

[021-001] The conclusion must be told in [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]’s words.

[021-002] “There was a big man sitting at a table drinking coffee, and when I saw

[021-003] him my heart jumped out of my skin. For it was the man I hunted with on

[021-004] the Pungwe in ’98—him whom the Kaffirs called ‘Buck’s Horn’, because of

[021-005] his long curled moustaches. He was a prince even then, and now he is a

[021-006] very great general. When I saw him, I ran forward and gripped his hand

[021-007] and cried, ‘_Hoe gat het, Mynheer_?’ and he knew me and shouted in

[021-008] Dutch, ‘Damn, if it isn’t old Peter Pienaar!’ Then he gave me coffee

[021-009] and ham and good bread, and he looked at my map.

[021-010] “‘What is this?’ he cried, growing red in the face.

[021-011] “‘It is the staff-map of one Stumm, a German _skellum_ who commands in

[021-012] yon city,’ I said.

[021-013] “He looked at it close and read the markings, and then he read the

[021-014] other paper which you gave me, Dick. And then he flung up his arms and

[021-015] laughed. He took a loaf and tossed it into the air so that it fell on

[021-016] the head of another general. He spoke to them in their own tongue, and

[021-017] they, too, laughed, and one or two ran out as if on some errand. I have

[021-018] never seen such merrymaking. They were clever men, and knew the worth

[021-019] of what you gave me.

[021-020] “Then he got to his feet and hugged me, all dirty as I was, and kissed

[021-021] me on both cheeks.

[021-022] “‘Before God, Peter,’ he said, ‘you’re the mightiest hunter since

[021-023] Nimrod. You’ve often found me game, but never game so big as this!’”

[021-024] CHAPTER XXI.

[021-025] The Little Hill

[021-026] It was a wise man who said that the biggest kind of [[courage-and-honour|courage]] was to be

[021-027] able to sit still. I used to feel that when we were getting shelled in

[021-028] the reserve trenches outside Vermelles. I felt it before we went over

[021-029] the parapets at [[loos|Loos]], but I never felt it so much as on the last two

[021-030] days in that cellar. I had simply to set my teeth and take a pull on

[021-031] myself. Peter had gone on a crazy errand which I scarcely believed

[021-032] could come off. There were no signs of [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]; somewhere within a

[021-033] hundred yards he was fighting his own battles, and I was tormented by

[021-034] the thought that he might get jumpy again and wreck everything. A

[021-035] strange Companion brought us food, a man who spoke only Turkish and

[021-036] could tell us nothing; Hussin, I judged, was busy about the horses. If

[021-037] I could only have done something to help on matters I could have

[021-038] scotched my anxiety, but there was nothing to be done, nothing but wait

[021-039] and brood. I tell you I began to sympathize with the general behind the

[021-040] lines in a battle, the fellow who makes the plan which others execute.

[021-041] Leading a charge can be nothing like so nerve-shaking a business as

[021-042] sitting in an easy-chair and waiting on the news of it.

[021-043] It was bitter cold, and we spent most of the day wrapped in our

[021-044] greatcoats and buried deep in the straw. [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] was a marvel. There

[021-045] was no light for him to play Patience by, but he never complained. He

[021-046] slept a lot of the time, and when he was awake talked as cheerily as if

[021-047] he were starting out on a holiday. He had one great comfort, his

[021-048] dyspepsia was gone. He sang hymns constantly to the benign Providence

[021-049] that had squared his duodenum.

[021-050] My only occupation was to listen for the guns. The first day after

[021-051] Peter left they were very quiet on the front nearest us, but in the

[021-052] late evening they started a terrific racket. The next day they never

[021-053] stopped from dawn to dusk, so that it reminded me of that tremendous

[021-054] forty-eight hours before Loos. I tried to read into this some proof

[021-055] that Peter had got through, but it would not work. It looked more like

[021-056] the opposite, for this desperate hammering must mean that the frontal

[021-057] assault was still the Russian game.

[021-058] Two or three times I climbed on the housetop for fresh air. The day was

[021-059] foggy and damp, and I could see very little of the countryside.

[021-060] Transport was still bumping southward along the road to the Palantuken,

[021-061] and the slow wagon-loads of wounded returning. One thing I noticed,

[021-062] however; there was a perpetual coming and going between the house and

[021-063] the city. Motors and mounted messengers were constantly arriving and

[021-064] departing, and I concluded that [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem was getting ready for

[021-065] her part in the defence of Erzerum.

[021-066] These ascents were all on the first day after Peter’s going. The second

[021-067] day, when I tried the trap, I found it closed and heavily weighted.

[021-068] This must have been done by our friends, and very right, too. If the

[021-069] house were becoming a place of public resort, it would never do for me

[021-070] to be journeying roof-ward.

[021-071] Late on the second night Hussin reappeared. It was after supper, when

[021-072] Blenkiron had gone peacefully to sleep and I was beginning to count the

[021-073] hours till the morning. I could not close an eye during these days and

[021-074] not much at night.

[021-075] Hussin did not light a lantern. I heard his key in the lock, and then

[021-076] his light step close to where we lay.

[021-077] “Are you asleep?” he said, and when I answered he sat down beside me.

[021-078] “The horses are found,” he said, “and the Master bids me tell you that

[021-079] we start in the morning three hours before dawn.”

[021-080] It was welcome news. “Tell me what is happening,” I begged; “we have

[021-081] been lying in this tomb for three days and heard nothing.”

[021-082] “The guns are busy,” he said. “The Allemans come to this place every

[021-083] hour, I know not for what. Also there has been a great search for you.

[021-084] The searchers have been here, but they were sent away empty.... Sleep,

[021-085] my lord, for there is wild work before us.”

[021-086] I did not sleep much, for I was strung too high with expectation, and I

[021-087] envied Blenkiron his now eupeptic slumbers. But for an hour or so I

[021-088] dropped off, and my old nightmare came back. Once again I was in the

[021-089] throat of a pass, hotly pursued, straining for some sanctuary which I

[021-090] knew I must reach. But I was no longer alone. Others were with me: how

[021-091] many I could not tell, for when I tried to see their faces they

[021-092] dissolved in mist. Deep snow was underfoot, a grey sky was over us,

[021-093] black peaks were on all sides, but ahead in the mist of the pass was

[021-094] that curious _castrol_ which I had first seen in my dream on the

[021-095] Erzerum road.

[021-096] I saw it distinct in every detail. It rose to the left of the road

[021-097] through the pass, above a hollow where great boulders stood out in the

[021-098] snow. Its sides were steep, so that the snow had slipped off in

[021-099] patches, leaving stretches of glistening black shale. The _kranz_ at

[021-100] the top did not rise sheer, but sloped at an angle of forty-five, and

[021-101] on the very summit there seemed a hollow, as if the earth within the

[021-102] rock-rim had been beaten by weather into a cup.

[021-103] That is often the way with a South African _castrol_, and I knew it was

[021-104] so with this. We were straining for it, but the snow clogged us, and

[021-105] our enemies were very close behind.

[021-106] Then I was awakened by a figure at my side. “Get ready, my lord,” it

[021-107] said; “it is the hour to ride.”

[021-108] Like sleep-walkers we moved into the sharp air. Hussin led us out of an

[021-109] old postern and then through a place like an orchard to the shelter of

[021-110] some tall evergreen trees. There horses stood, champing quietly from

[021-111] their nosebags. “Good,” I thought; “a feed of oats before a big

[021-112] effort.”

[021-113] There were nine beasts for nine riders. We mounted without a word and

[021-114] filed through a grove of trees to where a broken paling marked the

[021-115] beginning of cultivated land. There for the matter of twenty minutes

[021-116] Hussin chose to guide us through deep, clogging snow. He wanted to

[021-117] avoid any sound till we were well beyond earshot of the house. Then we

[021-118] struck a by-path which presently merged in a hard highway, running, as

[021-119] I judged, south-west by west. There we delayed no longer, but galloped

[021-120] furiously into the dark.

[021-121] I had got back all my exhilaration. Indeed I was intoxicated with the

[021-122] movement, and could have laughed out loud and sung. Under the black

[021-123] canopy of the night perils are either forgotten or terribly alive. Mine

[021-124] were forgotten. The darkness I galloped into led me to freedom and

[021-125] friends. Yes, and success, which I had not dared to hope and scarcely

[021-126] even to dream of.

[021-127] Hussin rode first, with me at his side. I turned my head and saw

[021-128] Blenkiron behind me, evidently mortally unhappy about the pace we set

[021-129] and the mount he sat. He used to say that horse-exercise was good for

[021-130] his liver, but it was a gentle amble and a short gallop that he liked,

[021-131] and not this mad helter-skelter. His thighs were too round to fit a

[021-132] saddle leather. We passed a fire in a hollow, the bivouac of some

[021-133] Turkish unit, and all the horses shied violently. I knew by Blenkiron’s

[021-134] oaths that he had lost his stirrups and was sitting on his horse’s

[021-135] neck.

[021-136] Beside him rode a tall figure swathed to the eyes in wrappings, and

[021-137] wearing round his neck some kind of shawl whose ends floated behind

[021-138] him. Sandy, of course, had no European ulster, for it was months since

[021-139] he had worn proper clothes. I wanted to speak to him, but somehow I did

[021-140] not dare. His stillness forbade me. He was a wonderful fine horseman,

[021-141] with his firm English hunting seat, and it was as well, for he paid no

[021-142] attention to his beast. His head was still full of unquiet thoughts.

[021-143] Then the air around me began to smell acrid and raw, and I saw that a

[021-144] fog was winding up from the hollows.

[021-145] “Here’s the devil’s own [[fate-and-providence|luck]],” I cried to Hussin. “Can you guide us in

[021-146] a mist?”

[021-147] “I do not know.” He shook his head. “I had counted on seeing the shape

[021-148] of the hills.”

[021-149] “We’ve a map and compass, anyhow. But these make slow travelling. Pray

[021-150] God it lifts!”

[021-151] Presently the black vapour changed to grey, and the day broke. It was

[021-152] little comfort. The fog rolled in waves to the horses’ ears, and riding

[021-153] at the head of the party I could but dimly see the next rank.

[021-154] “It is time to leave the road,” said Hussin, “or we may meet

[021-155] inquisitive folk.”

[021-156] We struck to the left, over ground which was for all the world like a

[021-157] Scotch moor. There were pools of rain on it, and masses of tangled

[021-158] snow-laden junipers, and long reefs of wet slaty stone. It was bad

[021-159] going, and the fog made it hopeless to steer a good course. I had out

[021-160] the map and the compass, and tried to fix our route so as to round the

[021-161] flank of a spur of the mountains which separated us from the valley we

[021-162] were aiming at.

[021-163] “There’s a stream ahead of us,” I said to Hussin. “Is it fordable?”

[021-164] “It is only a trickle,” he said, coughing. “This accursed mist is from

[021-165] Eblis.” But I knew long before we reached it that it was no trickle. It

[021-166] was a hill stream coming down in spate, and, as I soon guessed, in a

[021-167] deep ravine. Presently we were at its edge, one long whirl of yeasty

[021-168] falls and brown rapids. We could as soon get horses over it as to the

[021-169] topmost cliffs of the Palantuken.

[021-170] Hussin stared at it in consternation. “May Allah forgive my folly, for

[021-171] I should have known. We must return to the highway and find a bridge.

[021-172] My sorrow, that I should have led my lords so ill.”
