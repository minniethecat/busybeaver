# Dervish

> The hall was "hung round with trophies of travel, like Dervish implements." — narrator [005-085]

## Overview

The dervish — an Islamic mystic, particularly of the Sufi orders known for ecstatic dancing and spiritual devotion — appears in *Greenmantle* as both a concrete cultural reference and a symbolic archetype that illuminates the novel's central religious theme. The term is invoked directly only once, in the description of Sir Walter Bullivant's study decorated with "Dervish implements" [005-085], but the figure of the dervish — the holy man whose spiritual authority transcends political boundaries — haunts the novel's treatment of Greenmantle.

The Greenmantle prophecy itself draws on the dervish archetype: a figure of spiritual power who emerges from the Islamic mystical tradition to unite the faithful. The "dancing maniacs" led by "the man in the skin-cap" [012-039] represent the popular religious enthusiasm that the German conspiracy seeks to channel, and their performance echoes the ecstatic rituals of dervish orders.

## In Greenmantle

### The Dervish as Cultural Signifier

The single direct reference to dervishes occurs in the description of Sir Walter's study: "the hall was hung round with trophies of travel, like Dervish implements" [005-085]. This passing reference establishes Sir Walter's credentials as a man who knows the Islamic world — the dervish implements are trophies, evidence of encounters with the exotic East, displayed in the comfortable surroundings of a British intelligence chief's home.

The reference functions within the novel's Orientalist framework: the dervish is simultaneously fascinating (a marker of authentic Eastern spirituality) and slightly absurd (the "dancing" is presented as spectacle rather than religious practice). Buchan's readers in 1916 would have recognised the dervish as a stock figure of Eastern exoticism.

### The Dancing Maniacs

The novel's most vivid evocation of the dervish archetype comes in Sandy's description of the Greenmantle ceremony: "the man in the skin-cap, the leader of the dancing maniacs" [012-039]. These figures, whirling and howling "in the true Oriental way" [012-043], represent the popular religious emotion that the German conspiracy has harnessed.

The "dancing maniacs" are both participants in and audience for the Greenmantle prophecy. Their ecstatic devotion creates the atmosphere of religious mystery that gives the manufactured prophecy its power. Sandy's account makes clear that this is not authentic religious practice but something stage-managed — yet the emotion it generates is real enough to be dangerous.

### The Prophet and the Dervish

The prophet figure at the centre of the Greenmantle conspiracy draws on the dervish tradition: a holy man whose authority derives from spiritual charisma rather than institutional position, "of the blood of the Prophet" [012-070], who will "restore the Khalifate" [012-070]. The narrative specifies that this prophet "is aided by one of the few women who play much part in the mystery" [012-109] — Hilda von Einem — further emphasising the constructed nature of what appears to be spontaneous religious fervour.

The novel's resolution destroys both prophet and prophecy: "I destroyed your prophets, and now it is your turn to fail and disappear" [022-052]. The dervish archetype, having been exploited for political purposes, is decisively rejected.

## Thematic Significance

The dervish in *Greenmantle* represents the intersection of genuine religious tradition and political manipulation. The novel's German conspirators understand that the dervish archetype — the holy man whose authority transcends political structures — commands a loyalty that no political leader can match. Their genius (and their crime, in the novel's moral framework) is to manufacture a false dervish, a counterfeit prophet, to exploit that loyalty for strategic purposes.

The figure also serves the novel's broader engagement with the unknowability of Eastern spirituality. The dervish is mysterious, ecstatic, pre-rational — everything the British intelligence apparatus is not. The novel's triumph is the defeat of manufactured mystery by practical action.
