# Pan-Islamism

> "Islam is the one thing that can really bite us." — Sir Walter Bullivant [001-006]

## Overview

Pan-Islamism is the central geopolitical threat in *Greenmantle* — the German strategic objective of weaponising Islamic religious unity against the British Empire. Sir Walter Bullivant's opening analysis establishes that Germany has moved beyond crude military propaganda toward the deliberate manufacture of a religious movement intended to destabilise British rule from India to Egypt [001-005].

The concept in the novel is not theological but instrumental: Islam appears as a political force to be harnessed, a "dry wind blowing through the East" waiting for "the spark" [001-005]. The Germans understand that conventional military operations cannot defeat Britain alone; a pan-Islamic uprising that threatens the empire's Muslim subjects offers a path to decisive victory [001-006].

## In Greenmantle

### The German Calculation

Sir Walter explains the strategic logic: the Kaiser's proclamation of jihad has "fallen pretty flat" because the temporal authority of a Christian emperor carries no weight in the Islamic world [001-007]. The Germans have learned from this failure and are attempting something more sophisticated — not a proclamation from above but a prophecy from within, embodied in the figure of Greenmantle [001-031].

The stakes are explicitly colonial: "There is a jehad preparing," Sir Walter warns, and the question is whether it can be "swung round" to serve German interests [001-031]. Germany cannot "manufacture Holy Wars out of Krupp guns alone and a few staff officers" — it needs a spiritual instrument, and Greenmantle is that instrument [001-036].

### The Covenant

The German plan involves a secret "Covenant which will madden the remotest Moslem peasant with dreams of Paradise" [001-046]. This is the operational core of pan-Islamism as the novel presents it: not a spontaneous religious movement but a manufactured one, assembled from Islamic symbols and directed toward German strategic ends.

### Countering the Threat

The mission of Hannay and his companions is to disrupt this manufactured holy war before it can be ignited. Their journey to Constantinople and ultimately to Erzerum is a race to prevent the German pan-Islamic apparatus from achieving operational capability. The novel's resolution at Erzerum suggests that manufactured religious movements collapse when their political machinery is exposed [022-052].

## Thematic Significance

Pan-Islamism in *Greenmantle* reflects early-twentieth-century British imperial anxiety about the vulnerability of the empire's Muslim subjects to religious agitation. The novel engages with a genuine strategic concern — the Ottoman call for jihad in 1914 was a real event — but filters it through the conventions of the spy thriller, where geopolitical forces are embodied in conspiracies that can be disrupted by individual action.

The novel's treatment of Islam as a purely instrumental force — something to be "used" by Germany, "swung round" by Britain — is characteristic of its era's imperial assumptions. The religion itself is never explored on its own terms; it exists in the narrative only as a potential weapon.
