# Ch. 5: Further Adventures of the Same (cont'd, part 2)

[005-001] The heat put me into a half-doze and I began dreamily to wonder what

[005-002] other people were doing. Where had [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] been posting to in that

[005-003] train, and what was he up to at this moment? He had been hobnobbing

[005-004] with ambassadors and swells—I wondered if he had found out anything.

[005-005] What was [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] doing? I fervently hoped he was behaving himself, for I

[005-006] doubted if Peter had really tumbled to the delicacy of our job. Where

[005-007] was [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]], too? As like as not bucketing in the hold of some Greek

[005-008] coaster in the Aegean. Then I thought of my battalion somewhere on the

[005-009] line between Hulluch and La Bassee, hammering at the Boche, while I was

[005-010] five hundred miles or so inside the Boche frontier.

[005-011] It was a comic reflection, so comic that it woke me up. After trying in

[005-012] vain to find a way of stoking that stove, for it was a cold night, I

[005-013] got up and walked about the room. There were portraits of two decent

[005-014] old fellows, probably Gaudian’s parents. There were enlarged

[005-015] photographs, too, of engineering works, and a good picture of Bismarck.

[005-016] And close to the stove there was a case of maps mounted on rollers.

[005-017] I pulled out one at random. It was a geological map of Germany, and

[005-018] with some trouble I found out where I was. I was an enormous distance

[005-019] from my goal and moreover I was clean off the road to [[eastern-front|the East]]. To go

[005-020] there I must first go to Bavaria and then into Austria. I noticed the

[005-021] [[danube-river|Danube]] flowing eastwards and remembered that that was one way to

[005-022] Constantinople.

[005-023] Then I tried another map. This one covered a big area, all Europe from

[005-024] the [[rhine|Rhine]] and as far east as Persia. I guessed that it was meant to

[005-025] show the [[baghdad|Baghdad]] railway and the through routes from Germany to

[005-026] [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]]. There were markings on it; and, as I looked closer, I saw

[005-027] that there were dates scribbled in blue pencil, as if to denote the

[005-028] stages of a journey. The dates began in Europe, and continued right on

[005-029] into [[anatolia|Asia Minor]] and then south to Syria.

[005-030] For a moment my heart jumped, for I thought I had fallen by accident on

[005-031] the clue I wanted. But I never got that map examined. I heard footsteps

[005-032] in the corridor, and very gently I let the map roll up and turned away.

[005-033] When the door opened I was bending over the stove trying to get a light

[005-034] for my pipe.

[005-035] It was Gaudian, to bid me join him and Stumm in his study.

[005-036] On our way there he put a kindly hand on my shoulder. I think he

[005-037] thought I was bullied by Stumm and wanted to tell me that he was my

[005-038] friend, and he had no other language than a pat on the back.

[005-039] The soldier was in his old position with his elbows on the mantelpiece

[005-040] and his formidable great jaw stuck out.

[005-041] “Listen to me,” he said. “[[gaudian|Herr Gaudian]] and I are inclined to make use

[005-042] of you. You may be a charlatan, in which case you will be in the devil

[005-043] of a mess and have yourself to thank for it. If you are a rogue you

[005-044] will have little scope for roguery. We will see to that. If you are a

[005-045] fool, you will yourself suffer for it. But if you are a good man, you

[005-046] will have a fair chance, and if you succeed we will not forget it.

[005-047] Tomorrow I go home and you will come with me and get your orders.”

[005-048] I made shift to stand at attention and salute.

[005-049] Gaudian spoke in a pleasant voice, as if he wanted to atone for Stumm’s

[005-050] imperiousness. “We are men who love our Fatherland, [[hermann-brandt|Herr Brandt]],” he

[005-051] said. “You are not of that Fatherland, but at least you hate its

[005-052] enemies. Therefore we are allies, and trust each other like allies. Our

[005-053] victory is ordained by God, and we are none of us more than His

[005-054] instruments.”

[005-055] Stumm translated in a sentence, and his voice was quite solemn. He held

[005-056] up his right hand and so did Gaudian, like a man taking an oath or a

[005-057] parson blessing his congregation.

[005-058] Then I realized something of the might of Germany. She produced good

[005-059] and bad, cads and gentlemen, but she could put a bit of the fanatic

[005-060] into them all.

[005-061] CHAPTER VI.

[005-062] The Indiscretions of the Same

[005-063] I was standing stark naked next morning in that icy bedroom, trying to

[005-064] bathe in about a quart of water, when Stumm entered. He strode up to me

[005-065] and stared me in the face. I was half a head shorter than him to begin

[005-066] with, and a man does not feel his stoutest when he has no clothes, so

[005-067] he had the pull on me every way.

[005-068] “I have reason to believe that you are a liar,” he growled.

[005-069] I pulled the bed-cover round me, for I was shivering with cold, and the

[005-070] German idea of a towel is a pocket-handkerchief. I own I was in a

[005-071] pretty blue funk.

[005-072] “A liar!” he repeated. “You and that swine Pienaar.”

[005-073] With my best effort at surliness I asked what we had done.

[005-074] “You lied, because you said you know no German. Apparently your friend

[005-075] knows enough to talk treason and blasphemy.”

[005-076] This gave me back some heart.

[005-077] “I told you I knew a dozen words. But I told you Peter could talk it a

[005-078] bit. I told you that yesterday at the station.” Fervently I blessed my

[005-079] [[fate-and-providence|luck]] for that casual remark.

[005-080] He evidently remembered, for his tone became a trifle more civil.

[005-081] “You are a precious pair. If one of you is a scoundrel, why not the

[005-082] other?”

[005-083] “I take no responsibility for Peter,” I said. I felt I was a cad in

[005-084] saying it, but that was the bargain we had made at the start. “I have

[005-085] known him for years as a great hunter and a brave man. I knew he fought

[005-086] well against the English. But more I cannot tell you. You have to judge

[005-087] him for yourself. What has he done?”

[005-088] I was told, for Stumm had got it that morning on the telephone. While

[005-089] telling it he was kind enough to allow me to put on my trousers.

[005-090] It was just the sort of thing I might have foreseen. Peter, left alone,

[005-091] had become first bored and then reckless. He had persuaded the

[005-092] lieutenant to take him out to supper at a big [[berlin|Berlin]] restaurant. There,

[005-093] inspired by the lights and music—novel things for a backveld hunter—and

[005-094] no doubt bored stiff by his company, he had proceeded to get drunk.

[005-095] That had happened in my experience with Peter about once in every three

[005-096] years, and it always happened for the same reason. Peter, bored and

[005-097] solitary in a town, went on the spree. He had a head like a rock, but

[005-098] he got to the required condition by wild mixing. He was quite a

[005-099] gentleman in his cups, and not in the least violent, but he was apt to

[005-100] be very free with his tongue. And that was what occurred at the

[005-101] Franciscana.

[005-102] He had begun by insulting the Emperor, it seemed. He drank his health,

[005-103] but said he reminded him of a wart-hog, and thereby scarified the

[005-104] lieutenant’s soul. Then an officer—some tremendous swell at an

[005-105] adjoining table had objected to his talking so loud, and Peter had

[005-106] replied insolently in respectable German. After that things became

[005-107] mixed. There was some kind of a fight, during which Peter calumniated

[005-108] the German army and all its female ancestry. How he wasn’t shot or run

[005-109] through I can’t imagine, except that the lieutenant loudly proclaimed

[005-110] that he was a crazy Boer. Anyhow the upshot was that Peter was marched

[005-111] off to gaol, and I was left in a pretty pickle.

[005-112] “I don’t believe a word of it,” I said firmly. I had most of my clothes

[005-113] on now and felt more courageous. “It is all a plot to get him into

[005-114] disgrace and draft him off to the front.”

[005-115] Stumm did not storm as I expected, but smiled.

[005-116] “That was always his destiny,” he said, “ever since I saw him. He was

[005-117] no use to us except as a man with a rifle. Cannon-fodder, nothing else.

[005-118] Do you imagine, you fool, that this great Empire in the thick of a

[005-119] world-war is going to trouble its head to lay snares for an ignorant

[005-120] _traakhaar_?”

[005-121] “I wash my hands of him,” I said. “If what you say of his folly is true

[005-122] I have no part in it. But he was my companion and I wish him well. What

[005-123] do you propose to do with him?”

[005-124] “We will keep him under our eye,” he said, with a wicked twist of the

[005-125] mouth. “I have a notion that there is more at the back of this than

[005-126] appears. We will investigate the antecedents of Herr Pienaar. And you,

[005-127] too, my friend. On you also we have our eye.”

[005-128] I did the best thing I could have done, for what with anxiety and

[005-129] disgust I lost my temper.

[005-130] “Look here, Sir,” I cried, “I’ve had about enough of this. I came to

[005-131] Germany abominating the English and burning to strike a blow for you.

[005-132] But you haven’t given me much cause to love you. For the last two days

[005-133] I’ve had nothing from you but suspicion and insult. The only decent man

[005-134] I’ve met is Herr Gaudian. It’s because I believe that there are many in

[005-135] Germany like him that I’m prepared to go on with this business and do

[005-136] the best I can. But, by God, I wouldn’t raise my little finger for your

[005-137] sake.”

[005-138] He looked at me very steadily for a minute. “That sounds like honesty,”

[005-139] he said at last in a civil voice. “You had better come down and get

[005-140] your coffee.”

[005-141] I was safe for the moment but in very low spirits. What on earth would

[005-142] happen to poor old Peter? I could do nothing even if I wanted, and,

[005-143] besides, my first [[courage-and-honour|duty]] was to my mission. I had made this very clear to

[005-144] him at [[lisbon|Lisbon]] and he had agreed, but all the same it was a beastly

[005-145] reflection. Here was that ancient worthy left to the tender mercies of

[005-146] the people he most detested on earth. My only comfort was that they

[005-147] couldn’t do very much with him. If they sent him to the front, which

[005-148] was the worst they could do, he would escape, for I would have backed

[005-149] him to get through any mortal lines. It wasn’t much fun for me either.

[005-150] Only when I was to be deprived of it did I realize how much his company

[005-151] had meant to me. I was absolutely alone now, and I didn’t like it. I

[005-152] seemed to have about as much chance of joining Blenkiron and Sandy as

[005-153] of flying to the moon.

[005-154] After breakfast I was told to get ready. When I asked where I was going

[005-155] Stumm advised me to mind my own business, but I remembered that last

[005-156] night he had talked of taking me home with him and giving me my orders.

[005-157] I wondered where his home was.

[005-158] Gaudian patted me on the back when we started and wrung my hand. He was

[005-159] a capital good fellow, and it made me feel sick to think that I was

[005-160] humbugging him. We got into the same big grey car, with Stumm’s servant

[005-161] sitting beside the chauffeur. It was a morning of hard frost, the bare

[005-162] fields were white with rime, and the fir-trees powdered like a

[005-163] wedding-cake. We took a different road from the night before, and after

[005-164] a run of half a dozen miles came to a little town with a big railway

[005-165] station. It was a junction on some main line, and after five minutes’

[005-166] waiting we found our train. Once again we were alone in the carriage.

[005-167] Stumm must have had some colossal graft, for the train was crowded.
