# General Prjevalsky

General Prjevalsky is a Russian military commander referenced in John Buchan's 1916 novel *Greenmantle*. He commands the northern (right) wing of the Russian offensive against the Ottoman fortress of Erzerum, and his tactical position becomes central to the intelligence coup that drives the novel's climax.

## The Map Discovery

While imprisoned in Erzerum, Richard Hannay steals into Colonel von Stumm's room and photographs his staff map. On this map, "about two miles to the north-east there was a red cross and the name '_Prjevalsky_'" [^1] — marking the farthest point yet reached by the right wing of the Russian attack. The map reveals that Prjevalsky's advance threatens a critical gap in the fortress defences.

## The Critical Weakness

Stumm's notes, transcribed onto his map from a typewritten intelligence assessment, identify Prjevalsky's sector as the decisive point. The assessment warns: "No time to fortify adjacent peaks. Difficult for enemy to get batteries there, but not impossible. This the real point of danger, for if Prjevalsky wins the peaks Kara Gubek and Tafta must fall, and enemy will be on left rear of Deve Boyun main position" [^1]. In other words, Prjevalsky's northern thrust, if properly reinforced and directed at the unfortified heights, could turn the entire Erzerum defensive line.

## Hannay's Analysis

Studying the map, Hannay maps the Russian dispositions: "Prjevalsky in the north, the main force beyond Deve Boyun, and the southern columns up to the passes of the Palantuken but not yet across" [^1]. He recognises that the intelligence he now possesses — the precise location of the unfortified peaks — is exactly what Prjevalsky needs to transform a probing attack into a decisive breakthrough. The entire subsequent mission to reach the Grand Duke's staff is predicated on exploiting this vulnerability before German reinforcements from Gallipoli can arrive.

## Significance

Prjevalsky is a minor figure in the narrative but a critical one in the plot's military logic. His northern advance creates the tactical opportunity that Hannay's stolen intelligence can exploit. Without Prjevalsky's position threatening the Kara Gubek sector, the map would be of merely academic interest. His presence on the battlefield — and the German command's nervous awareness of his potential — transforms Hannay's espionage from information-gathering into a potentially war-winning act.
