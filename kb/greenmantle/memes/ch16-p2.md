# Ch. 16: The Battered Caravanserai (cont'd, part 2)

[016-001] I made him talk about her, for if he sat and brooded he would get

[016-002] worse. It was a strange thing that this man, the most imperturbable

[016-003] and, I think, about the most courageous I have ever met, should be

[016-004] paralysed by a slim woman. There was no doubt about it. The thought of

[016-005] her made the future to him as black as a thunder cloud. It took the

[016-006] power out of his joints, and if she was going to be much around, it

[016-007] looked as if [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] might be counted out.

[016-008] I suggested that he was in love with her, but this he vehemently

[016-009] denied.

[016-010] “No, Sir; I haven’t got no sort of affection for the lady. My trouble

[016-011] is that she puts me out of countenance, and I can’t fit her in as an

[016-012] antagonist. I guess we Americans haven’t got the right poise for

[016-013] dealing with that kind of female. We’ve exalted our womenfolk into

[016-014] little tin gods, and at the same time left them out of the real

[016-015] business of life. Consequently, when we strike one playing the biggest

[016-016] kind of man’s game we can’t place her. We aren’t used to regarding them

[016-017] as anything except angels and children. I wish I had had you boys’

[016-018] upbringing.”

[016-019] [[angora|Angora]] was like my notion of some place such as Amiens in the retreat

[016-020] from Mons. It was one mass of troops and transport—the neck of the

[016-021] bottle, for more arrived every hour, and the only outlet was the single

[016-022] eastern road. The town was pandemonium into which distracted German

[016-023] officers were trying to introduce some order. They didn’t worry much

[016-024] about us, for the heart of [[anatolia|Anatolia]] wasn’t a likely hunting-ground for

[016-025] suspicious characters. We took our passport to the commandant, who

[016-026] visaed them readily, and told us he’d do his best to get us transport.

[016-027] We spent the night in a sort of hotel, where all four crowded into one

[016-028] little bedroom, and next morning I had my work cut out getting a

[016-029] motor-car. It took four hours, and the use of every great name in the

[016-030] Turkish Empire, to raise a dingy sort of Studebaker, and another two to

[016-031] get the petrol and spare tyres. As for a chauffeur, love or money

[016-032] couldn’t find him, and I was compelled to drive the thing myself.

[016-033] We left just after midday and swung out into bare bleak downs patched

[016-034] with scrubby woodlands. There was no snow here, but a wind was blowing

[016-035] from the east which searched the marrow. Presently we climbed up into

[016-036] hills, and the road, though not badly engineered to begin with, grew as

[016-037] rough as the channel of a stream. No wonder, for the traffic was like

[016-038] what one saw on that awful stretch between Cassel and [[ypres|Ypres]], and there

[016-039] were no gangs of Belgian roadmakers to mend it up. We found troops by

[016-040] the thousands striding along with their impassive Turkish faces, ox

[016-041] convoys, mule convoys, wagons drawn by sturdy little Anatolian horses,

[016-042] and, coming in the contrary direction, many shabby Red Crescent cars

[016-043] and wagons of the wounded. We had to crawl for hours on end, till we

[016-044] got past a block. Just before the darkening we seemed to outstrip the

[016-045] first press, and had a clear run for about ten miles over a low pass in

[016-046] the hills. I began to get anxious about the car, for it was a poor one

[016-047] at the best, and the road was guaranteed sooner or later to knock even

[016-048] a Rolls-Royce into scrap iron.

[016-049] All the same it was glorious to be out in the open again. [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]’s face

[016-050] wore a new look, and he sniffed the bitter air like a stag. There

[016-051] floated up from little wayside camps the odour of wood-smoke and

[016-052] dung-fires. That, and the curious acrid winter smell of great

[016-053] wind-blown spaces, will always come to my memory as I think of that

[016-054] day. Every hour brought me peace of mind and resolution. I felt as I

[016-055] had felt when the battalion first marched from Aire towards the

[016-056] firing-line, a kind of keying-up and wild expectation. I’m not used to

[016-057] cities, and lounging about Constantinople had slackened my fibre. Now,

[016-058] as the sharp wind buffeted us, I felt braced to any kind of risk. We

[016-059] were on the great road to the east and the border hills, and soon we

[016-060] should stand upon the farthest battle-front of the war. This was no

[016-061] commonplace intelligence job. That was all over, and we were going into

[016-062] the firing-zone, going to take part in what might be the downfall of

[016-063] our enemies. I didn’t reflect that we were among those enemies, and

[016-064] would probably share their downfall if we were not shot earlier. The

[016-065] truth is, I had got out of the way of regarding the thing as a struggle

[016-066] between armies and nations. I hardly bothered to think where my

[016-067] sympathies lay. First and foremost it was a contest between the four of

[016-068] us and a crazy woman, and this personal antagonism made the strife of

[016-069] armies only a dimly-felt background.

[016-070] We slept that night like logs on the floor of a dirty khan, and started

[016-071] next morning in a powder of snow. We were getting very high up now, and

[016-072] it was perishing cold. The Companion—his name sounded like Hussin—had

[016-073] travelled the road before and told me what the places were, but they

[016-074] conveyed nothing to me. All morning we wriggled through a big lot of

[016-075] troops, a brigade at least, who swung along at a great pace with a fine

[016-076] free stride that I don’t think I have ever seen bettered. I must say I

[016-077] took a fancy to the Turkish fighting man: I remembered the testimonial

[016-078] our fellows gave him as a clean fighter, and I felt very bitter that

[016-079] Germany should have lugged him into this dirty business. They halted

[016-080] for a meal, and we stopped, too, and lunched off some brown bread and

[016-081] dried figs and a flask of very sour wine. I had a few words with one of

[016-082] the officers who spoke a little German. He told me they were marching

[016-083] straight for [[russia|Russia]], since there had been a great Turkish victory in

[016-084] the Caucasus. “We have beaten the French and the British, and now it is

[016-085] Russia’s turn,” he said stolidly, as if repeating a lesson. But he

[016-086] added that he was mortally sick of war.

[016-087] In the afternoon we cleared the column and had an open road for some

[016-088] hours. The land now had a tilt eastward, as if we were moving towards

[016-089] the valley of a great river. Soon we began to meet little parties of

[016-090] men coming from the east with a new look in their faces. The first lots

[016-091] of wounded had been the ordinary thing you see on every front, and

[016-092] there had been some pretence at organization. But these new lots were

[016-093] very weary and broken; they were often barefoot, and they seemed to

[016-094] have lost their transport and to be starving. You would find a group

[016-095] stretched by the roadside in the last stages of exhaustion. Then would

[016-096] come a party limping along, so tired that they never turned their heads

[016-097] to look at us. Almost all were wounded, some badly, and most were

[016-098] horribly thin. I wondered how my Turkish friend behind would explain

[016-099] the sight to his men, if he believed in a great victory. They had not

[016-100] the air of the backwash of a conquering army.

[016-101] Even Blenkiron, who was no soldier, noticed it.

[016-102] “These boys look mighty bad,” he observed. “We’ve got to hustle, Major,

[016-103] if we’re going to get seats for the last act.”

[016-104] That was my own feeling. The sight made me mad to get on faster, for I

[016-105] saw that big things were happening in [[eastern-front|the East]]. I had reckoned that

[016-106] four days would take us from Angora to Erzerum, but here was the second

[016-107] nearly over and we were not yet a third of the way. I pressed on

[016-108] recklessly, and that hurry was our undoing.

[016-109] I have said that the Studebaker was a rotten old car. Its steering-gear

[016-110] was pretty dicky, and the bad surface and continual hairpin bends of

[016-111] the road didn’t improve it. Soon we came into snow lying fairly deep,

[016-112] frozen hard and rutted by the big transport-wagons. We bumped and

[016-113] bounced horribly, and were shaken about like peas in a bladder. I began

[016-114] to be acutely anxious about the old boneshaker, the more as we seemed a

[016-115] long way short of the village I had proposed to spend the night in.

[016-116] Twilight was falling and we were still in an unfeatured waste, crossing

[016-117] the shallow glen of a stream. There was a bridge at the bottom of a

[016-118] slope—a bridge of logs and earth which had apparently been freshly

[016-119] strengthened for heavy traffic. As we approached it at a good pace the

[016-120] car ceased to answer to the wheel.

[016-121] I struggled desperately to keep it straight, but it swerved to the left

[016-122] and we plunged over a bank into a marshy hollow. There was a sickening

[016-123] bump as we struck the lower ground, and the whole party were shot out

[016-124] into the frozen slush. I don’t yet know how I escaped, for the car

[016-125] turned over and by rights I should have had my back broken. But no one

[016-126] was hurt. Peter was laughing, and Blenkiron, after shaking the snow out

[016-127] of his hair, joined him. For myself I was feverishly examining the

[016-128] machine. It was about as ugly as it could be, for the front axle was

[016-129] broken.

[016-130] Here was a piece of hopeless bad [[fate-and-providence|luck]]. We were stuck in the middle of

[016-131] Asia Minor with no means of conveyance, for to get a new axle there was

[016-132] as likely as to find snowballs on the Congo. It was all but dark and

[016-133] there was no time to lose. I got out the petrol tins and spare tyres

[016-134] and cached them among some rocks on the hillside. Then we collected our

[016-135] scanty baggage from the derelict Studebaker. Our only hope was Hussin.

[016-136] He had got to find us some lodging for the night, and next day we would

[016-137] have a try for horses or a lift in some passing wagon. I had no hope of

[016-138] another car. Every automobile in Anatolia would now be at a premium.

[016-139] It was so disgusting a mishap that we all took it quietly. It was too

[016-140] bad to be helped by hard swearing. Hussin and Peter set off on

[016-141] different sides of the road to prospect for a house, and Blenkiron and

[016-142] I sheltered under the nearest rock and smoked savagely.

[016-143] Hussin was the first to strike oil. He came back in twenty minutes with

[016-144] news of some kind of dwelling a couple of miles up the stream. He went

[016-145] off to collect Peter, and, humping our baggage, Blenkiron and I plodded

[016-146] up the waterside. Darkness had fallen thick by this time, and we took

[016-147] some bad tosses among the bogs. When Hussin and Peter overtook us they

[016-148] found a better road, and presently we saw a light twinkle in the hollow

[016-149] ahead.

[016-150] It proved to be a wretched tumble-down farm in a grove of poplars—a

[016-151] foul-smelling, muddy yard, a two-roomed hovel of a house, and a barn

[016-152] which was tolerably dry and which we selected for our sleeping-place.

[016-153] The owner was a broken old fellow whose sons were all at the war, and

[016-154] he received us with the profound calm of one who expects nothing but

[016-155] unpleasantness from life.

[016-156] By this time we had recovered our tempers, and I was trying hard to put

[016-157] my new Kismet philosophy into practice. I reckoned that if risks were

[016-158] foreordained, so were difficulties, and both must be taken as part of

[016-159] the day’s work. With the remains of our provisions and some curdled

[016-160] milk we satisfied our hunger and curled ourselves up among the pease

[016-161] straw of the barn. Blenkiron announced with a happy sigh that he had

[016-162] now been for two days quit of his dyspepsia.
