## Overview

The Eastern Front — the theatre of World War I stretching from the Baltic to the Caucasus —
is the geographical and strategic setting of *Greenmantle*.
While Hannay begins the novel in the trenches of the Western Front,
the entire mission unfolds in the East:
across Germany, through the Balkans, to Constantinople, and finally
to the Caucasus frontier at Erzerum [005-019].
Buchan deliberately contrasts the static trench warfare of France
with the mobile, unpredictable warfare of the East.

## In Greenmantle

Sir Walter Bullivant's briefing establishes the Eastern Front as the novel's strategic focus.
The German plan to ignite a pan-Islamic uprising targets British positions
in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and India — all Eastern theatres [001-103].
Hannay's journey traces the invasion route from Germany through the Danube basin
to Constantinople, the gateway between Europe and Asia [006-069].

Constantinople serves as the pivot point: the city where German military advisors,
Turkish nationalists, and British intelligence intersect [010-001].
From there the eastern theatre extends in three directions:
south to Mesopotamia and the British campaign [013-020],
east to the Caucasus and the Russian advance on Erzerum [016-106],
and west to the Gallipoli peninsula where Allied forces fight and die [P01-232].

The Eastern Front in *Greenmantle* is characterised by vast distances,
unreliable transport, and the intermingling of regular armies with irregular forces —
Kurdish tribesmen, Circassian cavalry, and Russian Cossacks.
This is a theatre where intelligence, not massed firepower, determines outcomes.

## Thematic Significance

The Eastern Front in *Greenmantle* represents the unknown — a theatre
where conventional military assumptions break down.
The novel draws on contemporary British anxieties about the vulnerability
of the Eastern empire to German intrigue.
Buchan's East is exotic, dangerous, and resistant to rational analysis [015-148],
embodying the strategic uncertainty that made the Eastern Front
a crucible of World War I.
