# Ch. 13: I Move in Good Society (cont'd, part 2)

[013-001] I am bound to confess the prospect staggered me. We might be let in for

[013-002] fighting—and worse than fighting—against our own side. I wondered if it

[013-003] wouldn’t be better to make a bolt for it, and said so.

[013-004] He shook his head. “I reckon not. In the first place we haven’t

[013-005] finished our inquiries. We’ve got Greenmantle located right enough,

[013-006] thanks to you, but we still know mighty little about that holy man. In

[013-007] the second place it won’t be as bad as you think. This show lacks

[013-008] cohesion, Sir. It is not going to last for ever. I calculate that

[013-009] before you and I strike the site of the garden that Adam and Eve

[013-010] frequented there will be a queer turn of affairs. Anyhow, it’s good

[013-011] enough to gamble on.”

[013-012] Then he got some sheets of paper and drew me a plan of the dispositions

[013-013] of the Turkish forces. I had no notion he was such a close student of

[013-014] war, for his exposition was as good as a staff lecture. He made out

[013-015] that the situation was none too bright anywhere. The troops released

[013-016] from [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]] wanted a lot of refitment, and would be slow in reaching

[013-017] the Transcaucasian frontier, where the Russians were threatening. The

[013-018] Army of Syria was pretty nearly a rabble under the lunatic Djemal.

[013-019] There wasn’t the foggiest chance of a serious invasion of Egypt being

[013-020] undertaken. Only in [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]] did things look fairly cheerful, owing

[013-021] to the blunders of British strategy. “And you may take it from me,” he

[013-022] said, “that if the old Turk mobilized a total of a million men, he has

[013-023] lost 40 per cent of them already. And if I’m anything of a prophet he’s

[013-024] going pretty soon to lose more.”

[013-025] He tore up the papers and enlarged on politics. “I reckon I’ve got the

[013-026] measure of the Young Turks and their precious Committee. Those boys

[013-027] aren’t any good. [[ennver-pasha|Enver]]’s bright enough, and for sure he’s got sand.

[013-028] He’ll stick out a fight like a Vermont game-chicken, but he lacks the

[013-029] larger vision, Sir. He doesn’t understand the intricacies of the job no

[013-030] more than a sucking-child, so the Germans play with him, till his

[013-031] temper goes and he bucks like a mule. Talaat is a sulky dog who wants

[013-032] to batter mankind with a club. Both these boys would have made good

[013-033] cow-punchers in the old days, and they might have got a living out West

[013-034] as the gun-men of a Labour Union. They’re about the class of Jesse

[013-035] James or Billy the Kid, excepting that they’re college-reared and can

[013-036] patter languages. But they haven’t the organizing power to manage the

[013-037] Irish vote in a ward election. Their one notion is to get busy with

[013-038] their firearms, and people are getting tired of the Black Hand stunt.

[013-039] Their hold on the country is just the hold that a man with a Browning

[013-040] has over a crowd with walking-sticks. The cooler heads in [[committee-of-union-and-progress|the Committee]]

[013-041] are growing shy of them, and an old fox like David is lying low till

[013-042] his time comes. Now it doesn’t want arguing that a gang of that kind

[013-043] has got to hang close together or they may hang separately. They’ve got

[013-044] no grip on the ordinary Turk, barring the fact that they are active and

[013-045] he is sleepy, and that they’ve got their guns loaded.”

[013-046] “What about the Germans here?” I asked.

[013-047] [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] laughed. “It is no sort of a happy family. But the Young

[013-048] Turks know that without the German boost they’ll be strung up like

[013-049] Haman, and the Germans can’t afford to neglect an ally. Consider what

[013-050] would happen if Turkey got sick of the game and made a separate peace.

[013-051] The road would be open for [[russia|Russia]] to the Aegean. Ferdy of Bulgaria

[013-052] would take his depreciated goods to the other market, and not waste a

[013-053] day thinking about it. You’d have [[rumania|Rumania]] coming in on the Allies’

[013-054] side. Things would look pretty black for that control of the Near East

[013-055] on which Germany has banked her winnings. Kaiser says that’s got to be

[013-056] prevented at all costs, but how is it going to be done?”

[013-057] Blenkiron’s face had become very solemn again. “It won’t be done unless

[013-058] Germany’s got a trump card to play. Her game’s mighty near bust, but

[013-059] it’s still got a chance. And that chance is a woman and an old man. I

[013-060] reckon our landlady has a bigger brain than Enver and Liman. She’s the

[013-061] real boss of the show. When I came here, I reported to her, and

[013-062] presently you’ve got to do the same. I am curious as to how she’ll

[013-063] strike you, for I’m free to admit that she impressed me considerable.”

[013-064] “It looks as if our job were a long way from the end,” I said.

[013-065] “It’s scarcely begun,” said Blenkiron.

[013-066] That talk did a lot to cheer my spirits, for I realized that it was the

[013-067] biggest of big game we were hunting this time. I’m an economical soul,

[013-068] and if I’m going to be hanged I want a good stake for my neck.

[013-069] Then began some varied experiences. I used to wake up in the morning,

[013-070] wondering where I should be at night, and yet quite pleased at the

[013-071] uncertainty. Greenmantle became a sort of myth with me. Somehow I

[013-072] couldn’t fix any idea in my head of what he was like. The nearest I got

[013-073] was a picture of an old man in a turban coming out of a bottle in a

[013-074] cloud of smoke, which I remembered from a child’s edition of the

[013-075] _Arabian Nights_. But if he was dim, the lady was dimmer. Sometimes I

[013-076] thought of her as a fat old German crone, sometimes as a harsh-featured

[013-077] woman like a schoolmistress with thin lips and eyeglasses. But I had to

[013-078] fit [[eastern-front|the East]] into the picture, so I made her young and gave her a touch

[013-079] of the languid houri in a veil. I was always wanting to pump Blenkiron

[013-080] on the subject, but he shut up like a rat-trap. He was looking for bad

[013-081] trouble in that direction, and was disinclined to speak about it

[013-082] beforehand.

[013-083] We led a peaceful existence. Our servants were two of [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]’s lot, for

[013-084] Blenkiron had very rightly cleared out the Turkish caretakers, and they

[013-085] worked like beavers under [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]’s eye, till I reflected I had never

[013-086] been so well looked after in my life. I walked about the city with

[013-087] Blenkiron, keeping my eyes open, and speaking very civil. The third

[013-088] night we were bidden to dinner at Moellendorff’s, so we put on our best

[013-089] clothes and set out in an ancient cab. Blenkiron had fetched a dress

[013-090] suit of mine, from which my own tailor’s label had been cut and a New

[013-091] York one substituted.

[013-092] [[liman|General Liman]] and Metternich the Ambassador had gone up the line to

[013-093] Nish to meet [[kaiser-wilhelm-ii|the Kaiser]], who was touring in those parts, so

[013-094] Moellendorff was the biggest German in the city. He was a thin,

[013-095] foxy-faced fellow, cleverish but monstrously vain, and he was not very

[013-096] popular either with the Germans or the Turks. He was polite to both of

[013-097] us, but I am bound to say that I got a bad fright when I entered the

[013-098] room, for the first man I saw was Gaudian. I doubt if he would have

[013-099] recognized me even in the clothes I had worn in Stumm’s company, for

[013-100] his eyesight was wretched. As it was, I ran no risk in dress-clothes,

[013-101] with my hair brushed back and a fine American accent. I paid him high

[013-102] compliments as a fellow engineer, and translated part of a very

[013-103] technical conversation between him and Blenkiron. Gaudian was in

[013-104] uniform, and I liked the look of his honest face better than ever.

[013-105] But the great event was the sight of Enver. He was a slim fellow of

[013-106] [[rasta-bey|Rasta]]’s build, very foppish and precise in his dress, with a smooth

[013-107] oval face like a girl’s, and rather fine straight black eyebrows. He

[013-108] spoke perfect German, and had the best kind of manners, neither pert

[013-109] nor overbearing. He had a pleasant trick, too, of appealing all round

[013-110] the table for confirmation, and so bringing everybody into the talk.

[013-111] Not that he spoke a great deal, but all he said was good sense, and he

[013-112] had a smiling way of saying it. Once or twice he ran counter to

[013-113] Moellendorff, and I could see there was no love lost between these two.

[013-114] I didn’t think I wanted him as a friend—he was too cold-blooded and

[013-115] artificial; and I was pretty certain that I didn’t want those steady

[013-116] black eyes as an enemy. But it was no good denying his quality. The

[013-117] little fellow was all cold [[courage-and-honour|courage]], like the fine polished blue steel

[013-118] of a sword.

[013-119] I fancy I was rather a success at that dinner. For one thing I could

[013-120] speak German, and so had a pull on Blenkiron. For another I was in a

[013-121] good temper, and really enjoyed putting my back into my part. They

[013-122] talked very high-flown stuff about what they had done and were going to

[013-123] do, and Enver was great on Gallipoli. I remember he said that he could

[013-124] have destroyed the whole British Army if it hadn’t been for somebody’s

[013-125] cold feet—at which Moellendorff looked daggers. They were so bitter

[013-126] about Britain and all her works that I gathered they were getting

[013-127] pretty panicky, and that made me as jolly as a sandboy. I’m afraid I

[013-128] was not free from bitterness myself on that subject. I said things

[013-129] about my own country that I sometimes wake in the night and sweat to

[013-130] think of.

[013-131] Gaudian got on to the use of water power in war, and that gave me a

[013-132] chance.

[013-133] “In my country,” I said, “when we want to get rid of a mountain we wash

[013-134] it away. There’s nothing on earth that will stand against water. Now,

[013-135] speaking with all respect, gentlemen, and as an absolute novice in the

[013-136] military art, I sometimes ask why this God-given weapon isn’t more used

[013-137] in the present war. I haven’t been to any of the fronts, but I’ve

[013-138] studied them some from maps and the newspapers. Take your German

[013-139] position in Flanders, where you’ve got the high ground. If I were a

[013-140] British general I reckon I would very soon make it no sort of

[013-141] position.”

[013-142] Moellendorff asked, “How?”

[013-143] “Why, I’d wash it away. Wash away the fourteen feet of soil down to the

[013-144] stone. There’s a heap of coalpits behind the British front where they

[013-145] could generate power, and I judge there’s ample water supply from the

[013-146] rivers and canals. I’d guarantee to wash you away in twenty-four

[013-147] hours—yes, in spite of all your big guns. It beats me why the British

[013-148] haven’t got on to this notion. They used to have some bright

[013-149] engineers.”

[013-150] Enver was on the point like a knife, far quicker than Gaudian. He

[013-151] cross-examined me in a way that showed he knew how to approach a

[013-152] technical subject, though he mightn’t have much technical knowledge. He

[013-153] was just giving me a sketch of the flooding in Mesopotamia when an

[013-154] aide-de-camp brought in a chit which fetched him to his feet.

[013-155] “I have gossiped long enough,” he said. “My kind host, I must leave

[013-156] you. Gentlemen all, my apologies and farewells.”

[013-157] Before he left he asked my name and wrote it down. “This is an

[013-158] unhealthy city for strangers, Mr Hanau,” he said in very good English.

[013-159] “I have some small power of protecting a friend, and what I have is at

[013-160] your disposal.” This with the condescension of a king promising his

[013-161] favour to a subject.

[013-162] The little fellow amused me tremendously, and rather impressed me too.

[013-163] I said so to Gaudian after he had left, but that decent soul didn’t

[013-164] agree.

[013-165] “I do not love him,” he said. “We are allies—yes; but friends—no. He is

[013-166] no true son of Islam, which is a noble faith and despises liars and

[013-167] boasters and betrayers of their salt.”
