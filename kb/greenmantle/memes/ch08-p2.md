# Ch. 8: The Essen Barges (cont'd, part 2)

[008-001] Next morning after breakfast I took my departure. It was drizzling

[008-002] weather, and I was feeling very lean. Before going I presented my

[008-003] hostess and the children with two sovereigns apiece. “It is English

[008-004] gold,” I said, “for I have to travel among our enemies and use our

[008-005] enemies’ money. But the gold is good, and if you go to any town they

[008-006] will change it for you. But I advise you to put it in your

[008-007] stocking-foot and use it only if all else fails. You must keep your

[008-008] home going, for some day there will be peace and your man will come

[008-009] back from the wars.”

[008-010] I kissed the children, shook the woman’s hand, and went off down the

[008-011] clearing. They had cried “Auf wiedersehen,” but it wasn’t likely I

[008-012] would ever see them again.

[008-013] The snow had all gone, except in patches in the deep hollows. The

[008-014] ground was like a full sponge, and a cold rain drifted in my eyes.

[008-015] After half an hour’s steady trudge the trees thinned, and presently I

[008-016] came out on a knuckle of open ground cloaked in dwarf junipers. And

[008-017] there before me lay the plain, and a mile off a broad brimming river.

[008-018] I sat down and looked dismally at the prospect. The exhilaration of my

[008-019] discovery the day before had gone. I had stumbled on a worthless piece

[008-020] of knowledge, for I could not use it. [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem, if such a person

[008-021] existed and possessed the great secret, was probably living in some big

[008-022] house in [[berlin|Berlin]], and I was about as likely to get anything out of her

[008-023] as to be asked to dine with [[kaiser-wilhelm-ii|the Kaiser]]. [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] might do something,

[008-024] but where on earth was Blenkiron? I dared say [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]] would value

[008-025] the information, but I could not get to Sir Walter. I was to go on to

[008-026] Constantinople, running away from the people who really pulled the

[008-027] ropes. But if I stayed I could do nothing, and I could not stay. I must

[008-028] go on and I didn’t see how I could go on. Every course seemed shut to

[008-029] me, and I was in as pretty a tangle as any man ever stumbled into.

[008-030] For I was morally certain that Stumm would not let the thing drop. I

[008-031] knew too much, and besides I had outraged his pride. He would beat the

[008-032] countryside till he got me, and he undoubtedly would get me if I waited

[008-033] much longer. But how was I to get over the border? My passport would be

[008-034] no good, for the number of that pass would long ere this have been

[008-035] wired to every police-station in Germany, and to produce it would be to

[008-036] ask for trouble. Without it I could not cross the borders by any

[008-037] railway. My studies of the Tourists’ Guide had suggested that once I

[008-038] was in Austria I might find things slacker and move about easier. I

[008-039] thought of having a try at the Tyrol and I also thought of Bohemia. But

[008-040] these places were a long way off, and there were several thousand

[008-041] chances each day that I would be caught on the road.

[008-042] This was Thursday, the 30th of December, the second last day of the

[008-043] year. I was due in Constantinople on the 17th of January.

[008-044] Constantinople! I had thought myself a long way from it in Berlin, but

[008-045] now it seemed as distant as the moon.

[008-046] But that big sullen river in front of me led to it. And as I looked my

[008-047] attention was caught by a curious sight. On the far eastern horizon,

[008-048] where the water slipped round a corner of hill, there was a long trail

[008-049] of smoke. The streamers thinned out, and seemed to come from some boat

[008-050] well round the corner, but I could see at least two boats in view.

[008-051] Therefore there must be a long train of barges, with a tug in tow.

[008-052] I looked to the west and saw another such procession coming into sight.

[008-053] First went a big river steamer—it can’t have been much less than 1,000

[008-054] tons—and after came a string of barges. I counted no less than six

[008-055] besides the tug. They were heavily loaded and their draught must have

[008-056] been considerable, but there was plenty of depth in the flooded river.

[008-057] A moment’s reflection told me what I was looking at. Once [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]], in one

[008-058] of the discussions you have in hospital, had told us just how the

[008-059] Germans munitioned their Balkan campaign. They were pretty certain of

[008-060] dishing Serbia at the first go, and it was up to them to get through

[008-061] guns and shells to the old Turk, who was running pretty short in his

[008-062] first supply. Sandy said that they wanted the railway, but they wanted

[008-063] still more the river, and they could make certain of that in a week. He

[008-064] told us how endless strings of barges, loaded up at the big factories

[008-065] of Westphalia, were moving through the canals from the [[rhine|Rhine]] or the

[008-066] Elbe to the [[danube-river|Danube]]. Once the first reached Turkey, there would be

[008-067] regular delivery, you see—as quick as the Turks could handle the stuff.

[008-068] And they didn’t return empty, Sandy said, but came back full of Turkish

[008-069] cotton and Bulgarian beef and Rumanian corn. I don’t know where Sandy

[008-070] got the knowledge, but there was the proof of it before my eyes.

[008-071] It was a wonderful sight, and I could have gnashed my teeth to see

[008-072] those loads of munitions going snugly off to the enemy. I calculated

[008-073] they would give our poor chaps hell in [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]]. And then, as I

[008-074] looked, an idea came into my head and with it an eighth part of a hope.

[008-075] There was only one way for me to get out of Germany, and that was to

[008-076] leave in such good company that I would be asked no questions. That was

[008-077] plain enough. If I travelled to Turkey, for instance, in the Kaiser’s

[008-078] suite, I would be as safe as the mail; but if I went on my own I was

[008-079] done. I had, so to speak, to get my passport inside Germany, to join

[008-080] some caravan which had free marching powers. And there was the kind of

[008-081] caravan before me—the [[essen|Essen]] barges.

[008-082] It sounded lunacy, for I guessed that munitions of war would be as

[008-083] jealously guarded as old Hindenburg’s health. All the safer, I replied

[008-084] to myself, once I get there. If you are looking for a deserter you

[008-085] don’t seek him at the favourite regimental public-house. If you’re

[008-086] after a thief, among the places you’d be apt to leave unsearched would

[008-087] be Scotland Yard.

[008-088] It was sound reasoning, but how was I to get on board? Probably the

[008-089] beastly things did not stop once in a hundred miles, and Stumm would

[008-090] get me long before I struck a halting-place. And even if I did get a

[008-091] chance like that, how was I to get permission to travel?

[008-092] One step was clearly indicated—to get down to the river bank at once.

[008-093] So I set off at a sharp walk across squelchy fields, till I struck a

[008-094] road where the ditches had overflowed so as almost to meet in the

[008-095] middle. The place was so bad that I hoped travellers might be few. And

[008-096] as I trudged, my thoughts were busy with my prospects as a stowaway. If

[008-097] I bought food, I might get a chance to lie snug on one of the barges.

[008-098] They would not break bulk till they got to their journey’s end.

[008-099] Suddenly I noticed that the steamer, which was now abreast me, began to

[008-100] move towards the shore, and as I came over a low rise, I saw on my left

[008-101] a straggling village with a church, and a small landing-stage. The

[008-102] houses stood about a quarter of a mile from the stream, and between

[008-103] them was a straight, poplar-fringed road.

[008-104] Soon there could be no doubt about it. The procession was coming to a

[008-105] standstill. The big tug nosed her way in and lay up alongside the pier,

[008-106] where in that season of flood there was enough depth of water. She

[008-107] signalled to the barges and they also started to drop anchors, which

[008-108] showed that there must be at least two men aboard each. Some of them

[008-109] dragged a bit and it was rather a cock-eyed train that lay in

[008-110] mid-stream. The tug got out a gangway, and from where I lay I saw half

[008-111] a dozen men leave it, carrying something on their shoulders.

[008-112] It could be only one thing—a dead body. Someone of the crew must have

[008-113] died, and this halt was to bury him. I watched the procession move

[008-114] towards the village and I reckoned they would take some time there,

[008-115] though they might have wired ahead for a grave to be dug. Anyhow, they

[008-116] would be long enough to give me a chance.

[008-117] For I had decided upon the brazen course. Blenkiron had said you

[008-118] couldn’t cheat the Boche, but you could bluff him. I was going to put

[008-119] up the most monstrous bluff. If the whole countryside was hunting for

[008-120] Richard Hannay, Richard Hannay would walk through as a pal of the

[008-121] hunters. For I remembered the pass Stumm had given me. If that was

[008-122] worth a tinker’s curse it should be good enough to impress a ship’s

[008-123] captain.

[008-124] Of course there were a thousand risks. They might have heard of me in

[008-125] the village and told the ship’s party the story. For that reason I

[008-126] resolved not to go there but to meet the sailors when they were

[008-127] returning to the boat. Or the captain might have been warned and got

[008-128] the number of my pass, in which case Stumm would have his hands on me

[008-129] pretty soon. Or the captain might be an ignorant fellow who had never

[008-130] seen a [[espionage|Secret Service]] pass and did not know what it meant, and would

[008-131] refuse me transport by the letter of his instructions. In that case I

[008-132] might wait on another convoy.

[008-133] I had shaved and made myself a fairly respectable figure before I left

[008-134] the cottage. It was my cue to wait for the men when they left the

[008-135] church, wait on that quarter-mile of straight highway. I judged the

[008-136] captain must be in the party. The village, I was glad to observe,

[008-137] seemed very empty. I have my own notions about the Bavarians as

[008-138] fighting men, but I am bound to say that, judging by my observations,

[008-139] very few of them stayed at home.

[008-140] That funeral took hours. They must have had to dig the grave, for I

[008-141] waited near the road in a clump of cherry-trees, with my feet in two

[008-142] inches of mud and water, till I felt chilled to the bone. I prayed to

[008-143] God it would not bring back my fever, for I was only one day out of

[008-144] bed. I had very little tobacco left in my pouch, but I stood myself one

[008-145] pipe, and I ate one of the three cakes of chocolate I still carried.

[008-146] At last, well after midday, I could see the ship’s party returning.

[008-147] They marched two by two and I was thankful to see that they had no

[008-148] villagers with them. I walked to the road, turned up it, and met the

[008-149] vanguard, carrying my head as high as I knew how.

[008-150] “Where’s your captain?” I asked, and a man jerked his thumb over his

[008-151] shoulder. The others wore thick jerseys and knitted caps, but there was

[008-152] one man at the rear in uniform.

[008-153] He was a short, broad man with a weather-beaten face and an anxious

[008-154] eye.

[008-155] “May I have a word with you, Herr Captain?” I said, with what I hoped

[008-156] was a judicious blend of authority and conciliation.

[008-157] He nodded to his companion, who walked on.

[008-158] “Yes?” he asked rather impatiently.

[008-159] I proffered him my pass. Thank Heaven he had seen the kind of thing

[008-160] before, for his face at once took on that curious look which one person

[008-161] in authority always wears when he is confronted with another. He

[008-162] studied it closely and then raised his eyes.
