# Islam

> "Islam is the one thing that can really bite us." — Sir Walter Bullivant on the strategic threat [001-006]

## Overview

Islam occupies a central position in *Greenmantle* not as a religion to be described or evaluated, but as a geopolitical force that the antagonists seek to harness. Sir Walter Bullivant's opening analysis frames the entire novel: Germany's strategy is to transform Islam from a dormant cultural presence into an active instrument of war against the British Empire [001-006].

The novel engages with Islam at three levels: as intelligence problem (how can Germany weaponize it?), as cultural encounter (how do British characters understand or misunderstand it?), and as dramatic antagonist (the force the heroes must neutralize). Throughout, the portrayal reflects early-20th-century British Orientalist assumptions while also demonstrating genuine awareness of Islam's political potency.

## Islam as Strategic Threat

### Bullivant's Analysis

Sir Walter's briefing establishes the terms of engagement: "Islam has played so small a part in most people's explanations" of Turkish entry into the war [001-006]. This is, he argues, a dangerous oversight. The British have been thinking of the Ottoman Empire in purely political terms — the Young Turks, German influence, military alliances — while neglecting the religious dimension that could transform the conflict.

"There is a dry wind blowing through the East, and the parched grasses wait the spark" [001-005]. Bullivant's metaphor captures the novel's thesis: Islam is not inherently hostile to Britain, but it is combustible material that German intelligence intends to ignite. The spark is Greenmantle.

### The Kaiser's Failed Holy War

The Kaiser's proclamation of jihad has "fallen pretty flat" [001-007], Sir Walter notes. This failure is instructive: the temporal authority of a Christian emperor cannot command Islamic spiritual allegiance. The Germans have learned this lesson and are attempting something more sophisticated — not a proclamation but a prophecy, not a command but a movement.

### The India Question

The strategic calculus centers on British India, where millions of Muslim subjects live under colonial rule. A genuine pan-Islamic uprising could threaten the jewel of the British Empire. "Islam is the one thing that can really bite us," Bullivant concludes [001-006], and the bite would be felt in the subcontinent.

## Cultural Encounter

### Sandy Arbuthnot's Expertise

Sandy Arbuthnot is the novel's vehicle for Islamic cultural knowledge. Unlike Hannay, who approaches the problem through the lens of military intelligence, Sandy understands the spiritual dimension: he has traveled in the Islamic world, speaks its languages, and grasps the symbolic logic that Western analysis misses.

Sandy's expertise is presented as exceptional — most British characters are portrayed as dangerously ignorant of Islamic culture. The novel implies that understanding Islam is not a matter of intelligence gathering alone but requires a kind of cultural immersion that few Westerners achieve.

### Orientalist Framing

The novel's portrayal of Islam reflects the Orientalist conventions of its era. The Islamic world is characterized as mysterious, potentially explosive, and comprehensible only to a few enlightened Westerners. The "dry wind" metaphor [001-005] positions Islam as a natural force — elemental, pre-rational, waiting to be channeled — rather than a complex religious civilization with its own intellectual traditions.

## In the Narrative

Islam functions less as a subject of exposition than as the medium through which the conspiracy operates. The German plan to harness Islamic sentiment provides the novel's dramatic engine; the question of whether and how that plan can be countered provides its suspense. The religion itself is not interrogated — it is the uses to which religion can be put that interest Buchan.

The resolution at Erzerum [Chapter 22] suggests that manufactured religious enthusiasm can be deflated by direct action. The novel's implicit argument is that religious movements mobilized for political purposes are vulnerable to the exposure of their political machinery — a thesis that would prove more complex in the century that followed.
