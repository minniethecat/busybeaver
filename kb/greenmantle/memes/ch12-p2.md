# Ch. 12: Four Missionaries See Light in Their Mission (cont'd, part 2)

[012-001] “I went straight to Smyrna,” he said. “It wasn’t difficult, for you see

[012-002] I had laid down a good many lines in former travels. I reached the town

[012-003] as a Greek money-lender from the Fayum, but I had friends there I could

[012-004] count on, and the same evening I was a Turkish gipsy, a member of the

[012-005] most famous fraternity in Western Asia. I had long been a member, and

[012-006] I’m blood-brother of the chief boss, so I stepped into the part ready

[012-007] made. But I found out that the Company of the Rosy Hours was not what I

[012-008] had known it in 1910. Then it had been all for the Young Turks and

[012-009] reform; now it hankered after the old regime and was the last hope of

[012-010] the Orthodox. It had no use for [[ennver-pasha|Enver]] and his friends, and it did not

[012-011] regard with pleasure the _beaux yeux_ of the Teuton. It stood for Islam

[012-012] and the old ways, and might be described as a Conservative-Nationalist

[012-013] caucus. But it was uncommon powerful in the provinces, and Enver and

[012-014] Talaat daren’t meddle with it. The dangerous thing about it was that it

[012-015] said nothing and apparently did nothing. It just bided its time and

[012-016] took notes.

[012-017] “You can imagine that this was the very kind of crowd for my purpose. I

[012-018] knew of old its little ways, for with all its orthodoxy it dabbled a

[012-019] good deal in magic, and owed half its power to its atmosphere of the

[012-020] uncanny. The Companions could dance the heart out of the ordinary Turk.

[012-021] You saw a bit of one of our dances this afternoon, Dick—pretty good,

[012-022] wasn’t it? They could go anywhere, and no questions asked. They knew

[012-023] what the ordinary man was thinking, for they were the best intelligence

[012-024] department in the Ottoman Empire—far better than Enver’s _Khafiyeh_.

[012-025] And they were popular, too, for they had never bowed the knee to the

[012-026] _Nemseh_—the Germans who are squeezing out the life-blood of the

[012-027] Osmanli for their own ends. It would have been as much as the life of

[012-028] [[committee-of-union-and-progress|the Committee]] or its German masters was worth to lay a hand on us, for

[012-029] we clung together like leeches and we were not in the habit of sticking

[012-030] at trifles.

[012-031] “Well, you may imagine it wasn’t difficult for me to move where I

[012-032] wanted. My dress and the pass-word franked me anywhere. I travelled

[012-033] from Smyrna by the new railway to Panderma on the Marmora, and got

[012-034] there just before Christmas. That was after Anzac and Suvla had been

[012-035] evacuated, but I could hear the guns going hard at [[cape-helles|Cape Helles]]. From

[012-036] Panderma I started to cross to Thrace in a coasting steamer. And there

[012-037] an uncommon funny thing happened—I got torpedoed.

[012-038] “It must have been about the last effort of a British submarine in

[012-039] those waters. But she got us all right. She gave us ten minutes to take

[012-040] to the boats, and then sent the blighted old packet and a fine cargo of

[012-041] 6-inch shells to the bottom. There weren’t many passengers, so it was

[012-042] easy enough to get ashore in the ship’s boats. The submarine sat on the

[012-043] surface watching us, as we wailed and howled in the true Oriental way,

[012-044] and I saw the captain quite close in the conning-tower. Who do you

[012-045] think it was? Tommy Elliot, who lives on the other side of the hill

[012-046] from me at home.

[012-047] “I gave Tommy the surprise of his life. As we bumped past him, I

[012-048] started the ‘Flowers of the Forest’—the old version—on the antique

[012-049] stringed instrument I carried, and I sang the words very plain. Tommy’s

[012-050] eyes bulged out of his head, and he shouted at me in English to know

[012-051] who the devil I was. I replied in the broadest Scots, which no man in

[012-052] the submarine or in our boat could have understood a word of. ‘Maister

[012-053] Tammy,’ I cried, ‘what for wad ye skail a dacent tinkler lad intil a

[012-054] cauld sea? I’ll gie ye your kail through the reek for this ploy the

[012-055] next time I forgaither wi’ ye on the tap o’ Caerdon.’

[012-056] “Tommy spotted me in a second. He laughed till he cried, and as we

[012-057] moved off shouted to me in the same language to ‘pit a stoot hert tae a

[012-058] stey brae’. I hope to Heaven he had the sense not to tell my father, or

[012-059] the old man will have had a fit. He never much approved of my

[012-060] wanderings, and thought I was safely anchored in the battalion.

[012-061] “Well, to make a long story short, I got to Constantinople, and pretty

[012-062] soon found touch with [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]. The rest you know. And now for

[012-063] business. I have been fairly lucky—but no more, for I haven’t got to

[012-064] the bottom of the thing nor anything like it. But I’ve solved the first

[012-065] of [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] [[sir-walter-bullivant|Bullivant]]’s riddles. I know the meaning of _Kasredin_.

[012-066] “Sir Walter was right, as Blenkiron has told us. There’s a great

[012-067] stirring in Islam, something moving on the face of the waters. They

[012-068] make no secret of it. Those religious revivals come in cycles, and one

[012-069] was due about now. And they are quite clear about the details. A seer

[012-070] has arisen of the blood of the Prophet, who will restore the Khalifate

[012-071] to its old glories and Islam to its old purity. His sayings are

[012-072] everywhere in the Moslem world. All the orthodox believers have them by

[012-073] heart. That is why they are enduring grinding poverty and preposterous

[012-074] taxation, and that is why their young men are rolling up to the armies

[012-075] and dying without complaint in [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]] and Transcaucasia. They

[012-076] believe they are on the eve of a great deliverance.

[012-077] “Now the first thing I found out was that the Young Turks had nothing

[012-078] to do with this. They are unpopular and unorthodox, and no true Turks.

[012-079] But Germany has. How, I don’t know, but I could see quite plainly that

[012-080] in some subtle way Germany was regarded as a collaborator in the

[012-081] movement. It is that belief that is keeping the present regime going.

[012-082] The ordinary Turk loathes the Committee, but he has some queer

[012-083] perverted expectation from Germany. It is not a case of Enver and the

[012-084] rest carrying on their shoulders the unpopular Teuton; it is a case of

[012-085] the Teuton carrying the unpopular Committee. And Germany’s graft is

[012-086] just this and nothing more—that she has some hand in the coming of the

[012-087] new deliverer.

[012-088] “They talk about the thing quite openly. It is called the

[012-089] _Kaába-i-hurriyeh_, the Palladium of Liberty. The prophet himself is

[012-090] known as Zimrud—‘the Emerald’—and his four ministers are called also

[012-091] after jewels—Sapphire, Ruby, Pearl, and Topaz. You will hear their

[012-092] names as often in the talk of the towns and villages as you will hear

[012-093] the names of generals in England. But no one knew where Zimrud was or

[012-094] when he would reveal himself, though every week came his messages to

[012-095] the faithful. All that I could learn was that he and his followers were

[012-096] coming from the West.

[012-097] “You will say, what about _Kasredin_? That puzzled me dreadfully, for

[012-098] no one used the phrase. The Home of the Spirit! It is an obvious

[012-099] cliche, just as in England some new sect might call itself the Church

[012-100] of Christ. Only no one seemed to use it.

[012-101] “But by and by I discovered that there was an inner and an outer circle

[012-102] in this mystery. Every creed has an esoteric side which is kept from

[012-103] the common herd. I struck this side in Constantinople. Now there is a

[012-104] very famous Turkish _shaka_ called _Kasredin_, one of those old

[012-105] half-comic miracle plays with an allegorical meaning which they call

[012-106] _orta oyun_, and which take a week to read. That tale tells of the

[012-107] coming of a prophet, and I found that the select of the faith spoke of

[012-108] the new revelation in terms of it. The curious thing is that in that

[012-109] tale the prophet is aided by one of the few women who play much part in

[012-110] the hagiology of Islam. That is the point of the tale, and it is partly

[012-111] a jest, but mainly a religious mystery. The prophet, too, is not called

[012-112] Emerald.”

[012-113] “I know,” I said; “he is called Greenmantle.”

[012-114] [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] scrambled to his feet, letting his pipe drop in the fireplace.

[012-115] “Now how on earth did you find out that?” he cried.

[012-116] Then I told them of Stumm and Gaudian and the whispered words I had not

[012-117] been meant to hear. Blenkiron was giving me the benefit of a steady

[012-118] stare, unusual from one who seemed always to have his eyes abstracted,

[012-119] and Sandy had taken to ranging up and down the room.

[012-120] “Germany’s in the heart of the plan. That is what I always thought. If

[012-121] we’re to find the _Kaába-i-hurriyeh_ it is no good fossicking among the

[012-122] Committee or in the Turkish provinces. The secret’s in Germany. Dick,

[012-123] you should not have crossed the [[danube-river|Danube]].”

[012-124] “That’s what I half feared,” I said. “But on the other hand it is

[012-125] obvious that the thing must come east, and sooner rather than later. I

[012-126] take it they can’t afford to delay too long before they deliver the

[012-127] goods. If we can stick it out here we must hit the trail ... I’ve got

[012-128] another bit of evidence. I have solved Harry Bullivant’s third puzzle.”

[012-129] Sandy’s eyes were very bright and I had an audience on wires.

[012-130] “Did you say that in the tale of _Kasredin_ a woman is the ally of the

[012-131] prophet?”

[012-132] “Yes,” said Sandy; “what of that?”

[012-133] “Only that the same thing is true of Greenmantle. I can give you her

[012-134] name.”

[012-135] I fetched a piece of paper and a pencil from Blenkiron’s desk and

[012-136] handed it to Sandy.

[012-137] “Write down Harry Bullivant’s third word.”

[012-138] He promptly wrote down “_v. I._”

[012-139] Then I told them of the other name Stumm and Gaudian had spoken. I told

[012-140] of my discovery as I lay in the woodman’s cottage.

[012-141] “The ‘I’ is not the letter of the alphabet, but the numeral. The name

[012-142] is Von Einem—[[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem.”

[012-143] “Good old Harry,” said Sandy softly. “He was a dashed clever chap.

[012-144] Hilda von Einem? Who and where is she? for if we find her we have done

[012-145] the trick.”

[012-146] Then Blenkiron spoke. “I reckon I can put you wise on that, gentlemen,”

[012-147] he said. “I saw her no later than yesterday. She is a lovely lady. She

[012-148] happens also to be the owner of this house.”

[012-149] Both Sandy and I began to laugh. It was too comic to have stumbled

[012-150] across Europe and lighted on the very headquarters of the puzzle we had

[012-151] set out to unriddle.

[012-152] But Blenkiron did not laugh. At the mention of Hilda von Einem he had

[012-153] suddenly become very solemn, and the sight of his face pulled me up

[012-154] short.

[012-155] “I don’t like it, gentlemen,” he said. “I would rather you had

[012-156] mentioned any other name on God’s earth. I haven’t been long in this

[012-157] city, but I have been long enough to size up the various political

[012-158] bosses. They haven’t much to them. I reckon they wouldn’t stand up

[012-159] against what we could show them in the U-nited States. But I have met

[012-160] the Frau von Einem, and that lady’s a very different proposition. The

[012-161] man that will understand her has got to take a biggish size in hats.”

[012-162] “Who is she?” I asked.

[012-163] “Why, that is just what I can’t tell you. She was a great excavator of

[012-164] Babylonish and Hittite ruins, and she married a diplomat who went to

[012-165] glory three years back. It isn’t what she has been, but what she is,

[012-166] and that’s a mighty clever woman.”

[012-167] Blenkiron’s respect did not depress me. I felt as if at last we had got

[012-168] our job narrowed to a decent compass, for I had hated casting about in

[012-169] the dark. I asked where she lived.

[012-170] “That I don’t know,” said Blenkiron. “You won’t find people unduly

[012-171] anxious to gratify your natural curiosity about Frau von Einem.”
