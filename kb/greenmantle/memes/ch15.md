# Ch. 15: An Embarrassed Toilet

[015-001] Once, I remember, a doctor on board ship who dabbled in hypnotism told

[015-002] me that I was the most unsympathetic person he had ever struck. He said

[015-003] I was about as good a mesmeric subject as Table Mountain. Suddenly I

[015-004] began to realize that this woman was trying to cast some spell over me.

[015-005] The eyes grew large and luminous, and I was conscious for just an

[015-006] instant of some will battling to subject mine. I was aware, too, in the

[015-007] same moment of a strange scent which recalled that wild hour in

[015-008] Kuprasso’s garden-house. It passed quickly, and for a second her eyes

[015-009] drooped. I seemed to read in them failure, and yet a kind of

[015-010] satisfaction, too, as if they had found more in me than they expected.

[015-011] “What life have you led?” the soft voice was saying.

[015-012] I was able to answer quite naturally, rather to my surprise. “I have

[015-013] been a mining engineer up and down the world.”

[015-014] “You have faced danger many times?”

[015-015] “I have faced danger.”

[015-016] “You have fought with men in battles?”

[015-017] “I have fought in battles.”

[015-018] Her bosom rose and fell in a kind of sigh. A smile—a very beautiful

[015-019] thing—flitted over her face. She gave me her hand. “The horses are at

[015-020] the door now,” she said, “and your servant is with them. One of my

[015-021] people will guide you to the city.”

[015-022] She turned away and passed out of the circle of light into the darkness

[015-023] beyond ...

[015-024] [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] and I jogged home in the rain with one of [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]’s skin-clad

[015-025] Companions loping at our side. We did not speak a word, for my thoughts

[015-026] were running like hounds on the track of the past hours. I had seen the

[015-027] mysterious [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem, I had spoken to her, I had held her hand.

[015-028] She had insulted me with the subtlest of insults and yet I was not

[015-029] angry. Suddenly the game I was playing became invested with a

[015-030] tremendous solemnity. My old antagonists, Stumm and [[rasta-bey|Rasta]] and the whole

[015-031] German Empire, seemed to shrink into the background, leaving only the

[015-032] slim woman with her inscrutable smile and devouring eyes. “Mad and

[015-033] bad,” [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] had called her, “but principally bad.” I did not think

[015-034] they were the proper terms, for they belonged to the narrow world of

[015-035] our common experience. This was something beyond and above it, as a

[015-036] cyclone or an earthquake is outside the decent routine of nature. Mad

[015-037] and bad she might be, but she was also great.

[015-038] Before we arrived our guide had plucked my knee and spoken some words

[015-039] which he had obviously got by heart. “The Master says,” ran the

[015-040] message, “expect him at midnight.”

[015-041] CHAPTER XV.

[015-042] An Embarrassed Toilet

[015-043] I was soaked to the bone, and while Peter set off to look for dinner I

[015-044] went to my room to change. I had a rubdown and then got into pyjamas

[015-045] for some dumb-bell exercises with two chairs, for that long wet ride

[015-046] had stiffened my arm and shoulder muscles. They were a vulgar suit of

[015-047] primitive blue, which Blenkiron had looted from my [[london|London]] wardrobe. As

[015-048] Cornelis Brandt I had sported a flannel nightgown.

[015-049] My bedroom opened off the sitting-room, and while I was busy with my

[015-050] gymnastics I heard the door open. I thought at first it was Blenkiron,

[015-051] but the briskness of the tread was unlike his measured gait. I had left

[015-052] the light burning there, and the visitor, whoever he was, had made

[015-053] himself at home. I slipped on a green dressing-gown Blenkiron had lent

[015-054] me, and sallied forth to investigate.

[015-055] My friend Rasta was standing by the table, on which he had laid an

[015-056] envelope. He looked round at my entrance and saluted.

[015-057] “I come from the Minister of War, sir,” he said, “and bring you your

[015-058] passports for tomorrow. You will travel by ...” And then his voice

[015-059] tailed away and his black eyes narrowed to slits. He had seen something

[015-060] which switched him off the metals.

[015-061] At that moment I saw it too. There was a mirror on the wall behind him,

[015-062] and as I faced him I could not help seeing my reflection. It was the

[015-063] exact image of the engineer on the [[danube-river|Danube]] boat—blue jeans, _loden_

[015-064] cloak, and all. The accursed mischance of my costume had given him the

[015-065] clue to an identity which was otherwise buried deep in the [[bosporus|Bosporus]].

[015-066] I am bound to say for Rasta that he was a man of quick action. In a

[015-067] trice he had whipped round to the other side of the table between me

[015-068] and the door, where he stood regarding me wickedly.

[015-069] By this time I was at the table and stretched out a hand for the

[015-070] envelope. My one hope was nonchalance.

[015-071] “Sit down, sir,” I said, “and have a drink. It’s a filthy night to move

[015-072] about in.”

[015-073] “Thank you, no, [[hermann-brandt|Herr Brandt]],” he said. “You may burn these passports

[015-074] for they will not be used.”

[015-075] “Whatever’s the matter with you?” I cried. “You’ve mistaken the house,

[015-076] my lad. I’m called Hanau—Richard Hanau—and my partner’s Mr John S.

[015-077] Blenkiron. He’ll be here presently. Never knew anyone of the name of

[015-078] Brandt, barring a tobacconist in Denver City.”

[015-079] “You have never been to [[rustchuk|Rustchuk]]?” he said with a sneer.

[015-080] “Not that I know of. But, pardon me, Sir, if I ask your name and your

[015-081] business here. I’m darned if I’m accustomed to be called by Dutch names

[015-082] or have my word doubted. In my country we consider that impolite as

[015-083] between gentlemen.”

[015-084] I could see that my bluff was having its effect. His stare began to

[015-085] waver, and when he next spoke it was in a more civil tone.

[015-086] “I will ask pardon if I’m mistaken, Sir, but you’re the image of a man

[015-087] who a week ago was at Rustchuk, a man much wanted by the Imperial

[015-088] Government.”

[015-089] “A week ago I was tossing in a dirty little hooker coming from

[015-090] Constanza. Unless Rustchuk’s in the middle of the [[black-sea|Black Sea]] I’ve never

[015-091] visited the township. I guess you’re barking up the wrong tree. Come to

[015-092] think of it, I was expecting passports. Say, do you come from [[ennver-pasha|Enver]]

[015-093] Damad?”

[015-094] “I have that [[courage-and-honour|honour]],” he said.

[015-095] “Well, Enver is a very good friend of mine. He’s the brightest citizen

[015-096] I’ve struck this side of the Atlantic.”

[015-097] The man was calming down, and in another minute his suspicions would

[015-098] have gone. But at that moment, by the crookedest kind of [[fate-and-providence|luck]], Peter

[015-099] entered with a tray of dishes. He did not notice Rasta, and walked

[015-100] straight to the table and plumped down his burden on it. The Turk had

[015-101] stepped aside at his entrance, and I saw by the look in his eyes that

[015-102] his suspicions had become a certainty. For Peter, stripped to shirt and

[015-103] breeches, was the identical shabby little companion of the Rustchuk

[015-104] meeting.

[015-105] I had never doubted Rasta’s pluck. He jumped for the door and had a

[015-106] pistol out in a trice pointing at my head.

[015-107] “_Bonne fortune_,” he cried. “Both the birds at one shot.” His hand was

[015-108] on the latch, and his mouth was open to cry. I guessed there was an

[015-109] orderly waiting on the stairs.

[015-110] He had what you call the strategic advantage, for he was at the door

[015-111] while I was at the other end of the table and Peter at the side of it

[015-112] at least two yards from him. The road was clear before him, and neither

[015-113] of us was armed. I made a despairing step forward, not knowing what I

[015-114] meant to do, for I saw no light. But Peter was before me.

[015-115] He had never let go of the tray, and now, as a boy skims a stone on a

[015-116] pond, he skimmed it with its contents at Rasta’s head. The man was

[015-117] opening the door with one hand while he kept me covered with the other,

[015-118] and he got the contrivance fairly in the face. A pistol shot cracked

[015-119] out, and the bullet went through the tray, but the noise was drowned in

[015-120] the crash of glasses and crockery. The next second Peter had wrenched

[015-121] the pistol from Rasta’s hand and had gripped his throat.

[015-122] A dandified Young Turk, brought up in Paris and finished in [[berlin|Berlin]], may

[015-123] be as brave as a lion, but he cannot stand in a rough-and-tumble

[015-124] against a backveld hunter, though more than double his age. There was

[015-125] no need for me to help him. Peter had his own way, learned in a wild

[015-126] school, of knocking the sense out of a foe. He gagged him

[015-127] scientifically, and trussed him up with his own belt and two straps

[015-128] from a trunk in my bedroom.

[015-129] “This man is too dangerous to let go,” he said, as if his procedure

[015-130] were the most ordinary thing in the world. “He will be quiet now till

[015-131] we have time to make a plan.”

[015-132] At that moment there came a knocking at the door. That is the sort of

[015-133] thing that happens in melodrama, just when the villain has finished off

[015-134] his job neatly. The correct thing to do is to pale to the teeth, and

[015-135] with a rolling, conscience-stricken eye glare round the horizon. But

[015-136] that was not Peter’s way.

[015-137] “We’d better tidy up if we’re to have visitors,” he said calmly.

[015-138] Now there was one of those big oak German cupboards against the wall

[015-139] which must have been brought in in sections, for complete it would

[015-140] never have got through the door. It was empty now, but for Blenkiron’s

[015-141] hatbox. In it he deposited the unconscious Rasta, and turned the key.

[015-142] “There’s enough ventilation through the top,” he observed, “to keep the

[015-143] air good.” Then he opened the door. A magnificent kavass in blue and

[015-144] silver stood outside. He saluted and proffered a card on which was

[015-145] written in pencil, “Hilda von Einem”.

[015-146] I would have begged for time to change my clothes, but the lady was

[015-147] behind him. I saw the black mantilla and the rich sable furs. Peter

[015-148] vanished through my bedroom and I was left to receive my guest in a

[015-149] room littered with broken glass and a senseless man in the cupboard.

[015-150] There are some situations so crazily extravagant that they key up the

[015-151] spirit to meet them. I was almost laughing when that stately lady

[015-152] stepped over my threshold.

[015-153] “Madam,” I said, with a bow that shamed my old dressing-gown and

[015-154] strident pyjamas. “You find me at a disadvantage. I came home soaking

[015-155] from my ride, and was in the act of changing. My servant has just upset

[015-156] a tray of crockery, and I fear this room’s no fit place for a lady.

[015-157] Allow me three minutes to make myself presentable.”

[015-158] She inclined her head gravely and took a seat by the fire. I went into

[015-159] my bedroom, and as I expected found Peter lurking by the other door. In

[015-160] a hectic sentence I bade him get Rasta’s orderly out of the place on

[015-161] any pretext, and tell him his master would return later. Then I hurried

[015-162] into decent garments, and came out to find my visitor in a brown study.

[015-163] At the sound of my entrance she started from her dream and stood up on

[015-164] the hearthrug, slipping the long robe of fur from her slim body.

[015-165] “We are alone?” she said. “We will not be disturbed?”

[015-166] Then an inspiration came to me. I remembered that Frau von Einem,

[015-167] according to Blenkiron, did not see eye to eye with the Young Turks;

[015-168] and I had a queer instinct that Rasta could not be to her liking. So I

[015-169] spoke the truth.

[015-170] “I must tell you that there’s another guest here tonight. I reckon he’s

[015-171] feeling pretty uncomfortable. At present he’s trussed up on a shelf in

[015-172] that cupboard.”

[015-173] She did not trouble to look round.

[015-174] “Is he dead?” she asked calmly.

[015-175] “By no means,” I said, “but he’s fixed so he can’t speak, and I guess

[015-176] he can’t hear much.”
