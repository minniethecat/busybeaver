# Young Turks

> "In 1910 it had been all for the Young Turks and progress." — Sandy Arbuthnot [012-008]

## Overview

The Young Turk movement — formally the Committee of Union and Progress (CUP) — was the revolutionary nationalist faction that controlled the Ottoman Empire during the events of *Greenmantle*. In the novel, the Young Turks represent the political architecture through which the German conspiracy operates: they are the Ottoman regime that has aligned with Germany, providing the territorial and institutional base for the Greenmantle plot.

Buchan's portrayal is ambivalent. The Young Turks are presented as modernisers who have displaced the old Ottoman order, but their modernisation is depicted as superficial — a veneer of European manners over what the novel consistently implies is Eastern political chaos. This ambivalence reflects contemporaneous British attitudes toward the Ottoman regime, which was simultaneously an enemy power and a subject of Orientalist fascination.

## In Greenmantle

### Sandy's Assessment

Sandy Arbuthnot, who knew Constantinople before the war, provides the novel's most sustained analysis of the Young Turk regime. "In 1910 it had been all for the Young Turks and progress," he recalls, contrasting the earlier enthusiasm with the present reality [012-008]. The Committee, he explains, has "no use for Enver and his friends" at the popular level, but is "uncommon powerful in the provinces" [012-013].

The Young Turks' security apparatus — the *Khafiyeh* — is described as "far better than Enver's" intelligence service, suggesting a regime organised around internal surveillance and political control [012-024]. This surveillance state is the environment through which Hannay and his companions must navigate.

### Enver Pasha as Embodiment

Enver Pasha serves as the novel's personification of the Young Turk regime. He appears in person at a dinner in Constantinople: "a slim fellow of middle height, very slender and very graceful" [013-105]. His intelligence is acknowledged — "Enver was on the point like a knife, far quicker than Gaudian" [013-150] — but his regime is presented as brittle, dependent on German support and vulnerable to the deeper currents of Islamic sentiment that the Germans seek to exploit.

Blenkiron, with characteristic American bluntness, dismisses the Young Turk leadership: "Those boys aren't any good. Enver's bright enough, and for sure he's got sand. But they're a half-baked bunch" [013-026]. His landlady in Constantinople, he claims, "has a bigger brain than Enver and Liman" [013-060].

### The German Relationship

The novel presents the Young Turk–German alliance as a marriage of convenience. The Germans provide military and technical support; the Young Turks provide access to the Islamic world. But the relationship is unstable: "there is no love lost between Enver and those I serve," as one character observes [017-131]. The Young Turks are nationalist modernisers; the Germans are orchestrating a religious movement that could escape political control. This tension runs beneath the surface of the narrative.

## Thematic Significance

The Young Turks in *Greenmantle* represent the novel's engagement with the political modernisation of the East. They are presented as an unstable halfway point between traditional Ottoman rule and something more modern — but their modernisation is depicted as mimicry rather than genuine transformation. This is consistent with the Orientalist framework that structures much of the novel's representation of the non-European world.

The movement also serves a narrative function: by embodying Ottoman political authority in specific, fallible individuals (Enver, the Committee), the novel makes the Eastern theatre comprehensible to its British readership. Geopolitics becomes personality.
