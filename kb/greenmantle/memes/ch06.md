# Ch. 6: The Indiscretions of the Same

[006-001] I had another three hours of complete boredom. I dared not smoke, and

[006-002] could do nothing but stare out of the window. We soon got into hilly

[006-003] country, where a good deal of snow was lying. It was the 23rd day of

[006-004] December, and even in war time one had a sort of feel of Christmas. You

[006-005] could see girls carrying evergreens, and when we stopped at a station

[006-006] the soldiers on leave had all the air of holiday making. The middle of

[006-007] Germany was a cheerier place than [[berlin|Berlin]] or the western parts. I liked

[006-008] the look of the old peasants, and the women in their neat Sunday best,

[006-009] but I noticed, too, how pinched they were. Here in the country, where

[006-010] no neutral tourists came, there was not the same stage-management as in

[006-011] the capital.

[006-012] Stumm made an attempt to talk to me on the journey. I could see his

[006-013] aim. Before this he had cross-examined me, but now he wanted to draw me

[006-014] into ordinary conversation. He had no notion how to do it. He was

[006-015] either peremptory and provocative, like a drill-sergeant, or so

[006-016] obviously diplomatic that any fool would have been put on his guard.

[006-017] That is the weakness of the German. He has no gift for laying himself

[006-018] alongside different types of men. He is such a hard-shell being that he

[006-019] cannot put out feelers to his kind. He may have plenty of brains, as

[006-020] Stumm had, but he has the poorest notion of psychology of any of God’s

[006-021] creatures. In Germany only the Jew can get outside himself, and that is

[006-022] why, if you look into the matter, you will find that the Jew is at the

[006-023] back of most German enterprises.

[006-024] After midday we stopped at a station for luncheon. We had a very good

[006-025] meal in the restaurant, and when we were finishing two officers

[006-026] entered. Stumm got up and saluted and went aside to talk to them. Then

[006-027] he came back and made me follow him to a waiting-room, where he told me

[006-028] to stay till he fetched me. I noticed that he called a porter and had

[006-029] the door locked when he went out.

[006-030] It was a chilly place with no fire, and I kicked my heels there for

[006-031] twenty minutes. I was living by the hour now, and did not trouble to

[006-032] worry about this strange behaviour. There was a volume of time-tables

[006-033] on a shelf, and I turned the pages idly till I struck a big railway

[006-034] map. Then it occurred to me to find out where we were going. I had

[006-035] heard Stumm take my ticket for a place called [[schwandom|Schwandorf]], and after a

[006-036] lot of searching I found it. It was away south in Bavaria, and so far

[006-037] as I could make out less than fifty miles from the [[danube-river|Danube]]. That cheered

[006-038] me enormously. If Stumm lived there he would most likely start me off

[006-039] on my travels by the railway which I saw running to [[vienna|Vienna]] and then on

[006-040] to [[eastern-front|the East]]. It looked as if I might get to Constantinople after all.

[006-041] But I feared it would be a useless achievement, for what could I do

[006-042] when I got there? I was being hustled out of Germany without picking up

[006-043] the slenderest clue.

[006-044] The door opened and Stumm entered. He seemed to have got bigger in the

[006-045] interval and to carry his head higher. There was a proud light, too, in

[006-046] his eye.

[006-047] “Brandt,” he said, “you are about to receive the greatest privilege

[006-048] that ever fell to one of your race. His Imperial Majesty is passing

[006-049] through here, and has halted for a few minutes. He has done me the

[006-050] [[courage-and-honour|honour]] to receive me, and when he heard my story he expressed a wish to

[006-051] see you. You will follow me to his presence. Do not be afraid. The

[006-052] All-Highest is merciful and gracious. Answer his questions like a man.”

[006-053] I followed him with a quickened pulse. Here was a bit of [[fate-and-providence|luck]] I had

[006-054] never dreamed of. At the far side of the station a train had drawn up,

[006-055] a train consisting of three big coaches, chocolate-coloured and picked

[006-056] out with gold. On the platform beside it stood a small group of

[006-057] officers, tall men in long grey-blue cloaks. They seemed to be mostly

[006-058] elderly, and one or two of the faces I thought I remembered from

[006-059] photographs in the picture papers.

[006-060] As we approached they drew apart, and left us face to face with one

[006-061] man. He was a little below middle height, and all muffled in a thick

[006-062] coat with a fur collar. He wore a silver helmet with an eagle atop of

[006-063] it, and kept his left hand resting on his sword. Below the helmet was a

[006-064] face the colour of grey paper, from which shone curious sombre restless

[006-065] eyes with dark pouches beneath them. There was no fear of my mistaking

[006-066] him. These were the features which, since Napoleon, have been best

[006-067] known to the world.

[006-068] I stood as stiff as a ramrod and saluted. I was perfectly cool and most

[006-069] desperately interested. For such a moment I would have gone through

[006-070] fire and water.

[006-071] “Majesty, this is the Dutchman I spoke of,” I heard Stumm say.

[006-072] “What language does he speak?” the Emperor asked.

[006-073] “Dutch,” was the reply; “but being a South African he also speaks

[006-074] English.”

[006-075] A spasm of pain seemed to flit over the face before me. Then he

[006-076] addressed me in English.

[006-077] “You have come from a land which will yet be our ally to offer your

[006-078] sword to our service? I accept the gift and hail it as a good omen. I

[006-079] would have given your race its freedom, but there were fools and

[006-080] traitors among you who misjudged me. But that freedom I shall yet give

[006-081] you in spite of yourselves. Are there many like you in your country?”

[006-082] “There are thousands, sire,” I said, lying cheerfully. “I am one of

[006-083] many who think that my race’s life lies in your victory. And I think

[006-084] that that victory must be won not in Europe alone. In South Africa for

[006-085] the moment there is no chance, so we look to other parts of the

[006-086] continent. You will win in Europe. You have won in the East, and it now

[006-087] remains to strike the English where they cannot fend the blow. If we

[006-088] take Uganda, Egypt will fall. By your permission I go there to make

[006-089] trouble for your enemies.”

[006-090] A flicker of a smile passed over the worn face. It was the face of one

[006-091] who slept little and whose thoughts rode him like a nightmare. “That is

[006-092] well,” he said. “Some Englishman once said that he would call in the

[006-093] New World to redress the balance of the Old. We Germans will summon the

[006-094] whole earth to suppress the infamies of England. Serve us well, and you

[006-095] will not be forgotten.”

[006-096] Then he suddenly asked: “Did you fight in the last South African War?”

[006-097] “Yes, Sir,” I said. “I was in the commando of that Smuts who has now

[006-098] been bought by England.”

[006-099] “What were your countrymen’s losses?” he asked eagerly.

[006-100] I did not know, but I hazarded a guess. “In the field some twenty

[006-101] thousand. But many more by sickness and in the accursed prison-camps of

[006-102] the English.”

[006-103] Again a spasm of pain crossed his face.

[006-104] “Twenty thousand,” he repeated huskily. “A mere handful. Today we lose

[006-105] as many in a skirmish in the Polish marshes.”

[006-106] Then he broke out fiercely.

[006-107] “I did not seek the war ... It was forced on me ... I laboured for

[006-108] peace ... The blood of millions is on the heads of England and [[russia|Russia]],

[006-109] but England most of all. God will yet avenge it. He that takes the

[006-110] sword will perish by the sword. Mine was forced from the scabbard in

[006-111] self-defence, and I am guiltless. Do they know that among your people?”

[006-112] “All the world knows it, sire,” I said.

[006-113] He gave his hand to Stumm and turned away. The last I saw of him was a

[006-114] figure moving like a sleep-walker, with no spring in his step, amid his

[006-115] tall suite. I felt that I was looking on at a far bigger tragedy than

[006-116] any I had seen in action. Here was one that had loosed Hell, and the

[006-117] furies of Hell had got hold of him. He was no common man, for in his

[006-118] presence I felt an attraction which was not merely the mastery of one

[006-119] used to command. That would not have impressed me, for I had never

[006-120] owned a master. But here was a human being who, unlike Stumm and his

[006-121] kind, had the power of laying himself alongside other men. That was the

[006-122] irony of it. Stumm would not have cared a tinker’s curse for all the

[006-123] massacres in history. But this man, the chief of a nation of Stumms,

[006-124] paid the price in war for the gifts that had made him successful in

[006-125] peace. He had imagination and nerves, and the one was white hot and the

[006-126] others were quivering. I would not have been in his shoes for the

[006-127] throne of the Universe ...

[006-128] All afternoon we sped southward, mostly in a country of hills and

[006-129] wooded valleys. Stumm, for him, was very pleasant. His imperial master

[006-130] must have been gracious to him, and he passed a bit of it on to me. But

[006-131] he was anxious to see that I had got the right impression.

[006-132] “The All-Highest is merciful, as I told you,” he said.

[006-133] I agreed with him.

[006-134] “Mercy is the prerogative of kings,” he said sententiously, “but for us

[006-135] lesser folks it is a trimming we can well do without.”

[006-136] I nodded my approval.

[006-137] “I am not merciful,” he went on, as if I needed telling that. “If any

[006-138] man stands in my way I trample the life out of him. That is the German

[006-139] fashion. That is what has made us great. We do not make war with

[006-140] lavender gloves and fine phrases, but with hard steel and hard brains.

[006-141] We Germans will cure the green-sickness of the world. The nations rise

[006-142] against us. Pouf! They are soft flesh, and flesh cannot resist iron.

[006-143] The shining ploughshare will cut its way through acres of mud.”

[006-144] I hastened to add that these were also my opinions.

[006-145] “What the hell do your opinions matter? You are a thick-headed boor of

[006-146] the veld ... Not but what,” he added, “there is metal in you slow

[006-147] Dutchmen once we Germans have had the forging of it!”

[006-148] The winter evening closed in, and I saw that we had come out of the

[006-149] hills and were in flat country. Sometimes a big sweep of river showed,

[006-150] and, looking out at one station I saw a funny church with a thing like

[006-151] an onion on top of its spire. It might almost have been a mosque,

[006-152] judging from the pictures I remembered of mosques. I wished to heaven I

[006-153] had given geography more attention in my time.

[006-154] Presently we stopped, and Stumm led the way out. The train must have

[006-155] been specially halted for him, for it was a one-horse little place

[006-156] whose name I could not make out. The station-master was waiting, bowing

[006-157] and saluting, and outside was a motor-car with big head-lights. Next

[006-158] minute we were sliding through dark woods where the snow lay far deeper

[006-159] than in the north. There was a mild frost in the air, and the tyres

[006-160] slipped and skidded at the corners.

[006-161] We hadn’t far to go. We climbed a little hill and on the top of it

[006-162] stopped at the door of a big black castle. It looked enormous in the

[006-163] winter night, with not a light showing anywhere on its front. The door

[006-164] was opened by an old fellow who took a long time about it and got well

[006-165] cursed for his slowness. Inside the place was very noble and ancient.

[006-166] Stumm switched on the electric light, and there was a great hall with

[006-167] black tarnished portraits of men and women in old-fashioned clothes,

[006-168] and mighty horns of deer on the walls.
