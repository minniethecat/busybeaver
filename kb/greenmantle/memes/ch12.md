# Ch. 12: Four Missionaries See Light in Their Mission

[012-001] A sudden revulsion leaves a man with a low vitality. I didn’t seem to

[012-002] care greatly. We were done, and there was an end of it. It was [[fate-and-providence|Kismet]],

[012-003] the act of God, and there was nothing for it but to submit. I hadn’t a

[012-004] flicker of a thought of escape or resistance. The game was utterly and

[012-005] absolutely over.

[012-006] A man who seemed to be a sergeant pointed to us and said something to

[012-007] Kuprasso, who nodded. We got heavily to our feet and stumbled towards

[012-008] them. With one on each side of us we crossed the yard, walked through

[012-009] the dark passage and the empty shop, and out into the snowy street.

[012-010] There was a closed carriage waiting which they motioned us to get into.

[012-011] It looked exactly like the Black Maria.

[012-012] Both of us sat still, like truant schoolboys, with our hands on our

[012-013] knees. I didn’t know where I was going and I didn’t care. We seemed to

[012-014] be rumbling up the hill, and then I caught the glare of lighted

[012-015] streets.

[012-016] “This is the end of it, [[peter-pienaar|Peter]],” I said.

[012-017] “_Ja_, Cornelis,” he replied, and that was all our talk.

[012-018] By and by—hours later it seemed—we stopped. Someone opened the door and

[012-019] we got out, to find ourselves in a courtyard with a huge dark building

[012-020] around. The prison, I guessed, and I wondered if they would give us

[012-021] blankets, for it was perishing cold.

[012-022] We entered a door, and found ourselves in a big stone hall. It was

[012-023] quite warm, which made me more hopeful about our cells. A man in some

[012-024] kind of uniform pointed to the staircase, up which we plodded wearily.

[012-025] My mind was too blank to take clear impressions, or in any way to

[012-026] forecast the future. Another warder met us and took us down a passage

[012-027] till we halted at a door. He stood aside and motioned us to enter.

[012-028] I guessed that this was the governor’s room, and we should be put

[012-029] through our first examination. My head was too stupid to think, and I

[012-030] made up my mind to keep perfectly mum. Yes, even if they tried

[012-031] thumbscrews. I had no kind of story, but I resolved not to give

[012-032] anything away. As I turned the handle I wondered idly what kind of

[012-033] sallow Turk or bulging-necked German we should find inside.

[012-034] It was a pleasant room, with a polished wood floor and a big fire

[012-035] burning on the hearth. Beside the fire a man lay on a couch, with a

[012-036] little table drawn up beside him. On that table was a small glass of

[012-037] milk and a number of Patience cards spread in rows.

[012-038] I stared blankly at the spectacle, till I saw a second figure. It was

[012-039] the man in the skin-cap, the leader of the dancing maniacs. Both Peter

[012-040] and I backed sharply at the sight and then stood stock still.

[012-041] For the dancer crossed the room in two strides and gripped both of my

[012-042] hands.

[012-043] “Dick, old man,” he cried, “I’m most awfully glad to see you again!”

[012-044] CHAPTER XII.

[012-045] Four Missionaries See Light in their Mission

[012-046] A spasm of incredulity, a vast relief, and that sharp joy which comes

[012-047] of reaction chased each other across my mind. I had come suddenly out

[012-048] of very black waters into an unbelievable calm. I dropped into the

[012-049] nearest chair and tried to grapple with something far beyond words.

[012-050] “[[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]],” I said, as soon as I got my breath, “you’re an incarnate

[012-051] devil. You’ve given Peter and me the fright of our lives.”

[012-052] “It was the only way, Dick. If I hadn’t come mewing like a tom-cat at

[012-053] your heels yesterday, [[rasta-bey|Rasta]] would have had you long before you got to

[012-054] your hotel. You two have given me a pretty anxious time, and it took

[012-055] some doing to get you safe here. However, that is all over now. Make

[012-056] yourselves at home, my children.”

[012-057] “Over!” I cried incredulously, for my wits were still wool-gathering.

[012-058] “What place is this?”

[012-059] “You may call it my humble home”—it was [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]’s sleek voice that

[012-060] spoke. “We’ve been preparing for you, Major, but it was only yesterday

[012-061] I heard of your friend.”

[012-062] I introduced Peter.

[012-063] “Mr Pienaar,” said Blenkiron, “pleased to meet you. Well, as I was

[012-064] observing, you’re safe enough here, but you’ve cut it mighty fine.

[012-065] Officially, a Dutchman called Brandt was to be arrested this afternoon

[012-066] and handed over to the German authorities. When Germany begins to

[012-067] trouble about that Dutchman she will find difficulty in getting the

[012-068] body; but such are the languid ways of an Oriental despotism. Meantime

[012-069] the Dutchman will be no more. He will have ceased upon the midnight

[012-070] without pain, as your poet sings.”

[012-071] “But I don’t understand,” I stammered. “Who arrested us?”

[012-072] “My men,” said Sandy. “We have a bit of a graft here, and it wasn’t

[012-073] difficult to manage it. Old Moellendorff will be nosing after the

[012-074] business tomorrow, but he will find the mystery too deep for him. That

[012-075] is the advantage of a Government run by a pack of adventurers. But, by

[012-076] Jove, Dick, we hadn’t any time to spare. If Rasta had got you, or the

[012-077] Germans had had the job of lifting you, your goose would have been

[012-078] jolly well cooked. I had some unquiet hours this morning.”

[012-079] The thing was too deep for me. I looked at Blenkiron, shuffling his

[012-080] Patience cards with his old sleepy smile, and Sandy, dressed like some

[012-081] bandit in melodrama, his lean face as brown as a nut, his bare arms all

[012-082] tattooed with crimson rings, and the fox pelt drawn tight over brow and

[012-083] ears. It was still a nightmare world, but the dream was getting

[012-084] pleasanter. Peter said not a word, but I could see his eyes heavy with

[012-085] his own thoughts.

[012-086] Blenkiron hove himself from the sofa and waddled to a cupboard.

[012-087] “You boys must be hungry,” he said. “My duo-denum has been giving me

[012-088] hell as usual, and I don’t eat no more than a squirrel. But I laid in

[012-089] some stores, for I guessed you would want to stoke up some after your

[012-090] travels.”

[012-091] He brought out a couple of Strassburg pies, a cheese, a cold chicken, a

[012-092] loaf, and three bottles of champagne.

[012-093] “Fizz,” said Sandy rapturously. “And a dry Heidsieck too! We’re in

[012-094] luck, Dick, old man.”

[012-095] I never ate a more welcome meal, for we had starved in that dirty

[012-096] hotel. But I had still the old feeling of the hunted, and before I

[012-097] began I asked about the door.

[012-098] “That’s all right,” said Sandy. “My fellows are on the stair and at the

[012-099] gate. If the _Metreb_ are in possession, you may bet that other people

[012-100] will keep off. Your past is blotted out, clean vanished away, and you

[012-101] begin tomorrow morning with a new sheet. Blenkiron’s the man you’ve got

[012-102] to thank for that. He was pretty certain you’d get here, but he was

[012-103] also certain that you’d arrive in a hurry with a good many inquirers

[012-104] behind you. So he arranged that you should leak away and start fresh.”

[012-105] “Your name is Richard Hanau,” Blenkiron said, “born in Cleveland, Ohio,

[012-106] of German parentage on both sides. One of our brightest

[012-107] mining-engineers, and the apple of Guggenheim’s eye. You arrived this

[012-108] afternoon from Constanza, and I met you at the packet. The clothes for

[012-109] the part are in your bedroom next door. But I guess all that can wait,

[012-110] for I’m anxious to get to business. We’re not here on a joy-ride,

[012-111] Major, so I reckon we’ll leave out the dime-novel adventures. I’m just

[012-112] dying to hear them, but they’ll keep. I want to know how our mutual

[012-113] inquiries have prospered.”

[012-114] He gave Peter and me cigars, and we sat ourselves in armchairs in front

[012-115] of the blaze. Sandy squatted cross-legged on the hearthrug and lit a

[012-116] foul old briar pipe, which he extricated from some pouch among his

[012-117] skins. And so began that conversation which had never been out of my

[012-118] thoughts for four hectic weeks.

[012-119] “If I presume to begin,” said Blenkiron, “it’s because I reckon my

[012-120] story is the shortest. I have to confess to you, gentlemen, that I have

[012-121] failed.”

[012-122] He drew down the corners of his mouth till he looked a cross between a

[012-123] music-hall comedian and a sick child.

[012-124] “If you were looking for something in the root of the hedge, you

[012-125] wouldn’t want to scour the road in a high-speed automobile. And still

[012-126] less would you want to get a bird’s-eye view in an aeroplane. That

[012-127] parable about fits my case. I have been in the clouds and I’ve been

[012-128] scorching on the pikes, but what I was wanting was in the ditch all the

[012-129] time, and I naturally missed it ... I had the wrong stunt, Major. I was

[012-130] too high up and refined. I’ve been processing through Europe like

[012-131] Barnum’s Circus, and living with generals and transparencies. Not that

[012-132] I haven’t picked up a lot of noos, and got some very interesting

[012-133] sidelights on high politics. But the thing I was after wasn’t to be

[012-134] found on my beat, for those that knew it weren’t going to tell. In that

[012-135] kind of society they don’t get drunk and blab after their tenth

[012-136] cocktail. So I guess I’ve no contribution to make to quieting Sir

[012-137] Walter [[sir-walter-bullivant|Bullivant]]’s mind, except that he’s dead right. Yes, Sir, he has

[012-138] hit the spot and rung the bell. There is a mighty miracle-working

[012-139] proposition being floated in these parts, but the promoters are keeping

[012-140] it to themselves. They aren’t taking in more than they can help on the

[012-141] ground-floor.”

[012-142] Blenkiron stopped to light a fresh cigar. He was leaner than when he

[012-143] left [[london|London]] and there were pouches below his eyes. I fancy his journey

[012-144] had not been as fur-lined as he made out. “I’ve found out one thing,

[012-145] and that is, that the last dream Germany will part with is the control

[012-146] of the Near East. That is what your statesmen don’t figure enough on.

[012-147] She’ll give up Belgium and Alsace-Lorraine and Poland, but by God!

[012-148] she’ll never give up the road to [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]] till you have her by the

[012-149] throat and make her drop it. Sir Walter is a pretty bright-eyed

[012-150] citizen, and he sees it right enough. If the worst happens, Kaiser will

[012-151] fling overboard a lot of ballast in Europe, and it will look like a big

[012-152] victory for the Allies, but he won’t be beaten if he has the road to

[012-153] [[eastern-front|the East]] safe. Germany’s like a scorpion: her sting’s in her tail, and

[012-154] that tail stretches way down into Asia.

[012-155] “I got that clear, and I also made out that it wasn’t going to be dead

[012-156] easy for her to keep that tail healthy. Turkey’s a bit of an anxiety,

[012-157] as you’ll soon discover. But Germany thinks she can manage it, and I

[012-158] won’t say she can’t. It depends on the hand she holds, and she reckons

[012-159] it a good one. I tried to find out, but they gave me nothing but

[012-160] eyewash. I had to pretend to be satisfied, for the position of John S.

[012-161] wasn’t so strong as to allow him to take liberties. If I asked one of

[012-162] the highbrows he looked wise and spoke of the might of German arms and

[012-163] German organization and German staff-work. I used to nod my head and

[012-164] get enthusiastic about these stunts, but it was all soft soap. She has

[012-165] a trick in hand—that much I know, but I’m darned if I can put a name to

[012-166] it. I pray to God you boys have been cleverer.”

[012-167] His tone was quite melancholy, and I was mean enough to feel rather

[012-168] glad. He had been the professional with the best chance. It would be a

[012-169] good joke if the amateur succeeded where the expert failed.

[012-170] I looked at Sandy. He filled his pipe again, and pushed back his skin

[012-171] cap from his brows. What with his long dishevelled hair, his high-boned

[012-172] face, and stained eyebrows he had the appearance of some mad mullah.
