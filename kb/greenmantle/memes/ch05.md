# Ch. 5: Further Adventures of the Same

[005-001] Stumm had risen with a brow of wrath, and looked as if he were going to

[005-002] pitch the intruder off the train. Then he seemed to halt and collect

[005-003] himself, and the other’s face broke into a friendly grin.

[005-004] “Why, it’s Colonel Stumm,” he cried. (He pronounced it like the first

[005-005] syllable in “stomach”.) “Very pleased to meet you again, Colonel. I had

[005-006] the [[courage-and-honour|honour]] of making your acquaintance at our Embassy. I reckon

[005-007] Ambassador Gerard didn’t cotton to our conversation that night.” And

[005-008] the new-comer plumped himself down in the corner opposite me.

[005-009] I had been pretty certain I would run across [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] somewhere in

[005-010] Germany, but I didn’t think it would be so soon. There he sat staring

[005-011] at me with his full, unseeing eyes, rolling out platitudes to Stumm,

[005-012] who was nearly bursting in his effort to keep civil. I looked moody and

[005-013] suspicious, which I took to be the right line.

[005-014] “Things are getting a bit dead at Salonika,” said Mr Blenkiron, by way

[005-015] of a conversational opening.

[005-016] Stumm pointed to a notice which warned officers to refrain from

[005-017] discussing military operations with mixed company in a railway

[005-018] carriage.

[005-019] “Sorry,” said Blenkiron, “I can’t read that tombstone language of

[005-020] yours. But I reckon that that notice to trespassers, whatever it

[005-021] signifies, don’t apply to you and me. I take it this gentleman is in

[005-022] your party.”

[005-023] I sat and scowled, fixing the American with suspicious eyes.

[005-024] “He is a Dutchman,” said Stumm; “South African Dutch, and he is not

[005-025] happy, for he doesn’t like to hear English spoken.”

[005-026] “We’ll shake on that,” said Blenkiron cordially. “But who said I spoke

[005-027] English? It’s good American. Cheer up, friend, for it isn’t the call

[005-028] that makes the big wapiti, as they say out west in my country. I hate

[005-029] John Bull worse than a poison rattle. The Colonel can tell you that.”

[005-030] I dare say he could, but at that moment, we slowed down at a station

[005-031] and Stumm got up to leave. “Good day to you, Herr Blenkiron,” he cried

[005-032] over his shoulder. “If you consider your comfort, don’t talk English to

[005-033] strange travellers. They don’t distinguish between the different

[005-034] brands.”

[005-035] I followed him in a hurry, but was recalled by Blenkiron’s voice.

[005-036] “Say, friend,” he shouted, “you’ve left your grip,” and he handed me my

[005-037] bag from the luggage rack. But he showed no sign of recognition, and

[005-038] the last I saw of him was sitting sunk in a corner with his head on his

[005-039] chest as if he were going to sleep. He was a man who kept up his parts

[005-040] well.

[005-041] There was a motor-car waiting—one of the grey military kind—and we

[005-042] started at a terrific pace over bad forest roads. Stumm had put away

[005-043] his papers in a portfolio, and flung me a few sentences on the journey.

[005-044] “I haven’t made up my mind about you, Brandt,” he announced. “You may

[005-045] be a fool or a knave or a good man. If you are a knave, we will shoot

[005-046] you.”

[005-047] “And if I am a fool?” I asked.

[005-048] “Send you to the Yser or the Dvina. You will be respectable

[005-049] cannon-fodder.”

[005-050] “You cannot do that unless I consent,” I said.

[005-051] “Can’t we?” he said, smiling wickedly. “Remember you are a citizen of

[005-052] nowhere. Technically, you are a rebel, and the British, if you go to

[005-053] them, will hang you, supposing they have any sense. You are in our

[005-054] power, my friend, to do precisely what we like with you.”

[005-055] He was silent for a second, and then he said, meditatively:

[005-056] “But I don’t think you are a fool. You may be a scoundrel. Some kinds

[005-057] of scoundrel are useful enough. Other kinds are strung up with a rope.

[005-058] Of that we shall know more soon.”

[005-059] “And if I am a good man?”

[005-060] “You will be given a chance to serve Germany, the proudest privilege a

[005-061] mortal man can have.” The strange man said this with a ringing

[005-062] sincerity in his voice that impressed me.

[005-063] The car swung out from the trees into a park lined with saplings, and

[005-064] in the twilight I saw before me a biggish house like an overgrown Swiss

[005-065] chalet. There was a kind of archway, with a sham portcullis, and a

[005-066] terrace with battlements which looked as if they were made of stucco.

[005-067] We drew up at a Gothic front door, where a thin middle-aged man in a

[005-068] shooting-jacket was waiting.

[005-069] As we moved into the lighted hall I got a good look at our host. He was

[005-070] very lean and brown, with the stoop in the shoulder that one gets from

[005-071] being constantly on horseback. He had untidy grizzled hair and a ragged

[005-072] beard, and a pair of pleasant, short-sighted brown eyes.

[005-073] “Welcome, my Colonel,” he said. “Is this the friend you spoke of?”

[005-074] “This is the Dutchman,” said Stumm. “His name is Brandt. Brandt, you

[005-075] see before you [[gaudian|Herr Gaudian]].”

[005-076] I knew the name, of course; there weren’t many in my profession that

[005-077] didn’t. He was one of the biggest railway engineers in the world, the

[005-078] man who had built the [[baghdad|Baghdad]] and Syrian railways, and the new lines in

[005-079] German East. I suppose he was about the greatest living authority on

[005-080] tropical construction. He knew [[eastern-front|the East]] and he knew Africa; clearly I

[005-081] had been brought down for him to put me through my paces.

[005-082] A blonde maidservant took me to my room, which had a bare polished

[005-083] floor, a stove, and windows that, unlike most of the German kind I had

[005-084] sampled, seemed made to open. When I had washed I descended to the

[005-085] hall, which was hung round with trophies of travel, like Dervish

[005-086] jibbahs and Masai shields and one or two good buffalo heads. Presently

[005-087] a bell was rung. Stumm appeared with his host, and we went in to

[005-088] supper.

[005-089] I was jolly hungry and would have made a good meal if I hadn’t

[005-090] constantly had to keep jogging my wits. The other two talked in German,

[005-091] and when a question was put to me Stumm translated. The first thing I

[005-092] had to do was to pretend I didn’t know German and look listlessly round

[005-093] the room while they were talking. The second was to miss not a word,

[005-094] for there lay my chance. The third was to be ready to answer questions

[005-095] at any moment, and to show in the answering that I had not followed the

[005-096] previous conversation. Likewise, I must not prove myself a fool in

[005-097] these answers, for I had to convince them that I was useful. It took

[005-098] some doing, and I felt like a witness in the box under a stiff

[005-099] cross-examination, or a man trying to play three games of chess at

[005-100] once.

[005-101] I heard Stumm telling Gaudian the gist of my plan. The engineer shook

[005-102] his head.

[005-103] “Too late,” he said. “It should have been done at the beginning. We

[005-104] neglected Africa. You know the reason why.”

[005-105] Stumm laughed. “The von Einem! Perhaps, but her charm works well

[005-106] enough.”

[005-107] Gaudian glanced towards me while I was busy with an orange salad. “I

[005-108] have much to tell you of that. But it can wait. Your friend is right in

[005-109] one thing. Uganda is a vital spot for the English, and a blow there

[005-110] will make their whole fabric shiver. But how can we strike? They have

[005-111] still the coast, and our supplies grow daily smaller.”

[005-112] “We can send no reinforcements, but have we used all the local

[005-113] resources? That is what I cannot satisfy myself about. Zimmerman says

[005-114] we have, but Tressler thinks differently, and now we have this fellow

[005-115] coming out of the void with a story which confirms my doubt. He seems

[005-116] to know his job. You try him.”

[005-117] Thereupon Gaudian set about questioning me, and his questions were very

[005-118] thorough. I knew just enough and no more to get through, but I think I

[005-119] came out with credit. You see I have a capacious memory, and in my time

[005-120] I had met scores of hunters and pioneers and listened to their yarns,

[005-121] so I could pretend to knowledge of a place even when I hadn’t been

[005-122] there. Besides, I had once been on the point of undertaking a job up

[005-123] Tanganyika way, and I had got up that country-side pretty accurately.

[005-124] “You say that with our help you can make trouble for the British on the

[005-125] three borders?” Gaudian asked at length.

[005-126] “I can spread the fire if some one else will kindle it,” I said.

[005-127] “But there are thousands of tribes with no affinities.”

[005-128] “They are all African. You can bear me out. All African peoples are

[005-129] alike in one thing—they can go mad, and the madness of one infects the

[005-130] others. The English know this well enough.”

[005-131] “Where would you start the fire?” he asked.

[005-132] “Where the fuel is dryest. Up in the North among the Mussulman peoples.

[005-133] But there you must help me. I know nothing about Islam, and I gather

[005-134] that you do.”

[005-135] “Why?” he asked.

[005-136] “Because of what you have done already,” I answered.

[005-137] Stumm had translated all this time, and had given the sense of my words

[005-138] very fairly. But with my last answer he took liberties. What he gave

[005-139] was: “Because the Dutchman thinks that we have some big card in dealing

[005-140] with the Moslem world.” Then, lowering his voice and raising his

[005-141] eyebrows, he said some word like “Ühnmantl”.

[005-142] The other looked with a quick glance of apprehension at me. “We had

[005-143] better continue our talk in private, Herr Colonel,” he said. “If Herr

[005-144] Brandt will forgive us, we will leave him for a little to entertain

[005-145] himself.” He pushed the cigar-box towards me and the two got up and

[005-146] left the room.

[005-147] I pulled my chair up to the stove, and would have liked to drop off to

[005-148] sleep. The tension of the talk at supper had made me very tired. I was

[005-149] accepted by these men for exactly what I professed to be. Stumm might

[005-150] suspect me of being a rascal, but it was a Dutch rascal. But all the

[005-151] same I was skating on thin ice. I could not sink myself utterly in the

[005-152] part, for if I did I would get no good out of being there. I had to

[005-153] keep my wits going all the time, and join the appearance and manners of

[005-154] a backveld Boer with the mentality of a British intelligence-officer.

[005-155] Any moment the two parts might clash and I would be faced with the most

[005-156] alert and deadly suspicion.

[005-157] There would be no mercy from Stumm. That large man was beginning to

[005-158] fascinate me, even though I hated him. Gaudian was clearly a good

[005-159] fellow, a white man and a gentleman. I could have worked with him for

[005-160] he belonged to my own totem. But the other was an incarnation of all

[005-161] that makes Germany detested, and yet he wasn’t altogether the ordinary

[005-162] German, and I couldn’t help admiring him. I noticed he neither smoked

[005-163] nor drank. His grossness was apparently not in the way of fleshly

[005-164] appetites. Cruelty, from all I had heard of him in German South West,

[005-165] was his hobby; but there were other things in him, some of them good,

[005-166] and he had that kind of crazy patriotism which becomes a religion. I

[005-167] wondered why he had not some high command in the field, for he had had

[005-168] the name of a good soldier. But probably he was a big man in his own

[005-169] line, whatever it was, for the Under-Secretary fellow had talked small

[005-170] in his presence, and so great a man as Gaudian clearly respected him.

[005-171] There must be no lack of brains inside that funny pyramidal head.

[005-172] As I sat beside the stove I was casting back to think if I had got the

[005-173] slightest clue to my real job. There seemed to be nothing so far. Stumm

[005-174] had talked of a von Einem woman who was interested in his department,

[005-175] perhaps the same woman as the [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] he had mentioned the day before to

[005-176] the Under-Secretary. There was not much in that. She was probably some

[005-177] minister’s or ambassador’s wife who had a finger in high politics. If I

[005-178] could have caught the word Stumm had whispered to Gaudian which made

[005-179] him start and look askance at me! But I had only heard a gurgle of

[005-180] something like “Ühnmantl”, which wasn’t any German word that I knew.
