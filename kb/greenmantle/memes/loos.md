## Overview

The Battle of Loos was a major British offensive on the Western Front of the First World War, fought in September--October 1915. In the narrative of *Greenmantle*, Loos serves as the defining military experience of Richard Hannay's wartime service before his intelligence mission to the East. Hannay's wounding at Loos provides the circumstance for his convalescence in Hampshire, where the story opens.

## Hannay's Experience

Hannay describes the battle with characteristic understatement: "Loos was no picnic, and we had had some" [001-018]. He was wounded in the fighting, suffering injuries that included damage to his leg and other effects that lingered throughout his subsequent mission. Hannay later reflects that "part of the damage I got at Loos was a" lasting physical reminder [006-100].

The battle is presented as a formative trial that prepared Hannay for the different kinds of danger he would face in the East. Sir Walter Bullivant tells him that the dangers of the Greenmantle mission will make the "fight at Loos was a Sunday-school picnic" by comparison [001-097].

## As Narrative Reference Point

Throughout the novel, Loos serves as Hannay's touchstone for physical courage and military experience. When facing danger in Constantinople, he reflects that at Loos "the danger at any rate had been clear" -- a known quantity -- whereas the intrigues of the East presented a more insidious threat [011-058].

Blenkiron, the American intelligence operative, recognizes the significance: "You have been fighting, Major? The Battle of Loos? Well, I guess that" establishes Hannay's credentials in his eyes [001-073].

The battle's memory accompanies Hannay through the novel's climax. In the final assault on the Erzerum defenses, he notes "I had never felt it so much as on the last two" days -- despite worse conditions than the Western Front [021-029].

## Dolly Riddell

Dolly Riddell, encountered by Hannay in a German prison camp, had been the brigade machine-gun officer at Loos: "if it wasn't Dolly Riddell, who was our brigade machine-gun officer at Loos. I had heard that the Germans had got him when they blew up a mine at the Quarries" [004-091].

## The Fallen

In the novel's closing reflections, Hannay thinks of his battalion comrades: "many of whom had fallen on the Loos parapets." The battle's cost gives weight to his sense of having been spared for a purpose [022-147].

## Significance

Loos is more than a battle reference -- it is the crucible that forges Hannay's identity as a soldier before he becomes a spy. The physical and psychological scars of Loos are the baseline against which all subsequent dangers are measured, and the shared experience of "the bloody 25th day of September" creates bonds of recognition between British soldiers scattered across the theaters of war.
