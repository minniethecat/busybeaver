# Ch. 9: The Return of the Straggler

[009-001] “Well, Sir?” he said. “I observe your credentials. What can I do for

[009-002] you?”

[009-003] “I take it you are bound for Constantinople?” I asked.

[009-004] “The boats go as far as [[rustchuk|Rustchuk]],” he replied. “There the stuff is

[009-005] transferred to the railway.”

[009-006] “And you reach Rustchuk when?”

[009-007] “In ten days, bar accidents. Let us say twelve to be safe.”

[009-008] “I want to accompany you,” I said. “In my profession, Herr Captain, it

[009-009] is necessary sometimes to make journeys by other than the common route.

[009-010] That is now my desire. I have the right to call upon some other branch

[009-011] of our country’s service to help me. Hence my request.”

[009-012] Very plainly he did not like it.

[009-013] “I must telegraph about it. My instructions are to let no one aboard,

[009-014] not even a man like you. I am sorry, Sir, but I must get authority

[009-015] first before I can fall in with your desire. Besides, my boat is

[009-016] ill-found. You had better wait for the next batch and ask Dreyser to

[009-017] take you. I lost Walter today. He was ill when he came aboard—a disease

[009-018] of the heart—but he would not be persuaded. And last night he died.”

[009-019] “Was that him you have been burying?” I asked.

[009-020] “Even so. He was a good man and my wife’s cousin, and now I have no

[009-021] engineer. Only a fool of a boy from Hamburg. I have just come from

[009-022] wiring to my owners for a fresh man, but even if he comes by the

[009-023] quickest train he will scarcely overtake us before [[vienna|Vienna]] or even

[009-024] Buda.”

[009-025] I saw light at last.

[009-026] “We will go together,” I said, “and cancel that wire. For behold, Herr

[009-027] Captain, I am an engineer, and will gladly keep an eye on your boilers

[009-028] till we get to Rustchuk.”

[009-029] He looked at me doubtfully.

[009-030] “I am speaking truth,” I said. “Before the war I was an engineer in

[009-031] Damaraland. Mining was my branch, but I had a good general training,

[009-032] and I know enough to run a river-boat. Have no fear. I promise you I

[009-033] will earn my passage.”

[009-034] His face cleared, and he looked what he was, an honest, good-humoured

[009-035] North German seaman.

[009-036] “Come then in God’s name,” he cried, “and we will make a bargain. I

[009-037] will let the telegraph sleep. I require authority from the Government

[009-038] to take a passenger, but I need none to engage a new engineer.”

[009-039] He sent one of the hands back to the village to cancel his wire. In ten

[009-040] minutes I found myself on board, and ten minutes later we were out in

[009-041] mid-stream and our tows were lumbering into line. Coffee was being made

[009-042] ready in the cabin, and while I waited for it I picked up the captain’s

[009-043] binoculars and scanned the place I had left.

[009-044] I saw some curious things. On the first road I had struck on leaving

[009-045] the cottage there were men on bicycles moving rapidly. They seemed to

[009-046] wear uniform. On the next parallel road, the one that ran through the

[009-047] village, I could see others. I noticed, too, that several figures

[009-048] appeared to be beating the intervening fields.

[009-049] Stumm’s cordon had got busy at last, and I thanked my stars that not

[009-050] one of the villagers had seen me. I had not got away much too soon, for

[009-051] in another half-hour he would have had me.

[009-052] CHAPTER IX.

[009-053] The Return of the Straggler

[009-054] Before I turned in that evening I had done some good hours’ work in the

[009-055] engine-room. The boat was oil-fired, and in very fair order, so my

[009-056] duties did not look as if they would be heavy. There was nobody who

[009-057] could be properly called an engineer; only, besides the furnace-men, a

[009-058] couple of lads from Hamburg who had been a year ago apprentices in a

[009-059] ship-building yard. They were civil fellows, both of them consumptive,

[009-060] who did what I told them and said little. By bedtime, if you had seen

[009-061] me in my blue jumper, a pair of carpet slippers, and a flat cap—all the

[009-062] property of the deceased Walter—you would have sworn I had been bred to

[009-063] the firing of river-boats, whereas I had acquired most of my knowledge

[009-064] on one run down the Zambesi, when the proper engineer got drunk and

[009-065] fell overboard among the crocodiles.

[009-066] The captain—they called him [[schenk|Schenk]]—was out of his bearings in the job.

[009-067] He was a Frisian and a first-class deep-water seaman, but, since he

[009-068] knew the [[rhine|Rhine]] delta, and because the German mercantile marine was laid

[009-069] on the ice till the end of war, they had turned him on to this show. He

[009-070] was bored by the business, and didn’t understand it very well. The

[009-071] river charts puzzled him, and though it was pretty plain going for

[009-072] hundreds of miles, yet he was in a perpetual fidget about the pilotage.

[009-073] You could see that he would have been far more in his element smelling

[009-074] his way through the shoals of the Ems mouth, or beating against a

[009-075] northeaster in the shallow Baltic. He had six barges in tow, but the

[009-076] heavy flood of the [[danube-river|Danube]] made it an easy job except when it came to

[009-077] going slow. There were two men on each barge, who came aboard every

[009-078] morning to draw rations. That was a funny business, for we never lay to

[009-079] if we could help it. There was a dinghy belonging to each barge, and

[009-080] the men used to row to the next and get a lift in that barge’s dinghy,

[009-081] and so forth. Six men would appear in the dinghy of the barge nearest

[009-082] us and carry off supplies for the rest. The men were mostly Frisians,

[009-083] slow-spoken, sandy-haired lads, very like the breed you strike on the

[009-084] Essex coast.

[009-085] It was the fact that Schenk was really a deep-water sailor, and so a

[009-086] novice to the job, that made me get on with him. He was a good fellow

[009-087] and quite willing to take a hint, so before I had been twenty-four

[009-088] hours on board he was telling me all his difficulties, and I was doing

[009-089] my best to cheer him. And difficulties came thick, because the next

[009-090] night was New Year’s Eve.

[009-091] I knew that that night was a season of gaiety in Scotland, but Scotland

[009-092] wasn’t in it with the Fatherland. Even Schenk, though he was in charge

[009-093] of valuable stores and was voyaging against time, was quite clear that

[009-094] the men must have permission for some kind of beano. Just before

[009-095] darkness we came abreast a fair-sized town, whose name I never

[009-096] discovered, and decided to lie to for the night. The arrangement was

[009-097] that one man should be left on guard in each barge, and the other get

[009-098] four hours’ leave ashore. Then he would return and relieve his friend,

[009-099] who should proceed to do the same thing. I foresaw that there would be

[009-100] some fun when the first batch returned, but I did not dare to protest.

[009-101] I was desperately anxious to get past the Austrian frontier, for I had

[009-102] a half-notion we might be searched there, but Schenk took his

[009-103] _Sylvesterabend_ business so seriously that I would have risked a row

[009-104] if I had tried to argue.

[009-105] The upshot was what I expected. We got the first batch aboard about

[009-106] midnight, blind to the world, and the others straggled in at all hours

[009-107] next morning. I stuck to the boat for obvious reasons, but next day it

[009-108] became too serious, and I had to go ashore with the captain to try and

[009-109] round up the stragglers. We got them all in but two, and I am inclined

[009-110] to think these two had never meant to come back. If I had a soft job

[009-111] like a river-boat I shouldn’t be inclined to run away in the middle of

[009-112] Germany with the certainty that my best [[fate-and-providence|fate]] would be to be scooped up

[009-113] for the trenches, but your Frisian has no more imagination than a

[009-114] haddock. The absentees were both watchmen from the barges, and I fancy

[009-115] the monotony of the life had got on their nerves.

[009-116] The captain was in a raging temper, for he was short-handed to begin

[009-117] with. He would have started a press-gang, but there was no superfluity

[009-118] of men in that township: nothing but boys and grandfathers. As I was

[009-119] helping to run the trip I was pretty annoyed also, and I sluiced down

[009-120] the drunkards with icy Danube water, using all the worst language I

[009-121] knew in Dutch and German. It was a raw morning, and as we raged through

[009-122] the river-side streets I remember I heard the dry crackle of wild geese

[009-123] going overhead, and wished I could get a shot at them. I told one

[009-124] fellow—he was the most troublesome—that he was a disgrace to a great

[009-125] Empire, and was only fit to fight with the filthy English.

[009-126] “God in Heaven!” said the captain, “we can delay no longer. We must

[009-127] make shift the best we can. I can spare one man from the deck hands,

[009-128] and you must give up one from the engine-room.”

[009-129] That was arranged, and we were tearing back rather short in the wind

[009-130] when I espied a figure sitting on a bench beside the booking-office on

[009-131] the pier. It was a slim figure, in an old suit of khaki: some cast-off

[009-132] duds which had long lost the semblance of a uniform. It had a gentle

[009-133] face, and was smoking peacefully, looking out upon the river and the

[009-134] boats and us noisy fellows with meek philosophical eyes. If I had seen

[009-135] General French sitting there and looking like nothing on earth I

[009-136] couldn’t have been more surprised.

[009-137] The man stared at me without recognition. He was waiting for his cue.

[009-138] I spoke rapidly in Sesutu, for I was afraid the captain might know

[009-139] Dutch.

[009-140] “Where have you come from?” I asked.

[009-141] “They shut me up in _tronk_,” said [[peter-pienaar|Peter]], “and I ran away. I am tired,

[009-142] Cornelis, and want to continue the journey by boat.”

[009-143] “Remember you have worked for me in Africa,” I said. “You are just home

[009-144] from Damaraland. You are a German who has lived thirty years away from

[009-145] home. You can tend a furnace and have worked in mines.”

[009-146] Then I spoke to the captain.

[009-147] “Here is a fellow who used to be in my employ, Captain Schenk. It’s

[009-148] almighty luck we’ve struck him. He’s old, and not very strong in the

[009-149] head, but I’ll go bail he’s a good worker. He says he’ll come with us

[009-150] and I can use him in the engine-room.”

[009-151] “Stand up,” said the Captain.

[009-152] Peter stood up, light and slim and wiry as a leopard. A sailor does not

[009-153] judge men by girth and weight.

[009-154] “He’ll do,” said Schenk, and the next minute he was readjusting his

[009-155] crews and giving the strayed revellers the rough side of his tongue. As

[009-156] it chanced, I couldn’t keep Peter with me, but had to send him to one

[009-157] of the barges, and I had time for no more than five words with him,

[009-158] when I told him to hold his tongue and live up to his reputation as a

[009-159] half-wit. That accursed _Sylvesterabend_ had played havoc with the

[009-160] whole outfit, and the captain and I were weary men before we got things

[009-161] straight.

[009-162] In one way it turned out well. That afternoon we passed the frontier

[009-163] and I never knew it till I saw a man in a strange uniform come aboard,

[009-164] who copied some figures on a schedule, and brought us a mail. With my

[009-165] dirty face and general air of absorption in [[courage-and-honour|duty]], I must have been an

[009-166] unsuspicious figure. He took down the names of the men in the barges,

[009-167] and Peter’s name was given as it appeared on the ship’s roll—Anton

[009-168] Blum.

[009-169] “You must feel it strange, [[hermann-brandt|Herr Brandt]],” said the captain, “to be

[009-170] scrutinized by a policeman, you who give orders, I doubt not, to many

[009-171] policemen.”

[009-172] I shrugged my shoulders. “It is my profession. It is my business to go

[009-173] unrecognized often by my own servants.” I could see that I was becoming

[009-174] rather a figure in the captain’s eyes. He liked the way I kept the men

[009-175] up to their work, for I hadn’t been a nigger-driver for nothing.
