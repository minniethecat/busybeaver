# Ch. 1: A Mission is Proposed (cont'd, part 2)

[001-001] “I knew he had knocked about [[eastern-front|the East]], but I didn’t know he was that

[001-002] kind of swell. [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]’s not the chap to buck about himself.”

[001-003] “He wouldn’t,” said [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]]. “He had always a more than Oriental

[001-004] reticence. I’ve got another colleague for you, if you like him.”

[001-005] He looked at his watch. “You can get to the Savoy Grill Room in five

[001-006] minutes in a taxi-cab. Go in from the Strand, turn to your left, and

[001-007] you will see in the alcove on the right-hand side a table with one

[001-008] large American gentleman sitting at it. They know him there, so he will

[001-009] have the table to himself. I want you to go and sit down beside him.

[001-010] Say you come from me. His name is [[john-blenkiron|Mr John Scantlebury Blenkiron]], now a

[001-011] citizen of Boston, Mass., but born and raised in Indiana. Put this

[001-012] envelope in your pocket, but don’t read its contents till you have

[001-013] talked to him. I want you to form your own opinion about Mr Blenkiron.”

[001-014] I went out of the [[british-intelligence|Foreign Office]] in as muddled a frame of mind as any

[001-015] diplomatist who ever left its portals. I was most desperately

[001-016] depressed. To begin with, I was in a complete funk. I had always

[001-017] thought I was about as brave as the average man, but there’s [[courage-and-honour|courage]]

[001-018] and courage, and mine was certainly not the impassive kind. Stick me

[001-019] down in a trench and I could stand being shot at as well as most

[001-020] people, and my blood could get hot if it were given a chance. But I

[001-021] think I had too much imagination. I couldn’t shake off the beastly

[001-022] forecasts that kept crowding my mind.

[001-023] In about a fortnight, I calculated, I would be dead. Shot as a spy—a

[001-024] rotten sort of ending! At the moment I was quite safe, looking for a

[001-025] taxi in the middle of [[foreign-office|Whitehall]], but the sweat broke on my forehead. I

[001-026] felt as I had felt in my adventure before the war. But this was far

[001-027] worse, for it was more cold-blooded and premeditated, and I didn’t seem

[001-028] to have even a sporting chance. I watched the figures in khaki passing

[001-029] on the pavement, and thought what a nice safe prospect they had

[001-030] compared to mine. Yes, even if next week they were in the Hohenzollern,

[001-031] or the Hairpin trench at the Quarries, or that ugly angle at Hooge. I

[001-032] wondered why I had not been happier that morning before I got that

[001-033] infernal wire. Suddenly all the trivialities of English life seemed to

[001-034] me inexpressibly dear and terribly far away. I was very angry with

[001-035] Bullivant, till I remembered how fair he had been. My [[fate-and-providence|fate]] was my own

[001-036] choosing.

[001-037] When I was hunting the Black Stone the interest of the problem had

[001-038] helped to keep me going. But now I could see no problem. My mind had

[001-039] nothing to work on but three words of gibberish on a sheet of paper and

[001-040] a mystery of which Sir Walter had been convinced, but to which he

[001-041] couldn’t give a name. It was like the story I had read of Saint Teresa

[001-042] setting off at the age of ten with her small brother to convert the

[001-043] Moors. I sat huddled in the taxi with my chin on my breast, wishing

[001-044] that I had lost a leg at [[loos|Loos]] and been comfortably tucked away for the

[001-045] rest of the war.

[001-046] Sure enough I found my man in the Grill Room. There he was, feeding

[001-047] solemnly, with a napkin tucked under his chin. He was a big fellow with

[001-048] a fat, sallow, clean-shaven face. I disregarded the hovering waiter and

[001-049] pulled up a chair beside the American at the little table. He turned on

[001-050] me a pair of full sleepy eyes, like a ruminating ox.

[001-051] “Mr Blenkiron?” I asked.

[001-052] “You have my name, Sir,” he said. “Mr John Scantlebury Blenkiron. I

[001-053] would wish you good morning if I saw anything good in this darned

[001-054] British weather.”

[001-055] “I come from Sir Walter Bullivant,” I said, speaking low.

[001-056] “So?” said he. “Sir Walter is a very good friend of mine. Pleased to

[001-057] meet you, Mr—or I guess it’s Colonel—”

[001-058] “Hannay,” I said; “[[richard-hannay|Major Hannay]].” I was wondering what this sleepy

[001-059] Yankee could do to help me.

[001-060] “Allow me to offer you luncheon, Major. Here, waiter, bring the carte.

[001-061] I regret that I cannot join you in sampling the efforts of the

[001-062] management of this hotel. I suffer, Sir, from dyspepsia—duodenal

[001-063] dyspepsia. It gets me two hours after a meal and gives me hell just

[001-064] below the breast-bone. So I am obliged to adopt a diet. My nourishment

[001-065] is fish, Sir, and boiled milk and a little dry toast. It’s a melancholy

[001-066] descent from the days when I could do justice to a lunch at Sherry’s

[001-067] and sup off oyster-crabs and devilled bones.” He sighed from the depths

[001-068] of his capacious frame.

[001-069] I ordered an omelette and a chop, and took another look at him. The

[001-070] large eyes seemed to be gazing steadily at me without seeing me. They

[001-071] were as vacant as an abstracted child’s; but I had an uncomfortable

[001-072] feeling that they saw more than mine.

[001-073] “You have been fighting, Major? The Battle of Loos? Well, I guess that

[001-074] must have been some battle. We in America respect the fighting of the

[001-075] British soldier, but we don’t quite catch on to the de-vices of the

[001-076] British Generals. We opine that there is more bellicosity than science

[001-077] among your highbrows. That is so? My father fought at Chattanooga, but

[001-078] these eyes have seen nothing gorier than a Presidential election. Say,

[001-079] is there any way I could be let into a scene of real bloodshed?”

[001-080] His serious tone made me laugh. “There are plenty of your countrymen in

[001-081] the present show,” I said. “The French Foreign Legion is full of young

[001-082] Americans, and so is our Army Service Corps. Half the chauffeurs you

[001-083] strike in France seem to come from the States.”

[001-084] He sighed. “I did think of some belligerent stunt a year back. But I

[001-085] reflected that the good God had not given John S. Blenkiron the kind of

[001-086] martial figure that would do credit to the tented field. Also I

[001-087] recollected that we Americans were nootrals—benevolent nootrals—and

[001-088] that it did not become me to be butting into the struggles of the

[001-089] effete monarchies of Europe. So I stopped at home. It was a big

[001-090] renunciation, Major, for I was lying sick during the Philippines

[001-091] business, and I have never seen the lawless passions of men let loose

[001-092] on a battlefield. And, as a stoodent of humanity, I hankered for the

[001-093] experience.”

[001-094] “What have you been doing?” I asked. The calm gentleman had begun to

[001-095] interest me.

[001-096] “Waal,” he said, “I just waited. The Lord has blessed me with money to

[001-097] burn, so I didn’t need to go scrambling like a wild cat for war

[001-098] contracts. But I reckoned I would get let into the game somehow, and I

[001-099] was. Being a nootral, I was in an advantageous position to take a hand.

[001-100] I had a pretty hectic time for a while, and then I reckoned I would

[001-101] leave God’s country and see what was doing in Europe. I have counted

[001-102] myself out of the bloodshed business, but, as your poet sings, peace

[001-103] has its victories not less renowned than war, and I reckon that means

[001-104] that a nootral can have a share in a scrap as well as a belligerent.”

[001-105] “That’s the best kind of neutrality I’ve ever heard of,” I said.

[001-106] “It’s the right kind,” he replied solemnly. “Say, Major, what are your

[001-107] lot fighting for? For your own skins and your Empire and the peace of

[001-108] Europe. Waal, those ideals don’t concern us one cent. We’re not

[001-109] Europeans, and there aren’t any German trenches on Long Island yet.

[001-110] You’ve made the ring in Europe, and if we came butting in it wouldn’t

[001-111] be the rules of the game. You wouldn’t welcome us, and I guess you’d be

[001-112] right. We’re that delicate-minded we can’t interfere and that was what

[001-113] my friend, President Wilson, meant when he opined that America was too

[001-114] proud to fight. So we’re nootrals. But likewise we’re benevolent

[001-115] nootrals. As I follow events, there’s a skunk been let loose in the

[001-116] world, and the odour of it is going to make life none too sweet till it

[001-117] is cleared away. It wasn’t us that stirred up that skunk, but we’ve got

[001-118] to take a hand in disinfecting the planet. See? We can’t fight, but, by

[001-119] God! some of us are going to sweat blood to sweep the mess up.

[001-120] Officially we do nothing except give off Notes like a leaky boiler

[001-121] gives off steam. But as individooal citizens we’re in it up to the

[001-122] neck. So, in the spirit of Jefferson Davis and Woodrow Wilson, I’m

[001-123] going to be the nootralist kind of nootral till Kaiser will be sorry he

[001-124] didn’t declare war on America at the beginning.”

[001-125] I was completely recovering my temper. This fellow was a perfect jewel,

[001-126] and his spirit put purpose into me.

[001-127] “I guess you British were the same kind of nootral when your Admiral

[001-128] warned off the German fleet from interfering with Dewey in Manila Bay

[001-129] in ’98.” Mr Blenkiron drank up the last drop of his boiled milk and lit

[001-130] a thin black cigar.

[001-131] I leaned forward. “Have you talked to Sir Walter?” I asked.

[001-132] “I have talked to him, and he has given me to understand that there’s a

[001-133] deal ahead which you’re going to boss. There are no flies on that big

[001-134] man, and if he says it’s good business then you can count me in.”

[001-135] “You know that it’s uncommonly dangerous?”

[001-136] “I judged so. But it don’t do to begin counting risks. I believe in an

[001-137] all-wise and beneficent Providence, but you have got to trust Him and

[001-138] give Him a chance. What’s life anyhow? For me, it’s living on a strict

[001-139] diet and having frequent pains in my stomach. It isn’t such an almighty

[001-140] lot to give up, provided you get a good price in the deal. Besides, how

[001-141] big is the risk? [[About]] one o’clock in the morning, when you can’t

[001-142] sleep, it will be the size of Mount Everest, but if you run out to meet

[001-143] it, it will be a hillock you can jump over. The grizzly looks very

[001-144] fierce when you’re taking your ticket for the Rockies and wondering if

[001-145] you’ll come back, but he’s just an ordinary bear when you’ve got the

[001-146] sight of your rifle on him. I won’t think about risks till I’m up to my

[001-147] neck in them and don’t see the road out.”

[001-148] I scribbled my address on a piece of paper and handed it to the stout

[001-149] philosopher. “Come to dinner tonight at eight,” I said.

[001-150] “I thank you, Major. A little fish, please, plain-boiled, and some hot

[001-151] milk. You will forgive me if I borrow your couch after the meal and

[001-152] spend the evening on my back. That is the advice of my noo doctor.”

[001-153] I got a taxi and drove to my club. On the way I opened the envelope Sir

[001-154] Walter had given me. It contained a number of jottings, the dossier of

[001-155] Mr Blenkiron. He had done wonders for the Allies in the States. He had

[001-156] nosed out the Dumba plot, and had been instrumental in getting the

[001-157] portfolio of Dr Albert. Von Papen’s spies had tried to murder him,

[001-158] after he had defeated an attempt to blow up one of the big gun

[001-159] factories. Sir Walter had written at the end: “The best man we ever

[001-160] had. Better than Scudder. He would go through hell with a box of

[001-161] bismuth tablets and a pack of Patience cards.”

[001-162] I went into the little back smoking-room, borrowed an atlas from the

[001-163] library, poked up the fire, and sat down to think. Mr Blenkiron had

[001-164] given me the fillip I needed. My mind was beginning to work now, and

[001-165] was running wide over the whole business. Not that I hoped to find

[001-166] anything by my cogitations. It wasn’t thinking in an arm-chair that

[001-167] would solve the mystery. But I was getting a sort of grip on a plan of

[001-168] operations. And to my relief I had stopped thinking about the risks.

[001-169] Blenkiron had shamed me out of that. If a sedentary dyspeptic could

[001-170] show that kind of nerve, I wasn’t going to be behind him.
