# Ch. 2: The Gathering of the Missionaries (cont'd, part 2)

[002-001] There were just two other passengers on that beastly boat, and they

[002-002] never appeared till we were out of the Bay. I was pretty bad myself,

[002-003] but managed to move about all the time, for the frowst in my cabin

[002-004] would have sickened a hippo. The old tub took two days and a night to

[002-005] waddle from Ushant to Finisterre. Then the weather changed and we came

[002-006] out of snow-squalls into something very like summer. The hills of

[002-007] Portugal were all blue and yellow like the Kalahari, and before we made

[002-008] the Tagus I was beginning to forget I had ever left Rhodesia. There was

[002-009] a Dutchman among the sailors with whom I used to patter the taal, and

[002-010] but for “Good morning” and “Good evening” in broken English to the

[002-011] captain, that was about all the talking I did on the cruise.

[002-012] We dropped anchor off the quays of [[lisbon|Lisbon]] on a shiny blue morning,

[002-013] pretty near warm enough to wear flannels. I had now got to be very

[002-014] wary. I did not leave the ship with the shore-going boat, but made a

[002-015] leisurely breakfast. Then I strolled on deck, and there, just casting

[002-016] anchor in the middle of the stream, was another ship with a blue and

[002-017] white funnel I knew so well. I calculated that a month before she had

[002-018] been smelling the mangrove swamps of Angola. Nothing could better

[002-019] answer my purpose. I proposed to board her, pretending I was looking

[002-020] for a friend, and come on shore from her, so that anyone in Lisbon who

[002-021] chose to be curious would think I had landed straight from Portuguese

[002-022] Africa.

[002-023] I hailed one of the adjacent ruffians, and got into his rowboat, with

[002-024] my kit. We reached the vessel—they called her the _Henry the

[002-025] Navigator_—just as the first shore-boat was leaving. The crowd in it

[002-026] were all Portuguese, which suited my book.

[002-027] But when I went up the ladder the first man I met was old [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]

[002-028] Pienaar.

[002-029] Here was a piece of sheer monumental [[fate-and-providence|luck]]. Peter had opened his eyes

[002-030] and his mouth, and had got as far as “Allemachtig”, when I shut him up.

[002-031] “Brandt,” I said, “Cornelis Brandt. That’s my name now, and don’t you

[002-032] forget it. Who is the captain here? Is it still old Sloggett?”

[002-033] “_Ja,_” said Peter, pulling himself together. “He was speaking about

[002-034] you yesterday.”

[002-035] This was better and better. I sent Peter below to get hold of Sloggett,

[002-036] and presently I had a few words with that gentleman in his cabin with

[002-037] the door shut.

[002-038] “You’ve got to enter my name in the ship’s books. I came aboard at

[002-039] Mossamedes. And my name’s Cornelis Brandt.”

[002-040] At first Sloggett was for objecting. He said it was a felony. I told

[002-041] him that I dared say it was, but he had got to do it, for reasons which

[002-042] I couldn’t give, but which were highly creditable to all parties. In

[002-043] the end he agreed, and I saw it done. I had a pull on old Sloggett, for

[002-044] I had known him ever since he owned a dissolute tug-boat at Delagoa

[002-045] Bay.

[002-046] Then Peter and I went ashore and swaggered into Lisbon as if we owned

[002-047] De Beers. We put up at the big hotel opposite the railway station, and

[002-048] looked and behaved like a pair of lowbred South Africans home for a

[002-049] spree. It was a fine bright day, so I hired a motor-car and said I

[002-050] would drive it myself. We asked the name of some beauty-spot to visit,

[002-051] and were told Cintra and shown the road to it. I wanted a quiet place

[002-052] to talk, for I had a good deal to say to Peter Pienaar.

[002-053] I christened that car the Lusitanian Terror, and it was a marvel that

[002-054] we did not smash ourselves up. There was something immortally wrong

[002-055] with its steering gear. Half a dozen times we slewed across the road,

[002-056] inviting destruction. But we got there in the end, and had luncheon in

[002-057] an hotel opposite the Moorish palace. There we left the car and

[002-058] wandered up the slopes of a hill, where, sitting among scrub very like

[002-059] the veld, I told Peter the situation of affairs.

[002-060] But first a word must be said about Peter. He was the man that taught

[002-061] me all I ever knew of veld-craft, and a good deal about human nature

[002-062] besides. He was out of the Old Colony—Burgersdorp, I think—but he had

[002-063] come to the Transvaal when the Lydenburg goldfields started. He was

[002-064] prospector, transport-rider, and hunter in turns, but principally

[002-065] hunter. In those early days he was none too good a citizen. He was in

[002-066] Swaziland with Bob Macnab, and you know what that means. Then he took

[002-067] to working off bogus gold propositions on Kimberley and Johannesburg

[002-068] magnates, and what he didn’t know about salting a mine wasn’t

[002-069] knowledge. After that he was in the Kalahari, where he and Scotty Smith

[002-070] were familiar names. An era of comparative respectability dawned for

[002-071] him with the Matabele War, when he did uncommon good scouting and

[002-072] transport work. Cecil Rhodes wanted to establish him on a stock farm

[002-073] down Salisbury way, but Peter was an independent devil and would call

[002-074] no man master. He took to big-game hunting, which was what God intended

[002-075] him for, for he could track a tsessebe in thick bush, and was far the

[002-076] finest shot I have seen in my life. He took parties to the Pungwe

[002-077] flats, and Barotseland, and up to Tanganyika. Then he made a speciality

[002-078] of the Ngami region, where I once hunted with him, and he was with me

[002-079] when I went prospecting in Damaraland.

[002-080] When the Boer War started, Peter, like many of the very great hunters,

[002-081] took the British side and did most of our [[espionage|intelligence work]] in the

[002-082] North Transvaal. Beyers would have hanged him if he could have caught

[002-083] him, and there was no love lost between Peter and his own people for

[002-084] many a day. When it was all over and things had calmed down a bit, he

[002-085] settled in Bulawayo and used to go with me when I went on trek. At the

[002-086] time when I left Africa two years before, I had lost sight of him for

[002-087] months, and heard that he was somewhere on the Congo poaching

[002-088] elephants. He had always a great idea of making things hum so loud in

[002-089] Angola that the Union Government would have to step in and annex it.

[002-090] After Rhodes Peter had the biggest notions south of the Line.

[002-091] He was a man of about five foot ten, very thin and active, and as

[002-092] strong as a buffalo. He had pale blue eyes, a face as gentle as a

[002-093] girl’s, and a soft sleepy voice. From his present appearance it looked

[002-094] as if he had been living hard lately. His clothes were of the cut you

[002-095] might expect to get at Lobito Bay, he was as lean as a rake, deeply

[002-096] browned with the sun, and there was a lot of grey in his beard. He was

[002-097] fifty-six years old, and used to be taken for forty. Now he looked

[002-098] about his age.

[002-099] I first asked him what he had been up to since the war began. He spat,

[002-100] in the Kaffir way he had, and said he had been having hell’s time.

[002-101] “I got hung up on the Kafue,” he said. “When I heard from old Letsitela

[002-102] that the white men were fighting I had a bright idea that I might get

[002-103] into German South West from the north. You see I knew that [[louis-botha|Botha]]

[002-104] couldn’t long keep out of the war. Well, I got into German territory

[002-105] all right, and then a _skellum_ of an officer came along, and

[002-106] commandeered all my mules, and wanted to commandeer me with them for

[002-107] his fool army. He was a very ugly man with a yellow face.” Peter filled

[002-108] a deep pipe from a kudu-skin pouch.

[002-109] “Were you commandeered?” I asked.

[002-110] “No. I shot him—not so as to kill, but to wound badly. It was all

[002-111] right, for he fired first on me. Got me too in the left shoulder. But

[002-112] that was the beginning of bad trouble. I trekked east pretty fast, and

[002-113] got over the border among the Ovamba. I have made many journeys, but

[002-114] that was the worst. Four days I went without water, and six without

[002-115] food. Then by bad luck I fell in with “Nkitla—you remember, the

[002-116] half-caste chief. He said I owed him money for cattle which I bought

[002-117] when I came there with Carowab. It was a lie, but he held to it, and

[002-118] would give me no transport. So I crossed the Kalahari on my feet. Ugh,

[002-119] it was as slow as a vrouw coming from _nachtmaal_. It took weeks and

[002-120] weeks, and when I came to Lechwe’s kraal, I heard that the fighting was

[002-121] over and that Botha had conquered the Germans. That, too, was a lie,

[002-122] but it deceived me, and I went north into Rhodesia, where I learned the

[002-123] truth. But by then I judged the war had gone too far for me to make any

[002-124] profit out of it, so I went into Angola to look for German refugees. By

[002-125] that time I was hating Germans worse than hell.”

[002-126] “But what did you propose to do with them?” I asked.

[002-127] “I had a notion they would make trouble with the Government in those

[002-128] parts. I don’t specially love the Portugoose, but I’m for him against

[002-129] the Germans every day. Well, there was trouble, and I had a merry time

[002-130] for a month or two. But by and by it petered out, and I thought I had

[002-131] better clear for Europe, for South Africa was settling down just as the

[002-132] big show was getting really interesting. So here I am, Cornelis, my old

[002-133] friend. If I shave my beard will they let me join the Flying Corps?”

[002-134] I looked at Peter sitting there smoking, as imperturbable as if he had

[002-135] been growing mealies in Natal all his life and had run home for a

[002-136] month’s holiday with his people in Peckham.

[002-137] “You’re coming with me, my lad,” I said. “We’re going into Germany.”

[002-138] Peter showed no surprise. “Keep in mind that I don’t like the Germans,”

[002-139] was all he said. “I’m a quiet Christian man, but I’ve the devil of a

[002-140] temper.”

[002-141] Then I told him the story of our mission. “You and I have got to be

[002-142] [[manie-maritz|Maritz]]’s men. We went into Angola, and now we’re trekking for the

[002-143] Fatherland to get a bit of our own back from the infernal English.

[002-144] Neither of us knows any German—publicly. We’d better plan out the

[002-145] fighting we were in—Kakamas will do for one, and Schuit Drift. You were

[002-146] a Ngamiland hunter before the war. They won’t have your _dossier_, so

[002-147] you can tell any lie you like. I’d better be an educated Afrikander,

[002-148] one of Beyers’s bright lads, and a pal of old Hertzog. We can let our

[002-149] imagination loose about that part, but we must stick to the same yarn

[002-150] about the fighting.”

[002-151] “_Ja_, Cornelis,” said Peter. (He had called me Cornelis ever since I

[002-152] had told him my new name. He was a wonderful chap for catching on to

[002-153] any game.) “But after we get into Germany, what then? There can’t be

[002-154] much difficulty about the beginning. But once we’re among the

[002-155] beer-swillers I don’t quite see our line. We’re to find out about

[002-156] something that’s going on in Turkey? When I was a boy the predikant

[002-157] used to preach about Turkey. I wish I was better educated and

[002-158] remembered whereabouts in the map it was.”

[002-159] “You leave that to me,” I said; “I’ll explain it all to you before we

[002-160] get there. We haven’t got much of a spoor, but we’ll cast about, and

[002-161] with luck will pick it up. I’ve seen you do it often enough when we

[002-162] hunted kudu on the Kafue.”

[002-163] Peter nodded. “Do we sit still in a German town?” he asked anxiously.

[002-164] “I shouldn’t like that, Cornelis.”
