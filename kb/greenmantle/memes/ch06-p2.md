# Ch. 6: The Indiscretions of the Same (cont'd, part 2)

[006-001] There seemed to be no superfluity of servants. The old fellow said that

[006-002] food was ready, and without more ado we went into the

[006-003] dining-room—another vast chamber with rough stone walls above the

[006-004] panelling—and found some cold meats on the table beside a big fire. The

[006-005] servant presently brought in a ham omelette, and on that and the cold

[006-006] stuff we dined. I remember there was nothing to drink but water. It

[006-007] puzzled me how Stumm kept his great body going on the very moderate

[006-008] amount of food he ate. He was the type you expect to swill beer by the

[006-009] bucket and put away a pie in a sitting.

[006-010] When we had finished, he rang for the old man and told him that we

[006-011] should be in the study for the rest of the evening. “You can lock up

[006-012] and go to bed when you like,” he said, “but see you have coffee ready

[006-013] at seven sharp in the morning.”

[006-014] Ever since I entered that house I had the uncomfortable feeling of

[006-015] being in a prison. Here was I alone in this great place with a fellow

[006-016] who could, and would, wring my neck if he wanted. [[berlin|Berlin]] and all the

[006-017] rest of it had seemed comparatively open country; I had felt that I

[006-018] could move freely and at the worst make a bolt for it. But here I was

[006-019] trapped, and I had to tell myself every minute that I was there as a

[006-020] friend and colleague. The fact is, I was afraid of Stumm, and I don’t

[006-021] mind admitting it. He was a new thing in my experience and I didn’t

[006-022] like it. If only he had drunk and guzzled a bit I should have been

[006-023] happier.

[006-024] We went up a staircase to a room at the end of a long corridor. Stumm

[006-025] locked the door behind him and laid the key on the table. That room

[006-026] took my breath away, it was so unexpected. In place of the grim

[006-027] bareness of downstairs here was a place all luxury and colour and

[006-028] light. It was very large, but low in the ceiling, and the walls were

[006-029] full of little recesses with statues in them. A thick grey carpet of

[006-030] velvet pile covered the floor, and the chairs were low and soft and

[006-031] upholstered like a lady’s boudoir. A pleasant fire burned on the hearth

[006-032] and there was a flavour of scent in the air, something like incense or

[006-033] burnt sandalwood. A French clock on the mantelpiece told me that it was

[006-034] ten minutes past eight. Everywhere on little tables and in cabinets was

[006-035] a profusion of knickknacks, and there was some beautiful embroidery

[006-036] framed on screens. At first sight you would have said it was a woman’s

[006-037] drawing-room.

[006-038] But it wasn’t. I soon saw the difference. There had never been a

[006-039] woman’s hand in that place. It was the room of a man who had a passion

[006-040] for frippery, who had a perverted taste for soft delicate things. It

[006-041] was the complement to his bluff brutality. I began to see the queer

[006-042] other side to my host, that evil side which gossip had spoken of as not

[006-043] unknown in the German army. The room seemed a horribly unwholesome

[006-044] place, and I was more than ever afraid of Stumm.

[006-045] The hearthrug was a wonderful old Persian thing, all faint greens and

[006-046] pinks. As he stood on it he looked uncommonly like a bull in a

[006-047] china-shop. He seemed to bask in the comfort of it, and sniffed like a

[006-048] satisfied animal. Then he sat down at an escritoire, unlocked a drawer

[006-049] and took out some papers.

[006-050] “We will now settle your business, friend Brandt,” he said. “You will

[006-051] go to Egypt and there take your orders from one whose name and address

[006-052] are in this envelope. This card,” and he lifted a square piece of grey

[006-053] pasteboard with a big stamp at the corner and some code words

[006-054] stencilled on it, “will be your passport. You will show it to the man

[006-055] you seek. Keep it jealously, and never use it save under orders or in

[006-056] the last necessity. It is your badge as an accredited agent of the

[006-057] German Crown.”

[006-058] I took the card and the envelope and put them in my pocket-book.

[006-059] “Where do I go after Egypt?” I asked.

[006-060] “That remains to be seen. Probably you will go up the Blue Nile. Riza,

[006-061] the man you will meet, will direct you. Egypt is a nest of our agents

[006-062] who work peacefully under the nose of the English [[espionage|Secret Service]].”

[006-063] “I am willing,” I said. “But how do I reach Egypt?”

[006-064] “You will travel by Holland and [[london|London]]. Here is your route,” and he

[006-065] took a paper from his pocket. “Your passports are ready and will be

[006-066] given you at the frontier.”

[006-067] This was a pretty kettle of fish. I was to be packed off to Cairo by

[006-068] sea, which would take weeks, and God knows how I would get from Egypt

[006-069] to Constantinople. I saw all my plans falling to pieces about my ears,

[006-070] and just when I thought they were shaping nicely.

[006-071] Stumm must have interpreted the look on my face as fear.

[006-072] “You have no cause to be afraid,” he said. “We have passed the word to

[006-073] the English police to look out for a suspicious South African named

[006-074] Brandt, one of [[manie-maritz|Maritz]]’s rebels. It is not difficult to have that kind

[006-075] of a hint conveyed to the proper quarter. But the description will not

[006-076] be yours. Your name will be Van der Linden, a respectable Java merchant

[006-077] going home to his plantations after a visit to his native shores. You

[006-078] had better get your _dossier_ by heart, but I guarantee you will be

[006-079] asked no questions. We manage these things well in Germany.”

[006-080] I kept my eyes on the fire, while I did some savage thinking. I knew

[006-081] they would not let me out of their sight till they saw me in Holland,

[006-082] and, once there, there would be no possibility of getting back. When I

[006-083] left this house I would have no chance of giving them the slip. And yet

[006-084] I was well on my way to [[eastern-front|the East]], the [[danube-river|Danube]] could not be fifty miles

[006-085] off, and that way ran the road to Constantinople. It was a fairly

[006-086] desperate position. If I tried to get away Stumm would prevent me, and

[006-087] the odds were that I would go to join [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] in some infernal

[006-088] prison-camp.

[006-089] Those moments were some of the worst I ever spent. I was absolutely and

[006-090] utterly baffled, like a rat in a trap. There seemed nothing for it but

[006-091] to go back to London and tell [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]] the game was up. And that was

[006-092] about as bitter as death.

[006-093] He saw my face and laughed. “Does your heart fail you, my little

[006-094] Dutchman? You funk the English? I will tell you one thing for your

[006-095] comfort. There is nothing in the world to be feared except me. Fail,

[006-096] and you have cause to shiver. Play me false and you had far better

[006-097] never have been born.”

[006-098] His ugly sneering face was close above mine. Then he put out his hands

[006-099] and gripped my shoulders as he had done the first afternoon.

[006-100] I forget if I mentioned that part of the damage I got at [[loos|Loos]] was a

[006-101] shrapnel bullet low down at the back of my neck. The wound had healed

[006-102] well enough, but I had pains there on a cold day. His fingers found the

[006-103] place and it hurt like hell.

[006-104] There is a very narrow line between despair and black rage. I had about

[006-105] given up the game, but the sudden ache of my shoulders gave me purpose

[006-106] again. He must have seen the rage in my eyes, for his own became cruel.

[006-107] “The weasel would like to bite,” he cried. “But the poor weasel has

[006-108] found its master. Stand still, vermin. Smile, look pleasant, or I will

[006-109] make pulp of you. Do you dare to frown at me?”

[006-110] I shut my teeth and said never a word. I was choking in my throat and

[006-111] could not have uttered a syllable if I had tried.

[006-112] Then he let me go, grinning like an ape.

[006-113] I stepped back a pace and gave him my left between the eyes.

[006-114] For a second he did not realize what had happened, for I don’t suppose

[006-115] anyone had dared to lift a hand to him since he was a child. He blinked

[006-116] at me mildly. Then his face grew as red as fire.

[006-117] “God in heaven,” he said quietly. “I am going to kill you,” and he

[006-118] flung himself on me like a mountain.

[006-119] I was expecting him and dodged the attack. I was quite calm now, but

[006-120] pretty helpless. The man had a gorilla’s reach and could give me at

[006-121] least a couple of stone. He wasn’t soft either, but looked as hard as

[006-122] granite. I was only just from hospital and absurdly out of training. He

[006-123] would certainly kill me if he could, and I saw nothing to prevent him.

[006-124] My only chance was to keep him from getting to grips, for he could have

[006-125] squeezed in my ribs in two seconds. I fancied I was lighter on my legs

[006-126] than him, and I had a good eye. Black Monty at Kimberley had taught me

[006-127] to fight a bit, but there is no art on earth which can prevent a big

[006-128] man in a narrow space from sooner or later cornering a lesser one. That

[006-129] was the danger.

[006-130] Backwards and forwards we padded on the soft carpet. He had no notion

[006-131] of guarding himself, and I got in a good few blows.

[006-132] Then I saw a queer thing. Every time I hit him he blinked and seemed to

[006-133] pause. I guessed the reason for that. He had gone through life keeping

[006-134] the crown of the causeway, and nobody had ever stood up to him. He

[006-135] wasn’t a coward by a long chalk, but he was a bully, and had never been

[006-136] struck in his life. He was getting struck now in real earnest, and he

[006-137] didn’t like it. He had lost his bearings and was growing as mad as a

[006-138] hatter.

[006-139] I kept half an eye on the clock. I was hopeful now, and was looking for

[006-140] the right kind of chance. The risk was that I might tire sooner than

[006-141] him and be at his mercy.

[006-142] Then I learned a truth I have never forgotten. If you are fighting a

[006-143] man who means to kill you, he will be apt to down you unless you mean

[006-144] to kill him too. Stumm did not know any rules to this game, and I

[006-145] forgot to allow for that. Suddenly, when I was watching his eyes, he

[006-146] launched a mighty kick at my stomach. If he had got me, this yarn would

[006-147] have had an abrupt ending. But by the mercy of God I was moving

[006-148] sideways when he let out, and his heavy boot just grazed my left thigh.

[006-149] It was the place where most of the shrapnel had lodged, and for a

[006-150] second I was sick with pain and stumbled. Then I was on my feet again

[006-151] but with a new feeling in my blood. I had to smash Stumm or never sleep

[006-152] in my bed again.

[006-153] I got a wonderful power from this new cold rage of mine. I felt I

[006-154] couldn’t tire, and I danced round and dotted his face till it was

[006-155] streaming with blood. His bulky padded chest was no good to me, so I

[006-156] couldn’t try for the mark.

[006-157] He began to snort now and his breath came heavily. “You infernal cad,”

[006-158] I said in good round English, “I’m going to knock the stuffing out of

[006-159] you,” but he didn’t know what I was saying.

[006-160] Then at last he gave me my chance. He half tripped over a little table

[006-161] and his face stuck forward. I got him on the point of the chin, and put

[006-162] every ounce of weight I possessed behind the blow. He crumpled up in a

[006-163] heap and rolled over, upsetting a lamp and knocking a big china jar in

[006-164] two. His head, I remember, lay under the escritoire from which he had

[006-165] taken my passport.
