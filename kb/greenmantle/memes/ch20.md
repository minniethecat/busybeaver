# Ch. 20: Peter Pienaar Goes to the Wars

[020-001] “The whole half-dozen. They are very worried already about what has

[020-002] happened. She made me sound them in her presence, and they were quite

[020-003] ready to accept me as Greenmantle’s successor. But they have their

[020-004] suspicions about what happened at the villa, and they’ve no love for

[020-005] the woman ... They’d follow me through hell if I bade them, but they

[020-006] would rather it was my own show.”

[020-007] “That’s all right,” I cried. “It is the one thing I’ve been doubtful

[020-008] about. Now observe this map. Erzerum isn’t invested by a long chalk.

[020-009] The Russians are round it in a broad half-moon. That means that all the

[020-010] west, south-west, and north-west is open and undefended by trench

[020-011] lines. There are flanks far away to the north and south in the hills

[020-012] which can be turned, and once we get round a flank there’s nothing

[020-013] between us and our friends ... I’ve figured out our road,” and I traced

[020-014] it on the map. “If we can make that big circuit to the west and get

[020-015] over that pass unobserved we’re bound to strike a Russian column the

[020-016] next day. It’ll be a rough road, but I fancy we’ve all ridden as bad in

[020-017] our time. But one thing we must have, and that’s horses. Can we and

[020-018] your six ruffians slip off in the darkness on the best beasts in this

[020-019] township? If you can manage that, we’ll do the trick.”

[020-020] [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] sat down and pondered. Thank heaven, he was thinking now of

[020-021] action and not of his own conscience.

[020-022] “It must be done,” he said at last, “but it won’t be easy. Hussin’s a

[020-023] great fellow, but as you know well, Dick, horses right up at the

[020-024] battle-front are not easy to come by. Tomorrow I’ve got some kind of

[020-025] infernal fast to observe, and the next day that woman will be coaching

[020-026] me for my part. We’ll have to give Hussin time ... I wish to heaven it

[020-027] could be tonight.” He was silent again for a bit, and then he said: “I

[020-028] believe the best time would be the third night, the eve of the

[020-029] Revelation. She’s bound to leave me alone that night.”

[020-030] “Right-o,” I said. “It won’t be much fun sitting waiting in this cold

[020-031] sepulchre; but we must keep our heads and risk nothing by being in a

[020-032] hurry. Besides, if [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] wins through, the Turk will be a busy man by

[020-033] the day after tomorrow.”

[020-034] The key turned in the door and Hussin stole in like a shade. It was the

[020-035] signal for Sandy to leave.

[020-036] “You fellows have given me a new lease of life,” he said. “I’ve got a

[020-037] plan now, and I can set my teeth and stick it out.”

[020-038] He went up to Peter and gripped his hand. “Good [[fate-and-providence|luck]]. You’re the

[020-039] bravest man I’ve ever met, and I’ve seen a few.” Then he turned

[020-040] abruptly and went out, followed by an exhortation from [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] to

[020-041] “Get busy about the quadrupeds.”

[020-042] Then we set about equipping Peter for his crusade. It was a simple job,

[020-043] for we were not rich in properties. His get-up, with his thick

[020-044] fur-collared greatcoat, was not unlike the ordinary Turkish officer

[020-045] seen in a dim light. But Peter had no intention of passing for a Turk,

[020-046] or indeed of giving anybody the chance of seeing him, and he was more

[020-047] concerned to fit in with the landscape. So he stripped off the

[020-048] greatcoat and pulled a grey sweater of mine over his jacket, and put on

[020-049] his head a woollen helmet of the same colour. He had no need of the map

[020-050] for he had long since got his route by heart, and what was once fixed

[020-051] in that mind stuck like wax; but I made him take Stumm’s plan and

[020-052] paper, hidden below his shirt. The big difficulty, I saw, would be

[020-053] getting to the Russians without getting shot, assuming he passed the

[020-054] Turkish trenches. He could only hope that he would strike someone with

[020-055] a smattering of English or German. Twice he ascended to the roof and

[020-056] came back cheerful, for there was promise of wild weather.

[020-057] Hussin brought in our supper, and Peter made up a parcel of food.

[020-058] Blenkiron and I had both small flasks of brandy and I gave him mine.

[020-059] Then he held out his hand quite simply, like a good child who is going

[020-060] off to bed. It was too much for Blenkiron. With large tears rolling

[020-061] down his face he announced that, if we all came through, he was going

[020-062] to fit him into the softest berth that money could buy. I don’t think

[020-063] he was understood, for old Peter’s eyes had now the faraway absorption

[020-064] of the hunter who has found game. He was thinking only of his job.

[020-065] Two legs and a pair of very shabby boots vanished through the trap, and

[020-066] suddenly I felt utterly lonely and desperately sad. The guns were

[020-067] beginning to roar again in the east, and in the intervals came the

[020-068] whistle of the rising storm.

[020-069] CHAPTER XX.

[020-070] Peter Pienaar Goes to the Wars

[020-071] This chapter is the tale that Peter told me—long after, sitting beside

[020-072] a stove in the hotel at Bergen, where we were waiting for our boat.

[020-073] He climbed on the roof and shinned down the broken bricks of the outer

[020-074] wall. The outbuilding we were lodged in abutted on a road, and was

[020-075] outside the proper _enceinte_ of the house. At ordinary times I have no

[020-076] doubt there were sentries, but Sandy and Hussin had probably managed to

[020-077] clear them off this end for a little. Anyhow he saw nobody as he

[020-078] crossed the road and dived into the snowy fields.

[020-079] He knew very well that he must do the job in the twelve hours of

[020-080] darkness ahead of him. The immediate front of a battle is a bit too

[020-081] public for anyone to lie hidden in by day, especially when two or three

[020-082] feet of snow make everything kenspeckle. Now hurry in a job of this

[020-083] kind was abhorrent to Peter’s soul, for, like all Boers, his tastes

[020-084] were for slowness and sureness, though he could hustle fast enough when

[020-085] haste was needed. As he pushed through the winter fields he reckoned up

[020-086] the things in his favour, and found the only one the dirty weather.

[020-087] There was a high, gusty wind, blowing scuds of snow but never coming to

[020-088] any great fall. The frost had gone, and the lying snow was as soft as

[020-089] butter. That was all to the good, he thought, for a clear, hard night

[020-090] would have been the devil.

[020-091] The first bit was through farmlands, which were seamed with little

[020-092] snow-filled water-furrows. Now and then would come a house and a patch

[020-093] of fruit trees, but there was nobody abroad. The roads were crowded

[020-094] enough, but Peter had no use for roads. I can picture him swinging

[020-095] along with his bent back, stopping every now and then to sniff and

[020-096] listen, alert for the foreknowledge of danger. When he chose he could

[020-097] cover country like an antelope.

[020-098] Soon he struck a big road full of transport. It was the road from

[020-099] Erzerum to the Palantuken pass, and he waited his chance and crossed

[020-100] it. After that the ground grew rough with boulders and patches of

[020-101] thorn-trees, splendid cover where he could move fast without worrying.

[020-102] Then he was pulled up suddenly on the bank of a river. The map had

[020-103] warned him of it, but not that it would be so big.

[020-104] It was a torrent swollen with melting snow and rains in the hills, and

[020-105] it was running fifty yards wide. Peter thought he could have swum it,

[020-106] but he was very averse to a drenching. “A wet man makes too much

[020-107] noise,” he said, and besides, there was the off-chance that the current

[020-108] would be too much for him. So he moved up stream to look for a bridge.

[020-109] In ten minutes he found one, a new-made thing of trestles, broad enough

[020-110] to take transport wagons. It was guarded, for he heard the tramp of a

[020-111] sentry, and as he pulled himself up the bank he observed a couple of

[020-112] long wooden huts, obviously some kind of billets. These were on the

[020-113] near side of the stream, about a dozen yards from the bridge. A door

[020-114] stood open and a light showed in it, and from within came the sound of

[020-115] voices.... Peter had a sense of hearing like a wild animal, and he

[020-116] could detect even from the confused gabble that the voices were German.

[020-117] As he lay and listened someone came over the bridge. It was an officer,

[020-118] for the sentry saluted. The man disappeared in one of the huts. Peter

[020-119] had struck the billets and repairing shop of a squad of German sappers.

[020-120] He was just going ruefully to retrace his steps and try to find a good

[020-121] place to swim the stream when it struck him that the officer who had

[020-122] passed him wore clothes very like his own. He, too, had had a grey

[020-123] sweater and a Balaclava helmet, for even a German officer ceases to be

[020-124] dressy on a mid-winter’s night in [[anatolia|Anatolia]]. The idea came to Peter to

[020-125] walk boldly across the bridge and trust to the sentry not seeing the

[020-126] difference.

[020-127] He slipped round a corner of the hut and marched down the road. The

[020-128] sentry was now at the far end, which was lucky, for if the worst came

[020-129] to the worst he could throttle him. Peter, mimicking the stiff German

[020-130] walk, swung past him, his head down as if to protect him from the wind.

[020-131] The man saluted. He did more, for he offered conversation. The officer

[020-132] must have been a genial soul.

[020-133] “It’s a rough night, Captain,” he said in German. “The wagons are late.

[020-134] Pray God, Michael hasn’t got a shell in his lot. They’ve begun putting

[020-135] over some big ones.”

[020-136] Peter grunted good night in German and strode on. He was just leaving

[020-137] the road when he heard a great halloo behind him.

[020-138] The real officer must have appeared on his heels, and the sentry’s

[020-139] doubts had been stirred. A whistle was blown, and, looking back, Peter

[020-140] saw lanterns waving in the gale. They were coming out to look for the

[020-141] duplicate.

[020-142] He stood still for a second, and noticed the lights spreading out south

[020-143] of the road. He was just about to dive off it on the north side when he

[020-144] was aware of a difficulty. On that side a steep bank fell to a ditch,

[020-145] and the bank beyond bounded a big flood. He could see the dull ruffle

[020-146] of the water under the wind.

[020-147] On the road itself he would soon be caught; south of it the search was

[020-148] beginning; and the ditch itself was no place to hide, for he saw a

[020-149] lantern moving up it. Peter dropped into it all the same and made a

[020-150] plan. The side below the road was a little undercut and very steep. He

[020-151] resolved to plaster himself against it, for he would be hidden from the

[020-152] road, and a searcher in the ditch would not be likely to explore the

[020-153] unbroken sides. It was always a maxim of Peter’s that the best

[020-154] hiding-place was the worst, the least obvious to the minds of those who

[020-155] were looking for you.

[020-156] He waited until the lights both in the road and the ditch came nearer,

[020-157] and then he gripped the edge with his left hand, where some stones gave

[020-158] him purchase, dug the toes of his boots into the wet soil and stuck

[020-159] like a limpet. It needed some strength to keep the position for long,

[020-160] but the muscles of his arms and legs were like whipcord.

[020-161] The searcher in the ditch soon got tired, for the place was very wet,

[020-162] and joined his comrades on the road. They came along, running, flashing

[020-163] the lanterns into the trench, and exploring all the immediate

[020-164] countryside.
