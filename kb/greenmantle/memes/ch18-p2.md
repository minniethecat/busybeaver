# Ch. 18: Sparrows on the Housetops (cont'd, part 2)

[018-001] I was awakened by a pressure below my left ear. I thought it was [[peter-pienaar|Peter]],

[018-002] for it is the old hunter’s trick of waking a man so that he makes no

[018-003] noise. But another voice spoke. It told me that there was no time to

[018-004] lose and to rise and follow, and the voice was the voice of Hussin.

[018-005] Peter was awake, and we stirred [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] out of heavy slumber. We were

[018-006] bidden take off our boots and hang them by their laces round our necks

[018-007] as country boys do when they want to go barefoot. Then we tiptoed to

[018-008] the door, which was ajar.

[018-009] Outside was a passage with a flight of steps at one end which led to

[018-010] the open air. On these steps lay a faint shine of starlight, and by its

[018-011] help I saw a man huddled up at the foot of them. It was our sentry,

[018-012] neatly and scientifically gagged and tied up.

[018-013] The steps brought us to a little courtyard about which the walls of the

[018-014] houses rose like cliffs. We halted while Hussin listened intently.

[018-015] Apparently the coast was clear and our guide led us to one side, which

[018-016] was clothed by a stout wooden trellis. Once it may have supported

[018-017] fig-trees, but now the plants were dead and only withered tendrils and

[018-018] rotten stumps remained.

[018-019] It was child’s play for Peter and me to go up that trellis, but it was

[018-020] the deuce and all for Blenkiron. He was in poor condition and puffed

[018-021] like a grampus, and he seemed to have no sort of head for heights. But

[018-022] he was as game as a buffalo, and started in gallantly till his arms

[018-023] gave out and he fairly stuck. So Peter and I went up on each side of

[018-024] him, taking an arm apiece, as I had once seen done to a man with

[018-025] vertigo in the Kloof Chimney on Table Mountain. I was mighty thankful

[018-026] when I got him panting on the top and Hussin had shinned up beside us.

[018-027] We crawled along a broadish wall, with an inch or two of powdery snow

[018-028] on it, and then up a sloping buttress on to the flat roof of the house.

[018-029] It was a miserable business for Blenkiron, who would certainly have

[018-030] fallen if he could have seen what was below him, and Peter and I had to

[018-031] stand to attention all the time. Then began a more difficult job.

[018-032] Hussin pointed out a ledge which took us past a stack of chimneys to

[018-033] another building slightly lower, this being the route he fancied. At

[018-034] that I sat down resolutely and put on my boots, and the others

[018-035] followed. Frost-bitten feet would be a poor asset in this kind of

[018-036] travelling.

[018-037] It was a bad step for Blenkiron, and we only got him past it by Peter

[018-038] and I spread-eagling ourselves against the wall and passing him in

[018-039] front of us with his face towards us. We had no grip, and if he had

[018-040] stumbled we should all three have been in the courtyard. But we got it

[018-041] over, and dropped as softly as possible on to the roof of the next

[018-042] house. Hussin had his finger on his lips, and I soon saw why. For there

[018-043] was a lighted window in the wall we had descended.

[018-044] Some imp prompted me to wait behind and explore. The others followed

[018-045] Hussin and were soon at the far end of the roof, where a kind of wooden

[018-046] pavilion broke the line, while I tried to get a look inside. The window

[018-047] was curtained, and had two folding sashes which clasped in the middle.

[018-048] Through a gap in the curtain I saw a little lamp-lit room and a big man

[018-049] sitting at a table littered with papers.

[018-050] I watched him, fascinated, as he turned to consult some document and

[018-051] made a marking on the map before him. Then he suddenly rose, stretched

[018-052] himself, cast a glance at the window, and went out of the room, making

[018-053] a great clatter in descending the wooden staircase. He left the door

[018-054] ajar and the lamp burning.

[018-055] I guessed he had gone to have a look at his prisoners, in which case

[018-056] the show was up. But what filled my mind was an insane desire to get a

[018-057] sight of his map. It was one of those mad impulses which utterly cloud

[018-058] right reason, a thing independent of any plan, a crazy leap in the

[018-059] dark. But it was so strong that I would have pulled that window out by

[018-060] its frame, if need be, to get to that table.

[018-061] There was no need, for the flimsy clasp gave at the first pull, and the

[018-062] sashes swung open. I scrambled in, after listening for steps on the

[018-063] stairs. I crumpled up the map and stuck it in my pocket, as well as the

[018-064] paper from which I had seen him copying. Very carefully I removed all

[018-065] marks of my entry, brushed away the snow from the boards, pulled back

[018-066] the curtain, got out and refastened the window. Still there was no

[018-067] sound of his return. Then I started off to catch up the others.

[018-068] I found them shivering in the roof pavilion. “We’ve got to move pretty

[018-069] fast,” I said, “for I’ve just been burgling old Stumm’s private

[018-070] cabinet. Hussin, my lad, d’you hear that? They may be after us any

[018-071] moment, so I pray Heaven we soon strike better going.”

[018-072] Hussin understood. He led us at a smart pace from one roof to another,

[018-073] for here they were all of the same height, and only low parapets and

[018-074] screens divided them. We never saw a soul, for a winter’s night is not

[018-075] the time you choose to saunter on your housetop. I kept my ears open

[018-076] for trouble behind us, and in about five minutes I heard it. A riot of

[018-077] voices broke out, with one louder than the rest, and, looking back, I

[018-078] saw lanterns waving. Stumm had realized his loss and found the tracks

[018-079] of the thief.

[018-080] Hussin gave one glance behind and then hurried us on at break-neck

[018-081] pace, with old Blenkiron gasping and stumbling. The shouts behind us

[018-082] grew louder, as if some eye quicker than the rest had caught our

[018-083] movement in the starlit darkness. It was very evident that if they kept

[018-084] up the chase we should be caught, for Blenkiron was about as useful on

[018-085] a roof as a hippo.

[018-086] Presently we came to a big drop, with a kind of ladder down it, and at

[018-087] the foot a shallow ledge running to the left into a pit of darkness.

[018-088] Hussin gripped my arm and pointed down it. “Follow it,” he whispered,

[018-089] “and you will reach a roof which spans a street. Cross it, and on the

[018-090] other side is a mosque. Turn to the right there and you will find easy

[018-091] going for fifty metres, well screened from the higher roofs. For

[018-092] Allah’s sake keep in the shelter of the screen. Somewhere there I will

[018-093] join you.”

[018-094] He hurried us along the ledge for a bit and then went back, and with

[018-095] snow from the corners covered up our tracks. After that he went

[018-096] straight on himself, taking strange short steps like a bird. I saw his

[018-097] game. He wanted to lead our pursuers after him, and he had to multiply

[018-098] the tracks and trust to Stumm’s fellows not spotting that they all were

[018-099] made by one man.

[018-100] But I had quite enough to think of in getting Blenkiron along that

[018-101] ledge. He was pretty nearly foundered, he was in a sweat of terror, and

[018-102] as a matter of fact he was taking one of the biggest risks of his life,

[018-103] for we had no rope and his neck depended on himself. I could hear him

[018-104] invoking some unknown deity called Holy Mike. But he ventured

[018-105] gallantly, and we got to the roof which ran across the street. That was

[018-106] easier, though ticklish enough, but it was no joke skirting the cupola

[018-107] of that infernal mosque. At last we found the parapet and breathed more

[018-108] freely, for we were now under shelter from the direction of danger. I

[018-109] spared a moment to look round, and thirty yards off, across the street,

[018-110] I saw a weird spectacle.

[018-111] The hunt was proceeding along the roofs parallel to the one we were

[018-112] lodged on. I saw the flicker of the lanterns, waved up and down as the

[018-113] bearers slipped in the snow, and I heard their cries like hounds on a

[018-114] trail. Stumm was not among them: he had not the shape for that sort of

[018-115] business. They passed us and continued to our left, now hid by a

[018-116] jutting chimney, now clear to view against the sky line. The roofs they

[018-117] were on were perhaps six feet higher than ours, so even from our

[018-118] shelter we could mark their course. If Hussin were going to be hunted

[018-119] across Erzerum it was a bad look-out for us, for I hadn’t the foggiest

[018-120] notion where we were or where we were going to.

[018-121] But as we watched we saw something more. The wavering lanterns were now

[018-122] three or four hundred yards away, but on the roofs just opposite us

[018-123] across the street there appeared a man’s figure. I thought it was one

[018-124] of the hunters, and we all crouched lower, and then I recognized the

[018-125] lean agility of Hussin. He must have doubled back, keeping in the dusk

[018-126] to the left of the pursuit, and taking big risks in the open places.

[018-127] But there he was now, exactly in front of us, and separated only by the

[018-128] width of the narrow street.

[018-129] He took a step backward, gathered himself for a spring, and leaped

[018-130] clean over the gap. Like a cat he lighted on the parapet above us, and

[018-131] stumbled forward with the impetus right on our heads.

[018-132] “We are safe for the moment,” he whispered, “but when they miss me they

[018-133] will return. We must make good haste.”

[018-134] The next half-hour was a maze of twists and turns, slipping down icy

[018-135] roofs and climbing icier chimney-stacks. The stir of the city had gone,

[018-136] and from the black streets below came scarcely a sound. But always the

[018-137] great tattoo of guns beat in the east. Gradually we descended to a

[018-138] lower level, till we emerged on the top of a shed in a courtyard.

[018-139] Hussin gave an odd sort of cry, like a demented owl, and something

[018-140] began to stir below us.

[018-141] It was a big covered wagon, full of bundles of forage, and drawn by

[018-142] four mules. As we descended from the shed into the frozen litter of the

[018-143] yard, a man came out of the shade and spoke low to Hussin. Peter and I

[018-144] lifted Blenkiron into the cart, and scrambled in beside him, and I

[018-145] never felt anything more blessed than the warmth and softness of that

[018-146] place after the frosty roofs. I had forgotten all about my hunger, and

[018-147] only yearned for sleep. Presently the wagon moved out of the courtyard

[018-148] into the dark streets.

[018-149] Then Blenkiron began to laugh, a deep internal rumble which shook him

[018-150] violently and brought down a heap of forage on his head. I thought it

[018-151] was hysterics, the relief from the tension of the past hour. But it

[018-152] wasn’t. His body might be out of training, but there was never anything

[018-153] the matter with his nerves. He was consumed with honest merriment.

[018-154] “Say, Major,” he gasped, “I don’t usually cherish dislikes for my

[018-155] fellow men, but somehow I didn’t cotton to Colonel Stumm. But now I

[018-156] almost love him. You hit his jaw very bad in Germany, and now you’ve

[018-157] annexed his private file, and I guess it’s important or he wouldn’t

[018-158] have been so mighty set on steeple-chasing over those roofs. I haven’t

[018-159] done such a thing since I broke into neighbour Brown’s woodshed to

[018-160] steal his tame “possum, and that’s forty years back. It’s the first

[018-161] piece of genooine amusement I’ve struck in this game, and I haven’t

[018-162] laughed so much since old Jim Hooker told the tale of ‘Cousin Sally

[018-163] Dillard’ when we were hunting ducks in Michigan and his wife’s brother

[018-164] had an apoplexy in the night and died of it.”
