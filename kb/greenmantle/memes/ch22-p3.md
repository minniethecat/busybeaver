# Ch. 22: The Guns of the North (cont'd, part 3)

[022-001] But I knew there was no choice. With [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] crippled we were pinned

[022-002] to the _castrol_. Our numbers were up all right.

[022-003] CHAPTER XXII.

[022-004] The Guns of the North

[022-005] But no more shells fell.

[022-006] The night grew dark and showed a field of glittering stars, for the air

[022-007] was sharpening again towards frost. We waited for an hour, crouching

[022-008] just behind the far parapets, but never came that ominous familiar

[022-009] whistle.

[022-010] Then [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] rose and stretched himself. “I’m hungry,” he said. “Let’s

[022-011] have out the food, Hussin. We’ve eaten nothing since before daybreak. I

[022-012] wonder what is the meaning of this respite?”

[022-013] I fancied I knew.

[022-014] “It’s Stumm’s way,” I said. “He wants to torture us. He’ll keep us

[022-015] hours on tenterhooks, while he sits over yonder exulting in what he

[022-016] thinks we’re enduring. He has just enough imagination for that ... He

[022-017] would rush us if he had the men. As it is, he’s going to blow us to

[022-018] pieces, but do it slowly and smack his lips over it.”

[022-019] Sandy yawned. “We’ll disappoint him, for we won’t be worried, old man.

[022-020] We three are beyond that kind of fear.”

[022-021] “Meanwhile we’re going to do the best we can,” I said. “He’s got the

[022-022] exact range for his whizz-bangs. We’ve got to find a hole somewhere

[022-023] just outside the _castrol_, and some sort of head-cover. We’re bound to

[022-024] get damaged whatever happens, but we’ll stick it out to the end. When

[022-025] they think they have finished with us and rush the place, there may be

[022-026] one of us alive to put a bullet through old Stumm. What do you say?”

[022-027] They agreed, and after our meal Sandy and I crawled out to prospect,

[022-028] leaving the others on guard in case there should be an attack. We found

[022-029] a hollow in the glacis a little south of the _castrol_, and, working

[022-030] very quietly, managed to enlarge it and cut a kind of shallow cave in

[022-031] the hill. It would be no use against a direct hit, but it would give

[022-032] some cover from flying fragments. As I read the situation, Stumm could

[022-033] land as many shells as he pleased in the _castrol_ and wouldn’t bother

[022-034] to attend to the flanks. When the bad shelling began there would be

[022-035] shelter for one or two in the cave.

[022-036] Our enemies were watchful. The riflemen on the east burnt Very flares

[022-037] at intervals, and Stumm’s lot sent up a great star-rocket. I remember

[022-038] that just before midnight hell broke loose round [[palantuken|Fort Palantuken]]. No

[022-039] more Russian shells came into our hollow, but all the road to the east

[022-040] was under fire, and at the Fort itself there was a shattering explosion

[022-041] and a queer scarlet glow which looked as if a magazine had been hit.

[022-042] For about two hours the firing was intense, and then it died down. But

[022-043] it was towards the north that I kept turning my head. There seemed to

[022-044] be something different in the sound there, something sharper in the

[022-045] report of the guns, as if shells were dropping in a narrow valley whose

[022-046] rock walls doubled the echo. Had the Russians by any blessed chance

[022-047] worked round that flank?

[022-048] I got Sandy to listen, but he shook his head. “Those guns are a dozen

[022-049] miles off,” he said. “They’re no nearer than three days ago. But it

[022-050] looks as if the sportsmen on the south might have a chance. When they

[022-051] break through and stream down the valley, they’ll be puzzled to account

[022-052] for what remains of us ... We’re no longer three adventurers in the

[022-053] enemy’s country. We’re the advance guard of the Allies. Our pals don’t

[022-054] know about us, and we’re going to be cut off, which has happened to

[022-055] advance guards before now. But all the same, we’re in our own

[022-056] battle-line again. Doesn’t that cheer you, Dick?”

[022-057] It cheered me wonderfully, for I knew now what had been the weight on

[022-058] my heart ever since I accepted [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]]’s mission. It was the

[022-059] loneliness of it. I was fighting far away from my friends, far away

[022-060] from the true fronts of battle. It was a side-show which, whatever its

[022-061] importance, had none of the exhilaration of the main effort. But now we

[022-062] had come back to familiar ground. We were like the Highlanders cut off

[022-063] at Cite St Auguste on the first day of [[loos|Loos]], or those Scots Guards at

[022-064] Festubert of whom I had heard. Only, the others did not know of it,

[022-065] would never hear of it. If [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] succeeded he might tell the tale, but

[022-066] most likely he was lying dead somewhere in the no-man’s-land between

[022-067] the lines. We should never be heard of again any more, but our work

[022-068] remained. Sir Walter would know that, and he would tell our few

[022-069] belongings that we had gone out in our country’s service.

[022-070] We were in the _castrol_ again, sitting under the parapets. The same

[022-071] thoughts must have been in Sandy’s mind, for he suddenly laughed.

[022-072] “It’s a queer ending, Dick. We simply vanish into the infinite. If the

[022-073] Russians get through they will never recognize what is left of us among

[022-074] so much of the wreckage of battle. The snow will soon cover us, and

[022-075] when the spring comes there will only be a few bleached bones. Upon my

[022-076] soul it is the kind of death I always wanted.” And he quoted softly to

[022-077] himself a verse of an old Scots ballad:

[022-078] “Mony’s the ane for him maks mane,

[022-079] But nane sall ken whar he is gane.

[022-080] Ower his white banes, when they are bare,

[022-081] The wind sall blaw for evermair.”

[022-082] “But our work lives,” I cried, with a sudden great gasp of happiness.

[022-083] “It’s the job that matters, not the men that do it. And our job’s done.

[022-084] We have won, old chap—won hands down—and there is no going back on

[022-085] that. We have won anyway; and if Peter has had a slice of [[fate-and-providence|luck]], we’ve

[022-086] scooped the pool ... After all, we never expected to come out of this

[022-087] thing with our lives.”

[022-088] Blenkiron, with his leg stuck out stiffly before him, was humming

[022-089] quietly to himself, as he often did when he felt cheerful. He had only

[022-090] one song, “John Brown’s Body”; usually only a line at a time, but now

[022-091] he got as far as the whole verse:

[022-092] “He captured Harper’s Ferry, with his nineteen men so true,

[022-093] And he frightened old Virginny till she trembled through and

[022-094] through.

[022-095] They hung him for a traitor, themselves the traitor crew,

[022-096] But his soul goes marching along.”

[022-097] “Feeling good?” I asked.

[022-098] “Fine. I’m about the luckiest man on God’s earth, Major. I’ve always

[022-099] wanted to get into a big show, but I didn’t see how it would come the

[022-100] way of a homely citizen like me, living in a steam-warmed house and

[022-101] going down town to my office every morning. I used to envy my old dad

[022-102] that fought at Chattanooga, and never forgot to tell you about it. But

[022-103] I guess Chattanooga was like a scrap in a Bowery bar compared to this.

[022-104] When I meet the old man in Glory he’ll have to listen some to me.”

[022-105] It was just after Blenkiron spoke that we got a reminder of Stumm’s

[022-106] presence. The gun was well laid, for a shell plumped on the near edge

[022-107] of the castro. It made an end of one of the Companions who was on guard

[022-108] there, badly wounded another, and a fragment gashed my thigh. We took

[022-109] refuge in the shallow cave, but some wild shooting from the east side

[022-110] brought us back to the parapets, for we feared an attack. None came,

[022-111] nor any more shells, and once again the night was quiet.

[022-112] I asked Blenkiron if he had any near relatives.

[022-113] “Why, no, except a sister’s son, a college-boy who has no need of his

[022-114] uncle. It’s fortunate that we three have no wives. I haven’t any

[022-115] regrets, neither, for I’ve had a mighty deal out of life. I was

[022-116] thinking this morning that it was a pity I was going out when I had

[022-117] just got my duo-denum to listen to reason. But I reckon that’s another

[022-118] of my mercies. The good God took away the pain in my stomach so that I

[022-119] might go to Him with a clear head and a thankful heart.”

[022-120] “We’re lucky fellows,” said Sandy; “we’ve all had our whack. When I

[022-121] remember the good times I’ve had I could sing a hymn of praise. We’ve

[022-122] lived long enough to know ourselves, and to shape ourselves into some

[022-123] kind of decency. But think of those boys who have given their lives

[022-124] freely when they scarcely knew what life meant. They were just at the

[022-125] beginning of the road, and they didn’t know what dreary bits lay before

[022-126] them. It was all sunshiny and bright-coloured, and yet they gave it up

[022-127] without a moment’s doubt. And think of the men with wives and children

[022-128] and homes that were the biggest things in life to them. For fellows

[022-129] like us to shirk would be black cowardice. It’s small credit for us to

[022-130] stick it out. But when those others shut their teeth and went forward,

[022-131] they were blessed heroes....”

[022-132] After that we fell silent. A man’s thoughts at a time like that seem to

[022-133] be double-powered, and the memory becomes very sharp and clear. I don’t

[022-134] know what was in the others’ minds, but I know what filled my own...

[022-135] I fancy it isn’t the men who get most out of the world and are always

[022-136] buoyant and cheerful that most fear to die. Rather it is the

[022-137] weak-engined souls who go about with dull eyes, that cling most

[022-138] fiercely to life. They have not the joy of being alive which is a kind

[022-139] of earnest of immortality ... I know that my thoughts were chiefly

[022-140] about the jolly things that I had seen and done; not regret, but

[022-141] gratitude. The panorama of blue noons on the veld unrolled itself

[022-142] before me, and hunter’s nights in the bush, the taste of food and

[022-143] sleep, the bitter stimulus of dawn, the joy of wild adventure, the

[022-144] voices of old staunch friends. Hitherto the war had seemed to make a

[022-145] break with all that had gone before, but now the war was only part of

[022-146] the picture. I thought of my battalion, and the good fellows there,

[022-147] many of whom had fallen on the Loos parapets. I had never looked to

[022-148] come out of that myself. But I had been spared, and given the chance of

[022-149] a greater business, and I had succeeded. That was the tremendous fact,

[022-150] and my mood was humble gratitude to God and exultant pride. Death was a

[022-151] small price to pay for it. As Blenkiron would have said, I had got good

[022-152] value in the deal.

[022-153] The night was getting bitter cold, as happens before dawn. It was frost

[022-154] again, and the sharpness of it woke our hunger. I got out the remnants

[022-155] of the food and wine and we had a last meal. I remember we pledged each

[022-156] other as we drank.

[022-157] “We have eaten our Passover Feast,” said Sandy. “When do you look for

[022-158] the end?”

[022-159] “After dawn,” I said. “Stumm wants daylight to get the full savour of

[022-160] his revenge.”

[022-161] Slowly the sky passed from ebony to grey, and black shapes of hill

[022-162] outlined themselves against it. A wind blew down the valley, bringing

[022-163] the acrid smell of burning, but something too of the freshness of morn.

[022-164] It stirred strange thoughts in me, and woke the old morning vigour of

[022-165] the blood which was never to be mine again. For the first time in that

[022-166] long vigil I was torn with a sudden regret.

[022-167] “We must get into the cave before it is full light,” I said. “We had

[022-168] better draw lots for the two to go.”

[022-169] The choice fell on one of the Companions and Blenkiron. “You can count

[022-170] me out,” said the latter. “If it’s your wish to find a man to be alive

[022-171] when our friends come up to count their spoil, I guess I’m the worst of

[022-172] the lot. I’d prefer, if you don’t mind, to stay here. I’ve made my

[022-173] peace with my Maker, and I’d like to wait quietly on His call. I’ll

[022-174] play a game of Patience to pass the time.”
