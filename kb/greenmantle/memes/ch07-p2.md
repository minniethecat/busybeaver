# Ch. 7: Christmastide (cont'd, part 2)

[007-001] To make things worse my job was not to escape—though that would have

[007-002] been hard enough—but to get to Constantinople, more than a thousand

[007-003] miles off, and I reckoned I couldn’t get there as a tramp. I had to be

[007-004] sent there, and now I had flung away my chance. If I had been a

[007-005] Catholic I would have said a prayer to St Teresa, for she would have

[007-006] understood my troubles.

[007-007] My mother used to say that when you felt down on your [[fate-and-providence|luck]] it was a

[007-008] good cure to count your mercies. So I set about counting mine. The

[007-009] first was that I was well started on my journey, for I couldn’t be

[007-010] above two score miles from the [[danube-river|Danube]]. The second was that I had

[007-011] Stumm’s pass. I didn’t see how I could use it, but there it was. Lastly

[007-012] I had plenty of money—fifty-three English sovereigns and the equivalent

[007-013] of three pounds in German paper which I had changed at the hotel. Also

[007-014] I had squared accounts with old Stumm. That was the biggest mercy of

[007-015] all.

[007-016] I thought I’d better get some sleep, so I found a dryish hole below an

[007-017] oak root and squeezed myself into it. The snow lay deep in these woods

[007-018] and I was sopping wet up to the knees. All the same I managed to sleep

[007-019] for some hours, and got up and shook myself just as the winter’s dawn

[007-020] was breaking through the tree tops. Breakfast was the next thing, and I

[007-021] must find some sort of dwelling.

[007-022] Almost at once I struck a road, a big highway running north and south.

[007-023] I trotted along in the bitter morning to get my circulation started,

[007-024] and presently I began to feel a little better. In a little I saw a

[007-025] church spire, which meant a village. Stumm wouldn’t be likely to have

[007-026] got on my tracks yet, I calculated, but there was always the chance

[007-027] that he had warned all the villages round by telephone and that they

[007-028] might be on the look-out for me. But that risk had to be taken, for I

[007-029] must have food.

[007-030] It was the day before Christmas, I remembered, and people would be

[007-031] holidaying. The village was quite a big place, but at this hour—just

[007-032] after eight o’clock—there was nobody in the street except a wandering

[007-033] dog. I chose the most unassuming shop I could find, where a little boy

[007-034] was taking down the shutters—one of those general stores where they

[007-035] sell everything. The boy fetched a very old woman, who hobbled in from

[007-036] the back, fitting on her spectacles.

[007-037] “Gruss Gott,” she said in a friendly voice, and I took off my cap. I

[007-038] saw from my reflection in a saucepan that I looked moderately

[007-039] respectable in spite of my night in the woods.

[007-040] I told her the story of how I was walking from [[schwandom|Schwandorf]] to see my

[007-041] mother at an imaginary place called Judenfeld, banking on the ignorance

[007-042] of villagers about any place five miles from their homes. I said my

[007-043] luggage had gone astray, and I hadn’t time to wait for it, since my

[007-044] leave was short. The old lady was sympathetic and unsuspecting. She

[007-045] sold me a pound of chocolate, a box of biscuits, the better part of a

[007-046] ham, two tins of sardines and a rucksack to carry them. I also bought

[007-047] some soap, a comb and a cheap razor, and a small Tourists’ Guide,

[007-048] published by a Leipzig firm. As I was leaving I saw what seemed like

[007-049] garments hanging up in the back shop, and turned to have a look at

[007-050] them. They were the kind of thing that Germans wear on their summer

[007-051] walking tours—long shooting capes made of a green stuff they call

[007-052] _loden_. I bought one, and a green felt hat and an alpenstock to keep

[007-053] it company. Then wishing the old woman and her belongings a merry

[007-054] Christmas, I departed and took the shortest cut out of the village.

[007-055] There were one or two people about now, but they did not seem to notice

[007-056] me.

[007-057] I went into the woods again and walked for two miles till I halted for

[007-058] breakfast. I was not feeling quite so fit now, and I did not make much

[007-059] of my provisions, beyond eating a biscuit and some chocolate. I felt

[007-060] very thirsty and longed for hot tea. In an icy pool I washed and with

[007-061] infinite agony shaved my beard. That razor was the worst of its

[007-062] species, and my eyes were running all the time with the pain of the

[007-063] operation. Then I took off the postman’s coat and cap, and buried them

[007-064] below some bushes. I was now a clean-shaven German pedestrian with a

[007-065] green cape and hat, and an absurd walking-stick with an iron-shod

[007-066] end—the sort of person who roams in thousands over the Fatherland in

[007-067] summer, but is a rarish bird in mid-winter.

[007-068] The Tourists’ Guide was a fortunate purchase, for it contained a big

[007-069] map of Bavaria which gave me my bearings. I was certainly not forty

[007-070] miles from the Danube—more like thirty. The road through the village I

[007-071] had left would have taken me to it. I had only to walk due south and I

[007-072] would reach it before night. So far as I could make out there were long

[007-073] tongues of forest running down to the river, and I resolved to keep to

[007-074] the woodlands. At the worst I would meet a forester or two, and I had a

[007-075] good enough story for them. On the highroad there might be awkward

[007-076] questions.

[007-077] When I started out again I felt very stiff and the cold seemed to be

[007-078] growing intense. This puzzled me, for I had not minded it much up to

[007-079] now, and, being warm-blooded by nature, it never used to worry me. A

[007-080] sharp winter night on the high-veld was a long sight chillier than

[007-081] anything I had struck so far in Europe. But now my teeth were

[007-082] chattering and the marrow seemed to be freezing in my bones.

[007-083] The day had started bright and clear, but a wrack of grey clouds soon

[007-084] covered the sky, and a wind from the east began to whistle. As I

[007-085] stumbled along through the snowy undergrowth I kept longing for bright

[007-086] warm places. I thought of those long days on the veld when the earth

[007-087] was like a great yellow bowl, with white roads running to the horizon

[007-088] and a tiny white farm basking in the heart of it, with its blue dam and

[007-089] patches of bright green lucerne. I thought of those baking days on the

[007-090] east coast, when the sea was like mother-of-pearl and the sky one

[007-091] burning turquoise. But most of all I thought of warm scented noons on

[007-092] trek, when one dozed in the shadow of the wagon and sniffed the

[007-093] wood-smoke from the fire where the boys were cooking dinner.

[007-094] From these pleasant pictures I returned to the beastly present—the

[007-095] thick snowy woods, the lowering sky, wet clothes, a hunted present, and

[007-096] a dismal future. I felt miserably depressed, and I couldn’t think of

[007-097] any mercies to count. It struck me that I might be falling sick.

[007-098] [[About]] midday I awoke with a start to the belief that I was being

[007-099] pursued. I cannot explain how or why the feeling came, except that it

[007-100] is a kind of instinct that men get who have lived much in wild

[007-101] countries. My senses, which had been numbed, suddenly grew keen, and my

[007-102] brain began to work double quick.

[007-103] I asked myself what I would do if I were Stumm, with hatred in my

[007-104] heart, a broken jaw to avenge, and pretty well limitless powers. He

[007-105] must have found the car in the sandpit and seen my tracks in the wood

[007-106] opposite. I didn’t know how good he and his men might be at following a

[007-107] spoor, but I knew that any ordinary Kaffir could have nosed it out

[007-108] easily. But he didn’t need to do that. This was a civilized country

[007-109] full of roads and railways. I must some time and somewhere come out of

[007-110] the woods. He could have all the roads watched, and the telephone would

[007-111] set everyone on my track within a radius of fifty miles. Besides, he

[007-112] would soon pick up my trail in the village I had visited that morning.

[007-113] From the map I learned that it was called Greif, and it was likely to

[007-114] live up to that name with me.

[007-115] Presently I came to a rocky knoll which rose out of the forest. Keeping

[007-116] well in shelter I climbed to the top and cautiously looked around me.

[007-117] Away to the east I saw the vale of a river with broad fields and

[007-118] church-spires. West and south the forest rolled unbroken in a

[007-119] wilderness of snowy tree-tops. There was no sign of life anywhere, not

[007-120] even a bird, but I knew very well that behind me in the woods were men

[007-121] moving swiftly on my track, and that it was pretty well impossible for

[007-122] me to get away.

[007-123] There was nothing for it but to go on till I dropped or was taken. I

[007-124] shaped my course south with a shade of west in it, for the map showed

[007-125] me that in that direction I would soonest strike the Danube. What I was

[007-126] going to do when I got there I didn’t trouble to think. I had fixed the

[007-127] river as my immediate goal and the future must take care of itself.

[007-128] I was now certain that I had fever on me. It was still in my bones, as

[007-129] a legacy from Africa, and had come out once or twice when I was with

[007-130] the battalion in Hampshire. The bouts had been short for I had known of

[007-131] their coming and dosed myself. But now I had no quinine, and it looked

[007-132] as if I were in for a heavy go. It made me feel desperately wretched

[007-133] and stupid, and I all but blundered into capture.

[007-134] For suddenly I came on a road and was going to cross it blindly, when a

[007-135] man rode slowly past on a bicycle. Luckily I was in the shade of a

[007-136] clump of hollies and he was not looking my way, though he was not three

[007-137] yards off. I crawled forward to reconnoitre. I saw about half a mile of

[007-138] road running straight through the forest and every two hundred yards

[007-139] was a bicyclist. They wore uniform and appeared to be acting as

[007-140] sentries.

[007-141] This could only have one meaning. Stumm had picketed all the roads and

[007-142] cut me off in an angle of the woods. There was no chance of getting

[007-143] across unobserved. As I lay there with my heart sinking, I had the

[007-144] horrible feeling that the pursuit might be following me from behind,

[007-145] and that at any moment I would be enclosed between two fires.

[007-146] For more than an hour I stayed there with my chin in the snow. I didn’t

[007-147] see any way out, and I was feeling so ill that I didn’t seem to care.

[007-148] Then my chance came suddenly out of the skies.

[007-149] The wind rose, and a great gust of snow blew from the east. In five

[007-150] minutes it was so thick that I couldn’t see across the road. At first I

[007-151] thought it a new addition to my troubles, and then very slowly I saw

[007-152] the opportunity. I slipped down the bank and made ready to cross.

[007-153] I almost blundered into one of the bicyclists. He cried out and fell

[007-154] off his machine, but I didn’t wait to investigate. A sudden access of

[007-155] strength came to me and I darted into the woods on the farther side. I

[007-156] knew I would be soon swallowed from sight in the drift, and I knew that

[007-157] the falling snow would hide my tracks. So I put my best foot forward.

[007-158] I must have run miles before the hot fit passed, and I stopped from

[007-159] sheer bodily weakness. There was no sound except the crush of falling

[007-160] snow, the wind seemed to have gone, and the place was very solemn and

[007-161] quiet. But Heavens! how the snow fell! It was partly screened by the

[007-162] branches, but all the same it was piling itself up deep everywhere. My

[007-163] legs seemed made of lead, my head burned, and there were fiery pains

[007-164] over all my body. I stumbled on blindly, without a notion of any

[007-165] direction, determined only to keep going to the last. For I knew that

[007-166] if I once lay down I would never rise again.
