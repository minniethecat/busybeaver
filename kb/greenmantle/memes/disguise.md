## Overview

Disguise — the assumption of a false identity — is the essential mechanism
by which the plot of *Greenmantle* unfolds.
Richard Hannay travels under the alias Cornelis Brandt, a Boer rebel,
with dyed hair and skin darkened to pass as a South African of Dutch descent [002-165].
Passports, forged documents, and impersonation drive the action
across every phase of the mission from London to Erzerum.

## In Greenmantle

Hannay's disguise as Cornelis Brandt is constructed before he leaves England.
The Foreign Office provides a passport in that name, and Hannay adopts
the persona of a disaffected Boer who has turned against the British [002-031].
His appearance is altered: hair dyed dark, skin stained to match [002-165].

The passport becomes a recurring plot device.
German officers examine it at checkpoints [006-050].
The Rotterdam agent supplies additional forged passports for the journey [003-174].
At the German border, Hannay must produce his papers under hostile scrutiny [008-033].
The tension of identity exposure runs through the entire novel:
every encounter with German or Turkish authority is a potential unmasking.

Multiple characters operate under false identities.
Sandy Arbuthnot appears in various Eastern disguises.
John Blenkiron's American businessman persona is itself a cover
for his intelligence work [005-154].
The novel's spy-thriller structure depends on the constant threat
that any disguise might fail.

## Thematic Significance

Disguise in *Greenmantle* is more than a spy-thriller convention.
It reflects the novel's deeper concern with identity:
who someone truly is beneath their national, ethnic, or political labels.
Hannay's Boer disguise forces him to inhabit the perspective of Britain's enemy,
blurring the line between loyalty and performance.
The theme of disguise connects to the novel's treatment of the East:
a region where, to Western eyes, nothing is as it seems [015-148].
