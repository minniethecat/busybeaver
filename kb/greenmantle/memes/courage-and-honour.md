## Overview

Courage and honour — the martial virtues of bravery under fire,
loyalty to comrades, and adherence to a moral code —
form the ethical backbone of *Greenmantle*.
Buchan's protagonists are defined not by ideology or nationality
but by their possession of these qualities [001-017].
The novel repeatedly tests characters against the standard of courage,
and those who fail — the cowardly, the treacherous — are condemned
more severely than the enemy.

## In Greenmantle

Courage is established as Hannay's defining trait from the opening pages:
"There's courage and courage, and mine was certainly not the right kind" [001-017].
This self-deprecation masks the physical courage that Hannay demonstrates
throughout the mission, but Buchan distinguishes between mere fearlessness
and the deeper courage of moral commitment.

Peter Pienaar embodies cold, professional courage:
"The little fellow was all cold courage, like fine polished blue steel" [013-117].
John Blenkiron, despite his physical limitations, displays the courage
of persistence and loyalty [P01-096].
Sandy Arbuthnot's courage takes the form of psychological resilience —
the ability to inhabit enemy territory without losing himself.

Honour functions as a binding code among men of different nations.
German officers acknowledge Hannay's courage even as they suspect him [004-125].
Blenkiron gives "my word of honour as an American citizen" [015-103],
and the novel treats such pledges as sacred.
When Stumm betrays the code of honour through brutality,
he marks himself as the novel's true villain.

Duty complements courage and honour: Hannay repeatedly reminds himself
that "my first duty was to my mission" [005-143], subordinating
personal inclination to the demands of service.
Nerve — the psychological capacity to withstand fear —
is portrayed as the fragile foundation on which courage rests [020-061].

## Thematic Significance

Courage and honour in *Greenmantle* constitute a universal moral language
that transcends national enmity.
Buchan's gentleman-spy ideal — brave, honourable, dutiful, loyal —
established a template for British adventure fiction
that would persist for decades.
The novel suggests that these virtues are not merely personal qualities
but strategic assets: the mission succeeds not because Hannay is cleverer
than his enemies, but because he is more honourable,
and honour attracts loyalty where intimidation breeds betrayal.
