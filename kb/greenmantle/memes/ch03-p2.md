# Ch. 3: Peter Pienaar (cont'd, part 2)

[003-001] We were dressing when a man in a lieutenant’s uniform came in with a

[003-002] paper in his hand. He was a fresh-faced lad of about twenty, with

[003-003] short-sighted spectacled eyes.

[003-004] “[[hermann-brandt|Herr Brandt]],” he called out.

[003-005] I nodded.

[003-006] “And this is Herr [[peter-pienaar|Pienaar]]?” he asked in Dutch.

[003-007] He saluted. “Gentlemen, I apologize. I am late because of the slowness

[003-008] of the Herr Commandant’s motor-car. Had I been in time you would not

[003-009] have been required to go through this ceremony. We have been advised of

[003-010] your coming, and I am instructed to attend you on your journey. The

[003-011] train for [[berlin|Berlin]] leaves in half an hour. Pray do me the [[courage-and-honour|honour]] to join

[003-012] me in a bock.”

[003-013] With a feeling of distinction we stalked out of the ordinary ruck of

[003-014] passengers and followed the lieutenant to the station restaurant. He

[003-015] plunged at once into conversation, talking the Dutch of Holland, which

[003-016] Peter, who had forgotten his school-days, found a bit hard to follow.

[003-017] He was unfit for active service, because of his eyes and a weak heart,

[003-018] but he was a desperate fire-eater in that stuffy restaurant. By his way

[003-019] of it Germany could gobble up the French and the Russians whenever she

[003-020] cared, but she was aiming at getting all the Middle East in her hands

[003-021] first, so that she could come out conqueror with the practical control

[003-022] of half the world.

[003-023] “Your friends the English,” he said grinning, “will come last. When we

[003-024] have starved them and destroyed their commerce with our under-sea boats

[003-025] we will show them what our navy can do. For a year they have been

[003-026] wasting their time in brag and politics, and we have been building

[003-027] great ships—oh, so many! My cousin at Kiel—” and he looked over his

[003-028] shoulder.

[003-029] But we never heard about that cousin at Kiel. A short sunburnt man came

[003-030] in and our friend sprang up and saluted, clicking his heels like a pair

[003-031] of tongs.

[003-032] “These are the South African Dutch, Herr Captain,” he said.

[003-033] The new-comer looked us over with bright intelligent eyes, and started

[003-034] questioning Peter in the taal. It was well that we had taken some pains

[003-035] with our story, for this man had been years in German South West, and

[003-036] knew every mile of the borders. [[captain-zorn|Zorn]] was his name, and both Peter and I

[003-037] thought we remembered hearing him spoken of.

[003-038] I am thankful to say that we both showed up pretty well. Peter told his

[003-039] story to perfection, not pitching it too high, and asking me now and

[003-040] then for a name or to verify some detail. Captain Zorn looked

[003-041] satisfied.

[003-042] “You seem the right kind of fellows,” he said. “But remember”—and he

[003-043] bent his brows on us—“we do not understand slimness in this land. If

[003-044] you are honest you will be rewarded, but if you dare to play a double

[003-045] game you will be shot like dogs. Your race has produced over many

[003-046] traitors for my taste.”

[003-047] “I ask no reward,” I said gruffly. “We are not Germans or Germany’s

[003-048] slaves. But so long as she fights against England we will fight for

[003-049] her.”

[003-050] “Bold words,” he said; “but you must bow your stiff necks to discipline

[003-051] first. Discipline has been the weak point of you Boers, and you have

[003-052] suffered for it. You are no more a nation. In Germany we put discipline

[003-053] first and last, and therefore we will conquer the world. Off with you

[003-054] now. Your train starts in three minutes. We will see what [[stumm|von Stumm]]

[003-055] will make of you.”

[003-056] That fellow gave me the best “feel” of any German I had yet met. He was

[003-057] a white man and I could have worked with him. I liked his stiff chin

[003-058] and steady blue eyes.

[003-059] My chief recollection of our journey to Berlin was its commonplaceness.

[003-060] The spectacled lieutenant fell asleep, and for the most part we had the

[003-061] carriage to ourselves. Now and again a soldier on leave would drop in,

[003-062] most of them tired men with heavy eyes. No wonder, poor devils, for

[003-063] they were coming back from the Yser or the [[ypres|Ypres]] salient. I would have

[003-064] liked to talk to them, but officially of course I knew no German, and

[003-065] the conversation I overheard did not signify much. It was mostly about

[003-066] regimental details, though one chap, who was in better spirits than the

[003-067] rest, observed that this was the last Christmas of misery, and that

[003-068] next year he would be holidaying at home with full pockets. The others

[003-069] assented, but without much conviction.

[003-070] The winter day was short, and most of the journey was made in the dark.

[003-071] I could see from the window the lights of little villages, and now and

[003-072] then the blaze of ironworks and forges. We stopped at a town for

[003-073] dinner, where the platform was crowded with drafts waiting to go

[003-074] westward. We saw no signs of any scarcity of food, such as the English

[003-075] newspapers wrote about. We had an excellent dinner at the station

[003-076] restaurant, which, with a bottle of white wine, cost just three

[003-077] shillings apiece. The bread, to be sure, was poor, but I can put up

[003-078] with the absence of bread if I get a juicy fillet of beef and as good

[003-079] vegetables as you will see in the Savoy.

[003-080] I was a little afraid of our giving ourselves away in our sleep, but I

[003-081] need have had no fear, for our escort slumbered like a hog with his

[003-082] mouth wide open. As we roared through the darkness I kept pinching

[003-083] myself to make myself feel that I was in the enemy’s land on a wild

[003-084] mission. The rain came on, and we passed through dripping towns, with

[003-085] the lights shining from the wet streets. As we went eastward the

[003-086] lighting seemed to grow more generous. After the murk of [[london|London]] it was

[003-087] queer to slip through garish stations with a hundred arc lights

[003-088] glowing, and to see long lines of lamps running to the horizon. Peter

[003-089] dropped off early, but I kept awake till midnight, trying to focus

[003-090] thoughts that persistently strayed. Then I, too, dozed and did not

[003-091] awake till about five in the morning, when we ran into a great busy

[003-092] terminus as bright as midday. It was the easiest and most unsuspicious

[003-093] journey I ever made.

[003-094] The lieutenant stretched himself and smoothed his rumpled uniform. We

[003-095] carried our scanty luggage to a _droschke_, for there seemed to be no

[003-096] porters. Our escort gave the address of some hotel and we rumbled out

[003-097] into brightly lit empty streets.

[003-098] “A mighty dorp,” said Peter. “Of a truth the Germans are a great

[003-099] people.”

[003-100] The lieutenant nodded good-humouredly.

[003-101] “The greatest people on earth,” he said, “as their enemies will soon

[003-102] bear witness.”

[003-103] I would have given a lot for a bath, but I felt that it would be

[003-104] outside my part, and Peter was not of the washing persuasion. But we

[003-105] had a very good breakfast of coffee and eggs, and then the lieutenant

[003-106] started on the telephone. He began by being dictatorial, then he seemed

[003-107] to be switched on to higher authorities, for he grew more polite, and

[003-108] at the end he fairly crawled. He made some arrangements, for he

[003-109] informed us that in the afternoon we would see some fellow whose title

[003-110] he could not translate into Dutch. I judged he was a great swell, for

[003-111] his voice became reverential at the mention of him.

[003-112] He took us for a walk that morning after Peter and I had attended to

[003-113] our toilets. We were an odd pair of scallywags to look at, but as South

[003-114] African as a wait-a-bit bush. Both of us had ready-made tweed suits,

[003-115] grey flannel shirts with flannel collars, and felt hats with broader

[003-116] brims than they like in Europe. I had strong-nailed brown boots, Peter

[003-117] a pair of those mustard-coloured abominations which the Portuguese

[003-118] affect and which made him hobble like a Chinese lady. He had a scarlet

[003-119] satin tie which you could hear a mile off. My beard had grown to quite

[003-120] a respectable length, and I trimmed it like General Smuts’. Peter’s was

[003-121] the kind of loose flapping thing the _taakhaar_ loves, which has

[003-122] scarcely ever been shaved, and is combed once in a blue moon. I must

[003-123] say we made a pretty solid pair. Any South African would have set us

[003-124] down as a Boer from the back-veld who had bought a suit of clothes in

[003-125] the nearest store, and his cousin from some one-horse dorp who had been

[003-126] to school and thought himself the devil of a fellow. We fairly reeked

[003-127] of the sub-continent, as the papers call it.

[003-128] It was a fine morning after the rain, and we wandered about in the

[003-129] streets for a couple of hours. They were busy enough, and the shops

[003-130] looked rich and bright with their Christmas goods, and one big store

[003-131] where I went to buy a pocket-knife was packed with customers. One

[003-132] didn’t see very many young men, and most of the women wore mourning.

[003-133] Uniforms were everywhere, but their wearers generally looked like

[003-134] dug-outs or office fellows. We had a glimpse of the squat building

[003-135] which housed the [[military-command|General Staff]] and took off our hats to it. Then we

[003-136] stared at the Marinamt, and I wondered what plots were hatching there

[003-137] behind old Tirpitz’s whiskers. The capital gave one an impression of

[003-138] ugly cleanness and a sort of dreary effectiveness. And yet I found it

[003-139] depressing—more depressing than London. I don’t know how to put it, but

[003-140] the whole big concern seemed to have no soul in it, to be like a big

[003-141] factory instead of a city. You won’t make a factory look like a house,

[003-142] though you decorate its front and plant rose-bushes all round it. The

[003-143] place depressed and yet cheered me. It somehow made the German people

[003-144] seem smaller.

[003-145] At three o’clock the lieutenant took us to a plain white building in a

[003-146] side street with sentries at the door. A young staff officer met us and

[003-147] made us wait for five minutes in an ante-room. Then we were ushered

[003-148] into a big room with a polished floor on which Peter nearly sat down.

[003-149] There was a log fire burning, and seated at a table was a little man in

[003-150] spectacles with his hair brushed back from his brow like a popular

[003-151] violinist. He was the boss, for the lieutenant saluted him and

[003-152] announced our names. Then he disappeared, and the man at the table

[003-153] motioned us to sit down in two chairs before him.

[003-154] “Herr Brandt and Herr Pienaar?” he asked, looking over his glasses.

[003-155] But it was the other man that caught my eye. He stood with his back to

[003-156] the fire leaning his elbows on the mantelpiece. He was a perfect

[003-157] mountain of a fellow, six and a half feet if he was an inch, with

[003-158] shoulders on him like a shorthorn bull. He was in uniform and the

[003-159] black-and-white ribbon of the Iron Cross showed at a buttonhole. His

[003-160] tunic was all wrinkled and strained as if it could scarcely contain his

[003-161] huge chest, and mighty hands were clasped over his stomach. That man

[003-162] must have had the length of reach of a gorilla. He had a great, lazy,

[003-163] smiling face, with a square cleft chin which stuck out beyond the rest.

[003-164] His brow retreated and the stubby back of his head ran forward to meet

[003-165] it, while his neck below bulged out over his collar. His head was

[003-166] exactly the shape of a pear with the sharp end topmost.

[003-167] He stared at me with his small bright eyes and I stared back. I had

[003-168] struck something I had been looking for for a long time, and till that

[003-169] moment I wasn’t sure that it existed. Here was the German of

[003-170] caricature, the real German, the fellow we were up against. He was as

[003-171] hideous as a hippopotamus, but effective. Every bristle on his odd head

[003-172] was effective.
