# Ch. 4: Adventures of Two Dutchmen on the Loose

[004-001] The man at the table was speaking. I took him to be a civilian official

[004-002] of sorts, pretty high up from his surroundings, perhaps an

[004-003] Under-Secretary. His Dutch was slow and careful, but good—too good for

[004-004] [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]. He had a paper before him and was asking us questions from it.

[004-005] They did not amount to much, being pretty well a repetition of those

[004-006] [[captain-zorn|Zorn]] had asked us at the frontier. I answered fluently, for I had all

[004-007] our lies by heart.

[004-008] Then the man on the hearthrug broke in. “I’ll talk to them,

[004-009] Excellency,” he said in German. “You are too academic for those outland

[004-010] swine.”

[004-011] He began in the _taal_, with the thick guttural accent that you get in

[004-012] German South West. “You have heard of me,” he said. “I am the Colonel

[004-013] [[stumm|von Stumm]] who fought the Hereros.”

[004-014] Peter pricked up his ears. “_Ja_, Baas, you cut off the chief Baviaan’s

[004-015] head and sent it in pickle about the country. I have seen it.”

[004-016] The big man laughed. “You see I am not forgotten,” he said to his

[004-017] friend, and then to us: “So I treat my enemies, and so will Germany

[004-018] treat hers. You, too, if you fail me by a fraction of an inch.” And he

[004-019] laughed loud again.

[004-020] There was something horrible in that boisterousness. Peter was watching

[004-021] him from below his eyelids, as I have seen him watch a lion about to

[004-022] charge.

[004-023] He flung himself on a chair, put his elbows on the table, and thrust

[004-024] his face forward.

[004-025] “You have come from a damned muddled show. If I had [[manie-maritz|Maritz]] in my power

[004-026] I would have him flogged at a wagon’s end. Fools and pig-dogs, they had

[004-027] the game in their hands and they flung it away. We could have raised a

[004-028] fire that would have burned the English into the sea, and for lack of

[004-029] fuel they let it die down. Then they try to fan it when the ashes are

[004-030] cold.”

[004-031] He rolled a paper pellet and flicked it into the air. “That is what I

[004-032] think of your idiot general,” he said, “and of all you Dutch. As slow

[004-033] as a fat vrouw and as greedy as an aasvogel.”

[004-034] We looked very glum and sullen.

[004-035] “A pair of dumb dogs,” he cried. “A thousand Brandenburgers would have

[004-036] won in a fortnight. Seitz hadn’t much to boast of, mostly clerks and

[004-037] farmers and half-castes, and no soldier worth the name to lead them,

[004-038] but it took [[louis-botha|Botha]] and Smuts and a dozen generals to hunt him down. But

[004-039] Maritz!” His scorn came like a gust of wind.

[004-040] “Maritz did all the fighting there was,” said Peter sulkily. “At any

[004-041] rate he wasn’t afraid of the sight of the khaki like your lot.”

[004-042] “Maybe he wasn’t,” said the giant in a cooing voice; “maybe he had his

[004-043] reasons for that. You Dutchmen have always a feather-bed to fall on.

[004-044] You can always turn traitor. Maritz now calls himself Robinson, and has

[004-045] a pension from his friend Botha.”

[004-046] “That,” said Peter, “is a very damned lie.”

[004-047] “I asked for information,” said Stumm with a sudden politeness. “But

[004-048] that is all past and done with. Maritz matters no more than your old

[004-049] Cronjes and Krugers. The show is over, and you are looking for safety.

[004-050] For a new master perhaps? But, man, what can you bring? What can you

[004-051] offer? You and your Dutch are lying in the dust with the yoke on your

[004-052] necks. The Pretoria lawyers have talked you round. You see that map,”

[004-053] and he pointed to a big one on the wall. “South Africa is coloured

[004-054] green. Not red for the English, or yellow for the Germans. Some day it

[004-055] will be yellow, but for a little it will be green—the colour of

[004-056] neutrals, of nothings, of boys and young ladies and chicken-hearts.”

[004-057] I kept wondering what he was playing at.

[004-058] Then he fixed his eyes on Peter. “What do you come here for? The game’s

[004-059] up in your own country. What can you offer us Germans? If we gave you

[004-060] ten million marks and sent you back you could do nothing. Stir up a

[004-061] village row, perhaps, and shoot a policeman. South Africa is counted

[004-062] out in this war. Botha is a cleverish man and has beaten you

[004-063] calves’-heads of rebels. Can you deny it?”

[004-064] Peter couldn’t. He was terribly honest in some things, and these were

[004-065] for certain his opinions.

[004-066] “No,” he said, “that is true, Baas.”

[004-067] “Then what in God’s name can you do?” shouted Stumm.

[004-068] Peter mumbled some foolishness about nobbling Angola for Germany and

[004-069] starting a revolution among the natives. Stumm flung up his arms and

[004-070] cursed, and the Under-Secretary laughed.

[004-071] It was high time for me to chip in. I was beginning to see the kind of

[004-072] fellow this Stumm was, and as he talked I thought of my mission, which

[004-073] had got overlaid by my Boer past. It looked as if he might be useful.

[004-074] “Let me speak,” I said. “My friend is a great hunter, but he fights

[004-075] better than he talks. He is no politician. You speak truth. South

[004-076] Africa is a closed door for the present, and the key to it is

[004-077] elsewhere. Here in Europe, and in the east, and in other parts of

[004-078] Africa. We have come to help you to find the key.”

[004-079] Stumm was listening. “Go on, my little Boer. It will be a new thing to

[004-080] hear a _taakhaar_ on world-politics.”

[004-081] “You are fighting,” I said, “in East Africa; and soon you may fight in

[004-082] Egypt. All the east coast north of the Zambesi will be your

[004-083] battle-ground. The English run about the world with little expeditions.

[004-084] I do not know where the places are, though I read of them in the

[004-085] papers. But I know my Africa. You want to beat them here in Europe and

[004-086] on the seas. Therefore, like wise generals, you try to divide them and

[004-087] have them scattered throughout the globe while you stick at home. That

[004-088] is your plan?”

[004-089] “A second Falkenhayn,” said Stumm, laughing.

[004-090] “Well, England will not let East Africa go. She fears for Egypt and she

[004-091] fears, too, for India. If you press her there she will send armies and

[004-092] more armies till she is so weak in Europe that a child can crush her.

[004-093] That is England’s way. She cares more for her Empire than for what may

[004-094] happen to her allies. So I say press and still press there, destroy the

[004-095] railway to the Lakes, burn her capital, pen up every Englishman in

[004-096] Mombasa island. At this moment it is worth for you a thousand

[004-097] Damaralands.”

[004-098] The man was really interested and the Under-Secretary, too, pricked up

[004-099] his ears.

[004-100] “We can keep our territory,” said the former; “but as for pressing, how

[004-101] the devil are we to press? The accursed English hold the sea. We cannot

[004-102] ship men or guns there. South are the Portuguese and west the Belgians.

[004-103] You cannot move a mass without a lever.”

[004-104] “The lever is there, ready for you,” I said.

[004-105] “Then for God’s sake show it me,” he cried.

[004-106] I looked at the door to see that it was shut, as if what I had to say

[004-107] was very secret.

[004-108] “You need men, and the men are waiting. They are black, but they are

[004-109] the stuff of warriors. All round your borders you have the remains of

[004-110] great fighting tribes, the Angoni, the Masai, the Manyumwezi, and above

[004-111] all the Somalis of the north, and the dwellers on the upper Nile. The

[004-112] British recruit their black regiments there, and so do you. But to get

[004-113] recruits is not enough. You must set whole nations moving, as the Zulu

[004-114] under Tchaka flowed over South Africa.”

[004-115] “It cannot be done,” said the Under-Secretary.

[004-116] “It can be done,” I said quietly. “We two are here to do it.”

[004-117] This kind of talk was jolly difficult for me, chiefly because of

[004-118] Stumm’s asides in German to the official. I had, above all things, to

[004-119] get the credit of knowing no German, and, if you understand a language

[004-120] well, it is not very easy when you are interrupted not to show that you

[004-121] know it, either by a direct answer, or by referring to the interruption

[004-122] in what you say next. I had to be always on my guard, and yet it was up

[004-123] to me to be very persuasive and convince these fellows that I would be

[004-124] useful. Somehow or other I had to get into their confidence.

[004-125] “I have been for years up and down in Africa—Uganda and the Congo and

[004-126] the Upper Nile. I know the ways of the Kaffir as no Englishman does. We

[004-127] Afrikanders see into the black man’s heart, and though he may hate us

[004-128] he does our will. You Germans are like the English; you are too big

[004-129] folk to understand plain men. ‘Civilize,’ you cry. ‘Educate,’ say the

[004-130] English. The black man obeys and puts away his gods, but he worships

[004-131] them all the time in his soul. We must get his gods on our side, and

[004-132] then he will move mountains. We must do as John Laputa did with Sheba’s

[004-133] necklace.”

[004-134] “That’s all in the air,” said Stumm, but he did not laugh.

[004-135] “It is sober common sense,” I said. “But you must begin at the right

[004-136] end. First find the race that fears its priests. It is waiting for

[004-137] you—the Mussulmans of Somaliland and the Abyssinian border and the Blue

[004-138] and White Nile. They would be like dried grasses to catch fire if you

[004-139] used the flint and steel of their religion. Look what the English

[004-140] suffered from a crazy Mullah who ruled only a dozen villages. Once get

[004-141] the flames going and they will lick up the pagans of the west and

[004-142] south. This is the way of Africa. How many thousands, think you, were

[004-143] in the Mahdi’s army who never heard of the Prophet till they saw the

[004-144] black flags of the Emirs going into battle?”

[004-145] Stumm was smiling. He turned his face to the official and spoke with

[004-146] his hand over his mouth, but I caught his words. They were: “This is

[004-147] the man for [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]].” The other pursed his lips and looked a little

[004-148] scared.

[004-149] Stumm rang a bell and the lieutenant came in and clicked his heels. He

[004-150] nodded towards Peter. “Take this man away with you. We have done with

[004-151] him. The other fellow will follow presently.”

[004-152] Peter went out with a puzzled face and Stumm turned to me.

[004-153] “You are a dreamer, Brandt,” he said. “But I do not reject you on that

[004-154] account. Dreams sometimes come true, when an army follows the

[004-155] visionary. But who is going to kindle the flame?”

[004-156] “You,” I said.

[004-157] “What the devil do you mean?” he asked.

[004-158] “That is your part. You are the cleverest people in the world. You have

[004-159] already half the Mussulman lands in your power. It is for you to show

[004-160] us how to kindle a holy war, for clearly you have the secret of it.

[004-161] Never fear but we will carry out your order.”

[004-162] “We have no secret,” he said shortly, and glanced at the official, who

[004-163] stared out of the window.

[004-164] I dropped my jaw and looked the picture of disappointment. “I do not

[004-165] believe you,” I said slowly. “You play a game with me. I have not come

[004-166] six thousand miles to be made a fool of.”

[004-167] “Discipline, by God,” Stumm cried. “This is none of your ragged

[004-168] commandos.” In two strides he was above me and had lifted me out of my

[004-169] seat. His great hands clutched my shoulders, and his thumbs gouged my

[004-170] armpits. I felt as if I were in the grip of a big ape. Then very slowly

[004-171] he shook me so that my teeth seemed loosened and my head swam. He let

[004-172] me go and I dropped limply back in the chair.

[004-173] “Now, go! _Futsack!_ And remember that I am your master. I, Ulric von

[004-174] Stumm, who owns you as a Kaffir owns his mongrel. Germany may have some

[004-175] use for you, my friend, when you fear me as you never feared your God.”
