# Ch. 10: The Garden-House of Suliman the Red

[010-001] “Was that when you resolved to get on one of the river-boats?”

[010-002] “_Ja_, Cornelis. As soon as I heard of the boats I saw where my chance

[010-003] lay. But you might have knocked me over with a straw when I saw you

[010-004] come on shore. That was good fortune, my friend... I have been thinking

[010-005] much about the Germans, and I will tell you the truth. It is only

[010-006] boldness that can baffle them. They are a most diligent people. They

[010-007] will think of all likely difficulties, but not of all possible ones.

[010-008] They have not much imagination. They are like steam engines which must

[010-009] keep to prepared tracks. There they will hunt any man down, but let him

[010-010] trek for open country and they will be at a loss. Therefore boldness,

[010-011] my friend; for ever boldness. Remember as a nation they wear

[010-012] spectacles, which means that they are always peering.”

[010-013] [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] broke off to gloat over the wedges of geese and the strings of

[010-014] wild swans that were always winging across those plains. His tale had

[010-015] bucked me up wonderfully. Our [[fate-and-providence|luck]] had held beyond all belief, and I

[010-016] had a kind of hope in the business now which had been wanting before.

[010-017] That afternoon, too, I got another fillip. I came on deck for a breath

[010-018] of air and found it pretty cold after the heat of the engine-room. So I

[010-019] called to one of the deck hands to fetch me up my cloak from the

[010-020] cabin—the same I had bought that first morning in the Greif village.

[010-021] “_Der grune Mantel_?” the man shouted up, and I cried, “Yes”. But the

[010-022] words seemed to echo in my ears, and long after he had given me the

[010-023] garment I stood staring abstractedly over the bulwarks.

[010-024] His tone had awakened a chord of memory, or, to be accurate, they had

[010-025] given emphasis to what before had been only blurred and vague. For he

[010-026] had spoken the words which Stumm had uttered behind his hand to

[010-027] Gaudian. I had heard something like “Ühnmantl,” and could make nothing

[010-028] of it. Now I was as certain of those words as of my own existence. They

[010-029] had been “_Grune Mantel_”. _Grune Mantel_, whatever it might be, was

[010-030] the name which Stumm had not meant me to hear, which was some talisman

[010-031] for the task I had proposed, and which was connected in some way with

[010-032] the mysterious von Einem.

[010-033] This discovery put me in high fettle. I told myself that, considering

[010-034] the difficulties, I had managed to find out a wonderful amount in a

[010-035] very few days. It only shows what a man can do with the slenderest

[010-036] evidence if he keeps chewing and chewing on it ...

[010-037] Two mornings later we lay alongside the quays at Belgrade, and I took

[010-038] the opportunity of stretching my legs. Peter had come ashore for a

[010-039] smoke, and we wandered among the battered riverside streets, and looked

[010-040] at the broken arches of the great railway bridge which the Germans were

[010-041] working at like beavers. There was a big temporary pontoon affair to

[010-042] take the railway across, but I calculated that the main bridge would be

[010-043] ready inside a month. It was a clear, cold, blue day, and as one looked

[010-044] south one saw ridge after ridge of snowy hills. The upper streets of

[010-045] the city were still fairly whole, and there were shops open where food

[010-046] could be got. I remember hearing English spoken, and seeing some Red

[010-047] Cross nurses in the custody of Austrian soldiers coming from the

[010-048] railway station.

[010-049] It would have done me a lot of good to have had a word with them. I

[010-050] thought of the gallant people whose capital this had been, how three

[010-051] times they had flung the Austrians back over the [[danube-river|Danube]], and then had

[010-052] only been beaten by the black treachery of their so-called allies.

[010-053] Somehow that morning in Belgrade gave both Peter and me a new purpose

[010-054] in our task. It was our business to put a spoke in the wheel of this

[010-055] monstrous bloody Juggernaut that was crushing the life out of the

[010-056] little heroic nations.

[010-057] We were just getting ready to cast off when a distinguished party

[010-058] arrived at the quay. There were all kinds of uniforms—German, Austrian,

[010-059] and Bulgarian, and amid them one stout gentleman in a fur coat and a

[010-060] black felt hat. They watched the barges up-anchor, and before we began

[010-061] to jerk into line I could hear their conversation. The fur coat was

[010-062] talking English.

[010-063] “I reckon that’s pretty good noos, General,” it said; “if the English

[010-064] have run away from Gally-poly we can use these noo consignments for the

[010-065] bigger game. I guess it won’t be long before we see the British lion

[010-066] moving out of Egypt with sore paws.”

[010-067] They all laughed. “The privilege of that spectacle may soon be ours,”

[010-068] was the reply.

[010-069] I did not pay much attention to the talk; indeed I did not realize till

[010-070] weeks later that that was the first tidings of the great evacuation of

[010-071] [[cape-helles|Cape Helles]]. What rejoiced me was the sight of [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]], as bland as a

[010-072] barber among those swells. Here were two of the missionaries within

[010-073] reasonable distance of their goal.

[010-074] CHAPTER X.

[010-075] The Garden-House of Suliman the Red

[010-076] We reached [[rustchuk|Rustchuk]] on January 10th, but by no means landed on that

[010-077] day. Something had gone wrong with the unloading arrangements, or more

[010-078] likely with the railway behind them, and we were kept swinging all day

[010-079] well out in the turbid river. On the top of this [[schenk|Captain Schenk]] got an

[010-080] ague, and by that evening was a blue and shivering wreck. He had done

[010-081] me well, and I reckoned I would stand by him. So I got his ship’s

[010-082] papers, and the manifests of cargo, and undertook to see to the

[010-083] trans-shipment. It wasn’t the first time I had tackled that kind of

[010-084] business, and I hadn’t much to learn about steam cranes. I told him I

[010-085] was going on to Constantinople and would take Peter with me, and he was

[010-086] agreeable. He would have to wait at Rustchuk to get his return cargo,

[010-087] and could easily inspan a fresh engineer.

[010-088] I worked about the hardest twenty-four hours of my life getting the

[010-089] stuff ashore. The landing officer was a Bulgarian, quite a competent

[010-090] man if he could have made the railways give him the trucks he needed.

[010-091] There was a collection of hungry German transport officers always

[010-092] putting in their oars, and being infernally insolent to everybody. I

[010-093] took the high and mighty line with them; and, as I had the Bulgarian

[010-094] commandant on my side, after about two hours’ blasphemy got them

[010-095] quieted.

[010-096] But the big trouble came the next morning when I had got nearly all the

[010-097] stuff aboard the trucks.

[010-098] A young officer in what I took to be a Turkish uniform rode up with an

[010-099] aide-de-camp. I noticed the German guards saluting him, so I judged he

[010-100] was rather a swell. He came up to me and asked me very civilly in

[010-101] German for the way-bills. I gave him them and he looked carefully

[010-102] through them, marking certain items with a blue pencil. Then he coolly

[010-103] handed them to his aide-de-camp and spoke to him in Turkish.

[010-104] “Look here, I want these back,” I said. “I can’t do without them, and

[010-105] we’ve no time to waste.”

[010-106] “Presently,” he said, smiling, and went off.

[010-107] I said nothing, reflecting that the stuff was for the Turks and they

[010-108] naturally had to have some say in its handling. The loading was

[010-109] practically finished when my gentleman returned. He handed me a neatly

[010-110] typed new set of way-bills. One glance at them showed that some of the

[010-111] big items had been left out.

[010-112] “Here, this won’t do,” I cried. “Give me back the right set. This

[010-113] thing’s no good to me.”

[010-114] For answer he winked gently, smiled like a dusky seraph, and held out

[010-115] his hand. In it I saw a roll of money.

[010-116] “For yourself,” he said. “It is the usual custom.”

[010-117] It was the first time anyone had ever tried to bribe me, and it made me

[010-118] boil up like a geyser. I saw his game clearly enough. Turkey would pay

[010-119] for the lot to Germany: probably had already paid the bill: but she

[010-120] would pay double for the things not on the way-bills, and pay to this

[010-121] fellow and his friends. This struck me as rather steep even for

[010-122] Oriental methods of doing business.

[010-123] “Now look here, Sir,” I said, “I don’t stir from this place till I get

[010-124] the correct way-bills. If you won’t give me them, I will have every

[010-125] item out of the trucks and make a new list. But a correct list I have,

[010-126] or the stuff stays here till Doomsday.”

[010-127] He was a slim, foppish fellow, and he looked more puzzled than angry.

[010-128] “I offer you enough,” he said, again stretching out his hand.

[010-129] At that I fairly roared. “If you try to bribe me, you infernal little

[010-130] haberdasher, I’ll have you off that horse and chuck you in the river.”

[010-131] He no longer misunderstood me. He began to curse and threaten, but I

[010-132] cut him short.

[010-133] “Come along to the commandant, my boy,” I said, and I marched away,

[010-134] tearing up his typewritten sheets as I went and strewing them behind me

[010-135] like a paper chase.

[010-136] We had a fine old racket in the commandant’s office. I said it was my

[010-137] business, as representing the German Government, to see the stuff

[010-138] delivered to the consignee at Constantinople ship-shape and

[010-139] Bristol-fashion. I told him it wasn’t my habit to proceed with cooked

[010-140] documents. He couldn’t but agree with me, but there was that wrathful

[010-141] Oriental with his face as fixed as a Buddha.

[010-142] “I am sorry, [[rasta-bey|Rasta Bey]],” he said; “but this man is in the right.”

[010-143] “I have authority from [[committee-of-union-and-progress|the Committee]] to receive the stores,” he said

[010-144] sullenly.

[010-145] “Those are not my instructions,” was the answer. “They are consigned to

[010-146] the Artillery commandant at [[chataldja|Chataldja]], [[von-oesterzee|General von Oesterzee]].”

[010-147] The man shrugged his shoulders. “Very well. I will have a word to say

[010-148] to General von Oesterzee, and many to this fellow who flouts the

[010-149] Committee.” And he strode away like an impudent boy.

[010-150] The harassed commandant grinned. “You’ve offended his Lordship, and he

[010-151] is a bad enemy. All those damned Comitadjis are. You would be well

[010-152] advised not to go on to Constantinople.”

[010-153] “And have that blighter in the red hat loot the trucks on the road? No,

[010-154] thank you. I am going to see them safe at Chataldja, or whatever they

[010-155] call the artillery depot.”

[010-156] I said a good deal more, but that is an abbreviated translation of my

[010-157] remarks. My word for “blighter” was _trottel_, but I used some other

[010-158] expressions which would have ravished my young Turk friend to hear.

[010-159] Looking back, it seems pretty ridiculous to have made all this fuss

[010-160] about guns which were going to be used against my own people. But I

[010-161] didn’t see that at the time. My professional pride was up in arms, and

[010-162] I couldn’t bear to have a hand in a crooked deal.

[010-163] “Well, I advise you to go armed,” said the commandant. “You will have a

[010-164] guard for the trucks, of course, and I will pick you good men. They may

[010-165] hold you up all the same. I can’t help you once you are past the

[010-166] frontier, but I’ll send a wire to Oesterzee and he’ll make trouble if

[010-167] anything goes wrong. I still think you would have been wiser to humour

[010-168] Rasta Bey.”

[010-169] As I was leaving he gave me a telegram. “Here’s a wire for your Captain

[010-170] Schenk.” I slipped the envelope in my pocket and went out.

[010-171] Schenk was pretty sick, so I left a note for him. At one o’clock I got

[010-172] the train started, with a couple of German Landwehr in each truck and

[010-173] Peter and I in a horse-box. Presently I remembered Schenk’s telegram,

[010-174] which still reposed in my pocket. I took it out and opened it, meaning

[010-175] to wire it from the first station we stopped at. But I changed my mind

[010-176] when I read it. It was from some official at Regensburg, asking him to

[010-177] put under arrest and send back by the first boat a man called Brandt,

[010-178] who was believed to have come aboard at Absthafen on the 30th of

[010-179] December.
