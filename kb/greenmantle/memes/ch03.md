# Ch. 3: Peter Pienaar

[003-001] “We move gently eastward to Constantinople,” I said.

[003-002] [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] grinned. “We should cover a lot of new country. You can reckon on

[003-003] me, friend Cornelis. I’ve always had a hankering to see Europe.”

[003-004] He rose to his feet and stretched his long arms.

[003-005] “We’d better begin at once. God, I wonder what’s happened to old Solly

[003-006] [[manie-maritz|Maritz]], with his bottle face? Yon was a fine battle at the drift when I

[003-007] was sitting up to my neck in the Orange praying that Brits’ lads would

[003-008] take my head for a stone.”

[003-009] Peter was as thorough a mountebank, when he got started, as [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]

[003-010] himself. All the way back to [[lisbon|Lisbon]] he yarned about Maritz and his

[003-011] adventures in German South West till I half believed they were true. He

[003-012] made a very good story of our doings, and by his constant harping on it

[003-013] I pretty soon got it into my memory. That was always Peter’s way. He

[003-014] said if you were going to play a part, you must think yourself into it,

[003-015] convince yourself that you were it, till you really were it and didn’t

[003-016] act but behaved naturally. The two men who had started that morning

[003-017] from the hotel door had been bogus enough, but the two men that

[003-018] returned were genuine desperadoes itching to get a shot at England.

[003-019] We spent the evening piling up evidence in our favour. Some kind of

[003-020] republic had been started in Portugal, and ordinarily the cafes would

[003-021] have been full of politicians, but the war had quieted all these local

[003-022] squabbles, and the talk was of nothing but what was doing in France and

[003-023] [[russia|Russia]]. The place we went to was a big, well-lighted show on a main

[003-024] street, and there were a lot of sharp-eyed fellows wandering about that

[003-025] I guessed were spies and police agents. I knew that Britain was the one

[003-026] country that doesn’t bother about this kind of game, and that it would

[003-027] be safe enough to let ourselves go.

[003-028] I talked Portuguese fairly well, and Peter spoke it like a Lourenco

[003-029] Marques bar-keeper, with a lot of Shangaan words to fill up. He started

[003-030] on curacao, which I reckoned was a new drink to him, and presently his

[003-031] tongue ran freely. Several neighbours pricked up their ears, and soon

[003-032] we had a small crowd round our table.

[003-033] We talked to each other of Maritz and our doings. It didn’t seem to be

[003-034] a popular subject in that cafe. One big blue-black fellow said that

[003-035] Maritz was a dirty swine who would soon be hanged. Peter quickly caught

[003-036] his knife-wrist with one hand and his throat with the other, and

[003-037] demanded an apology. He got it. The Lisbon _boulevardiers_ have not

[003-038] lost any lions.

[003-039] After that there was a bit of a squash in our corner. Those near to us

[003-040] were very quiet and polite, but the outer fringe made remarks. When

[003-041] Peter said that if Portugal, which he admitted he loved, was going to

[003-042] stick to England she was backing the wrong horse, there was a murmur of

[003-043] disapproval. One decent-looking old fellow, who had the air of a ship’s

[003-044] captain, flushed all over his honest face, and stood up looking

[003-045] straight at Peter. I saw that we had struck an Englishman, and

[003-046] mentioned it to Peter in Dutch.

[003-047] Peter played his part perfectly. He suddenly shut up, and, with furtive

[003-048] looks around him, began to jabber to me in a low voice. He was the very

[003-049] picture of the old stage conspirator.

[003-050] The old fellow stood staring at us. “I don’t very well understand this

[003-051] damned lingo,” he said; “but if so be you dirty Dutchmen are sayin’

[003-052] anything against England, I’ll ask you to repeat it. And if so be as

[003-053] you repeats it I’ll take either of you on and knock the face off him.”

[003-054] He was a chap after my own heart, but I had to keep the game up. I said

[003-055] in Dutch to Peter that we mustn’t get brawling in a public house.

[003-056] “Remember the big thing,” I said darkly. Peter nodded, and the old

[003-057] fellow, after staring at us for a bit, spat scornfully, and walked out.

[003-058] “The time is coming when the Englander will sing small,” I observed to

[003-059] the crowd. We stood drinks to one or two, and then swaggered into the

[003-060] street. At the door a hand touched my arm, and, looking down, I saw a

[003-061] little scrap of a man in a fur coat.

[003-062] “Will the gentlemen walk a step with me and drink a glass of beer?” he

[003-063] said in very stiff Dutch.

[003-064] “Who the devil are you?” I asked.

[003-065] “_Gott strafe England!_” was his answer, and, turning back the lapel of

[003-066] his coat, he showed some kind of ribbon in his buttonhole.

[003-067] “Amen,” said Peter. “Lead on, friend. We don’t mind if we do.”

[003-068] He led us to a back street and then up two pairs of stairs to a very

[003-069] snug little flat. The place was filled with fine red lacquer, and I

[003-070] guessed that art-dealing was his nominal business. Portugal, since the

[003-071] republic broke up the convents and sold up the big royalist grandees,

[003-072] was full of bargains in the lacquer and curio line.

[003-073] He filled us two long tankards of very good Munich beer.

[003-074] “_Prosit_,” he said, raising his glass. “You are from South Africa.

[003-075] What make you in Europe?”

[003-076] We both looked sullen and secretive.

[003-077] “That’s our own business,” I answered. “You don’t expect to buy our

[003-078] confidence with a glass of beer.”

[003-079] “So?” he said. “Then I will put it differently. From your speech in the

[003-080] cafe I judge you do not love the English.”

[003-081] Peter said something about stamping on their grandmothers, a Kaffir

[003-082] phrase which sounded gruesome in Dutch.

[003-083] The man laughed. “That is all I want to know. You are on the German

[003-084] side?”

[003-085] “That remains to be seen,” I said. “If they treat me fair I’ll fight

[003-086] for them, or for anybody else that makes war on England. England has

[003-087] stolen my country and corrupted my people and made me an exile. We

[003-088] Afrikanders do not forget. We may be slow but we win in the end. We two

[003-089] are men worth a great price. Germany fights England in East Africa. We

[003-090] know the natives as no Englishmen can ever know them. They are too soft

[003-091] and easy and the Kaffirs laugh at them. But we can handle the blacks so

[003-092] that they will fight like devils for fear of us. What is the reward,

[003-093] little man, for our services? I will tell you. There will be no reward.

[003-094] We ask none. We fight for hate of England.”

[003-095] Peter grunted a deep approval.

[003-096] “That is good talk,” said our entertainer, and his close-set eyes

[003-097] flashed. “There is room in Germany for such men as you. Where are you

[003-098] going now, I beg to know.”

[003-099] “To Holland,” I said. “Then maybe we will go to Germany. We are tired

[003-100] with travel and may rest a bit. This war will last long and our chance

[003-101] will come.”

[003-102] “But you may miss your market,” he said significantly. “A ship sails

[003-103] tomorrow for Rotterdam. If you take my advice, you will go with her.”

[003-104] This was what I wanted, for if we stayed in Lisbon some real soldier of

[003-105] Maritz might drop in any day and blow the gaff.

[003-106] “I recommend you to sail in the _Machado_,” he repeated. “There is work

[003-107] for you in Germany—oh yes, much work; but if you delay the chance may

[003-108] pass. I will arrange your journey. It is my business to help the allies

[003-109] of my fatherland.”

[003-110] He wrote down our names and an epitome of our doings contributed by

[003-111] Peter, who required two mugs of beer to help him through. He was a

[003-112] Bavarian, it seemed, and we drank to the health of Prince Rupprecht,

[003-113] the same blighter I was trying to do in at [[loos|Loos]]. That was an irony

[003-114] which Peter unfortunately could not appreciate. If he could he would

[003-115] have enjoyed it.

[003-116] The little chap saw us back to our hotel, and was with us the next

[003-117] morning after breakfast, bringing the steamer tickets. We got on board

[003-118] about two in the afternoon, but on my advice he did not see us off. I

[003-119] told him that, being British subjects and rebels at that, we did not

[003-120] want to run any risks on board, assuming a British cruiser caught us up

[003-121] and searched us. But Peter took twenty pounds off him for travelling

[003-122] expenses, it being his rule never to miss an opportunity of spoiling

[003-123] the Egyptians.

[003-124] As we were dropping down the Tagus we passed the old _Henry the

[003-125] Navigator_.

[003-126] “I met Sloggett in the street this morning,” said Peter, “and he told

[003-127] me a little German man had been off in a boat at daybreak looking up

[003-128] the passenger list. Yon was a right notion of yours, Cornelis. I am

[003-129] glad we are going among Germans. They are careful people whom it is a

[003-130] pleasure to meet.”

[003-131] CHAPTER IV.

[003-132] Adventures of Two Dutchmen on the Loose

[003-133] The Germans, as Peter said, are a careful people. A man met us on the

[003-134] quay at Rotterdam. I was a bit afraid that something might have turned

[003-135] up in Lisbon to discredit us, and that our little friend might have

[003-136] warned his pals by telegram. But apparently all was serene.

[003-137] Peter and I had made our plans pretty carefully on the voyage. We had

[003-138] talked nothing but Dutch, and had kept up between ourselves the role of

[003-139] Maritz’s men, which Peter said was the only way to play a part well.

[003-140] Upon my soul, before we got to Holland I was not very clear in my own

[003-141] mind what my past had been. Indeed the danger was that the other side

[003-142] of my mind, which should be busy with the great problem, would get

[003-143] atrophied, and that I should soon be mentally on a par with the

[003-144] ordinary backveld desperado.

[003-145] We had agreed that it would be best to get into Germany at once, and

[003-146] when the agent on the quay told us of a train at midday we decided to

[003-147] take it.

[003-148] I had another fit of cold feet before we got over the frontier. At the

[003-149] station there was a King’s Messenger whom I had seen in France, and a

[003-150] war correspondent who had been trotting round our part of the front

[003-151] before Loos. I heard a woman speaking pretty clean-cut English, which

[003-152] amid the hoarse Dutch jabber sounded like a lark among crows. There

[003-153] were copies of the English papers for sale, and English cheap editions.

[003-154] I felt pretty bad about the whole business, and wondered if I should

[003-155] ever see these homely sights again.

[003-156] But the mood passed when the train started. It was a clear blowing day,

[003-157] and as we crawled through the flat pastures of Holland my time was

[003-158] taken up answering Peter’s questions. He had never been in Europe

[003-159] before, and formed a high opinion of the farming. He said he reckoned

[003-160] that such land would carry four sheep a morgen. We were thick in talk

[003-161] when we reached the frontier station and jolted over a canal bridge

[003-162] into Germany.

[003-163] I had expected a big barricade with barbed wire and entrenchments. But

[003-164] there was nothing to see on the German side but half a dozen sentries

[003-165] in the field-grey I had hunted at Loos. An under-officer, with the

[003-166] black-and-gold button of the Landsturm, hoicked us out of the train,

[003-167] and we were all shepherded into a big bare waiting-room where a large

[003-168] stove burned. They took us two at a time into an inner room for

[003-169] examination. I had explained to Peter all about this formality, but I

[003-170] was glad we went in together, for they made us strip to the skin, and I

[003-171] had to curse him pretty seriously to make him keep quiet. The men who

[003-172] did the job were fairly civil, but they were mighty thorough. They took

[003-173] down a list of all we had in our pockets and bags, and all the details

[003-174] from the passports the Rotterdam agent had given us.
