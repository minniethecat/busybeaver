# Louis Botha

Louis Botha (1862–1919) was a Boer general and the first Prime Minister of the Union of South Africa. In John Buchan's 1916 novel *Greenmantle*, he is referenced as the British-allied leader who crushed the Boer rebellion of 1914 and conquered German South West Africa — representing the opposite pole from the Maritz rebels whose identities Hannay and Pienaar assume.

## A Missed Assignment

Botha first enters the narrative through Hannay's frustration with British military bureaucracy. Despite his knowledge of Damaraland (German South West Africa), Hannay complains that "instead of being put on Botha's staff, as I applied to be, I was kept in Hampshire mud till the campaign in German South West Africa was over" [^1]. This early reference establishes Botha as the competent commander whom the British system fails to utilise properly — a theme that runs throughout Buchan's critique of officialdom.

## The Conqueror of German South West

Peter Pienaar's wandering journey through Africa is shaped by news of Botha's campaign. Hearing "that the fighting was over and that Botha had conquered the Germans" [^1] — a rumour that proves premature — Peter changes direction northward. The factual basis is accurate: Botha's forces did defeat the German colonial troops in South West Africa in July 1915, a campaign that paralleled the novel's composition.

## The Interrogation Exchange

Botha's name becomes a weapon in Colonel von Stumm's interrogation of Peter. Stumm sneers that "it took Botha and Smuts and a dozen generals to hunt him down" [^1] — referring to the German commander Seitz — and mocks that Maritz "has a pension from his friend Botha" [^1]. Peter's indignant denial ("That is a very damned lie") reveals his genuine loyalty to the British-aligned Botha, nearly compromising his cover as a Maritz rebel. Stumm drives the point home: "Botha is a cleverish man and has beaten you calves'-heads of rebels. Can you deny it?" [^1] — and Peter, "terribly honest in some things," cannot.

## Significance

Botha functions in the novel as the absent authority figure validating the British side of the Boer divide. His military competence contrasts with German arrogance, while his defeat of the Maritz rebellion provides the historical backdrop against which Hannay's cover story plays out. His presence in the narrative, though entirely off-stage, anchors the South African dimension of the plot in real history.
