## Overview

Captain Schenk is a Frisian deep-water sailor assigned to command a convoy of six river barges transporting German military stores down the Danube River. Richard Hannay serves under him as a crew member during the early stages of his journey toward Constantinople, using the cover identity of Cornelis Brandt.

## Background and Character

Schenk is a "Frisian and a first-class deep-water seaman" who, because "the German mercantile marine was laid on the ice till the end of war," was reassigned to river transport duty [008-007]. He is described as fundamentally "out of his bearings in the job" — skilled in ocean navigation but unfamiliar with the intricacies of river pilotage [008-007].

The river charts puzzle him, and "though it was pretty plain going for hundreds of miles, yet he was in a perpetual fidget about the pilotage" [008-007]. Hannay observes that Schenk "would have been far more in his element smelling his way through the shoals of the Ems mouth, or beating against a northeaster in the shallow Baltic" [008-007].

Despite his professional displacement, Schenk is depicted as "a good fellow and quite willing to take a hint" [008-025]. Hannay quickly establishes a working rapport with him, and within twenty-four hours Schenk is "telling me all his difficulties, and I was doing my best to cheer him" [008-031].

## The New Year's Eve Incident

Schenk's character is most fully revealed during the New Year's Eve passage. Though "in charge of valuable stores and was voyaging against time," Schenk insists that his crew must have "permission for some kind of beano" for _Sylvesterabend_ [008-037]. Hannay, anxious about passing the Austrian frontier where they might be searched, finds Schenk's commitment to the celebration "so seriously that I would have risked a row if I had tried to argue" [008-061].

This episode illustrates both Schenk's essential decency as a commander who cares for his men's morale, and the tension between German cultural traditions and wartime military necessity that runs through the novel's depiction of the German military.

## Crew Composition

The barge crew are described as "mostly Frisians, slow-spoken, sandy-haired lads, very like the breed you strike on the Essex coast" [008-019]. Schenk's nationality and the crew's regional background — Frisian rather than Prussian — is significant to the novel's nuanced portrayal of German identity.
