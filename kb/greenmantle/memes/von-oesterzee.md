## Overview

General von Oesterzee is a German artillery officer serving as commandant at Chataldja, the Ottoman Empire's defensive line west of Constantinople. He appears in the narrative when Richard Hannay and Peter Pienaar deliver German military stores to his command, providing a brief haven from the Turkish authorities who are pursuing them.

## Character

Von Oesterzee is described as "the regular gunner-officer, not thinking about anything except his guns and shells" [010-049]. Unlike many of the German officers in the novel who are caught up in political intrigue, von Oesterzee is depicted as a professional soldier focused solely on his technical duties. Hannay notes that "he was altogether very civil and" hospitable, giving Hannay and Pienaar luncheon after verifying the stores [010-049].

His reaction to Hannay's report about Rasta Bey's interference reveals his pragmatic nature: "It didn't make him as mad as I expected, because, you see, he got his stuff safe in any case" [010-053]. The political complications between the German military and their Ottoman allies are of secondary concern to his primary mission of maintaining his artillery command.

## Role in the Mission

Von Oesterzee's role in the narrative is that of an unwitting facilitator. By accepting Hannay's delivery of stores and providing him with a receipt and hospitality, he helps validate Hannay's cover identity and provides a momentary safe passage point. Hannay later reflects that "if von Oesterzee had wanted me he wouldn't have taken the trouble to stalk me" [010-095], realizing that the pursuit comes from Rasta Bey and Turkish agents rather than the German military command.

The receipt von Oesterzee provides becomes an important document that Hannay retains, serving as proof of his legitimate dealings with the German military establishment.
