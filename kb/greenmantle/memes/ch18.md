# Ch. 18: Sparrows on the Housetops

[018-001] But my main business that afternoon was pure nonsense. I was trying to

[018-002] find my little hill. At every turn of the road I expected to see the

[018-003] _castrol_ before us. You must know that ever since I could stand I have

[018-004] been crazy about high mountains. My father took me to Basutoland when I

[018-005] was a boy, and I reckon I have scrambled over almost every bit of

[018-006] upland south of the Zambesi, from the Hottentots Holland to the

[018-007] Zoutpansberg, and from the ugly yellow kopjes of Damaraland to the

[018-008] noble cliffs of Mont aux Sources. One of the things I had looked

[018-009] forward to in coming home was the chance of climbing the Alps. But now

[018-010] I was among peaks that I fancied were bigger than the Alps, and I could

[018-011] hardly keep my eyes on the road. I was pretty certain that my _castrol_

[018-012] was among them, for that dream had taken an almighty hold on my mind.

[018-013] Funnily enough, I was ceasing to think it a place of evil omen, for one

[018-014] soon forgets the atmosphere of nightmare. But I was convinced that it

[018-015] was a thing I was destined to see, and to see pretty soon.

[018-016] Darkness fell when we were some miles short of the city, and the last

[018-017] part was difficult driving. On both sides of the road transport and

[018-018] engineers’ stores were parked, and some of it strayed into the highway.

[018-019] I noticed lots of small details—machine-gun detachments, signalling

[018-020] parties, squads of stretcher-bearers—which mean the fringe of an army,

[018-021] and as soon as the night began the white fingers of searchlights began

[018-022] to grope in the skies.

[018-023] And then, above the hum of the roadside, rose the voice of the great

[018-024] guns. The shells were bursting four or five miles away, and the guns

[018-025] must have been as many more distant. But in that upland pocket of plain

[018-026] in the frosty night they sounded most intimately near. They kept up

[018-027] their solemn litany, with a minute’s interval between each—no _rafale_

[018-028] which rumbles like a drum, but the steady persistence of artillery

[018-029] exactly ranged on a target. I judged they must be bombarding the outer

[018-030] forts, and once there came a loud explosion and a red glare as if a

[018-031] magazine had suffered.

[018-032] It was a sound I had not heard for five months, and it fairly crazed

[018-033] me. I remembered how I had first heard it on the ridge before Laventie.

[018-034] Then I had been half-afraid, half-solemnized, but every [[courage-and-honour|nerve]] had been

[018-035] quickened. Then it had been the new thing in my life that held me

[018-036] breathless with anticipation; now it was the old thing, the thing I had

[018-037] shared with so many good fellows, my proper work, and the only task for

[018-038] a man. At the sound of the guns I felt that I was moving in natural air

[018-039] once more. I felt that I was coming home.

[018-040] We were stopped at a long line of ramparts, and a German sergeant

[018-041] stared at us till he saw the lieutenant beside me, when he saluted and

[018-042] we passed on. Almost at once we dipped into narrow twisting streets,

[018-043] choked with soldiers, where it was hard business to steer. There were

[018-044] few lights—only now and then the flare of a torch which showed the grey

[018-045] stone houses, with every window latticed and shuttered. I had put out

[018-046] my headlights and had only side lamps, so we had to pick our way

[018-047] gingerly through the labyrinth. I hoped we would strike [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]’s

[018-048] quarters soon, for we were all pretty empty, and a frost had set in

[018-049] which made our thick coats seem as thin as paper.

[018-050] The lieutenant did the guiding. We had to present our passports, and I

[018-051] anticipated no more difficulty than in landing from the boat at

[018-052] Boulogne. But I wanted to get it over, for my hunger pinched me and it

[018-053] was fearsome cold. Still the guns went on, like hounds baying before a

[018-054] quarry. The city was out of range, but there were strange lights on the

[018-055] ridge to the east.

[018-056] At last we reached our goal and marched through a fine old carved

[018-057] archway into a courtyard, and thence into a draughty hall.

[018-058] “You must see the _Sektionschef_,” said our guide. I looked round to

[018-059] see if we were all there, and noticed that Hussin had disappeared. It

[018-060] did not matter, for he was not on the passports.

[018-061] We followed as we were directed through an open door. There was a man

[018-062] standing with his back towards us looking at a wall map, a very big man

[018-063] with a neck that bulged over his collar. I would have known that neck

[018-064] among a million. At the sight of it I made a half-turn to bolt back. It

[018-065] was too late, for the door had closed behind us and there were two

[018-066] armed sentries beside it.

[018-067] The man slewed round and looked into my eyes. I had a despairing hope

[018-068] that I might bluff it out, for I was in different clothes and had

[018-069] shaved my beard. But you cannot spend ten minutes in a death-grapple

[018-070] without your adversary getting to know you.

[018-071] He went very pale, then recollected himself and twisted his features

[018-072] into the old grin.

[018-073] “So,” he said, “the little Dutchmen! We meet after many days.”

[018-074] It was no good lying or saying anything. I shut my teeth and waited.

[018-075] “And you, Herr [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]? I never liked the look of you. You babbled

[018-076] too much, like all your damned Americans.”

[018-077] “I guess your personal dislikes haven’t got anything to do with the

[018-078] matter,” said Blenkiron, calmly. “If you’re the boss here, I’ll thank

[018-079] you to cast your eye over these passports, for we can’t stand waiting

[018-080] for ever.”

[018-081] This fairly angered him. “I’ll teach you manners,” he cried, and took a

[018-082] step forward to reach for Blenkiron’s shoulder—the game he had twice

[018-083] played with me.

[018-084] Blenkiron never took his hands from his coat pockets. “Keep your

[018-085] distance,” he drawled in a new voice. “I’ve got you covered, and I’ll

[018-086] make a hole in your bullet head if you lay a hand on me.”

[018-087] With an effort Stumm recovered himself. He rang a bell and fell to

[018-088] smiling. An orderly appeared to whom he spoke in Turkish, and presently

[018-089] a file of soldiers entered the room.

[018-090] “I’m going to have you disarmed, gentlemen,” he said. “We can conduct

[018-091] our conversation more pleasantly without pistols.”

[018-092] It was idle to resist. We surrendered our arms, [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] almost in tears

[018-093] with vexation. Stumm swung his legs over a chair, rested his chin on

[018-094] the back and looked at me.

[018-095] “Your game is up, you know,” he said. “These fools of Turkish police

[018-096] said the Dutchmen were dead, but I had the happier inspiration. I

[018-097] believed the good God had spared them for me. When I got [[rasta-bey|Rasta]]’s

[018-098] telegram I was certain, for your doings reminded me of a little trick

[018-099] you once played me on the [[schwandom|Schwandorf]] road. But I didn’t think to find

[018-100] this plump old partridge,” and he smiled at Blenkiron. “Two eminent

[018-101] American engineers and their servant bound for [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]] on business

[018-102] of high Government importance! It was a good lie; but if I had been in

[018-103] Constantinople it would have had a short life. Rasta and his friends

[018-104] are no concern of mine. You can trick them as you please. But you have

[018-105] attempted to win the confidence of a certain lady, and her interests

[018-106] are mine. Likewise you have offended me, and I do not forgive. By God,”

[018-107] he cried, his voice growing shrill with passion, “by the time I have

[018-108] done with you your mothers in their graves will weep that they ever

[018-109] bore you!”

[018-110] It was Blenkiron who spoke. His voice was as level as the chairman’s of

[018-111] a bogus company, and it fell on that turbid atmosphere like acid on

[018-112] grease.

[018-113] “I don’t take no stock in high-falutin’. If you’re trying to scare me

[018-114] by that dime-novel talk I guess you’ve hit the wrong man. You’re like

[018-115] the sweep that stuck in the chimney, a bit too big for your job. I

[018-116] reckon you’ve a talent for romance that’s just wasted in soldiering.

[018-117] But if you’re going to play any ugly games on me I’d like you to know

[018-118] that I’m an American citizen, and pretty well considered in my own

[018-119] country and in yours, and you’ll sweat blood for it later. That’s a

[018-120] fair warning, Colonel Stumm.”

[018-121] I don’t know what Stumm’s plans were, but that speech of Blenkiron’s

[018-122] put into his mind just the needed amount of uncertainty. You see, he

[018-123] had Peter and me right enough, but he hadn’t properly connected

[018-124] Blenkiron with us, and was afraid either to hit out at all three, or to

[018-125] let Blenkiron go. It was lucky for us that the American had cut such a

[018-126] dash in the Fatherland.

[018-127] “There is no hurry,” he said blandly. “We shall have long happy hours

[018-128] together. I’m going to take you all home with me, for I am a hospitable

[018-129] soul. You will be safer with me than in the town gaol, for it’s a

[018-130] trifle draughty. It lets things in, and it might let things out.”

[018-131] Again he gave an order, and we were marched out, each with a soldier at

[018-132] his elbow. The three of us were bundled into the back seat of the car,

[018-133] while two men sat before us with their rifles between their knees, one

[018-134] got up behind on the baggage rack, and one sat beside Stumm’s

[018-135] chauffeur. Packed like sardines we moved into the bleak streets, above

[018-136] which the stars twinkled in ribbons of sky.

[018-137] Hussin had disappeared from the face of the earth, and quite right too.

[018-138] He was a good fellow, but he had no call to mix himself up in our

[018-139] troubles.

[018-142] “I’ve often regretted,” said Blenkiron, “that miracles have left off

[018-143] happening.”

[018-144] He got no answer, for I was feeling the walls for something in the

[018-145] nature of a window.

[018-146] “For I reckon,” he went on, “that it wants a good old-fashioned

[018-147] copper-bottomed miracle to get us out of this fix. It’s plumb against

[018-148] all my principles. I’ve spent my life using the talents God gave me to

[018-149] keep things from getting to the point of rude violence, and so far I’ve

[018-150] succeeded. But now you come along, Major, and you hustle a respectable

[018-151] middle-aged citizen into an aboriginal mix-up. It’s mighty indelicate.

[018-152] I reckon the next move is up to you, for I’m no good at the

[018-153] housebreaking stunt.”

[018-154] “No more am I,” I answered; “but I’m hanged if I’ll chuck up the

[018-155] sponge. Sandy’s somewhere outside, and he’s got a hefty crowd at his

[018-156] heels.”

[018-157] I simply could not feel the despair which by every law of common sense

[018-158] was due to the case. The guns had intoxicated me. I could still hear

[018-159] their deep voices, though yards of wood and stone separated us from the

[018-160] upper air.

[018-161] What vexed us most was our hunger. Barring a few mouthfuls on the road

[018-162] we had eaten nothing since the morning, and as our diet for the past

[018-163] days had not been generous we had some leeway to make up. Stumm had

[018-164] never looked near us since we were shoved into the car. We had been

[018-165] brought to some kind of house and bundled into a place like a

[018-166] wine-cellar. It was pitch dark, and after feeling round the walls,

[018-167] first on my feet and then on Peter’s back, I decided that there were no

[018-168] windows. It must have been lit and ventilated by some lattice in the

[018-169] ceiling. There was not a stick of furniture in the place: nothing but a

[018-170] damp earth floor and bare stone sides, The door was a relic of the Iron

[018-171] Age, and I could hear the paces of a sentry outside it.

[018-172] When things get to the pass that nothing you can do can better them,

[018-173] the only thing is to live for the moment. All three of us sought in

[018-174] sleep a refuge from our empty stomachs. The floor was the poorest kind

[018-175] of bed, but we rolled up our coats for pillows and made the best of it.
