# Ch. 8: The Essen Barges

[008-001] When I was a boy I was fond of fairy tales, and most of the stories I

[008-002] remembered had been about great German forests and snow and charcoal

[008-003] burners and woodmen’s huts. Once I had longed to see these things, and

[008-004] now I was fairly in the thick of them. There had been wolves, too, and

[008-005] I wondered idly if I should fall in with a pack. I felt myself getting

[008-006] light-headed. I fell repeatedly and laughed sillily every time. Once I

[008-007] dropped into a hole and lay for some time at the bottom giggling. If

[008-008] anyone had found me then he would have taken me for a madman.

[008-009] The twilight of the forest grew dimmer, but I scarcely noticed it.

[008-010] Evening was falling, and soon it would be night, a night without

[008-011] morning for me. My body was going on without the direction of my brain,

[008-012] for my mind was filled with craziness. I was like a drunk man who keeps

[008-013] running, for he knows that if he stops he will fall, and I had a sort

[008-014] of bet with myself not to lie down—not at any rate just yet. If I lay

[008-015] down I should feel the pain in my head worse. Once I had ridden for

[008-016] five days down country with fever on me and the flat bush trees had

[008-017] seemed to melt into one big mirage and dance quadrilles before my eyes.

[008-018] But then I had more or less kept my wits. Now I was fairly daft, and

[008-019] every minute growing dafter.

[008-020] Then the trees seemed to stop and I was walking on flat ground. It was

[008-021] a clearing, and before me twinkled a little light. The change restored

[008-022] me to consciousness, and suddenly I felt with horrid intensity the fire

[008-023] in my head and bones and the weakness of my limbs. I longed to sleep,

[008-024] and I had a notion that a place to sleep was before me. I moved towards

[008-025] the light and presently saw through a screen of snow the outline of a

[008-026] cottage.

[008-027] I had no fear, only an intolerable longing to lie down. Very slowly I

[008-028] made my way to the door and knocked. My weakness was so great that I

[008-029] could hardly lift my hand.

[008-030] There were voices within, and a corner of the curtain was lifted from

[008-031] the window. Then the door opened and a woman stood before me, a woman

[008-032] with a thin, kindly face.

[008-033] “Gruss Gott,” she said, while children peeped from behind her skirts.

[008-034] “Gruss Gott,” I replied. I leaned against the door-post, and speech

[008-035] forsook me.

[008-036] She saw my condition. “Come in, sir,” she said. “You are sick and it is

[008-037] no weather for a sick man.”

[008-038] I stumbled after her and stood dripping in the centre of the little

[008-039] kitchen, while three wondering children stared at me. It was a poor

[008-040] place, scantily furnished, but a good log-fire burned on the hearth.

[008-041] The shock of warmth gave me one of those minutes of self-possession

[008-042] which comes sometimes in the middle of a fever.

[008-043] “I am sick, mother, and I have walked far in the storm and lost my way.

[008-044] I am from Africa, where the climate is hot, and your cold brings me

[008-045] fever. It will pass in a day or two if you can give me a bed.”

[008-046] “You are welcome,” she said; “but first I will make you coffee.”

[008-047] I took off my dripping cloak, and crouched close to the hearth. She

[008-048] gave me coffee—poor washy stuff, but blessedly hot. Poverty was spelled

[008-049] large in everything I saw. I felt the tides of fever beginning to

[008-050] overflow my brain again, and I made a great attempt to set my affairs

[008-051] straight before I was overtaken. With difficulty I took out Stumm’s

[008-052] pass from my pocket-book.

[008-053] “That is my warrant,” I said. “I am a member of the Imperial Secret

[008-054] Service and for the sake of my work I must move in the dark. If you

[008-055] will permit it, mother, I will sleep till I am better, but no one must

[008-056] know that I am here. If anyone comes, you must deny my presence.”

[008-057] She looked at the big seal as if it were a talisman.

[008-058] “Yes, yes,” she said, “you will have the bed in the garret and be left

[008-059] in peace till you are well. We have no neighbours near, and the storm

[008-060] will shut the roads. I will be silent, I and the little ones.”

[008-061] My head was beginning to swim, but I made one more effort.

[008-062] “There is food in my rucksack—biscuits and ham and chocolate. Pray take

[008-063] it for your use. And here is some money to buy Christmas fare for the

[008-064] little ones.” And I gave her some of the German notes.

[008-065] After that my recollection becomes dim. She helped me up a ladder to

[008-066] the garret, undressed me, and gave me a thick coarse nightgown. I seem

[008-067] to remember that she kissed my hand, and that she was crying. “The good

[008-068] Lord has sent you,” she said. “Now the little ones will have their

[008-069] prayers answered and the Christkind will not pass by our door.”

[008-070] CHAPTER VIII.

[008-071] The [[essen|Essen]] Barges

[008-072] I lay for four days like a log in that garret bed. The storm died down,

[008-073] the thaw set in, and the snow melted. The children played about the

[008-074] doors and told stories at night round the fire. Stumm’s myrmidons no

[008-075] doubt beset every road and troubled the lives of innocent wayfarers.

[008-076] But no one came near the cottage, and the fever worked itself out while

[008-077] I lay in peace.

[008-078] It was a bad bout, but on the fifth day it left me, and I lay, as weak

[008-079] as a kitten, staring at the rafters and the little skylight. It was a

[008-080] leaky, draughty old place, but the woman of the cottage had heaped

[008-081] deerskins and blankets on my bed and kept me warm. She came in now and

[008-082] then, and once she brought me a brew of some bitter herbs which greatly

[008-083] refreshed me. A little thin porridge was all the food I could eat, and

[008-084] some chocolate made from the slabs in my rucksack.

[008-085] I lay and dozed through the day, hearing the faint chatter of children

[008-086] below, and getting stronger hourly. Malaria passes as quickly as it

[008-087] comes and leaves a man little the worse, though this was one of the

[008-088] sharpest turns I ever had. As I lay I thought, and my thoughts followed

[008-089] curious lines. One queer thing was that Stumm and his doings seemed to

[008-090] have been shot back into a lumber-room of my brain and the door locked.

[008-091] He didn’t seem to be a creature of the living present, but a distant

[008-092] memory on which I could look calmly. I thought a good deal about my

[008-093] battalion and the comedy of my present position. You see I was getting

[008-094] better, for I called it comedy now, not tragedy.

[008-095] But chiefly I thought of my mission. All that wild day in the snow it

[008-096] had seemed the merest farce. The three words [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] [[sir-walter-bullivant|Bullivant]] had

[008-097] scribbled had danced through my head in a crazy fandango. They were

[008-098] present to me now, but coolly and sanely in all their meagreness.

[008-099] I remember that I took each one separately and chewed on it for hours.

[008-100] _Kasredin_—there was nothing to be got out of that. _Cancer_—there were

[008-101] too many meanings, all blind. _v. I._—that was the worst gibberish of

[008-102] all.

[008-103] Before this I had always taken the I as the letter of the alphabet. I

[008-104] had thought the v. must stand for von, and I had considered the German

[008-105] names beginning with I—Ingolstadt, Ingeburg, Ingenohl, and all the rest

[008-106] of them. I had made a list of about seventy at the British Museum

[008-107] before I left [[london|London]].

[008-108] Now I suddenly found myself taking the I as the numeral One. Idly, not

[008-109] thinking what I was doing, I put it into German.

[008-110] Then I nearly fell out of the bed. _Von Einem_—the name I had heard at

[008-111] Gaudian’s house, the name Stumm had spoken behind his hand, the name to

[008-112] which [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] was probably the prefix. It was a tremendous discovery—the

[008-113] first real bit of light I had found. Harry Bullivant knew that some man

[008-114] or woman called von Einem was at the heart of the mystery. Stumm had

[008-115] spoken of the same personage with respect and in connection with the

[008-116] work I proposed to do in raising the Moslem Africans. If I found von

[008-117] Einem I would be getting very warm. What was the word that Stumm had

[008-118] whispered to Gaudian and scared that worthy? It had sounded like

[008-119] _Ühnmantl_. If I could only get that clear, I would solve the riddle.

[008-120] I think that discovery completed my cure. At any rate on the evening of

[008-121] the fifth day—it was Wednesday, the 29th of December—I was well enough

[008-122] to get up. When the dark had fallen and it was too late to fear a

[008-123] visitor, I came downstairs and, wrapped in my green cape, took a seat

[008-124] by the fire.

[008-125] As we sat there in the firelight, with the three white-headed children

[008-126] staring at me with saucer eyes, and smiling when I looked their way,

[008-127] the woman talked. Her man had gone to the wars on the Eastern front,

[008-128] and the last she had heard from him he was in a Polish bog and longing

[008-129] for his dry native woodlands. The struggle meant little to her. It was

[008-130] an act of God, a thunderbolt out of the sky, which had taken a husband

[008-131] from her, and might soon make her a widow and her children fatherless.

[008-132] She knew nothing of its causes and purposes, and thought of the

[008-133] Russians as a gigantic nation of savages, heathens who had never been

[008-134] converted, and who would eat up German homes if the good Lord and the

[008-135] brave German soldiers did not stop them. I tried hard to find out if

[008-136] she had any notion of affairs in the West, but she hadn’t, beyond the

[008-137] fact that there was trouble with the French. I doubt if she knew of

[008-138] England’s share in it. She was a decent soul, with no bitterness

[008-139] against anybody, not even the Russians if they would spare her man.

[008-140] That night I realized the crazy folly of war. When I saw the splintered

[008-141] shell of [[ypres|Ypres]] and heard hideous tales of German doings, I used to want

[008-142] to see the whole land of the Boche given up to fire and sword. I

[008-143] thought we could never end the war properly without giving the Huns

[008-144] some of their own medicine. But that woodcutter’s cottage cured me of

[008-145] such nightmares. I was for punishing the guilty but letting the

[008-146] innocent go free. It was our business to thank God and keep our hands

[008-147] clean from the ugly blunders to which Germany’s madness had driven her.

[008-148] What good would it do Christian folk to burn poor little huts like this

[008-149] and leave children’s bodies by the wayside? To be able to laugh and to

[008-150] be merciful are the only things that make man better than the beasts.

[008-151] The place, as I have said, was desperately poor. The woman’s face had

[008-152] the skin stretched tight over the bones and that transparency which

[008-153] means under-feeding; I fancied she did not have the liberal allowance

[008-154] that soldiers’ wives get in England. The children looked better

[008-155] nourished, but it was by their mother’s sacrifice. I did my best to

[008-156] cheer them up. I told them long yarns about Africa and lions and

[008-157] tigers, and I got some pieces of wood and whittled them into toys. I am

[008-158] fairly good with a knife, and I carved very presentable likenesses of a

[008-159] monkey, a springbok, and a rhinoceros. The children went to bed hugging

[008-160] the first toys, I expect, they ever possessed.

[008-161] It was clear to me that I must leave as soon as possible. I had to get

[008-162] on with my business, and besides, it was not fair to the woman. Any

[008-163] moment I might be found here, and she would get into trouble for

[008-164] harbouring me. I asked her if she knew where the [[danube-river|Danube]] was, and her

[008-165] answer surprised me. “You will reach it in an hour’s walk,” she said.

[008-166] “The track through the wood runs straight to the ferry.”
