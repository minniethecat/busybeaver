## Overview

Chataldja (Çatalca) was the primary defensive line protecting Constantinople from the west during the First World War. Located approximately 50 kilometers west of the Ottoman capital, it served as a fortified artillery position and supply depot for the German military mission. In *Greenmantle*, Chataldja is where Richard Hannay and Peter Pienaar deliver German military stores to General von Oesterzee, and where Rasta Bey attempts to intercept them.

## Strategic Significance

The Chataldja lines were the last line of defense before Constantinople itself. The location's importance is reflected in the heavy German military presence there, with "the Artillery commandant at Chataldja, General von Oesterzee" serving as the senior officer responsible for receiving and distributing military stores [010-028].

The stores Hannay delivers are "consigned to the Artillery commandant at Chataldja," and the German officer at the Constantinople docks insists that von Oesterzee alone has authority to receive them: "Those are not my instructions. They are consigned to the Artillery commandant at Chataldja, General von Oesterzee" [010-028].

## The Chataldja Confrontation

Chataldja becomes the site of a critical confrontation. Rasta Bey pursues Hannay and Pienaar by train and intercepts them at Chataldja station: "It was Rasta, with half a dozen Turkish gendarmes" [010-019]. The Turk attempts to take custody of Hannay and the stores, declaring "I take over from you here. Hand me the papers" [010-019].

Hannay outmaneuvers Rasta, and von Oesterzee receives them warmly: "We got safely to Chataldja, and were received by von Oesterzee like long-lost brothers" [010-049]. The general's professional focus on "his guns and shells" rather than political intrigue provides a temporary safe haven.

## Aftermath

The Chataldja confrontation has lasting consequences. Rasta's failed attempt "to trace us from Chataldja" [011-079] escalates his pursuit, and the receipt von Oesterzee provides becomes important documentation for Hannay's cover. Hannay later worries "that the telegram had come to Chataldja" [010-095], fearing Rasta has used the telegraph to alert the authorities there.
