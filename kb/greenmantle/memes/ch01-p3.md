# Ch. 1: A Mission is Proposed (cont'd, part 3)

[001-001] I went back to my flat about five o’clock. My man Paddock had gone to

[001-002] the wars long ago, so I had shifted to one of the new blocks in Park

[001-003] Lane where they provide food and service. I kept the place on to have a

[001-004] home to go to when I got leave. It’s a miserable business holidaying in

[001-005] an hotel.

[001-006] [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] was devouring tea-cakes with the serious resolution of a

[001-007] convalescent.

[001-008] “Well, Dick, what’s the news? Is it a brass hat or the boot?”

[001-009] “Neither,” I said. “But you and I are going to disappear from His

[001-010] Majesty’s forces. Seconded for special service.”

[001-011] “O my sainted aunt!” said Sandy. “What is it? For Heaven’s sake put me

[001-012] out of pain. Have we to tout deputations of suspicious neutrals over

[001-013] munition works or take the shivering journalist in a motor-car where he

[001-014] can imagine he sees a Boche?”

[001-015] “The news will keep. But I can tell you this much. It’s about as safe

[001-016] and easy as to go through the German lines with a walking-stick.”

[001-017] “Come, that’s not so dusty,” said Sandy, and began cheerfully on the

[001-018] muffins.

[001-019] I must spare a moment to introduce Sandy to the reader, for he cannot

[001-020] be allowed to slip into this tale by a side-door. If you will consult

[001-021] the Peerage you will find that to Edward Cospatrick, fifteenth Baron

[001-022] Clanroyden, there was born in the year 1882, as his second son,

[001-023] Ludovick Gustavus Arbuthnot, commonly called the Honourable, etc. The

[001-024] said son was educated at Eton and New College, Oxford, was a captain in

[001-025] the Tweeddale Yeomanry, and served for some years as honorary attache

[001-026] at various embassies. The Peerage will stop short at this point, but

[001-027] that is by no means the end of the story. For the rest you must consult

[001-028] very different authorities. Lean brown men from the ends of the earth

[001-029] may be seen on the [[london|London]] pavements now and then in creased clothes,

[001-030] walking with the light outland step, slinking into clubs as if they

[001-031] could not remember whether or not they belonged to them. From them you

[001-032] may get news of Sandy. Better still, you will hear of him at little

[001-033] forgotten fishing ports where the Albanian mountains dip to the

[001-034] Adriatic. If you struck a Mecca pilgrimage the odds are you would meet

[001-035] a dozen of Sandy’s friends in it. In shepherds’ huts in the Caucasus

[001-036] you will find bits of his cast-off clothing, for he has a knack of

[001-037] shedding garments as he goes. In the caravanserais of Bokhara and

[001-038] Samarkand he is known, and there are shikaris in the Pamirs who still

[001-039] speak of him round their fires. If you were going to visit Petrograd or

[001-040] Rome or Cairo it would be no use asking him for introductions; if he

[001-041] gave them, they would lead you into strange haunts. But if Fate

[001-042] compelled you to go to Llasa or Yarkand or Seistan he could map out

[001-043] your road for you and pass the word to potent friends. We call

[001-044] ourselves insular, but the truth is that we are the only race on earth

[001-045] that can produce men capable of getting inside the skin of remote

[001-046] peoples. Perhaps the Scots are better than the English, but we’re all a

[001-047] thousand per cent better than anybody else. Sandy was the wandering

[001-048] Scot carried to the pitch of genius. In old days he would have led a

[001-049] crusade or discovered a new road to the Indies. Today he merely roamed

[001-050] as the spirit moved him, till the war swept him up and dumped him down

[001-051] in my battalion.

[001-052] I got out [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]]’s half-sheet of note-paper. It was not the

[001-053] original—naturally he wanted to keep that—but it was a careful tracing.

[001-054] I took it that [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] Bullivant had not written down the words as a memo

[001-055] for his own use. People who follow his career have good memories. He

[001-056] must have written them in order that, if he perished and his body was

[001-057] found, his friends might get a clue. Wherefore, I argued, the words

[001-058] must be intelligible to somebody or other of our persuasion, and

[001-059] likewise they must be pretty well gibberish to any Turk or German that

[001-060] found them.

[001-061] The first, “_Kasredin_”, I could make nothing of. I asked Sandy.

[001-062] “You mean Nasr-ed-din,” he said, still munching crumpets.

[001-063] “What’s that?” I asked sharply.

[001-064] “He’s the General believed to be commanding against us in [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]].

[001-065] I remember him years ago in Aleppo. He talked bad French and drank the

[001-066] sweetest of sweet champagne.”

[001-067] I looked closely at the paper. The “K” was unmistakable.

[001-068] “Kasredin is nothing. It means in Arabic the House of Faith, and might

[001-069] cover anything from Hagia Sofia to a suburban villa. What’s your next

[001-070] puzzle, Dick? Have you entered for a prize competition in a weekly

[001-071] paper?”

[001-072] “_Cancer,_” I read out.

[001-073] “It is the Latin for a crab. Likewise it is the name of a painful

[001-074] disease. It is also a sign of the Zodiac.”

[001-075] “_V. I_,” I read.

[001-076] “There you have me. It sounds like the number of a motor-car. The

[001-077] police would find out for you. I call this rather a difficult

[001-078] competition. What’s the prize?”

[001-079] I passed him the paper. “Who wrote it? It looks as if he had been in a

[001-080] hurry.”

[001-081] “Harry Bullivant,” I said.

[001-082] Sandy’s face grew solemn. “Old Harry. He was at my tutor’s. The best

[001-083] fellow God ever made. I saw his name in the casualty list before [[kut|Kut]].

[001-084] ... Harry didn’t do things without a purpose. What’s the story of this

[001-085] paper?”

[001-086] “Wait till after dinner,” I said. “I’m going to change and have a bath.

[001-087] There’s an American coming to dine, and he’s part of the business.”

[001-088] Mr [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] arrived punctual to the minute in a fur coat like a

[001-089] Russian prince’s. Now that I saw him on his feet I could judge him

[001-090] better. He had a fat face, but was not too plump in figure, and very

[001-091] muscular wrists showed below his shirt-cuffs. I fancied that, if the

[001-092] occasion called, he might be a good man with his hands.

[001-093] Sandy and I ate a hearty meal, but the American picked at his boiled

[001-094] fish and sipped his milk a drop at a time. When the servant had cleared

[001-095] away, he was as good as his word and laid himself out on my sofa. I

[001-096] offered him a good cigar, but he preferred one of his own lean black

[001-097] abominations. Sandy stretched his length in an easy chair and lit his

[001-098] pipe. “Now for your story, Dick,” he said.

[001-099] I began, as Sir Walter had begun with me, by telling them about the

[001-100] puzzle in the Near East. I pitched a pretty good yarn, for I had been

[001-101] thinking a lot about it, and the mystery of the business had caught my

[001-102] fancy. Sandy got very keen.

[001-103] “It is possible enough. Indeed, I’ve been expecting it, though I’m

[001-104] hanged if I can imagine what card the Germans have got up their sleeve.

[001-105] It might be any one of twenty things. Thirty years ago there was a

[001-106] bogus prophecy that played the devil in Yemen. Or it might be a flag

[001-107] such as Ali Wad Helu had, or a jewel like Solomon’s necklace in

[001-108] Abyssinia. You never know what will start off a [[holy-war|jehad]]! But I rather

[001-109] think it’s a man.”

[001-110] “Where could he get his purchase?” I asked.

[001-111] “It’s hard to say. If it were merely wild tribesmen like the Bedouin he

[001-112] might have got a reputation as a saint and miracle-worker. Or he might

[001-113] be a fellow that preached a pure religion, like the chap that founded

[001-114] the Senussi. But I’m inclined to think he must be something extra

[001-115] special if he can put a spell on the whole Moslem world. The Turk and

[001-116] the Persian wouldn’t follow the ordinary new theology game. He must be

[001-117] of the Blood. Your Mahdis and Mullahs and Imams were nobodies, but they

[001-118] had only a local prestige. To capture all Islam—and I gather that is

[001-119] what we fear—the man must be of the Koreish, the tribe of the Prophet

[001-120] himself.”

[001-121] “But how could any impostor prove that? For I suppose he’s an

[001-122] impostor.”

[001-123] “He would have to combine a lot of claims. His descent must be pretty

[001-124] good to begin with, and there are families, remember, that claim the

[001-125] Koreish blood. Then he’d have to be rather a wonder on his own

[001-126] account—saintly, eloquent, and that sort of thing. And I expect he’d

[001-127] have to show a sign, though what that could be I haven’t a notion.”

[001-128] “You know [[eastern-front|the East]] about as well as any living man. Do you think that

[001-129] kind of thing is possible?” I asked.

[001-130] “Perfectly,” said Sandy, with a grave face.

[001-131] “Well, there’s the ground cleared to begin with. Then there’s the

[001-132] evidence of pretty well every secret agent we possess. That all seems

[001-133] to prove the fact. But we have no details and no clues except that bit

[001-134] of paper.” I told them the story of it.

[001-135] Sandy studied it with wrinkled brows. “It beats me. But it may be the

[001-136] key for all that. A clue may be dumb in London and shout aloud at

[001-137] [[baghdad|Baghdad]].”

[001-138] “That’s just the point I was coming to. Sir Walter says this thing is

[001-139] about as important for our cause as big guns. He can’t give me orders,

[001-140] but he offers the job of going out to find what the mischief is. Once

[001-141] he knows that, he says he can checkmate it. But it’s got to be found

[001-142] out soon, for the mine may be sprung at any moment. I’ve taken on the

[001-143] job. Will you help?”

[001-144] Sandy was studying the ceiling.

[001-145] “I should add that it’s about as safe as playing chuck-farthing at the

[001-146] [[loos|Loos]] Cross-roads, the day you and I went in. And if we fail nobody can

[001-147] help us.”

[001-148] “Oh, of course, of course,” said Sandy in an abstracted voice.

[001-149] Mr Blenkiron, having finished his after-dinner recumbency, had sat up

[001-150] and pulled a small table towards him. From his pocket he had taken a

[001-151] pack of Patience cards and had begun to play the game called the Double

[001-152] Napoleon. He seemed to be oblivious of the conversation.

[001-153] Suddenly I had a feeling that the whole affair was stark lunacy. Here

[001-154] were we three simpletons sitting in a London flat and projecting a

[001-155] mission into the enemy’s citadel without an idea what we were to do or

[001-156] how we were to do it. And one of the three was looking at the ceiling,

[001-157] and whistling softly through his teeth, and another was playing

[001-158] Patience. The farce of the thing struck me so keenly that I laughed.

[001-159] Sandy looked at me sharply.

[001-160] “You feel like that? Same with me. It’s idiocy, but all war is idiotic,

[001-161] and the most whole-hearted idiot is apt to win. We’re to go on this mad

[001-162] trail wherever we think we can hit it. Well, I’m with you. But I don’t

[001-163] mind admitting that I’m in a blue funk. I had got myself adjusted to

[001-164] this trench business and was quite happy. And now you have hoicked me

[001-165] out, and my feet are cold.”

[001-166] “I don’t believe you know what fear is,” I said.

[001-167] “There you’re wrong, Dick,” he said earnestly. “Every man who isn’t a

[001-168] maniac knows fear. I have done some daft things, but I never started on

[001-169] them without wishing they were over. Once I’m in the show I get easier,

[001-170] and by the time I’m coming out I’m sorry to leave it. But at the start

[001-171] my feet are icy.”

[001-172] “Then I take it you’re coming?”

[001-173] “Rather,” he said. “You didn’t imagine I would go back on you?”

[001-174] “And you, sir?” I addressed Blenkiron.

[001-175] His game of Patience seemed to be coming out. He was completing eight

[001-176] little heaps of cards with a contented grunt. As I spoke, he raised his

[001-177] sleepy eyes and nodded.

[001-178] “Why, yes,” he said. “You gentlemen mustn’t think that I haven’t been

[001-179] following your most engrossing conversation. I guess I haven’t missed a

[001-180] syllable. I find that a game of Patience stimulates the digestion after

[001-181] meals and conduces to quiet reflection. John S. Blenkiron is with you

[001-182] all the time.”
