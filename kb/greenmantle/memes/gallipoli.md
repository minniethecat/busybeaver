# Gallipoli

The **Gallipoli Peninsula** in *Greenmantle* is the setting of the recent failed Allied campaign that colours the strategic outlook of 1916. Although the novel's action takes place after the Gallipoli campaign, its shadow hangs over every strategic calculation, serving as a cautionary example of Allied overreach and Ottoman resilience.

## Strategic Context

### The Recent Disaster

Published in 1916, *Greenmantle* is set in the immediate aftermath of the Gallipoli campaign (February 1915–January 1916). The campaign's failure is a fresh wound in the British military consciousness. Hannay reflects:

> I know what is going to happen at Gallipoli and Salonika. I gather that they are about through with us on the Peninsula. [P01-232]

### Ottoman Morale

The Gallipoli victory was a tremendous boost to Ottoman morale, and Enver Pasha is described as being particularly proud of it:

> Enver was great on Gallipoli. I remember he said that he could turn our flank any day if he wanted. [013-123]

## In the Novel

### German Exploitation

In Chapter 8, the German intelligence apparatus is shown using Gallipoli as a propaganda tool:

> ...it would give our poor chaps hell in Gallipoli. [008-073]

### Shell Shortage

Hilda von Einem reveals the extent of German material support to the Ottomans, including shells for the Gallipoli defences:

> ...shells to blow up the Gallipoli peninsula. All kinds of shells. [009-012]

### Troop Quality

The novel acknowledges the bravery of both sides at Gallipoli:

> ...dying without complaint in Gallipoli and Transcaucasia. They had the guts of the Turkish army there. [012-075]
