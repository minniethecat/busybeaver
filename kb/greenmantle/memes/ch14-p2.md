# Ch. 14: The Lady of the Mantilla (cont'd, part 2)

[014-001] “There is a house half-left in front of us,” said [[peter-pienaar|Peter]].

[014-002] I peered till my eyes ached and saw nothing.

[014-003] “Well, for heaven’s sake, guide me to it,” I said, and with Peter in

[014-004] front we set off down the hill.

[014-005] It was a wild journey, for darkness clung as close to us as a vest.

[014-006] Twice we stepped into patches of bog, and once my horse saved himself

[014-007] by a hair from going head forward into a gravel pit. We got tangled up

[014-008] in strands of wire, and often found ourselves rubbing our noses against

[014-009] tree trunks. Several times I had to get down and make a gap in

[014-010] barricades of loose stones. But after a ridiculous amount of slipping

[014-011] and stumbling we finally struck what seemed the level of a road, and a

[014-012] piece of special darkness in front which turned out to be a high wall.

[014-013] I argued that all mortal walls had doors, so we set to groping along

[014-014] it, and presently found a gap. There was an old iron gate on broken

[014-015] hinges, which we easily pushed open, and found ourselves on a back path

[014-016] to some house. It was clearly disused, for masses of rotting leaves

[014-017] covered it, and by the feel of it underfoot it was grass-grown.

[014-018] We dismounted now, leading our horses, and after about fifty yards the

[014-019] path ceased and came out on a well-made carriage drive. So, at least,

[014-020] we guessed, for the place was as black as pitch. Evidently the house

[014-021] couldn’t be far off, but in which direction I hadn’t a notion.

[014-022] Now, I didn’t want to be paying calls on any Turk at that time of day.

[014-023] Our job was to find where the road opened into the lane, for after that

[014-024] our way to Constantinople was clear. One side the lane lay, and the

[014-025] other the house, and it didn’t seem wise to take the risk of tramping

[014-026] up with horses to the front door. So I told Peter to wait for me at the

[014-027] end of the back-road, while I would prospect a bit. I turned to the

[014-028] right, my intention being if I saw the light of a house to return, and

[014-029] with Peter take the other direction.

[014-030] I walked like a blind man in that nether-pit of darkness. The road

[014-031] seemed well kept, and the soft wet gravel muffled the sounds of my

[014-032] feet. Great trees overhung it, and several times I wandered into

[014-033] dripping bushes. And then I stopped short in my tracks, for I heard the

[014-034] sound of whistling.

[014-035] It was quite close, about ten yards away. And the strange thing was

[014-036] that it was a tune I knew, about the last tune you would expect to hear

[014-037] in this part of the world. It was the Scots air: “Ca’ the yowes to the

[014-038] knowes,” which was a favourite of my father’s.

[014-039] The whistler must have felt my presence, for the air suddenly stopped

[014-040] in the middle of a bar. An unbounded curiosity seized me to know who

[014-041] the fellow could be. So I started in and finished it myself.

[014-042] There was silence for a second, and then the unknown began again and

[014-043] stopped. Once more I chipped in and finished it. Then it seemed to me

[014-044] that he was coming nearer. The air in that dank tunnel was very still,

[014-045] and I thought I heard a light foot. I think I took a step backward.

[014-046] Suddenly there was a flash of an electric torch from a yard off, so

[014-047] quick that I could see nothing of the man who held it.

[014-048] Then a low voice spoke out of the darkness—a voice I knew well—and,

[014-049] following it, a hand was laid on my arm. “What the devil are you doing

[014-050] here, Dick?” it said, and there was something like consternation in the

[014-051] tone.

[014-052] I told him in a hectic sentence, for I was beginning to feel badly

[014-053] rattled myself.

[014-054] “You’ve never been in greater danger in your life,” said the voice.

[014-055] “Great God, man, what brought you wandering here today of all days?”

[014-056] You can imagine that I was pretty scared, for [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] was the last man to

[014-057] put a case too high. And the next second I felt worse, for he clutched

[014-058] my arm and dragged me in a bound to the side of the road. I could see

[014-059] nothing, but I felt that his head was screwed round, and mine followed

[014-060] suit. And there, a dozen yards off, were the acetylene lights of a big

[014-061] motor-car.

[014-062] It came along very slowly, purring like a great cat, while we pressed

[014-063] into the bushes. The headlights seemed to spread a fan far to either

[014-064] side, showing the full width of the drive and its borders, and about

[014-065] half the height of the over-arching trees. There was a figure in

[014-066] uniform sitting beside the chauffeur, whom I saw dimly in the reflex

[014-067] glow, but the body of the car was dark.

[014-068] It crept towards us, passed, and my mind was just getting easy again

[014-069] when it stopped. A switch was snapped within, and the limousine was

[014-070] brightly lit up. Inside I saw a woman’s figure.

[014-071] The servant had got out and opened the door and a voice came from

[014-072] within—a clear soft voice speaking in some tongue I didn’t understand.

[014-073] Sandy had started forward at the sound of it, and I followed him. It

[014-074] would never do for me to be caught skulking in the bushes.

[014-075] I was so dazzled by the suddenness of the glare that at first I blinked

[014-076] and saw nothing. Then my eyes cleared and I found myself looking at the

[014-077] inside of a car upholstered in some soft dove-coloured fabric, and

[014-078] beautifully finished off in ivory and silver. The woman who sat in it

[014-079] had a mantilla of black lace over her head and shoulders, and with one

[014-080] slender jewelled hand she kept its fold over the greater part of her

[014-081] face. I saw only a pair of pale grey-blue eyes—these and the slim

[014-082] fingers.

[014-083] I remember that Sandy was standing very upright with his hands on his

[014-084] hips, by no means like a servant in the presence of his mistress. He

[014-085] was a fine figure of a man at all times, but in those wild clothes,

[014-086] with his head thrown back and his dark brows drawn below his skull-cap,

[014-087] he looked like some savage king out of an older world. He was speaking

[014-088] Turkish, and glancing at me now and then as if angry and perplexed. I

[014-089] took the hint that he was not supposed to know any other tongue, and

[014-090] that he was asking who the devil I might be.

[014-091] Then they both looked at me, Sandy with the slow unwinking stare of the

[014-092] gipsy, the lady with those curious, beautiful pale eyes. They ran over

[014-093] my clothes, my brand-new riding-breeches, my splashed boots, my

[014-094] wide-brimmed hat. I took off the last and made my best bow.

[014-095] “Madam,” I said, “I have to ask pardon for trespassing in your garden.

[014-096] The fact is, I and my servant—he’s down the road with the horses and I

[014-097] guess you noticed him—the two of us went for a ride this afternoon, and

[014-098] got good and well lost. We came in by your back gate, and I was

[014-099] prospecting for your front door to find someone to direct us, when I

[014-100] bumped into this brigand-chief who didn’t understand my talk. I’m

[014-101] American, and I’m here on a big Government proposition. I hate to

[014-102] trouble you, but if you’d send a man to show us how to strike the city

[014-103] I’d be very much in your debt.”

[014-104] Her eyes never left my face. “Will you come into the car?” she said in

[014-105] English. “At the house I will give you a servant to direct you.”

[014-106] She drew in the skirts of her fur cloak to make room for me, and in my

[014-107] muddy boots and sopping clothes I took the seat she pointed out. She

[014-108] said a word in Turkish to Sandy, switched off the light, and the car

[014-109] moved on.

[014-110] Women had never come much my way, and I knew about as much of their

[014-111] ways as I knew about the Chinese language. All my life I had lived with

[014-112] men only, and rather a rough crowd at that. When I made my pile and

[014-113] came home I looked to see a little society, but I had first the

[014-114] business of the Black Stone on my hands, and then the war, so my

[014-115] education languished. I had never been in a motor-car with a lady

[014-116] before, and I felt like a fish on a dry sandbank. The soft cushions and

[014-117] the subtle scents filled me with acute uneasiness. I wasn’t thinking

[014-118] now about Sandy’s grave words, or about [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]’s warning, or about

[014-119] my job and the part this woman must play in it. I was thinking only

[014-120] that I felt mortally shy. The darkness made it worse. I was sure that

[014-121] my companion was looking at me all the time and laughing at me for a

[014-122] clown.

[014-123] The car stopped and a tall servant opened the door. The lady was over

[014-124] the threshold before I was at the step. I followed her heavily, the wet

[014-125] squelching from my field-boots. At that moment I noticed that she was

[014-126] very tall.

[014-127] She led me through a long corridor to a room where two pillars held

[014-128] lamps in the shape of torches. The place was dark but for their glow,

[014-129] and it was as warm as a hothouse from invisible stoves. I felt soft

[014-130] carpets underfoot, and on the walls hung some tapestry or rug of an

[014-131] amazingly intricate geometrical pattern, but with every strand as rich

[014-132] as jewels. There, between the pillars, she turned and faced me. Her

[014-133] furs were thrown back, and the black mantilla had slipped down to her

[014-134] shoulders.

[014-135] “I have heard of you,” she said. “You are called Richard Hanau, the

[014-136] American. Why have you come to this land?”

[014-137] “To have a share in the campaign,” I said. “I’m an engineer, and I

[014-138] thought I could help out with some business like [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]].”

[014-139] “You are on Germany’s side?” she asked.

[014-140] “Why, yes,” I replied. “We Americans are supposed to be nootrals, and

[014-141] that means we’re free to choose any side we fancy. I’m for [[kaiser-wilhelm-ii|the Kaiser]].”

[014-142] Her cool eyes searched me, but not in suspicion. I could see she wasn’t

[014-143] troubling with the question whether I was speaking the truth. She was

[014-144] sizing me up as a man. I cannot describe that calm appraising look.

[014-145] There was no sex in it, nothing even of that implicit sympathy with

[014-146] which one human being explores the existence of another. I was a

[014-147] chattel, a thing infinitely removed from intimacy. Even so I have

[014-148] myself looked at a horse which I thought of buying, scanning his

[014-149] shoulders and hocks and paces. Even so must the old lords of

[014-150] Constantinople have looked at the slaves which the chances of war

[014-151] brought to their markets, assessing their usefulness for some task or

[014-152] other with no thought of a humanity common to purchased and purchaser.

[014-153] And yet—not quite. This woman’s eyes were weighing me, not for any

[014-154] special [[courage-and-honour|duty]], but for my essential qualities. I felt that I was under

[014-155] the scrutiny of one who was a connoisseur in human nature.

[014-156] I see I have written that I knew nothing about women. But every man has

[014-157] in his bones a consciousness of sex. I was shy and perturbed, but

[014-158] horribly fascinated. This slim woman, poised exquisitely like some

[014-159] statue between the pillared lights, with her fair cloud of hair, her

[014-160] long delicate face, and her pale bright eyes, had the glamour of a wild

[014-161] dream. I hated her instinctively, hated her intensely, but I longed to

[014-162] arouse her interest. To be valued coldly by those eyes was an offence

[014-163] to my manhood, and I felt antagonism rising within me. I am a strong

[014-164] fellow, well set up, and rather above the average height, and my

[014-165] irritation stiffened me from heel to crown. I flung my head back and

[014-166] gave her cool glance for cool glance, pride against pride.
