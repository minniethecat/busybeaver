# Ch. 1: A Mission is Proposed

[001-001] “You are an intelligent fellow, and you will ask how a Polish

[001-002] adventurer, meaning [[ennver-pasha|Enver]], and a collection of Jews and gipsies should

[001-003] have got control of a proud race. The ordinary man will tell you that

[001-004] it was German organization backed up with German money and German arms.

[001-005] You will inquire again how, since Turkey is primarily a religious

[001-006] power, Islam has played so small a part in it all. The Sheikh-ul-Islam

[001-007] is neglected, and though [[kaiser-wilhelm-ii|the Kaiser]] proclaims a Holy War and calls

[001-008] himself Hadji Mohammed Guilliamo, and says the Hohenzollerns are

[001-009] descended from the Prophet, that seems to have fallen pretty flat. The

[001-010] ordinary man again will answer that Islam in Turkey is becoming a back

[001-011] number, and that Krupp guns are the new gods. Yet—I don’t know. I do

[001-012] not quite believe in Islam becoming a back number.”

[001-013] “Look at it in another way,” he went on. “If it were Enver and Germany

[001-014] alone dragging Turkey into a European war for purposes that no Turk

[001-015] cared a rush about, we might expect to find the regular army obedient,

[001-016] and Constantinople. But in the provinces, where Islam is strong, there

[001-017] would be trouble. Many of us counted on that. But we have been

[001-018] disappointed. The Syrian army is as fanatical as the hordes of the

[001-019] Mahdi. The Senussi have taken a hand in the game. The Persian Moslems

[001-020] are threatening trouble. There is a dry wind blowing through [[eastern-front|the East]],

[001-021] and the parched grasses wait the spark. And that wind is blowing

[001-022] towards the Indian border. Whence comes that wind, think you?”

[001-023] [[sir-walter-bullivant|Sir Walter]] had lowered his voice and was speaking very slow and

[001-024] distinct. I could hear the rain dripping from the eaves of the window,

[001-025] and far off the hoot of taxis in [[foreign-office|Whitehall]].

[001-026] “Have you an explanation, Hannay?” he asked again.

[001-027] “It looks as if Islam had a bigger hand in the thing than we thought,”

[001-028] I said. “I fancy religion is the only thing to knit up such a scattered

[001-029] empire.”

[001-030] “You are right,” he said. “You must be right. We have laughed at the

[001-031] Holy War, the [[holy-war|jehad]] that old Von der Goltz prophesied. But I believe

[001-032] that stupid old man with the big spectacles was right. There is a [[pan-islamism|jehad]]

[001-033] preparing. The question is, How?”

[001-034] “I’m hanged if I know,” I said; “but I’ll bet it won’t be done by a

[001-035] pack of stout German officers in _pickelhaubes_. I fancy you can’t

[001-036] manufacture Holy Wars out of Krupp guns alone and a few staff officers

[001-037] and a battle cruiser with her boilers burst.”

[001-038] “Agreed. They are not fools, however much we try to persuade ourselves

[001-039] of the contrary. But supposing they had got some tremendous sacred

[001-040] sanction—some holy thing, some book or gospel or some new prophet from

[001-041] the desert, something which would cast over the whole ugly mechanism of

[001-042] German war the glamour of the old torrential raids which crumpled the

[001-043] Byzantine Empire and shook the walls of [[vienna|Vienna]]? Islam is a fighting

[001-044] creed, and the mullah still stands in the pulpit with the Koran in one

[001-045] hand and a drawn sword in the other. Supposing there is some Ark of the

[001-046] Covenant which will madden the remotest Moslem peasant with dreams of

[001-047] Paradise? What then, my friend?”

[001-048] “Then there will be hell let loose in those parts pretty soon.”

[001-049] “Hell which may spread. Beyond Persia, remember, lies India.”

[001-050] “You keep to suppositions. How much do you know?” I asked.

[001-051] “Very little, except the fact. But the fact is beyond dispute. I have

[001-052] reports from agents everywhere—pedlars in South [[russia|Russia]], Afghan

[001-053] horse-dealers, Turcoman merchants, pilgrims on the road to Mecca,

[001-054] sheikhs in North Africa, sailors on the [[black-sea|Black Sea]] coasters,

[001-055] sheep-skinned Mongols, Hindu fakirs, Greek traders in the Gulf, as well

[001-056] as respectable Consuls who use cyphers. They tell the same story. The

[001-057] East is waiting for a revelation. It has been promised one. Some

[001-058] star—man, prophecy, or trinket—is coming out of the West. The Germans

[001-059] know, and that is the card with which they are going to astonish the

[001-060] world.”

[001-061] “And the mission you spoke of for me is to go and find out?”

[001-062] He nodded gravely. “That is the crazy and impossible mission.”

[001-063] “Tell me one thing, Sir Walter,” I said. “I know it is the fashion in

[001-064] this country if a man has a special knowledge to set him to some job

[001-065] exactly the opposite. I know all about Damaraland, but instead of being

[001-066] put on [[louis-botha|Botha]]’s staff, as I applied to be, I was kept in Hampshire mud

[001-067] till the campaign in German South West Africa was over. I know a man

[001-068] who could pass as an Arab, but do you think they would send him to the

[001-069] East? They left him in my battalion—a lucky thing for me, for he saved

[001-070] my life at [[loos|Loos]]. I know the fashion, but isn’t this just carrying it a

[001-071] bit too far? There must be thousands of men who have spent years in the

[001-072] East and talk any language. They’re the fellows for this job. I never

[001-073] saw a Turk in my life except a chap who did wrestling turns in a show

[001-074] at Kimberley. You’ve picked about the most useless man on earth.”

[001-075] “You’ve been a mining engineer, Hannay,” Sir Walter said. “If you

[001-076] wanted a man to prospect for gold in Barotseland you would of course

[001-077] like to get one who knew the country and the people and the language.

[001-078] But the first thing you would require in him would be that he had a

[001-079] nose for finding gold and knew his business. That is the position now.

[001-080] I believe that you have a nose for finding out what our enemies try to

[001-081] hide. I know that you are brave and cool and resourceful. That is why I

[001-082] tell you the story. Besides ...”

[001-083] He unrolled a big map of Europe on the wall.

[001-084] “I can’t tell you where you’ll get on the track of the secret, but I

[001-085] can put a limit to the quest. You won’t find it east of the

[001-086] [[bosporus|Bosporus]]—not yet. It is still in Europe. It may be in Constantinople,

[001-087] or in Thrace. It may be farther west. But it is moving eastwards. If

[001-088] you are in time you may cut into its march to Constantinople. That much

[001-089] I can tell you. The secret is known in Germany, too, to those whom it

[001-090] concerns. It is in Europe that the seeker must search—at present.”

[001-091] “Tell me more,” I said. “You can give me no details and no

[001-092] instructions. Obviously you can give me no help if I come to grief.”

[001-093] He nodded. “You would be beyond the pale.”

[001-094] “You give me a free hand.”

[001-095] “Absolutely. You can have what money you like, and you can get what

[001-096] help you like. You can follow any plan you fancy, and go anywhere you

[001-097] think fruitful. We can give no directions.”

[001-098] “One last question. You say it is important. Tell me just how

[001-099] important.”

[001-100] “It is life and death,” he said solemnly. “I can put it no higher and

[001-101] no lower. Once we know what is the menace we can meet it. As long as we

[001-102] are in the dark it works unchecked and we may be too late. The war must

[001-103] be won or lost in Europe. Yes; but if the East blazes up, our effort

[001-104] will be distracted from Europe and the great _coup_ may fail. The

[001-105] stakes are no less than victory and defeat, Hannay.”

[001-106] I got out of my chair and walked to the window. It was a difficult

[001-107] moment in my life. I was happy in my soldiering; above all, happy in

[001-108] the company of my brother officers. I was asked to go off into the

[001-109] enemy’s lands on a quest for which I believed I was manifestly

[001-110] unfitted—a business of lonely days and nights, of [[courage-and-honour|nerve]]-racking strain,

[001-111] of deadly peril shrouding me like a garment. Looking out on the bleak

[001-112] weather I shivered. It was too grim a business, too inhuman for flesh

[001-113] and blood. But Sir Walter had called it a matter of life and death, and

[001-114] I had told him that I was out to serve my country. He could not give me

[001-115] orders, but was I not under orders—higher orders than my Brigadier’s? I

[001-116] thought myself incompetent, but cleverer men than me thought me

[001-117] competent, or at least competent enough for a sporting chance. I knew

[001-118] in my soul that if I declined I should never be quite at peace in the

[001-119] world again. And yet Sir Walter had called the scheme madness, and said

[001-120] that he himself would never have accepted.

[001-121] How does one make a great decision? I swear that when I turned round to

[001-122] speak I meant to refuse. But my answer was Yes, and I had crossed the

[001-123] Rubicon. My voice sounded cracked and far away.

[001-124] Sir Walter shook hands with me and his eyes blinked a little.

[001-125] “I may be sending you to your death, Hannay—Good God, what a damned

[001-126] task-mistress duty is!—If so, I shall be haunted with regrets, but you

[001-127] will never repent. Have no fear of that. You have chosen the roughest

[001-128] road, but it goes straight to the hill-tops.”

[001-129] He handed me the half-sheet of note-paper. On it were written three

[001-130] words—“_Kasredin_”, “_cancer_”, and “_v. I._”

[001-131] “That is the only clue we possess,” he said. “I cannot construe it, but

[001-132] I can tell you the story. We have had our agents working in Persia and

[001-133] [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]] for years—mostly young officers of the Indian Army. They

[001-134] carry their lives in their hands, and now and then one disappears, and

[001-135] the sewers of [[baghdad|Baghdad]] might tell a tale. But they find out many things,

[001-136] and they count the game worth the candle. They have told us of the star

[001-137] rising in the West, but they could give us no details. All but one—the

[001-138] best of them. He had been working between Mosul and the Persian

[001-139] frontier as a muleteer, and had been south into the Bakhtiari hills. He

[001-140] found out something, but his enemies knew that he knew and he was

[001-141] pursued. Three months ago, just before [[kut|Kut]], he staggered into

[001-142] Delamain’s camp with ten bullet holes in him and a knife slash on his

[001-143] forehead. He mumbled his name, but beyond that and the fact that there

[001-144] was a Something coming from the West he told them nothing. He died in

[001-145] ten minutes. They found this paper on him, and since he cried out the

[001-146] word ‘Kasredin’ in his last moments, it must have had something to do

[001-147] with his quest. It is for you to find out if it has any meaning.”

[001-148] I folded it up and placed it in my pocket-book.

[001-149] “What a great fellow! What was his name?” I asked.

[001-150] Sir Walter did not answer at once. He was looking out of the window.

[001-151] “His name,” he said at last, “was [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] Bullivant. He was my son. God

[001-152] rest his brave soul!”

[001-153] CHAPTER II.

[001-154] The Gathering of the Missionaries

[001-155] I wrote out a wire to [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]], asking him to come up by the two-fifteen

[001-156] train and meet me at my flat.

[001-157] “I have chosen my colleague,” I said.

[001-158] “Billy Arbuthnot’s boy? His father was at Harrow with me. I know the

[001-159] fellow—Harry used to bring him down to fish—tallish, with a lean,

[001-160] high-boned face and a pair of brown eyes like a pretty girl’s. I know

[001-161] his record, too. There’s a good deal about him in this office. He rode

[001-162] through Yemen, which no white man ever did before. The Arabs let him

[001-163] pass, for they thought him stark mad and argued that the hand of Allah

[001-164] was heavy enough on him without their efforts. He’s blood-brother to

[001-165] every kind of Albanian bandit. Also he used to take a hand in Turkish

[001-166] politics, and got a huge reputation. Some Englishman was once

[001-167] complaining to old Mahmoud Shevkat about the scarcity of statesmen in

[001-168] Western Europe, and Mahmoud broke in with, ‘Have you not the Honourable

[001-169] Arbuthnot?’ You say he’s in your battalion. I was wondering what had

[001-170] become of him, for we tried to get hold of him here, but he had left no

[001-171] address. Ludovick Arbuthnot—yes, that’s the man. Buried deep in the

[001-172] commissioned ranks of the New Army? Well, we’ll get him out pretty

[001-173] quick!”
