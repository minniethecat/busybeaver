# Hussin

> "If we could find our way back to the road, it would take us at least three days, and I calculated that after one day's trot I should probably be a lump of ice. Our only hope was Hussin." [016-140]

## Overview

Hussin is Hannay's Turkish companion and guide during the desperate flight across Anatolia in the novel's final movement. He is introduced late in the narrative but becomes immediately indispensable — a resourceful, silent figure whose local knowledge and physical endurance make survival possible. As Hannay's small party races toward the Russian lines to prevent catastrophe, Hussin emerges as the group's most reliable operative. Hannay describes him with genuine warmth: "Hussin, my lad" [018-070], a rare term of endearment for the typically reserved narrator.

## Role in the Story

### The Journey Across Anatolia

Hussin first appears as Hannay's party begins their flight: "My companion — his name sounded like Hussin" [016-072]. From this point he becomes central to the group's survival. When their car fails in the wilderness, Hannay explicitly names Hussin as their sole hope: "Our only hope was Hussin" [016-135].

### The Scout and Interpreter

Hussin's value lies in his combination of physical skill and linguistic ability. He and Peter Pienaar scout ahead together: "Hussin and Peter set off on foot, and they were away for two hours" [016-140]. When the group encounters locals, Hussin interprets: "which Hussin interpreted. There was somebody coming" [017-054]. This dual role — scout and interpreter — makes him the linchpin of the party's movement through hostile terrain.

### The Desert Fortress

At the critical juncture where the group must locate a hidden passage, Hussin again proves essential. Hannay recognizes the voice in the darkness: "And the voice was the voice of Hussin" [018-004]. Hussin leads the way up a cliff: "Hussin had shinned up beside us" [018-026] and points out a crucial ledge [018-032]. His silent leadership — "Hussin had his finger on his lips" [018-045] — guides the party through the most dangerous passage.

### The Final Breakout

Hussin's understanding and execution are equally physical and instinctive: "Hussin understood. He led us at a smart pace" [018-072]. Blenkiron's direct address — "Hussin, my lad, d'you hear that?" [018-070] — reflects the group's dependence on his judgment.

## Character Analysis

### The Silent Operative

Hussin is remarkable among Buchan's characters for what he is not: he is neither a British gentleman nor a German officer nor a larger-than-life antagonist. He is a local operative whose competence is entirely practical. He speaks little, acts decisively, and serves without ideological alignment. This makes him a counterpoint to the novel's more flamboyant characters — while Sandy Arbuthnot and John Blenkiron perform elaborate deceptions, Hussin simply navigates, climbs, and survives.

### The Unspoken Bond

Despite the language barrier and cultural distance, Hannay forms a genuine bond with Hussin. The narrator's use of the possessive "my lad" [018-070] signals an affection that transcends the tactical. Hussin's loyalty is never questioned, and his competence never fails — a rarity among Buchan's supporting characters, most of whom stumble at least once.

### Ottoman Realities

Through Hussin, the novel briefly gestures toward the complexity of Ottoman society. He is neither aligned with Enver's Young Turks nor with the German military mission. His motivations remain personal rather than political, serving Hannay's group rather than any national cause. This neutrality makes him, paradoxically, the most trustworthy ally in a landscape defined by shifting allegiances.

## Relationships

- **Richard Hannay**: Hussin's primary point of contact. Hannay's trust is immediate and unwavering, and he addresses Hussin with rare warmth [018-070].

- **Peter Pienaar**: The two scout together during the flight [016-140], suggesting a working partnership built on mutual competence.

- **John Blenkiron**: Blenkiron's direct address to Hussin [018-070] indicates the American's recognition of Hussin's essential role.

## Significance

Hussin represents the novel's tacit acknowledgment that British success in the Near East depends on local allies. He is not idealized or exoticized — Buchan gives him no grand speeches or dramatic moments — but his quiet competence makes survival possible. In a novel dominated by elaborate schemes and political conspiracies, Hussin's physical skill and local knowledge ground the narrative in practical reality. Without Hussin, the mission fails not from lack of intelligence but from simple inability to cross the terrain.
