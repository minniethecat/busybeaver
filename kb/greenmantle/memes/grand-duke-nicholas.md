# Grand Duke Nicholas

Grand Duke Nicholas Nikolaevich of Russia (1856–1929) is a historical figure who appears as an off-stage but narratively crucial character in John Buchan's 1916 novel *Greenmantle*. As the commander-in-chief of the Russian Caucasus Army, he directs the winter offensive against the Ottoman fortress of Erzerum — and becomes the intended recipient of the intelligence that drives the novel's climax.

## Role in the Novel

The Grand Duke never appears directly in the narrative, but his military campaign provides the strategic backdrop for the novel's final act. Hannay, imprisoned in Erzerum, steals Colonel von Stumm's staff map which reveals the weak point in the fortress defences: the unfortified peaks adjacent to Kara Gubek. Hannay immediately grasps the map's significance: "if it could be conveyed to the Grand Duke he would have Erzerum in his power in a day" [^1].

## The Map Mission

Recognising that only the Russian high command can exploit the intelligence, Hannay devises a plan to smuggle the map through the lines. Peter Pienaar volunteers for the mission, prompting Hannay to declare: "inside of a week we'll be having tea with the Grand Duke Nicholas" [^1]. The odds are steep — Hannay privately estimates "five to one" against success [^1] — but the stakes justify the risk. If Peter reaches the Grand Duke's staff, "Stumm's goose is cooked. In three days the Cossacks will be in the streets of Erzerum" [^1].

## Historical Context

The historical Grand Duke Nicholas Nikolaevich was the grandson of Tsar Nicholas I and served as Commander-in-Chief of the Russian Army from 1914 to 1915 before being reassigned to the Caucasus front. The Erzerum offensive depicted in *Greenmantle* was a real campaign: Russian forces captured Erzerum in February 1916, roughly contemporaneous with the novel's publication. Buchan telescopes the timeline for dramatic effect, placing Hannay's fictional intelligence coup at the decisive moment of the actual battle.

## Significance

The Grand Duke functions as an unseen but essential narrative destination — the figure whose receipt of Hannay's intelligence will resolve the plot. His off-stage presence also underscores the novel's geographical and political scope, linking the intimate spy-thriller in Constantinople and Erzerum to the vast military operations of the Eastern Front.
