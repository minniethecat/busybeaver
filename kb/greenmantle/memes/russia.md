# Russia

## Overview

Russia is the great unseen Allied power in *Greenmantle* — a military force whose eastern offensive against the Ottoman Empire provides both the strategic context and the climactic resolution of the novel. The Russian advance toward Erzerum is the tide that sweeps through the final chapters, disrupting the German conspiracy and giving Hannay's mission its ultimate urgency (001-052).

## In the Narrative

Russia appears in the novel not as a depicted country but as a direction: the destination toward which the heroes flee, the army whose shells they hear bursting, and the force that makes the German-Ottoman position at Erzerum untenable. Sir Walter Bullivant's intelligence network extends "everywhere — pedlars in South Russia, Afghan horse-dealers" (001-052), making Russia part of the novel's geography of espionage from the first chapter.

During the climactic sequence at Erzerum, the Russian bombardment is a constant presence. "The Russians are round it in a broad half-moon" (020-009), Hannay observes of the fortress. The sound of "bursting of Russian shells" (022-151) provides the acoustic backdrop to the final action, and Hannay's escape plan depends on reaching "the Russian lines somehow before morning" (021-074).

In a rare moment of humor, Blenkiron refers to the Russians as "the Musky Jaws," prompting Hannay's clarification: "I don't know what the Musky Jaws are, but if you mean the Russians I'm on your side" (021-089). The novel's German characters regard Russia with contempt — "a gigantic nation of savages, heathens who had never been converted" (008-133) — reflecting the racialized propaganda of the Eastern Front.

## Strategic Significance

The Russian offensive in the Caucasus is the military fact that undermines the Greenmantle conspiracy. While Germany hopes to ignite a pan-Islamic uprising that would threaten British India, the Russian army's steady advance on Erzerum makes the conspiracy's base of operations militarily indefensible. The novel's geography is shaped by this pressure: every journey toward the east moves toward Russian lines, and safety is defined by proximity to the Russian front.

The Grand Duke Nicholas, the Russian commander, is mentioned as the architect of this offensive. The Russian advance on "a broad front" (019-049) prevents the German-Ottoman forces from concentrating their defense — a strategic reality that Hannay exploits in his escape planning.

## Key Events

- The Russian offensive opens the novel's eastern theatre: Sir Walter briefs Hannay on the strategic situation in the Caucasus (001-052)
- Russian shells batter the Deve Boyun pass east of Erzerum, providing cover for Hannay's movements (019-032)
- Hannay plans his escape toward the Russian lines, studying "the nearest Russian positions" on the map (019-083)
- The Russian hour arrives — "It was the Russian hour" (021-152) — as the offensive reaches its climax
- Russian guns keep the German defenders occupied while Hannay makes his break (022-162)
