# Gaudian

> "Of all the men I have met in this business, the one I like the best is Herr Gaudian. It's because I believe that he is a good man, a really good man, that I have hopes for Germany when the war is over." — Richard Hannay [005-134]

## Overview

Herr Gaudian is a German engineer serving with the military mission in Anatolia, and one of the most morally complex characters in *Greenmantle*. Unlike Colonel von Stumm, whose brutality defines him, Gaudian earns Hannay's genuine respect — an extraordinary concession from a British protagonist toward a German officer in wartime fiction. Hannay's assessment is unequivocal: "Herr Gaudian is a really good man" [005-134]. Gaudian's technical expertise, personal decency, and professional dedication make him a bridge figure between the novel's adversarial camps.

## Role in the Story

### First Encounters

Gaudian first appears indirectly through photographs of his family observed at the German headquarters: "there were old fellows, probably Gaudian's parents" [005-014]. This domestic detail immediately distinguishes him from the other German officers — he has a family, a history, a life beyond the military.

His formal introduction comes at Stumm's invitation: "It was Gaudian, to bid me join him and Stumm" [005-035]. The setting is ominous, but Gaudian's manner is notably different from Stumm's menace: "Gaudian spoke in a pleasant voice, as if he was making the talk of a friendly afternoon" [005-049].

### The Oath-Taking

In one of the novel's strangest scenes, Stumm and Gaudian participate in what appears to be a quasi-ceremonial act: "Stumm held up his right hand and so did Gaudian, like a man taking an oath or making a pledge" [005-056]. This moment, witnessed by Hannay while pretending to be drunk, hints at the secret compact underlying the German operation — and Gaudian's participation suggests that even decent men are implicated in the machinery of empire.

### The Whispered Secret

Gaudian is central to one of the novel's key plot mechanisms. The word that Stumm whispers to Gaudian [005-178] — the crucial piece of intelligence Hannay strains to overhear — is the hinge on which much of the mission turns. Hannay later recounts: "I told them of Stumm and Gaudian and the whispered words I had heard" [012-116] and "I told them of the other name Stumm and Gaudian had spoken" [012-139].

### The Erzerum Reunion

When Hannay reaches Erzerum, Gaudian reappears: "for the first man I saw was Gaudian" [013-098]. The reunion is tense: "I doubt if he would have recognized me" [013-098]. The connection between Gaudian and Blenkiron becomes evident: "There was a constant coming and going between him and Blenkiron" [013-103], and Blenkiron draws Gaudian into impromptu lessons about American industry: "Gaudian got on to the use of water power" [013-131].

During the crucial confrontation, Enver and Gaudian are compared: "Enver was on the point like a knife, far quicker than Gaudian" [013-150] — a moment that simultaneously acknowledges Gaudian's intellectual limitations while confirming his essential difference from the more menacing Enver.

## Character Traits

### The Good German

Gaudian represents a rhetorical necessity in Buchan's novel. For the moral argument to function — that what Britain fights is not the German people but German militarism — there must be a character who embodies German decency. Gaudian fills this role. Hannay's declaration that Gaudian gives him "hopes for Germany when the war is over" [005-134] is as much political positioning as character assessment.

### Engineer vs. Soldier

Gaudian is defined by his technical expertise rather than martial identity. When Blenkiron draws him into discussions of water power and industrial engineering [013-131], the implication is clear: Gaudian's natural domain is construction, not destruction. This aligns with the novel's broader critique of German militarism as a corruption of the nation's genuine talents.

### Between Stumm and Hannay

Gaudian is suspended between the novel's moral poles — respectful toward Stumm's authority ("so great a man as Gaudian clearly respected him" [005-170]) yet genuinely cordial to Hannay. This liminal position makes him the novel's most interesting German character, neither villain nor hero but a decent man in an indecent system.

## Relationships

- **Colonel von Stumm**: Gaudian's superior. The power dynamic is one of deference: Gaudian respects Stumm [005-170] and participates in Stumm's quasi-ritualistic gestures [005-056]. The whispered communication between them [005-178] suggests a closeness that excludes others.

- **Richard Hannay**: The relationship is marked by mutual if cautious respect. Gaudian "patted me on the back when we parted" [005-158], and Hannay's assessment of Gaudian's goodness [005-134] is one of the novel's most generous cross-national judgments.

- **John Blenkiron**: Blenkiron cultivates Gaudian at Erzerum, recognizing him as a useful contact. The constant traffic between them [013-103] suggests Blenkiron's operational patience.

- **Enver Pasha**: The comparison at the Erzerum reception [013-150] casts Gaudian as slower but, implicitly, more genuine than the Ottoman leader.

## Significance

In Buchan's moral geography, Gaudian occupies a crucial middle ground. He is German but decent, military but technically oriented, complicit in Stumm's schemes but personally honorable. His presence allows the novel to maintain the distinction — essential to wartime propaganda — between the German people and the German military machine. Hannay's hope for "Germany when the war is over" [005-134] is projected onto Gaudian's broad shoulders, making the engineer not just a character but an argument about the possibility of postwar reconciliation.
