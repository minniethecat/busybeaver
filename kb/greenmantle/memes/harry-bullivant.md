# Harry Bullivant

## Overview

Harry Bullivant was the son of Sir Walter Bullivant and a British intelligence operative who died pursuing the same conspiracy that Hannay is tasked with investigating. His death gives the Greenmantle mission its profound personal dimension for Sir Walter [001-151].

## Role in the Story

Harry Bullivant does not appear directly in the narrative — he is dead before the story begins. His presence is felt through his father's grief and determination. Sir Walter reveals Harry's fate to Hannay with a simple, devastating admission:

> "His name was Harry Bullivant. He was my son. God rest his brave soul!" [001-151]-[001-152]

Harry's death transforms the mission from a professional assignment into a personal quest for Sir Walter. The three cryptic words Harry scribbled before he died — _Kasredin_, _Cancer_, and _v. I._ — become the central clues that drive the entire investigation [001-078].

## Key Appearances

Harry is referenced several times throughout the novel. The narrator introduces him in the front matter by noting that Sandy Arbuthnot "occupies the post that once was Harry Bullivant's" at Basra [P01-094]. This detail connects Sandy's trajectory to Harry's legacy and underscores the continuity of British intelligence operations in the region.

Sir Walter, in recommending Sandy as Hannay's colleague, casually mentions that "Harry used to bring him down to fish" [001-159], revealing a pre-war friendship between Harry and Sandy that adds depth to Sandy's later determination to solve Harry's riddles.

## The Three Words

Harry Bullivant's lasting contribution to the plot is the coded message he left behind. Hannay reflects on its obsessive pull:

> "The three words Harry Bullivant had scribbled had danced through my head in a crazy fandango." [008-096]

The words prove to be the key to the entire conspiracy. Sandy later announces that he has decoded the first:

> "I've solved the first of Harry Bullivant's riddles. I know the meaning of _Kasredin_." [012-065]

And Hannay himself cracks the third word, unveiling the name at the heart of the mystery:

> "Harry Bullivant knew that some man or woman called von Einem was at the heart of the mystery." [008-113]

## Relationships

Harry is Sir Walter Bullivant's son — a relationship that gives the spymaster's otherwise clinical demeanor its underlying pathos. Sandy Arbuthnot knew Harry personally; when Hannay shows him the coded paper, Sandy's response reveals genuine grief:

> "Sandy's face grew solemn. 'Old Harry. He was at my tutor's. The best fellow God ever made. I saw his name in the casualty list before Kut. ... Harry didn't do things without a purpose.'" [001-082]-[001-084]

Sandy later, upon learning that the third clue decodes to Hilda von Einem, pays quiet tribute:

> "'Good old Harry,' said Sandy softly. 'He was a dashed clever chap.'" [012-143]

## Significance

Harry Bullivant serves as the emotional anchor of the novel's intelligence plot. His death ensures that the Greenmantle mission is never merely a geopolitical chess game — it is also a father's attempt to complete his son's unfinished work. The three-word cipher Harry left behind demonstrates his professionalism and foresight: even in extremis, he ensured his work could be carried forward by those who came after him [002-247]. His memory also binds Sandy to the mission through personal loyalty, not just duty.
