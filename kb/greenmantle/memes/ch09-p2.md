# Ch. 9: The Return of the Straggler (cont'd, part 2)

[009-001] Late on that Sunday night we passed through a great city which the

[009-002] captain told me was [[vienna|Vienna]]. It seemed to last for miles and miles, and

[009-003] to be as brightly lit as a circus. After that, we were in big plains

[009-004] and the air grew perishing cold. [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] had come aboard once for his

[009-005] rations, but usually he left it to his partner, for he was lying very

[009-006] low. But one morning—I think it was the 5th of January, when we had

[009-007] passed Buda and were moving through great sodden flats just sprinkled

[009-008] with snow—the captain took it into his head to get me to overhaul the

[009-009] barge loads. Armed with a mighty type-written list, I made a tour of

[009-010] the barges, beginning with the hindmost. There was a fine old stock of

[009-011] deadly weapons—mostly machine-guns and some field-pieces, and enough

[009-012] shells to blow up the [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]] peninsula. All kinds of shell were

[009-013] there, from the big 14-inch crumps to rifle grenades and

[009-014] trench-mortars. It made me fairly sick to see all these good things

[009-015] preparing for our own fellows, and I wondered whether I would not be

[009-016] doing my best service if I engineered a big explosion. Happily I had

[009-017] the common sense to remember my job and my [[courage-and-honour|duty]] and to stick to it.

[009-018] Peter was in the middle of the convoy, and I found him pretty unhappy,

[009-019] principally through not being allowed to smoke. His companion was an

[009-020] ox-eyed lad, whom I ordered to the look-out while Peter and I went over

[009-021] the lists.

[009-022] “Cornelis, my old friend,” he said, “there are some pretty toys here.

[009-023] With a spanner and a couple of clear hours I could make these maxims

[009-024] about as deadly as bicycles. What do you say to a try?”

[009-025] “I’ve considered that,” I said, “but it won’t do. We’re on a bigger

[009-026] business than wrecking munition convoys. I want to know how you got

[009-027] here.”

[009-028] He smiled with that extraordinary Sunday-school docility of his.

[009-029] “It was very simple, Cornelis. I was foolish in the cafe—but they have

[009-030] told you of that. You see I was angry and did not reflect. They had

[009-031] separated us, and I could see would treat me as dirt. Therefore, my bad

[009-032] temper came out, for, as I have told you, I do not like Germans.”

[009-033] Peter gazed lovingly at the little bleak farms which dotted the

[009-034] Hungarian plain.

[009-035] “All night I lay in _tronk_ with no food. In the morning they fed me,

[009-036] and took me hundreds of miles in a train to a place which I think is

[009-037] called Neuburg. It was a great prison, full of English officers ... I

[009-038] asked myself many times on the journey what was the reason of this

[009-039] treatment, for I could see no sense in it. If they wanted to punish me

[009-040] for insulting them they had the chance to send me off to the trenches.

[009-041] No one could have objected. If they thought me useless they could have

[009-042] turned me back to Holland. I could not have stopped them. But they

[009-043] treated me as if I were a dangerous man, whereas all their conduct

[009-044] hitherto had shown that they thought me a fool. I could not understand

[009-045] it.

[009-046] “But I had not been one night in that Neuburg place before I thought of

[009-047] the reason. They wanted to keep me under observation as a check upon

[009-048] you, Cornelis. I figured it out this way. They had given you some very

[009-049] important work which required them to let you into some big secret. So

[009-050] far, good. They evidently thought much of you, even yon Stumm man,

[009-051] though he was as rude as a buffalo. But they did not know you fully,

[009-052] and they wanted to check on you. That check they found in Peter

[009-053] Pienaar. Peter was a fool, and if there was anything to blab, sooner or

[009-054] later Peter would blab it. Then they would stretch out a long arm and

[009-055] nip you short, wherever you were. Therefore they must keep old Peter

[009-056] under their eye.”

[009-057] “That sounds likely enough,” I said.

[009-058] “It was God’s truth,” said Peter. “And when it was all clear to me I

[009-059] settled that I must escape. Partly because I am a free man and do not

[009-060] like to be in prison, but mostly because I was not sure of myself. Some

[009-061] day my temper would go again, and I might say foolish things for which

[009-062] Cornelis would suffer. So it was very certain that I must escape.

[009-063] “Now, Cornelis, I noticed pretty soon that there were two kinds among

[009-064] the prisoners. There were the real prisoners, mostly English and

[009-065] French, and there were humbugs. The humbugs were treated, apparently,

[009-066] like the others, but not really, as I soon perceived. There was one man

[009-067] who passed as an English officer, another as a French Canadian, and the

[009-068] others called themselves Russians. None of the honest men suspected

[009-069] them, but they were there as spies to hatch plots for escape and get

[009-070] the poor devils caught in the act, and to worm out confidences which

[009-071] might be of value. That is the German notion of good business. I am not

[009-072] a British soldier to think all men are gentlemen. I know that amongst

[009-073] men there are desperate _skellums_, so I soon picked up this game. It

[009-074] made me very angry, but it was a good thing for my plan. I made my

[009-075] resolution to escape the day I arrived at Neuburg, and on Christmas Day

[009-076] I had a plan made.”

[009-077] “Peter, you’re an old marvel. Do you mean to say you were quite certain

[009-078] of getting away whenever you wanted?”

[009-079] “Quite certain, Cornelis. You see, I have been wicked in my time and

[009-080] know something about the inside of prisons. You may build them like

[009-081] great castles, or they may be like a backveld _tronk_, only mud and

[009-082] corrugated iron, but there is always a key and a man who keeps it, and

[009-083] that man can be bested. I knew I could get away, but I did not think it

[009-084] would be so easy. That was due to the bogus prisoners, my friends, the

[009-085] spies.

[009-086] “I made great pals with them. On Christmas night we were very jolly

[009-087] together. I think I spotted every one of them the first day. I bragged

[009-088] about my past and all I had done, and I told them I was going to

[009-089] escape. They backed me up and promised to help. Next morning I had a

[009-090] plan. In the afternoon, just after dinner, I had to go to the

[009-091] commandant’s room. They treated me a little differently from the

[009-092] others, for I was not a prisoner of war, and I went there to be asked

[009-093] questions and to be cursed as a stupid Dutchman. There was no strict

[009-094] guard kept there, for the place was on the second floor, and distant by

[009-095] many yards from any staircase. In the corridor outside the commandant’s

[009-096] room there was a window which had no bars, and four feet from the

[009-097] window the limb of a great tree. A man might reach that limb, and if he

[009-098] were active as a monkey might descend to the ground. Beyond that I knew

[009-099] nothing, but I am a good climber, Cornelis.

[009-100] “I told the others of my plan. They said it was good, but no one

[009-101] offered to come with me. They were very noble; they declared that the

[009-102] scheme was mine and I should have the fruit of it, for if more than one

[009-103] tried, detection was certain. I agreed and thanked them—thanked them

[009-104] with tears in my eyes. Then one of them very secretly produced a map.

[009-105] We planned out my road, for I was going straight to Holland. It was a

[009-106] long road, and I had no money, for they had taken all my sovereigns

[009-107] when I was arrested, but they promised to get a subscription up among

[009-108] themselves to start me. Again I wept tears of gratitude. This was on

[009-109] Sunday, the day after Christmas, and I settled to make the attempt on

[009-110] the Wednesday afternoon.

[009-111] “Now, Cornelis, when the lieutenant took us to see the British

[009-112] prisoners, you remember, he told us many things about the ways of

[009-113] prisons. He told us how they loved to catch a man in the act of escape,

[009-114] so that they could use him harshly with a clear conscience. I thought

[009-115] of that, and calculated that now my friends would have told everything

[009-116] to the commandant, and that they would be waiting to bottle me on the

[009-117] Wednesday. Till then I reckoned I would be slackly guarded, for they

[009-118] would look on me as safe in the net ...

[009-119] “So I went out of the window next day. It was the Monday afternoon ...”

[009-120] “That was a bold stroke,” I said admiringly.

[009-121] “The plan was bold, but it was not skilful,” said Peter modestly. “I

[009-122] had no money beyond seven marks, and I had but one stick of chocolate.

[009-123] I had no overcoat, and it was snowing hard. Further, I could not get

[009-124] down the tree, which had a trunk as smooth and branchless as a blue

[009-125] gum. For a little I thought I should be compelled to give in, and I was

[009-126] not happy.

[009-127] “But I had leisure, for I did not think I would be missed before

[009-128] nightfall, and given time a man can do most things. By and by I found a

[009-129] branch which led beyond the outer wall of the yard and hung above the

[009-130] river. This I followed, and then dropped from it into the stream. It

[009-131] was a drop of some yards, and the water was very swift, so that I

[009-132] nearly drowned. I would rather swim the Limpopo, Cornelis, among all

[009-133] the crocodiles than that icy river. Yet I managed to reach the shore

[009-134] and get my breath lying in the bushes ...

[009-135] “After that it was plain going, though I was very cold. I knew that I

[009-136] would be sought on the northern roads, as I had told my friends, for no

[009-137] one could dream of an ignorant Dutchman going south away from his

[009-138] kinsfolk. But I had learned enough from the map to know that our road

[009-139] lay south-east, and I had marked this big river.”

[009-140] “Did you hope to pick me up?” I asked.

[009-141] “No, Cornelis. I thought you would be travelling in first-class

[009-142] carriages while I should be plodding on foot. But I was set on getting

[009-143] to the place you spoke of (how do you call it? Constant Nople?), where

[009-144] our big business lay. I thought I might be in time for that.”

[009-145] “You’re an old Trojan, Peter,” I said; “but go on. How did you get to

[009-146] that landing-stage where I found you?”

[009-147] “It was a hard journey,” he said meditatively. “It was not easy to get

[009-148] beyond the barbed-wire entanglements which surrounded Neuburg—yes, even

[009-149] across the river. But in time I reached the woods and was safe, for I

[009-150] did not think any German could equal me in wild country. The best of

[009-151] them, even their foresters, are but babes in veldcraft compared with

[009-152] such as me ... My troubles came only from hunger and cold. Then I met a

[009-153] Peruvian smouse[1], and sold him my clothes and bought from him these.

[009-154] I did not want to part with my own, which were better, but he gave me

[009-155] ten marks on the deal. After that I went into a village and ate

[009-156] heavily.”

[009-157] [1] Peter meant a Polish-Jew pedlar.

[009-158] “Were you pursued?” I asked.

[009-159] “I do not think so. They had gone north, as I expected, and were

[009-160] looking for me at the railway stations which my friends had marked for

[009-161] me. I walked happily and put a bold face on it. If I saw a man or woman

[009-162] look at me suspiciously I went up to them at once and talked. I told a

[009-163] sad tale, and all believed it. I was a poor Dutchman travelling home on

[009-164] foot to see a dying mother, and I had been told that by the [[danube-river|Danube]] I

[009-165] should find the main railway to take me to Holland. There were kind

[009-166] people who gave me food, and one woman gave me half a mark, and wished

[009-167] me God speed ... Then on the last day of the year I came to the river

[009-168] and found many drunkards.”
