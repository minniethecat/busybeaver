# Ch. 22: The Guns of the North (cont'd, part 4)

[022-001] He would take no denial, so we drew again, and the lot fell to [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]].

[022-002] “If I’m the last to go,” he said, “I promise I don’t miss. Stumm won’t

[022-003] be long in following me.”

[022-004] He shook hands with his cheery smile, and he and the Companion slipped

[022-005] over the parapet in the final shadows before dawn.

[022-006] [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] spread his Patience cards on a flat rock, and dealt out the

[022-007] Double Napoleon. He was perfectly calm, and hummed to himself his only

[022-008] tune. For myself I was drinking in my last draught of the hill air. My

[022-009] contentment was going. I suddenly felt bitterly loath to die.

[022-010] Something of the same kind must have passed through Blenkiron’s head.

[022-011] He suddenly looked up and asked, “Sister Anne, Sister Anne, do you see

[022-012] anybody coming?”

[022-013] I stood close to the parapet, watching every detail of the landscape as

[022-014] shown by the revealing daybreak. Up on the shoulders of the Palantuken,

[022-015] snowdrifts lipped over the edges of the cliffs. I wondered when they

[022-016] would come down as avalanches. There was a kind of croft on one

[022-017] hillside, and from a hut the smoke of breakfast was beginning to curl.

[022-018] Stumm’s gunners were awake and apparently holding council. Far down on

[022-019] the main road a convoy was moving—I heard the creak of the wheels two

[022-020] miles away, for the air was deathly still.

[022-021] Then, as if a spring had been loosed, the world suddenly leaped to a

[022-022] hideous life. With a growl the guns opened round all the horizon. They

[022-023] were especially fierce to the south, where a _rafale_ beat as I had

[022-024] never heard it before. The one glance I cast behind me showed the gap

[022-025] in the hills choked with fumes and dust.

[022-026] But my eyes were on the north. From Erzerum city tall tongues of flame

[022-027] leaped from a dozen quarters. Beyond, towards the opening of the

[022-028] [[euphrates-river|Euphrates]] glen, there was the sharp crack of field-guns. I strained

[022-029] eyes and ears, mad with impatience, and I read the riddle.

[022-030] “Sandy,” I yelled, “[[peter-pienaar|Peter]] has got through. The Russians are round the

[022-031] flank. The town is burning. Glory to God, we’ve won, we’ve won!”

[022-032] And as I spoke the earth seemed to split beside me, and I was flung

[022-033] forward on the gravel which covered [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von Einem’s grave.

[022-034] As I picked myself up, and to my amazement found myself uninjured, I

[022-035] saw Blenkiron rubbing the dust out of his eyes and arranging a

[022-036] disordered card. He had stopped humming, and was singing aloud:

[022-037] “He captured Harper’s Ferry, with his nineteen men so true

[022-038] And he frightened old Virginny ...”

[022-039] “Say, Major,” he cried, “I believe this game of mine is coming out.”

[022-040] I was now pretty well mad. The thought that old Peter had won, that we

[022-041] had won beyond our wildest dreams, that if we died there were those

[022-042] coming who would exact the uttermost vengeance, rode my brain like a

[022-043] fever. I sprang on the parapet and waved my hand to Stumm, shouting

[022-044] defiance. Rifle shots cracked out from behind, and I leaped back just

[022-045] in time for the next shell.

[022-046] The charge must have been short, for it was a bad miss, landing

[022-047] somewhere on the glacis. The next was better and crashed on the near

[022-048] parapet, carving a great hole in the rocky _kranz_. This time my arm

[022-049] hung limp, broken by a fragment of stone, but I felt no pain. Blenkiron

[022-050] seemed to bear a charmed life, for he was smothered in dust, but

[022-051] unhurt. He blew the dust away from his cards very gingerly and went on

[022-052] playing.

[022-053] Then came a dud which dropped neatly inside on the soft ground. I was

[022-054] determined to break for the open and chance the rifle fire, for if

[022-055] Stumm went on shooting the _castrol_ was certain death. I caught

[022-056] Blenkiron round the middle, scattering his cards to the winds, and

[022-057] jumped over the parapet.

[022-058] “Don’t apologize, Major,” said he. “The game was as good as won. But

[022-059] for God’s sake drop me, for if you wave me like the banner of freedom

[022-060] I’ll get plugged sure and good.”

[022-061] My one thought was to get cover for the next minutes, for I had an

[022-062] instinct that our vigil was near its end. The defences of Erzerum were

[022-063] crumbling like sand-castles, and it was a proof of the tenseness of my

[022-064] nerves that I seemed to be deaf to the sound. Stumm had seen us cross

[022-065] the parapet, and he started to sprinkle all the surroundings of the

[022-066] _castrol_. Blenkiron and I lay like a working-party between the lines

[022-067] caught by machine-guns, taking a pull on ourselves as best we could.

[022-068] Sandy had some kind of cover, but we were on the bare farther slope,

[022-069] and the riflemen on that side might have had us at their mercy.

[022-070] But no shots came from them. As I looked east, the hillside, which a

[022-071] little before had been held by our enemies, was as empty as the desert.

[022-072] And then I saw on the main road a sight which for a second time made me

[022-073] yell like a maniac. Down that glen came a throng of men and galloping

[022-074] limbers—a crazy, jostling crowd, spreading away beyond the road to the

[022-075] steep slopes, and leaving behind it many black dots to darken the

[022-076] snows. The gates of the South had yielded, and our friends were through

[022-077] them.

[022-078] At that sight I forgot all about our danger. I didn’t give a cent for

[022-079] Stumm’s shells. I didn’t believe he could hit me. The [[fate-and-providence|fate]] which had

[022-080] mercifully preserved us for the first taste of victory would see us

[022-081] through to the end.

[022-082] I remember bundling Blenkiron along the hill to find Sandy. But our

[022-083] news was anticipated. For down our own side-glen came the same broken

[022-084] tumult of men. More; for at their backs, far up at the throat of the

[022-085] pass, I saw horsemen—the horsemen of the pursuit. Old Nicholas had

[022-086] flung his cavalry in.

[022-087] Sandy was on his feet, with his lips set and his eye abstracted. If his

[022-088] face hadn’t been burned black by weather it would have been pale as a

[022-089] dish-clout. A man like him doesn’t make up his mind for death and then

[022-090] be given his life again without being wrenched out of his bearings. I

[022-091] thought he didn’t understand what had happened, so I beat him on the

[022-092] shoulders.

[022-093] “Man, d’you see?” I cried. “The Cossacks! The Cossacks! God! How

[022-094] they’re taking that slope! They’re into them now. By heaven, we’ll ride

[022-095] with them! We’ll get the gun horses!”

[022-096] A little knoll prevented Stumm and his men from seeing what was

[022-097] happening farther up the glen, till the first wave of the rout was on

[022-098] them. He had gone on bombarding the _castrol_ and its environs while

[022-099] the world was cracking over his head. The gun team was in the hollow

[022-100] below the road, and down the hill among the boulders we crawled,

[022-101] Blenkiron as lame as a duck, and me with a limp left arm.

[022-102] The poor beasts were straining at their pickets and sniffing the

[022-103] morning wind, which brought down the thick fumes of the great

[022-104] bombardment and the indescribable babbling cries of a beaten army.

[022-105] Before we reached them that maddened horde had swept down on them, men

[022-106] panting and gasping in their flight, many of them bloody from wounds,

[022-107] many tottering in the first stages of collapse and death. I saw the

[022-108] horses seized by a dozen hands, and a desperate fight for their

[022-109] possession. But as we halted there our eyes were fixed on the battery

[022-110] on the road above us, for round it was now sweeping the van of the

[022-111] retreat.

[022-112] I had never seen a rout before, when strong men come to the end of

[022-113] their tether and only their broken shadows stumble towards the refuge

[022-114] they never find. No more had Stumm, poor devil. I had no ill-will left

[022-115] for him, though coming down that hill I was rather hoping that the two

[022-116] of us might have a final scrap. He was a brute and a bully, but, by

[022-117] God! he was a man. I heard his great roar when he saw the tumult, and

[022-118] the next I saw was his monstrous figure working at the gun. He swung it

[022-119] south and turned it on the fugitives.

[022-120] But he never fired it. The press was on him, and the gun was swept

[022-121] sideways. He stood up, a foot higher than any of them, and he seemed to

[022-122] be trying to check the rush with his pistol. There is power in numbers,

[022-123] even though every unit is broken and fleeing. For a second to that wild

[022-124] crowd Stumm was the enemy, and they had strength enough to crush him.

[022-125] The wave flowed round and then across him. I saw the butt-ends of

[022-126] rifles crash on his head and shoulders, and the next second the stream

[022-127] had passed over his body.

[022-128] That was God’s judgement on the man who had set himself above his kind.

[022-129] Sandy gripped my shoulder and was shouting in my ear:

[022-130] “They’re coming, Dick. Look at the grey devils ... Oh, God be thanked,

[022-131] it’s our friends!”

[022-132] The next minute we were tumbling down the hillside, Blenkiron hopping

[022-133] on one leg between us. I heard dimly Sandy crying, “Oh, well done our

[022-134] side!” and Blenkiron declaiming about Harper’s Ferry, but I had no

[022-135] voice at all and no wish to shout. I know the tears were in my eyes,

[022-136] and that if I had been left alone I would have sat down and cried with

[022-137] pure thankfulness. For sweeping down the glen came a cloud of grey

[022-138] cavalry on little wiry horses, a cloud which stayed not for the rear of

[022-139] the fugitives, but swept on like a flight of rainbows, with the steel

[022-140] of their lance-heads glittering in the winter sun. They were riding for

[022-141] Erzerum.

[022-142] Remember that for three months we had been with the enemy and had never

[022-143] seen the face of an Ally in arms. We had been cut off from the

[022-144] fellowship of a great cause, like a fort surrounded by an army. And now

[022-145] we were delivered, and there fell around us the warm joy of comradeship

[022-146] as well as the exultation of victory.

[022-147] We flung caution to the winds, and went stark mad. Sandy, still in his

[022-148] emerald coat and turban, was scrambling up the farther slope of the

[022-149] hollow, yelling greetings in every language known to man. The leader

[022-150] saw him, with a word checked his men for a moment—it was marvellous to

[022-151] see the horses reined in in such a break-neck ride—and from the

[022-152] squadron half a dozen troopers swung loose and wheeled towards us. Then

[022-153] a man in a grey overcoat and a sheepskin cap was on the ground beside

[022-154] us wringing our hands.

[022-155] “You are safe, my old friends”—it was Peter’s voice that spoke—“I will

[022-156] take you back to our army, and get you breakfast.”

[022-157] “No, by the Lord, you won’t,” cried Sandy. “We’ve had the rough end of

[022-158] the job and now we’ll have the fun. Look after Blenkiron and these

[022-159] fellows of mine. I’m going to ride knee by knee with your sportsmen for

[022-160] the city.”

[022-161] Peter spoke a word, and two of the Cossacks dismounted. The next I knew

[022-162] I was mixed up in the cloud of greycoats, galloping down the road up

[022-163] which the morning before we had strained to the _castrol_.

[022-164] That was the great hour of my life, and to live through it was worth a

[022-165] dozen years of slavery. With a broken left arm I had little hold on my

[022-166] beast, so I trusted my neck to him and let him have his will. Black

[022-167] with dirt and smoke, hatless, with no kind of uniform, I was a wilder

[022-168] figure than any Cossack. I soon was separated from Sandy, who had two

[022-169] hands and a better horse, and seemed resolute to press forward to the

[022-170] very van. That would have been suicide for me, and I had all I could do

[022-171] to keep my place in the bunch I rode with.
