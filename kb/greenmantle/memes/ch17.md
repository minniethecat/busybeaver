# Ch. 17: Trouble By the Waters of Babylon

[017-001] That night, I remember, I had a queer dream. I seemed to be in a wild

[017-002] place among mountains, and I was being hunted, though who was after me

[017-003] I couldn’t tell. I remember sweating with fright, for I seemed to be

[017-004] quite alone and the terror that was pursuing me was more than human.

[017-005] The place was horribly quiet and still, and there was deep snow lying

[017-006] everywhere, so that each step I took was heavy as lead. A very ordinary

[017-007] sort of nightmare, you will say. Yes, but there was one strange feature

[017-008] in this one. The night was pitch dark, but ahead of me in the throat of

[017-009] the pass there was one patch of light, and it showed a rum little hill

[017-010] with a rocky top: what we call in South Africa a _castrol_ or saucepan.

[017-011] I had a notion that if I could get to that _castrol_ I should be safe,

[017-012] and I panted through the drifts towards it with the avenger of blood at

[017-013] my heels. I woke, gasping, to find the winter morning struggling

[017-014] through the cracked rafters, and to hear [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] say cheerily that

[017-015] his duodenum had behaved all night like a gentleman. I lay still for a

[017-016] bit trying to fix the dream, but it all dissolved into haze except the

[017-017] picture of the little hill, which was quite clear in every detail. I

[017-018] told myself it was a reminiscence of the veld, some spot down in the

[017-019] Wakkerstroom country, though for the life of me I couldn’t place it.

[017-020] I pass over the next three days, for they were one uninterrupted series

[017-021] of heart-breaks. Hussin and [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] scoured the country for horses,

[017-022] Blenkiron sat in the barn and played Patience, while I haunted the

[017-023] roadside near the bridge in the hope of picking up some kind of

[017-024] conveyance. My task was perfectly futile. The columns passed, casting

[017-025] wondering eyes on the wrecked car among the frozen rushes, but they

[017-026] could offer no help. My friend the Turkish officer promised to wire to

[017-027] [[angora|Angora]] from some place or other for a fresh car, but, remembering the

[017-028] state of affairs at Angora, I had no hope from that quarter. Cars

[017-029] passed, plenty of them, packed with staff-officers, Turkish and German,

[017-030] but they were in far too big a hurry even to stop and speak. The only

[017-031] conclusion I reached from my roadside vigil was that things were

[017-032] getting very warm in the neighbourhood of Erzerum. Everybody on that

[017-033] road seemed to be in mad haste either to get there or to get away.

[017-034] Hussin was the best chance, for, as I have said, the Companions had a

[017-035] very special and peculiar graft throughout the Turkish Empire. But the

[017-036] first day he came back empty-handed. All the horses had been

[017-037] commandeered for the war, he said; and though he was certain that some

[017-038] had been kept back and hidden away, he could not get on their track.

[017-039] The second day he returned with two—miserable screws and deplorably

[017-040] short in the wind from a diet of beans. There was no decent corn or hay

[017-041] left in the countryside. The third day he picked up a nice little Arab

[017-042] stallion: in poor condition, it is true, but perfectly sound. For these

[017-043] beasts we paid good money, for Blenkiron was well supplied and we had

[017-044] no time to spare for the interminable Oriental bargaining.

[017-045] Hussin said he had cleaned up the countryside, and I believed him. I

[017-046] dared not delay another day, even though it meant leaving him behind.

[017-047] But he had no notion of doing anything of the kind. He was a good

[017-048] runner, he said, and could keep up with such horses as ours for ever.

[017-049] If this was the manner of our progress, I reckoned we would be weeks in

[017-050] getting to Erzerum.

[017-051] We started at dawn on the morning of the fourth day, after the old

[017-052] farmer had blessed us and sold us some stale rye-bread. Blenkiron

[017-053] bestrode the Arab, being the heaviest, and Peter and I had the screws.

[017-054] My worst forebodings were soon realized, and Hussin, loping along at my

[017-055] side, had an easy job to keep up with us. We were about as slow as an

[017-056] ox-wagon. The brutes were unshod, and with the rough roads I saw that

[017-057] their feet would very soon go to pieces. We jogged along like a

[017-058] tinker’s caravan, about five miles to the hour, as feckless a party as

[017-059] ever disgraced a highroad.

[017-060] The weather was now a drizzle, which increased my depression. Cars

[017-061] passed us and disappeared in the mist, going at thirty miles an hour to

[017-062] mock our slowness. None of us spoke, for the futility of the business

[017-063] clogged our spirits. I bit hard on my lip to curb my restlessness, and

[017-064] I think I would have sold my soul there and then for anything that

[017-065] could move fast. I don’t know any sorer trial than to be mad for speed

[017-066] and have to crawl at a snail’s pace. I was getting ripe for any kind of

[017-067] desperate venture.

[017-068] [[About]] midday we descended on a wide plain full of the marks of rich

[017-069] cultivation. Villages became frequent, and the land was studded with

[017-070] olive groves and scarred with water furrows. From what I remembered of

[017-071] the map I judged that we were coming to that champagne country near

[017-072] Siwas, which is the granary of Turkey, and the home of the true Osmanli

[017-073] stock.

[017-074] Then at the turning of the road we came to the caravanserai.

[017-075] It was a dingy, battered place, with the pink plaster falling in

[017-076] patches from its walls. There was a courtyard abutting on the road, and

[017-077] a flat-topped house with a big hole in its side. It was a long way from

[017-078] any battle-ground, and I guessed that some explosion had wrought the

[017-079] damage. Behind it, a few hundred yards off, a detachment of cavalry

[017-080] were encamped beside a stream, with their horses tied up in long lines

[017-081] of pickets.

[017-082] And by the roadside, quite alone and deserted, stood a large new

[017-083] motor-car.

[017-084] In all the road before and behind there was no man to be seen except

[017-085] the troops by the stream. The owners, whoever they were, must be inside

[017-086] the caravanserai.

[017-087] I have said I was in the mood for some desperate deed, and lo and

[017-088] behold [[fate-and-providence|providence]] had given me the chance! I coveted that car as I have

[017-089] never coveted anything on earth. At the moment all my plans had

[017-090] narrowed down to a feverish passion to get to the battle-field. We had

[017-091] to find Greenmantle at Erzerum, and once there we should have [[hilda-von-einem|Hilda]] von

[017-092] Einem’s protection. It was a time of war, and a front of brass was the

[017-093] surest safety. But, indeed, I could not figure out any plan worth

[017-094] speaking of. I saw only one thing—a fast car which might be ours.

[017-095] I said a word to the others, and we dismounted and tethered our horses

[017-096] at the near end of the courtyard. I heard the low hum of voices from

[017-097] the cavalrymen by the stream, but they were three hundred yards off and

[017-098] could not see us. Peter was sent forward to scout in the courtyard. In

[017-099] the building itself there was but one window looking on the road, and

[017-100] that was in the upper floor.

[017-101] Meantime I crawled along beside the wall to where the car stood, and

[017-102] had a look at it. It was a splendid six-cylinder affair, brand new,

[017-103] with the tyres little worn. There were seven tins of petrol stacked

[017-104] behind as well as spare tyres, and, looking in, I saw map-cases and

[017-105] field-glasses strewn on the seats as if the owners had only got out for

[017-106] a minute to stretch their legs.

[017-107] Peter came back and reported that the courtyard was empty.

[017-108] “There are men in the upper room,” he said; “more than one, for I heard

[017-109] their voices. They are moving about restlessly, and may soon be coming

[017-110] out.”

[017-111] I reckoned that there was no time to be lost, so I told the others to

[017-112] slip down the road fifty yards beyond the caravanserai and be ready to

[017-113] climb in as I passed. I had to start the infernal thing, and there

[017-114] might be shooting.

[017-115] I waited by the car till I saw them reach the right distance. I could

[017-116] hear voices from the second floor of the house and footsteps moving up

[017-117] and down. I was in a fever of anxiety, for any moment a man might come

[017-118] to the window. Then I flung myself on the starting handle and worked

[017-119] like a demon.

[017-120] The cold made the job difficult, and my heart was in my mouth, for the

[017-121] noise in that quiet place must have woke the dead. Then, by the mercy

[017-122] of Heaven, the engine started, and I sprang to the driving seat,

[017-123] released the clutch, and opened the throttle. The great car shot

[017-124] forward, and I seemed to hear behind me shrill voices. A pistol bullet

[017-125] bored through my hat, and another buried itself in a cushion beside me.

[017-126] In a second I was clear of the place and the rest of the party were

[017-127] embarking. Blenkiron got on the step and rolled himself like a sack of

[017-128] coals into the tonneau. Peter nipped up beside me, and Hussin scrambled

[017-129] in from the back over the folds of the hood. We had our baggage in our

[017-130] pockets and had nothing to carry.

[017-131] Bullets dropped round us, but did no harm. Then I heard a report at my

[017-132] ear, and out of a corner of my eye saw Peter lower his pistol.

[017-133] Presently we were out of range, and, looking back, I saw three men

[017-134] gesticulating in the middle of the road.

[017-135] “May the devil fly away with this pistol,” said Peter ruefully. “I

[017-136] never could make good shooting with a little gun. Had I had my

[017-137] rifle...”

[017-138] “What did you shoot for?” I asked in amazement. “We’ve got the fellows’

[017-139] car, and we don’t want to do them any harm.”

[017-140] “It would have saved trouble had I had my rifle,” said Peter, quietly.

[017-141] “The little man you call [[rasta-bey|Rasta]] was there, and he knew you. I heard him

[017-142] cry your name. He is an angry little man, and I observe that on this

[017-143] road there is a telegraph.”

[017-144] CHAPTER XVII.

[017-145] Trouble by The Waters of Babylon

[017-146] From that moment I date the beginning of my madness. Suddenly I forgot

[017-147] all cares and difficulties of the present and future and became

[017-148] foolishly light-hearted. We were rushing towards the great battle where

[017-149] men were busy at my proper trade. I realized how much I had loathed the

[017-150] lonely days in Germany, and still more the dawdling week in

[017-151] Constantinople. Now I was clear of it all, and bound for the clash of

[017-152] armies. It didn’t trouble me that we were on the wrong side of the

[017-153] battle line. I had a sort of instinct that the darker and wilder things

[017-154] grew the better chance for us.

[017-155] “Seems to me,” said Blenkiron, bending over me, “that this joy-ride is

[017-156] going to come to an untimely end pretty soon. Peter’s right. That young

[017-157] man will set the telegraph going, and we’ll be held up at the next

[017-158] township.”

[017-159] “He’s got to get to a telegraph office first,” I answered. “That’s

[017-160] where we have the pull on him. He’s welcome to the screws we left

[017-161] behind, and if he finds an operator before the evening I’m the worst

[017-162] kind of a Dutchman. I’m going to break all the rules and bucket this

[017-163] car for what she’s worth. Don’t you see that the nearer we get to

[017-164] Erzerum the safer we are?”

[017-165] “I don’t follow,” he said slowly. “At Erzerum I reckon they’ll be

[017-166] waiting for us with the handcuffs. Why in thunder couldn’t those hairy

[017-167] ragamuffins keep the little cuss safe? Your record’s a bit too

[017-168] precipitous, Major, for the most innocent-minded military boss.”
