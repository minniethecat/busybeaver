## Overview

Fort Palantuken, situated in the Palantuken mountain range south of Erzerum, is one of the key fortresses defending the city's southern approaches during the First World War. In *Greenmantle*, the Palantuken positions represent the strategic linchpin of the Erzerum defenses, and the Russian assault on these positions forms the military climax of the novel.

## Strategic Position

The Palantuken range lies south of Erzerum, forming a natural defensive barrier: "South lay the Palantuken range of" mountains [019-012]. Together with the Deve Boyun pass and the northern forts, Palantuken completes the ring of defenses that made Erzerum one of the most heavily fortified positions in the Ottoman Empire.

Hannay observes the "roads running to the passes" of the Palantuken and notes that "the Russian front was somewhere in the foothills of the Palantuken" [019-046]. The Russian strategy depends on taking the Palantuken positions to flank the main Erzerum defenses.

## The Russian Assault

The Russian offensive targets the Palantuken passes as a primary objective. Hannay describes how "southern columns" advance "up to the passes of the Palantuken" [019-036], while transport "was still bumping southward along the road to the Palantuken" [021-069].

Peter Pienaar makes his way through the mountains: "from Erzerum to the Palantuken pass, and he waited his chance and crossed" [020-167]. The terrain is described as rising "towards a spur of the Palantuken" with sightlines to "the topmost cliffs" [021-085].

The assault culminates in a night bombardment: "just before midnight hell broke loose round Fort Palantuken" [023-043]. Hannay sees the aftermath "up on the shoulders of the Palantuken" in the revealing daybreak, as the Russian forces consolidate their capture [023-103].

## The Palantuken Glen

In the novel's closing passages, Hannay describes "the Palantuken glen fading" as he departs, and "the great burst of light" as they emerge from the mountain shadows [024-013]. The Palantuken's fall seals the fate of Erzerum and, symbolically, the defeat of the German-Ottoman conspiracy.
