# Manie Maritz

Salomon Gerhardus "Manie" Maritz (1876–1940) was a Boer military officer who led a pro-German rebellion against the Union of South Africa in 1914. In John Buchan's 1916 novel *Greenmantle*, Maritz serves as a crucial off-stage figure whose identity provides the cover story for Richard Hannay and Peter Pienaar's covert mission into Germany.

## The Maritz Cover

Hannay and Pienaar's entire journey through Germany depends on a single fiction: that they are Boer rebels from Maritz's command, fleeing British justice after the failed 1914 uprising. Hannay adopts the name Cornelis Brandt, while Peter poses as a fellow Maritz veteran. As Sir Walter Bullivant explains, "we have passed the word to the English police to look out for a suspicious South African named Brandt, one of Maritz's rebels" [^1] — a deliberate leak designed to make the cover story credible to German intelligence.

## A Contested Reputation

The novel uses Maritz as more than a plot device: his contested reputation becomes a point of dramatic tension. Peter Pienaar, an honest Boer who fought on the British side, dismisses Maritz as "a dirty swine who would soon be hanged" and insists that "Maritz did all the fighting there was" [^1] in the German South West Africa campaign. Colonel von Stumm counters with devastating sarcasm: "Maritz now calls himself Robinson, and has a pension from his friend Botha" [^1]. This exchange — part interrogation, part political argument — reveals the bitter divisions within Boer society that the war had exposed.

## The Method Performance

Peter's advice for maintaining the cover is to "talk to each other of Maritz and our doings" [^1], embedding the false identity in natural conversation rather than rehearsed statements. He notes that "Maritz might drop in any day and blow the gaff" [^1] — meaning the real Maritz could appear and expose them — adding permanent tension to every encounter with German authorities. The method proves effective: "Maritz's men, which Peter said was the only way to play a part well" [^1], allows both travellers to pass repeated interrogations.

## Historical Note

The historical Maritz led the "Maritz Rebellion" of 1914, an armed uprising of Boer nationalists who opposed South Africa's entry into WWI on the British side. After the rebellion was crushed by forces under Louis Botha and Jan Smuts, Maritz fled to Europe. Buchan's use of Maritz reflects contemporary British awareness of the Boer rebel diaspora as a potential pro-German fifth column.
