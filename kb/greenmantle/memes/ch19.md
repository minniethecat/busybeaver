# Ch. 19: Greenmantle

[019-001] To the accompaniment of [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]]’s chuckles I did what [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] had done

[019-002] in the first minute, and fell asleep.

[019-003] When I woke it was still dark. The wagon had stopped in a courtyard

[019-004] which seemed to be shaded by great trees. The snow lay deeper here, and

[019-005] by the feel of the air we had left the city and climbed to higher

[019-006] ground. There were big buildings on one side, and on the other what

[019-007] looked like the lift of a hill. No lights were shown, the place was in

[019-008] profound gloom, but I felt the presence near me of others besides

[019-009] Hussin and the driver.

[019-010] We were hurried, Blenkiron only half awake, into an outbuilding, and

[019-011] then down some steps to a roomy cellar. There Hussin lit a lantern,

[019-012] which showed what had once been a storehouse for fruit. Old husks still

[019-013] strewed the floor and the place smelt of apples. Straw had been piled

[019-014] in corners for beds, and there was a rude table and a divan of boards

[019-015] covered with sheepskins.

[019-016] “Where are we?” I asked Hussin.

[019-017] “In the house of the Master,” he said. “You will be safe here, but you

[019-018] must keep still till the Master comes.”

[019-019] “Is the Frankish lady here?” I asked.

[019-020] Hussin nodded, and from a wallet brought out some food—raisins and cold

[019-021] meat and a loaf of bread. We fell on it like vultures, and as we ate

[019-022] Hussin disappeared. I noticed that he locked the door behind him.

[019-023] As soon as the meal was ended the others returned to their interrupted

[019-024] sleep. But I was wakeful now and my mind was sharp-set on many things.

[019-025] I got Blenkiron’s electric torch and lay down on the divan to study

[019-026] Stumm’s map.

[019-027] The first glance showed me that I had lit on a treasure. It was the

[019-028] staff map of the Erzerum defences, showing the forts and the field

[019-029] trenches, with little notes scribbled in Stumm’s neat small

[019-030] handwriting. I got out the big map which I had taken from Blenkiron,

[019-031] and made out the general lie of the land. I saw the horseshoe of Deve

[019-032] Boyun to the east which the Russian guns were battering. Stumm’s was

[019-033] just like the kind of squared artillery map we used in France, 1 in

[019-034] 10,000, with spidery red lines showing the trenches, but with the

[019-035] difference that it was the Turkish trenches that were shown in detail

[019-036] and the Russian only roughly indicated. The thing was really a

[019-037] confidential plan of the whole Erzerum _enceinte_, and would be worth

[019-038] untold gold to the enemy. No wonder Stumm had been in a wax at its

[019-039] loss.

[019-040] The [[deve-boyun|Deve Boyun]] lines seemed to me monstrously strong, and I remembered

[019-041] the merits of the Turk as a fighter behind strong defences. It looked

[019-042] as if [[russia|Russia]] were up against a second Plevna or a new [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]].

[019-043] Then I took to studying the flanks. South lay the Palantuken range of

[019-044] mountains, with forts defending the passes, where ran the roads to Mush

[019-045] and Lake Van. That side, too, looked pretty strong. North in the valley

[019-046] of the [[euphrates-river|Euphrates]] I made out two big forts, [[tafta|Tafta]] and [[kara-gubek|Kara Gubek]],

[019-047] defending the road from Olti. On this part of the map Stumm’s notes

[019-048] were plentiful, and I gave them all my attention. I remembered

[019-049] Blenkiron’s news about the Russians advancing on a broad front, for it

[019-050] was clear that Stumm was taking pains about the flank of the fortress.

[019-051] Kara Gubek was the point of interest. It stood on a rib of land between

[019-052] two peaks, which from the contour lines rose very steep. So long as it

[019-053] was held it was clear that no invader could move down the Euphrates

[019-054] glen. Stumm had appended a note to the peaks—“_not fortified_”; and

[019-055] about two miles to the north-east there was a red cross and the name

[019-056] “_[[general-prjevalsky|Prjevalsky]]_”. I assumed that to be the farthest point yet reached by

[019-057] the right wing of the Russian attack.

[019-058] Then I turned to the paper from which Stumm had copied the jottings on

[019-059] to his map. It was typewritten, and consisted of notes on different

[019-060] points. One was headed “_Kara Gubek_” and read: “_No time to fortify

[019-061] adjacent peaks. Difficult for enemy to get batteries there, but not

[019-062] impossible. This the real point of danger, for if Prjevalsky wins the

[019-063] peaks Kara Gubek and Tafta must fall, and enemy will be on left rear of

[019-064] Deve Boyun main position_.”

[019-065] I was soldier enough to see the tremendous importance of this note. On

[019-066] Kara Gubek depended the defence of Erzerum, and it was a broken reed if

[019-067] one knew where the weakness lay. Yet, searching the map again, I could

[019-068] not believe that any mortal commander would see any chance in the

[019-069] adjacent peaks, even if he thought them unfortified. That was

[019-070] information confined to the Turkish and German staff. But if it could

[019-071] be conveyed to [[grand-duke-nicholas|the Grand Duke]] he would have Erzerum in his power in a

[019-072] day. Otherwise he would go on battering at the Deve Boyun ridge for

[019-073] weeks, and long ere he won it the Gallipoli divisions would arrive, he

[019-074] would be out-numbered by two to one, and his chance would have

[019-075] vanished.

[019-076] My discovery set me pacing up and down that cellar in a perfect fever

[019-077] of excitement. I longed for wireless, a carrier pigeon, an

[019-078] aeroplane—anything to bridge over that space of half a dozen miles

[019-079] between me and the Russian lines. It was maddening to have stumbled on

[019-080] vital news and to be wholly unable to use it. How could three fugitives

[019-081] in a cellar, with the whole hornet’s nest of Turkey and Germany stirred

[019-082] up against them, hope to send this message of life and death?

[019-083] I went back to the map and examined the nearest Russian positions. They

[019-084] were carefully marked. Prjevalsky in the north, the main force beyond

[019-085] Deve Boyun, and the southern columns up to the passes of the Palantuken

[019-086] but not yet across them. I could not know which was nearest to us till

[019-087] I discovered where we were. And as I thought of this I began to see the

[019-088] rudiments of a desperate plan. It depended on Peter, now slumbering

[019-089] like a tired dog on a couch of straw.

[019-090] Hussin had locked the door and I must wait for information till he came

[019-091] back. But suddenly I noticed a trap in the roof, which had evidently

[019-092] been used for raising and lowering the cellar’s stores. It looked

[019-093] ill-fitting and might be unbarred, so I pulled the table below it, and

[019-094] found that with a little effort I could raise the flap. I knew I was

[019-095] taking immense risks, but I was so keen on my plan that I disregarded

[019-096] them. After some trouble I got the thing prised open, and catching the

[019-097] edges of the hole with my fingers raised my body and got my knees on

[019-098] the edge.

[019-099] It was the outbuilding of which our refuge was the cellar, and it was

[019-100] half filled with light. Not a soul was there, and I hunted about till I

[019-101] found what I wanted. This was a ladder leading to a sort of loft, which

[019-102] in turn gave access to the roof. Here I had to be very careful, for I

[019-103] might be overlooked from the high buildings. But by good [[fate-and-providence|luck]] there was

[019-104] a trellis for grape vines across the place, which gave a kind of

[019-105] shelter. Lying flat on my face I stared over a great expanse of

[019-106] country.

[019-107] Looking north I saw the city in a haze of morning smoke, and, beyond,

[019-108] the plain of the Euphrates and the opening of the glen where the river

[019-109] left the hills. Up there, among the snowy heights, were Tafta and Kara

[019-110] Gubek. To the east was the ridge of Deve Boyun, where the mist was

[019-111] breaking before the winter’s sun. On the roads up to it I saw transport

[019-112] moving, I saw the circle of the inner forts, but for a moment the guns

[019-113] were silent. South rose a great wall of white mountain, which I took to

[019-114] be the Palantuken. I could see the roads running to the passes, and the

[019-115] smoke of camps and horse-lines right under the cliffs.

[019-116] I had learned what I needed. We were in the outbuildings of a big

[019-117] country house two or three miles south of the city. The nearest point

[019-118] of the Russian front was somewhere in the foothills of the Palantuken.

[019-119] As I descended I heard, thin and faint and beautiful, like the cry of a

[019-120] wild bird, the muezzin from the minarets of Erzerum.

[019-121] When I dropped through the trap the others were awake. Hussin was

[019-122] setting food on the table, and viewing my descent with anxious

[019-123] disapproval.

[019-124] “It’s all right,” I said; “I won’t do it again, for I’ve found out all

[019-125] I wanted. Peter, old man, the biggest job of your life is before you!”

[019-126] CHAPTER XIX.

[019-127] Greenmantle

[019-128] Peter scarcely looked up from his breakfast.

[019-129] “I’m willing, Dick,” he said. “But you mustn’t ask me to be friends

[019-130] with Stumm. He makes my stomach cold, that one.”

[019-131] For the first time he had stopped calling me “Cornelis”. The day of

[019-132] make-believe was over for all of us.

[019-133] “Not to be friends with him,” I said, “but to bust him and all his

[019-134] kind.”

[019-135] “Then I’m ready,” said Peter cheerfully. “What is it?”

[019-136] I spread out the maps on the divan. There was no light in the place but

[019-137] Blenkiron’s electric torch, for Hussin had put out the lantern. Peter

[019-138] got his nose into the things at once, for his [[espionage|intelligence work]] in the

[019-139] Boer War had made him handy with maps. It didn’t want much telling from

[019-140] me to explain to him the importance of the one I had looted.

[019-141] “That news is worth many a million pounds,” said he, wrinkling his

[019-142] brows, and scratching delicately the tip of his left ear. It was a way

[019-143] he had when he was startled.

[019-144] “How can we get it to our friends?”

[019-145] Peter cogitated. “There is but one way. A man must take it. Once, I

[019-146] remember, when we fought the Matabele it was necessary to find out

[019-147] whether the chief Makapan was living. Some said he had died, others

[019-148] that he’d gone over the Portuguese border, but I believed he lived. No

[019-149] native could tell us, and since his kraal was well defended no runner

[019-150] could get through. So it was necessary to send a man.”

[019-151] Peter lifted up his head and laughed. “The man found the chief Makapan.

[019-152] He was very much alive, and made good shooting with a shot-gun. But the

[019-153] man brought the chief Makapan out of his kraal and handed him over to

[019-154] the Mounted Police. You remember Captain Arcoll, Dick—Jim Arcoll? Well,

[019-155] Jim laughed so much that he broke open a wound in his head, and had to

[019-156] have a doctor.”

[019-157] “You were that man, Peter,” I said.

[019-158] “_Ja_. I was the man. There are more ways of getting into kraals than

[019-159] there are ways of keeping people out.”

[019-160] “Will you take this chance?”

[019-161] “For certain, Dick. I am getting stiff with doing nothing, and if I sit

[019-162] in houses much longer I shall grow old. A man bet me five pounds on the

[019-163] ship that I could not get through a trench-line, and if there had been

[019-164] a trench-line handy I would have taken him on. I will be very happy,

[019-165] Dick, but I do not say I will succeed. It is new country to me, and I

[019-166] will be hurried, and hurry makes bad stalking.”

[019-167] I showed him what I thought the likeliest place—in the spurs of the

[019-168] Palantuken mountains. Peter’s way of doing things was all his own. He

[019-169] scraped earth and plaster out of a corner and sat down to make a little

[019-170] model of the landscape on the table, following the contours of the map.

[019-171] He did it extraordinarily neatly, for, like all great hunters, he was

[019-172] as deft as a weaver bird. He puzzled over it for a long time, and

[019-173] conned the map till he must have got it by heart. Then he took his

[019-174] field-glasses—a very good single Zeiss which was part of the spoils

[019-175] from [[rasta-bey|Rasta]]’s motor-car—and announced that he was going to follow my

[019-176] example and get on to the house-top. Presently his legs disappeared

[019-177] through the trap, and Blenkiron and I were left to our reflections.
