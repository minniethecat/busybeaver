# Ch. 14: The Lady of the Mantilla

[014-001] That was the verdict of one honest man on this ruler in Israel. The

[014-002] next night I got another from [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] on a greater than [[ennver-pasha|Enver]]. He had

[014-003] been out alone and had come back pretty late, with his face grey and

[014-004] drawn with pain. The food we ate—not at all bad of its kind—and the

[014-005] cold east wind played havoc with his dyspepsia. I can see him yet,

[014-006] boiling milk on a spirit-lamp, while [[peter-pienaar|Peter]] worked at a Primus stove to

[014-007] get him a hot-water bottle. He was using horrid language about his

[014-008] inside.

[014-009] “My God, Major, if I were you with a sound stomach I’d fairly conquer

[014-010] the world. As it is, I’ve got to do my work with half my mind, while

[014-011] the other half is dwelling in my intestines. I’m like the child in the

[014-012] Bible that had a fox gnawing at its vitals.”

[014-013] He got his milk boiling and began to sip it.

[014-014] “I’ve been to see our pretty landlady,” he said. “She sent for me and I

[014-015] hobbled off with a grip full of plans, for she’s mighty set on

[014-016] Mesopotamy.”

[014-017] “Anything about Greenmantle?” I asked eagerly.

[014-018] “Why, no, but I have reached one conclusion. I opine that the hapless

[014-019] prophet has no sort of time with that lady. I opine that he will soon

[014-020] wish himself in Paradise. For if Almighty God ever created a female

[014-021] devil it’s Madame von Einem.”

[014-022] He sipped a little more milk with a grave face.

[014-023] “That isn’t my duodenal dyspepsia, Major. It’s the verdict of a ripe

[014-024] experience, for I have a cool and penetrating judgement, even if I’ve a

[014-025] deranged stomach. And I give it as my considered conclusion that that

[014-026] woman’s mad and bad—but principally bad.”

[014-027] CHAPTER XIV.

[014-028] The Lady of the Mantilla

[014-029] Since that first night I had never clapped eyes on [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]. He had gone

[014-030] clean out of the world, and Blenkiron and I waited anxiously for a word

[014-031] of news. Our own business was in good trim, for we were presently going

[014-032] east towards [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]], but unless we learned more about Greenmantle

[014-033] our journey would be a grotesque failure. And learn about Greenmantle

[014-034] we could not, for nobody by word or deed suggested his existence, and

[014-035] it was impossible of course for us to ask questions. Our only hope was

[014-036] Sandy, for what we wanted to know was the prophet’s whereabouts and his

[014-037] plans. I suggested to Blenkiron that we might do more to cultivate Frau

[014-038] von Einem, but he shut his jaw like a rat-trap.

[014-039] “There’s nothing doing for us in that quarter,” he said. “That’s the

[014-040] most dangerous woman on earth; and if she got any kind of notion that

[014-041] we were wise about her pet schemes I reckon you and I would very soon

[014-042] be in the [[bosporus|Bosporus]].”

[014-043] This was all very well; but what was going to happen if the two of us

[014-044] were bundled off to [[baghdad|Baghdad]] with instructions to wash away the British?

[014-045] Our time was getting pretty short, and I doubted if we could spin out

[014-046] more than three days more in Constantinople. I felt just as I had felt

[014-047] with Stumm that last night when I was about to be packed off to Cairo

[014-048] and saw no way of avoiding it. Even Blenkiron was getting anxious. He

[014-049] played Patience incessantly, and was disinclined to talk. I tried to

[014-050] find out something from the servants, but they either knew nothing or

[014-051] wouldn’t speak—the former, I think. I kept my eyes lifting, too, as I

[014-052] walked about the streets, but there was no sign anywhere of the skin

[014-053] coats or the weird stringed instruments. The whole Company of the Rosy

[014-054] Hours seemed to have melted into the air, and I began to wonder if they

[014-055] had ever existed.

[014-056] Anxiety made me restless, and restlessness made me want exercise. It

[014-057] was no good walking about the city. The weather had become foul again,

[014-058] and I was sick of the smells and the squalor and the flea-bitten

[014-059] crowds. So Blenkiron and I got horses, Turkish cavalry mounts with

[014-060] heads like trees, and went out through the suburbs into the open

[014-061] country.

[014-062] It was a grey drizzling afternoon, with the beginnings of a sea fog

[014-063] which hid the Asiatic shores of the straits. It wasn’t easy to find

[014-064] open ground for a gallop, for there were endless small patches of

[014-065] cultivation and the gardens of country houses. We kept on the high land

[014-066] above the sea, and when we reached a bit of downland came on squads of

[014-067] Turkish soldiers digging trenches. Whenever we let the horses go we had

[014-068] to pull up sharp for a digging party or a stretch of barbed wire. Coils

[014-069] of the beastly thing were lying loose everywhere, and Blenkiron nearly

[014-070] took a nasty toss over one. Then we were always being stopped by

[014-071] sentries and having to show our passes. Still the ride did us good and

[014-072] shook up our livers, and by the time we turned for home I was feeling

[014-073] more like a white man.

[014-074] We jogged back in the short winter twilight, past the wooded grounds of

[014-075] white villas, held up every few minutes by transport-wagons and

[014-076] companies of soldiers. The rain had come on in real earnest, and it was

[014-077] two very bedraggled horsemen that crawled along the muddy lanes. As we

[014-078] passed one villa, shut in by a high white wall, a pleasant smell of

[014-079] wood smoke was wafted towards us, which made me sick for the burning

[014-080] veld. My ear, too, caught the twanging of a zither, which somehow

[014-081] reminded me of the afternoon in Kuprasso’s garden-house.

[014-082] I pulled up and proposed to investigate, but Blenkiron very testily

[014-083] declined.

[014-084] “Zithers are as common here as fleas,” he said. “You don’t want to be

[014-085] fossicking around somebody’s stables and find a horse-boy entertaining

[014-086] his friends. They don’t like visitors in this country; and you’ll be

[014-087] asking for trouble if you go inside those walls. I guess it’s some old

[014-088] Buzzard’s harem.” Buzzard was his own private peculiar name for the

[014-089] Turk, for he said he had had as a boy a natural history book with a

[014-090] picture of a bird called the turkey-buzzard, and couldn’t get out of

[014-091] the habit of applying it to the Ottoman people.

[014-092] I wasn’t convinced, so I tried to mark down the place. It seemed to be

[014-093] about three miles out from the city, at the end of a steep lane on the

[014-094] inland side of the hill coming from the Bosporus. I fancied somebody of

[014-095] distinction lived there, for a little farther on we met a big empty

[014-096] motor-car snorting its way up, and I had a notion that the car belonged

[014-097] to the walled villa.

[014-098] Next day Blenkiron was in grievous trouble with his dyspepsia. [[About]]

[014-099] midday he was compelled to lie down, and having nothing better to do I

[014-100] had out the horses again and took Peter with me. It was funny to see

[014-101] Peter in a Turkish army-saddle, riding with the long Boer stirrup and

[014-102] the slouch of the backveld.

[014-103] That afternoon was unfortunate from the start. It was not the mist and

[014-104] drizzle of the day before, but a stiff northern gale which blew sheets

[014-105] of rain in our faces and numbed our bridle hands. We took the same

[014-106] road, but pushed west of the trench-digging parties and got to a

[014-107] shallow valley with a white village among the cypresses. Beyond that

[014-108] there was a very respectable road which brought us to the top of a

[014-109] crest that in clear weather must have given a fine prospect. Then we

[014-110] turned our horses, and I shaped our course so as to strike the top of

[014-111] the long lane that abutted on the down. I wanted to investigate the

[014-112] white villa.

[014-113] But we hadn’t gone far on our road back before we got into trouble. It

[014-114] arose out of a sheep-dog, a yellow mongrel brute that came at us like a

[014-115] thunderbolt. It took a special fancy to Peter, and bit savagely at his

[014-116] horse’s heels and sent it capering off the road. I should have warned

[014-117] him, but I did not realize what was happening, till too late. For

[014-118] Peter, being accustomed to mongrels in Kaffir kraals, took a summary

[014-119] way with the pest. Since it despised his whip, he out with his pistol

[014-120] and put a bullet through its head.

[014-121] The echoes of the shot had scarcely died away when the row began. A big

[014-122] fellow appeared running towards us, shouting wildly. I guessed he was

[014-123] the dog’s owner, and proposed to pay no attention. But his cries

[014-124] summoned two other fellows—soldiers by the look of them—who closed in

[014-125] on us, unslinging their rifles as they ran. My first idea was to show

[014-126] them our heels, but I had no desire to be shot in the back, and they

[014-127] looked like men who wouldn’t stop short of shooting. So we slowed down

[014-128] and faced them.

[014-129] They made as savage-looking a trio as you would want to avoid. The

[014-130] shepherd looked as if he had been dug up, a dirty ruffian with matted

[014-131] hair and a beard like a bird’s nest. The two soldiers stood staring

[014-132] with sullen faces, fingering their guns, while the other chap raved and

[014-133] stormed and kept pointing at Peter, whose mild eyes stared unwinkingly

[014-134] at his assailant.

[014-135] The mischief was that neither of us had a word of Turkish. I tried

[014-136] German, but it had no effect. We sat looking at them and they stood

[014-137] storming at us, and it was fast getting dark. Once I turned my horse

[014-138] round as if to proceed, and the two soldiers jumped in front of me.

[014-139] They jabbered among themselves, and then one said very slowly: “He ...

[014-140] want ... pounds,” and he held up five fingers. They evidently saw by

[014-141] the cut of our jib that we weren’t Germans.

[014-142] “I’ll be hanged if he gets a penny,” I said angrily, and the

[014-143] conversation languished.

[014-144] The situation was getting serious, so I spoke a word to Peter. The

[014-145] soldiers had their rifles loose in their hands, and before they could

[014-146] lift them we had the pair covered with our pistols.

[014-147] “If you move,” I said, “you are dead.” They understood that all right

[014-148] and stood stock still, while the shepherd stopped his raving and took

[014-149] to muttering like a gramophone when the record is finished.

[014-150] “Drop your guns,” I said sharply. “Quick, or we shoot.”

[014-151] The tone, if not the words, conveyed my meaning. Still staring at us,

[014-152] they let the rifles slide to the ground. The next second we had forced

[014-153] our horses on the top of them, and the three were off like rabbits. I

[014-154] sent a shot over their heads to encourage them. Peter dismounted and

[014-155] tossed the guns into a bit of scrub where they would take some finding.

[014-156] This hold-up had wasted time. By now it was getting very dark, and we

[014-157] hadn’t ridden a mile before it was black night. It was an annoying

[014-158] predicament, for I had completely lost my bearings and at the best I

[014-159] had only a foggy notion of the lie of the land. The best plan seemed to

[014-160] be to try and get to the top of a rise in the hope of seeing the lights

[014-161] of the city, but all the countryside was so pockety that it was hard to

[014-162] strike the right kind of rise.

[014-163] We had to trust to Peter’s instinct. I asked him where our line lay,

[014-164] and he sat very still for a minute sniffing the air. Then he pointed

[014-165] the direction. It wasn’t what I would have taken myself, but on a point

[014-166] like that he was pretty near infallible.

[014-167] Presently we came to a long slope which cheered me. But at the top

[014-168] there was no light visible anywhere—only a black void like the inside

[014-169] of a shell. As I stared into the gloom it seemed to me that there were

[014-170] patches of deeper darkness that might be woods.
