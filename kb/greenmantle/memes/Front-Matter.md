[P01-076] To

[P01-077] Caroline Grosvenor

[P01-078] During the past year, in the intervals of an active life, I have amused

[P01-079] myself with constructing this tale. It has been scribbled in every kind

[P01-080] of odd place and moment—in England and abroad, during long journeys, in

[P01-081] half-hours between graver tasks; and it bears, I fear, the mark of its

[P01-082] gipsy begetting. But it has amused me to write, and I shall be well

[P01-083] repaid if it amuses you—and a few others—to read.

[P01-084] Let no man or woman call its events improbable. The war has driven that

[P01-085] word from our vocabulary, and melodrama has become the prosiest

[P01-086] realism. Things unimagined before happen daily to our friends by sea

[P01-087] and land. The one chance in a thousand is habitually taken, and as

[P01-088] often as not succeeds. Coincidence, like some new Briareus, stretches a

[P01-089] hundred long arms hourly across the earth. Some day, when the full

[P01-090] history is written—sober history with ample documents—the poor romancer

[P01-091] will give up business and fall to reading Miss Austen in a hermitage.

[P01-092] The characters of the tale, if you think hard, you will recall. [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]]

[P01-093] you know well. That great spirit was last heard of at Basra, where he

[P01-094] occupies the post that once was [[harry-bullivant|Harry]] [[sir-walter-bullivant|Bullivant]]’s. Richard Hannay is

[P01-095] where he longed to be, commanding his battalion on the ugliest bit of

[P01-096] front in the West. Mr [[john-blenkiron|John S. Blenkiron]], full of [[courage-and-honour|honour]] and wholly

[P01-097] cured of dyspepsia, has returned to the States, after vainly

[P01-098] endeavouring to take Peter with him. As for Peter, he has attained the

[P01-099] height of his ambition. He has shaved his beard and joined the Flying

[P01-100] Corps.

[P01-101] CHAPTER I.

[P01-102] A Mission is Proposed

[P01-103] I had just finished breakfast and was filling my pipe when I got

[P01-104] Bullivant’s telegram. It was at Furling, the big country house in

[P01-105] Hampshire where I had come to convalesce after [[loos|Loos]], and Sandy, who was

[P01-106] in the same case, was hunting for the marmalade. I flung him the flimsy

[P01-107] with the blue strip pasted down on it, and he whistled.

[P01-108] “Hullo, Dick, you’ve got the battalion. Or maybe it’s a staff billet.

[P01-109] You’ll be a blighted brass-hat, coming it heavy over the hard-working

[P01-110] regimental officer. And to think of the language you’ve wasted on

[P01-111] brass-hats in your time!”

[P01-112] I sat and thought for a bit, for the name “Bullivant’ carried me back

[P01-113] eighteen months to the hot summer before the war. I had not seen the

[P01-114] man since, though I had read about him in the papers. For more than a

[P01-115] year I had been a busy battalion officer, with no other thought than to

[P01-116] hammer a lot of raw stuff into good soldiers. I had succeeded pretty

[P01-117] well, and there was no prouder man on earth than Richard Hannay when he

[P01-118] took his Lennox Highlanders over the parapets on that glorious and

[P01-119] bloody 25th day of September. Loos was no picnic, and we had had some

[P01-120] ugly bits of scrapping before that, but the worst bit of the campaign I

[P01-121] had seen was a tea-party to the show I had been in with Bullivant

[P01-122] before the war started. [[[richard-hannay|Major Hannay]]’s narrative of this affair has

[P01-123] been published under the title of _The Thirty-nine Steps_.]

[P01-124] The sight of his name on a telegram form seemed to change all my

[P01-125] outlook on life. I had been hoping for the command of the battalion,

[P01-126] and looking forward to being in at the finish with Brother Boche. But

[P01-127] this message jerked my thoughts on to a new road. There might be other

[P01-128] things in the war than straightforward fighting. Why on earth should

[P01-129] the [[british-intelligence|Foreign Office]] want to see an obscure Major of the New Army, and

[P01-130] want to see him in double-quick time?

[P01-131] “I’m going up to town by the ten train,” I announced; “I’ll be back in

[P01-132] time for dinner.”

[P01-133] “Try my tailor,” said Sandy. “He’s got a very nice taste in red tabs.

[P01-134] You can use my name.”

[P01-135] An idea struck me. “You’re pretty well all right now. If I wire for

[P01-136] you, will you pack your own kit and mine and join me?”

[P01-137] “Right-o! I’ll accept a job on your staff if they give you a corps. If

[P01-138] so be as you come down tonight, be a good chap and bring a barrel of

[P01-139] oysters from Sweeting’s.”

[P01-140] I travelled up to [[london|London]] in a regular November drizzle, which cleared

[P01-141] up about Wimbledon to watery sunshine. I never could stand London

[P01-142] during the war. It seemed to have lost its bearings and broken out into

[P01-143] all manner of badges and uniforms which did not fit in with my notion

[P01-144] of it. One felt the war more in its streets than in the field, or

[P01-145] rather one felt the confusion of war without feeling the purpose. I

[P01-146] dare say it was all right; but since August 1914 I never spent a day in

[P01-147] town without coming home depressed to my boots.

[P01-148] I took a taxi and drove straight to the Foreign Office. Sir Walter did

[P01-149] not keep me waiting long. But when his secretary took me to his room I

[P01-150] would not have recognized the man I had known eighteen months before.

[P01-151] His big frame seemed to have dropped flesh and there was a stoop in the

[P01-152] square shoulders. His face had lost its rosiness and was red in

[P01-153] patches, like that of a man who gets too little fresh air. His hair was

[P01-154] much greyer and very thin about the temples, and there were lines of

[P01-155] overwork below the eyes. But the eyes were the same as before, keen and

[P01-156] kindly and shrewd, and there was no change in the firm set of the jaw.

[P01-157] “We must on no account be disturbed for the next hour,” he told his

[P01-158] secretary. When the young man had gone he went across to both doors and

[P01-159] turned the keys in them.

[P01-160] “Well, Major Hannay,” he said, flinging himself into a chair beside the

[P01-161] fire. “How do you like soldiering?”

[P01-162] “Right enough,” I said, “though this isn’t just the kind of war I would

[P01-163] have picked myself. It’s a comfortless, bloody business. But we’ve got

[P01-164] the measure of the old Boche now, and it’s dogged as does it. I count

[P01-165] on getting back to the front in a week or two.”

[P01-166] “Will you get the battalion?” he asked. He seemed to have followed my

[P01-167] doings pretty closely.

[P01-168] “I believe I’ve a good chance. I’m not in this show for honour and

[P01-169] glory, though. I want to do the best I can, but I wish to heaven it was

[P01-170] over. All I think of is coming out of it with a whole skin.”

[P01-171] He laughed. “You do yourself an injustice. What about the forward

[P01-172] observation post at the Lone Tree? You forgot about the whole skin

[P01-173] then.”

[P01-174] I felt myself getting red. “That was all rot,” I said, “and I can’t

[P01-175] think who told you about it. I hated the job, but I had to do it to

[P01-176] prevent my subalterns going to glory. They were a lot of fire-eating

[P01-177] young lunatics. If I had sent one of them he’d have gone on his knees

[P01-178] to Providence and asked for trouble.”

[P01-179] Sir Walter was still grinning.

[P01-180] “I’m not questioning your caution. You have the rudiments of it, or our

[P01-181] friends of the Black Stone would have gathered you in at our last merry

[P01-182] meeting. I would question it as little as your courage. What exercises

[P01-183] my mind is whether it is best employed in the trenches.”

[P01-184] “Is the War Office dissatisfied with me?” I asked sharply.

[P01-185] “They are profoundly satisfied. They propose to give you command of

[P01-186] your battalion. Presently, if you escape a stray bullet, you will no

[P01-187] doubt be a Brigadier. It is a wonderful war for youth and brains. But

[P01-188] ... I take it you are in this business to serve your country, Hannay?”

[P01-189] “I reckon I am,” I said. “I am certainly not in it for my health.”

[P01-190] He looked at my leg, where the doctors had dug out the shrapnel

[P01-191] fragments, and smiled quizzically.

[P01-192] “Pretty fit again?” he asked.

[P01-193] “Tough as a sjambok. I thrive on the racket and eat and sleep like a

[P01-194] schoolboy.”

[P01-195] He got up and stood with his back to the fire, his eyes staring

[P01-196] abstractedly out of the window at the wintry park.

[P01-197] “It is a great game, and you are the man for it, no doubt. But there

[P01-198] are others who can play it, for soldiering today asks for the average

[P01-199] rather than the exception in human nature. It is like a big machine

[P01-200] where the parts are standardized. You are fighting, not because you are

[P01-201] short of a job, but because you want to help England. How if you could

[P01-202] help her better than by commanding a battalion—or a brigade—or, if it

[P01-203] comes to that, a division? How if there is a thing which you alone can

[P01-204] do? Not some _embusque_ business in an office, but a thing compared to

[P01-205] which your fight at Loos was a Sunday-school picnic. You are not afraid

[P01-206] of danger? Well, in this job you would not be fighting with an army

[P01-207] around you, but alone. You are fond of tackling difficulties? Well, I

[P01-208] can give you a task which will try all your powers. Have you anything

[P01-209] to say?”

[P01-210] My heart was beginning to thump uncomfortably. Sir Walter was not the

[P01-211] man to pitch a case too high.

[P01-212] “I am a soldier,” I said, “and under orders.”

[P01-213] “True; but what I am about to propose does not come by any conceivable

[P01-214] stretch within the scope of a soldier’s duties. I shall perfectly

[P01-215] understand if you decline. You will be acting as I should act myself—as

[P01-216] any sane man would. I would not press you for worlds. If you wish it, I

[P01-217] will not even make the proposal, but let you go here and now, and wish

[P01-218] you good [[fate-and-providence|luck]] with your battalion. I do not wish to perplex a good

[P01-219] soldier with impossible decisions.”

[P01-220] This piqued me and put me on my mettle.

[P01-221] “I am not going to run away before the guns fire. Let me hear what you

[P01-222] propose.”

[P01-223] Sir Walter crossed to a cabinet, unlocked it with a key from his chain,

[P01-224] and took a piece of paper from a drawer. It looked like an ordinary

[P01-225] half-sheet of note-paper.

[P01-226] “I take it,” he said, “that your travels have not extended to the

[P01-227] East.”

[P01-228] “No,” I said, “barring a shooting trip in East Africa.”

[P01-229] “Have you by any chance been following the present campaign there?”

[P01-230] “I’ve read the newspapers pretty regularly since I went to hospital.

[P01-231] I’ve got some pals in the [[mesopotamia|Mesopotamia]] show, and of course I’m keen to

[P01-232] know what is going to happen at [[gallipoli|Gallipoli]] and Salonika. I gather that

[P01-233] Egypt is pretty safe.”

[P01-234] “If you will give me your attention for ten minutes I will supplement

[P01-235] your newspaper reading.”

[P01-236] Sir Walter lay back in an arm-chair and spoke to the ceiling. It was

[P01-237] the best story, the clearest and the fullest, I had ever got of any bit

[P01-238] of the war. He told me just how and why and when Turkey had left the

[P01-239] rails. I heard about her grievances over our seizure of her ironclads,

[P01-240] of the mischief the coming of the _Goeben_ had wrought, of [[ennver-pasha|Enver]] and

[P01-241] his precious Committee and the way they had got a cinch on the old

[P01-242] Turk. When he had spoken for a bit, he began to question me.
