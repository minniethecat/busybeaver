# Ch. 10: The Garden-House of Suliman the Red (cont'd, part 2)

[010-001] I whistled and showed it to [[peter-pienaar|Peter]]. The sooner we were at Constantinople

[010-002] the better, and I prayed we would get there before the fellow who sent

[010-003] this wire repeated it and got the commandant to send on the message and

[010-004] have us held up at [[chataldja|Chataldja]]. For my back had fairly got stiffened

[010-005] about these munitions, and I was going to take any risk to see them

[010-006] safely delivered to their proper owner. Peter couldn’t understand me at

[010-007] all. He still hankered after a grand destruction of the lot somewhere

[010-008] down the railway. But then, this wasn’t the line of Peter’s profession,

[010-009] and his pride was not at stake. We had a mortally slow journey. It was

[010-010] bad enough in Bulgaria, but when we crossed the frontier at a place

[010-011] called Mustafa Pasha we struck the real supineness of [[eastern-front|the East]]. Happily

[010-012] I found a German officer there who had some notion of hustling, and,

[010-013] after all, it was his interest to get the stuff moved. It was the

[010-014] morning of the 16th, after Peter and I had been living like pigs on

[010-015] black bread and condemned tin stuff, that we came in sight of a blue

[010-016] sea on our right hand and knew we couldn’t be very far from the end.

[010-017] It was jolly near the end in another sense. We stopped at a station and

[010-018] were stretching our legs on the platform when I saw a familiar figure

[010-019] approaching. It was [[rasta-bey|Rasta]], with half a dozen Turkish gendarmes.

[010-020] I called Peter, and we clambered into the truck next our horse-box. I

[010-021] had been half expecting some move like this and had made a plan.

[010-022] The Turk swaggered up and addressed us. “You can get back to [[rustchuk|Rustchuk]],”

[010-023] he said. “I take over from you here. Hand me the papers.”

[010-024] “Is this Chataldja?” I asked innocently.

[010-025] “It is the end of your affair,” he said haughtily. “Quick, or it will

[010-026] be the worse for you.”

[010-027] “Now, look here, my son,” I said; “you’re a kid and know nothing. I

[010-028] hand over to [[von-oesterzee|General von Oesterzee]] and to no one else.”

[010-029] “You are in Turkey,” he cried, “and will obey the Turkish Government.”

[010-030] “I’ll obey the Government right enough,” I said; “but if you’re the

[010-031] Government I could make a better one with a bib and a rattle.”

[010-032] He said something to his men, who unslung their rifles.

[010-033] “Please don’t begin shooting,” I said. “There are twelve armed guards

[010-034] in this train who will take their orders from me. Besides, I and my

[010-035] friend can shoot a bit.”

[010-036] “Fool!” he cried, getting very angry. “I can order up a regiment in

[010-037] five minutes.”

[010-038] “Maybe you can,” I said; “but observe the situation. I am sitting on

[010-039] enough toluol to blow up this countryside. If you dare to come aboard I

[010-040] will shoot you. If you call in your regiment I will tell you what I’ll

[010-041] do. I’ll fire this stuff, and I reckon they’ll be picking up the bits

[010-042] of you and your regiment off the [[gallipoli|Gallipoli Peninsula]].”

[010-043] He had put up a bluff—a poor one—and I had called it. He saw I meant

[010-044] what I said, and became silken.

[010-045] “Good-bye, sir,” he said. “You have had a fair chance and rejected it.

[010-046] We shall meet again soon, and you will be sorry for your insolence.”

[010-047] He strutted away and it was all I could do to keep from running after

[010-048] him. I wanted to lay him over my knee and spank him.

[010-049] We got safely to Chataldja, and were received by von Oesterzee like

[010-050] long-lost brothers. He was the regular gunner-officer, not thinking

[010-051] about anything except his guns and shells. I had to wait about three

[010-052] hours while he was checking the stuff with the invoices, and then he

[010-053] gave me a receipt which I still possess. I told him about Rasta, and he

[010-054] agreed that I had done right. It didn’t make him as mad as I expected,

[010-055] because, you see, he got his stuff safe in any case. It was only that

[010-056] the wretched Turks had to pay twice for the lot of it.

[010-057] He gave Peter and me luncheon, and was altogether very civil and

[010-058] inclined to talk about the war. I would have liked to hear what he had

[010-059] to say, for it would have been something to get the inside view of

[010-060] Germany’s Eastern campaign, but I did not dare to wait. Any moment

[010-061] there might arrive an incriminating wire from Rustchuk. Finally he lent

[010-062] us a car to take us the few miles to the city.

[010-063] So it came about that at five past three on the 16th day of January,

[010-064] with only the clothes we stood up in, Peter and I entered

[010-065] Constantinople.

[010-066] I was in considerable spirits, for I had got the final lap successfully

[010-067] over, and I was looking forward madly to meeting my friends; but, all

[010-068] the same, the first sight was a mighty disappointment. I don’t quite

[010-069] know what I had expected—a sort of fairyland Eastern city, all white

[010-070] marble and blue water, and stately Turks in surplices, and veiled

[010-071] houris, and roses and nightingales, and some sort of string band

[010-072] discoursing sweet music. I had forgotten that winter is pretty much the

[010-073] same everywhere. It was a drizzling day, with a south-east wind

[010-074] blowing, and the streets were long troughs of mud. The first part I

[010-075] struck looked like a dingy colonial suburb—wooden houses and corrugated

[010-076] iron roofs, and endless dirty, sallow children. There was a cemetery, I

[010-077] remember, with Turks’ caps stuck at the head of each grave. Then we got

[010-078] into narrow steep streets which descended to a kind of big canal. I saw

[010-079] what I took to be mosques and minarets, and they were about as

[010-080] impressive as factory chimneys. By and by we crossed a bridge, and paid

[010-081] a penny for the privilege. If I had known it was the famous Golden Horn

[010-082] I would have looked at it with more interest, but I saw nothing save a

[010-083] lot of moth-eaten barges and some queer little boats like gondolas.

[010-084] Then we came into busier streets, where ramshackle cabs drawn by lean

[010-085] horses spluttered through the mud. I saw one old fellow who looked like

[010-086] my notion of a Turk, but most of the population had the appearance of

[010-087] [[london|London]] old-clothes men. All but the soldiers, Turk and German, who

[010-088] seemed well-set-up fellows.

[010-089] Peter had paddled along at my side like a faithful dog, not saying a

[010-090] word, but clearly not approving of this wet and dirty metropolis.

[010-091] “Do you know that we are being followed, Cornelis?” he said suddenly,

[010-092] “ever since we came into this evil-smelling dorp.”

[010-093] Peter was infallible in a thing like that. The news scared me badly,

[010-094] for I feared that the telegram had come to Chataldja. Then I thought it

[010-095] couldn’t be that, for if von Oesterzee had wanted me he wouldn’t have

[010-096] taken the trouble to stalk me. It was more likely my friend Rasta.

[010-097] I found the ferry of [[ratchik|Ratchik]] by asking a soldier and a German sailor

[010-098] there told me where the Kurdish Bazaar was. He pointed up a steep

[010-099] street which ran past a high block of warehouses with every window

[010-100] broken. [[sandy-arbuthnot|Sandy]] had said the left-hand side coming down, so it must be

[010-101] the right-hand side going up. We plunged into it, and it was the

[010-102] filthiest place of all. The wind whistled up it and stirred the

[010-103] garbage. It seemed densely inhabited, for at all the doors there were

[010-104] groups of people squatting, with their heads covered, though scarcely a

[010-105] window showed in the blank walls.

[010-106] The street corkscrewed endlessly. Sometimes it seemed to stop; then it

[010-107] found a hole in the opposing masonry and edged its way in. Often it was

[010-108] almost pitch dark; then would come a greyish twilight where it opened

[010-109] out to the width of a decent lane. To find a house in that murk was no

[010-110] easy job, and by the time we had gone a quarter of a mile I began to

[010-111] fear we had missed it. It was no good asking any of the crowd we met.

[010-112] They didn’t look as if they understood any civilized tongue.

[010-113] At last we stumbled on it—a tumble-down coffee house, with [[kuprasso|A. Kuprasso]]

[010-114] above the door in queer amateur lettering. There was a lamp burning

[010-115] inside, and two or three men smoking at small wooden tables.

[010-116] We ordered coffee, thick black stuff like treacle, which Peter

[010-117] anathematized. A negro brought it, and I told him in German I wanted to

[010-118] speak to Mr Kuprasso. He paid no attention, so I shouted louder at him,

[010-119] and the noise brought a man out of the back parts.

[010-120] He was a fat, oldish fellow with a long nose, very like the Greek

[010-121] traders you see on the Zanzibar coast. I beckoned to him and he waddled

[010-122] forward, smiling oilily. Then I asked him what he would take, and he

[010-123] replied, in very halting German, that he would have a sirop.

[010-124] “You are Mr Kuprasso,” I said. “I wanted to show this place to my

[010-125] friend. He has heard of your garden-house and the fun there.”

[010-126] “The Signor is mistaken. I have no garden-house.”

[010-127] “Rot,” I said; “I’ve been here before, my boy. I recall your shanty at

[010-128] the back and many merry nights there. What was it you called it? Oh, I

[010-129] remember—the Garden-House of Suliman the Red.”

[010-130] He put his finger to his lip and looked incredibly sly. “The Signor

[010-131] remembers that. But that was in the old happy days before war came. The

[010-132] place is long since shut. The people here are too poor to dance and

[010-133] sing.”

[010-134] “All the same I would like to have another look at it,” I said, and I

[010-135] slipped an English sovereign into his hand.

[010-136] He glanced at it in surprise and his manner changed. “The Signor is a

[010-137] Prince, and I will do his will.” He clapped his hands and the negro

[010-138] appeared, and at his nod took his place behind a little side-counter.

[010-139] “Follow me,” he said, and led us through a long, noisome passage, which

[010-140] was pitch dark and very unevenly paved. Then he unlocked a door and

[010-141] with a swirl the wind caught it and blew it back on us.

[010-142] We were looking into a mean little yard, with on one side a high

[010-143] curving wall, evidently of great age, with bushes growing in the cracks

[010-144] of it. Some scraggy myrtles stood in broken pots, and nettles

[010-145] flourished in a corner. At one end was a wooden building like a

[010-146] dissenting chapel, but painted a dingy scarlet. Its windows and

[010-147] skylights were black with dirt, and its door, tied up with rope,

[010-148] flapped in the wind.

[010-149] “Behold the Pavilion,” Kuprasso said proudly.

[010-150] “That is the old place,” I observed with feeling. “What times I’ve seen

[010-151] there! Tell me, Mr Kuprasso, do you ever open it now?”

[010-152] He put his thick lips to my ear.

[010-153] “If the Signor will be silent I will tell him. It is sometimes open—not

[010-154] often. Men must amuse themselves even in war. Some of the German

[010-155] officers come here for their pleasure, and but last week we had the

[010-156] ballet of Mademoiselle Cici. The police approve—but not often, for this

[010-157] is no time for too much gaiety. I will tell you a secret. Tomorrow

[010-158] afternoon there will be dancing—wonderful dancing! Only a few of my

[010-159] patrons know. Who, think you, will be here?”

[010-160] He bent his head closer and said in a whisper—

[010-161] “The Compagnie des Heures Roses.”

[010-162] “Oh, indeed,” I said with a proper tone of respect, though I hadn’t a

[010-163] notion what he meant.

[010-164] “Will the Signor wish to come?”

[010-165] “Sure,” I said. “Both of us. We’re all for the rosy hours.”

[010-166] “Then the fourth hour after midday. Walk straight through the cafe and

[010-167] one will be there to unlock the door. You are new-comers here? Take the

[010-168] advice of Angelo Kuprasso and avoid the streets after nightfall.

[010-169] Stamboul is no safe place nowadays for quiet men.” I asked him to name

[010-170] a hotel, and he rattled off a list from which I chose one that sounded

[010-171] modest and in keeping with our get-up. It was not far off, only a

[010-172] hundred yards to the right at the top of the hill.
