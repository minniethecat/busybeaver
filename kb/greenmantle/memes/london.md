# London

**London** in *Greenmantle* serves as the headquarters of British Intelligence, the nerve centre from which Sir Walter Bullivant orchestrates Richard Hannay's secret mission to the Near East. It is the departure point and the symbolic anchor of British order, contrasted throughout the novel with the chaotic and exotic landscapes of the Ottoman Empire.

## Role in the Narrative

### Hannay's Recruitment

Hannay travels to London at the novel's opening, summoned by Sir Walter Bullivant. The city is described in bleak November weather, reflecting Hannay's restless mood after his convalescence from the Battle of Loos:

> I travelled up to London in a regular November drizzle, which cleared up about Wimbledon to a pale, grudging sunshine. I never could stand London [P01-140] [P01-141]

> I took a taxi and drove straight to the Foreign Office. Sir Walter did not keep me waiting long. But when his secretary took me to his room I would not have recognized the man I had known eighteen months before. His big frame seemed to have dropped flesh and there was a stoop in the square shoulders [P01-148] [P01-149] [P01-150]

### The London Flat Briefing

In Hannay's Park Lane flat, Sir Walter's enigmatic briefing leaves the team with only a scrap of paper and a sense of looming peril:

> "A clue may be dumb in London and shout aloud at Baghdad." [001-136] [001-137]

> Here were we three simpletons sitting in a London flat and projecting a mission into the enemy's citadel without an idea what we were to do or how we were to do it [001-154] [001-155]

### Intelligence Headquarters

London functions as the command centre for British secret operations. It is from London that Bullivant briefs Hannay on the mysterious plot involving "*the three of the East*" and dispatches him on his mission.

### Contrast with the Continent

Once abroad, Hannay finds London serves as his yardstick for measuring foreign places. Crossing Germany by train, the bright garish stations strike him against the memory of home:

> After the murk of London it was queer to slip through garish stations with a hundred arc lights glowing, and to see long lines of lamps running to the horizon [003-086] [003-087]

Later, Berlin itself is judged wanting:

> depressing -- more depressing than London. I don't know how to put it, but the whole big concern seemed to have no soul in it, to be like a big factory instead of a city [003-139] [003-140]

### Return Point

Throughout the novel, London remains the ultimate destination -- the place to which Hannay must report. When his plans go awry, the imperative is always to return to London and inform Sir Walter:

> ...to go back to London and tell Sir Walter the game was up [006-091]

## Geographic and Symbolic Function

London serves a dual function in the novel:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Narrative hub** | Starting and ending point of Hannay's circular journey |
| **Symbolic anchor** | Represents order, civilization, and the British establishment |
| **Contrast device** | Juxtaposed against the wild terrain of Germany and the Ottoman Empire |

## Key Connections

- **Sir Walter Bullivant** -- Hannay's superior, operates from London
- **Lisbon** -- Hannay's first stop en route to the East
- **Berlin** -- The enemy capital, contrasted with London
- **Constantinople** -- The destination that Hannay never quite reaches

## Chapter Appearances

London appears or is referenced in chapters P01, 001, 003, 006, 008, 012, 013, and 015, marking Hannay's departure, his nostalgic reflections, and the ultimate goal of return.
