# Constantinople

> "Constantinople! I had thought myself a long way from it in Berlin, but now it seemed as distant as the moon." — Hannay on the difficulty of reaching his destination [008-044]

## Overview

Constantinople (modern Istanbul) is the Ottoman capital and the operational center of the German-led conspiracy in *Greenmantle*. As the bridge between Europe and Asia, controlling the Bosphorus strait and the sea route to Russia, Constantinople holds immense strategic significance. Sir Walter Bullivant identifies it as the key node in Germany's Eastern strategy: the city from which a pan-Islamic uprising could be launched against British India and Egypt [001-005].

Hannay and his companions converge on Constantinople in the novel's middle section, using the city as their base for intelligence gathering and planning before traveling east to Erzerum for the final confrontation. The city's cosmopolitan character — Turks, Germans, Arabs, and Europeans mingling in its bazaars and back streets — provides both cover and danger for the Allied agents.

## Arrival and First Impressions

Hannay and Peter Pienaar enter Constantinople on the 16th of January, having traveled down the Danube disguised as Dutch engineers.

> "So it came about that at five past three on the 16th day of January, with only the clothes we stood up in, Peter and I entered Constantinople. I was in considerable spirits, for I had got the final lap successfully over, and I was looking forward madly to meeting my friends; but, all the same, the first sight was a mighty disappointment." [010-063 to 010-068]

The winter reality shatters Hannay's Eastern fantasies:

> "I don't quite know what I had expected -- a sort of fairyland Eastern city, all white marble and blue water, and stately Turks in surplices, and veiled houris, and roses and nightingales, and some sort of string band discoursing sweet music. I had forgotten that winter is pretty much the same everywhere. It was a drizzling day, with a south-east wind blowing, and the streets were long troughs of mud." [010-068 to 010-074]

> "Then we got into narrow steep streets which descended to a kind of big canal. I saw what I took to be mosques and minarets, and they were about as impressive as factory chimneys." [010-078 to 010-080]

The city's population strikes him as drab rather than exotic:

> "I saw one old fellow who looked like my notion of a Turk, but most of the population had the appearance of London old-clothes men. All but the soldiers, Turk and German, who seemed well-set-up fellows." [010-085 to 010-088]

## Strategic Importance

### The Bosphorus and Russian Supply Lines

Constantinople's position on the Bosphorus gives it control over the maritime route between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea. For the Allies, keeping this route open for Russian supply is a strategic imperative. For Germany, closing it — which the Ottoman Empire did by entering the war — was a critical blow to the Allied war effort.

### Gateway to the East

Constantinople serves as the launch point for German operations deeper into Anatolia and toward the Caucasus. Through-routes from Germany converge on the city [005-025], making it the logistical hub for the military mission to Erzerum. The city's rail connections, telegraph networks, and intelligence infrastructure make it the natural headquarters for both the German military mission and the Greenmantle conspiracy.

Hannay's intense focus on reaching Constantinople spans the novel's entire first half. Stranded in Germany, he fixates on the Danube as his lifeline eastward:

> "I was well on my way to the East, the Danube could not be fifty miles off, and that way ran the road to Constantinople. It was a fairly desperate position." [006-084 to 010-065]

> "To make things worse my job was not to escape -- though that would have been hard enough -- but to get to Constantinople, more than a thousand miles off, and I reckoned I couldn't get there as a tramp." [007-001 to 010-065]

### Political Center of the Ottoman Empire

As the seat of the Sultan and the Committee of Union and Progress, Constantinople is where German influence operates at the highest levels. Enver Pasha and his allies govern from Constantinople, and it is here that "Germany's graft" [012-085] has its greatest effect on Ottoman policy.

## In the Narrative

Constantinople appears primarily in Chapters 11–13, after Hannay's escape from Germany. The city is rendered through its atmosphere rather than detailed topography: crowded streets, anonymous lodging-houses, the constant presence of German officers, and the undercurrent of intrigue that pervades every encounter.

Hannay's reunion with Blenkiron occurs in Constantinople [011-007], and the subsequent council with Sandy Arbuthnot [012-062] takes place in a safe house in the city. It is in Constantinople that the full scope of the Greenmantle conspiracy is revealed.

### The Safe House and Intelligence Operations

From their base in the city, the three agents piece together the conspiracy. Blenkiron has established himself as the nerve centre of their intelligence network:

> "What's the matter with that for a post-office? I'm the best informed man in Constantinople, for old General Liman only hears one side, and mostly lies at that, and Enver prefers not to listen at all." [013-005 to 013-007]

Sandy Arbuthnot, operating through the esoteric Company of the Rosy Hours, has penetrated the innermost circles of the Ottoman capital:

> "I struck this side in Constantinople. Now there is a very famous Turkish _shaka_ called _Kasredin_, one of those old half-comic miracle plays with an allegorical meaning which they call _orta oyun_, and which take a week to read. That tale tells of the coming of a prophet, and I found that the select of the faith spoke of the new revelation in terms of it." [012-103 to 012-108]

### The German-Ottoman Nexus

Constantinople reveals itself as a city of uneasy alliance. The Young Turks depend on German support but resent their patrons, while the Germans view Constantinople as a stepping-stone to the East:

> "The ordinary Turk loathes the Committee, but he has some queer perverted expectation from Germany. It is not a case of Enver and the rest carrying on their shoulders the unpopular Teuton; it is a case of the Teuton carrying the unpopular Committee. And Germany's graft is just this and nothing more -- that she has some hand in the coming of the new deliverer." [012-082 to 012-087]

Within the German enclave, a still more powerful figure emerges -- Hilda von Einem:

> "I reckon our landlady has a bigger brain than Enver and Liman. She's the real boss of the show. When I came here, I reported to her, and presently you've got to do the same. I am curious as to how she'll strike you, for I'm free to admit that she impressed me considerable." [013-060 to 013-063]

The city itself offers a labyrinth of concealment. Even Rasta, the powerful German-Turkish agent, can be hidden away indefinitely:

> "There's a big unknown area in Constantinople to hide a man, into which the _Khafiyeh_ never enter." [015-107 to 015-108]

### Leaving Constantinople

The week in Constantinople weighs heavily on Hannay, a restless man far more at home in open country than in urban intrigue. Once they depart for Erzerum, the contrast is sharp:

> "I'm not used to cities, and lounging about Constantinople had slackened my fibre. Now, as the sharp wind buffeted us, I felt braced to any kind of risk." [016-057 to 016-058]

Looking back from the eastern front, Hannay reflects:

> "I could never again get back to the beastly stagnation of that Constantinople week." [019-017]
