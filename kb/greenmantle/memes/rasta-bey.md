## Overview

Rasta Bey is a Turkish official operating in Constantinople as an agent of Enver Pasha and the Committee of Union and Progress. Throughout the Greenmantle mission, he serves as a persistent antagonist to Richard Hannay and his companions, repeatedly attempting to intercept their movements and expose their cover identities.

## Role in the Mission

Rasta Bey first appears at the Constantinople docks, attempting to intercept German military stores that Hannay is escorting to General von Oesterzee at Chataldja. He claims authority from the Committee to receive the stores, but is overruled by the German officer present [010-019]. This encounter establishes him as an adversary with both influence and a determination to interfere with Hannay's operation.

> "I am sorry, Rasta Bey," he said; "but this man is in the right." [010-142]

After this initial confrontation, Rasta pursues Hannay and Peter Pienaar by train, intercepting them at Chataldja station "with half a dozen Turkish gendarmes" and attempting to take custody of them and their cargo [010-019]. Hannay manages to outmaneuver him, but Rasta remains a persistent threat throughout the Constantinople phase of the mission.

> "It was Rasta, with half a dozen Turkish gendarmes. ... 'You can get back to Rustchuk,' he said. 'I take over from you here. Hand me the papers.'" [010-019]

Hannay reflects that "at all costs we had to keep Rasta safe" after capturing him, refusing to hand him over for murder despite the danger he posed [015-080].

> "On the other hand, I had been spotted by Rasta, and had got the envoy of the most powerful man in Constantinople locked in a cupboard. At all costs we had to keep Rasta safe." [015-080]

Rasta ultimately identifies Hannay to the German authorities at Erzerum, sending a telegram labeling Hannay and his companions as "notorious spies" [017-122]. Hannay counters this by laughing it off as a personal vendetta: "He's one of Enver's satellites... This Rasta thought he had found a chance of delaying us" [017-124].

> "I see no reason why I shouldn't give you his name. It was Rasta Bey. You've picked an awkward fellow to make an enemy of." [017-122]

## Character

Blenkiron describes Rasta as having "Rasta's build, very foppish and precise in his dress" [013-106]. Despite being an antagonist, Hannay acknowledges his courage: "I had never doubted Rasta's pluck" [015-089]. When attacked by Sandy Arbuthnot with a weighted food tray, Rasta "jumped for the door" and fought back, demonstrating quick reflexes and toughness.

> "I am bound to say for Rasta that he was a man of quick action. In a trice he had whipped round to the other side of the table between me and the door, where he stood regarding me wickedly." [015-066]

Hannay's assessment of Rasta evolves from seeing him as a dangerous obstacle to recognizing him as Enver Pasha's tool with personal grievances: "Rasta's got a grievance against you" [017-143]. Hannay notes that "easily Rasta might have a big graft at headquarters" [017-166], suggesting Rasta's motivations are as much personal and financial as political.
