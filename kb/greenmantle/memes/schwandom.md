# Schwandorf

**Schwandorf** is a Bavarian railway town that becomes an unwitting turning point in Hannay's journey across Germany. Never actually visited by the protagonist, it serves as the destination on a railway ticket that precipitates his flight from Colonel Stumm's custody and his encounter with Hilda von Einem.

## In the Novel

Hannay first learns of Schwandorf when he overhears Stumm purchase his ticket:

> ...heard Stumm take my ticket for a place called Schwandorf, and after a... [006-035]

The town becomes a destination he is pressured to reach. An official at a railway junction insists:

> ...matter is urgent you must go to Schwandorf. [007-042]

But Hannay has no desire to follow Stumm's itinerary:

> ...for I had no anxiety to visit Schwandorf, when I heard behind me the... [007-097]

## The Flight

When pursued, Hannay fabricates a story about a companion disembarking near Schwandorf:

> ...fellow who wanted to go to Schwandorf, and then changed his mind. [007-117]

He deliberately avoids continuing "on to Schwandorf" [007-132], instead hiding his car and fleeing into the woods. This decision leads him to the cottage where he meets Hilda von Einem, and the Schwandorf road becomes a memory that Hilda herself later recalls:

> ...you once played me on the Schwandorf road. [018-099]

## Strategic Significance

Schwandorf's narrative role is as a pivot point: the town Hannay must *not* reach. His refusal to follow the prescribed route to Schwandorf initiates the chain of events that brings him into direct contact with Hilda von Einem, setting up the novel's central romantic and political confrontation.
