# Ypres

The **Ypres Salient** in *Greenmantle* represents the Western Front — the grinding, static warfare from which Richard Hannay has been temporarily lifted by his secret mission. It appears in the novel as a point of reference, a reminder of the conventional war that continues while Hannay operates in the shadows of the East.

## In the Novel

### A Soldier's Memory

Ypres is invoked as part of Hannay's reflection on the contrast between the Western Front and the intrigue of his current mission. The mention of the Ypres salient alongside the Yser evokes the geography of the Flanders theatre:

> ...coming back from the Yser or the Ypres salient. I would have given anything to be there. [003-063]

### The Contrast with the East

Later in the novel, Hannay draws a direct comparison between the terrain of Anatolia and the familiar landscapes of the Western Front:

> ...the dull stretch between Cassel and Ypres... [016-038]

This comparison ties the exotic Anatolian journey back to the known battlefields of France, grounding the narrative in Hannay's soldierly perspective.

## Historical Context

The Ypres Salient was one of the most deadly sectors of the Western Front. By the time of *Greenmantle*'s publication in 1916, the Second Battle of Ypres (April–May 1915) — which saw the first large-scale use of poison gas — was a recent and traumatic memory.
