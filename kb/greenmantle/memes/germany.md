# Germany

> "Germany's in the heart of the plan. The secret's in Germany." — Sandy Arbuthnot [012-120]

## Overview

Germany is the principal antagonistic power in *Greenmantle*, driving the conspiracy to ignite an Islamic holy war against the Allies. Sir Walter Bullivant identifies Germany's Eastern strategy as the novel's central threat: "German organization backed up with German money and German arms" has placed the Committee of Union and Progress in control of the Ottoman Empire [001-004].

Germany appears in the novel primarily through its agents, military infrastructure, and geopolitical ambitions rather than as a physical setting. Yet its presence permeates every layer of the narrative — from the Berlin police network that nearly traps Hannay [008-035], to the Erzerum fortress commanded by Colonel von Stumm, to the whispered intelligence that reveals the scope of German planning [012-120].

## Political Landscape

### The Eastern Strategy

With the Western Front stalemated, German strategic planners turned east. Sir Walter explains that Germany could "gobble up the French and the Russian armies" if it could destabilize the British Empire through a pan-Islamic uprising [003-019]. The Eastern campaign is not a sideshow but Germany's bid for decisive breakthrough: "Germany's got a trump card to play" [013-058].

The strategy's audacity lies in its harnessing of religious sentiment as a weapon. Germany aims not merely to court the Ottoman Empire as an ally, but to transform Islam itself into an instrument of war against the Allies.

### Relations with the Ottoman Empire

Germany's relationship with Turkey is complex and ambivalent. Sandy Arbuthnot notes that "in some subtle way Germany was regarded as a collaborator, not a master" [012-080]. The Turks are not puppet allies but partners with their own agenda. Yet German money and organization have been decisive: "Germany's graft is working," Sandy reports [012-085], describing how German influence has penetrated Ottoman institutions.

"There is no love lost between the two" [017-131], a Turkish observer remarks of the German-Turkish relationship. The alliance is pragmatic, not sentimental — both parties using the other for their own strategic ends.

### The Kaiser and Prussian Militarism

The Kaiser appears indirectly, a distant figure of immense power. "Kaiser" is invoked as the ultimate authority behind German operations [013-055]. The Prussian military culture that Stumm embodies — "In Germany we put discipline first" [003-052] — is portrayed as both formidable and spiritually empty. Hannay's encounters with German officers reveal a system that prizes obedience above reason, force above persuasion.

## In the Narrative

### As a Physical Setting

Hannay's journey through Germany occupies Chapters 2–8, taking him from the Dutch border through the German countryside to Berlin. The landscape is described with a traveler's eye: "a geological map of Germany" spread before Stumm [005-017], through-routes marked from Germany to the East [005-025]. Berlin appears as a city of police stations and surveillance: "every police-station in Germany" has Hannay's description [008-035].

### Through Its Agents

Germany is most vividly present through its representatives: Colonel von Stumm, whose corpulence and brutality embody Prussian militarism [004-014]; the engineer Gaudian, who represents German technical excellence [005-035]; and the network of officials and police who pursue Hannay across the country.

Hilda von Einem complicates the picture. "You are on Germany's side?" she asks Hannay [014-139]. Her allegiance to Germany is instrumental — "I am on Germany's side" [015-049] — but her loyalty is ultimately to her own vision, not to the Kaiser's government.

### The Intelligence Dimension

"Germany's in the heart of the plan. The secret's in Germany" [012-120]. Sandy's revelation reframes the entire mission: the conspiracy is not an Ottoman operation with German support, but a German operation using Ottoman territory. The whispered name that Stumm and Gaudian exchange [005-178] — the clue that drives Hannay to Erzerum — originated in German intelligence.

## Strategic Significance

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Military position | Western Front stalemate; Eastern strategy as breakthrough bid |
| Key asset | Ottoman alliance; pan-Islamic agitation capability |
| Method | Money, organization, propaganda, military missions |
| Vulnerability | Overextension; Turkish resentment of German domination |
| Narrative role | Principal antagonist power; source of conspiracy |

Germany's Eastern campaign is "the side-show which might become the main attraction" [010-060]. The novel's central dramatic question — can the heroes prevent Germany from weaponizing Islam? — establishes Germany as both the strategic adversary and the ideological foil to British values.
