# Ch. 7: Christmastide

[007-001] I picked up the key and unlocked the door. In one of the gilded mirrors

[007-002] I smoothed my hair and tidied up my clothes. My anger had completely

[007-003] gone and I had no particular ill-will left against Stumm. He was a man

[007-004] of remarkable qualities, which would have brought him to the highest

[007-005] distinction in the Stone Age. But for all that he and his kind were

[007-006] back numbers.

[007-007] I stepped out of the room, locked the door behind me, and started out

[007-008] on the second stage of my travels.

[007-009] CHAPTER VII.

[007-010] Christmastide

[007-011] Everything depended on whether the servant was in the hall. I had put

[007-012] Stumm to sleep for a bit, but I couldn’t flatter myself he would long

[007-013] be quiet, and when he came to he would kick the locked door to

[007-014] matchwood. I must get out of the house without a minute’s delay, and if

[007-015] the door was shut and the old man gone to bed I was done.

[007-016] I met him at the foot of the stairs, carrying a candle.

[007-017] “Your master wants me to send off an important telegram. Where is the

[007-018] nearest office? There’s one in the village, isn’t there?” I spoke in my

[007-019] best German, the first time I had used the tongue since I crossed the

[007-020] frontier.

[007-021] “The village is five minutes off at the foot of the avenue,” he said.

[007-022] “Will you be long, sir?”

[007-023] “I’ll be back in a quarter of an hour,” I said. “Don’t lock up till I

[007-024] get in.”

[007-025] I put on my ulster and walked out into a clear starry night. My bag I

[007-026] left lying on a settle in the hall. There was nothing in it to

[007-027] compromise me, but I wished I could have got a toothbrush and some

[007-028] tobacco out of it.

[007-029] So began one of the craziest escapades you can well imagine. I couldn’t

[007-030] stop to think of the future yet, but must take one step at a time. I

[007-031] ran down the avenue, my feet cracking on the hard snow, planning hard

[007-032] my programme for the next hour.

[007-033] I found the village—half a dozen houses with one biggish place that

[007-034] looked like an inn. The moon was rising, and as I approached I saw that

[007-035] there was some kind of a store. A funny little two-seated car was

[007-036] purring before the door, and I guessed this was also the telegraph

[007-037] office.

[007-038] I marched in and told my story to a stout woman with spectacles on her

[007-039] nose who was talking to a young man.

[007-040] “It is too late,” she shook her head. “The Herr Burgrave knows that

[007-041] well. There is no connection from here after eight o’clock. If the

[007-042] matter is urgent you must go to [[schwandom|Schwandorf]].”

[007-043] “How far is that?” I asked, looking for some excuse to get decently out

[007-044] of the shop.

[007-045] “Seven miles,” she said, “but here is Franz and the post-wagon. Franz,

[007-046] you will be glad to give the gentleman a seat beside you.”

[007-047] The sheepish-looking youth muttered something which I took to be

[007-048] assent, and finished off a glass of beer. From his eyes and manner he

[007-049] looked as if he were half drunk.

[007-050] I thanked the woman, and went out to the car, for I was in a fever to

[007-051] take advantage of this unexpected bit of [[fate-and-providence|luck]]. I could hear the

[007-052] post-mistress enjoining Franz not to keep the gentleman waiting, and

[007-053] presently he came out and flopped into the driver’s seat. We started in

[007-054] a series of voluptuous curves, till his eyes got accustomed to the

[007-055] darkness.

[007-056] At first we made good going along the straight, broad highway lined

[007-057] with woods on one side and on the other snowy fields melting into haze.

[007-058] Then he began to talk, and, as he talked, he slowed down. This by no

[007-059] means suited my book, and I seriously wondered whether I should pitch

[007-060] him out and take charge of the thing. He was obviously a weakling, left

[007-061] behind in the conscription, and I could have done it with one hand. But

[007-062] by a fortunate chance I left him alone.

[007-063] “That is a fine hat of yours, mein Herr,” he said. He took off his own

[007-064] blue peaked cap, the uniform, I suppose, of the driver of the

[007-065] post-wagon, and laid it on his knee. The night air ruffled a shock of

[007-066] tow-coloured hair.

[007-067] Then he calmly took my hat and clapped it on his head.

[007-068] “With this thing I should be a gentleman,” he said.

[007-069] I said nothing, but put on his cap and waited.

[007-070] “That is a noble overcoat, mein Herr,” he went on. “It goes well with

[007-071] the hat. It is the kind of garment I have always desired to own. In two

[007-072] days it will be the holy Christmas, when gifts are given. Would that

[007-073] the good God sent me such a coat as yours!”

[007-074] “You can try it on to see how it looks,” I said good-humouredly.

[007-075] He stopped the car with a jerk, and pulled off his blue coat. The

[007-076] exchange was soon effected. He was about my height, and my ulster

[007-077] fitted not so badly. I put on his overcoat, which had a big collar that

[007-078] buttoned round the neck.

[007-079] The idiot preened himself like a girl. Drink and vanity had primed him

[007-080] for any folly. He drove so carelessly for a bit that he nearly put us

[007-081] into a ditch. We passed several cottages and at the last he slowed

[007-082] down.

[007-083] “A friend of mine lives here,” he announced. “Gertrud would like to see

[007-084] me in the fine clothes which the most amiable Herr has given me. Wait

[007-085] for me, I will not be long.” And he scrambled out of the car and

[007-086] lurched into the little garden.

[007-087] I took his place and moved very slowly forward. I heard the door open

[007-088] and the sound of laughing and loud voices. Then it shut, and looking

[007-089] back I saw that my idiot had been absorbed into the dwelling of his

[007-090] Gertrud. I waited no longer, but sent the car forward at its best

[007-091] speed.

[007-092] Five minutes later the infernal thing began to give trouble—a nut loose

[007-093] in the antiquated steering-gear. I unhooked a lamp, examined it, and

[007-094] put the mischief right, but I was a quarter of an hour doing it. The

[007-095] highway ran now in a thick forest and I noticed branches going off now

[007-096] and then to the right. I was just thinking of turning up one of them,

[007-097] for I had no anxiety to visit Schwandorf, when I heard behind me the

[007-098] sound of a great car driven furiously.

[007-099] I drew in to the right side—thank goodness I remembered the rule of the

[007-100] road—and proceeded decorously, wondering what was going to happen. I

[007-101] could hear the brakes being clamped on and the car slowing down.

[007-102] Suddenly a big grey bonnet slipped past me and as I turned my head I

[007-103] heard a familiar voice.

[007-104] It was Stumm, looking like something that has been run over. He had his

[007-105] jaw in a sling, so that I wondered if I had broken it, and his eyes

[007-106] were beautifully bunged up. It was that that saved me, that and his

[007-107] raging temper. The collar of the postman’s coat was round my chin,

[007-108] hiding my beard, and I had his cap pulled well down on my brow. I

[007-109] remembered what [[john-blenkiron|Blenkiron]] had said—that the only way to deal with the

[007-110] Germans was naked bluff. Mine was naked enough, for it was all that was

[007-111] left to me.

[007-112] “Where is the man you brought from Andersbach?” he roared, as well as

[007-113] his jaw would allow him.

[007-114] I pretended to be mortally scared, and spoke in the best imitation I

[007-115] could manage of the postman’s high cracked voice.

[007-116] “He got out a mile back, Herr Burgrave,” I quavered. “He was a rude

[007-117] fellow who wanted to go to Schwandorf, and then changed his mind.”

[007-118] “Where, you fool? Say exactly where he got down or I will wring your

[007-119] neck.”

[007-120] “In the wood this side of Gertrud’s cottage ... on the left hand. I

[007-121] left him running among the trees.” I put all the terror I knew into my

[007-122] pipe, and it wasn’t all acting.

[007-123] “He means the Henrichs’ cottage, Herr Colonel,” said the chauffeur.

[007-124] “This man is courting the daughter.”

[007-125] Stumm gave an order and the great car backed, and, as I looked round, I

[007-126] saw it turning. Then as it gathered speed it shot forward, and

[007-127] presently was lost in the shadows. I had got over the first hurdle.

[007-128] But there was no time to be lost. Stumm would meet the postman and

[007-129] would be tearing after me any minute. I took the first turning, and

[007-130] bucketed along a narrow woodland road. The hard ground would show very

[007-131] few tracks, I thought, and I hoped the pursuit would think I had gone

[007-132] on to Schwandorf. But it wouldn’t do to risk it, and I was determined

[007-133] very soon to get the car off the road, leave it, and take to the

[007-134] forest. I took out my watch and calculated I could give myself ten

[007-135] minutes.

[007-136] I was very nearly caught. Presently I came on a bit of rough heath,

[007-137] with a slope away from the road and here and there a patch of black

[007-138] which I took to be a sandpit. Opposite one of these I slewed the car to

[007-139] the edge, got out, started it again and saw it pitch head-foremost into

[007-140] the darkness. There was a splash of water and then silence. Craning

[007-141] over I could see nothing but murk, and the marks at the lip where the

[007-142] wheels had passed. They would find my tracks in daylight but scarcely

[007-143] at this time of night.

[007-144] Then I ran across the road to the forest. I was only just in time, for

[007-145] the echoes of the splash had hardly died away when I heard the sound of

[007-146] another car. I lay flat in a hollow below a tangle of snow-laden

[007-147] brambles and looked between the pine-trees at the moonlit road. It was

[007-148] Stumm’s car again and to my consternation it stopped just a little

[007-149] short of the sandpit.

[007-150] I saw an electric torch flashed, and Stumm himself got out and examined

[007-151] the tracks on the highway. Thank God, they would be still there for him

[007-152] to find, but had he tried half a dozen yards on he would have seen them

[007-153] turn towards the sandpit. If that had happened he would have beaten the

[007-154] adjacent woods and most certainly found me. There was a third man in

[007-155] the car, with my hat and coat on him. That poor devil of a postman had

[007-156] paid dear for his vanity.

[007-157] They took a long time before they started again, and I was jolly well

[007-158] relieved when they went scouring down the road. I ran deeper into the

[007-159] woods till I found a track which—as I judged from the sky which I saw

[007-160] in a clearing—took me nearly due west. That wasn’t the direction I

[007-161] wanted, so I bore off at right angles, and presently struck another

[007-162] road which I crossed in a hurry. After that I got entangled in some

[007-163] confounded kind of enclosure and had to climb paling after paling of

[007-164] rough stakes plaited with osiers. Then came a rise in the ground and I

[007-165] was on a low hill of pines which seemed to last for miles. All the time

[007-166] I was going at a good pace, and before I stopped to rest I calculated I

[007-167] had put six miles between me and the sandpit.

[007-168] My mind was getting a little more active now; for the first part of the

[007-169] journey I had simply staggered from impulse to impulse. These impulses

[007-170] had been uncommon lucky, but I couldn’t go on like that for ever. _Ek

[007-171] sal “n plan maak_, says the old Boer when he gets into trouble, and it

[007-172] was up to me now to make a plan.

[007-173] As soon as I began to think I saw the desperate business I was in for.

[007-174] Here was I, with nothing except what I stood up in—including a coat and

[007-175] cap that weren’t mine—alone in mid-winter in the heart of South

[007-176] Germany. There was a man behind me looking for my blood, and soon there

[007-177] would be a hue-and-cry for me up and down the land. I had heard that

[007-178] the German police were pretty efficient, and I couldn’t see that I

[007-179] stood the slimmest chance. If they caught me they would shoot me beyond

[007-180] doubt. I asked myself on what charge, and answered, “For knocking about

[007-181] a German officer.” They couldn’t have me up for [[espionage]], for as far

[007-182] as I knew they had no evidence. I was simply a Dutchman that had got

[007-183] riled and had run amok. But if they cut down a cobbler for laughing at

[007-184] a second lieutenant—which is what happened at Zabern—I calculated that

[007-185] hanging would be too good for a man that had broken a colonel’s jaw.
