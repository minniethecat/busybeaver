# Panama

## Overview

Panama — "the Cup of Gold" — is the geographical and symbolic center of Steinbeck's novel. It is the richest Spanish city on the Spanish Main, the seat of colonial administration and the Catholic Church in the region, and the ultimate prize of Henry Morgan's buccaneering career. In the novel's symbolic economy, Panama is conflated with La Santa Roja (the Red Saint) and with the golden cup of ultimate desire itself: to reach Panama is to drink from the Cup of Gold, and to find the cup empty.

## Geography

Panama stands on the Pacific coast of the Isthmus, connected to the Caribbean by the Chagres River and a brutal overland route through jungle and mountains. Steinbeck emphasizes the city's isolation: it is protected by the isthmus itself, by Spanish fortifications at Chagres and San Lorenzo, and by the sheer difficulty of the crossing. The buccaneer army's march across the isthmus — through fever-ridden jungle, harassed by Spanish ambushes — is one of the novel's most vivid sequences.

## In the Narrative

Panama enters the novel as rumor before it appears as place. The buccaneers at Tortuga speak of it in hushed tones: a city of unimaginable wealth, guarded by walls and cavalry, and — more dangerously — inhabited by La Santa Roja, the woman whose beauty has become a legend across the Spanish Main.

Morgan's obsession crystalizes around Panama. He sends a ruby to its governor, Don Juan Perez de Guzman, with the message: "I will go to the Cup of Gold within the year" (009-061). The expedition that follows is the novel's centerpiece: thirty-seven ships gather at Tortuga, the articles are drawn, and the buccaneer army crosses the isthmus.

The battle for Panama takes place on a plain outside the city. The Spanish governor releases a stampede of bulls against the buccaneers — an ingenious tactic that fails when the bulls are turned by gunfire. The Spanish cavalry charges into a marsh and is destroyed. Panama falls.

Inside the conquered city, Morgan's triumph unravels. He finds the actual golden chalice — "a lovely, slender chalice with long curved handles and a rim of silver" (010-281) — and drinks from it. He finds La Santa Roja — and is refused. He murders Coeur de Gris in a corridor. The city burns. The Cup of Gold is taken, and it is ashes.

## Historical Context

The historical Panama City (founded 1519) was the first European settlement on the Pacific coast of the Americas and the terminus of the Spanish treasure route from Peru. It was sacked and burned by Henry Morgan in 1671. The city was rebuilt at a more defensible location a few miles away (now known as Casco Viejo); the ruins of the original city (Panamá Viejo) are a UNESCO World Heritage site.
