# Indenture System

## Definition

The indenture system was the primary labor institution of 17th-century English Caribbean colonies, by which individuals — often poor migrants, convicts, or those kidnapped ("spirited") — were bound to a master for a fixed term (typically 4–7 years) in exchange for passage to the New World. In Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold*, the indenture system is the mechanism of Henry Morgan's entry into the Caribbean world: sold by Tim the Bo's'n for eleven pounds to James Flower, a Barbados planter, Morgan serves five years of bonded labor that function as his apprenticeship in the realities of the colonial economy.

## In the Novel

Henry is sold into indenture in chapter 8, after his arrival in the Caribbean aboard the *Bristol Girl*. The betrayal is casual: Tim, the bo's'n who had befriended Henry on the crossing, sells him to a "skull-faced Puritan master" for eleven pounds (008-122). The scene is rendered with brutal economy: Henry wakes from a drugged sleep to find himself bound, inspected like livestock, and purchased. The indenture contract is mentioned only in passing — Steinbeck is less interested in the legal mechanics than in the existential shock.

James Flower, the planter who buys Henry, is an atypical master. An intellectual recluse with a library of classical and modern works, he treats Henry more as a student than a servant. It is on Flower's plantation that Henry reads Caesar's *Commentaries* and absorbs the tactical thinking that will later serve him as a buccaneer captain: "From Caesar he learned that all military operations were, at base, glorified trickery" (008-176).

When Flower sets Henry free at Christmas — an act of genuine generosity — Henry leaves Barbados with a ship of his own (*Elizabeth*), purchased with Flower's help. The indenture has been a crucible: the Welsh farm boy has emerged as a man who understands the operations of power, trade, and violence that structure the Caribbean world.

## Key Passages

## Symbolic Significance

Indenture functions in the novel as both literal institution and metaphorical passage. It is Henry's Abred — the "circle of earth and water, where all things struggle" that Gwenliana prophesied (005-031). The five years of servitude strip away the Welsh identity without yet providing a Caribbean one. Henry emerges neither Welsh nor colonial, but something new: a man who has learned that the world is organized by power, and that power can be taken. The indenture is the novel's economic counterpoint to its psychological themes — the material reality that underlies the myth of the self-made buccaneer.
