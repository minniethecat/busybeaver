# The Effects of Alcoholic Spirits, Momentary and Perpetual

***The Effects of Alcoholic Spirits, Momentary and Perpetual*** is a temperance tract by an unnamed Separatist that appears in *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]* as the accidental catalyst for [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s exile to [[barbados|Barbados]]. The book's pseudo-scientific "verification" by Flower produced the one spontaneous idea of his life — and cost him his place in English society.

## Description

Steinbeck describes the book with ironic precision:

> A Separatist of fanatic intensity had written a violent book of the best scientific manner — *The Effects of Alcoholic Spirits, Momentary and Perpetual*. [008-146]

The title parodies the conventions of seventeenth-century Puritan tracts, with their exhaustive subtitles and claims to scientific rigor. The phrase "of the best scientific manner" is Steinbeck's jab at the genre: the tract dresses religious fanaticism in the borrowed robes of empirical inquiry. It is a "violent book" — not in physical form but in rhetorical temperature, the sort of work that could drive a man to "verify its more fantastic theories" [008-[[TOC|14]]6] through direct experiment.

## Role in the Narrative

The book serves as the pivot point in [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s backstory. Before its intervention, Flower was a failed poet and uncomfortable member of English clerical society. After it:

> This work fell into the hands of James Flower, and he set out one evening to verify some of its more fantastic theories. In the midst of his investigation the spirit of induction left him, and, without cause or warning, he assaulted one of His Majesty's guardsmen with a potted plant. Had he only known it, this was the one spontaneous idea of his life. [008-146]

The assault with a potted plant is pure Steinbeckian comedy — a weapon absurdly domestic, an act of violence without dignity. But the consequences are severe: Flower's small fortune is invested in a [[barbados|Barbados]] plantation, and he is exiled. The book, in effect, created the plantation where [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] would later serve his [[indenture-system|indenture]] and receive his education.

The irony is layered. A temperance tract — a book *against* alcohol — leads directly to Flower's violent, drunken-looking outburst. The book's effect is the opposite of its intention: instead of promoting sobriety, it produces chaos. This is Steinbeck's commentary on the distance between doctrinal conviction and human behavior.

## Symbolism

The tract represents the danger of taking ideas too literally. [[james-flower|James Flower]], the man who "had cried to be a creator" [008-[[TOC|14]]7], finds his one moment of genuine spontaneity not in art but in acting out a fanatic's theories. The book is a dark mirror of Flower's own mind: like the Separatist author, Flower is full of convictions he cannot assimilate into a coherent life. The potted plant — the weapon of his assault — is itself a symbol of cultivated, contained nature, the very opposite of the wild freedom Flower sought and never found. That this absurd incident determines the entire setting of the novel's central action (Morgan's Barbadian education) makes *The Effects of Alcoholic Spirits* perhaps the most consequentially ridiculous book in American fiction.
