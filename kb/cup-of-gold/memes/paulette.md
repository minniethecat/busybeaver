# Paulette

## Role

Paulette is a young slave girl of mixed ancestry whom Henry Morgan purchases at the Port Royal slave dock and keeps as a servant and concubine. She serves as the novel's embodiment of purely physical love — "flesh-love" — standing in thematic opposition to the spiritual, soul-centered love Henry felt for [[elizabeth]]. Through Paulette's desperate attachment to Henry and her incomprehension of the "soul," Steinbeck explores the gulf between bodily passion and the transcendent yearning that drives Henry toward the sea.

## Biography

Henry Morgan first encountered Paulette at the slave dock in Port Royal, where he bought her to serve as a house servant [008-206]. She was of mixed heritage: "Spanish and Carib and Negro and French," with "hair like a cataract of black water, eyes as blue as the sea, set in oriental slits, and a golden, golden skin" [008-207]. Henry built her a small vine-clad house roofed with banana leaves, where he "played at love" [008-208].

Initially grateful for the comfort and ease Henry provided, Paulette soon fell frenziedly in love with him [008-208]. Her devotion was tinged with terror: she knew that if Henry abandoned her, she would be sent to the fields and bred with a black slave to produce strong laboring children [008-216]. This fear drove her to desperate measures.

While Henry was away on a trading voyage, Paulette encountered a drunken Irish sailor on the beach who offered to marry her, giving her the idea that she might secure Henry through matrimony [008-220]. Upon Henry's return, she laid a snare: a flagon of Peruvian wine and a priest bribed with a stolen coin, waiting in the shadow of a tree [008-225].

As Henry drank, Paulette repeated her anxious refrain — "Do you love Paulette?" — while he offered increasingly distracted reassurances [008-227–232]. When the wine had loosened his tongue, Henry's thoughts drifted to [[elizabeth]], the girl he had loved as a boy in Wales. He confessed that while he loved Paulette's body, Elizabeth had possessed a "white winged soul" — something Paulette could never offer [008-235].

When Paulette asked what a soul was and how she might obtain one, Henry responded with cruelty: "You are just a little animal! a pretty little golden animal, for sure, but a form of flesh — no more" [008-238–241]. Paulette was bewildered; the concept of an immaterial soul lay entirely beyond her understanding.

Before departing to join [[edward-mansveldt|Mansveldt's]] buccaneer fleet, Henry secured Paulette's future by extracting a promise from [[james-flower|James Flower]] that she would never be sent to the fields, whipped, or bred with the slaves [008-256–259].

## Relationships

- **[[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]]** — Master and lover. Henry cared for Paulette materially but could not love her beyond the physical. His protection of her after his departure reveals a lingering tenderness.
- **[[elizabeth]]** — Unwitting rival. Paulette could never compete with Henry's idealized memory of his first love, the woman who possessed the "soul" Paulette lacked.
- **[[james-flower|James Flower]]** — The planter who employed Henry during his indenture. Henry entrusted Paulette's safekeeping to him.

## Quotes

> "She was lithe, yet rounded; fierce and gentle in a moment. Poor little slave of the jumbled bloods, she was Spanish and Carib and Negro and French. The heritage of this ragbag ancestry was hair like a cataract of black water, eyes as blue as the sea, set in oriental slits, and a golden, golden skin." [008-207]

> "You? Love you? Why, you are just a little animal! a pretty little golden animal, for sure, but a form of flesh — no more. May one worship a god merely because he is big, or cherish a land which has no virtue save its breadth, or love a woman whose whole realm is her flesh?" [008-240]

> "What is this soul? And how may I get one if I have not one already? And where is this soul of yours that I have never seen it or heard it at all?" [008-241]
