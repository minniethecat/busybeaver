# Sack of Panama

## Summary

The Sack of Panama is the climactic military event of *Cup of Gold* — Henry Morgan's 1671 expedition across the Isthmus of Panama with a fleet of thirty-seven ships and an army of buccaneers, culminating in the capture and burning of the richest Spanish city in the New World. The event occupies chapters 9-10 and functions as the novel's narrative and symbolic fulcrum: everything before it is pursuit, everything after it is aftermath.

## Context

The expedition is motivated by Morgan's obsession with the "Cup of Gold" — Panama itself, conflated in his imagination with La Santa Roja, the legendary Red Saint. Earlier successes (Puerto Bello, Maracaibo) have established Morgan's reputation, but Panama is a different order of ambition: it lies on the Pacific coast, across a brutal isthmian crossing, defended by Spanish cavalry and fortifications.

The buccaneer fleet assembles at Tortuga, where the council of captains debates the target. The articles of agreement are drawn — shares specified for captain, surgeon, carpenter, and compensation for lost limbs. Morgan outbids rival captains for crew, promising shares of a prize larger than any single Spanish galleon.

## Narrative Account

The crossing of the Caribbean and the isthmus is rendered in vivid, punishing detail. The buccaneer army — a multinational force of cutthroats, adventurers, and desperate men — marches through jungle and swamp, harassed by Spanish ambushes, ravaged by fever. The Spanish governor, Don Juan Perez de Guzman, prepares his defense: the cavalry, the fortifications at Chagres, and — as a final, desperate measure — a stampede of bulls.

The battle on the plain outside Panama is the novel's most sustained action sequence. The Spanish cavalry charges and is destroyed when the buccaneers' gunfire spooks the horses into a marsh. The stampede of bulls — Guzman's last innovation — is turned by disciplined volleys. Panama falls.

Inside the conquered city, Morgan's triumph unravels. He finds the actual golden chalice — the Cup of Gold materialized — and drinks from it. He finds La Santa Roja, who refuses him. He murders Coeur de Gris. The city burns — whether by Morgan's order or accident is left ambiguous. The sack that was supposed to be the ultimate achievement becomes the site of moral catastrophe.

## Outcome

The sack makes Morgan the most famous buccaneer in the world — and destroys him. The achievement of his ultimate desire extinguishes the desire that gave his life meaning. He sickens with "a disease called mediocrity" (010-393). The expedition's strategic success is also its psychological failure: the Cup of Gold, once drunk, is empty.

## Historical Basis

The historical Sack of Panama occurred in January 1671. Morgan led approximately 1,400 buccaneers against the city. The Spanish governor did attempt to use a stampede of cattle as a defensive tactic, though its effectiveness is debated. The city was burned — whether by Morgan's men or by Spanish defenders attempting to deny the buccaneers their loot is unclear. Steinbeck's fictional additions include La Santa Roja, the murder of Coeur de Gris, and the novel's psychological interpretation of Morgan's subsequent decline.
