# The Spear Song

**"The Spear Song"** is a bardic composition by [[merlin|Merlin]], the Welsh poet-seer of Crag-top. Paired with "[[the-sorrow-of-plaith|The Sorrow of Plaith]]," it represents the martial strain in [[merlin|Merlin]]'s creative output — a song of war and weaponry from a man who would spend his final decades in silence, surrounded by harps and bronze spearheads.

## Description

The title — *The Spear Song* — suggests a martial or heroic theme, appropriate to Welsh bardic tradition. The spear (*gwaywffon* in Welsh) is a central image in Celtic mythology and poetry, from the spear of Lugh to the weapon that wounded Arthur at Camlann. [[merlin|Merlin]]'s house on Crag-top is decorated with "harp and spearhead, harp and spearhead all the way around; small Welsh harps and the great bronze leaf spears of the Britons" [003-010] — the song's title and the room's decoration mirror each other, the creative and the martial paired as equal expressions of [[wales|Cambria]]n identity.

## Role in the Narrative

Like "The Sorrow of Plaith," "The Spear Song" is mentioned only once, in the paragraph that establishes [[merlin|Merlin]]'s lost greatness:

> Once Merlin had been a fine poet, the old people said, and might have been a greater. They would softly sing "The Sorrow of Plaith" or "The Spear Song," to prove it. [003-002]

The pairing of a lament song ("Sorrow of Plaith") with a martial song ("Spear Song") is characteristic of the bardic repertoire, which encompassed both elegy and war poetry. That the old people "softly sing" a spear song — a martial piece rendered as a gentle, private memory — captures the diminishment of [[merlin|Merlin]]'s legacy: songs once performed for kings and warriors now survive only as whispers among farmers.

## Symbolism

The spear, in Celtic tradition, is the weapon of heroes and kings — an instrument of honor, not ambush. In [[merlin|Merlin]]'s room, spearheads hang beside harps, suggesting that for the Welsh, poetry and war were not opposites but partners: the bard sang the deeds the warrior performed. "The Spear Song" is the artifact of that unity.

But in the narrative present of *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]*, the unity is broken. [[merlin|Merlin]] no longer creates; the spears hang silent on the wall; the harps "echoed him jeeringly" [003-045]. The young [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] will go to the [[caribbean|Caribbean]] and wield real spears — or rather, real swords and cannon — but no bard will sing of him. The "spear song" of the New World will be written in ledgers and treaties, not verse. [[merlin|Merlin]]'s silence prefigures the novel's broader elegy for a world where heroic action still had a singer to commemorate it.
