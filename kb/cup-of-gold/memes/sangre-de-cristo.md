# Sangre de Cristo

The ***Sangre de Cristo*** ("Blood of Christ") was a Spanish supply ship captured by Captain Dawes's [[buccaneering|buccaneers]] during the campaign against [[panama-city|Panama]]. Her name — invoking the blood of Christ — is characteristic of Spanish colonial ship naming, where vessels carried sacred names into profane waters. Her capture represents the routine predation that sustained the buccaneer economy between major raids.

## Description

The *Sangre de Cristo* is mentioned only in passing, identified as a "supply ship" [008-335] taken by Captain Dawes. As a supply vessel rather than a treasure galleon, she carried provisions rather than gold — food, water, naval stores — the mundane necessities of colonial warfare. Her capture would have provided material sustenance to the buccaneer fleet rather than spectacular wealth.

## Role in the Narrative

The ship appears in a tavern scene in [[goaves|Goaves]], where buccaneers celebrate a recent prize:

> "By God! you will so drink when Captain Dawes that took the supply ship *Sangre de Cristo* this day week asks you." [008-335]

The line is delivered by a "lowering man" who draws a pistol on [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] — a moment of casual buccaneer violence that underscores the world Morgan has entered. The *Sangre de Cristo* is not the subject of the scene but its occasion: a captured ship means drink, and drink means confrontation. The sacred name of the vessel ("Blood of Christ") juxtaposed with the profane violence of the tavern is a small instance of the novel's persistent irony about the collision of Spanish Catholicism and buccaneer brutality.

## Symbolism

The name *Sangre de Cristo* — Blood of Christ — is freighted with the religious language of the [[spanish-empire|Spanish Empire]]. [[spanish-empire|Spain]] justified its New World dominion in Catholic terms; its ships carried the names of saints, sacraments, and holy mysteries. That a vessel named for the central symbol of Christian redemption should end as a buccaneer prize — its cargo consumed in a tavern brawl — is a microcosm of the novel's treatment of empire. The sacred gives way to the profane; the blood of Christ becomes just another commodity to be captured, consumed, and forgotten.
