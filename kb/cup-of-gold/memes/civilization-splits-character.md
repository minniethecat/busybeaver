# Civilization Splits Character

## Definition

"Civilization will split up a character, and he who refuses to split goes under" (013-052) is the philosophical thesis of Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold*, articulated by the dying Sir Henry Morgan as he reflects on his transformation from Welsh farm boy to buccaneer captain to knighted Lieutenant-Governor. The concept describes the cost of social respectability: to be civilized is to be divided against oneself, to maintain a public face at war with private truth.

## In the Novel

The concept structures Morgan's entire life trajectory. As a boy in Wales, he is whole — a single, hungry self that "wanted the moon." As a buccaneer, he remains whole: the captain who says "I will go to the Cup of Gold" (009-061) acts on undivided desire. But when he accepts his knighthood from Charles II and becomes Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, the split begins. He must now enforce the law against the very men with whom he once sailed. He hangs buccaneers in the morning and presides over colonial society in the evening.

The concept is dramatized most vividly in the trial scene (chapter 13), where Morgan sits as judge over two Burgundian pirates. They are guilty — of the same crimes Morgan once committed. His duty requires him to condemn them; his identity screams against it. The "duty of appearances" — the phrase Morgan uses to describe his marriage, his governorship, his entire public life — forces him to perform a self that is not his self.

In the deathbed vision (chapter 14), the split is literalized: Morgan's consciousness descends into a grotto where his faceless deeds interrogate him. The "duty of appearances" has created a ledger of actions that no longer belong to the man who performed them. He has become so divided that he cannot recognize his own life.

The concept ties back to Merlin's prophecy: "you will become a great man — if only you remain a little child" (003-033). The child is unified desire; the great man is split. To remain a child — to refuse the split — is, as Morgan says, to "go under." The novel suggests there is no third way.

## Symbolic Significance

This concept is Steinbeck's early formulation of a theme that would dominate his later work: the tension between individual authenticity and social conformity. It anticipates the moral psychology of *East of Eden* and the social criticism of *The Grapes of Wrath*. In *Cup of Gold*, it is rendered as tragedy: the price of success is self-division, and the only alternative is destruction.
