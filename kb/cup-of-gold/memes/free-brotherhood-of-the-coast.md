# Free Brotherhood of the Coast

## Definition

The Free Brotherhood of the Coast was the loose confederation of buccaneers — ships and crews of all nations — that operated in the Caribbean during the 17th century. In Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold*, it is depicted as "a curious organization" with "no laws, but customs which were more binding than laws" (009-002). It was not a formal government but a shared culture: the articles of agreement before each expedition, the council of captains, the division of spoils by shares, and the tacit permission granted by England and France to prey upon Spanish shipping.

## In the Novel

The Brotherhood functions as the social world Morgan enters after his indenture. It is introduced through the historical interludes of chapters 8 and 9, where Steinbeck catalogs its founding figures: Pierre le Grand, who "with a single canoe and twenty-eight men, captured a Spanish admiral's flagship" (009-003); Bartolomeo Portugues, who escaped captivity by swimming leagues through shark-infested waters; Roche Braziliano, who "tortured prisoners for the pleasure of hearing them scream" (009-009); and L'Ollonais, the most terrifying of all, who "burned cities merely to see the flames reflect on the sea" (009-011).

The Brotherhood's great rendezvous is at Tortuga, where Morgan assembles his Panama fleet: "Every morning new sails appeared on the horizon, and every evening the taverns were more crowded with ruffians from every corner of the world" (010-042). Here the articles are drawn — shares specified for captain, surgeon, carpenter, and compensation for loss of limb or eye. The council of captains debates the target: Panama.

The Brotherhood's internal contradiction is that it needs state sanction to survive but cannot survive state sanction. Mansveldt dreams of transforming it into a proper republic (009-021); England and France, fearing a rival state, forbid it. After Morgan's knighthood, the Brotherhood is suppressed — its former captains hanged by their former comrade. The Brotherhood represents a freedom that civilization cannot tolerate.

## Key Passages

> "The Free Brotherhood of the Coast was a curious organization. It had no laws, but customs which were more binding than laws. It had no government, but a council of captains whose decisions were final. It owned no land, but claimed the whole Caribbean for its hunting ground." (009-002)

> "Mansveldt dreamed of a buccaneer nation. He pictured a republic on the sea, a new country to be created out of a colony wrested from the Spanish, where men of all nations should be free and equal." (009-021)

> "The ships of the Free Brotherhood gathered. They came singly from every hidden cove and secret harbor of the Spanish Main." (010-042)

> "No prey, no pay. That was the Brotherhood's first and last commandment." (009-005)

## Symbolic Significance

The Free Brotherhood represents Steinbeck's early exploration of utopian community: a society of outlaws that operates on honor, consensus, and shared purpose — and is doomed precisely because it works. The Brotherhood is the novel's counterpoint to the civilization that "splits up a character" (013-052). Its destruction is part of the price Morgan pays for respectability.
