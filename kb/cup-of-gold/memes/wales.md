# Wales

## Overview

Wales is Henry Morgan's homeland and the novel's opening world. The first seven chapters of *Cup of Gold* are set in the Welsh valleys — a landscape of black glens, high rocks, ancient farmhouses, and Crag-top, where Merlin tends his speaking harps. Wales represents everything Henry leaves behind, everything that cannot follow him to the Caribbean, and everything that haunts his memory to the deathbed.

## Geography

Steinbeck's Wales is rendered in atmospheric rather than cartographic detail. The Morgan farmhouse stands "beside the high-road which lined the valley, ran up through a cleft in the hills, and so out into the world" (001-003). Crag-top rises above the valley, Merlin's dwelling visible from the farm below. The geography is symbolic: the valley is the world of ordinary life (Robert's porridge, Mother Morgan's kitchen), while the crag is the world of vision and prophecy (Merlin's harps, Gwenliana's Cumric chants). Henry moves between them but belongs to neither.

## In the Narrative

Wales dominates the novel's first half. The opening chapter establishes the Morgan farmhouse, the coming of winter, and the ancestral weight of the Morgan name — pikes on the walls from Glendower's rebellion, a great brass-bound chest, a lineage of farmers who once fought. Chapter 2 introduces Old Robert's theology of purple porridge. Chapter 3 sends Henry up Crag-top to sit at Merlin's feet.

The Welsh chapters establish the novel's entire symbolic vocabulary: wanting the moon, the golden cup, the firefly, the dark brother, the three circles of Cumric cosmology. When Henry leaves Wales at fifteen, the novel leaves it too — but it returns in memory, in Merlin's prophecies echoing across the Caribbean, and finally in the deathbed vision where "Merlin was there, herding dreams in Avalon" (014-066).

Wales is the novel's answer to the question: what did Henry Morgan sacrifice for his Cup of Gold? He sacrificed the valley, the porridge, the harps — the world where desire could be satisfied by warmth and music rather than by the sack of cities.

## Historical Context

Wales in the 17th century was a principality of the Kingdom of England, still culturally and linguistically distinct. The Cumric tongue (Welsh) was spoken in rural areas, and pre-Christian traditions — the Druidic circles, the Arthurian legends, the harp-bards — survived alongside Anglican Christianity. Steinbeck's portrayal of a Wales caught between its Celtic past and its English present is historically grounded, though the character of Merlin is a deliberate anachronism — a fragment of the Arthurian age surviving into modernity.
