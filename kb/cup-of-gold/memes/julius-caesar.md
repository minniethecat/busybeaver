Julius Caesar exists in *Cup of Gold* as a textual presence — his *[[commentaries|Commentaries]]* on the Gallic Wars sit in [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s library in Barbados, one of the few books in a colonial plantation house. The *[[commentaries|Commentaries]]* represent the classical military tradition against which the buccaneers' improvised warfare might be measured — Caesar's disciplined legions versus Morgan's ragtag Brotherhood, Roman imperium versus buccaneer republic.

[[james-flower|James Flower]], the novel's resident intellectual, keeps Caesar's work alongside other volumes that shape his peculiar worldview: King James's *Defense of Witchcraft*, Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*, and a treatise on the effects of alcohol. The *[[commentaries|Commentaries]]* represents the most prestigious of these texts — the work of a man who was both conqueror and author, general and historian. Flower's possession of it signals the residual humanism of a planter whose life has been reduced to managing sugar and slaves.

The novel does not develop Caesar as a thematic presence beyond this library scene, but the *[[commentaries|Commentaries]]* serves a quiet structural purpose: it reminds the reader that the ambition driving Henry Morgan has ancient precedents. The difference is that Caesar wrote his own story, while Morgan's is written by the forces — economic, political, and biological — that ultimately consume him.
