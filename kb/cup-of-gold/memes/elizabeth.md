# Elizabeth

**Elizabeth** is a central female character in [[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]]'s emotional landscape throughout *Cup of Gold*. She first appears as a tenant farmer's daughter in [[wales|Wales]], becomes the namesake of Morgan's ship *Elizabeth*, reappears as his orphaned cousin in [[london|London]], and ultimately becomes his wife in [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. Across these transformations, Elizabeth functions as the novel's moral compass — the "white winged soul" against which [[ysobel|Ysobel]], [[la-santa-roja|La Santa Roja]], and all other women are measured and found wanting.

## In Wales: The First Love

Elizabeth first enters the narrative through [[merlin|Merlin]]'s pointed questions to the young [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] before his departure for the Indies. Merlin discerns immediately that Henry's bluster masks a deeper anxiety about leaving Elizabeth behind:

> "I think you should be speaking to Elizabeth, not to me. Your father should have known better." [003-021]

Henry's relationship with Elizabeth in Wales is characterized by ambivalence and shame. He vacillates between hatred and desire, experiencing dreams that merge her image with that of his mother — dreams that leave him feeling monstrous:

> "Sometimes he dreamed of her, and waked in agony lest she should ever know his dream. And sometimes it was a strange, shadowy composite of Elizabeth and his mother that came to him in the night." [004-005]

The night before his departure, Henry goes to Elizabeth's hut but cannot bring himself to speak to her. He whistles a bird-call, watches her silhouette framed in the doorway, then runs away "gasping and almost sobbing under his breath" [004-020]. This failure to say goodbye becomes one of the defining regrets of his life.

## The Ship Elizabeth

Years later in [[barbados|Barbados]], having earned enough to purchase his first vessel, Morgan names her *Elizabeth* — an act that merges his childhood love with his new mistress, the sea:

> "Henry careened the ship, and when he had scraped and painted her, he named her Elizabeth and put to sea. He had what is known as 'hands' to a horseman, a warm feeling of the personality of his boat." [008-200]

The [[the-sea-motif|ship Elizabeth]] becomes the instrument of Morgan's rise. Through her, he learns navigation and seamanship, and it is aboard her that he first tastes the freedom of the [[caribbean|Caribbean]].

## The Idealized Memory

Throughout his years of [[buccaneering|buccaneering]], Morgan preserves Elizabeth as an idealized memory — the standard by which all other women fail. To [[la-santa-roja|Paulette]] in [[panama-city|Panama]], he delivers a devastating comparison:

> "May one worship a god merely because he is big, or cherish a land which has no virtue save its breadth, or love a woman whose whole realm is her flesh? Ah, Paulette! you have no soul at all! Elizabeth had a white winged soul. I love you — yes — with what you have to be loved — the body. But Elizabeth — I loved Elizabeth with my soul." [008-240]

To [[coeur-de-gris|Coeur de Gris]], Morgan recounts Elizabeth's story as the foundational tragedy of his life — the "bitter thing the years cannot forget" [008-236] that justifies all his subsequent hunger for gold and power.

## In London: The Cousin

When Morgan returns to [[london|London]] a wealthy buccaneer, he encounters a second Elizabeth: his cousin, the orphaned daughter of his uncle Sir Edward Morgan. This Elizabeth is barely fourteen, a "little girl" [008-324] who has been raised as a gentlewoman. Her father's death leaves her impoverished, and she is dispatched to [[jamaica|Jamaica]] to seek the protection of her famous cousin.

The parallel to Morgan's own story — a young person torn from home and sent across the sea — creates an ironic symmetry. Where young Henry was kidnapped and sold into [[indenture-system|indenture]], young Elizabeth is shipped off to be married.

## Marriage in Jamaica

In [[port-royal|Port Royal]], under the orchestration of Lady Moddyford, Elizabeth and Henry are brought together. Henry, now the Lieutenant Governor, plays the role of protector with genuine tenderness:

> "I am here to take your trouble from your shoulders. I will help you and care for you, Elizabeth. I wonder how you bore the grief that fell on you." [011-077]

But even in courtship, Henry's confusion between his two Elizabeths is evident. When he proposes, the childhood memory bleeds through:

> "I have startled you and hurt you. But, dear Elizabeth, there is no crime or sin about it. Many cousins marry. And we know all about each other, and our family is one. You must marry me, Elizabeth. Truly I love you, Elizabeth." [011-085]

The marriage is a settling — the fiery buccaneer trading [[wanting-the-moon|the quest for the moon]] for the comfort of "seeing the thing I expected come about" [011-054]. Elizabeth represents domestic order, a counterpoint to the chaotic ambition that defined his youth.

## The Deathbed Vision

In [[morgans-death|Morgan's final moments]], Elizabeth appears one last time — not as the wife of his later years, but as the golden-haired girl of Wales:

> "Why, it was Elizabeth coming toward him — little Elizabeth, with golden hair and a wise young look on her face. She was girdled with cornflowers, and her eyes were strangely puzzled and bright." [014-053]

This final apparition confirms Elizabeth's symbolic role: she is the road not taken, the innocence lost, the home that Morgan abandoned in his pursuit of [[the-cup-of-gold|the Cup of Gold]]. Like everything else in Morgan's dying vision, she dissolves — "in her place there was a red smoldering ember, and the light was dying out of it" [014-061].
