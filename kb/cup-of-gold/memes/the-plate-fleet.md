## Definition

The plate fleet (*flota de Indias*) in *Cup of Gold* represents the concentrated wealth of the Spanish Empire — an annual convoy of treasure ships carrying American silver, gold, and precious goods from the colonies to Spain. For the buccaneers, the plate fleet is both a military target and a symbolic one: to capture a plate ship is to strike at the heart of Spanish power and to seize the material embodiment of colonial extraction [009-002].

## In the Novel

The plate fleet appears in the novel primarily as an object of buccaneer desire and strategic calculation. The Free Brotherhood's power grows to the point where it can threaten the Spanish treasure system itself — a development that transforms the buccaneers from nuisance raiders into a geopolitical force that England and France must reckon with [009-002].

Old Robert's tales of buccaneer exploits include vivid accounts of plate-ship captures: "We took the tall plate ship they call a galleon" [001-040], with descriptions of Spanish soldiers pushed into the sea still wearing their breastplates [001-041]. These stories plant the seeds of Morgan's ambition — the plate fleet becomes the symbolic prize that represents everything the Indies can yield to a man bold enough to seize it.

The novel frames the plate fleet within a broader critique of Spanish wealth extraction. Spain's American gold funds European wars and builds cathedrals, but it also creates a moral vacuum — a hunger that no amount of treasure can satisfy. The buccaneers who prey on the plate fleet are, in this reading, symptoms of the same disease they exploit.

## Key Passages

> "The Free Brotherhood had grown to be a terrible force, strong enough to threaten the plate fleet itself." [009-002]

This passage marks the Brotherhood's transformation from scattered raiders to a systemic threat capable of disrupting the Spanish imperial economy.

> "We took the tall plate ship they call a galleon. Some we pushed into the sea with their breast plates shining." [001-040–041]

Old Robert's recollection captures both the violence and the visual spectacle of plate-ship captures — the shining breastplates sinking into the sea as emblems of Spanish wealth returning to the deep.

## Related Characters

- **Henry Morgan** — hears tales of plate ships as a boy and spends his career pursuing their equivalents
- **Old Robert** — the storyteller whose accounts of plate-fleet captures inspire young Henry [001-040]
- **The Free Brotherhood** — the buccaneer confederation whose growing power enables plate-fleet predation [009-002]
- **Edward Mansveldt** — the Dutch admiral whose vision of a buccaneer republic threatened to create a permanent rival to Spanish maritime power

## Symbolic Significance

The plate fleet embodies the central economic metaphor of the novel: wealth as a circulatory system. Gold flows from American mines to Spanish coffers, then to English and French pockets through privateering, then to London courts through knighthood purchases. The plate fleet is the artery through which this wealth initially travels, and the buccaneer who intercepts it is both a parasite on empire and an agent of redistribution. The novel suggests that all the participants in this system — Spanish officials, English privateers, Caribbean merchants — are ultimately serving the same god of gold, merely at different points along its circulatory path.
