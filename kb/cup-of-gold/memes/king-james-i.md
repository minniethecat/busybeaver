King James I appears in *Cup of Gold* only through his written work — *[[defense-of-witchcraft|Defense of Witchcraft]]*, a treatise on demonology that was immensely influential in early seventeenth-century England. The novel uses James's book as a darkly comic plot device: young [[james-flower|James Flower]], reading the king's work, becomes convinced that incantations and hashish will enable him to fly from his roof. The experiment ends with two broken legs [008-??] and sets in motion the chain of events that leads Flower to Barbados, where he will eventually purchase Henry Morgan's indenture.

James's role in the novel is thus that of an unwitting catalyst — his royal authority lent credence to witchcraft beliefs that, in the novel's treatment, produce only farce and injury. The episode serves as a minor satire of royal pretension: a king's learned treatise on the supernatural leads a young man to jump off a roof.

The novel does not engage with James's reign in any substantive historical sense. He exists purely as the author of one book in [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s library, alongside Reginald Scot's skeptical counter-text *[[discoverie-of-witchcraft|Discoverie of Witchcraft]]* — the two books representing the dialectic of belief and skepticism that shaped Flower's peculiar intellectual formation.
