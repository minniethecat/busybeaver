# Morgan's Marriage

**Morgan's marriage to his cousin [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]** in [[port-royal|Port Royal]], [[jamaica|Jamaica]], is the pivotal event that transforms [[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]] from a restless [[buccaneering|buccaneer]] into a settled colonial governor. Orchestrated by Lady Moddyford and accepted by both parties for reasons more practical than passionate, the marriage encapsulates the novel's central theme: the trade of [[wanting-the-moon|wild ambition]] for comfortable certainty.

## Prelude: The Orphan Cousin

The marriage is set in motion by the death of Sir Edward Morgan, Henry's uncle and the Lieutenant Governor of Jamaica. His daughter [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]] — the same Elizabeth Henry left behind in [[wales|Wales]] years before, now revealed as his cousin — is left impoverished:

> "I have several things to speak of, and the time is very short. What will you do? There is little money left. We have been living on my salary ever since the King made his last general suggestion for a loan." [009-168]

Sir Edward's dying instruction to Elizabeth is pragmatic to the point of bitterness: "Perhaps your cousin who has come to such fame through robbery will care for you, Elizabeth. I am pained at the thought, but — but — it is necessary to live — very necessary" [009-170].

## Lady Moddyford's Orchestration

In [[port-royal|Port Royal]], Lady Moddyford — the governor's wife — takes charge of the matchmaking with the efficiency of a military campaign. She dismisses Elizabeth's scruples about marrying a cousin:

> "Dear Elizabeth, don't you know by now that almost any woman can marry almost any man as long as some other woman doesn't interfere? And I shall arrange matters so that no one will get in your way." [011-034]

Elizabeth, for her part, resolves to deploy her musical accomplishments as weapons of courtship:

> "I shall play for him. I have heard how music affects these fierce men. I shall play him my new pieces — 'The Elves' Concourse,' and 'God Bears the Weary Soul to Rest.'" [011-035]

## The Proposal

When Henry and Elizabeth finally meet in Jamaica, the proposal unfolds with a mix of genuine tenderness and revealing confusion. Henry slips from formal concern to emotional declaration:

> "I am here to take your trouble from your shoulders. I will help you and care for you, Elizabeth. I wonder how you bore the grief that fell on you. You have been brave to hold your head so high when misery was tugging at your spirit." [011-077]

But when he finally speaks the word "marry," it comes with the startling force of something held back too long:

> "Oh, surely I meant marry. What else could you have thought I meant? I am only clumsy and crude about it. I have startled you and hurt you. But, dear Elizabeth, there is no crime or sin about it. Many cousins marry." [011-085]

## The Meaning of the Marriage

The marriage represents Morgan's surrender to the ordinary. The man who once told [[la-santa-roja|Paulette]] that he loved Elizabeth "with my soul" [008-240] now marries her with the weariness of a man who has stopped reaching for the moon:

> "Unsure things are distracting. Chance has not the tug on me it once had. I was a fool, Elizabeth. I went sailing and sailing looking for something — well, something that did not exist, perhaps. And now that I have lost my unnamable desires, I may not be happier, but there is more content on me." [011-054]

This is the philosophy of a man who has traded [[the-cup-of-gold|the Cup of Gold]] for the certainty of a well-tuned harpsichord — "It is nice to know that everything is to be in its place — even notes" [011-054].
