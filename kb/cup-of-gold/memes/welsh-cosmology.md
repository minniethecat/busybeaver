# Welsh Cosmology

## Definition

The Welsh cosmological system embedded in Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold* derives from Cumric (Welsh Druidic) tradition, as transmitted through the character of Gwenliana, Henry Morgan's grandmother. It describes three concentric circles of existence: **Abred**, the outer circle of earth and water where souls struggle through successive incarnations; **Gwynfyd**, the middle circle of purity and light; and **Annwn**, the inner void — chaos, annihilation, the unformed. Gwenliana's prophecy, delivered in the Cumric tongue over the infant Henry, traces his soul's journey through all three.

## In the Novel

Gwenliana's prophecy (chapter 5) is the novel's mythological seed. Speaking in Cumric — a language no one else in the household fully understands — she chants over Henry: "This child will pass through Abred, the circle of earth and water, where all things struggle. He will rise through Gwynfyd, the circle of purity, where all things are known. And he will escape Annwn, the circle of the void, though it will reach for him with dark hands" (005-031).

The prophecy is overheard by the men of the household but dismissed. Old Robert calls it "woman's foolishness." Merlin, however, takes it seriously. The three circles map precisely onto Henry's life: Abred is his buccaneer years (earth=Panama, water=the Caribbean); Gwynfyd is his knighthood and governorship (purity=respectability); Annwn is the void that reaches for him in his deathbed vision — Brother Death's grotto.

The cosmology also connects to the novel's Arthurian layer. Merlin speaks of "the Chair of Arthur" and "the Cauldron of Annwn" — Welsh mythological artifacts that place Henry's story within a longer Celtic tradition of quest, transformation, and return. The Christian framework (baptism, the Virgin, the Te Deum) and the Druidic framework coexist uneasily in the novel, neither fully triumphing — a tension that mirrors Morgan's own divided soul.

## Symbolic Significance

The Welsh cosmological framework provides the novel with a non-Christian eschatology that parallels but does not replace the Christian imagery (baptism, the Virgin, the Te Deum). The two systems coexist in productive tension — as do the novel's Celtic and Caribbean worlds. Gwenliana's prophecy is never disproved; it is simply forgotten by the men who dismiss it, while quietly unfolding across the novel's fourteen chapters.
