# Plymouth

## Overview

Plymouth is an English Channel port mentioned in the novel as a node in the economic network that connects metropolitan England to the Caribbean buccaneer world. It represents the English side of the colonial equation — the home ports from which ships sail and to which news and treasure return, and the market where "Plymouth men would buy old, useless ships and sell them to the backward Spanish colonies at a fine profit" (010-045).

## Geography

Plymouth is a major port city on the south coast of Devon, England, situated on Plymouth Sound where the River Plym and River Tamar meet the English Channel. It has been England's principal naval base on the Channel since the sixteenth century. Sir Francis Drake sailed from Plymouth, and it was from Plymouth that the Mayflower departed in 1620 — referenced obliquely in the novel's mention of "the colony at Plymouth" (008-027).

## In the Narrative

Plymouth appears in three contexts that triangulate England's relationship to the colonial and buccaneer world.

First, as a site of religious admonition: the novel's Welsh farm-dwellers hear sermons that mention "the colony at Plymouth" (008-027) as an example of Puritan settlement — a reference to the Plymouth Colony in Massachusetts, not the English port, but which echoes the port's historical association with colonial departure.

Second, as a destination for news: Morgan's reputation is such that "his words found their way to Plymouth, and Nieuw Amsterdam" (010-041). The pairing of Plymouth and New Amsterdam (New York) maps the Anglo-Dutch Atlantic — the metropolitan ports where colonial news is consumed and acted upon.

Third, and most tellingly, as a market for exploitation: "these Plymouth men would buy old, useless ships and sell them to the backward Spanish colonies at a fine profit" (010-045). This cynical observation captures the economic ecology in which buccaneering operates — English merchants profit from selling substandard ships to the same Spanish colonies that English buccaneers raid. Plymouth thus represents the respectable side of the same colonial extraction economy that Morgan's violence serves.

## Historical Context

Plymouth was one of England's principal naval and commercial ports throughout the early modern period. Sir Francis Drake, Sir John Hawkins, and other Elizabethan sea dogs sailed from Plymouth, establishing its association with maritime adventure and privateering. The city was a major point of embarkation for colonists bound for North America and the Caribbean. The Plymouth Company and the port's merchants were heavily involved in the Atlantic trade, including the triangular trade in sugar, slaves, and manufactured goods.
