# Henry's Indenture

## Summary

Henry Morgan's indenture on James Flower's Barbados plantation is the foundational trauma of the novel — the experience of being sold into servitude, stripped of freedom and name, that transforms a Welsh farm boy into the man who will spend the rest of his life accumulating power to ensure he can never be owned again. It is also the setting of his sexual awakening with Paulette and his intellectual awakening through Flower's classical library, making the indenture years a paradoxical period of degradation and formation.

## Context

Henry Morgan is captured and bound by unknown assailants on a dark night in Wales, his arms "pinned to my sides" while Elizabeth "was torn away from me" (009-105). He is placed on a ship and sold in Barbados as an indentured servant — a legal status that, in the seventeenth-century Caribbean, differed little from temporary slavery. The man who buys him is James Flower, a planter with a classical library and a streak of intellectual curiosity unusual among the colonial plantocracy.

## Narrative Account

The indenture years are recounted primarily in retrospect, through Morgan's confession to Coeur de Gris aboard the ship to Panama. "On one dark night my arms were pinned to my sides, and she—oh, dear Elizabeth!—was torn away from me. They placed me, still bound, in a ship, and sold me in Barbados" (009-105). The passive construction — he is acted upon, he does not act — captures the essence of indenture as the negation of agency.

On Flower's plantation, Henry encounters two formative forces. The first is Paulette, a slave girl of mixed ancestry who becomes his lover and initiates him into physical desire. She represents, in the novel's symbolic economy, "flesh-love" — the earthly, bodily passion that stands in opposition to the idealized, soul-love Henry associates with Elizabeth.

The second is James Flower's library. Flower, an intellectual manqué, gives Henry access to classical texts — a rare privilege for an indentured servant. This exposure to learning shapes Henry's sense of himself as more than his station, planting the seed of ambition that will later manifest as military genius.

Flower sets Henry free at Christmas — a gesture of paternalistic benevolence that does nothing to diminish Henry's resentment of the system that made such benevolence necessary. The Christmas release is, in narrative terms, a birth: the indentured boy dies and the buccaneer is born.

## Outcome

The indenture leaves Henry with an indelible scar — the knowledge that freedom is contingent and can be taken by force. His subsequent career as a buccaneer, his accumulation of wealth, his pursuit of knighthood and governorship: all can be read as attempts to build a fortress around a self that was once rendered utterly vulnerable. The bitterness of indenture "lies restlessly in my heart" (009-105) throughout his life, even when he has achieved everything the indentured boy could have dreamed of.

## Historical Basis

Henry Morgan's historical indenture is not clearly documented. Some sources suggest he went to the Caribbean as a member of the Cromwellian expedition that captured Jamaica in 1655, while others (including Exquemelin) claim he was indentured in Barbados. Steinbeck adopts the indenture narrative because it serves the novel's thematic architecture: the buccaneer as a man fleeing his own history of subjugation.
