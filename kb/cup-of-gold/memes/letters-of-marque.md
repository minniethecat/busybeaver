## Definition

A letter of marque in *Cup of Gold* is a legal commission issued by the Crown authorizing a private ship to capture enemy vessels during wartime. The document transforms what would otherwise be piracy into an act of sanctioned warfare. For Henry Morgan and his buccaneers, the letter of marque represents the fragile legal fiction that separates plunder from crime: with it, they are privateers serving the Crown; without it, they are pirates subject to the gibbet.

## In the Novel

The letter of marque functions as a recurring source of tension throughout Morgan's career. His raids on Spanish settlements exist in a legal gray zone: Spain considers them acts of piracy regardless of English commissions, while England selectively enforces the distinction depending on diplomatic convenience. After the sack of Panama, Morgan discovers that his letter of marque — which he believed authorized his campaign — is retroactively treated as void by a Crown that needs to appease Spanish diplomacy.

The novel portrays letters of marque as instruments of sovereign convenience rather than genuine legal protection. The Crown issues them to project power without expense, then disavows them when the political winds shift. Morgan's arrest and transportation to England after Panama illustrates the letter's fundamental fragility: the same document that legitimized his raids cannot protect him when those raids become diplomatically inconvenient.

The buccaneers themselves are acutely aware of the letter's ambiguity. Edward Mansveldt's dream of a buccaneer republic [009-??] represents a desire to escape the letter-of-marque system entirely — to create a nation that needs no Crown's permission to exist.

## Key Passages

> "The king had only to grant him land in the Indies... for the good of the English crown." [008-004]

This passage, while not explicitly about letters of marque, captures the Crown's instrumental use of legal documents to delegate violence while retaining deniability.

## Related Characters

- **Henry Morgan** — his career oscillates between privateer and pirate depending on which letter of marque is currently recognized
- **Edward Mansveldt** — the Dutch admiral who sought to transcend the letter-of-marque system by founding an independent buccaneer state
- **The King** (Charles II) — the ultimate source of letters of marque, who grants and revokes them according to political expediency
- **Lord Vaughn** — the court intermediary through whom the Crown's shifting legal positions are communicated to the colonies

## Symbolic Significance

The letter of marque embodies the novel's broader concern with the relationship between law and power. The document does not determine whether an act is piracy or legitimate warfare; power does. The letter is merely power's written form — valid when the powerful choose to honor it, meaningless when they choose to ignore it. Morgan's experience with letters of marque mirrors his experience with knighthood and royal favor: all are forms of institutional recognition that prove hollow when tested against political reality.
