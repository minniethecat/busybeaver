# Merlin

## Role

Merlin is the ancient Welsh seer and harper who inhabits Crag-top, a mountain above the Morgan farm. He functions as Henry Morgan's spiritual mentor, the keeper of pre-Christian Welsh memory, and the voice of the novel's founding metaphor. His harps — three instruments that "speak" rather than merely play — serve as narrative devices: they prophesy, lament, and preserve the stories of Arthur, Avalon, and the Celtic past that Christianity has not quite extinguished in the Welsh valleys.

## Biography

Merlin lives alone on Crag-top, his dwelling a cave-like space filled with harps. He is old beyond reckoning — the novel hints he may be the Merlin of Arthurian legend, survived into a diminished age. The local villagers regard him with a mixture of fear and reverence. He speaks Cumric (ancient Welsh) and communes with a world that predates the Christianization of Wales.

When the boy Henry climbs Crag-top to visit him, Merlin studies the child's face and delivers the novel's defining diagnosis: "You are a little boy. You want the moon to drink from as a golden cup; and so, it is very likely that you will become a great man — if only you remain a little child" (003-033). The speech is prophecy and warning fused: greatness is possible, but only if the child's hunger is preserved into manhood.

Merlin's own story mirrors Henry's in miniature. He too "wanted the moon" — in his case, to become the greatest bard in Wales. He achieved it, and like Henry, found the achievement hollow: "I wanted it. Above all desires I wanted it. I reached for it and then — then I grew to be a man, and a failure" (003-035). Unlike Henry, Merlin found a kind of peace in his failure, retreating to Crag-top to tend his harps and watch the valley below.

His harps are extensions of his consciousness. They are named for the pieces they play: "The Elves' Concourse," "God Bears the Weary Soul to Rest," and a third that "speaks" rather than plays — telling the story of Arthur, the Chair, and Avalon. When Henry leaves Wales, Merlin's harp plays "The Sorrow of Plaith," an elegy that follows Henry across the ocean.

In the deathbed vision (chapter 14), Merlin appears — not as a character, but as an image: "Merlin was there, herding dreams in Avalon" (014-066). The image closes the circle: the boy who climbed Crag-top has become the dying governor, and the seer who named his hunger is still tending the dreams that outlast individual lives.

## Historical Basis

The Merlin of *Cup of Gold* is entirely fictional, though he draws on the Merlin of Arthurian legend — the wizard, prophet, and advisor to kings — as transmitted through Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae* and the Welsh *Mabinogion*. Steinbeck's innovation is to place Merlin in a realistic 17th-century Welsh setting, treating him not as a wizard but as a surviving remnant of the pre-Christian Celtic world, his magic reduced to music and memory.
