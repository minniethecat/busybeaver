# Mediocrity as Disease

## Definition

"Mediocrity as disease" is one of the most striking thematic inversions in Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold*. Conventionally, excessive ambition is the sickness and contentment the cure. Steinbeck — through Grippo, the old pirate — inverts this: it is the *absence* of desire, the settling into satisfaction, that constitutes illness. When Henry Morgan has taken Panama and achieved everything he wanted, he does not triumph. He sickens. Grippo diagnoses him: "A short and very bitter illness, a disease called mediocrity" (010-387).

## In the Novel

The concept is introduced in embryo in the novel's opening chapters. Old Robert Morgan, Henry's father, is the prototype: a man who "wanted the moon" as a boy, reached for it, failed, and settled into the quiet life of a Welsh farmer. Merlin tells the young Henry: "There is this gift for the failure: the knowledge that he has tried" (003-035). The implication is clear: the trying matters more than the succeeding, and the man who stops trying — regardless of his achievements — has failed in a deeper sense.

The concept crystallizes in the aftermath of Panama. Morgan has taken the richest city in the New World. He has found La Santa Roja, and she has refused him. He has murdered Coeur de Gris out of jealousy. And now, in the moment of his greatest worldly success, he collapses. Grippo finds him ill and delivers the diagnosis:

> "I think I mourn for some other thing which is dead. He might have been a vital half of me, which, dying, leaves me half a man. Do you know what is wrong with me, Grippo? ... A short and very bitter illness, a disease called mediocrity." (010-387, 010-393)

The illness is not physical. It is existential. Morgan has achieved everything — and now there is nothing left to want. Without desire, he is "half a man." The firefly, which envied the moon, has captured it — and found it cold.

The concept reaches its philosophical culmination in Morgan's deathbed chapter. As Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, surrounded by the trappings of respectability, he confronts his younger, ambitious self: "Civilization will split up a character, and he who refuses to split goes under" (013-052). Mediocrity, in this framing, is civilization's price: to be respectable is to be divided, to lose the unified hunger that drove the buccaneer.

## Symbolic Significance

The metaphor reframes ambition as vitality. To want is to be alive; to cease wanting is to sicken. This inverts the conventional morality of the cautionary tale — beware of wanting too much — into something closer to Nietzsche: the man who has exhausted his will to power has exhausted his reason for living. The novel's final judgment on Morgan is not that he was wicked, but that he stopped wanting.
