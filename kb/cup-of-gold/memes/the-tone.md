# The Tone

## Definition

The Tone is the sonic-metaphysical event that concludes Henry Morgan's deathbed vision and the novel itself. As Brother Death presents Morgan with the accumulated record of his deeds, the vision's visual and narrative elements dissolve into pure sound — a single tone that "grew and grew until it filled the universe" (014-065). It is not music, not voice, not noise, but the condition of all sound: the fundamental vibration beneath distinction. In merging with the Tone, Morgan ceases to be Henry Morgan. The novel ends.

## In the Novel

The Tone appears only in the final paragraphs of chapter 14, but it is prepared throughout the novel by the recurring motif of music — particularly Merlin's harps. The old seer's harps "speak" rather than play: they tell stories, prophesy, lament. "The Elves' Concourse" and "God Bears the Weary Soul to Rest" are named pieces that function as narrative devices — sound that carries meaning. The Tone is the culmination of this motif: sound that has shed all particularity and become pure presence.

The deathbed sequence moves through several stages: first the visual (the grotto, Brother Death, the faceless deeds), then the dialogic (Morgan's interrogation by his own actions), and finally the dissolution into the Tone. The narrative voice — which has followed Morgan from his Welsh boyhood — falls silent. "And then there was the Tone" (014-065). The absence of a period, the trailing off — Steinbeck's prose performs the dissolution it describes.

Merlin's prophecy is fulfilled in the Tone. Gwenliana had said Morgan would "escape Annwn, the circle of the void, though it will reach for him with dark hands" (005-031). The Tone is not Annwn — it is not void — but the passage through Annwn into something that cannot be named, only heard.

## Key Passages

> "And then there was the Tone. It began softly, a low humming that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere. It grew and grew until it filled the universe. It was the sound behind all sounds, the note that contains all notes." (014-065)

> "The Tone took him. He was the Tone. There was no Henry Morgan." (014-067)

> "You will escape Annwn, the circle of the void, though it will reach for you with dark hands." (005-031)

## Symbolic Significance

The Tone is Steinbeck's answer to the problem of ending a life-narrative. A conventional death scene — last words, reconciliation, closure — would falsify the novel's vision of a divided self. Morgan cannot be reconciled because he cannot recognize his own deeds. The only honest ending is dissolution: the self, unable to integrate its record, simply ceases to be a self. The Tone is not redemption but erasure — and, paradoxically, it is the most peaceful moment in the novel.
