## Definition

Colonial ambition in *Cup of Gold* is the pragmatic, worldly counterpart to the novel's central metaphor of unattainable desire. Where "[[wanting-the-moon|Wanting the Moon]]" represents metaphysical yearning — the hunger for something beyond reach — colonial ambition represents the concrete pursuit of gold, land, titles, and power within the reachable world of the seventeenth-century Caribbean. The novel treats colonial ambition as both the engine of individual achievement and a form of spiritual emptiness.

## In the Novel

Henry Morgan embodies colonial ambition from his first departure from Wales. His decision to sail for the Indies is framed not as a quest for adventure but as a calculated response to limited options: a younger son without inheritance, he seeks wealth in the colonies because the old world offers nothing [008-001–006].

Morgan's trajectory maps the arc of colonial ambition from promise to disillusionment. He rises from indentured servant to buccaneer admiral to knighted governor — an ascent that colonial ambition makes possible. Yet each achievement brings him further from contentment.

The novel systematically contrasts colonial ambition with deeper forms of desire. Morgan's pursuit of Panama is framed in colonial-ambition terms — gold, glory, strategic advantage — but his private reflections reveal a hollowness beneath the material goals. After achieving everything colonial ambition can offer — wealth, a knighthood, the governorship of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] — Morgan dies isolated and haunted, suggesting that colonial ambition fulfills its promises without satisfying the soul.

The novel presents colonial ambition as a force that consumes its practitioners. The buccaneers, the Spanish officials, the English planters, and Morgan himself are all caught in a system where ambition drives relentless expansion but delivers diminishing returns. The "ambition" that once sent Morgan across the Atlantic becomes, by the novel's end, indistinguishable from exhaustion.

## Key Passages

> "Younger sons, spendthrifts, ruined gentlemen sailed out for the Indies. It was a fine way to be rid of a dangerous man." [008-005]

The Crown's perspective on colonial emigration frames ambition as a form of social waste disposal — the ambitious are exported to the colonies where their energies can be safely consumed.

> "Two days ago I planned to tear a continent out of the set order and crown it with a capital of gold for you. In my mind, I built up an empire for you, and planned the diadem you should wear. And now I dimly remember the person who thought these things." [011-??]

Morgan's retrospective reflection on his own ambition reveals its essential strangeness — the ambitious self becomes unrecognizable once the ambition cools.

## Related Characters

- **Henry Morgan** — the novel's central study in colonial ambition; his arc traces ambition's rise, fulfillment, and hollow aftermath
- **Edward Mansveldt** — the Dutch buccaneer whose dream of a buccaneer republic represents the ultimate colonial ambition: not merely to raid empire but to found one
- **James Flower** — Morgan's bookish companion whose classical education frames colonial ambition within the longer history of empire and conquest
- **The Buccaneers** — collectively, they represent ambition stripped of ideology, pursuing wealth with neither national loyalty nor moral pretense

## Symbolic Significance

Colonial ambition functions as the novel's critique of worldly success. By paralleling Morgan's material ascent with his spiritual decline, Steinbeck suggests that colonial ambition is a zero-sum transaction: what it grants in wealth and status, it extracts in authenticity and peace. The concept is distinct from the Cup of Gold — which represents a purer, more existential form of desire — but the two are linked: colonial ambition is what happens when the desire for transcendence is channeled into the pursuit of gold.
