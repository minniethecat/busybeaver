# Sir Henry Morgan

## Role

Sir Henry Morgan is the protagonist of John Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold* (1929), a historical fiction loosely based on the life of the Welsh buccaneer who sacked Panama in 1671. The novel traces Morgan's entire arc: from a restless farm boy in the Welsh valleys, through five years of indentured servitude in Barbados, to his rise as the most successful captain of the Free Brotherhood of the Coast, his conquest of the "Cup of Gold" (Panama), his knighthood and appointment as Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, and his deathbed reckoning with the accumulated record of his deeds.

## Biography

### Wales: The Boy Who Wanted the Moon

Henry Morgan is born on a farm in the Welsh valleys, the son of Old Robert Morgan and Mother Morgan. As a boy, he climbs Crag-top to sit at the feet of Merlin, the ancient seer and harper. Merlin recognizes in him the hunger that defines greatness: "You want the moon to drink from as a golden cup; and so, it is very likely that you will become a great man — if only you remain a little child" (003-033). This diagnosis becomes the novel's organizing metaphor.

Henry's restlessness is contrasted with his father's contentment. Old Robert once wanted the moon too, but "grew to be a man, and a failure" (003-035) — a failure who nevertheless possesses "the knowledge that he has tried." Henry is determined not to follow his father's path.

At fifteen, driven by a mysterious inner compulsion he cannot name, Henry leaves Wales. Merlin's parting gift is a prophecy: Henry will be "a soldier and a conqueror" and will marry "a pale, proud lady" (005-032). Gwenliana, his grandmother, chants a darker prophecy in Cumric: he will pass through the three circles — Abred (struggle), Gwynfyd (purity), and escape Annwn (the void).

### Barbados: The Indenture

Henry's entry into the Caribbean is brutal. Tim the Bo's'n, a Welsh sailor who befriended him on the crossing, betrays him — selling him to a skull-faced Puritan master for eleven pounds (008-122). Henry is indentured to James Flower, a Barbados planter and intellectual recluse.

The five years on Flower's plantation are transformative. Flower treats Henry as a student, giving him access to a library of classical and modern works. From Caesar's *Commentaries*, Henry learns that "all military operations were, at base, glorified trickery" (008-176). From Xenophon, he learns the management of men. When Flower sets him free at Christmas, Henry purchases a ship — the *Elizabeth*, named for his childhood love — and begins his independent life in the Caribbean.

### The Free Brotherhood: Rise to Captaincy

Henry rises through the ranks of the Free Brotherhood of the Coast with a combination of tactical brilliance and ruthless calculation. He serves under Grippo aboard the *Ganymede*, buying command for five hundred pounds. His early expeditions — Puerto Bello, Maracaibo — establish his reputation. The sack of Maracaibo yields two hundred and fifty thousand pieces of eight (009-112), but the treasure does not satisfy him.

What drives Morgan is not gold — or not gold alone. The legend of La Santa Roja, the Red Saint of Panama, has infected the buccaneer world like a fever. Morgan hears of her from captured Spaniards and fellow captains alike: a woman of unearthly beauty, an heiress of Cordova with silver mines in Peru, worshipped almost as a saint. She becomes conflated in Morgan's mind with Panama itself — the Cup of Gold. He sends her a great ruby with a message: "I will go to the Cup of Gold within the year" (009-061).

### Panama: The Conquest

The Panama expedition is the novel's centerpiece. Morgan assembles a fleet of thirty-seven ships at Tortuga, draws the articles of agreement, and sails for the Isthmus. The crossing is brutal: jungle, fever, Spanish ambushes. Henry's army of buccaneers defeats the Spanish cavalry on the plain outside Panama — the governor's ingenious stampede of bulls is turned — and the city falls.

In the conquered city, Morgan finds the actual golden chalice that materializes the metaphor: "a lovely, slender chalice with long curved handles and a rim of silver. Around its outer edge four grotesque lamias chased each other" (010-281). He drinks from it. He also finds La Santa Roja — and is refused. She tells him: "You will take no more cups of gold. You will turn no more vain dreams into unsatisfactory conquests" (010-393).

The conquest's darkest moment is personal: Morgan murders Coeur de Gris, his young French lieutenant and friend, in a fit of jealousy over La Santa Roja (010-292). The murder haunts him to his deathbed.

### Jamaica: The Governor

Morgan returns to England, is briefly imprisoned as a diplomatic gesture to Spain, then pardoned and knighted by Charles II. He is appointed Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica — the buccaneer turned enforcer of the King's law.

In Jamaica, Morgan marries his cousin Elizabeth — "the duty of appearances" (013-058) — and presides over the trial of two Burgundian pirates. The trial forces him to confront his divided self: the man who hangs buccaneers in the morning once led them in the evening. "Civilization will split up a character," he reflects, "and he who refuses to split goes under" (013-052).

### Death: The Grotto

Morgan's death (chapter 14) is the novel's metaphysical summit. His consciousness descends into a grotto where Brother Death presents him with the faceless record of his deeds. Morgan cannot recognize them as his own. The vision dissolves into the Tone — a sound beyond sound that absorbs all distinction. "He was the Tone. There was no Henry Morgan" (014-067).

## Historical Basis

The historical Sir Henry Morgan (c.1635–1688) was a Welsh privateer who became Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica. Steinbeck's novel is loosely biographical but freely fictionalized: Morgan's Welsh childhood, his relationship with Merlin, his obsession with La Santa Roja, and his deathbed vision are all inventions. The historical Morgan did sack Panama (1671), was briefly imprisoned, was knighted, and died in Port Royal — probably of dropsy or tuberculosis, not metaphysical dissolution. Steinbeck uses the historical skeleton to explore themes of ambition, identity, and the cost of success that transcend the historical record.
