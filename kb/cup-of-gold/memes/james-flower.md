# James Flower

## Role

James Flower is a Barbadian planter who purchases Henry Morgan's indenture and becomes, over five years of evening conversations, an unlikely mentor and father figure to the young Welshman. A quiet, kindly, and ineffectual gentleman with thwarted intellectual ambitions, Flower provides Henry with the classical and philosophical education that later distinguishes Morgan from his buccaneer peers. His dependence on Henry's competence reverses the master–servant relationship, and their parting is one of the novel's most tender scenes.

## Biography

James Flower was born into an English family of modest means. His early life was shaped by intellectual ambition: he immersed himself in the philosophical currents of his time, including the system of Descartes, and dreamed of reducing all philosophy to a single postulate [008-145]. An ill-advised attempt to verify the "fantastic theories" of atomism — the idea that all matter is composed of indivisible particles — led to his disgrace when he inadvertently burned down a wing of his family home [008-146]. His family's small fortune was subsequently invested in a Barbados plantation, and Flower was sent to the island in effective exile [008-146].

In Barbados, Flower became "a quiet, kindly little gentleman, somewhat ineffectual and very inefficient" [008-147]. He lacked the force of personality to command his slaves and indentured servants, retreating instead into his library of classical and philosophical works.

When Henry Morgan arrived as an indentured boy, something in Henry's "silent misery" touched Flower [008-150]. Rather than treating Henry as mere labor, Flower took him under his wing. In the evenings, the two would sit on the veranda, and Flower "poured his unrelated facts into the ears of young Henry Morgan" [008-161] — teaching him of Rome, Greece, military history, and philosophy.

Within a year, Henry had gained Flower's "paramount respect" by demonstrating a brilliance that Flower himself lacked — the ability not merely to accumulate facts but to synthesize them into strategy [008-175]. Henry gradually became "master of the plantation," while Flower "pitifully leaned on his advice and his convictions" [008-185]. For Flower, this was a deliverance: "James Flower was deliciously happy — happier than he had ever been — for this boy had lifted the hideous weight of the plantation from his shoulders" [008-186].

When Henry declared his intention to leave for [[port-royal|Port Royal]] and take up buccaneering, Flower was devastated. "It will be very lonely here without you," he said — words that echoed Old Robert Morgan's farewell in Wales [008-253]. Henry felt "a curious, shamefaced love for James Flower" [008-201] and secured Flower's promise to protect [[paulette]] before departing. Flower accompanied Henry to the shore, sitting "very red and very silent" in the canoe's stern [008-266].

## Relationships

- **[[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]]** — Indentured servant, then protégé, then de facto master of the plantation. Their relationship inverted the master–servant hierarchy; Henry became the son Flower never had, and Flower became the intellectual father Henry needed.
- **[[paulette]]** — Henry's slave concubine. Flower promised Henry he would protect her after Henry's departure, though he struggled even to remember her name.
- **[[old-robert-morgan|Old Robert Morgan]]** — Parallel figure. Both older men served as father figures to Henry, and both were left lonely by his departure.
