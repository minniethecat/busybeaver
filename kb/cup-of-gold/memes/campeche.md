# Campeche

## Overview

Campeche is a Spanish colonial port on the Gulf of Mexico, one of the cities mentioned in the novel's roll call of buccaneer conquests. Its sack by the buccaneer Lewis Scot — "Lewis Scot pillaged Campeche and left it a black, smoking ruin" (009-010) — is cited as a precedent for Morgan's own ambitions, part of the escalating sequence of buccaneer attacks on Spanish colonial cities.

## Geography

Campeche (San Francisco de Campeche) lies on the western coast of the Yucatán Peninsula, facing the Gulf of Mexico. A walled city in the Spanish colonial period, it was a major port for the export of Yucatán dyewood (logwood) and other colonial products. Its fortifications were built in response to repeated pirate and buccaneer attacks.

## In the Narrative

Campeche appears in the novel's historical survey of buccaneer exploits that precedes Morgan's own career. The sack of Campeche by Lewis Scot is listed alongside Mansveldt's dream of a buccaneer republic and L'Ollonais's atrocities as part of the buccaneer tradition Morgan inherits. A "boat loaded with supplies for Campeche" (008-343) is mentioned as a prize that draws buccaneer interest, suggesting the city's role as a supply hub in the Spanish colonial network.

The reference serves a structural function: by the time Morgan plans his Panama expedition, Campeche has already been sacked. The escalation of buccaneer ambition — from small ports to great cities — is plotted geographically across the Caribbean, and Campeche is one of the rungs on that ladder.

## Historical Context

The historical Campeche was repeatedly attacked by pirates and buccaneers throughout the seventeenth century. Lewis Scot's raid on Campeche is documented in Exquemelin's *The Buccaneers of America*. The city's massive walls, still standing today as a UNESCO World Heritage site, were built largely in response to the buccaneer threat. Campeche was a key port in the Spanish colonial system, exporting dyewood, hardwoods, and agricultural products from the Yucatán.
