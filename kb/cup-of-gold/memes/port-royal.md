# Port Royal

## Overview

Port Royal is the principal town and harbor of Jamaica, and the seat of Sir Henry Morgan's power as Lieutenant-Governor. It represents the final phase of Morgan's life: the buccaneer turned enforcer of the Crown's law, the predator domesticated. When Morgan arrives as governor, Port Royal is a muddy, vice-ridden buccaneer port — "the wickedest city in the world," in contemporary accounts. When he dies there, it has become a respectable colonial outpost, and he has become a ghost.

## In the Narrative

Port Royal enters the novel late (chapters 12-14) but dominates its final movement. Morgan arrives as Lieutenant-Governor, knighted and pardoned, to govern the very buccaneers he once led. The irony is structural: the man who sacked Panama now hangs men for sacking Spanish settlements.

The trial of the two Burgundian pirates (chapter 13) is the novel's dramatization of Port Royal's divided nature. Morgan sits as judge over men who committed the same crimes he committed. His duty requires him to condemn them. "Civilization will split up a character," he reflects, "and he who refuses to split goes under" (013-052).

Morgan's death in Port Royal (chapter 14) is the novel's conclusion. The governor's palace — his seat of respectability — becomes the site of his metaphysical dissolution. Brother Death comes to him here, in the heart of the civilization he helped build.

## Historical Context

The historical Port Royal was founded by the English in 1655 and became the most important English port in the Caribbean. It was notorious for its concentration of buccaneers, prostitutes, and taverns. In 1692 — four years after Morgan's death — a massive earthquake destroyed two-thirds of the city, which contemporary preachers called God's judgment on a wicked city. The remains of Port Royal are now submerged.
