# Dafydd

## Role

Dafydd is the old buccaneer who returns to the Welsh valleys from the Caribbean, bringing with him tales of adventure and plunder that ignite young Henry Morgan's desire to go to the Indies. He serves as the novel's first bridge between the grey Welsh domestic world and the violent, glittering Caribbean that awaits Henry.

## Biography

Dafydd returns to the Welsh valleys after years in the Caribbean, unrecognizable at first — "gray-white and toughened like a dry hide," his face so stiff and thick that he "seemed to change expression with slow, conscious effort" `[001-029]`. Old Robert Morgan identifies him: "Can it be Dafydd? our little farm lad Dafydd that went away to sea years past?" `[001-026]`. Dafydd's reply is haunted: "It's Dafydd, sure, and rich — and cold," spoken with "a wistfulness like a recurring pain" `[001-028]`.

Mother Morgan treats him as though he had never left, sitting him down with a steaming cup and noting how young Henry "gobbles you with his eyes" — already seeing the hunger for the Indies in the boy's gaze `[001-034]`. Dafydd keeps silence at first, "straining back at a desire to talk," still frightened of Mother Morgan as he had been as a towheaded farm boy `[001-035]`.

When he does speak, his tales are vivid and terrible. He describes pushing Spanish soldiers into the sea "with their breast plates shining and shimmering as they went down — grand Spanish soldiers and bubbles coming out of their mouths" `[001-041]`. But Dafydd is haunted by what he has seen and done. He tells Robert that something has gone out of him "like a little winking light," and that the poison in his blood is "shriveling me like an old orange" `[001-045]`. His soul, he says, "leaks out of a man the very first thing in the Indies" `[001-045]`.

Despite his haunted condition, Dafydd's tales of the Caribbean are magnetic. He speaks of the Indians of Darien — "lithe as snakes they are, and quick and quiet and brown as ferrets" — who fear "only two things — dogs and slavery" `[001-051]`. He tells of Pierre le Grand's capture of the Spanish galleon, a story that plants the seed of buccaneer ambition in young Henry.

The effect of Dafydd's visit is decisive. Robert Morgan tells his wife: "Then Dafydd came with his tales. Henry told me last night that he must go" `[002-006]`. When Dafydd departs, there is "a longing in the child's eyes that will never be satisfied at all" — a hunger for the distant and the unattainable `[002-008]`.
