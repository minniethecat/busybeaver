Sir Francis Drake appears in *Cup of Gold* not as a character but as a historical precedent — the Elizabethan privateer whose exploits established the template that [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] and his buccaneers would follow a century later. Drake's ship, the *[[golden-hind|Golden Hind]]*, is mentioned as having "sailed forbidden oceans" [008-??], setting the example of English maritime ambition that disregarded Spanish claims to exclusive dominion over the New World.

Drake's significance in the novel is structural rather than direct. He represents the Elizabethan moment when English privateering was unambiguously heroic — a patriot's work against the Spanish enemy. By Morgan's time, the moral calculus has shifted: privateering exists in a legal gray zone between sanctioned warfare and piracy, and the Crown that once celebrated Drake now arrests Morgan when his raids become diplomatically inconvenient.

The novel uses Drake to measure the distance between Elizabethan glory and Restoration cynicism. Drake was knighted on the deck of his ship by a grateful queen; Morgan must purchase his knighthood for two thousand pounds. The comparison is not made explicit in the text but is implicit in the novel's treatment of English maritime ambition as a tradition in decline.
