# Morgan's Knighthood

## Summary

Morgan's knighthood is the pivotal event in chapter 12 of *Cup of Gold* — the ceremony at the court of Charles II in which the buccaneer who sacked Panama is pardoned, knighted, and appointed Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica. The event marks Morgan's transition from outlaw to establishment, from predator to guardian of the very order he once defied. It is the moment civilization claims him — and the moment his self begins its irreversible split.

## Context

After the Sack of Panama (1671), Morgan is arrested and brought to England as a diplomatic gesture to appease Spain. The arrest is token: England has no intention of punishing the man who delivered the richest prize in the New World. In the novel's telling, Morgan's imprisonment is brief, comfortable, and followed by an audience with the king.

## Narrative Account

The court scene is rendered through the eyes of John Evelyn, the historical diarist, who serves as the chapter's narrative lens. Evelyn observes the buccaneer — "a man of middle stature, with a face that had been handsome before it was weathered by sun and salt" (012-001) — as he is brought before Charles II.

The king pardons Morgan for his "services to the Crown" — the phrase is carefully chosen, neither acknowledging nor denying that those services were acts of piracy against a nation with which England was technically at peace. Morgan kneels. He is tapped with the sword. He rises Sir Henry Morgan.

The ancestors' portraits are said to "smirk, as though they knew how you bought it" (012-008). The line is the novel's judgment on the knighthood: an honor purchased with violence, a respectability built on the sack of cities.

Morgan's appointment as Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica follows the knighthood. The buccaneer who once defied the Crown is now its representative in the Caribbean. He will spend the rest of his life enforcing laws against the very crimes he committed — hanging buccaneers in the morning, presiding over colonial society in the evening.

## Outcome

The knighthood seals Morgan's division. He is now two men: the buccaneer who sacked Panama and the governor who must suppress buccaneering. "Civilization will split up a character," he will later reflect, "and he who refuses to split goes under" (013-052). The knighthood is the moment the split becomes institutional, irreversible, and permanent.

## Historical Basis

The historical Henry Morgan was arrested in 1672, brought to England, and knighted by Charles II in 1674. He was appointed Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica and served in that role, with interruptions, until his death in 1688. The knighthood was controversial — Spain protested, and Morgan's reputation as a pirate made him an awkward fit for the English establishment. Steinbeck's fictional additions include John Evelyn as witness and the psychological interpretation of the knighthood as self-division.
