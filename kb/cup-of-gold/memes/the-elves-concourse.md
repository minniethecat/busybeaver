# The Elves' Concourse

**"The Elves' Concourse"** is a piano composition by [[elizabeth-morgan|Elizabeth Morgan]], performed for [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] during their courtship at the Governor's house in [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. Paired with the more somber "[[god-bears-the-weary-soul|God Bears the Weary Soul to Rest]]," it represents the lighter, more decorative side of [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s musical personality — and her strategic deployment of art as a weapon of attraction.

## Description

The piece is introduced as one of [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s "new pieces" [011-035], suggesting she is an active composer, not merely a performer of others' works. She describes it in simple, pictorial terms:

> "Now this is 'The Elves' Concourse.' Listen! You can hear their little feet pattering on the grass. Everybody says it is very sweet and pretty." [011-050]

The programmatic description — elves pattering on grass — indicates a piece in the descriptive or "character piece" tradition, popular in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The music mimics small, light footfalls, evoking a faerie gathering. Steinbeck's choice of "Concourse" — meaning a coming together, a throng — suggests multiplicity: not one elf but many, a crowded, playful assembly.

## Role in the Narrative

The piece is part of [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s calculated campaign to win [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]]. She has heard "how music affects these fierce men" [011-035] and selects her repertoire accordingly. "The Elves' Concourse" is the sweet opener, the piece designed to charm and disarm:

> Her fingers methodically worked at the strings. Henry thought her hands lovely as they flew about. He forgot about the music in watching her hands. [011-050]

The irony is deliberate: [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]] deploys her music to captivate Morgan, but Morgan is captivated instead by her hands — the physical instrument of the music rather than the music itself. The "Elves' Concourse" succeeds in its purpose, but not through the channel its composer intended. [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s art becomes a frame for her physical presence, and Morgan, the man of action, responds to the body rather than the composition.

## Symbolism

"The Elves' Concourse" embodies the tension between art as expression and art as instrument. [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]] composes and performs not for the music's own sake but as a tool — a means of capturing a man she wants. The piece's subject (elves, faerie gatherings, Welsh folk mythology) connects her to the world of [[merlin|Merlin]] and the Tylwyth Teg, but [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]] deploys it in a [[jamaica|Jamaica]]n drawing room, far from the Welsh crags. The elvish concourse becomes a kind of displaced magic: the old Welsh spirits, transported across the Atlantic, are pressed into service as romantic strategy. Where [[merlin|Merlin]]'s songs expressed genuine vision, [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s piece is a performance for an audience of one — beautiful, accomplished, and slightly mercenary.
