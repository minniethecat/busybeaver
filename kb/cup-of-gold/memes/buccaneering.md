# Buccaneering

## Definition

Buccaneering, as depicted in Steinbeck's *Cup of Gold*, is a distinct maritime culture of the 17th-century Caribbean — neither simple piracy nor legitimate naval warfare, but a liminal profession that existed in the space between Crown sanction and outlawry. The buccaneers were originally hunters of wild cattle on Hispaniola (the "boucan" process of smoking meat), who turned to raiding Spanish ships and settlements. By the time of Henry Morgan, buccaneering had evolved into a semi-organized confederation with its own codes, customs, and an unspoken understanding with England.

## In the Novel

Steinbeck treats buccaneering as a complete social world with its own internal logic. The Free Brotherhood of the Coast operates by consensus: "No prey, no pay" is the governing principle, and articles of agreement are drawn before each expedition, specifying the division of spoils — so many shares for the captain, so many for the loss of an eye or a leg. The buccaneers gather at Tortuga, a rocky island off Hispaniola, where "all the pirate ships of the Caribbean rendezvoused" and "the streets were crowded with seamen of every nation" (010-042).

The novel distinguishes buccaneering from mere piracy through its historical framing. In the retrospective chapters 8 and 9, Steinbeck catalogs the great buccaneer captains — Pierre le Grand, Bartolomeo Portugues, Roche Braziliano, Lewis Scot, L'Ollonais, Edward Mansveldt — each with a distinctive style and fate. Mansveldt dreams of a buccaneer republic, "a nation of free men living under their own laws" on St. Catherine's Isle (009-021). His vision is forbidden by both England and France, and he is garroted by the Spanish — but the dream persists.

For Henry Morgan, buccaneering is not an end but a means. He uses the Brotherhood's resources and reputation to pursue his private obsession with the Cup of Gold — Panama. Once he has achieved his conquest and received his knighthood, he turns against the buccaneers, hanging his former comrades as Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica. The novel thus traces the arc of buccaneering itself: from wild freedom to Crown co-option.

## Key Passages

> "The Free Brotherhood of the Coast — that loose confederation of the ships of all nations — was a curious organization. It had no laws, but customs which were more binding than laws." (009-002)

> "Mansveldt dreamed of a buccaneer nation. He pictured a republic on the sea, a new country to be created out of a colony wrested from the Spanish." (009-021)

> The ships gathered at Tortuga: "Every morning new sails appeared on the horizon, and every evening the taverns were more crowded with ruffians from every corner of the world." (010-042)

> "You are very like the captains of the Free Brotherhood — you speak of duty and laws, but you mean 'I want' or 'I do not want.'" (010-393)
