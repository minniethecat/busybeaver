# The Second Sight

## Definition

The Second Sight is the Welsh prophetic faculty possessed by Gwenliana and, in diminished form, by Merlin in *Cup of Gold*. It manifests through the Cumric tongue — the ancient Welsh language that the household no longer fully understands — and through associated phenomena: corpse-candles (lights that foretell death), the sight of the Tylwyth Teg (Welsh fairies), and visions of the three circles of Cumric cosmology. The Second Sight is not presented as fantasy but as a genuine, if ambiguous, mode of knowledge — one that the novel's Christian and rationalist characters dismiss at their peril.

## In the Novel

Gwenliana is the novel's primary bearer of the Second Sight. Her prophecy over the infant Henry (chapter 5) is the most concentrated supernatural event in the book: she chants in Cumric, her eyes "filmed over" with the vision, and she traces Henry's entire life — military glory, conquest, marriage, and "a great shining for the Divine" (005-032). The men of the household dismiss it as "woman's foolishness," but every element of the prophecy comes to pass.

Merlin's relationship to the Second Sight is more complex. He lives on Crag-top, surrounded by harps that "speak" — instruments that seem to possess knowledge independent of their player. His vision of Henry's nature — "you want the moon" — is not prophecy in Gwenliana's sense but an intuitive penetration of character that functions identically. Merlin's authority derives from his connection to the pre-Christian Welsh past: Arthur, Avalon, the Chair of Arthur.

The novel treats the Second Sight as epistemologically valid but socially dangerous. Gwenliana is marginalized within her own household; Merlin is an outcast living on a mountain. The truth they speak is real, but it cannot be spoken in English, and it cannot be spoken in the valley where ordinary life proceeds. The Second Sight is truth exiled to the margins.

## Symbolic Significance

The Second Sight represents Steinbeck's interest in modes of knowledge that exceed rationalism. It is the novel's acknowledgment that certain truths — about character, destiny, the shape of a life — cannot be reached by induction. The marginalization of the seers (Gwenliana dismissed, Merlin on the mountain) mirrors the novel's larger theme: the truths that matter most are the ones civilization cannot hear.
