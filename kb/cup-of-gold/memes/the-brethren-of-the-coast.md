# The Brethren of the Coast

The **[[free-brotherhood-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]]** is the historical term for the loose confederation of [[buccaneering|buccaneers]] who operated in the [[caribbean|Caribbean]] during the seventeenth century. In *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]*, the term is used interchangeably with the "[[the-free-brotherhood|Free Brotherhood]]," though "[[free-brotherhood-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]]" carries more of the historical weight — anchoring Steinbeck's fiction in the real pirate democracy that briefly flourished between the empires of Europe.

## Description

The Brethren of the Coast were not a formal organization but a distributed network of crews who shared a common economic model (prize-taking), a common enemy ([[spanish-empire|Spain]]), and a common code (the articles each ship adopted before sailing). Their society was remarkable for its democracy: captains were elected, plunder was shared by agreed formula, and major decisions were made by vote. For men who had fled the rigid hierarchies of Europe, the Brethren offered a kind of freedom available nowhere else in the Atlantic world.

## Role in the Narrative

The term "[[free-brotherhood-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]]" appears in the novel as part of the historical framing of [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]]'s world. It grounds the novel's buccaneer society in documented history — the real Brethren who sacked Spanish cities and briefly created an alternative social order in the Caribbean.

Morgan's relationship to the Brethren is complex. He is their most successful leader, but he is never truly one of them. His ambition — to capture [[panama-city|Panama]], to win [[elizabeth-morgan|Elizabeth]], to be knighted — exceeds the Brethren's horizons. They want plunder; he wants glory. [[the-free-brotherhood|The Brethren]] are the ladder he climbs and eventually transcends.

## Historical Basis

The historical [[free-brotherhood-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]] operated primarily from [[tortuga|Tortuga]] and, after 1655, from [[port-royal|Port Royal]], [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. They were composed of French *boucaniers* (hunters who smoked meat on *boucans*), Dutch privateers, and English adventurers — the same multi-ethnic mix Steinbeck depicts. [[the-free-brotherhood|The Brethren]]'s code included compensation for injuries (so many pieces of eight for a lost eye, a lost limb), shared prize money after deductions for the wounded and dead, and the election of officers.

## Symbolism

The Brethren of the Coast represent one of the novel's central tensions: the conflict between democratic freedom and individual ambition. [[the-free-brotherhood|The Brethren]]'s society is egalitarian, but Morgan's ambition is aristocratic; he wants to be a knight, not a brother. The historical Brethren were eventually suppressed by the very European powers that had once sponsored them — and Morgan, who accepts a knighthood and the governorship of [[jamaica|Jamaica]], embodies that transition from outlaw to officer, from brother to sir.
