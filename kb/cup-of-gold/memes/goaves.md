# Goaves

## Overview

Goaves (Les Goâves, on the western coast of Hispaniola) is one of the buccaneer ports of the Caribbean, a counterpart to Tortuga in the novel's geography. It is the town where Bras de Fer took his ships home (009-018), where Coeur de Gris's mother — one of the "free women" — lived, and where the buccaneer community gathered between voyages. Together with Tortuga, it represents the French buccaneer sphere of the Caribbean.

## Geography

Goaves is a port town on the western coast of Hispaniola (modern Haiti), west of Port-au-Prince. In the seventeenth century, it was one of the principal French settlements on the island, together with Petit-Goâve and Grand-Goâve. The surrounding region was controlled by French buccaneers and *engagés* (indentured laborers), with Tortuga as the buccaneer headquarters nearby.

## In the Narrative

Goaves first enters the novel in the voice of Dafydd, the old buccaneer returned from the Indies, who tells young Henry: "that's where I've been; to Goaves and to Tortuga" (001-033). The name recurs as part of the buccaneer geography Morgan inherits when he goes to sea.

For Coeur de Gris, Goaves is the place of his origin. His mother was "one of the free women of Goaves" (009-080), sent there as a vagrant after her Huguenot family's destruction in the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre. Bras de Fer found her there, and she gave birth to Coeur de Gris at sixteen. This genealogy makes Goaves the narrative's point of intersection between European religious conflict and Caribbean buccaneer society.

In the wider buccaneer world, Goaves functions as a port of return: Bras de Fer "took [ships] home to Goaves" (009-018), and the buccaneers' "numberless men" cheered Morgan's name "in Tortuga and Goaves" (009-040) as the Panama expedition assembled. The legend of La Santa Roja is whispered "in the streets of Goaves" (009-046), its power spreading through the same port-town networks as the buccaneers themselves.

Steinbeck pairs Goaves with Tortuga as a compound buccaneer geography: the "brothel keepers of Tortuga and Goaves" (008-100) represent the shore-side world that awaits the buccaneers' return from their raids, "like children throwing sand" — a world of consumption and dissipation that consumes the spoils of violence.

## Historical Context

Les Goâves was a real French settlement on the western coast of Hispaniola in the seventeenth century. It served as a base for French buccaneers and privateers operating against Spanish shipping in the Caribbean. The "free women" reference reflects the historical practice of sending women — particularly convicted criminals, vagrants, and orphans — to the French colonies as part of population-building programs, notably the *filles du roi* scheme. In the Caribbean context, these women often became partners of buccaneers and settlers, forming the demographic foundation of French colonial society in Saint-Domingue.
