# Coeur de Gris

## Role

Coeur de Gris (French for "Grey Heart") is Henry Morgan's young lieutenant aboard the Panama expedition — the son of the buccaneer Bras de Fer, and a figure of doomed innocence in the novel's moral economy. His murder by Morgan, in a fit of jealous rage over La Santa Roja, is the protagonist's irreversible moral wound: the deed that haunts his deathbed vision and for which Brother Death's accounting can offer no absolution.

## Biography

Coeur de Gris is introduced as part of the buccaneer gathering at Tortuga, where Morgan assembles his Panama fleet. He is young, French, and comes from a buccaneer lineage — his father, Bras de Fer, was a famous captain. He serves as Morgan's trusted lieutenant during the Panama campaign.

Like every buccaneer in Tortuga, Coeur de Gris has been infected by the legend of La Santa Roja. "Sir, I have dreamed of her and called to her in my sleep. Who has not? Who in all this gathering of the Free Brotherhood has not pictured her?" (009-091). His confession to Morgan is charged with dramatic irony: he confides his obsession to the very man who will kill him for it.

The murder occurs in conquered Panama. Morgan, having met La Santa Roja and been refused, encounters Coeur de Gris in a corridor of the captured city. Whether the young Frenchman has also encountered the Red Saint is left ambiguous, but Morgan's jealousy — already inflamed by her rejection — seizes on the possibility. He shoots Coeur de Gris (010-292). The act is impulsive, irreversible, and psychologically catastrophic.

Coeur de Gris's death marks the novel's moral turning point. Before this moment, Morgan's violence has been professional — buccaneering, conquest, the taking of cities. This murder is different: it is personal, intimate, and motiveless in any strategic sense. It is the moment Morgan's hunger for the moon consumes not an enemy but a friend. From this point forward, Morgan is a divided man, accumulating a ledger of deeds he will not be able to face.

In the deathbed vision, Coeur de Gris is among the faceless figures Brother Death presents. The murder cannot be undone, cannot be justified, and cannot be integrated into the self Morgan has constructed. It is the purest instance of the novel's theme: the cost of ambition is not failure but success — and the record of what was done to achieve it.

## Historical Basis

Coeur de Gris is a fictional character. There is no record of a French buccaneer by this name in the historical accounts of Morgan's expeditions. Steinbeck invents him as the novel's sacrificial figure — the innocent whose death marks the point at which ambition becomes irredeemable.
