# John Evelyn

## Role

John Evelyn appears in the novel as a courtier at the court of King Charles II, serving as a narrative lens through which the reader witnesses Henry Morgan's post-conquest audience with the King. A historical figure and celebrated diarist, Evelyn provides the civilized, ironic counterpoint to Morgan's rough buccaneer presence.

## Biography

John Evelyn sits with King Charles II in a tiny library, warmed by a crackling fire, when the King announces he has knighted Henry Morgan that afternoon for "two thousand pounds" `[012-002]`. Evelyn murmurs that "certain tradesmen will, perhaps, bless his knighthood" `[012-003]`, his dry tone underscoring the transaction's sordid nature. When the King reveals Morgan took "about a million out of Panama" `[012-004]`, Evelyn suggests creating a title, noting "it seems wasteful to let a million get away without even trying" `[012-007]`.

The King describes Morgan before his arrival as "lumpish," a man who "moves as though he pushed his own invisible cage ahead of him" `[012-006]`. Evelyn observes Morgan's audience with quiet detachment. When Morgan recounts the story of La Santa Roja — Ysobel, who poisoned herself rather than submit to him — Evelyn watches the King's response with knowing eyes `[012-035]`. The King smiles ironically; Evelyn drums the table with his fingers `[012-035]`.

Evelyn also serves as the King's conversational foil, asking the pointed questions that draw out Morgan's character. He asks "how it is that such a great soldier can be so defeated by a woman" `[012-044]`, and when the King observes that Morgan "cannot bear about a woman," Evelyn prompts him to elaborate `[012-055]`. The King notes that Evelyn has "a way of being secretly insulting" `[012-057]`, capturing the diarist's sly perceptiveness.

## Historical Basis

The historical John Evelyn (1620–1706) was a renowned English diarist whose journals provide one of the most important records of seventeenth-century English life. He was a founding fellow of the Royal Society and a close associate of King Charles II's court. Steinbeck uses Evelyn as a naturalistic framing device — the diarist observing history in real time — lending the fictionalized account of Morgan's knighthood an air of documentary authenticity.
