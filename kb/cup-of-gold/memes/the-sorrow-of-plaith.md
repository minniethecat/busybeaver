# The Sorrow of Plaith

**"The Sorrow of Plaith"** is a bardic song composed by [[merlin|Merlin]], the old Welsh poet who lived on Crag-top above the Morgan farm. Together with "[[the-spear-song|The Spear Song]]," it represents the summit of [[merlin|Merlin]]'s creative achievement — a body of work that won him acclaim at the Eisteddfod before he shut his song away and grew old in silence.

## Description

The song's title — *The Sorrow of Plaith* — is Welsh in its cadence and subject. "Plaith" appears to be a proper name, possibly a place or a person from Welsh legend, though Steinbeck provides no further detail. What matters is not the song's content but its function in the narrative: it is cited as proof of what [[merlin|Merlin]] once was before he became what he is.

## Role in the Narrative

The song appears in a single, densely packed paragraph that sketches [[merlin|Merlin]]'s entire artistic biography:

> Once Merlin had been a fine poet, the old people said, and might have been a greater. They would softly sing "The Sorrow of Plaith" or "The Spear Song," to prove it. Several times he had taken the chief prize of the Eisteddfod, and would have been chosen First Bard if an aspirant of the House of Rhys had not entered against him. Then, without known cause, and Merlin a young man, too, he had shut up his song in the stone house on Crag-top and kept it a strict prisoner there while he grew old and old — and those who had sung his songs forgot them, or died. [003-002]

This passage is the novel's most concentrated meditation on artistic silence. [[merlin|Merlin]]'s withdrawal is a mystery — "without known cause" — and the songs' survival depends entirely on living memory. As the old people who sang them die, the songs die too. "The Sorrow of Plaith" thus becomes a symbol of fragile transmission: art that exists only as long as someone remembers to sing it.

## Symbolism

The song's title — *The Sorrow of Plaith* — is itself a statement. A "sorrow" in the bardic tradition is a lament, an elegy for something lost. [[merlin|Merlin]], who once created laments for Welsh heroes or places, has himself become the subject of lament. The novel frames his silence as a kind of living death — he "shut up his song" and "kept it a strict prisoner" [003-002] — and the song's sorrow, whatever its original subject, now encompasses its own creator. The old people who sing "The Sorrow of Plaith" are not just remembering a song; they are mourning a poet who is still alive but no longer creating.

This connects directly to [[merlin|Merlin]]'s plea to the young [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]]: "I don't want to be forgotten" [003-043]. The songs he wrote in youth are all that remain of him, and even they are fading as the oral tradition thins. "The Sorrow of Plaith" is the artifact of a voice that chose silence — and in that choice, Steinbeck suggests, lies the truest sorrow of all.
