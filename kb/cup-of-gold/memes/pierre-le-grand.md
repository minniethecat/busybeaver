# Pierre le Grand

## Role

Pierre le Grand is a legendary French buccaneer whose daring capture of a Spanish treasure galleon from a canoe made him a foundational figure in the mythology of the Caribbean Free Brotherhood. Mentioned across multiple chapters of the novel, he serves as the archetype of buccaneer audacity against whom later figures like Henry Morgan measure themselves.

## Biography

Pierre le Grand slipped out of the woods of Hispaniola with a little band of hunters and, from a canoe, captured the Vice-Admiral of the Spanish plate fleet — an exploit that transformed the Free Brotherhood from scattered outlaws into "a terrible thing" `[009-002]`. With only twenty-four ragged men armed with pistols and the long knives used for cutting jungle trails, Pierre's band did "horrid things with those same long knives" to take the tall galleon `[001-040]`.

The story of Pierre le Grand is first introduced to young Henry Morgan through Dafydd, an old buccaneer returned from the Caribbean. Dafydd speaks of Pierre as a figure of wealth and terror intertwined: "I'm rich along with him they call Pierre le Grand" — though his dry, cold voice suggests that riches cannot balance the horror of what was done `[001-030]`. Dafydd had longed to tell the tale of "the green jungle and the brown Indians that live in it, and about him they call Pierre le Grand," but finds that something has "gone out of me like a little winking light" — the boastful energy that once animated his dreams has given way to haunted memory `[001-039]`.

Years later, Henry Morgan invokes Pierre le Grand's example when discussing buccaneer strategy: "There was one called Pierre le Grand who was no fool" `[008-111]`. But his interlocutor counters that Pierre "took one rich ship and then ran home to France," branding him "a fearful gambler, not a wise man" who "may yet come back to the Coast and lose it all and his head too" `[008-112]`. This judgment — that a single spectacular success followed by retreat is not wisdom — echoes Morgan's own ambition to go further than his predecessors.

## Historical Basis

The historical Pierre le Grand (also known as Pierre Le Grand) was a 17th-century French buccaneer credited with one of the earliest major buccaneer victories — the capture of a Spanish galleon near Hispaniola, reportedly from a small boat or canoe. Details of his life are sparse, and some historians consider him semi-legendary. Steinbeck uses him as a mythic founding figure whose single famous exploit establishes the template that Morgan and others would later follow on a far grander scale.
