# Duty of Appearances

## Definition

"The duty of appearances" is Sir Henry Morgan's phrase for the performance of social identity that civilization demands. It names the gap between the performed self (the knight, the governor, the husband) and the felt self (the buccaneer, the hungry boy from Wales). In the novel's moral economy, the duty of appearances is civilization's primary instrument for "splitting up a character" (013-052). It is not hypocrisy — Morgan is not pretending to be someone he is not for gain — but something more tragic: the gradual replacement of the real self by the performed one.

## In the Novel

The phrase crystallizes in chapter 13, during Morgan's reflection on his marriage to Elizabeth. He married her, he says, for "the duty of appearances" — not for love, not for ambition, but because a man in his position was expected to marry an appropriate woman. "That is what I married. That is what I govern. A ghost" (013-058). The language is striking: the duty of appearances produces ghosts, not lives.

The concept operates at every level of Morgan's late life. As Lieutenant-Governor, he presides over trials and hangs buccaneers — performing justice — while knowing he committed the same crimes. As a husband, he maintains a household — performing domesticity — while his interior life is elsewhere, in the past, with the dead. As a knight, he wears the honor — performing virtue — while the ancestors' portraits "smirk because they know how you bought it" (012-008).

The tragedy is that the performance is not optional. Morgan cannot refuse the duty of appearances without "going under" — losing the position, the power, the identity. Civilization offers no path back to wholeness. The choice is between a divided public life and no life at all.

## Symbolic Significance

The duty of appearances is Steinbeck's critique of social respectability as a form of self-annihilation. It prefigures his later explorations of performance and authenticity — the tension between public and private selves that runs through *East of Eden* and *The Winter of Our Discontent*. The phrase's power lies in its recognition that performance is not optional for those who have risen: the duty is real, and its cost is real.
