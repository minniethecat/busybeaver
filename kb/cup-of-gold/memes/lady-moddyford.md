# Lady Moddyford

## Role

Lady Moddyford is the wife of the Governor of Jamaica and the principal architect of Elizabeth and Henry Morgan's marriage. A shrewd, worldly woman skilled in the arts of social arrangement, she embodies the domestic politics that transform Henry from buccaneer into gentleman.

## Biography

Lady Moddyford takes charge of Elizabeth after the death of her father, hosting her in the Governor's household in Jamaica. When Henry Morgan arrives, Lady Moddyford immediately recognizes an opportunity. She takes Elizabeth aside for earnest counsel: "Dear Elizabeth, don't you know by now that almost any woman can marry almost any man as long as some other woman doesn't interfere? And I shall arrange matters so that no one will get in your way. You may trust me for that" `[011-034]`.

She advises Elizabeth on the art of managing Morgan's perception: "Make him feel that you are a poor, helpless little creature completely hemmed in with tigers. But you must arrange it in your own way. You have a good start" `[011-038]`. When Elizabeth considers playing fine music for Henry, Lady Moddyford judiciously intervenes: "No, I wouldn't do that if I were you. He might not like fine music. There are better ways" `[011-036]`. Her understanding of male psychology is practical and unsentimental.

The morning after her intervention, Lady Moddyford brings Henry and Elizabeth together and — "as soon as she gracefully could" — leaves them alone `[011-039]`. Her work done, she withdraws, having set in motion the domestic machinery that will transform the buccaneer's life. When Elizabeth, flustered by Henry's sudden proposal, cries that she must talk to Lady Moddyford because "she will know what to say" `[011-086]`, the invocation of her name confirms her role as the orchestrator of this pivotal union.
