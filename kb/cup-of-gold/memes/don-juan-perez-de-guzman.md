# Don Juan Perez de Guzman

## Role

Don Juan Perez de Guzman is the Spanish Governor of Panama and the principal antagonist during Henry Morgan's sack of Panama City. A quiet nobleman devoted to gentlemanly formalities, he serves as the human face of the "Cup of Gold" — the glittering prize Morgan has pursued across the Spanish Main.

## Biography

Don Juan Perez de Guzman was Governor of Panama, "a quiet nobleman whose life was devoted to being a complete gentleman and nothing else" `[010-017]`. He occupied his days drilling his small army, changing uniforms, and arranging the marriages of his relatives. Having been a soldier all his life, he was "not a good campaigner, but an exceedingly efficient martinet" `[010-017]`. Under his governance, Panama society lived happily, alternating between country retreats in the hot season and the balls and receptions of the town during the rains `[010-018]`.

When news arrived that Henry Morgan was approaching to conquer the city, the governor's world unraveled. The people of Panama rushed to churches to confess and kiss relics as horrific stories of buccaneer cruelty spread through the city `[010-019]`. Don Juan responded in character: he ordered out his two thousand troops, reviewed them with satisfaction, and then returned to kneel in the Cathedral, reasoning that "anything that cost as much as the masses he had paid for must have some effect" `[010-037]`.

Earlier, Morgan had sent a messenger to Don Juan with a pistol as a warning, declaring he would bring "a great number" of such weapons `[009-055]`. Don Juan replied with elegant mockery, returning the pistol along with a large square emerald and a message begging Morgan not to trouble himself to come to Panama, "for then his duty would overwhelm his admiration and force him to hang you to a tree" `[009-058]`. Morgan received the reply with grim amusement, remarking he would "like to meet with Don Juan if only at swords' points" `[009-059]`.

On the day of battle, Don Juan stood with his neat army in perfect formation. He watched the Spanish cavalry wheel through intricate parade-ground evolutions — triangles, hollow squares — and "groaned with admiration" at the spectacle `[010-153]`. But when the buccaneers' rough tactics shattered his elegant formations, leaving a plain of "writhing, mangled bodies," the governor looked on dazedly and "burst into sobs like a child who has seen his bright toy broken in the road" `[010-156]`. He did not know what to do; his brain was "heavy with a red sorrow," and he turned to go hear a mass in the Cathedral `[010-156]`.

When Morgan arrived at the Governor's Palace to establish his headquarters, Don Juan Perez de Guzman stood in the doorway "with a naked rapier in his hand" `[010-165]`. He moved forward and cried, "Defend yourself!" `[010-168]` — a final gesture of gentlemanly honor in the face of utter defeat.

## Historical Basis

The historical Don Juan Perez de Guzman was indeed Governor of Panama at the time of Morgan's attack in 1671. Steinbeck's portrayal captures the tension between Spanish aristocratic formality and the brutal pragmatism of the buccaneers, though the novel amplifies the governor's gentlemanly fastidiousness for dramatic effect. The historical Don Juan did lose Panama to Morgan's forces, and the city was burned during the sack — a conflagration that both sides blamed on the other.
