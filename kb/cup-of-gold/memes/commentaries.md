# Commentaries

**Caesar's Commentaries** (*Commentarii de Bello Gallico*) is the classical Roman military text by [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]] that appears in *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]* as part of [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s library on [[barbados|Barbados]]. The book symbolizes a particular kind of learning — vast, disorganized, and ultimately inert — that Steinbeck contrasts with [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]]'s instinctive, will-driven intelligence.

## Description

Caesar's *Commentaries* are Julius Caesar's firsthand accounts of the Gallic Wars, written in a famously clear and direct Latin prose. For centuries they served as a standard text for young men studying military strategy and classical rhetoric. In [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s library, they represent the pinnacle of the Western martial tradition — the record of a conqueror who was also a writer.

## Role in the Narrative

The *Commentaries* appear at a crucial moment in the novel's intellectual argument. Steinbeck describes [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s library:

> His library was the finest in the Indies, and, as far as information went, he was the most learned man anywhere about. But his learning formed no design of the whole. He had learned without absorbing, remembered without assimilating. His mind was a sad mass of unrelated facts and theories. In his brain, as on his shelves, Caesar's *Commentaries* stood shoulder to shoulder with Democritus and a treatise on spontaneous generation. [008-147]

The image of Caesar's *Commentaries* "shoulder to shoulder with Democritus" is deliberately absurd — a classical military text shelved beside Greek atomism and a pseudo-scientific treatise. This juxtaposition is Steinbeck's critique of the colonial intellectual: Flower possesses all the ingredients of wisdom but none of the synthesis.

[[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] reads these books during his [[indenture-system|indenture]] and absorbs from them what he needs. Unlike Flower, who "remembered without assimilating" [008-[[TOC|14]]7], Henry reads with purpose: "I want to read the books of war and sailing" [008-173]. He later contemplates "the campaigns of Alexander and Xenophon and Caesar in their wars" [008-176] as models for his own conquest of [[panama-city|Panama]].

## Symbolism

The *Commentaries* represent the paradox of colonial knowledge. In [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s hands, they are sterile — a gentleman's ornament, no more useful than a treatise on spontaneous generation. But in [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]]'s reading, they become a manual for conquest. The book thus embodies the novel's central argument about knowledge: it is not the possession of facts but the will to use them that separates the creator from the collector. Flower "had cried to be a creator" but became only a curator [008-[[TOC|14]]7]; [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]], reading the same books, would become the thing Caesar wrote about.
