# Don Guierrmo

## Role

Don Guierrmo is a citizen of Panama whose terrified reports help spread the panic that grips the city before Henry Morgan's assault. He serves as a narrative device for conveying the psychological impact of buccaneer terror on the Spanish colonial population.

## Biography

As rumors of Morgan's approach sweep through Panama, Don Guierrmo becomes a source of the city's mounting dread. Greeting Don Pedro with "The Virgin's blessing," he receives a concerned inquiry about the truth of the spreading stories `[010-022]`. Don Guierrmo's reply is grim: "Ah! horrible, Don Guierrmo; horrible. They are said to be not men at all" `[010-024]`.

The stories Don Guierrmo carries grow more fantastic with each telling. He reports that the buccaneers are "animal things with heads like crocodiles and lions' claws" — tales so extreme that "grave men discussed such possibilities in the streets" `[010-020]`. When challenged on his sources, Don Pedro asks whether he had the report directly: "You say you had it of Don Guierrmo? Surely he would not tell a questionable thing" `[010-028]`. The reply confirms that Don Guierrmo "was certain of it" — asserting that the pirates breathed "sulphurous flames" `[010-029]`.

Don Guierrmo's function in the novel is emblematic: he represents the rumor-mill that transforms distant buccaneers into supernatural monsters, a psychological weapon as effective as any sword. The terror he spreads through Panama's drawing rooms and churches does half of Morgan's work before a single buccaneer sets foot on the isthmus.
