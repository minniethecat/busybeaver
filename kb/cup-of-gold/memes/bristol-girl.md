# Bristol Girl

The **Bristol Girl** is the slave ship that carries the kidnapped [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] from [[wales|Wales]] to [[barbados|Barbados]], transforming him from a free farmer's son into an [[indenture-system|indentured servant]]. Though briefly mentioned in the novel, the *Bristol Girl* represents the machinery of empire — the ships that moved human cargo across the Atlantic to feed the colonial plantation economy.

## Description

The *Bristol Girl* is never described in physical detail in the novel. Steinbeck names her only in passing, allowing the horror of her function to stand in for any physical description. She is one of the many ships that carried "felons gathered out of the prisons, and vagrants from the streets of [[london|London]]" [008-006] to work the Caribbean plantations.

## Role in the Narrative

The *Bristol Girl* is the vessel of Morgan's violent transformation. The free Welsh boy who boards her emerges in [[barbados|Barbados]] as property — his body sold to pay for his passage, his will subjected to the plantation's brutal discipline. The ship is the instrument of the trauma that defines Morgan's early life and fuels his later hunger for [[wanting-the-moon|power and revenge]].

Morgan's own experience on the *Bristol Girl* echoes the wider colonial traffic in human beings. The novel explicitly links the kidnapping of white laborers to the African slave trade:

> "Then felons were gathered out of the prisons, and vagrants from the streets of London; beggars who stood all day before the church doors; those suspected of witchcraft or treason or leprosy or papism; and all these were sent to work the plantations under orders of indenture." [008-006]

The *Bristol Girl* is part of this system — a cog in the empire's engine of human exploitation.

## Symbolism

The *Bristol Girl* and her sister ships represent the dark underside of the [[the-sea-motif|sea]] in the novel. Where Morgan's own ship *[[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]* symbolizes freedom and self-mastery, the *Bristol Girl* symbolizes captivity and subjection. Every buccaneer captain who sails freely was once — or could have been — a captive on a ship like the *Bristol Girl*.

The ship's name carries bitter irony: "Bristol" is an English port, a center of the slave trade, and "Girl" feminizes the vessel in the nautical tradition — but this "girl" is a prison, not a lover.
