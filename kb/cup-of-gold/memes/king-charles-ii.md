# King Charles II

## Role

King Charles II is the English monarch who receives Henry Morgan after the sack of Panama, granting him a knighthood and the Lieutenant-Governorship of Jamaica. His scenes in the novel illuminate the ironic gap between Morgan's buccaneer reality and the civilized forms of Restoration court politics.

## Biography

King Charles II knights Henry Morgan in a transactional arrangement — "a knighthood for two thousand pounds" `[012-002]` — though he acknowledges privately to John Evelyn that Morgan "took about a million out of Panama" `[012-004]`. The King is amused by his own bargain: a pardon and a title in exchange for a fraction of the plunder.

Before Morgan arrives for his evening audience, the King describes him to Evelyn with the detachment of a naturalist: "He is — lumpish, I think is the word. You get the impression that a great mass is planted before you; and he moves as though he pushed his own invisible cage ahead of him" `[012-006]`. The description captures the existential weariness that defines Morgan in the novel's latter half.

During the audience, the King's manner shifts between ironic amusement and genuine curiosity. He listens to Morgan's account of La Santa Roja — the woman who poisoned herself rather than yield to him — and his reaction reveals the courtly pleasure in others' humiliations. As Morgan tells his story, "the King was smiling ironically" while "John Evelyn drummed the table with his fingers" `[012-035]`.

The King is also a sharp observer of character. He remarks to Evelyn that Morgan "cannot bear to hear about a woman" and notes that all men lie "to or about a woman" `[012-055]`. He chides Evelyn for his habit of "being secretly insulting," observing that the diarist is "sometimes not even conscious of your insults" — advice the King gives as a veteran of court politics `[012-057]`.

Earlier, arrangements had been made through court channels to bring Morgan's cousin to England, with the understanding that "a knighthood might be arranged" and Morgan's wife would become "Lady Morgan" `[011-030]`. These machinations reveal the King's court as a marketplace where titles, marriages, and fortunes are traded with equal pragmatism.

## Historical Basis

Charles II (1630–1685) was restored to the English throne in 1660 after the Cromwellian interregnum. The historical Charles II did indeed knight Henry Morgan and appoint him Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, transforming the buccaneer into a representative of Crown authority. Steinbeck's portrayal emphasizes the ironic contradiction at the heart of this transaction — the pirate becomes the law — while depicting Charles as a shrewd, worldly monarch who understood that a knighthood cost less than a military campaign.
