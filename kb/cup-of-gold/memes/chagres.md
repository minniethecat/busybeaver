# Chagres

## Overview

Chagres is the Atlantic gateway to Panama — a fortress settlement guarding the mouth of the River Chagres, the water route that leads to the isthmus crossing and ultimately to Panama City on the Pacific. In Morgan's campaign, it is the first military objective: the buccaneers must take Chagres before they can advance inland. The taking of Chagres and the subsequent river passage represent the novel's most sustained sequence of military action.

## Geography

The River Chagres flows from the central Panamanian highlands to the Caribbean Sea, near the modern-day Panama Canal's Atlantic entrance. In the seventeenth century, the fortress of San Lorenzo el Real de Chagres guarded its mouth. From Chagres, the route to Panama City involved river travel inland as far as navigable, followed by an overland crossing of the isthmus. This was the route used by Spanish treasure shipments — gold from Peru would be transshipped across the isthmus and loaded onto the plate fleet at the Caribbean terminus.

## In the Narrative

Chagres appears in Chapter 10 as the first phase of Morgan's assault on Panama. When the buccaneer fleet reaches the Panamanian coast, "their stated purpose was the conquest and pillage of the Cup of Gold" (010-031), but Chagres stands between them and their objective. The fortress must be taken.

The river passage from Chagres is a trial of endurance. Morgan's longboats "floating on the River Chagres, each one taxed to the limit with its load of men and arms" (010-087), push inland against jungle heat and Spanish resistance. The river itself becomes an antagonist: "Chagres twisted on ahead in loops and unexpected curves, a muddy, alligator-haunted stream overhung with poisonous vines" (010-090). The journey is a test of the buccaneers' physical limits — men collapse from fever, and the jungle closes in.

Morgan's return to Chagres after the sack of Panama is the operation's denouement. The treasure is carried "across the isthmus on the backs of mules" to Chagres, where the division of spoils is to take place. Morgan's threat — "In Chagres, fool! Do you think I would divide the treasure here?" (010-341) — anchors the location as the bookkeeping endpoint of conquest. It is at Chagres that Morgan famously cheats his men, sailing away with the lion's share of the Panama loot under cover of darkness.

## Historical Context

The fortress of San Lorenzo el Real de Chagres was a real Spanish fortification, built to guard the mouth of the River Chagres and the approach to the isthmus crossing. Henry Morgan's historical 1671 Panama expedition did indeed take San Lorenzo as its first objective, scaling its walls in a bloody assault. The river journey and overland crossing followed the Spanish *Camino Real* (Royal Road). The charges that Morgan cheated his men at Chagres during the division of spoils are historically attested in contemporary accounts, including Exquemelin's *The Buccaneers of America*. The ruins of San Lorenzo still stand today, a UNESCO World Heritage site.
