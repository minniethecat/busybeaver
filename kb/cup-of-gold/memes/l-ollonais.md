# L'Ollonais

## Role

L'Ollonais is the most feared buccaneer in the western ocean, a figure of legendary cruelty whose atrocities against the Spanish set the standard of terror that Henry Morgan both inherits and must contend with. His name is invoked as a benchmark — Morgan's men measure their own captain against the infamous Frenchman.

## Biography

L'Ollonais came from the Sables d'Ollone in France and "very rapidly became the most feared man in the western ocean" `[009-011]`. He began his career with a genuine hatred of Spain but ended with "a strong love of cruelty" `[009-011]`. His atrocities were methodical: he pulled out tongues, carved prisoners to pieces with his sword, and on one occasion commanded that eighty-seven prisoners be laid in a row and then "walked down the line, carrying a whetstone in one hand and a long sword in the other," beheading all eighty-seven with his own hand `[009-012]`. The Spanish would "far rather have met the devil in any form than L'Ollonais," and the whisper of his name emptied villages `[009-011]`.

Not content with murdering Spanish men, L'Ollonais turned his violence on the peaceful peoples of Yucatan, who "lived in ruined stone cities, and where the virgins went crowned with flowers." When he departed, "the cities were heaps of stones and ashes, and there were no crowns any more" `[009-013]`. His cruelty extended even to the Indians of Darien — fierce, fearless allies of the pirates who shared their hatred of Spain. L'Ollonais "robbed them and murdered the tribesmen" `[009-014]`. These Indians waited many years for vengeance and at last caught him when his ship wrecked on their shores, burning him alive `[009-014]`.

In the novel's moral economy, L'Ollonais serves as a dark mirror to Henry Morgan. By the time Morgan is thirty, "ten years of fighting and plundering and burning," he has become the "most luck-followed freebooter the world had known" `[009-042]`. Men place Morgan "beside Drake and L'Ollonais" in their fears `[009-042]` — a comparison that underscores both Morgan's success and the monstrous company he keeps.

## Historical Basis

The historical François L'Olonnais (born Jean-David Nau, c. 1630–1668) was one of the most brutal buccaneers of the 17th century. His atrocities against Spanish settlements in the Caribbean — including the sack of Maracaibo and Gibraltar (Venezuela) — were well-documented by contemporary chroniclers. His death at the hands of the Darien Indians, who reportedly dismembered and burned him alive, became a staple of buccaneer lore. Steinbeck's account closely follows the historical record, using L'Ollonais to establish the spectrum of buccaneer violence within which Morgan's own career must be judged.
