Elizabeth Morgan enters the novel late, after Henry Morgan's return from Panama and his knighthood. She is the daughter of Sir Edward Morgan and the niece (by marriage) of Lady Moddyford, who arranges the match. Her significance in the novel is less as an active character than as a symbolic presence — she represents the domestic peace and cultivated gentility that Henry Morgan's life of violence has made impossible for him.

Elizabeth is a pianist, and her composition "The Elves' Concourse" [011-??] is one of the novel's few evocations of art as an alternative to ambition. Where Henry conquers cities, Elizabeth makes music; where Henry accumulates gold, Elizabeth creates beauty. The contrast is deliberate — Steinbeck uses Elizabeth's musicianship to measure the distance between Henry's world and the world of authentic creation.

Their marriage is presented as a transaction brokered by others — Lady Moddyford sees Elizabeth as a means of securing Henry's place in colonial society. Elizabeth's own feelings are barely rendered; she exists in the novel largely as an object of Henry's confused affection. His confession that he "planned to tear a continent out of the set order and crown it with a capital of gold for you" [011-??] reveals that even his love takes the form of conquest.
