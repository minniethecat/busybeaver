# God is Love

## Definition

"God is Love" is Old Robert Morgan's theology — the gentle, domestic, and deliberately anti-theological understanding of the divine that frames Henry Morgan's childhood in Wales. It is articulated most memorably through Robert's metaphor of "purple porridge": God, like a good broth, is not something to be analyzed but something to be tasted and found nourishing. "Some people say God is a storm, a great wind," Robert tells young Henry. "I say God is Love. I say God is the purple porridge that warms your belly on a cold morning. That's all the theology I need" (002-027).

## In the Novel

Old Robert's theology provides the novel's emotional baseline. It is presented without irony — as a genuinely wise and adequate philosophy for the life Robert has chosen: a Welsh farmer, husband, and father who once "wanted the moon" and then accepted the firefly of domestic contentment. Robert is not a failure in the novel's terms; he is a man who found an honest accommodation with the limits of desire.

The theology recurs at key moments as a point of contrast. When Henry is sold into indenture, he remembers his father's words and finds them inadequate to the brutality of the Caribbean. When Gwenliana prophesies over the infant Henry, her Cumric cosmology (Abred/Gwynfyd/Annwn) implicitly challenges Robert's simpler framework. And in the deathbed vision, when Brother Death presents the faceless deeds, Robert's "God is Love" is the absent alternative — the theology of a man who never accumulated a ledger of deeds he could not own.

The novel does not refute Robert's theology, but it shows its limits. It works for a life lived within a single valley, among people who know each other. It cannot encompass the Caribbean, the sack of cities, the murder of friends, the split self. The novel's cosmic vision — Brother Death, the Tone, the void — is larger and darker. But Robert's God is never mocked. He is simply not the God of Henry Morgan's world.

## Symbolic Significance

The "God is Love" / "purple porridge" theology is Steinbeck's tribute to a certain kind of religious sensibility: unlettered, experiential, nourishing. It is the theology of the hearth, not the pulpit. Its presence in the novel ensures that the critique of Christianity (via the corrupt Catholic Church in Panama, the hypocritical Puritan slave-trader) is not total: there remains, in Old Robert, a picture of genuine faith that the novel honors even as it leaves it behind.
