# Morgan's Arrest

## Summary

Following the sack and burning of Panama, international pressure forces the English crown to act. Morgan and Governor Moddyford are arrested and summoned to England to answer for violating the 1670 Treaty of Madrid, which had ended hostilities between England and Spain. In a supreme irony, Morgan's arrest leads not to punishment but to elevation: King Charles II, recognizing Morgan's value as a colonial asset, knights him and returns him to Jamaica as Lieutenant-Governor. The arrest is thus the novel's great reversal — the moment when the buccaneer is transformed into the establishment.

## Context

The 1670 Treaty of Madrid (also known as the Godolphin Treaty) was signed between England and Spain, formally ending hostilities in the Caribbean. Crucially, Morgan's Panama expedition occurred after the treaty was signed but before news of it reached the Caribbean — a temporal gap that created legal ambiguity. The Spanish crown, outraged by the destruction of Panama City, demanded that England punish Morgan as a pirate rather than recognize him as a privateer operating under a valid commission. The diplomatic crisis threatened the fragile peace between the two powers.

## Narrative Account

The arrest is presented in the novel through the filter of Lady Moddyford's report: "Sir Charles and your cousin have been ordered to England" (011-013). The plural — Morgan and Moddyford together — emphasizes that the arrest targets not just the buccaneer commander but the entire Jamaican colonial establishment that authorized and profited from his expedition.

The journey to England is a passage from the Caribbean periphery to the metropolitan center — from a world where Morgan's violence was instrumental to imperial expansion to one where it has become a diplomatic liability. The novel's laconic treatment of the arrest itself — it is referenced more than narrated — mirrors Morgan's own passivity in the face of forces larger than himself: he cannot fight the Crown as he fought the Spanish.

What follows is the novel's most dramatic irony. Morgan, summoned to face punishment, instead receives a knighthood. The King, recognizing that a man who could take Panama could also defend Jamaica, co-opts the buccaneer into the imperial system. The transformation is captured in Elizabeth's voice: "Sir Charles thinks a knighthood might be arranged" (011-015). The man who was to stand trial becomes Sir Henry Morgan.

## Outcome

The arrest and its paradoxical resolution complete Morgan's transformation. He arrives in England a buccaneer accused of piracy; he leaves a knight and Lieutenant-Governor. The novel presents this transformation not as redemption but as co-optation — the buccaneer is absorbed into the system he once defied, and the violence that was criminal when directed against Spain becomes legitimate when directed in service of the Crown. Morgan's deathbed vision, in which the deeds of his buccaneer years return to haunt him, suggests that the knighthood did not cancel the moral ledger — it only changed the handwriting.

## Historical Basis

The historical arrest of Henry Morgan is well documented. After the 1670 Treaty of Madrid, Spanish pressure forced Charles II to recall both Morgan and Sir Thomas Modyford (Moddyford) to England in 1672. Modyford was briefly imprisoned in the Tower of London. Morgan, however, was never tried. He spent two years in London, where he was treated as a celebrity rather than a criminal, and was knighted in 1674 before returning to Jamaica as Lieutenant-Governor — a remarkable rehabilitation that reflected the Crown's pragmatic recognition of Morgan's value as a colonial administrator and defender of Jamaica against future Spanish or French aggression.
