# Gwenliana

## Role

Gwenliana is Henry Morgan's aged grandmother and the matriarchal seeress of the Morgan household. She practices folk divination — reading sheep bones, interpreting signs, and holding "necromantic court" in her garden for credulous farm lads. Her prophecies bookend the novel: in Henry's youth she foretells his greatness, and on her deathbed she delivers a cryptic final prophecy that the world will end. Gwenliana embodies the ancient Welsh folk tradition of the supernatural, standing in contrast to her practical daughter-in-law [[mother-morgan|Mother Morgan]] and framing the mystical dimension of Henry's destiny.

## Biography

In her old age, Gwenliana spent her days before the fire, "her mind lost in the swimming future" [001-036]. She was a figure of folk magic in the Welsh countryside: "Any day when she sat in the garden holding her necromantic court, you might see some farm lad blushing and hugging his hat across his chest while he listened to Gwenliana's magic" [001-008].

The family indulged her claims to second sight with affectionate tolerance. Henry's father [[old-robert-morgan|Robert]] later reflected that they "humored her because she took such joy of it" [009-204]. Even so, she was respected as the family matriarch, and her presence around the hearth — alongside Robert and young Henry — formed the novel's primal image of the Morgan household: three dreamers gazing into the fire, "whose eyes looked out beyond the walls and saw unbodied things" [001-017].

When the fifteen-year-old Henry was about to depart for the Indies, Gwenliana performed a divination using "the shoulder bone of a new-killed sheep" [005-029], reading the future in the play of firelight on the bone. Her prophecy — that Henry would go to "a far, foreign land" and be "greatly considered" — filled her with happiness at the perceived accuracy of her art [005-039].

Years later, after Henry had achieved wealth and knighthood, the news of Gwenliana's final prophecy reached him through his father: "This night the world ends," she had declared, "and there will be no more earth to walk upon" [009-185]. This deathbed utterance resonates with the novel's recurring theme of worlds ending — the end of Henry's Welsh childhood, the closing of his buccaneering era, and the ultimate futility of earthly ambition.

## Relationships

- **[[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]]** — Grandson. Gwenliana's prophecy shaped the family's understanding of Henry's destiny. In his later years, Henry reflected on the meaning of her words.
- **[[old-robert-morgan|Old Robert Morgan]]** — Son. Robert was Gwenliana's chief caretaker and the keeper of her memory after her death. He confessed that he "taught old Gwenliana many things which had to do with magic" [009-205].
- **[[mother-morgan|Mother Morgan]]** — Daughter-in-law. The practical, earth-bound Mother Morgan had little patience for Gwenliana's abstractions and resented "the foolishness" of her prophecies [001-011].
