# Darien

## Overview

Darien is the dense, mountainous jungle region of eastern Panama, bordering Colombia — a place that, in the novel's geography, represents the untamable margin of the buccaneer world. It is the territory of "fierce" indigenous people who resist both Spanish and buccaneer incursions, and it serves as the site of L'Ollonais's gruesome death — a cautionary tale about the limits of buccaneer power.

## Geography

The Darien region spans eastern Panama and northwestern Colombia, encompassing dense tropical rainforest, mountains, and mangrove swamps. In the colonial period, it was one of the most impenetrable regions of the Spanish Main, never fully controlled by European powers. The indigenous Kuna (Cuna) people maintained their independence there against both Spanish and later Scottish colonial attempts. The terrain is extremely hostile — the "fierce Indians of Darien lived in trees and caves" (010-006), adapted to an environment that Europeans found lethal.

## In the Narrative

Darien appears in the novel primarily as a site of danger and boundary. It frames the buccaneer world geographically: buccaneer "reputations" were made "along the coast of Darien and among the green Caribbean islands" (009-001). The "coasts of Darien and Castilla de Oro" (009-021) are the hunting grounds of Mansveldt's generation, the earlier buccaneer captains who established the patterns Morgan inherits.

But Darien's most powerful narrative function is as the site of L'Ollonais's death. "The Indians of Darien were different; fierce, and filled with a persistent hatred of the buccaneers" (009-014). When the "most feared buccaneer of the western ocean" is captured there, the Indians do not simply kill him — they burn him alive. The death is retributive: L'Ollonais, who "slaughtered whole cities," meets an end proportionate to his violence.

Darien thus represents a boundary that buccaneer power cannot cross. The Spanish cities can be taken, the plate fleet can be raided, but Darien resists. Its indigenous inhabitants are outside the colonial and buccaneer systems alike, and their justice — burning alive the man who burned their villages — imposes a final limit on the buccaneer project.

The region also serves as a geographical premonition of Morgan's own campaign. When the buccaneers prepare to cross the isthmus to Panama, they know that "the fierce Indians of Darien lived in trees and caves" (010-006) nearby — the jungle that swallowed L'Ollonais is never far from the route Morgan's men will take.

## Historical Context

The Darien region was historically one of the last strongholds of indigenous resistance in Central America. The Spanish never fully subdued the Kuna people. In the 1690s, the Scottish attempted to establish a colony in Darien (the Darien Scheme), which ended disastrously and contributed to Scotland's financial ruin and eventual union with England in 1707. The historical L'Ollonais (Jean-David Nau, also spelled L'Olonnais) was indeed captured and killed by indigenous people in the Darien region in 1668, though accounts of his death vary — some sources say he was eaten by cannibals, others that he was burned. Exquemelin's account, which Steinbeck drew on, describes a particularly gruesome death in which L'Ollonais was "torn in pieces alive."
