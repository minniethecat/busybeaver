# Discoverie of Witchcraft (Reginald Scot)

**Reginald Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*** is the 1584 skeptical treatise that appears in *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]* as the rationalist antidote to [[defense-of-witchcraft|King James's *Defense of Witchcraft*]]. [[james-flower|James Flower]] encountered it too late — while healing from the broken legs he sustained trying to prove the *Defense* true by flying off his roof.

## Description

Published in 1584, *The Discoverie of Witchcraft* was one of the earliest English-language works to argue that witchcraft accusations were based on superstition, fraud, and mental illness rather than demonic power. Reginald Scot, a Kentish gentleman, wrote it in direct opposition to the continental witch-hunting manuals that had begun to influence English jurisprudence. The book was so controversial that King James I, upon his accession to the English throne, ordered copies burned. James's own *[[defense-of-witchcraft|Daemonologie]]* (1597) was written partly as a rebuttal.

## Role in the Narrative

The book appears as the second half of [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s intellectual arc:

> It was while his two broken legs were healing that he came on Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*. [008-144]

The timing is everything. Flower discovers the skeptical text only after he has already acted on the credulous one. The *Discoverie* confirms what his broken body already tells him — that King James was wrong — but it can only explain the error, not undo its consequences. This is Steinbeck's miniature tragedy of reading: the right book, at the wrong time, is useless.

The *Discoverie* joins [[commentaries|Caesar's *Commentaries*]] on Flower's shelves, part of a library vast in scope but inert in effect. Flower "had learned without absorbing, remembered without assimilating" [008-[[TOC|14]]7]; even the right books could not save him from the wrong ones.

## Symbolism

The *Discoverie of Witchcraft* represents the tragedy of belated knowledge. It is a book that *would have* saved Flower — if he had read it first. But books do not arrive in the order we need them, and the sequence of reading is itself a kind of fate. The *Discoverie* is also a symbol of the English rationalist tradition that Steinbeck both admires and gently mocks: Scot's skepticism is correct, but it arrives as an afterthought, a footnote to the damage already done by royal superstition. In this, the *Discoverie* reflects the novel's larger skepticism about the power of reason to govern human action — a theme embodied in [[henry-morgan|Henry Morgan]] himself, whose conquest of [[panama-city|Panama]] is driven not by reason but by a desire as irrational as the urge to fly.
