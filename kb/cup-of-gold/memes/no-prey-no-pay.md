# No Prey, No Pay

## Definition

"No prey, no pay" was the founding economic principle of the Free Brotherhood of the Coast: buccaneers received no wages or salaries, only shares of the captured prize, distributed according to articles of agreement drawn before each expedition. The captain received a fixed multiple of the common share; specialists (surgeon, carpenter, gunner) received additional fractions; and compensation was specified for loss of limb or eye. If no prize was taken, no one was paid. This principle made buccaneering a radically collective enterprise — closer to a workers' cooperative than to a naval hierarchy.

## In the Novel

Steinbeck presents the articles system as the institutional backbone of buccaneer culture. Before the Panama expedition, the council of captains at Tortuga draws the articles: "so many shares for the captain, so many for the surgeon, so many for the carpenter, and compensation for the loss of a right arm, a left arm, a right leg, a left leg, an eye" (009-005). The specificity is both comic and chilling — the body as balance sheet.

Morgan understands the articles system well enough to manipulate it. He promises larger shares to crews that join his Panama fleet, outbidding rival captains. The promise of gold — more gold than any single Spanish prize could yield — fills Tortuga's harbor with ships. When the Panama expedition succeeds beyond all expectation, the shares make every surviving buccaneer rich.

The irony is that Morgan himself eventually ceases to operate under the articles. As Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, he receives a fixed salary from the Crown. The man who rose to power through "no prey, no pay" ends his life on a payroll. Grippo's taunt — "You will take no more cups of gold" (010-393) — has an economic as well as a psychological dimension. The predator has become a pensioner.

## Symbolic Significance

"No prey, no pay" is the economic expression of the novel's deeper theme: reward must be proportional to risk, and compensation must be transparent if men are to risk their lives together. The articles system is, in its way, more honest than the Crown's system of knighthoods and governorships. The Crown rewards Morgan not for his risk but for his compliance — a transaction the novel treats as corrupting. The buccaneer articles, by contrast, represent a form of economic justice that civilization cannot match.
