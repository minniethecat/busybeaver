# Tortuga

## Overview

Tortuga (Île de la Tortue) is the rocky island off the north coast of Hispaniola that served as the primary stronghold of the Free Brotherhood of the Coast in the 17th century. In Steinbeck's novel, it is the gathering place where Henry Morgan assembles his Panama fleet: a chaotic, cosmopolitan port where "every morning new sails appeared on the horizon, and every evening the taverns were more crowded with ruffians from every corner of the world" (010-042).

## Geography

Tortuga is a small, mountainous island approximately 40 kilometers long, separated from Hispaniola by a narrow channel. Its natural harbor made it an ideal base for buccaneers: defensible, close to Spanish shipping lanes, and beyond the effective reach of colonial authorities. The novel emphasizes the contrast between the island's physical beauty — green mountains rising from blue water — and the violence of its inhabitants.

## In the Narrative

Tortuga is the novel's buccaneer capital. It is here that the council of captains debates the Panama expedition, that the articles of agreement are drawn ("so many shares for the captain, so many for the loss of an eye"), and that Morgan's fleet assembles. The gathering at Tortuga is described in almost mythic terms: "The ships of the Free Brotherhood gathered. They came singly from every hidden cove and secret harbor of the Spanish Main" (010-042).

The island functions as a threshold: once the fleet departs Tortuga, there is no turning back. The crossing of the Caribbean, the march across the isthmus, and the sack of Panama all follow from the decision made in Tortuga's smoky taverns.

## Historical Context

The historical Tortuga was contested by Spanish, French, and English forces throughout the 17th century and served as the primary base of the Caribbean buccaneers from approximately 1640 to 1670. The French eventually established formal control, and the buccaneers dispersed to other bases (notably Port Royal, Jamaica) after Morgan's era.
