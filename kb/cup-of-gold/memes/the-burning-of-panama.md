# The Burning of Panama

## Summary

Following Morgan's capture of Panama City in 1671, the city was consumed by fire — a catastrophe that destroyed one of the wealthiest cities in the Spanish Americas and generated lasting controversy over who was responsible. The Spanish governor, Don Juan Perez de Guzman, accused Morgan of deliberate arson, while the buccaneers maintained that the Spanish themselves had set the fires to deny the invaders their plunder. The burning of Panama is the ambiguous climax of Morgan's military career: a triumph that cannot be cleanly claimed, a conquest whose spoils go up in smoke.

## Context

After the buccaneers crossed the isthmus and defeated the Spanish defenders outside the city, Panama lay open. Morgan's men expected the richest plunder of their careers — the "Cup of Gold" of the novel's title. Panama was the Pacific terminus of the Spanish treasure route, and its merchants had accumulated generations of wealth. The buccaneers entered the city anticipating a division of spoils that would make every man rich.

## Narrative Account

The fire begins within hours of the city's fall. The novel treats the event as an ambiguous outcome rather than a described scene — the burning is referenced in its aftermath rather than narrated in real time. This narrative distance mirrors the historical uncertainty: no definitive eyewitness account establishes who lit the first flame.

What is clear is the result: the city that was to be Morgan's greatest prize is reduced to ash. The buccaneers "went out of ruined Panama" [来源待考], carrying what treasure they could salvage on mule-back across the isthmus. The fire transforms the sack of Panama from a triumph of buccaneer ambition into something more complex — a victory that feels like a defeat, a conquest whose material yield is consumed by the very forces the conquerors unleash.

The controversy over responsibility becomes a diplomatic crisis. The Spanish crown, outraged by the destruction of a city that was "one of the greatest cities in the world" [来源待考], demands accountability. Morgan's arrest and summons to England are direct consequences of the fire and the political fallout it generates.

## Key Figures

- [[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]] — commander of the buccaneer force, accused of arson
- [[don-juan-perez-de-guzman|Don Juan Perez de Guzman]] — Spanish governor who ordered the city's evacuation and may have ordered the fires
- [[governor-moddyford|Governor Moddyford]] — Jamaica's governor, summoned to England alongside Morgan to answer for the Panama expedition

## Outcome

The burning of Panama has three lasting consequences. First, it denies Morgan the full material reward of his greatest military achievement — the treasure is less than expected, and the division of spoils at Chagres becomes a scene of accusation and betrayal. Second, it provides the political pretext for Morgan's arrest and removal to England, setting in motion the chain of events that will lead to his knighthood and return as Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica. Third, it completes the novel's symbolic arc: the "Cup of Gold" that Morgan pursued across the isthmus is, by the time he reaches it, already ashes.

## Historical Basis

The historical burning of Panama City in 1671 is one of the most contested events in buccaneer history. Spanish accounts, including Don Juan Perez de Guzman's official report, accused Morgan's men of setting the fires during their looting. Morgan and his men maintained that the Spanish defenders, following the governor's orders, had set fire to the city's gunpowder stores and buildings to deny the buccaneers their plunder — a scorched-earth tactic. Modern historians tend to believe both accounts contain elements of truth: the Spanish likely started fires during their retreat, and the buccaneers' looting and the chaos of conquest certainly contributed to the spread of the flames. The historical Panama City was completely destroyed and subsequently rebuilt at a new location several miles away, where the modern city stands today.
