# Bras de Fer

## Role

Bras de Fer (French for "Iron Arm") is a figure of buccaneer legend in the novel's world — a captain of the earlier generation whose name alone commands silence and respect on the Tortuga docks. His role is genealogical and mythic: he is the father of Coeur de Gris, and his mysterious origins establish the pattern of concealed identities that runs through the buccaneer brotherhood. The name itself — "Bras de Fer" — is a title conferred by reputation, not a given name, and "no one ever questioned him farther" (009-016) about his true identity.

## Biography

Bras de Fer belongs to the generation before Henry Morgan — the era of Mansveldt, the Dutch admiral who dreamed of a buccaneer republic. When Morgan first went to sea "sailing with black Grippo in the *Ganymede*," Bras de Fer was already "an old man" (009-024). His career thus spans the period when buccaneering was consolidating from scattered privateers into an organized force.

The mystery of his name is emblematic of the buccaneer world: "It was never known whether his name was hidden for a shame or a sorrow or a hatred, but all the Coast came to know of him as a great, brave captain" (009-016). This concealment prefigures the way his son, Coeur de Gris, also operates under an assumed name — "remembering his" father's (009-080).

Bras de Fer had a relationship with a Huguenot woman of ancient family, one of the "free women of Goaves," who was sixteen when she gave birth to Coeur de Gris. She had been sent to the Caribbean as a vagrant after her family's destruction in the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre — a detail that roots Bras de Fer's personal history in the wider currents of European religious conflict.

The epithet "Iron Arm" and the stories of his strength — including the famous tale of throwing a rum keg with superhuman force, recounted in Chapter 5 — place Bras de Fer in the tradition of buccaneer tall tales that blur the line between historical fact and frontier legend. He is less a character than an origin point: the father whose legendary status his son both inherits and cannot escape.

## Historical Basis

Bras de Fer does not correspond to a documented historical buccaneer. The name (French for "Iron Arm") follows the pattern of buccaneer *noms de guerre* — like L'Ollonais or Rock Braziliano — in which a descriptive epithet replaces a birth name. Steinbeck may have drawn on the general type of the mysteriously-named French buccaneer captain, a figure attested in Exquemelin's *The Buccaneers of America*, but Bras de Fer as a specific character is fictional.
