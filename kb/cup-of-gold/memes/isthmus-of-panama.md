# Isthmus of Panama

## Overview

The Isthmus of Panama is the geographical fact that determines the shape of the novel's military campaign and, more broadly, the logic of the Spanish colonial economy. It is the narrow strip of land connecting the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans — "nigh impassable but for the burro trail" (010-155) — across which Spanish treasure from Peru was carried to the Caribbean for shipment to Spain. For Morgan's buccaneers, crossing the isthmus is the ordeal that separates the fleet's arrival at Chagres from the sack of Panama City.

## Geography

The Isthmus of Panama is the narrowest point between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans in the Americas, approximately 50 miles wide at its narrowest. In the seventeenth century, the Spanish *Camino Real* (Royal Road) crossed it, connecting the Caribbean port of Nombre de Dios (later Portobelo) to Panama City on the Pacific. The terrain was mountainous, densely jungled, and extremely hostile — "fierce Indians of Darien lived in trees and caves" along its margins (010-006). The Spanish transported Peruvian silver and gold along this route using mule trains, making it one of the most valuable transit corridors in the world.

## In the Narrative

The isthmus functions as both a physical obstacle and a symbolic threshold in the novel. On the one hand, it is the real military problem: "How will you do this thing? The city is strongly guarded with walls and troops, and the way across the isthmus is nigh impassable but for the burro trail" (010-155). Coeur de Gris's question articulates the strategic impossibility Morgan is determined to overcome.

On the other hand, the isthmus represents the boundary between the known and the forbidden. The "little rumor" of La Santa Roja "had come stealthily across the isthmus, had floated among the islands and stolen aboard the ships" (009-134). The legend precedes the invasion — desire crosses the isthmus before armies do. Morgan, on his march, reaches into his "memory for the speeches he had built on his way across the isthmus" (010-212), as if the crossing itself is the space where ambition takes its final, irrevocable form.

The isthmus also anchors the novel's economic backstory. Drake, the original English interloper, "set a trap for the sacred treasure train across the isthmus" [来源待考], establishing the pattern Morgan inherits. The treasure that flows across the isthmus funds "the kingdom of Spain — paying for the King's new palaces and wars" [来源待考]. In this sense, the isthmus is the circulatory system of empire, and Morgan's assault on it is an act of economic warfare as much as piracy.

The merchant class that "soon dominated all the isthmus" [来源待考] after the conquest represents the transformation of the Spanish colonial project from military occupation to commercial exploitation — a pattern Morgan's own governorship of Jamaica will later replicate on the English side.

## Historical Context

The Isthmus of Panama was the critical transit point for Spanish treasure from the sixteenth through eighteenth centuries. Silver from Potosí (modern Bolivia) was shipped to Panama City, then transported overland to Nombre de Dios or Portobelo for the Atlantic crossing. The route was vulnerable to pirate and privateer attacks, and Francis Drake's 1572–73 raid on the isthmus treasure train was a model for later English adventurers. The isthmus crossing was renowned for its difficulty — disease, terrain, and native resistance made it one of the most dangerous transit routes in the Spanish empire. The Panama Canal, completed in 1914, finally eliminated the need for the overland crossing.
