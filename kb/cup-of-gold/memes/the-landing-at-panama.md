# The Buccaneer Landing at Panama

## Summary

The landing of Morgan's fleet on the Panamanian coast marks the transition from buccaneer raiding to military invasion — the moment when Henry Morgan's ambition escalates from the taking of port cities to the conquest of an imperial capital. The buccaneers' longboats, "floating on the River Chagres, each one taxed to the limit with its load of men and arms" (010-087), represent the largest amphibious operation ever attempted by the Brethren of the Coast.

## Context

The Panama expedition is the culmination of a decade of escalating buccaneer ambition. Morgan has taken Maracaibo, sacked Portobelo, and terrorized the Spanish Main, but Panama City — the Pacific terminus of the Spanish treasure route — represents a target of a different order. It is guarded by walls, troops, and the near-impassable isthmus crossing. When Coeur de Gris asks, "How will you do this thing? The city is strongly guarded with walls and troops, and the way across the isthmus is nigh impassable but for the burro trail" (010-155), he articulates the strategic consensus that Panama cannot be taken.

## Narrative Account

The landing is described in Chapter 10 as an operation of immense logistical scale. Morgan assembles the largest buccaneer fleet ever gathered — ships from Tortuga, Goaves, and every buccaneer haven in the Caribbean. The fleet sails west along the coast of Darien, past the "fierce Indians" (010-006) who killed L'Ollonais, to the mouth of the River Chagres.

The fortress of San Lorenzo de Chagres, guarding the river mouth, is the first obstacle. The buccaneers take it by storm — a bloody frontal assault that establishes the tone for the campaign to come. Casualties are heavy, and the Spanish defenders fight to the last, knowing that the fortress is the only thing standing between Morgan's fleet and the isthmus crossing.

Once Chagres falls, the landing proper begins. The buccaneers transfer from their ships into longboats for the river passage — the transition from the sea, their native element, to the jungle interior, where the Spanish hold every advantage of terrain and supply. The image of the overloaded boats pushing upriver, men cramped under the tropical sun with "poisonous vines" (010-090) hanging overhead, captures the buccaneers' vulnerability at this stage of the operation.

## Key Figures

- [[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]] — commander of the expedition, whose reputation compels thousands to follow him
- [[coeur-de-gris|Coeur de Gris]] — Morgan's young lieutenant, who questions the feasibility of the landing
- [[grippo|Grippo]] — the old buccaneer captain among Morgan's commanders

## Outcome

The successful landing at Chagres sets the stage for the isthmus crossing and the assault on Panama City. More importantly, it establishes Morgan as a commander capable of operations beyond the hit-and-run tactics of conventional buccaneering. The transition from sea to land is the transition from piracy to conquest, and it is this achievement — the ability to project force inland — that distinguishes Morgan from his predecessors and makes him a figure the English crown must reckon with rather than merely tolerate.

## Historical Basis

Morgan's historical Panama expedition assembled approximately 1,400 men in 36 ships — the largest buccaneer fleet ever gathered. The assault on San Lorenzo de Chagres occurred in January 1671 and was, as Steinbeck depicts, a costly frontal assault in which the Spanish garrison fought to virtually the last man. The river passage and isthmus crossing that followed were grueling: the buccaneers ran out of food and were reduced to eating leather and foraging in the jungle. Exquemelin's account of the expedition, which Steinbeck used as a primary source, emphasizes the physical suffering of the crossing as much as the military achievement of the conquest.
