# Defense of Witchcraft (King James)

**King James's *Defense of Witchcraft*** (properly titled *Daemonologie*) is the 1597 treatise on witchcraft by [[king-james-i|James I of England]] that appears in *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]* as a catalyst for one of [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s most absurd misadventures. The book represents the collision of royal authority, occult credulity, and the disastrous consequences of taking ideas literally.

## Description

*Daemonologie* was King James's contribution to the European witchcraft debate — a work that affirmed the reality of witchcraft and prescribed methods for its detection and punishment. Written in the form of a philosophical dialogue, it was a direct response to Reginald Scot's skeptical *[[discoverie-of-witchcraft|Discoverie of Witchcraft]]* (1584). In Steinbeck's novel, the book is presented as dangerous precisely because it is authoritative: a king's imprimatur on supernatural belief.

## Role in the Narrative

The book appears in [[james-flower|James Flower]]'s backstory as the intellectual trigger for a catastrophic experiment:

> King James' *Defense of Witchcraft* came into his hands, and he set about to prove it true. With the aid of ancient incantations and a black lotion which contained a number of filthy ingredients together with a large amount of hashish, he attempted to fly from the roof of his house. It was while his two broken legs were healing that he came on Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*. [008-144]

The sequence is classic Steinbeckian irony: Flower reads a king's defense of witchcraft and decides to *prove* it — not through scholarship but through direct experiment. The "black lotion" with its "filthy ingredients" and hashish suggests that the "proof" required substantial chemical assistance. The outcome — two broken legs — is simultaneously tragic and comic, a literal fall that precedes Flower's social fall from English [[duty-of-appearances|respectability]].

The healing period introduces the counter-text: Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*, the skeptical response. But by then the damage — both orthopedic and social — is done. The two books bracket Flower's intellectual biography: the credulous work that drove him to the roof, and the skeptical work he read while bedridden with the consequences.

## Symbolism

The *Defense of Witchcraft* represents the peril of mixing authority with credulity. A king writes that witches fly; a commoner tries to fly and breaks his legs. Steinbeck's implicit point is that ideas have material consequences when taken seriously by people without the social protection to survive failure. King James could write *Daemonologie* and remain a king; [[james-flower|James Flower]] tried to verify it and lost his place in English society. The book also prefigures the novel's broader concern with belief and delusion: from [[merlin|Merlin]]'s Welsh magic to the buccaneers' worship of [[la-santa-roja|La Santa Roja]], characters in *[[the-cup-of-gold|Cup of Gold]]* constantly act on beliefs that reason would reject — and sometimes those actions reshape the world.
