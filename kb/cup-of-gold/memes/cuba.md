# Cuba

## Overview

Cuba appears in the novel as part of the buccaneer geography — an island in the Spanish colonial network that serves as a reference point for maritime operations, legends, and the reach of buccaneer reputation. It is associated with both the early buccaneer exploits (Pierre le Grand's capture of a Spanish ship "in a storm off Cuba," 009-008) and the romantic legends that follow buccaneers like Coeur de Gris, who "left a trail of broken hearts from Cuba to Saint Kit's" (009-066).

## Geography

Cuba is the largest island in the Caribbean, lying between the Florida Straits to the north and the Yucatán Channel to the west. In the seventeenth century, it was a key Spanish possession, with Havana serving as the principal Spanish port in the Caribbean and the assembly point for the plate fleets returning to Spain.

## In the Narrative

Cuba is referenced in three contexts. First, as the setting of Pierre le Grand's legendary exploit — the earliest buccaneer success story, in which a small boat of freebooters captured a Spanish vice-admiral's ship "in a storm off Cuba" (009-008). The location gives the story geographical specificity, grounding buccaneer legend in actual Caribbean waters.

Second, Cuba is the place where "the Spaniards strangled him in Cuba" (009-038), referring to one of the buccaneer captains who fell to Spanish justice. The juxtaposition — one buccaneer succeeds off Cuba, another is executed there — establishes the island as a space of risk and consequence in the buccaneer world.

Third, Cuba marks the western boundary of Coeur de Gris's amorous geography: "a trail of broken hearts from Cuba to Saint Kit's" (009-066). The span from Cuba in the west to Saint Kitts (Saint Kit's) in the eastern Caribbean maps the reach of the young French buccaneer's reputation, using the archipelago itself as a measure of legendary status.

## Historical Context

Cuba was the administrative center of the Spanish Caribbean from the sixteenth century onward. Havana's harbor was the designated meeting point for the Spanish treasure fleets (the *Flota de Indias*), making Cuba the linchpin of the Spanish colonial economy. English buccaneers and privateers frequently operated in Cuban waters, though a full-scale assault on Havana itself did not occur until the British capture of the city in 1762, well after Morgan's era.
