# Morgan Meets Elizabeth

## Summary

Henry Morgan's first meeting with Elizabeth is the emotional origin story of the novel — the moment when a Welsh farm boy falls in love with a woman above his station, and the forced separation that follows becomes the formative wound of his life. Elizabeth, torn from him on "one dark night" (009-105) by her powerful father, becomes the impossible object of desire that Henry pursues across oceans and decades, only to discover that what he wanted was not the woman but the wanting itself.

## Context

Henry Morgan grows up on a Welsh farm, the son of Robert Morgan, in a household dominated by his grandmother Gwenliana's prophecies and his mother's skepticism. Elizabeth belongs to a higher social stratum — her father is a "powerful lord" (009-105) who regards the Morgan boy as an unsuitable match. The class barrier between them is absolute, enforced by the rigid hierarchy of seventeenth-century rural Wales.

## Narrative Account

The novel does not dramatize the first meeting directly — it is told in retrospect, through Morgan's confession to Coeur de Gris aboard the ship to Panama, decades after the event. This narrative strategy makes the meeting less a scene than a scar: it is defined by its wounding consequences rather than its initial joy.

What Morgan remembers is the separation, not the meeting. "On one dark night my arms were pinned to my sides, and she—oh, dear Elizabeth!—was torn away from me. They placed me, still bound, in a ship, and sold me in Barbados" (009-105). The violence of the language — "pinned," "torn," "bound" — collapses the distinction between romantic separation and abduction. Elizabeth is not merely taken from him; he is taken from himself.

The wound never heals. "During these years, her face has followed me in all my wanderings. Somehow I feel that I might have made some later move — but her father was a powerful lord" (009-105). The conditional — "might have made some later move" — is the grammar of a life defined by what did not happen. Morgan's entire subsequent career can be read as an attempt to become the kind of man whose social position would make such a refusal impossible.

The irony of the novel is that when Morgan finally reunites with Elizabeth — now Lady Morgan, his wife — he discovers that the woman he married is not the woman he remembered. The Elizabeth of his memory was a projection, an idealized figure who served as the focus of ambition that needed no real object. In this sense, the first meeting is the novel's master key: it creates the desire that drives the plot, and the desire's object is always already absent.

## Outcome

The meeting and forced separation produce the novel's central psychological dynamic: Henry Morgan's ambition is powered not by desire for what he lacks but by the memory of having something taken from him. This inversion — ambition as revenge rather than aspiration — explains why Morgan's achievements never satisfy him. Each conquest, from Maracaibo to Panama, is an attempt to prove that he cannot be refused, but the original refusal cannot be undone. His deathbed vision, in which a faceless figure presents him with a ledger of his deeds, is the final accounting of a life spent trying to repay a debt that was never monetary.
