# Governor Moddyford

## Role

Governor Moddyford is the English Governor of Jamaica, whose brief but pivotal appearance in the novel sets in motion Henry Morgan's transition from Caribbean buccaneer to knight of the English realm. He delivers the news that both he and Morgan have been summoned to England.

## Biography

Governor Moddyford summons Captain Morgan with news he is uncertain how to characterize: "I don't know whether my news is good or bad. Word of your conquest has come to the ears of the King. Both of us are ordered to England" `[011-010]`. The summons carries an implicit threat — Morgan's sack of Panama, conducted without explicit Crown authorization, could result in punishment rather than reward.

When Moddyford finally broaches a subject he has been waiting to raise, the conversation shifts from matters of state to matters of family `[011-019]`. The Governor's household becomes the stage on which the reunion between Henry and his cousin Elizabeth is arranged, and where Lady Moddyford takes charge of orchestrating their marriage.

Moddyford's role in the novel is emblematic of the colonial administration that profited from buccaneer violence while maintaining the forms of civilized governance. He is the intermediary figure between the lawless world of the Spanish Main and the courtly world of Restoration England.

## Historical Basis

The historical Sir Thomas Modyford (c. 1620–1679) was Governor of Jamaica from 1664 to 1671. He issued privateering commissions to Henry Morgan and was recalled to England alongside Morgan after the sack of Panama, which had violated a peace treaty with Spain. Modyford was briefly imprisoned in the Tower of London but was later released and returned to Jamaica as Chief Justice. Steinbeck condenses Modyford's role to the pivotal moment of the summons, using him as a narrative hinge between Morgan's buccaneer career and his transformation into a Crown officer.
