## Overview

The Isthmus of Darien is the narrowest land passage between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, a dense tropical wilderness that presented both a formidable obstacle and a strategic corridor for Henry Morgan's Panama expedition. The crossing of Darien is one of the novel's central physical ordeals — a test of endurance that strips Morgan's army of its weaker members and foreshadows the moral dissolution awaiting them at Panama.

## In the Novel

Morgan's decision to cross Darien rather than sail around Cape Horn is presented as a calculated gamble: the overland route is shorter but deadlier. The buccaneers must navigate trackless jungle, hostile native territory, and dwindling supplies. The crossing functions as a narrative crucible — those who survive Darien are hardened into instruments of conquest; those who cannot endure are left behind.

The isthmus is associated with violence that predates Morgan. The "Indians of Darien" are remembered as the people who burned the buccaneer L'Ollonais alive — a story that haunts the buccaneer imagination and reinforces Darien's reputation as a place where European power meets its limits.

The novel uses Darien's geography symbolically: the narrow strip of land between two oceans mirrors the narrow margin between civilization and savagery, between sanctioned warfare and piracy, between ambition and ruin. Morgan's passage through Darien is a passage between identities — the man who enters the jungle is not the same man who emerges on the Pacific shore.
