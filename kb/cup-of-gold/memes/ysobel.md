# Ysobel

**Doña Ysobel Espinoza, Valdez y los Gabilanes** — known to the common people of [[panama-city|Panama]] as [[la-santa-roja|La Santa Roja]] (the Red Saint) — is the woman who humiliates [[henry-morgan|Sir Henry Morgan]] at the height of his power. When Morgan [[sack-of-panama|captures Panama]], he expects to take whatever he desires, including the beautiful noblewoman. But Ysobel, armed with only a pin and her laughter, proves that there are conquests beyond even the greatest buccaneer's reach.

## The Red Saint of Panama

Ysobel enters the narrative in the aftermath of the [[sack-of-panama|Sack of Panama]], when Morgan, now master of the city, learns of a woman the people call [[la-santa-roja|La Santa Roja]] — the Red Saint. The epithet comes from her striking red hair, an unusual feature among the Spanish colonials that marks her as extraordinary:

> "She is the Doña Ysobel Espinoza, Valdez y los Gabilanes, Señor. The simple people of the city have called her La Santa Roja." [010-326]

She is no ordinary captive. Her grandfather owns ten silver mines and fifty leagues of fertile land in [[peru|Peru]], making her one of the wealthiest heiresses in the Spanish colonial empire. Her capture during the sack of Panama is a political and financial event of the first order [010-435].

## The Courtship

Morgan, accustomed to taking what he wants by force or by charm, attempts to seduce Ysobel with grand romantic speeches. His approach reveals both his vanity and his confusion — in the midst of his declarations, he accidentally calls her by [[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]'s name:

> "You must marry me, Elizabeth — Ysobel. I think I love you, Ysobel. You must come away with me and live with me and be my wife, under the protection of my name and of my hand." [010-217]

This slip exposes the essential emptiness of Morgan's passion. Ysobel is not a person to him but a placeholder — another iteration of the feminine ideal he has been chasing since [[wales|Wales]]. His elaborate seduction rhetoric, full of poetic hyperbole about "flaming white fire" and "deathless oil" [010-220], collapses under Ysobel's quiet amusement.

## The Defeat

Where other women in the novel are conquered, seduced, or idealized from a distance, Ysobel alone defeats Morgan directly. When gentle mockery fails to deter him, she resorts to physical resistance:

> "En garde!" she cried. Then the captain rushed at her. His arms encircled her shoulders and his hands were tearing at her clothing. Ysobel stood quite still, but one hand darted about with its pin — striking, striking — like a small white serpent. Little spots of blood appeared on Henry's cheeks, on his throat. [010-270]

> "Your eyes next, Captain," she said quietly, and stabbed him thrice on the cheekbone. [010-271]

The pin is a brilliantly ironic weapon — a feminine tool turned to defense, capable of wounding the most feared buccaneer in the Caribbean. Steinbeck's narration makes the lesson explicit:

> "A man may beat — may subject to every violation — a woman who cries and runs away, but he is helpless before one who stands her ground and only laughs." [010-271]

## The Aftermath

Ysobel's resistance shatters Morgan's confidence. His fear is not of her but of his own men discovering his humiliation:

> "Groups of pirates would be silent as he passed, and when he had gone they would break into sharp laughter. This hidden ridicule was terrifying to Henry Morgan." [010-250]

In a desperate attempt to reassert his mastery, Morgan kills a man at random, reasoning that "he had just killed a man to prove it" [010-267]. But the logic collapses — violence cannot restore the authority Ysobel has stripped away.

The episode also poisons Morgan's relationship with [[coeur-de-gris|Coeur de Gris]], whom he suspects of having witnessed his defeat. Where Ysobel's contempt is direct, Coeur de Gris's would be worse: "sympathy and understanding" — "the pity of a young, handsome man who condones the amorous failure of one not so handsome" [010-282].

## Ransom and Departure

Ysobel is ultimately ransomed back to her husband for twenty thousand pieces of eight — a sum her messenger notes is trivial compared to her inheritance [010-435]. Her departure marks a turning point in Morgan's conquest of Panama. The city yields its gold, but its most valuable treasure — the Red Saint — remains beyond his grasp.

In their final exchange, Morgan acknowledges his defeat with uncharacteristic honesty:

> "It does not seem worth the while to lie any more, and that is only one more proof of dead youth. But now, goodbye, Ysobel. I wish I loved you now as I thought I did yesterday." [010-397]

## Symbolic Role

| Aspect | Ysobel | Elizabeth | Paulette |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------|
| Social status | Noble heiress | Tenant's daughter / cousin | Prostitute |
| Morgan's approach | Romantic conquest | Idealized memory | Physical desire |
| Outcome | Defeats Morgan | Marries Morgan | Discarded by Morgan |
| Agency | Active resistance | Passive acceptance | Fleeting independence |

Ysobel is the only woman in the novel who refuses to be defined by Morgan's desire. She is neither the idealized virgin ([[elizabeth|Elizabeth]]) nor the disposable lover ([[la-santa-roja|Paulette]]). She is simply herself — and in being herself, she exposes the hollowness of Morgan's [[wanting-the-moon|grand ambitions]].
