# Barbados

## Overview

Barbados is the English sugar colony where Henry Morgan spends his five years of indentured servitude — the personal "Abred" (circle of struggle) that Gwenliana prophesied. It is here, on James Flower's plantation, that Henry reads Caesar, learns the mechanics of colonial power, and emerges from indenture with a ship of his own and the knowledge that "all military operations were, at base, glorified trickery" (008-176).

## In the Narrative

Barbados occupies chapter 8 and functions as Henry's Caribbean education. He arrives as a betrayed, bound laborer — sold by Tim the Bo's'n for eleven pounds to a "skull-faced Puritan master" (008-122). He is purchased by James Flower, an atypical planter: an intellectual recluse who treats Henry less as a servant than as a student.

Flower's library — Caesar, Xenophon, Descartes, Bacon — is Henry's university. From the *Commentaries*, he absorbs the principles of military deception. From Xenophon, he learns "the management of men" (008-176). The classical education is complemented by a carnal one: Paulette, a slave woman of mixed ancestry, initiates Henry into the realities of desire and manipulation, attempting to trap him into marriage.

The Barbados chapter ends with liberation: Flower, honoring a Christmas tradition, sets Henry free. With Flower's financial help, Henry purchases a two-masted ship, the *Elizabeth*, and sails away from the island — no longer a Welsh farm boy but a man who has learned that the world is organized by power, and that power can be taken.

## Historical Context

Historical Barbados was England's richest sugar colony in the 17th century, its economy built on enslaved African labor and indentured European servants. The indenture system brought thousands of poor English, Welsh, and Irish migrants to the island on contracts of 4-7 years. Henry Morgan's indenture is plausible within this context, though no historical record confirms it. Barbados was also a launching point for buccaneering expeditions against the Spanish Main.
