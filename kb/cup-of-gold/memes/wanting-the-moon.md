# Wanting the Moon

## Definition

"Wanting the moon" is the primal metaphor of *Cup of Gold*, articulated by Merlin when he first meets the boy Henry Morgan on Crag-top. "You are a little boy," Merlin says. "You want the moon to drink from as a golden cup; and so, it is very likely that you will become a great man — if only you remain a little child. All the world's great have been little boys who wanted the moon; running and climbing, they sometimes caught a firefly" (003-033). The metaphor encodes the novel's entire philosophical architecture: ambition is essentially childish, the object of desire is essentially unattainable (the moon), and greatness consists not in achieving the moon but in continuing to reach for it.

## In the Novel

The metaphor is introduced in chapter 3 and echoes through every subsequent chapter. Henry's departure from Wales, his indenture in Barbados, his rise through the buccaneer ranks, his conquest of Panama — each stage is a reach for the moon. The metaphor's twist is that the moon is not a real target: it stands for whatever ultimate desire animates a particular soul. For Henry, it is first "the Indies" (the mythic abroad), then Panama (the Cup of Gold), then La Santa Roja (the woman conflated with the city).

Merlin's qualification — "running and climbing, they sometimes caught a firefly" — is the novel's tragic insight. The moon is unattainable by definition. The best the striver can catch is a firefly: a small, terrestrial light that resembles the moon only in the mind of the child who caught it. Henry Morgan catches Panama — his firefly — and discovers it is not the moon. The rest of the novel traces the consequences of this discovery.

The metaphor is explicitly recalled in Morgan's deathbed chapter. As he faces Brother Death, the voice of the dying man remembers "a little boy who wanted the moon" (014-053). The circular structure — from Merlin's prophecy to deathbed recollection — gives the novel its formal coherence: a life bounded by a single metaphor.

## Symbolic Significance

The moon-golden-cup-firefly chain is the novel's central symbolic structure. It links the cosmic (moon), the material (gold), the ritual (cup), and the terrestrial (firefly) into a single image-complex. The metaphor's power lies in its reversibility: wanting the moon is both the condition of greatness and the guarantee of failure. The novel does not resolve this paradox — it inhabits it.
