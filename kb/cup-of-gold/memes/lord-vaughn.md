# Lord Vaughn

## Role

Lord Vaughn is a minor figure of the English court who appears indirectly in Chapter 13 through Lady Morgan's report of his opinions. He represents the metropolitan establishment's view of buccaneers — that they are "like vicious dogs, to be exterminated as soon as possible" (013-087) — and serves as a narrative foil to Sir Henry Morgan, who knows the buccaneer world from the inside and disputes Vaughn's authority on the subject.

## Biography

Lord Vaughn appears only through the filter of Elizabeth Morgan's conversation with her husband. The Morgans are to dine with "the Vaughns" (013-071), marking them as a couple of the Port Royal social circle. Lady Morgan, who prides herself on having made Henry "a knight and a gentleman" (013-057), deploys Lord Vaughn's opinions as an external authority to validate her own anti-buccaneer sentiments.

Vaughn's views on buccaneers are unambiguous: "They are a positive danger even to ourselves. He says they may stop fighting Spain at any moment and start on us. He says they are like vicious dogs, to be exterminated as soon as possible" (013-087). This position perfectly aligns with Lady Morgan's social ambitions — she feels "a little safer every time one of them is out of the way" (013-087).

Henry Morgan's attempted rebuttal — "Lord Vaughn knows nothing about buccaneers, while I—" (013-088) — is cut off by his wife, who has no interest in his expertise on the matter. The exchange is emblematic of Henry's position in English society: his experience counts for nothing against court opinion, and his past identity as a buccaneer commander cannot be admitted in polite company.

The Vaughns as a social unit also serve to anchor the Morgans' domestic life. Lady Morgan's anxiety about the drunken coachman before "dinner with the Vaughns" (013-071) paints a picture of a colonial elite performing metropolitan respectability — a performance in which Henry is always slightly out of step.

## Historical Basis

Lord Vaughn does not correspond to an identifiable historical figure in the records of the Restoration court. He is likely a composite or invented character serving Steinbeck's need for a voice of metropolitan establishment opinion — the kind of courtier who would have populated Charles II's circle and whose views on colonial affairs would have carried weight disproportionate to their actual knowledge of the Caribbean.
