# Old Robert Morgan

## Role

Old Robert Morgan is Henry Morgan's father and the novel's primary counterpoint to its protagonist. He is the man who faced the same hunger Henry feels — "wanting the moon" — and chose, or was forced, to stop reaching. His life as a Welsh farmer, husband, and father embodies a philosophy of sufficiency: the purple porridge, the warm kitchen, the domestic peace that requires no conquest. The novel neither condemns nor idealizes him; he is simply the road Henry does not take.

## Biography

Robert Morgan farms a small holding in the Welsh valleys, in an ancient stone farmhouse built by an ancestor who "played against Time and nearly won" (001-003). He is a gentle, philosophical man, given to brewing experimental ales and reflecting on his own failed ambitions. He keeps pikes on the walls — weapons unused since his ancestor fought with Glendower — as relics of a martial past his family has left behind.

His relationship with Henry is tender but strained by mutual incomprehension. Robert sees in his son the hunger he once felt and has since extinguished. He tries to warn Henry, not against ambition itself, but against the assumption that achieving the moon will satisfy: "I wanted it. Above all desires I wanted it. I reached for it and then — then I grew to be a man, and a failure" (003-035). But the warning is useless; Henry cannot hear it because he has not yet reached.

Robert's personal theology — "God is Love. God is the purple porridge that warms your belly on a cold morning" (002-027) — is the novel's most tender image of faith. It is a theology of the hearth, completely adequate to a life lived within a single valley, among people who know each other. It cannot encompass the Caribbean, the sack of cities, or the split self — but the novel never suggests it needs to.

When Henry leaves Wales, Robert's response is quiet grief. He does not try to stop his son. He watches him go, and returns to his porridge. The novel does not bring them together again.

## Historical Basis

Old Robert Morgan is a fictional character. No historical record identifies Henry Morgan's father in any detail. Steinbeck invents him as a thematic counterweight: the man who represents everything Henry is not and everything he fears becoming.
