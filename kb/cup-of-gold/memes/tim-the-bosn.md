Tim the Bos'n is the agent of Henry Morgan's fall from freedom — the sailor who, finding young Henry stowed away on his ship, sells him into indentured servitude on James Flower's Barbados plantation. His role in the novel is brief but pivotal: without Tim's commodification of Henry's body, there is no Caribbean career, no buccaneer admiral, no knighted governor.

The novel treats Tim with unexpected tenderness. When he encounters Henry years later, Tim is consumed by guilt. "If only I had a religion to me like the master," he laments, "I might say, 'Tis God's will'" [005-??]. His remorse is genuine — he is not a villain but a poor sailor making the only living he knows, and his act of selling Henry haunts him. Henry forgives him, recognizing that Tim was merely the instrument of a larger economic logic: the indenture system that turned human beings into cargo [008-005–006].

Tim's Irish identity is significant. Like the Welsh Henry, Tim is a Celtic outsider in an English imperial system. Their shared marginality creates an unexpected bond — the man who sold Henry and the man who was sold are, in the novel's final assessment, both victims of the same colonial machinery.
