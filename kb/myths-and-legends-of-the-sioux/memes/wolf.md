## Overview

The **wolf** occupied a distinctive place in Sioux storytelling — neither wholly animal nor wholly spirit, the wolf was a being with its own social structure (wolf chiefs, wolf villages), capable of interacting with humans on its own terms. Wolves appeared as hunters and as hunted, as husbands and as threats. Their howling served as a signal, their cunning as a model, and their presence on the battlefield as a grim aftermath of war.

## In the Stories

### The Wolf Village

The most developed wolf narrative involves a young woman who was taken to "the wolf village" (010-042) and married to a wolf chief. The "wolf chief led the young woman to" (010-012) his village, where she lived among wolf-people who had their own social organization mirroring human society. The wolf chief asked practical questions: "prepare it for eating?" (010-018) and "make the meat ready?" (010-023) — domestic concerns within a wolf-human marriage. After a set period, "the wolf chief said to her" (010-029) that she could return, showing the wolf as a being of his word.

### Transformation and Howling

The boundary between human and wolf was permeable. The young woman, returned from the wolf village, "began to howl like a wolf, loudly" (010-058) — having absorbed wolf nature during her sojourn. This howling was not madness but a signal: "In a moment the earth seemed covered with wolves" (010-059) — her wolf kin answered her call. The howl bridged species, summoning the wolf nation to human concerns.

### The Wolf as Hunter

Wolves were skilled hunters whose methods humans could learn from. A "prairie wolf" (034-061) was imitated by a clever character as a hunting ruse — the "imitation of a prairie wolf" (034-061) served as camouflage. In a trap design, a space was left large "enough to admit the body of a wolf" (027-045), and when "the wolf, scenting the meat" (027-045) entered, the trap "thus released would hold the wolf fast" (027-048). The wolf's keen nose made it both a formidable hunter and vulnerable to bait.

### Wolf in the Bestiary

Wolves were listed among the great animals of the Sioux world: "buffalo, deer, antelope, bear, wolf, wildcat, mountain lion" (037-046) — the wolf ranked alongside these noble creatures. A "noise as though a wolf or cat" (020-155) signaled danger, and wolves "covered" (010-059) the battlefield after combat — the wolf as scavenger at the margins of human violence.

## Cultural Significance

- Wolves had their own social structure (chiefs, villages) mirroring human society
- Marriage between human women and wolf chiefs was a narrative motif — the permeable human-animal boundary
- Wolf howling was a communicative act that could summon the pack across distances
- The prairie wolf was a model for hunting cunning — humans imitated wolves as a strategy
- Wolves appeared in the Sioux bestiary alongside buffalo, bear, and deer as noble creatures
- Wolves as battlefield scavengers ("the earth seemed covered with wolves") connected them to death and war's aftermath
