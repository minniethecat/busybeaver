## Overview

The **lodge** (or **tipi**) is the fundamental domestic space in Sioux culture and folklore. More than mere shelter, the lodge represents family, hospitality, and the boundary between the human world and the wilderness beyond. Characters in the McLaughlin corpus constantly move between the lodge and the outside world, and what happens *inside* versus *outside* the lodge often marks the difference between safety and danger, the ordinary and the supernatural.

## In the Stories

### Domestic Life and Family

The lodge is where families live, grandmothers instruct, and children grow. [[rabbit]] lives with his grandmother in their lodge when the Bear with the [[flint]] Body threatens them. The lodge walls mark the boundary of family protection:

> "The Rabbit and his grandmother were in dire straits, for the Bear with the Flint Body had taken their provisions and threatened their lodge." (009-001–009-005)

### Hospitality and Encounter

The lodge is also a place of encounter between humans and the supernatural. In Chapter 020, Rabbit discovers "a nice little log hut" while traveling:

> "Several days after this the Rabbit was traveling along the banks of a river. In the distance stood a nice little log hut. Rabbit was wondering who lived there." (020-082–020-084)

Inside, he finds the old man with whom he impulsively trades his youth — a transformation that could only occur within the charged space of another's dwelling.

### The Council Lodge

The lodge serves as a gathering place for governance and decision-making. After one of the young men kills Unktomi's companion, the [[council]] convenes:

> "The young man who had been present reported the matter to the council, which was held in the great council lodge." (021-021–021-025)

### Contrast with the [[butte]]

| Space | Character | Encounters |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Lodge** | Domestic, enclosed, safe | Family, council, hospitality |
| **Butte** | Exposed, elevated, liminal | Giants, supernatural beings, visions |

The movement between lodge and butte structures many Sioux narratives: characters leave the safety of the lodge, encounter danger or transformation on the butte or in the wild, and return changed.

## Construction and Material Culture

The traditional Sioux tipi was constructed of buffalo hides stretched over a framework of lodgepole pines. Its conical shape shed wind efficiently and its smoke flaps could be adjusted for ventilation. The lodge was the woman's domain — she owned it, erected it, and maintained it — making it a space of female authority within the broader patriarchal hunting culture.
