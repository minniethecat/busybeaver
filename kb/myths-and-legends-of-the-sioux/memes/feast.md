## Overview

The **feast** (Lakota: **Wicohan**) is the central social ceremony in Sioux folklore — a shared meal that transcends mere eating to become an act of community building, conflict resolution, and spiritual observance. Throughout the McLaughlin corpus, feasts mark transitions: the successful [[buffalo]] hunt, the arrival of guests, the making of peace, and the reinforcement of [[council]] decisions.

## In the Stories

### The Hunting Feast

The most common feast in the corpus follows a successful buffalo hunt. In Chapter 022, after [[bear]] and [[rabbit]] have hunted six buffalo, the bounty becomes a feast — though its distribution reveals Bear's exploitative nature:

> "Bear got busy butchering and poor Rabbit, thinking he would get a chance at the meat, waited hungrily." (022-049)

The hunting feast was both practical (distributing meat before it spoiled) and ceremonial — affirming the hunter's status and the community's gratitude to the animal spirits.

### The Peace Feast

In Chapter 017, a feast serves diplomatic purposes. The sharing of food — particularly [[wasna]] (pemmican) and dried meat — accompanies the [[peace-pipe]] ceremony as a gesture of goodwill:

> "They had a good dinner for him, for Unktomi is always hungry." (017-095–017-097)

The combination of feast and peace pipe establishes a ritual grammar: eating together makes enemies into kin.

### The Ceremonial Feast

Feasts also mark spiritual occasions. The [[corn]] harvest, the return of warriors, and the arrival of strangers all prompt ceremonial gatherings. In Chapter 035, a feast is prepared to welcome a mysterious visitor:

> "There was always a plentiful supply in his lodge, the stranger soon had a feast prepared." (035-082)

| Feast Type | Occasion | Social Function |
|-----------|----------|----------------|
| Hunting feast | Successful buffalo kill | Distribute meat, honor hunters |
| Peace feast | Conflict resolution | Seal alliances, share food with former enemies |
| Welcome feast | Arrival of guests | Establish hospitality bonds |
| Harvest feast | Corn harvest | Celebrate agricultural bounty |
| Council feast | After council decisions | Affirm governance through shared meal |

## Cultural Significance

The feast was not merely a meal but a **social contract enacted through food**. To share meat at a feast was to acknowledge membership in the community; to be excluded from the feast (as Rabbit often was by Bear) was to be denied that membership. The distribution of food at feasts reinforced social hierarchies while also providing a mechanism for their temporary suspension — in the feast circle, all who ate together were, for that moment, equals.

The feast also served as the primary context for storytelling. Many of the tales in the McLaughlin collection would have been told around feast fires, making the feast both the *subject* of stories and the *setting* for their telling.
