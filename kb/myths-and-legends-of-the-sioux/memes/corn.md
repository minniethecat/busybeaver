## Overview

**Corn** (Lakota: **Wagmiza**) occupies a central position in Sioux agriculture, spirituality, and folklore. It appears not merely as food but as a sacred gift — sometimes discovered through vision, sometimes provided by supernatural beings. Three distinct stories in the McLaughlin corpus address corn's origins, its proper cultivation, and its role as sustenance for the people.

## In the Stories

### The Forgotten Ear of Corn (Chapter 003)

An Arikara woman, gathering corn in the field, deliberately leaves behind a single ear — a "forgotten" offering. This act of neglect or sacrifice becomes the seed of a tale about the relationship between humans and the plant world.

> An Arikara woman was once gathering corn from the field to store away
> for winter use. She passed from stalk to stalk, tearing off the ears and
> dropping them into her folded robe. When all was gathered she started to
> go, when she heard a faint voice, like a child's, weeping and calling:
>
> "Oh, do not leave me! Do not go away without me."
>
> The woman was astonished. "What child can that be?" she asked herself.
> "What babe can be lost in the cornfield?"
>
> (001-001–001-008)

### The Signs of Corn (Chapter 017)

This tale embeds corn within a system of natural signs and agricultural knowledge. The people learn to read the indicators that tell them when to plant:

> "When the leaves of the oak are the size of a squirrel's ear, then it is time to plant corn." (017-001–017-005)

The story weaves practical knowledge with narrative, transmitting agricultural wisdom across generations through storytelling.

### The Hermit, or the Gift of Corn (Chapter 025)

The most explicitly sacred corn narrative. A solitary hermit, living apart from the tribe, receives corn as a divine gift — not through his own effort, but through supernatural intervention. The tale establishes corn as something that comes to humanity through grace rather than invention:

| Story | Aspect of Corn |
|-------|---------------|
| The Forgotten Ear (003) | Offering and sacrifice |
| The Signs of Corn (017) | Agricultural knowledge and timing |
| The Gift of Corn (025) | Divine origin and sacredness |

## Cultural Significance

In Sioux and broader Plains Indian culture, corn was one of the **Three Sisters** (along with beans and squash) — the agricultural foundation of settled life. Unlike buffalo, which was hunted, corn required planting, tending, and harvest — a relationship of care rather than pursuit. The stories reflect this: corn is not taken but *received*, through wisdom, vision, or divine gift.

Corn also features in ceremonial contexts. The Green Corn Dance and other harvest rituals marked the agricultural calendar, blending practical celebration with spiritual observance.
