## Overview

The **flint arrowhead** is a recurring motif in Sioux mythology, appearing as both an essential hunting tool and a weapon of supernatural retribution. Across multiple stories, arrowheads are linked to mythic beings — the Bear Chief whose body is the source of all flint, and Unktomi (the great spider) who crafts them — underscoring their significance beyond mere implements.

## Mythological Origins

In the tale of "The Rabbit and the Bear with the Flint Body," the origin of flint for arrowheads is explained through the figure of the Bear Chief. All the flint in the world resided in the bear's body — one half flesh, the other half solid flint (009-004). When the [[rabbit|Rabbit]] found himself without arrows for the fall hunt, his grandmother directed him to seek flint from the old bear chief (009-005). Visitors would strike flakes of flint from the bear's body with a stone hammer, taking care not to strike too hard, thus obtaining the raw material for their arrowheads.

> "You must make some flint arrowheads," said his grandmother. "Then you
> will be able to kill game."
>
> (009-005–009-006)

## Unktomi the Arrowhead Maker

The spider-being [[unktomi|Unktomi]] is depicted as the craftsman of flint arrowheads. In the story "Unktomi and the Arrowheads," two young men ascending a hill heard a ticking sound "as if small stones or pebbles were being struck together" (021-010). They discovered a large spider "busily engaged making the flint rocks into arrow heads" (021-010), working steadily on a piece of flint "nearly completed into another arrowhead" (021-013).

> Looking around they discovered a large spider sitting in the midst of
> a great many flint arrowheads. The spider was busily engaged making the
> flint rocks into arrow heads. They looked at the spider, but he never
> moved, but continued hammering away on a piece of flint which he had
> nearly completed into another arrowhead.
>
> (021-010–021-013)

Unktomi's work was a service to the people. The chief of the Unktomi tribe later explained: "seeing that your tribe was running short of arrowheads, I set a great many of my tribe to work making flint arrowheads for you" (021-035). When engaged in this task, the arrowhead-makers did not wish to be disturbed (021-048).

## The Deadly Arrowhead

Arrowheads in Sioux mythology could also serve as instruments of supernatural justice. When a thoughtless young man struck Unktomi with an arrowhead while the spider was working (021-018), Unktomi retaliated by shooting him with "a very tiny arrowhead" (021-041). This produced a hemorrhage that caused the young man's death (021-041). The episode established a lasting taboo: ever afterward, when Indians heard a ticking sound in the grass, they would go out of their way to avoid it, saying "Unktomi is making arrowheads; we must not disturb him" (021-048, 021-050).

A quiver "full of flint arrowheads" (022-078) also appears as part of a warrior's ceremonial attire in the story of the [[rabbit|Rabbit]] dressing his son in finery, symbolizing readiness and martial prowess.

## Cultural Significance

- Arrowheads represent the intersection of the practical and the supernatural — necessary for hunting yet tied to mythic beings
- The Bear Chief story explains the scarcity and value of flint as a raw material
- Unktomi's role as arrowhead-maker elevates a mundane craft to a sacred, undisturbed activity
- The tiny arrowhead that kills serves as a warning: even the smallest tool can be lethal when wielded in justified retribution
