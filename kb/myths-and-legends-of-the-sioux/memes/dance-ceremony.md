## Overview

**Dance** and **song** were essential forms of Sioux ceremonial expression, spanning sacred ritual, martial preparation, social courtship, and entertainment. Dances were performed in circles, accompanied by singing, and often held deep symbolic meaning — from the Sun Dance of thanksgiving to the war dance that honored warriors and prepared them for battle.

## In the Stories

### The Sun Dance and Sacred Ceremony

The most solemn dance was the Sun Dance, associated with the medicine pole and offerings of thanks "to the Great" Spirit (018-039). This was not merely performance but a sacred act of devotion, held at significant communal gatherings and tied to the ritual calendar.

### The War Dance

Before and after battle, the Sioux held great war dances. When the Lone Warrior returned, word spread "that there would be a great war dance given in honor of the Lone" Warrior (023-162), and the people "turned out and had a great war dance that was kept" going through the night (023-164). In the center of "the dance circle" (023-167), trophies and gifts were placed, and the Lone Warrior "gave to each" participant a token of honor (023-167). The war dance served to celebrate courage, distribute honors, and strengthen communal bonds.

### Courtship and Comic Dance

Dance was also a medium of courtship and humor. A young suitor "sprang up and began to dance, at the same time gesturing" to win favor (015-031), later explaining "I sang and danced for her" (015-046) — though his dancing in a tepee where mourners gathered was considered "a strange thing for a lad to dance" (015-049). He "did the best that he knew. He danced to make the mother" understand his intentions (015-059).

Comic dance featured in trickster tales. The raccoon entertained by dancing in a circle while "singing a comic song" (015-032), his antics drawing laughter as he "kept up his comic song" (015-036) with exaggerated gestures.

### The Song of Love and Death

Music accompanied dance. A warrior's "deep voice" carried "the beautiful love song of Pretty" (030-092), while warriors riding into battle sang "their death song" (031-078) — a solemn acceptance of mortality that transformed fear into courage. Songs marked every transition: hunting parties "came by, singing their song of the chase" (030-210).

## Cultural Significance

- Dance served four distinct functions: sacred devotion (Sun Dance), martial honor (war dance), social courtship, and entertainment
- The war dance reinforced hierarchy — gifts and honors distributed in the circle reflected status
- Circle formation was universal: "a great circle about him and danced" (011-020)
- Song and dance were inseparable — "singing the comic song" while dancing (015-032)
- The death song embodied the Sioux warrior ideal: accepting mortality with song
- Dancing in inappropriate contexts (mourning, tepee) was a social transgression, revealing dance's regulated place in Sioux life
