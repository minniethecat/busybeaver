[018-001]When corn is to be planted by the Indians, it is the work of the women

[018-002]folk to see to the sorting and cleaning of the best seed. It is also the

[018-003]women’s work to see to the planting. (This was in olden times.)

[018-004]After the best seed has been selected, the planter measures the corn,

[018-005]lays down a layer of hay, then a layer of corn. Over this corn they

[018-006]sprinkle warm water and cover it with another layer of hay, then bind

[018-007]hay about the bundle and hang it up in a spot where the warm rays of the

[018-008]sun can strike it.

[018-009]While the corn is hanging in the sun, the ground is being prepared to

[018-010]receive it. Having finished the task of preparing the ground, the woman

[018-011]takes down her seed corn which has by this time sprouted. Then she

[018-012]proceeds to plant the corn.

[018-013]Before she plants the first hill, she extends her hoe heavenwards and

[018-014]asks the Great Spirit to bless her work, that she may have a good yield.

[018-015]After her prayer she takes four kernels and plants one at the north,

[018-016]one at the south, one at the east and one at the west sides of the first

[018-017]hill. This is asking the Great Spirit to give summer rain and sunshine

[018-018]to bring forth a good crop.

[018-019]For different growths of the corn, the women have an interpretation as

[018-020]to the character of the one who planted it.

[018-021]1st. Where the corn grows in straight rows and the cob is full of

[018-022]kernels to the end, this signifies that the planter of this corn is of

[018-023]an exemplary character, and is very truthful and thoughtful.

[018-024]2nd. If the rows on the ears of corn are irregular and broken, the

[018-025]planter is considered careless and unthoughtful. Also disorderly and

[018-026]slovenly about her house and person.

[018-027]3rd. When an ear of corn bears a few scattering kernels with spaces

[018-028]producing no corn, it is said that is a good sign that the planter will

[018-029]live to a ripe old age. So old will they be that like the corn, their

[018-030]teeth will be few and far between.

[018-031]4th. When a stalk bears a great many nubbins, or small ears growing

[018-032]around the large one, it is a sign that the planter is from a large and

[018-033]respectable family.

[018-034]After the corn is gathered, it is boiled into sweet corn and made into

[018-035]hominy; parched and mixed with buffalo tallow and rolled into round

[018-036]balls, and used at feasts, or carried by the warriors on the warpath as

[018-037]food.

[018-038]When there has been a good crop of corn, an ear is always tied at the

[018-039]top of the medicine pole, of the sun dance, in thanks to the Great

[018-040]Spirit for his goodness to them in sending a bountiful crop.
