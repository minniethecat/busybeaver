## Overview

**Stealing** in Sioux folklore operates across a moral spectrum. At one end is horse stealing — "Mr. Crow was always planning a horse stealing trip" (031-029) — a sanctioned warrior activity that demonstrates courage and cunning. At the other end is the stealing of a person — "I will steal her and make her my wife" (013-006) — a transgressive act that disrupts social order. Between these poles lie ambiguous thefts: "did you steal it?" (015-043) — the question itself revealing that the morality of stealing depends on what is stolen and from whom.

## In the Stories

### Horse Stealing as Warrior Enterprise

Horse stealing was a recognized warrior activity. "Mr. Crow was always planning a horse stealing trip" (031-029) — the phrase "always planning" suggests regularity, not anomaly. Warriors would "plan for a horse stealing trip" (031-029), and success brought honor: "stealing some of their horses" (033-112) from enemy tribes demonstrated the stealth and courage valued in Sioux martial culture.

### Stealing a Bride

The most transgressive theft is of a person: "I will steal her and make her my wife" (013-006). Bride stealing violates both family authority and individual consent — it is acquisition by force rather than by gift or agreement. The threat of stealing a woman appears alongside the threat of suicide: "feared to use force lest she kill herself" (015-011), showing that bride stealing was coercive enough to provoke desperate resistance.

### The Question of Theft

When property goes missing, the question "did you steal it?" (015-043) is asked. The accused responds: "how could I force it from her or steal it when she" (015-045) was unwilling. The response distinguishes between taking from a willing giver and stealing from an unwilling owner — the moral line is consent, not the act of taking itself.

### Stealth as Skill

The word "stealthily" appears in a warrior context: "he stealthily approached the camp" (024-016). While not theft, the same skill set — silence, darkness, patience — serves both the horse thief and the scout. Stealing is thus connected to the broader warrior skill of moving unseen.

## Cultural Significance

- **Horse stealing as honorable acquisition**: Stealing horses from enemy tribes was a recognized path to wealth and status — the risk legitimized the taking
- **Bride stealing as transgression**: Taking a woman without consent violated fundamental social norms, distinguished from marriage by gift or negotiation
- **The consent boundary**: The moral distinction between taking and stealing rested on the owner's consent (015-045)
- **Stealth as warrior virtue**: The skills of the thief — silence, invisibility, patience — were also the skills of the successful warrior
