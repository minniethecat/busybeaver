## Overview

The **Grouse Girls** (or Grouse Maidens) are a flock of anthropomorphized prairie grouse who appear in the Sioux tale "The Rabbit and the Grouse Girls." Dressed in grey and speckled blankets, they are playful and innocent — coasting down a snowy hill on a board for sport. Their trust in the [[rabbit]]'s smooth words leads them into danger, but their quick thinking and the compassion of the Rabbit's grandmother secure their escape.

## In the Stories

### The Rabbit and the Grouse Girls (006-001)

One winter day, the Rabbit comes upon the grouse girls on the prairie:

> "On the side of a hill away from the wind he found a great company of girls all with grey and speckled blankets over their backs. They were the grouse girls and they were coasting down hill on a board." (006-003–006-004)

The Rabbit, ever the trickster, criticizes their simple sled and offers to fetch them something finer — a skin bag with red stripes and tinkling bangles. The grouse girls are wary at first:

> "'Come and get inside,' he said to the grouse girls. 'Oh, no, we are afraid,' they answered. 'Don't be afraid, I can't hurt you. Come, one of you,' said the rabbit. Then as each hung back he added coaxingly: 'If each is afraid alone, come all together. I can't hurt you _all_.'" (006-009–006-012)

The Rabbit's cunning persuasion overcomes their caution, and he tricks the entire flock into the bag. He slings it over his back and brings it to his grandmother, asking her to watch it while he fetches willow sticks for spits.

#### Escape and Triumph

Once the Rabbit departs, the grouse girls cry out to the grandmother:

> "'Grandmother, let us out.' 'Who are you?' asked the old woman. 'Your dear grandchildren,' they answered." (006-017–006-020)

The grandmother is moved by their plea and unties the bag. The entire flock bursts forth — achuck-a-chuck-achuck — knocking over the old woman and flying out through the smoke hole of the lodge:

> "The old woman caught only one grouse as it flew up and held it, grasping a leg with each hand. When the rabbit came home with the spits she called out to him: 'Grandson, come quick. They got out but I have caught two.' When he saw what had happened he was quite angry, yet could not keep from laughing. 'Grandmother, you have but one grouse,' he cried, 'and it is a very skinny one at that.'" (006-029–006-035)

## Character and Role

The grouse girls embody innocence and collective strength. Though individually vulnerable and easily deceived, together they overwhelm the Grandmother and escape. Their story serves as a caution about the danger of trusting a sweet-talking stranger — and a demonstration that even the Rabbit's clever schemes can be outdone by the resourcefulness of those he preys upon.
