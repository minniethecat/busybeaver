[035-001]There once lived a young couple who were very happy. The young man was

[035-002]noted throughout the whole nation for his accuracy with the bow and

[035-003]arrow, and was given the title of “Dead Shot,” or “He who never

[035-004]misses his mark,” and the young woman, noted for her beauty, was named

[035-005]Beautiful Dove.

[035-006]One day a stork paid this happy couple a visit and left them a fine big

[035-007]boy. The boy cried “Ina, ina” (mother, mother). “Listen to our son,”

[035-008]said the mother, “he can speak, and hasn’t he a sweet voice?” “Yes,”

[035-009]said the father, “it will not be long before he will be able to walk.”

[035-010]He set to work making some arrows, and a fine hickory bow for his son.

[035-011]One of the arrows he painted red, one blue, and another yellow. The rest

[035-012]he left the natural color of the wood. When he had completed them, the

[035-013]mother placed them in a fine quiver, all worked in porcupine quills,

[035-014]and hung them up over where the boy slept in his fine hammock of painted

[035-015]moose hide.

[035-016]At times when the mother would be nursing her son, she would look up at

[035-017]the bow and arrows and talk to her baby, saying: “My son, hurry up and

[035-018]grow fast so you can use your bow and arrows. You will grow up to be

[035-019]as fine a marksman as your father.” The baby would coo and stretch his

[035-020]little arms up towards the bright colored quiver as though he understood

[035-021]every word his mother had uttered. Time passed and the boy grew up to a

[035-022]good size, when one day his father said: “Wife, give our son the bow and

[035-023]arrows so that he may learn how to use them.” The father taught his son

[035-024]how to string and unstring the bow, and also how to attach the arrow to

[035-025]the string. The red, blue and yellow arrows, he told the boy, were to be

[035-026]used only whenever there was any extra good shooting to be done, so the

[035-027]boy never used these three until he became a master of the art. Then he

[035-028]would practice on eagles and hawks, and never an eagle or hawk continued

[035-029]his flight when the boy shot one of the arrows after him.

[035-030]One day the boy came running into the tent, exclaiming: “Mother, mother,

[035-031]I have shot and killed the most beautiful bird I ever saw.” “Bring

[035-032]it in, my son, and let me look at it.” He brought the bird and upon

[035-033]examining it she pronounced it a different type of bird from any she had

[035-034]ever seen. Its feathers were of variegated colors and on its head was

[035-035]a topknot of pure white feathers. The father, returning, asked the boy

[035-036]with which arrow he had killed the bird. “With the red one,” answered

[035-037]the boy. “I was so anxious to secure the pretty bird that, although I

[035-038]know I could have killed it with one of my common arrows, I wanted to

[035-039]be certain, so I used the red one.” “That is right, my son,” said the

[035-040]father. “When you have the least doubt of your aim, always use one of

[035-041]the painted arrows, and you will never miss your mark.”

[035-042]The parents decided to give a big feast in honor of their son killing

[035-043]the strange, beautiful bird. So a great many elderly women were called

[035-044]to the tent of Pretty Dove to assist her in making ready for the big

[035-045]feast. For ten days these women cooked and pounded beef and cherries,

[035-046]and got ready the choicest dishes known to the Indians. Of buffalo,

[035-047]beaver, deer, antelope, moose, bear, quail, grouse, duck of all kinds,

[035-048]geese and plover meats there was an abundance. Fish of all kinds, and

[035-049]every kind of wild fruit were cooked, and when all was in readiness, the

[035-050]heralds went through the different villages, crying out: “Ho-po, ho-po”

[035-051](now all, now all), “Dead Shot and his wife, Beautiful Dove, invite all

[035-052]of you, young and old, to their tepee to partake of a great feast, given

[035-053]by them in honor of a great bird which their son has killed, and also to

[035-054]select for their son some good name which he will bear through life. So

[035-055]all bring your cups and wooden dishes along with your horn spoons, as

[035-056]there will be plenty to eat. Come, all you council men and chiefs,

[035-057]as they have also a great tent erected for you in which you hold your

[035-058]council.”

[035-059]Thus crying, the heralds made the circle of the village. The guests soon

[035-060]arrived. In front of the tent was a pole stuck in the ground and painted

[035-061]red, and at the top of the pole was fastened the bird of variegated

[035-062]colors; its wings stretched out to their full length and the beautiful

[035-063]white waving so beautifully from its topknot, it was the center of

[035-064]attraction. Half way up the pole was tied the bow and arrow of the young

[035-065]marksman. Long streamers of fine bead and porcupine work waved from

[035-066]the pole and presented a very striking appearance. The bird was faced

[035-067]towards the setting sun. The great chief and medicine men pronounced the

[035-068]bird “Wakan” (something holy).

[035-069]When the people had finished eating they all fell in line and marched in

[035-070]single file beneath the bird, in order to get a close view of it. By the

[035-071]time this vast crowd had fully viewed the wonderful bird, the sun was

[035-072]just setting clear in the west, when directly over the rays of the

[035-073]sun appeared a cloud in the shape of a bird of variegated colors. The

[035-074]councilmen were called out to look at the cloud, and the head medicine

[035-075]man said that it was a sign that the boy would grow up to be a great

[035-076]chief and hunter, and would have a great many friends and followers.

[035-077]This ended the feast, but before dispersing, the chief and councilmen

[035-078]bestowed upon the boy the title of White Plume.

[035-079]One day a stranger came to the village, who was very thin and nearly

[035-080]starved. So weak was he that he could not speak, but made signs for

[035-081]something to eat. Luckily the stranger came to Dead Shot’s tent, and as

[035-082]there was always a plentiful supply in his lodge, the stranger soon had

[035-083]a good meal served him. After he had eaten and rested he told his story.

[035-084]“I came from a very great distance,” said he. “The nations where I came

[035-085]from are in a starving condition. No place can they find any buffalo,

[035-086]deer nor antelope. A witch or evil spirit in the shape of a white

[035-087]buffalo has driven all the large game out of the country. Every day this

[035-088]white buffalo comes circling the village, and any one caught outside of

[035-089]their tent is carried away on its horns. In vain have the best marksmen

[035-090]of the tribe tried to shoot it. Their arrows fly wide off the mark, and

[035-091]they have given up trying to kill it as it bears a charmed life. Another

[035-092]evil spirit in the form of a red eagle has driven all the birds of the

[035-093]air out of our country. Every day this eagle circles above the village,

[035-094]and so powerful is it that anyone being caught outside of his tent

[035-095]is descended upon and his skull split open to the brain by the sharp

[035-096]breastbone of the Eagle. Many a marksman has tried his skill on this

[035-097]bird, all to no purpose.

[035-098]“Another evil spirit in the form of a white rabbit has driven out all

[035-099]the animals which inhabit the ground, and destroyed the fields of corn

[035-100]and turnips, so the nation is starving, as the arrows of the marksmen

[035-101]have also failed to touch the white rabbit. Any one who can kill these

[035-102]three witches will receive as his reward, the choice of two of the most

[035-103]beautiful maidens of our nation. The younger one is the handsomer of the

[035-104]two and has also the sweetest disposition. Many young, and even old men,

[035-105]hearing of this (our chief’s) offer, have traveled many miles to try

[035-106]their arrows on the witches, but all to no purpose. Our chief, hearing

[035-107]of your great marksmanship, sent me to try and secure your services to

[035-108]have you come and rid us of these three witches.”

[035-109]Thus spoke the stranger to the hunter. The hunter gazed long and

[035-110]thoughtfully into the dying embers of the camp fire. Then slowly his

[035-111]eyes raised and looked lovingly on his wife who sat opposite to him.

[035-112]Gazing on her beautiful features for a full minute he slowly dropped his

[035-113]gaze back to the dying embers and thus answered his visitor:

[035-114]“My friend, I feel very much honored by your chief having sent such

[035-115]a great distance for me, and also for the kind offer of his lovely

[035-116]daughter in marriage, if I should succeed, but I must reject the great

[035-117]offer, as I can spare none of my affections to any other woman than to

[035-118]my queen whom you see sitting there.”

[035-119]White Plume had been listening to the conversation and when his father

[035-120]had finished speaking, said: “Father, I am a child no more. I have

[035-121]arrived at manhood. I am not so good a marksman as you, but I will go to

[035-122]this suffering tribe and try to rid them of their three enemies. If this

[035-123]man will rest for a few days and return to his village and inform them

[035-124]of my coming, I will travel along slowly on his trail and arrive at the

[035-125]village a day or two after he reaches there.”

[035-126]“Very well, my son,” said the father, “I am sure you will succeed, as

[035-127]you fear nothing, and as to your marksmanship, it is far superior to

[035-128]mine, as your sight is much clearer and aim quicker than mine.”

[035-129]The man rested a few days and one morning started off, after having

[035-130]instructed White Plume as to the trail. White Plume got together what

[035-131]he would need on the trip and was ready for an early start the next

[035-132]morning. That night Dead Shot and his wife sat up away into the night

[035-133]instructing their son how to travel and warning him as to the different

[035-134]kinds of people he must avoid in order to keep out of trouble. “Above

[035-135]all,” said the father, “keep a good look out for Unktomi (spider); he is

[035-136]the most tricky of all, and will get you into trouble if you associate

[035-137]with him.”

[035-138]White Plume left early, his father accompanying him for several miles.

[035-139]On parting, the father’s last words were: “Look out for Unktomi, my son,

[035-140]he is deceitful and treacherous.” “I’ll look out for him, father;” so

[035-141]saying he disappeared over a hill. On the way he tried his skill on

[035-142]several hawks and eagles and he did not need to use his painted arrows

[035-143]to kill them, but so skillful was he with the bow and arrows that he

[035-144]could bring down anything that flew with his common arrows. He was

[035-145]drawing near to the end of his destination when he had a large tract of

[035-146]timber to pass through. When he had nearly gotten through the timber he

[035-147]saw an old man sitting on a log, looking wistfully up into a big tree,

[035-148]where sat a number of prairie chickens.

[035-149]“Hello, grandfather, why are you sitting there looking so downhearted?”

[035-150]asked White Plume. “I am nearly starved, and was just wishing some one

[035-151]would shoot one of those chickens for me, so I could make a good meal on

[035-152]it,” said the old man. “I will shoot one for you,” said the young man.

[035-153]He strung his bow, placed an arrow on the string, simply seemed to raise

[035-154]the arrow in the direction of the chicken (taking no aim). Twang went

[035-155]out the bow, zip went the arrow and a chicken fell off the limb, only

[035-156]to get caught on another in its descent. “There is your chicken,

[035-157]grandfather.” “Oh, my grandson, I am too weak to climb up and get it.

[035-158]Can’t you climb up and get it for me?” The young man, pitying the old

[035-159]fellow, proceeded to climb the tree, when the old man stopped him,

[035-160]saying: “Grandson, you have on such fine clothes, it is a pity to spoil

[035-161]them; you had better take them off so as not to spoil the fine porcupine

[035-162]work on them.” The young man took off his fine clothes and climbed up

[035-163]into the tree, and securing the chicken, threw it down to the old

[035-164]man. As the young man was scaling down the tree, the old man said:

[035-165]“Iyashkapa, iyashkapa,” (stick fast, stick fast). Hearing him say

[035-166]something, he asked, “What did you say, old man?” He answered, “I was

[035-167]only talking to myself.” The young man proceeded to descend, but he

[035-168]could not move. His body was stuck fast to the bark of the tree. In vain

[035-169]did he beg the old man to release him. The old Unktomi, for he it was,

[035-170]only laughed and said: “I will go now and kill the evil spirits, I have

[035-171]your wonderful bow and arrows and I cannot miss them. I will marry the

[035-172]chief’s daughter, and you can stay up in that tree and die there.”

[035-173]So saying, he put on White Plume’s fine clothes, took his bow and arrows

[035-174]and went to the village. As White Plume was expected at any minute, the

[035-175]whole village was watching for him, and when Unktomi came into sight the

[035-176]young men ran to him with a painted robe, sat him down on it and slowly

[035-177]raising him up they carried him to the tent of the chief. So certain

[035-178]were they that he would kill the evil spirits that the chief told him to

[035-179]choose one of the daughters at once for his wife. (Before the arrival

[035-180]of White Plume, hearing of him being so handsome, the two girls had

[035-181]quarreled over which should marry him, but upon seeing him the younger

[035-182]was not anxious to become his wife.) So Unktomi chose the older one of

[035-183]the sisters, and was given a large tent in which to live. The younger

[035-184]sister went to her mother’s tent to live, and the older was very proud,

[035-185]as she was married to the man who would save the nation from starvation.

[035-186]The next morning there was a great commotion in camp, and there came the

[035-187]cry that the white buffalo was coming. “Get ready, son-in-law, and kill

[035-188]the buffalo,” said the chief.

[035-189]Unktomi took the bow and arrows and shot as the buffalo passed, but the

[035-190]arrow went wide off its mark. Next came the eagle, and again he shot and

[035-191]missed. Then came the rabbit, and again he missed.

[035-192]“Wait until tomorrow, I will kill them all. My blanket caught in my bow

[035-193]and spoiled my aim.” The people were very much disappointed, and the

[035-194]chief, suspecting that all was not right, sent for the young man who had

[035-195]visited Dead Shot’s tepee. When the young man arrived, the chief asked:

[035-196]“Did you see White Plume when you went to Dead Shot’s camp?” “Yes, I

[035-197]did, and ate with him many times. I stayed at his father’s tepee all the

[035-198]time I was there,” said the young man. “Would you recognize him if you

[035-199]saw him again?” asked the chief. “Any one who had but one glimpse of

[035-200]White Plume would surely recognize him when he saw him again, as he is

[035-201]the most handsome man I ever saw,” said the young man.

[035-202]“Come with me to the tent of my son-in-law and take a good look at him,

[035-203]but don’t say what you think until we come away.” The two went to the

[035-204]tent of Unktomi, and when the young man saw him he knew it was not White

[035-205]Plume, although it was White Plume’s bow and arrows that hung at the

[035-206]head of the bed, and he also recognized the clothes as belonging to

[035-207]White Plume. When they had returned to the chief’s tent, the young man

[035-208]told what he knew and what he thought. “I think this is some Unktomi who

[035-209]has played some trick on White Plume and has taken his bow and arrows

[035-210]and also his clothes, and hearing of your offer, is here impersonating

[035-211]White Plume. Had White Plume drawn the bow on the buffalo, eagle and

[035-212]rabbit today, we would have been rid of them, so I think we had better

[035-213]scare this Unktomi into telling us where White Plume is,” said the young

[035-214]man.

[035-215]“Wait until he tries to kill the witches again tomorrow,” said the

[035-216]chief.

[035-217]In the meantime the younger daughter had taken an axe and gone into the

[035-218]woods in search of dry wood. She went quite a little distance into the

[035-219]wood and was chopping a dry log. Stopping to rest a little she heard

[035-220]some one saying: “Whoever you are, come over here and chop this tree

[035-221]down so that I may get loose.” Going to where the big tree stood, she

[035-222]saw a man stuck onto the side of the tree. “If I chop it down the fall

[035-223]will kill you,” said the girl. “No, chop it on the opposite side from

[035-224]me, and the tree will fall that way. If the fall kills me, it will be

[035-225]better than hanging up here and starving to death,” said White Plume,

[035-226]for it was he.

[035-227]The girl chopped the tree down and when she saw that it had not killed

[035-228]the man, she said: “What shall I do now?” “Loosen the bark from the tree

[035-229]and then get some stones and heat them. Get some water and sage and put

[035-230]your blanket over me.” She did as told and when the steam arose from

[035-231]the water being poured upon the heated rocks, the bark loosened from his

[035-232]body and he arose. When he stood up, she saw how handsome he was. “You

[035-233]have saved my life,” said he. “Will you be my wife?” “I will,” said she.

[035-234]He then told her how the old man had fooled him into this trap and took

[035-235]his bow and arrows, also his fine porcupine worked clothes, and had gone

[035-236]off, leaving him to die. She, in turn, told him all that had happened

[035-237]in camp since a man, calling himself White Plume, came there and married

[035-238]her sister before he shot at the witches, and when he came to shoot at

[035-239]them, missed every shot. “Let us make haste, as the bad Unktomi may

[035-240]ruin my arrows.” They approached the camp and whilst White Plume waited

[035-241]outside, his promised wife entered Unktomi’s tent and said: “Unktomi,

[035-242]White Plume is standing outside and he wants his clothes and bow and

[035-243]arrows.” “Oh, yes, I borrowed them and forgot to return them; make haste

[035-244]and give them to him.”

[035-245]Upon receiving his clothes, he was very much provoked to find his fine

[035-246]clothes wrinkled and his bow twisted, while the arrows were twisted

[035-247]out of shape. He laid the clothes down, also the bows and arrows, and

[035-248]passing his hand over them, they assumed their right shapes again. The

[035-249]daughter took White Plume to her father’s tent and upon hearing the

[035-250]story he at once sent for his warriors and had them form a circle around

[035-251]Unktomi’s tent, and if he attempted to escape to catch him and tie him

[035-252]to a tree, as he (the chief) had determined to settle accounts with him

[035-253]for his treatment of White Plume, and the deception employed in winning

[035-254]the chief’s eldest daughter. About midnight the guard noticed something

[035-255]crawling along close to the ground, and seizing him found it was Unktomi

[035-256]trying to make his escape before daylight, whereupon they tied him to a

[035-257]tree. “Why do you treat me thus,” cried Unktomi, “I was just going out

[035-258]in search of medicine to rub on my arrows, so I can kill the witches.”

[035-259]“You will need medicine to rub on yourself when the chief gets through

[035-260]with you,” said the young man who had discovered that Unktomi was

[035-261]impersonating White Plume.

[035-262]In the morning the herald announced that the real White Plume had

[035-263]arrived, and the chief desired the whole nation to witness his

[035-264]marksmanship. Then came the cry: “The White Buffalo comes.” Taking his

[035-265]red arrow, White Plume stood ready. When the buffalo got about opposite

[035-266]him, he let his arrow fly. The buffalo bounded high in the air and came

[035-267]down with all four feet drawn together under its body, the red arrow

[035-268]having passed clear through the animal, piercing the buffalo’s heart. A

[035-269]loud cheer went up from the village.

[035-270]“You shall use the hide for your bed,” said the chief to White Plume.

[035-271]Next came a cry, “the eagle, the eagle.” From the north came an enormous

[035-272]red eagle. So strong was he, that as he soared through the air his wings

[035-273]made a humming sound as the rumble of distant thunder. On he came, and

[035-274]just as he circled the tent of the chief, White Plume bent his bow, with

[035-275]all his strength drew the arrow back to the flint point, and sent the

[035-276]blue arrow on its mission of death. So swiftly had the arrow passed

[035-277]through the eagle’s body that, thinking White Plume had missed, a great

[035-278]wail went up from the crowd, but when they saw the eagle stop in his

[035-279]flight, give a few flaps of his wings, and then fall with a heavy thud

[035-280]into the center of the village, there was a greater cheer than before.

[035-281]“The red eagle shall be used to decorate the seat of honor in your

[035-282]tepee,” said the chief to White Plume. Last came the white rabbit. “Aim

[035-283]good, aim good, son-in-law,” said the chief. “If you kill him you will

[035-284]have his skin for a rug.” Along came the white rabbit, and White Plume

[035-285]sent his arrow in search of rabbit’s heart, which it found, and stopped

[035-286]Mr. Rabbit’s tricks forever.

[035-287]The chief then called all of the people together and before them all

[035-288]took a hundred willows and broke them one at a time over Unktomi’s back.

[035-289]Then he turned him loose. Unktomi, being so ashamed, ran off into the

[035-290]woods and hid in the deepest and darkest corner he could find. This is

[035-291]why Unktomis (spiders) are always found in dark corners, and anyone who

[035-292]is deceitful or untruthful is called a descendant of the Unktomi tribe.
