## Overview

**Crawfish** appears in Sioux animal tales as a cautious but curious figure. In "The Raccoon and the Crawfish," he wanders the river bank looking for carrion to feast upon — only to discover that what appears dead might be very much alive and hungry. His story illustrates the value of skepticism and careful inspection.

## In the Stories

### The Raccoon and the Crawfish (Chapter 011)

The Crawfish wanders along a river bank one evening, searching for something dead to feast upon (011-003). A raccoon, also hunting, spots him and forms a plan (011-005). The raccoon lies down on the bank and feigns death (011-006):

> "Ho," thought the Crawfish, "here is a feast indeed; but is he really dead? I will go near and pinch him with my claws and find out." (011-001–011-012)

The Crawfish's cautious nature serves him well. Approaching the apparently dead raccoon, he tests the body with his claws (011-010). When the raccoon flinches, the Crawfish realizes the deception and escapes:

> The Crawfish, warned by the first pinch, scuttled backward into the water, leaving the raccoon with nothing but a sore nose and an empty stomach. (011-013–011-025)

## Character Traits

Crawfish represents **caution rewarded** — the small creature who survives by testing appearances before committing. Unlike the [[elk]], who falls prey to his own pride, Crawfish's wary nature saves him. He embodies the survival wisdom of the small and vulnerable: trust nothing at face value.
