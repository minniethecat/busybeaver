[031-001]Once upon a time there came to a large village a plague of crows. So

[031-002]thick were they that the poor women were sorely tried keeping them out

[031-003]of their tepees and driving them away from their lines of jerked buffalo

[031-004]meat. Indeed they got so numerous and were such a great nuisance that

[031-005]the Chief finally gave orders to his camp criers or heralds to go out

[031-006]among the different camps and announce the orders of their Chief, that

[031-007]war should be made upon the crows to extermination; that their nests

[031-008]were to be destroyed and all eggs broken. The war of extermination was

[031-009]to continue until not a crow remained, except the youngest found was to

[031-010]be brought to him alive.

[031-011]For a week the war on the crows continued. Thousands of dead crows were

[031-012]brought in daily, and at the end of the week not a bird of that species

[031-013]could be seen in the neighborhood. Those that escaped the deadly arrow

[031-014]of the warriors, flew away, never to return to those parts again.

[031-015]At the end of the war made upon the crows, there was brought to the

[031-016]Chief’s tepee the youngest found. Indeed, so young was the bird that it

[031-017]was only the great medicine of the Chief that kept him alive until he

[031-018]could hop about and find his own food. The Chief spent most of his time

[031-019]in his lodge teaching the young crow to understand and talk the language

[031-020]of the tribe. After the crow had mastered this, the Chief then taught

[031-021]him the languages of the neighboring tribes. When the crow had mastered

[031-022]these different languages the chief would send him on long journeys to

[031-023]ascertain the location of the camps of the different enemies.

[031-024]When the crow would find a large Indian camp he would alight and hop

[031-025]about, pretending to be picking up scraps, but really keeping his ears

[031-026]open for anything he might hear. He would hang around all day, and at

[031-027]night when they would all gather in the large council tent (which always

[031-028]stood in the center of the village) to determine upon their next raid,

[031-029]and plan for a horse stealing trip, Mr. Crow was always nearby to hear

[031-030]all their plans discussed. He would then fly away to his master (the

[031-031]Chief) and tell him all that he had learned.

[031-032]The Chief would then send a band of his warriors to lie in ambush for

[031-033]the raiding party, and, as the enemy would not suspect anything they

[031-034]would go blindly into the pitfall of death thus set for them. Thus the

[031-035]crow was the scout of this chief, whose reputation as a Wakan (Holy man)

[031-036]soon reached all of the different tribes. The Chief’s warriors would

[031-037]intercept, ambush and annihilate every war party headed for his camp.

[031-038]So, finally learning that they could not make war on this chief’s people

[031-039]unbeknown to them, they gave up making war on this particular band. When

[031-040]meat was running low in the camp this chief would send the crow out to

[031-041]look for buffalo. When he discovered a herd he would return and report

[031-042]to his master; then the chief would order out the hunters and they

[031-043]would return laden with meat. Thus the crow kept the camp all the time

[031-044]informed of everything that would be of benefit to them.

[031-045]One day the crow disappeared, over which there was great grief among the

[031-046]tribe. A week had passed away, when Mr. Crow reappeared. There was

[031-047]great rejoicing upon his return, but the crow was downcast and would not

[031-048]speak, but sat with a drooping head perched at the top of the chief’s

[031-049]tepee, and refused all food that was offered to him.

[031-050]In vain did the chief try to get the crow to tell him the cause of his

[031-051]silence and seeming grief. The crow would not speak until the chief

[031-052]said: “Well, I will take a few of my warriors and go out and try to

[031-053]ascertain what has happened to cause you to act as you do.”

[031-054]Upon hearing this, the crow said: “Don’t go. I dreaded to tell you what

[031-055]I know to be a fact, as I have heard it from some great medicine men.

[031-056]I was traveling over the mountains west of here, when I spied three old

[031-057]men sitting at the top of the highest peak. I very cautiously dropped

[031-058]down behind a rock and listened to their talk. I heard your name

[031-059]mentioned by one of them, then your brother’s name was mentioned. Then

[031-060]the third, who was the oldest, said: ‘in three days from today the

[031-061]lightning will kill those two brothers whom all the nations fear.’”

[031-062]Upon hearing what the crow stated the tribe became grief stricken. On

[031-063]the morning of the third day the chief ordered a nice tepee placed upon

[031-064]the highest point, far enough away from the village, so that the peals

[031-065]of thunder would not alarm the babies of the camp.

[031-066]A great feast was given, and after the feasting was over there came in

[031-067]six young maidens leading the war horses of the two brothers. The horses

[031-068]were painted and decorated as if for a charge on the enemy. One maiden

[031-069]walked ahead of the chief’s horse bearing in her hands the bow and

[031-070]arrows of the great warrior. Next came two maidens, one on either side

[031-071]of the prancing war steed, each holding a rein. Behind the chief’s horse

[031-072]came the fourth maiden. Like the first, she bore in her hands the bow

[031-073]and arrows of the chief’s brother. Then the fifth and sixth maidens

[031-074]each holding a rein, walked on either side of the prancing horse of

[031-075]the chief’s brother. They advanced and circled the large gathering and

[031-076]finally stopped directly in front of the two brothers, who immediately

[031-077]arose and taking their bows and arrows vaulted lightly upon their war

[031-078]steeds, and singing their death song, galloped off amid a great cry of

[031-079]grief from the people who loved them most dearly.

[031-080]Heading straight for the tepee that had been placed upon the highest

[031-081]point, adjacent to the village, they soon arrived at their destination

[031-082]and, dismounting from their horses, turned, waved their hands to their

[031-083]band, and disappeared within the tepee. Scarcely had they entered the

[031-084]lodge when the rumblings of distant thunder could be heard. Nearer, and

[031-085]nearer, came the sound, until at last the storm overspread the locality

[031-086]in all its fury. Flash upon flash of lightning burst forth from the

[031-087]heavens. Deafening peals of thunder followed each flash. Finally, one

[031-088]flash brighter than any of the others, one peal more deafening than

[031-089]those preceding it, and the storm had passed.

[031-090]Sadly the warriors gathered together, mounted their horses and slowly

[031-091]rode to the tepee on the high point. Arriving there they looked inside

[031-092]the lodge and saw the two brothers lying cold and still in death, each

[031-093]holding the lariat of his favorite war horse. The horses also lay dead

[031-094]side by side in front of the tent. (From this came the custom of killing

[031-095]the favorite horse of a dead warrior at the burial of the owner).

[031-096]As the Indians sadly left the hill to return home, they heard a noise at

[031-097]the top of the tepee, and looking up they saw the crow sitting on one

[031-098]of the splintered tepee poles. He was crying most pitifully, and as they

[031-099]rode off he flew up high in the air and his pitiful “caw” became fainter

[031-100]and fainter till at last they heard it no more. And from that day, the

[031-101]story goes, no crow ever goes near the village of that band of Indians.
