[036-001]There was once a baby boy who came into the world with a small cluster

[036-002]of different colored feathers grown fast to his forehead. From this he

[036-003]derived his name, “Pretty Feathered Forehead.” He was a very pleasant

[036-004]boy as well as handsome, and he had the respect of the whole tribe. When

[036-005]he had grown up to be a young man, he never, like other young men, made

[036-006]love to any of the tribe’s beauties. Although they were madly in love

[036-007]with him, he never noticed any of them. There were many handsome girls

[036-008]in the different camps, but he passed them by.

[036-009]One day he said: “Father, I am going on a visit to the Buffalo nation.”

[036-010]The father gave his consent, and away went the son. The father and

[036-011]mother suspected the object of their son’s visit to the Buffalo nation,

[036-012]and forthwith commenced preparing a fine reception for their intended

[036-013]daughter-in-law. The mother sewed together ten buffalo hides and painted

[036-014]the brave deeds of her husband on them. This she made into a commodious

[036-015]tent, and had work bags and fine robes and blankets put inside. This was

[036-016]to be the tent of their son and daughter-in-law. In a few weeks the son

[036-017]returned, bringing with him a beautiful Buffalo girl. The parents of the

[036-018]boy gave a big feast in honor of the occasion, and the son and his wife

[036-019]lived very happily together.

[036-020]In the course of time a son came to the young couple, and the father was

[036-021]very proud of his boy. When the boy became a year old, the father said

[036-022]to his wife: “I am going for a visit to the Elk nation.” The mother

[036-023]was very sad, as she knew her husband was going after another wife. He

[036-024]returned, bringing with him a very beautiful elk girl. When the Buffalo

[036-025]woman saw the elk girl she was very downcast and sad, but the husband

[036-026]said: “Don’t be sad; she will do all the heavy work for you.”

[036-027]They lived quite happily together for a long time. The Elk girl also

[036-028]became the mother of a fine boy. The two boys had grown up large enough

[036-029]to play around. One day the Elk woman was tanning hides outside and the

[036-030]two boys were playing around near their mothers, when all at once the

[036-031]buffalo boy ran across the robe, leaving his tracks on the white robe

[036-032]which his step-mother had nearly completed. This provoked the elk woman

[036-033]and she gave vent to her feelings by scolding the boy: “You clumsy flat

[036-034]mouth, why couldn’t you run around my work, instead of across it?” The

[036-035]buffalo cow standing in the door, heard every word that the elk woman

[036-036]had said, and when she heard her son called flat mouth it made her

[036-037]very angry, although she did not say a word to any one. She hurriedly

[036-038]gathered some of her belongings and, calling her son, she started off in

[036-039]a westerly direction.

[036-040]The husband being absent on a hunting expedition did not return until

[036-041]late in the afternoon. Upon his return his oldest boy always ran out

[036-042]to meet him, but this time as the boy did not put in an appearance, the

[036-043]father feared that something had happened to the boy. So hurriedly going

[036-044]to his tent he looked around, but failing to see the boy or his mother,

[036-045]he asked his elk wife, where the boy and his mother were. The elk

[036-046]wife answered: “She took her boy on her back and started off in that

[036-047]direction,” (pointing towards the west). “How long has she been gone?”

[036-048]“Since early morning.” The husband hurriedly caught a fresh horse and,

[036-049]without eating anything, rode off in the direction taken by his buffalo

[036-050]wife and boy. Near dark he ascended a high hill and noticed a small tent

[036-051]down in the valley. It was a long distance down to the tent, so it was

[036-052]very late when he arrived there. He tethered his horse and went into

[036-053]the tent and found the boy and his mother fast asleep. Upon lying down

[036-054]beside them the boy awoke, and upon seeing his father, motioned to him

[036-055]to go outside with him.

[036-056]On going outside the boy told his father that it would be useless for

[036-057]him to try and coax his mother to return, as she was too highly insulted

[036-058]by the elk wife to ever return. Then the boy told about what the elk

[036-059]wife had said and that she had called him flat mouth. “My mother is

[036-060]determined to return to her people, but if you want to follow us you

[036-061]may, and perhaps, after she has visited with her relatives a little

[036-062]while, you may induce her to return with you. In the morning we are

[036-063]going to start very early, and as the country we will travel through

[036-064]is very hard soil, I will stamp my feet hard so as to leave my tracks

[036-065]imprinted in the softest places, then you will be able to follow the

[036-066]direction we will take.”

[036-067]The two went into the tent and were soon fast asleep. The father, being

[036-068]very much fatigued, slept very soundly, and when he awoke the sun was

[036-069]beating down upon him. The mother and boy were nowhere to be seen. The

[036-070]tent had been taken down from over him so carefully that he had not been

[036-071]awakened. Getting his horse, he mounted and rode after the two who had

[036-072]left him sleeping. He had no trouble in following the trail, as the boy

[036-073]had stamped his feet hard and left his little tracks in the soft places.

[036-074]That evening he spied the little tent again and on getting to it found

[036-075]them both asleep. The boy awoke and motioned for his father to go

[036-076]outside. He again told his father that the next day’s travel would be

[036-077]the hardest of all. “We will cross a great plain, but before we get

[036-078]there we will cross a sandy hollow. When you get to the hollow, look at

[036-079]my tracks; they will be deep into the sand, and in each track you will

[036-080]see little pools of water. Drink as much as you can, as this is the only

[036-081]chance you will get to have a drink, there being no water from there to

[036-082]the big ridge, and it will be dark by the time you get to the ridge. The

[036-083]relations of my mother live at that ridge and I will come and talk to

[036-084]you once more, before I leave you to join my mother’s people.”

[036-085]Next morning, as before, he awoke to find himself alone. They had left

[036-086]him and proceeded on their journey. He mounted again and when he arrived

[036-087]at the sandy hollow, sure enough, there, deep in the sand, were the

[036-088]tracks of his son filled to the top with water. He drank and drank until

[036-089]he had drained the last one. Then he arose and continued on the trail,

[036-090]and near sundown he came in sight of their little tent away up on the

[036-091]side of the ridge. His horse suddenly staggered and fell forward dead,

[036-092]having died of thirst.

[036-093]From there he proceeded on foot. When he got to where the tent stood he

[036-094]entered, only to find it empty. “I guess my son intends to come here and

[036-095]have his last talk with me,” thought the father. He had eaten nothing

[036-096]for three days, and was nearly famished. He lay down, but the pangs of

[036-097]hunger kept sleep away. He heard footsteps outside and lay in readiness,

[036-098]thinking it might be an enemy. Slowly opening the covering of the door,

[036-099]his son looked in and seeing his father lying awake, drew back and ran

[036-100]off up the ridge, but soon returned bringing a small parcel with him.

[036-101]When he entered he gave the parcel to his father and said: “Eat, father;

[036-102]I stole this food for you, so I could not get very much.” The father

[036-103]soon ate what his son had brought. When he had finished, the son said:

[036-104]“Tomorrow morning the relatives of my mother will come over here and

[036-105]take you down to the village. My mother has three sisters who have their

[036-106]work bags made identically the same as mother’s. Were they to mix them

[036-107]up they could not each pick out her own without looking inside so as to

[036-108]identify them by what they have in them. You will be asked to pick out

[036-109]mother’s work bag, and if you fail they will trample you to death. Next

[036-110]they will tell you to pick out my mother from among her sisters, and you

[036-111]will be unable to distinguish her from the other three, and if you fail

[036-112]they will bury you alive. The last they will try you on, in case you

[036-113]meet the first and second tests successfully, will be to require you

[036-114]to pick me out from my three cousins, who are as much like me as my

[036-115]reflection in the water. The bags you can tell by a little pebble I will

[036-116]place on my mother’s. You can pick my mother out by a small piece of

[036-117]grass which I will put in her hair, and you can pick me out from my

[036-118]cousins, for when we commence to dance, I will shake my head, flop my

[036-119]ears and switch my tail. You must choose quickly, as they will be very

[036-120]angry at your success, and if you lose any time they will make the

[036-121]excuse that you did not know, that they may have an excuse to trample

[036-122]you to death.”

[036-123]The boy then left, after admonishing his father to remember all that

[036-124]he had told him. Early next morning the father heard a great rumbling

[036-125]noise, and going outside, he saw the whole hillside covered with

[036-126]buffalo. When he appeared they set up a loud bellowing and circled

[036-127]around him. One old bull came up and giving a loud snort, passed on by,

[036-128]looking back every few steps. The man, thinking he was to follow this

[036-129]one, did so, and the whole herd, forming a half circle around him,

[036-130]escorted him down the west side of the range out on to a large plain,

[036-131]where there stood a lone tree. To this tree the old bull led him and

[036-132]stopped when he reached the tree. A large rock at the foot of the tree

[036-133]served as a seat for the man. As soon as he was seated there came four

[036-134]female buffaloes, each bearing a large work box. They set the boxes

[036-135]down in a row in front of the man, and the herd crowded around closer in

[036-136]order to get a good view. The old bull came to the front and stood close

[036-137]to the bags, which had been taken out of the four boxes.

[036-138]The man stood up, and looking at the bags, noticed a small pebble

[036-139]resting on the one next to the left end. Stepping over he pulled the bag

[036-140]towards him and secretly pushed the little pebble off the bag, so that

[036-141]no one would notice it. When they saw that he had selected the right

[036-142]one, they set up a terrific bellow.

[036-143]Then came the four sisters and stood in a line before the man. Glancing

[036-144]along from the one on the right to the last one on the left, he stepped

[036-145]forward and placed his hand on the one next to the right. Thanks to his

[036-146]boy, if he hadn’t put that little stem of grass on his mother’s hair,

[036-147]the father could never have picked out his wife, as the four looked

[036-148]as much alike as four peas. Next came the four boy calves, and as they

[036-149]advanced they commenced dancing, and his son was shaking his head and

[036-150]flopping his ears and switching his tail. The father was going to pick

[036-151]out his boy, when a fainting spell took him, and as he sank to the

[036-152]ground the old bull sprang forward on top of him, and instantly they

[036-153]rushed upon him and he was soon trampled to a jelly. The herd then moved

[036-154]to other parts.

[036-155]The elk wife concluded that something had happened to her husband and

[036-156]determined upon going in search of him. As she was very fleet of foot it

[036-157]did not take her long to arrive at the lone tree. She noticed the blood

[036-158]splashed on the base of the tree, and small pieces of flesh stamped

[036-159]into the earth. Looking closer, she noticed something white in the dust.

[036-160]Stooping and picking it out of the dust, she drew forth the cluster

[036-161]of different colored feathers which had been fastened to her husband’s

[036-162]forehead. She at once took the cluster of feathers, and going to the

[036-163]east side of the ridge, heated stones and erected a wickieup, placed the

[036-164]feathers inside, and getting water, she sprinkled the stones, and this

[036-165]caused a thick vapor in the wickieup. She continued this for a long

[036-166]time, when she heard something moving inside the wickieup. Then a voice

[036-167]spoke up, saying: “Whoever you are, pour some more water on and I will

[036-168]be all right.” So the woman got more water and poured it on the rocks.

[036-169]“That will do now, I want to dry off.” She plucked a pile of sage and in

[036-170]handing it in to him, he recognized his elk wife’s hand.

[036-171]They went back home and shortly after the buffalo, hearing about him

[036-172]coming back to life, decided to make war on him and kill him and his

[036-173]wife, she being the one who brought him back to life. The woman, hearing

[036-174]of this, had posts set in the ground and a strong platform placed on

[036-175]top. When the buffalo came, her husband, her son and herself, were

[036-176]seated upon the bough platform, and the buffalo could not reach them.

[036-177]She flouted her red blanket in their faces, which made the buffalo wild

[036-178]with rage. The hunter’s friends came to his rescue, and so fast were

[036-179]they killing the buffalo that they took flight and rushed away, never

[036-180]more to bother Pretty Feather Forehead.
