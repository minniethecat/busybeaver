## Overview

The **Raccoon** (known to the Sioux as **Spotted Face**) is a cunning trickster figure in Sioux folklore who appears in the tale "The Raccoon and the Crawfish." Sharp and clever, he exemplifies the trickster archetype — using deception and wit rather than strength to achieve his ends. Where other characters like [[unktomi]] use intimidation, the raccoon uses patience and play-acting to turn the tables on those who would eat him.

## In the Stories

### The Raccoon and the Crawfish (011-001)

The raccoon's sole major appearance is in the story of his encounter with the [[crawfish]]. One evening, while both are out searching for food along a river bank, the raccoon spies the crawfish and devises a plan:

> "Sharp and cunning is the raccoon, say the Indians, by whom he is named Spotted Face. A crawfish one evening wandered along a river bank, looking for something dead to feast upon. A raccoon was also out looking for something to eat. He spied the crawfish and formed a plan to catch him." (011-001–011-004)

The raccoon feigns death, lying motionless on the bank. The crawfish approaches cautiously, pinching the raccoon's nose, paws, and ribs to test whether he is truly dead. The raccoon withstands the pinching without flinching — though he can barely keep from laughing. Convinced, the crawfish hurries back to report the find.

The crawfish villagers arrive in a grand procession — warriors with weapons, women with children — and form a circle around the "dead" raccoon. They dance and sing of the feast to come:

> "We shall have a great feast / On the spotted-faced beast, with soft smooth paws: / He is dead! / He is dead! / We shall dance! / We shall have a good time! / We shall feast on his flesh." (011-020–011-028)

At the climax of their celebration, the raccoon springs to his feet and turns the tables entirely:

> "But as they danced, the raccoon suddenly sprang to his feet. 'Who is that you say you are going to eat? He has a spotted face, has he? He has soft, smooth paws, has he? I'll break your ugly backs. I'll break your rough bones. I'll crunch your ugly, rough paws.' And he rushed among the crawfish, killing them by scores. The crawfish warriors fought bravely and the women ran screaming, all to no purpose. They did not feast on the raccoon; the raccoon feasted _on them!_" (011-029–011-035)
