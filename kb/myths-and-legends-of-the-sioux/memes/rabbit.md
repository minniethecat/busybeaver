## Overview

**Rabbit** (Lakota: **Mastincala**), also called **Mr. Rabbit** and **Witkotko** ("the mischievous one"), is one of the most frequently appearing characters in Sioux folklore. He features in at least seven tales across the corpus, ranging from childhood adventures with his grandmother to elaborate tricks against giants, [[bear]]s, and wolves. Like many trickster figures in Native American traditions, Rabbit is clever but impulsive — his schemes often succeed, but his recklessness costs him his tail, his youth, and sometimes his dignity.

## In the Stories

### The Rabbit and the Bear with the Flint Body (Chapter 009)

Rabbit lives with his grandmother when they encounter a [[bear]] whose body is made of flint. The story establishes Rabbit's early domestic setting and his relationship with his grandmother, who warns and protects him:

> "The Rabbit and his grandmother were in dire straits, for the Bear with the Flint Body had taken their provisions and threatened their lodge." (009-001–009-005)

### How the Rabbit Lost His Tail (Chapter 020)

This is Rabbit's most elaborate adventure. Having attained full growth, his brother declares him **Witkotko** — mischievous — and Rabbit sets out on a journey. He encounters and cleverly outwits a giant by claiming to be stronger than he appears:

> "Why, you don't think you could make a toothful of me," said Rabbit, "I am as strong as you are, if not stronger." (020-029–020-032)

Rabbit challenges the giant to contests of strength, consistently bluffing his way to victory. When a storm threatens, he tricks the giant into climbing a tree in his place:

> "Rabbit disrobed and climbed up. The giant hurriedly got into Rabbit's clothes, and just as he finished, the wind blew very hard. Rabbit was nearly blown off the tree." (020-063–020-065)

Later, Rabbit encounters an old man and impulsively trades places with him, exchanging his youth for old age and blindness:

> "Let us change places. I think I would like to be old for a change." (020-105)

> "They changed clothes, and Rabbit became old and blind, whilst the old man became young and handsome." (020-114–020-115)

The story culminates when Rabbit encounters a pack of wolves pulling at buffalo meat. Confident in his long tail, he recklessly intertwines it with theirs — and loses it:

> "My tail is long and strong," said Rabbit, and the wolves assenting, Rabbit interlocked his long tail with that of the wolves. They pulled so hard that they pulled Rabbit's tail off at the second joint. (020-160–020-163)

> "Oh, my! I have lost my tail," cried Rabbit. (020-165)

### The Bear and the Rabbit Hunt Buffalo (Chapter 022)

Rabbit's friendship with Bear is tested as they hunt buffalo together. Bear repeatedly takes the meat while Rabbit goes hungry, until Rabbit's adopted son — born from a blood clot — intervenes:

> "Rabbit, you come along home with me," said Bear, keeping all the meat for himself while poor Rabbit would get nothing. (022-013–022-016)

## Character Traits

Rabbit embodies the classic **trickster archetype**: he is small and physically weak but uses wit and bluff to overcome larger adversaries. However, his impulsiveness repeatedly leads to misfortune — he loses his tail through recklessness and trades away his youth on a whim. His brother and grandmother serve as protective figures who repeatedly rescue him from the consequences of his own actions.

Despite his flaws, Rabbit is neither malicious nor heroic — he is fundamentally *human* in his contradictions, making him one of the most relatable figures in the Sioux legendary cycle.
