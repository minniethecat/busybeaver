## Overview

The Sioux, also known as the Dakota, are a Native American people of the northern Great Plains. The myths and legends collected in this volume were told to the author, Marie L. McLaughlin — herself of one-fourth Sioux blood — by "the older men and women of the Sioux" (P02-038). McLaughlin, born at Wabasha, Minnesota in 1842, acquired a thorough knowledge of the Sioux language and lived on Indian reservations for over forty years, giving her exceptional access to Sioux oral traditions (P02-032).

The stories reflect a people living "in intimate contact and friendship with the big out-of-doors that we call Nature" — a "simple, grave, and sincere people" whose tales carry the distinctive "timbre" of Sioux thought (P02-052).

## In the Stories

### Customs and Daily Life

Sioux customs are woven throughout the narratives. Dogs were kept by the Sioux to alert owners to the approach of strangers by their barking (024-018). The Dakota camping circle faced all tents toward the center, a practice noted in the organization of village life (016-052).

### Marriage and Courtship

Sioux marriage customs appear prominently. A Dakota girl married a man who promised to treat her kindly, reflecting the importance of courtship rituals (010-001). A Dakota man married an Arikara woman, illustrating inter-tribal marriage between the Sioux and neighboring peoples (012-001). The practice of bride service and gift exchange — including the giving of spotted ponies — is depicted in Big Eagle's marriage into the Crow tribe (024-064).

### Warfare and Inter-Tribal Relations

Warfare features prominently in Sioux stories. The Sioux fought major battles with the Chippewa, where Sioux numerical superiority often determined the outcome (028-041–028-044). A Sioux war party of seven young men scouted enemy camps, with noted warriors like Big Eagle performing brave reconnaissance missions (024-001–024-009). The Sioux also warred with the Crow people, with whom they had complex relations including marriage alliances (024-068).

Big Eagle is described as "one of the bravest of Sioux warriors who ever took the scalp of an enemy" — a man who gave up his home, parents, and friends for the love of his dead wife, ultimately killed on the field of battle by his own tribe (024-103–024-106).

### Spiritual Beliefs

Sioux spiritual life included medicine men whose wisdom was sought for healing (029-059). The Great Mystery was acknowledged as a higher power, and respect for corn and the earth was central to Sioux agricultural spirituality (001-017).
