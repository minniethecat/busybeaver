## Overview

The Arikara were a neighboring tribe of the Sioux (Dakota) who appear in Sioux oral traditions. In these stories, Arikara individuals participate in significant events within the Sioux world — a woman gathering corn whose discovery shapes agricultural ritual, and a wife whose jealous grief becomes a sacred landmark.

## In the Stories

### The Forgotten Ear of Corn (Chapter 1)

An Arikara woman gathering corn in the field heard a faint voice like a child weeping — a single forgotten ear of corn crying out not to be left behind (001-001–001-005). She searched until she found it hidden under the leaves. This origin story explains why Sioux women thereafter gathered their corn crop with great care, "so that the succulent food product should not even to the last small nubbin be neglected or wasted" (001-013–001-017).

### The Legend of Standing Rock (Chapter 12)

A Dakota man married an Arikara woman, and they had one child. When he took a second wife, the Arikara woman grew jealous. When the village broke camp, she refused to move from her place on the ground, sitting with her babe on her back as the tent was taken down around her. Her husband sent his brothers back to fetch her, but:

> The elder spoke: "Sister-in-law, get up. We have come for you. The camp awaits you." She did not answer, and he put out his hand and touched her head. She had turned to stone!
>
> (012-001–012-014)

This petrified woman became Standing Rock — a sacred landmark known to the Sioux.
