[034-001]There was once upon a time a man who did not care to live with his tribe

[034-002]in a crowded village, but preferred a secluded spot in the deep forest,

[034-003]there to live with his wife and family of five children. The oldest of

[034-004]the children (a boy) was twelve years of age, and being the son of a

[034-005]distinguished hunter, soon took to roaming through the forest in search

[034-006]of small game.

[034-007]One day during his ramblings, he discovered a crane’s nest, with only

[034-008]one young crane occupying it. No doubt some fox or traveling weasel

[034-009]had eaten the rest of the crane’s brothers and sisters. The boy said to

[034-010]himself, “I will take this poor little crane home and will raise him as

[034-011]a pet for our baby. If I leave him here some hungry fox will be sure to

[034-012]eat the poor little fellow.” He carried the young crane home and it grew

[034-013]to be nearly as tall as the boy’s five-year-old sister.

[034-014]Being brought up in a human circle, it soon grew to understand all the

[034-015]family said. Although it could not speak it took part in all the games

[034-016]played by the children. The father of the family was, as I have before

[034-017]mentioned, a great hunter. He always had a plentiful supply of deer,

[034-018]antelope, buffalo and beaver meats on hand, but there came a change.

[034-019]The game migrated to some other locality, where no deadly shot like

[034-020]“Kutesan” (Never Miss) would be around to annihilate their fast

[034-021]decreasing droves. The hunter started out early one morning in hopes of

[034-022]discovering some of the game which had disappeared as suddenly as though

[034-023]the earth had swallowed them. The hunter traveled the whole day, all to

[034-024]no purpose. It was late in the evening when he staggered into camp. He

[034-025]was nearly dead with fatigue. Hastily swallowing a cup of cherry bark

[034-026]tea (the only article of food they had in store), he at once retired

[034-027]and was soon in the sweet land of dreams. The children soon joined their

[034-028]father and the poor woman sat thinking how they could save their dear

[034-029]children from starvation. Suddenly out upon the night air rang the cry

[034-030]of a crane. Instantly the pet crane awoke, stepped outside and answered

[034-031]the call. The crane which had given the cry was the father of the pet

[034-032]crane, and learning from Mr. Fox of the starving condition of his son

[034-033]and his friends, he flew to the hunting grounds of the tribe, and as

[034-034]there had been a good kill that day, the crane found no trouble in

[034-035]securing a great quantity of fat. This he carried to the tent of the

[034-036]hunter and, hovering over the tent he suddenly let the fat drop to

[034-037]the earth and at once the pet crane picked it up and carried it to the

[034-038]woman.

[034-039]Wishing to surprise the family on their awakening in the morning she

[034-040]got a good stick for a light, heaped up sticks on the dying embers, and

[034-041]started up a rousing fire and proceeded to melt or try out the fat, as

[034-042]melted fat is considered a favorite dish. Although busily occupied she

[034-043]kept her ears open for any strange noises coming out of the forest,

[034-044]there being usually some enemies lurking around. She held her pan in

[034-045]such a position that after the fat started to melt and quite a lot of

[034-046]the hot grease accumulated in the pan, she could plainly see the tent

[034-047]door reflected in the hot grease, as though she used a mirror.

[034-048]When she had nearly completed her task, she heard a noise as though some

[034-049]footsteps were approaching. Instantly her heart began to beat a tattoo

[034-050]on her ribs, but she sat perfectly quiet, calling all her self-control

[034-051]into play to keep from making an outcry. This smart woman had already

[034-052]studied out a way in which to best this enemy, in case an enemy it

[034-053]should be. The footsteps, or noise, continued to advance, until at last

[034-054]the woman saw reflected in the pan of grease a hand slowly protruding

[034-055]through the tent door, and the finger pointed, as if counting, to the

[034-056]sleeping father, then to each one of the sleeping children, then to her

[034-057]who sat at the fire. Little did Mr. Enemy suppose that the brave woman

[034-058]who sat so composed at her fire, was watching every motion he was

[034-059]making. The hand slowly withdrew, and as the footsteps slowly died

[034-060]away, there rang out on the still night air the deep fierce howl of the

[034-061]prairie wolf. (This imitation of a prairie wolf is the signal to the war

[034-062]party that an enemy has been discovered by the scout whom they have sent

[034-063]out in advance). At once she aroused her husband and children. Annoyed

[034-064]at being so unceremoniously disturbed from his deep sleep, the husband

[034-065]crossly asked why she had awakened him so roughly. The wife explained

[034-066]what she had seen and heard. She at once pinned an old blanket around

[034-067]the crane’s shoulders and an old piece of buffalo hide on his head for a

[034-068]hat or head covering. Heaping piles of wood onto the fire she instructed

[034-069]him to run around outside of the hut until the family returned, as they

[034-070]were going to see if they could find some roots to mix up with the fat.

[034-071]Hurriedly she tied her blanket around her middle, put her baby inside of

[034-072]it, and then grabbed her three year old son and packed him on her back.

[034-073]The father also hurriedly packed the next two and the older boy took

[034-074]care of himself.

[034-075]Immediately upon leaving the tent they took three different directions,

[034-076]to meet again on the high hill west of their home. The reflection from

[034-077]the fire in the tent disclosed to them the poor pet crane running around

[034-078]the tent. It looked exactly like a child with its blanket and hat on.

[034-079]Suddenly there rang out a score of shots and war whoops of the dreaded

[034-080]Crow Indians. Finding the tent deserted they disgustedly filed off and

[034-081]were swallowed up in the darkness of the deep forest.

[034-082]The next morning the family returned to see what had become of their pet

[034-083]crane. There, riddled to pieces, lay the poor bird who had given up his

[034-084]life to save his dear friends.
