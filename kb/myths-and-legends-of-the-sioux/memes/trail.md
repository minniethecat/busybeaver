## Overview

The **trail** in Sioux stories is a thread of movement etched across the land — the trace of driven herds, the path of hunting parties, the track of a fleeing enemy, and the supernatural guide left by corn kernels or spirit runners. Characters "strike the trail of the herd" (023-099), "follow the trail of our party" (030-256), and travel "along slowly on his trail" (035-124). The trail is a spatial record: it shows where someone has been and invites where someone may go. It appears across six chapters as a narrative device that propels pursuit, enables tracking, and marks the boundary between the known and the hidden.

## In the Stories

The trail's richest narrative appears in Chapter 023, where a young hunter pursues stolen horses. "Striking the trail of the herd driven off by" (023-099) raiders, his enemies set "their camp on the trail of our young friend" (023-101), traveling "on his trail, and just as the sun was sinking they caught sight of him" (023-102). The young man, "looking back along the trail, saw some dark" (023-104) figures approaching. From a high butte he looks "over his trail and saw coming over a" (023-107) rise a larger force. He "concealed himself close to the trail made by the drove of" (023-110) stolen horses, and later "followed the trail of the drove to where they had entered the" (023-119) timber. At chapter's end, he is "looking back along his trail in search of any of the enemy who might" (023-135) still pursue.

In Chapter 024, pursuers act swiftly: "they hurriedly started on their trail for" (024-043) the chase, the trail a line of urgency drawn between hunter and hunted.

Chapter 026 presents the most enigmatic trail in Sioux lore — the corn trail. A character follows scattered kernels until he "came to a very small knoll the trail ended. At the end of the trail was a large circle, from which the grass had been scraped off" (026-040–041). An elder explains: "The corn trail stops at the edge of this circle" (026-043). The corn "was scattered along the trail" (026-051), deliberately laid by a mysterious runner — the trail as supernatural breadcrumb, one of the few magical trails in Sioux oral tradition.

Chapter 030 describes the broad trail of mass movement. The "trail left by the hunting party" (030-182) guides a lone character following a hundred horses. An elder warns: "cover your trail, but nevertheless, don't stop traveling for three days" (030-194). Scouts "crawled down to the trail and lay waiting for the old man" (030-211) — the trail as ambush site, where those who follow meet those who wait. A father instructs: "you follow the trail of our party and go on ahead with the horses" (030-256), and describes "driving them along the broad trail left by the hundreds of travois" (030-261) — the trail widened by sheer numbers into a road across the prairie.

In Chapter 035, the trail becomes a guide for the hero White Plume. The voice says "I will travel along slowly on his trail and arrive at the" (035-124) destination, and White Plume is "instructed... as to the trail" (035-130). He gathers his party and sets out — the trail as instruction, a map spoken rather than drawn.

In Chapter 036, a pursuer awakens and finds the quarry's trace: "He had no trouble in following the trail, as the boy" (036-072) had left clear marks. After pausing to drink, "he arose and continued on the trail" (036-089) — the trail as persistence, resumed after every rest.

## Cultural Significance

- **Trail as pursuit**: The trail is most commonly followed in hunt or war — it is a line of intent drawn by the pursued and read by the pursuer (023-099, 023-102)
- **Trail as ambush**: Those who follow trails are themselves followed; scouts "lay waiting" along the trail (030-211), turning the tracker into the tracked
- **Trail as supernatural guide**: The corn trail (026-040–051) is rare magic — a trail laid not by footfall but by scattered kernels, ending in a mysterious scraped circle
- **Trail as mass record**: The "broad trail" of hundreds of travois (030-261) transforms the prairie, a mark of collective passage that endures
- **Trail as concealment**: The instruction to "cover your trail" (030-194) acknowledges that trails are dangerous — they tell stories to those who can read them
