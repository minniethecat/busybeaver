[015-001]There was a man and his wife who had one daughter. Mother and daughter

[015-002]were deeply attached to one another, and when the latter died the mother

[015-003]was disconsolate. She cut off her hair, cut gashes in her cheeks and sat

[015-004]before the corpse with her robe drawn over her head, mourning for her

[015-005]dead. Nor would she let them touch the body to take it to a burying

[015-006]scaffold. She had a knife in her hand, and if anyone offered to come

[015-007]near the body the mother would wail:

[015-008]“I am weary of life. I do not care to live. I will stab myself with this

[015-009]knife and join my daughter in the land of spirits.”

[015-010]Her husband and relatives tried to get the knife from her, but could

[015-011]not. They feared to use force lest she kill herself. They came together

[015-012]to see what they could do.

[015-013]“We must get the knife away from her,” they said.

[015-014]At last they called a boy, a kind of simpleton, yet with a good deal of

[015-015]natural shrewdness. He was an orphan and very poor. His moccasins were

[015-016]out at the sole and he was dressed in wei-zi (coarse buffalo skin,

[015-017]smoked).

[015-018]“Go to the tepee of the mourning mother,” they told the simpleton, “and

[015-019]in some way contrive to make her laugh and forget her grief. Then try to

[015-020]get the knife away from her.”

[015-021]The boy went to the tent and sat down at the door as if waiting to be

[015-022]given something. The corpse lay in the place of honor where the dead

[015-023]girl had slept in life. The body was wrapped in a rich robe and wrapped

[015-024]about with ropes. Friends had covered it with rich offerings out of

[015-025]respect to the dead.

[015-026]As the mother sat on the ground with her head covered she did not at

[015-027]first see the boy, who sat silent. But when his reserve had worn away

[015-028]a little he began at first lightly, then more heavily, to drum on the

[015-029]floor with his hands. After a while he began to sing a comic song.

[015-030]Louder and louder he sang until carried away with his own singing he

[015-031]sprang up and began to dance, at the same time gesturing and making all

[015-032]manner of contortions with his body, still singing the comic song. As he

[015-033]approached the corpse he waved his hands over it in blessing. The mother

[015-034]put her head out of the blanket and when she saw the poor simpleton

[015-035]with his strange grimaces trying to do honor to the corpse by his solemn

[015-036]waving, and at the same time keeping up his comic song, she burst out

[015-037]laughing. Then she reached over and handed her knife to the simpleton.

[015-038]“Take this knife,” she said. “You have taught me to forget my grief. If

[015-039]while I mourn for the dead I can still be mirthful, there is no reason

[015-040]for me to despair. I no longer care to die. I will live for my husband.”

[015-041]The simpleton left the tepee and brought the knife to the astonished

[015-042]husband and relatives.

[015-043]“How did you get it? Did you force it away from her, or did you steal

[015-044]it?” they said.

[015-045]“She gave it to me. How could I force it from her or steal it when she

[015-046]held it in her hand, blade uppermost? I sang and danced for her and she

[015-047]burst out laughing. Then she gave it to me,” he answered.

[015-048]When the old men of the village heard the orphan’s story they were very

[015-049]silent. It was a strange thing for a lad to dance in a tepee where there

[015-050]was mourning. It was stranger that a mother should laugh in a tepee

[015-051]before the corpse of her dead daughter. The old men gathered at last in

[015-052]a council. They sat a long time without saying anything, for they did

[015-053]not want to decide hastily. The pipe was filled and passed many times.

[015-054]At last an old man spoke.

[015-055]“We have a hard question. A mother has laughed before the corpse of her

[015-056]daughter, and many think she has done foolishly, but I think the woman

[015-057]did wisely. The lad was simple and of no training, and we cannot expect

[015-058]him to know how to do as well as one with good home and parents to teach

[015-059]him. Besides, he did the best that he knew. He danced to make the mother

[015-060]forget her grief, and he tried to honor the corpse by waving over it his

[015-061]hands.”

[015-062]“The mother did right to laugh, for when one does try to do us good,

[015-063]even if what he does causes us discomfort, we should always remember

[015-064]rather the motive than the deed. And besides, the simpleton’s dancing

[015-065]saved the woman’s life, for she gave up her knife. In this, too, she did

[015-066]well, for it is always better to live for the living than to die for the

[015-067]dead.”
