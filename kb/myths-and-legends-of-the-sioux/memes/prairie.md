## Overview

The **prairie** (makob) is the vast open grassland that defines Sioux country. Unlike the enclosed [[forest]] or the sheltered [[village]], the prairie exposes those who cross it — "crossing a long flat prairie" (023-134) is an ordeal of distance and visibility. In winter, "the rabbit once went out on the prairie in winter time" (006-001) and found children coasting — the prairie's openness makes it a place of play as well as hardship. The prairie is also the domain of specific creatures: the "prairie wolf" (034-061), the "prairie chickens" (035-148), the "prairie mouse" (002-001) — each named for this defining landscape. It is the arena of the great hunt: "they should go out on the prairies, and see if they would have" (030-057) success.

## Geographic Presence

The prairie is defined by its characteristics:

| Feature | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Flatness | "A long flat prairie" (023-134) — unobstructed visibility, no hiding |
| Exposure | "Lie out on these open prairies and freeze to death" (030-134) — no shelter |
| Winter hardship | "On the prairie in winter time" (006-001) — cold amplified by openness |
| Fauna | Prairie wolf, prairie chickens, prairie mouse — species named for the biome |

## In the Stories

### The Winter Prairie

The prairie in winter is a place of stark beauty and danger. "The rabbit once went out on the prairie in winter time. On the side of" (006-001) a hill he found children playing — the prairie's openness becomes a playground when snow covers it. But the same openness threatens: a character fears "to lie out on these open prairies and freeze to death" (030-134) — exposure is the prairie's lethal aspect.

### The Journey Across

Warriors "crossing a long flat prairie, ascended a long chain of hills" (023-134) — the prairie is a trial to be crossed, its length testing endurance. A hunting party debates whether "they should go out on the prairies, and see if they would have" (030-057) success — the prairie as the arena of the hunt, where buffalo roam and hunters follow.

### The Prairie's Creatures

The prairie mouse "busied herself all fall" (002-001) gathering — the prairie as home, not just passage. The prairie wolf howls — "prairie wolf. (This imitation of a" (034-061) — the prairie's voice. Prairie chickens sit (035-148) — the prairie's flock. Each creature is defined by its relationship to this vast grassland.

## Cultural Significance

- **Prairie as the open**: Unlike the forest's concealment, the prairie offers no hiding — it is the landscape of exposure and visibility
- **Prairie as trial**: Crossing the prairie (023-134) tests endurance; surviving the prairie winter (030-134) tests fortitude
- **Prairie as home**: Named creatures — prairie mouse, prairie wolf, prairie chicken — show that the prairie is not empty but inhabited
- **Prairie as hunting ground**: "Go out on the prairies" (030-057) to hunt — the prairie is the domain of the buffalo and the hunters who follow them
