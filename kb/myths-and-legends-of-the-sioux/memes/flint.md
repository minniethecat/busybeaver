## Overview

**Flint** holds a dual significance in Sioux folklore and material culture. As a practical resource, it was the essential material for crafting arrowheads, knives, and fire-starting tools. In the legendary cycle, flint takes on symbolic power — most dramatically in the figure of the [[bear]] with the Flint Body (Chapter 009), whose invulnerable form represents flint's hardness taken to supernatural extremes.

## In the Stories

### The Bear with the Flint Body (Chapter 009)

The most significant flint narrative centers on Rabbit's quest to obtain flint from the Bear. Rabbit's grandmother instructs him to make flint arrowheads, revealing that all the flint in the world resides within the Bear's body:

> "You must make some flint arrowheads," said his grandmother. "Where shall I get the flint?" asked the rabbit. "All the flint in the world is in the bear's body." (009-005–009-009)

When Rabbit visits the Bear's village, he discovers the Bear's body is composed half of soft material and half of hard flint. With careful aim, Rabbit chips away a flake:

> "Rabbit struck off a little flake of flint from the bear's body. The soft half fell away and only the flint part remained." (009-030–009-039)

### Unktomi the Arrowhead Maker (Chapter 021)

Flint craftsmanship is personified in [[unktomi]] (the Spider), who sits atop a hill manufacturing flint arrowheads on a massive scale:

> "Looking around they discovered a large spider sitting in the midst of a great many flint arrowheads. The spider was busily engaged making the flint rocks into arrowheads." (021-010–021-012)

Unktomi's production of arrowheads serves the tribe's hunting needs:

> "We come to ask you to supply us with the flint arrowheads which we use to provide meat for our village." (021-016)

### Flint as Fire and Tool

Beyond arrowheads, flint appears in practical contexts throughout the corpus. In Chapter 010, a bundle contains "punk, flint and steel" for fire-starting. In Chapter 017, hunters carry "flint and packs of dried meat" as essential supplies. The recurrence of flint in these practical passages underscores its centrality to daily Sioux life.

## Cultural Significance

| Aspect | Manifestation |
|--------|--------------|
| **Weaponry** | Arrowheads, spear points (chapters 009, 021, 022, 026, 035) |
| **Fire-making** | Flint and steel kits (chapter 010, 017) |
| **Symbolic power** | Invulnerable Flint Body of the Bear (chapter 009) |
| **Craftsmanship** | Unktomi's arrowhead manufacture (chapter 021) |
| **Trade goods** | Flint requested as tribute from the Spider (021-043) |

The recurring motif of flint connects multiple narrative threads: Rabbit's cleverness in stealing it, Bear's power embodied in it, Unktomi's craftsmanship transforming it, and hunters' survival depending on it. This web of associations makes flint one of the most richly cross-referenced concepts in the Sioux legendary corpus.
