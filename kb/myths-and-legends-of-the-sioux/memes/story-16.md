[016-001]A village of Indians moved out of winter camp and pitched their tents

[016-002]in a circle on high land overlooking a lake. A little way down the

[016-003]declivity was a grave. Choke cherries had grown up, hiding the grave

[016-004]from view. But as the ground had sunk somewhat, the grave was marked by

[016-005]a slight hollow.

[016-006]One of the villagers going out to hunt took a short cut through the

[016-007]choke cherry bushes. As he pushed them aside he saw the hollow grave,

[016-008]but thought it was a washout made by the rains. But as he essayed to

[016-009]step over it, to his great surprise he stumbled and fell. Made curious

[016-010]by his mishap, he drew back and tried again; but again he fell. When he

[016-011]came back to the village he told the old men what had happened to him.

[016-012]They remembered then that a long time before there had been buried there

[016-013]a medicine woman or conjurer. Doubtless it was her medicine that made

[016-014]him stumble.

[016-015]The story of the villager’s adventure spread thru the camp and made many

[016-016]curious to see the grave. Among others were six little boys who were,

[016-017]however, rather timid, for they were in great awe of the dead medicine

[016-018]woman. But they had a little playmate named Brave, a mischievous little

[016-019]rogue, whose hair was always unkempt and tossed about and who was never

[016-020]quiet for a moment.

[016-021]“Let us ask Brave to go with us,” they said; and they went in a body to

[016-022]see him.

[016-023]“All right,” said Brave; “I will go with you. But I have something to

[016-024]do first. You go on around the hill _that_ way, and I will hasten around

[016-025]_this_ way, and meet you a little later near the grave.”

[016-026]So the six little boys went on as bidden until they came to a place near

[016-027]the grave. There they halted.

[016-028]“Where is Brave?” they asked.

[016-029]Now Brave, full of mischief, had thought to play a jest on his little

[016-030]friends. As soon as they were well out of sight he had sped around the

[016-031]hill to the shore of the lake and sticking his hands in the mud had

[016-032]rubbed it over his face, plastered it in his hair, and soiled his hands

[016-033]until he looked like a new risen corpse with the flesh rotting from his

[016-034]bones. He then went and lay down in the grave and awaited the boys.

[016-035]When the six little boys came they were more timid than ever when they

[016-036]did not find Brave; but they feared to go back to the village without

[016-037]seeing the grave, for fear the old men would call them cowards.

[016-038]So they slowly approached the grave and one of them timidly called out:

[016-039]“Please, grandmother, we won’t disturb your grave. We only want to see

[016-040]where you lie. Don’t be angry.”

[016-041]At once a thin quavering voice, like an old woman’s, called out:

[016-042]“Han, han, takoja, hechetuya, hechetuya! Yes, yes, that’s right, that’s

[016-043]right.”

[016-044]The boys were frightened out of their senses, believing the old woman

[016-045]had come to life.

[016-046]“Oh, grandmother,” they gasped, “don’t hurt us; please don’t, we’ll go.”

[016-047]Just then Brave raised his muddy face and hands up thru the choke cherry

[016-048]bushes. With the oozy mud dripping from his features he looked like some

[016-049]very witch just raised from the grave. The boys screamed outright. One

[016-050]fainted. The rest ran yelling up the hill to the village, where each

[016-051]broke at once for his mother’s tepee.

[016-052]As all the tents in a Dakota camping circle face the center, the boys as

[016-053]they came tearing into camp were in plain view from the tepees. Hearing

[016-054]the screaming, every woman in camp ran to her tepee door to see what

[016-055]had happened. Just then little Brave, as badly scared as the rest, came

[016-056]rushing in after them, his hair on end and covered with mud and crying

[016-057]out, all forgetful of his appearance:

[016-058]“It’s me, it’s me!”

[016-059]The women yelped and bolted in terror from the village. Brave dashed

[016-060]into his mother’s tepee, scaring her out of her wits. Dropping pots and

[016-061]kettles, she tumbled out of the tent to run screaming with the rest. Nor

[016-062]would a single villager come near poor little Brave until he had gone

[016-063]down to the lake and washed himself.
