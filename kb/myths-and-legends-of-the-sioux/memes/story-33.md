[033-001]There once lived an old couple who had an only daughter. She was a

[033-002]beautiful girl, and was very much courted by the young men of the

[033-003]tribe, but she said that she preferred single life, and to all their

[033-004]heart-touching tales of deep affection for her she always had one

[033-005]answer. That was “No.”

[033-006]One day this maiden fell ill and day after day grew worse. All the best

[033-007]medicine men were called in, but their medicines were of no avail, and

[033-008]in two weeks from the day that she was taken ill she lay a corpse. Of

[033-009]course there was great mourning in the camp. They took her body several

[033-010]miles from camp and rolled it in fine robes and blankets, then they laid

[033-011]her on a scaffold which they had erected. (This was the custom of burial

[033-012]among the Indians). They placed four forked posts into the ground and

[033-013]then lashed strong poles lengthwise and across the ends and made a bed

[033-014]of willows and stout ash brush. This scaffold was from five to seven

[033-015]feet from the ground. After the funeral the parents gave away all of

[033-016]their horses, fine robes and blankets and all of the belongings of

[033-017]the dead girl. Then they cut their hair off close to their heads, and

[033-018]attired themselves in the poorest apparel they could secure.

[033-019]When a year had passed the friends and relatives of the old couple tried

[033-020]in vain to have them set aside their mourning. “You have mourned long

[033-021]enough,” they would say. “Put aside your mourning and try and enjoy a

[033-022]few more pleasures of this life while you live. You are both growing old

[033-023]and can’t live very many more years, so make the best of your time.” The

[033-024]old couple would listen to their advice and then shake their heads and

[033-025]answer: “We have nothing to live for. Nothing we could join in would be

[033-026]any amusement to us, since we have lost the light of our lives.”

[033-027]So the old couple continued their mourning for their lost idol. Two

[033-028]years had passed since the death of the beautiful girl, when one evening

[033-029]a hunter and his wife passed by the scaffold which held the dead girl.

[033-030]They were on their return trip and were heavily loaded down with game,

[033-031]and therefore could not travel very fast. About half a mile from the

[033-032]scaffold a clear spring burst forth from the side of a bank, and from

[033-033]this trickled a small stream of water, moistening the roots of the

[033-034]vegetation bordering its banks, and causing a growth of sweet green

[033-035]grass. At this spring the hunter camped and tethering his horses, at

[033-036]once set about helping his wife to erect the small tepee which they

[033-037]carried for convenience in traveling.

[033-038]When it became quite dark, the hunter’s dogs set up a great barking

[033-039]and growling. “Look out and see what the dogs are barking at,” said the

[033-040]hunter to his wife. She looked out through the door and then drew back

[033-041]saying: “There is the figure of a woman advancing from the direction of

[033-042]the girl’s scaffold.” “I expect it is the dead girl; let her come,

[033-043]and don’t act as if you were afraid,” said the hunter. Soon they heard

[033-044]footsteps advancing and the steps ceased at the door. Looking down at

[033-045]the lower part of the door the hunter noticed a pair of small moccasins,

[033-046]and knowing that it was the visitor, said: “Whoever you are, come in and

[033-047]have something to eat.”

[033-048]At this invitation the figure came slowly in and sat down by the door

[033-049]with head covered and with a fine robe drawn tightly over the face. The

[033-050]woman dished up a fine supper and placing it before the visitor, said:

[033-051]“Eat, my friend, you must be hungry.” The figure never moved, nor

[033-052]would it uncover to eat. “Let us turn our back towards the door and our

[033-053]visitor may eat the food,” said the hunter. So his wife turned her back

[033-054]towards the visitor and made herself very busy cleaning the small pieces

[033-055]of meat that were hanging to the back sinews of the deer which had been

[033-056]killed. (This the Indians use as thread.) The hunter, filling his pipe,

[033-057]turned away and smoked in silence. Finally the dish was pushed back to

[033-058]the woman, who took it and after washing it, put it away. The figure

[033-059]still sat at the door, not a sound coming from it, neither was it

[033-060]breathing. The hunter at last said: “Are you the girl that was placed

[033-061]upon that scaffold two years ago?” It bowed its head two or three times

[033-062]in assent. “Are you going to sleep here tonight; if you are, my wife

[033-063]will make down a bed for you.” The figure shook its head. “Are you going

[033-064]to come again tomorrow night to us?” It nodded assent.

[033-065]For three nights in succession the figure visited the hunter’s camp. The

[033-066]third night the hunter noticed that the figure was breathing. He saw one

[033-067]of the hands protruding from the robe. The skin was perfectly black and

[033-068]was stuck fast to the bones of the hand. On seeing this the hunter arose

[033-069]and going over to his medicine sack which hung on a pole, took down the

[033-070]sack and, opening it, took out some roots and mixing them with skunk oil

[033-071]and vermillion, said to the figure:

[033-072]“If you will let us rub your face and hands with this medicine it will

[033-073]put new life into the skin and you will assume your complexion again and

[033-074]it will put flesh on you.” The figure assented and the hunter rubbed the

[033-075]medicine on her hands and face. Then she arose and walked back to the

[033-076]scaffold. The next day the hunter moved camp towards the home village.

[033-077]That night he camped within a few miles of the village. When night came,

[033-078]the dogs, as usual, set up a great barking, and looking out, the wife

[033-079]saw the girl approaching.

[033-080]When the girl had entered and sat down, the hunter noticed that the girl

[033-081]did not keep her robe so closely together over her face. When the wife

[033-082]gave her something to eat, the girl reached out and took the dish, thus

[033-083]exposing her hands, which they at once noticed were again natural. After

[033-084]she had finished her meal, the hunter said: “Did my medicine help you?”

[033-085]She nodded assent. “Do you want my medicine rubbed all over your body?”

[033-086]Again she nodded. “I will mix enough to rub your entire body, and I will

[033-087]go outside and let my wife rub it on for you.” He mixed a good supply

[033-088]and going out left his wife to rub the girl. When his wife had completed

[033-089]the task she called to her husband to come in, and when he came in he

[033-090]sat down and said to the girl: “Tomorrow we will reach the village. Do

[033-091]you want to go with us?” She shook her head. “Will you come again to our

[033-092]camp tomorrow night after we have camped in the village?” She nodded

[033-093]her head in assent. “Then do you want to see your parents?” She nodded

[033-094]again, and arose and disappeared into the darkness.

[033-095]Early the next morning the hunter broke camp and traveled far into the

[033-096]afternoon, when he arrived at the village. He instructed his wife to go

[033-097]at once and inform the old couple of what had happened. The wife did

[033-098]so and at sunset the old couple came to the hunter’s tepee. They were

[033-099]invited to enter and a fine supper was served them. Soon after they had

[033-100]finished their supper the dogs of the camp set up a great barking. “Now

[033-101]she is coming, so be brave and you will soon see your lost daughter,”

[033-102]said the hunter. Hardly had he finished speaking when she entered the

[033-103]tent as natural as ever she was in life. Her parents clung to her and

[033-104]smothered her with kisses.

[033-105]They wanted her to return home with them, but she would stay with the

[033-106]hunter who had brought her back to life, and she married him, becoming

[033-107]his second wife. A short time after taking the girl for his wife, the

[033-108]hunter joined a war party and never returned, as he was killed on the

[033-109]battlefield.

[033-110]A year after her husband’s death she married again. This husband was

[033-111]also killed by a band of enemies whom the warriors were pursuing for

[033-112]stealing some of their horses. The third husband also met a similar fate

[033-113]to the first. He was killed on the field of battle.

[033-114]She was still a handsome woman at the time of the third husband’s death,

[033-115]but never again married, as the men feared her, saying she was holy, and

[033-116]that any one who married her would be sure to be killed by the enemy.

[033-117]So she took to doctoring the sick and gained the reputation of being the

[033-118]most skilled doctor in the nation. She lived to a ripe old age and when

[033-119]she felt death approaching she had them take her to where she had rested

[033-120]once before, and crawling to the top of the newly erected scaffold,

[033-121]wrapped her blankets and robes about her, covered her face carefully,

[033-122]and fell into that sleep from which there is no more awakening.
