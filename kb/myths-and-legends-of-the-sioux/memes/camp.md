## Overview

The **camp** (ti or oyate tipi) in Sioux folklore is the community in transit — a village unmoored from its permanent site, carried on ponies and set down where the journey pauses. The preface situates the entire collection "at the camp fires of the past" (P02-053), making the campfire the originary site of storytelling itself. A camping party "left the village and" (004-030) set out, the camp as expedition. When a woman refuses to move, "the rest of the camp with her husband" (012-005) continues without her — the camp is not a fixed place but a collective decision to go or stay. "The camp awaits you" (012-012) is a call to rejoin the living, the moving, the social body.

## In the Stories

Chapter 004 opens with a family "making ready to go on a camping journey" (004-030). The camp is preparation, anticipation, the gathering of provisions. The "camping party left the village and" (004-030) moved into the unknown, the camp as adventure. But "when the camping party came" (004-034) upon the enemy, the camp transforms from expedition to battleground — the temporary settlement is vulnerable in ways the permanent village is not.

Chapter 012 presents the camp as collective will. "When the time came for the village to break camp she refused" (012-003) — one woman's refusal pits individual against community. "The rest of the camp with her husband" (012-005) moved on, and soon the abandoned woman's brother returned: "We have come for you. The camp awaits you" (012-012). The camp is not geography but people — to be without the camp is to be without society.

In Chapter 010, a lookout "saw the camp coming in a long line over the" (010-044) prairie — the camp as spectacle, a moving ribbon of people and ponies. "All the men of the camp mounted their ponies" (010-052) — the camp as military unit, its men ready to defend or attack on command.

## Cultural Significance

- **Camp as mobile community**: The camp (004-030, 012-003) is the village unfixed from place — community defined by people, not structures
- **Campfire as narrative origin**: "At the camp fires of the past" (P02-053) — storytelling and the camp are inseparable; the tales exist because camps existed
- **Camp as collective will**: "The camp awaits you" (012-012) — to leave the camp is to leave society; the camp moves as one body
- **Camp as vulnerable settlement**: Without permanent defenses, the camp (004-034) can become a battleground — mobility trades security for freedom
