[022-001]Once upon a time there lived as neighbors, a bear and a rabbit. The

[022-002]rabbit was a good shot, and the bear being very clumsy could not use the

[022-003]arrow to good advantage. The bear was very unkind to the rabbit. Every

[022-004]morning, the bear would call over to the rabbit and say: “Take your bow

[022-005]and arrows and come with me to the other side of the hill. A large herd

[022-006]of buffalo are grazing there, and I want you to shoot some of them for

[022-007]me, as my children are crying for meat.”

[022-008]The rabbit, fearing to arouse the bear’s anger by refusing, consented,

[022-009]and went with the bear, and shot enough buffalo to satisfy the hungry

[022-010]family. Indeed, he shot and killed so many that there was lots of meat

[022-011]left after the bear and his family had loaded themselves, and packed all

[022-012]they could carry home. The bear being very gluttonous, and not wanting

[022-013]the rabbit to get any of the meat, said: “Rabbit, you come along home

[022-014]with us and we will return and get the remainder of the meat.”

[022-015]The poor rabbit could not even taste the blood from the butchering, as

[022-016]the bear would throw earth on the blood and dry it up. Poor Rabbit would

[022-017]have to go home hungry after his hard day’s work.

[022-018]The bear was the father of five children. The youngest boy was very kind

[022-019]to the rabbit. The mother bear, knowing that her youngest was a very

[022-020]hearty eater, always gave him an extra large piece of meat. What the

[022-021]baby bear did not eat, he would take outside with him and pretend to

[022-022]play ball with it, kicking it toward the rabbit’s house, and when he

[022-023]got close to the door he would give the meat such a great kick, that it

[022-024]would fly into the rabbit’s house, and in this way poor Rabbit would get

[022-025]his meal unknown to the papa bear.

[022-026]Baby bear never forgot his friend Rabbit. Papa bear often wondered why

[022-027]his baby would go outside after each meal. He grew suspicious and asked

[022-028]the baby where he had been. “Oh, I always play ball outside, around the

[022-029]house, and when I get tired playing I eat up my meat ball and then come

[022-030]in.”

[022-031]The baby bear was too cunning to let papa bear know that he was keeping

[022-032]his friend rabbit from starving to death. Nevertheless, papa bear

[022-033]suspected baby and said: “Baby, I think you go over to the rabbit’s

[022-034]after every meal.”

[022-035]The four older brothers were very handsome, but baby bear was a little

[022-036]puny fellow, whose coat couldn’t keep out much cold, as it was short and

[022-037]shaggy, and of a dirty brown color. The three older brothers were very

[022-038]unkind to baby bear, but the fourth one always took baby’s part, and was

[022-039]always kind to his baby brother.

[022-040]Rabbit was getting tired of being ordered and bullied around by papa

[022-041]bear. He puzzled his brain to scheme some way of getting even with Mr.

[022-042]Bear for abusing him so much. He studied all night long, but no scheme

[022-043]worth trying presented itself. Early one morning Mr. Bear presented

[022-044]himself at Rabbit’s door.

[022-045]“Say, Rabbit, my meat is all used up, and there is a fine herd of

[022-046]buffalo grazing on the hillside. Get your bow and arrows and come with

[022-047]me. I want you to shoot some of them for me.”

[022-048]“Very well,” said Rabbit, and he went and killed six buffalo for Bear.

[022-049]Bear got busy butchering and poor Rabbit, thinking he would get a chance

[022-050]to lick up one mouthful of blood, stayed very close to the bear while he

[022-051]was cutting up the meat. The bear was very watchful lest the rabbit get

[022-052]something to eat. Despite bear’s watchfulness, a small clot of blood

[022-053]rolled past and behind the bear’s feet. At once Rabbit seized the clot

[022-054]and hid it in his bosom. By the time Rabbit got home, the blood clot

[022-055]was hardened from the warmth of his body, so, being hungry, it put Mr.

[022-056]Rabbit out of sorts to think that after all his trouble he could not eat

[022-057]the blood.

[022-058]Very badly disappointed, he lay down on his floor and gazed up into the

[022-059]chimney hole. Disgusted with the way things had turned out, he grabbed

[022-060]up the blood clot and threw it up through the hole. Scarcely had it

[022-061]hit the ground when he heard the voice of a baby crying, “Ate! Ate!”

[022-062](father, father). He went outside and there he found a big baby boy. He

[022-063]took the baby into his house and threw him out through the hole again.

[022-064]This time the boy was large enough to say “Ate, Ate, he-cun-sin-lo.”

[022-065](Father, father, don’t do that). But nevertheless, he threw him up and

[022-066]out again. On going out the third time, there stood a handsome youth

[022-067]smiling at him. Rabbit at once adopted the youth and took him into his

[022-068]house, seating him in the seat of honor (which is directly opposite

[022-069]the entrance), and saying: “My son, I want you to be a good, honest,

[022-070]straightforward man. Now, I have in my possession a fine outfit, and

[022-071]you, my son, shall wear it.”

[022-072]Suiting his action to his words, he drew out a bag from a hollow tree

[022-073]and on opening it, drew out a fine buckskin shirt (tanned white as

[022-074]snow), worked with porcupine quills. Also a pair of red leggings worked

[022-075]with beads. Moccasins worked with colored hair. A fine otter skin robe.

[022-076]White weasel skins to intertwine with his beautiful long black locks. A

[022-077]magnificent center eagle feather. A rawhide covered bow, accompanied by

[022-078]a quiver full of flint arrowheads.

[022-079]The rabbit, having dressed his son in all the latest finery, sat back

[022-080]and gazed long and lovingly at his handsome son. Instinctively

[022-081]Rabbit felt that his son had been sent him for the purpose of being

[022-082]instrumental in the downfall of Mr. Bear. Events will show.

[022-083]The morning following the arrival of Rabbit’s son, Mr. Bear again

[022-084]presents himself at the door, crying out: “You lazy, ugly rabbit, get up

[022-085]and come out here. I want you to shoot some more buffalo for me.”

[022-086]“Who is this, who speaks so insultingly to you, father?” asked the son.

[022-087]“It is a bear who lives near here, and makes me kill buffalo for his

[022-088]family, and he won’t let me take even one little drop of blood from the

[022-089]killing, and consequently, my son, I have nothing in my house for you to

[022-090]eat.”

[022-091]The young man was anxious to meet Mr. Bear but Rabbit advised him to

[022-092]wait a little until he and Bear had gone to the hunt. So the son obeyed,

[022-093]and when he thought it time that the killing was done, he started out

[022-094]and arrived on the scene just as Mr. Bear was about to proceed with his

[022-095]butchering.

[022-096]Seeing a strange shadow on the ground beside him, Mr. Bear looked up and

[022-097]gazed into the fearless eyes of rabbit’s handsome son.

[022-098]“Who is this?” asked Mr. Bear of poor little Rabbit.

[022-099]“I don’t know,” answered Rabbit.

[022-100]“Who are you?” asked the bear of Rabbit’s son. “Where did you come

[022-101]from?”

[022-102]The rabbit’s son not replying, the bear spoke thus to him: “Get out of

[022-103]here, and get out quick, too.”

[022-104]At this speech the rabbit’s son became angered, and fastened an arrow to

[022-105]his bow and drove the arrow through the bear’s heart. Then he turned on

[022-106]Mrs. Bear and served her likewise. During the melee, Rabbit shouted:

[022-107]“My son, my son, don’t kill the two youngest. The baby has kept me from

[022-108]starving and the other one is good and kind to his baby brother.”

[022-109]So the three older brothers who were unkind to their baby brother met a

[022-110]similar fate to that of their selfish parents.

[022-111]This (the story goes) is the reason that bears travel only in pairs.
