## Overview

**Courtship** among the Sioux followed established customs: a suitor approached the family, parental consent was sought, and a maiden's choice — though guided by family — carried weight. Marriage was a social contract that joined families, and the dynamics of jealousy, multiple wives, and inter-tribal unions appear throughout the stories.

## In the Stories

### The Suitor's Approach

Young men declared their intentions directly. A suitor would approach and say: "Be my wife. I have little but I am young" (007-008), offering honesty about his circumstances. The maiden held the power of refusal: "For a long time the maiden did not answer" (007-010), her silence itself a form of deliberation. When she finally accepted, "the maiden arose. In her hands were beautiful" gifts (007-103), symbolizing the exchange that sealed the union.

### Competition Among Suitors

A desirable maiden attracted many pursuers. The chief's daughter was so sought after that all the young men of "the village wanted to have her for wife, and were all eager" (007-002). But status did not guarantee success — a suitor from "a mean family" (007-005) could win a chief's daughter through sincerity and perseverance, while richer rivals were refused.

### Parental Consent

Parents held authority over marriage decisions. Fathers granted or withheld "consent to the marriage of their daughter" (024-036). A father might promise his "daughter in marriage" (028-029) as a reward, or set conditions — "daughter in marriage, if I should succeed" (035-116) — tying the union to the suitor's accomplishments. Inter-tribal marriage also appears: "the most beautiful maiden of the Chippewa" (028-030) was sought by Sioux warriors.

### Taking a Wife

The phrase "take a wife" (017-007) captures the formal act of marriage. After marriage, a man might later take "another wife" (012-002), though polygyny carried its own tensions — "the first wife was jealous" (012-002). A wife could also be claimed by force: "I will steal her and make her my wife" (013-006), reflecting a darker current in courtship dynamics.

### Loss and Mourning

The bond of courtship extended into death. When a beloved suitor died, "the chief's daughter mourned for her lover as for" a husband (007-096). A mother who lost her daughter was devastated, and the community recognized the depth of these attachments built through the courtship process.

> He threw his robe over her head while he whispered in her ear:
> "Be my wife. I have little but I am young and strong. I will treat you
> well, for I love you."
>
> (007-005–007-010)

## Cultural Significance

- Maiden's consent was meaningful — refusal was respected, and deliberation was part of the process
- Honesty about poverty was valued over false promises in suitor declarations
- Parental consent was required, but a daughter's preference could override status considerations
- Polygyny existed but produced familial tension, particularly around jealousy
- Marriage served inter-tribal diplomacy — unions crossed band and tribal lines
- Gift exchange marked the formalization of the union
