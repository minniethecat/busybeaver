## Overview

The **eagle** was the most revered bird in Sioux culture — a sacred creature whose feathers marked the highest honors, whose fat was used in ritual healing, and whose spirit-form, the **red eagle**, appeared as a formidable supernatural entity that terrorized villages. Young men practiced their archery on eagles to prove their skill, and eagle imagery permeated war regalia, ceremony, and narrative. The eagle was not merely hunted but was approached with spiritual respect.

## In the Stories

### The Red Eagle Spirit

The most dramatic eagle narrative appears in the White Plume story. A powerful spirit "in the form of a red eagle has driven all the birds of the country" (035-092), and "every day this eagle circles above the village" (035-093) in menace. White Plume's bow-test — shooting "the eagle, and again he shot and the arrow went through the eagle's body" (035-277) — proved his supernatural prowess. After his victory, the proclamation: "The red eagle shall be used to decorate the head of the one who wins" (035-281), cementing the eagle as the highest war honor.

> ...another evil spirit in the form of a red eagle has driven all the birds of the
> air out of our country. Every day this eagle circles above the village,
> and so powerful is it that anyone being caught outside of his tent
> is descended upon and his skull split open to the brain by the sharp...
>
> (035-092–035-093)

### Eagle as Archery Test

Young warriors practiced their marksmanship on eagles and hawks: a boy "would practice on eagles and hawks, and never an eagle or hawk came within his range that he did not bring down" (035-028). This practice served dual purposes — it honed combat skills and provided feathers for ceremonial use. The hunter who could down eagles "did not need to use his painted arrows" (035-142) even for the most difficult shots, showing eagle-hunting as a mark of exceptional skill.

### Eagle Fat as Medicine

The eagle had medicinal and ritual uses beyond its feathers. A spirit instructed: "in your bed you will find a piece of eagle fat. Build a fire and melt the fat" (030-294), then apply it as a healing unguent. Eagle fat was potent medicine, its power derived from the eagle's sacred status — consuming or applying eagle substances transferred the bird's spiritual force to the recipient.

> "At the foot of my bed you will find a piece of eagle fat. Build a fire and
> melt it for me. I will drink it and then we can go."
>
> Chaske quickly started a fire, got out the piece of fat and melted it.
> She drank it at one draught, and was about to arise when she suddenly...
>
> (030-294–030-296)

### The Eagle Feather Centerpiece

The regalia of war emphasized the eagle feather above all others: a "magnificent center eagle feather" (022-077) adorned a rawhide-covered bow, positioned as the centerpiece. The eagle feather was not one ornament among many but the focal point, the feather that declared its wearer's status unambiguously.

## Cultural Significance

- The **red eagle** was a spirit-being powerful enough to drive away all other birds — a supernatural adversary requiring a hero to defeat
- Eagle **fat** served as ritual medicine, melted and applied for healing
- Eagle **hunting** was the ultimate test of archery — "never an eagle came within his range that he did not bring down"
- The **center eagle feather** was the premier war honor, reserved for the most distinguished warriors
- Eagle imagery in archery contests and war regalia tied martial achievement directly to this sacred bird
