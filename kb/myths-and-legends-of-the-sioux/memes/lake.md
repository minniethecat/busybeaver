## Overview

The **lake** (mde or ble) in Sioux folklore is a body of still water that holds both danger and mystery. It is "the big lake" (019-015) into which despairing frogs throw themselves, a "little lake, where the tribe always" (025-003) gathers turtles for ceremony. A muskrat "in the lake below lived" (008-003) in his underwater dwelling, proof that the lake has its own society beneath the surface. Warriors wash themselves in the lake after battle (016-063), and an artichoke "on the shore of a lake stood" (008-001) as sentinel. Lakes are thresholds — between the breathing world and the drowned, between the tribe's needs and the creatures that live beneath.

## In the Stories

Chapter 008 opens with a surreal image: "On the shore of a lake stood an artichoke with its" (008-001) — a plant-person stationed at the water's edge. "In the lake below lived a muskrat in his" (008-003) home, the underwater world as real and populous as the shore.

In Chapter 019, frogs, "decided to do; so going to the lake they were about to jump" (019-016) in mass suicide. "All going over to the big lake and drowning ourselves" (019-015) — the lake receives death collectively, its surface closing over the desperate without distinction. The other frogs see their companions "jumping into the lake" (019-018) and the image lingers as one of the collection's darkest moments.

Chapter 025 shows a different lake: "turtles would be at the little lake, where the tribe always" (025-003) gathered them. Scouts are sent "to the lake to ascertain whether" (025-031) the turtles have arrived. "On drawing near the lake he" (025-006) approached carefully — the lake is anticipated, approached with purpose. A turtle, spotted, "jumped into the lake with a great" (025-012) splash, the water receiving another creature.

A warrior in Chapter 016 goes "down to the lake and washed himself" (016-063) — the lake as purifier, cleansing the body after violence.

## Cultural Significance

- **Lake as inhabited depth**: The muskrat's underwater dwelling (008-003) gives the lake an unseen population — the surface conceals a world
- **Lake as collective grave**: Frogs drowning themselves (019-015) make the lake a place of mass death, its stillness the stillness of the dead
- **Lake as sacred site**: The turtle lake (025-003) is known, named, and returned to — a fixed point in tribal geography
- **Lake as boundary**: The artichoke on the shore (008-001) marks the line between land and water, between two orders of being


Travelers pause at a mysterious lake before their journey:

> lake at the foot of a green knoll that rose from its shore. The knoll
> was covered with green grass and somehow as they looked at it they had

> uncanny.
