## Overview

The **Two Widows** are sisters living in a remote part of a great forest, each with a baby to care for. Their story is one of deception: the trickster **Unktomi** (Spider) visits their tent bearing red plums and tricks them into leaving their children behind to search for a phantom plum grove. The tale serves as a cautionary narrative about trust and the dangers posed by cunning strangers — a recurring theme in the Unktomi cycle of Sioux folklore.

## In the Stories

### The Unktomi (Spider), Two Widows and the Red Plums (Chapter 038)

The two sisters live in isolation, tending their babies in a remote forest dwelling (038-001–038-002). Their quiet life is disrupted when Unktomi arrives with a handful of red plums and a scheme (038-001–038-005):

> There once lived, in a remote part of a great forest, two widowed sisters, with their little babies. One day there came to their tent a visitor who was called Unktomi (spider). He had found some nice red plums during his wanderings in the forest, and he said to himself, "I will keep these plums and fool the two widows with them." (038-001–038-005)

Unktomi presents the plums to the unsuspecting sisters, who are astonished by the fruit's beauty (038-005–038-008):

> After the widows had bidden him be seated, he presented them with the plums. On seeing them they exclaimed "hi nu, hi nu (an exclamation of surprise), where did you get those fine plums?" (038-005–038-010)

Deviously, Unktomi points to a crimson-tipped cloud on the horizon and claims it reflects a vast patch of plums growing beneath it:

> Unktomi arose and pointing to a crimson tipped cloud, said: "You see that red cloud? Directly underneath it is a patch of plums. So large is the patch and so red and beautiful are the plums that it is the reflection of them on the cloud that you see." (038-010–038-015)

The widows, eager to gather the fruit, entrust their babies to Unktomi — who had claimed kinship with everyone he met — and set off on a fruitless quest (038-013–038-019). The story warns of the trickster's ability to exploit desire, and the widows' plight illustrates the vulnerability of those isolated from the protection of the larger community.
