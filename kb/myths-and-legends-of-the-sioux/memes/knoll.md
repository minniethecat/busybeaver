## Overview

The **knoll** (paha) in Sioux geography is a small, rounded hill — distinct from the mountain or butte in its modest scale. In the stories, the knoll draws the eye and invites approach. Children race toward it (007-029), hunters use it as lookout (030-190), and trails end mysteriously at its base (026-040). The knoll is intimate rather than imposing: a green rise "that rose from its shore" (007-024), a neighboring height "to a different one" (010-043), a place where smoke "ascending from a little knoll" (037-096) signals habitation or spirit presence.

## In the Stories

The most vivid knoll sequence opens Chapter 007: a lake stands "at the foot of a green knoll that rose from its shore" (007-024). A child, "gazing at the knoll" (007-029), challenges his friend: "Let's run and" (007-029) race to it. They run toward "the knoll" (007-035), and "having reached the top of the knoll all" (007-036) seems well — until "suddenly they stopped — the knoll had begun to move" (007-038). The knoll itself becomes animate, a living landscape feature that rises and travels, transforming play into wonder.

In Chapter 010, a woman watches from a "neighboring knoll" (010-034) as figures approach, then retreats "again to a neighboring knoll, though to a different one" (010-043) — the knoll as observation post and refuge, its rounded summit offering concealment behind its curve.

In Chapter 026, a tracker follows a trail until "he came to a very small knoll" (026-040) where "the trail ended" — the knoll as terminus, the place where pursuit meets mystery. In Chapter 030, a warrior calls others to "come over to the high knoll" (030-190); they "started for the high knoll" (030-237) as a rendezvous point. In Chapter 037, smoke seen "ascending from a little knoll" (037-096) draws a traveler near — the knoll as dwelling place, a "little" rise that holds a home within its bank.

## Cultural Significance

- **Knoll as play-space**: Children race to knolls (007-029), their modest height inviting competition rather than fear
- **Knoll as animate**: The moving knoll (007-038) embodies the Sioux sense of a landscape that is alive and responsive
- **Knoll as vantage**: Warriors and women alike use the knoll to see without being seen (010-034, 030-190)
- **Knoll as endpoint**: Trails end at knolls (026-040), marking them as significant points in the terrain
