[026-001]In a deep forest, far from the villages of his people, lived a hermit.

[026-002]His tent was made of buffalo skins, and his dress was made of deer skin.

[026-003]Far from the haunts of any human being this old hermit was content to

[026-004]spend his days.

[026-005]All day long he would wander through the forest studying the different

[026-006]plants of nature and collecting precious roots, which he used as

[026-007]medicine. At long intervals some warrior would arrive at the tent of

[026-008]the old hermit and get medicine roots from him for the tribe, the old

[026-009]hermit’s medicine being considered far superior to all others.

[026-010]After a long day’s ramble in the woods, the hermit came home late, and

[026-011]being very tired, at once lay down on his bed and was just dozing off

[026-012]to sleep, when he felt something rub against his foot. Awakening with a

[026-013]start, he noticed a dark object and an arm was extended to him, holding

[026-014]in its hand a flint pointed arrow.

[026-015]The hermit thought, “This must be a spirit, as there is no human being

[026-016]around here but myself!” A voice then said: “Hermit, I have come to

[026-017]invite you to my home.” “How (yes), I will come,” said the old hermit.

[026-018]Wherewith he arose, wrapped his robe about him and followed.

[026-019]Outside the door he stopped and looked around, but could see no signs of

[026-020]the dark object.

[026-021]“Whoever you are, or whatever you be, wait for me, as I don’t know

[026-022]where to go to find your house,” said the hermit. Not an answer did

[026-023]he receive, nor could he hear any noises as though anyone was walking

[026-024]through the brush. Re-entering his tent he retired and was soon fast

[026-025]asleep. The next night the same thing occurred again, and the hermit

[026-026]followed the object out, only to be left as before.

[026-027]He was very angry to think that anyone should be trying to make sport of

[026-028]him, and he determined to find out who this could be who was disturbing

[026-029]his night’s rest.

[026-030]The next evening he cut a hole in the tent large enough to stick an

[026-031]arrow through, and stood by the door watching. Soon the dark object came

[026-032]and stopped outside of the door, and said: “Grandfather, I came to--,”

[026-033]but he never finished the sentence, for the old man let go his arrow,

[026-034]and he heard the arrow strike something which produced a sound as though

[026-035]he had shot into a sack of pebbles. He did not go out that night to see

[026-036]what his arrow had struck, but early next morning he went out and looked

[026-037]at the spot about where he thought the object had stood. There on the

[026-038]ground lay a little heap of corn, and from this little heap a small line

[026-039]of corn lay scattered along a path. This he followed far into the woods.

[026-040]When he came to a very small knoll the trail ended. At the end of the

[026-041]trail was a large circle, from which the grass had been scraped off

[026-042]clean.

[026-043]“The corn trail stops at the edge of this circle,” said the old man, “so

[026-044]this must be the home of whoever it was that invited me.” He took his

[026-045]bone knife and hatchet and proceeded to dig down into the center of the

[026-046]circle. When he had got down to the length of his arm, he came to a sack

[026-047]of dried meat. Next he found a sack of Indian turnips, then a sack of

[026-048]dried cherries; then a sack of corn, and last of all another sack, empty

[026-049]except that there was about a cupful of corn in one corner of it, and

[026-050]that the sack had a hole in the other corner where his arrow had pierced

[026-051]it. From this hole in the sack the corn was scattered along the trail,

[026-052]which guided the old man to the cache.*

[026-053]From this the hermit taught the tribes how to keep their provisions when

[026-054]traveling and were overloaded. He explained to them how they should dig

[026-055]a pit and put their provisions into it and cover them with earth. By

[026-056]this method the Indians used to keep provisions all summer, and when

[026-057]fall came they would return to their cache, and on opening it would find

[026-058]everything as fresh as the day they were placed there.

[026-059]The old hermit was also thanked as the discoverer of corn, which had

[026-060]never been known to the Indians until discovered by the old hermit.

[026-061]*Hiding place.
