## Overview

**Mourning** in Sioux folklore is not private grief but public ritual. When a death occurs, "she cut off her hair for mourning and all her little girl" (003-022) — the body is physically marked. A "mourning feast for the little rabbit" (003-027) is held — grief is fed, shared, and structured around food. A widow "went in mourning for her husband a long time" (017-002), the duration of mourning being socially prescribed. "Great was the mourning in the Crow village" (024-100) — mourning scales to fill entire communities. And crucially, mourning has an end: "put aside your mourning and try and enjoy a" (033-020) life again — grief is a season, not a permanent state. "You have mourned long" (033-020) — the community decides when mourning is complete.

## In the Stories

### The Rituals of Mourning

> wept bitterly. She cut off her hair for mourning and all her little girl friends wailed with her. Her mother, too, mourned with them.
>
> (003-022)

Mourning begins with physical transformation: hair is cut (003-022), the body marked by loss. A "mourning feast for the little rabbit" (003-027) follows — the community gathers around food, grief given structure and ceremony. The mother who "had her robe drawn over her head, mourning for her" (015-004) child — the covered head signaling to all that she is in mourning, her state visible and legible to the community.

### The Duration of Mourning

Mourning has a prescribed duration. A widow "went in mourning for her husband a long time" (017-002). Eventually, "I am tired of mourning for my husband and caring for" (017-009) children alone — the emotional exhaustion of protracted mourning. The decision to "lay aside my mourning and paint myself" (017-014) is transformative — paint replaces grief, visibility replaces the covered head. "She painted and decked herself" (017-014) — the end of mourning is marked by beautification, the return to social visibility.

### Community Mourning

Mourning scales to entire communities: "great was the mourning in the Crow village" (024-100) after a warrior's death. "There was great mourning over" (007-091) the loss of a youth — the community grieves collectively. "There was great mourning in the camp" (033-009) — the entire camp participates. Mourning is not solitude but solidarity.

### The End of Mourning

> in vain to have them set aside their mourning. “You have mourned long enough,” they would say. “Put aside your mourning and try and enjoy a
>
> (033-020)

Crucially, mourning is finite. Elders tell the bereaved "to have them set aside their mourning" (033-020). "'You have mourned long enough,' they would say. 'Put aside your mourning and try and enjoy a'" (033-020-021) life. The community that authorizes mourning also authorizes its end. Yet some resist: "the old couple continued their mourning for their lost idol" (033-027) — mourning can persist beyond its social authorization, becoming a form of devotion or stubbornness.

### Mourning as Deception

A warrior "told the parents to go in mourning for their son, as he" (023-151) was reportedly dead — but the report may be false. Mourning can be premature, weaponized, or manipulated. The command to mourn is an act of power — who can tell whom to grieve?

## Cultural Significance

- **Mourning as visible transformation**: Cut hair, covered heads, unpainted faces — mourning is written on the body and legible to all
- **Mourning as communal event**: The mourning feast (003-027) and village-wide mourning (024-100) show grief as collective ritual, not private emotion
- **Prescribed duration**: Mourning has a socially defined length; the community that initiates mourning also terminates it (033-020)
- **The end as beautification**: "Lay aside my mourning and paint myself" (017-014) — the end of mourning is the return of beauty, color, and social participation
- **Forced mourning**: Being told to mourn (023-151) reveals that mourning could be commanded, not just felt — a social obligation as much as an emotional state
