[001-001]An Arikara woman was once gathering corn from the field to store away

[001-002]for winter use. She passed from stalk to stalk, tearing off the ears and

[001-003]dropping them into her folded robe. When all was gathered she started to

[001-004]go, when she heard a faint voice, like a child’s, weeping and calling:

[001-005]“Oh, do not leave me! Do not go away without me.”

[001-006]The woman was astonished. “What child can that be?” she asked herself.

[001-007]“What babe can be lost in the cornfield?”

[001-008]She set down her robe in which she had tied up her corn, and went back

[001-009]to search; but she found nothing.

[001-010]As she started away she heard the voice again:

[001-011]“Oh, do not leave me. Do not go away without me.”

[001-012]She searched for a long time. At last in one corner of the field, hidden

[001-013]under the leaves of the stalks, she found one little ear of corn. This

[001-014]it was that had been crying, and this is why all Indian women have since

[001-015]garnered their corn crop very carefully, so that the succulent food

[001-016]product should not even to the last small nubbin be neglected or wasted,

[001-017]and thus displease the Great Mystery.
