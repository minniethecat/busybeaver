## Overview

The **Old Hermit** is a revered figure in Sioux folklore — a solitary medicine man who lived deep in the forest, far from the villages of his people. His story appears in "The Hermit, or the Gift of Corn." Through a supernatural encounter with a mysterious dark spirit, the hermit becomes the discoverer of [[corn]] and the teacher of food preservation to the Sioux tribes. He represents wisdom obtained through solitude and the gifts that come from patient observation of nature.

## In the Stories

### The Hermit, or the Gift of Corn (026-001)

The hermit dwelt alone in the deep forest, his tent made of buffalo skins and his clothing of deer skin. He spent his days studying the plants of the forest and gathering roots for medicine:

> "In a deep forest, far from the villages of his people, lived a hermit. His tent was made of buffalo skins, and his dress was made of deer skin. Far from the haunts of any human being this old hermit was content to spend his days." (026-001–026-003)

His medicine was highly prized — warriors occasionally traveled to his remote tent seeking his healing roots, for his remedies were considered far superior to all others.

#### The Spirit's Visitation

After a long day of gathering, the hermit was awoken by a dark figure that extended an arm holding a flint-pointed arrow. The figure spoke, inviting the hermit to its home. Suspicious after being led into the dark twice without finding the host, the hermit devised a plan. The third night, he cut a hole in his tent and shot an arrow at the dark visitor:

> "The next evening he cut a hole in the tent large enough to stick an arrow through, and stood by the door watching. Soon the dark object came and stopped outside of the door... The old man let go his arrow, and he heard the arrow strike something which produced a sound as though he had shot into a sack of pebbles." (026-020–026-025)

#### Discovery of Corn

The next morning, the hermit found a small heap of corn where the figure had stood, with a trail of scattered kernels leading into the woods. Following the trail to a small knoll, he dug into a cleared circle and uncovered a buried cache:

> "When he had got down to the length of his arm, he came to a sack of dried meat. Next he found a sack of Indian turnips, then a sack of dried cherries; then a sack of corn, and last of all another sack, empty except that there was about a cupful of corn in one corner of it, and that the sack had a hole in the other corner where his arrow had pierced it." (026-040–026-048)

From this discovery, the hermit taught the tribes how to cache provisions underground — digging pits and covering them with earth to preserve food through the summer:

> "From this the hermit taught the tribes how to keep their provisions when traveling and were overloaded. He explained to them how they should dig a pit and put their provisions into it and cover them with earth." (026-053–026-055)

The old hermit was thereafter honored as the discoverer of corn, which had been unknown to the Sioux before his encounter.
