## Overview

The **Medicine Woman** appears in the Sioux tale "Little Brave and the Medicine Woman" (Chapter 016) — not as a living character, but as a deceased healer whose grave becomes central to the story's events. When a hunter stumbles upon her sunken grave, the village elders recall her burial and attribute the hunter's fall to her lingering spiritual power. The grave later becomes the setting for a prank by a boy named Brave, who terrifies his playmates by pretending to be her risen corpse. Her role in the tale reflects the Sioux belief that the dead retain influence over the living and that burial sites demand respect.

## In the Stories

### Little Brave and the Medicine Woman (Chapter 016)

A village moves to winter camp on high ground overlooking a lake. Near the camp, choke cherry bushes conceal an old, sunken grave:

> A village of Indians moved out of winter camp and pitched their tents in a circle on high land overlooking a lake. A little way down the declivity was a grave. Choke cherries had grown up, hiding the grave from view. But as the ground had sunk somewhat, the grave was marked by a slight hollow. (016-001–016-005)

A hunter taking a shortcut stumbles upon the grave and cannot cross it without falling. Reporting the incident to the village, the old men identify its occupant:

> One of the villagers going out to hunt took a short cut through the choke cherry bushes. As he pushed them aside he saw the hollow grave, but thought it was a washout made by the rains. But as he essayed to step over it, to his great surprise he stumbled and fell. Made curious by his mishap, he drew back and tried again; but again he fell. When he came back to the village he told the old men what had happened to him. They remembered then that a long time before there had been buried there a medicine woman or conjurer. Doubtless it was her medicine that made him stumble. (016-006–016-014)

The Medicine Woman does not appear as a living character in the tale. Her presence is felt only through the spiritual power still emanating from her grave — enough to trip a strong hunter and enough to terrify the village children. Her "medicine" (spiritual power) endures beyond death, a testament to the respect accorded to healers and conjurers in Sioux tradition.
