## Overview

**Chaske** (meaning "first born" in the Sioux language) is the noble-hearted son of a leading war chief. He appears as the central protagonist in "Story of the Two Young Friends." Unlike his wealthy peers, Chaske judges people by their character rather than their appearance — choosing the orphan [[hake]] as his lifelong friend over the vain sons of rich families. His defining act of sacrifice — giving up his beloved Pretty Feather so that Hake may have her — exemplifies the highest Sioux ideals of friendship, honor, and generosity.

## In the Stories

### Story of the Two Young Friends (030-001)

#### A Friend Chosen by Character

Chaske was born into wealth and status as the son of a great war chief, but from an early age he showed a discerning nature:

> "Chaske did not look at the clothes of any boy whom he chose as a friend, but mingled with all boys regardless of how they were clad, and would study their dispositions. The well dressed he found were vain and conceited. The fairly well dressed he found selfish and spiteful. The poorly clad he found to be generous and truthful, and from all of them he chose 'Hake' for his 'Koda' (friend)." (030-009–030-014)

His choice of the orphan Hake was a radical act in a status-conscious society. It was a lucky day for Hake, who was adopted into Chaske's family and given fine clothing. The two grew inseparable.

#### The Sacrifice of Love

As young men, both friends fell in love. Chaske loved Pretty Feather, the daughter of the great [[medicine-man]], and courted her with his friend's help. But when the truth emerged — that Pretty Feather loved Hake, not him — Chaske made an extraordinary sacrifice:

> "'My friend, we have been together for the last twenty years and I have yet the first time to deceive you in any way... The only brother's love I know is yours. The only sister's love I will know will be Pretty Feather's, for brother, last night she told me she loved none but you and would marry you and you only. So, brother, I am going to take my antelope to my sister-in-law's tent and deposit it at her door.'" (030-074–030-088)

> "So now, brother, take the flower of the nation for your wife, and I will be content to continue through life a lonely bachelor, as never again can I give any woman the place which Pretty Feather had in my heart." (030-091–030-095)

#### Bravery on the Warpath

Before the wedding, the two friends embarked on a warpath journey against the enemy, seeking glory. At Ghost Creek they encountered the restless spirit of a slain warrior who sought revenge against his cowardly killer. Chaske and Hake bravely listened to the spirit's plea and agreed to help:

> "'We will,' said the friends in one voice." (030-159)

They ambushed the old coward who had killed the spirit, then pursued two enemy riders across the plain. Chaske pursued the one on the right and struck him down. When his ammunition ran out, Chaske showed true nobility — capturing rather than killing his disarmed opponent:

> "Not so. Chaske was too honorable and noble to kill an unarmed man, and especially one who had put up such a brave fight as had this man." (030-321–030-323)

#### Rescue of Pretty Feather

Returning to find the village moved and Pretty Feather left for dead, Chaske alone discovered her in a death-like trance and revived her. He then fought a lone duel against two enemy scouts, killing one and capturing the other, before escorting her safely to the village. He presented the captive as a wedding gift to Hake, speaking of the two hardest fights of his life:

> "The hardest fight of my life was when I gave up Pretty Feather. You have them both. To the Toka (enemy) be kind, and he will do all your biddings. To Pretty Feather be a good husband." (030-355–030-360)

So saying, Chaske lived the remainder of his days a confirmed bachelor — true to his word and his code of honor.
