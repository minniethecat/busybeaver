[025-001]A boy went on a turtle hunt, and after following the different streams

[025-002]for hours, finally came to the conclusion that the only place he would

[025-003]find any turtles would be at the little lake, where the tribe always

[025-004]hunted them.

[025-005]So, leaving the stream he had been following, he cut across country to

[025-006]the lake. On drawing near the lake he crawled on his hands and knees in

[025-007]order not to be seen by the turtles, who were very watchful, as they had

[025-008]been hunted so much. Peeping over the rock he saw a great many out on

[025-009]the shore sunning themselves, so he very cautiously undressed, so

[025-010]he could leap into the water and catch them before they secreted

[025-011]themselves. But on pulling off his shirt one of his hands was held up

[025-012]so high that the turtles saw it and jumped into the lake with a great

[025-013]splash.

[025-014]The boy ran to the shore, but saw only bubbles coming up from the

[025-015]bottom. Directly the boy saw something coming to the surface, and soon

[025-016]it came up into sight. It was a little man, and soon others, by the

[025-017]hundreds, came up and swam about, splashing the water up into the air to

[025-018]a great height. So scared was the boy that he never stopped to gather

[025-019]up his clothes but ran home naked and fell into his grandmother’s tent

[025-020]door.

[025-021]“What is the trouble, grandchild,” cried the old woman. But the boy

[025-022]could not answer. “Did you see anything unnatural?” He shook his head,

[025-023]“no.” He made signs to the grandmother that his lungs were pressing so

[025-024]hard against his sides that he could not talk. He kept beating his side

[025-025]with his clenched hands. The grandmother got out her medicine bag,

[025-026]made a prayer to the Great Spirit to drive out the evil spirit that had

[025-027]entered her grandson’s body, and after she had applied the medicine, the

[025-028]prayer must have been heard and answered, as the boy commenced telling

[025-029]her what he had heard and seen.

[025-030]The grandmother went to the chief’s tent and told what her grandson had

[025-031]seen. The chief sent two brave warriors to the lake to ascertain whether

[025-032]it was true or not. The two warriors crept to the little hill close to

[025-033]the lake, and there, sure enough, the lake was swarming with little men

[025-034]swimming about, splashing the water high up into the air. The warriors,

[025-035]too, were scared and hurried home, and in the council called on their

[025-036]return told what they had seen. The boy was brought to the council

[025-037]and given the seat of honor (opposite the door), and was named “Wankan

[025-038]Wanyanka” (sees holy).

[025-039]The lake had formerly borne the name of Truth Lake, but from this time

[025-040]on was called “Wicasa-bde”--Man Lake.
