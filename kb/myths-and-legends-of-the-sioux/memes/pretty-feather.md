## Overview

**Pretty Feather** is the daughter of an old medicine man and the central female figure in "The Story of the Two Young Friends" (Chapter 030). She is courted by many young men in the village, but her heart belongs to the orphan Hake — a choice that tests the bond between Hake and his lifelong friend Chaske, the chief's son who also loved her. Her role in the narrative embodies the Sioux ideal of a woman whose worth is recognized through the sacrifices men are willing to make for her.

## In the Stories

### The Story of the Two Young Friends (Chapter 030)

Pretty Feather was much courted by the young men of the village. Chaske, the son of the leading war chief, loved her deeply and enlisted his friend Hake to help win her affection. But the girl's choice fell upon Hake himself:

> The girl's choice was Chaske's friend Hake. Although he had never made love to her for himself, he had always used honeyed words to her and was always loud in his praises for his friend Chaske. (030-036–030-038)

Hake, though he loved Pretty Feather in return, declared he must first prove himself on the warpath before he could marry the medicine man's daughter (030-097–030-098).

#### The Offering and Acceptance

When Hake returned from a successful war party, he offered gifts to Pretty Feather's mother. The offering — a fine spotted horse — was accepted, signifying the mother's blessing on the match:

> The mother of Pretty Feather did not know whether to take the horse or not, but Pretty Feather, seeing by this offering that Hake was serious in his intentions, accepted. (030-106–030-107)

#### Hake's Sacrifice

Despite winning Pretty Feather's love, Hake ultimately chose friendship over romance. The deepest pain of his life came from giving up Pretty Feather for his friend Chaske:

> "The saddest, the most woeful day of my life was when I gave up Pretty Feather." (030-371)

In the end, he urged Chaske: "To Pretty Feather be a good husband" (030-373).

## Character Significance

Pretty Feather represents more than a romantic figure — she is the catalyst through which the story explores the Sioux values of friendship, sacrifice, and honor. Her choice of the impoverished orphan over the wealthy chief's son affirms that character, not status, determines worth. And the willingness of both men to sacrifice for her — Hake through his warpath deeds, and ultimately through giving her up — elevates her from mere love interest to the measure by which warrior virtue is tested.
