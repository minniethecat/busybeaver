## Overview

**Ceremonial smoking** was far more than a casual habit in Sioux culture — it was a structured social and spiritual practice. Before councils, journeys, or important decisions, participants sat together and smoked. The pipe was passed, the smoke rose through the lodge's square opening, and the act of shared smoking created bonds of trust and sacred witness. The practice was distinct from the [[peace-pipe]] as an object — ceremonial smoking was the _action_, the ritual behavior that activated the pipe's sacred function.

## In the Stories

### Smoking Before Council

The most formal expression of ceremonial smoking was the pre-council smoke. When a warrior visited the bear chief, they "sat together awhile and smoked" (009-021) before any business was discussed. This established a shared space of trust and deliberation. The smoking created a pause — time for reflection before speech — that was integral to Sioux decision-making.

### The Peace Smoke

Unktomi Tanka, after his machinations, "finished his peace smoke" (021-044) — a phrase suggesting that peace-making and smoking were conceptually fused. The peace smoke was a distinct ceremonial form, associated with resolving conflicts and establishing alliances. To smoke together was to commit to peace.

### Smoking Before Travel

Before setting out on a journey, warriors observed the smoking ritual: "Let us sit down and have a smoke before we start back" (030-071). Chaske and his companion smoked before mounting their ponies for the return journey (030-074). The smoke was both a practical pause and a ritual safeguard — seeking protection before undertaking travel through potentially hostile territory.

### The Shared Pipe

The mechanics of ceremonial smoking involved a shared pipe: when visitors arrived at a lodge, "after they had smoked a pipe and knocked out the ashes" (017-127), the ceremony was complete. The knocking-out of ashes marked the formal conclusion. "Their pipes being smoked out they mounted their ponies" (030-091) — the transition from ritual space to travel space was marked by the pipe's emptying.

### Smoking as Contemplation

Smoking could be a solitary contemplative act. A character "commenced to smoke, wondering how the old woman" (037-197) managed her tricks, using the smoking pause to think. The "smoke he arose to pretend to go" (037-199) — smoking provided cover for observation and strategy. A dish turned away and "smoked in silence" (033-057) during a tense domestic moment, the smoking ritual serving as a face-saving pause.

## Cultural Significance

- Ceremonial smoking was a structured ritual, not casual consumption — it marked formal transitions
- The shared pipe created a bond of trust; to smoke together was to commit to peace
- Smoking preceded councils, journeys, and important decisions — a ritual pause for reflection
- The knocking-out of ashes marked the formal conclusion of the ceremony
- Pipe smoking was distinct from the pipe as object — ceremonial smoking was the ritual _action_
- Solitary smoking served as contemplative cover, allowing observation and strategic thought
