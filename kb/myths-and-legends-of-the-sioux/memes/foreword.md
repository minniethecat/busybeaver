[P02-001]In publishing these “Myths of the Sioux,” I deem it proper to state that

[P02-002]I am of one-fourth Sioux blood. My maternal grandfather, Captain Duncan

[P02-003]Graham, a Scotchman by birth, who had seen service in the British Army,

[P02-004]was one of a party of Scotch Highlanders who in 1811 arrived in the

[P02-005]British Northwest by way of York Factory, Hudson Bay, to found what was

[P02-006]known as the Selkirk Colony, near Lake Winnipeg, now within the

[P02-007]province of Manitoba, Canada. Soon after his arrival at Lake Winnipeg he

[P02-008]proceeded up the Red River of the North and the western fork thereof

[P02-009]to its source, and thence down the Minnesota River to Mendota, the

[P02-010]confluence of the Minnesota and Mississippi Rivers, where he located. My

[P02-011]grandmother, Ha-za-ho-ta-win, was a full-blood of the Medawakanton Band

[P02-012]of the Sioux Tribe of Indians. My father, Joseph Buisson, born near

[P02-013]Montreal, Canada, was connected with the American Fur Company, with

[P02-014]headquarters at Mendota, Minnesota, which point was for many years the

[P02-015]chief distributing depot of the American Fur Company, from which the

[P02-016]Indian trade conducted by that company on the upper Mississippi was

[P02-017]directed.

[P02-018]I was born December 8, 1842, at Wabasha, Minnesota, then Indian country,

[P02-019]and resided thereat until fourteen years of age, when I was sent to

[P02-020]school at Prairie du Chien, Wisconsin.

[P02-021]I was married to Major James McLaughlin at Mendota, Minnesota,

[P02-022]January 28, 1864, and resided in Minnesota until July 1, 1871, when I

[P02-023]accompanied my husband to Devils Lake Agency, North Dakota, then Dakota

[P02-024]Territory, where I remained ten years in most friendly relations with

[P02-025]the Indians of that agency. My husband was Indian agent at Devils Lake

[P02-026]Agency, and in 1881 was transferred to Standing Rock, on the Missouri

[P02-027]River, then a very important agency, to take charge of the Sioux who

[P02-028]had then but recently surrendered to the military authorities, and been

[P02-029]brought by steamboat from various points on the upper Missouri, to be

[P02-030]permanently located on the Standing Rock reservation.

[P02-031]Having been born and reared in an Indian community, I at an early age

[P02-032]acquired a thorough knowledge of the Sioux language, and having lived on

[P02-033]Indian reservations for the past forty years in a position which brought

[P02-034]me very near to the Indians, whose confidence I possessed, I have,

[P02-035]therefore, had exceptional opportunities of learning the legends and

[P02-036]folk-lore of the Sioux.

[P02-037]The stories contained in this little volume were told me by the older

[P02-038]men and women of the Sioux, of which I made careful notes as related,

[P02-039]knowing that, if not recorded, these fairy tales would be lost to

[P02-040]posterity by the passing of the primitive Indian.

[P02-041]The notes of a song or a strain of music coming to us through the night

[P02-042]not only give us pleasure by the melody they bring, but also give us

[P02-043]knowledge of the character of the singer or of the instrument from which

[P02-044]they proceed. There is something in the music which unerringly tells us

[P02-045]of its source. I believe musicians call it the “timbre” of the sound. It

[P02-046]is independent of, and different from, both pitch and rhythm; it is the

[P02-047]texture of the music itself.

[P02-048]The “timbre” of a people’s stories tells of the qualities of that

[P02-049]people’s heart. It is the texture of the thought, independent of its

[P02-050]form or fashioning, which tells the quality of the mind from which it

[P02-051]springs.

[P02-052]In the “timbre” of these stories of the Sioux, told in the lodges and

[P02-053]at the camp fires of the past, and by the firesides of the Dakotas of

[P02-054]today, we recognize the very texture of the thought of a simple, grave,

[P02-055]and sincere people, living in intimate contact and friendship with the

[P02-056]big out-of-doors that we call Nature; a race not yet understanding all

[P02-057]things, not proud and boastful, but honest and childlike and fair; a

[P02-058]simple, sincere, and gravely thoughtful people, willing to believe that

[P02-059]there may be in even the everyday things of life something not yet fully

[P02-060]understood; a race that can, without any loss of native dignity, gravely

[P02-061]consider the simplest things, seeking to fathom their meaning and to

[P02-062]learn their lesson--equally without vain-glorious boasting and trifling

[P02-063]cynicism; an earnest, thoughtful, dignified, but simple and primitive

[P02-064]people.

[P02-065]To the children of any race these stories can not fail to give pleasure

[P02-066]by their vivid imaging of the simple things and creatures of the great

[P02-067]out-of-doors and the epics of their doings. They will also give an

[P02-068]intimate insight into the mentality of an interesting race at a most

[P02-069]interesting stage of development, which is now fast receding into the

[P02-070]mists of the past.

[P02-071]MARIE L. McLAUGHLIN (Mrs. James McLaughlin).

[P02-072]McLaughlin, S. D., May 1, 1913.
