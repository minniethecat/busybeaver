## Overview

The **Muskrat** is a personified animal character in a brief Sioux tale of pride and self-regard. Living in a tepee beneath a lake, the Muskrat encounters the **Artichoke** growing on the shore above. The two engage in a verbal contest, each claiming superiority — the Artichoke for its blossoms, the Muskrat for its fine fur and the value that humans place upon it. The tale illustrates the folly of vanity and the Sioux tradition of teaching moral lessons through animal characters.

## In the Stories

### The Artichoke and the Muskrat (Chapter 008)

The Muskrat lives beneath the waters of a lake, making its home in a tepee below the surface:

> In the lake below lived a muskrat in his tepee, and in the world above grew an artichoke by the water's edge. (008-003)

When the Artichoke boasts of its beautiful blossoms, the Muskrat counters with pride in its own lineage:

> "I am the muskrat, and I, too, belong to a large family. I live in the water, and my fur keeps me warm in the coldest winter." (008-009)

The Artichoke mocks the Muskrat's appearance, but the Muskrat responds with wounded pride:

> "You are jealous of my fine fur," sneered the muskrat. "I may build my home beneath the water, but I am warm and comfortable there." (008-013)

Ultimately, the Muskrat asserts its value in the eyes of humans, who prize its fur for trade and use:

> "That is true," said the muskrat. "But men think well of me, and my fur is valued among them for its warmth and beauty." (008-017)

The encounter between the Artichoke and the Muskrat serves as a gentle moral fable about pride and the different kinds of worth — the Artichoke's transient beauty above ground versus the Muskrat's practical value and hidden home below.
