## Overview

The **river** (wakpa) in Sioux folklore is not merely a geographic feature — it is a threshold between worlds. A grieving lover "sadly walked to the river, sprang in, and lying down in" (007-075) the water, transforming from man to fish. The river receives the desperate: "the girl went down to the river" (017-027) for clandestine meetings. A traveler "came to a small river" (020-083) at a moment of narrative transition. The riverbank is where women gather water (014-004) and young men wander (011-003). The river flows through the stories as both setting and agent — it carries bodies, reflects grief, and separates the living from the transformed.

## In the Stories

The most extended river sequence occurs in Chapter 007, where a lover, "worn with fatigue" (007-046), repeatedly returns to the river. His friend "filled the kettle at the river" (007-067) — the river as resource. But the lover, told "go to the river and drink your fill from the" (007-070), instead "walked to the river, sprang in, and lying down in" (007-075) the water, letting it take him. He "swam to the middle of the river and there" (007-089) transformed. "In the river" (007-092) the community searched for him. Finally, they "let the waters run free" (007-119), the river released from whatever obstruction held it.

A young man "wandered along a river bank, looking for" (011-003) adventure or discovery. A girl's aunt "came down to the river, wondering what kept her niece" (014-031) — the river as the border of the domestic world, where girls linger and aunts worry.

## Cultural Significance

- **River as transformer**: Entering the river changes a person (007-075) — the water itself has transformative power
- **River as women's space**: Women draw water (014-004), meet at the riverbank, and the river becomes a zone of female activity distinct from the male hunting grounds
- **River as boundary**: Crossing a river (020-083) marks a narrative transition — the traveler enters new territory
- **River as grave**: The drowned lover (007-089) makes the river a burial place, its waters holding the transformed dead
