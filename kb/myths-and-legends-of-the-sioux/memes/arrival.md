## Overview

**Arrival** in Sioux tales is a narrative hinge. A warrior's arrival at his village (024-083) signals the end of a warpath and the beginning of recognition. A visitor's arrival at a home (022-083, 032-044) sets social dynamics in motion — hospitality, suspicion, or conflict. A character's "arrival at the starting point" (037-269) closes a loop, completing a journey. Arrivals structure the rhythm of the stories: departures begin them, arrivals end them, and the tension between the two drives the plot.

## In the Stories

### The Warrior's Return

After campaigns, warriors return home to be recognized. "On his arrival at his home village, they received him" (024-083) — the arrival triggers the community's acknowledgment of deeds done in distant places. Similarly, "on his arrival at the village of the Crows" (024-094), a warrior enters enemy territory, his arrival carrying danger rather than safety.

### The Morning After

Arrivals can bring immediate consequence: "the morning following the arrival of Rabbit's son, Mr. Bear again" (022-083) appeared — the arrival of a new character disrupts the existing order almost instantly, Bear arriving to test or confront the newcomer.

### Homecoming and Family

"On their arrival at the village Hake took his" (030-103) family or companion into the community — arrival as integration. "Upon his arrival home he told his uncles to get" (037-358) ready — arrival as the moment of reporting, of transferring knowledge from the journey to the home.

### The Accidental Arrival

Not all arrivals are triumphant: the "arrival at home after the accident" (037-310) is a diminished return, the character coming back changed or diminished by what happened on the journey. The contrast between the departure's ambition and the arrival's reality is a recurring narrative pattern.

### The Fateful Arrival

"On the arrival of Unktomi and his family, no" (032-044) welcome awaited — Unktomi the trickster's arrival is met with wariness or hostility, the reputation preceding the person. Arrival reveals how the community truly regards the arriver.

## Cultural Significance

- **Arrival as judgment**: The way a community receives an arrival reveals its true feelings — welcome, suspicion, or rejection
- **The journey-arrival pair**: Sioux narratives are built on departure and arrival; the space between is where growth, danger, and transformation occur
- **Hospitality imperative**: Arrival triggers hospitality obligations — even Unktomi, distrusted as he is, must be acknowledged
- **Arrival as closure**: "Upon his arrival at the starting point" (037-269) marks narrative completion — the circle closes


Big Eagle returns to his people after they believed him dead:

> On his arrival at his home village, they received him as one returned
> from the dead, as they were sure he had been killed the night he had
> been sent to reconnoiter the lone camp. There was great feasting and
> dancing in honor of his return, and the horses were distributed among the people.
>
> (024-083–024-086)
