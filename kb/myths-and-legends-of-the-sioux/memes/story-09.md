[009-001]The Rabbit and his grandmother were in dire straits, because the rabbit

[009-002]was out of arrows. The fall hunt would soon be on and his quiver was all

[009-003]but empty. Arrow sticks he could cut in plenty, but he had nothing with

[009-004]which to make arrowheads.

[009-005]“You must make some flint arrowheads,” said his grandmother. “Then you

[009-006]will be able to kill game.”

[009-007]“Where shall I get the flint?” asked the rabbit.

[009-008]“From the old bear chief,” said his old grandmother. For at that time

[009-009]all the flint in the world was in the bear’s body.

[009-010]So the rabbit set out for the village of the Bears. It was winter time

[009-011]and the lodges of the bears were set under the shelter of a hill where

[009-012]the cold wind would not blow on them and where they had shelter among

[009-013]the trees and bushes.

[009-014]He came at one end of the village to a hut where lived an old woman. He

[009-015]pushed open the door and entered. Everybody who came for flint always

[009-016]stopped there because it was the first lodge on the edge of the village.

[009-017]Strangers were therefore not unusual in the old woman’s hut, and she

[009-018]welcomed the rabbit. She gave him a seat and at night he lay with his

[009-019]feet to the fire.

[009-020]The next morning the rabbit went to the lodge of the bear chief. They

[009-021]sat together awhile and smoked. At last the bear chief spoke.

[009-022]“What do you want, my grandson?”

[009-023]“I have come for some flint to make arrows,” answered the rabbit.

[009-024]The bear chief grunted, and laid aside his pipe. Leaning back he pulled

[009-025]off his robe and, sure enough, one half of his body was flesh and the

[009-026]other half hard flint.

[009-027]“Bring a stone hammer and give it to our guest,” he bade his wife. Then

[009-028]as the rabbit took the hammer he said: “Do not strike too hard.”

[009-029]“Grandfather, I shall be careful,” said the rabbit. With a stroke he

[009-030]struck off a little flake of flint from the bear’s body.

[009-031]“Ni-sko-ke-cha? So big?” he asked.

[009-032]“Harder, grandson; strike off bigger pieces,” said the bear.

[009-033]The rabbit struck a little harder.

[009-034]“Ni-sko-ke-cha? So big?” he asked.

[009-035]The bear grew impatient. “No, no, strike off bigger pieces. I can’t be

[009-036]here all day. Tanka kaksa wo! Break off a big piece.”

[009-037]The rabbit struck again--hard! “Ni-sko-ke-cha?” he cried, as the hammer

[009-038]fell. But even as he spoke the bear’s body broke in two, the flesh part

[009-039]fell away and only the flint part remained. Like a flash the rabbit

[009-040]darted out of the hut.

[009-041]There was a great outcry in the village. Openmouthed, all the bears

[009-042]gave chase. But as he ran the rabbit cried: “Wa-hin-han-yo (snow, snow)

[009-043]Ota-po, Ota-po--lots more, lots more,” and a great storm of snow swept

[009-044]down from the sky.

[009-045]The rabbit, light of foot, bounded over the top of the snow. The bears

[009-046]sunk in and floundered about helpless. Seeing this, the rabbit turned

[009-047]back and killed them one by one with his club. That is why we now have

[009-048]so few bears.
