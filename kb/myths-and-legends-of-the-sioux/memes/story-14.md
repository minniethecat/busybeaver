[014-001]A young man lived with his grandmother. He was a good hunter and wished

[014-002]to marry. He knew a girl who was a good moccasin maker, but she belonged

[014-003]to a great family. He wondered how he could win her.

[014-004]One day she passed the tent on her way to get water at the river. His

[014-005]grandmother was at work in the tepee with a pair of old worn-out sloppy

[014-006]moccasins. The young man sprang to his feet. “Quick, grandmother--let me

[014-007]have those old sloppy moccasins you have on your feet!” he cried.

[014-008]“My old moccasins, what do you want of them?” cried the astonished

[014-009]woman.

[014-010]“Never mind! Quick! I can’t stop to talk,” answered the grandson as he

[014-011]caught up the old moccasins the old lady had doffed, and put them on. He

[014-012]threw a robe over his shoulders, slipped through the door, and hastened

[014-013]to the watering place. The girl had just arrived with her bucket.

[014-014]“Let me fill your bucket for you,” said the young man.

[014-015]“Oh, no, I can do it.”

[014-016]“Oh, let me, I can go in the mud. You surely don’t want to soil your

[014-017]moccasins,” and taking the bucket he slipped in the mud, taking care

[014-018]to push his sloppy old moccasins out so the girl could see them. She

[014-019]giggled outright.

[014-020]“My, what old moccasins you have,” she cried.

[014-021]“Yes, I have nobody to make me a new pair,” he answered.

[014-022]“Why don’t you get your grandmother to make you a new pair?”

[014-023]“She’s old and blind and can’t make them any longer. That’s why I want

[014-024]you,” he answered.

[014-025]“Oh, you’re fooling me. You aren’t speaking the truth.”

[014-026]“Yes, I am. If you don’t believe--come with me _now!_”

[014-027]The girl looked down; so did the youth. At last he said softly:

[014-028]“Well, which is it? Shall I take up your bucket, or will you go with

[014-029]me?”

[014-030]And she answered, still more softly: “I guess I’ll go with you!”

[014-031]The girl’s aunt came down to the river, wondering what kept her niece so

[014-032]long. In the mud she found two pairs of moccasin tracks close together;

[014-033]at the edge of the water stood an empty keg.
