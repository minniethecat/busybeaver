[017-001]There once lived a widow with two children--the elder a daughter and the

[017-002]younger a son. The widow went in mourning for her husband a long time.

[017-003]She cut off her hair, let her dress lie untidy on her body and kept her

[017-004]face unpainted and unwashed.

[017-005]There lived in the same village a great chief. He had one son just come

[017-006]old enough to marry. The chief had it known that he wished his son to

[017-007]take a wife, and all of the young women in the village were eager to

[017-008]marry the young man. However, he was pleased with none of them.

[017-009]Now the widow thought, “I am tired of mourning for my husband and caring

[017-010]for my children. Perhaps if I lay aside my mourning and paint myself

[017-011]red, the chief’s son may marry me.”

[017-012]So she slipped away from her two children, stole down to the river and

[017-013]made a bathing place thru the ice. When she had washed away all signs of

[017-014]mourning, she painted and decked herself and went to the chief’s tepee.

[017-015]When his son saw her, he loved her, and a feast was made in honor of her

[017-016]wedding.

[017-017]When the widow’s daughter found herself forsaken, she wept bitterly.

[017-018]After a day or two she took her little brother in her arms and went to

[017-019]the tepee of an old woman who lived at one end of the village. The old

[017-020]woman’s tumble down tepee was of bark and her dress and clothing was of

[017-021]old smoke-dried tent cover. But she was kind to the two waifs and took

[017-022]them in willingly.

[017-023]The little girl was eager to find her mother. The old woman said to her:

[017-024]“I suspect your mother has painted her face red. Do not try to find her.

[017-025]If the chief’s son marries her she will not want to be burdened with

[017-026]you.”

[017-027]The old woman was right. The girl went down to the river, and sure

[017-028]enough found a hole cut in the ice and about it lay the filth that the

[017-029]mother had washed from her body. The girl gathered up the filth and went

[017-030]on. By and by she came to a second hole in the ice. Here too was filth,

[017-031]but not so much as at the previous place. At the third hole the ice was

[017-032]clean.

[017-033]The girl knew now that her mother had painted her face red. She went at

[017-034]once to the chief’s tepee, raised the door flap and went in. There sat

[017-035]her mother with the chief’s son at their wedding feast.

[017-036]The girl walked up to her mother and hurled the filth in her mother’s

[017-037]face.

[017-038]“There,” she cried, “you who forsake your helpless children and forget

[017-039]your husband, take that!”

[017-040]And at once her mother became a hideous old woman.

[017-041]The girl then went back to the lodge of the old woman, leaving the camp

[017-042]in an uproar. The chief soon sent some young warriors to seize the girl

[017-043]and her brother, and they were brought to his tent. He was furious with

[017-044]anger.

[017-045]“Let the children be bound with lariats wrapped about their bodies and

[017-046]let them be left to starve. Our camp will move on,” he said. The chief’s

[017-047]son did not put away his wife, hoping she might be cured in some way and

[017-048]grow young again.

[017-049]Everybody in camp now got ready to move; but the old woman came close to

[017-050]the girl and said:

[017-051]“In my old tepee I have dug a hole and buried a pot with punk and steel

[017-052]and flint and packs of dried meat. They will tie you up like a corpse.

[017-053]But before we go I will come with a knife and pretend to stab you, but

[017-054]I will really cut the rope that binds you so that you can unwind it from

[017-055]your body as soon as the camp is out of sight and hearing.”

[017-056]And so, before the camp started, the old woman came to the place where

[017-057]the two children were bound. She had in her hand a knife bound to the

[017-058]end of a stick which she used as a lance. She stood over the children

[017-059]and cried aloud:

[017-060]“You wicked girl, who have shamed your own mother, you deserve all the

[017-061]punishment that is given you. But after all I do not want to let you lie

[017-062]and starve. Far better kill you at once and have done with it!” and

[017-063]with her stick she stabbed many times, as if to kill, but she was really

[017-064]cutting the rope.

[017-065]The camp moved on; but the children lay on the ground until noon the

[017-066]next day. Then they began to squirm about. Soon the girl was free, and

[017-067]she then set loose her little brother. They went at once to the old

[017-068]woman’s hut where they found the flint and steel and the packs of dried

[017-069]meat.

[017-070]The girl made her brother a bow and arrows and with these he killed

[017-071]birds and other small game.

[017-072]The boy grew up a great hunter. They became rich. They built three great

[017-073]tepees, in one of which were stored rows upon rows of parfleche bags of

[017-074]dried meat.

[017-075]One day as the brother went out to hunt, he met a handsome young

[017-076]stranger who greeted him and said to him:

[017-077]“I know you are a good hunter, for I have been watching you; your

[017-078]sister, too, is industrious. Let me have her for a wife. Then you and I

[017-079]will be brothers and hunt together.”

[017-080]The girl’s brother went home and told her what the young stranger had

[017-081]said.

[017-082]“Brother, I do not care to marry,” she answered. “I am now happy with

[017-083]you.”

[017-084]“But you will be yet happier married,” he answered, “and the young

[017-085]stranger is of no mean family, as one can see by his dress and manners.”

[017-086]“Very well, I will do as you wish,” she said. So the stranger came into

[017-087]the tepee and was the girl’s husband.

[017-088]One day as they were in their tent, a crow flew overhead, calling out

[017-089]loudly,

[017-090]“Kaw, Kaw,

[017-091]“They who forsook the children have no meat.”

[017-092]The girl and her husband and brother looked up at one another.

[017-093]“What can it mean?” they asked. “Let us send for Unktomi (the spider).

[017-094]He is a good judge and he will know.”

[017-095]“And I will get ready a good dinner for him, for Unktomi is always

[017-096]hungry,” added the young wife.

[017-097]When Unktomi came, his yellow mouth opened with delight at the fine

[017-098]feast spread for him. After he had eaten he was told what the crow had

[017-099]said.

[017-100]“The crow means,” said Unktomi, “that the villagers and chief who bound

[017-101]and deserted you are in sad plight. They have hardly anything to eat and

[017-102]are starving.”

[017-103]When the girl heard this she made a bundle of choicest meat and called

[017-104]the crow.

[017-105]“Take this to the starving villagers,” she bade him.

[017-106]He took the bundle in his beak, flew away to the starving village and

[017-107]dropped the bundle before the chief’s tepee. The chief came out and the

[017-108]crow called loudly:

[017-109]“Kaw, Kaw!

[017-110]“The children who were forsaken have much meat; those who forsook them

[017-111]have none.”

[017-112]“What can he mean,” cried the astonished villagers.

[017-113]“Let us send for Unktomi,” said one, “he is a great judge; he will tell

[017-114]us.”

[017-115]They divided the bundle of meat among the starving people, saving the

[017-116]biggest piece for Unktomi.

[017-117]When Unktomi had come and eaten, the villagers told him of the crow and

[017-118]asked what the bird’s words meant.

[017-119]“He means,” said Unktomi, “that the two children whom you forsook have

[017-120]tepees full of dried meat enough for all the village.”

[017-121]The villagers were filled with astonishment at this news. To find

[017-122]whether or not it was true, the chief called seven young men and sent

[017-123]them out to see. They came to the three tepees and there met the girl’s

[017-124]brother and husband just going out to hunt (which they did now only for

[017-125]sport).

[017-126]The girl’s brother invited the seven young men into the third or sacred

[017-127]lodge, and after they had smoked a pipe and knocked out the ashes on a

[017-128]buffalo bone the brother gave them meat to eat, which the seven devoured

[017-129]greedily. The next day he loaded all seven with packs of meat, saying:

[017-130]“Take this meat to the villagers and lead them hither.”

[017-131]While they awaited the return of the young men with the villagers, the

[017-132]girl made two bundles of meat, one of the best and choicest pieces, and

[017-133]the other of liver, very dry and hard to eat. After a few days the camp

[017-134]arrived. The young woman’s mother opened the door and ran in crying:

[017-135]“Oh, my dear daughter, how glad I am to see you.” But the daughter

[017-136]received her coldly and gave her the bundle of dried liver to eat. But

[017-137]when the old woman who had saved the children’s lives came in, the

[017-138]young girl received her gladly, called her grandmother, and gave her the

[017-139]package of choice meat with marrow.

[017-140]Then the whole village camped and ate of the stores of meat all the

[017-141]winter until spring came; and withal they were so many, there was such

[017-142]abundance of stores that there was still much left.
