## Overview

**Unktomi** (the Spider), also known as **Unktomi Tanka** (Big Spider), is a prominent trickster figure in Sioux folklore. He appears across multiple stories, sometimes as a craftsman making flint arrowheads, sometimes as a hungry predator pursuing the Pemmican Man, and sometimes as a feared but respected figure whose mere name inspires caution. Like many trickster figures across Native American traditions, Unktomi is neither purely good nor purely evil — he is clever, dangerous, and commands a certain respect.

## In the Stories

### Unktomi and the Arrowheads (021-001)

In this tale, two young men discover Unktomi busily making flint arrowheads atop a high hill:

> "Looking around they discovered a large spider sitting in the midst of a great many flint arrowheads. The spider was busily engaged making the flint rocks into arrowheads. They looked at the spider, but he never moved, but continued hammering away on a piece of flint which he had nearly completed into another arrowhead." (021-009–021-013)

When one of the young men impulsively strikes Unktomi with an arrowhead, the spider retaliates with a deadly cough that kills the offender. The surviving young man reports the incident to the council, who decline to punish Unktomi — they recognize he was merely defending himself.

### The Wasna (Pemmican Man) and the Unktomi (032-001)

Unktomi encounters the Pemmican Man — a figure made of buffalo fat — and immediately sees him as food for his starving children. Unktomi and his family pursue the Pemmican Man relentlessly around a lake, but the Pemmican Man outwits them, eventually escaping while Unktomi's family is distracted.

### The Unktomi, Two Widows and the Red Plums (038-001)

In this tale, Unktomi preys upon two widowed sisters and their babies in a remote forest, demonstrating his predatory nature and cunning.
