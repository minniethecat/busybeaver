## Overview

**Bear** (Lakota: **Mato**) appears in Sioux folklore as a figure of immense physical power — sometimes literally invulnerable with a body of flint, sometimes an exploitative hunting partner who takes advantage of [[rabbit]]'s smaller size. Unlike the purely trickster figures, Bear represents raw strength unchecked by cleverness or morality. His encounters with Rabbit form a recurring motif of the weak outwitting the strong.

## In the Stories

### The Rabbit and the Bear with the Flint Body (Chapter 009)

In this tale, Bear possesses a body of flint — a substance so hard that arrows cannot pierce it. He threatens Rabbit and his grandmother, stealing their provisions and terrorizing their lodge:

> "The Rabbit and his grandmother were in dire straits, for the Bear with the Flint Body had taken their provisions and threatened their lodge." (009-001–009-005)

The flint body is significant in Sioux material culture — [[flint]] was the primary material for arrowheads and tools, and a creature made of flint represents the ultimate in invulnerability. This Bear cannot be defeated by ordinary means.

### The Bear and the Rabbit Hunt Buffalo (Chapter 022)

Bear's relationship with Rabbit is deeply unequal. When they hunt [[buffalo]] together, Bear consistently claims all the meat for himself while Rabbit goes hungry:

> "Rabbit, you come along home with me," said Bear, keeping all the meat for himself while poor Rabbit would get nothing. (022-013–022-016)

Rabbit, growing tired of this exploitation, plots revenge. The turning point comes when Rabbit's adopted son — a youth born from a blood clot — confronts Bear and defeats both him and Mrs. Bear:

> "During the melee, Rabbit shouted encouragement to his son as Mrs. Bear was served likewise." (022-106)

The defeat of Bear represents a reversal of the natural order — the small and clever overcoming the large and powerful through a combination of patience, alliance, and timing.

## Character Traits

| Trait | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| **Predatory** | Steals provisions from Rabbit's lodge (009) |
| **Exploitative** | Takes all buffalo meat, leaving Rabbit none (022) |
| **Physically dominant** | Flint body makes him invulnerable to arrows (009) |
| **Complacent** | Underestimates Rabbit and his allies, leading to downfall (022) |
| **Greedy** | Butchers six buffalo for himself while Rabbit starves (022) |

Bear functions as a **foil to the trickster archetype**: where Rabbit relies on wit and cunning, Bear relies solely on size and strength. His consistent pattern of exploitation and eventual defeat reinforces a central Sioux narrative theme — that power without wisdom is ultimately self-defeating.

## Cultural Significance

In Sioux hunting culture, the bear was both a feared predator and a prized quarry. Bear fat, claws, and hide held both practical and ceremonial value. The figure of the Bear in these tales may reflect real anxieties about the dangers of the hunt, transformed into narratives where human cleverness (embodied by Rabbit) ultimately prevails.
