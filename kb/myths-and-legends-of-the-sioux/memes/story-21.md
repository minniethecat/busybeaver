[021-001]There were once upon a time two young men who were very great friends,

[021-002]and were constantly together. One was a very thoughtful young man, the

[021-003]other very impulsive, who never stopped to think before he committed an

[021-004]act.

[021-005]One day these two friends were walking along, telling each other of

[021-006]their experiences in love making. They ascended a high hill, and on

[021-007]reaching the top, heard a ticking noise as if small stones or pebbles

[021-008]were being struck together.

[021-009]Looking around they discovered a large spider sitting in the midst of

[021-010]a great many flint arrowheads. The spider was busily engaged making the

[021-011]flint rocks into arrow heads. They looked at the spider, but he never

[021-012]moved, but continued hammering away on a piece of flint which he had

[021-013]nearly completed into another arrowhead.

[021-014]“Let’s hit him,” said the thoughtless one. “No,” said the other, “he is

[021-015]not harming any one; in fact, he is doing a great good, as he is making

[021-016]the flint arrowheads which we use to point our arrows.”

[021-017]“Oh, you are afraid,” said the first young man. “He can’t harm you, just

[021-018]watch me hit him.” So saying, he picked up an arrowhead and throwing it

[021-019]at “Unktomi,” hit him on the side. As Unktomi rolled over on his side,

[021-020]got up and stood looking at them, the young man laughed and said: “Well,

[021-021]let us be going, as your grandfather, “Unktomi,” doesn’t seem to like

[021-022]our company.” They started down the hill, when suddenly the one who had

[021-023]hit Unktomi took a severe fit of coughing. He coughed and coughed, and

[021-024]finally small particles of blood came from his mouth. The blood kept

[021-025]coming thicker and in great gushes. Finally it came so thick and fast

[021-026]that the man could not get his breath and fell upon the ground dead.

[021-027]The thoughtful young man, seeing that his friend was no more, hurried

[021-028]to the village and reported what had happened. The relatives and friends

[021-029]hurried to the hill, and sure enough, there lay the thoughtless young

[021-030]man still and cold in death. They held a council and sent for the chief

[021-031]of the Unktomi tribe. When he heard what had happened, he told the

[021-032]council that he could do nothing to his Unktomi, as it had only defended

[021-033]itself.

[021-034]Said he: “My friends, seeing that your tribe was running short of

[021-035]arrowheads, I set a great many of my tribe to work making flint

[021-036]arrowheads for you. When my men are thus engaged they do not wish to

[021-037]be disturbed, and your young man not only disturbed my man, but grossly

[021-038]insulted him by striking him with one of the arrowheads which he had

[021-039]worked so hard to make. My man could not sit and take this insult, so

[021-040]as the young man walked away the Unktomi shot him with a very tiny

[021-041]arrowhead. This produced a hemorrhage, which caused his death. So now,

[021-042]my friends, if you will fill and pass the peace pipe, we will part

[021-043]good friends and my tribe shall always furnish you with plenty of flint

[021-044]arrowheads.” So saying, Unktomi Tanka finished his peace smoke and

[021-045]returned to his tribe.

[021-046]Ever after that, when the Indians heard a ticking in the grass, they

[021-047]would go out of their way to get around the sound, saying, Unktomi is

[021-048]making arrowheads; we must not disturb him.

[021-049]Thus it was that Unktomi Tanka (Big Spider) had the respect of this

[021-050]tribe, and was never after disturbed in his work of making arrowheads.
