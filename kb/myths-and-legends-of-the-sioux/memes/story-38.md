[038-001]There once lived, in a remote part of a great forest, two widowed

[038-002]sisters, with their little babies. One day there came to their tent

[038-003]a visitor who was called Unktomi (spider). He had found some nice red

[038-004]plums during his wanderings in the forest, and he said to himself, “I

[038-005]will keep these plums and fool the two widows with them.” After the

[038-006]widows had bidden him be seated, he presented them with the plums.

[038-007]On seeing them they exclaimed “hi nu, hi nu (an exclamation of

[038-008]surprise), where did you get those fine plums?” Unktomi arose and

[038-009]pointing to a crimson tipped cloud, said: “You see that red cloud?

[038-010]Directly underneath it is a patch of plums. So large is the patch and so

[038-011]red and beautiful are the plums that it is the reflection of them on the

[038-012]cloud that you see.”

[038-013]“Oh, how we wish some one would take care of our babies, while we go

[038-014]over there and pick some,” said the sisters. “Why, I am not in any

[038-015]particular hurry, so if you want to go I will take care of my little

[038-016]nephews until you return.” (Unktomi always claimed relationship with

[038-017]everyone he met). “Well brother,” said the older widow, “take good care

[038-018]of them and we will be back as soon as possible.”

[038-019]The two then took a sack in which to gather the plums, and started off

[038-020]towards the cloud with the crimson lining. Scarcely had they gone from

[038-021]Unktomi’s sight when he took the babies out of their swinging hammocks

[038-022]and cut off first one head and then the other. He then took some old

[038-023]blankets and rolled them in the shape of a baby body and laid one in

[038-024]each hammock. Then he took the heads and put them in place in their

[038-025]different hammocks. The bodies he cut up and threw into a large kettle.

[038-026]This he placed over a rousing fire. Then he mixed Indian turnips and

[038-027]arikara squash with the baby meat and soon had a kettle of soup. Just

[038-028]about the time the soup was ready to serve the widows returned. They

[038-029]were tired and hungry and not a plum had they. Unktomi, hearing the

[038-030]approach of the two, hurriedly dished out the baby soup in two wooden

[038-031]dishes and then seated himself near the door so that he could get out

[038-032]easily. Upon the entrance of the widows, Unktomi exclaimed: “Sisters, I

[038-033]had brought some meat with me and I cooked some turnips and squash with

[038-034]it and made a pot of fine soup. The babies have just fallen asleep, so

[038-035]don’t waken them until you have finished eating, for I know that you

[038-036]are nearly starved.” The two fell to at once and after they had somewhat

[038-037]appeased their appetites, one of them arose and went over to see how

[038-038]her baby was resting. Noting an unnatural color on her baby’s face,

[038-039]she raised him up only to have his head roll off from the bundle of

[038-040]blankets. “‘My son! my son!” she cried out. At once the other hastened

[038-041]to her baby and grabbed it up, only to have the same thing happen. At

[038-042]once they surmised who had done this, and caught up sticks from the fire

[038-043]with which to beat Unktomi to death. He, expecting something like this

[038-044]to happen, lost very little time in getting outside and down into a hole

[038-045]at the roots of a large tree. The two widows not being able to follow

[038-046]Unktomi down into the hole, had to give up trying to get him out, and

[038-047]passed the rest of the day and night crying for their beloved babies.

[038-048]In the meantime Unktomi had gotten out by another opening, and fixing

[038-049]himself up in an entirely different style, and painting his face in a

[038-050]manner that they would not recognize him, he cautiously approached the

[038-051]weeping women and inquired the cause of their tears.

[038-052]Thus they answered him: “Unktomi came here and fooled us about some

[038-053]plums, and while we were absent killed our babies and made soup out of

[038-054]their bodies. Then he gave us the soup to eat, which we did, and when

[038-055]we found out what he had done we tried to kill him, but he crawled down

[038-056]into that hole and we could not get him out.”

[038-057]“I will get him out,” said the mock stranger, and with that he crawled

[038-058]down into the hole and scratched his own face all over to make the

[038-059]widows believe he had been fighting with Unktomi. “I have killed him,

[038-060]and that you may see him I have enlarged the hole so you can crawl in

[038-061]and see for yourselves, also to take some revenge on his dead body.”

[038-062]The two foolish widows, believing him, crawled into the hole, only to

[038-063]be blocked up by Unktomi, who at once gathered great piles of wood and

[038-064]stuffing it into the hole, set it on fire, and thus ended the last of

[038-065]the family who were foolish enough to let Unktomi tempt them with a few

[038-066]red plums.
