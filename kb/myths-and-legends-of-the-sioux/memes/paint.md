## Overview

**Paint** was a visual language in Sioux culture — a person's painted or unpainted face declared their emotional state, social role, and ritual intention without words. Warriors painted themselves before battle, mourners left their faces unpainted and unwashed, beautiful women were recognized by their painted appearance, and honored individuals were seated on painted robes. The act of painting or not painting was never casual — it was a deliberate social signal read by the entire community.

## In the Stories

### Mourning and the Unpainted Face

The most poignant paint code appears in mourning ritual. A grieving woman kept her "face unpainted and unwashed" (017-004) as the visible sign of her sorrow. To resume normal life required a deliberate transformation: "if I lay aside my mourning and paint myself" (017-010), she said, framing the act of painting as the threshold between grief and renewed engagement with life. When she emerged from mourning, "she painted and decked herself" (017-014), reclaiming her place in the social world.

### Red Paint — Warning and Beauty

Red paint carried dual meanings. A warning: "I suspect your mother has painted her face red. Do not try to lift her" (017-024) — red face paint here signified danger or supernatural transformation, a warning that the painted person was not to be approached. The daughter, when she "knew now that her mother had painted her face red, she went at once" (017-033) to investigate, despite the warning. Yet red could also signal beauty and readiness: "a beautiful woman. She was painted" (013-003) describes the White Buffalo Woman's appearance in painted splendor.

### War Paint and Battle Adornment

Before battle, painting was ceremonial preparation. Warriors were instructed to "paint their faces and dress in their finest clothes" (011-016) as if going to a celebration — battle was a sacred occasion warranting full ritual adornment. Warriors "were painted and decorated as if for a celebration" (031-068), showing that the war-paint ritual transformed combat into a ceremonial act. The finest regalia — painted and splendid — was worn into battle because death in war was an honorable end deserving of one's best appearance.

### Painted Objects and Honor

Paint extended beyond bodies to objects. A "painted robe" (035-176) was spread to seat a returning hero — the painted surface distinguished the robe as ceremonial rather than ordinary. "Together ten buffalo hides and painted" (036-013) describes the creation of ceremonial objects for a contest. Painted surfaces marked items as set apart for honorable or sacred use.

### Painted Arrows and Supernatural Aid

White Plume's father prepared special arrows: "one of the arrows he painted red, one blue, and another yellow" (035-011). These painted arrows carried supernatural power — "with these painted arrows, you will never fail" (035-041). The painter believed paint itself had protective, empowering properties: some had "great faith in painting their feet, claiming that the paint would make them swift" (037-050). A sister would "get her paint and mix it with the deer tallow and rub the paint on her brother's feet, painting them" (037-054) as a pre-race ritual.

## Cultural Significance

- **Unpainted face** = mourning; **painted face** = social re-entry; the face was a canvas of emotional state
- **Red face paint** could signal either beauty or supernatural danger, context-dependent
- **War paint** transformed battle from violence into ceremonial act — warriors painted as if for celebration
- **Painted arrows** carried supernatural power through color symbolism (red/blue/yellow)
- **Painted robes** and objects were set apart for honored/sacred use
- **Foot painting** with deer tallow mixtures was believed to confer swiftness
