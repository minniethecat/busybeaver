## Overview

The **ambush** is warfare by concealment — warriors "lay in ambush for the two daring horsemen" (023-111), using terrain and surprise to overcome superior numbers. But ambush also carries a moral shadow: it can be the method of the assassin rather than the warrior. When a character is "shot from ambush" (030-166), the killing lacks the honor of open battle. The ambush thus occupies a contested space in Sioux martial ethics — tactically sound but spiritually diminished compared to face-to-face combat.

## In the Stories

### The War Party's Ambush

A war party positions itself strategically: horses are hidden, and warriors "lay in ambush for the two daring horsemen who" (023-111) were expected to pass. The ambush here is a legitimate tactic of intertribal warfare — the ambushers are warriors, not assassins, and their target is an armed enemy.

### The Leader's Command

A war leader "ordered a band of his warriors to lie in ambush for" (031-032) the approaching enemy. The command to "intercept, ambush and annihilate every war party" (031-037) shows ambush as a systematic military strategy, not a last resort. The scale — multiple bands positioned to intercept — indicates sophisticated tactical coordination.

### The Assassin's Ambush

Not all ambushes are honorable. A character encounters "the man who laid in ambush and shot me, killing me before" (030-166) — the ambush here is murder, not warfare. The victim's spirit recounts the betrayal, and the killing from concealment denies the victim the warrior's death he deserved. This is the dark face of ambush: the tool of the coward, not the brave.

## Cultural Significance

- **Ambush as tactic vs. ambush as treachery**: The same act is judged differently depending on context — intertribal warfare (honorable) vs. personal assassination (dishonorable)
- **Terrain as weapon**: The ambusher uses the land — sagebrush, ravines, darkness — as an ally, reflecting the Sioux warrior's deep knowledge of landscape
- **The unarmed vs. the armed**: Ambush ethics may hinge on whether the target is armed and expecting combat — surprising an armed war party differs from shooting an unsuspecting individual
- **Spiritual consequence**: The spirit of the ambush victim (030-166) cannot join his ancestors properly, showing that the manner of death affects the afterlife


The ghost of a slain warrior recounts his dishonorable death:

> the man who laid in ambush and shot me, killing me before I could get
> a chance to defend myself. I want that man's scalp, because he has been
> the cause of my wanderings for a great many years. Had he killed me on
> the battlefield my spirit would have at once joined my brothers in the happy hunting grounds.
>
> (030-166–030-169)
