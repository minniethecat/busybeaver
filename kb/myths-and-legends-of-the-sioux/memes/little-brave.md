## Overview

**Brave** (referred to as "little Brave" in the narrative) is a playful and unruly boy whose hair is "always unkempt and tossed about" and who "was never quiet for a moment" (016-018–016-020). When six timid playmates ask him to accompany them to visit the grave of a medicine woman — a site that has stirred the village's curiosity — Brave seizes the opportunity for mischief. His prank of disguising himself as a mud-covered corpse and hiding in the grave to frighten his friends backfires spectacularly when his own appearance terrifies the entire village.

## In the Stories

### Little Brave and the Medicine Woman (Chapter 016)

After a hunter's mysterious stumble at a hidden grave, curiosity spreads through the camp. Six little boys wish to see the grave but are too frightened to go alone. They enlist their rowdy playmate Brave:

> The story of the villager's adventure spread thru the camp and made many curious to see the grave. Among others were six little boys who were, however, rather timid, for they were in great awe of the dead medicine woman. But they had a little playmate named Brave, a mischievous little rogue, whose hair was always unkempt and tossed about and who was never quiet for a moment. (016-015–016-020)

Brave agrees but secretly plans a trick. Sending the boys the long way, he rushes to the lake:

> Now Brave, full of mischief, had thought to play a jest on his little friends. As soon as they were well out of sight he had sped around the hill to the shore of the lake and sticking his hands in the mud had rubbed it over his face, plastered it in his hair, and soiled his hands until he looked like a new risen corpse with the flesh rotting from his bones. He then went and lay down in the grave and awaited the boys. (016-029–016-034)

When the six boys arrive at the grave they are terrified. One timidly addresses the "grandmother," and Brave answers in a thin, quavering voice — pretending to be the risen medicine woman. He then rises from the bushes covered in mud:

> Just then Brave raised his muddy face and hands up thru the choke cherry bushes. With the oozy mud dripping from his features he looked like some very witch just raised from the grave. The boys screamed outright. One fainted. The rest ran yelling up the hill to the village. (016-047–016-051)

Brave, now as frightened as the others, chases after them — forgetting his own ghastly appearance. His mud-covered face and wild hair send the women of the village fleeing in terror, and even his own mother bolts from their tepee:

> Just then little Brave, as badly scared as the rest, came rushing in after them, his hair on end and covered with mud and crying out, all forgetful of his appearance: "It's me, it's me!" The women yelped and bolted in terror from the village. Brave dashed into his mother's tepee, scaring her out of her wits. Dropping pots and kettles, she tumbled out of the tent to run screaming with the rest. Nor would a single villager come near poor little Brave until he had gone down to the lake and washed himself. (016-055–016-063)

The tale of Brave is a humorous episode in Sioux folklore — one where a boy's prank spirals beyond his control, and where the boundary between mischief and genuine terror blurs when the dead are involved.

## Character Traits

Brave represents the **trickster-child** archetype in Sioux storytelling. Unlike the malevolent Unktomi (Spider), Brave's mischief is playful rather than cruel, and he ultimately suffers the consequences when his joke turns on him. His tale illustrates the Sioux cultural tension between proper respect for the dead and the irrepressible impulses of youth.
