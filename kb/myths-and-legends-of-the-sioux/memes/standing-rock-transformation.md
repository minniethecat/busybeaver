## Overview

**The Standing Rock Transformation** is the legendary event that gives the Standing Rock Sioux Reservation its name. It tells of an Arikara woman who, consumed by jealousy after her Dakota husband took a second wife, refused to move when the village broke camp — and was transformed into a block of stone. The stone was thereafter revered as _wakan_ (holy) and carried with the people on all their journeys.

## Narrative

A Dakota man had married an Arikara woman and they had one child. When he took a second wife, the first wife grew jealous and withdrew. As the village prepared to move, she sat on the ground with her baby on her back and refused to rise — even after the tent was taken down around her. The camp moved on without her.

At noon, her husband grew concerned and sent his two brothers back for her:

> "Sister-in-law, get up. We have come for you. The camp awaits you." She did not answer, and he put out his hand and touched her head. She had turned to stone! (012-012–012-014)

The brothers raced back to camp with the news. The husband refused to believe them, thinking his wife had killed herself and his brothers were hiding the truth. The entire village returned to find the woman — now a solid block of stone — sitting exactly where they had left her (012-016–012-019).

> "The Indians were greatly excited. They chose out a handsome pony, made a new travois and placed the stone in the carrying net. Pony and travois were both beautifully painted and decorated with streamers and colors. The stone was thought _'wakan'_ (holy), and was given a place of honor in the center of the camp." (012-020–012-024)

## Significance

The transformation of the Arikara woman into Standing Rock is more than a cautionary tale about jealousy — it is an origin story for one of the most sacred physical landmarks in Sioux territory. The stone was carried for generations before being placed at Standing Rock Agency, where it remains today on a brick pedestal. The event connects the physical landscape of the Dakotas to the spiritual world of Sioux legend, embodying the belief that the sacred can manifest in stone, earth, and the places where extraordinary things have happened.
