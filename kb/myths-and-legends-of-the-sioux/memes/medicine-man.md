## Overview

The **Medicine Man** (also referred to as a **conjurer** or **shaman**) held a vital role in Sioux society as a spiritual leader, healer, and interpreter of omens. Medicine men were called upon to cure the sick, bless hunting expeditions, and communicate with the spirit world. Their power derived from sacred medicine bundles, roots, and rituals passed down through generations.

## In the Stories

### Healers and Decision-Makers

Medicine men frequently appear as arbiters of difficult decisions. In one instance, when a band faced a crisis, they "finally left it to their medicine man to decide" (019-010). On another occasion, a medicine man counseled against despair: "We will not drown ourselves," said the medicine man, "we have found a..." (019-019).

### The Power of Medicine

The medicine man's remedies were highly esteemed. One hermit's "medicine being considered far superior to all others" (026-009), and warriors traveled great distances to obtain his medicine roots for their tribes. When a chief fell ill, "the best medicine men of the tribe [were] sent for" (029-059), though their medicine was sometimes "of no avail" (029-059).

Medicine women also practiced: one character was described as "a medicine woman or conjurer. Doubtless it was her medicine that made" her powerful (016-013).

### Courtship and Family

Medicine men were community members with families. The "daughter of an old medicine man... was very much courted" (030-031), and "porcupine work was tied at the medicine man's tepee in offering" for her hand (030-033). The medicine man's lodge was a recognized landmark in the village (030-250).

### Warpath and Ritual Medicine

Medicine played a crucial role in warfare and hunting. Warriors sought "medicine to rub on my arrows, so I can kill the witches" (035-258). The "medicine pole, of the sun dance," was raised "in thanks to the Great" Spirit (018-039).

### Limits of Medicine

The stories acknowledge that medicine was not always effective. When a young girl died, "medicine men were called in, but their medicines were of no avail" (033-007). This honesty reflects the Sioux understanding that spiritual power had its limits.

> The daughter of an old medicine man was very much courted by the other
> young men of the tribe, and many a horse loaded with robes and fine
> porcupine work was tied at the medicine man's tepee in offering for the
> hand of his daughter, but the horses, laden as when tied there, were
> turned loose, signifying that the offer was not accepted.
>
> (030-031–030-035)

## Cultural Significance

- The medicine man served as intermediary between the physical and spirit worlds
- Medicine bundles contained sacred roots, herbs, and ritual objects
- The position could be held by both men and women (medicine women)
- Medicine men were called for: healing sickness, blessing expeditions, interpreting omens, preparing warriors, and officiating ceremonies
- The Great Spirit was the ultimate source of medicine power
