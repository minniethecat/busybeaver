## Overview

In Sioux belief, the **ghost** (_wanagi_) is the spirit of a deceased person — not inherently malevolent, but in need of proper ceremonial release to reach the land of spirits (013-024). A ghost could become a restless, wandering presence if the death was dishonorable or if burial rites were neglected. The ghost was also associated with the sacred — described as _Wakan_ (holy) — and its power could be both feared and respected.

## Releasing the Ghost

The ceremonial release of a ghost required specific sacred objects. In the Story of the Peace Pipe, a buffalo cow woman instructed the people: "When you set free the ghost (the spirit of deceased persons) you must have a white buffalo cow skin" (013-024). This established the white buffalo cow skin as an essential element of ghost-release ceremonies, tying the practice to the sacred origins of the peace pipe.

> She repeated to them what she had already told the young men and added: "When you set free the ghost (the spirit of deceased persons) you must have a white buffalo cow skin." She gave the pipe to the medicine men of the village, turned again to a buffalo cow and fled away to the land of buffaloes.
>
> (013-023–013-027)

## The Wakan Ghost

Not all ghosts were merely spirits awaiting release. In the Story of the Two Young Friends, old warriors warned travelers about a haunted creek: "there is a ghost who haunts that creek, and any one who camps there is disturbed all through the night, and besides they never return, because the ghost is Wakan (holy)" (030-121, 030-123). This ghost was not simply a lost soul — it was a sacred, powerful presence whose territory was to be avoided. The description of the ghost as Wakan elevated it beyond a mere apparition to something with genuine spiritual authority.

## The Wandering Spirit

The manner of death determined a spirit's fate. A warrior who died honorably in battle joined his ancestors immediately, but one who died dishonorably was condemned to wander. A ghostly warrior explained: had he died on the battlefield his spirit "would have at once joined my ancestors," but because of a dishonorable death, his spirit was doomed to roam as "the wandering spirit of a once great warrior" (030-155, 030-169). This reveals the core Sioux death ethic: the quality of one's death directly shaped the quality of one's afterlife.

## Cultural Significance

- The ghost was not a monster but a spirit requiring proper ritual attention
- Ceremonial release required sacred objects (white buffalo cow skin) with mythic origins
- A Wakan ghost was to be respected, not confronted — avoidance was the proper response
- Dishonorable death trapped the spirit in a wandering state between worlds
- Ghost beliefs were integrated with the peace pipe ceremony, warrior ethics, and burial customs
