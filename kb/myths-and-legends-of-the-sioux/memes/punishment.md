## Overview

**Punishment** in Sioux folklore is not merely social discipline but often supernatural retribution. The most elaborate punishment sequence involves Rabbit's brother, who places wrongdoers in a state of suspended suffering: "punishment, by Rabbit's brother" (020-059), "for punishment to the man for having placed" (020-074), "this man was placed here for punishment by Rabbit's brother. He had killed" (020-109). These punishments are not executions but protracted states — the punished are left suspended, visible but unreachable. "Whom he had placed here for punishment, but none had seen him" (020-133) — the punisher remains unseen, adding a supernatural dimension to the justice delivered.

## In the Stories

### Rabbit's Brother as Punisher

Chapter 020 contains the most detailed punishment narrative. Rabbit's brother serves as a supernatural enforcer, placing wrongdoers in a state of torment. The punishment is described as "that punishment that is given you" (017-061) — a recognized category of consequence. The first wrongdoer "was placed here for punishment by Rabbit's brother" (020-109) because "he had killed" — the punishment fits the crime, death for death, but the method is supernatural suspension rather than execution.

> "My, you have a fine place up there. Let us change. You can come down and I will take your place." (Now this man had been placed up there for punishment, by Rabbit's brother, and he could not get down unless some one came along and proposed to take his place on the tree). "Very well," said the man. "Take off your clothes and come up. I will fasten you in the limbs and you can have all the fun you want."
>
> (020-057–020-062)

### The Unseen Punisher

"Whom he had placed here for punishment, but none had seen him" (020-133) — the punisher operates invisibly. The punished are left in place, visible to passersby but impossible to rescue. This invisibility of the punisher transforms punishment from personal revenge into cosmic justice — no one can appeal to or retaliate against an unseen agent.

### Threatened Punishment

Punishment is also threatened as deterrence: "the punishment that is given you. But after" (017-061) suggests a system where certain violations carry known penalties. The threat of punishment — "far better kill you at once" (017-062) — can be more merciful than the punishment itself, suggesting that punishments could be worse than death.

### The Punishment Cycle

The phrase "for punishment to the man for having placed" (020-074) suggests a recursive quality — punishment for having placed something (or someone) in the wrong place. The punished become warnings: visible, suffering, permanent.

## Cultural Significance

- **Supernatural justice**: Punishment by Rabbit's brother is not human justice but cosmic enforcement — the punisher is unseen, the punishment is absolute
- **Punishment as spectacle**: The punished are left visible — "none had seen him" refers to the punisher, not the punished, who remain on display as warnings
- **Graded punishment**: "The punishment that is given you" implies a known scale — different violations receive different punishments
- **Punishment vs. execution**: "Far better kill you at once" implies punishments could be worse than death, a prolonged state of suffering
