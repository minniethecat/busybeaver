[008-001]On the shore of a lake stood an artichoke with its green leaves waving

[008-002]in the sun. Very proud of itself it was, and well satisfied with the

[008-003]world. In the lake below lived a muskrat in his tepee, and in the

[008-004]evening as the sun set he would come out upon the shore and wander over

[008-005]the bank. One evening he came near the place where the artichoke stood.

[008-006]“Ho, friend,” he said, “you seem rather proud of yourself. Who are you?”

[008-007]“I am the artichoke,” answered the other, “and I have many handsome

[008-008]cousins. But who are you?”

[008-009]“I am the muskrat, and I, too, belong to a large family. I live in the

[008-010]water. I don’t stand all day in one place like a stone.”

[008-011]“If I stand in one place all day,” retorted the artichoke, “at least I

[008-012]don’t swim around in stagnant water, and build my lodge in the mud.”

[008-013]“You are jealous of my fine fur,” sneered the muskrat. “I may build

[008-014]my lodge in the mud, but I always have a clean coat. But you are half

[008-015]buried in the ground, and when men dig you up, you are never clean.”

[008-016]“And your fine coat always smells of musk,” jeered the artichoke.

[008-017]“That is true,” said the muskrat. “But men think well of me,

[008-018]nevertheless. They trap me for the fine sinew in my tail; and handsome

[008-019]young women bite off my tail with their white teeth and make it into

[008-020]thread.”

[008-021]“That’s nothing,” laughed the artichoke. “Handsome young warriors,

[008-022]painted and splendid with feathers, dig me up, brush me off with their

[008-023]shapely hands and eat me without even taking the trouble to wash me

[008-024]off.”
