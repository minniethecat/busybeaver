[004-001]There was a chief’s daughter once who had a great many relations so that

[004-002]everybody knew she belonged to a great family.

[004-003]When she grew up she married and there were born to her twin sons. This

[004-004]caused great rejoicing in her father’s camp, and all the village women

[004-005]came to see the babes. She was very happy.

[004-006]As the babes grew older, their grandmother made for them two saddle bags

[004-007]and brought out a donkey.

[004-008]“My two grandchildren,” said the old lady, “shall ride as is becoming

[004-009]to children having so many relations. Here is this donkey. He is patient

[004-010]and surefooted. He shall carry the babes in the saddle bags, one on

[004-011]either side of his back.”

[004-012]It happened one day that the chief’s daughter and her husband were

[004-013]making ready to go on a camping journey. The father, who was quite proud

[004-014]of his children, brought out his finest pony, and put the saddle bags on

[004-015]the pony’s back.

[004-016]“There,” he said, “my sons shall ride on the pony, not on a donkey; let

[004-017]the donkey carry the pots and kettles.”

[004-018]So his wife loaded the donkey with the household things. She tied the

[004-019]tepee poles into two great bundles, one on either side of the donkey’s

[004-020]back; across them she put the travois net and threw into it the pots and

[004-021]kettles and laid the skin tent across the donkey’s back.

[004-022]But no sooner done than the donkey began to rear and bray and kick. He

[004-023]broke the tent poles and kicked the pots and kettles into bits and tore

[004-024]the skin tent. The more he was beaten the more he kicked.

[004-025]At last they told the grandmother. She laughed. “Did I not tell you the

[004-026]donkey was for the children,” she cried. “He knows the babies are

[004-027]the chief’s children. Think you he will be dishonored with pots and

[004-028]kettles?” and she fetched the children and slung them over the donkey’s

[004-029]back, when he became at once quiet again.

[004-030]The camping party left the village and went on their journey. But the

[004-031]next day as they passed by a place overgrown with bushes, a band of

[004-032]enemies rushed out, lashing their ponies and sounding their war whoop.

[004-033]All was excitement. The men bent their bows and seized their lances.

[004-034]After a long battle the enemy fled. But when the camping party came

[004-035]together again--where were the donkey and the two babes? No one knew.

[004-036]For a long time they searched, but in vain. At last they turned to go

[004-037]back to the village, the father mournful, the mother wailing. When they

[004-038]came to the grandmother’s tepee, there stood the good donkey with the

[004-039]two babes in the saddle bags.
