## Overview

**Pretty Feathered Forehead** (commonly called **Pretty Feather**) is the daughter of a medicine man and the central figure in a tale of friendship and courtship. Two young friends, Chaske and Hake, both love her, but only Chaske is chosen to court her. When Chaske proves himself on the warpath to win her hand, Hake must confront his own feelings and ultimately help rescue Pretty Feather when she is abducted. Her story illustrates Sioux courtship customs and the bonds of male friendship tested by love.

## In the Stories

### The Story of the Two Young Friends (Chapter 030)

Pretty Feather is the daughter of an old medicine man and is much courted by the young men of the village. Hake, one of the two young friends, is the first to notice her and confides his feelings to his friend Chaske, describing her as the most beautiful maiden in the village. Chaske, unaware that he himself will become her suitor, encourages Hake:

> Hake kept wondering what "Pretty Feather" (the girl whom his friend loved) would think of him. (030-051)

When Chaske proves himself worthy by bringing horses as an offering, Pretty Feather's mother is uncertain, but Pretty Feather recognizes the sincerity of the gift:

> Pretty Feather, seeing by this offering that her most devoted suitor had proven himself, accepted Chaske's courtship. (030-107)

After Chaske leaves on an extended journey, Pretty Feather is taken by a rival. Hake, putting aside his own feelings, tracks her abductor and finds her:

> Hake started on with the horses, following the trail until he saw Pretty Feather's spotted horse tied to the side of the tent. (030-264)

Hake gazes upon her face, the face of his friend's lost love, and resolves to restore her to Chaske:

> Turning quickly, he saw Pretty Feather looking at him. Overjoyed, he knew he must return her safely. (030-286)

When the village learns of her safe return, there is great celebration:

> There were cries of joy when it was known that Pretty Feather had been found and was being brought back. (030-349)

At the end of the tale, Hake relinquishes his own claim, telling Chaske: "The saddest moment of my life was when I gave up Pretty Feather." Chaske, grateful, replies: "To Pretty Feather be a good husband." (030-371–030-373)

### Pretty Feathered Forehead (Chapter 036)

The chapter bearing her name tells of how she derived the title "Pretty Feathered Forehead" — a name that speaks to her distinctive beauty and the ornamentation she wore. Her father, described as the great medicine man, plays a protective role in her story.
