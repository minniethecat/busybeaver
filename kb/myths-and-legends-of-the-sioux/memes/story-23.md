[023-001]WARRIOR

[023-002]There was once a young man whose parents were not overburdened with the

[023-003]riches of this world, and consequently could not dress their only son in

[023-004]as rich a costume as the other young men of the tribe, and on account of

[023-005]not being so richly clad as they, he was looked down upon and shunned by

[023-006]them. He was never invited to take part in any of their sports; nor was

[023-007]he ever asked to join any of the war parties.

[023-008]In the village lived an old man with an only daughter. Like the other

[023-009]family, they were poor, but the daughter was the belle of the tribe. She

[023-010]was the most sought after by the young men of the village, and warriors

[023-011]from tribes far distant came to press their suit at winning her for

[023-012]their bride. All to no purpose; she had the same answer for them as she

[023-013]had for the young men of the village.

[023-014]The poor young man was also very handsome despite his poor clothes, but

[023-015]having never killed an enemy nor brought home any enemies’ horses he was

[023-016]not (according to Indian rules) allowed to make love to any young or old

[023-017]woman. He tried in vain to join some of the war parties, that he might

[023-018]get the chance to win his spurs as a warrior. To all his pleadings,

[023-019]came the same answer: “You are not fit to join a war party. You have no

[023-020]horses, and if you should get killed our tribe would be laughed at and

[023-021]be made fun of as you have such poor clothes, and we don’t want the

[023-022]enemy to know that we have any one of our tribe who dresses so poorly as

[023-023]you do.”

[023-024]Again, and again, he tried different parties, only to be made fun of and

[023-025]insulted.

[023-026]One night he sat in the poor tepee of his parents. He was in deep study

[023-027]and had nothing to say. His father, noticing his melancholy mood, asked

[023-028]him what had happened to cause him to be so quiet, as he was always of a

[023-029]jolly disposition. The son answered and said:

[023-030]“Father, I am going on the warpath alone. In vain I have tried to be

[023-031]a member of one of the war parties. To all of my pleadings I have got

[023-032]nothing but insults in return.”

[023-033]“But my son, you have no gun nor ammunition. Where can you get any and

[023-034]how can you get it? We have nothing to buy one for you with,” said the

[023-035]father.

[023-036]“I don’t need any weapons. I am going to bring back some of the enemies’

[023-037]horses, and I don’t need a gun for that.”

[023-038]Early the next morning (regardless of the old couple’s pleadings not

[023-039]to go unarmed) the young man left the village and headed northwest, the

[023-040]direction always taken by the war parties.

[023-041]For ten days he traveled without seeing any signs of a camp. The evening

[023-042]of the tenth day, he reached a very high butte, thickly wooded at the

[023-043]summit. He ascended this butte, and as he sat there between two large

[023-044]boulders, watching the beautiful rays of the setting sun, he was

[023-045]suddenly startled to hear the neigh of a horse. Looking down into the

[023-046]beautiful valley which was threaded by a beautiful creek fringed with

[023-047]timber, he noticed close to the base of the butte upon which he sat, a

[023-048]large drove of horses grazing peacefully and quietly. Looking closer, he

[023-049]noticed at a little distance from the main drove, a horse with a saddle

[023-050]on his back. This was the one that had neighed, as the drove drifted

[023-051]further away from him. He was tied by a long lariat to a large sage

[023-052]bush.

[023-053]Where could the rider be, he said to himself. As if in answer to his

[023-054]question, there appeared not more than twenty paces from him a middle

[023-055]aged man coming up through a deep ravine. The man was evidently in

[023-056]search of some kind of game, as he held his gun in readiness for instant

[023-057]use, and kept his eyes directed at every crevice and clump of bush.

[023-058]So intent was he on locating the game he was trailing, that he never

[023-059]noticed the young man who sat like a statue not twenty paces away.

[023-060]Slowly and cautiously the man approached, and when he had advanced to

[023-061]within a few paces of the young man he stopped and turning around, stood

[023-062]looking down into the valley. This was the only chance that our brave

[023-063]young friend had. Being unarmed, he would stand no show if the enemy

[023-064]ever got a glimpse of him. Slowly and noiselessly he drew his hunting

[023-065]knife (which his father had given him on his departure from home) and

[023-066]holding it securely in his right hand, gathered himself and gave a leap

[023-067]which landed him upon the unsuspecting enemy’s shoulders. The force with

[023-068]which he landed on the enemy caused him (the enemy) to lose his hold on

[023-069]his gun, and it went rattling down into the chasm, forty feet below.

[023-070]Down they came together, the young man on top. No sooner had they struck

[023-071]the ground than the enemy had out his knife, and then commenced a hand

[023-072]to hand duel. The enemy, having more experience, was getting the best of

[023-073]our young friend. Already our young friend had two ugly cuts, one across

[023-074]his chest and the other through his forearm.

[023-075]He was becoming weak from the loss of blood, and could not stand the

[023-076]killing pace much longer. Summoning all his strength for one more trial

[023-077]to overcome his antagonist, he rushed him toward the chasm, and in his

[023-078]hurry to get away from this fierce attack, the enemy stepped back one

[023-079]step too far, and down they both went into the chasm. Interlocked in

[023-080]each other’s arms, the young man drove his knife into the enemy’s

[023-081]side and when they struck the bottom the enemy relaxed his hold and

[023-082]straightened out stiff and dead.

[023-083]Securing his scalp and gun, the young man proceeded down to where the

[023-084]horse was tied to the sage bush, and then gathering the drove of horses

[023-085]proceeded on his return to his own village. Being wounded severely he

[023-086]had to ride very slowly. All the long hours of the night he drove the

[023-087]horses towards his home village.

[023-088]In the meantime, those at the enemies’ camp wondered at the long absence

[023-089]of the herder who was watching their drove of horses, and finally seven

[023-090]young men went to search for the missing herder. All night long they

[023-091]searched the hillsides for the horses and herder, and when it had grown

[023-092]light enough in the morning they saw by the ground where there had been

[023-093]a fierce struggle.

[023-094]Following the tracks in the sand and leaves, they came to the chasm

[023-095]where the combatants had fallen over, and there, lying on his back

[023-096]staring up at them in death, was their herder. They hastened to the camp

[023-097]and told what they had found. Immediately the warriors mounted their war

[023-098]ponies (these ponies are never turned loose, but kept tied close to the

[023-099]tepee of the owner), and striking the trail of the herd driven off by

[023-100]our young friend, they urged forth their ponies and were soon far from

[023-101]their camp on the trail of our young friend. All day long they traveled

[023-102]on his trail, and just as the sun was sinking they caught sight of him

[023-103]driving the drove ahead over a high hill. Again they urged forth their

[023-104]tired ponies. The young man, looking back along the trail, saw some dark

[023-105]objects coming along, and, catching a fresh horse, drove the rest ahead

[023-106]at a great rate. Again all night he drove them, and when daylight came

[023-107]he looked back (from a high butte) over his trail and saw coming over a

[023-108]distant raise, two horsemen. These two undoubtedly rode the best ponies,

[023-109]as he saw nothing of the others. Driving the horses into a thick belt

[023-110]of timber, he concealed himself close to the trail made by the drove of

[023-111]horses, and lay in ambush for the two daring horsemen who had followed

[023-112]him so far. Finally they appeared on the butte from where he had looked

[023-113]back and saw them following him. For a long time they sat there scouring

[023-114]the country before them in hopes that they might see some signs of their

[023-115]stolen horses. Nothing could they see. Had they but known, their horses

[023-116]were but a few hundred yards from them, but the thick timber securely

[023-117]hid them from view. Finally one of them arose and pointed to the timber.

[023-118]Then leaving his horse in charge of his friend, he descended the butte

[023-119]and followed the trail of the drove to where they had entered the

[023-120]timber. Little did he think that he was standing on the brink of

[023-121]eternity. The young man hiding not more than a hundred yards from him

[023-122]could have shot him there where he stood, but wanting to play fair, he

[023-123]stepped into sight. When he did, the enemy took quick aim and fired. He

[023-124]was too hasty. Had he taken more careful aim he might have killed our

[023-125]young friend, but his bullet whizzed harmlessly over the young man’s

[023-126]head and buried itself in a tree. The young man took good aim and fired.

[023-127]The enemy threw up both hands and fell forward on his face. The other

[023-128]one on the hill, seeing his friend killed, hastily mounted his horse

[023-129]and leading his friend’s horse, made rapidly off down the butte in the

[023-130]direction from whence he had come. Waiting for some time to be sure the

[023-131]one who was alive did not come up and take a shot at him, he finally

[023-132]advanced upon the fallen enemy and securing his gun, ammunition and

[023-133]scalp, went to his horse and drove the herd on through the woods and

[023-134]crossing a long flat prairie, ascended a long chain of hills and sat

[023-135]looking back along his trail in search of any of the enemy who might

[023-136]continue to follow him.

[023-137]Thus he sat until the long shadows of the hills reminded him that it

[023-138]would soon be sunset, and as he must get some sleep, he wanted to find

[023-139]some creek bend where he could drive the bunch of ponies and feel safe

[023-140]as to their not straying off during the night. He found a good place for

[023-141]the herd, and catching a fresh horse, he picketed him close to where he

[023-142]was going to sleep, and wrapping himself in his blanket, was soon fast

[023-143]asleep. So tired and sleepy was he that a heavy rain which had come up,

[023-144]during the night, soaked him through and through, but he never awakened

[023-145]until the sun was high in the east.

[023-146]He awoke and going to the place where he had left the herd, he was

[023-147]glad to find them all there. He mounted his horse and started his herd

[023-148]homeward again. For two days he drove them, and on the evening of the

[023-149]second day he came in sight of the village.

[023-150]The older warriors, hearing of the young man going on this trip alone

[023-151]and unarmed, told the parents to go in mourning for their son, as he

[023-152]would never come back alive. When the people of the village saw this

[023-153]large drove of horses advancing towards them, they at first thought

[023-154]it was a war party of the enemy, and so the head men called the young

[023-155]warriors together and fully prepared for a great battle. They advanced

[023-156]upon the supposed enemy. When they got close enough to discern a lone

[023-157]horseman driving this large herd, they surrounded the horses and lone

[023-158]warrior, and brought him triumphantly into camp. On arriving in the camp

[023-159](or village) the horses were counted and the number counted up to one

[023-160]hundred and ten head.

[023-161]The chief and his criers (or heralds) announced through the whole

[023-162]village that there would be a great war dance given in honor of the Lone

[023-163]Warrior.

[023-164]The whole village turned out and had a great war dance that was kept

[023-165]up three days and three nights. The two scalps which the young man had

[023-166]taken were tied to a pole which was placed in the center of the dance

[023-167]circle. At this dance, the Lone Warrior gave to each poor family five

[023-168]head of horses.

[023-169]Being considered eligible now to pay his respects to any girl who took

[023-170]his fancy, he at once went to the camp of the beautiful girl of the

[023-171]tribe, and as he was always her choice, she at once consented to marry

[023-172]him.

[023-173]The news spread through the village that Lone Warrior had won the belle

[023-174]of the nation for his bride, and this with the great feat which he had

[023-175]accomplished alone in killing two enemies and bringing home a great herd

[023-176]of horses, raised him to the rank of chief, which he faithfully filled

[023-177]to the end of his days. And many times he had to tell his grandchildren

[023-178]the story of how he got the name of the Lone Warrior.
