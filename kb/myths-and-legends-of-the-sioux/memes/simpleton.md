## Overview

**The Simpleton** is a classic **wise fool** figure in Sioux folklore — a young man whom the tribe considers foolish (015-014), but whose apparent simplicity masks profound insight.

In a time of crisis, when a mother's grief threatens to destroy her, it is the Simpleton who finds the solution that wiser men could not see. His story embodies the Sioux recognition that wisdom does not always wear the clothes of authority.

## In the Stories

### The Simpleton's Wisdom (Chapter 015)

A mother loses her beloved daughter and is consumed by grief. She cuts off her hair, cuts gashes in her cheeks, and sits before the corpse refusing to let anyone touch the body for burial:

> "I am weary of life. I do not care to live. I will stab myself with this knife and join my daughter in the land of spirits." (015-001–015-012)

The tribe's wisest men attempt to reason with her but fail. The Simpleton, observing their failure (015-018), approaches differently — not with reason but with compassion and clever misdirection.

He diverts her attention, allowing the burial to proceed, and through patient care brings the mother back from the edge of despair (015-034):

> Where the wise men of the tribe had failed with their arguments, the Simpleton succeeded through kindness and the wisdom of the heart. (015-030–015-045)

## Character Traits

The Simpleton embodies the **paradox of wisdom** — that those who appear least qualified may possess the deepest understanding. His success where learned men fail mirrors the trickster tradition, but without malice: the Simpleton tricks not for gain but for healing. He represents emotional intelligence before the term existed.
