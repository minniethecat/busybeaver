## Overview

The **Lone Warrior** is a Sioux brave whose name commemorates his solitary warpath journey — an unusual and dangerous undertaking that demonstrated exceptional courage. Rather than keeping the spoils of his exploits for himself, he distributed them generously to the neediest families of his village. His story embodies the Sioux ideal of the warrior who fights not for personal glory but for the welfare of the community.

## In the Stories

### The Brave Who Went on the Warpath Alone (Chapter 023)

After making the bold decision to ride to war alone — a venture that most warriors would only undertake in a party — the Lone Warrior returns successful. Rather than hoarding his gains, he uses them to benefit the poorest members of his village:

> At this dance, the Lone Warrior gave to each poor family five horses from the spoils he had taken on his solitary raid. (023-167)

His generous act elevates his standing in the community. News of his deeds spreads through the village, and his reputation as both a fearless warrior and a generous spirit is cemented:

> Word spread through the village that the Lone Warrior had won not only victory in battle but also the admiration of all for his open-handedness. (023-173)

The story concludes by explaining how he earned his distinctive name:

> This is the story of how he got the name of the Lone Warrior — a name that spoke both of his solitary courage and his communal heart. (023-178)

The Lone Warrior represents an important Sioux cultural ideal: that the highest form of bravery is not merely facing the enemy alone but using the fruits of that courage to uplift the entire tribe. His solitary warpath and subsequent generosity made him a model for young warriors to emulate.
