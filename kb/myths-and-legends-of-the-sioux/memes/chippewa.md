## Overview

The Chippewa were a neighboring tribe of the Sioux who appear in Sioux folklore. They lived in villages near lakes and interacted with the Sioux through both conflict and cultural exchange. They figure most prominently in the story of the Wonderful Turtle, where they are depicted as an established community with their own chiefs and warriors.

## In the Stories

### The Wonderful Turtle (Chapter 28)

The primary story featuring the Chippewa is that of the Wonderful Turtle. Near a Chippewa village lay a large lake in which lived an enormous and wise turtle who regularly visited the Chippewa head chief to smoke and talk (028-001–028-005). The turtle was recognized for his wisdom and eventually appointed as judge over tribal disputes (028-006–028-028). The chief gave his daughter, "the most beautiful maiden of the Chippewa nation," to the turtle in marriage (028-029–028-031).

### Sioux–Chippewa Conflict

The tension between the two tribes is directly described. The Chippewas and Sioux had a great battle in which the Sioux overwhelmed their rivals:

> The Chippewas and Sioux had a great battle, and the Sioux, too numerous for the Chippewas, were killing them off so fast that they had to run for their lives.
>
> (028-041–028-044)

Later, the Sioux made an attack upon the Chippewas and every one deserted the village (028-053–028-054). The turtle was left behind and slept through the winter underwater (028-055–028-063). Upon waking, he searched for the Chippewa camp to reunite with his wife (028-064–028-067).

## Inter-Tribal Relations

Beyond warfare, the stories record more complex relations. Sioux warriors traveled to court the Chippewa chief's daughter, crossing tribal boundaries for marriage (028-030–028-032). This practice appears in the broader pattern of Sioux courtship customs, where unions between members of different tribes were recognized if the father consented.
