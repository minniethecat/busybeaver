## Overview

**Turtle** (Lakota: **Keya**) appears in Sioux and neighboring tribal folklore as a figure of wisdom and prudence. In the tales collected by McLaughlin, Turtle serves as judge for his tribe and cleverly defends his honor against young men who mock his flat stomach.

A separate tale tells of a boy's turtle hunt, establishing Turtle's dual role as both character and quarry.

## In the Stories

### The Wonderful Turtle (Chapter 028)

Turtle lives in a large lake near a Chippewa village and regularly visits the head chief, smoking and talking with him. When a dispute threatens to cause bloodshed, the chief summons Turtle to judge (028-013):

> "I will call Mr. Turtle. He will judge for you." (028-001–028-015)

Turtle hears both sides attentively and delivers a verdict that satisfies both parties — each is partly right and partly wrong — which earns him the tribe's lasting respect:

> "You are both in the right in some ways and wrong in others. Therefore, I will say that you both are equally in the right." (028-016–028-030)

The chief is so pleased that he gives Turtle his only daughter in marriage. When young men mock Turtle's flat stomach, he turns their mockery into a story about surviving a Sioux war party (028-040):

> "My friends, had you been in my place, you too would have flat stomachs." (028-031–028-055)

### The Boy and the Turtles (Chapter 025)

A boy goes hunting for turtles at a little lake where the tribe always hunted them, treating turtles as game animals valued for their meat and shells.

## Character Traits

Turtle embodies the **wise counselor** archetype: slow-moving but deep-thinking, physically unimpressive but intellectually formidable. His ability to resolve disputes without bloodshed marks him as a peacemaker, a role highly valued in Plains tribal societies.
