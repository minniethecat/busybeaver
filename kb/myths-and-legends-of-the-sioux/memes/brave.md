## Overview

In Sioux tradition, a **brave** was not merely a fighter but a warrior who had proven his courage through individual acts of bravery. Unlike the generic role of warrior, the title of "brave" carried social recognition — it was a status earned, not inherited. "Whether I shall be brave or not I do not know" (007-016) reflects the uncertainty every young warrior faced before proving himself in combat.

A brave's deeds were recounted and remembered. "He was one who was noted for his bravery, and many were the brave acts he had performed" (024-009–024-010). The community measured a man by his courage: individual fights revealed "the one who put up such a brave fight as had this man" (030-333).

The council recognized braves whose actions served the people (see [[council]]). Even animal figures in the tales could be called brave — "The Turtle is brave. We will bother him no more" (028-052) — showing that the quality transcended human warriors alone. The bravest among them — "one of the bravest of Sioux warriors who ever lived" (024-103) — were celebrated in oral tradition, their careers ended only by death in battle.

> Among the scouts was one who was noted for his bravery, and many were the brave acts he had performed. His name was Big Eagle. This man they selected to go to the lone camp and obtain the information for which they were waiting.
>
> (024-009–024-012)
