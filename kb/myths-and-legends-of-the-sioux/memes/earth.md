## Overview

In Sioux belief, the **Earth** was not mere ground but a conscious, maternal being — _Mother Earth_ — the source of all life and partner to the Great Mystery. Prayers addressed "the Great Mystery and to mother earth" (013-020), pairing the sky-father principle with the earth-mother in a dual cosmic reverence. The earth received the dead in burial, witnessed sacred ceremonies, and was invoked as a witness to truth and oaths.

## In the Stories

### Mother Earth in Prayer

The most explicit Sioux theological statement about the earth appears in the White Buffalo Woman's ceremonial instructions: offerings were made "to the Great Mystery and to mother earth" (013-020). This pairing reveals the earth as a divine feminine counterpart to the masculine Great Mystery — a recurring motif in Plains Indian cosmology. The earth was not a passive stage but an active, revered participant in spiritual life.

### Earth in Battle and Death

When warriors faced overwhelming odds, the earth held their bodies in death. After a great battle, "the earth seemed covered with wolves" (010-059) as scavengers descended upon the fallen — a stark image of the earth receiving the dead. The earth also offered concealment: a hunter noticed a character "scratching the earth" (020-156) as part of a ruse, and in another tale a mother "noticed a small hole in the earth" (036-159) that revealed hidden tracks.

> In a moment the earth seemed covered with wolves. They fell greedily on
> the meat pile and in a short time had eaten the last scrap.
>
> (010-059)

### Earth as Substance and Shelter

The bear clan used earth ritually: "the bear would throw earth on the blood and dry it up" (022-016), suggesting earth's absorptive and purifying qualities. Burial customs involved covering the dead: hunters "put them into it and cover them with earth" (026-055), returning bodies to the maternal ground. In one tale, a magical tree root that "grew very fast to the earth" (029-058) held a person captive, showing earth as both anchor and prison.

### Earth Trembling

The earth could tremble with supernatural force. A great battle produced "a roar that fairly shook the earth" (037-382), and warriors felt "the earth beneath their feet" (030-326) tremble under the impact of thousands of charging warriors and buffalo. These passages suggest the earth as a responsive entity, capable of registering and amplifying great events.

## Cultural Significance

- Earth is feminine and maternal ("mother earth"), paired with the masculine Great Mystery in prayer
- The earth received the dead — burial customs returned bodies to the earth
- Earth had absorptive and purifying qualities: dried blood and covered remains
- Earth could conceal (holes, scratching), anchor (roots), and tremble in response to great events
- The phrase "on this earth" was used to assert purpose or futility (019-012–014), showing earth as the stage of meaningful existence
- Earth's centrality in Sioux cosmology mirrors broader Plains Indian belief systems
