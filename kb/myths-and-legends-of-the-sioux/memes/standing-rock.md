## Overview

**Standing Rock** (_Inyan Wakan_ — "Holy Stone" in Sioux) is both a physical landmark on the Standing Rock Sioux Reservation along the Missouri River and a sacred site with a powerful origin legend. According to Sioux tradition, the rock is the petrified form of an Arikara woman who refused to move from her camp when her husband took a second wife. The stone is considered _wakan_ (holy) and was carried with the people whenever they moved camp.

## In the Stories

The legend of Standing Rock (012-001) tells of a Dakota man who married an Arikara woman and later took a second wife. The first wife, consumed by jealousy, refused to move when the village broke camp:

> "She did not answer, and he put out his hand and touched her head. She had turned to stone!" (012-013–012-014)

The entire village returned to find her — a block of stone where the woman had sat. Rather than leave her behind:

> "The stone was thought _'wakan'_ (holy), and was given a place of honor in the center of the camp. Whenever the camp moved the stone and travois were taken along." (012-023–012-025)

The stone was carried for many years before finally being brought to Standing Rock Agency, where it now rests upon a brick pedestal in front of the Agency office.

## Modern Location

The Standing Rock Sioux Reservation spans the border between North and South Dakota along the Missouri River. The stone itself is housed at the Standing Rock Agency office, a tangible link to the legend that has endured for generations.
