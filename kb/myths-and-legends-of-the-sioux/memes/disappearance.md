## Overview

**Disappearance** in Sioux folklore is rarely mundane. Characters who vanish do so into clouds (020-048), into the forest (020-068), into the western sky with thunder beings (029-083), or into darkness itself (033-094). These disappearances mark boundaries between the ordinary and the supernatural — a person who disappears has entered a different layer of reality, whether the spirit world, the sky realm, or the domain of a powerful being.

## In the Stories

### The Cloud Disappearance

In one tale, a figure vanishes into a cloud: "and the cloud disappeared" (020-048) — but the cloud itself had contained a person, and its dissolution marked their departure from the visible world. Moments later, "the man disappeared in the forest" (020-068), and "scarcely had he disappeared, when the brother arrived" (020-069), the timing suggesting an intentional supernatural evasion.

### The Thunder Being's Bride

One of the most vivid disappearances occurs when "the girl ascended and disappeared into the west with her Thunder Beings" (029-083). This is not an abduction but a translation to the sky realm — the girl is taken as a bride by thunder spirits, and her disappearance from the earthly village marks her new status as a sky-dweller.

### The Final Disappearance

Some disappearances are permanent: a figure "disappeared and was seen no more by any woman" (030-240), a terminal vanishing that ends the character's presence in the narrative. Others are brief — the crow who "disappeared, over which there was great grief" (031-045) returns, making the disappearance a test of loyalty.

### Into the Darkness

A spirit "arose and disappeared into the darkness" (033-094), the night swallowing the figure as if it had never been. Game animals "disappeared as suddenly as though" (034-022) taken by an unseen hand. A trickster "disappeared over a hill" (035-141), the landscape itself becoming an accomplice to vanishing.

## Cultural Significance

- **Disappearance as transformation**: Unlike death, disappearance in Sioux tales often signals a change of state — becoming a sky-being, a spirit, or a creature of another realm
- **The West as destination**: Thunder-being disappearances consistently point westward, aligning with the Lakota association of the West with the thunder beings (Wakinyan) and the spirit world
- **Supernatural timing**: Disappearances frequently occur at narratively significant moments — just before an arrival, just after a transgression, marking the threshold between story acts
- **Grief and return**: Grief over a disappearance (031-045) tests the community, and return from disappearance carries the weight of rebirth
