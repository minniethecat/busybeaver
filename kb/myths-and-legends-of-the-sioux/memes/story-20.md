[020-001]Once upon a time there were two brothers, one a great Genie and the

[020-002]other a rabbit. Like all genie, the older could change himself into any

[020-003]kind of an animal, bird, fish, cloud, thunder and lightning, or in fact

[020-004]anything that he desired.

[020-005]The younger brother (the rabbit) was very mischievous and was

[020-006]continually getting into all kinds of trouble. His older brother was

[020-007]kept busy getting Rabbit out of all kinds of scrapes.

[020-008]When Rabbit had attained his full growth he wanted to travel around and

[020-009]see something of the world. When he told his brother what he intended to

[020-010]do, the brother said: “Now, Rabbit, you are Witkotko (mischievous), so

[020-011]be very careful, and keep out of trouble as much as possible. In case

[020-012]you get into any serious trouble, and can’t get out by yourself, just

[020-013]call on me for assistance, and no matter where you are, I will come to

[020-014]you.”

[020-015]Rabbit started out and the first day he came to a very high house,

[020-016]outside of which stood a very high pine tree. So high was the tree that

[020-017]Rabbit could hardly see the top. Outside the door, on an enormous stool,

[020-018]sat a very large giant fast asleep. Rabbit (having his bow and arrows

[020-019]with him) strung up his bow, and, taking an arrow from his quiver, said:

[020-020]“I want to see how big this man is, so I guess I will wake him up.” So

[020-021]saying he moved over to one side and took good aim, and shot the giant

[020-022]upon the nose. This stung like fire and awoke the giant, who jumped up,

[020-023]crying: “Who had the audacity to shoot me on the nose?” “I did,” said

[020-024]Rabbit.

[020-025]The giant, hearing a voice, looked all around, but saw nothing, until he

[020-026]looked down at the corner of the house, and there sat a rabbit.

[020-027]“I had hiccoughs this morning and thought that I was going to have a

[020-028]good big meal, and here is nothing but a toothful.”

[020-029]“I guess you won’t make a toothful of me,” said Rabbit, “I am as strong

[020-030]as you, though I am little.” “We will see,” said the giant. He went into

[020-031]the house and came out, bringing a hammer that weighed many tons.

[020-032]“Now, Mr. Rabbit, we will see who can throw this hammer over the top of

[020-033]that tree.” “Get something harder to do,” said Rabbit.

[020-034]“Well, we will try this first,” said the giant. With that he grasped the

[020-035]hammer in both hands, swung it three times around his head and sent it

[020-036]spinning thru the air. Up, up, it went, skimming the top of the tree,

[020-037]and came down, shaking the ground and burying itself deep into the

[020-038]earth.

[020-039]“Now,” said the giant, “if you don’t accomplish this same feat, I am

[020-040]going to swallow you at one mouthful.” Rabbit said, “I always sing to my

[020-041]brother before I attempt things like this.” So he commenced singing and

[020-042]calling his brother. “Cinye! Cinye!” (brother, brother) he sang. The

[020-043]giant grew nervous, and said: “Boy, why do you call your brother?”

[020-044]Pointing to a small black cloud that was approaching very swiftly,

[020-045]Rabbit said: “That is my brother; he can destroy you, your house, and

[020-046]pine tree in one breath.”

[020-047]“Stop him and you can go free,” said the giant. Rabbit waved his paws

[020-048]and the cloud disappeared.

[020-049]From this place Rabbit continued on his trip towards the west. The next

[020-050]day, while passing thru a deep forest, he thought he heard some one

[020-051]moaning, as though in pain. He stopped and listened; soon the wind blew

[020-052]and the moaning grew louder. Following the direction from whence came

[020-053]the sound, he soon discovered a man stripped of his clothing, and caught

[020-054]between two limbs of a tall elm tree. When the wind blew the limbs would

[020-055]rub together and squeeze the man, who would give forth the mournful

[020-056]groans.

[020-057]“My, you have a fine place up there. Let us change. You can come down

[020-058]and I will take your place.” (Now this man had been placed up there for

[020-059]punishment, by Rabbit’s brother, and he could not get down unless some

[020-060]one came along and proposed to take his place on the tree). “Very well,”

[020-061]said the man. “Take off your clothes and come up. I will fasten you in

[020-062]the limbs and you can have all the fun you want.”

[020-063]Rabbit disrobed and climbed up. The man placed him between the limbs and

[020-064]slid down the tree. He hurriedly got into Rabbit’s clothes, and just as

[020-065]he had completed his toilet, the wind blew very hard. Rabbit was nearly

[020-066]crazy with pain, and screamed and cried. Then he began to cry “Cinye,

[020-067]Cinye” (brother, brother). “Call your brother as much as you like, he

[020-068]can never find me.” So saying the man disappeared in the forest.

[020-069]Scarcely had he disappeared, when the brother arrived, and seeing Rabbit

[020-070]in the tree, said: “Which way did he go?” Rabbit pointed the direction

[020-071]taken by the man. The brother flew over the top of the trees, soon found

[020-072]the man and brought him back, making him take his old place between the

[020-073]limbs, and causing a heavy wind to blow and continue all afternoon and

[020-074]night, for punishment to the man for having placed his brother up there.

[020-075]After Rabbit got his clothes back on, his brother gave him a good

[020-076]scolding, and wound up by saying: “I want you to be more careful in the

[020-077]future. I have plenty of work to keep me as busy as I want to be, and I

[020-078]can’t be stopping every little while to be making trips to get you out

[020-079]of some foolish scrape. It was only yesterday that I came five hundred

[020-080]miles to help you from the giant, and today I have had to come a

[020-081]thousand miles, so be more careful from this on.”

[020-082]Several days after this the Rabbit was traveling along the banks of a

[020-083]small river, when he came to a small clearing in the woods, and in the

[020-084]center of the clearing stood a nice little log hut. Rabbit was wondering

[020-085]who could be living here when the door slowly opened and an old man

[020-086]appeared in the doorway, bearing a tripe water pail in his right hand.

[020-087]In his left hand he held a string which was fastened to the inside of

[020-088]the house. He kept hold of the string and came slowly down to the river.

[020-089]When he got to the water he stooped down and dipped the pail into it and

[020-090]returned to the house, still holding the string for guidance.

[020-091]Soon he reappeared holding on to another string, and, following this

[020-092]one, went to a large pile of wood and returned to the house with it.

[020-093]Rabbit wanted to see if the old man would come out again, but he came

[020-094]out no more. Seeing smoke ascending from the mud chimney, he thought

[020-095]he would go over and see what the old man was doing. He knocked at the

[020-096]door, and a weak voice bade him enter. He noticed that the old man was

[020-097]cooking dinner.

[020-098]“Hello Tunkasina (grandfather), you must have a nice time, living here

[020-099]alone. I see that you have everything handy. You can get wood and water,

[020-100]and that is all you have to do. How do you get your provisions?”

[020-101]“The wolves bring my meat, the mice my rice and ground beans, and the

[020-102]birds bring me the cherry leaves for my tea. Yet it is a hard life, as I

[020-103]am all alone most of the time and have no one to talk to, and besides, I

[020-104]am blind.”

[020-105]“Say, grandfather,” said Rabbit, “let us change places. I think I would

[020-106]like to live here.”

[020-107]“If we exchange clothes,” said the other, “you will become old and

[020-108]blind, while I will assume your youth and good looks.” (Now, this old

[020-109]man was placed here for punishment by Rabbit’s brother. He had killed

[020-110]his wife, so the genie made him old and blind, and he would remain so

[020-111]until some one came who would exchange places with him).

[020-112]“I don’t care for youth and good looks,” said Rabbit, “let us make the

[020-113]change.”

[020-114]They changed clothes, and Rabbit became old and blind, whilst the old

[020-115]man became young and handsome.

[020-116]“Well, I must go,” said the man. He went out and cutting the strings

[020-117]close to the door, ran off laughing. “You will get enough of your living

[020-118]alone, you crazy boy,” and saying this he ran into the woods.

[020-119]Rabbit thought he would like to get some fresh water and try the string

[020-120]paths so that he would get accustomed to it. He bumped around the room

[020-121]and finally found the tripe water bucket. He took hold of the string and

[020-122]started out. When he had gotten a short distance from the door he came

[020-123]to the end of the string so suddenly, that he lost the end which he

[020-124]had in his hand, and he wandered about, bumping against the trees, and

[020-125]tangling himself up in plum bushes and thorns, scratching his face

[020-126]and hands so badly that the blood ran from them. Then it was that he

[020-127]commenced again to cry, “Cinye! Cinye!” (brother, brother). Soon his

[020-128]brother arrived, and asked which way the old man had gone.

[020-129]“I don’t know,” said Rabbit, “I couldn’t see which path he took, as I

[020-130]was blind.”

[020-131]The genie called the birds, and they came flying from every direction.

[020-132]As fast as they arrived the brother asked them if they had seen the man

[020-133]whom he had placed here for punishment, but none had seen him. The owl

[020-134]came last, and when asked if he had seen the man, he said “hoo-hoo.”

[020-135]“The man who lived here,” said the brother. “Last night I was hunting

[020-136]mice in the woods south of here and I saw a man sleeping beneath a plum

[020-137]tree. I thought it was your brother, Rabbit, so I didn’t awaken him,”

[020-138]said the owl.

[020-139]“Good for you, owl,” said the brother, “for this good news, you shall

[020-140]hereafter roam around only at night, and I will fix your eyes, so the

[020-141]darker the night the better you will be able to see. You will always

[020-142]have the fine cool nights to hunt your food. You other birds can hunt

[020-143]your food during the hot daylight.” (Since then the owl has been the

[020-144]night bird).

[020-145]The brother flew to the woods and brought the man back and cut the

[020-146]strings short, and said to him: “Now you can get a taste of what you

[020-147]gave my brother.”

[020-148]To Rabbit he said: “I ought not to have helped you this time. Any one

[020-149]who is so crazy as to change places with a blind man should be left

[020-150]without help, so be careful, as I am getting tired of your foolishness,

[020-151]and will not help you again if you do anything as foolish as you did

[020-152]this time.”

[020-153]Rabbit started to return to his home. When he had nearly completed his

[020-154]journey he came to a little creek, and being thirsty took a good long

[020-155]drink. While he was drinking he heard a noise as though a wolf or cat

[020-156]was scratching the earth. Looking up to a hill which overhung the creek,

[020-157]he saw four wolves, with their tails intertwined, pulling with all their

[020-158]might. As Rabbit came up to them one pulled loose, and Rabbit saw that

[020-159]his tail was broken.

[020-160]“Let me pull tails with you. My tail is long and strong,” said Rabbit,

[020-161]and the wolves assenting, Rabbit interlocked his long tail with those

[020-162]of the three wolves and commenced pulling and the wolves pulled so

[020-163]hard that they pulled Rabbit’s tail off at the second joint. The wolves

[020-164]disappeared.

[020-165]“Cinye! Cinye! (Brother, brother.) I have lost my tail,” cried Rabbit.

[020-166]The genie came and seeing his brother Rabbit’s tail missing, said: “You

[020-167]look better without a tail anyway.”

[020-168]From that time on rabbits have had no tails.
