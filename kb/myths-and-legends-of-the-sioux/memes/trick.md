## Overview

The **trick** is a fundamental narrative mechanism in Sioux folklore. Trickster figures — Rabbit, Unktomi (the spider), and others — use cunning and deception to achieve their ends, often against stronger opponents. The trick can be playful (the elk pretending to be caught in Rabbit's snare), malicious (Unktomi's various schemes), or self-defeating (the trickster outwitted by a wiser opponent). Across the tales, the trick serves as a leveling force: the weak use cunning to overcome the strong, and the clever survive where the powerful cannot.

## In the Stories

### Rabbit's Tricks

Rabbit, the primary trickster of Sioux folklore, appears in numerous stories. His tricks include: pretending that an elk has been caught in his bowstring when the elk was merely playing dead (005-006–005-010); deceiving the grouse girls into a bag (006-006–006-008); and escaping death through quick thinking and clever lies.

### Unktomi's Tricks

Unktomi (the spider) is another trickster figure. His trickery often backfires, as when his schemes against others are discovered and turned against him. In Chapter 035, White Plume's party encounters Unktomi's tricks, including an entire village of spider people who attempt to deceive them (035-136, 035-209, 035-286).

### The Trick That Fails

Not all tricks succeed. The elk's trick on Rabbit, pretending to be dead in the snare, leads to Rabbit's joyous (and premature) declaration of success (005-009–005-010). The trick that fails is as instructive as the trick that succeeds — it reminds listeners that no deception is foolproof.

## Cultural Significance

- **Trick as leveling force**: The physically weak (Rabbit) uses tricks to overcome the strong (Bear, Elk)
- **Trick as moral test**: Characters who rely on trickery often face consequences when their deceptions are discovered
- **Trick as entertainment**: The tales celebrate the clever trick, inviting listeners to admire ingenuity even when it serves selfish ends
