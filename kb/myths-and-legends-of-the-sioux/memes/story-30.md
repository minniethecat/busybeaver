[030-001]There were once in a very large Indian camp two little boys who were

[030-002]fast friends. One of the boys, “Chaske” (meaning first born), was the

[030-003]son of a very rich family, and was always dressed in the finest of

[030-004]clothes of Indian costume. The other boy, “Hake” (meaning last

[030-005]born), was an orphan and lived with his old grandmother, who was very

[030-006]destitute, and consequently could not dress the boy in fine raiment.

[030-007]So poorly was the boy dressed that the boys who had good clothes always

[030-008]tormented him and would not play in his company.

[030-009]Chaske did not look at the clothes of any boy whom he chose as a friend,

[030-010]but mingled with all boys regardless of how they were clad, and would

[030-011]study their dispositions. The well dressed he found were vain and

[030-012]conceited. The fairly well dressed he found selfish and spiteful. The

[030-013]poorly clad he found to be generous and truthful, and from all of them

[030-014]he chose “Hake” for his “Koda” (friend). As Chaske was the son of the

[030-015]leading war chief he was very much sought after by the rest of the boys,

[030-016]each one trying to gain the honor of being chosen for the friend and

[030-017]companion of the great chief’s son; but, as I have before said, Chaske

[030-018]carefully studied them all and finally chose the orphan Hake.

[030-019]It was a lucky day for Hake when he was chosen for the friend and

[030-020]companion of Chaske. The orphan boy was taken to the lodge of his

[030-021]friend’s parents and dressed up in fine clothes and moccasins. (When the

[030-022]Indians’ sons claim any one as their friend, the friend thus chosen is

[030-023]adopted into the family as their own son).

[030-024]Chaske and Hake were inseparable. Where one was seen the other was not

[030-025]far distant. They played, hunted, trapped, ate and slept together. They

[030-026]would spend most of the long summer days hunting in the forests.

[030-027]Time went on and these two fast friends grew up to be fine specimens of

[030-028]their tribe. When they became the age to select a sweetheart they would

[030-029]go together and make love to a girl. Each helping the other to win the

[030-030]affection of the one of his choice. Chaske loved a girl who was the

[030-031]daughter of an old medicine man. She was very much courted by the other

[030-032]young men of the tribe, and many a horse loaded with robes and fine

[030-033]porcupine work was tied at the medicine man’s tepee in offering for the

[030-034]hand of his daughter, but the horses, laden as when tied there, were

[030-035]turned loose, signifying that the offer was not accepted.

[030-036]The girl’s choice was Chaske’s friend Hake. Although he had never made

[030-037]love to her for himself, he had always used honeyed words to her and

[030-038]was always loud in his praises for his friend Chaske. One night the two

[030-039]friends had been to see the girl, and on their return Chaske was very

[030-040]quiet, having nothing to say and seemingly in deep study. Always of

[030-041]a bright, jolly and amiable disposition, his silence and moody spell

[030-042]grieved his friend very much, and he finally spoke to Chaske, saying:

[030-043]“Koda, what has come over you? You who were always so jolly and full of

[030-044]fun? Your silence makes me grieve for you and I do not know what you are

[030-045]feeling so downhearted about. Has the girl said anything to you to make

[030-046]you feel thus?”

[030-047]“Wait, friend,” said Chaske, “until morning, and then I will know how to

[030-048]answer your inquiry. Don’t ask me anything more tonight, as my heart is

[030-049]having a great battle with my brain.”

[030-050]Hake bothered his friend no more that night, but he could not sleep. He

[030-051]kept wondering what “Pretty Feather” (the girl whom his friend loved)

[030-052]could have said to Chaske to bring such a change over him. Hake never

[030-053]suspected that he himself was the cause of his friend’s sorrow, for

[030-054]never did he have a thought that it was himself that Pretty Feather

[030-055]loved.

[030-056]The next morning after they had eaten breakfast, Chaske proposed that

[030-057]they should go out on the prairies, and see if they would have the good

[030-058]luck to kill an antelope. Hake went out and got the band of horses, of

[030-059]which there were over a hundred. They selected the fleetest two in the

[030-060]herd, and taking their bows and arrows, mounted and rode away towards

[030-061]the south.

[030-062]Hake was overjoyed to note the change in his friend. His oldtime jollity

[030-063]had returned. They rode out about five miles, and scaring up a drove

[030-064]of antelope they started in hot pursuit, and as their horses were very

[030-065]fleet of foot soon caught up to the drove, and each singling out his

[030-066]choice quickly dispatched him with an arrow. They could easily have

[030-067]killed more of the antelope, but did not want to kill them just for

[030-068]sport, but for food, and knowing that they had now all that their horses

[030-069]could pack home, they dismounted and proceeded to dress their kill.

[030-070]After each had finished packing the kill on his horse, Chaske said:

[030-071]“Let us sit down and have a smoke before we start back. Besides, I have

[030-072]something to tell you which I can tell better sitting still than I can

[030-073]riding along.” Hake came and sat down opposite his friend, and while

[030-074]they smoked Chaske said:

[030-075]“My friend, we have been together for the last twenty years and I

[030-076]have yet the first time to deceive you in any way, and I know I can

[030-077]truthfully say the same of you. Never have I known you to deceive me nor

[030-078]tell me an untruth. I have no brothers or sisters. The only brother’s

[030-079]love I know is yours. The only sister’s love I will know will be Pretty

[030-080]Feather’s, for brother, last night she told me she loved none but you

[030-081]and would marry you and you only. So, brother, I am going to take my

[030-082]antelope to my sister-in-law’s tent and deposit it at her door. Then she

[030-083]will know that her wish will be fulfilled. I thought at first that

[030-084]you had been playing traitor to me and had been making love to her for

[030-085]yourself, but when she explained it all to me and begged me to intercede

[030-086]for her to you, I then knew that I had judged you wrongfully, and that,

[030-087]together with my lost love, made me so quiet and sorrowful last night.

[030-088]So now, brother, take the flower of the nation for your wife, and I will

[030-089]be content to continue through life a lonely bachelor, as never again

[030-090]can I give any woman the place which Pretty Feather had in my heart.”

[030-091]Their pipes being smoked out they mounted their ponies and Chaske

[030-092]started up in a clear, deep voice the beautiful love song of Pretty

[030-093]Feather and his friend Hake.

[030-094]Such is the love between two friends, who claim to be as brothers among

[030-095]the Indians. Chaske gave up his love of a beautiful woman for a man who

[030-096]was in fact no relation to him.

[030-097]Hake said, “I will do as you say, my friend, but before I can marry the

[030-098]medicine man’s daughter, I will have to go on the warpath and do

[030-099]some brave deed, and will start in ten days.” They rode towards home,

[030-100]planning which direction they would travel, and as it was to be their

[030-101]first experience on the warpath, they would seek advice from the old

[030-102]warriors of the tribe.

[030-103]On their arrival at the village Hake took his kill to their own tent,

[030-104]while Chaske took his to the tent of the Medicine Man, and deposited it

[030-105]at the door and rode off towards home.

[030-106]The mother of Pretty Feather did not know whether to take the offering

[030-107]or not, but Pretty Feather, seeing by this offering that her most

[030-108]cherished wish was to be granted, told her mother to take the meat and

[030-109]cook it and invite the old women of the camp to a feast in honor of the

[030-110]son-in-law who was soon to keep them furnished with plenty of meat. Hake

[030-111]and his friend sought out all of the old warriors and gained all the

[030-112]information they desired. Every evening Hake visited his intended wife

[030-113]and many happy evenings they spent together.

[030-114]The morning of the tenth day the two friends left the village and

[030-115]turned their faces toward the west where the camps of the enemy are

[030-116]more numerous than in any other direction. They were not mounted and

[030-117]therefore traveled slowly, so it took about ten days of walking before

[030-118]they saw any signs of the enemy. The old warriors had told them of a

[030-119]thickly wooded creek within the enemies’ bounds. The old men said, “That

[030-120]creek looks the ideal place to camp, but don’t camp there by any means,

[030-121]because there is a ghost who haunts that creek, and any one who camps

[030-122]there is disturbed all through the night, and besides they never return,

[030-123]because the ghost is Wakan (holy), and the enemies conquer the travelers

[030-124]every time.” The friends had extra moccasins with them and one extra

[030-125]blanket, as it was late in the fall and the nights were very cold.

[030-126]They broke camp early one morning and walked all day. Along towards

[030-127]evening, the clouds which had been threatening all day, hurriedly opened

[030-128]their doors and down came the snowflakes thick and fast. Just before it

[030-129]started snowing the friends had noticed a dark line about two miles in

[030-130]advance of them. Chaske spoke to his friend and said: “If this storm

[030-131]continues we will be obliged to stay overnight at Ghost Creek, as

[030-132]I noticed it not far ahead of us, just before the storm set in.” “I

[030-133]noticed it also,” said Hake. “We might as well entertain a ghost all

[030-134]night as to lie out on these open prairies and freeze to death.” So they

[030-135]decided to run the risk and stay in the sheltering woods of Ghost Creek.

[030-136]When they got to the creek it seemed as if they had stepped inside a big

[030-137]tepee, so thick was the brush and timber that the wind could not be felt

[030-138]at all. They hunted and found a place where the brush was very thick and

[030-139]the grass very tall. They quickly pulled the tops of the nearest willows

[030-140]together and by intertwining the ends made them fast, and throwing

[030-141]their tent robe over this, soon had a cosy tepee in which to sleep.

[030-142]They started their fire and cooked some dried buffalo meat and buffalo

[030-143]tallow, and were just about to eat their supper when a figure of a man

[030-144]came slowly in through the door and sat down near where he had entered.

[030-145]Hake, being the one who was doing the cooking, poured out some tea into

[030-146]his own cup, and putting a piece of pounded meat and marrow into a small

[030-147]plate, placed it before the stranger, saying: “Eat, my friend, we are

[030-148]on the warpath and do not carry much of a variety of food with us, but I

[030-149]give you the best we have.”

[030-150]The stranger drew the plate towards him, and commenced eating

[030-151]ravenously. He soon finished his meal and handed the dish and cup back.

[030-152]He had not uttered a word so far. Chaske filled the pipe and handed it

[030-153]to him. He smoked for a few minutes, took one last draw from the pipe

[030-154]and handed it back to Chaske, and then he said: “Now, my friends, I am

[030-155]not a living man, but the wandering spirit of a once great warrior, who

[030-156]was killed in these woods by the enemy whom you two brave young men are

[030-157]now seeking to make war upon. For years I have been roaming these woods

[030-158]in hopes that I might find some one brave enough to stop and listen

[030-159]to me, but all who have camped here in the past have run away at my

[030-160]approach or fired guns or shot arrows at me. For such cowards as these

[030-161]I have always found a grave. They never returned to their homes. Now I

[030-162]have found two brave men whom I can tell what I want done, and if you

[030-163]accomplish what I tell you to do, you will return home with many horses

[030-164]and some scalps dangling from your belts. Just over this range of hills

[030-165]north of us, a large village is encamped for the winter. In that camp is

[030-166]the man who laid in ambush and shot me, killing me before I could get

[030-167]a chance to defend myself. I want that man’s scalp, because he has been

[030-168]the cause of my wanderings for a great many years. Had he killed me on

[030-169]the battlefield my spirit would have at once joined my brothers in the

[030-170]happy hunting grounds, but being killed by a coward, my spirit is doomed

[030-171]to roam until I can find some brave man who will kill this coward and

[030-172]bring me his scalp. This is why I have tried every party who have camped

[030-173]here to listen to me, but as I have said before, they were all cowards.

[030-174]Now, I ask you two brave young men, will you do this for me?”

[030-175]“We will,” said the friends in one voice. “Thank you, my boys. Now, I

[030-176]know why you came here, and that one of you came to earn his feathers by

[030-177]killing an enemy, before he would marry; the girl he is to marry is

[030-178]my granddaughter, as I am the father of the great Medicine Man. In the

[030-179]morning there will pass by in plain sight of here a large party. They

[030-180]will chase the buffalo over on that flat. After they have passed an old

[030-181]man leading a black horse and riding a white one will come by on the

[030-182]trail left by the hunting party. He will be driving about a hundred

[030-183]horses, which he will leave over in the next ravine. He will then

[030-184]proceed to the hunting grounds and get meat from the different hunters.

[030-185]After the hunters have all gone home he will come last, singing the

[030-186]praises of the ones who gave him the meat. This man you must kill and

[030-187]scalp, as he is the one I want killed. Then take the white and black

[030-188]horse and each mount and go to the hunting grounds. There you will see

[030-189]two of the enemy riding about picking up empty shells. Kill and scalp

[030-190]these two and each take a scalp and come over to the high knoll and I

[030-191]will show you where the horses are, and as soon as you hand me the old

[030-192]man’s scalp I will disappear and you will see me no more. As soon as I

[030-193]disappear, it will start in snowing. Don’t be afraid as the snow will

[030-194]cover your trail, but nevertheless, don’t stop traveling for three days

[030-195]and nights, as these people will suspect that some of your tribe have

[030-196]done this, and they will follow you until you cross your own boundary

[030-197]lines.”

[030-198]When morning came, the two friends sat in the thick brush and watched

[030-199]a large party pass by their hiding place. So near were they that the

[030-200]friends could hear them laughing and talking. After the hunting party

[030-201]had passed, as the spirit had told them, along came the old man, driving

[030-202]a large band of horses and leading a fine looking coal black horse. The

[030-203]horse the old man was riding was as white as snow. The friends crawled

[030-204]to a little brush covered hill and watched the chase after the shooting

[030-205]had ceased. The friends knew it would not be long before the return of

[030-206]the party, so they crawled back to their camp and hurriedly ate some

[030-207]pounded meat and drank some cherry tea. Then they took down their robe

[030-208]and rolled it up and got everything in readiness for a hurried flight

[030-209]with the horses. Scarcely had they got everything in readiness when the

[030-210]party came by, singing their song of the chase. When they had all gone

[030-211]the friends crawled down to the trail and lay waiting for the old man.

[030-212]Soon they heard him singing. Nearer and nearer came the sounds of the

[030-213]song until at last at a bend in the road, the old man came into view.

[030-214]The two friends arose and advanced to meet him. On he came still

[030-215]singing. No doubt he mistook them for some of his own people. When

[030-216]he was very close to them they each stepped to either side of him and

[030-217]before he could make an outcry they pierced his cowardly old heart with

[030-218]two arrows. He had hardly touched the ground when they both struck him

[030-219]with their bows, winning first and second honors by striking an enemy

[030-220]after he has fallen. Chaske having won first honors, asked his friend to

[030-221]perform the scalping deed, which he did. And wanting to be sure that the

[030-222]spirit would get full revenge, took the whole scalp, ears and all, and

[030-223]tied it to his belt. The buffalo beef which the old man had packed upon

[030-224]the black horse, they threw on the top of the old man. Quickly mounting

[030-225]the two horses, they hastened out across the long flat towards the

[030-226]hunting grounds. When they came in sight of the grounds there they saw

[030-227]two men riding about from place to place. Chaske took after the one

[030-228]on the right, Hake the one on the left. When the two men saw these two

[030-229]strange men riding like the wind towards them, they turned their horses

[030-230]to retreat towards the hills, but the white and the black were the

[030-231]swiftest of the tribe’s horses, and quickly overtook the two fleeing

[030-232]men. When they came close to the enemy they strung their arrows onto the

[030-233]bowstring and drove them through the two fleeing hunters. As they were

[030-234]falling they tried to shoot, but being greatly exhausted, their bullets

[030-235]whistled harmlessly over the heads of the two friends. They scalped the

[030-236]two enemies and took their guns and ammunition, also secured the two

[030-237]horses and started for the high knoll. When they arrived at the place,

[030-238]there stood the spirit. Hake presented him with the old man’s scalp and

[030-239]then the spirit showed them the large band of horses, and saying, “Ride

[030-240]hard and long,” disappeared and was seen no more by any war parties,

[030-241]as he was thus enabled to join his forefathers in the happy hunting

[030-242]grounds.

[030-243]The friends did as the spirit had told them. For three days and three

[030-244]nights they rode steadily. On the fourth morning they came into their

[030-245]own boundary. From there on they rode more slowly, and let the band

[030-246]of horses rest and crop the tops of long grass. They would stop

[030-247]occasionally, and while one slept the other kept watch. Thus they got

[030-248]fairly well rested before they came in sight of where their camp had

[030-249]stood when they had left. All that they could see of the once large

[030-250]village was the lone tent of the great Medicine Man. They rode up on to

[030-251]a high hill and farther on towards the east they saw smoke from a great

[030-252]many tepees. They then knew that something had happened and that the

[030-253]village had moved away.

[030-254]“My friend,” said Chaske, “I am afraid something has happened to the

[030-255]Medicine Man’s lodge, and rather than have you go there, I will go alone

[030-256]and you follow the trail of our party and go on ahead with the horses.

[030-257]I will take the black and the white horses with me and I will follow on

[030-258]later, after I have seen what the trouble is.”

[030-259]“Very well, my friend, I will do as you say, but I am afraid something

[030-260]has happened to Pretty Feather.” Hake started on with the horses,

[030-261]driving them along the broad trail left by the hundreds of travois.

[030-262]Chaske made slowly towards the tepee, and stopping outside, stood and

[030-263]listened. Not a sound could he hear. The only living thing he saw was

[030-264]Pretty Feather’s spotted horse tied to the side of the tent. Then he

[030-265]knew that she must be dead. He rode off into the thick brush and tied

[030-266]his two horses securely. Then he came back and entered the tepee. There

[030-267]on a bed of robes lay some one apparently dead. The body was wrapped

[030-268]in blankets and robes and bound around and around with parfleche ropes.

[030-269]These he carefully untied and unwound. Then he unwrapped the robes and

[030-270]blankets and when he uncovered the face, he saw, as he had expected

[030-271]to, the face of his lost love, Pretty Feather. As he sat gazing on her

[030-272]beautiful young face, his heart ached for his poor friend. He himself

[030-273]had loved and lost this beautiful maiden, and now his friend who had won

[030-274]her would have to suffer the untold grief which he had suffered.

[030-275]What was that? Could it have been a slight quivering of the nostrils

[030-276]that he had seen, or was it mad fancy playing a trick on him? Closer

[030-277]he drew to her face, watching intently for another sign. There it was

[030-278]again, only this time it was a long, deep drawn breath. He arose, got

[030-279]some water and taking a small stick slowly forced open her mouth and

[030-280]poured some into it. Then he took some sage, dipped it into the water

[030-281]and sprinkled a little on her head and face. There were many parfleche

[030-282]bags piled around the tepee, and thinking he might find some kind of

[030-283]medicine roots which he could use to revive her he started opening

[030-284]them one after the other. He had opened three and was just opening

[030-285]the fourth, when a voice behind him asked: “What are you looking for?”

[030-286]Turning quickly, he saw Pretty Feather looking at him. Overjoyed, he

[030-287]cried, “What can I do so that you can get up and ride to the village

[030-288]with me? My friend and I just returned with a large band of horses and

[030-289]two scalps. We saw this tent and recognized it. My friend wanted to

[030-290]come, but I would not let him, as I feared if he found anything had

[030-291]happened to you he would do harm to himself, but now he will be anxious

[030-292]for my return, so if you will tell me what you need in order to revive

[030-293]you, I will get it, and we can then go to my friend in the village.” “At

[030-294]the foot of my bed you will find a piece of eagle fat. Build a fire and

[030-295]melt it for me. I will drink it and then we can go.”

[030-296]Chaske quickly started a fire, got out the piece of fat and melted it.

[030-297]She drank it at one draught, and was about to arise when she suddenly

[030-298]said: “Roll me up quick and take the buffalo hair rope and tie it about

[030-299]my spotted horse’s neck; tie his tail in a knot and tie him to the door.

[030-300]Then run and hide behind the trees. There are two of the enemy coming

[030-301]this way.”

[030-302]Chaske hurriedly obeyed her orders, and had barely concealed himself

[030-303]behind the trees, when there came into view two of the enemy. They saw

[030-304]the horse tied to the door of the deserted tent, and knew that some dead

[030-305]person occupied the tepee, so through respect for the dead, they turned

[030-306]out and started to go through the brush and trees, so as not to pass the

[030-307]door. (The Indians consider it a bad omen to pass by the door of a tepee

[030-308]occupied by a dead body, that is, while in the enemy’s country). So

[030-309]by making this detour they traveled directly towards where Chaske was

[030-310]concealed behind the tree. Knowing that he would be discovered, and

[030-311]there being two of them, he knew the only chance he had was for him

[030-312]to kill one of them before they discovered him, then he stood a better

[030-313]chance at an even combat. On they came, little thinking that one of them

[030-314]would in a few minutes be with his forefathers.

[030-315]Chaske noiselessly slipped a cartridge into the chamber of his gun,

[030-316]threw it into action and took deliberate aim at the smaller one’s

[030-317]breast. A loud report rang out and the one he had aimed at threw up his

[030-318]arms and fell heavily forward, shot through the heart.

[030-319]Reloading quickly Chaske stepped out from behind the tree. He could

[030-320]easily have killed the other from his concealed position, but, being a

[030-321]brave young man, he wanted to give his opponent a fair chance. The other

[030-322]had unslung his gun and a duel was then fought between the two lone

[030-323]combatants. They would spring from side to side like two great cats.

[030-324]Then advance one or two steps and fire. Retreat a few steps, spring to

[030-325]one side and fire again. The bullets whistled past their heads, tore up

[030-326]the earth beneath their feet, and occasionally one would hit its mark,

[030-327]only to cause a flesh wound.

[030-328]Suddenly the enemy aimed his gun and threw it upon the ground. His

[030-329]ammunition was exhausted, and slowly folding his arms he stood facing

[030-330]his opponent, with a fearless smile upon his face, expecting the next

[030-331]moment to fall dead from a bullet from the rifle of Chaske. Not

[030-332]so. Chaske was too honorable and noble to kill an unarmed man, and

[030-333]especially one who had put up such a brave fight as had this man. Chaske

[030-334]advanced and picked up the empty gun. The Toka (enemy) drew from a

[030-335]scabbard at his belt a long bowie knife, and taking it by the point

[030-336]handed it, handle first, to Chaske. This signified surrender. Chaske

[030-337]scalped the dead Toka and motioned for his prisoner to follow him. In

[030-338]the meantime Pretty Feather had gotten up and stood looking at the duel.

[030-339]When she heard the first shot she jumped up and cut a small slit in the

[030-340]tent from which she saw the whole proceedings. Knowing that one or both

[030-341]of them must be wounded, she hurriedly got water and medicine roots, and

[030-342]when they came to the tent she was prepared to dress their wounds.

[030-343]Chaske had a bullet through his shoulder and one through his hand. They

[030-344]were very painful but not dangerous. The prisoner had a bullet through

[030-345]his leg, also one through the muscle of his left arm. Pretty Feather

[030-346]washed and dressed their wounds, and Chaske went and brought the black

[030-347]and white horses and mounting Pretty Feather upon the white horse, and

[030-348]the prisoner on her spotted one, the three soon rode into the village,

[030-349]and there was a great cry of joy when it was known that Pretty Feather

[030-350]had come back to them again.

[030-351]Hake, who was in his tent grieving, was told that his friend had

[030-352]returned and with him Pretty Feather. Hearing this good news he at

[030-353]once went to the Medicine Man’s tent and found the Medicine Man busily

[030-354]dressing the wounds of his friend and a stranger. The old Medicine Man

[030-355]turned to Hake and said:

[030-356]“Son-in-law, take your wife home with you. It was from grief at your

[030-357]absence that she went into a trance, and we, thinking she was dead, left

[030-358]her for such. Hadn’t it been for your friend here, she would surely have

[030-361]Hake and his beautiful bride went home, where his adopted mother had a

[030-362]fine large tent put up for them. Presents of cooking utensils, horses,

[030-363]robes and finely worked shawls and moccasins came from every direction,

[030-364]and last of all Chaske gave as a present to his friend the Toka man whom

[030-365]he had taken as prisoner. On presenting him with this gift, Chaske spoke

[030-366]thus:

[030-367]“My friend, I present to you, that you may have him as a servant to

[030-368]look after your large band of horses, this man with whom I fought a

[030-369]two hours’ duel, and had his ammunition lasted he would probably have

[030-370]conquered me, and who gave me the second hardest fight of my life.

[030-371]The hardest fight of my life was when I gave up Pretty Feather. You

[030-372]have them both. To the Toka (enemy) be kind, and he will do all your

[030-373]biddings. To Pretty Feather be a good husband.”

[030-374]So saying, Chaske left them, and true to his word, lived the remainder

[030-375]of his days a confirmed bachelor.
