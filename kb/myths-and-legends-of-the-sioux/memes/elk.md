## Overview

**Elk** (Lakota: **Hehaka**) appears in Sioux folklore as a figure of misplaced pride. In the tale of "The Rabbit and the Elk," the Elk discovers Rabbit's hunting bow and decides to play a joke — pretending to be caught in the bowstring. His jest backfires when Rabbit takes the "catch" at face value, and the Elk's pride becomes Rabbit's provision.

## In the Stories

### The Rabbit and the Elk (Chapter 005)

[[rabbit]] lives with his old grandmother, who needs a new dress. Rabbit announces his intention to hunt (005-002):

> "I will go out and trap a deer or an elk for you," he said. "Then you shall have a new dress." (005-001–005-003)

Setting his bow in the path while checking his snares, Rabbit leaves it unattended. The Elk comes by and, seeing the bow (005-005), cannot resist a jest:

> "I will play a joke on the rabbit," said the elk to himself. "I will make him think I have been caught in his bow string." (005-004–005-007)

The Elk puts one foot on the string and lies down as if dead. When Rabbit returns and sees the Elk, he is overjoyed — but the Elk's joke has trapped him. The Elk later springs to his feet laughing (005-013). Rabbit, taking the scene literally, claims the Elk as his catch. The Elk's pride has made him prey.

## Character Traits

Elk represents the **proud fool** — a figure whose cleverness turns against him. Unlike [[bear]], who is a genuine threat, Elk is not malicious but vain. His downfall comes not from predation but from the impulse to mock someone smaller. The story serves as a cautionary tale about the dangers of underestimating others.
