## Overview

The **Only Daughter** is the beloved child of an old couple in a Sioux village. Extraordinarily beautiful, she is courted by many young men but refuses them all, declaring her preference for the single life. When she falls gravely ill and dies despite the efforts of the best medicine men, her body is placed on a burial scaffold according to Sioux custom. A young suitor, unwilling to accept her death, undertakes a vigil that results in her miraculous return to life — a tale that speaks to the power of devotion over death itself.

## In the Stories

### The Resuscitation of the Only Daughter (Chapter 033)

The daughter of an old couple is renowned for her beauty and equally for her steadfast refusal of every suitor:

> There once lived an old couple who had an only daughter. She was a beautiful girl, and was very much courted by the young men of the tribe, but she said that she preferred single life, and to all their heart-touching tales of deep affection for her she always had one answer. That was "No." (033-001)

Her sudden illness confounds the village. The most skilled healers are summoned, but their efforts prove futile:

> One day this maiden fell ill and day after day grew worse. All the best medicine men were called in, but their medicines were of no avail, and in two weeks from the day that she was taken ill she lay a corpse. (033-001–033-010)

Following Sioux burial custom, her body is wrapped in fine robes and placed upon an elevated scaffold. But one young man, who had loved her deeply, refuses to accept the finality of her death. His vigil at the scaffold, undertaken against the counsel of the elders, leads to a supernatural intervention — and the only daughter's return from the land of spirits.

The story reflects Sioux beliefs about the permeable boundary between life and death, the power of steadfast love, and the possibility of supernatural rescue when conventional medicine has failed.
