## Overview

The **scalp** (pa-ha) was the warrior's proof — physical evidence that an enemy had been killed in combat. A young man declares "I must go on a war party and bring back the scalp of an enemy" (007-014), framing the scalp as the mission's objective. "I will try to take a scalp for your sake" (007-017) — the scalp is a gift, an offering to those who doubted the warrior's courage. After battle, "securing his scalp and gun" (023-083) — the scalp is gathered alongside weapons, the two types of proof: the tool and the trophy. At the war dance, "the two scalps which the young man had" (023-165) are displayed for three days — the scalp as spectacle, honor made visible.

## In the Stories

### The Scalp as Mission

> must go on a war party and bring back the scalp of an enemy.” The young man answered modestly, “I will try to do as you bid me. I am
>
> (007-014)

The war party's purpose is explicitly scalp-taking: "must go on a war party and bring back the scalp of an enemy" (007-014). The scalp is not incidental to warfare — it is the objective. A hunter, not yet proven as a warrior, declares "I will try to take a scalp for your sake" (007-017) — the scalp is proof that transforms a hunter into a warrior.

### Securing the Trophy

After a kill, the warrior's first act is "securing his scalp and gun" (023-083). The scalp is secured alongside the weapon — both are tools of war, one for killing and one for proving the kill. The warrior then "went to his horse and drove" (023-133) away, the scalp already taken and stored.

### The Scalp Dance

> up three days and three nights. The two scalps which the young man had taken were tied to a pole which was placed in the center of the dance
>
> (023-165)

The scalp's social function culminates in the war dance: "there would be a great war dance given in honor of the Lone Warrior" (023-162). "The two scalps which the young man had" (023-165) are placed "in the center of the dance circle" (023-166–167). For "three days and three nights" (023-165) the scalps are displayed — the community gathers around the proof of the warrior's deeds.

### The Vengeance Scalp

A spirit demands revenge: "I want that man's scalp, because he has been" (030-167) a murderer. The scalp here is not a war trophy but an instrument of justice — taken from the killer to avenge the killed. Warriors are described with "some scalps dangling from your belts" (030-164) — the scalp as adornment, a permanent record worn on the body.

### The Crow Village Mourning

When a warrior is killed, "great was the mourning in the Crow village" (024-100) — but the scalp of an enemy was also "took the scalp of an enemy" (024-104), the taking and the losing of scalps forming a cycle of grief and honor that bound communities in reciprocity.

## Cultural Significance

- **Scalp as proof**: The scalp was physical evidence that transformed a claim into verified achievement — without it, a kill could not be counted
- **Scalp as gift**: "I will try to take a scalp for your sake" (007-017) — the scalp was given to honor others, not just the self
- **Scalp as spectacle**: Placement in the center of the dance circle (023-166) for three days made the scalp a communal event, not a private trophy
- **Scalp as justice**: Taking the scalp of a murderer (030-167) blurred the line between war trophy and legal remedy
- **Scalp as adornment**: Worn on belts (030-164), scalps were visible status markers integrated into daily dress
