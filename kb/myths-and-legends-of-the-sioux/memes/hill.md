## Overview

The **hill** (paha) punctuates the Sioux landscape — a rise in the prairie, a lookout over a valley, a landmark on a journey. A village is placed "on high land overlooking a lake" (016-002), the hill offering both view and defense. Warriors use hills as tactical positions: "looking down into the valley on the" (024-048) enemy. Children transform hills into playgrounds: "they were coasting down hill on a board" (006-004). The rabbit finds "a hill away from the wind" (006-002) — even a small rise provides shelter. A traveler "passed around a hill and came to a little ravine" (013-002) — hills are waypoints on the journey, each one revealing new terrain beyond.

## In the Stories

### The Hill as Home

A village is sited "on high land overlooking a lake. A little way down the" (016-002) hill lies the shore. The hill provides prospect — the ability to see approaching danger or arriving visitors. The character goes "down to the lake and washed himself" (016-063), moving from the high village to the low water, the hill organizing vertical space.

### The Hill as Lookout

Warriors use hills for surveillance. One party positions itself on "a high hill, looking down into the valley on the" (024-048) enemy — the hill as tactical advantage. Another warrior watches from "the shore of the lake and sticking his hands in the" (016-031) water, the hill behind him.

### The Hill as Playground

Children find joy on hills: "they were coasting down hill on a board. When the rabbit saw" (006-004) them, he joins in. The rabbit's intervention — "not a good way to coast down hill" (006-006) — suggests that hill play had recognized techniques, some better than others.

### The Heap Between Hills

A striking image: hunters pile meat into "a great heap between two hills which she pointed out" (010-055) — so much that "the tops of the two hills were bridged level between" (010-056). The hills become a measure of abundance — the kill is so great it fills the space between two rises.

## Cultural Significance

- **Hill as prospect**: Villages on high land (016-002) embody the strategic value of elevation — to see is to be safe
- **Hill as boundary**: Passing around a hill (013-002) reveals new terrain — hills divide one landscape from another
- **Hill as measure**: "A great heap between two hills" (010-055) — the space between hills becomes a unit of abundance
- **Hill as shelter**: The lee of a hill (006-002) provides the only windbreak on the open prairie
