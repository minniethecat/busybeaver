[007-001]There once lived a chief’s daughter who had many relations. All the

[007-002]young men in the village wanted to have her for wife, and were all eager

[007-003]to fill her skin bucket when she went to the brook for water.

[007-004]There was a young man in the village who was industrious and a good

[007-005]hunter; but he was poor and of a mean family. He loved the maiden

[007-006]and when she went for water, he threw his robe over her head while he

[007-007]whispered in her ear:

[007-008]“Be my wife. I have little but I am young and strong. I will treat you

[007-009]well, for I love you.”

[007-010]For a long time the maiden did not answer, but one day she whispered

[007-011]back.

[007-012]“Yes, you may ask my father’s leave to marry me. But first you must do

[007-013]something noble. I belong to a great family and have many relations. You

[007-014]must go on a war party and bring back the scalp of an enemy.”

[007-015]The young man answered modestly, “I will try to do as you bid me. I am

[007-016]only a hunter, not a warrior. Whether I shall be brave or not I do not

[007-017]know. But I will try to take a scalp for your sake.”

[007-018]So he made a war party of seven, himself and six other young men. They

[007-019]wandered through the enemy’s country, hoping to get a chance to strike a

[007-020]blow. But none came, for they found no one of the enemy.

[007-021]“Our medicine is unfavorable,” said their leader at last. “We shall have

[007-022]to return home.”

[007-023]Before they started they sat down to smoke and rest beside a beautiful

[007-024]lake at the foot of a green knoll that rose from its shore. The knoll

[007-025]was covered with green grass and somehow as they looked at it they had

[007-026]a feeling that there was something about it that was mysterious or

[007-027]uncanny.

[007-028]But there was a young man in the party named the jester, for he was

[007-029]venturesome and full of fun. Gazing at the knoll he said: “Let’s run and

[007-030]jump on its top.”

[007-031]“No,” said the young lover, “it looks mysterious. Sit still and finish

[007-032]your smoke.”

[007-033]“Oh, come on, who’s afraid,” said the jester, laughing. “Come on

[007-034]you--come on!” and springing to his feet he ran up the side of the

[007-035]knoll.

[007-036]Four of the young men followed. Having reached the top of the knoll all

[007-037]five began to jump and stamp about in sport, calling, “Come on, come

[007-038]on,” to the others. Suddenly they stopped--the knoll had begun to move

[007-039]toward the water. It was a gigantic turtle. The five men cried out in

[007-040]alarm and tried to run--too late! Their feet by some power were held

[007-041]fast to the monster’s back.

[007-042]“Help us--drag us away,” they cried; but the others could do nothing. In

[007-043]a few moments the waves had closed over them.

[007-044]The other two men, the lover and his friend, went on, but with heavy

[007-045]hearts, for they had forebodings of evil. After some days, they came to

[007-046]a river. Worn with fatigue the lover threw himself down on the bank.

[007-047]“I will sleep awhile,” he said, “for I am wearied and worn out.”

[007-048]“And I will go down to the water and see if I can chance upon a dead

[007-049]fish. At this time of the year the high water may have left one stranded

[007-050]on the seashore,” said his friend.

[007-051]And as he had said, he found a fish which he cleaned, and then called to

[007-052]the lover.

[007-053]“Come and eat the fish with me. I have cleaned it and made a fire and it

[007-054]is now cooking.”

[007-055]“No, you eat it; let me rest,” said the lover.

[007-056]“Oh, come on.”

[007-057]“No, let me rest.”

[007-058]“But you are my friend. I will not eat unless you share it with me.”

[007-059]“Very well,” said the lover, “I will eat the fish with you, but you must

[007-060]first make me a promise. If I eat the fish, you must promise, pledge

[007-061]yourself, to fetch me all the water that I can drink.”

[007-062]“I promise,” said the other, and the two ate the fish out of their

[007-063]war-kettle. For there had been but one kettle for the party.

[007-064]When they had eaten, the kettle was rinsed out and the lover’s friend

[007-065]brought it back full of water. This the lover drank at a draught.

[007-066]“Bring me more,” he said.

[007-067]Again his friend filled the kettle at the river and again the lover

[007-068]drank it dry.

[007-069]“More!” he cried.

[007-070]“Oh, I am tired. Cannot you go to the river and drink your fill from the

[007-071]stream?” asked his friend.

[007-072]“Remember your promise.”

[007-073]“Yes, but I am weary. Go now and drink.”

[007-074]“Ek-hey, I feared it would be so. Now trouble is coming upon us,” said

[007-075]the lover sadly. He walked to the river, sprang in, and lying down in

[007-076]the water with his head toward land, drank greedily. By and by he called

[007-077]to his friend.

[007-078]“Come hither, you who have been my sworn friend. See what comes of your

[007-079]broken promise.”

[007-080]The friend came and was amazed to see that the lover was now a fish from

[007-081]his feet to his middle.

[007-082]Sick at heart he ran off a little way and threw himself upon the ground

[007-083]in grief. By and by he returned. The lover was now a fish to his neck.

[007-084]“Cannot I cut off the part and restore you by a sweat bath?” the friend

[007-085]asked.

[007-086]“No, it is too late. But tell the chief’s daughter that I loved her to

[007-087]the last and that I die for her sake. Take this belt and give it to her.

[007-088]She gave it to me as a pledge of her love for me,” and he being then

[007-089]turned to a great fish, swam to the middle of the river and there

[007-090]remained, only his great fin remaining above the water.

[007-091]The friend went home and told his story. There was great mourning over

[007-092]the death of the five young men, and for the lost lover. In the river

[007-093]the great fish remained, its fin just above the surface, and was called

[007-094]by the Indians “Fish that Bars,” because it bar’d navigation. Canoes had

[007-095]to be portaged at great labor around the obstruction.

[007-096]The chief’s daughter mourned for her lover as for a husband, nor would

[007-097]she be comforted. “He was lost for love of me, and I shall remain as his

[007-098]widow,” she wailed.

[007-099]In her mother’s tepee she sat, with her head covered with her robe,

[007-100]silent, working, working. “What is my daughter doing,” her mother asked.

[007-101]But the maiden did not reply.

[007-102]The days lengthened into moons until a year had passed. And then the

[007-103]maiden arose. In her hands were beautiful articles of clothing, enough

[007-104]for three men. There were three pairs of moccasins, three pairs of

[007-105]leggings, three belts, three shirts, three head dresses with beautiful

[007-106]feathers, and sweet smelling tobacco.

[007-107]“Make a new canoe of bark,” she said, which was made for her.

[007-108]Into the canoe she stepped and floated slowly down the river toward the

[007-109]great fish.

[007-110]“Come back my daughter,” her mother cried in agony. “Come back. The

[007-111]great fish will eat you.”

[007-112]She answered nothing. Her canoe came to the place where the great fin

[007-113]arose and stopped, its prow grating on the monster’s back. The maiden

[007-114]stepped out boldly. One by one she laid her presents on the fish’s back,

[007-115]scattering the feathers and tobacco over his broad spine.

[007-116]“Oh, fish,” she cried, “Oh, fish, you who were my lover, I shall not

[007-117]forget you. Because you were lost for love of me, I shall never marry.

[007-118]All my life I shall remain a widow. Take these presents. And now leave

[007-119]the river, and let the waters run free, so my people may once more

[007-120]descend in their canoes.”

[007-121]She stepped into her canoe and waited. Slowly the great fish sank, his

[007-122]broad fin disappeared, and the waters of the St. Croix (Stillwater) were

[007-123]free.
