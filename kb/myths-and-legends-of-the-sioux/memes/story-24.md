[024-001]A war party of seven young men, seeing a lone tepee standing on the edge

[024-002]of a heavy belt of timber, stopped and waited for darkness, in order to

[024-003]send one of their scouts ahead to ascertain whether the camp which they

[024-004]had seen was the camp of friend or enemy.

[024-005]When darkness had settled down on them, and they felt secure in not

[024-006]being detected, they chose one of their scouts to go on alone and find

[024-007]out what would be the best direction for them to advance upon the camp,

[024-008]should it prove to be an enemy.

[024-009]Among the scouts was one who was noted for his bravery, and many were

[024-010]the brave acts he had performed. His name was Big Eagle. This man they

[024-011]selected to go to the lone camp and obtain the information for which

[024-012]they were waiting.

[024-013]Big Eagle was told to look carefully over the ground and select the best

[024-014]direction from which they should make the attack. The other six would

[024-015]await his return. He started on his mission, being careful not to make

[024-016]any noise. He stealthily approached the camp. As he drew near to the

[024-017]tent he was surprised to note the absence of any dogs, as these animals

[024-018]are always kept by the Sioux to notify the owners by their barking of

[024-019]the approach of anyone. He crawled up to the tepee door, and peeping

[024-020]through a small aperture, he saw three persons sitting inside. An

[024-021]elderly man and woman were sitting at the right of the fireplace, and a

[024-022]young woman at the seat of honor, opposite the door.

[024-023]Big Eagle had been married and his wife had died five winters previous

[024-024]to the time of this episode. He had never thought of marrying again, but

[024-025]when he looked upon this young woman he thought he was looking upon the

[024-026]face of his dead wife. He removed his cartridge belts and knife, and

[024-027]placing them, along with his rifle, at the side of the tent, he at once

[024-028]boldly stepped inside the tepee, and going over to the man, extended his

[024-029]hand and shook first the man’s hand, then the old woman’s, and lastly

[024-030]the young woman’s. Then he seated himself by the side of the girl, and

[024-031]thus they sat, no one speaking.

[024-032]Finally, Big Eagle made signs to the man, explaining as well as possible

[024-033]by signs, that his wife had died long ago, and when he saw the girl she

[024-034]so strongly resembled his dead wife that he wished to marry her, and

[024-035]he would go back to the enemy’s camp and live with them, if they would

[024-036]consent to the marriage of their daughter.

[024-037]The old man seemed to understand, and Big Eagle again made signs to him

[024-038]that a party were lying in wait just a short distance from his camp.

[024-039]Noiselessly they brought in the horses, and taking down the tent, they

[024-040]at once moved off in the direction from whence they had come. The war

[024-041]party waited all night, and when the first rays of dawn disclosed to

[024-042]them the absence of the tepee, they at once concluded that Big Eagle had

[024-043]been discovered and killed, so they hurriedly started on their trail for

[024-044]home.

[024-045]In the meantime, the hunting party, for this it was that Big Eagle

[024-046]had joined, made very good time in putting a good distance between

[024-047]themselves and the war party. All day they traveled, and when evening

[024-048]came they ascended a high hill, looking down into the valley on the

[024-049]other side. There stretched for two miles, along the banks of a small

[024-050]stream, an immense camp. The old man made signs for Big Eagle to remain

[024-051]with the two women where he was, until he could go to the camp and

[024-052]prepare them to receive an enemy into their village.

[024-053]The old man rode through the camp and drew up at the largest tepee in

[024-054]the village. Soon Big Eagle could see men gathering around the tepee.

[024-055]The crowd grew larger and larger, until the whole village had assembled

[024-056]at the large tepee. Finally they dispersed, and catching their horses,

[024-057]mounted and advanced to the hill on which Big Eagle and the two women

[024-058]were waiting. They formed a circle around them and slowly they returned

[024-059]to the village, singing and riding in a circle around them.

[024-060]When they arrived at the village they advanced to the large tepee, and

[024-061]motioned Big Eagle to the seat of honor in the tepee. In the village was

[024-062]a man who understood and spoke the Sioux language. He was sent for, and

[024-063]through him the oath of allegiance to the Crow tribe was taken by Big

[024-064]Eagle. This done he was presented with the girl to wife, and also with

[024-065]many spotted ponies.

[024-066]Big Eagle lived with his wife among her people for two years, and during

[024-067]this time he joined in four different battles between his own people

[024-068](the Sioux) and the Crow people, to whom his wife belonged.

[024-069]In no battle with his own people would he carry any weapons, only a long

[024-070]willow coup-stick, with which he struck the fallen Sioux.

[024-071]At the expiration of two years he concluded to pay a visit to his own

[024-072]tribe, and his father-in-law, being a chief of high standing, at once

[024-073]had it heralded through the village that his son-in-law would visit his

[024-074]own people, and for them to show their good will and respect for him by

[024-075]bringing ponies for his son-in-law to take back to his people.

[024-076]Hearing this, the herds were all driven in and all day long horses were

[024-077]brought to the tent of Big Eagle, and when he was ready to start on his

[024-078]homeward trip, twenty young men were elected to accompany him to within

[024-079]a safe distance of his village. The twenty young men drove the gift

[024-080]horses, amounting to two hundred and twenty head, to within one day’s

[024-081]journey of the village of Big Eagle, and fearing for their safety from

[024-082]his people, Big Eagle sent them back to their own village.

[024-083]On his arrival at his home village, they received him as one returned

[024-084]from the dead, as they were sure he had been killed the night he had

[024-085]been sent to reconnoiter the lone camp. There was great feasting and

[024-086]dancing in honor of his return, and the horses were distributed among

[024-087]the needy ones of the village.

[024-088]Remaining at his home village for a year, he one day made up his mind

[024-089]to return to his wife’s people. A great many fancy robes, dresses, war

[024-090]bonnets, moccasins, and a great drove of horses were given him, and his

[024-091]wife, and he bade farewell to his people for good, saying, “I will never

[024-092]return to you again, as I have decided to live the remainder of my days

[024-093]with my wife’s people.”

[024-094]On his arrival at the village of the Crows, he found his father-in-law

[024-095]at the point of death. A few days later the old man died, and Big Eagle

[024-096]was appointed to fill the vacancy of chief made by the death of his

[024-097]father-in-law.

[024-098]Subsequently he took part in battles against his own people, and in the

[024-099]third battle was killed on the field. Tenderly the Crow warriors bore

[024-100]him back to their camp, and great was the mourning in the Crow village

[024-101]for the brave man who always went into battle unarmed, save only the

[024-102]willow wand which he carried.

[024-103]Thus ended the career of one of the bravest of Sioux warriors who ever

[024-104]took the scalp of an enemy, and who for the love of his dead wife, gave

[024-105]up home, parents, and friends, to be killed on the field of battle by

[024-106]his own tribe.
