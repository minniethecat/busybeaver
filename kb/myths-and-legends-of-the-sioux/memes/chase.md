## Overview

The **chase** in Sioux tales is pursuit at speed — the rabbit who "gave chase" (009-042) to an enemy, the hunters who "chase the buffalo over on that flat" (030-180), the watchers who "watched the chase after the shooting" (030-204). Unlike the organized [[hunt]], which is planned and provisioned, the chase is reactive and urgent — something flees, and something follows. It is the event that bridges the moment of encounter and the moment of capture or escape.

## In the Stories

### Rabbit's Chase

When an enemy flees, the rabbit "gave chase" (009-042) immediately — no planning, no war party, just the instinct to pursue. "But as he ran the rabbit" (009-042) — the chase is running, pure speed and determination. Rabbit, despite his small size, is a relentless chaser — his speed compensating for his lack of strength.

### The Buffalo Chase

The most vivid chase scene involves buffalo: hunters "will chase the buffalo over on that flat" (030-180). The chase is organized but still urgent — the buffalo are driven across specific terrain, the hunters coordinating their pursuit. Spectators positioned on "a covered hill watched the chase after the shooting" (030-204) — the chase is a spectacle, watched and narrated. After the chase, hunters return "singing their song of the chase" (030-210) — the chase has its own song, its own celebration distinct from the hunt's.

### The Chase's End

"When they had all gone" (030-210) — the chase ends not with the kill but with the departure of the hunters. The chase is the event; the kill is its conclusion. The song sung is "the song of the chase" — not the song of the kill, or the song of the hunt, but specifically the chase. This suggests that the pursuit itself, not just its outcome, was culturally marked as a distinct event worth celebrating.

## Cultural Significance

- **Chase as spectacle**: Spectators watch the chase from hills (030-204), making it a communal event rather than a private pursuit
- **The chase song**: The existence of "the song of the chase" (030-210) marks chasing as a culturally recognized event type with its own ceremonial expression
- **Speed as virtue**: The chase elevates speed and endurance — the rabbit's chase (009-042) demonstrates that even small creatures can be formidable pursuers
- **Urgency vs. planning**: Unlike the hunt, the chase is reactive — something is already fleeing when the chase begins
