[037-001]Alone and apart from their tribe dwelt four orphan brothers. They had

[037-002]erected a very comfortable hut, although the materials used were only

[037-003]willows, hay, birch bark, and adobe mud. After the completion of their

[037-004]hut, the oldest brother laid out the different kinds of work to be done

[037-005]by the four of them. He and the second and third brothers were to do all

[037-006]the hunting, and the youngest brother was to do the house work, cook the

[037-007]meals, and keep plenty of wood on hand at all times.

[037-008]As his older brothers would leave for their hunting very early every

[037-009]morning, and would not return till late at night, the little fellow

[037-010]always found plenty of spare time to gather into little piles fine dry

[037-011]wood for their winter use.

[037-012]Thus the four brothers lived happily for a long time. One day while out

[037-013]gathering and piling up wood, the boy heard a rustling in the leaves

[037-014]and looking around he saw a young woman standing in the cherry bushes,

[037-015]smiling at him.

[037-016]“Who are you, and where did you come from?” asked the boy, in surprise.

[037-017]“I am an orphan girl and have no relatives living. I came from the

[037-018]village west of here. I learned from rabbit that there were four orphan

[037-019]brothers living here all alone, and that the youngest was keeping house

[037-020]for his older brothers, so I thought I would come over and see if I

[037-021]couldn’t have them adopt me as their sister, so that I might keep house

[037-022]for them, as I am very poor and have no relations, neither have I a

[037-023]home.”

[037-024]She looked so pitiful and sad that the boy thought to himself, “I will

[037-025]take her home with me, poor girl, no matter what my brothers think or

[037-026]say.” Then he said to her: “Come on, tanke (sister). You may go home

[037-027]with me; I am sure my older brothers will be glad to have you for our

[037-028]sister.”

[037-029]When they arrived at the hut, the girl hustled about and cooked up a

[037-030]fine hot supper, and when the brothers returned they were surprised to

[037-031]see a girl sitting by the fire in their hut. After they had entered the

[037-032]youngest brother got up and walked outside, and a short time after the

[037-033]oldest brother followed him out. “Who is that girl, and where did she

[037-034]come from?” he asked his brother. Whereupon the brother told him the

[037-035]whole story. Upon hearing this the oldest brother felt very sorry for

[037-036]the poor orphan girl and going back into the hut he spoke to the

[037-037]girl, saying: “Sister, you are an orphan, the same as we; you have no

[037-038]relatives, no home. We will be your brothers, and our poor hut shall be

[037-039]your home. Henceforth call us brothers, and you will be our sister.”

[037-040]“Oh, how happy I am now that you take me as your sister. I will be to

[037-041]you all as though we were of the same father and mother,” said the girl.

[037-042]And true to her word, she looked after everything of her brothers and

[037-043]kept the house in such fine shape that the brothers blessed the day that

[037-044]she came to their poor little hut. She always had an extra buckskin

[037-045]suit and two pairs of moccasins hanging at the head of each one’s bed.

[037-046]Buffalo, deer, antelope, bear, wolf, wildcat, mountain lion and beaver

[037-047]skins she tanned by the dozen, and piled nicely in one corner of the

[037-048]hut.

[037-049]When the Indians have walked a great distance and are very tired, they

[037-050]have great faith in painting their feet, claiming that paint eases the

[037-051]pain and rests their feet.

[037-052]After their return from a long day’s journey, when they would be lying

[037-053]down resting, the sister would get her paint and mix it with the deer

[037-054]tallow and rub the paint on her brother’s feet, painting them up to

[037-055]their ankles. The gentle touch of her hands, and the soothing qualities

[037-056]of the tallow and paint soon put them into a deep, dreamless steep.

[037-057]Many such kind actions on her part won the hearts of the brothers, and

[037-058]never was a full blood sister loved more than was this poor orphan girl,

[037-059]who had been taken as their adopted sister. In the morning when they

[037-060]arose, the sister always combed their long black silken scalp locks and

[037-061]painted the circle around the scalp lock a bright vermillion.

[037-062]When the hunters would return with a goodly supply of beef, the sister

[037-063]would hurry and relieve them of their packs, hanging each one high

[037-064]enough from the ground so the prowling dogs and coyotes could not reach

[037-065]them. The hunters each had a post on which to hang his bow and flint

[037-066]head arrows. (Good hunters never laid their arrows on the ground, as it

[037-067]was considered unlucky to the hunter who let his arrows touch the earth

[037-068]after they had been out of the quiver). They were all perfectly happy,

[037-069]until one day the older brother surprised them all by saying: “We have a

[037-070]plentiful supply of meat on hand at present to last us for a week or so.

[037-071]I am going for a visit to the village west of us, so you boys all stay

[037-072]at home and help sister. Also gather as much wood as you can and I will

[037-073]be back again in four days. On my return we will resume our hunting and

[037-074]commence getting our year’s supply of meat.”

[037-075]He left the next morning, and the last they saw of him was while he

[037-076]stood at the top of the long range of hills west of their home. Four

[037-077]days had come and gone and no sign of the oldest brother.

[037-078]“I am afraid that our brother has met with some accident,” said the

[037-079]sister. “I am afraid so, too,” said the next oldest. “I must go and

[037-080]search for him; he may be in some trouble where a little help would

[037-081]get him out.” The second brother followed the direction his brother had

[037-082]taken, and when he came to the top of the long range of hills he sat

[037-083]down and gazed long and steadily down into the long valley with a

[037-084]beautiful creek winding through it. Across the valley was a long plain

[037-085]stretching for miles beyond and finally ending at the foot of another

[037-086]range of hills, the counterpart of the one upon which he sat.

[037-087]After noting the different landmarks carefully, he arose and slowly

[037-088]started down the slope and soon came to the creek he had seen from the

[037-089]top of the range. Great was his surprise on arriving at the creek to

[037-090]find what a difference there was in the appearance of it from the range

[037-091]and where he stood. From the range it appeared to be a quiet, harmless,

[037-092]laughing stream. Now he saw it to be a muddy, boiling, bubbling torrent,

[037-093]with high perpendicular banks. For a long time he stood, thinking which

[037-094]way to go, up or down stream. He had just decided to go down stream,

[037-095]when, on chancing to look up, he noticed a thin column of smoke slowly

[037-096]ascending from a little knoll. He approached the place cautiously and

[037-097]noticed a door placed into the creek bank on the opposite side of the

[037-098]stream. As he stood looking at the door, wondering who could be living

[037-099]in a place like that, it suddenly opened and a very old appearing woman

[037-100]came out and stood looking around her. Soon she spied the young man, and

[037-101]said to him: “My grandchild, where did you come from and whither are you

[037-102]bound?” The young man answered: “I came from east of this ridge and am

[037-103]in search of my oldest brother, who came over in this direction five

[037-104]days ago and who has not yet returned.”

[037-105]“Your brother stopped here and ate his dinner with me, and then left,

[037-106]traveling towards the west,” said the old witch, for such she was. “Now,

[037-107]grandson, come across on that little log bridge up the stream there

[037-108]and have your dinner with me. I have it all cooked now and just stepped

[037-109]outside to see if there might not be some hungry traveler about, whom I

[037-110]could invite in to eat dinner with me.” The young man went up the stream

[037-111]a little distance and found a couple of small logs which had been placed

[037-112]across the stream to serve as a bridge. He crossed over and went down to

[037-113]the old woman’s dugout hut. “Come in grandson, and eat. I know you must

[037-114]be hungry.”

[037-115]The young man sat down and ate a real hearty meal. On finishing he arose

[037-116]and said: “Grandmother, I thank you for your meal and kindness to me. I

[037-117]would stay and visit with you awhile, as I know it must be very lonely

[037-118]here for you, but I am very anxious to find my brother, so I must be

[037-119]going. On my return I will stop with my brother and we will pay you a

[037-120]little visit.”

[037-121]“Very well, grandson, but before you go, I wish you would do me a little

[037-122]favor. Your brother did it for me before he left, and cured me, but it

[037-123]has come back on me again. I am subject to very severe pains along the

[037-124]left side of my backbone, all the way from my shoulder blade down to

[037-125]where my ribs attach to my backbone, and the only way I get any relief

[037-126]from the pain is to have some one kick me along the side.” (She was a

[037-127]witch, and concealed in her robe a long sharp steel spike. It was placed

[037-128]so that the last kick they would give her, their foot would hit the

[037-129]spike and they would instantly drop off into a swoon, as if dead.)

[037-130]“If I won’t hurt you too much, grandmother, I certainly will be glad to

[037-131]do it for you,” said the young man, little thinking he would be the one

[037-132]to get hurt.

[037-133]“No, grandson, don’t be afraid of hurting me; the harder you kick the

[037-134]longer the pain stays away.” She laid down on the floor and rolled over

[037-135]on to her right side, so he could get a good chance to kick the left

[037-136]side where she said the pain was located.

[037-137]As he moved back to give the first kick, he glanced along the floor

[037-138]and he noticed a long object wrapped in a blanket, lying against the

[037-139]opposite wall. He thought it looked strange and was going to stop and

[037-140]investigate, but just then the witch cried out as if in pain. “Hurry up,

[037-141]grandson, I am going to die if you don’t hurry and start in kicking.” “I

[037-142]can investigate after I get through with her,” thought he, so he started

[037-143]in kicking and every kick he would give her she would cry: “Harder, kick

[037-144]harder.” He had to kick seven times before he would get to the end of

[037-145]the pain, so he let out as hard as he could drive, and when he came to

[037-146]the last kick he hit the spike, and driving it through his foot, fell

[037-147]down in a dead swoon, and was rolled up in a blanket by the witch and

[037-148]placed beside his brother at the opposite side of the room.

[037-149]When the second brother failed to return, the third went in search of

[037-150]the two missing ones. He fared no better than the second one, as he

[037-151]met the old witch who served him in a similar manner as she had his two

[037-152]brothers.

[037-153]“Ha! Ha!” she laughed, when she caught the third, “I have only one more

[037-154]of them to catch, and when I get them I will keep them all here a

[037-155]year, and then I will turn them into horses and sell them back to their

[037-156]sister. I hate her, for I was going to try and keep house for them and

[037-157]marry the oldest one, but she got ahead of me and became their sister,

[037-158]so now I will get my revenge on her. Next year she will be riding and

[037-159]driving her brothers and she won’t know it.”

[037-160]When the third brother failed to return, the sister cried and begged the

[037-161]last one not to venture out in search of them. But go he must, and go he

[037-162]did, only to do as his three brothers had done.

[037-163]Now the poor sister was nearly distracted. Day and night she wandered

[037-164]over hills and through woods in hopes she might find or hear of some

[037-165]trace of them. Her wanderings were in vain. The hawks had not seen them

[037-166]after they had crossed the little stream. The wolves and coyotes told

[037-167]her that they had seen nothing of her brothers out on the broad plains,

[037-168]and she had given them up for dead.

[037-169]One day, as she was sitting by the little stream that flowed past their

[037-170]hut, throwing pebbles into the water and wondering what she should do,

[037-171]she picked up a pure white pebble, smooth and round, and after looking

[037-172]at it for a long time, threw it into the water. No sooner had it hit the

[037-173]water than she saw it grow larger. She took it out and looked at it and

[037-174]threw it in again. This time it had assumed the form of a baby. She took

[037-175]it out and threw it in the third time and the form took life and began

[037-176]to cry: “Ina, ina” (mother, mother). She took the baby home and fed it

[037-177]soup, and it being an unnatural baby, quickly grew up to a good sized

[037-178]boy. At the end of three months he was a good big, stout youth. One day

[037-179]he said: “Mother, why are you living here alone? To whom do all these

[037-180]fine clothes and moccasins belong?” She then told him the story of

[037-181]her lost brothers. “Oh, I know now where they are. You make me lots of

[037-182]arrows. I am going to find my uncles.” She tried to dissuade him from

[037-183]going, but he was determined and said: “My father sent me to you so that

[037-184]I could find my uncles for you, and nothing can harm me, because I am

[037-185]stone and my name is ‘Stone Boy’.”

[037-186]The mother, seeing that he was determined to go, made a whole quiver

[037-187]full of arrows for him, and off he started. When he came to the old

[037-188]witch’s hut, she was nowhere to be seen, so he pushed the door in and

[037-189]entered. The witch was busily engaged cooking dinner.

[037-190]“Why, my dear grandchild, you are just in time for dinner. Sit down and

[037-191]we will eat before you continue your journey.” Stone boy sat down and

[037-192]ate dinner with the old witch. She watched him very closely, but when

[037-193]she would be drinking her soup he would glance hastily around the room.

[037-194]Finally he saw the four bundles on the opposite side of the room, and

[037-195]he guessed at once that there lay his four uncles. When he had finished

[037-196]eating he took out his little pipe and filled it with “kini-kinic,” and

[037-197]commenced to smoke, wondering how the old woman had managed to fool

[037-198]his smart uncles. He couldn’t study it out, so when he had finished his

[037-199]smoke he arose to pretend to go. When the old woman saw him preparing

[037-200]to leave, she said: “Grandson, will you kick me on the left side of my

[037-201]backbone. I am nearly dead with pain and if you kick me good and hard

[037-202]it will cure me.” “All right, grandma,” said the boy. The old witch lay

[037-203]down on the floor and the boy started in to kick. At the first kick he

[037-204]barely touched her. “Kick as hard as you can, grandson; don’t be afraid

[037-205]you will hurt me, because you can’t.” With that Stone Boy let drive and

[037-206]broke two ribs. She commenced to yell and beg him to stop, but he kept

[037-207]on kicking until he had kicked both sides of her ribs loose from the

[037-208]backbone. Then he jumped on her backbone and broke it and killed the old

[037-209]witch.

[037-210]He built a big fire outside and dragged her body to it, and threw her

[037-211]into the fire. Thus ended the old woman who was going to turn his uncles

[037-212]into horses.

[037-213]Next he cut willows and stuck them into the ground in a circle. The

[037-214]tops he pulled together, making a wickieup. He then took the old woman’s

[037-215]robes and blankets and covered the wickieup so that no air could get

[037-216]inside. He then gathered sage brush and covered the floor with a good

[037-217]thick bed of sage; got nice round stones and got them red hot in the

[037-218]fire, and placed them in the wickieup and proceeded to carry his

[037-219]uncles out of the hut and lay them down on the soft bed of sage. Having

[037-220]completed carrying and depositing them around the pile of rocks, he got

[037-221]a bucket of water and poured it on the hot rocks, which caused a great

[037-222]vapor in the little wickieup. He waited a little while and then listened

[037-223]and heard some breathing inside, so he got another bucket and poured

[037-224]that on also. After awhile he could hear noises inside as though some

[037-225]one were moving about. He went again and got the third bucket and after

[037-226]he had poured that on the rocks, one of the men inside said: “Whoever

[037-227]you are, good friend, don’t bring us to life only to scald us to death

[037-228]again.” Stone boy then said: “Are all of you alive?” “Yes,” said the

[037-229]voice. “Well, come out,” said the boy. And with that he threw off the

[037-230]robes and blankets, and a great cloud of vapor arose and settled around

[037-231]the top of the highest peak on the long range, and from that did Smoky

[037-232]Range derive its name.

[037-233]The uncles, when they heard who the boy was, were very happy, and they

[037-234]all returned together to the anxiously waiting sister. As soon as they

[037-235]got home, the brothers worked hard to gather enough wood to last them

[037-236]all winter. Game they could get at all times of the year, but the

[037-237]heavy fall of snow covered most of the dry wood and also made it very

[037-238]difficult to drag wood through the deep snow. So they took advantage of

[037-239]the nice fall weather and by the time the snow commenced falling they

[037-240]had enough wood gathered to last them throughout the winter. After the

[037-241]snow fell a party of boys swiftly coasted down the big hill west of the

[037-242]brothers’ hut. The Stone boy used to stand and watch them for hours at

[037-243]a time. His youngest uncle said: “Why don’t you go up and coast with

[037-244]them?” The boy said: “They may be afraid of me, but I guess I will try

[037-245]once, anyway.” So the next morning when the crowd came coasting, Stone

[037-246]boy started for the hill. When he had nearly reached the bottom of the

[037-247]coasting hill all of the boys ran off excepting two little fellows who

[037-248]had a large coaster painted in different colors and had little bells

[037-249]tied around the edges, so when the coaster was in motion the bells made

[037-250]a cheerful tinkling sound. As Stone boy started up the hill the two

[037-251]little fellows started down and went past him as though shot from a

[037-252]hickory bow.

[037-253]When they got to the end of their slide, they got off and started back

[037-254]up the hill. It being pretty steep, Stone boy waited for them, so as

[037-255]to lend a hand to pull the big coaster up the hill. As the two little

[037-256]fellows came up with him he knew at once that they were twins, as they

[037-257]looked so much alike that the only way one could be distinguished from

[037-258]the other was by the scarfs they wore. One wore red, the other black. He

[037-259]at once offered to help them drag their coaster to the top of the hill.

[037-260]When they got to the top the twins offered their coaster to him to try

[037-261]a ride. At first he refused, but they insisted on his taking it, as they

[037-262]said they would sooner rest until he came back. So he got on the coaster

[037-263]and flew down the hill, only he was such an expert he made a zigzag

[037-264]course going down and also jumped the coaster off a bank about four

[037-265]feet high, which none of the other coasters dared to tackle. Being

[037-266]very heavy, however, he nearly smashed the coaster. Upon seeing this

[037-267]wonderful jump, and the zigzag course he had taken going down, the twins

[037-268]went wild with excitement and decided that they would have him take them

[037-269]down when he got back. So upon his arrival at the starting point, they

[037-270]both asked him at once to give them the pleasure of the same kind of a

[037-271]ride he had taken. He refused, saying: “We will break your coaster. I

[037-272]alone nearly smashed it, and if we all get on and make the same kind of

[037-273]a jump, I am afraid you will have to go home without your coaster.”

[037-274]“Well, take us down anyway, and if we break it our father will make us

[037-275]another one.” So he finally consented. When they were all seated ready

[037-276]to start, he told them that when the coaster made the jump they must

[037-277]look straight ahead. “By no means look down, because if you do we will

[037-278]go over the cut bank and land in a heap at the bottom of the gulch.”

[037-279]They said they would obey what he said, so off they started swifter

[037-280]than ever, on account of the extra weight, and so swiftly did the sleigh

[037-281]glide over the packed, frozen snow, that it nearly took the twins’

[037-282]breath away. Like an arrow they approached the jump. The twins began to

[037-283]get a little nervous. “Sit steady and look straight ahead,” yelled Stone

[037-284]boy. The twin next to Stone boy, who was steering behind, sat upright

[037-285]and looked far ahead, but the one in front crouched down and looked

[037-286]into the coulee. Of course, Stone boy, being behind, fell on top of the

[037-287]twins, and being so heavy, killed both of them instantly, crushing them

[037-288]to a jelly.

[037-289]The rest of the boys, seeing what had happened, hastened to the edge

[037-290]of the bank, and looking down, saw the twins laying dead, and Stone boy

[037-291]himself knocked senseless, lying quite a little distance from the twins.

[037-292]The boys, thinking that all three were killed, and that Stone boy had

[037-293]purposely steered the sleigh over the bank in such a way that it would

[037-294]tip and kill the twins, returned to the village with this report. Now,

[037-295]these twins were the sons of the head chief of the Buffalo Nation. So at

[037-296]once the chief and his scouts went over to the hill to see if the boys

[037-297]had told the truth.

[037-298]When they arrived at the bank they saw the twins lying dead, but where

[037-299]was Stone boy? They looked high and low through the gulch, but not a

[037-300]sign of him could they find. Tenderly they picked up the dead twins and

[037-301]carried them home, then held a big council and put away the bodies of

[037-302]the dead in Buffalo custom.

[037-303]A few days after this the uncles were returning from a long journey.

[037-304]When they drew near their home they noticed large droves of buffalo

[037-305]gathered on their side of the range. Hardly any buffalo ever ranged on

[037-306]this east side of the range before, and the brothers thought it strange

[037-307]that so many should so suddenly appear there now.

[037-308]When they arrived at home their sister told them what had happened to

[037-309]the chief’s twins, as her son had told her the whole story upon his

[037-310]arrival at home after the accident.

[037-311]“Well, probably all the buffalo we saw were here for the council and

[037-312]funeral,” said the older brother. “But where is my nephew?” (Stone boy)

[037-313]he asked his sister. “He said he had noticed a great many buffalo around

[037-314]lately and he was going to learn, if possible, what their object was,”

[037-315]said the sister. “Well, we will wait until his return.”

[037-316]When Stone boy left on his trip that morning, before the return of his

[037-317]uncles, he was determined to ascertain what might be the meaning of

[037-318]so many buffalo so near the home of himself and uncles. He approached

[037-319]several bunches of young buffalo, but upon seeing him approaching they

[037-320]would scamper over the hills. Thus he wandered from bunch to bunch,

[037-321]scattering them all. Finally he grew tired of their cowardice and

[037-322]started for home. When he had come to within a half mile or so of home

[037-323]he saw an old shaggy buffalo standing by a large boulder, rubbing on it

[037-324]first one horn and then the other. On coming up close to him, the boy

[037-325]saw that the bull was so old he could hardly see, and his horns so

[037-326]blunt that he could have rubbed them for a year on that boulder and not

[037-327]sharpened them so as to hurt anyone.

[037-328]“What are you doing here, grandfather?” asked the boy.

[037-329]“I am sharpening my horns for the war,” said the bull.

[037-330]“What war?” asked the boy.

[037-331]“Haven’t you heard,” said the old bull, who was so near sighted he did

[037-332]not recognize Stone boy. “The chief’s twins were killed by Stone boy,

[037-333]who ran them over a cut bank purposely, and the chief has ordered all

[037-334]of his buffalo to gather here, and when they arrive we are going to kill

[037-335]Stone boy and his mother and his uncles.”

[037-336]“Is that so? When is the war to commence?”

[037-337]“In five days from now we will march upon the uncles and trample and

[037-338]gore them all to death.”

[037-339]“Well, grandfather, I thank you for your information, and in return

[037-340]will do you a favor that will save you so much hard work on your blunt

[037-341]horns.” So saying he drew a long arrow from his quiver and strung his

[037-342]bow, attached the arrow to the string and drew the arrow half way back.

[037-343]The old bull, not seeing what was going on, and half expecting some kind

[037-344]of assistance in his horn sharpening process, stood perfectly still.

[037-345]Thus spoke Stone boy:

[037-346]“Grandfather, you are too old to join in a war now, and besides if you

[037-347]got mixed up in that big war party you might step in a hole or stumble

[037-348]and fall and be trampled to death. That would be a horrible death, so I

[037-349]will save you all that suffering by just giving you this.” At this word

[037-350]he pulled the arrow back to the flint head and let it fly. True to his

[037-351]aim, the arrow went in behind the old bull’s foreleg, and with such

[037-352]force was it sent that it went clear through the bull and stuck into a

[037-353]tree two hundred feet away.

[037-354]Walking over to the tree, he pulled out his arrow. Coolly straightening

[037-355]his arrow between his teeth and sighting it for accuracy, he shoved it

[037-356]back into the quiver with its brothers, exclaiming: “I guess, grandpa,

[037-357]you won’t need to sharpen your horns for Stone boy and his uncles.”

[037-358]Upon his arrival home he told his uncles to get to work building three

[037-359]stockades with ditches between and make the ditches wide and deep

[037-360]so they will hold plenty of buffalo. “The fourth fence I will build

[037-361]myself,” he said.

[037-362]The brothers got to work early and worked until very late at night. They

[037-363]built three corrals and dug three ditches around the hut, and it took

[037-364]them three days to complete the work. Stone boy hadn’t done a thing

[037-365]towards building his fence yet, and there were only two days more left

[037-366]before the charge of the buffalo would commence. Still the boy didn’t

[037-367]seem to bother himself about the fence. Instead he had his mother

[037-368]continually cutting arrow sticks, and as fast as she could bring them he

[037-369]would shape them, feather and head them. So by the time his uncles had

[037-370]their fences and corrals finished he had a thousand arrows finished

[037-371]for each of his uncles. The last two days they had to wait, the uncles

[037-372]joined him and they finished several thousand more arrows. The evening

[037-373]before the fifth day he told his uncles to put up four posts, so they

[037-374]could use them as seats from which to shoot.

[037-375]While they were doing this, Stone boy went out to scout and see how

[037-376]things looked. At daylight he came hurriedly in saying, “You had better

[037-377]get to the first corral; they are coming.” “You haven’t built your

[037-378]fence, nephew.” Whereupon Stone boy said: “I will build it in time;

[037-379]don’t worry, uncle.” The dust on the hillsides rose as great clouds of

[037-380]smoke from a forest fire. Soon the leaders of the charge came in sight,

[037-381]and upon seeing the timber stockade they gave forth a great snort

[037-382]or roar that fairly shook the earth. Thousands upon thousands of mad

[037-383]buffalo charged upon the little fort. The leaders hit the first stockade

[037-384]and it soon gave way. The maddened buffalo pushed forward by the

[037-385]thousands behind them; plunged forward, only to fall into the first

[037-386]ditch and be trampled to death by those behind them. The brothers were

[037-387]not slow in using their arrows, and many a noble beast went down before

[037-388]their deadly aim with a little flint pointed arrow buried deep in his

[037-389]heart.

[037-390]The second stockade stood their charge a little longer than did the

[037-391]first, but finally this gave way, and the leaders pushed on through,

[037-392]only to fall into the second ditch and meet a similar fate to those

[037-393]in the first. The brothers commenced to look anxiously towards their

[037-394]nephew, as there was only one more stockade left, and the second ditch

[037-395]was nearly bridged over with dead buffalo, with the now thrice maddened

[037-396]buffalo attacking the last stockade more furiously than before, as they

[037-397]could see the little hut through the openings in the corral.

[037-398]“Come in, uncles,” shouted Stone boy. They obeyed him, and stepping to

[037-399]the center he said: “Watch me build my fence.” Suiting the words, he

[037-400]took from his belt an arrow with a white stone fastened to the point and

[037-401]fastening it to his bow, he shot it high in the air. Straight up into

[037-402]the air it went, for two or three thousand feet, then seemed to stop

[037-403]suddenly and turned with point down and descended as swiftly as it had

[037-404]ascended. Upon striking the ground a high stone wall arose, enclosing

[037-405]the hut and all who were inside. Just then the buffalo broke the last

[037-406]stockade only to fill the last ditch up again. In vain did the leaders

[037-407]butt the stone wall. They hurt themselves, broke their horns and mashed

[037-408]their snouts, but could not even scar the wall.

[037-409]The uncles and Stone boy in the meantime rained arrows of death into

[037-410]their ranks.

[037-411]When the buffalo chief saw what they had to contend with, he ordered the

[037-412]fight off. The crier or herald sang out: “Come away, come away, Stone

[037-413]boy and his uncles will kill all of us.”

[037-414]So the buffalo withdrew, leaving over two thousand of their dead and

[037-415]wounded on the field, only to be skinned and put away for the feasts

[037-416]of Stone boy and his uncles, who lived to be great chiefs of their own

[037-417]tribe, and whose many relations soon joined them on the banks of Stone

[037-418]Boy Creek.
