**Florrie Keefe** (née Florence) is Jack Keefe's wife and one of the most important characters in the letters. She appears first as a love interest, then as a wife whose domestic concerns, suspicions about Jack's fidelity, and surprising baseball acumen create both comedy and pathos.

## Biography

Jack meets Florrie during his first season with the White Sox and marries her between seasons. She is from Chicago and brings a city sensibility that contrasts with Jack's small-town naivety. They have a son, "Little Al" (named after Jack's friend Al Blanchard). Florrie follows baseball closely and develops strong opinions about Jack's career and teammates.

## In the Stories

Florrie is one of the few characters who can puncture Jack's self-regard. Her observations about baseball are often sharper than Jack's:

> "Florence knows a lot about baseball for a girl and you would be surprised" (002-157)

She worries about Jack's behavior with other women, leading to domestic tension. Jack recounts:

> "I caught her today Al with that look in her eye" (019-072)

Florrie also provides a domestic perspective on baseball life:

> "her husband is batting fungos and getting splinters in his hand" (004-008)

She is protective of their son and concerned about finances:

> "you don't make no big salary and you got no children" (013-069)

Jack's affection for her is genuine, expressed in his characteristic style:

> "I wouldn't trade Florrie for a dozen like she" (016-005)

## Relationships

- **Jack Keefe**: Husband — the source of both her pride and her frustration
- **Little Al**: Son, named after Al Blanchard
- **Al Blanchard**: Jack's friend, whom Florrie likely views as a rival for Jack's attention
- **Charley Comiskey**: Employer — Florrie has opinions about Jack's contract
