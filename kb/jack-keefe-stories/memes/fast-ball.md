The **fast ball** is the fundamental pitch in baseball — thrown at maximum velocity with minimal spin deflection. For Jack Keefe, it is the cornerstone of his pitching identity: his proudest weapon when working and his greatest concern when it fails him.

## Definition

In the 1910s, the fast ball was understood as the pitcher's basic offering — a pitch delivered with maximum speed and minimal sideways movement. Unlike modern baseball's taxonomy (four-seam, two-seam, cutter, splitter), Jack's era recognized fewer variants. The key quality was "hop" — the illusion that the ball rose or jumped as it reached the plate, making it harder to square up.

Jack's terminology includes "fast ball," "fast one," and "smoke," reflecting the colloquial language of early professional baseball.

## In the Stories

The fast ball is the most frequently discussed pitch in Jack's letters. He reports using it as his primary weapon:

> "I used my fast ball most of the while but showed a few curves" (001-042)

Recognition from veterans confirms his self-assessment:

> "You got some fast ball there boy" (001-043)

When his fast ball is working, Jack is confident. When it's not, he has explanations ready:

> "my fast ball didn't hop like it had ought to" (002-119)

The relationship between fast ball and curve ball is a recurring theme. Jack notes that his fast ball and curve are interdependent:

> "I got on my curve ball only when my fast 1 is hoping" (008-012)

Other pitchers' limitations are framed through the same lens:

> "He ain't got nothing but a fast ball" (002-033)

> "a fast ball that looks like my slow one" (003-059)

Jack also recounts a moment of frustration with catcher Ray Schalk's pitch selection:

> "Schalk kept signing for my fast ball and I kept giving it to him" (001-123)

## Historical Context

The fast ball was every pitcher's foundation in the dead-ball era. With home runs scarce and the ball itself softer and less lively than today, pitchers relied on velocity, movement, and deception rather than overpowering stuff alone. The "hop" Jack describes was likely a perceptual illusion, but it was widely discussed and valued among pitchers and scouts.

The contrast between a "hopping" fast ball and a straight one was a key distinction. A fast ball that didn't move was, in Jack's words, "as straight as a string" (009-089) — hittable by major league standards.
