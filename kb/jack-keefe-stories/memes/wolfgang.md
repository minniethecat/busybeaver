Wolfgang is a member of the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] organization who appears briefly but memorably in [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe]]'s letters as a workout companion of [[red-faber|Red Faber]]. Though his role on the club — pitcher, coach, or training-camp hand — is never explicitly defined in the stories, his pairing with Faber during spring preparation places him among the team's inner circle of arms.

## Biography

What little is known of Wolfgang comes entirely from two references in the letters. Historically, the name matches Mellie Wolfgang, a right-handed pitcher who appeared briefly for the White Sox during the 1910s. Mellie Wolfgang's major league career was short — a handful of appearances spread across several seasons — but his presence in a major league camp during the same period as Jack Keefe makes him the most plausible identification. In the stories themselves, Wolfgang is simply a name in the morning routine of a ball club preparing for the season.

## In the Stories

### The Morning Workout

Wolfgang appears in an early-morning scene during spring training. Jack, Faber, and Wolfgang form a small group that breaks away from the rest of the squad:

> "I had breakfast with Faber and Wolfgang and we decided to go out and get some exercise before the rest of the boys was up." [024-031]

The trio's initiative — getting their work in before anyone else — suggests a level of dedication that Jack plainly admires. The phrasing is collegial: they are not ordered to work out but "decide" together, three professionals taking their craft seriously.

### Part of the Pitching Group

Earlier, the scene is set with a note about who is expected to report for work:

> "They was suppose to work out with Faber and Wolfgang while the rest of the boys is taking it easy." [024-029]

The pairing of Wolfgang with Faber — a regular starter and one of the staff's more established pitchers — implies that Wolfgang held enough standing to be included in the serious work, not shunted aside with the reserves.
