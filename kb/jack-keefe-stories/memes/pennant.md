The **pennant** is the league championship in professional baseball — won by the team with the best regular-season record and granting entry to the World Series. In Jack Keefe's letters, the pennant represents the highest aspiration of a ballplayer and the measure of a team's season.

## Definition

In baseball's two-league structure (American and National), each league's champion is said to have "won the pennant." The term originates from the practice of awarding a championship flag or pennant to the winning club, which would fly it over their ballpark the following season. Before the modern playoff system, the pennant was won by finishing first in the regular season standings — a grueling test of consistency over 154 games.

## In the Stories

The pennant appears in Jack's letters as an object of both ambition and rationalization. He expresses confidence that his pitching can make the difference:

> "if I do we will win the pennant sure because Detroit has got a rotten pitching staff" (004-006)

But he also acknowledges the limits of individual contribution:

> "One man can't win no pennant" (005-052)

By 1915, as his career stabilizes, Jack frames his own aspirations in terms of the pennant race:

> "His heart set on the pennant" (013-054)

And he takes pride in his team being competitive:

> "them being in the fight for the pennant and they can't nobody say I'm not doing my share" (013-067)

The pennant also appears in a lighter moment when Jack fills out a form:

> "I had a notion to put down 'pennant' for a joke but the man says he means occupation" (013-042)

## Historical Context

In the 1910s, the American League pennant was fiercely contested among the Philadelphia Athletics (winners 1910–11, 1913–14), Boston Red Sox (1912, 1915–16), and Chicago White Sox (1917). The National League saw dominance by the New York Giants and Chicago Cubs.

Jack Keefe's fictional career intersects with several real pennant races. The White Sox of his era were building toward their 1917 championship, and Jack's letters capture the day-to-day experience of a player on a contending but not yet dominant team.
