**George "Buck" Weaver** (1890–1956) was a real-life Chicago White Sox third baseman and shortstop. In Jack Keefe's letters, Weaver appears as a teammate whose defensive play draws both praise and criticism from the pitcher watching from the mound.

## Biography

Weaver was one of the best third basemen in the American League during the 1910s, known for his enthusiastic play and versatility (he played both shortstop and third base). He was a key member of the 1917 World Series champion White Sox. He is perhaps best known historically as one of the eight "Black Sox" players banned after the 1919 World Series scandal — though Weaver took no money and played to win, he was banned for knowledge of the fix.

## In the Stories

Weaver appears in Jack's game narratives as a frequent participant in offensive and defensive action:

> "In our half of the seventh inning Weaver and Schalk got on" (001-111)

Jack is protective but critical of Weaver's fielding:

> "a ball that Weaver had to go back of second base for" (002-035)

Weaver's defense and effort are noted alongside others:

> "Weaver or Jackson ought to of caught in a hollow tooth" (019-024)

The name "Weaver" appears in Jack's breathless play-by-play as one of the familiar figures populating his baseball world.

## Historical Note

Buck Weaver batted .324 in the 1919 World Series and made no errors, playing clean baseball throughout. His lifetime ban remains one of baseball's most debated punishments. Lardner, who knew the White Sox intimately, would later write about the scandal — but "You Know Me Al" predates it.
