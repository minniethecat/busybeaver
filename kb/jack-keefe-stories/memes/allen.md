Allen is a left-handed pitcher who serves as [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] roommate during spring training with the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] and later becomes his reluctant brother-in-law. A product of the Pacific Coast League, Allen's career trajectory and personal entanglements with Jack provide some of the most sustained narrative threads in the early letters of "[[you-know-me-al|You Know Me Al]]."

## Biography

Allen arrives at spring training as "a lefthander from the Coast League" [001-033], part of the annual influx of hopefuls competing for roster spots. His early promise is evident — Jack notes him as one of the "lefthanders" who "done good work" early in camp [001-043]. However, his fortunes decline rapidly: while Jack secures a spot with the White Sox, "Poor Allen had to go with the second club back to Los Angeles" [001-051] when the team makes its final cuts.

Despite this setback, Allen reappears in subsequent seasons. By the second year of their correspondence, he has joined the White Sox and lives in [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]], where Jack describes visiting "Allen's flat to play cards" [002-040] and notes that "Allen was going awful bad in Cleveland" [002-058] during a rough pitching stretch. Manager [[jimmy-callahan|Jimmy Callahan]] used Allen as part of the pitching rotation alongside Scott, Russell, and [[eddie-cicotte|Eddie Cicotte]] [002-113].

Off the field, Allen marries Marie, the sister of Jack's eventual wife [[florrie-keefe|Florrie]]. This familial connection — making Allen and Jack brothers-in-law — becomes the source of extensive domestic comedy and conflict throughout the letters.

## In the Stories

### Spring Training and Early Days

Allen first appears as Jack's roommate during the White Sox spring training camp. Jack's early impressions are favorable, noting Allen among the promising young pitchers. But the competitive reality of baseball quickly separates them: "Poor Allen had to go with the second club back to Los Angeles" while Jack stays with the big league team [001-051]. Jack later exults in Allen's misfortune with characteristic schadenfreude when "that lucky lefthander Allen shut out Cleveland with two hits" [001-153] — though the praise is backhanded at best.

### The Flat-Sharing Years

When Jack returns to Chicago, the Allen connection intensifies. Jack initially stays with the Allens: "I am going with Allen and his wife and her sister Florrie" [002-149]. This arrangement — where Jack meets his future wife Florrie through Allen's wife Marie — sets up the domestic triangle that drives much of the narrative.

Jack moves into "a flat across the hall from the Allens" [003-031], and the proximity breeds conflict. The Thanksgiving dinner at the Allens' flat [003-046] marks a turning point: Jack's behavior leads to a physical altercation when "Marie says I had insulted Allen and he gets up and slaps my face" [003-065]. Jack's indignation is palpable: "I thought this Allen had some sense" [003-057].

### The Falling Out and Reconciliation

The domestic crisis escalates when Jack, feeling financially exploited — "Allen had gave her enough money to go home but she spent it" [003-154] — contemplates drastic action: "For a minute I thought I would follow Allen and his wife down to the deep blue sea" [003-156]. Jack later reflects bitterly that "Marie and her brother-in-law Allen grafted off of me" [004-010].

> "Allen told me his wife wanted to take Florrie out to the coast league but I said No she is going to stay here and marry me." [004-087]

Yet the relationship endures. Jack gradually reconciles with his brother-in-law, moving back into "the spair room at Allen's flat" [004-139] and eventually acknowledging: "I have tooken my baggage over to Allen's and I am there now writeing" [004-142].

### The Baby and the Name

In a final ironic twist, Jack and Florrie name their newborn son Allen — though Jack is at pains to clarify that "we are going to name him Allen which Florrie thinks is after her sister's husband but it is after the coach that got me my job with the White Sox" [004-146]. Jack's protest is characteristically unconvincing: "I wisht I had never named him Allen" [005-032]. When the baby cries at the sight of the real Allen, Jack notes with satisfaction: "When he begin to cry I chased Allen out of the room" [005-008].
