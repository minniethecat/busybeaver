A **contract** in professional baseball is the binding agreement between a player and a club, stipulating salary, term, and conditions of employment. In Jack Keefe's letters, contract negotiations are a recurring drama — a test of his worth, his nerve, and his relationship with White Sox owner Charley Comiskey.

## Definition

Major League Baseball contracts in the 1910s were governed by the reserve clause, which bound a player to his team in perpetuity. Once a player signed, the club held exclusive rights to his services. Players could be traded, sold, or released at the club's discretion. The player's only leverage was the threat of holding out — refusing to sign until terms improved. Free agency would not exist for another six decades.

## In the Stories

Jack's first encounter with a major league contract sets the pattern for his relationship with the business of baseball:

> "He told me I could sign as well as not. He says Comiskey is out of town but it won't make no difference" (001-015)

He states his demands with characteristic bravado:

> "I won't sign no contract unless I get the salary you and I talked of, three thousand dollars" (001-017)

But his resolve quickly crumbles:

> "He says I guess you are pretty wise. So I signed up" (001-022)

The gap between Jack's stated demands and his actual settlements is a running joke:

> "My yearly salary will be fifteen hundred dollars" (001-023) — half of his initial demand

By the season's end, Jack is already planning his next negotiation:

> "I won't sign for no less than five thousand" (002-148)

The contract also serves as a measure of status — Jack cites his contract terms to his friend Al as proof of his value:

> "My contract calls for twenty-eight hundred" (002-160)

## Historical Context

The reserve clause was the defining economic institution of baseball from 1879 until its overthrow by the Messersmith-McNally arbitration decision in 1975. Under the reserve system, players were effectively bound to one team for their entire careers unless traded or released. Salaries were suppressed — even stars rarely earned more than $10,000 per year.

Jack's experience — demanding a raise, threatening to hold out, then signing for less — was typical. Players had minimal bargaining power, and owners like Comiskey held all the cards. The disparity between Jack's bravado ("I won't sign for less than five thousand") and reality ("fifteen hundred") reflects the economic reality of the dead-ball era.
