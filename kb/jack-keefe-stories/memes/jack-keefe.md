**Jack Keefe** is the fictional narrator and protagonist of Ring Lardner's "You Know Me Al," a series of letters written to his hometown friend Al Blanchard. Jack is a young, right-handed pitcher from Bedford, Indiana, who ascends from the minors to the Chicago White Sox. His voice — boastful yet insecure, naive yet shrewd — is one of the great creations of American literary humor.

## Biography

Jack Keefe is a rookie pitcher who begins the story having just been sold to the Chicago White Sox. He is unmarried at the outset, courting a woman named Violet before eventually marrying Florrie. Jack's pitching repertoire includes a fast ball, curve ball, and spitter — the last of which he considers his most effective weapon despite his catcher's reluctance to call for it.

His career trajectory: Bedford (minor league) → Terre Haute → Chicago White Sox → San Francisco (brief demotion) → White Sox → military service (World War I). Throughout, he writes to Al with a combination of self-promotion, complaint, and unintentional self-revelation.

## In the Stories

Jack's letters reveal his character through action and self-report. He is endlessly proud of his pitching:

> "I used my fast ball most of the while but showed them a few spitters and they missed them a foot" (001-042)

He is quick to blame others for his failures:

> "I come back to the bench pretty hot and I says You're a fine manager" (001-111)

And he exhibits the wounded pride of a man who cannot see his own limitations:

> "One man can't win no pennant" (005-052)

Even in military service, his identity remains tied to baseball:

> "it will always be just plain Jack Keefe" (015-034)

> "Corp. Jack Keefe" (015-039)

## Relationships

Jack's most important relationships structure the letters:

- **Al Blanchard**: The recipient of all the letters, a hometown friend in Bedford who serves as Jack's confessor and audience
- **Florrie Keefe**: Jack's wife, whose practicality and jealousy create domestic comedy
- **Charley Comiskey**: The White Sox owner, whom Jack alternately praises and resents over contract negotiations
- **Jimmy Callahan**: The White Sox manager, whose decisions Jack frequently questions
- **Ray Schalk**: The catcher whose pitch-calling frustrates Jack
