**Ray Schalk** (1892–1970) was a real-life Hall of Fame catcher for the Chicago White Sox (1912–1928). In Jack Keefe's letters, Schalk is a recurring figure of tension — the man behind the plate whose pitch selections Jack questions constantly but whose judgment is usually vindicated.

## Biography

Schalk was one of the finest defensive catchers in baseball history, known for his durability, throwing arm, and game-calling. He caught four no-hitters in his career and was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1955. In Jack's fictional timeline, Schalk is the established regular catcher whose authority Jack chafes against.

## In the Stories

The central conflict between Jack and Schalk is pitch selection. Jack wants to throw his spitter; Schalk wants fast balls:

> "I would of handed him a spitter but Schalk kept signing for fast balls" (001-110)

> "I wanted to give him a spitter but Schalk signs me for the fast one" (001-127)

Jack eventually concedes Schalk's judgment:

> "I had to tell Schalk not to ask me for my spitter much because my fast one looked pretty good" (002-093)

But he also implies that Schalk's pitch-calling cost him outs:

> "Schalk had of let me throw him spitters instead of fast ones" (002-048)

> "I could of whiffed him but Schalk makes me give him my fast one" (002-058)

Schalk appears in game action alongside Jack:

> "In our half of the seventh inning Weaver and Schalk got on" (001-111)

And in the ongoing contest between pitcher and baserunner:

> "Schalk signs for a spitter and Cobb is still talking" (001-127)

## Relationships

- **Jack Keefe**: Batterymate — the pitcher-catcher relationship is one of the story's central dynamics
- **Jimmy Callahan**: Manager — Schalk and Callahan share authority over game strategy
- **Ty Cobb**: Opponent — Schalk tries to control Cobb's baserunning

## Historical Note

The historical Ray Schalk was renowned for his defense and game-calling. He was the White Sox catcher during the 1919 World Series and was one of the "Clean Sox" who played honestly. Lardner's portrayal captures the tension inherent in the pitcher-catcher relationship — the catcher calling pitches, the pitcher wanting autonomy.
