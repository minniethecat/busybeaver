Eddie Collins is a second baseman for the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] who appears in [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters as one of the team's most respected hitters and a regular presence at the training table alongside [[kid-gleason|Kid Gleason]], [[ping-bodie|Ping Bodie]], and the other veterans.

## Biography

Collins was already an established star by the time Jack Keefe joined the White Sox. He had previously played for the [[philadelphia-athletics|Philadelphia Athletics]], where he was part of the famed "$100,000 Infield." With Chicago, Collins was a fixture in the lineup — a left-handed hitter known for his batting eye and his ability to make contact. Jack, with characteristic blend of respect and self-regard, describes him simply as "one of the best batters in the bunch" [001-043].

## In the Stories

### At the Training Table

Collins is most frequently glimpsed in the dining room of the team hotel, where Jack reports sitting "at the same table in the dining room along with Gleason and Collins and Bodie and Fournier and Allen" [001-044]. This recurring image establishes Collins as part of the veteran core — the players who have already made it, whose status Jack both envies and aspires to.

### A Compliment, and a Warning

Early in Jack's first spring training, Collins offers what sounds like encouragement:

> "You got some fast ball there boy." [001-044]

But [[kid-gleason|Kid Gleason]] immediately undercuts the praise, warning Jack: "Don't believe what he tells you boy. If the pitchers in this league weren't no faster than you I would still be playing ball and I would be the best hitter in the country" [001-044]. The exchange captures the veteran's dynamic — Collins playing the good cop, Gleason keeping the rookie's ego in check.

### In the Field

Collins also appears in moments of defensive action. During a game against Detroit, Jack grudgingly acknowledges that a good inning was partly thanks to his second baseman: "Callahan says Yes but you wouldn't look so good if Collins hadn't jumped up on the fence and catched that one off Crawford" [001-132].

> "Baker didn't hit one hard all afternoon and I didn't have no trouble with Collins neither. I let them down with five blows all though the papers give them seven." [002-036]

### A Tough Out

Jack recounts facing Collins in an intrasquad game during his first spring training. "I struck Collins out and he is one of the best batters in the bunch" [001-043] — Jack framing the strikeout as a testament to his own stuff, as he does with every success.
