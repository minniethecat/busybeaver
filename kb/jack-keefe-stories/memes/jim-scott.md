Jim Scott is a right-handed pitcher for the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] who appears throughout [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters as both a rotation-mate and a rival. A veteran with a signature "fadeaway" pitch, Scott occupies the established role that Jack is forever trying to claim for himself — and Jack's accounts of Scott's performances are always filtered through the lens of his own competitive ambition.

## Biography

Scott was a mainstay of the White Sox pitching staff during the mid-1910s, part of a rotation that included [[ed-walsh|Ed Walsh]], [[eddie-cicotte|Eddie Cicotte]], [[joe-benz|Joe Benz]], and eventually Jack Keefe himself. Known as "Death Valley Jim" in baseball lore for his durability, Scott appears in Jack's letters as a pitcher whose status Jack simultaneously respects and resents.

## In the Stories

### The Veteran in the Rotation

Scott is referenced from Jack's very first days with the club. During spring training, Jack notes that manager [[jimmy-callahan|Jimmy Callahan]] "asks Scott and Russell and I to warm up easy and pitch a few to the batters" [001-036], grouping the rookie with the veterans for the first time. But Jack quickly forms his own opinion of Scott's methods: "Scott pitched to them first and kept laying them right over with nothing on them. I don't believe a man gets any batting practice that way" [001-036].

### The Fadeaway

Jack's curiosity about pitching technique leads to a revealing exchange with Scott:

> "I was talking to Lord and Scott afterward and I say to Scott How do you pitch to Baker? He says I use my fadeaway. I says How do you throw it? He says Just like you throw a fast ball to anybody else. I says Why do you call it a fadeaway then? He says Because when I throw it to Baker it fades away over the fence." [001-080]

The deadpan reply — a joke at his own expense — shows Scott as a veteran who has learned not to take himself too seriously, a quality Jack has yet to acquire.

### Opening Day and Its Aftermath

When the season opens in Cleveland, Scott gets the start while Jack watches from the bench. Jack's account is characteristically self-centered: "Scott was in there and he didn't have nothing" [001-106]. Later, Callahan reveals his plans for the rotation: "Callahan is saveing up Scott and Allen and Russell and Cicotte and I for the big show. Walsh isn't in no shape and neither is Benz" [002-114], grouping Jack with the trusted starters.

### A Rivalry with Weaver?

Jack hints at a strained relationship between Scott and third baseman [[george-weaver|Buck Weaver]], suggesting that a costly error might have been intentional: "I would not be surprised if he done it from spitework because him and Scott is pals and probily he is jellus of me getting all the headlines" [005-005]. As always, Jack interprets events through the lens of his own central importance.
