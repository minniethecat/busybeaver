The Chicago Cubs are the National League baseball club based in [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] and the cross-town rival of the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]]. In [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters, the Cubs serve as both a measuring stick for his own abilities and the primary antagonist in the interleague City Series — an annual postseason exhibition that looms large in Jack's competitive imagination.

## Overview

The Cubs were the dominant Chicago baseball institution during the era of Jack's letters, playing in the National League while Jack's White Sox competed in the [[american-league|American League]]. The cross-town rivalry culminated each autumn in the City Series, a best-of-series exhibition that Jack treats with the gravity of a pennant race.

By the mid-1910s, the Cubs were perennial contenders — Jack notes they "finished second in their league" [002-112] — making them the ideal foil for Jack's boasts and anxieties about his own pitching prowess.

## In the Stories

### The City Series

The Cubs-Whoite Sox City Series is the central competitive axis of Jack's letters. Every fall, anticipation builds: "Every body in this town is wild over it. The Cubs finished second in their league and we have got the pennant" [002-112]. Jack's confidence in these contests is unshakeable, bordering on delusional:

> "I can beat them Cubs if I get any kind of decent support." [002-135]

When he actually faces them, Jack's self-assessment is characteristically triumphant: "I had everything and the Cubs done well to score a run. I was giveing them my fast ball and they was swatting at it like a lot of old women" [002-147].

### Jack's Psychological Advantage

Jack believes he owns the Cubs. Throughout multiple seasons, he asserts this ownership with increasing confidence:

> "I did not have nobody but the Cubs to pitch against all season" [005-069]

He describes "makeing them Cubs look like a lot of rummys" [005-091] and concludes definitively that "the Cubs could not never hit me" [005-102]. After one particularly satisfying outing, he crows: "I guess the Cubs won't never want to play us again" [005-115].

### Later Seasons

As Jack's career progresses, his dominance over the Cubs remains a constant. Even when traded or threatened with a trade, he reflects: "I wisht I was going over North and see what the Cubs look like and I wish I pitched for them" [013-014] — a rare moment of hypothetical admiration for the rival club.

## Key Members

The Cubs roster is never detailed in Jack's letters (his focus remains on his own team), but their collective identity as "them Cubs" [002-102] — always referred to with a mixture of contempt and competitive respect — makes them one of the most frequently invoked organizations in the stories.
