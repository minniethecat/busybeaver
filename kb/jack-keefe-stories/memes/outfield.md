The **outfield** refers to the grassy area of a baseball field beyond the infield dirt, patrolled by the left fielder, center fielder, and right fielder. In Jack Keefe's letters, outfielders are a frequent subject — sometimes praised, often criticized, and occasionally the butt of his jokes.

## Definition

In the standard baseball defensive alignment, the outfield is the outermost defensive zone. The left fielder, center fielder, and right fielder are responsible for catching fly balls, fielding balls hit through or over the infield, and throwing the ball back to the infield to prevent runners from advancing.

Outfielders in the dead-ball era typically played shallower than modern outfielders because the emphasis on contact hitting and bunting meant fewer deep drives. Speed, a strong throwing arm, and the ability to read the ball off the bat were prized outfield skills.

## In the Stories

Jack Keefe, as a pitcher, has a vested interest in the quality of his outfield defense. He expresses concern about field conditions and their effect on outfielders:

> "I'm afraid some of our outfielders will run their legs off and get lost out there" (001-136)

He recounts stories of outfielders in extreme conditions:

> "I bet them outfielders would not never of found the ball" (008-007)

> "the outfielders would probily half to ride horseback" (012-029)

Bushers — players from the minor leagues — are often described as outfielders, reflecting the positions where teams most often auditioned new talent:

> "He is a bush outfielder from the New England League" (001-070)

Jack himself has a memorable day in the outfield:

> "the day I played the outfield in Terre Haute when Burns and me got into a fight" (014-027)

Young prospects and veteran castoffs alike populate the outfield in Jack's world:

> "a young outfielder from a bush league" (019-027)

> "this new outfielder they have got name Flagstaff" (025-012)

## Historical Context

The dead-ball era outfield was a different landscape from today's game. Outfields were often larger and more idiosyncratic (Polo Grounds' cavernous center field, Fenway Park's left field wall), and the softer, scuffed baseballs of the era didn't carry as far. Outfielders played shallow, were expected to cover more ground, and had more defensive responsibility on balls hit in front of them.

"Cannons for arms" were less critical than quick releases and accurate throws — the hit-and-run and aggressive baserunning of the era meant outfielders had to get the ball back to the infield quickly.
