An **inning** is the basic structural unit of a baseball game, consisting of each team's turn at bat. A regulation professional game consists of nine innings. In Jack Keefe's letters, innings serve as the narrative clock of his accounts — marking when events happen, how long he pitches, and the rhythm of his performances.

## Definition

Each inning is divided into two halves: the top (visiting team bats) and the bottom (home team bats). An inning ends when the fielding team records three outs. A full nine-inning game thus comprises 54 outs (27 per team). In Jack's era, starting pitchers were expected to complete games — going "the full nine innings" was the standard of durability, not the exception.

## In the Stories

Jack uses inning counts to establish his workload and endurance. In spring training, he builds up gradually:

> "I have not went over five innings yet" (001-057)

Complete games are a point of pride and a measure of his value:

> "I went the full nine innings both times" (001-073)

Specific innings mark crucial game moments. Jack is sent out to pitch a decisive inning:

> "in the eight inning Callahan told me to go out and pitch" (001-105)

The half-inning structure provides the framework for Jack's play-by-play narration:

> "In our half of the seventh inning Weaver and Schalk got on" (001-111)

Managers and catchers give inning-by-inning instruction:

> "when you go out next inning or Cobb will steal it off you" (001-129)

Jack's performance evaluation often centers on innings pitched and results within them:

> "I pitched three innings for the recruts and shut them out" (001-042)

> "I only worked one inning but I guess them Browns is glad of that" (001-110)

## Historical Context

The nine-inning standard was well-established by the 1910s, having been adopted in 1857 by the National Association of Base Ball Players. Before that, games were played to 21 runs ("aces"). The dead-ball era featured notably shorter game times than today — typically under two hours — because pitchers worked quickly, few relievers were used, and balls stayed in play more.

Complete games were the norm: in 1910, American League pitchers completed 73% of their starts. Jack's expectation that he should finish what he starts reflects the era's standards.
