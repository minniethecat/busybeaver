The **baseball uniform** is the standardized clothing worn by players during games, consisting of jersey, pants, cap, and stockings, each bearing the team's colors and insignia. For Jack Keefe, putting on a major league uniform — specifically, the Chicago White Sox uniform — is a transformative experience that marks his entry into professional baseball.

## Definition

In the 1910s, baseball uniforms were made of heavy wool flannel, with lace-up fronts, standing collars, and baggy pants that ended just below the knee (worn with long stockings). The home uniform was typically white or light gray; the road uniform was darker to hide dirt from travel. Team logos were embroidered on the chest or sleeve. Numbers on uniforms would not become standard until the late 1920s (the Yankees were first in 1929).

## In the Stories

Jack's first experience in a White Sox uniform is a moment of self-admiration and identity transformation:

> "I ain't a bad looking guy in the White Sox uniform Al. I will have my picture taken and send you some" (001-032)

The uniform is a marker of professional status, something to be shown off. He also records a colorful detail about Ty Cobb's intimidation tactics:

> "He is a busher and he ain't scared of having his uniform stole off his back" (002-009)

The uniform takes on additional meaning during wartime:

> "you ought to see me in my officers' uniform" (014-023)

A manager's advice ties the uniform to practical game concerns:

> "You better leave your uniform in here when you go out next inning or Cobb will steal it off you" (001-129)

Even in the military, the contrast between baseball and army uniforms is noted:

> "regular soldiers even without no uniforms" (014-008)

## Historical Context

Baseball uniforms evolved significantly between 1900 and 1920. At the turn of the century, players wore heavy wool even in summer heat, and uniforms were recycled between players with minimal washing. By the 1910s, lighter fabrics and better laundry practices improved comfort. The iconic White Sox uniform of Jack's era featured a distinctive "Sox" logo and pinstripes — one of baseball's classic looks.

The uniform also reflected baseball's social structure. Major league uniforms were clean, tailored, and professional; minor league and semi-pro uniforms were hand-me-downs. Jack's pride in his White Sox uniform is the pride of a man who has made it to the top of his profession.
