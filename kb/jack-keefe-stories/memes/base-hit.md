A **base hit** is the primary offensive event in baseball: the batter strikes the ball and reaches first base (or beyond) safely without benefit of an error or fielder's choice. In Jack Keefe's letters, base hits are both earned and contested — the line between hit and error is a matter of perennial scorekeeping debate, and Jack has strong opinions.

## Definition

A base hit is recorded when a batter reaches base as a direct result of hitting a fair ball that is not caught before touching the ground, and the defensive team fails to put the batter-runner out. Hits are classified by the number of bases reached: single (one base), double (two bases), triple (three bases), or home run (four bases, scoring a run). The official scorer's judgment determines whether a batted ball is a hit or an error.

## Historical Context

In the dead-ball era, batting averages were lower than today, and the distinction between a .250 hitter (ordinary) and a .300 hitter (star) was significant. The 1910 American League batting champion, Nap Lajoie, hit .384 — but the league average was around .245. Home runs were scarce (the entire White Sox team hit 7 in 1910), so base hits were the currency of offense.

The official scorer's role was more powerful and more contested than today. Scorers were often local sportswriters with hometown loyalties, and pitchers like Jack had every reason to view their decisions with suspicion.
