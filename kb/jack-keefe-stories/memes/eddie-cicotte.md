Eddie Cicotte is a right-handed pitcher for the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] who appears as a recurring figure in [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters, both as a respected teammate in the pitching rotation and — through his later involvement in the 1919 Black Sox scandal — as one of the most historically significant players Jack ever shared a clubhouse with.

## Biography

Cicotte was an established major league pitcher by the time Jack Keefe joined the White Sox. He had previously pitched for the [[boston-red-sox|Boston Red Sox]], who released him — a slight that Jack notes still stings: "Eddie hates to beat this Boston club on acct. they canned him" [025-069]. With Chicago, Cicotte became a mainstay of the pitching staff, known for his knuckleball and his durability.

During Jack's years with the team, Cicotte appears as part of the regular rotation alongside pitchers like Scott, Russell, and [[allen|Allen]] [002-113]. He was, by Jack's account, a pitcher who could be counted on — though Jack's assessments of teammates are always filtered through his own competitive self-regard.

## In the Stories

### A Fellow Pitcher

Cicotte appears in Jack's letters primarily as a fellow member of the pitching staff. Jack describes manager [[jimmy-callahan|Jimmy Callahan]] "saveing up Scott and Allen and Russell and Cicotte" for key games [002-113], positioning Cicotte as one of the trusted arms in the rotation.

### Uneven Performances

Jack recounts one game where "Eddie Cicotte didn't have nothing and the so and so's beat us" [018-113], displaying his characteristic blend of sympathy and superiority when discussing a teammate's poor outing. Yet Cicotte could also be dominant: "Eddie Cicotte was in there with everything he had and we win a close one" [025-069].

### Off-Field Presence

Cicotte is glimpsed in the social world of the team as well. Jack describes "walking with Buck Weaver and Eddie Cicotte and this here Belden" [024-022] — a casual image of teammates moving through a city together, unaware of the historical weight their names would later carry.

> "I had some of my best stuff but they got to me for 1 run in the forth and then saved the other for Eddie Cicotte in the 9th inning and shut out after that." [004-137]

This passage captures the rhythm of a pitching staff — Jack starts, Cicotte relieves, and together they "shut out" the opposition. It is the ordinary stuff of a baseball season, preserved in Jack's unpolished prose.
