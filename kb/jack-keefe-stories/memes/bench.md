The **bench** is both a physical space — the dugout or seating area where players sit during games — and a professional condition: to be "on the bench" or "benched" means being removed from the game or not included in the starting lineup. In Jack Keefe's letters, the bench represents the anxious space between participation and obsolescence.

## Definition

In the ballparks of the 1910s, the bench (or dugout) was a simple, often uncovered seating area along the first- and third-base lines, below field level. Players not in the game sat here, watching the action and awaiting their turn. To be "benched" meant being removed by the manager — a professional judgment that could be temporary (a bad inning) or ominous (loss of the starting role).

## In the Stories

The bench is where Jack's dramatic moments often begin or end. He is summoned from it to pitch in relief:

> "Callahan called Benz to the bench and sent for me. I was down in the bullpen warming up" (001-110)

And he returns to it in frustration after a poor performance:

> "I come back to the bench pretty hot and I says You're a fine manager" (001-111)

The bench is also where instruction and criticism are delivered. Manager Callahan uses it as a classroom:

> "I come in to the bench and Callahan says Are your friends from Bedford up in the grandstand?" (001-129)

Jack describes the tension of watching and waiting:

> "I was looking at the bench all the time expecting Callahan to call me in" (001-134)

Even equipment becomes part of the bench tableau:

> "he throwed his bat to the bench" (001-035)

## Historical Context

In the dead-ball era, the bench had a different character from today's dugout. Rosters were smaller (typically 20–22 players), and the reserve players often included a "bullpen catcher," a utility infielder, and perhaps a fourth outfielder. Everyone else was expected to play — there was no room for specialists.

The relationship between the bench and the manager was more intimate and more exposed than today. With no bullpen phone, no video review, and no coaching staff beyond perhaps one assistant, the manager's bench-side decisions were immediate, visible, and final.
