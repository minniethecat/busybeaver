A **double-header** is a scheduling format in which two complete baseball games are played between the same two teams on a single day. In Jack Keefe's letters, double-headers represent both a test of endurance and an opportunity for his characteristic complaints about the business side of baseball.

## Definition

Double-headers are typically scheduled to make up postponed games (rainouts) or to accommodate holiday crowds (notably Independence Day). In the dead-ball era, before night baseball and air travel, double-headers were the primary mechanism for fitting a 154-game schedule into the available summer days. Players would play both games back-to-back with only a brief intermission, and starting pitchers were often expected to throw complete games in both ends — a physical demand almost unheard of in modern baseball.

## In the Stories

Jack's most vivid reflections on double-headers center on the physical toll:

> "I was tireder then if Id pitched 2 double headers in 1 p.m." (012-014)

The logistics of travel and scheduling around double-headers are a recurring concern:

> "we had to leave at 6 in the a.m. to get in all the double headers" (012-029)

Jack describes the social and culinary interludes between games:

> "between the 2 games when theys a double header. And we was gave a dinner party" (012-053)

He also uses the double-header as a social opportunity:

> "when we're playing St. Louis a double header so I ast Comiskey to shake hands with Florrie's uncle" (012-091)

The double-header's impact on players' appearance is noted with characteristic humor — a player's wife:

> "looked like she had pitched a double header in St. Louis or something" (014-007)

Jack records the scheduling reason matter-of-factly:

> "tomorrow is Sunday and we have a double header Monday on account of the rain" (002-076)

And the holiday tradition:

> "with St. Louis tomorrow and a double header on the 4th of July" (004-156)

## Historical Context

Double-headers were far more common in the early 20th century than today. With no lights for night games, no off days for travel, and Sunday baseball restrictions in some cities, teams had fewer days to complete their schedules. The American League's 154-game schedule packed into roughly 180 available days meant teams averaged nearly one double-header per week.

The practice of "twi-night" double-headers (late afternoon + evening) would not emerge until the 1930s with the advent of stadium lights. Jack's double-headers were daytime affairs, often played under summer sun with no relief from the heat.
