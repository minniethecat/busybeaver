A ballplayer's **salary** is the money paid by the club for his services during the season. In Jack Keefe's letters, salary is perhaps the most frequent non-baseball topic — the measure of his value, the subject of constant comparison with teammates, and the source of both pride and anxiety.

## Definition

Major League Baseball salaries in the 1910s ranged from roughly $1,200 (rookie minimum) to $10,000+ for established stars like Ty Cobb or Walter Johnson. Players were paid only during the regular season (roughly April through October). Off-season income was the player's own responsibility. The reserve clause prevented players from shopping their services to other clubs, so salary negotiations were essentially take-it-or-leave-it propositions.

## In the Stories

Jack's salary negotiations are a masterclass in self-delusion. He opens with an aggressive demand:

> "I won't sign no contract unless I get the salary you and I talked of, three thousand dollars" (001-017)

But settles for half:

> "My yearly salary will be fifteen hundred dollars" (001-023)

By season's end, the number has grown:

> "My contract calls for twenty-eight hundred" (002-160)

Salary appears in Jack's domestic life as a point of tension. His wife Florrie questions his spending:

> "you don't make no big salary and you got no children" (013-069)

The contrast between peacetime baseball salary and wartime military pay is stark and disillusioning:

> "a man that give up a big league salary and the world serious dough to help Uncle Sam out" (015-025)

> "the salary is $36.00 per mo. instead of $2800.00 per yr." (015-033)

## Historical Context

Baseball salaries in the dead-ball era were modest by any standard and shockingly low compared to modern figures. In 1910, the average major league salary was approximately $2,500 — roughly $70,000 in 2024 dollars. Even the game's biggest stars earned less than $10,000. The minimum salary was not formally established until 1946.

The reserve clause depressed wages by eliminating competition for player services. When the Federal League (1914–1915) briefly offered an alternative employer, salaries spiked — demonstrating what a free market could mean for players. The Federal League's collapse restored the owners' monopsony and the salary structure Jack knew.
