**Charles "Charley" Comiskey** (1859–1931) was the real-life owner of the Chicago White Sox from 1901 until his death. In Jack Keefe's letters, Comiskey appears as the ultimate authority figure — the man who signs the checks, approves the contracts, and whose favor Jack desperately seeks.

## Biography

Comiskey was a former player (first baseman) and manager who became one of the founding owners of the American League. He built Comiskey Park (opened 1910) and owned the White Sox during their dominant period leading up to the 1917 World Series championship and the subsequent 1919 Black Sox scandal. In Jack's fictional timeline (1914–1918), Comiskey is the established patriarch of the franchise.

## In the Stories

Jack's relationship with Comiskey is defined by money, recognition, and the gap between Jack's self-image and his actual standing. Their first meeting establishes the dynamic:

> "I signed my contract yesterday afternoon. He is a fine man" (001-022)

Jack projects warmth onto business transactions:

> "Comiskey ought to feel pretty good about me winning and I guess he will give me a contract for anything I want" (002-148)

Comiskey occasionally shows personal attention that Jack treasures:

> "I ast Comiskey to shake hands with Florrie's uncle" (012-091)

And Jack frames his career ambitions in terms of Comiskey's approval:

> "win a pennant for Charley Comiskey the old Roman" (018-114)

## Relationships

- **Jack Keefe**: Employee — Comiskey signs Jack's contracts and receives his letters
- **Jimmy Callahan**: Manager — Comiskey's on-field representative
- **Chicago White Sox**: The franchise Comiskey built

## Historical Note

The historical Comiskey was a controversial figure — beloved by some for his baseball acumen and generosity, criticized by others for his treatment of players (the low salaries and poor conditions that contributed to the 1919 Black Sox scandal). Lardner's portrayal of Comiskey through Jack's admiring but naive eyes captures one side of this complex figure.
