Ed Walsh is the ace of the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] pitching staff and the most accomplished pitcher [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe]] encounters in his early major league career. A future Hall of Famer whose best years were behind him by the time Jack arrived, Walsh appears in the letters as a complex figure — part mentor, part rival, and a constant measuring stick against which Jack measures his own ambitions.

## Biography

Walsh was one of the dominant pitchers of the dead-ball era, winning 40 games in 1908 with a 1.42 ERA — still among the greatest single seasons in baseball history. By the time Jack Keefe joined the White Sox around 1914–1915, Walsh's arm was beginning to fade, but his presence in the clubhouse remained immense. Jack's letters capture him in this twilight period: still respected, still vocal, but no longer the workhorse he once was.

## In the Stories

### The Salary Benchmark

Even before Jack arrives in Chicago, Walsh's name is invoked as the standard of compensation. Jack's advisor tells him to demand $3,000, and Jack notes: "I heard that Walsh was getting twice as much as that" [001-018]. From the very beginning, Walsh represents the summit Jack intends to climb.

### First Encounters

Walsh stays behind at the hotel when the team goes on a long walk during spring training, and he offers the rookie some pointed advice:

> "When Callahan comes back you better keep out of sight or tell him you are sick. I says I don't care nothing for Callahan. He says No but Callahan is crazy about you. He says You better obey orders and you will git along better. I guess Walsh thinks I am some rube." [001-034]

### The Spitball and the Jealousy

After Jack impresses in an intrasquad game, Walsh walks back to the hotel with him and offers technical advice wrapped in what Jack interprets as jealousy:

> "I guess I must of got Walsh's goat with my spitter because him and I walked back to the hotel together and he talked like he was kind of jealous. He says You will have to learn to cover up your spitter. He says I could stand a mile away and tell when you was going to throw it." [001-043]

### The Tenor Voice

Walsh's singing becomes a running joke. Gleason tells Jack: "I would take you to the grand opera only it would be throwing money away because we can hear Ed Walsh for nothing. Walsh has got some voice Al a loud high tenor" [002-076]. The detail humanizes the Hall of Famer — a man whose voice, like his fastball, was once formidable and is now something the team can laugh about together.

### Fading from the Picture

As the season progresses, Walsh's physical decline becomes apparent. Callahan's assessment is blunt: "Walsh isn't in no shape and neither is Benz" [002-114]. The veteran ace is being supplanted by younger arms — including, eventually, the busher himself.
