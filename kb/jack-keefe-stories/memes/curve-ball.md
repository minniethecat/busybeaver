A **curve ball** is a type of pitch in baseball that breaks or curves as it approaches the batter, making it difficult to hit. In Jack Keefe's world of early 20th-century baseball, the curve ball was a centerpiece of pitching strategy and a frequent topic of his boasting, complaints, and colorful observations.

## Definition

In baseball terminology of the 1910s, the curve ball (also known as a "hook" or "bender") is a pitch thrown with spin that causes it to deviate from a straight path. Unlike a fastball that relies on speed, the curve ball uses movement to deceive the batter. A well-thrown curve "breaks" — meaning it drops downward or moves sideways — making the batter swing where the ball was rather than where it ends up.

Jack Keefe uses the colloquial term "Hookem" in one letter, explaining the team's coded language:

> "when you want to say curve ball you say Hookem" (008-087)

## In the Stories

The curve ball appears throughout Jack Keefe's letters as both a source of pride and a subject of complaint. Jack frequently assesses other pitchers' curve balls with characteristic bluntness:

> "He ain't got no more curve ball than a rabbit" (002-131)

Jack's own curve ball is a point of frustration. He admits to mechanical flaws:

> "I got on my curve ball only when my fast 1 is hoping" (008-012)

And he describes the consequences of a curve that won't break:

> "what to do when you cant make your curve ball brake and your fast 1 is as straight as a string" (009-089)

Jack also mocks opponents whose curve balls lack effectiveness:

> "His curve ball broke about ½ a inch and you could of wrote a 4 act play on it" (004-102)

> "could not fool a blind man with his curve ball and if he was to hit a man in the head with it the man would think somebody called him" (006-032)

## Historical Context

The curve ball was well-established in professional baseball by the time of Jack Keefe's stories (1910s). Pitchers like Christy Mathewson had made the "fadeaway" (a variant curve/screwball) famous in the preceding decade. In Jack's era, the spitball and other "trick" deliveries were still legal, and the curve ball was considered an essential weapon in any pitcher's arsenal.

Jack's references to the "dinky curve ball" (003-059) (003-126) reflect a distinction pitchers made between a sharp, effective curve and a lazy, looping one that major league hitters could easily track. The half-inch break Jack mocks (004-102) underscores that late, sharp movement — not just any curve — was the standard.
