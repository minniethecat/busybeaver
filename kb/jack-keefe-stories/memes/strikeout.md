A **strikeout** occurs when a pitcher retires a batter by accumulating three strikes during an at-bat. In Jack Keefe's letters, the strikeout — or "whiff," in his colloquial language — is the definitive measure of a pitcher's dominance and a frequent subject of his self-evaluation.

## Definition

A strike is called when the batter swings and misses, when a pitch passes through the strike zone without a swing, or when the batter hits a foul ball with fewer than two strikes (a foul on the third strike is not an out unless caught). Three strikes retire the batter. In the dead-ball era, strikeouts were less common than today — batters prioritized contact, and pitchers conserved energy for complete games. But the strikeout remained the most emphatic way for a pitcher to assert control.

## In the Stories

Jack measures himself by his strikeouts. In spring training, he reports with evident satisfaction:

> "I whiffed eight men in five innings in practice" (001-057)

Against major league competition, strikeouts confirm his self-belief. Facing Hall of Famer Eddie Collins:

> "I struck Collins out and he is one of the hardest men to whiff in the league" (001-042)

He recounts specific victims with pride:

> "Anyway I whiffed Johnston" (001-110)

> "I whiffed Crawford" (002-046)

But the strikeout is also a site of tension between pitcher and catcher. Jack is frustrated when Schalk overrules his instincts:

> "I could of whiffed him but Schalk makes me give him my fast one" (002-058)

And he celebrates multi-strikeout performances:

> "I whiffed the next two" (002-094)

The ultimate feather in Jack's cap comes against Babe Ruth:

> "struck him out twice and they claim he is a sucker for a left hander" (025-069)

He even imagines easy strikeouts against non-professionals:

> "I bet I could of struck a bout 20 of them out" (010-038)

## Historical Context

Strikeout rates in the dead-ball era were roughly half of modern levels. In 1910, the American League leader in strikeouts per nine innings was Walter Johnson at 6.8 — modest by today's standards. Batters choked up on the bat, swung to make contact, and considered striking out a professional embarrassment.

The strikeout's status as a test of manhood is reflected in Jack's language. "Whiffing" a batter is a personal triumph. Being "whiffed" is a humiliation. This psychological dimension — the pitcher-batter confrontation as a contest of wills — is central to Jack's narrative voice and to the culture of early baseball.
