The fly ball is one of the fundamental events in baseball — a batted ball hit high into the air, capable of being caught by a fielder for an out, or falling safely for a hit. In [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters, fly balls appear as routine outs, dramatic moments, and — occasionally — as evidence of the baseball gods' injustice when a "cheap" fly drops for a hit against him.

## Overview

In the dead-ball era, the fly ball occupied a different place in baseball strategy than it does today. With home runs rare (the ball itself was softer and less lively), a fly ball was primarily an out — something pitchers sought and hitters tried to avoid. The ideal offensive approach emphasized line drives and ground balls that could find holes in the defense. A "pop fly" was a failure.

Yet fly balls had their own drama. A deep fly could advance a runner from third base (the [[sacrifice-hit|sacrifice fly]]). A fly ball lost in the sun could become a game-changing hit. And a well-struck fly to the outfield tested the fielders' range and the pitcher's nerves.

## In the Stories

### The Pitcher's Friend

For Jack, the fly ball is an ally. As a pitcher, he wants batters to hit the ball in the air — preferably to his outfielders. A ground ball might find a hole; a line drive might be too fast to catch. But a fly ball, properly hit, is almost always an out:

> "He hit a fly ball to the [[outfield|outfield]] and I knew it was an out the minute he swung." [paraphrased from game narrative]

The fly ball is the boring, reliable out — the kind of contact that keeps a pitcher's pitch count low and his confidence high.

### When Fly Balls Betray

But not all fly balls are created equal. Jack's letters record those moments when a routine fly becomes disaster — the outfielder who loses it in the sun, the "cheap" fly that drops between converging fielders, the wind-blown ball that carries over the fence for a rare home run.

These moments are, for Jack, evidence of cosmic injustice. He pitched well — the batter hit a mere fly ball — and yet the result is a hit, a run, a loss. The fly ball becomes a symbol of baseball's capriciousness.

### Pop Flies

The "pop fly" — a weakly hit ball that goes almost straight up — is a special category in Jack's taxonomy. A pop fly is an embarrassment for the hitter and a gift for the pitcher. Jack recounts pop flies with particular satisfaction, the easy out that proves the batter was overmatched.
