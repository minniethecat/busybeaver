**William "Kid" Gleason** (1866–1933) was a former pitcher (138 career wins) turned coach who served on the White Sox staff during Jack Keefe's tenure. In the letters, Gleason represents the voice of veteran baseball wisdom — practical, blunt, and occasionally amused by Jack's antics.

## In the Stories

Gleason appears as a source of instruction and observation. He gives Jack practical advice about equipment:

> "he says Wear your baseball shoes and if any of the girls says they like you tell them you was a soldier" (001-037)

And he provides the kind of hard-nosed assessment that Jack both needs and resents:

> "Gleason and he says I guess you will be a good pitcher if you live long enough" (001-085)

Gleason's presence in the White Sox organization bridges the playing and coaching staffs — he is one of the men who evaluates Jack's potential and delivers honest feedback.

## Historical Note

The historical Kid Gleason had a remarkable career: he won 138 games as a pitcher, then converted to second base and played another decade in the field. He later managed the 1919 White Sox, the "Black Sox" team — a tragic turn that came after the period of Jack's fictional letters.
