The New York Giants were one of the premier franchises of the National League during the era of [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters. Managed by the legendary John McGraw, the Giants appear in Jack's correspondence as a measuring stick for excellence — a team whose stature in the baseball world Jack both envies and resents.

## Overview

During the 1910s, the New York Giants were a dominant force in the National League, winning pennants in 1911, 1912, 1913, and 1917. Their home field, the Polo Grounds in upper Manhattan, was one of baseball's great cathedrals. For Jack Keefe, pitching in the [[american-league|American League]] with the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]], the Giants represented the other side of the baseball world — the National League, the big city, and a standard of excellence he measured himself against.

## In the Stories

### A Benchmark for Greatness

Jack's references to the Giants are few but telling. He invokes New York as a destination — "I says St. Louis and Boston and New York" [001-044] — during contract negotiations, naming the Giants' city alongside other major league towns. His manager's dismissive reply ("St. Louis and Boston and New York can have you") positions New York as the ultimate "big time" — a place a busher from the Coast League can only dream of.

### The Polo Grounds

The Giants' home ballpark, the [[polo-grounds|Polo Grounds]], was one of the most distinctive venues in baseball — a bathtub-shaped field with impossibly short foul lines and a vast center field. While Jack never describes pitching there in detail, the stadium looms in the background of any discussion of New York baseball.

### Cross-Town Parallel

The Giants served as a National League mirror to the [[chicago-cubs|Chicago Cubs]] — a dominant NL franchise in a major city, parallel to the Cubs' role in Chicago. Jack's confidence against the Cubs ("I can beat them Cubs if I get any kind of decent support" [002-135]) implies a similar — if untested — bravado about the Giants.
