A **bunt** is a batting technique in which the batter holds the bat loosely in the path of the ball rather than swinging, causing the ball to roll slowly into the infield. In Jack Keefe's era, the bunt was a central strategic element of "inside baseball" — the aggressive, tactical style of play that dominated the dead-ball era.

## Definition

The bunt is executed by sliding the top hand up the barrel of the bat and holding it horizontally over the plate. The goal is to deaden the ball so it rolls slowly along the infield grass, forcing fielders to charge in and make a difficult play. Successful bunts advance baserunners or allow the batter to reach base safely.

In the 1910s, bunting was far more common than in modern baseball. Teams built offensive strategies around the bunt, the hit-and-run, and the stolen base rather than waiting for extra-base hits.

## In the Stories

The bunt appears frequently in Jack's game accounts, often as a source of frustration. Opposing teams use it to exploit defensive weaknesses:

> "They started bunting in the fifth and Lord and his buntin was something awful" (001-126)

Jack criticizes his own fielding of bunts, quoting manager Callahan's assessment:

> "you don't go after bunts and you don't cover first base" (001-085)

The bunt also features in crucial game moments:

> "The next guy bunts and Chase tries to pull off the old play" (004-110)

Jack's ability to field bunts becomes a point of pride — and a managerial test:

> "They say Alcock is one of the best bunters in the league and Callahan tells me to watch for it" (004-104)

Ty Cobb himself uses the bunt against Jack:

> "Cobb bunts it right at me. I would of throwed him out but I had to wait for the ball to come down" (001-132)

## Historical Context

The bunt was a signature tactic of the dead-ball era (roughly 1900–1919). With the ball itself less lively and home runs rare, teams manufactured runs through bunting, stealing, and hit-and-run plays. The "scientific" or "inside" game prized execution of these small-ball tactics.

Managers like John McGraw of the New York Giants built dynasties on bunt-heavy offenses. The Chicago White Sox of Jack's acquaintance were practitioners of the same style, and Jack's struggles with bunt defense reflect the premium placed on pitchers who could field their position.
