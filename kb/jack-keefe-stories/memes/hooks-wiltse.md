Hooks Wiltse is a left-handed pitcher for the [[new-york-giants|New York Giants]] who crosses paths with [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe]] during the White Sox–Giants world tour of 1913–1914. A veteran with a reputation for cleverness on and off the field, Wiltse appears in Jack's letters as one of the more colorful members of the Giants contingent — an opponent who earns Jack's grudging respect through his pitching and his personality.

## Biography

George "Hooks" Wiltse pitched for the New York Giants from 1904 to 1914, winning 139 games under manager [[john-mcgraw|John McGraw]]. By the time of the world tour, he was near the end of his career, but his sharp tongue and competitive fire remained intact. The tour — an ambitious around-the-world exhibition that took both clubs across the United States, through Asia, and into Europe — threw the two teams into constant proximity, and Wiltse emerges from Jack's accounts as a distinctive presence in that traveling circus.

## In the Stories

### A Voice Across the Aisle

On the ocean voyage, the Giants players become part of the daily social landscape. Jack recounts a shipboard conversation: "I and Schaefer was talking together and he says Well may be this is the last time we will ever see the good old U.S." [006-051]. Wiltse is part of this floating community of ballplayers, two rival clubs thrown together on a long journey.

### Spring Training Competition

Back on American soil, the Giants and White Sox continue to cross paths during spring training. Jack reports on a series of exhibition games, noting the competitive edge between the two clubs. Wiltse, as a veteran Giants pitcher, represents the National League style — crafty, experienced, and not easily impressed by the young American League busher.

### The Joke Game

During the tour, the teams played "joke games" — exhibitions where the regulars took it easy and the atmosphere was loose. Jack reports: "they played an other joke game today and of coarse the giants had all the luck this time on acct of me not pitching" [011-065]. Wiltse, a veteran of many such exhibitions, would have been in his element — a pitcher who understood that not every game was a war.

### The Lucky Giants

After the tour, the White Sox players debate who was the luckiest participant. "Callahan says its the N.Y. giants because they was lucky to win all them penants but Evans says you cant call the giants lucky" [010-099]. Wiltse, who had been part of those pennant-winning Giants teams, embodies the success that Jack — whose White Sox had not yet won — envied.
