The **spitter** (or spitball) is an illegal pitch in which the pitcher applies saliva, petroleum jelly, or another substance to the ball, causing it to move unpredictably. In Jack Keefe's era, the spitter was technically legal until 1920, and it features prominently in his letters as his most effective — and most debated — secondary pitch.

## Definition

The spitter works by reducing friction on one side of the ball, creating asymmetric air resistance that makes the ball "knuckle" or drop sharply as it nears the plate. The pitch was notoriously difficult to control but equally difficult to hit when executed well. Pitchers would doctor the ball surreptitiously — licking fingers, touching the brim of a sweaty cap, or applying substances kept in the glove.

## In the Stories

Jack Keefe considers his spitter a key weapon and is frustrated when his catcher, Ray Schalk, won't call for it:

> "I would of handed him a spitter but Schalk kept signing for fast balls" (001-110)

> "I wanted to give him a spitter but Schalk signs me for the fast one" (001-127)

The tension between Jack's preference for the spitter and Schalk's game-calling is a recurring theme:

> "I had to tell Schalk not to ask me for my spitter much because my fast one looked pretty good" (002-093)

Jack also attributes the spitter's absence to his unsuccessful outings:

> "Schalk had of let me throw him spitters instead of fast ones. At that I got him in the ninth" (002-048)

The spitter appears in game action, including a memorable encounter with Ty Cobb:

> "I pitched a spitter and Cobb bunts it right at me" (001-132)

In spring training, Jack showcases his spitter to impress:

> "I used my fast ball most of the while but showed them a few spitters and they missed them a foot" (001-042)

## Historical Context

The spitball was legal in Major League Baseball until 1920, when it was banned in the wake of the Ray Chapman tragedy (Chapman was killed by a pitched ball in 1920, though the pitch that struck him was not a spitter). Seventeen established spitball pitchers were "grandfathered" and allowed to continue throwing the pitch for the remainder of their careers — the last legal spitball was thrown by Burleigh Grimes in 1934.

In Jack's era (1910s), the spitter was controversial but widely used. Pitchers like Ed Walsh built Hall of Fame careers around the pitch. Jack's eagerness to throw it reflects its effectiveness and the competitive culture of the dead-ball era.
