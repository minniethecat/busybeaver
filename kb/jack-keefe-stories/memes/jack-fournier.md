Jack Fournier is a first baseman for the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]] who appears in [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe]]'s letters as a member of the club's social circle — a dinner-table companion and card player who, along with [[ping-bodie|Ping Bodie]] and [[eddie-collins|Eddie Collins]], helped fill the long hours between games during the baseball season.

## Biography

Fournier played first base for the White Sox during the mid-1910s. Historically, Jack Fournier was a capable major league first baseman whose career extended into the 1920s; with the White Sox, he was part of a roster that included future Hall of Famers like Eddie Collins and Ray Schalk. In Jack Keefe's accounts, Fournier appears less as a player on the field and more as a presence in the team's off-field life — the restaurants, the card games, the endless conversations that knit a ball club together across a long season.

## In the Stories

### Part of the Inner Circle

Fournier is first mentioned alongside the regulars in the White Sox clubhouse as Jack surveys his new teammates:

> "I and Scott and Collins and Bodie and Fournier and Allen the young lefthander was all together at the same table." [001-043]

The grouping places Fournier at the center of team social life — sharing meals with the starting infield and the pitching staff.

### The Card Games

The White Sox passed their downtime with poker, and Fournier was a regular at the table. Kid Gleason, the coach, is unimpressed with the quality of play:

> "Gleason says I'm surprised at you guys playing poker with Weaver and Fournier. He says they can't even draw to two card flushes." [002-083]

The remark is affectionate ribbing — the kind of clubhouse needle that fills Jack's letters. A subsequent note adds that "Fournier and Weaver have strained their ankles running to the poker table" [002-084], a joke about their eagerness to gamble.

### A Betting Man

Fournier's confidence in his own hitting — or his willingness to wager on it — surfaces in a card-game exchange. When Jack boasts about his ability to beat a certain pitcher, Fournier is quick with a challenge:

> "Then Fournier says Oh we will get you a couple of runs and you can beat him." [002-085]

The banter captures the easy competitive spirit of the White Sox clubhouse.

### In the Field

Fournier's on-field presence is glimpsed only once in the surviving letters. On a sharply hit ball that might have been trouble for a lesser first baseman, Jack notes: "Fournier would of eat it up if it had been hit his way" [004-058]. The backhanded compliment suggests Fournier was regarded as a competent defender — the kind of player whose reliability was taken for granted.
