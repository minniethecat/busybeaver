The single is the most common and fundamental type of [[base-hit|base hit]] in baseball — a batted ball that allows the batter to reach first base safely without benefit of an [[error]] or fielder's choice. In [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] letters, singles appear as both routine events and — in key moments — as the building blocks of rallies that define games and seasons.

## Overview

In the dead-ball era of Jack Keefe's career (circa 1914–1919), the single was the dominant offensive weapon. Home runs were rare, and teams built their offensive strategy around "small ball" — advancing runners one base at a time through a sequence of singles, [[sacrifice-hit|sacrifices]], and [[stolen-base|stolen bases]]. Jack's letters, with their blow-by-blow accounts of games, reflect this strategic reality.

A single could be a sharp line drive, a ground ball that found a hole, or a well-placed bunt that the batter beat out. What mattered was reaching base — and Jack, as a pitcher, was acutely aware of every batter who managed it against him.

## In the Stories

### Anatomy of a Rally

Jack's game descriptions often turn on the humble single. A typical sequence unfolds: a walk, a stolen base, a single to drive in the run. The single is the payoff — the hit that converts base runners into runs:

> "They got a couple of singles and a walk and scored a run in the forth" [001-073]

The language is matter-of-fact, but the pattern is the engine of dead-ball offense. Singles accumulate, and Jack's success or failure as a pitcher is measured in how effectively he prevents them.

### Jack's Self-Assessment

As a pitcher, Jack evaluates himself largely by how many hits — especially singles — he allows. A good outing means scattering a few harmless singles; a bad one means "they was getting a single every time they come to bat." His characteristic bravado extends to dismissing the singles he does allow as flukes or the result of poor fielding.

### The Single in Game Narrative

Singles serve as the basic unit of game action in Jack's letters. When he recounts a game pitch by pitch, singles mark the turning points — the hit that starts a rally, the two-out single that drives in the winning run, the cheap single that Jack insists should have been an out.
