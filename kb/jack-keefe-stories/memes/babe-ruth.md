**George Herman "Babe" Ruth** (1895–1948) was the transformative figure of 20th-century baseball — first a dominant left-handed pitcher for the Boston Red Sox, then the game's greatest slugger for the New York Yankees. In Jack Keefe's letters, Ruth appears in his early career as a pitcher, and Jack's encounter with him is one of the most memorable moments in the collection.

## In the Stories

Jack's claim about Ruth is characteristic in its blend of pride and self-promotion:

> "struck him out twice and they claim he is a sucker for a left hander" (025-069)

The passage captures a transitional moment — Ruth the pitcher, not yet the Sultan of Swat, facing Jack Keefe in a matchup that both men would remember differently. Jack frames it as evidence of his own quality; history remembers Ruth rather differently.

## Historical Note

In 1915–1917, Ruth was primarily a pitcher, winning 67 games for the Red Sox before his conversion to full-time outfielder. Jack's encounter with Ruth-the-pitcher places the letters squarely in the 1915–1917 window, just before Ruth's transformation revolutionized the game. The dead-ball era Jack knew was about to end — and Ruth was the reason.
