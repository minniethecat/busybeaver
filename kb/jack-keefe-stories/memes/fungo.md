A **fungo** is a baseball practice drill in which a coach or player tosses a ball into the air and hits it to fielders for defensive practice. The term refers both to the act of hitting ("hitting fungos") and the specialized lightweight bat used ("fungo stick"). In Jack Keefe's letters, fungos are a recurring feature of pre-game warmups and training camp routines.

## Definition

A fungo bat is longer and lighter than a standard game bat, with a thinner barrel, designed to hit balls repeatedly without fatiguing the hitter. The hitter — typically a coach — tosses the ball up with one hand and strikes it with the other, directing it to specific fielders. The purpose is to give outfielders and infielders live practice reading balls off the bat and working on defensive positioning.

## In the Stories

Fungo hitting appears throughout Jack's letters as part of the daily rhythm of baseball life. In spring training, it's part of the warmup routine:

> "we tossed the ball round and hit fungos and run round" (001-035)

Jack uses fungo hitting as a metaphor for easy pitching to face, describing an opposing pitcher he dominates:

> "this bird was just like hitting fungos. His curve ball broke about ½ a inch" (004-102)

The fungo stick itself appears in a memorable image from spring training travel:

> "we had to drive it down again with a fungo stick because he didn't have no other kind of a bat" (009-097)

As Jack's career develops, fungo hitting becomes a marker of his evolving role. A fellow player is described dismissively as doing nothing but:

> "hit fungos once in a while or warm up when somebody is getting ready to go in" (014-028)

Jack's own wife, Florrie, humorously enters the fungo discourse:

> "her husband is batting fungos and getting splinters in his hand" (004-008)

## Historical Context

The fungo bat and fungo hitting have been part of baseball since the sport's earliest organized days in the mid-19th century. The origin of the word "fungo" is uncertain — theories trace it to the Scottish "fung" (to pitch or toss) or to a blend of "fun" and "go," reflecting the drill's purpose of giving fielders practice.

In Jack's era, pre-game routines were less structured than today's carefully timed batting practice sessions. Fungo hitting was a flexible, informal drill that could be done anywhere there was open grass — including the train-stop fields Jack describes.
