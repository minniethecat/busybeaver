The military draft of [[world-war-i|World War I]] was a transformative event in baseball history and in [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe's]] personal narrative. As the United States entered the war in 1917, major league players — like millions of other American men — faced the prospect of trading their baseball uniforms for military ones. Jack's letters trace his response to this upheaval, from initial nonchalance to resigned participation.

## Background

When the United States declared war on Germany in April 1917, Major League Baseball faced an existential question: was professional baseball an "essential" industry whose players should be exempt from military service, or would the game have to pause while its players fought? The "work or fight" order of 1918 resolved this ambiguity — baseball players, like other able-bodied men, were subject to the draft.

For the [[chicago-white-sox|Chicago White Sox]], the war meant the loss of key players. [[red-faber|Red Faber]], one of the team's best pitchers, enlisted in the Navy. Other players were drafted into the Army. Jack, characteristically, processes this disruption through the lens of his own career — what does the draft mean for his playing time, his salary, his standing on the team?

## The Event

### The Draft Looms

Jack's letters register the draft's approach with characteristic self-absorption. While other players worry about combat, Jack worries about his spot in the rotation. The departure of players like Faber creates opportunities — but also exposes Jack to new scrutiny. If he can't perform with the wartime roster, even the draft won't save his job.

### Military Service

Jack eventually finds himself in the [[us-army|U.S. Army]], where his experiences form the basis of several letters collected under titles like "The Busher Reenlists" and related war-themed stories. His military service is, predictably, narrated as a series of grievances: incompetent officers, unfair treatment, the indignity of taking orders from men who don't understand baseball.

> "I am in the army now Al and it is a fine life if you don't weaken." [from The Busher Reenlists period]

The draft transforms Jack from a baseball player into a soldier — temporarily — and the letters from this period offer a unique window into how the war intersected with the world of professional sports.

### The Baseball Draft

Jack also frets about the baseball draft — the system by which players were allocated to teams. The [[trade|trade]] and draft mechanisms of baseball's reserve system were themselves a source of anxiety: "They have got the draft now and I wisht they would draft me to some club that appreciates me" [025-013].

## Aftermath

The war ended in November 1918, and baseball returned to full strength in 1919. Players came back from service, and the 1919 season — which would end with the Black Sox scandal — began. For Jack, the war was an interlude: a period of disruption that, like everything else in his life, he survived mainly by complaining.

The draft's legacy in baseball was profound. The war-shortened 1918 season, the loss of players to military service, and the subsequent return to normalcy all shaped the game Jack returned to. And for Jack personally, military service added a new dimension to his identity — he was no longer just "the Busher" but also a veteran.
