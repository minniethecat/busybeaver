Little Al is the infant son of [[jack-keefe|Jack Keefe]] and [[florrie-keefe|Florrie Keefe]], born during the period covered by the later letters. His arrival marks one of the few genuine emotional arcs in Jack's correspondence — the transformation of the self-absorbed young busher into a father, however hapless, with a new set of worries and affections that compete with box scores and ERA for space in his letters.

## Biography

Little Al is born to Jack and Florrie Keefe sometime in the middle portion of the correspondence. Jack's letters, which for many chapters are devoted almost exclusively to baseball, begin to include domestic bulletins: the baby's health, his appearance, and Jack's own bewildered reactions to fatherhood. The name "Al" is presumably given in honor of someone — perhaps Al Blanchard, Jack's old friend from the minor leagues, though Jack never explicitly states the inspiration.

## In the Stories

### First Impressions

Jack's first description of his son is characteristically blunt and unvarnished:

> "I come home and everything was all OK. Little Al is a homely little skate but I guess all babies looks the same at first." [004-147]

The line is pure Jack Keefe — the affection is genuine but the phrasing is unsentimental, almost comic. The word "skate" (a dated slang term for a rascal or a cheap fellow) is applied to his own newborn son, a choice that captures both Jack's limited vocabulary for tenderness and the rough-and-tumble world he inhabits.

### The Cross-Eyed Rumor

One of the running jokes in the letters concerns a rumor that Little Al is cross-eyed. Jack is fiercely defensive about it:

> "He says Is that so? and he shut up. Little Al is not no more X-eyed than you are." [005-058]

Jack's retort is characteristically aggressive — the double negative ("not no more") underlining his indignation. That someone would tease Jack about his infant son and that Jack would report the exchange in a letter reveals how fatherhood has given the busher a new vulnerability, a new target for the clubhouse needling he usually dishes out himself.

### A New Set of Priorities

Though the references to Little Al are few, they signal a shift in Jack's emotional landscape. Where earlier letters are consumed entirely with salaries, rotation spots, and the opinions of managers, the later letters make room — however briefly — for the small, human reality of a baby at home. Little Al is the one character in the stories who depends on Jack Keefe, and the responsibility, however awkwardly Jack wears it, deepens the portrait of the busher beyond the diamond.
