**Walter "The Big Train" Johnson** (1887–1946) was arguably the greatest pitcher in baseball history, winning 417 games for the Washington Senators with a devastating fastball. In Jack Keefe's letters, Johnson is the benchmark — the pitcher against whom all others (including Jack himself) are measured.

## In the Stories

Jack references Johnson sparingly but significantly. The comparison is implicit in his boasts about his own fastball. When Jack reflects on being "whiffed" by an opposing pitcher, the name is invoked:

> "Johnson whiffs Weaver and Chase and makes Lord look like a sucker" (002-094)

Jack also identifies with Johnson's fastball style. When his own fastball is working, he feels kinship with the great man; when it's not, the distance between Bedford and Washington seems vast.

Jack's manager Callahan invokes Johnson as a standard:

> "he said a man that had Johnson's fast ball ought to of been a better pitcher than what he was" (025-069)

## Historical Note

Walter Johnson's career ERA of 2.17 and 3,508 strikeouts stood as records for decades. He pitched entirely for the Washington Senators (1907–1927), leading a perpetually mediocre team with transcendent individual brilliance. His fastball was so feared that, according to legend, batters would sometimes step out rather than face him.
