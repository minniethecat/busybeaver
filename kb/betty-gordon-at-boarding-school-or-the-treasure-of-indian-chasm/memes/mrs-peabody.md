# Mrs. Peabody

## Overview

**Mrs. Peabody** was a childhood friend of [[bob-henderson|Bob Henderson]]'s mother who, together with her husband, took [[bob-henderson|Bob]] into their farm home after his mother passed away. (002-013) Though [[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s time under the Peabody roof was marked by hard farm labor rather than familial warmth, Mrs. Peabody represented a crucial waypoint in [[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s journey — the last stop before he struck out on his own and eventually found his way to [[betty-gordon|Betty Gordon]] and [[littell-family|the Littells]].

## Role in the Story

### A Childhood Friend's Duty

When [[bob-henderson|Bob Henderson]] was orphaned, Mrs. Peabody — "a childhood friend of his mother's" who "had married a farmer" — agreed to take the boy in. (002-013) The arrangement was one of practical necessity rather than affection, and [[bob-henderson|Bob]] spent several months living and working on the Peabody farm.

### Hard Months at the Farm

[[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s experience with the Peabodys was far from idyllic. "The months she spent with the Peabodys" — the narrative occasionally shifts perspective here — were among the hardest of [[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s young life. (002-014) He labored as a farmhand, "more than earning his board," and received little in the way of comfort or encouragement. After leaving, [[bob-henderson|Bob]] "had not seen a book or a magazine since leaving the Peabody roof." (005-017)

### Mr. Peabody's Encounter

Mr. Peabody made a brief and memorable appearance when he encountered [[bob-henderson|Bob]] at a hotel. [[bob-henderson|Bob]] borrowed a dollar, and "[[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s dollar that Mr. Peabody paid him" became the legendary seed [[class-and-wealth|money]] for [[bob-henderson|Bob]]'s resourceful independence. (005-025) Later, [[louise-littell|Louise Littell]] recalled the scene: "Didn't we have a glorious time the day we saw Mr. Peabody tackle [[bob-henderson|Bob]] in the hotel?" (007-041) Mr. Peabody had demanded [[bob-henderson|Bob]] work for him another week to pay off a supposed debt — a claim that revealed the Peabodys' mercenary attitude toward the boy.

### A Remembered Saying

Despite the difficult circumstances, one of Mrs. Peabody's sayings stayed with [[bob-henderson|Bob]]. "Peabody used to call it" — [[bob-henderson|Bob]] recalled a phrase she employed, though the context was not fond. (017-007) The memory of life under the Peabody roof served primarily as a contrast to the warmth and generosity [[bob-henderson|Bob]] later found with the [[littell-family|Littells]] and at [[salsette-academy|Salsette Academy]].
