# Chapter 19: BOB'S SOLUTION

[019-001]"If you wanted twenty-five dollars and I had it," said [[betty-gordon|Betty]] persuasively, "I'd give it to you without asking a solitary question."

[019-002]Rob's lips twitched.

[019-003]"But, Betty--" he began. Then--"Oh, do play fair," he urged. "You're younger than I am. [[uncle-dick|Uncle Dick]] expects me to look after you. Goodness knows I don't want to pry into your affairs, but when you borrow fifteen dollars and then want twenty-five the same week, what's a fellow to think? If some one is borrowing from you, it's time to call a halt; you're not fair to yourself."

[019-004]Betty looked startled. How could [[bob-henderson|Bob]] possibly guess so near the truth?

[019-005]She began to think that the better part of wisdom was to confide in this keen young man.

[019-006]"Come on, Betty, tell me what you want it for, and you shall have twice twenty-five," said [[bobby-littell|Bob]] earnestly. "I've most of my allowance in the school bank. It's all yours, if you'll let me have an inkling of the reason you need money."

[019-007]"Well," said Betty, slowly, "I didn't promise I wouldn't tell--only that I wouldn't tell Bobby or Mrs. Eustice. It's [[libbie-littell|Libbie]] who has to have the money."

[019-008]She sketched Libbie's story for him rapidly, Bob listening in silence. At the end he asked a single question.

[019-009]"Have you any of those notes asking for money?"

[019-010]"Here's one." Betty thrust her hand into the pocket of her sweater and pulled out the crumpled paper that Libbie had shaken out of the bottle that morning.

[019-011]"Were they all written on this same kind of paper?" asked Bob, reading the note.

[019-012]"Ye-s, that is, I think so," hesitated Betty. "I really haven't noticed. Why?"

[019-013]"Because I don't think any man wrote this," announced Bob confidently.

[019-014]"I think some girl at school has done it, either as a joke or to torment Libbie."

[019-015]"But it's grown-up writing," protested Betty. "Though, come to think of it, we don't know any of the girls' handwriting," she added thoughtfully.

[019-016]"What girl would be likely to do it?" asked Bob. "Can you recall a practical joker? This is copy book paper torn from an ordinary theme book. Yes, I'll bet a cookie a girl wrote it."

[019-017]"Ada Nansen or Ruth Gladys Royal might do it to plague Libbie," said Betty slowly. "They don't like any of our crowd, and Libbie is so good at French she turns Ada green with envy. The more I think of it, the surer I am it is Ada. Ruth doesn't dislike any one actively enough to exert herself."

[019-018]"Ada Nansen?" repeated Bob. "Isn't she that girl we saw on the train and who plumped herself down in my seat? I thought so--I remember you told me. Well, from the sidelight I have on her character, I believe she is the one at the bottom of this. That will explain, too, why you never catch any one digging up the bottle--she knows exactly when you are busy and when you are not."

[019-019]"Bottle!" said Betty explosively, to Bob's amazement. "Oh, Bob! this morning Miss Jessup was talking to us about association of ideas, and she asked Ada what bee meant to her. We thought she'd say 'honey,' of course, but she said 'bottle.' Doesn't that show--"

[019-020]"I should say it did!" Bob's voice was eager. "She took it for the letter 'B' and bottle was in her mind. You may depend upon it, that girl is at the back of all this fuss! Gee, when I've nothing else to do, I'm going to study up on this association of ideas stuff."

[019-021]"You don't need it--you can get at things without a bit of trouble,"

[019-022]Betty assured him affectionately.

[019-023]"How will you go about pinning down Ada?" Bob asked anxiously.

[019-024]"I'll cut out Latin to-morrow afternoon when she has a study period,"

[019-025]planned Betty. "She'll think Libbie is reciting, and she'll not think of me at all, and I'll slip out and watch to see if she goes near the bottle. But what can I do if she does prove to be the right one? She'll tell Mrs. Eustice, and poor Libbie will be in a peck of trouble. I really think Mrs. Eustice would send her home if she knew."

[019-026]"And serve Libbie right for being such an idiot!" pronounced Bob severely. "However, I think she has been pretty thoroughly punished through fear. I only wish you'd told me this before, Betty, because I know exactly how you can deal with Ada."

[019-027]"You do? Oh, Bob, what should I ever do without you!" cried Betty, forgetting that a few moments before she had berated him for his insistence. "Tell me, quick."

[019-028]"Well, a crowd of us fellows happened to be over in [[edentown|Edentown]] last Friday night, and we saw Ada and Ruth at the movies," said Bob. "They didn't see us, for we sat back. They were the only girls from [[boarding-school-life|Shadyside]], and [[tommy-tucker|Tommy]] and I decided they had sneaked out after dinner and walked all that distance. Now threatening isn't a very nice performance, Betty, but sometimes you have to meet like with like. I think, if when you see Ada digging up the bottle, you go to her and say that unless she returns the money and Libbie's first note to you and promises to let the matter drop--forever--you will expose her Edentown trip to Mrs. Eustice, she will listen to reason."

[019-029]"So do I," agreed Betty. "I don't think she has touched the money--she has plenty. But I must have the note so that Libbie can destroy it. Mrs.

[019-030]Eustice never lets us go to town at night, and I'm sure Ada and Ruth had to go down the fire-escape. Goodness, didn't they take a chance of being discovered!"

[019-031]"Well, as I've already missed half an algebra recitation, and you know you have no business over here at this time of day, I move we begin our penance," suggested Bob. "Paddle home, Betsey, and if our hunch turns out wrong, we'll tackle another one."

[019-032]"Oh, it won't--I'm sure you're right," said Betty gratefully. "Thank you ever so much, Bob. And the next time I'll tell you everything at the very first."

[019-033]"Don't let me hear of another time," Bob called after her, with mock severity.

[019-034]"Well, I never!" gasped Libbie, astonished, when Betty told her of Bob's suspicions. "Oh, Betty, wouldn't it be wonderful if it should be true!"

[019-035]"I'm going to cut Latin this afternoon and find out," said Betty vigorously. "If Miss Sharpe asks for me, you don't know where I am; she never does anything but give you double lines to translate."

[019-036]Betty knew that Ada had a study period, which she usually spent in her room, directly after lunch.

[019-037]Directly after she left the dining room that noon Betty sped away to the foot of [[coasting-hill|the hill]]. There were several stubby bushes about half-filled with wind-blown leaves and old rubbish and affording an excellent screen.

[019-038]Betty crouched down behind one of these.

[019-039]She had not long to wait. Ada, in her beautiful mink furs, which she clung to persistently, though the fall weather so far had been very mild, was presently seen coming across the grass. She walked straight to the spot where the bottle was buried, and, stooping down, brushed away the leaves and dirt. She lifted the bottle.

[019-040]"Pshaw, it's empty!" she said aloud.

[019-041]"Yes, it's empty," echoed Betty, stepping out from behind the bush. "And you are to give the money back to me, and Libbie's note with it."

[019-042]"Is that so?" said Ada contemptuously. "I have something to say about that. I intend to see that that note reaches the proper person--Mrs. Eustice."

[019-043]Betty took a step nearer, her dark eyes blazing.

[019-044]"I can play the kind of game you play--if I must," she said in a curiously repressed tone. "What about the trip you and Ruth Gladys made to Edentown last Friday night?"

[019-045]Ada glared at her.

[019-046]"Were you there? How did you know?" she stammered jerkily. "If you were up to the same trick, you'll look nice tattle-telling on us, won't you?"

[019-047]"I wasn't there, but I have witnesses whom I can summon to say you were," declared Betty, wishing her voice did not tremble with nervousness. "You were the only girls from [[shadyside-school|Shadyside]], and you must have climbed down the fire--"

[019-048]Ada raised her hand that held the bottle.

[019-049]"You--you tell-tale!" she screamed threateningly.

[019-050]Betty flung up her arm to knock the bottle aside, missed Ada's hand and hit her shoulder. Ada went down, Betty on top of her.

[019-051]"Girls! For mercy's sake!" Miss Anderson stood beside them, scandalized.

[019-052]"Betty, get up. Ada, what are you thinking of? I saw you from the gym windows. You'll have the whole school out here presently. Betty, I thought you had Latin at this period?"

[019-053]"I have," admitted Betty, so meekly that Miss Anderson looked away lest she laugh. "Only I had to see Ada."

[019-054]"I don't know what you were quarreling about," said Miss Anderson, with characteristic frankness. "But I do know that both of you are old enough to know better than to revert to small-boy tactics. You've a hole in your stocking, Betty, that would do credit to a little brother."

[019-055]"I ripped it on that stone," said Betty regretfully.

[019-056]Ada stood sullenly, unconscious of two dead leaves hanging to her hat which completely destroyed her usual effect of studied elegance.

[019-057]"Go on in, Betty," said the physical culture teacher, who labored under no delusions about the duties of a peacemaker. To tell the truth, she did not believe in forced reconciliation. "Ada will come with me."

[019-058]"Ada has something I want," said Betty stubbornly. "She has to promise to give it to me first."

[019-059]Ada looked at the resolute little figure facing her. Betty, she knew, was capable of doing exactly what she had said. Mrs. Eustice had no more rigid rule than the one against going to town, day or night, without permission. Ada gave in.

[019-060]"I'll leave it in your room before dinner--you didn't think I carried it with me, did you?" she snapped.

[019-061]"Both?" said Betty significantly, meaning the note and the money.

[019-062]"Everything!" cried the exasperated Ada, on the verge of angry tears.

[019-063]"Then you have my promise never to say a word," Betty assured her blithely.

[019-064]"Do you want this bottle?" Miss Anderson called after her, as she started for the school.

[019-065]Miss Anderson had been studying both girls as she waited quietly.

[019-066]Now Betty turned, smiled radiantly, and took the bottle the teacher held out to her. With careful aim, worthy of Bob's training, she fixed her eye on a handy rock, hurled the bottle with all her strength, and had the satisfaction of seeing it dashed into a thousand fragments as it struck the target squarely.

[019-067]Then she trotted sedately on to her delayed recitation, and Miss Anderson and the scowling Ada followed more slowly.

[019-068]Just before dinner that night there came a knock on Betty's door, and Virgie Smith, one of Ada's friends, thrust a package at Bobby, who had answered the tap.

[019-069]Betty managed to turn aside her chum's curiosity and to get away to Libbie and give her the note. They burned it in the flame of a candle, and counted the money. It was all there, folded just as Libbie had placed it in the bottle. Evidently Ada had never carried it.

[019-070]Libbie paid Louise the money she had borrowed of her and gave Betty the amount she owed her, most of which was Bob's.

[019-071]"Now do try to be more sensible, Libbie," pleaded Betty, turning to go back to Bobby. "When you want to do something romantic think twice and count a hundred."

[019-072]"I will!" promised Libbie fervently. "I'll never be so silly again, Betty."

[019-073]But dear me, she was, a hundred times! But in a different way each time.

[019-074]Libbie would be Libbie to the end of the chapter.

[019-075]Betty, rushing back to brush her hair for dinner, heard a sound suspiciously like a sob as she passed [[norma-guerin|Norma Guerin]]'s door. It was unlatched, and as no one answered when she tapped Betty gently pushed it open and stepped into the room.

[019-076]Norma lay on her bed crying as though her heart would break, and [[alice-guerin|Alice]], looking very forlorn and solemn, was holding a letter in her hand.
