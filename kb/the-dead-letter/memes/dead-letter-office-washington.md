# Dead Letter Office, Washington

The Dead Letter Office in Washington, D.C., is the institutional setting where [[richard-redfield|Richard Redfield]] spends eighteen years as a clerk and where the novel's pivotal discovery occurs. As a real 19th-century institution, it grounds the novel's more sensational elements in bureaucratic reality.

## Description

The Dead Letter Office was the repository for [[dead-letter|undeliverable mail]] in the United States. Clerks opened [[the-letter-as-device|letter]]s in hopes of identifying senders or recipients; those that could not be resolved were eventually destroyed. [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] describes the office's routine — the daily processing of abandoned correspondence — as monotonous but, in this instance, [[fate-and-providence|fate]]ful.

## Significance

The Dead Letter Office represents the intersection of bureaucracy and [[fate-and-providence|providence]]. That the crucial [[the-letter-as-device|letter]] should arrive at [[richard-redfield|Redfield]]'s desk — out of thousands processed annually — is the novel's central act of narrative [[fate-and-providence|fate]]. The office also symbolizes the theme of failed communication that [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] mystery works to redeem: a place where messages go to die becomes the source of the message that brings the truth to life.
