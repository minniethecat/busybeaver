# Richard Redfield

Richard Redfield is the narrator and protagonist of *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]*. The novel is his [[memory-and-the-past|retrospect]]ive account — told years after the events — of how a dead [[the-letter-as-device|letter]] that passed through his hands at the [[dead-letter-office-washington|Dead Letter Office]] led to the resolution of a murder that had haunted him since his youth. He is at once participant and observer, emotionally entangled in the mystery and professionally positioned to investigate it.

## Role Overview

Redfield occupies a distinctive position in early detective fiction: he is neither the professional detective (that role belongs to [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]]) nor a detached chronicler (like Watson) but an active investigator in his own right. His employment at the [[dead-letter-office-washington|Dead Letter Office]] gives him institutional access to [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]]; his personal connection to the victim and the victim's fiancée gives him emotional motivation. [[014-001]]

His narrative voice is [[memory-and-the-past|retrospect]]ive — the older Redfield looks back on his younger self with a mixture of tenderness and ironic detachment. This temporal distance creates a dual perspective: the reader experiences the events both as immediate and as mediated.

The immediacy comes through the vividness of Redfield's [[memory-and-the-past|recollection]]; the mediation through the wisdom of hindsight. This technique, unusual for detective fiction of the 1860s, gives the novel a psychological depth that anticipates later developments in the genre.

## Background and Identity

Redfield is a young man of professional aspirations whose life is disrupted by [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]. Before [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]], he was on the verge of a partnership with [[james-argyll|Mr. Argyll]] and, implicitly, marriage to [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor Argyll]]. After [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] — and after being "driven from the friendship and presence of the [[james-argyll|Argyll]]s" — he relocates to Washington, where his uncle secures him a position at the [[dead-letter-office-washington|Dead Letter Office]] [[014-002]].

His eighteen-year tenure at the Department represents a social demotion: from prospective business partner to government clerk. The novel is frank about this decline, treating it not as a chosen path but as a consequence of circumstances beyond Redfield's control. He describes himself as having become "bitter, sneering, skeptical" during this period [[014-002]].

The [[dead-letter-office-washington|Dead Letter Office]] itself becomes a metaphor for Redfield's condition — a repository for messages that never reached their destination, much as his own life has become defined by an interrupted trajectory. His daily work of sorting [[dead-letter|undeliverable mail]] is an exercise in futility that mirrors his emotional stagnation.

## Character and Motivation

Redfield's defining trait is his concealed love for [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor Argyll]] — a feeling he harbors "secretly, in silence and regret, against all hope and reason" [[002-030]]. This unspoken love is the emotional engine of the novel: it drives his investigation, colors his perceptions, and creates a tension between his public role as impartial seeker of [[justice-and-retribution|justice]] and his private identity as a man still in love with the victim's fiancée.

He is morally self-aware, capable of recognizing his own flaws. He admits to "folly" and to the hardening of his nature. Yet he is also persistent — the investigation spans years, and Redfield never abandons it. His patience is rewarded when the dead [[the-letter-as-device|letter]] arrives, and he immediately recognizes its significance.

### The Burden of Unspoken Love

Redfield's silence about his feelings for [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]] is not merely a matter of propriety — it is a structural constraint of the narrative. Because [[henry-moreland|Moreland]] was his friend and [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]] his friend's betrothed, Redfield cannot declare himself. His love must remain hidden, and this concealment generates much of the novel's emotional tension. When he watches [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]] grieve for [[henry-moreland|Moreland]], he suffers doubly: for her pain and for the impossibility of comforting her as he wishes.

> "I pitied her from my soul, although my pity was mixed with envy as I thought of the dead." [[002-031]]

This admission — that his compassion is tainted by jealousy — is [[honor-and-reputation|character]]istic of Redfield's honesty as a narrator. He does not present himself as noble; he presents himself as human.

## Key Plot Events

1. **The night of [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]]** (October 17, 1857): Redfield is at [[moreland-villa|Moreland Villa]] when [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] fails to return. He participates in the search and is present when the body is discovered. [[002-064]]
2. **Banishment from the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] circle**: After circumstances turn against him, [[redfields-banishment|Redfield leaves]] [[blankville|Blankville]] and returns to his mother. [[014-002]]
3. **Employment at the [[dead-letter-office-washington|Dead Letter Office]]**: He becomes a clerk in Washington, where he will remain for eighteen years. [[014-016]]
4. **Discovery of the dead [[the-letter-as-device|letter]]**: A time-stained epistle arrives in the ordinary course of business, and Redfield immediately recognizes its connection to [[murder-of-henry-moreland|Moreland's murder]]. [[014-001]]
5. **Investigation with [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]]**: Redfield partners with [[mr-burton|the detective]], participating in the mesmeric sittings with [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]] and the pursuit of [[george-thorley|George Thorley]]. [[022-015]]

### The Redfield–Burton Partnership

The dynamic between Redfield and [[mr-burton|Burton]] is one of the novel's most carefully drawn relationships. [[mr-burton|Burton]] is the older, more experienced man — a professional who has seen "more of the world" than Redfield. Redfield is at once [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s pupil and his collaborator. He observes [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s methods, absorbs his philosophy of [[criminal-detection|detection]], and gradually develops his own investigative instincts.

> "Mr. Burton had a theory that every crime left its mark somewhere — that Providence itself kept a record, if men could only find it." [[005-022]]

This passage reveals both [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s investigative philosophy and Redfield's sympathetic reception of it. The two men share a worldview in which truth is ultimately accessible to patient inquiry — a worldview that the novel's plot vindicates.

## Narrative Significance

Redfield is more than the novel's protagonist; he is its organizing consciousness. Every event is filtered through his perception, every [[honor-and-reputation|character]] seen through his eyes. This first-person [[memory-and-the-past|narration]] creates an epistemological frame: the reader knows only what Redfield knows, suspects only what Redfield suspects. The gradual revelation of truth mirrors his own gradual understanding.

His position as narrator also allows the novel to explore the psychology of investigation. Redfield does not simply report facts; he reflects on their meaning, weighs their implications, and shares his doubts. This interiority — the sense of a mind at work — distinguishes *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]* from the more plot-driven detective fiction that would follow.

## Character Arc

Redfield moves from youthful hopefulness through bitterness and isolation to a hard-won resolution. The novel's [[memory-and-the-past|retrospective narration]] — told by an older Redfield looking back — suggests that the events he recounts, painful as they were, ultimately led him to a more mature understanding of himself and others. The investigation that begins as a quest for [[justice-and-retribution|justice]] becomes also a process of personal healing.
