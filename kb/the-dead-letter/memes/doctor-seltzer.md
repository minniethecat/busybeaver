# Doctor Seltzer

Doctor Seltzer is the false identity assumed by [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] after fleeing the United States in the aftermath of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]'s murder. Under this alias, [[george-thorley|Thorley]] — a self-taught former apothecary from [[blankville|Blankville]] — passed himself off as a physician, won the confidence of the wealthy Mexican landowner [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]], and married his beautiful daughter. [[018-024]]

He is the hidden antagonist of *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]* — [[george-thorley|the murderer]] [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]] has pursued for two years across a continent. His [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]] is confirmed not by arrest but by his own written confession and subsequent flight. [[018-025]]

## 角色概述

Doctor Seltzer represents the novel's most sophisticated treatment of criminal concealment. He appears not as a suspect but as a figure whose life has been rebuilt upon the ruin of another — [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] — through medical imposture, social manipulation, and sheer audacity. [[018-025]]

His identity as [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] — the [[blankville|Blankville]] apothecary who murdered [[henry-moreland|Moreland]] for two thousand dollars — is known only to [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]] and to the reader who follows [[mr-burton|the detective]]'s reasoning.

In the architecture of the plot, Seltzer/[[george-thorley|Thorley]] is the absent center: his existence is logically necessary from the first chapter, but he materializes as a named antagonist only in the novel's penultimate movement. [[018-025]]

When [[mr-burton|Burton]] finally confronts him on the mountainside above [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s hacienda, Seltzer's first instinct — attempted murder — reveals the depth of his criminal [[honor-and-reputation|character]]. His second instinct — a forced, written confession — provides the narrative with its climactic evidentiary resolution. [[019-031]]

## 背景与身份

Doctor Seltzer's background is the background of [[george-thorley|George Thorley]]. As [[richard-redfield|Richard Redfield]] recalls, [[george-thorley|Thorley]] "used to have an apothecary shop in the lower part of [[blankville|the village]]" of [[blankville|Blankville]], and fled "some three years ago, to escape the talk occasioned by a suspicious case of malpractice." [[016-032]] [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] notes that [[george-thorley|Thorley]] "had a face which made people look twice at him."

[[leesy-sullivan|Leesy Sullivan]] provides the fullest account of [[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s origins. According to her [[proof-and-evidence|testimony]], [[george-thorley|Thorley]] was an orphan of English extraction who obtained a menial position in a doctor's office — "one of those humble situations where he was expected to take care of the physician's horse, drive the carriage, put up medicines, attend upon orders." [[016-037]]

During idle hours behind the counter, he studied the doctor's medical books and observed patient consultations, "picking up a really surprising smattering of science." He attended lectures on surgery in [[new-york|New York]] and spent time in a hospital — enough to give him the veneer of medical competence. With energy and obscure capital, he established his apothecary shop in [[blankville|Blankville]].

The scandal that drove him from [[blankville|Blankville]] involved "causing the [[death-and-mourning|death]] of a person by malpractice." [[016-042]] Fleeing to [[new-york|New York]], [[george-thorley|Thorley]] married Nora Sullivan — [[leesy-sullivan|Leesy]]'s cousin — but abandoned her within months, leaving her pregnant and destitute. Nora died shortly after childbirth.

By the time [[george-thorley|Thorley]] boarded a steamer for [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], he had already committed [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]], stabbed for two thousand dollars on October 17, 1857. [[019-033]]

His transformation into "Doctor Seltzer" occurred through happenstance and opportunism. When the steamer stopped at [[acapulco|Acapulco]], a street brawl broke out among rough [[new-york|New York]] miners, during which [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] — a wealthy hacienda owner — was stabbed. [[george-thorley|Thorley]] dressed the wound competently, impressing the grateful Don. [[018-024]]

[[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] "conceived a warm liking for the young man" and offered him the position of physician on his vast estates, with the promise of benefits "much more important than his salary." [[018-024]]

Within a year, [[george-thorley|Thorley]] had married [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s only daughter and taken charge of the silver-mining operations, introducing machinery he had partially invented. [[019-021]]

## 性格与动机

Seltzer/[[george-thorley|Thorley]] is [[honor-and-reputation|character]]ized through morally damning traits: jealousy, cruelty, a violent temper, and fundamental dishonesty. [[leesy-sullivan|Leesy Sullivan]] describes him as possessing "a quick temper" and "a jealous disposition," adding that "there was something dark and hidden in the workings of his mind, from which I shrunk." [[016-038]]

[[leesy-sullivan|Leesy]] knew him to be cruel, noting "I could see it in his manner of treating children and animals; there was nothing he liked so well as to practice his half-learned art of surgery upon some unfortunate sufferer." [[016-038]]

The novel's physical description reinforces this moral portrait. When Seltzer first encounters [[mr-burton|Burton]] at [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s supper-room, the narrator observes that "Dr. Seltzer might have been good-looking had his eyes not possessed the shifting, uncertain glance that plays before a soul which dares not frankly meet its fellows, and had not an evil expression predominated on his features." [[019-015]]

His face "was one which would have been distrusted in any intelligent company of our own people." However, among the Spaniards, "accustomed to treachery and untruth," he passed without [[secrecy-and-suspicion|suspicion]]. "A Spaniard he was at heart, and he had found, in his present life, a congenial sphere." [[019-015]]

Seltzer's core motivation is survival through concealment. Every action — from dressing [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s wound to marrying his daughter — serves the purpose of burying his identity as [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] beneath the respectability of Doctor Seltzer.

When [[mr-burton|Burton]] appears, survival dictates murder. Seltzer recognizes [[mr-burton|the detective]] "within five minutes after sitting down to the supper-table" — having once seen [[mr-burton|Burton]] in a [[new-york|New York]] courtroom. [[019-059]] He attempts murder twice: first by entering [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s bedroom at night, then by attempting to hurl him into a mountain ravine. [[019-028]]

His motivation for the original murder is purely pecuniary: he killed [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] for two thousand dollars, acting as a hired assassin.

[[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] later reflected that if [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] had been committed from passion, "he would not have thought so badly of the young man; but that it should have been done for *money* was to him an irreparable crime and disgrace." [[020-002]]

## 关键情节线

1. **The [[blankville|Blankville]] apothecary (pre-narrative).** [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] establishes a small apothecary shop in [[blankville|Blankville]], courts [[leesy-sullivan|Leesy Sullivan]] obsessively, and is suspected of causing a [[death-and-mourning|death]] through malpractice. [[016-032]] [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] recalls seeing him about [[blankville|the village]] — "he had a face which made people look twice at him." [[016-032]]

2. **Marriage to Nora Sullivan and abandonment (pre-narrative).** After being rejected by [[leesy-sullivan|Leesy]], [[george-thorley|Thorley]] marries her cousin Nora to provoke jealousy, then abandons the pregnant Nora within months. Nora dies shortly after childbirth, leaving her infant daughter to [[leesy-sullivan|Leesy]]'s care. [[016-042]] [[016-045]]

3. **The murder of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] (October 17, 1857).** [[george-thorley|Thorley]] murders [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] for two thousand dollars, paid by an accomplice. The crime is committed with a surgical instrument bearing [[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s initials, recovered from the victim's body. [[019-033]]

4. **Flight and transformation into Doctor Seltzer.** Boarding a steamer for [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] under an assumed name, [[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s [[fate-and-providence|fortune]]s turn at [[acapulco|Acapulco]], where he dresses [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s stab wound and is offered a position as the hacienda's physician. [[018-024]] He registers on the passenger-list as "Mr." rather than "Dr." — a detail [[mr-burton|Burton]] later identifies as suspicious. [[018-025]]

5. **Life at [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s hacienda.** Over two years, Seltzer ingratiates himself into [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s family, marries the Donna, and manages the silver mines. He introduces machinery and improvements that enrich his patron. [[019-021]] The Mexican passenger who first told his story remarked that "he had to me the air of an adventurer." [[018-024]]

6. **[[mr-burton|Burton]]'s arrival and Seltzer's recognition.** When [[mr-burton|Burton]] arrives at the hacienda posing as a scientific explorer, Seltzer recognizes him immediately. His "slight start of surprise" reveals "the first impulse of an ever-ready apprehension — an alarm, covered instantly by a false warmth of manner." [[019-013]] [[mr-burton|Burton]] inwardly cries: "*This is the man!*" [[019-013]]

7. **Attempted murder on the mountainside.** On a narrow mountain path above a torrent, Seltzer raises a Spanish dirk to strike [[mr-burton|Burton]] from behind. [[mr-burton|Burton]] turns and seizes Seltzer's arm "in a clasp of steel." [[019-028]] Seltzer makes "desperate efforts to hurl his antagonist into the torrent." [[019-029]] [[mr-burton|Burton]] disarms him.

8. **The forced confession.** At gunpoint, Seltzer sits on a stone and writes his confession, signed with his real name. [[019-055]] [[mr-burton|Burton]] confirms the confession matches the facts he had already deduced.

9. **Exposure, flight, and theft.** Returning from the mountain, Seltzer "suddenly whirled his horse and set off toward [[acapulco|Acapulco]], at his utmost speed." [[020-001]] [[mr-burton|Burton]] informs [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]. During the Don's absence, Seltzer returns and steals everything of value, including the Donna's jewels, then flees with his wife "to some unknown region." [[020-003]]

10. **The poisoned oranges.** While [[mr-burton|Burton]] waits in [[acapulco|Acapulco]] for a steamer home, an old Indian woman attempts to sell him poisoned oranges, which [[mr-burton|Burton]] attributes to Seltzer's vengeance. When [[mr-burton|Burton]] feeds a piece to a stray pig, the animal dies. [[020-004]]

## 人物弧光

Seltzer/[[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s arc is one of ascent built on concealment, followed by catastrophic exposure. He begins in [[blankville|Blankville]] as a small-town apothecary with pretensions to medical science — a man whose ambition exceeds his moral constraints. The murder of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] marks his crossing of an irreversible threshold: from shady [[honor-and-reputation|character]] to killer.

His transformation into Doctor Seltzer represents a kind of diabolical resurrection. In Mexico, [[george-thorley|Thorley]] ceases to exist; Seltzer takes his place, enjoying wealth, professional respect, and the love of a beautiful wife.

The life he builds at [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s hacienda is a material success. Under his management, "[[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] was growing a rich man faster than he previously had any idea was possible." [[019-021]] This competence sharpens the moral indictment: he is not a bumbling villain but a capable one, whose evil is the more dangerous because his talents are real.

The arc resolves in exposure and flight. Stripped of his alias, Seltzer becomes again [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] — a murderer who steals his wife's jewels and vanishes "to some unknown region." [[020-003]]

The novel denies him either punishment or redemption; he simply disappears from the narrative. This open-endedness is thematically significant: in a world of imperfect [[justice-and-retribution|justice]], some criminals escape the scaffold — though not the moral accounting [[mr-burton|the detective]]'s work has rendered.
