# Park Bank

Park Bank is a financial institution in [[new-york|New York]] that becomes a significant site in the investigation of [[henry-moreland]]'s murder. It is where the stolen money — taken from [[henry-moreland|Moreland]]'s person on the night of his [[death-and-mourning|death]] — was deposited, and where [[mr-burton]] and [[richard-redfield]] trace the distinctive large bill that becomes a crucial piece of [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]].

## Overview

Park Bank appears in the novel as a node in the financial trail that [[mr-burton|Burton]] follows. The murder of [[henry-moreland]] involved not only the taking of a life but the theft of money — a detail that transforms the crime from a purely personal act into one with a traceable financial dimension. [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] records his anxiety "to give notice of the robbery at the bank, and to ascertain if they could identify any of the money, especially the large bill, which, being new, I hoped they would have on record" [[005-022]].

The bank represents the institutional infrastructure that makes [[criminal-detection|detection]] possible. In an era before forensic science, financial records — deposit ledgers, bill tracking, banking hours — provide the hard [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] that [[proof-and-evidence|witness]] [[proof-and-evidence|testimony]] and [[mesmeric-clairvoyance|mesmeric trance]] cannot supply.

## Institutional Background

As a 19th-century American bank, Park Bank would have operated under the banking regulations of the period — a time when state-chartered banks issued their own notes and record-keeping was manual but systematic. The "large bill, which, being new" that [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] hopes the bank can identify [[005-022]] reflects the material reality of 19th-century currency: distinctive bills could be traced through the institutions that handled them.

## Role in the Investigation

Park Bank serves the investigation in two ways:

1. **Evidence of the robbery**: The bank's records confirm that money was taken from [[henry-moreland|Moreland]], establishing that the crime involved theft as well as murder — a detail that narrows the range of possible motives.

2. **Traceable currency**: The distinctive large bill deposited at the bank becomes a trail that [[mr-burton|Burton]] follows. The bill's movement through financial channels provides a parallel track of [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] to the psychological and mesmeric dimensions of the investigation.

## Narrative Function

Park Bank serves as a narrative anchor for the novel's [[betrothal-and-marriage|engagement]] with material [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]]. In a story where [[mesmeric-clairvoyance]] provides paranormal insight and psychological perception reads hidden emotions, the bank offers something different: the hard, impersonal [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] of financial records.

This duality — mesmeric vision alongside bank ledgers — is [[honor-and-reputation|character]]istic of *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]*'s transitional position in the history of detective fiction. The novel does not choose between rational and supernatural [[criminal-detection|detection]]; it uses both, assigning each to its appropriate domain.
