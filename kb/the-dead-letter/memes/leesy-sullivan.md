# Leesy Sullivan

Leesy Sullivan is an Irish orphan seamstress whose hidden, consuming love for [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] places her at the center of [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] investigation. Initially the prime suspect, she is ultimately exonerated by [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]].

[[mr-burton|Burton]] recognizes her as an innocent woman whose tragic proximity to three dangerous men — [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]]ed [[henry-moreland|Moreland]], the stalker [[george-thorley|George Thorley]], and the investigator [[richard-redfield|Richard Redfield]] — has left her hunted, heartbroken, and dying of consumption. Her [[proof-and-evidence|testimony]] in Chapter 16 provides the crucial name that unlocks the entire mystery.

## 角色概述

Leesy Sullivan functions in the novel as a red herring whose conspicuous presence near the crime scene draws [[secrecy-and-suspicion|suspicion]] away from the true perpetrator. She is introduced in Chapter 4 when [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] and an officer trace her to her aunt's tenement in [[blankville|Blankville]] [[004-001]]--[[004-007]].

Suspicion falls on her swiftly: as a poor, working-[[social-class-and-status|class]] Irish woman, she embodies the [[social-class-and-status|class]] dynamics of 19th-century [[justice-and-retribution|justice]]. [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]], however, sees through the [[proof-and-evidence|circumstantial evidence]]. He recognizes her as a woman whose emotional intensity has made her a victim rather than a criminal [[013-004]].

## 背景与身份

Leesy is an orphan of Irish descent, taken in by her aunt Mrs. Sullivan at the age of five and raised in [[blankville|Blankville]]'s working-[[social-class-and-status|class]] quarter near the railroad depot [[004-011]]. Her aunt gave her an education beyond her station: "She can read and write like the ladies of the land" [[004-011]].

She became a skilled seamstress, employed summers by the [[henry-moreland|Moreland]] family for six weeks each year, where her quickness and neatness with the needle earned their satisfaction [[004-018]]. Her speech and manner, as [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] notes, "was not that of her station." She possessed an aspiring, ambitious mind that reached beyond her circumstances [[004-051]].

In the year before [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]], she left [[blankville|Blankville]] for [[new-york|New York City]], taking employment at a fancy-goods store on Broadway at four dollars a week [[004-013]]. Her aunt attributed this move to a change in her disposition: "There was something come across her — she appeared homesick like, and strange" [[004-020]].

In truth, her departure was driven by her hopeless love for [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]] — a "wild, mad, but sinless infatuation" that she later confessed to [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]] [[016-041]].

## 性格与动机

Leesy is defined by an almost terrifying capacity for passionate devotion. [[richard-redfield|Redfield]], observing her unseen at [[moreland-villa|Moreland Villa]], describes "a face made to express passion" with a low but fair forehead, heavy dark brows, and an oval cheek and chin. The face conveyed despair so intense it "grew like apathy" [[004-041]].

Her nature combined pride, ambition, and a fierce independence. She held herself aloof from suitors of her own [[social-class-and-status|class]] because "I felt that I had thoughts and tastes they could not understand — that my life was above theirs in hope, in aspiration" [[016-037]].

The core motivation driving her actions is a selfless, absolute love for [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]. Unlike the calculating passions of the novel's actual villains, Leesy's love is entirely self-abnegating.

As she tells [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]]: "God is love. When I stand in his presence, I shall glory in that power of love, which in this bleak world has only fretted and wasted my life" [[016-041]]. This capacity for devotion makes her vulnerable both to [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]'s kindness and to [[george-thorley|George Thorley]]'s predatory obsession.

## 关键情节线

**The Night of the Murder:** Leesy arrives in [[blankville|Blankville]] on the Saturday morning train, the same day as [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]'s murder, but does not go directly to her aunt's. She sits alone in the [[henry-moreland|Moreland]] summerhouse until the rain begins [[004-065]].

Her unexplained absence from three o'clock until after [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]], combined with her late-night visit to the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] parlor window — [[proof-and-evidence|witness]]ed by [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] — makes her the investigation's first suspect [[004-033]], [[004-045]].

**The First Suspicion:** [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] and an officer visit Mrs. Sullivan, posing as customers seeking shirt-making. They learn of Leesy's connection to the [[henry-moreland|Moreland]]s and her extreme reaction to [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] news — "she gave a scream and dropped right down" [[004-020]]. [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] dispatches an officer to [[new-york|New York]] to put a detective "on the track of Leesy Sullivan" [[004-033]].

Later that day, [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] encounters her at [[moreland-villa|Moreland Villa]], where he studies her unseen for half an hour, [[proof-and-evidence|witness]]ing "blank despair" in her expression [[004-040]]. When confronted, she is defiant: "I tell you the sorrow of that beautiful lady he was to have married is light compared with trouble that some feel" [[004-053]].

**The Handkerchief Evidence:** While searching the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] grounds, [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] discovers a woman's handkerchief embroidered with the initials "L.S." and a delicate spray of flowers, beaten into the grass near the bay-window by the rain [[006-045]]--[[006-046]]. [[mr-burton|Burton]] notes the [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] suggests an accomplice rather than a lone actor [[006-051]]--[[006-052]].

**Flight and the Haunted Villa:** Frightened by the investigation, Leesy flees with her infant cousin Nora, eventually taking refuge in the vacant [[moreland-villa|Moreland Villa]]. She lives secretly in the attic for weeks, surviving on provisions left in the house and venturing out only by night [[013-022]]. Her candlelight, visible through the skylight, gives rise to rumors that [[moreland-villa|the villa]] is haunted [[012-024]].

Jim, the [[james-argyll|Argyll]]s' manservant, reports seeing a ghostly woman near the bay-window on the night of [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] — a "wraith" with "very large, bright eyes, which shone when the candle threw the light on them, as if they had been made of fire" [[006-063]].

**Discovery and Interrogation:** [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] and [[mr-burton|Burton]] stake out [[moreland-villa|the villa]] on a stormy night. They [[proof-and-evidence|witness]] Leesy standing before [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]'s portrait, holding a candle, finally sobbing a single word: "[[henry-moreland|Henry]]!" [[012-047]]--[[012-048]]. When discovered, she confronts them with fierce dignity [[012-053]].

After a three-hour private interview, [[mr-burton|Burton]] emerges convinced of her innocence: "I am perfectly, entirely convinced that she is innocent of any participation in [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]" [[013-031]].

**The Revelation:** In her final appearance, Leesy — now visibly dying of consumption, with hollow cheeks "aflame with the fire of fever" and eyes that "almost blazed with light" [[016-002]] — reluctantly provides the name that solves the mystery: [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] [[016-029]].

She reveals [[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s obsessive pursuit of her, his marriage to her cousin Nora out of jealousy, his abandonment of his pregnant wife, and his threatening reappearances demanding she marry him [[016-037]]--[[016-052]]. Her description matches perfectly the profile [[mr-burton|Burton]] had constructed from the [[dead-letter]]'s handwriting [[016-058]].

**Death and Legacy:** By the novel's end, Leesy has died of consumption. [[mr-burton|Burton]] reports that she "was living quietly in the city, subsisting mostly upon donations from himself, she being too far gone with consumption to exert herself much with the needle" [[014-033]]. Her adopted daughter Nora survives her, with [[mr-burton|Burton]] promising to protect the child [[016-059]].

## 人物弧光

Leesy's arc traces a tragic parabola from obscure seamstress to hunted fugitive to vindicated [[proof-and-evidence|witness]]. She begins as an ambitious young woman of the working [[social-class-and-status|class]], proud of her skill and her education, nursing a secret love for a man far above her station.

The murder transforms her into public enemy — suspected, pursued, driven into hiding in the very house where her beloved had planned to make his home. Her lowest point comes as she haunts the deserted villa, a living ghost standing before [[henry-moreland|Henry]]'s portrait in the dead of night.

[[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]]'s intervention reverses her trajectory: his recognition of her innocence grants her, for the first time, a protector rather than a persecutor. In her final scenes, she moves from defiant silence to reluctant, complete confession, providing the [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] that will bring the true murderer to [[justice-and-retribution|justice]].

She dies in obscurity but not in disgrace — her devotion to [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]], however hopeless, is ultimately vindicated as "sinless" and her role in the plot redeemed from suspect to key [[proof-and-evidence|witness]] [[016-041]].

## 叙述学注记

Leesy Sullivan is one of the novel's most fully realized minor [[honor-and-reputation|character]]s. Her passionate interior life, conveyed through [[richard-redfield|Redfield]]'s external observation and two extended confessional monologues (Chapters 13 and 16), rivals the psychological depth of the protagonists.

Her story functions as a dark mirror to [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor Argyll]]'s: both women loved [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]], both were devastated by his [[death-and-mourning|death]], but their [[social-class-and-status|class]] positions determine entirely different relationships to [[justice-and-retribution|justice]]. [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]] is sheltered by wealth and [[honor-and-reputation|social standing]]; Leesy is exposed, suspected, and hunted.

The novel uses her to critique the [[social-class-and-status|class]]-based assumptions of [[criminal-detection|criminal investigation]], while also acknowledging that her very visibility as a suspect is what ultimately brings her into contact with [[mr-burton|Burton]] — the one man capable of seeing through [[proof-and-evidence|circumstantial evidence]] to the truth.
