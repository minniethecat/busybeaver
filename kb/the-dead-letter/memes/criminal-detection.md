# Criminal Detection

Criminal detection in mid-19th-century America was an emerging professional field, transitioning from amateur constables and private watchmen toward organized detective police forces. In Metta Victor's *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]*, criminal detection is personified by [[mr-burton]], a private detective whose methods blend rational investigation with mesmeric [[mesmeric-clairvoyance|clairvoyance]] — a combination that reflects the era's fluid boundary between science and the supernatural.

## Definition

By the 1850s, American cities had begun establishing formal detective departments within municipal police forces. Unlike uniformed patrol officers, detectives worked in plain clothes, conducting interviews, following suspects, and gathering [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]]. The novel accurately depicts this institutional context: the [[honor-and-reputation|character]]s visit "the office of [[mr-burton|the detective]]-police" in [[new-york|New York]] [[005-030]].

Private detectives also operated independently, often hired by wealthy families to handle sensitive matters outside public scrutiny. [[mr-burton]] occupies this role — he is not a police employee but a specialist retained by the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] family. His work combines several investigative modes:

- **Witness interviews**: questioning servants, family members, and bystanders
- **Physical [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] collection**: examining [[murder-of-henry-moreland|the murder]] scene and preserving clues
- **Surveillance**: monitoring suspects' movements and behavior
- **Psychological observation**: reading the emotional responses of persons of interest
- **Mesmeric inquiry**: using trance states to access subconscious perception in sensitive subjects

## In the Novel

Criminal detection in *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]* is presented as a morally charged endeavor. The detective is not merely a technician but an agent of [[justice-and-retribution|justice]]:

> "On exchange, in the halls of justice, and in the high and low places of the great metropolis, the harvest of crime is ready for the reaper, whoever has the courage to put in his sickle." [[005-070]]

[[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]] operates within the "secret detective-police" — an unofficial network of informants and investigators who work "only working when some great case was before the public, or some large reward was pending" [[005-071]]. This arrangement highlights the economic reality of 19th-century detection: cases were often pursued based on the availability of reward money rather than public [[duty-and-moral-obligation|duty]].

The novel's narrator, [[richard-redfield]], observes [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s method with admiration and occasional unease. [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s approach is notably psychological — he studies facial expressions, detects inconsistencies in [[proof-and-evidence|testimony]], and uses [[mesmeric-clairvoyance|mesmeric trance]] to access information from [[proof-and-evidence|witness]]es who cannot consciously recall it.

The detective's work unfolds over years, not days — a realistic departure from the compressed timelines of later detective fiction. The [[murder-of-henry-moreland]] occurs in October 1857; the resolution does not come until years later, after [[mr-burton|Burton]] has painstakingly accumulated [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] and traced [[the-letter-as-device|the dead letter]] to its source.

## Narrative Significance

1. **Genre innovation**: *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]* (1866) is among the earliest American detective novels, predating Anna Katharine Green's *The Leavenworth Case* (1878) by over a decade. Its portrayal of systematic criminal detection helped establish the conventions of the genre.

2. **The detective as moral center**: [[mr-burton|Burton]] embodies the idea that detection is a form of moral inquiry. He pursues not only the legal truth but also a broader sense of "[[justice-and-retribution|justice]] and [[justice-and-retribution|righteousness]]."

3. **The amateur-professional partnership**: The collaboration between [[mr-burton|Burton]] (the professional) and [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] (the amateur with personal stakes) anticipates the Holmes-Watson dynamic and establishes a template for detective-sidekick relationships.

4. **Detection and the supernatural**: [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s use of [[mesmeric-clairvoyance|mesmeric trance]] as an investigative tool blurs the line between rational detection and spiritualism — a distinctive feature that sets the novel apart from its more strictly rationalist successors.

## Historical Context

The 1850s–1860s saw the birth of organized detective work in America:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1845 | [[new-york|New York City]] establishes its first police detective bureau |
| 1850 | Allan Pinkerton founds the Pinkerton National Detective Agency |
| 1857 | The Metropolitan Police Act reorganizes [[new-york|New York]]'s police force |
| 1866 | *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]* is published, one of the first American novels to feature a detective protagonist |
