# The Entrapment of George Thorley

After years of patient investigation, [[mr-burton|Mr. Burton]] assembles the accumulated [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] — the surgical instrument, the dead [[the-letter-as-device|letter]], the mesmeric [[proof-and-evidence|testimony]] — and confronts [[george-thorley|George Thorley]]. The resulting confession brings the long mystery to its conclusion.

## Summary

[[mr-burton|Burton]] presents the [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] to the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] family, fitting the surgical instrument found at the crime scene to the piece extracted from [[henry-moreland|Moreland]]'s body and showing the initials of [[george-thorley|George Thorley]] upon it. He then produces [[george-thorley|Thorley]]'s written confession, which contains "in a plain statement, the facts" [[022-015]]. The resolution brings legal and emotional closure, though the novel acknowledges that [[justice-and-retribution|justice]] cannot restore what was lost.

## Narrative Role

As the **climax**, the entrapment resolves the central question of the novel — who killed [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]? — and fulfills the promise of [[justice-and-retribution|justice]] that has driven the investigation since Chapter 2.
