# Express Company

The Express Company is a courier and financial transfer service that appears at a critical juncture in the investigation of [[henry-moreland]]'s murder. It is through tracing the "[[park-bank|Park Bank]] bill to the Express Company" that [[mr-burton]] establishes a concrete financial link connecting [[george-thorley]] to the stolen money — a piece of hard [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] in a case built substantially on psychological perception and mesmeric intuition.

## Overview

The Express Company appears late in the novel, in the phase of the investigation where [[mr-burton|Burton]] is assembling the documentary [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] that will corroborate his mesmeric findings. [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] records the crucial discovery: the tracing "of the [[park-bank|Park Bank]] bill to the Express Company" [[018-001]], followed by the revelation of how [[george-thorley|Thorley]] "had disposed of [the bill] to the Express Company" [[018-002]].

The Express Company thus serves as a relay point in the financial chain — the institution that received the stolen bill from [[george-thorley|Thorley]], creating a transaction record that [[mr-burton|Burton]] could follow years later.

## Institutional Background

Express companies were a distinctive feature of 19th-century American commerce. Firms like Adams Express (founded 1839) and American Express (founded 1850) provided courier services, money transfer, and freight transport — filling gaps in the postal system and banking infrastructure.

These companies operated extensive networks of offices and agents, maintained detailed transaction records, and handled significant volumes of currency. Their institutional practices — systematic record-keeping, agent networks, transaction documentation — made them valuable sources of [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] in [[criminal-detection|criminal investigation]]s, as [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s use of their records demonstrates.

## Narrative Function

The Express Company serves as a [[the-letter-as-device|narrative device]] for grounding the novel's more speculative elements in material fact. [[mr-burton|Burton]]'s mesmeric sittings with [[eleanor-argyll]] produce visions and intuitions; his psychological reading of suspects yields impressions and [[secrecy-and-suspicion|suspicion]]s. But the Express Company's records provide something different: documentary [[proof-and-evidence|proof]].

This combination — the supernatural and the documentary — is [[honor-and-reputation|character]]istic of *The [[dead-letter|Dead Letter]]*'s transitional position in the history of detective fiction. The novel does not ask the reader to choose between [[proof-and-evidence|evidence]] types; it demonstrates that a complete investigation requires both.
