# Redfield's Banishment

In the aftermath of [[henry-moreland|Henry Moreland]]'s murder, [[richard-redfield|Richard Redfield]] found himself cut off from the [[james-argyll|Argyll]] family — the people who had been his friends, his employer, and the object of his secret love. His departure from Blankville marks the end of his youthful aspirations and the beginning of his long exile in Washington.

## Summary

[[richard-redfield|Redfield]] describes the rupture in bitter terms: "driven — driven is the word you must use, [[richard-redfield|Richard]], haughty and sensitive though you may be — from the friendship and presence of the [[james-argyll|Argyll]]s, and from my prospects of a long-cherished settlement in life" [[014-002]]. The cause of the banishment is left partially ambiguous — [[richard-redfield|Redfield]]'s own account suggests that circumstances and misunderstandings conspired against him — but its effect is clear: the destruction of his professional future and the severing of his connection to [[eleanor-argyll|Eleanor]].

## Narrative Role

As a **complication**, the banishment separates [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] from the investigation's center and establishes the conditions for his eventual redemptive return. It also deepens the novel's emotional stakes — [[richard-redfield|Redfield]] is not merely solving a crime but reclaiming a life.
