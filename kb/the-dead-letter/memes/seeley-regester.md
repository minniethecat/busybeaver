# Seeley Regester (Metta Victoria Fuller)

Metta Victoria Fuller (1831–1885) wrote under the pen name **Seeley Regester** and was a prolific American author of dime novels and sensation fiction. Her novel *The Dead Letter*, first published in 1866, stands as one of the earliest American examples of the detective genre — predating Anna Katharine Green's *The Leavenworth Case* (1878) by over a decade.

The Standard Ebooks edition used as the source text for this wiki credits the work to **Metta Victor** (her married name), reflecting the varied naming under which her works circulated in the 19th century.

## Definition

Seeley Regester was one of several pen names used by Metta Victoria Fuller Victor (1831–1885), who began her literary career in her teens and became one of the most commercially successful American dime novelists of the mid-19th century. She wrote across multiple genres — romance, sensation fiction, detective stories, and frontier tales — for the house of Beadle and Adams, the dominant publisher of cheap popular fiction in the United States.

The dime novel format was a transformative force in American publishing: inexpensive paperbound books priced at ten cents, distributed through newsstands and mail order rather than traditional bookstores. This format brought fiction to a mass readership for the first time, and women writers like Metta Victor were central to its success. Her output was prodigious — she authored or edited dozens of dime novels across her career.

## In the Novel

The novel's source text itself contains limited direct information about its author. The Standard Ebooks edition title page credits:

> "By **Metta Victor**" [[024-003]]

and the colophon records:

> "was published in 1866 by Metta Victor." [[024-002]]

Additionally, the wiki's [[About]] page notes the work is:

> "a detective novel by Metta Victoria Fuller Victor (published 1866 under the pseudonym Seeley Regester). It is considered one of the earliest detective novels in American literature, predating Anna Katharine Green's The Leavenworth Case by over a decade."

*The Dead Letter* was first serialized in **Beadle's Monthly**, a literary magazine published by Beadle and Adams that served as a sister publication to their more famous dime novel series. The serial publication model — releasing stories in monthly installments — was standard practice for 19th-century fiction and shaped the pacing and chapter structure of the novel as we now read it.

The novel's detective framework — a crime investigated through unorthodox means (mesmeric clairvoyance) — reflects the era's fascination with both scientific detection and supernatural phenomena, positioning the work at the intersection of rational inquiry and spiritualist belief that characterized mid-19th-century American popular culture.

## Narrative Significance

1. **An early American detective novel**: *The Dead Letter* (1866) was published only two decades after Edgar Allan Poe's Dupin stories (1841–1845) established the detective genre. It predates Anna Katharine Green's *The Leavenworth Case* (1878), often cited as the first American detective novel, by twelve years.

2. **A woman pioneer in genre fiction**: Metta Victor's career demonstrates that women writers were not merely participants but pioneers in the early development of American detective and crime fiction — a fact often obscured by the male-dominated canon of "classic" detective literature (Poe, Doyle).

3. **The dime novel as democratization of reading**: The publication context — Beadle's Monthly and the dime novel ecosystem — represents a significant moment in American literary history when fiction became affordable and accessible to a mass audience. The novel's sensational elements (murder, mesmeric detection, romance) were crafted for this broad readership.

4. **Serial form and narrative structure**: The chapter-by-chapter structure, with its cliffhangers and revelations paced across installments, reflects the constraints and opportunities of the serial publication format in which *The Dead Letter* first appeared.
