# Violante

Violante is the nurse-child of [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] during [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s self-imposed exile in Italy in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Her devotion to [[ida-culvers|Ida]] and her entanglement with a young Englishman who proposes marriage form a significant subplot in the novel's later chapters.

## Role in the Story

Violante serves as a witness to [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s character during her years of exile. Her "devoted" attachment to [[ida-culvers|Ida]] (018-006) testifies to the qualities that [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s English family failed to appreciate. The narrative of Violante's own story—an offer of marriage from a "handsome young Englishman" (018-010), his subsequent abandonment, and [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s practical assistance—mirrors and illuminates [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s own experience of male inconstancy and female resilience.

When [[ida-culvers|Ida]] needs money for the journey, Violante gives her "every penny she had" (019-038), a gesture of selfless loyalty that contrasts with the calculating financial arrangements of the English [[person-index|characters]].

## Character Analysis

### Traits

Violante is defined by loyalty, simplicity, and vulnerability. She is "the darling and pet of the village" (018-009)—a beloved figure whose goodness is recognised by all who know her. Her devotion to [[ida-culvers|Ida]] is absolute, and her willingness to sacrifice "every penny she had" (019-038) for [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s journey reveals a generosity that the novel presents as morally superior to the careful calculations of the English gentry.

### Development

Violante's arc is one of passive suffering redeemed by [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s eventual return. Her grief at a death (likely a child or relative) (019-036), her abandonment by the Englishman, and her penniless state might constitute a tragedy in another novel; here, they serve as the conditions that [[ida-and-clive-reunion|Ida's return]] is meant to remedy. The promise to offer "[[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]] to the discarded Violante" (019-050) brings the subplot to a hopeful close.

## Historical / Social Context

Violante's situation—a "half-educated, and penniless" (018-013) Italian girl proposed to by an aristocratic Englishman—reflects the power imbalance inherent in cross-class and cross-national romantic entanglements in the Victorian period. The Englishman's inability to bring her home to his "aristocratic English family" (018-013) speaks to the rigid class and national boundaries that governed respectable marriage.
