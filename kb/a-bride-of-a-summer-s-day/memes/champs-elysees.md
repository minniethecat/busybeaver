# Champs Élysées

The Champs Élysées is a Parisian avenue briefly mentioned in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day* as the place where [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] walks to clear his mind during the tense hours of the Paris investigation.

## Role in the Story

On a hot Paris evening, with "a thunderstorm seeming threatening," [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] feels "stifled within four walls" and decides to "take a turn in the Champs Élysées and see if the fresh air would clear his brain and put some fresh ideas into it" (015-015). He has an hour to fill before keeping his appointment at the Italian café with the [[organ-boy|organ-boy]], and the Champs Élysées offers a momentary respite from the intensity of the investigation.

## Function

The Champs Élysées functions as a space of suspension — a grand, public promenade where private anxieties can be walked off, where the mind can work on its problems without the pressure of immediate action. Its brief appearance in the novel provides a breath of open air in a narrative otherwise confined to rooms, offices, and narrow streets.
