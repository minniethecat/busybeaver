# Red Cotton Nightcap Country

*Red Cotton Nightcap Country*, or *Turf and Towers*, is a narrative poem by Robert Browning, published in 1873. In *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*, [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] uses it as a prop — "making believe to read" while a message of grave importance is [[About|about]] to be delivered (010-001).

## Work Type

A long narrative poem in blank verse, based on a contemporary French legal case involving a disputed inheritance and a suspicious death. Browning subtitled it *Turf and Towers* to suggest its central contrast between worldly business and spiritual aspiration.

## Context in the Novel

The poem appears at a moment of high tension in Chapter X. [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], waiting for news of [[ida-culvers|Ida]], sits with a book she is not really reading:

> "[[clive-redway|Clive]] found her sitting in the drawing-room at [[dering-hall|Dering]], ostensibly deep in a novel, and making believe to read *Red Cotton Nightcap Country*, when a message was brought to her." (010-001)

The choice of Browning is characteristic of Pirkis's literary referencing. Browning's poem deals with a mysterious death, contested inheritance, and the difficulty of distinguishing truth from [[beauty-and-appearance|appearance]] — [[concept-index|themes]] that mirror the novel's own plot. [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s pretense of reading while actually waiting for news about the [[carmelite-friars|Carmelite Friars]] ceremony and the diamond brooch underscores the gap between surface composure and inner anxiety that defines her character throughout.

## Significance

The reference functions on multiple levels. For the Victorian reader who recognized Browning's poem, it would evoke the themes of legal entanglement, disputed marriage, and death under suspicious circumstances — precisely the elements driving the novel's second act. For the inattentive reader, it is simply a detail of domestic verisimilitude: a young woman passing time with a book.

Within the novel's system of literary allusion, the reference to Browning — a poet associated with psychological complexity and moral ambiguity — signals that the story is moving beyond the conventional marriage-plot into darker territory. [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] is not reading a romance; she is "making believe" to read a poem about the legal and moral consequences of a death.
