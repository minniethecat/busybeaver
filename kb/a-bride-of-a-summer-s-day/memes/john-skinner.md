# John Skinner

John Skinner is a minor but structurally important character in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*: the brother of [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]] and [[miss-skinner|Miss Skinner]], whose mercenary testimony about [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s scheme provides the final breakthrough in the Paris investigation.

Pirkis draws Skinner's character through unflattering physical detail:

> Assuredly the personal appearance of John Skinner was not such as to inspire confidence. With his hat removed, he looked even less attractive than he had on the previous night. He was short in stature, with a flat head, small eyes, and hair, complexion, and whiskers of a sandy hue. The expression on his face was that of cunning of a low type combined with servility.
>
> (016-009)

## Role in the Story

Skinner appears late in the novel, shortly after [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]]'s visit to the dying [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]] in the [[rue-corot|Rue Corot]]. He presents himself to [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] and [[clive-redway|Clive]] at their hotel, offering information — for a price. His motive is transparently financial: he is willing to tell secrets once it has been made "worth his while" (017-033).

From Skinner, [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] and [[clive-redway|Clive]] learn the full scope of [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s scheme: the plan to invoke the clause of reversion in the [[marriage-settlement|marriage settlement]], the removal of diamonds from [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s brooch, and the network of shady characters surrounding [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] in Paris.

## Character Analysis

Skinner is drawn in unflattering detail: "short in stature, with a flat head, small eyes, and hair, complexion, and whiskers of a sandy hue. The expression on his face was that of cunning of a low type combined with servility" (017-010). He is a man of the margins — a vagrant career, a mercenary disposition, and a willingness to sell family secrets for cash. His physical description aligns him with the novel's taxonomy of untrustworthy physical types, a Victorian literary convention.

Yet for all his mercenary qualities, Skinner's information is accurate and crucial. He represents the morally ambiguous informant — the man whose venality serves the cause of truth. His testimony about [[marie-schira|Marie]]'s debts and her possible role in removing the brooch stones (017-033) adds a layer of complexity to the story: the woman whose deathbed summons brought [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] to the Rue Corot may herself have been complicit in the crime.
