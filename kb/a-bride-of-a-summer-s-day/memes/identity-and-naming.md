# Identity and Naming

Names and naming form a persistent motif in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*, marking transformations of identity, social position, and moral character. From [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]]'s triple transformation of her name to [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s passage through a series of relational designations, the novel treats naming as a register of selfhood.

## Assumed Names

The most explicit engagement with naming is [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]]'s self-reinvention. Born Mary Skinner, she "Frenchified" her Christian name to Marie and "Italianised" the family patronymic "into Schira" (017-005). The name is neither French nor Italian but a hybrid invention — a linguistic costume as theatrical as her stage roles. It conceals her English origins and her family connections, enabling her life in the Parisian demimonde.

The name "Schira" — with its suggestions of *schiera* (Italian: "rank, array") or *Schirm* (German: "screen, shield") — functions as both disguise and revelation: it hides Mary Skinner while advertising the act of hiding.

## Marital Names

[[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] moves through the novel under a shifting set of designations: Miss Culvers, Mrs. Sefton Culvers, "the bride," "the missing bride," "the widow." Each name marks a different social identity, and the instability of her naming reflects the instability of her position. She is a bride who never became a wife, a wife who became a fugitive, a widow whose widowhood was a liberation.

## Titles and Social Identity

The novel is attentive to the social grammar of titles. [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]]'s title is the prize of her marriage to [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]; the fact that she was formerly [[miss-pigott|Miss Pigott]], the daughters' governess, is a fact that her title both announces (she is Lady Culvers) and conceals (she was not born to it). The wedding guests' gossip about "a man in his position marrying his daughter's governess and chaperon" (001-012) registers the social impropriety that the title cannot fully erase.

## Naming as Moral Act

The novel's resolution involves an act of naming: [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s recognition of [[violante|Violante]] as a person with legitimate claims. To name [[violante|Violante]] as [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s daughter — rather than as a shameful secret — is to confer identity and entitlement. The novel's moral arc moves from concealment to recognition, from assumed names to acknowledged ones.
