# Porte Saint Martin

The Porte Saint Martin is a Paris quarter in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*, notable as the district containing the [[rue-corot|Rue Corot]] and the [[theatre-beaumarchais|Théâtre Beaumarchais]].

## Location Type

The Porte Saint Martin is a real district of Paris, historically associated with the theatre and popular entertainment. In the novel, it is described as "the unfashionable quarter of the Porte Saint Martin, in close vicinity to the Théâtre Beaumarchais" (015-025). Its "unfashionable" character distinguishes it from the grand Boulevards and the aristocratic quarters, marking it as a zone of working-class and bohemian life.

## Description

The Porte Saint Martin is the setting for the novel's most poignant scenes: the [[rue-corot|Rue Corot]], where [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]] lies dying, is "a narrow and somewhat noisy thoroughfare of tall seven-storied houses that are let and sublet to all sorts and conditions of men" (015-025). The district's social heterogeneity — "all sorts and conditions of men" — mirrors the moral heterogeneity of the Paris subplot, where actresses, priests, gamblers, and police all occupy adjacent spaces.

## Function

The Porte Saint Martin represents the Paris that the English characters would never willingly visit — the city behind the Boulevards, where poverty, performance, and mortality coexist in close quarters. Its distance from the [[hotel-bristol|Hôtel Bristol]] and the [[palais-de-justice|Palais de Justice]] measures the social distance [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] must cross to uncover the truth about [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]].
