# Ida Culvers's Wedding Day

The wedding of [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] to [[sefton-culvers|Captain Sefton Culvers]] at [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints Church]], Hastings]], is the inciting event of *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. What begins as a conventional Victorian society wedding — complete with bells, flowers, and wedding guests — unfolds into the mystery that drives the entire novel.

## Timeline

1. **The ceremony** — [[ida-culvers|Ida]] and Sefton are married at [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints Church]]. Bells from [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints]] tower and old Saint Clement's ring out; children scatter flowers (001-001).
2. **The [[ida-culvers|bride]]'s first "odd fancy"** — [[ida-culvers|Ida]] chooses [[hastings|Hastings]] for her wedding because her mother lies buried in [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints]] churchyard (001-011).
3. **The wedding breakfast** — Guests gather at [[glynde-lodge|Glynde Lodge]], a house lent to [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] for the occasion (001-006).
4. **[[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s farewell** — [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] bids her twin sister an emotional goodbye, the cloud on her face betraying her distress (001-023).
5. **[[clive-redway|Clive]] and [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] in the garden** — [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] questions [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] [[About|about]] the circumstances of the marriage (001-031).
6. **The bouquet on the grave** — [[ida-culvers|Ida]] announces her second "odd fancy": she will lay her bridal bouquet on her mother's grave before departing (001-018).
7. **The disappearance** — [[ida-culvers|Ida]] walks to the churchyard, rounds the east end of the church, and vanishes from sight (002-006).

## Location

The wedding takes place at [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints Church]] in [[hastings]], a seaside [[london|town]] on the south coast of England. [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s choice of location — driven by her desire to be married where her mother is buried — is repeatedly described as an "odd fancy" by the wedding guests (001-011). The churchyard itself becomes the last place [[ida-culvers|Ida]] is seen, as she disappears while laying her bouquet on her mother's grave.

## Narrative Summary

The wedding is presented through the eyes of the wedding guests, whose gossip provides the reader with essential exposition about the [[culvers-family|Culvers family]]: [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]'s second marriage to the former governess, [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s [[temper-and-will|strong will]] and temper, the financial logic of the match, and the twins' "odd whims and fancies" (001-010).

Two "strange fancies" mark the day as unusual. The first — [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s choice of [[hastings|Hastings]] for the wedding — is known from the start. The second — her insistence on laying her bridal bouquet on her mother's grave before departing for her honeymoon — is announced only as she leaves (001-018). It is in fulfilling this second fancy that [[ida-culvers|Ida]] vanishes.

## Plot Significance

The wedding day serves as both the structural and thematic foundation of the novel. Structurally, it establishes all the major [[person-index|characters]], their relationships, and the central conflict. Thematically, the juxtaposition of wedding and funeral — the bells and flowers against the graveside visit — establishes the novel's preoccupation with the boundary between marriage and death, love and loss.

The "odd fancy" of the graveside visit also establishes [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s character: her fierce loyalty to her dead mother, her indifference to social convention, and the strength of will that will sustain her through the trials to come. That she disappears while performing this act of filial devotion transforms what might have been merely eccentric into something ominous.

> "It was like the sound of a funeral-bell in the midst of a feast." (001-019)
