# Alta Lauria

Alta Lauria is a remote mountain hamlet in the Calabrian region of southern Italy in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. It is the place where [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] took refuge after fleeing England, and it becomes the destination of [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s final redemptive journey.

## Location Type

Alta Lauria is a fictional mountain hamlet — described as "the last place in the world to which anyone would voluntarily make a pilgrimage" (017-045) — situated in the mountainous region of [[la-sila|La Sila]] in Calabria. It is a place of extreme remoteness, home to "vinedressers and shepherds" (018-009) and accessible only by mule-track.

Sefton's own description of the hamlet reveals his bitter ambivalence:

> He ended his story, saying that he had never in remotest fancy connected Ida's disappearance with this episode in his life; nor could he in any way explain how nor by whom she had been inveigled into that "accursed Alta Lauria — a nest of wild, hot-blooded ruffians."
>
> (018-017)

## Description

The novel presents Alta Lauria with ambivalence that reflects [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s own contradictory feelings. To him, it is an "accursed" place — "a nest of wild, hot-blooded Calabrian mountaineers" (018-017).

Yet it is also where he found refuge, where his child [[violante|Violante]] was born and raised, and where [[francesca-xardez|Francesca Xardez]] practised her healing arts among the mountain ravines (018-003).

The hamlet embodies the clash between Sefton's aristocratic English identity and the raw, uncompromising Italian world he both needed and despised.

## Key Events

- **Sefton's refuge**: After some undisclosed trouble, Sefton retreats to Alta Lauria, making it his base in southern Italy (018-002)
- **[[violante|Violante]]'s birth and upbringing**: The hamlet is where [[violante|Violante]] was "the darling and pet" of the community (018-009)
- **Sefton's flight**: He eventually flees Alta Lauria, describing it as an "accursed" place (018-015, 018-017)
- **Ida's return**: In the novel's closing movement, [[ida-culvers|Ida]] resolves to return to Alta Lauria and offer the estate to "the discarded [[violante|Violante]]" (019-050)

## Narrative Function

Alta Lauria is the novel's symbolic antipode to [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]]. Where [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]] represents English order, social performance, and the constraints of aristocratic life, Alta Lauria represents the wild, the passionate, and the ungovernable. That the novel's resolution requires a return to this remote hamlet — and an act of restitution to its inhabitants — suggests a moral arc that moves from English evasion to Italian accountability.

## Historical / Social Context

The [[la-sila|Calabrian mountains]] in the late nineteenth century were among the most remote and least governed regions of the new Italian state. Unified Italy (1861) had done little to change the isolation of villages like Alta Lauria — [[place-index|places]] reached by mule-track rather than rail, where local identity outweighed national allegiance. The novel's depiction of Alta Lauria as a law unto itself, where a bandit like [[peter-the-calabrian|Peter the Calabrian]] could operate and where foreign aristocrats could vanish, reflects contemporary English perceptions of the Italian south as a zone of picturesque lawlessness.

Sefton's description of Alta Lauria as "a nest of wild, hot-blooded Calabrian mountaineers" (018-017) deploys the language of Victorian racial and cultural hierarchy, but the novel subtly undermines him: it is the English aristocrat who has behaved dishonourably, and the "wild" Calabrians who raised his child with love. The hamlet ultimately represents not savagery but a moral simplicity that judges Sefton's conduct more truly than English society ever does.
