# Temper and Will

"Temper and will" is a recurring motif in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*, describing the combination of strong emotion and determined purpose that characterizes the Culvers women — particularly [[ida-culvers|Ida]] and [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]. The concept is treated ambivalently in the novel: it is both a source of strength and a social liability, both inherited and cultivated.

## Definition

In the novel's terms, "temper" refers not merely to anger but to the full spectrum of strong feeling — the "restless, excitable temperament" (001-009) that [[twin-bond|Ida and Juliet]] inherit from their mother. "Will" is the determination to act on that feeling, to impose one's desires on the world regardless of social convention. Together they produce what [[ida-wedding|the wedding]] guests call "odd whims and fancies" (001-010) and what the narrative presents as a form of integrity.

## Manifestations

### In Ida Culvers

[[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s temper and will are established from the novel's opening. [[ida-wedding|The wedding]] guests note that "[[ida-culvers|Ida]], in spite of her loveliness, had a temper and a will of her own, and had known how to stand up not only for her own rights, but also for those of her twin-sister, [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]" (001-007). Her decision to marry at [[hastings|Hastings]] — for the sole reason that her mother is buried there — is presented as an act of will that defies social expectation: "If she had been my daughter I would not have given way to such a ridiculous whim" (001-011).

[[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s will culminates in [[ida-wedding|the wedding]]-day decision to lay her bouquet on her mother's grave — an act that leads directly to her disappearance and the novel's central mystery.

### In Juliet Culvers

[[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] shares her sister's temperament but expresses it with a lighter touch. Her sharp tongue — the retort to [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]] that carries the subtext "If it had not been for my father's odd fancies, you would be [[lady-culvers|Miss Pigott]] at the present moment" (001-021) — and her playful defiance of [[clive-redway|Clive]]'s questions demonstrate a will that is no less strong than [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s, but channeled into social performance rather than solitary gesture.

### In Lord Culvers

The "odd fancies" that [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] inherits from her father represent a milder, more eccentric form of the same trait. [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]'s "placid, easygoing temper" (001-009) is the inverse of his daughters' intensity, but his willingness to marry his daughter's governess — an act that defied social convention — marks him as a man of quiet but real determination.

## Victorian Context

The novel's treatment of "temper and will" reflects a distinctively late-Victorian anxiety [[About|about]] feminine agency. The ideal Victorian woman was expected to be gentle, submissive, and self-effacing — the "angel in the house." The Culvers sisters, with their strong wills and sharp tongues, represent a challenge to this ideal that the novel simultaneously celebrates and problematizes.

The phrase "odd whims and fancies" (001-010) captures the social world's response to female will: it is dismissed as eccentricity rather than recognized as agency. The wedding guests' gossip — "If they were not beauties and heiresses, no one would put up with their odd whims and fancies" (001-010) — reveals the economic basis of this tolerance: will is permitted to women who can pay for it.

The inheritance of temperament from mother to daughters also engages Victorian debates about heredity and character. That the first [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]]'s "restless, excitable temperament" (001-009) passes to her daughters while [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]'s placidity does not suggests a view of character as matrilineal — and perhaps a critique of the patriarchal fiction that fathers shape their children's natures.
