# Marie Schira

Marie Schira — born Mary Skinner — is a Parisian actress whose deathbed confession provides a crucial breakthrough in the search for [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Her connection to [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] and her knowledge of his scheme make her one of the novel's most important secondary figures.

## Role in the Story

Marie Schira enters the narrative only at its end, when [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] receives a message that "a poor penitent, lying at the point of death at No. 11, [[rue-corot|Rue Corot]], has a story to tell that may interest Lord Culvers" (015-012).

She is an actress at the Théâtre Beaumarchais who, three nights before, had set her gauze sleeve on fire with the candles on her dressing-table and been so severely burned that her life was despaired of (015-017).
> He ventured to address another question or two to the girl, and elicited the fact that Marie Schira, while dressing in her tiny dressing-room behind the scenes at the Théâtre Beaumarchais, had set her gauze sleeve on fire with the candles on her table, and, before assistance could be procured, had been so severely burned that her life was despaired of. This had happened three nights ago.
>
> (015-022)
 [[clive-redway|Clive]] goes to the Rue Corot and, through her brother [[john-skinner|John Skinner]], learns the full story of [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s scheme.

## Background

Marie was born Mary Skinner in England. As a young woman — "beautiful but frivolous" — she "had run away from home, when little more than a child, to join a company of strolling players" (017-005). She had been estranged from her family for years. Her brother [[john-skinner|John]], "in his somewhat vagrant career," had later found her on the racecourse at Chantilly, "with her first name Frenchified, and the family patronymic of Skinner Italianised into Schira" (017-005). Under this *nom de guerre*, she had made something of a reputation as an actress of low comedy parts.

The name "Schira" — neither French nor quite Italian — epitomises the world of assumed identities and blurred nationalities that characterises the Parisian demimonde of the novel.

## What She Revealed

Marie Schira had knowledge of [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s plan to claim [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s fortune under the clause of reversion in the [[marriage-settlement|marriage settlement]]. She was also connected to the removal of diamonds from [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s brooch. Her brother [[john-skinner|John Skinner]] later speculated that "Marie was deeply in debt" and that "she herself removed the stones from the brooch, and disposed of them as best she could" (017-033).

Before she could tell her story directly, Marie Schira passed away — "So Marie Schira passed away with her story untold" (017-020) — but her brother [[john-skinner|John Skinner]], motivated by the offer of payment, revealed what he knew.

## Character Analysis

Marie Schira is a figure of the Parisian margins — an Englishwoman remade under a continental alias, an actress of "low comedy parts" at "an inferior theatre" (017-005), a woman whose debts entangled her with dangerous men. Her death by fire in her dressing-room — a ghastly theatrical irony — makes her a tragic figure: a performer whose final costume change was into a shroud. The novel treats her with a blend of pity and moral seriousness rare in its depiction of minor characters.
