# Juliet and Arthur Glynde's Engagement

[[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]]'s eventual engagement to [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]] is the quiet resolution of her romantic storyline in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. After a long engagement to [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] marked by her reluctance to set a wedding date, [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] transfers her affections to Glynde, the patient poet whose devotion has been steady throughout the novel.

## Timeline

- **Preceded by**: [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s prolonged engagement to [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]], during which she repeatedly defers setting a wedding date (009-045)
- **Followed by**: The novel's close, with [[ida-culvers|Ida]] urging [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] to "send for [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]], and fix your wedding-day" (024-080)
- **Narrative time**: Two years after the [[event-index|events]] of [[ida-wedding|the wedding]] day

## Location

The engagement develops at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]] and its surrounding neighbourhood. Glynde lives at [[glynde-lodge|Glynde Lodge]], a neighbouring estate, making his courtship of [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] a matter of local social life rather than distant pursuit.

## Narrative Summary

[[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s engagement to [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]], announced early in the novel, is characterised by her resistance to setting a date. When pressed—"Can you fix a date for the ending of your engagement?" (009-045)—she deflects, and she acknowledges that if she broke off the engagement she would "immediately want to be engaged to him again" (007-036). This ambivalence creates space for [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]]'s patient courtship.

Glynde, a poet and neighbour, listens to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] in "the impassioned tone which he reserved for any talk with her" (007-004) and is "a very general favourite" (007-012) in local society. His steady presence contrasts with the uncertainty of [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s engagement to [[clive-redway|Clive]], and over the two years of the novel's middle span, her affections gradually shift.

By the novel's closing chapter, the transition is complete. [[ida-culvers|Ida]] archly asks, "What [[About|about]] [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]]? Is he as indifferent to you as ever?" (024-028), and [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s protestations of indifference are transparently unconvincing. The novel's final movement urges her to "send for [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]], and fix your wedding-day at once" (024-080).

## Plot Significance

Juliet's second engagement provides the novel's romantic resolution. Where [[ida-wedding|Ida's marriage]] to [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] was a disaster born of financial calculation, and her eventual reunion with [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] a hard-won redemption, [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s path to Glynde represents a quieter, more conventional form of happiness—a love that grows through patience rather than passion.
