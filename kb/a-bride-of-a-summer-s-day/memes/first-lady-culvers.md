# First Lady Culvers

The first Lady Culvers is the deceased mother of [[ida-culvers|Ida]] and [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Though she died some twenty years before the novel's events (001-012), her presence suffuses the story through her daughters' inherited temperament, her portrait, and the choice of her gravesite as the location for [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s wedding.

## Role in the Story

The first Lady Culvers never appears directly but is a constant, haunting presence. The wedding guests discuss "the restless, excitable temperament of his first wife — a temperament which there could not be a doubt she had bequeathed, together with her beauty, to her daughters" (001-009). Her portrait, hanging over the mantelpiece at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]], depicts features that "expressed in every line and tint the vivacity, waywardness, and love of fun which in [[twin-bond|Ida and Juliet]] had fascinated their friends and lovers, and had made their father's life off and on a burden to him" (005-049).

Her most significant posthumous act is the location of [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s wedding: "To think that Ida chose to be married at Hastings for the whole and sole reason that her mother lies buried in All Saints churchyard!" (001-011). This choice transforms the wedding from a social occasion into an act of filial remembrance — [[ida-culvers|Ida]] passing from her mother's grave to her own marriage.

## Character Analysis

The first Lady Culvers functions as the novel's absent origin — the source of both the twins' beauty and their [[temper-and-will|wilfulness]]. The wedding guests' gossip establishes her as a woman of intense personality whose legacy is a doubled gift: "If they were not beauties and heiresses, no one would put up with their odd whims and fancies" (001-010).

Her portrait serves as a visual memento of this legacy. When [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] laments his domestic turmoil — "Gracious Heaven what have I done that my life should be filled with turmoil from year's end to year's end?" (005-047) — his eyes rise "as if magnetically drawn" to her portrait. The narrative's answer is implicit: he married a woman of passion and spirit, and her daughters are her living repetition.

The burial at [[all-saints-hastings|All Saints, Hastings]] anchors her memory in the novel's geography. [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s bridal visit to the grave — laying her wedding bouquet on her mother's resting place (002-003) — is the novel's most poignant gesture of continuity between generations of Culvers women.

## Key Quotations

(005-049)

> "To think that Ida chose to be married at Hastings for the whole and sole reason that her mother lies buried in All Saints churchyard! If she had been my daughter I would not have given way to such a ridiculous whim." (001-011)
