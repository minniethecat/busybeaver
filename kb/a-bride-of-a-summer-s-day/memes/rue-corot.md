# Rue Corot

The Rue Corot is a Paris street in the unfashionable quarter of the Porte Saint Martin where the dying actress [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]] occupies rooms at No. 11 in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. It is the site of the novel's crucial deathbed confession scene.

## Location Type

The Rue Corot lies "in the unfashionable quarter of the Porte Saint Martin, in close vicinity to the Théâtre Beaumarchais. It is a narrow and somewhat noisy thoroughfare of tall seven-storied houses that are let and sublet to all sorts and conditions of men" (015-025).

## Description

The Rue Corot is the novel's most vividly rendered Parisian interior. No. 11 is a world of "gaudiness and untidiness" where "pathetic traces of the sad episode that was ending poor Marie's career" (016-007) mix with the detritus of theatrical life: a heavy cloak with its lining burned away, "a pair of tiny, silver trimmed slippers, scorched and blackened," and "a portrait of Marie Schira, that of a beaming, brilliant brunette," smiling down on these tokens of her last tragedy (016-007).

[[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] arrives at the Rue Corot after receiving a mysterious message that "a poor penitent, lying at the point of death at No. 11, Rue Corot, has a story to tell that may interest Lord Culvers" (015-012). He is met by the concierge and directed to the second floor, where [[marie-schira|Marie Schira]] lies dying of burns sustained in her dressing-room at the Théâtre Beaumarchais.

The street's atmosphere — noisy, crowded, indifferent to the tragedy unfolding within its walls — epitomises the novel's vision of Paris as a city where life and death, performance and reality, truth and deception coexist in unsettling proximity.
