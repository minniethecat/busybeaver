# Sub Signo et Sub Rosa

"Sub signo et sub rosa" ("Under the sign and under the rose") is the Latin motto engraved on an amethyst seal inherited by the Culvers twins from their grandmother. In Catherine Louisa Pirkis's 1891 novel *A Bride of a Summer's Day*, this motto becomes the central secret communication device by which [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] attempts to reach her vanished twin sister [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]].

## The Physical Object

The seal is an amethyst "cut with a rose surrounded with the motto: '*Sub signo et sub rosa*'" (017-005). It was part of a set of trinkets given by the Culvers grandmother to the twin girls, including a matching ring — a "jasper set with diamonds, a long, coffin-shaped stone" bearing the same rose device (017-021). [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] keeps the seal "always in my writing-desk and carry about with me" (017-005), treating it as a portable instrument of private communication.

The matching ring passed to [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] through his father (the grandmother's son), making the rose-and-motto device a shared family emblem whose significance none of the parties fully understood until it became a plot mechanism (017-017–017-024).

## The Advertisement Signal

The communication system was devised two years before the novel's events, when [[ida-culvers|Ida]] went to Biarritz. The twins agreed that [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] would signal when they were at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]] by sealing a letter or wrapper with the grandmother's seal. Letters from [[ida-culvers|Ida]] would then come through [[the-sanctuary|Goody's cottage]] to avoid [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]]'s surveillance (017-005).

After [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance, [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] adapts this system: unable to send a physically sealed letter, she places the motto itself — "*Sub signo et sub rosa*" (007-079) — as a classified advertisement in "all the leading English and Continental journals" (017-006), through [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]]. The advertisement's purpose is to catch [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s eye, telling her that the family is at Dering and that she can write safely to [[the-sanctuary|Goody's cottage]] again.

## The Double Confusion

The advertisement creates an unintended consequence. [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]], who inherited the matching rose ring, sees the "Sub signo et sub rosa" advertisement and becomes agitated — "his face was flushed, his manner very excited" (013-018). He confronts [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], demanding to know who inserted it, threatening to "teach people to meddle with his private affairs" (013-018). 

This reaction initially deepens [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]]'s suspicion that [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] knows more about [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s fate than he admits. Only later, in [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s letter of explanation (Chapter XVII), does it become clear that the motto belonged to a shared family device, and [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]]'s agitation stemmed from his own guilty associations with the ring he had given to [[violante-xardez|Violante]].

## The Motto's Meaning

"Sub rosa" (under the rose) is a classical and medieval European phrase signifying confidentiality — the rose was a symbol of secrecy, hung in council chambers to indicate that proceedings were private. "Sub signo" (under the sign/seal) reinforces the notion of sealed, protected communication. Together, the phrase encodes the twins' need for a private channel in a household where [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]] routinely read their correspondence — [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] notes she "had known her take my blotting paper to the looking-glass" (017-005).


> "Sub signo et sub rosa," nothing more. It is horrid to be driven to such straits, to have to live under the cover of a motto!
>
> (013-018)

## Significance

The "Sub signo et sub rosa" device operates on multiple narrative levels. As a *plot mechanism*, it is the twins' imperfect countermeasure against domestic surveillance — the advertisement that was meant to reunite them instead alerts [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] and accelerates the Italian climax. As a *symbol*, it represents the shared inheritance (both material and temperamental) that links [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], [[ida-culvers|Ida]], and [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] through their common grandmother. And as a *structural echo*, the concept of communication "under the rose" — in secret, at risk of exposure — mirrors the novel's broader concerns with [[disguise-and-deception|secrecy]], [[identity-and-naming|identity]], and the difficulty of truthful expression within the constraints of Victorian domestic life.
