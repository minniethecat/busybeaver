# Belgrave Square

Belgrave Square is the London residence of [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. It represents the aristocratic world of the Culvers family — the sphere of social performance, family obligation, and the London Season.

The novel illustrates Lord Culvers's imperturbable temperament through a characteristic anecdote set at Belgrave Square:

> The incident alluded to had exhibited Lord Culvers in a most characteristic light. Two or three frightened servants had rushed into his room in the dead of night, exclaiming, "A fire, my lord, in the mews at the back of the house; pray get up." "A fire," Lord Culvers had repeated, calmly, without opening his eyes; "well, I dare say it will go out again," and he had turned over on his other side to finish his night's rest.
>
> (004-009)

## Location Type

Belgrave Square is a real address in Belgravia, one of the most fashionable districts of Victorian London. In the novel, Lord Culvers's house there is the family's town residence, distinct from their country seat at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]]. The address signals the Culvers family's position near the apex of the Victorian social hierarchy.

## Description

The house in Belgrave Square is primarily a site of anxiety for [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]. In the aftermath of [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance, "in a flash of fancy the unlucky father saw a long string of carriages outside his house in Belgrave Square, and heard an uninterrupted succession of knocks, rings, and enquiries for the missing bride" (003-009). The Square becomes, in his imagination, the epicentre of scandal — the place where Society's judgement will be delivered.

The house is also the site of a remembered fire — [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] asks [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]] "Do you remember the fire at the back of his house in Belgrave Square?" (004-022) — suggesting a history of domestic drama that predates the novel's events.

Later, [[francesca-xardez|Francesca Xardez]], [[violante|Violante]], and [[peter-the-calabrian|Peter the Calabrian]] use a relative's London connections and reference works — an *Army List* and a *Burke's Peerage* — to trace [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] to his family's "town house in Belgrave Square" (021-010).
