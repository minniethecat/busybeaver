# Sefton Culvers's Flight to Italy

Sefton Culvers's flight from England to the remote Calabrian hamlet of [[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]] is the event that divides the novel's geography into English order and Italian exile in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*.

## Background

After his scheme to claim [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s fortune through the [[marriage-settlement|marriage settlement]]'s clause of reversion is exposed in Paris, [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] flees England. His destination is [[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]], a remote mountain hamlet in the [[la-sila|La Sila]] region of Calabria — "the last place in the world to which anyone would voluntarily make a pilgrimage" (017-045).

## Life in Alta Lauria

> It was no wonder that when Francesca was called in to administer her remedies to the handsome young Englishman, Violante should accompany her, nor that, later on, when the Englishman, restored to health, called on his skilful doctress to offer her his thanks, Violante should have been found in the cottage eating eggs and vermicelli with her peasant foster-brothers.
>
> (018-009)

In [[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]], [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] establishes a new life among "vinedressers and shepherds" (018-009). He fathers a child, [[violante|Violante]], with an Italian woman, and lives under the protection — or surveillance — of [[francesca-xardez|Francesca Xardez]], the local wise-woman.

His relationship to the hamlet is profoundly ambivalent. To him, it is an "accursed" place — "a nest of wild, hot-blooded Calabrian mountaineers" (018-017) — yet it is also his refuge, the only place where he can live beyond the reach of English law and English social judgement.

## The Second Flight

[[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] eventually flees [[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]] as well, abandoning [[violante|Violante]] as he had abandoned [[ida-culvers|Ida]]. His description of the hamlet as "accursed" (018-015, 018-017) is the language of a man who cannot acknowledge his own failures and must instead vilify the place that sheltered him.

## Significance

The flight to Italy completes the geographic and moral trajectory of [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]]'s character. From English Army officer to Paris gambler to Calabrian fugitive, each move southward is a step away from respectability and accountability. Italy is not merely a hiding place but a revelation: in the "wild" mountains of Calabria, stripped of the protections of English class and convention, [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]]'s nature stands fully exposed.
