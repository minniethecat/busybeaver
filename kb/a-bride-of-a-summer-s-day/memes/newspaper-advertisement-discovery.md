# The Newspaper Advertisement and the Brooch's Discovery

The newspaper advertisement for [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s missing diamond brooch and its subsequent discovery in a Paris church is the pivotal event that redirects the search for [[ida-culvers|Ida]] from England to France in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*.

## The Advertisement

The advertisement is the brainchild of [[joshua-redway|Joshua Redway]], the shrewd financier father of [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]]. Recognising that "an advertisement more or less, for one of their brooches, would not be likely to attract much attention among their friends" (011-002), he proposes placing a notice describing the brooch in the leading London and provincial journals.

The advertisement is routed through [[hunt-and-locke|Messrs. Hunt and Locke]] of Chancery Lane — "not Lord Culvers's family lawyers, but a firm of solicitors noted for their successful conduct of complicated criminal cases" (011-003) — a choice that signals the transition from family matter to criminal investigation.

## The Discovery

The advertisement yields an unexpected result. A letter arrives from [[father-baldwin|Father Baldwin]], an English priest officiating at the Church of the [[carmelite-friars|Carmelite Friars]] in the [[rue-bellarmine|Rue Bellarmine]], Paris. He reports that "on the previous Sunday at the midday celebration of mass a diamond brooch, answering in all respects to the one described in the advertisement, had been dropped into the offertory bag" (012-001).

The brooch had been damaged: several stones were missing and the pin was broken. [[father-baldwin|Father Baldwin]] could not say by whom it had been dropped — "whether by a penitent as an offering, or by a thief in order to escape detection" (012-001).

## Significance

The advertisement and discovery mark the novel's structural turn from domestic mystery to criminal investigation. The recovery of the brooch — damaged, in a foreign city, under suspicious circumstances — transforms what had been a family's private anxiety into a matter for the [[prefect-of-police|Paris Prefect of Police]]. It also provides the first concrete evidence that [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance is connected to [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s Paris life.

Hunt and Locke, Chancery Lane, London."
