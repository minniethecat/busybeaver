# Sefton Culvers

Captain Sefton Culvers is introduced as the bridegroom at the summer wedding that opens the novel. Though handsome and possessing a soldier's bearing, his character is marked by moral weakness — a "pleasure-loving, vacillating nature" that the narrator signals from his first [[beauty-and-appearance|appearance]].

## Appearance

Tall and lithe in figure, with a nose as straight and eyes as "large and luminous" as his [[ida-culvers|bride]]'s (001-005). But the similarity ends there: "the receding chin and long, uncleft upper lip, which mark the pleasure-loving, vacillating nature, would at once have stood revealed" were it not for his concealing moustache (001-005).

## Role in the Novel

Sefton is [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s cousin and her new husband as of Chapter I. The marriage is described by wedding guests in transactional terms: "A very suitable marriage; she has the money, he will have the title" (001-006). This framing immediately establishes the marriage as something less than a love match, and Sefton's character as shaped by the expectations of his class and family.

## Character Traits

- **Pleasure-loving**: His "receding chin and long, uncleft upper lip" physically mark his nature (001-005)
- **Vacillating**: The narrator explicitly characterizes him as lacking moral firmness (001-005)
- **A product of his class**: A former officer in the Royal Hussars, he represents the Victorian military-gentry type whose financial needs drive the marriage transaction (001-005) (001-006)
