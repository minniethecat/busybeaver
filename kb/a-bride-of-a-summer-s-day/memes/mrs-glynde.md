# Mrs. Glynde

Mrs. Glynde is the mother of [[arthur-glynde|Arthur Glynde]] and the owner of [[glynde-lodge|Glynde Lodge]] in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Her generosity in lending her house for [[ida-wedding|Ida Culvers's wedding]] provides the novel's opening setting, and her matchmaking ambitions for her son make her a significant figure in the novel's social comedy.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Glynde first appears indirectly: [[glynde-lodge|Glynde Lodge]], "the house that had been lent to Lord Culvers for the wedding" (001-010), is her property, and she has left her "horses and carriages behind" for the occasion — a gesture of hospitality that [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] acknowledges as "kind" (002-017).

She appears in person in [[ch-vii]] when she pays an unexpected visit to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]]. Her ostensible purpose is to unburden herself of her "broken heart" — the distress of learning that [[arthur-glynde|Arthur]] has decided to go abroad — but her real purpose quickly reveals itself as matchmaking. She has "a reputation, which Juliet was not inclined to gainsay, of being one of the cleverest matchmakers that Society numbered in its ranks" (007-035).
> Mrs. Glynde's friends were thoroughly aware of the fact that at its lowest computation her age could not be far off fifty. Dress, however, and the use of toilet accessories, reduced it in appearance to about five-and-thirty.
>
> (009-015)


## Character Analysis

Mrs. Glynde is one of the novel's most vivid comic creations. At "its lowest computation her age could not be far off fifty," but "dress, however, and the use of toilet accessories, reduced it in appearance to about five-and-thirty" (007-017). She is described as "invariably lively and entertaining, and in manner sympathetic" (007-019) — a social performer whose artifice is so accomplished that it has become indistinguishable from her character.

Her conversation with [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] is a masterpiece of indirect suggestion. She comes to lament [[arthur-glynde|Arthur]]'s travel plans but stays to probe [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s intentions toward [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]]. Her matchmaking ambition — to secure an "heiress and a beauty" for her son (007-035) — aligns her with the novel's broader critique of marriage as a financial transaction.

Yet Mrs. Glynde is never merely mercenary. Her affection for [[arthur-glynde|Arthur]] is genuine, and her social skill — unlike the clumsier manipulations of [[lady-culvers|Lady Culvers]] — has a certain artistry that the novel regards with wry appreciation.
