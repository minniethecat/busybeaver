# The Newspaper Advertisement for the Brooch

The newspaper advertisement for [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s missing diamond brooch is a pivotal document in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Placed in the leading London and provincial journals, it elicits the response from [[father-baldwin|Father Baldwin]] that redirects the search for [[ida-culvers|Ida]] from England to Paris.

## Creation

The advertisement is the brainchild of [[joshua-redway|Joshua Redway]], who proposes it as a discreet way of tracing the brooch without alerting Society to [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance. It is placed through [[hunt-and-locke|Messrs. Hunt and Locke]] of Chancery Lane — a firm "noted for their successful conduct of complicated criminal cases" (011-003) rather than [[lord-george-culvers|Lord Culvers]]'s family lawyers, a choice that signals the transition from domestic matter to criminal investigation.

## Content and Function

The advertisement describes the brooch in terms specific enough to enable identification but general enough to avoid attracting attention: "a diamond brooch, answering in all respects to the one described in the advertisement in the daily papers" (012-001). Its strategic ambiguity — it could be an ordinary notice of lost property — is essential to the family's strategy of investigating without publicising.

## Consequences

The advertisement yields a response from [[father-baldwin|Father Baldwin]], the English priest at the [[carmelite-friars|Carmelite Friars]] church in the [[rue-bellarmine|Rue Bellarmine]], Paris. His letter reports that the brooch was dropped into the offertory bag during mass — a discovery that sets in motion [[lord-culvers-paris-journey|Lord Culvers's journey to Paris]], the [[prefect-of-police|Prefect of Police]]'s investigation, and ultimately the exposure of [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s scheme.

## Key Quotations

> Simultaneously with its publication in the London newspapers, the advertisement appeared in the principal Continental journals.
>
> (005-006)

## Significance

The advertisement is a small document with large consequences. It represents the convergence of [[joshua-redway|Joshua Redway]]'s commercial shrewdness (the strategic thinking of a financier applied to a family crisis), the professional competence of [[hunt-and-locke|Messrs. Hunt and Locke]] (criminal law brought to bear on a domestic mystery), and the conscientiousness of [[father-baldwin|Father Baldwin]] (religious duty generating secular evidence). In a novel about the failure of aristocratic institutions — family, marriage, inheritance — the advertisement is a triumph of competent, professional, bourgeois problem-solving.
