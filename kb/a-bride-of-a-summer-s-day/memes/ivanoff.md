# Ivanoff

Ivanoff is the Russian proprietor of the boarding-house at No. 15 [[rue-vervien|Rue Vervien]], Paris, in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. His establishment is [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s Paris residence and a centre of the novel's Parisian criminal demimonde.

## Role in the Story

Ivanoff never appears directly in the narrative but is referenced in a letter as the keeper of the house where [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] lodged. The house at 15 Rue Vervien is described as "not bearing a very good reputation" — "kept by a Russian — Ivanoff by name — and is the resort of all sorts of shady people, British and foreign — gamblers, sporting and betting-men, in fact of adventurers of every class" (012-015).

His establishment provides the setting for much of the novel's Parisian intrigue. [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] stakes out the Rue Vervien and observes the comings and goings of figures connected to [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s schemes from outside Ivanoff's door.

## Character Analysis

Ivanoff represents the cosmopolitan criminal infrastructure of late-nineteenth-century Paris — a world in which national boundaries blur and identities are provisional. His Russian name amidst the French setting, his boarding-house that shelters "British and foreign" adventurers, and his apparent indifference to the character of his tenants all mark him as a functionary of the demimonde rather than an active villain.

The fact that Ivanoff is Russian is significant: in the Victorian imagination, Russia often signified intrigue, secrecy, and moral opacity — associations that colour the Rue Vervien and everything connected with it. His name appears only once, but it carries the weight of an entire shadow economy.


> "The house where Captain Culvers has taken up his abode No. 15, Rue Vervien—does not bear a very good reputation. It is kept by a Russian—Ivanoff by name—and is the resort of all sorts of shady people, British and foreign—gamblers, sporting and betting-men, in fact of adventurers of every class."
