# Rue Vervien

The Rue Vervien is a Paris street in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day* whose No. 15 serves as [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s Paris residence and the headquarters of his schemes.

## Location Type

The Rue Vervien is a "quiet, old-fashioned thoroughfare, with tall but irregularly-built houses, that were evidently occupied by tenants of various degrees of social rank" (013-018). It is reached from the Avenue de l'Opéra, "down a narrow street lined on either side with cafés and restaurants," then "off this street at right angles" (013-017).

## Description

No. 15 Rue Vervien is the boarding-house kept by the Russian [[ivanoff|Ivanoff]], described as not bearing "a very good reputation" — "the resort of all sorts of shady people, British and foreign — gamblers, sporting and betting-men, in fact of adventurers of every class" (012-015).

[[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] makes a reconnaissance of the street after learning [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s address. Standing opposite No. 15, he observes an Italian [[organ-boy|organ-boy]] with a monkey grinding a "doleful melody" (013-022) — the same figure who had delivered [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s note at [[glynde-lodge|Glynde Lodge]]. This coincidence confirms his suspicions about the house and its principal tenant.

The Rue Vervien functions as the novel's symbolic centre of moral corruption — a Parisian address whose quiet exterior belies the schemes transacted within. It is the antithesis of [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]]: where the English estate represents inherited order, the Rue Vervien represents the chaotic, international, and morally unmoored world that [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] has chosen.


> "By the way, I am told on good authority, that the house where Captain Culvers has taken up his abode No. 15, Rue Vervien—does not bear a very good reputation. It is kept by a Russian—Ivanoff by name—and is the resort of all sorts of shady people, British and foreign—gamblers, sporting and betting-men, in fact of adventurers of every class."
