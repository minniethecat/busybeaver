# Clive Redway

Clive Redway is the affianced lover of [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] in *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Son of the methodical [[mr-redway|Mr. Redway]], Clive combines his father's earnestness with a romantic devotion that marks him as one of the novel's most sympathetic figures.

## Role in the Story

Clive Redway's central narrative function is to provide the romantic counterpoint to [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]]'s disastrous marriage. Where [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] marries for money and brings misery, Clive loves sincerely and is rewarded—though not without a long period of uncertainty. His engagement to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], announced early in the novel, is tested by [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s reluctance to set a wedding date and by the upheaval surrounding [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance.

Clive also serves as an emotional barometer for the novel's [[event-index|events]]. His "impatient movement" (001-050) and "gesture of annoyance" (001-040) in the opening chapter signal the tension beneath [[ida-wedding|the wedding]]'s surface gaiety, while his enduring devotion across the years of separation testifies to the novel's faith in patient, steadfast love.

## Character Analysis

### Traits

Clive Redway is characterised by earnestness, loyalty, and a certain impatience born of frustrated devotion. His "gesture of annoyance" (001-040) and "impatient movement" (001-050) in the opening chapter suggest a man whose emotions run close to the surface—a contrast to his father's reserve. Yet his love for [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] is unwavering; even as she deflects his questions [[About|about]] their wedding date and flirts with the idea of indefinite engagement, Clive remains steadfast.

His entrance "in the easy, familiar fashion of one who was quite at home" (005-032) in the [[culvers-family|Culvers household]] signals his integration into the family—a belonging that is simultaneously a source of comfort and a source of frustration, as proximity to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] without full commitment becomes increasingly painful.

### Development

Clive's arc is one of endurance rewarded. The long period between the opening wedding and the closing resolution tests his patience to its limit—[[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] herself raises the "responsibility of ruining Clive Redway's health and happiness" (024-054)—but his constancy is ultimately vindicated. His final [[beauty-and-appearance|appearance]] under the beeches (024-063) brings his narrative full circle: the impatient lover of Chapter I has become the patient, waiting figure whose reward is at hand.

## Historical / Social Context

Clive Redway represents the Victorian ideal of romantic love within the framework of a suitable match. His father's professional standing and the [[culvers-family|Culvers family]]'s titled status place the engagement within the era's pattern of inter-class marriage—the Redways bring competence and earnestness to an alliance with a family that has title but needs money. Clive's sincere attachment to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], however, elevates the match above mere calculation, embodying the Victorian novel's faith that love and social compatibility can—and should—coincide.
