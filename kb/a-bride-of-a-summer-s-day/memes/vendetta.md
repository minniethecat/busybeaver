# Vendetta

Vendetta — the Calabrian tradition of blood-feud and retributive violence — is a menacing background force in the later chapters of the novel, representing the lawless world into which [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] has placed herself through her association with [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s Italian past.

## Clive Redway's Warning

The concept is most explicitly articulated by [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] when he warns [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] of the dangers her sister faces in [[alta-lauria|Alta Lauria]]. He describes a community "glad of any excuse to execute a vendetta" and insists that vendetta is "far from being a thing of the past in Italy" — noting that in some places "the authorities take no notice whatever of its perpetration." (021-012) His warning frames the vendetta as both a genuine physical threat and an indictment of the weakness of state authority in the Italian south.

> "To my mind," he said, gloomily, "fever is the very least of her dangers. Supposing that you had offended past forgiveness, not one person only, but a whole community, a set of lawless, insolent, ill-conditioned people, glad of any excuse to execute a vendetta — a vendetta, believe me, is far from being a thing of the past in Italy, in some places the authorities take no notice whatever of its perpetration."
>
> (021-012)

## The Vendetta Threat Against Sefton

The vendetta is embodied in the character of [[giorno|Giorno Xardez]], the eldest son of [[francesca-xardez|Francesca Xardez]], whose fellow vinedressers pooled their resources to send him across the sea to England. Giorno's deep bass voice delivers the threat: "If she dies let him look out — that's all." (019-043) The threat is directed at [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]], whose betrayal of [[violante|Violante da Nava]] — proposing marriage and then abandoning her — has offended the entire community.

The vendetta thus functions as a form of communal justice outside the law: Sefton's offense is not merely against one person but against a community that has its own methods of redress.

## Retributive Justice

The theme of retribution extends beyond the Italian vendetta. Earlier in the novel, [[sefton-culvers|Sefton]] himself reflects on the satisfaction of "retributive justice" — the act of paying back a wrong, which "seemed to him sweeter than" ordinary pleasure. (011-030) This parallel suggests that the impulse toward vengeance is not confined to Calabrian peasants but operates, in more refined forms, within the English aristocracy as well.
