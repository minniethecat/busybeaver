# Ida and Clive's Reunion

The reunion of [[ida-culvers|Ida Culvers]] with [[clive-redway|Clive Redway]] marks the emotional resolution of *A [[ida-culvers|Bride]] of a Summer's Day*. Throughout the novel, [[clive-redway|Clive]] — officially engaged to [[juliet-culvers|Juliet Culvers]] — has been portrayed as a man whose deeper loyalty lies with [[ida-culvers|Ida]], the woman who "threw herself away" on [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]] (001-041).

## Timeline

- **Preceded by**: [[sefton-culvers|Sefton Culvers]]'s death]], which releases [[ida-culvers|Ida]] from the marriage
- **Followed by**: The quiet wedding — the novel's final domestic settlement
- **Narrative time**: The closing chapters, after the Italian tragedy has run its course

## Location

The reunion takes place at [[dering-hall|Dering Hall]], the [[culvers-family|Culvers family]] seat. After the displacements of [[london|London]], [[paris|Paris]], and Italy, the return to [[dering-hall|Dering]] signifies restoration — the family reconstituted on its ancestral ground.

## Narrative Summary

The reunion reverses the novel's opening trajectory. Where the [[ida-wedding|wedding at Hastings]] separated [[ida-culvers|Ida]] from her family — sending her into a marriage that became a disappearance — the reunion brings her back. [[ida-culvers|Ida]], who had returned from [[florence|Florence]] to England before [[ida-wedding|the wedding]] under mysterious circumstances (001-047), now returns definitively.

[[clive-redway|Clive]]'s role throughout has been ambiguous. At [[ida-wedding|the wedding]] breakfast, he questioned [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]] insistently [[About|about]] the circumstances of [[ida-wedding|Ida's marriage]]: "What was it brought [[ida-culvers|Ida]] home from [[florence|Florence]] in such a hurry, and made her accept Sefton?" (001-047). His impatience with [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]]'s evasions — "[[clive-redway|Clive]] interrupted her impatiently" (002-036) — suggests an investment in [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s fate that exceeds brotherly concern. The reunion resolves this submerged attachment within the proprieties of the Victorian novel's marriage-plot conventions.

## Plot Significance

The reunion is the novel's emotional payoff. While [[sefton-culvers-death|Sefton's death]] provides the legal resolution — severing the marriage bond — the reunion provides the emotional one, restoring [[ida-culvers|Ida]] to the family circle and resolving the triangulated affections among [[ida-culvers|Ida]], [[juliet-culvers|Juliet]], and [[clive-redway|Clive]].

The event also completes the novel's pattern of departure and return. [[ida-culvers|Ida]]'s disappearance at [[hastings|Hastings]] was a vanishing into marriage; her reunion at [[dering-hall|Dering]] is a return to self. The [[twin-bond|twin bond]] between [[twin-bond|Ida and Juliet]] — strained throughout by the secret of [[ida-wedding|Ida's marriage]] and the divided loyalties it created — is reaffirmed.
