Artistic identity in *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* centers on the protagonist's lifelong negotiation between her identity as a painter and the demands placed on her as a woman. Sigrid Undset explores how creative ambition conflicts with romantic love, motherhood, and societal expectations in early 20th-century Europe.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Through Jenny

[[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] defines herself first and foremost as a painter. Her decision to live in [[rome|Rome]] and pursue art rather than settle into a conventional Norwegian life is the founding act of her adult identity. Throughout the novel, she measures herself against her artistic ideals, questioning whether she has the talent and discipline to be a true artist.

The tension between art and love is dramatized most sharply in her relationship with [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]]. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] initially resists romantic entanglement, recognizing it as a threat to her independence. When she does fall in love, the relationship destabilizes her creative focus and sense of self.

### Through Other Characters

[[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] represents the possibility of a balanced identity — a writer who takes his work seriously without sacrificing his humanity. Cesca Jahrman offers a contrasting model of feminine artistic identity, more pragmatic and less tormented than [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]. Helge's father embodies the path not taken — the artist who gave up his dream for domesticity.

### Narrative Arc

In Part I, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s artistic identity appears secure and confident. Part II, set in Norway, subjects her identity to the pressure of family expectations and conventional morality. Part III shows her attempting to rebuild her artistic life after tragedy, but the earlier confidence is gone — replaced by a harder-won but more fragile sense of purpose.
