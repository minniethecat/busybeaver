The Christmas celebration at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] is the novel's only holiday scene in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911), occurring in Part I Chapter VI. The Scandinavian expatriate artist community gathers at [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] and [[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Jahrman]]'s shared studio for a Christmas Eve that is simultaneously a faithful recreation of Norwegian tradition and an acknowledgment of displacement — the rituals of home transplanted to a Roman setting where they can never feel quite authentic. The celebration provides the most complete group portrait of the novel's artist circle and marks a pivotal moment in the deepening intimacy between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge.

## Narrative Context

### The Artist Circle

By Chapter VI, [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]] has become a regular presence in the group that orbits around [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Cesca's studio at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] 111 (003-071). The circle includes [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]], the Norwegian writer; [[ahlin]], the Swedish sculptor; [[lennart]], the Swedish painter; and a rotating cast of other Scandinavian artists and intellectuals living in [[rome|Rome]]. The studio serves as the social hub — "As a rule they were both in" (006-003) when visitors called — and it is natural that the group would gather there for Christmas rather than each celebrating alone in rented rooms.

### The Significance of Displacement

The Christmas celebration foregrounds the central tension of expatriate life: the effort to recreate home in a place that is not home. The characters are all displaced — Norwegians, Danes, and Swedes living in [[rome|Rome]] — and the holiday, with its powerful associations of family and national tradition, sharpens that sense of distance from their origins.

## The Celebration

### Christmas Eve at the Club and Midnight Mass

The evening begins outside the studio. "On Christmas Eve they all went to the club, and afterwards to the midnight mass at S. Luigi de Franchesi" (006-010). The choice of S. Luigi de Franchesi — the French national church in [[rome|Rome]], near Piazza Navona — reflects the cosmopolitan character of the community: they attend a French church for a Catholic mass that none of them, as Scandinavians, would have celebrated in quite this way at home. The evening is thus a hybrid of borrowed traditions.

### The Norwegian Christmas Table

After the mass, the group returns to [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]], where "the table was laid with Norwegian Christmas fare, decorated with daisies from the [[campagna|Campagna]] and candles in seven-branched candlesticks" (006-024). The juxtaposition is striking and deliberate: Norwegian food traditions maintained with fidelity, but decorated with Roman daisies rather than the spruce and holly of a northern Christmas. The seven-branched candlesticks add a further layer — an element more associated with Jewish tradition or Italian church decor than with Scandinavian Lutheranism, hinting at the syncretic nature of expatriate cultural practice.

### Ambivalence and the "Christmas Feeling"

The celebration is shadowed by ambivalence. One of the guests articulates the central doubt: "This gives you any sort of real Christmas feeling, do you? It's like an imitation Christmas" (006-011). The question hangs over the gathering — can a Christmas celebrated in [[rome|Rome]], with daisies instead of snow and a French mass instead of a Lutheran service, ever feel real? The scene captures the expatriate condition in miniature: the effort to maintain identity through ritual, and the persistent sense that something essential has been lost in translation.

### Christmas Morning Reflections

The celebration extends into the early hours. "That it is Christmas morning. I wonder if they got home safely" (006-066) — the reflection on family back in Norway introduces a note of homesickness and separation. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] recalls childhood Christmases: "It was always so nice on Christmas morning to go in and look at the presents" (006-068). The memory connects the Roman celebration to a Norwegian past, even as it emphasizes the distance between them.

The evening concludes with "Good night, and a happy Christmas to you, Gunnar" (006-070) — a quiet, almost solemn valediction that closes the celebration on a note of tenderness rather than revelry.

## Aftermath

### Jenny and Helge's Deepening Bond

The Christmas gathering strengthens the bond between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and [[helge-gram|Helge]]. In the days following, "during Christmas week Gram went into a trattoria" (007-001) where the social dynamics continue to evolve. The shared celebration has created new intimacy within the group and new expectations. Helge's presence at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] becomes increasingly regular: "he was almost a daily visitor now at the [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]]; he could not help it" (007-007).

### Group Dynamics and Foreshadowing

The Christmas scene also reveals the fault lines within the artist community. Cesca's complicated romantic history, Gunnar's emotional reserve, [[ahlin|Ahlin]]'s intensity, and [[lennart|Lennart]]'s detachment are all on display. The warmth of the celebration cannot entirely mask the individual lonelinesses that characterize each member of the circle — lonelinesses that will drive the novel's subsequent events.

## Analysis

### Narrative Function

Undset uses the Christmas celebration as a structural device: it is the novel's single group scene in which all the major characters are present simultaneously. It functions as a tableau of the artist community at its most cohesive, before the centrifugal forces of individual desire, jealousy, and tragedy begin to pull it apart. The placement in Chapter VI — the midpoint of Part I — is strategic: it consolidates the character relationships established in the opening chapters and prepares the ground for the romantic intensification that follows.

### Thematic Significance

The scene develops the novel's preoccupation with authenticity and performance. The characters are performing Norwegianness — the food, the rituals, the memories — in a setting that makes that performance visible as performance. The question "does this give you any sort of real Christmas feeling?" (006-011) resonates beyond the holiday: the entire expatriate life is, in a sense, an imitation of life — a borrowed existence in a borrowed city, sustained by rituals that can never quite bridge the gap between [[rome|Rome]] and home.

### Cultural Hybridity

The Christmas at Via Vantaggio is a study in cultural hybridity: Norwegian food on an Italian table, decorated with Roman flowers, following a French Catholic mass. The scene captures the specific texture of expatriate Scandinavian life in early twentieth-century [[rome|Rome]] — neither fully Norwegian nor fully Italian, but something new and unstable, created in the space between.
