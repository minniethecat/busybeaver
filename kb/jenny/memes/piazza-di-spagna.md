The Piazza di Spagna — the piazza at the foot of the Spanish Steps in [[rome|Rome]] — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a familiar landmark in the expatriate artists' mental map of the city. Located near both [[via-condotti|Via Condotti]] and [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]], the Piazza di Spagna anchors the neighborhood where [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]], [[cesca-jahrman|Cesca Jahrman]], and their circle live and work.

## In the Novel

The piazza is first mentioned as a directional reference, establishing its role in the characters' spatial orientation:

> I know it is not far from the Piazza di Spagna.

(002-185)

This casual reference — spoken by one character to another — reveals the piazza as a shared point of reference, a landmark everyone knows. It reappears in Part III at a moment of tension:

> They had reached the Piazza di Spagna when she stopped.

(029-023)

The pause at the piazza — the stopping mid-movement at a familiar place — marks a shift in the conversation or emotional register, the square serving as a natural punctuation point in the characters' perambulations.

## Key Scenes

The Piazza di Spagna functions less as a scene-setting location than as a node in the characters' movement through [[rome|Rome]]:

1. **The landmark reference (Part I)**: "Not far from the Piazza di Spagna" — the piazza is invoked to locate another place, demonstrating its status as a primary coordinate in the artists' Roman geography.
2. **The pause (Part III)**: Reaching the piazza, a character stops — the familiar square becoming the site of an emotional threshold, a place where forward motion halts and something must be said.

## Significance

The Piazza di Spagna operates as geographical infrastructure rather than a symbolic landscape:

- **The artists' neighborhood**: Unlike the [[campagna|Campagna]] (a destination requiring an excursion) or the [[colosseum|Colosseum]] (a monument of the ancient city), the Piazza di Spagna is part of the daily environment — passed through, not visited.
- **A coordinate of belonging**: Knowing that something is "not far from the Piazza di Spagna" is a mark of insider knowledge, the kind of spatial fluency that separates the expatriate residents from the tourists.
- **The pause as structure**: The piazza's reappearance in Part III — "they had reached the Piazza di Spagna when she stopped" — uses the familiar to frame the unexpected. The known place becomes the site of an unknown conversation.

In the real [[rome|Rome]] of the early 20th century, the area around the Spanish Steps was indeed the heart of the foreign artists' quarter — the very neighborhood Sigrid Undset herself inhabited during her Roman sojourn of 1909–1910.
