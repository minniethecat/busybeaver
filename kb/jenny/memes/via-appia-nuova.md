The Via Appia Nuova — the "New Appian Way" extending southeast from [[rome|Rome]] toward the [[campagna|Campagna]] — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as the setting for one of the novel's key social gatherings: [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s birthday dinner. The small *osteria* on this road becomes the stage for a turning point in several characters' relationships.

## Jenny's Birthday Dinner

The central scene on Via Appia Nuova occurs in Part I, Chapter VII:

> Jenny's birthday was on the seventeenth of January. She and Francesca were having a dinner-party in the Campagna, in a small *osteria* in the Via Appia Nuova. Ahlin, Heggen, Gram, and Miss Palm, the Danish nurse, made up the party.

(007-027)

The guest list brings together the core Scandinavian circle: [[ahlin|Ahlin]] the Swedish sculptor, [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] the Norwegian writer, [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]] the Danish-Norwegian archaeologist, [[cesca-jahrman|Cesca Jahrman]] the Norwegian painter, and [[miss-palm|Miss Palm]], a Danish nurse.

## The Walk on the Hill

After the dinner, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge separate from the group:

> The *osteria* lay at the foot of a small hill. Gram and Jenny walked up the slope at random. She picked small wild flowers that grew in the yellow earth.

(007-031)

This walk away from the group marks a shift in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge's relationship — a private moment carved out of a social occasion, where their connection deepens beyond the group dynamic.

When they return, they find the party changed:

> They returned to the *osteria* in silence; on the slope, which stretched right down to where they had been lunching, they noticed that Ahlin was lying forward over the table, his head on his arms. Francesca in her bright green gown bent over him, her arms round his neck, trying to lift his head.

(007-047)

The juxtaposition is striking: [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge's private walk toward intimacy, while [[ahlin|Ahlin]] collapses at the table — a moment of emotional exposure that Cesca must manage alone.

## Helge Transformed

The Via Appia Nuova dinner is also the occasion for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] to observe a change in Helge:

> As they walked back to the *osteria*, Helge chatted merrily about all sorts of things, telling her about the learned Germans he had met in the course of his work. Jenny stole a glance at him now and again; he used not to be like that, so free and easy. He was really handsome as he walked, looking straight ahead, and his light brown eyes were radiant like amber in the sun.

(007-056)

The Roman setting — the road, the hill, the sunlight — becomes inseparable from Helge's transformation in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s eyes. He is no longer the awkward newcomer of Chapter I; he is "free and easy," radiant.

## The Osteria as Social Space

The *osteria* on Via Appia Nuova is part of a broader pattern in the novel: the roadside tavern as the expatriates' social space outside the city. Throughout *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]*, osterias in the [[campagna|Campagna]] serve as destinations for meals, conversations, and emotional revelations — places where the constraints of Roman urbanity loosen and characters reveal more of themselves. The Via Appia Nuova osteria, with its view of the hill and the small wild flowers, embodies this function at a critical narrative juncture.
