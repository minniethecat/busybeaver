Wergelandsveien — a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] (present-day Oslo), running near the Royal Palace and the Castle gardens — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as the location of the Winge family home and the site of [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s melancholy return to Norway.

## Jenny's Return

The street enters the novel at the beginning of Part II, as [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] arrives back in Christiania after her years in [[rome|Rome]]:

> Outside a garden gate in Wergelandsveien a young couple stood kissing each other good night. A few stars twinkled in the clear deep blue sky above the naked trees in the Castle gardens. A smell of mouldering leaves came through the carriage window, reminding her of melancholy springs of old.

(012-015)

The image is carefully layered: the kissing couple outside the garden gate — a vision of romantic intimacy that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] herself has known in [[rome|Rome]] — contrasts with the "naked trees" and the "smell of mouldering leaves." The "melancholy springs of old" that the scent evokes signal that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s return to Norway is not a homecoming but a regression, a return to a place associated with earlier, less formed versions of herself.

## Significance

Wergelandsveien's brief appearance carries considerable symbolic weight:

- **The geography of return**: After the sunlit streets of [[rome|Rome]] — the [[corso|Corso]], [[via-condotti|Via Condotti]], [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] — Wergelandsveien represents the narrower, colder world of Christiania, where [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] must re-encounter her family and her past.
- **Romance observed from outside**: [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] watches the couple kissing from inside a carriage — she is no longer a participant in the romantic life of the streets, but an observer, separated by glass and by the weight of her Roman experiences.
- **The Castle gardens**: The proximity to the Royal Palace gardens frames the scene within respectable, bourgeois Christiania — the world of "decent" family life that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s mother embodies and that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]], after her expatriate freedom, finds suffocating.
