The expatriate experience is the novel's social framework. Nearly all major characters are Scandinavians living in [[rome|Rome]], and their distance from home shapes their behavior, relationships, and sense of identity. Undset, herself a Norwegian who spent significant time in [[rome|Rome]], depicts expatriate life with both affection and clear-eyed realism.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Rome as Refuge

For [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and her circle, [[rome|Rome]] represents escape from the constraints of Norwegian society. Here they can be artists, live unconventionally, and form relationships outside the scrutiny of family and hometown. The city itself — its light, its history, its foreignness — becomes a character in the novel.

### The Cost of Distance

Expatriate life is not purely liberating. Distance from family creates guilt and obligation ([[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s mother, Helge's father). Cultural dislocation leaves the characters without a stable social identity. When crises arise — [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s pregnancy, Helge's breakdown — the expatriate community provides support but not the institutional safety net of home.

### Return to Norway

Part II's Norwegian setting functions as a counterpoint. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s return to her family highlights how much she has changed — and how little Norway has. The expatriate identity, once a source of pride, becomes a source of alienation when she is back among people who cannot understand her life.
