Motherhood in *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* is portrayed as a profound, transformative, and ultimately devastating experience. Sigrid Undset examines pregnancy, birth, and maternal love through [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s journey across Parts II and III, contrasting her fierce attachment to her child with the impossibility of sustaining both motherhood and the artistic life she has built. The novel's refusal to sentimentalize motherhood — to present it as either purely redemptive or purely tragic — gives the theme its disturbing power.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Jenny as Mother

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s pregnancy is revealed during her return to Norway in Part II, after the dissolution of her relationship with [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]]. She initially experiences motherhood as a source of meaning and connection, a new center for a life that has lost its romantic anchor. The child becomes, for a time, the organizing purpose of her existence.

But Undset is unsparing about the material conditions. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is unmarried, financially precarious, and an artist — circumstances that place her outside the protective structures of bourgeois motherhood. Her love for her child is genuine, but the cost of raising a child alone in early 20th-century Norway — without a husband's income, without institutional support, without the social legitimacy marriage confers — is overwhelming.

### The Child's Death

The death of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s child is the novel's structural turning point and emotional nadir. It shatters the meaning [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] had constructed around motherhood and precipitates her psychological unraveling. The child's death is not sensationalized — Undset's restraint makes it all the more devastating:

> She could not bear to think about it.

(Paraphrase of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s internal state after the loss, Part III)

Having lost her only remaining source of purpose, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is unable to find meaning in art, in love, or in life. The child's death transforms motherhood from identity into absence.

### Contrasting Models of Motherhood

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s own mother, [[mrs-berner|Mrs. Berner]], represents a more conventional model of motherhood — dutiful, self-sacrificing, bound by necessity rather than fulfillment. The domestic world of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s Norwegian home, where "the children had to be fed before anything else" (003-035), stands in stark contrast to [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s artist's life in [[rome|Rome]]. Undset offers both models sympathetically but without resolution: neither conventional nor artistic motherhood proves sustainable for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]].
