Frascati — a town in the Alban Hills southeast of [[rome|Rome]], famous for its white wine — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a dining destination debated among the expatriate artist community. Unlike the [[campagna|Campagna]] or [[ponte-molle|Ponte Molle]], which are sites of intimate encounter, Frascati represents the social dimension of the artists' Roman life: the shared meals, the group excursions, and the tensions that surface in company.

## In the Novel

Frascati enters the novel in Part I during a conversation about where to dine. The suggestion provokes immediate resistance from [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]:

> I am not in the mood for Frascati tonight.

(001-074)

Her refusal is categorical and unreasoned — she is simply not in the mood — capturing the casual way the artists' group negotiated their social plans. The exchange escalates:

> Well, not Frascati anyway — ugh! sit there and mope.

(001-076)

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s reaction — "ugh!" — goes beyond mere preference into something closer to revulsion, hinting that Frascati, for her, is associated not with pleasure but with a particular emotional atmosphere she wishes to avoid.

## Key Scenes

### The Frascati Debate

[[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] pursues the topic with teasing persistence:

> I would have been obliged to go to Frascati, to which you seem to have taken such a violent dislike.

(002-023)

And [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] fires back:

> I would not have gone to Frascati, you may be sure of that, Gunnar.

(002-024)

The exchange reveals the dynamic of the group — teasing, performative, and charged with unspoken tensions. Frascati, as a destination, becomes a site of negotiation rather than a simple restaurant choice.

## Significance

Frascati's role in the novel is modest but revealing:

- **Social negotiation**: The debate over whether to go to Frascati exposes the group's decision-making dynamic — a blend of affection, irritation, and performance.
- **[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s agency**: Her firm refusal ("you may be sure of that") demonstrates her unwillingness to be dragged into social obligations she doesn't want, a small assertion of the independence that is a central theme of her character.
- **The underside of expatriate life**: While [[rome|Rome]] offers freedom and artistic community, the Frascati debate shows the friction inherent in constant togetherness — the "moping" [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] associates with the trip suggests that not all shared experiences among the artists were joyful.

In the geography of *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]*, Frascati occupies an ambiguous position: proposed, debated, and ultimately — in the recorded scenes — not visited. It exists as a possibility rather than an experience, a reminder that the novel's [[rome|Rome]] includes not just the places the characters go but also the places they refuse.
