Stazione Termini — [[rome|Rome]]'s central railway station — appears in the opening chapter of Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a destination [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]] cannot find. Lost in the streets of an unfamiliar city on his first night in [[rome|Rome]], he encounters two Norwegian-speaking women who direct him — and in doing so, set the entire plot in motion.

## In the Novel

Termini enters the novel through Helge's disorientation. He has arrived from Florence that day and wandered away from the station without orienting himself. Now, in the evening, he needs to find his way back:

> But those cars don't go to the Termini.

(001-045)

The line is spoken by a young girl — one of two Norwegian women Helge has overheard on the street. Her correction of her companion's suggestion initiates the conversation that leads to [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] giving Helge directions.

> Those that have San Pietro, stazione Termini, written on them.

(001-046)

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s practical instruction — look for trams marked with the destination — is the first thing she ever says that Helge understands, and it reveals her competence in the city where he is lost. The exchange continues:

> The tram marked San Pietro, stazione Termini, No. 1.

(001-050)

## Key Scenes

The Termini scene functions as the novel's catalyst:

1. **Helge's arrival**: He has come from Florence that morning by train, but Termini is not described — it is the place he left without noticing, and now cannot find his way back to.
2. **The overheard conversation**: Helge recognizes Norwegian being spoken and lingers near the two women, hoping for help.
3. **The directions**: [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s clear, practical instruction about the tram contrasts with Helge's helplessness and establishes their initial dynamic — she knows [[rome|Rome]], he does not.

## Significance

Termini serves several functions in the novel's architecture:

- **The threshold of [[rome|Rome]]**: As the point of entry and exit, Termini represents the boundary between the familiar ([[christiania|Christiania]], Helge's Danish-Norwegian world) and the unknown (expatriate [[rome|Rome]], the artist community, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]).
- **Disorientation and guidance**: Helge's inability to find Termini on his own is the first sign of his broader passivity and dependence — a character trait that will shape his entire relationship with [[jenny-winge|Jenny]].
- **The comic meet-cute**: The scene has a light, almost comedic quality — a lost tourist, a chance encounter, a tram number — that the novel's later darkness will retrospectively color with irony.

Without Termini — and Helge's inability to find it — the meeting between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and [[helge-gram|Helge]] would not have occurred in the same way. The station is thus not merely a setting but a narrative mechanism, the hinge on which the plot turns.
