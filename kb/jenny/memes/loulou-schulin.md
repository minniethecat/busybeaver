Loulou Schulin is a minor Danish artist in the Scandinavian expatriate circle of [[rome|Rome]]. She appears primarily in Part III, Chapter XXIX, during a late-night café scene that reveals the social dynamics and undercurrents of the artist community. Her character serves as both comic relief and a cautionary figure — a woman who has made unconventional choices and paid a social price for them.

## Role in the Novel

Schulin makes her principal appearance during a raucous late-night gathering of Scandinavian artists and a few Germans in a [[rome|Rome]] café. She sits next to [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] and competes with Dr. Broager for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s attention, alternately protecting [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] from Broager's advances and inserting herself into the role of companion and defender:

> Loulou Schulin leaned against Jenny, stroking her arms. "Now I will take care of these poor, beautiful hands. Look!" She lifted one of them to be admired by the company round the table. (029-008)

Her background is introduced with terse narrative efficiency: she had been briefly married to a Norwegian author, left both husband and child after about a year, and re-entered the world under her maiden name, styling herself "Miss" and an artist. This biographical detail hangs over her presence in the scene, lending weight to what might otherwise read as merely tipsy frivolity:

> As to Miss Schulin, she had been married to a Norwegian author, but after a year or so of married bliss had left him and the child, gone out into the world under her maiden name as "Miss," and calling herself an artist. (029-012)

Schulin also receives an indirect mention in Chapter XXX, where it is noted that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] had promised to visit her to view her paintings — a detail that suggests she possesses some artistic seriousness despite her frivolous presentation.

## Character Analysis

Schulin functions on two levels in the narrative. On the surface, she is a minor comic figure — "the little Danish artist" who says "Ugh!" at male advances and wraps [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s hands in a green motor veil as an improvised mosquito net against Dr. Broager. Her diminutive physicality ("little") emphasizes her role as a foil to the larger, more consequential figures around her.

On a deeper level, Schulin represents a path [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] might take — or is in danger of taking. When [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] warns [[jenny-winge|Jenny]], "I would not like to see you come down and end as something like Loulou" (029-056), he articulates the novel's underlying anxiety about women who abandon conventional roles. Schulin has left a marriage and a child, and the narrative treats this not as liberation but as a kind of social death in life — the "Miss" in quotation marks, the "artist" designation given without conviction. She is what [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] might become if her drinking, her late nights, and her detachment from social norms accelerate.

## Key Relationships

- **[[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]** — Friend and companion in [[rome|Rome]]'s nightlife. Schulin is both protective of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] (fending off Broager) and a participant in the dissolute atmosphere Heggen deplores.
- **[[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]]** — The sober observer who uses Schulin as a negative example when cautioning [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] about her lifestyle.
- **Norwegian author (ex-husband)** — Briefly referenced. The failed marriage and abandoned child are the defining biographical facts of Schulin's pre-[[rome|Rome]] life.

## Notable Passages

> "Ugh!" said Loulou, shrugging her shoulders. "How can you stand it? Don't you think he is disgusting, Jenny?" (029-004)

Schulin's characteristic interjection — comic, exaggerated, performatively fastidious — establishes her voice in the narrative.

> By the light of the moon he saw Miss Schulin taking Jenny's arm, both running towards a cab, which some of the others were storming. (029-014)

The image of Schulin and [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] running together toward the cab captures their shared momentum in the late-night scene — two women in a group of revelers, bound by proximity and alcohol rather than deep friendship.
