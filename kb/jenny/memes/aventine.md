The Aventine Hill — one of the seven hills of [[rome|Rome]] — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a place where the protagonist paints and later returns to with [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]]. It serves as a site of artistic production in Part I and becomes a charged landscape of memory in Part III.

## In the Novel

The Aventine appears first as a painting location: [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] brings [[helge-gram|Helge]] there for breakfast after an early morning encounter, establishing it as part of their shared geography of [[rome|Rome]]. The hill's elevated views and ancient ruins make it a natural subject for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s painterly eye.

> He went with me to the Aventine and had breakfast; then he went home to get ready for the office.

(004-080)

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s artistic engagement with the Aventine extends beyond casual sketching — she produces a finished work of the hill, one that attracts commercial interest:

> You have got a bid for the Aventine picture already — are you going to sell it?

(021-013)

The "Aventine picture" becomes a marker of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s professional success, a tangible outcome of her Roman period.

## Key Scenes

### The Painting

The Aventine sketch occupies a special place in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s body of work. She dates it precisely to a significant moment:

> The sketch of the Aventine was finished the day I met Helge.

(021-017)

This temporal anchoring — the painting completed on the day she met Helge — gives the Aventine piece a biographical weight beyond its aesthetic merits. It stands as a document of the moment before her life changed.

### Return in Memory

In Part III, the Aventine resurfaces not as a physical location but as a memory invoked in conversation:

> Do you remember that evening in Rome, when we were at Aventine?

(032-085)

The question, posed in the novel's final chapters, transforms the hill from a painting site into a site of shared memory — one of the places where [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge's relationship was built, now irretrievable.

## Significance

The Aventine functions in two registers:

- **Artistic production**: As a subject for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s painting, it represents her professional identity and the creative productivity of her Roman period.
- **Romantic memory**: The hill's association with Helge — from the morning breakfast to the fateful coincidence of the painting's completion date — makes it one of several Roman landmarks (alongside [[monte-mario|Monte Mario]] and the [[campagna|Campagna]]) that become indices of the relationship's rise and fall.

The Aventine scene also demonstrates how place and art intertwine in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s life: the hill is not merely a backdrop but the subject of a painting whose commercial fate (the bid) and personal significance (painted the day she met Helge) intertwine.
