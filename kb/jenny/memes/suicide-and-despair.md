Suicide is the novel's devastating conclusion — and the culmination of the themes Undset has developed throughout. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s decision to end her life is not presented as a sudden impulse but as the logical endpoint of accumulated losses: her artistic confidence, her romantic hopes, and finally her child. The novel treats suicide with gravity and psychological realism rather than melodrama.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Accumulation of Loss

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s despair accumulates across the three parts. Each loss — her faith in love, her sense of artistic purpose, her child — removes a pillar of her identity. By Part III, she is hollowed out, unable to find meaning in art or human connection.

### Undset's Treatment

Undset portrays suicide not as romantic escape but as the exhaustion of hope. The novel's final scenes are quiet rather than dramatic — [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s decision emerges from a place of utter depletion rather than passionate despair.
