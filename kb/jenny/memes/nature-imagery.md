The natural landscape in *Jenny* functions as more than scenic background — it is an active emotional register, shifting with the protagonist's inner states and marking the transitions between the novel's three parts. Undset uses the contrast between Roman light and Norwegian severity, between urban gardens and wild mountains, to map Jenny Winge's psychological geography.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Roman Light: Part I and III

The novel opens with Helge Gram's arrival in Rome at sunset, the sky filled with "clouds all over the western sky, close together like small pale grey lambkins" as the sun "painted the borders of the clouds with a tinge of light" (001-004, 001-006). This luminous opening establishes Rome as a city of transformative light — a place where the Northern European characters are bathed in a radiance absent from their homeland.

The play of light becomes a recurring motif. Jenny's studio catches the Roman sun, and the streets she walks are crossed by "paths of light across the river" (001-017). The "faint light of the sky" outlines church domes and tree-lined streets (001-027). This luminous quality suffuses the characters' artistic lives — they are painters, after all, working in a city whose light has drawn artists for centuries.

### The Campagna and the Outskirts

The Roman Campagna — the countryside surrounding the city — provides a space of escape and contemplation. Jenny and Helge walk there, and the landscape mirrors their relationship's early, hopeful phase. The "Alban mountains in the distance, beyond the Campagna" (003-043) are described as distant, shimmering presences. The mountains "with small white villages" (007-029) appear mirage-like, "the mountains and the clouds melted together in a light blue haze" (007-036).

Yet the same landscape can shift to desolation. One walk takes them past "bare and desolate" mountains that "rise above" a ruined castle, a landscape that prefigures the emotional devastation to come (007-041).

### Norwegian Mountains: Part II

When the narrative moves to Norway in Part II, the landscape transforms entirely. The soft Roman light is replaced by snow-covered mountains, ski-runners, and winter clarity. Helge is described as "a well-known ski-runner and mountaineer in the Jotunheim" (008-023), grounding his Norwegian identity in the northern landscape. The mountains around Christiania are places of physical exertion and childhood memory — "tobogganing in the mountains" (006-068) — rather than aesthetic contemplation.

The Norwegian landscape appears with what Undset calls "the conspicuously clear and shiny" quality of northern nature, where "everything in nature seemed conspicuously clear" (012-002). This visual clarity parallels the emotional directness of Part II's domestic confrontations — no soft Roman haze to blur the edges of painful truth.

### Return to Roman Landscape: Part III

The novel's return to Rome in Part III brings back the landscape of light, but now suffused with Jenny's grief and isolation. The sky is "wide, ever restless" (026-023), the clouds traveling across it as Jenny's emotional state fragments. The city and "the church domes that seemed floating in the haze" (029-048) are now distant, disconnected from a protagonist who has retreated inward. The landscape that once promised freedom and artistic fulfillment now mirrors her emotional exhaustion.

## Key Passages

> Clouds all over the western sky, close together like small pale grey lambkins, and as the sun sank behind him it painted the borders of the clouds with a tinge of light. (001-004, 001-006)

> The Alban mountains in the distance, beyond the Campagna, promised to be warm. (003-043)

> The mountains, with small white villages, appeared a mirage only — the mountains and the clouds melted together in a light blue haze. (007-029, 007-036)

> And yet the mountains rise above it bare and desolate. (007-041)

> Everything in nature seemed conspicuously clear and shiny — the needles on the pines glistened. (012-002)

> The clouds travelled across the wide, ever restless sky. (026-023)
