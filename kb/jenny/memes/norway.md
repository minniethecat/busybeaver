# Norway

**Norway** is the Scandinavian homeland of Jenny Winge and the primary setting for Part II of Sigrid Undset's *Jenny* (1911).
The novel contrasts Norway's provincial culture and family obligations with the artistic freedom of Rome,
using the return journey as a narrative pivot for Jenny's personal reckoning.

## Role in the Novel

Norway serves as the gravitational pull away from expatriate life. When Jenny travels home in Part II,
she re-enters a world of familial duty, class expectations, and social conservatism that stands in
sharp relief against the bohemian circles of Rome.

Nils Berner, Jenny's brother-in-law, articulates the national mood with pride:

> "Now we are national up here in Norway. You know that the best praise they can give a man is to say that he has the national stamp upon him." [020-012]

Later, as Jenny faces motherhood alone, the option of staying in Norway becomes entangled with the question of independence:

> "You need not come back to Norway, nor even live under the same roof with me." [027-024]

## Norwegian Locations

Key Norwegian locations in the novel include **Christiania** (Oslo), the family home on **Wergelandsveien**,
the rural retreat at **Holmestrand**, the forested hills of **Nordmarken**, and the ski destination of **Holmenkollen**.
