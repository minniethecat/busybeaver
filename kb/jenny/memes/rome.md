Rome is the central setting of Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911), framing the novel's two outer sections: Part I (the expatriate idyll) and Part III (return and tragedy). The city functions as more than backdrop — it represents freedom, artistic possibility, and escape from the constraints of Norwegian bourgeois life. Undset's careful rendering of Rome's streets, landmarks, and landscapes grounds the novel in a specific early 20th-century geography while suffusing it with the city's ancient atmosphere.

## Geographic Context

Rome in *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* is not the Rome of tourists but the living city of a working artist community. The characters inhabit a network of interconnected spaces:

## The Expatriate Artist Community

Rome in Undset's rendering is a magnet for Scandinavian artists, writers, and intellectuals. They live modestly — renting rooms, sharing studios, gathering in cafes and trattorias. The community is multi-national: Norwegians ([[jenny-winge|Jenny]], Gunnar), Swedes ([[ahlin|Ahlin]], [[lennart|Lennart]]), Danes (Helge, through his father [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]]). This mix creates a cosmopolitan atmosphere in which national identities are both softened and sharpened.

The [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] studio — shared by [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Cesca — serves as the community's informal headquarters. Here, artists discuss work, share meals, and negotiate the personal entanglements that drive the novel's drama.

## Rome and Identity

For [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]], Rome represents the life she has chosen: the artist's life, free from the expectations of her Norwegian family and the suffocating conventions of bourgeois womanhood. Her return to Norway in Part II is, in effect, a departure from everything Rome allows her to be. Her return to Rome in Part III, now a mother, is shadowed by loss — the city that once meant freedom now holds only the remnants of a broken life.

## Key Passages

> In Rome the artists belong to a colony of their own — a collection of all the different nations. They are not the same as the women at home who have household cares and a husband and children who are their whole world.

(Paraphrase of novel's thematic content, Ch. I–III)

> She thought of the days in Rome, when she first arrived — how she had felt free.

(Paraphrase of novel's thematic content, Part II)
