Jenny Winge is a Norwegian painter living in [[rome|Rome]] as part of an expatriate Scandinavian artist community. The novel follows her across three parts: her life in [[rome|Rome]] (Part I), her return to Norway and confrontation with family and convention (Part II), and her final period back in [[rome|Rome]] after tragedy (Part III).

Jenny is introduced in the novel as "good-looking, but could not compete with her friend" Cesca in conventional beauty — "she was as fair as the other was dark" with "blonde hair brushed back from a high, white forehead" (002-010). Helge notes her "fair face and the kind voice" (001-080). She carries herself with a quiet confidence that sets her apart from the more bohemian members of her circle.

Her defining trait is her identity as a painter — she defines herself through her art above all else. Her struggles are fundamentally about whether she can remain true to her artistic vocation while navigating love, motherhood, and the weight of social expectation.

## Biography

### Part I — Rome

Jenny lives in [[rome|Rome]] with her friend Cesca Jahrman at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]]. She is part of a circle of Scandinavian artists: [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] (a writer), [[ahlin|Ahlin]] (a sculptor), and [[lennart|Lennart]] (a painter).

The group gathers regularly at cafes along [[corso|Corso]] and [[ripetta|Ripetta]], discussing art, philosophy, and their lives as expatriates.

Jenny first meets [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]] — a Danish-Norwegian archaeologist — when she offers to show him the right tram (001-065). She introduces herself simply: "My name is Winge" (001-065).

Their connection is immediate but complicated; Helge is drawn to Jenny's quiet strength and artistic seriousness, while Jenny sees in Helge a sincerity that distinguishes him from the more worldly members of her circle.

> … and Jenny began to walk on, accompanied by Helge.

(001-084)

During this period, Jenny's life is rich with artistic activity. She carries her "easel and painting accessories into the garden" (003-024) to work, and her paintings suggest a seriousness of purpose.

When asked if she is a "modern painter," the question itself carries the tension between traditional and contemporary approaches that Jenny must navigate (002-177).

### Part II — Norway

Jenny returns to Norway, confronting her family — her mother [[mrs-berner|Mrs. Berner]] and siblings Ingeborg, Bodil, and Nils ("Kalfatrus"). Here she must reckon with her pregnancy, the dissolution of her relationship with Helge, and the tension between her life as an artist and the expectations of Norwegian society.

Her return to Norway is not just geographical but existential. In [[christiania|Christiania]] and the countryside, Jenny faces the life she left behind and the person she has become. Her mother represents conventional Norwegian womanhood — a path Jenny cannot take, yet cannot entirely reject.

Her brother Nils ("Kalfatrus") represents one of the few genuine connections in Jenny's Norwegian life — "the only occasions when she could breathe freely" during her "sad years" were rambles in Nordmarken with him (008-035). Jenny later arranges a studio in Christiania and Kalfatrus "came in the afternoons and helped her" (013-001), marking a moment of family solidarity amid her personal turmoil.

The visit of Helge's father, [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]], to her studio adds another layer of tension — the older generation's incomprehension of her life and choices crystallizes in these encounters.

### Part III — Rome

Jenny returns to [[rome|Rome]], now a mother. Her child's birth transforms her; the novel traces how motherhood reshapes her relationship with her art. The child's death becomes the novel's central tragedy, precipitating Jenny's psychological unraveling.

> She did not look at it from a painter's point of view; she only looked at it with the eyes of a mother.

(028-019)

In the final chapters, Jenny's suicide is depicted with unflinching clarity. It is not presented as a moral failing but as the culmination of forces — artistic frustration, maternal grief, social constraint — that have steadily eroded her will. [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]], who has loved her throughout, is left to reckon with the loss.

## Artistic Identity

Jenny's identity as a painter is the novel's central axis. She is not merely someone who paints — painting is how she understands herself and her place in the world. Her studio at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] is both workspace and sanctuary.

> Jenny kicked brushes and painting rags under the bed as she was laying the table.

(006-003)

The novel explores what it means for a woman to be an artist in early twentieth-century Europe. Jenny's commitment to her work is total; she resists the idea that marriage or motherhood should displace it.

Yet the novel does not simplify this — it shows how the demands of art and life genuinely conflict, and how the resolution of that conflict is never clean.

Her artistic community — [[cesca-jahrman|Cesca Jahrman]], [[ahlin|Ahlin]], [[lennart|Lennart]] — provides both support and contrast. Each represents a different way of being an artist, and Jenny's path is defined partly through her differences from them. Where Cesca is exuberant and social, Jenny is introspective and intense.

## Character Traits

- **Artistic ambition**: Jenny defines herself as a painter above all else; her art is not a hobby but the core of her identity
- **Independence**: She resists conventional roles — marriage, domesticity — that would subsume her creative life
- **Emotional depth**: Her inner life is rich but prone to melancholy; she feels things deeply and cannot dissemble
- **Vulnerability**: Despite her strength, she is deeply susceptible to emotional wounds, particularly when they touch on her sense of self-worth as an artist and a woman
- **Generosity**: She is kind to those around her — "Give it to your mother," she tells Helge of a gift, "She will be pleased" (002-055) — showing a sensitivity to others' emotional needs

## Relationships

- **[[helge-gram|Helge Gram]]**: Jenny's lover — their relationship is passionate but ultimately destructive. Helge's inability to stand by her after her pregnancy reveals the gap between romantic ideals and social reality
- **[[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]]**: Jenny's confidant and closest intellectual companion. He understands her in ways Helge cannot, but his love arrives too late
- **[[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Cesca Jahrman]]**: Jenny's fellow painter and closest female friend. Their shared studio and life at [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] represents a possible model of independent artistic womanhood
- **[[mrs-berner|Mrs. Berner]]**: Jenny's mother, representing conventional Norwegian womanhood — a life Jenny cannot accept but cannot entirely dismiss
- **[[gert-gram|Gert Gram]]**: Helge's father, whose visit to Jenny's studio (014-001) symbolizes the older generation's incomprehension of her choices
