# Jenny Meets Helge's Parents

**[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s introduction to [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]]'s parents** took place shortly after her return to Norway in Part II of the novel. The encounter unfolded over two days: a tense visit to the Gram household in Welhavensgate, [[christiania|Christiania]], followed by [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]]'s unexpected call at her studio. These meetings exposed the class and cultural gulf between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s world and Helge's family background, foreshadowing the eventual dissolution of [[jennys-engagement-to-helge|their engagement]].

## Background

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]] had been back in Norway for twelve days before she finally called on the Grams, despite Helge's repeated requests in his letters (013-006). She had been putting off the visit, consumed by longing for Helge and reading his letters repeatedly (013-005). The delay reflected her deep ambivalence about this encounter — a "senseless feeling of oppression each time she had to face anything new" (013-010).

## Visit to Mrs. Gram

### The Household

The Grams lived on the top floor of a grey house in Welhavensgate — a street [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] knew well from her school years (013-008). She had passed it daily for four years, past the small shops, the majolica pots in windows, and the narrow gateways leading to dark backyards (013-008). The door bore a brass plate engraved with "G. Gram" (013-010).

[[mrs-gram|Mrs. Gram]] herself answered the door (013-011). She led [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] through a long, narrow hall cluttered with cupboards, boxes, and outdoor clothing (013-014) into a small drawing-room furnished in moss-green plush (013-015):

> "I am afraid it is very untidy here. I have not had time to dust for several days," said Mrs. Gram. "We don't use this room every day, and I have no servant just now." (013-016)

### The Encounter

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]] had formed an idealized picture of Helge's mother from his descriptions, imagining a woman whose tyrannical mother-love was born of loneliness and unrequited affection. She had wanted to love [[mrs-gram|Mrs. Gram]] (013-019). The reality proved jarring:

> Jenny felt an almost physical antipathy towards Mrs. Gram as she talked on and on. (013-020)

[[mrs-gram|Mrs. Gram]]'s appearance — the same features as Helge but hardened — struck [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]: "an expression about her mouth as if everything she said were spiteful, and a malicious and scornful look about the fine wrinkles of the face" (013-021). Her conversation was marked by complaint — about servants, housekeeping, and the liberties of the young (013-016). She made pointed remarks about Francesca Jahrman's reputation (013-028) and expressed resentment at how much of Helge's time the artists had consumed in [[rome|Rome]] (013-030).

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]], who had intended to speak warmly of Helge, found she could not even refer to him by his first name, calling him "Mr. Gram" instead (013-023).

## Gert Gram's Studio Visit

Two days later, [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]] called at [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s studio (014-001). The contrast with the previous meeting was immediate:

> He was thin, and had a slight stoop — not the stoop of an old man, but rather of one too slender for his height. His eyes too were young, though sad and tired and so big and blue that they gave one a curious impression of being wide open, surprised, and at the same time suspicious. (014-001)

[[gert-gram|Gert Gram]] had not asked for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s address — he had seen her on the stairs and followed her to the studio building (014-009), an admission that recalled how Helge had first approached [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Cesca on the street (014-010). When [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] remarked on the similarity, Gram's reaction was telling:

> Gram frowned, and sat quiet an instant. Jenny realized that she had said the wrong thing. (014-011)

He then made a painful admission: "[[jenny-winge|Miss Winge]], you must not be afraid that Helge is like me in other things. I don't think he takes after his father in anything — fortunately" (014-013).

The visit was brief but left [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] with a favourable impression. She liked his smile and his voice, and felt a warmth toward him that she could not muster for his wife (014-003).

## Significance

The two encounters with Helge's parents revealed tensions that would ultimately contribute to the breakdown of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s relationship with Helge:

| Theme | Manifestation |
|-------|--------------|
| **Class divide** | [[jenny-winge|Jenny]], an independent artist, entered a household defined by middle-class domestic anxieties and social propriety |
| **Family dysfunction** | [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]]'s unhappiness in his marriage was palpable; [[mrs-gram|Mrs. Gram]]'s bitterness had hardened into permanent resentment |
| **Helge's inheritance** | [[gert-gram|Gert Gram]]'s admission that Helge did not take after him hinted at deeper anxieties about his son's character |
| **[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s displacement** | [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s inability to speak Helge's name in his mother's presence signalled her alienation from the world he came from |

These scenes established the domestic background against which [[helge-abandons-jenny|Helge's eventual abandonment of Jenny]] would unfold. The cultural gap between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s expatriate artist life and the Grams' constrained Norwegian middle-class existence proved unbridgeable.
