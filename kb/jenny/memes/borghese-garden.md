The Borghese Garden — the landscaped park surrounding the Villa Borghese in [[rome|Rome]] — appears in the closing pages of Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as the setting for a scene of grief. Two days after the funeral of [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s infant child, the garden becomes the space where mourning finds its first outward expression.

## In the Novel

The Borghese Garden appears only once, but at a structurally crucial moment — the novel's penultimate chapter, after the death of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s child:

> It was in the Borghese garden, two days after the funeral.

(033-024)

The brevity of the reference is itself significant: Undset does not linger on the garden's beauty or features. The place is named and the grief is located within it, but description gives way to the rawness of the moment.

## Key Scenes

The Borghese Garden scene serves as the novel's primary articulation of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s maternal grief:

1. **The timing**: "Two days after the funeral" — the wound is fresh, the rituals completed, and now [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] faces the empty aftermath.
2. **The garden as witness**: Unlike the [[campagna|Campagna]] (active, blooming, romantic) or the Roman streets (social, bustling), the Borghese Garden provides a space of stillness where emotion can surface without an audience.
3. **The threshold of the end**: This scene occurs in the chapter immediately preceding [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s [[jennys-suicide|suicide]], making the garden the last place where she is seen in a state of acknowledged — rather than terminal — sorrow.

## Significance

The Borghese Garden's role, though confined to a single reference, is thematically dense:

- **Gardens and temporality**: Alongside the [[medici-garden|Medici Garden]] (a space of aesthetic contemplation in Part I), the Borghese Garden forms part of a pair. The [[medici-garden|Medici Garden]] represents [[rome|Rome]] as seen by a young painter in love with the city; the Borghese Garden represents [[rome|Rome]] as experienced by a woman who has lost everything.
- **The private in the public**: Like all gardens, the Borghese Garden is a cultivated space — nature shaped by human intention — which mirrors the way [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s grief is shaped by social expectation, even in its most private moments.
- **Mourning before the end**: The garden scene is the novel's last depiction of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] in a state of simple grief, before her sorrow transforms into the resolve that leads to her death.
