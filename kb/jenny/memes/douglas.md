Norman Douglas is a minor figure in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911), appearing as a member of the Scandinavian expatriate artist circle in [[rome|Rome]]. His primary narrative function is as one of [[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Cesca Jahrman]]'s romantic involvements — part of the series of relationships that Cesca recounts in her confessional narrative to [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]].

## Appearances

Douglas appears in a handful of scenes in Part I. He is mentioned as having accompanied Cesca home for tea — "Do you remember that time when Douglas came home with me to tea?" (004-028) — and is described by [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] with characteristic irony:

> "Norman Douglas, I tell you, Cesca, was a splendid fellow — a dead failure, but a splendid fellow."

(002-116)

## Romantic Involvement

Like [[Hjerrild]] and [[Hans]], Douglas is one of the men drawn into Cesca's romantic orbit. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] recalls that Cesca "was angry with me because of Douglas" (004-077), suggesting that Douglas's presence created tension within the friendship between [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Cesca — likely a romantic rivalry or misunderstanding.

In Cesca's retrospective account of her romantic history, Douglas is grouped with the others: "[[hans|Hans Hermann]], Norman Douglas, and [[hjerrild|Hjerrild]], and all the rest of her achievements" (020-033). The dismissive categorization suggests that Douglas, like the others, was ultimately a transient figure in Cesca's life.
