Rome's churches dominate the visual landscape of the novel from its opening pages, when Helge Gram first arrives in the city and is struck by "masses of church cupolas floating" above the rooftops (001-007). The constant visual presence of ecclesiastical architecture creates a backdrop of sacred grandeur against which the characters' secular, often morally ambiguous lives unfold.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Through the Roman Setting

The novel's Rome is saturated with churches. Helge's first walk through the city takes him past "the dark mass of the church at the bottom of the street" with its "dome and the row of saints along the top" (001-026, 001-027). He passes "a large, dark church with a dome" by the bridge (001-030), and the streets are lined with "miserable hovels, and small church-fronts in between the rows of houses" (001-031). The omnipresence of churches — from grand basilicas to modest neighborhood chapels — establishes the city as a fundamentally religious space, even for characters who rarely engage with faith on a personal level.

Jenny and Helge visit a small church during one of their early walks, finding it "dark; in the two side chapels candles were burning" (002-144, 002-145). The quiet, candle-lit space offers a moment of intimacy and reflection amid the social whirl of Roman life. Cesca Jahrman, typically irreverent, is nonetheless moved to whisper to Jenny: "Remember you are in church" (006-012) during the Christmas midnight mass at San Luigi dei Francesi — a moment when even the most bohemian characters acknowledge the sacred boundary (006-010).

### Through Character Attitudes

The novel's characters, mostly secular Norwegians living abroad, engage with religious imagery primarily as aesthetic experience rather than spiritual practice. Jenny, the protagonist, never explicitly wrestles with religious faith, but she is drawn to the beauty and quiet of church spaces. The small church with "the pillared portico" and walls "covered with roses" near her studio becomes part of her daily visual landscape, not a place of worship (003-015, 003-018).

Helge Gram, for his part, is practical about religious ceremony — he mentions church or civil marriage as interchangeable options, reflecting a modern Scandinavian indifference to institutional religion (025-072). Yet the novel's emotional language is shot through with religious vocabulary: "faith" appears repeatedly in contexts of love, trust, and self-belief rather than divine worship. Jenny speaks of losing "the least particle of faith in men — or in myself" (021-042), and Gunnar Heggen observes that "the advantage of having faith — it did not matter so much what you believed in" (021-085).

### Through Contrast: Rome vs. Norway

The religious imagery of the novel is almost entirely Roman. When the narrative shifts to Norway in Part II, churches are notably absent from the descriptive landscape — Christiania and the rural Norwegian settings are secular spaces, their emotional weight carried by domestic interiors and natural scenery. The return to Rome in Part III brings back the "church domes that seemed floating in the haze" (029-048), linking the city's sacred architecture with the intensifying emotional crisis of the protagonist.

## Key Passages

> Above it all masses of church cupolas were floating — the huge dome and the row of saints along the top. (001-007)

> The little church was dark; in the two side chapels candles were burning. (002-145)

> And afterwards to the midnight mass at S. Luigi de Franchesi. Helge found it very impressive. The church was in semidarkness, in spite of the many candles. (006-010)

> Cesca! Remember you are in church. (006-012)

> She no longer possessed the least particle of faith in men — or in myself. (021-042)

> Probably the advantage of having faith — it did not matter so much what you believed in. (021-085)
