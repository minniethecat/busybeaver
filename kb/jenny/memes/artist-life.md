The novel *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* by Sigrid Undset portrays the expatriate artist community in early 20th-century [[rome|Rome]] as both a setting and a thematic framework. The artist life represents freedom, creativity, and escape from bourgeois Norwegian conventions — but also isolation, financial precarity, and emotional rootlessness.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Through Jenny

[[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]], a Norwegian painter, lives and works in [[rome|Rome]] as part of a close-knit circle of Scandinavian artists. Her identity as an artist is central to her self-conception, yet she struggles to reconcile it with her desires for love and family. The artist life offers her independence but also leaves her vulnerable when romantic entanglements threaten her creative focus.

### The Artist Community

The Scandinavian artists in [[rome|Rome]] form a surrogate family. They gather for evening meals, discuss art and philosophy, and support each other through creative and personal crises. This community includes [[gunnar-heggen|Gunnar Heggen]] (a writer), Cesca Jahrman (a painter), [[ahlin|Ahlin]] (a sculptor), and [[lennart|Lennart]] (a painter). Their shared experience as expatriates creates bonds that transcend nationality and class.

[[helge-gram|Helge Gram]], an archaeologist, observes this community as an outsider. His perspective highlights the gap between conventional life and the bohemian freedom of the artists. Through his eyes, the reader sees both the allure and the precariousness of the artist life.

### Contrasting Paths

The novel contrasts the artist path with conventional life through several characters. Helge's father wanted to be an artist but married instead, living vicariously through others. [[mrs-gram|Mrs. Gram]] envies the artists' freedom while acknowledging she cannot understand it. These contrasts underscore the novel's central tension: the artist life demands sacrifices that not everyone can make.

## Narrative Arc

In Part I, the artist life is idealized — [[rome|Rome]] represents freedom and creative fulfillment. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s circle is vibrant and supportive. In Part II, set in Norway, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] confronts the bourgeois world she escaped, and the tension between her artist identity and conventional expectations becomes acute. In Part III, returning to [[rome|Rome]], the artist community remains but [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s relationship to it has changed — she is now a mother, and the carefree bohemianism of Part I is no longer accessible.
