Hans Hermann is a figure from [[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Cesca Jahrman]]'s past in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911). Though he never appears directly in the novel's present action, his presence haunts Cesca's emotional life and her marriage to [[Lennart]]. Hans is the archetype of the charismatic but unreliable lover — a man whose betrayal leaves wounds that shape Cesca's choices long afterward. His role as the absent cause of ongoing emotional damage makes him one of the novel's most significant offstage characters.

## Role in the Novel

### The Backstory

Hans Hermann is Cesca's former lover, a relationship that ended in betrayal. The details emerge through Cesca's confessions to [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] across Parts I and II. Hans needed money and acted in a way that compromised Cesca — the exact nature of the betrayal is left partially ambiguous, but its impact is devastating. Cesca describes the aftermath with bitterness and self-recrimination:

> "I have never told you about it. Hans said he had to have money."

(004-061)

Heggen later reveals that "Hans Hermann was discovered" (006-038), hinting at a scandal that forced Cesca to leave Berlin and remake her life in [[rome|Rome]].

### Cesca's Wound

Hans occupies a unique position in Cesca's psychology: he is the lover she cannot forget and cannot forgive. Even after marrying [[lennart|Lennart]], she remains preoccupied with the Hans affair. She measures other men — including [[lennart|Lennart]] — against the intensity of what she felt for Hans:

> "Not even Hans Hermann, whom she loved, could have resisted her tears."

(008-003)

This comparison, made by the narrator, suggests that Hans was the emotional benchmark of Cesca's romantic life — the one who set the bar that no subsequent relationship could clear.

### The Shadow Over Marriage

Hans's betrayal casts a long shadow over Cesca's marriage to [[lennart|Lennart]]. In her confession, Cesca reveals that she married [[lennart|Lennart]] partly as a way to escape the aftermath of the Hans affair:

> "I married Lennart because Hans began to write to me when he was divorced."

(024-033)

The ghost of Hans — his letters, his return, the possibility of renewed contact — continues to destabilize Cesca's life even after she has committed to [[lennart|Lennart]].

### Narrative Function

Hans serves as a structural device: the lover-who-betrayed creates the emotional backstory that explains Cesca's present fragility. His absence from the novel's present-tense action underscores the theme that the past is never truly past — that earlier wounds continue to shape present choices, just as [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s past with Helge will shape her own tragic trajectory.

## Character Traits

- **Charismatic**: Evidenced by Cesca's lasting emotional attachment
- **Unreliable**: Betrayed Cesca for money; "Hans said he had to have money — a hundred crowns" (004-061)
- **Absent**: Never appears in the novel's present action; functions as a haunting memory
- **Destabilizing**: His continued correspondence with Cesca after her marriage threatens her fragile stability
