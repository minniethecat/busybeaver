Part II of Sigrid Undset's *Jenny* (Chapters I–X, PN prefix 012–021) shifts the novel's setting from Rome to Norway, mapping Jenny Winge's return to her homeland after Helge Gram's abandonment. The ten chapters of Part II form a narrative interlude between the Roman freedom of Part I and the Roman tragedy of Part III — a domestic, claustrophobic middle section that dramatizes the consequences of Jenny's choices in a world far from the expatriate artist's life.

The shift in setting is also a shift in tone: the soft Roman light is replaced by the harsh clarity of Norwegian winter, and the chosen family of artists gives way to the biological family Jenny has tried to escape. The landscape — Christiania's domestic interiors, the mountain wilderness of Nordmarken, the small towns of Holmestrand and Frederikshald — mirrors Jenny's emotional confinement.

## Narrative Function

Part II serves three structural purposes in the novel's architecture:

1. **Consequence**: The freedom of Part I had a cost. Jenny's pregnancy, her return to Norway, and her confrontation with her mother make the consequences of her Roman choices concrete and inescapable.
2. **Contrast**: The Norwegian world — bourgeois, constrained, and familial — provides the foil against which the Roman artist's life is measured. Jenny sees her mother's sacrifices, her siblings' constrained futures, and understands what she has been fleeing.
3. **Transformation**: By Part II's end, Jenny has decided to keep her child and return to Rome — a resolution that carries her into the tragedy of Part III. The Norwegian interlude is not an escape from the novel's central crisis but its deepening.
