Nils Berner is [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s stepfather, introduced in the novel through [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s childhood recollections. A colleague of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s mother at her office, he marries her when [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is about ten years old and becomes the most significant positive male influence in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s formative years. His death in a mountaineering accident when [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is sixteen marks a profound loss that shapes her emotional independence.

## Role in the Novel

Berner enters [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s life during her childhood in Christiania, initially provoking jealousy and resistance from the young girl. Over time, through patience and shared outdoor pursuits, he earns her trust and affection. His mentorship extends beyond practical skills — he teaches [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] to observe nature, to navigate with map and compass, and through his belief in her artistic talent, he becomes the first person to take her drawing seriously.

> She got to like Nils Berner against her will; she preferred him almost to her mother in the first period of their married life. He claimed no authority over his stepdaughter, but by his wise and kind, frank ways he won her over. (008-019)

The Berner marriage brings three more children — Ingeborg, Bodil, and Nils — and [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] transitions into an aunt-like role toward her half-siblings. Berner remains a steady presence: teaching her perspective drawing, accompanying her on sketching trips, and modeling a quiet, undemonstrative form of paternal care.

### The Mountaineering Accident

Berner falls to his death in a summer mountaineering accident when [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is sixteen. His death devastates [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s mother and leaves [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] emotionally orphaned for the second time, having lost her biological father Jens Winge at an even younger age. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s refusal to sell or rehome Leddy, Berner's grieving dog, and her decision to have the dog euthanized by a family friend rather than abandon it, reveals the depth of her unspoken grief:

> Berner fell one summer when out mountaineering, and was killed on the spot. Jenny was then sixteen. Her mother's grief was boundless; she had loved her husband and been worshipped by him. Jenny tried to help her as much as she could. How deeply she herself mourned her stepfather she never told anybody; she knew that she had lost the best friend she had ever had. (008-030)

## Character Analysis

Berner represents an ideal of quiet, competent masculinity in the novel — a counterpoint to the more complex and emotionally fraught male figures [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] encounters in adulthood. Unlike [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]], whose love for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is tangled with dependency and inadequacy, Berner offers guidance without demanding emotional return. He claims no authority over his stepdaughter, instead winning her through shared experience.

His outdoor ethos — the Sunday hikes in [[nordmarken|Nordmarken]], the mountain *saeter* holidays below Rondane, the lessons in weather-reading and botany — shapes [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s sensibility. The novel suggests that Berner's influence, more than any formal education, fostered the observational acuity that underpins her work as a painter:

> Berner taught her to use map and compass, he talked to her as to a friend, and he taught her to observe the signs of wind and clouds, which brought about a change in the weather, and to reckon time and distance by the sun. He made her familiar with animals and plants — root and stalk, leaf and bud, blossom and fruit. (008-022)

## Key Relationships

- **[[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]** — Stepfather and formative mentor. Berner wins [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s trust through patience and shared outdoor adventures after initial resistance. His death leaves her with a lasting sense of emotional self-reliance.
- **[[mrs-berner|Mrs. Berner]]** — Husband. The marriage restores [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s mother to youth and happiness after the death of her first husband. Berner's anxiety during her second pregnancy reveals the depth of his devotion.
- **Ingeborg, Bodil, and Nils Winge** — Biological children with [[mrs-berner|Mrs. Berner]]. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] relates to them as an aunt rather than a sister.

## Notable Passages

> The first thing Berner did was to take the girl with him to the woods; Sunday after Sunday they went to Nordmarken, in broiling sunshine or pouring rain, in the thaws of spring, and in winter on ski. (008-021)

[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s outdoor education begins immediately after her mother's remarriage, establishing the pattern of Berner's approach — action over words, shared experience over instruction.

> Berner had always been interested in her drawings; he had been the first to teach her perspective and such things — all he knew about it himself. He had believed she had some talent. (008-031)

Berner is the first adult to validate [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s artistic ability, providing the foundational encouragement that eventually leads her to pursue painting in [[rome|Rome]].

> What Berner had been to her — a friend and a comrade — she tried to be to his children. (008-033)

After his death, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] attempts to repay Berner's gift by caring for his biological children, though the age gap prevents true intimacy.

> She was to have painted Berner — papa. She had begun to call him thus when his own children started to talk. (008-037)

The shift from "Berner" to "papa" marks [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s full acceptance of him as a father figure, a transition that coincides with the arrival of his biological children.
