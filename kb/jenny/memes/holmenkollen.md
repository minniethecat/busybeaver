Holmenkollen — the forested hillside rising north of [[christiania|Christiania]] (present-day Oslo) — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) only in memory. It is mentioned once, by [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] while she is still in [[rome|Rome]]:

> "I know a beech grove by the old road to Holmenkollen, where there are real scented violets."

(009-021)

## Memory and Landscape

The reference occurs in Part I — [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] is in [[rome|Rome]], speaking of Norway from a distance. The specificity of the memory is telling: she knows not just "Holmenkollen" generally but "a beech grove by the old road," a precise location held in her mind with sensory detail ("real scented violets"). The word "real" carries weight — set against the Roman landscape of ruins, pines, and [[campagna|Campagna]] wildflowers, the violets of Holmenkollen represent an authenticity of nature that belongs to home.

## Significance

Holmenkollen's single appearance functions as:

- **The geography of homesickness**: Unlike the grand Roman landmarks — the [[colosseum|Colosseum]], the [[palatine|Palatine]], the Forum — Holmenkollen is a modest, personal landscape, valued for violets and beech trees rather than history or monumentality.
- **Norwegian nature as memory**: Throughout the novel, Norwegian nature exists in tension with Roman nature. Where the [[campagna|Campagna]] hosts romance and artistic community, Holmenkollen and places like it exist in the register of childhood, family, and the person [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] was before [[rome|Rome]].
- **Foreshadowing**: [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s fond recollection of Holmenkollen anticipates her return to Norway in Part II, where the reality of Christiania — family obligations, class tensions, Helge's abandonment — will prove far more difficult than the memory of its violets.
