Frederikshald (modern Halden) — a Norwegian town near the Swedish border — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) primarily through its association with [[cesca-jahrman|Francesca "Cesca" Jahrman]]'s family. It represents the provincial respectability that Cesca has fled — and that continues to define her in the eyes of others.

## In the Novel

Frederikshald first appears as a point of transit, a place of waiting:

> There was a wait of several minutes at Frederikshald — time for a cup of coffee.

(012-001)

This brief, functional mention at the start of Part II ([[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s train journey from the continent to Christiania) establishes Frederikshald as a border town — literally a threshold between the wider world and Norway.

The town acquires deeper significance through Cesca's family history:

> He talked about her respectable family in Frederikshald, her unstained virtue.

(017-029)

The ironic tone — "unstained virtue" — reveals the gap between Cesca's family's perception of her and the reality of her bohemian life in [[rome|Rome]]. Frederikshald becomes, in the novel's moral geography, the seat of provincial judgment.

## Key Scenes

Frederikshald itself is never visited directly in the novel — it exists as a reference point, a place of origin that exerts gravitational pull on the characters:

1. **The train stop**: The "several minutes at Frederikshald" — time enough for coffee, not enough for exploration — mirrors the novel's treatment of the town itself: acknowledged but not dwelt upon.
2. **Cesca's family**: The "respectable family in Frederikshald" serves as the invisible audience for Cesca's Roman life — the people she has left behind but cannot entirely escape.

## Significance

Frederikshald contributes to the novel's mapping of Norwegian provincial life:

- **Respectability as constraint**: The town embodies the social norms that Cesca — like [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] — has sought to escape. It is where families are "respectable" and virtue is "unstained," where the freedoms of expatriate [[rome|Rome]] would be incomprehensible.
- **The border function**: As a border town (near Sweden), Frederikshald literalizes the novel's concern with thresholds — between countries, between social worlds, between the life one is born into and the life one chooses.
- **Cesca's double life**: The distance between Frederikshald and [[rome|Rome]] measures the gap between Cesca's family identity and her artistic self — a gap the novel never fully resolves.
