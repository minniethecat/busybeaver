Borghild is a figure in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) who exists primarily as a correspondent and offstage presence — a friend of [[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Cesca Jahrman]] based in Berlin. Though Borghild never appears in the novel's present-tense action, her letters carry news that shapes the emotional landscape of the [[rome|Rome]] artist circle. She functions as a thread connecting the expatriate world of [[rome|Rome]] to the wider European networks of Berlin and beyond.

## Role in the Novel

### The Berlin Connection

Borghild is introduced through a letter she sends from Berlin, which arrives at the [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] studio and causes immediate tension:

> "From Berlin, from Borghild." Jenny rose from the dressing-table.

(004-042)

The letter carries explosive news: "Borghild writes that [[hans|Hans Hermann]] was married last week" (004-043). This announcement — that Cesca's former lover [[Hans]] has married someone else — precipitates an emotional crisis and opens the floodgates of Cesca's confession about her past.

### The Voice of Moral Judgment

Borghild is portrayed as someone who takes a strong moral stance on Cesca's behavior. Through Cesca's account, Borghild emerges as a figure of conventional judgment:

> "Borghild was furious about it. She probably is right."

(004-060)

> "Borghild was angry with me because I perhaps have not always been good."

(004-061)

This judgment is not presented as cruel — Cesca herself seems to accept its validity — but as the voice of a world that Cesca has left behind. Borghild represents the norms and expectations that the expatriate artist life in [[rome|Rome]] has allowed Cesca (and [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]) to escape, but which continue to exert pressure from a distance.

### The Colonel's Household

Borghild is associated with a "Colonel" — likely a relative or household head — whose movements structure the Norwegian domestic world that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and her siblings navigate. Cesca recounts:

> "A good thing the Colonel and Borghild had not been there."

(023-015)

And later: "the Colonel and Borghild had returned and gone again" (024-001). These passing references suggest Borghild occupies a middle ground between the bohemian freedom of [[rome|Rome]] and the bourgeois order of Norway — a link between two worlds that the novel keeps in productive tension.

### Paris and Beyond

In the novel's later sections, Borghild is mentioned in connection with travel plans: "he is going to give Borghild the same for her trip to Paris" (024-070). This detail suggests Borghild's trajectory continues beyond the novel's frame — a life moving through the European capitals, parallel to but distinct from the [[rome|Rome]] circle's narrower orbit.

## Character Traits

- **Judgmental**: Exercises moral scrutiny over Cesca's romantic choices
- **Connected**: Links [[rome|Rome]] to Berlin, Norway to Paris — a node in the novel's European network
- **Offstage**: Never appears directly; known only through letters and reported speech
- **Conventional**: Represents the norms of the world the artists have fled

## Relationships

- **[[cesca-jahrman|Francesca Cesca Jahrman]]**: Cesca's friend and occasional moral critic
- **[[hans|Hans Hermann]]**: Knows and reports on [[hans|Hans]]'s life in Berlin
- **The Colonel**: Household figure associated with Borghild's domestic context
- **[[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]**: Mentioned in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s presence, suggesting mutual acquaintance
