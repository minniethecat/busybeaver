Nordmarken — the vast forested region north of [[christiania|Christiania]] (present-day Oslo) — appears throughout Part II of Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a site of family excursions, childhood memory, and natural refuge. It provides the Norwegian counterpoint to the [[campagna|Campagna]] of Part I: where the [[campagna|Campagna]] hosts romance, Nordmarken hosts the bonds of family and the weight of the past.

## In the Novel

Nordmarken enters the narrative through [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s memories of her younger brother Nils, called Kalfatrus:

> I used to cycle home and go for a stroll in Nordmarken with Kalfatrus. He is such a clever boy.

(004-018)

This early reference, from Part I (a letter from [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] in [[rome|Rome]]), establishes Nordmarken as a place she associates with her brother and with the uncomplicated affections of family life. Once [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] returns to Norway in Part II, Nordmarken becomes a recurring destination:

> Sunday after Sunday they went to Nordmarken, in broiling sunshine or pouring rain.

(008-021)

The insistence — "Sunday after Sunday" — suggests both ritual and obligation, the family's attempt to maintain traditions that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]], after her years in [[rome|Rome]], now experiences as slightly alien.

## Key Scenes

### Family Excursions

Nordmarken is where [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s family spends their leisure time, but the outings are tinged with her growing estrangement:

> The rambles in Nordmarken with Kalfatrus were the only thing that gave her any pleasure.

(008-035)

The forest walks with her younger brother are [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s one point of genuine connection in an otherwise oppressive home environment.

### Jenny and Helge

Nordmarken also becomes part of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s vision of a shared future:

> Think of Nordmarken. We shall go there together.

(011-020)

She imagines bringing [[helge-gram|Helge]] into this landscape — a hope that the novel's later events will render impossible. Yet she also acknowledges its emotional complexity:

> I have been to all these places — Vester Aker, Nordmarken — so often alone that I dread to go there again.

(011-021)

### Log Huts and Gossip

Nordmarken serves multiple social functions across the novel. In a later chapter, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] contemplates:

> She could stay some weeks in the log huts in Nordmarken.

(013-004)

And it becomes a site of social observation:

> They went out together for excursions in Nordmarken, and the road to the butcher's shop.

(021-106)

> She had seen them sporting with their admirers in Nordmarken.

(022-055)

## Significance

Nordmarken functions on several symbolic levels:

- **Norwegian nature vs. Roman urbanity**: Where [[rome|Rome]]'s landscapes (the [[campagna|Campagna]], the [[tiber|Tiber]]) frame [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s artistic and romantic self, Nordmarken represents the pull of home, family, and the person she was before [[rome|Rome]].
- **Childhood and regression**: The walks with Kalfatrus return [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] to a quasi-maternal, pre-romantic role — a foreshadowing of the motherhood themes that dominate the novel's later sections.
- **Social constraint disguised as nature**: Despite being a "wilderness," Nordmarken is where respectable Christiania society performs its leisure — the outings, the admirers, the gossip — making it as much a social arena as the city streets.

Nordmarken is the Norwegian [[campagna|Campagna]]: a landscape that appears to offer freedom but is, in [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s experience, saturated with expectation and memory.
