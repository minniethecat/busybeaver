The Ripetta (Via di Ripetta) is a street running near the [[tiber|Tiber River]] in [[rome|Rome]]'s historic center. In Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911), it is where [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]] takes lodgings — a choice that places him at a slight geographical remove from [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] and the other artists, reflecting his ambiguous position in their community.

## In the Novel

The Ripetta appears in Part I, soon after Helge has met the group of expatriate artists. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] describes her morning routine, which includes lunch at the Ripetta:

> I drew for an hour before lunch and ate at the Ripetta, you know, and when I came home I met Helge outside our gate.

(004-005)

This casual reference establishes the Ripetta as part of the artists' daily geography — a place for meals, encounters, and the informal rhythms of expatriate life. Later, the narrative confirms that Helge has chosen the area as his base:

> Gram liked his lodgings by the Ripetta. It seemed easy to do good work there.

(006-001)

## Key Scenes

The Ripetta serves as Helge's foothold in [[rome|Rome]] — a place where he can imagine himself as part of the world [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] inhabits:

- **Daily proximity**: Helge's lodgings place him within walking distance of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Cesca's [[via-vantaggio|Via Vantaggio]] studio, enabling the frequent encounters that fuel the developing romance.
- **Productive solitude**: The line about doing "good work" suggests that Helge, in his Ripetta rooms, momentarily believes he can become the productive scholar he has always claimed to be — a hope the novel will eventually dismantle.

## Significance

The Ripetta functions subtly in the novel's geography of belonging:

- **Proximity without membership**: Helge lives near the artists but not among them — a spatial arrangement that mirrors his social position as an outsider gradually admitted to the group's periphery.
- **A borrowed Roman identity**: Unlike [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s deeply inhabited Roman life (her studio, her painting locations), Helge's Ripetta is a rented approximation, a place he "likes" rather than a place that shapes him.
- **The domestic vs. the artistic**: The Ripetta represents Helge's attempt at domestic stability, contrasting with the more bohemian living arrangements of the other artists.
