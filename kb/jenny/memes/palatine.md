The Palatine Hill — one of the seven hills of [[rome|Rome]] and the site of the imperial palaces — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as part of the ruin-strewn landscape that the novel's characters inhabit and contemplate. Never the setting of a scene itself, the Palatine functions as a visual anchor in the Roman cityscape, its "yellow masses of ruins" (003-043) forming a constant backdrop to the expatriate artists' lives.

## In the Novel

The Palatine enters the novel first as a detail in a landscape viewed from a garden:

> In the orchard below grew rows of vegetables, small innocent tufts of green on the grey soil, and on the far side of the meadow one could see the yellow masses of ruins on the Palatine against the dark foliage.

(003-043)

This early glimpse, in Part I, establishes the Palatine as part of the layered Roman panorama — ancient ruins set against cultivated present-day gardens.

In Part III, the Palatine reappears as [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] walks home after an unproductive sketching session:

> She came back through the avenue below the south side of the Palatine. The ruins showed dull grey against the palms of the convent on the mountaintop; the evergreen shrubs hung on the slope, powdered with chalky dust.

(032-001)

The "dull grey" of the ruins and the "chalky dust" on the shrubs create an atmosphere of weariness and decline — a mood that mirrors [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s own emotional state in the novel's final section.

The Palatine is then grouped with [[rome|Rome]]'s other great ruins:

> Some shivering postcard-sellers loitered about outside the Constantin arch on the Piazza, where the ruins of the Colosseum, the Palatine, and the Forum lay.

(032-002)

Here Undset frames the Palatine alongside the [[colosseum|Colosseum]] and [[trajan-forum|Forum]] as components of a single monumental ruin-scape, now reduced to a backdrop for tourist commerce.

## Significance

The Palatine's role in the novel is primarily atmospheric:

- **The Eternal City's layers**: The Palatine — palace of emperors, now a crumbling hillside — embodies [[rome|Rome]]'s quality of layered time, where the imperial past coexists with the present. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and the other Scandinavian artists live amid this stratification daily.
- **Visual mood-setting**: The Palatine's appearances are concentrated at moments of stillness or melancholy ([[jenny-winge|Jenny]] sitting in a garden, [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] walking home after a failed day's work), using the ruins to amplify the emotional register.
- **Distance and perspective**: The Palatine is always *seen from a distance* — from a garden, from an avenue, from a piazza — never inhabited. This reinforces the expatriate artists' position as observers of [[rome|Rome]] rather than participants in its deeper life.
