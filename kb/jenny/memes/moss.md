Moss — a coastal town on the eastern shore of the Oslofjord in Norway — appears at several points in Part II of Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911). It serves as a waypoint on [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]]'s return journey from [[rome|Rome]] to [[christiania|Christiania]], a shopping destination in the narrative present, and a scenic landmark where the fjord opens to view.

## In the Novel

Moss first appears as [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s train approaches Christiania at the start of Part II:

> When the train left Moss, the mountain ridge stood dark against a reddish-yellow sky.

(012-009)

This is [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s first sight of the Norwegian landscape after her years in [[rome|Rome]] — the dark mountain ridge and colored sky marking a return to the geography of her childhood. The town also serves as a practical destination:

> Cesca had gone to Moss one day to do some shopping.

(024-008)

Later, the fjord landscape near Moss provides one of the novel's Norwegian scenic set-pieces:

> The fjord became visible near Moss, a town built along the canal with small wooden houses.

(026-009)

## Key Scenes

Moss functions as a landscape of transition:

1. **The arrival (Part II opening)**: The train leaving Moss marks [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s entry into the landscape of home — a visual threshold between the Roman world she left and the Norwegian world she must now inhabit.
2. **The shopping trip**: [[cesca-jahrman|Cesca]]'s excursion to Moss situates the town within the ordinary rhythms of Norwegian life — a contrast to the grand Roman settings of Part I.
3. **The fjord vista**: The view of the fjord near Moss opens the landscape, providing the expansive natural scenery that will characterize the novel's Norwegian sections.

## Significance

Moss represents the texture of ordinary Norwegian life that [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] must re-adapt to:

- **The provincial vs. the cosmopolitan**: Where [[rome|Rome]] offers ruins, palaces, and the [[campagna|Campagna]], Moss offers a canal, wooden houses, and shopping — a deliberate scaling-down that mirrors [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s contracted circumstances in Part II.
- **Transit and stasis**: As a train stop, Moss is a place passed through rather than a destination — an appropriate symbol for [[jenny-winge|Jenny]]'s experience of Part II, where she is in motion but not moving forward.
- **The fjord landscape**: The view of the fjord near Moss is one of several Norwegian landscapes (alongside [[holmestrand|Holmestrand]] and [[nordmarken|Nordmarken]]) that collectively define the novel's Norwegian visual vocabulary.
