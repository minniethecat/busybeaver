The Medici Garden — the gardens of the Villa Medici, perched above the [[pincio|Pincio]] and the [[piazza-di-spagna|Spanish Steps]] in [[rome|Rome]] — appears in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911) as a vantage point from which the city's beauty is contemplated. The garden's pines, silhouetted against the sky, provide one of the novel's enduring visual images.

## In the Novel

The Medici Garden enters the novel in Part I during a panoramic description of [[rome|Rome]]'s skyline at dusk:

> The evening sky, where the pines in the Medici garden and the small church towers stood out black against the pale, clear air.

(002-163)

This image — pines and church towers against a pale sky — captures the [[rome|Rome]] of the expatriate painters: a city seen as a composition, evaluated for its visual qualities. It is the [[rome|Rome]] that [[jenny-winge|Jenny Winge]] would paint, and the [[rome|Rome]] that [[helge-gram|Helge Gram]], newly arrived, is learning to see.

Later, in Part III, the Medici name resurfaces in a different context:

> The book opened by itself at Lorenzo di Medici's carnival song.

(028-013)

This later reference shifts from the spatial to the historical — from the garden to the dynasty that gave it its name, connecting the modern city to its Renaissance past.

## Key Scenes

The Medici Garden's appearances are brief but visually precise:

1. **The skyline view (Part I)**: The garden is seen as part of a composed landscape — pines, towers, sky — viewed from a distance. It is a painter's [[rome|Rome]], framed and aestheticized.
2. **The book reference (Part III)**: The chance opening of a book to a Medici carnival song creates an echo — the garden of Part I's visual beauty reverberates in Part III's textual encounter.

## Significance

The Medici Garden exemplifies a mode of place-description characteristic of *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]*:

- **The painterly gaze**: Undset's narrator describes [[rome|Rome]] as a painter would — as an arrangement of forms against light — and the Medici Garden's pines are a recurring element in this visual vocabulary.
- **Continuity across parts**: The garden in Part I and the Medici name in Part III create a subtle thread, one of many that connect the novel's two Roman sections across the Norwegian interlude of Part II.
- **Gardens as contemplative space**: Alongside the [[borghese-garden|Borghese Garden]] (the site of a post-funeral scene in Part III), the Medici Garden belongs to [[rome|Rome]]'s green spaces — places of stillness and observation rather than action.
