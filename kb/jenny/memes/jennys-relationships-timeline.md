Jenny Winge's emotional life is the novel's central thread. Her relationships — romantic, familial, and artistic — trace a trajectory from hope to disillusionment across the three parts of Sigrid Undset's *Jenny*. This timeline maps the key moments in her connections with Helge Gram, Gunnar Heggen, and the people who shape her journey.

## Core Relationships

### Jenny and Helge Gram

The novel's central romantic relationship, spanning Parts I and II, moves from mutual attraction through engagement to devastating abandonment. Helge, a Danish-Norwegian archaeologist, embodies the tension between conventional morality and bohemian ideals — he wants Jenny without the obligations that come with her. Their relationship is defined by six key moments:

1. **First meeting** — in the Roman streets, an encounter charged with artistic sensibility and mutual recognition
2. **Engagement** — a commitment made against the backdrop of Roman freedom
3. **Physical consummation** — the crossing of a boundary that changes the terms of their bond
4. **Helge's abandonment** — the rupture that sends Jenny back to Norway, pregnant and alone
5. **Jenny's refusal of renewal** — Helge's later attempt to rekindle the relationship, which Jenny rejects
6. **Aftermath** — the emotional legacy that shadows Jenny through Part III

### Jenny and Gunnar Heggen

Gunnar Heggen, a Norwegian writer, represents an alternative model of masculinity and companionship. Where Helge is indecisive and morally conventional beneath a bohemian surface, Gunnar offers steady, unromantic loyalty. His proposal to Jenny in Part III — offered after she has been devastated by Helge's abandonment and the death of her child — represents both a lifeline and a burden Jenny cannot accept.

### Jenny and Cesca Jahrman

Francesca "Cesca" Jahrman, Jenny's fellow Norwegian painter and roommate in Rome, is the novel's most significant female friendship. Their relationship — creative, competitive, affectionate, and sometimes strained — models an alternative to the heterosexual romance plot. Cesca's independent spirit and her own romantic entanglements provide counterpoint and commentary on Jenny's choices.

### Jenny and Her Family

The Winge family of Part II — Jenny's mother Mrs. Berner, and her siblings Ingeborg, Bodil, and Nils — represent the Norwegian domestic world Jenny has fled. Her return to them, pregnant and abandoned, is both a homecoming and an imprisonment. The family scenes dramatize the class and gender constraints that Jenny's artistic life in Rome was meant to escape.
