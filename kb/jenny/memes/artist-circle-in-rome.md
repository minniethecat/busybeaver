The Scandinavian artist community in Rome forms the social backbone of Parts I and III of Sigrid Undset's *Jenny*. Centered around the studios of Via Vantaggio and the cafes of the Corso, this loose circle of painters, sculptors, and writers creates a bohemian microcosm — a space of creative freedom, romantic entanglement, and communal ritual that contrasts sharply with the bourgeois Norwegian world of Part II.

The core members each represent different artistic temperaments: Ahlin the established mentor, Lennart the lighthearted companion, Cesca the fiercely independent woman artist, and Hjerrild the reserved observer. Their collective life — studio visits, dinners in the Campagna, Christmas celebrations — is the novel's most sustained portrayal of artistic community.

- [[pincio]] — the hilltop park for sunset views and conversation

## Narrative Role

The artist circle serves three narrative functions:

1. **Community as freedom**: The Rome circle represents a chosen family, free from the constraints of biological kinship — a sharp contrast to the Winge family scenes in Part II Norway.
2. **Artistic dialogue**: Through the characters' conversations about art, technique, and the artist's life, Undset dramatizes the aesthetic debates of early 20th century Scandinavian art — impressionism, symbolism, the role of the female artist.
3. **Emotional network**: The circle is both support system and pressure chamber. Jenny's relationships with Helge, Gunnar, and her female friends all play out within its boundaries. When the circle fractures — through death, departure, or emotional estrangement — Jenny's isolation in Part III is the more devastating for the contrast.
