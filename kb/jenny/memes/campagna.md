The Campagna — the rolling countryside surrounding [[rome|Rome]] — serves as the novel's primary pastoral space in Sigrid Undset's *[[jenny-winge|Jenny]]* (1911). It appears most prominently in Part I, where it functions as a site of escape from the city's social pressures: a place for artist excursions, romantic trysts, and moments of unguarded emotion. The Campagna's seasonal transformations — brown in winter, green in spring, hazy in summer — mirror the emotional arc of [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge's relationship.

## Landscape and Atmosphere

Undset renders the Campagna with a painter's eye, emphasizing its vastness, its ruins, and its seasonal shifts:

> "Everything was in the air, the brown Campagna had a greyish-green tinge."

(007-028)

They walked through the Campagna's varied terrain — "the short grass was springing up everywhere, and variegated thistle-leaves and some big, silver-grey ones were basking in the sun" (007-036) — the landscape alive with the newness of spring.

The landscape is punctuated by ancient ruins, osterias (rustic taverns), and the ever-present daisies that become a leitmotif of the lovers' early days together.

## Narrative Significance

### The Excursion (Part I)

The most sustained Campagna sequence occurs during a group dinner at an osteria on the [[via-appia-nuova|Via Appia Nuova]], where [[ahlin|Ahlin]], Heggen, Gram, and several women companions gather. The evening becomes a turning point: [[ahlin|Ahlin]] breaks down in tears after Cesca's rejection, and the group's easy conviviality gives way to exposed vulnerability.

### Jenny and Helge's Romance

The Campagna is the space where [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] and Helge's love blossoms. Undset repeatedly pairs the green countryside with their intimacy:

> "Their countless kisses out in the green Campagna."

(010-009)

> "Our last day in the Campagna," said Helge. "It's quite sad."

(011-011)

The landscape becomes a register of their relationship's trajectory — from the "glorious days in the Campagna, when he lay among the daisies" (016-009) to the elegiac final walks as Part I closes.

### Return and Memory

In Part III, the Campagna appears only in retrospect — a memory of what was lost. [[jenny-winge|Jenny]] recalls "that spring day on the Campagna" (032-025), and the novel's final paragraphs look "over the brown plains of the Campagna, where there were no ruins" (033-060), a closing image that strips the landscape of romance and leaves only emptiness.
