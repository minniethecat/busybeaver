## Definition & Context

Honour (*honor*) is the public recognition of merit, typically through political office, titles, and social prestige. For a Roman aristocrat like [[boethius|Boethius]], honour was the currency of public life — the consulship, the Senate, the trust of the king. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] argues that honour is external to the self, can be possessed by the unworthy, and cannot make its possessor virtuous. True honour resides not in the applause of the multitude but in the testimony of [[conscience|conscience]].

> "The highest honours of the State came to him" (001-078)

## Philosophical Background

The Roman concept of *honor* was central to the aristocratic ethos that shaped Boethius's world. The *cursus honorum* — the sequence of political offices culminating in the consulship — was the path to both personal distinction and ancestral immortality. **Cicero** had argued that the pursuit of honour, when directed toward the common good, was praiseworthy; but he also warned, in the *Somnium Scipionis*, that earthly honours are insignificant compared to the eternal rewards of virtue.

The **Stoic** critique of honour was more radical. Seneca argued that the wise person seeks not honour but virtue, and that popular acclaim is more often a sign of vice than of merit. **Christianity** radicalised this critique further: the only honour that matters is the honour that comes from God, and the pursuit of human honour is a form of vainglory (*vana gloria*) — one of the capital sins.

## Key Passages

On Boethius's honours:

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's argument against honour is deeply subversive of Roman aristocratic values. She does not merely moderate the pursuit of honour but rejects its premise: honour is not a genuine good because it does not belong to the person who receives it. It is a gift of [[fortune|Fortune]], and like all Fortune's gifts, it is external, mutable, and morally indifferent.

The argument reaches its most radical point when Philosophy considers the case of the wicked person who receives honour. If honour were a genuine good, it would benefit its possessor — but the wicked person, honoured by the crowd, is made worse by that honour because it confirms him in his wickedness. The applause of fools is more likely to corrupt than to reward; silence from the wise is more valuable than acclaim from the ignorant.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius had achieved everything the Roman aristocratic ethos could offer — the consulship, the admiration of the Senate, the trust of the king. His fall stripped him of all of it. Philosophy's argument that honour is "unsubstantial" is addressed to a man who possessed the highest honours and found that they could not protect him from false accusation, imprisonment, or the sentence of death. The therapeutic lesson is that Boethius's attachment to honour was part of his sickness — he had mistaken the shadow for the substance, the title for the virtue.
