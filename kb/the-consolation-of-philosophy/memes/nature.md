## Definition & Context

Nature in *The Consolation of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]* operates on multiple levels. At the most fundamental, it names the essential character of each created thing — what determines its proper end and its proper good. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s diagnosis of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s sickness begins with this very concept: "He has forgotten his own true nature" (004-006). To forget one's nature is to lose sight of what one truly is and therefore what one's true good consists in.

At the cosmic level, nature is the providential order through which God directs all things. "All nature is directed" toward its proper end (004-006), and this direction is not blind but purposive. The "bent of nature forces all things" toward unity, toward the supreme Good, such that anything severed from this unity loses its being. Nature thus becomes the medium through which divine [[providence|Providence]] operates — not as an independent force, but as the pattern of God's governance imprinted upon creation.

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s therapeutic method turns on restoring [[boethius|Boethius]] to a knowledge of his own nature: "Thou hast ceased to know thy own nature. So, then, I have now found out the other cause, or rather the chief cause, of thy sickness" (004-022). The cure is to reawaken the natural human orientation toward [[truth|the true]] Good.

## Key Passages

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s initial diagnosis identifies nature-forgetfulness as the root of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s misery:

> "He has forgotten his own true nature; and so he is fallen away exhausted. But let us begin to deal with him gently. Since it is only through misleading appearances that he has lapsed from the truth, let him first be brought back to health by gentle remedies." (004-006)

[[fortune|Fortune]]'s nature is explored as a limiting case — that which, by its very mutability, remains true to itself:

> "Thou deemest Fortune to have changed towards thee; thou mistakest. Such ever were her ways, ever such her nature. Rather in her very mutability hath she preserved towards thee her true constancy." (004-122–004-123)

The most powerful natural imperative is toward unity with the divine:

> "The bent of nature forces all things to strive toward this unity. They perish if they cannot attain it. And since the sole end is also happiness, it is plain that this good and happiness are in essence the same." (004-177, synthesised)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] deploys the concept of nature in a three-stage argument. First, she establishes nature as a diagnostic criterion: [[boethius|Boethius]] has "forgotten his own true nature" (004-006), meaning he has lost sight of the [[reason|ratio]]nal, contemplative being he essentially is and mistaken [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts for his proper good. This is not a trivial oversight — it is the "chief cause" of his sickness (004-022).

Second, she uses [[fortune|Fortune]]'s nature as a teaching device. [[fortune|Fortune]]'s mutability is not a defect — it is her nature. "Such ever were her ways, ever such her nature" (004-122). To complain that [[fortune|Fortune]] is fickle is to complain that a wheel turns. Understanding [[fortune|Fortune]]'s nature liberates [[boethius|Boethius]] from attachment to her gifts: if her nature is to change, then attachment to her gifts is attachment to the transient.

Third, she argues that the natural order is teleological — all things are directed toward their proper end by nature itself. "The bent of nature forces all things" toward [[good|the Good]] (003-078). This is the bridge from the therapeutic argument (releasing [[boethius|Boethius]] from false goods) to the constructive argument (directing him toward [[truth|the true]] Good). Nature is not only a diagnostic tool; it is the built-in compass pointing toward God.

## Boethius' Position

At the outset, [[boethius|Boethius]] has forgotten his nature — [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s opening diagnosis. His poetic laments in Book I are saturated with references to a natural order he feels excluded from, describing his "sorrow-shrunken frame" and "untimely" white hairs as violations of what nature intended. He stands outside nature, or so he feels.

By Book II, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] has begun to restore his grasp of natural order through the [[fortune|Fortune]] argument. The re[[knowledge|cognition]] that [[fortune|Fortune]]'s mutability is her nature — and therefore lawful, not chaotic — is the first step in [[boethius|Boethius]]'s recovery of philosophical perspective.

By Book III, the recovery is fuller. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument that "the bent of nature forces all things to strive toward this unity" provides [[boethius|Boethius]] with the intellectual framework to understand his own longing: what he mistook for regret at lost prosperity was actually the natural human desire for [[good|the Good]], misdirected toward [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts. The cure is not to suppress desire but to redirect it toward its natural object.
