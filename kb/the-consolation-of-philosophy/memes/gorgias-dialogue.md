## Overview

Plato's *Gorgias* is the dialogue in which Socrates argues, against the sophist Gorgias and his followers, that rhetoric without philosophy is mere flattery, and — most famously — that it is better to suffer injustice than to commit it, and better to be punished for one's wrongdoing than to escape punishment. This argument is directly taken up by [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] in Book IV of the *Consolation*.

## Role in the Consolation

The *Gorgias* argument that the wicked person who escapes [[punishment|punishment]] is more miserable than the one who is punished — because punishment can cure the soul while impunity confirms it in wickedness — is one of the most striking arguments in the *Consolation*. Philosophy extends it: the apparent prosperity of the wicked is not a sign of divine injustice but of the severity of their illness. The most dangerous patient is the one who feels no pain.
