# Section 20

[020-001] 'Since, then, every mode of judgment comprehends its objects conformably

[020-002] to its own nature, and since God abides for ever in an eternal present,

[020-003] His knowledge, also transcending all movement of time, dwells in the

[020-004] simplicity of its own changeless present, and, embracing the whole

[020-005] infinite sweep of the past and of the future, contemplates all that

[020-006] falls within its simple [[knowledge|cognition]] as if it were now taking place. And

[020-007] therefore, if thou wilt carefully consider that immediate presentment

[020-008] whereby it discriminates all things, thou wilt more rightly deem it not

[020-009] foreknowledge as of something future, but knowledge of a moment that

[020-010] never passes. For this cause the name chosen to describe it is not

[020-011] prevision, but providence, because, since utterly removed in nature from

[020-012] things mean and trivial, its outlook embraces all things as from some

[020-013] lofty height. Why, then, dost thou insist that the things which are

[020-014] surveyed by the Divine eye are involved in necessity, whereas clearly

[020-015] men impose no necessity on things which they see? Does the act of vision

[020-016] add any necessity to the things which thou seest before thy eyes?'

[020-017] 'Assuredly not.'

[020-018] 'And yet, if we may without unfitness compare God's present and man's,

[020-019] just as ye see certain things in this your temporary present, so does He

[020-020] see all things in His eternal present. Wherefore this Divine

[020-021] anticipation changes not the natures and properties of things, and it

[020-022] beholds things present before it, just as they will hereafter come to

[020-023] pass in time. Nor does it confound things in its judgment, but in the

[020-024] one mental view distinguishes alike what will come necessarily and what

[020-025] without necessity. For even as ye, when at one and the same time ye see

[020-026] a man walking on the earth and the sun rising in the sky, distinguish

[020-027] between the two, though one glance embraces both, and judge the former

[020-028] voluntary, the latter necessary action: so also the Divine vision in its

[020-029] universal range of view does in no wise confuse the characters of the

[020-030] things which are present to its regard, though future in respect of

[020-031] time. Whence it follows that when it perceives that something will come

[020-032] into existence, and yet is perfectly aware that this is unbound by any

[020-033] necessity, its apprehension is not opinion, but rather knowledge based

[020-034] on truth. And if to this thou sayest that what God sees to be about to

[020-035] come to pass cannot fail to come to pass, and that what cannot fail to

[020-036] come to pass happens of necessity, and wilt tie me down to this word

[020-037] necessity, I will acknowledge that thou affirmest a most solid truth,

[020-038] but one which scarcely anyone can approach to who has not made the

[020-039] Divine his special study. For my answer would be that the same future

[020-040] event is necessary from the standpoint of Divine knowledge, but when

[020-041] considered in its own nature it seems absolutely free and unfettered.

[020-042] So, then, there are two necessities--one simple, as that men are

[020-043] necessarily mortal; the other conditioned, as that, if you know that

[020-044] someone is walking, he must necessarily be walking. For that which is

[020-045] known cannot indeed be otherwise than as it is known to be, and yet this

[020-046] fact by no means carries with it that other simple necessity. For the

[020-047] former necessity is not imposed by the thing's own proper nature, but by

[020-048] the addition of a condition. No necessity compels one who is voluntarily

[020-049] walking to go forward, although it is necessary for him to go forward at

[020-050] the moment of walking. In the same way, then, if [[providence|Providence]] sees

[020-051] anything as present, that must necessarily be, though it is bound by no

[020-052] necessity of nature. Now, God views as present those coming events which

[020-053] happen of free will. These, accordingly, from the standpoint of the

[020-054] Divine vision are made necessary conditionally on the Divine

[020-055] cognizance; viewed, however, in themselves, they desist not from the

[020-056] absolute freedom naturally theirs. Accordingly, without doubt, all

[020-057] things will come to pass which God foreknows as about to happen, but of

[020-058] these certain proceed of free will; and though these happen, yet by the

[020-059] fact of their existence they do not lose their proper nature, in virtue

[020-060] of which before they happened it was really possible that they might not

[020-061] have come to pass.

[020-062] 'What difference, then, does the denial of necessity make, since,

[020-063] through their being conditioned by Divine knowledge, they come to pass

[020-064] as if they were in all respects under the compulsion of necessity? This

[020-065] difference, surely, which we saw in the case of the instances I formerly

[020-066] took, the sun's rising and the man's walking; which at the moment of

[020-067] their occurrence could not but be taking place, and yet one of them

[020-068] before it took place was necessarily obliged to be, while the other was

[020-069] not so at all. So likewise the things which to God are present without

[020-070] doubt exist, but some of them come from the necessity of things, others

[020-071] from the power of the agent. Quite rightly, then, have we said that

[020-072] these things are necessary if viewed from the standpoint of the Divine

[020-073] knowledge; but if they are considered in themselves, they are free from

[020-074] the bonds of necessity, even as everything which is accessible to sense,

[020-075] regarded from the standpoint of Thought, is universal, but viewed in its

[020-076] own nature particular. "But," thou wilt say, "if it is in my power to

[020-077] change my purpose, I shall make void providence, since I shall perchance

[020-078] change something which comes within its foreknowledge." My answer is:

[020-079] Thou canst indeed turn aside thy purpose; but since the truth of

[020-080] providence is ever at hand to see that thou canst, and whether thou

[020-081] dost, and whither thou turnest thyself, thou canst not avoid the Divine

[020-082] foreknowledge, even as thou canst not escape the sight of a present

[020-083] spectator, although of thy free will thou turn thyself to various

[020-084] actions. Wilt thou, then, say: "Shall the Divine knowledge be changed at

[020-085] my discretion, so that, when I will this or that, providence changes its

[020-086] knowledge correspondingly?"

[020-087] 'Surely not.'

[020-088] 'True, for the Divine vision anticipates all that is coming, and

[020-089] transforms and reduces it to the form of its own present knowledge, and

[020-090] varies not, as thou deemest, in its foreknowledge, alternating to this

[020-091] or that, but in a single flash it forestalls and includes thy mutations

[020-092] without altering. And this ever-present comprehension and survey of all

[020-093] things God has received, not from the issue of future events, but from

[020-094] the simplicity of His own nature. Hereby also is resolved the objection

[020-095] which a little while ago gave thee offence--that our doings in the

[020-096] future were spoken of as if supplying the cause of God's knowledge. For

[020-097] this faculty of knowledge, embracing all things in its immediate

[020-098] cognizance, has itself fixed the bounds of all things, yet itself owes

[020-099] nothing to what comes after.

[020-100] 'And all this being so, the freedom of man's will stands unshaken, and

[020-101] laws are not unrighteous, since their rewards and punishments are held

[020-102] forth to wills unbound by any necessity. God, who foreknoweth all

[020-103] things, still looks down from above, and the [[eternity|ever-present eternity]] of

[020-104] His vision concurs with the future character of all our acts, and

[020-105] dispenseth to the good rewards, to the bad punishments. Our hopes and

[020-106] prayers also are not fixed on God in vain, and when they are rightly

[020-107] directed cannot fail of effect. Therefore, withstand [[evil|vice]], practise

[020-108] virtue, lift up your souls to right hopes, offer humble prayers to

[020-109] Heaven. Great is the necessity of [[justice|righteousness]] laid upon you if ye will

[020-110] not hide it from yourselves, seeing that all your actions are done

[020-111] before the eyes of a Judge who seeth all things.'

EPILOGUE.

Within a short time of writing 'The Consolation of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]],' [[boethius|Boethius]]

died by a cruel death. As to the manner of his death there is some

uncertainty. According to one account, he was cut down by the swords of

the soldiers before the very judgment-seat of [[theodoric|Theodoric]]; according to

another, a cord was first fastened round his forehead, and tightened

till 'his eyes started'; he was then killed with a club.
