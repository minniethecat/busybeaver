# Neoplatonism

Neoplatonism is the philosophical tradition originating with Plotinus and developed by Porphyry, Iamblichus, and Proclus, which synthesises Platonic metaphysics, Aristotelian logic, and religious mysticism into a comprehensive system. It provides the foundational intellectual framework for *The Consolation of Philosophy*, shaping its metaphysics of participation, its cosmology of divine emanation, its epistemology of ascent, and its resolution of the problem of evil. Boethius was educated in the Neoplatonic schools of Athens and Alexandria, and his *Consolation* represents the most accomplished literary expression of Latin Christian Neoplatonism.

---

## Definition & Context

Neoplatonism is a hierarchical metaphysics that posits all reality as an emanation from a single, ineffable first principle — the One or the Good. From the One proceeds Intellect (*Nous*), and from Intellect proceeds Soul (*Psyche*), which in turn generates the material cosmos. Every lower level of being exists by **participation** (*methexis*) in the level above it, so that all things derive their reality, goodness, and unity from the supreme source.

In the *Consolation*, Lady Philosophy never uses the term "Neoplatonism" (the word is a modern coinage), but her entire argumentative structure is Neoplatonic. She leads Boethius from temporal suffering to eternal truth through a dialectical ascent that mirrors the Neoplatonic curriculum: from ethics (Book 2–3), through physics and cosmology (Book 3.M9, Book 4), to theology and the contemplation of divine eternity (Book 5). Boethius explicitly invokes Plato's *Timaeus* as the authoritative source for this cosmology (010-134), and his philosophical vocabulary — participation, the Good, the One, emanation, the World-Soul — is drawn directly from the Neoplatonic commentary tradition.

Philosophy describes herself as having been "nurtured in the Eleatic and Academic philosophies" (001-249), naming the two schools — Parmenidean monism and Platonic idealism — that Neoplatonism claimed as its dual inheritance.

## Key Passages

The most concentrated expression of Neoplatonic cosmology in the *Consolation* is the hymn "O qui perpetua mundum ratione gubernas" (3.M9), which adapts Plato's *Timaeus* 29–42 into a Christian framework:

> Maker of earth and sky, from age to age / Who rul'st the world by reason; at whose word / Time issues from Eternity's abyss: / To all that moves the source of movement, fixed / Thyself and moveless. Thee no cause impelled / Extrinsic this proportioned frame to shape / From shapeless matter; but, deep-set within / Thy inmost being, the form of perfect good, / From envy free; and Thou didst mould the whole / To that supernal pattern. (010-143 to 010-152)

The hymn describes the World-Soul — "A soul of threefold nature, moving all" (010-164) — emanating from the divine mind, and "lesser souls" sent forth to animate the cosmos before returning to their source (010-168 to 010-174). This is pure Neoplatonic emanationism: procession from, and return to, the One.

The doctrine of **recollection** (*anamnesis*) is explicitly linked to Plato, but its deployment in the *Consolation* is mediated through Neoplatonic epistemology:

> If Plato's teaching erreth not, / Each man but recollects / What he once knew. (012-040)

Boethius the prisoner acknowledges: "With all my heart I agree with Plato; indeed, this is now the second time that these things have been brought back to my mind — first I lost them through the clogging contact of the body; then after through the stress of heavy grief" (012-041). The body-as-prison image is Platonic in origin but developed within Neoplatonism into a full doctrine of the soul's descent and ascent.

The Neoplatonic theory of **modes of knowing** is articulated in Book 5, where Philosophy distinguishes four cognitive faculties arranged hierarchically: Sense, Imagination, Reason, and Intelligence (019-032 to 019-038). Each faculty knows its objects according to its own nature, not the nature of the object known — a principle that grounds the argument for God's timeless knowledge:

> All that is known is grasped not conformably to its own efficacy, but rather conformably to the faculty of the knower. (019-025 to 019-026)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's entire therapeutic programme is structured as a Neoplatonic ascent. She begins by diagnosing Boethius's condition: he has "forgotten himself" (001-065), having lost sight of his true nature as a rational soul whose homeland is the intelligible realm. His attachment to the Muses of Poetry — whom Philosophy dismisses as "harlots" (001-028) — represents the soul's entanglement with the lower, sensible world.

The ascent proceeds through four stages, each mapping onto a Neoplatonic level of reality:

**Stage 1 — Fortune and external goods (Book 2).** Philosophy demonstrates that wealth, power, fame, and pleasure are "shadows of the true good" (010-127). They are not goods in themselves but participate imperfectly in the supreme Good. This corresponds to the Neoplatonic turn away from the material world.

**Stage 2 — True happiness and the supreme Good (Book 3).** Philosophy argues that happiness is identical with God, who is the supreme Good. Since all things desire the good, and all things desire happiness, "God and true happiness are one and the same" (011-038). This identification of God with the Good is the central metaphysical claim of Neoplatonic theology.

**Stage 3 — The problem of evil (Book 4).** Drawing on the Neoplatonic doctrine of evil as privation, Philosophy argues that the wicked are not powerful but impotent, because they cannot attain the good they truly desire. "The wicked can indeed accomplish their desire but cannot fulfil their true will" (013-213).

**Stage 4 — Providence, fate, and eternity (Book 4–5).** Philosophy distinguishes between Providence — the divine reason itself, "abiding in the Divine mind" (016-008) — and Fate — the temporal unfolding of providential order. She then ascends to the highest Neoplatonic theme: the nature of divine eternity and its compatibility with human freedom (019-025 to 020-032).

The argument culminates in the definition of eternity: "Eternity is the perfect possession, all at once, of limitless life" (020-010). This definition, which became canonical in medieval theology, expresses the Neoplatonic insight that God transcends time not by lasting forever within it but by possessing the fullness of life in a single, simultaneous present.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius writes as an inheritor and transmitter of Neoplatonism, but he is not a passive conduit. His relationship to the tradition is creative and, at key points, corrective.

The *Consolation* is Boethius's most explicitly Neoplatonic work, but his earlier theological treatises (the *Opuscula Sacra*) already deploy Neoplatonic logical tools — particularly the Aristotelian categories as mediated by Porphyry — to address Christian doctrinal questions. In the *Consolation*, he achieves a synthesis more ambitious than any of his predecessors: he uses Neoplatonic metaphysics to construct a theodicy that reconciles divine justice with the suffering of the innocent, without appealing to specifically Christian revelation (Christ is never mentioned).

Boethius's distinctive contributions to the Neoplatonic tradition include:

1. **The definition of eternity** as "the perfect possession, all at once, of limitless life" (020-010). While Plotinus and Proclus had discussed eternity, Boethius's formulation is uniquely precise and became the standard reference point for medieval discussions of divine timelessness.

2. **The four modes of knowing** — Sense, Imagination, Reason, Intelligence — arranged in a hierarchy where each higher mode comprehends what the lower cannot (019-032 to 019-038). This systematises a Neoplatonic insight into a clear epistemological framework.

3. **The distinction between Providence and Fate** — Providence is the divine plan as it exists timelessly in God's mind, while Fate is its temporal execution (016-008 to 016-009). This allows Boethius to maintain both divine sovereignty and genuine contingency in the created order.

4. **The reconciliation of free will with foreknowledge** through the concept of God's eternal present (020-003 to 020-032). The Stoics could not solve this problem without collapsing freedom into determinism; Boethius solves it by arguing that God's knowledge is not *fore*-knowledge at all, but a simultaneous vision of all temporal events from the standpoint of eternity.

Boethius departs from pagan Neoplatonism at crucial points. He rejects the doctrine of the pre-existence of souls and the cyclical return — the *Consolation*'s universe is linear and providentially directed. He also departs from what "those imagine who think that on Plato's principles the created world is co-eternal with its Creator" (019-108). His eternity is not mere everlastingness but a qualitatively different mode of existence proper to God alone.
