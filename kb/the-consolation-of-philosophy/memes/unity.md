## Definition & Context

Unity (*unitas*) is, in [[boethius|Boethius]]'s philosophical argument, the fundamental principle by which all things exist and are good. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] establishes in Book III that unity and goodness are identical: "unity and goodness were shown to be the same" (007-225). This identity is not an abstract metaphysical claim but the logical pivot of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s entire theodicy — if all things desire goodness, and goodness is unity, then the universal desire for self-preservation is fundamentally a desire for unity.

The argument proceeds from observation: all living beings strive to preserve their own existence. Why? Because existence itself is good, and existence is the possession of unity. "Only as long as they preserve the unity of their being; when they lose this unity, they perish" (007-221). The corollary is immediate and devastating for [[boethius|Boethius]]'s despair: to be sick, distracted, or torn between desires is to be losing one's unity — and therefore one's participation in [[good|the Good]]. The therapeutic task of philosophy is to restore the soul's unity by redirecting its scattered desires toward the one true Good.

## Key Passages

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument linking unity to being and goodness is stated with clarity:

> "Only as long as they preserve the unity of their being; when they lose this unity, they perish" (007-221)

This establishes the existential stake: unity is not a pleasant attribute but the condition of continued existence. Beings that fragment, that are pulled apart by competing appetites, are on the path to non-being. This is precisely [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s diagnosis of [[boethius|Boethius]] in Book I: he is a man divided against himself, his [[reason|ratio]]nal soul pulled one way by knowledge and another by grief.

The identity thesis follows shortly:

> "Therefore, all things desire unity, for unity is essential to life. But unity and goodness were shown to be the same" (007-224–225)

The universal desire for goodness — established earlier in the argument that all things seek [[good|the Good]], whether they know it or not — is now revealed as the universal desire for unity. The wicked, by their very [[evil|wickedness]], are fragmenting themselves; the virtuous are becoming whole. This is the ground of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s claim that the wicked are weaker, not stronger, than the just.

The cosmological perspective appears in Book IV:

> "this unity abiding in the mind of God" (016-008)

From God's perspective, the universe is one undivided whole; from the human perspective unfolding in time, it appears as a sequence of disconnected events. The resolution of the foreknowledge problem in Book V depends on this distinction: divine knowledge sees all events in their unity, while human knowledge sees them as a temporal series.

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument about unity unfolds in three stages, each building on the last.

First, the teleological stage: she demonstrates that all natural beings seek self-preservation. Plants grow toward the sun, [[soul|anima]]ls flee from predators, and human beings cling to life. But what is self-preservation? It is the maintenance of unity — the body resisting dissolution, the mind resisting confusion. The "love of life" that "cometh not of conscious will" but of nature itself (011-218) is the desire for unity.

Second, the axiological stage: she proves the identity of unity and goodness. Whatever is good unifies; whatever is evil divides. "By the acquisition of justice men become just, and wise by the acquisition of wisdom" (011-048) — each virtue is a movement toward a unified state of being. Vice, by contrast, is a scattering: the avaricious man is pulled apart by his desires, never whole, never at rest.

Third, the theological stage: she identifies perfect unity with God. God is the being in whom unity is complete and undiminished. The universe is "ruled for the best if this unity abiding in the mind of God" (016-008) governs all things. [[evil|Evil]], on this account, is not a positive reality but the privation of unity — the degree to which a being fails to maintain its wholeness and thus falls away from [[good|the Good]].

## Boethius' Position

[[boethius|Boethius]]'s confusion in the middle books of the *Consolation* can be reframed as a crisis of unity. He is divided: his intellect assents to the truths [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] presents, but his passions rebel against them. He cannot reconcile the justice of God with the suffering of the innocent, the prosperity of the wicked with the order of the cosmos. These are not separate questions but manifestations of a single problem — the inability to see reality as a unified whole.

The resolution comes gradually as [[boethius|Boethius]]'s soul regains its unity through [[reason|ratio]]nal argument. By the end of Book V, he has been brought to see that what appeared contradictory — free will and foreknowledge, human autonomy and [[providence|divine providence]] — are coherent when viewed from the perspective of the divine unity that encompasses all time in a single eternal present. [[boethius|Boethius]]'s intellectual unification mirrors the metaphysical unity that [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] has been describing all along.
