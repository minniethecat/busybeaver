## Identity & Context

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC) was Rome's greatest orator, a statesman who served as consul in 63 BC, and the author of philosophical works that transmitted Greek thought to the Latin world. His *De Republica*, *De Divinatione*, and *Tusculan Disputations* shaped the philosophical vocabulary in which Boethius thought and wrote. Cicero was eventually proscribed and murdered by the agents of Mark Antony — his head and hands cut off and displayed on the Rostra in the Forum, the very platform from which he had delivered his greatest speeches.

For Boethius, Cicero represents both the possibility and the limits of philosophical achievement in public life. He is cited as an authority — "Cicero's days, as he himself somewhere says" (007-045) — but also as proof that even the greatest fame is fleeting. The man whose eloquence dominated the Roman senate ended as a mutilated corpse, his fame a matter for footnotes. This double vision of Cicero — honoured philosopher and victim of Fortune — mirrors Boethius's own self-understanding.

## Role in the Consolation

Cicero appears in the *Consolation* in two distinct philosophical contexts.

The first is the discussion of fame in Book II. Philosophy argues that fame is an empty good, limited by the narrowness of human reputation and the brevity of human life. "Cicero's days, as he himself somewhere says" (007-045) are invoked not for their glory but for their limits — even Cicero, whose name was known throughout the Roman world, was famous only within a fraction of the earth's surface, and only for a moment in the vastness of time. Fame is a province, not an empire, and Cicero's reach, vast by human standards, is infinitesimal against the backdrop of eternity.

The second is the discussion of foreknowledge in Book V. Philosophy says: "this matter is vigorously discussed by Cicero in his Divination; thou also hast long and earnestly considered it" (018-129). Cicero's *De Divinatione* had treated the problem of foreknowledge and free will in a sceptical mode, and Boethius — the translator and commentator — knew the work intimately. Philosophy's solution in Book V is, in part, a response to the Ciceronian framework, surpassing the sceptical aporia with a metaphysical resolution that Cicero's Stoic and Academic sources had not achieved.

## Key References

Philosophy's treatment of fame invokes Cicero's temporal limits:

The problem of foreknowledge is located within the Ciceronian tradition:

> "This matter is vigorously discussed by Cicero in his Divination; thou also hast long and earnestly considered it" (018-129)

The *De Republica* is cited in connection with the soul's post-mortem destiny:

> "Cicero, De Republica, VI. 20, in the Dream of Scipio" (020-111)

## Philosophical Significance

Cicero's philosophical significance in the *Consolation* is that of a transmitter and a foil. As a transmitter, he bequeathed to Boethius the vocabulary and conceptual framework of Hellenistic philosophy — Stoic and Academic arguments about providence, fate, and free will that Boethius both inherits and transcends. As a foil, his untimely death and the posthumous fate of his reputation demonstrate the very point that Philosophy seeks to drive home: that Fortune governs external goods, and that the wise man must locate his happiness in something Fortune cannot reach.

Yet there is also an unspoken identification. Boethius, like Cicero, was a statesman-philosopher — a man of public service who wrote philosophy in the intervals of political life, who believed that the contemplative and active lives could be integrated, and who died at the hands of the political enemies his virtue had made. Cicero's fate is Boethius's fate, and Philosophy's consolation must be adequate to both.
