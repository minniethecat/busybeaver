# Section 6

[006-001] V.

[006-002] 'But since my reasonings begin to work a soothing effect within thy

[006-003] mind, methinks I may resort to remedies somewhat stronger. Come,

[006-004] suppose, now, the gifts of [[fortune|Fortune]] were not fleeting and transitory,

[006-005] what is there in them capable of ever becoming truly thine, or which

[006-006] does not lose value when looked at steadily and fairly weighed in the

[006-007] balance? Are riches, I pray thee, precious either through thy nature or

[006-008] in their own? What are they but mere gold and heaps of money? Yet these

[006-009] fine things show their quality better in the spending than in the

[006-010] hoarding; for I suppose 'tis plain that greed Alva's makes men hateful,

[006-011] while liberality brings fame. But that which is transferred to another

[006-012] cannot remain in one's own possession; and if that be so, then money is

[006-013] only precious when it is given away, and, by being transferred to

[006-014] others, ceases to be one's own. Again, if all the money in the world

[006-015] were heaped up in one man's possession, all others would be made poor.

[006-016] Sound fills the ears of many at the same time without being broken into

[006-017] parts, but your riches cannot pass to many without being lessened in the

[006-018] process. And when this happens, they must needs impoverish those whom

[006-019] they leave. How poor and cramped a thing, then, is riches, which more

[006-020] than one cannot possess as an unbroken whole, which falls not to any one

[006-021] man's lot without the impoverishment of everyone else! Or is it the

[006-022] glitter of gems that allures the eye? Yet, how rarely excellent soever

[006-023] may be their splendour, remember the flashing light is in the jewels,

[006-024] not in the man. Indeed, I greatly marvel at men's admiration of them;

[006-025] for what can rightly seem beautiful to a being endowed with life and

[006-026] reason, if it lack the movement and structure of life? And although such

[006-027] things do in the end take on them more beauty from their Maker's care

[006-028] and their own brilliancy, still they in no wise merit your admiration

[006-029] since their excellence is set at a lower grade than your own.

[006-030] 'Does the beauty of the fields delight you? Surely, yes; it is a

[006-031] beautiful part of a right beautiful whole. Fitly indeed do we at times

[006-032] enjoy the serene calm of the sea, admire the sky, the stars, the moon,

[006-033] the sun. Yet is any of these thy concern? Dost thou venture to boast

[006-034] thyself of the beauty of any one of them? Art _thou_ decked with

[006-035] spring's flowers? is it _thy_ fertility that swelleth in the fruits of

[006-036] autumn? Why art thou moved with empty transports? why embracest thou an

[006-037] alien excellence as thine own? Never will fortune make thine that which

[006-038] the nature of things has excluded from thy ownership. Doubtless the

[006-039] fruits of the earth are given for the sustenance of living creatures.

[006-040] But if thou art content to supply thy wants so far as suffices nature,

[006-041] there is no need to resort to fortune's bounty. [[nature|Nature]] is content with

[006-042] few things, and with a very little of these. If thou art minded to force

[006-043] superfluities upon her when she is satisfied, that which thou addest

[006-044] will prove either unpleasant or harmful. But, now, thou thinkest it

[006-045] fine to shine in raiment of divers colours; yet--if, indeed, there is

[006-046] any pleasure in the sight of such things--it is the texture or the

[006-047] artist's skill which I shall admire.

[006-048] 'Or perhaps it is a long train of servants that makes thee happy? Why,

[006-049] if they behave viciously, they are a ruinous burden to thy house, and

[006-050] exceeding dangerous to their own master; while if they are honest, how

[006-051] canst thou count other men's virtue in the sum of thy possessions? From

[006-052] all which 'tis plainly proved that not one of these things which thou

[006-053] reckonest in the number of thy possessions is really thine. And if there

[006-054] is in them no beauty to be desired, why shouldst thou either grieve for

[006-055] their loss or find joy in their continued possession? While if they are

[006-056] beautiful in their own nature, what is that to thee? They would have

[006-057] been not less pleasing in themselves, though never included among thy

[006-058] possessions. For they derive not their preciousness from being counted

[006-059] in thy riches, but rather thou hast chosen to count them in thy riches

[006-060] because they seemed to thee precious.

[006-061] 'Then, what seek ye by all this noisy outcry about fortune? To chase

[006-062] away poverty, I ween, by means of abundance. And yet ye find the result

[006-063] just contrary. Why, this varied array of precious furniture needs more

[006-064] accessories for its protection; it is a true saying that they want most

[006-065] who possess most, and, conversely, they want very little who measure

[006-066] their abundance by nature's requirements, not by the superfluity of vain

[006-067] display. Have ye no good of your own implanted within you, that ye seek

[006-068] your good in things external and separate? Is the nature of things so

[006-069] reversed that a creature divine by right of reason can in no other way

[006-070] be splendid in his own eyes save by the possession of lifeless chattels?

[006-071] Yet, while other things are content with their own, ye who in your

[006-072] intellect are God-like seek from the lowest of things adornment for a

[006-073] nature of supreme excellence, and perceive not how great a wrong ye do

[006-074] your Maker. His will was that mankind should excel all things on earth.

[006-075] Ye thrust down your worth beneath the lowest of things. For if that in

[006-076] which each thing finds its good is plainly more precious than that whose

[006-077] good it is, by your own estimation ye put yourselves below the vilest of

[006-078] things, when ye deem these vile things to be your good: nor does this

[006-079] fall out undeservedly. Indeed, man is so constituted that he then only

[006-080] excels other things when he knows himself; but he is brought lower than

[006-081] the beasts if he lose this [[knowledge|self-knowledge]]. For that other creatures

[006-082] should be ignorant of themselves is natural; in man it shows as a

[006-083] defect. How extravagant, then, is this error of yours, in thinking that

[006-084] anything can be embellished by adornments not its own. It cannot be. For

[006-085] if such accessories add any lustre, it is the accessories that get the

[006-086] praise, while that which they veil and cover remains in its pristine

[006-087] ugliness. And again I say, That is no _good_, which injures its

[006-088] possessor. Is this untrue? No, quite true, thou sayest. And yet riches

[006-089] have often hurt those that possessed them, since the worst of men, who

[006-090] are all the more covetous by reason of their [[evil|wickedness]], think none but

[006-091] themselves worthy to possess all the gold and gems the world contains.

[006-092] So thou, who now dreadest pike and sword, mightest have trolled a carol

[006-093] "in the robber's face," hadst thou entered the road of life with empty

[006-094] pockets. Oh, wondrous blessedness of perishable wealth, whose

[006-095] acquisition robs thee of security!'

SONG V.

[006-096] THE FORMER AGE.

[006-097] Too blest the former age, their life

[006-098] Who in the fields contented led,

[006-099] And still, by luxury unspoiled,

[006-100] On frugal acorns sparely fed.

[006-101] No skill was theirs the luscious grape

[006-102] With honey's sweetness to confuse;

[006-103] Nor China's soft and sheeny silks

[006-104] T' empurple with brave Tyrian hues.

[006-105] The grass their wholesome couch, their drink

[006-106] The stream, their roof the pine's tall shade;

[006-107] Not theirs to cleave the deep, nor seek

[006-108] In strange far lands the spoils of trade.

[006-109] The trump of war was heard not yet,

[006-110] Nor soiled the fields by bloodshed's stain;

[006-111] For why should war's fierce madness arm

[006-112] When strife brought wound, but brought not gain?

[006-113] Ah! would our hearts might still return

[006-114] To following in those ancient ways.

[006-115] Alas! the greed of getting glows

[006-116] More fierce than Etna's fiery blaze.

[006-117] Woe, woe for him, whoe'er it was,

[006-118] Who first gold's hidden store revealed,

[006-119] And--perilous treasure-trove--dug out

[006-120] The gems that fain would be concealed!

[006-121] VI.

[006-122] 'What now shall I say of rank and power, whereby, because ye know not

[006-123] true power and dignity, ye hope to reach the sky? Yet, when rank and

[006-124] power have fallen to the worst of men, did ever an Etna, belching forth

[006-125] flame and fiery deluge, work such mischief? Verily, as I think, thou

[006-126] dost remember how thine ancestors sought to abolish the consular power,

[006-127] which had been the foundation of their liberties, on account of the

[006-128] overweening pride of the consuls, and how for that self-same pride they

[006-129] had already abolished the kingly title! And if, as happens but rarely,

[006-130] these prerogatives are conferred on virtuous men, it is only the virtue

[006-131] of those who exercise them that pleases. So it appears that honour

[006-132] cometh not to virtue from rank, but to rank from virtue. Look, too, at

[006-133] the nature of that power which ye find so attractive and glorious! Do ye

[006-134] never consider, ye creatures of earth, what ye are, and over whom ye

[006-135] exercise your fancied lordship? Suppose, now, that in the mouse tribe

[006-136] there should rise up one claiming rights and powers for himself above

[006-137] the rest, would ye not laugh consumedly? Yet if thou lookest to his body

[006-138] alone, what creature canst thou find more feeble than man, who

[006-139] oftentimes is killed by the bite of a fly, or by some insect creeping

[006-140] into the inner passage of his system! Yet what rights can one exercise

[006-141] over another, save only as regards the body, and that which is lower

[006-142] than the body--I mean fortune? What! wilt thou bind with thy mandates

[006-143] the free spirit? Canst thou force from its due tranquillity the mind

[006-144] that is firmly composed by reason? A tyrant thought to drive a man of

[006-145] free birth to reveal his accomplices in a conspiracy, but the prisoner

[006-146] bit off his tongue and threw it into the furious tyrant's face; thus,

[006-147] the tortures which the tyrant thought the instrument of his cruelty the

[006-148] sage made an opportunity for heroism. Moreover, what is there that one

[006-149] man can do to another which he himself may not have to undergo in his

[006-150] turn? We are told that Busiris, who used to kill his guests, was himself

[006-151] slain by his guest, Hercules. Regulus had thrown into bonds many of the

[006-152] Carthaginians whom he had taken in war; soon after he himself submitted

[006-153] his hands to the chains of the vanquished. Then, thinkest thou that man

[006-154] hath any power who cannot prevent another's being able to do to him what

[006-155] he himself can do to others?

[006-156] 'Besides, if there were any element of natural and proper good in rank

[006-157] and power, they would never come to the utterly bad, since opposites are

[006-158] not wont to be associated. Nature brooks not the union of contraries.

[006-159] So, seeing there is no doubt that wicked wretches are oftentimes set in

[006-160] high places, it is also clear that things which suffer association with

[006-161] the worst of men cannot be good in their own nature. Indeed, this

[006-162] judgment may with some reason be passed concerning all the gifts of

[006-163] fortune which fall so plentifully to all the most wicked. This ought

[006-164] also to be considered here, I think: No one doubts a man to be brave in

[006-165] whom he has observed a brave spirit residing. It is plain that one who

[006-166] is endowed with speed is swift-footed. So also music makes men musical,

[006-167] the healing art physicians, rhetoric public speakers. For each of these

[006-168] has naturally its own proper working; there is no confusion with the

[006-169] effects of contrary things--nay, even of itself it rejects what is

[006-170] incompatible. And yet wealth cannot extinguish insatiable greed, nor has

[006-171] power ever made him master of himself whom vicious lusts kept bound in

[006-172] indissoluble fetters; dignity conferred on the wicked not only fails to

[006-173] make them worthy, but contrarily reveals and displays their

[006-174] unworthiness. Why does it so happen? Because ye take pleasure in calling

[006-175] by false names things whose nature is quite incongruous thereto--by

[006-176] names which are easily proved false by the very effects of the things

[006-177] themselves; even so it is; these riches, that power, this dignity, are

[006-178] none of them rightly so called. Finally, we may draw the same conclusion

[006-179] concerning the whole sphere of Fortune, within which there is plainly

[006-180] nothing to be truly desired, nothing of intrinsic excellence; for she

[006-181] neither always joins herself to the good, nor does she make good men of

[006-182] those to whom she is united.'
