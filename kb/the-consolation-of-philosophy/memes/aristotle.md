## Identity & Context

Aristotle of Stagira (384–322 BC) was the student of Plato and tutor to Alexander the Great. In *The Consolation of Philosophy*, he is invoked more frequently than any other philosopher except Plato. Boethius himself translated Aristotle's works into Latin — his peculiar distinction was "to have handed on to the Middle Ages the tradition of Greek philosophy by his Latin translations of the works of Aristotle" (001-077). This act of transmission places Aristotle at the very foundation of Boethius's own intellectual formation.

Aristotle appears in the text as a philosophical authority whose definitions and doctrines carry weight in argument. Philosophy refers to him affectionately as "our good Aristotle" (017-146), signaling his integration into the Neoplatonic synthesis that Boethius inherited. His authority is cited across multiple domains: ethics (the nature of happiness and virtue), physics (chance and causality), and metaphysics (the eternity of the world).

## Role in the Consolation

Aristotle functions as a source of philosophical definitions that Philosophy deploys to advance her argument. His role is not dramatic — he does not appear as a character — but doctrinal: his concepts provide the intellectual scaffolding for the dialogue's central arguments.

In Book V, when Boethius questions whether chance truly exists, Philosophy answers "in conformity with Aristotle's definition (Phys., II. iv.), that chance is merely relative to human purpose" (017-076). This Aristotelian framework allows Philosophy to reconcile apparent randomness with divine Providence. Later, in her discussion of time and eternity, she invokes Aristotle's conception of the world's perpetuity: "as Aristotle deemed of the world, it never have either beginning or end" (019-108), before moving beyond it toward the Boethian distinction between *sempiternitas* and *aeternitas*.

In the ethical domain, Aristotle's *Ethics* (I., xi. 12) is cited in a gloss on the nature of moral beauty (001-005), and his *Physics* provides the concise definition of chance that Philosophy endorses as "closely in accordance with the truth" (017-146).

## Key References

Philosophy's reliance on Aristotle is most evident when she cites him for the definition of chance:

> "Philosophy answers, in conformity with Aristotle's definition (Phys., II. iv.), that chance is merely relative to human purpose." (017-076)

And later, she invokes him by name with evident warmth:

> "'Our good Aristotle,' says she, 'has defined it concisely in his *Physics*, and closely in accordance with the truth.'" (017-146)

Boethius also uses Aristotelian cosmology as a stepping-stone toward his own more radical doctrine of eternity:

> "Whatever, therefore, is subject to the condition of time, although, as Aristotle deemed of the world, it never have either beginning or end, and its life be stretched to the whole extent of time's infinity, it yet is not such that it may rightly be thought eternal." (019-108)

The Aristotelian vision of knowledge is invoked in an ethical simile: "if, as Aristotle says, men should see with the eyes of Lynceus, so that their sight might pierce through obstructions" (009-108) — the penetrating intellectual gaze that Philosophy seeks to restore in Boethius.

## Philosophical Significance

Aristotle occupies a double role in *The Consolation*. Historically, he is the philosopher whom Boethius laboured to transmit to posterity through translation, making him the bridge between Greek antiquity and Latin Christendom. Within the argument, he supplies the definitions and conceptual distinctions — on chance, on the nature of happiness as the highest good, on the eternity of the world — that Philosophy refines and at times surpasses.

The work's relationship to Aristotle is one of respectful engagement rather than subservience. Philosophy cites Aristotle as a reliable starting-point but does not hesitate to move beyond him, particularly in Book V, where the Boethian distinction between temporal sempiternity and divine eternity goes further than Aristotle's own cosmology. This pattern — incorporating the Philosopher's insights while transcending their limits — exemplifies the Neoplatonic method of the entire *Consolation*.
