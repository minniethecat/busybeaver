# Section 5

SONG II.

[005-001] MAN'S COVETOUSNESS.

[005-002] What though Plenty pour her gifts

[005-003] With a lavish hand,

[005-004] Numberless as are the stars,

[005-005] Countless as the sand,

[005-006] Will the race of man, content,

[005-007] Cease to murmur and lament?

[005-008] Nay, though God, all-bounteous, give

[005-009] Gold at man's desire--

[005-010] Honours, rank, and fame--content

[005-011] Not a whit is nigher;

[005-012] But an all-devouring greed

[005-013] Yawns with ever-widening need.

[005-014] Then what bounds can e'er restrain

[005-015] This wild lust of having,

[005-016] When with each new bounty fed

[005-017] Grows the frantic craving?

[005-018] He is never rich whose fear

[005-019] Sees grim Want forever near.

[005-020] III.

[005-021] 'If [[fortune|Fortune]] should plead thus against thee, assuredly thou wouldst not

[005-022] have one word to offer in reply; or, if thou canst find any

[005-023] justification of thy complainings, thou must show what it is. I will

[005-024] give thee space to speak.'

[005-025] Then said I: 'Verily, thy pleas are plausible--yea, steeped in the

[005-026] honeyed sweetness of music and rhetoric. But their charm lasts only

[005-027] while they are sounding in the ear; the sense of his misfortunes lies

[005-028] deeper in the heart of the wretched. So, when the sound ceases to

[005-029] vibrate upon the air, the heart's indwelling sorrow is felt with renewed

[005-030] bitterness.'

[005-031] Then said she: 'It is indeed as thou sayest, for we have not yet come to

[005-032] the curing of thy sickness; as yet these are but lenitives conducing to

[005-033] the treatment of a malady hitherto obstinate. The remedies which go deep

[005-034] I will apply in due season. Nevertheless, to deprecate thy

[005-035] determination to be thought wretched, I ask thee, Hast thou forgotten

[005-036] the extent and bounds of thy felicity? I say nothing of how, when

[005-037] orphaned and desolate, thou wast taken into the care of illustrious men;

[005-038] how thou wast chosen for alliance with the highest in the state--and

[005-039] even before thou wert bound to their house by marriage, wert already

[005-040] dear to their love--which is the most precious of all ties. Did not all

[005-041] pronounce thee most happy in [[virtue|the virtues]] of thy wife, the splendid

[005-042] honours of her father, and the blessing of male issue? I pass over--for

[005-043] I care not to speak of blessings in which others also have shared--the

[005-044] distinctions often denied to age which thou enjoyedst in thy youth. I

[005-045] choose rather to come to the unparalleled culmination of thy good

[005-046] fortune. If the fruition of any earthly success has weight in the scale

[005-047] of happiness, can the memory of that splendour be swept away by any

[005-048] rising flood of troubles? That day when thou didst see thy two sons ride

[005-049] forth from home joint consuls, followed by a train of senators, and

[005-050] welcomed by the good-will of the people; when these two sat in curule

[005-051] chairs in the Senate-house, and thou by thy panegyric on the king didst

[005-052] earn the fame of eloquence and ability; when in the Circus, seated

[005-053] between the two consuls, thou didst glut the multitude thronging around

[005-054] with the triumphal largesses for which they looked--methinks thou didst

[005-055] cozen Fortune while she caressed thee, and made thee her darling. Thou

[005-056] didst bear off a boon which she had never before granted to any private

[005-057] person. Art thou, then, minded to cast up a reckoning with Fortune? Now

[005-058] for the first time she has turned a jealous glance upon thee. If thou

[005-059] compare the extent and bounds of thy blessings and misfortunes, thou

[005-060] canst not deny that thou art still fortunate. Or if thou esteem not

[005-061] thyself favoured by Fortune in that thy then seeming prosperity hath

[005-062] departed, deem not thyself wretched, since what thou now believest to be

[005-063] calamitous passeth also. What! art thou but now come suddenly and a

[005-064] stranger to the scene of this life? Thinkest thou there is any stability

[005-065] in human affairs, when man himself vanishes away in the swift course of

[005-066] time? It is true that there is little trust that the gifts of chance

[005-067] will abide; yet the last day of life is in a manner the death of all

[005-068] remaining Fortune. What difference, then, thinkest thou, is there,

[005-069] whether thou leavest her by dying, or she leave thee by fleeing away?'

SONG III.

[005-070] ALL PASSES.

[005-071] When, in rosy chariot drawn,

[005-072] Phoebus 'gins to light the dawn,

[005-073] By his flaming beams assailed,

[005-074] Every glimmering star is paled.

[005-075] When the grove, by Zephyrs fed,

[005-076] With rose-blossom blushes red;--

[005-077] Doth rude Auster breathe thereon,

[005-078] Bare it stands, its glory gone.

[005-079] Smooth and tranquil lies the deep

[005-080] While the winds are hushed in sleep.

[005-081] Soon, when angry tempests lash,

[005-082] Wild and high the billows dash.

[005-083] Thus if [[nature|Nature]]'s changing face

[005-084] Holds not still a moment's space,

[005-085] Fleeting deem man's fortunes; deem

[005-086] Bliss as transient as a dream.

[005-087] One law only standeth fast:

[005-088] Things created may not last.

[005-089] IV.

[005-090] Then said I: 'True are thine admonishings, thou nurse of all excellence;

[005-091] nor can I deny the wonder of my fortune's swift career. Yet it is this

[005-092] which chafes me the more cruelly in the recalling. For truly in adverse

[005-093] fortune the worst sting of misery is to _have been_ happy.'

[005-094] 'Well,' said she, 'if thou art paying the penalty of a mistaken belief,

[005-095] thou canst not rightly impute the fault to circumstances. If it is the

[005-096] felicity which Fortune gives that moves thee--mere name though it

[005-097] be--come reckon up with me how rich thou art in the number and

[005-098] weightiness of thy blessings. Then if, by the blessing of [[providence|Providence]],

[005-099] thou hast still preserved unto thee safe and inviolate that which,

[005-100] howsoever thou mightest reckon thy fortune, thou wouldst have thought

[005-101] thy most precious possession, what right hast thou to talk of

[005-102] ill-fortune whilst keeping all Fortune's better gifts? Yet [[symmachus|Symmachus]],

[005-103] thy wife's father--a man whose splendid character does honour to the

[005-104] human race--is safe and unharmed; and while he bewails thy wrongs, this

[005-105] rare nature, in whom wisdom and virtue are so nobly blended, is himself

[005-106] out of danger--a boon thou wouldst have been quick to purchase at the

[005-107] price of life itself. Thy wife yet lives, with her gentle disposition,

[005-108] her peerless modesty and virtue--this the epitome of all her graces,

[005-109] that she is [[truth|the true]] daughter of her sire--she lives, I say, and for thy

[005-110] sake only preserves the breath of life, though she loathes it, and pines

[005-111] away in grief and tears for thy absence, wherein, if in naught else, I

[005-112] would allow some marring of thy felicity. What shall I say of thy sons

[005-113] and their consular dignity--how in them, so far as may be in youths of

[005-114] their age, the example of their father's and grandfather's character

[005-115] shines out? Since, then, the chief care of mortal man is to preserve his

[005-116] life, how happy art thou, couldst thou but recognise thy blessings, who

[005-117] possessest even now what no one doubts to be dearer than life!

[005-118] Wherefore, now dry thy tears. Fortune's hate hath not involved all thy

[005-119] dear ones; the stress of the storm that has assailed thee is not beyond

[005-120] measure intolerable, since there are anchors still holding firm which

[005-121] suffer thee not to lack either consolation in the present or hope for

[005-122] the future.'

[005-123] 'I pray that they still may hold. For while they still remain, however

[005-124] things may go, I shall ride out the storm. Yet thou seest how much is

[005-125] shorn of the splendour of my fortunes.'

[005-126] 'We are gaining a little ground,' said she, 'if there is something in

[005-127] thy lot wherewith thou art not yet altogether discontented. But I cannot

[005-128] stomach thy daintiness when thou complainest with such violence of grief

[005-129] and anxiety because thy happiness falls short of completeness. Why, who

[005-130] enjoys such settled felicity as not to have some quarrel with the

[005-131] circumstances of his lot? A troublous matter are the conditions of human

[005-132] bliss; either they are never realized in full, or never stay

[005-133] permanently. One has abundant riches, but is shamed by his ignoble

[005-134] birth. Another is conspicuous for his nobility, but through the

[005-135] embarrassments of poverty would prefer to be obscure. A third, richly

[005-136] endowed with both, laments the loneliness of an unwedded life. Another,

[005-137] though happily married, is doomed to childlessness, and nurses his

[005-138] wealth for a stranger to inherit. Yet another, blest with children,

[005-139] mournfully bewails the misdeeds of son or daughter. Wherefore, it is not

[005-140] easy for anyone to be at perfect peace with the circumstances of his

[005-141] lot. There lurks in each several portion something which they who

[005-142] experience it not know nothing of, but which makes the sufferer wince.

[005-143] Besides, the more favoured a man is by Fortune, the more fastidiously

[005-144] sensitive is he; and, unless all things answer to his whim, he is

[005-145] overwhelmed by the most trifling misfortunes, because utterly unschooled

[005-146] in adversity. So petty are the trifles which rob the most fortunate of

[005-147] [[happiness|perfect happiness]]! How many are there, dost thou imagine, who would

[005-148] think themselves nigh heaven, if but a small portion from the wreck of

[005-149] thy fortune should fall to them? This very place which thou callest

[005-150] exile is to them that dwell therein their native land. So true is it

[005-151] that nothing is wretched, but thinking makes it so, and conversely every

[005-152] lot is happy if borne with equanimity. Who is so blest by Fortune as not

[005-153] to wish to change his state, if once he gives rein to a rebellious

[005-154] spirit? With how many bitternesses is the sweetness of human felicity

[005-155] blent! And even if that sweetness seem to him to bring delight in the

[005-156] enjoying, yet he cannot keep it from departing when it will. How

[005-157] manifestly wretched, then, is the bliss of earthly fortune, which lasts

[005-158] not for ever with those whose temper is equable, and can give no perfect

[005-159] satisfaction to the anxious-minded!

[005-160] 'Why, then, ye children of mortality, seek ye from without that

[005-161] happiness whose seat is only within us? Error and ignorance bewilder

[005-162] you. I will show thee, in brief, the hinge on which perfect happiness

[005-163] turns. Is there anything more precious to thee than thyself? Nothing,

[005-164] thou wilt say. If, then, thou art master of thyself, thou wilt possess

[005-165] that which thou wilt never be willing to lose, and which Fortune cannot

[005-166] take from thee. And that thou mayst see that happiness cannot possibly

[005-167] consist in these things which are the sport of chance, reflect that, if

[005-168] happiness is the [[good|highest good]] of a creature living in accordance with

[005-169] reason, and if a thing which can in any wise be reft away is not the

[005-170] highest good, since that which cannot be taken away is better than it,

[005-171] it is plain that Fortune cannot aspire to bestow happiness by reason of

[005-172] its instability. And, besides, a man borne along by this transitory

[005-173] felicity must either know or not know its unstability. If he knows not,

[005-174] how poor is a happiness which depends on the blindness of ignorance! If

[005-175] he knows it, he needs must fear to lose a happiness whose loss he

[005-176] believes to be possible. Wherefore, a never-ceasing fear suffers him not

[005-177] to be happy. Or does he count the possibility of this loss a trifling

[005-178] matter? Insignificant, then, must be the good whose loss can be borne so

[005-179] equably. And, further, I know thee to be one settled in the belief that

[005-180] the souls of men certainly die not with them, and convinced thereof by

[005-181] numerous proofs; it is clear also that the felicity which Fortune

[005-182] bestows is brought to an end with the death of the body: therefore, it

[005-183] cannot be doubted but that, if happiness is conferred in this way, the

[005-184] whole human race sinks into misery when death brings the close of all.

[005-185] But if we know that many have sought the joy of happiness not through

[005-186] death only, but also through pain and suffering, how can life make men

[005-187] happy by its presence when it makes them not wretched by its loss?'

SONG IV.

[005-188] THE GOLDEN MEAN.

[005-189] Who founded firm and sure

[005-190] Would ever live secure,

[005-191] In spite of storm and blast

[005-192] Immovable and fast;

[005-193] Whoso would fain deride

[005-194] The ocean's threatening tide;--

[005-195] His dwelling should not seek

[005-196] On sands or mountain-peak.

[005-197] Upon the mountain's height

[005-198] The storm-winds wreak their spite:

[005-199] The shifting sands disdain

[005-200] Their burden to sustain.

[005-201] Do thou these perils flee,

[005-202] Fair though the prospect be,

[005-203] And fix thy resting-place

[005-204] On some low rock's sure base.

[005-205] Then, though the tempests roar,

[005-206] Seas thunder on the shore,

[005-207] Thou in thy stronghold blest

[005-208] And undisturbed shalt rest;

[005-209] Live all thy days serene,

[005-210] And mock the heavens' spleen.
