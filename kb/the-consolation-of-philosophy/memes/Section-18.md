# Section 18

[018-001] '"All things surveying, all things overhearing.'"

SONG II.

[018-002] THE TRUE SUN.

[018-003] Homer with mellifluous tongue

[018-004] Phoebus' glorious light hath sung,

[018-005] Hymning high his praise;

[018-006] Yet _his_ feeble rays

[018-007] Ocean's hollows may not brighten,

[018-008] Nor earth's central gloom enlighten.

[018-009] But the might of Him, who skilled

[018-010] This great universe to build,

[018-011] Is not thus confined;

[018-012] Not earth's solid rind,

[018-013] Nor night's blackest canopy,

[018-014] Baffle His all-seeing eye.

[018-015] All that is, hath been, shall be,

[018-016] In one glance's compass, He

[018-017] Limitless descries;

[018-018] And, save His, no eyes

[018-019] All the world survey--no, none!

[018-020] _Him_, then, truly name the Sun.

[018-021] III.

[018-022] Then said I: 'But now I am once more perplexed by a problem yet more

[018-023] difficult.'

[018-024] 'And what is that?' said she; 'yet, in truth, I can guess what it is

[018-025] that troubles you.'

[018-026] 'It seems,' said I, 'too much of a paradox and a contradiction that God

[018-027] should know all things, and yet there should be free will. For if God

[018-028] foresees everything, and can in no wise be deceived, that which

[018-029] providence foresees to be about to happen must necessarily come to pass.

[018-030] Wherefore, if from eternity He foreknows not only what men will do, but

[018-031] also their designs and purposes, there can be no freedom of the will,

[018-032] seeing that nothing can be done, nor can any sort of purpose be

[018-033] entertained, save such as a Divine providence, incapable of being

[018-034] deceived, has perceived beforehand. For if the issues can be turned

[018-035] aside to some other end than that foreseen by providence, there will not

[018-036] then be any sure foreknowledge of the future, but uncertain conjecture

[018-037] instead, and to think this of God I deem impiety.

[018-038] 'Moreover, I do not approve the reasoning by which some think to solve

[018-039] this puzzle. For they say that it is not because God has foreseen the

[018-040] coming of an event that _therefore_ it is sure to come to pass, but,

[018-041] conversely, because something is about to come to pass, it cannot be

[018-042] hidden from Divine providence; and accordingly the necessity passes to

[018-043] the opposite side, and it is not that what is foreseen must necessarily

[018-044] come to pass, but that what is about to come to pass must necessarily be

[018-045] foreseen. But this is just as if the matter in debate were, which is

[018-046] cause and which effect--whether foreknowledge of the future cause of the

[018-047] necessity, or the necessity of the future of the foreknowledge. But we

[018-048] need not be at the pains of demonstrating that, whatsoever be the order

[018-049] of the causal sequence, the occurrence of things foreseen is necessary,

[018-050] even though the foreknowledge of future events does not in itself

[018-051] impose upon them the necessity of their occurrence. For example, if a

[018-052] man be seated, the supposition of his being seated is necessarily true;

[018-053] and, conversely, if the supposition of his being seated is true, because

[018-054] he is really seated, he must necessarily be sitting. So, in either case,

[018-055] there is some necessity involved--in this latter case, the necessity of

[018-056] the fact; in the former, of the truth of the statement. But in both

[018-057] cases the sitter is not therefore seated because the opinion is true,

[018-058] but rather the opinion is true because antecedently he was sitting as a

[018-059] matter of fact. Thus, though the cause of the truth of the opinion comes

[018-060] from the other side,[P] yet there is a necessity on both sides alike. We

[018-061] can obviously reason similarly in the case of providence and the future.

[018-062] Even if future events are foreseen because they are about to happen, and

[018-063] do not come to pass because they are foreseen, still, all the same,

[018-064] there is a necessity, both that they should be foreseen by God as about

[018-065] to come to pass, and that when they are foreseen they should happen, and

[018-066] this is sufficient for the destruction of free will. However, it is

[018-067] preposterous to speak of the occurrence of events in time as the cause

[018-068] of eternal foreknowledge. And yet if we believe that God foresees future

[018-069] events because they are about to come to pass, what is it but to think

[018-070] that the occurrence of events is the cause of His supreme providence?

[018-071] Further, just as when I _know_ that anything is, that thing

[018-072] _necessarily_ is, so when I know that anything will be, it will

[018-073] _necessarily_ be. It follows, then, that things foreknown come to pass

[018-074] inevitably.

[018-075] 'Lastly, to think of a thing as being in any way other than what it is,

[018-076] is not only not knowledge, but it is false opinion widely different from

[018-077] the truth of knowledge. Consequently, if anything is about to be, and

[018-078] yet its occurrence is not certain and necessary, how can anyone foreknow

[018-079] that it will occur? For just as knowledge itself is free from all

[018-080] admixture of falsity, so any conception drawn from knowledge cannot be

[018-081] other than as it is conceived. For this, indeed, is the cause why

[018-082] knowledge is free from falsehood, because of necessity each thing must

[018-083] correspond exactly with the knowledge which grasps its nature. In what

[018-084] way, then, are we to suppose that God foreknows these uncertainties as

[018-085] about to come to pass? For if He thinks of events which possibly may not

[018-086] happen at all as inevitably destined to come to pass, He is deceived;

[018-087] and this it is not only impious to believe, but even so much as to

[018-088] express in words. If, on the other hand, He sees them in the future as

[018-089] they are in such a sense as to know that they may equally come to pass

[018-090] or not, what sort of foreknowledge is this which comprehends nothing

[018-091] certain nor fixed? What better is this than the absurd vaticination of

[018-092] Teiresias?

[018-093] '"Whate'er I say

[018-094] Shall either come to pass--or not."

[018-095] In that case, too, in what would Divine providence surpass human opinion

[018-096] if it holds for uncertain things the occurrence of which is uncertain,

[018-097] even as men do? But if at that perfectly sure Fountain-head of all

[018-098] things no shadow of uncertainty can possibly be found, then the

[018-099] occurrence of those things which He has surely foreknown as coming is

[018-100] certain. Wherefore there can be no freedom in human actions and designs;

[018-101] but the Divine mind, which foresees all things without possibility of

[018-102] mistake, ties and binds them down to one only issue. But this admission

[018-103] once made, what an upset of human affairs manifestly ensues! Vainly are

[018-104] rewards and punishments proposed for the good and bad, since no free and

[018-105] voluntary motion of the will has deserved either one or the other; nay,

[018-106] the punishment of the wicked and the reward of the righteous, which is

[018-107] now esteemed the perfection of justice, will seem the most flagrant

[018-108] injustice, since men are determined either way not by their own proper

[018-109] volition, but by the necessity of what must surely be. And therefore

[018-110] neither virtue nor [[evil|vice]] is anything, but rather good and ill desert are

[018-111] confounded together without distinction. Moreover, seeing that the whole

[018-112] course of events is deduced from providence, and nothing is left free to

[018-113] human design, it comes to pass that our vices also are referred to the

[018-114] Author of all good--a thought than which none more abominable can

[018-115] possibly be conceived. Again, no ground is left for hope or prayer,

[018-116] since how can we hope for blessings, or pray for mercy, when every

[018-117] object of desire depends upon the links of an unalterable chain of

[018-118] causation? Gone, then, is the one means of intercourse between God and

[018-119] man--the communion of hope and prayer--if it be true that we ever earn

[018-120] the inestimable recompense of the Divine favour at the price of a due

[018-121] humility; for this is the one way whereby men seem able to hold

[018-122] communion with God, and are joined to that unapproachable light by the

[018-123] very act of supplication, even before they obtain their petitions. Then,

[018-124] since these things can scarcely be believed to have any efficacy, if the

[018-125] necessity of future events be admitted, what means will there be whereby

[018-126] we may be brought near and cleave to Him who is the supreme Head of all?

[018-127] Wherefore it needs must be that the human race, even as thou didst

[018-128] erstwhile declare in song, parted and dissevered from its Source, should

[018-129] fall to ruin.'

FOOTNOTES:

[P] _I.e._, the necessity of the truth of the statement from the fact.

SONG III.

TRUTH'S PARADOXES.

Why does a strange discordance break

The ordered scheme's fair harmony?

Hath God decreed 'twixt truth and truth

There may such lasting warfare be,

That truths, each severally plain,

We strive to reconcile in vain?

Or is the discord not in truth,

Since truth is self consistent ever?

But, close in fleshly wrappings held,

The blinded mind of man can never

Discern--so faint her taper shines--

The subtle chain that all combines?

Ah! then why burns man's restless mind

[[truth|Truth]]'s hidden portals to unclose?

Knows he already what he seeks?

Why toil to seek it, if he knows?

Yet, haply if he knoweth not,

Why blindly seek he knows not what?[Q]

Who for a good he knows not sighs?

Who can an unknown end pursue?

How find? How e'en when haply found

Hail that strange form he never knew?

Or is it that man's inmost soul

Once knew each part and knew the whole?

Now, though by fleshly vapours dimmed,

Not all forgot her visions past;

For while the several parts are lost,

To the one whole she cleaveth fast;

Whence he who yearns the truth to find

Is neither sound of sight nor blind.

For neither does he know in full,

Nor is he reft of knowledge quite;

But, holding still to what is left,

He gropes in the uncertain light,

And by the part that still survives

To win back all he bravely strives.

FOOTNOTES:

[Q] Compare [[plato|Plato]], 'Meno,' 80; Jowett, vol. ii., pp. 39, 40.

IV.

Then said she: 'This debate about providence is an old one, and is

vigorously discussed by [[cicero|Cicero]] in his "Divination"; thou also hast long

and earnestly pondered the problem, yet no one has had diligence and

perseverance enough to find a solution. And the reason of this obscurity

is that the movement of human reasoning cannot cope with the simplicity

of the Divine foreknowledge; for if a conception of its nature could in

any wise be framed, no shadow of uncertainty would remain. With a view

of making this at last clear and plain, I will begin by considering the

arguments by which thou art swayed. First, I inquire into the reasons

why thou art dissatisfied with the solution proposed, which is to the

effect that, seeing the fact of foreknowledge is not thought the cause

of the necessity of future events, foreknowledge is not to be deemed any

hindrance to the freedom of the will. Now, surely the sole ground on

which thou arguest the necessity of the future is that things which are

foreknown cannot fail to come to pass. But if, as thou wert ready to

acknowledge just now, the fact of foreknowledge imposes no necessity on

things future, what reason is there for supposing the results of

voluntary action constrained to a fixed issue? Suppose, for the sake of

argument, and to see what follows, we assume that there is no

foreknowledge. Are willed actions, then, tied down to any necessity in

_this_ case?'

'Certainly not.'

'Let us assume foreknowledge again, but without its involving any actual

necessity; the freedom of the will, I imagine, will remain in complete

integrity. But thou wilt say that, even although the foreknowledge is

not the necessity of the future event's occurrence, yet it is a sign

that it will necessarily happen. Granted; but in this case it is plain

that, even if there had been no foreknowledge, the issues would have

been inevitably certain. For a sign only indicates something which is,

does not bring to pass that of which it is the sign. We require to show

beforehand that all things, without exception, happen of necessity in

order that a preconception may be a sign of this necessity. Otherwise,

if there is no such universal necessity, neither can any preconception

be a sign of a necessity which exists not. Manifestly, too, a proof

established on firm grounds of reason must be drawn not from signs and

loose general arguments, but from suitable and necessary causes. But how

can it be that things foreseen should ever fail to come to pass? Why,

this is to suppose us to believe that the events which providence

foresees to be coming were not about to happen, instead of our supposing

that, although they should come to pass, yet there was no necessity

involved in their own nature compelling their occurrence. Take an

illustration that will help to convey my meaning. There are many things

which we see taking place before our eyes--the movements of charioteers,

for instance, in guiding and turning their cars, and so on. Now, is any

one of these movements compelled by any necessity?'
