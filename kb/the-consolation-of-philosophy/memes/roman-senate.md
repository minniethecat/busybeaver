## Overview

The Roman Senate (*Senatus Romanus*) was the ancient deliberative and advisory body of the Roman state, surviving into the Ostrogothic period as an assembly of wealthy landowners and former magistrates. By the time of [[boethius|Boethius]], in the early sixth century, the Senate had lost most of its political power but retained immense social prestige. Boethius was a leading senator, and his father, his father-in-law [[symmachus|Symmachus]], and his sons had all held senatorial rank.

## Role in the Consolation

The Senate figures prominently in [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s analysis of political life. Boethius's defence of the Senate against the encroachments of [[theodoric|Theodoric]]'s court is what led to his accusation and imprisonment. He describes "implicating the whole senatorial order" (003-023) in his defence of Albinus, and he presents himself as a champion of senatorial independence against royal absolutism.

Philosophy treats the Senate with characteristic ambivalence. The institution that Boethius served and defended is, in her analysis, merely another stage for [[fortune|Fortune]]'s theatre — the Senate can confer [[honour|honour]] and [[fame|fame]] but cannot confer [[virtue|virtue]]. The Consul who devoted his life to the Senate must learn that his true citizenship is not in Rome but in the [[order|cosmic order]].

## Historical Significance

The Senate's survival into the Gothic period was a symbol of Roman continuity — a living link to the Republic and Empire. Boethius's defence of the Senate and his subsequent execution mark a turning point: the moment when the Gothic kingdom decisively broke with the Roman senatorial aristocracy, clearing the way for the more autocratic rule that would characterise the remaining decades of Ostrogothic Italy.
