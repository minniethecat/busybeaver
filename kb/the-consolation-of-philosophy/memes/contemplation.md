## Definition & Context

Contemplation (*contemplatio*, Greek *theoria*) is the intellectual activity by which the soul beholds eternal [[truth|truths]] directly, without the mediation of the senses or discursive reasoning. It is the highest mode of human knowing — the nearest approach to divine [[intelligence|intelligence]] that mortal beings can achieve. The *Consolation* itself is an exercise in contemplation: [[boethius|Boethius]], through dialogue with [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]], ascends from the particular (his own suffering) to the universal (the nature of the [[good|Good]], the structure of [[providence|Providence]], the relationship between [[eternity|eternity]] and time).

The contemplative life is the fully realised human life because it actualises the highest faculty of the soul — reason — in its highest activity — the apprehension of truth. Philosophy's therapeutic project aims not merely at Boethius's consolation but at his transformation into a contemplative, capable of seeing the world from the divine perspective.

## Key Passages

Philosophy leads Boethius through a gradual ascent from sensory particulars to intellectual universals. The hierarchy of cognition in Book V — sense, imagination, [[reason|reason]], [[intelligence|intelligence]] — describes the stages of contemplative ascent. The prisoner in Pavia, through the exercise of reason, achieves what the Consul in Rome could not: the vision of the world *sub specie aeternitatis*.
