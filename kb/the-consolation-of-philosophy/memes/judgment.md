## Definition & Context

Judgment (*iudicium*) is the rational faculty of discernment — the capacity to distinguish between [[truth|truth]] and falsehood, [[good|good]] and evil, reality and appearance. It is central to [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s diagnosis of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s condition: his suffering originates not in his external circumstances but in his flawed judgments about those circumstances.

> "It is in thy judgment that there is perverse confusion" (016-047)

## Philosophical Background

The concept of judgment spans epistemology and ethics. In **Aristotle's** logic, judgment is the act of combining or separating concepts in a proposition — asserting that something is or is not the case. In **Stoic** ethics, judgment (*krisis*) is the faculty by which we assent to or reject impressions — and the wise person is distinguished by having correct judgments about what is truly good and evil. Epictetus's famous maxim — "It is not things that trouble us but our judgments about things" — is the direct ancestor of Philosophy's therapeutic method.

**Boethius** inherits both traditions. The logical tradition shapes his epistemology (judgment as the act of reason that produces knowledge); the Stoic tradition shapes his ethics (judgment as the faculty whose correction produces happiness). The synthesis is this: the cure for suffering is the correction of judgment, and the correction of judgment is achieved through philosophical argument.

## Key Passages

On judgment as the source of perceived disorder:

> "Shouldst thou see anything in this world happening differently from thy expectation, doubt not but events are rightly ordered; it is in thy judgment that there is perverse confusion" (016-047–016-049)

On the weakness of human judgment:

> "Various and discordant men's judgments" (003-067)

On the need for right judgment about Providence:

> "True judgment concerning the world's government" (004-033)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's argument places judgment at the centre of the therapeutic process. Boethius's sickness consists in false judgments: he judges that [[fortune|Fortune]] has turned against him (false — Fortune has always been mutable), that wealth and power are goods (false — they are indifferents), that the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer (false — appearing to prosper is not the same as truly prospering).

The cure consists in correcting these judgments through philosophical argument. Philosophy does not change Boethius's circumstances — he remains in prison, awaiting execution — but changes his judgments about those circumstances, and thereby changes his experience of them. The Consolation is a dialogue about judgment: each book corrects a layer of false judgment until, by Book V, Boethius can judge rightly about God, fate, freedom, and his own condition.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's opening lament is a sustained expression of false judgment — he judges that his suffering proves cosmic disorder, that Fortune's change proves Fortune's injustice, that his innocence proves the meaningless of virtue. Philosophy's method is Socratic: she exposes the contradictions in these judgments until Boethius is forced to acknowledge that they cannot all be true.

The moment of recovery is the moment of right judgment — when Boethius can say with Philosophy that "it is in thy judgment that there is perverse confusion" (016-047) and mean it not as a concession but as a liberation. If the confusion is in my judgment, then correcting my judgment dispels the confusion. The world was never disordered; I was merely looking at it wrongly.
