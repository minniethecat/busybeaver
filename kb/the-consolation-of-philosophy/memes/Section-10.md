# Section 10

SONG VIII.

[010-001] HUMAN FOLLY.

[010-002] Alas! how wide astray

[010-003] Doth Ignorance these wretched mortals lead

[010-004] From [[truth|Truth]]'s own way!

[010-005] For not on leafy stems

[010-006] Do ye within the green wood look for gold,

[010-007] Nor strip the vine for gems;

[010-008] Your nets ye do not spread

[010-009] Upon the hill-tops, that the groaning board

[010-010] With fish be furnishèd;

[010-011] If ye are fain to chase

[010-012] The bounding goat, ye sweep not in vain search

[010-013] The ocean's ruffled face.

[010-014] The sea's far depths they know,

[010-015] Each hidden nook, wherein the waves o'erwash

[010-016] The pearl as white as snow;

[010-017] Where lurks the Tyrian shell,

[010-018] Where fish and prickly urchins do abound,

[010-019] All this they know full well.

[010-020] But not to know or care

[010-021] Where hidden lies the good all hearts desire--

[010-022] This blindness they can bear;

[010-023] With gaze on earth low-bent,

[010-024] They seek for that which reacheth far beyond

[010-025] The starry firmament.

[010-026] What curse shall I call down

[010-027] On hearts so dull? May they the race still run

[010-028] For wealth and high renown!

[010-029] And when with much ado

[010-030] The false good they have grasped--ah, then too late!--

[010-031] May they discern the true!

[010-032] IX.

[010-033] 'This much may well suffice to set forth the form of false happiness; if

[010-034] this is now clear to thine eyes, the next step is to show what true

[010-035] happiness is.'

[010-036] 'Indeed,' said I, 'I see clearly enough that neither is independence to

[010-037] be found in wealth, nor power in sovereignty, nor reverence in

[010-038] dignities, nor fame in glory, nor true joy in pleasures.'

[010-039] 'Hast thou discerned also the causes why this is so?'

[010-040] 'I seem to have some inkling, but I should like to learn more at large

[010-041] from thee.'

[010-042] 'Why, truly the reason is hard at hand. _That which is simple and

[010-043] indivisible by nature human error separates_, and transforms from the

[010-044] true and perfect to the false and imperfect. Dost thou imagine that

[010-045] which lacketh nothing can want power?'

[010-046] 'Certainly not.'

[010-047] 'Right; for if there is any feebleness of strength in anything, in this

[010-048] there must necessarily be need of external protection.'

[010-049] 'That is so.'

[010-050] 'Accordingly, the nature of independence and power is one and the same.'

[010-051] 'It seems so.'

[010-052] 'Well, but dost think that anything of such a nature as this can be

[010-053] looked upon with contempt, or is it rather of all things most worthy of

[010-054] veneration?'

[010-055] 'Nay; there can be no doubt as to that.'

[010-056] 'Let us, then, add reverence to independence and power, and conclude

[010-057] these three to be one.'

[010-058] 'We must if we will acknowledge the truth.'

[010-059] 'Thinkest thou, then, this combination of qualities to be obscure and

[010-060] without distinction, or rather famous in all renown? Just consider: can

[010-061] that want renown which has been agreed to be lacking in nothing, to be

[010-062] supreme in power, and right worthy of honour, for the reason that it

[010-063] cannot bestow this upon itself, and so comes to appear somewhat poor in

[010-064] esteem?'

[010-065] 'I cannot but acknowledge that, being what it is, this union of

[010-066] qualities is also right famous.'

[010-067] 'It follows, then, that we must admit that renown is not different from

[010-068] the other three.'

[010-069] 'It does,' said I.

[010-070] 'That, then, which needs nothing outside itself, which can accomplish

[010-071] all things in its own strength, which enjoys fame and compels reverence,

[010-072] must not this evidently be also fully crowned with joy?'

[010-073] 'In sooth, I cannot conceive,' said I, 'how any sadness can find

[010-074] entrance into such a state; wherefore I must needs acknowledge it full

[010-075] of joy--at least, if our former conclusions are to hold.'

[010-076] 'Then, for the same reasons, this also is necessary--that independence,

[010-077] power, renown, reverence, and sweetness of delight, are different only

[010-078] in name, but in substance differ no wise one from the other.'

[010-079] 'It is,' said I.

[010-080] 'This, then, which is one, and simple by nature, human perversity

[010-081] separates, and, in trying to win a part of that which has no parts,

[010-082] fails to attain not only that portion (since there are no portions), but

[010-083] also the whole, to which it does not dream of aspiring.'

[010-084] 'How so?' said I.

[010-085] 'He who, to escape want, seeks riches, gives himself no concern about

[010-086] power; he prefers a mean and low estate, and also denies himself many

[010-087] pleasures dear to nature to avoid losing the money which he has gained.

[010-088] But at this rate he does not even attain to independence--a weakling

[010-089] void of strength, vexed by distresses, mean and despised, and buried in

[010-090] obscurity. He, again, who thirsts alone for power squanders his wealth,

[010-091] despises pleasure, and thinks fame and rank alike worthless without

[010-092] power. But thou seest in how many ways his state also is defective.

[010-093] Sometimes it happens that he lacks necessaries, that he is gnawed by

[010-094] anxieties, and, since he cannot rid himself of these inconveniences,

[010-095] even ceases to have that power which was his whole end and aim. In like

[010-096] manner may we cast up the reckoning in case of rank, of glory, or of

[010-097] pleasure. For since each one of these severally is identical with the

[010-098] rest, whosoever seeks any one of them without the others does not even

[010-099] lay hold of that one which he makes his aim.'

[010-100] 'Well,' said I, 'what then?'

[010-101] 'Suppose anyone desire to obtain them together, he does indeed wish for

[010-102] happiness as a whole; but will he find it in these things which, as we

[010-103] have proved, are unable to bestow what they promise?'

[010-104] 'Nay; by no means,' said I.

[010-105] 'Then, happiness must certainly not be sought in these things which

[010-106] severally are believed to afford some one of the blessings most to be

[010-107] desired.'

[010-108] 'They must not, I admit. No conclusion could be more true.'

[010-109] 'So, then, the form and the causes of false happiness are set before

[010-110] thine eyes. Now turn thy gaze to the other side; there thou wilt

[010-111] straightway see the [[good|true happiness]] I promised.'

[010-112] 'Yea, indeed, 'tis plain to the blind.' said I. 'Thou didst point it out

[010-113] even now in seeking to unfold the causes of the false. For, unless I am

[010-114] mistaken, that is true and [[happiness|perfect happiness]] which crowns one with the

[010-115] union of independence, power, reverence, renown, and joy. And to prove

[010-116] to thee with how deep an insight I have listened--since all these are

[010-117] the same--that which can truly bestow one of them I know to be without

[010-118] doubt full and complete happiness.'

[010-119] 'Happy art thou, my scholar, in this thy conviction; only one thing

[010-120] shouldst thou add.'

[010-121] 'What is that?' said I.

[010-122] 'Is there aught, thinkest thou, amid these mortal and perishable things

[010-123] which can produce a state such as this?'

[010-124] 'Nay, surely not; and this thou hast so amply demonstrated that no word

[010-125] more is needed.'

[010-126] 'Well, then, these things seem to give to mortals shadows of the true

[010-127] good, or some kind of imperfect good; but the true and perfect good they

[010-128] cannot bestow.'

[010-129] 'Even so,' said I.

[010-130] 'Since, then, thou hast learnt what that true happiness is, and what men

[010-131] falsely call happiness, it now remains that thou shouldst learn from

[010-132] what source to seek this.'

[010-133] 'Yes; to this I have long been eagerly looking forward.'

[010-134] 'Well, since, as [[plato|Plato]] maintains in the "Timæus," we ought even in the

[010-135] most trivial matters to implore the Divine protection, what thinkest

[010-136] thou should we now do in order to deserve to find the seat of that

[010-137] highest good?'

[010-138] 'We must invoke the Father of all things,' said I; 'for without this no

[010-139] enterprise sets out from a right beginning.'

[010-140] 'Thou sayest well,' said she; and forthwith lifted up her voice and

[010-141] sang:

SONG IX.[I]

[010-142] INVOCATION.

[010-143] Maker of earth and sky, from age to age

[010-144] Who rul'st the world by reason; at whose word

[010-145] Time issues from [[eternity|Eternity]]'s abyss:

[010-146] To all that moves the source of movement, fixed

[010-147] Thyself and moveless. Thee no cause impelled

[010-148] Extrinsic this proportioned frame to shape

[010-149] From shapeless matter; but, deep-set within

[010-150] Thy inmost being, the form of perfect good,

[010-151] From envy free; and Thou didst mould the whole

[010-152] To that supernal pattern. Beauteous

[010-153] The world in Thee thus imaged, being Thyself

[010-154] Most beautiful. So Thou the work didst fashion

[010-155] In that fair likeness, bidding it put on

[010-156] Perfection through the exquisite perfectness

[010-157] Of every part's contrivance. Thou dost bind

[010-158] The elements in balanced harmony,

[010-159] So that the hot and cold, the moist and dry,

[010-160] Contend not; nor the pure fire leaping up

[010-161] Escape, or weight of waters whelm the earth.

[010-162] Thou joinest and diffusest through the whole,

[010-163] Linking accordantly its several parts,

[010-164] A soul of threefold nature, moving all.

[010-165] This, cleft in twain, and in two circles gathered,

[010-166] Speeds in a path that on itself returns,

[010-167] Encompassing mind's limits, and conforms

[010-168] The heavens to her true semblance. Lesser souls

[010-169] And lesser lives by a like ordinance

[010-170] Thou sendest forth, each to its starry car

[010-171] Affixing, and dost strew them far and wide

[010-172] O'er earth and heaven. These by a law benign

[010-173] Thou biddest turn again, and render back

[010-174] To thee their fires. Oh, grant, almighty Father,

[010-175] Grant us on reason's wing to soar aloft

[010-176] To heaven's exalted height; grant us to see

[010-177] The fount of good; grant us, the true light found,

[010-178] To fix our steadfast eyes in vision clear

[010-179] On Thee. Disperse the heavy mists of earth,

[010-180] And shine in Thine own splendour. For Thou art

[010-181] The true serenity and perfect rest

[010-182] Of every pious soul--to see Thy face,

[010-183] The end and the beginning--One the guide,

[010-184] The traveller, the pathway, and the goal.

X.

'Since now thou hast seen what is the form of the imperfect good, and

what the form of the perfect also, methinks I should next show in what

manner this perfection of felicity is built up. And here I conceive it

proper to inquire, first, whether any excellence, such as thou hast

lately defined, can exist in the nature of things, lest we be deceived

by an empty fiction of thought to which no true reality answers. But it

cannot be denied that such does exist, and is, as it were, the source of

all things good. For everything which is called imperfect is spoken of

as imperfect by reason of the [[evil|privation]] of some perfection; so it comes

to pass that, whenever imperfection is found in any particular, there

must necessarily be a perfection in respect of that particular also. For

were there no such perfection, it is utterly inconceivable how that

so-called _im_perfection should come into existence. [[nature|Nature]] does not

make a beginning with things mutilated and imperfect; she starts with

what is whole and perfect, and falls away later to these feeble and

inferior productions. So if there is, as we showed before, a happiness

of a frail and imperfect kind, it cannot be doubted but there is also a

happiness substantial and perfect.'

'Most true is thy conclusion, and most sure,' said I.

'Next to consider where the dwelling-place of this happiness may be. The

common belief of all mankind agrees that God, the supreme of all things,

is good. For since nothing can be imagined better than God, how can we

doubt Him to be good than whom there is nothing better? Now, reason

shows God to be good in such wise as to prove that in Him is perfect

good. For were it not so, He would not be supreme of all things; for

there would be something else more excellent, possessed of perfect good,

which would seem to have the advantage in priority and dignity, since it

has clearly appeared that all perfect things are prior to those less

complete. Wherefore, lest we fall into an infinite regression, we must

acknowledge the supreme God to be full of supreme and perfect good. But

we have determined that true happiness is the perfect good; therefore

true happiness must dwell in the supreme Deity.'

'I accept thy reasonings,' said I; 'they cannot in any wise be

disputed.'
