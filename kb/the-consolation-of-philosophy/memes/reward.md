## Definition & Context

Reward (*merces*, *praemium*) is the recompense that [[virtue|virtue]] deserves — the good that follows from acting rightly. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] addresses one of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s deepest complaints: that the righteous suffer while the wicked prosper. Her answer is that virtue is its own reward — the good person already possesses the [[good|Good]] — and that Providence ensures the wicked receive their due, even if the mechanism is not always visible from the temporal perspective.

> "The righteous never lack their reward, nor the wicked their punishment" (012-041)

## Philosophical Background

The problem of reward and punishment — why do the good suffer and the wicked prosper? — is the central moral challenge that the *Consolation* addresses. **Plato's** *Republic* takes it on directly: Glaucon's challenge is to prove that justice is desirable for its own sake, not merely for its rewards. Plato's answer is that the just soul is harmonious and therefore happy, regardless of external circumstances — the just person on the rack is happier than the unjust tyrant on the throne.

The **Stoics** radicalised this position: virtue is its own reward, and the virtuous person lacks nothing that is truly good, even in the worst external circumstances. **Christianity** introduced the dimension of eschatological reward — the full recompense of virtue is deferred to the afterlife, where divine justice is perfectly realised. Boethius synthesises the philosophical and theological perspectives.

## Key Passages

On the certainty of divine justice:

> "The righteous never lack their reward, nor the wicked their punishment" (012-041)

On the paradox of virtue's earthly fate:

> "It lacks its reward, but is even thrust down and trampled by the wicked" (013-017)

On the secret reward of conscience:

> "There is in a measure the secret reward of a good conscience" (003-029)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's treatment of reward operates at three levels. First, she argues that virtue is intrinsically rewarding — the virtuous person possesses the good and therefore possesses happiness, regardless of external recognition. "The reward of a good conscience" (003-029) is not a consolation prize; it is the thing itself.

Second, she argues that Providence ensures the wicked are punished, but the punishment often consists in the very condition that appears to be prosperity. The wicked person who achieves power, wealth, and fame is not truly rewarded — he is more miserable than before, because his external "goods" confirm him in his wickedness. The punishment is not in the external circumstances but in the soul's corruption.

Third, she gestures toward eschatological reward — the full recompense that awaits the righteous beyond this life. This is the least developed aspect of the argument (the *Consolation* is a philosophical work, not a theological one), but it provides the ultimate horizon within which the problem of earthly injustice is resolved.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's complaint about reward is deeply personal. He had served the state with integrity and been rewarded with false accusation, imprisonment, and the sentence of death. "I undergo the severest penalties in place of the rewards of true virtue" (003-031). Philosophy's answer — that virtue is its own reward — is not cold comfort but radical reorientation: Boethius must learn to find his reward not in what the world gives him but in what he is.
