## Identity & Context

Gaius Valerius Catullus (c. 84–54 BC) was a Roman poet of the late Republic, known for his lyric and satirical verse. Boethius invokes Catullus in Book III of the *Consolation* as a moral authority on the corruption of public office — specifically, Catullus's satirical attack on the unworthy office-holder Nonius (009-020).

Catullus's poetry often targeted the political corruption of his era, making him a natural reference for Boethius's argument that high office does not confer virtue — and may in fact expose vice more visibly.

## Role in the Consolation

In the *Consolation*, Philosophy argues that honors and high positions are not genuine goods because they often fall to the worst of men. To reinforce this point, Boethius quotes Catullus's description of Nonius as an "ulcer-spot" despite sitting in the curule chair — a position of the highest Roman magistracy (009-020).

The reference serves Philosophy's broader argument: external goods (honors, power, fame) are morally neutral; they do not make their possessor good, and may instead amplify existing vice through increased visibility.

## Key References

Boethius's use of Catullus is precise and pointed:

> Accordingly, Catullus calls Nonius an "ulcer-spot," though "sitting in the curule chair." Dost not see what infamy high position brings upon the bad? (009-020)

The image is deliberately visceral: a diseased growth on the body politic, made more conspicuous by the very elevation that was meant to distinguish merit. Philosophy's gloss — that "unworthiness will be less conspicuous" without high office — inverts the conventional wisdom that honors dignify their holders.

A second, annotative reference (020-111) confirms the source: Catullus, poem LII, line 2.

## Philosophical Significance

Catullus functions in the *Consolation* as a literary witness to a philosophical truth: that external markers of worth (office, title, acclaim) are categorically distinct from genuine virtue. By citing a poet rather than a philosopher, Boethius demonstrates that this insight is accessible to reason across genres and traditions.

The Catullus allusion also exemplifies Boethius's method of weaving classical literary authority into philosophical argument — a hallmark of the *Consolation*'s prosimetric form.
