[001-025] PREFACE.

[001-026] The book called 'The Consolation of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]' was throughout the

[001-027] Middle Ages, and down to the beginnings of the modern epoch in the

[001-028] sixteenth century, the scholar's familiar companion. Few books have

[001-029] exercised a wider influence in their time. It has been translated into

[001-030] every European tongue, and into English nearly a dozen times, from King

[001-031] Alfred's paraphrase to the translations of Lord Preston, Causton,

[001-032] Ridpath, and Duncan, in the eighteenth century. The belief that what

[001-033] once pleased so widely must still have some charm is my excuse for

[001-034] attempting the present translation. The great work of Boethius, with its

[001-035] alternate prose and verse, skilfully fitted together like dialogue and

[001-036] chorus in a Greek play, is unique in literature, and has a pathetic

[001-037] interest from the time and circumstances of its composition. It ought

[001-038] not to be forgotten. Those who can go to the original will find their

[001-039] reward. There may be room also for a new translation in English after an

[001-040] interval of close on a hundred years.

[001-041] Some of the editions contain a reproduction of a bust purporting to

[001-042] represent Boethius. Lord Preston's translation, for example, has such a

[001-043] portrait, which it refers to an original in marble at Rome. This I have

[001-044] been unable to trace, and suspect that it is apocryphal. The Hope

[001-045] Collection at Oxford contains a completely different portrait in a

[001-046] print, which gives no authority. I have ventured to use as a

[001-047] frontispiece a reproduction from a plaster-cast in the Ashmolean Museum,

[001-048] taken from an ivory diptych preserved in the Bibliotheca Quiriniana at

[001-049] Brescia, which represents Narius Manlius Boethius, the father of the

[001-050] philosopher. Portraiture of this period is so rare that it seemed that,

[001-051] failing a likeness of the author himself, this authentic representation

[001-052] of his father might have interest, as giving the consular dress and

[001-053] insignia of the time, and also as illustrating the decadence of

[001-054] contemporary art. The consul wears a richly-embroidered cloak; his right

[001-055] hand holds a staff surmounted by the Roman eagle, his left the _mappa

[001-056] circensis,_ or napkin used for starting the races in the circus; at his

[001-057] feet are palms and bags of money--prizes for the victors in the games.

[001-058] For permission to use this cast my thanks are due to the authorities of

[001-059] the Ashmolean Museum, as also to Mr. T.W. Jackson, Curator of the Hope

[001-060] Collection, who first called my attention to its existence.

[001-061] I have to thank my brother, Mr. L. James, of Radley College, for much

[001-062] valuable help and for correcting the proof-sheets of the translation.

[001-063] The text used is that of Peiper, Leipsic, 1874.

[001-064] PROEM.

[001-065] Anicus Manlius Severinus Boethius lived in the last quarter of the fifth

[001-066] century A.D., and the first quarter of the sixth. He was growing to

[001-067] manhood, when [[theodoric|Theodoric]], the famous Ostrogoth, crossed the Alps and made

[001-068] himself master of Italy. Boethius belonged to an ancient family, which

[001-069] boasted a connection with the legendary glories of the Republic, and was

[001-070] still among the foremost in wealth and dignity in the days of Rome's

[001-071] abasement. His parents dying early, he was brought up by [[symmachus|Symmachus]], whom

[001-072] the age agreed to regard as of almost saintly character, and afterwards

[001-073] became his son-in-law. His varied gifts, aided by an excellent

[001-074] education, won for him the reputation of the most accomplished man of

[001-075] his time. He was orator, poet, musician, philosopher. It is his peculiar

[001-076] distinction to have handed on to the Middle Ages the tradition of Greek

[001-077] philosophy by his Latin translations of the works of Aristotle. Called

[001-078] early to a public career, the highest honours of the State came to him

[001-079] unsought. He was sole Consul in 510 A.D., and was ultimately raised by

[001-080] Theodoric to the dignity of Magister Officiorum, or head of the whole

[001-081] civil administration. He was no less happy in his domestic life, in the

[001-082] virtues of his wife, Rusticiana, and the fair promise of his two sons,

[001-083] Symmachus and Boethius; happy also in the society of a refined circle of

[001-084] friends. Noble, wealthy, accomplished, universally esteemed for his

[001-085] virtues, high in the favour of the Gothic King, he appeared to all men a

[001-086] signal example of the union of merit and good fortune. His felicity

[001-087] seemed to culminate in the year 522 A.D., when, by special and

[001-088] extraordinary favour, his two sons, young as they were for so exalted an

[001-089] honour, were created joint Consuls and rode to the senate-house

[001-090] attended by a throng of senators, and the acclamations of the multitude.

[001-091] Boethius himself, amid the general applause, delivered the public speech

[001-092] in the King's honour usual on such occasions. Within a year he was a

[001-093] solitary prisoner at Pavia, stripped of honours, wealth, and friends,

[001-094] with death hanging over him, and a terror worse than death, in the fear

[001-095] lest those dearest to him should be involved in the worst results of his

[001-096] downfall. It is in this situation that the opening of the 'Consolation

[001-097] of Philosophy' brings Boethius before us. He represents himself as

[001-098] seated in his prison distraught with grief, indignant at the injustice

[001-099] of his misfortunes, and seeking relief for his melancholy in writing

[001-100] verses descriptive of his condition. Suddenly there appears to him the

[001-101] Divine figure of Philosophy, in the guise of a woman of superhuman

[001-102] dignity and beauty, who by a succession of discourses convinces him of

[001-103] the vanity of regret for the lost gifts of fortune, raises his mind once

[001-104] more to the contemplation of [[truth|the true]] good, and makes clear to him the

[001-105] mystery of the world's moral government.

[001-154] SUMMARY.

[001-155] Boethius' complaint (Song I.).--CH. I. Philosophy appears to

[001-156] Boethius, drives away the Muses of Poetry, and herself laments

[001-157] (Song II.) the disordered condition of his mind.--CH. II. Boethius

[001-158] is speechless with amazement. Philosophy wipes away the tears that

[001-159] have clouded his eyesight.--CH. III. Boethius recognises his

[001-160] mistress Philosophy. To his wondering inquiries she explains her

[001-161] presence, and recalls to his mind the persecutions to which

[001-162] Philosophy has oftentimes from of old been subjected by an ignorant

[001-163] world. CH. IV. Philosophy bids Boethius declare his griefs. He

[001-164] relates the story of his unjust accusation and ruin. He concludes

[001-165] with a prayer (Song V.) that the moral disorder in human affairs

[001-166] may be set right.--CH. V. Philosophy admits the justice of

[001-167] Boethius' self-vindication, but grieves rather for the unhappy

[001-168] change in his mind. She will first tranquillize his spirit by

[001-169] soothing remedies.--CH. VI. Philosophy tests Boethius' mental

[001-170] state by certain questions, and discovers three chief causes of his

[001-171] soul's sickness: (1) He has forgotten his own true nature; (2) he

[001-172] knows not the end towards which the whole universe tends; (3) he

[001-173] knows not the means by which the world is governed.

[001-195] O'er my head untimely sprinkled

[001-199] Blest is death that intervenes not

[001-200] In the sweet, sweet years of peace,

[001-201] But unto the broken-hearted,

[001-203] Yet Death passes by the wretched,

[001-204] Shuts his ear and slumbers deep;

[001-205] Will not heed the cry of anguish,

[001-206] Will not close the eyes that weep.

[001-207] For, while yet inconstant [[fortune|Fortune]]

[001-208] Poured her gifts and all was bright,

[001-209] Death's dark hour had all but whelmed me

[001-210] In the gloom of endless night.

[001-211] Now, because misfortune's shadow

[001-212] Hath o'erclouded that false face,

[001-213] Cruel Life still halts and lingers,

[001-214] Though I loathe his weary race.

[001-215] Friends, why did ye once so lightly

[001-216] Vaunt me happy among men?

[001-217] Surely he who so hath fallen

[001-218] Was not firmly founded then.

[001-219] I.

[001-220] While I was thus mutely pondering within myself, and recording my

[001-221] sorrowful complainings with my pen, it seemed to me that there appeared

[001-222] above my head a woman of a countenance exceeding venerable. Her eyes

[001-223] were bright as fire, and of a more than human keenness; her complexion

[001-224] was lively, her vigour showed no trace of enfeeblement; and yet her

[001-225] years were right full, and she plainly seemed not of our age and time.

[001-226] Her stature was difficult to judge. At one moment it exceeded not the

[001-227] common height, at another her forehead seemed to strike the sky; and

[001-228] whenever she raised her head higher, she began to pierce within the very

[001-229] heavens, and to baffle the eyes of them that looked upon her. Her

[001-230] garments were of an imperishable fabric, wrought with the finest threads

[001-231] and of the most delicate workmanship; and these, as her own lips

[001-232] afterwards assured me, she had herself woven with her own hands. The

[001-233] beauty of this vesture had been somewhat tarnished by age and neglect,

[001-234] and wore that dingy look which marble contracts from exposure. On the

[001-235] lower-most edge was inwoven the Greek letter [Greek: P], on the topmost

[001-236] the letter [Greek: Th],[A] and between the two were to be seen steps,

[001-237] like a staircase, from the lower to the upper letter. This robe,

[001-238] moreover, had been torn by the hands of violent persons, who had each

[001-239] snatched away what he could clutch.[B] Her right hand held a note-book;

[001-240] in her left she bore a staff. And when she saw the Muses of Poesie

[001-241] standing by my bedside, dictating the words of my lamentations, she was

[001-242] moved awhile to wrath, and her eyes flashed sternly. 'Who,' said she,

[001-243] 'has allowed yon play-acting wantons to approach this sick man--these

[001-244] who, so far from giving medicine to heal his malady, even feed it with

[001-245] sweet poison? These it is who kill the rich crop of reason with the

[001-246] barren thorns of passion, who accustom men's minds to disease, instead

[001-247] of setting them free. Now, were it some common man whom your allurements

[001-248] were seducing, as is usually your way, I should be less indignant. On

[001-249] such a one I should not have spent my pains for naught. But this is one

[001-250] nurtured in the Eleatic and Academic philosophies. Nay, get ye gone, ye

[001-251] sirens, whose sweetness lasteth not; leave him for my muses to tend and

[001-252] heal!' At these words of upbraiding, the whole band, in deepened

[001-253] sadness, with downcast eyes, and blushes that confessed their shame,

[001-254] dolefully left the chamber.
