## Overview

Plato's *Republic* is the most important philosophical precursor to [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *Consolation of Philosophy*. The *Republic*'s central argument — that [[justice|justice]] is better than injustice, that the just person is happier than the unjust person regardless of external circumstances — is the direct ancestor of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument about the relationship between [[virtue|virtue]] and [[happiness|happiness]].

## Role in the Consolation

The *Republic*'s influence on the *Consolation* is pervasive. Philosophy's hierarchy of cognition (sense, imagination, reason, intelligence) echoes the Divided Line. Her argument that the wicked are punished by their very wickedness echoes the argument of the *Gorgias* and *Republic*. Her vision of the philosopher who is forced to return to the political cave and is persecuted for it echoes the Allegory of the Cave. And her conclusion — that all things are ordered toward the Good, and that understanding this is the key to happiness — is Platonic to its core.

Boethius's *Consolation* is, in one light, a Latin Christian rewriting of the *Republic*'s central argument for a dying man in a prison cell.
