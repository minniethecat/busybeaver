# Consolation

The **consolation** (*consolatio*) is a literary and philosophical genre that offers comfort and healing in the face of suffering, death, or misfortune through rational argument. Boethius's *The Consolation of Philosophy* transforms this classical rhetorical form into a philosophical dialogue in which the therapeutic process itself becomes the vehicle for systematic inquiry into the nature of [[happiness]], [[evil]], [[providence]], and [[divine-foreknowledge|divine foreknowledge]].

---

## Definition & Context

The *consolatio* is a genre of philosophical writing that addresses grief, misfortune, or the fear of death with the aim of restoring tranquillity to a disturbed mind. Its defining move is the therapeutic application of reason to emotion: the consoler does not merely sympathise with suffering but seeks to cure it by correcting the cognitive errors that produce it.

In Boethius's hands, the consolation becomes a comprehensive philosophical therapy. It moves from diagnosing false beliefs about [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts to demonstrating the nature of [[true-happiness|true happiness]] and reconciling human freedom with divine foreknowledge (020-100, 020-101, 020-102).

[[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] articulates the genre's therapeutic logic when she explains that "it is not yet time for strong remedies" and that the mind, "when it casts off true opinions it straightway puts on false, wherefrom arises a cloud of confusion that disturbs its true vision" (004-037, 004-039, 004-040).

The consolation does not merely distract from suffering -- it restores the mind's capacity to see reality clearly: "thanks be to the Author of our health, the light of nature hath not yet left thee utterly" (004-032). Philosophy declares her method explicitly:

> "I will now try and disperse these mists by mild and soothing application, that so the darkness of misleading passion may be scattered, and thou mayst come to discern the splendour of the true light." (004-041, 004-042, 004-043)

The diagnosis is precise. Philosophy identifies three causes of Boethius's sickness: forgetfulness of his own nature (004-024), ignorance of the end of existence (004-026, 004-027), and failure to understand the means by which the world is governed (004-028, 004-029). These three deficiencies correspond to the three stages of the consolation's argument: the critique of Fortune, the ascent to the [[supreme-good|supreme Good]], and the reconciliation of [[providence]] with [[free-will|free will]].

## The Classical Consolatio Tradition

The consolation genre originated in Greek philosophy. **Crantor of Soli** (c. 335-275 BC), a member of the Old Academy, wrote the first philosophical treatise *On Grief* (*Peri Penthous*), which established the genre's conventions (001-026). The consoler acknowledges the reality of suffering, argues that grief is natural but must be moderated, and deploys a standard repertoire of arguments: death is not an evil, the deceased would not wish you to grieve, all humans are mortal, time heals all wounds.

**Cicero** developed the genre in Latin, composing a *Consolatio* on the death of his daughter Tullia (now lost) and treating grief extensively in the *Tusculan Disputations* (001-026). Cicero argued that the root of grief is opinion (*opinio*) -- the false judgment that something terrible has occurred -- and that philosophy's task is to correct this judgment through reasoned argument.

**Seneca** sharpened the Stoic argument in multiple works (*Ad Marciam*, *Ad Helviam*, *Ad Polybium*), contending that philosophical reason can overcome emotional suffering by demonstrating that external goods are indifferents, neither good nor evil in themselves (001-026). Boethius inherits this tradition's core conviction -- that suffering arises from cognitive error and can be cured by rational correction -- but transforms every feature of the genre.

## Boethius's Transformation of the Genre

### From Rhetoric to Dialogue

Where earlier consolations were letters or treatises addressed by a philosopher to a mourner, Boethius transforms the genre into a philosophical dialogue between the prisoner and [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] (001-035).

This formal innovation is therapeutically decisive: the consolation unfolds not as a static set of arguments but as a dramatic process of intellectual and spiritual healing. The patient's resistance, confusion, and gradual recovery are rendered visible. Philosophy does not simply present conclusions -- she draws them from Boethius through question and answer, in the Socratic manner, so that the cure is experienced as self-discovery rather than instruction.

Philosophy's therapeutic method follows a medical progression. She begins with "soothing remedies" (001-169), acknowledging Boethius's grief and recalling the blessings that remain to him -- his family, his father-in-law [[symmachus|Symmachus]], the "anchors still holding firm which suffer thee not to lack either consolation in the present or hope for the future" (005-121, 005-122).

Only when "reasonings begin to work a soothing effect within thy mind" does she turn to "remedies somewhat stronger" (006-002, 006-003). The movement from gentle to stronger medicine mirrors the dialogue's ascent from Fortune's gifts to the nature of the [[good|true Good]] itself.

### The Prosimetrum Form

Boethius alternates prose passages with verse (*prosimetrum*), following the Menippean satire tradition (001-035). The 39 poems punctuate the argument at crucial moments, offering aesthetic pleasure while advancing the philosophical content. The opening elegy (1.M1) dramatises Boethius's despair in verse before Philosophy's entrance; her own songs provide lyric summaries of arguments just made.

Philosophy explains the therapeutic function of this alternation when she calls upon "the sweet persuasiveness of Rhetoric" and "Music, my handmaid," bidding them "join with her singing, now in lighter, now in graver strain" (004-117, 004-118, 004-119). The lighter and graver registers correspond to the stages of treatment: gentler songs prepare "the way for stronger potions" (004-116). The prosimetrum form is not decorative but functional -- it calibrates the emotional register of the argument to the patient's condition.

### Philosophy Personified

The personification of Philosophy as a woman of simultaneously ancient and ageless appearance is Boethius's most original contribution to the genre (001-220, 001-224). She is both consoler and cure -- not merely offering arguments but actively restoring the prisoner's philosophical vision. Her stature is impossible to fix, at one moment not exceeding human height, at another "her forehead seemed to strike the sky" (001-227). The Greek letters on her robe, [Pi] and [Theta], mark the staircase from practical to theoretical life (001-234, 001-235, 001-236).

When Philosophy first appears, she does not immediately console -- she attacks. She drives away the Muses of Poetry, calling them "play-acting wantons" who, "so far from giving medicine to heal his malady, even feed it with sweet poison" (001-243, 001-244, 001-245). The Muses "kill the rich crop of reason with the barren thorns of passion" and "accustom men's minds to disease, instead of setting them free" (001-245, 001-246).

The implication is radical: poetry that merely expresses suffering, without subjecting it to rational scrutiny, is not consolation -- it is complicity in the disease. True consolation requires the replacement of lamentation with understanding.

### From Particular Loss to Universal Questions

While traditional consolations addressed specific bereavements or misfortunes, Boethius's *Consolation* begins with the loss of fortune, reputation, and freedom but ascends to universal questions (020-100, 020-101, 020-102).

Each book rises to a higher level of abstraction and consolation: Book I diagnoses the sickness (004-024), Book II treats the symptom (attachment to Fortune), Book III treats the cause (ignorance of the true Good), Book IV addresses the obstacle (the [[problem-of-evil|problem of evil]]), and Book V provides the ultimate perspective -- [[time-and-eternity|divine eternity]] (020-103, 020-104).

The genre becomes a vehicle for systematic philosophical inquiry. The particular suffering of one prisoner becomes the occasion for demonstrating the rational structure of reality itself. The consolation is complete only when Boethius sees his own situation *sub specie aeternitatis*.

## Key Passages

The therapeutic structure of the consolation is announced early in Book I, when Philosophy diagnoses the nature of Boethius's sickness and the path to its cure:

> "But seeing that it is not yet time for strong remedies, and that the mind is manifestly so constituted that when it casts off true opinions it straightway puts on false, wherefrom arises a cloud of confusion that disturbs its true vision, I will now try and disperse these mists by mild and soothing application, that so the darkness of misleading passion may be scattered, and thou mayst come to discern the splendour of the true light." (004-037, 004-038, 004-039, 004-040, 004-041, 004-042, 004-043)

The transition from gentle to stronger remedies marks Philosophy's confidence that the cure is taking hold:

> "But since my reasonings begin to work a soothing effect within thy mind, methinks I may resort to remedies somewhat stronger." (006-002, 006-003)

The personal dimension of consolation -- the acknowledgment that Boethius still has blessings -- provides the emotional ground for the philosophical ascent that follows:

> "Wherefore, now dry thy tears. Fortune's hate hath not involved all thy dear ones; the stress of the storm that has assailed thee is not beyond measure intolerable, since there are anchors still holding firm which suffer thee not to lack either consolation in the present or hope for the future." (005-118, 005-119, 005-120, 005-121)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's argument for the efficacy of rational consolation rests on three interconnected claims.

First, suffering arises from error. Philosophy's diagnosis in Book I identifies Boethius's grief as a consequence of false beliefs: "It is because forgetfulness of thyself hath bewildered thy mind that thou hast bewailed thee as an exile, as one stripped of the blessings that were his" (004-024, 004-025, 004-026).

His emotional state is a response not to objective misfortune but to cognitive distortion -- he has forgotten what he is (a rational being) and what he knows (that the world is governed by divine reason, not chance) (004-033, 004-034). The therapy follows from the diagnosis: correct the beliefs, and the emotions will follow.

Second, external goods cannot satisfy. Philosophy systematically demonstrates, through Books II and III, that Fortune's gifts -- wealth, power, fame, pleasure -- are neither truly ours nor truly good (005-168, 005-169, 005-170). The argument culminates in the demonstration that "the highest good of a creature living in accordance with reason" cannot be something that can be taken away -- which eliminates every candidate offered by Fortune (005-173).

Third, genuine happiness is internal and divine. The true Good is identified with God, and [[happiness]] consists in participation in that Good: "God is happiness itself" (012-041). The soul that has grasped this truth cannot be made miserable by external circumstances, because its happiness is grounded in something that no prison, no executioner, and no change of fortune can touch.

## Boethius's Position

Boethius enters the dialogue as a patient who has forgotten his own philosophical commitments. He acknowledges: "I once heard these things, but sorrow hath dulled my recollection" (004-007). The consolation is therefore not instruction from ignorance but recollection from forgetfulness -- *anamnesis* in the Platonic sense. The "germs of truth still lie within" (012-034); Philosophy's task is to "fan" them back to life.

His transformation is marked by a series of recognitions. In Book I, he acknowledges Philosophy but cannot answer her diagnostic questions (004-007). In Book II, he accepts her arguments about Fortune but protests that they are not yet sufficient to console him.

By Book III, he has begun to recover his philosophical vision, confessing that "these things I had not altogether forgotten, but sorrow had dulled my memory of them" (012-032). In Book IV, he raises the problem of evil as the last obstacle to full recovery -- a sign of progress, since he is now thinking philosophically rather than lamenting.

By Book V, he is an active interlocutor, pressing Philosophy on the hardest questions of divine foreknowledge and human freedom. The trajectory from patient to philosopher is the consolation's deepest argument: the process of being consoled is identical to the process of becoming a philosopher.
