## Identity & Context

Socrates (c. 470–399 BC) was an Athenian philosopher and the teacher of Plato. In the *Consolation of Philosophy*, Boethius invokes Socrates three times as a paradigmatic figure: a philosopher who faced an unjust death with equanimity, and whose commitment to truth serves as a moral exemplar (002-007).

Lady Philosophy recalls Socrates alongside other victims of persecution — Zeno, Anaxagoras, Seneca, and Soranus — to illustrate that the wise have always been subject to attacks by the ignorant (002-007). The cumulative weight of these examples serves Philosophy's argument that Boethius should not be surprised by his own suffering.

## Role in the Consolation

Socrates appears in Book I, where Philosophy diagnoses Boethius's forgetfulness of his philosophical heritage. The mention of Socrates's "victory of an unjust death" establishes a pattern: the philosopher who remains faithful to wisdom prevails even in apparent defeat (002-007).

In a later passage, Boethius explicitly invokes the "precept of Socrates" — the obligation to keep truth unconcealed and not to allow falsehood to pass unchallenged (007-032). This aligns with Socratic intellectual honesty and reinforces Boethius's own project of philosophical self-examination under Philosophy's guidance.

## Key References

Boethius's three invocations of Socrates form a coherent arc:

> with the rashness of folly? In his lifetime, too, Socrates, his master, won with my aid the victory of an unjust death. (002-007)

This passage frames Socrates's death as a moral victory rather than a defeat — a crucial consolation theme.

> thou knowest not of the banishment of Anaxagoras, of the poison draught of Socrates, nor of Zeno's torturing, because these things happened in a distant country (002-007)

Philosophy argues that ignorance of history makes suffering seem unique; knowing Socrates's fate contextualizes present misfortune.

> mindful of the precept of Socrates, I do not think it right either to keep the truth concealed or allow falsehood to pass. (007-032)

Boethius applies the Socratic imperative — truth-telling as a philosophical duty — to his own situation, justifying the frankness of the dialogue.

## Philosophical Significance

Socrates represents the conjunction of intellectual integrity and moral courage in the *Consolation*. His "precept" about truth-concealment frames Boethius's own act of writing the dialogue as a Socratic enterprise (007-032). The reference to his death by hemlock (002-007) places him in a lineage of philosophical martyrs, a lineage that Boethius himself would join.

Philosophy uses Socrates to illustrate a core argument: apparent injustice (execution, exile, torture) does not refute the power of wisdom; rather, the philosopher's endurance demonstrates wisdom's superior claim over fortune.
