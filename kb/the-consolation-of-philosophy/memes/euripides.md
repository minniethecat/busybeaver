## Identity & Context

Euripides (c. 480–406 BC) was the youngest of the three great Athenian tragedians, alongside Aeschylus and Sophocles. His plays — *Medea*, *The Bacchae*, *Hippolytus*, *The Trojan Women* — are marked by psychological realism, sceptical questioning of traditional religion, and an unflinching examination of human suffering that made him, in antiquity, the most controversial and the most influential of the tragedians.

Boethius refers to him with the striking possessiveness: "my servant Euripides" (009-108). The phrase echoes his similarly affectionate reference to "my servant Plato" (002-007) and signals that Euripides is being claimed as a philosophical ally — not merely a poet but a thinker whose insights align with Philosophy's own arguments. The Greek tragedian becomes, in Boethius's Latin prison cell, a witness for the prosecution against Fortune's gifts.

## Role in the Consolation

Euripides appears in a single passage, but one of strategic importance. In Book III, Philosophy is cataloguing the false goods that human beings mistake for happiness — wealth, power, fame, bodily pleasure. Children, she argues, belong to this category: they are commonly counted among the blessings of life, but they bring as much anxiety as joy.

She invokes Euripides as her authority: "I agree with my servant Euripides, who said that a man without children is fortunate" (009-108). The line comes from Euripides's *Andromache* (319–320; 020-111), and it is cited not as a counsel of despair but as a sober assessment of the human condition. Children, like all external goods, are gifts of Fortune — precious but fragile, a source of love that is also a source of vulnerability. The parent who has children fears for them; the parent who loses them suffers irreparably. To be without children is, in this limited sense, to be free of a particular form of Fortune's tyranny.

Philosophy's use of Euripides is characteristically dialectical. She does not endorse the tragic view that life is meaningless suffering — that would contradict her entire therapeutic project. Instead, she deploys Euripides's pessimism as a diagnostic tool: it exposes the error of attaching one's happiness to mutable goods, but it does so in order to redirect desire toward the immutable Good, which is God.

## Key References

Philosophy claims Euripides as an ally:

> "I agree with my servant Euripides, who said that a man without children is fortunate" (009-108)

The source is identified in the footnotes:

> "Euripides, Andromache, 319, 320" (020-111)

The claim is situated within the larger critique of false goods — children, like wealth and power, are Fortune's gifts, not the soul's true possession.

## Philosophical Significance

Euripides represents a particular strand of ancient wisdom that Boethius incorporates into his philosophical synthesis: the tragic insight into the fragility of human goods. Unlike Plato and Aristotle, who are cited as authorities on metaphysics and ethics, Euripides is cited as an authority on suffering. His plays are extended meditations on what it means to lose everything — children, home, honour, life itself — and his characters' laments are philosophically significant because they articulate the very condition that Philosophy seeks to cure.

The phrase "my servant Euripides" also reveals something about Boethius's intellectual world. An Athenian tragedian who died nine centuries before Boethius's birth is claimed as a personal intellectual resource — a "servant" whose insights are still live and usable. This is the humanist vision that the *Consolation* both embodies and transmits: the great minds of antiquity are not dead authorities but living conversation partners in the perennial task of understanding the human condition.
