# Divine Intellect

The **Divine Intellect** (*divina intelligentia*, Greek *Nous*) is the highest mode of cognition in [[boethius|Boethius]]'s hierarchy of knowing, presented in Book V of *The Consolation of Philosophy*. It is the cognitive faculty proper to God, by which He knows all things — past, present, and future — in a single, timeless act of understanding.

## The Hierarchy of Cognition

In one of the most philosophically dense passages of the *Consolation* (5.P4.24–5.P5.4), [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] outlines a fourfold hierarchy of cognitive faculties:

| Faculty | Object | Mode | Subject |
|---------|--------|------|---------|
| **Sense** (*sensus*) | Material bodies | Grasps shape in matter | Animals |
| **Imagination** (*imaginatio*) | Forms without matter | Grasps sensible forms apart from matter | Higher animals |
| **[[reason|Reason]]** (*ratio*) | Universal natures | Abstracts universals from particulars | Humans |
| **Intelligence** (*intelligentia*) | Simple Form itself | Sees all in one act, with the eye of the mind | God |

## The Distinction Between Reason and Intelligence

The crucial distinction is between reason (*ratio*) and intelligence (*intelligentia*):

- **Reason** knows discursively, moving step by step from premises to conclusions, from particular to universal. It operates within time, considering one thing after another. This is the human mode of knowing.

- **Intelligence** knows intuitively, grasping all [[truth|truths]] simultaneously in a single act. It transcends temporal succession entirely. This is the divine mode of knowing.

This distinction grounds Boethius's solution to the [[divine-foreknowledge|problem of foreknowledge]]: God does not "fore"-know in the temporal sense (looking ahead from a point in time) but rather knows all temporal events in His eternal present through intelligence (5.P6.1–5.P6.11).

## Neoplatonic Background

The concept of Divine Intellect derives from the [[neoplatonism|Neoplatonic]] triad of hypostases:

1. **The One** (τὸ Ἕν): the ineffable first principle, beyond being and thought
2. **Intellect** (*Nous*): the realm of Being and the Forms, where knower and known are identical
3. **[[soul|Soul]]** (*Psychē*): the principle of life and motion, operating in time

Boethius's Divine Intellect corresponds to the second hypostasis — the realm where divine knowing occurs. However, as a Christian philosopher, Boethius identifies the One and Intellect with the single God of Christian theology, simplifying the Neoplatonic hierarchy.

## The Hymn "O qui perpetua" (3.M9)

Boethius's most sustained treatment of the Divine Intellect appears not in prose argument but in the great hymn of Book III (3.M9), which describes the World-Soul emanating from the Divine Mind:

Philosophy's hymn invokes God as the one "who govern[s] the world with perpetual reason" and as "Sower of earth and heaven, who command[s] time to proceed from [[eternity]]" (012-041).

This hymn, modeled on [[plato|Plato]]'s *Timaeus*, depicts the Divine Intellect as the source of cosmic order, containing within itself the eternal patterns according to which the temporal world is arranged.

## Philosophical Significance

Boethius's distinction between reason and intelligence was profoundly influential:
- **Thomas Aquinas** adopted a similar distinction between human reason and angelic/divine intellect
- The concept of God's [[knowledge]] as non-discursive and atemporal became standard in scholastic theology
- The hierarchy of cognition provided a framework for understanding the relationship between philosophy (human reason) and theology (divine revelation)
