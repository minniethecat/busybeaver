# Free Will

The capacity of rational creatures to exercise voluntary choice (*liberum arbitrium*), defended by Lady Philosophy as both incompatible with fatalism and reconcilable with divine foreknowledge through the distinction between simple and conditional necessity.

---

## Definition & Context

Free will, in Boethius' argument, is the faculty of discriminative judgment inherent to every rational being. Lady Philosophy asserts that "nor, indeed, can any creature be rational, unless he be endowed with free will" (017-193). This faculty allows the mind to distinguish "what is to be shunned or desired" and to act upon that discrimination without external compulsion (017-193). Freedom of choice is presented as "a necessary attribute of reason," though Philosophy grants that human freedom is "a less perfect freedom than divine natures" (017-080, 017-081).

The problem occupies the culminating argument of the entire *Consolation* (Book V, Prose 2--6). Boethius poses the dilemma as a prisoner who has lost all external liberty yet asks whether the soul retains any inner freedom. Philosophy's task is to reconcile two apparently contradictory truths: that God foreknows all future events with certainty, and that human beings possess genuine freedom of the will (018-026, 018-027).

---

## Key Passages

> "There is freedom," said she; "nor, indeed, can any creature be rational, unless he be endowed with free will. For that which hath the natural use of reason has the faculty of discriminative judgment, and of itself distinguishes what is to be shunned or desired." (017-193)

> "For if God foresees everything, and can in no wise be deceived, that which providence foresees to be about to happen must necessarily come to pass. Wherefore, if from eternity He foreknows not only what men will do, but also their designs and purposes, there can be no freedom of the will." (018-027, 018-028, 018-030, 018-031)

> "So, then, there are two necessities--one simple, as that men are necessarily mortal; the other conditioned, as that, if you know that someone is walking, he must necessarily be walking. For that which is known cannot indeed be otherwise than as it is known to be, and yet this fact by no means carries with it that other simple necessity." (020-042, 020-043, 020-044, 020-045, 020-046)

> "And all this being so, the freedom of man's will stands unshaken, and laws are not unrighteous, since their rewards and punishments are held forth to wills unbound by any necessity." (020-100, 020-101)

---

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's response to the dilemma proceeds through several interlocking moves.

**The epistemological principle.** She establishes that knowledge depends not on the nature of the thing known but on the faculty of the knower (017-092, 017-093). Just as sense-perception and reason apprehend the same object differently, so divine intelligence apprehends temporal events in a mode transcending temporal succession. This undermines the assumption that God's foreknowledge operates like human prediction (019-010, 019-011).

**God's eternal present.** Philosophy redefines God's knowledge not as foreknowledge of the future but as knowledge of an eternal present. God "abides for ever in an eternal present" and "His knowledge, also transcending all movement of time, dwells in the simplicity of its own changeless present" (020-002, 020-003). Divine cognition "embracing the whole infinite sweep of the past and of the future, contemplates all that falls within its simple cognition as if it were now taking place" (020-004, 020-005, 020-006). She therefore insists it is more properly called *providence* (looking forth from a height) than *prevision* (020-011, 020-012).

**The spectator analogy.** Philosophy compares God's eternal vision to a human spectator watching events unfold in the present. "Even as ye, when at one and the same time ye see a man walking on the earth and the sun rising in the sky, distinguish between the two, though one glance embraces both, and judge the former voluntary, the latter necessary action: so also the Divine vision in its universal range of view does in no wise confuse the characters of the things it surveys" (020-025, 020-026, 020-027, 020-028). The act of seeing does not impose necessity on the thing seen (020-015, 020-016).

**Conditional vs. simple necessity.** The crucial distinction is between two kinds of necessity. Simple necessity belongs to a thing by its own nature (e.g., all humans are mortal). Conditional necessity arises only from the addition of a condition: if someone is walking, it is necessary that they walk *while* they walk, but no necessity compels them to walk in the first place (020-042, 020-048, 020-049). God sees free actions as present; they are conditionally necessary with respect to divine vision but retain their intrinsic freedom when considered in themselves (020-053, 020-054, 020-055, 020-056).

**Moral consequences.** Philosophy emphasizes that without free will, the entire moral order collapses: "rewards and punishments are unjust as well as useless; merit and demerit are mere names; God is the cause of men's wickednesses; prayer is meaningless" (017-085, 017-086, 017-087, 017-088). The defense of free will is therefore not merely metaphysical but ethical: "Great is the necessity of righteousness laid upon you if ye will not hide it from yourselves, seeing that all your actions are done before the eyes of a Judge who seeth all things" (020-109, 020-110, 020-111).

---

## Boethius' Position

Boethius begins Book V expressing sharp perplexity. He states the contradiction with precision: "It seems too much of a paradox and a contradiction that God should know all things, and yet there should be free will" (018-026, 018-027). He articulates the full cascade of consequences that would follow if free will were denied, including the collapse of justice, merit, and prayer (018-104, 018-105, 018-106, 018-107).

By the conclusion of Philosophy's argument, Boethius accepts the resolution. The text presents him as following Philosophy's reasoning "needfully" and agreeing with her premises before posing his own objections (017-193). His final position is one of reasoned acceptance: divine foreknowledge, properly understood as eternal present-knowledge, does not destroy human freedom but rather guarantees the moral order of the universe (020-100, 020-101).

The resolution is particularly significant given Boethius' personal circumstances. Imprisoned and awaiting execution, the question of whether any freedom remains to the soul was not merely an academic exercise but an existential one. Philosophy's vindication of free will affirms that even one whose body is in chains retains the voluntary motion of the rational soul (017-193).
