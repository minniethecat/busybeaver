# Supreme Good

The **Supreme Good** (*summum bonum*) is the central ethical and metaphysical concept of Book III of Boethius's *The Consolation of Philosophy*. Lady Philosophy argues that all human striving is ultimately directed toward happiness (*beatitudo*) — but true happiness can only be found in the Supreme Good, which she identifies with God (011-027). The argument proceeds through a rigorous dialectical chain: the insufficiency of all partial goods, the unity of the true goods, the identification of happiness with the good, and finally the identification of both with God (011-038).

---

## Definition & Context

In Boethius's philosophical system, the Supreme Good is the *summum bonum* — that which all things desire as their ultimate end (012-014). It is characterized by three essential attributes:

1. **Self-sufficiency**: the Supreme Good leaves nothing to be desired beyond itself. It is complete and lacks nothing, requiring no external supplement (011-086).
2. **Perfection**: it contains within itself all particular goods in their most perfect and unified form. Partial goods — wealth, power, honor, fame, pleasure — are mere "shadows of the true good" (010-126–010-128).
3. **Unity**: the Supreme Good is one and indivisible. What human error separates into distinct goods is in reality a single, unified perfection. "That which is simple and indivisible by nature human error separates, and transforms from the true and perfect to the false and imperfect" (010-042–010-044).

Philosophy's argument is set within the dialogue's larger therapeutic project: Boethius, imprisoned and awaiting execution, has forgotten his true nature and must be led back from attachment to perishable goods toward the recognition of the one true Good (010-130–010-132).

---

## Key Passages

> "All things, then, seek the good; indeed, you may express the fact by defining good as that which all desire. ... Either there is no single end to which all things are relative, or else the end to which all things universally hasten must be the highest good of all." — (012-010–012-014)

This passage crystallizes Philosophy's teleological argument: every being, by its very nature, pursues the good. The convergence of all desires toward a single end establishes that end as the highest good.

> "That which is simple and indivisible by nature human error separates, and transforms from the true and perfect to the false and imperfect. Dost thou imagine that which lacketh nothing can want power?" — (010-042–010-045)

Here Philosophy identifies the root error of human pursuit: we fragment what is essentially one, chasing wealth *or* power *or* honor *or* pleasure as if they were independent ends, when in truth each is only a distorted reflection of the one Supreme Good.

> "For universally nothing can be better in nature than the source from which it has come; therefore on most true grounds of reason would I conclude that which is the source of all things to be in its own essence the highest good." — (011-020–011-023)

This establishes the ontological priority of the Supreme Good: nothing derived can surpass its source. Since God is the source of all existence, God must be goodness itself — not merely good by participation, but good essentially.

> "So every man who is happy is a god; and though in nature God is One only, yet there is nothing to hinder that very many should be gods by participation in that nature." — (011-050–011-052)

The remarkable corollary of the argument: human beings who attain true happiness do so not by possessing goods *from* God but by participating *in* God, becoming divine by participation — a conclusion Boethius himself calls "a fair conclusion, and a precious deduction" (011-053).

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Lady Philosophy's argument for the Supreme Good unfolds through a carefully structured dialectical progression spanning Book III, prose sections 9 through 12.

### The Insufficiency of Partial Goods

After demonstrating in prose 3 through 8 that wealth, power, honor, fame, and pleasure each fail to deliver the happiness they promise, Philosophy asks Boethius whether any mortal and perishable thing can produce true satisfaction. Boethius concedes they cannot, and Philosophy draws the crucial conclusion: "these things seem to give to mortals shadows of the true good, or some kind of imperfect good; but the true and perfect good they cannot bestow" (010-126–010-128). This prepares the ground for the positive argument — if true happiness cannot be found among partial goods, where *can* it be found?

### The Unity of the True Goods

Philosophy next demonstrates that the goods people pursue separately — independence (self-sufficiency), power, reverence, renown, and joy — are in reality one and the same thing seen from different angles. She shows that what is truly self-sufficient cannot lack power (010-050); what has true power and self-sufficiency must be worthy of reverence (010-052–010-057); what has independence, power, and reverence must possess renown; and what possesses all of these must also be joyful. Therefore "the nature of independence and power is one and the same" (010-050), and all five goods converge into a single unified perfection.

The reason human beings fail to grasp this is that "that which is simple and indivisible by nature human error separates" (010-042–010-043). Partial goods differ from one another precisely because they are fragments of the true good: "Have we not shown that those things which most men desire are not true and perfect good precisely for this cause — that they differ severally one from another, and, seeing that one is wanting to another, they cannot bestow full and absolute good?" (011-144–011-147).

### The Identification of Good, Happiness, and God

The argument then proceeds through three decisive identifications. First, since all things are sought for the sake of the good, "it is transparently clear that the essence of absolute good and of happiness is one and the same" (011-099–011-100). Second, God cannot have received goodness from an external source — for then the giver would be superior to God — nor can goodness be in God as a logically distinct attribute, for then God and the highest good would be separate entities, which would mean God is not the highest good, an impious conclusion (011-005–011-018). Therefore "that which is the source of all things" must be "in its own essence the highest good" (011-022–011-023). Third: since the highest good has been admitted to be happiness (011-025), "it is necessary to acknowledge that God is very happiness" (011-027).

A second argument confirms this from the impossibility of two distinct supreme goods: "there cannot be two supreme goods distinct one from the other. For the goods which are different clearly cannot be severally each what the other is: wherefore neither of the two can be perfect, since to either the other is wanting; but since it is not perfect, it cannot manifestly be the supreme good" (011-032–011-036). Since both happiness and God have been concluded to be the supreme good, they must be identical — and "that which is highest Divinity must also itself necessarily be supreme happiness" (011-038–011-040).

### The Corollary of Deification

Philosophy adds a corollary "just as geometricians are wont to draw inferences from their demonstrations" (011-043–011-045): since men become happy by acquiring happiness, and happiness is Godship, men who attain happiness acquire Godship — and just as acquiring justice makes one just, acquiring Godship makes one a god by participation (011-045–011-052). This startling conclusion, which Boethius calls "a fair and precious deduction" (011-053), grafts Christian deification theology onto Neoplatonic participation metaphysics.

### The Argument from Unity

Prose 11 concludes with an argument from the nature of unity. All things subsist only so long as they remain one; when unity is lost, the thing perishes (011-164–011-166). But unity and goodness have been shown to be the same: "when things are far from being good while they are different, but become good as soon as they are one, is it not true that these become good by acquiring unity?" (011-153–011-155). And all things that are good are good by participation in goodness (011-157–011-158). Therefore "unity and goodness are the same" (011-160–011-162). Since all things desire to continue in being — which means all things desire unity (012-006) — and unity is the same as the good (012-008), "all things, then, seek the good" (012-010). The end toward which the whole universe tends is therefore the Supreme Good (012-014), and "we ought to acknowledge the end and aim of the whole universe to be 'the good'" (012-021–012-022).

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's position in the dialogue is that of a receptive but struggling pupil who must be led step by step from confusion to clarity. At the outset, his grief and despair have clouded his philosophical vision; he has forgotten the truths he once knew, having "lost them through the clogging contact of the body" and "through the stress of heavy grief" (012-041). He repeatedly assents to Philosophy's arguments — "I cannot gainsay my former admissions" (011-029), "No conclusion could be truer to fact, nor more soundly reasoned out, nor more worthy of God" (011-041–011-042) — but his assent is the recovery of knowledge already possessed, not the acquisition of new doctrine.

This is crucial to the dialogue's therapeutic function. The argument for the Supreme Good is not merely a theoretical demonstration but a remedy for Boethius's condition. He has been shattered by the loss of worldly goods — position, wealth, reputation, freedom — and Philosophy's task is to show him that what he has lost are mere shadows of the true good, which cannot be taken away (005-168–005-170). The Prisoner's gradual recognition that true happiness "cannot be found among the perishable things" (007-212–007-213) and that "its seat is in God" (007-214) is simultaneously a philosophical insight and a spiritual healing.

Boethius's own contribution to the tradition lies in his synthesis. As a Christian writing in the Neoplatonic philosophical idiom, he demonstrates that reason alone — without appeal to revelation — can establish the identity of the Supreme Good with God. The argument is constructed entirely from philosophical premises (the nature of desire, the logic of perfection and participation, the teleology of being), yet its conclusion — that "God's essence is seated in absolute good, and nowhere else" (011-105–011-106) — is fully consonant with Christian theology.

## Philosophical Significance

Boethius's argument synthesizes several major philosophical traditions:

- **Plato's Form of the Good** (*Republic* Book VI–VII), the ultimate principle that illuminates all other forms and is the source of being and knowability
- **Aristotle's** analysis of the highest good as the final end of all human action in the *Nicomachean Ethics*
- **Neoplatonic** emanation theory, in which all goods derive from and point back toward the One, the source of all being and unity
- **Christian** theology, which identifies the philosophical Absolute with the biblical God — not as a separate revelation but as the rational conclusion of philosophy

This synthesis became foundational for medieval philosophy, directly influencing Anselm's ontological argument (which draws on Boethius's reasoning that nothing can be conceived greater than God) and Aquinas's natural theology (which likewise argues that God is goodness itself, not merely good by participation).
