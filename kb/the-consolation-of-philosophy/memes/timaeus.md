## Overview

Plato's *Timaeus* is the ancient philosophical dialogue that most deeply informs the cosmological framework of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *Consolation of Philosophy*. The *Timaeus* describes the creation of the universe by a divine craftsman (the Demiurge), who imposes mathematical order on pre-existing matter and creates the World-Soul according to harmonic proportions.

## Role in the Consolation

The great hymn "O qui perpetua" (3.M9) is a direct imitation and adaptation of the *Timaeus*. Boethius follows Plato in describing the World-Soul, the mathematical structure of the cosmos, and the divine intellect that orders all things. The hymn translates Platonic cosmology into a Christian idiom — the Demiurge becomes the Christian God, and the World-Soul becomes an expression of divine [[providence|Providence]].

The *Timaeus* was one of the few Platonic dialogues available in Latin translation during Boethius's time (through Calcidius's partial translation and commentary). Boethius's engagement with it is both philosophical and poetic — he adopts its vision of an ordered cosmos governed by reason and expresses it in verse that rivals Plato's prose.
