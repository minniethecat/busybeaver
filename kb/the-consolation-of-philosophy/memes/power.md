## Definition & Context

Power (*potestas*) is the capacity to command, govern, or exercise authority over others, which [[boethius|Boethius]] once possessed as Consul and Master of Offices in the Ostrogothic court of [[theodoric|Theodoric]]. Philosophy systematically dismantles power as a candidate for [[good|true happiness]], arguing that it is external to the self, can be possessed by the wicked, and cannot make its possessor virtuous.

> "Now, if thou wilt consider the matter, thou wilt find that no power is complete in him who only exercises it over the bodies of others, but not over his own soul" (004-179)

## Philosophical Background

The critique of political power draws on both **Platonic** and **Stoic** sources. In Plato's *Republic*, the philosopher-king must first govern his own soul before he can govern others. The Stoics distinguished between what is *eph' hemin* (in our power) and what is not — only virtue and vice are truly within our control; everything else, including political authority, is indifferent.

Boethius's own experience gives the argument its existential weight. He had held the highest offices in the Roman world — consul, Master of Offices, trusted advisor to the king — and lost everything through false accusation. His argument that power is an illusory good is not abstract speculation but testimony from someone who possessed, exercised, and was destroyed by the very power he now critiques.

## Key Passages

On the nature of true power:

> "Those things that are in my power — now it is my will that none of these things shall be beyond your reach" (004-179)

Philosophy argues that the wicked, though they may hold political power, are in fact the most powerless of all, because they lack mastery over their own desires.

On the fragility of power:

> "She proveth her power; now tramples mighty kings beneath her feet; now sets the conquered in the victor's seat" (004-162)

The image of Fortune's wheel is particularly apt for political power: the same wheel that raises a man to the consulship can cast him into prison. Boethius experienced both extremes within a single year.

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument against power proceeds in three stages. First, she establishes that power is external — it does not belong to the person who exercises it but is a gift of [[fortune|Fortune]], and therefore subject to Fortune's mutability. "Happy and powerful; while, because thou hast forgotten by what means the earth is governed, thou deemest that fortune's changes ebb and flow without the restraint of a guiding hand" (004-028).

Second, she argues that power cannot be a genuine good because the wicked can possess it. If power were truly good, it would benefit its possessor; but history shows countless examples of the powerful who were also miserable. The tyrant who can kill but cannot control his own passions is, in truth, a slave — more powerless than the victim he persecutes.

Third, she redirects Boethius toward a higher understanding of power: the power of self-governance, the mastery of one's own desires and fears. This is the only power that cannot be taken away, because it resides entirely within the soul. The Consul who governed provinces but was governed by his own passions was never truly powerful; the prisoner who governs his own soul, though stripped of all political authority, possesses genuine power.

## Boethius' Position

[[boethius|Boethius]] begins the dialogue lamenting his loss of power and position. His opening elegy mourns not just his imprisonment but the contrast between his former dignity and his present abjection. Philosophy's therapeutic task is to show him that the power he lost was never really his — it was Fortune's, on loan, and its loss reveals not his degradation but his liberation from an illusion.

By the end of Book II, Boethius has begun to accept this argument grudgingly. By Book IV, he is ready to understand that true power consists not in commanding others but in participating in the divine order — that the wisest and most powerful being is God, who governs not by coercion but by the attraction of the Good.
