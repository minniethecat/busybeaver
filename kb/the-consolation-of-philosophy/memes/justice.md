## Definition & Context

Justice (*justitia*) in *The Consolation of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]* is the central problem that drives the entire dialogue. [[boethius|Boethius]]'s opening complaint is not about his personal suffering as such but about the apparent triumph of injustice. He is imprisoned and awaiting execution after a life of public service, "indignant at the injustice" (001-098) of his fate, while his accusers prosper. This is [[evil|the problem of evil]] applied to the specific question of justice: if God governs the world, why do the just suffer and the wicked thrive?

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] does not deny the premise. She "admits the justice of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s complaint" (001-166) in the sense that the facts are as he describes them — bad things do happen to good people. But she fundamentally reframes what justice means. Justice is not a balance sheet of earthly rewards and punishments; it is a metaphysical state that inheres in the soul. The just man is just because his soul is ordered toward [[good|the Good]]; the unjust man is unjust because his soul is disordered. And this internal condition is its own reward and its own punishment — the just possess [[good|the Good]], and the wicked have lost it.

## Key Passages

[[boethius|Boethius]]'s opening complaint establishes justice as the existential stake:

> "I had desired opportunity for action, lest opportunity should be lacking to the virtuous" (007-020)

His public career was motivated by "the love of justice" (002-007) — a desire to serve the common good. His imprisonment represents not merely personal misfortune but the apparent defeat of justice itself. The unjust are rewarded; the just are crushed. This is the wound that [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] must heal.

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s resolution comes through the argument that vice is its own punishment:

> "By the acquisition of justice men become just, and wise by the acquisition of wisdom" (011-048)

This apparently simple claim is the fulcrum of her theodicy. Justice is not an external reward for virtuous behavior but a quality of the soul itself. The just man possesses justice; the unjust man lacks it. And since justice is participation in [[good|the Good]], the just man already has what the wicked vainly seek through their [[evil|wickedness]]. The wicked are not "getting away with it" — they are suffering the worst possible punishment, which is the loss of their own humanity.

The eschatological dimension appears late in the argument:

> "Thou deemest the perfection of justice and scrupulous equity to be the mark of divine judgment" (016-043)

But [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] warns that from the human perspective, what counts as justice may appear as its opposite: "how esteemed the perfection of justice, will seem the most flagrant injustice" (018-107) when viewed from the temporal, unreflective human standpoint.

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument about justice operates by stages of increasing philosophical depth.

In the first stage (Books I–II), she challenges [[boethius|Boethius]]'s implicit assumption that justice means worldly success for the virtuous. [[fortune|Fortune]], she argues, is by nature fickle — it is not unjust that she withdraws her gifts from the good, because her gifts were never theirs by right. [[boethius|Boethius]]'s "love of justice" has been entangled with a love of outcomes — the gratitude of the senate, the success of just causes — that belong to [[fortune|Fortune]]'s domain, not to justice proper.

In the second stage (Book III), she establishes the identity of goodness and happiness. All human beings desire happiness, and happiness is the possession of [[good|the Good]]. The just man possesses [[good|the Good]] — or at least participates in it to the degree of his justice — while the wicked man, by his very [[evil|wickedness]], is separated from [[good|the Good]] and therefore unhappy. This is the "internalisation" of justice: reward and punishment are not external events added to virtue and vice but the intrinsic character of each state.

In the third stage (Books IV–V), she addresses the hardest case: what about the wicked who seem happy and powerful? Her answer is that they only "seem" so because we mistake the externals — wealth, power, physical pleasure — for genuine happiness. "Some, when they see the injustice which they suffer, are moved to indignation" (016-101), but this indignation is based on a category error. The wicked have already suffered the worst loss, which is the loss of their own moral being.

## Boethius' Position

[[boethius|Boethius]]'s relationship to justice is the emotional engine of the *Consolation*. He is a man who staked his life on justice — served the state, defended the innocent, resisted the powerful — and received death as his reward. His "bitter complaint of the injustice of the senate" (003-078) is not abstract philosophising; it is the cry of a man whose entire worldview has been shattered by experience.

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s therapy does not dismiss this experience but reframes it. She shows him that the justice he served was a shadow of true justice — good and noble, but still dependent on mutable institutions. True justice belongs to the soul's relationship with God, not to the senate's relationship with [[boethius|Boethius]]. By the end of the dialogue, [[boethius|Boethius]] has been brought to see that his suffering does not refute justice but reveals its true nature: justice is not about outcomes but about the soul's orientation toward [[good|the Good]]. The just man, whatever his earthly fate, already possesses what matters.
