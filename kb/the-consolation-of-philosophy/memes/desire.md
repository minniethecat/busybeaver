## Definition & Context

Desire (*cupiditas*, *appetitus*) is the soul's fundamental orientation toward what it perceives as good. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] builds her entire argument in Book III on the premise that all human beings naturally desire [[happiness|happiness]] (*beatitudo*) — the problem is not that we desire but that we misidentify the object of desire. We pursue [[wealth|wealth]], [[power|power]], [[fame|fame]], and [[pleasure|pleasure]] as if they were happiness itself, when in fact they are mere fragments that participate in the true [[good|Good]] without being identical to it.

> "Gold at man's desire" (005-009)

## Philosophical Background

The analysis of desire is central to ancient ethics. **Plato's** *Symposium* describes desire (*eros*) as the ladder by which the soul ascends from love of particular beautiful bodies to love of Beauty itself. **Aristotle** opens the *Nicomachean Ethics* with the observation that "every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action and pursuit, is thought to aim at some good" — desire is teleological, oriented toward an end. The **Stoics** distinguished between rational desire (*boulesis*), which aims at genuine goods, and irrational appetite (*epithumia*), which aims at apparent goods.

Boethius synthesises these traditions into a powerful argument: the universality of the desire for happiness proves both that happiness exists (nature does nothing in vain) and that it must be found in something that truly satisfies (since the desire is natural, its object must be attainable). The failure to achieve happiness is not a failure of desire but a failure of discernment — we desire the right thing (happiness) but pursue it in the wrong places (Fortune's gifts).

## Key Passages

On the universal desire for the good, Philosophy argues that all men naturally seek happiness:

> "Nothing to be truly desired, nothing of intrinsic excellence" — the false goods lack what would make them worthy of desire (006-180)

On the futility of desiring Fortune's gifts:

> "Why shouldst thou either grieve at the loss or rejoice at the possession of that in which there is no beauty to be desired?" (006-054)

On the desire for action and public service:

> "Yet I have desired opportunity for action, that virtue might not grow old in silence" (007-020)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's argument about desire is the turning point of the *Consolation*. In Book III, she shifts from the negative task (showing that Fortune's gifts do not satisfy) to the positive task (showing what does satisfy). The key move is to argue from the universality of desire to the existence of its object: if all human beings naturally desire happiness, happiness must be attainable — otherwise nature would have implanted a futile desire, which is incompatible with divine governance.

The argument then proceeds analytically: happiness must be the perfect good, the perfect good must be self-sufficient, and only God is self-sufficient. Therefore, the desire for happiness is ultimately a desire for God — whether the desirer knows it or not. The person who pursues wealth is really pursuing God, but mistakenly; the philosopher who pursues wisdom is pursuing God more directly. The desires are the same; the difference is in the accuracy of the targeting.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's desire for happiness is never in question — what he has forgotten is where happiness is to be found. His lament in Book I is the cry of a man whose desires have been frustrated: he desired justice but found persecution, desired honour but found disgrace, desired security but found a prison cell. Philosophy's response is not to suppress desire but to redirect it: desire better things, and your desire will be satisfied; desire the highest thing, and your satisfaction will be complete.
