## Definition & Context

Fear (*metus*) is the passion aroused by the anticipation of an impending evil — particularly the loss of [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts or the suffering of physical harm. In the *Consolation*, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] identifies fear as one of the chief passions that enslave the soul, alongside [[hope|hope]], [[desire|desire]], and [[joy|joy]]. The person who fears losing wealth, power, or reputation is already enslaved to them — the fear is evidence of the attachment.

> "Joy, hope, and fear" — Philosophy catalogues the passions that must be calmed before reason can operate (004-069)

## Philosophical Background

The analysis of fear has deep roots. **Plato's** *Republic* describes the tyrannical soul as enslaved by fear — the tyrant fears everyone, trusts no one, and is the most miserable of men despite his apparent power. **Aristotle's** *Rhetoric* defines fear as "a pain or disturbance arising from the impression of an impending evil that is destructive or painful." The **Stoics** classified fear as one of the four primary passions (alongside desire, pleasure, and distress) and argued that the wise person is free from fear because they recognize that the only genuine evil is vice — and vice is always within our control to avoid.

**Epicurus** offered a different cure: fear, particularly fear of death and the gods, is caused by false beliefs about the nature of the universe. Correct these beliefs through atomistic physics, and fear dissolves. Boethius's approach is closer to the Stoic one, but with a Neoplatonic metaphysical framework: fear is cured not by atomism but by understanding one's place in the providential order.

## Key Passages

On the passions that afflict Boethius:

> "Joy, hope and fear" (004-069) — Philosophy lists the emotions from which Boethius must recover

On fear of reproach:

> "Should I, thinkest thou, fear to incur reproach, or shrink from the truth?" (002-007)

On the absence of fear in the truly secure:

> "For truly there is no fear that one who is encompassed by such a guard should be overcome" (003-078)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy treats fear as both a symptom and a cause of Boethius's sickness. Fear is a symptom because it reveals his attachment to things that can be lost — if he did not value Fortune's gifts, he would not fear their withdrawal. But fear is also a cause because it clouds the reason, making clear thinking impossible. Philosophy's first therapeutic step — the "gentler remedies" of Book II — is designed to calm Boethius's fear before the "stronger medicines" of metaphysical argument can be administered.

The cure for fear, in Philosophy's framework, is understanding. When Boethius understands that [[providence|Providence]] governs all things, that the true [[good|Good]] cannot be lost, and that the soul's welfare does not depend on external circumstances, fear becomes groundless. The prisoner who knows his soul is safe has nothing left to fear — not even death.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius begins the dialogue in a state of terror — "a horror worse than death" (001-094) — facing execution on false charges. Philosophy does not tell him that his fear is unreasonable (he faces a real threat) but that his fear reveals a deeper confusion about what is truly valuable. His recovery consists in moving from the fear that paralyses to the courage that acts — the courage to think clearly, to question his own assumptions, and ultimately to accept his fate with equanimity.
