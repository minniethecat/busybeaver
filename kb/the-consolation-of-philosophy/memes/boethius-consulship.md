## Overview

The consulship of [[boethius|Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius]] in 510 AD was the crowning achievement of his political career — the highest office in the Roman state, held jointly with his father-in-law [[symmachus|Symmachus]]. The event exemplified both the heights of worldly [[honour|honour]] and, in retrospect, the mutability of [[fortune|Fortune]] — the man who reached the consulship would, within fifteen years, die a prisoner's death.

## Significance

The joint consulship of Boethius and Symmachus was a symbol of the political ascendancy of the Anician family and of the continuing prestige of the Roman Senate under Ostrogothic rule. Boethius describes his sons, still children, being carried in the consular procession — an image that Philosophy would later use to illustrate the vanity of worldly glory. The same children who were honoured in the Forum would see their father executed as a traitor.
