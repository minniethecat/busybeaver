# Homer

**Homer** (*Homerus*) is the legendary epic poet of ancient Greece, traditionally credited with the composition of the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*. In [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *The Consolation of Philosophy*, Homer is invoked as an exemplary figure — both as the supreme poet and as an illustration of the fragility of fame.

## Homer in the Consolation

Boethius references Homer at key moments in the *Consolation*:

### The Muses and the Poet's Task

At the opening of the work (1.P1.7–1.P1.12), Boethius is found composing elegiac verse, attended by the Muses of poetry. [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] drives them away, calling them "harlots" (*scenicae meretriculae*) who offer only the "sweet poison" of emotion rather than genuine healing. This rejection of the poetic Muses in favor of philosophical [[reason]] echoes a tension present already in [[plato|Plato]]'s *Republic*, where Homer is both honored and criticized.

### Fame and Impermanence

In Book II (2.P7.1–2.P7.10), [[philosophy-personified|Philosophy]] uses Homer's own words to demonstrate the vanity of fame: even the greatest poet cannot guarantee immortal renown, for Homer himself complains (through the mouth of characters like Achilles) about the obscurity that overtakes the most celebrated heroes. If even Homer's subjects are largely forgotten, what hope of lasting fame can lesser mortals entertain?

### The Consolation of Myth

Despite Philosophy's apparent rejection of poetry, the *Consolation* itself makes extensive use of mythological exempla — Orpheus, Ulysses, Hercules, Agamemnon — many of which are Homeric in origin. This suggests a more complex attitude: poetry is dangerous when it indulges emotion without [[reason]], but valuable when it serves philosophical insight.

## Biographical Tradition

Little is known of the historical Homer. The ancient biographical tradition places him variously in Ionia (western Anatolia), and dates range from the 12th to the 8th century BC. The Homeric Question — whether the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* were composed by a single poet or represent an oral tradition compiled over centuries — was already debated in antiquity and remains unresolved.

## Significance for Boethius

For Boethius, Homer represents both the highest achievement of pagan literary culture and its ultimate limitation: poetry can depict human [[fortune|suffering]] with unmatched power, but it cannot provide the philosophical [[consolation]] that alone heals the [[soul]]. The *Consolation* itself attempts to bridge this gap by uniting poetry with philosophy in its prosimetrum form.
