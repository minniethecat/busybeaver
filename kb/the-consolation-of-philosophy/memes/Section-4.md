# Section 4

[004-001] 'I scarcely understand what thou meanest,' I said, 'much less can I

[004-002] answer thy question.'

[004-003] 'Did I not say truly that something is missing, whereby, as through a

[004-004] breach in the ramparts, disease hath crept in to disturb thy mind? But,

[004-005] tell me, dost thou remember the universal end towards which the aim of

[004-006] all nature is directed?'

[004-007] 'I once heard,' said I, 'but sorrow hath dulled my recollection.'

[004-008] 'And yet thou knowest whence all things have proceeded.'

[004-009] 'Yes, that I know,' said I, 'and have answered that it is from God.'

[004-010] 'Yet how is it possible that thou knowest not what is the end of

[004-011] existence, when thou dost understand its source and origin? However,

[004-012] these disturbances of mind have force to shake a man's position, but

[004-013] cannot pluck him up and root him altogether out of himself. But answer

[004-014] this also, I pray thee: rememberest thou that thou art a man?'

[004-015] 'How should I not?' said I.

[004-016] 'Then, canst thou say what man is?'

[004-017] 'Is this thy question: Whether I know myself for a being endowed with

[004-018] reason and subject to death? Surely I do acknowledge myself such.'

[004-019] Then she: 'Dost know nothing else that thou art?'

[004-020] 'Nothing.'

[004-021] 'Now,' said she, 'I know another cause of thy disease, one, too, of

[004-022] grave moment. Thou hast ceased to know thy own nature. So, then, I have

[004-023] made full discovery both of the causes of thy sickness and the means of

[004-024] restoring thy health. It is because forgetfulness of thyself hath

[004-025] bewildered thy mind that thou hast bewailed thee as an exile, as one

[004-026] stripped of the blessings that were his; it is because thou knowest not

[004-027] the end of existence that thou deemest abominable and wicked men to be

[004-028] happy and powerful; while, because thou hast forgotten by what means the

[004-029] earth is governed, thou deemest that fortune's changes ebb and flow

[004-030] without the restraint of a guiding hand. These are serious enough to

[004-031] cause not sickness only, but even death; but, thanks be to the Author of

[004-032] our health, the light of nature hath not yet left thee utterly. In thy

[004-033] true judgment concerning the world's government, in that thou believest

[004-034] it subject, not to the random drift of chance, but to [[reason|divine reason]], we

[004-035] have the divine spark from which thy recovery may be hoped. Have, then,

[004-036] no fear; from these weak embers the vital heat shall once more be

[004-037] kindled within thee. But seeing that it is not yet time for strong

[004-038] remedies, and that the mind is manifestly so constituted that when it

[004-039] casts off true opinions it straightway puts on false, wherefrom arises a

[004-040] cloud of confusion that disturbs its true vision, I will now try and

[004-041] disperse these mists by mild and soothing application, that so the

[004-042] darkness of misleading passion may be scattered, and thou mayst come to

[004-043] discern the splendour of [[truth|the true]] light.'

BOOK II.

[004-075] THE VANITY OF FORTUNE'S GIFTS

[004-076] Summary

[004-077] CH. I. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] reproves [[boethius|Boethius]] for the foolishness of his

[004-078] complaints against [[fortune|Fortune]]. Her very nature is caprice.--CH. II.

[004-079] Philosophy in Fortune's name replies to Boethius' reproaches, and

[004-080] proves that the gifts of Fortune are hers to give and to take

[004-081] away.--CH. III. Boethius falls back upon his present sense of

[004-082] misery. Philosophy reminds him of the brilliancy of his former

[004-083] fortunes.--CH. IV. Boethius objects that the memory of past

[004-084] happiness is the bitterest portion of the lot of the unhappy.

[004-085] Philosophy shows that much is still left for which he may be

[004-086] thankful. None enjoy perfect satisfaction with their lot. But

[004-087] happiness depends not on anything which Fortune can give. It is to

[004-088] be sought within.--CH. V. All the gifts of Fortune are external;

[004-089] they can never truly be our own. Man cannot find his good in

[004-090] worldly possessions. Riches bring anxiety and trouble.--CH. VI.

[004-091] High place without virtue is an evil, not a good. Power is an empty

[004-092] name.--CH. VII. Fame is a thing of little account when compared

[004-093] with the immensity of the Universe and the endlessness of

[004-094] Time.--CH. VIII. One service only can Fortune do, when she reveals

[004-095] her own nature and distinguishes true friends from false.

BOOK II.

[004-096] I.

[004-097] Thereafter for awhile she remained silent; and when she had restored my

[004-098] flagging attention by a moderate pause in her discourse, she thus began:

[004-099] 'If I have thoroughly ascertained the character and causes of thy

[004-100] sickness, thou art pining with regretful longing for thy former fortune.

[004-101] It is the change, as thou deemest, of this fortune that hath so wrought

[004-102] upon thy mind. Well do I understand that Siren's manifold wiles, the

[004-103] fatal charm of the friendship she pretends for her victims, so long as

[004-104] she is scheming to entrap them--how she unexpectedly abandons them and

[004-105] leaves them overwhelmed with insupportable grief. Bethink thee of her

[004-106] nature, character, and deserts, and thou wilt soon acknowledge that in

[004-107] her thou hast neither possessed, nor hast thou lost, aught of any worth.

[004-108] Methinks I need not spend much pains in bringing this to thy mind,

[004-109] since, even when she was still with thee, even while she was caressing

[004-110] thee, thou usedst to assail her in manly terms, to rebuke her, with

[004-111] maxims drawn from my holy treasure-house. But all sudden changes of

[004-112] circumstances bring inevitably a certain commotion of spirit. Thus it

[004-113] hath come to pass that thou also for awhile hast been parted from thy

[004-114] mind's tranquillity. But it is time for thee to take and drain a

[004-115] draught, soft and pleasant to the taste, which, as it penetrates within,

[004-116] may prepare the way for stronger potions. Wherefore I call to my aid the

[004-117] sweet persuasiveness of Rhetoric, who then only walketh in the right way

[004-118] when she forsakes not my instructions, and Music, my handmaid, I bid to

[004-119] join with her singing, now in lighter, now in graver strain.

[004-120] 'What is it, then, poor mortal, that hath cast thee into lamentation and

[004-121] mourning? Some strange, unwonted sight, methinks, have thine eyes seen.

[004-122] Thou deemest Fortune to have changed towards thee; thou mistakest. Such

[004-123] ever were her ways, ever such her nature. Rather in her very mutability

[004-124] hath she preserved towards thee her true constancy. Such was she when

[004-125] she loaded thee with caresses, when she deluded thee with the

[004-126] allurements of a false happiness. Thou hast found out how changeful is

[004-127] the face of the blind goddess. She who still veils herself from others

[004-128] hath fully discovered to thee her whole character. If thou likest her,

[004-129] take her as she is, and do not complain. If thou abhorrest her perfidy,

[004-130] turn from her in disdain, renounce her, for baneful are her delusions.

[004-131] The very thing which is now the cause of thy great grief ought to have

[004-132] brought thee tranquillity. Thou hast been forsaken by one of whom no one

[004-133] can be sure that she will not forsake him. Or dost thou indeed set value

[004-134] on a happiness that is certain to depart? Again I ask, Is Fortune's

[004-135] presence dear to thee if she cannot be trusted to stay, and though she

[004-136] will bring sorrow when she is gone? Why, if she cannot be kept at

[004-137] pleasure, and if her flight overwhelms with calamity, what is this

[004-138] fleeting visitant but a token of coming trouble? Truly it is not enough

[004-139] to look only at what lies before the eyes; wisdom gauges the issues of

[004-140] things, and this same mutability, with its two aspects, makes the

[004-141] threats of Fortune void of terror, and her caresses little to be

[004-142] desired. Finally, thou oughtest to bear with whatever takes place within

[004-143] the boundaries of Fortune's demesne, when thou hast placed thy head

[004-144] beneath her yoke. But if thou wishest to impose a law of staying and

[004-145] departing on her whom thou hast of thine own accord chosen for thy

[004-146] mistress, art thou not acting wrongfully, art thou not embittering by

[004-147] impatience a lot which thou canst not alter? Didst thou commit thy sails

[004-148] to the winds, thou wouldst voyage not whither thy intention was to go,

[004-149] but whither the winds drave thee; didst thou entrust thy seed to the

[004-150] fields, thou wouldst set off the fruitful years against the barren. Thou

[004-151] hast resigned thyself to the sway of Fortune; thou must submit to thy

[004-152] mistress's caprices. What! art thou verily striving to stay the swing

[004-153] of the revolving wheel? Oh, stupidest of mortals, if it takes to

[004-154] standing still, it ceases to be the wheel of Fortune.'

SONG I.

[004-155] FORTUNE'S MALICE.

[004-156] Mad Fortune sweeps along in wanton pride,

[004-157] Uncertain as Euripus' surging tide;

[004-158] Now tramples mighty kings beneath her feet;

[004-159] Now sets the conquered in the victor's seat.

[004-160] She heedeth not the wail of hapless woe,

[004-161] But mocks the griefs that from her mischief flow.

[004-162] Such is her sport; so proveth she her power;

[004-163] And great the marvel, when in one brief hour

[004-164] She shows her darling lifted high in bliss,

[004-165] Then headlong plunged in misery's abyss.

[004-166] II.

[004-167] 'Now I would fain also reason with thee a little in Fortune's own words.

[004-168] Do thou observe whether her contentions be just. "Man," she might say,

[004-169] "why dost thou pursue me with thy daily complainings? What wrong have I

[004-170] done thee? What goods of thine have I taken from thee? Choose an thou

[004-171] wilt a judge, and let us dispute before him concerning the rightful

[004-172] ownership of wealth and rank. If thou succeedest in showing that any one

[004-173] of these things is the true property of mortal man, I freely grant those

[004-174] things to be thine which thou claimest. When nature brought thee forth

[004-175] out of thy mother's womb, I took thee, naked and destitute as thou wast,

[004-176] I cherished thee with my substance, and, in the partiality of my favour

[004-177] for thee, I brought thee up somewhat too indulgently, and this it is

[004-178] which now makes thee rebellious against me. I surrounded thee with a

[004-179] royal abundance of all those things that are in my power. Now it is my

[004-180] pleasure to draw back my hand. Thou hast reason to thank me for the use

[004-181] of what was not thine own; thou hast no right to complain, as if thou

[004-182] hadst lost what was wholly thine. Why, then, dost bemoan thyself? I have

[004-183] done thee no violence. Wealth, honour, and all such things are placed

[004-184] under my control. My handmaidens know their mistress; with me they come,

[004-185] and at my going they depart. I might boldly affirm that if those things

[004-186] the loss of which thou lamentest had been thine, thou couldst never have

[004-187] lost them. Am I alone to be forbidden to do what I will with my own?

[004-188] Unrebuked, the skies now reveal the brightness of day, now shroud the

[004-189] daylight in the darkness of night; the year may now engarland the face

[004-190] of the earth with flowers and fruits, now disfigure it with storms and

[004-191] cold. The sea is permitted to invite with smooth and tranquil surface

[004-192] to-day, to-morrow to roughen with wave and storm. Shall man's insatiate

[004-193] greed bind _me_ to a constancy foreign to my character? This is my art,

[004-194] this the game I never cease to play. I turn the wheel that spins. I

[004-195] delight to see the high come down and the low ascend. Mount up, if thou

[004-196] wilt, but only on condition that thou wilt not think it a hardship to

[004-197] come down when the rules of my game require it. Wert thou ignorant of my

[004-198] character? Didst not know how Croesus, King of the Lydians, erstwhile

[004-199] the dreaded rival of Cyrus, was afterwards pitiably consigned to the

[004-200] flame of the pyre, and only saved by a shower sent from heaven? Has it

[004-201] 'scaped thee how Paullus paid a meed of pious tears to the misfortunes

[004-202] of King Perseus, his prisoner? What else do tragedies make such woeful

[004-203] outcry over save the overthrow of kingdoms by the indiscriminate strokes

[004-204] of Fortune? Didst thou not learn in thy childhood how there stand at the

[004-205] threshold of Zeus 'two jars,' 'the one full of blessings, the other of

[004-206] calamities'? How if thou hast drawn over-liberally from the good jar?

[004-207] What if not even now have I departed wholly from thee? What if this very

[004-208] mutability of mine is a just ground for hoping better things? But listen

[004-209] now, and cease to let thy heart consume away with fretfulness, nor

[004-210] expect to live on thine own terms in a realm that is common to all.'
