# Section 7

SONG VI.

[007-001] NERO'S INFAMY.

[007-002] We know what mischief dire he wrought--

[007-003] Rome fired, the Fathers slain--

[007-004] Whose hand with brother's slaughter wet

[007-005] A mother's blood did stain.

[007-006] No pitying tear his cheek bedewed,

[007-007] As on the corse he gazed;

[007-008] That mother's beauty, once so fair,

[007-009] A critic's voice appraised.

[007-010] Yet far and wide, from East to West,

[007-011] His sway the nations own;

[007-012] And scorching South and icy North

[007-013] Obey his will alone.

[007-014] Did, then, high power a curb impose

[007-015] On [[nero|Nero]]'s phrenzied will?

[007-016] Ah, woe when to the evil heart

[007-017] Is joined the sword to kill!

[007-018] VII.

[007-019] Then said I: 'Thou knowest thyself that ambition for worldly success

[007-020] hath but little swayed me. Yet I have desired opportunity for action,

[007-021] lest virtue, in default of exercise, should languish away.'

[007-022] Then she: 'This is that "last infirmity" which is able to allure minds

[007-023] which, though of noble quality, have not yet been moulded to any

[007-024] exquisite refinement by the perfecting of [[virtue|the virtues]]--I mean, the love

[007-025] of glory--and fame for high services rendered to the commonweal. And yet

[007-026] consider with me how poor and unsubstantial a thing this glory is! The

[007-027] whole of this earth's globe, as thou hast learnt from the demonstration

[007-028] of astronomy, compared with the expanse of heaven, is found no bigger

[007-029] than a point; that is to say, if measured by the vastness of heaven's

[007-030] sphere, it is held to occupy absolutely no space at all. Now, of this so

[007-031] insignificant portion of the universe, it is about a fourth part, as

[007-032] Ptolemy's proofs have taught us, which is inhabited by living creatures

[007-033] known to us. If from this fourth part you take away in thought all that

[007-034] is usurped by seas and marshes, or lies a vast waste of waterless

[007-035] desert, barely is an exceeding narrow area left for human habitation.

[007-036] You, then, who are shut in and prisoned in this merest fraction of a

[007-037] point's space, do ye take thought for the blazoning of your fame, for

[007-038] the spreading abroad of your renown? Why, what amplitude or magnificence

[007-039] has glory when confined to such narrow and petty limits?

[007-040] 'Besides, the straitened bounds of this scant dwelling-place are

[007-041] inhabited by many nations differing widely in speech, in usages, in mode

[007-042] of life; to many of these, from the difficulty of travel, from

[007-043] diversities of speech, from want of commercial intercourse, the fame not

[007-044] only of individual men, but even of cities, is unable to reach. Why, in

[007-045] [[cicero|Cicero]]'s days, as he himself somewhere points out, the fame of the Roman

[007-046] Republic had not yet crossed the Caucasus, and yet by that time her

[007-047] name had grown formidable to the Parthians and other nations of those

[007-048] parts. Seest thou, then, how narrow, how confined, is the glory ye take

[007-049] pains to spread abroad and extend! Can the fame of a single Roman

[007-050] penetrate where the glory of the Roman name fails to pass? Moreover, the

[007-051] customs and institutions of different races agree not together, so that

[007-052] what is deemed praise worthy in one country is thought punishable in

[007-053] another. Wherefore, if any love the applause of fame, it shall not

[007-054] profit him to publish his name among many peoples. Then, each must be

[007-055] content to have the range of his glory limited to his own people; the

[007-056] splendid immortality of fame must be confined within the bounds of a

[007-057] single race.

[007-058] 'Once more, how many of high renown in their own times have been lost in

[007-059] oblivion for want of a record! Indeed, of what avail are written records

[007-060] even, which, with their authors, are overtaken by the dimness of age

[007-061] after a somewhat longer time? But ye, when ye think on future fame,

[007-062] fancy it an immortality that ye are begetting for yourselves. Why, if

[007-063] thou scannest the infinite spaces of eternity, what room hast thou left

[007-064] for rejoicing in the durability of thy name? Verily, if a single

[007-065] moment's space be compared with ten thousand years, it has a certain

[007-066] relative duration, however little, since each period is definite. But

[007-067] this same number of years--ay, and a number many times as great--cannot

[007-068] even be compared with endless duration; for, indeed, finite periods may

[007-069] in a sort be compared one with another, but a finite and an infinite

[007-070] never. So it comes to pass that fame, though it extend to ever so wide a

[007-071] space of years, if it be compared to never-lessening eternity, seems not

[007-072] short-lived merely, but altogether nothing. But as for you, ye know not

[007-073] how to act aright, unless it be to court the popular breeze, and win the

[007-074] empty applause of the multitude--nay, ye abandon the superlative worth

[007-075] of conscience and virtue, and ask a recompense from the poor words of

[007-076] others. Let me tell thee how wittily one did mock the shallowness of

[007-077] this sort of arrogance. A certain man assailed one who had put on the

[007-078] name of philosopher as a cloak to pride and vain-glory, not for the

[007-079] practice of real virtue, and added: "Now shall I know if thou art a

[007-080] philosopher if thou bearest reproaches calmly and patiently." The other

[007-081] for awhile affected to be patient, and, having endured to be abused,

[007-082] cried out derisively: "_Now_, do you see that I am a philosopher?" The

[007-083] other, with biting sarcasm, retorted: "I should have hadst thou held thy

[007-084] peace." Moreover, what concern have choice spirits--for it is of such

[007-085] men we speak, men who seek glory by virtue--what concern, I say, have

[007-086] these with fame after the dissolution of the body in death's last hour?

[007-087] For if men die wholly--which our reasonings forbid us to believe--there

[007-088] is no such thing as glory at all, since he to whom the glory is said to

[007-089] belong is altogether non-existent. But if the mind, conscious of its own

[007-090] rectitude, is released from its earthly prison, and seeks heaven in free

[007-091] flight, doth it not despise all earthly things when it rejoices in its

[007-092] deliverance from earthly bonds, and enters upon the joys of heaven?'

SONG VII.

[007-093] GLORY MAY NOT LAST.

[007-094] Oh, let him, who pants for glory's guerdon,

[007-095] Deeming glory all in all,

[007-096] Look and see how wide the heaven expandeth,

[007-097] Earth's enclosing bounds how small!

[007-098] Shame it is, if your proud-swelling glory

[007-099] May not fill this narrow room!

[007-100] Why, then, strive so vainly, oh, ye proud ones!

[007-101] To escape your mortal doom?

[007-102] Though your name, to distant regions bruited,

[007-103] O'er the earth be widely spread,

[007-104] Though full many a lofty-sounding title

[007-105] On your house its lustre shed,

[007-106] Death at all this pomp and glory spurneth

[007-107] When his hour draweth nigh,

[007-108] Shrouds alike th' exalted and the humble,

[007-109] Levels lowest and most high.

[007-110] Where are now the bones of stanch Fabricius?

[007-111] Brutus, Cato--where are they?

[007-112] Lingering fame, with a few graven letters,

[007-113] Doth their empty name display.

[007-114] But to know the great dead is not given

[007-115] From a gilded name alone;

[007-116] Nay, ye all alike must lie forgotten,

[007-117] 'Tis not _you_ that fame makes known.

[007-118] Fondly do ye deem life's little hour

[007-119] Lengthened by fame's mortal breath;

[007-120] There but waits you--when this, too, is taken--

[007-121] At the last a second death.

[007-122] VIII.

[007-123] 'But that thou mayst not think that I wage implacable warfare against

[007-124] [[fortune|Fortune]], I own there is a time when the deceitful goddess serves men

[007-125] well--I mean when she reveals herself, uncovers her face, and confesses

[007-126] her true character. Perhaps thou dost not yet grasp my meaning. Strange

[007-127] is the thing I am trying to express, and for this cause I can scarce

[007-128] find words to make clear my thought. For truly I believe that Ill

[007-129] Fortune is of more use to men than Good Fortune. For Good Fortune, when

[007-130] she wears the guise of happiness, and most seems to caress, is always

[007-131] lying; Ill Fortune is always truthful, since, in changing, she shows her

[007-132] inconstancy. The one deceives, the other teaches; the one enchains the

[007-133] minds of those who enjoy her favour by the semblance of delusive good,

[007-134] the other delivers them by the knowledge of the frail nature of

[007-135] happiness. Accordingly, thou mayst see the one fickle, shifting as the

[007-136] breeze, and ever self-deceived; the other sober-minded, alert, and wary,

[007-137] by reason of the very discipline of adversity. Finally, Good Fortune, by

[007-138] her allurements, draws men far from [[truth|the true]] good; Ill Fortune ofttimes

[007-139] draws men back to true good with grappling-irons. Again, should it be

[007-140] esteemed a trifling boon, thinkest thou, that this cruel, this odious

[007-141] Fortune hath discovered to thee the hearts of thy faithful friends--that

[007-142] other hid from thee alike the faces of the true friends and of the

[007-143] false, but in departing she hath taken away _her_ friends, and left thee

[007-144] _thine_? What price wouldst thou not have given for this service in the

[007-145] fulness of thy prosperity when thou seemedst to thyself fortunate?

[007-146] Cease, then, to seek the wealth thou hast lost, since in true friends

[007-147] thou hast found the most precious of all riches.'

SONG VIII.

[007-148] LOVE IS LORD OF ALL.

[007-149] Why are [[nature|Nature]]'s changes bound

[007-150] To a fixed and ordered round?

[007-151] What to leaguèd peace hath bent

[007-152] Every warring element?

[007-153] Wherefore doth the rosy morn

[007-154] Rise on Phoebus' car upborne?

[007-155] Why should Phoebe rule the night,

[007-156] Led by Hesper's guiding light?

[007-157] What the power that doth restrain

[007-158] In his place the restless main,

[007-159] That within fixed bounds he keeps,

[007-160] Nor o'er earth in deluge sweeps?

[007-161] [[love|Love]] it is that holds the chains,

[007-162] Love o'er sea and earth that reigns;

[007-163] Love--whom else but sovereign Love?--

[007-164] Love, high lord in heaven above!

[007-165] Yet should he his care remit,

[007-166] All that now so close is knit

[007-167] In sweet love and holy peace,

[007-168] Would no more from conflict cease,

[007-169] But with strife's rude shock and jar

[007-170] All the world's fair fabric mar.

[007-171] Tribes and nations Love unites

[007-172] By just treaty's sacred rites;

[007-173] Wedlock's bonds he sanctifies

[007-174] By affection's softest ties.

[007-175] Love appointeth, as is due,

[007-176] Faithful laws to comrades true--

[007-177] Love, all-sovereign Love!--oh, then,

[007-178] Ye are blest, ye sons of men,

[007-179] If the love that rules the sky

[007-180] In your hearts is throned on high!

BOOK III.

[007-181] TRUE HAPPINESS AND FALSE.

[007-182] SUMMARY

[007-183] CH. I. [[boethius|Boethius]] beseeches [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] to continue. She promises to

[007-184] lead him to [[good|true happiness]].--CH. II. [[happiness|Happiness]] is the one end which

[007-185] all created beings seek. They aim variously at (_a_) wealth, or

[007-186] (_b_) rank, or (_c_) sovereignty, or (_d_) glory, or (_e_)

[007-187] pleasure, because they think thereby to attain either (_a_)

[007-188] contentment, (_b_) reverence, (_c_) power, (_d_) renown, or (_e_)

[007-189] gladness of heart, in one or other of which they severally imagine

[007-190] happiness to consist.--CH. III. Philosophy proceeds to consider

[007-191] whether happiness can really be secured in any of these ways, (_a_)

[007-192] So far from bringing contentment, riches only add to men's

[007-193] wants.--CH. IV. (_b_) High position cannot of itself win respect.

[007-194] Titles command no reverence in distant and barbarous lands. They

[007-195] even fall into contempt through lapse of time.--CH. V. (_c_)

[007-196] Sovereignty cannot even bestow safety. History tells of the

[007-197] downfall of kings and their ministers. Tyrants go in fear of their

[007-198] lives. --CH. VI. (_d_) Fame conferred on the unworthy is but

[007-199] disgrace. The splendour of noble birth is not a man's own, but his

[007-200] ancestors'.--CH. VII. (_e_) Pleasure begins in the restlessness of

[007-201] desire, and ends in repentance. Even the pure pleasures of home may

[007-202] turn to gall and bitterness.--CH. VIII. All fail, then, to give

[007-203] what they promise. There is, moreover, some accompanying evil

[007-204] involved in each of these aims. [[beauty|Beauty]] and bodily strength are

[007-205] likewise of little worth. In strength man is surpassed by the

[007-206] brutes; beauty is but outward show.--CH. IX. The source of men's

[007-207] error in following these phantoms of good is that _they break up

[007-208] and separate that which is in its nature one and indivisible_.

[007-209] Contentment, power, reverence, renown, and joy are essentially

[007-210] bound up one with the other, and, if they are to be attained at

[007-211] all, must be attained _together_. True happiness, if it can be

[007-212] found, will include them all. But it cannot be found among the

[007-213] perishable things hitherto considered.--CH. X. Such a happiness

[007-214] necessarily exists. Its seat is in God. Nay, God is very happiness,

[007-215] and in a manner, therefore, the happy man partakes also of the

[007-216] Divine nature. All other ends are relative to this good, since they

[007-217] are all pursued only for the sake of good; it is _good_ which is

[007-218] the sole ultimate end. And since the sole end is also happiness, it

[007-219] is plain that this good and happiness are in essence the same.--CH.

[007-220] XI. [[unity|Unity]] is another aspect of goodness. Now, all things subsist so

[007-221] long only as they preserve the unity of their being; when they lose

[007-222] this unity, they perish. But the bent of nature forces all things

[007-223] (plants and inanimate things, as well as animals) to strive to

[007-224] continue in life. Therefore, all things desire unity, for unity is

[007-225] essential to life. But unity and goodness were shown to be the

[007-226] same. Therefore, good is proved to be the end towards which the

[007-227] whole universe tends.[E]--CH. XII. Boethius acknowledges that he is

[007-228] but recollecting truths he once knew. Philosophy goes on to show

[007-229] that it is goodness also by which the whole world is governed.[F]

[007-230] Boethius professes compunction for his former folly. But the

[007-231] paradox of evil is introduced, and he is once more perplexed.
