# Universals

The **problem of universals** — the question of whether general concepts like "humanity" or "justice" correspond to real entities — was one of the most debated topics in medieval philosophy — a question at the heart of how we acquire [[knowledge]] — and [[boethius|Boethius]]'s translations and commentaries played a foundational role in shaping the debate. His formulation of the problem in his *Second Commentary on Porphyry's Isagoge* became the classic statement that launched a millennium of philosophical discussion.

## Boethius's Contribution

### Translation and Transmission

Boethius translated Porphyry's *Isagoge* (Introduction to [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s *Categories*) into Latin and wrote two commentaries on it. In the *Second Commentary*, he addresses Porphyry's three questions about universals:

1. **Do genera and species exist in reality, or only in thought?**
2. **If they exist, are they corporeal or incorporeal?**
3. **If incorporeal, do they exist in sensible things or separately from them?**

Porphyry had raised these questions but declined to answer them, considering them too advanced for an introductory work. Boethius took up the challenge.

### Boethius's Solution

Boethius proposed a moderate realist position influenced by Alexander of Aphrodisias:

- Universals exist **in sensible things** as their common nature (e.g., humanity exists in individual humans)
- They exist **in the mind** as abstract concepts, separated from matter through intellectual contemplation
- They do **not** exist as separate Platonic Forms in a transcendent realm (contra [[plato|Plato]])

This solution — that universals are neither purely mental constructs nor independently existing entities but rather exist "in and from particulars" — became known as **moderate realism** and was influential through the early Middle Ages.

## The Problem in the Consolation

While the *Consolation* does not explicitly discuss the problem of universals, the metaphysical framework it assumes depends on Boethius's moderate realism, rooted in his [[neoplatonism|Neoplatonic]] understanding of participation. When [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] argues that all particular goods participate in the Supreme Good (3.P10–3.P11), she relies on a metaphysics of participation that presupposes the reality of universal natures.

## Historical Impact

Boethius's treatment of universals set the terms for medieval debate:

- **Early Middle Ages**: Boethius's commentaries were the primary source for the problem of universals
- **11th–12th centuries**: the debate between realists (universals exist) and nominalists (only particulars exist) was framed in Boethian terms
- **Peter Abelard**: developed his conceptualist solution in direct response to Boethius's formulation
- **Later scholasticism**: Aquinas, Scotus, and Ockham all engaged with the Boethian framework
