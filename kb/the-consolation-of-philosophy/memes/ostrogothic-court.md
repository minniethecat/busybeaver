## Overview

The Ostrogothic Court at Ravenna was the administrative centre of the Ostrogothic Kingdom under [[theodoric|Theodoric the Great]] (r. 493–526 AD). It was a unique political environment where Roman administrative traditions coexisted with Gothic military authority. [[boethius|Boethius]] served at this court as *Magister Officiorum* (Master of Offices), the highest civilian post in the kingdom, before his dramatic fall from favour.

## Political Dynamics

The court was characterised by tension between two factions: the old Roman senatorial aristocracy (represented by Boethius and [[symmachus|Symmachus]]) and the Gothic military nobility. Religious differences compounded the political divide — Theodoric was an Arian Christian, while the Roman aristocracy was orthodox (Nicene) Christian.

## Role in the Consolation

The Ostrogothic Court is the implied antagonist of the *Consolation* — the source of the false accusations, the forged letters, and the corrupt informers who brought about Boethius's downfall. Yet Philosophy does not dwell on the particulars of court intrigue. Her argument is more radical: the court is merely another stage for [[fortune|Fortune]], and even if the court had been just, the goods it could bestow — [[power|power]], [[honour|honour]], [[fame|fame]] — would still be insufficient for [[happiness|true happiness]].

The therapeutic insight is that Boethius's attachment to the court was part of his sickness. He had invested his identity in an institution that, by its very nature, could not be trusted. The court's betrayal of him was not an aberration but a revelation of what the court had always been.
