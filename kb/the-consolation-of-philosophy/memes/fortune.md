## Definition & Context

Fortune is the personification of all external, mutable goods that human beings mistakenly pursue as sources of happiness. In [[boethius|Boethius]]'s philosophical argument, Fortune represents everything that is outside our control: wealth, political power, fame, and sensory pleasure. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s first major therapeutic move is to demonstrate that Fortune's gifts — precisely because they are external and transient — cannot constitute [[good|true happiness]].

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] identifies [[boethius|Boethius]]'s sickness as "regretful longing for thy former fortune" and diagnoses that "it is the change, as thou deemest, of this fortune that hath so wrought upon thy mind" (004-100, 004-101). The core insight is that Fortune has not changed her nature — she has simply revealed it: "Thou deemest Fortune to have changed towards thee; thou mistakest. Such ever were her ways, ever such her nature. Rather in her very mutability hath she preserved towards thee her true constancy" (004-122, 004-123).

## Philosophical Background

The figure of Fortune has a rich literary and philosophical history. In **classical antiquity**, Fortuna was a Roman goddess — capricious, blind, associated with the wheel (*rota Fortunae*) that raises men up only to cast them down. The image appears in writers from Cicero to Seneca, always with the same moral: trust nothing that Fortune gives, for she can take it away. **Boethius** inherits this tradition but transforms it: Fortune is demoted from goddess to literary device — a personification deployed by Philosophy for therapeutic purposes. When Philosophy speaks "in Fortune's own voice" in Book II, it is a rhetorical strategy, not an acknowledgment of Fortune's reality.

The **Christian** contribution is decisive. In the Christian worldview, Fortune has no independent existence — there is only Providence. What pagans called Fortune is simply the temporal unfolding of God's eternal plan. Boethius makes this explicit in Book IV's distinction between Providence and Fate: Fortune is a name for the chain of secondary causes as experienced by those who cannot see the whole. The therapeutic task is to dissolve Fortune into Providence — to replace the image of a capricious wheel with the vision of a divine physician who "assigns what He knows to be suitable" (016-036).

## Key Passages

The most concentrated treatment of Fortune appears in Book II. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] speaks in Fortune's own voice, challenging [[boethius|Boethius]] to acknowledge the lawfulness of her caprice:

> "If thou likest her, take her as she is, and do not complain. If thou abhorrest her perfidy, turn from her in disdain, renounce her, for baneful are her delusions. The very thing which is now the cause of thy great grief ought to have brought thee tranquillity. Thou hast been forsaken by one of whom no one can be sure that she will not forsake him." (004-128–004-132)

The wheel image crystallises Fortune's nature:

> "Thou hast resigned thyself to the sway of Fortune; thou must submit to thy mistress's caprices. What! art thou verily striving to stay the swing of the revolving wheel? Oh, stupidest of mortals, if it takes to standing still, it ceases to be the wheel of Fortune." (004-151–004-154)

The poetic rendering in Song I drives home the same point:

> "Mad Fortune sweeps along in wanton pride, / Uncertain as Euripus' surging tide; / Now tramples mighty kings beneath her feet; / Now sets the conquered in the victor's seat." (004-156–004-159)

On the paradoxical benefit of adversity:

> "Ill Fortune is of more use to men than Good Fortune" (007-129)

And the ultimate insight — Fortune's very inconstancy is her constancy:

> "In her very mutability hath she preserved towards thee her true constancy" (004-122–004-123)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument about Fortune proceeds in three stages. First, she establishes that Fortune's mutability is not a defect but her defining characteristic — complaining that Fortune is fickle is like complaining that a wheel turns. "Such ever were her ways, ever such her nature" (004-122).

Second, she argues that the very unreliability of Fortune's gifts proves they have no intrinsic worth: "Or dost thou indeed set value on a happiness that is certain to depart? Again I ask, Is Fortune's presence dear to thee if she cannot be trusted to stay?" (004-134–004-135). The logic is compelling: if a good is genuinely good, it cannot harm; if Fortune's gifts can be lost (and they always can), they are not genuinely good.

Third, she shows that Fortune's apparent cruelty can serve a higher purpose. Ill Fortune is actually more beneficial than Good Fortune because it reveals the truth: "One service only can Fortune do, when she reveals her own nature and distinguishes true friends from false" (004-094). Good Fortune deceives with her smiles; Ill Fortune teaches by withdrawing them. "Ill Fortune is of more use to men than Good Fortune" (007-129): prosperity seduces us into believing we are self-sufficient; adversity strips away the illusion and reveals our dependence on the true Good.

The therapeutic strategy is brilliant: [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] does not try to console Boethius by promising that Fortune will turn her wheel back in his favour. Instead, she argues that Fortune is irrelevant — that whether the wheel turns or stands still makes no difference to genuine happiness. The consolation is not that fortune will improve but that fortune does not matter.

## Boethius' Position

At the outset of the dialogue, [[boethius|Boethius]] laments his changed Fortune in the opening song: "For, while yet inconstant Fortune / Poured her gifts and all was bright, / Death's dark hour had all but whelmed me / In the gloom of endless night" (001-207–001-210). After [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument in Book II, [[boethius|Boethius]] acknowledges that he is beginning to see Fortune's true nature: "Verily, thy pleas are plausible — yea, steeped in the honeyed sweetness of music and rhetoric" (005-025). By the opening of Book III, he has moved further — declaring himself no longer unequal to Fortune's blows, and eager for [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s stronger remedies.

This marks his transition from attachment to Fortune's gifts toward the pursuit of [[truth|the true]] Good — a good that Fortune can neither give nor take away. The progression is not merely intellectual but spiritual: Boethius must learn not only that Fortune's gifts are worthless but that his attachment to them was the cause of his suffering. The real consolation is the discovery that what he truly desires — happiness — was never in Fortune's power to bestow.
