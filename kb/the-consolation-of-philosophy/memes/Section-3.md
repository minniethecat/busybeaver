# Section 3

[003-001] 'What need to speak of the forged letters by which an attempt is made to

[003-002] prove that I hoped for the freedom of Rome? Their falsity would have

[003-003] been manifest, if I had been allowed to use the confession of the

[003-004] informers themselves, evidence which has in all matters the most

[003-005] convincing force. Why, what hope of freedom is left to us? Would there

[003-006] were any! I should have answered with the epigram of Canius when

[003-007] Caligula declared him to have been cognisant of a conspiracy against

[003-008] him. "If I had known," said he, "thou shouldst never have known." Grief

[003-009] hath not so blunted my perceptions in this matter that I should complain

[003-010] because impious wretches contrive their villainies against the virtuous,

[003-011] but at their achievement of their hopes I do exceedingly marvel. For

[003-012] evil purposes are, perchance, due to the imperfection of [[nature|human nature]];

[003-013] that it should be possible for scoundrels to carry out their worst

[003-014] schemes against the innocent, while God beholdeth, is verily monstrous.

[003-015] For this cause, not without reason, one of thy disciples asked, "If God

[003-016] exists, whence comes evil? Yet whence comes good, if He exists not?"

[003-017] However, it might well be that wretches who seek the blood of all honest

[003-018] men and of the whole senate should wish to destroy me also, whom they

[003-019] saw to be a bulwark of the senate and all honest men. But did I deserve

[003-020] such a fate from the Fathers also? Thou rememberest, methinks--since

[003-021] thou didst ever stand by my side to direct what I should do or say--thou

[003-022] rememberest, I say, how at Verona, when the king, eager for the general

[003-023] destruction, was bent on implicating the whole senatorial order in the

[003-024] charge of treason brought against Albinus, with what indifference to my

[003-025] own peril I maintained the innocence of its members, one and all. Thou

[003-026] knowest that what I say is the truth, and that I have never boasted of

[003-027] my good deeds in a spirit of self-praise. For whenever a man by

[003-028] proclaiming his good deeds receives the recompense of fame, he

[003-029] diminishes in a measure the secret reward of a good conscience. What

[003-030] issues have overtaken my innocency thou seest. Instead of reaping the

[003-031] rewards of true virtue, I undergo the penalties of a guilt falsely laid

[003-032] to my charge--nay, more than this; never did an open confession of guilt

[003-033] cause such unanimous severity among the assessors, but that some

[003-034] consideration, either of the mere frailty of human nature, or of

[003-035] fortune's universal instability, availed to soften the verdict of some

[003-036] few. Had I been accused of a design to fire the temples, to slaughter

[003-037] the priests with impious sword, of plotting the massacre of all honest

[003-038] men, I should yet have been produced in court, and only punished on due

[003-039] confession or conviction. Now for my too great zeal towards the senate I

[003-040] have been condemned to outlawry and death, unheard and undefended, at a

[003-041] distance of near five hundred miles away.[C] Oh, my judges, well do ye

[003-042] deserve that no one should hereafter be convicted of a fault like mine!

[003-043] 'Yet even my very accusers saw how honourable was the charge they

[003-044] brought against me, and, in order to overlay it with some shadow of

[003-045] guilt, they falsely asserted that in the pursuit of my ambition I had

[003-046] stained my conscience with sacrilegious acts. And yet thy spirit,

[003-047] indwelling in me, had driven from the chamber of my soul all lust of

[003-048] earthly success, and with thine eye ever upon me, there could be no

[003-049] place left for sacrilege. For thou didst daily repeat in my ear and

[003-050] instil into my mind the Pythagorean maxim, "Follow after God." It was

[003-051] not likely, then, that I should covet the assistance of the vilest

[003-052] spirits, when thou wert moulding me to such an excellence as should

[003-053] conform me to the likeness of God. Again, the innocency of the inner

[003-054] sanctuary of my home, the company of friends of the highest probity, a

[003-055] father-in-law revered at once for his pure character and his active

[003-056] beneficence, shield me from the very suspicion of sacrilege.

[003-057] Yet--atrocious as it is--they even draw credence for this charge from

[003-058] _thee_; I am like to be thought implicated in [[evil|wickedness]] on this very

[003-059] account, that I am imbued with _thy_ teachings and stablished in _thy_

[003-060] ways. So it is not enough that my devotion to thee should profit me

[003-061] nothing, but thou also must be assailed by reason of the odium which I

[003-062] have incurred. Verily this is the very crown of my misfortunes, that

[003-063] men's opinions for the most part look not to real merit, but to the

[003-064] event; and only recognise foresight where [[fortune|Fortune]] has crowned the issue

[003-065] with her approval. Whereby it comes to pass that reputation is the first

[003-066] of all things to abandon the unfortunate. I remember with chagrin how

[003-067] perverse is popular report, how various and discordant men's judgments.

[003-068] This only will I say, that the most crushing of misfortune's burdens is,

[003-069] that as soon as a charge is fastened upon the unhappy, they are believed

[003-070] to have deserved their sufferings. I, for my part, who have been

[003-071] banished from all life's blessings, stripped of my honours, stained in

[003-072] repute, am punished for well-doing.

[003-073] 'And now methinks I see the villainous dens of the wicked surging with

[003-074] joy and gladness, all the most recklessly unscrupulous threatening a new

[003-075] crop of lying informations, the good prostrate with terror at my danger,

[003-076] every ruffian incited by impunity to new daring and to success by the

[003-077] profits of audacity, the guiltless not only robbed of their peace of

[003-078] mind, but even of all means of defence. Wherefore I would fain cry out:

SONG V.

BOETHIUS' PRAYER.

'Builder of yon starry dome,

Thou that whirlest, throned eternal,

Heaven's swift globe, and, as they roam,

Guid'st the stars by laws supernal:

So in full-sphered splendour dight

Cynthia dims the lamps of night,

But unto the orb fraternal

Closer drawn,[D] doth lose her light.

'Who at fall of eventide,

Hesper, his cold radiance showeth,

Lucifer his beams doth hide,

Paling as the sun's light groweth,

Brief, while winter's frost holds sway,

By thy will the space of day;

Swift, when summer's fervour gloweth,

Speed the hours of night away.

'Thou dost rule the changing year:

When rude Boreas oppresses,

Fall the leaves; they reappear,

Wooed by Zephyr's soft caresses.

Fields that Sirius burns deep grown

By Arcturus' watch were sown:

Each the reign of law confesses,

Keeps the place that is his own.

'Sovereign Ruler, Lord of all!

Can it be that Thou disdainest

Only man? 'Gainst him, poor thrall,

Wanton Fortune plays her vainest.

Guilt's deserved punishment

Falleth on the innocent;

High uplifted, the profanest

On the just their malice vent.

'[[virtue|Virtue]] cowers in dark retreats,

Crime's foul stain the righteous beareth,

Perjury and false deceits

Hurt not him the wrong who dareth;

But whene'er the wicked trust

In ill strength to work their lust,

Kings, whom nations' awe declareth

Mighty, grovel in the dust.

'Look, oh look upon this earth,

Thou who on law's sure foundation

Framedst all! Have we no worth,

We poor men, of all creation?

Sore we toss on fortune's tide;

Master, bid the waves subside!

And earth's ways with consummation

Of Thy heaven's order guide!'

FOOTNOTES:

[D] The moon is regarded as farthest from the sun at the full, and, as

she wanes, approaching gradually nearer.

V.

When I had poured out my griefs in this long and unbroken strain of

lamentation, she, with calm countenance, and in no wise disturbed at my

complainings, thus spake:

'When I saw thee sorrowful, in tears, I straightway knew thee wretched

and an exile. But how far distant that exile I should not know, had not

thine own speech revealed it. Yet how far indeed from thy country hast

thou, not been banished, but rather hast strayed; or, if thou wilt have

it banishment, hast banished thyself! For no one else could ever

lawfully have had this power over thee. Now, if thou wilt call to mind

from what country thou art sprung, it is not ruled, as once was the

Athenian polity, by the sovereignty of the multitude, but "one is its

Ruler, one its King," who takes delight in the number of His citizens,

not in their banishment; to submit to whose governance and to obey

whose ordinances is perfect freedom. Art thou ignorant of that most

ancient law of this thy country, whereby it is decreed that no one

whatsoever, who hath chosen to fix there his dwelling, may be sent into

exile? For truly there is no fear that one who is encompassed by its

ramparts and defences should deserve to be exiled. But he who has ceased

to wish to dwell therein, he likewise ceases to deserve to do so. And so

it is not so much the aspect of this place which moves me, as thy

aspect; not so much the library walls set off with glass and ivory which

I miss, as the chamber of thy mind, wherein I once placed, not books,

but that which gives books their value, the doctrines which my books

contain. Now, what thou hast said of thy services to the commonweal is

true, only too little compared with the greatness of thy deservings. The

things laid to thy charge whereof thou hast spoken, whether such as

redound to thy credit, or mere false accusations, are publicly known. As

for the crimes and deceits of the informers, thou hast rightly deemed

it fitting to pass them over lightly, because the popular voice hath

better and more fully pronounced upon them. Thou hast bitterly

complained of the injustice of the senate. Thou hast grieved over my

calumniation, and likewise hast lamented the damage to my good name.

Finally, thine indignation blazed forth against fortune; thou hast

complained of the unfairness with which thy merits have been

recompensed. Last of all thy frantic muse framed a prayer that the peace

which reigns in heaven might rule earth also. But since a throng of

tumultuous passions hath assailed thy soul, since thou art distraught

with anger, pain, and grief, strong remedies are not proper for thee in

this thy present mood. And so for a time I will use milder methods, that

the tumours which have grown hard through the influx of disturbing

passion may be softened by gentle treatment, till they can bear the

force of sharper remedies.'

SONG VI.

ALL THINGS HAVE THEIR NEEDFUL ORDER

He who to th' unwilling furrows

Gives the generous grain,

When the Crab with baleful fervours

Scorches all the plain;

He shall find his garner bare,

Acorns for his scanty fare.

Go not forth to cull sweet violets

From the purpled steep,

While the furious blasts of winter

Through the valleys sweep;

Nor the grape o'erhasty bring

To the press in days of spring.

For to each thing God hath given

Its appointed time;

No perplexing change permits He

In His plan sublime.

So who quits the order due

Shall a luckless issue rue.

VI.

'First, then, wilt thou suffer me by a few questions to make some

attempt to test the state of thy mind, that I may learn in what way to

set about thy cure?'

'Ask what thou wilt,' said I, 'for I will answer whatever questions thou

choosest to put.'

Then said she: 'This world of ours--thinkest thou it is governed

haphazard and fortuitously, or believest thou that there is in it any

rational guidance?'

'Nay,' said I, 'in no wise may I deem that such fixed motions can be

determined by random hazard, but I know that God, the Creator, presideth

over His work, nor will the day ever come that shall drive me from

holding fast the truth of this belief.'

'Yes,' said she; 'thou didst even but now affirm it in song, lamenting

that men alone had no portion in the divine care. As to the rest, thou

wert unshaken in the belief that they were ruled by reason. Yet I

marvel exceedingly how, in spite of thy firm hold on this opinion, thou

art fallen into sickness. But let us probe more deeply: something or

other is missing, I think. Now, tell me, since thou doubtest not that

God governs the world, dost thou perceive by what means He rules it?'
