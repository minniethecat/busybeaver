# Wheel of Fortune

The **Wheel of Fortune** (*Rota Fortunae*) is the most enduring image from [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *[[consolation|The Consolation of Philosophy]]*. It depicts [[fortune|Fortune]] as a goddess who turns a great wheel, raising some to prosperity while casting others down — a visual metaphor for the instability of worldly goods that became one of the most influential symbols of the Middle Ages.

## The Image in the Consolation

In Book II, [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] invokes the Wheel to argue that change is Fortune's very nature:

Lady Philosophy declares that Fortune "has only practiced her constant custom. It was her very inconstancy that kept her constant" (007-132).

The wheel's motion illustrates several key arguments:

1. **Fortune's mutability is predictable**: the wheel always turns; those raised up will be brought low, and those cast down may rise again
2. **Worldly goods are loans, not possessions**: Fortune gives and takes back what was never truly ours
3. **Adversity is more instructive than prosperity**: when Fortune frowns, she reveals who your true friends are (2.P8.1–2.P8.10)

## Iconographic Development

Boethius's text does not describe the wheel in elaborate visual detail — the image is evoked through Philosophy's rhetoric rather than pictorial description. The elaborate iconography of the *Rota Fortunae* developed in the medieval reception:

- **Romanesque manuscripts** (10th–12th centuries): early illuminated *Consolation* manuscripts begin depicting Fortune turning a wheel with human figures clinging to it
- **Gothic cathedrals**: the Wheel appears in rose windows and sculptural programs (e.g., Amiens, Beauvais)
- **Chaucer and Dante**: the wheel becomes a standard literary motif
- **The *Carmina Burana***: opens with "O Fortuna," a lament to Fortune's wheel that draws directly on Boethius's imagery

## Philosophical Significance

The Wheel of Fortune is more than a vivid image — it embodies Boethius's philosophical argument about the relationship between the temporal and eternal realms. The wheel represents the circular, unstable nature of the sublunary world, contrasted with the unchanging stability of divine [[providence|Providence]]. By learning to see through Fortune's deceptions, the prisoner is led upward from dependence on mutable goods toward the immutable [[good|Good]].

## Cultural Legacy

The *Rota Fortunae* became one of the most recognizable symbols of the medieval worldview, appearing in:
- Literature (Dante, Chaucer, Boccaccio, Villon)
- Visual art (illuminated manuscripts, cathedral sculpture, tarot cards)
- Political philosophy (the theme of the fall of princes)
- Modern adaptations (the "wheel of fortune" game show concept, though stripped of philosophical content)
