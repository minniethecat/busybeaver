# Section 17

[017-001] 'I cannot deny it.'

[017-002] 'What of the good fortune which is given as reward of the good--do the

[017-003] vulgar adjudge it bad?'

[017-004] 'Anything but that; they deem it to be the best, as indeed it is.'

[017-005] 'What, then, of that which remains, which, though it is harsh, puts the

[017-006] restraint of just punishment on the bad--does popular opinion deem it

[017-007] good?'

[017-008] 'Nay; of all that can be imagined, it is accounted the most miserable.'

[017-009] 'Observe, then, if, in following popular opinion, we have not ended in a

[017-010] conclusion quite paradoxical.'

[017-011] 'How so?' said I.

[017-012] 'Why, it results from our admissions that of all who have attained, or

[017-013] are advancing in, or are aiming at virtue, the fortune is in every case

[017-014] good, while for those who remain in their [[evil|wickedness]] fortune is always

[017-015] utterly bad.'

[017-016] 'It is true,' said I; 'yet no one dare acknowledge it.'

[017-017] 'Wherefore,' said she, 'the wise man ought not to take it ill, if ever

[017-018] he is involved in one of fortune's conflicts, any more than it becomes a

[017-019] brave soldier to be offended when at any time the trumpet sounds for

[017-020] battle. The time of trial is the express opportunity for the one to win

[017-021] glory, for the other to perfect his wisdom. Hence, indeed, virtue gets

[017-022] its name, because, relying on its own efficacy, it yieldeth not to

[017-023] adversity. And ye who have taken your stand on virtue's steep ascent,

[017-024] it is not for you to be dissolved in delights or enfeebled by pleasure;

[017-025] ye close in conflict--yea, in conflict most sharp--with all fortune's

[017-026] vicissitudes, lest ye suffer foul fortune to overwhelm or fair fortune

[017-027] to corrupt you. Hold the mean with all your strength. Whatever falls

[017-028] short of this, or goes beyond, is fraught with scorn of happiness, and

[017-029] misses the reward of toil. It rests with you to make your fortune what

[017-030] you will. Verily, every harsh-seeming fortune, unless it either

[017-031] disciplines or amends, is punishment.'

SONG VII.

[017-032] THE HERO'S PATH.

[017-033] Ten years a tedious warfare raged,

[017-034] Ere Ilium's smoking ruins paid

[017-035] For wedlock stained and faith betrayed,

[017-036] And great Atrides' wrath assuaged.

[017-037] But when heaven's anger asked a life,

[017-038] And baffling winds his course withstood,

[017-039] The king put off his fatherhood,

[017-040] And slew his child with priestly knife.

[017-041] When by the cavern's glimmering light

[017-042] His comrades dear Odysseus saw

[017-043] In the huge Cyclops' hideous maw

[017-044] Engulfed, he wept the piteous sight.

[017-045] But blinded soon, and wild with pain--

[017-046] In bitter tears and sore annoy--

[017-047] For that foul feast's unholy joy

[017-048] Grim Polyphemus paid again.

[017-049] His labours for Alcides win

[017-050] A name of glory far and wide;

[017-051] He tamed the Centaur's haughty pride,

[017-052] And from the lion reft his skin.

[017-053] The foul birds with sure darts he slew;

[017-054] The golden fruit he stole--in vain

[017-055] The dragon's watch; with triple chain

[017-056] From hell's depths Cerberus he drew.

[017-057] With their fierce lord's own flesh he fed

[017-058] The wild steeds; Hydra overcame

[017-059] With fire. 'Neath his own waves in shame

[017-060] Maimed Achelous hid his head.

[017-061] Huge Cacus for his crimes was slain;

[017-062] On Libya's sands Antæus hurled;

[017-063] The shoulders that upheld the world

[017-064] The great boar's dribbled spume did stain.

[017-065] Last toil of all--his might sustained

[017-066] The ball of heaven, nor did he bend

[017-067] Beneath; this toil, his labour's end,

[017-068] The prize of heaven's high glory gained.

[017-069] Brave hearts, press on! Lo, heavenward lead

[017-070] These bright examples! From the fight

[017-071] Turn not your backs in coward flight;

[017-072] Earth's conflict won, the stars your meed!

BOOK V.

[017-073] FREE WILL AND GOD'S FOREKNOWLEDGE.

[017-074] SUMMARY.

[017-075] CH. I. [[boethius|Boethius]] asks if there is really any such thing as chance.

[017-076] [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] answers, in conformity with [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s definition

[017-077] (Phys., II. iv.), that chance is merely relative to human purpose,

[017-078] and that what seems fortuitous really depends on a more subtle form

[017-079] of causation.--CH. II. Has man, then, any freedom, if the reign of

[017-080] law is thus absolute? Freedom of choice, replies Philosophy, is a

[017-081] necessary attribute of reason. Man has a measure of freedom, though

[017-082] a less perfect freedom than divine natures.--CH. III. But how can

[017-083] man's freedom be reconciled with God's absolute foreknowledge? If

[017-084] God's foreknowledge be certain, it seems to exclude the possibility

[017-085] of man's free will. But if man has no freedom of choice, it

[017-086] follows that rewards and punishments are unjust as well as useless;

[017-087] that merit and demerit are mere names; that God is the cause of

[017-088] men's wickednesses; that prayer is meaningless.--CH. IV. The

[017-089] explanation is that man's reasoning faculties are not adequate to

[017-090] the apprehension of the ways of God's foreknowledge. If we could

[017-091] know, as He knows, all that is most perplexing in this problem

[017-092] would be made plain. For knowledge depends not on the nature of the

[017-093] thing known, but on the faculty of the knower.--CH. V. Now, where

[017-094] our senses conflict with our reason, we defer the judgment of the

[017-095] lower faculty to the judgment of the higher. Our present perplexity

[017-096] arises from our viewing God's foreknowledge from the standpoint of

[017-097] human reason. We must try and rise to the higher standpoint of

[017-098] God's immediate intuition.--CH. VI. To understand this higher form

[017-099] of [[knowledge|cognition]], we must consider God's nature. God is eternal.

[017-100] [[eternity|Eternity]] is more than mere everlasting duration. Accordingly, His

[017-101] knowledge surveys past and future in the timelessness of an eternal

[017-102] present. His foreseeing is seeing. Yet this foreseeing does not in

[017-103] itself impose necessity, any more than our seeing things happen

[017-104] makes their happening necessary. We may, however, if we please,

[017-105] distinguish two necessities--one absolute, the other conditional on

[017-106] knowledge. In this conditional sense alone do the things which God

[017-107] foresees necessarily come to pass. But this kind of necessity

[017-108] affects not the nature of things. It leaves the reality of free

[017-109] will unimpaired, and the evils feared do not ensue. Our

[017-110] responsibility is great, since all that we do is done in the sight

[017-111] of all-seeing [[providence|Providence]].

BOOK V.

[017-112] I.

[017-113] She ceased, and was about to pass on in her discourse to the exposition

[017-114] of other matters, when I break in and say: 'Excellent is thine

[017-115] exhortation, and such as well beseemeth thy high authority; but I am

[017-116] even now experiencing one of the many difficulties which, as thou saidst

[017-117] but now, beset the question of providence. I want to know whether thou

[017-118] deemest that there is any such thing as chance at all, and, if so, what

[017-119] it is.'

[017-120] Then she made answer: 'I am anxious to fulfil my promise completely, and

[017-121] open to thee a way of return to thy native land. As for these matters,

[017-122] though very useful to know, they are yet a little removed from the path

[017-123] of our design, and I fear lest digressions should fatigue thee, and thou

[017-124] shouldst find thyself unequal to completing the direct journey to our

[017-125] goal.'

[017-126] 'Have no fear for that,' said I. 'It is rest to me to learn, where

[017-127] learning brings delight so exquisite, especially when thy argument has

[017-128] been built up on all sides with undoubted conviction, and no place is

[017-129] left for uncertainty in what follows.'

[017-130] She made answer: 'I will accede to thy request;' and forthwith she thus

[017-131] began: 'If chance be defined as a result produced by random movement

[017-132] without any link of causal connection, I roundly affirm that there is no

[017-133] such thing as chance at all, and consider the word to be altogether

[017-134] without meaning, except as a symbol of the thing designated. What place

[017-135] can be left for random action, when God constraineth all things to

[017-136] order? For "ex nihilo nihil" is sound doctrine which none of the

[017-137] ancients gainsaid, although they used it of material substance, not of

[017-138] the efficient principle; this they laid down as a kind of basis for all

[017-139] their reasonings concerning nature. Now, if a thing arise without

[017-140] causes, it will appear to have arisen from nothing. But if this cannot

[017-141] be, neither is it possible for there to be chance in accordance with the

[017-142] definition just given.'

[017-143] 'Well,' said I, 'is there, then, nothing which can properly be called

[017-144] chance or accident, or is there something to which these names are

[017-145] appropriate, though its nature is dark to the vulgar?'

[017-146] 'Our good Aristotle,' says she, 'has defined it concisely in his

[017-147] "Physics," and closely in accordance with the truth.'

[017-148] 'How, pray?' said I.

[017-149] 'Thus,' says she: 'Whenever something is done for the sake of a

[017-150] particular end, and for certain reasons some other result than that

[017-151] designed ensues, this is called chance; for instance, if a man is

[017-152] digging the earth for tillage, and finds a mass of buried gold. Now,

[017-153] such a find is regarded as accidental; yet it is not "ex nihilo," for it

[017-154] has its proper causes, the unforeseen and unexpected concurrence of

[017-155] which has brought the chance about. For had not the cultivator been

[017-156] digging, had not the man who hid the money buried it in that precise

[017-157] spot, the gold would not have been found. These, then, are the reasons

[017-158] why the find is a chance one, in that it results from causes which met

[017-159] together and concurred, not from any intention on the part of the

[017-160] discoverer. Since neither he who buried the gold nor he who worked in

[017-161] the field _intended_ that the money should be found, but, as I said, it

[017-162] _happened_ by coincidence that one dug where the other buried the

[017-163] treasure. We may, then, define chance as being an unexpected result

[017-164] flowing from a concurrence of causes where the several factors had some

[017-165] definite end. But the meeting and concurrence of these causes arises

[017-166] from that inevitable chain of order which, flowing from the

[017-167] fountain-head of Providence, disposes all things in their due time and

[017-168] place.'

SONG I.

[017-169] CHANCE.

[017-170] In the rugged Persian highlands,

[017-171] Where the masters of the bow

[017-172] Skill to feign a flight, and, fleeing,

[017-173] Hurl their darts and pierce the foe;

[017-174] There the Tigris and Euphrates

[017-175] At one source[O] their waters blend,

[017-176] Soon to draw apart, and plainward

[017-177] Each its separate way to wend.

[017-178] When once more their waters mingle

[017-179] In a channel deep and wide,

[017-180] All the flotsam comes together

[017-181] That is borne upon the tide:

[017-182] Ships, and trunks of trees, uprooted

[017-183] In the torrent's wild career,

[017-184] Meet, as 'mid the swirling waters

[017-185] Chance their random way may steer.

[017-186] Yet the shelving of the channel

[017-187] And the flowing water's force

[017-188] Guides each movement, and determines

[017-189] Every floating fragment's course.

[017-190] Thus, where'er the drift of hazard

[017-191] Seems most unrestrained to flow,

[017-192] Chance herself is reined and bitted,

[017-193] And the curb of law doth know.

II.

'I am following needfully,' said I, 'and I agree that it is as thou

sayest. But in this series of linked causes is there any freedom left to

our will, or does [[fate|the chain of fate]] bind also the very motions of our

souls?'

'There is freedom,' said she; 'nor, indeed, can any creature be

rational, unless he be endowed with free will. For that which hath the

natural use of reason has the faculty of discriminative judgment, and of

itself distinguishes what is to be shunned or desired. Now, everyone

seeks what he judges desirable, and avoids what he thinks should be

shunned. Wherefore, beings endowed with reason possess also the faculty

of free choice and refusal. But I suppose this faculty not equal alike

in all. The higher Divine essences possess a clear-sighted judgment, an

uncorrupt will, and an effective power of accomplishing their wishes.

Human souls must needs be comparatively free while they abide in the

contemplation of the Divine mind, less free when they pass into bodily

form, and still less, again, when they are enwrapped in earthly members.

But when they are given over to vices, and fall from the possession of

their proper reason, then indeed their condition is utter slavery. For

when they let their gaze fall from the light of highest truth to the

lower world where darkness reigns, soon ignorance blinds their vision;

they are disturbed by baneful affections, by yielding and assenting to

which they help to promote the slavery in which they are involved, and

are in a manner led captive by reason of their very liberty. Yet He who

seeth all things from eternity beholdeth these things with the eyes of

His providence, and assigneth to each what is predestined for it by its

merits:
