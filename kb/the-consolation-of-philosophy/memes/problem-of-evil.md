# Problem of Evil

The **problem of evil** — *si Deus iustus est, unde malum?* ("if God is just, whence comes evil?") — is the existential and philosophical question that drives much of the dramatic tension in [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *The Consolation of Philosophy*. The prisoner's opening complaint is not abstract but personal: he suffers unjustly while the wicked prosper.

## The Problem in the Consolation

Boethius raises the problem of evil on two levels:

### The Existential Level

The work opens with Boethius lamenting his own unjust imprisonment (1.M1–1.P4). He has served the state honorably, yet now awaits execution while his accusers thrive. This personal dimension gives the philosophical arguments their existential urgency.

### The Philosophical Level

In Book IV, Philosophy addresses the problem in its universal form (4.P1.1–4.P1.7): if a good God governs the world [[providence|providentially]], why do the wicked flourish while the [[virtue|virtuous]] suffer? This is the classic formulation of the [[theodicy|problem of evil]].

## Boethius's Response

### Evil Has No Substance (4.P2)

Following the Neoplatonic-Augustinian tradition, Boethius argues that [[evil]] is not a positive reality but a privation — the absence of [[good|the Good]] where good should be. Just as blindness is not a thing but the absence of sight, moral evil is the absence of right order in the will. God does not cause evil; He permits free creatures to defect from the good.

### The Wicked Are Already Punished (4.P3–4.P4)

In a distinctive argument, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] claims that the wicked are punished simply by being wicked. To lose one's humanity through vice is a greater misfortune than any external penalty. The successful criminal is more miserable than the one who is caught, because punishment may correct the latter while the former sinks deeper into vice.

### Providence and Perspective (4.P6)

The ultimate resolution appeals to the limits of human cognition. From our limited temporal perspective, events appear chaotic and unjust; from the eternal vantage of Providence, all events are ordered toward the good. The apparent prosperity of the wicked is temporary, and their eventual fate is determined by divine [[justice]] operating on a scale we cannot fully perceive.

## Historical Context

Boethius's treatment synthesizes:
- **[[plato|Plato]]'s** argument in the *Republic* that the just person is happier than the unjust
- **Augustine's** privation theory of evil and his argument that evil serves a larger providential purpose
- **[[stoicism|Stoic]]** arguments about the sufficiency of virtue for happiness

## Legacy

The problem of evil as formulated by Boethius — with its emphasis on the relationship between providence, [[free-will]], and divine [[justice]] — shaped medieval discussions from Anselm to Aquinas. It remains a central problem in the philosophy of religion.
