# Section 16

[016-001] 'It is this causal series which moves heaven and the stars, attempers

[016-002] the elements to mutual accord, and again in turn transforms them into

[016-003] new combinations; _this_ which renews the series of all things that are

[016-004] born and die through like successions of germ and birth; it is _its_

[016-005] operation which binds the destinies of men by an indissoluble nexus of

[016-006] causality, and, since it issues in the beginning from unalterable

[016-007] providence, these destinies also must of necessity be immutable.

[016-008] Accordingly, the world is ruled for the best if this unity abiding in

[016-009] the Divine mind puts forth an inflexible order of causes. And this

[016-010] order, by its intrinsic immutability, restricts things mutable which

[016-011] otherwise would ebb and flow at random. And so it happens that, although

[016-012] to you, who are not altogether capable of [[knowledge|understanding]] this order, all

[016-013] things seem confused and disordered, nevertheless there is everywhere an

[016-014] appointed limit which guides all things to good. Verily, nothing can be

[016-015] done for the sake of evil even by the wicked themselves; for, as we

[016-016] abundantly proved, they seek good, but are drawn out of the way by

[016-017] perverse error; far less can this order which sets out from the supreme

[016-018] centre of good turn aside anywhither from the way in which it began.

[016-019] '"Yet what confusion," thou wilt say, "can be more unrighteous than that

[016-020] prosperity and adversity should indifferently befall the good, what

[016-021] they like and what they loathe come alternately to the bad!" Yes; but

[016-022] have men in real life such soundness of mind that their judgments of

[016-023] [[justice|righteousness]] and [[evil|wickedness]] must necessarily correspond with facts?

[016-024] Why, on this very point their verdicts conflict, and those whom some

[016-025] deem worthy of reward, others deem worthy of punishment. Yet granted

[016-026] there were one who could rightly distinguish the good and bad, yet would

[016-027] he be able to look into the soul's inmost constitution, as it were, if

[016-028] we may borrow an expression used of the body? The marvel here is not

[016-029] unlike that which astonishes one who does not know why in health sweet

[016-030] things suit some constitutions, and bitter others, or why some sick men

[016-031] are best alleviated by mild remedies, others by severe. But the

[016-032] physician who distinguishes the precise conditions and characteristics

[016-033] of health and sickness does not marvel. Now, the health of the soul is

[016-034] nothing but righteousness, and vice is its sickness. God, the guide and

[016-035] physician of the mind, it is who preserves the good and banishes the

[016-036] bad. And He looks forth from the lofty watch-tower of His providence,

[016-037] perceives what is suited to each, and assigns what He knows to be

[016-038] suitable.

[016-039] 'This, then, is what that extraordinary mystery of the order of [[fate|destiny]]

[016-040] comes to--that something is done by one who knows, whereat the ignorant

[016-041] are astonished. But let us consider a few instances whereby appears what

[016-042] is the competency of human reason to fathom the Divine unsearchableness.

[016-043] Here is one whom thou deemest the perfection of justice and scrupulous

[016-044] integrity; to all-knowing [[providence|Providence]] it seems far otherwise. We all know

[016-045] our Lucan's admonition that it was the winning cause that found favour

[016-046] with the gods, the beaten cause with Cato. So, shouldst thou see

[016-047] anything in this world happening differently from thy expectation, doubt

[016-048] not but events are rightly ordered; it is in thy judgment that there is

[016-049] perverse confusion.

[016-050] 'Grant, however, there be somewhere found one of so happy a character

[016-051] that God and man alike agree in their judgments about him; yet is he

[016-052] somewhat infirm in strength of mind. It may be, if he fall into

[016-053] adversity, he will cease to practise that innocency which has failed to

[016-054] secure his fortune. Therefore, God's wise dispensation spares him whom

[016-055] adversity might make worse, will not let him suffer who is ill fitted

[016-056] for endurance. Another there is perfect in all virtue, so holy and nigh

[016-057] to God that providence judges it unlawful that aught untoward should

[016-058] befall him; nay, doth not even permit him to be afflicted with bodily

[016-059] disease. As one more excellent than I[N] hath said:

[016-060] '"The very body of the holy saint

[016-061] Is built of purest ether."

[016-062] Often it happens that the governance is given to the good that a

[016-063] restraint may be put upon superfluity of wickedness. To others

[016-064] providence assigns some mixed lot suited to their spiritual nature; some

[016-065] it will plague lest they grow rank through long prosperity; others it

[016-066] will suffer to be vexed with sore afflictions to confirm their virtues

[016-067] by the exercise and practice of patience. Some fear overmuch what they

[016-068] have strength to bear; others despise overmuch that to which their

[016-069] strength is unequal. All these it brings to the test of their true self

[016-070] through misfortune. Some also have bought a name revered to future ages

[016-071] at the price of a glorious death; some by invincible constancy under

[016-072] their sufferings have afforded an example to others that virtue cannot

[016-073] be overcome by calamity--all which things, without doubt, come to pass

[016-074] rightly and in due order, and to the benefit of those to whom they are

[016-075] seen to happen.

[016-076] 'As to the other side of the marvel, that the bad now meet with

[016-077] affliction, now get their hearts' desire, this, too, springs from the

[016-078] same causes. As to the afflictions, of course no one marvels, because

[016-079] all hold the wicked to be ill deserving. The truth is, their punishments

[016-080] both frighten others from crime, and amend those on whom they are

[016-081] inflicted; while their prosperity is a powerful sermon to the good, what

[016-082] judgments they ought to pass on good fortune of this kind, which often

[016-083] attends the wicked so assiduously.

[016-084] 'There is another object which may, I believe, be attained in such

[016-085] cases: there is one, perhaps, whose nature is so reckless and violent

[016-086] that poverty would drive him more desperately into crime. _His_ disorder

[016-087] providence relieves by allowing him to amass money. Such a one, in the

[016-088] uneasiness of a conscience stained with guilt, while he contrasts his

[016-089] character with his fortune, perchance grows alarmed lest he should come

[016-090] to mourn the loss of that whose possession is so pleasant to him. He

[016-091] will, then, reform his ways, and through the fear of losing his fortune

[016-092] he forsakes his iniquity. Some, through a prosperity unworthily borne,

[016-093] have been hurled headlong to ruin; to some the power of the sword has

[016-094] been committed, to the end that the good may be tried by discipline, and

[016-095] the bad punished. For while there can be no peace between the righteous

[016-096] and the wicked, neither can the wicked agree among themselves. How

[016-097] should they, when each is at variance with himself, because his vices

[016-098] rend his conscience, and ofttimes they do things which, when they are

[016-099] done, they judge ought not to have been done. Hence it is that this

[016-100] supreme providence brings to pass this notable marvel--that the bad make

[016-101] the bad good. For some, when they see the injustice which they

[016-102] themselves suffer at the hands of evil-doers, are inflamed with

[016-103] detestation of the offenders, and, in the endeavour to be unlike those

[016-104] whom they hate, return to the ways of virtue. It is the Divine power

[016-105] alone to which things evil are also good, in that, by putting them to

[016-106] suitable use, it bringeth them in the end to some good issue. For order

[016-107] in some way or other embraceth all things, so that even that which has

[016-108] departed from the appointed laws of the order, nevertheless falleth

[016-109] within _an_ order, though _another_ order, that nothing in the realm of

[016-110] providence may be left to haphazard. But

[016-111] '"Hard were the task, as a god, to recount all, nothing omitting."

[016-112] Nor, truly, is it lawful for man to compass in thought all the mechanism

[016-113] of the Divine work, or set it forth in speech. Let us be content to

[016-114] have apprehended this only--that God, the creator of universal nature,

[016-115] likewise disposeth all things, and guides them to good; and while He

[016-116] studies to preserve in likeness to Himself all that He has created, He

[016-117] banishes all evil from the borders of His commonweal through the links

[016-118] of fatal necessity. Whereby it comes to pass that, if thou look to

[016-119] disposing providence, thou wilt nowhere find the evils which are

[016-120] believed so to abound on earth.

[016-121] 'But I see thou hast long been burdened with the weight of the subject,

[016-122] and fatigued with the prolixity of the argument, and now lookest for

[016-123] some refreshment of sweet poesy. Listen, then, and may the draught so

[016-124] restore thee that thou wilt bend thy mind more resolutely to what

[016-125] remains.'

SONG VI.

THE UNIVERSAL AIM.

Wouldst thou with unclouded mind

View the laws by God designed,

Lift thy steadfast gaze on high

To the starry canopy;

See in rightful league of love

All the constellations move.

Fiery Sol, in full career,

Ne'er obstructs cold Phoebe's sphere;

When the Bear, at heaven's height,

Wheels his coursers' rapid flight,

Though he sees the starry train

Sinking in the western main,

He repines not, nor desires

In the flood to quench his fires.

In true sequence, as decreed,

Daily morn and eve succeed;

Vesper brings the shades of night,

Lucifer the morning light.

[[love|Love]], in alternation due,

Still the cycle doth renew,

And discordant strife is driven

From the starry realm of heaven.

Thus, in wondrous amity,

Warring elements agree;

Hot and cold, and moist and dry,

Lay their ancient quarrel by;

High the flickering flame ascends,

Downward earth for ever tends.

So the year in spring's mild hours

Loads the air with scent of flowers;

Summer paints the golden grain;

Then, when autumn comes again,

Bright with fruit the orchards glow;

Winter brings the rain and snow.

Thus the seasons' fixed progression,

Tempered in a due succession,

Nourishes and brings to birth

All that lives and breathes on earth.

Then, soon run life's little day,

All it brought it takes away.

But One sits and guides the reins,

He who made and all sustains;

King and Lord and Fountain-head,

Judge most holy, Law most dread;

Now impels and now keeps back,

Holds each waverer in the track.

Else, were once the power withheld

That the circling spheres compelled

In their orbits to revolve,

This world's order would dissolve,

And th' harmonious whole would all

In one hideous ruin fall.

But through this connected frame

Runs one universal aim;

Towards [[good|the Good]] do all things tend,

Many paths, but one the end.

For naught lasts, unless it turns

Backward in its course, and yearns

To that Source to flow again

Whence its being first was ta'en.

VII.

'Dost thou, then, see the consequence of all that we have said?'

'Nay; what consequence?'

'That absolutely every fortune is good fortune.'

'And how can that be?' said I.

'Attend,' said she. 'Since every fortune, welcome and unwelcome alike,

has for its object the reward or trial of the good, and the punishing or

amending of the bad, every fortune must be good, since it is either just

or useful.'

'The reasoning is exceeding true,' said I, 'the conclusion, so long as I

reflect upon the providence and fate of which thou hast taught me, based

on a strong foundation. Yet, with thy leave, we will count it among

those which just now thou didst set down as paradoxical.'

'And why so?' said she.

'Because ordinary speech is apt to assert, and that frequently, that

some men's fortune is bad.'

'Shall we, then, for awhile approach more nearly to the language of the

vulgar, that we may not seem to have departed too far from the usages of

men?'

'At thy good pleasure,' said I.

'That which advantageth thou callest good, dost thou not?'

'Certainly.'

'And that which either tries or amends advantageth?'

'Granted.'

'Is good, then?'

'Of course.'

'Well, this is _their_ case who have attained virtue and wage war with

adversity, or turn from vice and lay hold on the path of virtue.'
