# Divine Foreknowledge

Divine foreknowledge (*praescientia divina*) is the doctrine that God knows all events -- past, present, and future -- with infallible certainty, and that this knowledge is compatible with genuine human free will. Book V of *The Consolation of Philosophy* presents Boethius' definitive treatment of the problem, distinguishing conditional from simple necessity (018-050) and grounding God's knowledge in the eternal present rather than temporal succession (020-002).

---

## Definition & Context

Boethius frames divine foreknowledge as the central theological-epistemological problem of Book V. If God possesses infallible knowledge of all future events, it seems to follow that those events occur necessarily, eliminating free will and, with it, moral responsibility (017-084). The problem had a long pedigree: Aristotle debated future contingents in *De Interpretatione* (Chapter 9), and Cicero discussed providence and divination in *De Divinatione* (018-129). Boethius, however, offers an original solution rooted in the nature of divine eternity (020-002).

The core of the difficulty is that human reason, bound to temporal succession, cannot grasp how a timeless God can know contingent events without compelling them (017-096). Lady Philosophy's response proceeds by redefining the nature of divine knowledge itself: God does not have "fore-knowledge" in the temporal sense but rather an eternal present vision in which all times are simultaneous (020-009).

## Key Passages

**The problem stated.** Boethius, through the voice of Philosophy, articulates the dilemma that drives Book V (018-030, 018-036):

> "Wherefore, if from eternity He foreknows not only what men will do, but also their designs and wishes, there can be no freedom of the will. For there cannot be any action, or any desire, but such as the Divine foreknowledge -- if it be infallible -- has anticipated. For if things could be turned aside to a different issue than was foreknown, then there would no longer be any sure foreknowledge of the future, but uncertain conjecture." (018-030, 018-036)

**The charioteer analogy.** Philosophy dismantles the idea that foreknowledge imposes necessity by comparing it to sensory perception (018-129):

> "Take an illustration that will help to convey my meaning. There are many things which we see taking place before our eyes -- the movements of charioteers, for instance, in guiding and turning their cars, and so on. Now, is any one of these movements compelled by any necessity?" (018-129)

Just as seeing a charioteer turn his car does not force him to turn it, God's vision of future events does not force them to occur. Knowledge registers what is the case; it does not cause it (019-009).

**The hierarchy of cognition.** Philosophy establishes that the mode of knowledge follows the nature of the knower, not the thing known (019-108):

> "Various modes of cognition belong to distinct and different substances. For to creatures void of motive power ... belongs Sense alone ... to beasts endowed with movement ... Imagination also. Thought pertains only to the human race, as Intelligence to Divinity alone." (019-108)

God knows through pure *Intelligence* (*intelligentia*), which grasps the simple form directly without abstraction (019-108). Human reason, one step lower, cannot judge divine cognition by its own temporal standards (019-108).

**The definition of eternity.** The crux of the Boethian solution is the definition of eternity (020-002):

> "Eternity is the possession of endless life whole and perfect at a single moment." (020-002)

This is not merely infinite duration (*sempiternitas*) but timeless simultaneity. From God's eternal vantage, what appears to us as past, present, and future is all equally and immediately present (020-020).

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Lady Philosophy constructs her argument in four distinct stages.

**1. Distinguishing two kinds of necessity (Book V, Prose 3).** She first distinguishes between *simple necessity*, which arises from a thing's own nature (e.g., all men are mortal), and *conditional necessity*, which arises from the truth of a proposition about a contingent fact (e.g., if I see a man walking, it is necessarily true that he walks, but he is not walking by simple necessity) (018-050). Divine foreknowledge imposes only conditional necessity: events foreknown by God are certain *qua* known, not compelled *qua* occurring (018-047).

**2. The modes of cognition (Book V, Prose 4).** Philosophy argues that the specific mode of knowing depends on the nature of the knowing faculty, not on the nature of the object known (019-108). Sense, imagination, reason, and intelligence form an ascending hierarchy of cognitive modes. God, as the highest being, knows through pure intelligence, which apprehends all things in a single timeless act (019-108). It is an error to model divine knowledge on the temporal, sequential mode of human reason (019-018).

**3. The nature of divine eternity (Book V, Prose 6).** Philosophy defines eternity as the simultaneous and perfect possession of unlimited life (020-002). She distinguishes this from mere everlastingness: the world may endure forever in time, but it does not possess its whole life at once -- it is *sempiternal*, not *eternal* (019-108). God alone exists in the eternal present, to which all temporal moments are immediately available (020-020).

**4. The resolution: God sees free acts as present (Book V, Prose 6).** Because God exists in the eternal present, His "foreknowledge" is more properly a vision of all things as if happening now (020-009). He sees free human choices not as determined future events but as freely occurring present actions (020-057). The distinction between simple and conditional necessity, combined with the eternal present doctrine, preserves free will while maintaining divine infallibility (020-090).

## Boethius' Position

Boethius begins Book V in a state of intellectual anguish. He has accepted Philosophy's earlier arguments about providence, fate, and the supreme good (Books I-IV), but the foreknowledge problem threatens to unravel the entire edifice: if God's foreknowledge is certain, nothing depends on human choice, and moral responsibility collapses (017-084).

Philosophy's initial distinction between conditional and simple necessity is not, by itself, sufficient to satisfy Boethius. He presses the objection: even if foreknowledge is merely a sign of, not the cause of, future events, does not its very existence as an infallible sign imply that things could not be otherwise (018-046)? If events can turn out differently than they are foreknown, then foreknowledge is not knowledge at all but conjecture (018-090).

The breakthrough comes with the hierarchy of cognition and the definition of eternity (020-002). Once Boethius grasps that God's knowledge is not temporal "fore"-knowledge at all, but a single eternal vision of all times simultaneously, the contradiction dissolves. God sees free choices as free precisely because He sees them from a standpoint not limited by before and after (020-020). The concession Philosophy extracts from Boethius -- that human reason must submit to divine intelligence as sense submits to reason (019-108) -- is the key move that allows the argument to succeed.

Boethius' final acceptance is implicit in his silence: the *Consolation* ends with Philosophy's resolution of the foreknowledge problem. By granting that God's eternal vision is compatible with free will, Boethius reconciles the rational order of providence with the moral imperatives of human responsibility (020-111).
