## Definition & Context

The body (*corpus*) is the physical, material component of human nature, distinguished from and inferior to the immortal [[soul|soul]] (*anima*). In the Neoplatonic framework adopted by [[boethius|Boethius]], the body belongs to the realm of becoming, change, and mortality, while the soul belongs to the realm of being, permanence, and divinity. The body is not evil in itself, but its desires and limitations can distract the soul from its true end — the contemplation of the divine.

> "If thou lookest to his body, thou wilt see that man is a weak and frail thing" (006-137)

## Philosophical Background

The body-soul dualism in the *Consolation* synthesises three traditions. From **Plato's** *Phaedo*, Boethius draws the image of the body as a prison (*soma sema*) — the soul is trapped in the body during earthly life and achieves liberation at death. From **Aristotle**, he takes the view that the soul is the form of the body, giving it life and organization, though Boethius's dualism is more radical than Aristotle's hylomorphism. From **Neoplatonism**, he inherits the hierarchical ontology in which body occupies the lowest rank of being, below soul, intellect, and the One.

The Christian contribution is decisive: the body is not merely a prison but a creation of God, and its ultimate destiny is not annihilation but resurrection. Boethius's treatment is philosophically Neoplatonic but implicitly compatible with Christian doctrine — the body is inferior but not evil, a temporary dwelling rather than an eternal enemy.

## Key Passages

On the distinction between body and soul:

> "Save only as regards the body, and that which is lower than the body — I mean fortune — what! wilt thou attach any worth to that?" (006-141–006-142)

On the dissolution of the body at death:

> "It cannot be doubted but that, if happiness is truly known to consist in the possession of the perfect good, it cannot come to an end with the death of the body" (005-182–005-183)

The body is the seat of the passions that cloud the soul's vision:

> "After the dissolution of the body in death's last hour, the soul is freed to return to its true home" (007-086)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s treatment of the body is consistently hierarchical: the body is lower than the soul, and the goods of the body (health, beauty, pleasure) are lower than the goods of the soul (wisdom, virtue, contemplation). Her therapeutic strategy involves turning Boethius's attention away from the body's suffering — his imprisonment, physical discomfort, fear of death — and toward the soul's capacity for understanding.

The argument is not that the body is worthless but that its worth is derivative. The body is an instrument of the soul, not its master. When the body's desires govern the soul, the natural order is inverted and the person becomes less than human — a slave to passions that cannot satisfy. When the soul governs the body, the person achieves their proper nature — a rational being oriented toward the divine.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's imprisonment makes the question of the body acutely personal. He faces torture and execution; his body is literally in chains. Philosophy's argument that the body is a "weak and frail thing" (006-137) is not a dismissal of his suffering but a redirection of his attention. The body can be harmed, imprisoned, and killed — but these afflictions cannot touch the soul unless the soul consents. The Consul who was free in body but enslaved to ambition was less free than the prisoner who, through philosophy, has liberated his soul from bodily attachment.
