# True Happiness

**True happiness** (*beatitudo*) is the central theme of Book III of [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *The Consolation of Philosophy*. [[lady-philosophy|Lady Philosophy]] argues that all human beings desire [[happiness]] by nature, but most pursue it through inadequate means — wealth, power, fame, pleasure — which cannot deliver the fulfillment they promise. True happiness, she contends, is found only in the [[supreme-good|Supreme Good]], which is God.

## The Universality of the Desire for Happiness

Philosophy begins Book III with the observation that every human being, regardless of their particular pursuits, acts for the sake of happiness (3.P2.1–3.P2.8):

Philosophy observes that all mortals strive to attain the highest good, though by different paths (005-168, 005-170).

This universal orientation toward happiness is not a contingent preference but a structural feature of human nature. Even those who pursue wicked ends do so because they mistakenly believe those ends will make them happy.

## The Five False Paths

In a systematic survey (3.P3–3.P8), Philosophy examines the five principal candidates for happiness and finds each wanting:

| False Good | Why It Fails |
|------------|-------------|
| **Wealth** | Creates anxiety about loss; cannot satisfy all desires |
| **Power** | Cannot guarantee security; tyrants live in perpetual fear |
| **Fame** | Limited by geography and time; depends on others' opinions |
| **Pleasure** | Fleeting; excessive pleasure brings pain; shared with animals |
| **[[virtue|Virtue]] alone** | Though highest among temporal goods, virtue without the Supreme Good is incomplete |

## What True Happiness Requires

Philosophy argues that true happiness must be (3.P9–3.P10):

1. **Self-sufficient** (*per se sufficientem*): it leaves nothing to be desired beyond itself
2. **Perfect**: it contains within itself all particular goods in their most perfect form
3. **One**: happiness is not a collection of separate goods but a single, unified state

## Happiness as Divinization

The argument reaches its climax with the claim that true happiness is union with God (3.P10.23–3.P12.37). Since God is the Supreme Good, and true happiness consists in possession of the Supreme Good, therefore:

Philosophy concludes that every happy person is therefore a god — by participation, to be sure (011-050, 011-052).

This remarkable statement — *omnis beatus deus* — does not claim literal deification but rather that happiness consists in participation in the divine nature, which is [[good|goodness]] itself.

## Distinction from Stoic and Epicurean Views

Unlike the [[stoicism|Stoics]], who located happiness in virtue alone, and the Epicureans, who identified it with pleasure, Boethius locates happiness in a transcendent source beyond the self. Human beings can participate in happiness, but they cannot generate it from within their own resources.
