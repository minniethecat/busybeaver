## Identity & Context

Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius lived in the last quarter of the fifth century A.D. and the first quarter of the sixth (001-065). He belonged to an ancient family which boasted a connection with the legendary glories of the Republic, and was still among the foremost in wealth and dignity in the days of Rome's abasement (001-068, 001-069, 001-070). His parents dying early, he was brought up by Symmachus, whom the age agreed to regard as of almost saintly character, and afterwards became his son-in-law (001-071, 001-072). His varied gifts, aided by an excellent education, won for him the reputation of the most accomplished man of his time — he was orator, poet, musician, philosopher (001-074, 001-075). It is his peculiar distinction to have handed on to the Middle Ages the tradition of Greek philosophy by his Latin translations of the works of Aristotle (001-076).

Called early to a public career, the highest honours of the State came to him unsought (001-078). He was sole Consul in 510 A.D., and was ultimately raised by Theodoric to the dignity of Magister Officiorum, or head of the whole civil administration (001-079, 001-080). His felicity seemed to culminate in the year 522 A.D., when his two sons, young as they were, were created joint Consuls and rode to the senate-house attended by a throng of senators and the acclamations of the multitude (001-088, 001-089). Boethius himself, amid the general applause, delivered the public speech in the King's honour usual on such occasions (001-091). Within a year he was a solitary prisoner at Pavia, stripped of honours, wealth, and friends, with death hanging over him (001-092, 001-093). His wife was Rusticiana, known for her virtues, and his two sons were named Symmachus and Boethius (001-081, 001-082).

## Role in the Consolation

Boethius is both author and protagonist. The dialogue opens with him seated in prison, distraught with grief, indignant at the injustice of his misfortunes, and seeking relief for his melancholy in writing verses descriptive of his condition (001-098, 001-099). He represents himself as composing a mournful complaint:

> "Who wrought my studious numbers / Smoothly once in happier days, / Now perforce in tears and sadness / Learn a mournful strain to raise." (001-175–001-178)

He describes himself as aged by sorrow: "O'er my head untimely sprinkled / These white hairs my woes proclaim, / And the skin hangs loose and shrivelled / On this sorrow-shrunken frame" (001-195–001-198). He laments that Fortune has abandoned him: "For, while yet inconstant Fortune / Poured her gifts and all was bright, / Death's dark hour had all but whelmed me / In the gloom of endless night" (001-207–001-210).

When Philosophy first appears, Boethius is mute with amazement: "But I, because my sight was dimmed with much weeping, and I could not tell who was this woman of authority so commanding — I was dumfoundered, and, with my gaze fastened on the earth, continued silently to await what she might do next" (002-001, 002-002, 002-003). Philosophy must wipe the tears from his eyes before he can recognise her (002-130, 002-131).

## Key References

> "Noble, wealthy, accomplished, universally esteemed for his virtues, high in the favour of the Gothic King, he appeared to all men a signal example of the union of merit and good fortune." (001-084–001-086)

Philosophy questions Boethius's mental state, asking: "Art thou that man who, erstwhile fed with the milk and reared upon the nourishment which is mine to give, had grown up to the full vigour of a manly spirit? And yet I had bestowed such armour on thee as would have proved an invincible defence, hadst thou not first cast it away" (002-106–002-113).

Later in the dialogue, Boethius protests his innocence, declaring that he defended the entire senatorial order when Theodoric sought their destruction: "Thou rememberest how at Verona, when the king, eager for the general destruction, was bent on implicating the whole senatorial order in the charge of treason brought against Albinus, with what indifference to my own peril I maintained the innocence of its members, one and all" (003-022–003-025).

## Philosophical Significance

Boethius occupies a pivotal position in Western intellectual history. His translations of Aristotle transmitted Greek philosophy to the Latin-speaking Middle Ages (001-076). Within *The Consolation*, his personal trajectory — from despair at Fortune's injustice to philosophical understanding of Providence — models the therapeutic power of philosophy itself. He is described by Philosophy as one "nurtured in the Eleatic and Academic philosophies" (001-250). His journey from lamentation to wisdom enacts the central argument of the work: that true happiness lies not in Fortune's gifts but in the contemplation of the supreme Good.
