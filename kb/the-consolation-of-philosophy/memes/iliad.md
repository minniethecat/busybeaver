## Overview

Homer's *Iliad* is one of the classical literary works referenced in [[boethius|Boethius]]'s *Consolation of Philosophy*. Boethius, thoroughly educated in the classical canon, draws on Homeric characters and situations as rhetorical and philosophical exempla — concrete illustrations of abstract arguments about [[fate|fate]], suffering, and the human condition.

## References in the Consolation

The *Iliad* appears explicitly in the footnotes and allusions of the *Consolation*. Homeric heroes — [[achilles|Achilles]], Agamemnon, Hector — represent different responses to fate and suffering. The epic's vision of a world governed by both divine will and human choice provides a literary antecedent for Boethius's philosophical reconciliation of [[providence|Providence]] and [[free-will|free will]].

The translator notes that the lyric intensity of Homer — "Iliad, I. 363" (020-111) — serves as a model for the poetic passages of the *Consolation*. Philosophy's verses, like Homer's, use the resources of poetry to articulate truths that prose alone cannot capture.

## Significance

The *Iliad*'s presence in the *Consolation* reflects Boethius's project of synthesising classical culture with Christian philosophy. Homer represents the highest achievement of pagan poetry — and Boethius, by incorporating Homeric material into a Christian philosophical framework, demonstrates that the wisdom of antiquity is not to be rejected but completed.
