## Definition & Context

Wealth (*divitiae*, *opes*) is the possession of material goods — money, land, property — that [[fortune|Fortune]] gives and takes away. It is one of the five false goods that human beings mistakenly pursue as constituents of [[happiness|true happiness]], along with [[power|power]], [[honour|honour]], [[fame|fame]], and [[pleasure|pleasure]]. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] argues that wealth fails as a candidate for happiness because it is external, divisible, and incapable of satisfying the soul's infinite desire.

> "Noble, wealthy, accomplished, universally esteemed" (001-084)

## Key Passages

On Boethius's former wealth:

> "Noble, wealthy, accomplished, universally esteemed for his learning and virtue" (001-084)

On the loss of wealth:

> "Stripped of honours, wealth, and friends, a solitary prisoner at Pavia" (001-093)

On the insufficiency of wealth for happiness:

> "Why shouldst thou either grieve at the loss or rejoice at the possession of that in which there is no beauty to be desired?" (006-054)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's critique of wealth follows the same pattern as her critique of other Fortune-gifts. Wealth is external (it does not belong to the self), mutable (it can be lost at any moment), and morally neutral (it can belong to the wicked as easily as to the virtuous). But wealth has a distinctive vulnerability among false goods: it can only be possessed by being diminished. Unlike [[knowledge|knowledge]], which grows when shared, wealth decreases when distributed — a structural proof that wealth cannot be the true Good, which must be shareable without loss.
