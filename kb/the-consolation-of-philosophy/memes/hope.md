## Definition & Context

Hope (*spes*) is the expectation of a future good. In the *Consolation*, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] distinguishes between false hope — which attaches to [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts and is inevitably disappointed — and true hope — which is grounded in the recognition that [[providence|Providence]] orders all things toward the [[good|Good]]. The person who hopes in Fortune is enslaved to Fortune; the person who hopes in God is free.

> "Joy, hope, and fear" — Philosophy lists the passions that must be calmed (004-069)

## Philosophical Background

Hope was treated ambivalently in ancient philosophy. **Plato** recognized hope as one of the soul's fundamental drives. The **Stoics** classified hope (*spes*) alongside fear, desire, and pleasure as a passion that the wise person transcends — hope implies attachment to an uncertain future, whereas the wise person lives in the certainty of the present moment. **Christianity** elevated hope to a theological virtue (alongside faith and charity), grounded in the promise of salvation.

Boethius occupies the intersection of these traditions. He retains the Stoic critique of hope attached to external goods but affirms the Christian understanding of hope as essential to the spiritual life — properly directed toward God, hope is not a passion to be extinguished but a virtue to be cultivated.

## Key Passages

On the passions that afflict the soul:

> "Joy, hope, and fear" (004-069)

On the groundlessness of false hope:

> "No ground is left for hope or prayer if all things come to pass by fate" (018-115)

On hope and recovery:

> "From which thy recovery may be hoped" (004-035)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy does not counsel the elimination of hope but its reorientation. The hope that Boethius felt for acquittal, restoration to office, or political reversal was false hope — it attached to outcomes that Fortune controls. Philosophy's argument that Fortune's gifts are illusory is simultaneously an argument that hoping for them is futile.

But the dialogue does not end in hopelessness. By Book V, the discussion of [[prayer|prayer]] establishes that hope is meaningful precisely because it is part of the providential order. True hope is not the expectation of a particular temporal outcome but the confidence that all temporal outcomes, whatever they may be, are ordered toward the good. The prisoner who hopes for release may be disappointed; the prisoner who hopes in Providence cannot be.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius's opening lament is saturated with disappointed hope — he had hoped for justice, for the protection of his innocence, for the gratitude of the state he served. All these hopes were crushed. Philosophy's task is not to restore them but to show that they were misplaced from the beginning. The recovery is not from hopelessness to hope but from false hope to true hope — from hoping in Fortune to hoping in the eternal order that Fortune, unwittingly, serves.
