## Definition & Context

Love (*amor*) in *The Consolation of [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]* operates at two distinct but connected levels. At the cosmic level, love is the binding principle by which God governs the created order — a force that holds the elements in harmony, guides the stars in their courses, and maintains the unity of all things. At the human level, love is the desire that orients the soul toward its object: misdirected toward mutable goods (wealth, fame, bodily pleasure), it becomes the source of suffering; properly directed toward the eternal Good, it becomes the path to [[good|true happiness]].

Most of what [[boethius|Boethius]] calls "love" in his lament is actually disordered desire. He speaks of his "love of justice" as the cause of his suffering (002-007), framing his public service as a virtuous love betrayed. [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] gently but firmly recategorises this as attachment to a mutable good — reputation, civic standing — and redirects [[boethius|Boethius]]'s love toward its proper object: the divine Good itself, which cannot be taken away by tyrants or fortune.

## Key Passages

The hymn to cosmic love in Book II is one of the *Consolation*'s most celebrated passages:

> "If the love that rules the sky / Govern earth and sea, / In sweet love and holy peace / All things shall agree" (007-179, 007-167)

Love is not a sentimental emotion but the metaphysical principle that "rules the sky" — that is, governs the movement of the celestial spheres in their perfect order. The same love, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] argues, could rule the human soul if [[boethius|Boethius]] would let it. His suffering is the consequence of resisting the cosmic order, not of being abandoned by it.

The universality of love's governance extends even to apparent conflict:

> "See in rightful league of love / All the constellations move" (016-125)

The constellations, symbols of fate and [[fate|destiny]] in ancient thought, are not instruments of blind mechanism but participants in a "rightful league of love." This reframes [[evil|the problem of evil]]: what appears as injustice or cosmic indifference is, from the divine perspective, a harmonious order governed by love — a truth that human reason can grasp but not fully comprehend from its temporal vantage point.

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s argument about love takes the form of a redirection rather than a denial. She does not tell [[boethius|Boethius]] to stop loving — that would be impossible, since love is [[nature|the natural]] movement of the soul toward what it perceives as good. Instead, she teaches him what is truly worthy of love.

The argument proceeds through a critique of false loves. Do you love wealth? "Thy love the good must have, the bad thy hate" (015-079) — but wealth can be possessed by the wicked, so it cannot be [[truth|the true]] Good. Do you love fame? Fame is "the applause of fame" sought by the many (007-053), a thing bestowed by the fickle multitude and easily withdrawn. Do you love life itself? The "love of life" shared by all creatures "cometh not of conscious will" (011-218) but of nature — it is a useful instinct for preservation, not a guide to happiness. Each false love is exposed as a misdirection of the soul's innate desire for [[good|the Good]].

True love, [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] concludes, is directed toward God, the unmoved source of all goodness, whose perfection suffers no diminishment and whose possession cannot be lost. The soul that loves [[good|the Good]] participates in the cosmic order governed by divine love. "In sweet love and holy peace, all things shall agree" (007-167) — this is not a description of the world as it appears but a promise about the world as it truly is, and as the soul can experience it when its love is rightly ordered.

## Boethius' Position

[[boethius|Boethius]] begins the dialogue in a state of disordered love. His lament over the "love of justice" that brought him to ruin (002-007) reveals a man whose love is still attached to earthly outcomes — the success of just causes, the gratitude of the senate — rather than to justice itself as a participation in divine goodness. His tears are the tears of a man whose loves have been disappointed, not of a man who has lost [[truth|the true]] object of love.

[[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s therapy gradually reorders his love. She does not demand that he stop caring about justice; she shows him that true justice is a quality of the soul and of God's cosmic order, not of human institutions. By the end of the dialogue, [[boethius|Boethius]]'s love has been reoriented from the mutable to the eternal. He no longer laments his earthly fate because his love has found its object in the unchanging Good.
