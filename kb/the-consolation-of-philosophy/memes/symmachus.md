## Identity & Context

Quintus Aurelius Memmius Symmachus (d. AD 525) was a Roman senator, consul, and the head of one of the last great senatorial families of the Western Empire. He was also Boethius's father-in-law — the father of Rusticiana, Boethius's wife — and after the early death of Boethius's own father, Symmachus became his guardian and mentor. "Being early bereaved of his father, he was brought up by Symmachus" (001-071) — this biographical note establishes Symmachus as the formative influence on Boethius's character and career.

Symmachus's historical significance extends beyond his relationship with Boethius. He was a patron of learning, a man of literary and philosophical cultivation who preserved the traditions of Roman civic virtue in an age of Gothic rule. His execution by Theodoric — which followed Boethius's own imprisonment and death — marked the end of an era: the last great Roman senator, killed by the barbarian king whose regime he had sought to civilise.

## Role in the Consolation

Symmachus appears in the *Consolation* primarily as a source of both comfort and anguish for Boethius. In his lament, he describes himself as "happy in his wife and in his sons; happy also in his father-in‑law, Symmachus" (001-083). The past tense — "happy *also* in his father-in‑law" — is painful because Symmachus is still alive, but Boethius can no longer enjoy his presence. The man who taught him virtue, who shaped his character, who embodied the union of philosophical wisdom and public service — Boethius is separated from him by prison walls and by the shame of his fall.

Philosophy uses Symmachus strategically. She reminds Boethius that "Fortune's better gifts" — the things that make life genuinely good — are still present in his life. "Yet Symmachus, your father-in‑law, still lives — a man whose wisdom and virtue are noble" (005-102). The survival of Symmachus is one of Philosophy's "gentle remedies" (004-006): a proof that Fortune has not taken everything. That Symmachus will later share Boethius's fate — executed by Theodoric in the same purge — lies outside the dialogue's frame, but it hovers as a dark irony over Philosophy's consolation.

## Key References

The biographical note establishes Symmachus as Boethius's guardian and mentor:

> "Being early bereaved of his father, he was brought up by Symmachus, whom he ever reverenced as his father" (001-071)

Boethius's lament includes Symmachus among his lost happiness:

> "Happy also in his father-in‑law Symmachus" (001-083)

Philosophy reminds Boethius that Symmachus survives:

> "Yet Symmachus, your father-in‑law, still lives — a man in whom wisdom and virtue are so nobly blended" (005-102)

## Philosophical Significance

Symmachus embodies the ideal that Boethius himself had sought to realise: the union of philosophical wisdom and public service. He is, within the *Consolation*'s world, the living proof that virtue can coexist with political engagement, that the contemplative life need not be pursued in isolation from the active one.

This makes Symmachus both Philosophy's strongest argument and Boethius's deepest wound. The strongest argument, because Symmachus's survival shows Fortune has not taken everything — Boethius still possesses what Philosophy insists are the true goods: family, virtue, the love of the wise. The deepest wound, because Symmachus's example reminds Boethius of what he himself has lost — his own standing, his own honour, his own ability to be the kind of man Symmachus is. The father-in‑law's continued virtue throws the son-in‑law's fallen state into sharper relief.
