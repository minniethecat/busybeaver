## Definition & Context

Pleasure (*voluptas*) is the gratification of bodily appetite — sensory enjoyment, comfort, and the satisfaction of desire. It is one of [[fortune|Fortune]]'s five false goods and, in [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]]'s hierarchy, the lowest — because it is shared with animals and degrades the rational nature of the human soul. Philosophy's critique of pleasure draws on both the Platonic tradition (the body as prison) and the Stoic tradition (pleasure as a passion that disturbs tranquillity).

> "Pleasure's sting" (001-133)

## Key Passages

On the paradoxical nature of bodily pleasure:

> "Pleasure's sting" (001-133) — the phrase captures the paradox: pleasure promises satisfaction but delivers pain

On the fleeting nature of pleasure:

> "If her flight overwhelms with calamity, what is this but the deceit of pleasure?" (004-137)

On the relationship between pleasure and the body:

> "These that were the pride and pleasure of the world" (001-187)

## Lady Philosophy's Argument

Philosophy's critique of bodily pleasure is the most uncompromising section of her attack on false goods. Pleasure is not merely insufficient for happiness; it is actively degrading. The person who pursues pleasure makes himself like a beast, because pleasure is the good that humans share with animals. Reason, not appetite, is the distinctive human faculty — and the life oriented toward pleasure is a life lived below the human.

The argument draws on the image of Circe's enchantment in Homer — those who drink the cup of pleasure are transformed into swine. Philosophy's version is more subtle: the transformation is not into a different species but into a degraded version of one's own — the human being who lives for pleasure has not ceased to be human but has ceased to live humanly.

## Boethius' Position

Boethius is not presented as particularly susceptible to the temptation of pleasure — his sickness is attachment to higher goods (justice, honour, the public good). But Philosophy's critique of pleasure completes her systematic demolition of all five false goods. Having shown that none of Fortune's gifts — from the lowest (pleasure) to the highest (honour) — can satisfy the soul, she prepares the ground for her positive argument: only the supreme Good, which is God, can truly satisfy.
