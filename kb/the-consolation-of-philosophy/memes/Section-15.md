# Section 15

[015-001] Then said I: 'While I follow thy reasonings, I am deeply impressed with

[015-002] their truth; but if I turn to the common convictions of men, I find few

[015-003] who will even listen to such arguments, let alone admit them to be

[015-004] credible.'

[015-005] 'True,' said she; 'they cannot lift eyes accustomed to darkness to the

[015-006] light of clear truth, and are like those birds whose vision night

[015-007] illumines and day blinds; for while they regard, not the order of the

[015-008] universe, but their own dispositions of mind, they think the license to

[015-009] commit crime, and the escape from punishment, to be fortunate. But mark

[015-010] the ordinance of eternal law. Hast thou fashioned thy soul to the

[015-011] likeness of the better, thou hast no need of a judge to award the

[015-012] prize--by thine own act hast thou raised thyself in the scale of

[015-013] excellence; hast thou perverted thy affections to baser things, look not

[015-014] for punishment from one without thee--thine own act hath degraded thee,

[015-015] and thrust thee down. Even so, if alternately thou turn thy gaze upon

[015-016] the vile earth and upon the heavens, though all without thee stand

[015-017] still, by the mere laws of sight thou seemest now sunk in the mire, now

[015-018] soaring among the stars. But the common herd regards not these things.

[015-019] What, then? Shall we go over to those whom we have shown to be like

[015-020] brute beasts? Why, suppose, now, one who had quite lost his sight

[015-021] should likewise forget that he had ever possessed the faculty of vision,

[015-022] and should imagine that nothing was wanting in him to human perfection,

[015-023] should we deem those who saw as well as ever blind? Why, they will not

[015-024] even assent to this, either--that they who do wrong are more wretched

[015-025] than those who suffer wrong, though the proof of this rests on grounds

[015-026] of reason no less strong.'

[015-027] 'Let me hear these same reasons,' said I.

[015-028] 'Wouldst thou deny that every wicked man deserves punishment?'

[015-029] 'I would not, certainly.'

[015-030] 'And that those who are wicked are unhappy is clear in manifold ways?'

[015-031] 'Yes,' I replied.

[015-032] 'Thou dost not doubt, then, that those who deserve punishment are

[015-033] wretched?'

[015-034] 'Agreed,' said I.

[015-035] 'So, then, if thou wert sitting in judgment, on whom wouldst thou decree

[015-036] the infliction of punishment--on him who had done the wrong, or on him

[015-037] who had suffered it?'

[015-038] 'Without doubt, I would compensate the sufferer at the cost of the doer

[015-039] of the wrong.'

[015-040] 'Then, the injurer would seem more wretched than the injured?'

[015-041] 'Yes; it follows. And so for this and other reasons resting on the same

[015-042] ground, inasmuch as baseness of its own nature makes men wretched, it is

[015-043] plain that a wrong involves the misery of the doer, not of the

[015-044] sufferer.'

[015-045] 'And yet,' says she, 'the practice of the law-courts is just the

[015-046] opposite: advocates try to arouse the commiseration of the judges for

[015-047] those who have endured some grievous and cruel wrong; whereas pity is

[015-048] rather due to the criminal, who ought to be brought to the judgment-seat

[015-049] by his accusers in a spirit not of anger, but of compassion and

[015-050] kindness, as a sick man to the physician, to have the ulcer of his fault

[015-051] cut away by punishment. Whereby the business of the advocate would

[015-052] either wholly come to a standstill, or, did men prefer to make it

[015-053] serviceable to mankind, would be restricted to the practice of

[015-054] accusation. The wicked themselves also, if through some chink or cranny

[015-055] they were permitted to behold the virtue they have forsaken, and were to

[015-056] see that by the pains of punishment they would rid themselves of the

[015-057] uncleanness of their vices, and win in exchange the recompense of

[015-058] [[justice|righteousness]], they would no longer think these sufferings pains; they

[015-059] would refuse the help of advocates, and would commit themselves wholly

[015-060] into the hands of their accusers and judges. Whence it comes to pass

[015-061] that for the wise no place is left for hatred; only the most foolish

[015-062] would hate the good, and to hate the bad is unreasonable. For if vicious

[015-063] propensity is, as it were, a disease of the soul like bodily sickness,

[015-064] even as we account the sick in body by no means deserving of hate, but

[015-065] rather of pity, so, and much more, should they be pitied whose minds are

[015-066] assailed by [[evil|wickedness]], which is more frightful than any sickness.'

SONG IV.

[015-067] THE UNREASONABLENESS OF HATRED.

[015-068] Why all this furious strife? Oh, why

[015-069] With rash and wilful hand provoke death's destined day?

[015-070] If death ye seek--lo! Death is nigh,

[015-071] Not of their master's will those coursers swift delay!

[015-072] The wild beasts vent on man their rage,

[015-073] Yet 'gainst their brothers' lives men point the murderous steel;

[015-074] Unjust and cruel wars they wage,

[015-075] And haste with flying darts the death to meet or deal.

[015-076] No right nor reason can they show;

[015-077] 'Tis but because their lands and laws are not the same.

[015-078] Wouldst _thou_ give each his due; then know

[015-079] Thy love the good must have, the bad thy pity claim.

[015-080] V.

[015-081] On this I said: 'I see how there is a happiness and misery founded on

[015-082] the actual deserts of the righteous and the wicked. Nevertheless, I

[015-083] wonder in myself whether there is not some good and evil in fortune as

[015-084] the vulgar understand it. Surely, no sensible man would rather be

[015-085] exiled, poor and disgraced, than dwell prosperously in his own country,

[015-086] powerful, wealthy, and high in honour. Indeed, [[wisdom|the work of wisdom]] is

[015-087] more clear and manifest in its operation when the happiness of rulers is

[015-088] somehow passed on to the people around them, especially considering that

[015-089] the prison, the law, and the other pains of legal punishment are

[015-090] properly due only to mischievous citizens on whose account they were

[015-091] originally instituted. Accordingly, I do exceedingly marvel why all this

[015-092] is completely reversed--why the good are harassed with the penalties due

[015-093] to crime, and the bad carry off the rewards of virtue; and I long to

[015-094] hear from thee what reason may be found for so unjust a state of

[015-095] disorder. For assuredly I should wonder less if I could believe that all

[015-096] things are the confused result of chance. But now my belief in God's

[015-097] governance doth add amazement to amazement. For, seeing that He

[015-098] sometimes assigns fair fortune to the good and harsh fortune to the bad,

[015-099] and then again deals harshly with the good, and grants to the bad their

[015-100] hearts' desire, how does this differ from chance, unless some reason is

[015-101] discovered for it all?'

[015-102] 'Nay; it is not wonderful,' said she, 'if all should be thought random

[015-103] and confused when the principle of order is not known. And though thou

[015-104] knowest not the causes on which this great system depends, yet forasmuch

[015-105] as a good ruler governs the world, doubt not for thy part that all is

[015-106] rightly done.'

SONG V.

[015-107] WONDER AND IGNORANCE.

[015-108] Who knoweth not how near the pole

[015-109] Bootes' course doth go,

[015-110] Must marvel by what heavenly law

[015-111] He moves his Wain so slow;

[015-112] Why late he plunges 'neath the main,

[015-113] And swiftly lights his beams again.

[015-114] When the full-orbèd moon grows pale

[015-115] In the mid course of night,

[015-116] And suddenly the stars shine forth

[015-117] That languished in her light,

[015-118] Th' astonied nations stand at gaze,

[015-119] And beat the air in wild amaze.[M]

[015-120] None marvels why upon the shore

[015-121] The storm-lashed breakers beat,

[015-122] Nor why the frost-bound glaciers melt

[015-123] At summer's fervent heat;

[015-124] For here the cause seems plain and clear,

[015-125] Only what's dark and hid we fear.

[015-126] Weak-minded folly magnifies

[015-127] All that is rare and strange,

[015-128] And the dull herd's o'erwhelmed with awe

[015-129] At unexpected change.

[015-130] But wonder leaves enlightened minds,

[015-131] When ignorance no longer blinds.

FOOTNOTES:

[M] To frighten away the monster swallowing the moon. The superstition

was once common. See Tylor's 'Primitive Culture,' pp. 296-302.

VI.

'True,' said I; 'but, since it is thy office to unfold the hidden cause

of things, and explain principles veiled in darkness, inform me, I pray

thee, of thine own conclusions in this matter, since the marvel of it is

what more than aught else disturbs my mind.'

A smile played one moment upon her lips as she replied: 'Thou callest me

to the greatest of all subjects of inquiry, a task for which the most

exhaustive treatment barely suffices. Such is its nature that, as fast

as one doubt is cut away, innumerable others spring up like Hydra's

heads, nor could we set any limit to their renewal did we not apply the

mind's living fire to suppress them. For there come within its scope the

questions of the essential simplicity of providence, of the order of

fate, of unforeseen chance, of the Divine knowledge and predestination,

and of the freedom of the will. How heavy is the weight of all this

thou canst judge for thyself. But, inasmuch as to know these things also

is part of the treatment of thy malady, we will try to give them some

consideration, despite the restrictions of the narrow limits of our

time. Moreover, thou must for a time dispense with the pleasures of

music and song, if so be that thou findest any delight therein, whilst I

weave together the connected train of reasons in proper order.'

'As thou wilt,' said I.

Then, as if making a new beginning, she thus discoursed: 'The coming

into being of all things, the whole course of development in things that

change, every sort of thing that moves in any wise, receives its due

cause, order, and form from the steadfastness of the Divine mind. This

mind, calm in the citadel of its own essential simplicity, has decreed

that the method of its rule shall be manifold. Viewed in the very purity

of the Divine intelligence, this method is called _providence_; but

viewed in regard to those things which it moves and disposes, it is

what the ancients called _fate_. That these two are different will

easily be clear to anyone who passes in review their respective

efficacies. [[providence|Providence]] is the Divine reason itself, seated in the

Supreme Being, which disposes all things; fate is the disposition

inherent in all things which move, through which providence joins all

things in their proper order. Providence embraces all things, however

different, however infinite; fate sets in motion separately individual

things, and assigns to them severally their position, form, and time.

'So the unfolding of this temporal order unified into the foreview of

the Divine mind is providence, while the same unity broken up and

unfolded in time is fate. And although these are different, yet is there

a dependence between them; for the order of [[fate|destiny]] issues from the

essential simplicity of providence. For as the artificer, forming in his

mind beforehand the idea of the thing to be made, carries out his

design, and develops from moment to moment what he had before seen in a

single instant as a whole, so God in His providence ordains all things

as parts of a single unchanging whole, but carries out these very

ordinances by fate in a time of manifold unity. So whether fate is

accomplished by Divine spirits as the ministers of providence, or by a

soul, or by the service of all nature--whether by the celestial motion

of the stars, by the efficacy of angels, or by the many-sided cunning of

demons--whether by all or by some of these the destined series is woven,

this, at least, is manifest: that providence is the fixed and simple

form of destined events, fate their shifting series in order of time, as

by the disposal of the Divine simplicity they are to take place. Whereby

it is that all things which are under fate are subjected also to

providence, on which fate itself is dependent; whereas certain things

which are set under providence are above the chain of fate--viz., those

things which by their nearness to the primal Divinity are steadfastly

fixed, and lie outside the order of fate's movements. For as the

innermost of several circles revolving round the same centre approaches

the simplicity of the midmost point, and is, as it were, a pivot round

which the exterior circles turn, while the outermost, whirled in ampler

orbit, takes in a wider and wider sweep of space in proportion to its

departure from the indivisible unity of the centre--while, further,

whatever joins and allies itself to the centre is narrowed to a like

simplicity, and no longer expands vaguely into space--even so whatsoever

departs widely from primal mind is involved more deeply in the meshes of

fate, and things are free from fate in proportion as they seek to come

nearer to that central pivot; while if aught cleaves close to supreme

mind in its absolute fixity, this, too, being free from movement, rises

above fate's necessity. Therefore, as is reasoning to pure intelligence,

as that which is generated to that which is, time to eternity, a circle

to its centre, so is the shifting series of fate to the steadfastness

and simplicity of providence.
