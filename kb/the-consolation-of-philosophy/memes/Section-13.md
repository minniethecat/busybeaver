# Section 13

BOOK IV.

[013-001] I.

[013-002] Softly and sweetly [[lady-philosophy|Philosophy]] sang these verses to the end without

[013-003] losing aught of the dignity of her expression or the seriousness of her

[013-004] tones; then, forasmuch as I was as yet unable to forget my deeply-seated

[013-005] sorrow, just as she was about to say something further, I broke in and

[013-006] cried: 'O thou guide into the way of true light, all that thy voice hath

[013-007] uttered from the beginning even unto now has manifestly seemed to me at

[013-008] once divine contemplated in itself, and by the force of thy arguments

[013-009] placed beyond the possibility of overthrow. Moreover, these truths have

[013-010] not been altogether unfamiliar to me heretofore, though because of

[013-011] indignation at my wrongs they have for a time been forgotten. But, lo!

[013-012] herein is the very chiefest cause of my grief--that, while there exists

[013-013] a good ruler of the universe, it is possible that evil should be at all,

[013-014] still more that it should go unpunished. Surely thou must see how

[013-015] deservedly this of itself provokes astonishment. But a yet greater

[013-016] marvel follows: While [[evil|wickedness]] reigns and flourishes, virtue not only

[013-017] lacks its reward, but is even thrust down and trampled under the feet of

[013-018] the wicked, and suffers punishment in the place of crime. That this

[013-019] should happen under the rule of a God who knows all things and can do

[013-020] all things, but wills only the good, cannot be sufficiently wondered at

[013-021] nor sufficiently lamented.'

[013-022] Then said she: 'It would indeed be infinitely astounding, and of all

[013-023] monstrous things most horrible, if, as thou esteemest, in the

[013-024] well-ordered home of so great a householder, the base vessels should be

[013-025] held in honour, the precious left to neglect. But it is not so. For if

[013-026] we hold unshaken those conclusions which we lately reached, thou shall

[013-027] learn that, by the will of Him of whose realm we are speaking, the good

[013-028] are always strong, the bad always weak and impotent; that vices never go

[013-029] unpunished, nor virtues unrewarded; that good fortune ever befalls the

[013-030] good, and ill fortune the bad, and much more of the sort, which shall

[013-031] hush thy murmurings, and stablish thee in the strong assurance of

[013-032] conviction. And since by my late instructions thou hast seen the form of

[013-033] happiness, hast learnt, too, the seat where it is to be found, all due

[013-034] preliminaries being discharged, I will now show thee the road which will

[013-035] lead thee home. Wings, also, will I fasten to thy mind wherewith thou

[013-036] mayst soar aloft, that so, all disturbing doubts removed, thou mayst

[013-037] return safe to thy country, under my guidance, in the path I will show

[013-038] thee, and by the means which I furnish.'

SONG I.

[013-039] THE SOUL'S FLIGHT.

[013-040] Wings are mine; above the pole

[013-041] Far aloft I soar.

[013-042] Clothed with these, my nimble soul

[013-043] Scorns earth's hated shore,

[013-044] Cleaves the skies upon the wind,

[013-045] Sees the clouds left far behind.

[013-046] Soon the glowing point she nears,

[013-047] Where the heavens rotate,

[013-048] Follows through the starry spheres

[013-049] Phoebus' course, or straight

[013-050] Takes for comrade 'mid the stars

[013-051] Saturn cold or glittering Mars;

[013-052] Thus each circling orb explores

[013-053] Through Night's stole that peers;

[013-054] Then, when all are numbered, soars

[013-055] Far beyond the spheres,

[013-056] Mounting heaven's supremest height

[013-057] To the very Fount of light.

[013-058] There the Sovereign of the world

[013-059] His calm sway maintains;

[013-060] As the globe is onward whirled

[013-061] Guides the chariot reins,

[013-062] And in splendour glittering

[013-063] Reigns the universal King.

[013-064] Hither if thy wandering feet

[013-065] Find at last a way,

[013-066] Here thy long-lost home thou'lt greet:

[013-067] 'Dear lost land,' thou'lt say,

[013-068] 'Though from thee I've wandered wide,

[013-069] Hence I came, here will abide.'

[013-070] Yet if ever thou art fain

[013-071] Visitant to be

[013-072] Of earth's gloomy night again,

[013-073] Surely thou wilt see

[013-074] Tyrants whom the nations fear

[013-075] Dwell in hapless exile here.

[013-076] II.

[013-077] Then said I: 'Verily, wondrous great are thy promises; yet I do not

[013-078] doubt but thou canst make them good: only keep me not in suspense after

[013-079] raising such hopes.'

[013-080] 'Learn, then, first,' said she, 'how that power ever waits upon the

[013-081] good, while the bad are left wholly destitute of strength.[K] Of these

[013-082] truths the one proves the other; for since good and evil are contraries,

[013-083] if it is made plain that good is power, the feebleness of evil is

[013-084] clearly seen, and, conversely, if the frail nature of evil is made

[013-085] manifest, the strength of good is thereby known. However, to win ampler

[013-086] credence for my conclusion, I will pursue both paths, and draw

[013-087] confirmation for my statements first in one way and then in the other.

[013-088] 'The carrying out of any human action depends upon two things--to wit,

[013-089] will and power; if either be wanting, nothing can be accomplished. For

[013-090] if the will be lacking, no attempt at all is made to do what is not

[013-091] willed; whereas if there be no power, the will is all in vain. And so,

[013-092] if thou seest any man wishing to attain some end, yet utterly failing to

[013-093] attain it, thou canst not doubt that he lacked the power of getting what

[013-094] he wished for.'

[013-095] 'Why, certainly not; there is no denying it.'

[013-096] 'Canst thou, then, doubt that he whom thou seest to have accomplished

[013-097] what he willed had also the power to accomplish it?'

[013-098] 'Of course not.'

[013-099] 'Then, in respect of what he can accomplish a man is to be reckoned

[013-100] strong, in respect of what he cannot accomplish weak?'

[013-101] 'Granted,' said I.

[013-102] 'Then, dost thou remember that, by our former reasonings, it was

[013-103] concluded that the whole aim of man's will, though the means of pursuit

[013-104] vary, is set intently upon happiness?'

[013-105] 'I do remember that this, too, was proved.'

[013-106] 'Dost thou also call to mind how happiness is absolute good, and

[013-107] therefore that, when happiness is sought, it is good which is in all

[013-108] cases the object of desire?'

[013-109] 'Nay, I do not so much call to mind as keep it fixed in my memory.'

[013-110] 'Then, all men, good and bad alike, with one indistinguishable purpose

[013-111] strive to reach good?'

[013-112] 'Yes, that follows.'

[013-113] 'But it is certain that by the attainment of good men become good?'

[013-114] 'It is.'

[013-115] 'Then, do the good attain their object?'

[013-116] 'It seems so.'

[013-117] 'But if the bad were to attain the good which is _their_ object, they

[013-118] could not be bad?'

[013-119] 'No.'

[013-120] 'Then, since both seek good, but while the one sort attain it, the other

[013-121] attain it not, is there any doubt that the good are endued with power,

[013-122] while they who are bad are weak?'

[013-123] 'If any doubt it, he is incapable of reflecting on the nature of things,

[013-124] or the consequences involved in reasoning.'

[013-125] 'Again, supposing there are two things to which the same function is

[013-126] prescribed in the course of nature, and one of these successfully

[013-127] accomplishes the function by natural action, the other is altogether

[013-128] incapable of that natural action, instead of which, in a way other than

[013-129] is agreeable to its nature, it--I will not say fulfils its function, but

[013-130] feigns to fulfil it: which of these two would in thy view be the

[013-131] stronger?'

[013-132] 'I guess thy meaning, but I pray thee let me hear thee more at large.'

[013-133] 'Walking is man's natural motion, is it not?'

[013-134] 'Certainly.'

[013-135] 'Thou dost not doubt, I suppose, that it is natural for the feet to

[013-136] discharge this function?'

[013-137] 'No; surely I do not.'

[013-138] 'Now, if one man who is able to use his feet walks, and another to whom

[013-139] [[nature|the natural]] use of his feet is wanting tries to walk on his hands,

[013-140] which of the two wouldst thou rightly esteem the stronger?'

[013-141] 'Go on,' said I; 'no one can question but that he who has the natural

[013-142] capacity has more strength than he who has it not.'

[013-143] 'Now, the [[good|supreme good]] is set up as the end alike for the bad and for

[013-144] the good; but the good seek it through the natural action of the

[013-145] virtues, whereas the bad try to attain this same good through all manner

[013-146] of concupiscence, which is not the natural way of attaining good. Or

[013-147] dost thou think otherwise?'

[013-148] 'Nay; rather, one further consequence is clear to me: for from my

[013-149] admissions it must needs follow that the good have power, and the bad

[013-150] are impotent.'

[013-151] 'Thou anticipatest rightly, and that as physicians reckon is a sign that

[013-152] nature is set working, and is throwing off the disease. But, since I see

[013-153] thee so ready at [[knowledge|understanding]], I will heap proof on proof. Look how

[013-154] manifest is the extremity of vicious men's weakness; they cannot even

[013-155] reach that goal to which the aim of nature leads and almost constrains

[013-156] them. What if they were left without this mighty, this well-nigh

[013-157] irresistible help of nature's guidance! Consider also how momentous is

[013-158] the powerlessness which incapacitates the wicked. Not light or

[013-159] trivial[L] are the prizes which they contend for, but which they cannot

[013-160] win or hold; nay, their failure concerns the very sum and crown of

[013-161] things. Poor wretches! they fail to compass even that for which they

[013-162] toil day and night. Herein also the strength of the good conspicuously

[013-163] appears. For just as thou wouldst judge him to be the strongest walker

[013-164] whose legs could carry him to a point beyond which no further advance

[013-165] was possible, so must thou needs account him strong in power who so

[013-166] attains the end of his desires that nothing further to be desired lies

[013-167] beyond. Whence follows the obvious conclusion that they who are wicked

[013-168] are seen likewise to be wholly destitute of strength. For why do they

[013-169] forsake virtue and follow vice? Is it from ignorance of what is good?

[013-170] Well, what is more weak and feeble than the blindness of ignorance? Do

[013-171] they know what they ought to follow, but lust drives them aside out of

[013-172] the way? If it be so, they are still frail by reason of their

[013-173] incontinence, for they cannot fight against vice. Or do they knowingly

[013-174] and wilfully forsake the good and turn aside to vice? Why, at this rate,

[013-175] they not only cease to have power, but cease to be at all. For they who

[013-176] forsake the common end of all things that are, they likewise also cease

[013-177] to be at all. Now, to some it may seem strange that we should assert

[013-178] that the bad, who form the greater part of mankind, do not exist. But

[013-179] the fact is so. I do not, indeed, deny that they who are bad are bad,

[013-180] but that they _are_ in an unqualified and absolute sense I deny. Just as

[013-181] we call a corpse a dead man, but cannot call it simply "man," so I would

[013-182] allow the vicious to be bad, but that they _are_ in an absolute sense I

[013-183] cannot allow. That only _is_ which maintains its place and keeps its

[013-184] nature; whatever falls away from this forsakes the existence which is

[013-185] essential to its nature. "But," thou wilt say, "the bad have an

[013-186] ability." Nor do I wish to deny it; only this ability of theirs comes

[013-187] not from strength, but from impotence. For their ability is to do evil,

[013-188] which would have had no efficacy at all if they could have continued in

[013-189] the performance of good. So this ability of theirs proves them still

[013-190] more plainly to have no power. For if, as we concluded just now, evil is

[013-191] nothing, 'tis clear that the wicked can effect nothing, since they are

[013-192] only able to do evil.'

[013-193] ''Tis evident.'

[013-194] 'And that thou mayst understand what is the precise force of this power,

[013-195] we determined, did we not, awhile back, that nothing has more power than

[013-196] supreme good?'

[013-197] 'We did,' said I.

[013-198] 'But that same highest good cannot do evil?'

[013-199] 'Certainly not.'

[013-200] 'Is there anyone, then, who thinks that men are able to do all things?'

[013-201] 'None but a madman.'

[013-202] 'Yet they are able to do evil?'

[013-203] 'Ay; would they could not!'

[013-204] 'Since, then, he who can do only good is omnipotent, while they who can

[013-205] do evil also are not omnipotent, it is manifest that they who can do

[013-206] evil have less power. There is this also: we have shown that all power

[013-207] is to be reckoned among things desirable, and that all desirable things

[013-208] are referred to good as to a kind of consummation of their nature. But

[013-209] the ability to commit crime cannot be referred to the good; therefore it

[013-210] is not a thing to be desired. And yet all power is desirable; it is

[013-211] clear, then, that ability to do evil is not power. From all which

[013-212] considerations appeareth the power of the good, and the indubitable

[013-213] weakness of the bad, and it is clear that [[plato|Plato]]'s judgment was true; the

[013-214] wise alone are able to do what they would, while the wicked follow their

[013-215] own hearts' lust, but can _not_ accomplish what they would. For they go

[013-216] on in their wilfulness fancying they will attain what they wish for in

[013-217] the paths of delight; but they are very far from its attainment, since

[013-218] shameful deeds lead not to happiness.'
