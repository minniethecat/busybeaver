# Section 11

[011-001] 'But, come, see how strictly and incontrovertibly thou mayst prove this

[011-002] our assertion that the supreme Godhead hath fullest possession of the

[011-003] [[good|highest good]].'

[011-004] 'In what way, pray?' said I.

[011-005] 'Do not rashly suppose that He who is the Father of all things hath

[011-006] received that highest good of which He is said to be possessed either

[011-007] from some external source, or hath it as a natural endowment in such

[011-008] sort that thou mightest consider the essence of the happiness possessed,

[011-009] and of the God who possesses it, distinct and different. For if thou

[011-010] deemest it received from without, thou mayst esteem that which gives

[011-011] more excellent than that which has received. But Him we most worthily

[011-012] acknowledge to be the most supremely excellent of all things. If,

[011-013] however, it is in Him by nature, yet is logically distinct, the thought

[011-014] is inconceivable, since we are speaking of God, who is supreme of all

[011-015] things. Who was there to join these distinct essences? Finally, when one

[011-016] thing is different from another, the things so conceived as distinct

[011-017] cannot be identical. Therefore that which of its own nature is distinct

[011-018] from the highest good is not itself the highest good--an impious thought

[011-019] of Him than whom, 'tis plain, nothing can be more excellent. For

[011-020] universally nothing can be better in nature than the source from which

[011-021] it has come; therefore on most true grounds of reason would I conclude

[011-022] that which is the source of all things to be in its own essence the

[011-023] highest good.'

[011-024] 'And most justly,' said I.

[011-025] 'But the highest good has been admitted to be happiness.'

[011-026] 'Yes.'

[011-027] 'Then,' said she, 'it is necessary to acknowledge that God is very

[011-028] happiness.'

[011-029] 'Yes,' said I; 'I cannot gainsay my former admissions, and I see clearly

[011-030] that this is a necessary inference therefrom.'

[011-031] 'Reflect, also,' said she, 'whether the same conclusion is not further

[011-032] confirmed by considering that there cannot be two supreme goods distinct

[011-033] one from the other. For the goods which are different clearly cannot be

[011-034] severally each what the other is: wherefore neither of the two can be

[011-035] perfect, since to either the other is wanting; but since it is not

[011-036] perfect, it cannot manifestly be the supreme good. By no means, then,

[011-037] can goods which are supreme be different one from the other. But we have

[011-038] concluded that both happiness and God are the supreme good; wherefore

[011-039] that which is highest Divinity must also itself necessarily be supreme

[011-040] happiness.'

[011-041] 'No conclusion,' said I, 'could be truer to fact, nor more soundly

[011-042] reasoned out, nor more worthy of God.'

[011-043] 'Then, further,' said she, 'just as geometricians are wont to draw

[011-044] inferences from their demonstrations to which they give the name

[011-045] "deductions," so will I add here a sort of corollary. For since men

[011-046] become happy by the acquisition of happiness, while happiness is very

[011-047] Godship, it is manifest that they become happy by the acquisition of

[011-048] Godship. But as by the acquisition of justice men become just, and wise

[011-049] by the acquisition of wisdom, so by parity of reasoning by acquiring

[011-050] Godship they must of necessity become gods. So every man who is happy is

[011-051] a god; and though in nature God is One only, yet there is nothing to

[011-052] hinder that very many should be gods by participation in that nature.'

[011-053] 'A fair conclusion, and a precious,' said I, 'deduction or corollary, by

[011-054] whichever name thou wilt call it.'

[011-055] 'And yet,' said she, 'not one whit fairer than this which reason

[011-056] persuades us to add.'

[011-057] 'Why, what?' said I.

[011-058] 'Why, seeing happiness has many particulars included under it, should

[011-059] all these be regarded as forming one body of happiness, as it were, made

[011-060] up of various parts, or is there some one of them which forms the full

[011-061] essence of happiness, while all the rest are relative to this?'

[011-062] 'I would thou wouldst unfold the whole matter to me at large.'

[011-063] 'We judge happiness to be good, do we not?'

[011-064] 'Yea, the supreme good.'

[011-065] 'And this superlative applies to all; for this same happiness is

[011-066] adjudged to be the completest independence, the highest power,

[011-067] reverence, renown, and pleasure.'

[011-068] 'What then?'

[011-069] 'Are all these goods--independence, power, and the rest--to be deemed

[011-070] members of happiness, as it were, or are they all relative to good as to

[011-071] their summit and crown?'

[011-072] 'I understand the problem, but I desire to hear how thou wouldst solve

[011-073] it.'

[011-074] 'Well, then, listen to the determination of the matter. Were all these

[011-075] members composing happiness, they would differ severally one from the

[011-076] other. For this is the nature of parts--that by their difference they

[011-077] compose one body. All these, however, have been proved to be the same;

[011-078] therefore they cannot possibly be members, otherwise happiness will seem

[011-079] to be built up out of one member, which cannot be.'

[011-080] 'There can be no doubt as to that,' said I; 'but I am impatient to hear

[011-081] what remains.'

[011-082] 'Why, it is manifest that all the others are relative to the good. For

[011-083] the very reason why independence is sought is that it is judged good,

[011-084] and so power also, because it is believed to be good. The same, too, may

[011-085] be supposed of reverence, of renown, and of pleasant delight. Good,

[011-086] then, is the sum and source of all desirable things. That which has not

[011-087] in itself any good, either in reality or in semblance, can in no wise be

[011-088] desired. Contrariwise, even things which by nature are not good are

[011-089] desired as if they were truly good, if they seem to be so. Whereby it

[011-090] comes to pass that goodness is rightly believed to be the sum and hinge

[011-091] and cause of all things desirable. Now, that for the sake of which

[011-092] anything is desired itself seems to be most wished for. For instance, if

[011-093] anyone wishes to ride for the sake of health, he does not so much wish

[011-094] for the exercise of riding as the benefit of his health. Since, then,

[011-095] all things are sought for the sake of the good, it is not these so much

[011-096] as good itself that is sought by all. But that on account of which all

[011-097] other things are wished for was, we agreed, happiness; wherefore thus

[011-098] also it appears that it is happiness alone which is sought. From all

[011-099] which it is transparently clear that the essence of absolute good and of

[011-100] happiness is one and the same.'

[011-101] 'I cannot see how anyone can dissent from these conclusions.'

[011-102] 'But we have also proved that God and [[happiness|true happiness]] are one and the

[011-103] same.'

[011-104] 'Yes,' said I.

[011-105] 'Then we can safely conclude, also, that God's essence is seated in

[011-106] absolute good, and nowhere else.'

SONG X.

[011-107] THE TRUE LIGHT.

[011-108] Hither come, all ye whose minds

[011-109] Lust with rosy fetters binds--

[011-110] Lust to bondage hard compelling

[011-111] Th' earthy souls that are his dwelling--

[011-112] Here shall be your labour's close;

[011-113] Here your haven of repose.

[011-114] Come, to your one refuge press;

[011-115] Wide it stands to all distress!

[011-116] Not the glint of yellow gold

[011-117] Down bright Hermus' current rolled;

[011-118] Not the Tagus' precious sands,

[011-119] Nor in far-off scorching lands

[011-120] All the radiant gems that hide

[011-121] Under Indus' storied tide--

[011-122] Emerald green and glistering white--

[011-123] Can illume our feeble sight;

[011-124] But they rather leave the mind

[011-125] In its native darkness blind.

[011-126] For the fairest beams they shed

[011-127] In earth's lowest depths were fed;

[011-128] But the splendour that supplies

[011-129] Strength and vigour to the skies,

[011-130] And the universe controls,

[011-131] Shunneth dark and ruined souls.

[011-132] He who once hath seen _this_ light

[011-133] Will not call the sunbeam bright.

[011-134] XI.

[011-135] 'I quite agree,' said I, 'truly all thy reasonings hold admirably

[011-136] together.'

[011-137] Then said she: 'What value wouldst thou put upon the boon shouldst thou

[011-138] come to the knowledge of the absolute good?'

[011-139] 'Oh, an infinite,' said I, 'if only I were so blest as to learn to know

[011-140] God also who is the good.'

[011-141] 'Yet this will I make clear to thee on truest grounds of reason, if only

[011-142] our recent conclusions stand fast.'

[011-143] 'They will.'

[011-144] 'Have we not shown that those things which most men desire are not true

[011-145] and perfect good precisely for this cause--that they differ severally

[011-146] one from another, and, seeing that one is wanting to another, they

[011-147] cannot bestow full and absolute good; but that they become [[truth|the true]] good

[011-148] when they are gathered, as it were, into one form and agency, so that

[011-149] that which is independence is likewise power, reverence, renown, and

[011-150] pleasant delight, and unless they are all one and the same, they have no

[011-151] claim to be counted among things desirable?'

[011-152] 'Yes; this was clearly proved, and cannot in any wise be doubted.'

[011-153] 'Now, when things are far from being good while they are different, but

[011-154] become good as soon as they are one, is it not true that these become

[011-155] good by acquiring unity?'

[011-156] 'It seems so,' said I.

[011-157] 'But dost not thou allow that all which is good is good by participation

[011-158] in goodness?'

[011-159] 'It is.'

[011-160] 'Then, thou must on similar grounds admit that unity and goodness are

[011-161] the same; for when the effects of things in their natural working differ

[011-162] not, their essence is one and the same.'

[011-163] 'There is no denying it.'

[011-164] 'Now, dost thou know,' said she, 'that all which is abides and subsists

[011-165] so long as it continues one, but so soon as it ceases to be one it

[011-166] perishes and falls to pieces?'

[011-167] 'In what way?'

[011-168] 'Why, take animals, for example. When soul and body come together, and

[011-169] continue in one, this is, we say, a living creature; but when this unity

[011-170] is broken by the separation of these two, the creature dies, and is

[011-171] clearly no longer living. The body also, while it remains in one form by

[011-172] the joining together of its members, presents a human appearance; but if

[011-173] the separation and dispersal of the parts break up the body's unity, it

[011-174] ceases to be what it was. And if we extend our survey to all other

[011-175] things, without doubt it will manifestly appear that each several thing

[011-176] subsists while it is one, but when it ceases to be one perishes.'

[011-177] 'Yes; when I consider further, I see it to be even as thou sayest.'

[011-178] 'Well, is there aught,' said she, 'which, in so far as it acts

[011-179] conformably to nature, abandons the wish for life, and desires to come

[011-180] to death and corruption?'

[011-181] 'Looking to living creatures, which have some faults of choice, I find

[011-182] none that, without external compulsion, forego the will to live, and of

[011-183] their own accord hasten to destruction. For every creature diligently

[011-184] pursues the end of self-preservation, and shuns death and destruction!

[011-185] As to herbs and trees, and inanimate things generally, I am altogether

[011-186] in doubt what to think.'

[011-187] 'And yet there is no possibility of question about this either, since

[011-188] thou seest how herbs and trees grow in places suitable for them, where,

[011-189] as far as their nature admits, they cannot quickly wither and die. Some

[011-190] spring up in the plains, others in the mountains; some grow in marshes,

[011-191] others cling to rocks; and others, again, find a fertile soil in the

[011-192] barren sands; and if you try to transplant these elsewhere, they wither

[011-193] away. [[nature|Nature]] gives to each the soil that suits it, and uses her

[011-194] diligence to prevent any of them dying, so long as it is possible for

[011-195] them to continue alive. Why do they all draw their nourishment from

[011-196] roots as from a mouth dipped into the earth, and distribute the strong

[011-197] bark over the pith? Why are all the softer parts like the pith deeply

[011-198] encased within, while the external parts have the strong texture of

[011-199] wood, and outside of all is the bark to resist the weather's

[011-200] inclemency, like a champion stout in endurance? Again, how great is

[011-201] nature's diligence to secure universal propagation by multiplying seed!

[011-202] Who does not know all these to be contrivances, not only for the present

[011-203] maintenance of a species, but for its lasting continuance, generation

[011-204] after generation, for ever? And do not also the things believed

[011-205] inanimate on like grounds of reason seek each what is proper to itself?

[011-206] Why do the flames shoot lightly upward, while the earth presses downward

[011-207] with its weight, if it is not that these motions and situations are

[011-208] suitable to their respective natures? Moreover, each several thing is

[011-209] preserved by that which is agreeable to its nature, even as it is

[011-210] destroyed by things inimical. Things solid like stones resist

[011-211] disintegration by the close adhesion of their parts. Things fluid like

[011-212] air and water yield easily to what divides them, but swiftly flow back

[011-213] and mingle with those parts from which they have been severed, while

[011-214] fire, again, refuses to be cut at all. And we are not now treating of

[011-215] the voluntary motions of an intelligent soul, but of the drift of

[011-216] nature. Even so is it that we digest our food without thinking about it,

[011-217] and draw our breath unconsciously in sleep; nay, even in living

[011-218] creatures the love of life cometh not of conscious will, but from the

[011-219] principles of nature. For oftentimes in the stress of circumstances will

[011-220] chooses the death which nature shrinks from; and contrarily, in spite of

[011-221] natural appetite, will restrains that work of reproduction by which

[011-222] alone the persistence of perishable creatures is maintained. So entirely

[011-223] does this love of self come from drift of nature, not from animal

[011-224] impulse. [[providence|Providence]] has furnished things with this most cogent reason

[011-225] for continuance: they must desire life, so long as it is naturally

[011-226] possible for them to continue living. Wherefore in no way mayst thou

[011-227] doubt but that things naturally aim at continuance of existence, and

[011-228] shun destruction.'
