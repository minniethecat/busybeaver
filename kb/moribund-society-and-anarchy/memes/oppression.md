# Oppression

## The Ubiquity of Oppression

Oppression is not a peripheral concern in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchism — it is the central fact that anarchist theory exists to understand and anarchist practice exists to destroy. Across the twenty-three chapters of *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, the language of oppression appears in nineteen distinct passages spanning twelve chapters, from the historical-evolutionary analysis of the opening to the revolutionary manifesto of the conclusion.

> Everything is linked together in this system which is crushing us. To be animated with good intentions is not enough to obtain the result desired; no amelioration is possible save by the destruction of the system. It is established solely for exploitation and oppression. We do not want to modify exploitation and oppression but to destroy them. [019-001]

This passage — with its twice-repeated pairing of "exploitation and oppression" and its insistence on destruction rather than modification — condenses Grave's entire political program into a single paragraph.

## The Evolutionary Roots of Oppression

Grave traces oppression to the deepest origins of human social organization. In the "struggle for existence" of early humanity, "the stronger oppressing the weaker" was the sole law, a condition that persisted "until superseded by the cunning, until money-value should displace force-value" [002-008]. Oppression thus has an evolutionary history: brute physical force gave way to cunning, which gave way to the impersonal oppression of the money system.

But this history is not destiny. The very fact of evolution means that what has evolved can evolve further. The "instincts of combativeness, of oppression towards the victim," stored up in the brain by past generations, can be overcome through the development of the countervailing instinct of solidarity — "this other and higher vital law, *assistance in struggle*" [004-008].

> All our aspirations are hindered by the present social organization; the mass is always oppressed for the benefit of a small minority which takes more from the collectivity than it gives back. [020-010]

## The Machinery of Oppression

The existing social order is not simply oppressive in its outcomes — it is organized as a system of oppression in its very structure. Grave identifies multiple interlocking mechanisms:

The **State** is the central apparatus: "this machine for oppression called the State" [007-010]. Workers are led to believe "that by changing the men in power they could change the system of exploitation which oppresses us into a system from which welfare and felicity for all would result." This is a "profound error" that "allows the intriguing to lead the workers astray, in pursuit of illusory reforms, incapable of bringing about any change in their situation" [007-009].

The **law** and **magistracy** complete the machinery. The "inviolability and supreme character" with which the magistracy invests itself must be stripped away to reveal "what is really hidden by these — oppression and exploitation of one class by another, fraud and violence elevated into a principle and transformed into 'social institutions'" [009-022].

**Property** itself is the foundation: "the fundamental vice of the social organization being recognized, we shall show by the evidence that in order to destroy criminals we must destroy the social conditions which beget them" — conditions rooted in a "social compact" that "must be distorted by arbitrariness and oppression" [009-016].

## The Reproduction of Oppression

One of Grave's most penetrating insights concerns the self-reproducing character of oppression. Capitalist society is so suffocating that its very oppressions generate resistance:

> Capitalistic society is so paltry, so narrow; large aspirations find themselves so cramped in it; it annihilates so many good intents, so many hopes, crushing and killing so many individualities that cannot stoop to its narrow views, that, could it succeed in stifling the voice of every living Anarchist, its oppressions would raise up new ones, equally implacable. [001-025]

Oppression, in other words, creates its own grave-diggers. But this reproductive cycle can also be co-opted: the ruling class maintains its position precisely by making "people believe themselves exploited and gagged in their own interest" [004-009]. The worker is made to feel that his oppression serves his own security.

## The Paradox of "Social Authority"

Grave identifies a peculiar paradox in the ideology of oppression. "Social authority, while continuing to preach the oppression of the individual for the sake of the collectivity — this dogma has contributed to its maintenance even as much as force has — social authority has had to modify itself, to concede a larger share to individuality" [002-014]. The rhetoric of collective good masks individual oppression, and even the concessions wrung from authority serve to perpetuate the system by making it appear reformable.

This is why Grave is so insistent on the distinction between reform and revolution. "The exploited have made their complaints and lamentations heard; they have struggled without pause or relaxation to obtain concessions from their masters, which might soften their lot." Yet "this condition has always remained the same. Often their masters have been compelled to make room for them in legislative halls; often they have had to grant the reforms demanded... Have the governed been less oppressed, the exploited less squeezed? Has not poverty been as intense as ever, wealth concentrated in the hands of a privileged few, day by day becoming fewer?" [020-004]. Reform changes the personnel and the forms of oppression; it does not end it.

## Liberty Without Oppression

Grave's alternative is not a differently organized oppression but its complete abolition. "The individual has a right to his entire liberty, to the satisfaction of all his needs." But liberty is bounded by the equal liberty of others: "as there exist more than a billion of individuals on the earth, with equal rights if not with equal needs, it follows that all these rights must be satisfied without encroaching on one another; otherwise that would be oppression, which would render the success of the revolution futile" [002-006].

This is the anarchist wager: that a society without authority, without property, without the State can organize itself without replicating the oppression it has overthrown. The revolution succeeds only if it refuses to become what it fights — only if the liberation of each is the condition for the liberation of all.
