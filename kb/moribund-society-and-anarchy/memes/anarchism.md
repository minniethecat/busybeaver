# Anarchism

## Definition

Anarchism, in [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s formulation, is the negation of authority in all its forms. Grave defines anarchy as the rejection of coercive power that "claims to justify its existence by the necessity of defending social institutions — the family, religion, property, etc." (001-001). The anarchist critique extends beyond mere political opposition to encompass all social and economic structures that perpetuate hierarchy and domination.

## Historical Development

### Early Formulations

Grave traces the anarchist idea back through multiple historical phases. He identifies [[francois-rabelais|Rabelais]] as "one of the first who formulated it in its intuitional stage" through his description of the Abbey of Thelemes, though Grave notes that Rabelais limited its application to "a minority of privileged persons" (001-006). During the French Revolution of 1793, [[jacques-roux|Jacques Roux]] and "the madmen" were, in Grave's view, "the ones who… saw most clearly, and sought the best means of turning it to the benefit of the people" (001-007).

### Proudhon and Bakunin

The anarchist idea began to take definite shape with [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]], who positioned anarchy as "the adversary of authority and power" (001-007). However, Grave critiques Proudhon for leaving "in existence, under different names, the administrative machinery which is the very essence of government" (001-007). It was [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]], through the Jurassian Federation in Switzerland, who "propagated the idea of Proudhon — Anarchy the enemy of authority — but developing, enlarging, incarnating it in social demands" (001-007). Grave marks this as "the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement" (001-007).

### Modern Growth

Grave observes that from modest beginnings, anarchist ideas "have radiated through all classes of society" (001-008). He notes that "Art, science, literature, are impregnated with the new ideas and serve as vehicles for them" (001-008). The movement's growth has been global: "Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian — a little everywhere and in all idioms" (001-008).

## Core Principles

### Negation of Authority

The foundational principle of anarchism is the rejection of all coercive institutions. Grave emphasizes that anarchists teach "that not only must the people not be contented with changing the persons in power, with partially modifying the institutions which govern them, but that, before all, the people must destroy that which makes men bad" (001-010). The critique is directed at the institutional basis of society, not at individuals.

### Attack on Private Property

Grave argues that "the basis of society is [[private-property|private property]]" and that "Authority has but one excuse for existence: the defense of capital" (001-010). The anarchist program therefore necessarily includes the abolition of private property as the foundation of economic [[exploitation|exploitation]]. "Bureaucracy, the family, the army, the magistracy, flow directly from private property" (001-010).

### Communism and Anarchy

Grave addresses the apparent tension between [[anarchism|anarchy]] and [[communism]], arguing that they are complementary rather than contradictory: "individuality cannot develop except in the community; the latter cannot exist unless the former evolves freely; and they mutually complement each other" (001-003). He dismisses critics who claim anarchy and communism are incompatible as "dishonest adversaries, little anxious to throw light upon the question" (002-002).

### Solidarity

Grave insists that anarchists "say that society should be based on the strictest solidarity" (002-007). This [[solidarity]] is not imposed by external authority but emerges naturally from mutual need and voluntary cooperation. No anarchist "thinks of subordinating the existence of the individual to the progress of society" (002-004).

## Anarchism's Appeal

Grave attributes the rapid spread of anarchist ideas to their resonance with fundamental human aspirations. Anarchism offers "that ideal of well-being and liberty which [humanity] had scarcely dared to outline in its hopeful dreams" (001-010). The anarchist idea "shelters and draws to it all those who have the feeling of personal dignity, the thirst after the beautiful and true" (001-015). Unlike political movements that offer personal advancement, anarchism "offered no place to personal preoccupations or paltry ambitions" (001-011), which Grave argues is precisely what attracted sincere thinkers while repelling opportunists.

## Repression and Resilience

Grave acknowledges that anarchism has faced severe repression: "Anarchy has its victims — its dead, its imprisoned, its exiled; but it remains alive and strong, the number of its propagandists constantly increasing" (001-014). He asserts that "governmental fury, oppressive measures, the rage of frustrated ambition, may set themselves against the ideas and their propagators; today the opening is made; they can no longer be prevented from making headway" (001-015). The resilience of anarchism, in Grave's analysis, stems from its truth: "the truth can neither be suppressed nor silenced. Sooner or later it will be reckoned with" (001-013).
