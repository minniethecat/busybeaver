# Machinery

## The Paradox of Machinery Under Capitalism

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of machinery is one of the most sustained dialectical arguments in *Moribund Society and Anarchy*. Machinery, he argues, is "a development begotten by all our acquired knowledge transmitted from generation to generation, and which consequently ought to benefit every human being, rendering all lives broader and easier by the fact that it increases their power of production and furnishes them the means of producing much more while working fewer hours." Yet under capitalism, "machinery has brought nothing but an increase of misery and privation to the workers" [005-018]. This is not a failure of machinery itself but proof of the fundamental viciousness of the social organization that controls it.

> For the rest the best proof that there is something fundamentally vicious in the social organization, is that machinery, a development begotten by all our acquired knowledge... has brought nothing but an increase of misery and privation to the workers.

## Machinery and the Reserve Army of Labor

The most immediate effect of machinery under capitalism is the displacement of workers. "The development of machinery has permitted the exploiters to reduce the number of their hands; the unemployed, become more and more numerous, have diminished wages and multiplied periods of enforced idleness" [005-016]. This is not a temporary disruption but a permanent structural feature: "the development of machinery daily increases the number of the unemployed. These, in order to obtain employment, are forced to compete with each other, to work for a lower wage" [019-030].

The mechanism is self-reinforcing. Each new machine throws more workers onto the labor market, which drives down wages through competition, which in turn forces those still employed to accept worse conditions. "The wage-earner in good circumstances," Grave concludes, "tends more and more to become a myth" [005-017].

## The Concentration of Capital

Machinery requires "the concentration of enormous capital and leaves no room for the isolated workman" [005-017]. This structural requirement systematically eliminates the possibility of independent production. "With the concentration of capital, the continuous development of machinery, ever demanding the putting of more and more enormous capital into operation," small-scale cooperative efforts "break to pieces in their hands before they have produced any results" [019-018].

This extends to agriculture as well: "Does not the use of agricultural machines result in giving impetus to the formation of agricultural syndicates and establishing those powerful anonymous companies already dominant in the manufacturing world?" [019-026]. Machinery drives concentration across every sector, and concentration consolidates the power of the capitalist class.

## The Worker Reduced to a Machine

Beyond economic displacement, machinery exerts a dehumanizing effect on those who remain in the workshop. "Today the workingman is no longer anything but a machine, utterly unacquainted with his exploiters most of the time; in this direction, therefore, there has been a moral loss" [020-006]. The pre-industrial artisan who worked at his own pace and knew his employer has been replaced by a worker "harnessed to a machine" whose speed and rhythm are dictated by mechanical imperatives rather than human needs.

The intensification of machine-paced labor has direct physiological consequences. Grave cites evidence that "in the majority of cotton and silk manufactures the excessive strain exacted by machine work (the speed of machinery having been accelerated to an extraordinary degree of late years) appears to be one of the causes of the extreme mortality resulting from pulmonary affections" [019-019]. The machine does not merely impoverish — it kills.

## The Reform Trap

Grave is particularly attentive to the way machinery undermines reformist strategies. His analysis of the eight-hour day movement illustrates this: "the reduction of the working day would have the effect of stimulating the perfection of mechanical appliances and hastening the displacement of the workman of flesh by the workman of iron, which in a well-organized society would be progress, but would be found to be an aggravation of misery to the worker in our present society" [019-017].

He cites empirical evidence from British industry, quoting a factory inspector's report via Marx: "To maintain the quantity of our products, we have had recourse to the usage, on a large scale, of machines which render skilled workmen superfluous. The nine-hour factory law has had the effect of hastening the introduction of machinery" [019-018]. The reform cancels itself: shorter hours induce mechanization, mechanization displaces workers, and the displaced workers' condition is worse than before. "The displacing of the worker by the machine, increase of liability to sickness for those who remain in the workshop, annihilation of the reform — such are the advantages of this blessed reform!" [019-021].

## Machinery as Revolutionary Force

Yet machinery is not only an instrument of oppression. Its development creates objective conditions that push toward revolution. "Crises, enforced idleness, the development of machinery, political complications, all conspire to throw the workers upon the street, and compel them to revolt in order to affirm their right to existence" [022-010]. The very processes that immiserate workers also concentrate them, organize them (however involuntarily), and make the parasitic character of the capitalist class increasingly visible.

Moreover, machinery reveals the fundamental power relation: "the capitalists, with all their capital, all their machinery, could produce nothing without the cooperation of the workers, whilst the latter, by coming to an understanding among themselves and uniting their forces, could produce very well without the assistance of capital" [005-019]. The capitalists own the machines but are useless without the workers who operate them; the workers, once united, could operate the machines themselves.

## The Anarchist Program: Machinery for All

The solution is not to destroy machinery — a Luddite fantasy that Grave would reject — but to reclaim it. "Without doubt the first work of the Anarchists towards making the revolution a success, will be to seize social wealth, to call upon the disinherited to take possession of stores, machinery, and the soil" [001-037]. In a rational society, machinery would fulfill its original promise: reducing necessary labor, increasing abundance, and liberating humanity from toil.

> Oh, truly we should ask nothing better than that the bourgeoisie should themselves understand the odious role they play, give up exploiting the workers, make restitution of their machinery, houses, lands, and mines to the collectivity, which would thereupon organize itself in order to put all these into operation for the benefit of all. [016-012]

Grave adds that this voluntary restitution is impossible — capitalists will never surrender what they have stolen — but the program is clear: machinery, freed from the constraints of private property and profit, becomes a common resource for the liberation of all.
