# War

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], war is not an external catastrophe that occasionally disrupts an otherwise peaceful social order but the concentrated expression of the permanent war that [[capitalism|capitalist society]] [[wage-labor|wages]] against its own members. "Founded upon this struggle between individuals, society makes of every creature the enemy of all others; it provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors which are attributed to the evil nature of man" [010-006]. The war of nation against nation is simply the war of class against class, writ large and draped in patriotic rhetoric.

## War as Social Logic

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most penetrating insight is that war is not a failure of civilization but its logical culmination. The same competitive logic that pits worker against worker in the labor market, capitalist against capitalist in the marketplace, and class against class in the social order, finds its ultimate expression in armed conflict between nations. "This struggle between individuals has the effect of leading the possessors to make war upon each other" [010-007], and "the same war which goes on among the capitalists goes on also among the workers" [010-008].

> Founded upon this struggle between individuals, society makes of every creature the enemy of all others; it provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors which are attributed to the evil nature of man. [010-006]

This is a structural analysis, not a moral one. [[jean-grave|Grave]] is not saying that people are wicked and therefore make war; he is saying that a social system based on competition and private property necessarily produces war, regardless of the intentions of the individuals caught up in it. The problem is not in human nature but in social organization.

## The Class Character of War

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is acutely aware of the class dimension of war. The workers who die on the battlefield are not the ones who benefit from the conflict. War is made by the [[class|ruling class]]es — "if it pleases those who exploit us to make war, the issue of another battle may cause a portion of the territory to be annexed" [011-005] — but fought by the exploited. The patriotism that drives men to kill and die for their country is an ideology manufactured by those who profit from war.

> How can you expect people not to tear each other in pieces, when they must ask themselves how they and theirs are to eat on the morrow if their competitor obtain the place in the workshop which they themselves covet? [010-012]

The desperation that drives workers to enlist — the need to feed their families, the absence of economic alternatives — is itself a product of the system that war serves. The worker who joins [[militarism|the army]] to escape starvation is not choosing war; war has already been chosen for him by the economic logic that made starvation the alternative.

## War and the Anarchist Critique

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of war is inseparable from his critique of authority. The Anarchists are described as "making war upon authority" [003-005] — but this is war metaphorically, not literally. The anarchist [[wage-labor|wages]] war against the principle of war itself, against the institutions that make war inevitable. The remedy is not pacifism within the existing order — which would leave the causes of war intact — but the abolition of the conditions that produce war.

> Denying the necessity of providential men, making war upon authority, and claiming for each individual the right and the duty to act under the pressure of his own impulses only, of submitting to no compulsion but that of his own reason. [003-005]

This passage reveals the strategic inversion at the heart of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s method. The anarchist "makes war upon authority" — uses the language of war to describe the struggle against war. The goal is not to win a war and impose a new order but to abolish the conditions — authority, property, [[government|the state]] — that make war a permanent feature of human existence.
