# Colonialism

## Definition

Colonialism, in [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s analysis, is the extension of [[exploitation|economic exploitation]] and [[authority|political domination]] beyond national borders under the guise of civilization and progress. He defines it as "this hybrid product of patriotism and mercantilism" (014-002), a system that combines nationalist ideology with capitalist economic interests to justify the conquest and exploitation of foreign lands and peoples.

## Grave's Critique

### Economic Motivations

Grave strips away the moral pretensions of colonialism to reveal its economic core. He argues that "no nation is reputed to be a colonizing one save when it has succeeded in getting out of a country the maximum product it is capable of yielding" (014-003). The measure of colonial "success" is purely extractive — how thoroughly a nation can drain a territory of its resources and labor. Grave uses England as the archetypal example: she "knows how to reward her colonies with the prosperity of the people she sends out to rule them" (014-003), a deeply ironic observation about the true beneficiaries of colonial administration.

### National Comparisons

Grave draws a sharp contrast between English and French colonialism, though his critique spares neither. He observes that "in France it is different; we are not colonizers" (014-004), noting that French colonial ventures have been less commercially successful than British ones. However, he warns that "the French bourgeoisie have so well understood this that they have launched headlong into the colonial adventure" (014-006), recognizing that the logic of capital compels all capitalist nations toward imperial expansion regardless of national character.

### The Ideology of Civilization

Grave is scathing toward the rhetoric of the "civilizing mission" used to justify colonial violence. He recounts "how many repugnant actions are naively recounted to you as altogether natural occurrences" (014-009) by colonial apologists. When colonized peoples resist, they are labeled as rebellious savages; when they are killed, their deaths are justified by the supposed benefits of European civilization. Grave dismisses this hypocrisy: "Go to, go to, philanthropists of commerce, civilizers by the sword! Forbear your tirades on the benefits of civilization!" (014-012). What the colonizers call civilization, Grave contends, is merely "the maximum product" extracted from subjugated peoples.

## Connection to Other Institutions

### Patriotism and Colonialism

Grave situates colonialism within the broader framework of [[patriotism|nationalist ideology]]. He promises to "return to this question in a special chapter upon colonization; let us confine ourselves for the moment to the patriotism of the governing classes" (012-018), explicitly linking colonial expansion to the patriotic rhetoric used by ruling classes to mobilize popular support for their economic projects. [[patriotism|Patriotism]] provides the ideological cover for colonial exploitation, transforming economic predation into national glory.

### Race and Colonialism

The question of colonialism, Grave argues, "immediately brings up that of the so-called inferior races" (015-002). The concept of racial hierarchy is an ideological construct designed to justify colonial domination. Grave rejects the notion that any peoples are inherently inferior, arguing that apparent differences in development are the product of historical and environmental conditions, not biological destiny. The colonial powers' treatment of indigenous peoples as inferior is a self-serving rationalization for theft and violence.

### Property and Conquest

Grave traces the connection between domestic [[property]] relations and international conquest: "robbery, pillage, assassination, disguised under the pompous names of conquest and war, were the only means known of increasing one's share" (011-007). The same logic of violent appropriation that established private property within nations operates internationally through colonialism. The "manifest destiny" of civilized nations is merely the extension of domestic property relations to a global scale.

## Grave's Conclusion

For Grave, colonialism is not an aberration of capitalism but its logical extension. The same forces that exploit workers at home inevitably seek new territories to exploit abroad. The anarchist response is to recognize that "our enemy is the master, no matter to what nationality he belongs" (011-010). Colonial liberation cannot be achieved through national independence alone — it requires the abolition of the entire system of [[authority|authoritarian]] exploitation, both domestic and international.
