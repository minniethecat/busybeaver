# Militarism

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[war|militarism]] is defined as a "frightful plague of humanity" — the institution through which the [[class|ruling class]] [[force-and-violence|force]]s the working masses to sacrifice their youth and liberty for the defense of bourgeois privileges [013-002]. It is inseparable from the ideology of patriotism and the construct of "the country," functioning as the coercive armature that makes [[government|the state]]'s claim on individual lives both physical and absolute [013-002].

## The Barracks as a School of Brutalization

A substantial portion of Chapter XIII is devoted to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s first-hand phenomenology of barracks life, which he describes as a systematic process of dehumanization. The recruit is stripped of individuality: "once matriculated you are no longer a man, but an automaton bound to obey the nod and beck of him who commands" [013-006]. Daily existence is governed by endless inspections — of arms, cleanliness, hospital examinations, kitchen inspections, and divisional reviews — each reinforcing the absolute hierarchy of command [013-008][013-009].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] emphasizes that the thousand petty minutiae of regulation are more psychologically crushing than overt [[force-and-violence|violence]]: "what exasperates you most are the thousand and one minutiae of the trade, the meddlings, the annoyances of rule" [013-007]. The requirement to wax the soles of boots, to arrange personal effects according to "illustrative placards" posted on bunk-room walls, represents a deliberate assault on autonomous will [013-007][013-009].

> Ah! One must have sojourned in that hell to understand all that a man of refined sensibilities can suffer there; one must have worn the uniform to know all the vileness and idiocy it engenders. [013-006]

## Critique of Military Virtues

[[jean-grave|Grave]] attacks the bourgeois literary glorification of military life. The "brave old soldier" of romance fiction — the model of loyalty, probity, and self-sacrifice — is exposed as a fabrication [013-004]. It was the introduction of short-term conscription that finally shattered this mythology, when bourgeois youth themselves experienced the "rebuffs and coarseness" of military discipline and "commenced to pull off the mask from the infamy" [013-005].

> The soldier, including the officer, began to make his appearance before the public in his true character — that is to say in the character of an alcoholic brute, an unconscious machine! [013-005]

The "school of equality" that military propagandists celebrate is in reality a school of "equality in brutalization" [013-008]. After three years of this regime, [[jean-grave|Grave]] concludes, men emerge "fit for nothing but policemen and detectives" — the army is "nothing but a school for demoralization; it can produce nothing but spies, drones, and drunkards" [013-012].

## Historical Development of the Warrior Caste

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the origins of [[war|militarism]] to the earliest social stratifications. The warrior caste was "one of the first to be constituted and to impose its authority upon other members of the clan or tribe" [013-002]. Initially a closed caste that recruited from within and conducted wars on its own account, it evolved through successive stages: lords arming peasants during emergencies, the crown attracting enlistees through bounties, and finally — with the rise of the bourgeoisie — the perfection of the system into universal compulsory military service [013-002].

> The bourgeoisie has perfected the system by forcing the workers to devote a certain portion of their youth to the defense of their masters. But since it could not, without some danger, put arms into their hands and say to them: "Protect me in my possessions," it invented the worship of "the country." [013-002]

## Militarism, Patriotism, and the State

What gives [[war|militarism]] its particular virulence in the modern era is its fusion with the ideology of the nation-[[the-state|state]]. The country, its frontiers, and its arbitrary boundaries are exposed as instruments of class rule: "your country exploits us, your frontiers stifle us, your nationalities are strangers to us" [013-015]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] appeals to a universalist alternative: "We are men, citizens of the universe; all men are our brothers; our only enemies are our masters" [013-015].

The chapter closes with a direct warning to conscripts: the barracks is a prison "whence you cannot reissue without being debased, brutalized, corrupted" [013-017]. The preservation of human dignity demands refusal.
