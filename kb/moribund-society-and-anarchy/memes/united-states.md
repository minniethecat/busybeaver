# United States

## Overview

The United States is referenced in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* primarily through the lens of translator Voltairine de Cleyre, who adapted Jean Grave's critique of militarism for an American readership. De Cleyre observed that America, like France before it, was succumbing to the "gigantic stride toward militarism" that Grave's work condemned.

## In Grave's Text

The translator's preface explicitly addresses the American context. De Cleyre notes that she was initially skeptical about the relevance of Grave's anti-militarism chapter to "the unmilitary American public," but the situation had changed dramatically [013-013]. She describes how America's standing army had been "increased fourfold" and workingmen were enlisting "for the abject service of man-killing on foreign soils at the rate of $15.60 per month" [013-013].

Grave himself references the United States in his discussion of colonial brutality. He includes "the conquests in America by Spain" among the histories that undermine any claim to civilizational superiority [488-488]. He also invokes North and South America in his economic analysis, noting that wherever workers achieved higher wages, "articles of consumption have increased proportionately" [866-866].

> ... now that our standing army has been increased fourfold, and military place-hunting is the ambition of the hour... [013-013]

> ... the Anarchists want the abolition of private property, the destruction of authority, complete autonomy of the individual without any restriction. Whether in France, Spain, Italy, England, America, or Australia... [630-630]

The endnotes include a reference to "the present brutal war of the Americans against the Filipinos" as a counterpart to the colonial atrocities Grave describes [864-864].
