# Individualism

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, individualism — specifically what he calls "the most exaggerated individualism" — is the philosophical and economic doctrine that treats human beings as isolated, self-sufficient atoms whose interests are fundamentally separate from and opposed to those of others [002-005]. This conception of the individual is not a description of human nature but a product of specific historical conditions: the rise of private property, the dissolution of feudal bonds, and the competitive structure of [[capitalism|capitalist society]]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] does not reject individuality — he rejects the ideology that severs the individual from the social whole and denies the reality of mutual dependence.

## The Egoist in Modern Society

[[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that modern society produces extreme forms of egoism — "the Delobelles, the Hjalmar Eikdals" — individuals so absorbed in their own interests that they become incapable of recognizing their connection to others [002-005]. These figures are not moral aberrations but logical products of a social system built on "antagonism of interests," in which each person is pitted against every other in the scramble for subsistence [010-006].

> Without meeting any great number of them, it is sometimes enough to meet a single one to disgust one with humanity. [002-005]

The problem is not that some individuals are selfish but that the institutional framework of property and competition systematically rewards selfishness and penalizes solidarity. The "partisans of the most exaggerated individualism" have "ever had the intention of living as parasites upon society" — theirs is not a genuine doctrine of [[liberty|individual freedom]] but a justification for exploitation [002-005].

## Individualism and Solidarity: The Synthesis

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most distinctive contribution to the anarchist treatment of individualism is his dialectical resolution. Egoism (pure self-interest) and altruism (pure self-sacrifice) are each "pushed to its extreme" and in that extreme form "pernicious to the individual and society" alike [002-012]. But "united they are resolved into a third term, which is the [[the-law|law]] of future societies" — a synthesis in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all [002-012].

> Though egoism or altruism, separately, each pushed to its extreme, is pernicious to the individual and society, united they are resolved into a third term, which is the law of future societies. This law is solidarity. [002-012]

This synthesis is not a compromise but a transformation. In a society organized around solidarity rather than competition, the individual does not sacrifice their interests to the collective — they discover that their genuine interests are inseparable from those of others. The opposition between self and society is revealed as an artifact of property relations, not a permanent feature of the human condition.

## The Anarchist Alternative

[[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes anarchist individualism from bourgeois individualism. The anarchist seeks "the uplifting of human dignity and character, independence of the will which makes us resent command" — an individuality that flourishes through [[cooperation|voluntary cooperation]], not through isolation [017-013]. To be free is "to make use of liberty, acting up to the best of its inspirations, rejecting the tutelage of no matter whom" [017-010]. This [[emancipation|freedom]] is not diminished by association — it is realized through it.

The pernicious influence of society "upon the morals of the individual being discovered, it is easy to suppress the bad instincts and develop the good" [010-014]. A society founded on solidarity rather than antagonism produces individuals who are neither atomized egoists nor self-abnegating altruists but fully developed human beings — autonomous yet connected, free yet responsible.
