# Reform

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist framework, reform designates any attempt to ameliorate the conditions of the exploited within the existing structures of [[capitalism|capitalist society]] — shorter hours, higher [[wage-labor|wages]], expanded suffrage, progressive taxation — without abolishing the institutions that generate exploitation: private property, [[government|the state]], and wage labor. [[jean-grave|Grave]] does not dismiss reforms entirely, but he subjects them to a rigorous critique. His central argument is that "there are no true and effective reforms save those which attack the roots of the evil" [020-009]. Palliative reforms that leave the structure intact are at best temporary, at worst a mechanism for defusing r[[progress|evolution]]ary energy.

## The Structural Limits of Reform

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of reform is built on a recognition of the fundamental asymmetry of power in [[capitalism|capitalist society]]. Because "no melioration can be granted to the exploited class without lessening the privileges of the exploiting class," every reform faces the organized resistance of those whose interests it threatens [019-002]. The [[class|ruling class]]es, who control [[government|the state]] apparatus, the economy, and the means of ideological production, possess multiple mechanisms for neutralizing reforms:

First, reforms can be simply blocked or delayed — the legislative process is controlled by the very interests that reform would constrain. Second, when reforms are enacted, employers can compensate by intensifying labor, raising prices, or substituting machinery — so that "the situation returns to the identical place it started from" [019-021]. Third, reforms can be co-opted — stripped of their transformative content and transformed into symbolic concessions that leave the structure of power intact.

> Reforms! Reforms! When will men discover that they have exhausted their best energies upon reforms without ever getting anything but promises. [019-037]

## The Reformist Illusion

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a persistent pattern: the exploited invest their hopes in reforms, struggle to achieve them, and then find that "at the end of their efforts, after the blood shed and lives laid down, they have found themselves confronted by new masters — face to face with the same exploitation" [020-012]. The cycle of reformist hope and disillusionment is not accidental; it is the mechanism by which the system reproduces itself. Each new generation of the exploited is taught to believe that this time, this reform, will make the difference — and each generation discovers the same structural limits.

The reformist position — "let us seek gradually to improve what we have; slowly, indeed, but surely" — is critiqued by [[jean-grave|Grave]] on two grounds [020-003]. First, it offers nothing to "those who suffer, perish, through this so-called security" — for those in im[[press|media]]te need, gradualism is a death sentence. Second, it ignores the historical record: reforms have been tried for centuries without altering the fundamental relations of exploitation. The "plasters" recommended by reformers have never cured the disease because they have never addressed its cause.

## Which Reforms, and for What Purpose?

[[jean-grave|Grave]] does not argue that workers should refuse all reforms. The reduction of working hours, for instance, is a legitimate demand — but it must be understood as a tactical gain in an ongoing struggle, not as a substitute for the abolition of wage labor. The difference lies in whether the reform strengthens the autonomous organizational capacity of the exploited or merely "lulls the intelligence of the workers to sleep by lying promises" [020-014].

A reform that increases the power of workers to organize, to withhold their labor, to resist exploitation — this is not a reformist reform but a r[[progress|evolution]]ary reform, one that pushes against the limits of the system rather than reinforcing them. The metric is not the reform's [[the-state|state]]d intention but its effect on the balance of class [[force-and-violence|force]]s. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchism is not a rejection of all im[[press|media]]te struggle but an insistence that im[[press|media]]te struggles must be oriented toward the abolition of the conditions that make them necessary.
