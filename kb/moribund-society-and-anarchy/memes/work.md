# Work

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] treats work not merely as economic activity but as the central human relationship with nature and society. Under [[capitalism]], work is organized around the imperative "to procure money enough to enable one to buy what he wants" (002-014), reducing the worker to a mere instrument of production. Grave contrasts this with an anarchist vision in which work becomes a freely chosen expression of human creativity, organized through voluntary [[cooperation]] rather than capitalist compulsion.

## Work Under Capitalism

### The Degradation of Labor

Grave observes that under capitalism, "manual labor does not even" provide dignity — the worker is dependent upon "the toil of others to obtain the things necessary for existence" (002-014). The capitalist system, founded on the [[antagonism]] of [[interest|interests]], ensures that work is performed under compulsion rather than as a natural expression of human need. Grave argues that "by its organization, based upon the antagonism of interests, our bourgeois society charges itself with the task of bringing the workers to a [[revolution]]" (003-004).

### The Cooperative Alternative

Grave envisions a society where work is organized on the principle of free [[association]] rather than wage-slavery. He argues that [[anarchism|anarchist]] ideas "have only to analyze this enormous work, to coordinate it, to extract its essence" — the task is to demonstrate "that it is not by changing governors that the ills from which we suffer may be cured" (003-003). The solution lies in workers "coming to an understanding among themselves and uniting their forces" (003-040), recognizing that "the capitalists, with all their capital, all their machinery, could produce nothing without the cooperation of the workers" (003-040).

## Work and the Social Question

Grave links work to the broader social transformation envisioned by [[anarchism]]. He argues that "if the next revolution were to confine its objects to the sole problem of material life, it would greatly risk being arrested on the way" (002-016). True emancipation requires not merely better conditions of work but the complete reorganization of productive activity on voluntary, cooperative lines. The worker, Grave insists, "has a right to the first claim only, permitting him to replace his capital expended; that is to say, to feed, clothe, and lodge himself and finally to repair the waste of his organism" (003-034).
