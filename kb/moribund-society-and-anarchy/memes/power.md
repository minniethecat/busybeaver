# Power

## Definition

Power, in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, is the coercive force that [[authority]] deploys to maintain existing social institutions. [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] defines power as the mechanism through which "authority claims to justify its existence by the necessity of defending social institutions — the family, religion, property, etc. — and it has created a mass of tools to secure it in the exercise of its alleged mission" (001-002). Power is thus inseparable from the institutional structures it protects; it is not a neutral instrument but an inherently oppressive force.

## Power and the State

Grave traces the relationship between power and the [[the-state|state]], arguing that political power concentrates in governmental apparatus that serves the interests of the ruling class. The state's claim to legitimacy rests on the pretense that "power is necessary to reform mankind" (003-012) — a justification Grave systematically dismantles. He observes that authoritarians "base their justification of the power they wish to establish" on "the contention that man is too evil to know how to govern himself" (003-012), a circular argument that uses the effects of power to justify its perpetuation.

## The Critique of Power

### Anarchist Negation

The anarchist response to power is not reform but abolition. Grave asserts that the anarchist program requires attacking "every institution of which power had constituted itself the defender" (001-003). This extends beyond political institutions to encompass [[private-property|property]], the [[the-family|family]], [[religion]], and all structures through which power reproduces itself. The scope of this critique is comprehensive precisely because "the scope of Anarchistic ideas" necessarily encompasses everything power defends (001-003).

### Historical Persistence

Grave notes that despite [[revolution|revolutions]] and reforms, power has proven remarkably resilient. Even in movements that sought liberation, "many of the germs of authoritarianism" and "many of the elements of the authoritarian conception" survived (001-008). The French Revolution, for all its radicalism, replaced one form of power with another. Grave's analysis implies that power cannot be incrementally reformed away — it must be directly confronted and dismantled.

## Power and the People

Grave warns that the working class has historically "forever allowed the benefits thereof to be taken from them by the [[bourgeoisie]]" after revolutions (003-004). The seizure of power by new elites is a recurring pattern that anarchism must break. The task, in Grave's view, is to ensure that when the existing order collapses — and he sees it as hastening toward "a speedy fall" (003-010) after "so short a period of power" — the result is not a new form of domination but genuine liberation.
