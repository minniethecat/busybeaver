# Majority and Minority

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the principle of majority rule — the idea that the numerical majority has the right to impose its will on the minority — is not a democratic safeguard but a mechanism of intellectual and social tyranny. It substitutes the arbitrariness of numbers for the arbitrariness of kings, without altering the fundamental logic of authority: that some are entitled to command and others obliged to obey. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist principle is clear: "a majority, however imposing it may be, cannot have any claim to govern minorities, just as a minority has no more right to impose its will upon the majority" [007-004]. No aggregation of votes can override the sovereignty of each person over their own life. The majority principle is not merely philosophically wrong — it is practically destructive: it "gives birth to the reign of nonentities" and is "a school of cowardice, in which citizens learn to disguise their sentiments" [007-005]. "What matters the choice of the hand that strikes you? It is not to be struck at all, that one should aim at!" [007-013]

## The Quantitative Fallacy

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of majority rule begins with a simple observation: truth is not determined by counting heads. The fact that more people believe something does not make it true, and the fact that fewer believe it does not make it false. Throughout history, every advance in human understanding has begun as a minority view — sometimes a minority of one — opposed by the weight of majority opinion. "That Anarchistic ideas have been able to develop with this energy and rapidity, is because, while running counter to accepted opinions and established prejudices, while frightening, at the first exposure, a goodly number of individuals" — they speak to deeper truths that majority sentiment cannot reach [001-010].

> We do not wish to institute a trial of universal suffrage at this point, nor to examine all the correctives, all the improvements that different peoples have sought to bring to the electoral system; we simply declare that a majority, however imposing it may be, cannot have any claim to govern minorities, just as a minority has no more right to impose its will upon the majority. [007-004]

This is a radical egalitarianism of will: no person or group, regardless of size, has the right to govern another. "The theory of the supernatural origin of man being set aside, the idea that society, such as exists, with its divisions of rich and poor, governed and governing, is the result of a natural and fatal [[progress|evolution]]" — and this [[progress|evolution]] has no moral authority, regardless of how many endorse it [005-007]. The question of "who rules" is the wrong question; the anarchist question is "why must anyone rule?" The substitution of majority for monarch changes the identity of the ruler but not the relationship of ruling.

## The Reign of Mediocrity

More damaging than the philosophical fallacy, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s view, is the practical effect of majority rule on social life. Universal suffrage "gives birth to the reign of nonentities" — it systematically elevates those who offend no one, who promise everything and deliver nothing, who are skilled not at thinking but at pleasing [007-005].

> Yet all this is still of minor importance. Universal suffrage has a still more disastrous effect, viz.: that it gives birth to the reign of nonentities; that it is a school of cowardice, in which citizens learn to disguise their sentiments in order not to shock the majority, to conceal their opinions in order to gain its votes, to deceive it in order to lead it more completely. [007-005]

Majority rule does not merely select the wrong leaders; it trains citizens in dishonesty. Under its regime, "citizens learn to disguise their sentiments in order not to shock the majority, to conceal their opinions in order to gain its votes" [007-005]. It is a mechanism that produces conformity not through explicit censorship but through the structural incentive to pander.

## The Stifling of Individual Initiative

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a deeper harm: majority rule extinguishes the very quality on which [[progress|social progress]] depends — individual initiative. When all decisions are referred to the counting of votes, no one feels responsible for the outcome:

> Universal suffrage is a means of stifling individual initiative which we proclaim, and which we should on the contrary seek to develop by all possible means; for, according to us, it is to the free play of individual initiative that we must look for the evolution of the race. [017-006]

The person who knows their fate will be decided by a distant aggregation of strangers has no incentive to think critically, to experiment boldly, or to take responsibility. The vote becomes a substitute for action — casting a [[suffrage|ballot]] relieves the individual of the burden of actually *doing* anything. This is the pacifying function of electoral democracy: it channels discontent into a symbolic gesture that changes nothing.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] illustrates this through a broader observation about all representative institutions:

> If the workings of all manner of groups be thoroughly examined — committees, representative congresses, associations for mutual help, societies of artists, etc. — the same phenomenon will always be observed: the few leading the mass. [007-006]

The promise of democracy is that the majority governs; the reality is that "the few" always "lead the mass" — majority rule is a fiction that masks minority domination [007-006].

## Anarchist Self-Determination

Against the quantitative logic of majority rule, [[jean-grave|Grave]] proposes a qualitative alternative: each individual decides for themselves, and collective action proceeds only through the free agreement of all concerned. This is not simply minority veto — it is the abolition of the majority-minority framework altogether. If no decision is binding on those who dissent, the distinction between majority and minority loses its political meaning.

This requires a different conception of social decision-making: not the vote but the contract, not the decree but the agreement, not the command but the negotiation. "What an abyss of contradictions is the human mind! If one discusses with individuals even slightly intelligent, they will readily agree that if all men were reasonable there would be no need of [[the-state|government]]" — yet they persist in demanding that someone govern [007-010]. When association is voluntary, the only decisions that can be implemented are those to which all affected parties consent. The "minority" is not overruled; it simply does not participate, and the association adjusts or dissolves accordingly.

The anarchist alternative is not a better [[suffrage|voting]] system but a different mode of social coordination altogether: "Whatever the name in which the new authority clothes itself, however democratic it may proclaim itself, it will be no less authority — that is to say, the right of some to command others" [007-013]. The demand is not for fairer counting but for the abolition of the count.

> What matters the choice of the hand that strikes you? It is not to be struck at all, that one should aim at! [007-013]
