# Equality

Equality in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist framework is not the formal equality of bourgeois democracy — the equal right to vote, to own property, to make contracts — but a substantive condition in which "all these rights must be satisfied without encroaching on one another; otherwise that would be oppression, which would render the success of the revolution futile" [001-019].

## Beyond Formal Equality

Grave's starting point is the recognition that formal equality under law coexists with — and indeed legitimates — the most extreme material inequality. "The individual has a right to his entire liberty, to the satisfaction of all his needs; that is understood. Only, as there exist more than a billion of individuals on the earth, with equal rights if not with equal needs, it follows that all these rights must be satisfied without encroaching on one another" [001-019].

This is not a call for uniformity. Grave recognizes that needs vary: individuals have "equal rights if not with equal needs." The goal is not to make everyone the same but to ensure that every person's needs can be met without anyone's satisfaction coming at the expense of another's deprivation. This is the principle of [[solidarity]] applied to the distribution of social goods.

## The Egoist and the Community

Grave addresses the objection that radical equality would extinguish individuality. He critiques "the perfect egoist" — the person "who think only of themselves, who see nothing in life but their own persons" — not by preaching self-sacrifice but by showing that such egoism is self-defeating [001-018].

"We do not believe that the sincere partisans of the most exaggerated individualism have ever had the intention of giving us this type as the ideal of future humanity; no more than the Communist-Anarchists have meant to preach abnegation and renunciation for the individual in the society which they anticipate" [001-018]. The false opposition between individualism and communism is precisely what anarchism overcomes: true individual flourishing requires the abolition of the conditions that force individuals into competition.

"Man is always egoistic, he always tends to make of his ego the centre of the universe. But with the development of his intelligence he comes to understand that if his ego wishes to be satisfied there are other egos that equally wish to be satisfied" [001-021]. Enlightened self-interest, not self-abnegation, is the basis of anarchist equality.

## Equality and the Critique of State "Justice"

The pretense of equality before the law is exposed in Grave's critique of the magistracy. The "implacable men that hurl your anathema against the accused whom public force has brought before your bar" have never asked themselves "whether, if placed in the same circumstances and surroundings under which this man acted, you would not have done worse" [006-006].

Social inequality — produced by the division of society "into a number of castes, which may be resolved into two principle ones: governors and possessors on the one side, the governed and non-possessors on the other" — makes a mockery of legal equality [008-008]. "On the side of the first contentment and plethora; on the side of the second misery, privation, and anaemia" [008-008]. No law can be just when the social conditions it presumes to adjudicate are themselves unjust.

## Equality Through the Abolition of Property

For Grave, substantive equality cannot be achieved through redistribution within the existing system. It requires the abolition of [[private-property]] — the institution that enables "a handful of persons to enslave their fellows" [006-006]. Once private property is abolished, "universal harmony will result from the free action of all men" [006-006].

This is the anarchist conception of equality: not equality of outcome imposed by a state, nor equality of opportunity within a competitive market, but the condition in which no person can use ownership to dominate another. Each person's needs are met through voluntary [[cooperation]] and [[solidarity]], and no one's satisfaction comes at the expense of another's.
