# Mikhail Bakunin

## Biography

Mikhail Alexandrovich Bakunin (1814–1876) was a Russian revolutionary and one of the most influential figures in the history of anarchism. Born into a landowning noble family, Bakunin abandoned a [[militarism|military]] career to pursue revolutionary politics, participating in the 1848 revolutions across Europe. His break with Karl Marx at the [[first-international|First International]] — the famous Hague Congress of 1872 — crystallized the fundamental division between anarchism and [[the-state|state]] socialism: Bakunin insisted that the [[class|working class]] could not liberate itself by seizing [[the-state|state]] power but must abolish [[government|the state]] itself. His major works include *God and the State* (1882) and *Statism and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* (1873).

## Bakunin and the First International

Bakunin's involvement with the [[first-international|International Workingmen's Association]] (the First International) marked a decisive turning point in the history of the socialist movement. While Bakunin initially admired Marx and translated *Das Kapital* into Russian, their strategic divergence grew irreconcilable. Marx advocated for the working class to capture [[the-state|state]] power through political parties and parliamentary means; Bakunin countered that the [[the-state|state]] could never be an instrument of liberation — it must be smashed entirely.

The conflict came to a head at the Hague Congress of 1872, where Marx's supporters, holding a slim majority, expelled Bakunin and his allies from the International. Bakunin's faction, centered in the [[jurassian-federation|Jurassian Federation]] of watchmakers and artisans in the Swiss Jura mountains, rejected the expulsion and formed an anti-authoritarian International that continued until 1877. As Grave recounts, it was in the Jurassian Federation that "Bakunin propagated the idea of Proudhon — Anarchy the enemy of [[authority]] — but developing, enlarging, incarnating it in social demands. From this epoch dates the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement" [001-007].

> The Bakuninists, those who, as we have seen, formed, in the International, a fraction known under the name of the Jurassian Federation, were the ones that understood that to be revolutionary it was necessary to accept the consequences of the revolutionary idea, — the anarchist idea. [001-007]

Bakunin's core objection to Marx's strategy was both practical and philosophical. He foresaw that a "dictatorship of the proletariat" would not wither away but would consolidate into a new ruling class — a "Red bureaucracy" exercising control over both production and political life. The anarchist critique of state socialism, which Grave later systematized in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, originates in this Bakuninist insight: that the means of revolution determine its ends, and that authoritarian methods can never produce a libertarian society. As Grave puts it, "nothing more urgent than the stomach question! Only it would be dangerous to the success of the revolution to stop there, for then one might just as well accept the Socialistic State, which could, and would, secure all in the satisfaction of their physical needs" [001-008].

The split at the Hague Congress permanently divided the European labor movement into two streams: social democratic and parliamentary on one side, anarchist and revolutionary on the other. This schism shaped working-class politics across France, Spain, Italy, and Switzerland for the next half-century, and its reverberations are still felt in debates about [[the-state|state]] power, [[reform]], and [[revolution]].

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

Grave references Bakunin implicitly through his sustained critique of authoritarian socialism and his insistence on the inseparability of means and ends in revolutionary struggle. Grave's argument — that the emancipation of the [[class|working class]] cannot be achieved by transferring power from one ruling group to another but requires the complete abolition of [[government|the state]] — is fundamentally Bakuninist [001-007]. The anti-statist, anti-parliamentary orientation of *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* reflects Bakunin's lasting influence on [[france|French]] anarchist thought.

Grave traces the intellectual lineage of anarchism through Bakunin to [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]], noting that it was Bakunin who "propagated the idea of Proudhon — Anarchy the enemy of authority — but developing, enlarging, incarnating it in social demands" [001-007]. This transmission — from Proudhon's philosophical critique through Bakunin's revolutionary practice to Grave's systematic exposition — forms the backbone of the anarchist communist tradition.

## Influence on Anarchism

Bakunin's collectivist anarchism — which proposed the collective ownership of the means of production by [[association|voluntary association]]s of producers — provided the bridge between [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]]'s individualist mutualism and the communist anarchism of [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]]. His emphasis on the revolutionary potential of the peasantry and the "lumpenproletariat," his critique of the "dictatorship of the proletariat," and his vision of a federalist, decentralized social order shaped the anarchist movement's political strategy for generations.

Bakunin's most enduring contribution was perhaps his insistence on the inseparability of means and ends. He understood that authoritarian methods of struggle — vanguard parties, centralized conspiracies, transitional dictatorships — would reproduce authoritarian outcomes regardless of the liberatory intentions of those who wielded them. This conviction, which Grave inherits and develops, is the philosophical foundation of anarchism's rejection of all forms of [[the-state|state]] power, including those claimed to be provisional or transitional.

## Bakunin's Revolutionary Philosophy

Bakunin's political thought rests on three interconnected pillars: anti-statism, federalism, and collectivism. His anti-statism was absolute — he held that all forms of [[government|the state]], regardless of their ideological coloring, inherently concentrate power in the hands of a minority and suppress the initiative of the majority. This principle leads directly to the anarchist rejection of parliamentary politics and universal suffrage, a stance Grave rigorously defends: "universal suffrage is a means of stifling individual initiative which we proclaim, and which we should on the contrary seek to develop with all our might. Suffrage is an instrument of [[authority]], and what we are after is the complete enfranchisement of human individuality" [001-017].

Bakunin's federalism proposed that society should be organized from the bottom up through [[free-association|free association]]s of producers, communes, and federations, each retaining its autonomy while cooperating voluntarily for common purposes. No central [[authority]] would impose decisions; coordination would emerge through mutual agreement and the free play of individual and collective initiative. Grave echoes this vision in his insistence that "in Anarchy those who know teach those who do not know; the first to conceive an idea puts it into practice, explaining it to those whom he wishes to interest in it. But there is no temporary abdication, no [[authority]]; there are only equals who mutually aid each other according to their respective faculties, abandoning none of their rights, no part of their autonomy" [001-017].

Bakunin's collectivism distinguished him from both [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]] and [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]]. Like [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]], Bakunin believed that the means of production should be collectivized rather than individualized; unlike him, Bakunin insisted that land, factories, and instruments of labor should be held in common. Yet Bakunin stopped short of full communism: he argued that each worker should be remunerated according to labor contributed — "from each according to his faculties, to each according to his deeds" — rather than according to need. This intermediate position created the theoretical bridge between mutualism and [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]]'s communist anarchism.

Crucially, Bakunin rejected the reformist gradualism that characterized both liberal and social democratic politics. He understood that the possessing classes would never voluntarily surrender their [[private-property|privilege]]s, and that only a [[revolution]] — a complete rupture with existing institutions — could clear the ground for a new social order. Grave develops this Bakuninist conviction at length, arguing that reforms are "but snares to deceive the exploited, and that, to prevent the evils we wish to cure, we must attack their principal source, the present proprietary and capitalistic organization" [001-005]. The choice, in Bakunin's framework and Grave's application, is stark: either the workers continue to submit to exploitation, or they revolt and destroy the institutional machinery — the [[the-law|law]], the [[militarism|army]], the magistracy, the bureaucracy — that makes exploitation possible.

Bakunin also anticipated Grave's psychological argument about the corrupting effect of [[authority]]. Power does not merely enable exploitation; it transforms those who wield it into oppressors, regardless of their original intentions. This is why Bakunin was so hostile to the idea of a revolutionary vanguard or transitional state: he recognized that the habits of command and obedience, once established, reproduce themselves in every new ruling stratum. Grave's observation that "in the political and authoritarian Socialist parties an ambitious person can bring about his 'conversion' by insensible degrees" [001-007] is a direct inheritance from Bakunin's warnings about the seductions of power.

## Bakunin vs Marx: The Core Disagreement

The Bakunin-Marx split was not a personal rivalry but a fundamental disagreement about the nature of power and the path to liberation. Marx believed that the state, once captured by the working class, could be used to dismantle [[class|class rule]] and eventually "wither away." Bakunin argued that this was a dangerous fantasy: the state is not a neutral tool but a structure that inherently generates hierarchy and domination. Those who wield state power, whatever their intentions, become a new ruling class.

Grave restates this Bakuninist position in his critique of state socialism. He warns that even well-intentioned revolutionaries who seek to use the state as an instrument of liberation will find themselves corrupted by the very power they exercise. "Nothing more urgent than the stomach question!" he concedes, but "it would be dangerous to the success of the revolution to stop there, for then one might just as well accept the Socialistic State" [001-008]. The anarchist revolution must go further: it must dismantle not only the economic privileges of the [[bourgeoisie]] but the political structures — the state, the bureaucracy, the standing army — through which those privileges are maintained and through which new [[authority|authorities]] would inevitably arise.

> The political and authoritarian Socialists, who want the conquest of political power, know very well that the State being the organization of the exploitation of the majority by a minority, the political machinery will, under the name of the Socialistic State, stay what it is to-day — exploitation of the majority by a minority composed of the leaders of the new party. [001-007]

## Bakunin's Legacy in French Anarchism

Bakunin's influence on [[france|French]] anarchism was mediated primarily through the Jurassian Federation and the personal connections forged during his years in Switzerland. After the suppression of the [[paris-commune|Paris Commune]] in 1871, many exiled French revolutionaries found refuge in the Jura, where they absorbed Bakunin's anti-authoritarian communism and carried it back to France. This transmission shaped the development of French anarchism from its earliest organized expressions through the syndicalist movement of the early twentieth century.

[[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] stands as one of the most significant conduits of Bakuninist thought in France. Though Grave eventually adopted [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]]'s communist anarchism rather than Bakunin's collectivism, his political method remained profoundly Bakuninist. The strategic framework of *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* — relentless critique of all existing institutions, refusal of parliamentary compromise, insistence that emancipation must be the work of the exploited themselves — traces directly to Bakunin's teachings. Grave's declaration that the Anarchist movement "offered no place to personal preoccupations or paltry ambitions, and could in no way serve as a stepping-stone to those who see, in the demands of the workers, nothing but a means of carving themselves a position among the exploiters" [001-007] restates Bakunin's warning against the professionalization of revolutionary politics.

The Bakuninist lineage in France was not limited to Grave. Through newspapers like *Le Revolte* (later *La Revolte* and *Les Temps Nouveaux*) and *Le Pere Peinard*, through the tireless organizing of figures such as Grave, Emile Pouget, and Sebastien Faure, Bakunin's ideas about [[free-association|free association]], direct action, and the general strike became embedded in the practices of the French labor movement. The revolutionary syndicalism of the Confederation Generale du Travail (CGT) — with its rejection of political parties, its emphasis on workers' self-organization, and its commitment to the general strike as the lever of social transformation — represented the practical application of Bakuninist federalism on a mass scale.

Bakunin's legacy in France is perhaps most clearly visible in the anarchist movement's consistent refusal to participate in electoral politics. While German social democracy marched toward parliamentarism and the conquest of the state, French anarchism — shaped by Bakunin's critique of the Hague Congress — maintained its insurrectionary and anti-statist orientation well into the twentieth century. The anarchist position that "the surest means of making Anarchy triumph is to act like an Anarchist" [001-017] is a direct descendant of Bakunin's conviction that the new world must be built within the shell of the old, through the practice of liberty rather than the seizure of power.
