# Bernard Lazare

## Biography

Bernard Lazare (1865–1903) was a [[france|French]] Jewish anarchist journalist, literary critic, and polemicist. Born Lazare Marcus Manassé Bernard in Nîmes, he moved to Paris where he became active in symbolist literary circles before gravitating toward anarchist politics. His 1894 book *Antisemitism: Its History and Causes* offered one of the first systematic critiques of antisemitism from an anarchist perspective. When Alfred Dreyfus was wrongly convicted of treason in 1894, Lazare was among the first public intellectuals to take up his defense, publishing *A Judicial Error: The Truth [[About]] the [[dreyfus-affair|Dreyfus Affair]]* (1896) and working tirelessly to expose the [[militarism|military]]'s frame-up.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

Lazare was a contemporary and colleague of [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] in the [[france|French]] anarchist movement. Though not directly mentioned in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, Lazare's intellectual concerns — the critique of authority, the defense of the oppressed, the exposure of [[the-state|state]] injustice — align closely with [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s themes. Lazare's involvement in the [[dreyfus-affair|Dreyfus Affair]] exemplified the anarchist principle of defending the persecuted against the machinery of [[government|the state]], regardless of the victim's identity.

## Influence on Anarchism

Lazare's significance for anarchism lies in his demonstration that the anarchist commitment to justice is universal — that it extends to all victims of oppression, whether they share the anarchist's political commitments or not. His work on antisemitism also anticipated later anarchist engagements with questions of race, ethnicity, and identity, showing that these axes of oppression are not separate from [[exploitation|class exploitation]] but intertwined with it.
