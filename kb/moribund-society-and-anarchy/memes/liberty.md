# Liberty

## Definition

Liberty occupies a foundational position in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist philosophy: it is simultaneously the premise from which all anarchist argumentation proceeds and the end toward which all anarchist action aims. [[jean-grave|Grave]] defines liberty not as a legal permission or a zone of non-interference granted by authority, but as an inalienable condition of human flourishing — "complete [[emancipation|freedom]] in all their modes of action" is the natural entitlement of every individual [002-004]. This maximalist conception distinguishes anarchist liberty from liberal or republican formulations that circumscribe [[emancipation|freedom]] within the bounds of [[the-law|law]] and [[the-state|state]].

## Liberty and the Individual

The individual's claim to liberty is absolute and pre-political. "The individual has a right to his entire liberty, to the satisfaction of all his needs" — a formulation that links liberty directly to material well-being [002-006]. Freedom is not merely the absence of [[force-and-violence|coercion]] but the positive capacity to act, to create, to satisfy one's needs through one's own efforts in association with others.

> What we demand is the freedom of the people, complete freedom in all their modes of action, liberty without any restriction. [002-004]

This insistence on unrestricted liberty places [[jean-grave|Grave]] firmly in the tradition of anarchist individualism, yet he does not fall into the trap of atomism. Liberty is exercised in society, through association, and its realization depends on the equal liberty of others — a dynamic relationship explored more fully in the dialectic between [[individualism]] and [[solidarity]].

## Liberty Against the State

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], every form of [[the-state|state]] authority represents a negation of liberty. The [[the-state|state]] does not grant [[emancipation|freedom]]; it consumes it. "Hence renunciation of liberty — or people will remain free to dispose of themselves" — this stark choice structures the entire anarchist critique of [[the-state|government]] [007-013]. The institutions of [[government|the state]] — [[the-law|law]], magistracy, police, army — are not protectors of [[emancipation|freedom]] but its systematic suppressors.

The historical record, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s reading, confirms this antagonism. The [[emancipation|freedom]] of the medieval communes was wrested from the baronage through struggle, not granted by benevolent lords [003-009]. Each extension of liberty has been won against the resistance of authority, and each generation must defend and extend these gains.

> They who think they can be really free only when their liberty ceases to harm that of others — this is the sophism of all authoritarians. [022-008]

## The Material Conditions of Liberty

[[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that liberty cannot be reduced to a purely formal or juridical category. A person who "does not know whether he will have work tomorrow" is not free, regardless of the constitutional guarantees they nominally enjoy [010-014]. Genuine liberty requires the abolition of economic dependency — the condition in which one person's subsistence depends on the will of another.

The "influences of environment, education, or heredity" that shape human character are not obstacles to liberty that can be overcome through willpower alone; they require transformation of the social conditions themselves [002-015]. Liberty is therefore inseparable from the broader anarchist project of abolishing private property, the [[wage-labor|wage system]], and all institutions of hierarchical authority.
