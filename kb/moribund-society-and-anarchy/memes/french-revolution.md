# French Revolution

## Overview

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was a period of profound social and political transformation that dismantled the ancien régime, abolished feudal privileges, and established — however briefly — the first [[france|French Republic]]. Beyond the familiar narrative of 1789, the [[revolution|Revolution]] contained within it a more radical egalitarian current: the sans-culottes who demanded bread as well as liberty, the Enragés who denounced the National Convention for betraying the poor, and the conspirators who imagined a society without masters. It is this radical undercurrent that [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] recovers as part of anarchism's prehistory.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes careful attention to the radical democratic and egalitarian dimensions of the [[revolution|Revolution]], arguing that the anarchist tradition has deeper roots than its nineteenth-century formulation would suggest. He writes:

> In 1793 the Anarchists were much talked of. Jacques Roux and "the madmen" appear to us to have been the ones who, during the Revolution, saw most clearly, and sought the best means of turning it to the benefit of the people. Hence the bourgeois historians have revenged themselves for this by effacing them as much as possible from the pages of history. [001-007]

This historical recovery serves a polemical purpose: by demonstrating that anarchist ideas emerged within the [[revolution|Revolution]] itself, [[jean-grave|Grave]] challenges the conservative claim that demands for radical equality are modern aberrations. The [[revolution|Revolution]], in his reading, contained a libertarian potential that was crushed — not by counter-r[[progress|evolution]]aries, but by the r[[progress|evolution]]ary [[the-state|government]] itself.

## Significance for Anarchism

The French Revolution's significance for anarchism lies not in its liberal achievements — the Declaration of the Rights of Man, the abolition of feudal dues — but in the radical egalitarian tradition it spawned and then suppressed. The fate of [[jacques-roux|Jacques Roux]], the Enragés, and Babeuf's Conspiracy of Equals demonstrated that political r[[progress|evolution]] without economic transformation merely replaces one [[class|ruling class]] with another. This lesson became foundational for the anarchist distinction between political and social r[[progress|evolution]].
