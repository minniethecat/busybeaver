---

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] identifies hierarchy as one of the fundamental structural principles of authoritarian society. Alongside [[authority]], [[the-family|the family]], the magistracy, the army, "and every hierarchic and bureaucratic organization, which stifles and devours us," hierarchy proceeds from the same principle — "the right of property pushed to its logical conclusion" [005-003]. For [[jean-grave|Grave]], hierarchy is not a natural ordering of human relations but a constructed system of domination, disguised by the ideology of merit and natural inequality.

Crucially, [[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a sharp distinction between the observable interdependence of living beings and the social institution of hierarchy. The food chain — "the vegetable sustains itself at the expense of the mineral and the atmosphere, the animal at the expense of the vegetable" — is not evidence of a designed ranking among creatures. There are "no preconceived ideas in this, having in view the establishment of any hierarchy among beings, on the part of a creator, or of a nature-entity, who should have created the vegetable to serve as food for the animal, the animal and vegetable to feed man or to be slaves to the human race in order to create the happiness of the elect" [005-004]. What exists in nature is an "evolving sequence of natural laws," not a ladder of dominion warranting human social ranking. By severing the supposed natural foundation of hierarchy, [[jean-grave|Grave]] exposes it as a human construction — and therefore a human choice, capable of being unmade.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of hierarchy reflects the anarchist engagement with the social organization of the [[france|French]] Third Republic, where a formal rhetoric of equality and meritocracy coexisted with a deeply hierarchical class structure. The army, the bureaucracy, and the educational system all served as mechanisms for reproducing hierarchy while claiming to reward individual merit — a contradiction that [[jean-grave|Grave]] exposes throughout *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*.

The historical depth of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is evident in his account of how hierarchy evolved from primitive instincts into institutionalized caste structures. "The struggle for existence" having begun as "the sole law for individuals," the "instincts of combativeness, of oppression towards the victim" were stored up "in the brain by past generations" and "sought to gain like precedence in the organized collectivity" [005-005]. Those who possessed these instincts "in the highest degree imposed their will upon those who possessed it in a lesser degree" [005-005]. This evolutionary account refuses hierarchy any permanent or divine justification: hierarchy is a contingent product of human history, not a necessary feature of human association. And because it was made, it can be unmade — "that which force alone sustains force can destroy" [005-003].

## Grave's Analysis---

tags: ["authority", "social-organization", "class", "power"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of hierarchy centers on its function as a mechanism of social control. Society has been made into "a hierarchy, with the top of the social scale (considered as a reward for merit, intelligence, and industry) reserved precisely for those who have never known what it is to earn their living by the sweat of their brow" [010-010]. The claim that hierarchy rewards merit is exposed as ideology: those at the top have not earned their position through labor or talent but have inherited or seized it through the operations of [[private-property|property]] and [[authority]].

> You have made society a hierarchy, with the top of the social scale (considered as a reward for merit, intelligence, and industry) reserved precisely for those who have never done anything! Those who by one means or another have succeeded in perching on the summit, eat, drink, and wanton, without the slightest employment for their ten fingers. [010-010]

The hierarchical organization of society does not merely distribute rewards unequally — it actively deforms those subjected to it. The military hierarchy, for example, "after having ground him for three years in the multiple gearings of its hierarchy, it will give you back a shapeless rag, if it have not completely depraved its man" [013-013]. Hierarchy grinds down individuality, replacing independent judgment with obedience and initiative with submission. [[jean-grave|Grave]] describes this as a "horrible machine for brutalization" that "breaks a man utterly, crushes his spirit, deforms his character, destroys his will" [013-013].

### Hierarchy and the Social Scale

The metaphor of the "social scale" or "ladder" is central to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis. He describes a society organized as a vertical ranking in which the mechanism of labor exploitation and the mechanism of status are fused. "The greater the number of slaves a person manages to exploit, the higher his situation and the more respect he receives; the greater, likewise, the amount of income he gets out of it" [010-010]. Position on the ladder is not earned through contribution but through successful predation — those who extract the most from others occupy the top.

This has a double effect on those at the bottom. First, work itself is degraded by its association with subordination: "work is considered degrading, reserved for the Pariahs of society" [010-010]. Second, the social scale sets up a structure of incentives that rewards the renunciation of productive activity. The "ideal, the goal of attainment for everybody who wants to rise in the world is to succeed in amassing, by no matter what means, money enough to live without doing anything or by making others work" [010-010]. The hierarchy does not merely rank people — it inverts the very values by which contribution might be measured, placing the idle above the productive and the exploiter above the worker.

At the bottom of this scale, those who produce are locked into a competition that prevents them from recognizing their common interest. "Forced to struggle among themselves in order to snatch the vacancies in these dungeons which the capitalists offer them, the workers regard each other as so many enemies while they are led to consider him who exploits them as a benefactor" [010-009]. Hierarchy thus functions as a strategy of division — the vertical ordering of society is sustained by compelling those at the bottom to fight one another for position rather than combining against their common subordination.

## Hierarchy and Bureaucracy---

type: concept

tags: ["authority", "social-organization", "class", "power"]

---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends his critique to bureaucratic and representative organizations, observing that "if the workings of all manner of groups be thoroughly examined — committees, representative congresses, associations for mutual help, societies of artisans, etc. — the same phenomenon may be observed" [007-006]. Even organizations created for mutual benefit tend to reproduce hierarchical structures, as power concentrates in the hands of a few who come to govern in their own interests rather than those of the membership.

This analysis anticipates later anarchist critiques of organizational oligarchy, including [[robert-michels|Robert Michels]]'s "iron law of oligarchy." [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s solution is not to abandon organization but to insist on forms of [[free-association|association]] that resist the gravitational pull toward hierarchy — associations "having nothing forced in it, nothing arbitrary, prompted only by some need of our being" [002-013].

Crucially, [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies an intimate link between hierarchy and mediocrity. In organizations governed by [[universal-suffrage|universal suffrage]] and hierarchical structure, "the offices in these hierarchic organizations, elected by universal suffrage, held by persons who, setting aside their ambitious desire of showing themselves off, getting themselves talked about, or creating a situation at the expense of their colleagues, and a certain capacity for intrigue, are the most mediocre of the lot" [007-006]. The hierarchical mechanism does not select for competence or vision — it selects for the capacity to offend no one and to manipulate the machinery of election. "The more flat, mediocre, and insipid he is, the more chance he has of being elected" [007-006]. The person of original ideas "who seeks the truth and would make it prevail, has no time to stoop to the shabby wire-pulling behind the scenes" and is inevitably defeated by those who accept received opinions and avoid challenging the status quo [007-006].

This convergence of hierarchy and mediocrity is not accidental. Hierarchy requires conformity — it cannot tolerate the independent judgment that original thought demands. The hierarchic organization, whether in government, the workplace, or the voluntary association, tends to elevate those who will preserve the structure rather than those who might transform it.

## Hierarchy, Militarism, and Caste---

tags: ["authority", "social-organization", "class", "power"]
quality: basic
---

The military is the paradigmatic instance of hierarchy for [[jean-grave|Grave]]. In the evolution of authoritarian institutions, "the military caste was not the last to form, develop, and become preponderant everywhere" [011-001]. Where it yielded the "foremost rank" to the sacerdotal caste, this was "merely an honorary precedence," since it was the military caste that ensured "stability of power in the hands of those who held that power" [011-001]. Hierarchy, in this analysis, is inseparable from force — it is the organizational form through which armed coercion is projected into social relations.

The military hierarchy not only enforces the existing order externally but functions internally as an engine of degradation. [[jean-grave|Grave]] describes a "brutal and abject discipline, which breaks a man utterly, crushes his spirit, deforms his character, destroys his will" [013-013]. The hierarchical structure of the barracks — with its system of ranks, its rituals of submission, its cultivation of "military virtues" that demand the soldier be "nothing more than killing machines, passive brutes" [013-014] — represents hierarchy at its most concentrated and destructive. Whatever the soldier's rank, the system demands the suppression of personal dignity: "If some drunken veteran is pleased to insult you, and if he have stripes upon his sleeves, take care to hide the jerk which in spite of you will twitch your muscle under the insult; the hand which you have lifted to strike the insulter in the face — carry it with military precision to your cap and salute" [013-014].

Moreover, the hierarchy of military rank serves a specific class function. It furnishes the [[bourgeoisie]] with "the chance to bestow rank, honors, and emoluments upon those of your relations incapable of performing more elevated functions, the aforesaid ranks and emoluments serving at the same time to stimulate the unhealthy ambitions of those who abandon the class whence they sprang to become your convict-keepers" [013-014]. Hierarchy is not merely an organizational form but a reward structure — the distribution of rank binds individuals to the system by offering them places within it, turning potential rebels into its defenders.

## Anti-Hierarchy and Anarchism---

type: concept
label: "Hierarchy"

quality: basic
---

The rejection of hierarchy is constitutive of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s [[anarchy|anarchism]]. If hierarchy proceeds from [[private-property|property]], then the abolition of property is the abolition of the material basis of hierarchy. If hierarchy is sustained by [[authority]], then the destruction of authority removes its political expression. Anarchist [[free-association|association]] would replace the vertical ordering of command with the horizontal coordination of free individuals pursuing common goals.

This does not mean the abolition of all differentiation — [[jean-grave|Grave]] acknowledges that people have different capacities and inclinations. But it means the abolition of the systematic ranking of human beings into positions of command and submission, into "the top of the social scale" and the bottom. In an anarchist society, cooperation would replace command, and [[solidarity]] would replace subordination.

The persistence of hierarchical thinking even among revolutionaries is a concern [[jean-grave|Grave]] addresses directly. He criticizes those who "consider the masses as plastic dough, which may be moulded at will and about which there is no necessity of troubling oneself," warning that such attitudes are "an inheritance from our authoritarian past" [023-004]. The assumption that revolutionaries would "lead" the masses — "Verily a dictator would not talk differently" — reproduces the hierarchical logic it claims to oppose. [[jean-grave|Grave]] insists instead that "in the time of revolution, the only weight the Anarchists can have with the masses will be through action: putting our ideas in practice, preaching by example; by this means only can the crowd be led" [023-004]. Anti-hierarchy must be practiced, not merely proclaimed.
