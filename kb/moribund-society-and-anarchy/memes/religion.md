# Religion

## Definition

Religion appears in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis as one of the foundational institutions of the authoritarian social order. In the opening exposition of anarchist principles, he identifies it among the key institutions that "authority claims to justify its existence by the necessity of defending" — alongside the family and property [001-002]. It is not that anarchists have a special grievance against religion as such; rather, religion is one of the pillars of the social edifice that must be critically examined because authority has made itself its protector.

## The Historical Role of Religion

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates religion within the historical [[progress|evolution]] of social institutions. "Religion, property, authority, the family" all "slowly evolved from human aspirations" and "became gradually defined" [011-002]. In their origins, these institutions may have answered genuine human needs. But as they crystallized into fixed forms, as their purposes grew clear, "they became transformed, changed into engines of oppression" [011-002]. What began as an expression of human solidarity became a mechanism of social control.

> Religion, property, authority, the family, having slowly evolved from human aspirations, became gradually defined; but as they became precise in conception, as their purposes grew clear, they became transformed, changed into engines of oppression. [011-002]

This [[progress|evolution]]ary perspective is characteristic of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s method. He does not simply denounce religion as false; he traces its historical development, showing how an institution that once expressed human aspirations became a tool of domination. The critique is historical and material, not merely philosophical.

## Religion and Science

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s discussion of religion is notably abbreviated — he acknowledges that "science, bourgeois though it be, has killed that" [005-003]. In this he reflects the confidence of late nineteenth-century rationalism: the intellectual battle against religious dogma has been largely won, at least among the educated classes. The remaining task is not to refute theology but to dismantle the social conditions that make religion necessary.

Yet [[jean-grave|Grave]] also recognizes that the function of religion — providing meaning, justifying suffering, promising a justice deferred to another world — survives the intellectual refutation of its doctrines. The bourgeoisie may have abandoned religious belief for themselves, but they continue to find it useful for others. Like Voltaire, "they have frontiers between their slaves, but for themselves they mock at all frontiers" [011-012].

> They are like Voltaire, their patron: he did not believe in God, but judged that some religion was necessary to the common people; they have frontiers between their slaves, but for themselves they mock at all frontiers. [011-012]

## Religion and Solidarity

One of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s more subtle observations concerns the relationship between religion and the natural human sentiment of solidarity. "This sentiment of solidarity has become such a necessity that religions have carried it to the extreme of sacrifice, preaching charity and self-renunciation" [010-009]. Religion, in other words, appropriates and channels a genuine human impulse — the impulse toward mutual aid — and redirects it into forms that rein[[force-and-violence|force]] rather than challenge the existing order.

> This sentiment of solidarity has become such a necessity that religions have carried it to the extreme of sacrifice, preaching charity and self-renunciation, and therein finding a new element for exploitation. [010-009]

Charity is the religious form of solidarity, and it is fundamentally conservative. It alleviates the worst symptoms of exploitation without challenging its causes. The rich give alms and are praised for their generosity; the structural [[force-and-violence|violence]] that makes alms necessary continues unchecked. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s point is that the religious appropriation of solidarity is not simply ineffective — it actively rein[[force-and-violence|force]]s the exploitation it pretends to mitigate.

## Beyond Religion

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s vision of a post-religious society is not one of mere atheism — the intellectual rejection of theological propositions — but of a transformed social order in which the needs that religion once answered are met by human solidarity itself. When property is held in common and production is organized for need, when human relations are governed by mutual aid rather than competition, the consolations of religion will no longer be necessary. The kingdom of heaven will be realized on earth, not by divine intervention but by human action.
