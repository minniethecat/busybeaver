# Parliamentarianism

## Definition

Parliamentarianism — the system of government by elected representatives sitting in a legislative assembly — appears in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist analysis not as the realization of democratic self-rule but as a mechanism that distances the governed from power while maintaining the appearance of popular consent. The parliament, far from being an instrument of the people's will, functions as a theatre of managed opposition that "could produce nothing but an authoritarian political revolution" [017-004].

## The Origin of Representative Government

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the rise of parliamentary institutions to the same crisis of legitimacy that gave birth to [[universal-suffrage]]. With the collapse of divine-right monarchy and the erosion of noble privilege, the [[bourgeoisie]] required a new form of political organization — one that could preserve class rule while appearing to transfer power to the people. Property qualifications for voting were the first instrument: "the bourgeoisie has had to seek a new and more solid basis for them. Having themselves destroyed the basis of divine right, and helped to combat that of the right of force, they sought to substitute therefore that of money, by causing the chambers to be elected under the quit-rent regime" [007-002].

When the excluded demanded inclusion, the system adapted. [[universal-suffrage]] was conceded not as an act of democratic conviction but as a strategic retreat — and the bourgeoisie soon discovered that the parliamentary game posed no genuine threat: "it was only in the long run, through seeing it in operation, that they came to understand that it was not dangerous to their privileges, that it was but a fiddle upon which one must know how to play" [007-002].

## The Mechanics of Representation

The representative mechanism itself, in Grave's analysis, is structurally incapable of expressing the popular will. A voter casting a ballot for one deputy among several hundred "casts his ballot for only one, and consequently his will goes for less than one five-hundredth of the general will, still further reduced by the veto of the senate" [007-004]. The mathematics of representation dissolve individual sovereignty into nothing.

The selection pressure of electoral competition guarantees that the parliament will be populated not by the most capable or most principled, but by the most electable — a very different quality. The successful candidate "will take good care not to offend the prejudices of his constituents or to shock received opinions" [007-005]. To maximize votes, "he must round off his sharp corners and select a stock of commonplaces to get off before those whose suffrages he covets" [007-005]. The result is that "the more flat, mediocre, and insipid he is, the more chance he has of being elected" [007-005].

This dynamic is not a lamentable accident but a structural necessity of the parliamentary form. Every new idea begins in the minority; the genuinely original thinker "has no time to stoop to the shabby wire-pulling behind the scenes" and "will surely be beaten in the electoral lists by him who, having no original ideas, accepting those received by the greater number, will have less trouble in insinuating his projecting angles (which he has not) in a manner to offend no one" [007-006].

## Parliament and Class Rule

Beneath the formal equality of the ballot box, parliamentarianism functions to preserve class interests. Grave observes that the bourgeoisie "will not allow its own existence to be imperiled; so long as it holds the power it will make use of it to defend the system of organization which forms its strength" [020-009]. The parliamentary chamber, whatever its party composition, operates within constraints set by the existing property system. The only [[legislation]] permitted is that "which will not touch the institutions whence its privileges are derived; the only remedies it will consent to have tried are those which will attempt to mitigate the evils without attacking the causes" [020-009].

The history of parliamentary [[reform]] confirms this analysis. Reforms are dangled before the electorate as proofs of the system's responsiveness, but "so long as they are projects only, set the worker floating on an ocean of felicities, but which being adopted, lead to no sensible results — lucky, indeed, if they do not become a new means of exploitation and enslavement in the hands of our masters" [020-009].

## The Anarchist Rejection

For anarchists, parliamentarianism is irredeemable not because it is imperfectly implemented but because its very principle — the delegation of authority to representatives — contradicts the goal of individual autonomy. Representative institutions "stifle individual initiative which we proclaim, and which we should on the contrary seek to develop with all our might" [017-006]. The act of choosing a representative is an act of self-subordination: "From the moment he deposits his ballot in the box he has signed his abdication" [007-003].

The anarchist alternative to parliamentarianism is not a better electoral system or a more representative chamber, but the abolition of the principle that some may make binding decisions for others. In place of the parliament, Grave envisions the direct exercise of individual will through [[free-association]] and voluntary cooperation — a society in which no one is governed and no one governs.
