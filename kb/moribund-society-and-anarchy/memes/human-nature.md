# Human Nature

The question of **human nature** — whether human beings are inherently good, evil, or neutral — is a foundational issue in anarchist philosophy. In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, Jean Grave devotes an entire chapter ("Is Man Evil?") to refuting the claim that humanity's allegedly evil nature necessitates [[authority]], [[the-law]], and [[punishment]]. Grave argues that what appears as human evil is in fact produced by the social environment.

## The "Evil Nature" Argument

Defenders of the existing social order consistently invoke human nature to justify authority. The argument runs that without laws, [[police]], and punishment, humanity's innate depravity would lead to chaos. Grave identifies this circular reasoning:

> "The boldest — and they are rare — admit indeed in principle, that the social organization is bad, that it needs reforms, that some of its institutions beget misdemeanors; but to them the grand culprit is still the evil nature of mankind which necessitates a bridle upon their passions, and which society, defective as it is, can alone succeed in repressing." [004-326]

This argument serves to deflect responsibility from [[society]] onto the individual, protecting the institutions of [[capitalism]] and [[the-state]] from scrutiny.

## The Shaping Power of Environment

Against the "evil nature" thesis, Grave insists that human behavior is overwhelmingly the product of environmental influences:

> "Science, today, admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of forces to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist. Environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences, act upon man in turn." [004-286]

The implication is clear: to change human behavior, one must change the conditions that produce it — not intensify [[punishment]]:

> "According to his heredity, his education and environment in which he lives, the individual will be more or less docile to the stimulus of certain forces, more or less refractory to certain others; but it is none the less sure that his personality is but the product of these forces." [004-286]

## The Anarchist Response

Grave's anarchism offers a fundamentally different anthropology. Human beings are neither inherently good nor inherently evil — they are malleable creatures shaped by their conditions. A society organized around [[solidarity]], [[mutual-aid]], and [[cooperation]] rather than [[competition]] and [[exploitation]] would produce different kinds of human beings.

The "evil nature" argument is not merely factually wrong — it is a self-serving ideology that justifies the continued oppression of the many by the few. By insisting that authority is necessary to restrain innate depravity, its defenders avoid confronting the possibility that authority itself is the source of the depravity they claim to combat.
