# Crime

## Social Origins

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], crime is not an expression of individual wickedness but a predictable outcome of a social organization founded on [[private-property|private property]], competition, and class antagonism. "Based upon antagonism of interests," he writes, society "opposes individual against individual within each class; it sows warfare among them by its institution of private property which forces people to hoard in order to secure themselves against the morrow" [010-016]. This structural antagonism — not the "evil nature of man" — is what "provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors" that bourgeois society then attributes to individual depravity [010-016].

> Founded upon this struggle between individuals, society makes of every creature the enemy of all others; it provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors which are attributed to the evil nature of man, though they are but the consequence of the social order. [010-016]

The question Grave poses is not how to punish criminals more effectively, but whether the social order that produces crime is itself defensible: "If this regime involves so many crimes as an inevitable reaction it must be very illogical, it must crush out many interests" [009-014].

## Critique of Criminal Anthropology

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes sustained attention to the school of criminal anthropology led by [[cesare-lombroso|Cesare Lombroso]], who attempted to identify a "born criminal" type through physical anomalies. Grave ridicules this approach: Lombroso's supposed markers — old wounds, skin anomalies, deformations of the ears and nose, tattooing — have "no more relation to a person's mentality than the foregoing" [009-006]. He notes with irony that "Lombroso concedes that many cases which he instances as anomalies are frequently found among those whom he calls honest people. These, then, are anomalies tending to become generalities!" [009-007].

> Lombroso himself concedes that many cases which he instances as anomalies are frequently found among those whom he calls honest people. These, then, are anomalies tending to become generalities! [009-007]

Grave draws on the work of Dr. Manouvrier, who in his course on Criminal Anthropology at the Anthropological Society in 1890–91 "refuted, in an admirable manner, the theories of Lombroso" [009-009]. Manouvrier demonstrated that the criminal type was constructed from "observations only individuals already deformed by prison life or by an abnormal existence," and that the same aptitudes — muscular strength, contempt of danger, craftiness — could serve indifferently "the vices of the criminal or the virtues demanded of the soldier" [009-009]. A cunning disposition "may make the swindler who thinks of nothing but schemes for robbery and fraud; but they are also the qualities required to make an admirable detective or examining magistrate" [009-009].

## Property Crime and Class Justice

The most telling evidence against the existing order, Grave argues, is what the law actually punishes. "Outside of crimes of passion, which are very rare… attacks upon property furnish the largest contingent of crimes or misdemeanors" [009-014]. This leads to the fundamental question: "Is property just? Is an organization which creates such a number of crimes defensible?" [009-014].

The law's selective enforcement reveals its class character. "You punish as a thief the unfortunate who, having no work, risks imprisonment to get hold of a piece of bread to appease his hunger; but you take off your hat and bow to the millionaire monopolist" [009-015]. The financier who "by a stroke on the bourse, has ruined hundreds of families to enrich himself from the spoil" is treated with deference, while "the tramp who steals an apple in passing" faces imprisonment [009-015]. Grave concludes that the law is "the humble servant of the privileged against the demands of the barefooted mob" [009-015].

> You punish him who operates on a small scale, but you support whole armies that you may send them oversea to operate on a large scale against peoples unable to defend themselves. [009-015]

This double standard extends to [[colonialism|colonization]], which Grave calls "brigandage and highway robbery for the benefit of the ruling classes" — acts that would be criminal if committed by an individual but are celebrated as "civilizing" when carried out by the [[the-state|state]] [014-009].

## The Contradiction of Punishment

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a profound contradiction in the position of the criminologists. Having established that "man acts under the influence of causes external to himself; hence he is not responsible for his acts," they nevertheless "decide for energetic repression, life imprisonment, etc." [009-001]. Science has demonstrated that "free will does not exist" and that personality is "but the product of these forces" — yet the savants conclude in favor of [[punishment]] [009-001].

This contradiction, Grave suggests, arises because the scientists remain trapped within "the prejudices of education, comparative ignorance of scientific questions which they have not studied" — they "cannot allow that humanity is able to go forward without leading-strings, dungeons, and chains" [009-013].

> You punish him who operates on a small scale, but you support whole armies that you may send them oversea to operate on a large scale against peoples unable to defend themselves. [009-015]

## Crime and the Economic System

Grave deepens his analysis by examining how the economic structure of capitalism systematically produces criminal behavior. The [[competition|competitive]] logic of the market transforms economic survival into a predatory struggle. Workers are pitted against each other for scarce employment; merchants against merchants for customers; nations against nations for markets and resources. Each participant in this struggle is incentivized to exploit every advantage, to cut every corner, to treat every counterpart as an adversary. The boundary between "legitimate" business practice and criminal predation is not a moral boundary but a legal one — and the law, as Grave has shown, is written by the propertied to protect their interests.

The distinction between the "criminal" and the "respectable" citizen is therefore largely a function of class position. The worker who steals bread is imprisoned; the employer who steals wages through systematic underpayment is honored. The street thief who takes a wallet is a criminal; the financier whose speculation destroys livelihoods is a captain of industry. "You punish as a thief the unfortunate who, having no work, risks imprisonment to get hold of a piece of bread to appease his hunger; but you take off your hat and bow to the millionaire monopolist" [009-015].

> Make it so that society be organized upon conditions that will put it within the reach of everyone to live by working, and you will see crime diminish immediately. [009-014]

The implication is radical: crime is not a dysfunction of capitalism but one of its normal products. A social order that makes survival contingent on successful competition will predictably generate, at every level, behaviors that subordinate ethics to self-interest. The only permanent solution is to abolish the institutional structure — [[private-property|private property]], the wage system, the competitive market — that makes predation rational and solidarity irrational.

## Abolishing Crime Through Social Transformation

The anarchist prescription is not more rigorous punishment but the abolition of the social conditions that generate crime. "Let society once be so arranged that every individual shall be assured of the satisfaction of all his needs; that nothing shall fetter his free evolution; that in the social organization there shall be no more institutions of which he may avail himself to enslave his fellows, and you will see crime disappear" [009-014]. If any "isolated natures so corrupted or degenerated" remain, "such cases will be taken up by science and not by the executioner, the paid assassin of capitalistic and authoritarian society" [009-014].

> From the present organization you reap a harvest of wars, crimes, thefts, fraud, and misery. This is the result of private property and authority… If you would have a society in which reign confidence, solidarity, and well-being for all, base it upon liberty, reciprocity, and equality. [010-021]

Grave's vision is not a utopian fantasy of a crime-free world — he acknowledges that "isolated natures so corrupted or degenerated" might persist [009-014]. But these would be medical and social questions, not criminal ones. The anarchist society treats social harm as a problem to be solved, not an offense to be punished. This transformation — from retribution to restoration, from the executioner to the scientist — is for Grave the logical culmination of the scientific understanding of human behavior that the criminologists themselves have developed but lack the courage to apply.
