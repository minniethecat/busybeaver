# Mutual Aid

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, mutual aid appears as an implicit but foundational principle underlying [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s positive vision of anarchist society. While [[jean-grave|Grave]] does not use the term "mutual aid" as frequently as his contemporary Peter Kropotkin — whose *Mutual Aid: A Factor of [[evolution|Evolution]]* would be published just a few years later — the concept permeates his analysis of how free individuals relate to one another in the absence of coercive authority. Mutual aid, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], is the spontaneous expression of human solidarity: the recognition that individual flourishing depends on collective well-being and that [[cooperation|voluntary cooperation]], not competitive struggle, is the natural basis of social life [002-013].

## Historical Context

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s treatment of mutual aid reflects the intellectual [[environment|milieu]] of [[france|French]] anarchism in the 1890s, shortly before Kropotkin's systematic elaboration of the concept. [[jean-grave|Grave]] was a friend and collaborator of Kropotkin's; his journal *Les Temps Nouveaux* published Kropotkin's early articles on mutual aid. While [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own analysis is less developed than Kropotkin's — lacking the biological and historical evidence that Kropotkin would marshal — it shares the same fundamental intuition: that [[association|cooperation]], not competition, is the driving [[force-and-violence|force]] of social [[progress|evolution]], and that capitalist ideology systematically obscures this fact.


## Grave's Analysis

[[jean-grave|Grave]] approaches mutual aid through the lens of his broader critique of capitalist social relations. Under capitalism, human interactions are structured by competition, exploitation, and the war of all against all. The [[class|ruling class]] promotes an ideology of individualism that atomizes workers, making collective resistance difficult. Yet [[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that this competitive individualism is not natural but imposed — a product of "a bad organization" of society that pits individuals against one another for survival.

> Many of us will combine with the intention of realizing one of our aspirations. This association having nothing forced in it, nothing arbitrary, prompted only by some need of our being, it is quite evident that the more pressing the need the more force will it have. [002-013]

The logic of mutual aid, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s framework, is the inverse of the logic of capital. Where capital divides, mutual aid unites. Where capital extracts, mutual aid shares. Where capital compels, mutual aid invites. The transition from capitalism to anarchy is, in this sense, the transition from coerced [[association|cooperation]] under the boss's command to voluntary mutual aid among equals.

## Solidarity as Mutual Aid in Practice

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s treatment of solidarity — a term he uses more frequently than mutual aid — can be read as the practical expression of the mutual aid principle. [[solidarity|Solidarity]], for [[jean-grave|Grave]], is not an abstract moral sentiment but a concrete practice of collective action. It is the recognition that "the adversity of one" should be "felt by others, in order that all may be led to diminish or prevent it" [004-004].

The present society, [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes, systematically undermines solidarity. It is structured so that personal well-being does not "flow from general well-being" but rather exists in opposition to it [004-004]. The task of [[propaganda|anarchist propaganda]] is to awaken the latent sense of solidarity that [[capitalism|capitalist society]] suppresses — to make workers recognize their common interests and act upon them collectively.
