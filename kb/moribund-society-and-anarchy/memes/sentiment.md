# Sentiment

## Sentiment as the Emotional Basis of Anarchism

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], anarchist ideas draw their power not only from rational argument but from their resonance with deep-seated human sentiments. "That Anarchistic ideas have been able to develop with this energy and rapidity, is because, while running counter to accepted opinions and established prejudices, they have their roots in the secret feelings, the intimate aspirations of individuals" [001-010]. The rapid spread of anarchism cannot be explained by intellectual persuasion alone; it corresponds to something people already feel.

This connection between ideas and sentiment is what gives anarchism its resilience. "Anarchy has its victims—its dead, its imprisoned, its exiled; but it remains alive and strong, the number of its propagandists constantly increasing" [001-014]. People are willing to sacrifice for anarchist principles because those principles speak to their deepest convictions about [[justice]] and human dignity.

> It is this close relation with the secret sentiments of individuals which explains their rapid extension, which gives them their strength and renders them indestructible. [001-015]

## The Corruption of Natural Sentiment

Grave argues that the existing social order systematically corrupts natural human sentiment. [[competition|Competition]] and [[private-property|private property]] foster an "unreasoned egoism which seems to have become the sole motive of human actions" [002-014]. The result is that people come to believe—wrongly—that "men cannot live without fighting each other" [002-007].

Yet Grave insists that generous sentiments persist beneath the surface of competitive society. "Aspirations towards the good and the beautiful must be perennial in the human race, not to have been choked by the rapacity and narrow, unreasoning egoism which official society has inculcated in it from the cradle" [004-005]. The moral sense is resilient—it can be suppressed but not destroyed.

> With the development of his intelligence he comes to understand that if his ego wishes to be satisfied there are other egos that equally wish to be satisfied. [002-010]

## Reason and Sentiment as Complementary Forces

Grave does not oppose sentiment to [[reason]]; he sees them as complementary. Reason demonstrates the logic of free association, but sentiment provides the motivation to act on that understanding. "Instead of confining our efforts to capturing people through sentiment, we seek above all to win them through logic and reason" [023-007]—but this is a matter of emphasis, not exclusion. Anarchist propaganda must address both the intellect and the heart.

The anarchist ideal itself—"the ideal of man released from all fetters, all constraint" [001-015]—is as much a matter of sentiment as of logic. "Have not all the divers revolutions he has wrought, pursued this end?" [001-015]. The drive toward freedom is not a philosophical abstraction but a deeply felt human need.

## Sentiment and Moral Responsibility

Grave's treatment of sentiment intersects with his determinist view of [[free-will|free will]]. If human beings are products of their environment, then their sentiments are shaped by the conditions in which they live. "There is not a single person who has not at times uttered his cry of revolt or indignation against this society, still governed by the dead, which seems to hold man forever enchained" [004-009]. The sentiment of revolt is universal—it is the social order that is unnatural.

This has an important practical consequence: changing the social environment will change human sentiment. Abolish the institutions that reward egoism, and the natural sentiments of [[solidarity]] and mutual aid will reassert themselves.
