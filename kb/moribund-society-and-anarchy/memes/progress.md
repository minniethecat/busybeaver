# Progress

## The Nature of Progress

[[jean-grave|Grave]] grounds his understanding of progress in a materialist and evolutionary framework. Humanity is not guided by a providential plan toward a predetermined destination; rather, progress is the cumulative result of environmental pressures, adaptive responses, and the conscious striving of individuals and groups to overcome the constraints that limit their development. "Man, then, like all other animals, is but the product of an evolution worked out under the influence of the environment in which he lives and the conditions of existence he is [[force-and-violence|force]]d to submit to or combat" [013-003]. Unlike other animals, however, man "has come to reason upon his origin, to formulate aspirations for his future" — and it is this capacity for conscious self-direction that makes progress possible [013-003].

Progress is not linear or guaranteed. The institutions that structure social life — property, authority, [[government|the state]] — can accelerate or retard it. Under capitalism, the very advances that ought to improve human life — science, machinery, the accumulation of knowledge — are perverted into instruments of exploitation and misery. The task of the anarchist is to remove the institutional obstacles that prevent the full development of human potential.

## Development as the Law of Life

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], progress is not a choice but a biological and social imperative. "It is a [[the-law|law]] of life that that which no longer advances, perishes and disintegrates, in order to give birth to other organisms having a period of evolution to run through" [006-005]. Institutions that claim immovability — the family, property, [[government|the state]] — are already in decay precisely because they resist the evolutionary current. The bourgeoisie's own concessions, such as the admission of divorce, demonstrate that even the most rigid institutions must adapt or collapse.

> There is no reason why that which has evolved at all should not evolve further. Their affirmation would prove only one thing, which is that if these two institutions were not to progress any more, they must be very near their decadence. [006-005]

This developmental logic applies to the individual as well as to society. Man "has reached his present development and formed conceptions of which he was formerly incapable" — what reason, [[jean-grave|Grave]] asks, "why he should stop and go no further?" [013-005]. To deny the possibility of continued progress is as absurd as it would have been to tell the cave-dweller that he would never build cities or harness electricity.

## Progress, Science, and the Individual

[[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that true progress is inseparable from the development of individual autonomy. [[anarchy|Anarchism]] "proclaiming individual initiative as the basis of all progress and of every truly libertarian association," cannot content itself with making passive believers; it must create individuals capable of reasoning for themselves [008-008]. This marks a fundamental break with authoritarian models of social change, in which progress is delivered from above by leaders, parties, or [[the-state|state]]s. Genuine progress arises from below, through the accumulated initiatives of free individuals who have learned "how to reason and to combine their initiative" [011-024].

> Anarchistic principles have come to overthrow all this. Denying the necessity of providential men, making war upon authority, and claiming for each individual the right and the duty to act under the pressure of his own impulses only… Anarchism cannot content itself with making believers; it must, above all, aim to convince. [008-008]

[[science|Science]] plays a crucial role in this conception of progress. The scientific understanding of evolution — from the formation of the earth out of "a nucleus of cosmic matter" to the emergence of organic life and the slow development of human consciousness — demystifies progress and reveals it as a natural process rather than a divine plan [003-001]. By comprehending the [[the-law|law]]s of their own development, human beings acquire the power to direct it consciously.

## The Obstacle of Revolution and the Fear of Regression

[[jean-grave|Grave]] confronts a common objection to revolutionary change: that revolution risks "wrecking everything without producing any good," bringing "about a reaction not less violent than the attack and thus causing a setback to progress for several centuries" [017-001]. His response is that the existing order is already a catastrophe for those who suffer under it. "There are people who suffer, perish, through this so-called security, this so-called civilization" [017-005]. To postpone [[emancipation|liberation]] in the name of gradual progress is to condemn yet another generation to exploitation and misery.

> If we answer them that there are people who suffer, perish, through this so-called security, this so-called civilization, they do not hesitate to admit that the capitalist class is ignoble in its exploitation; that by its rapacity it justifies the revolt of the exploited; some even admit that the revolution is inevitable, "but," they add, "it is to be regretted." [017-005]

Moreover, [[jean-grave|Grave]] notes that every great social transformation has required a rupture with the past. The bourgeoisie did not gradually persuade the aristocracy to surrender its privileges — it seized them. The workers must do the same, not to establish a new class rule but to abolish class entirely.
