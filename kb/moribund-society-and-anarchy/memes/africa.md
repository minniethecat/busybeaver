# Africa

## Overview

Africa is referenced by Jean Grave in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* primarily in the context of European colonialism and critiques of racial superiority theories. Grave uses African peoples as examples to refute the notion of "inferior races" advanced by criminologists and colonial apologists of his era.

## In Grave's Text

Jean Grave invokes Africa across three distinct contexts:

**Colonization**: In Chapter XIV ("Colonization"), Grave cites the French conquest of Africa alongside Spanish colonization of America and British colonization of India as examples of "civilizing" missions that were in fact brutal subjugation:

> "They are savages; they must be civilized." Let anyone take up the history of conquests, and then tell us which were the most savage—those who were called so, or the "civilized." … Take the history of the conquests in America by Spain, of India by England, of Africa, Cochin China, and Tonkin by France, and then boast about "civilization." [014-008]

**Racial Theory**: In Chapter XV ("There Are No Inferior Races"), Grave examines African tribal cultures to challenge Lombroso's criminal anthropology. He notes that Egyptian monuments depict African types still existing in his day, arguing that ten thousand years of apparent stagnation proves nothing about inherent racial capacity [015-010]. Grave points out the contradiction that Egyptians, once classed among "superior" races of antiquity, are now classed among "inferior" races under British rule [015-010].

**Tattooing and Atavism**: In Chapter IX, Grave mentions African tribal practices of body modification—cutting lips, nose cartilage, or ear lobes to insert ornaments—as part of his critique of Lombroso's criminal type theory, which classed tattooing as a mark of criminality [009-011].

## Historical Context

By the late nineteenth century, the "Scramble for Africa" was at its height. The Berlin Conference of 1884–1885 had formalized European partitioning of the continent. France was actively colonizing large portions of West and North Africa, as well as Madagascar. Grave's references to Africa reflect the anarchist critique of imperialism as an extension of state authority and capitalist exploitation, consistent with the broader anti-colonial stance of the anarchist movement of his era.
