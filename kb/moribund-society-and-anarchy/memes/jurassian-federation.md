# Jurassian Federation

## Overview

The Jurassian Federation (Fédération Jurassienne) was a [[association|federation]] of anarchist sections of the [[first-international|International Workingmen's Association]] ([[first-international|First International]]) based in the Jura mountains of Switzerland. Founded in 1870 by James Guillaume and other anti-authoritarian socialists, the Federation became the organizational center of [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]]ist anarchism following the split with Marx's General Council at the Hague Congress of 1872. The Federation's watchmakers and artisans developed a distinctive form of revolutionary syndicalism that emphasized federalism, direct action, and the general strike as instruments of working-class emancipation.

## Organizational Principles

The Jurassian Federation was organized on principles that directly embodied its political theory. Each local section retained complete autonomy over its internal affairs. The Federation had no permanent executive body and no paid officials. Decisions were made by congress, with each section sending delegates bound by imperative mandate — they could report their section's views but could not commit the section to decisions they had not authorized. This was not merely a procedural preference but a deliberate effort to prevent the emergence of a bureaucratic layer that would separate itself from the membership and develop interests of its own.

The Federation's internal culture was characterized by vigorous theoretical debate. The watchmakers who filled its sections were described by contemporaries as studious and argumentative — they read [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]] and [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]] in their workshops, discussed revolutionary strategy in their meetings, and treated political education as an integral part of their organizing. This intellectual vitality, combined with the Federation's insistence on rank-and-file democracy, made it a unique experiment in prefigurative politics — an organization that tried to embody in its own structure the principles of the society it sought to create.

## Historical Formation

The Jura region of Switzerland in the late nineteenth century was characterized by small-scale watchmaking workshops rather than large factories. The skilled artisans who populated these workshops — independent, literate, accustomed to managing their own labor — provided a social base uniquely receptive to federalist and anti-authoritarian ideas. Unlike the industrial proletariat of Manchester or the Ruhr, the Jura watchmakers were not massed in factories under direct supervision; their work was dispersed across small shops and home workshops, and they retained a strong sense of craft autonomy. This material condition shaped the political culture of the Federation, which rejected the hierarchical party model favored by Marx and instead insisted on the autonomy of each local section.

The Federation was formally constituted in October 1870 at a congress in Saint-Imier. Its leading figure was James Guillaume (1844–1916), a Swiss teacher and writer who became [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]]'s closest collaborator and the Federation's most prominent theoretician. Guillaume edited the Federation's newspaper, the *Bulletin de la Fédération Jurassienne*, which circulated among the watchmaking communities and served as the primary vehicle for the anti-authoritarian position within the International.

From its inception, the Jurassian Federation stood in opposition to the centralizing tendency of the General Council in London, controlled by [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] and his allies. The conflict was simultaneously theoretical and organizational. At the theoretical level, the Jurassians rejected the dictatorship of the proletariat and insisted that the emancipation of the working class must be the work of the working class itself — not of a vanguard party acting in its name. At the organizational level, they rejected the concentration of power in the General Council and demanded that the International remain a loose federation of autonomous sections.

## The Hague Congress and the Split

The conflict came to a head at the Hague Congress of September 1872. Marx, recognizing that his control of the International was threatened by the growing strength of the anti-authoritarian sections, manipulated the credentials process to pack the Congress with his supporters. The Congress voted to expel [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]] and James Guillaume from the International and to transfer the seat of the General Council to New York — a move designed to place it beyond the reach of the European anti-authoritarians.

The Jurassian sections rejected the legitimacy of the Hague Congress. Meeting at Saint-Imier immediately afterward, they held their own international congress and declared the decisions of the Hague null and void. The Saint-Imier Congress adopted resolutions affirming that "the destruction of all political power is the first duty of the proletariat" and that "any organization of political power, even if it calls itself provisional and revolutionary, can only be a new deception." These resolutions became foundational documents of the anarchist movement.

The split was definitive. The Marx-controlled International withered into irrelevance over the following years, formally dissolving in 1876. The anti-authoritarian International — often called the "Saint-Imier International" — continued to hold congresses until 1877, maintaining the federalist principles that the Jurassian Federation had championed from the beginning.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

The Jurassian Federation represents the organizational tradition out of which [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s own political activity emerged. The federalist, anti-parliamentary principles that [[jean-grave|Grave]] defends in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* — the rejection of [[the-state|state]] socialism, the insistence on [[association|voluntary association]], the commitment to direct action — were developed and tested in the Jurassian sections. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s argument that workers must "come to an understanding among themselves" rather than delegating their power to representatives reflects the Jurassian critique of parliamentarism and the dictatorship of the proletariat [005-020].

The Jurassian experience also informs [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s understanding of how anarchist ideas spread. The Federation never had more than a few hundred active members, yet its influence on the development of the international anarchist movement was immense — not because it commanded large numbers but because it demonstrated a viable alternative to both capitalist parliamentarism and Marxist statism. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own career as an anarchist propagandist and organizer in France followed this pattern: modest numbers, persistent agitation, and an unwavering commitment to principle over expediency.

## Significance for Anarchism

The Jurassian Federation's importance extends far beyond its modest size. It demonstrated that anarchist organization was possible — that revolutionary workers could build durable [[association|federation]]s without reproducing the hierarchical structures of [[government|the state]] or the capitalist enterprise. Its emphasis on the autonomy of local sections, its rejection of permanent leadership, and its commitment to theoretical debate within a framework of practical solidarity became the model for subsequent anarchist organizations, including the anarchist movement in [[france|France]] where [[jean-grave|Grave]] was active.

The Federation also contributed to the development of revolutionary syndicalism — the strategy, later adopted by the French CGT and Spanish CNT, of building revolutionary unions that would conduct the general strike and expropriate the means of production directly, without mediation by political parties. The Jurassian watchmakers, with their craft autonomy and their rejection of parliamentary politics, were among the first to articulate this vision: the working class organized in its workplaces, acting directly on its own behalf, without leaders or intermediaries.

## Decline and Legacy

The Jurassian Federation began to decline in the late 1870s. The collapse of the watchmaking industry in the Jura region, the emigration of many of its most active members, and the general repression that followed the Paris Commune all took their toll. The Federation held its last congress in 1880.

But the legacy endured. The federalist principles developed in the Jura mountains informed the organizational practice of anarchist movements across Europe and the Americas. The idea that revolutionary organizations should prefigure the society they seek to create — that the means must be consistent with the end — became a defining principle of anarchist practice. The Jurassian Federation, small and short-lived though it was, demonstrated that this principle was not merely a theoretical aspiration but a practical possibility.
