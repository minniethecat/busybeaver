# Environment

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s materialist analysis, environment is the ensemble of physical, social, and moral conditions that shape human beings from birth — determining their character, beliefs, and conduct far more than any supposed innate nature or free will. "Man is not created unique, either morally or physically," [[jean-grave|Grave]] writes; "Like other animals, of which he is but a superior specimen, he is the product of a concourse of circumstances, of combination and association of matter" [010-004]. This recognition of environmental determinism is not merely a scientific observation but a political weapon — it undermines the moralistic justifications for punishment, hierarchy, and inequality that sustain the existing social order.

## The Science of Environmental Influence

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws on the scientific consensus of his era to establish that "the modifying influence of environments upon organized beings is no longer combated save by the old fogies of official science" [010-002]. The soil, the climate, the obstacles and advantages encountered by organisms — all exert an influence "as great as the other [[the-law|law]]s" governing development and adaptation. This applies with equal [[force-and-violence|force]] to humans: the physical environment shapes the body, and "with still greater reason the intellectual and moral environment must influence his psychological constitution" [004-003].

The implications are radical. If human character is the product of environment rather than free will, then the entire apparatus of moral judgment — the [[the-law|law]], the magistracy, the prison — rests on a foundation of error. "[[science|Science]], today, admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of [[force-and-violence|force]]s to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist" [009-002]. The criminal is not a moral failure but a product of "environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences" acting in combination [009-002].

> Man is not created unique, either morally or physically. Like other animals, of which he is but a superior specimen, he is the product of a concourse of circumstances, of combination and association of matter. He has struggled to develop himself, and if he has contributed in a large measure to the transformation of the environment wherein he is situated, the latter has in turn influenced the customs he has adopted, his manner of living, thinking, and acting. [010-004]

## Environment and Crime

The doctrine of environmental determinism serves as the foundation for [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of the punitive system. If free will is an illusion, then the moral condemnation directed at "criminals" is irrational. The judge who pronounces sentence "does not know the circumstances of environment, heredity, or even chance, which influenced the mind" of the accused — circumstances that, had the judge himself been subjected to them, would have led him to the same act [008-008].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends this analysis to the class character of crime. The most antisocial and common crimes — those against property — are the direct product of a social environment organized around property itself. "Attacks upon property furnish the great majority" of crimes, yet it is "the lust for gain, the passion for money, which society develops the most" [009-015]. An environment that exalts wealth while denying it to the majority inevitably produces theft; an environment that glorifies [[force-and-violence|violence]] through [[war|militarism]] and colonization inevitably produces [[force-and-violence|violence]]. The criminal is not an aberration but a reflection of the society that condemns them.

## The Reciprocity of Organism and Environment

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s environmentalism is not a crude one-way determinism. He recognizes that humans are not merely passive products of their surroundings — "if he has contributed in a large measure to the transformation of the environment wherein he is situated, the latter has in turn influenced the customs he has adopted" [010-004]. There is a dialectical relationship between human beings and their environment: we are shaped by the world we inherit, but we also transform it, and the transformed world shapes us in turn.

This reciprocity is crucial to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s r[[progress|evolution]]ary project. If environment determines character, and humans can transform their environment, then the r[[progress|evolution]]ary transformation of the social environment makes possible the emergence of new kinds of human beings — cooperative rather than competitive, solidaristic rather than egoistic. The anarchist society is not a utopian wish that human nature will magically improve; it is the creation of an environment in which the best human potentials, already visible "in spite of all the stimulus of evil surroundings," can flourish [010-013].

## The Political Implications

The environmental thesis has profound political consequences. If crime, laziness, and [[individualism|egoism]] are products of environment, then the appropriate response is not punishment but the transformation of the conditions that produce them. "Make work attractive. Instead of riveting it upon a small minority of society to whom it becomes a torture, do away with all your State machinery, your useless offices, and organize your society in such a way that each shall be led, by mere [[force-and-violence|force]] of circumstances and not by any authority whatever, to cooperate in social production" [010-015].

The entire institutional structure of bourgeois society — the [[the-law|law]], the magistracy, the police, the prison — is revealed as an elaborate mechanism for punishing the symptoms of a diseased environment while protecting the disease itself. The anarchist response is not to reform the punitive apparatus but to abolish the conditions that require it, creating an environment in which "the dream of human solidarity" is no longer a dream but the natural expression of social life.
