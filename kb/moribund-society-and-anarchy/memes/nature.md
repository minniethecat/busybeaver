# Nature

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] interrogates the concept of nature — particularly "human nature" — as it is deployed to defend [[authority]] and the existing social order. Grave rejects the claim that hierarchy and domination are natural, arguing instead that human beings are shaped by their [[environment]] and social institutions. The appeal to "nature" as justification for social arrangements is, in Grave's analysis, a circular argument that naturalizes what is historically and socially produced.

## The "State of Nature" Fallacy

Grave considers the hypothetical "state of nature" invoked by defenders of the existing order. He acknowledges that "if men could live isolated, if they could return to the state of nature, there would be no discussion as to how they should live: each would live as he pleased" (002-008). But this is a fantasy — humans are inherently social beings. The question is not whether we live in society but what kind of society we construct. Grave observes that when "man first began to group together with his fellows, he must still have been much more of an animal than a human being; ideas of morality and justice did not exist in him" (003-018). Human nature has evolved, and continues to evolve, through social existence.

## Human Nature as a Justification for Authority

### The "Man Is Evil" Argument

Grave systematically challenges the authoritarian claim that "man is too evil to know how to govern himself" (003-012). This argument, he contends, is self-serving: [[power]] structures create the very conditions of deprivation and conflict that are then cited as evidence of human depravity. The response "power is necessary to reform mankind" (003-012) is revealed as circular — power creates the conditions it claims to remedy.

### Nature vs. Nurture

Grave anticipates modern debates about nature versus nurture by emphasizing the primacy of social environment. He argues that vices attributed to human nature "would most naturally disappear were they not supported and developed by the social organization which is based upon them" (003-104). The problem is not innate human evil but the institutional structures — [[private-property]], [[authority]], the [[the-state|state]] — that incentivize antisocial behavior and punish [[solidarity]].

## The Natural and the Social

### Evolution and Plasticity

Grave draws on evolutionary thought to argue that humans are not fixed in their nature. "Having had to struggle against other animals, against all nature" (003-018), early humans developed cooperative instincts for survival. The history of humanity is one of increasing social complexity and the development of moral ideas. Grave insists that "there are no preconceived ideas" in nature (003-028) — what we call "human nature" is the product of historical development and can be transformed through changes in social organization.

### The Anarchist Alternative

The anarchist vision is not a return to a primitive state of nature but a deliberate reconstruction of society on principles of [[freedom|liberty]] and [[solidarity]]. Grave envisions a society where "the type of the humane egoist, perfectly developed, is to suffer with the sufferings of those who surround him" (002-015) — a human nature cultivated by free institutions rather than corrupted by coercive ones.
