# Dominion

## Definition

Dominion, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s usage, refers to the psychological impulse toward mastery and control — the drive to subordinate other beings to one's will. He describes it as a "spirit of struggle and domination, stored up in the brain by past generations" [003-014], identifying it as an inherited predisposition shaped by millennia of competitive and hierarchical social organization. Dominion is not, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], an inalterable feature of [[human-nature]] but rather an acquired tendency that can be understood, resisted, and ultimately transcended through conscious social reorganization.

## The Evolutionary Origins

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates the impulse toward dominion within an evolutionary framework. The "struggle for existence" that characterized pre-human and early human life favored individuals and groups with strong domineering tendencies. Over generations, these tendencies became neurologically encoded — "stored up in the brain" — and transmitted as inherited dispositions. However, [[jean-grave|Grave]] is careful to avoid biological determinism: the fact that dominion has evolutionary roots does not make it ineradicable or desirable. "His ideas of domination are so strong and tenacious" that they persist even when they have become maladaptive, but this tenacity is a challenge to be met, not a destiny to be accepted [005-008].

The same evolutionary perspective that explains dominion's origins also points toward its possible transcendence. Just as the environment of scarcity and competition shaped domineering impulses, an environment of [[solidarity|cooperation]] and [[mutual-aid|mutual aid]] can reshape human psychology toward more cooperative dispositions. The transformation of the "spirit of domination" into a spirit of voluntary association is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], one of the central tasks of anarchist social reconstruction.

## Dominion and the Denial of Nature

One of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most striking arguments concerns the relationship between dominion and humanity's understanding of its place in nature. The scientific revolution — particularly the work of Copernicus, Galileo, and Darwin — progressively displaced humanity from the center of the cosmos, revealing the "insignificant place" humanity held in the natural order [004-018]. Yet "man's spirit of domination was incapable of resolving to accept this new point of view" [006-002]. Rather than embracing the lessons of [[science]] and recognizing human interdependence with the natural world, the domineering impulse sought new arenas for its exercise — intensifying the exploitation of nature and other peoples even as the old cosmological justifications crumbled.

This refusal to accept natural limits is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], a defining pathology of the civilized mind. The dominator cannot accept equality with other beings — human or non-human — because equality is experienced as a diminishment. The anarchist alternative is not passivity but a different mode of agency: acting in harmony with natural laws rather than in futile rebellion against them.

## Institutionalized Dominion

The impulse toward dominion does not remain a private psychological fact; it becomes embodied in institutions. The [[the-state|state]] is the most comprehensive institutionalization of dominion, concentrating the means of coercion and claiming a monopoly on legitimate violence. [[authority|Authority]] in all its forms — patriarchal, ecclesiastical, economic, political — represents the translation of the domineering impulse into social structure. The path toward an anarchist society therefore requires not only the abolition of specific institutions but the conscious cultivation of anti-dominating dispositions — what [[jean-grave|Grave]] elsewhere calls the sentiment of [[solidarity]].
