# Pierre-Joseph Proudhon

## Biography

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865) was a [[france|French]] philosopher, economist, and typographer who became the first thinker to explicitly declare himself an "anarchist." Born in Besançon to a working-class family, Proudhon experienced poverty firsthand — an experience that profoundly shaped his critique of [[private-property|property]] and economic inequality. Largely self-educated, he worked as a typographer and proofreader before devoting himself to political writing, producing a vast body of work that spanned economics, political philosophy, and social theory.

His 1840 work *What Is Property?* (*Qu'est-ce que la propriété?*) — with its famous answer, "Property is theft!" — established the foundational argument that property rights are not natural or God-given but are sustained by [[the-state|state]] violence and legal artifice. The book earned him notoriety and the lasting hostility of the French establishment, setting the pattern for a life of polemical engagement, prosecution, and exile.

## Theoretical Contributions

Proudhon's mutualism — a system of voluntary exchange, [[cooperation|cooperative production]], and interest-free credit organized through a "People's Bank" — offered the first systematic anarchist economic theory. Unlike later communist anarchists, Proudhon did not advocate the abolition of all private possession; rather, he distinguished between "property" (which enables exploitation through rent, interest, and profit) and "possession" (the right of individuals and associations to control the means of their own production). This distinction runs through [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own economic analysis in *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, where he critiques private property not as the possession of goods for use but as the monopolization of the means of production.

Proudhon's federalist political vision, elaborated in *The Principle of Federation* (1863), proposed the decentralization of political authority into [[association|voluntary association]]s bound by contract rather than coercion. The state, in his analysis, was not a neutral arbiter but the instrument by which property owners enforced their claims against the propertyless — a thesis [[jean-grave|Grave]] extends throughout his critique of [[government|government]] as "the representative and the agent of the ruling class."

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Grave]] explicitly situates Proudhon within the longer genealogy of anarchist thought. In discussing the revolutionary tradition that preceded his own era, [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that "one must come down to Proudhon before he sees Anarchy positively affirmed" — a recognition that Proudhon represented the threshold at which inchoate revolutionary aspirations crystallized into a coherent anti-authoritarian doctrine.

Grave's extended analysis of property in Chapter V of *Moribund Society and Anarchy* draws directly on Proudhon's foundational insight that property is not a natural right but a social convention enforced by the state. Where Proudhon declared "property is theft," Grave pushes the argument further: private property "is accessible only to those who exploit their fellows," and "the history of humanity shows that this form of property was not that of the first human associations" [005-021].

## Influence on the Anarchist Movement

Proudhon's influence extended far beyond his own mutualist followers. His critique of the state anticipated Bakunin's revolutionary anti-statism, while his federalism prefigured [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]]'s vision of decentralized communes. [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]], who knew Proudhon personally in Paris during the 1840s, acknowledged that Proudhon was "the master of us all" in matters of federalist theory, even as he rejected mutualism's gradualism in favor of revolutionary insurrection.

The Paris Commune of 1871 — which occurred six years after Proudhon's death — was heavily influenced by his federalist ideas, with many Communards citing his works. His insistence that [[france|French]] workers must "prepare themselves intellectually" [003-010] for the revolutionary transformation of society became a central tenet of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own propagandist strategy: anarchist ideas must be disseminated widely and understood deeply before lasting social change is possible.

Proudhon's legacy in anarchist thought is thus twofold: he provided the theoretical foundation for the critique of property and the state, and he established that anarchism must be understood not as mere negation but as a positive doctrine of social organization — a vision of society organized through [[free-association|free association]] and [[mutual-aid|mutual aid]] rather than authority and exploitation.
