# Spain

## Overview

Spain is referenced by Jean Grave in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* in two distinct contexts: as a historical example of colonial conquest and brutality in the Americas, and as one of the European countries where anarchist ideas and propagandist organs had been established. These references reflect Spain's dual significance to the nineteenth-century anarchist imagination: the Spanish conquest of the Americas stood as the archetypal example of "civilized" barbarism, while the Spanish anarchist movement of the late nineteenth century was one of the largest and most militant in Europe.

## In Grave's Text

### Spanish Colonialism as Civilized Savagery

Grave invokes Spain in his critique of colonization, calling upon readers to "take the history of the conquests in America by Spain" alongside those of India by England and Africa by France, and then "boast about civilization" [488-488]. The Spanish conquests serve as the primary exemplar of the savagery of supposedly "civilized" nations against inoffensive indigenous peoples. The choice of Spain is not arbitrary: the Spanish conquest of the Americas, beginning with Columbus's voyages in 1492 and continuing through the destruction of the Aztec and Inca empires by Cortés and Pizarro, was the original template for European colonial violence.

> Take the history of the conquests in America by Spain, of India by England, of Africa, Cochin China, and Tonkin by France, and then boast about "civilization." [488-488]

Grave's rhetorical strategy is to turn the colonial narrative on its head. The colonizers claimed to be bringing "[[civilization]]" to "savage" peoples. Grave asks: who were the real savages — those who welcomed their invaders with open arms, or those who tortured and decimated them? He notes that the historical record preserves only "the 'great events' whose importance has left traces," but behind these lie "all the 'little events'" — the daily brutalities, the unrecorded atrocities — that make up the true texture of colonial rule. If these were brought to light, Grave insists, "you would recoil affrighted before these horrors!" [488-488].

This passage is significant because it ties Grave's anti-colonial argument to a specific historical tradition. The "Black Legend" — the critique of Spanish colonial cruelty that circulated in Protestant Europe from the sixteenth century onward — had been used by England and France to justify their own imperial projects as more "humane" alternatives. Grave refuses this self-serving distinction: England's conquest of India and France's conquest of Africa and Indochina are no different in kind from Spain's conquest of the Americas. The common denominator is the violence of "[[civilization]]" itself.

### Spain and the International Anarchist Movement

In his survey of the spread of anarchist ideas, Grave lists Spain among the countries where "groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created" [044-044]. This brief mention understates the actual scope and significance of the Spanish anarchist movement, which by the 1890s was one of the largest in the world.

> Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian — a little everywhere and in all idioms. [044-044]

The Spanish anarchist movement had deep roots. Bakunin's emissary Giuseppe Fanelli had visited Spain in 1868, and the Spanish sections of the First International had become overwhelmingly anarchist in orientation, rejecting the centralizing politics of Marx's General Council. By the 1870s and 1880s, Spanish anarchism had established a strong base among Andalusian peasants and Catalan industrial workers — a mass movement that survived waves of repression, including the notorious *Mano Negra* (Black Hand) trials of 1883–84, in which the authorities fabricated a secret terrorist society to justify the mass arrest and execution of anarchist militants.

Grave further includes Spain in his enumeration of places where anarchists share a common program: "Whether in [[france|France]], Spain, [[italy|Italy]], [[england|England]], [[america|America]], or [[australia|Australia]], the Anarchists want the abolition of [[private-property|private property]], the destruction of [[authority|authority]], complete autonomy of the individual without any restriction" [630-630]. This framing emphasizes the transnational unity of the anarchist movement: Spanish anarchists, no less than their French, Italian, and American comrades, sought not national reform but the universal abolition of the authoritarian and proprietarian order.

### Historical Context: Anarchism in Spain

Spain's anarchist movement during Grave's era (the 1890s) was notable for several distinctive features. First, it had a strong rural base — anarchist ideas resonated powerfully among landless peasants in Andalusia, who saw in anarchist communism a vision of the land redistribution that parliamentary liberalism had failed to deliver. Second, it developed an organizational form — the affinity group and the federation — that was deeply adapted to the conditions of repression, allowing the movement to survive despite periodic waves of state violence. Third, Spanish anarchism was characterized by a distinctive fusion of millenarian religious sentiment with radical anti-clericalism: the anarchist vision of a transformed society drew on popular Catholic notions of justice and redemption even as it attacked the institutional Church as an instrument of class rule.

This movement would continue to grow after the publication of Grave's book, reaching its apogee during the Spanish Civil War (1936–39), when anarchist militias and collectivized industries demonstrated — for a brief, tragic period — the practical viability of anarchist social organization. The CNT (Confederación Nacional del Trabajo), the anarcho-syndicalist union federation founded in 1910, would eventually count over a million members, making it the largest anarchist organization in history.

## Significance

Spain occupies a dual position in Grave's argument. As a colonial power, it exemplifies the barbarism that European "[[civilization]]" has visited upon the rest of the world — a barbarism made invisible by the ideological equation of "civilization" with progress. As a site of anarchist organizing, it exemplifies the counter-force: the international movement that rejects both colonial violence and the statist politics that authorize it. The juxtaposition is telling: the same European civilization that produced the conquistador also produced the anarchist militant, and it is the anarchist — not the conquistador — who represents the genuine promise of human progress.
