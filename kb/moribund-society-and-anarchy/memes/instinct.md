In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] develops a nuanced account of human instinct that resists both the pessimistic view of human nature as irredeemably selfish and the optimistic view of humans as naturally good. For [[jean-grave|Grave]], instincts are neither fixed nor uniform — they are products of [[evolution|evolutionary]] history, shaped by the social environment, and capable of being "suppressed" or "developed" depending on the conditions of collective life [010-014]. The crucial question is not which instincts are "natural" but which instincts are cultivated by the existing social order and which would flourish under a different one.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s account of instinct reflects the influence of [[evolution|evolutionary]] thought on late nineteenth-century anarchism, particularly the work of [[peter-kropotkin|Peter Kropotkin]], whose *[[mutual-aid|Mutual Aid]]* (1902) argued that cooperation was as important a factor in evolution as competition. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s emphasis on "heredity" as the mechanism by which instincts are transmitted places him within the Lamarckian tradition that dominated French evolutionary thought before the modern synthesis — a tradition that made it easier to believe in the rapid transformation of human nature through changed conditions.

However, [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s central insight — that observed human behavior is a product of social institutions as much as of innate disposition, and that different institutions would elicit different behaviors — can be reformulated in terms compatible with contemporary evolutionary biology and [[science|social science]]. The question he poses — which instincts does a given social order cultivate, and which does it suppress? — remains vital for any serious inquiry into the relationship between human nature and social transformation.

## Grave's Analysis---

tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] rejects the idea that human beings are "a model of perfection." He acknowledges that man "is a sorry animal enough, who, when he is not crushing his fellows under foot in pursuit of his own desire, is crushing them with the very best intentions" [010-003]. This frank assessment of human imperfection is the starting point for his materialist analysis: if instincts are shaped by environment, then the problem is not human nature but the social institutions that amplify destructive instincts and suppress constructive ones.

> When men first became organized into societies they based these societies upon their instincts for struggle and mastery; and this explains why society is so badly constructed. [010-009]

The instincts for "struggle and mastery" were adaptive in certain phases of human development, but they have been institutionalized and perpetuated by [[authority]] and [[property]] long after they ceased to serve any collective purpose. Society "has remained bad" because "its authority resting in the hands of a minority, the latter have turned it to their own profit" [010-009].

> Man, then, like all other animals, is but the product of an evolution worked out under the influence of the environment in which he lives and the conditions of existence he is forced to submit to or combat; only, more than other animals, or at least in a higher degree, has he come to reason upon his origin, to formulate aspirations for his future. [004-006]

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], this means that instinct is never fully determining — human beings possess the capacity to reflect upon and reshape their own dispositions. The very fact that people "formulate aspirations" points to a human capacity that transcends mere instinct.

## The Evolutionary Origins of Instinct---
id: instinct
type: concept
label: "Instinct"
aliases: ["human instincts", "natural instincts", "social instincts"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, instinct represents the innate drives and dispositions that shape human behavior — including both the competitive instincts for struggle and mastery that sustain oppressive institutions, and the cooperative instincts for solidarity that make anarchist social organization possible."
tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces human instincts back to the earliest phases of social organization. The first human groups were formed not out of moral ideals but from material necessity:

> When man first began to group together with his fellows, he must still have been much more of an animal than a human being; ideas of morality and justice did not exist in him. Having had to struggle against other animals, against all nature, his first groups were formed out of the necessity for an association of forces, not by the desire for solidarity. [004-003]

These early associations were temporary and instrumental — "limited to the capture of the game to be hunted or the overthrow of the obstacle to be conquered" [004-003]. It was "only by thus practicing association that men were brought to understand its importance; and the societies thus formed continued to live and became permanent" [004-003].

Yet this evolutionary trajectory was not uniformly progressive. The same conditions that fostered cooperation also cultivated destructive impulses:

> But on the other hand this life of continual struggle could not help developing the sanguinary and despotic instinct in people. The weaker had to submit to the rule of the stronger when they did not serve the latter as food. [004-003]

[[jean-grave|Grave]] thus presents a dual inheritance: human beings carry both the "sanguinary and despotic instinct" born of endless conflict and the cooperative dispositions nurtured by mutual assistance. Neither set is more "natural" than the other; both are products of the same evolutionary history. The political question is which instincts will be reinforced by the social environment.

> The evolutional origin of man being admitted it becomes evident to all that, when the first thinking beings appeared upon the earth, there could no longer be any need of a tutelary providence to facilitate their development. [005-006]

## Solidarity as Evolved Instinct---

type: concept
label: "Instinct"
aliases: ["human instincts", "natural instincts", "social instincts"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, instinct represents the innate drives and dispositions that shape human behavior — including both the competitive instincts for struggle and mastery that sustain oppressive institutions, and the cooperative instincts for solidarity that make anarchist social organization possible."
tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is not entirely negative. Alongside the competitive instincts, human beings have developed a "sentiment of [[solidarity]]" — an instinct for cooperation that "he had already obeyed in founding the first groups" [010-009]. As the brain developed and the means of subsistence increased, this cooperative instinct grew stronger.

> But on the other hand this life of continual struggle could not help developing the sanguinary and despotic instinct in people. But when they commenced unconsciously to practice this other and higher vital law, assistance in struggle, heredity having developed in them the instinct of solidarity, they found themselves led to group themselves. [005-009]

This instinct for [[solidarity]] is not a moral ideal but a "higher vital law" — an evolutionary adaptation that proved itself in practice. [[jean-grave|Grave]] understands the transmission of instinct through [[evolution|heredity]]. When a cooperative practice is repeated across generations, it becomes "stored up in the brain by past generations" [005-007]. This Lamarckian framework — in which acquired characteristics are inherited — underlies his conviction that changed social conditions can produce lasting changes in human disposition.

> However "the struggle for existence" having begun as the sole law for individuals, to eat in order not to be eaten was their sole preoccupation. But when they commenced unconsciously to practice this other and higher vital law, *assistance in struggle*, heredity having developed in them the instincts of combativeness, of oppression towards the victim, (everything being a prey to man, even man himself) it follows according to all the evidence that this spirit of struggle and domination, stored up in the brain by past generations, sought to gain like precedence in the organized collectivity. [005-007]

Heredity transmits *both* the "instincts of combativeness" and the cooperative disposition — and the balance depends on which is reinforced by collective life. [[jean-grave|Grave]] also observes that the powerful exploit the cooperative instinct: religions "have carried it to the extreme of sacrifice, preaching charity and self-renunciation, and therein finding a new element for exploitation" [010-009]. The problem is not that humans lack cooperative instincts but that the institutions of present society systematically frustrate them:

> To what dreams of social reorganization, of plans for the happiness of humanity has the longing to live harmoniously with our fellows not given birth! But society was there, stifling with all its weight the good instincts of man, reviving in him his savage primitive egoism. [010-009]

## Instincts and Social Transformation---

---

The political implication of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is clear: if instincts are shaped by social conditions, then changing those conditions will change human behavior. "The pernicious influence of society upon the morals of the individual being discovered, it is easy to suppress the bad instincts and develop the good" [010-014]. This is not a naive faith in transformation but a recognition that what appears as "human nature" is largely a product of the institutions within which humans live.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s position occupies a middle ground between two extremes: the conservative claim that human selfishness makes anarchism impossible, and the utopian claim that humans are naturally good and merely need to be liberated. His answer is that instincts are real, powerful, and historically formed — and that a different social organization would cultivate different instincts. The task of [[anarchy|anarchism]] is to create the conditions in which the instinct of [[solidarity]] can triumph over the instincts of struggle and mastery.

> In spite of all the stimulus of evil surroundings, man has been able to develop aspirations towards solidarity, harmony, and justice; and even these good sentiments have been exploited by those who live at his expense. [010-012]

This is perhaps [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most hopeful observation: even under conditions designed to stimulate competitive instincts, human beings continue to develop cooperative aspirations. The persistence of these aspirations — despite systematic punishment and exploitation — suggests that the cooperative instinct represents the direction of human [[evolution]] itself.

## The Malleability of Instinct---

tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s environmental determinism raises a crucial question: if instincts are products of the environment, how can the environment ever be changed? The answer lies in his account of human agency as itself a product of evolutionary development. Human beings are not merely passive recipients of environmental influence but are "able to transform the environment" in which they live [010-001]. Over the course of evolution, humans have increasingly become agents of their own transformation:

> He has struggled to develop himself, and if he has contributed in a large measure to the transformation of the environment wherein he is situated, the latter has in turn influenced the customs he has adopted, his manner of living, thinking, and acting. [010-004]

This reciprocal relationship between human action and environmental influence provides the mechanism for deliberate social change. As people come to understand the causal relationship between social institutions and human behavior, they can act to transform those institutions — creating an environment that in turn cultivates different instincts.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] illustrates this point through his discussion of racial inequality debates. Different environmental conditions have "led to develop instincts and faculties which in turn have awakened others, and thus launched them upon the pathway of progress" [011-012]. Peoples living in conditions of ease, "having wherewith to satisfy their primary needs without work," may "very easily have failed to experience that quickening of faculties which continued to lie dormant" [011-012]. This demonstrates the formative power of environmental conditions upon instinct and capacity.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] also acknowledges the countervailing force of atavism — the re-emergence of ancestral traits. "That atavism may sometimes be more powerful than the faculty of perfectibility nobody denies; but such examples in nowise prove the imperfectibility of the race, since the same individuals subjected to European education did certainly, during that period of their existence, advance along the path marked out by their educators" [011-013].

## Instincts and the Objections to Anarchism---

tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] repeatedly confronts the objection that [[anarchy]] is impractical because of human nature. His opponents argue that "it is necessary to repress the evil instincts by which the rest of humanity are possessed" [020-005] and that "men need some tangible power to govern them" [022-001]. These objections rest on the assumption that competitive and destructive instincts are the fundamental and unalterable core of human nature.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s response inverts the burden of proof. If competitive instincts are reinforced by the existing social order, then the persistence of competition under that order proves nothing about what human beings would be like under different conditions. He points to the relative stability of social life as evidence that cooperative instincts are already powerful enough to partially counteract the destructive incentives built into existing institutions:

> We say that he must have strong tendencies to become good or society would get on worse than it does, and crimes and disasters would be of more frequent occurrence. [010-011]

If human beings were as irredeemably selfish as the pessimists claim, society — even a bad society — could not function at all. [[jean-grave|Grave]] also notes that even those who acknowledge anarchism's theoretical validity but call it impractical betray their own recognition of cooperative potential. Their "ideal vanishes to make room for the instinct towards the preservation of the existing order of things" [022-001] — a concession that what prevents change is not human nature but the comfort of the status quo.

## The Suppression and Survival of Instinct---
id: instinct
type: concept
label: "Instinct"
aliases: ["human instincts", "natural instincts", "social instincts"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, instinct represents the innate drives and dispositions that shape human behavior — including both the competitive instincts for struggle and mastery that sustain oppressive institutions, and the cooperative instincts for solidarity that make anarchist social organization possible."
tags: ["human-nature", "evolution", "solidarity", "struggle"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] pays particular attention to how [[authority|authoritarian]] institutions actively suppress rebellious instincts. His analysis of the military offers a vivid case: the soldier is threatened with death "whenever the instincts of rebellion begin buzzing beneath your skull" [013-012]. The military exemplifies the broader authoritarian project — to extinguish the natural human resistance to domination while extinguishing cooperative sentiments that would make domination unnecessary.

Yet even within the barracks, "instincts of rebellion" persist — they "buzz beneath the skull" despite the threat of death. The task of [[authority]] is never complete; it must constantly renew its efforts to suppress what it cannot eliminate. This is grounds for optimism: the persistence of rebellious and cooperative instincts under the most adverse conditions testifies to their depth and resilience. [[jean-grave|Grave]] also observes that even accidental propagandists of anarchist ideas are driven by "their secret sentiments and their instincts of justice and truth" [001-018] — instincts that no amount of institutional pressure has been able to extinguish.
