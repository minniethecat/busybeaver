# Consumption

## Definition

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, consumption appears primarily in the context of cooperative associations for production and consumption — a movement that flourished in France during the years following the Paris Commune of 1871. Jean Grave treats these cooperatives as a symptom of revolutionary energy diverted into reformist channels. For Grave, cooperative consumption societies represent a "misled and misleading movement" that fell short of the systemic transformation anarchism demands [042-001].

## Grave's Analysis

Grave situates the cooperative consumption movement within the broader history of anarchist development. After the defeat of the Commune, when direct revolutionary action was suppressed, many workers turned to cooperative associations as a means of economic self-help. Grave acknowledges this impulse as sincere but judges it as ultimately "impotent" — a solution that left the fundamental structures of capital and authority intact [042-001]. The cooperative movement, in his analysis, accepted the framework of the existing economic order rather than challenging its foundations.

Grave's treatment of consumption is inseparable from his analysis of production. He argues that the capitalist system distorts both: production is oriented toward profit rather than human need, and consumption is unequally distributed, with the possessing classes consuming far more than they produce while workers are denied the fruits of their labor. The anarchist alternative, as Grave envisions it, would reorganize both production and consumption on the basis of voluntary association and the principle "from each according to ability, to each according to need."
