In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] develops [[association|cooperation]] as a foundational principle of anarchist social organization. Cooperation, properly understood, is the [[association|voluntary association]] of free individuals pursuing common goals — a mode of social interaction fundamentally different from the coerced "[[association|cooperation]]" that characterizes capitalist production, where workers must collaborate under the employer's direction or face destitution [005-020]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes authentic [[association|cooperation]] from the reformist vision of cooperative societies within capitalism, which he regards as inadequate palliatives that leave the basic structure of exploitation unchanged [019-006].

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s treatment of [[association|cooperation]] reflects the debates within the [[france|French]] labor movement of the 1890s, where the cooperative movement — associated with figures like Charles Gide and the Nîmes school — offered a gradualist alternative to r[[progress|evolution]]ary syndicalism. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s position aligns with the r[[progress|evolution]]ary syndicalist rejection of cooperativism as a strategy for social transformation, while retaining the principle of voluntary cooperation as the basis for post-r[[progress|evolution]]ary social organization. This distinction between [[association|cooperation]] as r[[progress|evolution]]ary practice and cooperativism as reformist strategy would remain central to anarchist economic thought throughout the twentieth century.

## Grave's Analysis---

tags: ["economics", "social-organization", "solidarity", "mutual-aid"]
quality: standard
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of [[association|cooperation]] begins from a materialist understanding of human social [[progress|evolution]]. [[private-property|Property]], the family, and authority all developed "along parallel lines" as human beings "united their efforts under the pressure of a common need, of some obstacle to be overcome against which individual efforts had exhausted themselves" [006-002]. Cooperation is thus not a moral ideal imposed from without but a practical necessity arising from the conditions of human existence.

> On the other hand it is certain that the capitalists, with all their capital, all their machinery, could produce nothing without the cooperation of the workers, whilst the latter, by coming to an understanding among themselves and uniting their forces, could very easily dispense with the capitalists. [005-020]

This passage encapsulates [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s strategy of immanent critique: the capitalist class presents itself as the indispensable organizer of production, but in reality, it is the workers whose [[association|cooperation]] makes production possible. [[private-property|Capital]] is parasitic on labor; remove the parasite, and [[association|cooperation]] among workers can sustain and enhance social production.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] emphasizes that authentic [[association|cooperation]] must be voluntary. An association "having nothing [[force-and-violence|force]]d in it, nothing arbitrary, prompted only by some need of our being" is fundamentally different from the compulsory [[association|cooperation]] of the factory floor [002-013]. The more pressing a need, the more [[force-and-violence|force]]ful the motivation to cooperate, but this urgency must express itself through free choice, not external compulsion.

## Cooperation vs. Cooperatives---
id: cooperation
type: concept
label: "Cooperation"
aliases: ["mutual cooperation", "voluntary cooperation", "cooperative production"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, cooperation represents the voluntary association of individuals for mutual benefit, distinguished sharply from the coerced 'cooperation' of capitalist production — where workers must cooperate under the employer's command or starve — and from reformist cooperative schemes that leave the fundamental structure of exploitation intact."
tags: ["economics", "social-organization", "solidarity", "mutual-aid"]
quality: standard
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is careful to distinguish anarchist [[association|cooperation]] from the reformist cooperative movement of his time. The formation of syndicates and their "transformation into cooperative societies for production" represents, in his view, "all the reform baggage of those who seek to transform society by [[progress|evolution]]" — and "in quantity it is meager" [019-006]. Cooperative enterprises operating within a capitalist market are subject to the same competitive pressures as any other business; they may ameliorate conditions for their members but they cannot transform the system.

The anarchist vision of [[association|cooperation]] is more radical: the abolition of the [[wage-labor|wage system]] itself, the dissolution of the capitalist class, and the reorganization of production on the basis of [[association|free association]] and common ownership. In such a society, "each sharing the necessary labor," work would cease to be a "torture" inflicted on a minority and become a shared responsibility of all [010-015].
