# Police

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, the police are the visible, everyday face of class power — the institution that translates the abstract authority of [[the-law|law]] into the concrete [[force-and-violence|violence]] of arrest, baton, and cell. They are not, as official ideology would have it, the impartial guardians of public order but the en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment arm of a social order built on exploitation. The police exist because "those who possess need some [[force-and-violence|force]] to protect them in the secure enjoyment of their privileges and to put down those who have nothing" [007-012].

## Police as Class Enforcers

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of the police is embedded in his broader critique of [[the-state|the government]]al apparatus. The police, the magistracy, and [[militarism|the army]] form a unified system of class en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment. The police "maintain the decisions of the magistracy" not because those decisions are just but because the magistracy serves the same class interests the police exist to protect [007-012]. The chain of command — from property-owner to legislator to magistrate to police officer — is a chain of class solidarity.

> In fact there is a necessity for government, for laws, for deputies to make these laws and a magistracy to apply them, for a police-force to maintain the decisions of the magistracy, because those who possess need some force to protect them in the secure enjoyment of their privileges. [007-012]

The function of the police is revealed not in their [[the-state|state]]d mission but in their actual operation. They do not equally protect all citizens — they protect property. The poor person who calls the police to report a theft by a rich employer will find the police uninterested; the rich person who calls the police to report a theft by a poor employee will find them prompt and efficient.

## The Police Courts and Class Justice

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends his critique to the operation of police courts, where the class character of law enforcement is most nakedly visible. He observes that "every person who is tried for 'outrages' or 'resistance' against the police, is always advised by his [[the-law|law]]yer not to deny the accusation, even though it be false" [020-025]. The deck is stacked so completely against the accused that the only practical advice is to confess and hope for leniency.

> It is notorious among those who frequent police courts that every person who is tried for "outrages" or "resistance" against the police, is always advised by his lawyer not to deny the accusation, even though it be false, for fear of exasperating the magistrate. [020-025]

"The maximum penalty is always given to those who are bold enough to contradict the conclusions of the magistrate, or the testimony of the police" [020-025]. The police officer's word is taken as truth; the defendant's word is taken as obstruction. This is not a malfunction of the system but its designed operation. The courts exist to legitimate police [[force-and-violence|violence]], not to constrain it.

## The Police and the Social Order

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of the police is not reformist. He is not arguing for better training, more accountability, or community oversight. Such reforms, however well-intentioned, miss the fundamental point: the police exist to en[[force-and-violence|force]] a social order based on property, and that function cannot be reformed away. As long as society is divided into possessors and dispossessed, there will be a [[force-and-violence|force]] to keep the dispossessed in their place.

The anarchist alternative is not a better police force but a social order in which police are unnecessary — a society in which property is held in common, production is organized for need, and social conflicts are resolved through [[association|free association]] rather than armed en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment. The abolition of police is not a demand for chaos but a demand for a society that does not require armed guardians of inequality.

> From the moment it should perceive that universal suffrage would tend to take its authority away, its bureaucracy, magistracy, police, and army would immediately be put in motion to arrest the destroyers of their privileges. [020-009]

This passage captures the essential insight: all the institutions of [[government|the state]] — police, magistracy, army, bureaucracy — will resist any genuine challenge to their power, regardless of the democratic forms through which that challenge is expressed. The police are not neutral instruments awaiting democratic direction; they are active defenders of the class order that sustains them.
