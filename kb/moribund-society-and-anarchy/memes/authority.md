# Authority

## Definition

Authority is, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist framework, the coercive power that "claims to justify its existence by the necessity of defending social institutions — the family, religion, property, etc." (001-002). It creates "a mass of tools to secure it in the exercise of its functions and its sanctioning privilege," including "the [[the-law|law]], the magistracy, [[militarism|the army]], the legislative and executive power" (001-002).

## Historical Examples of Authority's Failures

### The French Revolution and the Erased Anarchists

[[jean-grave|Grave]] locates a crucial precedent in the French Revolution of 1793, when "the Anarchists were much talked of" (001-004). He identifies [[jacques-roux|Jacques Roux]] and "the madmen" (*les enrages*) as those who, during the Revolution, "saw most clearly, and sought the best means of turning it to the benefit of the people" (001-004). Yet their contribution has been systematically erased from historical memory: "the bourgeois historians have left them in the shade. Their history is still to be written; the documents buried in the archives and the libraries still await him who shall have the time and the courage to dig them up, bring them to light, and reveal the secret of things still very incomprehensible to us, in that tragic period of history" (001-004). This erasure is not accidental — it reveals how authority, even in ostensibly revolutionary moments, works to silence those who threaten not merely the personnel of power but the principle of power itself. The Thermidorian reaction that crushed the popular movement was not a betrayal of the Revolution but the logical operation of a revolution that had only changed the face of authority rather than abolishing it.

### The Pattern of Revolutionary Betrayal

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a recurring historical pattern that spans modern revolutions. The workers, deceived into believing that "by changing the men in power they could change the system of exploitation which oppresses us," are led into a "profound error, which allows the intriguing to lead the workers astray, in pursuit of illusory reforms, incapable of bringing about any change in their situation, and accustoming them to expect everything from a change of personnel in this machine for oppression called the State" (007-008). This error, in every revolution, "has permitted schemers to juggle away popular victories, to install themselves in the sinecures of those who have been swept away by the revolutionary tempest, and to form a new caste of exploiters by creating around them new interests, which, once established, have succeeded in imposing their authority, reducing to silence those who had had the naivete to carry them to the pinnacle of power" (007-008).

### The Cooperative Movement After the Commune

After the [[paris-commune|Paris Commune]], the anarchist movement in France "foundered in the misled and misleading movement of cooperative associations for production and consumption" (001-004). This was not a failure of anarchist ideas but of the attempt to seek emancipation through organizational forms that left the structure of [[private-property|private property]] and [[the-state|state authority]] intact. The cooperative movement, by operating within rather than against existing institutions, could not escape the gravitational pull of the system it sought to reform. This historical experiment confirmed for [[jean-grave|Grave]] that partial solutions that do not attack the institutional bases of society are doomed to absorption and neutralization.

### The Boulangist Episode

[[jean-grave|Grave]] also invokes the more recent example of the [[boulangism|Boulangist movement]], which "became abortive only through the stupidity and cowardice of its leaders; but where they failed others may succeed" (018-001). The Boulangist episode demonstrated how widespread discontent — the same discontent that fuels revolutionary movements — could be channeled by authoritarian personalities into movements that ultimately reinforce rather than destroy the existing order. The failure was not in the depth of popular dissatisfaction but in the absence of a clearly articulated anarchist alternative.

### The Dreyfus Affair

Though [[jean-grave|Grave]] was himself imprisoned for two years for the chapter on [[militarism]] in this very book, the [[dreyfus-affair|Dreyfus affair]] that subsequently unfolded served as a historical confirmation of his central thesis. As the translator [[voltairine-de-cleyre|Voltairine de Cleyre]] notes, "the famous Zola trial has given a farther demonstration of the government's intention to make Dreyfus the scapegoat of its Judases in high places" (endnote 10). The military hierarchy, the [[the-magistracy|magistracy]], and the executive power — the very "mass of tools" (001-002) that authority creates to secure itself — conspired together to protect institutional prestige at the cost of justice. For [[jean-grave|Grave]], this was not an aberration but the predictable operation of an authority system that must defend its own legitimacy above all else.

### The Empirical Record of Reform

The empirical method that [[jean-grave|Grave]] invokes in later chapters compiles the accumulated evidence of authority's failures across centuries. For "three or four thousand years, for the Latin races only, these experiments and step-by-step policies have been going on," yet "the exploited have awaited the realization of the promises made to them, have waxed passionate and struggled to obtain the same reforms forever promised though their situation has in nowise altered" (020-013). The pattern is invariant: "Often their masters have been compelled to make room for them in legislative halls; often they have had to grant the reforms demanded; very often they themselves have been compelled to put restrictions upon their exploitations and authority. Have the governed been less oppressed, the exploited less squeezed? Has not poverty been as intense as ever, wealth concentrated in the hands of a privileged few, day by day becoming fewer?" (020-013). The empirical record demonstrates conclusively that reforms that do not abolish authority merely perpetuate exploitation under new forms.

## Grave's Analysis

[[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that authority "has but one excuse for existence: the defense of capital" (001-010). He maintains that all the apparatus of [[government|the state]] flows from the need to protect private property and the privileges of the [[class|ruling class]]. The anarchist critique of authority is not directed at individuals in power but at the institution itself: anarchists teach "that not only must the people not be contented with changing the persons in power, with partially modifying the institutions which govern them, but that, before all, the people must destroy that which makes men bad" (001-010).

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is especially critical of those who seek reforms through existing power structures. He argues that asking [[government|the state]] to intervene in social relations is to "give proof of the greatest want of logic, for its interference can turn out profitably only to him whose defender it is" (019-004). The [[the-state|state]] cannot be reformed into an instrument of [[emancipation|liberation]] because its very nature is to defend the existing order.

> Bureaucracy, the family, the army, the magistracy, flow directly from private property. The work of the Anarchists, then, has been to demonstrate the iniquity of the monopoly of the soil and of the fruits of the labor of past generations, by an idle minority; to sap authority by showing it to be detrimental to human development. (001-010)

## Grave's Critique of Authority

### Universal Suffrage as a Perfected Instrument of Authority

The transition from divine right to popular sovereignty did not, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, diminish the coercive power of authority — it refined it. Having destroyed the basis of divine right themselves, the [[bourgeoisie]] sought to substitute the right of money, first through property qualifications for voting, then through the quit-rent regime. When the workers revolted to obtain universal suffrage, the bourgeois members they carried into power were "eager to trick them out of this newly-acquired right" (007-001). Grave's verdict is devastating: universal suffrage is "but a perfected instrument of authority, which enslaved those who made use of it at the very moment they expected to emancipate themselves" (007-001).

What is universal suffrage, [[jean-grave|Grave]] asks:

> What is universal suffrage, if not the right of the governed to choose their master, the right of choosing the rod to be whipped with? The voter is sovereign — so far as to be able to choose his master! But he has not the right to dispense with him" (007-002). The moment the voter deposits his ballot, "he has signed his abdication; he has no more to do but bend to the caprices of the masters of his choice; they will make the laws, will apply them to him, and throw him into prison if he resist. (007-002)

### The Illusion of Majority Rule

> Authority has but one excuse for existence: the defense of capital. All the apparatus of the state flows from the need to protect private property and the privileges of the ruling class. The anarchist critique of authority is not directed at individuals in power but at the institution itself. (001-010)


[[jean-grave|Grave]] unmasks the pretense of majority rule through a rigorous step-by-step analysis that reveals universal suffrage as an elaborate system of minority domination. First, women and children — who submit equally to the laws — are arbitrarily excluded from voting. Of those eligible, many do not vote, reducing the electorate to "a first minority, recognized most arbitrarily as the only ones fit to choose masters for all" (007-003). Second, with votes divided among six, eight, ten, or more candidates, the winner emerges from "a second minority" (007-003). Third, within the legislative assembly, opinions are divided into "groups and subgroups innumerable," such that "small cliques of ambitious persons, standing between the extreme parties, decide the vote by lending their voices to those that offer them the most for it" (007-003). When the legislative process is further diluted by a second chamber, the senate, and when each voter's ballot represents less than one five-hundredth of the general will, "individual sovereignty enters in so infinitesimal a quantity into the national sovereignty that at last we do not find it at all" (007-003).

### The Production of Mediocrity

Perhaps [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most original contribution to the critique of authority is his argument that universal suffrage systematically selects for mediocrity. Every new idea that is in advance of its epoch is, by the very fact of such advance, "always in the minority at the start" (007-004). The bulk of the masses professes average current ideas; it is they who compose the majority and who will choose the representative. To be elected, a candidate must "take good care not to offend the prejudices of his constituents or to shock received opinions" and must "round off his sharp corners and select a stock of commonplaces to get off before those whose suffrages he covets" (007-004). The conclusion is stark: "The more flat, mediocre, and insipid he is, the more chance he has of being elected" (007-004).

This principle extends beyond formal politics to all hierarchic organizations — committees, congresses, associations — where "the offices in these hierarchic organizations, elected by universal suffrage, held by persons who, setting aside their ambitious desire of showing themselves off, getting themselves talked about, or creating a situation at the expense of their colleagues, and a certain capacity for intrigue, are the most mediocre of the lot" (007-005). The original mind, who "seeks the truth and would make it prevail, has no time to stoop to the shabby wire-pulling behind the scenes. He will surely be beaten in the electoral lists by him who, having no original ideas, accepting those received by the greater number, will have less trouble in insinuating his projecting angles (which he has not) in a manner to offend no one" (007-005).

### The Habit of Submission

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a profound psychological paradox at the heart of authority's persistence. Though authority is "discussed, jeered at, lashed, it is, unfortunately, far from having disappeared from our customs" (007-006). People submit to it with reluctance, scoff at it, violate it when they can, yet "believe themselves lost when anyone talks of doing away with it" (007-006). They have grown so accustomed to "the gendarme's cap, the belted paunch of the mayor, the meddling and official insolence of the bureaucracy, the sorry-looking countenances of judge and policeman" appearing "in their lives at every turn, that they have reached the point of becoming accustomed to these filthy promiscuities, considering them as things which are certainly disagreeable... but which they cannot imagine disappearing without humanity's being dislocated at once" (007-006). This contradiction is compounded by the fact that "the ideal of each individual in regard to 'good' government, is to have one which he would have the chance to cashier the moment it tried to prevent him from acting as he pleased" (007-007).

### The Logical Contradiction of Authority

[[jean-grave|Grave]] exposes a further logical flaw in the defense of authority. Those who argue that government is necessary "to keep people straight" are making an incoherent claim: "taken in a lump, people are too bad to come to an understanding among themselves, but... taken individually or in fractions, they know how to govern others" (007-009). If human beings are incapable of self-government, on what basis could any subset of them be qualified to govern everyone else? The rationalization collapses under its own weight.

### The Fundamental Dilemma

[[jean-grave|Grave]] poses an inescapable dilemma to all defenders of authority. Regardless of the name in which a new authority clothes itself, however benign it may seek to appear, whatever amendments it may propose, whatever be the mode of recruiting its personnel:

> Either its decisions will have the force of law and be obligatory upon all, in which case all our existing institutions will be needed to apply them and enforce respect for them — hence renunciation of liberty — or people will remain free to discuss governmental decisions, conform to them if they please, or send authority hunting a job if it annoys them — in which case liberty remains intact, but the government is useless though remaining a fetter and a menace! (007-012)

The conclusion follows inexorably: **No government** (007-013).

## The Anarchist Alternative: Organizing Without Authority

### Natural Guidance Without Command

[[jean-grave|Grave]] anticipates the perennial objection that complex social organization requires authority by drawing a crucial distinction between natural guidance and hierarchical command. He uses an everyday analogy: "When we go out in camping parties with a number of friends, for example, and put ourselves under the guidance of one of our company having better knowledge of the spot selected, does it follow that we have made him our master and that we are bound to follow him blindly everywhere he pleases to lead us? Do we give him the power to constrain us in case we should refuse to follow him? No" (017-009). The group follows the guide because they suppose him capable of leading them where they all want to go — but "we have in no sense abdicated our own will and initiative" (017-009). If another member perceives the guide is mistaken, they make use of their initiative to inform the group and suggest a different route. This distinction — between voluntarily following someone's superior knowledge in a specific domain and surrendering one's will to an authority — is fundamental to the anarchist conception of [[free-association|free association]].

### The Rejection of Temporary Abdication

Some anarchists and fellow travelers argue that while authority may be undesirable in normal times, it is necessary during revolutionary periods to coordinate action effectively. [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies this as a dangerous "remnant of the prejudices with which we have been imbued, atavistic return begotten of our education, which makes us, while proclaiming liberty with loud voices, recoil before its consequences and deny its efficacy, and leads us to demand authority in order to obtain — liberty! O inconsistency!" (017-008).

The anarchist principle is unambiguous: "In Anarchy those who know teach those who do not know; the first to conceive an idea puts it into practice, explaining it to those whom he wishes to interest in it. But there is no temporary abdication, no authority; there are only equals who mutually aid each other according to their respective faculties, abandoning none of their rights, no part of their autonomy" (017-010). [[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a vivid analogy to child-rearing: "Did you ever see anybody begin by fettering the legs of a child that he wanted to teach to walk?" (017-009). The best means of becoming free is to practice liberty, not to defer it.

### Decentralized Revolutionary Action

The anarchist approach to revolutionary struggle flows directly from the rejection of authority. Rather than forming armies and engaging in pitched battles — which inherently require command structures and hierarchies — anarchists advocate a fundamentally different mode of action. The struggle must be directed "chiefly towards the destruction of institutions" (017-009). This means "the burning up of deeds, registers of land-surveys, proceedings of notaries and solicitors, tax-collectors' books; the ignoring of the limits of holdings, destruction of the regulations of the civil staff, etc.; the expropriation of the capitalists, taking possession in the name of all, putting articles of consumption freely at the disposal of all" (017-009).

This is "the work of small and scattered groups, of skirmishes, not regular battles" — warfare designed to "harass governments, compel them to scatter their forces; tire them out and decimate them piecemeal" (017-009). Crucially, "no need of leaders for blows like these; as soon as someone realizes what should be done he preaches by example, acting so as to attract others to him, who follow him if they are partisans of the enterprise but do not, by the fact of their adherence, abdicate their own initiative in following him who seems most fit to direct the enterprise" (017-009). Initiative can and must shift fluidly among participants as circumstances change.

### The Principle of Self-Emancipation

At the foundation of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s alternative to authority is a profound commitment to individual [[autonomy]] and initiative. He argues that "the uplifting of human dignity and character, independence of the will which makes us resent command, makes us rebels against despotism and repudiate what seems false and absurd to us" (017-011) is itself the true engine of social transformation. This is not a utopian fantasy but the logical consequence of anarchist principles: if one wants a thing well done, one must do it oneself and delegate it to nobody; if one wishes to be free, one must commission no one to direct them. "The surest means of making Anarchy triumph is to act like an Anarchist" (017-010).

### The Question of Practicability

To the perennial objection that anarchist ideas are "all right in theory, but they are not practicable" — that "men need some tangible power to govern them and force them to respect the social contract" (022-001) — [[jean-grave|Grave]] offers a historically grounded counter-argument. "If the first thinkers who fought the Church and the monarchy on behalf of natural ideas and independence; who faced the executioner and the scaffold in order to proclaim these, had said 'it is not possible,' while dreaming of their ideal, we should, today, still be bound by mystical conceptions and seignorial rights" (022-002). He invokes the philosopher [[jean-marie-guyau|Guyau]]'s principle from *Outlines of a Morality Without Authority or Duty*: "He who does not act as he thinks, thinks incompletely" (022-002). When one is thoroughly convinced of an idea, it is impossible not to seek to spread it and endeavor to realize it.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] fully acknowledges that anarchist ideas are not immediately realizable — "the number of persons who have understood them is yet too small a minority to exercise any immediate influence upon events" (022-005). But this is not an argument against them; it is an argument for intensified propagation and practice. "The greater the intensity of the propaganda the nearer the hour of realization. It is not by yielding to existing institutions that we shall do battle with them, nor yet by hiding our light under a bushel" (022-005).

### Organization Through Free Understanding

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is careful to specify that the rejection of authority is not a rejection of organization. Individual action "does not exclude a common understanding where collective action is in question; that organization follows from this understanding — a sort of division of labor rendering every individual interdependent upon the others, impelling him to adapt his actions to those of his companions in struggle or in production" (017-009). The crucial difference is that this organization emerges from free agreement among equals rather than being imposed by a command structure. It is an organization "so much the more durable that it is the result of practice, and not of a factitious understanding made up of concessions" (018-003).

In a [[revolution|revolutionary]] context, [[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that the anarchist method must prefigure its goal. The means must be adapted to the end, "under pain of producing the exact contrary of one's expectations" (017-014). An authoritarian revolution cannot produce an anarchist society. Only by acting as anarchists — rejecting all temporary abdication, all delegation of will, all surrender of initiative — can anarchists hope to bring about anarchy. The means and the ends are inseparable.
