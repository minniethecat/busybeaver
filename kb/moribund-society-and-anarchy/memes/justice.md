# Justice

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist framework, "justice" as an institution of the bourgeois [[the-state|state]] is revealed through analysis to be something quite different from the ideal it claims to embody. Justice, one savant acknowledged, "is but a figment, and is, in fact, nothing but revenge exercised by society, which substitutes itself for the individual wronged" [009-014]. It is not an impartial weighing of right and wrong but a mechanism for enforcing the class order — protecting the property of the rich, punishing the offenses of the poor, and providing ideological cover for the structural [[force-and-violence|violence]] of exploitation.

## Justice as Class Instrument

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most powerful critique of justice is empirical rather than philosophical. He examines what [[the-magistracy|the courts]] actually do — what crimes they punish, what criminals they protect — and finds a systematic class bias. The most antisocial and most common crimes are attacks upon property, yet the [[the-law|law]] itself "develops the most" the "lust for gain, the passion for money" that produces these crimes [009-015]. The system that creates the conditions for crime then punishes the criminals it has created.

Meanwhile, the far greater [[force-and-violence|violence]] committed by the [[class|ruling class]]es — the exploitation that "exhausts entire generations, crushing them with overwork, cutting down the working-life of the laborer" — is not merely unpunished but legally protected [009-016]. The thief who steals a loaf of bread faces the full severity of the [[the-law|law]]; the capitalist who steals the product of a lifetime's labor is celebrated as a pillar of society.

> Your justice cannot find rigors enough for the thieves in rags, but it protects those who operate upon a class, an entire nation! [009-016]

## The Illusion of Impartiality

The claim that justice is blind — that it weighs each case on its merits without regard to the station of the accused — is belied by every aspect of the judicial system. The [[the-law|law]] is written by and for the possessing classes; the [[the-magistracy|magistrates]] who interpret it are drawn from and identify with those classes; the police who en[[force-and-violence|force]] it "maintain the decisions of the magistracy, because those who possess need some [[force-and-violence|force]] to protect them in the secure enjoyment of their privileges" [007-012]. From [[the-law|legislation]] to en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment, the justice system is structured to serve the interests of property.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends this critique to the philosophical foundations of justice. The doctrine of free will, on which the moral condemnation of criminals depends, has been demolished by science. If human conduct is determined by "environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences," then the notion that a criminal "deserves" punishment is as unscientific as the notion that a disease "deserves" to afflict its victim [009-002]. The justice system's pretense of moral judgment conceals a system of social control.

## The Ideal of Justice and Its Co-optation

[[jean-grave|Grave]] does not reject the concept of justice itself — he recognizes that "ideas of morality, of justice, of solidarity have developed" and "have so much weight that the privileged, to succeed in maintaining their privileges, are obliged to take them into account" [004-009]. The aspiration toward justice is a genuine human achievement. But this aspiration has been co-opted by the institutions that claim to embody it.

The ideal justice — "to each according to his needs" — is "that which each regards from his own point of view, according to the condition in which he is placed" [009-018]. The [[class|ruling class]]es see justice as the protection of property; the workers see it as the satisfaction of needs. There is no neutral ground between these perspectives, and the justice system — funded by the rich, staffed by the privileged, enforcing [[the-law|law]]s written by property-owners — predictably adopts the former.

## Beyond Bourgeois Justice

The anarchist project does not seek to perfect the justice system but to abolish the conditions that require it. When property is held in common and production is organized for need rather than profit, the crimes that fill [[the-magistracy|the courts]] — theft, fraud, [[force-and-violence|violence]] born of desperation — will largely disappear. The aspirations towards solidarity, harmony, and justice already present in humanity, visible "in spite of all the stimulus of evil [[environment|surroundings]]," will no longer be "punished as subversive of the social order" but given free expression [010-013]. Justice, in the anarchist vision, is not administered by courts but embodied in social relations.
