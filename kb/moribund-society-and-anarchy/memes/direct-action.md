# Direct Action

## Definition

Direct action, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s framework, is the anarchist principle that meaningful social change comes not from petitioning authority but from acting independently of it. It is the "action" that is "the flowering of thought" — the practical expression of ideas that refuse to wait for permission from above [017-015]. Direct action encompasses a spectrum of practices: from the individual refusal to obey unjust [[the-law|law]]s, to the collective organization of cooperative enterprises, to the r[[progress|evolution]]ary transformation of society itself. Its unifying thread is the rejection of [[press|media]]tion by representatives, officials, or institutions of authority.

## Propaganda by Deed

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies "propaganda by deed" as a specific form of direct action — the use of exemplary acts to communicate anarchist ideas and inspire emulation [001-009]. This concept, associated with the late nineteenth-century anarchist movement, holds that actions speak louder than pamphlets: a single cooperative workshop, a successful strike, an act of resistance to authority, can do more to spread anarchist ideas than volumes of theory.

> They begin to understand and proclaim action, propaganda by deed; that is to say, rebellion against authority, the refusal to obey unjust laws, the organization of resistance against exploitation. [001-009]

[[propaganda|Propaganda]] by deed is not, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], synonymous with [[force-and-violence|violence]]. It is the principle that ideas must be realized in practice — that "the best means of becoming free" is "to make use of liberty, acting up to the best of its inspirations, rejecting the tutelage of no matter whom" [017-010]. The insistence on action reflects a deeper epistemological commitment: truth is demonstrated, not merely asserted.

## Revolution and Direct Action

[[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes between r[[progress|evolution]]ary direct action and reformist [[reform|gradualism]]. The reformer seeks to modify existing institutions through legal channels — to "make the penalty fit the crime" within the existing framework [001-009]. The anarchist, by contrast, recognizes that institutions "flow directly from private property" and cannot be genuinely transformed while their foundation remains intact [001-010]. Direct action addresses root causes, not symptoms.

> Now, as to action our position is this: We have already said that action is the flowering of thought; but furthermore this action must have an aim, we must know what it is about, it must tend towards a definite end. [017-015]

This last point is crucial: direct action is not impulsive or directionless. It is "action with an aim" — conscious, purposeful intervention in the social world oriented toward a clear vision of emancipation. The anarchist does not act for the sake of acting but to bring about specific transformations in the organization of social life.

## Methods and Divergences

[[jean-grave|Grave]] acknowledges that anarchists diverge on questions of method. "There is this divergence among Anarchists in the way of looking upon methods of action" — some are "more carried away by temperament than controlled by principles" [018-002]. But this diversity of views does not undermine the shared commitment to direct action as the means of social transformation. What unites anarchists across tactical differences is the recognition that "you must commission no one to direct you" — that [[emancipation|freedom]] cannot be delegated and emancipation cannot be outsourced [001-012].

The insistence on direct action is also a refusal of the distinction between means and ends. If the goal is a society of free and equal individuals governing their own affairs through [[cooperation|voluntary cooperation]], then the means must prefigure that goal. One cannot create [[emancipation|freedom]] through authoritarian methods, just as one cannot "begin by fettering the legs" of someone one claims to be teaching to walk [017-010].
