# Evolution

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Grave]] deploys the language of **[[progress|evolution]]** in two interrelated senses. First, drawing on the natural sciences of his era, he invokes biological [[progress|evolution]] as the process through which humanity emerged from animality — a process that demonstrates that living beings are products of their environment rather than fixed essences. Second, he applies the [[progress|evolution]]ary framework to society itself, arguing that social institutions are not eternal but historical products, subject to transformation. This dual usage serves a strategic purpose: it undermines conservative claims that existing society is "natural" while simultaneously arguing that r[[progress|evolution]], not gradual reform, is the appropriate mechanism for social transformation.

> Man, then, like all other animals, is but the product of an evolution worked out under the influence of the environment in which he lives and the conditions of existence he is forced to submit to or combat. (004-010)

## Historical Context

### Darwin, Spencer, and the Evolutionary Paradigm

[[jean-grave|Grave]] wrote at the height of [[progress|evolution]]ary thinking's cultural influence. Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859) and *The Descent of Man* (1871) had transformed biology; Herbert Spencer had attempted to systematize [[progress|evolution]] as a universal [[the-law|law]] covering society as well as nature. [[jean-grave|Grave]] draws on this framework critically — accepting [[progress|evolution]] as a scientific fact while rejecting Spencerian social Darwinism, which used "survival of the fittest" to justify competitive capitalism.

### Letourneau and Evolutionary Sociology

[[jean-grave|Grave]] cites [[charles-letourneau|Charles Letourneau]] extensively, particularly his work on the [[progress|evolution]] of social institutions. Letourneau's [[progress|evolution]]ary sociology provided [[jean-grave|Grave]] with a scientific vocabulary for arguing that property, the family, [[the-law|law]], and [[government|the state]] are not eternal but historically developed — and therefore can be historically superseded.

### The Evolutionary Framework in French Anarchism

[[jean-grave|Grave]] was part of a broader current in late-nineteenth-century [[france|French]] anarchism that embraced [[progress|evolution]]ary science. [[elisee-reclus|Elisée Reclus]], the geographer and anarchist, similarly argued that [[progress|evolution]] and r[[progress|evolution]] were complementary rather than opposed. This distinguished the [[france|French]] anarchist-communists from both the insurrectionary anarchists (who emphasized the r[[progress|evolution]]ary moment over structural analysis) and the reformist socialists (who believed [[progress|evolution]] alone, without r[[progress|evolution]]ary rupture, could transform society).


## Grave's Analysis

### The Natural History of Humanity

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates humanity within a continuous chain of natural development, drawing on the scientific consensus of his time. The [[progress|evolution]]ary perspective serves to demystify human nature — not as a special creation with fixed moral qualities, but as a product of material processes:

> Behold the modest beginnings of humanity! So modest that it is only very much later, after a long series of evolutions, after the formation of a certain number of types in the chain of beings, that we come to distinguish the animal from the vegetable. To trace the whole series up to man would be to rewrite here the history of evolution, which modern science explains in a manner so clear and comprehensive that no doubt remains. (005-006)

This naturalistic foundation is crucial to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s political argument. If humanity is a product of [[progress|evolution]], then human nature is malleable — shaped by environment rather than fixed by essence. The "bad" qualities that defenders of the existing order attribute to innate human depravity are in fact products of a defective [[environment|social environment]]:

> Man is not created unique, either morally or physically. Like other animals, of which he is but a superior specimen, he is the product of a concourse of circumstances, of combination and association of matter. He has struggled to develop himself, and if he has contributed in a large measure to the transformation of the environment wherein he is situated, the latter has in turn influenced the customs he has adopted, his manner of living, thinking, and acting. (010-004)

### Evolution vs. Gradualism

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s use of [[progress|evolution]]ary language presents a strategic tension that runs throughout the book. On one hand, he acknowledges that nature transforms through "a slow process of [[progress|evolution]], by an uninterrupted sequence of progress acquired little by little" (016-003). On the other hand, he insists that r[[progress|evolution]] — a sharp, violent break — is necessary:

> It is true that everything in nature is transformed by a slow process of evolution, by an uninterrupted sequence of progress acquired little by little, imperceptible if followed throughout its course, bursting upon the eye only when we pass suddenly from one period to another. It is thus that life progressed upon our globe, it is thus that man emerged from animality. (016-003)

The resolution of this tension lies in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of how [[progress|evolution]] actually works. Evolutionary change, while gradual in its accumulation, produces qualitative leaps — "bursting upon the eye only when we pass suddenly from one period to another." [[revolution|Revolution]], in this framework, is not the opposite of [[progress|evolution]] but its culmination: the moment when accumulated contradictions produce a sudden reorganization.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] warns against a naive [[religion|faith]] in [[reform|gradualism]] that would see anarchism achieved through a steady accumulation of reforms. The existing society, being "based upon the antagonism of interests" (003-004), systematically absorbs and neutralizes reforms:

> By its organization, based upon the antagonism of interests, our bourgeois society charges itself with the task of bringing the workers to a revolution. (003-004)

### The Evolutionary Imperative

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s argument is not that r[[progress|evolution]] is *chosen* against [[progress|evolution]], but that r[[progress|evolution]] is *[[force-and-violence|force]]d by* the [[progress|evolution]]ary trajectory of a dying society. The moribund society cannot evolve further within its existing structure — its contradictions have exhausted its adaptive capacity:

> It depends upon him to conjure this fatality of "evil" — if we must use this word — by modifying his surroundings in a way to favor the development of his altruistic tendencies, instead of stimulating his brutal appetites as the existing social organization does. (004-010)

The anarchist project, then, is not to impose a utopian blueprint but to create the environmental conditions under which human [[progress|evolution]] can continue in a cooperative rather than antagonistic direction. This is the deepest sense in which [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchism is [[progress|evolution]]ary rather than utopian.


This evolutionary framework leads [[jean-grave|Grave]] to a crucial conclusion: the existing social order is not merely imperfect but *obsolete*. Just as biological organisms that fail to adapt to changed environments perish, so too must a social organization that systematically thwarts human development give way to a higher form. The scientific study of society reveals not the permanence of authority but its historically contingent character:

> The study of the mechanism of society shows us that everything is in a state of perpetual transformation. Institutions, ideas, customs, laws — nothing is immutable, everything changes, everything is transformed under the pressure of economic necessities and the aspirations of men. (010-003)

This insight — that society is governed by laws of development as much as the natural world — is the foundation of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s optimism. The same scientific method that explains the [[environment|influence of environment]] on organisms also reveals the possibility of deliberately reshaping the social environment. Anarchism, in this view, is not utopian dreaming but the application of evolutionary science to social organization:

> If societies are subject to the law of evolution, they can be modified. Man can act upon his environment; he can modify it, and by modifying it, modify himself. This is the whole secret of social transformation. (010-004)
