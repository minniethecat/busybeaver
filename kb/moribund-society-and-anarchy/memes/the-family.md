# The Family

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the family is not a natural institution grounded in affection or biological necessity but a historically contingent form of social organization that emerged alongside private property and authority. He traces its [[progress|evolution]] from primitive promiscuity through matriarchal recognition to patriarchal dominance, drawing on the anthropology of his era to argue that "the first human association must have been, as it still is among certain mammiferous animals — some of the anthropoid apes — the nucleus of the family" [006-002]. The present form — the indissoluble, male-headed, property-transmitting legal family — exists to "insure the supremacy of man over woman" and to guarantee "the fruit of his rapine to his descendants" [006-009]. The anarchist position is not to "destroy the family" but to abolish the property antagonisms that have deformed it, "to base the family upon affection in order to render it more permanent" [006-009].

## The Historical Evolution of the Family

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates the family within an [[progress|evolution]]ary framework that denies the bourgeoisie's claim that it rests upon "unassailable and immovable bases." He draws on the anthropology of his era to demonstrate the institution's mutability:

> Property, the family, authority, have developed along parallel lines; of this there is no doubt. Granting that men united their efforts under the pressure of a common need, of some obstacle to be overcome against which individual efforts had exhausted themselves in vain, it follows that the benefits resulting from this cooperation of forces were shared in common. [006-002]

The earliest human groups practiced "the most complete promiscuity" — the male "cohabits with the first female that comes in his way" [006-003]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] documents transitional stages: the prohibition of unions between those of the same totem, the emergence of maternal right (where "the children belong to the tribe of the mother"), and the custom of *couvade*, in which the man mimics childbirth to affirm paternity [006-002]. These customs demonstrate that the patriarchal family is not primordial but the product of a long historical struggle.

The turning point comes with the emergence of private property. The man who owns property, "wishing to transmit the fruit of his rapine to his descendants," fashions the indissoluble family as a mechanism of inheritance [006-009]. The woman is reduced from associate to property: "having been considered up till now as inferior, and rather as property than as an associate" [006-009]. The legal family is thus an economic institution first and an affective one only incidentally.

## The Family as a Property Institution

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies the family as one of the four pillars — alongside property, authority, and religion — upon which existing society rests. The connection is structural:

> Protection to private property and hereditary transmission of the same in families — this is the principle upon which existing society rests. Authority, the family, the magistracy, the army, and every hierarchic and bureaucratic organization, which stifles and devours us, proceed from this principle. [005-003]

The family in its present form cannot be understood apart from the defense of property. It ensures that wealth remains concentrated across generations, that women's reproductive labor is controlled, and that children are raised as "things — property — by their progenitors" [006-009]. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique is not of kinship bonds as such but of the legal form that transforms them into instruments of domination.

The historical record confirms this: private property "was not that of the first human associations" — it emerged only "when the family commenced to emerge from promiscuity" [005-021]. The coincidence of these two developments is not accidental. The family becomes the vehicle through which property is transmitted, and property becomes the interest that must be protected through the legal en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment of family bonds.

## Marriage and Its Discontents

The institution of marriage is the legal sanction that en[[force-and-violence|force]]s the property-family. The Anarchists "reject the institution of marriage" because "two beings who love each other have no need of the permission of a third in order to go to bed together" [006-006]. The ceremony is a hypocrisy that claims to bind what no external authority can guarantee.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] deploys the bourgeoisie's own concession — divorce — as an argument against the institution. If marriage were truly indissoluble, divorce would not exist; the fact that "three old women in gowns and caps can declare null and void" what a ceremony consecrated proves that the bond is legal, not natural [006-005]. The reformist admission of divorce is "an argument against the stability of the family" and an unwitting confession "in favor of free unions" [006-005].

Nor has the legal family ever succeeded in regulating human desire:

> Has not the love of man and woman always been stronger than all laws, all prudery, all the reprobation, which men have sought to attach to the performance of the sexual act? [006-007]

The result of centuries of repression is not virtue but hypocrisy. "All these prejudices have only helped to make us frauds and hypocrites; that is all" [006-007].

## The Anarchist Alternative: Free Union

The anarchist position is not the abolition of the family but its [[emancipation|liberation]] from the legal and property forms that deform it:

> The Anarchists, who have been accused of wanting to destroy the family, want only to destroy this antagonism; to base the family upon affection in order to render it more permanent. [006-009]

Free union means that a man and woman — or indeed any configuration of human affection — unite and separate by mutual will alone. "What they have made of their own free will they can unmake of their own free will" [006-006]. No [[the-law|law]], no ceremony, no third-party authority can determine what only the participants can know.

This is not a prescription for chaos but a recognition of reality: the feelings of mankind "elude all regulations" and "entire liberty is necessary in order that they may unfold completely and normally" [006-008]. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s confidence in liberty is grounded in his [[progress|evolution]]ary naturalism — he trusts that the [[the-law|law]] of free [[progress|evolution]] will favor "the fittest, the best endowed" and that permanent, monogamous unions, if they are the natural tendency, will prove themselves "the surest guarantee" without legal compulsion [006-009].

> Let us be less puritanic and we shall be more candid, more moral. [006-008]
