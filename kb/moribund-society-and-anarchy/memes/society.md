# Society

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s anarchist analysis, **society** refers to the totality of human social organization — its institutions, economic arrangements, class structure, and the web of relationships they produce. [[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes sharply between *existing society* (the "moribund" capitalist-authoritarian order) and the *future society* that anarchism envisions: one based on [[association|free association]], solidarity, and the abolition of authority and property.

> Our present society exhibits some of these perfect egoists: the Delobelles, the Hjalmar Eikdals, are not rare; they are found not only in romances. Without meeting any great number of them, it is sometimes given to us to run up against these types who think only of themselves, who see nothing in life but their own persons. (002-005)

## Historical Context

### Nineteenth-Century Social Thought

[[jean-grave|Grave]] wrote at the intersection of several intellectual currents. The rise of [[progress|evolution]]ary biology (Darwin, Spencer), the development of environmental sociology (Letourneau), and the experience of working-class movements (the Paris Commune, the [[first-international|First International]]) all fed into his analysis that society is not fixed but transformable. His view that environment shapes character — "the modifying [[environment|influence of environment]]s upon organized beings" (010-002) — reflects a broad scientific consensus of his era.

### Anarchist-Comintern Tensions

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of society also reflects the strategic debates within the left of his time. Against those who argued that piecemeal reforms could gradually improve society, [[jean-grave|Grave]] insisted that the structure itself was the problem — [[reform|reformism]] within a "moribund" framework was futile. Against those who argued that anarchism was a utopian dream, he pointed to the existing society's own failure as proof that radical alternatives were not only desirable but necessary.


## Grave's Analysis

### Society as Antagonism

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s central thesis is that existing society is "defective and ill-conceived" because it is organized around a fundamental antagonism — the division of humanity into two classes with opposing interests. This is not an accident of bad leadership but a structural feature of the system:

> But the defective and ill-conceived organization of society into two distinct classes does not stop here in its pernicious effects. Based upon antagonism of interests it opposes individual against individual within each class; it sows warfare among them by its institution of private property which forces people to hoard in order to secure themselves against the morrow those necessaries which society cannot guarantee to them. Private competition is the great actuating force of the present society. (010-006)

This analysis treats society not as a neutral container for human activity but as an active [[force-and-violence|force]] that shapes character, behavior, and possibility. [[jean-grave|Grave]] contends that the ills attributed to "human nature" — selfishness, crime, laziness — are products of the [[environment|social environment]]:

> To study the mechanism of capitalistic society is sufficient to discover that it can produce nothing good. Aspirations towards the good and the beautiful must be perennial in the human race, not to have been choked by the rapacity and narrow, unreasoning egoism which official society has inculcated in it from the cradle. (004-005)

### The Paradox of Crime and Property

[[jean-grave|Grave]] develops a sharp critique of how society defines crime. What the legal code labels "criminal" overwhelmingly involves attacks on property — yet the society itself, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues, is built on systematic theft:

> If we study what crimes are the most antisocial, most common, and against which the code is chiefly directed, we shall soon discover that outside of crimes of passion, which are very rare, and concerning which judges and physicians agree that leniency should be used, attacks upon property furnish the largest contingent of crimes or misdemeanors. Hence arises the question to which only those who have studied society in its nature and effects can reply: "Is property just? Is an organization which is based upon theft entitled to the protection of its laws?" (009-015)

The [[the-law|legal system]], in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s view, does not protect society from criminals — it protects a particular class's hold on property:

> You say you make war upon thieves and assassins; but what is a thief, or an assassin? Persons who claim the right to live without being useful, at the expense of society, you will say. But cast a glance over your society and you will discover that it is swarming with thieves, and that, far from punishing them, your laws are made for the express purpose of protecting them. Far from punishing laziness, society holds it up as an ideal, and awards the pleasure of doing nothing to those who can, by nature of their birth, afford it. (009-016)

### The Moribund Diagnosis

The concept of a "moribund" society — the very title of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s work — signifies not merely a society with problems to be reformed, but one that is structurally dying, incapable of producing human flourishing. The society that exists has exhausted its capacity to generate good:

> Its authority resting in the hands of a minority, the latter have turned it to their own profit; and the more society has evolved, the more the governing classes have consolidated their power, and the more the people have been despoiled. (010-009)

Yet [[jean-grave|Grave]] does not advocate despair. His argument is that the very development of society — including the growth of scientific understanding about environment, [[progress|evolution]], and human nature — has created the conditions for a radical break:
[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends this analysis to the psychological dimensions of social decay. The moribund society does not merely fail to produce material well-being; it systematically deforms human character, producing individuals who are at once victims and agents of the system's logic. The very institutions that claim to uphold order — the [[the-law|courts]], the [[police]], the [[the-magistracy|magistracy]] — are themselves expressions of the antagonism they purport to regulate:

> If we pass from the moral order to the physical order, we shall see that society knows no more how to protect the health of its members than it does to protect their honesty. Filth, misery, overwork — these are the causes of the diseases that decimate the population. (010-012)

This dual failure — moral and physical — is at the heart of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s indictment. The society that cannot protect its members from theft or disease, that in fact generates both through its structure of [[competition]] and exploitation, has forfeited any claim to legitimacy. The point is not to reform such a society but to replace it with one organized around [[solidarity]] rather than antagonism, around [[free-association|free association]] rather than [[authority]].

> Society is not a being of reason; it is a being of instinct and of passion. It is led by its appetites and its interests, not by principles. (010-013)


> When men first became organized into societies they based these societies upon their instincts for struggle and mastery; and this explains why society is so badly constructed. Only, society has remained bad. (010-009)

## Society Types in Grave's Analysis

| Type | Basis | Character | Outcome |
|------|-------|-----------|---------|
| Existing Capitalist Society | Private property + authority | Antagonism of interests, competition, class division | Moribund — structurally incapable of producing good |
| Anarchist Future Society | Free association + solidarity | Cooperation, mutual aid, abolition of class | Human flourishing, voluntary organization |
