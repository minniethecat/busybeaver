# Cesare Lombroso

Cesare Lombroso (1835–1909) was an Italian criminologist and physician, best known as the founder of the Italian School of Positivist Criminology. His theory of the "born criminal" — the idea that criminality was an inherited trait detectable through physical stigmata — was highly influential in late-nineteenth-century criminology. In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] subjects Lombroso's theories to sustained critique.

## In Moribund Society and Anarchy

### Critique of Criminal Anthropology

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes a significant portion of Chapter IX ("The Right to Punish and the Savants") to dismantling Lombroso's criminal anthropology. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s strategy is to use the scientific consensus of his time — particularly the rejection of free will and the recognition of environmental determinism — against Lombroso's attempt to locate criminality in biology:

> Science, today, admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of forces to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist. Environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences, act upon man in turn. (009-002)

The irony [[jean-grave|Grave]] exposes is that Lombroso and the criminal anthropologists claimed scientific authority while producing what [[jean-grave|Grave]] regarded as speculative physiognomy dressed in scientific language.

### The "Born Criminal" Theory

Lombroso's central claim was that criminals could be identified by physical characteristics — asymmetrical features, prominent jaws, peculiar ear or nose shapes, and the practice of tattooing, which Lombroso interpreted as evidence of atavism. [[jean-grave|Grave]] ridicules this approach:

> To have an ear or a nose badly shaped — the nose especially! Nothing can be more disagreeable — above all if this defective conformation is carried to the extreme limit of the ludicrous! (009-009)

[[jean-grave|Grave]] points to tattooing as evidence of the circularity of Lombroso's reasoning. Lombroso treated tattooing as a mark of criminal atavism, while it was equally common among non-criminal populations:

> As to tattooing, we had not up to the present taken it as an indication of very elevated aesthetics. O no. It is a remnant of atavism which leads certain men "to heighten their natural beauty" by means of embellishments pricked into the skin, precisely as our ancestors of the stone age might have done. (009-011)

### The Political Criminal

Lombroso extended his typology to the "political criminal," attempting to classify anarchists and r[[progress|evolution]]aries as a distinct criminal type. [[jean-grave|Grave]] notes this attempt was based on fabricated evidence:

> Lombroso has also tried very hard to discover a type of the political criminal, supporting the theory upon information quite as imaginary. (009-012)

The translator, Voltairine de Cleyre, adds a devastating footnote:

> I do not know whether Jean Grave had seen Prof. Lombroso's article on the "Physiognomy of the Chicago Anarchists"... wherein he admits in a footnote that his analysis was based upon portraits in Capt. Schaack's book, which, as he had learned later, were incorrect! (024-003)

### Counter-Criticism from Within Science

[[jean-grave|Grave]] notes that Lombroso's theories were criticized not only by anarchists but by fellow scientists. He cites Dr. Manouvrier, whose course on criminal anthropology:

> victoriously demonstrated the lack of consistency in the pretended criminal characters sought to be attributed to those designated by that label. (009-013)

## Legacy

Lombroso's theories of the "born criminal" were largely discredited by the early twentieth century, though his emphasis on empirical methods contributed to the development of criminology as a discipline. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* represents an early anarchist engagement with scientific criminology, arguing that crime is a product of social organization rather than biological inheritance.
