# England

## Overview

England in the nineteenth century was the world's dominant industrial and imperial power — the "workshop of the world" whose factories, financial institutions, and naval supremacy sustained a global empire spanning India, Africa, and the Americas. For anarchist theorists like [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]], England represented both the most advanced form of capitalist development and the most sophisticated ideological apparatus for justifying exploitation: the combination of parliamentary democracy, free trade rhetoric, and imperial [[patriotism|nationalism]] that masked the [[force-and-violence|violence]] at the heart of the system.

## Colonization and Imperialism

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes sustained attention to England's colonial project as a case study in the true nature of "civilization." In Chapter XIV, he argues that "no nation is reputed to be a colonizing one save when it has succeeded in getting out of a country the maximum product it is capable of yielding," and that "England is a colonizing country, because she knows how to 'reward' her colonies with the prosperity of the people she sends out to rule them, how to gather back into her coffers the taxes with which she burdens them" [014-003].

The colonial enterprise is presented not as an aberration of English policy but as the logical extension of the same principles that govern domestic capitalism. The conquest of India by England is cited alongside "the conquests in America by Spain" and "Africa, Cochin China, and Tonkin" as instances of the same pattern: "civilized" nations enriching themselves through the expropriation, enslavement, and extermination of peoples they deem "savage" [014-008]. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s rhetorical question — "which were the most savage — those who were called so, or the 'civilized'" — encapsulates his argument that the violence of the conqueror, not the supposed backwardness of the conquered, defines the colonial relation [014-008].

## Parliamentarism and Class Rule

The English model of liberal parliamentarism — often held up by [[france|French]] republicans as an ideal — is, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s view, merely a more refined mechanism of class rule. He argues that "[[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]]" is no more than "the right of the governed to choose their master," and the English parliamentary system, with its long-established traditions and apparent stability, demonstrates this most clearly: the orderly alternation of parties masks the fundamental continuity of class interests.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] notes that "anarchist propaganda" has spread "in France, Spain, Italy, England, America, or Australia" — wherever capitalism has developed, the anarchist critique has followed [018-004]. England's advanced industrialization thus made it simultaneously a model of capitalist success and a proving ground for the anarchist argument that economic development does not automatically produce human liberation.

## Industrial Capitalism and Its Discontents

England's position as the first industrial nation made it the prototype of capitalist development — and its social costs the prototype of capitalist immiseration. [[jean-grave|Grave]] references England as demonstrating that "the reduction of the working day would have the effect of stimulating the perfection of mechanical appliances and hastening the displacement of the workman of flesh by the workman of iron" — a process that "in a well-organized society would be progress, but would be found to be an aggravation of misery to the worker in our present society" [019-016]. The English experience proved that technological improvement under capitalism benefits the capitalist, not the worker.
