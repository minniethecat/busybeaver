# Patriotism

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist analysis, patriotism is the ideological mechanism by which the [[class|ruling class]]es persuade the working masses to identify their interests with those of the nation-[[the-state|state]] — a political entity that, in reality, serves only the property-owning minority. The "country" that patriots are called upon to love and defend "does not and will not belong to them" — it belongs to "a minority of property-owners, who, sheltered from all danger, bask tranquilly in their chimney-corners, while the workers foolishly go out to slay each other" [011-007]. Patriotism is thus a form of mass deception: it transforms [[exploitation|class exploitation]] into national duty, and cross-border working-class solidarity into treason.

## The Class Character of Patriotism

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is careful to distinguish between the patriotism of the [[class|ruling class]]es and that of the workers. The patriotism of the governing classes is not sentiment but calculation — "recent events have laid it bare in all its hideous reality" [012-018]. Behind the "sounding words 'country,' 'patriotism,' 'national honor'" that "vibrate in the ears of the naive" lie material interests: [[militarism|military]] contracts, colonial markets, financial schemes, and the maintenance of permanent armies that protect property at home while expanding it abroad [012-014].

The workers who believe in patriotism are victims of a confidence trick. The soil they defend was never theirs; the frontiers they die to protect serve only to divide them from other workers who share their exploitation. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s solution is radical: "if the workers were logical, instead of defending 'the country' by fighting other workers, they would begin by getting rid of those who command and exploit them; they would invite all the workers, of whatever nationality, to do the same, and would all unite in production and consumption at their ease" [011-007].

> The workers, if they were logical, instead of defending "the country" by fighting other workers, they would begin by getting rid of those who command and exploit them; they would invite all the workers, of whatever nationality, to do the same, and would all unite in production and consumption at their ease. [011-007]

## Patriotism and Militarism

Patriotism is inseparable from [[war|militarism]] — it provides the ideological fuel for the war machine. [[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes a full chapter to "The Patriotism of the Governing [[class|Class]]es" and another to "[[militarism|Militarism]]," arguing that the two form a self-reinforcing cycle. Patriotism justifies the existence of standing armies; the armies, in turn, inculcate patriotic sentiment through discipline, pageantry, and the shared experience of [[force-and-violence|violence]]. The worker conscripted into [[militarism|military]] service is "brutalized" into accepting the very system that exploits him.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] notes the historical contingency of patriotic borders with bitter irony. Provinces "tossed first to this side then to that" find their patriotism consisting in "fighting sometimes under one flag, sometimes under another" — the national identity for which men are expected to die is revealed as a function of dynastic accident and imperial bargaining [011-005].

## The Decline of Patriotism and the Rise of "Civilization"

[[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that "patriotism is beginning to decline" as a mobilizing ideology, and that the [[class|ruling class]]es have adapted by supplementing it with a new justification: "civilization" [012-015]. This substitution does not change the underlying reality — workers are still being sent to kill and die for interests not their own — but it reveals the instrumental character of patriotic rhetoric. When "country" loses its persuasive power, "[[civilization|civilizing mission]]" takes its place; the form changes but the function — mobilizing the exploited to serve their exploiters — remains constant.

> For now that patriotism is beginning to decline, this new mockery — "civilization" — is used a great deal in launching the workers on a crusade against their fellows. [012-015]

## Against Patriotism: The Internationalism of the Exploited

The anarchist response to patriotism is not a competing nationalism but the international solidarity of the exploited. "Your country exploits us, your frontiers stifle us, your nationalities are strangers to us!" [[jean-grave|Grave]] declares; "We are men, citizens of the universe; all for each, each for all" [013-015]. The interests that unite workers across borders — the common condition of exploitation, the shared enemy in capital and [[government|the state]] — are far more fundamental than the artificial divisions that patriotism en[[force-and-violence|force]]s.
