# Wage Labor

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist economics, wage labor designates not simply work performed for pay, but a specific social relation in which the worker, deprived of independent access to the means of production, is compelled to sell their labor power to an employer. The wage received is not the full value of what the worker produces but only the minimum necessary to "feed, clothe, and lodge himself and finally to repair the strength which he has depleted" [005-014]. The surplus — the profit claimed by the employer — is the product of labor that the worker never sees. Wage labor is thus, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s framework, the institutional form that exploitation takes under capitalism.

## The Economics of the Wage Relation

[[jean-grave|Grave]] challenges the classical economists' claim that wages represent a fair exchange between equal parties. On the contrary, the wage relation is fundamentally unequal: the employer, by virtue of owning the means of production, dictates the terms, while the worker, having nothing to sell but their labor power, must accept or starve. The supposed "contract" between capital and labor is a formality that masks a relation of compulsion.

The employer's profit, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues, is not a reward for intelligence or risk-taking but simply "the product of the labor of the employees, which the master pockets because he has the power" [005-015]. The worker who labors "ten, eleven, or twelve hours" produces far more than the value of their wage — the employer "calculates that he must have the workman" for these long hours "in order to get out of him the estimated profit" [019-014]. The wage is not a share of the product but a cost of production, kept as low as competition and the threat of unemployment will allow.

> The workman, who is paid right along for his labor and consequently runs no risks, has a right to the first claim only, permitting him to replace his capital expended; that is to say, to feed, clothe, and lodge himself and finally to repair the strength which he has depleted. [005-014]

## The Illusion of Reform: Wages and Hours

A significant portion of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis addresses proposals to reform the wage system from within — specifically the reduction of working hours and the establishment of a minimum wage. These reforms, he acknowledges, "seem at first sight very seductive" [019-012]. Yet he is skeptical that they can fundamentally alter the exploitative character of wage labor.

The problem is structural. When the working day is reduced, employers respond not by accepting lower profits but by intensifying labor — "producing in eight hours what is now produced in twelve" — or by raising prices to compensate [019-015]. The minimum wage, similarly, is neutralized by the employer's power to "catch up" through price increases or speed-ups. The fundamental asymmetry of power in the wage relation means that reforms that leave the relation intact are perpetually vulnerable to reversal.

> Reduce the working day to eight hours, and the employer, unable to reduce the wage proportionately for that would cause the desertion of his hands, will make up for it by increasing the price of his goods. [019-015]

This is not, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], an argument against workers organizing for better conditions. It is an argument against mistaking better conditions within exploitation for the abolition of exploitation itself. [[reform|Reform]]s may momentarily improve the worker's position, but as long as the wage system persists, the structural pressure to extract maximum labor at minimum cost reasserts itself.

## Wage Labor and the Social Order

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates wage labor within the broader architecture of class domination. The wage relation is not an isolated economic arrangement but is sustained by the entire institutional framework of bourgeois society: private property excludes the worker from independent production; [[government|the state]] en[[force-and-violence|force]]s the employer's property rights; [[war|militarism]] and patriotism divide workers who share a common interest against exploitation; and the educational and [[the-law|legal system]]s inculcate the ideology that wages are freely contracted and fair.

The wage worker is doubly exploited. At the point of production, surplus value is extracted from their labor. In the sphere of consumption, the same employer class captures their wages back through the sale of commodities at prices inflated above their cost of production. The worker "creates prosperity for the 'elect'" but receives in return only enough to reproduce their capacity to labor — to "reproduce other beasts of burden who in their turn may work out the happiness of the descendants of the 'elect'" [015-002].

## Beyond the Wage System

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the abolition of wage labor is inseparable from the abolition of private property and the establishment of [[communism|anarchist communism]]. When the means of production are collectively held and production is organized through [[association|free association]] rather than the employer-employee relation, the category of "wages" itself dissolves. Workers no longer sell their labor to a master; they contribute their productive capacity to a cooperative enterprise in which they are full participants, sharing in the product according to need rather than being compensated at a rate determined by the market for labor.
