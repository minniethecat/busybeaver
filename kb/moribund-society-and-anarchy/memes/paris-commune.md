# Paris Commune

## Overview

The Paris Commune (March 18 – May 28, 1871) was a revolutionary socialist government that briefly ruled Paris following [[france|France]]'s defeat in the Franco-Prussian [[war|War]]. Formed when the National Guard repudiated the authority of the national [[the-state|government]] and established a municipal council elected by [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]], the Commune enacted a series of radical measures — the separation of [[religion|church]] and [[the-state|state]], the abolition of night work for bakers, the remission of rents owed during the siege — before being crushed by the French army in the "Bloody Week" (Semaine Sanglante) of May 21–28, during which an estimated 20,000 Communards were executed.

The Commune represented the first major historical example of working-class self-governance on a city-wide scale. For the anarchist movement, it became both an inspiration and a cautionary tale — proof that workers could organize society without a centralized [[the-state|state]], and proof that the ruling class would employ unrestrained violence to prevent such organization from succeeding.

## Significance for Anarchism

The Paris Commune was a watershed event for the international anarchist movement. Many anarchists participated directly in the Commune: [[elisee-reclus|Élisée Reclus]], [[elie-reclus|Élie Reclus]], and Louise Michel fought on the barricades. Its experiment in direct democracy, federalism, and workers' self-management became a model for subsequent revolutionary movements. As [[jean-grave|Grave]] observed, the Commune demonstrated that "the movement was launched; the idea grew, purified itself, and became more and more defined," even though the propagandist organization at the time "still contained many of the germs of authoritarianism" [001-008].

The Commune's suppression demonstrated the lengths to which the ruling class would go to prevent working-class self-governance. [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that "ideas strong enough to inspire men with a conviction which makes them fight and suffer for the propagation thereof, without expecting anything directly from those ideas" were worth studying [001-013] — and the Commune proved that the idea of workers' self-government was precisely such an idea. The bourgeoisie, he writes, sought to prove that "the truth can neither be suppressed nor silenced" [001-013], yet they deployed the full force of the state to do exactly that.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Grave]] references the Commune's fate in connection with his broader critique of [[war|militarism]] and patriotism. The working class of Paris, he observes, is mobilized to fight wars for the benefit of the capitalist class, yet when workers attempt to govern themselves, the same army is turned against them with savage brutality. The Commune thus exemplifies Grave's thesis that [[government|the state]]'s military apparatus exists not to defend the nation but to defend the propertied [[class|class]] against the people.

For Grave, the Commune also illustrates a crucial strategic lesson: that the working class must prepare itself intellectually, not merely react with force. "If the bourgeoisie was able in 1789 to substitute itself for 'divine right,' it was because it had prepared itself intellectually for such substitution," he writes [003-010]. The implication is clear: the Commune's defeat was not inevitable but reflected insufficient intellectual and organizational preparation. The lesson for anarchists is that revolutionary action must be preceded by thoroughgoing education and propaganda.

## Historical Context

The Commune emerged from the confluence of several crises. France had just suffered a humiliating defeat in the [[franco-prussian-war|Franco-Prussian War]] (1870–71), and Paris had endured a four-month siege by Prussian forces. The national government, led by Adolphe Thiers, fled to Versailles and sought to disarm the Paris National Guard — a move that triggered the uprising of March 18, 1871, when Parisian workers, artisans, and members of the Guard refused to surrender their cannons.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates the Commune within a longer tradition of revolutionary movements reaching back to 1793, when "the Anarchists were much talked of" and "Jacques Roux and 'the madmen' appear to us to have been the ones who, during the Revolution, saw most clearly, and sought the best means of turning it to the benefit of the people" [001-007]. This lineage — from the Enragés of 1793 through the revolutions of 1848 to the Commune of 1871 — demonstrates that the working class's aspiration to self-governance was not an isolated outburst but an enduring current in French political life.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The Bloody Week of May 1871 saw approximately 20,000 Communards executed, 38,000 arrested, and more than 7,000 deported to New Caledonia. The repression was so thorough that it took the French anarchist movement decades to recover. Yet the Commune's memory proved indelible. [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]]'s federalist ideas — which had influenced many Communards — gained renewed attention, and the experience of the Commune shaped the anti-authoritarian turn within the First International, contributing to the split between Marx and Bakunin at the Hague Congress of 1872.

The Commune's radical measures — including the abolition of the standing army, the separation of church and state, free and secular education, and the election of all public officials subject to immediate recall — anticipated many later anarchist and socialist programs. For [[jean-grave|Grave]] and his generation of anarchist propagandists, the Commune stood as the definitive demonstration that the working class could govern itself without a centralized state, even as its violent suppression warned of what they faced.
