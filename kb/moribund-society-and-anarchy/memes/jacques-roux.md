# Jacques Roux

## Biography

Jacques Roux (1752–1794), known as "the Red Priest," was a radical Catholic priest who became one of the most uncompromising voices of the [[france|French]] [[revolution|Revolution]]. As leader of the *Enragés* (the "madmen" or "enraged ones"), Roux agitated for price controls, the punishment of speculators, and the redistribution of wealth to the poor. His "Manifesto of the Enragés" (1793) denounced the National Convention for failing to address the suffering of the common people. Arrested during the Terror, Roux committed suicide in prison rather than face the guillotine.

Roux's trajectory illustrates the internal dynamics of revolutionary movements. He began as a parish priest in the working-class Gravilliers section of [[paris-commune|Paris]], where his daily contact with poverty radicalized his politics. By 1791 he had abandoned any pretense of ecclesiastical orthodoxy, transforming his pulpit into a platform for economic egalitarianism. His sermons denounced not only the monarchy and the aristocracy but the emerging commercial bourgeoisie — the speculators, monopolists, and provisions merchants who profited from scarcity while ordinary Parisians starved.

The escalating confrontation between Roux and the revolutionary government reached its climax in June 1793, when Roux led a delegation of the Gravilliers section to the Convention and delivered an address that has come to be known as the "Manifesto of the Enragés." He told the deputies: "Liberty is nothing but a vain phantom when one class of men can starve another with impunity. Equality is nothing but a vain phantom when the rich, by their monopolies, exercise the right of life and death over their fellow men." The Convention responded not with reform but with repression. Roux was denounced by Marat, expelled from the Cordeliers Club, and ultimately arrested.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] explicitly identifies Jacques Roux and the Enragés as precursors to anarchism, writing:

> In 1793 the Anarchists were much talked of. Jacques Roux and "the madmen" appear to us to have been the ones who, during the Revolution, saw most clearly, and sought the best means of turning it to the benefit of the people. [001-007]

Grave notes that "the bourgeois historians have revenged themselves for this by effacing them as much as possible from the pages of history" [001-007]. This act of historical recovery is characteristic of anarchist historiography: Grave insists that the [[france|French]] [[revolution|Revolution]] contained within it a radical egalitarian current — represented by Roux, Jean Varlet, and the Enragés — that both Jacobin and bourgeois histories systematically suppress.

The suppression of Roux from revolutionary memory is not, for Grave, a mere scholarly oversight. It reflects the class character of historical writing itself. Bourgeois historians, committed to the narrative that the Revolution achieved liberty and equality through republican institutions, cannot acknowledge Roux without confronting the uncomfortable fact that the Revolution's most consistent egalitarians were crushed not by royalists but by the revolutionary government itself. The erasure of Roux is thus the erasure of the contradiction at the heart of the revolutionary project: that political equality without economic transformation merely replaces one ruling class with another.

## Roux's Political Program

Roux's demands were strikingly concrete. He called for: (1) the death penalty for speculators and monopolists who hoarded grain and other necessities; (2) strict price controls on essential commodities; (3) the requisition of grain from wealthy farmers at fixed prices; (4) the establishment of public granaries to ensure supply; (5) the prohibition of the export of specie, which he argued enriched financiers at the expense of the population; and (6) constitutional guarantees of the right to subsistence.

What distinguished Roux from other Jacobin radicals was his insistence that political rights were meaningless without economic guarantees. The Declaration of the Rights of Man proclaimed liberty and equality, but Roux pointed out that the liberty to starve was no liberty at all and that equality before the law meant nothing when vast inequalities of wealth rendered the law an instrument of the rich against the poor. He spoke for the [[sans-culottes]] — the artisans, journeymen, and small shopkeepers who had made the Revolution possible through their militant action but who had seen its benefits flow exclusively to the propertied.

Roux's program prefigures, in embryonic form, the anarchist communist insistence that political [[revolution]] without economic revolution is incomplete and indeed deceptive. Like [[jean-grave|Grave]] a century later, Roux understood that the liberation of the [[class|working class]] could not be achieved by transferring political power from one faction of the propertied to another — it required the complete transformation of economic relations. The early anarchists who "began to be uneasy concerning the period of the Revolution" [001-007] were asking the same question Roux had asked: for whom is this revolution being made?

## The Enragés and Popular Democracy

The Enragés represented the furthest leftward reach of the French Revolution — further than the Jacobins, further than the Hébertists. Where Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety sought to consolidate revolutionary power in a centralized state, the Enragés advocated for direct popular democracy: the permanent participation of the common people in political decision-making, unmediated by representatives.

This commitment to direct action rather than delegation is what links Roux to the anarchist tradition. Grave's anarchism is built on the same premise: that [[authority]] cannot be delegated without being abused, that representatives inevitably betray those they claim to represent, that the only guarantee of liberty is the active, continuous exercise of it by every individual. Roux did not have the theoretical vocabulary of anarchism — the word itself was only beginning to be used in a political sense during his lifetime — but his practice embodied its essential principle: the refusal to entrust one's liberation to anyone but oneself and one's comrades.

The fate of the Enragés is instructive. Having agitated for popular democracy, they were suppressed by the very revolutionary government they had helped bring to power. Roux was arrested; Varlet was imprisoned; Leclerc was silenced. The Committee of Public Safety, having consolidated its authority, would not tolerate a revolutionary current to its left. As Grave observes of the anarchists of 1793: "The bourgeois historians have revenged themselves for this by effacing them as much as possible from the pages of history" [001-007]. The lesson was not lost on later anarchists: revolutions produce their own counter-revolutions, and the revolutionary state is the principal instrument through which the popular classes are disarmed and disciplined.

## Roux and the Anarchist Tradition

Roux's significance for anarchism lies in his demonstration that revolutionary movements contain internal contradictions that cannot be resolved within the framework of [[the-state|state]] power. The Enragés' demand for economic as well as political equality — for bread as well as liberty — prefigured the anarchist insistence that political revolution without economic transformation merely replaces one [[class|ruling class]] with another. Roux's fate — crushed by the revolutionary [[the-state|government]] he helped bring to power — became a cautionary tale for later anarchists about the dangers of entrusting revolution to [[government|the state]].

The historiographical recovery of Roux by Grave and other anarchist writers serves a dual purpose. First, it establishes a lineage: anarchism is not a foreign import or a nineteenth-century novelty but emerges from the most radical currents of the great democratic revolutions. Second, it provides a critical perspective: the revolutionary tradition contains within it both the promise of liberation and the mechanisms of its betrayal. The task of the anarchist historian is to recover those moments of radical possibility that official history suppresses, and to learn from their defeat.

Roux's insistence on economic rights alongside political rights anticipates one of the central themes of Grave's *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*: that "nothing more urgent than the stomach question" [001-008], but that the satisfaction of material needs alone is not enough. The revolution must go beyond bread to liberty — the complete enfranchisement of the human individual from all external [[authority]], economic as well as political.
