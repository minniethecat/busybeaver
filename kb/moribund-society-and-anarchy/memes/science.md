# Science

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, science occupies an ambivalent position in the struggle between authority and anarchy. On one hand, genuine scientific inquiry — the disinterested study of natural and social phenomena — is a liberatory [[force-and-violence|force]] that has "helped us to lift the veil" and "furnished us with the weapons which have assisted to strip the colossus" of metaphysical and religious authority [009-019]. On the other hand, "bourgeois science" is a form of ideological production that takes "social facts resulting from a bad organization" and sets them up "as 'natural [[the-law|law]]s,' making them the cause of what exists while they are but the effects" [005-010]. The task for anarchists is to distinguish the emancipatory potential of science from its ideological abuse, and to insist that scientific inquiry be conducted with "the same strictness and disinterestedness" in the social domain as in the natural [009-019].

## Science as a Weapon Against Authority

[[jean-grave|Grave]] repeatedly invokes science to dismantle the intellectual foundations of the existing order. Modern science, he argues, has disproven the divine origin of authority and property — these institutions are human artifacts, not natural or supernatural necessities. The doctrine of free will, central to the moral justification of punishment, has been demolished: "Science, today, admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of [[force-and-violence|force]]s to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist" [009-002].

Criminal anthropology — the attempt to identify born criminals by physical characteristics — is refuted by more rigorous scientific investigation. [[jean-grave|Grave]] cites Dr. Manouvrier's refutation of [[cesare-lombroso|Lombroso]]'s theories as evidence that "some few more enlightened savants themselves have not been slow to offer criticism upon the by far too fanciful theories of the criminalistic school" [009-013]. The scientific method, properly applied, exposes the pseudo-science that serves power.

> Science has helped us to lift the veil; it has furnished us with the weapons which have assisted to strip the colossus. It is too late for it to be able effectually to turn backward and endeavor to reconstitute in the name of the metaphysical entity, society, what it has wrested from the metaphysical entity, divinity. [009-019]

## The Critique of Bourgeois Science

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is not a naive scientism. He recognizes that science, as an institution embedded in bourgeois society, is vulnerable to the same corruptions as every other institution. The economists "decorating these absurdities with the name of science" have "pretended to legitimize the most monstrous social crimes, the most heinous piracies of capitalism" [005-010]. Scientific authority is invoked to naturalize inequality, to racialize hierarchy, and to provide apparently objective grounds for class domination.

The "savants" themselves are not immune to this corruption. [[jean-grave|Grave]] calls on them to "eliminate from themselves, completely, the bourgeois education they have received" — to recognize that their training and institutional position have predisposed them to see the existing order as natural rather than contingent [009-019]. The genuinely scientific stance is one that subjects social institutions to the same critical scrutiny as natural phenomena, refusing to accept the "natural [[the-law|law]]s" that rationalize exploitation.

## Science, Progress, and Revolution

The defenders of the existing order deploy a particular argument against r[[progress|evolution]]: that it would destroy "the triumphs of science" and risk "retrogression" [020-017]. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s response is sharp. What value do the triumphs of science have for "those who suffer unjustly and are tired of suffering"? The progress that science enables is monopolized by the possessing classes — "every fresh step in progress profits him, and him only, who has the wherewithal to put it into operation" [020-010]. The marvels of science coexist with the misery of the exploited, and to ask the exploited to patiently endure their condition for the sake of scientific progress is to ask them to sacrifice themselves for a civilization that has already sacrificed them.

The solution is not to destroy science but to liberate it from its subordination to capital and [[government|the state]]. "If you do not want progress to be stranded, or the marvels of science to disappear, stop opposing the claims of the disinherited" [020-018]. Science flourishes not under the protection of authority but in the clearing of obstacles that authority creates. The anarchist r[[progress|evolution]], far from threatening scientific progress, is the condition of its genuine realization — freed from the distorting pressures of class interest and put in the service of human need.

## The Experimental Method

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own methodological approach, articulated in his chapter "The Experimental Method," reflects his engagement with scientific thinking. He advocates for the application of experimental reasoning to social questions: hypotheses must be tested against evidence; claims must be verified; the effects of institutions must be observed before they are accepted. This scientific temper — skeptical, empirical, unwilling to take authority's word for anything — is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], the intellectual correlate of the anarchist political stance. Both refuse to accept that what exists must exist; both insist on the possibility of transformation through understanding.
