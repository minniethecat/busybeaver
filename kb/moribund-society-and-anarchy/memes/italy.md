# Italy

## Overview

Italy is referenced by Jean Grave in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* in multiple contexts: as one of the countries where anarchist ideas and propagandist organs had been established, as the origin of Cesare Lombroso's criminal anthropology school which Grave devotes an entire chapter to refuting, and as a nation participating in the broader European anarchist movement of the late nineteenth century.

## In Grave's Text

### Anarchist Presence

Grave notes that the original French edition of his work had been "translated in German, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, Jewish; only in English it remained untranslated" before de Cleyre undertook the work [011-011]. This indicates that Italy had an established anarchist readership sufficient to justify a translation — part of a broader pattern of Italian anarchist organizing that included figures like Errico Malatesta and the International Workingmen's Association sections that had been active since the 1870s.

Italy is listed among the countries where "groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created" as the anarchist movement spread internationally [044-044]. This enumeration is significant: it demonstrates that the anarchist movement was not a French or Russian peculiarity but a genuinely international phenomenon with active organizations across Europe and beyond.

> Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia... [044-044]

The inclusion of Italy alongside [[spain|Spain]] and [[france|France]] as a site of anarchist organizing reflects the historical reality: Italy had one of the most vibrant anarchist movements in Europe during this period. The First International had strong Italian sections, and anarchist ideas found fertile ground among Italian peasants, artisans, and workers disillusioned with both the monarchy and the parliamentary left.

### The Italian School of Criminal Anthropology

Grave's most sustained engagement with Italy comes through his critique of Cesare Lombroso and the school of criminal anthropology, which occupies the entirety of Chapter IX. Lombroso (1835–1909), a professor at the University of Turin, had achieved international prominence for his theory of the "born criminal" — the claim that criminality was an inherited biological trait that could be identified through physical stigmata such as cranial asymmetry, facial anomalies, tattoos, and other "atavistic" characteristics.

Grave's critique proceeds on two fronts. First, he exposes the logical contradiction at the heart of Lombroso's project. Science, Grave notes, "admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of forces to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist" [009-001]. If human action is determined by "environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences," then the concept of individual criminal responsibility collapses. Yet Lombroso and his followers, having acknowledged this determinism, "decide for energetic repression, life imprisonment, etc." [009-001]. The savants "recognize this, and therefore decide for — repression!" [009-001]. The irony is devastating: the scientists who demonstrate that the criminal is not responsible for their acts then prescribe the harshest possible punishment.

Second, Grave exposes the empirical bankruptcy of Lombroso's methods. Lombroso's "criminal type" was constructed by examining prison inmates — individuals already "deformed by prison life or by an abnormal existence" — and then attributing to innate criminality the physical characteristics produced by poverty, malnutrition, and incarceration [009-001]. Grave cites Dr. Manouvrier's systematic refutation of Lombroso's theories, delivered before the Anthropological Society of Paris in 1890–91:

> Dr. Manouvrier, in his course on "Criminal Anthropology," before the Anthropological Society in 1890, '91, refuted, in an admirable manner, the theories of Lombroso and the criminalistic school concerning the alleged born criminal. After having demonstrated the falsity of the observations upon which the Italian savant and his imitators depended... [305-305]

Manouvrier's refutation was decisive on multiple grounds. He demonstrated that Lombroso's supposed criminal stigmata — asymmetrical features, abnormal ear shape, tattoos — were distributed throughout the general population and could not distinguish criminals from non-criminals. More fundamentally, he argued that even genuine biological predispositions do not determine criminal behavior: "a certain sort of aptitudes might indifferently, according to the circumstances, prompt the person to do an act reputed honorable, as well as one reputed criminal." A powerful physique may make a man a strangler — or a police officer. A crafty disposition may produce a swindler — or a detective [305-305].

Grave's critique of Lombroso was not merely academic. The criminal anthropology school provided scientific legitimation for the increasingly harsh penal policies of the late nineteenth century — policies that, as Grave demonstrates in the same chapter, were systematically biased toward punishing the poor while protecting the crimes of the powerful. The "born criminal" theory gave a veneer of scientific objectivity to what was, in reality, the class-based administration of punishment.

### Shared Revolutionary Goals

Grave includes Italy among the nations where anarchists share the common goal of "the abolition of private property, the destruction of authority, complete autonomy of the individual without any restriction" [630-630]. This framing emphasizes the transnational character of anarchist politics: Italian anarchists, like their French, Spanish, and Russian counterparts, sought not national reform but the universal abolition of the [[authority|authoritarian]] and [[private-property|proprietarian]] order.

### Historical Context

During the period when Grave was writing *Moribund Society and Anarchy* (the 1890s), Italy was a relatively young nation-state, unified only in 1861. The unification — the *Risorgimento* — had disappointed many radicals who had hoped for a democratic republic rather than a constitutional monarchy under the House of Savoy. The new state was dominated by the northern aristocracy and industrial interests, while the south remained mired in poverty and the *latifundia* system of large landed estates. This disillusionment with the state drove many Italian radicals toward anarchism, which offered a vision of social transformation that transcended the national question entirely.

Italy's anarchist movement was also notable for its internationalism. Italian anarchists like Errico Malatesta spent years in exile, organizing across Europe and the Americas, establishing the networks of solidarity that Grave celebrates in his closing pages. The Italian anarchist press — including journals like *L'Avvenire Sociale* and *La Questione Sociale* — formed part of the international network of "propagandist organs" that Grave describes. Malatesta himself, who had known Grave since their shared participation in the international anarchist congresses of the 1880s and 1890s, embodied the principle that anarchist organization was necessarily international: repression in one country could be countered by solidarity from comrades in another.

### Lombroso and the Politics of Science

The Lombroso episode reveals a deeper theme in Grave's work: the relationship between science and power. Lombroso's criminal anthropology was not politically neutral. It was science placed in the service of the existing order — science that provided a rationale for the prison, the gallows, and the criminalization of poverty. Grave's critique is not a rejection of science but a demand for a science liberated from "the prejudices of education" — a science that follows its own premises to their logical conclusion rather than stopping short when those conclusions threaten the social order [009-013].

This is why Grave includes the Lombroso chapter in a book otherwise devoted to the positive case for anarchism. The "born criminal" theory is not merely a scientific error; it is an ideological weapon. By naturalizing criminality — by making it a biological destiny rather than a social product — Lombroso's school deflects attention from the true causes of crime: "the defective and ill-conceived organization of society" [010-016]. Italy, as the birthplace of this theory, is therefore more than a geographic reference in Grave's text; it is the origin point of one of the most pernicious ideological justifications for the penal apparatus that Grave seeks to dismantle.
