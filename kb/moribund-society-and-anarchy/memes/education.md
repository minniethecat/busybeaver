# Education

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, education is one of the fundamental [[force-and-violence|force]]s that shape human conduct — alongside "environment, heredity, climatic and atmospheric influences" — exercising "an undeniable influence upon his brain" [009-002]. But education in existing society is not a neutral transmission of knowledge. It is a class instrument, designed to reproduce the prejudices and obedience required by the social order. The anarchist project therefore demands not the reform of education within bourgeois institutions but its radical transformation into a tool of emancipation.

## Education as Class Conditioning

[[jean-grave|Grave]] sees the education provided by bourgeois society as fundamentally ideological. The "prejudices of education" combine with other environmental influences to dictate conclusions to even well-intentioned [[science|savants]] "unknown to themselves" [009-014]. The educated classes are not educated to question the social order but to rationalize it. Even scientists, who pride themselves on objectivity, carry "the bourgeois education they have received" into their study of social phenomena [009-019].

> Here it is that the influence of environment, the prejudices of education, comparative ignorance of scientific questions which they have not studied, unknown to themselves combine to dictate to them conclusions which they would refuse to admit if they knew all the elements of the problem. [009-014]

This is a subtle but devastating critique. Bourgeois education does not simply teach falsehoods; it structures the very categories of thought, making certain conclusions seem natural and others unthinkable. The educated person is not immune to ideology but is often its most committed carrier.

## Education and Human Determinism

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s discussion of education is embedded in his broader argument against the doctrine of free will — the idea that individuals are morally responsible for their actions in a way that justifies punishment. If conduct is determined by "heredity, his education and environment in which he lives," then the notion of moral desert collapses [009-002]. The criminal is no more responsible for his crimes than the [[the-law|law]]-abiding citizen is for his virtue — both are products of [[force-and-violence|force]]s largely beyond their control.

> According to his heredity, his education and environment in which he lives, the individual will be more or less docile to the stimulus of certain forces, more or less refractory to certain others; but it is always the strongest forces that decide his conduct. [009-002]

This deterministic view has radical implications. If education shapes conduct, then society has a responsibility to provide an education that fosters solidarity rather than competition, [[association|cooperation]] rather than exploitation. The present system, which educates the poor to accept their poverty as a moral failing and the rich to regard their wealth as a natural entitlement, is a crime against human potential.

## Self-Education and Emancipation

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is not fatalistic. If workers are to emancipate themselves, they must recognize that "self-education is one of the forms of the social struggle" [003-008]. The oppressed cannot wait for their oppressors to educate them — the masters' education will always serve the masters' interests. Workers must take the work of education into their own hands.

> If the workers want to emancipate themselves they must understand that this emancipation will not come of itself; that they must obtain it; and that self-education is one of the forms of the social struggle. [003-008]

This call for self-education is not a retreat from collective action into individual self-improvement. It is recognition that the intellectual and practical dimensions of emancipation are inseparable. Workers who do not understand the nature of their oppression cannot effectively organize against it.

## Education and the Possibility of Progress

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s [[religion|faith]] in education is ultimately tied to his belief in human perfectibility. Even those who point to failed attempts at education as proof of innate inferiority miss the point. The question is not whether "there have been unfruitful attempts at such education" but "what were the conditions under which these attempts were made" [015-015]. Under different conditions — in a society organized for [[association|cooperation]] rather than competition — education would produce different results.

The "education of undeveloped tribes" might proceed peacefully, "bringing into civilization new elements, capable in the course of their adaptation, of putting new life into it" [014-012]. This vision of education as a two-way exchange — the "civilized" learning from the "undeveloped" as much as the reverse — is a radical departure from the colonial model of education as imposition.
