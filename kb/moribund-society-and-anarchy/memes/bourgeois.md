# Bourgeois

## Bourgeois Society as Antagonistic Order

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], "bourgeois society" is not merely the rule of a particular class but a comprehensive social order founded on the antagonism of interests. "By its organization, based upon the antagonism of interests," he writes, "our bourgeois society charges itself with the task of bringing the workers to a revolution." The structure of private property, competition, and class division creates a permanent state of warfare between individuals and classes, making every person "the enemy of his neighbor." This antagonism is not accidental but essential — it is the very principle upon which the existing order rests.

> Founded upon this struggle between individuals, society makes of every creature the enemy of all others; it provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors which are attributed to the evil nature of man, though they are but the consequence of the social order. [010-016]

## Bourgeois Science and Ideology

[[jean-grave|Grave]] acknowledges that bourgeois science has contributed to the critique of traditional authority. "Divine right having gotten a powerful shock in the decapitation of Louis XVI," he notes, the old justifications of power could no longer stand. "But the divine origin of authority and property being denied by bourgeois science itself, the bourgeoisie have sought to give them solid and natural bases." The economists and savants of the bourgeois order have constructed elaborate theories — the "naturalness" of private property, the "laws" of supply and demand, the supposed "frugality" of the capitalist — to mask the exploitative character of their system. Yet even this bourgeois science, "having set itself to demolish all the creeds which served as basis for established authority, has helped us to lift the veil."

> Science has helped us to lift the veil; it has furnished us with the weapons which have assisted to strip the colossus. [014-003]

Grave observes that science, "bourgeois though it be," has killed religion as a credible foundation for authority. But this same science, when it touches upon property and class relations, becomes evasive and self-serving, reducing "everything to capital — products, men, intelligence" while refusing to interrogate the justice of the property relations that produce capital in the first place [005-010].

## Bourgeois Historians and the Suppression of Dissent

The intellectual functionaries of the bourgeois order are not neutral observers. Grave notes how "the bourgeois historians have left them in the shade" — referring to [[jacques-roux|Jacques Roux]] and the *enragés* of the French Revolution, whose radical egalitarianism threatened both the old aristocracy and the emerging bourgeoisie. The documents of their struggle "buried in the archives and the libraries still await him who shall have the time and the courage to dig them up" [001-006]. This erasure is not incidental: the bourgeois historical narrative serves to naturalize the existing order by suppressing the memory of alternatives.

## Bourgeois Rhetoric and Worker Emancipation

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is sharply critical of those who would have the workers look to bourgeois reformers for their liberation. "This role is too well played by the bourgeois rhetoricians for us to attempt to supersede them in it," he writes. The task of the Anarchists is not to compete with bourgeois politicians in promising reforms within the existing system, but to demonstrate that the system itself is irredeemable. "If the workers want to emancipate themselves they must understand that this emancipation will not be the work of a day, nor of a single effort; that it cannot be obtained by the simple substitution of new governors for old ones, but only by the total destruction of the institutions that oppress them" [003-015].

The capitalists, Grave observes, "declaim so loudly against the frightful Anarchists that have the audacity to demonstrate to the workers how their interest is antagonistic to that of the employers." Yet history shows that "the workers have always made revolutions, only to be duped and exploited by the bourgeoisie, who gathered the fruits" [003-012]. The Anarchist's role is to ensure this pattern does not repeat.

## The Bourgeois and Patriotism

The hypocrisy of bourgeois patriotism is a recurring theme in Grave's critique. "Messieurs the bourgeois being embarrassed with products which they cannot dispose of, find nothing better to do than to go and force peoples who ask nothing of them, to buy their merchandise at the point of the bayonet" [014-005]. The rhetoric of national glory masks the economic interests of the capitalist class: "As to 'the needs of commerce,' here, indeed, we have the genuine motive." What is celebrated as patriotic devotion is in reality the violent pursuit of markets under the protection of the state.

## Transcending Bourgeois Society

The ultimate answer to bourgeois society is not reform but replacement. The Anarchists "have only to analyze this enormous work, to coordinate it, to extract its essence." Their role is "limited to proving that it is not by changing governors that the ills from which we suffer may be cured; that it is not by merely modifying the machinery of the social organism that we shall prevent it from producing those evil effects which the very principles upon which it rests inevitably produce" [003-008]. What is required is a complete social transformation — the abolition of private property and authority, and the construction of a society founded on liberty, reciprocity, and solidarity.
