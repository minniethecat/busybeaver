# Anarchy

## Definition

Anarchy, as defined by [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]], signifies "the negation of authority" (001-002). It is not merely a political doctrine but a comprehensive critique of all social institutions that claim to justify their existence through the defense of established order — the family, religion, property, and [[government|the state]] apparatus. The anarchist analysis must "attack all social prejudices, go to the bottom of all human knowledge" to demonstrate that anarchist conceptions conform to "the physiological and psychological nature of man" (001-002).

## Historical Context

The anarchist movement of the late 19th century emerged from the broader socialist movement but distinguished itself by its rejection of both [[the-state|state]] power and parliamentary politics. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s work, published in [[france|France]] in the 1890s, represents the mature phase of anarchist communist thought following the decline of mutualism and the aftermath of the Paris Commune (1871). By the time of the Congrès du Centre (c. 1886), anarchist ideas had achieved sufficient coherence to be publicly affirmed, despite facing "those satisfied with the present social order" and "pseudo-r[[progress|evolution]]aries who only see in popular demands a means of grasping at power" (001-008).

The rapid growth of anarchist ideas in the 1880s-1890s — spreading to [[france|France]], Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, and beyond (001-008) — demonstrated what [[jean-grave|Grave]] calls the "expansive [[force-and-violence|force]]" of the idea. He attributes this success to the fact that anarchism offered answers to "secret sentiments" and "undefined aspirations" that existing society could not satisfy (001-010).


## Grave's Analysis

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the development of anarchist thought from its earliest expressions to its crystallization as [[communism|anarchist communism]]. He identifies Rabelais's Abbey of Thelemes as one of the first formulations of the anarchist idea "in its intuitional stage," though he notes it was limited to "a minority of privileged persons" (001-006). During the [[france|French]] [[revolution|Revolution]], Jacques Roux and "the madmen" (the Enragés) are credited by [[jean-grave|Grave]] as those who "saw most clearly" the r[[progress|evolution]]ary potential for the people (001-007).

The decisive turn toward modern anarchism came with Pierre-Joseph Proudhon, who first positioned Anarchy as "the adversary of authority and power" in a definite form. However, [[jean-grave|Grave]] criticizes Proudhon for leaving "in existence, under different names, the administrative machinery which is the very essence of [[the-state|government]]" (001-007). It was Mikhail Bakunin, through the Jurassian Federation in Switzerland, who developed and enlarged Proudhon's ideas, "incarnating it in social demands" — marking "the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement" (001-007).

> Starting out, with a simple political negation, the Anarchist has had to attack economic and social prejudices also, to find a formula which, while denying private property, the basis of our present economic order, should at the same time affirm our aspirations for a future organization. Hence the word Communism naturally came to be coupled with the word Anarchy. (001-003)

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies the basis of society as private property, and argues that "[[authority|Authority]] has but one excuse for existence: the defense of capital" (001-010). He asserts that bureaucracy, the family, [[militarism|the army]], and the magistracy all "flow directly from private property" (001-010). The anarchist task is therefore to "demonstrate the iniquity of the monopoly of the soil and of the fruits of the labor of past generations, by an idle minority" (001-010).
