# Money

## Money as the Representation of Capital

"Bourgeois society being founded upon capital," [[jean-grave|Grave]] writes, "and this being represented by money, the economists, in order to mask the peculiar role it plays in the work of production and exchange, have reduced everything to capital — products, men, intelligence" [005-010]. Money is not, in this analysis, a mere convenience of exchange. It is the concrete form that capital takes in bourgeois society, the visible token of a deeper system of exploitation. By reducing all human activity and all social relations to their monetary expression, bourgeois economics naturalizes what is in fact a historically specific and deeply unjust social arrangement.

## The Economists' Mystification of Money

[[jean-grave|Grave]] exposes the circular reasoning by which economists justify the existence of money-capital. "As all this reduction of human activity to capital does not explain the origin of money-capital, the economists have discovered that 'money-capital is that portion of labor which industrious and frugal persons have set aside and accumulated from their earnings'" [005-011]. This moralistic fable — that capital arises from the thrift and industry of its possessors — collapses under examination. Grave points to the reality: "But the employer — Oh! it is a different affair with him! In the first place he invests an original capital, the money-value of which was originally stolen from the workers" [005-013].

The economists' account systematically ignores that the original accumulation of money-capital was achieved through violence, dispossession, and the exploitation of labor. "By whatever sophisms the bourgeois economists seek to prop up their system in order to justify the existence of capitalists, one thing is sure: Capital does not reproduce itself and can be nothing but accumulated labor — but the labor of others accumulated in the hands of one person — a robber!" [005-015].

## Money, Labor, and the Degradation of Work

In the existing social order, [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes, "manual labor does not even enable one to keep himself from starving." The consequence is social perversion: "he who has only this resource, seeks to obtain money by every means except work, becoming an official, journalist, or whatever will bring him in more money than he could earn by the sweat of his brow" [002-012]. Money does not reward productive labor — it rewards proximity to the mechanisms of appropriation. Those who actually produce wealth are the poorest; those who manipulate money and power are the richest.

This inversion of values is not accidental. "In our present society, wherein we are accustomed to depend upon the toil of others to obtain the things necessary for existence, there is but one object: to procure money" [002-010]. The universal pursuit of money as an end in itself — rather than the satisfaction of human needs — distorts every social relation, turning cooperation into competition and solidarity into mutual predation.

## Money-Value and Force-Value

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates money within a broader evolutionary framework of social domination. Human society, he argues, has moved through successive forms of power: from brute force to cunning, and from cunning to the impersonal power of accumulated wealth. "It would be the cycle of evolution already run through and recommencing — the stronger oppressing the weaker until superseded by the cunning, until money-value should displace force-value" [002-008]. Money represents the most sophisticated form of domination yet devised: impersonal, invisible in its operation, and all the more effective for appearing natural and inevitable.

## Money and the Social War

The concentration of money in the hands of a minority is not a peaceful process. "The same war which goes on among the capitalists goes on also among the workers; and while the first compromises the stability of the bourgeois edifice, the second helps to secure its continued existence" [012-005]. Competition for money pits worker against worker, capitalist against capitalist, nation against nation. The universal scramble for monetary wealth produces "war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors" that bourgeois society then attributes to individual depravity rather than to the structural imperatives of its own economic order.

## The Anarchist Alternative

Against the rule of money, the Anarchists propose the direct satisfaction of human needs through free association and common ownership. "Without doubt the first work of the Anarchists towards making the revolution a success, will be to seize social wealth, to call upon the disinherited to take possession of stores, machinery, and the instruments of labor" [002-015]. The abolition of money is implicit in the abolition of the wage system and private property — for once production is organized for use rather than exchange, and once the means of production are held in common, the very function that money serves in capitalist society disappears.
