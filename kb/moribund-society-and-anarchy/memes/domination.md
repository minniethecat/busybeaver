# Domination

## Definition

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, domination refers to the asymmetrical power relations that structure the existing social order. Jean Grave treats domination not as an occasional abuse but as the organizing principle of state, capital, and church — institutions that together form an interlocking system of control over the individual. The abolition of all forms of domination is the central objective of Grave's anarchism.

## Grave's Analysis

Grave identifies multiple overlapping systems of domination in modern society. The state dominates through law, police, army, and bureaucracy — the "administrative machinery which is the very essence of government" [042-001]. Capital dominates through the ownership of the means of production, reducing workers to wage-dependence and extracting surplus value from their labor. The church dominates through moral authority and ideological control, teaching submission to earthly powers as a religious duty.

What makes Grave's analysis distinctive is his insistence that these systems of domination are mutually reinforcing. The state protects property; property funds the state; the church legitimizes both. No single element can be eliminated in isolation — partial reform that addresses one form of domination while leaving others intact will inevitably be subverted. This is why Grave rejects the cooperative movement and other reformist strategies: they accept the framework of domination while attempting to soften its effects, rather than challenging the framework itself.

Grave connects domination to the moral and psychological dimensions of human life. The person who dominates is diminished by the act of domination, just as the person dominated is diminished by subjection. Full human development — the "complete expansion of individuality" — is possible only when all relations of domination have been replaced by relations of voluntary association and mutual respect [036-001].
