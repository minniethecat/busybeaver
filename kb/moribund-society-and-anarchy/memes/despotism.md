In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] treats despotism as the concentrated expression of [[authority]] — the arbitrary and unchecked exercise of power by one person or group over others. Despotism is not an aberration from normal social functioning but the logical endpoint of the principle of [[authority]] itself: when power is concentrated and unaccountable, it inevitably becomes despotic. The "sanguinary and despotic instinct" [004-008] is not a rare pathological trait but a potential latent in the structure of command.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s understanding of despotism is rooted in his broader analysis of how the principle of [[authority]] pervades and corrupts every institution it touches. The present social organization, "established contrary to all logic and common sense, causes our societies to be unstable, overturned by revolutions, themselves occasioned by the accumulated hatred of those who are crushed under these arbitrary institutions" [001-002]. Arbitrariness — the absence of any rational or consensual basis for power — is the defining quality of despotic rule.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] wrote in the context of the [[france|French]] Third Republic, a regime that presented itself as democratic while maintaining despotic relations in its colonies and in its treatment of the working class. The contradiction between republican rhetoric and despotic practice was a central target of anarchist critique in this period, and [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of despotism as the inevitable companion of [[authority]] — regardless of the formal political structure — remains one of his most enduring contributions.

## Grave's Analysis---
id: despotism
type: concept
label: "Despotism"
aliases: ["despotic power", "tyranny", "despotic instinct"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, despotism represents the arbitrary exercise of power over others — a mode of domination that corrupts both the despot and the despotized, fostered by the institutions of authority and property."
tags: ["authority", "power", "government", "tyranny"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the despotic instinct to the conditions of social life under [[authority]]. "This life of continual struggle could not help developing the sanguinary and despotic instinct in people" [004-008]. The competitive, hierarchical organization of society cultivates precisely those traits — the will to dominate, the pleasure in mastery — that despotism embodies in its purest form.

At a deeper level, [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies the despotic impulse as a product of the historical conditions under which human association first emerged. In the earliest human groupings, "the struggle for existence" was the sole law, and "heredity having developed in them the instincts of combativeness, of oppression towards the victim, (everything being a prey to man, even man himself) it follows according to all the evidence that this spirit of struggle and domination, stored up in the brain by past generations, sought to gain like precedence in the organized collectivity. Those persons who possessed it in the highest degree imposed their will upon those who possessed it in a lesser degree" [005-009]. Despotism is thus not a deviation from human nature but a survival within it — an inheritance from an epoch in which domination was the organizing principle of social life. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s conclusion points toward a different possibility: [[authority]] "will maintain itself until man, freed from error and prejudice, reconquers himself entirely, renouncing the imposition of his will upon others in order not to have to submit to the will of those stronger than himself" [005-009].

Despotism also operates at the level of national sovereignty. [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that allowing a conquered territory to be "dismembered would be to permit a step backward, to lose the fruit of past struggles; for, vanquished by a despotic power, what would become of a small nation?" [011-008]. Here despotism appears not only as internal tyranny but as the principle governing relations between states — the strong dominate the weak, and the weak are told to accept their subordination as the price of survival.

## The Psychology of Despotism---
id: despotism
type: concept
label: "Despotism"
aliases: ["despotic power", "tyranny", "despotic instinct"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, despotism represents the arbitrary exercise of power over others — a mode of domination that corrupts both the despot and the despotized, fostered by the institutions of authority and property."
tags: ["authority", "power", "government", "tyranny"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] offers a distinctive psychological account of how despotism operates — not merely as an external imposition but as a dynamic that reshapes the character of both the despot and the despotized. The despotic relationship is a two-way corruption: it cultivates arrogance and cruelty in those who wield power while instilling fear, servility, and the habit of submission in those who endure it.

The psychology of command itself generates a structural antagonism that makes despotism inevitable. [[jean-grave|Grave]] puts the matter with devastating clarity:

> So long as there are persons to give commands, will they not necessarily be in antagonism with those they command? Will not those in power, if they be sincere, have ideas of their own to further? And these ideas, though they may be good, may also be very bad. Drowned in the mass, they will remain without power; with authority in the hands of those who profess them, they will be thrust upon those who reject them. And the more sincere the individuals in power the more pitiless would they be against those who should revolt against their way of seeing things, being convinced that they were working for the good of humanity. [007-011]

This is one of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most penetrating insights: sincerity and good intentions do not mitigate despotism — they intensify it. The ruler who genuinely believes in the rightness of his cause will be all the more ruthless in suppressing dissent, precisely because he cannot conceive of opposition as anything other than error or malice. The [[authority|authoritarian]] reformer, the benevolent despot, the revolutionary who becomes a tyrant — all follow the same logic. Power does not merely corrupt; it convinces the powerful that their corruption is virtue.

This psychological dynamic is reinforced by the institutional structure in which command operates. [[jean-grave|Grave]] shows how even putatively democratic institutions reproduce despotic psychology. Universal suffrage, far from checking despotism, is "but a perfected instrument of authority, which enslaved those who made use of it at the very moment they expected to emancipate themselves" [007-001]. The voter, having cast his ballot, "has signed his abdication; he has no more to do but bend to the caprices of the masters of his choice" [007-002]. The despotic relationship is thus reproduced through the very mechanisms that are supposed to prevent it — a deeper and more insidious form of domination than open tyranny, because it persuades the dominated that they have freely chosen their chains.

## Despotism and the Law---
id: despotism
type: concept
label: "Despotism"
aliases: ["despotic power", "tyranny", "despotic instinct"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, despotism represents the arbitrary exercise of power over others — a mode of domination that corrupts both the despot and the despotized, fostered by the institutions of authority and property."
tags: ["authority", "power", "government", "tyranny"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of despotism extends to the institution of [[the-law|law]] itself, which he argues is not a check on arbitrary power but its codification. The shift from divine right to the rule of law, far from eliminating despotism, merely disguised it:

> The codification of authority, according to these, its censer-bearers, had the immediate effect of legitimizing the most shameless arbitrariness. From then on Frenchmen were all to be equal; the people no longer had anything to demand. Thereafter there was to be but one master, before whom, it is true, all had to bow, which had the effect of equalizing their situations. This master was the "law." [008-003]

Under the ancien regime, the king's commands were obeyed because he had the sword; no one pretended they were just. Under the regime of legality, the same despotic relations are enforced, but now accompanied by the moral claim that obedience is a duty. This is despotism perfected — not merely compelling submission but demanding that the submissive regard their submission as righteous.

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the core problem is that the laws are made without the consent of those who must obey them. The anarchist critique of [[authority]] teaches "that not only must the people not be contented with changing the persons in power, with partially modifying the institutions which govern them, but that, before all, the people must destroy that which makes men bad, which makes it possible for a minority to make use of social forces to oppress the majority" [001-010]. Despotism is not a malfunction of the system but its normal mode of operation.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends the analysis of despotism to the colonial project. The colonized peoples "endure the same injustices, the same turpitudes, with which they are helping to burden others; and they no longer feel the ignominy of their condition" [014-009]. The despotic relationship is corrosive on both sides — it degrades the colonized and brutalizes the colonizer, normalizing domination until it becomes invisible to those who practice it.

This analysis places [[jean-grave|Grave]] within the tradition of anarchist anti-colonialism, which recognized the connection between domestic [[authority]] and imperial domination. The same structures of command that oppress workers at home are exported abroad in the form of colonial administration, and the same despotic instincts are cultivated in both contexts.

## Resisting Despotism---
id: despotism
type: concept
label: "Despotism"
aliases: ["despotic power", "tyranny", "despotic instinct"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, despotism represents the arbitrary exercise of power over others — a mode of domination that corrupts both the despot and the despotized, fostered by the institutions of authority and property."
tags: ["authority", "power", "government", "tyranny"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s response to despotism is characteristically direct: "What may we preach as believers in the [[revolution]] that we may the more certainly bring it about? The uplifting of human dignity and character, independence of the will which makes us resent command, makes us rebels against despotism and repudiate what seems false and absurd to us" [017-013].

This is not a call for reform of despotic institutions but for the cultivation of a character that refuses command as such. The resistance to despotism begins not with political action but with the internal rejection of the legitimacy of [[authority]] — the refusal to accept that any person has the right to command another. [[anarchy|Anarchism]], in this sense, is the systematic cultivation of anti-despotic character.

The psychology of resistance mirrors but inverts the psychology of despotism. Where despotism relies on the habit of submission — the "strange contradiction of the human mind" by which people "submit to this authority with reluctance, they scoff at it, violate it when they can, and believe themselves lost when anyone talks of doing away with it" [007-006] — resistance requires the recovery of independence of will. The task is not merely to overthrow particular despots but to root out the despotic instinct in oneself: the desire to command, the willingness to be commanded, the habit of looking to others for direction.
