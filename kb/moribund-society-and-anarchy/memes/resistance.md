# Resistance

## Resistance as a Natural Force

[[jean-grave|Grave]] develops a sustained geological metaphor to explain the nature of resistance to social oppression. Just as rain "gnaws into the mountains, filters through them, and disintegrates the hardest granite" over centuries, social institutions that uphold exploitation are slowly eroded by the accumulated pressure of discontent [016-001]. "Nothing reveals the slow work of disintegration which is going on, or betrays it to the eye," yet "some fine day the mountain crumbles, dragging down forests and villages" — a revolution in which the barriers of the old order collapse [016-001].

> It is the same with our societies: the social organization, the institutions created to defend this organization, represent the barriers which are opposed to progress. [016-003]

The sea analogy deepens this vision: "the sea, borrowing new strength from the very resistance it meets upon its march, will invade the plain, destroying everything in its course until it is arrested at the foot of a new barrier" [016-002]. Resistance is not a static force but one that gains power from the obstacles it encounters, transforming them into weapons: "this stone, the waves roll it away with them and, in the next attack, hurl it against the wall whence it was torn, make use of it as a battering ram to tear out others" [016-003].

## The Right to Resist

[[jean-grave|Grave]] grounds the right to resist in the fundamentally coercive nature of [[authority]] and [[the-law|law]]. "In our society," he observes, "there is not a single law which does not injure some of its members, either in their material interests or their ideas" [008-007]. Since law is "nothing but the will of the strongest, one is obliged to obey it only when too weak to resist it" [008-007]. The famous "legality" that the ruling classes invoke is "only a question of more or less force" — a recognition that transforms resistance from a moral transgression into a practical necessity [008-007].

The [[government|workers]] who secured universal suffrage through revolt were subsequently "tricked out of this newly-acquired right" by the bourgeoisie — only to learn that "it was but a perfected instrument of authority, which enslaved those who made use of it at the very moment they expected to emancipate themselves" [007-005]. Resistance, then, cannot be channeled through institutional mechanisms; it must directly confront the institutions themselves.

## Evolution or Revolution

[[jean-grave|Grave]] emphasizes that he would prefer a peaceful transformation: "Most assuredly we should ask no better than that the evolution of our society should be accomplished in a slow but continuous fashion" [016-004]. But the bourgeoisie, by refusing every concession, make revolution inevitable. "They are not willing to yield up any portion of their privileges. Like the cliffs they deem themselves invulnerable to the surges that attack them" [016-005].

> We have contributed to evolution; let them take it upon themselves and their senseless resistance if it be transformed into revolution! [016-005]

The responsibility for revolutionary violence therefore lies with those who refuse to yield. Just as the sea's accumulated pressure cannot be held back indefinitely by cliffs that refuse to erode, so the masses' accumulated grievances will break through any barrier — peacefully if possible, by force if necessary.

## The Necessity of Intelligent Resistance

Not all acts of resistance are equally valid. [[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes between intelligent, targeted resistance and blind destruction. The "incendiary burning of a factory in full operation" that employs large numbers of workmen, without "rhyme or reason," serves only to throw workers into the street and turn them against the ideas that prompted the act [020-003]. By contrast, acts of resistance directed at "some employers more cruel than others, who by their exactions have necessitated" a strike — these are "intelligent deeds" whose meaning is unmistakeable and which "will be applauded by the whole laboring world" [020-003].

He who "is incapable of making any sacrifice for the principles he claims to profess, does not really believe in them at all" [023-004]. True resistance demands the willingness to act in accordance with one's convictions, whatever the cost.
