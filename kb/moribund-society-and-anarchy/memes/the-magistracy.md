# The Magistracy

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist critique, the magistracy is the institutional embodiment of class justice — the corps of judges, prosecutors, and court officials who apply the [[the-law|law]], not with the impartiality they claim, but in the service of the property-owning classes whose interests the [[the-law|law]] exists to defend. [[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the magistracy's historical origins and its role in the contemporary social order, arguing that it is not a neutral arbiter of disputes but a pillar of class domination — one of the "tools" that authority "has created to secure it in the exercise of its functions and its sanctioning privilege" [001-002].

## Historical Origins

The magistracy, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues, was created not to serve justice but to serve power. It was "founded" as an instrument of "the king and the nobles" — a mechanism for enforcing the will of the [[class|ruling class]] against those they ruled [008-002]. When the bourgeoisie seized power in 1789, "they took care not to destroy this pillar of social order." The r[[progress|evolution]] that overthrew the monarchy preserved the magistracy intact, simply transferring its allegiance from the feudal aristocracy to the capitalist class.

The shift in the magistracy's legitimating ideology reflects this transfer of power. The divine right of kings could no longer serve as the basis of judicial authority after the decapitation of Louis XVI — "the magistracy could not continue to lean upon the said right without the risk of likewise passing under this equalizing leveler" [008-003]. In place of divine right, the bourgeoisie substituted the myth of popular sovereignty and the rule of [[the-law|law]]. But the function remained unchanged: the en[[force-and-violence|force]]ment of property rights and the suppression of challenges to the established order.

> When the bourgeoisie seized the power, in 1789, they took care not to destroy this pillar of social order. [008-002]

## The Magistracy as Class Retainer

[[jean-grave|Grave]] addresses the magistrates directly with a rhetorical challenge: "Recognize, gentlemen of the magistracy, that you are its retainers, and let us alone with your big words and fine phrases" [008-008]. The "big words" — justice, impartiality, the rule of [[the-law|law]] — are ideological cover for a simple function: "Maintain the privileges whose care you have assumed." The magistrate who imposes a prison sentence on a thief while dismissing the crimes of the capitalist is not administering justice; he is protecting property.

The magistracy, together with the police and [[militarism|the army]], forms the coercive apparatus of [[government|the state]]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] describes this as an integrated system: "[[authority|Authority]], the family, the magistracy, [[militarism|the army]], and every hierarchic and bureaucratic organization, which stifles and devours us, proceed from this principle" — the principle of private property [005-003]. Each institution rein[[force-and-violence|force]]s the others; the magistracy provides the legal sanction for the [[force-and-violence|violence]] that the police and army execute.

## The Scientific Refutation of Judicial Authority

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws on contemporary science to undermine the intellectual foundations of the magistracy. The doctrine of free will — the premise that individuals choose their actions and can therefore be held morally accountable — has been disproven. If human conduct is the product of "environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences," then the moral condemnation pronounced by judges is irrational [009-002]. The accused in the dock is not a free moral agent but a product of circumstances that, had the judge been subjected to them, would have produced the same result.

The criminal anthropology that claimed to identify born criminals by physical characteristics has been exposed as pseudo-science by "more enlightened [[science|savants]]" [009-013]. The entire apparatus of judicial judgment — the assessment of guilt, the determination of moral responsibility, the imposition of punishment — rests on premises that science has demolished. The magistracy continues to function not because it is intellectually defensible but because it serves a class function that the possessing classes will not relinquish.

## The Abolition of the Magistracy

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the magistracy cannot be reformed — it is, by its nature, an instrument of class rule. The abolition of the magistracy is inseparable from the abolition of the conditions that require it: private property, class hierarchy, and [[government|the state]] itself. In an anarchist society, disputes would be resolved not by professional judges applying [[the-law|law]]s written by the powerful but by the free agreement of the parties involved, [[press|media]]ted by the community when necessary. The "metaphysical entity" of justice, "with which the magistracy re-invested itself in order to keep itself above society, must likewise disappear" [009-019].
