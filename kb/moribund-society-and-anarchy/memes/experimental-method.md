# Experimental Method

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, Jean Grave devotes Chapter XX to a sustained critique of how the **experimental method** is invoked by defenders of the existing social order. Reformists and gradualists claim that anarchism is unscientific because it has not been "tested," while insisting that change must proceed slowly through piecemeal reforms. Grave argues that this position is both logically inconsistent and morally bankrupt — it demands that the exploited classes endure indefinite suffering while their oppressors conduct endless "experiments" that never address root causes.

## The Reformist Position

Grave characterizes the gradualism argument as follows:

> "The rational and experimental method only can lead to any results, and Anarchism is in no sense scientific; it is a pure speculation emanating from praiseworthy sentiments but based upon no experimentation." [020-724]

The reformist stance holds that [[revolution]] risks losing the relative security that existing [[society]] provides, and that gradual improvement is the safer path:

> "'Bad as it is, society still ensures a relative degree of security, a certain rate of progress, which a revolution might sacrifice. Let us,' say they, 'seek gradually to improve what we have; slowly, indeed, but surely.'" [020-724]

## The Medical Analogy

Grave deploys a powerful medical analogy to refute gradualism. When a physician discovers the true cause of a disease, the correct treatment may reverse all previous practice:

> "In physiology, when, after considerable groping in darkness the causes of a disease which up till then had been known only by its effects, are at length discovered, it may happen that the method of curing it is completely reversed; what had before been forbidden to the patient may now be prescribed for him." [020-732]

The physician who innovates is treated as a madman by the medical establishment — just as anarchists are treated as visionaries:

> "The physician who experiments with a new method, does on a small scale what the Anarchists want to do on a large scale with our entire society." [020-732]

## Who Are the True Empiricists?

Grave reverses the charge of empiricism against the reformists themselves. Centuries of [[reform]] have demonstrated that piecemeal changes never resolve fundamental injustices:

> "Ah, well! Whatever our opponents say, it is they who are the empiricists; the experiments they have been making for centuries with the reforms and plasters which they recommend to us, prove to us that there are no true and effective reforms save those which attack the institutions, the bases, upon which society rests." [020-736]

The [[bourgeoisie]], holding power through institutions like [[the-state]], [[the-magistracy]], the [[police]], and the army ([[militarism]]), will never permit reforms that threaten its existence:

> "Now, to touch institutions is the Revolution. The bourgeoisie, which holds the power, will not allow its own existence to be imperiled." [020-736]

## The Falsity of "Step-by-Step" Progress

Grave notes the contradiction in gradualism: humanity has been experimenting with authority-based social organization for millennia with the same results — exploitation of the many by the few:

> "They forget that here it is three or four thousand years, for the Latin races only, that these experiments and step-by-step policies have been going on, that the exploited have awaited the realization of the promises made to them, have waxed passionate and struggled to obtain the same reforms forever promised though their situation has in nowise altered." [020-742]

Every new invention, every technological [[progress]], under the current system benefits only the possessing class while workers are thrown into unemployment and wage [[competition]]:

> "Ah! It is because society is so organized that everything beautiful and good must finally concentrate in the hands of those who possess capital. Every fresh step in progress profits him, and him only, who has the wherewithal to put it into operation." [020-738]

## The Demand for Proof

Ultimately, Grave argues, the demand for "proof" that anarchism will work is circular — it can only be tested by implementing it:

> "They want proofs that society will go on as we foresee. We may draw conclusions from the logic of events, form a comparison of them through the arguments we may gather from the analysis thereof. But palpable proofs! Experiment alone can give them to us, and these experiments can only be made by commencing with the overthrow of existing society!" [020-785]

The refusal to allow such experimentation, while simultaneously demanding experimental evidence, reveals the reformist position as intellectually dishonest — a mechanism for preserving the status quo indefinitely.
