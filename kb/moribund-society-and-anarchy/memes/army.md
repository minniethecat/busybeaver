# Army

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, the army is "one of the first [castes] to be constituted and to impose its authority upon other members of the clan or tribe" [013-002]. From its origins as a closed warrior caste, it evolved into the bourgeoisie's perfected instrument for forcing "the workers to devote a certain portion of their youth to the defense of their masters" [013-002].

## Historical Development

The army did not begin as the institution of mass conscription known to modernity. Originally a closed caste "recruiting itself from within, making war upon its own account," the warrior class was one of the earliest social stratifications [013-002]. The "military caste was not the last to form, develop, and become preponderant everywhere" — wherever it ceded precedence to the priestly caste, it was "merely an honorary precedence" [013-001]. "Was it not at bottom the military caste which could, by its cooperation, ensure stability of power?" [013-001].

Over centuries, successive transformations occurred: "lords began to arm the peasants on their domains during emergencies," then "by means of bounties or by stratagem, they attracted such as it was desired to have enlist in the king's armies" [013-002]. But the decisive turn came with the bourgeoisie, which "threw the burden of its defense entirely upon its slaves" [013-002]. The modern conscript army is "the bourgeoisie which has perfected the system by forcing the workers to devote a certain portion of their youth to the defense of their masters" [013-002].

## The Army and the State

The army is inseparable from the broader apparatus of state authority. As one of the "mass of tools" created by authority "to secure it in the exercise of its functions and its sanctioning privilege," the army stands alongside "the law, the magistracy, the legislative and executive power" as instruments of class domination [001-003]. Together with "bureaucracy, the family, the magistracy," the army "flows directly from private property" [001-010]. Its fundamental purpose is not national defense but the protection of "the defense of capital" [001-010].

> "It is well to observe that the idea of the country, the nation, as it is called, summed up the whole of the people, their rights and institutions, rather than the soil itself." [013-003]

The ideology of patriotism was invented precisely because the bourgeoisie "could not, without some danger, put arms into their hands and say to them: 'Protect me in my possessions'" — so "it invented the worship of 'the country'" [013-002]. The army becomes the physical enforcement of a metaphysical construct.

## Critique of Military Service

Grave's critique is reinforced by the chapter on [[militarism]], where the barracks is described as a dungeon where workers "rot, morally and physically" for years "without anyone dreaming of resisting or escaping, without a single voice being raised to inquire by what right people are asked to be transformed... into automatons, machines for killing and food for cannon" [013-002]. The army is not a school of civic virtue but a "school of equality in brutalization" [013-008].

The class character of military service is explicit: "up to our own day, it has been the inferior classes, those whose influence counts the least, who have generally furnished the common soldiers. The wealthy classes have escaped by a money sacrifice" [013-014]. The army's officer corps, drawn from the bourgeoisie, "conferred the monopoly of the remunerative offices of the military profession" upon the already-privileged [013-014].

## War and Working-Class Interests

Grave argues that war — the army's primary function — "can bring no good to the workers; we have no interests engaged in it, nothing to defend but our skins" [013-020]. The bourgeoisie "have an interest in war; it enables them to preserve the armies which keep the people respectful, and defend their institutions" [013-020]. They "alone subscribe to the loans which war necessitates, the interest upon which we, the workers, alone pay" [013-020].

The army's deployment against the working class is not hypothetical but demonstrated by history. Capitalists "do not hide from themselves the fact that they see nothing in war but a means of continuing their exploitation of the workers" [013-016]. "To them armies are created with the sole view of furnishing place and rank to those of their dependents by whom they would otherwise be importuned" [013-016].
