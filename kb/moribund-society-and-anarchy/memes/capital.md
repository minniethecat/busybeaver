# Capital

## Definition

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, capital refers to the accumulated wealth, property, and means of production that are held in private hands and used to extract surplus value from labor. Jean Grave treats capital not merely as an economic category but as a social relation — the institutionalized power of owners over workers that defines the existing social order. The "capitalistic regime" is, for Grave, one of the principal targets of anarchist critique, alongside the state and organized religion [044-001].

## Grave's Analysis

Grave describes the defenders of the "capitalistic regime" as actively working to "injure and persecute" the anarchist movement, a sign he interprets as evidence of the regime's fear of anarchism's growing strength [044-001]. He traces the development of capitalism in France through the post-Commune period, noting how the revolutionary energy of 1871 gave way to "the misled and misleading movement of cooperative associations for production and consumption" [042-001] — a reformist detour that Grave views as a retreat from genuine revolutionary transformation.

Grave connects capital to the broader structures of authority and oppression. In his analysis, the capitalist class — the bourgeoisie — maintains its position not only through economic power but through the entire apparatus of law, government, and ideology. The juridical system, the police, the army, and the educational institutions all serve, in his view, to perpetuate the dominance of capital over labor. Grave consistently emphasizes that capital cannot be reformed away but must be abolished along with the state itself, as both are mutually reinforcing systems of domination.
