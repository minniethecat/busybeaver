# Conquest

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist vocabulary, conquest is not a neutral historical process but a form of organized violence and theft elevated to a principle of state policy. He systematically demystifies conquest by stripping it of its civilizing pretensions and revealing it as "robbery, pillage, assassination, disguised under the pompous names of conquest, colonization, civilization, patriotism" [001-006]. This rhetorical unmasking is central to his method: the language of power conceals crimes beneath euphemisms, and the anarchist's task is to name them plainly.

## Conquest as State Brigandage

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a direct parallel between the violence of the individual criminal and the violence of the conquering state, arguing that the only difference is one of scale and impunity. "A private individual goes into his neighbor's house, steals or kills" — this is called crime, and society punishes it. Yet when the same acts are performed by "armed bands" in the service of the [[the-state|state]], they are celebrated as heroic conquests [008-012]. The hypocrisy lies in the fact that "what the [[ruling-class|ruling classes]] must have is new markets for their products" — colonial conquest serves economic interests, not civilization.

The history of conquest, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s reading, reveals a consistent pattern: the conquering power arrives with superior weaponry, declares the indigenous population "savages," and proceeds to dispossess and exterminate them under the banner of bringing civilization. "Let anyone take up the history of conquests, and then tell us which were the most savage" — the colonizers or the colonized [008-020].

## The Ideology of Conquest

Conquest is sustained not merely by military superiority but by an ideological apparatus that naturalizes domination. [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies several ideological supports: [[patriotism]], which transforms predatory expeditions into sacred national missions; religion, which blesses the conqueror's cause; and pseudo-scientific racism, which constructs the conquered as innately inferior beings in need of tutelage.

The rhetorical formula — "They are savages; they must be civilized" — recurs throughout the history of European expansion and serves, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], as the quintessential example of how language obscures material interests [008-018]. Underneath every civilizing mission lies the drive for [[exploitation|economic exploitation]]: new territories to extract resources from, new populations to conscript as labor, and new markets to absorb surplus commodities.

## Consequences

Wherever the "right of conquest" has been exercised — in the Americas by Spain, in India by England, in [[africa|Africa]] by the European powers — the result has been the same: "everywhere the native populations decrease" [016-014]. Conquest does not integrate peoples into a higher civilization; it destroys existing societies, disrupts their modes of subsistence, and condemns survivors to servitude or marginalization. The demographic catastrophe that follows conquest is not an unfortunate side-effect but the logical outcome of a system that treats human beings as obstacles to the extraction of wealth.
