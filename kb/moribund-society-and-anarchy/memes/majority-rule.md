# Majority Rule

## Definition

Majority rule is the principle that political decisions should be determined by the numerical majority, typically expressed through [[universal-suffrage|universal suffrage]] and representative [[government|government]]. [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] subjects this principle to sustained critique, arguing that majority rule is not the democratic safeguard it purports to be but rather a mechanism by which a small minority perpetuates its [[authority|domination]] under the guise of popular consent.

## Grave's Three-Layered Critique

Grave develops his critique of majority rule through a systematic analysis of how representative democracy actually functions, rather than how it is theoretically supposed to function.

### First Layer: The Circumscription of Candidates

Grave observes that "in the first place, the candidates among whom the voter is to choose are not selected by him" (007-004). The pool of candidates is pre-filtered by party machines, wealthy patrons, and established political networks. The voter is presented with a menu of options already curated by the ruling class, ensuring that whoever wins will be broadly acceptable to existing power structures. This "original circumscription" means that the electoral process is never truly open.

### Second Layer: The Manufactured Majority

"In the second place," Grave continues, "it is theoretically the majority which on election day decides who is elected out of this original circumscription; but practically, it is a very small minority of intriguers, wire-pullers, and talkers who control the election" (007-004). The apparent power of the majority is systematically undermined by campaign financing, media manipulation, and the structural advantages enjoyed by established political actors. The majority does not choose — it ratifies choices already made by others.

### Third Layer: The Illusion of Parliamentary Decision

"In the third place," Grave argues, "those elected being once assembled, it is again the majority which, theoretically always, is supposed to decide among them; but here again it is the wire-pullers, the orators, who by skillfully arranged parliamentary maneuvers, succeed in passing measures of which the majority never dreamed" (007-004). Even within the legislature, the principle of majority rule is subverted by procedural manipulation, party discipline, and the influence of external interests.

## The Character of the Majority

Grave's critique goes beyond procedural manipulation to question the very nature of majority opinion. He argues that "the bulk of the masses professes average current ideas; it is they who compose the majority; it is they who will choose the representative, who, in order to be elected, will have to flatter them, and appropriate their average ideas" (007-005). In a society where traditional [[prejudice|prejudices]], [[religion|religious]] dogma, and [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] values dominate popular consciousness, majority rule serves to reinforce rather than challenge existing power structures.

The majority, shaped by the very institutions that oppress it, tends to elect representatives who will preserve those institutions. Democratic procedures thus become a feedback loop: an ignorant majority elects self-interested representatives who maintain the conditions that keep the majority ignorant.

## Beyond Majority Rule

Grave does not propose reforming majority rule to make it more genuine. His conclusion is more radical: "there ought not to be a majority-law any more than a divine-law" (007-004). The anarchist alternative is not better democracy but the abolition of all forms of rule, whether by one, by few, or by many.

This position follows from Grave's foundational commitment to individual [[liberty|freedom]]. If each individual is the best judge of their own interests, then no external authority — not even a democratic majority — has the right to compel obedience. Grave asks: "If this law injures my interests or violates my liberty why should I be compelled to obey it, and what is the unalterable compact which can justify this subjugation?" (008-004). Majority rule offers no satisfactory answer to this question — the mere fact that more people support a law does not make it just to enforce it against those who do not.

## Universal Suffrage as a Perfected Instrument

Grave's analysis of [[universal-suffrage|universal suffrage]] connects directly to his critique of majority rule. Universal suffrage, he argues, is "but a perfected instrument of authority, which enslaved those who made use of it at the very moment they expected to emancipate themselves" (007-001). The extension of the vote to all citizens did not diminish the power of the ruling class — it refined and legitimized it, giving the appearance of popular sovereignty while preserving the reality of minority rule.
