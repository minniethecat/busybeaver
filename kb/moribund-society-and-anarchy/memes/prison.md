# Prison

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] treats the prison as a paradigmatic institution of authoritarian society — a concrete manifestation of [[government|the state]]'s function as an instrument of class rule. The prison, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, does not deter crime, rehabilitate offenders, or dispense justice; it serves to discipline the poor, to punish dissent, and to legitimate the existing order by creating a visible category of "criminals" against whom "honest" citizens can define themselves [009-014]. The very existence of prisons, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues, is an indictment of a society that produces the conditions of crime while punishing its victims.

## Grave's Analysis

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of the prison system appears most prominently in his critique of the "[[science|savants]]" — the criminologists and penal theorists who provide intellectual cover for the carceral apparatus. He notes with approval that "some few more enlightened [[science|savants]] themselves have not been slow to offer criticism upon the by far too fanciful theories of the criminalistic school" [009-013]. The Lombrosian theory of the "born criminal" — which claimed to identify criminal types by physical stigmata — is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], a pseudo-scientific justification for class oppression.

> Drawn on by the truth of his argument the doctor did not, moreover, hesitate to acknowledge that, very often, it is difficult to distinguish the alleged criminal from the alleged honest man; and that many an individual out of prison ought to be in it, and vice versa. [009-014]

This insight — that the distinction between "criminal" and "honest man" is socially constructed rather than biologically determined — is central to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique. The true criminals, in his view, are those who exploit and oppress: the capitalist who extracts surplus value, the militarist who [[wage-labor|wages]] war, the colonial administrator who exterminates indigenous peoples. These crimes go unpunished while the poor are incarcerated for offenses born of the very misery the system creates.

## The Prison and Militarism

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a striking parallel between the prison and the [[militarism|military]] barracks, both of which he regards as institutions that "debase, brutalize, corrupt" those who pass through them [013-017]. The soldier and the prisoner share a common condition: both are subjected to a total institution that strips away individuality and en[[force-and-violence|force]]s compliance through [[force-and-violence|violence]] and degradation.

> "But if it be impossible for us to remain calm," you will ask; "if, in spite of us, the blood rise to our heads making us blush?" Then there is but one thing for you: do not set foot in this prison, whence you cannot reissue without being debased, brutalized, corrupted. If you wish to remain men, do not renounce your will. [013-017]

This passage, though nominally about [[militarism|military]] conscription, captures [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s broader critique of coercive institutions. The prison, like [[militarism|the army]], is a machine for the destruction of human autonomy. To enter it is to be "debased, brutalized, corrupted" — to surrender one's will to an external authority that recognizes no limits to its power over the individual.

## Historical Context

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of the prison reflects the broader anarchist engagement with penal reform in late nineteenth-century [[france|France]]. The [[france|French]] anarchist movement maintained close ties with the penal reform movement, and anarchist journals regularly published exposés of prison conditions and critiques of criminological theory. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s argument that prisons punish poverty rather than crime, and that the true criminals occupy the seats of power, was a common theme in [[propaganda|anarchist propaganda]] of the period. It anticipated the prison abolitionist perspective that would develop more fully in the twentieth century.
