# The Press

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the press — despite its self-presentation as an independent "fourth e[[the-state|state]]" that holds power to account — is in practice one of the most effective instruments by which the possessing classes manufacture consent for their rule. The formal freedom of the press is belied by its material dependence on capital, its structural subordination to [[the-state|government]], and its ideological function in shaping public opinion to accept the existing order. It is not that the press occasionally errs or is sometimes biased; it is that the press, as an institution, is *constituted* by the same property relations it claims to scrutinize. [[jean-grave|Grave]] himself experienced this directly: his own work was "published by P. V. Stock, printer of numerous works pertaining to [[anarchy|Anarchy]]" — an independent publisher outside the commercial press establishment [[TOC|P03]]-001]. When [[government|the state]] suppressed his book, an alternative publisher, "E. Pouget, the daring publisher of *Père Peinard*," brought out another edition [[TOC|P03]]-004] — demonstrating both the press's dependence on the goodwill of power and the possibility of resistance through alternative publishing channels. The anarchist press thus operates under the same structural pressures that "everything is linked together in this system which is crushing us" — "no amelioration is possible save by the destruction of this social order" that sustains the commercial media [019-036] [020-026].

## The Material Basis of Press Freedom

The formal liberty of the press — the absence of [[the-state|state]] censorship — is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], an insufficient guarantee of genuine [[emancipation|freedom]]. A press that is formally free but materially dependent on capital is not free in any meaningful sense. The costs of printing, distribution, and staffing mean that newspapers are enterprises like any other, requiring capital investment and generating returns for their owners.

This material dependence has consequences. The newspaper cannot consistently attack the property system on which its own existence as a commercial enterprise depends. It may criticize this or that abuse, this or that politician, but it cannot question the foundations of the order that sustains it. The press is free, in practice, only "for those who own a press." [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s own experience is instructive: the original edition of his book was "published by P. V. Stock, printer of numerous works pertaining to [[anarchy|Anarchy]]" — a niche publisher, not a mass-circulation newspaper [[TOC|P03]]-001]. When the [[france|French]] [[the-state|state]] suppressed this edition, "E. Pouget, the daring publisher of *Père Peinard*, brought out another, ostensibly published in London" [[TOC|P03]]-004]. The suppression and relocation of a single book illustrates the structural vulnerability of dissenting voices. [[jean-grave|Grave]] notes that even "ideas strong enough to inspire men with a conviction which makes them fight and suffer for the propagation thereof" must struggle against a media system where "everything is linked together in this system which is crushing us" — and "finally, if every branch of human knowledge be reviewed, it will be seen that all our aspirations are hindered by the present social organization" [019-036] [020-026].

The result is that the range of opinion represented in the commercial press is narrowed not by censorship but by the market. "In our present society, wherein we are accustomed to depend upon the toil of others to obtain the things necessary for existence, there is but one object: to procure money enough to enable one to buy" — and this imperative governs the press as thoroughly as any other industry [002-014]. Views that threaten the interests of property are simply not profitable to disseminate; they attract no advertising, invite legal harassment, and alienate the affluent readership that advertisers seek.

## The Press as an Instrument of Government

Beyond its material dependence on capital, the press is structurally aligned with [[the-state|government]]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] notes how the major newspapers function as transmission belts for official narratives:

> Governmental fury, oppressive measures, the rage of frustrated ambition, may set themselves against the ideas and their propagators; today the opening is made; they can no longer be prevented from making their way. [001-015]

The press participates in this [[the-state|government]]al fury — not always at the direct [[education|instruction]] of [[government|the state]] but through the convergence of interests. The newspaper that depends on [[the-state|government]] advertising, access to officials, and the goodwill of the powerful has strong incentives to align its coverage with the interests of those powers. [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that the commercial press is an integral part of "this exploiting society which is crushing us" — "it is not enough to modify its machinery, to improve its mode of acting, for us to believe its results can thereby be rendered less fatal" [019-023]. [[reform|Reform]] of the press is impossible because the press is not an aberration of the system but an expression of it.

Even the "opposition" press serves a governing function: it channels discontent into safe channels, creates the impression of debate without threatening the framework within which debate occurs. "If the republic has enjoyed so much credit among the workers; if, after so many deceptions, [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] is still considered by the governed as a means of [[emancipation|enfranchisement]], it is because they have not yet understood" [007-009] — and this persistent misunderstanding is sustained, in large part, by a press that frames the choice as between different parties rather than between authority and liberty. The existence of newspapers that criticize [[the-state|the government]] while accepting the system of [[the-state|government]] is not evidence of press [[emancipation|freedom]] but of its co-optation.

## The Manufacture of Public Opinion

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is attentive to the press's role in shaping what passes for "public opinion." The opinions that citizens hold are not spontaneously generated from direct experience — they are mediated by the information environment in which they live. The press does not merely report what the public thinks; it *produces* what the public thinks by selecting which events are newsworthy, which interpretations are plausible, and which voices are authoritative.

This function is particularly important in modern societies where most citizens have no direct experience of the events that determine their lives. The worker who never meets a [[the-state|government]] minister, never visits a colony, never sees a balance sheet — this worker's understanding of the world is almost entirely constructed by the press. The press thus exercises a power more profound than [[the-law|legislation]]: it shapes the categories through which [[the-law|legislation]] is understood. [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies this as a systemic feature — "social authority, while continuing to preach the oppression of the individual for the sake of the collectivity — this dogma has contributed to its maintenance even as much as [[force-and-violence|force]] has" [002-011]. The press is one of the primary vehicles through which this dogma is disseminated and rein[[force-and-violence|force]]d. "It is this diversity of problems to attack and to solve which has made the success of Anarchistic ideas" precisely because anarchism must contend not only with [[force-and-violence|force]] but with a manufactured consensus sustained by the press [001-005].

The anarchist press — publications like [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s own *Les Temps Nouveaux* — exists as a counterweight to this manufactured consensus. But it operates under constant material pressure: "Without heeding the clamors of some, the rancor of others, the attempts of [[the-state|government]]s, the idea grows and progresses without cessation" [001-013]. The growth of anarchist ideas occurs despite the commercial press, not because of it. The very existence of an anarchist press demonstrates that "the individual has a right to his entire liberty, to the satisfaction of all his needs" — including the need to communicate without the mediation of capital [002-006].

## The Press and Revolutionary Ideas

Despite his critique, [[jean-grave|Grave]] is not a pessimist about the possibility of communication. Ideas, he insists, have a power that no apparatus of control can fully contain. "By its amplitude the Anarchistic idea shelters and draws to it all those who have the feeling of personal dignity, the thirst after the beautiful and the true" [001-015]. The growth of anarchist ideas is a historical fact that no amount of press hostility can reverse: "launched forth by a group of unknown people, without any means of propaganda, without journals, without money" — the ideas spread [001-005].

> By its amplitude the Anarchistic idea shelters and draws to it all those who have the feeling of personal dignity, the thirst after the beautiful and the good, which every man bears within him. [001-015]

The anarchist press is the practical demonstration that alternative media are possible — that workers can fund their own publications, that ideas can circulate without the permission of capital, that communication need not be mediated by the market. The existence of such publications is itself a refutation of the claim that the commercial press is the only possible form of journalism.

The task is not to reform the existing press — an impossibility given its structural determination by property — but to build alternative institutions of communication that are materially independent of the system they critique.
