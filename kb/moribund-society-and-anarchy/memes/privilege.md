# Privilege

## The Structure of Privilege

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], privilege is not a matter of individual advantage but a structural feature of the capitalist-authoritarian order. The institutions of [[the-state|the state]], [[the-law|the law]], and [[private-property|private property]] exist to defend the privileges of a minority against the interests of the majority. "Authority claims to justify its existence by the necessity of defending social institutions—the family, religion, property, etc.—and it has created a whole arsenal of laws for the protection of privilege" [001-002].

Grave emphasizes that privilege is maintained through force, not merit or natural superiority. The privileged classes "have thus far managed to bolster up their sway, to send the workers to sleep with fine phrases, to promise them marvelous reforms" [004-007], but behind these promises stands the apparatus of coercion: [[police]], courts, and armies.

> So much the worse for those who fall. [004-007]

This callous maxim, Grave suggests, is the unspoken principle underlying the privileged classes' attitude toward those they exploit.

## Monopoly and the Fruits of Labor

A central mechanism of privilege in Grave's analysis is monopoly—the exclusive control over land, capital, and the accumulated products of past generations. "The work of the Anarchists, then, has been to demonstrate the iniquity of the monopoly of the soil and of the fruits of the labor of past generations, accumulated by our ancestors, and which should belong to the whole of humanity" [001-010].

The privileged monopolists extract wealth from the labor of others while contributing nothing. Grave contrasts this with the anarchist vision of common ownership: "The sum of human learning is so great that the most privileged brains can appropriate only a portion; likewise the conception of Anarchism though condensed into a few lines, draws its elements from every branch of human knowledge" [002-016]. Knowledge, like land and capital, should be the common inheritance of all, not the exclusive property of a privileged few.

## The Law as Servant of Privilege

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies [[the-law|the law]] as the primary instrument for defending privilege. In his analysis of crime and punishment, he exposes the class character of legal institutions: "You punish as a thief the unfortunate who, having no work, risks imprisonment to get hold of a piece of bread to appease his hunger; but you take off your hat and bow to the millionaire monopolist" [009-015].

> The law is the humble servant of the privileged against the demands of the barefooted mob. [009-015]

This double standard extends to international relations: "You punish him who operates on a small scale, but you support whole armies that you may send them oversea to operate on a large scale against peoples unable to defend themselves" [009-015]. [[colonization|Colonization]] is theft on a grand scale, conducted by the privileged classes with the full sanction of law.

## Privilege and the Illusion of Shared Interest

Grave argues that the privileged classes have persuaded the [[bourgeoisie|middle classes]] and even sections of the working class to defend a system that exploits them. "It is necessary to make him understand further how the capitalistic classes have interested him in the existing society, persuaded him to defend the privileges from which he does not benefit" [003-003].

This is the ideological function of [[patriotism]], [[religion]], and electoral politics: they create the illusion that workers share an interest with their exploiters. "If the workers were logical, instead of defending 'the country' by fighting—other workers, they would begin by getting rid of those who command and exploit them" [011-007].

## Abolishing Privilege

The anarchist solution is not to redistribute privilege more fairly but to abolish the conditions that make privilege possible. This requires the elimination of [[private-property|private property]], which is "the monopoly of the soil and of the fruits of the labor of past generations" [001-010], and the dissolution of [[the-state|the state]], which exists "to defend the existing order of things" [019-012].

> From the present organization you reap a harvest of wars, crimes, thefts, fraud, and misery. This is the result of private property and authority… If you would have a society in which reign confidence, solidarity, and well-being for all, base it upon liberty, reciprocity, and equality. [010-021]
