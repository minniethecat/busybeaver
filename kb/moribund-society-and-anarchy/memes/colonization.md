# Colonization

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist analysis, colonization is not the peaceful spread of civilization but rather "the hybrid product of patriotism and mercantilism combined — brigandage and highway robbery for the benefit of the [[class|ruling class]]es" [014-002]. It is [[government|the state]]-organized expropriation of foreign lands and peoples, justified by the rhetoric of [[civilization|civilizing mission]]s and national interest, but driven fundamentally by the commercial needs of the bourgeoisie and the strategic requirements of ruling [[the-state|government]]s. The colonizing enterprise exposes the logic of [[government|the state]] in its most naked form — conquest, plunder, and systematic exploitation, dressed in the language of progress.

## Colonization as Organized Brigandage

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a stark contrast between the treatment of individual crime and [[the-state|state]]-sponsored conquest. A private individual who enters a neighbor's house, breaks everything, and seizes property is condemned as a criminal. But when a [[the-state|government]] declares war on remote peoples too feeble to resist, takes possession of their country, subjects them to exploitation, [[force-and-violence|force]]s products upon them, and massacres those who resist — this is celebrated as moral, even noble [014-002]. The scale of the operation transforms its moral character: what would be called robbery or assassination in one context is rebranded as "civilizing" undeveloped peoples when performed by [[government|the state]].

> Colonization is extending too widely, in the present epoch, for us to neglect to treat separately of this hybrid product of patriotism and mercantilism combined — brigandage and highway robbery for the benefit of the ruling classes! [014-002]

This double standard is not incidental but essential to the functioning of [[government|the state]] system. The same institutions that punish individual theft organize theft on a continental scale, and the same legal order that condemns murder authorizes mass slaughter in conquered territories.

## The Economic Logic of Colonial Enterprise

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], the true motive behind colonization is straightforwardly economic. The bourgeoisie, "embarrassed with products which they cannot dispose of," find nothing better than to declare war on "poor devils powerless to defend themselves, in order to impose these products upon them" [014-010]. Colonization creates captive markets for manufactured goods, new sources of raw materials, and opportunities for the investment of surplus capital. The colonizing nation is defined precisely by its success in extracting maximum product from subject territories — "No nation is reputed to be a colonizing one save when it has succeeded in getting out of a country the maximum product it is capable of yielding" [014-003].

This economic predation benefits a narrow class of rulers, merchants, and manufacturers. [[jean-grave|Grave]] emphasizes that the commercial treaties, colonial annexations, and [[militarism|military]] conquests that characterize colonization serve "the advantage of the privileged alone" [011-010]. The workers who are sent to fight and die in colonial wars gain nothing from the enterprise — the conquered soil "does not and will not belong to them" but remains the property of the minority who command and exploit them [011-007].

> No nation is reputed to be a colonizing one save when it has succeeded in getting out of a country the maximum product it is capable of yielding. Thus England is a colonizing country, because she knows how to "reward" her colonies with the prosperity of the people she sends out to rule them, how to gather back into her coffers the taxes with which she burdens them. [014-003]

## The Ideological Apparatus: Civilization and Patriotism

Colonization requires an ideological justification, and [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies two principal rhetorical strategies: the discourse of civilization and the appeal to patriotism. The claim that colonized peoples "must be civilized" masks the [[force-and-violence|violence]] of conquest, while patriotism mobilizes the [[class|working class]] to fight and die for interests that are not their own.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] challenges the civilization narrative by asking who is truly savage — the indigenous peoples who "generally welcomed their invaders," or the armed men who arrived to slaughter them [014-008]? The historical record, he argues, shows that European conquest brought not enlightenment but devastation: "everywhere the native populations decrease and disappear; everywhere drunkenness, syphilis, and other European importations decimate the citizens of those countries" [014-011]. The civilizers brought disease, exploitation, and cultural destruction.

> How glorious might the role of the so-called civilized man have been, had he but understood it, and had not he himself been afflicted with these two pests, government and mercantilism — two frightful plagues, of which he would do well to consider how to rid himself before seeking to civilize others. [014-012]

## Who Benefits: The Anatomy of Colonial Interests

[[jean-grave|Grave]] provides a detailed accounting of the class interests served by colonial conquest. The bourgeoisie benefits directly through financial schemes, land monopolization, and the opening of new markets for manufactured goods [014-006]. Colonial administration provides employment for "a whole army of office-seekers and ambitious persons" who might otherwise become a domestic political liability. The permanent armies maintained for colonial campaigns absorb "a whole pack of idiots and worthless persons by whom the bourgeoisie would be much embarrassed," transforming them into the "most furious defenders" of the bourgeois order [014-006].

The workers, by contrast, are the primary victims of colonization on both sides. The colonized suffer massacre, dispossession, and exploitation. The workers of the colonizing power are sent to kill and be killed — "stupidly permitting themselves to take up arms for the purpose of wresting from others the soil which will serve their masters as a means to exploit themselves still further" [011-007]. Both groups have a common interest in opposing the colonial enterprise, yet the ideological machinery of patriotism divides them against each other.

> The bourgeoisie is interested in these strokes of brigandage; they serve as a pretext for maintaining permanent armies; they occupy the praetorians who, during these slaughters, set their hands to more serious "labor." ... But what astonishes and disheartens us is that there are workers who approve of these infamies; who feel no remorse in lending a hand to these rascalities. [014-006]

## The Colonial System as State Logic

Colonization is not an aberration within [[government|the state]] system but its logical extension. [[jean-grave|Grave]] traces a direct line from the domestic institutions of authority — private property, the magistracy, [[war|militarism]] — to their colonial manifestations. The same logic that dispossesses the worker at home dispossesses the indigenous person abroad; the same [[militarism|military]] that suppresses domestic dissent conducts foreign conquest; the same legal order that protects property rights sanctions colonial plunder. The chapter on colonization in *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* thus serves as a case study in how [[government|the state]] functions when unrestrained by the minimal constraints of domestic politics — revealing its essential character as an instrument of class domination.
