# The Country

## The Invention of the Fatherland

[[jean-grave|Grave]] traces the concept of "the country" as a modern ideological construct. While group conflict existed throughout history — "group fought against group, tribe against tribe" — "the notion of a 'fatherland' was still very vague and uncertain" until modern times [011-001]. It was the French Revolution of 1789 that elevated "the country" alongside [[the-law|the law]] as a metaphysical entity demanding devotion: "It was an idea congenial to the bourgeoisie to substitute the authority of the nation for that of divine right, to present it to the workers as a synthesis of all rights" [011-001].

This substitution was ideologically ingenious. The old regime demanded obedience in the name of God and the king — a claim that was increasingly difficult to sustain in an age of skepticism. The new regime demanded obedience in the name of the nation — a claim that could be presented as democratic, even emancipatory, while serving the same function: securing the submission of the many to the few. The "nation" was "the country" was "the fatherland" — a chain of emotionally charged abstractions that, when examined, dissolved into the concrete interests of the property-owning class.

The idea evolved from representing the people and their institutions to a narrower, more manipulative form: "It was only little by little… that the idea of the country shrunk and shriveled to the narrow sense taught today, of *love of the soil* without concern for those who live upon it" [011-001]. This narrowing served a specific function — to make the defense of the soil a pretext for maintaining the army "necessary to the support of their privileges" [011-001].

## The Arbitrariness of Frontiers

The supposed naturalness of nations dissolves under scrutiny, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues. "States have only arbitrary limits; such limitation most frequently depends upon the issue of battles" [011-003]. The idea that frontiers reflect racial unity is equally bankrupt, for "every nation is itself but an amalgamation of races quite different from each other" [011-004]. The boundaries that men are asked to die for are "fictitious lines" that shift with each war — the "patriotism of these provinces, tossed first to this side then to that, consisted in fighting sometimes under one flag, sometimes under another, in killing their allies of the day before" [011-003].

> What can be more arbitrary than frontiers? For what reason do men located on this side of a fictitious line belong to a nation more than those on the other side? [011-004]

The implications are devastating for the entire edifice of [[patriotism]]. If the nation is not a natural entity but a historical accident, if its boundaries are not expressions of some organic unity but the residue of forgotten battles, then the demand that men kill and die for it loses whatever moral force it might claim. The soldier who dies for France is dying not for a people or a culture but for a set of lines drawn on a map by long-dead generals and diplomats — lines that might have been drawn differently had a single battle gone the other way.

## A Soil That Does Not Belong to Its Defenders

The deepest contradiction of patriotism is that the workers who are called upon to defend "their" country own none of it. "This soil belongs to a minority of property-owners, who, sheltered from all danger, bask tranquilly in their chimney-corners, while the workers foolishly go out to slay each other" [011-005]. The workers "stupidly" permit themselves to be armed "for the purpose of wresting from others the soil which will serve — their masters, as a means to exploit themselves — the workers — still further" [011-005].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] rejects the argument that a foreign invasion would bring worse conditions: "Our enemy is *the master*, no matter to what nationality he belongs!" [011-008]. The history of the proletariat proves that "national governments are not afraid to shoot down their 'subjects' when the latter demand a few liberties" — so "what more, then, could foreign exploiters do?" [011-008].

This is one of Grave's most radical arguments, and he makes it without hesitation. The worker has no country because the country is not his — it belongs to the class that exploits him. Whether the exploiter speaks French, German, or Russian makes no difference to the fact of exploitation. The worker who dies defending "his" country dies defending the property of the class that oppresses him — dies, in effect, in the service of his own enslavement.

## The Bourgeoisie's True Interest in Patriotism

The real function of patriotic ideology is transparent: "the defense of the soil was but too good a pretext for maintaining the army necessary to the support of their privileges" [011-001]. War serves the bourgeoisie in multiple ways: it preserves the armies that "keep the people respectful," it forces open "new markets with cannon shots," and it justifies the loans on which "we, the workers, alone pay" the interest [011-011].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] predicts that the bourgeoisie, for all their patriotic rhetoric, would not hesitate to appeal to foreign armies if their privileges were threatened: "let us endanger the privileges of the bourgeoisie, and it will not be long till we see those who preach patriotism to *us*, appealing to the armies of their conquerors, be they German, Russian, or of no matter what country" [011-011]. They are "like Voltaire, their patron: he did not believe in God, but judged that some religion was necessary to the common people; they have frontiers between their slaves, but for themselves they mock at such when their interests are at stake" [011-011].

This prediction was borne out in the decades following Grave's writing. The French bourgeoisie of 1871, faced with the [[paris-commune|Paris Commune]], did not hesitate to collaborate with the Prussian army that had just defeated France in war. Patriotic rhetoric about the sacred soil of the fatherland evaporated the moment the property of the bourgeoisie was threatened. The nation, so precious when it serves to mobilize workers for war, becomes a negotiable abstraction when the class enemy is at the gates.

## Patriotism and Class Consciousness

The function of patriotic ideology is, above all, to prevent the development of [[class]] consciousness. So long as the French worker identifies with the French bourgeoisie against the German worker — rather than with the German worker against the French bourgeoisie — he is harmless. The nation is a device for dividing the international working class, ensuring that workers direct their hostility at each other rather than at their common exploiters.

Grave's internationalism is not a sentimental preference for peace over war but a strategic analysis of the conditions for working-class emancipation. The worker cannot liberate himself within national boundaries because the forces that oppress him — [[capitalism]], [[authority]], the state system — are international in scope. The only effective response is international solidarity: workers refusing to kill each other on behalf of their masters, refusing to compete with each other for wages, refusing to recognize the frontiers that divide them.

## Beyond the Country

The anarchist alternative dissolves national boundaries entirely. "There is no 'country' for the man truly worthy of the name; or at least there is but one — that in which he struggles for true right, in which he lives and has his affections; but it may extend over the whole earth!" [011-012]. The earth is "vast enough to support everybody" — it is not scarcity but the "iniquitous wars to which the desires of rulers, the rivalries of the ambitious, the commercial competition of the great capitalists have given birth" that divide peoples [011-006].

> Instead of going on cutting each other's throats, as they have done up to the present, they ought to stretch out their hands across the frontiers and unite all their efforts in making war upon their real, their only, enemies: authority and capital. [011-012]

This is not a vision of rootless cosmopolitanism — Grave is not asking workers to abandon their cultures, their languages, their local attachments. He is asking them to recognize that these attachments do not require the apparatus of the nation-state to flourish, and that love of one's home does not entail hatred of one's neighbor. The worker who loves his village, his landscape, his language — this worker has concrete attachments that the abstraction "the country" exploits and distorts. The anarchist does not ask him to abandon these attachments but to see that they are not served by armies, borders, and flags.
