# Switzerland

## Overview

Switzerland holds a foundational place in Jean Grave's *Moribund Society and Anarchy* as the birthplace of the organized anarchist movement. It was in Switzerland that the International Workingmen's Association gave rise to the Jurassian Federation, where Mikhail Bakunin developed and propagated anarchist ideas, marking what Grave calls "the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement."

## In Grave's Text

**Birth of the Anarchist Movement**: In Chapter I ("The Anarchistic Idea and Its Development"), Grave identifies Switzerland as the critical point of origin for organized anarchism. After the defeat of the Paris Commune (1871), when French anarchism "foundered in the misled and misleading movement of cooperative associations," the movement found new life across the border:

> In Switzerland, the International had given birth to the "Jurassian Federation" in which Bakunin propagated the idea of Proudhon—Anarchy the enemy of authority—but developing, enlarging, incarnating it in social demands. From this epoch dates the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement. [001-007]

Grave situates Switzerland at the pivot point between Proudhon's theoretical mutualism and the fully-developed anarchist program that would spread across Europe. The Jurassian Federation, based in the Jura mountains of western Switzerland, represented the anti-authoritarian wing of the International in its split with the Marxist General Council.

## Historical Context

The Jurassian Federation, centered in Swiss watchmaking towns like Saint-Imier, La Chaux-de-Fonds, and Sonvilier, was a stronghold of Bakuninist anarchism within the First International during the 1870s. The 1872 Saint-Imier Congress, held after Bakunin's expulsion from the International at the Hague Congress, formally established the anti-authoritarian International and is considered a founding moment of the organized anarchist movement. James Guillaume, a key figure in the federation, was a close associate of both Bakunin and later Peter Kropotkin. Switzerland's political neutrality and federal structure made it a relative haven for political exiles and revolutionary organizing throughout the late nineteenth century.
