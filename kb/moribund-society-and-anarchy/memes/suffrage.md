# Suffrage

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], suffrage — and [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] in particular — is the mechanism by which the governed are made complicit in their own subjection. It is not, as its defenders claim, a means by which the people govern themselves, but rather the procedure through which they select those who will govern them. [[jean-grave|Grave]] cuts through the democratic mystification with characteristic bluntness: "indeed what is [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] if not the right of the governed to choose their master, the right of choosing the rod to be whipped with?" [007-003] The voter "abdicates his sovereignty and delegates it to a representative who is supposed to represent him; but in reality it is the representative who imposes his will on the voter" [007-003]. The anarchist objection is fundamental: "What matters the choice of the hand that strikes you? It is not to be struck at all, that one should aim at!" [007-013]

## Suffrage as the Right to Choose One's Master

[[jean-grave|Grave]] cuts through the democratic mystification of the vote with characteristic directness. Universal suffrage is not self-[[the-state|government]] but the delegation of [[the-state|government]] — and delegation is abdication:

> Indeed what is universal suffrage if not the right of the governed to choose their master, the right of choosing the rod to be whipped with? The voter abdicates his sovereignty and delegates it to a representative who is supposed to represent him; but in reality it is the representative who imposes his will on the voter. [007-003]

The act of voting transfers power from the citizen to the representative. Once transferred, that power is no longer the citizen's to control. The representative "imposes his will on the voter" — the relationship is not one of accountability but of command [007-003]. The voter who believed they were exercising sovereignty has in fact renounced it. [[jean-grave|Grave]] sharpens the point: "if the workings of all manner of groups be thoroughly examined — committees, representative congresses, associations for mutual help, societies of artists, etc. — the same phenomenon will always be observed: the few leading the mass" [007-006]. The form of selection — election, appointment, self-selection — makes no difference to the outcome.

This analysis exposes the central deception of representative democracy: it presents itself as the rule of the people while structurally ensuring the rule of a political class. The "choice" offered at the ballot box is a choice among candidates preselected by parties, funded by capital, and answerable after election to interests other than their constituents'.

## The Nullifying Defects of the Electoral System

[[jean-grave|Grave]] enumerates the structural defects that render suffrage not merely inadequate but actively harmful:

> Universal suffrage is a means of stifling individual initiative which we proclaim, and which we should on the contrary seek to develop by all possible means; for, according to us, it is to the free play of individual initiative that we must look for the evolution of the race. [017-006]

The vote is a substitute for action. The citizen who casts a ballot every few years feels they have "participated" — and is thereby pacified. Their energy, which might have been directed toward direct collective action, is channeled into a symbolic ritual that changes nothing. "It is easy to say with some of our opponents that under [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] each individual has a share in [[the-state|the government]] of the country" [017-004] — but this is a transparent fiction. The worker who votes does not thereby govern the factory; the tenant who votes does not thereby own their home.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies another nullifying defect: the electoral system systematically excludes radical alternatives. The machinery of elections — candidacy requirements, ballot access [[the-law|law]]s, [[press|media]] gatekeeping, campaign finance — ensures that only those who accept the existing framework can compete. "The voter can choose only among candidates who accept the existing social order" [017-004]. The choice is never *whether* to have masters but only *which* masters to have.

## The Historical Record

[[jean-grave|Grave]] grounds his critique in the empirical evidence of his time. Despite decades of [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] and republican [[the-state|government]], the condition of the [[class|working class]] had not improved in any fundamental sense:

> If the republic has enjoyed so much credit among the workers; if, after so many deceptions, universal suffrage is still considered by the governed as the safeguard of their liberties, the remedy for their evils, it is because they have not yet understood that the Republic, with its train of functionaries, is as expensive and as oppressive as the monarchy. [007-009]

The persistence of this illusion — that changing the form of [[the-state|government]] changes the condition of the governed — is itself a product of the electoral system, which requires citizens to believe that voting matters in order to continue participating. "Whatever our opponents say, it is they who are the empiricists; the experiments they have been making for centuries with the reforms and palliatives have not cured the disease" [020-009]. The system manufactures the consent it requires, and each election cycle renews the fiction that *this time* the vote will make a difference. [[jean-grave|Grave]] is emphatic that it never has and never will: "the question of property is so mixed up with that of authority that in treating of the former in its special chapter we could not do otherwise than treat of the latter at the same time" [007-002]. Suffrage does not touch property, and property is the root of the disease.

## The Anarchist Alternative to Voting

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s rejection of suffrage is not a rejection of collective decision-making but a demand for direct participation. If the anarchist refuses to vote, it is not from apathy but from a commitment to more genuine forms of involvement. Universal suffrage "is a means of stifling individual initiative which we proclaim, and which we should on the contrary seek to develop by all possible means" [017-006].

The alternative to the ballot is the assembly — the direct gathering of those affected by a decision, in which all participate as equals and none delegates their voice. The alternative to representation is [[association|federation]] — the coordination of autonomous groups through mandates that are revocable and specific. The alternative to [[majority-and-minority|majority rule]] is consensus — the patient work of finding solutions that all can accept, rather than imposing the will of fifty-one percent on forty-nine.

The demand is not for better elections but for their abolition. "It is easy to say with some of our opponents that under [[majority-and-minority|universal suffrage]] each individual has a share in [[the-state|the government]] of the country — a pretty big share!" — this is a transparent fiction [017-004]. The worker who votes does not thereby govern; the tenant who votes does not thereby own.

> Whatever the name in which the new authority clothes itself, however democratic it may proclaim itself, it will be no less authority — that is to say, the right of some to command others. [007-013]

Anarchist organization proceeds not by counting votes but by building agreements. The goal is not to win but to convince — not to outnumber but to align.
