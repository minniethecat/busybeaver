# Tradition

## Definition

Tradition, in [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s anarchist framework, represents the accumulated weight of inherited beliefs, [[custom|customs]], and social practices that serve to perpetuate existing [[authority|authoritarian]] institutions. Grave argues that "everything in our traditional society tends to split up mankind" (004-005), identifying tradition not as a source of social cohesion but as a force of division and oppression.

## Tradition as Obstacle to Progress

Grave situates tradition among the primary obstacles to human [[emancipation|liberation]]. Throughout *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, he repeatedly identifies "accepted ideas, routine, [[prejudice]], and ignorance" as forces that "have been till now stronger than [humanity's] dreams and desires for emancipation, precipitating it, after it has driven away the reigning masters, into a fresh abandonment of them, at the very moment it expects to free itself" (001-015).

The mechanism by which tradition operates is largely unconscious. People internalize the values and assumptions of the society they are born into, treating historically contingent arrangements as eternal truths. Grave observes that even revolutionaries who overthrow existing regimes often reproduce authoritarian structures because they have not freed themselves from traditional ways of thinking about [[government|power]] and social organization.

## Tradition and Authority

The relationship between tradition and authority is mutually reinforcing. Grave notes that authority was "so long propped up by its supernatural origin — a fable which has contributed at least as much as brute force to maintain it" (005-007). Traditional religious beliefs provided the ideological justification for hierarchical power, making authority appear divinely ordained rather than historically constructed.

Even after the divine justification collapsed under the scrutiny of modern science, traditional habits of obedience persisted. Grave argues that the [[bourgeoisie]] simply replaced one traditional justification with another, substituting the supposed naturalness of property and authority for their former divine sanction. The form of the argument changed, but the traditional function — making contingent power relations appear necessary — remained the same.

## Breaking with Tradition

The anarchist project, as Grave conceives it, requires a fundamental break with traditional modes of thought. It is not enough to change institutions; one must also transform the mental habits that make authoritarian institutions seem natural. This is why Grave places such emphasis on education and the propagation of anarchist ideas — they serve to "bring light into the minds not only of the workers, but also of thinkers of every category; helping them to analyze their own feelings, stripping bare the causes of misery and indicating the means of destroying them" (001-015).

Grave's analysis suggests that tradition operates most effectively when it is invisible — when people follow customs without recognizing them as customs, when they obey authority without recognizing it as authority. The first step toward liberation is therefore making tradition visible as tradition, revealing inherited practices as human creations that can be humanly undone.

## Tradition and Revolution

For Grave, the persistence of traditional thinking explains why previous revolutions have failed to achieve lasting liberation. Revolutionary movements that focus solely on seizing power or redistributing [[property]] without challenging the traditional assumptions underlying those institutions are doomed to reproduce the very systems they overthrew. True revolution requires not just new institutions but new ways of thinking — a break with tradition at the deepest level of social consciousness.
