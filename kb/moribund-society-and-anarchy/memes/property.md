# Property

## Definition

Property, in [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]]'s anarchist analysis, is not merely an economic arrangement but the foundational institution upon which the entire edifice of [[authority|authoritarian]] society rests. Grave argues that "authority has but one excuse for existence: the defense of capital" and that "bureaucracy, the family, the army, the magistracy, flow directly from private property" (001-010). The institution of property is therefore the root cause from which all other forms of oppression derive.

## Historical Evolution

Grave declines to provide a comprehensive history of property but emphasizes that its forms have shifted over time. He observes that "Property, the family, authority, have developed along parallel lines; of this there is no doubt" (006-002). The property system has "rested upon other bases than those upon which it is today supported, has had another method of division, other methods of exploitation" (006-005).

The key historical transition Grave identifies occurred during the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] of 1789, when "property changed hands" — the [[bourgeoisie]] replaced the aristocracy as the owning class, but the institution of exclusive ownership itself remained intact (003-003). The people "revolted, hoping thereby to force it to change hands again" (003-003), but Grave argues that merely transferring property from one class to another does not address the fundamental injustice of the institution itself.

## Property and Authority

The relationship between property and authority is symbiotic. Grave traces how "authority and property have succeeded in establishing themselves" together (002-009), each reinforcing the other. The [[the-state|state]] exists primarily to defend property relations: "all the apparatus of government flows from the need to protect private property and the privileges of the ruling class."

Grave notes that the divine justification for property has been undermined by modern science: "the divine origin of authority and property being denied by bourgeois science itself, the bourgeoisie have sought to give them solid and natural bases" (005-010). These secular justifications, however, fare no better under Grave's scrutiny — he maintains that property rests on arbitrary appropriation and violence, not natural right.

## The Anarchist Critique

### Expropriation and Legitimacy

Grave challenges the property-owning classes' moral outrage at anarchist demands for expropriation. He asks pointedly: "did those who declaim so much against the Anarchists for demanding expropriation by force distress themselves very much about expropriation when it was they who profited by it?" (005-021). The original accumulation of property, in Grave's view, was itself an act of violent expropriation — the anarchist demand to abolish property is simply the reversal of a prior injustice.

### Property as Discussion

Grave offers a dialectical observation: "property and authority, being placed under discussion, are on the highroad to extinction, for what is discussed is scarcely revered" (005-007). The very act of questioning the legitimacy of property signals its vulnerability — institutions that require ceaseless justification are institutions in decline.

### Revolutionary Transformation

The anarchist program calls for the complete abolition of private property, not its reform or redistribution. Grave exhorts: "The revolutionists should destroy all the old parchments which guarantee the functioning of property; the offices of bailiffs, notaries, register of lands" (002-016). The goal is not to redistribute property more fairly but to eliminate the institution of exclusive ownership entirely, replacing it with communal possession and free access.

## Property and Human Nature

Grave rejects the claim that property is a natural or necessary institution. He argues that "when the first thinking beings appeared upon the earth, there could no longer be any question of individual property in the soil" (005-009) — property is a historical construct, not a biological imperative. The anarchist wager is that human beings can organize production and distribution without the institution of exclusive ownership, through [[solidarity|voluntary cooperation]] and [[mutual-aid|mutual aid]].
