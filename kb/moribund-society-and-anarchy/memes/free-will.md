# Free Will

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, Jean Grave argues that **free will** does not exist as traditionally conceived. Human beings are shaped and driven by forces outside their control — environment, heredity, education, and social conditioning. This deterministic view is central to Grave's critique of the [[punishment]] system and the concept of individual criminal responsibility, and it serves as the philosophical foundation for his entire social theory.

## The Scientific Consensus

Grave notes that modern science has decisively rejected the notion of free will:

> "Science, today, admits without dispute that man is the sport of a multitude of forces to whose play he is subjected, and that free will does not exist. Environment, heredity, education, climatic and atmospheric influences, act upon man in turn, now clashing with each other, now combining, but exercising an undeniable influence upon his brain." [004-286]

This scientific understanding stands in stark contradiction to the legal and moral framework of existing [[society]], which is built upon the assumption that individuals freely choose their actions and can therefore be held criminally responsible. The entire edifice of [[the-law|law]], [[punishment]], and [[the-magistracy|magistracy]] presupposes the very free will that science has demonstrated to be an illusion.

## The Philosophical Tradition

Grave's determinism does not emerge from a vacuum. It draws on a tradition that includes the eighteenth-century materialists — d'Holbach, Helvétius, Diderot — who argued that human character is the product of education and environment, and the nineteenth-century positivists who extended this insight with the tools of the emerging biological and social sciences. The anarchist appropriation of determinism adds a distinctive element: the recognition that the environment shaping human beings is itself a human creation — the product of specific institutions ([[private-property|property]], [[authority]], [[the-state|the state]]) that can be transformed through collective action.

This distinguishes the anarchist determinism from the conservative version. Conservative determinists — from Malthus to the Social Darwinists — used environmental theories to justify existing inequalities as natural and inevitable. The poor are poor because they are biologically or morally inferior, and no change in social arrangements can alter this fact. The anarchist determinist draws the opposite conclusion: if human beings are products of their environment, then changing the environment — abolishing the institutions that produce misery, crime, and servility — will change human beings. The biological determinism of the reactionary becomes, in anarchist hands, an argument for revolutionary transformation.

## Implications for Criminal Justice

The denial of free will has radical implications for how society responds to crime. Grave points out the contradiction in the work of criminal anthropologists like [[cesare-lombroso]], who accept determinism yet still advocate for harsh [[punishment]]:

> "Man acts under the influence of causes external to himself; hence he is not responsible for his acts. The savants recognize this, and therefore decide for — repression!" [004-286]

This contradiction — acknowledging that human behavior is determined while simultaneously demanding punitive responses — reveals the gap between scientific insight and social practice. The scientists who study crime cannot bring themselves to follow their own premises to their logical conclusion:

> "Having denied free will; having recognized that justice is but a figment, and is, in fact, nothing but revenge exercised by society, which substitutes itself for the individual wronged, the doctor unfortunately, stops short." [004-307]

The reason for this failure, Grave suggests, is that accepting the full implications of determinism would require accepting the need for a total transformation of [[society]]. If "man is the product of his environment," then to change man it is necessary "to change the environment" — and to change the environment is to abolish the property system, the state, and the entire apparatus of [[authority]] [004-307]. This is a conclusion that "savants" employed by the state, dependent on its institutions, and shaped by its prejudices are constitutionally incapable of reaching.

## The Critique of Cesare Lombroso

[[cesare-lombroso|Lombroso]]'s criminal anthropology represents, for Grave, the paradigmatic case of scientific insight captured by state interests. Lombroso and his school accepted the determinist premise — the criminal does not freely choose to commit crime but is driven by biological and social forces — yet they used this premise to justify not the transformation of the environment but the permanent incapacitation of the "born criminal." The scientist who begins by denying free will ends by erecting a new scaffold.

Grave's critique of Lombroso is not merely an attack on a particular thinker but an analysis of the structural position of the intellectual in class society. The scientist who accepts the premises of the existing order — who takes for granted the legitimacy of property, authority, and punishment — cannot think his way to revolutionary conclusions no matter how rigorous his method. The chain of reasoning is broken at the point where it threatens to touch the foundations of the social order. Lombroso stops where he must stop — not because the evidence fails him but because the logic of his position as a state-employed intellectual forbids him to go further.

## Environment and Moral Evolution

Grave extends the determinist analysis to moral development. Just as physical organisms are shaped by their environment, human moral character is the product of social conditions:

> "It is acknowledged today that the soil and climate, the obstacles or advantages in the way of living found by the organisms of a continent, have an influence upon their development as great as the other laws by which, exclusively, their adaptation or their tendencies to variability have heretofore been sought to be explained." [004-326]

This ecological determinism — the recognition that physical and social environments shape human beings as thoroughly as they shape other organisms — is the scientific foundation of Grave's political optimism. If human beings are products of their environment, then transforming the environment — abolishing the institutions of [[competition]], [[private-property|property]], and [[authority]] — will transform human beings. The "evil nature" that conservatives invoke to justify hierarchy and repression is not a permanent feature of the human condition but a contingent product of a particular social order.

Even those who accept determinism in principle often fail to follow it to its logical conclusion — that the entire [[authority]]-based social organization must be transformed:

> "Even some of those who deny free will, who recognize that man acts only under the pressure of external circumstances — even some of those cannot accept the law in all its consequences — that is to say, so far as to trace the causes of man's criminality to the entire social organization and to demand the transformation of the latter." [004-326]

## Free Will and Anarchist Social Theory

The denial of free will is not merely a negative, critical move in Grave's argument — it is the positive foundation of his constructive social theory. If human beings are products of their environment, then the anarchist project of creating a new social environment — one based on [[cooperation]] rather than [[competition]], [[solidarity]] rather than antagonism, free association rather than [[authority]] — is the only scientific path to human improvement.

This distinguishes Grave's anarchism from liberal reformism. The liberal reformer accepts the determinist premise but limits its application: individuals may be shaped by their environment, but the reformer proposes only incremental adjustments to that environment — better schools, more humane prisons, temperance campaigns. The anarchist, by contrast, follows the determinist logic to its revolutionary conclusion: if the environment makes the person, the environment itself must be remade entirely.

Grave's determinism also disposes of the moralistic language that pervades both conservative and reformist discourse about [[crime]] and vice. The criminal is not a sinner who has chosen evil, nor a defective individual who has failed to take advantage of the opportunities society offers. The criminal is a product — as inevitable as any other natural phenomenon — of an environment organized around [[private-property|property]], inequality, and the war of all against all:

> In a society where it is necessary to fight for bread, there will always be some who, rather than work for a starvation wage, will risk prison to seize what they need. [009-014]

## Determinism and Revolutionary Agency

A natural objection to Grave's position is that it seems to undermine the very possibility of revolutionary action. If human beings are determined by their environment, how can they freely choose to revolt against it? Grave addresses this implicit objection not through abstract philosophical argument but through the concrete analysis of social determination. The same environmental forces that produce criminals also produce revolutionaries — the difference lies not in some metaphysical residue of free will but in the specific configuration of forces acting on different individuals.

The worker who grows up in a family with a tradition of militancy, who reads [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]] or [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]], who experiences exploitation and draws political conclusions from it — this worker is as determined by their environment as the criminal or the conformist. The point is not that some individuals escape determination through an act of will but that the environment itself contains contradictory forces. The same [[society]] that produces submission also produces revolt; the same conditions that crush some people radicalize others. The anarchist's task is not to appeal to free will but to create the conditions — the organizations, the publications, the examples of collective action — under which more people will be determined toward revolt rather than submission.

## Personal Relationships and Autonomy

Grave applies the principle of free choice — made without external compulsion — to personal relationships, particularly in his critique of marriage:

> "What they have made of their own free will they can unmake of their own free will. Under the empire of passion, the pressure of desire, they saw each other's good qualities only; they shut their eyes to each other's defects." [002-211]

This more everyday usage — the ability to make uncoerced personal choices — exists alongside the philosophical denial of metaphysical free will, reflecting the dual registers in which the concept operates. In the anarchist vision, the absence of [[authority]] and external compulsion liberates individuals to act on their genuine desires and reasoned judgments — not "free will" in the metaphysical sense of uncaused choice, but freedom in the practical sense of action unconstrained by coercive institutions, economic necessity, or the internalized commands of hierarchical authority.
