# Germany

## Overview

Germany appears in Jean Grave's *Moribund Society and Anarchy* primarily as a geopolitical reference—the German Empire as both a military rival of France and a country where anarchist ideas spread through translations. Grave's references invoke Germany to illustrate how nationalism and patriotism serve ruling-class interests at the expense of workers.

## In Grave's Text

**War and Patriotism**: In Chapter XI ("The Country"), Grave argues that the bourgeoisie would readily appeal to "German" armies to suppress working-class revolt, just as they maintain religious belief for the masses while privately mocking it [011-012]. This reflects the anarchist view that national armies are tools of class domination regardless of which flag they march under.

**Anarchist Propagation**: In Chapter I ("The Anarchistic Idea and Its Development"), Grave notes that anarchist writings were translated into German as part of the movement's international spread. He mentions "groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian—a little everywhere and in all idioms" [ch01].

**Franco-Prussian War Context**: The discussion of the 1871 war indemnity of five billion francs paid to Prussia/Germany forms a significant backdrop to Grave's critique of patriotism in Chapter XI. The war and its aftermath demonstrated, in Grave's analysis, how workers bear the costs of conflicts that benefit only the bourgeoisie.

## Historical Context

At the time of Grave's writing (circa 1890s), the German Empire under Kaiser Wilhelm II was France's primary continental rival. The Franco-Prussian War (1870–1871) and the German annexation of Alsace-Lorraine remained a source of French resentment. German anti-socialist laws (1878–1890) had suppressed left-wing organizing, including anarchist activity. Despite repression, a significant German anarchist movement persisted, with figures like Johann Most and the newspaper *Freiheit* influencing the international anarchist movement.
