# Worker

## Definition

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] centers the worker as both the victim of [[capitalism|capitalist]] exploitation and the agent of revolutionary change. The worker's position in the productive process gives them both the motivation and the capacity to transform society. Grave insists that "the capitalists, with all their capital, all their machinery, could produce nothing without the cooperation of the workers" (003-040) — an observation that reveals the fundamental dependence of the ruling class on the labor of the exploited.

## The Condition of the Worker

### Exploitation and Dependency

Grave describes the worker's condition under capitalism as one of systematic subordination. The worker, unlike the capitalist, "has a right to the first claim only, permitting him to replace his capital expended; that is to say, to feed, clothe, and lodge himself" (003-034). This "right" is no right at all — it is mere subsistence, the minimum necessary to maintain the worker as a productive instrument. Grave contrasts this with the capitalist, who extracts surplus value from the worker's [[labor]] without contributing to production.

### The Worker's Lack of Stake

Grave poses a devastating question: "the worker — what has *he* to defend? What matters to him all this governmental paraphernalia, the expense for whose maintenance he alone bears, without deriving any profit therefrom, and which is there solely to teach him that he has no rights?" (003-065). The [[the-state|state]], the [[army]], the legal system — all are maintained by the worker's taxes yet serve only to reinforce the worker's subjection. The worker, in Grave's analysis, has no stake in the existing order.

## The Worker as Revolutionary Agent

### Historical Pattern

Grave acknowledges a tragic historical pattern: "the workers have always made revolutions, but have forever allowed the benefits thereof to be taken from them by the [[bourgeoisie]]" (003-004). The task for anarchism is to break this cycle — to ensure that the next revolution is not captured by new elites but results in genuine worker emancipation. This requires not merely a change of rulers but the abolition of [[authority]] itself.

### The Necessity of Consciousness

Grave argues that worker consciousness must transcend immediate material grievances. The worker must understand that "it is not by changing governors that the ills from which we suffer may be cured" (003-003). Anarchist propaganda addresses itself to workers not with promises of material improvement but with the demand for complete liberation — "complete freedom in all their modes of action" (002-004). Grave believes that the working class, once awakened to its true interests, possesses the collective power to remake society.

## The Worker and Civilization

Grave warns of the broader stakes of worker exploitation. "At the rate your civilization is going on," he writes, "if the workers are bound to succumb to the struggle to which you deliver them up, you, in your turn, will not be long in succumbing likewise under your indolence and laziness, even as fell the Greek and Roman civilizations" (003-173). The exploitation of the worker is not only unjust — it is ultimately self-destructive for the entire society.
