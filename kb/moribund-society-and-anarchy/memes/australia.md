## Australia in Moribund Society and Anarchy

Australia appears in Jean Grave's *Moribund Society and Anarchy* in several distinct contexts: as a site where Anarchist ideas took root, as a reference point for the global spread of civilization, and as an example in Grave's critique of European colonialism and its treatment of indigenous peoples.

### Anarchist Propagation in Australia

In the opening chapter, Grave catalogues the international reach of Anarchist ideas, noting that "Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian—a little everywhere and in all idioms" [001-008]. This places Australia among the countries where Anarchist propaganda gained a foothold in the late nineteenth century.

Grave returns to this theme in a later chapter, affirming that "Whether in France, Spain, Italy, England, America, or Australia, the Anarchists want the abolition of private property, the destruction of authority, complete autonomy of the individual without any restriction. This is the common basis of the idea" [018-004]. Australia thus serves as evidence of the international unity of Anarchist convictions.

### Australian Indigenous Peoples and the Boomerang

In his discussion of race and civilization, Grave uses Australian Aboriginal peoples to argue for the latent capacities of so-called "inferior" races. He points to the boomerang as an example of indigenous ingenuity that European science could not replicate:

> Thus for example, did not these miserable Australians, so low in the scale of humanity, invent the boomerang, that projective missile with its curious power of returning to the thrower, which the Europeans, in spite of their ability, have not succeeded in imitating, and which all their science of ballistics is unable to explain? [015-019]

Grave asks rhetorically: if Aboriginal Australians could produce such a unique invention under adverse conditions, what might they have achieved under different circumstances? This argument serves his broader anti-colonial case against the destruction of indigenous cultures.

### Australian Tattooing and Atavism

Grave also references "certain Australian and African tribes" in his discussion of tattooing and bodily modification, juxtaposing their practices with European customs of ear-piercing and hair-dyeing to challenge the presumption of European cultural superiority [009-011].

### Australia and Global Civilization

In a passage on the obsolescence of racial categories, Grave's interlocutors argue that "civilization from France to Russia and from America to Australia being the same civilization everywhere" [015-021]. While Grave largely agrees that racial distinctions are dissolving through global interconnection, Australia—as the farthest geographic reach of European civilization—illustrates the spatial extent of the modern capitalist order.
