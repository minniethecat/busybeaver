# Government

## Definition

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], [[the-state|government]] is the institutional embodiment of authority — the administrative, legislative, and executive machinery by which a privileged minority maintains its dominion over the producing majority. It is not, as its apologists contend, a neutral arbiter of social conflicts or a necessary coordinator of collective life, but a class instrument. The anarchist conclusion is stark: "No [[the-state|government]]" [007-014]. This negation is not nihilistic but analytical — [[the-state|government]] exists because property must be defended, and property must be defended because it is unequally distributed.

## Government as Class Instrument

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of [[the-state|government]] is grounded in a materialist analysis of its function. The entire [[the-state|government]]al apparatus — [[the-law|law]]s, deputies, magistracy, police, army — exists because "those who possess need some [[force-and-violence|force]] to protect them in the secure enjoyment of their privileges" [007-012]. Government is not a service provided to all citizens equally but a protection racket operated by and for the possessing classes.

> In fact there is a necessity for government, for laws, for deputies to make these laws and a magistracy to apply them, for a police-force to maintain the decisions of the magistracy, because those who possess need some force to protect them in the secure enjoyment of their privileges and to put down those who have nothing. [007-012]

The worker, by contrast, has nothing to defend. All the "[[the-state|government]]al paraphernalia" is maintained at the worker's expense, yet "he alone bears" that cost "without deriving any profit therefrom" [007-013]. The [[the-state|state]] taxes the producer to fund the apparatus that keeps the producer in subjection.

## The Illusion of Good Government

A recurring theme in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is the futility of reforming [[the-state|government]]. The prejudice that what is needed is "good" [[the-state|government]] — honest officials, just [[the-law|law]]s, responsive institutions — persists even among those who recognize its present corruption. But this is a contradiction: if all men were reasonable and just, "there would be no need of [[the-state|government]]" [007-010]. The very existence of [[the-state|government]] presupposes the conflicts it claims to resolve — conflicts that arise from the property relations it exists to protect.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies a profound contradiction in the reformist position. Even intelligent individuals "will readily agree that if all men were reasonable there would be no need of [[the-state|government]]," yet they persist in demanding better [[the-state|government]] rather than the abolition of the conditions that make [[the-state|government]] necessary [007-010]. The reformer's ideal of "good" [[the-state|government]] is one that "he would have made to order for himself, which would govern according to his own ideas and interests" — but this is simply a demand that the class instrument serve one's own class rather than another's [007-008].

## Government and Historical Progress

Despite the pretensions of [[the-state|state]]smen, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that [[the-state|government]] is not the engine of historical progress but its obstacle. "Without heeding the clamors of some, the rancor of others, the attempts of [[the-state|government]]s, the idea grows and progresses without cessation" [001-013]. The great transformations of human society — the development of science, the spread of emancipatory ideas, the growth of solidarity — occur not because of [[the-state|government]] but in spite of it.

> Governmental fury, oppressive measures, the rage of frustrated ambition, may set themselves against the ideas and their propagators; today the opening is made; they can no longer be prevented from making their way. [001-015]

This is a dialectical observation: [[the-state|government]]al repression of ideas testifies to their power. The [[the-state|state]] does not bother to suppress what does not threaten it. The very persecution of anarchist ideas confirms their r[[progress|evolution]]ary potential.

## The Anarchist Alternative

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s rejection of [[the-state|government]] is not a rejection of social organization. He distinguishes between the authoritarian organization imposed from above by [[the-state|government]] and the [[association|free association]] that emerges from below when people coordinate their own affairs. The choice is not between [[the-state|government]] and chaos but between heteronomous rule and autonomous self-organization.

The key insight is that "whatever the name in which the new authority clothes itself, however democratic it may proclaim itself," [[the-state|government]] remains [[the-state|government]] — a separate apparatus standing above society, claiming the right to command [007-013]. The anarchist alternative is not a better [[the-state|government]] but the abolition of the social conditions — above all, private property — that make [[the-state|government]] both possible and necessary.

> What matters the choice of the hand that strikes you? It is not to be struck at all, that one should aim at! [007-013]
