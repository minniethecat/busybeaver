# Community

## Definition

In *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, community represents the natural form of human social organization — a voluntary association of individuals bound by mutual interests, shared needs, and collective solidarity. For Jean Grave, community is not opposed to individuality but is its necessary condition: "individuality cannot develop except in the community" [036-001]. This reciprocal relationship forms one of the foundational principles of Grave's anarchist philosophy.

## Grave's Analysis

Grave argues against those who "repudiate organization" and "swear by the individual alone," declaring that they "despise the community" [073-001]. He contends that such extreme individualists "can never have studied the psychological and physiological nature of man" and "have no idea of what constitutes the real life of man, its physical, moral, and intellectual needs" [073-001]. For Grave, human beings are inherently social creatures whose fullest development requires community.

The concept of community in Grave's thought is intimately linked to his understanding of the egoist. He distinguishes between the "perfect egoist" — who thinks only of themselves and treats others solely as means — and the "humane egoist" who "is to suffer with the sufferings of those who surround him" [095-001]. The latter type finds their own fulfillment inseparable from the well-being of the community. Grave sees this as the natural endpoint of human moral development: the recognition that one's own interests and the community's interests ultimately coincide.

Grave traces the ideal of community back to Rabelais's Abbey of Thelemes, noting however that in Rabelais's vision the community was "reserved to a minority of privileged persons" [040-001]. The anarchist community, by contrast, would be universal and inclusive — the natural form of association for all people once the artificial constraints of state and capital are removed.
