# Competition

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis, competition is not a natural expression of human nature but a systemic feature of capitalist economic organization. It is "the great actuating [[force-and-violence|force]]" of the capitalist system, the mechanism through which the antagonism of individual interests is expressed and reproduced [010-006]. Competition stands in direct opposition to solidarity — where solidarity represents the natural [[the-law|law]] of mutual aid, competition represents the artificial [[the-law|law]] of the market imposed by private property.

## The Mechanism of Competition

Under capitalism, competition operates as a coercive [[force-and-violence|force]] that shapes the material conditions of workers' lives. The antagonism between workers is established through the structure of the labor market itself: "competition between the unemployed and those who work" allows capitalists to depress [[wage-labor|wages]] and intensify exploitation [005-019]. When workers are thrown out of employment, "the prospecting for vacancies again sharpens the competition, causing them to offer themselves at a lower price" — surrendering to the employer on worse terms [010-008].

> Private competition is the great actuating force of the capitalist system. Antagonism of individuals, the competition of their interests, has been presented as a natural law. [010-004][010-006]

This system operates at every scale: workers compete with workers for employment, small capitalists compete with large capitalists for market share, and nations compete with nations for colonies and commercial advantage [014-010]. At each level, competition produces concentration — the strong devour the weak [019-031].

## Competition vs. Solidarity

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s central argument is that competition and solidarity represent opposing organizing principles for human society. [[solidarity|Solidarity]] is the natural condition — "the social instinct" rooted in [[progress|evolution]]ary biology and the interdependence of social existence — while competition is an artifact of specific institutional arrangements, above all private property [016-009].

> The reign of solidarity must replace that of competition. But can anyone seriously hope to establish solidarity while maintaining the cause which engenders competition? [016-009]

The point is not to reform competition or mitigate its effects but to remove its structural cause. As long as private property creates opposing material interests, "the antagonism of individuals, the competition of their interests" will persist regardless of moral exhortation or regulatory intervention [010-004].

## Competition in Commerce and Colonization

[[jean-grave|Grave]] extends his analysis of competition to international relations. Commercial competition among the great capitalist powers drives colonial expansion: "one must figure on competition; 'commerce must be protected'" becomes the rationale for [[militarism|military]] intervention and conquest [014-010]. The "pompous names of conquest, colonization, civilization, patriotism" are ideological masks for the competitive struggle over markets and resources [011-007].

## The Socialist Alternative

The anarchist solution to competition is not [[the-state|state]] regulation or nationalization — which [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues would merely transfer competitive dynamics to the political sphere — but the abolition of private property and the establishment of [[association|free association]] on the basis of solidarity. When no one fears for their subsistence, "people need not fear the competition of their fellows" [010-014].

> Make it so that nobody need fear for the morrow, that everyone may produce according to his aptitudes and consume according to his needs — then competition disappears. [010-014]
