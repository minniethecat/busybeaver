# Charles Letourneau

## Biography

Charles Letourneau (1831–1902) was a [[france|French]] anthropologist and sociologist whose work focused on the [[progress|evolution]] of human societies, particularly the development of morality, property, and political institutions. A prolific author, his major works include *L'Évolution de la propriété* (The [[evolution|Evolution]] of [[private-property|Property]], 1889) and *L'Évolution de la morale* (The [[evolution|Evolution]] of Morality, 1887). Letourneau's [[progress|evolution]]ary approach to social phenomena placed him within the broader current of nineteenth-century positivist sociology.

## Connection to Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Grave]] references Letourneau in the endnotes of *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]* as an anthropological authority on the [[progress|evolution]] of property relations [024-001]. Letourneau's [[progress|evolution]]ary analysis of property — tracing its development from primitive communism through various forms of collective and individual ownership — provides empirical support for [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s central argument that private property is not a natural or eternal institution but a historically contingent development:

> Man, then, like all other animals, is but the product of an evolution worked out under the influence of the environment in which he lives and the conditions of existence he is forced to submit to or combat. [004-010]

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws on Letourneau's data to challenge the bourgeois economists' treatment of property as a "natural [[the-law|law]]," showing instead that property relations have varied dramatically across cultures and historical periods [005-010] [024-020].

## Intellectual Significance

Letourneau's work represents the application of [[progress|evolution]]ary theory to social institutions — a methodological approach that [[jean-grave|Grave]] shares. By demonstrating that property, morality, and [[the-law|law]] are products of social [[progress|evolution]] rather than divine decree or natural [[the-law|law]], Letourneau's anthropology provided anarchist theorists with empirical ammunition against conservative ideologies that naturalize existing power relations.
