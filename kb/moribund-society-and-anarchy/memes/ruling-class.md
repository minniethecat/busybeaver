# Ruling Class

## Definition

The "ruling class" is, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], the structural antagonist of the producing masses. It is not defined merely by wealth — though wealth is both its instrument and its reward — but by its relationship to the instruments of social power: ownership of the means of production, control of the [[the-state|state apparatus]], and command of the ideological institutions that legitimize the existing order. The ruling class is, in this sense, synonymous with the "exploiting class" — its existence depends on the [[exploitation]] of those who labor.

## The Mechanisms of Rule

[[jean-grave|Grave]] does not treat ruling-class dominance as a simple matter of brute force, though force is always available as a last resort. The ruling class maintains its position through a more sophisticated combination of mechanisms: [[the-state|state]] institutions (law, police, military, magistracy) that directly coerce; economic structures ([[wage-labor|wage system]], private property, [[competition]]) that compel submission through material necessity; and ideological apparatuses ([[religion|the church]], the press, the education system) that manufacture consent.

What distinguishes [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis is his attention to the intersection of economic and political power. The state is not a neutral arbiter between classes but "the institution which the exploiting class has established to defend those privileges which can only be secured by the spoliation of the masses" [001-005]. Every organ of government — from the legislature that enacts [[the-law|laws]] to the police who enforce them — exists to protect ruling-class interests.

## Patriotism and Mercantilism

One of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most incisive critiques concerns the fusion of [[patriotism]] and economic interest in ruling-class ideology. He describes colonial expansion and international trade policy as "patriotism and mercantilism combined — brigandage and highway robbery for the benefit of the ruling classes" [011-008]. The language of national interest, national honor, and national security serves to disguise what is, in reality, the pursuit of ruling-class economic advantage.

"What the [[ruling-class|ruling classes]] must have is new markets for their products" [012-004]. The working people who are conscripted to fight wars for these markets derive no benefit from the conquests — they pay in blood for profits that accrue to others. The cynical manipulation of patriotic sentiment to serve economic predation is a recurring theme in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s writing.

## The Hypocrisy of "Civilization"

The ruling class's self-presentation as the bearer of [[civilization]] is subjected to withering critique in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s work. "But civilization has nothing in common with your highway-robber practices" — this direct address captures his method of exposing the gap between rhetoric and reality [010-006]. The ruling class claims to represent order, culture, and progress, yet its actual practices — exploitation at home and [[conquest|conquest]] abroad — are indistinguishable from the brigandage it condemns when performed by individuals outside its ranks.
