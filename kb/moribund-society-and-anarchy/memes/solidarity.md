# Solidarity

## Definition

Solidarity, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist philosophy, is the natural principle of mutual interdependence among human beings. It stands in opposition to the competition and "antagonism of interests" that characterize [[capitalism|capitalist society]]. Grave presents solidarity not as a moral ideal but as a scientific fact grounded in the "physiological and psychological nature of man" [001-002].

> We are for Anarchy, that is to say for the negation of all authority, and we are also Anarchist-Communists, that is, while denying private property, the basis of our present economic order, we at the same time affirm our aspirations for a future organization, in which the land and all the instruments of labor shall be placed at the free disposition of all — an organization based, on the contrary, upon the strictest solidarity. [001-003]

## Scientific Basis of Solidarity

Grave grounds solidarity not in sentimental appeals to brotherhood but in a materialist, evolutionary understanding of human nature. He draws on the scientific consensus of his era to argue that solidarity is a law of social life as fundamental as any law of physics or biology.

The evolutionary origin of humanity itself demonstrates the primacy of association. Grave traces the emergence of life from "a species of organic jelly, without definite form, without organs, without consciousness" through eons of development to the appearance of thinking beings [005-002]. At every stage, organisms succeeded not through isolated struggle but through interdependence: the vegetable sustained itself "at the expense of the mineral and the atmosphere," the animal at the expense of the vegetable, each form of life building upon and requiring others [005-002]. Humanity is not separate from this natural order but its product — "man, like all other animals, is but the product of an evolution worked out under the influence of the environment in which he lives" [004-005].

Grave's evolutionary argument for solidarity proceeds through three interlocking claims. First, primitive human groups formed not from any abstract moral ideal but from "the necessity for an association of forces" — hunting game, overcoming obstacles, repelling assailants [004-004]. These early associations were "temporary at the start, limited to the capture of the game to be hunted or the overthrow of the obstacle to be conquered," but through repeated practice, humans "were brought to understand its importance; and the societies thus formed continued to live and became permanent" [004-004]. Solidarity, in other words, is a learned capacity, discovered through experience and confirmed by its practical benefits.

> In life everything is solidary. The vegetable sustains itself at the expense of the mineral and the atmosphere. The animal lives only by feeding on the vegetable or on other animals which themselves fed on the vegetable. Mineral, vegetable and animal — it is always the same matter which circulates and produces life under its different forms. [005-002]

Second, the physical and moral environment exercises an "enormous influence upon the physiological constitution of man" — and "with still greater reason the intellectual and moral environment must influence his psychological constitution" [004-002]. Human beings are not born with fixed natures, good or evil; they are shaped by the conditions under which they live. In a society organized around antagonism, they become antagonistic. In a society organized around solidarity, they would become solidary. This environmental determinism is the scientific foundation of Grave's entire political project: change the conditions of life and you change human character itself.

Third, the evolution of moral ideas — including solidarity — is itself a historical fact observable in human societies. "Little by little man has evolved and does evolve every day. His ideas are constantly modified. Physical force, though sometimes thrusting itself upon him, is no longer admired in the same degree. Ideas of morality, of justice, of solidarity have developed" [004-004]. These ideas now have "so much weight that the privileged, to succeed in maintaining their privileges, are obliged to make people believe themselves exploited and gagged in their own interest" [004-004]. The very fact that exploiters must cloak their exploitation in the language of the common good proves that solidarity has become a felt human need.

Grave thus anticipates the more systematic arguments later developed by [[peter-kropotkin]] in *[[mutual-aid|Mutual Aid]]*, though his presentation is more polemical and less taxonomic. Where [[peter-kropotkin|Kropotkin]] would catalog species-by-species evidence of cooperation in nature, Grave focuses on the human trajectory: the slow, uneven, but unmistakable movement from brute force toward coordinated association. His scientific claim is that solidarity is not a utopian fantasy but a law of human development — one that existing institutions violently suppress but cannot extinguish.

## Grave's Analysis

Grave argues that the present social organization is "established contrary to all logic and common sense" [001-002], creating artificial antagonisms between people who would naturally cooperate. Under capitalism, people are [[force-and-violence|force]]d to "regard each other as enemies ready to devour each other for the sake of getting a mouthful of bread or taking another's job in some exploiter's sweatshop" [021-002].

The abolition of authority and property would, according to Grave, allow social harmony to "reestablish itself" naturally. He envisions a society "based, on the contrary, upon the strictest solidarity," where "people, being sure of the morrow and no longer having to hoard up provision against the future, will cease to regard each other as enemies" [021-002]. The logic is straightforward: "the more abundant the products the more easily will harmony among people be established, since they will no longer be under the necessity of measuring the means of existence" [021-002].

> Conceiving then an organization based, on the contrary, upon the strictest solidarity. Make it so that private interests shall no longer be opposed to each other, nor contrary to public interests. Make it so that personal well-being shall flow from the general well-being. Restore to each all that is necessary for his physical, intellectual, and moral development, and you will see all the ugly passions disappear. [010-004]

Grave explicitly rejects the claim that anarchist [[individualism]] and communist solidarity are contradictory. He argues that "individuality cannot develop except in the community" and that "the latter cannot exist unless the former evolves freely; and that they mutually complement each other" [001-004].

## Solidarity vs Competition

The central opposition in Grave's analysis is between solidarity as a natural principle and competition as an artificial imposition. Capitalist society, he argues, does not reflect any natural law of human behavior; it systematically *perverts* natural sociability into hostility.

"To study the mechanism of capitalistic society is sufficient to discover that it can produce nothing good" [004-002]. Grave catalogs the ways in which the existing order manufactures antagonism: "The seller's interest is opposed to the buyer's; the stock-raiser and the agriculturist ask for nothing better than a 'good epidemic and a good hailstorm' among their neighbors, in order to raise the price of their commodities" [004-002]. The development of machinery, far from liberating workers, "tends to greater and greater division of the workers, throwing them out of employment and leading them to disputes among themselves for the chance to take each other's jobs." In sum, "everything in our traditional 'society' tends to split up mankind" [004-002].

> Everything in our society of to-day is so arranged as to create antagonism of interests and thus set men of all classes at war with each other, from the highest to the lowest rung of the social ladder. [002-001]

This is not an accident but the logical consequence of a society "based upon the antagonism of interests" [002-001]. Private property and the wage system force individuals into a permanent condition of rivalry. "Founded upon this struggle between individuals, society makes of every creature the enemy of all others; it provokes war, crime, theft, and all the misdemeanors which are attributed to the evil nature of man, though they are but the consequence of the social order" [010-003]. The competitive instinct is not innate; it is produced and reproduced daily by institutions that make survival contingent on outcompeting one's neighbor.

Grave is careful to distinguish between the artificial competition of capitalism and the benign emulation that might persist in an anarchist society. "Some competition between the divers groups may indeed arise, some emulation in attaining what is best," but "this competition must be but brief, since neither mercantile, proprietary, nor governmental interest will stand in the way, and those competitors who are behindhand will have every facility for assimilating the progress made by their happier competitors" [021-001]. The difference is structural: under capitalism, competition is a fight for survival in which the loser starves; under anarchy, emulation is a collaborative pursuit of excellence in which the less advanced freely adopt the innovations of the more advanced.

The intellectual tragedy of capitalist society, in Grave's view, is that its defenders mistake effects for causes. They observe the competitive, grasping behavior that capitalism produces and conclude that human beings are naturally selfish, therefore a competitive system is the only one possible. But "the noble sentiments of humanity, its aspirations after liberty and justice have been hunted down and punished" precisely because "those who had succeeded in ridding themselves of the narrow and ferocious egoism which helps to perpetuate the present society" had begun to ask why society "having been constituted for the advantage of all, turns out only to secure the privileges of the few" [010-004]. The aspiration toward solidarity exists; the existing order exists to crush it.

This analysis leads to a strategic imperative: "Conceive, then, an organization based, on the contrary, upon the strictest solidarity. Make it so that private interests shall no longer be opposed to each other, nor contrary to public interests. Make it so that personal well-being shall flow from the general well-being" [010-004]. The transformation from competition to solidarity is not a matter of moral exhortation but of institutional redesign. Abolish the structures that pit people against each other, and people will cease to act as enemies.

## Solidarity in Practice

How would solidarity actually function in an anarchist society? Grave provides a concrete, if necessarily speculative, account grounded in the logic of voluntary cooperation.

The fundamental mechanism is the synchronization of individual desire with collective production. "Many of us will combine with the intention of realizing one of our aspirations. This association having nothing forced in it, nothing arbitrary, prompted only by some need of our being, it is quite evident that the more pressing the need the more force and activity shall we contribute to the association" [002-003]. People associate freely around shared needs; because the need is genuine and felt, they contribute energetically; because all have contributed, all share in the result. No external authority is required to compel participation or to allocate rewards.

Crucially, the logic is expansive rather than zero-sum. "Man's needs being infinite, infinite will be his means of satisfying them, and it is this variety of needs which will concur in the establishment of general harmony" [002-003]. The diversity of human desires does not produce conflict; it produces a richer, more varied field of production in which each person's specific contribution finds its place. A person with a strong desire "will come to produce not only sufficient to satisfy the co-participants, but also those in whom desire would not have been awakened but for the sight of the thing produced" [002-003]. Supply creates its own demand — not in the capitalist sense of producing for profit, but in the human sense that new creations awaken new desires in others, which in turn spur further creation.

Grave is attentive to the material preconditions of solidarity. Abundance is essential: "the more abundant the products the more easily will harmony among people be established" [021-002]. This is why the anarchist revolution must seize the full productive capacity developed under capitalism — its machinery, its land, its mines, its stores — and redirect it toward universal consumption. In a normally constituted society, "the quicker the production, the faster the perfection of mechanical appliances proceeded, the more rapidly would the amount of productive labor incumbent upon each be reduced, the sooner would it become what it really ought to be, a mere gymnastic exercise requisite for the health of the muscles" [021-002]. Technology, freed from the imperative of profit, becomes the servant of leisure and human development.

> Man's needs being infinite, infinite will be his means of satisfying them, and it is this variety of needs which will concur in the establishment of general harmony. [002-003]

The practical working of solidarity also requires a transformation of work itself. "Make work attractive. Instead of riveting it upon a small minority of society to whom it becomes a torture, do away with all your State machinery, your useless offices, and organize your society in such a way that each shall be led, by mere force of circumstances and not by any authority whatever, to cooperate in social production. Make work useful, necessary, and so that it may be a hygienic exercise instead of a torture" [010-005]. In the present society, the ideal for "everybody who wants to rise in the world is to succeed in amassing, by no matter what means, money enough to live without doing anything or by making others work" [010-004]. Under solidarity, this perverse incentive disappears because idleness no longer confers privilege — it simply means one contributes less to the shared enterprise from which all benefit.

Grave's vision of solidarity in practice is not a blueprint but a principle: the forms of cooperation will emerge from the free initiative of associated individuals, adapting to circumstances that no theorist can fully anticipate. What matters is that the structural conditions — the abolition of property and authority — make solidarity rational from the standpoint of each individual's self-interest, properly understood. In the new society, an injury to any person would be felt by all, because personal well-being would genuinely flow from general well-being [002-001]. This reciprocal identification of interests is the practical meaning of solidarity: not self-sacrifice, but the recognition that one's own flourishing depends on the flourishing of everyone else.
