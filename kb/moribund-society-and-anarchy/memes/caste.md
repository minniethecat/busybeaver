# Caste

Caste, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s historical analysis, represents the earliest form of institutionalized social hierarchy — the division of society into hereditary groups with unequal rights, privileges, and functions. It is the primordial form from which [[class]] society and [[the-state|state]] authority evolved.

## Origins of Caste

Grave traces caste to the earliest social differentiations. "Religion, property, authority, the family, having slowly evolved from human aspirations, became gradually defined; but as they became precise in conception, as their purposes grew clear, they became the nucleus of an evolution which... gradually transformed them into well-defined castes, each having its attributes and privileges" [013-001].

Among these earliest castes, the military was paramount: "Of these the military caste was not the last to form, develop, and become preponderant everywhere. For wherever it was compelled to cede the foremost rank to the sacerdotal caste, it yielded merely an honorary precedence" [013-001]. The military caste's power was grounded in its practical indispensability to rulers: "Was it not at bottom the military caste which could, by its cooperation, ensure stability of power in the hands of those who held that power? Did it not furnish the nominal or real chiefs in whom was summed up the omnipotence of caste?" [013-001].

The warrior caste was "one of the first to be constituted and to impose its authority upon other members of the clan or tribe," later "re-divided into chiefs and simple warriors, as a former step in advance had divided the tribe into warriors and non-warriors, all members of the clan having originally been warriors in case of need" [013-002].

## From Caste to Class

While caste and class are distinct concepts, Grave shows how caste structures evolved into the class system of bourgeois society. The key transformation is not the abolition of hierarchy but its modernization — hereditary privilege replaced by property-based privilege, divine sanction replaced by economic "natural laws."

Society has "found nothing better to do than to divide them into a number of castes, which may be resolved into two principle ones: governors and possessors on the one side, the governed and non-possessors on the other" [008-008]. "On the side of the first contentment and plethora; on the side of the second misery, privation, and anaemia" [008-008]. The result of this division is to pose "these two categories of individuals as enemies, between whom a ferocious war is perpetuated — a war which can end only in the irretrievable enslavement of the second or the complete destruction — so far as concerns class privilege at least — of the first" [008-008].

## The Magistracy as Caste Institution

The [[the-magistracy|magistracy]] exemplifies the evolution of caste into modern institutional form. "Authority... combining with religious sentiment and the support of the priests, it claimed to be of divine origin, assumed the form of an exclusive caste, and eventually succeeded in resisting the brutal power of the king and the nobles: thus the magistracy was founded" [006-003].

When the bourgeoisie seized power in 1789, "they took care not to destroy this pillar of social order" [006-003]. The magistracy was preserved because it served the new ruling class as effectively as it had served the old: "Maintain the privileges whose care is confided to you, use the force which ignorance concedes to you, but leave justice in peace; she has nothing to do with you!" [006-006]. The judiciary, like the army, was never neutral — it was always an instrument of the ruling caste.

## The Revolutionary Solution

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of caste leads to a revolutionary conclusion: superficial political changes that merely replace one caste with another are insufficient. In every revolution, political schemers have managed to "install themselves in the sinecures of those who have been swept away by the revolutionary tempest, and to form a new caste of exploiters by creating around them new interests" [007-002].

The anarchist alternative is not to replace one ruling caste with another but to abolish the structural conditions — [[private-property]] and [[authority]] — that make caste and class possible. The goal is a society without "governors and possessors" versus "the governed and non-possessors" — a society organized on the principle of [[solidarity]] rather than hierarchy.
