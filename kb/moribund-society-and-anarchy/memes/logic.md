# Logic

## Logic as a Weapon Against Authority

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], logic is not merely a formal exercise but a practical tool of social criticism. The anarchist project requires subjecting all received institutions to logical scrutiny: "The more difficult the problems to be solved the more need is there to acquaint ourselves with arguments and logic" [003-003]. Without logical rigor, anarchist ideas remain at the level of sentiment rather than convincing demonstration.

Grave's method is to take the premises of the existing order and follow them to their logical conclusions, revealing contradictions that the defenders of authority prefer to leave unexamined. "From the moment it is admitted that the capitalists cannot put their capital into use without the cooperation of the worker, the latter becomes the most important factor in production, and in all logic ought to receive the greater share of the product" [005-020]. The capitalist system's own logic, consistently applied, undermines its legitimacy.

## The Illogic of the Existing Order

A central theme in Grave's work is that the institutions of authority—the [[the-state|state]], [[the-law|law]], [[private-property|property]]—cannot survive logical examination. "If this regime involves so many crimes as an inevitable reaction it must be very illogical, it must crush out many interests" [009-015]. The prevalence of [[crime]], [[war]], and [[exploitation]] is not a failure of the system but its logical outcome.

> Based upon antagonism of interests, society opposes individual against individual within each class; it sows warfare among them by its institution of private property. [010-016]

This statement is characteristic of Grave's logical method: he traces surface phenomena (crime, conflict) back to their structural causes (private property, competition) and shows that the society built on these foundations logically produces the very evils it claims to deplore.

## Logical Contradictions of Reformism

Grave deploys logic with particular force against those who advocate gradual reform within existing institutions. To ask the state to intervene on behalf of workers against capital is "to give proof of the greatest want of logic, for its interference can turn out profitable only to those who control it" [019-012]. The state's purpose is defense of the existing order; expecting it to act against that order is a logical absurdity.

> At first sight this reasoning seems logical, but nothing can be falser to him who has thoroughly considered the phenomena engendered by the vicious organization which today is by courtesy called society. [019-013]

Similarly, Grave dismisses the electoral path to social transformation. "Since we tell people not to deliver themselves up to masters, to act according to their own inspirations, not to submit to the repression which forces them to do what they think wrong, we cannot, under pain of being illogical, tell them to be plastic under the intrigues behind the scenes of an electoral campaign" [017-006]. Consistency demands that anarchists reject all forms of delegating power to representatives.

## Logic and Human Nature

Grave's arguments about [[human-nature]] are structured as logical deductions from evolutionary premises. If human beings are products of their environment, then "the intellectual and moral environment must influence his psychological constitution" with "still greater reason" than the physical environment [004-003]. From this it follows logically that changing the social environment—abolishing [[authority]] and [[competition]]—will transform human behavior.

The authoritarian counterargument—that "man is too evil to know how to govern himself"—is itself illogical, for it assumes both that the existing order is a product of human nature (and therefore cannot be changed) and that authority is necessary to reform human nature (which would be impossible if it were fixed). Grave exposes this circularity without mercy.

## The Logic of the Natural Order

Grave's positive vision is of a society organized according to "the logic of the natural order of facts" [003-006]. This is not a utopian blueprint but a method: observe how things actually work—how cooperation emerges spontaneously, how individuals coordinate without command—and deduce the principles of a free society from these observations. "If his memory is capable of storing up the facts, deducing consequences and drawing a logical conclusion from them," the worker can see through the mystifications of authority [011-003].

> O unhappy logic! How human reasoning doth trip thee up! [007-010]

This exclamation captures the tragic dimension of Grave's logic: the conclusions are clear, yet most people refuse to follow where reason leads, because they are trapped by [[prejudice]], custom, and fear.
