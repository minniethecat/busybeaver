# Propaganda

## Definition

In [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s usage, propaganda is the strategic communication of anarchist ideas — not merely as abstract theory but as a practical intervention in the consciousness of the exploited. He distinguishes between two forms: the "propaganda of the word," conducted through writing, speech, and organizing; and the "[[direct-action|propaganda by deed]]," which involves direct action that demonstrates anarchist principles in practice. Both are necessary, [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues, but the relationship between them — and particularly the proper scope of [[direct-action|propaganda by deed]] — was a matter of intense controversy within the movement.

## Propaganda by Deed: The Controversy

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes sustained attention to the debate over [[direct-action|propaganda by deed]], acknowledging both its power and its dangers. "How it has been wrangled over! What an amount of fallacy has been uttered apropos of it, both by those who condemn and by those who exalt it" [017-014]. The concept is susceptible to distortion: some use it to justify isolated acts of [[force-and-violence|violence]] that [[jean-grave|Grave]] regards as "injurious to the propaganda and contrary to the end in view" [017-013].

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s position is nuanced. He does not reject [[direct-action|propaganda by deed]] outright but insists on strict criteria. An act of [[direct-action|propaganda by deed]] must be such that the individual undertaking it "is certain of being able to stand by it" — that is, prepared to accept the consequences without recantation or concealment [018-007]. It must be clearly connected to a political objective, not merely an expression of individual rage. And it must be understood as one element of a broader strategy of education and organization, not a substitute for either.

> It is likewise with "the propaganda by deed." How it has been wrangled over! What an amount of fallacy has been uttered apropos of it, both by those who condemn and by those who exalt it. [017-014]

## The Propaganda of the Word

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s primary commitment is to the patient work of intellectual persuasion. The anarchist movement, "launched forth by a group of unknown persons without means of propaganda," succeeded because its ideas addressed real needs and real suffering — not because of spectacular actions [001-005]. The "drop of water which infiltrates, dissolves the minerals, scoops a pathway" is [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s metaphor for the slow accumulation of intellectual and moral influence that propaganda achieves [016-006].

This does not mean that propaganda is merely academic. [[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that effective propaganda must speak to the concrete conditions of the exploited. Taking up "less elevated subjects" might make "more fruitful propaganda" — the abstractions of theory must be grounded in the im[[press|media]]te experiences of workers [003-002]. The anarchist propagandist must "explain it in plain, clear, precise" language, translating complex ideas into terms that resonate with those they seek to reach [003-008].

## The Perils of Caricature

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is acutely aware that propaganda can be manipulated. He warns of "a kind of propaganda — subsidized to be sure — which has slipped in among us" — agents provocateurs and sensationalists whose actions serve to discredit the movement [018-005]. The "exaggerative temperament of some fair and square comrades" can be exploited by the enemies of anarchism to paint the entire movement as violent and irrational. [[jean-grave|Grave]] calls for discipline and clarity: propaganda must represent the movement accurately, not provide ammunition to its opponents.

## Propaganda and Revolution

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], propaganda is not an alternative to r[[progress|evolution]] but its precondition. The r[[progress|evolution]] cannot succeed unless "the majority of the population really desires it," and that desire must be cultivated through years of patient propaganda [017-004]. The task of propaganda is to make the exploited conscious of their exploitation, to demonstrate the possibility of an alternative, and to build the organizational capacities that will be needed when the existing order collapses. It is, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s conception, the essential work that makes all other work possible.
