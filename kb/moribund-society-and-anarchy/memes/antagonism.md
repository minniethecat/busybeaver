# Antagonism of Interests

The antagonism of interests is one of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s most fundamental diagnostic concepts. It names the structural condition of capitalist society in which individuals — far from being bound together by [[solidarity]] — are systematically set against one another as competitors, each person's gain becoming another's loss.

## The Nature of Social Antagonism

Grave defines the problem with precision. Capitalist society is "based upon the antagonism of interests" and "makes every individual the enemy of his neighbor" [005-009]. This is not a moral failing of individuals but a structural feature of the economic system:

> "The seller's interest is opposed to the buyer's; the stock-raiser and the agriculturist ask for nothing better than a 'good epidemic and a good hailstorm' among their neighbors, in order to raise the price of their commodities... the development of mechanical appliances tends to greater and greater division of the workers, throwing them out of employment and leading them to disputes among themselves for the chance to take each other's jobs." [005-009]

The result is that "everything in our traditional 'society' tends to split up mankind" [005-009]. Society, which should be a form of cooperation for mutual benefit, becomes a battlefield where each individual wages economic war against every other.

## Historical Permanence, Changing Forms

Grave is careful to note that while society has undergone revolutions and reforms, the fundamental antagonism has persisted. "Changes of detail have been brought about by revolutions; power and property have changed hands, passed from one caste to another; but society itself has not ceased to be based upon the antagonism of individuals, the competition of their interests" [008-007].

This is the deepest critique of merely political revolution: it changes who holds power but not the structural antagonism that power enforces. The bourgeois revolution of 1789 substituted one ruling class for another; it did not abolish class rule. The antagonism of interests survived because [[private-property]] survived.

## The Effect on Human Character

The antagonism of interests shapes not only economic relations but human psychology itself. "What tends greatly to befog our ideas is that this adulterate society which governs us, based upon the antagonism of interests, has made people prey upon one another, and forces them to tear each other to pieces in order to secure to themselves the possibility of living" [001-020]. In such a society, "one must be either robber or robbed; there is no middle way" [001-020].

This produces a corrosive cynicism: "today the one who helps a neighbor runs great risk of being duped; hence the belief, among those who do not reason, that men cannot live without fighting each other" [001-020]. The antagonism of interests naturalizes itself — because people must compete to survive under capitalism, they come to believe that competition is human nature.

## The Anarchist Alternative: Solidarity

Against the antagonism of interests, the anarchists propose [[solidarity]] as the organizing principle of social life. "The Anarchists, however, say that society should be based on the strictest solidarity. In that society which they wish to realize, it must not be that individual happiness, were it only in its very least important division, be attained at the expense of another individual" [001-020].

The concept of personal well-being is redefined: "personal well-being must flow from the general well-being; when an individual feels himself injured in his autonomy or in his belongings, all other individuals must feel the same injury in order that they may remedy it" [001-020]. This is not altruism but enlightened self-interest — the recognition that in a truly cooperative society, my flourishing depends on yours.

Society has instead "found nothing better to do than to divide them into a number of castes, which may be resolved into two principle ones: governors and possessors on the one side, the governed and non-possessors on the other" [008-008]. The result is "a ferocious war... which can end only in the irretrievable enslavement of the second or the complete destruction — so far as concerns class privilege at least — of the first" [008-008].
