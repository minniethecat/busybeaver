# First International

The International Workingmen's Association (IWA), commonly known as the First International, was founded in London in 1864. It brought together trade unionists, socialists, anarchists, and republicans from across Europe in the first sustained attempt to organize the international [[class|working class]]. The International is historically significant both as a milestone in labor organizing and as the site of the decisive split between the anarchist ([[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]]ist) and Marxist wings of the socialist movement.

## In Moribund Society and Anarchy

[[jean-grave|Jean Grave]], writing in the 1890s, was a product of the [[france|French]] anarchist movement that had developed in the wake of the International's dissolution. While the International itself is not extensively discussed in the text, its legacy shapes [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s political framework in several ways.

### The Anarchist-Communist Tradition

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s anarchist-communism — which advocated the abolition of both [[government|the state]] and private property, organized through free [[association|federation]] of producers' associations — developed within the faction of the International aligned with [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]] and the [[jurassian-federation|Jurassian Federation]]. This tradition rejected the Marxist strategy of using [[government|the state]] as a transitional instrument, arguing instead that the workers' movement must prefigure [[government|the state]]less society it sought to create.

### The Jurassian Federation

The [[jurassian-federation|Jurassian Federation]], the Swiss-based anarchist section of the International, was particularly influential on the [[france|French]] anarchist movement. Its emphasis on federalism, direct action, and the general strike as a r[[progress|evolution]]ary tactic fed directly into the syndicalist and anarchist-communist traditions that [[jean-grave|Grave]] represented.

### Internationalism vs. Patriotism

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s inter[[patriotism|nationalism]] — his rejection of patriotism and national boundaries as tools of ruling-class manipulation — reflects the International's founding principle that "the working men have no country." Chapter XI ("The Country") and Chapter XIV ("[[colonization|Colonization]]") develop this argument at length, treating [[patriotism|nationalism]] as a deliberate strategy by which the bourgeoisie diverts workers' attention from [[exploitation|class exploitation]].

## Historical Context

The First International was dissolved in 1876 after intense internal conflict between the centralizing (Marxist) and federalist (anarchist) factions. The expulsion of [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]] and James Guillaume at the 1872 Hague Congress marked the formal break. For [[jean-grave|Grave]] and the [[france|French]] anarchist-communists, the International's history demonstrated two key lessons: first, that cross-border working-class solidarity was both possible and essential; second, that any centralized organization would inevitably reproduce the authoritarian structures it claimed to oppose.
