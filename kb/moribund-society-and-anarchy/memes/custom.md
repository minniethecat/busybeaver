In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] analyzes custom as a fundamental social force that shapes human conduct alongside formal institutions like [[the-law|law]] and [[authority]]. Customs are the accumulated habits and practices that emerge from collective life — sometimes reinforcing existing power structures, sometimes eroding them through slow, imperceptible change [016-005]. [[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes between customs that serve genuine human needs and those that are mere survivals of obsolete social conditions, kept alive by inertia and the weight of tradition.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis of custom draws on the evolutionary anthropology of his time, particularly the work of [[charles-letourneau|Charles Letourneau]] and other nineteenth-century ethnographers who sought to reconstruct the development of human institutions through the comparative study of "savage" and "civilized" peoples. This methodological framework — now recognized as deeply problematic in its assumptions about linear [[progress]] — was nevertheless put to radical use by [[jean-grave|Grave]] and his anarchist contemporaries. By demonstrating that customs vary across time and place, they sought to denaturalize the institutions of [[private-property|property]], [[the-family|the family]], and [[the-state|the State]], showing them to be historical products rather than eternal necessities.

## Grave's Analysis---

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s treatment of custom is grounded in his materialist understanding of social evolution. Human beings, "like other animals, of which he is but a superior specimen," are "the product of a concourse of circumstances" [010-004]. The environment shapes customs, and customs in turn shape the environment. "He has struggled to develop himself, and if he has contributed in a large measure to the transformation of the environment wherein he is situated, the latter has in turn influenced the customs he has adopted, his manner of living, thinking, and acting" [010-004].

This reciprocal relationship between custom and environment is central to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s critique of [[authority]]. Customs do not arise from abstract principles or legislative decrees; they emerge organically from the conditions of social life. Yet once established, they acquire a weight of their own — "customs are so superimposed upon each other, eliminating one here, another there, that it is only by studying the observations of former travelers and still existing tribes that we are enabled to form an approximate idea of human [[progress|evolution]]" [006-004].

> Customs do, indeed, disappear, or a prejudice is effaced; but these disappearances have been brought about so slowly that they take place without anyone being conscious thereof; nobody but old men who compare the customs of their youth with those of the youth that have succeeded them notices that manners have changed. [016-005]

## Custom and Authority---

type: concept
label: "Custom"
aliases: ["social customs", "tradition", "habit"]

tags: ["social-organization", "tradition", "evolution", "authority"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that while [[authority]] is "discussed, jeered at, lashed," it is "far from having disappeared from our customs" [007-007]. People have become so accustomed to external direction that "they would imagine themselves lost the moment there was no longer anybody to keep them tied" [007-007]. This habituation to [[authority]] — the internalization of command as normal and natural — is one of custom's most pernicious effects.

> They are so accustomed to seeing the gendarme's cap, the belted paunch of the mayor, the meddling and official insolence of the bureaucracy, the sorry-looking countenances of judge and policeman, appear in their lives at every turn, that they have reached the point of becoming accustomed to these filthy promiscuities, considering them as things which are certainly disagreeable ... but which they cannot imagine disappearing without humanity's being dislocated at once! [007-007]

This analysis reveals a central paradox in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s thought: people submit to [[authority]] reluctantly, mock it, and violate it when they can — yet they believe themselves lost when anyone proposes its abolition. The problem, [[jean-grave|Grave]] concludes, is "a matter of habit" [007-007].

## The Couvade: Custom as Historical Evidence---
id: custom
type: concept
label: "Custom"
aliases: ["social customs", "tradition", "habit"]
description: "In Jean Grave's anarchist framework, custom represents the accumulated habits, traditions, and social practices that shape human behavior — sometimes reinforcing authority and sometimes undermining it through gradual transformation."
tags: ["social-organization", "tradition", "evolution", "authority"]
quality: basic
---

[[jean-grave|Grave]] uses the anthropological custom of *couvade* to illustrate how customs preserve traces of earlier social arrangements. In societies practicing couvade, "when the woman is in confinement, it is the man who goes to bed, swallows drugs, and receives congratulations upon his delivery" [006-002].

> In this one feels that the man, in order to affirm his authority over his progeny, needs facts to prove his paternity. He would not need them if it were not contested by anterior customs, which have perhaps disappeared, but the memory of which is perpetuated by the practice of retroactive customs that the former gave rise to. [006-002]

This example serves [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s broader argument about the [[evolution]] of [[the-family|the family]] and [[property]]: customs are not eternal or natural but are the products of specific historical struggles. The couvade preserves, in ritual form, the memory of a transition from maternal to paternal authority — a transition that was contested, incomplete, and left its traces in practice long after its original context had faded [006-002].
