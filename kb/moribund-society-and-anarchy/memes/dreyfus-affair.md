# Dreyfus Affair

The Dreyfus Affair was a major political scandal that convulsed [[france|France]] from 1894 to 1906. Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish officer in the [[france|French]] Army, was wrongfully convicted of treason and sentenced to life [[prison|imprisonment]] on Devil's Island. The affair exposed deep divisions in [[france|French]] society — between [[militarism|the army]] and civil society, between antisemitic and republican factions, and between defenders of [[the-state|state]] authority and advocates of justice.

## In Moribund Society and Anarchy

The Dreyfus Affair was ongoing during the period when [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] wrote *La Société mourante et l'anarchie* (1892) and its English translation was being prepared (1899). The affair is directly referenced in the opening line of Voltairine de Cleyre's Preface:

> Moribund Society and Anarchy first appeared in France about a decade since, published by P. V. Stock, printer of numerous works pertaining to Anarchy. The conscience (?) of the French army, which the Dreyfus affair has since revealed in all its delicate scrupulosity, was immediately incensed by the work and seized the first edition. (P03-001)

De Cleyre's sarcastic reference to "the conscience (?) of the [[france|French]] army" — with the question mark — captures the anarchist perspective on the affair: the [[militarism|military]] establishment that had framed Dreyfus was the same institution that suppressed anarchist publications.

The translator adds a pointed footnote regarding the Zola trial, which was part of the Dreyfus Affair:

> Since the above was written the famous Zola trial has given a farther demonstration of the government's intention to make Dreyfus the scapegoat of its Judases in high places. — Translator (024-010)

## Anarchist Perspective

The anarchist movement was divided on the Dreyfus Affair. Some anarchists, including [[jean-grave|Grave]], were initially skeptical of the Dreyfusard movement, seeing it as a bourgeois internal conflict that distracted from the [[class|class struggle]]. Others, including [[bernard-lazare|Bernard Lazare]] (who was one of the first to publicly defend Dreyfus), saw the affair as a crucial battle against [[war|militarism]], antisemitism, and [[the-state|state]] injustice.

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s reference, [[press|media]]ted through de Cleyre's Preface, uses the affair to illustrate a broader anarchist argument: that [[the-state|state]] institutions — particularly the [[militarism|military]] and [[the-magistracy|the judiciary]] — are structurally incapable of delivering justice because they exist to protect the [[class|ruling class]] and its interests.

## Significance

The Dreyfus Affair marked a turning point in [[france|French]] political history. It galvanized the [[france|French]] intellectual class (Zola's "J'Accuse...!" in 1898), strengthened republican institutions against monarchist and [[militarism|military]] reaction, and contributed to the 1905 separation of [[religion|church]] and [[the-state|state]] in [[france|France]]. For anarchists, it provided a vivid demonstration of how [[government|the state]]'s repressive apparatus operates with impunity when not challenged by organized public pressure.
