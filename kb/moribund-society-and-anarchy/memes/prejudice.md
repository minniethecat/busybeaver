# Prejudice

## Prejudice as Obstacle to Clear Thought

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], prejudice is one of the principal obstacles to social transformation. It is not merely individual ignorance but a systematic product of authoritarian institutions that inculcate unexamined beliefs from childhood. "Starting out with a simple political negation, the Anarchist has had to attack economic and social prejudices also, to find a formula which, while destroying the present society, would constitute a new social organization" [001-003].

The anarchist project requires going beyond surface-level political critique to examine the deep-seated beliefs that sustain the existing order. Grave traces anarchist ideas back to their origins in the Enlightenment's war on prejudice: the anarchists had to "attack all social prejudices, go to the bottom of all human knowledge, in order to demonstrate that its conceptions were in conformity with the true interests of man living in society" [001-002].

> Chief among these are the law, the magistracy, the army, the legislative and executive power, etc., so that, forced to reply to all these, the idea of Anarchy had to attack all social prejudices. [001-002]

## The Persistence of Illogical Notions

Grave acknowledges that even early anarchist and socialist thinkers were not free from prejudice. "Certainly many prejudices still existed, many illogical notions appeared in the ideas promulgated" [001-008]. The development of anarchist thought has been a process of progressively purging itself of inherited assumptions--about [[the-family|the family]], [[religion]], the necessity of [[government]], and the nature of [[human-nature|human nature]].

This self-critical dimension is essential to Grave's method. If anarchism is to be a truly scientific approach to social organization, it must submit its own premises to the same scrutiny it applies to authoritarian institutions. "Should not the ideal of man be released from all fetters, all constraint? Have not all the divers revolutions he has wrought, pursued this end?" [001-015].

The difficulty of this self-purging should not be underestimated. Even within the anarchist movement, Grave observes, authoritarian habits of thought persist. Some comrades, "imagining themselves very logical, carry their reasoning to absurdity; with Anarchy as an excuse they accept a mass of ideas which have nothing to do with it" [017-003]. The struggle against prejudice is thus not only directed outward at existing institutions but inward at the "remnant of the prejudices with which we have been imbued, atavistic return begotten of our education, which makes us, while proclaiming liberty with loud voices, recoil before its consequences and deny its efficacy" [017-015].

## Science Against Prejudice

Grave contrasts prejudice with [[science]], which he regards as the antidote to unexamined belief. The scientific method--observation, hypothesis, verification--provides a model for how social questions should be approached: not through deference to tradition but through empirical investigation and logical deduction.

Yet Grave is aware that science itself can become a vehicle for prejudice when scientists remain trapped within "the prejudices of education, comparative ignorance of scientific questions which they have not studied" [009-013]. The criminologists he critiques--including [[cesare-lombroso|Lombroso]]--are scientists who "cannot allow that humanity is able to go forward without leading-strings, dungeons, and chains" [009-013]. Their prejudice is all the more dangerous for wearing the mantle of scientific authority.

Grave insists that the scientific spirit properly understood demands the elimination of all preconceptions: "The savants must manage to eliminate from themselves, completely, the bourgeois education they have received, and study social phenomena with the same strictness and disinterestedness with which they have approached the study of any special science" [009-014]. Only when investigators are "no longer influenced by considerations or prejudices foreign to science" can they reach conclusions that serve human emancipation rather than the existing order [009-014].

## The Social Production of Prejudice

Grave locates the origin of prejudice not in individual failings but in the structure of society itself. From the moment of birth, the individual is surrounded by an environment that transmits the accumulated errors of the past. "Surrounded by society they are born into the world, within the environment it offers them they acquire their first ideas, and learn a mass of prejudices and lies which they come to recognize as false only after many centuries of criticism and discussion" [010-010].

This transmission of prejudice operates through every channel of social life. The family inculcates deference to authority; the school system teaches obedience rather than independent thought; religion instills supernatural explanations that discourage rational inquiry; the press and public discourse reinforce received ideas. The weight of these institutions is immense: "the influence of the social environment upon the individual is immense, that it weighs upon him with all the heft of its institutions, with the collective strength of its members and that acquired by the long duration of its existence, whilst the individual, in reacting upon it, is reduced solely to his unaided strength" [010-010].

The result is that prejudice becomes self-perpetuating. Those who have internalized the beliefs of their society pass them on to the next generation, and the cycle continues. "The present society," Grave writes, "prevents the development of new generations by bequeathing to them a heritage of prejudices and lies which it preserves alive" [021-014]. This process ensures that even those who suffer most from the existing order may defend it, having been taught to regard it as natural and inevitable.

> It is the interest of the governing classes, of the preponderance which they hold and for which they are indebted precisely to a continuation of the state of war, which artificially maintains that state among civilized peoples. [012-003]

## The Most Stubborn Prejudice: Government

The deepest and most tenacious prejudice Grave identifies is the belief that government is necessary. "This prejudice is so much the more illogical, so much the more stupid, when the ideal of each individual in regard to 'good' government, is to have one which he would have the chance to cashier the moment it tried to prevent him from acting as he pleased" [007-008].

People cling to government not because it serves them but because they cannot imagine life without it--a failure of imagination that centuries of authoritarian [[education]] have deliberately cultivated. "But this fear of the unknown is so strong, so tenacious, that after having admitted the logic of all our objections and agreed to the truth of all our deductions, our opponent begins again: 'Yes, that is all true; but would it not be better to act prudently?'" [021-004].

This governmental prejudice is reinforced by the operation of representative institutions. In Grave's analysis, universal suffrage itself protects prejudice by selecting for mediocrity: the representative "will take good care not to offend the prejudices of his constituents or to shock received opinions. On the contrary, in order to succeed in collecting as many people as possible under his banner, he must round off his sharp corners and select a stock of commonplaces" [007-006]. Rather than challenging the prejudices of the electorate, the successful politician reinforces them, creating a feedback loop that preserves the status quo.

> To trace the whole series up to man would be to rewrite here the history of evolution, which modern science explains in a manner so clear and comprehensible that in the face of such authority one can only bow and acknowledge the evidence. [005-006]

## Prejudice and the Resistance to Revolution

Prejudice plays a critical role in preventing people from embracing revolutionary change. Grave observes that even those who admit the justice of anarchist criticisms recoil from the practical conclusions they entail. "Overcome by inherited prejudices, the legacy of our ancestors and nurtured by education, relations, and apathy, they hasten to add that it is scarcely realizable--and forever remain in a state of speculation" [020-002].

This is not merely intellectual timidity. It is the grip of deeply ingrained habits of thought that make the existing order seem natural and alternatives unimaginable. "The most sincere people in the world, affected by their surroundings, education, and habituation to authority, likewise believe that everything is lost when they see it disappear from their horizon; and having no further answer to make, regularly come back, without perceiving it, to their first argument, unable to imagine a society without laws, judges, or policemen" [021-003].

Those who claim that anarchist ideas are not "practicable" are, in Grave's view, simply expressing the depth of their own prejudice rather than offering a reasoned objection. "They perpetuate existing prejudices by flattering those to whom they speak, and seek personally to profit as much as possible from the present situation; before long their ideal vanishes to make room for the instinct towards the preservation of the existing order of things" [022-006].

## Overcoming Prejudice

The anarchist response to prejudice is not coercion but education, reasoned argument, and the demonstration of viable alternatives. Since prejudice is a product of environment and upbringing, changing the environment--abolishing the institutions that depend on and reproduce prejudice--will allow individuals to think freely.

Grave's [[experimental-method|experimental method]] is designed precisely to overcome the tendency to accept received ideas without examination. Each institution must be "passed through the sieve of reason" [009-014]; nothing is exempt from critical scrutiny. But this rational examination is only half the battle. Prejudice must also be combated through action. "Every attack upon prejudice, everything which destroys an error or proclaims a truth, comes under their domain. The Anarchists disdain no contribution, reject no assistance, and ask no better than to join hands with all who have something new to offer" [016-018].

The slow erosion of prejudice is itself a sign of progress, even when invisible to those living through it. "Customs do, indeed, disappear, or a prejudice is effaced; but these disappearances have been brought about so slowly that they take place without anyone being conscious thereof; nobody but old men who compare the customs of their youth with those of the youth that have succeeded them notices that manners have changed" [016-007]. This gradual process, however, must eventually culminate in revolutionary rupture when the accumulated weight of obsolete institutions can no longer be sustained.

## The Prejudice of Vanguardism

Grave is notably critical of a prejudice he detects even among some anarchists: the belief that the masses are passive material to be shaped by an enlightened minority. "Another very generally accepted prejudice among Anarchists is to consider the masses as plastic dough, which may be moulded at will and about which there is no necessity of troubling oneself. This notion comes from the fact that, having made one step in advance of the rest, these people consider themselves in a way as prophets" [023-016].

This attitude, Grave argues, is "an inheritance from our authoritarian past" [023-016]. It reproduces within the revolutionary movement the very hierarchy and contempt for ordinary people that anarchism is supposed to abolish. Against this temptation, Grave insists that the role of the anarchist is not to lead but to explain, not to command but to convince. "We must get used to looking at things coldly, and not persist in regarding the object of our desires through magnifying glasses, and the thing to be dreaded through the little end of the lorgnette" [023-005].

The antidote to this prejudice is intellectual honesty. "To give more force to our thoughts, more direction to our acts, we must see our way clearly, warding off all illusions, freeing ourselves from every prejudice which might lead us astray" [023-004]. Revolutionary transformation requires not that a vanguard impose its will upon the masses, but that the masses themselves--freed from the prejudices that have kept them in subjection--take their emancipation into their own hands.

> In times of revolution its precursors are always outdone by the masses. Let us spread our ideas, explain them, elucidate them, remodel them if necessary. Let us not fear to look the truth in the face. [023-017]
