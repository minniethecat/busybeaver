# Natural Law

## Definition

[[jean-grave|Grave]] draws a sharp distinction between natural law and juridical law. Natural laws are "an evolving sequence" of observable regularities that govern the physical, biological, and social worlds [005-008]. They are discovered, not decreed. Unlike [[the-law|human legislation]], which is the product of authority and can be changed by a vote or a decree, natural laws describe the actual operating principles of reality — "a principle as inevitable as a natural law, which one cannot transgress without bringing about disastrous consequences" [009-006].

## Natural Law in Scientific Understanding

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s conception of natural law is grounded in the scientific method. He observes that "when the savants after great research and labor at length formulate what is called a natural law, it is not because a majority or 'chamber' has pronounced in its favor" [008-016]. A natural law is proclaimed "because it has been verified by experience" — its authority derives from its correspondence to observed reality, not from any legislative act or popular vote. This is a deliberate contrast with political [[the-law|law]], whose authority rests entirely on the coercive power of the [[the-state|state]].

The scientific model of natural law provides [[jean-grave|Grave]] with both a critical tool and a constructive principle. Critically, it allows him to expose the pretensions of political legislators, who claim to create binding obligations through mere words. Constructively, it suggests that a society organized "in conformity with the physiological and psychological nature of man and adequate to the observance of natural laws" would be a free and harmonious society [004-002].

## Critique of Pseudo-Scientific Natural Law

While affirming genuine natural laws discovered through science, [[jean-grave|Grave]] is sharply critical of the way the concept has been appropriated to justify existing social arrangements. "The economists have taken social facts resulting from a bad organization, and setting them up as 'natural laws,' making them the cause of what exists" [006-012]. This is a classic ideological inversion: contingent features of a particular economic system — such as [[competition]], wage inequality, or the concentration of wealth — are declared to be immutable natural laws against which reform is futile.

This critique extends to the Malthusian and social Darwinist doctrines of [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s era, which claimed that poverty and inequality were natural outcomes of immutable biological laws. [[jean-grave|Grave]] counters that the "pretended 'natural laws'" of the economists "are violated by their own operation" — the very fact that these so-called laws produce widespread suffering and fail to maintain the population demonstrates that they are not genuine natural laws at all [007-004].

## Natural Law and Morality

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], a correct understanding of natural law has profound implications for [[morality]]. Human beings, as part of the natural world, are subject to the same evolutionary and causal processes as everything else. [[morality|Moral]] principles, properly understood, are not arbitrary conventions or divine commands but principles of conduct derived from an understanding of human nature and social life — principles that can be investigated scientifically and refined through experience.

The anarchist society [[jean-grave|Grave]] envisions is not one in which people are freed from all constraint, but one in which the only constraints are those imposed by natural laws themselves — constraints that operate not through police and prisons, but through the natural consequences of actions, which intelligent beings can understand and voluntarily respect.
