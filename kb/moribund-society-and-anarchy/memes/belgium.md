# Belgium

## Overview

Belgium is referenced by Jean Grave in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* as one of the European countries where anarchist propagandist organizations had taken root during the movement's international expansion. Grave mentions Belgium within a sweeping enumeration of nations where "groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created" as anarchist ideas spread globally [044-044].

## In Grave's Text

In his survey of the growth of the anarchist movement, Grave places Belgium alongside other European nations where the movement had established a presence despite widespread opposition. He writes that "in spite of the crusade of those who could consider themselves, in some degree, leaders of any of the divers divisions of public opinion, in spite of calumnies, excommunications, condemnations, in spite of the prison, the idea of Anarchy has made headway" [044-044].

> Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian — a little everywhere and in all idioms. [044-044]

The Belgian anarchist movement was part of the broader international expansion Grave describes, wherein anarchist ideas "have radiated through all classes of society" and "art, science, literature, are impregnated with the new ideas and serve as vehicles for them" [044-044].

## Historical Context

By the late nineteenth century, Belgium had a developing anarchist and socialist movement centered in its industrial regions. Belgian workers were active in the International Workingmen's Association (the First International), and Belgian delegates participated in international anarchist congresses. The country's position between France, Germany, and the Netherlands made it a crossroads for the circulation of radical ideas across Europe.
