# Autonomy

## Definition

Autonomy occupies a central place in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s conception of anarchist society. It is, along with [[solidarity]] and equality, one of the three foundational principles upon which a free society must be built. [[jean-grave|Grave]] defines it as "complete autonomy of the individual without any restriction" — a condition in which each person acts "under the pressure of his own impulses only" and submits to "no constraint or restriction of his autonomy" [002-006]. This is not the limited autonomy of liberal theory, which operates within the framework of laws and institutions, but an absolute autonomy that brooks no external authority whatsoever.

## Autonomy and Association

The apparent paradox of autonomy is its compatibility with social life. How can individuals who are entirely autonomous coexist without their freedoms colliding? [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s answer lies in the principles of [[free-association|free association]] and mutual aid. Autonomous individuals do not need to be coerced into cooperation; they naturally associate when common interests are recognized. In a society "based upon solidarity, entire equality, and absolute autonomy for the individual, without authority" [005-011], coordination emerges not from command but from the voluntary convergence of individual wills.

The key condition is that each person retains "none of their rights, no part of their autonomy" in the associative relationship [007-003]. Association must be a means of extending individual capacities, not a pretext for submerging the individual in the collective or imposing majority decisions on dissenters.

## The Material Preconditions

[[jean-grave|Grave]] is clear that autonomy cannot flourish under conditions of material dependency. A person who "does not know whether he will have work tomorrow" is not autonomous, regardless of their formal legal freedoms. The wage-worker who must submit to an employer's direction to survive has surrendered effective autonomy, even if no law compels the submission. Genuine autonomy therefore requires the abolition of [[exploitation|economic exploitation]] and the establishment of conditions in which each person can satisfy their needs through their own productive activity in voluntary cooperation with others.

There are also psychological and educational preconditions. [[jean-grave|Grave]] acknowledges that the individual must "study to the bottom and solve" the problems of social life "if he desires to be able to profit by this autonomy which he demands" [004-018]. Autonomy is not simply a gift conferred by the absence of government; it is a capacity that must be cultivated through [[education]], practice, and the experience of self-directed action.

## Autonomy Against Authority

Autonomy and [[authority]] are, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], fundamentally incompatible. Every exercise of authority — whether political, economic, religious, or patriarchal — is a negation of someone's autonomy. The anarchist project is therefore, at its core, the project of maximizing autonomy by eliminating all forms of authority. This does not mean the elimination of all influence or interdependence — humans are social beings who learn from and depend on each other — but it does mean the elimination of all relations in which one person's will is substituted for another's.
