# Bourgeoisie

The **bourgeoisie** is the capitalist class that controls the means of production, capital, and land in modern society. In Jean Grave's anarchist analysis in *Moribund Society and Anarchy*, the bourgeoisie represents not merely an economic class but a social force that actively obstructs human emancipation by perpetuating exploitation and maintaining the machinery of government for its own benefit.

## Definition

Grave defines the bourgeoisie as the class that has succeeded the aristocracy in exercising authority over society, yet retains the same fundamental relationship of domination. The Anarchists' task is complicated, Grave notes, "precisely because the ideas we have to controvert are precisely those which the bourgeoisie has put forward to the people as a panacea" [003-003]. Having overthrown divine right, the bourgeoisie erected new justifications — "natural laws," economic necessity, and the rhetoric of individual merit — to legitimize its continued exploitation of the working class. As Grave observes, "it is not by changing governors that the ills from which we suffer may be cured" [003-003].

## Role in Social Exploitation

Grave argues that the bourgeoisie maintains its position not through merit but through the inherited structures of property and authority. The class system ensures that wealth and power remain concentrated:

> "For the man born, educated, brought up in the hothouses of wealth and luxury, it is very difficult, even impossible, save under exceptional circumstances, to come to doubt the legitimacy of the situation he occupies. For the parvenu it is still more difficult, for he believes he owes his situation to his talent and his work." [010-095]

The mechanisms of [[capitalism]], [[private-property]], and [[authority]] work together to preserve bourgeois dominance. Grave emphasizes that the exploited classes are systematically persuaded to defend the interests of their exploiters:

> "It is necessary to make him understand further how the capitalistic classes have interested him in the existing society, persuaded him to defend the privileges of his exploiters while he believes himself defending his own interests in an organization which, in fact, has nothing for him but promises never to be realized." [001-108]

## Ideological Apparatus and "Natural Laws"

Grave devotes particular attention to how the bourgeoisie constructs ideological justifications for its rule. "The divine origin of authority and property being denied by bourgeois science itself," he writes, "the bourgeoisie have sought to give them solid and natural bases." The economists "have taken social facts resulting from a bad organization, and setting them up as 'natural laws,' making them the cause of what exists while they are but the effects" [005-010]. This ideological inversion — presenting the consequences of exploitation as its natural preconditions — is, for Grave, the bourgeoisie's most insidious achievement.

The rhetoric of "natural laws" serves to render the existing order seemingly immutable. If the market operates according to laws as inexorable as gravity, then poverty, inequality, and exploitation appear not as social choices but as facts of nature. Grave's task is to expose this mystification: to show that what the economists call "laws" are in fact the contingent products of a historically specific, deliberately maintained system of class domination.

## Historical Emergence and the 1789 Model

Grave offers a nuanced historical analysis of how the bourgeoisie came to power. In 1789, he argues, "the bourgeoisie was able to substitute itself for 'divine right'" precisely "because it had prepared itself intellectually for such substitution" [003-010]. This historical precedent carries a direct lesson for the working class: if the bourgeoisie needed decades of intellectual preparation to overthrow the aristocracy, the workers must undertake the same preparation to overthrow the bourgeoisie.

The French Revolution thus serves as both inspiration and warning. The bourgeoisie succeeded not through spontaneous violence but through the patient construction of an alternative worldview — the ideas of liberty, equality, and fraternity that delegitimized aristocratic privilege. Yet once in power, the bourgeoisie simply replaced one form of exploitation with another.

## Historical Suppression of Anarchist Ideas

Grave notes how bourgeois historians have deliberately obscured revolutionary figures like [[jacques-roux]] who challenged property relations during the French Revolution:

> "Hence the bourgeois historians have left them in the shade. Their history is still to be written; the documents buried in the archives and the libraries still await him who shall have the time and the courage to dig them up." [001-007]

This suppression extends beyond historiography to the broader anarchist movement. Despite systematic repression, Grave observes that "without heeding the clamors of some, the rancor of others, the attempts of governments, the idea grows and progresses without cessation, proving to the bourgeoisie that the truth can neither be suppressed nor silenced. Sooner or later it will find its echo" [001-013].

## Critique of Bourgeois Philanthropy

Grave is particularly critical of bourgeois charity and philanthropy, which he sees as a way for the privileged to ease their conscience without addressing the root causes of poverty:

> "When the influences of environment, education, or heredity, leave them leisure to reflect upon social misery and turpitude; when they reckon up their existence, they try as much as possible to remedy misery with charity. Whence, philanthropic works! But the habit of believing society normally constituted, the habit of considering poverty eternal, the result of the laborer's misconduct, engenders an unfeeling character, inquisitorial in its philanthropy." [010-095]

The [[revolution]] Grave advocates is not merely a transfer of property from one class to another — as occurred in 1789 — but a fundamental reorganization of society that abolishes class distinctions entirely. Mere [[reform]] within the existing system cannot resolve the contradictions created by bourgeois rule.

## Relationship to Government and the State

Grave's analysis shows that the bourgeoisie and [[the-state]] are intertwined in a relationship of mutual dependence. The bourgeoisie depends on government institutions — the [[police]], the [[the-magistracy|magistracy]], and the [[militarism|military]] — to protect its property and suppress dissent. Political movements that seek only to replace bourgeois governors with new ones fail to address the structural nature of exploitation:

> "Hence it is not a question of saying or writing that the laborer is exploited; it is necessary to explain to him above all how in changing masters he does not cease to be exploited, and how, were he to put himself in his master's place, he would in turn become an exploiter." [001-108]

## The Intellectual Preparation of the Working Class

The historical precedent of 1789 offers Grave both a model and a warning. "If the bourgeoisie was able in 1789 to substitute itself for 'divine right,' it was because it had prepared itself intellectually for such substitution," he writes, adding that "the more rapid the downfall the more haste should we workers make to prepare ourselves intellectually, not to replace the bourgeoisie in exercising authority but to abolish all authority" [003-010]. The lesson is clear: the anarchist revolution requires not merely a change in who holds power but the abolition of the power structure itself — and this requires intellectual preparation at least as thorough as the bourgeoisie undertook before 1789.
