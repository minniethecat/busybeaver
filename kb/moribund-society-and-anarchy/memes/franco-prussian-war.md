# Franco-Prussian War

## Overview

The **Franco-Prussian War** (1870–1871) was a major European conflict between the Second [[france|French]] Empire under [[napoleon-iii|Napoleon III]] and the Kingdom of Prussia under [[otto-von-bismarck|Otto von Bismarck]]. The war ended in decisive Prussian victory, the collapse of the [[france|French]] Empire, the loss of Alsace-Lorraine, and the imposition of a five-billion-franc war indemnity on [[france|France]]. Most significantly for the anarchist tradition, the war's aftermath produced the [[paris-commune|Paris Commune]] of 1871 — the first large-scale experiment in workers' self-government.

## Grave's Analysis

In *Moribund [[society|Society]] and [[anarchy|Anarchy]]*, [[jean-grave|Jean Grave]] discusses the Franco-Prussian War not as military history but as a case study in the class dynamics of nationalist war. His focus is on the war indemnity and how it was financed — an analysis that exposes "capitalistic '[[patriotism]]' in all its splendor" [012-019].

> What did you do in 1871, in the Franco-Prussian war, which terminated for us, as everybody knows, in paying an indemnity of five billion francs? To whose interest was it to pay this indemnity, if not to that of the bourgeoisie alone in order to remain sole master of the power to exploit the "country"? [012-019]

[[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that the [[bourgeoisie]], having to pay the war indemnity, borrowed the necessary money from themselves: "the capitalists alone subscribed to the loan, thus lending to themselves the money which they needed" [012-019]. But repayment fell entirely on the working class: "the workers alone will have to toil for ninety-nine years to repay this loan, principal and interest, which never entered their pockets" [012-019].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] situates this analysis within a broader argument about the inherent class character of all wars. For the [[bourgeoisie]], war is not a sacrifice but a self-interested enterprise: "The bourgeoisie, on the other hand, have an interest in war; it enables them to preserve the armies which keep the people respectful, and defend their institutions; through it they can succeed in forcing the products of 'their industry' on others, opening up new markets with cannon shots" [011-012]. The common soldier, by contrast, is sent to fight for soil that "does not and will not belong to them" — it "belongs to a minority of property-owners, who, sheltered from all danger, bask tranquilly in their chimney-corners, while the workers foolishly go out to slay each other" [011-007].

## Class Dynamics of War Finance

[[jean-grave|Grave]]'s analysis reveals a "sleight-of-hand trick" at the heart of nationalist war finance. The bourgeoisie needed to pay the Prussians to regain control of the state apparatus — "in order to get the Prussians out of power and pocket the taxes themselves" [012-019]. A loan was issued, subscribed to by the capitalists, and guaranteed by future taxes. Since only workers produce wealth through their labor, and since taxes fall ultimately on productive activity, the workers bore the entire cost of a war they had no interest in fighting.

This critique is fundamental to [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s broader argument against [[patriotism]] and nationalism: wars are fought in the name of the nation, but their costs are distributed along class lines. The bourgeoisie profits from war through loans, contracts, and the restoration of their political dominance; the workers pay with their lives and their labor. As [[jean-grave|Grave]] summarizes the point, "War, consequently, can bring no good to the workers; we have no interests engaged in it, nothing to defend but our skins" [011-012].

## The Indemnity and the Ninety-Nine Year Burden

The five-billion-franc indemnity that [[jean-grave|Grave]] dissects was one of the largest war indemnities in modern history. [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s genius was to see through the nationalist rhetoric surrounding its payment to the underlying class transaction. The indemnity was paid not by "[[france|France]]" as an abstract national entity but by a specific mechanism: the [[bourgeoisie]] lent the money to the state (i.e., to themselves), and the state guaranteed repayment through taxes that fell overwhelmingly on workers.

The "ninety-nine years" that [[jean-grave|Grave]] invokes is not mere hyperbole — it refers to the actual structure of the French government bonds issued to finance the indemnity. These bonds were amortized over many decades, meaning that generations of workers would pay, through indirect and direct taxation, for a war fought before they were born. The [[bourgeoisie]], who floated the loan and collected the interest, faced none of this burden. Instead, as [[jean-grave|Grave]] points out, they received a dual benefit: first, the removal of the Prussian occupation that prevented them from collecting taxes; second, a steady stream of interest payments guaranteed by the state's taxing power over the working population. "They alone subscribe to the loans which war necessitates, the interest upon which we, the workers, alone pay" [011-012].

This analysis extends beyond the Franco-Prussian War itself. [[jean-grave|Grave]] identifies war finance as a general mechanism of class exploitation: "the massacres which they organize serve to rid them of the surplus which encumbers the market" [012-014]. War, in his view, performs a double economic function — it eliminates excess labor power through conscription and death, while creating new debt obligations that bind the surviving workers more tightly to the productive apparatus.

## The Bourgeoisie's Stake in War

[[jean-grave|Grave]] does not confine himself to the indemnity mechanism. He situates the Franco-Prussian War within a broader indictment of how the governing classes use war as an instrument of class rule. Drawing on the political economists of his day, [[jean-grave|Grave]] quotes their own frank admissions: "it is the interest of the governing classes, of the preponderance which they hold and for which they are indebted precisely to a continuation of the state of war, which artificially maintains that state among civilized peoples" [012-002, quoting from 012-004]. He further notes that "the true motive of every war is always the interest of the class or party in possession of the government — an interest which must not be confounded with that of the nation" [012-002, quoting from 012-006].

These admissions are damning precisely because they come from the [[bourgeoisie]]'s own theorists. [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that "our capitalists do not hide from themselves the fact that they see nothing in war but a means of continuing their exploitation of the workers" [012-014]. The patriotic rhetoric — "the country," "national honor," "civilization" — is cultivated for popular consumption, while the ruling class privately acknowledges that "the 'sacred love' of the metaphysical entity, 'country,' is nothing more than exploitation and 'small profits'" [012-011].

[[jean-grave|Grave]] also documents the material corruption that accompanies war: "the secrets of our armaments and defenses betrayed, through the complicity of the employees of the bureau of the minister of war; the most disgraceful intrigues, operating with this whirlpool of billions to the detriment of the taxpayers' pocketbooks" [012-018]. He points to "the great manufacturing metallurgists — deputies for the most part" who become "furnishers of arms, cannon, armor-plate ships, powder, and other explosives, to foreign nations" without concern that these weapons "may one day serve against our army" [012-018]. This hypocrisy — preaching [[patriotism]] to the workers while selling arms to potential enemies — exposes, in [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s view, the true content of bourgeois [[patriotism]]: "If you could parcel out your 'country' and sell it in shares you would do it speedily!" [012-018].

## Significance for Anarchism

The Franco-Prussian War is a pivotal event in anarchist history for two reasons. First, the collapse of the [[france|French]] state created the political vacuum in which the [[paris-commune|Paris Commune]] emerged — an event that demonstrated, however briefly, the possibility of workers' self-government without a centralized state apparatus. Second, the war's financing exemplified the mechanism by which the [[bourgeoisie]] converts national crisis into class advantage — a pattern that [[jean-grave|Grave]] and subsequent anarchist theorists would identify in war after war.

The war's aftermath also shaped the organizational trajectory of the anarchist movement. In the period immediately following the Commune, the anarchist idea in [[france|France]] "foundered in the misled and misleading movement of cooperative associations for production and consumption" [001-007]. It was only when a "sprout had detached itself from the springing tree" — the [[first-international|Jurassian Federation]] in Switzerland, where [[mikhail-bakunin|Bakunin]] "propagated the idea of Proudhon — Anarchy the enemy of authority — but developing, enlarging, incarnating it in social demands" — that "the true dawn of the present Anarchist movement" emerged [001-007].

Thus the Franco-Prussian War, through its twin offspring — the Paris Commune and the anarchist revival in the Jura — served as the crucible in which the modern anarchist movement was forged. The war demonstrated that the [[bourgeoisie]] would rather capitulate to a foreign power and impose crushing debt on their own population than surrender their class privileges. It demonstrated that the [[working-class|workers]], abandoned by their rulers, could organize their own defense and governance. And it demonstrated, in the [[first-international|International]]'s bitter split between Marxists and Bakuninists at the Hague Congress of 1872, the fundamental strategic divergence that would define the left for the next century: whether liberation would come through the conquest of [[the-state|state]] power or through its abolition.

## The War's Legacy for Anarchism

The Franco-Prussian War crystallized several principles that became central to anarchist thought. First, the war validated [[jean-grave|Grave]]'s maxim that "Our enemy is *the master*, no matter to what nationality he belongs!" [011-010] — a principle that would guide anarchist anti-militarism through two world wars and beyond. Second, the indemnity mechanism demonstrated that national debt is a class weapon: the [[bourgeoisie]] lend to the state in the name of the nation, then collect repayment from workers who had no voice in the borrowing. Third, the Paris Commune's suppression by the [[france|French]] provisional government — which preferred to massacre its own citizens rather than cede power — proved that class solidarity trumps national solidarity every time.

For [[jean-grave|Grave]], writing a quarter-century after the war, the lesson remained urgent. The same mechanisms of war finance, the same patriotic mystification, the same alliance between [[militarism]] and [[capital]], were operating in his own time — in the colonial expeditions to "Senegal, Tonkin, the Congo, Madagascar" that he denounces as "brigandage, pure and simple" [012-017]. The Franco-Prussian War was not an anomaly but a particularly clear instance of the general rule: that all wars in capitalist society are wars for "small profits," and that the [[working-class|workers]] of all nations have no stake in any of them.
