# Portugal

## Overview

Portugal is referenced by Jean Grave in *Moribund Society and Anarchy* in two distinct contexts: as one of the countries where anarchist groups and propagandist organs were established during the international expansion of the movement, and as one of the languages into which an important anarchist work was translated early on [011-011] [044-044].

## In Grave's Text

In his discussion of how persecution accelerated the spread of anarchist ideas, Grave notes that the prosecution of a "poor shoemaker" in France led to widespread interest in the banned work, which was subsequently "translated in German, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, Jewish" — yet notably not into English at the time [011-011]. The Portuguese translation was part of the work's dissemination across Europe.

> The work was circulated, translated in German, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, Jewish; only in English it remained untranslated. [011-011]

In his broader survey of the movement's global reach, Grave lists Portugal alongside other European nations where anarchist organizations had been founded:

> Groups have been founded, propagandist organs have been created in France, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Holland, England, Norway, America, Australia, in the Slavic tongue, in German, Jewish, Czech, Armenian — a little everywhere and in all idioms. [044-044]

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, anarchist and socialist ideas found a receptive audience among Portuguese workers, particularly in Lisbon and Porto. The Portuguese anarchist movement developed in close connection with its Spanish counterpart, with significant cross-border exchanges of publications and organizers. Portugal's anarchist press and trade union activism contributed to the broader Iberian anarchist tradition that would become one of the most significant in Europe.
