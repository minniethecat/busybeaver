# Revolution

## Definition

Revolution, for [[jean-grave|Grave]], is not a political coup but the complete overthrow of existing social institutions. It is "a means, not an end" — the necessary mechanism for destroying the structures of authority and property that cannot be reformed from within. [[jean-grave|Grave]] emphasizes that anarchists do not choose r[[progress|evolution]] but are driven to it by "the [[force-and-violence|force]] of circumstances" (021-004).

## Historical Context

[[jean-grave|Grave]] writes in the aftermath of the Paris Commune (1871), which demonstrated both the possibility of popular r[[progress|evolution]] and the consequences of failing to destroy [[the-state|state]] structures completely. The late 19th century saw intensifying class conflict across Europe, with anarchist movements gaining strength amid recurring economic crises and [[the-state|government]] repression.

## Grave's Analysis

[[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that the "poverty and discontent engendered by our bad social organization leads directly to revolt" (019-001). The economic crises, involuntary idleness, and "difficulties of getting a living more and more pronounced every day" mean that "we are marching steadily towards this r[[progress|evolution]], which will be brought about by the [[force-and-violence|force]] of circumstances, which nothing can prevent" (021-004).

The anarchist's task is not to foment r[[progress|evolution]] for its own sake but "to be ready to take part in it in order to turn it to the profit of the principles we champion" (021-004). [[jean-grave|Grave]] distinguishes the anarchist r[[progress|evolution]]ary from the political r[[progress|evolution]]ary: the former seeks to destroy institutions, not to seize them.

On the question of r[[progress|evolution]]ary [[force-and-violence|violence]], [[jean-grave|Grave]] is uncompromising. He argues that "the masses never go too far" and that "no silly sentimentalism" should restrain the r[[progress|evolution]]ary impulse — the present system "devours daily for the sake of the all-powerful belly of the bourgeoisie" (021-006) far more victims than any r[[progress|evolution]] could claim. He is clear that "it is institutions they must attack" and "no vestige of them allowed to remain" (021-005).

## The Inevitability of Revolution

[[jean-grave|Grave]] constructs a comprehensive argument that revolution is not a matter of choice but a structural inevitability built into the nature of social organization under [[capitalism]]. He draws upon geological metaphors to illustrate how gradual evolution, when obstructed, transforms irreversibly into catastrophic rupture.

In his extended analogy, [[jean-grave|Grave]] observes that "everything in nature is transformed by a slow process of evolution" — mountains erode imperceptibly over centuries, coastlines advance or retreat by centimeters per generation — but "in order that this evolution should go on without shocks it must meet no obstacles in its way; if it does, and the acquired impulse is stronger than the obstacles, it breaks them" (016-003). The mountain, undermined by countless rains and infiltrations, crumbles one day in a cataclysm that buries villages and alters the courses of rivers. The sea, having gnawed at its barrier for millennia, at last rushes through in a final devastating surge. In every case, the slow accumulation of pressure reaches a threshold where gradual change becomes impossible without destroying the existing order.

Applied to [[society]], the analogy becomes precise: "the social organization, the institutions created to defend this organization, represent the barriers which are opposed to progress" (016-005). Ideas and customs evolve continuously beneath the surface of institutions, sapping their foundations. A generation may pass without observing change, but the pressure accumulates. "The struggle may last for thousands of years; the cliffs seem undiminished till some day when, undermined, they fall before a new assault, leaving a free passage to the triumphant waves" (016-005). Revolution, in this framework, is not an aberration in the evolutionary process but its culmination — evolution itself becoming revolution when it encounters rigid resistance.

[[jean-grave|Grave]] locates this resistance squarely in the [[class]] interests of the bourgeoisie. "The bourgeoisie alone is interested in having this transformation take place without jars," he observes. "They are not willing to yield up any portion of their privileges" (016-007). Having extracted concessions over a century, the possessing class believes itself invulnerable, yet "the wave has made the breach; with the very materials torn from the exploiters it renews the attack, creating therefrom a weapon to finish their destruction" (016-007). The anarchist propagandist contributes to evolution by spreading ideas; the bourgeoisie's "senseless resistance" is what transforms evolution into revolution.

The material conditions of late 19th-century [[capitalism]] make revolution not merely possible but imminent. [[jean-grave|Grave]] catalogues the converging pressures: "the commercial crisis is increasing: the task of employing the workers becomes more and more difficult; those who are out of work behold the constant lengthening of their periods of enforced idleness; the army of the unemployed becomes more and more numerous" (018-002). The visible destitution — "beggars shivering from the nippings of frost and hunger, anxiously awaiting at the gates of barracks, hospitals, restaurants" (018-002) — creates a situation that "cannot be prolonged forever, as people will end by getting tired of starving, they will revolt" (018-002). An economic crisis, "complicated with some political occurrence, will be sufficient to fire the powder and provoke the outburst" (018-002).

Crucially, [[jean-grave|Grave]] does not believe the anarchists will create this revolution themselves. "We are convinced that the revolution will come from without our ranks and before we shall be numerous enough to provoke it" (018-002). The anarchist role is preparatory: to spread [[anarchy|Anarchistic]] ideas so thoroughly that when the inevitable explosion comes, those who participate will "know what institutions are hurting them and must be destroyed" (020-015). The revolution will occur with or without anarchist participation; the only question is whether it will be harnessed for genuine [[emancipation]] or dissipated in political substitutions. As [[jean-grave|Grave]] concludes, "revolutions are not the creations of men alone, but of institutions which bar the path of progress, and that consequently they are necessary and inevitable" (019-036).

## Revolution vs Reform

[[jean-grave|Grave]] devotes substantial analysis to dismantling the case for [[reform]] as an alternative to revolution, arguing that gradualism within the existing institutional framework is structurally impossible and ethically bankrupt. His critique proceeds from a rigorous examination of the reform proposals current in his era — income tax, reduction of working hours, minimum wage, inheritance taxes — demonstrating that each either fails to survive contact with [[capitalism]]'s self-preserving logic or actively worsens the condition of the working class.

The core of Grave's argument is that "the capitalistic organization being given, the separation of society into two classes, one of which lives at the expense of the other, no melioration can be granted to the exploited class without lessening the privileges of the exploiting class" (019-002). From this premise follow two possibilities, both damning for reformism: "either the reform is illusory, a decoy used for the purpose of lulling the worker to sleep and making him exhaust his energies in the conquest of soap-bubbles which burst in his hands when he seeks to grasp them, or, if it really might alter the situation, the privileged class, which is in possession of the power, will put forth every effort to prevent its application or turn it to their own profit" (019-002). The endpoint of either path is the same: "we must always come to the same ultima ratio — force" (019-002).

Grave's examination of the eight-hour day illustrates this dynamic in concrete detail. When the working day is shortened by law, the capitalist — "his special labor as a capitalist consisting of getting his profit" (019-014) — does not absorb the loss. Instead, he accelerates machinery and intensifies labor, compelling workers "to produce in eight hours the same amount they now do in twelve" (019-014). Drawing on Karl Marx's *Capital* for evidence, Grave demonstrates that in England the nine-hour factory legislation "had the effect of hastening the introduction of machinery" and "caused an intensification of labor which attacks the health of the worker" (019-016, 019-019). The reform arrives back "to the identical place it started from — without counting additional aggravations" (019-021).

The income tax fares no better. Grave argues that since capital "does not reproduce itself and can be nothing but the product of labor" (019-010), any tax on incomes will ultimately be passed down to producers through higher prices and depressed wages. "Whatever means the State may employ to attack their incomes, these attacks must in the end recoil upon the producers, since the incomes themselves come only from labor" (019-011). The reform agenda in its entirety, he concludes, is "meager" in quantity and destructive in quality (019-006).

Beneath the technical critique lies a deeper structural insight. [[jean-grave|Grave]] argues that "the bourgeoisie, which holds the power, will not allow its own existence to be imperiled; so long as it holds the power it will make use of it to defend the system of organization which forms its strength" (020-009). The only reforms permitted will be those "which will not touch the institutions whence its privileges are derived; the only remedies it will consent to have tried are those which will attempt to mitigate the evils without attacking the causes" (020-009). But "to touch institutions is the Revolution" (020-009). Reforms that leave the institutional bases of [[authority]] and [[private-property]] intact are, by definition, incapable of addressing the sources of [[exploitation]] and misery.

At its most pointed, Grave's critique becomes an accusation of bad faith. Those who counsel patience and gradual improvement while acknowledging the evil of present conditions "find themselves necessarily on the side of the exploiters" (020-014). Their "good will and intentions in desiring to relieve our sufferings are nothing but narcotics, which, while dulling the edge of the miseries of the poor, deliver us over, bound hand and foot, to our exploiters" (020-014). The anarchist position is not opposition to reform as such — "it is not the reforms themselves which we fight, but the lies of those who would set them up as an ultimate goal for the workers" (019-037). The historical record, Grave insists, proves "that so long as the organic bases of society are unchanged these so-called reforms will always turn to the profit of those who hold wealth and power" (020-013).

The question thus becomes not whether to improve society but whether to persist with methods that have failed for centuries. "The people are tired of struggling for utopias more futile than that of individual enfranchisement, since the only objection which can be raised against the latter is that it is unrealizable — a purely gratuitous assertion, seeing that it has never been tried, while the realization of any reform is sufficient to demonstrate its ineffectualness" (019-037). For Grave, the reformist experiment has already run its course — and produced its verdict. What remains is the clear imperative: "We do not want to modify exploitation and oppression but to destroy them" (019-036).

## Revolutionary Ethics

[[jean-grave|Grave]] does not treat revolution merely as a strategic calculation or a sociological prediction; he invests it with a powerful moral imperative grounded in lived experience, human dignity, and the irreducible right to existence. His ethical framework refuses to separate ends from means, insisting that the revolution must be conducted in a manner consistent with the principles it seeks to realize.

The starting point of Grave's revolutionary ethics is the moral impossibility of inaction. He poses a series of rhetorical questions that function as a kind of conscience test: "Can one endure the meddling of officials after one understands the contemptible part they play? Can one submit to the insolence of lawyers when reflection has robbed them of the sacred aureole by which they were formerly surrounded? Can one respect the rich man wallowing in his luxury when one knows that it is wrought from the misery of hundreds of families?" (016-012). Once a person has understood the mechanisms of [[exploitation]], continued submission is not prudence but complicity. "It is impossible for the Anarchists to be pacific, even if they so wished; they will be urged into action by the sheer force of circumstances" (016-012). The awakened conscience cannot coexist peacefully with injustice; it is driven inexorably toward action.

This moral compulsion extends to the very definition of revolutionary character. [[jean-grave|Grave]] insists that "it is not by resignation or by hope that one changes his condition, but by action; now the best way to act is to get rid of the obstacles trammeling your route" (016-010). The revolutionary subject is not someone who waits for salvation from providential leaders but "men conscious of themselves and of their strength, knowing how to make their [[liberty]] respected without becoming tyrants over others, expecting nothing from anyone else but everything from themselves, from their own initiative, activity and energy" (016-010). Grave's revolution is simultaneously the expression and the training ground of individual autonomy — a conception that distinguishes anarchist revolutionary ethics sharply from authoritarian models that demand submission to a vanguard or party.

On the difficult question of revolutionary [[force-and-violence|violence]], Grave constructs a moral calculus that is unflinching in its logic. The present social order is itself a system of perpetual violence: "the thousands of victims which the present social Minotaur devours daily for the sake of the all-powerful belly of the bourgeoisie" (021-007) vastly outnumber whatever casualties revolution might claim. Against those who counsel restraint out of humanitarian concern, Grave counters that moral responsibility lies with the system, not with those who finally rise against it. "To silence our pity we have only to recall" the routine carnage of poverty, disease, and starvation that the existing order inflicts daily on the working class (021-007). The "silly sentimentalism" that would spare a few defenders of the system while ignoring the mass suffering it produces is not morality but its perversion.

Yet Grave does not embrace indiscriminate violence. He insists that "it is institutions they must attack" and that "the doing away with individuals can be of but small moment to the workers" (021-007). Capitalist society "is strong only by virtue of its institutions" — reduced to their own unaided strength, the capitalist class could not resist, "and how many of them would have the slightest will to do so?" (021-007). The ethical focus must remain on destroying the systemic causes of oppression, not on personal vengeance. Individual acts, when they occur, are to be understood as products of the same system that generated the conditions for their emergence.

Most fundamentally, Grave locates the ethical justification for revolution in the irreconcilable choice facing every worker: "On one side we have the existing society with its cortege of poverty, uncertainty for the morrow, privations and sufferings without hope of allayment — a society in which we are stifled, in which our brains sicken for want of light, in which we must crush down deep into the obscurity of our being all our sentiments of beauty, goodness, [[justice]], and love; — on the other side, the future! — An ideal of [[liberty]], happiness, intellectual and physical satisfaction, the complete unfolding of our individuality!" (021-008). Under these conditions, the moral weight falls not on those who revolt but on those who counsel patience. "We have nothing to lose and everything to gain by a change. Society fetters us; well, let us overthrow it. So much the worse for those who get crushed in the fall; it will be because they tried to shelter themselves under its walls, or to cling to its rotten supports" (021-008).

The ethics of revolution, in Grave's final analysis, is an ethics of [[solidarity]] with the suffering and the disinherited. It does not ask for guarantees of success — "If we waited to be sure of the victory before demanding our rights, we might wait centuries for our emancipation" (021-007) — but demands only that one take one's stand unequivocally on the side of those who seek [[emancipation]]. "Whoever is not with the people is against them" (021-007). This is not a tactical slogan but a moral axiom: in a society organized around [[exploitation]], neutrality is impossible; every position ultimately serves either the preservation or the destruction of the existing order.
