## Biography

Marie Charlotte Hippolyte de Campet de Saujon, Countess of Boufflers, was a French aristocrat and writer born around 1725. She was the mistress of Louis François de Bourbon, Prince of Conti, through whom she exercised considerable influence in court circles. She moved between the Prince's palace at the Temple in Paris and the social world of the Maréchal de Luxembourg at Montmorency, where she became a close friend of Madame de Luxembourg.

Madame de Boufflers was also a writer. She composed a tragedy in prose entitled *L'Esclave Généreux* ("The Generous Slave"), which circulated in the society of the Prince of Conti and was praised in those circles.

> Madam de Boufflers was attacked with the mania of writing. She wrote a tragedy in prose, which, in the first place, was read, handed about, and highly spoken of in the society of the Prince Conti, and upon which, not satisfied with the encomiums she received, she would absolutely consult me for the purpose of having mine. (011-024)

Rousseau, characteristically, gave his candid opinion, noting that the work closely resembled the English tragedy *Oroonoko* — a fact apparently unknown to her Parisian audience. She had a daughter, Amélie de Boufflers, later the Duchess of Lauzun, whom Rousseau described as "a charming girl" of "maiden beauty, mildness and timidity."

## Relation to Rousseau

Madame de Boufflers entered Rousseau's orbit through her friendship with Madame de Luxembourg. She visited Rousseau at his cottage at Mont Louis, and he was immediately struck by her charm. At nearly fifty years old, Rousseau found himself drawn to her.

> She came rather frequently to see me with the Chevalier de Lorenzy. She was yet young and beautiful, affected to be whimsical, and my mind was always romantic, which was much of the same nature. I was near being laid hold of; I believe she perceived it. (011-006)

Rousseau claims that he resisted this attraction — he was, at fifty, conscious of the absurdity of falling for a younger woman, and he had just published his letter to D'Alembert lecturing on the dangers of the passions. He noted dryly that Madame de Boufflers, having perceived his emotion, may also have observed that he "triumphed over it." Whether she herself felt anything for him is ambiguous; Rousseau suggests, with characteristic self-consciousness, that she may have felt only "curiosity" — and perhaps resentment at not having that curiosity satisfied.

Their most significant interactions came during the crisis following the condemnation of *Émile* in 1762. As the Parlement of Paris prepared to issue a warrant for Rousseau's arrest, Madame de Boufflers — drawing on the influence of the Prince of Conti — attempted to intervene.

> Madam de Boufflers seemed rather uneasy. She appeared agitated, strained herself a good deal, assured me the Prince of Conti was taking great pains to ward off the blow about to be directed against my person. (011-069)

When it became clear that Rousseau must flee, she urged him to go to England. Rousseau refused, for reasons he himself could not fully articulate.

> Madam de Boufflers highly disapproved this resolution, and renewed her efforts to induce me to go to England, but all she could say was of no effect; I had never loved England nor the English, and the eloquence of Madam de Boufflers, far from conquering my repugnancy, seemed to increase it without my knowing why. (011-079)

On the day of his departure, she was among those who gathered at the Luxembourg castle to bid him farewell.

> Madam de Boufflers also embraced me, and said to me many civil things. (011-082)

## Significance

Madame de Boufflers exemplifies the type of aristocratic woman who played a mediating role between the world of the *philosophes* and the ancien régime court. As both a writer and a woman of influence, she occupied a distinctive position in the cultural landscape of mid-eighteenth-century France. Her tragedy, though now forgotten, represents the literary ambitions of women in the orbit of the great princes — writing that circulated in manuscript and salon readings rather than through print publication.

For Rousseau, the encounter with Madame de Boufflers was one of those moments when the power dynamics of rank and gender intertwined in ways that both attracted and disturbed him. His admission that he was "near being laid hold of" by her charm, and his subsequent pride in having resisted, reveals the same psychological pattern visible in his relations with other high-born women: attraction followed by vigilance, the fear of being captured by a world he had set himself against. Her embrace at his departure — and his surprise at it — captures the ambiguity of his status among the great: honored and cast out in the same gesture.
