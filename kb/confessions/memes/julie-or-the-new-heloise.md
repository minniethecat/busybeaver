## Overview

*Julie, or the New Heloise* (*Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse*, 1761) is [[Rousseau]]'s only novel and was the best-selling work of fiction in eighteenth-century Europe. An epistolary novel in six parts, it tells the story of Julie d'Étange, a noblewoman who falls in love with her tutor, Saint-Preux — a man beneath her social station. Forced by her father to marry the older Baron de Wolmar, Julie must reconcile passion with duty, ultimately finding a form of virtue in domestic life.

The novel's title alludes to the medieval story of Héloïse and Abélard, recasting the theme of forbidden love in a contemporary setting. Rousseau used the epistolary form to explore questions of love, virtue, social class, and the possibility of moral reform — themes that also pervade his philosophical writings.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

Rousseau composed *Julie* during a period of intense emotional experience. He was living at the Hermitage near [[Montmorency]] and had fallen passionately in love with Sophie d'Houdetot, the mistress of his friend Saint-Lambert. This impossible love infused the novel with authentic feeling:

> I gave a description in my New Eloisa. [009-019]

The first two parts were written rapidly during Rousseau's stay at the Hermitage in 1756–57. He continued working on the later parts while increasingly isolated and suspicious of those around him. The novel's composition coincided with his growing estrangement from [[Denis Diderot]] and [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]].

The publication was a triumph. The novel appeared in early 1761 and was met with extraordinary enthusiasm:

> The New Eloisa afterwards appeared with the same facility, I dare add; the faith of this Eloisa at the point of death is exactly similar to that of my Julie. [009-056]

Rousseau took special care with the edition, working with the engraver on illustrations and personally distributing copies to friends, including Madame d'Houdetot:

> Madame d'Houdetot a copy of the New Eloisa. She was desirous to have one to read in the country. [010-150]

## Literary Significance

*Julie* revolutionized the European novel. Its emotional intensity, its landscape descriptions (which helped spark the Romantic appreciation of nature), and its philosophical depth set it apart from the more formulaic fiction of the period. Readers wrote to Rousseau in tears, reporting that the book had changed their lives.

The novel's exploration of class barriers, female virtue, and the possibility of moral regeneration through love made it both controversial and immensely popular. It established Rousseau not merely as a philosopher but as one of the great literary figures of his age.
