The Abbe de Condillac (Étienne Bonnot de Condillac, 1714–1780) was a French philosopher of the [[Enlightenment]], known for his work on sensation and empiricism. He was a close associate of Rousseau, [[Denis Diderot|Diderot]], and other figures in the [[Encyclopédie]] circle.

## Biography

Condillac was born in Grenoble in 1714, the younger brother of the Abbe de Mably. As a young man, according to Rousseau, he was considered slow and unremarkable:

> The younger one, named Condillac, appeared stupid and fretful... (006-122)

> The little Condillac was still more embarrassing... (006-123)

This early impression proved misleading, as Condillac later emerged as one of the leading philosophical minds of the French Enlightenment. He published his *Essai sur l'Origine des Connaissances Humaines* (1746) and *Traité des Sensations* (1754), works that developed a systematic empiricist philosophy.

## Relation to Rousseau

Condillac and Rousseau met in Paris through their shared connection to Diderot. Rousseau mentions having already formed a connection with Condillac early in his Paris years:

> ...I had already done with the Abbe de Condillac, both of whom were on a visit... (007-010)

He was part of Rousseau's intellectual circle, listed alongside other notable figures:

> ...Gresset, Bordes, Abbe Malby, Condillac, and several others, of whom... (007-028)

Rousseau maintained his connection with Condillac even as literary politics intensified. He notes that Condillac, like himself, had not acquired literary fame as rapidly as some contemporaries:

> I was also connected with the Abbe de Condillac, who had acquired no more literary fame than myself. (008-043)

The friendship between Rousseau, Diderot, and Condillac later intersected with [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm|Grimm]]:

> ...connection with Diderot and the Abbe de Condillac, formed with Grimm a new one... (009-070)

Condillac is also mentioned by Rousseau as an example of a genuine man of letters, contrasted with those who merely pursued social advancement:

> ...for instance as the Abbes de Condillac and De Malby, MM. de Mairan... (010-127)

## Significance

Condillac stands as one of the most important philosophical figures in Rousseau's Paris circle. Unlike [[Denis Diderot|Diderot]] and [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm|Grimm]], whose friendships with Rousseau ended in bitter rupture, there is no record of a dramatic falling-out with Condillac — suggesting that their relationship was more temperate and professionally grounded. Condillac's empiricist philosophy shared certain affinities with Rousseau's own thinking about sensation and natural man, though they diverged on fundamental questions of civilization and human nature.
