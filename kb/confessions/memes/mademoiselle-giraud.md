## Biography

Mademoiselle Giraud was a Genevese woman living in Annecy, working as a quilter. She was a friend of [[Merceret]], Madame de Warens's chambermaid, and part of the wider social circle surrounding the young Rousseau. Rousseau describes her with characteristic bluntness: "her thirty-seven years, hare's eyes, daubed nose, shrill voice, and black skin." (004-030)

Giraud "took it in her head to have an inclination for" Rousseau, "always pressing Merceret, when she returned her visits, to bring me with her." (004-005) Despite Rousseau's evident disinclination — he found that "nothing could increase the aversion I had for her" — Giraud persisted in her attentions. When she drew near him "with her dried black snout, smeared with Spanish snuff, it was with the utmost difficulty that I could refrain from expressing my distaste." (004-005)

She worked occasionally at [[Madame Galley]]'s household, which "procured her free admission to the house" — a connection Rousseau later attempted to exploit when he needed a messenger to deliver a letter to the Galley and Graffenried girls. (004-029) Giraud proved perceptive: "At the very first word, Giraud discovered me." (004-030)

## Relation to Rousseau

Mademoiselle Giraud's persistent, unrequited affection for Rousseau is one of several episodes in the _Confessions_ where the author portrays himself as the unwilling object of female desire. When the time came for Merceret to return to Fribourg, "the persuasions of Giraud determined her; nay more, she intimated it was proper someone should conduct her to her father's and proposed me." (004-031) Giraud thus engineered the journey that took Rousseau away from Annecy.

Despite Rousseau's aversion, he acknowledges that Giraud's scheme "in which I found more delicacy and moderation than I had expected" showed a certain practical intelligence. (004-030) She "had sense enough to conclude that her thirty-seven years... stood no chance against two young charmers." (004-030) Rousseau contrasts her with Merceret, noting Merceret was "younger and less artful than Giraud" and "never made me so many advances." (004-032)

## Significance

Mademoiselle Giraud is a minor but vivid character in the Annecy chapters of the _Confessions_. Her portrayal reveals Rousseau's willingness to be unflinchingly — even cruelly — honest about the physical appearances and social pretensions of those around him. The contrast he draws between Giraud's "daubed nose" and "black skin" and the youthful grace of [[Mademoiselle Galley]] and [[Mademoiselle de Graffenried]] serves his autobiographical strategy of contrasting coarseness with idealized beauty.

Giraud also plays a structural role in Rousseau's narrative: her machinations precipitate his journey with Merceret, which marks another transition in the peripatetic young man's life. Her unrequited affection is presented as one more example of the misunderstandings and unwanted entanglements that characterized Rousseau's relations with the women of Annecy.
