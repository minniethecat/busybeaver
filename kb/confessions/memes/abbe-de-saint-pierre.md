The **Abbé de Saint-Pierre** (Charles-Irénée Castel, 1658–1743) was a French author and political philosopher known for his reformist writings on government and international peace. He was a member of the [[French Academy]] until his expulsion, and his works became the subject of one of Rousseau's major literary undertakings in the 1750s. Rousseau was commissioned to extract and condense Saint-Pierre's sprawling twenty-three volumes of writings, producing abridged versions of *Perpetual Peace* and *Polysynodie* before abandoning the project.

## Biography

Charles-Irénée Castel, known as the Abbé de Saint-Pierre, was born in 1658 into the lesser nobility of Normandy. He entered the Church but devoted his life to writing on political and social reform. He was elected to the French Academy, but was expelled during the Regency period on account of remarks in his work *Polysynodie* (Plurality of Councils) that displeased the Duchess of Maine and Cardinal de Polignac. (009-091)

Saint-Pierre moved in the highest intellectual circles of Paris. He was a regular guest at the salon of [[Madam Dupin]], where he mingled alongside [[Fontenelle]], [[Buffon]], and [[Voltaire]]. (007-055) In his later years, Rousseau had "been a little acquainted with the Abbe de St. Pierre, in his old age" and held a genuine veneration for his memory. (009-091)

Despite his prolific output, Saint-Pierre's works were notoriously difficult to read. Rousseau described them as containing "excellent things, but so badly told that the reading of them was almost insupportable." (009-057) He noted that Saint-Pierre "looked upon his readers as schoolboys" yet "spoke to them as men"—taking little care to make his ideas accessible.

## Relation to Rousseau

After Rousseau's return from Geneva, the Abbé [[Malby]] proposed—through Madam Dupin—that Rousseau undertake the task of extracting and condensing Saint-Pierre's works. Madam Dupin had been one of Saint-Pierre's favorites, and she "preserved for the memory of the good man a respect and an affection which did honor to them both." (009-057) Her "self-love would have been flattered by seeing the still-born works of her friend brought to life by her secretary."

Rousseau accepted the commission, receiving Saint-Pierre's manuscripts from his nephew, the Comte de Saint-Pierre, at the solicitation of [[Saint-Lambert]]. (009-057) He began with *Perpetual Peace*, which he considered "the greatest and most elaborate of all the works which composed the collection." (009-091) He then proceeded to *Polysynodie* before abruptly stopping.

Rousseau's decision to abandon the project was political. Saint-Pierre's writings contained frank observations on the French government, and though the Abbé himself had been tolerated as "a kind of preacher rather than a real politician" whom "nobody listened to," Rousseau feared that reviving these critiques would draw unwanted attention. (009-093) As a foreigner in France, Rousseau recognized that "by repeating his censures, although in his own name, I exposed myself to be asked, rather rudely, but without injustice, what it was with which I meddled." (009-093)

Later in the *Confessions*, Rousseau reproached himself with falling into the same error he had criticized in Saint-Pierre: an excessive faith in sincerity, believing that showing each party the merits of their opponents could reconcile them. (009-125)

## Significance

The Abbé de Saint-Pierre stands as an important figure in the early French Enlightenment's tradition of political reform writing. For Rousseau, the commission to edit Saint-Pierre's works provided both an intellectual challenge and a sobering lesson about the limits of free expression in ancien régime France. The episode illustrates Rousseau's growing awareness of his vulnerable position as a foreign intellectual in Paris and his developing strategy of self-censorship to avoid persecution.
