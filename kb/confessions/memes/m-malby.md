## Biography

Monsieur Malby was the chief provost (chief magistrate) of Lyons, France. He was the brother of two distinguished intellectuals: the Abbé de Malby (Gabriel Bonnot de Mably, the political philosopher) and the Abbé de Condillac (Étienne Bonnot de Condillac, the philosopher). This made the Malby household a significant node in the French Enlightenment's intellectual network.

Rousseau was introduced to Monsieur Malby through Madam de Deybens, an acquaintance in Grenoble, whose husband was a friend of the provost. Madam Deybens arranged for the young Rousseau to be taken into the Malby household in Lyons to receive training in proper conduct and social refinement: "Madam Deybens, in recommending me to her friend Madam de Malby, had requested her to form my manners, and endeavor to give me an air of the world" (006-124).

## Character

Rousseau's portrait of Monsieur Malby is nuanced and ultimately generous. On the surface, the provost's manner matched the severity of his office; yet beneath this exterior lay genuine goodness:

> In all this Monsieur Malby conducted himself with prudence and politeness, being really a very deserving man, who, under a manner as harsh as his employment, concealed a real gentleness of disposition. (006-126)

This characterization — the harsh exterior concealing a gentle nature — is one of Rousseau's more penetrating psychological observations, capturing the tension between public office and private character.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau lived in the Malby household for a period during his youth. His stay was marked by the famous incident of the stolen wine: "when, though surrounded by a number of little things that I could easily have pilfered, and which appeared no temptation, I took it into my head to covert some white Arbois wine, some glasses of which I had drank with much pleasure at their table" (006-125). Rousseau devised an elaborate scheme to steal the wine covertly, though his pleasure in the theft was diminished by his inability to share it with companions.

Rousseau recognized that he did not fit in the household and made the decision to leave on his own initiative: "Monsieur Malby saw this as clearly as myself, though I am inclined to think he would never have dismissed me had I not spared him the trouble, which was an excess of condescension in this particular, that I shall never forget" (006-127). Rousseau's acknowledgment of Malby's forbearance reflects genuine gratitude.

Years later, Rousseau was welcomed back to the Malby household: "M. and Madam de Malby seemed pleased to see me again, and several times invited me to dinner" (007-010). It was through the Malby connection that Rousseau met the Abbé de Malby, who gave him letters of introduction to Paris, including ones to Fontenelle and the Comte de Caylus (007-010).

## Significance

Monsieur Malby occupies a distinctive place in Rousseau's narrative as one of the few authority figures whom Rousseau remembers with unqualified respect. Unlike many patrons and employers in the *Confessions* who are portrayed as exploitative or indifferent, Malby emerges as genuinely decent — a man who recognized Rousseau's unsuitability for the household regimen yet never humiliated him for it, and who welcomed him back warmly after his departure. The Malby household also served as a crucial junction connecting Rousseau to the broader intellectual world of the French Enlightenment through the provost's distinguished brothers.
