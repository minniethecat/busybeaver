# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

**Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer of the Enlightenment era, whose political philosophy influenced the French Revolution and modern democratic thought. He is the author and protagonist of the _Confessions_, one of the earliest and most influential autobiographies in Western literature.

## Biography

Rousseau was born in Geneva in 1712 to a modest family of watchmakers:

> I was born at Geneva, in 1712, son of Isaac Rousseau and Susannah Bernard, citizens. My father's share of a moderate competency, which was divided among fifteen children, being very trivial, his business of a watchmaker was his only dependence. (001-004)

His mother died shortly after his birth, and his childhood was marked by both precocious reading and emotional intensity. At a young age he read romances with his father through the night:

> I sometimes find J. J. Rousseau of six years old, converse with his father on moral and affecting subjects to the great surprise of the family. (002-102)

After a peripatetic youth that took him from Geneva to Annecy, Turin, and eventually Paris, Rousseau rose to intellectual prominence in the 1750s. His name became a subject of wordplay and speculation:

> Why might not Marshal Rousseau be the same? My imagination wandering in this boundless space, soon got a fixed point. (004-070)

## Relation to Rousseau (in the Confessions)

As both author and subject of the _Confessions_, Rousseau occupies a unique dual position. In the work, he presents himself with unflinching candor:

> I mean to present my fellow-mortals with a man in all the integrity of nature; and this man shall be myself. (001-001)

He chronicles his relationships with numerous figures including [[Madame de Warens]], his benefactress and lover; [[Denis Diderot]], his fellow philosophe turned rival; [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], whose friendship gave way to enmity; and [[Madame d'Houdetot]], the great unrequited love of his life.

His self-portrayal oscillates between self-justification and self-accusation. The theft of a ribbon, unjustly blamed on a servant girl, becomes a lifelong source of guilt; his abandonment of his five children to the foundling hospital is defended with elaborate reasoning that has convinced few readers.

## Significance

Rousseau's influence on modern thought is difficult to overstate:

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| _Discourse on the Arts and Sciences_ | 1750 | First major work; won the [[Academy of Dijon]] prize |
| _Discourse on Inequality_ | 1755 | Foundational text of modern social critique |
| _Julie, or the New Heloise_ | 1761 | Bestselling novel of the 18th century |
| _The Social Contract_ | 1762 | "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains" |
| _Émile, or On Education_ | 1762 | Revolutionary treatise on education |
| _Confessions_ | 1782 (posth.) | Pioneering modern autobiography |

His musical contributions, including the opera _Le Devin du Village_ and his writings on musical notation, were also significant:

> Pressing my hand, said: "M. Rousseau, this is transporting harmony! I never heard anything more excellent." (007-136)
