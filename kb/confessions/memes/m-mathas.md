## Biography

M. Mathas was a fiscal procurer (a legal officer responsible for financial and fiscal matters) who lived near [[Montmorency]]. He was a married man with a garden, and his household included a maid-servant who Rousseau noted was also a person of sincere piety.

> Such, with my host M. Mathas, who was a good man, were my principal country acquaintance. (010-114)

Mathas was described by Rousseau in the warmest terms as a person of exceptional moral character:

> My host, M. Mathas, one of the best men in the world. (010-164)

## Relation to Rousseau

M. Mathas became Rousseau's host during a critical period when Rousseau was preparing to leave the [[Hermitage]]. Rousseau confided his resolution to depart to Mathas and his wife, trusting them as intimate friends:

> I began to prepare for sending away my effects, resolving to leave them in the open field rather than not give up the key in the course of the week: for I was determined everything should be done before a letter could be written. I had the greatest confidence in Mathas and his wife, my intimate friends, in whose garden I was shut up, and from whom I expected no treachery. (010-056)

Mathas's garden provided Rousseau with a place of retreat during this anxious period. Rousseau's trust in Mathas extended to sharing his unpublished works: he gave Mathas a copy of *[[Emile]]* before its official publication.

Mathas, in turn, lent the copy to M. de Blaire, a counsellor in the parliament of Strasbourg, who was an old acquaintance. When M. de Blaire returned the book, he reportedly expressed alarm at its contents:

> Mathas, to whom I also had given a copy before the publication, lent it to M. de Blaire, counsellor in the parliament of Strasbourg. M. de Blaire had a country-house at St. Gratien, and Mathas, his old acquaintance, sometimes went to see him there. He made him read Emilius before it was published. When he returned it to him, M. de Blaire expressed himself in the following terms, which were repeated to me the same day: "M. Mathas, this is a fine work, but it will make a great noise." (011-063)

This incident contributed to the growing controversy surrounding the work.

## Significance

M. Mathas exemplifies the type of private, non-literary friendship that Rousseau valued and contrasted with the treacherous relationships he perceived among the *philosophes*. Rousseau explicitly distinguished Mathas from his Paris literary acquaintances, placing him among the "country acquaintance" who provided genuine companionship without intellectual rivalry.

Mathas also played an unwitting role in the pre-publication controversy of *Emile* by sharing Rousseau's manuscript. His lending of the book to M. de Blaire demonstrates how Rousseau's ideas circulated — and alarmed readers — even before formal publication.
