The Abbe Morrellet (André Morellet, 1727–1819) was a French economist, philosopher, and contributor to the [[Encyclopédie]]. He was a member of the Enlightenment circle in [[Paris]] and became entangled in the literary quarrels that surrounded Rousseau and his associates.

## Biography

Morrellet was educated in theology but devoted himself primarily to philosophy, economics, and literary pursuits. He contributed articles on theology and philosophy to the Encyclopédie and published works on economic liberty. In 1760, he became involved in a literary feud when he responded to the playwright Palissot's satire *Les Philosophes*, which had mocked Rousseau, [[Denis Diderot|Diderot]], and other Enlightenment figures:

> ...turn found an avenger in the Abbe Morrellet, who wrote against Palissot a sharp pamphlet... (010-190)

Morrellet's pamphlet, *Préface de la Comédie des Philosophes*, was considered so incendiary that he was imprisoned in the Bastille — an event that drew expressions of concern from Rousseau.

## Relation to Rousseau

When Morrellet was confined, Rousseau felt genuine distress on his behalf:

> ...the pain the confinement of the Abbe Morrellet gave me. She knows my concern... (010-192)

Rousseau appealed to [[Madame de Luxembourg]] to intercede for Morrellet's release:

> ...to the enlargement of the Abbe Morrellet, she will not accept the tribute... (010-193)

However, Rousseau encountered obstacles in his efforts to help:

> ...as possible towards the Abbe Morrellet; but he finds some obstacles... (010-196)

Despite Rousseau's concern for Morrellet, the relationship later soured. Rousseau came to suspect that Morrellet had contributed to the campaign against him:

> I am far from suspecting the Abbe Morrellet of having contributed to my disgrace... (010-200)

This passage is characteristically ambiguous — Rousseau both denies and implicitly raises the suspicion, reflecting the pervasive paranoia that marked his later years in Paris.

## Significance

Morrellet exemplifies the factional nature of Enlightenment literary politics. His willingness to defend the philosophes with his pen — at the cost of his own liberty — contrasts with Rousseau's growing sense of isolation from the very circle Morrellet defended. The episode also illustrates Rousseau's complex relationship with patronage: he turned to [[Madame de Luxembourg]] for Morrellet's release, yet later felt that his own concerns were never given the same attention.
