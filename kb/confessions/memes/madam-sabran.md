## Biography

Madam Sabran was the wife of Mr. Sabran, a householder with whom the young Rousseau lodged during his travels in Book II of the *Confessions*. Little is known of her life beyond Rousseau's brief but vivid account. Rousseau describes her as "a tolerable, good sort of woman" (002-088), a phrase that suggests a modest but genuine approval.

Her most memorable characteristic, as Rousseau recalls, was her nocturnal restlessness: "more peaceable by day than by night; as I slept in the same chamber I was frequently disturbed by her wakefulness, and should have been more so had I comprehended the cause of it" (002-088). This passage is characteristic of Rousseau's youthful naivety — he sleeps in the same chamber as a married woman and remains blithely unaware of what her wakefulness might signify.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's stay with the Sabrans was associated with a pedestrian journey through mountainous terrain — an experience that left one of the most enduring pleasant impressions on his memory:

> As we were obliged to walk Madam Sabran's pace, it rather appeared an agreeable jaunt than a fatiguing journey; there still remains the most pleasing impressions of it on my mind, and the idea of a pedestrian excursion, particularly among the mountains, has from this time seemed delightful. (002-090)

The Sabran household was also the setting for a modest culinary arrangement: Rousseau notes that "at Mr. Sabran's table what I eat could scarce be missed" (002-090), suggesting both the abundance of the household and Rousseau's peripheral, unobtrusive place within it.

The episode concludes on a sour note, however. At the journey's end, Rousseau discovered he had been thoroughly robbed: "Madam Sabran found means to deprive me of everything I had, even to a ribbon embroidered with silver, with which Madam de Warens had adorned the hilt of my sword; this I regretted more than all the rest" (002-095). The loss of the ribbon — a gift from Madam de Warens — was especially painful, as it represented a sentimental attachment far exceeding its material value.

## Significance

Madam Sabran illustrates Rousseau's recurring pattern of recounting seemingly minor characters whose interactions nevertheless leave a lasting mark. She embodies a duality that runs through the *Confessions*: the simultaneous experience of genuine pleasure (the delightful mountain walk) and subsequent disillusionment (the theft). The episode is also significant for showing Rousseau's attachment to objects associated with Madam de Warens — the silver-embroidered ribbon functions as a token of his devotion, and its theft prefigures the many losses he would later endure in his relationship with her.
