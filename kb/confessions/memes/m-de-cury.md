**M. de Cury** served as Intendant des Menus-Plaisirs du Roi—the office responsible for court entertainments, ceremonies, and theatrical productions—during the reign of Louis XV. He played a consequential role in Rousseau's musical career by securing the court performance of *Le Devin du Village* at Fontainebleau in 1752, the event that brought Rousseau to the brink of a royal pension and an audience with the king.

## Biography

As Intendant des Menus, M. de Cury oversaw the administrative and logistical apparatus of court spectacle: from opera and ballet to ceremonial decorations and fireworks. His office exercised authority over which works were presented before the royal family. He was a figure of some influence in the cultural politics of Versailles, and his intervention on behalf of Rousseau's opera marked a turning point in the composer's fortunes.

## Relation to Rousseau

Cury first encountered Rousseau's work at the rehearsal of *Le Devin du Village* in Paris. The piece had created a sensation among those present, and Cury immediately recognized its suitability for the court:

> [008-116] …Everybody present was so delighted with it, that, on the next day, nothing else was spoken of in the different companies. M. de Cury, Intendant des Menus, who was present at the rehearsal, demanded the piece to have it performed at court.

A dispute ensued with Duclos, who knew Rousseau's intentions and initially refused to release the work. The conflict escalated to the point of a threatened duel before the Duke d'Aumont intervened and the piece was surrendered to the court:

> [008-116] …Duclos, who knew my intentions, and thought I should be less master of my work at the court than at Paris, refused to give it. Cury claimed it authoratively. Duclos persisted in his refusal, and the dispute between them was carried to such a length, that one day they would have gone out from the opera-house together had they not been separated.

On the day of the performance at Fontainebleau, Cury personally conducted Rousseau into the theatre:

> [008-121] …I was conducted to a box by M. de Cury, and which belonged to him. It was very spacious, upon the stage and opposite to a lesser, but more elevated one, in which the king sat with Madam de Pompadour.

After the triumphant reception of the opera, Cury conveyed the summons that would precipitate one of the most famous moments of hesitation in Rousseau's life:

> [008-124] …The same evening the Duke d' Aumont sent to desire me to be at the palace the next day at eleven o'clock, when he would present me to the king. M. de Cury, who delivered me the message, added that he thought a pension was intended, and that his majesty wished to announce it to me himself.

## Significance

M. de Cury was the administrative hinge upon which Rousseau's brief moment of court favor turned. Without his insistence—bordering on authoritarian—*Le Devin du Village* might never have reached Fontainebleau, and Rousseau would not have faced the defining choice between royal patronage and personal independence. Cury's role, though purely official, inadvertently set the stage for one of the most consequential decisions of Rousseau's life: his flight from the palace on the morning of his scheduled presentation to the king, preferring liberty and his "infirmity" to the prospect of a pension.
