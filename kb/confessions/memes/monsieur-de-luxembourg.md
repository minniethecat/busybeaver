# M. de Luxembourg

M. de Luxembourg, the Maréchal de Luxembourg, was one of the highest-ranking nobles in France and [[Rousseau]]'s most devoted patron during the Montmorency years (1757–1762). It was through the Luxembourg household that Rousseau gained both protection from his growing enemies and a social setting in which to complete some of his most important works, including _[[Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse]]_ and _[[Émile]]_. Unlike many of Rousseau's aristocratic acquaintances, the Maréchal offered genuine personal affection, and Rousseau responded with a rare trust—though the relationship would ultimately be tested by the condemnation of _Émile_ in 1762.

## Biography

Charles-François-Frédéric de Montmorency-Luxembourg (1702–1764) was a Marshal of France and a member of one of the kingdom's most illustrious noble families. He had served with distinction in the French military and enjoyed the favor of the court at Versailles.

Rousseau was introduced to the Luxembourg household through [[Madame de Luxembourg]], who had heard of the author of the _Discourse on Inequality_ and desired to meet him. The Maréchal quickly developed a warm regard for Rousseau, and the couple became his most important protectors during his years at [[Montmorency]].

M. de Luxembourg at intervals suffered from a pain in his knee, and Rousseau records with affection the daily visits he made to the Maréchal's chamber at ten o'clock, where the Maréchal would be present and "the door was open" to his friend. (010-146)

## Relation to Rousseau

The friendship between Rousseau and the Maréchal de Luxembourg was exceptional in the _Confessions_ for its apparent sincerity on both sides. Rousseau, who distrusted aristocratic patronage on principle, found in the Maréchal a man "of mild and easy character" who asked nothing in return for his protection. (011-018)

> M. de Luxembourg loves and embraces you with all his heart. (010-153)

Rousseau recorded a moment of rare emotional openness: "I once said to M. de Luxembourg, embracing him: 'Ah! Monsieur le Maréchal, I hated all great men before I knew you.'" This sentiment captures the transformative effect that the Luxembourg friendship had on Rousseau's view of the nobility. (010-164)

The Maréchal's protection was not merely social but practical. When the Parlement of Paris condemned _Émile_, it was the Luxembourg household that warned Rousseau and facilitated his flight. (010-148) Yet even this friendship had its limits: the Maréchal could not shield Rousseau from the full force of state and ecclesiastical persecution.

When the Maréchal's health declined, Rousseau's "afflictions" increased, feeling himself "still more attached to him." (011-020) The death of M. de Luxembourg in 1764 removed Rousseau's last major protector in France and contributed to his deepening sense of isolation.

## Significance

M. de Luxembourg holds a unique place in the _Confessions_:

- **Patronage without corruption**: Unlike Rousseau's relations with [[M. de Francueil]] or [[Madame d'Epinay]], the Luxembourg friendship was not transactional. The Maréchal gave without demanding.

- **The Montmorency years protected**: Without the Luxembourg shelter, Rousseau's most productive period—the years that produced _Julie_, _Émile_, and _The Social Contract_—might not have been possible.

- **Counter-example to the "conspiracy"**: In the later books of the _Confessions_, as Rousseau's paranoia about a "grand conspiracy" against him grew, the Luxembourg friendship stood as a conspicuous counter-example—a relationship in which Rousseau acknowledged that he was genuinely loved and protected.

- **Social commentary**: The Rousseau-Luxembourg bond dramatizes the _Confessions_' central theme of authenticity across class lines. The Maréchal's ability to treat a Geneva watchmaker's son as an equal was, in Rousseau's telling, a triumph of personal virtue over social convention.

> M. de Luxembourg, being and highly deserving to be, the best friend I had in the world. (010-141)
