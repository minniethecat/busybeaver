Jeanne Antoinette Poisson, the Marquise de Pompadour, was the official mistress of [[Louis XV]] and one of the most powerful figures at the French court during the 1740s and 1750s. As a patron of the arts, literature, and philosophy, she intersected with Rousseau's career at several points — first as a potential benefactor, then as an emblem of the courtly world he despised, and finally as one of those he believed had conspired against him.

## The Petition

Rousseau's most direct engagement with Madam de Pompadour came during the imprisonment of his friend and fellow musician. Rousseau took the unusual step of writing directly to the king's mistress to seek a favor.

> I wrote to Madam de Pompadour, beseeching her to release him — an act that required Rousseau to overcome his aversion to courtly petitioning, motivated by loyalty to a friend in distress. (008-045)

The fact that Rousseau, who prided himself on his independence from court patronage, was willing to petition the royal mistress reflects both the desperation of the situation and Rousseau's capacity for pragmatic action when friendship demanded it. The outcome of the petition is not recorded in the _Confessions_, but the very act of writing it placed Rousseau in uncomfortable proximity to the world of courtly intrigue he otherwise professed to despise.

## The Court Performance

Rousseau's opera _Le Devin du Village_ brought him into indirect contact with the court at [[Fontainebleau]] and, through it, with Madam de Pompadour. At a performance of the work at the Théâtre des Petits Cabinets at [[Versailles]], the king was present, seated beside Madam de Pompadour herself.

> The king sat with Madam de Pompadour at the performance — an image of royal and courtly authority that both impressed and alienated Rousseau, who attended in his characteristically disheveled dress. (008-121)

The performance earned Rousseau a substantial gratuity: "fifty louis from Madam de Pompadour, for the performance at Bellevue" (008-137). The payment placed Rousseau in the ambiguous position of being materially supported by the very court system he criticized in his writings — a tension he never fully resolved.

## The Paranoia of Persecution

In the later books of the _Confessions_, Madam de Pompadour is transformed from a distant patron into an agent of Rousseau's persecution. By Book XI, Rousseau had come to believe that a vast conspiracy had been organized against him, orchestrated by [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], [[Denis Diderot]], and their allies — and extending to the highest levels of the French court.

> Rousseau listed Madam de Pompadour among his persecutors, alongside the parliament, [[M. de Choiseul]], Grimm, and Diderot — a remarkable escalation from the petitioner who had once beseeched her favor. (011-088)

Rousseau's conviction that Madam de Pompadour harbored malevolence toward him (011-053) was entangled with his broader belief that the Jesuits wielded influence through her — she was "not upon bad terms with them" (011-048), and in Rousseau's conspiratorial logic, this placed her in the enemy camp. He had come to consider her "as a specimen" of the courtly type (011-023) — a reductive judgment that said more about Rousseau's state of mind than about Pompadour herself.

## Historical Perspective

Madam de Pompadour died in 1764, before the _Confessions_ were written, and could not respond to Rousseau's accusations. Modern historians view her as a complex figure: a woman of extraordinary political influence in a system that formally excluded women from power, a serious patron of the arts whose commissions shaped French culture, and a political operator whose alliances with ministers like Choiseul defined French foreign policy. Rousseau's characterization of her as a mere persecutor flattens this complexity into the single dimension required by his narrative of victimhood — a testament to how thoroughly the lens of paranoia could distort even Rousseau's penetrating intelligence.
