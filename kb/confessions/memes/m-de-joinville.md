**M. de Joinville** was a French diplomat who served as envoy to the Republic of Genoa. He appears in Rousseau's *Confessions* both as a benefactor—securing Rousseau's early release from quarantine—and later as a friend whose sudden coldness produced a permanent estrangement.

## Biography

M. de Joinville served as French envoy (minister) to Genoa during the period of Rousseau's employment at the French embassy in Venice. After Rousseau's return from Italy, Joinville maintained a residence in Paris, where he was connected to the office of foreign affairs. He possessed a notable personal peculiarity—a singular collection described by Rousseau:

> [010-125] This was a complete collection of songs of the court and Paris for upwards of fifty years past, in which many anecdotes were to be found that would have been sought for in vain elsewhere.

Rousseau considered Joinville "certainly an honest man, and even amiable in certain respects," though he noted that his understanding was "beneath mediocrity" and that he was "rather fond of his person and tolerably fatiguing."

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's acquaintance with Joinville began at Genoa, when Rousseau was confined to the lazaretto (quarantine station) during his return journey from Venice in 1743. Confined for what was supposed to be twenty-eight days of quarantine, Rousseau managed to send a letter to the French envoy:

> [007-068] …had not M. Joinville, envoy from France, to whom I found means to send a letter, vinegared, perfumed, and half burnt, procured eight days of the time to be taken off…

Joinville was "extremely civil," and his secretary Dupont introduced Rousseau to several Genoese families, making the remainder of his stay agreeable. The acquaintance continued after Rousseau's return to Paris:

> [010-124] This was M. de Joinville, who continued after his return from Genoa to show me much friendship.

Joinville became "so desirous of seeing me, that he in some measure laid me under constraint." The two dined together regularly, and when Joinville went to his country seat he urged Rousseau to accompany him.

The friendship ended abruptly and mysteriously. One day, Joinville received Rousseau with a coldness utterly unlike his customary manner. Despite Rousseau's attempts to discover the cause, Joinville offered no explanation:

> [010-126] …after having given him an opportunity to explain, and even having begged him to do it, I left his house with a resolution, in which I have persevered, never to return to it again…

Rousseau conjectured that the cause might have been some misunderstanding connected with a supper at the apartment of "some girls of his acquaintance," but he never received confirmation. The rupture was permanent—though Joinville later made overtures of reconciliation, Rousseau's pride prevented a renewal of the friendship.

## Significance

The Joinville episode embodies a recurring theme in Rousseau's autobiographical narrative: the inexplicable dissolution of friendships. Rousseau's inability to discern what had caused Joinville's coldness, and his refusal to press for an explanation when the first attempt failed, exemplify the combination of sensitivity and stubbornness that characterized his social relations. The episode also demonstrates Rousseau's maxim of "never speaking but in an honorable manner of the houses I frequented"—even in estrangement, he refrained from maligning his former friend.
