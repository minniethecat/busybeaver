## Biography

Jean-Jacques Dortous de Mairan was a distinguished French scientist and member of the Academy of Sciences.
Born in 1678, he was a generation older than Rousseau and had established himself as a prominent figure in the French scientific establishment by the time Rousseau arrived in Paris.

De Mairan was best known for his work in geophysics and his studies of the aurora borealis, but his intellectual range extended across the sciences and into the republic of letters.

## Relation to Rousseau

### Examining the Musical Notation System

When Rousseau presented his new system of musical notation to the Academy of Sciences on August 22, 1742, de Mairan was one of the three academicians appointed to examine it: "The persons appointed to examine my system were M. Mairan, M. Hellot, and M. de Fouchy, all three men of merit."
Rousseau added a characteristic reservation: "but not one of them understood music, at least not enough of composition to enable them to judge of my project." (007-033)

Despite Rousseau's doubts about their musical qualifications, the examination was conducted seriously, and de Mairan's participation placed the young Genevan's work before one of the leading scientific minds in France.

### Defending the Discourse on Inequality

More than a decade later, de Mairan reappeared in Rousseau's narrative as a public supporter.
When Rousseau's *Discourse on the Inequality of Mankind* was published, dedicated to the Republic of Geneva, the reception in Geneva was cool: "I did not perceive that a single Genevese was pleased with the hearty zeal found in the work."

At a dinner at Madam Dupin's house in Clichy, with the Genevan resident Crommelin present, "M. de Mairan, the latter openly declared the council owed me a present and public honors for the work, and that it would dishonor itself if it failed in either."
Crommelin "dared not reply in my presence, but he made a frightful grimace, which however forced a smile from Madam Dupin." (009-022)

This episode reveals de Mairan as a man willing to speak candidly about political matters, defending Rousseau's work before a representative of the very republic that had received it coldly.

### The Journal des Savans Circle

Later, when M. de Margency offered Rousseau a position at the *Journal des Savans*, the post would have brought Rousseau into a society of "men of letters of the first merit; M. de Mairan, Clairaut, De Guignes and the Abbe Barthelemi."
Rousseau noted that he had "already made an acquaintance" with de Mairan and Clairaut, and that acquaintance with the other two "was very desirable." (010-131)

Rousseau's decision to decline the position — driven by his conviction that he could only write "from the warmth of imagination" — meant he never joined de Mairan as a colleague at the Journal. (010-131)

### The Montmorency Years

De Mairan was among the acquaintances Rousseau continued to see occasionally during his years at Montmorency.
Rousseau listed him among "MM. de Mairan, de Lalive, De Boisgelou, Vatelet, Ancelet, and others" whom he "sometimes saw in the country, either at my own house or that of some neighbor." (010-127)

## Significance

De Mairan represents the segment of the French scientific establishment that treated Rousseau with genuine respect, both as a thinker and as a man.
Unlike the *philosophes* who eventually became Rousseau's enemies, de Mairan appears to have maintained a cordial, disinterested relationship with him over many years.

His public defense of the *Discourse on Inequality* is particularly notable: as a respected academician, his words carried weight, and his willingness to criticize the Genevan council's indifference in front of its representative showed both independence of mind and a sincere appreciation of Rousseau's work.

De Mairan also exemplifies the path not taken. Had Rousseau accepted the *Journal des Savans* position, he would have worked alongside de Mairan and entered the French academic establishment through its front door — a trajectory radically different from the solitary, persecuted path he ultimately followed.
