## Biography

M. Lambercier was a Protestant minister residing at [[Bossey]], near [[Geneva]]. Together with his sister, Miss Lambercier, he ran a boarding establishment where young Jean-Jacques Rousseau and his cousin were sent to receive their education.

> I remained under the tuition of my uncle Bernard, who was at that time employed in the fortifications of Geneva. He had lost his eldest daughter, but had a son about my own age, and we were sent together to Bossey, to board with the Minister Lambercier. Here we were to learn Latin, with all the insignificant trash that has obtained the name of education. (001-054)

Rousseau described Lambercier as an exemplary educator:

> M. Lambercier was a worthy, sensible man, who, without neglecting our instruction, never made our acquisitions burthensome, or tasks tedious. What convinces me of the rectitude of his method is, that notwithstanding my extreme aversion to restraint, the recollection of my studies is never attended with disgust. (001-056)

## Relation to Rousseau

The Lambercier household was the setting for some of the most formative experiences of Rousseau's childhood. Rousseau and his cousin boarded there, and the daily life of the household — including its discipline, affections, and tensions — left a profound imprint on the young boy.

The household included Miss Lambercier, who exercised a maternal authority over the children:

> As Miss Lambercier felt a mother's affection, she sometimes exerted a mother's authority, even to inflicting on us when we deserved it, the punishment of infants. (001-061)

A pivotal incident occurred in the Lambercier household: the broken comb episode. When a maid's combs were found damaged, Rousseau was wrongly accused:

> One day, while I was studying in a chamber contiguous to the kitchen, the maid set some of Miss Lambercier's combs to dry by the fire, and on coming to fetch them some time after, was surprised to find the teeth of one of them broken off. Who could be suspected of this mischief? No one but myself had entered the room: I was questioned, but denied having any knowledge of it. (001-071)

This false accusation marked Rousseau deeply. He later reflected on how this experience of injustice at the hands of those he loved and respected contributed to his lifelong sensitivity to unfairness:

> Affection, respect, intimacy, confidence, no longer attached the pupils to their guides; we beheld them no longer as divinities, who could read the secrets of our hearts; we were less ashamed of committing faults, more afraid of being accused of them: we learned to dissemble, to rebel, to lie. (001-078)

## Significance

The Lambercier household represents a crucial chapter in Rousseau's autobiographical narrative and his philosophical thought. The two years at Bossey provided raw material for some of his most influential ideas about childhood, education, and moral psychology.

Rousseau's experiences with M. and Miss Lambercier directly informed his educational philosophy in *[[Emile]]*, particularly his beliefs about the importance of gentle, non-coercive education and the psychological damage caused by unjust punishment. The contrast between the "worthy, sensible" M. Lambercier and the traumatic injustice of the comb incident illustrates Rousseau's nuanced view that even well-intentioned authority figures can cause lasting harm through failures of trust.

The pastoral setting of Bossey, with its walnut tree on the terrace and the surrounding countryside, also contributed to Rousseau's lifelong association of nature with moral purity and childhood innocence — a theme that runs throughout his major works.
