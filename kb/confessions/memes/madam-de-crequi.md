**Madam de Créqui** (the Marchioness of Créqui) was a member of the French aristocracy who became a steadfast friend to Rousseau during his early years in Paris. She was the niece of M. le Bailli de Froulay, ambassador from Malta, and maintained a salon frequented by men of letters. Among Rousseau's shifting circle of acquaintances, she stands out as someone whose loyalty did not waver when his friendships with the philosophes soured.

## Biography

Madam de Créqui was connected by family to the diplomatic world: her uncle, M. le Bailli de Froulay, served as ambassador from Malta, and her brother had preceded M. de Montaigu in the embassy to Venice. She moved in the Parisian literary world and hosted a salon where Rousseau encountered figures such as M. Saurin, author of *Spartacus* and *Barnevelt*. In her later years she became devout and withdrew from the company of most men of letters, though she preserved her friendship with Rousseau.

## Relation to Rousseau

Madam de Créqui sought Rousseau's acquaintance during the period of his first literary successes in Paris, and unlike many whose curiosity was fleeting, she became durably attached to him:

> [008-102] …this was the Marchioness of Crequi, niece to M. le Bailli de Froulay, ambassador from Malta, whose brother had preceded M. de Montaigu in the embassy to Venice, and whom I had gone to see on my return from that city. Madam de Crequi wrote to me: I visited her: she received me into her friendship.

Through her, Rousseau gained access to unpublished manuscripts that shaped his evaluation of the Abbé de Saint-Pierre's political thought:

> [009-088] …I confirmed myself by these moral writings in the idea I had conceived from some of his letters, shown me by Madam de Crequi, that he had more sense and ingenuity than at first I had imagined.

When Grimm systematically absorbed Rousseau's friends, Madam de Créqui alone held firm. She refused to admit Grimm into her company, and Rousseau's loyalty to her cost him contact with the broader Grimm circle:

> [009-214] …I had given to Grimm all my friends without exception, they were become his. I was so inseparable from him, that I should have had some difficulty in continuing to visit at a house where he was not received. Madam de Crequi was the only person who refused to admit him into her company, and whom for that reason I have seldom since seen.

Even in her later years, when she had become devout and closed her doors to D'Alembert, Marmontel, and nearly every other man of letters, she maintained warm relations with Rousseau:

> [010-117] …I had likewise for a friend Madam de Crequi, who, having become devout, no longer received D'Alembert, Marmontel, nor a single man of letters, except, I believe the Abbe Trublet, half a hypocrite, of whom she was weary. I, whose acquaintance she had sought, lost neither her good wishes nor intercourse. She sent me young fat pullets from Mons, and her intention was to come and see me the year following.

## Significance

Madam de Créqui represents a rare constant in Rousseau's Parisian social life. At a time when his relationships with the Encyclopédistes were deteriorating—and when Grimm was methodically drawing his friends away—she was the sole member of his circle who explicitly refused to receive Grimm and thereby preserved an independent friendship with Rousseau. Her later religious turn, which led her to exclude the philosophes while keeping Rousseau in her affections, underscores the distinct character of Rousseau's position: neither fully of the Enlightenment camp nor of the devout party, yet capable of being claimed as a friend by individuals on both sides.
