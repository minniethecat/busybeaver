## Biography

M. de Gauffecourt was the son of a clock-maker from Geneva, and would have followed his father's trade had not his personal merits and connections elevated him to a higher station. (006-008) Through the patronage of M. de la Closure, the French Resident at Geneva, he obtained connections in Paris and secured the privilege of furnishing the salt provisions for the Valais region, a position that brought him considerable wealth and standing. (006-008)

Rousseau describes Gauffecourt as "one of the most amiable men that ever existed," noting that it was "impossible to see him without loving him, or to live with him without feeling a sincere attachment." (006-007)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's acquaintance with Gauffecourt dates from his early years, and their friendship endured for decades. Despite "every effort to disunite us," as Rousseau notes, Gauffecourt remained his steadfast friend. Rousseau writes poignantly: "his affection terminated only with his life — death alone could put a period to our friendship." (006-007)

Gauffecourt rendered Rousseau several important services. When Rousseau's father died at about sixty years of age, it was Gauffecourt who assisted him in financial matters, helping Rousseau claim what remained of his mother's property. (008-016)

In 1749, Gauffecourt proposed a journey to Geneva, which Rousseau accepted. They travelled together with Thérèse in a private carriage. (009-015) During this journey, Rousseau experienced for the first time at age forty-two a "diminution of my natural confidence" when Thérèse showed uneasiness at being left alone in the carriage with Gauffecourt. (009-016)

Gauffecourt also helped Rousseau understand social dynamics in Paris, explaining why certain ladies held an aversion to him — attributing one cause to Madame de la Poplinière's friendship for Rameau, who would not "suffer a competitor." (008-015)

## Role in Rousseau's Circle

Gauffecourt was a connecting figure in Rousseau's social network. He introduced Rousseau to Madam de Chenonceaux (008-097) and was acquainted with M. de la Poplinière, to whom he had recommended Rousseau. (007-136) He nicknamed Thérèse's mother and her associates "the governesses," observing their influence over Rousseau's household with wry amusement. (008-094)

## Later Years

At Lyons, Rousseau parted from Gauffecourt to take the road to Savoy. (009-017) Rousseau later arranged matters so that he would "not again have to travel with Gauffecourt." (009-022) Despite this cooling, their friendship survived. Rousseau records that he was among the first to run to "poor Gauffecourt" when the latter suffered a stroke of apoplexy. (009-194)

## Significance

M. de Gauffecourt represents the Genevese connection in Rousseau's Parisian life — a childhood friend who had risen through merit and patronage, and who remained loyal despite the complications of Rousseau's increasingly troubled social relationships. His story illustrates the network of Genevese expatriates in Paris who supported one another, and the complex interplay of friendship, obligation, and independence that characterized Rousseau's personal relationships.
