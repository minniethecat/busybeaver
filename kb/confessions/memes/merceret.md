## Biography

Merceret was a young chambermaid from Fribourg who served in Madame de Warens's household at Annecy. She was part of the small household staff that included Claude Anet, a valet, a cook, and two hired chairmen (003-035).

Rousseau described her as "something older than myself, not pretty, but tolerably agreeable; good-natured, free from malice, having no fault to my knowledge but being a little refractory with her mistress" (004-005). She had some knowledge of music and occasionally sang with Rousseau under Le Maitre's direction.

> Mademoiselle Merceret, Madam de Warens' chambermaid, knew something of music; I shall never forget a little piece that M. le Maitre made me sing with her, and which her mistress listened to with great satisfaction. (003-075)

When Madame de Warens departed for Paris, leaving Merceret behind, the chambermaid eventually decided to return to her family in Fribourg. A friend, Mademoiselle Giraud, proposed that Rousseau escort her—an arrangement that Merceret eagerly accepted.

> As I happened to be agreeable to little Merceret, she approved the idea, and the same day they mentioned it to me as a fixed point. Finding nothing displeasing in the manner they had disposed of me, I consented, thinking it could not be above a week's journey at most. (004-031)

## Relation to Rousseau

Merceret developed a fondness for Rousseau during their shared time in Madame de Warens's household. After her mistress's departure, she and Rousseau remained in contact, and he often visited her.

> I often went to see her; she was an old acquaintance, who recalled to my remembrance one more beloved, and this made her dear to me. (004-005)

On the journey to Fribourg, Merceret—though shy—showed clear signs of romantic interest. "Merceret, younger and less artful than Giraud, never made me so many advances, but she imitated my manners, my actions, repeated my words, and showed me all those little attentions I ought to have had for her" (004-032). She arranged for them to share a chamber during the journey, a circumstance that "usually produces some consequences between a lad of twenty and a girl of twenty-five" (004-032).

However, Rousseau was too absorbed in thoughts of Madame de Warens to reciprocate. "Though Merceret was by no means a disagreeable person, I never felt the least inclination to say anything tender to her" (004-033). After reaching Fribourg, Rousseau's connection with Merceret faded, and her politeness "rather diminished" (004-036).

## Significance

Merceret represents a recurring phenomenon in Rousseau's early life: young women who showed him affection and attention, but whom he could not love because his emotional world was dominated by his attachment to Madame de Warens. Her presence in the narrative also provides a glimpse into the household economy of Annecy's minor gentry—where a chambermaid, a valet, and a cook constituted a modest establishment.

The journey to Fribourg episode further illustrates Rousseau's tendency to drift passively into situations arranged by others, a pattern of agreeable compliance that repeatedly shaped the course of his young adulthood.
