## Biography

Madame de Vulson (referred to as "Miss Vulson" or "Mademoiselle de Vulson" in the *Confessions*) was a young Genevan woman who was among Rousseau's earliest romantic attachments. She appears in Book II of the *Confessions* during Rousseau's account of his boyhood in Geneva. Rousseau describes her as a lady who showed him particular kindness: "A Madame de Vulson, in particular, loaded me with every caress" (002-001). Their relationship was characterized by a chivalric devotion on Rousseau's part and a tender, serene affection.

## Relation to Rousseau

Madame de Vulson was the object of one of Rousseau's first loves, which he distinguishes from his simultaneous and more passionate infatuation with Mademoiselle Goton. He describes his feelings for Vulson as calm but deep:

> The pleasure I felt on approaching Miss Vulson was sufficiently ardent, though unattended with uneasy sensations. (002-005)

Rousseau's attachment manifested in possessive jealousy: "I so publically and tyrannically claimed Miss Vulson, that I could not suffer any other of my sex to approach her" (002-002). Yet the relationship was fundamentally innocent and sentimental:

> I could have passed my whole life with Miss Vulson, without forming a wish to quit her; but then, my satisfaction was attended with a pleasing serenity. (002-004)

They conducted a tender correspondence that left a lasting impression:

> Though my correspondence with Miss Vulson was less animated, it was perhaps more endearing; we never separated without tears, and it can hardly be conceived what a void I felt in my heart. (002-006)

The romance ended in betrayal — Rousseau believed Vulson had been unfaithful — and he swore "never more to see the perfidious girl" (002-009). Yet many years later, during a visit to his father, Rousseau encountered her again on Lake Geneva. When he asked who the ladies in a nearby boat were, his father replied: "What!... does not your heart inform you? It is your former flame, Miss Vulson" (002-009). She had married, and the encounter was a poignant reminder of his lost youth.

## Significance

Madame de Vulson serves as the archetype of Rousseau's early sentimental attachments — relationships defined more by imagination and sensibility than by physical desire. The contrast Rousseau draws between his feelings for Vulson (serene, endearing) and those for Goton (furious, overwhelming) establishes a taxonomy of love that recurs throughout the *Confessions*. Vulson's reappearance decades later on Lake Geneva also illustrates one of Rousseau's central themes: the persistence of early emotional experiences and the melancholy of recognizing that the people and passions of youth have been transformed by time.
