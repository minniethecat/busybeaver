The **Abbé Raynal** (Guillaume Thomas François Raynal, 1713–1796) was a French Catholic clergyman, writer, and prominent figure of the Enlightenment. He is best known as the author of the *Histoire des deux Indes*, a multivolume history of European colonialism that became one of the most widely read and controversial works of the late eighteenth century. In Rousseau's *Confessions*, Raynal appears as one of the few men of letters who offered genuine friendship and material assistance without ulterior motive.

## Biography

Raynal was ordained as a priest and taught at the Collège de Clermont in Paris before turning to writing and editorial work. He contributed to the *Mercure de France* and moved in the circles of the Encyclopédistes. His major work, the *Histoire philosophique et politique des établissements et du commerce des Européens dans les deux Indes*, first published in 1770, offered a sweeping critique of European colonialism, slavery, and religious intolerance. The work was condemned by the French authorities and placed on the Index of Prohibited Books, forcing Raynal into exile.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau knew Raynal before Grimm did, and he retained a high regard for him based on personal experience of the abbé's character. While Grimm's other connections offered Rousseau no friendship, Raynal proved himself an exception:

> [008-097] …I except the Abbe Raynal, who, although his friend, gave proofs of his being mine; and in cases of need, offered me his purse with a generosity not very common. But I knew the Abbe Raynal long before Grimm had any acquaintance with him, and had entertained a great regard for him on account of his delicate and honorable behavior to me upon a slight occasion, which I shall never forget.

Raynal's warmth of character was further demonstrated during the bizarre episode of Grimm's lethargic illness. When Grimm fell into a catatonic state after being rejected by Mademoiselle Fel, Raynal and Rousseau shared the burden of watching over him day and night:

> [008-098] …The Abbe Raynal is certainly a warm friend; of this I saw a proof, much about the time of which I speak, with respect to Grimm himself, with whom he was very intimate. …The Abbe Raynal and myself watched over him; the abbe, more robust, and in better health than I was, by night, and I by day, without ever both being absent at one time.

Raynal was also among the party that accompanied Rousseau to Fontainebleau for the rehearsal of *Le Devin du Village*:

> [008-118] …I went with Mademoiselle Fel, Grimm, and I think the Abbe Raynal, in one of the stages to the court.

## Significance

Raynal occupies a distinctive position in Rousseau's Parisian social landscape. Unlike most of the philosophes who became his antagonists, Raynal managed to remain on good terms with both Grimm and Rousseau during the period when their friendship was unravelling. The purse he offered Rousseau "with a generosity not very common" suggests that Raynal recognized the precariousness of Rousseau's financial situation as a music copyist attempting to move in literary circles. His later fame as the author of the *Histoire des deux Indes* would make him one of the most celebrated—and persecuted—figures of the late Enlightenment.
