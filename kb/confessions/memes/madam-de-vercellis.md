## Biography

The Countess of Vercellis was a middle-aged widow without children, of noble appearance and cultivated understanding. Rousseau, who served in her household, noted that her husband was Piedmontese but suspected she herself was Savoyard, observing that "a native of Piedmont could speak such good French, and with so pure an accent" (002-141).

She was deeply fond of French literature and well versed in it. Rousseau described her letters as possessing "the expression, and almost the elegance of Madam de Savigne's" (002-141). During Rousseau's time in her service, she suffered from breast cancer, which prevented her from writing herself; Rousseau's primary duty was to write from her dictation.

Madam de Vercellis possessed not only a cultivated mind but also what Rousseau called "a strong and elevated soul":

> Madam de Vercellis not only possessed a good understanding, but a strong and elevated soul. I was with her during her last illness, and saw her suffer and die, without showing an instant of weakness, or the least effort of constraint; still retaining her feminine manners, without entertaining an idea that such fortitude gave her any claim to philosophy; a word which was not yet in fashion, nor comprehended by her in the sense it is held at present. (002-142)

This strength of character, however, extended to a certain emotional distance. Rousseau observed that she appeared "to feel as little for others as herself," and when she relieved the unfortunate, "it was rather for the sake of acting right, than from a principle of real commiseration" (002-142).

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau entered the service of Madam de Vercellis after leaving his previous situation in Turin. His main employment — "by no means displeasing" to him — was to write from her dictation (002-141). He remained with her for approximately three months, during her final illness.

Their relationship was marked by coldness and distance. Rousseau recalled that she "never addressed a word to me which seemed to express affection, pity, or benevolence" (002-144). He believed she judged him not by what he truly was but by the subordinate position she had placed him in:

> She drew her judgment less from what I really was, than from what she had made me, and by considering me as a footman prevented my appearing otherwise. (002-144)

Rousseau suspected that the countess's domestics — particularly a certain M. Lorenzy, his wife, and their niece Mademoiselle Pontal — deliberately kept him from her presence, fearing that she might recognize his abilities and reduce their own share of any inheritance. He wrote that "for eight days before she made her will, I had not been permitted to enter the chamber" (002-145). Despite this, Rousseau wept for her sincerely, "being afflicted at the sufferings of the unhappy lady, whom I truly respected and beloved for the calmness and fortitude with which she bore her illness" (002-145).

When she died, Madam de Vercellis did nothing for Rousseau — a fact he attributed either to her judgment that he was unworthy, or to the self-interested maneuvering of those around her (002-142).

### The Stolen Ribbon

The most consequential event of Rousseau's time in the Vercellis household was the theft of a ribbon, which became one of the defining moral crises of his life. A young Mauriennese cook named Marion was accused, and Rousseau — to his lasting shame — falsely accused her:

> Marion was not only pretty, but had that freshness of color only to be found among the mountains, and, above all, an air of modesty and sweetness, which made it impossible to see her without affection; she was besides a good girl, virtuous, and of such strict fidelity, that everyone was surprised at hearing her named. (002-150)

Rousseau himself was the thief. When the ribbon was discovered missing and Marion was brought before the household, Rousseau "with infernal impudence" confirmed his false accusation. Marion, "bursting into tears," said: "Ah, Rousseau! I thought you a good disposition — you render me very unhappy, but I would not be in your situation" (002-150). The Count de la Roque, Madam de Vercellis's nephew and heir, dismissed them both with the words: "The conscience of the guilty would revenge the innocent" (002-150).

## Significance

Madam de Vercellis occupies a small but morally pivotal place in Rousseau's *Confessions*. Her household was the setting for the stolen ribbon episode — an event Rousseau carried as "an insupportable weight of remorse which yet hangs on my conscience, and whose bitter recollection, far from weakening, during a period of forty years, seems to gather strength as I grow old" (002-148). Rousseau feared that his false accusation may have caused Marion "to perish with shame and misery" (002-148).

Beyond the ribbon incident, Madam de Vercellis represents one of Rousseau's early encounters with the French aristocracy — an encounter that disappointed him. Her emotional coldness toward him, and her failure to recognize his abilities, prefigured a pattern of frustrated relationships with patrons and benefactors that would recur throughout his life. Her household also introduced Rousseau to the dynamics of servant intrigue, which he came to see as a microcosm of the social manipulation he would later critique in his philosophical works.

After leaving her service, Rousseau retained some acquaintances made there, including the Savoyard abbé [[M. Gaime]], from whom he "reaped advantages far more precious" — lessons of morality and sound judgment that served him throughout his life (003-002).
