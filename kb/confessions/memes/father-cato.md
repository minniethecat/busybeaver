**Father Cato** was a Cordelier (Franciscan) monk who became a close companion to [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] and [[Madame de Warens]] during Rousseau's youthful years in Annecy. A man of polished manners, musical talent, and worldly sophistication, Cato was beloved in good society but ultimately destroyed by the jealousy of his fellow monks, who could not tolerate his superiority.

## Biography

Father Cato was a Bachelor of the Sorbonne who had spent considerable time in Paris among the great world. He was particularly favored by the Marquis d'Antremont, then Ambassador from Sardinia. (005-046) Rousseau described him in vivid detail: "He was tall and well made; full faced, with very fine eyes, and black hair, which formed natural curls on each side of his forehead. His manner was at once noble, open, and modest." (005-046)

Cato first appears in the *Confessions* in connection with the unfortunate Le Maitre. When Rousseau and [[Le Maitre]] arrived in Lyons, Le Maitre visited Cato and the Abbé Dortan, "both of whom received him well, but afterwards betrayed him." (003-087) Together with the Count d'Ortan, Cato "had caused the music of poor Le Maitre to be seized at Lyons; which action was far from being the brightest trait in his history." (005-046)

Despite this moral blemish, Rousseau's portrait of Cato is overwhelmingly affectionate. He noted that Cato was not deeply learned but excelled as a man of the world: "Having lived much in the world, he had rather attached himself to agreeable acquirements than to solid learning; had sense, made verses, spoke well, sang better, and aided his good voice by playing on the organ and harpsichord." (005-046)

## Relation to Rousseau

Father Cato became acquainted with Madame de Warens at the Marquis d'Antremont's residence. Learning of her musical gatherings, he "wished to assist at them, and by his company rendered our meetings truly agreeable." (005-047) He quickly formed a bond with Rousseau through their "mutual taste for music, which in both was a most lively passion, with this difference, that he was really a musician, and myself a bungler." (005-047)

The musical evenings at Cato's apartment were among Rousseau's fondest memories of his Annecy years. Cato would join the concerts organized by Madame de Warens, singing alongside her while Rousseau conducted. (005-045) Rousseau recalled these evenings with rapture: "Father Cato was charming, Madame de Warens adorable, and the Abbe Palais, with his rough voice, was the butt of the company. Pleasing moments of sportive youth, how long since have ye fled!" (005-047)

Remarkably for a monk, Cato was "generous, profuse, and loved good cheer, without the least tincture of greediness." (005-047) After concerts, he would stay to supper, and the company conversed with "the greatest gayety and good-humor."

## Tragic End

Father Cato's very merits became his undoing. His brother monks, "jealous, or rather exasperated to discover in him a merit and elegance of manners which favored nothing of monastic stupidity, conceived the most violent hatred to him, because he was not as despicable as themselves." (005-048) The monastic superiors "combined against this worthy man" and incited the envious rank-and-file to attack.

Cato suffered "a thousand indignities": he was degraded from his office, stripped of his elegantly furnished apartment, and eventually banished. Rousseau concluded: "In short, these wretches overwhelmed him with so many evils, that his honest and proud soul sank under the pressure, and, after having been the delight of the most amiable societies, he died of grief, on a wretched bed, hid in some cell or dungeon." (005-048)

## Significance

Father Cato embodies a recurring theme in the *Confessions*: the destruction of a superior individual by the mediocrity and envy of the many. His story also illuminates Rousseau's early musical world in Annecy—a period of happiness he would recall throughout his life. Despite Cato's role in the betrayal of Le Maitre, Rousseau's final verdict on him is generous: he was "lamented by all worthy people of his acquaintance, who could find no fault in him, except his being a monk." (005-048)
