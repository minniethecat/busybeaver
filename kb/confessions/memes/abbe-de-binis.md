The **Abbé de Binis** was a young cleric who served under the French ambassador [[M. de Montaigu]] in Venice. He worked as a subordinate writer in the embassy and developed a friendly relationship with [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] during Rousseau's tenure as secretary to the ambassador. Binis was described by Rousseau as "a good creature" and proved to be a loyal ally, particularly when he warned Rousseau of the ambassador's attempt to have him arrested.

## Biography

The Abbé de Binis was already in the service of the French embassy in Venice when Rousseau arrived in 1743 to take up the post of secretary to the ambassador, M. de Montaigu. At that time, Binis "wrote under the secretary, and was unfit to succeed him." (007-063) His position was subordinate, handling clerical and writing tasks within the embassy.

Despite his modest rank, Binis appears to have been well-liked. Rousseau shared with him a portion of the proceeds from passport fees—a gesture Binis had not expected and did not claim as a right. (007-070) The two lived together "on the best of terms" throughout Rousseau's stay in Venice.

Binis was also present at the ambassador's private table, eating alongside Rousseau and the ambassador's pages in conditions that Rousseau described as worse than "the most paltry ale-house." (007-086)

## Relation to Rousseau

Binis played a significant role during the final crisis of Rousseau's employment with Montaigu. The ambassador had submitted a memoir to the Venetian senate seeking Rousseau's arrest after a dispute. It was Binis who informed Rousseau of this dangerous development: "On being informed of this by the Abbe de Binis, I resolved to remain a fortnight longer, instead of setting off the next day as I had intended." (007-092)

This warning allowed Rousseau to act strategically. His conduct had been "known and approved of by everybody," and the senate refused to act on Montaigu's memoir, instead assuring Rousseau he could remain in Venice as long as he wished.

Earlier, Montaigu had also employed Binis to write private letters—including one to [[M. de Maurepas]] concerning the affair of Captain Olivet—in which Montaigu deliberately omitted Rousseau's contributions. (007-090) Binis's role in this matter highlights both his position as a trusted clerk and the ambassador's tendency to sideline Rousseau.

## Significance

The Abbé de Binis represents one of the few figures in Rousseau's Venetian episode who treated him with consistent kindness. While Rousseau's tenure in Venice was marked by conflict with Montaigu and financial hardship, his relationship with Binis remained cordial throughout. Binis's warning about the arrest warrant was a crucial act of loyalty that gave Rousseau the time and knowledge to defend himself against the ambassador's machinations.
