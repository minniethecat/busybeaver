# Madame d'Épinay

**Madame d'Épinay** (1726–1783), born Louise-Florence-Pétronille Tardieu d'Esclavelles, was a French writer, salonnière, and a significant patron of [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] during the 1750s. She is best known in the _Confessions_ as the friend who offered Rousseau the Hermitage, a small house on her estate at Montmorency, where he lived from 1756 to 1757.

## Biography

Born into the minor nobility, she married M. d'Épinay, son of a farmer-general. Rousseau describes his first acquaintance with her:

> I here mark that of my first acquaintance with Madam D'Epinay, whose name will frequently appear in these memoirs. She was a Mademoiselle D'Esclavelles, and had lately been married to M. D'Epinay, son of M. de Lalive de Bellegarde, a farmer general. (008-038)

Her circle included prominent Enlightenment figures. [[Denis Diderot]], [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], and the [[Baron d'Holbach]] were frequent guests at her gatherings. She was also an accomplished writer, though her major works — including _Histoire de Madame de Montbrillant_ — were published posthumously.

> She was amiable, had wit and talent, and was by no means deficient in understanding; but her heart was dry, and her disposition, far from being one of the best. (008-040)

## Relation to Rousseau

Madame d'Épinay played a pivotal role in Rousseau's life between 1755 and 1757. She offered him the Hermitage, a small country house on the grounds of her estate at La Chevrette, near Saint-Denis:

> This solitary and very agreeable place had been, from time immemorial, called the Hermitage. The situation was romantic, and the building, which had fallen much out of repair, I thought interesting. (009-023)

Rousseau moved into the Hermitage in April 1756 with [[Thérèse Levasseur]] and her mother. This arrangement, while generous, created a dynamic of dependence that Rousseau came to question:

> I felt, as I have remarked in my Emilius, that unless an author be a man of intrigue, when he endeavors to render his works of any utility to his country, it matters not where he composes them, provided they are read. (009-054)

Their relationship soured over several issues: Rousseau's growing attachment to her sister-in-law [[Madame d'Houdetot]], Grimm's influence over her household, and Rousseau's perception that she was controlling and manipulative. Their final rupture in 1757 was one of the great emotional crises recounted in the _Confessions_.

## Significance

Madame d'Épinay's significance extends beyond her role in Rousseau's biography to her place in the French Enlightenment:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Literary work | _Histoire de Madame de Montbrillant_ (published posthumously), extensive correspondence |
| Circle | Diderot, Grimm, d'Holbach, Gauffecourt, Saint-Lambert |
| Patronage | Provided the Hermitage to Rousseau (1756–1757) |
| Legacy | Her correspondence with Galiani and others offers valuable insight into Enlightenment society |

Her own writings, especially the autobiographical novel _Histoire de Madame de Montbrillant_, offer a counterpoint to Rousseau's depiction of her. Where Rousseau portrays her as manipulative, her own work reveals a woman constrained by the limitations placed on her sex in eighteenth-century society.
