## Biography

The Count de Gauvon was Master of the Horse to the Queen of Sardinia and chief of the illustrious House of Solar. (003-008) An old and dignified nobleman, he was the father of the Marquis de Breil (then ambassador at Vienna), the Abbé de Gauvon, and Mademoiselle de Breil. (003-009) (003-013)

Rousseau describes him as "this respectable old man," noting how the "air of dignity conspicuous" in him was tempered by an affability that made his reception of the young Rousseau all the more memorable. (003-008)

## Relation to Rousseau

The Count de Gauvon first encountered Rousseau when the latter was presented to him in Turin, shortly after Rousseau's conversion to Catholicism. The Count questioned Rousseau "with evident interest," and Rousseau replied with sincerity. The Count remarked to another nobleman that Rousseau's "features were agreeable" and promised that the young man's story "did not end there." (003-008)

Though initially Rousseau's "trifling talents" attracted little attention in the household, the Count eventually took notice of him. After an evening entertainment, he had a half-hour conversation with Rousseau that "appeared to satisfy him, and absolutely enchanted" the young man. (003-012) Rousseau reflected that the Count "had less sense than Madam de Vercellis, but possessed more feeling." (003-012)

## The House of Solar's Plan

Rousseau later inferred, from words "dropped at random," that the House of Solar harbored ambitions in diplomacy and perhaps the ministry. They sought "a person of merit and talents, who depending entirely on them, might obtain their confidence, and be of essential service." (003-015) The Count's project for Rousseau was "judicious, magnanimous, and truly worthy of a great nobleman." (003-015) Had Rousseau followed this path, he might have risen through the ranks of diplomacy — a career he later recalled with some regret. (007-120)

## Family

The Count's younger son, the Abbé de Gauvon, was "designed by his family for a bishopric" and had studied at the University of Sienna. (003-013) The Abbé taught Rousseau to "read less eagerly" and to moderate his headlong approach to scholarship. (003-047) He also made Rousseau a present of "a very pretty heron fountain" which became central to Rousseau's ill-fated departure from Turin. (003-024)

The Count's granddaughter, Mademoiselle de Breil, was "about my own age, tolerably handsome," with a combination of dark hair and fair complexion that Rousseau found irresistible. (003-010)

## Significance

The Count de Gauvon represents a crucial turning point in Rousseau's youth — a moment when patronage by a powerful noble house might have set him on a conventional path to success. Rousseau's abandonment of this opportunity, choosing instead to wander with his friend Bacle and a toy fountain, exemplifies his lifelong pattern of rejecting security in favor of freedom. The Count's household also introduced Rousseau to the complexities of aristocratic society, which he would later both navigate and critique.
