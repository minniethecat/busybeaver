## Biography

M. Roguin was a resident of [[Yverdon]], a town in the Swiss canton of Vaud, where he had retired several years before Rousseau's exile from France. He was a man of considerable standing in the local community, connected by family ties to several other figures who became important in Rousseau's life, including his niece Madame Boy de la Tour and Colonel Roguin.

Rousseau consistently referred to Roguin with exceptional warmth, calling him "my old friend," "my old and respectable friend," and "the oldest friend I have in the world."

> In the first place I had my old and respectable friend Roguin. This was a good old-fashioned friend for whom I was not indebted to my writings but to myself, and whom for that reason I have always preserved. (010-115)

## Relation to Rousseau

Roguin's friendship with Rousseau predated Rousseau's literary fame — a point Rousseau emphasized with pride, noting that this friendship was owed "not to my writings but to myself." This distinction mattered deeply to Rousseau, who increasingly distrusted relationships formed through his literary reputation.

During Rousseau's exile from France following the condemnation of *[[Emile]]*, Roguin provided crucial refuge. When Rousseau fled [[Montmorency]] for Switzerland, he resolved to stop at Yverdon:

> When I set off from Montmorency to go into Switzerland, I had resolved to stop at Yverdon, at the house of my old friend Roguin, who had several years before retired to that place, and had invited me to go and see him. (011-089)

At Yverdon, Roguin introduced Rousseau to his extended family, who welcomed him warmly:

> During my stay at Yverdon, I became acquainted with all the family of my friend Roguin, and amongst others with his niece, Madam Boy de la Tour, and her daughters. (012-002)

The Roguin family urged Rousseau to remain at Yverdon, and it was Colonel Roguin who insisted on accompanying Rousseau across the mountain to his new residence at [[Motiers]]:

> Colonel Roguin insisted on crossing the mountain with me, and installing me at Moiters. (012-011)

Rousseau's account closes with a poignant note: "Roguin dying a year or two after my departure from that city" (012-091) and "old papa Roguin had the candor to inform me with grief..." (012-092), suggesting the friendship remained significant until the end.

## Significance

M. Roguin embodies the ideal of disinterested friendship that Rousseau championed throughout the *Confessions*. Unlike the philosophe circle in Paris — whom Rousseau came to view as treacherous and self-interested — Roguin represented a pre-literary, authentic connection rooted in personal affection rather than intellectual reputation.

Roguin's role during Rousseau's Swiss exile was practically essential: the Yverdon stay provided a buffer between Rousseau's traumatic departure from France and his eventual settlement at Motiers. The Roguin family network — including Madame Boy de la Tour and her daughters — formed the social fabric of Rousseau's life during this period of displacement.

The relationship also illustrates the Swiss dimension of Rousseau's identity. Though Rousseau spent much of his adult life in France, his Swiss connections — embodied by figures like Roguin — provided emotional and practical support during his moments of greatest vulnerability.
