M. le Blond was the French consul in [[Venice]] during Rousseau's service as secretary to the French ambassador, the Comte de Montaigu. He was one of the few figures in Rousseau's Venetian episode who emerges from the _Confessions_ as unequivocally decent — an honest official who offered practical help, social connection, and later, in [[Paris]], a painful reminder of Rousseau's own ingratitude.

## The Venetian Consul

Rousseau encountered Le Blond in Venice during 1743–1744, when the consul had been charged with managing the affairs of French subjects in the Republic. When Rousseau found himself in disputes with Venetian authorities — including the theater manager Zanetto over a promised musical composition — he turned to Le Blond for intervention.

> Rousseau went to M. le Blond to beg he would speak to the ambassador about the affair with Zanetto. The consul had been charged with affairs of this nature in the name of the king. (007-069, 007-072)

When Rousseau's composition was performed without his consent and without payment, he again sought Le Blond's help. The consul promised three sequins as a settlement, but "when Le Blond carried him the note, even the modest sum proved difficult to collect" (007-077). The episode revealed both Le Blond's willingness to intervene and the limits of consular authority in Venetian affairs.

## Social Circle in Venice

Le Blond was among a small group of men of merit whose society Rousseau cultivated in Venice. Alongside "de St. Cyr, Carrio Altuna, and a few others," Le Blond formed part of the intellectual and diplomatic circle that made Rousseau's Venetian sojourn tolerable despite his conflicts with the ambassador Montaigu (007-093).

> The daughters of M. le Blond were very amiable, but difficult of access — Rousseau noted the family's social station placed them somewhat above the circles in which he ordinarily moved. (007-100)

Le Blond's household became a venue where Rousseau aired his ambitions. One day Rousseau spoke of a desire at Le Blond's residence, and the consul's response — "If you are so desirous..." — led to an arrangement that "laughed at my surprise" (007-097, 007-098). Rousseau's tone in recounting this is warm, suggesting genuine affection for the consul's good-natured pragmatism.

## The Paris Reunion and Rousseau's Negligence

Years later, Le Blond reappeared in Rousseau's Paris life. Rousseau acknowledged the consul as "the honest M. le Blond, who had done me a service at Venice" (010-119). But the reunion was shadowed by Rousseau's own failing: he had promised to deliver a package of Le Blond's papers to Paris and had neglected the task entirely.

> Le Blond could not but be justly offended by Rousseau's negligence, which had caused him considerable inconvenience. (010-123)

Rousseau's admission of this fault is characteristically unsparing. The honest consul, who had helped him in Venice without expectation of return, was repaid with carelessness — a debt Rousseau records in the _Confessions_ as part of his larger project of moral self-examination.

## Character Assessment

Le Blond stands out in the Venetian chapters as a figure of uncomplicated integrity. Unlike the scheming ambassadors, rival secretaries, and self-interested courtesans who populate Rousseau's Venetian narrative, Le Blond is simply what he appears to be: a competent official, a kind man, and a reliable friend. That Rousseau failed to reciprocate his kindness becomes, in the _Confessions_, one more entry in the ledger of youthful failings the memoir was designed to confess.
