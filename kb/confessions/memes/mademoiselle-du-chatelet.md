## Biography

Mademoiselle du Chatelet lived at the Chasattes, a boarding house in Lyons, and was a friend of [[Madame de Warens]]. Rousseau had first met her when he passed through Lyons with Le Maitre, carrying a letter of introduction from Madam de Warens. By the time of Rousseau's return to Lyons on his search for his benefactress, the acquaintance was already established (005-009).

Rousseau described her as "neither young nor handsome, but did not want for elegance; she was easy and obliging while her understanding gave price to her familiarity" (005-014). What set her apart was her intellectual disposition: "She had a taste for that kind of moral observation which leads to the knowledge of mankind, and from her originated that study in myself" (005-014). This is a remarkable admission from Rousseau, crediting her with awakening in him the systematic study of human nature that would later define his philosophical and autobiographical writing.

She was an admirer of the novels of Le Sage, particularly *Gil Blas*, which she lent to Rousseau. At the time, he was not yet mature enough as a reader to appreciate it fully: "I read this performance with pleasure, but my judgment was not yet ripe enough to relish that sort of reading. I liked romances which abounded with high-flown sentiments" (005-014).

## Relation to Rousseau

When Rousseau arrived in Lyons, impoverished and searching for Madam de Warens, Mademoiselle du Chatelet received him warmly and on equal terms. "It was not an appearance of coolness that withheld me, on the contrary, I was very kindly received, treated on the footing of equality" (005-009). She informed him that Madam de Warens had recently passed through Lyons and offered to write for information about her whereabouts, suggesting he remain in Lyons until a reply arrived.

Rousseau accepted but did not disclose his desperate financial situation, finding it unbearable "to descend from a companion to a miserable beggar" (005-009). During the week or so he waited, he visited her frequently:

> I remained at Lyons seven or eight days to wait for some little commissions with which Madam de Warens had charged Mademoiselle du Chatelet, who during this interval I visited more assiduously than before, having the pleasure of talking with her of her friend, and being no longer disturbed by the cruel remembrance of my situation, or painful endeavors to conceal it. (005-014)

While he waited for her reply, Rousseau passed several nights sleeping in the streets of Lyons, having spent his last coins on bread rather than lodging. He recalled lying "in the open air, stretched on the earth, or on a bench," sleeping "as soundly as if reposing on a bed of roses," all the while "patiently waited an answer to Mademoiselle du Chatelet's letter" (005-011).

Days later, when the reply arrived with news that Madam de Warens had been found, Mademoiselle du Chatelet urged him to hire a horse for the journey. Rousseau declined — "I was certainly right, for by so doing I should have lost the pleasure of the last pedestrian expedition I ever made" (005-016).

## Significance

Mademoiselle du Chatelet occupies a modest but meaningful place in Rousseau's intellectual development. His statement that "from her originated" his study of moral observation and the knowledge of mankind places her among the small number of early influences who shaped the analytical habits that would make him one of the Enlightenment's most penetrating observers of human nature.

She also exemplifies a recurring pattern in Rousseau's early life: older women of intelligence and kindness who offered him hospitality, conversation, and intellectual stimulation at critical moments. Her recommendation of *Gil Blas* — a picaresque novel about a young man navigating French society — was particularly apt for Rousseau at that stage of his wandering youth.

Rousseau's tribute to her conversation is striking: "the interesting and sensible conversation of a deserving woman is more proper to form the understanding of a young man than all the pedantic philosophy of books" (005-015). This sentiment, from a man whose own books would become pillars of eighteenth-century philosophy, suggests the lasting impression she made.
