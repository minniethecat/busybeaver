# Rousseau's Opera Career

Rousseau's engagement with opera and musical composition forms one of the most persistent and revealing threads in the _Confessions_. From his first arrival in [[Paris]] in 1742 with a new system of musical notation, through the composition of his successful opera _[[Le Devin du Village]]_, to his polemical interventions in the Querelle des Bouffons, music was the field in which Rousseau most consistently sought recognition, achieved success, and experienced the tensions between creative ambition and philosophical conviction.

## Early Musical Ambitions

Rousseau arrived in Paris convinced that his system of numerical musical notation would revolutionize the art. "I presented my project to the Academy of Sciences," he records, but the reception was lukewarm. (007-036) The academicians, while acknowledging the ingenuity of the system, judged it impractical—a verdict that Rousseau accepted with outward grace but inward resentment.

> I was then passionately fond of music; I had ideas, and a taste for the art, which I believed was sufficient to enable me to compose an opera. (007-060)

His first major operatic attempt was _Les Muses Galantes_, a ballet héroïque inspired by his exposure to French opera. "I entitled this opera _Les Muses Galantes_," he writes. (007-061) The work was never fully staged, but excerpts performed in salon settings earned him enough encouragement to persevere.

## Le Devin du Village

The pinnacle of Rousseau's operatic career was _Le Devin du Village_ (The Village Soothsayer), a one-act _intermède_ composed and written in 1752. The work was performed before the court at Fontainebleau, and its success was immediate and intoxicating:

> Never since French operas existed was there such a concert of applause. (004-048)

The king himself was said to have been charmed, and Rousseau was offered a royal pension—which, in a moment of characteristic perversity, he declined. This refusal, driven by his fear of losing independence, became one of the emblematic episodes of the _Confessions_, illustrating the conflict between Rousseau's craving for recognition and his terror of dependence.

## Music Theory and the Encyclopédie

Rousseau's musical expertise was sufficiently respected that [[Denis Diderot]] commissioned him to write the music articles for the _Encyclopédie_. These articles—eventually collected and expanded into his _Dictionnaire de Musique_ (1767)—established Rousseau as a serious musical theorist.

> These subjects of music and opera still engaged my attention during my residence at Paris. (007-060)

Yet Rousseau's musical theorizing was not merely technical. It was inseparable from his broader philosophical commitments. In the Querelle des Bouffons—the heated Parisian debate over the relative merits of French and Italian opera—Rousseau aligned himself with the Italian party, championing melody over harmony and arguing that French was fundamentally unsuitable for musical setting. This position was of a piece with his critique of civilization: Italian music was natural, expressive, close to the voice of the people; French music was artificial, complex, and decadent.

## Music, Identity, and Social Critique

Rousseau's operatic career illuminates several key themes of the _Confessions_:

- **The tension between art and authenticity**: Rousseau's success as an opera composer sat uneasily with his philosophical critique of luxury and artifice. How could the author of the _Discourse on the Sciences and the Arts_ also be the composer of a court entertainment?

- **Music as self-knowledge**: The _Confessions_ traces Rousseau's musical development as a form of autobiography. His changing tastes—from French to Italian opera, from composition to botany—map onto his evolving sense of self.

- **Music and social mobility**: Opera was Rousseau's most direct route to recognition, patronage, and independence. It represented the possibility of a life lived on his own terms—a possibility that his philosophical principles both enabled and undermined.

> I soon became so fond of the opera that, tired of babbling, eating, and drinking in the boxes, I frequently left the company to place myself below. (007-093)

Rousseau's operatic career was not a detour from his philosophical path but an integral part of it. The man who composed _Le Devin du Village_ was the same man who wrote the _Confessions_: a figure suspended between the craving for recognition and the love of [[Nature]], between the city and the solitary walk.
