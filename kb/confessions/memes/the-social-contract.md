## Overview

*The Social Contract* (*Du contrat social*, 1762) is [[Rousseau]]'s most influential work of political philosophy. Its famous opening line — "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains" — announces its central question: how can legitimate political authority be reconciled with individual freedom? Rousseau's answer, the theory of the general will, would become one of the foundational concepts of modern democratic thought.

The work builds on themes Rousseau had first explored in his *Discourse on Inequality*, arguing that the social contract is not a historical fact but a normative ideal — the terms under which free individuals could consent to be governed without sacrificing their freedom. Though a slim volume, it contains some of the most quoted and contested passages in political philosophy.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

Rousseau worked on *The Social Contract* alongside *[[Émile]]* during his most productive period at [[Montmorency]]. He saw the two works as complementary — one addressing the education of man, the other the constitution of society. The writing was laborious; Rousseau described extracting the work from a larger, abandoned project on political institutions.

The book drew on ideas Rousseau had long developed. As he noted:

> Every strong idea in the Social Contract had been before published in the Discourse on Inequality. [009-056]

The completion of the manuscript came at a fraught moment. Rousseau was under surveillance and feared his papers would be seized:

> In the time I finished the 'Social Contract', and sent it to Rey, fixing the price, I was extremely uneasy. [011-023]

The book was published in Amsterdam by Marc-Michel Rey in April 1762, shortly before *Émile*. Both works were promptly condemned in [[Paris]] and [[Geneva]], and Rousseau fled into exile.

## Historical Significance

*The Social Contract* has had an extraordinary afterlife. Its concepts of popular sovereignty, the general will, and civic religion influenced the French Revolution, inspired generations of democratic theorists, and remain central to debates about legitimacy, freedom, and collective decision-making. Rousseau himself acknowledged the book's explosive potential, writing that he "had spoken of the Corsicans in the 'Social Contract' as a new people" — a remark that led to an invitation from Corsican patriots to draft a constitution for their island.

The work's reception in Rousseau's own time was hostile. The authorities saw it as seditious, and even some fellow philosophes distanced themselves from its radical implications. Yet Rousseau never wavered in his conviction that it represented his most important contribution to political thought.
