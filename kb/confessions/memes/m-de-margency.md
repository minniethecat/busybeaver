## Biography

M. de Margency was a gentleman-in-ordinary of the king of France and a man of letters associated with the Parisian intellectual circles of the eighteenth century.
He was an "ancient member of the 'Coterie Holbachique'" who, like Rousseau, had separated from Baron d'Holbach's circle. (010-127)
Margency owned an estate at Margency, near Montmorency, which he let to M. and Madam de Verdelin, making him Rousseau's neighbor in the countryside. (010-127)

Margency was also an old friend of Madam d'Épinay, from whom he separated around the same time Rousseau did. (010-127)
His shared experiences with Rousseau — leaving the Holbach circle and distancing himself from Madam d'Épinay — created "a certain conformity of experience" that drew the two men closer together. (010-127)

## Relation to Rousseau

### The d'Holbach Confrontation

Margency was present at one of the most painful episodes of Rousseau's social life: the confrontation at Baron d'Holbach's house.
When d'Holbach attacked Rousseau "without reason or pretence, and with such brutality, in presence of Diderot, who said not a word," Margency witnessed the scene.
According to Rousseau, Margency "since that time has often told me how much he admired the moderation and mildness of my answers." (008-137)

### The Journal des Savans Offer

At the beginning of one winter, Margency wrote to Rousseau proposing him a position at the *Journal des Savans*, presenting the offer as coming from himself.
Rousseau, however, "easily perceived from the manner of the letter that he was dictated to and authorized" — Margency later confirmed he had been asked by M. de Malesherbes to make the offer. (010-131)

The position required only two abstracts per month and carried a salary of eight hundred livres, with no need to visit Paris.
Despite these favorable terms, Rousseau declined, explaining to Margency that his talent lay not in routine literary work but in "a certain warmth of mind" that could not be commanded by schedule.
Rousseau wrote Margency "a letter of thanks, in the politest terms possible," and both Margency and Malesherbes approved of his refusal "without receiving me less politely." (010-131)

### The Verdelin Connection

Margency was "the friend of madam [de Verdelin], and became that of monsieur." (010-167)
He had let his castle of Margency to the Verdelins, and through this connection, Rousseau's path intersected with Madam d'Houdetot.
Madam de Verdelin gave Madam d'Houdetot a key to pass through the garden of Margency on her way to Mont Olympe, and Rousseau "crossed it several times with her." (010-167)

### The d'Épinay Intrusion

Madam d'Épinay, suspicious of Rousseau's relationship with Madam d'Houdetot, "one day invited herself with M. de Margency to dinner at the Hermitage, for the first time since I resided there."
During the visit, she seized the moment when Rousseau was walking with Margency to enter his private closet and press Rousseau's household to show her Madam d'Houdetot's letters. (009-157)

## Significance

M. de Margency represents one of the few figures in Rousseau's narrative who maintained a consistent friendship with him through multiple ruptures.
Unlike Diderot or Grimm, Margency separated from the Holbach circle on his own terms, lending his connection with Rousseau a quality of genuine sympathy rather than obligation.

Margency also served as a trusted intermediary between Rousseau and M. de Malesherbes, the French official responsible for oversight of the book trade.
That Malesherbes chose Margency to convey the Journal des Savans offer suggests Margency was regarded as a man of discretion — a quality confirmed by Rousseau's note that "the secret was so well kept that it was never known to the public." (010-131)

However, Margency's association with Madam de Verdelin complicated Rousseau's view of him.
Rousseau noted that Madam de Verdelin "did not see with an evil eye she looked upon with one of ridicule, and her friend Margency was not excepted" — suggesting that even Margency was not spared her sharp tongue. (010-168)
