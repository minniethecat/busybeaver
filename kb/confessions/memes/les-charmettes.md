## Overview

Les Charmettes is a small country house located in a valley about one mile south of Chambéry in the Duchy of Savoy. It sits on a hillside with views of the surrounding mountains and vineyards. The estate belonged to M. de Conzié, a local nobleman.

Rousseau and Madame de Warens took up residence at Les Charmettes in the summer of 1736 after searching for a more suitable home outside Chambéry. (006-035) The house gave them space for a garden, fresh air, and the tranquility that Rousseau craved.

## In Rousseau's Life

The years at Les Charmettes (1736–1740) were, by Rousseau's own account, the happiest of his life. Here, free from the demands of urban society, he devoted himself to study, reading philosophy, literature, and science. He gardened, studied botany, and composed music.

"The first day we went to sleep at Charmettes," Rousseau recalled, "the way being up-hill, and Madam de Warens rather heavy, she was carried in a chair, while I followed on foot." (006-042) The image captures the domestic tenderness of their life together.

At Les Charmettes, Rousseau undertook his first systematic program of self-education, reading through an entire library of books on philosophy, mathematics, and natural science. This period laid the intellectual foundation for all his subsequent work.

His departure from Les Charmettes for Lyon in 1740 marked the end of his idyllic youth and the beginning of his entry into the wider world of letters and society.

## Historical Context

Les Charmettes was typical of the small country houses that Savoyard nobility maintained outside the main towns. The house and its surroundings provided Rousseau with direct experience of the natural world that would later inform his philosophy of nature and education.

The house survives today as a museum dedicated to Rousseau, maintained by the city of Chambéry. It remains a pilgrimage site for admirers of Rousseau's philosophy.
