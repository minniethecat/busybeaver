## Biography

Madame le Vasseur was the mother of Thérèse Levasseur, Rousseau's companion of over three decades. She was originally from Orleans, where her husband had been an officer of the Mint at that city. After business losses, the family fell into poverty, and Thérèse's needlework became their primary source of income.

> Her father was an officer of the Mint at Orleans, and her mother a shopkeeper; they had many children. The function of the father was discontinued; he had no employment; and the mother having suffered losses, was reduced to narrow circumstances. She quitted business and came to Paris with her husband and daughter, who, by her industry, maintained all the three. (008-053)

Rousseau met Thérèse at the Hôtel Saint-Quentin around 1745, and soon the entire Levasseur family became part of his life. He supported them financially throughout his years in Paris. Madame le Vasseur lived with or near Rousseau and her daughter for an extended period, making her a constant presence in his household.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's relationship with Madame le Vasseur was deeply ambivalent. He employed her as a secretary, having her copy out his musical compositions — a practical arrangement that also kept her close.

> I made Madam le Vasseur my secretary; I had lodged her with her daughter, and husband, nearer to myself; and she, to save me the expense of a servant, came every morning to light my fire, and do such other little things as were necessary. (008-053)

Rousseau acknowledged that Madame le Vasseur was not unintelligent — but her intelligence was of a kind he distrusted. She was, in his view, cunning rather than wise, skilled at manipulation rather than honest counsel.

> Madam le Vasseur did not want sense, that is address; and pretended to have a great regard for me. She had given her daughter a confidence that was her masterpiece of counsel. (008-058)

The "masterpiece of counsel" Rousseau refers to was Madame le Vasseur's advice to Thérèse: she encouraged her daughter to dissemble with Rousseau while separately managing the family's affairs behind his back. Rousseau acknowledges, however, that she was "a tolerably good mother" in the sense that she acted in what she believed to be her daughter's interests.

The practical tensions of cohabitation were considerable. When Rousseau moved to the Hermitage in 1756, Madame le Vasseur was dissatisfied with the rural isolation and the simpler fare.

> As to the resolution I had taken to reside at the Hermitage, Madam le Vasseur seemed dissatisfied with her situation, and was continually saying she should have been much more comfortable in Paris. (009-178)

> Madam le Vasseur, who ate a great deal, and with a stomach that in all probability was capable of digesting iron, could not easily put up with short commons — a suffering which to her daughter was by no means trifling. (009-179)

When Rousseau was arranging for the secret of his children (whom he had abandoned to the foundling hospital) to be kept, Madame le Vasseur actively undermined his efforts, spreading word of what he had done among her acquaintance even as she assured him of her discretion.

> Whilst I was thus communicating what I had done, Madam le Vasseur did the same thing amongst her acquaintance, but with a very different intention. ... The mother confided to [Madame Dupin] the secret of the daughter. (008-068)

Despite these tensions, Rousseau felt a genuine obligation toward the Levasseur family. He repeatedly sought situations and financial provisions for M. and Madam le Vasseur, and even when he grew exasperated with her, he could not bring himself to cast them off entirely. Even Rousseau's friends recognized her as the primary source of discord in his domestic life — Gauffecourt referred to the Levasseur women as "the governesses" who controlled Rousseau's household.

## Significance

Madame le Vasseur is a figure of considerable importance in understanding Rousseau's domestic life and his psychology. She represents the class of the Parisian poor — resourceful, unsentimental, and perpetually calculating — whom Rousseau, despite his democratic sympathies, could never quite bring himself to trust or respect. Their relationship illuminates the tension in Rousseau's thought between his theoretical love of the common people and his personal discomfort with their manners.

Her presence in Rousseau's household also sheds light on his relationship with Thérèse. Many of Rousseau's friends believed that Madame le Vasseur was the true source of the couple's difficulties, manipulating her timid daughter and exploiting Rousseau's sense of obligation. The triangular arrangement — Rousseau, Thérèse, and her mother — was one of the most durable and dysfunctional domestic configurations in literary history, lasting over twenty years and shaping the conditions under which Rousseau wrote many of his major works.
