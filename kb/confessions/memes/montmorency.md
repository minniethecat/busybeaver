# Montmorency

Montmorency was the setting for the most productive and—by Rousseau's own account—the happiest years of his life. From 1757 to 1762, Rousseau lived in this small town at the edge of a great forest north of [[Paris]], first in [[the Hermitage Period|the Hermitage]] on the estate of [[Madame d'Epinay]], and then at Mont-Louis, a modest house placed at his disposal by [[M. de Luxembourg|the Maréchal de Luxembourg]]. It was here that he wrote _[[Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse]]_, _[[Émile]]_, and _[[The Social Contract]]_—the three works that would define his legacy and, ultimately, force him into exile.

## Geography and Setting

Montmorency lay about twelve miles north of Paris, on the edge of a vast forest that Rousseau came to love with an almost religious intensity:

> The forest of Montmorency, which was almost at my door, would become my study, my cabinet of natural history, and the confidant of all my meditations. (009-042)

The landscape combined the advantages of solitude with proximity to the capital. Rousseau could walk for hours among the trees, botanizing and composing, yet remain within reach of the Luxembourg household at the Château de Montmorency. The "valley and the pond of Montmorency" provided a prospect that, from the window of Mont-Louis, "presented to me, as the only furniture of my chamber, a view of the most enchanting landscape." (010-081)

## Life at Mont-Louis

After his expulsion from the Hermitage in December 1757, Rousseau moved to Mont-Louis, a small house with a garden on the Luxembourg estate. The transition was providential: what began as a crisis—homeless in winter, estranged from his patroness—became the occasion for a deeper and more rewarding attachment to the Luxembourg household.

> My retreat to Montmorency seemed to disconcert Madam d'Épinay. (010-063)

At Mont-Louis, Rousseau established a routine that balanced creative work, botanical study, and social intercourse. He received visitors: "I was also acquainted at Montmorency with several fathers of the oratory," (010-112) and "several persons who came to see me." (010-104) Yet the dominant tone of his life there was productive solitude.

The forest was his true study. "I had made in the neighborhood a number of botanical excursions," he records, (010-109) and the plants he collected became the basis for a serious engagement with natural history that would continue through his exile and into his final years.

## The Works of Montmorency

The Montmorency years produced three masterpieces, each representing a different facet of Rousseau's genius:

1. **_Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse_** (1761): An epistolary novel of forbidden love, written in a state of creative ecstasy in the forest. It became one of the best-selling books of the century and established Rousseau as the apostle of sentiment.

2. **_Émile, or On Education_** (1762): A philosophical treatise on education that Rousseau considered his most important work. The book's heterodox religious views—expressed in the "Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar"—provoked the condemnation that forced Rousseau into exile.

3. **_The Social Contract_** (1762): Rousseau's contribution to political philosophy, arguing for popular sovereignty and the general will. Like _Émile_, it was condemned by the Paris Parlement.

The simultaneous appearance of _Émile_ and _The Social Contract_ in 1762 transformed Rousseau from a celebrated author into a fugitive. The decree of the Parlement of Paris, issued on June 9, 1762, ordered his arrest. Warned by the Luxembourg household, Rousseau fled Montmorency under cover of darkness, never to return.

## Legacy

Montmorency occupies a central place in the symbolic geography of the _Confessions_. It represents:

- **The pastoral ideal**: Rousseau's lifelong fantasy of simple, rural existence realized—however briefly—in a real place
- **The price of truth**: The condemnation of _Émile_ demonstrated that the authentic philosopher could not live at peace with the powers of the age
- **The pattern of paradise and fall**: Like [[Les Charmettes]] before it, Montmorency was a lost Eden—a place of happiness from which Rousseau was expelled, and which he thereafter mourned

> The six years I passed at the Hermitage and at Montmorency be considered as the happiest of my life. (009-042)
