## Biography

The Count de Favria was the grandson of the Count de Gauvon, Master of the Horse to the Queen and Chief of the illustrious House of Solar in Turin.
Rousseau described him as "a giddy youth" — young, impulsive, and full of the careless energy of the nobility. (003-008)

He was the brother (or perhaps nephew) of the Abbé de Gauvon, who served as Rousseau's mentor in the household.

## Relation to Rousseau

### The Gauvon Household

When Rousseau arrived at the Count de Gauvon's household in Turin, the old count treated him with remarkable kindness, instructing that Rousseau should "get behind no coach, nor follow any one out of the house" — exempting him from the duties of a common footman.
The Count de Favria, however, initially tested this boundary: as Rousseau recorded, "the Count de Favria (a giddy youth) having commanded me to get behind his coach, his grandfather ordered that I should get behind no coach." (003-008)

Despite this early friction, Rousseau's diligent service won him favor.
He noted, "I did, within doors, the business of a footman; but I did it, as it were, of my own free will, without being appointed to any particular service; and except writing some letters, which were dictated to me, and cutting out some ornaments for the Count de Favria, I was almost the absolute master of my time." (003-008)

### Speaking to the King

As Rousseau's reputation grew within the household, the Count de Favria became an active advocate for him.
The Abbé de Gauvon "thoroughly pleased with me, expressed his satisfaction to everyone, while his father had such a singular affection for me, that I was assured by the Count de Favria, that he had spoken of me to the king." (003-014)

This was a mark of extraordinary favor: a young footman-secretary being mentioned to the king by a noble family spoke to the impression Rousseau had made in the Gauvon household.

### The Farewell Counsel

When Rousseau decided to leave Turin — seduced by the prospect of traveling with a young acquaintance named Bâcle — the Count de Favria emerged as an unexpected voice of wisdom.
"The Count de Favria, though young and giddy, talked to me on this occasion in the most sensible and serious manner: I might add, if it would not be thought vain, with the utmost tenderness." (003-021)

Favria "reminded me, in the most flattering terms, of the cares of his uncle, and intentions of his grandfather" and, "after having drawn in lively colors what I was sacrificing to ruin, he offered to make my peace, without stipulating any conditions, but that I should no more see the worthless fellow who had seduced me." (003-021)

Rousseau recorded that he was to have spoken with the Count de Favria on the morning of his departure, but "being sensible that my head was so far turned as to render it possible for me to disobey the injunction," the household steward withheld Rousseau's final payment until after that interview. (003-020)

## Significance

The Count de Favria illustrates a recurring pattern in Rousseau's early life: the kindness of aristocratic patrons whom Rousseau ultimately disappointed through impulsive decisions.
The Gauvon household had offered Rousseau a path to preferment — the Abbé's tutelage, the old Count's protection, and Favria's personal advocacy all pointed toward a respectable career.

Favria's farewell counsel, delivered "with the utmost tenderness" by a young man Rousseau had dismissed as "giddy," reveals the depth of attachment Rousseau inspired even in those he was about to abandon.
That Favria — youthful and frivolous by Rousseau's own description — could summon such gravity for a departing servant's sake speaks to Rousseau's gift for forming bonds across the barriers of rank.
It also underscores the tragedy of Rousseau's departure: he had been loved in the Gauvon household, and he threw it away for a whim.
