## Biography

Madame Dupin, born Louise de Fontaine in 1706, was the daughter of the financier Samuel Bernard and Madame Fontaine. She was one of three sisters renowned for their beauty, known collectively as "the three graces."

> Madam Dupin was, as every one in Paris knows, the daughter of Samuel Bernard and Madam Fontaine. There were three sisters, who might be called the three graces. ... Madam Dupin, more beautiful than either of her sisters, and the only one who has not been reproached with some levity of conduct. (007-052)

She was given in marriage to Claude Dupin, a financier who became a farmer general. The marriage brought Dupin an immense fortune, arranged by Madame Fontaine in recognition of the hospitality Dupin had shown her.

> She was the reward of the hospitality of M. Dupin, to whom her mother gave her in marriage with the place of farmer general and an immense fortune, in return for the good reception he had given her in his province. (007-052)

Madame Dupin presided over one of the most distinguished salons in Paris. Her gatherings were selective and prized for the quality of their company, attracting men of letters, statesmen, and members of the nobility.

> Her house, at that time, as brilliant as any other in Paris, was frequented by societies the less numerous, as the persons by whom they were composed were chosen on account of some distinguished merit. She was fond of seeing every one who had claims to a marked superiority; the great men of letters, and fine women. (007-055)

She maintained her salon at the Hôtel de Languedoc on the Rue de Grenelle-Saint-Honoré in Paris, and also owned the Château de Chenonceaux, which she later passed to her daughter-in-law, Madame de Chenonceaux. Even in her later years, her home remained one of the best in Paris.

> The house of Madam Dupin, which, notwithstanding its being less brilliant than in the young days of Madam Dupin, was still, by the merit of the heads of the family, and the choice of company which assembled there, one of the best houses in Paris. (010-116)

Madame Dupin lived to the age of 93, dying in 1799, having witnessed the French Revolution but surviving it unscathed.

## Relation to Rousseau

Madame Dupin played a significant role in Rousseau's life during his early years in Paris. He was introduced to her by a mutual acquaintance and was immediately captivated by her beauty.

> When I first saw her I was violently smitten by Madam Dupin. (007-053)

She received him kindly, showing interest in his work and his musical plans. Rousseau was invited to dine, and she accompanied herself on the harpsichord while singing — an occasion Rousseau described as making him "almost forget" his station. The first visit, however, left him embarrassed by his awkwardness in her polished company.

Rousseau's relationship with Madame Dupin was marked by inconsistency. Shortly after their first meetings, she sent word that his visits were too frequent and he should discontinue them, only to reverse course days later:

> I should entirely have ceased calling on them, had not Madam Dupin, by another unexpected caprice, sent to desire I would for a few days take care of her son, who changing his preceptor, remained alone during that interval. I passed eight days in such torments as nothing but the pleasure of obeying Madam Dupin could render supportable. (007-057)

Despite this erratic beginning, Madame Dupin eventually became one of Rousseau's most reliable patrons. He worked as a secretary in her household, writing to her dictation and conducting erudite research. She and her son-in-law, M. de Francueil, recognized that Rousseau's modest income was insufficient and voluntarily raised his salary.

> M. de Francueil and Madam Dupin, clearly perceiving that eight or nine hundred livres a year were unequal to my wants, increased, of their own accord, my salary to fifty guineas; and Madam Dupin, having heard I wished to furnish myself lodgings, assisted me with some articles for that purpose. (008-057)

When Rousseau fell gravely ill, Madame Dupin sent the celebrated physician Morand to treat him, though the prognosis was bleak — Morand declared Rousseau would not survive six months. She also extended her generosity to those Rousseau cared about, including Thérèse Levasseur and her mother:

> Madam Dupin, who is generous and kind, and to whom she never told how attentive I was to her, notwithstanding my moderate resources, in providing for everything, provided on her part for what was necessary, with a liberality which, by order of her mother, the daughter concealed from me during my residence in Paris. (008-068)

Rousseau maintained friendly relations with Madame Dupin throughout his life. Even after his break with Parisian society, he continued to visit her at Clichy and later at Deuil, where she lived near his own residence at the Hermitage. He remained "always certain of being well received by Madam Dupin."

## Significance

Madame Dupin represents the essential role of salonnières in the intellectual life of eighteenth-century France. Her salon was one of the conduits through which Enlightenment ideas circulated among the Parisian elite. Though she herself did not publish, her gatherings brought together philosophers, statesmen, and artists, creating the social infrastructure that sustained the Republic of Letters.

For Rousseau specifically, Madame Dupin was more than a patron — she was a bridge into a world he both admired and resented. His ambivalent relationship with her, oscillating between infatuation and frustration, encapsulates his lifelong struggle with the aristocracy: drawn to its refinement and repelled by its artificiality. The financial support and social access she provided were material conditions that made his early literary career possible, even as he later rejected the values her circle embodied.
