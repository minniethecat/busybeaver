## Biography

Father Hemet was a Jesuit priest who served as confessor to both Rousseau and Madame de Warens during their stay at Les Charmettes, near Chambéry. At a time when Rousseau was drawn to the severe theology of Jansenism through his reading of Port-Royal, Father Hemet provided a gentle counterbalance.

> My confessor, who was hers likewise, contributed all in his power to keep up my hopes. This was a Jesuit, named Father Hemet; a good and wise old man, whose memory I shall ever hold in veneration. (006-073)

Despite Rousseau's growing antipathy toward Jesuit doctrine — which he considered "dangerous" — he distinguished Father Hemet from his order. The old priest possessed a quality that transcended institutional affiliation:

> Though a Jesuit, he had the simplicity of a child, and his manners, less relaxed than gentle, were precisely what was necessary to balance the melancholy impressions made on me by Jansenism. (006-073)

## Relation to Rousseau

Father Hemet, accompanied by his fellow Jesuit Father Coppier, regularly visited Rousseau and Madame de Warens at Les Charmettes despite the difficult journey for men of their age:

> This good man and his companion, Father Coppier, came frequently to visit us at Charmette, though the road was very rough and tedious for men of their age. These visits were very comfortable to me. (006-073)

Rousseau occasionally returned the visits, going to see them at their convent in Chambéry, where he had free access to the library. The memory of these Jesuits became inseparably linked with Rousseau's recollection of his happiest years:

> The remembrance of that happy time is so connected with the idea of those Jesuits, that I love one on account of the other, and though I have ever thought their doctrines dangerous, could never find myself in a disposition to hate them cordially. (006-073)

Years later, Rousseau explicitly contrasted Hemet's warmth with the behavior of other Jesuits he encountered. After his falling out with Father Castel, he noted:

> The intriguing and tyrannical spirit of his brethren, so different from the cordiality of the good Father Hemet, gave me such a disgust for their conversation. (007-119)

## Significance

Father Hemet represents a rare figure in Rousseau's memoirs: a member of an institution Rousseau mistrusted who nevertheless earned his lasting affection through personal character rather than doctrine. The contrast Rousseau draws between Hemet and other Jesuits — especially Father Castel — illustrates his conviction that individual virtue could survive within corrupt institutions. Hemet's childlike simplicity and genuine warmth provided the young Rousseau with spiritual comfort during a period of theological anxiety.
