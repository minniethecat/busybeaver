**Father Berthier** was a Jesuit priest and professor of natural philosophy at the Oratory, whom [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] came to know both in Paris and later at [[Montmorency]]. Initially charmed by Berthier's apparent good nature, Rousseau eventually came to see him as a calculating figure whose outward cordiality concealed ulterior motives.

## Biography

Father Berthier was a professor of natural philosophy and a member of the Jesuit order. Rousseau first encountered him in Paris at the home of [[M. Dupin]], where Berthier "labored with all his might at the refutation of Montesquieu." (007-119) This early association placed Berthier in the intellectual milieu of the French Counter-Enlightenment, aligned against the *philosophes*.

At Montmorency, Berthier was among "several fathers of the oratory" with whom Rousseau became acquainted. Rousseau described him as having "notwithstanding some little tincture of pedantry... a certain air of cordial good nature which I observed in him." (010-112) Yet Rousseau noted a contradiction: "I had, however, some difficulty to reconcile this great simplicity with the desire and the art he had of everywhere thrusting himself into the company of the great, as well as that of the women, devotees, and philosophers." (010-112) Berthier "knew how to accommodate himself to every one."

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau was initially taken with Berthier and "spoke of my satisfaction to all my other acquaintances." When Berthier heard of this, he thanked Rousseau—but his reaction betrayed something unsettling. Rousseau observed "something in his forced smile which, in my eyes, totally changed his physiognomy, and which has since frequently occurred to my mind." (010-112) He compared the smile to "that of Panurge purchasing the Sheep of Dindenaut"—an allusion to Rabelais suggesting cunning and duplicity.

Berthier played a pivotal role in the affair of [[Thérèse Levasseur]]'s mother. He wrote to Rousseau announcing that [[Grimm]] had offered to maintain the elderly [[Madam le Vasseur]] with a pension of three hundred livres, asking Rousseau's permission. (010-112) Rousseau was deeply unsettled by the offer, which he saw as part of a larger scheme, but gave his consent nonetheless. He later reflected that "Father Berthier afterwards cured me a little of my opinion of his good nature and cordiality, with which I had so unthinkingly charged him." (010-112)

Berthier was also the connection through which Rousseau met the mysterious Jansenists [[Ferrand]] and [[Minard]], whom Thérèse nicknamed "the gossips." (010-113) These two men, "children of Melchisedec" of unknown origin, passed for priests in disguise and became occasional chess partners for Rousseau at Montmorency.

During the crisis over the publication of *Émile*, Rousseau's paranoid imagination fixed on the Jesuits—including Berthier—as conspirators. He recalled that "notwithstanding the wheedling of Father Berthier... the Jesuits did not like me, not only as an Encyclopedist, but because all my principles were more in opposition to their maxims and influence than the incredulity of my colleagues." (011-048)

## Significance

Father Berthier exemplifies the complex, often duplicitous relationships Rousseau navigated with the clergy. His story illustrates a recurring pattern in the *Confessions*: Rousseau's initial warmth toward a person, followed by disillusionment when their true character emerges. Berthier's involvement in the Grimm-Levasseur pension affair also places him at the center of one of the webs of intrigue that Rousseau believed surrounded him during his Montmorency years.
