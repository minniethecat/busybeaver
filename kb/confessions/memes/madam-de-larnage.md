## Biography

Madame de Larnage was the wife of M. de Larnage and the mother of Mademoiselle de Larnage. Rousseau first encountered the family in Lyon, where Mademoiselle de Larnage lived and where her mother visited periodically. The daughter was strikingly beautiful but devoid of personality, while the mother compensated with abundant spirit.

> Madam de Larnage added to her natural vivacity that portion of sprightliness which should have belonged to the daughter. She was a little, ugly, lively trollop, with small twinkling ferret eyes, and marked with smallpox. (005-057)

Rousseau describes M. de Larnage as "a good quiet fellow, the true original of his daughter" — a man of placid temperament who seemed untroubled by his wife's flirtatious nature. Madame de Larnage would welcome Rousseau with kisses on the lips in her husband's presence, done with such "carelessness and simplicity" that it appeared entirely innocent.

Little is known of her life outside of Rousseau's account of their encounter in Book VI of the Confessions. She was travelling from Lyon towards Saint-Andiol near the Pont du Saint-Esprit, in the company of a newly married lady, Madame du Colombier, and the Marquis de Torignan.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau met Madame de Larnage in 1737 while he was travelling to Montpellier to seek treatment for what he believed was a polypus on his heart. They shared the road, inns, and dining tables, which made acquaintance unavoidable for the pathologically shy Rousseau.

> With my natural timidity it will not be conjectured that I was very ready at forming an acquaintance with these fine ladies, and the company that attended them; but travelling the same road, lodging at the same inns, and being obliged to eat at the same table, the acquaintance seemed unavoidable. (006-083)

When Madame du Colombier parted from the group at Romans, Madame de Larnage took charge of Rousseau's attention. He was, as ever with women, slow to perceive her intentions. She made the first advances, and Rousseau — in a characteristic self-deprecation — recounts that he mistook her genuine affection for mockery.

> The more obvious her advances were, the more I was confirmed in my mistake, and what increased my torment, I found I was really in love with her. I frequently said to myself, and sometimes to her, sighing, "Ah! why is not all this real? then should I be the most fortunate of men." (006-087)

The breakthrough came at an inn outside Valence, where Madame de Larnage proposed a walk along the city moats while the marquis, who disliked walking, remained behind. Rousseau launched into a long description of his illness, and she responded with tender sympathy. That evening, in the garden of the inn:

> Madam de Larnage took a more favorable resolution, and suddenly interrupted this silence by throwing her arms round my neck, while, at the same instant, her lips spoke too plainly on mine to be any longer misunderstood. This was reposing that confidence in me the want of which has almost always prevented me from appearing myself: for once I was at ease, my heart, eyes and tongue, spoke freely what I felt. (006-090)

The liaison lasted four or five days of travel together, which Rousseau would later remember as one of the purest periods of happiness in his life.

> This happiness lasted four or five days, during which time I was intoxicated with delight, which I tasted pure and serene without any alloy; an advantage I could never boast before; and, I may add, it is owing to Madam de Larnage that I did not go out of the world without having tasted real pleasure. (006-093)

Rousseau is careful to distinguish his feeling for Madame de Larnage from what he felt for Madame de Warens. It was not "precisely love" but "a very tender return of what she testified for me" — the encounters were delightful precisely because they lacked the torment that accompanied his deeper passions.

After they parted, Rousseau continued to Montpellier while Madame de Larnage went ahead to prepare for his reception. They corresponded — Rousseau wrote to her from the baths at Vals — but the relationship appears not to have survived the distance. She had advised him to continue his journey for his health rather than follow her immediately, a decision Rousseau later regretted as it allowed the connection to cool.

## Significance

Madame de Larnage occupies a unique place in Rousseau's emotional history. Among the many women he loved from afar or with tormenting self-consciousness, she alone offered him an experience of reciprocal pleasure unburdened by guilt, anxiety, or idealization. The episode is revealing of Rousseau's psychology: he required a woman to take the initiative — to "repose confidence" in him — before he could be himself. That a woman of no particular beauty or social distinction could unlock this capacity in him, where far more celebrated figures could not, underscores the centrality of emotional candor to Rousseau's conception of intimacy. The Larnage interlude also serves as a counterpoint within the Confessions to his tortured relationship with Madame de Warens, demonstrating that happiness, for Rousseau, lay not in grand passion but in simple, honest affection.
