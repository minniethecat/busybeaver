## Biography

Mademoiselle de Graffenried (referred to in the *Confessions* as "Mademoiselle de G——") was a young woman from Berne who appears in one of the most celebrated episodes of the *Confessions* — the idyllic day at Toune with Mademoiselle Galley. Rousseau describes her as "a young lady of Berne, very amiable; who, having been sent from that country for some youthful folly, had imitated Madam de Warens, at whose house I had sometimes seen her" (004-010). The reference to a "youthful folly" that led to her exile from Berne hints at a scandal or indiscretion, though Rousseau provides no further details.

Unlike Madam de Warens, Graffenried had no pension and was dependent on the patronage of others. She found her situation through Mademoiselle Galley, "who had prevailed on her mother to engage her young friend as a companion, till she could be otherwise provided for" (004-010).

## The Cherry Orchard Episode

The most vivid account of Mademoiselle de Graffenried appears in Book IV, during the celebrated excursion to the Château de Toune. Rousseau encountered Graffenried and Galley on horseback near a brook they could not cross. After helping them ford the stream, Rousseau was playfully taken prisoner and compelled to join their day's outing.

Rousseau rode behind Graffenried on her horse, an experience that stirred his romantic sensibilities: "Leaping behind Mademoiselle de G——, I trembled with joy, and when it became necessary to clasp her in order to hold myself on, my heart beat so violently that she perceived it, and told me hers beat also from a fear of falling" (004-011). The episode unfolds as a pastoral idyll, with the three young people sharing a simple meal, wandering through orchards, and Rousseau climbing a cherry tree to throw down fruit to the two young women seated below.

Rousseau later reflected on the emotional complexity of the day: "though I should have been happy to have had Mademoiselle de G——, for a mistress, it would have been still better to have had her for a confidante" (004-018). This distinction reveals Rousseau's awareness that the bond he felt with both young women was more tender and delicate than merely amorous.

## Later Correspondence

After the enchanted day, Rousseau determined to maintain contact: "At length, weary of playing the Spanish lover, and having no guitar, I determined to write to Mademoiselle de G——" (004-029). He arranged correspondence through Mademoiselle Giraud, a Genevan acquaintance who served as intermediary. The letters were kept secret from Galley, creating a romantic intrigue that sustained Rousseau's attachment.

Many years later, Rousseau recalled Graffenried among the women who had stirred his emotions: "Mademoiselle Galley, Mademoiselle de Graffenried, Mademoiselle de Breil, Madam Basile, Madam de Larnage, my pretty scholars, and even the bewitching Zulietta, whom my heart could not forget" (009-102).

## Significance

Mademoiselle de Graffenried belongs to a constellation of young women in the *Confessions* who represent Rousseau's ideal of innocent, pastoral love. Her presence in the cherry orchard episode transforms what might have been a solitary Rousseau-Galley encounter into a triangular dynamic that is richer and more complex. Graffenried functions as both participant and witness — she shares in the day's pleasures while also serving as the addressee of Rousseau's subsequent romantic correspondence, creating a discreet counterpoint to his more visible devotion to Galley.
