## Biography

M. Buttafuoco (Matteo Buttafuoco) was a Corsican nobleman from "one of the first families in the island" (012-143). He belonged to the generation of Corsican patriots who, following the Corsican Republic's declaration of independence from Genoa in 1755 under Pasquale Paoli, sought to establish legitimate political institutions for the island. Buttafuoco served as a liaison between Corsica and sympathetic French intellectuals, most notably Jean-Jacques Rousseau, who was invited to draft a constitution for the fledgling republic.

## Relation to Rousseau

In the mid-1760s, during Rousseau's exile from France, Buttafuoco reached out to him with a remarkable proposal: that Rousseau should compose a constitutional framework for Corsica. Rousseau was both flattered and daunted by the request:

> I did not conceal my fears from M. Buttafuoco, who rather relieved me from them. (012-144)

Rousseau recognized that the task required first-hand knowledge of the island and its people, not merely abstract political theory. He wrote to Buttafuoco expressing his wish for a personal meeting:

> I much wished for an interview with M. Buttafuoco, as that was certainly the best means of clearing up my doubts. (012-145)

Buttafuoco agreed with Rousseau's assessment: "This I wrote to M. Buttafuoco, and he felt as I did" (012-146). He encouraged Rousseau to travel to Corsica and provided detailed directions for the journey. Rousseau seriously considered relocating to the island, seeing it as a refuge from his European persecutors, and planned to "follow there with the directions of M. Buttafuoco as soon as this was possible" (012-150).

However, the plan for Rousseau to travel to Corsica was never realized. The persecutions at Motiers forced Rousseau to flee earlier than anticipated:

> I was not prepared for a long journey, especially to Corsica. I expected to hear from Buttafuoco; I took refuge in the island of St. Pierre. (012-152)

## Significance

Buttafuoco's invitation to Rousseau represents one of the most intriguing what-ifs of Enlightenment political history. The *Projet de constitution pour la Corse* (Project for a Constitution for Corsica), which Rousseau eventually drafted between 1765 and 1768, became a seminal text in constitutional theory despite never being implemented — France annexed Corsica in 1768, rendering the project moot. Buttafuoco's role as the initiator of this extraordinary commission illustrates the international reputation Rousseau had achieved as a political thinker by the 1760s, and the genuine hopes that smaller nations placed in Enlightenment philosophy as a foundation for state-building.
