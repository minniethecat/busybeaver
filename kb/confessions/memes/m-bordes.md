## Biography

M. Bordes was a resident of Lyons who moved in literary and intellectual circles. He was acquainted with several notable figures, including the Abbe Malby and Condillac, and was well-connected enough to provide Rousseau with recommendations to [[Paris]].

> I likewise saw M. Bordes, with whom I had been long acquainted, and who had frequently obliged me with the greatest cordiality and the most real pleasure. He it was who enabled me to sell my books; and he also gave me from himself good recommendations to Paris. (007-011)

Through M. Bordes's introduction, Rousseau gained access to the [[Duke-de-Richelieu]], marking an important social connection during his time in the capital.

## Relation to Rousseau

M. Bordes was one of the many acquaintances Rousseau made during his early career. Their relationship illustrates a recurring pattern in Rousseau's life: receiving generous assistance from patrons and friends, only to later neglect the relationship out of what Rousseau himself described as "invincible indolence."

> I was much obliged to all these good people, but I afterwards neglected them all; not from ingratitude, but from that invincible indolence which so often assumes its appearance. The remembrance of their services has never been effaced from my mind, nor the impression they made from my heart. (007-024)

When Rousseau visited [[Paris]], it was through an address provided by M. Bordes that he found lodgings at the Hotel St. Quentin, establishing his base in the capital during an important period of his career (007-028).

Their relationship later soured. About ten years after their initial friendship, M. Bordes became an adversary, angered by Rousseau's apparent neglect:

> In a little time I had another adversary whom I had not expected; this was the same M. Bordes, of Lyons, who ten years before had shown me much friendship, and from whom I had received several services. I had not forgotten him, but had neglected him from idleness, and had not sent him my writings for want of an opportunity. (008-089)

## Significance

M. Bordes represents the network of provincial literary patrons who supported Rousseau's early ambitions. His assistance — helping sell Rousseau's books, providing letters of recommendation — was instrumental in facilitating Rousseau's entry into Parisian intellectual circles.

Bordes was also among those to whom Rousseau read his early musical compositions, including his opera *Iphis and Anaxarete*, alongside other notable figures such as Gresset and Trublet (007-060). His presence in Rousseau's narrative also serves as a case study in the author's self-confessed tendency toward ingratitude, a theme Rousseau returns to repeatedly in the *Confessions*.
