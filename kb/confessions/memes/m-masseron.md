**M. Masseron** was the City Register of Geneva in the early eighteenth century. He appears briefly but memorably in Rousseau's *Confessions* as the master of the young Rousseau's first abortive apprenticeship—a humiliating episode that helped propel Rousseau toward his eventual flight from Geneva.

## Biography

M. Masseron held the office of City Register (Greffier) in Geneva, a respectable municipal position involving the keeping of official records. His clerks worked in the registry office, a setting that Rousseau found stifling and demeaning. Masseron's verdict on Rousseau's abilities—that the boy was fit only "to handle a file"—was a wound that stayed with Rousseau for years.

## Relation to Rousseau

After the death of Rousseau's mother and the departure of his father, the young Jean-Jacques was placed by his uncle Bernard with various masters to learn a trade. Masseron was his first formal placement, and it proved a thorough failure on both sides. Rousseau loathed the work and the subordination it demanded:

> [002-010] I was sent to Mr. Masseron, the City Register, to learn (according to the expression of my uncle Bernard) the thriving occupation of a scraper.

Masseron, for his part, had no higher opinion of his charge. He treated the boy with open contempt, accusing the uncle of having misrepresented Rousseau's abilities:

> [002-011] Mr. Masseron, who was not better pleased with my abilities than I was with the employment, treated me with disdain, incessantly upbraiding me with being a fool and blockhead…

The episode ended with Rousseau being ignominiously dismissed—"turned out of the registry, with the additional ignominy of being pronounced a fool by all Mr. Masseron's clerks, and fit only to handle a file." This public humiliation preceded Rousseau's apprenticeship to an engraver, M. Ducommon, which also ended badly and precipitated his flight from Geneva.

## Significance

Masseron's dismissal of Rousseau was more than a personal slight; it became emblematic of a pattern in Rousseau's life. He later reflected that Masseron's judgment was neither the first nor the last time he was underestimated:

> [003-054] …neither was it the last; the judgment of M. Masseron having been repeatedly confirmed.

> [003-055] …whatever M. Masseron, M. d'Aubonne and many others may have said, I cannot help thinking them mistaken.

Rousseau used this recurring misjudgment to frame his self-portrait as a man whose true capacities were slow to manifest—whose "ideas are produced slowly, with great embarrassment and after much afterthought." Masseron thus serves in the *Confessions* as the first in a series of authority figures who failed to recognize Rousseau's latent genius.
