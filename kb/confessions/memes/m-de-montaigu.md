## Biography

The Count de Montaigu was a French aristocrat and diplomat who served as ambassador to the Republic of Venice from 1743 to 1744. He came from a noble family; his brother, the Chevalier de Montaigu, served as *gentilhomme de la manche* (gentleman of the sleeve) to the Dauphin of France, which gave the family access to court circles. Montaigu obtained his ambassadorship not through merit but through assiduous cultivation of Barjac, a powerful figure in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

> The Count de Montaigu, captain in the guards, had just been appointed ambassador to Venice. He was an ambassador made by Barjac, to whom he assiduously paid his court. (007-063)

Montaigu's tenure in Venice was a disaster. He was, by Rousseau's account, incapable of the most basic functions of his office — he could neither dictate a despatch nor write legibly. This incompetence was compounded by arrogance, petty greed, and a fatal tendency to trust flatterers over the competent subordinates who might have saved him from himself.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau entered Montaigu's service as secretary to the embassy in 1743. The initial negotiation was unpromising: Rousseau asked for fifty guineas; Montaigu would offer only a thousand livres plus travel expenses, a sum Rousseau considered insufficient for the dignity of the post. A first agreement fell through, and Montaigu departed for Venice with another secretary, M. Follau.

> I stayed, and M. de Montaigu set out on his journey, taking with him another secretary, one M. Follau, who had been recommended to him by the office of foreign affairs. They no sooner arrived at Venice than they quarrelled. Follau perceiving he had to do with a madman, left him there. (007-063)

With no one else capable of the work, Montaigu urgently recalled Rousseau, who accepted the lower salary and departed for Venice. Their working relationship began cordially enough. Rousseau was genuinely useful to Montaigu, who recognized his value and initially treated him well. Rousseau was given the functions of secretary to the embassy — a title that carried real diplomatic weight in dealings with the Venetian Senate.

The goodwill did not last. Rousseau soon discovered Montaigu's profound incompetence in all matters of state.

> It was not to such a man as M. de Montaigu that government would confide a negotiation of even the most trifling importance. (007-069)

Montaigu was ignorant of diplomatic affairs and, worse, insisted on meddling in ways that made Rousseau's work nearly impossible. He would insert his own poorly written paragraphs into despatches after Rousseau had carefully composed them, forcing Rousseau to recopy everything:

> I thought myself happy when M. de Montaigu did not take it into his head to cram into them an impromptu of a few lines after his manner. This obliged me to return, and hastily transcribe the whole despatch decorated with his new nonsense, and honor it with the cipher, without which he would have refused his signature. (007-074)

The ambassador surrounded himself with disreputable characters, chief among them a highwayman from Mantua named Dominic Vitali, whom he made his favorite and household manager. Vitali, predictably, became Rousseau's enemy.

> The second gentleman, chosen by M. de Montaigu, was an outlaw highwayman from Mantua, called Dominic Vitali, to whom the ambassador intrusted the care of his house, and who had by means of flattery and sordid economy, obtained his confidence. (007-083)

The final rupture came over money. Rousseau had successfully negotiated the abolition of a tax on French subjects — the sequin duty — and expected to receive the customary perquisites from such negotiations. Montaigu tried to claim the money for himself.

> More filled with indignation at this meanness, than concern for my own interest, I rejected his proposal. He insisted, and I grew warm. "No, sir," said I, with some heat, "your excellency may keep what belongs to you, but do not take from me that which is mine." (007-070)

Rousseau left Montaigu's service and Venice itself, resolving to go to Paris to present his side of the affair to the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Montaigu attempted to cheat him even after his departure, charging him an extortionate rate for the carriage of a small box of personal effects — billing it at eleven hundred pounds when customs records showed it weighed only forty-five.

> In the apothecary's bill he offered me in payment of my salary, and which he wrote out himself, he stated the weight of this box, which he called a bale, at eleven hundred pounds, and charged me with the carriage of it at an enormous rate. (007-115)

Montaigu's career did not recover. After Rousseau left, he hired "a real attorney" who robbed him of twenty to thirty thousand livres within a year. Montaigu had the man imprisoned, but his own reputation was destroyed. He was recalled from Venice in disgrace.

> The person whom he procured to succeed me was a real attorney, who in less than a year robbed him of twenty or thirty thousand livres. He discharged him, and sent him to prison, dismissed his gentleman with disgrace, and, in wretchedness, got himself everywhere into quarrels, received affronts which a footman would not have put up with, and, after numerous follies, was recalled, and sent from the capital. (007-120)

Rousseau learned of Montaigu's death years later from the Gazette. He summarized his former employer with characteristic bluntness: "He was as fit for the functions of an ambassador as in my infancy I had been for those of Grapignan."

## Significance

M. de Montaigu is important to the Confessions not for his own sake but for what his story reveals about the ancien régime diplomatic service and about Rousseau himself. Montaigu was a pure product of the system of patronage and purchased offices — a man raised to high position without the least qualification for it, sustained only by court connections and the tolerance of an indifferent bureaucracy. His tenure in Venice is a case study in aristocratic incompetence: a man who could not write his own despatches insisting on "improving" those written by his secretary, a diplomat who neglected French citizens because he never thought to invite them to his table.

For Rousseau, the eighteen months in Montaigu's service were formative. It was his first sustained exposure to the world of high diplomacy and the operations of government, and it confirmed his lifelong conviction that rank and merit were inversely correlated. The experience also sharpened Rousseau's diplomatic and administrative skills — he effectively ran the embassy during his tenure — and gave him the self-confidence to demand justice from his social superiors. The elaborate documentation he assembled to refute Montaigu's charges foreshadows the methodical self-justification that would characterize the Confessions itself.
