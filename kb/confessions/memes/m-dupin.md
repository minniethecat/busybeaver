## Biography

M. Dupin (Claude Dupin) was a farmer-general — a member of the powerful corporation of tax collectors in pre-revolutionary France. His immense fortune placed him among the most opulent families in Paris. He was the father of M. de Francueil and the husband of Madame Dupin, in whose celebrated salon Rousseau first made his entry into Parisian high society.

Madame Dupin's mother had given her daughter in marriage to M. Dupin in recognition of his hospitality:

> She was the reward of the hospitality of M. Dupin, to whom her mother gave her in marriage with the place of farmer general and an immense fortune, in return for the good reception he had given her in his province. (007-053)

The Dupin family also owned the château of Chenonceaux, a royal mansion on the River Cher, where Rousseau later spent a productive autumn:

> This beautiful place, and which is now in the possession of M. Dupin, a farmer general. We amused ourselves very agreeably in this beautiful place. (008-020)

## Relation to Rousseau

The Dupin household was Rousseau's gateway into the world of Parisian wealth and culture. After his initial encounter with Madame Dupin, Rousseau was drawn into the family's orbit. M. de Francueil, M. Dupin's son, became a friend and collaborator:

> M. de Francueil, son to M. Dupin, and son-in-law to Madam Dupin, was much the same with herself and me. He had wit, a good person, and might have pretensions. (007-056)

Rousseau lodged near the Dupin residence in the Rue Platiere, and M. de Francueil engaged him to study chemistry:

> That I might be nearer at hand, I left my hotel at Quentin, and went to lodge at the Tennis Court, Rue Verdelet, which leads into the Rue Platiere, where M. Dupin lived. (007-058)

When Rousseau's early literary fame faded and he needed reliable income, he returned to the Dupin household as a kind of secretary. The household remained welcoming even after Rousseau chose to live independently:

> I had free access to the house of M. Dupin, which, less brilliant than in the young days of Madam Dupin, was still, by the merit of the heads of the family, and the choice of company which assembled there, one of the best houses in Paris. (010-116)

## Significance

M. Dupin represents the type of wealthy patron upon whom men of letters depended in eighteenth-century Paris. While Rousseau would later denounce the corrupting influence of such patronage, his own career was shaped by the opportunities the Dupin household provided — from introductions to Voltaire and Buffon to the material support that enabled his early musical and literary experiments.
