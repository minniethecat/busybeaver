## Biography

Madam de Broglie, the Marchioness of Broglie, was the daughter of the Baroness of Beuzenval and a figure in Parisian high society. Father Castel, who introduced Rousseau to her circle, described her simply as "a woman of wit" (007-043). She lived with her mother and moved in the orbit of influential figures including the President de Lamoignon, whom Rousseau described as "very intimate in the family" (007-044).

She possessed what Rousseau called mastery of "all the modish and fashionable small talk jargon of Paris" — a social language in which the awkward young Jean-Jacques found himself unable to participate (007-044). Despite this cultural gap, she became one of Rousseau's early and steadfast supporters in the capital.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's introduction to Madam de Broglie came through Father Castel, who had taken an interest in the struggling young musician. Castel advised Rousseau to "change the string, and apply to the women," telling him: "You will find at her house Madam de Broglie, her daughter, who is a woman of wit" (007-043).

When Rousseau finally summoned the courage to call on Madam de Beuzenval, the visit nearly ended in humiliation. Madam de Beuzenval, "too full of her illustrious Polish nobility," invited him to dine — but with the servants. Madam de Broglie intervened:

> Madam de Broglie approached her mother, and whispered in her ear a few words which had their effect. Madam de Beuzenval rose to prevent me from going, and said, "I expect that you will do us the honor to dine with us." (007-044)

Rousseau was deeply touched: "The goodness of Madam de Broglie had besides made an impression upon me, and rendered her interesting in my eyes. I was very glad to dine with her, and hoped, that when she knew me better, she would not regret having procured me that honor" (007-044).

During the dinner, Rousseau remained mostly silent — unable to keep up with the fashionable conversation of de Lamoignon and Madam de Broglie. But after the meal, he read a verse epistle he had written in Lyons, "which I increased by my manner of reading, and made them all three shed tears." He believed the moment vindicated her faith in him:

> Whether it was vanity, or really the truth, I thought the eyes of Madam de Broglie seemed to say to her mother: "Well, mamma, was I wrong in telling you this man was fitter to dine with us than with your women?" (007-045)

### A Mentor for Society

Convinced Rousseau would "immediately acquire fame in Paris, and become a favorite with fine ladies," Madam de Broglie gave him a book to guide his inexperience: the confessions of the Count de ---. "This book," she told him, "is a Mentor, of which you will stand in need in the great world. You will do well by sometimes consulting it" (007-045).

Rousseau kept the book for over twenty years "with a sentiment of gratitude to her from whose hand I had received it," though he also "frequently laughed at the opinion the lady seemed to have of my merit in gallantry" (007-045). The book's author later became, in Rousseau's words, "the only real friend I ever possessed amongst men of letters" (007-045).

### Career Patronage

Madam de Broglie's support extended beyond social encouragement. When the Count de Montaigu was appointed ambassador to Venice and needed a secretary, "Madam de Broglie having heard the ambassador was seeking a secretary, proposed me to him" (007-062). This position — though negotiations over salary ultimately failed — represented one of the first concrete career opportunities Rousseau's Parisian patrons attempted to secure for him.

Rousseau continued to visit both Madam de Beuzenval and Madam de Broglie regularly, and they "had not forgotten me" even as he attached himself to other patrons like the Dupin family (007-062).

## Significance

Madam de Broglie represents a type of patron essential to Rousseau's early Paris years: the society woman of intelligence and influence who recognized his talent and worked to advance it. Her intervention at the dinner table — a small but telling gesture — exemplified the social navigation required for an unknown provincial to gain a foothold in the capital's literary circles. Without the advocacy of women like Madam de Broglie, Rousseau's entry into Parisian intellectual life might never have occurred.

Her gift of the Count's confessions also offers a rare glimpse of how Rousseau was perceived by his early supporters — as a man of talent who needed guidance in the arts of society and, perhaps, gallantry. The disconnect between this perception and Rousseau's own nature would become one of the defining tensions of his life.
