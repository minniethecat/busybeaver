## Biography

Louise Éléonore de Warens was born in 1699 in Vevey, in the Pays de Vaud (now Switzerland), to the noble family of La Tour de Pit. She married very young to a M. de Warens of the house of Loys in Lausanne. The marriage was unhappy and produced no children. (002-066)

Fleeing domestic troubles, she crossed Lake Geneva and threw herself at the feet of Victor Amadeus, King of Sardinia, who was then at Evian. She converted to Catholicism and received a pension from the king, who employed her in converting others. (002-066)

She settled in Annecy, where Rousseau first met her in 1728, when she was twenty-eight years old. She was described as having a charm and grace that immediately captivated the young Rousseau. (002-068)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau met Madame de Warens on Palm Sunday 1728, and from the first glance she inspired in him "a lively attachment" and "perfect confidence." (002-072) He called her "Maman," and she became his protector, benefactress, and eventually his lover.

She converted Rousseau to Catholicism and arranged his education, sending him to Turin and later taking him into her household at Les Charmettes near Chambéry. Their years together there provided Rousseau with the stability and intellectual environment that nurtured his early development.

## Significance

Madame de Warens occupies a central place in Rousseau's _Confessions_ as the formative figure of his youth. His relationship with her shaped his views on love, education, and the nature of human attachment.

She represents the complex intertwining of maternal, romantic, and intellectual bonds that Rousseau explores throughout his autobiographical work. Her influence extended to his musical education and his early exposure to Enlightenment ideas.
