# The Hermitage Period

The Hermitage Period—Rousseau's twenty-month residence at a small cottage on the estate of [[Madame d'Epinay]] in the forest of [[Montmorency]]—was a watershed in his life and career. It was here that Rousseau made his decisive break with Parisian society, produced some of his most important literary works, and experienced the personal ruptures—with [[Denis Diderot]], [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], and Madame d'Épinay herself—that would confirm his identity as a solitary and fuel the paranoid vision of his later years.

## Background

By 1756, Rousseau had achieved literary celebrity with his _Discourse on the Sciences and the Arts_ (1750) and the _Discourse on Inequality_ (1755). Yet he chafed at the demands of Parisian literary life and the expectations of his _philosophe_ friends. Madame d'Épinay, a wealthy patron of the arts and the lover of [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], offered Rousseau the use of a cottage on her estate—an offer that Rousseau accepted with characteristic ambivalence.

> This solitary and very agreeable dwelling was situated on a rising ground in the forest of Montmorency. (009-023)

The Hermitage was a small house, "so much out of repair" that it required renovation. Yet for Rousseau, it represented the realization of a lifelong fantasy: to live simply, close to nature, free from the corrupting influence of the city and the demands of society.

## The Creative Harvest

The Hermitage Period was one of the most productive in Rousseau's life. Freed from Parisian distractions, he produced an extraordinary body of work:

| Work | Type | Status | Significance |
|------|------|--------|-------------|
| _La Nouvelle Héloïse_ | Novel | Completed at Hermitage | His masterpiece of sentiment, published 1761 |
| _Letter to d'Alembert_ | Polemic | Written Oct 1757–Mar 1758 | Defense of Geneva against theater; public break with Diderot |
| _The Morality of Sensitive Beings_ | Philosophical | Drafted | Unpublished treatise on ethics |
| Musical compositions | Creative | Ongoing | Continued work on _Le Devin du Village_ |
| Botanical studies | Scientific | Ongoing | Herb collecting in the Montmorency forest |

> I was thus provided with materials for a work which I had long meditated, and which I now began to execute with ardor. (009-061)

The work that absorbed him most completely was _La Nouvelle Héloïse_, the epistolary novel of forbidden love that would become one of the best-selling books of the 18th century. Rousseau wrote its most passionate passages while walking through the forest of [[Montmorency]], drawing inspiration from the landscape that surrounded him.

## The Rupture with Diderot and Grimm

The Hermitage Period was also the setting for the most painful personal rupture of Rousseau's life. His relationships with [[Denis Diderot]] and [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], already strained by philosophical differences, collapsed during these months.

The immediate catalyst was a line in Diderot's play _Le Fils Naturel_ (1757): "The good man lives in society; only the wicked man lives alone." Rousseau took this as a personal attack on his retreat to the Hermitage, and the correspondence between the two former friends grew increasingly bitter.

> As soon as it was known in his circle that I had retired to the Hermitage, the storm of persecution began. (009-026)

The rupture with Grimm was equally definitive. Rousseau came to believe that Grimm had turned Madame d'Épinay against him and was orchestrating a broader campaign of defamation. These suspicions, not entirely groundless, marked the beginning of Rousseau's conviction that he was the victim of a "grand conspiracy"—a belief that would dominate his later years.

The final break came in December 1757, when Rousseau was asked to leave the Hermitage after a series of quarrels with Madame d'Épinay. He departed in the dead of winter:

> My impatience to inhabit the Hermitage not permitting me to wait until the spring, I took possession of it in the month of April; and I quitted it in December, after a residence of twenty months. (009-038)

## The Legacy of the Hermitage

The Hermitage Period was brief but transformative:

1. **Literary**: It produced the core works of Rousseau's middle period and defined the themes—nature, solitude, authentic sentiment—that would characterize his mature philosophy.

2. **Personal**: It confirmed Rousseau's identity as a solitary. After the Hermitage, he would never again attempt to live as a member of a literary circle.

3. **Paranoid turn**: The ruptures of the Hermitage period seeded the persecution complex that dominates the later books of the _Confessions_. The Hermitage was both Rousseau's Eden and his Fall.

4. **Autobiographical**: The experience of living in nature, recording his feelings, and reflecting on his past directly shaped the confessional method. The Hermitage was where Rousseau learned to be alone with himself—and to write about what he found there.

> The six years I passed at the Hermitage and at Montmorency be considered as the happiest of my life. (009-042)
