## Overview

*Émile, or On Education* (1762) is [[Rousseau]]'s most comprehensive work on education and human nature. Structured as a novel following the education of a fictional boy, Émile, from infancy to adulthood, the book presents Rousseau's philosophy that children should learn through direct experience with nature rather than through books and formal instruction.

The work is divided into five books, each corresponding to a stage of Émile's development. Book IV contains the "Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar," a lengthy exposition of natural religion that provoked fierce condemnation from both Catholic and Protestant authorities. The book's publication led to its banning in [[Paris]] and [[Geneva]], and an arrest warrant was issued for Rousseau, forcing him into years of exile.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

*Émile* represented the culmination of two decades of reflection. Rousseau described it as a work that had cost him "twenty years' meditation and three years' composition." The writing was deeply personal, drawing on memories of his own unconventional education:

> Uniting M. Gatier with M. Gamu, I conceived an idea, which were deeply engraven on my heart, struck me when I wrote Emilius. [003-070]

Rousseau acknowledged that *Émile* incorporated ideas first explored in his earlier works:

> I felt, as I have remarked in my Emilius, that unless an author be a man of intrigue, when he wishes to render his works really useful... [009-054]

The book's condemnation in June 1762 marked a turning point in Rousseau's life. The [[Parliament of Paris]] ordered the book burned and its author arrested, forcing Rousseau to flee France. This began the period of persecution and exile that would dominate his later years.

## Connection to Other Works

Rousseau saw *Émile* and *[[The Social Contract]]* as complementary works, addressing respectively the education of the individual and the organization of society. Both were published in 1762 and both were condemned:

> I had six months before sent him the first two parts of my 'Eloisa' to print... The 'Social Contract' appeared a month or two before 'Emilius.' [011-023]

Rousseau intended a sequel to *Émile*, *Émile et Sophie, ou les Solitaires*, which he partially drafted but never completed, depicting the tragic failure of Émile's education when tested by the corrupting influence of Parisian society.
