## Summary

David Hume, the Scottish philosopher and historian, offered Rousseau refuge in England after the condemnation of _Émile_. Hume arranged Rousseau's passage, found him a house in Wootton in Staffordshire, and secured a pension from King George III. Their friendship appeared warm and sincere.

Within months, it collapsed. Rousseau became convinced that Hume was part of the conspiracy against him, that Hume had opened his letters, and that Hume had colluded with Rousseau's French enemies to humiliate him. "Hume joined a very republican mind to the English Paradoxes in favor of luxury," Rousseau wrote, already hinting at philosophical incompatibility. (012-088)

## Context

Rousseau arrived in England in January 1766, accompanied by Hume, who had been persuaded by Madame de Boufflers to help the persecuted philosopher. Hume "took upon himself" to assist Rousseau with "the lively friendship he expressed for me, and his anxiety to do me the honors of England." (012-088)

But Rousseau's paranoia, fueled by years of feeling persecuted by the _philosophes_, quickly alienated his protector. A satirical letter supposedly written by Horace Walpole (but actually penned by Walpole himself as a hoax) purported to be from the King of Prussia, mocking Rousseau. Rousseau believed Hume was behind it.

## Aftermath

The quarrel became a public scandal across Europe. Both men published their correspondence, appealing to the tribunal of public opinion. Hume emerged with his reputation largely intact; Rousseau's paranoia seemed confirmed by his behavior.

The rupture symbolized the impossibility of Rousseau's integration into Enlightenment society. The man who had declared "I was better than that man" could sustain no friendship that did not eventually confirm his sense of persecution.
