# Madame d'Houdetot

**Madame d'Houdetot** (1730–1813), born Élisabeth-Sophie-Françoise de Bellegarde, was the sister-in-law of [[Madame d'Épinay]] and the great romantic passion of Rousseau's life, commemorated in Book IX of the _Confessions_. She was married to the Comte d'Houdetot and was the acknowledged mistress of the poet [[Saint-Lambert]].

## Biography

Sophie was the daughter of a farmer-general and sister to M. d'Épinay. Rousseau first encountered her at La Chevrette, the country estate of the d'Épinay family:

> My acquaintance with M. D'Epinay procured me that of her sister-in-law, Mademoiselle de Bellegarde, who soon afterwards became Countess of Houdetot. The first time I saw her she was just upon the point of marriage. (008-041)

She married Claude-Constant-César, Comte d'Houdetot, and moved in the highest circles of Enlightenment society. Rousseau describes her at the time of their intense friendship in 1757:

> The Countess d'Houdetot was nearly thirty years of age, and not at all handsome; her face was marked with the small-pox; her complexion wanted freshness, and she was near-sighted to a degree of extreme. (009-134)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's passion for Madame d'Houdetot erupted in the spring of 1757, while he was living at the Hermitage, the property of her sister-in-law [[Madame d'Épinay]]. The intensity of his feelings is one of the most vividly rendered episodes in the _Confessions_:

> Then was it my eyes were opened to feel I had a heart; that I was capable of friendship, of love; that I was born for the delights of the tender passions. I could not think of anything but Madam d'Houdetot. (009-136)

Their relationship remained chaste — she was devoted to Saint-Lambert, and Rousseau respected this boundary even as his passion consumed him. The triangular situation eventually contributed to the rupture between Rousseau and the d'Épinay circle:

> She incessantly putting in mind of her duty to her lover, and keeping within the bounds of the strictest virtue. (009-143)

The emotional turmoil of this period directly fed into the writing of _[[Julie, or the New Heloise]]_, Rousseau's celebrated epistolary novel. The character of Julie was, by his own account, inspired by Madame d'Houdetot.

## Significance

Madame d'Houdetot's significance lies primarily in her role as muse and catalyst in Rousseau's emotional and creative life:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Literary inspiration | Inspired the character of Julie in _La Nouvelle Héloïse_ |
| Emotional catalyst | Awakened Rousseau's capacity for romantic love at age 45 |
| Social context | Embodied the refined sensibility of Enlightenment salon culture |
| Legacy | Her friendship with Saint-Lambert endured for decades |

She survived Rousseau by many years, dying in 1813 at the age of eighty-three. Her letters and the accounts of contemporaries suggest a woman of genuine charm and intelligence, warmer and less calculating than Rousseau's sometimes overwrought depiction might imply.
