## Biography

M. Simon was the judge major of Annecy during the period of Rousseau's youth. He was a man of natural good sense, improved by reading, and known for his sociable and gallant character. Rousseau describes him as "ever entertaining the ladies with soft tales, and carrying the decoration of his person even to foppery." (004-024)

Simon had a taste for music and poetry. He was so pleased with a couplet Rousseau composed on an air from one of Mouret's operas that he "determined to make another in answer to it." (004-021) He wished the young Venture to write one as well, so that "they might see couplets start up next day like incidents in a comedy." (004-021)

Rousseau and Venture dined with M. Simon, "who treated us very politely." The conversation was agreeable between the two men of natural good sense. Simon "appeared satisfied with my behavior; indeed, it was almost all he saw of me at this interview." (004-022) Though they had often met at [[Madame de Warens]]'s, Simon had never paid much attention to Rousseau until this dinner.

## Relation to Rousseau

M. Simon entered Rousseau's life through the circle of Madame de Warens at Annecy. Their most notable encounter was a dinner where Rousseau, still young and uncertain, listened rather than spoke — a restraint that Simon appreciated. Rousseau later recalled Simon as "my good old friend Monsieur Simon, who greatly promoted my rising" in his musical career. (006-023)

Despite Simon's flamboyant personality — he gave audiences in bed, and a visiting countryman once mistook his beribboned head-dress for a lady's, offering "the supposed lady a hundred apologies," sending Simon "in a rage" — Rousseau seems to have regarded him with genuine fondness. (004-025) The dinner at Simon's house represented one of Rousseau's early exposures to the sociable world of Annecy's provincial elite.

## Significance

M. Simon exemplifies the minor provincial figures who populated Rousseau's early world. As a judge and man of culture in Annecy, he represents the kind of enlightened provincial society that nurtured Rousseau's emerging talents. Simon's appreciation for Rousseau's musical couplet — and his playful, competitive response in kind — shows the informal literary culture of the provinces that existed alongside the more famous Parisian salons.

The episode also illustrates Rousseau's early social persona: the silent observer who impressed not through conversation but through his compositions, a pattern that would recur throughout his career in [[Paris]] and beyond.
