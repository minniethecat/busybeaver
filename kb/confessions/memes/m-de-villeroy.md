**M. de Villeroy** (the Duke of Villeroy) was a French nobleman connected by marriage to the [[M. de Luxembourg|Luxembourg]] family. He was among the high-ranking visitors who paid court to Rousseau at Mont Louis, and he consistently treated Rousseau with respect and kindness—in marked contrast to his nephew and heir, the young Marquis of Villeroy, who openly mocked Rousseau.

## Biography

M. de Villeroy was a member of the highest French aristocracy, brother to the Duchess of Luxembourg. His family was connected to the Maréchal de Luxembourg, who in 1761 lost "his sister, the Duchess of Villeroy"—a loss that deeply affected the aging maréchal. (011-018)

The Duke was among the distinguished visitors who made "the pilgrimage of Mont Louis" to visit Rousseau at his modest dwelling. Alongside M. and Madame de Luxembourg, the Duke of Villeroy, the Prince of Tingry, and others "of the first rank" climbed "a very fatiguing mountain" to pay their respects to the author. (010-164) Rousseau acknowledged that these attentions were owed entirely to the favor of the Luxembourg family.

## Relation to Rousseau

M. de Villeroy showed Rousseau "his usual marks of goodness" throughout their acquaintance. He not only came to see Rousseau in person but "had several times invited me to Villeroy." (011-026) When Rousseau's vague replies were taken as acceptance, the Duke arranged with Madame de Luxembourg a journey of a fortnight, intending Rousseau to join the party. Rousseau was obliged to decline on account of his health, but the Duke continued his benevolent treatment undiminished.

During the crisis following the condemnation of *Émile* in 1762, M. de Villeroy proved a practical ally. As Rousseau fled Paris, he passed through Villeroy and was provided with a letter from the Duke to help him avoid difficulties with the authorities in Lyons. (011-085) Rousseau ultimately did not pass through Lyons and never used the letter, which "still remains sealed up amongst my papers."

## The Young Marquis de Villeroy

The Duke's nephew and heir, the young [[Marquis of Villeroy]], was an altogether different character. Rousseau described him as "harebrained" and found his manner "insupportable." (011-026) The Marquis publicly humiliated Rousseau at a dinner by mocking him over his dog's name—Rousseau had named the dog "Duke" but changed it to "Turk" out of deference, and the Marquis seized on this to embarrass him before the assembled company, including M. de Luxembourg and his son. Rousseau noted that the Marquis's aunt severely reprimanded him the next day.

## Significance

M. de Villeroy exemplifies the genuine aristocratic patrons who respected Rousseau for his literary achievements without condescension. His consistent kindness, from the Mont Louis visits to the protective letter during Rousseau's flight, stands as a counterpoint to the intrigues and hostilities Rousseau faced from other quarters of high society. The contrast between the Duke's benevolence and his nephew's insolence also highlights the generational and personal variation within the French aristocracy.
