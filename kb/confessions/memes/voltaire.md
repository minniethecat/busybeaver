## Biography

Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet) was one of the most celebrated writers of the French Enlightenment. By the time Rousseau encountered him in the Parisian literary world, Voltaire was already a towering figure — author of numerous plays, poems, and philosophical works, and a correspondent of Frederick the Great of Prussia.

> The correspondence between Voltaire and the Prince Royal of Prussia was then much spoken of in Paris. (006-011)

Voltaire's literary output included dramatic works such as "La Princesse de Navarre," philosophical tales, and historical writings. His niece, Madame Denis, frequently accompanied him in social circles:

> The party consisted of Madam Denis, niece to Voltaire, who, at that time, was nothing more than his housekeeper, and afterwards became his residuary legatee. (008-111)

He established a residence near Geneva, which proved to be a strategic choice:

> Another reason, which contributed to my determining, was the residence Voltaire had chosen near Geneva; I easily foresaw this man would cause a revolution in the city. (009-024)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's relationship with Voltaire was complex and ultimately adversarial. Their first interaction came in 1745, when Rousseau was asked to revise Voltaire's opera libretto for "Les Fêtes de Ramire." Rousseau wrote to Voltaire requesting permission:

> Authorized by M. de Voltaire, and not under the necessity of giving myself the least concern about M. Rameau, who endeavored to injure me, I set to work, and in two months my undertaking was finished. (008-008)

Initially, Voltaire responded with great politeness, believing Rousseau to be in favor with Madame de Richelieu. Over time, however, the correspondence between them soured considerably. Rousseau later noted a crucial difference in their approaches to intellectual disputes:

> Disputes, after the manner of Voltaire. I never combat but with dignity. (012-032)

Their most famous exchange concerned the Lisbon earthquake of 1755. Rousseau wrote to Voltaire about the disaster, and Voltaire responded with his poem on the subject:

> He had printed in his journal my letter to Voltaire upon the disaster at Lisbon. (010-202)

Voltaire later published his response, which Rousseau felt misrepresented his position:

> Since that time Voltaire has published the answer he promised me, but which I have never received. This answer is nothing more nor less than the novel of Candide. (009-110)

## Significance

Voltaire stands as a pivotal figure in Rousseau's intellectual development and public image. While Rousseau admired Voltaire's literary achievements, he came to see him as emblematic of the sophisticated but morally compromised Parisian literary establishment. Their rivalry came to define a major fault line in the Enlightenment: Voltaire's urbane skepticism versus Rousseau's conviction that the arts and sciences had corrupted human virtue.

> I was more wounded by criticisms than Voltaire, was overwhelmed by them. (009-194)

Despite their mutual antagonism, Voltaire's influence permeated Rousseau's circle. Figures such as D'Alembert and Mademoiselle de Lespinasse moved between both camps:

> Mademoiselle de Lespinasse, both intimate with Voltaire and the friends of D'Alembert. (011-025)
