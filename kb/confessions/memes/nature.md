## Definition

"I mean to present my fellow-mortals with a man in all the integrity of nature; and this man shall be myself." (001-001) With these opening words, Rousseau establishes nature as the benchmark of human authenticity.

Nature, for Rousseau, is the original condition of humanity — not a historical state but a normative concept. The "natural man" is not the primitive but the uncorrupted: a being governed by self-preservation (_amour de soi_) and natural pity, before society introduces comparison, vanity, and _amour-propre_.

## In Rousseau's Thought

Rousseau's concept of nature informed his critiques of civilization, education, and politics. The _Discourse on Inequality_ (1755), a work "more to the taste of Diderot than any of my other writings," traced human corruption to the invention of property. (008-141)

_Émile_ (1762) proposed an education "according to nature" — protecting the child from society's corrupting influence until the natural conscience is strong enough to resist it. The _Social Contract_ sought political institutions that could restore something of natural freedom within civil society.

In the _Confessions_, nature appears as a personal refuge. Rousseau's solitary walks and botanical studies offered a return to natural contentment, away from "the persecutions of her children." (009-042)

## Broader Context

Rousseau's concept of nature broke with both Hobbes (for whom the state of nature was a war of all against all) and the Enlightenment faith in progress. For Rousseau, civilization had not improved humanity but corrupted it.

This counter-narrative of decline rather than progress made Rousseau a foundational figure for Romanticism and modern environmental thought. His insistence on nature as the standard of value remains influential in debates about technology, education, and ecological ethics.
