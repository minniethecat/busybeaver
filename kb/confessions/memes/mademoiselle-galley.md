## Biography

Mademoiselle Galley was a young French woman living near Annecy during Rousseau's youth. She is known to us exclusively through Rousseau's account in Book IV of the *Confessions*, where he describes a memorable day spent in her company. Rousseau encountered her by chance while walking in the countryside:

> I turn round, hear myself called by name, and approaching, find two young people of my acquaintance, Mademoiselle de G—— and Mademoiselle Galley, who, not being very excellent horsewomen, could not manage their horses to cross the water. (004-009)

Rousseau describes her physical appearance and character in admiring terms. She was one year younger than her companion Mademoiselle de G——, and he found her:

> handsomer, more delicate, more ingenious, and to complete all, extremely well made. (004-010)

The two young women were close friends: "They loved each other tenderly, and the good disposition of both could not fail to render their union durable, if some lover did not derange it" (004-010).

## Relation to Rousseau

The encounter with Mademoiselle Galley occurred during a period when Rousseau was living with Madame de Warens. During a country walk, he happened upon Galley and her friend Mademoiselle de G——, who invited him to spend the day with them. The trio shared a picnic in an orchard, and Rousseau was deeply affected by Galley's presence:

> Had I pleased myself, I should certainly have reversed this order, for the glance of Mademoiselle Galley had reached my heart, but I dared not mention it. (004-017)

Rousseau's attraction was intense but restrained by shyness. He describes a moment of innocent intimacy: "I was once kissing the hand of Mademoiselle Galley" (004-016). After the day ended, he became preoccupied with finding her again:

> As soon as I found myself at liberty, I ran into the street where Mademoiselle Galley lived, flattering myself that I should see someone go in or out. (004-028)

Despite his feelings, Rousseau never pursued her directly. He wrote instead to Mademoiselle de G——, considering it "more proper to begin with her to whom I owed the acquaintance" (004-029). Much later, in Book IX, he still recalled her among the figures of his youth who had "given me emotion" (009-102).

## Significance

Mademoiselle Galley represents the type of idealized romantic encounter that recurs throughout the *Confessions*. The episode illustrates Rousseau's characteristic pattern of intense but unconsummated attraction, where his imagination and sensibility overwhelm his capacity for decisive action. The detailed rendering of the day — the cherry orchard, the shared meal, the ride home — exemplifies Rousseau's ability to transform a brief encounter into a lasting literary tableau. Galley herself remains elusive, known only through Rousseau's retrospective gaze, making her a representative figure of the lost pleasures of his youth.
