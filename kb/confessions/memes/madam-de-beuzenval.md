## Biography

Madam de Beuzenval was a baroness of Polish nobility residing in Paris.
She was the mother of Madam de Broglie (the Marchioness of Broglie) and was connected to the Polish community in the French capital through her son and husband.

Rousseau described her as "a very good kind of woman, but of a confined understanding, and too full of her illustrious Polish nobility," adding that "she had no idea of the respect due to talents." (007-044)

## Relation to Rousseau

### First Introduction

Rousseau was introduced to Madam de Beuzenval by Father Castel, the Jesuit mathematician who was one of his earliest Parisian connections.
Castel, seeing Rousseau's musical notation system was not succeeding with the academic establishment, advised him to "change the string, and apply to the women."
He told Rousseau: "I have spoken of you to Madam de Beuzenval; go to her from me; she is a good woman who will be glad to see the countryman of her son and husband." (007-043)

### The Servants' Hall Incident

Rousseau's first visit to Madam de Beuzenval became one of the memorable humiliations of his early Paris years.
She received him kindly, and when Rousseau prepared to leave around one o'clock, she invited him to stay and dine.
A quarter of an hour later, Rousseau "understood, by a word, that the dinner to which she had invited me was that of her servants' hall."

Rousseau was stung: "I had too long forgotten the way to the place where they eat to be inclined to take it again."
He made an excuse to leave, but Madam de Broglie, realizing the awkwardness, approached her mother and "whispered in her ear a few words which had their effect."
Madam de Beuzenval then rose to prevent his departure, saying, "I expect that you will do us the honor to dine with us."
Rousseau, judging that "to show pride would be a mark of folly," decided to stay. (007-044)

### Securing the Venice Position

Despite this awkward beginning, Madam de Beuzenval and her daughter Madam de Broglie proved genuinely helpful.
When the Count de Montaigu was appointed ambassador to Venice and was seeking a secretary, "Madam de Broglie having heard the ambassador was seeking a secretary, proposed me to him." (007-062)
This led to Rousseau's appointment as secretary to the French embassy in Venice, a position that — despite its eventual disastrous end — gave Rousseau his first experience of diplomatic life and public affairs.

### The Falling-Out

After Rousseau's quarrel with Ambassador Montaigu and his return to Paris, he found that Madam de Beuzenval's support had vanished.
"The only person by whom I was ill received, and from whom I should have least expected such an injustice, was Madam de Beuzenval.
Full of the prerogatives of rank and nobility, she could not conceive it was possible an ambassador could ever be in the wrong with respect to his secretary."

Rousseau's response was characteristically fierce: "I was so piqued at it that, immediately after leaving her, I wrote her perhaps one of the strongest and most violent letters that ever came from my pen, and since that time I never once returned to her house." (007-119)

### A Late Recollection

Years later, when the Maréchal de Luxembourg invited Rousseau to supper at his castle at Montmorency with repeated politeness, Rousseau drew an explicit contrast: "This called to my recollection Madam Beuzenval sending me to dine in the servants' hall.
Times were changed; but I was still the same man." (010-138)

## Significance

Madam de Beuzenval exemplifies the ambiguous role of aristocratic patronage in Rousseau's early career.
She was genuinely helpful — it was through her household, and specifically her daughter's advocacy, that Rousseau obtained the Venice secretaryship.
Yet her help was always tempered by the casual condescension of the old nobility toward talent without rank.

The servants' hall episode became for Rousseau a symbolic moment: it crystallized his awareness that, in the Paris of the ancien régime, a man of genius without birth was never quite a guest at the table.
His later satisfaction in refusing the Luxembourg invitations — "I did not choose to be sent to dine in the servants' hall, and was but little desirous of appearing at the table of the great" — shows how deeply Beuzenval's slight had marked him. (010-138)
