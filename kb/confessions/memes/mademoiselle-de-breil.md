Mademoiselle de Breil was a young noblewoman at the household of the Count de Gauvon in [[Turin]], where Rousseau served as a footman in his youth. She became the object of Rousseau's first romantic infatuation within the aristocratic world.

## Biography

Mademoiselle de Breil was the daughter of the Marquis de Breil and granddaughter (or daughter-in-law) of the Count de Gauvon, the First Equerry to the Queen of Sardinia:

> ...he went to the Marchioness de Breil, his daughter-in-law... (003-008)

She was roughly Rousseau's age when they met, and he described her appearance favorably:

> Mademoiselle de Breil was about my own age, tolerably handsome... (003-010)

The Breil family was part of the high aristocracy in Turin, connected to the royal court of [[Sardinia]].

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau, then a young footman in the Gauvon household, developed a secret admiration for Mademoiselle de Breil. During a dinner when the guests struggled to complete a Latin motto on the Gauvon family arms, Rousseau — who had been studying Latin — offered the correct reading. This display of learning astonished the company:

> Mademoiselle de Breil was about my own age, tolerably handsome... The explanation I had given of the motto was not lost upon her. (003-010)

Her approving glance at Rousseau made a deep impression on him and stirred his early romantic sensibilities. However, as a servant, Rousseau could not act on his feelings. His position in the antechamber of Madam de Breil was humiliating:

> ...in the antechamber of Madam de Breil, I could not obtain one mark of attention... (003-011)

The disdain shown by Madam de Breil was compensated by the kindness of others in the household:

> The disdain of Madam de Breil was fully compensated by the kindness of... (003-012)

Later in life, Rousseau listed Mademoiselle de Breil alongside other women from his youth when reminiscing about his past attachments:

> ...Mademoiselle de Graffenried, Mademoiselle de Breil, [[Madam Basile]], [[Madam de Larnage]]... (009-102)

## Significance

Mademoiselle de Breil represents one of Rousseau's earliest experiences of social aspiration and romantic longing within the rigid class hierarchy of the ancien régime. The episode at the Gauvon household — where his intellectual ability briefly elevated him above his station — prefigures themes that run throughout the Confessions: the tension between natural talent and social position, and the power of a woman's approving glance to shape a young man's destiny. She is part of the constellation of early female figures, alongside [[Madam Basile]] and [[Madam de Warens]], who influenced Rousseau's emotional development.
