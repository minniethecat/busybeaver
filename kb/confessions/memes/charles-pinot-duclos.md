## Biography

Charles Pinot Duclos was a French writer and historian who held the position of perpetual secretary of the Académie Française. In the Parisian literary world of the 1750s, Duclos stood apart for his integrity and "wise severity" — qualities that earned him Rousseau's deep respect.

> Another acquaintance which, as soon as I had any pretensions to it, was converted into friendship, was that of M. Duclos. (008-101)

Rousseau first met Duclos at the Chevrette, the country house of Madame d'Épinay. Duclos, impressed by Rousseau's talents, actively sought his acquaintance. Rousseau, held back by timidity, was eventually drawn to him.

> Duclos, endowed with too great talents not to be a friend to those in whom the like were found, was prepossessed in my favor, and invited me to go and see him. (008-101)

Through this friendship, Rousseau learned a lesson that shaped his outlook: "uprightness and probity may sometimes be connected with the cultivation of letters."

## Relation to Rousseau

Duclos proved to be one of Rousseau's most steadfast allies during the turbulent period surrounding the "Devin du Village" and the subsequent cabal against him. When Rousseau feared presenting his opera under his own name after the failure of the "Muses Gallantes," Duclos intervened decisively:

> Duclos relieved me from this difficulty, and engaged to get the piece rehearsed without mentioning the author. (008-113)

Duclos further defended Rousseau's interests when M. de Cury demanded the piece for court performance:

> Duclos, who knew my intentions, and thought I should be less master of my work at the court than at Paris, refused to give it. (008-116)

In gratitude, Rousseau dedicated the "Devin du Village" to Duclos, declaring it to be his only dedication.

As the intrigues of Grimm and Diderot intensified, they tried to recruit Duclos to their side. He refused:

> They likewise wished to gain over Duclos and d'Holbach, but the former constantly refused their proposals. (008-130)

When a faction formed against Rousseau, Duclos remained loyal:

> Duclos, the most worthy of my confidence, was entreated by the confederates to join them. They made the greatest efforts to make Duclos enter into their views; but these he invariably rejected. (009-217–009-218)

After Rousseau's retreat from Paris, Duclos was the one friend he still counted among men of letters:

> Duclos, of whom since my retreat to the Hermitage I had entirely given up the acquaintance, was the only one of the men of letters, amongst whom Duclos, was the only friend I reckoned as such. (010-009, 010-114)

## Significance

Duclos represents a rare figure in Rousseau's memoirs: a literary man who combined talent with genuine integrity. At a time when Rousseau increasingly saw the world of letters as corrupt, Duclos served as the exception that proved the rule — a man who refused to join conspiracies, who honored friendship over faction, and whose "wise severity" inspired respect even in Rousseau's most embittered years.
