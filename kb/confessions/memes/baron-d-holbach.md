# Baron d'Holbach

**Paul-Henri Thiry, Baron d'Holbach** (1723–1789) was a German-born French philosopher, encyclopedist, and a central figure of the French Enlightenment. He was the host of the celebrated _coterie holbachique_, a salon that gathered the leading intellectuals of the age. Rousseau knew him during his Paris years and later fell out with him during the crisis of 1757.

## Biography

Born Paul Heinrich Dietrich in the Palatinate, he was raised and educated in Paris after being adopted by his wealthy uncle. He inherited the title and fortune that enabled him to become one of the Enlightenment's great patrons. Rousseau describes his background:

> This said baron was the son of a man of no rank, who had acquired great riches, and who left a considerable fortune to his son, which he had well deserved. (008-100)

His salon became the intellectual hub of radical Enlightenment thought, frequented by [[Denis Diderot]], [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], d'Alembert, Helvétius, and many others:

> All my friends became his: this was natural: but not one of his friends ever became mine. (008-097)

D'Holbach was a prolific author and translator, producing numerous works of materialist philosophy and scientific treatises. His most famous work, _The System of Nature_ (1770), presented a thoroughgoing materialist and atheistic worldview that shocked even many of his Enlightenment contemporaries.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's relationship with d'Holbach was marked by mutual suspicion and eventual rupture. He was introduced to the Holbach circle through [[Madame d'Épinay]] and [[Denis Diderot]], but always felt like an outsider:

> I had become connected almost in spite of myself. (008-097)

The tension was partly intellectual — Rousseau's deism and emotionalism clashed with Holbachian materialism — and partly personal. Rousseau came to believe, rightly or wrongly, that d'Holbach was part of a conspiracy against him:

> The idea of M. d'Holbach's having done it was so extraordinary that it was some time before it entered my head. (009-029)

> I was considered as a cipher in everything; and this not only in the company of Madam D'Epinay, but in that of M. d'Holbach, and in every place where Grimm gave the 'ton'. (009-062)

## Significance

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Major works | _The System of Nature_ (1770), _The Social System_ (1773), numerous scientific translations |
| Salon | The _coterie holbachique_, center of radical Enlightenment |
| Philosophy | Systematic materialism, atheism, determinism |
| Encyclopédie | Major contributor of scientific and technical articles |
| Rousseau connection | Represented the "philosophical party" Rousseau came to distrust |

D'Holbach represents the wing of the French Enlightenment most opposed to Rousseau's sensibility — rationalist, materialist, and cosmopolitan where Rousseau was sentimental, deistic, and drawn to rustic simplicity. Their mutual incomprehension illustrates a fundamental divide within eighteenth-century thought.
