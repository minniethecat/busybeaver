The Chevalier de Lorenzy was a French courtier and one of [[Rousseau]]'s friends in Paris during the late 1750s. He belonged to the court of [[France]] and was part of the intellectual circle that included Rousseau, [[Denis Diderot|Diderot]], and other Enlightenment figures.

## Biography

The Chevalier de Lorenzy was attached to the court of France and moved in aristocratic and literary circles. He was a skilled chess player, reportedly surpassing Rousseau:

> I knew he beat the Chevalier de Lorenzy, who played better than I did. (011-004)

He maintained connections with prominent figures of the Parisian intellectual scene and frequented the Temple, a social center for literary figures.

## Relation to Rousseau

The Chevalier de Lorenzy was one of the few friends who remained loyal to Rousseau during the controversies surrounding his works. Rousseau mentions him favorably on multiple occasions, describing him as someone who "professed himself my friend" even when others distanced themselves:

> ...nor at the Temple, except the Chevalier de Lorenzy, who professed himself my friend. (011-027)

When Rousseau was considering writing projects, de Lorenzy actively encouraged him:

> ...this however did not prevent the Chevalier de Lorenzy from proposing to me to write... (011-023)

De Lorenzy also acted as an intermediary between Rousseau and [[M. de Malesherbes]], speaking favorably of Rousseau's work to the censor:

> ...to M. de Malesherbes and the Chevalier de Lorenzy, who spoke favorably of it. (011-035)

He frequently visited Rousseau, accompanied at times by other acquaintances:

> ...frequently to see me with the Chevalier de Lorenzy. She was yet young and beautiful... (011-006)

Even after Rousseau's troubles intensified, de Lorenzy maintained contact, and was among Rousseau's numerous visitors:

> ...visitors were numerous. The Chevalier de Lorenzy had even written it down to give... (012-009)

Rousseau also recalled sharing personal matters with him, about which de Lorenzy responded with humor:

> I mentioned this to the Chevalier de Lorenzy, who laughed at me... (010-130)

## Significance

The Chevalier de Lorenzy represents the type of aristocratic friend and patron who supported Rousseau during his most difficult years in Paris. Unlike figures such as [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm|Grimm]], who eventually turned against Rousseau, de Lorenzy appears to have maintained his friendship and offered practical assistance, including facilitating communication with Malesherbes. His presence in Rousseau's narrative illustrates the network of courtly connections that sustained Rousseau's literary career before his break with Parisian society.
