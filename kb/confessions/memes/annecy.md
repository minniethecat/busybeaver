## Overview

Annecy is a town in the Duchy of Savoy (now in southeastern France), situated at the northern tip of Lake Annecy. In the 18th century, it was under the rule of the Kingdom of Sardinia and was a center of Catholic missionary activity aimed at converting Protestants.

Rousseau arrived in Annecy on Palm Sunday 1728, directed there by M. de Pontverre, who told him he would find "a good and charitable lady, whom the bounty of the king enables to turn souls from those errors she has happily renounced." (002-061) This lady was Madame de Warens.

## In Rousseau's Life

Annecy represents the decisive turning point in Rousseau's youth. At sixteen, having fled Geneva and his apprenticeship, he arrived in the town and met Madame de Warens. "At length I arrived at Annecy, and saw Madam de Warens," he records simply. (002-061)

The meeting transformed Rousseau's life. Madame de Warens took him in, arranged for his conversion to Catholicism, and sent him to Turin for further instruction. After his return from Turin, he lived in her household in Annecy, receiving an education in music, letters, and society.

The town also hosted a small Catholic seminary where Rousseau briefly studied, preparing for the priesthood — a path he quickly abandoned.

## Historical Context

In the 1720s, Annecy was part of the Duchy of Savoy, which had been ceded to the Kingdom of Sardinia in 1720. The town was a significant center for the Counter-Reformation effort to reclaim Protestant souls, with the Bishop of Geneva (in exile at Annecy) and King Victor Amadeus financing missionary work.

The "good and charitable lady" system was a formal program: the King of Sardinia paid pensions to Catholic converts who would in turn proselytize to visiting Protestants. Madame de Warens was one such pensioner, receiving approximately 1,500 livres annually for this purpose.
