## Biography

Étienne François, Duke de Choiseul, was one of the most powerful French statesmen of the mid-eighteenth century, serving as minister of foreign affairs and later as secretary of state for war and the navy. He had studied under the Jesuits in his youth (011-048). Rousseau's assessment of him was shaped by both personal gratitude and political admiration, though it would later darken into suspicion.

Rousseau first took note of Choiseul through the "family compact" — the alliance between France and Spain — which appeared to him "to evince a statesman of the first order" (011-023). This positive impression was reinforced when Choiseul expressed interest in Rousseau's diplomatic abilities:

> During the residence of M. de Luxembourg at Montmorency, M. de Choiseul sometimes came to supper at the castle. He arrived there one day after I had left it. My name was mentioned, and M. de Luxembourg related to him what had happened at Venice between me and M. de Montaigu. M. de Choiseul said it was a pity I had quitted that track, and that if I chose to enter it again he would most willingly give me employment. (011-023)

This gesture of a powerful minister offering to revive Rousseau's diplomatic career made a deep impression. Rousseau wrote that it "gained him my attachment" and that ambition — which rarely held power over him — might have driven him to accept, had his health permitted (011-023).

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's relationship with Choiseul evolved through distinct phases: initial admiration, literary praise, and eventual suspicion.

### The Social Contract Eulogy

Rousseau's most significant public engagement with Choiseul came through his writing. While composing the conclusion of the *Social Contract*, he included a passage praising Choiseul's administration in implicit but identifiable terms. He acknowledged acting "contrary to my most constant maxim" by mixing praise and censure of ministers without naming names, believing his language was sufficiently clear to those who needed to understand it (011-023).

When rumors later circulated that Rousseau had spoken ill of Choiseul in the *Social Contract*, M. de Luxembourg confronted him directly. Rousseau's response was vehement:

> "I?" said I, retreating a few steps with surprise; "no, I swear to you I have not; but on the contrary, I have made on him, and with a pen not given to praise, the finest eulogium a minister ever received." (011-067)

Rousseau had been confident that "M. de Choiseul, already well disposed towards me, and sensible of the eulogium of his administration, which my esteem for him had induced me to make in the work, would support me against the malevolence of Madam de Pompadour" (011-053).

### Rivalry with Madam de Pompadour

Rousseau positioned Choiseul in opposition to [[Madam de Pompadour]], for whom he had felt "an antipathy" even before her elevation to favor. When it was reported that one of the two would expel the other from power, Rousseau wrote: "I thought I offered up prayers for the honor of France when I wished that M. de Choiseul might triumph" (011-023). The Chevalier de Lorenzy once proposed that Rousseau write something in praise of Pompadour to gain advantage — a suggestion that "excited my indignation" (011-023).

### The Corsica Affair

During Rousseau's Swiss exile, Choiseul reappeared in the context of Corsican affairs. M. Buttafuoco, a Corsican patriot, had "frequent journeys to Versailles and Fontainebleau" and "conversations with M. de Choiseul" regarding France's intentions toward Corsica (012-144). Rousseau was uneasy about French intervention but was reassured by Buttafuoco.

### Suspicions of Persecution

Rousseau's view of Choiseul darkened dramatically during his exile. He came to believe the minister was orchestrating his persecution:

> I had always suspected M. de Choiseul to be the secret author of all the persecutions I suffered in Switzerland. The conduct of the resident of Geneva, and that of the ambassador at Soleure but too much confirmed my suspicion; I perceived the secret influence of France in everything that happened to me at Berne, Geneva and Neuchatel, and I did not think I had any powerful enemy in that kingdom, except the Duke de Choiseul. (012-155)

This accusation marked the final transformation of a relationship that had begun with mutual admiration and ended in Rousseau's conviction that the minister was the hidden hand behind his misfortunes.

## Significance

M. de Choiseul embodies the complex and often treacherous relationship between Enlightenment intellectuals and the absolutist state. Rousseau's trajectory with Choiseul — from grateful admirer to suspicious accuser — mirrors the broader arc of his relationship with the French establishment. Choiseul was both a potential patron and, in Rousseau's later view, a persecutor.

The episode also illustrates Rousseau's political naivety: his belief that a literary eulogy would earn a minister's protection, and his later conclusion that the same minister was the hidden architect of his suffering, both reflect a mind that interpreted political actions through the lens of personal loyalty and betrayal rather than statecraft.
