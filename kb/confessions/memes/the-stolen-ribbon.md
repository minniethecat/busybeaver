## Summary

While employed in the household of Madame de Vercellis in Turin, the young Rousseau stole a small pink and silver ribbon. When the theft was discovered, he falsely accused Marion, a young Mauriennese cook, of giving it to him. (002-149)

Marion was sent for. In front of many witnesses, Rousseau "accused her boldly." She remained "confused and speechless," unable to defend herself against the accusation from a boy she had always treated with kindness. (002-150) Both were dismissed.

## Context

Rousseau was about sixteen years old, recently converted to Catholicism and placed in Madame de Vercellis's household. He was socially awkward, insecure, and "perplexed" when confronted about the ribbon. (002-149) Rather than admit his theft, he seized on Marion as a convenient scapegoat.

Marion was a young woman from Maurienne, described by Rousseau as "not only pretty, but had that freshness of color only to be found among the mountains" with "an air of modesty and sweetness." (002-150) She had been the cook and was respected in the household.

## Aftermath

The incident haunted Rousseau for the rest of his life. He identifies it as "the weight" that "has remained heavy on my conscience to this day." (002-151) He confesses that the desire to relieve himself of this burden was one of the motivations for writing the _Confessions_ themselves.

"Poor Marion has found so many avengers in this world," Rousseau writes, "that however great my offence towards her, I do not fear to bear the guilt with me." (002-153) The episode has become the most famous example of Rousseau's confessional project — his willingness to expose even the most shameful acts as proof of his radical sincerity.
