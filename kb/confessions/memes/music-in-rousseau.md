## Definition

Music was Rousseau's first intellectual passion, preceding his philosophical and literary fame. He credited his aunt Suzanne with instilling his love for melody: "Though my taste, or rather passion, for music, did not show itself until a considerable time after, I am fully persuaded it is to her I am indebted for it." (001-039)

For Rousseau, music was not merely an art but a mode of emotional expression and social connection. He developed a distinctive theory of melody as the vehicle of human passion, in opposition to Rameau's emphasis on harmony.

## In Rousseau's Thought

Rousseau's musical career encompassed composition, theory, and notation. He devised a new system of musical notation, wrote the opera _Le Devin du Village_ (performed before the King at Fontainebleau), and contributed the musical articles to the _Encyclopédie_. His "passion for music" began to show itself during his time in Turin. (002-120)

His _Letter on French Music_ (1753), written after attending a performance that convinced him of Italian superiority, sparked a national controversy (008-056) by arguing that French was inherently unmusical compared to Italian. This polemic established him as a major cultural critic and earned him enemies among the Parisian musical establishment.

Rousseau's theoretical work _Essay on the Origin of Languages_ linked music to the birth of human speech, arguing that the first languages were sung, not spoken — an idea that influenced later Romantic theories of art.

## Broader Context

In the 18th century, music was a central part of salon culture and intellectual debate. The Querelle des Bouffons (1752–1754), a pamphlet war over French versus Italian opera, divided Parisian society. Rousseau's intervention on the Italian side positioned him against Rameau, the leading French composer-theorist.

Rousseau's musical thinking influenced the development of opera and the Romantic conception of music as the most direct expression of emotion.
