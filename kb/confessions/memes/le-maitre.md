## Biography

Le Maitre was a Parisian musician who served as music master at the Annecy Cathedral. A lively and talented composer, he was described by Rousseau as "a good composer, very lively, gay, young, well made, of little understanding, but, upon the whole, a good sort of man" (003-075). He directed the weekly concerts at Madame de Warens's house and took Rousseau under his tutelage.

> A convenient opportunity offered; once a week, at least, she had a concert at her house, and the music-master from the cathedral, who directed this little band, came frequently to see her. This was a Parisian, named M. le Maitre, a good composer, very lively, gay, young, well made, of little understanding, but, upon the whole, a good sort of man. (003-075)

Le Maitre's position at the cathedral brought him into conflict with the canons, particularly the Abbe de Vidonne, who treated him with disdain due to his inferior social rank. The situation escalated during Passion Week, leading Le Maitre to resolve to abandon his post.

> M. le Maitre could not bear these indignities patiently; and this year, during passion week, they had a more serious dispute than ordinary. At an institution dinner that the bishop gave the canons, and to which M. Maitre was always invited, the abbe failed in some formality, adding, at the same time, some harsh words, which the other could not digest; he instantly formed the resolution to quit them the following Easter. (003-083)

After leaving Annecy, Le Maitre traveled toward Lyons with Rousseau, carrying a heavy case of his music compositions. He suffered from epileptic seizures during the journey. Later, his precious music—the work of his lifetime—was seized at Lyons through the intervention of Count Dortan and the Cathedral chapter.

> His box of music, containing all his fortune, that precious box, preserved with so much care and fatigue, had been seized on at Lyons by means of Count Dortan, who had received information from the Chapter of our having absconded with it. In vain did Le Maitre reclaim his property, his means of existence, the labor of his life. (004-001)

> Thus poor Le Maitre lost the fruit of his talents, the labor of his youth, and principal dependence for the support of old age. (004-001)

## Relation to Rousseau

Le Maitre became Rousseau's first formal music teacher during the winter of 1730–1731 at Annecy. Introduced by Madame de Warens, Rousseau lived at Le Maitre's chambers and studied music under his direction. Rousseau recalled this as a period of great contentment.

> Madam de Warens made us acquainted; I attached myself to him, and he seemed not displeased with me. A pension was talked of, and agreed on; in short, I went home with him, and passed the winter the more agreeably at his chambers, as they were not above twenty paces distant from Madam de Warens', where we frequently supped together. (003-075)

When Le Maitre decided to flee Annecy, Madame de Warens arranged for Rousseau to accompany him to Lyons. However, at the end of the journey, Le Maitre suffered an epileptic seizure on the street, and Rousseau abandoned him—an act he later judged harshly.

> As to my desertion of Le Maitre (all things considered) I did not find it so very culpable. I had been serviceable to him at his retreat; it was not in my power to give him any further assistance. Had I remained with him in France it would not have cured his complaint. (004-002)

Rousseau acknowledged that the five or six months spent with Le Maitre improved his musical abilities, though not enough to qualify him as a professional teacher.

> If the five or six months passed with Le Maitre had improved me, they could not be supposed sufficient to qualify me for such an undertaking. (004-043)

## Significance

Le Maitre represents one of the earliest professional influences on Rousseau's musical education. His role in the *Confessions* illustrates the precarious position of talented artisans in the rigid social hierarchy of the ancien régime, where a musician of genuine ability could be humiliated by the nobility of the Church. His tragic fate—the loss of his life's work through legal machinations—serves as a minor but poignant example of injustice in Rousseau's narrative.

The music seized from Le Maitre later figured in a concert arranged by Madame de Warens, where Father Cato—the Cordelier who had helped cause the seizure—was among the guests (005-046), illustrating the small-world connections and moral complexities that thread through Rousseau's autobiographical account.
