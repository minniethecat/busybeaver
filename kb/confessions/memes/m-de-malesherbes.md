Chrétien-Guillaume de Lamoignon de Malesherbes was a French statesman who served as director of the _Cour des aides_ and chief censor of the royal book trade. During Rousseau's most controversial period, [[M. de Malesherbes]] acted as a crucial protector, shielding his works from suppression and maintaining a personal correspondence with the author that revealed a mutual respect rare between an official of the _ancien régime_ and a philosopher under surveillance.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

Malesherbes first came into Rousseau's orbit during the publication of _[[Émile]]_ and _[[The Social Contract]]_. As director of the book trade, Malesherbes held the power to authorize or suppress publications. Rather than blocking Rousseau's works, he took a personal interest in them, writing to the author and offering a channel of communication that bypassed the usual bureaucratic channels.

> Rousseau communicated his manuscript to Malesherbes directly, an act of trust uncommon for a writer facing official censure. (010-128)

Rousseau came to regard Malesherbes as "a man whose uprightness was never in question" (010-129), a judgment he would repeat throughout the _Confessions_. When the [[Condemnation of Émile]] forced Rousseau to flee, Malesherbes's support did not waver.

## Correspondence and Protection

The letters between Rousseau and Malesherbes form a substantial body of correspondence, including the four famous autobiographical letters Rousseau wrote to Malesherbes in 1762, which became foundational documents for the _Confessions_ itself. These letters detailed Rousseau's character, his motivations, and his self-understanding — material later reworked into the memoirs.

> Rousseau later noted that Malesherbes himself did not write all the replies attributed to him, suggesting that some of the letters Rousseau received were drafted by intermediaries. (011-032)

Despite this, Rousseau maintained that the correspondence was invaluable. He read portions of it to trusted friends, including the Chevalier de Lorenzy (011-035). When Rousseau came to believe that his letters to Malesherbes had been tampered with or circulated without his consent, the breach caused him genuine distress (011-061).

## Relationship with Madam de Luxembourg

Malesherbes was drawn into the circle of [[Madam de Luxembourg]], who recognized his importance to Rousseau's safety. In 1762, as the [[Parlement of Paris]] moved against Rousseau, Madam de Luxembourg sought to enlist Malesherbes's aid.

> She found means to make M. de Malesherbes enter into her views. He wrote on Rousseau's behalf and used his influence to mitigate the severity of the proceedings. (010-182)

Rousseau, however, grew suspicious of the alliance between Malesherbes and [[Madam de Luxembourg]]. He "did not perceive in what manner" they might coordinate against him (011-069), but the ambiguity of their relationship unsettled him. When Malesherbes was away in the country during critical moments, Rousseau felt exposed to dangers that only Malesherbes's personal intervention could deflect (011-048, 011-066).

## Historical Significance

Malesherbes occupies a distinctive position in Enlightenment history: a royal official who protected the very philosophers the regime sought to suppress. His relationship with Rousseau exemplifies the porous boundary between authority and dissent in pre-revolutionary France. Later in life, Malesherbes would defend [[Louis XVI]] at his trial — an act of principle that cost him his life during the Terror. Rousseau, who died in 1778, did not live to see his protector guillotined in 1794, but the consistent courage Malesherbes displayed in both episodes testifies to the character Rousseau admired.
