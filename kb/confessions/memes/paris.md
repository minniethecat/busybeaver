# Paris

Paris is the great antagonist-city of Rousseau's _Confessions_—the place where he achieved everything he sought and then rejected everything he achieved. It was in [[Paris]] that Rousseau arrived as an obscure Genevan musician in 1742; it was there that he became a celebrated author and a central figure of the [[Enlightenment]]; and it was from Paris that he fled, first to [[Montmorency]] and then into exile, convinced that the city's literary and social establishment had united in a conspiracy against him. No place in the _Confessions_ is as densely layered with meaning, or as ambivalently rendered, as Paris.

## Rousseau's Paris

### Arrival and Early Years

Rousseau first entered Paris in his late twenties, carrying little more than a system of musical notation and an introduction to influential figures. His first impressions were colored by disappointment:

> I had imagined a city as beautiful as it was grand, of the most imposing appearance, where nothing was to be seen but superb streets and palaces of marble and gold. On entering by the Faubourg Saint-Marceau, I perceived nothing but dirty, stinking little streets, hideous black houses, an air of filthiness and poverty. (001-051)

This disjunction between expectation and reality—between the ideal city of the imagination and the actual city of experience—would prove thematic. Paris repeatedly promised more than it delivered, and Rousseau's relationship with the city oscillated between infatuation and disgust.

### The World of Letters

Paris was the center of the European Republic of Letters, and it was here that Rousseau met the men who would shape his intellectual life: [[Denis Diderot]], [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], d'Alembert, Condillac, and the circle of the [[Encyclopédie]].

> It was in Paris: I was walking with M. de Francueil, with whom I was then living in the capacity of secretary. (002-038)

Through the salon of [[Madame d'Epinay]] and the Dupin household, Rousseau gained access to a world of refinement and intellect that his Genevan upbringing could never have provided. Yet he never felt entirely at home in this world. His famous essay on the corruption of the arts and sciences—written for a prize competition he learned about while walking to Vincennes—was, in part, a sublimated critique of the Parisian literary culture that had adopted him.

### The City as Moral Danger

In the _Confessions_, Paris functions as a kind of moral test that Rousseau both passes and fails. He passes insofar as he resists the city's corruptions—refusing a royal pension, maintaining his independence, fleeing to the [[Hermitage Period|Hermitage]]. He fails insofar as the city seduces him into behaviors he later regrets: the abandonment of his children, the theatrical self-display of salon life, the indulgence in _amour-propre_.

> I was a long time at Paris, wishing to meet with two companions of my own humor. (002-092)

The search for "companions of my own humor" in a city of three-quarters of a million souls—and the failure of that search—is the Parisian tragedy in miniature. Paris offered Rousseau everything except what he most wanted: authentic friendship on terms of equality.

## Paris in the Conspiracy Narrative

After 1762, Paris became the imagined headquarters of Rousseau's persecution. The Parlement of Paris condemned _[[Émile]]_ and issued a warrant for his arrest. The literary establishment—[[Denis Diderot]], [[Friedrich Melchior Grimm]], [[Madame d'Epinay]]—seemed to Rousseau to be orchestrating a campaign of defamation from their Parisian salons.

Whether this conspiracy was real or imagined has been debated for two centuries. What is beyond dispute is that Paris served, in the symbolic geography of the _Confessions_, as the antithesis of the natural and the authentic:

| Paris | Nature |
|-------|--------|
| Society | Solitude |
| Artifice | Sincerity |
| _Amour-propre_ | _Amour de soi_ |
| Corruption | Virtue |
| Conspiracy | Freedom |
