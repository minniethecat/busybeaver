## Biography

M. Gatier was a young abbé from Faucigny who studied at the seminary of Annecy. Rousseau describes him as "the gentlest of men" and paints an affectionate portrait: "Never did I see a more pleasing countenance than that of M. Gatier. He was fair complexioned, his beard rather inclined to red; his behavior like that of the generality of his countrymen (who under a coarseness of countenance conceal much understanding), marked in him a truly sensible and affectionate soul. In his large blue eyes there was a mingled softness, sensibility, and sadness." (003-068)

Out of complaisance for [[M. Gras]] and humanity toward Rousseau, Gatier "spared some time from the prosecution of his own studies in order to direct mine." (003-068) He became Rousseau's tutor during a critical period at the seminary.

When the time of ordination arrived, Gatier "returned to his province as deacon, leaving me with gratitude, attachment, and sorrow for his loss." (003-070) Years later, Rousseau learned that Gatier, serving as vicar of a parish, had fathered a child with a young woman — a scandal that ended his ecclesiastical career. Despite Gatier's favorable account of Rousseau's studies, "the improvement I received bore no proportion to the pains taken to instruct me," and Rousseau was sent back to Madame de Warens as unpromising material. (003-074)

## Relation to Rousseau

M. Gatier was one of Rousseau's most cherished mentors during his brief and unsuccessful period at the seminary. The contrast Rousseau draws between the harsh [[M. Gras]] and the gentle Gatier is stark and deliberate — Gatier was placed with him "by another yet more striking contrast" to save him from Gras's severity. (003-068)

Gatier's kindness left a lasting impression. Rousseau later wrote that the lessons "which were deeply engraven on my heart, struck me when I wrote Emilius." (003-070) He consciously "uniting M. Gatier with M. Gaime... formed from these two worthy priests the character of the Savoyard Vicar," one of the most celebrated figures in Rousseau's philosophical novel _Émile_. (003-070) Rousseau adds with characteristic self-regard: "I flatter myself the imitation has not dishonored the originals." (003-070)

## Significance

M. Gatier occupies a small but meaningful place in Rousseau's intellectual biography. Along with [[M. Gaime]], he provided the human model for the Savoyard Vicar — the voice of natural religion and moral education in _Émile_ that so profoundly influenced European educational thought. That Rousseau chose to immortalize these two obscure Savoyard priests in one of his most important philosophical works speaks to the depth of their impression on him.

Gatier's story — the gentle abbé who fell from grace through an affair with a parishioner — also reflects Rousseau's nuanced view of clerical morality. He does not condemn Gatier; instead, he presents him as a man of genuine sensibility whose human weakness did not diminish the value of his teachings. This compassion toward clerical frailty aligns with Rousseau's broader critique of institutional religion in favor of natural piety.
