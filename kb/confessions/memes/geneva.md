## Overview

Geneva was an independent Calvinist city-republic in the 18th century, situated on Lake Geneva (Lac Léman) in present-day Switzerland. It was a center of watchmaking, banking, and Reformed Protestant theology.

Rousseau was born in Geneva in 1712, at 40 Grand-Rue in the upper city, son of Isaac Rousseau, a watchmaker, and Susannah Bernard. (001-004) He proudly identified as a "Citizen of Geneva" throughout his life, signing many of his works with that title.

## In Rousseau's Life

Geneva shaped Rousseau's earliest identity. His father was a watchmaker and citizen of the republic, and Rousseau inherited both the citizenship and the craft tradition. After his mother's death and his father's exile, the young Rousseau was placed under the care of his uncle Bernard, who worked on the city's fortifications. (001-054)

Rousseau's relationship with Geneva was complex. He converted to Catholicism in Turin but later returned to Calvinism and reclaimed his Genevan citizenship in 1754. The city's political controversies, particularly the conflict between the patriciate and the citizens, influenced his political writings.

His works _Discourse on Inequality_ and _The Social Contract_ were both condemned in Geneva, leading to a permanent rupture between Rousseau and his native city.

## Historical Context

In the 18th century, Geneva was a republic governed by a General Council of citizens, though power was increasingly concentrated in the patrician Small Council. The city was also a key center of the Calvinist Reformation, with a theocratic element to its governance.

Geneva's watchmaking industry and banking sector gave it economic importance disproportionate to its size. As a francophone Protestant enclave, it served as a refuge for Huguenots and attracted thinkers including Voltaire, who settled nearby at Ferney.
