## Biography

The Marchioness of Verdelin was a French noblewoman who became Rousseau's neighbor when her husband bought a country house at Soisy, near Montmorency (010-167). She was born Mademoiselle d'Ars, daughter of the Comte d'Ars, a man of fashion but modest means. She married M. de Verdelin, whom Rousseau described as "old, ugly, deaf, uncouth, brutal, jealous, with gashes in his face, and blind of one eye, but, upon the whole, a good man when properly managed, and in possession of a fortune of from fifteen to twenty thousand a year" (010-167). Despite his brutish exterior, her husband always ended up doing whatever she wished.

Madam de Verdelin was closely connected to several figures in Rousseau's circle. M. de Margency, a mutual friend, had let them his castle of Margency near Eaubonne and Andilly. Through Madam d'Aubeterre, she became acquainted with [[Madame d'Houdetot]], and the garden of Margency lay on the path to Madam d'Houdetot's favorite walk at Mont Olympe (010-167).

Her personality presented a sharp contrast to Rousseau's own. He described their connection as "rather boisterously" begun and noted that "there never reigned in it a real calm":

> The turn of mind of Madam de Verdelin was too opposite to mine. Malignant expressions and pointed sarcasms came from her with so much simplicity, that a continual attention too fatiguing for me was necessary to perceive she was turning into ridicule the person to whom she spoke. (010-168)

Rousseau recounted a characteristic example: when he spoke of fitting out a frigate without diminishing its speed, she replied with perfect naturalness, "Yes, no more cannon are taken than are necessary for fighting" (010-168).

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's acquaintance with Madam de Verdelin began shortly after his return to Mont Louis, against his natural inclination. She pursued the connection with determination — calling at his house when he was out, and finally sending him pots of flowers for his terrace, which forced him to visit and thank her (010-167). "This was all she wanted, and we thus became acquainted" (010-167).

Despite the difficult start, their friendship deepened through shared suffering:

> By frequently seeing this lady I became attached to her. She had her troubles as well as I had mine. Reciprocal confidence rendered our conversations interesting. Nothing so cordially attaches two persons as the satisfaction of weeping together. (010-168)

A shadow fell over their relationship through the meddling of Coindet, an enterprising and bold acquaintance of Rousseau who "soon introduced himself in my name to the house of Madam de Verdelin, and, unknown to me, shortly became there more familiar than myself" (010-172). Coindet would never tell Rousseau what passed in these circles, creating an uncomfortable asymmetry.

### Visit to Motiers

During Rousseau's exile in Switzerland, Madam de Verdelin visited him at Motiers with her daughter, staying two or three days. This gesture of loyalty deeply moved him:

> By her attention and cares, she at length conquered my long repugnancy; and my heart, won by her endearing manner, made her a return of all the friendship of which she had long given me proofs. (012-087)

During her stay, she witnessed the persecution Rousseau suffered — stones thrown at his windows, a bench reared against his door. Her servants investigated and spoke freely with the villagers, but she herself affected not to notice, answering only "in a few words" when Rousseau raised the matter (012-087).

### The Hume Connection

Madam de Verdelin played a crucial role in connecting Rousseau with [[David Hume]]. She frequently spoke of Hume during her visit to Motiers, pressing Rousseau to write to him and take advantage of his offer to facilitate a move to England:

> She pressed me a good deal to take advantage of this zeal and to write to him. As I had not naturally an inclination to England, and did not intend to go there until the last extremity, I refused to write or make any promise; but I left her at liberty to do whatever she should think necessary to keep Mr. Hume favorably disposed towards me. (012-088)

After returning to Paris, she continued promoting Rousseau's relocation, writing several letters about a Lord Walpole who offered Rousseau asylum at one of his country houses. She provided detailed descriptions of the lodging and subsistence arrangements (012-109). Her departure from Motiers marked an escalation in Rousseau's persecution — "after the departure of Madam de Verdelin the fermentation increased" (012-106).

## Significance

Madam de Verdelin represents a rare figure in Rousseau's life: a friend with whom the connection was difficult yet enduring. Their relationship illustrates Rousseau's complex attitudes toward the aristocracy — he was simultaneously drawn to and repelled by her world. Her role in facilitating the connection with Hume makes her a consequential figure in one of the most famous intellectual quarrels of the Enlightenment.

She also appears in the reception history of Rousseau's novel *Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse*. Madam de Polignac wrote to her asking her to "prevail upon me to show her the portrait of Julia," believing Rousseau must have written from personal experience (011-014). This reflects the widespread contemporary belief that Rousseau and his fictional characters were one and the same.
