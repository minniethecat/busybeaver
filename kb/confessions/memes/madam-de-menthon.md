**Madam de Menthon** (the Countess of Menthon) was a noblewoman of Chambery during Rousseau's residence there in the 1730s. She employed Rousseau as a singing-master for her daughter and briefly sought to enlist his talents for her satirical pursuits.

## Biography

The Countess of Menthon was a woman of considerable wit and social influence in Chambery society. She was, by Rousseau's account, "a woman of great wit, and reckoned to possess, at least, an equal share of mischief." Her reputation included having caused numerous quarrels, among them one that "terminated fatally for the house of D'Antremont." She was the mother of a daughter, Mademoiselle de Menthon, who became one of Rousseau's music pupils, and maintained a country residence where she entertained the local gentry.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's connection to Madam de Menthon arose through his work as a music teacher at Chambery. Mademoiselle de Menthon, the countess's daughter, was among his pupils. Rousseau described the young woman with a painterly attention to detail:

> [005-056] Mademoiselle de Menthon, whom I attended in the afternoon, was ever so. She made an equally pleasing, but quite different impression on me. Her hair was flaxen, her person delicate, she was very timid and extremely fair…

The Countess's interest in Rousseau, however, was not maternal solicitude but rather an appraisal of his potential usefulness. She "loved to be surrounded by brilliant company" and had a "very lively passion for ridicule"—she amused herself by composing satirical songs and lampoons about those who displeased her:

> [005-062] …she bestowed some attention on me, not for the sake of my person, which she certainly did not regard, but for the reputation of wit which I had acquired, and which might have rendered me convenient to her predominant inclination.

Rousseau recognized the danger. Had he proven adept at versifying, Madam de Menthon would have enlisted him as her collaborator in a campaign of social mockery—and, he believed, would have sacrificed him as the scapegoat when the libels were traced to their source:

> [005-098] …these libels would have been traced to their source, Madam de Menthon would have saved herself by sacrificing me, and I should have been cooped up in prison…

Fortunately for Rousseau, his wit did not impress her. After "two or three days" of dining at her table and conversing, she concluded he was "too dull for her purpose":

> [005-060] I remained, therefore, Madam de Menthon's daughter's singing-master, and nothing more! but I lived happily, and was ever well received at Chambery…

Rousseau also recorded a social incident at M. de Menthon's house involving the young Marquis of Sennecterre, who tested Rousseau's musical abilities by having him notate a song for Mademoiselle de Menthon on the spot:

> [006-005] …he proposed my noting a song for Mademoiselle de Menthon, in such a manner that I could not avoid it. He sang this song, and I wrote from his voice…

Rousseau passed the test, earning the Marquis's approval for his accurate notation.

## Significance

The Madam de Menthon episode reveals Rousseau's social position at Chambery—poised between the aristocracy and the servant class, valued for his talents but never fully admitted to the inner circle. His self-deprecating account of being judged too dull to serve as the countess's satirical collaborator is characteristic of the ironic distance with which he narrates his younger self's social misadventures. As he wryly concluded, he "should rather have been obliged to my stupidity for keeping me out of the reach of danger."
