## Overview

The [[Academy of Dijon]] was a provincial French learned society founded in 1725 in Dijon, the capital of Burgundy. Like many provincial academies of the [[Enlightenment]], it organized annual essay competitions on philosophical and moral questions, drawing submissions from across France and beyond.

Though less prestigious than the [[Academy of Sciences]] or the Académie Française in [[Paris]], the Academy of Dijon played an unexpectedly pivotal role in the intellectual history of the eighteenth century through its connection to [[Rousseau]].

The academy's two most famous essay questions — on the moral effects of the arts and sciences (1749), and on the origin of human inequality (1753) — provided the occasion for Rousseau's first major philosophical works. These two "Discourses" established his reputation as a radical critic of civilization.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

The Academy of Dijon transformed Rousseau's life at a single stroke. In the summer of 1749, while walking to visit [[Denis Diderot]] imprisoned at Vincennes, Rousseau read the academy's question in the *Mercure de France*:

> [I] came to the following question proposed by the academy of Dijon, for the premium of the ensuing year, 'Has the progress of sciences and arts contributed to corrupt or purify morals?' [008-050]

The effect on Rousseau was electrifying: "The moment I had read this, I seemed to behold another world, and became a different man." Encouraged by [[Denis Diderot|Diderot]], Rousseau entered the competition and won the prize. His *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* (1750) was published to immense acclaim, making him one of the most discussed writers in Europe.

> [Diderot] encouraged me to pursue my ideas, and to become a competitor for the premium. I did so, and from that moment I was ruined. [008-050]

Rousseau returned to the academy a few years later. In 1753, the Academy of Dijon proposed a second landmark question, asking about the origin of inequality among mankind. Struck by the boldness of the question, Rousseau immediately undertook the discussion:

> [I]t was, I think, this year, 1753, that the programma of the Academy of Dijon upon the 'Origin of the Inequality of Mankind' made its appearance. Struck with this great question, I was surprised the academy had dared to propose it: but since it had shown sufficient courage to do it, I thought I might venture to treat it, and immediately undertook the discussion. [008-139]

He retreated to [[Saint-Germain]] for a week to compose it, wandering in the forest and "seeking for and finding there the image of the primitive ages." Though this second discourse did not win the prize, it became one of the foundational texts of modern political philosophy.

## Historical Significance

The Academy of Dijon's essay competitions served as the catalyst for Rousseau's philosophical career. Without the academy's questions, Rousseau might have remained an obscure musician and composer. Instead, the two Discourses propelled him to European fame and set the intellectual agenda for his later masterpieces, including *[[The Social Contract]]* and *[[Émile]]*.

The academy's role illustrates a broader feature of the [[Enlightenment]]: the network of provincial learned societies that created a public sphere for intellectual debate outside the control of church and court.

The Academy of Dijon was not the most eminent of these institutions, but its choice of essay topics — reflecting eighteenth-century anxieties about progress, civilization, and human nature — proved extraordinarily fertile.

Rousseau's relationship with the academy was paradoxical. He won its prize by arguing that the arts and sciences corrupt morals — an implicit critique of the very institution honoring him. He later broke with the Enlightenment establishment, but the Academy of Dijon remained the accidental midwife of his philosophical awakening.
