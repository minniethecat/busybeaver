## Biography

Mademoiselle Fel (Marie Fel, 1713–1794) was a celebrated French operatic soprano who enjoyed a distinguished career at the Paris Opera and the court of Louis XV. She was among the most admired singers of her generation, known for her voice and her dramatic presence. She appears in Rousseau's *Confessions* primarily in connection with the Parisian musical and intellectual circles of the 1750s, where she moved among writers, composers, and *philosophes*.

## Relation to Rousseau

Mademoiselle Fel crossed paths with Rousseau during his years in Paris as a composer and music theorist. She performed in his works, notably the opera *Le Devin du village* (The Village Soothsayer), which was a great success at court. Rousseau recounts traveling with her to Fontainebleau for a rehearsal:

> I went with Mademoiselle Fel, Grimm, and I think the Abbe Raynal, in one of the stages to the court. (008-118)

Fel was also the object of a passionate pursuit by Rousseau's friend-turned-rival Friedrich Melchior Grimm. According to Rousseau, Grimm "fell violently in love with her, and wished to supplant Cahusac" — her previous lover (008-098). Fel, however, "piquing herself on her constancy, refused her new admirer" (008-098). This rejection was, in Rousseau's retrospective view, one of the sources of Grimm's lasting resentment toward him, as Grimm believed Rousseau had not sufficiently supported his suit.

Rousseau also mentions composing music that Fel performed: he "had composed for Mademoiselle Fel" a piece "which she had sung at the concert" (009-206). The relationship was primarily professional, but Rousseau later recalled "the rigors of Mademoiselle Fel" (009-213) as one of the grievances Grimm held against the world.

## Significance

Mademoiselle Fel occupies a pivotal position in the social dynamics of Rousseau's Paris years. Her rejection of Grimm's advances became entangled in the complex web of personal animosities that ultimately led to the rupture between Rousseau and the *philosophes*. More broadly, her presence in the *Confessions* testifies to Rousseau's immersion in the musical culture of mid-eighteenth-century Paris — an immersion that produced both his greatest theatrical success (*Le Devin du village*) and his most acrimonious intellectual battles (the Querelle des Bouffons). Fel herself represents the type of the independent artistic woman who moved through Enlightenment Paris with a degree of autonomy rare for the period.
