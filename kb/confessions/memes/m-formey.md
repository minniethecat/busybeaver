**M. Formey** (Jean Henri Samuel Formey) was a man of letters and the Perpetual Secretary of the Berlin Academy. In Rousseau's *Confessions*, he appears as a journal editor who printed Rousseau's private letter to Voltaire concerning the Lisbon earthquake without the author's consent, triggering a chain of recriminations.

## Biography

Jean Henri Samuel Formey (1711–1797) was a prolific Franco-German writer, journalist, and philosopher. He served as the Perpetual Secretary of the Berlin Academy of Sciences and edited a learned journal. Formey was a correspondent of the Abbé Trublet and moved in the same Enlightenment circles as Rousseau, Voltaire, and Diderot. Rousseau characterized him bluntly as:

> [010-209] …an impudent pilferer, who, without ceremony, made himself a revenue by the works of others.

## Relation to Rousseau

The central episode involving Formey concerns Rousseau's 1756 letter to Voltaire on the subject of the Lisbon earthquake disaster and the problem of providence. In June 1760, the Abbé Trublet wrote to Rousseau with the news that Formey's journal had printed the letter. Rousseau was deeply perturbed—the letter had been a private communication, never intended for publication:

> [010-202] …M. Formey, his friend and correspondent, had printed in his journal my letter to Voltaire upon the disaster at Lisbon.

Rousseau's investigation revealed that Formey had obtained the manuscript not through Voltaire but through some other channel. He suspected that a copy had leaked from one of the three persons to whom he had entrusted it—Madam de Chenonceaux, the Comtesse d'Houdetot, or Grimm:

> [010-203] …notwithstanding all Trublet could say, Formey had not found the letter printed, and that the first impression of it came from himself.

The affair forced Rousseau into the uncomfortable position of justifying himself to Voltaire in a second letter (dated 17 June 1760), in which he explained the chain of custody and denied responsibility for the unauthorized publication:

> [010-210] …the copy, either in manuscript or print, fallen into the hands of M. de Formey, could never have reached them except by your means (which is not probable) or of those of one of the three persons I have mentioned.

Voltaire did not reply to this letter, and the incident became one more grievance in the growing enmity between the two philosophers.

## Significance

The Formey episode illustrates the fraught dynamics of intellectual property and private correspondence in eighteenth-century literary culture. For Rousseau, it was a painful reminder of his vulnerability to unauthorized publication and of the networks of gossip and appropriation that characterized the republic of letters. The incident also deepened the rift between Rousseau and the Encyclopédie circle, contributing to his growing sense of persecution.
