## Biography

The Abbé Nicolas Charles Joseph Trublet (1697–1770) was a French literary critic, essayist, and member of the clergy who moved in Enlightenment circles. Rousseau describes him as "half a hypocrite," noting that Madame de Créqui, who had become devout and ceased receiving D'Alembert and Marmontel, still admitted Trublet — though "of whom she was weary." (010-117)

Trublet was a correspondent of M. Formey, the Berlin Academy secretary who edited a literary journal in which Rousseau's private letter to Voltaire on the Lisbon disaster was published without authorization. (010-202)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau had "but a slight acquaintance" with Trublet and "had but seldom seen" him. (010-202) Their most significant interaction came in June 1760, when Trublet wrote to Rousseau informing him that Formey had printed Rousseau's letter to Voltaire in his journal. Trublet, in what Rousseau called his "jesuitical manner," asked Rousseau's opinion without offering his own. (010-202)

Rousseau immediately suspected that Formey had not merely found the letter already printed, but had been the first to publish it. He characterized Formey as "an impudent pilferer, who, without ceremony, made himself a revenue by the works of others." (010-203)

Trublet informed Rousseau that he found the letter in Formey's papers with an advertisement dated October 23, 1759, stating the editor had found the letter "a few weeks before... in the shops of the booksellers of Berlin." (010-209) Trublet offered to keep the paper in reserve and not lend it without Rousseau's consent. (010-211)

## Role in Literary Culture

Trublet was a transitional figure in French literary culture — a clergyman-critic who maintained connections with both the devout and the philosophes. Though Rousseau held him in low regard, Trublet's position as a literary intermediary gave him access to the correspondence and unpublished works circulating within the Republic of Letters.

## Significance

The Trublet episode illustrates the precarious nature of intellectual property and private correspondence in the eighteenth century. Rousseau's letter to Voltaire, written as a personal communication, was circulated, published, and became a matter of public controversy without his consent. Trublet, as the messenger who brought this uncomfortable news, occupies a minor but revealing role in the complex web of Enlightenment sociability and its discontents.
