The **Abbé Blanchard** was a French churchman and music-master who taught composition at the cathedral of Besançon and later served at the Chapel of Versailles. He appears in Rousseau's *Confessions* during the young man's restless period at Chambéry, when Rousseau—having been inspired by Venture de Villeneuve's praise—determined to travel to Besançon to study under him.

## Biography

The Abbé Blanchard was recommended to Rousseau by Venture de Villeneuve, who had himself studied composition under him. At the time of Rousseau's attempted visit, Blanchard held the post of music-master to the cathedral at Besançon. He later advanced to the same position at the Chapel of Versailles, suggesting a career of steady musical distinction within the French ecclesiastical establishment.

## Relation to Rousseau

During his stay at Chambéry with Madame de Warens, Rousseau grew discontented with his situation and sought a new direction. Venture's high praise of Blanchard provided one:

> [005-093] …Venture had spoken to me in very high terms of the Abbe Blanchard, who had taught him composition; a deserving man, possessed of great talents, who was music-master to the cathedral at Besancon, and is now in that capacity at the Chapel of Versailles.

Rousseau resolved to go to Besançon and persuaded Madame de Warens to finance the journey—a decision that, in characteristically contradictory fashion, involved her spending eight hundred livres to assist a project aimed at preventing her financial ruin:

> [005-094] …I therefore determined to go to Besancon, and take some lessons from the Abbe Blanchard, and the idea appeared so rational to me, that I soon made Madam de Warens of the same opinion, who immediately set about the preparations for my journey, in the same style of profusion with which all her plans were executed.

Blanchard received Rousseau kindly upon his arrival and promised instruction. However, the enterprise was cut short when Rousseau's portmanteau was seized at the French border on suspicion of containing seditious materials. The ensuing bureaucratic labyrinth forced Rousseau to abandon his belongings and his musical aspirations together:

> [005-096] …I arrived at Besancon, and was kindly received by the Abbe Blanchard, who promised me his instruction, and offered his services in any other particular. We had just set about our music, when I received a letter from my father, informing me that my portmanteau had been seized and confiscated at Rousses, a French barrier on the side of Switzerland.

Rousseau returned to Chambéry "without having learned anything of the Abbe Blanchard," and the episode marked another in a long series of enterprises that misfortune interrupted before they could bear fruit.

## Significance

The Blanchard episode is emblematic of Rousseau's early adult years: a pattern of enthusiastic resolution, impulsive departure, external misfortune, and resigned return. It also illustrates the extent to which Madame de Warens was willing to fund Rousseau's projects, even at the cost of her own financial stability. Blanchard himself, though a minor figure in the narrative, represents the gravitational pull that music—Rousseau's enduring passion—exerted on the young man's imagination throughout his wanderings.
