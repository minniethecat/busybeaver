## Biography

M. Dastier (full name Dastier de Carpentras) was a retired French military officer whom Rousseau encountered later in life. He was "an old officer who had his cross of St. Louis" (012-050), indicating distinguished service in the French army. Dastier had previously served on the island of Corsica under the command of M. de Maillebois, giving him first-hand knowledge of conditions there: "Dastier, who having formerly served in the island under M. de Maillebois, was necessarily acquainted with it" (012-146).

Rousseau was immediately drawn to Dastier's "open countenance" and found himself "more particularly pleased" with him than with other members of a company he describes (012-051).

## Relation to Rousseau

Dastier played a significant advisory role during a critical moment in Rousseau's life, when the philosopher was considering relocating to Corsica. Rousseau consulted Dastier about the practicalities of moving to the island:

> I mentioned it to M. Dastier, who having formerly served in the island under M. de Maillebois, was necessarily acquainted with it. (012-146)

The picture Dastier painted was sobering. According to his descriptions, Corsica lacked the most basic conveniences of civilized life: "According to what M. Dastier had told me of Corsica, I could not expect to find there the most simple conveniences of life, except such as I should take with me; linen, clothes, plate, kitchen furniture, and books, all were to be conveyed thither" (012-151). Dastier also warned Rousseau of the dangers posed by the island's inhabitants, whom he described in harsh terms that gave Rousseau serious pause before committing to the Corsican venture (012-151).

## Significance

M. Dastier represents the practical, worldly perspective that served as a counterweight to Rousseau's idealistic impulses. Rousseau's interest in Corsica — then in revolt against Genoa and seeking to establish an independent republic — reflected his political idealism. Dastier's sobering account of the island's primitive conditions and dangerous inhabitants brought Rousseau back to earth, contributing to his eventual decision not to undertake the Corsican expedition. Dastier thus functions in the *Confessions* as a figure who grounds Rousseau's romantic political imagination in practical reality.
