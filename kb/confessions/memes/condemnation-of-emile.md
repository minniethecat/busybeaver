## Summary

In June 1762, the Parliament of Paris condemned Rousseau's _Émile_ and _The Social Contract_, ordering the books to be burned and issuing a warrant for Rousseau's arrest. The decree transformed Rousseau from a celebrated author into a fugitive.

Rousseau learned of the impending action while staying at Montmorency with the Maréchal de Luxembourg. A counsellor told him: "When the parliament proceeds with the utmost rigor, it is then they do the least injustice." (011-067) But the parliamentarians were heard to declare that "burning books was of no effect, the authors also should be burned with them." (011-067)

## Context

_Émile, or On Education_ was published in May 1762. Its religious profession of faith, "The Creed of a Savoyard Vicar," was seen as an attack on both Catholic and Protestant orthodoxy. _The Social Contract_, published the same year, challenged the divine right of kings and the foundations of monarchical authority.

Rousseau had anticipated trouble. The Prince de Conti and Madame de Luxembourg urged him to flee to England. He hesitated, unwilling to be separated from Thérèse and believing the storm would pass. (011-069)

## Aftermath

On the night of June 9, 1762, Rousseau was awakened by a messenger from Madame de Luxembourg: the decree had been issued, and officers were on their way to arrest him. He fled immediately, beginning an eight-year period of exile that took him to Switzerland, Neuchâtel (then under Prussian rule), and eventually England.

The condemnation marked the definitive rupture between Rousseau and the French establishment. It transformed his reputation from that of an eccentric philosopher into a persecuted martyr for liberty of conscience.
