# Madame de Luxembourg

**Madeleine Angélique de Neufville de Villeroy, Maréchale de Luxembourg** (1707–1787) was a French noblewoman of the highest rank who became one of Rousseau's most generous and influential patrons during his years at Montmorency. She was the wife of Charles-François-Frédéric de Montmorency-Luxembourg, Maréchal de France.

## Biography

The daughter of the Duc de Villeroy, she married into the illustrious Montmorency-Luxembourg family. By the time Rousseau met her in the late 1750s, she was one of the grandest ladies of the French court, yet she took a personal interest in the irritable and reclusive philosopher:

> I remember Madam de Luxembourg told me one day in raillery, that she believed it was to recount my gallantries with the princesses of the court I had taken a journey to Paris. (005-037)

She and the Maréchal inhabited the Château de Montmorency, near the Hermitage where Rousseau lived from 1756 to 1757 and afterward at Montlouis. She extended her protection to Rousseau during his most vulnerable years, shielding him from the consequences of the controversy surrounding _[[Émile, or On Education]]_ and _[[The Social Contract]]_.

## Relation to Rousseau

Madame de Luxembourg became acquainted with Rousseau through mutual connections. Their relationship deepened during his residence at Montmorency, and she proved a steadfast friend:

> I knew she was amiable as to disposition; I had proofs of it; not only from the obliging manner in which she had treated me, but from the little I had seen of her natural character. (010-140)

She intervened repeatedly on Rousseau's behalf with powerful figures, attempted to reconcile him with his enemies, and offered material support without the strings attached that had made his dependence on [[Madame d'Épinay]] so painful. When the Parlement of Paris condemned _Émile_ and ordered Rousseau's arrest in 1762, it was the Luxembourg family who helped arrange his flight:

> The Maréchal Duke de Luxembourg who then inhabited this house had been extremely civil to me on my arrival. (010-138)

Rousseau's account of her in the _Confessions_ is notably warmer than his portraits of most other patrons and aristocratic acquaintances. He portrays her as genuinely kind, if sometimes limited by the conventions of her rank:

> She sometimes amused M. de Luxembourg with her 'qui pro quos', often very laughable mistakes. (007-132)

## Significance

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Patronage | Protected Rousseau during the _Émile_ crisis (1762) |
| Rank | Maréchale de France, one of the highest-ranking women in France |
| Circle | Connected Rousseau to the highest levels of French society |
| Legacy | One of the few patrons Rousseau praised without reservation |

Her significance in Rousseau's life extends beyond material patronage. At a time when he saw conspiracies everywhere — in d'Holbach's salon, in Grimm's intrigues, in Diderot's betrayals — Madame de Luxembourg offered an example of disinterested kindness that tempered his growing paranoia.
