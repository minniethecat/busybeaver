The **Marquis de Bonac** was a French diplomat who served as ambassador at the Porte (the Ottoman Empire) and later at Soleurre in the Swiss Confederation. He appears in Rousseau's *Confessions* at a pivotal moment, when his intervention redirected the course of the young Rousseau's wandering adolescence.

## Biography

The Marquis de Bonac had served as French ambassador to the Ottoman Empire before assuming the post at Soleurre. His diplomatic household included M. de la Martiniere as secretary of the embassy and M. de Marianne as secretary-in-waiting, the latter of whom eventually succeeded to the secretaryship. Madam de Bonac, the ambassador's wife, was a woman of cultivation and kindness who joined her husband in receiving the young Rousseau.

## Relation to Rousseau

In 1728, Rousseau arrived at Soleurre as interpreter to a Greek Archimandrite who was travelling through Europe soliciting funds for the restoration of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem. The ambassador, well acquainted with Eastern ecclesiastical affairs, granted the prelate an audience but quickly perceived the irregularity of the situation:

> [004-065] …chanced to be the Marquis de Bonac, who had been ambassador at the Porte, and was acquainted with every particular relative to the Holy Sepulchre.

After the Archimandrite's audience, Rousseau was detained and brought before the ambassador. With characteristic candor, the young man disclosed his true circumstances. The Marquis, moved by Rousseau's frankness, presented him to the ambassadress and resolved to keep him at the embassy:

> [004-067] …the Marquis de Bonac, who was so well pleased with my little history, and the ingenuousness with which I had related it, that he led me to the ambassadress, and presented me, with an abridgment of my recital.

During his stay, Rousseau—encouraged by M. de la Martiniere's comparison to a literary predecessor—composed his first poetic work in honor of their hostess:

> [004-066] …my first essay in a cantata in praise of Madam de Bonac. This inclination was not permanent, though from time to time I have composed tolerable verses.

When the ambassador consulted Rousseau on his future plans, the young man expressed a resolve to go to Paris. The Marquis readily acquiesced and, through his interpreting secretary M. de Merveilleux, arranged for Rousseau to enter the service of a Swiss colonel named Godard. Rousseau departed for Paris shortly thereafter.

## Significance

The Marquis de Bonac's intervention at Soleurre was one of the decisive turning-points in Rousseau's early life. Without it, he might have continued as a rootless wanderer following the Greek prelate. Instead, the ambassador's practical assistance set him on the path toward Paris. Rousseau reflected on this episode with characteristic self-awareness, noting:

> [004-068] …I did not fall in love with Madam de Bonac, but also felt I did not stand much chance of succeeding in the service of her husband.

The Marquis thus exemplifies the role of chance aristocratic patronage in shaping Rousseau's trajectory during the years before his emergence as a major intellectual figure.
