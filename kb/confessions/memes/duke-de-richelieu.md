## Biography

Louis François Armand de Vignerot du Plessis, Duke de Richelieu (1696–1788), was a French marshal, diplomat, and one of the most prominent noblemen of the Ancien Régime. A grandnephew of Cardinal Richelieu, he held significant military commands and was a celebrated patron of the arts.

Rousseau was introduced to the Duke de Richelieu during his time in Paris. (007-011) The Duke quickly became an important figure in Rousseau's career, commissioning theatrical works and interceding on his behalf in court circles.

Though initially impressed by Rousseau, "M. de Richelieu, who at that time frequently" engaged with the author, the Duke later "recovered from his error" of enthusiasm for Rousseau's work, reflecting the fickle nature of aristocratic patronage. (007-136) (007-137)

## Relation to Rousseau

The Duke de Richelieu played a crucial role in Rousseau's professional life as a musical composer. "M. de Richelieu thought of me, and sent to demand" revisions to Rousseau's opera, and "a few months ago the Duke de Richelieu commanded me to make, absolutely" new compositions. (008-001) (008-002)

Rousseau experienced the Duke's capriciousness firsthand. When Rousseau composed a funeral anthem, "M. de Richelieu very judiciously began by informing" him of the requirements, but the commission was ultimately dropped without explanation. (008-012)

The relationship was mediated by "Madam Richelieu," at whose court Rousseau had some favor — Madame de Richelieu was the Duke's wife, and "the courtly suppleness" of the milieu shaped Rousseau's interactions with the household. (008-007) When Rousseau "wished to wait upon M. de Richelieu, but it was too late; he had already" departed, he felt the sting of aristocratic indifference. (008-014) Rousseau later imagined "M. de Richelieu had forgotten me," a sentiment that prefigured his broader disenchantment with the Parisian elite. (008-018)

## Significance

The Duke de Richelieu exemplifies the precarious position of the artist in Ancien Régime France. Rousseau's dependence on aristocratic patrons like Richelieu exposed him to the whims and power games of the nobility — experiences that informed his critique of inequality and artifice in society.

The relationship also illustrates how Rousseau's musical ambitions, which he initially saw as his primary path to success, were thwarted by the very patronage system that was supposed to support them. This frustration contributed to his turn toward philosophical writing and his eventual rejection of Parisian society altogether.
