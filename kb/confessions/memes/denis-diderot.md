## Biography

Denis Diderot (1713–1784) was a French philosopher, art critic, and writer. He was the co-founder, chief editor, and contributor to the _Encyclopédie_, a landmark work of the Enlightenment.

Rousseau first met Diderot through their mutual friend Roguin, and they quickly became close. (007-028) Diderot was "much about my own age," and the two shared intellectual passions that led to the _Encyclopédie_. (007-040)

Diderot suffered imprisonment at Vincennes for his philosophical writings, an event that deeply affected Rousseau. During these years, they formed the core of a circle of _philosophes_ that included Grimm, Condillac, and d'Alembert. (008-043)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau and Diderot were "two friends" among a numerous acquaintance. (008-097) Together they planned a periodical entitled _Le Persifleur_, and Diderot recruited Rousseau to write the musical articles for the _Encyclopédie_. (008-044)

Their friendship deteriorated dramatically after Rousseau's success with the _Discourse on Inequality_ and his retreat to the Hermitage. Rousseau came to believe that Diderot had joined a "grand conspiracy" against him, alongside Grimm. (009-002)

The final rupture came in 1758, formalized by Rousseau in the preface to his _Letter to d'Alembert_, where he publicly referenced a passage from Ecclesiasticus. The break was never repaired.

## Significance

The Rousseau-Diderot friendship and its collapse is one of the defining intellectual dramas of the Enlightenment. It represents the tension between collaborative ethos and solitary conviction.

Diderot appears throughout the later books of the _Confessions_ as both friend and antagonist, embodying the world of Parisian letters that Rousseau would ultimately reject in favor of his solitary path.
