# The Encyclopédie

The _Encyclopédie, ou Dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers_ (1751–1772) was the most ambitious publishing project of the French Enlightenment—a comprehensive dictionary of human knowledge edited by [[Denis Diderot]] and Jean d'Alembert. Rousseau contributed the articles on music and the important entry on "Political Economy" to the early volumes, placing him at the center of the intellectual enterprise that defined his age. His eventual break with the Encyclopedist circle was one of the defining ruptures of his life, dramatizing the tension between collaborative Enlightenment and solitary conviction.

## Overview

The _Encyclopédie_ was conceived as a systematic survey of all human knowledge, organized according to the rational principles of Francis Bacon's classification of the sciences. Its 28 volumes (17 of text, 11 of plates) contained over 70,000 articles by more than 140 contributors, making it the largest reference work of the 18th century.

For Rousseau, involvement in the _Encyclopédie_ represented entry into the highest circles of French intellectual life. Diderot recruited him early, and the musical articles—eventually collected into Rousseau's _Dictionnaire de Musique_—established him as a serious technical authority.

> This undertaking of the 'Encyclopédie' was interrupted by his imprisonment. (008-045)

The reference to Diderot's imprisonment at Vincennes (1749) situates the _Encyclopédie_ within the political struggles that defined it. The project was repeatedly threatened by censorship, and its contributors risked official persecution.

## Role in Rousseau's Life

Rousseau's relationship with the _Encyclopédie_ evolved through three phases:

| Phase | Period | Rousseau's Position |
|-------|--------|---------------------|
| Contributor | 1749–1757 | Music articles + "Political Economy"; enthusiastic participant |
| Alienation | 1757–1758 | Break with Diderot and Grimm; the "storm" around the _Encyclopédie_ |
| Opposition | 1758–1762 | _Letter to d'Alembert_ (1758) publicly opposed d'Alembert's "Geneva" article |

The transition from contributor to opponent was gradual but definitive. Rousseau's _Letter to d'Alembert on the Theatre_ (1758) was provoked by d'Alembert's _Encyclopédie_ article on Geneva, which suggested that the city should permit a theater. Rousseau's vehement rejection of this proposal marked his public break with the Encyclopedist party.

> The storm brought on by the 'Encyclopédie', far from being appeased, was at its height. (009-125)

By the time of this passage, Rousseau had already retreated to [[the Hermitage Period|the Hermitage]]. The "storm" around the _Encyclopédie_—its condemnation by the Paris Parlement in 1759—created an atmosphere of political tension that made Rousseau's position increasingly precarious.

The final rupture came through a series of exchanges that Rousseau records with bitter precision. When the _Encyclopédie_ supporters sought his return to the fold, offering encomiums in exchange for favorable treatment of certain parties, Rousseau refused:

> My answer to this letter was a simple refusal, which I expressed in terms of the most profound respect. (010-191)

## Historical Significance

The _Encyclopédie_ serves multiple functions in the _Confessions_:

1. **Institutional context**: It was the institution through which Rousseau joined the French Enlightenment and the institution against which he defined his mature philosophical position.

2. **The limits of collaboration**: The _Encyclopédie_ project represented collaborative knowledge production, which Rousseau came to see as incompatible with authentic philosophical insight. His break with the Encyclopedists was also a break with the very idea of knowledge-by-committee.

3. **Political stakes**: The controversies surrounding the _Encyclopédie_—censorship, persecution, exile—established the pattern for Rousseau's own later experience with _Émile_ and _The Social Contract_.

4. **Intellectual legacy**: Rousseau's _Encyclopédie_ articles, particularly "Political Economy," contain the seeds of ideas that would flower in _The Social Contract_ and shape the development of modern political thought.

The _Encyclopédie_ represented everything Rousseau simultaneously craved and rejected: recognition, intellectual community, and the collaborative production of knowledge. His departure from its orbit was both a personal tragedy and a philosophical necessity.
