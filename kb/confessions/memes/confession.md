## Definition

Confession, in Rousseau's sense, is the act of revealing one's innermost self with complete honesty — "sometimes vile and despicable, at others, virtuous, generous and sublime." (001-003) It is both a literary form and a moral undertaking.

Rousseau announces his project as "without example, whose accomplishment will have no imitator." (001-001) He presents the _Confessions_ not merely as autobiography but as a unique experiment in total self-disclosure before the "sovereign judge." (001-003)

## In Rousseau's Thought

Rousseau frames confession as an act of moral courage. "I have made the first, most difficult step, in the obscure and painful maze of my Confessions," he declares. (001-069) The difficulty lies not in recounting events but in confronting shameful truths without evasion.

The weight of undisclosed deeds haunts him. The theft of the ribbon, for instance, remains on his conscience for decades: "The weight has remained heavy on my conscience to this day." (002-151) Confession becomes a form of moral liberation.

Rousseau's confessional impulse is also strategic — by preemptively revealing his faults, he seeks to disarm his enemies and establish the sincerity that underwrites his philosophical critiques of society.

## Broader Context

Rousseau's _Confessions_ (published posthumously, 1782–1789) was modeled on Saint Augustine's _Confessions_ but secularized the form. Where Augustine confessed to God, Rousseau confessed to his fellow men, inviting them to "blush at my depravity" and "tremble at my sufferings."

The work inaugurated modern autobiography as a genre of psychological depth and moral self-examination, influencing Goethe, Tolstoy, and the Romantic tradition.
