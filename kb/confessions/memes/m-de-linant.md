**M. de Linant** served as tutor to the son of M. d'Épinay, the wealthy fermier-général, and as confidant to Madame d'Épinay at the Chevrette. He appears in Rousseau's *Confessions* in two distinct episodes: first as the supplier of Latin verses for a motet Rousseau composed for the Épinay chapel, and later as an intermediary in the chain of events that precipitated Rousseau's rupture with the Épinay-Diderot circle.

## Biography

De Linant held the position of preceptor in the household of M. d'Épinay at the Chevrette, responsible for the education of the Épinays' son. He moved in the literary orbit of the Épinay salon, where Rousseau encountered him during the period of his residence at the Hermitage. His role as confidant to Madame d'Épinay placed him at the intersection of the personal and literary intrigues that swirled around the household.

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau's first significant interaction with de Linant was collaborative. In an effort to dispel M. d'Épinay's suspicions about his relationship with Madame d'Épinay, Rousseau offered to compose a motet for the dedication of the Chevrette chapel. He asked d'Épinay to choose the words, and the task fell to de Linant:

> [009-206] …He directed de Linant, the tutor to his son, to furnish me with these. De Linant gave me words proper to the subject, and in a week after I had received them the motet was finished.

The motet, beginning with *Ecce sedes hic Tonantis*, was a success. Rousseau later discovered that the words had been taken from the neo-Latin poet Santeuil without acknowledgment:

> [009-206] …I have since learned these were by Santeuil, and that M. de Linant had without scruple appropriated them to himself.

De Linant reappeared at a critical juncture. When Madame d'Épinay announced her sudden decision to travel to Geneva to consult the physician Tronchin, she mentioned de Linant as her travelling companion, casually asking Rousseau whether he would join them:

> [010-010] …She said her son and M. de Linant; and afterwards carelessly added, "And you, dear, will not you go also?"

Rousseau declined, and the matter seemed settled. But shortly afterward, Diderot's note urging Rousseau to accompany Madame d'Épinay was delivered to de Linant rather than to Rousseau directly—a circumstance Rousseau found revealing:

> [010-011] …A few days afterwards I received from Diderot the note I am going to transcribe. This note, simply doubled up, so that the contents were easily read, was addressed to me at Madam d'Épinay's, and sent to M. de Linant, tutor to the son, and confidant to the mother.

## Significance

M. de Linant occupies a minor but symbolically charged place in Rousseau's narrative. The motet episode—in which de Linant passed off Santeuil's verses as his own—foreshadows the atmosphere of dissimulation Rousseau would increasingly perceive at the Chevrette. His role as the recipient of Diderot's note, read through Rousseau's suspicious lens, made him an unwitting participant in the pressure campaign that Rousseau believed was being orchestrated against him. De Linant thus serves as a minor thread in the complex fabric of mistrust that led to Rousseau's flight from the Hermitage.
