## Biography

The Count de Bellegarde was the son of the Marquis of Antremont. He had long resided in Dresden, the cultural capital of Saxony, and returned from there after the death of King Augustus. (005-097) A nobleman with cultivated tastes, he possessed a passion for music that drew Rousseau into his orbit.

Bellegarde was connected by family to the farming-general elite of Paris. He was the father of Mademoiselle de Bellegarde, who later became the Comtesse d'Houdetot — the woman who would inspire the most intense romantic passion of Rousseau's life. (008-041)

## Relation to Rousseau

Bellegarde's connection to Rousseau came through music. Rousseau, still struggling to master Rameau's theories, found encouragement in Bellegarde's appreciation. Though an earlier "mischance had rather dampened my musical ardor," Rousseau persevered, and through "repeated efforts, was at length able to understand it, and to make some little attempts at composition." Bellegarde's encouragement was instrumental in this progress. (005-097)

Rousseau met Bellegarde through Madame de Warens. The introduction occurred when M. de Conzié, a friend of the nobility of Savoy, came from the baths of Aix to Chambéry specifically to visit the young Count de Bellegarde and his father. Madame de Warens introduced Rousseau on that occasion. (006-009)

## Family and Legacy

The Bellegarde family tree connects several important figures in Rousseau's life. The Count's daughter became the Comtesse d'Houdetot, whose sister-in-law was Madame d'Épinay. The Count was also related to the Lalive family of farmers-general, including M. de Lalive de Bellegarde, whose son married Mademoiselle d'Esclavelles — later Madame d'Épinay. (008-038) (009-116)

Rousseau records that when he first met Mademoiselle de Bellegarde, she was "upon the point of marriage" and "conversed with me a long time, with that charming familiarity which was natural to her." He found her "very amiable," though he was "far from perceiving that this young person would lead me, though innocently, into the abyss in which I now am." (008-041)

## Significance

M. de Bellegarde occupies a pivotal position at the intersection of Rousseau's musical ambitions and his social ascent. Through Bellegarde, Rousseau connected with the network of Savoyard and Parisian nobility who would shape his later career. The Bellegarde family, through the Count's daughter Sophie, would become central to the emotional drama of Rousseau's middle years — making this early musical patron an unwitting architect of Rousseau's destiny.
