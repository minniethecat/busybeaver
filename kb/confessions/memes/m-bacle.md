## Biography

M. Bacle was a Genevese who had been Rousseau's companion during his apprenticeship in Geneva. (003-018) Little is known of his early life, but he was among a circle of Genevese expatriates in Turin, including M. Mussard, a miniature painter nicknamed "Wryneck." (003-018)

Rousseau describes Bacle as a "drole" — "rather giddy than deficient in point of sense." (003-029) He possessed a certain practical cunning, demonstrated in the scheme that would determine both young men's immediate futures.

## The Heron Fountain Adventure

The defining episode of Bacle's presence in Rousseau's life was their joint departure from Turin. The Abbé de Gauvon had given Rousseau a "very pretty heron fountain" as a present, and Rousseau was "highly delighted" with it. (003-024) Bacle, described by Rousseau as "the sage Bacle," conceived the idea that displaying this fountain along their journey could earn them enough money to travel indefinitely.

Together they imagined themselves showing the fountain to curious onlookers in every village, using the proceeds to "lengthen our journey." What Bacle lacked in formal education he made up for in entrepreneurial imagination — the fountain, he argued, was "so curious" that it would provide "infinite advantage." (003-024) Rousseau was easily persuaded, and they set off together.

> Farewell to the capital; adieu to the court, ambition, love, the fair, and all the great adventures into which hope had led me during the preceding year! I departed with my fountain and my friend Bacle, a purse lightly filled, but a heart saturated with joy. (003-026)

## Separation

The partnership did not last. Rousseau, increasingly eager to reach Madame de Warens in Annecy, grew tired of Bacle's company. He "prefaced this separation by an affected coldness during the last day's journey." (003-029) To Rousseau's surprise, Bacle was not offended: "nothing affected my friend Bacle, and I never since heard of him." (003-029)

The contrast with Rousseau's other youthful companion, M. Venture, is instructive. Rousseau later reflected that his attachment to Venture, "more reasonable in its cause, was also less extravagant in its effects, though more lively and durable than that I had conceived for M. Bacle." (003-081)

## Significance

Bacle represents the impulsive, freedom-seeking side of Rousseau's character. By following Bacle's scheme, Rousseau abandoned a secure future with the House of Solar for the life of "a real vagabond." (003-026) The Bacle episode, comic in its details — two young men wandering Italy with a toy fountain — embodies the tension between Rousseau's ideals of liberty and the practical consequences of his impulsive decisions. Bacle himself vanishes from the historical record after their parting, a fleeting figure whose lasting impact was to facilitate one of Rousseau's most consequential acts of self-sabotage.
