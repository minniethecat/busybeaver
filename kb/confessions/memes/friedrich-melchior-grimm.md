## Biography

Friedrich Melchior Grimm (1723–1807) was a German-born writer, music critic, and diplomat who became a central figure in Parisian intellectual life. He arrived in Paris in modest circumstances, serving as a reader to the Comte de Friese. (008-047)

Grimm rose through the Paris salons, eventually becoming editor of the _Correspondance littéraire_, a manuscript newsletter for the crowned heads of Europe. His social ascent was marked by an affair with Madame d'Épinay, who became his principal patron.

Rousseau introduced Grimm to Diderot and helped establish him in Parisian intellectual circles: "With the greatest pleasure I procured him all I could." (008-097)

## Relation to Rousseau

Rousseau initially regarded Grimm as one of his two closest friends, alongside Diderot. (008-097) They shared musical interests, spending hours together at the harpsichord. (008-056)

The friendship soured after Grimm's return from military service in Westphalia. Rousseau found him transformed, treating him with coldness at Madame d'Épinay's table at La Chevrette. (009-207)

Rousseau came to see Grimm as the architect of the "grand conspiracy" against him, a man "false by nature" who "never loved me." (010-185) In the _Confessions_, Grimm emerges as the central antagonist of Rousseau's Paris years.

## Significance

Grimm occupies a unique position in the _Confessions_ as Rousseau's most thoroughly vilified former friend. His role illustrates the memoirist's conviction that he was the victim of a coordinated conspiracy by the _philosophes_.

The Grimm-Rousseau rupture epitomizes the collision between Rousseau the solitary moralist and the worldly, networking culture of the Parisian Enlightenment that he grew to despise.
