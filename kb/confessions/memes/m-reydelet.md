## Biography

M. Reydelet served as curate of [[Seyssel]] and held the position of canon at St. Peter's. He was consequently known to [[M-le-Maitre]], the music master with whom the young Rousseau was traveling. Despite being someone from whom M. le Maitre — who had abandoned his position at the cathedral — should have been hiding, Reydelet proved to be a generous and helpful patron.

> M. Reydelet, curate of Seyssel, was canon of St. Peter's, consequently known to M. le Maitre, and one of the people from whom he should have taken most pains to conceal himself; my advice, on the contrary, was to present ourselves to him and ask for assistance. (003-085)

## Relation to Rousseau

During Rousseau's youthful journey with M. le Maitre across southeastern France, M. Reydelet provided crucial support. Acting on Rousseau's advice to approach him openly rather than hide, the travelers were received with unexpected generosity.

> According to the information given to M. Reydelet, we passed our Easter holidays at Bellay, and though not expected there, were received by the music-master, and welcomed by every one with great pleasure. (003-086)

Reydelet's assistance extended beyond mere hospitality. He helped the travelers arrange their onward journey:

> Having passed four or five days very agreeably at Bellay, we departed, and continuing our journey without meeting with any accidents, except those I have just spoken of, arrived at Lyons, and were lodged at Notre Dame de Pitie. While we waited for the arrival of the before-mentioned case (which by the assistance of another lie, and the care of our good patron, M. Reydelet, we had embarked on the Rhone)... (003-087)

Rousseau's approach to Reydelet — presenting themselves openly rather than concealing M. le Maitre's identity — illustrates a theme Rousseau develops throughout the *Confessions*: his belief that audacity and honesty often succeed where caution and deception fail. He presents this episode as an example of his own contrary character, acting boldly in a situation that called for discretion.

## Significance

M. Reydelet appears only briefly in the *Confessions*, but his role exemplifies the network of clerical patronage that facilitated travel and social mobility in eighteenth-century France. As a canon of St. Peter's and curate of Seyssel, Reydelet occupied a position of modest but real influence in the region between [[Geneva]] and [[Lyons]].

The episode also reveals Rousseau's early talent for navigating social situations — reading character, assessing risk, and making bold decisions that his companions would not have made. At this stage of his life, before his literary career, Rousseau was developing the interpersonal skills and self-confidence that would later enable him to move through the highest circles of French society.
