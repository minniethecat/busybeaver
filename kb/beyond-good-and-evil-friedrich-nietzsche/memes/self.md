# Self

## Overview

Nietzsche's conception of the *Selbst* (self) breaks decisively with both the Christian soul and the Cartesian *cogito*. The self is not a unitary, substantial entity but a dynamic field of forces, drives, and valuations — embodied in the "great reason" of the body. Throughout BGAE, the self appears in compound forms that reveal Nietzsche's psychological depth: *Selbstbezwingung* (self-conquest), *Selbstprüfung* (self-examination), and *Selbst-Zerstörung* (self-destruction). These are not deviations from a stable self but the very processes through which a self is constituted and transformed.

## Key Passages

- (003-009) — Nietzsche describes the saint as a "riddle of self-conquest and intentional final renunciation" (*Selbstbezwingung und absichtlichen letzten Entbehrung*). The powerful have always bowed before the saint, intuiting in this self-overcoming a superior strength of will — a "will to power" that compels even the mighty to stop and question.

- (003-014) — The "monastic labor of self-examination" (*Selbstprüfung*) — the delicate composure that calls itself "prayer" — required external idleness. Nietzsche contrasts this with modern noisy industriousness, which he diagnoses as the true source of unbelief: busy-ness leaves no time for self-scrutiny.

- (009-022) — In a passage of extraordinary psychological insight, Nietzsche describes the type of person whose bitterness turns against their own fate and "makes an attempt at self-destruction" (*Selbst-Zerstörung*). The psychologist recognizes in the well-ordered, average person not health but someone "in need of healing, a kind of flight and forgetting."

- (001-004) — Nietzsche asks what qualities might belong to the genuinely philosophical drive, listing "the true, the truthful, the selfless" (*dem Wahren, dem Wahrhaftigen, dem Selbstlosen*). Even here, selflessness is not a moral ideal but a possible psychological disposition — and one Nietzsche will later subject to critique.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The Nietzschean self is a process, not a substance. Where Christian anthropology posits an immortal soul and modern philosophy posits a transcendental ego, Nietzsche offers a self constituted by the hierarchical organization of drives — a "social structure of the drives and affects" that is always in flux. The self is something one *becomes*, not something one *is*.

This dynamic self is essential to Nietzsche's ethics. Self-overcoming, the signature moral ideal of BGAE, presupposes a self that can be reorganized from within — a self with the plasticity and internal multiplicity to turn against its own dominant drives without appealing to an external standard. The self as embodied, plural, and self-transcending is the anthropological foundation for the entire edifice of master morality, noble egoism, and the affirmation of life.
