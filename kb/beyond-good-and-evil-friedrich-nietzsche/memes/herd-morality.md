# Herd Morality

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche develops a sustained critique of modern morality as "herd-animal morality" (*Heerdenthier-Moral*). This concept describes a system of values that has emerged not from the commanding heights of human possibility but from the instinct of the gregarious animal — the instinct to conform, to avoid danger, and to reduce all human beings to a common, manageable type.

Herd morality is distinguished from slave morality in Nietzsche's typology. While slave morality originates in the *ressentiment* of the oppressed against the noble, herd morality is the *normalized* version — it is what slave morality becomes when it has won and established itself as the only permissible value system. In modern Europe, Nietzsche argues, the herd animal presents itself as the only permitted type of human being, celebrating its own timidity as virtue.

## Key Passages

- [005-011] Nietzsche identifies the herd (*die Heerde*) as the true driver of moral philosophy since Plato: "in matters of morality, the instinct has so far been victorious — or, as the Christians call it, 'faith,' or as I call it, 'the herd'" (*die Heerde*). The entire history of moral philosophy is unmasked as the rationalization of herd instinct.
- [005-018] Nietzsche observes that "there have always been human herds" (*Menschenheerden*) — and that "the strange limitation of human development" results from the fact that "the herd-instinct of obedience is inherited best, and at the expense of the art of commanding" (*der Heerden-Instinkt des Gehorsams am besten und auf Kosten der Kunst des Befehlens vererbt wird*).
- [005-019] "Today the herd-man in Europe gives himself the appearance of being the only permitted type of human being" (*der Heerdenmensch in Europa das Ansehn, als sei er die einzig erlaubte Art Mensch*) — and glorifies his own traits (sociability, benevolence, consideration) as the "truly human virtues." This is Nietzsche's diagnosis of modern democratic culture.
- [005-019] Even modern representative constitutions are unmasked as attempts "to replace the commanders by adding together clever herd-men" (*durch Zusammen-Addiren kluger Heerdenmenschen die Befehlshaber zu ersetzen*) — an experiment that produces not leadership but herd governance.
- [005-022] The herd instinct "draws its conclusion step by step": the highest and strongest drives, when they drive an individual above the average, are felt as a danger to the community. "The high independent intellectuality, the will to stand alone, great reason itself are already felt as danger" (*Die hohe unabhängige Geistigkeit, der Wille, zum Alleinstehn, die grosse Vernunft schon werden als Gefahr empfunden*).
- [005-024] Nietzsche's definitive formulation: "Morality in Europe today is herd-animal morality" (*Moral ist heute in Europa Heerdenthier-Moral*). This is not a description of a fringe phenomenon but of the dominant value system of modernity.
- [005-028] The endpoint of this trajectory: "The total degeneration of man, down to what today appears to the socialist blockheads and flatheads as their 'man of the future' — as their ideal! — this degeneration and diminution of man into the perfect herd animal" (*zur Verkleinerung des Menschen zum vollkommenen Heerdenthiere*).

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Herd morality is the organizing concept of Nietzsche's critique of modernity. It connects his analysis of democracy, socialism, utilitarianism, and the "religion of pity" into a single diagnostic framework. All these movements, despite their surface differences, share a common core: the reduction of the human being to a safe, manageable, average type.

The concept also illuminates Nietzsche's aristocratic radicalism. Against herd morality, he posits the "pathos of distance" — the sense of rank and hierarchy that alone makes possible the production of higher types. The danger of herd morality is not that it is "wrong" in some philosophical sense, but that it makes greatness impossible by pathologizing the very qualities — independence, hardness, the will to command — that greatness requires.
