# Skepticism

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche offers a nuanced and ambivalent analysis of skepticism (*Skepsis*). On one hand, skepticism is diagnosed as a symptom of physiological and cultural decline — the "spiritual expression of a certain complex physiological condition" that arises when long-separated races and classes are suddenly mixed. The modern European, product of radical class-mixing, is "skeptical in all heights and depths," suffering from what Nietzsche calls "will paralysis." On the other hand, Nietzsche identifies a "harder" form of skepticism — the skepticism of "daring masculinity" associated with Frederick the Great — that is a necessary stage in the education of the genuine philosopher.

This dual character of skepticism reflects Nietzsche's broader method: rather than simply refuting or embracing a philosophical position, he asks what type of human being it expresses. The skepticism of modern Europe expresses weakness; the skepticism of the future philosopher expresses strength and intellectual courage.

## Key Passages

- [006-012] Nietzsche offers his physiological diagnosis: "Skepticism is the most spiritual expression of a certain complex physiological condition that in common language is called nervous weakness and sickliness." It arises when long-separated races or classes suddenly cross, producing disequilibrium in "body and soul."
- [006-013] Modern Europe, "the stage of an insanely sudden attempt at radical class- and consequently race-mixing," is therefore "skeptical in all heights and depths" — a skepticism that Nietzsche diagnoses as "will paralysis" dressed up in the "beautiful show- and lie-costumes" of objectivity and scientific detachment.
- [006-016] Against this weak skepticism, Nietzsche identifies "that more dangerous and harder new kind of skepticism" that grew in the young Frederick the Great — "the skepticism of daring masculinity, which is closely related to the genius for war and conquest." This form of skepticism "despises and nevertheless seizes; it undermines and takes possession; it does not believe, but does not lose itself in the process."
- [006-018] The philosophers of the future will be "critics and experimenters" who must have passed through skepticism but are not defined by it. They will possess "the certainty of standards of value, the conscious handling of a unity of method, the shrewd courage of standing alone."

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Skepticism is a key case study in Nietzsche's method of reading philosophical positions as symptoms of underlying physiological and psychological conditions. Rather than arguing for or against skepticism on epistemological grounds, Nietzsche asks: what does it mean about a culture that it has become skeptical? His answer — that modern skepticism is a form of "will paralysis" caused by the mixing of classes and races — transforms an epistemological problem into a cultural-diagnostic one.

At the same time, Nietzsche preserves a constructive role for skepticism in the education of the philosopher. The "hard" skepticism of Frederick the Great — the capacity to doubt without being destroyed by doubt, to suspend belief while retaining the capacity for decisive action — is a crucial preparation for the philosopher's ultimate task of creating values. The future philosopher must have been a skeptic, but must ultimately overcome skepticism.
