# Honesty

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, intellectual honesty (*Redlichkeit*) occupies a unique and unstable position among Nietzsche's virtues. It is "our virtue, from which we cannot escape, we free spirits" ([007-015]) — the one moral remnant that survives the critique of morality. Unlike the other virtues, which Nietzsche subjects to genealogical dissolution, honesty is treated as genuinely binding: it is "the conscience of method" ([002-018]), the imperative to follow a thought wherever it leads, even when it threatens our dearest beliefs.

Yet this very honesty is ambiguous. It is the "youngest virtue," still immature and uncertain of its own character. It can become a form of moral vanity — "a sort of cruelty, a majestic, ultimate form of cruelty" toward oneself ([007-015]). And most significantly, honesty contains within itself a self-destructive potential: if pursued consistently, it must eventually turn against morality itself, asking whether the will to truth — and the honesty that serves it — is ultimately life-affirming or life-denying. Nietzsche's treatment of honesty thus enacts the very self-overcoming of morality that his philosophy prescribes.

## Key Passages

- [007-015] Nietzsche's most extended treatment: "Honesty, supposing that this is our virtue, from which we cannot escape, we free spirits — well, we want to work on it with all malice and love, and not grow tired of 'perfecting' ourselves in our virtue, the only one left to us." But he immediately foresees a danger: "And should our honesty one day grow weary and sigh and stretch its limbs and find us too hard, and want things better, easier, gentler, like a pleasant vice: let us remain *hard*, we last Stoics!"

- [007-016] Nietzsche warns of honesty's tendency to become a form of moral display: "Let us be on our guard that we do not, out of honesty, finally become saints and bores!" The pursuit of honesty can itself become a new form of moral pretension — precisely the kind of self-deception that honesty is meant to combat.

- [002-006] Cynicism is identified as an intermediate form of honesty: "Cynicism is the only form in which common souls touch upon what honesty is; and the higher human being has to prick up his ears at every coarser and finer cynicism." The cynic approximates honesty through negation and mockery, but genuine honesty requires something more — the affirmative capacity to face truth without resentment.

- [002-018] Methodological honesty is commanded: "In the end it is not only permitted to make this attempt: it is *commanded* by the conscience of method." The philosopher, unlike the dogmatist, must follow the argument wherever it leads — even if it leads to the dissolution of the philosophical project itself.

- [001-002] The will to truth — and the honesty behind it — has a history: "The will to truth, which will still seduce us into many a wager, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers have hitherto spoken with reverence — what questions has this will to truth not already laid before us!" Honesty has driven philosophy since its inception, but its own foundations remain unexamined.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Honesty is the pivot on which Nietzsche's critique of morality turns. It is itself a moral remnant — the last surviving virtue after the death of God — but it is the one virtue that can be turned against morality itself. If we are honest enough, we will eventually ask: *why truth?* The will to truth, served by honesty, may itself be a disguised will to death — a form of the ascetic ideal. The person who is most honest may be the one who can finally dispense with the unconditional demand for truth.

This makes honesty the most Nietzschean of virtues: it is a virtue that contains its own self-overcoming. To be truly honest is to recognize that honesty itself is not an absolute value but a tool of life — and that there are conditions under which life requires illusion, simplification, and untruth. The "free spirit" does not abandon honesty but learns to wield it without the moral fanaticism that would make it into a new categorical imperative.

Nietzsche's treatment of honesty distinguishes it from mere truthfulness (*Wahrhaftigkeit*). Truthfulness is the disposition to tell the truth; honesty is the deeper disposition to *face* the truth, including truths about oneself and one's own motives. It is a form of intellectual courage — the willingness to think against oneself.
