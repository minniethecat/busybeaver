# Ressentiment

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, ressentiment is the psychological mechanism through which slave morality originates. It is the reactive emotion of the powerless — the feeling that arises when one cannot act on one's impulses and instead turns the impulse inward, nursing grievance into a moral system. Ressentiment is not merely anger or envy; it is a *creative* force that invents new values. The slave's inability to take revenge becomes "forgiveness"; his weakness becomes "meekness"; his fear becomes "piety."

Nietzsche's analysis of ressentiment in *Beyond Good and Evil* builds on but differs from the more famous treatment in *On the Genealogy of Morals*. Here the concept is developed through the distinction between master morality and slave morality — the master creates values spontaneously out of self-affirmation, while the slave creates values *reactively*, by first saying "no" to what is outside, to what is different, to what is *not himself*.

## Key Passages

- [005-019] The herd animal in Europe presents itself as the only permitted type and glorifies its own qualities as "the properly human virtues." This self-glorification — sociability, benevolence, pity — is the mature expression of ressentiment: values created by saying "no" to masterly qualities of strength, independence, and command.
- [009-005] Nietzsche draws the fundamental distinction: moral value-distinctions have arisen either "under a ruling kind which became conscious of its difference from the ruled with a feeling of well-being — or among the ruled, the slaves and dependents of every degree." In the second case, ressentiment generates the opposition "good" and "evil" — a revenge taken in the realm of values.
- [009-006] The noble human being helps the unfortunate not out of pity but "out of an impulse generated by the over-abundance of power (*aus einem Drang, den der Überfluss von Macht erzeugt*)." The contrast with ressentiment-driven pity is crucial: the noble helps from fullness, the slave from reactive identification with suffering.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Ressentiment is arguably the most important psychological concept in Nietzsche's moral philosophy. It explains how values that appear to be the product of free moral reasoning are actually the disguised expression of powerlessness. The "slave revolt in morals" — the historical process by which Judeo-Christian values displaced aristocratic values — was driven by ressentiment and succeeded by making strength feel guilty about itself.

The concept also illuminates Nietzsche's critique of modern egalitarianism. Democracy, socialism, and humanitarian morality are, in Nietzsche's analysis, secularized expressions of the same ressentiment that produced Christian morality. The demand for equal rights is the demand that no one be stronger than anyone else — the political form of the slave's "no" to the master.
