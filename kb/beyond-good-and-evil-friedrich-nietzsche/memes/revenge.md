# Revenge

## Overview

*Rache* (revenge) appears in BGAE primarily in connection with the psychology of the religious disposition. Nietzsche analyzes the ascetic ideal as, in part, "a protracted revenge upon life" — a form of retaliation against existence itself, carried out through self-denial and the devaluation of the world. Distinct from *ressentiment* (which is the passive, internalized rancor of the powerless), revenge retains an active dimension: it seeks to strike back, even if the target is displaced onto life as such.

## Key Passages

- (003-017) — Nietzsche identifies in the ascetic ideal "a protracted revenge upon life." The ascetic does not merely withdraw from the world but punishes it symbolically, enacting through self-mortification a judgment against the worth of existence. This is revenge raised to the level of a metaphysical posture.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Revenge occupies a pivotal position in Nietzsche's moral psychology, standing between *ressentiment* (the passive internalization of hostility) and active *cruelty* (the direct exercise of power over another). The ascetic's revenge-upon-life is a hybrid: it is active in its directedness but reactive in its ultimate source — a response to suffering that would rather annihilate the world than fail to find a culprit.

Understanding revenge is essential for grasping Nietzsche's critique of morality. He argues that the demand for justice, the concept of guilt, and even the notion of a moral world-order are all sublimated forms of the revenge instinct. The noble type, by contrast, is characterized by the capacity to let go of injury — not from moral superiority but from a fullness that has no need of retroactive satisfaction.
