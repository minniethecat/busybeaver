# Christianity

## Overview

Christianity occupies a central position in BGAE as the historical vehicle of the slave revolt in morals — the force that, according to Nietzsche, inverted the natural hierarchy of values and declared the virtues of the noble type to be vices. Nietzsche's critique ranges from the psychological (Christianity as sacrifice of freedom and pride), through the philosophical (Christianity as anti-philosophical and anti-skeptical), to the cultural (Catholicism as more integral to Latin peoples than Protestantism to Northerners). Part 3, "The Religious Disposition," contains the most concentrated treatment, but Christian themes thread through the entire work.

## Key Passages

- (003-003) — "Christian faith is from the beginning sacrifice: sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-certainty of the spirit; at the same time enslavement and self-mockery, self-mutilation." Nietzsche depicts the psychological structure of Christian belief as a will to self-abasement.

- (003-008) — Christianity "prepared itself" when fear came to dominate religion among the Greek masses — the democratization of religious sentiment laid the ground for a religion of the fearful, the suffering, the *pöbel*.

- (003-020) — Nietzsche finds in Christianity and Buddhism a shared art: "teaching even the lowest to place themselves through piety in an illusory higher order of things." This is an analysis of religion's psychological function rather than a metaphysical claim about its truth.

- (003-023) — In one of the most compressed formulations, Nietzsche declares: "Christianity was hitherto the most fateful kind of self-conceit" (*die verhängnissvollste Art von Selbst-Überhebung*). The Christian claim to possess absolute truth about the good is, for Nietzsche, a form of arrogance disguised as humility.

- (004-016) — "Christianity gave Eros poison to drink: he did not die of it, but degenerated, into vice." The Christian condemnation of the erotic drive did not eliminate it but drove it underground, where it festered as pathological sexuality.

- (004-007) — "Not their love of humanity, but the impotence of their love of humanity prevents the Christians of today from — burning us." A penetrating observation on the transformation of Christian intolerance into a merely impotent resentment.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Christianity is the counter-type to everything Nietzsche affirms. Where he affirms the body, Christianity preaches the mortification of the flesh. Where he affirms hierarchy, Christianity preaches equality before God. Where he affirms self-overcoming, Christianity preaches self-sacrifice. The critique of Christianity is thus not a peripheral polemic but the negative image through which Nietzsche's positive philosophy — master morality, will to power, the free spirit — comes into focus.

The analysis of Christianity also exemplifies Nietzsche's genealogical method. He asks not "is Christianity true?" but "what type of human being does Christianity select for, and what does it do to the human type?" The answer — it promotes the sick, the timid, the mediocre — is the basis of his moral critique.
