# Morality

## Overview

Morality (*Moral*, *Moralität*) is the central target of Nietzsche's critical project in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Nietzsche approaches morality not as a philosopher defending or refining a particular ethical system, but as a natural historian and psychologist who asks: where does morality come from, what psychic needs does it serve, and what has it cost humanity? The book's pivotal chapter, Part 5 ("Toward a Natural History of Morals"), explicitly reframes moral philosophy as a descriptive rather than prescriptive enterprise.

Nietzsche's key insight is that there is no single morality — there are multiple, competing moral systems, each expressing the will to power of a particular human type. The fundamental distinction between [[Master-Morality]] and [[Slave-Morality]], introduced in Part 9, is the culmination of this analysis. Morality is not a divine command or a rational deduction but a historically evolved set of valuations that serve particular forms of life. The task of the philosopher is to see *through* morality rather than to build within it.

## Key Passages

- [005-001] Nietzsche announces the project of a "natural history of morals" (*Naturgeschichte der Moral*) — treating morality as a phenomenon to be studied, not a framework to be applied.
- [005-002] The moral sentiment in Europe is described as "fine, late, manifold" — a historical achievement, not a universal constant.
- [009-005] The declaration that there exist "master-morality and slave-morality" (*Herren-Moral und Sklaven-Moral*) — the conceptual centerpiece of Nietzsche's moral typology.
- [009-006] Moral value-designations were first applied to *persons* and only derivatively to *actions* — a genealogical claim that inverts traditional ethical reasoning.
- [009-014] The claim that the "morality of the herd" has become the sole morality recognized as having meaning — a diagnosis of modernity's moral flattening.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Morality is the axis around which Nietzsche's entire critique turns. His "immoralism" is not a rejection of all values but a meta-ethical position: existing moralities are symptoms of underlying physiological and psychological conditions, not deliverances of reason or revelation. By historicizing and psychologizing morality, Nietzsche aims to open the possibility of a "supra-moral" (*aussermoralische*) perspective — a standpoint from which new values can be created.

This analysis connects directly to Nietzsche's doctrine of the [[Will to Power]]: every moral system is an expression of a particular form of life seeking to impose its values on the world. The revaluation of all values (*Umwertung aller Werte*) is the practical consequence.
