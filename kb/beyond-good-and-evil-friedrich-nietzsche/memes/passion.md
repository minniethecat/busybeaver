# Passion

## Overview

*Leidenschaft* (passion) carries an ambivalent charge in BGAE. On one side, Nietzsche inherits the long philosophical tradition — from Plato through the Stoics to Christianity — that treats the passions as disturbances to be mastered or extinguished. On the other, he inaugurates a rehabilitation: the passions are the raw material of greatness, and the attempt to eliminate them is not wisdom but a form of life-denial. The key distinction is between a passion that serves life and a passion that dominates destructively — a distinction that maps onto the larger contrast between active and reactive forces in Nietzsche's thought.

## Key Passages

- (005-017) — Nietzsche describes how each person relates to the "degree of dangerousness in which the individual person lives with themselves; recipes against their passions, their good and bad inclinations, insofar as they have the will to power." The phrase is revealing: passions are not morally neutral but expressions of will to power; the question is not whether to have passions but which passions serve one's own ascent and which are recipes for self-diminishment.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The rehabilitation of the passions is a cornerstone of Nietzsche's anti-Stoic and anti-Christian ethics. Where the dominant moral traditions counsel *apatheia* (freedom from passion) or the mortification of the flesh, Nietzsche insists that the passions are not optional — they are constitutive of life itself — and that the attempt to extirpate them is itself the expression of a particular passion: the will to nothingness.

The concept of passion also illuminates Nietzsche's theory of self-overcoming. One does not overcome a passion from some passion-free standpoint; one passion overcomes another. The disciplined, organized self is not passionless but hierarchically ordered — a "social structure of drives and affects" in which some passions rule and others serve. The noble type is distinguished not by the absence of strong passions but by the capacity to give them form.
