# Command

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche develops a distinctive analysis of commanding (*Befehlen*) and obeying (*Gehorchen*) that cuts across psychology, morality, and social philosophy. Command is not merely a social relation between persons; it is first and foremost an internal structure of the will itself. Every act of willing contains within it a plurality of affects — a "commanding" part and an "obeying" part — and the experience of freedom of the will is the subjective feeling of the commander identifying with the successful execution of the command ([001-022]).

Nietzsche extends this psychological insight into a social typology. Human beings are distinguished by whether they are "born to command" or "born to obey." The noble type is characterized by the inherited art of commanding — an unselfconscious certainty in giving orders and taking responsibility. The herd type, by contrast, requires a commander external to itself — it cannot command itself and therefore seeks a shepherd. Modern European society, Nietzsche argues, suffers from a peculiar condition: a "moral hypocrisy of the commanders," in which those who occupy commanding positions cannot admit to themselves that they command, and instead disguise their will as obedience to higher principles ([005-019]).

## Key Passages

- [001-022] Nietzsche identifies the "most wondrous thing" about the will: "insofar as we are in any given case the one commanding *and* the one obeying at the same time... we are accustomed to disregard this duality and deceive ourselves about it by means of the synthetic concept 'I'." The feeling of freedom is the affect of superiority of the commander over the one who must obey. The will is not a simple faculty but a complex social relation internalized.

- [001-023] The "will" as commonly understood is an aftereffect: "Because in the great majority of cases willing occurs only where the effect of the command, that is, obedience, that is, the action, was *expected* to occur, the *appearance* has translated itself into the feeling, as if there were a *necessary effect*." The experience of the will as causally efficacious is an inference from the regularity of command-obedience relations.

- [008-022] Nietzsche describes the noble type's inheritance: "The essential feature of the good and healthy aristocracy... is that it does not feel itself as function but as *meaning* and highest justification — that it therefore accepts with a good conscience the sacrifice of countless human beings who must be *degraded* and diminished for its sake into incomplete human beings, into slaves, into tools." The commanding caste embodies "the inherited art of commanding and obeying — in both, that is the art of the commanding gaze, the commanding gesture, the noble attitude of the body."

- [005-019] Nietzsche diagnoses modern Europe's "moral hypocrisy of the commanders": "They do not know how to protect themselves from their bad conscience other than by playing the role of executors of older or higher commands... the herd-human in Europe gives himself the appearance of being the only *permissible* kind of human, and glorifies his qualities, which make him tame, peaceful, and useful to the herd, as the genuine human virtues." The modern commander cannot admit to himself that he commands.

- [003-018] The strong and independent are "destined, prepared, and predetermined for commanding, in whom reason and the art of a governing race become embodied." Religion historically provided these natural commanders with "more refined and more spiritual stimulations" for their commanding instincts.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The analysis of command and obedience is central to Nietzsche's critique of free will and his revaluation of moral psychology. By decomposing the will into a social structure of commanding and obeying parts, Nietzsche undermines the metaphysical notion of a simple, causally efficacious will. What we call "freedom of the will" is the affective experience of the commanding part's superiority — it is a feeling, not a metaphysical fact.

This analysis connects directly to Nietzsche's larger project in *Beyond Good and Evil*. The will to power — the fundamental drive that characterizes all life — expresses itself as command. Life itself is "will to power" because every living thing seeks to discharge its strength, to command rather than obey. The distinction between commanding and obeying types is therefore not merely sociological but ontological: it reflects different relations to the will to power itself.

The "moral hypocrisy of the commanders" is Nietzsche's diagnosis of the democratic age. Modern Europe has produced a situation in which command is still exercised — by states, by institutions, by economic powers — but no one acknowledges themselves as a *commander*. Everyone claims to be obeying something higher: the law, the people, morality, reason. This is the herd's ultimate triumph: it has made command itself shameful. Nietzsche's analysis thus serves both as psychology and as cultural critique, exposing the bad conscience at the heart of modern political authority.
