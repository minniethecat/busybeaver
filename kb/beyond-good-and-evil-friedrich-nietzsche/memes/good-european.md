# Good European

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Europe*, the "good European" (*gute Europäer*) is Nietzsche's term for a post-national type of human being emerging from the democratic mixing of peoples and classes in modern Europe. The good European is not a patriot of any particular nation but a citizen of the continent's shared cultural destiny — someone who has overcome the "national fever" that Nietzsche sees as a temporary and retrograde political pathology.

Nietzsche's vision of the good European is deeply ambivalent. On one hand, the process producing this type — the democratic leveling of classes, the mixing of races, the creation of a homogenized herd — is itself a symptom of decadence. On the other hand, this same process creates the *conditions* for a new kind of exceptional human being: the "good European" who, precisely because he belongs to no single nation, can command the entire continent's cultural resources. The good European is both the product of modern leveling and its potential overcoming.

## Key Passages

- [008-008] Nietzsche describes "the slow emergence of an essentially supra-national and nomadic type of human being which, physiologically speaking, possesses as its typical distinction a maximum of the art and power of adaptation." This "process of the *becoming European*" may be slowed by nationalist reactions but gains in vehemence and depth through them. The result is a useful, industrious, highly employable herd animal — but also, as the rare exception, "exception-humans of the most dangerous and attractive quality."
- [003-023] The "good European" is prefigured negatively in Nietzsche's critique of Christianity: those who governed Europe's destiny through "equal before God" were "not noble enough to see the abysmally different order of rank and rank-cleft between man and man." The modern European — "a diminished, almost ridiculous species, a herd animal" — is the product of two millennia of Christian leveling.
- [006-013] Nietzsche's diagnosis of the European condition: "Our Europe of today, the stage of an insanely sudden attempt at radical class- and consequently race-mixing, is therefore skeptical in all heights and depths" — suffering from "will paralysis." The good European must overcome this paralysis and acquire "one will, a long terrible own will."

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The concept of the good European reflects Nietzsche's engagement with the political reality of his time — the rise of nationalism, the unification of Germany, the emergence of mass democracy — and his attempt to think beyond it. Against both nationalist particularism and democratic leveling, Nietzsche posits a vision of European unity that is *aristocratic* rather than egalitarian: the good European is not the "citizen of the world" of liberal cosmopolitanism but a commanding type who has absorbed the continent's diverse cultural resources and forged them into a single, powerful will.

This concept also illuminates Nietzsche's critique of German nationalism, which he saw as a regression from the European destiny that figures like Napoleon and Goethe represented. The "good European" is, among other things, an anti-German figure — a rebuke to the *Reich* and its petty politics. Nietzsche's vision of a unified Europe under the rule of a new aristocratic caste anticipates, in a distorted form, later twentieth-century European integration — though Nietzsche's version is emphatically anti-democratic.
