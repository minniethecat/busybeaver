# Thing-in-Itself

## Overview

The *Ding an sich* (thing-in-itself) — Kant's term for reality as it exists independently of human perception — is among Nietzsche's most persistent targets in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Nietzsche does not merely dispute whether we can know the thing-in-itself; he argues that the very concept is a *contradictio in adjecto*, a logical absurdity generated by the "seduction of words" (*Verführung der Worte*). This critique belongs to Nietzsche's broader demolition of metaphysical realism and the correspondence theory of truth, clearing the ground for his perspectival epistemology.

## Key Passages

- (001-003) — Nietzsche traces the metaphysical impulse to posit a "thing-in-itself" to the human need to locate the highest values outside the world of becoming: "Rather in the womb of being, in the imperishable, in the hidden God, in the 'thing-in-itself' — there must their ground lie, and nowhere else!" (*im Schoosse des Sein's, im Unvergänglichen, im verborgenen Gotte, im „Ding an sich" — da muss ihr Grund liegen*). This is not an epistemological claim but a psychological diagnosis: the thing-in-itself is the projection of a metaphysical need.

- (001-018) — Nietzsche exposes the assumption behind "immediate certainties" like "I think" and "I will": these are treated as if cognition could grasp its object "pure and naked, as 'thing-in-itself'" (*rein und nackt seinen Gegenstand zu fassen bekäme, als „Ding an sich"*). The Cartesian cogito, no less than the Kantian noumenon, smuggles in the thing-in-itself as a presupposition.

- (001-019) — The decisive formulation: "'immediate certainty,' just like 'absolute knowledge' and 'thing-in-itself,' involves a *contradictio in adjecto*" (*„unmittelbare Gewissheit", ebenso wie „absolute Erkenntniss" und „Ding an sich", eine contradictio in adjecto in sich schliesst*). Nietzsche promises to repeat this "a hundred times": one should finally free oneself from the seduction of words. A certainty that is "immediate" is no longer a certainty; knowledge that is "absolute" is no longer knowledge; a thing that is "in-itself" is no longer a thing-for-a-knower.

- (002-015) — The belief in "immediate certainties" is diagnosed as "a moral naiveté" (*eine moralische Naivetät*). The epistemological commitment to the thing-in-itself is not a theoretical error but a moral one: it reflects the philosopher's need for a guarantee, a fixed point outside the flux of appearance.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's attack on the thing-in-itself is not a minor technical objection to Kantian epistemology — it is the pivot on which his entire anti-metaphysical project turns. If there is no "real world" behind the apparent one, then the entire structure of Platonic-Christian-metaphysical thought collapses: no Being behind Becoming, no noumenal self behind the empirical self, no moral law inscribed in the fabric of reality.

The rejection of the thing-in-itself clears the way for Nietzsche's perspectivism: the world that appears to us is *the* world, and the distinction between "appearance" and "reality" is itself a perspectival artifact. What we call "knowledge" is not the grasp of a mind-independent object but an interpretation that has become dominant, useful, and eventually taken for truth. This is why Nietzsche repeatedly ties the critique of the thing-in-itself to the critique of "immediate certainties": both are strategies by which philosophers try to stop thinking at a point that comforts them.
