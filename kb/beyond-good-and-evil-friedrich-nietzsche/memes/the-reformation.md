# The Reformation

The **Reformation** occupies an important but ambivalent position in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*. While Nietzsche devotes less explicit attention to the Reformation than to events like [[The Napoleonic Era|the French Revolution and the Napoleonic era]], his scattered references to Luther, the German Reformation, and their cultural consequences reveal a consistent analysis: the Reformation both continued and transformed Christianity's suppression of [[Instinct|noble instincts]], while inadvertently contributing to the development of [[Conscience|individual conscience]] that would later undermine religious authority itself.

## Overview

Nietzsche's treatment of the Reformation in *Beyond Good and Evil* reflects his broader genealogy of Christianity and its psychological effects. He sees Luther as a figure who intensified certain Christian tendencies — particularly the internalization of authority as conscience — while also, paradoxically, contributing to the "democratization" of religious experience that would eventually secularize into modern egalitarianism. The Reformation, in Nietzsche's framework, is a key transitional event between medieval Christianity and [[The Napoleonic Era|modern European culture]].

## Key Passages

- [003-003] Nietzsche contrasts the faith demanded by early Christianity — achieved "in the midst of a skeptical and southern-free-spirited world that had a centuries-long struggle of philosophical schools behind it and in it" — with later, less demanding forms of Christian belief. This passage implicitly frames the Reformation as part of Christianity's long transformation from a demanding aristocratic faith to a popularized, democratized religion.
- [003-008] Nietzsche traces the trajectory from Greek popular religion to Christianity: "Later, when the rabble comes to predominance in Greece, fear overgrows even in religion; and Christianity prepared itself." The Reformation represents, in this genealogy, a further stage in the popularization and internalization of religious fear.
- [008-017] Nietzsche makes a striking claim about Luther's cultural significance: "Compared with Luther's Bible, almost everything else is only 'literature' — a thing that did not grow in Germany." This ambivalent praise acknowledges Luther's immense linguistic and cultural impact on German identity while implicitly questioning whether this impact was ultimately beneficial.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The Reformation is significant for Nietzsche's thought in several interconnected ways:

1. **The genealogy of conscience**: Luther's emphasis on individual conscience before God represents a crucial stage in the development of what Nietzsche calls "bad conscience" — the internalization of authority that characterizes the moral epoch.
2. **German cultural identity**: Luther's translation of the Bible shaped the German language and, in Nietzsche's view, contributed to a certain German self-seriousness and moral profundity that Nietzsche both acknowledges and critiques.
3. **The dialectic of Christianity**: Nietzsche sees the Reformation as part of Christianity's self-undermining — by emphasizing individual conscience and direct access to scripture, it unwittingly prepared the ground for the Enlightenment's secularization of Christian values.
