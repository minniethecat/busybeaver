# Neighbor

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche subjects the moral concept of the neighbor (*der Nächste*) and neighbor-love (*Nächstenliebe*) to genealogical critique. Far from being a natural expression of human goodness, concern for the neighbor arises from fear — specifically, the fear of the neighbor once society has been secured against external threats. Neighbor-love, in Nietzsche's analysis, is not the opposite of egoism but a disguised form of the herd's demand for conformity, leveling, and the suppression of dangerous individuality.

## Key Passages

- [005-022] Nietzsche traces the origin of neighbor-morality to a specific historical moment: "After the structure of society as a whole has been established and secured against external dangers, it is this fear of the neighbor which again creates new perspectives of moral valuation" (*ist es diese Furcht vor dem Nächsten, welche wieder neue Perspektiven der moralischen Abschätzung schafft*). Once external enemies are neutralized, the neighbor — the person next to you — becomes the new source of threat, and morality emerges as a mechanism for managing this internal danger.
- [005-023] Nietzsche completes the genealogical picture: "Whoever makes the neighbor afraid is from now on called evil; the fair, modest, self-subordinating, equalizing disposition, the mean of desires, arrives at moral names and honors" (*wer dem Nächsten Furcht macht, heisst von nun an böse*). Morality is a domestication device: the dangerous individual is labeled "evil," while the tame, conforming individual receives moral approval. Neighbor-love is the positive face of this system of fear-management.
- [005-021] Nietzsche provides the contrast-type: artists like Leonardo da Vinci represent the stronger type that appears in the same periods when "that weaker type, with its desire for rest, comes to the foreground" (*wo jener schwächere Typus, mit seinem Verlangen nach Ruhe, in den Vordergrund tritt*). The morality of the neighbor belongs to the weaker type — it seeks rest, security, and the absence of disturbance from exceptional individuals.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's analysis of the neighbor is a microcosm of his broader critique of herd morality. What presents itself as altruism — love of neighbor — is revealed as a fear-based system of mutual surveillance and leveling. The Christian commandment to love one's neighbor is, in this light, the codification of the herd's demand that no one stand out, that no one be dangerous. The free spirit must learn to see through the moral prestige of neighbor-love and recognize it as one of the mechanisms by which the exceptional individual is held back.
