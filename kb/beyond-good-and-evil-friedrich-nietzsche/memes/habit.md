# Habit

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche analyzes habit (*Gewohnheit*) as a fundamental psychological mechanism underlying both the experience of willing and the resistance to unfamiliar truths. When we "will" something, a habitual muscular feeling (*eine Art Gewohnheit*) plays its part even without actual movement of arms and legs — habit is woven into the very fabric of volition. At the same time, habit operates epistemologically: our ears and intellectual habits (*meine Ohren und Gewohnheiten*) are so "antipodal" to certain truths that our first response is rejection — until our "last anger" learns to love the antipodes. Habit is thus both the mechanism that makes the will feel unified and the inertial force that must be overcome by the free spirit.

## Key Passages

- [001-022] In his analysis of the will, Nietzsche identifies habit as one of its constituent elements: "an accompanying muscular feeling, which, even without our setting 'arms and legs' in motion, through a kind of habit, as soon as we 'will,' begins its play" (*ein begleitendes Muskelgefühl, welches, auch ohne dass wir „Arme und Beine" in Bewegung setzen, durch eine Art Gewohnheit, sobald wir „wollen", sein Spiel beginnt*). The feeling of willing, which seems so immediate and unified, is actually a complex of multiple feelings, a commanding thought, and a habitual muscular sensation. The "freedom of the will" is essentially the affect of commanding — and habit is the bodily substrate that makes this affect feel natural.

- [003-008] Nietzsche recounts his encounter with a French author's religious sentiment: "These sentences are so antipodal to my ears and habits" (*Diese Sätze sind meinen Ohren und Gewohnheiten so sehr antipodisch*) that his first anger scribbled beside them "la niaiserie religieuse par excellence!" — until his "last anger" came to love these sentences with their "truth turned on its head." It is "so charming, so distinguishing, to have one's own antipodes!" (*Es ist so artig, so auszeichnend, seine eignen Antipoden zu haben!*). Habit here is the intellectual inertia that makes alien truths feel instinctively wrong — but the free spirit learns to overcome this initial revulsion and even to cherish what contradicts his habits.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Habit operates at the intersection of Nietzsche's psychology of the will and his epistemology of the free spirit. In the will, habit is the bodily feeling that naturalizes the affect of commanding — what we call "free will" is partly the sedimentation of habitual muscular sensations that accompany every act of volition. This analysis undermines the metaphysical idea of a free will standing outside the causal order: the will is a complex of feelings, thoughts, and habits, not a simple faculty. In epistemology, habit is the resistance that every new truth must overcome — the antipodal ears that instinctively reject what contradicts ingrained patterns of thought. The free spirit's achievement is not the absence of habit but the capacity to recognize habit as habit and to override it when truth demands. The noble type "has reverence for itself" — a habit so deep it becomes indistinguishable from character.
