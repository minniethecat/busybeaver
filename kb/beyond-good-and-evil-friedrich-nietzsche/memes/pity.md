# Pity

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche mounts a sustained critique of pity (*Mitleid*) as a moral value. Against the Christian and Schopenhauerian elevation of compassion to the supreme virtue, Nietzsche argues that pity is neither disinterested nor life-affirming. It belongs to the "religion of pity" (*Religion des Mitleidens*) that characterizes modern democratic culture — a culture that, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, values the alleviation of suffering above the production of greatness.

Pity is a key term in Nietzsche's genealogy of slave morality. Where the noble soul helps others out of an overflowing of power, the pitying soul helps out of weakness — identifying with the sufferer in a way that drags both down. Nietzsche's "revaluation of values" demands that pity itself be subjected to critique: who benefits from the universalization of pity? What type of human being does it produce?

## Key Passages

- [002-023] Nietzsche warns against remaining "attached to a pity" (*Nicht an einem Mitleiden hängen bleiben*), even when it concerns higher human beings whose rare torment we have glimpsed — suggesting that pity, even for the exceptional, is a trap that prevents clear judgment.
- [005-026] Nietzsche identifies "the religion of pity" (*die Religion des Mitleidens*) as one of the unifying features of modern democratic culture, extending its sympathy downward to animals and upward to God — an "extravagance of pity" that belongs to the democratic age.
- [007-008] "Where pity is preached today — and, properly heard, no other religion is preached anymore" — Nietzsche calls on the psychologist to listen carefully, detecting vanity behind the rhetoric of compassion.
- [007-014] Nietzsche draws a sharp contrast between two kinds of pity: the pity that targets "the creature in man" (*dem Geschöpf im Menschen*) — that which must be formed, broken, forged, and must suffer — versus his own "reversed pity" (*umgekehrtes Mitleid*) that resists this leveling compassion as "the worst of all softenings and weaknesses." Pity against pity: *Mitleid also gegen Mitleid!*
- [009-006] The noble human being helps the unfortunate not out of pity but "out of an impulse generated by the over-abundance of power" (*aus einem Drang, den der Überfluss von Macht erzeugt*) — a crucial distinction between noble assistance and slave compassion.
- [009-036] "When such a man has pity, well! this pity has value!" Nietzsche insists that pity in the higher type is qualitatively different from the "unmanliness" of what passes for pity in sentimental circles.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's critique of pity is central to his broader project of the revaluation of all values. Pity is the paradigmatic value of slave morality: it appears selfless, but Nietzsche unmasks it as a disguised will to power — the weak, unable to compete with the strong in strength, assert their power by moralizing weakness and making the strong feel guilty for their strength.

The critique of pity also connects to Nietzsche's immoralism, his rejection of Schopenhauer's ethics, and his argument that the "unconditional will to truth" is itself a form of the ascetic ideal. If pity is the supreme value, then suffering — which Nietzsche sees as essential to all genuine achievement and self-overcoming — becomes something to be eliminated rather than embraced. The result is a culture that produces comfort but not greatness.
