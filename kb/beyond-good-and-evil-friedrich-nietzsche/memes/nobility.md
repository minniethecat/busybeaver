# Nobility

## Overview

Nobility (*Vornehmheit*) is for Nietzsche not merely a sociological category but a *type of soul* — a way of being characterized by the instinct for hierarchy, the feeling of difference and distance from the lower, and a spontaneous self-affirmation that does not require external validation. Part 9 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, titled "What Is Noble" (*Was ist vornehm*), is devoted entirely to this concept.

Nietzsche's analysis of nobility has two dimensions. The historical dimension traces how noble societies produced value systems based on self-affirmation (master-morality), which were later overturned by the slave revolt in morality. The psychological dimension describes the noble type's characteristic traits: a "pathos of distance" (*Pathos der Distanz*), an unreflective certainty of one's own worth, and a responsibility that extends only to equals. The noble soul "accepts its own existence with gratitude" — it does not need to justify itself.

## Key Passages

- [009-001] Nietzsche opens Part 9 by declaring that every enhancement of the human type has been the work of an aristocratic society — one that believes in a "long ladder of rank order" and the pathos of distance.
- [009-002] The noble type of man feels *himself* as determining values — he does not need approval. His morality is self-reverence.
- [009-003] The distinction between noble and base: the noble soul is characterized by the instinct for rank, the base by the instinct for equality.
- [009-005] Nietzsche describes the noble person's concept of justice as fundamentally different from the ressentiment-driven demand for equal treatment.
- [009-006] The noble type's relationship to suffering: the noble person does not deny suffering but incorporates it into a larger, life-affirming perspective.
- [003-008] The historical psychology of the noble type — how aristocratic value equations (good = noble = powerful = beautiful = happy = beloved by god) were established and later inverted.
- [005-010] The noble morality is associated with "the capacity for, and the duty of, long gratitude and long revenge" — both equally signs of strength.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nobility is the anthropological foundation of [[Master-Morality]]. While master-morality describes a system of values (good/bad, self-affirmation), nobility describes the *type of person* who embodies those values. Nietzsche's interest in nobility is thus not nostalgic — he is not calling for a return to feudal aristocracy — but diagnostic and projective: he wants to understand what human qualities have been lost in the democratic leveling of modernity and what might be recovered in a future "new nobility."

The concept is deeply connected to Nietzsche's critique of [[Slave-Morality]], which he sees as the historical force that undermined noble values. It also connects to [[Will to Power]] — the noble type's spontaneous self-affirmation is a direct expression of the will to power, unmediated by ressentiment.
