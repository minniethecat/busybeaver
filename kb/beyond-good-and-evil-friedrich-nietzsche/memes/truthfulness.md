## Overview

Truthfulness (*Wahrhaftigkeit*) occupies a singular position in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*: it is the virtue that philosophy has always celebrated, yet it is also the virtue that philosophy has never been honest enough to examine. The opening of the book places truthfulness at the center of Nietzsche's critical project: "Der Wille zur Wahrheit, der uns noch zu manchem Wagnisse verführen wird, jene berühmte Wahrhaftigkeit, von der alle Philosophen bisher mit Ehrerbietung geredet haben" (The will to truth, which will yet seduce us into many a venture, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers have hitherto spoken with reverence).

Nietzsche's question is not whether truthfulness is a virtue, but what truthfulness itself *is*, what it *wants*, and what it *costs*. This reflexive turn — applying the demand for truthfulness to truthfulness itself — is the characteristic Nietzschean move. The result is not the abandonment of truthfulness but its radicalization: a truthfulness that no longer spares its own presuppositions.

## Key Passages

- **[001-001]** — The book's opening gesture places truthfulness at the heart of the inquiry: "Der Wille zur Wahrheit, der uns noch zu manchem Wagnisse verführen wird, jene berühmte Wahrhaftigkeit, von der alle Philosophen bisher mit Ehrerbietung geredet haben: was für Fragen hat dieser Wille zur Wahrheit uns schon vorgelegt!" (The will to truth, which will yet seduce us into many a venture, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers have hitherto spoken with reverence: what questions this will to truth has already laid before us!). Truthfulness is both the subject and the instrument of Nietzsche's critique — it is truthfulness that demands we interrogate the value of truth itself.

- **[001-002]** — Nietzsche deepens the interrogation: "welche Fragen! welcher wunderlichen schlimmen fragwürdigen Fragen!" (What questions! What strange, wicked, questionable questions!). The questions that truthfulness forces upon us are not comfortable — they are "schlimm" (wicked, bad) and "fragwürdig" (questionable in a double sense: both worth questioning and of questionable character). Truthfulness leads not to reassurance but to vertigo.

- **[004-017]** — The aphorism that distills the entire problem: "über Das, was 'Wahrhaftigkeit' ist, war vielleicht noch Niemand wahrhaftig genug" (about what "truthfulness" is, perhaps no one has yet been truthful enough). This is the reflexive crisis: truthfulness has never been applied to itself. Every philosopher who has praised truthfulness has done so without ever truly interrogating what they were praising — what desires, what fears, what will to power speaks through the demand for truth.

## Significance

1. **Truthfulness as the Self-Consuming Virtue**: Nietzsche's treatment of truthfulness reveals it as a virtue that, when pursued consistently, consumes its own foundations. Truthfulness demands that we question the value of truth; it demands that we ask what will to power speaks through the will to truth. A truthfulness that refuses this reflexive turn is not truthfulness at all but a disguised form of piety.

2. **The Opening Gambit of BGAE**: The placement of truthfulness at the very opening of *Beyond Good and Evil* (001-001, 001-002) is strategically crucial. Nietzsche does not begin with a doctrine or a thesis but with a *question* — a question forced upon us by the very virtue that philosophy has always claimed as its own. The entire book unfolds from this self-interrogation of truthfulness.

3. **The Gap Between Profession and Practice**: Nietzsche's aphorism (004-017) identifies a structural hypocrisy in the philosophical tradition: philosophers have *spoken* of truthfulness with reverence while never *being* truthful about truthfulness itself. This gap between profession and practice is not incidental; it is constitutive of the philosophical tradition's self-understanding.

4. **Beyond Nihilism**: Nietzsche's radicalization of truthfulness does not lead to the abandonment of truth but to its revaluation. The will to truth, driven to its logical extreme, turns against the moral prejudices that have hitherto accompanied it — including the prejudice that truth is always valuable, always life-serving, always preferable to illusion.

## Related Concepts

- **[[Will to Truth]]** — Truthfulness is the virtue-form of the will to truth; Nietzsche's critique of truthfulness is inseparable from his critique of the will to truth.
- **[[Knowledge]]** — The pursuit of knowledge has traditionally been justified by the value of truthfulness; Nietzsche's interrogation of truthfulness destabilizes this justification.
- **[[Skepticism]]** — Nietzsche's truthfulness is a form of skepticism turned inward — a skepticism that doubts not only external claims but its own motives and presuppositions.
- **[[Free Spirit]]** — The free spirit is defined in part by this radicalized truthfulness: the capacity to question what is most sacred, including the sacredness of truth itself.
