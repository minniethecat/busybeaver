# French Revolution

The **French Revolution** (1789–1799) serves as a pivotal reference point in Nietzsche's analysis of European morality in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Nietzsche treats the Revolution not merely as a political event but as a moral and psychological watershed that reveals the transformation of European values.

## Overview

For Nietzsche, the French Revolution represents the culmination of Enlightenment ideals and their practical consequences. He analyzes it as a manifestation of the "slave revolt in morals" — the moment when modern egalitarian and democratic values achieved political expression. Nietzsche's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: he recognizes the Revolution's world-historical significance while critiquing the moral assumptions underlying it.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The French Revolution embodies the tension at the heart of Nietzsche's political thought. It represents both the triumph of slave-morality (egalitarianism, rights of man) and the expression of will-to-power on a historical scale. Nietzsche's analysis of the Revolution informs his critique of democracy, socialism, and all movements that seek to level human differences.
