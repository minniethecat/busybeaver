## Overview

Courage is pervasive in *Beyond Good and Evil* — not primarily as physical bravery but as *intellectual* courage: the willingness to think dangerous thoughts, to question the most deeply held convictions, and to stand alone against the herd. Nietzsche treats courage as both a diagnostic sign of the noble type and a prerequisite for genuine philosophy. The philosopher must have the courage to venture where conventional morality fears to tread, to "live dangerously" (*gefährlich leben*) in the realm of ideas.

Courage is also the virtue that enables all other virtues of the free spirit. Without courage, [[Honesty|intellectual honesty]] collapses into mere cynicism; without courage, [[Solitude|solitude]] becomes mere isolation; without courage, the [[Will to Truth|will to truth]] becomes a timid academic exercise. Courage is what transforms curiosity into philosophy.

## Key Passages

- **[004-009]** — "Die grossen Epochen unsres Lebens liegen dort, wo wir den Muth gewinnen, unser Böses als unser Bestes umzutaufen" (The great epochs of our life lie where we gain the courage to rebaptize our evil as our best). Courage is the act of transvaluation: calling what was condemned "evil" by the name of "best."

- **[006-017]** — Nietzsche describes the new German spirit as characterized by "Unerschrockenheit des Blicks, als Tapferkeit und Härte der zerlegenden Hand, als zäher Wille zu gefährlichen Entdeckungsreisen" (fearlessness of the glance, courage and hardness of the dissecting hand, tenacious will to dangerous voyages of discovery). Philosophical courage is exploratory, surgical, and relentless.

- **[002-009]** — Independence is "ein Vorrecht der Starken" (a privilege of the strong). The courage to be independent — to stand without the support of dogma, tradition, or the herd — is the foundational virtue of the free spirit.

## Significance

1. **Courage and Transvaluation**: The act of "rebaptizing" evil as best (004-009) is the core of Nietzsche's transvaluation of all values. This requires not just insight but *courage* — the will to affirm what has been condemned.

2. **Courage and Knowledge**: For Nietzsche, knowledge is not a passive reception but an active *conquest*. The will to know is a form of courage — "der zähe Wille zu gefährlichen Entdeckungsreisen" (the tenacious will to dangerous voyages of discovery) (006-017).

3. **Courage and Nobility**: The noble type is characterized by "Tapferkeit" — not as one virtue among others but as the condition of all noble virtues. The noble does not seek safety; the noble seeks danger and overcomes it.
