## Overview

Honor (*Ehre*) appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in two fundamentally opposed senses. On one side, there is the "philosopher's honor" — a self-bestowed distinction that arises from intellectual integrity and independence from moral prejudice. On the other, there are "moral honors" — the esteem distributed by the herd to those who conform to its values.

Nietzsche's treatment of honor thus mirrors his larger distinction between [[Master-Morality|master-morality]] and [[Slave-Morality|slave-morality]]: honor can be *self-created* or *other-bestowed*.

The philosopher's honor is closely tied to [[Honesty|intellectual honesty]] (*Redlichkeit*). To have the "honor of philosophers" is to refuse to be "merely moral" — to place truthfulness above comfort, above social approval, above morality itself.

## Key Passages

- **[002-016]** — Nietzsche speaks of what "Philosophen Ehre macht" (makes the honor of philosophers): the refusal to be "nur moralische Menschen" (merely moral human beings). The philosopher's honor is defined by a transcendence of morality.

- **[002-016]** (continued) — "Von der Moral abgesehn, ist jener Glaube eine Dummheit, die uns wenig Ehre macht!" (Apart from morality, that belief is a stupidity that does us little honor!). The philosopher measures honor not by moral conformity but by intellectual courage.

- **[005-022]** — Nietzsche describes how "die gegensätzlichen Triebe und Neigungen kommen zu moralischen Ehren" (the opposing drives and inclinations come to moral honors). The herd instinct draws its conclusions about what deserves honor, and those conclusions always favor the gregarious and the safe.

- **[006-009]** — The "objective man" (der objektive Mensch) receives "die höchsten Ehren" (the highest honors) from his contemporaries. Nietzsche's description drips with irony: the objective man is honored because he is harmless, a mirror rather than a commander.

- **[001-002]** — Philosophers have historically spoken with "Ehrerbietung" (honor-paying reverence) of the will to truth. But this reverence is itself unexamined. Nietzsche's project begins by questioning what philosophers have honored without question.

## Significance

1. **Honor and Moral Independence**: For Nietzsche, genuine honor is incompatible with conformity to prevailing moral norms. The philosopher who seeks "moral honors" from the herd has already ceased to be a philosopher. True honor comes from intellectual independence (002-016).

2. **Honor as Diagnostic**: The distribution of honors in a society reveals its values. That the "objective man" receives the highest honors in the modern age is a symptom of democratic leveling. What a culture honors tells you what it values and what it fears (006-009).

3. **Honor and the Noble Type**: In noble morality, honor is self-bestowed — the noble soul confers value upon itself. In slave morality, honor must be given by others — by the herd, by public opinion, by institutions. Nietzsche's philosopher must recover the noble capacity for self-honoring (005-022).

4. **Honor and Truthfulness**: The philosopher's honor is inseparable from [[Honesty|Redlichkeit]] — the intellectual conscience that refuses to deceive oneself. The honorable philosopher follows the will to truth without regard for the honors or dishonors it may bring (001-002).
