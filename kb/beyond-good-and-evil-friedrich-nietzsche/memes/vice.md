# Vice

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche treats vice (*Laster*) not as a simple moral category but as a phenomenon requiring genealogical analysis. Vice is what virtue becomes when it degenerates under hostile conditions — Christianity "gave Eros poison to drink: he did not die of it, but degenerated, into vice" (*entartete, zum Laster*). Conversely, Nietzsche's own "vice" is an insatiable curiosity that drives philosophical inquiry — "a curiosity of my kind remains after all the most agreeable of all vices" (*die angenehmste aller Laster*). The concept thus operates on two levels: the critique of how moral systems convert healthy drives into vices, and the reclaiming of "vice" as a name for the philosopher's dangerous passion for truth.

## Key Passages

- [004-016] In one of the most striking epigrams of Part 4, Nietzsche declares: "Christianity gave Eros poison to drink: — he did not die of it, but degenerated, into vice" (*Das Christenthum gab dem Eros Gift zu trinken: — er starb zwar nicht daran, aber entartete, zum Laster*). The aphorism encapsulates Nietzsche's genealogical method: what moral systems condemn as "vice" is often a healthy drive that has been poisoned and deformed. Eros — the life-affirming erotic drive — does not disappear under Christian repression; it mutates into something pathological that Christianity can then point to as proof of humanity's sinfulness. The poison creates the disease it claims to cure.

- [007-013] Nietzsche distances his own philosophical pity from pity for "the vicious and broken from the beginning" (*mit Lasterhaften und Zerbrochnen von Anbeginn*). His pity is a "higher, more far-sighted pity" (*ein höheres fernsichtigeres Mitleiden*): he sees how humanity is being diminished, not by vice in the conventional sense, but by the moralists who would eliminate suffering itself. The coupling of "vicious" with "broken" is significant — vice here names not active evil but the passive wreckage produced by moral systems that weaken the human type.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Vice occupies a strategic position in Nietzsche's critique of moral dualism. Where conventional morality treats virtue and vice as opposites, Nietzsche shows them as points on a continuum: virtue can degenerate into vice under hostile conditions (as Eros under Christianity), and what is called "vice" can be the expression of a healthy, powerful nature that refuses moral domestication. This is not a simple inversion — Nietzsche does not praise vice for its own sake — but a revaluation: the question is not whether something is a vice, but what conditions turned it into one, and what drive it expresses. The philosopher's own "vice" — insatiable curiosity, the will to know at any cost — is precisely what distinguishes genuine philosophy from scholarly timidity.
