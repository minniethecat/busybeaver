## Overview

The artist (*Künstler*) is one of Nietzsche's richest concepts in *Beyond Good and Evil*, appearing across the Preface and five chapters. The artist is not merely a producer of artworks but a fundamental human type — a creator whose relationship to truth, deception, and form makes them both kin to and rival of the [[Philosopher|philosopher]].

Nietzsche's treatment of the artist spans several dimensions: the artist as exaggerator and transformer who "makes larger" (006-019); the artist as master of concealment whose finest artistry goes unheard by the common ear (008-016); the artist as the highest development of the expressive drive — the "artists of world-literary education" who unite and mix the arts (008-031); and the artist as the figure whose relationship to truth is always mediated by appearance and deception. The artist, for Nietzsche, is the type who most fully embodies the doctrine that [[Appearance|appearance]] is more valuable than reality — because the artist *creates* appearances and knows them as creations.

## Key Passages

- **[001-015]** — Nietzsche lists artists alongside "Mystikern, Dreiviertels-Christen und politischen Dunkelmännern aller Nationen" (mystics, three-quarter Christians, and political obscurantists of all nations) as those to whom the Germans owe their gratitude. This ironic grouping places the artist in the company of those who traffic in concealment and obscurity — the artist is not a revealer of truth but a cultivator of depth and darkness.

- **[004-012]** — Nietzsche describes how the "massigen Künstler" (moderate artist) can be "einen sehr merkwürdigen Menschen" (a very remarkable person). The artist is remarkable precisely because of their capacity for measure — the "moderate" artist is one who has mastered the art of self-limitation, of knowing when to stop. This connects to Nietzsche's broader theme of [[Taste|taste]] as the discriminating faculty.

- **[005-021]** — In a brief but suggestive reference, Nietzsche mentions "Leonardo da Vinci" as an example of the artist whose creative power transcends the categories of his age. Leonardo represents the artist as the type who stands above the moral valuations of his time — the artist as creator of values, not servant of them.

- **[006-019]** — Nietzsche describes the experience of encountering a work of art: "jenes Werk entzückt mich: wie sollte es nicht schön sein?" (that work delights me: how should it not be beautiful?). The rhetorical question — "Oder: 'jener Künstler vergrössert mich: wie sollte er nicht gross sein?'" (Or: "that artist enlarges me: how should he not be great?") — captures Nietzsche's criterion for artistic greatness. The great artist *enlarges* the viewer. Art is not about representation but about enhancement — the artist who makes us feel larger is, by that very fact, great.

- **[008-005]** — Nietzsche analyzes the German style, describing it through the figure of the artist: the artist seems to say "sie gehört zu meiner Absicht" (it belongs to my intention). The German artist's deliberate heaviness, "etwas Willkürlich-Barbarisches und Feierliches" (something willfully barbaric and ceremonial), is a mask — a stylistic choice that conceals as much as it reveals. The artist's intention is itself part of the artwork's surface.

- **[008-016]** — Nietzsche observes that "die stärksten Gegensätze des Stils nicht gehört, und die feinste Künstlerschaft ist wie vor Tauben verschwendet" (the strongest contrasts of style are not heard, and the finest artistry is wasted as if before the deaf). The finest artistry — *Künstlerschaft* — requires a refined ear. Most readers are deaf to it. This is Nietzsche's lament for the artist in a democratic age: the subtlest creation is lost on an audience without the capacity for discrimination.

- **[008-031]** — Nietzsche describes "die ersten Künstler von weltlitterarischer Bildung" (the first artists of world-literary education) as "meistens sogar selber Schreibende, Dichtende, Vermittler und Vermischer der Künste und der Sinne" (mostly themselves writing, composing, mediating and mixing the arts and the senses). These artists — Wagner, Delacroix — are "Fanatiker des Ausdrucks 'um jeden Preis'" (fanatics of expression "at any price") and "grosse Entdecker im Reiche des Erhabenen" (great discoverers in the realm of the sublime). The artist as discoverer of the sublime — this is Nietzsche's highest praise for the artistic type.

## Significance

1. **The Artist as Enlarger**: Nietzsche's criterion for artistic greatness is not truth or beauty in the traditional sense but *enlargement*. The great artist "vergrössert mich" — makes the viewer larger, stronger, more capable. Art is evaluated by its effect on life, not by its correspondence to reality (006-019).

2. **The Artist and Deception**: Unlike the philosopher who seeks truth, the artist cultivates appearance and deception. But for Nietzsche, this is not a defect — it is a different relationship to the fundamental insight that [[Appearance|appearance]] is more valuable than reality. The artist knows what the philosopher merely suspects: that the surface is the depth.

3. **The Artist's Ear**: Nietzsche's lament that the finest artistry is "wasted before the deaf" is a critique of democratic culture. Artistic greatness requires an audience capable of discrimination — and democratic modernity, with its leveling of taste, produces deafness. The artist in a democratic age is a lonely figure (008-016).

4. **The Artist-Philosopher**: Nietzsche's ideal — implicit throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* — is the fusion of artist and philosopher. The philosopher must create values as the artist creates forms; the artist must think as deeply as the philosopher. The "artists of world-literary education" who are also "discoverers in the realm of the sublime" point toward this synthesis (008-031).

5. **Art and the Will to Power**: The artist is the most visible embodiment of the [[Will to Power|will to power]] as creative force. The artist imposes form on chaos, creates where nothing was, and transforms the given into the made. Artistic creation is will to power in its most affirmative mode.

## Related Concepts

- **[[Appearance]]** — The artist is the master of appearance (Schein); for the artist, the surface is not a lie but a creation.
- **[[Philosopher]]** — The artist is the philosopher's kin and rival; both create values, but through different means.
- **[[Music]]** — Music is the art form Nietzsche most associates with the Dionysian; Wagner and the "artists of world literature" are primarily musical figures.
- **[[Taste]]** — The artist requires taste (Geschmack) as the discriminating faculty that governs creation and self-limitation.
- **[[Depth]]** — The artist's finest artistry is hidden in the depths, unheard by the common ear; only the deep can hear the deep.
- **[[Will to Power]]** — Artistic creation is will to power as creative transformation — the imposition of form on chaos.
- **[[Culture]]** — The artist is culture's primary producer; culture without artists is mere civilization.
