# Johann Wolfgang von Goethe

**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** (1749–1832) appears throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* as one of Nietzsche's most consistently invoked exemplars of [[Nobility|noble spirit]]. Unlike [[Immanuel Kant]], whom Nietzsche subjects to sustained critique, or [[Arthur Schopenhauer]], whom he ambivalently admires and rejects, Goethe represents for Nietzsche a rare instance of life-affirmation — a spirit who embraced the fullness of the affects, resisted the leveling currents of [[The Napoleonic Era|modern democratic movements]], and achieved what Nietzsche calls the "instinct for rank" in aesthetic and intellectual form.

## Overview

Nietzsche's references to Goethe in *Beyond Good and Evil* span literary style, the philosophy of the affects, the question of German national identity, and the relationship between greatness and historical context. Goethe serves as a counterpoint to several of Nietzsche's critical targets: against the heaviness of German prose style, against the moralistic suspicion of the passions, against the democratic reduction of human types to a single measure. He is, for Nietzsche, a touchstone of what European culture could produce before the triumph of [[Slave-Morality|slave morality]] flattened its highest possibilities.

## Key Passages

- [002-008] Nietzsche contrasts the "solemn, heavy-footed, ceremoniously clumsy" German prose tradition with Goethe's style — an implicit recognition of Goethe as an exception to German stylistic heaviness.
- [005-018] Nietzsche praises "the bold surrender to the affects, as Hafis and Goethe taught it, that daring letting-go of the reins" — a celebration of what he calls the "spiritual-physical *licentia morum*" that belongs to the exceptional case of older, wiser spirits.
- [007-027] Nietzsche quotes Goethe's translation of Dante: "the Eternal-Feminine draws us upward" (*das Ewig-Weibliche zieht uns hinan*), noting that Goethe, like Dante, believed something about woman that Nietzsche finds characteristic of a certain noble idealism.
- [008-010] In the context of German nationalism, Nietzsche observes that Goethe "thought differently about the Germans than Jean Paul" — Goethe was not deceived by Fichte's patriotic flatteries, maintaining a skeptical distance from the nationalist fervor following the French Revolution.
- [008-030] Goethe is named alongside [[Socrates|Napoleon]], Beethoven, Stendhal, Heinrich Heine, Schopenhauer, and Richard Wagner as one of those exceptional humans about whom one should not allow oneself to be deceived by their own "self-misunderstandings."

## Relationship to Nietzsche's Philosophy

Goethe embodies for Nietzsche what he calls the "Dionysian" affirmation of life in its totality — a stance Nietzsche would later develop more fully but which is already prefigured in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Goethe's life-affirmation manifests in three forms that Nietzsche repeatedly endorses:

1. **Aesthetic**: Goethe's stylistic lightness and grace as an exception to German heaviness
2. **Psychological**: Goethe's embrace of the affects rather than their moralistic suppression
3. **Political**: Goethe's resistance to democratic leveling and his preservation of the pathos of distance

Nietzsche's engagement with Goethe also reveals his complex relationship to German identity. Goethe serves as evidence that a "good European" spirit was possible on German soil — a spirit that transcended the narrow nationalism Nietzsche saw emerging in Bismarck's Germany.
