## Overview

Fear (*Furcht*) occupies a foundational role in Nietzsche's moral psychology in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Where traditional philosophy treats morality as arising from reason, sympathy, or divine command, Nietzsche traces it to a more primal source: fear. Specifically, morality arises from *fear of the neighbor* — the fear of what others can do to us, and what we might do to each other, in the absence of shared norms.

Fear is also what the [[Nobility|noble type]] overcomes. The noble soul is characterized not by the absence of fear but by the capacity to act in spite of it. The herd, by contrast, organizes its entire existence around the management and minimization of fear — building institutions, moralities, and religions to protect itself from what is frightening: the strong, the dangerous, the exceptional.

## Key Passages

- **[005-022]** — "das Gefüge der Gesellschaft im Ganzen festgestellt und gegen äussere Gefahren gesichert erscheint, ist es diese Furcht vor dem Nächsten, welche wieder neue Perspektiven der moralischen Werthschätzung erschliesst" (once the structure of society is established and secured against external dangers, it is this fear of the neighbor that opens new perspectives of moral valuation). Fear of the neighbor — not reason or sympathy — is the engine of moral development.

- **[005-023]** — Nietzsche identifies the core imperative of modern European conscience as "den Imperativ der Heerden-Furchtsamkeit" (the imperative of herd-fearfulness): "wir wollen, dass es irgendwann einmal Nichts mehr zu fürchten giebt!" (we want that someday there will be nothing more to fear!). The herd's ultimate goal is the abolition of fear — and therefore the abolition of danger, greatness, and life itself.

- **[003-017]** — "Es ist die tiefe argwöhnische Furcht vor einem unheilbaren Pessimismus, der ganze Jahrtausende zwingt, sich mit den Zähnen in eine religiöse Interpretation des Daseins zu verbeissen" (It is the deep, suspicious fear of an incurable pessimism that forces entire millennia to sink their teeth into a religious interpretation of existence). Religious faith itself is driven by fear — fear of meaninglessness, fear of the void.

- **[005-012]** — Fear is among the most basic affects: "bei den 'einfachsten' Vorgängen der Sinnlichkeit [herrschen schon] die Affekte, wie Furcht, Liebe, Hass, eingeschlossen die passiven Affekte der Faulheit" (in the "simplest" processes of sensuality, the affects already rule — fear, love, hatred, including the passive affects of laziness). Fear is not a secondary response but a primal affect.

- **[003-009]** — The powerful of the world learned "eine neue Furcht" (a new fear) before the saint — the saint represented a new kind of power, the "will to power" in an unexpected form. Even the mighty are susceptible to fear when confronted with a form of power they do not understand.

## Significance

1. **Fear as the Hidden Foundation of Morality**: For Nietzsche, morality is not a triumph over fear but an institutionalization of it. The moral law is the expression of collective fear — fear of the strong, fear of the exceptional, fear of what lies beyond the herd's comprehension. The genealogical method reveals this unpalatable origin (005-022, 005-023).

2. **Fear and the Noble Soul**: The noble soul is defined partly by its relationship to fear. It does not seek to eliminate fear (which would be to eliminate life) but to *overcome* it. The herd seeks safety; the noble seeks danger and the self-mastery that facing danger requires (005-023).

3. **Fear and Religion**: Nietzsche traces religious belief to fear — not fear of punishment in the crude sense, but fear of meaninglessness, fear of the "incurable pessimism" that honest confrontation with existence would reveal. Religion is a fear-management system (003-017).

4. **Fear and the Genealogy of Morals**: Fear of the neighbor is the engine that drives the evolution of moral systems. As external dangers are neutralized, internal dangers — the strong, the independent, the dangerous individual — become the new objects of fear, and morality adapts to neutralize them (005-022).
