# Baruch Spinoza

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677) was a Dutch-Jewish philosopher of the Rationalist school. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche engages with Spinoza's philosophy with a mixture of admiration and critique, particularly regarding Spinoza's rejection of teleology and free will.

## Overview

Spinoza occupies an ambiguous position in Nietzsche's assessment of philosophers. Nietzsche admires Spinoza's intellectual courage — his rejection of final causes and his willingness to follow arguments to their logical conclusions — but critiques the persistence of theological language in Spinoza's pantheism.

## Relationship to Nietzsche's Philosophy

Spinoza's rejection of free will, teleology, and anthropomorphic conceptions of God makes him one of Nietzsche's most important predecessors. However, Nietzsche criticizes Spinoza's mathematical method and his retention of the concept of God (even as *Deus sive Natura*). Spinoza's *conatus* (the striving to persist in being) is transformed by Nietzsche into the will to power — a drive not merely to persist but to expand and overcome.
