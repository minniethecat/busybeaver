# Socrates

**Socrates** occupies a uniquely pivotal position in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*: he is the figure at whom the great historical turn from [[Instinct|instinct]] to reason, from the pre-moral to the moral epoch, first becomes visible. For Nietzsche, Socrates is not merely one philosopher among others but the embodiment of a decisive transformation in the history of [[Conscience|human self-understanding]] — the moment when reason was elevated above instinct and [[Master-Morality|life-affirmation]] began its long decline into [[Slave-Morality|reactive morality]].

## Overview

Nietzsche's treatment of Socrates in *Beyond Good and Evil* is characteristically layered. On one level, he offers a historical-psychological diagnosis: Socrates, an ugly plebeian who belonged to the "men of exhausted instinct" [006-022], turned against the instincts out of personal necessity and elevated his own compensatory rationalism into a universal moral principle. On another level, Nietzsche recognizes in Socrates a figure of immense historical consequence — the origin-point of a two-thousand-year error that systematically devalued instinct in favor of reason, body in favor of soul, and life in favor of abstraction.

## Key Passages

- [000-003] In the preface, Nietzsche alludes to Socrates with characteristic irony: "has the evil Socrates corrupted him after all? Would Socrates have been the corrupter of youth?" This sets up the frame of Nietzsche's engagement — an ironic inversion of the Athenian charge against Socrates.
- [005-010] In one of the most important passages, Nietzsche locates in Socrates the origin of the moral problem: Socrates, "with the taste of his talent — that of a superior dialectician — initially sided with reason" against instinct. When one is not up to the demands of instinct, Nietzsche argues, "one must side with reason." This was Socrates' personal necessity, later mistaken for a universal truth.
- [005-009] Nietzsche credits Plato with having "done everything to interpret something fine and noble into the proposition of his master" — namely, that reason=virtue=happiness — but the underlying impulse was, in Nietzsche's view, far less noble.
- [006-022] In a diagnostic passage, Nietzsche places Socrates among the "men of the exhausted instinct" in conservative old Athens — those who "let themselves go" in the name of happiness and pleasure, against whom Socrates' fierce rationalism emerged as a counter-movement.
- [006-012] Nietzsche cites Socrates' famous "I know that I know nothing" as an instance of skepticism — but the larger point is that even this admission belongs to a historical moment when instinctual confidence had collapsed.

## Relationship to Nietzsche's Philosophy

Socrates is, for Nietzsche, the founding figure of what he calls the "moral epoch" of humanity — the period in which actions came to be judged by their conformity to rational principles rather than by their consequences or their expression of noble character. This epoch-making shift carries several consequences that Nietzsche traces through the subsequent history of philosophy:

1. **The denigration of instinct**: By siding with reason against instinct, Socrates established the template for all subsequent moral philosophy, from Plato through Christianity to Kant.
2. **The invention of the "soul" as rational**: Socrates' equation of knowledge with virtue required a conception of the human being as essentially rational — a conception Nietzsche treats as a harmful fiction.
3. **The dialectical method as symptom**: Nietzsche interprets Socrates' dialectical method itself as a symptom of decadence — the triumph of argument over authority, of the plebeian over the noble.
