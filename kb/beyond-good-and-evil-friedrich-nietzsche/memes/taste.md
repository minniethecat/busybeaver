## Overview

Taste (*Geschmack*) is, for Nietzsche in *Beyond Good and Evil*, far more than a matter of aesthetic preference. It is a faculty of discrimination that operates at the intersection of aesthetics, morality, and cultural physiology. Taste is what distinguishes the noble from the base, the genuine from the counterfeit, the healthy from the decadent — and it does so not through rational argument but through immediate, almost visceral judgment.

Nietzsche's treatment of taste in BGAE has a double edge. On one hand, he deploys taste as a critical weapon against German cultural provincialism, diagnosing the coarseness of German taste as a symptom of deeper cultural sickness. On the other hand, he recognizes that taste itself can be perverted — the "good taste" of the herd can become a mechanism of exclusion that protects mediocrity from the challenge of genuine excellence.

## Key Passages

- **[002-021]** — Nietzsche invokes Stendhal as an exemplar of the "freigeisterischen Philosophen" (free-spirited philosopher), adding a characteristic emphasis: "den ich um des deutschen Geschmacks willen nicht unterlassen will zu unterstreichen" (whom I do not wish to neglect to underline for the sake of German taste). The implication is clear: German taste needs correction, and Stendhal — French, aristocratic, psychologically acute — is the corrective.

- **[002-024]** — "Es muss ihnen wider den Stolz gehn, auch wider den Geschmack, wenn ihre Wahrheiten gar noch Wahrheiten für jedermann werden sollen" (It must go against their pride, and also against their taste, if their truths should become truths for everyone). Truths that please everyone are, by that very fact, in poor taste. Taste is here aligned with intellectual aristocracy: what is universally palatable is suspect.

- **[007-008]** — "ein Körnchen Unrecht gehört sogar zum guten Geschmack" (a little grain of injustice even belongs to good taste). This provocative aphorism links taste not to fairness but to its opposite — a willingness to be slightly unjust, to risk being wrong, as a mark of intellectual vitality. The demand for perfect justice is, from this perspective, a form of bad taste.

- **[007-012]** — "Vielleicht steht unsre grosse Tugend des historischen Sinns in einem nothwendigen Gegensatz zum guten Geschmacke, mindestens zum allerbesten Geschmacke" (Perhaps our great virtue of the historical sense stands in a necessary opposition to good taste, at least to the very best taste). The modern historical sense — the capacity to understand and appreciate everything — may be the enemy of the capacity to judge and rank anything.

- **[008-007]** — A critique of the state-making spirit: "mache seinen Geist eng, seinen Geschmack 'national'" (makes his spirit narrow, his taste "national"). "National" taste is placed in scare quotes — it is not genuine taste at all but a political deformation of the aesthetic faculty. The state, in demanding national allegiance, corrupts the very organ of discrimination.

- **[008-012]** — "welche bäurische Gleichgültigkeit gegen 'Geschmack'! Wie steht da das Edelste und Gemeinste neben einander!" (what boorish indifference to "taste"! How the noblest and the most common stand next to each other there!). German culture — its arts and customs — is diagnosed as suffering from a profound incapacity to distinguish rank, to separate the noble from the common.

- **[009-031]** — "Man muss, um sich diesen wirklichen Luxus von Geschmack und Moralität gestatten zu dürfen, nicht unter Tölpeln des Geistes leben" (One must, in order to be able to permit oneself this real luxury of taste and morality, not live among louts of the spirit). Taste is a *luxury* — it requires an environment of spiritual refinement; among the coarse, taste cannot flourish.

## Significance

1. **Taste as Rank-Ordering**: Nietzsche's concept of taste is fundamentally about the capacity to distinguish, to rank, to establish hierarchies. Bad taste is the inability or unwillingness to discriminate between high and low, noble and base. The modern democratic sensibility, with its imperative to appreciate everything, is from this perspective a form of taste-corruption.

2. **Taste and National Culture**: Nietzsche's deployment of taste as a critical concept is directed with particular force against German cultural nationalism. "National" taste is a contradiction in terms — genuine taste is necessarily supra-national, a matter of European (and ultimately human) excellence rather than parochial pride (008-012).

3. **Taste and Truth**: The connection Nietzsche draws between taste and truth (002-024) is striking: truths that are palatable to everyone are in poor taste. This inverts the democratic-epistemological assumption that truth should be universally accessible and acceptable. Genuine truths — like genuine tastes — are exclusive.

4. **Taste as Injustice**: The aphorism that "a little grain of injustice even belongs to good taste" (007-008) is one of Nietzsche's most challenging provocations. It links taste not to the even-handedness of the judge but to the partiality of the connoisseur — the willingness to prefer, to exclude, to rank, which is always "unjust" from the perspective of egalitarian morality.
