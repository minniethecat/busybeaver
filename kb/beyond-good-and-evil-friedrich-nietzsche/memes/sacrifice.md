# Sacrifice

## Overview

Sacrifice (*Opfer*, *Aufopferung*) appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in several distinct but related forms: the psychologist's "sacrifice" of intellectual comfort, the philosopher's "martyrdom for truth," the Christian faith as an engine of sacrificial self-abasement, and the primordial sacrifices of early religions. Nietzsche does not treat sacrifice as a uniform concept; he distinguishes between sacrifice that serves the enhancement of life — the sacrifice of a partial self for a greater whole — and sacrifice that serves [[Christianity|Christian]]-ascetic self-denial, which he diagnoses as a disguised form of [[Will to Power|will to power]].

## Key Passages

- **[001-030]** — The psychologist "who thus brings sacrifice" (*welcher dergestalt „Opfer bringt"*) opens a deeper world of insight. Nietzsche immediately clarifies: "it is not the *sacrifizio dell'intelletto*, on the contrary!" (*es ist nicht das sacrifizio dell'intelletto, im Gegentheil!*). The psychologist's sacrifice is not the surrender of intellect but its intensification — a willingness to see what is ugly, dangerous, and painful in the human soul.

- **[003-003]** — "The Christian faith is from the beginning sacrifice: sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-certainty of the spirit; simultaneously servitude and self-mockery, self-mutilation" (*Der christliche Glaube ist von Anbeginn Opferung: Opferung aller Freiheit, alles Stolzes, aller Selbstgewissheit des Geistes; zugleich Verknechtung und Selbst-Verhöhnung, Selbst-Verstümmelung*). This is Nietzsche's most concentrated description of Christianity as a sacrificial system — it does not merely demand sacrifice; it *is* sacrifice, a total devaluation of earthly life in favor of a fictional beyond.

- **[003-012]** — Nietzsche invokes the primordial sacrifices of early religions: "the first-fruit sacrifices of all primordial religions, and also the sacrifice of the Emperor Tiberius in the Mithras grotto" (*die Erstlings-Opfer aller Vorzeit-Religionen, dahin auch das Opfer des Kaisers Tiberius in der Mithrasgrotte*). These sacrifices belong to the history of cruelty in religion — the human instinct to inflict suffering on what one loves most, as a transaction with the divine.

- **[002-004]** — The "martyrdom of the philosopher, his 'sacrifice for the truth'" (*Das Martyrium des Philosophen, seine „Aufopferung für die Wahrheit"*) reveals the agitator and the actor in him. Nietzsche suspects that the philosopher's proclaimed self-sacrifice for truth is often a theatrical performance, a bid for authority dressed as humility. The genuine thinker does not sacrifice himself *to* truth; he uses truth as an instrument of life.

- **[002-023]** — A warning: "Not to cling to our own virtues and as a whole become the sacrifice of some singular part of us, for example our 'hospitality'" (*Nicht an unsern eignen Tugenden hängen bleiben und als Ganzes das Opfer irgend einer Einzelheit an uns werden*). This is the danger of noble and rich souls who are prodigal with themselves — they risk being consumed by a single virtue. Nietzsche advocates for the whole, not the part; sacrifice must serve the total self, not diminish it.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's analysis of sacrifice cuts to the core of his critique of [[Morality|morality]] and [[Religion|religion]]. The Christian valorization of sacrifice — of self, of intellect, of earthly joy — is not, as it presents itself, an act of love. It is a refined expression of cruelty turned inward, a "*Grausamkeit gegen sich selbst*" (cruelty against oneself) that the [[Ascetic Ideal|ascetic priest]] channels into a system of control.

The alternative is the sacrifice Nietzsche describes in the psychologist: a sacrifice that is not a surrender but a sharpening — not the *sacrifizio dell'intelletto* but the willingness to think *against* comfort, *against* the herd, *against* one's own cherished beliefs. This is the sacrifice proper to the [[Free Spirit|free spirit]].

The distinction between these two kinds of sacrifice — life-diminishing and life-enhancing — maps onto Nietzsche's broader distinction between [[Slave Morality|slave morality]] (which values sacrifice as submission) and [[Master-Morality|master morality]] (which values sacrifice as self-overcoming). The question is never *whether* to sacrifice, but *what* is sacrificed and *for what*. Sacrifice in the service of a higher self is noble; sacrifice in the service of a fictional "beyond" is decadent.

## Related Concepts

- [[Christianity]] — the paradigmatic system of sacrificial self-denial, according to Nietzsche
- [[Cruelty]] — the psychological substrate of sacrifice; the pleasure in making suffer, even when the object is oneself
- [[Free Spirit]] — the type that practices intellectual sacrifice without self-abasement
- [[Religion]] — sacrifice as the original transaction with the divine, from primordial offering to Christian crucifixion
- [[Master-Morality]] — the value system in which sacrifice serves self-enhancement, not self-denial
