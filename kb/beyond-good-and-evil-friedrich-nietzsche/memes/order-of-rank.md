# Order of Rank

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, order of rank (*Rangordnung*) is Nietzsche's foundational principle for understanding values, cultures, and human types. It is the assertion that not all values are equal, not all human types are equal, and not all cultures stand at the same height. The recognition of order of rank — and the capacity to perceive it — is, for Nietzsche, the distinguishing mark of the noble soul and the precondition for any genuine enhancement of humanity.

Order of rank is not merely a social or political principle. It operates at every level: within the individual soul (which drives rule, which obey), between moral systems (master morality ranks above slave morality), between cultures (aristocratic cultures produce higher types than democratic ones), and even between philosophical problems and the states of soul capable of addressing them. The denial of order of rank — the egalitarian insistence that all are equal — is, for Nietzsche, the defining symptom of modern decadence.

## Key Passages

- [002-010] Nietzsche contrasts the exoteric and the esoteric as expressions of order of rank: the exoteric sees things "from below upward," the esoteric "from above downward." There are heights of the soul from which even tragedy ceases to be tragic — a claim that presupposes the reality of ranked perspectives on existence.
- [009-001] "Every enhancement of the type 'man' has so far been the work of an aristocratic society — and it will be so again and again: a society that believes in a long ladder of order of rank and difference of value between man and man and requires slavery in some sense or other." Without the pathos of distance between classes, there can be no internal pathos of distance within the soul — no self-overcoming.
- [009-015] Nietzsche identifies "an instinct for rank which, more than anything, is already the sign of a *high* rank." This instinct manifests as a pleasure in the nuances of reverence — a sensitivity to distinctions of value that coarse natures lack.
- [009-036] In a striking passage at the end of the work, Nietzsche proposes "an order of rank of philosophers according to the rank of their laughter — up to those capable of *golden* laughter." Even the capacity for laughter admits of hierarchy — and the highest philosophers laugh at the expense of all earnestness.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Order of rank is the unifying principle behind Nietzsche's critique of modernity and his vision of a higher culture. Modernity's cardinal error is not any particular moral judgment but its denial that there *is* an order of rank — its insistence that all values, all cultures, and all human types are fundamentally equal. This denial is self-serving: it allows the herd to present its mediocrity as the only permitted form of humanity.

The principle also connects Nietzsche's moral philosophy to his epistemology and aesthetics. Perspectivism — the view that there are no facts, only interpretations — presupposes that some perspectives are higher than others, that some interpretations are more life-affirming, more creative, more truthful in an extra-moral sense. Order of rank is what prevents perspectivism from collapsing into nihilistic relativism. The task of the philosopher is precisely to *rank* — to create and legislate values, to determine what is high and what is low.
