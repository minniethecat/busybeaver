# Life

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, *life* (*Leben*) is not a biological category but a normative one. It is the ultimate standard against which all values, truths, and moralities are to be measured. Nietzsche's fundamental question, repeated throughout the book, is: does this judgment, this morality, this truth *serve life* or does it *deny life*? The "value of life cannot be estimated" ([003-001]) — life is not something that can be judged from outside, because any judgment is itself an expression of life. Life is the condition of all valuation and cannot itself be weighed on any scale external to it.

Life, for Nietzsche, is essentially *will to power*: "Above all, something living wants to *discharge* its strength — life itself is will to power" ([001-016]). This is not merely a descriptive claim about organisms. It is a claim about the character of existence as such: reality is ceaseless becoming, growth, self-overcoming. The philosophical tradition's preference for being over becoming is a symptom of life-denial — a refusal to affirm the restless, self-transforming character of actual existence.

The imperative to "live according to nature" or "live according to life" is Nietzsche's answer to the Stoic and Kantian moral imperatives. But he immediately deconstructs this imperative: how could one do otherwise than live according to life? The demand reveals itself as a disguised will to *impose* a particular form on life — to make life conform to an ideal rather than to affirm life as it is.

## Key Passages

- [001-016] Nietzsche's clearest statement of life's character: "Above all, something living wants to *discharge* its strength — life itself is will to power: self-preservation is only one of the indirect and most frequent *consequences* of it." Life does not seek primarily to preserve itself but to *expand*, to *overcome*, to *command*. Self-preservation is a secondary effect, not the fundamental drive.

- [001-009] Life is distinguished from nature's indifference: "Life — is that not precisely wanting-to-be-*different* than this nature is? Is life not estimating, preferring, being-unjust, being-limited, wanting-to-be-different?" Life is not a harmonious continuation of nature's processes but a *revolt* against them — an imposition of form, value, and meaning on the indifferent flux of becoming.

- [001-010] Nietzsche deconstructs the Stoic imperative: "And assuming your imperative 'live according to nature' meant at bottom as much as 'live according to life' — how could you *not* do that? Why make a principle out of what you yourselves are and must be?" The moral demand to live "according to nature" is revealed as a concealed demand that nature conform to *our* ideal — "your pride wants to prescribe and incorporate *your* morality, your ideal, into nature."

- [001-024] Life as the ground of morality: morality is "understood as the doctrine of the relations of mastery under which the phenomenon 'life' arises." Morality is not imposed on life from outside but is the expression of the mastery-relations that constitute life itself. Different moralities express different forms of life — noble or base, ascending or declining.

- [004-009] The rhythm of life and self-overcoming: "The great epochs of our life lie where we gain the courage to rebaptize our evil as our best." Life's transformations are marked by revaluations — moments when what was condemned becomes affirmed, when the "evil" in us is recognized as our greatest strength.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The concept of life is the keystone of Nietzsche's entire philosophical project. It is the answer to the question that drives *Beyond Good and Evil*: on what basis can we judge values, if not on the basis of other values that are themselves in question? Nietzsche's answer is that life itself provides the standard — but not life as a fixed nature, for life has no fixed nature. Life is the *process* of valuation, the ceaseless activity of estimating, preferring, and creating hierarchies. The question "does this serve life?" is the ultimate philosophical question, and it replaces the older question "is this true?"

This has far-reaching consequences. Moral systems that preach self-denial, pity, equality, and the suppression of the instincts are condemned as *anti-life* — they are expressions of a declining, exhausted form of life that can only affirm itself by negating the conditions of life itself. The "slave revolt in morality" is a revolt of the weak against the strong, but more fundamentally it is a revolt of the *sick* against the *healthy* — of declining life against ascending life.

The affirmation of life — the "Yes-saying" that characterizes Nietzsche's ideal — is not a cheerful optimism. It is the capacity to affirm life *including* its suffering, its cruelty, its injustice, its necessary destruction. "The discipline of suffering, of *great* suffering," Nietzsche writes, "has created all enhancements of humanity" ([007-021]). Life is not gentle, and the affirmation of life requires the courage to affirm its hardness as well.
