# Scholar

## Overview

In Part 6 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, "We Scholars" (*wir Gelehrten*), Nietzsche develops one of his most sustained typological analyses: the distinction between the scholar (*der Gelehrte*) and the genuine philosopher. The scholar is not simply a less accomplished philosopher; he is a fundamentally different *type* of human being, animated by different instincts, serving different values, and producing a different kind of knowledge. "The scholar has, as is only fair, also the sicknesses and bad habits of an ignoble kind," Nietzsche writes ([006-008]) — the scholar's epistemic virtues are inseparable from his psychological flaws.

The modern age has witnessed what Nietzsche calls "the independence-declaration of the scientific man" — his emancipation first from theology (his old "maidservant" role) and now from philosophy itself. This emancipation is "one of the more subtle aftereffects of the democratic essence and non-essence" ([006-002]): the scientific man's rebellion against philosophy is the epistemic equivalent of the slave revolt in morals, the leveling instinct applied to the realm of knowledge. The scholar wants to be judged by his peers, by the democratic tribunal of the scientific community; the philosopher recognizes no judge but himself.

Nietzsche's portrait of the scholar is not merely polemical but diagnostic. He identifies specific psychological traits — the scholar's envy, his Luchsauge (lynx-eye) for the weaknesses of his competitors, his "objective" pose that masks a deep indifference to life — that constitute a coherent character type. The scholar is the "objective man," the mirror that reflects everything and creates nothing ([006-009]).

## Key Passages

- [006-001] Nietzsche opens Part 6 with a warning: "On the danger that moralizing here too will turn out to be what it always was — namely, an undaunted *montrer ses plaies*, after Balzac." The analysis of the scholar type is not a neutral sociological description; it is itself a dangerous provocation that risks revealing the wounds from which modern science suffers.
- [006-002] The democratic character of the scholar's emancipation is made explicit: "The independence-declaration of the scientific man, his emancipation from philosophy, is one of the more subtle aftereffects of the democratic essence and non-essence." The scholar's rebellion against the philosophical "master" mirrors the herd's rebellion against the noble — it is an assertion of equality that disguises itself as an assertion of intellectual autonomy.
- [006-003] Nietzsche characterizes the scholar through his memory: "My memory — the memory of a scientific man, with your permission! — bristles with naïvetés of arrogance." The scholar's arrogance is paradoxically naive — he does not know what he does not know, and his confidence rests on ignorance of the philosophical questions that his specialized research cannot address.
- [006-008] The psychological portrait is drawn with clinical precision: "The scholar has, as is only fair, also the sicknesses and bad habits of an ignoble kind: he is rich in small envy and has a lynx-eye for what is base in natures to whose heights he cannot reach." The scholar's envy — *kleiner Neid* — is the defining passion of the type: unable to rise, he devotes himself to bringing others down.
- [006-009] The "objective man" represents the scholar's ideal self-image: "The objective man, who no longer curses and scolds like the pessimist, the *ideal scholar* in whom the scientific instinct, after thousands of total and semi-failures, once blossoms and blooms forth." But this ideal is itself a diminution — the objective man is "an instrument, something of a slave though certainly the most sublime kind of slave, in himself nothing."

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The scholar-philosopher distinction is central to Nietzsche's diagnosis of modernity. The hypertrophy of the sciences — their "enormous tower and ramparts" ([006-005]) — threatens to make the philosopher impossible. The young person of talent is absorbed into specialization before she can develop the philosophical gaze; she becomes a "mirror" with a "smooth, lustrous surface" ([006-009]) that reflects objects with precision but creates nothing. The "objective man" is, in Nietzsche's devastating formulation, "in himself nothing" — a pure instrument of knowledge without a self to give that knowledge value.

Nietzsche's critique of the scholar is also a critique of the modern university, which institutionalizes the separation of knowledge from wisdom. The scholar is trained to be "industrious, grim, vengeful" — to accumulate facts and publish papers — but not to ask what any of it is *for*. The question of the value of knowledge — "not about the sciences, but about life and the value of life" ([006-006]) — is the philosopher's question, and the scholar is constitutionally incapable of asking it.

The scholar's positivism — his faith in "facts" as the foundation of knowledge — is for Nietzsche a symptom of philosophical immaturity. "There are no facts, only interpretations" — but the scholar cannot acknowledge this without undermining his own authority. The philosopher, by contrast, knows that every "fact" is already an interpretation, already the product of a particular will to power.
