# Future

## Overview

For Nietzsche, the future (*Zukunft*) is not merely what happens next but the primary horizon of philosophical activity. The genuine philosopher is a legislator who "determines the Wherefore and the Whither of man" — a shaper of the future human. This orientation toward the future distinguishes the philosopher from the scholar, who merely describes what already exists. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, the future appears as a contested space: it is the object of competing wills, the domain where new values must be created, and the temporal dimension in which Europe's cultural unification may unfold.

## Key Passages

- [005-027] Nietzsche declares that the task is "to force upon man the future of man as his will" (*Dem Menschen die Zukunft des Menschen als seinen Willen*). The future is not something that merely arrives — it must be actively willed and shaped through the imposition of new values.
- [005-028] Nietzsche reflects on how nature "has hitherto played its game regarding the future of man" (*in Hinsicht auf die Zukunft des Menschen ihr Spiel spielt*), suggesting that the future has been left to chance and that philosophical intervention is required to give it direction.
- [008-021] Europe is described in relation to "the future" (*die Zukunft*), tied to Nietzsche's vision of European unification and the emergence of a post-national cultural identity.
- [008-029] Nietzsche writes that whoever "dreams of the future of music" (*von der Zukunft der Musik träumt*) must also dream of a corresponding transformation of the human spirit, linking aesthetic futurity to broader cultural evolution.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The future is the proving ground of Nietzsche's philosophical project. His critique of existing morality, religion, and metaphysics is not an end in itself — it clears the ground for a future revaluation of values. The "philosophers of the future" whom Nietzsche invokes are those who will complete this task. The future thus functions as both a critical concept (exposing the inadequacy of present values) and a constructive one (orienting philosophical labor toward what is to be created). This forward-looking stance is integral to Nietzsche's anti-historicism: the historical sense must serve the future, not paralyze it.
