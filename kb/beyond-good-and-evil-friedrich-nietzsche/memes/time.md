# Time

## Overview

Time (*Zeit*, *Zeitalter*) in *Beyond Good and Evil* is not an abstract metaphysical category but a diagnostic tool. Nietzsche thinks in historical epochs, each with its own character, virtues, and pathologies. The modern age — Nietzsche's own "time" — is diagnosed as an *Auflösungs-Zeitalter* (dissolution age), a period in which the racial and cultural certainties of earlier epochs have been thrown into confusion. The philosopher's task is to be a "physician of the age" (*Arzt der Zeit*), diagnosing its sickness and prescribing against its prevailing pieties.

Unlike [[History|history]] (*Historie*), which Nietzsche treats in its own right as a concept, *Zeit* denotes the felt quality of an era — its speed, its taste, its unconscious assumptions. To think about time is to think about what it means to be *contemporary*, and for Nietzsche, true contemporaneity often requires standing *against* one's time.

## Key Passages

- **[005-020]** — The modern human is "a human from a dissolution-age, which throws the races through one another" (*Der Mensch aus einem Auflösungs-Zeitalter, welches die Rassen durch einander wirft*). This mixing produces a "multiplicity of ancestors" in a single body, resulting in internal chaos. The dissolution-age human carries contradictory instincts — "he has inherited much too much" — and must either master this chaos or be mastered by it. This is the biological substrate of Nietzsche's diagnosis of modernity.

- **[006-012]** — Nietzsche invokes the "physicians of the age" (*Ärzte der Zeit*) who prescribe skepticism "against the 'spirit' and its rumbling under the floor." Skepticism is a medicine for an age of noisy, ungrounded intellectual activity. The image of the philosopher-physician is central to Nietzsche's self-understanding: the thinker does not simply reflect his age but intervenes in it diagnostically and therapeutically.

- **[008-013]** — "'The good old' time is gone, in Mozart it sang itself out" (*Die „gute alte" Zeit ist dahin, in Mozart hat sie sich ausgesungen*). Nietzsche's nostalgia here is complex — it is not a longing for the past but a recognition that a certain European grace, embodied in Mozart's rococo, is irrecoverable. The "good old time" is not good because it was morally superior but because it had a coherence and lightness that the dissolution-age lacks.

- **[005-021]** — Nietzsche observes that in the same periods where the weak type — the dissolution-human — proliferates, the strong type also appears: "the genius (like Caesar and Napoleon), the predatory human, the tempter." This is Nietzsche's argument against simple historical pessimism: dissolution and greatness are not opposites but twins. The dissolution-age makes the herd sicker, but it also makes the exception more exceptional.

- **[000-002]** — Reflecting on the history of philosophy, Nietzsche writes: "the time is perhaps very near when one will again and again grasp what actually sufficed to lay the cornerstone of such sublime and unconditional philosopher-buildings" — namely, "some folk-superstition from immemorial time (*irgend ein Volks-Aberglaube aus unvordenklicher Zeit*)." Past time is the source of the prejudices that philosophy mistakes for eternal truths.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's treatment of time reveals him as a thinker of historical consciousness without historical determinism. The dissolution-age is not a stage in a necessary progression; it is a specific condition with specific symptoms — the mixing of races, the collision of values, the loss of a unifying taste. Against the historicism of his century (Hegel, Marx), Nietzsche insists that the meaning of an age is not given by a dialectical logic but by the values that dominate it — and values can be challenged, reversed, transvalued.

The philosopher as "physician of the age" is one of Nietzsche's most important self-portraits. It distinguishes the genuine philosopher from the [[Scholar|scholar]], who merely describes the age, and from the "free spirit" of democratic taste, who merely conforms to it. The physician diagnoses the age's sickness but does not share it; he is *untimely* (*unzeitgemäss*) — a concept Nietzsche developed earlier in his *Untimely Meditations* and which continues to operate in *Beyond Good and Evil*.

The dual rhythm of dissolution and greatness — that the same age produces both the herd-human and the genius — is crucial to Nietzsche's rejection of both optimism and pessimism about modernity. The question is not whether the age is "good" or "bad" but what possibilities it opens for the type of human that can master its chaos.
