# Responsibility

## Overview

*Verantwortlichkeit* (responsibility) enters BGAE not as a universal moral category but as the specific burden of the genuine philosopher. In Part 6, "We Scholars," Nietzsche draws a sharp distinction between the scholar, who responsibly administers a specialized domain of knowledge, and the philosopher, whose responsibility is of an entirely different order: to create values, to legislate meaning, to determine the hierarchy of the sciences themselves. This "more" (*mehr*) that the philosopher bears is not an additional duty layered onto ordinary morality but a transvaluation of the very concept of responsibility.

## Key Passages

- (006-005) — Nietzsche describes the philosopher's relationship to "this 'more' and its responsibility" (*diesem "mehr" und seiner Verantwortlichkeit*). The scholar may be a capable instrument of knowledge, but the philosopher bears the burden of determining what knowledge is *for* — a responsibility that cannot be delegated to method or institution.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The concept of responsibility in BGAE marks a radical departure from both Kantian and utilitarian ethics. For Kant, responsibility is grounded in the universal moral law to which every rational being is equally subject. For Nietzsche, the philosopher's responsibility is precisely *not* universal — it is the specific burden of the exceptional individual whose task is to create the values by which others will live.

This revaluation of responsibility is inseparable from Nietzsche's critique of modern scholarly culture. The scholar's "responsibility" to objectivity, to method, to peer review — all of these are, from Nietzsche's perspective, evasions of the true philosophical responsibility: to take a stand, to value, to rank. The philosopher who hides behind methodological scruple has abdicated the very responsibility that defines the philosophical vocation.
