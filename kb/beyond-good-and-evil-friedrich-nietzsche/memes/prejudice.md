# Prejudice

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, "prejudice" (*Vorurteil*) is not merely a cognitive error to be corrected. Nietzsche elevates prejudice to a central philosophical category: the unexamined moral commitments that structure philosophical systems from their foundations. Part 1 of the book is titled "On the Prejudices of Philosophers" (*Von den Vorurtheilen der Philosophen*), signaling that the critique of prejudice is the starting point of Nietzsche's entire philosophical project.

Nietzsche's innovation is to treat philosophical systems — from Plato's Forms to Kant's categorical imperative — not as discoveries of truth but as expressions of their creators' moral prejudices. The philosopher who believes they are pursuing pure truth is, Nietzsche argues, actually operating within a framework of inherited moral values that have become so naturalized as to be invisible. The task of the "new philosopher" is to expose these prejudices and to philosophize *despite* them — or better, *through* them, using them as tools rather than being used by them.

## Key Passages

- [001-001] Nietzsche's opening question about the will to truth immediately frames truth-seeking itself as a prejudice: "What really drives us to truth? The problem of the value of truth stepped before us."
- [001-003] The "faith in opposite values" — the assumption that truth is opposite to error, good to evil — is identified as the fundamental prejudice of metaphysics. Nietzsche questions whether these opposites are "merely foreground appraisals."
- [001-006] Nietzsche diagnoses the philosopher's "prejudice" in favor of conscious thinking over instinctive activity — a reversal of his own view that "the greater part of conscious thinking must be counted among instinctive activities."
- [001-021] The prejudice of "free will" is exposed as a theological residue: philosophers want to ascribe free will to humans in order to make them "accountable" and thus punishable.
- [001-028] Language as the source of philosophical prejudice: the subject-predicate structure of Indo-European grammar seduces philosophers into believing that every event must have a subject-agent.
- [001-029] The "atomism of the soul" — the prejudice that the self is a unified, indivisible substance — is traced to the grammatical habit of positing an "I" as the subject of every action.
- [002-016] The prejudice that truth is more valuable than appearance — Nietzsche calls this "the worst of all" prejudices, the one that devalues the only world we have.
- [009-007] The nobility's prejudice in favor of itself — a productive rather than limiting prejudice.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The critique of prejudice is the methodological engine of Nietzsche's philosophy. It connects directly to [[Perspectivism]] (every view is conditioned by prior commitments), the genealogy of morals (moral systems are expressions of psychological types, not discoveries of moral facts), and the project of revaluation (if existing values are prejudices, new values can and must be created).

Nietzsche does not claim to be free of prejudices — that would be the prejudice of the Enlightenment. Instead, he claims to *recognize* his prejudices as prejudices and to use them consciously. The [[Free Spirit]] is precisely one who has "the will to self-responsibility" with respect to their own prejudices.
