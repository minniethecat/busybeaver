# Pride

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, pride (*Stolz*) is a cardinal virtue of the noble soul. It is neither vanity nor arrogance in the conventional sense but the fundamental self-affirmation of a being who values himself and his own existence. "The belief in oneself, the pride in oneself, a fundamental hostility and irony toward 'selflessness' belongs just as definitely to noble morality as a slight disdain and caution before sympathetic feelings and the 'warm heart'" ([009-007]). Pride is the affective ground of the master's value-creating activity — it is because he is proud that he can say Yes to himself and legislate values.

Pride, for Nietzsche, is the opposite of Christian humility, which he interprets as a disguised form of self-hatred and ressentiment. "The Christian faith is from the beginning sacrifice: sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-certainty of the spirit; at the same time enslavement and self-mockery, self-mutilation" ([003-003]). To sacrifice pride is to sacrifice the very capacity for self-valuation that distinguishes the higher type. The noble soul does not ask others to validate his worth — he *knows* it, and this knowledge is pride.

## Key Passages

- [009-007] Nietzsche's definition of noble pride: "The belief in oneself, the pride in oneself, a fundamental hostility and irony toward 'selflessness' belongs just as definitely to noble morality as a slight disdain and caution before sympathetic feelings and the 'warm heart.'" Pride is the inner certainty of worth that makes selfless morality not merely unnecessary but contemptible. The powerful are those who know how to honor — and honor presupposes pride, for only one who values himself can genuinely value another.
- [009-025] The pride of the sufferer of knowledge: "the pride of the chosen one of knowledge, of the 'initiated,' of the nearly sacrificed finds all forms of disguise necessary to protect himself from contact with importunate and pitying hands." This is the pride that deep suffering produces — an instinctive separation from those who have not suffered, a spiritual aristocracy of pain that cannot be communicated to the untroubled.
- [002-024] Philosophical pride refuses universality: "It must go against their pride, and also against their taste, if their truth should even be a truth for everyone." The genuine philosopher's pride is offended by the idea that his truth could be accessible to all — truth is not democratic, and the pride of the knower insists on the *distinction* of his knowledge.
- [003-003] Pride as what Christianity must destroy: "The Christian faith is from the beginning sacrifice: sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-certainty of the spirit." Christianity, for Nietzsche, is a systematic attack on pride — it demands that the spirit abase itself, renounce its own judgment, and submit to an external authority. This is why Christianity is a religion for slaves: it makes a virtue of the absence of pride.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Pride is the emotional foundation of Nietzsche's entire aristocratic value-system. The "pathos of distance" — the feeling of distinction between higher and lower types — is a form of pride: the noble soul *feels* its own elevation and this feeling is the source of its value-creating activity. Without pride, there is no self-legislation, no capacity to impose one's own measure on existence.

The opposition between pride and pity (*Mitleid*) is one of the structuring tensions of the book. Pity dissolves the boundaries between souls; pride maintains them. Pity says: "all suffering is the same, all sufferers are equal." Pride says: "my suffering is *mine* — it has made me what I am, and it separates me from those who have not endured it." The attempt to abolish suffering is, at bottom, an attack on pride — for it is through suffering that pride is forged.

Nietzsche distinguishes pride from vanity (*Eitelkeit*). Vanity is the need for external validation — it is the slave's pride, dependent on the gaze of others. "Our vanity is hardest to wound precisely when our pride has been wounded" ([004-008]). Genuine pride is self-sufficient; it does not seek confirmation from the herd. The noble soul "gives as it takes, out of the passionate and irritable instinct of recompense" ([009-018]) — it acts from fullness, not from need.
