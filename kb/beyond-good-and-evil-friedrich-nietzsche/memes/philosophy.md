# Philosophy

## Overview

*Beyond Good and Evil* is not only a work of philosophy — it is a work *about* philosophy. Nietzsche opens the book with "On the Prejudices of Philosophers" (*Von den Vorurtheilen der Philosophen*), signaling that his primary target is not this or that doctrine but the enterprise of philosophy itself as it has been practiced. His meta-philosophical reflections range from the psychology of the philosopher — what unconscious drives animate the "will to truth" — to the historical forms of dogmatism, to the kind of thinking that might succeed traditional philosophy.

For Nietzsche, philosophy has been a disguised form of moral advocacy, a "tyrannical drive" (*Tyrannei-Trieb*) that presents personal convictions as universal truths. The task of the [[Philosophers of the Future|philosophers of the future]] is to philosophize *beyond* the prejudices that have structured Western thought — including the prejudice that truth is the highest value.

## Key Passages

- **[001-001]** — The book's opening: "The will to truth, which will still seduce us to many a venture, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers have hitherto spoken with reverence — what questions has this will to truth not already laid before us!" (*Der Wille zur Wahrheit, der uns noch zu manchem Wagnisse verführen wird, jene berühmte Wahrhaftigkeit, von der alle Philosophen bisher mit Ehrerbietung geredet haben — was für Fragen hat dieser Wille zur Wahrheit uns schon vorgelegt!*). Nietzsche immediately turns philosophy's own weapon — the question — against philosophy itself: what is the *value* of truth?

- **[000-001]** — The suspicion that "all philosophers, insofar as they were dogmatists, understood little of women" (*alle Philosophen, sofern sie Dogmatiker waren, sich schlecht auf Weiber verstanden*) opens a deeper claim: "all dogmatizing in philosophy, however solemn, however final and ultimate its bearing, may have been only a noble childishness and beginner's work" (*eine edle Kinderei und Anfangerei*). Dogmatic philosophy is the childhood of thinking, not its maturity.

- **[001-008]** — Nietzsche's genealogy of philosophy: philosophy began as tragedy. The original philosophers — "all of them" — "already once did philosophy" (*schon einmal Philosophie getrieben haben*), but under the spell of demons and kobolds. The rationalist self-image of philosophy is a late development; underneath, philosophy has always been driven by instincts, fears, and the unconscious.

- **[003-011]** — "What does the whole of modern philosophy actually do? Since Descartes — and indeed more out of defiance against him than on the ground of his precedent — all philosophers have made an attempt on the old concept of the soul" (*Was thut denn im Grunde die ganze neuere Philosophie? Seit Descartes — und zwar mehr aus Trotz gegen ihn, als auf Grund seines Vorgangs — macht man seitens aller Philosophen einen Anschlag auf den alten Seelenbegriff*). Modern philosophy is defined by its relation to the Cartesian legacy — either extending it or reacting against it.

- **[006-017]** — Nietzsche describes "a new concept of the German spirit in which the trait of masculine skepticism decisively predominates" (*ein neuer Begriff vom deutschen Geiste fest, in dem der Zug zur männlichen Skepsis entscheidend hervortritt*). This is not chauvinism but a characterization of a philosophy that faces reality without metaphysical comfort. The German spirit in philosophy is the spirit that takes skepticism seriously — unlike French clarity or English utilitarianism.

- **[005-009]** — Nietzsche's reading of Plato's moral philosophy: "There is something in Plato's morality that does not properly belong to Plato, but is found only in his philosophy, one could say in spite of Plato" (*Es giebt Etwas in der Moral Plato's, das nicht eigentlich zu Plato gehört, sondern sich nur an seiner Philosophie vorfindet, man könnte sagen, trotz Plato*). The philosophy outruns the philosopher; the text contains forces the author did not intend.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's meta-philosophy is not a "philosophy of philosophy" in the detached academic sense — it is an intervention meant to change how philosophy is done. By exposing the psychological and moral roots of seemingly pure theoretical commitments, Nietzsche opens the possibility of a philosophy that *knows itself* — that is aware of its own drives, its own will to power, its own perspectival character.

The key move is the question Nietzsche directs at the will to truth: "what is the value of truth?" This is not skepticism in the traditional sense (which doubts whether truth can be known) but a deeper challenge: even if truth *can* be known, *should* it be the highest value? Might life require untruth, illusion, simplification? This question — the question of the *value* of truth — is Nietzsche's most original contribution to meta-philosophy.

The diagnosis of dogmatism as "noble childishness" (000-001) is not a dismissal but a developmental claim: philosophy must grow up. The maturity of philosophy consists in recognizing that its "truths" have been interpretations, that its "certainties" have been habits, and that its "systems" have been autobiographies of their authors. The philosophers of the future will philosophize *with* this knowledge, not *despite* it.
