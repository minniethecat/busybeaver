## Overview

Thought (*Gedanke*, *Denken*) in *Beyond Good and Evil* is subjected to one of Nietzsche's most radical critiques. Against the Cartesian tradition that treats thinking as the indubitable activity of a sovereign subject, Nietzsche argues that "ein Gedanke kommt, wenn 'er' will, und nicht wenn 'ich' will" (a thought comes when "it" wills, and not when "I" will, 001-020). The "I think" is not an immediate certainty but a grammatical habit — a false inference from the structure of language to the structure of reality.

This critique has far-reaching consequences. If thoughts are not the actions of a unified subject but events that happen *to* a multiplicity of drives, then the entire edifice of rationalist philosophy — its claims to certainty, its faith in logic, its trust in conscious deliberation — rests on a grammatical error. Thought becomes something to be interpreted genealogically rather than taken at face value.

## Key Passages

- **[001-020]** — Nietzsche's central critique of the Cartesian *cogito*: "ein Gedanke kommt, wenn 'er' will, und nicht wenn 'ich' will; so dass es eine Fälschung des Thatbestandes ist, zu sagen: das Subjekt 'ich' ist die Bedingung des Prädikats 'denke'" (a thought comes when "it" wills, and not when "I" will; so that it is a falsification of the facts to say: the subject "I" is the condition of the predicate "think"). The grammatical structure of "I think" misleads us into positing a thinker behind the thought.

- **[003-010]** — Nietzsche reflects on the "traurigen Gedanken" (sad thoughts) that the progress of man from Asia might provoke, contrasting the depth of ancient pessimism with the shallow optimism of modern "educated" Christians. The quality of one's thoughts is inseparable from the depth of one's engagement with the tragic character of existence.

- **[007-017]** — Nietzsche observes that there is "nicht einmal eine wirkliche Historie des früher Gedachten" (not even a real history of earlier thought) — a radical claim that the history of philosophy, as conventionally written, is a fabrication that misses the actual psychological genesis of ideas. Thought has a history that thought itself does not know.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

1. **The Critique of the Subject**: The deconstruction of "I think" is foundational to Nietzsche's broader critique of the subject. If the "I" is an inference rather than a given, then the entire Kantian and Cartesian apparatus of transcendental subjectivity collapses.

2. **Thought as Drive-Relation**: Nietzsche suggests that "Denken ist nur ein Verhalten dieser Triebe zu einander" (thinking is only a relation of these drives to one another, 002-017). Thought is not the activity of a disembodied mind but the expression of an economy of instincts.

3. **The Involuntariness of Thinking**: The claim that thoughts come when "they" will has implications for intellectual responsibility: one cannot simply decide what to think, and the pretense of voluntary cognition is itself a form of self-deception.

## Related Concepts

- **[[Will to Truth]]** — The drive to truth is one of the instincts that shapes thought; Nietzsche asks whether this drive can justify itself.
- **[[Conscience]]** — Intellectual conscience is the demand that one's thoughts be honest, even when — especially when — they are uncomfortable.
- **[[Knowledge]]** — If thought is an involuntary event, the status of knowledge as a possession or achievement becomes questionable.
- **[[Self]]** — The critique of the thinking subject is inseparable from Nietzsche's reconception of the self as a multiplicity.
