## Overview

Nature (*Natur*) is a pivotal concept in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil* — but it is a concept under erasure. Nietzsche does not simply invoke nature as a normative standard; he subjects the very idea of "living according to nature" to devastating critique. The Stoic imperative to follow nature is unmasked as a projection: nature does not prescribe; it is we who read our moral ideals into nature and then claim to derive them from it.

Yet Nietzsche does not abandon the concept of nature. Instead, he redirects it. The "natural history of morality" (*Naturgeschichte der Moral*) — the title of Part Five — proposes a new kind of inquiry: not a philosophical justification of morality, but a genealogical investigation of how moral phenomena actually arose, what they cost, and what they serve. Nature, in this redirected sense, is what the moral idealist refuses to see: the wasteful, indifferent, and magnificent reality that underlies all human pieties.

## Key Passages

- **[001-010]** — A direct critique of the Stoic imperative: "gesetzt, euer Imperativ 'gemäss der Natur leben' bedeute im Grunde soviel als 'gemäss dem Leben leben' — wie könntet ihr's denn nicht?" (supposing your imperative "live according to nature" meant at bottom as much as "live according to life" — how could you not do that?). Nietzsche exposes the Stoic formula as either a tautology (you cannot do otherwise) or a disguised moral projection (you imagine nature as you wish yourselves to be).

- **[001-028]** — "'Ni dieu, ni maître' — so wollt auch ihr's: und darum 'hoch das Naturgesetz'!" ("Neither god nor master" — that is what you want too: and therefore "long live the law of nature!"). The appeal to natural law is unmasked as a disguised form of atheism and anti-authoritarianism. Nature is invoked not because it has been discovered but because it serves as a rhetorical weapon against God and traditional authority.

- **[005-001]** — "Zur Naturgeschichte der Moral" (Toward the Natural History of Morality) — the title of Part Five. This framing is essential: Nietzsche proposes not a critique of morality from a superior moral standpoint but a *natural history* of morality — an investigation of how moral feelings, judgments, and institutions actually developed as natural phenomena.

- **[005-007]** — "hier wie überall zeigt sich 'die Natur', wie sie ist, in ihrer ganzen verschwenderischen und gleichgültigen Verschwendung" (here as everywhere "nature" shows itself as it is, in its whole wasteful and indifferent prodigality). Nature is not a moral order but a spectacle of waste and indifference — "verschwenderisch" (wasteful, prodigal) is the key term. The moral order that humans project onto nature is a falsification of this fundamental reality.

- **[009-036]** — "ein Mann, der von Natur Herr ist" (a man who is by nature master). Here Nietzsche uses "von Natur" in a descriptive rather than prescriptive sense: some people are, as a matter of natural fact, masters — not by right or by choice but by constitution. This usage exemplifies Nietzsche's naturalism: moral and political phenomena are to be understood as expressions of natural types, not as choices made by free subjects.

## Significance

1. **The Critique of Natural Law**: Nietzsche's treatment of nature dismantles one of the oldest moves in moral philosophy — the appeal to nature as a normative foundation. Whether in Stoic, Christian, or Enlightenment form, the imperative to "follow nature" is exposed as a rhetorical strategy that projects human values onto an indifferent cosmos and then claims to discover them there (001-010, 001-028).

2. **Natural History as Method**: The concept of a "natural history of morality" (005-001) represents a methodological innovation. Instead of asking "What is the good?" Nietzsche asks "How did the sense of 'good' arise? What conditions produced it? What does it serve?" This is genealogy before the term became central — a naturalistic inquiry into the origins and functions of moral phenomena.

3. **Nature as Waste and Indifference**: Nietzsche's vision of nature as "wasteful and indifferent" (005-007) is the metaphysical ground of his anti-moralism. If nature does not care about human values, then human values cannot appeal to nature for justification. The cosmos is not ordered toward the good; it is prodigal, excessive, and unconcerned with human purposes.

4. **Natural Types and Hierarchy**: When Nietzsche speaks of someone being "by nature master" (009-036), he is not making a normative claim about who *should* rule. He is making a descriptive claim about the existence of natural hierarchies — differences in capacity, constitution, and type that no amount of moral leveling can erase.

## Related Concepts

- **[[Genealogy]]** — The "natural history of morality" is Nietzsche's earliest formulation of the genealogical method that would become central to his later work.
- **[[Morality]]** — Nietzsche's naturalistic treatment of morality undercuts all attempts to ground morality in transcendent or metaphysical foundations.
