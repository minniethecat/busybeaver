# The Napoleonic Era

The **Napoleonic Era** represents, in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*, a decisive historical watershed — the moment when Europe definitively transitioned from the age of [[Master-Morality|aristocratic command]] to the age of [[Slave-Morality|democratic leveling]]. Nietzsche's references to [[Socrates|Napoleon Bonaparte]] and the era he defined are concentrated in the later parts of the book, particularly Part 8 ("Peoples and Fatherlands"), where Nietzsche develops his diagnosis of modern European political psychology and the fate of the exceptional individual in an age of [[Instinct|herd instinct]].

## Overview

Nietzsche treats Napoleon not primarily as a military commander or a political figure but as a psychological phenomenon — the "last great testimony" to the European herd's need for a commander. The effect that Napoleon's appearance produced, Nietzsche argues, reveals the deep structure of European political psychology: the masses crave authority even as their democratic ideologies deny it. Napoleon thus becomes a diagnostic tool for understanding the contradictions of [[The Reformation|modern European culture]] — a culture that preaches equality while secretly yearning for greatness.

## Key Passages

- [005-019] Nietzsche presents Napoleon as evidence for the European herd-animal's need for an unconditional commander: "the effect that Napoleon's appearance made was the last great testimony" to this need. The history of Napoleon's effect, Nietzsche adds, is "almost the history of the higher happiness that this entire century attained in its worthiest men and moments."
- [007-026] In a passage on women and politics, Nietzsche notes that "it happened to the benefit of woman when Napoleon gave the all-too-eloquent Madame de Staël to understand: *mulier taceat in politicis!*" — let woman be silent in political matters. Nietzsche uses this to illustrate what he sees as Napoleon's instinctual understanding of natural hierarchy.
- [008-010] Nietzsche observes that what made Goethe "look up more joyfully" was not "the Wars of Liberation" nor the French Revolution, but rather Napoleon — the event for whose sake Goethe "finished his *Faust*, indeed the whole problem of 'man.'"
- [008-014] In a sweeping historical characterization, Nietzsche describes the "great interlude, that transition of Europe from Rousseau to Napoleon and to the rise of democracy" — framing Napoleon as the pivot between the sentimental egalitarianism of Rousseau and the democratic movements of the nineteenth century.
- [008-030] Napoleon is listed alongside [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe|Goethe]], Beethoven, Stendhal, Heine, Schopenhauer, and Wagner as an exceptional human being — one of those figures whose greatness requires us to look past their "own self-misunderstandings."

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Napoleon serves three interrelated functions in Nietzsche's thought:

1. **Psychological evidence**: Napoleon's effect on Europe demonstrates that the herd-instinct for obedience persists even in an age of democratic ideology, revealing the superficiality of modern egalitarianism.
2. **Historical watershed**: Napoleon marks the pivot between the aristocratic order of old Europe and the democratic movements that would dominate the nineteenth century. He is simultaneously the last expression of aristocratic command and the unwitting catalyst of democratic leveling.
3. **Exemplar of greatness**: Napoleon belongs to Nietzsche's pantheon of exceptional individuals whose instinctual certainty and capacity for command place them beyond the reach of conventional moral judgment.
