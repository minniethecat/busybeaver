# Instinct

**Instinct** (German: *Instinkt*) is a foundational concept in Nietzsche's naturalistic psychology. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche repeatedly insists that what appears as rational deliberation or moral conviction is in fact directed by underlying instincts — a claim that undermines the traditional philosophical privileging of [[Conscience|conscious reason]] and opens the way for a revaluation of [[Master-Morality|morality]] grounded in the hierarchy of drives. His analysis ranges from the instinctual guidance of [[Truth|philosophical thinking]] to the herd instinct that shapes [[Slave-Morality|modern egalitarian values]].

## Overview

Nietzsche's concept of instinct operates at multiple levels. At the most general level, he argues that "the greater part of conscious thinking must be counted among instinctual activities" [001-004], a claim that reverses the traditional hierarchy of reason over instinct. At the social level, the "herd instinct" (*Heerden-Instinkt*) explains the convergence of moral judgments across Europe. At the individual level, the "instinct for rank" (*Instinkt für den Rang*) distinguishes noble souls from common ones. Together, these analyses form a naturalistic alternative to both rationalist and theological accounts of human judgment.

## Key Passages

- [001-004] Nietzsche declares that after long observation of philosophers, he has concluded that "the greater part of conscious thinking must be counted among instinctual activities" — even in the case of philosophical thinking.
- [001-005] The conscious thinking of a philosopher "is secretly guided and forced into definite channels by his instincts."
- [005-010] In a dense passage on Socrates, Nietzsche frames the ancient conflict between instinct and reason: Socrates, "with the taste of his talent — that of a superior dialectician — initially sided with reason" against instinct, setting the course for two millennia of moral philosophy.
- [007-009] Nietzsche identifies the "historical sense" as a "divinatory instinct" (*divinatorischer Instinkt*) — the capacity to quickly discern the rank-order of value judgments by which a people, society, or individual has lived.
- [007-029] The thinker who proves shallow at the dangerous question of woman — "shallow in instinct!" (*flach im Instinkte!*) — reveals a more fundamental deficiency than mere intellectual error.
- [009-015] Nietzsche identifies "an instinct for rank which, more than anything, is already the sign of a *high* rank" — a pleasure in the nuances of reverence that points to noble descent and habituation.

## Types of Instinct in *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche distinguishes several forms of instinct throughout the text:

| Type of Instinct | German Term | Function |
|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| Herd instinct | *Heerden-Instinkt* | Produces conformity of moral judgment; underlies democratic and egalitarian movements |
| Intellectual instinct | *Instinkt der Erkenntniss* | Drives the scholar's pursuit of knowledge; can be a mask for mediocrity |
| Instinct for rank | *Instinkt für den Rang* | The noble soul's immediate perception of hierarchy; basis of pathos of distance |
| Religious instinct | *religiöser Instinkt* | The deep drive toward religious interpretation of existence; in decline in modern Europe |
| Instinct of cleanliness | *Instinkt der Reinlichkeit* | The highest spiritualization of disgust; drives the saint into isolation |

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The concept of instinct is central to Nietzsche's project of translating "man back into nature" [001-230 of *Beyond Good and Evil*]. By revealing that moral judgments, philosophical positions, and even scholarly objectivity are expressions of underlying instincts, Nietzsche undermines the claim of any value system to transcendent authority. At the same time, his typology of instincts provides the basis for a new hierarchy — one that ranks instincts according to their life-enhancing or life-diminishing character rather than according to moral categories of good and evil.

The "instinct for rank" is particularly significant as the psychological foundation of [[Nobility|noble morality]]. Unlike the herd instinct, which produces uniformity, the instinct for rank produces differentiation — it is the capacity to perceive and honor qualitative distinctions between human beings.
