# Society

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche develops a sustained critique of modern society (*Gesellschaft*) that is inseparable from his aristocratic radicalism.

Against the democratic and egalitarian currents of the nineteenth century, Nietzsche insists on the irreducibly hierarchical character of all genuine culture: "Jede Erhöhung des Typus 'Mensch' war bisher das Werk einer aristokratischen Gesellschaft — und so wird es immer wieder sein" (009-001) —

every elevation of the type "human being" has hitherto been the work of an aristocratic society, and so it will always be.

This is not nostalgia for a lost feudal order but a philosophical claim about the conditions under which human excellence can emerge.

The fundamental principle of Nietzsche's social philosophy is that society does not exist for its own sake.

"Ihr Grundglaube muss eben sein, dass die Gesellschaft nicht um der Gesellschaft willen dasein dürfe, sondern nur als Unterbau und Gerüst, an dem sich eine ausgesuchte Art Wesen zu ihrer höheren Aufgabe … hinaufheben kann" (009-003) — society must exist only as a substructure and scaffolding upon which a select kind of being can raise itself to its higher task.

This inverts the democratic principle that society exists for the benefit of all its members: for Nietzsche, the many exist for the sake of the few, and any society that forgets this becomes an engine of leveling and mediocrity.

Nietzsche's critique of modern democratic society is not primarily political but physiological and psychological.  Democracy, socialism, and the "modern ideas" are symptoms of a broader decline — the "Gesammt-Entartung des Menschen" (total degeneration of the human being) toward the herd animal (005-028).

The modern European "braucht schlechterdings ein Kostüm: er hat die Historie nöthig als die Vorrathskammer der Kostüme" (007-008) — the modern human being needs a costume, and needs history as the wardrobe of costumes.  Cut off from genuine cultural tradition, modern society can only play at being aristocratic, romantic, or classical — nothing fits.
## Key Passages

- (009-001) Nietzsche lays out his aristocratic sociology without apologetics: "die Wahrheit ist hart" — the truth is hard. Every elevation of humanity has been the work of an aristocratic society that believes in "eine lange Leiter der Rangordnung und Werthverschiedenheit von Mensch und Mensch" — a long ladder of rank order and value difference between human beings.

The pathos of distance, the longing for ever-wider distance within the soul itself, is the psychological engine of culture.

- (009-003) The principle that society must serve something beyond itself: society exists "nur als Unterbau und Gerüst" — only as foundation and framework. To make society an end in itself is "Wille zur Verneinung des Lebens, als Auflösungs- und VerfallsPrincip" — will to the negation of life, a principle of dissolution and decay.

This is Nietzsche's most compressed statement of why egalitarian politics are, in his view, anti-life.

- (005-022) The genealogy of morality is traced to the structural conditions of society: once "das Gefüge der Gesellschaft im Ganzen festgestellt und gegen äussere Gefahren gesichert erscheint" — once the structure of society is established and secured against external dangers — the fear of one's neighbor generates new perspectives of moral valuation.

Morality, in this account, is a function of social structure rather than an autonomous realm of value.

- (007-009) The "historical sense" — "die Fähigkeit, die Rangordnung von Werthschätzungen schnell zu errathen, nach welchen ein Volk, eine Gesellschaft, ein Mensch gelebt hat" — is the capacity to quickly divine the rank order of valuations according to which a people, a society, a human being has lived.

This divinatory instinct is the mark of the modern European's semi-barbarism: he understands all societies precisely because he belongs to none.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Society is the field on which the struggle between noble and herd morality plays out at the largest scale. Three dimensions of Nietzsche's analysis are particularly significant:

First, society is the **medium of the will to power at the collective level**. Every social order is a system of domination relations — "die Ausbeutung gehört nicht einer verderbten oder unvollkommnen und primitiven Gesellschaft an: sie gehört in's Wesen des Lebendigen, als organische Grundfunktion" (009-004).

Exploitation is not a defect of imperfect societies but belongs to the essence of the living as an organic basic function. To dream of a society without exploitation is to dream of life without the will to power — a contradiction in terms.

Second, the tension between **aristocratic and democratic societies** is the central political drama of modernity. Nietzsche's critique of democracy is not a defense of any existing aristocracy but a diagnosis of the leveling tendencies that he sees as inherent in democratic culture.

The "freien Geister" of the democratic age are, in truth, "beredte und schreibfingrige Sklaven des demokratischen Geschmacks" (002-025) — eloquent and scribble-fingered slaves of democratic taste, who mistake their liberation from old authorities for genuine freedom.

Third, society provides the **conditions for the emergence of the philosopher**. The genuine philosopher — as opposed to the scholarly laborer — requires a social order that makes distance, solitude, and hierarchy possible. A society that treats all its members as equal cannot produce the kind of human being for whom philosophy is not a profession but a way of life.

This is why Nietzsche's critique of modern society is, at bottom, a critique of the conditions that make genuine philosophy impossible.
