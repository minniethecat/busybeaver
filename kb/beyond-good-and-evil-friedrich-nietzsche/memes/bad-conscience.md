# Bad Conscience

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche develops the concept of bad conscience (*schlechtes Gewissen*) as a central mechanism in the genealogy of morality. Bad conscience is not an innate moral faculty but a historical product: it arises when aggressive instincts that can no longer be discharged outward are turned inward against the self. This internalization of cruelty is, for Nietzsche, the origin of guilt, self-punishment, and the entire "moral" epoch of human history.

Bad conscience plays a structural role in Nietzsche's typology of morals. The commanding type suffers from bad conscience when exercising authority — he must deceive himself into believing he merely obeys higher laws. The herd type, by contrast, has bad conscience bred into him as a permanent condition of self-surveillance. The concept thus illuminates the psychological cost of moral internalization across all human types.

## Key Passages

- [002-012] Nietzsche describes the young soul "martyred by sheer disappointments" turning suspiciously against itself — its pangs of conscience (*Gewissensbisse*) become the medium through which it learns self-overcoming. The soul punishes itself through mistrust of its own feeling, torturing its enthusiasm with doubt, and even feeling good conscience as a danger.
- [005-019] In a crucial passage on commanding and obeying, Nietzsche diagnoses the condition of modern Europe: commanding types "suffer inwardly from a bad conscience (*am schlechten Gewissen*) and must first deceive themselves into believing they merely obey higher laws" — whether ancestors, constitution, law, or God. This he calls "the moral hypocrisy of those who command."
- [009-035] Nietzsche's culminating genealogical insight: humanity — a "multiple, mendacious, artificial, and opaque animal" — *invented* good conscience in order to enjoy its soul as *simple*. The simplification of the soul through the fiction of a unified conscience is itself a symptom of the herd's need for comfort.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Bad conscience is foundational to Nietzsche's critique of morality because it reveals how external social commands become internalized as self-punishment. The mechanism is not divine or transcendental — it is psychological and historical. When the human animal is enclosed within the walls of society and can no longer discharge its aggressive instincts outward, those instincts turn inward, producing the entire inner world of guilt, conscience, and self-torment that moral systems depend upon.

The concept also illuminates Nietzsche's distinction between master morality and slave morality. The master acts without bad conscience — his "good" is defined by his own affirmation of power. The slave, unable to act outward, develops bad conscience as the inner theater in which ressentiment festers and eventually moralizes weakness into virtue. Bad conscience is thus the psychological engine of the slave revolt in morals.
