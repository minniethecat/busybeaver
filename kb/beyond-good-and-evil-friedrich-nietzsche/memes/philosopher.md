# Philosopher

## Overview

The "philosopher" (*Philosoph*) is one of the most examined and critiqued types in *Beyond Good and Evil*. Nietzsche opens the book with a sustained assault on the dogmatic philosophers of the tradition — those who have built "unconditioned philosopher-edifices" on the basis of unexamined moral prejudices. For Nietzsche, the philosopher has historically been less a seeker of truth than a disguised advocate for their own moral convictions, dressing personal valuations in the language of objective necessity.

However, Nietzsche does not merely attack philosophy. He distinguishes between the traditional dogmatic philosopher and a new type — the "free-spirited philosopher" (*freigeisterischer Philosoph*) of the future. This figure, exemplified by Nietzsche himself and prefigured by figures like Stendhal, does not seek timeless truths but rather engages in the dangerous work of creating new values and questioning the most fundamental assumptions of the Western tradition. Philosophy thus becomes not a system-building exercise but a form of intellectual courage and self-overcoming.

## Key Passages

- [000-001] Nietzsche's opening critique: all philosophers, insofar as they have been dogmatists, have built on unexamined foundations — their "truths" were actually prejudices.
- [000-002] The image of "unconditioned philosopher-edifices" that have collapsed under the weight of critical scrutiny, marking the end of dogmatic philosophy.
- [002-016] Against the merely "moral" human being: philosophers should not be confused with moralists; the genuine philosopher makes one "honorable" precisely by refusing moral simplifications.
- [002-021] Stendhal as a model for the "free-spirited philosopher" — a literary figure who embodies the philosophical temperament Nietzsche values.
- [002-024] All philosophers have "loved their truths" too much — the possessive attachment to one's own doctrines is the philosopher's chronic weakness.
- [006-003] Nietzsche's self-identification: playing the philosopher is not a matter of academic posturing but of embodying a certain dangerous independence.
- [006-024] The conflation of the "scientific person" with the philosopher is a modern error — the philosopher is something distinct and more dangerous than the scholar.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's treatment of the philosopher is foundational to his entire project. The critique of philosophical dogmatism — the claim that philosophers have been "prejudiced" rather than impartial — clears the ground for Nietzsche's own perspectivism. If all previous philosophy has been covert moral advocacy, then a genuinely new philosophy must begin by acknowledging its own perspectival character.

The philosopher of the future, as Nietzsche envisions them, is not a system-builder but a "tempter" and experimenter. This figure stands in contrast to both the traditional metaphysician and the modern scientific specialist. The true philosopher legislates values rather than merely describing reality — a conception that links directly to the [[Will to Power]] as the fundamental drive underlying all intellectual activity.
