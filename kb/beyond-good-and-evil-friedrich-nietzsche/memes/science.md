# Science

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche subjects modern science (*Wissenschaft*) to a sustained philosophical critique. Science presents itself as objective, disinterested, and emancipated from the prejudices of religion and philosophy. Nietzsche reverses this self-understanding: science is not the opposite of faith but a refinement of it — "the will to knowledge" rests upon "the will to non-knowledge, to uncertainty, to untruth" ([002-002]). The edifice of science is built on a foundation of unexamined presuppositions.

Nietzsche distinguishes sharply between the scientific worker — the specialized scholar who accumulates facts — and the genuine philosopher, whose task is to create values and legislate meaning. The modern emancipation of science from philosophy is, for Nietzsche, a symptom of democratic leveling: science becomes "the self-glorification and self-exaltation of the scholar" at the expense of genuine philosophical authority ([006-002]). The hypertrophy of science — its "enormous tower and ramparts" ([006-005]) — threatens to bury the philosopher under the weight of specialized learning, making genuine philosophical judgment impossible.

## Key Passages

- [002-002] Nietzsche identifies the foundational paradox of science: "It was only on this now firm and granite foundation of ignorance that science could hitherto rise — the will to knowledge on the basis of a far mightier will, the will to non-knowledge, to uncertainty, to untruth." Science does not begin from radical doubt but from an unacknowledged commitment to simplification and false oppositions.
- [005-002] Nietzsche ridicules the pretension of scientific morality: moral philosophers demanded "something far higher, more pretentious, more solemn" when they approached morality as a science — but "the word 'science of morality' is far too arrogant and contrary to good taste." The very project of a "science" of morals betrays a failure to recognize the *problematic* character of morality itself ([005-003]).
- [006-005] The hypertrophy of the sciences threatens the philosopher's task: "The scope and tower-construction of the sciences has grown monstrously, and with it the probability that the philosopher will grow weary as a learner, or let himself be held fast and 'specialized' somewhere: so that he no longer reaches his proper height." The philosopher must resist absorption into specialized knowledge-production.
- [006-002] Science has successfully thrown off theology, its old "maidservant," only to turn against philosophy with "wanton arrogance and lack of understanding." The independence-declaration of the scientific man is "one of the more subtle aftereffects of the democratic essence and non-essence" — the leveling instinct of the herd applied to the realm of knowledge.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Science occupies an ambiguous position in Nietzsche's thought. He is not anti-scientific in the Romantic sense — indeed, he calls for psychology to be recognized "again as queen of the sciences, for whose service and preparation the other sciences exist" ([001-030]). His naturalism and his call to "translate man back into nature" ([007-023]) are affirmations of the scientific spirit in its proper domain.

But Nietzsche's critique strikes at the heart of science's claim to self-sufficiency. Science cannot answer the question of its own *value* — "the philosopher demands of himself a judgment, a Yes or No, not about the sciences, but about life and the value of life" ([006-006]). By treating knowledge as an end in itself, science perpetuates the ascetic ideal in secularized form. It is the "youngest virtue" — intellectual honesty (*Redlichkeit*) — that may ultimately turn science against itself, revealing the will to truth as a disguised will to death.

The "scientific man" (*der wissenschaftliche Mensch*) is for Nietzsche a distinct type, fundamentally different from the philosopher. The scholar is a specialist, a "mirror" with a "smooth, lustrous surface" ([006-009]), reflecting objects without creating anything new. The genuine philosopher, by contrast, is a commander and legislator who imposes form on chaos.
