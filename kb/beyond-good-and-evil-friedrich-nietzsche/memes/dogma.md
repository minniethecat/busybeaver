# Dogma

## Overview

*Dogma* and *Dogmatismus* (dogmatism) appear throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* not merely as theological terms but as Nietzsche's diagnosis of a fundamental philosophical pathology: the will to make one's truth *universal*. Dogmatism, for Nietzsche, is not a specific doctrine but a *mode of assertion* — the claim that what is true for one type of being must be true for all. The Preface to BGAE opens with this critique, naming Plato's dogmatism as the original sin of Western philosophy and setting the stage for Nietzsche's alternative: a philosophy that acknowledges the perspectival, tentative, and type-conditioned character of all truth claims.

## Key Passages

- (000-003) — The Preface delivers Nietzsche's most concentrated attack on dogmatism: "the worst, most enduring, and most dangerous of all errors hitherto has been a dogmatist's error — namely, Plato's invention of the pure spirit and the good-in-itself" (*der schlimmste, langwierigste und gefährlichste aller Irrthümer bisher ein Dogmatiker-Irrthum gewesen ist, nämlich Plato's Erfindung vom reinen Geiste und vom Guten an sich*). Yet Nietzsche also grants dogmatism its historical grandeur: all great things have first walked the earth as "monstrous and fear-inspiring masks" (*ungeheure und furchteinflössende Fratzen*).

- (002-024) — The philosophers of the future, Nietzsche announces, "will certainly not be dogmatists" (*werden es keine Dogmatiker sein*). It must offend their pride and their taste if their truth should be a truth for everyone — "what was hitherto the secret wish and hidden meaning of all dogmatic endeavors" (*der geheime Wunsch und Hintersinn aller dogmatischen Bestrebungen*). The anti-dogmatic philosopher says: "My judgment is *my* judgment: another does not easily have a right to it."

- (002-025) — The "free spirits" falsely so called — the levelers, the democrats of the spirit — are exposed as unwitting dogmatists of a new kind. They preach equality of rights and compassion for all suffering, making *their* truth a universal demand no less than the Christian dogmatists they think they have surpassed. The form changes; the dogmatic impulse remains.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's anti-dogmatism is the methodological key to his entire philosophical project. The refusal to assert universal truths is not skepticism — it is the recognition that all philosophizing is *perspectival*, conditioned by the drives, history, and type of the philosopher. Dogmatism is the denial of this conditionality; it is the claim to speak from nowhere, for everyone — which is precisely what makes it dishonest.

This critique extends beyond metaphysics to politics and morality. The democratic demand for equal rights, the Christian demand for universal love, the utilitarian demand for the greatest happiness — all are forms of dogmatism insofar as they refuse to acknowledge their own perspectival origins and impose themselves as unconditional demands. Nietzsche's alternative is not a new dogma but a new *discipline*: the cultivation of the capacity to hold one's judgments without needing them to be everyone's.
