# History

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche engages with history (*Geschichte*, *Historie*) on multiple fronts: as a diagnostic tool for reading the human soul, as a symptom of modern culture's semi-barbarism, and as a problem that philosophy must confront rather than simply celebrate.

The nineteenth century's historical consciousness — its unprecedented capacity to understand all past cultures — is for Nietzsche both an achievement and a danger: "Wir Menschen des 'historischen Sinns': wir haben als solche unsre Tugenden, es ist nicht zu bestreiten" (007-011) — we human beings of the "historical sense" have our virtues, it cannot be denied.

But these virtues come at a cost that Nietzsche is uniquely attuned to.

The historical sense is the defining capacity of the modern European: "der historische Sinn (oder die Fähigkeit, die Rangordnung von Werthschätzungen schnell zu errathen, nach welchen ein Volk, eine Gesellschaft, ein Mensch gelebt hat)" (007-009) — the ability to quickly divine the rank order of valuations according to which a people, a society, a human being has lived.

This "divinatory instinct" for the relationships between valuations is unparalleled in any previous age.  Yet Nietzsche diagnoses it as a form of semi-barbarism: the modern European understands all cultures precisely because he belongs fully to none.

"Der europäische Mischmensch … braucht schlechterdings ein Kostüm: er hat die Historie nöthig als die Vorrathskammer der Kostüme" (007-008) — the European half-breed needs a costume, and needs history as the wardrobe of costumes.  The historical sense, for all its apparent sophistication, is a symptom of cultural rootlessness.

The philosophical alternative to mere historicism is genealogy — the tracing of moral concepts back through their contingent histories to reveal their hidden origins.

"Wer der Geschichte einer einzelnen Wissenschaft nachgegangen ist, der findet in ihrer Entwicklung einen Leitfaden zum Verständniss der ältesten und gemeinsten Vorgänge alles 'Wissens und Erkennens'" (005-011) — whoever has traced the history of a single science finds a guiding thread for understanding the oldest and most common processes of all knowing.

History, properly practiced, is not the accumulation of costumes but the exposure of the contingent origins of what presents itself as eternal.
## Key Passages

- (007-009)

Nietzsche defines the "historical sense" with precision: the capacity to quickly divine the rank order of valuations according to which a people, a society, or a human being has lived — "der divinatorische Instinkt für die Beziehungen dieser Werthschätzungen,

für das Verhältniss der Autorität der Werthe zu der Autorität der wirkenden Kräfte." This instinct is the defining possession of the modern European and the source of both his insight and his sickness.

- (007-011) Nietzsche catalogues the virtues of those with the historical sense: "wir sind anspruchslos, selbstlos, bescheiden, tapfer, voller Selbstüberwindung, voller Hingebung, sehr dankbar, sehr geduldig, sehr entgegenkommend" — we are unassuming, selfless, modest, brave, full of self-overcoming, full of devotion, very grateful, very patient, very accommodating.

Yet this litany of virtues conceals a deep ambivalence: the historical sense may stand in "einem nothwendigen Gegensatz zum guten Geschmacke" — a necessary opposition to good taste, at least to the very best taste.

- (005-011) The methodological principle of genealogy: "dort wie hier ist das Vorzeitige und Überkluge, sammt den Einbildungen, sammt dem guten Willen der Herren, zuerst da" — the premature and over-clever, along with their fantasies, along with the good will of the masters, are there first.

The history of a science reveals that what appears as finished truth is actually the contingent residue of a long process of error, imagination, and the will to power.

- (001-010) History as eternal recurrence of philosophical self-deception: "Aber dies ist eine alte ewige Geschichte: was sich damals mit den Stoikern begab, begiebt sich heute noch, sobald nur eine Philosophie anfängt, an sich selbst zu glauben" — this is an old eternal story: what happened with the Stoics happens still today, as soon as a philosophy begins to believe in itself.

Philosophy creates the world in its own image; this is the "tyrannical drive" of the will to power.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

History plays a paradoxical role in Nietzsche's thought — it is simultaneously an indispensable instrument and a dangerous seduction. Three dimensions of this paradox are central:

First, history is the **instrument of genealogy**.

Nietzsche's method of tracing moral concepts back to their origins requires historical consciousness of the highest order. "Die menschliche Seele und ihre Grenzen, der bisher überhaupt erreichte Umfang menschlicher innerer Erfahrungen … die ganze bisherige Geschichte der Seele" (003-002) — the entire previous history of the soul — must be mapped before the soul can be understood.

The genealogist is a hunter in the "Geschichte der menschlichen Seele," tracking the origins of moral values through the dense undergrowth of forgotten centuries.

Second, history is a **symptom of modernity's sickness**.

The hypertrophy of the historical sense — the compulsion to understand every past culture, to try on every costume — is for Nietzsche evidence that modern Europeans have no culture of their own. "Freilich bemerkt er dabei, dass ihm keines recht auf den Leib passt, — er wechselt und wechselt" (007-008) — of course he notices that nothing really fits him, so he changes and changes.

The historical sense, unchecked by the needs of life, becomes a form of paralysis: the ability to understand everything becomes the inability to create anything.

Third, history is subject to the **needs of life**. Following his earlier *Untimely Meditation* on the uses and disadvantages of history for life, Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil* insists that history must serve life, not the reverse.

The "historical sense" of the modern European — for all its brilliance — is ultimately in the service of the "Heerdenthier" (herd animal) that adapts to everything and creates nothing. The noble type uses history selectively, as material for self-creation, rather than being overwhelmed by it.
