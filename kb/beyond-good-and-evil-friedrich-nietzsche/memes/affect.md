# Affect

## Overview

The German word *Affekt* appears throughout BGAE as Nietzsche's term for the primitive forces that underlie both cognition and morality. Rather than treating affects as disturbances to be overcome by reason, Nietzsche inverts the traditional hierarchy: ideas are secondary formations derived from the more fundamental reality of the affects. In the moral domain, he argues that systems of morality are ultimately nothing more than "sign-language of the affects" — a thesis that anticipates his genealogical method throughout the work.

## Key Passages

- (002-018) — Nietzsche argues that what Berkeley and Schopenhauer called a "representation" or "idea" should instead be understood as sharing the same "range of reality" that our affect itself possesses, as a more primitive form of the world of affects. This is a direct attack on the epistemological priority of conscious representation.

- (004-005) — "The same affects in man and woman are yet different in tempo: that is why man and woman never cease to misunderstand one another." This aphoristic observation connects affect theory to the psychology of gender and communication.

- (004-009) — "The will to overcome an affect is ultimately only the will of another, or several other, affects." This is a crucial formulation: there is no affect-free Archimedean point from which to judge or overcome affect — every attempt at self-mastery is itself the work of another affect.

- (005-012) — In discussing the "simplest" sensory processes, Nietzsche lists fear, love, hate, and even the passive affects of laziness as constitutive of sensory experience itself. The Enlightenment model of a purely receptive, affect-free perception is a fiction.

- (005-018) — Nietzsche praises the "playful and wanton surrender to the affects" as taught by Hafis and Goethe — the bold letting-go of the reins. This points toward his affirmative attitude toward the affects, in contrast to the Stoic and Christian project of extirpation.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The theory of affects is foundational to Nietzsche's moral psychology. By showing that moral judgments are expressions of underlying affective configurations rather than rational deductions, he undercuts the claim of any morality to universal validity. Moralities are "sign-language of the affects" — what we call a "moral principle" is a symptom of which affects dominate in a given person, class, or epoch. This insight feeds directly into the master/slave morality distinction and the critique of the philosophical prejudice that the good is grounded in the rational.

Nietzsche's affect theory also prefigures his concept of the will to power: affects are not merely passive states but active forces that strive against one another. The dynamic of domination and submission among affects maps onto the dynamic of wills in the broader ontology he sketches.
