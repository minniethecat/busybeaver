# Exception

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, the Exception (*Ausnahme*) is the counter-principle to the Rule — the individual case that eludes, resists, and ultimately revalues the norms that govern the many. Nietzsche frames the entire history of morality as a struggle between rule and exception: morality as the Rule wants to make everyone the same; the Exception is the human being who cannot be made the same, who grows stronger under conditions that would crush others.

The concept carries both descriptive and normative weight. Descriptively, Nietzsche observes that in every age, certain individuals emerge who do not fit the prevailing moral type. Normatively, he argues that the Exception is the source of all genuine elevation in human life — the creator of new values, the experiment in what humanity might become.

## Key Passages

- [007-005] "Study, you psychologists, the philosophy of the 'Rule' in struggle with the 'Exception' (*die Philosophie der „Regel" im Kampfe mit der „Ausnahme"*): there you have a spectacle good enough for gods and godlike malice!" This passage, from the chapter "Our Virtues," is one of Nietzsche's most compressed statements of his moral methodology. The moral psychologist must study not the average case but the dynamic tension between norms and those who break them.
- [008-009] In the exceptional case (*im Einzel- und Ausnahmefall*), Nietzsche argues, the strong human being will turn out "stronger and richer than he has perhaps ever turned out before — thanks to the lack of prejudice in his schooling, thanks to the tremendous multiplicity of practice, art, and mask." The modern European condition, with its mixing of peoples and values, produces a uniquely favorable environment for the Exception.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The Exception is not merely a sociological category but the engine of Nietzsche's entire philosophy of value. Values do not evolve through collective deliberation; they are created by exceptional individuals who impose new standards. The Rule — democratic norms, herd morality, the "common good" — is always a reaction against the Exception, an attempt to domesticate what is dangerous and singular.

This concept also grounds Nietzsche's critique of modern egalitarianism. A society that treats equality as its highest value is, by definition, hostile to the Exception. The Exception requires hierarchy, distance, and the freedom to be different — precisely what democratic modernity dismantles.
