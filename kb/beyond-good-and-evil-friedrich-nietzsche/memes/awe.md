## Overview

Awe (*Ehrfurcht*) is, for Nietzsche, one of the defining emotions of aristocratic culture. Unlike fear (*Furcht*), which is a reactive and defensive emotion, awe is an *active* reverence — a recognition of genuine superiority that elevates the one who feels it rather than diminishing them. Awe is the capacity to recognize and honor greatness, hierarchy, and the "pathos of distance" that separates the noble from the common.

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, awe functions as a diagnostic sign. The capacity for awe reveals the presence of a noble disposition; the absence of awe — the "shamelessness" of the modern, democratic type who treats everything as equal — reveals decadence. The modern age, Nietzsche suggests, has lost the capacity for awe, and with it the capacity for greatness.

## Key Passages

- **[008-027]** — Nietzsche speaks of "der Ehrfurcht vor der 'kleinen Zahl'" (awe before the "small number") as the condition that makes possible "eine Art Kammermusik der Litteratur" (a kind of chamber music of literature). Awe before the few — the select, the noble, the rare — is the precondition for cultural refinement. Without awe, culture flattens into mass entertainment.

- **[009-007]** — "Die tiefe Ehrfurcht vor dem Alter und vor dem Herkommen — das ganze Recht steht auf dieser doppelten Ehrfurcht" (Deep awe before age and before tradition — the whole of law rests on this double awe). Law, tradition, and social order are founded not on rational contract but on the pre-rational emotion of awe. When awe dissolves, so does the authority of law.

- **[009-015]** — "Es giebt eine Lust an den Nuancen der Ehrfurcht, die auf vornehme Abkunft und Gewohnheiten rathen lässt" (There is a pleasure in the nuances of awe that betrays noble descent and habits). The noble soul takes pleasure not merely in feeling awe but in distinguishing its *degrees* — who deserves what level of reverence, in what context, for what reason.

- **[009-016]** — Nietzsche acknowledges that "die Art, mit der im Ganzen bisher die Ehrfurcht vor der Bibel in Europa aufrecht erhalten wird, ist vielleicht das beste Stück Zucht und Verfeinerung der Sitte, das Europa dem Christenthume verdankt" (the way awe before the Bible has been maintained in Europe is perhaps the best piece of discipline and refinement of custom that Europe owes to Christianity). Even from a critical standpoint, Nietzsche recognizes that the capacity for awe cultivated by religion has positive civilizational effects.

- **[002-012]** — The youthful disposition: "Das Zornige und Ehrfurchtige, das der Jugend eignet" (the wrathful and awe-filled that belongs to youth). Youth is characterized by the capacity for both wrath and awe — strong, unmediated emotional responses that later, in the democratic adult, are smoothed over into indifferent tolerance.

## Significance

1. **Awe and Rank**: Awe is the emotional correlate of Nietzsche's [[Order of Rank|order of rank]]. Without the capacity to feel awe, hierarchy becomes unintelligible — all distinctions collapse into democratic equality. Awe is the psychological organ that perceives rank (009-015).

2. **Awe and the Noble Type**: The noble soul is characterized by a refined sensitivity to the nuances of awe — who and what deserves reverence, and in what degree. This sensitivity is a form of taste, a discriminatory capacity that the democratic type lacks (009-015).

3. **Awe vs. Fear**: Awe (*Ehrfurcht*) must be distinguished from fear (*Furcht*). Fear is reactive, defensive, and seeks its own abolition. Awe is active, elevating, and seeks to *preserve* its object. Fear wants the fearful thing eliminated; awe wants the awesome thing protected (008-027, 005-023).

4. **The Decline of Awe**: Nietzsche's critique of [[Democracy|democracy]] is in part a lament for the decline of awe. Democratic culture treats everything as equal, which means nothing is awesome. The "shamelessness" of the modern type is the inability to feel awe — to recognize that some things, some people, some ideas are genuinely higher (009-016).

## Related Concepts

- **[[Democracy]]** — Democracy is the political expression of the loss of awe; it treats all as equal and thus nothing as awesome.
