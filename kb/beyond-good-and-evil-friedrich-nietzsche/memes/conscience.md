# Conscience

**Conscience** (German: *Gewissen*) occupies a dual role in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*: it is both the internalized voice of [[Herd Morality|herd morality]] that constrains the exceptional individual, and — when transformed into *intellectual* conscience — the highest demand for honesty that Nietzsche places upon the [[Free Spirit|free spirit]]. This tension between conscience-as-constraint and conscience-as-virtue runs through Nietzsche's critique of [[Master-Morality|morality]] and his vision of philosophical integrity.

## Overview

Nietzsche's treatment of conscience in *Beyond Good and Evil* belongs to his broader genealogical project: tracing how moral concepts emerged from pre-moral instincts and social structures. Conscience, for Nietzsche, is not a divine voice or a transcendental faculty but a historical product — one that has undergone multiple transformations across what he calls the "pre-moral," "moral," and "extra-moral" periods of human history. In its most familiar form (bad conscience), it represents the internalization of herd authority; in its rarest form (intellectual conscience), it becomes the philosopher's most demanding master.

## Key Passages

- [001-029] Nietzsche argues that a genuine "physio-psychology" must struggle against unconscious resistances in the heart of the researcher — "it has 'the heart' against it" — because it threatens the good conscience of moral assumptions.
- [002-012] The young soul, "martyred by sheer disappointments," turns suspiciously against itself; its pangs of conscience become the medium through which it learns self-overcoming.
- [002-018] Nietzsche invokes the "conscience of method" (*vom Gewissen der Methode aus*) as justification for his experimental approach to questioning the value of truth itself.
- [005-019] Those who command often suffer inwardly from a bad conscience (*am schlechten Gewissen*) and must first deceive themselves into believing they merely obey higher laws before they can command.
- [006-006] The intellectual conscience (*intellektuelles Gewissen*) of the genuine scholar causes him to hesitate, to resist the seduction of dilettantism, and to maintain rigor even when it delays his progress.
- [007-003] What was once called "good conscience" is revealed as an old-fashioned concept-tail (*Begriffs-Zopf*) inherited from ancestors — yet "we still wear their pigtail."
- [009-035] In a culminating genealogical insight, Nietzsche suggests that humanity — a "multiple, mendacious, artificial, and opaque animal" — *invented* good conscience in order to enjoy its soul as *simple*.

## Genealogical Structure

Nietzsche's analysis traces conscience through three historical stages:

| Period | Form of Conscience | Key Mechanism |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Pre-moral | Conscience as fear of consequences | External authority |
| Moral | Bad conscience as internalized authority | Self-punishment, guilt |
| Extra-moral | Intellectual conscience as self-discipline | Methodological rigor |

This framework parallels Nietzsche's broader narrative in *On the Genealogy of Morals* but is developed here in aphoristic form across all nine parts of *Beyond Good and Evil*.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Conscience is central to Nietzsche's critique of [[Master-Morality|morality]] because it reveals how external commands become internalized as self-punishment. The bad conscience of the herd-individual — who cannot command without first deceiving himself that he merely obeys — exemplifies the psychological cost of moral internalization. Against this, Nietzsche posits the intellectual conscience: a form of self-discipline that demands honesty without the metaphysical baggage of guilt.

This concept also illuminates Nietzsche's relationship to science and scholarship. The intellectual conscience is what distinguishes the genuine philosopher from the mere "philosophical laborer" — it is the capacity to sustain doubt, to resist premature conclusions, and to follow a question wherever it leads, regardless of personal comfort.
