# Becoming

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche's commitment to a metaphysics of becoming (*Werden*) stands as the ontological foundation of his entire philosophical project. Against the dominant tradition of Western philosophy — which from Plato onward privileged being over becoming, the eternal over the temporal — Nietzsche affirms a world of ceaseless flux, process, and self-overcoming. "The philosophers," Nietzsche writes, "have hitherto loved their truths. Certainly, however, they will be no dogmatists" ([002-024]) — the will to truth must itself be recognized as a form of becoming, not a grasp of static reality.

The concept of becoming is not merely a metaphysical thesis for Nietzsche; it carries profound implications for morality, knowledge, and the self. If the world is becoming rather than being, then all claims to eternal truth, fixed moral laws, and unchanging essences are revealed as human projections — "little bad poems" written behind reality. The task Nietzsche sets for the genuine philosopher is to affirm becoming without nostalgia for being, to think and live without the consolation of permanence.

Nietzsche's becoming connects directly to his doctrines of will to power and self-overcoming. Every entity is not a static substance but a dynamic process of overcoming — "always again must be overcome" ([006-008]). Europe itself "wants to become one" ([008-030]); the self is not a fixed soul but a "social structure of drives and affects" in perpetual transformation. The noble soul lives *dangerously* precisely because it embraces becoming rather than clinging to the security of being.

## Key Passages

- [002-024] Nietzsche locates the philosophical prejudice against becoming at the heart of the dogmatic tradition: philosophers "loved their truths" — but truth itself must be understood as something that *becomes*, not something eternally possessed. The dogmatist's error is the refusal to recognize that truth is a process, not a possession: "it must go against their pride, also against their taste, if their truth should be a truth for everyone."
- [008-030] The historical dimension of becoming is visible in Nietzsche's diagnosis of Europe: "Europe wants to become one." This is not a simple political observation — it expresses the deeper logic by which all things strive toward transformation, unification, and self-overcoming. The "deeper and more comprehensive people of this century" recognize this becoming as the fundamental movement of their age, even as the shallow resist it.
- [009-003] The political implications of becoming are drawn out in Nietzsche's critique of social leveling: human beings "must be pressed down and diminished to slaves, to tools." The metaphysics of becoming entails a hierarchy — not all becoming is equal; some forms of becoming (the noble soul's self-overcoming) are higher than others (the herd's adaptation). A society that forgets becoming in favor of static equality denies the fundamental character of life itself.
- [009-016] Nietzsche describes the "becoming-still of all gestures" (*Stillewerden aller Gebärden*) as the bodily expression of reverence — the soul that feels the proximity of the most venerable. Becoming is not only aggressive self-overcoming but also the capacity for stillness, reverence, and the recognition of distance. This is the "pathos of distance" made visible in bodily comportment.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Becoming is the master concept that unifies Nietzsche's critiques of metaphysics, epistemology, and morality. If the world is fundamentally becoming, then:

First, **knowledge** cannot be a matter of correspondence between mind and static reality. The will to truth is itself a form of becoming — one that must be interrogated genealogically. Science, which presents itself as the discovery of timeless laws, is actually a particular mode of the will to power that has forgotten its own contingent origins. The "will to non-knowledge, to uncertainty, to untruth" ([002-002]) that underlies all knowing reveals that knowledge is a creative process, not a passive reception.

Second, **morality** cannot appeal to eternal laws or unchanging human nature. The "natural history of morals" that Nietzsche undertakes in Part 5 is precisely a genealogy that traces how moral values *became*, how they transformed, and how they might be overcome. The very distinction between good and evil is a historical product of becoming, not a reflection of eternal truth. Morality is "the doctrine of the relations of domination under which the phenomenon 'life' arises" — a dynamic process, not a static code.

Third, the **self** is not a substance but a process — a "social structure" of competing drives. The noble soul is distinguished not by what it statically *is* but by its mode of becoming: it overcomes itself, commands itself, shapes its own character. The herd animal, by contrast, merely adapts to its environment — a passive mode of becoming that Nietzsche holds in contempt.
