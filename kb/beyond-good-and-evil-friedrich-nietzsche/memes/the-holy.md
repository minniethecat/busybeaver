# The Holy

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche approaches the holy (*das Heilige*) not as a theological category but as a psychological phenomenon requiring explanation. Why have the most powerful people throughout history bowed before the saint? The holy is a riddle (*Räthsel*) — a puzzle of self-conquest and deliberate renunciation that even the strong have found compelling. Nietzsche's analysis strips holiness of its supernatural aura and reveals it as a human achievement of self-mastery, one whose psychological mechanism deserves genealogical investigation.

## Key Passages

- [003-009] Nietzsche poses the central question: "Hitherto the most powerful people have always bowed reverently before the holy one, as before the riddle of self-conquest and intentional ultimate renunciation" (*Bisher haben sich die mächtigsten Menschen immer noch verehrend vor dem Heiligen gebeugt, als dem Räthsel der Selbstbezwingung und absichtlichen letzten Entbehrung*). The holy fascinates power not because it is otherworldly but because it represents a form of power — power over oneself, the capacity for extreme discipline.
- [009-026] Nietzsche associates "the highest solitude" with being "a holy one" (*höchste Vereinsamung, als einen Heiligen*). Holiness and solitude are intertwined: the holy one stands apart from the herd, and this apartness — this extreme individuation — is itself what the herd reveres and fears. "For that is holiness itself" (*denn eben das ist Heiligkeit*).
- [009-037] In a characteristically irreverent passage, Nietzsche observes that "gods are fond of mockery: it seems they cannot refrain from laughter even at holy actions" (*Götter sind spottlustig: es scheint, sie können selbst bei heiligen Handlungen das Lachen nicht lassen*). This is Nietzsche's parting shot at the pretension of holiness — even the gods, if they existed, would find holy solemnity comic. The holy is not exempt from the gay science's laughter.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The holy occupies an ambiguous position in Nietzsche's thought. On one hand, the saint represents a genuine achievement of self-overcoming — the discipline to subordinate instinct to a higher ideal. On the other hand, this achievement is purchased at the cost of life-denial: the holy one renounces the world, the body, and instinctual life. Nietzsche's critique of the ascetic ideal (developed fully in the *Genealogy of Morals*) is prefigured here: the holy is a form of will to power turned inward, a cruelty directed against the self. Understanding the psychology of holiness is essential to understanding how slave morality achieved its historical victory — through the awe that self-denial inspires.
