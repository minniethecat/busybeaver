## Overview

The term "barbarian" (*Barbar*) plays a complex and ambivalent role in *Beyond Good and Evil*. On one hand, Nietzsche identifies the barbarian caste as the historical origin of the [[Nobility|noble type]]: every aristocracy began as a conquering barbarian horde that imposed its values on a weaker population. On the other hand, "barbarism" describes a persistent feature of creative vitality — the "half-barbarian" condition of modern Europeans gives them access to cultural possibilities that a fully "civilized" (i.e., domesticated) people would lack.

Nietzsche's treatment of the barbarian thus cuts against both progressive narratives that see civilization as a pure good and reactionary narratives that romanticize the primitive. The barbarian is both the origin of rank and hierarchy *and* a perpetual resource that civilization must periodically draw upon to renew itself. The danger is that civilization may succeed too well in eliminating its own barbarian roots.

## Key Passages

- **[003-007]** — Nietzsche identifies Northern Europeans as descended "aus Barbaren-Rassen" (from barbarian races), which explains both their poor aptitude for religion and their capacity for cultural conquest. The barbarian inheritance is a biological and cultural fact that shapes the European disposition toward religion: barbarians are "schlecht für sie begabt" (poorly gifted for religion).

- **[009-001]** — In a passage on the origins of nobility: "Menschen mit einer noch natürlichen Natur, Barbaren in jedem furchtbaren Verstände des Wortes, Raubmenschen, noch im Besitz ungebrochner Willenskraft" (human beings with a still-natural nature, barbarians in every terrible sense of the word, predatory human beings, still in possession of unbroken will-power). The barbarian is defined by unbroken will — a force that civilization seeks to break.

- **[009-002]** — "Die vornehme Kaste war im Anfang immer die Barbaren-Kaste: ihr Übergewicht lag nicht vorerst in der physischen Kraft, sondern in der seelischen" (The noble caste was at the beginning always the barbarian caste: its preponderance lay not primarily in physical strength but in spiritual strength). The barbarian's power is not brute force but *psychic* wholeness — they are "die ganzeren Menschen" (the more whole human beings).

- **[007-012]** — Nietzsche describes modern humans as "wir modernen Menschen, wir Halbbarbaren" (we modern human beings, we half-barbarians). This half-barbarism is not a defect but a *privilege*: it gives access to cultural labyrinths that a fully civilized (or fully barbarian) people could never enter. The half-barbarian has "geheime Zugänge überallhin" (secret access everywhere).

- **[004-012]** — "In der Rache und in der Liebe ist das Weib barbarischer, als der Mann" (In revenge and in love, woman is more barbaric than man). The barbarian element persists not only in culture but in the psychological constitution of the sexes.

- **[006-013]** — Nietzsche refers to the modern scholar's elaborate intellectual costume as concealing a disease — but a disease dressed in "die schönsten Prunk- und Lügenkleider" (the most beautiful pompous and lying garments). The implication is that scholarship has become a form of disguised barbarism, or perhaps a form of civilization that has lost the honesty of the barbarian.

## Significance

1. **Barbarian as Origin of Nobility**: Every aristocratic order begins with barbarian conquest. The noble type does not emerge from civilization but from its opposite — from the "Raubmenschen" (predatory human beings) who impose their will on weaker populations. This genealogical insight is central to Nietzsche's understanding of [[Master-Morality|master-morality]] (009-001, 009-002).

2. **Half-Barbarism as Cultural Resource**: Modern Europeans are "half-barbarians" — neither fully civilized nor fully primitive. This is Nietzsche's description of a creative condition. The half-barbarian has access to cultural possibilities that the fully civilized person — the "letzter Mensch" (last man) — cannot even imagine (007-012, 007-010).

3. **Barbarian Wholeness**: The barbarian's superiority lies not in brute force but in psychic wholeness — "die ganzeren Menschen" (the more whole human beings). Civilization fragments the psyche; the barbarian retains an integrated will. This wholeness is what the noble caste brings to culture (009-002).

4. **Barbarian and the Critique of Modernity**: Nietzsche's use of "barbarian" is part of his broader critique of democratic modernity's self-image. Modernity prides itself on having overcome barbarism, but Nietzsche suggests that this "overcoming" may be a loss — the loss of the very energies that make culture and greatness possible (003-007).
