# Truth

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche subjects the concept of truth to a genealogical critique. Rather than asking "what is truth?" — a question that already presupposes truth's unconditional value — Nietzsche asks: *why* do we value truth? What drives the "will to truth" (*Wille zur Wahrheit*), and what does this will reveal about the human beings who pursue it?

Nietzsche's answer is provocative: the unconditional will to truth is not the pure, disinterested pursuit it claims to be. It is a disguised form of the ascetic ideal — a residue of the Christian-Platonic devaluation of the world of appearances in favor of a "true world." The philosopher who claims to love truth unconditionally is, Nietzsche suggests, driven by a moral prejudice that has become unconscious.

## Key Passages

- [000-001] Nietzsche opens with the famous question about the will to truth: "What in us really wants to go after truth? Why not rather untruth? And uncertainty? Even ignorance?" — framing truth as something that itself needs to be questioned.
- [000-003] The Sphinx-like nature of the question: Nietzsche suggests that the problem of the value of truth has never been posed before, and that answering it may be the "most dangerous" task.
- [001-002] Critique of the metaphysicians' "faith in the antithesis of values" — the assumption that truth is opposite to appearance, good opposite to evil. Nietzsche questions whether these oppositions are not "foreground appraisals, a kind of provisional perspective."
- [001-003] The will to truth is diagnosed as a form of the ascetic ideal: philosophers claim to love truth, but their truth-seeking is driven by a moral impulse that they refuse to examine.
- [001-010] Nietzsche attacks the "will to truth" that seeks certainty: the need for certainty is a sign of weakness, not strength.
- [001-017] Truth is identified with a "mobile army of metaphors, metonyms, and anthropomorphisms" — language does not reflect reality but constructs it.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's critique of truth is not a rejection of truth altogether but a rejection of the *unconditional* valuation of truth. He does not advocate for untruth; rather, he calls for an honest recognition that the pursuit of truth is always motivated, always perspectival, and always entangled with power. The "free spirit" is one who can bear this recognition without succumbing to nihilism.

The critique of truth connects to [[Perspectivism]] (all knowledge is perspectival), [[Nihilism]] (the will to truth ultimately undermines itself), and the revaluation of all values (the standard of truth itself must be revalued). Nietzsche's own experimental style — his use of aphorism, provocation, and mask — is a practical expression of this critique: a mode of philosophizing that refuses the claim to unconditional truth.
