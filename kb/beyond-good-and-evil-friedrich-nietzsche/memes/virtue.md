# Virtue

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche does not simply reject virtue — he revalues it. The seventh part of the book, titled "Our Virtues" (*unsere Tugenden*), opens with a question: "Our virtues? — It is probable that we, too, still have our virtues, although in all fairness they will not be those hearty and four-square virtues on account of which we hold our grandfathers in honor, but also a little at arm's length" ([007-002]). The free spirit has virtues, but they are not the virtues of tradition — they are subtler, more dangerous, and inseparable from the "dangerous curiosity" and "art of disguise" that characterize the modern European spirit.

The fundamental Nietzschean move is to historicize and psychologize virtue. Virtues are not eternal, universally binding norms — they are expressions of particular human types, shaped by specific historical conditions, and answerable to the question: *who* has this virtue, and what does it *serve*? "At one who, for example, was determined and made for commanding, self-denial and modest stepping-back would not be a virtue, but the *squandering* of a virtue" ([007-007]). The value of a virtue depends on the type of human being who possesses it, not on any abstract moral calculus.

## Key Passages

- [007-002] Nietzsche interrogates the very concept of "our" virtues: "We Europeans of the day after tomorrow, we firstlings of the twentieth century — with all our dangerous curiosity, our multiplicity and art of disguise, our mellow and as it were sweetened cruelty in spirit and senses — we will presumably, if we must have virtues, have only those that have learned to get along best with our most secret and heartfelt inclinations." The virtues of the free spirit are not opposed to his deepest drives — they are their refinement.
- [007-015] The one remaining virtue: "Honesty (*Redlichkeit*) — granted that this is *our* virtue, from which we cannot get free, we free spirits — well, let us work on it with all malice and love and not grow weary of 'perfecting' ourselves in *our* virtue, the only one left to us." Intellectual honesty is the virtue that survives the critique of all other virtues — it is the virtue that can turn against itself.
- [007-016] Nietzsche's aphoristic warning about the danger of virtue: "Every virtue tends toward stupidity, every stupidity toward virtue; 'stupid to the point of holiness' they say in Russia — let us take care that we do not, out of honesty, finally become saints and bores!" Virtue, when pursued to its extreme, becomes its opposite — a form of stupidity that petrifies into sanctimony.
- [007-007] The type-relativity of virtue: "At one who, for example, was determined and made for commanding, self-denial and modest stepping-back would not be a virtue, but the *squandering* of a virtue: so it seems to me." What is virtue for the herd (self-effacement, obedience, humility) is vice for the commanding type, whose virtue lies in imposing his own measure.
- [007-012] The tension between virtues: "Perhaps our great virtue of the historical sense stands in a necessary opposition to *good* taste, at least to the very best taste." The historical sense — the capacity to understand and appreciate alien cultures and moralities — may be incompatible with the capacity for decisive judgment. Nietzsche does not resolve this tension but insists on living within it.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's treatment of virtue is the pivot on which his revaluation of all values turns. Traditional moral philosophy treated virtue as a fixed set of qualities — courage, justice, temperance, wisdom — to be cultivated by all rational beings. Nietzsche dissolves this universality. The question is never "Is X a virtue?" but "For *whom* is X a virtue? What type of human being does it express and serve?"

This perspectival approach to virtue is itself an expression of the virtue of honesty. The free spirit cannot rest in any moral certainty — he must continually interrogate his own virtues, recognizing that they too are historical products, potentially masks for baser drives. "And is there anything more beautiful than *searching* for one's own virtues? Does this not almost mean: *believing* in one's own virtue? But this 'believing in one's virtue' — is this not at bottom the same thing that was once called one's 'good conscience'?" ([007-002]). The free spirit does not possess virtues as fixed possessions — he *seeks* them, and this seeking is itself his virtue.

The concept of virtue also illuminates Nietzsche's distinction between master and slave morality. Slave morality takes the virtues of the weak — pity, humility, patience, self-denial — and universalizes them as moral absolutes. Master morality affirms the virtues of the strong — pride, courage, hardness, self-assertion — but does not demand that everyone possess them. The master recognizes that his virtues would be poison to the weak: "What serves the higher type of man as nourishment or refreshment must be almost poison to a very different and lower type" ([002-011]).
