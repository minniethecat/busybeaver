# Immanuel Kant

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) was a German philosopher and a central figure of the Enlightenment. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche repeatedly critiques Kant's moral philosophy, particularly the categorical imperative and the concept of the "thing-in-itself."

## Overview

Kant's critical philosophy represents for Nietzsche the epitome of dogmatic philosophy that disguises moral prejudices as universal truths. Nietzsche targets Kant's ethics as an expression of what he later calls "slave-morality" — a system that claims universality while being rooted in specific cultural and psychological conditions.

## Relationship to Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche treats Kant as both an adversary and an important predecessor. Kant's limitation of reason to make room for faith represents, for Nietzsche, a disguised theological impulse. The categorical imperative is unmasked as a secularized version of Christian morality, and Kant's "thing-in-itself" is rejected as a remnant of metaphysical thinking.
