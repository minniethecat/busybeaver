# Decadence

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, decadence (*décadence*) is Nietzsche's term for the cultural and physiological decline he diagnoses in modern European civilization. Decadence is not merely a moral judgment but a descriptive category: it names the process by which a culture loses its organizing instincts, its capacity for hierarchy, and its ability to produce exceptional human types. The decadent culture values comfort over greatness, equality over distinction, and pity over power.

Nietzsche's analysis of decadence operates on multiple levels — physiological (the weakening of the organism's capacity to integrate drives), cultural (the democratization of values and the mixing of classes), and psychological (the reactive type's triumph over the active). Decadence is the condition against which Nietzsche's entire project of revaluation is directed.

## Key Passages

- [003-023] Nietzsche attacks Christianity as "the most fateful kind of self-presumption" — people not high or hard enough to shape humanity as artists, not noble enough to see "the abysmally different order of rank and rank-cleft between man and man." Such people, with their "equal before God," have governed Europe's destiny until a "diminished, almost ridiculous species, a herd animal, something good-natured, sickly, and mediocre, has been bred — the modern European."
- [005-019] Nietzsche describes the condition of modern Europe in which "the herd animal in Europe presents itself as the only permitted kind of human being and glorifies its qualities — sociability, benevolence, consideration, industriousness, moderation, modesty, indulgence, pity — as the properly human virtues." This self-glorification of the herd is the symptom par excellence of decadence.
- [009-001] Every enhancement of the type "man" has been the work of an aristocratic society — one that believes in "a long ladder of order of rank and difference of value between man and man and requires slavery in some sense or other." The pathos of distance, born of class distinction, is the precondition for the soul's internal self-overcoming. Decadence is the erosion of this pathos.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Decadence is the unifying diagnosis behind Nietzsche's critiques of Christianity, democracy, modern philosophy, and European culture. It is not a single problem but the condition within which all modern problems arise. The "revaluation of all values" is Nietzsche's proposed counter-movement to decadence: where decadence levels, revaluation creates hierarchy; where decadence weakens, revaluation strengthens; where decadence looks backward in resentment, revaluation looks forward in affirmation.

The concept also connects to Nietzsche's physiological thinking. Decadence has a bodily dimension — the organism that cannot organize its drives hierarchically is decadent. This physiological decadence expresses itself culturally in moral systems that devalue strength, in art that celebrates the ugly, and in philosophy that denies the body. The critique of decadence is thus also a call for a higher culture built on the affirmation of life.
