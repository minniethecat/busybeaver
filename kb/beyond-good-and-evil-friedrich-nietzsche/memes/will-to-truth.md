# Will to Truth

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, the will to truth (*Wille zur Wahrheit*) is itself subjected to critique. Rather than accepting the philosopher's claim to love truth unconditionally, Nietzsche asks: *who* is it that wills truth? *What* in us drives us toward truth rather than untruth, uncertainty, or ignorance? And — the most dangerous question — what is the *value* of truth? With these questions, Nietzsche transforms truth from philosophy's unquestioned goal into its most urgent problem.

The will to truth has a history. It emerges from particular psychological and social conditions and, Nietzsche argues, may ultimately be a disguised form of the ascetic ideal — the metaphysical faith that truth is *good*, that it is *better* than untruth, that the world as it *is* (rather than as it appears) is what deserves our allegiance. This moral valuation of truth is an article of faith that Nietzsche's genealogy exposes. At the limit, the will to truth turns against itself, questioning its own value — and this self-reflexive move is, for Nietzsche, the distinctive gesture of genuine philosophy.

## Key Passages

- [001-001] Nietzsche's opening question frames the entire work: "The will to truth that will still seduce us into many a venture, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers have spoken with veneration — what questions this will to truth has already laid before us! What strange, wicked, questionable questions!" The problem of the value of truth is the gateway to *Beyond Good and Evil*.
- [001-002] Nietzsche radicalizes the question: "Granted, we want truth: *why not rather untruth?* And uncertainty? Even ignorance?" The assumption that truth is preferable to untruth is itself a moral prejudice that has never been seriously questioned. "The problem of the value of truth stepped before us."
- [003-017] Nietzsche traces the will to truth to its opposite: the *fear* of truth. Piety — "life in God" — is "the finest and last offspring of the *fear* of truth," the "will to the inversion of truth, to untruth at any price." The religious impulse is driven by the terror that we might become possessed of truth "too early, before the human being has become strong enough, hard enough, artist enough."
- [006-021] In a culminating passage, Nietzsche identifies the philosopher's will to truth with the will to power itself: "Their 'knowing' is *creating*, their creating is a *legislation*, their will to truth is — *Will to Power*." The genuine philosopher does not find truth but *makes* it — and the will to truth is the will to impose one's own values on reality.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The critique of the will to truth is the methodological engine of Nietzsche's entire philosophical project. By refusing to accept truth as an unquestioned value, Nietzsche opens the space for perspectivism — the view that there are no facts, only interpretations — and for the revaluation of all values. If the will to truth is itself a moral valuation, then the fundamental question is not "what is true?" but "what kind of human being does this truth serve?"

The concept also illuminates Nietzsche's relationship to science and scholarship. The "philosophical laborers" — the scholars and scientists — serve the will to truth without questioning it. The genuine philosopher questions the value of truth itself and, in doing so, discovers that "truth" is a creation, not a discovery. This is the liberating, and terrifying, core of Nietzsche's epistemology: the will to truth, pushed to its extreme, abolishes the very idea of truth as something given.
