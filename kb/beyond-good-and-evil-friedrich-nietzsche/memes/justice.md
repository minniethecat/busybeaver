# Justice

## Overview

*Gerechtigkeit* (justice) occupies a complex position in Nietzsche's moral vocabulary. On one hand, he is deeply suspicious of the demand for justice insofar as it expresses *ressentiment* — the cry of the powerless for retribution dressed in the language of moral principle. On the other hand, he recognizes a form of justice that belongs to the noble type: an active, constructive fairness that proceeds from strength rather than weakness, and that seeks equilibrium not out of fear but from a superabundance of power. This distinction between reactive justice and active justice is one of the subtlest threads in BGAE's moral tapestry.

## Key Passages

- (009-013) — Nietzsche describes "the virtues under the name of 'justice'" as characteristic of a particular type. Justice is not a universal moral principle but the expression of a particular configuration of drives — the virtue of a type that can afford to be fair because it is strong enough not to need revenge.

- (007-026) — In a striking passage on the limits of insight, Nietzsche asks whether "the profoundest head or a woman's heart" has ever been granted *Gerechtigkeit*. Justice here is not a procedural matter but a form of recognition — the capacity to see and acknowledge the depth of another being, which is itself a rare and noble achievement.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The treatment of justice exemplifies Nietzsche's method of taking a traditional moral concept and revealing the will-to-power dynamics it conceals. The justice that presents itself as impartial and universal is, on examination, a disguised form of revenge — the demand that the strong be brought low. But rather than abandoning the concept, Nietzsche retrieves a counter-tradition of justice as the virtue of the strong: the capacity to judge fairly, to acknowledge genuine worth, to maintain proportion without the anxious calculus of egalitarianism.

This reconstructed justice is inseparable from the concept of order of rank. True justice does not treat all as equal but accords to each what their rank merits. It is the virtue of the legislator, not the litigant — the one who creates and maintains a hierarchy of values, not the one who demands compensation within an existing system.
