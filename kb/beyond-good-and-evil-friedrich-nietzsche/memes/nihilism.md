# Nihilism

**Nihilism** (German: *Nihilismus*) is a critical diagnostic concept in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*. For Nietzsche, nihilism is not merely a philosophical doctrine but a cultural-psychological condition — the state in which traditional values have lost their authority and nothing seems worth affirming.

## Overview

Nietzsche identifies nihilism as a symptom of a "despairing, death-weary soul" (001-012) — a condition that arises when the will to truth turns against itself, exposing the groundlessness of all inherited values. Rather than celebrating nihilism, Nietzsche diagnoses it as a dangerous transitional stage that must be overcome through the creation of new values.

## Key Passages

- Nihilism diagnosed as a despairing, death-weary condition (001-012)
- The will to truth turning against morality itself (001-012)
- Russian nihilism as a form of active negation (009-208)
- Perspectivism as an alternative to nihilistic despair (001-012)
- The role of the free spirit in overcoming nihilism (002-027)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

In Part 1, Nietzsche describes nihilism as the endpoint of the "will to truth" when it becomes self-destructive: after centuries of using truth to undermine religious and metaphysical claims, the will to truth finally questions the value of truth itself. This produces a crisis: either one embraces nihilistic resignation, or one finds the strength to create new values beyond the old framework of good and evil.
