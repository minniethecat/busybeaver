# Desire

## Overview

*Begierde* (desire, longing, appetite) occupies a pivotal position in Nietzsche's moral psychology. Against the philosophical and religious tradition that treats desire as a disturbance to be subdued — from Plato's charioteer to Schopenhauer's will-denial — Nietzsche insists that desires are the irreducible constituents of life itself. The question is not whether one has desires, but how they are organized: are they given form, sublimated, and integrated into a ruling hierarchy, or are they allowed to run anarchically? The noble soul does not lack strong desires; it possesses the *order* that turns strong desires into effective action.

## Key Passages

- (001-004) — At the very opening of the book, Nietzsche raises the subversive possibility "that to appearance, to the will to deception, to self-interest and desire, a higher and more fundamental value for all life would have to be ascribed" (*dass dem Scheine, dem Willen zur Täuschung, dem Eigennutz und der Begierde ein für alles Leben höherer und grundsätzlicherer Werth zugeschrieben werden müsste*). Desire is placed alongside appearance and self-interest as a candidate for the *highest* value — a deliberate provocation against two millennia of moral philosophy.

- (002-017) — In one of the most radical passages of the book, Nietzsche proposes: "Assuming that nothing else is 'given' as real except our world of desires and passions, that we can descend or ascend to no other 'reality' than the reality of our drives — for thinking is only a relation of these drives to one another — is it not permitted to make the experiment and ask whether this given is not *sufficient* to understand the so-called mechanistic (or 'material') world as well?" (*Gesetzt, dass nichts Anderes als real „gegeben" ist als unsre Welt der Begierden und Leidenschaften*). The entire world, including the material world, may be intelligible as a configuration of desires — this is Nietzsche's will-to-power hypothesis in embryo.

- (005-023) — Nietzsche traces the moralization of desire through the slave revolt: "The moderate, modest, self-ordering, equality-seeking disposition, the *mean of desires* comes to moral names and honors" (*das Mittelmaass der Begierden kommt zu moralischen Namen und Ehren*). What was once weakness — the inability to desire strongly — is renamed as virtue. The moderation of desires is not a moral achievement but a *symptom*: it signals not mastery but exhaustion.

- (004-016) — In a compressed aphorism, Nietzsche captures a psychological truth: "One loves in the end one's desire, and not the desired" (*Man liebt zuletzt seine Begierde, und nicht das Begehrte*). Desire is self-referential; what we truly love is the state of desiring itself, not the object that occasions it. This is a characteristically Nietzschean move: to redirect attention from the object to the activity.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Desire for Nietzsche is not an obstacle to philosophy but its very substance. The philosopher's thinking is not a dispassionate contemplation that rises above desire — it is the expression of a particular configuration of desires, one in which the desire for truth, for mastery, for solitude, and for creation have achieved dominance. The "ascetic ideal" that has governed Western philosophy is itself the expression of a desire — the desire to escape desire, the will to nothingness.

By revaluing desire, Nietzsche removes the ground from under the ascetic condemnation of the body, the passions, and the world of becoming. If desire is not a stain on the soul but its constitutive force, then the task is not to eliminate desire but to *cultivate* it — to give it form, direction, and hierarchy. This is the bridge from Nietzsche's moral psychology to his ethics of self-creation.
