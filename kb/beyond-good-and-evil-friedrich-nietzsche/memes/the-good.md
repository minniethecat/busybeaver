# The Good

## Overview

*Das Gute* (the Good) in Nietzsche's usage is not the name of a positive moral ideal but the target of a sustained critique. Throughout *Beyond Good and Evil*, the notion of a "good-in-itself" (*das Gute an sich*) — a transcendent, universal standard of value — is treated as the founding error of philosophical dogmatism, traceable to Plato and perpetuated by two millennia of Christian-Platonic thought. Nietzsche's project is not to replace one conception of the Good with another but to expose the very idea of an unconditional good as a symptom of the metaphysical need to deny the perspectival character of all valuation.

## Key Passages

- (000-003) — In the Preface, Nietzsche identifies "Plato's invention of the pure spirit and the good-in-itself" (*die Erfindung vom reinen Geiste und vom Guten an sich*) as "the worst, most enduring, and most dangerous of all errors" — a dogmatist's error that inverted truth by denying *das Perspektivische*, the perspectival condition of all life. To speak of spirit and the Good as Plato did is, Nietzsche claims, to stand truth on its head.

- (005-011) — Nietzsche describes how "since Plato, all theologians and philosophers have been on the same track" — moving "toward a goal, toward the Good, toward 'God'" (*ein Ziel zugehen, auf das Gute, auf "Gott"*). In matters of morality, he argues, instinct — or what Christians call "faith," what he calls "the herd" — has triumphed, dressed in the language of reason.

- (005-014) — On the diversity of value standards: "The difference among men shows itself not only in the difference of their tables of goods" (*die Verschiedenheit ihrer Gütertafeln*) but even more in what they count as truly *possessing* a good. This passage anticipates the central Nietzschean thesis that value is type-relative — there is no good that all types would recognize as equally binding.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The critique of "the good-in-itself" is foundational to Nietzsche's revaluation of values. By tracing the belief in an unconditional Good to Plato's metaphysical dualism, he exposes moral universalism as a disguised form of dogmatism — one that denies the perspectival, embodied, and type-conditioned nature of all valuation. The attack on *das Gute an sich* clears the ground for Nietzsche's alternative: value as the expression of a particular configuration of drives, the "tables of goods" (*Gütertafeln*) that each type erects according to its own conditions of life.

This critique also connects to the broader themes of *Beyond Good and Evil*: the title itself signals that Nietzsche aims to think beyond the opposition of good and evil as fixed categories. The rejection of a transcendent Good is the first step toward a philosophy that acknowledges the full spectrum of evaluative perspectives without reducing them to a single hierarchy.
