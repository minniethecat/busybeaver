# Freedom

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche offers a radical reconception of freedom (*Freiheit*) that breaks with both the metaphysical doctrine of free will and the political ideal of liberation.  For Nietzsche, freedom is not a property of the will existing prior to action but an affect — specifically, the affect of superiority felt by the one who commands over the one who obeys.

"Das, was 'Freiheit des Willens' genannt wird, ist wesentlich der Überlegenheits-Affekt in Hinsicht auf Den, der gehorchen muss: 'ich bin frei, er muss gehorchen'" (001-022) — what is called "freedom of the will" is essentially the affect of superiority with respect to the one who must obey:

"I am free, he must obey." This redefinition locates freedom not in the absence of constraint but in the experience of commanding — including commanding oneself.

The metaphysical doctrine of free will, Nietzsche argues, is a conceptual confusion rooted in the experience of willing.  Because every act of willing involves both commanding and obeying within the same person, we experience ourselves as both the source of the command and the resistance to it.

"Das Verlangen nach 'Freiheit des Willens', in jenem metaphysischen Superlativ-Verständе" (001-025) — the desire for "freedom of the will" in that metaphysical superlative sense — is the desire to be the sole and ultimate cause of one's actions without responsibility for their consequences.

It is, at bottom, the desire to have one's cake and eat it too: to be the sovereign cause while escaping the chain of effects.

Genuine freedom, for Nietzsche, is not a given but an achievement — the product of long discipline and self-overcoming.

"Der wunderliche Thatbestand ist aber, dass Alles, was es von Freiheit, Feinheit, Kühnheit, Tanz und meisterlicher Sicherheit auf Erden giebt oder gegeben hat … gerade durch die Tyrannei solcher Willkür-Gesetze gross geworden ist" (005-005) —

the strange fact is that everything that exists or has existed of freedom, subtlety, boldness, dance, and masterly sureness on earth has become great precisely through the tyranny of such arbitrary laws.

Freedom is not the absence of constraint but the product of the right kind of constraint — the discipline that shapes raw impulse into cultivated power.
## Key Passages

- (001-022) The deconstruction of free will: "ich bin frei, 'er' muss gehorchen" — this consciousness lies in every act of willing. Nietzsche's analysis reveals that the feeling of freedom is not evidence of metaphysical liberty but the experience of the commanding self over the obeying self.

The illusion of free will arises from identifying exclusively with the commanding side of this internal relation and forgetting that we are also the one who obeys.

- (005-005) Freedom as the product of long compulsion: "Alles, was es von Freiheit … auf Erden giebt … ist gerade durch die Tyrannei solcher Willkür-Gesetze gross geworden." This is Nietzsche's most compressed statement of his counter-intuitive doctrine: freedom is not the opposite of discipline but its highest fruit.

The "laisser aller" — letting oneself go — that passes for freedom in the modern age is, in truth, the mark of weakness and lack of style.

- (003-003) The Christian sacrifice of freedom: "Der christliche Glaube ist von Anbeginn Opferung: Opferung aller Freiheit, alles Stolzes, aller Selbstgewissheit des Geistes; zugleich Verknechtung und Selbst-Verhöhnung, Selbst-Verstümmelung" — the Christian faith is from the beginning sacrifice: sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-certainty of the spirit;

at the same time enslavement and self-mockery, self-mutilation. For Nietzsche, Christian morality represents the most thoroughgoing attack on genuine freedom in Western history.

- (006-024) The paradox of creative freedom: "gerade dann, wo sie Nichts mehr 'willkürlich' und Alles nothwendig machen, ihr Gefühl von Freiheit, Feinheit, Vollmacht" — precisely when artists make nothing arbitrary and everything necessary, their feeling of freedom, subtlety, and full power reaches its height.

This paradox — that the highest freedom coincides with the most rigorous inner necessity — reveals freedom as an aesthetic achievement, not a metaphysical given.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Freedom in Nietzsche's sense operates at three interconnected levels:

First, freedom is **an affect, not a faculty**. By relocating freedom from the metaphysical "will" to the psychological experience of commanding, Nietzsche undermines the entire free will/determinism debate. The question is not whether the will is "free" in some noumenal sense but what kind of freedom is experienced by what kind of human being.

The noble soul experiences freedom as the pleasure of commanding; the slave experiences freedom as the absence of a master. These are different affects, not different metaphysical positions.

Second, freedom is **an achievement, not a right**. "Gebunden Herz, freier Geist" (004-005) — bound heart, free spirit. The free spirit achieves freedom not by casting off all constraints but by binding the heart (the passions, the instincts) so that the spirit can range freely.

This is the Nietzschean version of self-mastery: not the elimination of the passions but their organization under a single commanding will. The "free spirits" of the democratic age, by contrast, mistake the absence of external authority for genuine freedom — they are, in truth, slaves to the democratic taste they have internalized.

Third, freedom is **dangerous**. "Sie giebt dem Geiste gefahrliche Freiheit aber sie hält die Seele streng" (006-016) — the strong skepticism of the genuine philosopher gives the spirit dangerous freedom but holds the soul strict. Freedom without discipline is chaos; discipline without freedom is slavery.

The philosopher's task is to hold both in tension — to give the spirit the most dangerous freedom while maintaining the strictest discipline of the soul. This is the tightrope that the free spirit walks.
