# Duty

## Overview

*Pflicht* (duty) undergoes a radical transformation in Nietzsche's hands. Against the Kantian conception of duty as a universal moral law binding on all rational beings, Nietzsche insists that duties are type-relative — what is a duty for the philosopher is not a duty for the scholar, and what the noble soul owes to itself has nothing to do with what the herd demands of its members. The highest form of duty is not obedience to an external law but the *self-imposed necessity* that flows from the possession of a rare and demanding nature.

## Key Passages

- (006-005) — Nietzsche describes a class of scholars who lay claim to philosophers' honors "without having a right to this 'more' and its responsibility" (*ohne ein Recht zu diesem "mehr" und seiner Verantwortlichkeit zu haben*). The philosopher's "more" — his greater range of vision, his synthetic task, his capacity to create values — carries with it a corresponding weight of responsibility that the specialist neither bears nor understands.

- (006-013) — In the analysis of the "mixed" modern European, Nietzsche notes the atrophy of the will: "they no longer know independence in resolve, the brave feeling of pleasure in willing" (*das Unabhängige im Entschlüsse, das tapfere Lustgefühl im Wollen*). Duty in the noble sense presupposes a strong will — one capable of commanding itself, not one that requires external legislation to act.

- (009-013) — The virtues under the name of "justice" are discussed in relation to the philosopher's duties: the capacity to judge fairly does not come from a rule but from the strength to see each thing as it is, without resentment or the need to equalize. This is duty as *active capacity*, not passive obedience.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Nietzsche's critique of duty is directed at the idea that morality consists in obeying rules that apply equally to all. For him, the highest duties are those that arise from the *privilege* of a particular nature — the duties of the philosopher, the duties of the noble, the duties of the creator. These are not lighter than the duties of the Kantian moral agent; they are heavier, because they are self-legislated and answerable only to the internal standard of what one is capable of becoming.

This represents a fundamental inversion of the Kantian framework. Where Kant binds duty to universality — act only according to that maxim which you can will to become a universal law — Nietzsche binds duty to *singularity*: act according to what your nature demands, what your rank makes possible, what your task requires. The philosopher's duty is to be what he is, and to carry that "more" with the full weight of its responsibility.
