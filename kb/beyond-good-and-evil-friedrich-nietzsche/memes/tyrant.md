## Overview

The figure of the tyrant (*Tyrann*) appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in two distinct but related contexts. In the Preface, Nietzsche invokes the figure of Dionysius's flatterer — "Tyrannen-Zubehör und Speichellecker" (tyrant-accessory and bootlicker) — as a figure for the philosopher's uncomfortable relationship to truth and power. In Chapter 5, the "tyranny of arbitrary laws" is named as the mechanism by which human nature was disciplined into morality.

These two uses reveal a productive tension in Nietzsche's thought. The tyrant as a political figure represents the coercive imposition of will — something Nietzsche both critiques (when the tyrant's laws are arbitrary) and admires (when the imposition produces genuine culture). The tyrant is thus an ambivalent figure: the same will to power that creates great art and philosophy also produces the "tyranny" of moral systems. The philosopher, in Nietzsche's vision, must be something of a tyrant in the realm of thought — commanding, legislating, imposing form — while avoiding the arbitrary cruelty of the actual political tyrant.

## Key Passages

- **[001-009]** — In the Preface, Nietzsche describes the difficulty of genuine philosophy. The philosopher who seeks truth has always been mistaken for something else: "im Vordergrunde 'Schmeichler des Dionysios', also Tyrannen-Zubehör und Speichellecker" (in the foreground "flatterer of Dionysius," that is, tyrant-accessory and bootlicker). The philosopher appears to the common view as the tyrant's servant. Nietzsche adds: "das sind Alles Schauspieler, daran ist nichts Achtes" (these are all actors, there is nothing genuine about them). The philosopher is neither tyrant nor tyrant's servant — but is *mistaken* for both. The philosopher's relationship to power is more complex than the political categories allow.

- **[005-006]** — Nietzsche describes how morality emerged: human beings first developed moral sentiments "vermöge der 'Tyrannei solcher Willkür-Gesetze'" (by means of the "tyranny of such arbitrary laws"). Morality did not emerge from nature or reason but from the arbitrary imposition of rules by force. Nietzsche then makes the provocative claim that "die Wahrscheinlichkeit dafür ist nicht gering, dass gerade dies 'Natur' und 'natürlich' sei — und nicht jenes laisser aller!" (the probability is not low that precisely *this* is "nature" and "natural" — and not that letting-everyone-go!). The tyranny of arbitrary laws may be more natural than liberal freedom. This inverts the Enlightenment narrative: coercion, not freedom, is the natural human condition.

## Significance

1. **The Philosopher as Anti-Tyrant**: The philosopher is neither tyrant nor flatterer. Nietzsche's ambivalent use of *Tyrann* reflects his broader ambivalence about power: the philosopher must command (a tyrannical function) but also resist the temptations of political power (001-009).

2. **Tyranny as the Origin of Morality**: Nietzsche's genealogical claim that morality began with the "tyranny of arbitrary laws" is a central thesis of his moral philosophy. [[Morality]] is not the fruit of reason or divine revelation but of coercive social discipline. What we call "conscience" is internalized tyranny (005-006).

3. **The Naturalness of Tyranny**: Nietzsche's radical claim that tyranny is "natural" and laissez-faire is not inverts the liberal-progressive worldview. The human animal is not naturally free; it is naturally subjected to arbitrary rules. Freedom is the exception, not the rule (005-006).

4. **Tyranny and Culture**: The same coercive will that produces moral tyranny also produces cultural greatness. [[Culture]], for Nietzsche, requires the imposition of form on chaos — and every imposition of form is a kind of tyranny. The question is not whether to have tyranny but what kind.
