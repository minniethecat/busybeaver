# Feeling

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche accords feeling (*Gefühl*) a philosophical primacy that the rationalist tradition had systematically denied it.

For Nietzsche, feeling is not the irrational opposite of thought but its constitutive ground: every philosophical system, every moral valuation, every act of willing is built upon a "Mehrheit von Gefühlen" (plurality of feelings) that reason retrospectively rationalizes (001-021).

The belief that thought operates independently of feeling is itself a symptom — one that Nietzsche's psychological method exposes as a disguised moral prejudice.

Nietzsche's analysis of willing reveals the complex architecture of feeling that underlies what appears to be a simple mental act.

"In jedem Wollen ist erstens eine Mehrheit von Gefühlen, nämlich das Gefühl des Zustandes, von dem weg, das Gefühl des Zustandes, zu dem hin" (001-021) — every act of will contains a multiplicity of feelings: the feeling away from one state, the feeling toward another, the feeling of the movement itself.

What the tradition calls "will" is actually a "Gesellschaftsbau der Triebe und Affekte" — a social structure of drives and affects (001-016) — and feeling is the medium through which these competing forces register themselves in consciousness.

The moral significance of feeling becomes explicit in Nietzsche's critique of pity (*Mitleid*).  The religion of pity — "die Religion des Mitleidens, im Mitgefühl, soweit nur gefühlt, gelebt, gelitten wird" (005-026) — represents a hypertrophy of a particular feeling that Nietzsche diagnoses as dangerous to life.

To feel *with* the sufferer is not morally neutral; it is an extension of the will to power that can, under certain conditions, become a "Verdüsterung und Verhässlichung Europa's" — a darkening and uglification of Europe (007-008). Nietzsche's point is not that feeling should be eliminated but that feelings must be disciplined, ranked, and commanded — the task of the genuine philosopher.

## Key Passages

- (001-021) Nietzsche decomposes the act of willing into its constituent feelings: "in jedem Wollen ist erstens eine Mehrheit von Gefühlen" — the feeling of the state *from which* one wills, the feeling of the state *toward which* one wills, and the feeling of the movement between them.

This analysis undermines the metaphysical doctrine of "free will" by revealing that willing is not a simple, unitary act but a complex affective process. The illusion of free will arises from ignoring the plurality of feelings that constitute it.

- (002-011) The demand for unconditional truth is diagnosed as a failure of affective taste: "bis der Mensch lernt, etwas Kunst in seine Gefühle zu legen" — until the human being learns to put some art into their feelings.

Nietzsche calls not for the elimination of feeling but for its aesthetic refinement: the free spirit treats feeling as an artistic medium rather than a source of metaphysical certainty.

- (005-026) In the religion of pity, Nietzsche identifies a dangerous affective monomania: "die Ausschweifung eines 'Mitleidens mit Gott'" — an orgy of feeling that extends pity outward to all suffering and upward to God himself. This universalization of a single feeling obliterates the rank order of feelings that a healthy culture requires.

The noble soul does not abolish pity but subordinates it to higher feelings — courage, pride, the pathos of distance.

- (006-024) Artists possess a "feinere Witterung" (finer scent) for the relationship between necessity and freedom: "gerade dann, wo sie Nichts mehr 'willkürlich' und Alles nothwendig machen, ihr Gefühl von Freiheit, Feinheit, Vollmacht" — precisely when they make nothing arbitrary and everything necessary, their feeling of freedom reaches its height.

This paradoxical insight — that the highest feeling of freedom coincides with the most rigorous necessity — reveals feeling as a cultivated achievement, not a raw given.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

Feeling plays three interconnected roles in Nietzsche's philosophical project:

First, feeling is **epistemologically primary**.

Philosophical "truths" are, at bottom, "ihre Vorurtheile" (their prejudices) — affective commitments disguised as rational conclusions. "Jeder Künstler weiss, wie fern vom Gefühl des Sich-gehen-lassens sein 'natürlichster' Zustand ist" (005-006) — the artist knows that the "most natural" state is farthest from a feeling of letting oneself go.

Genuine thinking is a form of affective discipline, not the absence of feeling. The philosopher who believes he reasons from pure intellect is merely unaware of the feelings that drive his reasoning.

Second, feeling is the **medium of moral transformation**. The "Selbstüberwindung der Moral" (self-overcoming of morality) that Nietzsche describes as the secret work of the finest and most honest spirits involves "etwas Kunst in seine Gefühle legen" — putting art into one's feelings (002-014).

Moral progress is not the replacement of feeling by reason but the refinement and re-ranking of feeling: learning to feel differently, to feel from a higher perspective, to feel with greater nuance and discrimination.

Third, feeling is the **index of health and sickness**. "Es giebt wenig so empfindliche Schmerzen, als einmal gesehn, errathen, mitgefühlt zu haben, wie ein ausserordentlicher Mensch aus seiner Bahn gerieth und entartete" (005-027) — there are few more sensitive pains than to have seen a great human being go astray and degenerate.

Nietzsche's psychological method reads feelings as diagnostic signs: what and how a person feels reveals their rank, their health, their proximity to or distance from the noble type.
