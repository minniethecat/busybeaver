# Star

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche employs the image of the star as a cosmological metaphor in two distinct but related senses. First, the star represents the transcendent "above you" — a perspective that the knower must overcome. Second, in a more complex image drawn from astronomy, Nietzsche describes how two suns (stars) can jointly determine the orbit of a single planet, a metaphor for how a moral or philosophical life can be shaped by multiple, sometimes contradictory, value-systems.

The star metaphor belongs to Nietzsche's broader project of dismantling transcendent perspectives while acknowledging the plurality of determining forces in human life. It connects to his critique of the "beyond" and his insistence on immanence.

## Key Passages

- [004-003] "So long as you still feel the stars as an 'above you,' you still lack the gaze of the knower." Here Nietzsche uses the stars as a metaphor for transcendence: to feel something "above" oneself — whether God, moral law, or metaphysical truth — is to remain in a pre-philosophical state. The knower (*der Erkennende*) must overcome this sense of a vertical cosmos.
- [007-003] "As in the realm of stars there are sometimes two suns that determine the orbit of a single planet, as in certain cases suns of different colors shine around a single planet..." Nietzsche deploys an astronomical image to illustrate how a human life can be governed by multiple moral and philosophical forces — a departure from the monotheistic model of a single commanding value-center.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The star metaphor crystallizes Nietzsche's critique of transcendence in a single vivid image. The first passage (004-003) is a compressed statement of his entire anti-metaphysical project: philosophy begins when one stops feeling anything "above" oneself. The second passage (007-003) anticipates his later perspectivism — the idea that no single value-system monopolizes truth, and that a richer human type might be shaped by multiple, even conflicting, moral forces.
