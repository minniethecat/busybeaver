# Work

## Overview

In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche draws a sharp and consequential distinction between philosophical work (*Arbeit*) and genuine philosophy. The "philosophical workers" (*philosophischen Arbeiter*) — among whom he numbers Kant and Hegel — perform the indispensable labor of inventorying, systematizing, and making manageable the vast store of past value-judgments. But this work, however noble in its model, is not itself philosophy: the worker serves, the philosopher commands. The philosopher must have stood upon all the stages on which his servants remain standing, but he must also go beyond them. Work is thus both the soil in which the philosopher grows and the temptation to mistake scholarly diligence for creative thought — a temptation that Nietzsche sees as characteristic of the modern age, with its "noisy, time-buying, self-proud, stupid-proud industriousness" (*lärmende, Zeit-auskaufende, stolze, dumm-stolze Arbeitsamkeit*) that educates toward unbelief.

## Key Passages

- [006-020] Nietzsche insists: "I maintain that one should finally stop confusing the philosophical workers and scientific men in general with the philosophers — that precisely here one should strictly render 'to each his own' and not give too much to the former nor too little to the latter" (*Ich bestehe darauf, dass man endlich aufhöre, die philosophischen Arbeiter und überhaupt die wissenschaftlichen Menschen mit den Philosophen zu verwechseln*). For the education of the genuine philosopher it may be necessary that he himself once stood upon all the stages on which his servants, the scientific workers of philosophy, remain standing — *remain standing must*. The philosopher must perhaps himself have been critic, skeptic, dogmatist, historian, and beyond that poet, collector, traveler, riddle-solver, moralist, seer, and "free spirit" — almost everything — in order to traverse the circumference of human values and to *create* values. The workers inventory; the philosopher legislates.

- [006-021] The genuine philosophers, Nietzsche continues, are "commanders and lawgivers" (*Befehlshaber und Gesetzgeber*): they reach with creative hand toward the future, and everything that is and was becomes for them a means, a tool, a hammer. Their "knowing" is *creating*, their creating is a legislating, their will to truth is — *will to power*. The work of the philosophical laborers is the preparatory condition that makes this commanding possible, but the two functions must not be confused: the one grinds the lens, the other looks through it.

- [009-020] In a passage on the formation of peoples, Nietzsche describes how "when human beings have lived together for a long time under similar conditions (of climate, soil, danger, needs, work)" (*wenn Menschen lange unter ähnlichen Bedingungen (des Klima's, des Bodens, der Gefahr, der Bedürfnisse, der Arbeit) zusammen 'gelebt haben*), something emerges that "understands itself" — a people. In all souls a similar number of frequently recurring experiences gains the upper hand over rarer ones: upon these shared experiences, mutual understanding becomes faster and faster. Work is one of the fundamental conditions that shapes the shared experiential base of a people.

- [003-014] Nietzsche contrasts the modern industriousness with religious contemplation: "the modern, noisy, time-buying, self-proud, stupid-proud industriousness [*Arbeitsamkeit*], more than anything else, educates and prepares precisely toward 'unbelief'" (*die moderne, lärmende, Zeit-auskaufende, stolze, dumm-stolze Arbeitsamkeit, mehr als alles Übrige, gerade zum „Unglauben" erzieht und vorbereitet*). The constant busyness of modern life leaves no space for the "favorite work of self-examination" (*Lieblings-Arbeit der Selbstprüfung*) that characterized the religious temperament. The very tempo of modern work is hostile to the conditions under which philosophical depth becomes possible.

## Significance in Nietzsche's Philosophy

The concept of work in Nietzsche serves a double function: it provides the causal account of how shared conditions produce shared understanding (the formation of peoples, of language, of cultural "common sense"), and it establishes the hierarchical distinction between preparatory intellectual labor and genuine philosophical creation. The first function is sociological — work, alongside climate, soil, and danger, is one of the material conditions that shape human communities. The second is typological — the distinction between the philosophical worker and the philosopher proper is central to Nietzsche's polemic against the modern confusion of scholarship with philosophy. The scholar's diligence, patience, and methodological rigor are genuine virtues, but they are *servant* virtues; the philosopher's task begins where the worker's ends. This distinction is not a denigration of work but a defense of the difference in kind between inventorying past values and creating new ones.
