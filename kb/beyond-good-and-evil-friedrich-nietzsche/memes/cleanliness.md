## Overview

Cleanliness (*Reinlichkeit*) is one of Nietzsche's most striking psychological concepts in *Beyond Good and Evil*. It describes an instinct for intellectual and spiritual purity that drives the individual away from contamination — away from religious contact, away from common opinions, away from anything that would soil the integrity of the soul. At its highest development, this instinct becomes holiness itself.

Nietzsche uses *Reinlichkeit* to describe a specifically modern psychological type: the person whose intellectual integrity is so refined that it becomes a form of isolation. The instinct of cleanliness does not make one morally superior in the conventional sense — it makes one *separate*. The clean person cannot tolerate proximity to what is unclean, and this intolerance is both a strength and a danger: it can lead to the "most wonderful and dangerous isolation" (009-026). The concept thus connects Nietzsche's psychological observations to his broader themes of [[Solitude|solitude]], [[Nobility|nobility]], and the conditions for genuine [[Philosophy|philosophy]].

## Key Passages

- **[003-016]** — Nietzsche describes how the modern person's "Widerwillen gegen die eignen religiösen Empfindungen und deren anspruchsvolle Gültigkeit" (aversion to their own religious feelings and their presumptuous validity) tends "sich zur Behutsamkeit und Reinlichkeit zu sublimiren" (to sublimate itself into caution and cleanliness). This sublimated cleanliness makes contact with religious people and things something to be *avoided*. The depth of a person's tolerance — their humanity — may itself drive them to this avoidance, to spare themselves the distress that tolerating religion brings. This is cleanliness not as puritanism but as self-protection.

- **[009-026]** — Nietzsche makes his most striking claim about cleanliness: "Instinkt der Reinlichkeit stellt den mit ihm Behafteten in die wunderlichste und gefährlichste Vereinsamung, als einen Heiligen: denn eben das ist Heiligkeit — die höchste Vergeistigung des genannten Instinktes" (The instinct of cleanliness places the one afflicted with it into the most wonderful and dangerous isolation, like a saint: for that is precisely holiness — the highest spiritualization of the named instinct). Holiness is not a supernatural gift but the extreme development of an instinct for purity. The saint is the person whose cleanliness has become absolute — and whose solitude is therefore absolute as well.

## Significance

1. **Cleanliness as Sublimation**: Nietzsche's account of cleanliness is a model of his psychological method. What begins as a religious impulse (aversion to one's own religious feelings) is transformed — sublimated — into an intellectual instinct. Cleanliness is religion transformed into philosophy (003-016).

2. **Cleanliness and Holiness**: The radical claim that holiness is "the highest spiritualization" of the instinct of cleanliness reinterprets sanctity in purely psychological terms. The saint is not touched by God; the saint is the person in whom the instinct for purity has become total. This is Nietzsche's naturalistic reduction of the sacred (009-026).

3. **Cleanliness and Solitude**: The instinct of cleanliness is profoundly isolating. The cleaner one becomes, the less one can tolerate proximity to the unclean — and the unclean includes most of human society. This connects cleanliness to Nietzsche's praise of [[Solitude|solitude]] as a condition for philosophical greatness (009-026).

4. **Cleanliness and the Free Spirit**: The free spirit's independence is partly a function of this instinct. To think freely requires the capacity to recoil from common opinions as one recoils from dirt. Cleanliness is the instinct that makes free thinking possible.

## Related Concepts

- **[[Solitude]]** — Cleanliness leads inexorably to solitude; the cleaner one becomes, the more isolated one must be.
- **[[Instinct]]** — Cleanliness is an *Instinkt* — a deep, pre-rational drive that shapes the whole personality.
- **[[Religion]]** — Cleanliness is the sublimation of the religious impulse into intellectual form.
- **[[Nobility]]** — The instinct of cleanliness is a mark of the noble type, who cannot tolerate proximity to the base.
- **[[Skepticism]]** — The skeptic's doubt can be understood as a form of intellectual cleanliness — a refusal to be contaminated by unexamined convictions.
