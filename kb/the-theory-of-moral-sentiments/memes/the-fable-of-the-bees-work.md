# The Fable of the Bees

Mandeville's provocative argument that civilization, commerce, and morality itself are built on selfish passions rather than genuine virtue — the work that Smith devotes an extended section of Part VII to refuting.

## Overview

*The Fable of the Bees* began as a short poem, "The Grumbling Hive" (1705), and expanded through multiple editions into a comprehensive philosophical work. Mandeville's central claim: what we call "virtue" is self-interest in disguise, and the "vices" of greed, luxury, and vanity are the true engines of economic prosperity and social order.

## Author

[[bernard-mandeville]] — Dutch-English physician and philosopher (1670–1733).

## Relevance to TMS

Smith treats Mandeville's system as the most serious challenge to his own. If Mandeville is right — if all apparently virtuous conduct is really disguised self-interest — then Smith's entire moral psychology collapses. Smith's response is twofold: first, sympathy is a genuine other-regarding sentiment, not disguised self-interest; second, the felt quality of moral experience cannot be explained by Mandeville's reduction.

> "The system of Dr. Mandeville... represents whatever is done from a sense of propriety, from a regard to what is commendable and praise-worthy, as being done merely from a regard to what is the object of vanity."(038-025)
