# Jonathan Swift

The satirist whose vision of human nature — as expressed in the Yahoos of *Gulliver's Travels* — represents the cynical extreme that Smith's moral psychology is designed to refute: humans are not merely selfish brutes but beings capable of genuine sympathy and moral judgment.

## Biographical Sketch

Jonathan Swift (1667–1745) was the greatest English-language satirist. Born in Dublin to English parents, he became Dean of St. Patrick's Cathedral while writing works that savaged the political, religious, and intellectual follies of his age. *Gulliver's Travels* (1726) — particularly the final voyage, in which humans appear as brutish Yahoos ruled by rational horses — is one of the most devastating satires of human nature ever written.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### The Anatomy of Human Folly

Swift's satires do not articulate a philosophical "doctrine" in the sense that Hutcheson or Hume do, but they embody a philosophical position: human nature is fundamentally corrupt, and the effort to idealize it is self-deception. The Yahoos represent what humans would be without the thin veneer of civilization — and Swift seems to suggest the veneer is very thin indeed.

### The Uses of Satire

Swift's method — using exaggeration to expose hidden truths — is itself a philosophical position. Satire implies that human beings systematically deceive themselves about their own motives and that only the shock of grotesque exaggeration can break through this self-deception.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith does not engage with Swift directly, but Swift represents the misanthropic pole that Smith's entire moral psychology is designed to avoid. Smith's opening sentence — "How selfish soever man may be supposed" — is a response not just to Hobbes and Mandeville but to the Swiftian vision of humanity.

Smith's answer to Swift: yes, humans are capable of selfishness, folly, and cruelty. But they are also capable of sympathy — of genuinely entering into the feelings of others. The two capacities coexist, and moral psychology must account for both. Swift sees only the Yahoos; Smith sees the impartial spectator as well.
