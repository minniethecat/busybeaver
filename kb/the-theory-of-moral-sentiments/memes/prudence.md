# Prudence

The virtue of careful, far-sighted pursuit of one's own welfare — which Smith treats with characteristic nuance: respectable and useful, but falling short of the demanding standard that self-command and benevolence set for full moral virtue.

## Smith's Account

Prudence occupies an intermediate position in Smith's hierarchy of virtues. It is higher than mere self-interest (which may be short-sighted) but lower than full moral virtue (which requires rising above self-interest when justice or benevolence demands it).

### Core Definition

Prudence is the union of "the best head" with "the best heart" — combining sound judgment with self-restraint in the service of long-term welfare. The prudent person is not the selfish person but the person who pursues their interests wisely:

> "The care of the health, of the fortune, of the rank and reputation of the individual, the objects upon which his comfort and happiness in this life are supposed principally to depend, is considered as the proper business of that virtue which is commonly called Prudence."(010-003)

### Key Features

**An "inferior" virtue.** Smith explicitly ranks prudence below the higher virtues of self-command and benevolence. The prudent person may be respectable, but they are not admirable in the way the person of self-command is. Prudence "commands a certain cold esteem, but seems not entitled to any very ardent love or admiration."

**Individual rather than social.** Unlike benevolence (which looks to others' welfare) or justice (which protects others' rights), prudence is primarily self-regarding. This is not a defect — Smith thinks prudence is genuinely valuable — but it limits its moral reach.

**The foundation of worldly success.** Smith acknowledges that prudence, combined with "probity and industry," is the reliable path to worldly success for those not born to wealth and status(017-005).

## Role in the Moral System

Prudence is the virtue that regulates self-love — transforming blind selfishness into wise self-regard. It is necessary for a well-lived life but insufficient for moral excellence. Smith's ranking of prudence below self-command and benevolence reflects his view that the highest virtues involve transcending self-interest, not merely managing it wisely.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Self-Love**: The raw material that prudence shapes into wise self-regard.
- **Self-Command**: The higher virtue that prudence serves but cannot replace.
- **Benevolence**: The social virtue that prudence, being self-regarding, must be balanced against.

## Historical Context

Smith's treatment of prudence engages with the Aristotelian tradition of *phronesis* (practical wisdom) and the Epicurean elevation of prudence as the supreme virtue. Smith rejects the Epicurean view: prudence is good but not supreme. The highest virtue requires something more — the capacity to override self-interest for the sake of justice or benevolence.

## Key Passages

> "The care of the health, of the fortune, of the rank and reputation of the individual... is considered as the proper business of that virtue which is commonly called Prudence."(010-003)

> "Probity and prudence, generosity and frankness, must characterize his behaviour upon all ordinary occasions."(017-005)
