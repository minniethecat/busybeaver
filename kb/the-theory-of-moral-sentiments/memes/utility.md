# Utility

The quality of serving some purpose or producing beneficial effects — which Smith treats as a genuine but secondary source of moral approbation, one that reinforces our natural moral judgments without being their foundation.

## Smith's Account

Smith devotes careful attention to utility because it was central to David Hume's moral theory. Hume had argued that our sense of virtue ultimately reduces to the perception of utility. Smith disagrees: utility can enhance our approbation, but it cannot be its original source.

### Core Definition

Smith argues that utility is a real but secondary source of approval. We do sometimes approve of actions and institutions because they are useful — but this is an effect of our approbation, not its cause:

> "The utility of those qualities, it may be thought, is what first recommends them to our approbation."(006-003)

Smith allows that this is "no doubt" partly true — but insists it is not the whole truth. Our primary moral judgments arise from sympathy, not utility.

### Key Features

**Secondary, not primary.** Utility can explain why we continue to approve of something, but it cannot explain why we first approved of it. Original moral judgment arises from the immediate correspondence or dissonance of sentiments.

**The beauty of adaptation.** Smith offers a remarkable psychological observation: we take pleasure not just in useful outcomes but in the mere "aptness" of means to ends. A well-designed watch pleases us even if we never use it. Similarly, we may approve of social arrangements because of their elegant adaptation to human needs — independent of whether they actually serve those needs.

**Does not explain resentment.** Smith's most pointed argument against the utility theory: utility cannot explain our sense of justice. We do not punish criminals because punishment is useful; we punish because we resent the injury. "When a single man is injured or destroyed, we demand his defence."

## Role in the Moral System

Smith's treatment of utility is largely negative — he uses it to show what moral judgment is not. By demonstrating that utility cannot be the foundation of morality, he clears the ground for his own account based on sympathy and the impartial spectator.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Approbation**: Utility can reinforce approbation but not create it.
- **Sympathy**: The true foundation of moral judgment, compared to which utility is secondary.
- **Propriety**: The standard of judgment that utility sometimes obscures.

## Historical Context

Smith's critique of utility-based moral theories is directed primarily at Hume, his close friend and philosophical mentor. Smith agrees with Hume that sympathy is the mechanism of moral judgment but disagrees that the judgment itself reduces to the perception of utility. This is one of the most significant philosophical disagreements between the two thinkers.

## Key Passages

> "The utility of those qualities, it may be thought, is what first recommends them to our approbation."(006-003)

> "When a single man is injured or destroyed, we demand his defence, or we demand his punishment, not so much from a concern for the general interest of society, as from a concern for the individual who has been injured."

Smith applies this directly to the sentiment of approbation: "But though the utility of those passions to the individual" concerned is often what first recommends them to us, it is not the sole foundation — the immediate sympathy with the passion is primary(012-004).
