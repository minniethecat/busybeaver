# Aristotle

The philosopher whose virtue ethics — particularly the doctrine that virtue is a mean between extremes — profoundly influenced Smith's account of propriety, though Smith transformed Aristotle's rationalist framework into a sentimentalist one grounded in sympathy.

## Biographical Sketch

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) studied under Plato at the Academy and later founded his own school, the Lyceum. His ethical works — the *Nicomachean Ethics* and *Eudemian Ethics* — develop the theory that virtue is a settled disposition (*hexis*) to choose the mean between excess and deficiency, as determined by practical wisdom (*phronesis*).

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine of the Mean

Aristotle's most famous ethical doctrine: every virtue is a mean between two vices — one of excess, one of deficiency. Courage is the mean between cowardice and rashness; generosity between miserliness and prodigality. The mean is not a mathematical average but what is "relative to us" — what a person of practical wisdom would determine is appropriate in the circumstances.

### Doctrine on Practical Wisdom

*Phronesis* (practical wisdom) is the intellectual virtue that enables a person to determine the mean in particular situations. It is not theoretical knowledge but a capacity for judgment cultivated through experience.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith's concept of propriety — the fit between passion and its cause — is deeply Aristotelian in structure. When Smith says that we judge a passion by whether it is "proportioned or disproportioned to the cause which excites it"(005-010), he is essentially restating Aristotle's doctrine of the mean in the vocabulary of sympathy.

### Points of Agreement

The central shared insight: moral judgment is about proportion and context, not the application of fixed rules. What is proper anger in one situation is excessive in another. Both Smith and Aristotle reject the idea that morality can be codified into exceptionless principles.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith replaces Aristotle's *phronesis* (an intellectual virtue) with sympathy (a felt response). The standard of propriety is not what a person of practical wisdom would determine, but what the impartial spectator can sympathize with. This shifts the basis of moral judgment from reason to sentiment while preserving the Aristotelian emphasis on context and proportion.

## Key Passages in TMS

> "In the suitableness or unsuitableness, in the proportion or disproportion which the affection seems to bear to the cause or object which excites it, consists the propriety or impropriety."(005-007)
