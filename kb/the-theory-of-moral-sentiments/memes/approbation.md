# Approbation

The sentiment of moral approval that arises when we perceive the correspondence between a person's passion or action and what we ourselves would feel in their situation — the felt verdict of the sympathetic mechanism.

## Smith's Account

Approbation is not a separate moral faculty for Smith, but the natural feeling that accompanies successful sympathy. When the passions of another correspond to what we imagine we would feel in their situation, approval arises spontaneously.

### Core Definition

Smith argues that approbation is identical with the perception of sympathetic correspondence:

> "To approve of the passions of another, therefore, as suitable to their objects, is the same thing as to observe that we entirely sympathize with them; and not to approve of them as such, is the same thing as to observe that we do not entirely sympathize with them."(005-002)

This is a radical simplification of moral psychology. Approbation is not a mysterious moral sense, a deduction from rational principles, or a calculation of utility — it is simply the felt recognition that our feelings align with another's.

### Key Features

**Rooted in sympathy, not reason.** Smith rejects rationalist accounts that make approbation a judgment of truth. We approve of sentiments the way we see colors — immediately and without reasoning:

> "To approve or disapprove, therefore, of the opinions of others is acknowledged, by everybody, to mean no more than to observe their agreement or disagreement with our own. But this is equally the case with regard to our approbation or disapprobation of the sentiments or passions of others."(005-003)

**Conditional approbation.** We can approve even when we do not actually feel the corresponding passion. Smith gives the example of approving a stranger's grief at his father's death — we do not feel the grief ourselves, but we know from experience that such a cause naturally produces such a feeling, and on "the consciousness of this conditional sympathy" our approbation is founded(005-005).

**Heightened by wonder.** Ordinary approbation becomes admiration when joined with surprise at the unusual degree of a virtue. "Approbation heightened by wonder and surprise, constitutes the sentiment which is properly called admiration"(006-002).

**Self-approbation.** The most important form of approbation is self-approbation — the approval of one's own conduct by the impartial spectator within. This is the reward of virtue, Smith argues, and "the love of it is the love of virtue"(016-015).

## Role in the Moral System

Approbation is the output of the entire moral judgment system. Sympathy supplies the mechanism, propriety defines the standard, and approbation is the verdict. Without approbation, we could know what is proper without caring about it — moral knowledge would lack motive force.

Smith's account of approbation is also the foundation of his critique of alternative moral theories in Part VII. Systems that make virtue consist in prudence, benevolence, or reason all fail, in Smith's view, to adequately explain what approbation actually feels like — the warm, immediate sense of approval that accompanies successful sympathy.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Sympathy**: The mechanism — approbation arises when sympathy succeeds.
- **Propriety**: The standard — we approve of what is proper.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The ideal judge whose approbation we most value.
- **Self-Approbation**: The internalized form — being able to approve of oneself.

## Historical Context

Smith's account of approbation is a direct response to the debate between moral sense theorists (Shaftesbury, Hutcheson) and rationalists (Clarke, Wollaston). Smith agrees with the moral sense school that approbation is a feeling, not a deduction. But he rejects the idea that it requires a special moral sense — ordinary sympathy, operating on ordinary human feelings, produces approbation naturally.

## Key Passages

> "To approve of the passions of another, therefore, as suitable to their objects, is the same thing as to observe that we entirely sympathize with them."(005-002)

> "It is upon the consciousness of this conditional sympathy, that our approbation of his sorrow is founded, even in those cases in which that sympathy does not actually take place."(005-005)

> "The sentiment of complete sympathy and approbation, mixed and animated with wonder and surprise, constitutes what is properly called admiration."(016-014)
