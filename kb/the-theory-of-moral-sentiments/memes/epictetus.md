# Epictetus

The Stoic philosopher whose doctrine that virtue consists in accepting what is beyond our control and mastering what is within it deeply shaped Smith's treatment of self-command — though Smith transformed the Stoic elimination of passion into a more humane regulation of it.

## Biographical Sketch

Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was born a slave in Phrygia (modern Turkey), studied Stoic philosophy in Rome, and after being freed, taught until banished by Emperor Domitian. His teachings were recorded by his student Arrian in the *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* (Handbook). His central teaching — that we must distinguish what is in our power (our judgments, desires, aversions) from what is not (our body, property, reputation) — became the core of later Stoicism.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on the Dichotomy of Control

"Some things are up to us and some are not." This is Epictetus's foundational principle. Our opinions, impulses, desires, and aversions are within our control; everything else — health, wealth, reputation, the actions of others — is not. Virtue consists in concerning ourselves only with what is within our power and accepting everything else with equanimity.

### Doctrine on the Discipline of Assent

Epictetus taught a rigorous practice of examining every impression before giving it assent. "It is not things that disturb men, but their judgments about things." The path to freedom is the discipline of refusing to assent to false judgments — particularly the judgment that externals are good or evil.

### Points of Agreement

The core Stoic insight that Smith adopts: self-command — the mastery of passion — is a central moral virtue. Smith's "awful and respectable" virtues are essentially Stoic virtues. The idea that we should bring our passions into conformity with what is proper echoes Epictetus's discipline.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's crucial departure: the Stoics sought to eliminate passion (to achieve *apatheia*); Smith seeks only to regulate it to the pitch at which the impartial spectator can sympathize. "The man who feels the full distress of the calamity which has befallen him" and yet controls its expression is more admirable, for Smith, than the Stoic sage who claims to feel nothing.
