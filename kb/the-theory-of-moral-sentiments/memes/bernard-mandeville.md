# Bernard Mandeville

The philosopher whose "licentious system" reduced all virtue to disguised self-interest — provoking Smith to articulate a more psychologically adequate account in which genuine other-regarding sentiments coexist with self-love.

## Biographical Sketch

Bernard Mandeville (1670–1733) was a Dutch-born physician and philosopher who settled in London. His major work, *The Fable of the Bees* (1714), argued that private vices produce public benefits — that civilization, commerce, and even morality itself arise from selfish passions rather than genuine virtue. The book caused a scandal and was denounced as immoral by philosophers and clergymen alike.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Virtue as Disguised Self-Interest

Mandeville's central and most provocative claim: what we call "virtue" is merely self-interest in disguise. The person who gives to charity does so for the pleasure of being seen as generous. The person who acts justly does so from fear of punishment. The person who cultivates self-command does so to gain social advantage. "Moralists and philosophers of all ages" have conspired to call these self-interested actions "virtue" to flatter mankind into behaving well.

### Doctrine on Private Vices, Public Benefits

Mandeville's second major thesis holds that private vices — greed, luxury, vanity, ambition — are the engines of economic prosperity. The spendthrift creates employment; the ambitious creates innovation; the vain creates culture. Without these "vices," society would stagnate.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith devotes an extended section of Part VII to Mandeville — a sign of how seriously he takes the challenge Mandeville poses. If Mandeville is right, Smith's entire project of describing genuine moral sentiments collapses.

### Points of Agreement

Smith concedes that Mandeville has identified something real. Vanity and the desire for approbation *do* play a role in motivating apparently virtuous conduct. "The desire of being believed to do our duty... unites with the love of virtue itself." Smith acknowledges that self-interest and vanity often mix with genuine moral motives.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's core objection: Mandeville's "system" cannot account for the *felt quality* of moral experience. We do not merely act as if we cared about others — we actually do care. Sympathy is real. The impartial spectator's verdict is not a calculation of advantage but a genuine moral sentiment. "It is not the love of our neighbour, it is not the love of mankind, which upon many occasions prompts us to the practice of those divine virtues. It is a stronger love, a more powerful affection... the love of what is honourable and noble."

## Key Passages in TMS

> "The system of Dr. Mandeville... represents whatever is done from a sense of propriety, from a regard to what is commendable and praise-worthy, as being done merely from a regard to what is the object of vanity."(038-025)
