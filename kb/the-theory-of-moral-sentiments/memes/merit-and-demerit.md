# Merit and Demerit

The second fundamental dimension of moral judgment in Smith's system, distinct from propriety: the sense that an action deserves reward (merit) or punishment (demerit), grounded in our sympathetic response to the gratitude or resentment the action naturally provokes.

## Smith's Account

After establishing propriety as the first dimension of moral judgment (does the passion fit its cause?), Smith introduces merit as the second (what does the action deserve?). Merit and demerit are not merely intensified approval and disapproval — they are a distinct kind of moral judgment that looks forward to consequences rather than backward to causes.

### Core Definition

Smith draws a careful distinction between the two dimensions:

> "In the beneficial or hurtful nature of the effects which the affection aims at, or tends to produce, consists the merit or demerit of the action, the qualities by which it is entitled to reward, or is deserving of punishment."(005-008)

While propriety concerns the relation of passion to its cause, merit concerns the relation of action to its effects. An action may be improper (the passion was disproportionate) yet meritorious (it produced great benefit); or proper (the passion was exactly right) yet neutral in merit (it produced no significant effect).

### Key Features

**Grounded in gratitude and resentment.** Merit is not an abstract calculation of utility. It is the felt sense that someone deserves reward because they have provoked gratitude — and the impartial spectator can sympathize with that gratitude:

> "The sentiment which most immediately and directly prompts us to reward, is gratitude; that which most immediately and directly prompts us to punish, is resentment."(022-007)

**Double sympathy required.** For an action to have full merit, we must sympathize with both the agent's motive (propriety) and the beneficiary's gratitude (merit). These two sympathetic judgments are distinct but complementary.

**Distinguished from utility.** Smith explicitly argues against Hume's view that our sense of merit reduces to the perception of utility. We do not approve of an action because it is useful; we approve of it because we sympathize with the gratitude it naturally provokes. Utility may reinforce this judgment but is not its foundation.

## Role in the Moral System

The merit/demerit dimension completes Smith's moral judgment framework. Together with propriety, it provides a comprehensive account of how we evaluate actions:

- Propriety: Is the passion proportionate to its cause? → Decency/indecency
- Merit: Does the action deserve reward or punishment? → Reward/punishment

This two-dimensional structure is what allows Smith to explain, for example, why we can approve of a well-intentioned action that fails (proper but not meritorious) or a beneficial action done for the wrong reasons (meritorious in effect but improper in motive).

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Propriety**: The first dimension — does the passion fit its cause?
- **Gratitude**: The natural response to benefit, grounds merit.
- **Resentment**: The natural response to injury, grounds demerit.
- **Approbation**: The broader sentiment of moral approval, encompassing both dimensions.

## Historical Context

Smith's distinction between propriety and merit is one of his most original contributions to moral philosophy. It allows him to synthesize insights from both the sentimentalist tradition (which emphasized the felt quality of moral judgment) and the consequentialist tradition (which emphasized the effects of actions), without reducing either to the other.
