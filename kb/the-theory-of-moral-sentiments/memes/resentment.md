# Resentment

The passion that arises from perceived injury or injustice — in Smith's system, the natural foundation of punishment and the guardian of justice, provided it is moderated to the pitch the impartial spectator can approve.

## Smith's Account

Resentment occupies a unique and difficult position in Smith's taxonomy of the passions. Unlike the social passions (generosity, compassion) which we readily sympathize with, resentment is "disagreeable"(004-005) — an unsocial passion that requires special effort for the spectator to enter into. Yet without it, justice would have no enforcement mechanism.

### Core Definition

Resentment is the immediate emotional response to injury. Smith analyzes it carefully, distinguishing justified resentment (which the impartial spectator approves) from excessive resentment (which the spectator rejects):

> "The man who resents the injuries that have been done to me, and observes that I resent them precisely as he does, necessarily approves of my resentment."(005-002)

The key insight is that the propriety of resentment is measured by sympathetic correspondence, just like every other passion. The impartial spectator sympathizes with the victim's resentment but only to the extent that the injury warrants — and this "conditional" resentment is what makes punishment legitimate.

### Key Features

**An unsocial passion.** Unlike love, which is "agreeable," resentment is "disagreeable" to the spectator(004-005). We do not naturally want to share another's anger. This means resentment requires special justification — we must first understand the provocation before we can sympathize:

> "The furious behaviour of an angry man is more likely to exasperate us against himself than against his enemies."(003-007)

**The guardian of justice.** Despite its disagreeable nature, resentment serves an essential social function. It is the passion that motivates punishment, and without punishment, justice would be an empty ideal. Smith thus treats resentment as the "guardian" of justice — a disagreeable but necessary passion.

**Proportionality is key.** The impartial spectator's approval of resentment depends entirely on its proportionality to the injury. "If my animosity goes beyond what the indignation of my friend can correspond to"(005-002), it becomes improper. Resentment is thus subject to the same standard of propriety as every other passion.

**Distinguished from revenge.** Smith is careful to distinguish properly proportioned resentment from mere revenge. The spectator approves of resentment that matches the injury; revenge that exceeds it is condemned.

## Role in the Moral System

Resentment is the bridge between propriety and justice. Propriety tells us when a passion is appropriate; resentment is the specific passion that responds to injustice; and justice is the system of rules that resentment enforces.

Without resentment, we would have no natural motivation to punish wrongdoing. The impartial spectator's sympathetic response to the victim's resentment is what transforms private anger into public justice.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Justice**: The system of rules that resentment enforces.
- **Sympathy**: The mechanism through which the spectator judges the propriety of resentment.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The ideal judge who limits resentment to what is proportionate to the injury.
- **Punishment**: Resentment guided by the impartial spectator's approval.

## Historical Context

Smith's treatment of resentment is a direct response to the Stoic tradition, which condemned anger entirely. Smith argues that resentment has a natural and proper place in human moral life — it is not a defect to be eliminated but a passion to be regulated. This represents Smith's broader departure from Stoicism: passions are not the enemy of virtue but its raw material, to be shaped by sympathy and propriety.
