# An Inquiry into the Original of Our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue

Hutcheson's foundational work of sentimentalist ethics, arguing that humans possess an innate "moral sense" — a perceptual faculty that immediately perceives moral qualities in actions, analogous to the sense of beauty.

## Overview

Published in 1725, Hutcheson's *Inquiry* developed the most systematic version of the moral sense theory. Against Hobbes and Mandeville, Hutcheson argued that moral perception is innate, not learned; against rationalists like Clarke, he insisted that moral distinctions are sensed, not deduced. The work established the sentimentalist tradition in British moral philosophy that Smith would both inherit and transform.

## Author

[[francis-hutcheson]] — Smith's teacher and the leading figure of early Scottish Enlightenment moral philosophy.

## Relevance to TMS

Smith adopts Hutcheson's sentimentalist starting point — moral judgment is a matter of feeling — but rejects the hypothesis of a special moral sense. Smith's innovation is to replace the moral sense with sympathy — an ordinary psychological mechanism, not a special faculty — which can explain everything the moral sense was supposed to explain without multiplying entities.

Smith also rejects Hutcheson's reduction of all virtue to benevolence, arguing that self-command, prudence, and justice are genuine virtues that cannot be analyzed as forms of benevolence.
