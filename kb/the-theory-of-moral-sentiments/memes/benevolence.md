# Benevolence

Benevolence is the disposition to promote the happiness of others — the social affection that wishes and does good, but which Smith insists is always free and cannot be extorted by force.

## Smith's Account

For Smith, benevolence belongs to the class of "amiable virtues" — those soft and gentle excellences that make society flourish and happy, but which are not strictly required for its bare survival. Throughout *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, he draws a sustained and architectonic contrast between benevolence and justice: the former is the ornament of the edifice of society, the latter its foundation. This distinction is Smith's most distinctive contribution to the philosophical treatment of benevolence.

### Core Definition

Smith defines benevolence by what it is not — namely, justice — and through its voluntary character. The core passage appears in Part II, where Smith establishes the famous distinction between the two social virtues:

> Beneficence is always free, it cannot be extorted by force, the mere want of it exposes to no punishment; because the mere want of beneficence tends to do no real positive evil. It may disappoint of the good which might reasonably have been expected, and upon that account it may justly excite dislike and disapprobation: it cannot, however, provoke any resentment which mankind will go along with. (028-003)

Benevolence is thus what Smith calls a "free" virtue: its observance "is not left to the freedom of our own wills" (as justice is) but rather is something we may choose to exercise or not, without deserving punishment (028-005). The man who fails in benevolence is blameworthy but not punishable.

### Key Features

Several distinctive features mark Smith's concept of benevolence:

**The ornament/foundation distinction.** Smith's architectural metaphor is the most memorable encapsulation of his view. Beneficence "is the ornament which embellishes, not the foundation which supports, the building" (030-004). Justice, by contrast, "is the main pillar that upholds the whole edifice." Society can subsist without benevolence, though uncomfortably; but "the prevalence of injustice must utterly destroy it" (030-003).

**Proper benevolence as a constituent of perfect virtue.** In Part VI, Smith places "proper benevolence" alongside prudence and justice as one of the three components of the perfectly virtuous character. "The man who acts according to the rules of perfect prudence, of strict justice, and of proper benevolence, may be said to be perfectly virtuous" (057-001). Yet even perfect knowledge of these rules is insufficient without self-command to execute them (057-004).

**Degrees of benevolence.** Smith distinguishes the ordinary degree of kindness from praiseworthy benevolence. "The ordinary degree itself seems neither blamable nor praiseworthy" (028-006). What merits praise is going beyond the ordinary — "he who surprises us by extraordinary and unexpected, though still proper and suitable kindness" (028-006). This graded account integrates benevolence into Smith's broader theory that virtue consists in excellence above the ordinary, not mere propriety.

**Benevolence and reward.** While benevolence does not admit of punishment for its absence, its positive exercise naturally attracts the highest reward: "Though the mere want of beneficence seems to merit no punishment from equals, the greater exertions of that virtue appear to deserve the highest reward" (028-009). The reason is that benevolent actions, "by being productive of the greatest good, they are the natural and approved objects of the liveliest gratitude."

**Universal benevolence.** In its most extended form, benevolence can embrace the whole universe. "Our goodwill is circumscribed by no boundary, but may embrace the immensity of the universe" (056-001). Smith grounds this universal benevolence in the conviction that a benevolent Deity directs creation toward "the greatest possible quantity of happiness" (056-005). However, he cautions that such universal benevolence can provide no solid happiness unless one is "thoroughly convinced that all the inhabitants of the universe ... are under the immediate care and protection of that great, benevolent, and all-wise Being" (056-002).

**The benevolent affections and perfection of human nature.** In the opening section on propriety, Smith identifies the indulgence of benevolent affections as essential to human excellence: "to feel much for others and little for ourselves, that to restrain our selfish, and to indulge our benevolent affections, constitutes the perfection of human nature" (007-005).

## Role in the Moral System

Benevolence sits at the center of Smith's taxonomy of the virtues and his theory of moral judgment. It is the virtue most closely associated with the "amiable" side of human nature — that which wins love rather than commands respect. Smith's treatment of benevolence also serves as the critical pivot in his critique of his teacher Francis Hutcheson's moral system.

### Relation to Other Concepts

**Benevolence and justice.** This is the master contrast of Smith's moral philosophy. Justice can be extorted; benevolence cannot. Justice is a "negative virtue" that "only hinders us from hurting our neighbour"; benevolence does "real positive good" (028-009). The violation of justice provokes resentment and calls for punishment; the want of benevolence provokes only disapprobation (028-003). Nature enforces justice through "the terrors of merited punishment" but recommends beneficence only through "the pleasing consciousness of deserved reward" (030-004).

**Benevolence and sympathy.** Benevolence is not itself sympathy, but it engages the mechanism of double sympathy that Smith describes. Benevolent actions please the agent, please the beneficiary, and please the spectator who sympathizes with both: "proper benevolence is the most graceful and agreeable of all the affections, that it is recommended to us by a double sympathy" (065-004). This double sympathy accounts for the peculiar agreeableness of benevolent conduct.

**Benevolence and self-command.** While benevolence is one of the three components of perfect virtue (alongside prudence and justice), it requires self-command to be sustained against countervailing temptations. Smith urges us "never to suffer the benevolence of our temper to be damped or discouraged by the malice and ingratitude of the individuals towards whom it may have been exercised" (057-011). Self-command is the executive power that enables benevolence to persist.

**Benevolence and propriety.** The benevolent passions have a special relationship to propriety. Unlike every other passion, benevolence "can exert themselves without any regard or attention to propriety, and yet retain something about them which is engaging" (065-004). Excessive resentment is disgusting; excessive selfishness is contemptible; but "the most excessive indulgence even of partial friendship is not so offensive." There is "something pleasing even in mere instinctive goodwill" (065-004).

## Historical Context

Smith's account of benevolence must be understood against the background of Francis Hutcheson, Smith's teacher at Glasgow and the philosopher who made benevolence the centerpiece of his moral system. Hutcheson held that "virtue consists in benevolence" — that the sole motive capable of conferring moral worth on an action is pure, disinterested goodwill toward others (060-003, 065-003).

> That virtue consists in benevolence is a notion supported by many appearances in human nature. It has been observed already, that proper benevolence is the most graceful and agreeable of all the affections, that it is recommended to us by a double sympathy, that as its tendency is necessarily beneficent, it is the proper object of gratitude and reward, and that upon all these accounts it appears to our natural sentiments to possess a merit superior to any other. (065-004)

Smith acknowledges the force of Hutcheson's system, calling him "undoubtedly, beyond all comparison, the most acute, the most distinct, the most philosophical, and what is of the greatest consequence of all, the soberest and most judicious" of its proponents (065-003). He grants that benevolence "bestows upon those actions which proceed from it, a beauty superior to all others" (065-005).

However, Smith rejects the reduction of all virtue to benevolence. The system "seems to have the contrary defect, of not sufficiently explaining from whence arises our approbation of the inferior virtues of prudence, vigilance, circumspection, temperance, constancy, firmness" (065-015). For Smith, a system adequate to human nature must recognize that "so imperfect a creature as man, the support of whose existence requires so many things external to him, must often act from many other motives" besides benevolence (065-018). Prudence, self-regarding virtues, and above all justice, cannot be reduced to benevolence without distortion.

In the ancient tradition, Smith notes that the system making virtue consist in benevolence "seems to have been the doctrine of the greater part of those philosophers who, about and after the age of Augustus, called themselves Eclectics" — the later Platonists who held that "benevolence or love was the sole principle of action" in the divine nature (065-001, 065-002).

## Key Passages

> Beneficence is always free, it cannot be extorted by force, the mere want of it exposes to no punishment; because the mere want of beneficence tends to do no real positive evil. (028-003)

> Beneficence, therefore, is less essential to the existence of society than justice. Society may subsist, though not in the most comfortable state, without beneficence; but the prevalence of injustice must utterly destroy it. (030-003)

> Though Nature, therefore, exhorts mankind to acts of beneficence, by the pleasing consciousness of deserved reward, she has not thought it necessary to guard and enforce the practice of it by the terrors of merited punishment in case it should be neglected. It is the ornament which embellishes, not the foundation which supports, the building. (030-004)

> The man who acts according to the rules of perfect prudence, of strict justice, and of proper benevolence, may be said to be perfectly virtuous. (057-001)

> This universal benevolence, how noble and generous soever, can be the source of no solid happiness to any man who is not thoroughly convinced that all the inhabitants of the universe, the meanest as well as the greatest, are under the immediate care and protection of that great, benevolent, and all-wise Being. (056-002)

> That virtue consists in benevolence is a notion supported by many appearances in human nature. It has been observed already, that proper benevolence is the most graceful and agreeable of all the affections. (065-004)
