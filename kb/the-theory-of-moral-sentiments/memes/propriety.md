# Propriety

Smith's standard of moral judgment: the correspondence between a person's passion or action and its cause, as measured by the sympathetic response of an impartial spectator.

## Smith's Account

Propriety is the central evaluative concept in Smith's moral theory. It is not a fixed code of rules but a relational standard — an action is proper when the passion behind it is proportionate to its cause, as judged by an impartial spectator through the mechanism of sympathy.

### Core Definition

Smith defines propriety as the fit between sentiment and situation. The judgment of propriety is not a deduction from abstract principles but a felt response:

> "In the suitableness or unsuitableness, in the proportion or disproportion which the affection seems to bear to the cause or object which excites it, consists the propriety or impropriety, the decency or ungracefulness of the consequent action."(005-007)

This standard is applied through sympathy. We judge another's passion by comparing it to what we would feel in their situation:

> "When the original passions of the person principally concerned are in perfect concord with the sympathetic emotions of the spectator, they necessarily appear to this last just and proper, and suitable to their objects."(005-002)

### Key Features

**A relational, not absolute, standard.** Propriety depends entirely on the relation between passion and cause — not on the intrinsic character of the passion itself. A violent grief may be proper if the loss is great; a slight resentment may be improper if the provocation was trivial.

**Measured by sympathy, not reason.** Smith insists that the standard of propriety is felt, not deduced:

> "Every faculty in one man is the measure by which he judges of the like faculty in another. I judge of your sight by my sight, of your ear by my ear, of your reason by my reason, of your resentment by my resentment, of your love by my love. I neither have, nor can have, any other way of judging about them."(005-011)

**Conditional sympathy suffices.** We need not actually feel the corresponding passion to judge an action proper. When we know from experience that a certain cause naturally produces a certain passion, we approve even without feeling it ourselves, "upon the consciousness of this conditional sympathy"(005-005).

**Distinguished from merit.** Propriety concerns the relation of passion to its cause; merit concerns the relation of action to its effects. Smith carefully separates these two dimensions of moral judgment(005-008).

## Role in the Moral System

Propriety is the first and most fundamental moral judgment in Smith's system. Before we ask whether an action deserves reward or punishment (merit), we ask whether the passion behind it is proportionate (propriety).

The entire edifice of *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* is organized around propriety. Part I is devoted to "The Propriety of Action" — Smith's most extensive treatment of any single moral concept — precisely because propriety is the foundation on which all other moral judgments rest.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Sympathy**: The mechanism through which we judge propriety. Without sympathetic imagination, we could never compare another's passion to its cause.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The idealized perspective from which propriety judgments are most reliable — stripping away self-preference and partiality.
- **Self-Command**: The virtue of moderating one's own passions to the pitch at which the impartial spectator can sympathize — self-command is essentially the art of maintaining propriety.

## Historical Context

Smith's concept of propriety draws from classical sources — particularly the Stoic notion of *decorum* (τὸ πρέπον) — but transforms them through the mechanism of sympathy. While Cicero and the Stoics grounded propriety in rational conformity to nature, Smith grounds it in the felt correspondence between spectator and agent. This represents a shift from a rationalist to a sentimentalist standard of moral judgment.

## Key Passages

> "To approve of the passions of another, therefore, as suitable to their objects, is the same thing as to observe that we entirely sympathize with them; and not to approve of them as such, is the same thing as to observe that we do not entirely sympathize with them."(005-002)

> "If, upon bringing the case home to our own breast, we find that the sentiments which it gives occasion to, coincide and tally with our own, we necessarily approve of them as proportioned and suitable to their objects; if otherwise, we necessarily disapprove of them, as extravagant and out of proportion."(005-010)
