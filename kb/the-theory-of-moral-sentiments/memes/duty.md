# Duty

The felt obligation to act in accordance with moral rules — arising, in Smith's naturalistic account, from the generalization of sympathetic judgments into principles that the internalized impartial spectator (conscience) enforces.

## Smith's Account

Smith offers a naturalistic account of duty that grounds moral obligation in human psychology rather than divine command or rational deduction. Moral rules emerge from repeated sympathetic judgments, which become generalized over time. Once internalized as conscience, these rules acquire the force of duty.

### Core Definition

Duty, for Smith, is what conscience commands. It is not a deduction from abstract principles but the felt verdict of the impartial spectator within:

> "It is reason, principle, conscience, the inhabitant of the breast, the man within, the great judge and arbiter of our conduct."(039-004)

The sense of duty is the sense that certain actions are required of us regardless of our inclinations or the opinions of others. It is the most powerful motive in moral life, capable of overriding even self-love.

### Key Features

**Generalized from sympathy.** Moral rules are not innate or revealed — they are generalizations from countless particular sympathetic judgments. Over time, we learn what kinds of actions typically provoke approbation or disapprobation.

**Enforced by conscience.** The internalized impartial spectator — conscience — enforces these rules with the authority of "the man within the breast"(034-001). This internal enforcement is what gives duty its distinctive felt quality.

**Independent of actual opinion.** Duty may conflict with what others actually think. The man of conscience may "appeal from the verdict of the actual spectator to that of the supposed impartial and well-informed spectator." This is the foundation of moral autonomy.

**The "general rules" of morality.** Smith describes how particular judgments crystallize into "general rules" that guide conduct. These rules are not infallible — they can be corrupted by custom and fashion — but they provide the stability that makes moral life possible.

## Role in the Moral System

Duty is what transforms Smith's descriptive moral psychology into a normative ethics. Without the sense of duty, moral judgments would be mere feelings with no binding force. With duty, the sympathetic mechanism produces not just descriptions of what we feel but obligations about what we must do.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Conscience**: The internal faculty that enforces duty.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The perspective from which duty is judged.
- **Sympathy**: The mechanism that generates the judgments duty generalizes.
- **General Rules**: The crystallized principles that duty enforces.

## Historical Context

Smith's naturalistic account of duty engages with and departs from several traditions. Against divine command theorists, he grounds obligation in human psychology. Against rationalists (Clarke, Wollaston), he insists that duty is felt, not deduced. Against pure sentimentalists, he explains how feelings become rules. In this, Smith anticipates elements of both Kant (the internal, universalizing character of duty) and Hume (the naturalistic, sentimentalist origin).
