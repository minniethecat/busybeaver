# Impartial Spectator

Smith's most distinctive philosophical innovation: the imagined figure of a disinterested observer whose sympathetic responses serve as the standard of moral judgment, particularly of our own conduct.

## Smith's Account

The impartial spectator is the culmination of Smith's theory of sympathy. While sympathy explains how we judge others, the impartial spectator explains how we judge ourselves — by imagining how a disinterested observer would react to our own conduct and passions.

### Core Definition

Smith introduces the impartial spectator as the mechanism of self-judgment. We cannot directly observe our own conduct from a neutral perspective, so we must imagine how an impartial person would view it:

> "We endeavour to examine our own conduct as we imagine any other fair and impartial spectator would examine it. If, upon placing ourselves in his situation, we thoroughly enter into all the passions and motives which influenced it, we approve of it by sympathy with the approbation of this supposed equitable judge."(037-002)

### Key Features

**Internalized as conscience.** What begins as an external social process — seeing ourselves through others' eyes — becomes internalized as "the man within the breast," Smith's vivid metaphor for conscience(034-001). This internal judge is not infallible but is less susceptible to the distortions of self-love.

**The standard of self-command.** The impartial spectator defines how much passion is appropriate. "Magnanimity, or a regard to maintain our own rank and dignity in society, is the only motive which can ennoble the expressions of this disagreeable passion"(012-008). Self-command is the virtue of moderating passion to the pitch that the impartial spectator can enter into.

**Distinct from actual spectators.** The impartial spectator is not simply what other people actually think. Real spectators may be biased, ignorant, or absent. The impartial spectator is an idealized perspective that corrects for these limitations — though Smith acknowledges that the "actual spectator" (real social feedback) is an important check on self-deception.

**The foundation of duty.** The impartial spectator transforms social propriety into moral duty. When we ask not merely "what would others think?" but "what would a fully informed, impartial judge think?" we move from social conformity to genuine moral conscience.

## Role in the Moral System

The impartial spectator is the keystone of Smith's moral psychology, connecting sympathy (the mechanism of fellow-feeling) to conscience (the internalized moral judge). It provides the answer to a question that sympathy alone cannot resolve: how can we judge our own conduct, given that we cannot step outside ourselves?

The impartial spectator also explains why we sometimes feel moral obligations that contradict social opinion. When the "man within the breast" condemns what the crowd applauds, conscience speaks with a higher authority — anticipating Kant's categorical imperative while remaining firmly grounded in Smith's sentimentalist framework.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Sympathy**: The impartial spectator's judgments are sympathetic judgments — they are what a sympathetic observer would feel. The spectator is impartial precisely because they are not the agent.
- **Propriety**: The standard by which the impartial spectator judges — an action is proper when the spectator can sympathize with the passion behind it.
- **Conscience**: The internalized impartial spectator, operating even when no real observer is present.

## Historical Context

Smith's impartial spectator is often compared to Kant's categorical imperative and to Freud's superego. All three are internalized mechanisms of self-regulation. But Smith's version is distinctive: it operates through imagination and sympathy rather than through duty or repression. The impartial spectator does not command — it feels. We approve or disapprove of ourselves by imagining how we would appear to a disinterested observer, not by applying abstract rules.

## Key Passages

> "We endeavour to examine our own conduct as we imagine any other fair and impartial spectator would examine it."(037-002)

> "It is he who shows us the propriety of generosity and the deformity of injustice; the propriety of resigning the greatest interests of our own, for the yet greater interests of others, and the deformity of doing the smallest injury to another, in order to obtain the greatest benefit to ourselves."(034-001)
