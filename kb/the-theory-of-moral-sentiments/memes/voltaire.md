# Voltaire

The towering figure of the French Enlightenment, whose critiques of religious intolerance and philosophical optimism Smith engages with — respecting Voltaire's moral passion while finding his philosophical framework less systematic than his own.

## Biographical Sketch

François-Marie Arouet (1694–1778), known as Voltaire, was the most famous writer of the French Enlightenment. Exiled to England from 1726–1729, he encountered Newtonian science and English political liberty, which shaped his lifelong campaign against superstition, intolerance, and arbitrary power. His works span philosophy, history, poetry, drama, and the philosophical tale — most famously *Candide*.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Critique of Optimism

In *Candide*, Voltaire devastatingly satirized Leibniz's doctrine that this is "the best of all possible worlds" — a philosophical optimism that Voltaire saw as morally complacent in the face of real suffering. Smith shared Voltaire's impatience with abstract philosophical systems that ignore the actual texture of human misery.

### Religious Toleration

Voltaire's *Treatise on Toleration* argued that religious persecution is both irrational and immoral. Smith, writing in a more tolerant Britain, did not need to make this argument as forcefully — but his moral psychology, with its emphasis on the impartial spectator's rejection of cruelty, provides philosophical grounding for tolerance.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith met Voltaire during his grand tour of Europe (1764–1766) as tutor to the young Duke of Buccleuch. Smith reports this meeting with characteristic understatement, but the engagement with Voltaire's ideas runs throughout TMS. Voltaire represents for Smith a certain kind of moral energy — fierce, satirical, committed — that is admirable but incomplete without the systematic moral psychology that Smith provides.

### Points of Agreement

Smith shares Voltaire's moral seriousness and his rejection of philosophical systems that ignore lived experience. Both thinkers insist that philosophy must answer to the actual sentiments of actual human beings.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith would find Voltaire's critique strong on exposure but weak on construction. Satire can show what is wrong; it cannot provide the systematic account of moral psychology needed to understand why it is wrong and how to do better.

## Key Passages in TMS

Smith does not name Voltaire directly in TMS, but his treatment of the "love of system" and the dangers of abstract philosophical schemes reflects Voltaire's influence.

## Key Works

- **Candide** (1759) — Voltaire's satirical masterpiece, published the same year as TMS
- **Treatise on Toleration** (1763) — argument for religious freedom
