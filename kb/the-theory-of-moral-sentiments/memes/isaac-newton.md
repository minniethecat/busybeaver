# Isaac Newton

The scientific genius whose method — reducing the apparent chaos of nature to a few simple principles — served as Smith's methodological model: the theory of moral sentiments aims to do for moral phenomena what the *Principia* did for physical phenomena.

## Biographical Sketch

Isaac Newton (1642–1727) transformed physics, mathematics, and astronomy. His *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) demonstrated that the same universal law of gravitation explains both the fall of an apple and the orbits of planets. For the eighteenth century, Newton was the model of what systematic knowledge could achieve — and Smith explicitly aspired to be "the Newton of the moral world."

## Smith's Engagement

Smith's engagement with Newton is methodological rather than philosophical. He does not apply Newtonian physics to ethics but adopts the Newtonian ideal of explanation: diverse phenomena explained through a few simple principles. Just as Newton unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics through gravitation, Smith aims to unify diverse moral phenomena — approval, disapproval, resentment, gratitude, conscience — through the single principle of sympathy.

The phrase "theory of moral sentiments" itself signals this Newtonian ambition. A "theory" in the eighteenth-century sense is a system that derives many phenomena from few principles — precisely what Smith's systematic deployment of sympathy achieves.

## Key Works

- **Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica** (1687) — Newton's masterpiece
- **Opticks** (1704) — Newton's work on light and color
