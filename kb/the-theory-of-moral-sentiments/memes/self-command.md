# Self-Command

The virtue of regulating one's passions so that they remain within the range that the impartial spectator can enter into and approve — one of the two cardinal virtues in Smith's moral system, alongside sensibility.

## Smith's Account

Self-command is, for Smith, the virtue that makes all other virtues possible. Without the capacity to moderate our passions, we cannot bring them into conformity with what propriety requires. It is "the great school of self-command" — the demands of social life — that shapes moral character.

### Core Definition

Smith defines self-command not as the suppression of passion but as its regulation to a socially appropriate pitch. The person of self-command does not feel less; they control the expression and intensity of what they feel:

> "The man who has the most perfect self-command, who is most perfectly master of his own feelings and passions, is upon all occasions, and in every situation, the same person."(007-003)

### Key Features

**The two sets of virtues.** Smith organizes the virtues into two complementary categories: the "amiable" virtues of sensibility (feeling with others) and the "awful" virtues of self-command (regulating oneself). Both are necessary — sensibility without self-command is weakness; self-command without sensibility is hardness(007-006).

**Acquired through social discipline.** Self-command is not innate but learned through social interaction:

> "A very young child has no self-command; but, whatever are its emotions, whether fear, or grief, or anger, it endeavours always, by the violence of its outcries, to alarm, as much as it can, the attention of its nurse, or of its parents."(039-022)

It is only through "the great school of self-command, in the bustle and business of the world" that we learn to moderate our passions(039-025).

**The measure of dignity.** For Smith, self-command is the foundation of human dignity. The person who cannot control their passions forfeits the respect of others, regardless of the justice of their cause. "What noble propriety and grace do we feel in the conduct of those who exert that recollection and self-command which constitute the dignity of every passion"(007-003).

**Graded by situation.** The degree of self-command required varies with circumstances. The same passion that is proper in private grief may be excessive in public. Smith provides a detailed phenomenology of the degrees of self-command required in different life situations(039-021).

## Role in the Moral System

Self-command is the bridge between the judgment of propriety (knowing what is proper) and the practice of virtue (doing what is proper). Sympathy and the impartial spectator tell us what standard our passions should meet; self-command is the capacity to actually meet that standard.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Propriety**: Self-command is the virtue that enables us to maintain propriety — to keep our passions proportionate to their causes even under pressure.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The impartial spectator sets the standard; self-command achieves it.
- **Sensibility**: The complementary virtue — feeling what others feel — which self-command must regulate.
- **Sympathy**: Self-command moderates our passions to the pitch at which others can sympathize with them.

## Historical Context

Smith's treatment of self-command is deeply indebted to Stoicism — particularly Epictetus and Marcus Aurelius — but with a crucial difference. Where the Stoics sought to eliminate passion, Smith seeks to regulate it. The Stoic sage is *apatheia* (without passion); Smith's person of self-command feels the full range of human passion but controls its expression. This reflects Smith's more positive view of the passions as natural guides to moral life.

## Key Passages

> "What noble propriety and grace do we feel in the conduct of those who exert that recollection and self-command which constitute the dignity of every passion, and which bring it down to what others can enter into."(007-003)

> "A very young child has no self-command; but, whatever are its emotions, whether fear, or grief, or anger, it endeavours always, by the violence of its outcries, to alarm, as much as it can, the attention of its nurse, or of its parents."(039-022)

> "The man who feels the full distress of the calamity which has befallen him, feels also the contempt which is due to the weakness of that excessive grief which he observes in himself, and therefore strives to keep down."(007-008)
