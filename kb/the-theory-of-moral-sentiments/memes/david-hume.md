# David Hume

Adam Smith's closest friend and philosophical ally — the thinker whose theory of sympathy most directly influenced *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, even though Smith departs from Hume on key points, particularly the role of utility in moral judgment.

## Biographical Sketch

David Hume (1711–1776) was the central figure of the Scottish Enlightenment. His *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40) developed an empirical science of human psychology, and his *Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751) argued that sympathy — not reason — is the foundation of moral distinctions. Hume and Smith were close friends from the 1750s until Hume's death; Smith's account of Hume's final days is a classic of philosophical biography.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Sympathy as the Foundation of Morals

Hume argued that moral distinctions arise from sentiment, not reason. "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." Sympathy — our capacity to share the feelings of others — is the mechanism through which we come to approve or disapprove of character and conduct. Smith adopted this framework but refined the mechanism: for Smith, sympathy involves imaginatively placing ourselves in another's situation, not merely catching their emotion.

### Doctrine on Utility

Hume held that utility — the tendency of a quality or action to produce beneficial effects — is a primary source of moral approbation. We approve of justice because it is useful to society. Smith rejects this: utility can reinforce approbation but is not its source. Our approval of justice arises from resentment against the injurer, not from the perception of social benefit.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith's relationship to Hume is one of deep agreement on fundamentals combined with systematic revision on specifics. Smith adopts Hume's sentimentalism and his emphasis on sympathy but provides a more detailed psychological account.

### Points of Agreement

Both hold that moral judgments are sentiments, not deductions. Both hold that sympathy — our capacity to share others' feelings — is the foundation of moral life. Both hold that ethics must be based on the empirical study of human nature, not abstract reason.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith departs from Hume on three major points. First, utility is secondary, not primary (as argued in Part IV). Second, Hume's account of sympathy is too passive — Smith's version involves active imaginative projection. Third, Smith's impartial spectator provides a more developed account of how we judge our own conduct.

## Key Passages in TMS

Smith does not name Hume directly in TMS, but his critique of utility-based theories (Part IV) is a sustained — though respectful — engagement with Hume's position.

- **An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals** (1751) — Hume's mature ethical theory
