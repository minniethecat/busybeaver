# Plato

The foundational figure of Western rationalist ethics — Smith engages with Plato's theory of virtue as the harmony of the soul, finding both insight and limitation in the view that virtue consists in the proper ordering of the parts of human nature.

## Biographical Sketch

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was a student of Socrates and the teacher of Aristotle, founder of the Academy in Athens, and the most influential philosopher in the Western tradition. His ethical works — particularly the *Republic* — developed the theory that virtue consists in the proper relation among the parts of the soul: reason ruling over spirit and appetite.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Virtue as Harmony

Plato held that the soul has three parts — reason, spirit (*thymos*), and appetite — and that virtue consists in the proper harmony among them, with reason governing the others. Justice in the soul is analogous to justice in the city: each part doing its proper work. This is a fundamentally rationalist account: virtue is a kind of order, and order is grasped by reason.

### Doctrine on the Form of the Good

Plato's theory culminates in the Form of the Good — an abstract, transcendent standard of value that reason apprehends. Moral knowledge, for Plato, is knowledge of this Form, and the philosopher who has grasped it is thereby equipped to rule both himself and the city.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith discusses Plato primarily in Part VII, where he surveys systems that make virtue consist in propriety. Plato's theory is, in Smith's taxonomy, the most sophisticated version of the view that virtue is a kind of rational order.

### Points of Agreement

Smith shares Plato's intuition that there is something right about the idea of "proportion" or "fitness" in moral judgment. The concept of propriety — the fit between passion and cause — echoes Plato's emphasis on harmony and proper order.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's fundamental disagreement with Plato is the same as with all rationalist systems: moral judgment is not the perception of rational order but a felt response of sympathy. We do not deduce that a proportion is right — we feel it when our sentiments correspond to another's. "It is not reason which is the source of moral distinctions, but immediate sense and feeling."
