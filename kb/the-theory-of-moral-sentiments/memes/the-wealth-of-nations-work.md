# The Wealth of Nations

Smith's foundational work of modern economics — a systematic analysis of how self-interested action, channeled through market exchange, can produce social benefits that no individual intended.

## Overview

*An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776) is Smith's second major work and the foundational text of classical economics. Where TMS analyzes moral psychology, *The Wealth of Nations* analyzes economic behavior — but the two works share a common vision of human nature. Both assume that humans are driven by a mix of self-love and other-regarding sentiments, and that social order emerges from the interaction of these motives.

## Author

[[adam-smith]] — Scottish moral philosopher and economist.

## Relevance to TMS

The relationship between TMS and *The Wealth of Nations* has been a central topic of Smith scholarship. The so-called "Adam Smith Problem" — an alleged contradiction between the sympathy-based ethics of TMS and the self-interest-based economics of WN — has been largely resolved by recognizing that Smith never claimed self-interest is humanity's only motive. Sympathy and self-interest coexist in Smith's psychology, each dominant in its proper sphere.

Smith's famous "invisible hand" appears in both works.
