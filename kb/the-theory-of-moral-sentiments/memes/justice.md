# Justice

The negative virtue that consists in not harming others — the indispensable foundation of social order, enforced by the natural passion of resentment and the threat of punishment.

## Smith's Account

Justice holds a unique place in Smith's moral system as the only virtue whose violation calls forth a specific natural passion — resentment — that demands punishment. This makes justice fundamentally different from all other virtues: it is precise, enforceable, and essential to society's survival.

### Core Definition

Smith distinguishes justice from other virtues by the nature of the response its violation provokes. While the absence of benevolence may disappoint us, the violation of justice provokes active resentment:

> "There is, however, another virtue, of which the observance is not left to the freedom of our own wills, which may be extorted by force, and of which the violation exposes to resentment, and consequently to punishment. This virtue is justice."(022-007)

### Key Features

**A negative virtue.** Unlike benevolence, which requires positive action, justice merely requires refraining from harm. We can be just by doing nothing, but we cannot be benevolent without active effort. This asymmetry makes justice the primary concern of law and government.

**Precise and rule-like.** While propriety and benevolence are matters of degree, justice is "scrupulous and inflexible"(017-004). Smith argues that the rules of justice "are accurate in the highest degree, and admit of no exceptions or modifications" — a striking contrast with the loose, contextual standards of other virtues.

**Enforced by resentment.** The natural passion that guards justice is resentment. When someone violates justice, the impartial spectator sympathizes with the victim's resentment and approves of punishment(006-003). This makes justice the only virtue backed by the threat of force.

**Essential to society.** Smith uses his famous metaphor: society can survive without benevolence, "but the prevalence of injustice must utterly destroy it." Justice is the "main pillar that upholds the whole edifice" of society.

## Role in the Moral System

Justice is the foundation on which all other virtues rest. Without justice — without the security that others will not harm us — the sympathetic mechanisms that make moral life possible cannot function. Justice creates the condition of security within which benevolence, propriety, and self-command can flourish.

The relationship between justice and resentment is crucial to Smith's theory of punishment. Punishment is not mere vengeance — it is resentment guided and approved by the impartial spectator, who sympathizes with the victim's resentment but limits it to what is proportionate.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Resentment**: The natural passion that enforces justice — the impartial spectator's sympathetic response to injustice.
- **Benevolence**: The positive complement to justice — "mere justice is, upon most occasions, but a negative virtue."
- **Sympathy**: The mechanism through which we judge whether resentment is proportionate and punishment is deserved.

## Historical Context

Smith's treatment of justice draws on the natural law tradition (Grotius, Pufendorf) but transforms it through the mechanism of sympathy. While natural law theorists grounded justice in reason and divine will, Smith grounds it in the natural human response of resentment — a sentiment that, when approved by the impartial spectator, becomes the legitimate basis of punishment.
