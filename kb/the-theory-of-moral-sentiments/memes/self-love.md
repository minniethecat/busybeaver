# Self-Love

The natural human concern for one's own welfare — a legitimate motive in Smith's system, distinguished from selfishness, but one that must be disciplined by the impartial spectator to prevent it from distorting moral judgment.

## Smith's Account

Smith's treatment of self-love is central to his engagement with rival moral theories. Against Bernard Mandeville, who reduced all virtue to disguised self-love, and against the Epicurean tradition that made self-interest the sole principle of human action, Smith insists that self-love is real but not sovereign.

### Core Definition

Self-love, for Smith, is a natural and ineradicable human motive — but it is not the only motive, nor the most important one for moral life. The key question is not whether we are motivated by self-love (we are) but whether self-love can be disciplined by the impartial spectator:

> "It is a stronger power, a more forcible motive, which exerts itself upon such occasions. It is reason, principle, conscience, the inhabitant of the breast, the man within, the great judge and arbiter of our conduct."(039-004)

### Key Features

**Distinguished from selfishness.** Smith does not equate self-love with selfishness. We can and do act from genuinely other-regarding motives — sympathy, benevolence, a sense of duty. Self-love is one motive among many.

**The source of self-deception.** Self-love is dangerous precisely because it distorts moral perception. We are prone to "the misrepresentations of self-love concerning what is fit and proper to be done"(040-012). The impartial spectator exists to correct for this bias.

**Necessary but subordinate.** Self-love is necessary for survival and flourishing — "every man is, no doubt, by nature, first and principally recommended to his own care." But it must yield to the claims of justice and, in some cases, benevolence.

**The foil for virtue.** In Smith's system, virtue often consists precisely in overriding self-love when it conflicts with what propriety or justice requires. Self-command, the cardinal virtue, is largely the capacity to say no to self-love.

## Role in the Moral System

Self-love plays a complex dual role in Smith's theory. On one hand, it is the primary obstacle to moral judgment — the bias that the impartial spectator must overcome. On the other hand, it is the engine of worldly activity that Smith vividly describes in his account of "the toil and bustle of this world"(017-001) — the pursuit of wealth, status, and admiration that drives economic and social life.

This creates a productive tension: self-love motivates action, sympathy provides moral standards, and the impartial spectator adjudicates between them. The well-ordered moral life is not one without self-love but one in which self-love is properly regulated.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Impartial Spectator**: The internal judge that corrects self-love's distortions.
- **Self-Command**: The virtue of overriding self-love when it conflicts with propriety.
- **Sympathy**: The counterweight to self-love — our capacity to feel what others feel.
- **Vanity**: Self-love's social expression — the desire for admiration and attention.

## Historical Context

Smith's treatment of self-love is a careful navigation between two extremes. Against Hobbes and Mandeville, he denies that all human action reduces to self-interest. Against Hutcheson and the benevolence-only theorists, he acknowledges that self-love is a legitimate and powerful human motive. Smith's position — that humans are motivated by both self-love and other-regarding sentiments — anticipates the nuanced psychological realism of contemporary moral psychology.

## Key Passages

> "It is a stronger power, a more forcible motive, which exerts itself upon such occasions. It is reason, principle, conscience, the inhabitant of the breast, the man within, the great judge and arbiter of our conduct."(039-004)

> "The man of the most perfect virtue, the man whom we naturally love and revere the most, is he who joins, to the most perfect command of his own original and selfish feelings, the most exquisite sensibility both to the original and sympathetic feelings of others."(007-006)
