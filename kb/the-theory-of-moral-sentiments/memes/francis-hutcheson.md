# Francis Hutcheson

Scottish Enlightenment philosopher, Smith's most important teacher, and the foremost exponent of the moral sense theory that Smith both inherited and transformed in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*.

## Biographical Sketch

Francis Hutcheson (1694–1746) was born in Ireland to a Presbyterian family and educated at the University of Glasgow. He returned to Glasgow as Professor of Moral Philosophy in 1729, where Adam Smith became his student. Hutcheson was the leading figure of the Scottish Enlightenment before Hume and Smith, known for his theory of an innate "moral sense" and his doctrine that virtue consists in benevolence.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on the Moral Sense

Hutcheson argued that human beings possess an innate "moral sense" — a perceptual faculty, analogous to the sense of beauty, that immediately perceives moral qualities in actions. This sense operates independently of reason, self-interest, or divine command. Smith adopted the sentimentalist framework — that moral judgment is a matter of feeling, not deduction — but replaced Hutcheson's special moral sense with the ordinary mechanism of sympathy.

### Doctrine on Benevolence as the Supreme Virtue

Hutcheson held that the moral quality of any action is measured by the quantity of benevolence it expresses — the "greatest happiness for the greatest numbers" principle that would later influence utilitarianism. He argued that actions motivated by self-interest have no moral worth whatsoever.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith's relationship to Hutcheson is one of profound respect combined with systematic revision. Smith adopts Hutcheson's sentimentalist starting point — that moral distinctions are felt, not deduced — but rejects the specific mechanisms Hutcheson proposed.

### Points of Agreement

Smith agrees with Hutcheson that moral judgment is a matter of sentiment, not reason alone. He also shares Hutcheson's emphasis on the social nature of morality and the importance of benevolence. Smith's vivid description of the amiable virtues — "the virtues of candid condescension and indulgent humanity" — draws directly on Hutcheson's moral vision.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith rejects Hutcheson's moral sense as an unnecessary hypothesis. Sympathy — an ordinary psychological mechanism, not a special moral faculty — can explain everything the moral sense was supposed to explain. Smith also rejects Hutcheson's reduction of all virtue to benevolence. For Smith, self-command, prudence, and justice are genuine virtues that cannot be analyzed as forms of benevolence. "The man who acts according to the dictates of prudence, without any view to the good of others, does not act viciously."

## Key Passages in TMS

> "There is another system which attempts to account for the origin of our moral sentiments from sympathy, distinct from that which endeavours to account for them from what is called the moral sense."(038-001)

> "The amiable virtues consist in that degree of sensibility which surprises by its exquisite and unexpected delicacy and tenderness. The awful and respectable virtues consist in that degree of self-command which astonishes by its amazing superiority over the most ungovernable passions of human nature."(007-006)

## Key Works

- **An Inquiry into the Original of Our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue** (1725) — Hutcheson's major work on moral philosophy, developing the moral sense theory
- **A System of Moral Philosophy** (1755, posthumous) — comprehensive treatment of ethics, published after Smith's TMS
