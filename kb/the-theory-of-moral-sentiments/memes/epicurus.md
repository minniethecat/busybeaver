# Epicurus

The philosopher whose reduction of all goods — including virtue — to pleasure represents, for Smith, the most systematic version of the self-interest theory of morality, one that Smith respects for its consistency while rejecting its account of human nature.

## Biographical Sketch

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) founded his school, the Garden, in Athens around 307 BCE. Unlike Plato's Academy or Aristotle's Lyceum, the Garden admitted women and slaves. Epicurus taught that the good life is the pleasant life — but that the highest pleasure is not sensual indulgence but the tranquillity (*ataraxia*) that comes from understanding nature and freeing oneself from fear of death and the gods.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Pleasure as the Sole Good

Epicurus held that pleasure is the only intrinsic good and pain the only intrinsic evil. All virtues — justice, courage, temperance — are valuable only instrumentally, insofar as they contribute to pleasure or reduce pain. This is not crude hedonism: Epicurus distinguished "kinetic" pleasures (active enjoyment) from "katastematic" pleasures (the stable state of painlessness), with the latter being superior.

### Doctrine on the Irrelevance of Death

"Death is nothing to us." Epicurus's famous argument: when death is present, we are not; when we are present, death is not. Therefore, rational fear of death is impossible. This doctrine was meant to free humans from the greatest source of anxiety — but Smith found it psychologically implausible.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith treats Epicurus's system as the most sophisticated version of the view that virtue reduces to prudence. He respects its logical consistency but argues it fails the test of lived moral experience.

### Points of Agreement

Smith acknowledges that prudence is a genuine virtue and that Epicurus was right to emphasize its importance. The prudent pursuit of one's own welfare is indeed a respectable quality of character.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's key objection: Epicurus cannot explain why we admire acts of self-sacrifice, integrity, or justice that bring no pleasure to the agent. The felt experience of moral approbation cannot be reduced to the perception of utility or pleasure. "It is not the love of our neighbour, it is not the love of mankind, which upon many occasions prompts us to the practice of those divine virtues."
