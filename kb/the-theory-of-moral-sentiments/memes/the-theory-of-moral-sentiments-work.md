# The Theory of Moral Sentiments

Adam Smith's systematic account of moral psychology — the work that established sympathy, rather than reason or self-interest, as the foundation of moral judgment, and introduced the impartial spectator as the standard of ethical conduct.

## Overview

*The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (TMS) was first published in 1759 and went through six editions during Smith's lifetime, with the final revised edition appearing in 1790, just months before his death. The work is organized into seven parts, progressing from the psychology of sympathy through the analysis of virtue, duty, and conscience, to a critical survey of previous moral theories.

The book opens with one of the most famous sentences in moral philosophy: "How selfish soever man may be supposed, there are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others, and render their happiness necessary to him, though he derives nothing from it except the pleasure of seeing it."(003-001)

## Author

[[adam-smith]] — Scottish moral philosopher and economist (1723–1790), Professor of Moral Philosophy at the University of Glasgow.

## Relevance to TMS

As the primary text of this wiki, TMS is its own subject. The work develops its argument through detailed psychological observation, historical examples, and engagement with the philosophical tradition. Key doctrines include:

- Sympathy as the mechanism of moral judgment
- Propriety as the standard of appropriate passion
- The impartial spectator as the internalized moral judge
- The distinction between the "amiable" virtues (sensibility, benevolence) and the "awful" virtues (self-command)
- The naturalistic account of conscience and duty
- The critique of alternative moral theories (Hutcheson, Hume, Mandeville, Stoics, Epicureans)
