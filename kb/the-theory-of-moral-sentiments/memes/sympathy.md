# Sympathy

The foundational mechanism of Smith's moral philosophy: the imaginative capacity to place ourselves in another's situation and thereby share their feelings, which makes moral judgment possible.

## Smith's Account

Smith opens *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* with an extended analysis of sympathy, establishing it as the psychological foundation upon which all moral judgment rests. Rather than treating it as a narrow emotional response to suffering, Smith broadens the concept into a general mechanism of fellow-feeling with any passion whatever.

### Core Definition

Smith defines sympathy as our capacity to enter into the feelings of others through an act of imaginative projection:

> "By the imagination we place ourselves in his situation, we conceive ourselves enduring all the same torments, we enter as it were into his body, and become in some measure the same person with him, and thence form some idea of his sensations, and even feel something which, though weaker in degree, is not altogether unlike them."(003-002)

Crucially, Smith clarifies that sympathy is not limited to fellow-feeling with suffering. The term originally meant the same as "pity" or "compassion," but Smith deliberately broadens it:

> "Sympathy, though its meaning was, perhaps, originally the same, may now, however, without much impropriety, be made use of to denote our fellow-feeling with any passion whatever."(003-005)

### Key Features

**The primacy of situation over expression.** Smith argues that sympathy arises not from observing another's expressed emotion but from imagining ourselves in the situation that produced it:

> "Sympathy, therefore, does not arise so much from the view of the passion, as from that of the situation which excites it."(003-010)

This has the remarkable consequence that we can feel sympathy for someone who feels nothing themselves — as with the insane person who "laughs and sings perhaps, and is altogether insensible of his own misery" while the spectator feels deep anguish(003-011).

**The contagion of emotion.** In some cases, sympathy operates almost instantaneously, as when "a smiling face is, to everybody that sees it, a cheerful object"(003-006). Yet Smith notes this direct contagion is not universal: an angry person's fury does not inspire sympathy but rather disgust, because we lack knowledge of their provocation(003-007).

**Sympathy creates social bonds.** The desire for mutual sympathy is itself a fundamental social drive. We experience pleasure when others share our feelings, and pain when they do not:

> "The man whose sympathy keeps time to my grief, cannot but be admitted to be the most agreeable comforter."(005-002)

## Role in the Moral System

Sympathy is the engine of Smith's entire theory. Without it, we could form no moral judgments, for we would have no access — even imaginatively — to the feelings and motives of others.

### Relation to Propriety

Sympathy provides the standard by which we judge the propriety of any action. We approve of another's passion when our own sympathetic feeling corresponds to it; we disapprove when it does not. The impartial spectator — Smith's ideal moral judge — is precisely the perspective from which our sympathetic responses most reliably track what is proper.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **The Impartial Spectator**: An internalization of the sympathetic process — we imagine how an impartial observer would react to our own conduct.(034-001)
- **Self-Command**: The virtue of moderating one's passions to the pitch at which the spectator can sympathize — sympathy sets the standard that self-command strives to meet.
- **Benevolence**: Sympathy naturally inclines us toward benevolence, as we feel pleasure in others' happiness and are pained by their distress.
- **Justice**: While sympathy is the positive foundation of moral sentiment, resentment (whose propriety we judge through sympathetic identification with the injured party) is the foundation of justice.

## Historical Context

Smith's concept of sympathy draws from and transforms several philosophical traditions. From the Stoics, he inherited the notion that moral judgment requires an impartial perspective. From his teacher Francis Hutcheson, he took the idea that moral sense is rooted in natural human feeling rather than reason. Against the rationalists (such as Samuel Clarke and William Wollaston), Smith insisted that moral distinctions are felt, not deduced. And against Bernard Mandeville's cynical reduction of all virtue to disguised self-interest, Smith offered sympathy as a genuinely other-regarding principle that nature has implanted in human beings.

Hume had already made sympathy central to his moral theory, but Smith's treatment is more systematic. Where Hume used sympathy primarily to explain how we come to share the feelings of others across time and distance, Smith transformed it into the very mechanism through which propriety is judged.

## Key Passages

> "How selfish soever man may be supposed, there are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others, and render their happiness necessary to him, though he derives nothing from it except the pleasure of seeing it. Of this kind is pity or compassion, the emotion which we feel for the misery of others, when we either see it, or are made to conceive it in a very lively manner."(003-001)

> "As we have no immediate experience of what other men feel, we can form no idea of the manner in which they are affected, but by conceiving what we ourselves should feel in the like situation."(003-002)

> "In every passion of which the mind of man is susceptible, the emotions of the bystander always correspond to what, by bringing the case home to himself, he imagines should be the sentiments of the sufferer."(003-004)
