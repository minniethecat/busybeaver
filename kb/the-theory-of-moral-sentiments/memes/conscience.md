# Conscience

The internalized voice of the impartial spectator — the faculty through which we judge our own conduct, praise or condemn ourselves, and feel the force of duty even when no actual observer is present.

## Smith's Account

Conscience is, for Smith, the culmination of the sympathetic process turned inward. What begins as our awareness of how others see us becomes internalized as an autonomous moral judge — "the man within the breast" — whose verdict can contradict public opinion and whose approval is the deepest reward of virtue.

### Core Definition

Smith's account of conscience is a developmental one. We first learn to see ourselves through others' eyes; over time, this external perspective becomes internalized:

> "We endeavour to examine our own conduct as we imagine any other fair and impartial spectator would examine it."(037-002)

The crucial step is from the actual spectator (what others really think) to the impartial spectator (what a fully informed, unbiased observer would think). Conscience is this internalized impartial spectator — it judges us not by the standards of our society but by the standards of an idealized moral perspective.

### Key Features

**Stronger than self-love.** Smith argues that conscience exerts a force more powerful than self-interest:

> "It is a stronger power, a more forcible motive, which exerts itself upon such occasions. It is reason, principle, conscience, the inhabitant of the breast, the man within, the great judge and arbiter of our conduct."(039-004)

**Independent of social opinion.** While conscience develops from social feedback, it becomes autonomous. The "man within the breast" can condemn what society applauds, and approve what society condemns. This is the foundation of moral autonomy in Smith's system.

**The source of duty.** Conscience transforms social propriety into moral duty. When we act from conscience, we act not to please others but to satisfy an internal standard. This is what distinguishes genuine virtue from mere social conformity.

**The reward of virtue.** Self-approbation — the verdict of conscience — is, for Smith, the deepest reward of moral conduct. "The love of it is the love of virtue"(016-015).

## Role in the Moral System

Conscience is the bridge between Smith's descriptive moral psychology (how we actually make moral judgments) and his normative ethics (how we should live). The impartial spectator tells us what is proper; conscience makes us care about being proper. Without conscience, moral knowledge would have no motive force.

The relationship between conscience and the impartial spectator also explains Smith's reconciliation of nature and duty. Nature gives us sympathy, which produces the impartial spectator; the impartial spectator, internalized as conscience, produces the sense of duty. Moral obligation thus has a natural origin, not a supernatural one — but it is no less binding for that.

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Impartial Spectator**: The external perspective that conscience internalizes.
- **Self-Love**: The motive that conscience must overcome.
- **Self-Command**: The capacity to follow conscience against self-interest.
- **Propriety**: The standard that conscience applies.

## Historical Context

Smith's conscience — the internalized impartial spectator — is one of the most influential ideas in moral philosophy. It anticipates Kant's categorical imperative (an internal, universalizing moral standard) and Freud's superego (an internalized social judge), while remaining distinctively Smithian: conscience judges through sympathy and feeling, not through rules and deductions.
