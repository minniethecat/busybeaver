# Thomas Hobbes

The philosopher whose bleak vision of human nature — life as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — set the problem that Smith's moral psychology was designed to solve: how can cooperative, moral social life arise from self-interested individuals?

## Biographical Sketch

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) lived through the English Civil War, an experience that shaped his political philosophy. His masterpiece *Leviathan* (1651) argued that without a sovereign power to enforce order, human life would be a "war of all against all." Morality, for Hobbes, is not natural to humans — it is an artificial creation of political authority.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on the State of Nature

Hobbes's most famous argument: in the absence of government, human beings would be in a state of perpetual conflict, driven by competition, diffidence, and glory. There would be "no place for industry... no arts, no letters, no society." Morality exists only because we have collectively agreed to submit to a sovereign who enforces rules.

### Doctrine on Self-Interest as the Sole Motive

Hobbes held that all human action is ultimately motivated by self-interest. Even apparently altruistic acts — charity, pity — are really self-interested in disguise. Pity is "grief for the calamity of another... arising from the imagination that the like calamity may befall himself."

### Points of Agreement

Smith concedes that self-interest is a powerful human motive and that Hobbes was right to take it seriously. Smith's own account gives self-love its due.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's entire moral psychology is a refutation of Hobbesian egoism. Sympathy, benevolence, the impartial spectator, conscience — these are not reducible to self-interest. "There are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others, and render their happiness necessary to him."

## Key Passages in TMS

> "How selfish soever man may be supposed, there are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others, and render their happiness necessary to him, though he derives nothing from it except the pleasure of seeing it."(003-001)
