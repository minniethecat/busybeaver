# Vanity

The desire to be the object of others' attention and admiration — a passion Smith treats with remarkable psychological depth, recognizing it as the driving force behind much of human ambition while warning of its capacity to corrupt moral judgment.

## Smith's Account

Smith devotes extensive attention to vanity, particularly in his famous account of the "toil and bustle" of economic life. For Smith, vanity is not merely a personal failing — it is a fundamental human motivation that shapes social order, economic activity, and moral psychology.

### Core Definition

Vanity is the desire for the admiration of others, rooted in the natural human need for sympathetic attention:

> "It is the vanity, not the ease or the pleasure, which interests us."(017-001)

This is a striking claim: the pursuit of wealth, status, and power is driven not by material need but by the desire to be "observed, attended to, taken notice of with sympathy, complacency, and approbation"(017-001).

### Key Features

**Social, not material.** Smith insists that vanity is fundamentally about social standing, not material comfort. The poor man who works tirelessly to better his condition does so not for better food or shelter — those are already available — but to escape the shame of being overlooked.

**The engine of industry.** Smith sees vanity as economically productive: the desire for admiration drives the ambition that fuels commerce and industry. The "deception which rouses and keeps in continual motion the industry of mankind" is, in large part, vanity.

**Morally dangerous.** Vanity distorts moral judgment by making us value the appearance of virtue over its substance. The vain person cultivates whatever qualities will attract admiration, regardless of their genuine moral worth. This is why the impartial spectator is needed — to correct for vanity's distortions.

**Distinguished from pride.** Smith distinguishes vanity (the desire for admiration from others) from pride (a high estimate of oneself). Pride may be justified; vanity, by definition, seeks something external.

## Role in the Moral System

Vanity is the dark twin of sympathy. Just as sympathy makes us responsive to others' feelings, vanity makes us dependent on others' admiration. The moral life requires navigating between these two poles — neither ignoring others' opinions (which would be impossible) nor being enslaved by them (which is vanity).

### Relation to Other Concepts

- **Sympathy**: The mechanism that makes us care about others' opinions in the first place.
- **Self-Love**: Vanity is self-love directed outward, seeking social validation.
- **Impartial Spectator**: The internal judge that corrects for vanity's distortions.
- **Ambition**: Vanity directed at worldly success.

## Key Passages

> "It is the vanity, not the ease or the pleasure, which interests us."(017-001)

> "To be observed, to be attended to, to be taken notice of with sympathy, complacency, and approbation, are all the advantages which we can propose to derive from" the pursuit of wealth.(017-001)
