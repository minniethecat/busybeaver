# Cicero

The Roman philosopher and statesman whose concept of *decorum* — the fitting and the proper — directly influenced Smith's central concept of propriety, bridging classical Stoicism and modern moral philosophy.

## Biographical Sketch

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was Rome's greatest orator and a prolific philosophical writer. His ethical works — especially *De Officiis* (On Duties) — synthesized Stoic, Academic, and Peripatetic ideas into a practical philosophy of conduct. Cicero was widely read in eighteenth-century Britain; Smith cites him directly and his influence pervades the language of propriety and decorum.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Decorum

Cicero's concept of *decorum* — what is fitting, proper, or seemly — is the classical ancestor of Smith's "propriety." For Cicero, decorum is the visible manifestation of virtue in conduct: the appropriate word, gesture, and action for each person in each situation. It is not a separate virtue but the "orderly beauty" of virtuous action itself.

### Doctrine on the Four Personae

Cicero developed an influential theory of the four *personae* (roles) that each person must harmonize: our shared rational nature, our individual character, our circumstances, and our chosen profession. Moral conduct consists in acting consistently with all four — a framework that anticipates Smith's contextual, situation-sensitive ethics.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith's relationship to Cicero is less explicit than to Hutcheson or Mandeville, but the Ciceronian vocabulary pervades *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*. When Smith speaks of "propriety," "decency," "grace," and "fitness," he is using Cicero's terms — transformed by the mechanism of sympathy.

### Points of Agreement

The core Ciceronian insight that Smith adopts: moral conduct is not rule-following but the appropriate expression of character in context. What is proper for one person in one situation may be improper for another. This contextual, role-sensitive understanding of morality is deeply Ciceronian.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith replaces Cicero's Stoic rationalism with sentimentalism. For Cicero, decorum is perceived by reason; for Smith, propriety is felt through sympathy. The standard shifts from what reason judges fitting to what the impartial spectator can enter into.
