# Marcus Aurelius

The Roman emperor whose *Meditations* — a private notebook of Stoic self-discipline — represents the practical culmination of the Stoic tradition that Smith both deeply admires and critically transforms in his account of self-command.

## Biographical Sketch

Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) was Roman emperor from 161 until his death, the last of the "Five Good Emperors." His *Meditations*, written in Greek during military campaigns, was never intended for publication — it is a personal exercise in Stoic self-discipline, applying the doctrines of Epictetus to the concrete challenges of ruling an empire.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on the Inner Citadel

Marcus develops Epictetus's dichotomy of control into the image of an "inner citadel" — a core of rational judgment that external events cannot touch. "Things cannot touch the soul... the disturbance comes only from the judgment within." This radical interiority — the conviction that our well-being depends entirely on our own judgments — is the heart of Stoic ethics.

### Doctrine on Cosmic Citizenship

Marcus emphasizes that every human being is a citizen of the cosmic city, bound by *logos* (reason) to all other rational beings. This cosmopolitanism provides the foundation for Stoic benevolence: we should act for the common good not from sentiment but from recognition of our shared rational nature.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith engages with Marcus's Stoicism primarily through his treatment of the "awful and respectable" virtues. The person of perfect self-command — who "feels the full distress of the calamity which has befallen him" and yet controls its expression — is Smith's transformation of the Stoic ideal.

### Points of Agreement

Smith deeply admires the Stoic achievement: the cultivation of an inner life that is genuinely free, dignified, and self-governing. Self-command, as Smith treats it, is fundamentally a Stoic virtue.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's key departure: the Stoic inner citadel is too isolated. Human beings are social creatures whose moral life depends on sympathy — on sharing feelings with others. The Stoic sage who feels nothing is less admirable, for Smith, than the person who feels deeply and yet governs those feelings.

## Key Passages in TMS

Smith does not name Marcus Aurelius directly, but the Stoic system he describes in Part VII is the system Marcus lived. The person of "perfect self-command" is Smith's answer to the Stoic sage.
