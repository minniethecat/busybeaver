# Meditations

The private philosophical exercises of a Roman emperor — the most influential practical statement of Stoic ethics, which Smith engages with in his treatment of self-command and the "awful and respectable" virtues.

## Overview

The *Meditations* were written by Marcus Aurelius during his military campaigns, in Greek, as a personal exercise in Stoic self-discipline. They were never intended for publication. The work applies Epictetus's doctrines to the concrete challenges of ruling an empire — how to remain just, calm, and benevolent amid the pressures of power.

## Author

[[marcus-aurelius]] — Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher (121–180 CE).

## Relevance to TMS

Smith's treatment of the "awful and respectable" virtues — self-command, dignity, the regulation of passion — is fundamentally an engagement with the Stoic ideal that the *Meditations* represents. Smith admires this ideal but transforms it: where Marcus seeks to eliminate passion, Smith seeks only to regulate it to the pitch at which the impartial spectator can sympathize.
