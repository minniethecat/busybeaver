# Alexander Pope

The English poet whose *Essay on Man* gave elegant voice to philosophical optimism — the doctrine that the apparent evils of the world are part of a benevolent divine plan — which Smith subjects to psychological critique in his analysis of utility and "the invisible hand."

## Biographical Sketch

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century. His *Essay on Man* (1733–34) versified the optimistic philosophy of Lord Bolingbroke, arguing that the apparent disorder and suffering of the world are part of a providential design too vast for human comprehension. Pope's couplets — "One truth is clear, WHATEVER IS, IS RIGHT" — became the slogans of eighteenth-century optimism.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine of the Great Chain of Being

Pope's *Essay* articulates the idea of a hierarchical cosmic order — the "Great Chain of Being" — in which every creature has its proper place and every apparent imperfection serves the perfection of the whole. Human beings cannot see the entire design, but philosophy can teach them to trust it.

### The Limits of Human Knowledge

Pope emphasizes human cognitive limitations: "Know then thyself, presume not God to scan; / The proper study of mankind is man." Philosophy should not aspire to divine knowledge but to the patient understanding of human nature — a sentiment Smith would have endorsed.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith engages with Pope's optimism indirectly, through his analysis of our tendency to "confound the useful with the good." The psychological mechanism Smith describes — our pleasure in the mere "aptness" of means to ends — explains why we find optimistic systems emotionally satisfying even when they lack empirical support. We admire the elegant design of a providential universe, just as we admire a well-made watch, regardless of whether it actually benefits anyone.

Smith's famous "invisible hand" — the unintended social benefits of self-interested action — is often read optimistically, but Smith's point is more subtle than Pope's. The social order that emerges from self-interest is real but imperfect; wealth does not bring happiness; the "deception" that drives industry is, in a sense, a noble lie.
