# Zeno of Citium

The founder of Stoicism, whose doctrines — particularly that virtue consists in living according to nature and that the passions are errors of judgment — established the philosophical framework that Smith critically engages with throughout *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*.

## Biographical Sketch

Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BCE) was born in Cyprus and studied under the Cynic Crates before founding his own school in Athens around 300 BCE. His followers met at the *Stoa Poikile* (Painted Porch), giving the school its name. Zeno's original writings are lost, but his doctrines were preserved and developed by later Stoics — particularly Chrysippus, Cicero, Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius.

## Philosophical Doctrines

### Doctrine on Living According to Nature

Zeno's foundational principle: the good life consists in living in accordance with nature — that is, in accordance with reason, which is the distinctive nature of human beings. To live virtuously is to live rationally, and to live rationally is to live in harmony with the cosmic order (*logos*) that governs the universe.

### Doctrine on the Passions as Errors

Zeno held that the passions (*pathe*) are not merely excessive emotions but cognitive errors — false judgments about what is good or bad. Grief, for example, is the false judgment that something terrible has happened. The wise person (*sophos*) is free from passion (*apatheia*) because they make no such errors.

## Smith's Engagement

Smith discusses Zeno and the Stoic system extensively in Part VII. He treats Zeno's philosophy as one of the most powerful and internally consistent ethical systems ever developed — but one that makes demands on human nature that are ultimately impossible to meet.

### Points of Agreement

Smith deeply respects the Stoic account of self-command and recognizes its psychological insight. The idea that our passions should be proportionate to their objects — and that the wise person regulates passion through reason — is a genuine philosophical achievement.

### Points of Disagreement

Smith's fundamental objection: the Stoic ideal of *apatheia* is inhuman. Human beings are creatures of passion, and a philosophy that demands the elimination of passion demands the elimination of our humanity. Smith's ethics of sympathy is more realistic — it works with human nature as it is, not as Zeno wished it to be.
