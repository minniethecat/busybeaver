# Mr. Brentford

Mr. Brentford is a Blackstable publican who knew Edward Driffield in the novelist's later years. His recollections of Driffield's pub visits — offered in the vernacular, without literary pretension — provide one of the novel's most important counter-narratives to the sanitized version of Driffield's life being constructed by Kear and Amy Driffield. [023-028]

## Role Overview

Brentford functions as a vernacular witness — the kind of source a biographer like Kear would never consult, but whose testimony is, in its way, more truthful than the official record. He remembers Driffield not as a monument but as a man who liked "to sit in the bar and talk" [023-034] — the exact details that Amy Driffield wants expunged.

## Appearances in the Novel

### The Return to Blackstable

When Ashenden returns to Blackstable in the novel's later chapters, he encounters Brentford, who recognizes him and offers recollections of the old man in the public bar. The scene is significant because it restores the Driffield that the official biography is erasing — the man who "never cared who he talked to" [023-034] and who exasperated his second wife by frequenting the pub.

### The Pub Testimony

Brentford's account is vivid and unvarnished: "He used to be very partial to dropping in here and having his glass of bitter. Mind you, I don't say he ever got tiddly, but he used to like to sit in the bar and talk. My word, he'd talk by the hour and he never cared who he talked to." [023-034] The detail that "Mrs. Driffield didn't half like his coming here" [023-034] confirms the tension between the lived Driffield and the curated one.

## Relationships

- **With [[edward-driffield]]**: A casual, egalitarian relationship — the publican and the customer, talking over beer. The very ordinariness of the connection is its point: Driffield was at home in this world in a way he never was in the literary salons. [023-034]
- **With [[amy-driffield]]**: Implicit antagonist. She dislikes his establishment and what it represents — the unmanageable, popular, beer-drinking Driffield who resists biographical sanitization. [023-034]

## Character Analysis

Brentford is a minor figure, but his role is structurally crucial. In a novel about the gap between lived experience and literary reputation, he is a witness from the lived side — someone who knew Driffield without caring about his reputation, who remembers the man rather than the monument.

His testimony is the novel's implicit rebuke to Kear's project. The biography will not include Brentford's memories, because they do not serve the narrative of dignified eminence. But the novel includes them, and in doing so, it suggests that the truth about a writer's life is always larger and messier than any official version can capture.
