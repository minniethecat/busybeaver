# Blackstable

**Blackstable** is the Kentish seaside village that serves as the novel's foundational setting — the place where the narrator [[ashenden]] spent his boyhood as the Vicar's nephew, and where he first encountered [[edward-driffield]] and [[rosie-driffield]] in their years of obscure poverty. It is three miles from [[ferne-court]], the country house Driffield would later occupy in his eminence, and the proximity of the two locations encodes the novel's central irony: the distance between obscurity and fame is a matter of miles and decades, not of essential difference.

## Geography and Character

Blackstable is introduced in the novel's early chapters as a small coastal town that "lay in a hollow and the road from the station ran up-hill through the High Street" [003-003]. It is a place where everyone knows everyone's business, and knowing it is the chief social occupation. Ashenden describes it with the affectionate condescension of someone who has escaped:

> "It was called Blackstable and my uncle was the vicar. My father had died when I was a child and my mother, who was delicate, had passed away not long after, so that I was brought up in the vicarage." [003-003]

The village's geography — its distance from London, its self-enclosed social world, its watchful propriety — makes it the perfect setting for the novel's drama of reputation and respectability. In Blackstable, the Driffields' unconventional marriage is a scandal; in London, it would barely register.

## Blackstable and the Driffields

The novel's treatment of Blackstable is inseparable from its treatment of the young Edward Driffield and his first wife. The village knows Driffield only as a social problem — a man who married a barmaid and whose occupation (novelist) seemed less a profession than an excuse for not having one. [003-005]

The Vicar, Ashenden's uncle, forbids the boy from associating with the Driffields — a prohibition founded on the village's moral code. But Ashenden, curious and unsupervised, discovers them on his own:

> "I recognized him then as Edward Driffield, the author I had seen walking with a young woman on the front." [005-008]

The memory is tender because it is unpoliced. The narrator's boyhood self, not yet trained in the village's codes of exclusion, sees the Driffields simply as interesting neighbours. The village sees them as a scandal. The tension between these perspectives is the novel's ethical core.

## Narrative Function

Blackstable serves four distinct narrative functions in *Cakes and Ale*:

1. **Origin Story** — It is the site of Ashenden's childhood and his first, formative encounters with the people who will shape his adult understanding of literature and life.

2. **Moral Laboratory** — The village's tight social code creates the conditions under which the novel's themes — reputation, respectability, hypocrisy — can be tested. What scandalises Blackstable may seem trivial to a London reader, but the *mechanism* of scandal is identical.

3. **Contrast with Ferne Court** — The proximity of Blackstable (poverty and scandal) to [[ferne-court]] (wealth and eminence) literalises the novel's argument that reputation is a matter of real estate and social positioning, not of intrinsic desert.

4. **Return Journey** — When [[alroy-kear]] and Ashenden return to Blackstable in the novel's later chapters [022-001], the village becomes the site where past and present, memory and biography, collide.

## Real-World Correspondent

Blackstable is based on **Whitstable**, a seaside town on the north Kent coast, approximately five miles from Canterbury. W. Somerset Maugham spent part of his childhood in Whitstable as the nephew of the Vicar of All Saints' Church — Henry MacDonald Maugham — in circumstances that closely parallel Ashenden's fictional biography.

| Element | Blackstable (fictional) | Whitstable (real) |
|---------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Location | North Kent coast, seaside | North Kent coast, seaside |
| Vicarage | Ashenden's uncle is the vicar | Maugham's uncle was the vicar of All Saints' |
| Driffield's house | On the outskirts | Hardy's fictional equivalent in *The Hand of Ethelberta* |
| Distance to Ferne Court | 3 miles | Broadly corresponds to the countryside near Whitstable |

Maugham renames the town, presumably, to signal the fictional nature of the events he narrates — but the geographical and biographical parallels are precise enough that early readers would have recognised the setting.

## Atmosphere

The novel captures Blackstable's social atmosphere in a few swift strokes: the Vicar's study with its smell of floor-polish and old sermons; the High Street where the Driffields walk together, attracting glances; the gap between the town's moral self-regard and its appetite for gossip. It is a world of attentive surfaces — everyone watches, everyone judges, and the judgments are remembered for decades.

This atmosphere is not merely a backdrop; it is the medium in which the novel's ethical questions are suspended. Could the Driffields have been happy in a place that did not judge them? The novel does not ask this question directly, but it is implicit in every chapter set in Blackstable.
