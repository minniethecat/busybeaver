# Alroy Kear

**Alroy Kear** is the novel's great satirical creation — a successful, industrious, and emphatically mediocre English novelist who has secured the commission to write the official biography of [[edward-driffield]]. His presence frames the novel: the first sentence announces his lunch invitation, the last chapters report his biographical labours, and everything in between is coloured by [[ashenden]]'s wary awareness of being mined for material.

Kear is not a villain in any conventional sense. He is worse — he is "sincere" in the most devastating sense Maugham can deploy. His defining trait, repeated with careful irony throughout the opening chapter, is "the most shining characteristic of Alroy Kear … his sincerity" [001-041]. The narrator unpacks this for us: sincerity, when sustained for a quarter-century, becomes indistinguishable from genuine belief. Kear has performed the role of the earnest man of letters for so long that he has become it.

## Role in the Novel

Kear functions simultaneously as antagonist, narrative catalyst, and satirical vehicle. His phone call to Ashenden in the novel's opening paragraph sets the biographical plot in motion; his commission from [[amy-driffield]] to write the "official" life of Edward Driffield creates the dramatic tension that drives the remaining chapters. [001-001] [004-001]

Structurally, Kear is Ashenden's dark mirror. Both are novelists. Both knew Driffield. Both profit from his memory. But where Ashenden is ironic, self-aware, and sceptical of his own motives, Kear is earnest, ambitious, and oblivious to the machinery of which he is a part. The contrast between their sensibilities is the novel's principal engine of satire — Ashenden sees through the literary game; Kear is the game.

## Key Appearances

### The Lunch Invitation (Ch. 1–2)

The novel opens with Kear's telephone call: "Miss Fellows, my landlady, told me that Mr. Alroy Kear wished me to ring him up" [001-001]. Over lunch at his club, Kear proposes that Ashenden share his childhood memories of Driffield. Ashenden immediately senses an ulterior motive — "at the height of the London season Alroy Kear would waste an hour on a fellow writer unless he had something to gain" [002-029].

### The Biography Commission (Ch. 4)

Days after the lunch, Ashenden receives a letter from Amy Driffield inviting him to [[ferne-court]]. It emerges that Kear has already positioned himself as the authorized biographer, having convinced the widow of his "sincerity" and qualifications: "Roy, of course, will tell you about the book." [004-001]

### The Vision of the Book (Ch. 11)

In one of the novel's most revealing passages, Kear allows himself an unguarded moment of aesthetic reverie. He pictures his biography not as a work of scholarship or insight but as a beautiful physical object: he would have liked it published "royal octavo, slim and light in the hand" [011-069]. The passage exposes what Ashenden has suspected all along: Kear's investment is in the artefact of reputation, not the subject.

### Journey to Blackstable (Ch. 22)

Kear and Ashenden travel together to Blackstable, where Kear's literary ambitions and social anxieties are laid bare in the provincial setting that produced Driffield. [022-001] The journey is a study in contrast: Ashenden revisits his past; Kear mines it.

## The Quality of "Sincerity"

Kear's "sincerity" is the novel's most sustained and devastating irony. Maugham unpacks it across the first chapter in a passage that deserves to be quoted at length for its surgical precision:

> "The most shining characteristic of Alroy Kear was his sincerity. No one can be a humbug for five-and-twenty years. Hypocrisy is the most difficult and nerve-racking vice that any man can pursue; it needs an unceasing vigilance and a rare detachment of spirit. It cannot, like adultery or gluttony, be practised at spare moments; it is a whole-time job." [001-041]

The passage's wit lies in its logical inversion. The ostensible claim — that Kear cannot be a hypocrite because hypocrisy is too demanding — actually implies the opposite: Kear *has* been a hypocrite for twenty-five years, and the evidence that this "sincerity" is real is precisely the evidence that he has exhausted himself performing it. He is not pretending to be earnest; he has *become* earnest, which is far more dangerous.

## Literary Techniques

Kear embodies several of Maugham's targets in the literary world of his era:

1. **The Career of Networking** — Kear's success is built not on talent but on cultivating the right people, at the right time, with relentless energy. The narrator notes that "it sounds a little brutal to say that when he had got all he could out of people he dropped them" [001-038] — but it is true.

2. **Performance of Depth** — Kear cultivates a "hopeless passion for a married woman of rank" [001-045] as an accessory to his public image, the way other men cultivate a moustache. It signals sensitivity without requiring commitment.

3. **The Biography as Commodity** — Maugham anticipates by decades the modern critique of literary biography as reputation-management — a genre that serves the living (the biographer, the widow, the publishers) far more than it serves the dead.

## Real-Life Model

Alroy Kear is widely recognised as a portrait of **Hugh Walpole** (1884–1941), a best-selling English novelist and fixture of London literary society. Walpole, born in New Zealand and educated at Cambridge, was a prolific author whose output included *Mr. Perrin and Mr. Traill* and the *Herries Chronicle* series. He was knighted in 1937 and was, like Kear, a gregarious bachelor with a talent for literary friendship.

Maugham's depiction caused a scandal upon *Cakes and Ale*'s publication in 1930. Walpole, who had previously considered Maugham a friend, was deeply wounded. The portrait was not libellous — it was accurate enough to be recognisable, vague enough to be deniable, and sufficiently witty to ensure it would be widely discussed. This too is a form of the careerism the novel purports to satirise.
