# Kear's Novels

Alroy Kear's novels are a significant presence in *Cakes and Ale* — not as texts to be analyzed, but as evidence of a certain kind of literary career. They are commercially successful, widely read ("Miss Fellows … had read all Roy's books" [001-010]), and — the novel implies — fundamentally mediocre.

## Role in the Novel

Kear's novels function as the counter-example to Driffield's. Where Driffield's work is described in contradictory superlatives ("very bad English" [011-001] yet "instinct with beauty" [002-083]), Kear's is never described at all — its content, style, and themes are entirely absent from the narrative. The omission is the judgment: Kear's novels don't matter enough to be discussed. They are the product of industry, common sense, and "the efficient combination of means and ends" [001-046] — everything, in short, except genius.

## Selected Quotations

> "Miss Fellows was a great novel reader. I was sure that she had read all Roy's books." [001-010]
