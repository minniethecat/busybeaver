# Quentin Forde

**Quentin Forde** is a stocky, well-to-do gentleman who forms part of the intimate circle of regular visitors to the [[edward-driffield|Driffields']] Saturday afternoon gatherings at their home in Limpus Road. Along with [[harry-retford]] and [[lionel-hillier]], he is among "the most faithful" of the weekly regulars who "arrived early and stayed late" [014-018].

## Appearance and Demeanour

Maugham's physical description of Forde is precise and characteristically barbed: "a stocky little man with a fine head of the type that was afterward for a time much admired in the moving pictures, a straight nose and handsome eyes, neatly cropped gray hair, and a black moustache" [014-019]. The narrator adds archly that "if he had been four or five inches taller he would have been the perfect type of the otium cum dignitate of the Cheeryble Brothers" [014-019]. The qualification — that missing height — is pure Maugham: a detail that both builds and undercuts the portrait in the same breath.

Forde's gentlemanly presentation is a recurring motif. When he reacts with distaste to the boorish [[jack-kuyper]], the narrator notes that "with his white hair and long sallow face he looked incredibly gentlemanly" [017-010]. His physical type, the narrator observes, was one "much admired in the moving pictures" — an awareness that Forde's handsomeness belongs to a recognisable period style, slightly dated even as it is described.

## Role in the Driffield Circle

Forde's function in the novel is primarily as one of [[rosie-driffield|Rosie's]] admirers — and, the narrative strongly implies, lovers. The narrator [[ashenden]] observes that Rosie "liked luxury and Quentin Forde was well-to-do. He would fetch her in a cab and take her to dine at Kettner's or the Savoy, and she would put on her grandest clothes for him" [015-001].

The narrator's jealousy extends to Forde as it does to the other men around Rosie. He "wondered about Quentin Forde" [015-001] — whether Forde was sleeping with her. The question becomes a refrain: the young Ashenden, "only just over one and twenty" [017-006], regards Forde and the others as "old" and reassures himself that Rosie's relationships with them are only friendship, but this self-deception collapses by Chapter XVII when he is forced to acknowledge "that when she went out to dinner with Quentin Forde and Harry Retford and Lionel Hillier she went to bed with them just as she came to bed with me" [017-008].

## Relationship with Ashenden

The narrator and Forde share a wary, situational alliance. When the Dutch diamond merchant [[jack-kuyper]] arrives and monopolises Rosie's attention, Forde and Ashenden discover a mutual dislike for the interloper: "I found that Quentin Forde liked him as little as I; we almost became cordial with one another" [017-010]. Forde's assessment of Kuyper is delivered with patrician scorn: "'Mercifully he's not staying long.' Quentin Forde pursed his lips and raised his black eyebrows ... 'Women are always the same; they adore a bounder'" [017-010].

This fragile camaraderie is the closest Forde and Ashenden come to genuine friendship, and it is entirely opportunistic — born of shared jealousy rather than genuine affinity. The narrator had previously noted that Forde "looked upon me as a dull and insignificant young man (which of course I was) and though he had always been civil he had never taken any notice of me" [017-006].

## Thematic Significance

Forde, along with Retford and Hillier, represents the unrespectable reality of Rosie's life that [[amy-driffield]] and [[alroy-kear]] must erase for the official biography. The "skeleton in the cupboard" of the novel's subtitle is not merely Rosie's sexual past but the entire world of men like Forde — wealthy gentlemen who took barmaids to the Savoy, actors who arranged photographic sittings, painters who claimed to have "made" a woman's beauty. These men cannot appear in *[[the-life-and-letters|The Life and Letters of Edward Driffield]]* because they would shatter the carefully constructed image of the Grand Old Man's respectable first marriage.

Forde's very decency — his gentlemanliness, his wealth, his conventional good looks — makes him in some ways the most damning of Rosie's lovers. He is not a bounder like Kuyper or a raffish actor like Retford; he is a gentleman, and his presence in Rosie's bed demonstrates that her promiscuity was not confined to low company but ranged freely across class lines. For the literary establishment that Kear represents, this is more threatening than vulgarity would be.
