# Mrs. Greencourt

Mrs. Greencourt is a minor figure in Blackstable society whose tea party in Chapter VIII provides the setting for one of the novel's most important set-pieces: the drawing-room discussion in which Edward Driffield's literary reputation is first measured against the class prejudices of the Blackstable gentry. Her "adequate income" from the family crockery business marks her as occupying the precarious upper edge of the commercial class — respectable but not "quite quite." [007-007]

## 角色概述

Mrs. Greencourt functions primarily as a stage manager. Her social ambition — hosting a cousin who is the wife of an Oxford don — creates the conditions for the New Woman Mrs. Encombe to visit Blackstable and for the local gentry to gather and perform their attitudes toward literature, class, and morality. Her tea party is the novel's most concentrated depiction of provincial intellectual life, and Maugham uses it to anatomize the social forces that shaped Driffield's early reception.

The narrator's aside about Mrs. Greencourt's family background — her father made china, her grandfather was a factory hand — establishes the narrow social bandwidth of Blackstable's class distinctions. She is simultaneously elevated enough to host an Oxford don's wife and low enough that "of course she isn't quite quite." [007-007]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Mrs. Greencourt is introduced with the kind of parenthetical aside that is Maugham's signature narrative method — a social annotation that deflates pretension while acknowledging its reality:

> One day we went to tea at Mrs. Greencourt's, who had staying with her a cousin, the wife of an Oxford don, and we had been told that she was very cultivated. [008-063]

The "we" encompasses the collective Blackstable consciousness — the vicar, his wife, the young Ashenden — all participants in the ritual of the afternoon call.

### 关键场景

**The tea party and its social context** — Earlier, the narrator establishes Mrs. Greencourt's financial and social position with characteristic precision:

> (It is not unworthy of mention crockery — the source of Mrs. Greencourt's adequate income — in their place.) [007-007]

The parenthesis is Maugham's device for delivering the judgment that the Blackstable community makes but cannot state openly: Mrs. Greencourt's money comes from trade, and trade disqualifies one from being "quite quite."

**The dismissal of the commercial taint** — The narrator's candid social analysis:

> "Mrs. Greencourt is very funny; you never know what she'll do next. But of course she isn't quite quite." We could hardly forget that her father made china and that her grandfather had been a factory hand. [008-063]

In this single sentence, Maugham captures the entire mechanism of English class distinction: the patronizing tolerance ("very funny"), the ultimate disqualification ("not quite quite"), and the genealogical memory that enforces it (father's trade, grandfather's labor).

**Absence during the key conversation** — Notably, Mrs. Greencourt recedes from the narrative once the literary discussion begins. It is Mrs. Encombe who debates the vicar, Mrs. Hayforth who defends Ouida, and Mr. Galloway who introduces Driffield's name. Mrs. Greencourt, having provided the venue, becomes invisible — a hostess whose social ambitions have been fulfilled but whose own voice is absent from the record.

**The Mentone excursion** — Mrs. Greencourt makes one further appearance, mentioned in passing during a later conversation:

> "Nothing very much. Mrs. Greencourt went down to Mentone for six weeks and she's come back looking years younger." [010-008]

This brief reference, placed in a chapter set years later, confirms her continued presence in Blackstable as a familiar fixture — someone whose movements are noted and discussed.

## 人物关系

- **与 Mrs. Encombe ([[mrs-encombe]])**：Her cousin, the wife of an Oxford don. Mrs. Greencourt's ability to host Mrs. Encombe is her primary claim to cultural distinction. [008-063]
- **与 the Blackstable gentry**：She occupies the ambiguous social position of the commercially comfortable — wealthy enough to host, but forever marked by the source of her income. The vicar, the major, and the curate all attend her tea but maintain the mental reservation that "she isn't quite quite." [007-007] [008-063]
- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：She hosts the tea party at which Driffield's work is first seriously discussed, though she herself offers no opinion. Her drawing room is the arena for the class-based dismissal of Driffield by the major and the curate. [008-074]

## 人物分析

Mrs. Greencourt is a study in the social ambiguity of the commercially enriched in Edwardian England. Her income is "adequate" — a word that Maugham uses with the precision of a social realist — and her china-manufacturing background places her above the poor but below the gentry. She is both insider and outsider: hostess to the vicar and the major, but never their equal.

Her silence during the literary discussion is the most telling detail. She has brought culture to Blackstable (in the person of Mrs. Encombe), but she does not participate in it. Her role is facilitation, not engagement — she provides the teacups and the chairs, and the conversation happens around her. In this, she mirrors the novel's broader observation about the English class system: those who enable culture are not always those who create or appreciate it.

## 可能的现实原型

Mrs. Greencourt is likely a composite of several women in Maugham's Whitstable childhood — members of the commercial class who had risen to a level of comfort that allowed them to mix with the professional gentry while remaining marked by the source of their wealth. The family china business is a specific enough detail to suggest a real model.
