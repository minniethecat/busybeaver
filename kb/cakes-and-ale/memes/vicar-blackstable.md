# The Vicar of Blackstable

The vicar of Blackstable is Ashenden's maternal uncle and guardian, a lazy, narrow-minded, but not unkindly clergyman who embodies the conventional Victorian respectability against which Driffield's bohemian world is implicitly measured. He serves as the moral and social backdrop to Ashenden's childhood, a figure whose values the narrator must learn to navigate around rather than openly defy. [003-003]

## 角色概述

The vicar represents the established social order of late-Victorian rural England: a man who "never read anything but the Times and the Guardian," [003-036] who regards Driffield's novels as "trashy" and "rubbish," [003-036] [003-039] and who instinctively protects his nephew from any contact with people he considers beneath their station.

Yet Maugham's portrait is more amused than hostile. The vicar's laziness — keeping Galloway as curate primarily because "being extremely lazy, he was glad to have someone to take so much work off his shoulders" [003-011] — and his wife's genteel poverty and snobbery ("she refused to do so because he was in trade") are treated as absurdities of a fading world rather than moral failings. [003-003]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

The vicar is introduced in Chapter III, which opens with Ashenden's return to Blackstable for the summer holidays:

> I lived with an uncle and aunt on the outskirts of a little Kentish town by the sea. It was called Blackstable and my uncle was the vicar. [003-003]

The chapter immediately establishes his household's character: the impoverished German aunt who "had not, though married for more than thirty years to a modest parson with very little income beyond his stipend, forgotten that she was hochwohlgeboren," and the vicar's reflexive social conservatism. [003-003]

### 关键场景

**The tea-table discussion of Driffield** — Over tea at the vicarage, the vicar, his wife, and Galloway discuss the Driffields' marriage:

> "He married a Blackstable girl," said Mr. Galloway. "Is he coming to church?" asked my uncle. [003-017] [003-023]

The vicar's dismissive judgments accumulate: "I'd heard he was rather a harum-scarum," [003-027] "Jack of all trades and master of none," [003-030] "That won't last long" (of the marriage). [003-032] When Galloway mentions Driffield's writing, the vicar responds:

> "I wouldn't waste my time on rubbish in your place," said my uncle, who never read anything but the Times and the Guardian. [003-036]

He forbids Ashenden from reading Driffield's novels: "I should very much object to your reading trashy novels. During your holidays the best thing you can do is to keep out in the open air." [003-039]

**The Ferne Court luncheon** — In Chapter IV, the vicar and his wife appear as guests at Amy Driffield's luncheon for the visiting party. Seated "next to Lord Scallion" at the foot of the table, [004-033] he is absorbed into the county social apparatus, his earlier condescension toward Driffield now irrelevant before the novelist's celebrity. His silent presence at this literary-social ritual registers the transformation of Driffield's standing without the narrator needing to comment on it.

**The bicycle episode** — In Chapter V, the vicar appears as an offstage moral authority whose disapproval Ashenden must evade. After learning to ride with the Driffields' help, Ashenden conceals the encounter: "I think I boasted a good deal at dinner, but I did not say that I had met the Driffields." [005-031]

**Driffield's posthumous fame** — In a retrospective passage, the narrator notes the irony that the vicar's successor "twice removed" campaigned for Driffield's burial in Blackstable churchyard after Westminster Abbey refused him — a posthumous vindication the vicar himself could never have imagined. [003-041]

## 人物关系

- **与 [[ashenden]]**：The vicar is Ashenden's guardian during the summer holidays, a figure of mild authority whom Ashenden learns to work around rather than confront. The boy conceals his Driffield friendship to avoid the vicar's disapproval. [005-031]
- **与 [[galloway]]**：The vicar tolerates Galloway for his energy despite finding him "rather queer," being himself "extremely lazy." [003-011] Galloway, in turn, conceals his own Driffield friendship from the vicar. [009-001]
- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：The vicar embodies everything Driffield's art challenges: social snobbery, literary narrowness, and the assumption that a bailiff's son belongs in chapel rather than church. His dismissal of Driffield's novels as "rubbish" without having read them is one of the novel's sharpest satirical details. [003-036]
- **与 the Vicar's Wife**：Ashenden's German aunt, "a simple old lady, of a meek and Christian disposition" who maintains her aristocratic pretensions through the marquetry writing desk and engraved tumblers. [003-003]

## 人物分析

The vicar's function is to establish the social and moral framework that the novel's central themes will dismantle. His opposition between fresh air and fiction, between the respectable and the "harum-scarum," maps onto the larger tension between the public reputation Driffield acquires and the private reality Ashenden remembers.

The narrator's retrospective tone — "When I consider the greatness that Edward Driffield afterward achieved I cannot but smile as I remember the fashion in which he was discussed at my uncle's table" [003-041] — signals both affection for and distance from the vicar's world. The smile is the point: Maugham invites us to find the vicar absurd but not monstrous. He is a product of his time and class, and Ashenden's quiet evasions of his authority — learning to ride a bicycle with the Driffields, reading novels on the sly — are presented as the natural, even healthy, response of a curious boy to a world of rules he instinctively knows to be too small.

## 可能的现实原型

The vicar is widely considered to be based on Maugham's own uncle, Henry MacDonald Maugham, who was the vicar of Whitstable (the real-life model for Blackstable) and with whom the young Willie Maugham lived after his mother's death. The lazy, conservative vicar who "never read anything but the Times and the Guardian" closely matches descriptions of Henry Maugham from contemporaneous accounts.
