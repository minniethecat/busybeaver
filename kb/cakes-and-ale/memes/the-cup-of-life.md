# The Cup of Life

**_The Cup of Life_** is the novel by [[edward-driffield]] that the narrator [[ashenden]] considers "the most interesting" of all his works [018-002]. It provoked a storm of controversy upon publication — based on an actual child's death — and became the work by which Driffield's literary seriousness was measured by his contemporaries.

## Overview

Published during Driffield's middle period, _The Cup of Life_ was the book that divided opinion in literary London. To admire it "became a mark of aesthetic acumen: to be shocked by it was to confess yourself a philistine" [018-006]. [[mrs-barton-trafford]], Driffield's literary patroness, "had no hesitation in saying that it was a masterpiece" [018-006].

Ashenden, looking back after Driffield's death, finds the novel striking for its "cold ruthlessness that in all the sentimentality of English fiction strikes an original note. It is refreshing and astringent. It tastes of tart apples" [018-002]. The metaphor is precise: the novel is sharp, unsentimental, discomfiting — qualities that English fiction, in the narrator's view, rarely achieves.

## The Controversy

The storm around _The Cup of Life_ centered on its depiction of a child's death — an episode, the reader eventually learns, that was drawn from Driffield's own life with [[rosie-driffield]]. Their daughter died in childhood, and Driffield transmuted the experience into fiction with a severity that outraged readers accustomed to more sentimental treatments of death.

> "It was _The Cup of Life_ that had raised such a storm of protest; and it was the child's death and the episode that followed it that had especially brought down on Driffield's head such virulent abuse." [026-043]

Ashenden acknowledges the power of the writing: "It was harrowing. There was nothing sentimental in it; it did not excite the reader's tears, but his anger rather that such cruel suffering should be inflicted on a little child. You felt that God at the Judgment Day would have to account for such things as this" [026-043].

## Critical Reception

The novel's reception divided along lines that the narrator finds "strange (and instructive)" to revisit. There is "not a word that could bring a blush to the cheek of the most guileless, not an episode that could cause the most prudish to protest" [018-006] — yet the book provoked outrage. The gap between what the novel actually contains and what readers *felt* it contained is part of Maugham's larger argument about literary reputation: what is remembered about a book is often not what is in it.

## Autobiographical Basis

The novel's relationship to Driffield's biography is central to the plot of _Cakes and Ale_. [[alroy-kear]], writing his biography of Driffield, will need to decide how to handle the autobiographical dimension — especially since the real events involve Rosie, whom the biography is likely to minimize. The novel-within-the-novel becomes a test case for the ethics of literary biography.
