# Mrs. Gann

Mrs. Gann is the mother of [[rosie-driffield|Rosie Driffield (née Gann)]], an offstage presence in *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* who appears only indirectly — through her daughter's visits, through the small sums of money that arrived from America, and through the silence that followed her death.

## Role in the Novel

### Haversham Connections

Mrs. Gann lived in or near [[haversham]], the town where Rosie worked at the Prince of Wales's Feathers after leaving [[blackstable]]. The hotel where Rosie had once worked became a meeting place:

> "'No, not to Blackstable,' Rosie smiled. 'I don't know that I'd care to go there just yet. To Haversham. Mother comes over to meet me. I stay at the hotel where I used to work.'" [008-036]

The passage reveals the enduring connection between mother and daughter. Even after marriage to Driffield, Rosie returns to Haversham to see her mother — a domestic loyalty that contrasts with the social ambition that drives characters like [[alroy-kear]].

### The Money From America

After Rosie's elopement with [[lord-george-kemp]] and their emigration to America, the novel records a spare, affecting detail:

> "Small sums reached her mother occasionally, ten or twenty pounds, and they came in a registered letter with a New York postmark; but no address was given, no message enclosed, and they were presumed to come from Rosie only because no one else could possibly send Mrs. Gann money." [008-060]

The registered letters with no return address and no message are among the novel's most economical gestures. They testify to Rosie's continued care for her mother while also suggesting the finality of her departure — she has cut all ties with England except this anonymous, material thread.

### The Silence

The novel records Mrs. Gann's death in a single sentence:

> "Then in the fullness of years Rosie's mother died, and it may be supposed that in some way the news reached her, for the letters ceased to come." [008-060]

The cessation of the letters — the only sign that Rosie knew of her mother's death — is the novel's last word on Mrs. Gann. The "fullness of years" is a gentle touch, suggesting a life that ran its natural course, unlike the disrupted and scandal-marked life of her daughter.

## Character Function

Mrs. Gann is one of the novel's "ordinary" figures — the working people whose lives provide the ground against which the literary and aristocratic characters perform their ambitions. Her significance is thematic rather than dramatic:

### The World Rosie Came From

Mrs. Gann represents the Haversham working-class world that produced Rosie — a world of hotels and public houses, of labor and respectability at the humblest level. The biographer [[alroy-kear]] will find no use for Mrs. Gann in [[the-life-and-letters|*The Life and Letters*]]; she belongs to the "cakes and ale" that the official record erases.

### Maternal Loyalty

The twenty-pound registered letters are the novel's image of love across distance and silence. Rosie, who in Blackstable was judged incapable of "sticking to anybody," maintained her connection to her mother for decades — a fact that complicates the moral judgments the novel invites.
