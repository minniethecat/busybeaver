# The Bicycle Lessons

The bicycle lessons are the origin story of the friendship between young [[ashenden|William Ashenden]] and [[edward-driffield]], the celebrated novelist then living in obscure retirement in [[blackstable]]. It is through the simple act of Driffield teaching the boy to ride a bicycle that one of the novel's most significant relationships begins — a relationship that will, decades later, place Ashenden in the unique position of being able to challenge the official biography of a literary giant.

## The Event

### Background

Ashenden, living with his uncle the vicar in Blackstable, had persuaded his uncle to buy him a bicycle at the start of the summer holidays. The safety bicycle was still a novelty in remote Kent: "when you saw someone speeding along on solid tires you turned round and looked till he was out of sight." [005-001]

The boy's determination to master the machine was driven partly by the desire not to be humiliated before his schoolmates, who claimed to have learned in half an hour. After failing to teach himself — "I barked my shins against the pedals and got very hot and bothered" — Ashenden was on the point of despair when the Driffields appeared on the deserted coast road. [005-002]

### The Meeting

The first encounter between the two is described with comic self-deprecation. Ashenden, attempting to hide his failure, wheeled his bicycle to the side of the road and sat on a stile "looking out to sea in a nonchalant way." His pretense was shattered when Mrs. Driffield swerved and crashed into him: [005-002]

> "Oh, I'm sorry," she said. "I knew I should fall off the moment I saw you." [005-001]

Thus began the acquaintance that would shape the novel's central narrative. Driffield, recognizing the boy's predicament, offered to help, and "that was indeed how I first made his acquaintance." [005-001]

### The Lessons

Driffield's teaching method was patient and good-humoured. He held the bicycle steady while Ashenden mounted, ran alongside as the boy wobbled forward, and released him at the crucial moment. The lessons occupied several afternoons that summer, establishing a pattern of regular contact between the vicarage boy and the disreputable writer.

The bicycle rides expanded beyond the initial lessons. The Driffields and Ashenden began taking longer excursions along the Kentish lanes, through the marshes and past the sea-wall. On one occasion, Driffield took Ashenden's bicycle to demonstrate a maneuver, leaving the boy "reluctant but unable to refuse." [005-022]

### The Aftermath

The bicycle connection created the pathway for Ashenden's deeper involvement with the Driffields — sailing trips, watercolour excursions, and eventually visits to their cottage. The relationship that began with handlebars and pedals would provide Ashenden with the intimate knowledge of Driffield's first marriage that, decades later, Alroy Kear's biography would seek to erase or sanitize.

Years afterward, when Ashenden visited the elderly Driffield at [[ferne-court|Ferne Court]], the great man — now frail, toothless, supervised by his protective second wife — summarized their entire shared history in three words:

> "Shy," he replied. "I taught him to ride a bicycle." [004-059]

The line is a wink across decades, a private acknowledgment that this famous writer and this successful author both remember the boy on the stile.

## Narrative Significance

The bicycle lessons serve multiple narrative functions. They establish the authentic, unpretentious side of Driffield's character — the man who would spend an afternoon teaching a stranger's child to ride — in contrast to the Grand Old Man of Letters constructed by the literary establishment. They also position Ashenden as an eyewitness to the Driffields' early married life, lending authority to his later recollections.

The bicycle is also a structural motif. It recurs as a symbol of the "cakes and ale" period of Driffield's life: the simple, physical, unliterary pleasures that the biography project will systematically exclude. When the aged Driffield winks at Ashenden across the Ferne Court drawing room and utters his three-word summary, the bicycle becomes the novel's most economical emblem of everything the official record cannot capture.
