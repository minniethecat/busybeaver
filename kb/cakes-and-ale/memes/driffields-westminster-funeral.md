# Driffield's Westminster Abbey Funeral

The proposal to bury [[edward-driffield|Edward Driffield]] in Westminster Abbey is not a narrated scene but a reported fact — and its power lies precisely in this offhand quality. It is mentioned in a single, devastating sentence that encapsulates the novel's argument about literary reputation:

> "I consider the greatness that Edward Driffield afterward achieved I cannot but think that the subaltern I saw talking with the curate was a man of no consequence, and I cannot but wonder that from admirers to have him buried in Westminster Abbey the present incumbent at Blackstable should look upon as a feather in the cap of the parish." [003-041]

The sentence's structure performs the argument it makes. It moves from Ashenden's boyhood memory ("the subaltern I saw talking with the curate") to the machinery of canonisation ("admirers to have him buried in Westminster Abbey") to the provincial self-interest that appropriates the honour ("the present incumbent at Blackstable … a feather in the cap of the parish"). In one sentence, the arc of Driffield's posthumous reputation is traced — and the machinery that produced it is exposed.

## The Event

### Background: The Right to Westminster Abbey

Burial in Westminster Abbey is the highest posthumous honour available to an English writer. The Poets' Corner houses the remains or memorials of Chaucer, Spenser, Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden, and other canonical figures. To be interred there is to be declared, officially and permanently, part of the national literary heritage.

By the time of Driffield's death, his admirers had convinced themselves — and, it seems, the Dean — that Driffield deserved this honour. The novel implies, without saying directly, that the campaign was led by Kear and supported by the literary establishment that Driffield's patroness, [[mrs-barton-trafford]], had cultivated over decades.

### The Satire

Maugham's treatment of the funeral is characteristically indirect. He does not describe the service. He does not quote the eulogies. He reports the fact of the burial and lets the irony accumulate around it.

The burial in Westminster Abbey is the terminal point of a career that began in the disreputable obscurity of Blackstable. The man who married a barmaid, whose "English was very bad" [011-001], who was for decades "held in such small esteem" [011-001], has become — through the patient work of patrons, biographers, and the sheer longevity that Ashenden defines as "genius" [011-008] — a national treasure.

### The Provincial Mirror

The novel's sharpest cut is reserved for Blackstable's appropriation of the honour. The "present incumbent at Blackstable" — Ashenden's uncle's successor as vicar — regards the Westminster Abbey burial as "a feather in the cap of the parish" [003-041]. The village that shunned Driffield when he was alive now claims him as its own.

This is Maugham's final verdict on the machinery of reputation: it does not merely elevate the obscure to eminence; it rewrites the history of that obscurity as a prelude to greatness. Blackstable's vicar, who would have forbidden his nephew from associating with the Driffields, now basks in the reflected glory of the man he would have shunned.

## Narrative Significance

The Westminster Abbey burial serves as the novel's **consummating irony**. It is the physical enactment of everything the novel has argued about literary reputation: that it is socially constructed, that it serves the interests of the living more than the dead, and that it systematically erases the truth of the life it purports to honour.

Driffield in Westminster Abbey is the "cakes and ale" paradox made stone: the man whose vitality was inseparable from the barmaid wife who cuckolded him now lies among the canonised, his messy life sanitised into a "funeral oration" [011-004] that his biographer will polish into a slim, beautiful volume.

## Real-World Context

Maugham's satire gains force from its proximity to real events. Thomas Hardy, the principal model for Driffield, died in January 1928. A campaign for his burial in Westminster Abbey succeeded — but with a macabre compromise: his heart was buried in Stinsford churchyard, near his first wife Emma, while his ashes were interred in the Abbey. The bifurcated burial literalised the novel's thesis: the public man (Abbey) and the private man (Stinsford) could not occupy the same grave.

*Cakes and Ale* was published in 1930, two years after Hardy's death. Its contemporary readers would have recognised the reference and appreciated the satire's audacity.
