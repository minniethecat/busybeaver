# Miss Wolfe

Miss Wolfe is a background figure in the social history of Blackstable — the deceased landowner of Ferne Court, whose bailiff was Edward Driffield's father. Though she never appears in the narrative and is described only as "old Miss Wolfe," her estate serves as the gravitational center of Driffield's class origins, his lifelong attachment to Ferne Court, and the social tensions that surround his literary career. [003-016]

## 角色概述

Miss Wolfe exists in the novel only as a name and a property — she is the absent landlord whose estate arrangement defines the class position of the Driffield family. Her bailiff, Ted Driffield's father, was a working man on her land, and this connection between the Driffields and Ferne Court becomes the defining fact of Edward Driffield's social identity in Blackstable. Every mention of the Driffields' "origins" circles back to the phrase "old Miss Wolfe's bailiff." [003-016]

In the novel's social taxonomy, Miss Wolfe represents the traditional gentry order — landowners whose wealth and status are inherited and unquestioned, unlike the precarious class mobility of the Driffields. Her death before the novel's events means she exists as a ghostly referent, a fixed point in the class landscape against which Driffield's rise can be measured. [024-043]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Miss Wolfe first enters the narrative not as a character but as a genealogical fact — the explanation of who Driffield is and where he comes from. When the young Ashenden asks his uncle about the stranger on the bicycle, the vicar responds with the essential coordinates of Driffield's social position:

> "He was born in the parish," said my uncle. "His father was old Miss Wolfe's bailiff at Ferne Court. But they were chapel people." [003-016]

The phrase "chapel people" immediately follows the bailiff identification, linking class position with religious nonconformity — a double marker of social distance in the Church of England world of Blackstable.

### 关键场景

**Driffield's self-identification** — When Ted Driffield approaches the vicar on the street, he introduces himself not by his own achievements but through the same class formula: "My old governor was Miss Wolfe's bailiff." [006-002] This self-presentation suggests that in Blackstable, a man's social identity is inseparable from his father's employment and the landowner who employed him:

> "Good afternoon, Vicar," he said. "I wonder if you remember me. I used to sing in the choir when I was a boy. Ted Driffield. My old governor was Miss Wolfe's bailiff." [006-002]

**Ashenden's class consciousness** — The young narrator internalizes the bailiff connection as a mark of social inferiority. When he associates with Driffield, he frames it as condescension:

> I was under the impression that it was condescension on my part to consort with the son of Miss Wolfe's bailiff, and he what my uncle called a penny-a-liner. [007-001]

**The major's dismissal** — In the Blackstable drawing-room conversation about Driffield's literary reputation, the major deploys the same formula as a permanent disqualification:

> "We're not very proud of him," said the major. "He's the son of old Miss Wolfe's bailiff and he married a barmaid." [008-074]

**Ferne Court's return** — In the novel's final movement, Roy Kear reveals that Driffield's purchase of Ferne Court was an act of symbolic reclamation — the bailiff's son acquiring the manor that defined his social origins:

> "You see, it belonged to a certain Miss Wolfe, whose bailiff his father was, and he said that when he was a little boy his one idea was to own it himself and now he'd got it he was going to keep it." [024-043]

This late revelation transforms Miss Wolfe from a mere genealogical footnote into a key figure in Driffield's psychological biography — the absent authority whose property becomes the object of his lifelong aspiration.

## 人物关系

- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：Her bailiff was Driffield's father, and her estate Ferne Court became Driffield's eventual home. The "old Miss Wolfe's bailiff" formula is the novel's shorthand for Driffield's humble origins. [003-016] [024-043]
- **与 Driffield's father**：Employed Driffield's father as bailiff at Ferne Court. This employment relationship is the central biographical fact passed down about her. [003-016]
- **与 Blackstable society**：Her status as a landowner places her within the gentry class whose judgments define Driffield's social position throughout his career. The vicar, the major, and Mrs. Encombe all invoke her name as a social reference point. [008-074]

## 人物分析

Miss Wolfe functions as an absent signifier of class — her name is invoked not to describe a person but to fix a social position. She is never described physically, never quoted, never given a first name or a personality. She exists purely as property and as the measure of other people's status. This narrative treatment is itself thematically significant: in a novel about the relationship between art and social class, the landowner who never appears is the silent guarantor of the class system.

The recurrence of "old Miss Wolfe's bailiff" as a formula — repeated by the vicar, by Driffield himself, by the major, by Roy Kear — demonstrates how thoroughly Driffield's identity is socially determined. No matter what he writes or achieves, in Blackstable he remains the bailiff's son. His purchase of Ferne Court [024-043] is therefore the novel's most concentrated act of class reversal: the estate that defined his lowly origins becomes his possession.

## 可能的现实原型

No specific prototype for Miss Wolfe has been identified. She represents the small-scale English gentry — unmarried women of property who were common figures in the rural social landscape. Ferne Court's basis in Maugham's own childhood geography (Whitstable, Kent) suggests Miss Wolfe may be a composite of local landowners known to the author's family.
