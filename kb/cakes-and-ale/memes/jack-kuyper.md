# Jack Kuyper

**Jack Kuyper** is a Dutch Jewish diamond merchant from Amsterdam whose brief appearance in the Driffield circle during a London business trip precipitates one of the novel's most emotionally charged sequences. His affair with [[rosie-driffield|Rosie Driffield]] and his extravagant gift of a sable stole force the young narrator [[ashenden]] to confront the full scope of Rosie's sexual freedom — a realisation that shatters his romantic illusions.

## Arrival in Limpus Road

Kuyper enters the narrative abruptly: "toward the end of January, someone new appeared at Limpus Road. This was a Dutch Jew named Jack Kuyper, a diamond merchant from Amsterdam, who was spending a few weeks in London on business" [017-009]. The narrator admits he does not know how Kuyper came to know the [[edward-driffield|Driffields]] or "whether it was esteem for the author that brought him to the house" [017-009] — but adds, with characteristic Maugham dryness, "it was certainly not that which caused him to come again" [017-009].

## The Affair

The rhythm of Ashenden's life with Rosie is immediately disrupted. "For the next two or three weeks I saw next to nothing of Rosie. Jack Kuyper took her out night after night, to this smart restaurant and that, to one play after another. I was vexed and hurt" [017-013].

Kuyper's effect on the narrator is more visceral than that of the other admirers. [[quentin-forde]], [[harry-retford]], and [[lionel-hillier]] are familiar presences whose relationships with Rosie the narrator has learned to rationalise. Kuyper is an outsider — not merely foreign and Jewish but newly arrived, without history in the circle, and instantly in possession of Rosie's time and attention. The narrator's reaction — "vexed and hurt" — is childlike in its wounded simplicity, a register Maugham uses to convey the rawness of Ashenden's youthful jealousy.

## The Sable Stole

The emotional climax of Kuyper's episode is the gift of a sable stole. Rosie shows it to Ashenden after Kuyper has returned to Amsterdam:

"'Jack Kuyper gave it to me. We went and bought it yesterday just before he went away.' She stroked the smooth fur; she was as happy with it as a child with a toy. 'How much d'you think it cost?'" [017-034].

The narrator's reaction is immediate and devastating. He cannot believe "that anyone would make as expensive a present as that from pure friendship; what did it mean but that Jack Kuyper had been sleeping with Rosie, night after night, all the time he was in London, and now when he went away was paying her?" [017-034].

The sable stole crystallises everything the narrator finds intolerable. It is not merely evidence of sexual betrayal — he has by this point accepted Rosie's infidelity with the other admirers — but evidence of something he finds more degrading: the transaction. The gift reduces Rosie, in his eyes, from a woman freely giving her affection to a woman being paid. "How could she accept it? Didn't she see how it degraded her? Didn't she see how frightfully vulgar it was of him to give her a thing that cost so much?" [017-034].

## Class and Racial Anxieties

The narrator's response to Kuyper is coloured by anxieties the novel registers without endorsing. Kuyper is repeatedly identified as "a Dutch Jew" [017-009] — a marker of outsider status in the London literary world of the 1890s. The narrator's horror is in part class horror: the diamond merchant's wealth is new, commercial, and foreign, in contrast to Forde's gentlemanly independent means. And it is in part sexual horror: Kuyper is the only one of Rosie's admirers who is unambiguously presented as having paid for access to her.

The other members of the circle share this reaction. Forde, with whom Ashenden "almost became cordial" over their shared dislike, dismisses Kuyper as "a bounder" [017-010]. The solidarity of gentlemanly disdain briefly unites the rivals against the outsider.

## Rosie's Perspective

Crucially, the novel does not endorse the narrator's horror. Rosie's response to his silent fury is disarmingly direct: "'How can you be so silly? Why should you be angry because Jack Kuyper gives me a fur cape? You can't afford to give me one, can you?'" [017-062].

The question is logically unassailable and morally devastating to the narrator's position. Rosie does not share his category system; she does not see the sable as payment, only as a gift from a man who could afford it and wanted to please her. Her pragmatic acceptance — "You can't afford to give me one, can you?" — exposes the narrator's moral outrage as a form of economic resentment. He is not angry that she accepted a gift; he is angry that he cannot compete.

## Ashenden's Confession

The narrator's response to the Kuyper episode is the novel's rawest confession of wounded vanity:

"I could have borne the thought of the others; it was Jack Kuyper who horrified me. How could she? It was abominable to be poor. I longed to have enough money to tell her that if she would send the fellow back his beastly furs I would give her better ones instead" [017-034].

The admission — "it was abominable to be poor" — is the novel's most honest articulation of the economic dimension of sexual jealousy. The narrator's moral vocabulary (degradation, vulgarity) is revealed as a mask for economic impotence. He cannot compete with Kuyper's diamonds, and so he converts his envy into moral condemnation.

## Thematic Significance

Kuyper is the most vivid embodiment of the threat that Rosie's sexual freedom poses to the narrator's — and by extension the novel's — value system. The other admirers can be assimilated into a narrative of bohemian tolerance; Kuyper cannot, because his wealth makes the transactional dimension of Rosie's relationships uncomfortably visible.

Kuyper's brief appearance in the novel serves as a stress test for the narrator's romantic self-conception. The young Ashenden has constructed a narrative in which he is Rosie's true lover and the others are mere friends or, at worst, minor rivals. Kuyper shatters this construction not by being more attractive or more interesting than the narrator but by being richer — by demonstrating that the rules of the game are not what Ashenden thought they were.

In the novel's larger argument about biography and reputation, Kuyper represents precisely the kind of embarrassing fact that must be suppressed. *[[the-life-and-letters|The Life and Letters of Edward Driffield]]* cannot mention the diamond merchant from Amsterdam who gave the great novelist's first wife a sable stole and took her to smart restaurants night after night. Kuyper is the "skeleton in the cupboard" in its most literal form — the evidence that must be locked away for the official version to stand.
