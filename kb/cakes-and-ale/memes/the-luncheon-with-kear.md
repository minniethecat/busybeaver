# The Luncheon with Kear

The Luncheon with Alroy Kear is the event that opens *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* and sets its entire narrative in motion. Over lunch at Kear's club, the successful novelist proposes that [[ashenden]] — who knew [[edward-driffield]] as a boy in [[blackstable]] — share his memories for the official biography Kear has been commissioned to write. The invitation, and Ashenden's wary acceptance of it, establishes the novel's central dynamic: the tension between memory and biography, private truth and public reputation.

## The Event

### Background

By the time of the luncheon, Edward Driffield is recently dead — "the last time Edward Driffield ever came to London he lunched with me here" [002-036], Kear remarks, establishing his claim to intimacy with the departed great man. Amy Driffield, the widow, has commissioned Kear to write the official biography, selecting him — Ashenden later learns — because "she felt she could trust" him. [011-066]

Kear has been cultivating the commission through his careful management of literary connections. The luncheon with Ashenden is part of this campaign — a research expedition disguised as a social occasion.

### The Invitation

The novel opens with the telephone message:

> "Miss Fellows, my landlady, told me that Mr. Alroy Kear wished me to ring him up." [001-001]

Kear invites Ashenden to lunch at his club in St. James's Street. Ashenden accepts, but with misgivings born of experience: "At the height of the London season Alroy Kear would waste an hour on a fellow writer unless he had something very definite to get out of him." [002-029]

### The Lunch

The lunch itself occupies Chapter 2. In the club's dining room, over a carefully chosen meal, Kear deploys his considerable charm while Ashenden watches for the ulterior motive. The conversation ranges over literary gossip, mutual acquaintances, and the state of English letters — all of it, Ashenden suspects, preamble to the real request.

When Kear finally broaches the subject of Driffield's biography, he does so with the practised diffidence of a man who has learned that asking directly is less effective than allowing himself to be "persuaded":

> "It began to look as though luncheon would end without his finding an opportunity to say what he had in mind." [002-029]

The moment reveals Kear's method. He does not ask for what he wants; he creates the conditions under which it would be rude to refuse.

### The Proposition

Kear explains that Amy Driffield has commissioned him to write the official life of her late husband, and that he needs Ashenden's childhood memories of Driffield in Blackstable. He frames the request as a tribute to a great writer — "after all, Driffield wasn't anything like that … Driffield never wrote a page that wasn't instinct with beauty." [002-067] [002-083]

Ashenden consents to help, but the reader — already introduced to Kear's "sincerity" in Chapter 1's devastating anatomy of his character [001-041] — understands that the biography is as much an exercise in reputation management as in truth-telling.

## Narrative Significance

The Luncheon with Kear serves as the novel's **inciting incident** and **frame narrative**. Everything that follows — the recollections of Blackstable, the portrait of Rosie, the visit to Ferne Court — is occasioned by this single social engagement. The event also establishes the novel's governing irony: a biography commissioned to celebrate Driffield's genius will systematically suppress the most interesting thing about his life, the "cakes and ale" of his first marriage to [[rosie-driffield|Rosie]].

Structurally, the luncheon introduces the contrast between Kear's performative sincerity and Ashenden's sceptical memory that will drive the entire narrative. Kear wants Ashenden's memories for his book; Ashenden gives us, the readers, the memories that will never appear in it.
