# Westminster Abbey

Westminster Abbey is the site of the ultimate literary honor — burial in Poets' Corner — and its mention in the novel's closing pages crystallizes the question at the heart of *Cakes and Ale*: what does it mean for a writer to be canonized? "When he died a little while ago and an agitation arose among his admirers to have him buried in Westminster Abbey the present incumbent at Blackstable, my uncle's successor twice removed, wrote to the papers to say that Driffield had been born in the parish." [003-041]

## Overview

The Abbey represents the endpoint of the machinery of reputation — the institutional apotheosis that converts a living writer into a national monument. The irony is structural: Driffield, the man who liked to sit in the Bear and Key talking to anyone who would listen, is being argued over as a candidate for England's most exclusive burial ground.

## Significance

The Westminster Abbey debate is the novel's final, quiet joke at the expense of literary canonization. The Blackstable vicar "wrote to the papers to say that Driffield had been born in the parish" [003-041] — a claim of provincial ownership that is both touching and absurd, the last echo of a world that never understood the writer it produced.
