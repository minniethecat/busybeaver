# The Duke of Kent

The Duke of Kent is one of Blackstable's public houses, mentioned in the narrator's description of the town's topography: "the space between the Duke of Kent and the Bear and Key." [003-007] Along with the Bear and Key, it helps define Blackstable's social geography — a town measured by its pubs.

## Overview

The Duke of Kent appears only as a geographic marker, but its presence is significant: Blackstable's pubs are the novel's democratic counter-spaces to London's exclusive clubs. Where St. James's Street admits only members, the Duke of Kent (and the Bear and Key) admits anyone — including Edward Driffield.

## Significance

The pub's name — "Duke of Kent" — is mildly ironic. A royal ducal title attached to a provincial drinking establishment: the gap between name and reality is Blackstable in miniature.
