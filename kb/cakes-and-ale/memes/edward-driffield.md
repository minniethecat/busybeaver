# Edward Driffield

**Edward Driffield** (c. 1840 – c. 1928) is the absent centre around which *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* revolves — a celebrated English novelist whose posthumous biography, commissioned from the careerist [[alroy-kear]], occasions Maugham's most sustained satire of literary reputation-making. Already dead when the novel opens, Driffield exists entirely through the memories of those who knew him.

The irony is structural: a man whose collected edition fills thirty-seven volumes [002-067] is known to the reader only through the partial, self-interested recollections of others. He began life as a disreputable figure in [[blackstable]] and ended it as the "Grand Old Man of English Letters" [011-012] whose last editions sold at auction for seventy-eight pounds.

## Role in the Novel

Driffield serves as Maugham's case study in the alchemy of literary canonization. How does a man who was "for long thought to write very bad English" [011-001] come to be regarded as a master whose work fills "shelves … with the translations of Driffield's books into every language of civilized people" [002-081]? The answer, the novel suggests over its twenty-six chapters, has less to do with the work itself than with the social machinery — patrons, biographers, critics, widows — that converts obscurity into eminence, and lived life into manageable legend.

Driffield is the novel's great structural absence. He never speaks directly. Every fact about him is filtered through a remembering witness. Ashenden recalls a man of quiet charm who taught him to bicycle [005-001]; [[mrs-barton-trafford]] sees an author whose "genius" she alone can steward [014-014]; [[alroy-kear]] sees raw material for "a volume … royal octavo, slim and light in the hand" [011-069]; and Amy Driffield, his second wife, sees a reputation to protect. The reader must triangulate the truth from these incompatible views.

## Key Appearances (by Recollection)

### The Blackstable Years

In the 1890s, when Ashenden is a boy of fifteen, Edward Driffield and his wife [[rosie-driffield]] live in obscurity on the outskirts of [[blackstable]]. The local gentry regard him with suspicion — a man who had married a barmaid and wrote novels that nobody read. "All Blackstable knew," Ashenden recalls, "but only thought him puffed up because he dressed like a gentleman." [003-005] The Vicar, Ashenden's uncle, forbids the boy from associating with the Driffields — a prohibition Ashenden quietly ignores.

During this period, Edward teaches Ashenden to ride a bicycle — an act of ordinary kindness that becomes, in retrospect, the most honest human contact in the novel: "It was true that Edward Driffield had taught me to bicycle. That was the first thing I knew of him." [005-001]

### The Saturday Afternoons at Mrs. Barton Trafford's

Some years after Rosie's elopement with [[lord-george-kemp]], Driffield comes under the patronage of Mrs. Barton Trafford, who "made him quite presentable" [004-076] and launched him as a literary figure. Her salon becomes the engine of his reputation: "She gave luncheon parties for him … She took care that his books were noticed in the right places … She made it a point of honour to see that he never lacked for adulation." [014-006] Driffield becomes "a distinguished person" [014-005] — yet Maugham notes that it was Mrs. Trafford who selected which friends would surround him, curating not just his career but his social world.

### Ferne Court: The Grand Old Man

In his final years, Driffield lives at [[ferne-court]], a country house three miles from Blackstable, with his second wife, Amy, who functions as nurse, gatekeeper, and custodian of his legacy. The house is filled with editions, translations, and the paraphernalia of literary fame. When Ashenden visits, he finds Driffield physically frail but mentally alert — "Edward Driffield's indifferent health and great age obliged him to husband his strength" [004-032]. The visit reveals a man who has outlived his own vitality, surrounded by people who love his reputation more than they ever knew the man.

### The Photographs

In the novel's closing passage, Ashenden examines a sequence of photographs tracing Driffield's physical transformation across decades — from a "young sailorman" to a weary, refined literary figure, "the change in him wrought by experience, thought, and achieved ambition" [025-012]. The photographs become the novel's final meditation on the gap between a life as lived and a life as recorded.

## Character Analysis

### The Paradox of Greatness

Driffield embodies the novel's central paradox: literary greatness is a social construction, yet the work itself may still contain something irreducible. Maugham refuses to resolve this tension. Driffield's novels are described in contradictory terms — "very bad English" [011-001] yet "instinct with beauty" [002-083]; the work of "nothing like that" kind of genius [002-067] yet holding "an ever-increasing public" [002-081]. The point is not to judge the work but to expose the machinery of judgment itself: how "longevity is genius" [011-008] in a trade where merely surviving one's contemporaries is half the battle.

> "If, as I think, longevity is genius, few in our time have enjoyed it in a more conspicuous degree than Edward Driffield. When he was a young fellow in the sixties … his position was anything but assured. … He went on and on." [011-008]

### Cakes and Ale

The novel's title, drawn from *Twelfth Night*, frames Driffield as the "cakes and ale" figure — the genuine, sensual, messy life that polite literary culture tries to sanitize into respectability. His tragedy is that the very forces that secure his reputation are the forces that erase what was most alive in him. [[rosie-driffield|Rosie]], the barmaid who embodied the "cakes and ale" spirit, is the secret the biography must suppress; the irony is that without her, Driffield might never have written his best books.

### A Narrator's Distance

Ashenden's relationship with Driffield is defined by what he *doesn't* know. He witnessed the Blackstable years but was too young to understand; by the time he could, Driffield was already the property of Mrs. Barton Trafford and, later, Amy. The novel's structure — memory triggered by the biography's impending publication — makes the reader complicit in the very act of posthumous mining that Maugham satirizes.

## Real-Life Model

Driffield is loosely based on **Thomas Hardy** (1840–1928). The parallels are extensive:

| Element | Driffield | Hardy |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Birth | c. 1840, humble origins | 1840, son of a stonemason |
| Death | c. 1928, a literary monument | 1928, buried in Westminster Abbey |
| First wife | Rosie Gann, a barmaid | Emma Gifford, whom he married in 1874 |
| Second wife | Amy, nurse-companion | Florence Dugdale, his secretary |
| Posthumous biography | Commissioned from Kear | Controversial biographies by Florence Hardy and others |
| Literary standing | "Grand Old Man" of English letters | The "Grand Old Man" of English letters |

Maugham deliberately obscures the identification — Driffield is a composite, not a portrait — but the structural parallels are unmistakable.
