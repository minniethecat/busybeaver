# Genius

In *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]*, "genius" is a word used constantly and trusted never. The novel treats it not as a stable category but as a social token — something attributed to writers by patrons, critics, and biographers, whose motives for doing so are rarely disinterested. The word appears at strategic moments across the narrative, each time destabilised by the context in which it is deployed.

## Definitions in Context

The novel offers no single definition of genius; instead, it presents a series of competing claims, each refracted through the speaker's interests:

| Speaker | Claim | Context | PN |
|---------|-------|---------|-----|
| Kear (recalling a dinner speech) | "Genius was an infinite capacity for taking pains" | Reported second-hand, as an example of the kind of thing said at literary dinners | [001-018] |
| Ashenden (narrator) | "Longevity is genius" | His own sardonic formulation, observing Driffield's survival past all his contemporaries | [011-008] |
| Ashenden (narrator) | "You can't think how many geniuses I've seen acclaimed, enjoy their hour, and vanish into obscurity" | Reflection on the transience of literary reputation | [002-064] |
| Ashenden (on Mrs. Barton Trafford) | "Mrs. Barton Trafford had a genius for friendship" | Irony: "genius" applied to social manipulation rather than art | [014-008] |
| Ashenden (on Rosie) | "People went to the Driffields' because Edward was a genius, but because Rosie was beautiful" | The novel's key reversal: what people said they valued vs. what they actually valued | [014-019] |

## The Social Production of Genius

The novel's most sustained treatment of the concept appears in Chapter 11, where Ashenden reflects on what the critics wrote about Driffield and concludes it was "eyewash" [011-007]. He identifies longevity — not brilliance, not originality, not even craft — as the decisive factor in the attribution of genius:

> "If, as I think, longevity is genius, few in our time have enjoyed it in a more conspicuous degree than Edward Driffield. When he was a young fellow in the sixties … his position was anything but assured. … He went on and on." [011-008]

The argument has a grim plausibility. Driffield's contemporaries died or stopped writing; Driffield kept producing novels. By the time he was seventy, he was the last man standing — and "last man standing" is easily rebranded as "greatest."

## Genius and Its Gatekeepers

Mrs. Barton Trafford's role in the manufacture of Driffield's reputation is the novel's clearest illustration of how "genius" functions as a social attribution rather than an intrinsic quality. She "discovered" him when "his position was anything but assured" [011-008], and set about creating the conditions under which his genius could be "recognised":

> "She gave luncheon parties for him; she made him known to persons who might be useful to him; she took care that his books were noticed in the right places and she was indefatigable in her efforts to secure for him the praise that was his due." [014-006]

The verb "secure" is telling. Genius, in this account, is not something one has and others notice — it is something others *secure* on one's behalf. Driffield's achievement may be real, but it is invisible without the institutional apparatus that names and markets it.

## Genius Versus Beauty

The novel's most subversive move is to juxtapose "genius" (the word applied to Edward Driffield) with "beauty" (the word applied to [[rosie-driffield|Rosie]]). Ashenden reports that "people went to the Driffields' not, as they thought, because Edward was a genius, but because Rosie was beautiful" [014-019]. The implication is radical: what we call "genius" in art may be a secondary effect of something more elemental — the vitality, warmth, and sensual immediacy that Rosie embodies and that the literary world, with its prizes and prefaces and collected editions, cannot accommodate.

## The Temporary Nature of Acclaim

Ashenden's weary catalogue of forgotten "geniuses" serves as the novel's caution against taking any literary reputation as final:

> "You can't think how many geniuses I've seen acclaimed, enjoy their hour, and vanish into obscurity. I wonder what became of them now, those geniuses of a season." [002-064]

The passage performs a quiet demolition of the concept it names. If genius can vanish into obscurity, it was never the permanent quality the word implies. It was attention. And attention, like the London season, is a commodity that shifts with fashion.

## The Narrator's Position

Ashenden himself is notably reluctant to judge Driffield's work. He describes what others have said; he reports the contradictions; he refuses to settle the question. This agnosticism is itself a philosophical position: the narrator who has seen too many "geniuses of a season" come and go knows better than to pronounce on the permanence of any reputation, including Driffield's.

## Key Characters in This Dynamic

- **[[edward-driffield|Edward Driffield]]** — the "genius" around whom the novel's machinery of reputation revolves
- **[[mrs-barton-trafford]]** — the patroness who "secured" the recognition of his genius
- **[[alroy-kear]]** — the biographer who will manage the "genius" narrative for a new generation
- **[[rosie-driffield|Rosie Driffield]]** — the "beauty" that people actually came for, and that the literary world suppressed
- **[[ashenden]]** — the sceptical narrator who has seen geniuses come and go

## Related Concepts

- **[[sincerity]]** — the quality Alroy Kear performs, and which the attribution of genius often requires as supporting evidence
- **[[truth]]** — what the biography must suppress in order to sustain the "genius" narrative
- **[[reputation]]** — the commodity of which "genius" is the highest denomination
- **[[literary-establishment]]** — the machinery that produces, certifies, and distributes the attribution of genius
- **[[beauty]]** — the rival value to genius; what people actually respond to, as opposed to what they say they value
- **[[posterity]]** — the imagined future audience whose judgment is invoked to justify present canonisation
