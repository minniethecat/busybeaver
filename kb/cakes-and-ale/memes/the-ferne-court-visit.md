# The Visit to Ferne Court

The visit to [[ferne-court|Ferne Court]] in the novel's fourth chapter is the catalyst for the entire narrative of *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]*. When [[ashenden]] accompanies [[lady-hodmarsh]], [[the-duchess]], and [[lord-scallion]] to lunch with the aged [[edward-driffield]] and his protective second wife [[amy-driffield]], the encounter forces him to confront the gulf between the man he knew in [[blackstable]] and the monument to literary greatness that the world has constructed around him.

## The Event

### Background: Lady Hodmarsh's Invitation

Ashenden was staying for a long weekend near [[tercanbury]] with Lady Hodmarsh, "the clever and handsome American wife of a sporting baronet with no intelligence and charming manners." Lady Hodmarsh, who "neither read the books nor looked at the pictures of the people to whom she offered hospitality," cultivated artists and writers for the feeling it gave her "of being in the artistic know." [004-017]

When the conversation turned to Edward Driffield, "her most celebrated neighbour," and Ashenden mentioned that he had once known him well, Lady Hodmarsh proposed an expedition. The party assembled itself: Lord Scallion, "a young peer with literary inclinations so violent that, instead of ruling this country as the laws of man and nature have decreed, he devoted his energy to the composition of detective novels," and a "big young fat duchess" whose "admiration for the celebrated writer was so intense that she was prepared to cut an engagement in London." [004-017]

Ashenden tried to demur — he had not seen Driffield for thirty-five years — but found himself swept along.

### The Arrival at Ferne Court

The party drove over in "a bright yellow Rolls." Ferne Court was a stucco house built about 1840, "plain and unpretentious, but substantial." The drawing room was "furnished so exactly as you felt a drawing room in a country house of modest size should be furnished that it was slightly disconcerting" — chintz chairs, Oriental bowls of potpourri, watercolours, and "on the grand piano in silver frames photographs of celebrated actresses, deceased authors, and minor royalties." [004-022]

> "It was no wonder that the duchess cried out that it was a lovely room. It was just the kind of room in which a distinguished writer should spend the evening of his days." [004-024]

Mrs. Driffield received them "with modest assurance" — a trim, competent woman of about forty-five with "a small sallow face and neat, sharp features." She introduced the Vicar of Blackstable and his wife, who had been visiting. Lady Hodmarsh and the duchess "immediately assumed that cringing affability that persons of rank assume with their inferiors." [004-022]

### Driffield Appears

When Driffield entered, Ashenden was shocked:

> "He was smaller than I remembered and very thin, his head was barely covered with fine silvery hair, he was clean-shaven, and his skin was almost transparent. His blue eyes were very pale and the rims of his eyelids red. He looked an old, old man, hanging on to mortality by a thread." [004-025]

He was dressed immaculately in a new blue serge suit, his collar several sizes too large, revealing "a wrinkled, scraggy neck." Ashenden, who had known a bearded, bohemian Driffield, found the transformation — the product of Amy Driffield's decades of curation — almost as startling as the physical decay.

Driffield greeted his guests with polite civility, addressing Ashenden as though they had never met: "It's very good of a busy and successful man like you to come all this way to see an old fogey." [004-025]

### The Wink

The visit's decisive moment came during lunch. Ashenden, watching Driffield from across the table, caught his eye:

> "Perhaps he felt my eyes upon him, for he raised his. They rested on me for a while with a meditative look, mild and yet oddly scrutinizing, and then suddenly, unmistakably this time, he gave me another wink. The frivolous gesture in that old, withered face was more than startling, it was embarrassing." [004-031]

The wink was a private signal across decades — a suggestion that the Driffield of the Blackstable bicycle lessons still existed behind the monument, that the old man recognized the absurdity of his own apotheosis.

### The Warning

While lunch proceeded, the vicar's wife — acting as Amy Driffield's proxy — delivered a quiet warning to Ashenden:

> "His wife is anxious that you shouldn't call up old memories that might be painful to him. He's very frail, you know, and the least thing upsets him." [004-041]

The meaning was clear: do not mention [[rosie-driffield|Rosie]].

### The Tour and Departure

After lunch, Mrs. Driffield conducted the party through the study — "the sort of room that a devoted wife would evidently arrange for her literary husband" — displaying the blue morocco manuscript, the "period piece" writing desk, the shelves of unread presentation copies. The room "had the look not of a writer's workshop, but of a memorial to a great name." [004-052]

At the door, as the party took their leave, Lady Hodmarsh — "a woman of tact" — attempted to draw out the connection between Driffield and Ashenden:

> "'I was so interested to hear that you and Mr. Ashenden had known one another years and years ago. Was he a nice little boy?' Driffield looked at me for a moment with that level, ironic gaze of his. I had the impression that if there had been nobody there he would have put his tongue out at me. 'Shy,' he replied. 'I taught him to ride a bicycle.'" [004-059]

## Narrative Significance

The Ferne Court visit establishes the novel's frame. It introduces the tension between public reputation and private memory that [[alroy-kear]]'s biography project will exacerbate, and it reveals the machinery of literary reputation management — the protective widow, the curated domestic space, the careful suppression of inconvenient truths — that Ashenden's subsequent recollections will quietly subvert.

The visit also serves as Ashenden's initiation into the project of remembering. Amy Driffield's warning not to "call up old memories" has the opposite effect: it reminds Ashenden that he *has* memories worth calling up.
