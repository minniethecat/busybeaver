# Truth

In the world of *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]*, "truth" is a value that everyone professes and no one practises. The novel is structured around an act of biographical suppression: [[alroy-kear]]'s commission to write the "official" life of [[edward-driffield]] requires the systematic omission of the most important truth about the subject — his first wife, [[rosie-driffield|Rosie]], the barmaid whose warmth and infidelity scandalised Blackstable and may have been the condition of Driffield's best work.

## The Problem Stated

The novel's central ethical question is posed by the biography itself. What does it mean to tell "the truth" about a dead writer? Is truth a matter of factual accuracy (dates, publications, documented events) or of deeper, more damaging honesty (the messy private life that public memorialisation prefers to forget)? And who decides which truth matters?

Ashenden, the narrator, is the novel's truth-teller only in the sense that he refuses to lie. He does not publish his knowledge of Rosie; he tells it to us, the readers, in a memoir that is itself fiction. The truth, in *Cakes and Ale*, is everywhere deferred — present as an absence, legible only in what the official record omits.

## Truth and Biography

The biography that Alroy Kear will write is the novel's extended case study in the relationship between truth and genre. Biography, as a form, promises accuracy. But its institutional context — commissioned by a widow, published by a trade house, reviewed in the literary press — ensures that the truth it tells will be the truth that serves the living, not the dead.

Maugham's insight is that this is not a matter of simple mendacity. Kear will not lie about Driffield's dates or publications; those facts are checkable. He will simply *not mention* what the widow wants not mentioned. The truth of omission is the truth genre of polite letters.

## Truth and the Narrator

Ashenden's relationship to truth is complicated by his own complicity. He knows about Rosie; he has the evidence that would complicate Kear's sanitised portrait. But he does not publish it. Instead, he narrates it to us, the readers, in a novel that is frankly autobiographical. The effect is a kind of nested truth-telling — the narrator is honest with us about his own dishonesty to the world.

This self-awareness distinguishes Ashenden from Kear. Kear believes (or has come to believe) in his own sincerity; Ashenden knows he is "distorting the truth a little" [005-052] and tells us so. The distinction is the difference between the hypocrite and the ironist — and Maugham, like his narrator, is firmly on the side of irony.

## Truth and the Critics

The novel has a jaundiced view of critical judgment, treating it as another form of reputation management:

> "I do not think it would have been wise in me to attempt to be entirely candid. If you tell the truth they only say you're cynical and it does an author no good to get a reputation for cynicism." [011-068]

The passage turns on itself. Ashenden is telling us, the readers, that he would like to be more honest — and in telling us this, he *is* being more honest, while simultaneously performing the self-protective manoeuvre he describes. The truth about truth is that it is always partly strategic.

## What Truth Costs

The novel's most sober assessment of truth comes in a throwaway line about Kear's biography:

> "There was indeed a hateful truth in the criticism. But they didn't want to hear it; and what was the good of telling people what they didn't want to hear?" [025-016]

The line is devastating because it is reasonable. The biographical "truth" about Driffield — that he married a barmaid who cuckolded him with his best friend, that she ran off to America and he married his nurse — is not edifying. It does not improve the work. It only wounds the widow and complicates the reputation. What, then, is the "good" of telling it?

The novel does not answer this question. It stages it. And by staging it — by telling us the truth that the biography will suppress — it makes the reader answer it for herself.
