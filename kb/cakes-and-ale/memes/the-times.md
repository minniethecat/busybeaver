# The Times

The Times of London appears in *Cakes and Ale* as part of the institutional machinery of literary reputation — the newspaper of record whose judgments confer legitimacy and whose obituaries mark the final stage of canonization.

## Role in the Novel

The Times is referenced implicitly through the novel's concern with literary reputation-making. As the premier organ of establishment opinion, it represents the institutional validation that writers like Kear court and that Driffield, by the end of his career, has secured. Its obituary pages — where a writer's life is finally summarized and judged — are the ultimate destination of the biographical project the novel satirizes.

## Work and Reality

The Times (founded 1785) was, in Maugham's era as now, the British establishment's newspaper of record. Its literary coverage carried immense weight in shaping reputations. The novel's silence about specific Times reviews of Driffield's work is telling: reputation, Maugham suggests, is built not on specific judgments but on the accumulated weight of institutional approval.

## Selected Quotations

> The novel's satire of literary reputation-making implicitly targets the institutional organs — The Times among them — that manufacture literary eminence through the authority of their judgment.
