# Mr. Galloway

Mr. Galloway is the curate of Blackstable parish under Ashenden's uncle, the vicar. A "tall thin ungainly man with untidy black hair and a small sallow dark face," [003-011] he serves as a bridge figure for young Ashenden — simultaneously a semi-respectable clergyman and a clandestine participant in the socially marginal Driffield circle, who must keep his friendship with the novelist hidden from the disapproving vicar.

## 角色概述

Galloway functions as Ashenden's co-conspirator in the boy's secret visits to the Driffields' home. He embodies the tension between the respectable clerical world of Blackstable and the raffish literary-bohemian world of Edward and Rosie Driffield. Though young and physically awkward, he possesses genuine literary curiosity and a quiet courage that allows him to pursue friendships across class and propriety lines — and form a bond with a boy who shares his secret. [003-011] [008-062]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Galloway first appears in Chapter III, introduced on Ashenden's first day at the vicarage for the summer holidays. After completing his parish business, he stays for tea with Ashenden's aunt:

> The curate's name was Galloway; he was a tall thin ungainly man with untidy black hair and a small sallow dark face. I suppose he was quite young, but to me he seemed middle-aged. He talked very quickly and gesticulated a great deal. This made people think him rather queer and my uncle would not have kept him but that he was very energetic, and my uncle, being extremely lazy, was glad to have someone to take so much work off his shoulders. [003-011]

### 关键场景

**The Driffields' parlour — an evening of literary talk** — In Chapter VIII, Galloway is present at one of the Driffields' informal evening gatherings, along with Lord George Kemp. The evening features singing, drinking, and earnest literary discussion:

> Then I saw they were the curate, Mr. Galloway, and Lord George Kemp. I fancied that the curate shook my hand with constraint. [008-018]

He defends Driffield's novel *Fairhaven* against critical opinion, and relays Driffield's own literary theories — including the novelist's view that "the men in the colliers that run up to Newcastle and the fishermen and farm hands don't behave like ladies and gentlemen and don't talk like them." [008-081] When the conversation turns to Dickens, Galloway and Ashenden "looked down our noses" — sharing a silent complicity in their higher literary standards. [008-087]

**The conspiracy of silence** — After the evening at the Driffields', Galloway and Ashenden form a tacit alliance, united by their shared secret of consorting with the socially questionable Driffields:

> Our conspiracy of silence had made us friends and when we met at the vicarage or in the vestry after church we looked at one another archly. We did not talk about our secret, but we enjoyed it; I think it gave us both a good deal of satisfaction to know that we were making a fool of my uncle. [009-001]

**The Driffields' disgrace** — When the Driffields "shoot the moon" — abscond overnight to escape their debts — Galloway is among the creditors left unpaid, owed "four pounds seventeen and six for coal" and his tea muffins. [010-043] The narrator notes that "poor Mr. Galloway was dreadfully upset" by the scandal. [010-032]

## 人物关系

- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：Galloway is one of the few local residents who appreciates Driffield as a serious writer. He defends *Fairhaven* against the critics, relays Driffield's literary views, and identifies Ashenden to Driffield ("Galloway told me who you were"). [005-010] [008-045]
- **与 [[ashenden]]**：Galloway and young Ashenden form a conspiratorial friendship, united by their secret visits to the Driffields' home and their shared enjoyment of outwitting the disapproving vicar. Galloway is an older-brother figure in Ashenden's moral education. [009-001]
- **与 [[lord-george-kemp]]**：Both are regulars at the Driffields' evening gatherings. They represent the unlikely pair of local men — the curate and the coal merchant's son — drawn into the bohemian Driffield circle. [008-018]
- **与 the Vicar of Blackstable**：Galloway serves under Ashenden's uncle, who tolerates him for his energy despite finding him "rather queer." Galloway must conceal his Driffield friendship from the vicar. [003-011]

## 人物分析

Galloway is a study in small-c conservative courage. He does not openly defy the vicar's social prejudices, but his quiet, sustained friendship with the Driffields represents a choice of values over propriety. He is "quite an educated man" [003-024] in a parish that values clerical orthodoxy over literary sensibility, and his defence of Driffield's realism — the unvarnished depiction of working-class speech and behavior — shows genuine artistic sympathy.

He also functions as a mirror for Ashenden's own development. The "conspiracy of silence" between curate and boy marks a formative experience in Ashenden's moral education: the discovery that respectable authority figures can be wrong, and that loyalty to interesting people matters more than loyalty to conventional judgments. Galloway's "dreadfully upset" response to the Driffields' midnight flight is both comic and poignant — he has lost not just four pounds seventeen and six, but his access to a world more vivid than the vicarage.

## 可能的现实原型

No specific prototype for Galloway has been identified. He belongs to the gallery of minor clergy in Maugham's fiction, characters who often serve as points of contact between conventional society and more transgressive worlds. The detail of his physical ungainliness and rapid, gesticulating speech is typical of Maugham's economical character portraiture.
