# Harry Retford

**Harry Retford** is an actor and one of the inner circle of regular visitors to the [[edward-driffield|Driffields']] Saturday gatherings in Limpus Road. He is introduced alongside [[quentin-forde]] and [[lionel-hillier]] as among "the most faithful" of the weekly regulars [014-018]. Retford's significance in the novel extends beyond his role as one of [[rosie-driffield|Rosie's]] lovers: it is through his connection that the iconic photographs of Rosie are taken — images that survive into the novel's frame-narrative present, preserving her beauty across decades.

## Character and Circumstances

Retford is defined by a paradox: "though he never had a bob, behaved as if he had" [015-001]. Despite being perpetually short of money, he took Rosie "about in hansoms too and gave her dinner at Romano's or in one or other of the little restaurants that were becoming modish in Soho" [015-001]. He was, the narrator notes, "an actor and a clever one, but he was difficult to suit and so was often out of work" [015-001] — a portrait of theatrical talent undermined by a difficult temperament.

The narrator's retrospective assessment of Retford, delivered decades later when Rosie recounts her post-Driffield life in Yonkers, acknowledges his charm while noting his flaws: "I expect he was a bit unscrupulous and he was rather funny over money matters, but he could make you laugh and with all his faults he was a very good fellow" [026-005].

## The Photographic Sitting

Retford's most significant contribution to the novel's symbolic architecture is the photographic sitting he arranged for Rosie. "Harry Retford knew one of the fashionable photographers of the period and, arranging special terms, he took Rosie to be photographed" [014-021]. The resulting images capture Rosie in evening dress — a white satin gown with a long train — a vision the young Ashenden has never seen before: "I had never seen Rosie in evening dress" [014-021].

These photographs function as a talisman in the novel's frame narrative. When the elderly Ashenden, clearing out his rooms decades later, "came across the photographs that Harry Retford, the actor, had taken of Rosie" [025-013], they produce an immediate emotional effect: "It gave me a pang. That was how I best remembered her. Notwithstanding the old-fashioned gown, she was alive there and tremulous with the passion that filled her" [025-013].

The photographs represent a fixed point in the novel's preoccupation with memory, art, and the passage of time. They are one of the few material traces of Rosie's London years that survive into the narrative present, and their existence is owed to Retford's theatrical connections.

## The Photographs vs. the Painting

The novel sets Retford's photographs in implicit dialogue with [[lionel-hillier|Lionel Hillier's]] painted portrait of Rosie. When Hillier sees the photographs, he dismisses them: "'Rotten,' he said. 'What can a photograph give of Rosie? The thing is that all photographs are banal'" [014-021]. Hillier's painterly disdain for the mechanical image is a minor aesthetic argument embedded in the novel — but the photographs, not the painting, are what survive. The photograph, for all its alleged banality, preserves Rosie's image across decades; the painting exists only as a photographic reproduction of itself.

## Ashenden's Jealousy

Like the other men in Rosie's circle, Retford becomes an object of the narrator's jealous scrutiny. Ashenden "wondered about ... Harry Retford" [015-001] — wondering, that is, whether Retford was sleeping with Rosie. The question is answered with brutal finality in Chapter XVII: the narrator acknowledges "that when she went out to dinner with Quentin Forde and Harry Retford and Lionel Hillier she went to bed with them just as she came to bed with me" [017-008].

Yet Retford also extends a surprising overture of friendship to the younger man. He asks Ashenden to dine with him and go to the play [017-006], treating the narrator with a collegiality that the more patrician Forde withholds. The narrator's response — suspicion that Retford must want something — says more about Ashenden's insecurity than about Retford's motives.

## Thematic Significance

Of Rosie's three regular admirers, Retford occupies the middle ground between Forde's wealthy respectability and Hillier's artistic bohemianism. He is a performer — someone who understands surfaces, appearances, the gap between what is and what is shown. That it is Retford, the actor, who arranges for Rosie to be photographed is thematically appropriate: the photograph is a performance of beauty, a constructed image that captures something true despite (or because of) its artifice.

In the novel's larger critique of literary biography, Retford represents the kind of figure who must be erased from the official record. *[[the-life-and-letters|The Life and Letters of Edward Driffield]]* cannot acknowledge that the great novelist's first wife dined at Romano's with a raffish, impecunious actor who was almost certainly her lover. The photographs Retford arranged — the material evidence of Rosie's beauty and of the world she inhabited — would be inadmissible in [[alroy-kear|Kear's]] sanitised narrative.
