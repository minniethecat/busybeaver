# The Vicarage at Blackstable

The vicarage is Ashenden's boyhood home, where he lived with his uncle (the vicar) and his German aunt. "I lived with an uncle and aunt on the outskirts of a little Kentish town by the sea." [003-003] It is the physical and moral center of respectable Blackstable — the perspective from which the Driffields' disreputable life was first observed and judged.

## Overview

The vicarage represents institutional respectability. The vicar's table is where the town's judgments are formed and articulated — "when I consider the greatness that Edward Driffield afterward achieved I cannot but smile as I remember the fashion in which he was discussed at my uncle's table." [003-041] The house is a site of moral measurement, and Driffield, in those early years, was found wanting.

## Selected Quotations

> "I lived with an uncle and aunt on the outskirts of a little Kentish town by the sea. It was called Blackstable and my uncle was the vicar." [003-003]
