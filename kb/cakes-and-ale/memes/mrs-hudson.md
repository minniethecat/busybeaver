# Mrs. Hudson

Mrs. Hudson is a Blackstable boarding-house keeper whose warm recognition of the adult Ashenden — "Well, if that isn't Mr. Ashenden! Who ever thought of seeing you?" [012-008] — provides one of the novel's moments of unironic human connection. She represents the world of ordinary kindness that the literary world, for all its sophistication, cannot replicate.

## Role Overview

Mrs. Hudson's function is to preserve continuity with the past. When Ashenden returns to Blackstable, she is unchanged — "there was scarcely a white hair on her head, and her eyes, as black and shining as buttons, sparkled with fun" [012-009] — a stable point in a narrative otherwise occupied with transformation, decline, and the erasure of memory.

## Appearances in the Novel

### The Return

Ashenden's arrival at Mrs. Hudson's boarding-house is one of the novel's warmest scenes. Her immediate recognition — "Well, if that isn't Mr. Ashenden!" — bridges the decades without ceremony or calculation. She offers tea, remembers him, and asks no strategic questions. [012-008]

### The Tea

The domestic ritual — "Is the water boiling, 'Ester? You will 'ave a nice cup of tea, won't you?" [012-008] — is a miniature of everything the literary world is not: warm, unpretentious, genuinely hospitable. Where Alroy Kear's lunches are strategic negotiations, Mrs. Hudson's tea is simply tea.

### The Unchanged Face

The narrator lingers on her appearance: "Mrs. Hudson was a little heavier than when I first knew her and her movements were more deliberate, but there was scarcely a white hair on her head, and her eyes, as black and shining as buttons, sparkled with fun." [012-009] The detail is affectionate and precise — she is one of the few characters in the novel described with unqualified warmth.

## Relationships

- **With the Narrator (Ashenden)**: She remembers him from his boyhood and welcomes him without agenda. Their relationship is the novel's emotional baseline — the standard of genuine human connection against which all other interactions are measured.
- **With [[blackstable]]**: She is part of the town's fabric — a fixed point in its social geography. Her boarding-house is a repository of memory that the official biography cannot access.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Hudson is Maugham's quiet counter-argument to his own satire. In a novel devoted to exposing the machinery of reputation, she represents something that cannot be manufactured: genuine warmth, uncalculated kindness, the simple decency of remembering people without wanting anything from them.

Her "eyes, as black and shining as buttons, sparkled with fun" [012-009] — the metaphor is deliberately homely. Buttons are not elegant, but they are real, useful, and enduring. She is the novel's "cakes and ale" in human form.
