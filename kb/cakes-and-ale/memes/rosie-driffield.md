# Rosie Driffield

Rosie Driffield (née Gann) is the novel's emotional and symbolic heart — a former barmaid whose warmth, sensuality, and indifference to convention embody the "cakes and ale" of the title. Though she disappears from the narrative early, her presence haunts every subsequent discussion of Driffield's life and art.

## Role Overview

Rosie is the counterforce to everything Alroy Kear and Mrs. Barton Trafford represent. Where they manufacture literary reputation through calculation and social engineering, Rosie lives with unselfconscious vitality — a quality that the machinery of respectability must systematically erase. She is "the skeleton in the cupboard" of the title: the disreputable past that Driffield's official biography must manage, minimize, or silently delete. [005-072]

And yet Ashenden, the narrator, remembers her not with moral judgment but with deep, enduring affection — the novel's one unironic emotion. His memories of Rosie, preserved intact from boyhood through decades of literary sophistication, are the moral counterweight to everything Kear represents. She is the standard by which the novel measures what literary culture destroys.

## Appearance

### First Appearance

Rosie enters the narrative through gossip — the medium of Blackstable's respectable classes. The neighbors recall "Ted Driffield" marrying "Rosie Gann," the barmaid, and the scandal it caused: "when he told his mother what he was goin' to do she took to 'er bed and wouldn't speak to 'im for a week." [005-072] Her father Josiah Gann "went for a soldier and when he come back he 'ad a wooden leg," and Rosie "used to go to Sunday school" with the local children. [005-075] The cumulative portrait is of a woman whose humble origins make her, in Blackstable's eyes, permanently ineligible for respectability — and who, crucially, does not care.

### Key Scenes

1. **The Blackstable Household** — Young Ashenden visits the Driffields' modest home in Rye Lane, where Rosie's natural warmth transforms what the town sees as a scandalous mésalliance into something genuine and welcoming. When Driffield calls out "Rosie, it's young Ashenden," her greeting is immediate and unpretentious. [008-015] In a world of social calculation, her hospitality requires no calculation.

2. **Conversations with the Neighbors** — Mrs. Hudson and others provide the novel's oral history of Rosie — fragmentary, judgmental, yet unintentionally revealing. "There's very little that Rosie Gann didn't do," one neighbor remarks, and the ambiguity of the statement — is it moral condemnation or grudging admiration? — captures the community's complex response to her. [005-097]

3. **Rosie's Departure** — Rosie eventually leaves Driffield, running away with Lord George Kemp. [005-079] The novel handles this obliquely, through gossip and aftermath rather than direct narration — a narrative choice that preserves Rosie's mystery. She is never explained, only remembered.

4. **The Reunion** — Years later, Ashenden encounters Rosie in London — now remarried, prosperous, and unchanged in her essential warmth. Her spontaneous recognition — "Well, if that isn't Mr. Ashenden!" — is one of the novel's few moments of uncalculated human connection. [012-008] She has survived, prospered even, while the literary world continues its anxious reputation-management.

5. **Ashenden's Reflections** — In the present-day frame, Ashenden's memories of Rosie provide the novel's moral counterweight to Kear's biographical scheming. His affection for her — untainted by literary ambition, unchanged by decades — is the novel's argument that some things survive the machinery of reputation.

## Relationships

**With Edward Driffield**: Her first husband. Their marriage, though socially transgressive, was a genuine love match rooted in shared vitality rather than calculation. Her departure devastated him — yet paradoxically made his later canonization possible, by removing the embarrassing "real" wife whose disreputability would have complicated the official portrait. [005-072]

**With Ashenden (the narrator)**: Young Ashenden is drawn to Rosie's warmth and unpretentiousness. She treats him with a kindness that contrasts sharply with the social judgments of Blackstable's respectable classes. His memories of her — preserved across decades — are the novel's one relationship untainted by irony or instrumentalism. [008-015]

**With Lord George Kemp**: Rosie's lover, with whom she eventually elopes — or leaves with. [012-008] The relationship is neither condemned nor romanticized; it simply *is*, like Rosie herself — a fact of vitality rather than an object of moral judgment.

**With Blackstable Society**: The town regards her as "fast" and disreputable — a barmaid who married above her station and then proved their judgments correct by leaving. Their gossip reveals as much about their own narrowness as about Rosie. [005-079] The respectable classes need Rosie to be scandalous, because her existence threatens the categories on which their social order depends.

## Character Analysis

Rosie is Maugham's embodiment of the "cakes and ale" principle — the idea that life's genuine pleasures (sensuality, affection, spontaneity, uncalculated generosity) are systematically erased by the machinery of respectability. The novel's title comes from *Twelfth Night*: "Dost thou think, because thou art virtuous, there shall be no more cakes and ale?" Rosie *is* the cakes and ale that virtue would extinguish.

Her structural position in the novel is brilliant: she appears primarily in the early chapters, then vanishes — yet her absence is more powerful than her presence could be. The rest of the novel is, in a sense, an extended demonstration of what happens when someone like Rosie is removed from the record. Kear's biography, Mrs. Barton Trafford's social engineering, the entire apparatus of literary reputation — all of it exists to manufacture what Rosie possessed naturally: the appearance of life.

She functions as an implicit critique of literary culture's deepest assumptions. The qualities that make life worth living — warmth, generosity, sexual vitality, indifference to reputation — are precisely the qualities that biography must sanitize. Kear cannot acknowledge Rosie without undermining the dignified, monumental portrait he is constructing. [022-001] Her erasure from the official record is the price of Driffield's canonization — and the novel's deepest indictment of the biographical enterprise.

## Real-Life Model

Rosie Driffield is widely believed to be modeled on **Ethelwyn Sylvia Jones**, Maugham's early love and the woman who rejected his marriage proposal. The biographical parallel adds a layer of personal investment to Ashenden's enduring affection — the narrator's unironic warmth toward Rosie may reflect Maugham's own unhealed feeling toward Jones. The novel thus becomes, in part, a private tribute embedded within a public satire.
