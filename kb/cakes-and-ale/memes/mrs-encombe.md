# Mrs. Encombe

Mrs. Encombe is a minor but vivid character — the wife of an Oxford don who visits her cousin Mrs. Greencourt in Blackstable and becomes, for one afternoon, the center of the parish's intellectual life. She is introduced as a specimen of the "New Woman" and her presence in the Blackstable drawing room catalyzes the conversation in which Edward Driffield's literary worth is first publicly debated. [008-063]

## 角色概述

Mrs. Encombe functions in the novel as a window onto the wider literary world beyond Blackstable — a world where people actually know authors, discuss books seriously, and have opinions about contemporary literature. She operates as a cultural contrast agent: her Oxford connections, her cropped gray hair, her acquaintance with Mrs. Humphry Ward and Ouida all mark her as belonging to a different order of being than the provincial gentility of Blackstable. [008-063]

Her most significant narrative contribution is the question "Can he write?" [008-074] — asked of Edward Driffield when his name is raised. This question, coming from someone with literary connections, implicitly legitimizes Driffield as a subject worthy of serious discussion, even as the Blackstable establishment rushes to dismiss him on class grounds.

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Mrs. Encombe is introduced at Mrs. Greencourt's tea party, where she immediately disrupts Blackstable's visual and social norms:

> She was a Mrs. Encombe, a little woman with an eager wrinkled face; she surprised us very much because she wore her gray hair short and a black serge skirt that only just came down below the tops of her square-toed boots. She was the first example of the New Woman that had even been seen in Blackstable. [008-063]

The narrator's use of "we" — the collective Blackstable consciousness — emphasizes the insularity against which Mrs. Encombe's modernity is measured.

### 关键场景

**The literary name-drop** — Mrs. Encombe's conversation immediately establishes her as someone who moves in literary circles, impressing the Blackstable company:

> We all found it very interesting to hear Mrs. Encombe talk of the people she knew. My uncle had been at Oxford, but everyone he asked about seemed to be dead. Mrs. Encombe knew Mrs. Humphry Ward and admired *Robert Elsmere*. [008-064]

**The argument over Robert Elsmere** — Mrs. Encombe's appreciation of Mrs. Humphry Ward's controversial novel brings her into direct theological conflict with the vicar:

> My uncle considered it a scandalous work, and he was surprised that Mr. Gladstone, who at least called himself a Christian, had found a good word to say for it. They had quite an argument about it. [008-064]

Mrs. Encombe's defense — that Mrs. Humphry Ward was "a woman of the very highest character" who "had written it from the very highest motives" [008-065] — shows a sophistication the vicar cannot match: she judges art by intention and character, not doctrinal conformity.

**The Ouida revelation** — In the novel's most deliciously subversive moment, Mrs. Encombe reveals she has read Ouida and met her in Florence. When Mrs. Hayforth declares Ouida unfit for ladies, Mrs. Encombe responds with the qualifications of a true connoisseur:

> "I read one out of curiosity," said Mrs. Encombe. "I must say, it's more what you'd expect from a Frenchman than from an English gentlewoman." [008-069]

**"Can he write?"** — When Mr. Galloway introduces the subject of Edward Driffield, and the major dismisses him with the standard formula ("son of old Miss Wolfe's bailiff and he married a barmaid"), Mrs. Encombe asks the one question that cuts through class prejudice:

> "Can he write?" asked Mrs. Encombe. [008-074]

This is, in a sense, the novel's central question — and Mrs. Encombe is the only person in the Blackstable drawing room to ask it.

## 人物关系

- **与 Mrs. Greencourt**：Her cousin, with whom she stays in Blackstable. Mrs. Greencourt's social standing is complicated by her family's china-manufacturing origins, but her Oxford-don connection through Mrs. Encombe elevates her. [008-063]
- **与 the Vicar (Ashenden's uncle)**：They engage in a spirited argument about *Robert Elsmere*, revealing the gap between Oxford nostalgia and current intellectual life. [008-064]
- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：She never meets Driffield, but her question "Can he write?" is the novel's most direct articulation of the criterion by which a writer should be judged — not class, not manners, but literary ability. [008-074]
- **与 [[mrs-hayforth]]**：Her interlocutor in the debate about Ouida and the propriety of fiction. Their exchange dramatizes the tension between Victorian moralism and the emerging modern sensibility. [008-065] [008-069]

## 人物分析

Mrs. Encombe embodies what Blackstable lacks: a worldliness rooted in genuine intellectual engagement rather than social climbing. Unlike Lady Hodmarsh, who collects artists for status, Mrs. Encombe reads books, forms opinions, and meets authors out of curiosity. Her cropped hair and short skirt announce her modernity visually before she speaks, but her conversation reveals the substance behind the style.

Her question "Can he write?" [008-074] is the novel's moral compass in miniature. In a drawing room where the conversation has been dominated by class markers (bailiff's son, barmaid wife, chapel people), Mrs. Encombe asks the only question that should matter about a novelist. That the question is asked by an outsider — a visitor who will leave Blackstable — underscores the novel's thesis that literary judgment requires freedom from provincial social constraints.

## 可能的现实原型

Mrs. Encombe may be modelled on one of the many academic wives Maugham encountered through his Oxford and literary connections. Her acquaintance with Mrs. Humphry Ward places her in the late-Victorian literary milieu. The "New Woman" type — educated, independently minded, visually distinct from traditional femininity — was a recognized social phenomenon of the 1890s, and Mrs. Encombe's portrait is consistent with contemporary depictions.
