# Mrs. Reeves

Mrs. Reeves is a minor background figure in the social history of Blackstable — the landlady of the Railway Arms, the modest public house opposite the railway station where Rosie Gann (later Rosie Driffield) worked as a barmaid before her marriage. Her decision to dismiss Rosie for the scandal involving Lord George Kemp is a pivotal moment in Rosie's biography, narrated retrospectively by Mary-Ann. [005-081]

## 角色概述

Mrs. Reeves appears in the novel only through reported speech — she never enters the narrative directly. Her significance lies in her role as a guardian of respectability who is ultimately powerless to maintain order in the face of the disruption Rosie brings. The Railway Arms, "a very modest little public house" with "a sort of sinister gaiety," [005-081] is the setting for the social transgression that defines Rosie's pre-marital history.

Mrs. Reeves embodies the respectable working-class morality that Rosie's vitality continually breaches. Her decision to dismiss Rosie is presented by Mary-Ann as an act of practical housekeeping — the scandal has grown too large to contain — and the narration leaves ambiguous whether Mrs. Reeves acted from moral disapproval or simply from the desire to restore order to her establishment.

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Mrs. Reeves enters the narrative through Mary-Ann's account of Rosie's early years, delivered while washing dishes at the vicarage. The narrator has asked whether it is true that Rosie was a barmaid:

> "Yes, at the Railway Arms and then at the Prince of Wales's Feathers at Haversham. Mrs. Reeves 'ad her to 'elp in the bar at the Railway Arms, but it got so bad she had to get rid of her." [005-081]

The phrase "it got so bad" captures the essence of the story: Rosie's attractiveness, Lord George's pursuit, the scandal, and Mrs. Reeves's threshold of tolerance.

### 关键场景

**The Railway Arms described** — The narrator briefly sketches the setting Mrs. Reeves presided over:

> The Railway Arms was a very modest little public house just opposite the station of the London, Chatham & Dover Railway. It had a sort of sinister gaiety. On a winter's night as you passed by you saw the light within and the air was warm and heavy with hops. [005-081]

**The scandal and dismissal** — Mary-Ann's account of why Mrs. Reeves acted:

> And the talk it made. Well, it got so Mrs. Reeves said she wasn't going to put up with it another day and she gave her her wages and told her to pack her box and go. Good riddance to bad rubbish, that's what I said. [005-084]

The phrase "she wasn't going to put up with it another day" presents Mrs. Reeves as practical rather than moralistic — a businesswoman who recognizes when a situation has become unmanageable. Mary-Ann's appended judgment ("Good riddance to bad rubbish") should not be conflated with Mrs. Reeves's own attitude; the narrative carefully distinguishes the two voices.

**Lord George intervenes** — After the dismissal, the story continues beyond Mrs. Reeves's jurisdiction:

> "All I can tell you," said Mary-Ann, "is that when Mrs. Reeves gave her the sack Lord George got her a job at the Prince of Wales's Feathers at Haversham and he was always poppin' over there in his trap." [005-102]

Mrs. Reeves's dismissal thus has the opposite of its intended effect — rather than removing Rosie from the scene, it merely transfers the problem to another establishment, where Lord George's pursuit continues unabated.

## 人物分析

Mrs. Reeves is a structural figure — she represents the boundary of respectable working-class propriety that Rosie's erotic magnetism continually violates. Her arc in the narrative (employ → tolerate → dismiss) traces the arc of Rosie's effect on ordered society: Rosie enters a settled establishment, generates disturbance beyond the establishment's capacity to absorb, and is expelled — only to reappear elsewhere, unchanged.

The novel's treatment of Mrs. Reeves is notably neutral. She is neither villainized for dismissing Rosie nor celebrated for having employed her. Her actions are presented as the reasonable response of a businesswoman to an untenable situation. In the moral economy of *Cakes and Ale*, this practical neutrality reads as a form of decency — she does what needs doing without cruelty or moral grandstanding.

## 可能的现实原型

Mrs. Reeves is likely based on a publican known to Maugham from his childhood in Whitstable (the model for Blackstable). The Railway Arms corresponds to a real public house near the railway station. Given the roman à clef nature of the novel, she may be a lightly fictionalized portrait of an actual landlady.
