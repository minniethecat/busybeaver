# Amy Driffield

Amy Driffield is Edward Driffield's second wife — a former nurse who married the aging novelist and, after his death, became the vigilant guardian of his literary reputation. It is she who commissions Alroy Kear's biography, setting the entire plot in motion with a letter to Ashenden. [004-001]

## Role Overview

Where Rosie Driffield represents the unvarnished life that literary respectability must erase, Amy Driffield represents the machinery of erasure itself. She has dedicated herself to making Driffield "presentable" — managing his image, controlling access to his memory, and ensuring that the official version of his life conforms to the dignity expected of a "distinguished man of letters." [011-062]

## Appearances in the Novel

### First Appearance

Amy enters the novel through her letter to Ashenden, written after she learns he has been talking with Kear about Driffield: "I hear that you had a long talk with Roy last week about Edward Driffield and I am so glad to know that you spoke of him so nicely." [004-001] The letter's tone — warm, solicitous, carefully managed — establishes her character immediately.

### Key Scenes

1. **The Letter** — Her commissioning of the biography through a combination of flattery and moral suasion. She positions herself as the devoted widow seeking only to honor her husband's memory, while exercising firm control over what that memory will include. [004-001]

2. **The Sanitization** — In conversation with Ashenden, she reveals her embarrassment at Driffield's pub-going habits: "he was rather fond of going down to the Bear and Key at Blackstable and having a few beers in the public bar … it did make him rather conspicuous." [011-062] She wants the biography to omit such details.

3. **Managing the Past** — She refers to Driffield's financial troubles with careful euphemism: "I understand everything was paid up later … I don't think it's necessary to dwell on an incident that is not really of any importance in the history of his development." [011-060] Her instinct is always to smooth, to edit, to protect.

## Relationships

- **With [[edward-driffield]]**: She married him late in his life, serving as nurse-companion before becoming his wife. Her devotion is genuine but inseparable from her investment in his public standing. Kear describes her as having "done a great deal for him" — "made him quite presentable." [004-076]
- **With [[alroy-kear]]**: She commissions and collaborates with Kear on the biography, forming an alliance of mutual interest. Both are invested in producing a dignified portrait that will enhance — not complicate — Driffield's reputation. [004-001]
- **With [[rosie-driffield]]**: The contrast is structural. Amy is everything Rosie is not — respectable, controlled, reputation-conscious. The novel never stages a direct confrontation, but the implied comparison drives its satirical edge.

## Character Analysis

Amy Driffield is a more complex figure than she first appears. Her protectiveness is both touching and suspect — she genuinely loved Driffield, but her love takes the form of erasing the qualities that made him interesting. She is Maugham's portrait of how literary widows become, often unwittingly, agents of biographical sanitization.

Her background as a nurse is significant: she tends the patient (Driffield) but also manages him, a dynamic that carries over into her widowhood. She tends his reputation with the same clinical efficiency, suppressing symptoms (the pub visits, the financial embarrassments) that threaten the health of the public image.

## Real-Life Model

Amy Driffield may be loosely based on **Florence Hardy**, Thomas Hardy's second wife, who was also his former secretary and who fiercely guarded his legacy after his death. The parallel is deliberately blurred — Maugham was writing about a type, not an individual.
