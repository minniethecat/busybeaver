# The Vicar's Wife

The vicar's wife is Ashenden's maternal aunt, a German woman of noble descent who has spent more than thirty years married to a modest English country parson. She is a minor but carefully drawn figure whose presence establishes the household atmosphere of the Blackstable vicarage — a blend of impoverished gentility, social conservatism, and quiet domestic routine that forms the backdrop to Ashenden's childhood encounters with the Driffields. [003-003]

## 角色概述

The vicar's wife is introduced in the opening of Chapter III with a passage that economically captures her entire social position:

> My aunt was a German. She came of a very noble but impoverished family, and the only portion she brought her husband was a marquetry writing desk, made for an ancestor in the Seventeenth Century, and a set of tumblers. ... She was a simple old lady, of a meek and Christian disposition, but she had not, though married for more than thirty years to a modest parson with very little income beyond his stipend, forgotten that she was hochwohlgeboren. [003-003]

Her role is domestic and supervisory rather than dramatic. She takes tea with Galloway, monitors Ashenden's social contacts, and provides the quiet female presence in a household dominated (in theory) by the vicar's moral authority. Yet her aristocratic standards prove more inflexible than her husband's: when the banker calls on the vicar, it is she who "refused to do so because he was in trade." [003-003]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

She appears in the opening paragraphs of Chapter III, immediately after the setting is established:

> She was a simple old lady, of a meek and Christian disposition, but she had not, though married for more than thirty years to a modest parson with very little income beyond his stipend, forgotten that she was hochwohlgeboren. [003-003]

### 关键场景

**The banker's son** — When young Ashenden asks if he may bring the banker's son home, his aunt demurs:

> My aunt said I'd be wanting to go to the coal merchant's next. [003-003]

The line is revealing: her social philosophy is a ladder of exclusion, each rung justified by the one below it. She is not cruel, merely certain of distinctions that to her are as natural as breathing.

**Tea at the vicarage** — When Galloway visits, "she asked him to stay to tea," [003-011] a gesture of routine hospitality that places her in the domestic management of the vicarage's social life. Her question about Rosie Driffield — "Is it true that she was a barmaid at the Railway Arms?" [003-018] — shows the same concern with social classification, now focused on the novel's most disruptive social category: the barmaid who married a writer.

**The bicycle** — In Chapter V, she appears briefly as the voice of maternal caution: "though my aunt was against it, since she said I should only break my neck." [005-001] Her concern is practical and bodily rather than moral — a contrast to her husband's interest in policing Ashenden's reading material.

**Ashenden's social anxiety** — When the Driffields suggest riding back together after the bicycle lesson, Ashenden's fear of being seen reflects his aunt's social code internalized:

> It was just the time that my aunt and uncle would be coming home from their daily walk down the town and I did not like to run the risk of being seen with people whom they would not at all approve of. [005-027]

## 人物关系

- **与 [[ashenden]]**：She is his guardian during the summer holidays, providing domestic care and quiet affection within a framework of strict social rules. Her influence on Ashenden is evidenced by his internalization of her class anxieties.
- **与 [[vicar-blackstable]]**：Her husband of more than thirty years. Their marriage is one of gentle mutual accommodation — his laziness and her domestic diligence, his occasional social flexibility and her aristocratic rigidity.
- **与 [[galloway]]**：The curate is a regular visitor to the vicarage and is received by her with routine kindness.
- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：She shares the vicar's disapproval of the Driffields, though her concern is social (the barmaid wife) rather than moral or literary.

## 人物分析

The vicar's wife is a fine example of Maugham's gift for economy: she appears in perhaps a dozen sentences across the novel, yet the reader carries a clear sense of her. The detail of the marquetry writing desk — "made for an ancestor in the Seventeenth Century" — does the work of several paragraphs of exposition. She is defined by her possessions and her lineage because those are the only currencies remaining to an impoverished noblewoman married to a country parson.

The "hochwohlgeboren" passage is crucial. Her meekness and Christian disposition are genuine — "a simple old lady" — but the German honorific marks the persistence of a different system of value, one based on birth rather than piety or income. Maugham presents this not as hypocrisy but as a simple fact of character. "No one thought her a snob. It was accepted as perfectly reasonable." [003-003] The novel's genius for social observation lies precisely here: in showing that snobbery, when sufficiently embedded in custom and softened by personal kindness, can pass for natural order.

## 可能的现实原型

She may be partially drawn from the wife of Maugham's uncle Henry MacDonald Maugham, the vicar of Whitstable, with whom young Willie lived after his mother's death. The German connection may be invented or recombined from other relatives; Maugham had extensive German family connections through his father's legal work and his own education at Heidelberg.
