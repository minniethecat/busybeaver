# The Quarterly Review

**The Quarterly Review** (referred to familiarly as "the Quarterly") is a literary periodical that appears in the novel as a marker of critical respectability. It is in its pages that Mr. Barton Trafford, husband of [[mrs-barton-trafford]], published the article that helped transform [[edward-driffield]] from a controversial novelist into a critically recognized figure.

## Role in the Novel

The Quarterly is one of the institutional organs through which literary reputation is manufactured. Mrs. Barton Trafford arranges for an "important article on him for the Quarterly Review" [014-014], and though the article's publication is delayed — "this wasn't quite the moment for Barton's article in the Quarterly" [018-006] — when it finally appears, it consolidates Driffield's standing.

The timing is significant: the Quarterly article comes after the controversy over _The Cup of Life_ has subsided, marking the transition from notoriety to respectability. It serves as a kind of institutional endorsement — the literary equivalent of a seal of approval from an established authority.

## Symbolic Function

The Quarterly Review represents the institutional machinery of literary reputation that the novel continually satirizes. It is one of the organs — alongside the [[athenaeum]] (the periodical, not the club), the [[the-times]], and the literary salons of [[chelsea]] — through which a writer's career is managed, promoted, and eventually canonized.
