# Mrs. Hayforth

Mrs. Hayforth is a minor character in *Cakes and Ale* who appears in a single scene — the drawing-room literary discussion at Mrs. Greencourt's tea party in Chapter VIII. As the wife of the Blackstable doctor, she occupies a secure position in the parish's professional class and speaks with the authority of common sense rather than intellectual pretense. Her defense of popular fiction against Mrs. Encombe's moral scrutiny represents a distinctive middlebrow voice in the novel's chorus of literary opinions. [008-065]

## 角色概述

Mrs. Hayforth functions as the voice of unpretentious literary enjoyment in the Blackstable drawing room. Where the vicar judges books by their doctrinal conformity, Mrs. Encombe by their author's character, and the major by their author's class, Mrs. Hayforth judges simply by whether she enjoys them. Her plain-spoken defense of the novels of Rhoda Broughton — "I enjoy them, especially *Red as a Rose Is She*" [008-065] — is the most honest literary judgment offered in the entire scene.

Her practical morality around reading is embodied in a single, wonderfully domestic distinction: she would not give certain novels to her unmarried daughters "just yet perhaps," but "when they're married I should have no objection." [008-067] This is not hypocrisy but a sincere, if comically pragmatic, application of Victorian double standards to literary consumption.

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Mrs. Hayforth enters the conversation at the point where Mrs. Encombe has been discussing her literary acquaintances and opinions. The two women represent contrastive models of cultural authority — Mrs. Encombe the Oxford-connected intellectual, Mrs. Hayforth the provincial doctor's wife who reads for pleasure:

> "I don't see any harm in them," said Mrs. Hayforth, the doctor's wife. "I enjoy them, especially *Red as a Rose Is She*." [008-065]

The phrase "I don't see any harm in them" is the key to her character — she trusts her own experience over doctrinal or critical argument.

### 关键场景

**The daughters test** — When Mrs. Encombe challenges her with the moral test of whether she would let her daughters read such books, Mrs. Hayforth's answer is a masterpiece of pragmatic compromise:

> "Not just yet perhaps," said Mrs. Hayforth. "But when they're married I should have no objection." [008-067]

The logic is impeccably Victorian: marriage transforms a woman's relationship to knowledge of the world, and literature that is unsuitable for an innocent girl becomes permissible for a married woman. Mrs. Hayforth states this cultural assumption without irony or apology.

**The Ouida line** — When Mrs. Encombe reveals that she was introduced to Ouida in Florence, Mrs. Hayforth draws a firm line:

> "That's quite another matter," returned Mrs. Hayforth. "I can't believe that any lady would read a book by Ouida." [008-069]

This is the limit of her literary tolerance. Rhoda Broughton is acceptable (if deferred for unmarried daughters); Ouida is beyond the pale. The distinction is not argued but declared — a social fact rather than a literary judgment. The narrator offers no comment, allowing the absurdity of the distinction to speak for itself.

## 人物关系

- **与 [[mrs-encombe]]**：Her primary interlocutor in the drawing-room debate. Mrs. Hayforth provides the commonsensical counterpoint to Mrs. Encombe's intellectual name-dropping. [008-065] [008-069]
- **与 the Vicar (Ashenden's uncle)**：Present at the tea party but largely silent during the literary debate. The vicar's theological objection to *Robert Elsmere* [008-064] represents a different pole of literary judgment than Mrs. Hayforth's practical hedonism.
- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：Present when Driffield is discussed but offers no comment on him directly. Her silence when the conversation shifts from literature to the local author is consistent with her character — she has no opinion on Driffield because she has not read him, and unlike the others, she would not pretend otherwise.

## 人物分析

Mrs. Hayforth is the novel's most sympathetic portrait of the common reader — the person who reads for pleasure, trusts her own judgment, and refuses to be bullied by intellectual fashion. Her "I enjoy them" is a radical statement in a room full of people who judge books by criteria other than enjoyment. In the moral economy of *Cakes and Ale*, where authenticity is the highest virtue, Mrs. Hayforth's unapologetic hedonism reads as a kind of integrity.

At the same time, Maugham's irony is unmistakable. Mrs. Hayforth's willingness to censor her daughters' reading while enjoying the same books herself is a perfect miniature of Victorian hypocrisy. And her categorical rejection of Ouida reveals the arbitrary nature of literary respectability — Rhoda Broughton is acceptable, Ouida is not, and Mrs. Hayforth could not explain why, because the distinction is social rather than literary.

## 可能的现实原型

Mrs. Hayforth, like most of the Blackstable characters, is likely drawn from Maugham's memories of Whitstable society. The doctor's wife as a type — educated enough to read novels, respectable enough to enforce moral boundaries — was a familiar figure in provincial English life.
