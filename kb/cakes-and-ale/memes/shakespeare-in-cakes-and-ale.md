# Shakespeare in Cakes and Ale

[[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]] functions in *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* not as a character or a plot element, but as an always-present standard of comparison — the ultimate measure of literary greatness against which [[edward-driffield]]'s achievement is implicitly and explicitly judged. Shakespeare appears in the novel as a name invoked, a quotation half-remembered, and a cultural presence that defines the highest aspirations of English literature.

## In the Novel

Shakespeare is referenced six times across the novel, most often in the context of discussions about literary greatness. The references cluster around two poles: the biography project's rhetoric of celebration, and Ashenden's more sceptical meditations on the nature of literary reputation.

### Shakespeare as the Ultimate Benchmark

When the characters in *Cakes and Ale* reach for the highest conceivable literary comparison, they reach for Shakespeare. This rhetorical gesture is itself a satiric target. The novel is set in a literary world that routinely invokes Shakespeare while producing — and celebrating — work of considerably lesser stature.

The implicit question that runs through the Novel is: what would a biography of Shakespeare look like if written by an Alroy Kear? The answer — a monument of pious cliché that erases the messy humanity of the life — is precisely the fate that the novel's frame narrative is designed to resist.

### Shakespeare and Driffield

The comparison between Driffield and Shakespeare is never made directly by the narrator, but it hovers over the novel's treatment of literary reputation. Driffield's posthumous transformation — from bohemian writer living in obscure Kentish retirement to Grand Old Man of English Letters — echoes the cultural process by which Shakespeare, the Stratford glover's son, became "the Bard."

The difference, of course, is that Shakespeare's biography remains largely unknown — a blank that generations of scholars have filled with conjecture — while Driffield's has been deliberately suppressed by those closest to him. In both cases, the "official" story serves institutional needs rather than truth.

## Thematic Significance

Shakespeare's presence in the novel is part of Maugham's extended meditation on literary reputation. The novel asks: what does it mean to be "great"? Is literary greatness a property of the work, or a construction of the literary establishment? If Shakespeare were alive today, would he recognize himself in the biographies written about him?

The Shakespeare references also connect to the Novel's title. "Cakes and ale" is itself a Shakespearean phrase — from *Twelfth Night* (Act II, Scene iii), where Sir Toby Belch rebukes Malvolio's puritanism: "Dost thou think, because thou art virtuous, there shall be no more cakes and ale?" The title thus embeds the novel's central argument in a Shakespearean gesture: that life's simple pleasures are not cancelled by the claims of virtue or greatness.
