# By Their Fruits

***By Their Fruits*** is a novel by [[edward-driffield|Edward Driffield]], begun in the aftermath of the success of *[[the-cup-of-life|The Cup of Life]]* and finished under the patronage of [[mrs-barton-trafford|Mrs. Barton Trafford]], the literary hostess who had taken charge of Driffield's career and reputation. It occupies a significant position in the novel's anatomy of literary career-making: the book is explicitly presented as a collaborative production between the novelist and his patroness, blurring the line between artistic creation and social engineering.

## Composition and Publication

The novel was begun approximately six months after the excitement over *The Cup of Life* had subsided [019-001]. The narrator [[ashenden]], then a surgical dresser at [[st-lukes-hospital|St. Luke's Hospital]], receives a telegram at the hospital summoning him to Driffield's bedside — the event that frames the novel's composition within the narrative.

The novel was completed in Mrs. Barton Trafford's flat, a detail Maugham presents with characteristic ambiguity. "It was in her flat that he finished *By Their Fruits*. She was justified in looking upon it as her book and the dedication to her is a proof that Driffield was not unmindful of his debt" [022-006]. The phrase "justified in looking upon it as her book" is carefully balanced: it registers Mrs. Trafford's proprietorial claim while neither endorsing nor refuting it.

## Mrs. Barton Trafford's Patronage

The completion of *By Their Fruits* marks the high point of Mrs. Barton Trafford's management of Driffield's career. The narrator describes her as possessing "a heart of gold with an unerring eye for the main chance" [022-006] — a characterisation that captures the novel's ambivalent treatment of literary patronage. Mrs. Trafford's kindness and her careerism are inseparable; her nurturing of Driffield's art is also an investment in his market value.

The dedication of *By Their Fruits* to Mrs. Trafford is cited as evidence that "Driffield was not unmindful of his debt" [022-006]. The wording is delicately evasive: a dedication acknowledges an obligation, but whether that obligation is artistic, financial, or emotional is left deliberately unclear. The novel's title — "by their fruits ye shall know them" (Matthew 7:16) — carries an ironic resonance in this context. The "fruit" of the Driffield-Trafford collaboration is a novel whose authorship is shared between the named writer and the unnamed patroness who made its completion possible.

## Aftermath: Italy and Ruskin

Having secured the dedication, Mrs. Trafford proceeded to the next stage of her programme: cultural education. "She took him to Italy (with Barton of course, for Mrs. Trafford knew too well how malicious people were, to give occasion for scandal) and with a volume of Ruskin in her hand revealed to Edward Driffield the immortal beauties of that country" [022-006].

The image of Mrs. Trafford leading Driffield through Italian galleries with Ruskin as her guidebook is one of the novel's most compact satirical tableaux. Driffield, the self-taught writer from Blackstable who had spent his formative years in Kentish pubs and seaside lodging houses, is being systematically improved — his rough edges smoothed, his cultural gaps filled, his marketability enhanced. The Ruskin detail is particularly precise: Ruskin was the great Victorian arbiter of artistic taste, and Mrs. Trafford's deployment of him positions her as the intermediary between Driffield's raw talent and the cultivated audience she is preparing him for.

## Position in Driffield's Oeuvre

*By Their Fruits* belongs to the middle period of Driffield's career — after the breakthrough of *The Cup of Life* and before the late masterpieces. It represents the moment when Driffield ceased to be a struggling provincial writer and began to be a literary property, managed and promoted by the machinery of metropolitan patronage.

The novel is not, within Maugham's fiction, presented as one of Driffield's greatest achievements. It is a transitional work, notable less for its intrinsic merit than for the circumstances of its production. The narrative treats it as evidence of how literary careers are actually made — not through the solitary labour of genius but through the intersecting interests of patrons, hostesses, critics, and publishers.

## Thematic Significance

*By Their Fruits* embodies the novel's critique of the literary establishment's machinery. The title's biblical echo — "by their fruits ye shall know them" — is doubly ironic when applied to a novel whose "fruits" include a dedication bought with patronage and an Italian tour conducted with Ruskin. The question the novel implicitly poses is: whose fruits are these? Driffield's, Mrs. Trafford's, or the literary system's?

In the context of [[alroy-kear|Alroy Kear's]] later biographical project, *By Their Fruits* would represent precisely the kind of inconvenient history that *[[the-life-and-letters|The Life and Letters of Edward Driffield]]* must sanitise. The official biography cannot acknowledge that one of Driffield's novels was substantially the product of his patroness's ambition — any more than it can acknowledge his first wife's lovers. Both truths belong to the skeleton in the cupboard.
