# The Royal Academy of Arts

The **Royal Academy of Arts** appears in *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* not as an institution that produces or judges art, but as the setting for a particular kind of social performance: the private view, where London's literary and artistic world gathers not to see the paintings but to be seen seeing them. Maugham's treatment of the Academy is characteristically brief and devastating — two references that together constitute a miniature satire of culture as social credential.

## Literary Appearances

### The Private View

The Academy is first mentioned in the novel's opening chapter, in Ashenden's anatomy of [[alroy-kear]]'s social talents:

> "At the private view of the Royal Academy no one could be more cordial." [001-040]

The line is placed among a catalogue of Kear's social virtues — his handling of interviewers, his attentiveness to the wives of important men, his careful cultivation of everyone who might advance his career. The private view is, in this context, simply another venue for the performance of cordiality that is Kear's profession. It does not matter what paintings are on the walls; what matters is that Kear is seen being cordial in front of them.

### Amy Driffield's Exhaustion

The Academy appears a second time in connection with [[amy-driffield]], Edward's second wife, during the narrator's visit to [[ferne-court]]:

> "She told me of the terrible crowd at the Royal Academy. It had taken her two visits to see the pictures." [004-032]

The detail is exquisite in its precision. Amy complains of the "terrible crowd" — the other people who, like her, have come to perform cultural engagement — and notes that it took "two visits" to see the pictures. The implication is that the first visit was consumed by the social obligations that the private view exists to facilitate; only on the second could she actually look at the art.

## Institutional Significance

The Royal Academy was, in Maugham's era, the pre-eminent institution of the British art establishment. Founded in 1768 by George III, it occupied Burlington House on Piccadilly and held an annual Summer Exhibition that was a fixture of the London social season. To be elected a Royal Academician was the highest professional honour available to a British artist; to attend the private view was to participate in an event where art and society intersected.

Maugham's satire targets not the Academy itself but the social uses to which it is put. The private view is, in his rendering, a mechanism for converting cultural capital into social capital — a place where Kear can be "cordial" and Amy can be seen, where the paintings are an alibi for the networking that is the real business of the afternoon.

## The Novel's Implicit Critique

Maugham does not argue that the Royal Academy is fraudulent or that its exhibitions are without merit. He simply records how it is used — by Kear, by Amy — and lets the reader draw the inference. The critique is not of art institutions but of the people who treat them as social furniture, who attend the private view without looking at the pictures, who measure the event by the "crowd" rather than by anything on the walls.

This is consistent with the novel's larger argument: that the apparatus of culture — the academies, the journals, the private views, the prize committees — is structurally indifferent to what it purports to serve. It is a machine for producing reputations, and it will produce them with equal efficiency for a Driffield, a Kear, or anyone else who shows up and stays long enough.
