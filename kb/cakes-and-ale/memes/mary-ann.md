# Mary-Ann

Mary-Ann is a servant in Blackstable whose gossip about Rosie Driffield provides one of the novel's most vivid passages. Her voice — morally censorious, richly colloquial, and utterly compelling — represents the provincial public opinion that both condemns and preserves the scandalous truth about the Driffields. [005-084]

## Role Overview

Mary-Ann functions as a vernacular chorus — the voice of the community that witnessed the Driffield scandal firsthand and remembers it in granular, unflattering detail. Her gossip is the raw material that the official biography must suppress: where Kear will write elegant euphemisms, Mary-Ann delivers unvarnished judgment.

## Appearances in the Novel

### The Gossip

Young Ashenden overhears Mary-Ann's account of Rosie's infidelities — a passage remarkable for its energy and specificity: "There wasn't a man who come in to 'ave a drink that she didn't carry on with. No matter who they was. She couldn't stick to anybody, it was just one man after another. They tell me it was simply 'orrible." [005-084] The moral fervor is inseparable from the pleasure of the telling — Mary-Ann relishes the scandal she condemns.

### The Raspberry Tart

Mary-Ann is also associated with domestic warmth: "It was a beautiful raspberry tart. Mary-Ann made short pastry that melted in the mouth." [005-062] The detail is characteristic of Maugham's method — the same voice that relays scandal also bakes excellent pastry. The world is not divided into the virtuous and the vicious; it is all mixed together.

### The Daily Round

She appears in passing as part of Blackstable's ordinary life: the coach-house "was used by the gardener to keep the mower and the roller, and by Mary-Ann for her sack of meal for the chickens." [005-032] She is woven into the fabric of the place — not a character in the literary sense but a presence, a voice.

## Relationships

- **With [[rosie-driffield]]**: Mary-Ann is Rosie's moral judge and inadvertent chronicler. Her gossip preserves a version of Rosie that the biography will erase — scandalous, vivid, unforgettable. [005-084]
- **With the Narrator (Ashenden)**: Young Ashenden is the audience for Mary-Ann's gossip. His memory of her voice, decades later, is a form of loyalty to the unrespectable truth.

## Character Analysis

Mary-Ann is one of Maugham's signature minor characters — a figure who appears briefly but leaves an indelible impression. Her function is to provide the novel's moral counterweight to literary sophistication. She knows nothing of reputation-making, but she knows what happened, and she tells it without euphemism.

Her voice — rendered in careful dialect — is the novel's acknowledgment that the truth about Driffield's life is preserved not in the biography but in the memories of people like Mary-Ann: servants, neighbors, gossips. The people the official record erases.
