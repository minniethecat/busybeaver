# Lord George Kemp

George Kemp — known throughout Blackstable as "Lord George" — is a prosperous coal merchant whose affair with Rosie Driffield serves as the catalyst for the collapse of her marriage. His ironic nickname captures the gap between Blackstable's provincial reality and its social aspirations. [005-085]

## Role Overview

Kemp appears in the novel only through the retrospective gossip of Blackstable's residents. He is never directly present in a scene, yet his significance is structural: his affair with Rosie is the "skeleton in the cupboard" — the scandal that the official biography must suppress and that the novel's satirical machinery works to expose.

## Appearances in the Novel

### Through Gossip

Kemp is introduced through the servant Mary-Ann's prurient account of Rosie's infidelities: "There wasn't a man who come in to 'ave a drink that she didn't carry on with. No matter who they was … That was when it begun with Lord George." [005-084] The narrator then provides his own recollection, filling in the man behind the gossip.

### Key Details

The narrator knew Kemp personally: "I knew Lord George very well. His name was George Kemp and the title by which he was always known had been given him ironically owing to his grand manner." [005-085] Kemp is no aristocrat — he is a tradesman with pretensions, a coal merchant who "dabbled in house property" and "owned a share in one or two colliers." He "lived in a new brick house that stood in its own grounds and he drove his own trap." [005-085]

## Relationships

- **With [[rosie-driffield]]**: Kemp was Rosie's lover, and their affair was notorious enough to become the talk of Blackstable. It was the final straw in her marriage to Edward Driffield — "that was when it begun" the chain of events that led to her departure. [005-084]
- **With [[edward-driffield]]**: The cuckolded husband. Kemp's affair with Rosie humiliated Driffield and contributed to the disruption of his early life — the messy, disreputable past that later biography must sanitize.

## Character Analysis

Lord George Kemp is less a character than a structural function — he represents the scandalous reality that literary reputation-making must erase. His ironic nickname is Maugham's miniature satire of English class pretension: a coal merchant called "Lord George" for putting on airs, in a town that takes such performances seriously.

The fact that Ashenden remembers him with amused affection rather than moral judgment — "I knew Lord George very well" [005-085] — aligns the narrator with the novel's broader sympathy for the unrespectable. Kemp may be a philanderer and a social climber, but he is also real in a way that Alroy Kear, for all his sincerity, is not.
