# Miss Fellows

Miss Fellows is Ashenden's landlady, appearing only in the novel's opening pages. Yet her presence is thematically charged: she is the ordinary reader whose tastes and judgments form the invisible foundation of literary reputation. [001-001]

## Role Overview

It is Miss Fellows who takes Kear's telephone message and relays it to Ashenden — literally setting the plot in motion. But her deeper function is to represent the reading public: the people who buy novels, form opinions, and confer the commercial success that enables literary careers. She has read all of Alroy Kear's books "with admiration" [001-010] — a fact that simultaneously validates Kear's success and undercuts the novel's satire of his mediocrity.

## Appearances in the Novel

### The Telephone Message

The novel opens with Miss Fellows delivering Kear's message: "Mr. Alroy Kear wished me to ring him up at once." [001-001] Her mediation — she is the relay between the two literary men — is a minor but telling detail: even the most ordinary transactions of literary life pass through the hands of readers.

### The Judgmental Gaze

When Ashenden casually brushes off the message's urgency, "She pursed her lips … Her disapproval of my casualness suggested that she had read them with admiration." [001-010] Miss Fellows takes Kear seriously because she takes novels seriously — and her disapproval of Ashenden's casualness is, implicitly, a judgment of his own literary standing.

### The Note

That evening, Ashenden writes a reply: "I scribbled a note to Miss Fellows, knocked the ashes out of my pipe…" [001-046] She recedes into the background — a functional presence, like the furniture — but her role has been established.

## Relationships

- **With the Narrator (Ashenden)**: A landlord-tenant relationship, colored by her evident disapproval of his casual attitude toward literary eminence. She represents the kind of reader who takes authors seriously — perhaps too seriously.
- **With [[alroy-kear]]**: She has never met Kear, but she has read all his books and admires them. She is the audience he writes for — the audience whose taste sustains his career.

## Character Analysis

Miss Fellows is one of Maugham's small but precise touches. She is not developed as a character — she is a sketch, a vignette — but her presence grounds the novel's abstract satire in concrete social reality. Literary reputation is not just a matter of critics and salons; it is also built on the tastes of landladies who read novels in their spare time.

Her admiration for Kear is the novel's quietest irony. If Kear is a mediocrity, what does it mean that Miss Fellows — a representative reader — cannot tell the difference? The question haunts the novel without being directly answered.

## Selected Quotations
