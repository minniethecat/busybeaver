# The Eye of the Needle

*The Eye of the Needle* is [[edward-driffield]]'s most commercially successful novel. It is a fictional work within the universe of *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]*, cited by [[alroy-kear]] as evidence that Driffield achieved both critical acclaim and popular success. The novel's sales figures — "thirty-five thousand in England and eighty thousand in America" — are offered by Kear as the ultimate vindication of Driffield's genius. [001-041]

## In the Novel

*The Eye of the Needle* is mentioned only once in *Cakes and Ale*, and the reference comes not from Driffield himself but from Alroy Kear — who, in the novel's devastating Chapter 1 portrait, deploys the figure as a rhetorical prop in his performance of modesty:

> "I know I'm not a great novelist," he will tell you. "When I compare myself with the giants I simply don't exist. I used to think that one day I should write a really great novel, but I've long ceased even to hope for that. All I want people to say is that I do my best. I do work. I never let anything slipshod get past me. I think I can tell a good story and I can create characters that ring true. And after all the proof of the pudding is in the eating: *The Eye of the Needle* sold thirty-five thousand in England and eighty thousand in America, and for the serial rights of my next book I've got the biggest terms I've ever had yet." [001-041]

Kear's ventriloquism is the passage's satiric point. He is quoting himself quoting Driffield — or perhaps inventing a Driffield who sounds remarkably like Kear. The sales figures are deployed as an argument-stopper, the marketplace's final word on literary value, in a passage that otherwise insists on the distinction between commercial success and genuine artistry.

## Significance

### Commercial vs. Literary Value

*The Eye of the Needle* occupies an ambiguous position in Maugham's satire of the literary world. The sales figures are genuinely impressive — 35,000 in England in the early twentieth century represented a substantial success — yet the novel's title, with its biblical echo ("It is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God"), hints at a moral seriousness that the sales figures seem to undercut rather than confirm.

The title itself is Maugham's joke. The biblical "eye of the needle" suggests spiritual rigour and the rejection of worldly success — precisely the values that the sales figures contradict. The novel within the novel is thus an emblem of Driffield's (and perhaps any successful writer's) divided nature: the artist who produces work of genuine quality that the marketplace devours as commodity.

### Kear's Use of the Figure

Kear's invocation of *The Eye of the Needle*'s sales is characteristic of his method throughout the novel. He presents commercial success as the innocent confirmation of artistic merit, eliding the possibility that the two might be in tension. The passage is an object lesson in the rhetoric of literary reputation management — the kind of speech that will, in [[the-life-and-letters|*The Life and Letters*]], transform Driffield from a flawed human being into a monument of English letters.
