# Westminster

**Westminster** appears in the novel as the institutional heart of London — the seat of the Abbey, Parliament, and Westminster School. It is the place where literary reputation meets official recognition, most pointedly in the campaign by Driffield's admirers to have him buried in Westminster Abbey.

## Overview

Westminster is invoked in the novel not as a residential neighborhood but as a symbol of institutional authority. When Driffield dies, "some of his admirers wanted to have him buried in Westminster Abbey" [003-041], but the Dean — "the present incumbent" — refuses, on the grounds of Driffield's irregular private life. The Abbey's refusal is a précis of the novel's argument: literary canonization is administered by men whose moral judgments are no more reliable than anyone else's, but whose institutional power makes them binding.

The narrator's own connection to Westminster is modest and practical: "On the first floor lived a master at Westminster School. I paid a pound a week for my room" [012-001]. This lodging arrangement places Ashenden literally in the shadow of one of England's great institutions, while remaining — as he always does — slightly outside it.

## Westminster Abbey

The Abbey is the novel's most concentrated symbol of the machinery of reputation. It is the national pantheon, the place where Great Englishmen are buried. The campaign to have Driffield interred there, and its failure, dramatizes the gap between literary achievement and social respectability. Driffield wrote the books; the Dean of Westminster judges the man. The novel's sympathies are unambiguous.

The episode also prefigures the biographical project that is the novel's frame. [[alroy-kear]]'s biography of Driffield is, in its way, a continuation of the Abbey campaign — an attempt to secure institutional recognition for a writer whose life resists the sanitization that recognition requires.

## Westminster School

The master at Westminster School who is Ashenden's landlord [012-001] represents a quieter, more institutional London — the world of scholarships and schoolmasters, of steady incomes and respectable obscurity. He is the counter-type to Driffield: a man whose life would satisfy the Dean of Westminster, but whose name no one will remember.
