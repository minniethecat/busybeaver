# The Collected Edition of Driffield's Works

"The collected edition of his works is in thirty-seven volumes and the last set that came up at Sotheby's sold for seventy-eight pounds. That speaks for itself." [002-067] Kear's appeal to the collected edition as self-evident proof of Driffield's greatness is one of the novel's sharpest satirical moments. A writer's worth, he implies, can be measured in volumes and auction prices.

## Role in the Novel

The collected edition is the material evidence of canonization — the physical form that literary reputation takes. Its thirty-seven volumes [002-067] are the quantitative argument for Driffield's importance, an argument that requires no reference to the actual quality of the writing. The edition is the biography before the biography: a curated, dignified object that says "greatness" without having to prove it.
