# Lady Hodmarsh

An American expatriate married to a sporting baronet, Lady Hodmarsh is a secondary character who lives near Tercanbury and serves as the catalyst for the narrator's reunion with Edward Driffield after thirty-five years. Her country-house party provides the social mechanism through which the Ferne Court luncheon — the novel's first extended encounter with the aged Driffield — takes place. [004-017]

## 角色概述

Lady Hodmarsh functions as a narrative device: an intermediary who bridges Ashenden's past association with Driffield and the novel's present-day action. She embodies the type of aristocratic literary patron who values proximity to the arts without genuine intellectual engagement. Maugham uses her to satirize the social rituals of the English literary world, where connections to celebrated writers confer status. [004-017]

She is characterized as "the clever and handsome American wife of a sporting baronet with no intelligence and charming manners," who entertains artists and writers "to relieve the tedium of domestic life." [004-017] Her parties are described as "mixed and gay," where "members of the nobility and gentry mingled with astonishment and an uneasy awe with painters, writers, and actors." [004-017]

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Lady Hodmarsh first appears in Chapter IV, introduced by Ashenden as the hostess of a country-house party near Tercanbury. The narrator is staying with her when the conversation turns to Edward Driffield, and she proposes the impromptu visit to Ferne Court:

> I happened to be staying for a long weekend not far from Tercanbury with a certain Lady Hodmarsh, the clever and handsome American wife of a sporting baronet with no intelligence and charming manners. [004-017]

### 关键场景

**Arranging the Ferne Court visit** — When Ashenden mentions his past acquaintance with Driffield, Lady Hodmarsh immediately seizes the opportunity to arrange a visit. She orchestrates the guest list with social precision, limiting it to four — herself, Ashenden, Lord Scallion, and the Duchess:

> "That would make four of us," said Lady Hodmarsh. "I don't think they could manage more than that. I'll wire to Mrs. Driffield at once." [004-018]

**At Ferne Court — admiration of the writing desk** — During the tour of Driffield's study, Lady Hodmarsh's social curiosity manifests in a telling gesture:

> We all admired the writing table and Lady Hodmarsh, when she thought no one was looking, ran her fingers along its under edge to see if it was genuine. [004-052]

This small action reveals both her materialist instincts and her insecure grasp of the cultural world she inhabits.

**Securing Ashenden's private moment with Driffield** — As the party departs, Lady Hodmarsh shows genuine social perceptiveness by ensuring Ashenden has a brief private exchange with his former acquaintance:

> But Lady Hodmarsh was a woman of tact and it must have occurred to her that I, the excuse for the party, had scarcely had a word with Edward Driffield, for at the door, enveloping me with a friendly smile, she said to him: "I was so interested to hear that you and Mr. Ashenden had known one another years and years ago. Was he a nice little boy?" [004-056]

**Loyalty to Mrs. Driffield** — In the Rolls-Royce on the way home, when the Duchess speaks dismissively of Mrs. Driffield, Lady Hodmarsh defends her:

> "What did you think of Mrs. Driffield?" asked Lady Hodmarsh. ... "She's quite nice," said Lady Hodmarsh, warmly defending a friend. [004-068] [004-072]

## 人物关系

- **与 [[edward-driffield]]**：Her celebrated neighbour at Ferne Court, whom she treats with the deference due a literary eminence. At the luncheon she "was apparently telling him how to write a novel and giving him a list of a few that he really ought to read." [004-033]
- **与 [[ashenden]]**：Her weekend guest, whose past acquaintance with Driffield she uses as the excuse for the Ferne Court excursion. She shows tact in facilitating his brief reunion with the novelist. [004-056]
- **与 [[lord-scallion]]**：A fellow house-guest whose "boundless" curiosity to see Driffield makes him her immediate ally in arranging the visit. [004-017]
- **与 [[amy-driffield]]**：She treats Amy Driffield with the careful politeness due to the wife of a celebrated man, and defends her against the Duchess's dismissive remarks. [004-072]
- **与 the Duchess**：The Duchess is described as the "star guest" of Lady Hodmarsh's party. [004-017]

## 人物分析

Lady Hodmarsh illustrates the novel's satirical treatment of literary celebrity culture — a world in which proximity to a celebrated writer is a form of social currency. She "neither read the books nor looked at the pictures of the people to whom she offered hospitality, but she liked their company and enjoyed the feeling it gave her of being in the artistic know." [004-017]

Despite her superficiality, Lady Hodmarsh is not presented as a villain. Her small acts of tact — ensuring Ashenden gets his private word with Driffield, defending Mrs. Driffield in the car — suggest genuine social intelligence. She navigates the tension between the Duchess's condescension and the literary world's pretensions with grace. In the novel's moral landscape, she occupies a middle ground: neither the hypocritical careerist (Kear) nor the authentic original (Rosie), but a competent, charming woman doing what her social position requires with reasonable skill.

The detail of her surreptitiously checking the writing desk for authenticity [004-052] echoes the novel's broader concern with authenticity and forgery — the gap between what is genuinely valuable and what merely appears so.

## 可能的现实原型

No specific real-life model for Lady Hodmarsh has been definitively identified by scholars. She represents a familiar type in Maugham's social world: the wealthy American expatriate who married into the English aristocracy and cultivated artistic circles. Given Maugham's own extensive experience in the Edwardian and interwar literary scene, she is likely a composite of several such hostesses.
