# Beauty

Beauty is one of the central themes of *Cakes and Ale*, both as an aesthetic quality and as a disruptive social force. The novel explores how physical beauty — particularly feminine beauty — unsettles conventional morality and exposes the hypocrisy of those who claim to value art while recoiling from the messy lives of artists.

## 定义与语境

In the novel, beauty operates on multiple levels: the physical beauty of Rosie Driffield, the artistic beauty of Edward Driffield's novels, and the moral beauty (or lack thereof) of the literary establishment. Maugham presents beauty as something that cannot be domesticated by polite society — it is wild, amoral, and indifferent to reputation.

## 在小说中的展开

### 首次出现

The theme of beauty is most memorably introduced through Rosie Driffield, the former barmaid who became Edward Driffield's first wife. Ashenden describes her as possessing a natural, unselfconscious beauty that captivated everyone who met her.

### 核心论述

Alroy Kear, tasked with writing Driffield's official biography, struggles with how to handle Rosie. The literary establishment wants a sanitized version of the great novelist's life, but Rosie's beauty — and the sexual freedom it represented — resists domestication.

Ashenden reflects that Rosie possessed "the quality you must allow him: beauty" [[002-081]], and that even in old age, her face retained "a radiance that wasn't instinct with beauty" [[002-083]].

The novel's deeper argument is that beauty and virtue are not aligned — that the beautiful may be promiscuous, and the virtuous may be ugly. This tension drives much of the plot's irony.

## 人物关联

- **[[rosie-driffield]]**：The primary embodiment of beauty in the novel — a woman whose physical beauty and sexual freedom scandalize the literary establishment.
- **[[edward-driffield]]**：The great novelist whose work possesses artistic beauty, even as his personal life defied convention.
- **[[ashenden]]**：The narrator who, alone among the novel's characters, recognizes that Rosie's beauty was itself a form of truth.

## 叙事功能

Beauty functions in the novel as a test of character: those who can accept beauty on its own terms (Ashenden) are vindicated; those who try to moralize it (Kear, the literary establishment) are satirized.
