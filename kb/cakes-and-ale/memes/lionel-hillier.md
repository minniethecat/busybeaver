# Lionel Hillier

**Lionel Hillier** is a painter and one of the inner circle of regular visitors to the [[edward-driffield|Driffields']] Saturday gatherings in Limpus Road. He is the third member — alongside [[quentin-forde]] and [[harry-retford]] — of the trio of "most faithful" weekly regulars [014-018]. Hillier occupies a privileged position in the novel's thematic architecture: as the artist who claims to have "made" [[rosie-driffield|Rosie's]] beauty, he gives voice to one of the novel's central preoccupations — the extent to which beauty is a natural fact or a constructed perception.

## The Painter's Eye

Hillier's role as a painter is essential to his function in the novel. Where Forde sees Rosie as an object of desire and Retford sees her as a photographic subject, Hillier claims to have actively created what others merely admire. When the narrator remarks on the transformation of Rosie from "the fresh pleasant-looking young woman I had first known at Blackstable into the lovely creature whose beauty now practically everyone acknowledged" [015-012], Hillier's response is startlingly proprietorial:

"'I can explain that to you in half a jiffy,' said Lionel Hillier. 'She was only a fresh, buxom wench when you first met her. *I* made her beauty'" [015-013].

The claim is audacious — the italics on "I" are Maugham's — and it raises questions that reverberate through the novel. Can beauty be made, or only recognised? Is Hillier's assertion the arrogance of the artist or the candour of the craftsman?

## Driffield's Mediated Gaze

One of the novel's most psychologically acute passages concerns the triangulated gaze between Hillier, [[edward-driffield]], and Rosie. The narrator observes that when visitors "complimented Edward on her looks and his eyes rested on her for a moment, mine followed his" [014-021]. The implication is subtle but striking: Driffield himself has learned to see his wife's beauty through Hillier's painterly eye. The great novelist, the man of words, requires a painter to teach him how to look at his own wife.

This moment of mediated perception is characteristic of Maugham's narrative method. The narrator sees Driffield seeing Rosie through Hillier's vision — a chain of perception that places the painter as the originating source of aesthetic comprehension.

## The Studio and the Chop

In contrast to the glamour of Forde's Savoy dinners and Kuyper's sable stoles, Hillier's courtship of Rosie is conducted in the idiom of artistic bohemianism. "She was just as well pleased to go with Lionel Hillier to his studio and eat a chop that he and she cooked between them and spend the evening talking" [015-002]. The image of the painter and the barmaid-turned-model cooking a chop together in a studio is a deliberate counterpoint to the luxury of the other admirers. Rosie's equal contentment with both registers — the Savoy and the studio chop — is precisely the quality that makes her so illegible to the moralising literary establishment.

The studio itself, with its "large divan on which he slept, and a tiny little room" [015-002], is the novel's emblem of the artistic life that runs parallel to the respectable literary world. It exists in a different London from [[alroy-kear|Kear's]] carefully managed career — a London of working painters and impecunious actors, the London that the official biography must erase.

## The Portrait

Hillier painted Rosie's portrait, and a photograph of that painting, like Retford's photographs, survives into the novel's frame-narrative present. When the elderly Ashenden comes across "a photograph of the picture that Lionel Hillier had painted of her" [025-013], it produces the same pang of memory as Retford's photographs: "That was how I best remembered her. Notwithstanding the old-fashioned gown, she was alive there and tremulous with the passion that filled her. She seemed to offer herself to the assault of love" [025-013].

The portrait is the novel's most fully realised image of Rosie — fixed in paint, mediated through Hillier's vision, surviving in a photograph of a painting, and described in Ashenden's prose. The chain of representations (woman → painting → photograph → prose description) is itself a commentary on the novel's theme of mediated truth. We never see Rosie directly; we see her only as others have seen her.

## Ashenden's Unease

The narrator's relationship with Hillier is coloured by a specific anxiety. Ashenden suspects that Hillier knows about his affair with Rosie and is privately amused: "I thought that Lionel Hillier looked at me in a quizzical way, as if he were enjoying a good joke at my expense" [015-013]. The painter — the professional observer — sees what others miss, and the narrator's fear of being seen through is a recurring note in the novel's treatment of artistic perception.

## Thematic Significance

Hillier embodies the novel's argument about the constructedness of beauty and, by extension, of literary reputation. If Hillier can "make" Rosie's beauty, then [[alroy-kear]] can "make" Driffield's greatness — and both constructions, the novel slyly insists, are equally valid and equally suspect. The painter who claims to have created beauty where others saw only a "buxom wench" is a figure for the biographer who will later claim to have discovered genius where others saw only a prolific novelist.

Hillier's confident assertion — "*I* made her beauty" — echoes through the novel long after the painter himself has vanished from the narrative. It is one of the few moments when a character explicitly articulates the novel's central epistemological question: is reality discovered or invented?
