# Lord Scallion

Lord Scallion is a young peer who appears in Chapter IV as a guest at Lady Hodmarsh's country-house party. He is introduced as a satiric sketch of the aristocratic literary dilettante — a man with "literary inclinations so violent that, instead of ruling this country as the laws of man and nature have decreed, he devoted his energy to the composition of detective novels." [004-017] His eagerness to meet Driffield provides the social momentum that pushes the Ferne Court visit from suggestion to plan.

## 角色概述

Scallion is essentially a one-scene character: he appears at Lady Hodmarsh's party, propels the visit to Ferne Court, participates in the luncheon, and delivers a few post-visit quips in the Rolls-Royce. His function is satirical: Maugham uses him to mock the English aristocracy's dilettantish engagement with the arts — a man "whose curiosity to see Driffield was boundless" [004-017] but whose interest reduces the novelist to a picturesque curiosity.

## 在小说中的出现

### 首次出场

Scallion is introduced in the sweeping paragraph that sets up the Ferne Court visit:

> But there was a young peer there, a certain Lord Scallion, with literary inclinations so violent that, instead of ruling this country as the laws of man and nature have decreed, he devoted his energy to the composition of detective novels. His curiosity to see Driffield was boundless and the moment Lady Hodmarsh made her suggestion he said it would be too divine. [004-017]

### 关键场景

**The Ferne Court luncheon** — Seated next to the vicar of Blackstable at the foot of the table, [004-033] Scallion is engaged by Amy Driffield "in an animated discussion of the plays in the London theatres and the terrible crowd at the Royal Academy." [004-032] His participation in the conversation is described through Amy's social management rather than his own substance — a carefully crafted absence that emphasizes his role as a social placeholder.

**The Rolls-Royce drive home** — Scallion contributes two arch remarks to the post-visit conversation about Driffield's table manners:

> "You didn't really expect him to eat his peas with a knife, did you?" I asked. "I wish he had," said Scallion. "It would have been so picturesque." [004-064]

When the duchess and Lady Hodmarsh debate the difficulty of eating peas with a knife, Scallion offers technical advice: "You have to spear them." [004-066] The exchange reveals the party's underlying attitude toward Driffield: they hoped for a picturesque rustic, and found instead a man with nice manners — a disappointment Scallion registers with urbane irony.

## 人物分析

Scallion is a minor but precisely aimed satiric creation. His "violent" literary inclinations, expressed through the writing of detective novels rather than the governance his station demands, is a specific jab at the interwar fashion for aristocratic crime writers. The detail that he sits beside the vicar at the luncheon table — two figures of English establishment respectability displaced by the gravitational pull of literary celebrity — adds a structural irony to the scene.

The Rolls-Royce pea-eating exchange is one of Maugham's most economical satirical set-pieces. In four lines of dialogue, it reveals the entire visiting party's condescension toward Driffield, their disappointment that he didn't conform to their expectations of a "real" working-class writer, and their complete inability to see him as anything other than a picturesque specimen.

## 可能的现实原型

No specific prototype has been identified, but Scallion belongs to a recognizable type: the aristocratic amateur detective novelist, a figure familiar in interwar British literary life. Possible partial inspirations include Lord Gorell (a peer and writer) or any number of titled literary amateurs of the period.
