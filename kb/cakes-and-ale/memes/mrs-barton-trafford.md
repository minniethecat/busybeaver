# Mrs. Barton Trafford

Mrs. Barton Trafford is the novel's portrait of the literary patroness as social engineer — a woman whose gift for friendship and cultural promotion functions as a mechanism of canon-formation. Her "extraordinary, and charming" softness of manner [014-007] masks a formidable talent for identifying and elevating writers who will enhance her own cultural standing.

## Role Overview

She is the kingmaker behind Edward Driffield's reputation. Before her intervention, Driffield was "almost too bohemian" [004-076]; after she "made him quite presentable," [004-076] he became the center of a literary salon and, eventually, the subject of a thirty-seven-volume collected edition. [002-067] Her function in the novel is to expose the social machinery behind literary greatness — the parties, the introductions, the strategic cultivation of reviewers.

## Appearances in the Novel

### First Appearance

Mrs. Barton Trafford is introduced through the narrator's recollection of her Saturday afternoon parties, where "I discovered very much to my surprise that Edward Driffield was a distinguished person." [014-005] The surprise is central: her salon transforms perception.

### Key Scenes

1. **The Saturday Salon** — Her weekly gatherings brought together critics, writers, and the culturally ambitious. "The best judges admired" Driffield's work there, and "the friends who came to his house were agreed that one of these days he would be recognized." [014-005]

2. **The Making of Driffield** — The narrator describes her campaign on Driffield's behalf in terms of military strategy: "I do not know what prodigious strategy she employed … she nobbled Jasper Gibbons." [014-011] The language of hunting and capture is deliberate — literary reputation is a form of social predation.

3. **Kear's Intimacy** — In the later timeline, Kear has "become very intimate with Mrs. Barton Trafford," [022-001] suggesting the continuous operation of the machinery she set in motion — a new generation of careerists learning from the old master.

## Relationships

- **With [[edward-driffield]]**: Her greatest "discovery." She transformed him from an obscure, bohemian figure into a literary lion. "She's done a great deal for him … she's made him quite presentable." [004-076]
- **With [[alroy-kear]]**: Kear becomes her protégé and intimate — a second-generation beneficiary of her methods. Their closeness is presented as "inevitable" given their shared investment in literary reputation-making. [022-001]
- **With the Literary World**: She is less a person than a function — the node in a network that converts private admiration into public reputation. Her "friendship with the great" [014-007] is both genuine and instrumental.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Barton Trafford is Maugham's anatomy of how literary reputations are actually built. Her defining characteristic — softness — is also her weapon: "Everything was soft about her, her voice, her smile, her laugh; her eyes, which were small and pale, had the softness of flowers." [014-007] The repetition of "soft" is ironic — this softness is harder than steel.

She represents the necessary but uncomfortable truth that artistic greatness requires social machinery to be recognized. Without her, Driffield might have died obscure. With her, he becomes a monument — but a monument built at the cost of sanitizing the messy, vital life that actually produced the work.

## Selected Quotations

> "Everything was soft about her, her voice, her smile, her laugh; her eyes, which were small and pale, had the softness of flowers; her manner was as soft as the summer rain." [014-007]

> "She's done a great deal for him. I mean, she's made him quite presentable. Alroy Kear told me that before that he was almost too bohemian." [004-076]

> "I do not know what prodigious strategy she employed, what miracles of tact, what tenderness, what exquisite sympathy, what demure blandishments; I can only surmise and admire; she nobbled Jasper Gibbons." [014-011]
