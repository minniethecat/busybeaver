# Yonkers

**Yonkers** is the suburban town north of New York City where [[rosie-driffield]] — now Rose Iggulden — settled with her husband George Kemp after emigrating from England. It provides the setting for Chapter 26 of the novel, in which Ashenden, now a successful playwright visiting New York, receives a letter from Rosie and goes to see her after an absence of many years.

## Overview

Yonkers appears only in the novel's final chapter, but its narrative weight is disproportionate to its brief appearance. It is the place where Rosie's story ends — or rather, where it continues, far from the literary biography that has been constructed around her first husband. The reunion between Ashenden and Rosie at her Yonkers home is the emotional climax of the novel, and the setting — modest, suburban, un-literary — is part of its meaning.

## The Reunion

Ashenden receives a letter addressed in a handwriting he dimly recognizes: "I received one day a letter addressed in a handwriting I could not remember whose it was" [026-002]. The letter is from Rosie, now living in Yonkers. She signs herself "Rose Iggulden (formerly Driffield)" — the parenthetical carries a lifetime of explanation. Ashenden's first reaction is reluctance — "My first impulse was to make an excuse not to see her" [026-003] — but curiosity wins out.

When he arrives, Yonkers is described with the affectionate irony Ashenden reserves for things he wants to protect:

> "Yonkers, with its winding main street, its cinema, its modest shops, might have been a High Street in a London suburb." [026-024]

The comparison to a London suburb is precise and double-edged. Yonkers is both foreign and familiar, both Rosie's new life and a continuation of her old one — like her own character, which the novel has insisted all along is singular, unchangeable, immune to circumstance.

## Symbolic Weight

Yonkers represents exile and reinvention. George Kemp "had a little unpleasantness when he left Blackstable" and "thought in a new country he'd better start with a new name" [026-013]. The name change — from Kemp to Iggulden — echoes the broader theme of self-invention that runs through the novel. Driffield reinvented himself from grammar school boy to Grand Old Man of Letters. Rosie reinvented herself from barmaid to Mrs. Iggulden of Yonkers. The novel asks: which reinvention is the more honest?

Rosie's Yonkers is comfortable, modest, and entirely without pretension — much like Rosie herself. She serves tea and hot buttered scones: "That's one thing I've never been able to do without — my tea" [026-011]. The scene's domestic ordinariness is its point. Literary biography traffics in monuments; Rosie offers scones.

## Narrative Function

Yonkers serves as the novel's closing argument. After all the London literary politics, the biographical machinery, the satire of reputation and fame, the novel ends in a suburban sitting room with tea and buttered scones. The juxtaposition is not accidental. Maugham is reminding the reader what "cakes and ale" actually means: not the solemn canonization of Great Writers, but the simple pleasures of ordinary life, embodied in a woman who once scandalized Blackstable and now lives quietly in Yonkers.
