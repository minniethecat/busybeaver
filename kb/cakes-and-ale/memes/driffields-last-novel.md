# Driffield's Last Novel

Edward Driffield's final novel represents the culmination of a literary career spanning sixty years — a career that, by its end, had produced "the collected edition of his works … in thirty-seven volumes." [002-067] The "last novel" is less a specific text than a symbolic endpoint: the final product of a life spent writing, now being curated into official reputation by Kear's biography.

## Role in the Novel

Driffield's oeuvre functions in the novel as a presence rather than a subject of analysis. We are told that "the last set that came up at Sotheby's sold for seventy-eight pounds" [002-067] and that his books have been "translated into every language of civilized people" [002-081], but we never learn their titles, their plots, or their qualities. This absence is deliberate: the novel is about reputation, not about the work that reputation is supposedly based on.

## Work and Reality

The deliberate vagueness about Driffield's actual writing is one of Maugham's sharpest satirical moves. Driffield is described in contradictory terms — he "was for long thought to write very bad English" [011-001] yet "never wrote a page that wasn't instinct with beauty" [002-083] — because the point is not to judge the work but to expose how judgment is manufactured.

## Selected Quotations
