# The Bear and Key

The Bear and Key is the Blackstable pub where Edward Driffield spent many of his later evenings — drinking beer, talking to anyone who would listen, and exasperating his second wife, who regarded the establishment as beneath the dignity of a distinguished man of letters. [023-034]

## Overview

The pub is one of the novel's key symbolic spaces. Where Mrs. Barton Trafford's salon represents the machinery of reputation-making, the Bear and Key represents its opposite: democratic, unpretentious, indifferent to literary standing. Driffield "used to be very partial to dropping in here and having his glass of bitter. Mind you, I don't say he ever got tiddly, but he used to like to sit in the bar and talk." [023-034]

## Significance

The Bear and Key is mentioned alongside the Duke of Kent [003-007] as one of Blackstable's public houses. Its significance is structural: it is the space that Amy Driffield wants erased from the official record. "Mrs. Driffield didn't half like his coming here" [023-034] — the pub is the scandal that biography must suppress.
