# Mr. Galloway

Mr. Galloway is the curate of [[blackstable]], a minor character in *[[cakes-and-ale|Cakes and Ale]]* who performs the crucial narrative function of introducing the young [[ashenden]] to [[edward-driffield]]. His appearance, manner, and social position provide an early lesson in the snobbery that pervades the novel's Kentish setting.

## Role in the Novel

### First Appearance

Galloway first appears in Chapter 3, when Ashenden — home from school for the summer holidays — encounters him on the High Street:

> "Passing the bank, I called in to say how-do-you-do to the manager, who was my uncle's churchwarden, and when I came out met my uncle's curate. He stopped and shook hands with me. He was walking with a stranger. He did not introduce me to him." [003-008]

The stranger, whom the young Ashenden immediately sets down as "a cad" because of his bright brown knickerbocker suit and billycock hat, is Edward Driffield. The curate's failure to make introductions is the novel's first instance of the social barrier that separates the vicarage from the bohemian writer.

### At the Vicarage

Galloway comes to the vicarage that afternoon on parish business, and is described in detail:

> "The curate's name was Galloway; he was a tall thin ungainly man with untidy black hair and a small sallow dark face. I suppose he was quite young, but to me he seemed middle-aged. He talked very quickly and gesticulated a great deal. This made people think him rather queer and my uncle would not have kept him but that he was very energetic, and my uncle, being extremely lazy, was glad to have someone to take so much work off his shoulders." [003-011]

The portrait is characteristic of Maugham's minor characters: vivid, economical, faintly cruel. Galloway's social awkwardness and excessive energy mark him as a figure of gentle comedy, but his connection to Driffield proves decisive.

### The Introduction

It is Galloway who brings Driffield into the vicarage's orbit. When he mentions that the man in the knickerbocker suit was the writer Edward Driffield, the young Ashenden's curiosity is piqued. The curate's casual remark — "They've taken one of those houses in that street where the Congregational chapel is" — establishes the geography of the novel's central relationship. [003-012]

Through the curate, the vicarage boy and the bohemian writer are brought into contact. The bicycle lessons, the sailing trips, and the friendship that will provide the novel's emotional core all flow from this initial, unremarkable encounter on a Blackstable street, chaperoned by a gesticulating young clergyman.

## Character Function

Galloway is a functional character — a narrative hinge rather than a developed individual — but his presence illuminates two themes:

### Class and Social Boundaries

The curate's failure to introduce Driffield to Ashenden on the street reflects Blackstable's rigid social code. Driffield, in his loud knickerbockers, is visibly not a gentleman, and introductions across class lines are not casually made. The curate's subsequent visit to the vicarage — and his mention of Driffield there — provides the socially sanctioned channel through which the connection is eventually made.

### The Vicarage World

Galloway's portrait also contributes to the novel's depiction of the vicarage household — the uncle too lazy for parish work, the aunt of noble German descent, the household run on snobbery and pretense. The curate, tolerated for his energy despite his queerness, is part of the small, constrained world from which the young Ashenden will be partly liberated by his friendship with the Driffields.
