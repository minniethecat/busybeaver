# Ranelagh Gardens

The Ranelagh Pleasure Gardens in Chelsea, [[london|London]] — one of the great Georgian amusement venues, rivalling Vauxhall, where the novel stages a revealing encounter between [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia Carstares]] and her brother [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]].

## Overview

Ranelagh Gardens, opened in 1742 on the site of Ranelagh House in Chelsea, was one of [[london|London]]'s premier pleasure gardens. With its famous Rotunda, formal gardens, and Chinese Pavilion, it offered music, promenading, fireworks, and refreshments to [[fashion]]able society. In the novel, a chance meeting between [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia Carstares]] and [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] at Ranelagh provides a moment of dramatic irony — he observes her looking unwell, unaware that the cause is his own sinister actions (022-016).

Ranelagh was situated on the banks of the Thames in Chelsea, west of the main [[fashion]]able districts of [[st-jamess-london|St. James's]] and [[wyncham-house|Mayfair]]. Its riverside location and extensive grounds (some sixty acres) made it a popular destination for evening excursions during the [[london|London]] season.

## Historical Context

Ranelagh Gardens opened in 1742 on the grounds of Ranelagh House, the former residence of the Earl of Ranelagh. Its centrepiece was the Rotunda — a vast circular structure with an interior diameter of 150 feet, surrounded by tiers of boxes where visitors could sit, take refreshments, and observe the promenade below. The central orchestra performed music, and the building was lit by hundreds of lamps. Unlike Vauxhall Gardens (its older rival across the river), Ranelagh was considered more refined and exclusive, with admission costing half a crown. Morning concerts were held in the Rotunda, while evening entertainments included fireworks along the Chelsea Reach. The gardens remained [[fashion]]able until the early 19th century; the Rotunda was demolished in 1805. Canaletto painted the interior of the Rotunda in 1754, providing a visual record of the elegance that Heyer's characters would have experienced.
