# Lord Avon

Lord Avon is a minor supporting character in Georgette Heyer's *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*. An earl who hosts card parties at his house in Catharine Place, Avon is a convivial figure whose loud laugh and generous hospitality make him a fixture of the novel's social scenes.

## Role in the Novel

Avon's card-room serves as the setting for several key scenes, including the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover's]] entrance into London society [[gaming|and his]] high-stakes gambling. When Andover arrives, it is Avon who shakes a reproving finger at him, and Avon whose raucous laughter fills the room when Andover delivers a particularly cutting witticism.

> Lord Avon, leaning back in his chair at one of the tables, shook a reproving finger at him. (007-036)

> Lord Avon burst out laughing. "He had forgotten!" cried Avon, and went off into another of the noisy laughs that had more than once caused Mr. Nash to shudder and to close his august eyes. (007-054)

Avon also appears later in the novel, returning from [[bath|Bath]] to pay his respects to [[lavinia-carstares|Lady Lavinia Carstares]], bringing with him the latest gossip from the spa town — news of a duel in Crescent Fields and the waning power of Beau Nash.

## Host to the Duke of Andover

The Duke of Andover's entrance into Lord Avon's card-room marks one of the novel's memorable set-pieces. Avon's house in Catharine Place is "already crowded" when [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] is ushered in, greeted with ribald cries of "Oho, Belmanoir!" and "Where's the lady, Devil?" (007-034). Andover raises his quizzing-glass and surveys the room with "surprised hauteur," to which Avon responds with a reproving gesture that only encourages the Duke's sardonic performance (007-036).

Avon's own character — genial, unpretentious, given to great noisy laughs — provides the warm foil against which Andover's cold arrogance is measured. When Andover delivers a particularly cutting remark, it is Avon's explosive laughter that fills the room, a sound so unrestrained it has "more than once caused Mr. Nash to shudder and to close his august eyes" (007-054).

> "He had forgotten!" cried Avon, and went off into another of the noisy laughs. "You'll be the death of me, Devil! Gad! but you will!"
>
> (007-054)

## Significance

Lord Avon is part of Heyer's richly drawn Georgian social backdrop. He is not involved in the novel's central conflicts but provides the setting in which they unfold. His card-room is a microcosm of the masculine social world — a place of wit, wagers, and the casual cruelty of gentlemen at play.
