# Mr. Fudby

Mr. Fudby is a minor comic character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. A city merchant of considerable self-regard, he is robbed on the highway by [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] — an encounter that Fudby later transforms into a tale of his own imagined heroism.

## Role in the Novel

Fudby appears in the early chapters of the novel as the victim of a highway robbery. Stopped on the road by a masked gentleman highwayman, he is relieved of his cashbox containing two hundred guineas. The robbery is carried out with such elegant efficiency that Fudby — accompanied by his timid clerk [[mr-chilter|Mr. Chilter]] — has no opportunity to resist.

Once safely at the inn in Lewes, however, Fudby's account of the affair undergoes a remarkable transformation:

> The tale Mr. Fudby was telling bore surprisingly little resemblance to the truth. It was a much embellished version, in which he himself had behaved with quite remarkable gallantry. It had been gradually concocted during the journey to Lewes. (002-066)

Fudby summons the mayor, [[london|town]]-clerk, and beadle of Lewes to hear his complaint, enjoying the audience immensely despite the loss of his money. When [[john-carstares|Carstares]] — the very [[highway-robbery|highwayman]] who robbed him — enters the coffee-room and calmly inserts himself into the investigation, the dramatic irony is complete.

## Character

Fudby is pompous, self-important, and unattractive to his fellow men. The [[mr-hedges|mayor of Lewes]], summoned from his dinner, finds him tedious; the town-clerk is equally unimpressed. His treatment of Chilter reveals a bullying nature, and his embellished account of the robbery exposes a vanity that Heyer treats with gentle mockery.

> The more he saw and heard of Mr. Fudby, the less he liked him. Neither did the town-clerk care for him. There was that about Mr. Fudby that did not endear him to his fellow-men, especially when they chanced to be his inferiors in the social scale. (002-065)

## Significance

Fudby serves as a comic foil to the elegance of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]. Where Carstares is debonair, Fudby is blustering; where Carstares is genuinely gallant, Fudby merely pretends to be. He is one of Heyer's memorable minor grotesques — a small man whose pretensions are skewered with wit rather than cruelty.
