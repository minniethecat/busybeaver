# Bath

Bath is the novel's primary urban setting — the [[fashion]]able spa city to which Georgian society flocked for its health and its pleasures, and where much of *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*'s social drama unfolds. It is the world [[lavinia-carstares|Lady Lavinia]] longs for, the milieu in which [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] moves with predatory ease, and the place where [[diana-beauleigh|Diana Beauleigh]] first encounters both the Duke and her future husband.(006-008)(007-012)

## Description

Heyer's Bath is the Bath of the 1750s: a city of elegant crescents, crowded assembly rooms, and a constant traffic of titled visitors taking the waters and pursuing each other. "Titled folk of [[fashion]], and plain [[class|gentry]] who had come to Bath for their health's sake" — the city is a social crossroads, where aristocrats and gentlefolk mingle in ways that would be impossible in the more stratified world of [[london|London]].(007-013)

## In The Black Moth

Bath is the stage for the novel's central social encounters. It is here that [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] meets [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]] and instinctively recoils; here that she first encounters "[[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Ferndale]]" in a social setting; here that the novel's romantic and social plots intertwine in the crowded rooms of the [[fashion]]able world.(007-017)(007-018)

The city is also the setting for some of the novel's darkest scenes. The gossip that surrounds [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s possible [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] ("Goodness me, but a [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]]!") circulates in Bath's drawing rooms. The threat [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]] poses to [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] is most acute in Bath, where his social power is absolute and her social protection — her aunt, her respectability — is at its weakest.(019-161)
