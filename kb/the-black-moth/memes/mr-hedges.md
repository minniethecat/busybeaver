# Mr. Hedges

Mr. Hedges is a very minor comic character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. He is the mayor of Lewes, summoned from his dinner by [[mr-chilter|Mr. Chilter]] to hear [[mr-fudby|Mr. Fudby]]'s complaint of highway robbery.

## Character

Hedges is described as a "fussy little man who suffered from dyspepsia." He has no interest in [[mr-fudby|Fudby]]'s affairs, resents being called away from his meal, and finds Fudby personally unattractive. His forced participation in the investigation — sitting through Fudby's embellished account while internally longing for his interrupted dinner — provides one of the novel's small comic moments.

> He was a fussy little man who suffered from dyspepsia; he was not interested in the affair, and he did not see what was to be done for Mr. Fudby. Further, he had been haled from his dinner, and he was hungry; and, above all, he found Mr. Fudby very unattractive. (002-064)

## The Investigation

At the inn, Hedges immediately recognises the disguised [[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Fanshawe]] as a *grand seigneur* and wishes [[mr-fudby|Fudby]] would moderate his loud recital (002-068). When Sir Anthony eyes Fudby with cold disdain, Hedges hurriedly interposes himself to smooth the awkwardness (002-080):

> "Er—yes—" Mr. Hedges bowed. "Pray pardon my importuning you with our—"
>
> (002-083)

Hedges watches as Sir Anthony's lips twitch convulsively at Fudby's pompous display of bowing, sensing the aristocrat's suppressed amusement behind the frigid manner (002-089). When Sir Anthony turns his peculiarly sweet smile on [[mr-chilter|Mr. Chilter]] and then back to Hedges, the mayor finds himself the uneasy intermediary between the gentleman's charm and the merchant's bluster (002-092).
