# Tracy Belmanoir

Hugh Tracy Clare Belmanoir, Duke of [[andover-estate|Andover]] — known throughout society as "Devil Belmanoir" — is the antagonist of Georgette Heyer's *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*. Cold, charming, and magnificently indifferent to every moral code, Belmanoir represents the dark terminus of aristocratic privilege: the man for whom honour, love, and law are merely counters in an endless game of self-gratification.

## Role in the Novel

Tracy Belmanoir is the novel's antagonist and its most compelling figure of moral danger. He was present at [[the-card-party|the card party]] where [[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]] cheated and Jack took the blame — and he knew the truth, yet remained silent. This silence is not incidental to his character but essential to it: Belmanoir's power lies in what he knows and what he chooses not to reveal. He is the novel's keeper of secrets, and his knowledge gives [[carstares-household|him leverage ove]]r the Carstares family that he exploits with casual cruelty.(003-076)(003-086)

Belmanoir's function in the novel is to embody the corrupt extreme of the aristocratic code that the novel otherwise romanticises. Where [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]] proves that a [[honour|gentleman's honour]] can survive disgrace, Belmanoir demonstrates that a gentleman's privileges can survive the complete absence of honour. He is the threat that the novel's happy ending must neutralise — not by violence (though violence comes) but by exposure: the revelation that even Devil Belmanoir can be defied.(028-102)

## Biography

### Lineage and Family

The Duke of [[andover-estate|Andover]] is head of [[belmanoir-family|the Belmanoir family]], one of England'[[lavinia-carstares|s great]] aristocratic houses. His sister Lavinia married Richard Carstares, making him brother-in-law to the very family whose secret he holds. His younger brother is [[andrew-belmanoir|Lord Andrew Belmanoir]], a "mad, reckless rakehell" who shares Tracy's wildness but lacks his cold intelligence.(004-077)(018-059)

The Belmanoirs are defined by [[pride|pride]] — "she had all the [[pride|Belmanoir pride]]" is said of Lavinia, but it applies equally to Tracy, whose arrogance is the family trait raised to its most extreme and dangerous expression.(022-009)

### Key Actions and Events

**[[the-card-party|The Card Party]]**:[[richard-carstares| Belman]]oir watched Richard cheat and Jack take the blame. He knew the truth and said nothing — not out of kindness, but because the knowledge was a weapon he preferred to keep sheathed until it could be used to maximum effect.(003-076)(003-086)

**Society's "Devil"**: Throughout the novel, Belmanoir moves through [[london|London]] and Bath society with an aura of dangerous glamour. People murmur "Devil Belmanoir!" and wonder what fresh [[scandal|scandal]] he will create next. He treats this [[scandal|reputation]] with amused contempt — it is a tool, not a burden.(006-007)(007-034)(007-037)

**[[the-queensberry-rout|The Queensberry Rout]]**: At a fashionable gathering, Belmanoir exercises his charm and his menace in equal measure, demonstrating his ability to dominate any social situation through sheer force of personality.(007-016)

**[[the-climactic-confrontation|The Climactic Confrontation]] (Chapters XXVII–XXVIII)**: The novel's climax brings Belmanoir face to fa[[courage-and-recklessness|ce with]] Carstares in a scene that tests both men's courage. Belmanoir's cold brilliance meets Carstares's principled defiance. The resolution is not Belmanoir's destruction but his neutralisation — he is exposed, defied, and ultimately rendered irrelevant by the moral force of Carstares's integrity.(028-102)(028-180)

## Relationships

- **[[John Carstares]]** — The protagonist and Belmanoir's moral opposite. Belmanoir knows Carstares's secret and holds it as a weapon. Their relationship is one of mutual, unspoken recognition — the man of honour and the man without honour, bound by the same hidden truth.(003-086)
- **[[Lavinia Carstares]]** — His sister. Belmanoir's treatment of Lavinia reveals the limits of his coldness: he does not harm her directly, but his indifference to her happiness is itself a form of cruelty.(005-086)
- **[[Richard Carstares]]** — His brother-in-law. Belmanoir despises Richard's guilt-ridden weakness and exploits it when convenient.
- **[[Andrew Belmanoir]]** — His younger brother. Andrew shares Tracy's wildness but lacks his intelligence and control. Tracy treats him with a mixture of affection and contempt.(018-059)

## Characterisation

### Appearance and Manner

The novel's [[Prologue|Prologue]] opens with a portrait of Belmanoir at his most characteristic: "sat at the table in his magnificent library, idly surveying the tip of his gleaming sword." He is elegant, controlled, and possessed of an icy charm that draws people to him even as they fear him. His nickname — "Devil Belmanoir" — captures the duality: he is both satanic and alluring, a figure of terror who is also the centre of every social occasion.(P01-001)

### Personality and Traits

Belmanoir's defining trait is coldness — not the heat of passion but the chill of complete moral indifference. He does not rage or storm; he manipulates, observes, and acts with a calculating precision that makes him more dangerous than any hot-blooded villain. "Like all Belmanoirs, you care first for yourself," he tells [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]], and it is the novel's most concise summary of his character.(005-086)

He is capable of charm, even of something approaching warmth — his scenes with [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] suggest genuine fraternal feeling — but the charm is always instrumental, the warmth always conditional. He is a rake not out of uncontrollable appetite but out of contempt for the social codes that constrain others. His rakery is a philosophical position: an assertion that no code, no rule, no other person's feeling, has any claim on him.

### Narrative Arc

Belmanoir does not have an arc in the conventional sense; he does not change or reform. His narrative function is to be the obstacle that the hero must overcome, the darkness against which the novel's light must prove itself. His defeat is not destruction but marginalisation: he is rendered irrelevant by the force of [[john-carstares|Carstares's ]]integrity and Diana's love. The novel does not punish him; it simply ceases to need him.

## Literary Context

Tracy Belmanoir belongs to the tradition of the "charming villain" that runs through English literature from [[richard-carstares|Richard]]son's [[harold-lovelace|Lovelace]] through Byron's heroes to Wilde's Dorian Gray. He is the dark double of the romantic hero — possessing all of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s charm, courage, and aristocratic bearing, but none of his moral substance. The "Devil" nickname explicitly frames him as a Satanic figure: the tempter, the accuser, the beautiful fallen angel.

Heyer would return to this character type throughout her career — the cold, magnetic aristocrat whose charm conceals moral vacancy — but Belmanoir remains one of her most fully realised versions. He is, in some ways, more interesting than the hero: [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s goodness is static, a given; Belmanoir's wickedness is dynamic, a force that drives the plot forward even as the novel's moral framework condemns it.
