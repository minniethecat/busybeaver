# Highway Robbery

Highway robbery in Georgian England was at once a capital felony and a potent social myth. The figure of the "gentleman highwayman" — well-born, well-[[fashion|dress]]ed, and unfailingly courteous — haunted the public imagination, and in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*, Georgette Heyer transforms this ambiguous figure into the novel's central narrative mechanism.

## Definition

Highway robbery was the crime of waylaying [[coach|travel]]lers on public [[coach|road]]s and robbing them of money and valuables, typically at gunpoint or sword-point. Under the "Bloody Code" of 18th-century English law, it was a capital offence punishable by hanging. Yet the highwayman who combined [[courage-and-recklessness|daring]] with courtesy — who robbed with a jest and never harmed a lady — became a folk hero, celebrated in ballads, broadsheets, and popular prints.(008-001)

## Historical Background

The golden age of the English highwayman spanned roughly from the Restoration (1660) to the early 19th century. Mounted on a swift horse and armed with pistols, the highwayman exploited the isolation of England's turnpike [[coach|road]]s, where [[coach|travel]]lers carried jewellery, coin, and negotiable instruments. Famous practitioners — Claude Duval, James Maclaine, [[richard-carstares|Dick]] Turpin — were as celebrated for their style as condemned for their crimes. The paradox was baked into the institution: the same society that hanged highwaymen at Tyburn also bought the broadsheets romanticising their exploits.(001-002)(008-001)

By the mid-18th century, improved [[coach|road]]s, regular [[coach]] [[servant|service]]s, and more effective policing had begun to curtail the trade. But in the world of *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*, set in the 1750s, the highwayman remained a plausible — if increasingly anachronistic — figure.(008-001)

### Narrative Function

Heyer employs highway robbery as the novel's primary engine of identity and concealment. [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]]'s adoption of the highwayman's mask is not a descent into criminality but a costume assumed in [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]] — a way for the [[disgrace-and-exile|disgraced son]] of an earl to occupy a social role while remaining invisible to the world that has cast him out.(002-052)(008-045)

The highwayman's mask serves a double narrative function. In the literal sense, it conceals [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s identity from his victims and from the law. In the figurative sense, it allows him to act according to his true gentlemanly nature — refusing to rob women, challenging sporting victims to fair fights, redistributing his takings to the poor — while operating in the only role the world has left him.(008-001)(008-068)

The device also generates dramatic irony throughout the novel. The reader knows that the courteous highwayman is the [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]]d Earl's son; the other characters do not. Every encounter on the [[coach|road]] becomes a scene of near-recognition, as when [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] hears "her highwayman's" voice or when [[miles-ohara|Miles O'Hara]] puzzles over the highwayman's gentlemanly bearing.(009-038)(009-043)(010-022)

## Social Significance

The highwayman occupies a peculiar place in Georgian social history: a criminal whose legend was sustained by the very [[class]] he preyed upon. Aristocratic audiences applauded *The Beggar's Opera* (1728), whose hero Macheath was a highwayman; they bought prints of Claude Duval dancing a minuet with his victim on Hounslow Heath. The "gentleman highwayman" was wish-fulfilment in reverse — the fantasy that even in crime, a gentleman remained a gentleman.(008-068)(013-104)

Heyer exploits this ambiguity to the full. [[john-carstares|Carstares]] is both genuinely a criminal — he robs, he threatens, he could hang for his acts — and unquestionably a gentleman. The highwayman's mask is, paradoxically, the only identity under which he can fully express his gentlemanly code: "I relieved him of his cashbox and two hundred guineas. A present for the poor of Lewes."(002-007)
