# William Fleming

Mr. William Fleming is a minor character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. A country gentleman of Scottish descent, he is married to a cousin of the [[belmanoir-family|Belmanoir family]] — a marriage the aristocratic Belmanoirs consider a shocking *mésalliance*.

## Appearance and Manner

Fleming is described as a "short, tubby little man, [[fashion|dress]]ed in sober brown." His attire marks him unmistakably as a country gentleman rather than a man of [[fashion|fashion]] — his wig is of the scratch type favoured by rural squires, in stark contrast to the powdered and curled creations of London society.

> He was a short, tubby little man, dressed in sober brown, very neat as regards his wrists and neckband, but attired, so thought Lavinia, for the country, and not for town. His wig, too, was not at all modish, being of the scratch type that country gentlemen affected. (023-002)

## Background

Fleming's [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] to a [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]] cousin is a source of aristocratic embarrassment. His father had been a farmer, an[[marriage-and-matrimony|d the]] Belmanoirs have never quite accepted the match:

> She had married beneath her, had Mrs. Fleming, and the Belmanoirs had never quite forgiven the shocking *mésalliance*. William Fleming was nought but a simple Scotsman, whose father — even now the family shuddered at the thought — had been a farmer! (023-003)

## Role in the Novel

Fleming appears in a single significant scene: an evening at the theatre with [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] and [[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]], accompanied by his wife and their young kinsman [[charles-holt|Charles Holt]]. While his wife marvels at the fashions and his young relative points out notable figures in the audience, Fleming stands quietly with his hands behind his back — a country man out of his element in the glittering world of the London stage.

## Significance

Fleming and his wife provide a brief but telling glimpse of the social distances within the extended [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]-Carstares clan. His plainness and lack of pretension contrast with the fashionable excesses of the main [[characters-of-the-black-moth|characters, and h]]is presence at the theatre — on the night of Lavinia's planned elopement — adds an additional layer of ordinary, familial context to an extraordinary evening.
