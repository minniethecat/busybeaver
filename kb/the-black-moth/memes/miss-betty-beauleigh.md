# Miss Betty Beauleigh

Miss Betty [[mr-beauleigh|Beauleigh]] is Diana's aunt, chaperone, and moral ally. In a novel where older women are largely absent (the mothers are dead, the society matrons are peripheral), Miss Betty provides the sole instance of mature feminine wisdom. She is warm without being sentimental, protective without being controlling, and she supports Diana's choices even when those choices defy social convention.(007-001)(013-104)

## Role in the Novel

Miss Betty serves as [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s guardian in the practical sense (she chaperones her in Bath society) and in the moral sense (she trusts Diana's judgement and defends her right to make her own choices). When [[john-carstares|Carstares]], injured and recovering at the [[beauleigh-residence|Beauleigh home]], confess[[highway-robbery|es that he]] is "a common highwayman," it is Miss Betty who hears him — and Miss Betty who does not recoil.(013-104)

Her role is quiet but crucial. She provides the [[servant|domestic]] space — the home where [[john-carstares|Carstares]] recovers, the environment where Diana can come to know him — in which the novel's central relationship develops. Without Miss Betty's steady, undemonstrative support, the romantic plot would have no ground to stand on.

## Biography

Miss Betty is [[mr-beauleigh|Mr. Beauleigh's]] sister and Diana's aunt. She has, it seems, never married — a fact that the novel neither laments nor explains, treating her spinsterhood as simply her condition. She runs the household w[[john-carstares|ith quiet]] competence, nurses Carstares "in despite of" the doctor's bloodletting regime (she and the do[[london-season|ctor]] "had many arguments on the subject, in which he was completely routed"), and watches over Diana's romantic situation with shrewd, affectionate eyes.(013-001)

## Characterisation

Miss Betty's key quality is shrewd warmth. She is neither the comic spinster nor the stern chaperone of convention; she is simply a sensible, kind woman who loves her niece and trusts her judgement. Her defeat of the bloodletting doctor is characteristic: she knows what is right and she does it, without fuss or drama.

Her "[[pride|pride]] of her darling" — she is "filled with pride of her darling, and satisfaction at the reflection that she had been right from the start" — is the novel's most direct statement of maternal (or quasi-maternal) love. In a story otherwise focused on romantic passion, Miss Betty provides the steady warmth of familial affection.(014-001)
