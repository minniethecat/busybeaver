# Molly O'Hara

Lady Molly [[miles-ohara|O'Hara]] — called "the Midget" by her large husband and "Molly" by her friends — is the novel's most delightful minor character. Petite, birdlike, and possessed of an irrepressible curiosity, she provides warmth, humour, and a model of companionate [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] that stands in quiet contrast to the shadowed union of Richard and Lavinia.

## Role in the Novel

Molly serves as emotional foil and social bridge. Her [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] to Miles is the novel's ideal of domestic happiness: warm, affectionate, and free of the secrets that burden the [[john-carstares|Carstares]] marriage. She is the first person to be captivated by the story of the "gentleman-[[highway-robbery|highwayman]]" — a response that prefigures Diana's own romantic fascination. "How splendid, Miles!" she exclaims upon hearing of the mysterious highwayman, and her enthusiasm is both comic and prophetic.(009-043)

Her curiosity about "Cousin Harry" ([[john-carstares|Carstares]] in disguise) drives some of the novel's most charming scenes. When she breezily observes "you seem to know my [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]!" to Diana, she is closer to the truth than she knows — and her innocent remark crystallises the connection between the two women that will sustain the novel's romantic plot.(010-022)

## Biography

Molly is Irish, like her husband, and has been married to [[miles-ohara|Miles]] long enough to know how to manage him but not so long that she has lost the pleasure of doing so. Their breakfast scene in Chapter IX is a masterclass in marital comedy: Molly plying Miles with coffee and eggs, waiting for precisely the right moment to launch her interrogation, all the while maintaining an air of "the inquisitive kitten."(009-001)(009-002)

## Characterisation

Molly is "a pretty, birdlike little lady, with big eyes, and soft brown curls escaping from under a demure but very becoming mob cap. She measured five foot nothing in her stockings." Her physical smallness is affectionately noted throughout — [[miles-ohara|Miles]] calls her "the Midget" — but her personality fills every room. She is warm, curious, and possessed of a romantic sensibility that the novel treats with affectionate indulgence.(009-001)

Her discretion, when it matters, is absolute. Though she is desperate to know the truth about "Cousin Harry," she does not pry when the situation turns serious. The woman who exclaims over romantic [[highway-robbery|highwaymen]] is also the woman who keeps her friends' secrets.
