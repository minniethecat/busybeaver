# Jim Salter

Jim Salter is [[john-carstares|John Carstares]]'s servant, confidant, and partner in crime. He has been with Jack throughout the six years of exile, managing the practical details of the highwa[[honour|yman's]] life while his master attends to matters of honour, cravat, and chivalry. In a novel full of aristocrats, Jim is the sole consistent voice of practicality — and his devotion to Jack is absolute.(001-118)(002-001)

## Origins and Engagement

Jim was originally a [[servant|servant]] at Wyncham, the [[carstares-household|Carstares family]] seat, before following Jack into exile. Their partnership began by chance, as the novel relates:

> Then it was that he met Jim Salter, whom he engaged as his servant, and between them there sprang up a steady friendship that had lasted six years.(008-044)

The phrase "steady friendship" is significant. Heyer does not present Jim merely as a hired man but as a companion whose loyalty transcends the [[servant|servant]]-master relationship. The six-year duration of their bond — through poverty, danger, and the moral ambiguities of the highway — testifies to a fidelity that the novel's aristocrats, for all their codes of honour, rarely match with such consistency.

## Role in the Highwayman's Trade

Jim is the practical engine behind [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s romantic [[highway-robbery|highwayman]] persona. Chapter II opens with him in characteristic attendance:

> Jim Salter folded one of my lord's waistcoats with painstaking care and laid it in an open valise.(002-001)

The "painstaking care" is Jim's signature: he attends to the material world — the [[wigs-and-grooming|wigs]], the cravats, the waistcoats, the takings — with the same devotion that Jack reserves for matters of style and [[honour|honour]]. The division of labour is efficient but also comic: Jack supplies the gallantry, Jim the logistics.

His bluntness provides the novel's most direct critique of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s romantic impracticality. "If ye give away all ye get, sir, why do ye rob at all?" he asks, with the exasperated practicality of a man who has long since accepted that his master's [[honour|code of honour]] makes no economic sense. Jim is loyal not because he understands Jack but because he has decided, once and for all, that Jack is worth serving.(002-009)

The physical reality of their partnership — [[white-hart-inn|the White Hart]] inn, the carefully [[coach|pack]]ed valise, the road by moonlight — roots the [[servant|novel's ]]romance in domestic texture. Jim's presence reminds us that even a gentleman highwayman needs someone to press his coats, tend his wig, and worry about tomorrow's breakfast.

## Devotion and Anxiety

Jim's loyalty manifests most vividly in his anxiety. In Chapter XII, his vigilance is captured in a single image:

> He turned his head slightly and saw that Jim Salter was standing in the middle of the road, watching him.(012-165)

The participle "watching" carries the weight of six years. Jim's concern is not the formal deference of a [[servant|servant]] but the active, possessive worry of someone who has invested his entire life in another person's safety. He watches Jack the way a [[colonel-shepherd|shepherd]] watches a particularly reckless sheep — with love, exasperation, and the certainty that disaster is only one wrong turn away.

This anxiety reaches its peak in the novel's final chapters:

> No less worried was Jim Salter. He had returned from Fittering towards sunset, and having heard Jack's voice in the library, had come thither.(029-003)

The [[servant|domestic]] geography of this moment — Jim returning at sunset, drawn by Jack's voice to the library — is intimate and revealing. Jim does not need to be summoned; he gravitates towards Jack like a compass needle, his concern a permanent state of being rather than a response to any particular crisis.

When action is required, Jim's response is immediate and physical:

> Jim Salter hurried up to my lord.(029-024)

"Hurried" is uncharacteristically undignified for a [[servant|servant]], but Jim has long since ceased to care about the forms of his station. His priority is not propriety but presence — being where Jack needs him, when Jack needs him.

## The Servant Who Transcends Service

Jim embodies a paradox that runs through *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* and much of Heyer's later work: the servant who is more than a servant, whose worth is measured not by station but by character. He has no title, no lineage, no claim to gentility — yet his fidelity, courage, and practical competence make him one of the novel's most admirable figures.

The novel does not politicise this paradox — it does not use Jim to critique the [[class|class]] system — but it registers it honestly. Jim's devotion is freely given, not extracted; his partnership with Jack is a friendship in everything but name. If the [[class|aristocracy]]'s [[honour|code of honour]] is tested and found wanting in figures like the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]], Jim's simpler code — be there, be useful, be loyal — is never found wanting at all.
