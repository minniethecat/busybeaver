# Mr. Selwyn

Mr. Selwyn — referred to by his Christian name George — is a minor character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. A gentleman gambler with a notorious fascination for public executions and morbid spectacles, Selwyn moves easily through the novel's social world as a card player, a witty companion, and a former admirer of [[lavinia-carstares|Lady Lavinia Carstares]].

## Character and Role

Selwyn is first encountered at [[lord-avon|Lord Avon]]'s card table, where he shuffles and rattles dice alongside [[gregory-markham|Sir Gregory Markham]] and the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]]. He possesses a sharp tongue and an eye for social nuance — when Richard Carstares enters the card-room, it is Selwyn who pauses in his dice-rattling to observe Richard's grave countenance with innocent-seeming surprise.

> Selwyn, rattling his dice, paused, and looked round at Markham with a face of innocent surprise. Then he turned slowly and stared at Carstares' grave, almost stern countenance, with even more surprise. (018-122)

Selwyn is one of [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]]'s circle, and at the gaming table he wins her prized ruby earring — gallantly vowing to wear it next his heart forever.

> When she at length ceased, her luck had run out, and she had lost her much-prized ruby earring to Mr. Selwyn, who placed it carefully in his vest pocket, vowing he should wear it next his heart forever. (018-130)

## The "Love of Horrors"

Heyer makes a sly allusion to the historical George Selwyn (1719–1791), a real figure notorious for his fascination with executions and corpses. When [[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]] visits Newgate prison frequently — hoping against hope *not* to find his brother among the highwaymen imprisoned there — his friends tease him about it:

> He visited Newgate so often that his friends twitted him on it, vowing he had Selwyn's love of horrors. (021-053)

## Significance

Selwyn is a minor but vivid presence — a gambler, a wit, and a man whose peculiar tastes add texture to the novel's Georgian milieu. His name alone would have signalled to contemporary readers a certain type of society figure: worldly, raffish, and drawn to the macabre.
