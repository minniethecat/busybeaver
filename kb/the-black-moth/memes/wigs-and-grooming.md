# Wigs and Grooming in Georgian Society

No gentleman in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* appears in public without his wig properly powdered and his person carefully composed. The dressing-table is a stage on which identity is constructed, an[[paris|d the]] wig — whether a towering Parisian confectio[[coach|n or a]] sober brown travelling piece — tells the world who a man intends to be that day (001-033) (001-045).

## Definition

The wig, or periwig, was the most visible male accessory of the Georgian era. Full-bottomed wigs, tie-wigs, bag-wigs, and the scratch wigs of country gentlemen each signalled a distinct social position (023-012). Powder — typically made from wheat flour or starch, sometimes scented — whitened the hair into a uniform cloud that concealed age, baldness, and individual identity alike (007-013). Patches (*mouches*), cut from silk or velvet and glued to the face, completed the artifice: placed near the mouth they signalled coquetry; near the eye, a calculated menace (001-002).

The ritual of the toilet was laborious and public. A gentleman might spend an hour before his mirror while his valet [[fashion|dress]]ed his wig, adjusted his cravat, and applied rouge and powder (002-003). The toilet was not a private affair — [[fashion|fashion]]able ladies and gentlemen received visitors while being dressed, and the arrangement of a brooch or the placement of a patch was a social event (018-045).

### Narrative Function

Heyer uses wigs and grooming as her most efficient instrument of disguise. When [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]] must vanish into the persona of "Sir Anthony Ferndale," the transformation is achieved not by a mask but by a wig — "marvellously powdered and curled and smacking greatly of Paris" — and by the careful application of patches, rouge, and languor (001-033). When he rides out as himself, the "sober brown wig" replaces the powdered creation, and the man beneath emerges (001-045). Wig and identity are inseparable.

For [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]], the rejection of the powdered wig is a statement of power. He wears his own black hair, unpowdered, deliberately defying the Fashion that governed every other gentleman's head (005-003). His silver hair-ribbons at the card party flout the rule that "black alone should be worn to tie the hair" (007-032). The Devil Duke does not conceal; he does not need to. His grooming is a declaration of aristocratic immunity.

## Social Significance

The wig was among the last great expressions of masculine display before the "Great Masculine Renunciation" — the shift around the turn of the 19th century toward sober, dark-coloured male dress. In Heyer's Georgian world, a man's wig is a public document: it announces his wealth, his taste, his profession, his region, and even his moral character. To appear without one, or to wear one's natural hair as Tracy does, was to claim an exemption from the social order itself. The ritual of the toilet — with its valets, its mirrors, its powders and patches — was not vanity but obligation: a gentleman who neglected his grooming neglected his [[gentleman|duty]] to his [[class|rank]] (002-003).
