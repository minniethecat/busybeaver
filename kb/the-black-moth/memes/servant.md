# Servants and Service

Behind every gentleman in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* stands a servant — pouring the wine, holding the horse, carrying the message, and sometimes, in a crisis, saving the day.

## Definition

Domestic service was the largest employment sector in Georgian England. A great house might employ dozens of servants in a rigid hierarchy: butler, housekeeper, valet, lady's maid, footman, cook, housemaid, groom, and so on. Servants were expected to be invisible until needed, loyal beyond reason, and discreet about everything they saw. (001-001)

## Historical Background

The 18th-century servant [[class]] was vast and varied. At the top, a duke's valet or a lady's maid wielded considerable informal power — they knew their employers' secrets. At the bottom, a scullery maid worked from dawn to midnight for a pittance. Between them lay footmen (chosen for height and [[fashion|appearance]]), [[coach]]men, grooms, and an army of household staff. (002-014)

The relationship between master and servant was patriarchal in theory but often surprisingly intimate in practice. A gentleman's servant might accompany him into [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]], fight beside him, and know him better than his own family. (002-154)

### Narrative Function

Servants in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* serve Heyer's narrative in three ways. First, they provide practical support — [[jim-salter|Jim]]'s loyalty enables [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s double life. Second, they offer comic relief — the gap between a servant's perspective and a gentleman's is a reliable source of humour. Third, they function as a moral chorus — a servant's judgment of a character often carries weight because it comes from below, without social obligation. (002-014) (002-154)

[[jim-salter|Jim]], [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s servant, is the most fully realized. His loyalty transcends [[class]] and law — he follows his master into [[highway-robbery|highway robbery]] without question, and his gruff devotion humanizes [[john-carstares|Carstares]] more than any number of drawing-room compliments could. (002-052)

## Social Significance

The servant [[class]] in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* is both essential and invisible — essential to the plot machinery, invisible to the characters who depend on them. Heyer's portrayal is affectionate but not revolutionary: servants are loyal, comic, occasionally shrewd, but never questioning of the system that keeps them in their place. This is period-accurate, but it also reveals the limits of the novel's social imagination.
