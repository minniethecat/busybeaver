## Description

Thurze House stands near the village of Maltby, and is the country seat of [[miles-ohara|Sir Miles O'Hara]] and his wife [[molly-ohara|Lady Molly]]. It is a house defined less by its architecture than by its atmosphere: warm, Irish, unpretentious, and governed by the twin spirits of friendship and good humour. "No one who comes to Thurze House knows ye or anything about ye," O'Hara tells [[john-carstares|Carstares]] — and the assurance is characteristic of a household where a man is judged by what he is, not by what the world says of him. (015-102)

The household runs with comfortable efficiency: a competent staff, good stabling for horses ([[jim-salter|Jim Salter]] pronounces himself "highly contented" with Jenny's accommodation), and a dining room where the wine is excellent and Lady Molly enforces a sensible rule that the gentlemen are "not to sit too long over their wine." (015-179)(015-183)

## In The Black Moth

**Carstares's First Stay (Chapter XI)**: After the encounter on the road that brings [[miles-ohara|O'Hara]] and Carstares back into each other's lives, Carstares stays at Thurze House. It is here that the friendship, dormant for six years, is rekindled. O'Hara extracts a solemn promise that Carstares will not "drift gaily out of me life again," and Carstares, who has had no friend since his disgrace, assents with genuine gladness. When he departs — riding one of O'Hara's horses, having waved "a very regretful farewell" — he carries with him the memory of "a very hearty handshake" and a standing invitation to return. (011-001)(011-003)

**The Return (Chapter XV)**: Weeks later, after his painful departure from [[horton-house|Horton House]] and his parting from [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]], Carstares is prevailed upon by O'Hara to return to Thurze House. He arrives exhausted and in pain — "suspiciously white about the mouth, and there was once more a furrow — this time of pain — between his brows" — but rallies to greet Lady Molly "with fitting elegance" and to pay her "at least three neat, laughing compliments" before O'Hara marches him off to rest. (015-178)

**Lady Molly's Hospitality (Chapter XV)**: At dinner that evening, Carstares — dressed in "old gold and silver trimmings" — exerts himself to be the perfect guest, masking his private heartache beneath the polished surface of wit and gallantry that six years of exile have taught him. Lady Molly, who is famously hard to please, is entirely won over — "falling an easy victim to his charm." O'Hara watches with satisfaction as his capricious wife warms to his friend, knowing it is a rare tribute. (015-180)(015-182)
