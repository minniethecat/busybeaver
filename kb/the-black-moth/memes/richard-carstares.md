# Richard Carstares

Richard [[john-carstares|Carstares]] is the younger son who became the Earl — and the man whose single act of cheating at cards set the entire plot of *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* in motion. Haunted by guilt, bound by love for his wife Lavinia, and paralysed by the secret that could destroy them both, Richard is the novel's most psychologically complex character: the beneficiary of a sacrifice he can never acknowledge and a crime he can never confess.

## Role in the Novel

Richard [[john-carstares|Carstares]] occupies a unique position in the novel's moral economy. He is neither hero nor villain; he is the man whose moral failure created the conditions for both. His cheating at cards six years before the story begins was the act that [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] took the blame for — an act of self-sacrifice that transformed Jack from the heir to Wyncham into a disgraced highwayman, and transformed Richard from a guilt-ridden younger brother into the reluctant Earl.(003-073)(003-076)

The novel's treatment of Richard is remarkably unsentimental. He is not excused; he is understood. His guilt is real, his paralysis genuine, his love for [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] the one clean thing in a life built on a lie. His arc — from concealment to confession — i[[elopement|s the]] novel's most developed moral trajectory, the story of a man who must learn the courage his brother possessed all along.(004-020)

## Biography

### Lineage and Family

Richard is the younger son of the late Earl of [[wyncham|Wyncham]]. After John's [[scandal|disgrace]], Richard inherited the title and estate — an inheritance he took with "sore heart," knowing he was profiting from his brother's ruin. He married [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia Belmanoir]], whose brother Tracy is the Duke of Andover and the keeper of Richard's secret.(001-023)(003-073)

The [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] to Lavinia is the central fact of Richard's emotional life. He loves her deeply — it was to win her that he cheated at cards, desperate for money to sustain his courtship — and his love for her is now inextricably tangled with the guilt of the act that made their marriage possible.(003-068)(003-073)

### Key Actions and Events

**[[the-card-party|The Card Party]]**: Richard,[[debt| des]]perate after a debt to a man named Gundry, altered a card during play. [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] witnessed the act. When exposure came, John stepped forward and claimed the cheat as his own. Richard, paralysed by fear and shame, let him.(003-076)(003-086)

**Six Years of Guilt**: In the years since, Richard has lived as Earl of [[wyncham|Wyncham, mar]]ried Lavinia, and carried the weight of his secret in silence. He has sent his servant [[warburton|Warburton]] to find John and persuade him to return — an act of love and guilt intertwined.(001-056)(001-059)

**The Winter of Discontent (Chapters III–IV)**: Richard's scenes with [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] reveal the strain the secret places on their [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]]. He is moody, withdrawn, consumed by a guilt he cannot share. Lavinia senses something wrong but cannot name it.(004-020)(004-051)

**Confrontation and Confession**: The novel's resolution requires Richard to face his brother, acknowledge his crime, and accept the forgiveness that Jack has always been ready to give. His arc culminates not in a dramatic reveal but in the quiet [[courage-and-recklessness|courage]] of finally telling the truth.

## Relationships

- **[[John Carstares]]** — His elder brother, for whom Richard's guilt is both a burden and a bond. Jack bears Richard no resentment; Richard's inability to forgive himself is the real obstacle between them.(003-073)
- **[[Lavinia Carstares]]** — His wife. Richard's love for Lavinia was the motive for his crime; his fear of losing her is the reason he cannot confess. Their marriage is the most fully realised domestic relationship in the novel.(003-068)(006-032)
- **[[Tracy Belmanoir]]** — His brother-in-law and the keeper of his secret. Belmanoir's knowledge of Richard's crime gives him a hold over the Carstares family that he exploits with casual cruelty.(003-086)
- **[[Warburton]]** — The family solicitor, sent by Richard to find John and persuade him to return. Warburton's loyalty bridges the gap between the brothers.(001-056)

## Characterisation

### Appearance and Manner

Richard is described as less striking than his elder brother — where Jack is all "whimsical" charm and graceful bearing, Richard is heavier, more solid, prone to moodiness and silence. His guilt manifests physically: he is restless, irritable, unable to find peace in the estate he inherited or the [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] he won.(004-051)

### Personality and Traits

Richard's defining trait is guilt — not the guilt of the hardened sinner but the guilt of the weak man who did wrong and has never been able to undo it. He is not a bad man; he is a man who did a bad thing and has spent six years paying for it in the currency of self-loathing. His paralysis — his inability to confess, to act, to face what he has done — is the novel's most acute psychological portrait.(004-020)

His love for [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] is genuine and deep, but it is shadowed by the knowledge that their [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] was built on a lie. His relationship with Jack is simultaneously the source of his greatest shame (he let his brother take the blame) and his greatest hope (Jack's forgiveness, when it comes, is the absolution he craves).(003-068)(003-073)

### Narrative Arc

Richard's arc is the novel's most complete moral journey: from guilt and concealment, through the painful acquisition of [[courage-and-recklessness|courage]], to confession and absolution. His statement — "I have the courage to face them all now — after six years" — marks the turning point, the moment when he decides to stop hiding and start living.(004-020)

The arc is not one of transformation (Richard was always capable of [[courage-and-recklessness|courage]]; he simply failed to exercise it) but of recovery. By the novel's end, he has recovered the courage he lost six years before — and in doing so, he has recovered the brother he lost as well.

## Literary Context

Richard [[john-carstares|Carstares]] is a figure who would recur throughout Heyer's work: the weak but not wicked man, the character whose moral failure is a matter of a si[[gaming|ngle]] moment's cowardice rather than a settled disposition. He is the novel's most realistic character — not the romantic hero his brother is, nor the magnificent villain Belmanoir is, but a man of ordinary weakness who must learn ordinary courage.

His relationship with [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] — a [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]] shadowed by a secret — is one of Heyer's most psychologically acute creations. The scenes between them, with their mixture of love, frustration, and unspoken tension, read as remarkably modern in their emotional texture.
