# Madam Thompson

Madam Thompson is a kind-hearted but socially limited widow who provides the [[bath|Bath] setting in which [[diana-beauleigh|Diana Beauleigh first encoun]]ters Tracy Belmanoir. Her old friendship with [[[queen-square-bath|[lady-elizab]]eth-beauleigh|Miss Elizabeth Beauleigh]] brings Diana to Queen Square, and her own gentle unworldliness makes her the unwitting instrument through which Tracy — presenting himself as "Mr. Everard" — gains introduction to Diana.

## Role in the Novel

Madam Thompson's function is primarily structural: she provides the [[bath|Bath]] base from which [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s first romantic entanglement unfolds. As a widow of limited social circle, she is not equipped to recognise [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] for who he is, and her approval of the smooth-mannered "Mr. Everard" inadvertently enables his pursuit of Diana. Her character illustrates the vulnerability of provincial gentlewomen to polished, worldly gentlemen.

## Biography

Madam Thompson had been a widow for some fifteen years at the time of the novel's events. She had attended a seminary with [[lady-elizabeth-beauleigh|Miss Elizabeth Beauleigh]] (Betty) when both were girls, and they had maintained their friendship ever afterwards through occasional visits and regular correspondence — lengthy epistles "full of unimportant scraps of news and much gossip."(007-001)

When [[diana-beauleigh|Miss Beauleigh]] requested to visit Bath for a change of air after a long illness, Madam Thompson was delighted and prepared her very best bedchamber. It was [[diana-beauleigh|durin]]g this visit — Diana's first meeting with her hostess — that the novel's central romantic plot began to unfold.(007-001)(007-002)

## Characterisation

Madam Thompson is drawn with gentle comedy. She is amiable, hospitable, and well-meaning, but her conversation is "uninteresting and rambling," her social circle consists entirely of people "of her own age and habits," and she has few acquaintances in [[bath|Bath]]. Her regret that "if only my dear son George were at home" to entertain [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] reveals her awareness of her own limitations as a hostess for a young woman.(007-009)(007-012)

She is not foolish — simply unworldly. When [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] presents himself as "the simple Mr. Everard," Madam Thompson "could not be expected to recognise Devil Belmanoir" in him. Her endorsement of him — won by his "smoothness of manner and polished air" — is a function of her innocence, not her credulity.(007-016)
