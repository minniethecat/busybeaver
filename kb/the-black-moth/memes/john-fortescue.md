# Sir John Fortescue

Sir John Fortescue is an "austere patrician" — a gentleman of the older generation, the father of [[frank-fortescue|Frank Fortescue]], and a figure who moves through the novel's social world with a gravity that contrasts with the youthful rakishness of his son's circle.(019-063)

## Role in the Novel

Sir John Fortescue appears in the social spaces of the novel — walking the streets of Bath with the other gentlemen, and entering the box at the theatre where the fashionable world gathers. He is described as "austere," suggesting a man of formal manners and strict principles, the kind of elder statesman of society who provides the moral architecture against which the younger generation's transgressions are measured.(019-063)(023-011)

His most notable appearance is at the theatre, where he comes into the box alongside [[gregory-markham|Markham]], Wilding, and Devereux — all men of the Beau Monde whose presence frames the social world that [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]] has abandoned and to which [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]] aspires.(023-011)

## Characterisation

Fortescue is characterised by the single word "austere" — a term that suggests severity, self-discipline, and a lack of indulgence. He is the antithesis of the rakish set that includes his own son Frank: where Frank gambles and drifts, Sir John stands firm, a pillar of the establishment.
