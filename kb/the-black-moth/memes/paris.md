# Paris

Paris is the distant city of [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]] and intrigue in the backstory of *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* — the place where English gentlemen went when they could no longer remain in England. It represents the Continental alternative: more glamorous, more dangerous, and ultimately foreign to the novel's English moral framework.

## Description

In the Georgian imagination, Paris was simultaneously the centre of European civilisation and a sink of iniquity. For English aristocrats, it was the natural destination when [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]] or [[debt]] made England untenable (006-007) — a place where one could live cheaply, gamble endlessly, and wait for the [[scandal]] at home to fade. Versailles, the court of Louis XV, represented the apex of this world: magnificent, corrupt, and utterly unlike anything in England.

## In The Black Moth

Paris is mentioned only in passing, as part of the backstory of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]] — he spent time there, as did other [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]]d English gentlemen. Its presence on the novel's mental map reminds the reader that the world of the novel extends beyond England's shores, and that [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]], for a Georgian aristocrat, often meant the Continent.
