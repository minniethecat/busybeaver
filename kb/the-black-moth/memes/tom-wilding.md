# Tom Wilding

Tom Wilding is a minor gentleman of the London social scene, notable for his convivial nature and his presence at fashionable gatherings. He appears as a member of the set surrounding [[Gregory Markham|Markham]], [[Harold Lovelace|Lovelace]], and the Duke of Andover, and serves to populate the novel's depiction of Georgian high society.

## Role in the Novel

Wilding is a background figure who contributes to the texture of the novel's social world. He appears at card parties, at the theatre, and in the street scenes that give Heyer's [[london|London]] its vivid sense of busy, interconnected society. His function is primarily atmospheric — he is one of the many minor gentlemen whose comings and goings create the social fabric against which the main plot unfolds.

## Appearances

Wilding first appears in the card-party scene at [[ch07|Chapter VII]], where he presses forward with "a glass in one hand and a bottle of port in the other" to ask Tracy Belmanoir about the "black-haired beauty" — Diana Beauleigh.(007-056)

Later, he is seen at the opening of the Pavilion in company with [[gregory-markham|Markham,]] part of the fashionable crowd gathered for the Bath season.(018-033)

In a [[london|London]] street scene, he intercepts the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]] in Half Moon Street, eager to discuss "March's last bet with Edgecumbe" — a brief exchange that shows his absorption in the betting and gaming culture of London gentlemen.(020-094)

## Characterisation

Wilding is depicted as a typical young man of [[fashion|fashion]]: sociable, wine-loving, interested in gaming, and thoroughly embedded in the gossip and camaraderie of his social circle. He is not developed as an individual character so much as he is representa[[london|tive]] of a type — the gentleman-about-town who populates the clubs, parties, and streets of Georgian London.
