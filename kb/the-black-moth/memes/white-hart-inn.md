# The White Hart

The White Hart is the inn where [[john-carstares|John Carstares]], under the alias "[[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Ferndale]]," prepares for his exploits as a gentleman [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]. It is the novel's backstage — the place where the mask is adjusted, the cravat is pinned, and the man behind the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]'s persona is visible to the reader alone.(002-001)

## Description

The White Hart is rendered in intimate detail: a [[fashion|dress]]ing room where [[john-carstares|Carstares]] sits before the mirror, a gorgeous silk [[fashion|dress]]ing gown over the back of his chair, [[jim-salter|Jim Salter]] hovering anxiously over coat and waistcoat. It is a space of preparation and disguise — the place where the aristocrat becomes the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]], where "[[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Ferndale]]" is assembled each day from the raw materials of silk, satin, and rouge.(002-001)(002-002)

## In The Black Moth

The White Hart scenes are among the novel's most revealing. Here, alone with [[jim-salter|Jim]], [[john-carstares|Carstares]] drops the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]'s mask — though not the gentleman's — and the reader sees the man beneath the performance. His whimsical smile, his fastidious attention to his cravat, his casual redistribution of his takings to "the poor of Lewes" — all are the private [[john-carstares|Carstares]], the man the world has forgotten.(002-007)(002-010)
