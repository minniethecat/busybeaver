## Description

Horton House — also called Horton Manor — stands near the village of Littledean, seven miles west of Midhurst in Sussex. It is a gentleman's country house, not grand like [[wyncham|Wyncham]] but comfortable and well-kept, with gardens where [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] tends her flowers, her chickens, and a menagerie of pets that includes the spaniel Nellie, the dog Fido, and a collection of other animals in perpetual need of rescue. The nearest inn is The Pointing Finger, where strangers to the district put up. (020-103)(020-107)

It is an unpretentious household: [[mr-beauleigh|Mr. Beauleigh]], his sister [[miss-betty-beauleigh|Miss Betty]], and his daughter [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]], attended by a small staff that includes the servant Thomas and a groom. The atmosphere is warm and informal — a place where a wounded stranger can be taken in without question and where a young woman's gardening and poultry-keeping are not merely tolerated but encouraged. (014-001)(016-019)

## In The Black Moth

**Carstares's Convalescence (Chapters XII–XV)**: After [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] — travelling under the alias "Mr. Carr" — suffers a shoulder wound, [[miles-ohara|Miles O'Hara]] arranges for him to stay at Horton House. It is here that Carstares recovers, spending idyllic summer days helping Diana with her gardening and her pets. The intimacy of the setting — the quiet country routine, the shared work among the flowerbeds, the constant small emergencies of the animal kingdom — draws the pair steadily closer together, watched with satisfaction by Miss Betty and with growing apprehension by O'Hara, who knows that his friend is falling unconsciously in love. (014-001)

**The Departure (Chapter XIV)**: When Carstares at last realizes the depth of his feeling for Diana, he faces the situation with characteristic honour: as a disgraced man and a [[highway-robbery|highwayman]], he cannot in conscience speak of love to her. He informs Mr. Beauleigh of his decision to leave — and, in a moment of candour, of his profession. Mr. Beauleigh, who had been all smiling hospitality, becomes "frigidly polite" and makes no effort to detain him. Carstares gives [[jim-salter|Jim Salter]] orders to pack, and prepares to ride away from the happiness he has found. (014-003)

**Mr. Bettison's Courtship (Chapter XVI)**: In Carstares's absence, the house becomes the stage for the unwelcome courtship of Mr. Bettison, a neighbouring squire whom Mr. Beauleigh secretly hopes will become his son-in-law. Bettison's visits grow so frequent that "scarce a day passed without his strutting step being heard approaching the house." Diana, still preoccupied with thoughts of Carstares, snubs him persistently — but Bettison's self-regard is so impregnable that he mistakes her coldness for shyness. When he finally proposes, she refuses him with great firmness. His accusation that she is "infatuated by that overdressed popinjay — that insufferable Carr" provokes her to ring for the servant and have him shown out. (016-001)(016-021)

**The Duke's Spy (Chapter XX)**: The [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]], learning of Carstares's location, sends his man [[harper|Harper]] to Littledean with precise instructions: lodge at The Pointing Finger, watch the comings and goings at Horton House, and insinuate himself into the household by bribing away the Beauleighs' groom and offering his own services in his place. The scheme is characteristic of the Duke — patient, methodical, and entirely without scruple. (020-107)(020-111)
