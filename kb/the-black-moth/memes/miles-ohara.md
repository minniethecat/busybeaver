# Miles O'Hara

Miles O'Hara is the novel's embodiment of unconditional friendship. A big, indolent Irish gentleman married to the charming [[molly-ohara|Molly]], Miles is the first character to pierce [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]]'s disguise — and his response is not shock or condemnation but immediate, unhesitating welcome. He is the novel's warmest male presence, the friend who proves that Carstares's honour is recognised by those who know him best.

## Role in the Novel

Miles serves as the bridge between [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s life in exile and his return to society. When he recognises "[[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Ferndale]]" as his old friend Jack, he does not pause to calculate [[scandal|the soci]]al cost of associating with a disgraced man; he simply embraces his friend. His reaction — "Tare an' ouns, man! And is it meself that'll be caring what ye may or may not be?" — is the novel's most concise statement of the friendship that transcends social judgement.(010-007)

His function is to provide [[john-carstares|Carstares]] with an ally who knows his secret and accepts him anyway. This allyship is crucial to the plot: it gives Jack a base of operations in society, a place where he can be himself, and a mod[[diana-beauleigh|el of]] how others — Diana, ultimately — might respond to the truth.(010-025)

## Biography

Miles is an Irish gentleman of comfortable means, married to Lady [[molly-ohara|Molly O'Hara]]. Their marriage is presented as the novel's ideal: warm, affectionate, companionate. Miles is physically large, somewhat indolent, and possessed of an Irish charm that disarms social tension. He and Jack were friends before the disgrace, and his loyalty has survived six years of silence.(009-001)(010-012)

## Characterisation

Miles's key trait is loyalty without condition. He asks no questions, demands no explanations, offers no judgement. When he sees his old friend behind the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]'s mask, his only concern is for Jack's wellbeing. His Irish warmth — rendered through Heyer's somewhat stereotyped but affectionate dialect — provides the novel's emotional baseline: this is what friendship looks like when it works.(010-007)(010-025)
