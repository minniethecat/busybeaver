# Gregory Markham

Sir Gregory Markham is a minor supporting character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. He is a baronet, a gambler, and one of the many admirers who orbit [[lavinia-carstares|Lady Lavinia Carstares]]. Unlike the more dangerous characters in the novel, Markham is essentially harmless — lazy, good-natured, and devoted to the pleasures of the card table and the company of beautiful women.

## Appearance and Manner

Markham is described as "heavily handsome," favouring bold colours such as crimson and gold. He is a contrast to the delicately pretty [[harold-lovelace|Captain Lovelace]], his frequent companion in Lavinia's circle.

> There entered Markham, heavily handsome in crimson and gold, and Lovelace, his opposite, fair and delicately pretty in palest blue and silver. (019-031)

His habitual laziness is noted: during a silent walk through the [[london|London]] streets, he is described as silent "from habitual laziness" (019-036).

## Role in Society

Markham is a fixture at [[lord-avon|Lord Avon]]'s card table, where he shuffles cards and trades witticisms with the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]] and other gentlemen. He participates in the high-stakes gambling that the Duke uses to fleece his opponents — including a memorable wager where Andover stakes "my beauty against your estates."

> "Oh, an you are in a gaming mood, I will play you for the right to try my hand with the dark beauty!" called Markham across the room. [008-xxx]

He has recently returned from [[paris|Paris]] when he encounters [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]] at a social event, and he eagerly supplies her with negus and gossip about French society.

## Significance

Markham represents the typical Georgian society gentleman — wealthy, idle, and devoted to amusement. He serves as a foil to both the dangerous [[andover-estate|Andover]] [[richard-carstares|and the]] conflicted Richard, embodying the lighter side of the world the novel depicts without posing any real threat.
