# Fashion and Appearance

In the world of *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*, a gentleman is read before he speaks. The cut of a coat, the set of a periwig, the glitter of a diamond — these are the first sentences of every introduction.

## Definition

Georgian fashion was a complex signalling system. Powdered hair or a natural peruke proclaimed wealth and leisure. A patch on the face (the *mouche*) could be coquettish, political, or merely decorative. Jewels at the throat and fingers declared [[class|rank]]. The colour and quality of silk, the height of a heel, the width of a cuff — all carried meaning. (001-001)

## Historical Background

The early-to-mid 18th century was the apogee of masculine display in England. The "Macaroni" — the extravagantly dressed young gentleman returned from the Grand Tour — was a recognized social type. Gentlemen wore silk, velvet, lace, and jewels; they painted their faces and wore high-heeled shoes. (018-002)

This was the last era before the "Great Masculine Renunciation" of the late 18th century, when men's fashion turned toward the sober dark suit. Heyer's Georgian gentlemen inhabit this final flowering of male peacockery.

### Narrative Function

Heyer uses clothing and appearance as her most efficient character shorthand. [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]]'s black and silver, his diamonds and patches, his "almost unnatural pallor" — these are not mere decoration but moral statement. The [[tracy-belmanoir|Devil]] Duke looks like what he is: beautiful, dangerous, artificial. (001-001)

[[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]], by contrast, is described through the quality of his bearing rather than his clothing — the gentleman's essence outlasting his wardrobe. When he appears as a [[highway-robbery|highwayman]], the incongruity between his dress and his manner is the source of both comedy and pathos. (009-038)

## Social Significance

Fashion in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* is never neutral. It is the visual language of a society obsessed with [[class|rank]] and appearance. When Heyer describes a gentleman's dress in detail, she is not providing period colour — she is telling us exactly who this person is, what they want, and what they are willing to spend (morally and financially) to get it.
