# Debt and Credit

Debt is the shadow that follows every gentleman in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* — the unspoken reality behind the silks and diamonds, the [[coach]]-and-four, and the [[gaming]] table.

## Definition

In Georgian England, a gentleman's debt fell into two categories. "Debts of honour" — [[gaming|gambling]] losses and wagers — were morally binding and socially enforceable; failure to pay meant ruin. "Tradesmen's debts" — bills from tailors, vintners, and [[coach]]makers — were treated far more casually, often left unpaid for years. (004-130) (007-102)

## Historical Background

The 18th-century credit economy was built on the gentleman's promise. Tradesmen extended credit knowing they might wait years for payment; they charged accordingly, and the system functioned on trust and social pressure rather than legal enforcement. (004-086)

For younger sons and heirs, the gap between social expectations and actual income was bridged by debt. A gentleman was expected to maintain [[fashion|appearance]]s — the right clothes, the right ad[[fashion|dress]], the right entertainments — regardless of his means. (005-012)

### Narrative Function

Debt in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* is never merely financial — it is always moral. [[richard-carstares|Richard]]'s debt to Gundry triggers the card-cheating [[scandal]] that destroys his life. [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy]]'s debts are symptoms of his character: the charming rogue who expects others to pay. (003-073) (015-213)

Heyer uses debt to measure her characters: [[richard-carstares|Richard]] pays his debts with honour, even in [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]]; [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy]] pays none at all, yet society continues to extend him credit. The asymmetry is the novel's quiet indictment of a world that rewards [[fashion|appearance]] over substance. (004-132)

## Social Significance

Debt in *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]* illuminates the fundamental hypocrisy of Georgian society: a gentleman's word was his bond at the [[gaming]] table, yet his tailor might wait a decade for payment. The system rewarded charm and birth over fiscal responsibility — a pattern that Heyer exposes without ever quite condemning, because she understands that this very precariousness is what makes her world so vivid.
