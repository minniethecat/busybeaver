# Mr. Chilter

Mr. Chilter is a minor character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. He is the clerk to [[mr-fudby|Mr. Fudby]], a pompous city merchant, and appears during the aftermath of the highway robbery in which [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] — in his guise as a gentleman highwayman — relieves Fudby of his cashbox.

## Role in the Novel

Chilter is the hapless subordinate caught between his employer's bluster and the reality of the robbery. When [[mr-fudby|Fudby]] is robbed on the highway, it is Chilter who is blamed for cowardice, despite the fact that the robbery was carried out by an armed and mounted [[highway-robbery|highwayman]] against whom a clerk could do nothing.

> "I cannot but feel that if it had not been for your deplorably cowardly conduct, Chilter — yes, cowardly conduct, I say — I might never have been robbed of my two hundred!" (002-076)

Chilter is summoned to fetch the mayor, town-clerk, and beadle of Lewes to hear [[mr-fudby|Fudby]]'s heavily embellished account of the affair. During the subsequent investigation at the inn, Chilter provides a more accurate description of the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]]'s horse, inadvertently aiding the authorities' inquiry — and drawing the [[john-carstares|amused at]]tention of Carstares himself, who is present in the coffee-room.

> Jack perceived that it was time he took a hand in the game. He half turned in his chair and levelled his quizzing-glass at Mr. Chilter. (002-078)

## The Highwayman's Description

Under Fudby's peremptory command — "Describe him, Chilter!" — the clerk is pressed to provide a portrait of the robber (002-105). When the disguised Sir Anthony Fanshawe gently questions the description of the horse — an animal suspiciously like his own — Chilter obligingly embellishes:

> "Oh, quite!" lied Mr. Chilter. "And fat." (002-109)
>
> (002-109)

A moment later Chilter adds that the highwayman was "prodigious rough" and swore terribly (002-112). Sir Anthony's ironic response — offering Chilter snuff with exquisite courtesy, then suggesting they visit the stables together — heightens the dramatic irony: the clerk is unwittingly abetting the very man who committed the robbery (002-126)(002-128).

## Character

Chilter is timid, put-upon, and visibly uncomfortable with his employer's self-aggrandising version of events. He represents the ordinary man caught up in the schemes and vanities of his social superiors — a small but recognisable figure in Heyer's Georgian world.
