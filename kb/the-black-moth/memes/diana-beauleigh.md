# Diana Beauleigh

Diana [[mr-beauleigh|Beauleigh]] is the heroine of Georgette Heyer's *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*. Introduced as a reserved, clear-eyed young woman navigating the social world of Bath under the chaperonage of her aunt, Diana emerges over the course of the novel as a figure of quiet moral strength — the woman whose love proves capable of redeeming the disgraced hero and whose judgement proves sharper than the society that surrounds her.

## Role in the Novel

Diana is the novel's romantic centre and its moral compass. Unlike the hero, whose [[honour|honour]] is tested in combat and danger, Diana's tests are social and emotional: she must resist the advances of the predatory [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]], refuse a convenient but loveless proposal, and ultimately decide whether to accept a man whose disgrace would, by the standards of her world, make him unmarriageable.(007-018)(016-001)(014-095)

Her narrative function is to be the agent of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s redemption — not by saving him (he saves himself) but by choosing him, fully knowing his disgrace. Her question — "And she was willing to share your disgrace?" — is the novel's emotional climax, the moment when the honour code's absolute condemnation is overturned by the absolute commitment of love.(014-095)

## Biography

### Lineage and Family

Diana is the niece of [[miss-betty-beauleigh|Miss Betty Beauleigh]], with whom she lives (her parents, it is implied, are dead or absent). The Beauleighs are gentlefolk — respectable, comfortable, but not aristocratic. Diana's father, Mr. Beauleigh, appears in the novel as a kindly but somewhat ineffectual figure who "secretly hoped for Mr. Bettison as a son-in-law."(016-001)

Her lack of aristocratic connexion is significant: she is the object of [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]'s predatory attention precisely because she lacks the family protection that a duke's daughter would have. Her social vulnerability makes her moral strength — her clear-eyed refusal of both Belmanoir's advances and Bettison's convenience — all the more remarkable.(007-018)

### Key Actions and Events

**Encounter with [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]] (Chapter VII)**: At a social gathering in Bath, Diana meets the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]], who immediately marks her as prey. She "recoiled instinctively" from his advances — an instinct that proves entirely correct.(007-017)(007-018)

**Rescue by [[john-carstares|Carstares]] (Chapter XI)**: Diana is waylaid by [[highway-robbery|highwaymen]] and rescued by Carstares. The scene establishes the romantic bond: she is captivated by her rescuer's courage and gentleness, though she knows him only as "[[john-carstares|Sir Anthony Ferndale]]."(011-019)(011-079)

**Refusing [[mr-bettison|Mr. Bettison]] (Chapter XVI)**: Mr. Bettison, a respectable suitor, proposes marriage and is refused "with great firmness." Diana will not marry without love — a position that makes her eventual choice of the disgraced Carstares both principled and dramatically satisfying.(016-001)

**Learning the Truth (Chapter XIV and following)**: Diana gradually learns of [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s true identity and his disgrace. Her response is not horror but compassion: she sees the man beneath the mask and judges him by his actions, not by society's verdict.(014-095)

**The [[the-climactic-confrontation|Climax]]**: In the novel's final act, Diana's faith in [[john-carstares|Carstares]] is vindicated. Her love — steadfast, clear-eyed, and immune to social pressure — is the force that finally brings Carstares out of exile and into the light.

## Relationships

- **[[John Carstares]]** — The man she loves. She first knows him as her rescuer, then as "Sir Anthony Ferndale," and finally as the disgraced Lord John Carstares. Her love survives every revelation.(014-095)
- **[[Tracy Belmanoir]]** — The predator. Belmanoir pursues Diana with cold determination, seeing in her both a desirable conquest and a way to wound Carstares.(007-018)
- **[[Miss Betty Beauleigh]]** — Her aunt and chaperone. Miss Betty is Diana's protector and confidante — a warm, shrewd presence who supports Diana's choices even when they defy social convention.(013-104)
- **[[mr-beauleigh|Mr. Beauleigh]]** — Her father, kindly but conventional, who "secretly hoped for Mr. Bettison as a son-in-law."(016-001)
- **[[molly-ohara|Molly O'Hara]]** — Friend and ally. Molly's warm, companionate marriage to Miles provides Diana with a model of what love can be.(010-012)

## Characterisation

### Appearance and Manner

Diana is introduced with a description that emphasises her reserve and clarity: "Diana was cold. There was something of the untouched, watchful purity of a young goddess about her." She is beautiful — "a dainty creature," in the words of one observer — but her beauty is the setting for her character, not its substance.(007-017)(019-115)

### Personality and Traits

Diana's defining quality is a clear-eyed integrity. She does not deceive herself about people — she "recoiled instinctively" from [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]'s first approach, recognising what others had learned to tolerate. She refuses Bettison because she knows she does not love him. And when she learns the truth about Carstares, she does not flinch: she judges the man by what he has done, not by what society says he is.(007-018)(016-001)(014-095)

This clarity is allied with warmth. Despite the narrator's description of her as "cold," Diana is capable of deep feeling — her terror during the [[highway-robbery|highway robbery]], her admiration for her rescuer, her grief when she believes Carstares dead. The "coldness" is the surface of a character whose depths are rich with emotion, held in reserve for those who earn her trust.(011-079)(011-075)

### Narrative Arc

Diana's arc is one of growing moral authority. She begins the novel as a protected young woman, subject to the choices of others — her aunt's social arrangements, [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]'s predatory advances, Bettison's proposal. As the novel progresses, she increasingly becomes the agent of her own destiny: she refuses Bettison, defies Belmanoir, and ultimately chooses Carstares in full knowledge of his disgrace. By the novel's end, she is not the passive prize of the romantic plot but its active moral force.

## Literary Context

Diana [[mr-beauleigh|Beauleigh]] belongs to a tradition of heroines who combine moral clarity with romantic warmth — Jane Austen's Elizabeth Bennet is the most famous exemplar, and Heyer would develop the type throughout her career. What distinguishes Diana is the specific nature of her moral choice: she must not merely resist a bad suitor (the standard romantic-novel test) but actively choose a man who is, by every convention of her society, unchoosable. Her willingness to share Carstares's disgrace is a more radical act than any amount of witty banter or lively independence.

The [[Epilogue|Epilogue]] confirms that Diana and Jack marry and live at Wyncham, the estate restored to its rightful heir. The restoration is personal as well as social: Diana has chosen rightly, and the novel rewards her with the happy ending her courage has earned.(030-001)
