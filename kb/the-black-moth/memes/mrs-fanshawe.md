# Mrs. Fanshawe

Mrs. Fanshawe is a supporting character in *The Black Moth* who serves as a sympathetic confidante to [[John Carstares|Jack Carstares]] during his time in Bath. She is an established member of Bath society whose home becomes a place where Jack can unburden himself.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Fanshawe first appears at the end of Chapter XIX, when Jack (posing as Sir Anthony Ferndale) asks permission to call upon her, intrigued by her kindness (019-106). He "very soon availed himself of Mrs. Fanshawe's permission to call upon her" (021-001), and she quickly becomes his trusted confidante.

In a pivotal scene, Mrs. Fanshawe draws Jack down beside her on the sofa and coaxes from him the story of his past — his disgrace, his exile, and the scandal at the card table that cost him everything (021-003). When she hears his history, Mrs. Fanshawe does not recoil but instead becomes his champion.

> Mrs. Fanshawe drew him down beside her on the sofa, and after a little time had him confessing the whole history of the past six years. (021-003)

When Jack protests his unworthiness, Mrs. Fanshawe rebukes him warmly. She tells him society will receive him again one day, showing a faith in him that he struggles to accept himself (021-039). By Chapter XXII, all of Bath knows that Carstares is "at Mrs. Fanshawe's feet" (022-005).

## In the Epilogue

Mrs. Fanshawe is present at the homecoming celebration described in Lavinia's letter — "Mrs. Fanshawe, too, was there, for she knew Jack Abroad" — a testament to her enduring place in the circle of friends who surround Jack and Diana (030-004).

## Characterisation

Mrs. Fanshawe is one of the few characters in the novel who offers Jack unconditional kindness without ulterior motive. She is warm, perceptive, and maternal — a safe harbor in the swirling social world of Bath. Her drawing-room becomes a confessional where Jack can shed the masks of "Sir Anthony Ferndale" and "the highwayman" and simply be himself.
