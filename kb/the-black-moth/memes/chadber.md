# Mr. Chadber

Mr. Chadber, "florid of countenance, portly of person, and of manner pompous and urbane," is the landlord of [[chequers-inn|the Chequers Inn]] at Fallowfield and the novel's first named character. His inn — acquired by his great-grandfather in 1667 — is the stage on which the novel's opening drama plays out.(001-001)

## Role in the Novel

Chadber's function is to establish the novel's social world and historical moment. His [[tory-politics|Tory politics]], his pride in having sheltered Jacobite fugitives, and his self-importance as a man who can "both read and write" and once travelled "as far north as London town" create a vivid miniature of provincial English life in the 1750s. He is the ground against which the novel's aristocrats will figure — the sturdy, opinionated, essentially comic Englishman who represents the world that gentlemen are supposed to protect.(001-002)(001-003)

## Characterisation

Chadber is a triumph of comic portraiture. His pomposity, his [[tory-politics|Tory]] convictions, his delight in his own importance — all are rendered with affectionate precision. He is not mocked; he is enjoyed. He represents the novel's conviction that character is distributed across the social scale, and that a provincial innkeeper can be as vividly himself as any duke.
