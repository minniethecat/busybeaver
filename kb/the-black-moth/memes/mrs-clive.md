# Mrs. Clive

Mrs. Clive is a historical figure referenced in *The Black Moth* — the celebrated actress [Kitty Clive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kitty_Clive) (1711–1785), one of the most famous performers on the Georgian stage. In the novel, she appears as a figure to be observed at the theatre, part of the rich social texture of Bath's entertainment world.

## Historical Background

Catherine "Kitty" Clive was a dominant figure on the London stage for over forty years. She joined the Drury Lane company in 1728 and became renowned for her comic roles, particularly as a singer and comedienne in ballad operas and burlesques. She was closely associated with [David Garrick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Garrick), the century's greatest actor-manager, and was a favourite of [Horace Walpole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace_Walpole), who gave her a cottage at Strawberry Hill.

## In the Novel

Mrs. Clive is referenced in the [[bath|Bath]] theatre scenes that surround [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia Carstares]]'s planned elopement. The theatre with "the added [[fashion|attract]]ion of Mrs. Clive" draws the fashionable crowd, and her presence is noted as part of the social spectacle that the theatre represents — a place less for watching the play than for seeing and being seen.(023-001)

At one moment in the theatre, the orchestra begins the National Anthem, and "it was but Garrick leading Mrs. Clive forward" — a vignette that captures the reverence in which these theatrical figures were held. The pairing of Garrick and Clive would have been instantly recognisable to Heyer's contemporary readers as a marker of theatrical prestige.(023-078)

Her mention also serves a plot function: [[Lavinia Carstares|Lady Lavinia]] attends the theatre on the night of her planned elopement with [[Tracy Belmanoir|Lord Andover]], and the presence of notable figures like Mrs. Clive adds to the glittering distraction that surrounds her.
