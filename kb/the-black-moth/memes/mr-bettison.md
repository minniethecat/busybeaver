# Mr. Bettison

Mr. Bettison is [[diana-beauleigh|Diana Beauleigh]]'s respectable suitor — the man her father "secretly hoped for" as a son-in-law and the man she refused "with great firmness." He is the novel's embodiment [[marriage-and-matrimony|of th]]e conventional match: suitable, unobjectionable, and entirely inadequate to the romantic standard Diana has set for herself.(016-001)

## Role in the Novel

Bettison's narrative function is to be the foil against which [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s choice of [[john-carstares|Carstares]] is measured. He is not a bad man — he is, by all [[fashion|appearance]]s, perfectly decent — but he is not the man Diana loves, and her refusal of his proposal establishes the principle that will guide her: she will not marry for convenience. She will marry for love, or not at all.(016-001)

His brief [[fashion|appearance]]s in Chapter XIII, where he glares at the convalescent Carstares with barely concealed jealousy, give him a human pettiness that saves him from being merely a plot function. He knows, on some level, that he has already lost — and his resentment of the mysterious "Mr. Carr" is both comic and slightly pathetic.(013-196)(013-209)

## Characterisation

Bettison is characterised by respectability without distinction. He cannot compete with [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s romantic credentials, and his jealousy only underscores his inadequacy. He is the novel's quietest antagonist: not a villain, not a threat, just the representative of a life Diana has already decided against.(013-196)
