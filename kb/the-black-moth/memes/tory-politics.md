# Tory Politics

In *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*, set in the 1750s, the political divide between Tory (Jacobite) and Whig (Hanoverian) loyalties is recent living memory rather than abstract history. The failed Jacobite rising of 1745 still colours the loyalties of families like the [[john-carstares|Carstares]] and defines the political atmosphere of Fallowfield and [[chequers-inn|the Chequers Inn]].(001-001)(001-002)

## Definition

In the Georgian period, "Tory" had come to mean a supporter of the Stuart claim to the British throne — those loyal to the [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]]d James Francis Edward Stuart (the "Old Pretender") and his son Charles Edward Stuart ("Bonnie Prince Charlie"). A "Whig," by contrast, was a supporter of the Hanoverian succession, the Protestant monarchy established by George I in 1714.(001-001)(001-002)

The terms had deeper roots reaching back to the Exclusion Crisis of the 1670s-80s, but by the mid-18th century the defining question was Jacobite loyalty. To be a Tory was to hold that the rightful king was a Stuart, not a German-speaking Hanoverian. The disastrous defeat of the 'Forty-Five at Culloden (1746) left the Tory cause politically broken, but the loyalties — and the resentments — persisted in families and communities across England.(001-002)(001-003)(001-131)

### Narrative Function

Heyer uses political affiliation primarily as an identity marker — a shorthand for tradition, loyalty, and rootedness in an older England. To be a Tory is to belong to an ancient order of things, before the Hanoverian succession disrupted the natural chain of allegiance. [[chadber|Mr. Chadber]]'s Toryism is of a piece with his inn, which has stood in his family since 1667, "when the jovial Stuart King sat on the English throne, and the Hanoverian Electors were not yet dreamed of."(001-001)

For the [[carstares-household|Carstares family]], Toryism is a matter of inherited identity rather than active political engagement. Warburton, the old family l[[honour|awyer,]] treats it as almost a point of honour: the Carstares have always been Tories, and therefore they always will be. Jack's assertion of Whiggism is a personal rebellion — not against king or country but against the weight of family expectation that, in the aftermath of his disgrace, he no longer feels obliged to carry.(001-131)(001-132)

The political backdrop also provides a layer of verisimilitude. The novel's events unfold in the shadow of the 'Forty-Five: a world where men have recently risked their lives for a lost cause, where fugitives were sheltered in quiet inns, and where the question "Whose king do you serve?" was not rhetorical but urgent.(001-002)(001-003)

> A Tory was Mr. Chadber to the backbone. None so bitter 'gainst the little German as he, and surely none had looked forward more eagerly to the advent of the gallant Charles Edward. If he confined his patriotism to drinking success to Prince Charlie's campaign, who shall blame him?(001-002)

## Social Significance

Political affiliation in Georgian England was not merely a matter of opinion — it was woven into the fabric of family identity, social standing, and personal [[honour|honour]]. In the aftermath of the 'Forty-Five, Tory families faced a difficult negotiation between private loyalty and public prudence. Active Jacobitism was treason, yet to renounce the Stuarts was to betray one's family inheritance. Heyer captures this tension economically: [[warburton|Warburton]]'s simple declaration that "there has never been [[wyncham|a Whig ]]in the Wyncham family" carries the weight of generations.(001-131)(001-134)

[[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]]'s political indifference is itself politically significant. In a world where Tory and Whig identities are treated as immutable family characteristics, his lack of interest in politics marks him as somehow unmoored — a man without the convic[[class|tions]] that define his class and his name.(001-136)
