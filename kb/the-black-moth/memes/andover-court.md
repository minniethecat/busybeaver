# Andover Court

The country seat of the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]] ([[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]]), located seven miles beyond [[wyncham|Wyncham]] in Sussex, where the novel's central conflict reaches its [[the-climactic-confrontation|climax]] as [[diana-beauleigh|Diana Beauleigh]] is brought there against her will.

## Overview

Andover Court is the principal country estate of [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]], the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]]. Situated in Sussex, approximately seven miles from [[wyncham|Wyncham]] (026-047), it represents the dark mirror of the [[carstares-household|Carstares family]] seat — a place of aristocratic privilege turned to sinister purpose when [[diana-beauleigh|Diana Beauleigh]] is forcibly taken there (025-160).

## Location and Geography

- **Region**: Sussex
- **Proximity to other locations**: Seven miles beyond [[Wyncham House]] and [[wyncham|Wyncham]] village (026-047)

The estate is described as a substantial country property befitting a duke, isolated enough to serve as an effective prison but close enough to [[wyncham|Wyncham]] for the novel's climactic rescue to be feasible.

### Significance

Andover Court serves as the setting for the novel's climactic confrontation. [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s imprisonment there represents the ultimate threat posed by [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]]'s obsession, and the rescue that follows brings the novel's central conflict to its resolution. The estate's isolation and grandeur underscore the power imbalance between the Duke and his captive (025-160).

## Description from the Novel

> When they at last reached Andover Court, and his Grace assisted her to alight... (025-160)

> Miss Beauleigh off to Andover Court, seven miles beyond Wyncham (026-047)

The estate's distance from [[wyncham|Wyncham]] — seven miles — is a critical plot detail, creating urgency for the rescue that must be mounted when [[mr-beauleigh|Mr. Beauleigh]] learns of his daughter's whereabouts (026-065).

## Historical Context

A ducal country estate in early 18th-century Sussex would have been a substantial property, likely encompassing a manor house, gardens, stables, and surrounding farmland. The isolation of such estates — combined with the near-absolute authority of a duke over his domain — made them plausible [[places-of-the-black-moth|settings]] for the kind of genteel imprisonment depicted in the novel. The seven-mile distance from the nearest village is consistent with the geography of the Sussex Weald, where aristocratic estates were often separated by considerable stretches of countryside.
