# The Fallowfield Road

The [[coach|road]] near Fallowfield is the novel's primary highway setting — the stretch of turnpike where [[john-carstares|John Carstares]], masked and mounted, intercepts [[coach]]es and tests his [[gentleman|gentleman's code]] against the realities of the [[coach|road]]. It is the space where the novel's central tensions — honour and crime, disguise and recognition, danger and gallantry — play out in the most direct form.(001-024)(008-001)

## Description

Georgian turnpike [[coach|road]]s were the arteries of 18th-century England — improved, tolled, and policed (after a [[fashion]]), but still perilous for [[coach|travel]]lers carrying cash and valuables. The [[coach|road]] near Fallowfield, a quiet stretch between [[london|London]] and the south coast, is precisely the kind of isolated byway where a gentleman [[highway-robbery|highwayman]] could operate with a reasonable chance of success — and a constant risk of capture.

## In The Black Moth

The [[coach|road]] is the novel's liminal space — neither the safety of the inn nor the society of [[bath|Bath]], but a threshold where identities are suspended and revealed. It is on this [[coach|road]] that [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s [[coach]] is stopped in the novel's backstory, on this [[coach|road]] that he himself stops [[coach]]es as the [[highway-robbery|highwayman]], and on the [[coach|road]]s nearby that [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] is rescued. The [[coach|road]] is the novel's theatre of transformation, where gentlemen become criminals and criminals prove themselves gentlemen.(001-024)(008-001)
