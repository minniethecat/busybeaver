# Mr. Walpole

Mr. Walpole is a minor character in [[the-black-moth|The Black Moth]], appearing as a London society figure and host. He is Horace Walpole (1717–1797), the historical author, antiquarian, and man of letters, who plays a brief but vivid role in the novel's London scenes.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Walpole appears as an acquaintance of [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]], the Duke of Andover. He is mentioned in passing at White's club, where a gentleman remarks that "Horry Walpole" would know the gossip about Lord Andover (007-045). He later encounters Belmanoir in person on Arlington Street, London, where he detains him with conversation and invites him to a card-party at Twickenham (020-094). Andover parts from him gracefully after accepting the invitation (020-095).

## Biography

### Historical Figure

Horace Walpole was the youngest son of Sir Robert Walpole, Britain's first Prime Minister. He was a prominent writer, art historian, and antiquarian, best known for his Gothic novel *The Castle of Otranto* (1764) and his residence at Strawberry Hill, Twickenham. [[mrs-clive|Mrs. Clive]], the celebrated actress, was a favourite of Walpole's and lived in a cottage on his estate.

### In the Novel

Walpole is mentioned early in the novel as "Horry Walpole" in connection with gossip about the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover ]]("Devil") at White's chocolate-house (007-045). He appears in person in Chapter 20, where he waylays Andover on Arlington Street and invites him to [[richard-carstares|a ca]]rd-party at Twickenham. The [[mr-selwyn|guest list in]]cludes George Selwyn, Dick Edgecumbe, Lord March, Gilly William[[gregory-markham|s, Tom Wilding,]] and Gregory Markham (020-094). Andover accepts gracefully and departs (020-095).

## Literary Context

Heyer's inclusion of Horace Walpole is typical of her use of historical figures to ground her fiction in authentic Georgian [[london|London]]. Walpole was a fixture of 18th-century society — his letters and journals document the era's gossip, politics, and personalities. By having him appear briefly as a host and gossip, Heyer evokes the texture of London's social web without needing extended page-time.
