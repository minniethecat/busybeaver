# Crossdown Woods

Crossdown Woods is the wooded area near the [[mr-beauleigh|Beauleigh]] residence where some of the novel's most dramatic scenes take place. It is a liminal space — neither the safety of home nor the danger of the open [[coach|road]], but a threshold where the two worlds meet and characters are tested.(011-019)

## Description

The woods represent the novel's recurring geography of danger and rescue. It is on the [[coach|road]] through or near the woods that [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] is waylaid by [[highway-robbery|highwaymen]] and rescued by [[john-carstares|Carstares]]; it is in these wooded spaces that the boundaries between the [[servant|domestic]] world of [[beauleigh-residence|the Beauleighs]] and the dangerous world of the [[coach|road]] are most permeable.

## In The Black Moth

The woods function as the novel's testing ground — the place where [[john-carstares|Carstares]]'s [[courage-and-recklessness|courage]] is demonstrated, where [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]]'s admiration is kindled, and where the romantic plot is set irreversibly in motion. The "dear, brave gentleman" who rescues [[diana-beauleigh|Diana]] emerges from the woods as if from a fairy tale, and the woods themselves become, in retrospect, the novel's enchanted space — the place where love first appeared.(011-075)(011-078)
