# Barn Elms

Barn Elms was one of the most notorious [[duelling]] grounds of 18th-century England — a stretch of open land near the Thames, west of [[london|London]], where generations of gentlemen settled their affairs of honour. In *[[john-carstares|The Black Moth]]*, it is the site of [[tracy-belmanoir|Tracy Belmanoir]]'s [[duelling|duel]], fought "when deep in his cups over a quarrel about a lady."(024-126)

## Description

The real Barn Elms was a favoured location for [[duelling|duels]] throughout the Georgian period — sufficiently remote from central [[london|London]] to afford privacy, sufficiently accessible for gentlemen to reach before dawn. It was, along with Chalk Farm and Putney Heath, one of the places where the [[honour|code of honour]] was most bloodily enacted.

## In The Black Moth

[[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]'s [[duelling|duel]] at Barn Elms is recounted rather than depicted — part of the backstory that establishes his character. He fought it "when deep in his cups," a detail that transforms the [[duelling|duel]] from an [[duelling|affair of honour]] into an act of reckless self-indulgence. The location's historical reality anchors the novel's [[duelling]] culture in the actual geography of Georgian violence.(024-126)
