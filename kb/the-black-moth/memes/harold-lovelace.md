# Harold Lovelace

Captain Harold "Harry" Lovelace is a secondary antagonist in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. An old flame of [[lavinia-carstares|Lady Lavinia Carstares]], his reappearance in London society after five years abroad reignites a dangerous romantic entanglement that threatens her marriage to [[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]] and draws the predatory attention of the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]].

## Role in the Novel

Lovelace enters the narrative in Chapter XVIII as a former admirer of [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]], newly returned to London. His charm, good looks, and shared history with Lavinia quickly make him a fixture at the Carstares' Grosvenor Square home — to the growing distress of Richard, who watches his wife fall under the captain's spell once more. Lovelace's attentions to Lavinia become so pronounced that they attract the notice of the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]], who exploits the situation to further his own schemes against the [[carstares-household|Carstares family]].

> "Captain Lovelace!"—she put forward both her hands. (018-064)

## Appearance and Manner

Lovelace is described as strikingly handsome in a delicate, almost feminine way — a contrast to the darker, more dangerous beauty of the [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]]. He wears his hair in a loose, powdered wig and favours pale colours.

> Lovelace caught them in his, and bent his head over them so that the soft, powdered curls of his loose wig fell all about his face. (018-065)

[[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]], observing him for the first time, assesses him with reluctant admiration:

> He had ample opportunity for studying Lovelace as the good-looking pair drew near, and he could not but admire the delicate, handsome face with the grey eyes that held a laugh in them, the pleasure-loving, well-curved mouth, and the chin that spoke of determination. Here was not one of Lavinia's lisping, painted puppy-dogs, for in spite of the effeminate curls, it was easy to see that this man had character and a will of his own, and, above all, a great charm of manner. (018-105)

## Character and Conduct

Unlike the foppish admirers who usually surround [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia]], Lovelace possesses genuine charm and determination. He is a gambler, a wit, and a man of society. His friendship with Lavinia predates her [[marriage-and-matrimony|marriage]], and the ease of their intimacy alarms Richard precisely because it is not artificial.

Lovelace does not set out to destroy the Carstares' marriage; rather, he is drawn into Lavinia's orbit by genuine affection and her own encouragement. Nevertheless, his constant presence — whether at her boudoir, at balls, at Vauxhall, or at the gaming tables — becomes a source of torment for Richard.

> It seemed to Richard in the days that followed, that Captain Lovelace was never out of his house. If he went to his wife's boudoir, there was Lovelace, hanging over her while she played upon the spinet or glanced through the pages of the *Rambler*. (019-001)

## Relationship with the Duke of Andover

The [[tracy-belmanoir|Duke of Andover]] takes a keen interest in Lovelace's entanglement with Lavinia. At a society ball at Lady Davenant's, Andover deliberately draws Richard's attention to the pair and uses the situation to unsettle his brother-in-law:

> "Andrew is flirting with the Fletcher girl in the Blue Salon; I am here, and Lavinia is amusing herself with Lovelace. Yes, Richard, Lovelace! Be careful!" (020-078)

Andover's machinations eventually extend to hinting at darker consequences for the affair, planting the seed of scandal in Lavinia's mind and using her fear of exposure to manipulate her.

## Significance

Lovelace serves as both a romantic foil to [[richard-carstares|Richard Carstares]] and a narrative instrument. His presence destabilises the Carstares' household at a critical moment, creating the domestic vulnerability that the Duke of Andover exploits. He is not a villain in the mould of Andover — he is a charming, self-indulgent man whose genuine feelings for Lavinia collide with the obligations of honour and the schemes of more ruthless characters.
