# Isabella Fanshawe

Mrs. Isabella Fanshawe is a supporting character in Georgette Heyer's *The Black Moth*. A sprightly widow renowned for her wit and beauty, she first encountered [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] in Vienna years before the novel's main events. Her reappearance in London society inadvertently catalyses the marital crisis between [[richard-carstares|Richard]] and [[lavinia-carstares|Lavinia Carstares]], when Richard's clandestine visits to hear stories of his exiled brother are mistaken for a romantic affair.

## Appearance and Character

Mrs. Fanshawe is described as a beautiful and witty widow who moves easily in [[london|London]] society. Her house at No. 16, Mount Street is tastefully appointed, and she proves an excellent hostess. Beneath her social polish lies a kindness and perceptiveness that distinguish her from the more superficial [[characters-of-the-black-moth|characters]] in the novel.

> Carstares presently found himself by the side of one Isabella Fanshawe, a sprightly widow, greatly famed for her wittiness and good looks. (020-041)

## History with John Carstares

Mrs. Fanshawe first met [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] — then living under the alias "Sir Anthony Ferndale" — in Vienna in 1748, where she was travelling with her ailing husband. John became a frequent caller at their lodgings, grateful for the company of fellow Englishmen after four years of self-imposed exile. Their friendship was cemented when John rescued her from an uncomfortable situation with an Austrian count, fighting a duel in her defence.

> "He was so safe, and so English! The Count was furious, and at first I thought he would have his lackeys throw Anthony out. But when he heard all that Anthony had to say, he realised that it was useless to try to detain me — and I was taken home." (021-023)

## The Misunderstanding

When Richard Carstares learns that Mrs. Fanshawe knew his brother, he begins visiting her frequently — not for romance, but to hear every detail she can recall about John. His absorption in these reminiscences, combined with his distracted and feverish state, leads Lavinia to believe he has fallen in love with the widow.

The misunderstanding spirals into a full-blown marital crisis. Lavinia, convinced her husband no longer loves her, agrees to elope with Captain Lovelace. Meanwhile, Richard's mental state deteriorates under the strain of keeping his brother's secret.

> As he told Mrs. Fanshawe, when she exclaimed at his appearance — he could not rest; he must always be moving, thinking. She saw that he was not entirely himself, and counselled him to consult a doctor. His half-angry repudiation of all illness did not surprise her, but she was considerably startled when, in answer to her pleading that he should have a care for himself, he vehemently said: "If I could die, I should be glad!" (022-032)

Mrs. Fanshawe perceives Richard's true condition — mental rather than physical — but feels powerless to intervene, as she is not acquainted with Lavinia and fears presumption.

## Resolution

The misunderstanding is resolved in the novel's final chapters, when rstares|Richard]] and Lavinia finally speak openly. Lavinia's relieved question — "You don't love that horrid Mrs. Fanshawe, do you?" — prompts Richard's astonished denial, and the couple are reconciled.

> "Mrs. Fanshawe? Great heavens, no! You never thought that, surely?" [028-xxx]

## Significance

Mrs. Fanshawe is an example of Heyer's skill with secondary characters: she is not merely a plot device but a fully realised person — kind, perceptive, and possessed of her own history. Her connection to John Carstares's Viennese exile provides one of the few windows into his lost years, while her unwitting role in the novel's central misunderstanding demonstrates how innocent friendships can be misinterpreted in the hothouse of London society.
