# St. James's, London

St. James's is the aristocratic heart of Georgian [[london|London]] — the district of palaces, clubs, and [[fashion]]able residences where the novel's titled characters belong by right and from which the [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]]d [[john-carstares|John Carstares]] has been [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]]d. It is the novel's unspoken centre of social gravity, the place where [[scandal|reputation]]s are made and unmade.

## Description

In the 1750s, St. James's was the epicentre of English aristocratic life. St. James's Palace was the official royal residence; St. James's Square was surrounded by the town houses of the great families (020-094); St. James's Street was lined with the clubs — [[whites-chocolate-house|White's]], Brooks's — where gentlemen gambled, d[[class|rank]], and governed the country by informal consensus. It was the world the [[john-carstares|Carstares]] and [[tracy-belmanoir|Belmanoir]]s inhabited by birthright.

## In The Black Moth

St. James's is more implied than depicted. The novel's characters move between country estates and [[bath|Bath]], but their ultimate social horizon is always [[london|London]] — the place where the Season determines who is in favour, where the clubs judge a gentleman's [[debt|credit]], where the Court sets the standard of [[fashion]] and conduct. [[john-carstares|Jack Carstares]]'s [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]] from [[wyncham|Wyncham]] is also, implicitly, [[disgrace-and-exile|exile]] from St. James's — the world that would have been his had [[disgrace-and-exile|disgrace]] not intervened.
