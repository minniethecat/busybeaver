# Cyclone Mullins

**Cyclone Mullins** is a professional boxer who serves as an unwitting benchmark in Sally Nicholas's campaign to expose [[bugs-butler|Bugs Butler]]'s inflated reputation. Though Mullins appears only as a name in conversation, his boxing record provides the key evidence that punctures Butler's aura of invincibility.

## Role in the Story

Cyclone Mullins never appears in person; his significance lies entirely in the statistical record of his fights, which Sally deploys with devastating rhetorical precision. When Fillmore Nicholas and Butler's manager are busy promoting Butler as an unstoppable force, Sally uses Mullins's name to demonstrate that Butler's record is far less impressive than advertised (013-105).

The logic is simple but effective: if Butler took fifteen rounds to beat a mediocrity like Mullins — "ginks nobody has ever heard of" — and K-leg Binns knocked Mullins out in the fifth round, then Butler's claim to championship calibre is demonstrably hollow (013-041).

## Notable Scenes

### Sally's Cross-Examination (Chapter XIII)

Sally deploys the Mullins precedent during a tense exchange at Butler's training camp. When Butler's manager, [[lester-burrowes|Lester Burrowes]], and her brother [[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]] are extolling Butler's prowess, Sally cuts through the hype with a single factual question:

> "It took him fifteen rounds to get a referee's decision over Cyclone Mullins," said Sally severely, "and K-leg Binns…" (013-105)

The effect is immediate and, from Burrowes's perspective, catastrophic. The manager, who had been delivering an enthusiastic promotional monologue, is thrown into confusion. Sally, with a few well-chosen facts, has exposed the unimpressive reality behind Butler's carefully managed reputation.

### The Statistical Argument

Sally's full case against Butler rests on three comparative data points:
- Butler needed fifteen rounds to beat Cyclone Mullins by referee's decision (013-041)
- K-leg Binns knocked Mullins out in the fifth round (013-041)
- Therefore Butler's fighting ability is, at best, modest

The narrator later confirms Sally's logic, noting that "Cyclone Mullinses might withstand him for fifteen rounds where they yielded to a K-leg Binns in the fifth" (013-156).

## Significance

Cyclone Mullins is a classic Wodehouse comic device: the invisible character whose existence enables a moment of deflation. His boxing record functions as a piece of irrefutable evidence in Sally's broader project of managing her brother's career choices. By invoking Mullins, Sally demonstrates her capacity for clear-eyed, fact-based argument — a quality notably absent in most of the men around her.

The Mullins reference also illuminates the theme of reputation versus reality that runs through the novel. Butler's camp has constructed an elaborate edifice of promotional rhetoric; Sally dismantles it with three sentences and a boxer's record.
