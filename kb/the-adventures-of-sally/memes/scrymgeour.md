# Scrymgeour

Scrymgeour is a prominent London barrister, a Member of Parliament, and the pompous employer who dismisses [[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]] from his position as private secretary — an event that sets much of the novel's plot in motion. Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his presence looms over Ginger's backstory and the Carmyle family dynamics.

## Role in the Story

Scrymgeour functions as an offstage antagonist whose mistreatment of a dog triggers Ginger Kemp's employment crisis. Ginger describes him as "a pompous old ass who thinks he's going to be Prime Minister some day" and "a big bug at the Bar" who has "just got into Parliament" (005-074). The connection between Scrymgeour and the Carmyle family runs through [[bruce-carmyle|Bruce Carmyle]], who began his legal career as a "devil" (research assistant) to Scrymgeour before rising to prominence himself (005-076).

The "affaire Scrymgeour" — as the Carmyle family refers to it — becomes a lasting wound in family relations. When Ginger encounters his cousin Bruce Carmyle later in the novel, he is surprised at being spoken to at all, since "the Family, Ginger knew, were even now in session upon it" (008-168).

## Personality and Traits

Ginger Kemp's description of Scrymgeour is unequivocally hostile. He is, in Ginger's assessment, "one of those asses who isn't fit to own a dog" — "a blighted, pompous, bullying, shrivelled-souled old devil" (005-081). His defining vice is his treatment of animals: he insists on forcing his spaniel, Billy, to perform tricks, which "no self-respecting dog would do" (005-083).

> Scrymgeour used to make this spaniel of his do tricks \u2014 fool-things that no self-respecting dogs would do: and eventually poor old Billy got fed up and jibbed. He was too polite to bite, but he sort of shook his head and crawled under a chair. You\'d have thought anyone would have let it go at that, but would old Scrymgeour? Not a bit of it!
>
> (002-242)

When the spaniel finally refuses to perform and crawls under a chair, Scrymgeour's response is to beat the animal — the act that triggers Ginger's intervention. Ginger, a passionate dog lover who believes "dogs loathe" performing tricks because "they're frightfully sensitive" (005-083), throws a soda-water siphon at Scrymgeour and is promptly dismissed.

Scrymgeour also suffers from dyspepsia, a detail that is mentioned several times in the narrative. The red-haired young man's inquiry about "Scrymgeour's dyspepsia" (004-031) suggests the condition is a known and possibly characteristic affliction.

## Key Relationships

- **[[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]]**: Former private secretary. Ginger's employment with Scrymgeour ends dramatically when Ginger protests the barrister's mistreatment of his spaniel. Ginger's summary dismissal contributes to his precarious financial situation and his tour through a series of failed jobs.
- **[[bruce-carmyle|Bruce Carmyle]]**: Former "devil" (legal research assistant). Carmyle began his career assisting Scrymgeour and later used this connection to secure the secretary position for his cousin Ginger. The "affaire Scrymgeour" creates lasting tension between the cousins.
- **Billy the Spaniel**: Scrymgeour's dog and Ginger's "great pal." The spaniel's refusal to perform tricks and Scrymgeour's subsequent mistreatment are the catalyst for Ginger's dismissal.

## Notable Events

**The Dismissal of Ginger Kemp (Chapter IV–V)**. Ginger recounts his employment with Scrymgeour to Sally during their Roville conversations. The key events: Scrymgeour insists his spaniel perform tricks; the "very jolly little spaniel" refuses; Scrymgeour beats the dog; Ginger intervenes with a soda-water siphon; Ginger is fired. "He was too polite to bite," Ginger says of the spaniel, "but he sort of shook his head and crawled under a chair" (005-083).

**The Roville Dog Fight (Chapter IV)**. Before Sally knows Ginger's history with Scrymgeour, the barrister's name recurs as a mysterious presence. The red-haired young man (Ginger) repeatedly asks about "Scrymgeour's dyspepsia" (004-031), creating a comic running joke. When the Roville beach erupts into a dog fight involving Sally's borrowed poodle, it is Ginger — recently dismissed by Scrymgeour for defending a dog — who proves the "Master Mind" capable of stopping the canine combat (004-049).

**The Family Intervention**. By the novel's later chapters, Scrymgeour's dismissal of Ginger has become a family matter. Uncle Donald attempts to make Ginger apologize to Scrymgeour, leading to a "bit of a scrap" between them. Ginger, with characteristic stubbornness, "rather gave it a miss" (015-094).

## Significance

Scrymgeour is a classic Wodehouse offstage antagonist — a pompous authority figure whose absurdity is revealed through the accounts of other characters rather than direct depiction. His mistreatment of a dog and Ginger's principled (if impractical) response establishes Ginger's character as an impulsive but fundamentally decent young man. The "affaire Scrymgeour" also illuminates the Carmyle family dynamics, revealing the pressures of respectability and the gap between Ginger's instincts and his family's expectations.

The running joke of Scrymgeour's dyspepsia — mentioned repeatedly in the Roville scenes — is a minor but characteristic Wodehouse touch, suggesting that the barrister's pompous exterior conceals chronic digestive discomfort.
