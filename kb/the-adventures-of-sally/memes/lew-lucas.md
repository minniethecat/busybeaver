# Lew Lucas

Lew Lucas is the reigning lightweight boxing champion whose title defense against Bugs Butler at Jersey City forms the climax of the boxing subplot in *The Adventures of Sally*. Though he never appears directly as a speaking character, his reputation and his decisive victory over Butler make him an important offstage presence whose actions have significant consequences for several main characters.

## Role in the Story

Lucas serves as the measuring stick against which Bugs Butler's inadequacy is revealed. The office-boy at Fillmore Nicholas Theatrical Enterprises — Lucas's most ardent supporter — insists that "anybody thinks Bugs Butler can last six rounds with Lew Lucas, I've two bucks right here in my vest pocket that says it ain't so" [013-P01]. This prediction proves accurate: Lucas knocks Butler out in the middle of the third round [013-P07].

His victory is a moment of poetic justice. Sally, who has come to despise Butler after witnessing his treatment of Ginger Kemp at the training camp, reads the fight result "with fierce satisfaction" and drinks Lew Lucas's health in her morning coffee, hoping "his old mother was proud of him" [013-P08]. For Sally, Lucas functions as an unwitting avenger — "if he had been a personal friend of Ginger's he could not, considering the brief time at his disposal, have avenged him with more thoroughness" [013-P08].

## Personality & Traits

What little is known of Lucas comes through the admiring testimony of the office-boy, who describes him as "a hot sketch" and "a bird" [013-P01]. The boy's hero-worship is rooted in hometown pride: "He used to live on the next street to me... I've seen his old mother as close as I am to you. Say, I seen her a hundred times" [013-P01]. This detail grounds Lucas in a working-class background, making him a figure of authentic local accomplishment in contrast to the overhyped Butler.

In the ring, Lucas is described as "shifty" and a powerful puncher — the two "Wise Guys" at the training camp agree that "Lew Lucas is just as shifty, and he can punch" [013-P05]. His fighting is efficient and devastating: in round one he works on Butler's left eye, in round two on his body, and in round three he ends it with "a short left-hook followed by a right to the point of the jaw" [013-P07].

## Key Relationships

- **Bugs Butler**: Lucas's opponent in the title fight. Lucas defeats him decisively, knocking him out in the third round [013-P07].
- **The Office-Boy**: Lucas's most passionate fan, a pimpled youth who works at Fillmore's theatrical office and who publicly cheers when reading of Lucas's victory in the morning paper [013-P07].
- **Sally Nicholas**: Though they never meet, Sally feels deep gratitude toward Lucas for avenging Ginger Kemp. She "drank Lew Lucas's health in a cup of coffee" on the morning after the fight [013-P08].

## Notable Scenes

### The Championship Fight at Jersey City

The title bout takes place at Jersey City before a sparse crowd — the arena is less than a quarter full, the public having lost faith after Butler's embarrassing training-camp showing. Lucas makes quick work of his opponent: round one targets Butler's left eye, round two his body, and "in round three he had knocked him out" [013-P07]. The brevity and efficiency of Lucas's victory contrast sharply with Butler's flashy, ineffectual style.

### The Aftermath

The fight's outcome spreads happiness widely. Lucas's seconds are so delighted that "one of whom went so far as to kiss him." The crowd, who had bet heavily on the champion, are thrilled. The two Wise Guys win five hundred dollars. The office-boy "whooped gleefully" reading the news on the subway. And Sally, breakfasting in her apartment, reads the sporting page "with fierce satisfaction" [013-P07]–[013-P08].


> "Yes, *sir*!" said the stripling with emphasis. "Lew Lucas is a hot sketch. He used to live on the next street to me," he added as clinching evidence of his hero's prowess. ## Significance

Lew Lucas represents authentic competence in a novel full of pretenders and overconfident schemers. Unlike Fillmore Nicholas with his doomed speculations, or Bugs Butler with his untested reputation, Lucas is exactly what he claims to be — the legitimate champion. His decisive victory serves as narrative justice, punishing Butler's vanity and, by extension, the culture of hype and get-rich-quick thinking that Butler and Fillmore embody.
