# Jules

Jules is the night-clerk and lift attendant at the [[hotel-normandie|Hotel Normandie]]
in [[roville-sur-mer|Roville-sur-Mer]], described as "the stout young native who combined the offices of night-clerk and lift attendant at that establishment" (002-068). A figure of perpetual overwork who seemingly never leaves his post, Jules becomes an unwitting participant — and emotional witness — in the late-night encounters between
[[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]], [[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]], and [[bruce-carmyle|Bruce Carmyle]].

## Life at the Hotel Normandie

Jules embodies the overworked hotel employee of the French resort town. The novel establishes him as a fixture of the Normandie: "Jules was on duty in the hall throughout the night, but few of the Normandie's patrons made use of his services" (002-068). Sally observes that he "seemed to be on duty in some capacity or other all the time. His work, like women's, was never done" (002-069). Her "sympathetic heart, always at the disposal of the oppressed, had long ached for this overworked peon" (002-070). Throughout the night, Jules can be found "slumbering in a chair" behind the desk (002-069) or navigating the lift in a trance — until roused by guests requiring his services.

His one professional skill, by his own estimation, is operating the lift door. The novel describes it as "the one thing connected with his professional activities which he did really well — the opening, to wit, of the iron cage":

> "Jules' was the right way. He was accustomed to do it with a flourish, and generally remarked '*V'la!*' in a modest but self-congratulatory voice as though he would have liked to see another man who could have put through a job like that. Jules' opinion was that he might not be much to look at, but that he could open a lift door." (002-081)

## The Night Encounters

Jules plays a crucial, if comatose, role in the midnight meetings at the Normandie.
When Sally slips downstairs late at night and encounters first the red-haired young man
([[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]]) and later Bruce Carmyle, Jules is the drowsy witness to
these encounters. He navigates the lift "in a sort of somnambulistic trance" (002-076), is prodded
awake at the third floor, and conducts rapid-fire French dialogue with the hotel's
nocturnal visitors.

Wodehouse mines Jules's semi-conscious state for sustained comic effect. Jules could "do this sort of work without breaking his slumber. His brain, if you could call it that, was working automatically" (002-078). When called upon to explain himself, Jules launches into torrential French:

> "He explained very rapidly and at considerable length. The fact that neither of his hearers understood a word of what he was saying appeared not to have impressed itself upon him. Or, if he gave a thought to it, he dismissed the objection as trifling. He wanted to explain, and he explained. Words rushed from him like water from a geyser." (002-088)

Sally has developed a practical, wordless system for communicating with Jules: "All through her stay at Roville, she had found in dealing with the native population that actions spoke louder than words. If she wanted anything in a restaurant or at a shop, she pointed; and, when she wished the lift to stop, she prodded the man in charge. It was a system worth a dozen French conversation books" (002-080).

The scene reaches its emotional climax not through dialogue but through Jules's silent, tearful response to the forbearance of Sally and Bruce Carmyle, who have decided not to wake him further. The lift descends to the accompaniment of "brokenhearted gulps" as Jules sobs into "the bunch of cotton-waste which served him in the office of a pocket-handkerchief" (002-110).

## Character Significance

Jules serves multiple narrative functions. As a comic foil, his somnambulistic incompetence creates physical comedy that leavens the romantic tension of the late-night encounters. Wodehouse's description of Jules's "brain, if you could call it that" (002-078) typifies the author's affectionately condescending treatment of minor Continental characters.

But Jules also functions as an emotional barometer. His silent sobbing at Sally and Carmyle's kindness (002-110) externalizes the poignancy that the English characters — constrained by their national reticence — cannot express. The lift attendant who "might not be much to look at" becomes, however briefly, the novel's most emotionally transparent figure.

The comic set-piece of the lift descent also serves a structural purpose: it compresses Sally, Ginger, and Carmyle into a confined space, forcing the kind of proximity that Wodehouse uses throughout the novel to advance romantic plot. Jules's incompetence — stopping at wrong floors, launching into incomprehensible French — prolongs this enforced intimacy, becoming an unwitting agent of the romantic entanglement.

## Later References

Jules makes enough of an impression that he is recalled fondly later in the narrative.
In Chapter XII, Sally writes to Ginger with the parenthetical aside: "do you remember
Jules?" — suggesting that the overworked lift attendant has become a private joke
between them, a shared memory of their time in Roville-sur-Mer.
