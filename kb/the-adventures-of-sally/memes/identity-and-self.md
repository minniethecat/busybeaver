# Identity and Self

Wodehouse's comic characters are defined by their self-presentations, and *The Adventures of Sally* explores the gap between performed identity and authentic self. Sally's journey is fundamentally one of learning to see through others' performances and to trust her own nature.

## Performed Identities

Every major character in the novel performs a version of themselves. Fillmore performs the successful businessman — white waistcoat, hundred half-dollar cigars, talk of "big things" (001-064). Gerald performs the brilliant playwright whose talent only needs one chance (007-012). Mr. Faucitt performs the grand old actor, complete with "rolling periods" of oratory (001-028). Wodehouse's comedy lies in the gap between performance and reality.

## Sally's Authenticity

Sally stands apart through her relative authenticity. She sees through Fillmore's pretensions immediately (001-057) and cannot perform false politeness. Her speech to the party — honest, emotional, culminating in kissing Mr. Faucitt and bursting into tears (001-054) — is the opposite of Fillmore's disastrous non-speech (001-049). Sally's authenticity is her defining virtue.

## The Cost of Self-Deception

Gerald Foster's self-deception is the novel's cautionary example. He believes in his own genius so thoroughly that failure becomes a personal catastrophe rather than a professional setback. When his second play fails, "had another frost tonight. No good trying any more" (017-017), his identity collapses because it was built on illusion.

## Ginger's Unperformed Self

Ginger Kemp offers the novel's model of unperformed identity. He does not pretend to be more than he is — a boxer, a friend, eventually a husband. His authenticity makes him comic (he forgets the chops, 018-022) but also admirable. The novel's resolution endorses the unperformed life: Sally and Ginger's domestic happiness in Patchogue (018-003) requires no performance.
