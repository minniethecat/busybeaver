# The Flower Garden

A Broadway dancing resort — "a supper-club for after-theatre dining and dancing" (016-017) — that serves as the setting for several key scenes in the novel's New York chapters. Run by the genial Isadore Abrahams, the Flower Garden embodies the Jazz Age dancing craze of 1920s New York and provides Sally Nicholas with employment, a stage for romantic drama, and — through its caged songbird — the memory that crystallizes her life's decisive choice.

## Geography

The Flower Garden is located on Broadway in New York City (016-017; 008-003). It sits at the heart of the city's theatre and nightlife district, drawing a late-night crowd of after-theatre diners and dancers. Its proprietor, Isadore Abrahams, lives with his family at Far Rockaway, commuting to the establishment (014-281). The Flower Garden was "one of the many establishments of the same kind which had swum to popularity on the rising flood of New York's dancing craze" (016-017).

## Description in the Novel

Wodehouse presents the Flower Garden with characteristic comic ambivalence. The establishment's self-description is grandiose:

> "a supper-club for after-theatre dining and dancing," adding that "large and spacious, and sumptuously appointed," it was "one of the town's wonder-places, with its incomparable dance-floor, enchanting music, cuisine, and service de luxe." (016-017)

The novel's narrator adds a dry aside: "From which it may be gathered, even without his personal statements to that effect, that Isadore Abrahams thought well of the place" (016-017).

The decor reflects Abrahams's literal-minded fulfillment of the establishment's name. True to his advertising, Abrahams "had tried to give the public something as closely resembling a flower-garden as it was possible for an overcrowded, overheated, overnoisy Broadway dancing-resort to achieve" (016-051). The walls are festooned with paper roses, genuine tulips bloom in tubs by every pillar, and cages of songbirds hang from the ceiling (016-051).

Despite its pretensions, the Flower Garden's atmosphere is more raucous than refined. The orchestra "was of a calibre that discouraged vocal competition" (016-004), and the dancing floor regularly produces "the usual bumping struggle" that Sally endures with her partners (016-045). The master of ceremonies, Lee Schoenstein, presides over the nightly revels with gentlemanly efficiency (016-020).

Bruce Carmyle, visiting from England, provides the most damning outsider's verdict. After an evening in which "the clicking noise of the drumsticks maddened him" and "a loathing for the Flower Garden flowed over" him (016-098), he delivers the blunt assessment: "My God! What a place!" (016-109).

## Key Events Here

**Sally's Employment**: Having "been raised in enervating luxury," Sally's only marketable skill is ballroom dancing. She takes a job at the Flower Garden "as what is humorously called an 'instructress,' as if anybody could 'instruct' the men who came there. One was lucky if one saved one's life and wasn't quashed to death" (008-003).

**Mr. Cracknell's Romantic Agony**: Reginald Cracknell dances with Sally past the table where Mabel Hobson, the Flower Garden's newest "hostess," sits in cold silence. Miss Hobson "sat watching the revels with a distant hauteur" in "old gold and black," and "a sorrowful gulp escaped the stricken Mr. Cracknell as he shambled beneath her eye" (016-004).

**Bruce Carmyle's Proposal**: In the midst of the Flower Garden's noisy gaiety, Bruce Carmyle proposes to Sally. For a man of Carmyle's orthodox sensibilities — he "belonged to the orthodox school of thought which looks on moonlight and solitude as the proper setting for a proposal of marriage" — the surroundings "jarred upon him profoundly" (016-057). The proposal is interrupted by the establishment's resident comic performer, George Washington Williams, a trap-drummer who amuses patrons by mock-barbering them with drumsticks:

> "just as Mr. Carmyle was bending towards Sally in an access of manly sentiment, and was on the very verge of pouring out his soul in a series of well-phrased remarks, he was surprised and annoyed to find an Ethiopian to whom he had never been introduced leaning over him and taking quite unpardonable liberties with his back hair." (016-097)

Williams's "clicking noise of the drumsticks maddened him" and "a loathing for the Flower Garden flowed over Bruce Carmyle, and with it a feeling of suspicion and disapproval of everyone connected with the establishment" (016-098).

**The Bird's Song**: After the orchestra stops, one of the caged birds begins to sing. To Sally, wrestling with Carmyle's proposal, the song evokes a vision of Monk's Crofton — "cool, green, and peaceful under the mild English sun" (016-033) — and helps crystallize her decision. This moment — a caged bird's song in an overheated Broadway nightclub summoning the image of an English country garden — represents Wodehouse at his most delicately symbolic.

**Sally's Transformation**: The Flower Garden also hosts Sally's moment of unexpected pleasure. When Bruce Carmyle — an expert dancer — takes her onto the floor, Sally "found herself in the arms of a masterful expert, a man who danced better than she did, and suddenly there came to her a feeling that was almost gratitude, a miraculous slackening of her taut nerves, a delicious peace" (016-045). For a brief interval, the Flower Garden becomes not a comic battleground but a place of genuine pleasure.

## Cultural Context

The Flower Garden represents the Jazz Age phenomenon of the "dancing resort" that swept 1920s New York. In Wodehouse's comic vision, it functions as a site of social collision: the democratic informality of American nightlife (where a "coloured gentleman" may take liberties with an English aristocrat's hair) clashes with Bruce Carmyle's rigid class sensibilities. The establishment's literal-minded floral decor — paper roses, tub tulips, caged birds — serves as a gently satirical portrait of immigrant entrepreneurial aspiration (the Jewish Abrahams making good on his advertising promises).

The Flower Garden also functions as a stage for romantic farce. Carmyle's proposal amid drumsticks and birdsong exemplifies Wodehouse's technique of undermining high emotional stakes with absurd physical comedy, a pattern repeated throughout the novel's romantic plot. The caged bird's song — an intrusion of natural beauty into a space of artificial gaiety — becomes the novel's most delicate symbol, linking the Flower Garden to Monk's Crofton across the Atlantic and foreshadowing Sally's eventual choice of a simpler life.
