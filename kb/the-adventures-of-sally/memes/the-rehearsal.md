# The Detroit Rehearsals

The Detroit rehearsals of *The Primrose Way* represent the novel's central crisis — the
point at which accumulated tensions converge to demand decisive action. Stranded in
Detroit by the Spanish Flu epidemic that has closed theatres across America, Gerald
Foster's company endures weeks of dispiriting, repetitive rehearsals that sap morale
and threaten to destroy the production before it ever reaches Broadway.

## Context

Following Gerald Foster's [[success]] in securing a Detroit tryout for *The Primrose Way*
with Fillmore Nicholas's backing, the company travels to Detroit to prepare for opening.
The outbreak of Spanish Influenza closes every theatre in the city, leaving the cast
stranded in "strange hotels in a strange city" with nothing to do but rehearse lines
"which had been polished to the last syllable more than a week ago" (006-091). Sally,
in New York, receives the news from Mrs. Meecher: "Gone to Detroit, he has. Miss Doland,
too... They been rehearsing ever since you left" (005-006).

The epidemic serves as a narrative catalyst: an external force that transforms what
might have been a routine pre-Broadway tryout into an existential crisis for the
production. The quarantine conditions compress the company's interpersonal dynamics,
amplifying every grievance and insecurity until they become uncontainable.

Mr. Faucitt, who attended an early rehearsal before the closure, described the
experience with characteristic severity: "I attended a dogfight which I was informed
was a rehearsal. There is no rehearsing nowadays" (005-037). His more pointed
assessment concerned the leading lady, Mabel Hobson, whom he declared possessed "the
face of an angel and the histrionic ability of that curious suet pudding which our
estimable Mrs. Meecher is apt to give us on Fridays" (005-043). Faucitt's judgment,
though comically harsh, serves as an early warning signal that the production's
casting is fatally compromised.

## The Crisis

By the time Sally arrives in Detroit to investigate, she finds a company in crisis.
Gerald Foster explains the situation: "We've been hanging about here day after day,
getting bored to death all the time... The company's going all to pieces. They're sick
of rehearsing and rehearsing when nobody knows if we'll ever open" (006-055). The cast,
once "all keyed up a week ago," has been "sagging ever since" (006-055). The language
captures a precise theatrical pathology: the slow erosion of morale that comes from
endless preparation without performance — rehearsal without a horizon.

Sally attends a rehearsal at the theatre and witnesses the collapse firsthand. As she
enters the dark auditorium, "voices came to her with that thin and reedy effect which
is produced by people talking in an empty building" (006-080). She sees Gerald sitting
in the front row "beside a man with a bald head fringed with orange hair whom she took
correctly to be Mr. Bunbury, the producer" (006-080). The atmosphere is tense, the
acoustics of the empty theatre rendering every line hollow and exposed.

Wodehouse describes the theatre world as "simply a large nursery and its inhabitants
children who readily become fretful if anything goes wrong" (006-091). This metaphor
governs the entire rehearsal sequence: grown artists reduced to petulant squabbling by
the combination of enforced idleness and professional anxiety. The rehearsal features:

> The world of the theatre is simply a large nursery and its inhabitants children who readily become fretful if anything goes wrong. The waiting and the uncertainty, the loafing about in strange hotels in a strange city, the dreary rehearsing of lines which had been polished to the last syllable more than a week ago \u2014 these things had sapped the nerve of the Primrose Way Company and demoralization had set in. It would require only a trifle to produce an explosion.
>
> (006-091)

- Mr. Bunbury snapping: "Miss Winch, did I or did I not ask you to refrain from chewing
  gum during rehearsal?" (006-097)
- Teddy, the stage-manager, delivering lines "in an injured tone" (006-091)
- Elsa Doland, the one professional, "looking very attractive" and performing competently
  amid the chaos (006-080)

The crisis explodes when Mabel Hobson interrupts the rehearsal. "You bet your sorrowful
existence I'm interrupting the rehearsal," she declares. "Write up other people's parts?
Not while I have my strength!" (006-118). The rehearsal collapses into chaos as Mr.
Bunbury protests: "For heaven's sake! Are we rehearsing, or is this a debating society?"
(006-125). The line wittily collapses the distinction between theatre and its discontents:
the rehearsal has become a debate about the rehearsal, a performance of its own
impossibility.

Sally watches "with a sinking heart. It was all wrong." She sees that Miss Hobson's
"very physical attributes only served to emphasize and point her hopeless incapacity"
(006-135) — beauty rendered perverse by its misapplication. The final absurdity is
the missing paper-knife prop: "Miss Hobson took it after the rehearsal yesterday,"
Gladys Winch reveals, "and threw it negligently at the theatre cat" (006-161).

## Participants

- **Elsa Doland**: The one capable performer. Mr. Faucitt pronounced her "fine" (005-009). Her professionalism amid the chaos marks her as the production's moral centre.
- **Gerald Foster**: The anxious playwright, watching his first chance "just chucked away" (006-055). His despair opens the emotional space for Sally's intervention.
- **Mr. Bunbury**: The exasperated producer, "a man with a bald head fringed with orange hair" (006-080). His protests grow increasingly futile as the rehearsal unravels.
- **Mabel Hobson**: The beautiful but incapable leading lady, whose tantrum disrupts the rehearsal (006-118). Her very beauty becomes an indictment of the casting system that elevated her.
- **Gladys Winch**: The sardonic supporting actress who exposes Hobson's misdeeds (006-161). Her deadpan revelation about the paper-knife provides the scene's comic coda.
- **Fillmore Nicholas**: The beleaguered producer, "bleated" and "obsequious" before his leading lady (006-151).
- **Sally Nicholas**: The observer whose intervention — offering her legacy — rescues the play (006-204).

## Consequences

The rehearsal crisis becomes the turning point of the novel. Overhearing Gerald's
despair, Sally makes the pivotal decision to offer Fillmore her inheritance to buy out
the backers and take control of the production. Her words — "take it as a gift with my
blessing thrown in" (006-204) — set in motion the second half of the novel: the
successful Detroit opening, Sally's professional triumph, and her eventual independence.

This moment crystallizes the novel's central thematic concern: the relationship between
money and agency. Sally's inheritance, until this point a source of anxiety and
predatory suitors, becomes an instrument of liberation when she chooses to invest it
in something she believes in. The gesture transforms her from passive heiress to active
producer — a role that anticipates her ultimate professional identity.

## Narrative Function

The Detroit rehearsals serve as the novel's **complication**: the point at which existing
tensions — Gerald's artistic insecurity, Fillmore's ineffectual management, Mabel
Hobson's incompetence, the company's fraying morale — converge into a crisis demanding
Sally's intervention. The scene also functions as a **comic set-piece**, with Wodehouse
deploying the full arsenal of theatrical farce: the tyrannical director, the
chewing-gum rebellion, the missing prop, and the outraged diva.

Structurally, the Detroit episode mirrors the Roville chapters. Both are sites of
temporary exile where Sally encounters a crisis that tests her character. But where
Roville was a holiday — a suspension of ordinary life — Detroit is work: a crucible
in which Sally discovers her capacity for decisive, consequential action.
