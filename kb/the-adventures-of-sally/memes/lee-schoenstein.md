# Lee Schoenstein

**Lee Schoenstein** is the "gentlemanly master of ceremonies" at The Flower Garden, a dancing resort on [[broadway|Broadway]] (016-007). In his professional role, he manages the flow of dance partners and orchestrates the evening's social choreography — a function that alternately assists and obstructs the novel's romantic plot.

## Role in the Story

Schoenstein appears exclusively in the Flower Garden sequences of Chapter XVI, where his role as master of ceremonies makes him both a facilitator and an impediment to Sally's encounters with the men in her life. His function is to introduce new dance partners to patrons — a service that is welcome when it produces [[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]] and markedly less so when it delivers unwanted suitors.

## Notable Scenes

### The Unwanted Partner

On an evening when Sally is attending the Flower Garden, Schoenstein presents a new partner whom she finds distinctly unpromising. The narrator records her reaction with characteristic understatement: "She had seen him often, but this was the first time that Lee Schoenstein, the gentlemanly master of ceremonies, had inflicted him on her" (016-007). The verb "inflicted" captures both Schoenstein's impersonal efficiency and Sally's lack of agency in the partner-assignment process.

### The Interruption

At a critical moment — just as Sally is about to receive Ginger Kemp at her table — Schoenstein appears with yet another assigned partner:

> Lee Schoenstein was standing at the table, and by his side an expectant youth with a small moustache and pince-nez. (016-129)

The timing is exquisitely frustrating. Schoenstein, operating according to the mechanical logic of his job, has no awareness that he is interrupting a long-awaited romantic reunion. He is simply doing what masters of ceremonies do: presenting partners.

Sally's response — "That was Mr. Schoenstein" (016-131) — is delivered with a flatness that barely conceals her exasperation. The moment illustrates how the social machinery of the Flower Garden, however efficiently managed, operates without regard for the emotional lives of its patrons.

### The Routine Operation

Between these two incidents, Schoenstein operates in the background: "The orchestra, never silent for long at the Flower Garden, had started again, and Lee Schoenstein, the master of ceremonies, was presenting a new partner" (016-020). The sentence captures the endless cycle of the Flower Garden — music, partners, dancing, repetition — with Schoenstein as its tireless master of ceremonies.

## Personality and Traits

Schoenstein is characterized primarily by the epithet "gentlemanly," which suggests a surface politeness and professional competence. He has gold teeth — a detail Sally notes with a certain weary familiarity (016-032) — and he performs his duties with impersonal efficiency.

His defining trait is obliviousness: he cannot distinguish between a patron who wants a partner and one who emphatically does not, because the distinction is not part of his job description. He provides partners; whether they are welcome is not his concern.

## Significance

Schoenstein is a minor comic obstacle in the novel's romantic machinery. Like many Wodehouse functionaries, he exists to complicate the hero's path without ever understanding that he is doing so. His presence also helps establish the Flower Garden as a specific social environment — a place governed by its own routines and rituals, where romance must navigate around the institutional furniture.

The character also serves as a mild satire of the commercialized social space: the Flower Garden offers "dancing" as a commodity, complete with assigned partners and a master of ceremonies, but it cannot manufacture genuine connection. That must happen despite the machinery, not because of it.
