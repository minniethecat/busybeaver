# Fillmore Nicholas

Fillmore Nicholas is Sally's elder brother and the novel's primary comic character. A young man of fluctuating fortunes and unwavering self-regard, Fillmore moves from Wall Street clerk to theatrical producer and back, his pretensions consistently punctured by his sister's sharp tongue.

## Personality and Traits

Fillmore is defined by his pomposity. At the boarding-house party, he "smoothed a wrinkle out of his white waistcoat" and surveyed the room "with eyes so haughtily drooping" (001-045). He has not worn well: "At the age of seven he had been an extraordinarily beautiful child" (001-046), but adulthood has brought corpulence and self-importance. His expansion "like an indiarubber ball which has been sat on" when Sally lectures him (001-057) is a characteristic physical metaphor for his emotional resilience.
> "Fillmore Nicholas had not worn well. At the age of seven he had been an extraordinarily beautiful child, but after that he had gone all to pieces; and now, at the age of twenty-five, it would be idle to deny that he was something of a mess. For the three years preceding his twenty-fifth birthday, restricted means and hard work had kept his figure in check; but with money there had come an ever-increasing sleekness. He looked as if he fed too often and too well."
>
> (001-046)


## Key Relationships

- **Sally Nicholas**: Fillmore is intimidated by his younger sister, who "had always been able to prevent himself being intimidated by Sally" (001-057). She sees through his pretensions but loves him anyway, lending him twenty thousand dollars for his theatrical venture (007-003).

- **The Theatre Company**: As producer of *The Primrose Way*, Fillmore blossoms — putting on weight, buying hundred half-dollar cigars, and becoming "very fussy about what he had for lunch" (007-005).

## Notable Scenes

- **The Disastrous Speech** (ch01): Asked to respond to Mr. Faucitt's toast, Fillmore delivers the novel's most memorable anticlimax: "I'm sure you don't want a speech... Very good of you to drink our health. Thank you" (001-049).

- **The Manager's Glory** (ch07): Fillmore's transformation from "a serf looking after paper-knives" to a manager who "blossomed out into soft billowy curves" (007-005) is pure Wodehouse comedy.

## Narrative Arc

Fillmore's arc is circular — he rises and falls and rises again, unchanged by experience. Wodehouse treats him with affectionate mockery: his pretensions are absurd, but his resilience is admirable. He is the novel's most purely comic creation.

## Significance and Reception

Fillmore embodies the novel's critique of social climbing while making it impossible to dislike the climber. His self-importance is transparent, his failures are predictable, and his persistence is somehow touching. He is Wodehouse's argument that pretension, when sufficiently sincere, becomes a form of innocence.


Fillmore's character is revealed through the eyes of those who must endure him:

- **Sally's view**: Sally sees her brother with ruthless clarity — "She had always been able to prevent himself being intimidated by Sally" (001-057). Her affection coexists with a complete understanding of his absurdity. She lends him money knowing he will squander it, because her love does not depend on his competence.

- **Gerald Foster's view**: As a fellow theatrical, Gerald regards Fillmore with professional condescension. Fillmore is the money man rather than the artist — useful for financing, but never to be taken seriously as a producer of taste.

- **Mr. Faucitt's view**: The elderly thespian treats Fillmore with the benign contempt the seasoned professional reserves for the wealthy amateur. Faucitt's elegant toast at Sally's party (001-048) serves as an implicit rebuke to Fillmore's subsequent oratorical collapse.

- **The Boarding-House View**: The residents of Mrs. Meecher's observe Fillmore's trajectory with the amused detachment of people who have seen many young men rise and fall. His pretensions are a source of communal entertainment.

## Author's Narrative Technique

Wodehouse's handling of Fillmore showcases his comic method in miniature:

- **Physical Metaphor as Character**: Fillmore's body is his biography. His childhood beauty fading into "corpulence" (001-046), his "soft billowy curves" (007-005) during prosperity, and his deflation under Sally's criticism — Wodehouse uses physique as a running visual gag that doubles as character analysis.

- **The Failed Orator**: Fillmore's speech at the party (001-049) is a masterclass in comic anticlimax. The elaborate buildup — Mr. Faucitt's florid toast, the expectant silence — collapses into Fillmore's seven-word surrender. Wodehouse sets up the expectation of a long, pompous address and then delights in denying it.

- **Circular Structure as Comic Philosophy**: Unlike Sally, who grows and changes, Fillmore's arc is deliberately circular. He rises, falls, and begins again — unchanged. This is Wodehouse's comic worldview: some people cannot be improved, and the attempt to improve them is itself the joke.

- **Affectionate Mockery**: Wodehouse never condemns Fillmore. The mockery is too warm for satire and too precise for sentimentality. Fillmore is absurd but never contemptible — a balance that defines Wodehouse's comic voice.
