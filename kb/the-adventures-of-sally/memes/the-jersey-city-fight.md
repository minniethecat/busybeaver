# The Jersey City Fight

The Jersey City championship fight between Bugs Butler and Lew Lucas is the climax of the novel's boxing subplot, taking place approximately thirty hours after Butler's disastrous training-camp exhibition at White Plains. The fight's commercial failure — caused by the public's loss of confidence in Butler — completes Fillmore Nicholas's financial ruin and provides a moment of narrative justice that satisfies multiple characters.

## Background

Fillmore Nicholas, having lost his inheritance through a series of bad investments, pins his remaining hopes on promoting Butler's title challenge against reigning lightweight champion Lew Lucas. However, on the eve of the fight, Butler is knocked down by his own sparring-partner, Ginger Kemp, during a public training session at White Plains. The incident, witnessed by newspaper reporters, becomes a sensation that destroys Butler's credibility [013-P05]–[013-P06].

The public, "never had been any too sure that this fellow Bugs Butler had a chance of putting up a scrap with the champion that would be worth paying to see," abandons the fight entirely after reading "that he couldn't even stop his sparring-partners slamming him all around the place" [013-P08].

## The Fight

The bout lasts less than three full rounds. Lew Lucas, described by the "Wise Guys" as "just as shifty, and he can punch," makes quick work of his overmatched opponent [013-P05]:
- Round one: Lucas targets Butler's left eye
- Round two: Lucas works on Butler's body
- Round three: Lucas ends it with "a short left-hook followed by a right to the point of the jaw" [013-P07]

## Aftermath

> He thought he could get it all back by staging this fight of his that came off in Jersey City last night. And if everything had gone right he might have got afloat again. But it seems as if he can't touch anything without it turning to mud. On the very day before the fight was to come off, the poor mutt who was going against the champion goes and lets a sparring-partner of his own knock him down and fool around with him. With all the newspaper men there too! ... Well, that killed the whole thing. The house wasn't a quarter full, and after he'd paid these two pluguglies their guarantees, which they insisted on having before they'd so much as go into the ring, he was just about cleaned out.
>
> (013-070)


The fight's outcome produces widespread satisfaction. Lucas and his seconds celebrate — one second kisses the champion. The crowd who bet on Lucas are delighted. The two Wise Guys win five hundred dollars. The pimpled office-boy from Fillmore Nicholas Theatrical Enterprises cheers on the subway [013-P07].

Sally reads the result with "fierce satisfaction," having "acquired a lively distaste for Bugs Butler" after his treatment of Ginger. She drinks Lew Lucas's health in her coffee and hopes "his old mother was proud of him" [013-P08]. Ginger, when asked about Butler, shows characteristic fairness: "He isn't a bad sort of chap, really" [013-P08].

## Financial Consequences

For Fillmore, the fight is catastrophic. The arena is less than a quarter full. After paying the fighters' guaranteed purses, which they "insisted on having before they'd so much as go into the ring," Fillmore is "just about cleaned out" [013-P08]. This marks the definitive end of his attempts to recover his fortune through the fight game.

## Significance

The Jersey City fight serves as the novel's most concentrated example of poetic justice. Butler's vanity and cruelty toward Ginger are repaid with public humiliation and professional defeat. Fillmore's speculative instincts, which have led him from bad investments to worse ones, finally meet their logical endpoint. And Ginger's quiet integrity — refusing to gloat over his tormentor's downfall — is affirmed in Sally's eyes.
