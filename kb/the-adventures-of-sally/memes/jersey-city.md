# Jersey City

Jersey City, New Jersey, is the location of the ill-fated lightweight championship bout between Bugs Butler and Lew Lucas — the culminating event of the novel's boxing subplot and the instrument of Fillmore Nicholas's final financial ruin.

## The Championship Fight

The fight takes place "some thirty hours after the painful episode recorded in the last chapter" — the disastrous White Plains training session where Butler was knocked down by his own sparring-partner. Public confidence in Butler had evaporated, and the consequences are starkly visible at the arena: "the house wasn't a quarter full" [013-P07]–[013-P08].

Butler's defeat is swift and decisive. Lew Lucas knocks him out "in the middle of the third round" with "a short left-hook followed by a right to the point of the jaw" [013-P07]. In round one, Lucas works on Butler's left eye; in round two, on his body; and round three ends it.

## Aftermath

The fight's outcome spreads happiness widely:
- Lew Lucas and his seconds are "noticeably pleased," with one second going so far as to kiss the champion [013-P07]
- The crowd, who had bet heavily on Lucas, are delighted
- The two "Wise Guys" win five hundred dollars on their bet against Butler
- The pimpled office-boy from Fillmore Nicholas Theatrical Enterprises "whooped gleefully" when reading the news on the subway [013-P07]
- Sally herself reads the result "with fierce satisfaction," having developed "a lively distaste for Bugs Butler" [013-P08]

Only Butler himself is displeased, slouching from the ring with "a heavy frown" and "peevish bearing" that "struck a jarring note in the general gaiety" [013-P07].

## Financial Impact

For Fillmore Nicholas, the fight is catastrophic. He had staked everything on Butler's drawing power, but the near-empty arena meant he "was just about cleaned out" after paying the fighters' guarantees "which they insisted on having before they'd so much as go into the ring" [013-P08]. The Jersey City fight marks the end of Fillmore's career as a boxing promoter and the final chapter in his series of financial disasters.


> The only real happiness, we are told, is to be obtained by bringing happiness to others. Bugs Butler's mood, accordingly, when some thirty hours after the painful episode he awoke from a state of coma in the ring at Jersey City to discover that Mr. Lew Lucas had knocked him out in the middle of the third round, should have been one of quiet contentment. His inability to block a short left-hook followed by a right to the point of the jaw had ameliorated quite a number of existences.
>
> (013-068)

## Significance

Jersey City represents the endpoint of the speculative logic that drives the novel's subplots. Like Fillmore's stock market gambles and Broadway investments, the boxing match is a bet on appearance over substance — and like those other ventures, it fails. The location, a working-class New Jersey city across the Hudson from Manhattan, underscores the distance between Fillmore's Broadway dreams and the harsh realities of the fight game.
