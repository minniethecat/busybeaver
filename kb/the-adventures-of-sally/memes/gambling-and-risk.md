# Gambling and Risk

Risk runs through *The Adventures of Sally* as both a literal activity and a metaphor for life choices. Characters gamble with money, careers, and love, and Wodehouse uses the language of betting to illuminate character.

## Financial Gambling

The novel opens with a chorus of speculative advice. Augustus Bartlett recommends "a couple of hundred thousand in some good, safe bond-issue" and "a flutter in anything good that crops up. Multiple Steel's worth looking at" (001-005). The "mild young man" insists on Benny Whistler for the heavyweight championship (001-008). Wall Street and the boxing ring are presented as equally speculative.

## Monte Carlo Dreams

The "gloomy man" brightens at the mention of Monte Carlo: "if I'd had another fifty dollars... just fifty... I'd have..." (001-027). Monte Carlo represents a fantasy of risk rewarded — a place where chance might transform fortune. Sally herself jokes about losing money there "properly" (001-026), though she never goes.

## The Biggest Gamble: Love

Sally's romantic choices are the novel's deepest gambles. Her engagement to Gerald Foster is a bet on his potential that fails. Her eventual choice of Ginger Kemp is a different kind of risk — not a bet on future glory but a commitment to present reliability. The novel suggests that the best gambles are not the most speculative but the most genuine.

## Fillmore's Speculative Nature

Fillmore embodies the comic speculator. From Wall Street to theatrical producing, he follows the promise of quick success. His "deal... which is going to be big" (001-064) is never specified, a running joke about his grandiosity. His willingness to risk Sally's money on Gerald's play reveals both his loyalty and his poor judgment.
