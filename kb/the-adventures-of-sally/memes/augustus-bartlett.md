# Augustus Bartlett

Augustus Bartlett is a minor character who appears at the opening of the novel as a guest at [[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]]'s party at [[mrs-meechers-boardinghouse|Mrs. Meecher's Boardinghouse]]. He works in "an intensely subordinate position in the firm of Kahn, Morris and Brown, the Wall Street brokers" and affects "a brisk, incisive style of speech, as befitted a man in close touch with the great ones of Finance" (001-014).

## Role in the Story

Augustus Bartlett serves primarily as a comic type: the minor Wall Street functionary who dispenses financial advice with an authority his position does not warrant. At Sally's party, when the conversation turns to what she should do with her legacy, Bartlett leads the "conservative and prudent element," advising her to "sink a couple of hundred thousand in some good, safe bond-issue" and to consider speculative stocks like "Multiple Steel" which "they tell me it'll be up to a hundred and fifty before next Saturday" (001-014).

He is the first to recover from the shock when Sally reveals her inheritance is only twenty-five thousand dollars, not the rumored millions, immediately recalibrating his advice: "If I'd twenty-five thousand, I'd buy Amalgamated..." (001-020).

> "Let me tell you," said Augustus Bartlett, briskly, "what I'd do, if I were you." Augustus Bartlett, who occupied an intensely subordinate position in the firm of Kahn, Morris and Brown, the Wall Street brokers, always affected a brisk, incisive style of speech, as befitted a man in close touch with the great ones of Finance. "I'd sink a couple of hundred thousand in some good, safe bond-issue—we've just put one out which you would do well to consider—and play about with the rest. When I say play about, I mean have a flutter in anything good that crops up. Multiple Steel's worth looking at. They tell me it'll be up to a hundred and fifty before next Saturday."
>
> (001-005)

## Personality and Traits

Bartlett's defining characteristic is his brisk, self-important financial manner — a comic parody of Wall Street confidence. Despite his subordinate position, he speaks with the assurance of a master of the universe, dispensing investment advice as though he were J.P. Morgan himself. His "brisk, incisive style of speech" is described as being carefully cultivated "as befitted a man in close touch with the great ones of Finance" (001-014).

His conservatism provides comic contrast to the "more sporting section" at the party, who are more interested in betting Sally's money on a racehorse named Benny Whistler than in sound investments.

## Key Relationships

- **[[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]]**: Fellow boarder at Mrs. Meecher's. Bartlett is a guest at her party and takes an active interest in advising her on her inheritance.
- **[[elsa-doland|Elsa Doland]]**: Fellow boarder and party guest. Elsa sits on Bartlett's left at the dinner table and has "other views" about what Sally should do with her money — specifically, "Buy a theatre, Sally, and put on good stuff" (001-015).

## Notable Scenes

**The Investment Advice (Chapter I)**. Bartlett's moment in the narrative spotlight comes during the dinner-party discussion of Sally's inheritance. He proposes a split strategy: three hundred thousand in Liberty Bonds and the rest in "some safe real estate" (001-018). When Sally reveals the actual sum is far smaller, his pivot to "Amalgamated" is cut off by Sally's announcement that she plans to travel to Europe and then find "some nice cosy little business" (001-020).

**Fillmore's Waistcoat (Chapter I)**. Bartlett's reaction to Fillmore's evening attire provides one of the party's visual jokes: Sally notes that she "saw Augustus Bartlett curl up like a burnt feather when he caught sight of it" — Fillmore's white, bulging waistcoat with its pearl buttons offending Bartlett's conservative sensibilities (001-036).

## Significance

Augustus Bartlett is a minor comic character who adds texture to the novel's opening scene. His Wall Street affectations and investment advice contribute to the party's atmosphere of eager speculation about Sally's fortune, and his deflation — along with the other guests — when the true amount is revealed sets up Sally's declaration of independence. He represents the conventional financial wisdom that Sally, in her own impulsive way, will largely ignore.
