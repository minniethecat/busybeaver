# Bugs Butler

Bugs Butler is a professional pugilist billed as "the coming lightweight champion of the world," whose training camp at White Plains and subsequent championship bout against Lew Lucas form a key subplot in the latter half of *The Adventures of Sally*. Managed by Lester Burrowes, Butler is portrayed as a physically formidable but ultimately flawed fighter whose overconfidence and lack of discipline lead to a humiliating public embarrassment and a financial catastrophe for Fillmore Nicholas.

## Role in the Story

Bugs Butler functions as a catalyst for Fillmore Nicholas's final financial ruin. Fillmore, having already lost his inheritance through a series of bad investments, pins his hopes on promoting Butler's championship fight against Lew Lucas as a means of recovering his fortune. Butler's disastrous public training session — in which he is knocked down by his own sparring-partner, Ginger Kemp — destroys public confidence in the match, resulting in a near-empty arena and leaving Fillmore "just about cleaned out" [013-P08].

Butler's narrative role is that of the overhyped contender whose reputation far exceeds his actual ability, a classic Wodehouse archetype of the boastful figure brought low by his own vanity.

## Personality & Traits

Butler displays an almost comical self-assurance. In the ring during his public training session, "it would be too little to say that his bearing was confident: he comported himself with the carefree jauntiness of an infant about to demolish a Noah's Ark with a tack-hammer" [013-P05]. He is acutely aware of his audience, especially "bright eyes watching him," and prioritizes flashy, "scientific" boxing over effective fighting because "the girls like smooth work" [013-P05].

This vanity becomes his undoing. When Ginger Kemp lands a heavy blow to his ribs, Butler's composure shatters. He abandons his scientific approach and "lapsed back into the methods of his unskilled novitiate," swinging wildly and eventually stumbling into a knockdown before the assembled newspaper reporters [013-P06]. His manager, Lester Burrowes, watches in "helpless horror — the sort of horror an elder of the church might feel if he saw his favourite bishop yielding in public to the fascination of jazz" [013-P06].

## Key Relationships

- **Lester Burrowes**: Butler's manager, who has built a lucrative business around his protégé's cool, scientific fighting style. Burrowes is horrified when Butler abandons this approach in a fit of temper against a mere sparring-partner [013-P01].
- **Ginger Kemp**: Butler's sparring-partner, hired through Fillmore Nicholas. When Butler tries to make a fool of Ginger in front of Sally, Ginger retaliates with unexpected ferocity, knocking Butler down and triggering the chain of events that leads to the fight's commercial failure [013-P06].
- **Lew Lucas**: The reigning lightweight champion and Butler's opponent in the title fight. Lucas knocks Butler out in the middle of the third round at Jersey City with "a short left-hook followed by a right to the point of the jaw" [013-P07].
- **Fillmore Nicholas**: The fight's promoter, who stakes his remaining capital on Butler's drawing power and loses everything when the public stays away [013-P08].

## Notable Scenes

### The Training Camp at White Plains

> "Who *is* he! That's what they're all saying, all the wise guys. Who has Bugs Butler ever licked?" said the office-boy. "A lot of stiffs out on the coast, that's all. Ginks nobody has ever heard of, except Cyclone Mullins, and it took that false alarm fifteen rounds to get a referee's decision over *him*."
>
> (013-039)


Sally visits Butler's training camp at White Plains, a large room "half barn, half gymnasium" filled with athletic appliances and a roped-off boxing ring. The commercialization of the sport is evident: for fifty cents, the public may watch the contender train. Butler's manager, Lester Burrowes, laments that "not all of these... had parted with solid coin" — many attendees are newspapermen on the free list [013-P01].

### The Public Sparring Session

In front of an audience that includes Sally Nicholas, Butler attempts to show off against his sparring-partner Ginger Kemp. His showboating — "flicking" Ginger lightly and "circling sinuously about the ring" — is intended to impress, but it backfires when Ginger, stung by the insult of being used as an exhibition piece in front of Sally, fights back in earnest. In the ensuing brawl, Butler's feet get crossed and he falls, creating a newspaper sensation that destroys his credibility [013-P05]–[013-P06].

### The Championship Fight

The title bout against Lew Lucas at Jersey City is a disaster. With public confidence shattered by the training-camp fiasco, the arena is less than a quarter full. Butler is knocked out in the third round. The defeat spreads happiness widely — Lucas and his seconds are delighted, the "Wise Guys" who bet against Butler win five hundred dollars, and Sally herself reads the result "with fierce satisfaction," having "acquired a lively distaste for Bugs Butler" [013-P07]–[013-P08].

## Significance

Bugs Butler represents the theme of illusory [[success]] that runs through the novel. Like Fillmore's speculative investments, Butler's reputation is built on appearance rather than substance. His downfall serves as the instrument of Fillmore's final financial collapse, reinforcing the novel's critique of get-rich-quick thinking and the dangers of overconfidence.

Butler also provides the catalyst for Ginger Kemp's unexpected moment of heroism. Ginger's refusal to be humiliated in front of Sally — fighting back against a professional boxer despite having no chance of winning — reveals the depth of his feeling for her and marks a turning point in their relationship.
