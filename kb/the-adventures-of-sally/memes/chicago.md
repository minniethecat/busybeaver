# Chicago

Chicago is the city where *The Primrose Way* has its out-of-town tryout — and where the novel's theatrical plot reaches its zenith before its collapse. The decision to open in Chicago rather than New York, proposed by Ike Schumann, reshapes the novel's middle section.

## The Tryout

Fillmore receives the suggestion to "put it into Chicago before opening in New York" (008-012) and, after deliberation, accedes. Gerald Foster travels to Chicago to superintend the opening, leaving Sally waiting in New York. The distance created by this separation contributes to the gradual erosion of their relationship.

## The Triumph

Chicago embraces *The Primrose Way* with spectacular enthusiasm:

> "Chicago, it appeared from Fillmore's account, was paying little attention to anything except *The Primrose Way*. National problems had ceased to interest the citizens. Local problems left them cold. Their minds were riveted to the exclusion of all else on the problem of how to secure seats. The production of the piece, according to Fillmore, had been the most terrific experience that had come to stir Chicago since the great fire." (012-011)

## Elsa Doland's Rise

It is in Chicago that Elsa Doland, elevated from a minor part by Fillmore, becomes a star. Gladys Winch reports with honest generosity: "you can't realize, not having seen her play there, what an amazing hit she has made. She really is a sensation. Everybody says she's going to be the biggest thing on record" (013-008). This [[success]] inspires Fillmore's disastrous attempt to repeat the formula with Gladys Winch herself.

## Ginger's Mission

Fillmore dispatches Ginger Kemp to Chicago "to have a look at the show" (013-008). Ginger returns with devastating news: Gerald Foster has married Elsa Doland. "He was married the day before I left Chicago" (014-001). The Chicago tryout — the novel's great professional success — thus becomes the site of its central romantic catastrophe.

## Fillmore's Later Schemes

In the novel's closing pages, Fillmore's letter announces plans to expand Popp's Pork-pies to Chicago among other cities, demonstrating that his entrepreneurial restlessness has survived all reversals (022-023).

## Chicago and the Novel's Reversals

Chicago is the pivot on which the novel's fortunes turn. Everything that happens there — the triumph of *The Primrose Way*, Elsa Doland's stardom, Gerald's marriage — generates the reversals that drive the second half of the story. The city functions as a dramatic fulcrum: before Chicago, Sally's world is stable if uncertain; after Chicago, everything she has counted on collapses.

The geography of the Chicago episodes is significant. Chicago is far enough from New York to create real separation — letters and telegrams replace conversation, and Sally must interpret Fillmore's bulletins at a distance. This mediated communication allows Wodehouse to control the pace of revelation: good news arrives instantly, bad news is delayed, and the full extent of Gerald's betrayal reaches Sally only through Ginger's reluctant report.

Chicago also embodies the novel's ambivalence about American success. The city celebrates *The Primrose Way* "as the most terrific experience that had come to stir Chicago since the great fire" (012-011), and Ike Schumann "stamped it with the seal of his approval" (009-019). Yet this triumph is hollow: the man who wrote the play has married someone else, the manager who staged it will soon be bankrupt, and the star it created will never perform it again. Chicago's enthusiasm, like Fillmore's prosperity, proves temporary.
