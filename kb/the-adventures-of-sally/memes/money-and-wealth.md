# Money and Wealth

Money is the prime mover of *The Adventures of Sally*, setting every major plot line in motion from the opening scene. Sally Nicholas's inheritance of twenty-five thousand dollars (001-018) triggers the novel's central dramatic question — what will she do with it? — and each character's answer to that question reveals their moral nature.

## Definition in Context

In Wodehouse's fictional world, money is less a medium of exchange than a moral reagent: applied to any character, it reveals what that character truly is. Wealth in the novel is never earned through sustained labour — Sally inherits it, Fillmore borrows it, Gerald loses it, Ginger refuses it. The novel traces what each character does when money comes within reach, and the pattern is consistent: those who pursue money as an end (Fillmore, Gerald) are undone by it; those who treat it as a means to help others (Sally, Ginger) are liberated by it.

## Key Passages

The novel's treatment of money unfolds across three pivotal passages, each marking a turning point in the plot.

### The Legacy Revealed (Chapter I)

Sally's casual announcement of her inheritance — "I've just twenty-five thousand dollars" (001-018) — sets the entire narrative in motion. The boarding-house party immediately erupts into a chaos of financial advice:

> "Buy a theatre, Sally, and put on good stuff." (001-007)

> "If I had a few hundred thousand," said the mild young man, "I'd put every cent of it on Benny Whistler for the heavyweight championship. I've private information that Battling Tuke has been got at and means to lie down in the seventh…" (001-008)

Sally herself has simpler ambitions:

> "I'm going to start with a trip to Europe… France, specially. I've heard France well spoken of — as soon as I can get my passport; and after I've loafed there for a few weeks, I'm coming back to look about and find some nice cosy little business which will let me put money into it and keep me in luxury." (001-024)

### The Gift (Chapter VII)

Sally's loan of twenty thousand dollars to her brother Fillmore — four-fifths of her entire fortune — is the novel's most significant financial transaction. The effect is immediate and theatrical:

> It amazed Sally that any one mortal should have been able to spread such universal happiness as she had done by the simple act of lending her brother Fillmore twenty thousand dollars. If the Millennium had arrived, the members of the Primrose Way Company could not have been on better terms with themselves. (007-003)

Fillmore's response is characteristically material:

> Fillmore's took the shape of buying a new waistcoat and a hundred half-dollar cigars and being very fussy about what he had for lunch. It may have been an optical illusion, but he appeared to Sally to put on at least six pounds in weight on the first day of the new regime. (007-005)

### The Ruin (Chapter XIV)

The money Sally lent Fillmore vanishes into a cascade of theatrical failures. Ginger, recounting the wreckage, captures the speed of the disaster:

> After that Fillmore put on a play of Gerald Foster's and that was a frost, too… Best thing that could have happened, really, and I was glad at the time, because going on with it would only have meant wasting more money, and it had cost a fortune already. (014-215, 014-218)

And yet Sally's response to financial ruin is revealing: "I'm… I'm not thinking about my money" (014-230). For her, the money was never the point.

## Character Perspectives

Each major character embodies a distinct attitude toward money, and Wodehouse uses these contrasts for both comic effect and moral commentary.

| Character | Attitude Toward Money | Moral Outcome |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Sally Nicholas** | Generous indifference — gives it away freely (lends Fillmore $20,000, helps Elsa with rent) | Liberated — her generosity is rewarded with domestic happiness |
| **Fillmore Nicholas** | Status marker — money signals social arrival (white waistcoat, half-dollar cigars) | Comic humiliation — loses Sally's money, must be rescued by her |
| **Gerald Foster** | Entitlement — believes artistic genius deserves financial reward without discipline | Ruin — drinks away his opportunities, loses both career and Sally |
| **Ginger Kemp** | Principled refusal — "I can't take five thousand dollars off you" (014-122) | Integrity rewarded — eventually accepts Sally's investment in his farm |
| **Uncle Donald** | Instrument of control — wealth as the basis of family authority | Ineffectual — his power proves hollow against the younger generation |

Sally's relationship to money is defined by a single scene early in the novel: "Elsa darling, are you really broke? If you are, you know, I'll…" (001-014). The sentence trails off because the gesture matters more than the amount — Sally's generosity is reflexive, not calculated.

Fillmore, by contrast, uses money to perform social elevation. Returning to the boarding-house, he declares that "all that sort of thing, all that boardinghouse stuff, is a thing of the past. One's got beyond it. One wants to drop it. One wants to forget it, darn it!" (001-061). Money, for Fillmore, is a tool for distancing himself from his origins — and the novel punishes that impulse.

Ginger's attitude is the moral counterweight. Offered five thousand dollars by Sally, he responds with visceral discomfort: "I can't take five thousand dollars off you" (014-122). His refusal is not pride but principle — he cannot accept a woman's money without feeling diminished. The novel validates his position: he ultimately accepts Sally's investment only in the context of their marriage, where the money becomes *theirs* rather than *hers*.

## Narrative Significance

Money serves four structural functions in the novel:

1. **Plot engine**: The inheritance creates the conditions for every subsequent event — the trip to Europe, the loan to Fillmore, the theatrical investment, and ultimately Sally's financial ruin and retreat to Patchogue.

2. **Character revelation**: As argued above, money functions as a moral reagent. Wodehouse tests each character by giving them access to Sally's fortune and observing what they do with it.

3. **Social commentary**: The novel gently satirizes American attitudes toward wealth — Fillmore's status anxiety, the boarding-house residents' speculative fever, the theatrical world's dependence on "angels." The satire is affectionate but consistent.

4. **Comic structure**: Money provides the material for Wodehouse's most characteristic comic technique — the deflation of pretension. Fillmore's hundred half-dollar cigars, Uncle Donald's financial leverage, and the boxing-match betting all serve as setups for comic reversals.
