# The Influenza Outbreak

The Spanish influenza outbreak of 1918–1919, which killed millions worldwide, enters *The Adventures of Sally* as both a comic inconvenience and a serious plot mechanism. It closes theatres, strands the Primrose Way Company in Detroit, and forces Sally to choose between nursing duty and her own desires.

## Mr. Faucitt's Illness

The outbreak arrives at Mrs. Meecher's boarding-house in the person of Mr. Faucitt, who takes to his bed with a headache. Mrs. Meecher, with characteristic buoyancy, makes her diagnosis:

> "There's a lot of this Spanish influenza about. It might be that. Lots o' people have been dying of it, if you believe what you see in the papers." (007-002)

The doctor confirms: "It *is* this here new Spanish influenza. Not a bad attack. You want to stay in bed and keep warm" (007-007). Sally, newly returned from Roville and eager to reach Detroit for Gerald's opening, is asked to nurse the old gentleman.

## Theatre Closures

The more far-reaching consequence is the closure of theatres. Gerald, arriving at the boarding-house to find Sally, delivers the news:

> "Haven't you heard? They've closed all the theatres because of this infernal Spanish influenza. Nothing has been playing this week." (007-018)

Gerald's reaction is characteristically self-centred. He dismisses the epidemic as "all a fake scare" and inveighs against the inconsistency of closing theatres while allowing crowds in stores (007-019). His preoccupation is entirely with the damage to his professional prospects.

## Demoralization in Detroit

The closure strands the Primrose Way Company in Detroit. The "waiting and the uncertainty, the loafing about in strange hotels in a strange city, the dreary rehearsing of lines which had been polished to the last syllable more than a week ago" produces what Wodehouse calls "demoralization" (006-091) — the state from which Sally's loan later rescues them.

## Comic Aftermath

The influenza leaves its comic residue. When Mr. Faucitt recovers and receives news of his brother's death and inheritance, Mrs. Meecher observes with disappointment: "You'd think this here new Spanish influenza was a sort of a tonic or somep'n, the way he looks now" (010-011). The epidemic that threatened a character's life becomes, in Wodehouse's hands, the occasion for a joke about good news restoring health.
