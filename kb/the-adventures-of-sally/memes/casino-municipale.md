# Casino Municipale de Roville

The principal casino of Roville-sur-Mer, situated in the Palace Massena near the railway station. Unlike the more sedate casino on the Promenade, the Casino Municipale is "the haunt of the earnest gambler who means business" (003-005). It is the setting for Ginger Kemp's memorable roulette triumph — and his fateful reunion with Sally Nicholas on the eve of her departure from France.

## Geography

Roville-sur-Mer has two casinos. The one on the Promenade "goes in mostly for sea-air and a mild game called *boule*" (003-005). The Casino Municipale, by contrast, is located "down in the Palace Massena near the railway station" (003-005), positioning it as the serious gambling destination for visitors who mean to win or lose substantial sums.

## The Roulette Scene

Wodehouse devotes one of the novel's most sustained comic set-pieces to the Casino Municipale. Sally, having "conveyed her baggage to the station and left it in charge of an amiable porter," visits the casino at half-past six on her last evening in Roville, in "the faint hope of finding him even now" (003-004). She finds Ginger in spectacular form:

> "Ginger was going extremely strong. He was entrenched behind an opulent-looking mound of square counters: and, even as Sally looked, a wooden-faced croupier shoved a further instalment across the table to him at the end of his long rake." (003-005)

The French spectators are enthralled. "*Epatant!*" murmurs a wistful man at Sally's side, "removing an elbow from her ribs in order the better to gesticulate" (003-006). Wodehouse observes that "there is undoubtedly a certain altruism in the makeup of the spectators at a Continental roulette-table. They seem to derive a spiritual pleasure from seeing somebody else win" (003-006).

The scene's religious parody reaches its peak when Sally's ill-timed laugh breaks the spell:

> "There is something almost of religious ecstasy in the deportment of the spectators at a table where anyone is having a run of luck at roulette, and if she had guffawed in a cathedral she could not have caused a more pained consternation. The earnest worshippers gazed at her with shocked eyes." (003-008)

When the ball lands on the wrong color, ending Ginger's streak, "a shuddering groan ran through the congregation like that which convulses the penitents' bench at a negro revival meeting. More glances of reproach were cast at Sally. It was generally felt that her injudicious behaviour had changed Ginger's luck" (003-008).

## Key Events Here

**Ginger's Winning Streak**: On the evening of Sally's departure from Roville, she finds Ginger at the Casino Municipale, "entrenched behind an opulent-looking mound of square counters" (003-005). The crowd watches with religious intensity as his improbable luck continues.

**Sally Interrupts the Run**: Sally's laugh at the sight of Ginger's intense concentration breaks the spell. The crowd blames her for changing Ginger's luck (003-008). Ginger himself seems unconcerned; he immediately abandons the table:

> "The only person who did not appear to be concerned was Ginger himself. He gathered up his loot, thrust it into his pocket, and elbowed his way to where Sally stood, now definitely established in the eyes of the crowd as a pariah. There was universal regret that he had decided to call it a day. It was to the spectators as though a star had suddenly walked off the stage in the middle of his big scene." (003-009)

**The Aftermath**: Ginger's winnings at the Casino Municipale, though substantial, prove temporary. The gambling episode establishes a pattern in his character — impulsive risk-taking — that recurs throughout the novel, including his boxing-related ventures in America. His willingness to abandon his fortune immediately when Sally appears foreshadows his readiness to sacrifice anything for her.

After leaving the table, Ginger and Sally retreat to "the quiet anteroom outside the gambling-hall" where they sit "in a corner" with "nobody near except the gorgeously uniformed attendant over by the door" (003-012), beginning the conversation that will define their relationship.

## Cultural Context

The Casino Municipale functions in the novel as a site of comic spectacle — gambling transformed into public theatre. Wodehouse's description of the crowd as "congregation" and "earnest worshippers" (003-008) deploys religious parody to deflate the gambler's pretensions to seriousness. The extended metaphor — roulette as revival meeting, Sally as cathedral guffawer, Ginger as a "star" walking off "the stage in the middle of his big scene" (003-009) — layers religious, theatrical, and cinematic imagery to create the kind of absurdist collision that is Wodehouse's signature comic technique.

The casino also marks a turning point in Sally and Ginger's relationship. Their meeting here — Ginger instantly abandoning his fortune at the roulette table to talk to her — foreshadows his later willingness to sacrifice anything for Sally. It is their last meeting in France before both return separately to England and America, and the quiet conversation in the anteroom (003-012) establishes the emotional intimacy that will sustain their relationship through the transatlantic separations to come.
