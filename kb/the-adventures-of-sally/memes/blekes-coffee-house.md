# Bleke's Coffee House

Bleke's Coffee House is a venerable dining establishment located in the Strand, London. Described as a "relic of Old London" (004-035), it represents an older, staid, and deeply unfashionable stratum of the city — a world away from the glittering modernity of [[broadway|Broadway]] or the French Riviera that [[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]] inhabits. For the novel's characters, Bleke's functions as a kind of anti-setting: a place where vitality goes to be extinguished.

## Geography

Bleke's Coffee House is situated in the Strand, the historic London thoroughfare connecting Westminster to the City. Its entrance is characterized by a single "dingy door" (004-049) that gives no hint of hospitality or welcome. The Strand setting places it in the heart of what was, in Wodehouse's era, London's theatre and hotel district — though Bleke's itself stands in ironic opposition to the glamour of its surroundings.

## Description in the Novel

Wodehouse's portrait of Bleke's is unrelievedly grim, constructed through a series of escalating dysphemisms. The narrator calls it a "morgue" that "confined its custom principally to regular patrons who had not missed an evening there for half a century" (004-035). This is not a restaurant that seeks new customers; it is a ritual site for the elderly, the habituated, and the unimaginative.

The exterior offers no relief from the interior's funereal gloom. The taxi driver who delivers [[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]] to "the dingy door of Bleke's Coffee House in the Strand" at twenty-five minutes past seven finds himself "rather struck by his fare's manner and appearance" — Ginger's "determined-looking" expression suggesting a man steeling himself for an ordeal rather than anticipating a meal (004-049).

Ginger's own assessment is the most damning of all. To dine at Bleke's "was to touch something very near bedrock" (004-035) — a phrase that captures both the establishment's material grimness and its symbolic function as the absolute low point of Ginger's fortunes.

## Key Events Here

### Ginger's Dinner with Uncle Donald (Chapter IV)

The most significant scene set at Bleke's is Ginger Kemp's dinner with his uncle, Donald Kemp. Ginger approaches the evening in a state of deep depression, the restaurant's atmosphere amplifying his despair:

> A dinner with Uncle Donald would hardly have been a cheerful function, even in the surroundings of a banquet in the Arabian Nights. (004-035)

The narrator's logic is devastating: Donald Kemp's personality is such that he could "shed a gloom" over any gathering, and when this natural pessimism is combined with the mortuary atmosphere of Bleke's, the result is a uniquely cheerless occasion. Ginger's mood "had not passed when he began to dress for the dismal festivities of Bleke's Coffee House," scowling as he "struggled morosely with an obstinate tie" (004-045).

The narrator adds with characteristic dryness: "Ginger was warming up for a big evening" of discontent (004-045).

### Bruce Carmyle's Retrospective (Chapter XV)

The dinner at Bleke's acquires retrospective significance in the novel's later romantic drama. [[bruce-carmyle|Bruce Carmyle]], reflecting on his rival Ginger, recalls that "Ginger's behaviour at Bleke's Coffee House, on a certain notable occasion, had not been so utterly inexcusable as he had supposed" (015-019).

This brief allusion, occurring well after the dinner itself, suggests that whatever transpired at Bleke's marked a genuine turning point — if not in Ginger's fortunes, then at least in how others understood his actions. Carmyle, the novel's most fastidious character, extending retrospective sympathy to Ginger is itself a minor narrative event, and Bleke's serves as its anchoring reference point.

## Associated Characters

- [[ginger-kemp|Ginger Kemp]] — the diner whose misery defines the Bleke's experience
- [[bruce-carmyle|Bruce Carmyle]] — who retrospectively reconsiders Ginger's conduct there
- Uncle Donald Kemp — the gloomy host whose presence compounds the restaurant's cheerlessness

## Cultural Context

Bleke's Coffee House embodies the old, established, and unglamorous London that contrasts with the novel's other key settings. Where [[new-york-city|Broadway]] offers frenetic modernity, [[roville-sur-mer|Roville-sur-Mer]] offers sunny leisure, and [[mrs-meechers-boardinghouse|Mrs. Meecher's Boarding House]] offers comfortable domesticity, Bleke's offers only tradition without pleasure, permanence without charm.

The restaurant's name itself is suggestive: "Coffee House" harks back to an eighteenth-century London institution, and its survival into the twentieth century is presented not as continuity but as stagnation. Its patrons, unchanged for fifty years, are living monuments to the death of novelty.

For Ginger, Bleke's functions as a spatial metaphor for his circumstances: penniless, dependent on a gloomy relative, trapped in a city that offers him neither warmth nor opportunity. His subsequent journey toward Sally and America is, in part, a flight from the world that Bleke's represents.
