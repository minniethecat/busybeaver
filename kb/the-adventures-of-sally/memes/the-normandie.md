# The Normandie

## Location and Character

The Normandie is a pension-hotel in the French seaside resort of Roville-sur-Mer. It is decidedly on the economical end of the town's accommodation spectrum — one of the "pensions of the economical" where "early to bed is the rule," in stark contrast with the "gilded palaces along the front" that "keep deplorable hours, polluting the night air till dawn with indefatigable jazz." (002-068)

> Bedtime at Roville is an hour that seems to vary according to one's proximity to the sea. The gilded palaces along the front keep deplorable hours, polluting the night air till dawn with indefatigable jazz: but at the pensions of the economical like the Normandie, early to bed is the rule.
>
> (002-068)

## Clientele and Atmosphere

The Normandie "catered principally for the staid and middle-aged." (002-251) Its atmosphere is one of quiet respectability rather than glamour. The presence of a young man in evening dress at one o'clock in the morning is sufficiently unusual to be described as "practically amounted to a miracle." (002-251) This young visitor had, in fact, been obliged to make a hurried shift from the Hotel Splendide to the humbler Normandie after a disastrous evening at the Casino diminished his funds. (002-251)

## Jules — Night-Clerk and Lift Attendant

Jules, a "stout young native," combines the offices of night-clerk and lift attendant at the Normandie. He is on duty in the hall throughout the night, though few of the hotel's patrons make use of his services after hours. (002-068)

## Sally and Ginger at the Normandie

Sally Nicholas stays at the Normandie during her time in Roville, having chosen economy over the extravagance of the Hotel Splendide. When asked where she is staying, she replies simply: "The Normandie." (002-031) Ginger Kemp also stays at the Normandie, and it serves as the setting for their late-night encounter when he returns from a dance at the Splendide. (002-251)

## Class Contrast

The Normandie and the Hotel Splendide function as contrasting symbols in the Roville chapters. The Splendide represents wealth, fashion, and late-night revelry, while the Normandie embodies thrift, respectability, and quiet domestic order. This contrast mirrors Sally's own position — an heiress who has chosen to live economically — and the novel's broader engagement with themes of social class and financial prudence.
