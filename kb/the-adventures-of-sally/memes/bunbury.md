# Mr. Bunbury

Mr. Bunbury is the theatrical producer and director of [[the-primrose-way|The Primrose Way]],
Fillmore Nicholas's ill-fated play. He is first encountered in Chapter VI during a tense rehearsal
at a Broadway theatre, where his volcanic temperament is on full display.

## Appearance and Manner

Bunbury is described as "a man with a bald head fringed with orange hair" (006-080) and "a man of temperament" (006-083).
He is prone to dramatic outbursts — flinging his walking-stick across the theatre (006-083), springing to his
feet, waving his hands, and delivering impassioned bellows at his cast and crew. Sally, observing
one of his rehearsals, wonders "whether things were going particularly wrong today, or whether this
was one of Mr. Bunbury's ordinary mornings" (006-136).

## The Primrose Way Rehearsal

> "This is a rehearsal," snapped Mr. Bunbury. "If we are not going to do it properly, what's the use of doing it at all?"
>
> (006-090)


During the rehearsal that Sally attends, Bunbury's perfectionism is evident. He critiques every
detail of the performance — from a parlourmaid's entrance to the placement of props. When a vital
stage property goes missing, his wrath falls squarely on [[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]],
the assistant stage manager. True to theatrical orthodoxy, Bunbury holds that "if anything goes
wrong it must be the fault of the assistant stage manager."

His exchanges with the cast are marked by both fury and a strange, overwrought artistry. He invokes
the legendary actress Nazimova as a benchmark (006-163), and paces the aisles in earnest conference with
[[gerald-foster|Gerald Foster]], the play's author.

## Role in the Narrative

Bunbury's temperament embodies the chaotic, high-strung world of Broadway theatre into which
Fillmore has thrown his (and Sally's) money. His rehearsals are comedic set-pieces that expose
Fillmore's inadequacy as a theatrical manager and the fundamental unseriousness of the enterprise.
Though he appears primarily in Chapter VI, his name becomes a cultural touchstone — in Chapter VIII,
Ginger Kemp's thoughts are described with the parenthetical "(as Mr. Bunbury would have said)" (008-038)
