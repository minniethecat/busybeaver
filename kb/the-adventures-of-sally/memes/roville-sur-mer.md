# Roville-sur-Mer

Roville-sur-Mer is a fictional French seaside resort town where Sally Nicholas spends a month-long vacation following her inheritance. It serves as the setting for the novel's opening chapters, where several key relationships are established: Sally's first meeting with Ginger Kemp, her encounter with Scrymgeour, and the famous dog-fight scene that reveals Ginger's unexpected competence.

## Location & Character

Roville is described as a quintessential French resort town with "its blue sky, its Casino, its snow-white hotels along the Promenade, and its general glitter and gaiety" [002-P01]. The beach, or *plage*, is the center of morning social life, where "whiskered fathers of families made cheerful patches of colour" and "their female friends and relatives clustered in groups under gay parasols" [002-P01].

Like most French seashore resorts, "the morning is the time when the visiting population assembles in force on the beach" [002-P01]. The town is populated largely by French visitors — English speakers are "a novelty in Roville" — which leads to comedic linguistic difficulties for the English and American characters [002-P01].

## Notable Locations

### The Promenade and Casino

The Casino is a central feature of Roville's nightlife. Sally visits "the Casino to hear the band and afterwards had strolled on the moonlit promenade" [002-P02]. Along the beachfront, "gilded palaces... keep deplorable hours, polluting the night air till dawn with indefatigable jazz" [002-P02].

### Hotel Normandie

The Normandie is one of the "pensions of the economical" where Sally stays. Unlike the flashy beachfront hotels, it observes strict hours — "early to bed is the rule." Jules, "the stout young native who combined the offices of night-clerk and lift attendant," is a memorable minor character who works at the Normandie, perpetually half-asleep yet somehow managing his duties [002-P02].

## Story Events

### Sally's Vacation

Sally has spent "a magic month of lazy happiness... drifting luxuriously from one French town to another, till the charm of Roville" brought her to a halt. The vacation proves "an exception to the rule" that anticipation exceeds experience [002-P01]. She leaves only when called back to America by news of *The Primrose Way*'s Detroit opening.

### First Meeting with Ginger and Scrymgeour

On the beach, Sally overhears two English-speaking men — a "dark man" and a "red-haired young man" — the latter of whom sends her a box of sweets via a page boy. The dark man is revealed to be a representative of Scrymgeour, the red-haired man's cousin's lawyer, sent to pay off the impecunious young man. This chance encounter introduces Ginger Kemp to Sally [002-P01].

### The Dog Fight

The beach encounter is interrupted by "the prettiest dog fight that Roville had seen that season." In the ensuing chaos, Ginger Kemp — despite his awkwardness in social situations — proves to be the one "Master Mind" capable of stopping the melee. His intervention, calming the dogs "in a shorter time than one would have believed possible," offers Sally her first glimpse of his hidden competence [002-P04]–[002-P05].

### The Lift Incident

At the Hotel Normandie, Sally and Ginger find themselves trapped in a lift when the half-asleep Jules forgets the keys. Their shared confinement in the small, slow-moving lift — punctuated by Jules's emotional breakdown and their joint escape effort — develops their relationship through farce, a classic Wodehouse set piece [002-P02]–[002-P03].

## Significance

Roville-sur-Mer represents the world of leisure and possibility that Sally briefly inhabits before the complications of New York and the theatrical business close in. It is a liminal space — between her old life of modest means and her new life of wealth, between England and America — where the novel's central romance is seeded. The town's sun-drenched, comic atmosphere establishes the tone for the lighter half of the narrative.
