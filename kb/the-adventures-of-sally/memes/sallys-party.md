# Sally's Party

Sally's party opens the novel in high comic style, gathering the principal characters around a long table at [[mrs-meechers-boardinghouse|mrs-meechers-boardinghouse]] and establishing the social world that will sustain the entire story.
It is the occasion for Mr. Faucitt's florid toast, Fillmore's devastatingly short reply, and Sally's first display of the impulsive warmth that defines her character.
As a set-piece, it does what the best Wodehouse openings do: introduces everyone, sets the tone, and leaves chaos in its wake.

## The Scene

The party takes place at the [[mrs-meechers-boardinghouse|mrs-meechers-boardinghouse]], around a long table.
At one end sits Sally, "looking contentedly down the long table," happy that "her party, rallying after an uncertain start, was plainly the [[success]] she had hoped it would be" (001-003).
At the other end sits [[Fillmore Nicholas|fillmore-nicholas]], "hedged about with a sort of feudal dignity" (001-035).

Between them are arrayed the novel's key figures:
[[Elsa Doland|elsa-doland]], "the pretty girl with the big eyes" (001-006);
Augustus Bartlett, the Wall Street clerk;
the Marvellous Murphys, vaudeville performers;
and Mr. Faucitt, the veteran actor who will propose the toast.

The conversation turns on the "great vital topic of Sally's legacy and what she ought to do with it" (001-004).
The guests, most of whom have never seen twenty-five thousand dollars, speculate extravagantly about investments, revolutions in Paraguay, and the purchase of theatres.
The gap between their fantasies and Sally's actual situation — she has precisely twenty-five thousand, not the imagined four hundred thousand — is the first of many lessons the novel offers about money and its perception.

## The Toast

Mr. Faucitt's speech is a masterpiece of old-school theatrical oratory, delivered with the elaborate courtesy of a vanished age.
He rises "slowly, with that easy grace which actors of the old school learned in the days when acting was acting" (001-028), bows to the ladies, and quells "certain male members of the boardinghouse's younger set" with a single glance (001-029).

His speech is everything Sally could have hoped: gallant, sentimental, and full of the rolling periods that "were meat and drink" to him (001-047).
He calls Sally by her first name — "Sally" rather than "Miss Nicholas" — and the company cheers.

Then Fillmore rises to reply.

## Fillmore's Speech

Fillmore's reply is one of the great comic anticlimaxes in Wodehouse.
Sally has been counting on him: "Fillmore was a fluent young man, once a power in his college debating society" (001-043).
She had "mistrusted her ability to make a fitting reply" and relied on her brother to do justice to Mr. Faucitt's eloquence.

What she gets is:

> "I'm sure you don't want a speech... Very good of you to drink our health. Thank you." (001-049)

He sits down.

The effect on the company is mixed. Most feel "unmixed relief" at avoiding a long speech (001-051).
Mr. Faucitt, however, "was wearing such an expression of surprise and dismay as he might have worn had somebody unexpectedly pulled the chair from under him" (001-052).
He had been "all courteous attention," ready for rhetorical combat, and Fillmore had simply declined to fight.

## Sally's Response

Sally's reaction to her brother's failure is the scene's emotional climax.
She rises, directs "a malevolent look at the immaculate Fillmore" (001-053), and stammers through an improvised reply:

> "My brother has not said quite enough..." (001-053)

Then she kisses Mr. Faucitt and bursts into tears (001-054).

The kiss is a character-defining moment. It is impulsive, warm, and utterly without self-consciousness.
Mr. Faucitt, "the kindest critic," feels "no sense of anticlimax" (001-055).
Sally has, without saying much of anything, said exactly the right thing.

## The Aftermath

The party ends with Sally pursuing Fillmore into the street "to tell him what a pig he was" (001-056).
This street-corner confrontation — Sally lecturing, Fillmore "expanding like an indiarubber ball which has been sat on" (001-057) — flows directly into the entrance of [[Gerald Foster|gerald-foster]], who missed the party but arrives to share the news that his play will be produced.

The party, the speech, the fight, and the romantic revelation form a single continuous movement, establishing the novel's method: comedy and feeling intertwined, public performance giving way to private truth.

## Significance

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| The legacy discussion | Introduces the novel's theme of [[money-and-wealth]] |
| Mr. Faucitt's speech | Establishes the theatrical world and old-school style |
| Fillmore's failure | Defines his character: pompous, self-absorbed, inadequate |
| Sally's kiss | Defines her character: impulsive, warm, emotionally honest |
| Gerald's entrance | Launches the romantic plot and the [[theatre-business]] thread |

Sally's party is the novel in miniature.
It contains money and its discontents, theatrical ambition and its absurdities, family loyalty and its limits, and — in Sally's kiss and tears — the emotional directness that makes her one of Wodehouse's most appealing heroines.
No other scene in the novel brings so many characters together in a single room, and none so efficiently establishes the comic territory the story will explore.
