# Paris

Paris occupies a curious position in *The Adventures of Sally*'s geography: constantly invoked, never visited. The French capital functions as a symbol of missed connections and alternate lives — the city characters intend to stay in, pass through, or imagine as a holiday destination, but which remains stubbornly offstage.

## Geography

Paris lies on the route between Roville-sur-Mer (on the French Mediterranean coast) and Cherbourg, the Channel port from which Sally books her return passage to New York. It is also the implied European counterpoint to London — the "other" European capital, associated with leisure rather than obligation.

## Description in the Novel

Paris is described only through implication. When Bruce Carmyle first appears in the novel, he is surprised to find Ginger in Roville: "I was surprised at his being here. He told me he meant to stay in Paris" (002-018). This passing reference establishes Paris as the expected destination — the city where a young Englishman of means would naturally linger.

When Ginger's holiday plans are discussed during the hotel dinner, someone asks: "Paris, I believe?" (002-042) — confirming the city's status as default European holiday destination. Later, Mr. Faucitt asks the same question: "Where did you spend your holiday? Paris?" (005-035), revealing the deep-seated assumption that any European trip must include the French capital.

## Key Events Here

No scenes in *The Adventures of Sally* actually take place in Paris. The city exists solely in references:

**Ginger's Fictitious Paris (002-018)**. Ginger tells Bruce Carmyle he "meant to stay in Paris," a plan he abandoned in favour of Roville-sur-Mer. Whether this was a genuine intention or a convenient excuse is never clarified.


> "“I was surprised at his being here. He told me he meant to stay in Paris.”" (002-018)

**Sally's Transit (003-003)**. Returning from Roville to New York, Sally takes "the seven-fifteen express which was to take her to Paris, en route for Cherbourg and the liner whereon she had booked her passage for New York." Paris is merely a stop on the railway timetable, not a destination.

**The Paris Express (008-046)**. In a telling detail, Sally and Bruce Carmyle "parted on the Paris express" — their relationship ending on a train bound for a city neither character will explore. When Sally later fails to think of Bruce, Wodehouse notes: "Mr. Carmyle had been very little in Sally's — so little, indeed, that she had had to search her memory for a moment before she identified him" (008-046).

## Associated Characters

- **Ginger Kemp**: Claims Paris was his intended destination before he diverted to Roville (002-018).
- **Bruce Carmyle**: Parts from Sally on the Paris express (008-046).
- **Sally Nicholas**: Passes through Paris by rail, never stopping (003-003).
- **Mr. Faucitt**: Assumes Ginger's holiday included Paris (005-035).

## Cultural Context

Paris in Wodehouse's novel represents the road not taken — the conventional European experience that characters like Ginger are expected to pursue but which the novel's transatlantic energy consistently bypasses. While New York and London provide the novel's dramatic poles, and Roville-sur-Mer offers romantic idyll, Paris remains a blank on the map — a city whose only function is to be the place characters are *not* when the real action occurs elsewhere. Its absence is itself a comic statement about the novel's anti-romantic geography.
