# Detroit

Detroit is the first American city to host *The Primrose Way*, and the setting for some of the novel's most vivid theatrical comedy. The city serves a dual dramatic function: first as a place of enforced idleness and demoralization during the influenza closure, then as the site of the play's triumphant opening.

## The Stranded Company

Gerald Foster's letter informs Sally that *The Primrose Way* "was to be produced in Detroit, preliminary to its New York run" (002-008). Sally cuts short her Roville vacation to attend the opening, only to be prevented by the influenza outbreak that confined Mr. Faucitt to his bed and Sally to nursing duties (007-007). The theatres close, and the company — already rehearsed to exhaustion — finds itself stranded in a strange city with nothing to do, the demoralization described in Chapter VI.

## The Opening

When the theatres finally reopen, Detroit proves its worth as a tryout city:

> "Detroit, that city of amiable audiences, had liked *The Primrose Way*. The theatre, in fulfilment of Teddy's prophecy, had been allowed to open on the Tuesday, and a full house, hungry for entertainment after its enforced abstinence, had welcomed the play wholeheartedly. The papers... hailed Gerald as the coming author and Elsa Doland as the coming star." (009-018)

A brief mention of Fillmore Nicholas as "the coming manager" completes the trifecta of Detroit's endorsement, though Fillmore's pleasure is slightly marred by the critic who spells Gladys Winch's name "Wunch" in the cast list.

## Ike Schumann's Approval

While in Detroit supervising one of his own musical productions, the powerful theatre owner Ike Schumann attends a performance of *The Primrose Way* and "stamped it with the seal of his approval" (009-019). This validation from one of Broadway's most influential figures confirms the play's prospects.

## Aftermath

News of the Detroit [[success]] reaches New York through *Variety* magazine. Mrs. Meecher, who values *Variety* "next to the dog Toto," celebrates by tying a blue ribbon round Toto's neck and providing a chicken dinner in his interior (010-008).

## Detroit in the Novel's Structure

Detroit serves a dual dramatic function — first as a place of enforced idleness and demoralization during the influenza closure, then as the site of *The Primrose Way*'s triumphant opening. This structural double role is characteristic of Wodehouse's plotting: the same city that traps the company in miserable inactivity becomes, a week later, the source of their greatest validation.

The influenza episode in Detroit also establishes an important motif of chance and interruption. Sally, having cut short her Roville vacation to attend the opening, finds herself prevented from reaching the theatre at all. The outbreak that confined Mr. Faucitt to his bed and Sally to nursing duties (007-007) is an external event — no character causes it, no character can control it. Yet it reshapes the entire trajectory of the novel: had Sally been present at the Detroit opening, she might have witnessed Gerald's growing intimacy with Elsa Doland. Instead, she learns of his marriage second-hand, weeks later, through Ginger Kemp's reluctant report.

The Detroit triumph also validates Fillmore Nicholas's judgement in a way that makes his subsequent fall more dramatic. When Ike Schumann, "the powerful theatre owner" supervising one of his own musicals in Detroit, attends and "stamped it with the seal of his approval" (009-019), Fillmore's theatrical instincts appear vindicated. The fact that he will later lose everything — the play, the profits, and the company — makes Detroit's endorsement, in retrospect, a cruel irony.
