# Lester Burrowes

**Lester Burrowes** is the manager of the boxer [[bugs-butler|Bugs Butler]] and a former featherweight champion in his own right. A great talker with an evangelical faith in his fighter's excellence, Burrowes serves as a comic foil whose promotional rhetoric is decisively punctured by [[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]].

## Role in the Story

Burrowes appears during the Chapter XIII training-camp sequence, where his primary function is to promote Bugs Butler's excellence to anyone within earshot — particularly to potential investors. He has assembled "plutocrats to the number of about forty" at the camp, though he regrets that not all of them "had parted with solid coin" (013-061).

His relentless salesmanship is established immediately: he buttonholes [[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]] for a quarter of an hour before being called away on other business, leaving Fillmore "free to slide away to the hotel and get a bite to eat, which he sorely needed" (013-062). The implication is clear: Burrowes's conversation, however enthusiastic, is exhausting.

When Sally enters the scene, Burrowes initially fails to recognize her as a threat. Accustomed to treating women as decorative bystanders, he is wholly unprepared for someone who actually knows about boxing and can cite fight records from memory.

## Notable Scenes

### The Promotional Monologue

Burrowes's method of promotion is simple: absolute, unqualified assertion. He declares Butler "the cleverest lightweight since Joe Gans" and proceeds with growing fervour to enumerate his fighter's virtues (013-101). His technique involves physical as well as verbal persuasion — he "clutched at his coat-button with a swoop, and held him with a glittering eye" (013-098).

### The Cross-Examination

When Sally asks whether Butler has ever fought anyone of genuine standing — naming Lew Lucas as a benchmark — "the effect of this simple question on Mr. Burrowes was stupendous" (013-103). The narrator notes that:

> Females who took an interest in boxing had come into Mr. Burrowes' life before — in his younger days, when he was a famous featherweight, the first of his three wives had been accustomed to sit at the ringside during his contests and urge him in language of the severest technicality to knock opponents' blocks off — but somehow he had not supposed from her appearance and manner that Sally was one of the elect. (013-103)

Burrowes's world-view is momentarily destabilized. He rallies briefly — producing a signed picture-postcard of Butler as a peace offering (013-108, 013-111) — but Sally's relentless statistical logic, culminating in the [[cyclone-mullins|Cyclone Mullins]] comparison, leaves his promotional enterprise in ruins.

## Personality and Traits

Burrowes is defined by volubility and promotional zeal. He talks constantly, buttonholes acquaintances, and radiates an almost religious faith in his fighter's superiority. His energy is formidable but ultimately blunted by Sally's superior command of facts.

The detail that Burrowes has had three wives hints at a complicated personal history, though this serves principally as setup for the comic revelation that one of those wives was a ringside boxing enthusiast — a detail that makes Sally's knowledge of the sport slightly less unprecedented from Burrowes's perspective.

## Significance

Burrowes embodies the theme of inflated reputation. Like his fighter, he is a figure whose promotional apparatus far exceeds his substance. Sally's dismantling of his rhetoric is one of the novel's most satisfying comic set-pieces, demonstrating her intelligence and force of personality while exposing the hollowness of the boxing enterprise that threatens to consume her brother's fortune.

The fact that Burrowes was once a famous featherweight himself adds a note of pathos: he is a man whose own glory days are behind him, now reduced to talking up a mediocre fighter in hopes of one last payday.
