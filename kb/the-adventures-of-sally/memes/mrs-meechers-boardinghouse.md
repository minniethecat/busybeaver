# Mrs. Meecher's Boardinghouse

Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse, on an unnamed street on New York's West Side, is the novel's most important domestic space.
It is here that [[Sally Nicholas|sally-nicholas]] lives, here that she throws her fateful party, and here that the intersecting lives of the novel's central characters converge.
Run by the ex-actress Mrs. Meecher — a veteran of the first production of *Florodora* — the boardinghouse is part home, part theatrical green room, and part social experiment.

## Role in the Story

The boardinghouse functions as the novel's social crossroads.
Sally, [[Elsa Doland|elsa-doland]], [[Gerald Foster|gerald-foster]], [[Ginger Kemp|ginger-kemp]], and Mr. Faucitt all live under its roof at various points, and even [[Fillmore Nicholas|fillmore-nicholas]] — now a man of business in the West Eighties — is a former resident, "once a prune-eater among the proletariat" (001-085).

It is at the boardinghouse that Sally gives her party in Chapter I, that Gerald woos her in secret, and that the news of the legacy's investment and loss reverberates.
When characters leave and return, the boardinghouse is the fixed point they come back to.

## The Physical Space

The boardinghouse is a place of modest comfort and democratic proximity.
Wodehouse records that "very few of the rooms at Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse struck any note of luxury" (011-032).
Mrs. Meecher's approach to interior decoration is functional rather than fashionable: "She considered that when she had added a Morris chair to a room, she had done all that was necessary" (011-032).

This physical modesty is thematically significant.
The boardinghouse represents a world where social distinctions are collapsed by shared walls and shared meals.
A theatrical knight like Mr. Faucitt, a Wall Street clerk like Augustus Bartlett, an aspiring playwright, an actress, and an heiress all sit at the same table.

## Mrs. Meecher

Mrs. Meecher, the landlady, is one of Wodehouse's finest minor creations.
An ex-member of the theatrical profession — "having been in the first production of *Florodora*" (005-009) — she combines a practical interest in her boarders' rent with a ghoulish fascination with their misfortunes.

She "kept abreast of theatrical gossip" (005-009) and "enjoyed the tragedies of life" (005-011).
When Mr. Faucitt falls ill, she hovers with "an appraising eye at the invalid, as if to detect symptoms of approaching discoloration" (005-061), having heard that certain diseases cause the victim to turn black — a possibility she contemplates with "regret" when it fails to materialize (005-014).

Her combination of competence and comic morbidity makes her an ideal presiding spirit for a household of theatrical personalities.
She is simultaneously the guardian of domestic order and a one-woman audience, treating her boarders' lives as a continuing drama to be observed and annotated.
> "Mrs. Meecher was friendly and garrulous. *Variety*, the journal which, next to the dog Toto, was the thing she loved best in the world, had informed her on the Friday morning that Mr. Foster's play had got over big in Detroit, and that Miss Doland had made every kind of hit. It was not often that the old alumni of the boardinghouse forced their way after this fashion into the Hall of Fame, and, according to Mrs. Meecher, the establishment was ringing with the news. That blue ribbon round Toto's neck was worn in honour of the triumph. There was also, though you could not see it, a chicken dinner in Toto's interior, by way of further celebration."
>
> (008-098)

### The Party (Chapter I)

The novel opens with a dinner party at the boardinghouse that establishes the entire social world of the story.
Mr. Faucitt, with his old-school theatrical flourishes, proposes Sally's health in a speech of elaborate courtesy (001-029–001-042).
Fillmore's reply — "I'm sure you don't want a speech... Very good of you to drink our health. Thank you" (001-049) — deflates the occasion with comic brevity.

The party ends with Sally kissing Mr. Faucitt and bursting into tears (001-054), then pursuing Fillmore into the street to tell him "what a pig he was" (001-056).
In a few pages, the boardinghouse party establishes the novel's central tensions: family loyalty, theatrical ambition, social pretension, and the difficulty of giving a good speech.

### Daily Life

The boardinghouse represents a particular kind of urban domesticity — communal, improvised, and inseparable from the professional lives of its residents.
Lines between rehearsal and home life blur: Gerald discusses theatrical business at the breakfast table, Sally receives letters from absent friends and lovers, and the general noise of "the boardinghouse's younger set" (001-029) provides a constant background hum.

It is a world that Fillmore, in his new prosperity, dismisses as "a thing of the past" (001-061).
But the novel consistently values the boardinghouse over Fillmore's upward mobility, suggesting that community — however noisy, however inconvenient — is worth more than the alternative.

## Significance

Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse embodies the novel's democratic spirit.
Within its walls, social distinctions dissolve: the heiress, the actress, the playwright, the vaudeville performer, and the Wall Street clerk sit down to the same dinner, subject to the same Mrs. Meecher and her Friday suet pudding.

Structurally, the boardinghouse provides a theatrical stage in miniature — an enclosed space where characters enter and exit, where revelations occur in hallways and on doorsteps, and where the audience (Mrs. Meecher, always watching) observes the drama with professional interest.

In a novel about the instability of money and the uncertainty of love, the boardinghouse offers a different kind of security: not wealth, not romance, but the steady, unglamorous continuity of a place where people know your name and notice when you're gone.
