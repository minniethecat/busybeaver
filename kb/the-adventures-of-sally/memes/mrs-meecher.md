# Mrs. Meecher

Mrs. Meecher is the proprietress of the "select boardinghouse (transient and residential)" (001-012) on Broadway that serves as the New York home for [[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]], [[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]], [[maxwell-faucitt|mr-faucitt]], and an ever-changing cast of theatrical and business types. A former performer in the first production of *Florodora* — "though, unlike everybody else, not one of the original Sextette" (008-098) — she brings to her landlady duties a theatrical sensibility, a taste for gossip, and a morbid enthusiasm for bad news.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse is the novel's primary New York setting, and she herself functions as a kind of Greek chorus — delivering news, updating characters on events, and providing comic commentary on the boardinghouse's collective fortunes. She is present at every major transition: Sally's departure for Europe, her return, Mr. Faucitt's illness, Gerald Foster's theatrical [[success]], and Fillmore's business developments.

Her role is primarily that of a comic landlady — part maternal, part mercenary, always informed. She keeps abreast of theatrical gossip through *Variety*, "the journal which, next to the dog Toto, was the thing she loved best in the world" (014-127), and she dispenses news with a characteristic blend of relish and gloom.

> Variety, the journal which, next to the dog Toto, was the thing she loved best in the world, had informed her on the Friday morning that Mr. Foster\'s play had got over big in Detroit, and that Miss Doland had made every kind of hit. It was not often that the old alumni of the boardinghouse forced their way after this fashion into the Hall of Fame.
>
> (008-098)

## Personality and Traits

Mrs. Meecher's most distinctive trait is her morbid enthusiasm. When reporting that Mr. Faucitt is ill, she cannot resist speculating about the worst: "There's a lot of this Spanish influenza about. It might be that. Lots o' people have been dying of it, if you believe what you see in the papers" (008-104). She follows this with visible disappointment when informed the patient "ain't turned black," adding hopefully that "that may come later" (008-106).

She is also an inveterate gossip. When Sally returns from Europe, Mrs. Meecher immediately delivers a full account of everyone's doings: "Mr. Foster's play had got over big in Detroit... Miss Doland had made every kind of hit" (014-127). She enjoys the "tragedies of life" and delivers bad news with barely concealed satisfaction.

Her love for her dog Toto borders on delusional. She believes the small, woolly, perpetually yapping animal is a combination of "sick-nurse, soothing medicine, and a week at the seaside" (008-119). She insists on installing Toto in the sick Mr. Faucitt's room, oblivious to the fact that "each yap went through me like hammer-strokes on sheeted tin" (008-119). When good news arrives, she celebrates by tying a blue ribbon around Toto's neck and feeding him a chicken dinner (014-127).

## Key Relationships

- **[[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]]**: Her favorite boarder. Mrs. Meecher regards Sally with a mixture of affection and the landlady's instinct to assert dominance — Sally is the rare boarder who won their obligatory first-week battle, establishing a respectful détente (001-030).
- **[[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]]**: A former boarder whose theatrical success elevates the boardinghouse's prestige. Mrs. Meecher follows his career through *Variety* and takes reflected pride in his accomplishments.
- **[[maxwell-faucitt|mr-faucitt]]**: A long-term boarder whom Mrs. Meecher treats with proprietary concern. Her insistence on inflicting Toto upon him during his illness is a running comic motif.
- **Toto**: Her beloved dog, a "small woolly animal with a persistent and penetrating yap" (008-110). Mrs. Meecher's unwavering faith in Toto's therapeutic powers, despite all evidence to the contrary, is one of the novel's minor running jokes.

## Notable Scenes

**Sally's Party (Chapter I)**. The novel opens at Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse during Sally's party. Though Mrs. Meecher herself does not appear prominently, the boardinghouse atmosphere — the "uncomfortable restraint" caused by Fillmore's evening waistcoat, the eclectic mix of guests — establishes the setting she presides over (001-012).

**Mr. Faucitt's Illness (Chapter VIII)**. When the elderly actor falls ill, Mrs. Meecher's morbid prognostication and misguided insistence on Toto's curative powers create a comic counterpoint to Sally's genuine concern. Her cheerful speculation about Spanish influenza deaths and her disappointment that Faucitt hasn't turned black perfectly capture her character (008-103).

**Celebrating Theatrical Success (Chapter XIV)**. When *Variety* reports the Detroit success of *The Primrose Way*, Mrs. Meecher celebrates with characteristic excess: tying a blue ribbon around Toto and feeding him a celebratory chicken dinner. Her vicarious pride in the boardinghouse alumni who have "forced their way into the Hall of Fame" (014-127) is both comic and oddly touching.

## Significance

Mrs. Meecher serves as the anchoring presence of the novel's New York world. While the major characters travel — to Roville, to Detroit, to England — the boardinghouse remains constant, and Mrs. Meecher's news updates provide the connective tissue between episodes. She embodies the comic type of the garrulous landlady, and her morbid enthusiasm and canine delusions are pure Wodehouse: absurd, consistent, and endearing.
