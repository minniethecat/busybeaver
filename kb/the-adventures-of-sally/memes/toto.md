# Toto

Toto is Mrs. Meecher's woolly pet dog, a recurring comic presence in the boarding-house at Washington Square. Described as "a small woolly animal with a persistent and penetrating yap" who "stained the fame of the entire canine race by posing as a dog" (005-015), Toto is the bane of every resident except his devoted owner.

## Role in the Story

Toto functions as a running gag and minor antagonist, particularly to Ginger Kemp, who is forced into the humiliating position of walking the dog in public. Mrs. Meecher treasures Toto second only to her beloved *Variety* magazine (008-098), and the dog's presence at the boarding-house is as constant as the Friday suet pudding.

## Personality & Traits

Toto is characterised by three things: his "persistent and penetrating yap" (005-015), his suspicious nature, and his insatiable appetite for crackers. When Sally lures him out of Mr. Faucitt's sickroom with a cracker, Toto stands "with his head on one side, peering questioningly at the cracker. He was a suspicious dog" before being "lured into the passage" (005-021). His yapping is described as "strenuous" (008-111) and his physical appearance so undog-like that Ginger describes him as "a thing like a woolly rat" (009-055).


> "Sally’s concern increased. Like everyone who had ever spent any length of time in the house, she had strong views on Toto. This quadruped, who stained the fame of the entire canine race by posing as a dog, was a small woolly animal with a persistent and penetrating yap, hard to bear with equanimity in health and certainly quite outside the range of a sick man." (005-015)

## Key Relationships

- **Mrs. Meecher**: Toto's devoted owner. After seven years of close association, she retains an unshakeable faith in "her little pet's amiability and power to soothe" (005-015). She marks Sally's theatrical [[success]] by tying a blue ribbon round Toto's neck and feeding him a celebratory chicken dinner (008-098).

- **Ginger Kemp**: Toto's principal victim. Ginger, "for all his love of dogs, had never been able to bring himself to regard Toto with affection" (018-008). Mrs. Meecher forces him to walk Toto in public, a humiliation Ginger describes with a strong sense of injury: "no dog's got a right to be a dog like that" (009-055).

- **Maxwell Faucitt**: An unwilling patient. When Mr. Faucitt falls ill, Mrs. Meecher installs Toto as bedside companion: "He's quite happy. Toto's sitting with him" (005-014). The old actor's suffering is only relieved when Sally lures Toto out with a cracker (005-021).

- **Sally Nicholas**: Sally, "like everyone who had ever spent any length of time in the house," has "strong views on Toto" (005-015). She nevertheless shows practical kindness, arranging for Toto's country convalescence when the dog falls into a "dreadful lethargy" (018-008).

## Notable Scenes

**The Sickroom Rescue** (005-014–005-021). When Mr. Faucitt is ill, Sally arrives to find Toto installed as bedside companion. "You can hear Toto talking to him now," says Mrs. Meecher complacently (005-017). Sally lures Toto out with a cracker and shuts the door, allowing the old actor to draw his first deep breath.

**The Blue Ribbon Celebration** (008-098). When *Variety* reports Sally's success in Detroit, Mrs. Meecher celebrates by tying a blue ribbon round Toto's neck and feeding him a chicken dinner. The establishment "was ringing with the news."

**The Public Walk of Shame** (009-054–009-055). Ginger Kemp recounts his humiliation at being forced to walk Toto in the public streets. "One hates to be seen about with it," he protests.

**Phantom Rats** (009-100). Sally and Ginger come out into the passage "almost on top of Toto, who was stalking phantom rats," a small moment of physical comedy in a tense scene.

**Country Convalescence** (018-008–018-009). When Toto falls into a lethargy, Sally persuades Ginger to offer country air. After a month, Sally declares: "It's wonderful what you've done for Toto, angel. He's a different dog" (018-009). Ginger later advises a dieting plan: "you'll have to go in for a little judicious dieting, like Toto" (018-024).

## Significance

Toto serves a classic Wodehouse function: the comic animal whose very existence generates friction among the human characters. The dog provides running physical comedy — from his stalking of "phantom rats" (009-100) to his "curious leaps of welcome" that Ginger "frigidly" eludes (018-009). More importantly, Toto's presence reveals character: Mrs. Meecher's blind sentimentalism, Ginger's long-suffering good nature, and Sally's practical kindness. When Ginger finally shares a cottage with Toto for a month, the dog even becomes a small bonding agent between him and Sally.
