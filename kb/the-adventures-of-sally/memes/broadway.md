# Broadway

Broadway is the gravitational centre of the novel's theatrical ambitions. For Gerald Foster, Fillmore Nicholas, and the entire Primrose Way Company, Broadway represents the ultimate validation — the only stage that matters.

## Broadway as Metaphor

"Broadway" in Wodehouse's novel is less a street than a metonym for the American theatre industry. When Gerald Foster says "It's a Broadway production. That's all that matters" (003-012), he is invoking the prestige that comes with a Broadway staging, independent of casting quality or artistic merit. His dismissal of casting concerns — "I've been trying long enough to get a play on Broadway" — reveals the desperation beneath the ambition.

> "Worth it? Of course it's worth it. It's a Broadway production. That's all that matters. Good heavens! I've been trying long enough to get a play on Broadway, and it isn't likely that I'm going to chuck away my chance when it comes along just because one might do better in the way of casting."
>
> (001-124)

## The Flower Garden

Sally's workplace, The Flower Garden, is a Broadway dancing-resort — part of the entertainment ecosystem that surrounds the legitimate theatre. Sally describes it as "a place in Broadway called 'The Flower Garden'" where she works as "what is humorously called an 'instructress'" (004-011). Isadore Abrahams, the proprietor, has done his best to create "something as closely resembling a flower-garden as it was possible for an overcrowded, overheated, overnoisy Broadway dancing-resort to achieve" (021-009).

## Fillmore on Broadway

Fillmore Nicholas's transformation from assistant stage-manager to theatrical proprietor is measured by his Broadway presence. Returning from Detroit, he comes "to secure offices and generally make his presence felt along Broadway" (009-017). Later, visiting London, he "talked Broadway without a pause" at the Savoy Hotel, making Sally intensely homesick (017-013). The Savoy itself is described as "simply an annex to Broadway."

## The Theatre District

The Broadway of the novel is also a place of chance encounters. Mr. Faucitt runs into Fillmore "on Broadway only a few days ago" and notes the change in his manner (007-011). The street is the artery along which the characters' professional and social lives flow — where reputations are made, deals are struck, and fortunes shift.

## Broadway and Sally's Journey

Broadway frames Sally Nicholas's trajectory through the novel in three distinct phases. Before her inheritance, she is an employee in its dancing-resort ecosystem — working at The Flower Garden, a "Broadway dancing-resort" where she is "what is humorously called an 'instructress'" (004-011). She is of Broadway but not yet in command of it, dependent on the whims of Isadore Abrahams and the rhythm of the dinner hour.

After the legacy, Sally's relationship to Broadway becomes that of a spectator waiting for news. Gerald Foster and Fillmore Nicholas are both in Detroit, and Broadway — the destination of *The Primrose Way* — is the future that never arrives. Sally waits in her boarding-house, reading *Variety*, marking time. The distance between "waiting on Broadway" and "being on Broadway" becomes a measure of her emotional displacement.

In the novel's closing movement, Sally reclaims Broadway on her own terms. She no longer works there, no longer waits for news from there, and no longer invests her happiness in its verdicts. Fillmore's return "to secure offices and generally make his presence felt along Broadway" (009-017) no longer impresses her, and Gerald's Broadway career is now someone else's concern. Broadway — once the sun around which her world orbited — becomes simply a street.
