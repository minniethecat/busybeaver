# Success

A central thematic strand in *The Adventures of Sally*, Wodehouse contrasts male ambition with female emotional intelligence, showing how characters define and pursue "success" in fundamentally different ways.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Male Ambition

The male characters uniformly measure success by professional achievement and financial gain. Fillmore Nicholas abandons his boarding-house past for Wall Street pretensions, viewing business success as social elevation (001-046). Gerald Foster dreams of being a successful playwright, his entire identity contingent on his play *The Primrose Way* succeeding (007-012). Bruce Carmyle measures worth by social standing and family position.

### Sally's Perspective

Sally herself defines success differently. With her legacy of twenty-five thousand dollars (001-018), she chooses travel and experience over investment. She values relationships and personal happiness above professional achievement. Her disillusionment crystallizes when she realizes men "worshipped success" with "how little of themselves they had to spare for anything else" (007-014).

### The Theatre as Success Crucible

The theatre world serves as the novel's testing ground for success. Gerald's play represents his shot at recognition; Fillmore's financial backing makes him a producer; the influenza closure (006-012) threatens all their ambitions. The contrast between artistic merit and commercial viability mirrors the novel's larger success theme.

## Critical Passages

> Success! How men worshipped it, and how little of themselves they had to spare for anything else. (007-014)

Sally's insight captures the novel's central critique of male ambition. She realizes that "there might be a woman in each of their lives, but she came second — an afterthought — a thing for their spare time" (007-014).

## Character Perspectives

- **Sally Nicholas**: Rejects the pursuit of success as an end; values love, loyalty, and personal happiness
- **Gerald Foster**: Obsessed with theatrical success; his career defines his self-worth (007-012)
- **Fillmore Nicholas**: Uses money and business status to signal social climbing (001-046, 001-061)
- **Ginger Kemp**: A counter-example — succeeds through character rather than ambition, finding quiet contentment at "Journey's End" (018-003)

## Narrative Significance

The success theme structures the novel's emotional arc. Sally begins with naive enthusiasm for others' ambitions, experiences disillusionment in Detroit (007-014), and ultimately finds her own definition of success in domestic happiness with Ginger in Patchogue (018-003). Wodehouse's comic treatment ensures the critique never becomes heavy-handed — Fillmore's self-importance and Gerald's self-pity are played for laughs even as they illuminate real human failings.
