# Isadore Abrahams

Isadore Abrahams is the proprietor of the Flower Garden, a popular New York supper-club for after-theatre dining and dancing. A "genial, honest, domestic man" and "a credit to the community," Abrahams represents the solid, respectable business class that forms the backdrop to Sally's New York adventures [006-P05].

## Role in the Story

Abrahams appears primarily as the owner of the establishment where several key scenes take place. The Flower Garden, advertised as "one of the town's wonder-places, with its incomparable dance-floor, enchanting music, cuisine, and service de luxe," is one of several dancing clubs that "had swum to popularity on the rising flood of New York's dancing craze" [006-P06]. Unlike many of its rivals, it enjoyed "unvarying prosperity" — a testament to Abrahams's capable management [006-P06].

The club serves as a social crossroads where characters including Sally, Gerald Foster, and Reginald Cracknell intersect.

## Personality & Traits

Abrahams is portrayed as a contented family man. The narrator describes him dining "in the bosom of his family at his residence at Far Rockaway," with his wife Rebecca "beaming at him over her comfortable plinth of chins" and his children — David, Jacob, Morris, and Saide — "too busy at the moment ingurgitating goulash" [006-P05]. This domestic tableau establishes Abrahams as grounded and prosperous, a contrast to the speculative, restless characters who frequent his establishment.

His pride in the Flower Garden is evident: "even without his personal statements to that effect... Isadore Abrahams thought well of the place" [006-P06].

## Key Relationships

- **Rebecca Abrahams**: His wife, who beams at him across the dinner table [006-P05].
- **Lee Schoenstein**: The master of ceremonies at the Flower Garden, employed by Abrahams [006-P07].
- **Sally Nicholas**: A patron of the Flower Garden, where she encounters various characters from her New York circle.

## Notable Scenes

### The Family Dinner at Far Rockaway

The narrator's first introduction to Abrahams finds him at home, having just finished "a plateful of succulent goulash." The scene is warmly comic: "Round the table his children, David, Jacob, Morris and Saide, would have beamed at him if they had not been too busy at the moment ingurgitating goulash" [006-P05].

### The Flower Garden

The club, as described in its own advertisements, is "large and spacious, and sumptuously appointed." Its continued [[success]] while rivals fail speaks to Abrahams's business acumen and his commitment to running "a nice place... run nice" [006-P06].


> Mr. Isadore Abrahams, the founder and proprietor of that deservedly popular dancing resort poetically named "The Flower Garden," leaned back in his chair with a contented sigh and laid down the knife and fork with which he had been assailing a plateful of succulent goulash. He was dining, as was his admirable custom, in the bosom of his family at his residence at Far Rockaway. A genial, honest, domestic man was Mr. Abrahams, a credit to the community.
>
> (014-281)

## Significance

Isadore Abrahams represents the honest prosperity achieved through steady work rather than speculation — a counterpoint to Fillmore Nicholas's disastrous get-rich-quick schemes. His Flower Garden provides the setting for much of the novel's New York social life, and his domestic contentment at Far Rockaway offers a glimpse of the stable, family-centered life that stands in quiet contrast to the romantic and financial turbulence of the main characters.
