# The Roville Beach Encounter

Sally Nicholas's first meeting with Ginger Kemp on the beach at Roville-sur-Mer is the inciting incident of the novel's central romance. What begins as a comic misunderstanding — a box of chocolates sent by an anonymous admirer — develops into a scene that establishes the dynamic between Sally and Ginger and reveals, through the chaos of a dog fight, the first hint of Ginger's hidden depths.

## The Setting

Sally is spending the morning on the Roville *plage*, "sitting with her back against a hillock of golden sand, watching with half-closed eyes the denizens of Roville-sur-Mer at their familiar morning occupations" [002-P01]. The scene is typical of a French seaside resort: whiskered fathers, female relatives under "gay parasols," dogs roaming and children digging. Sally is enjoying "a magic month of lazy happiness" before the call of America — and *The Primrose Way*'s Detroit opening — pulls her back [002-P01].

## The Encounter

Two English-speaking men seat themselves nearby — a novelty in Roville — and Sally, on the verge of dozing off, is "jerked back from the borders of sleep" by the sound of the familiar tongue [002-P01]. One is a "dark man" of severe and correct bearing; the other is "a red-haired young man" who, after noticing Sally, sends a page boy to deliver a box of chocolates with a clumsily gallant note.

The dark man, revealed to be a solicitor named Scrymgeour, has been sent by Ginger's cousin to pay Ginger off — to remove him from England and the family's embarrassment. The exchange is awkward, made more so by the presence of Sally, who can overhear their conversation despite their assumption that the French beach is private enough for such business [002-P01].

## The Dog Fight

The tension of the scene is shattered by "the prettiest dog fight that Roville had seen that season" [002-P04]. In the ensuing chaos of snarling, yelping canines, it is Ginger — socially awkward, chronically embarrassed, apparently useless — who emerges as "the one Master Mind" capable of restoring order:

> "He had the same effect on the almost inextricably entwined belligerents as, in medieval legend, the Holy Grail, sliding down the sunbeam, used to have on battling knights... There was a magic in his soothing hands, a spell in his voice: and in a shorter time than one would have believed possible dog after dog had been sorted out and calmed down" [002-P05].

## Significance

The beach encounter establishes the pattern that will define Sally and Ginger's relationship: Ginger's surface awkwardness giving way to moments of unexpected competence, and Sally's growing recognition that there is more to this red-haired young man than meets the eye. The dog fight, a classic Wodehouse set piece, transforms what could have been mere romantic comedy into something more revealing — a demonstration that Ginger's "magic" lies precisely in the practical, physical realm where his social deficiencies don't matter.
