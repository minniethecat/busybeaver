# White Plains

White Plains, New York, serves as the location of Bugs Butler's training camp in *The Adventures of Sally*. It is here that the disastrous public sparring session takes place — the event that destroys Butler's credibility, sinks Fillmore Nicholas's boxing promotion, and provides Ginger Kemp with his unexpected moment of heroism.

## Location & Character

White Plains is a real town in Westchester County, New York, but appears in the novel primarily as the site of Butler's training facilities. The camp itself occupies "a large room, half barn, half gymnasium," filled with "athletic appliances of various kinds" and centered around "a wide roped-off space" — the boxing ring [013-P01].

## The Training Camp

The commercialization of boxing is evident in the camp's operations. "This is a commercial age, and the days when a prominent pugilist's training activities used to be hidden from the public gaze are over. Today, if the public can lay its hands on fifty cents, it may come and gaze its fill" [013-P01].

The audience at the fateful training session includes "plutocrats to the number of about forty," though many — to the regret of Lester Burrowes, Butler's manager — have not "parted with solid coin." Newspaper reporters on the free list provide coverage that will prove disastrous when the session goes wrong [013-P01].

## Story Events

### The Public Sparring Session

Sally Nicholas visits the camp, where Fillmore has found Ginger Kemp employment as a sparring-partner. What should be a routine training exhibition turns catastrophic when Butler, showing off in front of Sally and the assembled press, tries to humiliate Ginger. Stung by the insult, Ginger fights back with unexpected ferocity, and in the ensuing brawl Butler's feet become tangled, causing him to fall [013-P05]–[013-P06].

The knockdown, witnessed by the newspapermen, becomes a sensation that destroys public confidence in Butler's chances against champion Lew Lucas. The arena at Jersey City is less than a quarter full on fight night, and Fillmore loses everything he invested in the promotion [013-P07]–[013-P08].

### Sally's Surprise Appearance

Fillmore is stunned to find Sally at the camp — he had left her "not much more than a week ago in England, in Shropshire, at Monk's Crofton." Her sudden appearance "in Bugs Butler's training-camp at White Plains, in the State of New York" without warning is a characteristic Sally moment, demonstrating her independence and her habit of turning up where least expected [013-P01].


> Great men, in their moments of weakness, are to be pitied rather than scorned. If ever a man had an excuse for leaping like a young ram, Fillmore had it. He had left Sally not much more than a week ago in England, in Shropshire, at Monk's Crofton. She had said nothing of any intention on her part of leaving the country, the county, or the house. Yet here she was, in Bugs Butler's training-camp at White Plains, in the State of New York, speaking softly in his ear without even going through the preliminary of tapping him on the shoulder to advertise her presence.
>
> (013-061)

## Significance

White Plains functions as the stage for one of the novel's most dramatic turning points. The training camp scene brings together the boxing subplot, the Sally-Ginger romance, and Fillmore's financial downfall in a single, chaotic set piece. The location — half rustic barn, half commercial spectacle — mirrors the uneasy collision of sport and business that defines the Butler-Fillmore enterprise.
