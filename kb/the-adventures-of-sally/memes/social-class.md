# Social Class

P.G. Wodehouse's comic vision depends on precise observation of social class. In *The Adventures of Sally*, class boundaries are both rigid and porous, and much of the novel's humour comes from characters attempting to cross them.

## The Boarding-House Democracy

Mrs. Meecher's boarding-house is the novel's social melting pot. "Transient and residential" guests (001-003) mix without regard to background: actors, Wall Street clerks, vaudeville performers, and an heiress all share the same table. Mr. Faucitt, the elderly English actor, presides with old-world courtesy over this democratic gathering (001-028).

## Fillmore's Aspirations

Fillmore Nicholas is the novel's most explicit social climber. His white evening waistcoat at the boarding-house party (001-003, 001-045) is both a costume and a statement. He dismisses his former friends: "One's in a position where all these people are entitled to call themselves one's friends" (001-070). Wodehouse gently mocks his pretensions while never quite condemning them.
> "The gentleman referred to, who sat at the speaker's end of the table, acknowledged the tribute with a brief nod of the head. It was a nod of condescension; the nod of one who, conscious of being hedged about by social inferiors, nevertheless does his best to be not unkindly."

> (001-035)


## Anglo-American Contrast

The novel's transatlantic setting enables class comparisons. English characters like Mr. Carmyle represent an older, more established class system; Americans like Sally embody a more fluid social world. Mr. Faucitt, who has "taken out what are technically known as my 'papers'" (001-037), bridges both worlds as an Englishman who has become American.

## The Servant Question

Ginger Kemp's role as a sparring-partner and later as a kind of gentleman-farmer at Patchogue (018-003) touches on the servant class in a comic register. Characters drift between social roles — Ginger is at various times a boxer, a secretary, a farmer — with Wodehouse suggesting that character matters more than station.
