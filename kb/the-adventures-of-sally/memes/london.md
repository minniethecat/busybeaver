# London

London is the English pole of *The Adventures of Sally*'s transatlantic geography. While New York provides the novel's primary stage, London functions as alternate destination, holding action, and emotional landscape — the city to which characters flee, from which they pursue, and against which American energy is measured.

## Fog and Atmosphere

Mr. Faucitt, the elderly English actor long resident in America, gives the novel's most evocative description of London:

> "I confess that, happy as I have been in this country, there are times when I miss those wonderful London days, when a sort of cosy brown mist hangs over the streets and the pavements ooze with a perspiration of mud and water, and you see through the haze the yellow glow of the Bodega lamps shining in the distance like harbour-lights." (007-036)

This nostalgic vision is tempered by Wodehouse's comic simile of London fog lifting like "the sun suddenly bursting through a London fog" when an office-boy's scowl becomes a winning smile (017-012).

## Sally's London

Sally flees to London after heartbreak. Her letters home, addressed from "London, W.," record the disorientation of the returned exile's city. Mr. Faucitt, having been absent from England for nearly thirty years, finds the London of his youth transformed — the Empire Theatre no longer a music-hall, the Tivoli pulled down, his old Rupert Street baker's shop now a dressmaker's. He cheers up only when "a lovely magenta fog came on and showed him that some things were still going along as in the good old days" (017-004).

## Bleke's Coffee House

Bleke's Coffee House, where Uncle Donald hosts a dinner for Ginger Kemp, is described as "that relic of Old London" — a morgue-like establishment "which confined its custom principally to regular patrons who had not missed an evening there for half a century" (008-003). The dinner represents the lowest point of Ginger's depression during his hopeless love for Sally.

## The Savoy and Uncle Donald

Fillmore Nicholas, visiting London to arrange the English production of *The Primrose Way*, stays at the Savoy Hotel — "simply an annex to Broadway" where "there were Americans at every table as far as the eye could reach" (017-013). Uncle Donald, "the Family's chosen representative," occupies an armchair in Bruce Carmyle's South Audley Street apartment with his "two hundred and forty pounds" of flesh. He has "one of the three heartiest appetites in the southwestern postal division of London" (020-004).
