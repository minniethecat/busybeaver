# The Flower Garden

The Flower Garden is a Broadway dancing resort in [[new-york-city|New York]], owned and
operated by [[isadore-abrahams|Isadore Abrahams]]. Billed as "a nice place and run nice,"
it is one of many establishments that rose to popularity on the dancing craze that swept
New York, and unlike many of its rivals, it enjoyed "unvarying prosperity" (016-145).

## The Establishment

The Flower Garden is "poetically named" — a touch of whimsy from its proprietor
Mr. Abrahams, who "had tried to give the public something as closely resembling a
flower-garden as it was possible for an overcrowded, overheated, overnoisy Broadway
dancing-resort to achieve" (016-146). Its orchestra is of a calibre that "discouraged vocal
competition" (016-147), making conversation virtually impossible — a defining
characteristic of the venue's atmosphere.

The venue employs a small staff of "hostesses" — the newest of whom is
[[mabel-hobson|Miss Mabel Hobson]], who "sat watching the revels with a distant hauteur"
(016-146). [[lee-schoenstein|Lee Schoenstein]] serves as master of ceremonies, managing
the floor with professional showmanship. The nightly entertainment includes a specialty
act by George Washington Williams, a "talented coloured gentleman extracted from
small-time vaudeville" (016-147), whose performances provide a distinctive draw for
the establishment's clientele.

The Flower Garden's enduring [[success]] amid the transient Broadway dance-hall scene
reflects Abrahams's shrewd management. While competitors rose and fell with each
season's fashionable whims, the Flower Garden maintained its clientele through a
combination of reliable entertainment, professional staff, and a carefully cultivated
atmosphere that, however far it fell short of its floral namesake, offered patrons
a dependable evening's diversion.

> It was crowded, as always. The Flower Garden was one of the many establishments of the same kind which had swum to popularity on the rising flood of New York's dancing craze; and doubtless because, as its proprietor had claimed, it was a nice place and run nice, it had continued, unlike many of its rivals, to enjoy unvarying prosperity. In its advertisement, it described itself as "a supper-club for after-theatre dining and dancing," adding that "large and spacious, and sumptuously appointed," it was "one of the town's wonder-places, with its incomparable dance-floor, enchanting music, cuisine, and service de luxe." From which it may be gathered, even without his personal statements to that effect, that Isadore Abrahams thought well of the place.
>
> (016-017)

## Sally's Employment

[[sally-nicholas|Sally Nicholas]] secures a position at the Flower Garden as an
"instructress" — a role she describes with self-deprecating humour: "as if anybody could
'instruct' the men who came there" (016-145). It is through this job that she supports
herself after Fillmore's theatrical venture collapses, demonstrating her pragmatic
willingness to earn her living by any honest means, regardless of how far the work
falls from her social ambitions.

The contrast between Sally's boarding-house residence and the gaudy Flower Garden is
striking: by day she endures the pinched economies of Mrs. Meecher's establishment;
by night she moves through the overheated, overnoisy Broadway nightlife on Abrahams's
payroll, a double life emblematic of the precarious social mobility of working women
in the Prohibition-era city.

## The Proposal Scene

In Chapter XVI, the Flower Garden serves as the setting for [[bruce-carmyle|Bruce
Carmyle]]'s attempted proposal to Sally. The venue, however, offends Bruce's sensibilities.
He "belonged to the orthodox school of thought which looks on moonlight and solitude as
the proper setting for a proposal of marriage" (016-156), and the overheated, overcrowded,
noisy atmosphere of the Flower Garden "jarred upon him profoundly" (016-157). He leaves
the establishment in "stiff disgust," expressing "his unalterable sentiments concerning
the Flower Garden, that apple of Isadore Abrahams's eye, in a snort of loathing"
(016-158).

The setting's incompatibility with Bruce's romantic sensibilities serves a narrative
function: it underscores the fundamental mismatch between his stiff, aristocratic
English expectations and Sally's pragmatic, unpretentious American world. The Flower
Garden, for all its gaudiness, represents honest work and self-reliance — values that
Bruce, despite his genuine affection for Sally, cannot fully appreciate.

Despite Bruce's disapproval, the Flower Garden represents Sally's hard-won independence —
a place of honest work in the bustling heart of Broadway, and one of the novel's most
vividly rendered social spaces.

## The Dancing Craze Context

The Flower Garden belongs to the broader phenomenon of the post-World War I dancing
craze that transformed American urban nightlife. Abrahams's establishment capitalizes
on a moment when social dancing had moved from private ballrooms to commercial venues,
democratizing an activity once reserved for the wealthy. The "overcrowded, overheated,
overnoisy" quality that Bruce finds so offensive is, in this context, the very sign of
the venue's commercial success and its role in making entertainment accessible to a
broad middle-class public.
