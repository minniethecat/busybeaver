# Ambition vs. Contentment

The tension between ambition and contentment structures the novel's emotional arc. Wodehouse presents ambition as both admirable and corrosive, and contentment — in Sally and Ginger's Patchogue life — as the novel's true happy ending.

## The Cost of Ambition

Gerald Foster's ambition consumes him. "If this play gets over — and it's going to — I'll show 'em!" (007-012). His drive for theatrical success leaves no room for anything else, including Sally. Her realization that "there might be a woman in each of their lives, but she came second — an afterthought — a thing for their spare time" (007-014) crystallizes the novel's critique of ambition as a form of emotional blindness.

> Success! How men worshipped it, and how little of themselves they had to spare for anything else. Ironically, it was the theme of this very play of Gerald's which she had saved from destruction. Of all the men she knew, how many had any view of life except as a race which they must strain every nerve to win, regardless of what they missed by the wayside in their haste? Fillmore—Gerald—all of them. There might be a woman in each of their lives, but she came second—an afterthought—a thing for their spare time. Gerald was everything to her. His success would never be more than a side-issue as far as she was concerned. He himself, without any of the trappings of success, was enough for her. But she was not enough for him. A spasm of futile jealousy shook her. She shivered.
>
> (007-014)

## Fillmore's Restless Striving

Fillmore moves from Wall Street to theatrical producing, always seeking the next "big thing" (001-064). His ambition is comic — the hundred half-dollar cigars, the expanding waistcoat (007-005) — but also poignant. He genuinely wants to succeed, and his failure is softened by Wodehouse's affectionate treatment.

## Contentment as Resolution

"Journey's End" (018-003) presents contentment as the novel's genuine resolution. Sally and Ginger's life in Patchogue — the evening scents, the stars, the sound of Ginger's footsteps on the road (018-005) — represents a deliberate choice against ambition. "Life good," Sally thinks (018-003), and the simplicity of the statement is its point.

## The Wodehouse Formula

Wodehouse's comic vision makes ambition funny rather than tragic. Characters who strive too hard are gently mocked; characters who accept themselves are rewarded. Ginger, who "clean forgot" the chops (018-022), embodies a kind of contented imperfection that the novel endorses. The message is comic but genuine: striving less may mean living more.
