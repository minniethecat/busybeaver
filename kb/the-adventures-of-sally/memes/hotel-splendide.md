# Hotel Splendide

## Location and Prestige

The Hotel Splendide is the foremost hotel in the French seaside resort of Roville-sur-Mer — the kind of establishment where the wealthy and fashionable naturally gravitate. As one character observes of a rich acquaintance: "If there's one thing he's particular about, it's the sort of hotel he goes to. Ever since I've known him he has always wanted the best. I should have thought he would have gone to the Splendide." (002-038)

## Service and Atmosphere

The Splendide prides itself on formal, impersonal service. Unlike the more casual establishments in Roville, "at the Hotel Splendide the waiters never bent over you and breathed cordial suggestions down the side of your face." (003-100) The hotel hosts dances that draw the fashionable set, and its clientele represents the town's social elite. (002-251)

## Class Contrast with the Normandie

The Hotel Splendide and the Normandie form a deliberate contrast in the Roville chapters. When a young man suffers a disastrous evening at the Casino, he is obliged to make "a hurried shift from the Hotel Splendide to the humbler Normandie" — a descent that perfectly encapsulates the social geography of the town. (002-251) Where the Normandie embodies thrift and quiet domesticity, the Splendide represents wealth, fashion, and the glittering surface of Riviera society.

## Sally's Perspective

Sally Nicholas, though an heiress, chooses to stay at the Normandie rather than the Splendide — a decision that reflects her innate prudence and resistance to the ostentatious consumption that characterises her brother Fillmore's circle. Her perspective on the Splendide is informed by her role as an outsider to its world of formal dances and formal waiters.
