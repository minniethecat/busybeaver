# Marvellous Murphys

The **Marvellous Murphys** are an equilibristic (acrobatic) comedy duo who reside at [[mrs-meechers-boardinghouse|Mrs. Meecher's Boarding House]] and perform at the Bushwick theatre. Their presence at [[sallys-party|Sally's Party]] in Chapter I provides some of the novel's earliest comic energy and establishes the eclectic social mix of the boarding house.

## Role in the Story

The Murphys appear primarily in the opening chapter, where their disruptive instincts and theatrical sensibilities make them a memorable part of Sally's after-dinner party. They function as representatives of the vaudeville world — transients whose presence in the boarding house is noted with some suspicion by longer-term residents.

## Notable Scenes

### The After-Dinner Speeches

When the party moves into the formal after-dinner speech phase, the Murphys attempt to assert their comedic prerogative:

> A movement on the part of the Marvellous Murphys — new arrivals, who had been playing the Bushwick with their equilibristic act during the preceding week — to form a party of the extreme left and heckle the speaker, broke down under a cold look from their hostess. (001-030)

The phrase "party of the extreme left" is a political metaphor repurposed for comic effect: the Murphys are staging a parliamentary opposition within the microcosm of the boarding-house dining room. Sally's "cold look" is sufficient to quell the rebellion, establishing early in the novel her formidable capacity for social control.

### The Interjection

The smaller of the two Murphys speaks up during [[maxwell-faucitt|Mr. Faucitt's]] remarks, interjecting with a comment about the theatrical profession (001-033). The interruption is characteristic of the Murphys's comic function: they are hecklers by instinct, unable to resist the impulse to puncture formality.

### The Critical Standard

Despite their disruptive tendencies, the Murphys possess what the narrator presents as genuine, if idiosyncratic, critical judgment:

> In approving of Sally, the Marvellous Murphys had been guilty of no lapse from their high critical standard. (001-031)

This throwaway observation has a dual function: it elevates Sally (winning the approval of vaudeville professionals is no small feat) while simultaneously hinting at the absurdity of the Murphys's self-regard. The notion that equilibristic comedians possess a "high critical standard" from which endorsements might be a "lapse" is itself a joke.

## Personality and Traits

The Murphys are defined by three qualities: professional pride (they take their vaudeville act seriously), disruptive instinct (they cannot resist heckling), and underlying good nature (they are deterred by a single stern look from Sally). The smaller Murphy is the more vocal of the pair, acting as spokesperson for the duo.

The narrator refers to them as "the marvellous brethren" (001-041), a phrase that captures both their act's billing and the gently ironic distance from which Wodehouse observes them.

## Significance

The Murphys contribute to the novel's texture of comic variety. As representatives of vaudeville — a world adjacent to but distinct from the theatrical ambitions of [[fillmore-nicholas|Fillmore Nicholas]] — they broaden the social canvas of the boarding house. Their brief appearance also serves to demonstrate Sally's authority: even professional disrupters submit to her command.

The boarding house itself is characterized partly through its residents' reactions to the Murphys. A long-term resident "disapproved of the marvellous brethren on general grounds because, himself a resident of years standing, he considered that these transients from the vaudeville stage lowered the tone of the boardinghouse" (001-041). His more specific grievance — that the smaller Murphy addressed him as "grandpa" — neatly captures the generational and cultural tensions within the establishment.
