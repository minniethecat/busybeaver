# Transatlantic Culture

*The Adventures of Sally* moves between New York, England, France, and Detroit, making transatlantic culture a defining element. Wodehouse, himself an Anglo-American transplant, uses the Atlantic crossing as both plot device and comic lens.

## America as Opportunity

For Sally and her circle, America represents freedom and possibility. New York is a city of boarding-houses, Broadway theatres, and Wall Street firms (001-005). The novel's American characters — Sally, Fillmore, the boarding-house residents — inhabit a world where social mobility is assumed and energy is valued over pedigree.

## England as Tradition

English characters represent an older order. Mr. Faucitt, "speaking as an Englishman" (001-037), brings old-world theatrical dignity to the New York boarding-house. Bruce Carmyle embodies English gentility and social convention. The contrast between American informality and English formality generates much of the novel's gentle comedy.

## France as Escape

Sally's planned trip to France — "I'm going to start with a trip to Europe... France, specially" (001-024) — positions Europe as escape and adventure. Roville-sur-Mer becomes the setting for key romantic developments, representing a neutral ground between the Anglo-American poles.

## The Expatriate Experience

Mr. Faucitt, who has "taken out what are technically known as my 'papers'" (001-037), represents the naturalized American who retains English sensibilities. Ginger moves between countries with the ease of the rootless, while Sally's journey takes her from America to Europe and back.

## Wodehouse's Transatlantic Vision

Wodehouse himself lived between England, America, and France, and his comfort with transatlantic settings gives the novel its distinctive geographic breadth. National identity is treated as a comic attribute rather than a serious division — characters are more defined by their individual personalities than their national origins.


> "She had drifted luxuriously from one French town to another, till the charm of Roville, with its blue sky, its Casino, its snow-white hotels along the Promenade, and its general glitter and gaiety, had brought her to a halt. Here she could have stayed indefinitely, but the voice of America was calling her back."
>
> (002-004)
