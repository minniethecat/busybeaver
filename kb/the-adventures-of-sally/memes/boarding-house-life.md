# Boarding House Life

Mrs. Meecher's "select boardinghouse (transient and residential)" (001-003) serves as the novel's primary social setting. It is a democratic space where actors, clerks, heiresses, and vaudeville performers share meals and conversation.

## A Democratic Microcosm

The boarding-house brings together characters who would never meet in more stratified settings. Augustus Bartlett of Kahn, Morris and Brown, the Wall Street brokers (001-005), sits alongside the Marvellous Murphys, vaudeville performers (001-033). Mr. Faucitt, the veteran actor, presides with "easy grace which actors of the old school learned" (001-028). The boarding-house is Wodehouse's deliberate leveling device.
> "Mr. Faucitt eyed him with disfavour. He disapproved of the marvellous brethren on general grounds because, himself a resident of years standing, he considered that these transients from the vaudeville stage lowered the tone of the boardinghouse; but particularly because the one who had just spoken had, on his first evening in the place, addressed him as 'grandpa.'"
>
> (001-041)


## Mrs. Meecher's Domain

Mrs. Meecher herself is a background presence whose establishment provides the novel's gravitational center. Her concern for Toto, the "woolly dog" suffering from "a dreadful lethargy" (018-008), becomes a minor running joke that humanizes the boarding-house world.

## Meals as Social Theatre

Dinner at the boarding-house is performance. Sally's party in Chapter I, with Mr. Faucitt's speech and Fillmore's disastrous reply (001-049), demonstrates how the shared table becomes a stage. Characters perform their identities — Fillmore in his white waistcoat (001-045), the "gloomy-looking man with spectacles" recalling missed opportunities (001-022).

## The End of an Era

By the novel's end, the boarding-house has given way to domesticity. Sally and Ginger's life in Patchogue — their own home, their own kitchen, the "open kitchen door" (018-016) — represents the transition from communal boarding-house life to private domestic happiness. The boarding-house was a phase, necessary and formative, but not permanent.
