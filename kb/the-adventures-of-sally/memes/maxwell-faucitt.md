# Maxwell Faucitt

Maxwell Faucitt is "the boardinghouse's oldest inhabitant" at Mrs. Meecher's establishment, a retired actor whose irrepressible fondness for public speaking is both a source of comedy and a touchstone of the boardinghouse's eccentric social life [013-P04]. He embodies the old-fashioned theatrical world — all grace, dignity, and grandiloquence — set against the modern bustle of 1920s New York.

## Role in the Story

Faucitt's primary function is comic: he is the resident orator who can be counted upon to rise and speak at any occasion. At Sally Nicholas's farewell dinner party, his fellow boarders have long since abandoned hope that "some day produce an occasion which Mr. Faucitt would feel that he could allow to pass without saying a few words" [013-P04]. His compulsion to orate is so reliable that "they had given up hope" of ever escaping it.

Beneath the comedy, however, Faucitt also provides a moment of genuine pathos. When he is thrown off balance by an unexpected turn in the dinner conversation, Sally reacts with immediate sympathy: "Sally, catching sight of his face, uttered a sharp wordless exclamation as if she had seen a child fall down and hurt itself in the street. The next moment she had run round the table and was standing behind him with her arms round his neck" [013-P04].

## Personality & Traits

Faucitt is characterized by his theatrical dignity and his inability to resist an audience. He rises to speak "slowly, with that easy grace which actors of the old school learned in the days when acting was acting" [013-P04]. His speeches are tolerated partly because "Miss Nicholas' hospitality had left them with a genial feeling of repletion" [013-P04].

When his expectations are upset, his composure crumbles: "the poor old man was wearing such an expression of surprise and dismay as he might have worn had somebody unexpectedly pulled the chair from under him. He was feeling the sick shock which comes to those who tread on a nonexistent last stair" [013-P04].

## Key Relationships

- **Sally Nicholas**: Shows genuine affection for the old actor, rushing to comfort him when he is distressed [013-P04].
- **The Marvellous Murphys**: New arrivals at the boardinghouse who attempt to heckle Faucitt's speech, only to be silenced by "a cold look from their hostess" [013-P04].
- **Mrs. Meecher**: The landlady of the boardinghouse where Faucitt has long resided.

## Notable Scenes

### The Farewell Dinner Speech

At Sally's farewell dinner, Faucitt predictably rises to speak. The other boarders, "partly because they had braced themselves to it, but principally because Miss Nicholas' hospitality had left them with a genial feeling of repletion, they settled themselves to listen with something resembling equanimity" [013-P04]. The equilibristic Marvellous Murphys attempt to form "a party of the extreme left and heckle the speaker" but are quelled by Sally's cold stare.

### The Moment of Distress

When an unexpected remark throws Faucitt off balance, his theatrical dignity collapses, revealing vulnerability beneath the mannered exterior. Sally's instinctive response — running to embrace him — shows the depth of her sympathy and the genuine affection the boardinghouse residents feel for one another [013-P04].


> At the far end of the table there was a stir, a cough, and the grating of a chair on the floor; and slowly, with that easy grace which actors of the old school learned in the days when acting was acting, Mr. Maxwell Faucitt, the boardinghouse's oldest inhabitant, rose to his feet.
>
> (001-017)

## Significance

Maxwell Faucitt represents the old theatrical world in gentle decline — dignified, mannered, and slightly ridiculous, but treated with affection rather than scorn. His presence at the boardinghouse, alongside vaudeville performers and Broadway hopefuls, creates a microcosm of New York's stratified entertainment world. Sally's tenderness toward him reveals her capacity for compassion that extends even to the most eccentric members of her adopted community.
