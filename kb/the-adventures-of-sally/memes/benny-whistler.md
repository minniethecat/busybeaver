# Benny Whistler

Benny Whistler is a heavyweight boxing contender mentioned in the early chapters of *The Adventures of Sally*, notable primarily as the subject of the boardinghouse's most enthusiastic betting advice. Though he never appears in person, his name recurs as a symbol of the speculative, get-rich-quick mentality that Sally wisely resists.

## Role in the Story

Whistler appears in the narrative solely through the testimony of "the mild young man" at Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse, a "seasoned sport" who champions Whistler's prospects with evangelical fervor. When Sally's fellow boarders learn of her inheritance, the mild young man declares: "If I had a few hundred thousand... I'd put every cent of it on Benny Whistler for the heavyweight championship" [001-P05].

His rationale is insider information: "Battling Tuke has been got at and means to lie down in the seventh" [001-P05]. The sporting faction at the boardinghouse is so convinced that "if you went about the matter cannily and without precipitation, three to one might be obtained" [001-P05].

Sally's response is characteristically sensible: "I don't want your Benny Whistler. I wouldn't have him if you gave him to me. If I want to lose money, I'll go to Monte Carlo and do it properly" [001-P06]. Her refusal to bet on a fixed fight establishes her prudence — a quality that makes her later decision to invest in Fillmore's theatrical venture all the more significant.

## Personality & Traits

What little is known of Whistler comes filtered through his advocate's enthusiasm. The mild young man describes him as "the redoubtable Benny Whistler," and his confidence stems not from Whistler's skill but from the alleged fix — the certainty that Battling Tuke "means to lie down" [001-P05]. This detail makes Whistler more a vehicle for Wodehouse's satire of gambling culture than a fully realized character.

## Key Relationships

- **Battling Tuke**: Whistler's opponent, who has allegedly agreed to lose the fight [001-P05].
- **The Mild Young Man**: Whistler's most ardent promoter at Mrs. Meecher's boardinghouse, whose "inside information" drives the betting enthusiasm [001-P05].
- **Sally Nicholas**: Refuses to gamble on Whistler despite the boarders' insistence [001-P06].

## Notable Scenes

### The Boardinghouse Debate

> "And lose every bean you've got," said a mild young man, with a deep voice across the table. "If I had a few hundred thousand," said the mild young man, "I'd put every cent of it on Benny Whistler for the heavyweight championship. I've private information that Battling Tuke has been got at and means to lie down in the seventh…"
>
> (001-008)


The boardinghouse divides into two camps: the "conservative and prudent element, led by Augustus Bartlett," who favor Liberty Bonds, and "the smaller, more sporting section" who "had already placed Sally's money on Benny Whistler, doling it out cautiously in small sums so as not to spoil the market" [001-P05]. Sally must intervene to "correct certain misapprehensions" — namely, that her inheritance is theirs to gamble [001-P06].

## Significance

Benny Whistler represents the culture of speculation and easy money that the novel gently satirizes. His name is invoked alongside stock tips and get-rich-quick schemes as part of the atmosphere of financial fantasy that surrounds Sally. Her refusal to bet on him — despite the promised three-to-one odds — demonstrates the sound judgment that makes her, ultimately, the novel's moral center.
