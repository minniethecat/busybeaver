# Gerald Foster

Gerald Foster is Sally Nicholas's first love and the novel's antagonist — not villainous, but fundamentally incompatible with Sally's values. A playwright whose entire identity depends on professional [[success]], Gerald embodies the ambition that the novel critiques.

## Personality and Traits

Gerald is handsome, charming, and entirely self-absorbed. His mood swings with his professional fortunes: when prospects are good, "all his old charm had returned" (007-010); when they fail, he collapses into self-pity. Sally's crucial insight is that "he himself, without any of the trappings of success, was enough for her. But she was not enough for him" (007-014).

## Key Relationships

- **Sally Nicholas**: Gerald loves Sally but only as a secondary concern. His play *The Primrose Way* is his true passion, and Sally realizes she "came second — an afterthought — a thing for their spare time" (007-014).

- **The Theatre World**: Gerald's identity is inseparable from his play. "If this play gets over — and it's going to — I'll show 'em!" (007-012). His ambition is his defining trait and his fatal flaw.

## Notable Scenes

- **The Belle Isle Drive** (ch07): Driving with Sally, Gerald "held forth spaciously on the big things he intended to do" (007-011), oblivious to her growing despair.

- **The Drunken Confrontation** (ch17): A drunken Gerald stumbles into Sally's room — "Hullo, Sally!" — with "a foolish smile on his face" (017-005). His maudlin self-pity reveals his essential weakness: "I was a fool ever to try writing plays" (017-017).

- **The Second Failure**: Gerald's follow-up play fails — "had another frost tonight. No good trying any more" (017-017) — and he plans to return to newspaper work, his dreams abandoned.


> "“I was a fool ever to try writing plays,” he went on. “Got a winner first time, but can’t repeat. It’s no good. Ought to have stuck to newspaper work. I’m good at that. Shall have to go back to it. Had another frost tonight. No good trying any more. Shall have to go back to the old grind, damn it.”" (017-017)

## Narrative Arc

Gerald begins as the romantic ideal and ends as a cautionary example. His arc is downward, but Wodehouse treats him with more sympathy than the plot requires. Gerald is not evil; he is simply incapable of being what Sally needs — a partner who values love over ambition.

## Significance

Gerald represents the novel's critique of male ambition. His failure is not professional (many playwrights fail) but personal: he cannot love well because he cannot see past his own career. He is the foil against which Ginger Kemp's virtues are measured.

## Others' View of Gerald

Gerald is evaluated by those around him according to their own moral frameworks:

- **Sally's view**: Sally's perception of Gerald undergoes the novel's most complete reversal. Initially she sees a talented artist whose potential is waiting to be realized. The Belle Isle drive crystallizes her disillusionment: "Success! How men worshipped it, and how little of themselves they had to spare for anything else" (007-014). By the end, she sees him clearly — not a tragic artist, but a man whose vanity masquerades as dedication.

- **Elsa Doland's view**: As a fellow performer, Elsa understands Gerald's theatrical world from the inside. Her presence as his co-star creates a triangle that illuminates Gerald's shallowness: he values the role Sally can play in his career more than the person she actually is.

- **The Audience's Verdict**: The ultimate judgment on Gerald comes from the anonymous audiences who reject his plays. His professional failure is the novel's objective corrective to his self-estimation — the world does not share his opinion of his own talent.

- **Fillmore Nicholas's view**: Fillmore, as Gerald's producer, sees him through the lens of commerce. Gerald is an asset whose value fluctuates with the box office. This transactional perspective mirrors Gerald's own tendency to see relationships in terms of utility.

## Author's Narrative Technique

Wodehouse's treatment of Gerald reveals a darker register within his comic method:

- **The Antagonist as Foil, Not Villain**: Wodehouse refuses to make Gerald a villain, which makes him more effective as Sally's wrong choice. He is not cruel or malicious — simply incapable of the kind of love Sally needs. This restraint allows the novel's romantic critique to be moral rather than melodramatic.

- **Professional Failure as Character Judgment**: Gerald's play failures serve a double function — comic plot point and moral assessment. The world's indifference to *The Primrose Way* is the objective correlative of Gerald's emotional inadequacy: he invests everything in the wrong thing.

- **The Drunken Scene as Exposure**: Gerald's intoxicated appearance in Sally's room (017-005) strips away his charm. "His face was white and puffy, and about him there hung like a nimbus a sodden disreputableness" (017-005) — Wodehouse's prose, normally light, takes on a clinical quality to document Gerald's unraveling.

- **Sympathy Without Endorsement**: Wodehouse grants Gerald enough interiority — his professional hopes, his genuine bafflement at failure — that the reader feels the waste of his potential. The novel pities Gerald even as it discards him, a tonal complexity unusual in romantic comedy.
