# Misfortune

Misfortune pervades *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* as both a narrative engine and a moral crucible. Rowson carefully distinguishes between mis[[fate|fortune]] as external calamity — financial ruin, illness, accident — and the self-inflicted catastrophe of vice. This distinction is essential to the novel's [[sensibility|sentiment]]al ethics: some mis[[fate|fortune]]s elicit [[compassion]] and deserve [[comfort|relief]], while others, born of immoral choice, serve as justice.

The novel's opening domestic tragedy establishes this framework. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s financial collapse, narrated in the second chapter, exemplifies the "respectable" mis[[fate|fortune]] that tests [[reputation|character]] without implying moral failure. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s subsequent ruin, by contrast, occupies the ambiguous territory between external mis[[fate|fortune]] and self-willed [[ruin|destruction]] — a tension that drives the novel's didactic project.

## Mr. Temple's Misfortune

The novel's first major instance of mis[[fate|fortune]] be[[ruin|fall]]s [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], whose financial collapse is presented as wholly undeserved. He is described as having "sunk under his mis[[fate|fortune]]s," a phrase that emphasizes the passive, overwhelming nature of economic catastrophe [002-029]. A [[youth|young]] man's heart responds with instinctive sympathy to this [[distress|suffering]], establishing the proper [[sensibility|sentiment]]al response to undeserved mis[[fate|fortune]].

Crucially, [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] articulates a moral principle that echoes throughout the novel: "Our mis[[fate|fortune]]s are not our faults" [002-060]. This distinction — between what be[[ruin|fall]]s a person and what they deserve — forms the ethical backbone of Rowson's treatment of [[distress|suffering]]. It also creates the tragic irony of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story: unlike her father, her mis[[fate|fortune]]s are intimately entangled with her faults.

## The Worst Misfortune

In one of the novel's most quoted lines, the narrator declares through a [[reputation|character]]: "There is one mis[[fate|fortune]] which is worse than death" [014-053]. The context makes clear that this supreme mis[[fate|fortune]] is the loss of female [[virtue]] — a fate worse than physical death because it encompasses social death, spiritual death, and the death of reputation.

| Type of Misfortune | Example in Novel | Moral Status |
|-------------------|------------------|--------------|
| Financial ruin | [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s bankruptcy | Undeserved; tests [[reputation|character]] |
| Loss of virtue | [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction | Self-implicated; the "worst" mis[[fate|fortune]] |
| Illness and danger | [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s final illness | Consequence of prior mis[[fate|fortune]] |
| Emotional torment | [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s guilt and shame | Self-inflicted but elicits sympathy |
| Relational loss | Separation from parents | Consequence of elopement |

## Misfortune as Moral Torpor

The novel traces the psychological trajectory of mis[[fate|fortune]]. After her abandonment, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] sinks into "that torpor into which this mis[[fate|fortune]]" plunges her [015-060]. This torpor — a state of emotional numbness and spiritual paralysis — represents the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's sophisticated understanding of trauma. Misfortune does not merely cause [[melancholy|sadness]]; it can extinguish the capacity for action and [[sensibility|feeling]] altogether.

This psychological insight serves Rowson's moral purpose: mis[[fate|fortune]] untreated by [[friendship]], [[religion]], or [[repentance]] becomes self-perpetuating. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s decline is not instantaneous but gradual, each mis[[fate|fortune]] compounding the last until the will to recover is extinguished.

## Misfortune and Affection

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s response to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s plight is described paradoxically: "great mis[[fate|fortune]] he had conceived a sincere [[love|affection]]" for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [020-053]. Here, Rowson identifies a dangerous dynamic in which mis[[fate|fortune]] itself becomes the ground of attachment. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "sincere [[love|affection]]" — itself a product of witnessing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] — proves no protection against his subsequent cruelty. The passage suggests that [[love|affection]] born of [[compassion|pity]] is unstable and self-regarding, a subtle psychological observation embedded within the novel's moral framework.

## Misfortune as Narrative Justification

Throughout the novel, [[reputation|character]]s invoke mis[[fate|fortune]] to explain and sometimes excuse their actions. When [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] asks "what mis[[fate|fortune]] has be[[ruin|fall]]en my child?" [014-049], the question carries both parental [[fear|anxiety]] and a premonition of the answer. The word "be[[ruin|fall]]en" p[[modesty|reserve]]s the [[reason|sense]] of mis[[fate|fortune]] as something that descends from without — a visitation rather than a consequence. Yet the reader, having witnessed [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s choices, understands the inadequacy of this framing.

This gap between how [[reputation|character]]s perceive mis[[fate|fortune]] and how the narrator frames it constitutes one of the novel's most sophisticated narrative techniques. Characters reach for the language of external calamity; the narrator insists on moral accountability.
