Wealth in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is an ambivalent possession — a resource that enables virtue but also creates the conditions for vice. [[mr-franklin|Mr. Franklin]]'s "superfluous wealth" ([004-077]) enables him to rescue [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] from debtor's prison, and his reflection on wealth's proper use models the novel's moral economy. Yet [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[education|upbringing]] as "the only child of a wealthy man, almost idolized by her parents" ([005-001]) suggests that wealth, by removing material [[adversity|hardship]], may also remove the discipline that [[adversity|hardship]] instills. Rowson's treatment of wealth is thus [[reputation|character]]istically pragmatic: wealth is good when it serves [[benevolence|charity]], dangerous when it enables indulgence.

## Definition

Rowson distinguishes between wealth properly used — as a means of relieving [[distress|suffering]] and rewarding virtue — and wealth as a source of moral hazard. [[mr-franklin|Mr. Franklin]]'s meditation on his "superfluous wealth" ([004-077]) models the proper attitude: wealth is a [[trust]], not a possession, and its surplus belongs to those in need. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[education|upbringing]] in wealthy comfort is presented not as a blessing but as a contributing cause of her [[ruin|fall]], since it left her unprepared for the world's dangers. Wealth in the novel is thus a moral test that most [[reputation|character]]s fail.

## Key Passages

> "Is not my fortune affluence," continued he, "nay superfluous wealth, when compared to the extreme indigence of Eldridge" ([004-077])

> "She was the only child of a wealthy man, almost idolized by her parents" — the narrator's introduction of Charlotte ([005-001])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mr-temple]]: Wealth properly stewarded. His [[fate|fortune]] enables his rescue of [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] and his journey to reclaim [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], making wealth an instrument of moral action.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: Wealth's false security. Raised in comfort and indulgence, she is paradoxically made more vulnerable by the very prosperity that was meant to protect her.
- [[montraville]]: Wealth as [[seduction|seducer]]'s tool. His [[fate|fortune]] allows him to pursue [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] across the Atlantic and to maintain her in [[new-york|New York]], making his wealth complicit in her ruin.

## Narrative Function

Wealth is the economic substrate of the novel's moral argument. Rowson uses the contrast between wealth and poverty to structure the reader's understanding of seduction's stakes: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [[ruin|fall]]s from comfort to [[poverty|destitution]], and the distance between those poles measures the magnitude of her catastrophe. Wealth also enables the novel's moments of rescue — without [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s [[fate|fortune]], [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] would remain in prison and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] would die unvisited — making it a necessary though insufficient condition for moral action.

## Related Concepts

- [[poverty]]: Wealth's opposite and the novel's warning. The [[ruin|fall]] from wealth to poverty is the material shape of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy.
- [[charity]]: Wealth's proper use. The novel insists that wealth's moral justification lies in its capacity to relieve [[distress|suffering]].
- [[education]]: The safeguard that wealth should provide but often fails to. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s wealthy [[education|upbringing]] included insufficient moral education.
