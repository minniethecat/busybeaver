Youth in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is not celebrated as a golden age but portrayed as a condition of acute [[weakness|vulnerability]]. Rowson's treatment of youth is relentlessly [[prudence|caution]]ary: "the mind of youth eagerly catches at promised pleasure" ([006-026]) without the [[experience]] to distinguish genuine [[love|affection]] from seductive flattery. The novel's opening description of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as part of "an assemblage of youth and innocence" ([001-017]) already encodes the danger — youth and innocence attract predators precisely because they have not yet learned to recognize them. For Rowson, youth is the period when moral [[reputation|character]] is tested and too often found [[poverty|want]]ing.

## Definition

Rowson presents youth as a structural [[weakness|vulnerability]] rather than a moral failing. The young person's "mind... eagerly catches at promised pleasure" ([006-026]) not out of corruption but out of in[[experience]] — she is "innocent by nature" and therefore "thinks not of the dangers lurking beneath" ([006-027]). Youth's specific [[weakness|vulnerability]] is cognitive rather than physical: the young cannot imagine the consequences of their actions because they have never [[experience]]d them. The novel's educational mission is thus framed as a supplement to youth's [[nature|natural]] deficits — the novel itself becoming the [[experience]] that youth lacks.

## Key Passages

> "Such an assemblage of youth and innocence naturally attracted the attention of the young soldiers" — the novel's opening description of Charlotte at school ([001-017])

> "The mind of youth eagerly catches at promised pleasure" — the narrator's diagnosis of youthful vulnerability ([006-026])

> "The natural timidity of her disposition, the tenderness of her heart, and the entertaining emotions in her youthful bosom" — Charlotte's character sketch ([007-103])

> "Some passing daughter of youthful mirth" — the narrator's image of the heedless young woman who fails to learn from Charlotte's example ([018-068])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Youth embodied and sacrificed. At fifteen, she is on the threshold of adult society with "youth and innocence" her only dowry, and both are exploited by those with more [[experience]] and less conscience.
- [[montraville]]: Youth as a [[seduction|seducer]]'s tool. His own relative youth makes him appear less threatening to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and her guardians, but his [[experience]] — "having travelled in their youth" ([016-004]) as the narrator says of worldly officers — outstrips his apparent innocence.
- [[julia-franklin]]: Youth properly guarded. Though young, Julia possesses the [[reason|prudence]] and [[family]] protection that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] lacked, making her youth a period of [[virtue|virtuous]] development rather than precipitous ruin.

## Narrative Function

Youth is the novel's foundational condition — the reason [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story is a tragedy rather than merely a crime. Rowson's insistence on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s youth (she is fifteen at the novel's start) makes her [[ruin|fall]] a failure of protection rather than a failure of [[reputation|character]]. The concept also serves the novel's didactic purpose: by addressing itself to young female readers, the novel positions itself as the guardian that youth requires, offering vicarious [[experience]] to prevent real catastrophe.

## Related Concepts

- [[innocence]]: Youth's defining quality and its specific danger. Youthful innocence is morally admirable but practically hazardous.
- [[education]]: The remedy for youth's vulnerabilities. Proper education — including the novel's own warnings — can supply the [[experience]] that youth lacks.
- [[seduction]]: Youth's greatest threat. The [[seduction|seducer]] targets the young precisely because their in[[experience]] makes them credulous.
- [[experience]]: Youth's opposite and its only true teacher. The tragedy of *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* is that [[experience]] comes too late to save its heroine.
