# Family

The network of blood relations and domestic [[love|affection]]s that, in Rowson's moral geography, constitutes the primary defence of female [[virtue]] against the predatory social world. The novel's plot is fundamentally a story about family: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s departure from her family, her [[distress|suffering]] in the absence of family protection, and her partial restoration through her father's redemptive journey to [[new-york|New-York]]. Family is not merely a background institution in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* — it is the novel's moral anchor, the structure whose absence makes [[seduction]] possible and whose presence makes [[ruin]] survivable.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, family is defined by reciprocal obligations: parents owe their children protection, guidance, and prudent governance; children owe their parents obedience, gratitude, and — crucially — continued presence within the family circle. The novel presents [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s departure from her family not merely as an unfortunate circumstance but as a *moral violation*: she abandons her filial [[duty]] when she leaves [[england|England]] with [[montraville|Montraville]], and this abandonment is the enabling condition of her [[ruin|fall]].

The [[mr-temple|Temple]] household is established as the novel's normative model of family. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] is described as living "in a style of decent hospitality, consistent with the simplicity of his [[reputation|character]] and the liberality of his heart" (002-003). The adjectives — decent, simple, liberal — define a domestic ideal that is [[modesty|modest]], genuine, and generous. This is not the family of aristocratic display but of bourgeois domesticity: small-scale, [[love|affection]]ate, morally serious.

Rowson also explores the [[weakness|vulnerability]] of family to economic pressure. The narrator notes that [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s "narrow income and increasing family" (010-002) impose real constraints, acknowledging that even [[virtue|virtuous]] families face material challenges. But the novel's argument is that family, even under strain, remains the best available protection — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is not that her family was imperfect but that she left it.

The embedded story of [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s family (003-017) provides a darker variation on the family theme. Here, family loyalty leads to disaster: [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]]'s son is killed in a duel defending his father's honour, and his daughter Lucy is targeted by the predatory Lewis. But this embedded narrative also demonstrates family's redemptive potential — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] rescues [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] and marries Lucy, creating the family whose [[ruin|destruction]] and partial restoration constitutes the main plot. Family, in Rowson's hands, is both fragile and resilient: it can be broken by seduction but also rebuilt by [[love]].

## Key Passages

> "He saw his elder brother... left with a narrow income and increasing family" (010-002)

This passage acknowledges the material pressures on family life, grounding the novel's domestic ideal in economic reality. The [[mr-temple|Temple]] family is not wealthy but "decent" — its virtue is a function of [[reputation|character]], not of [[wealth|affluence]]. This democratic vision of domestic virtue (available to the [[modesty|modest]] gentleman as much as to the aristocrat) is [[reputation|character]]istic of [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction's social ethos.

> "she took her into her own family, and from thence recommended her to the school" (006-015)

This passage describing how [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] came to be at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school reveals the chain of family-like protections that can fail. Each link in the chain — the "amiable lady" who took [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] in, the school that employed her — is a family-substitute that proves inadequate. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s corruption within these institutions is the novel's warning that family-like institutions cannot replace actual family bonds.

> "affection and protection of her family" (024-052)

This phrase encapsulates the dual function Rowson assigns to family: [[love|affection]] (emotional nurture) and protection (physical and social safeguarding). [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is that she has forfeited both. The novel's didactic message is spatial: stay within the protective radius of family, or risk losing the protection that makes female virtue possible.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The daughter who leaves her family. Her seduction is simultaneously a moral [[ruin|fall]] and a geographic departure from the family circle.

Her [[death|deathbed]] scene — reunited with her father but [[death|dying]] — embodies the novel's argument that family can forgive but cannot fully undo the consequences of its breach.
- [[mr-temple]]: The ideal father. His virtues — [[reason|prudence]], [[benevolence|generosity]], steadfast [[affection|love]] — define the family as a protective institution. His journey to [[america|America]] to find [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is the novel's most powerful assertion of family obligation: a father's [[love]] transcends distance, [[shame|disgrace]], and even death.
- [[montraville]]: The [[seduction|seducer]] as family-destroyer. He does not merely [[seduction|seduce]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]; he removes her from her family and, in doing so, strips her of the protection that might have saved her. His belated attempt to provide for her financially is a weak substitute for the family protection he helped her forfeit.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The anti-family figure. Having lost her own place in respectable domesticity, she works to destroy [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s — the novel's argument that a woman who has "lost sight of the basis on which reputation, honour... rests" (007-092) becomes an active threat to other women's family bonds.
- [[colonel-crayton]]: Represents the remaking of family through marriage — but his marriage to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] imports corruption into the family structure, demonstrating that family bonds are only as strong as the individuals who form them.

## Narrative Function

Family is the novel's structuring absence. The plot is driven by [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s departure from her family and sustained by the consequences of that departure. Rowson uses this structure to advance a simple but powerful argument: female virtue is possible only within the protective framework of family. Remove the family, and the woman becomes vulnerable to every form of predation the novel catalogues.

The novel's resolution — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] adopting [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s orphaned daughter and returning to [[england|England]] — restores the family circle. The child, born in [[shame|disgrace]] in [[new-york|New-York]], is brought back to the [[england|English]] domestic sphere where family protection operates. This circular movement ([[england|England]] → [[america|America]] → [[england|England]], family intact → family broken → family restored) is the novel's deepest structural pattern and its most optimistic claim: family can be rebuilt after catastrophe.
