[021-001] Teach me to feel another's [[sorrow|woe]],

[021-002] To hide the fault I see,

[021-003] That [[compassion|mercy]] I to others show,

[021-004] That [[compassion|mercy]] show to me. POPE.

[021-005] WHEN [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] was dressed, she began to feel embarrassed at the

[021-006] thought of beginning an acquaintance with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], and was distressed

[021-007] how to make the first visit. “I cannot go without some introduction,”

[021-008] said she, “it will look so like impertinent curiosity.” At length

[021-009] recollecting herself, she stepped into the garden, and gathering a few

[021-010] fine cucumbers, took them in her hand by way of apology for her visit.

[021-011] A glow of conscious shame vermillioned Charlotte's face as Mrs.

[021-012] Beauchamp entered.

[021-013] “You will [[forgiveness|pardon]] me, Madam,” said she, “for not having before paid my

[021-014] respects to so amiable a neighbour; but we [[england|English]] people always keep up

[021-015] that [[modesty|reserve]] which is the characteristic of our nation wherever we go. I

[021-016] have taken the [[independence|liberty]] to bring you a few cucumbers, for I observed you

[021-017] had none in your garden.”

[021-018] Charlotte, though naturally polite and well-bred, was so confused she

[021-019] could hardly speak. Her kind visitor endeavoured to relieve her by

[021-020] not noticing her embarrassment. “I am come, Madam,” continued she, “to

[021-021] request you will spend the day with me. I shall be alone; and, as we are

[021-022] both strangers in this country, we may hereafter be extremely [[happiness|happy]] in

[021-023] each other's friendship.”

[021-024] “Your friendship, Madam,” said Charlotte blushing, “is an honour to

[021-025] all who are favoured with it. Little as I have seen of this part of the

[021-026] world, I am no stranger to Mrs. Beauchamp's [[kindness|goodness]] of heart and known

[021-027] humanity: but my friendship--” She paused, glanced her eye upon her own

[021-028] visible situation, and, spite of her endeavours to suppress them, burst

[021-029] into tears.

[021-030] Mrs. Beauchamp guessed the source from whence those tears flowed.

[021-031] “You seem unhappy, Madam,” said she: “shall I be thought worthy your

[021-032] confidence? will you [[trust|entrust]] me with the cause of your [[melancholy|sorrow]], and

[021-033] rest on my assurances to exert my utmost power to serve you.” Charlotte

[021-034] returned a look of gratitude, but could not speak, and Mrs. Beauchamp

[021-035] continued--“My heart was interested in your behalf the first moment I

[021-036] saw you, and I only lament I had not made earlier overtures towards an

[021-037] acquaintance; but I flatter myself you will henceforth consider me as

[021-038] your [[friendship|friend]].”

[021-039] “Oh Madam!” cried Charlotte, “I have forfeited the good opinion of all

[021-040] my friends; I have forsaken them, and undone myself.”

[021-041] “Come, come, my dear,” said Mrs. Beauchamp, “you must not indulge

[021-042] these gloomy thoughts: you are not I hope so miserable as you imagine

[021-043] yourself: endeavour to be composed, and let me be favoured with your

[021-044] company at dinner, when, if you can bring yourself to think me your

[021-045] friend, and repose a confidence in me, I am ready to convince you it

[021-046] shall not be abused.” She then arose, and bade her good morning.

[021-047] At the dining hour Charlotte repaired to Mrs. Beauchamp's, and during

[021-048] dinner assumed as composed an aspect as possible; but when the cloth

[021-049] was removed, she summoned all her resolution and determined to make Mrs.

[021-050] Beauchamp acquainted with every circumstance preceding her unfortunate

[021-051] elopement, and the earnest [[passion|desire]] she had to quit a way of life so

[021-052] repugnant to her feelings.

## Section 12

[021-053] With the benignant aspect of an angel of mercy did Mrs. Beauchamp listen

[021-054] to the artless tale: she was shocked to the soul to find how large a

[021-055] share [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] had in the seduction of this amiable girl, and a tear fell,

[021-056] when she reflected so vile a woman was now the wife of her father.

[021-057] When Charlotte had finished, she gave her a little time to collect her

[021-058] scattered spirits, and then asked her if she had never written to her

[021-059] friends.

[021-060] “Oh yes, Madam,” said she, “frequently: but I have broke their hearts:

[021-061] they are either dead or have cast me off for ever, for I have never

[021-062] received a single line from them.”

[021-063] “I rather suspect,” said Mrs. Beauchamp, “they have never had your

[021-064] letters: but suppose you were to hear from them, and they were willing

[021-065] to receive you, would you then leave this cruel [[montraville|Montraville]], and return

[021-066] to them?”

[021-067] “Would I!” said Charlotte, clasping her hands; “would not the poor

[021-068] sailor, tost on a tempestuous ocean, threatened every moment with

[021-069] death, gladly return to the shore he had left to trust to its deceitful

[021-070] calmness? Oh, my dear Madam, I would return, though to do it I were

[021-071] obliged to walk barefoot over a burning desert, and beg a scanty

[021-072] pittance of each traveller to support my existence. I would endure it

[021-073] all cheerfully, could I but once more see my dear, blessed mother, hear

[021-074] her pronounce my pardon, and bless me before I died; but alas! I shall

[021-075] never see her more; she has blotted the ungrateful Charlotte from her

[021-076] remembrance, and I shall sink to the grave loaded with her's and my

[021-077] father's curse.”

[021-078] Mrs. Beauchamp endeavoured to sooth her. “You shall write to them

[021-079] again,” said she, “and I will see that the letter is sent by the first

[021-080] packet that sails for England; in the mean time keep up your spirits,

[021-081] and hope every thing, by daring to deserve it.”

[021-082] She then turned the conversation, and Charlotte having taken a cup of

[021-083] tea, wished her benevolent friend a good evening.
