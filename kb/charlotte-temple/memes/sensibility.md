# Sensibility

The double-edged capacity at the heart of sentimental fiction: at once the moral faculty that enables [[charity|compassion]] and the unguarded emotional openness that leaves the novel's heroine prey to seduction. In Rowson's treatment, sensibility is both celebrated and warned against — essential to true virtue, yet fatal without the governance of reason and religious principle.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, sensibility denotes the capacity for acute and refined emotional response. Rowson inherits the concept from the eighteenth-century culture of sensibility, in which the ability to feel deeply — to weep at scenes of distress, to sympathise with [[distress|suffering]], to respond viscerally to beauty and pathos — was prized as a marker of moral refinement. The novel deploys the term in two distinct but related [[reason|sense]]s.

First, sensibility is a **[[virtue|moral virtue]]**: the quality that enables [[charity|compassion]] and sympathetic identification with the [[distress|suffering]] of others. The narrator appeals directly to "THE reader of sensibility" (032-001), assuming a community of feeling readers who will be "astonished" by the hard-heartedness of [[reputation|character]]s like [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]]. When [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] at last reunites with his [[family]] after the death of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], the narrator declares that "Every heart of sensibility can easily conceive their feelings" (035-004) — a universalising appeal that positions sensibility as the normative human response to scenes of joy and [[sorrow|grief]] alike. In its most didactic moment, the novel calls upon "the passing daughter of youthful mirth" to pause over the grave of the [[ruin|fall]]en woman: "has she a heart of sensibility, she will stop" and extend [[charity|compassion]] rather than censure (018-071).

Second, and critically, sensibility is a **dangerous [[weakness|vulnerability]]**. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]] is undone not by callousness or vice but by an excess of the very quality that makes her morally admirable. The narrator describes her as "the hapless girl who [[ruin|fall]]s a victim to her too great sensibility" (028-024), while [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] herself reflects that her "[[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" — however powerful a force — "never would have conquered my [[love|affection]] to you, my [[love|beloved]] parents" had she not been deceived by those she [[trust]]ed (022-026). The novel's treatment of sensibility thus captures an essential tension in sentimental fiction: the same emotional openness that enables moral feeling also disarms the defences necessary to p[[modesty|reserve]] virtue.

The novel also frames the *absence* of sensibility as a marker of moral corruption. The narrator warns against those who "blame a sensibility they never felt," countering that "the heart that is truly [[virtue|virtuous]] is ever inclined to [[compassion|pity]] and forgive the errors of its fellow-creatures" (020-124). Hardness of heart — the inability to feel — is consistently associated with the novel's villains, while true virtue and true sensibility are presented as inseparable.

## Key Passages

> "a heart bursting with disappointed love and wounded sensibility, retire to a bed which remorse had strewed with thorns" (018-034)

In this passage, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sensibility is presented as "wounded" — her capacity for deep feeling has become a source of exquisite [[distress|suffering]] rather than moral elevation. The pairing of "disappointed [[affection|love]]" with "wounded sensibility" suggests that sensibility intensifies the pain of [[deceit|betrayal]], transforming what might be mere disappointment into a wound that penetrates to the moral core of the self.

> "has she a heart of sensibility, she will stop, and thus address the unhappy victim of folly" (018-071)

The narrator's direct address to the "daughter of youthful mirth" frames sensibility as the mechanism of moral response. To possess "a heart of sensibility" is to be incapable of passing by [[distress|suffering]] without extending [[charity|compassion]]. The passage operationalises sensibility as the impulse that converts witnessing into moral action.

> "Let prudes and fools censure if they dare, and blame a sensibility they never felt; I will exultingly tell them that the heart that is truly virtuous is ever inclined to pity and forgive the errors of its fellow-creatures." (020-124)

This passage, spoken in the context of [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s [[charity|compassion]]ate response to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], articulates the novel's central thesis on sensibility: that those who lack it are morally impoverished — "prudes and fools" — and that genuine virtue cannot exist without the capacity for sympathetic feeling. Sensibility is the emotional infrastructure of virtue.

> "yet powerful as that passion is when operating in a young heart glowing with sensibility, it never would have conquered my affection to you, my beloved parents" (022-026)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s retrospective self-analysis reveals the novel's nuanced treatment of sensibility. She acknowledges that her heart was "glowing with sensibility" — that she felt deeply and intensely — but insists that even this force would not have overcome her [[duty|filial duty]] without the corrupting influence of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]. Sensibility is not inherently destructive; it becomes so only when manipulated by those without moral principle.

> "while the hapless girl who falls a victim to her too great sensibility, shall be loaded with ignominy and shame" (028-024)

The narrator's explicit acknowledgement that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "too great sensibility" made her a "victim" captures the tragic dimension of the concept. Sensibility in excess — ungoverned by [[reason|prudence]], unfortified by religious principle — becomes not a virtue but a liability, a point of entry through which seduction can operate. The word "victim" is crucial: it positions [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as someone acted upon, whose very capacity for feeling has been weaponised against her.

> "THE reader of sensibility may perhaps be astonished to find Mrs. Crayton could so positively deny any knowledge of Charlotte" (032-001)

The narrator's direct address to "THE reader of sensibility" constructs an implied audience defined by their capacity for refined feeling. The astonishment the narrator anticipates is itself a marker of sensibility: only a reader who feels correctly would be shocked by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]]'s hard-hearted denial. The passage demonstrates how Rowson uses the concept to build a moral community between narrator and reader.

> "Every heart of sensibility can easily conceive their feelings" (035-004)

This closing appeal to the universal heart of sensibility — applied to the reunion of [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], his wife, and her aged father after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death — frames sensibility as the shared human faculty that makes narrative itself morally effective. If the reader can "conceive" the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' mingled [[sorrow|grief]] and joy, it is because sensibility bridges the distance between fictional representation and lived [[experience]].

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- **[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]**: The novel's central embodiment of sensibility in both its aspects. Her "[[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-026) makes her capable of deep [[affection|love]], genuine [[regret|remorse]], and moral aspiration — but it also renders her vulnerable to [[montraville|Montraville]]'s appeals and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s manipulations. Her tragedy is that her finest quality is also the instrument of her [[ruin|undoing]]. She ends as "a victim to her too great sensibility" (028-024), her "wounded sensibility" (018-034) finding no [[comfort|relief]] but in death.
- **[[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]**: Represents sensibility properly governed — a "heart open to every generous feeling of humanity" (002-014) that expresses itself through benevolent action rather than passive victimhood. His tears at [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]]'s story and his impulse to relieve [[distress|suffering]] demonstrate sensibility as active moral agency. His example shows that sensibility, when allied with wisdom and principle, is an unambiguous good.
- **[[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]**: The novel's most striking il[[passion|lust]]ration of sensibility's absence. Having "stifled the [[reason|sense]] of shame in her own bosom" (007-093), she possesses no moral feeling whatsoever and exploits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sensibility as a tool of seduction. Her [[reputation|character]] demonstrates that the loss of sensibility — the capacity to feel for others — is the essence of moral corruption.

- **[[montraville|Montraville]]**: Embodies sensibility in its most dangerous male form — capable of feeling, and therefore capable of inspiring genuine [[love|affection]] in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], but too weak in principle to govern his [[passion|passions]]. His [[regret|remorse]] after abandoning [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], his ravings during illness, and his final [[sorrow|grief]] demonstrate that he possesses sensibility without the moral strength to act upon it. His "insensibility" (034-061) after the duel with [[belcour|Belcour]] literalises the collapse of feeling that follows moral catastrophe.
- **[[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]**: Functions as sensibility's moral ideal — her [[charity|compassion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], even after the latter's [[ruin|fall]], enacts the novel's argument that "the heart that is truly [[virtue|virtuous]] is ever inclined to [[compassion|pity]] and forgive" (020-124). She demonstrates that true sensibility produces action, not merely feeling.

## Narrative Function

Sensibility serves as the emotional engine of Rowson's didactic project. The novel's stated purpose — to warn "the [[youth|young]] and thoughtless of the fair sex" — depends on the reader's capacity for sympathetic identification, which is precisely what sensibility enables. Rowson does not merely argue her moral case; she makes the reader *feel* it, using sensibility as the bridge between fictional [[distress|suffering]] and real moral transformation.

The concept operates on three levels. As an **ideal**, sensibility is the mark of the [[virtue|virtuous]] heart — the capacity to feel for others that distinguishes the good (the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s, [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]) from the corrupt ([[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]]). As a **warning**, sensibility ungoverned by [[reason|prudence]] and religion is shown to be the pathway to ruin — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "too great sensibility" (028-024) is the tragic flaw that enables her seduction. And as a **rhetorical strategy**, the narrator's direct appeals to "the reader of sensibility" (032-001) construct an implied audience defined by refined feeling, positioning the act of reading itself as a test of moral capacity.

This ambivalence — sensibility as both virtue and [[weakness|vulnerability]] — distinguishes Rowson's treatment from simpler celebrations of feeling. The novel does not repudiate sensibility; it insists upon its necessity while dramatising its dangers. In this, *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* participates in the broader eighteenth-century debate about the moral status of feeling, offering a nuanced position: sensibility is essential to virtue, but it must be fortified by religion, reason, and parental guidance if it is not to become the means of its possessor's [[ruin|destruction]].

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Sensibility is the emotional foundation upon which virtue rests; "the heart that is truly [[virtue|virtuous]] is ever inclined to [[compassion|pity]]" (020-124). Without sensibility, virtue becomes mere rule-following; without virtue, sensibility becomes mere emotional indulgence
- [[innocence]]: The state in which sensibility is most acute and most vulnerable; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "[[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-026) is also a heart still innocent of the world's corruptions
- [[seduction]]: The process that exploits sensibility as its point of entry; seduction works by manipulating the very capacity for feeling that makes a [[youth|young]] woman morally admirable
- [[folly]]: The failure of [[reason|judgment]] that allows sensibility to operate ungoverned; "too great sensibility" (028-024) is sensibility untethered from [[reason|prudence]]
- [[compassion]]: The active expression of sensibility; the movement from feeling to action that distinguishes genuine moral response from passive sentiment
- [[love]]: The [[love|passion]] that, operating in a heart "glowing with sensibility" (022-026), becomes overwhelmingly powerful — capable of inspiring both the highest devotion and the most catastrophic mis[[reason|judgment]]
