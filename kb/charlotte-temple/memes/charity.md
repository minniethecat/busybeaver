Charity in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is a virtue tested to breaking point. The novel stages [[benevolence|charity]]'s failure as one of its most devastating scenes: when [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], destitute and desperate, begs a landlady for shelter and is met with the brutal retort "[[benevolence|charity]] begins at home" ([029-063]), Rowson exposes the gap between Christian precept and social practice. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s pleas — "for [[benevolence|charity]]'s sweet sake" ([030-030], [031-043]) — echo through the novel's closing chapters as an indictment not only of individual hard-heartedness but of a society that preaches [[benevolence|charity]] while practising indifference.

## Definition

Rowson presents [[benevolence|charity]] as an active rather than abstract virtue — it requires doing, not merely [[sensibility|feeling]]. The landlady's rejection of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] weaponizes a proverb ("[[benevolence|charity]] begins at home") to justify selfishness, turning the language of virtue into a shield for cruelty. Against this failure, Rowson offers [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s [[benevolence|charity]] as the counter-example: her visits, her gifts of food and wine, her presence at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] model [[benevolence|charity]] as the active expression of Christian compassion. Charity in the novel is thus defined negatively — by its absence — and positively — by its rare but transformative presence.

## Key Passages

> "Let me hope, as you see my unhappy situation, your charity—" Charlotte's plea to the landlady ([029-061])

> "Charity indeed: why, Mistress, charity begins at home, and I have seven children at home" — the landlady's cruel rejection ([029-063])

> "Only for charity's sweet sake receive me into your hospitable mansion" — Charlotte's plea ([030-030])

> "If you really have forgot me, oh for charity's sweet sake this night let me be sheltered from the winter's piercing cold" ([031-043])

> "Poor soul," said he, "you shall not lay in the street this night. I have a bed and a poor little hovel, where my wife and her..." — the benevolent servant's charity, the novel's counter-example to the landlady ([031-066])

> "When Mrs. Beauchamp entered the apartment of the poor sufferer, she started back with horror" — the beginning of Mrs. Beauchamp's charitable mission to Charlotte's sickroom ([033-001])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Charity's supplicant. Her degradation from gentlewoman to street beggar is measured by her dependence on the [[benevolence|charity]] of strangers — a dependence that is too often disappointed.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Charity's embodiment. Her visits to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sickroom, her practical gifts of food and wine, and her sustained attention to a fallen stranger represent the novel's ideal of active Christian compassion. Her "benevolent heart" ([033-019]) is the counterweight to the novel's many failures of charity.
- The landlady: Charity's antithesis. Her invocation of "[[benevolence|charity]] begins at home" as an excuse for cruelty represents the perversion of Christian language to justify indifference.
- The servant: Charity from below. His offer of shelter — "I have a bed and a poor little hovel" ([031-066]) — is the novel's most powerful example of charity, precisely because it comes from someone with almost nothing to give.

## Narrative Function

Charity is the virtue whose absence drives the novel's tragic conclusion and whose presence makes that conclusion bearable. Rowson stages [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s encounters with [[benevolence|charity]] — both its denial and its granting — to critique a society that allows the [[ruin|fall]]en to perish in the streets while congratulating itself on its Christian principles. The novel's didactic aim is clear: [[benevolence|charity]] is not a [[sensibility|sentiment]] but an obligation, and those who refuse it are complicit in the [[distress|suffering]] they decline to relieve. The structural irony is sharp: the landlady who refuses [[benevolence|charity]] has "seven children at home" ([029-063]) whom she claims as the reason for her hardness, while the servant who offers it has nothing but "a poor little hovel" ([031-066]).

## Related Concepts

- [[compassion]]: Charity's emotional foundation. [[compassion|Compassion]] is the [[sensibility|feeling]]; [[benevolence|charity]] is the action that flows from it.
- [[kindness]]: Charity's gentler expression. While [[benevolence|charity]] addresses material need, kindness addresses emotional need.
- [[poverty]]: The condition that [[benevolence|charity]] exists to relieve. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s poverty is the test that society's [[benevolence|charity]] fails.
- [[religion]]: Charity's theological frame. The novel's Christian vocabulary — "[[benevolence|charity]]'s sweet sake" — positions the virtue within a religious obligation that the landlady hypocritically invokes.
