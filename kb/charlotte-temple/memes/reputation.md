Reputation, in the moral world of Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is the public dimension of private virtue. For the novel's female characters, reputation is a social currency that determines their standing, their prospects, and ultimately their survival. Rowson presents reputation as both a legitimate moral concern and a source of hypocrisy — a theme she explores through characters who guard their own reputations while indifferent to the ruin of others.

## Definition

Rowson defines reputation as "the basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the heart of woman" rests (007-095). The phrase yokes reputation to honour and casts both as foundational to female identity. The novel distinguishes between genuine reputation, grounded in actual virtue, and the mere appearance of it, which characters like [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]] anxiously protect even while acting viciously. When [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] protests "But I hazard my dear reputation," Rowson exposes the absurdity of a woman who has facilitated seduction worrying about her own good name (011-073). The social world of the novel is one where people are "splendidly miserable" (002-007) — outwardly prosperous but inwardly hollow — and reputation is the currency of that world, valued more than genuine [[virtue|goodness]]. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s recommendation of [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] as "so worthy a character" (004-070) stands in contrast, showing that true reputation is earned through virtuous action, not mere appearance.

## Key Passages

> "Remember, my dear friend, that reputation is the basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the heart of woman, depends." (007-095)

> "But I hazard my dear reputation," replied Mademoiselle, bridling. (011-073)

> "so worthy a character" (004-070)

The phrase describes [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] as a man of genuine worth — an example of reputation grounded in real virtue rather than social performance.

> "splendidly miserable" (002-007)

Rowson's phrase for those who maintain the outward show of reputation while inwardly suffering — a critique of a social order that prizes appearance over substance.

> "Think of having her own reputation endangered by encouraging a woman of this description to remain in her family." (031-051)

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central case study in the [[ruin|destruction]] of reputation. Her elopement with [[montraville|Montraville]] instantly transforms her from a [[youth|young]] woman of good [[family]] to a social outcast, and the novel tracks every stage of that [[ruin|fall]] (007-095).
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: A hypocrite who lectures [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] on reputation while actively destroying it. Her concern with her own "dear reputation" (011-073) is the novel's sharpest satire of social hypocrisy.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: The character who risks her own reputation by extending kindness to the [[ruin|ruined]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. Her neighbour warns her of "having her own reputation endangered" (031-051), making her [[charity|compassion]] a test of moral courage.
- [[belcour]]: A male counterpoint — he enjoys the privileges of a reputation he does not deserve, illustrating the gender asymmetry Rowson critiques.
- [[captain-eldridge]]: A man whose reputation was unjustly destroyed by a false friend, yet whose "worthy character" (004-070) is ultimately vindicated — the novel's counterexample to the idea that reputation and virtue always align.

## Narrative Function

Reputation functions in the novel as a trap. It is what [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] uses to manipulate [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — persuading her that eloping will ruin her reputation and therefore she cannot go back — and it is what society uses to police female behaviour. Rowson critiques a world in which a [[seduction|seduce]]d woman loses her reputation while her [[seduction|seducer]] loses nothing, using the double standard as her sharpest social argument. The novel's pathos arises from the reader's knowledge that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s reputation, once lost, cannot be regained, no matter how much she [[suffering|suffer]]s. Yet the novel also exposes reputation's hollowness — characters who are "splendidly miserable" (002-007) maintain the appearance of respectability at the cost of genuine happiness.

## Related Concepts

- [[honour]]: Reputation and honour are explicitly linked in Rowson's formulation; honour is the internal quality, reputation its external recognition (007-095).
- [[ruin]]: The loss of reputation is synonymous with ruin in the novel's moral vocabulary — a woman without reputation is a "[[ruin|ruined]]" woman.
- [[folly]]: Rowson frames the loss of reputation as the [[nature|natural]] consequence of folly, warning [[youth|young]] women that imprudent actions carry irreversible social penalties.
