# Compassion

The [[sensibility|sentiment]]al capacity to feel genuine [[grief|sorrow]] for the [[distress|suffering]] of another and to be moved toward acts of kindness — in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, the emotional virtue that defines the novel's moral community, distinguishes the worthy from the merciless, and models the reader's own affective response to the [[ruin|fall]]en heroine.

## Definition

In Rowson's moral framework, [[charity|compassion]] is neither abstract [[charity|benevolence]] nor weak indulgence — it is an active emotional virtue, grounded in the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al ideal of sympathetic identification with another's distress. The narrator explicitly frames the novel itself as an act of [[charity|compassion]], opening with a metacritical gesture: "While the tear of [[charity|compassion]] still trembled in my eye for the fate of the un[[happiness|happy]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], I may have children of my own" (P01-017). This establishes [[charity|compassion]] as both the motive for telling [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story and the affective posture the reader is meant to adopt.

Compassion in the novel operates as a moral test: [[reputation|character]]s are judged by their capacity to extend it or their failure to do so. "The merciless world has barred the doors of [[charity|compassion]] against a poor weak girl" (020-072) — the indictment is not of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] but of a society that withholds [[charity|compassion]]ate recognition from the [[ruin|fall]]en. Conversely, the narrator promises that "the tear of [[charity|compassion]] shall [[ruin|fall]] for the fate of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]" (028-034), affirming that true moral community extends [[charity|compassion]] even to the erring.

## Key Passages

> "While the tear of compassion still trembled in my eye for the fate of the unhappy Charlotte, I may have children of my own" (P01-017)

> "What a pity! said Mrs. Beauchamp softly, (casting a most compassionate glance at her.) But surely her mind is not depraved" (017-069)

> "Yes, my young friends, the tear of compassion shall fall for the fate of Charlotte, while the name of La Rue shall be detested and despised" (028-034)

> "Extend your compassion to my helpless offspring, should it please heaven that it should survive its unhappy mother" (030-036)

> "Bless you, my sweet lady, said the soldier with a voice and look of compassion" (030-075)

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: The novel's primary exemplar of active [[charity|compassion]]. Upon learning [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation, she casts "a most [[charity|compassion]]ate glance" (017-069) and becomes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sole benefactor, providing "every tender attention which [[charity|compassion]] and [[friendship|friends]]hip could dictate" (030-050).
- [[charlotte-temple]]: The object of the reader's [[charity|compassion]], but also one who extends it. In her [[death|dying]] moments she entreats others to "extend your [[charity|compassion]] to my helpless offspring" (030-036), transforming from passive recipient into an agent of [[charity|compassion]]ate appeal.
- [[mr-temple]]: Represents the [[charity|compassion]] of parental [[affection|love]]. As he imagines his daughter "without the benignant look of [[charity|compassion]] to cheer, or the angelic voice of pity to pour the balm of [[comfort|consolation]]" (025-008), the father's [[charity|compassion]] becomes a measure of all that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has lost.
- the world: An abstract antagonist — "the merciless world" (020-072) that "bars the doors of [[charity|compassion]]" (020-072) against [[ruin|fall]]en women. The novel's critique is that society's refusal of [[charity|compassion]] perpetuates the very ruin it condemns.

## Narrative Function

Compassion is the affective engine of the novel's didactic project. Rowson uses [[charity|compassion]] to structure the reader's moral education: we are taught to feel [[charity|compassion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (the erring but not depraved), to admire [[charity|compassion]] in [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] (the [[virtue|virtuous]] woman), and to recognize the absence of [[charity|compassion]] in the merciless world as a social sin. The novel's famous direct addresses to "my [[youth|young]] [[friendship|friends]]" (028-034) are invitations to a shared [[charity|compassion]]ate posture. Compassion bridges the gap between moral warning and emotional identification — it makes the [[prudence|caution]]ary tale bearable and pedagogically effective by ensuring that [[reason|judgment]] is always tempered with sympathetic understanding.

## Related Concepts

- [[sensibility]]: Compassion is sensibility directed outward — the capacity to feel for another's [[distress|suffering]], not merely one's own
- [[virtue]]: Compassion is both a component of virtue and its practical expression in social conduct
- [[repentance]]: True repentance makes one a worthy object of [[charity|compassion]]; [[charity|compassion]], in turn, makes repentance socially meaningful
- [[friendship]]: Embodied in the [[mrs-beauchamp|Beauchamp]]-[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] relationship, where [[charity|compassion]] is the active content of [[friendship|friends]]hip
- [[duty]]: The duty to extend [[charity|compassion]] to the [[ruin|fall]]en is an implicit [[duty|moral obligation]] the novel urges upon its readers
