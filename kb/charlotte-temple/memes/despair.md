Despair, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is the emotional abyss at the far end of [[distress|suffering]] — the point at which hope dies and the soul confronts its own annihilation. Rowson uses despair sparingly but strategically, reserving it for the novel's most consequential moments: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents' loss of hope for their daughter's return, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s nightmare vision of filicide, and the terror of abandonment. Despair is the novel's limit case, the state beyond which only death or divine intervention can reach.

## Definition

Rowson's despair is the extinction of hope. When "hope was no more; despair usurped her place" in the [[mr-temple|Temple]] [[family|household]] ([015-047]), the language of usurpation signals that despair is an illegitimate sovereign — a state that displaces the proper order of the soul. Despair in the novel is both psychological and theological: it is the state of believing oneself beyond [[mercy|forgiveness]], beyond recovery, beyond [[affection|love]]. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s nightmare cry — "[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], thou hast murdered me!" followed by "Horror and despair" ([022-063]) — dramatizes despair as the ultimate self-accusation. Yet Rowson also shows despair as conquerable: [[belcour|Belcour]]'s cruel abandonment finds [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] "almost distracted with terror and despair" ([024-019]), but she is not utterly destroyed — her father's arrival restores the hope that despair had extinguished.

## Key Passages

> "At length hope was no more; despair usurped her place; and the mansion which was once the mansion of peace, became the habitation of pale, dejected melancholy" ([015-047])

> "I see my dear mother and my revered grand-father. I strive to raise you; you push me from you, and shrieking cry — 'Charlotte, thou hast murdered me!' Horror and despair tear every tortured nerve" ([022-063])

> "When Montraville spurned the weeping Charlotte from him, and left her almost distracted with terror and despair" ([024-019])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Despair as lived nightmare. Her dream of murdering her mother ([022-063]) externalizes the self-accusation at despair's core. Her despair is not permanent — [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s kindness and her father's arrival offer rescue — but it marks the novel's emotional nadir.
- [[mr-temple]]: The counter-force to despair. His refusal to abandon hope for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], even when "hope was no more" ([015-047]), makes him the novel's agent of restoration.
- [[mrs-temple]]: Her descent into "pale, dejected melancholy" ([015-048]) after hope's failure models despair as a domestic tragedy — the [[happiness|happy]] home transformed into a house of [[grief|sorrow]].

## Narrative Function

Despair is the novel's emotional floor — the lowest point from which recovery becomes imaginable. Rowson deploys despair at the exact centre of the novel's tragic arc: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents' despair for their lost daughter (Chapter 15), [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s own despair in her [[new-york|New York]] isolation (Chapters 18–24). By staging despair as conquerable — through [[friendship|friends]]hip, through parental [[affection|love]], through Christian hope — Rowson converts what could be mere tragedy into moral [[education|instruction]]. The novel's lesson is not that despair is inescapable but that it is the [[nature|natural]] terminus of sin, and that its only remedy lies in the bonds of [[affection|love]] and [[mercy|forgiveness]].

## Related Concepts

- [[grief]]: The [[grief|sorrow]] that, left unchecked and unconsoled, hardens into despair. [[grief|Grief]] is active [[grief|mourning]]; despair is [[sorrow|grief]] that has abandoned hope.
- [[melancholy]]: Despair's chronic form — the "pale, dejected melancholy" that settles in after acute despair subsides ([015-048]).
- [[hope]]: Despair's opposite and remedy. [[hope|Hope]] structures the novel's forward movement; despair is its suspension.
- [[suffering]]: The broader condition encompassing despair. Despair is [[distress|suffering]] at its most existentially threatening.
