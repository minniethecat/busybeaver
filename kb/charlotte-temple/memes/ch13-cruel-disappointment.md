[013-001] “WHAT pleasure,” cried [[captain-eldridge|Mr. Eldridge]], as he stepped into the chaise to go

[013-002] for his grand-daughter, “what pleasure expands the heart of an old

[013-003] man when he beholds the progeny of a [[love|beloved]] child growing up in every

[013-004] virtue that adorned the minds of her parents. I foolishly thought, some

[013-005] few years since, that every [[reason|sense]] of joy was buried in the graves of my

[013-006] dear partner and my son; but my [[lucy|Lucy]], by her [[filial-piety|filial affection]], soothed

[013-007] my soul to peace, and this dear [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has twined herself round my

[013-008] heart, and opened such new scenes of delight to my view, that I almost

[013-009] forget I have ever been unhappy.”

[013-010] When the chaise stopped, he alighted with the alacrity of youth; so much

[013-011] do the emotions of the soul influence the body.

[013-012] It was half past eight o'clock; the ladies were assembled in the school

[013-013] room, and [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]] was preparing to offer the morning sacrifice

[013-014] of prayer and praise, when it was discovered, that Mademoiselle and

[013-015] Charlotte were missing.

[013-016] “She is busy, no doubt,” said the governess, “in preparing Charlotte for

[013-017] her little excursion; but pleasure should never make us forget our duty

[013-018] to our Creator. Go, one of you, and bid them both attend prayers.”

[013-019] The lady who went to summon them, soon returned, and informed

[013-020] the governess, that the room was locked, and that she had knocked

[013-021] repeatedly, but obtained no answer.

[013-022] “Good heaven!” cried Madame Du Pont, “this is very strange:” and turning

[013-023] pale with terror, she went hastily to the door, and ordered it to be

[013-024] forced open. The apartment instantly discovered, that no person had been

[013-025] in it the preceding night, the beds appearing as though just made.

[013-026] The house was instantly a scene of confusion: the garden, the pleasure

[013-027] grounds were searched to no purpose, every apartment rang with the names

[013-028] of Miss [[mr-temple|Temple]] and Mademoiselle; but they were too distant to hear; and

[013-029] every face wore the marks of disappointment.

[013-030] Mr. Eldridge was sitting in the parlour, eagerly expecting his

[013-031] grand-daughter to descend, ready equipped for her journey: he heard

[013-032] the confusion that reigned in the house; he heard the name of Charlotte

[013-033] frequently repeated. “What can be the matter?” said he, rising and

[013-034] opening the door: “I fear some accident has befallen my dear girl.”

[013-035] The governess entered. The visible agitation of her countenance

[013-036] discovered that something extraordinary had happened.

[013-037] “Where is Charlotte?” said he, “Why does not my child come to welcome

[013-038] her doating parent?”

[013-039] “Be composed, my dear Sir,” said Madame Du Pont, “do not frighten

[013-040] yourself unnecessarily. She is not in the house at present; but as

[013-041] Mademoiselle is undoubtedly with her, she will speedily return

[013-042] in safety; and I hope they will both be able to account for this

[013-043] unseasonable absence in such a manner as shall remove our present

[013-044] uneasiness.”

[013-045] “Madam,” cried the old man, with an angry look, “has my child been

[013-046] accustomed to go out without leave, with no other company or protector

[013-047] than that French woman. Pardon me, Madam, I mean no reflections on your

[013-048] country, but I never did like [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]; I think she was a

[013-049] very improper person to be [[trust|entrusted]] with the care of such a girl

[013-050] as Charlotte Temple, or to be suffered to take her from under your

[013-051] immediate protection.”

[013-052] “You wrong me, Mr. Eldridge,” replied she, “if you suppose I have ever

[013-053] permitted your grand-daughter to go out unless with the other ladies.

[013-054] I would to heaven I could form any probable conjecture concerning her

[013-055] absence this morning, but it is a mystery which her return can alone

[013-056] unravel.” Servants were now dispatched to every place where there was

[013-057] the least hope of hearing any tidings of the fugitives, but in vain.

[013-058] Dreadful were the hours of horrid suspense which Mr. Eldridge passed

[013-059] till twelve o'clock, when that suspense was reduced to a shocking

[013-060] certainty, and every spark of hope which till then they had indulged,

[013-061] was in a moment extinguished.

[013-062] Mr. Eldridge was preparing, with a heavy heart, to return to his

[013-063] anxiously-expecting children, when Madame Du Pont received the following

[013-064] note without either name or date.

[013-065] “Miss Temple is well, and wishes to relieve the [[fear|anxiety]] of her

[013-066] parents, by letting them know she has voluntarily put herself under

[013-067] the protection of a man whose future study shall be to make her [[happiness|happy]].

[013-068] Pursuit is needless; the measures taken to avoid discovery are too

[013-069] effectual to be eluded. When she thinks her [[friendship|friends]] are reconciled to

[013-070] this precipitate step, they may perhaps be informed of her place of

[013-071] residence. Mademoiselle is with her.”

## Section 8

[013-072] As Madame Du Pont read these cruel lines, she turned pale as ashes, her

[013-073] limbs trembled, and she was forced to call for a glass of water. She

[013-074] loved Charlotte truly; and when she reflected on the innocence and

[013-075] [[kindness|gentleness]] of her disposition, she concluded that it must have been

[013-076] the advice and machinations of La Rue, which led her to this imprudent

[013-077] action; she recollected her agitation at the receipt of her mother's

[013-078] letter, and saw in it the conflict of her mind.

[013-079] “Does that letter relate to Charlotte?” said Mr. Eldridge, having waited

[013-080] some time in expectation of Madame Du Pont's speaking.

[013-081] “It does,” said she. “Charlotte is well, but cannot return today.”

[013-082] “Not return, Madam? where is she? who will detain her from her fond,

[013-083] expecting parents?”

[013-084] “You distract me with these questions, Mr. Eldridge. Indeed I know not

[013-085] where she is, or who has seduced her from her duty.”

[013-086] The whole truth now rushed at once upon Mr. Eldridge's mind. “She has

[013-087] eloped then,” said he. “My child is betrayed; the darling, the comfort

[013-088] of my aged heart, is lost. Oh would to heaven I had died but yesterday.”

[013-089] A violent gush of [[sorrow|grief]] in some measure relieved him, and, after several

[013-090] vain attempts, he at length assumed sufficient composure to read the

[013-091] note.

[013-092] “And how shall I return to my children?” said he: “how approach that

[013-093] mansion, so late the habitation of peace? Alas! my dear Lucy, how will

[013-094] you support these heart-rending tidings? or how shall I be enabled to

[013-095] console you, who need so much [[comfort|consolation]] myself?”

[013-096] The old man returned to the chaise, but the light step and cheerful

[013-097] countenance were no more; [[melancholy|sorrow]] filled his heart, and guided his

[013-098] motions; he seated himself in the chaise, his venerable head reclined

[013-099] upon his bosom, his hands were folded, his eye fixed on vacancy, and

[013-100] the large drops of [[grief|sorrow]] rolled silently down his cheeks. There was a

[013-101] mixture of [[distress|anguish]] and resignation depicted in his countenance, as if he

[013-102] would say, henceforth who shall dare to boast his happiness, or even

[013-103] in idea contemplate his treasure, lest, in the very moment his heart is

[013-104] exulting in its own felicity, the object which constitutes that felicity

[013-105] should be torn from him.
