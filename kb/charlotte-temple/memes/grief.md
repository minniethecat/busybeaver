Grief in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the emotional register of irrevocable loss. Unlike the inward-turning [[distress|anguish]] of [[regret|remorse]], [[sorrow|grief]] reaches outward — it is the [[distress|suffering]] of those left behind, the parents who lose a daughter, the daughter who loses her [[family]]. Rowson maps [[sorrow|grief]] across the novel's entire cast, creating a symphony of sorrow in which each [[reputation|character]]'s [[sorrow|grief]] illuminates a different dimension of the seduction tragedy: parental [[sorrow|grief]], filial [[sorrow|grief]], romantic [[sorrow|grief]], and the [[sorrow|grief]] of a community that has lost one of its own.

## Definition

Rowson treats [[sorrow|grief]] as the universal solvent of [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction — the emotion that binds reader to [[reputation|character]] through shared [[weakness|vulnerability]] to loss. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s "violent gush of [[sorrow|grief]]" ([013-089]) upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement establishes the novel's template: [[sorrow|grief]] is physical, involuntary, and morally purifying. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s lament that she has "no [[love|beloved]] mother" to whom she can "unbosom her [[sorrow|grief]]s" ([018-011]) reveals [[sorrow|grief]]'s social dimension — it requires a witness, a comforter. For Rowson, [[sorrow|grief]] unshared is [[sorrow|grief]] doubled; [[sorrow|grief]] witnessed is [[sorrow|grief]] that instructs.

## Key Passages

> "A violent gush of grief in some measure relieved him, and, after several vain attempts, he at length assumed sufficient composure to read the fatal epistle" ([013-089])

> "No kind friend of her own sex to whom she can unbosom her griefs, no beloved mother, no woman of character will appear in my company" ([018-011])

> "She threw herself on the bed, and gave vent to an agony of grief which it is impossible to describe" ([024-029])

> "A prey to sickness, grief, and penury" — the narrator's summary of Charlotte's condition ([027-125])

> "After the first tumult of grief was subsided, Mrs. Temple gave up the chief of her time to her grand-child" ([035-005])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Grief as lived [[experience]]. Her [[sorrow|grief]] is compound — [[sorrow|grief]] for her lost [[family]], for her lost reputation, for her lost future — and finds no adequate outlet until her [[death|dying]] reunion with her father.
- [[mr-temple]]: The novel's archetype of parental [[sorrow|grief]]. His "violent gush of [[sorrow|grief]]" ([013-089]) upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement is the novel's most visceral depiction of a father's [[affection|love]] transmuted into sorrow.
- [[mrs-temple]]: Grief that transforms into renewed purpose. After "the first tumult of [[sorrow|grief]] was subsided," she devotes herself to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s child, transmuting sorrow into care ([035-005]).
- [[montraville]]: Grief as belated recognition. His [[sorrow|grief]] over [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death belongs to the novel's closing moral reckoning.

## Narrative Function

Grief is the novel's primary affective engine. Rowson stages [[sorrow|grief]] not merely as a consequence of plot events but as the medium through which those events acquire moral significance. The reader is meant to grieve with the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s, to feel the loss of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as a loss of their own. Grief in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* is thus didactic: it teaches by making the reader feel the cost of seduction in the most intimate terms. The novel's closing tableau — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] dead, her parents grieving, her child orphaned — converts [[sorrow|grief]] into warning.

## Related Concepts

- [[suffering]]: The broader category encompassing [[sorrow|grief]]. Grief is emotional [[distress|suffering]], distinct from physical [[distress|suffering]] but often compounding it.
- [[melancholy]]: Grief's chronic form — the settled, ongoing sorrow that replaces acute [[distress|anguish]].
- [[despair]]: Grief's extreme terminus — the point at which sorrow extinguishes hope.
- [[family]]: The primary theatre of [[sorrow|grief]] in the novel. Grief is what binds the [[mr-temple|Temple]] [[family]] even after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]].
