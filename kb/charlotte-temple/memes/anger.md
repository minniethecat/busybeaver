# Anger

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, anger occupies a carefully delineated moral position. Rowson distinguishes between the righteous anger of a parent whose child has transgressed, the performative anger of the [[seduction|seducer]] manipulating his victim, and the petty angers of social rivalry. The novel treats anger as a legitimate response to moral wrong — but only when tempered by [[charity|compassion]] and directed toward restoration rather than [[ruin|destruction]].

## Definition

Rowson does not condemn anger outright. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s anger at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement is presented as proportionate and morally justified — the [[nature|natural]] response of a father whose [[trust]] has been betrayed. But anger becomes destructive when it is untethered from [[affection|love]]: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s irritation with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s envy, and the "little bickerings of anger" ([008-080]) that [[reputation|character]]ize school life. The narrator's moral framework holds that anger should serve virtue — defending the innocent, correcting the errant — not gratify the self.

## Key Passages

> "…affecting anger: 'I invited you to go in hope…'" ([007-011])

[[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s "affected" (feigned) anger at the tea-party incident is a tool of manipulation — anger performed rather than felt, used to control [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s perceptions.

> "…the little bickerings of anger, envy, and pride" ([008-080])

The narrator dismisses schoolgirl squabbles as trivial, linking anger with envy and pride as emotions that distract from true moral education.

> "Charlotte feared the anger of her governess" ([009-009])

Anger here functions as moral authority — the governess's anger is a proxy for social [[reason|judgment]], which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] instinctively [[fear|fears]] because she knows she has transgressed.

> "…incurred their anger" ([012-061])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s recognition that she has provoked her parents' righteous anger — a moment of moral clarity amid her confusion.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: [[fear|Fear]]s anger more than she feels it — her [[fear|dread]] of parental and social anger drives much of her concealment ([009-009])
- [[montraville]]: His anger at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation is self-serving — irritation at inconvenience rather than moral indignation at wrongdoing
- [[mr-temple]]: Embodies righteous paternal anger — his response to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]], though severe, is grounded in wounded [[affection|love]] ([012-061])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Uses performed anger as a tool of manipulation; her "affecting anger" ([007-011]) is counterfeit emotion in service of seduction
- [[belcour]]: His anger at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is cruel and dismissive — anger without [[charity|compassion]], serving only to justify his own mistreatment of her

## Narrative Function

Anger in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* serves primarily as a diagnostic emotion — how a [[reputation|character]] expresses anger reveals their moral [[reputation|character]]. Righteous anger ([[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s) seeks restoration; manipulative anger ([[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s) seeks control; selfish anger ([[montraville|Montraville]]'s) seeks escape from responsibility. The novel's didactic project treats anger as a secondary emotion that must be governed by the primary virtues of [[charity|compassion]] and duty. Unchecked anger — [[belcour|Belcour]]'s cruelty, [[montraville|Montraville]]'s irritation — accelerates the plot toward tragedy.

## Related Concepts

- [[revenge]]: Anger pursued beyond moral bounds becomes the [[passion|desire]] for revenge — a destructive force in the novel
- [[regret]]: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s anger gives way to regret only when it is too late — anger and regret are inversely related in the novel's moral economy
- [[guilt]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s guilt makes her fear anger; guilt transforms others' anger into an instrument of self-punishment
- [[cruelty]]: Anger without [[charity|compassion]] becomes cruelty — [[belcour|Belcour]]'s defining trait
- [[forgiveness]]: The novel's antidote to righteous anger; [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s [[mercy|forgiveness]] of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] resolves the anger that her transgression provoked
- [[virtue]]: [[virtue|Virtue]] moderates anger; the [[virtue|virtuous]] are slow to anger and quick to forgive
