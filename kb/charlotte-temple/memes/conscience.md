Conscience in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) functions as the internal moral monitor — the voice that accuses the guilty and warns the innocent. Rowson deploys conscience in two key registers: as the retrospective torment of the seducer [[montraville|Montraville]], and as the prospective safeguard of the reader. In both cases, conscience is the mechanism by which moral truth makes itself felt despite every effort to suppress it.

## Key Passages

> "Oh Charlotte, conscience tells me it was I, villain that I am, who first taught you the allurements of guilty pleasure" — Montraville ([026-060])

> "did conscience say, thus might it have been with me, had not Providence interposed to snatch me from destruction" — the narrator addressing the reader ([028-006])

## Narrative Function

Conscience is the novel's internalised moral authority. When [[montraville|Montraville]] cries out that "conscience tells me it was I," he enacts the novel's central didactic move: wrongdoing inevitably produces self-accusation. Conversely, when the narrator imagines the innocent reader hearing conscience say "thus might it have been with me," she transforms conscience from retrospective punishment into prospective protection — the moral equivalent of the "tale of truth" she claims to be writing. The [[innocence|innocent]] reader's conscience preserves her where [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s was overborne by [[temptation]].

## Related Concepts

- [[guilt]]: Conscience is guilt's voice — the internal mechanism that converts wrongdoing into [[distress|suffering]].
- [[temptation]]: Conscience and temptation are opposing forces; Charlotte falls when temptation overcomes conscience.
- [[innocence]]: The innocent still hear conscience; the fallen have ignored it.
