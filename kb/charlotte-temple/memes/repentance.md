# Repentance

Repentance is the moral mechanism through which Rowson's novel offers hope amid tragedy. While the worldly consequences of seduction are irreversible — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] loses her reputation, her [[family]], and ultimately her life — the novel insists that sincere repentance can restore the [[ruin|fall]]en woman to spiritual wholeness and divine favour. It is the bridge between ruin and redemption.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, repentance is defined not as mere regret or fear of consequences, but as "unfeigned repentance" (033-062) — a sincere, wholehearted turning from sin that engages both the emotions and the will. The novel distinguishes true repentance from its counterfeits. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]'s "[[regret|penitence]] which she professed" (006-014) is explicitly doubted: [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s predecessor "believed" it to be sincere, but the narration implies it was merely strategic — a performance to escape poverty. True repentance, by contrast, produces amendment of life, not merely protestations of [[grief|sorrow]].

The novel's most important theological statement about repentance occurs through Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]: "how nearly [[regret|penitence]] is allied to virtue" (025-045). This formulation is radically merciful within the seduction-novel tradition. It positions repentance not as the opposite of virtue but as its close relation — suggesting that the [[ruin|fall]]en woman who sincerely repents is not forever excluded from moral [[kindness|goodness]] but may be restored to something approaching it.

The narrator extends this argument in Chapter 28: "the tear of [[regret|penitence]] blots their offences from the book of fate, and they rise from the heavy, painful [[adversity|trial]], purified and fit for a mansion in the kingdom of eternity" (028-030). Here repentance is figured as both erasure and purification — it cancels the record of sin and transforms the sinner into something fit for heaven.

## Key Passages

> "tell her how nearly penitence is allied to virtue. Cannot we go and conduct her home, my love?" (025-045)

Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s words upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation articulate the novel's theology of redemption. That the [[virtue|virtuous]] mother — who has every reason to condemn her [[ruin|fall]]en daughter — instead speaks of [[regret|penitence]] as allied to virtue, and immediately seeks to "conduct her home," models the Christian response Rowson advocates: [[compassion|mercy]], restoration, and practical [[charity|compassion]].

> "the tear of penitence blots their offences from the book of fate, and they rise from the heavy, painful trial, purified and fit for a mansion in the kingdom of eternity" (028-030)

The narrator's most eloquent statement on the transformative power of repentance. The metaphor of "blotting" from the book of fate invokes both legal [[forgiveness|pardon]] and divine [[mercy|forgiveness]], while "purified" suggests that [[distress|suffering]] and [[regret|penitence]] together effect a genuine moral transformation, not merely a legal acquittal.

> "my sufferings in this state of mortality, joined to my unfeigned repentance, through his mercy, have blotted my offences from the sight of my offended maker" (033-062)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] confession enacts the theology the novel has been building. Her repentance is "unfeigned" — the word that distinguishes her sincerity from [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s performance — and she [[trust]]s in [[mercy|divine mercy]] rather than her own merit. The passage completes the novel's arc from warning to redemption.

> "Indeed I do repent," replied Charlotte, "from my soul: but while discretion points out the impropriety of my conduct, inclination urges me on to ruin." (011-067)

This early expression of repentance reveals its limits when unaccompanied by the will to act. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] repents in [[sensibility|feeling]] but not in deed — she knows her course is wrong but lacks the resolution to change it. The passage il[[passion|lust]]rates that repentance must be more than an emotion; it requires the active amendment of conduct.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's primary case study in repentance. Her trajectory moves from mere regret ("Indeed I do repent... from my soul" (011-067)) — which, unaccompanied by changed conduct, proves insufficient — to "unfeigned repentance" on her [[death|deathbed]] (033-062), which the novel presents as spiritually efficacious. Her death il[[passion|lust]]rates the novel's insistence that repentance can redeem the soul even when it cannot save the body.
- [[montraville]]: Embodies inadequate repentance. He feels [[regret|remorse]] after abandoning [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — "Poor girl," said he, "I will at least write and bid her adieu" — but his [[regret|penitence]] arrives too late to undo the harm he has caused. His incapacity to act on his [[regret|remorse]] contrasts with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s active, [[death|deathbed]] repentance and serves as a warning that belated [[sensibility|feeling]] without restitution is morally hollow.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Represents counterfeit repentance. Her "[[regret|penitence]] which she professed" (006-014) secured her position at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school but produced no amendment of life. She continues to act as an agent of seduction, demonstrating that professions of repentance without genuine moral change are not merely worthless but actively dangerous.

## Narrative Function

Repentance serves as the novel's theological resolution. The plot drives relentlessly toward [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|destruction]] — seduction, abandonment, poverty, childbirth, death — and without the concept of repentance, this trajectory would be merely punitive. Repentance transforms tragedy into redemption: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] dies, but she dies in a state of grace, reconciled to God and to her father.

This function is essential to Rowson's stated didactic purpose. The novel aims to warn [[youth|young]] women away from seduction, but it also addresses those who have already [[ruin|fall]]en. To the "[[youth|young]] and thoughtless of the fair sex" whom Rowson addresses in her Preface, repentance offers a path back — not to social rehabilitation, which the novel acknowledges is impossible, but to spiritual restoration, which the novel insists is always available.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: The quality to which [[regret|penitence]] is "nearly allied" (025-045); repentance is the mechanism by which virtue can be spiritually recovered after a [[ruin|fall]]
- [[innocence]]: The state that precedes the need for repentance; once lost through seduction, innocence cannot be restored, but repentance offers an alternative path to moral standing
- [[ruin]]: The worldly condition from which repentance offers spiritual escape; ruin is the "dark abyss" (012-044) that only repentance and [[mercy|divine mercy]] can transcend
- [[forgiveness]]: The human response to repentance modeled by Mr. and Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]], who receive their "[[ruin|ruined]], but repentant child" (033-082)
