[030-001] And what is friendship but a name,

[030-002] A charm that lulls to sleep,

[030-003] A shade that follows wealth and fame,

[030-004] But leaves the wretch to weep.

[030-005] WHEN [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] was left to herself, she began to think what course she

[030-006] must take, or to whom she could apply, to prevent her perishing for

[030-007] [[poverty|want]], or perhaps that very night falling a victim to the inclemency of

[030-008] the season. After many perplexed thoughts, she at last determined to

[030-009] set out for [[new-york|New-York]], and enquire out [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]], from whom she had no

[030-010] doubt but she should obtain immediate [[comfort|relief]] as soon as her distress was

[030-011] made known; she had no sooner formed this resolution than she resolved

[030-012] immediately to put it in execution: she therefore wrote the following

[030-013] little billet to Mrs. [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]], thinking if she should have company with

[030-014] her it would be better to send it in than to request to see her.

[030-015] TO MRS. CRAYTON.

[030-016] “MADAM,

[030-017] “When we left our native land, that dear, [[happiness|happy]] land which now contains

[030-018] all that is dear to the wretched Charlotte, our prospects were the same;

[030-019] we both, [[forgiveness|pardon]] me, Madam, if I say, we both too easily followed the

[030-020] impulse of our treacherous hearts, and trusted our happiness on a

[030-021] tempestuous ocean, where mine has been wrecked and lost for ever;

[030-022] you have been more fortunate--you are united to a man of honour and

[030-023] humanity, united by the most sacred ties, respected, esteemed, and

[030-024] admired, and surrounded by innumerable blessings of which I am bereaved,

[030-025] enjoying those pleasures which have fled my bosom never to return; alas!

[030-026] [[melancholy|sorrow]] and deep regret have taken their place. Behold me, Madam, a poor

[030-027] forsaken wanderer, who has no where to lay her weary head, wherewith to

[030-028] supply the wants of nature, or to shield her from the inclemency of the

[030-029] weather. To you I sue, to you I look for [[compassion|pity]] and relief. I ask not to

[030-030] be received as an intimate or an equal; only for [[benevolence|charity]]'s sweet sake

[030-031] receive me into your hospitable mansion, allot me the meanest apartment

[030-032] in it, and let me breath out my soul in prayers for your happiness; I

[030-033] cannot, I feel I cannot long bear up under the accumulated woes that

[030-034] pour in upon me; but oh! my dear Madam, for the love of heaven [[suffering|suffer]] me

[030-035] not to expire in the street; and when I am at peace, as soon I shall be,

[030-036] extend your [[charity|compassion]] to my helpless offspring, should it please heaven

[030-037] that it should survive its unhappy mother. A gleam of joy breaks in on

[030-038] my benighted soul while I reflect that you cannot, will not refuse your

[030-039] protection to the heart-broken. CHARLOTTE.”

[030-040] When Charlotte had finished this letter, late as it was in the

[030-041] afternoon, and though the snow began to [[ruin|fall]] very fast, she tied up a

[030-042] few necessaries which she had prepared against her expected confinement,

[030-043] and terrified lest she should be again exposed to the insults of her

[030-044] barbarous landlady, more dreadful to her wounded spirit than either

[030-045] storm or darkness, she set forward for New-York.

[030-046] It may be asked by those, who, in a work of this kind, love to cavil at

[030-047] every trifling omission, whether Charlotte did not possess any valuable

[030-048] of which she could have disposed, and by that means have supported

[030-049] herself till [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s return, when she would have been certain

[030-050] of receiving every tender attention which compassion and friendship

[030-051] could dictate: but let me entreat these wise, penetrating gentlemen to

[030-052] reflect, that when Charlotte left [[england|England]], it was in such haste that

[030-053] there was no time to purchase any thing more than what was wanted

[030-054] for immediate use on the voyage, and after her arrival at New-York,

[030-055] [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[love|affection]] soon began to decline, so that her whole

[030-056] wardrobe consisted of only necessaries, and as to baubles, with which

[030-057] fond lovers often load their mistresses, she possessed not one, except a

[030-058] plain gold locket of small value, which contained a lock of her mother's

[030-059] hair, and which the greatest extremity of want could not have forced her

[030-060] to part with.

[030-061] I hope, Sir, your prejudices are now removed in regard to the

[030-062] probability of my story? Oh they are. Well then, with your leave, I will

[030-063] proceed.

[030-064] The distance from the house which our [[distress|suffering]] heroine occupied, to

[030-065] New-York, was not very great, yet the snow fen so fast, and the cold so

[030-066] intense, that, being unable from her situation to walk quick, she found

[030-067] herself almost sinking with cold and fatigue before she reached the

[030-068] town; her garments, which were merely suitable to the summer season,

[030-069] being an undress robe of plain white muslin, were wet through, and

[030-070] a thin black cloak and bonnet, very improper habiliments for such a

[030-071] climate, but poorly defended her from the cold. In this situation she

[030-072] reached the city, and enquired of a foot soldier whom she met, the way

[030-073] to Colonel Crayton's.

[030-074] “Bless you, my sweet lady,” said the soldier with a voice and look of

[030-075] compassion, “I will shew you the way with all my heart; but if you are

[030-076] going to make a petition to Madam Crayton it is all to no purpose I

[030-077] assure you: if you please I will conduct you to [[mr-franklin|Mr. Franklin]]'s; though

[030-078] Miss Julia is married and gone now, yet the old gentleman is very good.”

[030-079] “[[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]],” said Charlotte; “is she not married to Montraville?”

[030-080] “Yes,” replied the soldier, “and may God bless them, for a better

[030-081] officer never lived, he is so good to us all; and as to Miss Julia, all

[030-082] the poor folk almost worshipped her.”

[030-083] “Gracious heaven,” cried Charlotte, “is Montraville unjust then to none

[030-084] but me.”

[030-085] The soldier now shewed her Colonel Crayton's door, and, with a beating

[030-086] heart, she knocked for admission.
