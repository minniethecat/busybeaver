[006-001] MADAME [[madame-du-pont|Du Pont]] was a woman every way calculated to take the care of

[006-002] [[youth|young]] ladies, had that care entirely devolved on herself; but it was

[006-003] impossible to attend the education of a numerous school without proper

[006-004] assistants; and those assistants were not always the kind of people

[006-005] whose conversation and morals were exactly such as parents of delicacy

[006-006] and refinement would wish a daughter to copy. Among the teachers

[006-007] at Madame Du Pont's school, was [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]], who added to a

[006-008] pleasing person and insinuating address, a liberal education and the

[006-009] manners of a gentlewoman. She was recommended to the school by a lady

[006-010] whose humanity overstepped the bounds of [[prudence|discretion]]: for though she

[006-011] knew Miss La Rue had eloped from a convent with a young officer, and, on

[006-012] coming to [[england|England]], had lived with several different men in open defiance

[006-013] of all moral and religious [[duty|duties]]; yet, finding her reduced to the

[006-014] most abject [[poverty|want]], and believing the [[repentance|penitence]] which she professed to be

[006-015] sincere, she took her into her own [[family]], and from thence recommended

[006-016] her to Madame Du Pont, as thinking the situation more suitable for

[006-017] a woman of her abilities. But Mademoiselle possessed too much of the

[006-018] spirit of intrigue to remain long without adventures. At church, where

[006-019] she constantly appeared, her person attracted the attention of a young

[006-020] man who was upon a visit at a gentleman's seat in the neighbourhood: she

[006-021] had met him several times clandestinely; and being invited to come out

[006-022] that evening, and eat some fruit and pastry in a summer-house belonging

[006-023] to the gentleman he was visiting, and requested to bring some of

[006-024] the ladies with her, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] being her favourite, was fixed on to

[006-025] accompany her.

[006-026] The mind of youth eagerly catches at promised pleasure: pure and

[006-027] innocent by nature, it thinks not of the dangers lurking beneath

[006-028] those pleasures, till too late to avoid them: when Mademoiselle asked

[006-029] Charlotte to go with her, she mentioned the gentleman as a relation,

[006-030] and spoke in such high terms of the elegance of his gardens, the

[006-031] sprightliness of his conversation, and the liberality with which he ever

[006-032] entertained his guests, that Charlotte thought only of the pleasure she

[006-033] should enjoy in the visit,--not on the [[prudence|imprudence]] of going without her

[006-034] governess's knowledge, or of the danger to which she exposed herself in

[006-035] visiting the house of a gay young man of fashion.

[006-036] Madame Du Pont was gone out for the evening, and the rest of the ladies

[006-037] retired to rest, when Charlotte and the teacher stole out at the back

[006-038] gate, and in crossing the field, were accosted by [[montraville|Montraville]], as

[006-039] mentioned in the first CHAPTER.

[006-040] Charlotte was disappointed in the pleasure she had promised herself

[006-041] from this visit. The levity of the gentlemen and the [[liberty|freedom]] of

[006-042] their conversation disgusted her. She was astonished at the liberties

[006-043] Mademoiselle permitted them to take; grew thoughtful and uneasy, and

[006-044] heartily wished herself at home again in her own chamber.

[006-045] Perhaps one cause of that wish might be, an earnest [[passion|desire]] to see the

[006-046] contents of the letter which had been put into her hand by Montraville.

[006-047] Any reader who has the least knowledge of the world, will easily

[006-048] imagine the letter was made up of encomiums on her beauty, and vows of

[006-049] everlasting love and constancy; nor will he be surprised that a heart

[006-050] open to every gentle, generous [[sensibility|sentiment]], should feel itself warmed by

[006-051] gratitude for a man who professed to feel so much for her; nor is it

[006-052] improbable but her mind might revert to the agreeable person and martial

[006-053] appearance of Montraville.

[006-054] In affairs of love, a young heart is never in more danger than

[006-055] when attempted by a handsome young soldier. A man of an indifferent

[006-056] appearance, will, when arrayed in a military habit, shew to advantage;

[006-057] but when beauty of person, elegance of manner, and an easy method of

[006-058] paying compliments, are united to the scarlet coat, smart cockade, and

[006-059] military sash, ah! well-a-day for the poor girl who gazes on him: she

[006-060] is in imminent danger; but if she listens to him with pleasure, 'tis all

[006-061] over with her, and from that moment she has neither eyes nor ears for

[006-062] any other object.

[006-063] Now, my dear sober matron, (if a sober matron should deign to turn over

[006-064] these pages, before she trusts them to the eye of a darling daughter,)

[006-065] let me intreat you not to put on a grave face, and throw down the book

[006-066] in a [[love|passion]] and declare 'tis enough to turn the heads of half the girls

[006-067] in England; I do solemnly protest, my dear madam, I mean no more by

[006-068] what I have here advanced, than to ridicule those romantic girls, who

[006-069] foolishly imagine a red coat and silver epaulet constitute the fine

[006-070] gentleman; and should that fine gentleman make half a dozen fine

[006-071] speeches to them, they will imagine themselves so much in love as

[006-072] to fancy it a meritorious action to jump out of a two pair of stairs

[006-073] window, abandon their [[friendship|friends]], and [[trust]] entirely to the honour of a

[006-074] man, who perhaps hardly knows the meaning of the word, and if he does,

[006-075] will be too much the modern man of refinement, to practice it in their

[006-076] favour.

[006-077] Gracious heaven! when I think on the miseries that must rend the heart

[006-078] of a doating parent, when he sees the darling of his age at first

[006-079] seduced from his protection, and afterwards abandoned, by the very

[006-080] wretch whose promises of love decoyed her from the paternal roof--when

[006-081] he sees her poor and wretched, her bosom tom between [[regret|remorse]] for her

[006-082] crime and love for her vile betrayer--when fancy paints to me the good

[006-083] old man stooping to raise the weeping [[repentance|penitent]], while every tear from

[006-084] her eye is numbered by drops from his bleeding heart, my bosom glows

[006-085] with honest [[anger|indignation]], and I wish for power to extirpate those

[006-086] monsters of seduction from the earth.

[006-087] Oh my dear girls--for to such only am I writing--listen not to the voice

[006-088] of love, unless sanctioned by paternal approbation: be assured, it is

[006-089] now past the days of romance: no woman can be run away with contrary

[006-090] to her own inclination: then kneel down each morning, and request kind

[006-091] heaven to keep you free from temptation, or, should it please to [[suffering|suffer]]

[006-092] you to be tried, pray for [[courage|fortitude]] to resist the impulse of inclination

[006-093] when it runs counter to the precepts of religion and virtue.
