[011-001] ALMOST a week was now gone, and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] continued every evening to

[011-002] meet [[montraville|Montraville]], and in her heart every meeting was resolved to be the

[011-003] last; but alas! when Montraville at parting would earnestly intreat one

[011-004] more interview, that treacherous heart betrayed her; and, forgetful

[011-005] of its resolution, pleaded the cause of the enemy so powerfully, that

[011-006] Charlotte was unable to resist. Another and another meeting succeeded;

[011-007] and so well did Montraville improve each opportunity, that the heedless

[011-008] girl at length confessed no idea could be so painful to her as that of

[011-009] never seeing him again.

[011-010] “Then we will never be parted,” said he.

[011-011] “Ah, Montraville,” replied Charlotte, forcing a smile, “how can it be

[011-012] avoided? My parents would never consent to our union; and even could

[011-013] they be brought to approve it, how should I bear to be separated from my

[011-014] kind, my [[love|beloved]] mother?”

[011-015] “Then you love your parents more than you do me, Charlotte?”

[011-016] “I hope I do,” said she, blushing and looking down, “I hope my [[love|affection]]

[011-017] for them will ever keep me from infringing the laws of [[duty|filial duty]].”

[011-018] “Well, Charlotte,” said Montraville gravely, and letting go her hand,

[011-019] “since that is the case, I find I have deceived myself with fallacious

[011-020] [[hope|hopes]]. I had flattered my fond heart, that I was dearer to Charlotte

[011-021] than any thing in the world beside. I thought that you would for my sake

[011-022] have braved the dangers of the ocean, that you would, by your affection

[011-023] and smiles, have softened the hardships of war, and, had it been my fate

[011-024] to [[ruin|fall]], that your tenderness would cheer the hour of death, and smooth

[011-025] my passage to another world. But farewel, Charlotte! I see you never

[011-026] loved me. I shall now welcome the [[friendship|friendly]] ball that deprives me of the

[011-027] sense of my [[distress|misery]].”

[011-028] “Oh stay, unkind Montraville,” cried she, catching hold of his arm, as

[011-029] he pretended to leave her, “stay, and to calm your [[fear|fears]], I will here

[011-030] protest that was it not for the fear of giving pain to the best of

[011-031] parents, and returning their kindness with [[gratitude|ingratitude]], I would follow

[011-032] you through every danger, and, in studying to promote your happiness,

[011-033] insure my own. But I cannot break my mother's heart, Montraville; I must

[011-034] not bring the grey hairs of my doating grand-father with [[melancholy|sorrow]] to the

[011-035] grave, or make my beloved father perhaps curse the hour that gave me

[011-036] birth.” She covered her face with her hands, and burst into tears.

[011-037] “All these distressing scenes, my dear Charlotte,” cried Montraville,

[011-038] “are merely the chimeras of a disturbed fancy. Your parents might

[011-039] perhaps grieve at first; but when they heard from your own hand that you

[011-040] was with a man of honour, and that it was to insure your [[happiness|felicity]] by an

[011-041] union with him, to which you feared they would never have given their

[011-042] assent, that you left their protection, they will, be assured, forgive

[011-043] an error which love alone occasioned, and when we return from [[america|America]],

[011-044] receive you with open arms and tears of joy.”

[011-045] [[belcour|Belcour]] and Mademoiselle heard this last speech, and conceiving it

[011-046] a proper time to throw in their advice and persuasions, approached

[011-047] Charlotte, and so well seconded the entreaties of Montraville, that

[011-048] finding Mademoiselle intended going with Belcour, and [[sensibility|feeling]] her own

[011-049] treacherous heart too much inclined to accompany them, the hapless

[011-050] Charlotte, in an evil hour, consented that the next evening they should

[011-051] bring a chaise to the end of the town, and that she would leave her

[011-052] friends, and throw herself entirely on the protection of Montraville.

[011-053] “But should you,” said she, looking earnestly at him, her eyes full

[011-054] of tears, “should you, forgetful of your promises, and repenting the

[011-055] engagements you here voluntarily enter into, forsake and leave me on a

[011-056] foreign shore--” “Judge not so meanly of me,” said he. “The moment we

[011-057] reach our place of destination, Hymen shall sanctify our love; and when

[011-058] I shall forget your [[kindness|goodness]], may heaven forget me.”

## Section 7

[011-059] “Ah,” said Charlotte, leaning on Mademoiselle's arm as they walked up

[011-060] the garden together, “I have forgot all that I ought to have remembered,

[011-061] in consenting to this intended elopement.”

[011-062] “You are a strange girl,” said Mademoiselle: “you never know your

[011-063] own mind two minutes at a time. Just now you declared Montraville's

[011-064] happiness was what you prized most in the world; and now I suppose

[011-065] you repent having insured that happiness by agreeing to accompany him

[011-066] abroad.”

[011-067] “Indeed I do repent,” replied Charlotte, “from my soul: but while

[011-068] [[prudence|discretion]] points out the impropriety of my conduct, inclination urges

[011-069] me on to ruin.”

[011-070] “Ruin! fiddlestick!” said Mademoiselle; “am I not going with you? and do

[011-071] I feel any of these qualms?”

[011-072] “You do not renounce a tender father and mother,” said Charlotte.

[011-073] “But I hazard my dear reputation,” replied Mademoiselle, bridling.

[011-074] “True,” replied Charlotte, “but you do not feel what I do.” She then

[011-075] bade her good night: but sleep was a stranger to her eyes, and the tear

[011-076] of [[grief|anguish]] watered her pillow.
