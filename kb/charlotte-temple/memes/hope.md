# Hope

An emotional state of anticipated future good, which in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* operates as a double-edged force: it sustains the [[virtue|virtuous]] through [[misfortune|adversity]] but also renders the innocent vulnerable to manipulation by those who exploit their hopeful nature.

## Definition

Rowson presents hope in its dual aspect throughout the novel. On one hand, it is the [[nature|natural]] optimism of youth and the [[comfort|consolation]] of the afflicted — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] exhorts [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to "hope" and see "much happiness" ahead ([002-085]). On the other, hope is the very mechanism through which seduction works: the [[seduction|seducer]] cultivates false hopes, and the victim's own hopeful disposition blinds her to danger. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is in large part a story of misplaced hope — hope that [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is a [[friendship|friend]], hope that [[montraville|Montraville]] will marry her, hope that her parents will forgive her. Each hope, disappointed in turn, drives her deeper into ruin.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her hopeful disposition — [[trust]]ing [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], believing [[montraville|Montraville]]'s promises, expecting parental [[mercy|forgiveness]] — is the engine of her [[weakness|vulnerability]] ([007-103])
- [[montraville]]: Exploits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s hopeful nature with promises he knows he may not keep ([012-097])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Manipulates [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s hopes to serve her own interests, offering false encouragement ([007-103])
- [[mr-temple]]: Embodies the [[virtue|virtuous]] form of hope — the father who hopes for his child's happiness ([002-085])

## Narrative Function

Hope is the psychological mechanism that Rowson uses to make [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] both plausible and instructive. By showing how [[nature|natural]] hopefulness enables the seduction plot, Rowson warns her [[youth|young]] readers that their own hopeful temperaments — however [[virtue|virtuous]] — can be weaponized against them. The progressive disappointment of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s hopes (in [[friendship|friends]]hip, in romantic [[affection|love]], in [[family|familial]] [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]) structures the novel's tragic arc. At the same time, hope serves as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s only [[comfort|consolation]] in her final [[distress|suffering]], and the narrator holds out hope as a Christian virtue that leads the repentant toward redemption.

## Related Concepts

- [[despair]]: The state that hope, when repeatedly disappointed, collapses into — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s final condition oscillates between these poles
- [[trust]]: Hope and [[trust]] are intertwined; misplaced [[trust]] generates false hope, and disappointed hope erodes the capacity to [[trust]]
- [[innocence]]: Innocent hopefulness is the very quality that makes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] susceptible to seduction
- [[guilt]]: The emotion that displaces hope in the later chapters, as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] recognizes her own responsibility
