[034-001] IN the mean time [[montraville|Montraville]] having received orders to return to

[034-002] [[new-york|New-York]], arrived, and having still some remains of compassionate

[034-003] tenderness for the woman whom he regarded as brought to shame by

[034-004] himself, he went out in search of [[belcour|Belcour]], to enquire whether she was

[034-005] safe, and whether the child lived. He found him immersed in dissipation,

[034-006] and could gain no other intelligence than that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] had left him,

[034-007] and that he knew not what was become of her.

[034-008] “I cannot believe it possible,” said Montraville, “that a mind once so

[034-009] pure as Charlotte [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s, should so suddenly become the mansion of

[034-010] vice. Beware, Belcour,” continued he, “beware if you have dared to

[034-011] behave either unjust or dishonourably to that poor girl, your life shall

[034-012] pay the forfeit:--I will revenge her cause.”

[034-013] He immediately went into the country, to the house where he had left

[034-014] Charlotte. It was desolate. After much enquiry he at length found the

[034-015] servant girl who had lived with her. From her he learnt the [[distress|misery]]

[034-016] Charlotte had endured from the complicated evils of illness, poverty,

[034-017] and a broken heart, and that she had set out on foot for New-York, on a

[034-018] cold winter's evening; but she could inform him no further.

[034-019] Tortured almost to madness by this shocking account, he returned to the

[034-020] city, but, before he reached it, the evening was drawing to a close.

[034-021] In entering the town he was obliged to pass several little huts, the

[034-022] residence of poor women who supported themselves by washing the cloaths

[034-023] of the officers and soldiers. It was nearly dark: he heard from a

[034-024] neighbouring steeple a solemn toll that seemed to say some poor mortal

[034-025] was going to their last mansion: the sound struck on the heart of

[034-026] Montraville, and he involuntarily stopped, when, from one of the houses,

[034-027] he saw the appearance of a funeral. Almost unknowing what he did, he

[034-028] followed at a small distance; and as they let the coffin into the grave,

[034-029] he enquired of a soldier who stood by, and had just brushed off a tear

[034-030] that did honour to his heart, who it was that was just buried. “An

[034-031] please your honour,” said the man, “'tis a poor girl that was brought

[034-032] from her [[friendship|friends]] by a cruel man, who left her when she was big with

[034-033] child, and married another.” Montraville stood motionless, and the man

[034-034] proceeded--“I met her myself not a fortnight since one night all wet and

[034-035] cold in the streets; she went to Madam [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]]'s, but she would not take

[034-036] her in, and so the poor thing went raving mad.” Montraville could bear

[034-037] no more; he struck his hands against his forehead with violence; and

[034-038] exclaiming “poor murdered Charlotte!” ran with precipitation towards the

[034-039] place where they were heaping the earth on her remains. “Hold, hold, one

[034-040] moment,” said he. “Close not the grave of the injured Charlotte Temple

[034-041] till I have taken [[revenge|vengeance]] on her murderer.”

[034-042] “Rash [[youth|young]] man,” said Mr. Temple, “who art thou that thus disturbest

[034-043] the last mournful rites of the dead, and rudely breakest in upon the

[034-044] [[sorrow|grief]] of an afflicted father.”

[034-045] “If thou art the father of Charlotte Temple,” said he, gazing at

[034-046] him with mingled horror and amazement--“if thou art her father--I am

[034-047] Montraville.” Then falling on his knees, he continued--“Here is my

[034-048] bosom. I bare it to receive the stroke I merit. Strike--strike now, and

[034-049] save me from the misery of reflexion.”

[034-050] “Alas!” said Mr. Temple, “if thou wert the [[seduction|seducer]] of my child, thy own

[034-051] reflexions be thy punishment. I wrest not the power from the hand of

[034-052] omnipotence. Look on that little heap of earth, there hast thou buried

[034-053] the only joy of a fond father. Look at it often; and may thy heart feel

[034-054] such true [[melancholy|sorrow]] as shall merit the [[compassion|mercy]] of heaven.” He turned from

[034-055] him; and Montraville starting up from the ground, where he had thrown

[034-056] himself, and at that instant remembering the perfidy of Belcour, flew

[034-057] like lightning to his lodgings. Belcour was intoxicated; Montraville

[034-058] impetuous: they fought, and the sword of the latter entered the heart

[034-059] of his adversary. He fell, and expired almost instantly. Montraville had

[034-060] received a slight wound; and overcome with the agitation of his mind and

[034-061] loss of blood, was carried in a state of [[sensibility|insensibility]] to his distracted

[034-062] wife. A dangerous illness and obstinate delirium ensued, during which

[034-063] he raved incessantly for Charlotte: but a strong constitution, and

[034-064] the tender assiduities of Julia, in time overcame the disorder. He

[034-065] recovered; but to the end of his life was subject to severe fits of

[034-066] melancholy, and while he remained at New-York frequently retired to the

[034-067] church-yard, where he would weep over the grave, and regret the untimely

[034-068] fate of the lovely Charlotte Temple.
