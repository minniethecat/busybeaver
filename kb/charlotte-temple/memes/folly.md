# Folly

The failure of [[reason|judgment]] that bridges innocence and ruin: in Rowson's moral framework, folly is the active misstep — the moment a [[youth|young]] woman sets aside [[reason|prudence]] and the precepts of virtue — that makes seduction possible and its consequences inevitable.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, folly denotes more than mere foolishness or intellectual deficiency. It names a specific moral failure: the abandonment of rational self-governance and prudent [[prudence|caution]] in favour of impulse, pleasure, or the influence of corrupt companions. Rowson consistently frames folly as the proximate cause of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]], positioning it between the [[weakness|vulnerability]] of innocence and the catastrophe of vice. It is the threshold concept: one may be innocent and yet not foolish, but once folly is committed, the descent into guilt and ruin follows with tragic momentum.

The narrator's direct address to "ye thoughtless daughters of folly" (008-037) personifies folly as a seductive force with its own constituency — those who act without thought, who pursue pleasure without calculating its cost. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s own retrospective [[reason|judgment]] confirms this framing: she acknowledges that "it was your own folly" (010-045) that brought her to [[poverty|penury]] and distress, and she credits a letter with saving her because "it has opened my eyes to the folly I was about to commit" (012-047). Folly is thus presented as a state of moral blindness from which one may be awakened — but often too late.

The novel's final position is that folly, while catastrophic, is not identical with wickedness. The narrator's appeal to the reader of sensibility to "address the un[[happiness|happy]] victim of folly" (018-072) implies that the foolish are victims as well as agents, deserving of [[charity|compassion]] even as they [[suffering|suffer]] the consequences of their actions. This ambivalence — folly as both culpable choice and pitiable condition — is central to the novel's moral complexity.

## Key Passages

> "ye thoughtless daughters of folly, have ye ever found the phantom" (008-037)

The narrator's direct apostrophe to the "daughters of folly" frames the concept as a category of moral identity. Those who belong to folly are "thoughtless" — they act without the reflective [[reason|judgment]] that virtue requires. The word "phantom" suggests that what folly pursues is illusory, a promised happiness that cannot be grasped.

> "reflected that it was your own folly and" (010-045)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moment of self-recognition: the [[distress|suffering]] she endures is the direct consequence of her own choices. The attribution of responsibility to "your own folly" is central to the novel's didactic purpose — the reader must understand that folly is not something that merely happens to one, but something one commits.

> "it has opened my eyes to the folly I was about to commit" (012-047)

The language of opened eyes suggests folly as a state of blindness from which one may be rescued — but the conditional "was about to commit" underscores the narrow window in which intervention is possible. Folly, once committed, cannot be undone.

> "the paths of virtue for those of vice and folly" (014-089)

Folly and vice are paired as the alternative to virtue's path. Their conjunction is significant: folly is the gateway, vice the destination. The spatial metaphor of "paths" reinforces the novel's imagery of moral choice as a journey with irreversible consequences.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- **[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]**: The novel's central case study in folly. Her decision to elope with [[montraville|Montraville]], her [[trust]] in [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s corrupt guidance, and her failure to heed the voice of [[reason|prudence]] each constitute acts of folly that together produce her ruin. Yet her capacity for self-recognition — her acknowledgment that "it was your own folly" (010-045) — distinguishes her from the novel's unrepentant [[reputation|character]]s and opens the possibility of redemption through [[regret|penitence]].
- **[[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]**: Represents folly's corrupting influence on others. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] does not act from folly but from calculated self-interest; her role is to exploit the folly of others, particularly [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s, for her own advancement. She embodies the world's readiness to profit from the thoughtlessness of the [[youth|young]] and innocent (014-089).

## Narrative Function

Folly serves as the hinge between innocence and guilt in Rowson's moral plot. The novel does not present [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as inherently vicious; rather, she commits a series of follies — each one making the next more likely, each one narrowing the path back to virtue. This incremental structure is essential to the novel's didactic strategy: by showing folly as a progressive condition, Rowson warns her [[youth|young]] readers that the first foolish step is the most dangerous because it makes the second seem less consequential.

The concept also enables the novel's ambivalent treatment of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s culpability. By framing her choices as "folly" rather than "vice," Rowson p[[modesty|reserve]]s sympathy for her heroine while maintaining the moral lesson. The reader is invited to [[compassion|pity]] "the un[[happiness|happy]] victim of folly" (018-072) even while recognising that the victim participated in her own [[ruin|destruction]]. This tension between agency and victimhood is central to the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's moral project.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Folly is the failure of the [[reason|prudence]] that virtue requires; the "paths of virtue" are abandoned for those of "vice and folly" (014-089)
- [[prudence]]: The faculty whose absence constitutes folly; [[reason|prudence]] is the rational self-governance that would have prevented [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]]
- [[seduction]]: The external force that exploits folly; seduction requires folly as its precondition — without [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s foolish [[trust]], [[montraville|Montraville]]'s designs could not succeed
- [[innocence]]: The state that precedes folly; innocence without [[reason|prudence]] is vulnerable to folly, and folly is what transforms innocence into guilt
- [[sensibility]]: The emotional capacity that, ungoverned, leads to folly; "too great sensibility" (028-024) is sensibility abandoned to folly
- [[ruin]]: The consequence of folly left unchecked; what begins as folly ends as ruin
- [[repentance]]: The moral response to recognised folly; [[regret|penitence]] is the acknowledgment that one's [[distress|suffering]] is the fruit of "her own folly" (018-056)
