# Happiness

The ultimate goal of moral conduct in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, defined not as momentary pleasure but as enduring domestic contentment grounded in virtue and [[reason|prudence]]. The novel frames happiness as both the [[nature|natural]] reward of right action and the thing most at risk when [[love|passion]] overrules reason.

## Definition

Rowson opens the novel by declaring her concern for "the happiness of that sex whose morals and conduct have so powerful an effect on mankind" ([P01-028]), establishing happiness as the text's organizing moral principle. In the novel's vocabulary, true happiness is always domestic and conjugal — it is what [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] finds with Lucy [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] through benevolent courtship, and what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] loses through seduction. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s maxim — "not sacrifice internal happiness for outward shew" ([002-008]) — serves as the novel's thesis: surface appearances (wealth, status, romantic excitement) are counterfeit currencies that cannot purchase genuine contentment. The narrator consistently links happiness to virtue and [[distress|misery]] to the erosion of moral [[reputation|character]].

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mr-temple]]: Embodies the pursuit of genuine domestic happiness — his courtship of Lucy [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] is a model of [[virtue|virtuous]] intention leading to lasting contentment ([005-030])
- [[charlotte-temple]]: The tragic case — she exchanges true happiness for a [[seduction|seducer]]'s false promises and spends the remainder of the novel in [[distress|misery]] ([011-032])
- [[montraville]]: Offers [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] the illusion of happiness while pursuing his own pleasure; his abandonment of her demonstrates that seduction's promises are counterfeit ([011-064])

## Narrative Function

Happiness serves as the novel's moral compass: every choice is evaluated by whether it leads toward or away from true happiness. The structure of the novel is essentially a study in contrast — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s courtship narrative (embedded in the early chapters) demonstrates how happiness is properly achieved, while [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction narrative demonstrates how it is lost. The narrator's repeated invocations of happiness function as direct addresses to the [[youth|young]] reader, urging her to recognize that "outward shew" and romantic adventure are poor substitutes for the quiet contentment of a [[virtue|virtuous]] life.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: The necessary precondition for happiness — without virtue, happiness is impossible in Rowson's moral framework
- [[ruin]]: The antithesis of happiness — the state of having permanently lost the possibility of domestic contentment
- [[hope]]: The emotional fuel of the pursuit of happiness, but also the mechanism by which false happiness deceives
- [[prudence]]: The practical wisdom that protects happiness from the temptations that would destroy it
