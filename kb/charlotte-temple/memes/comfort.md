# Comfort

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, comfort operates on two planes: the material comforts of a respectable middle-class life — home, [[family]], adequate means — and the emotional comfort of a clear conscience, loyal [[friendship|friends]], and religious [[religion|faith]]. Rowson's narrative traces [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s descent from the first and her failed search for the second, arguing implicitly that material comfort without virtue is precarious, and emotional comfort without religion is impossible.

## Definition

Rowson distinguishes between "comforts" (plural — material provisions, "many necessaries and comforts" (032-052)) and "comfort" (singular — emotional solace, "speak peace and comfort to her agitated soul" (025-041)). The former are the [[nature|natural]] accompaniments of virtue and industry; the latter is the exclusive province of religion and genuine human kindness. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is that she loses both: seduction strips her of material comfort, and guilt denies her spiritual comfort. The novel's few moments of genuine comfort — [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s visits, the clergyman's prayers — are brief respites that highlight by contrast the comfortlessness of her [[ruin|fall]]en state.

## Key Passages

> "…has an angel with him, who comforts and supports him" ([002-028])

[[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s [[religion|faith]] provides comfort even in prison — the novel's earliest and strongest statement that true comfort is spiritual, not material.

> "…the darling, the comfort" ([013-087])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is described as her parents' "comfort" — a word that captures both the emotional centrality of children and the devastating void her absence creates.

> "…has many comforts of which our poor Charlotte is deprived" ([018-042])

The narrator's direct comparison between a [[virtue|virtuous]] wife's comforts and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[poverty|destitution]] makes explicit the link between virtue and well-being.

> "…to sooth and comfort her" ([025-008])

[[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s visit to the [[death|dying]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] exemplifies the novel's highest form of comfort: disinterested kindness offered to the [[distress|suffering]].

> "…often find comfort at the bottom; the tear of penitence" ([028-030])

The narrator's moral: comfort is possible even in the deepest [[distress|misery]] through sincere repentance.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her arc is defined by the progressive loss of comfort — from her parents' home to a soldier's quarters to a stranger's hovel ([018-042])
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: The novel's primary agent of comfort — she brings both material relief and emotional solace to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] ([025-008])
- [[mr-temple]]: Provides the comfort of paternal protection, which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] only fully appreciates after losing it ([013-087])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Offers counterfeit comfort — her [[friendship|friends]]hip is a vehicle for seduction rather than genuine solace
- [[montraville]]: Withdraws comfort as his [[love|affection]] declines, il[[passion|lust]]rating the unreliability of [[seduction|seducer]]'s promises

## Narrative Function

Comfort serves as Rowson's index of moral status. The novel opens in scenes of domestic comfort (the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' home, the [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]]s' reunion) and closes with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death in the most minimal material circumstances. Between these poles, the presence or absence of comfort signals a [[reputation|character]]'s moral trajectory. The narrator repeatedly contrasts [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s former comforts with her present [[distress|misery]], using material deprivation to make moral degradation visible. The didactic message is clear: virtue safeguards comfort; vice destroys it. Only at the novel's end does comfort return — through the clergyman's spiritual counsel and the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' [[mercy|forgiveness]].

## Related Concepts

- [[suffering]]: The opposite of comfort; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] is measured by the comfort she has lost
- [[distress]]: The acute form of comfort's absence — a state of immediate crisis requiring relief
- [[poverty]]: The material dimension of comfort's loss; poverty in the novel is both physical deprivation and moral warning
- [[wealth]]: [[wealth|Wealth]] provides material comfort but, without virtue, cannot ensure emotional comfort
- [[religion]]: The novel's ultimate source of comfort — only [[religion|faith]] can comfort the truly [[repentance|penitent]]
- [[family]]: The [[nature|natural]] source of comfort; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s isolation from [[family]] is her deepest deprivation
- [[hope]]: Comfort's companion emotion; when comfort vanishes, hope sustains
