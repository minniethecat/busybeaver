# Mrs. Temple

Mrs. Temple is Charlotte's mother and one of the novel's moral centers. Where [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] represents paternal authority and principled [[reason|judgment]], Mrs. Temple embodies maternal tenderness, emotional intuition, and the capacity for unconditional [[mercy|forgiveness]]. Her journey from contented mother to [[sorrow|grief]]-stricken parent to redemptive presence at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] traces the novel's emotional arc and enacts its central moral lesson: that [[affection|love]], not [[reason|judgment]], is the proper response to [[regret|penitence]].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Temple opens the novel as the contented mother of an [[love|affection]]ate [[family]], "content dwelt in Mrs. Temple's bosom" ([008-091]). She is the voice of protective [[prudence|caution]] when [[montraville|Montraville]]'s intentions become known, and her devastating [[sorrow|grief]] upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement — "She is dead," cried Mrs. Temple. "Oh my dear [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]!" ([014-018]) — marks the novel's emotional turning point. She spends the remainder of the narrative in [[grief|mourning]], until the letter from [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] prompts her to seek her daughter. Her presence at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] — forgiving, weeping, comforting — provides the novel's redemptive resolution: "I would if possible fly to her" ([025-043]).

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Maternal tenderness ([008-017]), emotional intuition about [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[reputation|character]], capacity for [[mercy|forgiveness]] ([025-033]), unwavering [[affection|love]] despite [[deceit|betrayal]], religious [[religion|faith]]
- **Flaws**: Her emotional nature, while [[virtue|virtuous]], leaves her prostrate with [[sorrow|grief]] for much of the novel — "the struggle in the bosom of Mrs. Temple between maternal and conjugal [[love|affection]]" ([025-053]) suggests the tension between her roles as mother and wife

## Key Actions

- Cautions against [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s involvement with [[montraville|Montraville]], sensing danger where [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] sees opportunity ([008-001])
- Collapses in "breathless agitation" upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement ([014-014])
- Endures prolonged [[sorrow|grief]], the "smile which was wont to adorn the face of Mrs. Temple" replaced by [[grief|sorrow]] ([015-050])
- Receives [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[repentance|penitent]] letter and immediately resolves to find her: "I would if possible fly to her" ([025-043])
- Arrives at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]], offering the [[mercy|forgiveness]] that enables [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s peaceful death ([035-019])

## Relationships

- [[mr-temple]]: Her husband — a complementary partnership of maternal tenderness and paternal principle. Their shared [[sorrow|grief]] and shared [[mercy|forgiveness]] model ideal Christian marriage ([008-001])
- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her [[love|beloved]] daughter, whose [[ruin|fall]] destroys Mrs. Temple's happiness and whose repentance restores it in its final, tragic form ([014-018])
- [[lucy]]: Her [[youth|young]]er daughter, whose [[affection|filial affection]] sustains her parents through their [[sorrow|grief]] ([013-006])
- [[montraville]]: Views him with maternal suspicion — her instinctive dis[[trust]] proves tragically prescient

## Moral Significance

Mrs. Temple is Rowson's model of maternal virtue under the most extreme test. She demonstrates that a mother's [[affection|love]] survives even the deepest [[deceit|betrayal]], and that [[mercy|forgiveness]] — not punishment — is the Christian response to [[regret|penitence]]. Her journey from contented motherhood through devastation to redemptive [[affection|love]] mirrors the novel's own didactic project: to teach parents forbearance and children the cost of [[gratitude|ingratitude]]. "Oh my poor girl," said Mrs. Temple, "how must she be altered" ([025-014]) — her instinct is not to condemn but to seek and to heal.

> "…give the dear penitent?" said Mrs. Temple. "We will go and bring back our wandering daughter" ([025-033])

## Fate / Resolution

Mrs. Temple survives [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death, transformed by [[sorrow|grief]] into a figure of quiet endurance. Her final appearance, comforting [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] at the [[death|deathbed]], completes her arc from protected domestic happiness to tragic wisdom. She has lost her eldest daughter but has demonstrated that a mother's [[affection|love]] — and God's [[compassion|mercy]] — are sufficient even for the novel's darkest hour ([035-027]).
