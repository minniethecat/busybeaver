Nature, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is a capacious moral concept that encompasses both human [[weakness|vulnerability]] and human virtue. It is "nature, all-powerful nature" that binds parents to their children with unbreakable [[love|affection]] ([014-084]), and it is nature that "has implanted" [[modesty|modest]]y "in the female breast" ([024-095]). Yet nature also marks the limits of human endurance — "beyond the strength of human nature to endure" ([003-087]) — and the "weary nature" that seeks oblivion in sleep ([018-036]). Rowson's invocation of nature is neither Romantic nor proto-Rousseauian; it is the nature of Augustan moral psychology, a fixed human constitution whose laws can be violated only at great cost.

## Definition

Rowson uses "nature" in several distinct but related [[reason|sense]]s. It is the innate moral compass — the "innate [[modesty|modest]]y, which nature has implanted" ([024-095]) — that guides [[virtue|virtuous]] conduct. It is the limit of human endurance — the point "beyond the strength of human nature" ([003-087]) at which [[distress|suffering]] becomes unsustainable. And it is the affective bond that ties families together — "nature, all-powerful nature, has placed" [[affection|love]] for children in the parental heart ([014-084]). Across these meanings, nature functions as a normative standard: to act against nature is to court disaster; to act in accordance with nature is to find moral equilibrium.

The novel's most devastating critique of human ignorance is framed explicitly in terms of nature: "Alas poor Charlotte, how confined was her knowledge of human nature" ([029-040]). [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is not merely that she was seduced but that she lacked the knowledge of human nature that would have enabled her to recognize [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[deceit|deception]] and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s self-interest. The novel's didactic project is thus an education in human nature — teaching young readers what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] learned too late.

## Key Passages

> "Beyond the strength of human nature to endure" — Captain Eldridge on the extremity of his suffering ([003-087])

> "Nature, all-powerful nature, has placed it in our hearts" — the narrator on parental love ([014-084])

> "That comforter of weary nature (who seldom visits the unhappy) to come and steep her senses in oblivion" — on Charlotte's sleeplessness ([018-036])

> "That innate modesty, which nature has implanted in the female breast" ([024-095])

> "Alas poor Charlotte, how confined was her knowledge of human nature" — the narrator's explicit diagnosis of Charlotte's fatal ignorance ([029-040])

> "My adored father." "My long lost child." Nature... — the climactic reunion scene in which nature reclaims its authority over human relationships ([033-095])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Nature's victim and its beneficiary. Her natural innocence makes her vulnerable to seduction, but her natural bond with her parents — the "nature, all-powerful nature" that ties child to parent — ultimately enables her redemption through their [[mercy|forgiveness]]. Her "confined" knowledge of human nature ([029-040]) is both her tragedy and the novel's didactic occasion.
- [[mr-temple]]: Nature's ideal expression in parenthood. His [[affection|love]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is presented as the natural [[love|affection]] of a father, a bond that neither her transgression nor her absence can sever. The reunion scene — "My adored father" / "My long lost child" ([033-095]) — is the novel's most powerful dramatization of nature reclaiming what circumstance had severed.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Nature violated. Her lack of natural [[modesty|modest]]y, natural [[love|affection]], and natural conscience marks her as the novel's most thoroughly unnatural [[reputation|character]].

## Narrative Function

Nature provides the novel's moral framework without requiring explicit theological machinery. When Rowson invokes nature, she appeals to a shared understanding of human constitution that makes moral argument possible without sectarian religious language. The concept also serves a consolatory function: if parental [[affection|love]] is "nature, all-powerful nature," then [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents must forgive her, and the novel's tragic ending is softened by the assurance that the natural order, however violated, can ultimately be restored. The didactic function is equally important: by diagnosing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ignorance of human nature as the proximate cause of her fall, Rowson offers the novel itself as the remedy — reading it will teach young women what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] learned only through [[distress|suffering]].

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Nature as virtue's foundation. Virtuous conduct is conduct in accordance with nature; vice is nature's violation.
- [[innocence]]: The natural state of the [[youth|young]]. [[innocence|Innocence]] is nature's gift; its loss is nature's tragedy.
- [[passion]]: Nature's dangerous edge. [[passion|Passion]] is natural, but unchecked [[love|passion]] — "a youthful [[love|passion]]" — leads to nature's violation.
- [[conscience]]: Nature's internal monitor. The "blush of conscious shame" is nature's mechanism for moral self-regulation.
