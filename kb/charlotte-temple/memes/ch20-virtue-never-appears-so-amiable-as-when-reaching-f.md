[020-001] [[virtue|Virtue]] never appears so amiable as when reaching forth

[020-002] her hand to raise a fallen sister.

[020-003] CHAPTER OF ACCIDENTS.

[020-004] WHEN [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] awoke, she missed [[montraville|Montraville]]; but thinking he might have

[020-005] arisen early to enjoy the beauties of the morning, she was preparing

[020-006] to follow him, when casting her eye on the table, she saw a note, and

[020-007] opening it hastily, found these words--

[020-008] “My dear Charlotte must not be surprised, if she does not see me again

[020-009] for some time: unavoidable business will prevent me that pleasure: be

[020-010] assured I am quite well this morning; and what your fond imagination

[020-011] magnified into illness, was nothing more than fatigue, which a few hours

[020-012] rest has entirely removed. Make yourself [[happiness|happy]], and be certain of the

[020-013] unalterable friendship of

[020-014] “MONTRAVILLE.”

[020-015] “FRIENDSHIP!” said Charlotte emphatically, as she finished the note, “is

[020-016] it come to this at last? Alas! poor, forsaken Charlotte, thy doom is now

[020-017] but too apparent. Montraville is no longer interested in thy happiness;

[020-018] and shame, [[repentance|remorse]], and disappointed love will henceforth be thy only

[020-019] attendants.”

[020-020] Though these were the ideas that involuntarily rushed upon the mind

[020-021] of Charlotte as she perused the fatal note, yet after a few hours had

[020-022] elapsed, the syren [[hope|Hope]] again took possession of her bosom, and she

[020-023] flattered herself she could, on a second perusal, discover an air of

[020-024] tenderness in the few lines he had left, which at first had escaped her

[020-025] notice.

[020-026] “He certainly cannot be so base as to leave me,” said she, “and in

[020-027] styling himself my [[friendship|friend]] does he not promise to protect me. I will not

[020-028] torment myself with these causeless [[fear|fears]]; I will place a confidence in

[020-029] his honour; and sure he will not be so unjust as to abuse it.”

[020-030] Just as she had by this manner of reasoning brought her mind to some

[020-031] tolerable degree of composure, she was surprised by a visit from

[020-032] [[belcour|Belcour]]. The [[melancholy|dejection]] visible in Charlotte's countenance, her swoln

[020-033] eyes and neglected attire, at once told him she was unhappy: he made no

[020-034] doubt but Montraville had, by his coldness, alarmed her suspicions,

[020-035] and was resolved, if possible, to rouse her to jealousy, urge her to

[020-036] reproach him, and by that means occasion a breach between them. “If I

[020-037] can once convince her that she has a rival,” said he, “she will listen

[020-038] to my [[love|passion]] if it is only to revenge his slights.” Belcour knew but

[020-039] little of the female heart; and what he did know was only of those of

[020-040] loose and dissolute lives. He had no idea that a woman might [[ruin|fall]] a

[020-041] victim to [[prudence|imprudence]], and yet retain so strong a sense of honour, as to

[020-042] reject with horror and contempt every solicitation to a second fault.

[020-043] He never imagined that a gentle, generous female heart, once tenderly

[020-044] attached, when treated with unkindness might break, but would never

[020-045] harbour a thought of revenge.

[020-046] His visit was not long, but before he went he fixed a scorpion in the

[020-047] heart of Charlotte, whose venom embittered every future hour of her

[020-048] life.

[020-049] We will now return for a moment to [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]]. He had been three

[020-050] months married, and in that little time had discovered that the

[020-051] conduct of his lady was not so prudent as it ought to have been: but

[020-052] remonstrance was vain; her temper was violent; and to the Colonel's

[020-053] great misfortune he had conceived a sincere [[love|affection]] for her: she saw

[020-054] her own power, and, with the art of a Circe, made every action appear

[020-055] to him in what light she pleased: his acquaintance laughed at his

[020-056] blindness, his friends pitied his infatuation, his amiable daughter,

[020-057] [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], in secret deplored the loss of her father's affection,

[020-058] and grieved that he should be so entirely swayed by an artful, and, she

[020-059] much feared, infamous woman.

[020-060] Mrs. Beauchamp was mild and engaging; she loved not the hurry and bustle

[020-061] of a city, and had prevailed on her husband to take a house a few

[020-062] miles from [[new-york|New-York]]. Chance led her into the same neighbourhood with

[020-063] Charlotte; their houses stood within a short space of each other, and

[020-064] their gardens joined: she had not been long in her new habitation before

[020-065] the figure of Charlotte struck her; she recollected her interesting

[020-066] features; she saw the melancholy so conspicuous in her countenance,

[020-067] and her heart bled at the reflection, that perhaps deprived of honour,

[020-068] friends, all that was valuable in life, she was doomed to linger out a

[020-069] wretched existence in a strange land, and sink broken-hearted into

[020-070] an untimely grave. “Would to heaven I could snatch her from so hard

[020-071] a fate,” said she; “but the merciless world has barred the doors of

[020-072] [[charity|compassion]] against a poor weak girl, who, perhaps, had she one kind

[020-073] friend to raise and reassure her, would gladly return to peace and

[020-074] virtue; nay, even the woman who dares to [[compassion|pity]], and endeavour to recall

[020-075] a wandering sister, incurs the sneer of contempt and ridicule, for an

[020-076] action in which even angels are said to rejoice.”

[020-077] The longer Mrs. Beauchamp was a witness to the solitary life Charlotte

[020-078] led, the more she wished to speak to her, and often as she saw her

[020-079] cheeks wet with the tears of [[distress|anguish]], she would say--“Dear sufferer, how

[020-080] gladly would I pour into your heart the balm of [[comfort|consolation]], were it not

[020-081] for the fear of derision.”

[020-082] But an accident soon happened which made her resolve to brave even the

[020-083] scoffs of the world, rather than not enjoy the heavenly satisfaction of

[020-084] comforting a desponding fellow-creature.

[020-085] Mrs. Beauchamp was an early riser. She was one morning walking in the

[020-086] garden, leaning on her husband's arm, when the sound of a harp attracted

[020-087] their notice: they listened attentively, and heard a soft melodious

[020-088] voice distinctly sing the following stanzas:

[020-089] Thou glorious orb, supremely bright,

[020-090] Just rising from the sea,

[020-091] To cheer all nature with thy light,

[020-092] What are thy beams to me?

[020-093] In vain thy glories bid me rise,

[020-094] To hail the new-born day,

[020-095] Alas! my morning sacrifice

[020-096] Is still to weep and pray.

[020-097] For what are nature's charms combin'd,

[020-098] To one, whose weary breast

[020-099] Can neither peace nor comfort find,

[020-100] Nor friend whereon to rest?

[020-101] Oh! never! never! whilst I live

[020-102] Can my heart's [[grief|anguish]] cease:

[020-103] Come, friendly death, thy mandate give,

[020-104] And let me be at peace.

[020-105] “'Tis poor Charlotte!” said Mrs. Beauchamp, the pellucid drop of

[020-106] humanity stealing down her cheek.

[020-107] Captain Beauchamp was alarmed at her emotion. “What Charlotte?” said he;

[020-108] “do you know her?”

[020-109] In the accent of a pitying angel did she disclose to her husband

[020-110] Charlotte's unhappy situation, and the frequent wish she had formed of

[020-111] being serviceable to her. “I fear,” continued she, “the poor girl has

[020-112] been basely betrayed; and if I thought you would not blame me, I would

[020-113] pay her a visit, offer her my friendship, and endeavour to restore

[020-114] to her heart that peace she seems to have lost, and so pathetically

[020-115] laments. Who knows, my dear,” laying her hand affectionately on his arm,

[020-116] “who knows but she has left some kind, affectionate parents to lament

[020-117] her errors, and would she return, they might with rapture receive the

[020-118] poor [[repentance|penitent]], and wash away her faults in tears of joy. Oh! what a

[020-119] glorious reflexion would it be for me could I be the happy instrument of

[020-120] restoring her. Her heart may not be depraved, Beauchamp.”

[020-121] “Exalted woman!” cried Beauchamp, embracing her, “how dost thou rise

[020-122] every moment in my esteem. Follow the impulse of thy generous heart,

[020-123] my Emily. Let prudes and fools censure if they dare, and blame a

[020-124] sensibility they never felt; I will exultingly tell them that the heart

[020-125] that is truly virtuous is ever inclined to [[compassion|pity]] and forgive the errors

[020-126] of its fellow-creatures.”

[020-127] A beam of exulting joy played round the animated countenance of Mrs.

[020-128] Beauchamp, at these encomiums bestowed on her by a beloved husband, the

[020-129] most delightful sensations pervaded her heart, and, having breakfasted,

[020-130] she prepared to visit Charlotte.
