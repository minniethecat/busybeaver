[032-001] THE reader of sensibility may perhaps be astonished to find [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]]

[032-002] could so positively deny any knowledge of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]; it is therefore but

[032-003] just that her conduct should in some measure be accounted for. She had

[032-004] ever been fully sensible of the superiority of Charlotte's [[reason|sense]] and

[032-005] virtue; she was conscious that she had never swerved from [[virtue|rectitude]], had

[032-006] it not been for her bad precepts and worse example. These were things as

[032-007] yet unknown to her husband, and she wished not to have that part of her

[032-008] conduct exposed to him, as she had great reason to fear she had already

[032-009] lost considerable part of that power she once maintained over him. She

[032-010] trembled whilst Charlotte was in the house, lest the Colonel should

[032-011] return; she perfectly well remembered how much he seemed interested in

[032-012] her favour whilst on their passage from [[england|England]], and made no doubt, but,

[032-013] should he see her in her present distress, he would offer her an asylum,

[032-014] and protect her to the utmost of his power. In that case she feared the

[032-015] unguarded nature of Charlotte might discover to the Colonel the part

[032-016] she had taken in the unhappy girl's elopement, and she well knew the

[032-017] contrast between her own and Charlotte's conduct would make the former

[032-018] appear in no very respectable light. Had she reflected properly, she

[032-019] would have afforded the poor girl protection; and by enjoining her

[032-020] silence, ensured it by acts of repeated kindness; but vice in general

[032-021] blinds its votaries, and they discover their real characters to the

[032-022] world when they are most studious to preserve appearances.

[032-023] Just so it happened with Mrs. [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]]: her servants made no scruple of

[032-024] mentioning the cruel conduct of their lady to a poor distressed

[032-025] lunatic who claimed her protection; every one joined in reprobating her

[032-026] [[cruelty|inhumanity]]; nay even Corydon thought she might at least have ordered her

[032-027] to be taken care of, but he dare not even hint it to her, for he lived

[032-028] but in her smiles, and drew from her lavish fondness large sums to

[032-029] support an extravagance to which the state of his own finances was very

[032-030] inadequate; it cannot therefore be supposed that he wished Mrs. Crayton

[032-031] to be very liberal in her bounty to the afflicted suppliant; yet vice

[032-032] had not so entirely seared over his heart, but the sorrows of Charlotte

[032-033] could find a vulnerable part.

[032-034] Charlotte had now been three days with her humane preservers, but

[032-035] she was totally insensible of every thing: she raved incessantly for

[032-036] [[montraville|Montraville]] and her father: she was not conscious of being a mother, nor

[032-037] took the least notice of her child except to ask whose it was, and why

[032-038] it was not carried to its parents.

[032-039] “Oh,” said she one day, starting up on hearing the infant cry, “why, why

[032-040] will you keep that child here; I am sure you would not if you knew

[032-041] how hard it was for a mother to be parted from her infant: it is like

[032-042] tearing the cords of life asunder. Oh could you see the horrid sight

[032-043] which I now behold--there there stands my dear mother, her poor bosom

[032-044] bleeding at every vein, her gentle, affectionate heart torn in a

[032-045] thousand pieces, and all for the loss of a [[ruin|ruined]], ungrateful child.

[032-046] Save me save me--from her frown. I dare not--indeed I dare not speak to

[032-047] her.”

[032-048] Such were the dreadful images that haunted her distracted mind, and

[032-049] nature was sinking fast under the dreadful malady which medicine had

[032-050] no power to remove. The surgeon who attended her was a humane man; he

[032-051] exerted his utmost abilities to save her, but he saw she was in [[poverty|want]] of

[032-052] many necessaries and comforts, which the poverty of her hospitable host

[032-053] rendered him unable to provide: he therefore determined to make her

[032-054] situation known to some of the officers' ladies, and endeavour to make a

[032-055] collection for her [[comfort|relief]].

[032-056] When he returned home, after making this resolution, he found a message

[032-057] from [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], who had just arrived from Rhode-Island, requesting

[032-058] he would call and see one of her children, who was very unwell. “I do

[032-059] not know,” said he, as he was hastening to obey the summons, “I do not

[032-060] know a woman to whom I could apply with more hope of success than Mrs.

[032-061] Beauchamp. I will endeavour to interest her in this poor girl's behalf,

[032-062] she wants the soothing balm of [[friendship|friendly]] consolation: we may perhaps save

[032-063] her; we will try at least.”

[032-064] “And where is she,” cried Mrs. Beauchamp when he had prescribed

[032-065] something for the child, and told his little pathetic tale, “where is

[032-066] she, Sir? we will go to her immediately. Heaven forbid that I should

[032-067] be deaf to the calls of humanity. Come we will go this instant.” Then

[032-068] seizing the doctor's arm, they sought the habitation that contained the

[032-069] [[death|dying]] Charlotte.
