# Weakness

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, "weakness" is a multivalent term that Rowson deploys to negotiate the novel's central tension between sympathy for women's vulnerability and insistence on moral accountability. Physical weakness (the "extreme weakness of my frame" [003-092]) evokes [[charity|compassion]]; moral weakness (the "weakness of the father" [016-046]) invites [[reason|judgment]]. The novel's didactic project turns on distinguishing between these forms and assigning responsibility accordingly.

## Definition

Rowson uses weakness in three distinct registers. Physical weakness — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s declining health, [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s frailty — is a condition to be pitied and relieved. Moral weakness — [[montraville|Montraville]]'s failure to resist temptation, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s failure to refuse [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] — is a culpable failing that the novel condemns. And gendered weakness — "a woman's weakness" [014-035] — occupies an ambivalent middle ground: it is presented as [[nature|natural]] and therefore somewhat excusable, yet also as something that [[virtue|virtuous]] education and firm parenting should fortify against. The novel's moral arithmetic is clearest in its treatment of parental weakness: "without the weakness of the father" ([016-046]) is praise for Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s firmness, implicitly critiquing [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s earlier indulgence.

## Key Passages

> "…the extreme weakness of my frame prevented my feeling my distress so acutely" ([003-092])

[[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s physical weakness paradoxically serves as a [[compassion|mercy]] — the body's frailty moderating the mind's [[distress|suffering]].

> "…who could indulge a woman's weakness, let him" ([014-035])

The narrator's challenge to male readers: those who would exploit female vulnerability should consider the [[ruin|destruction]] they cause.

> "…without the weakness of the father: she was" ([016-046])

Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]] is praised for possessing paternal firmness without paternal weakness — Rowson's pointed critique of indulgent fatherhood as contributing to daughters' vulnerability.

> "…weakness of its mother." ([026-070])

The death of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s infant attributed to the mother's physical and moral state — weakness transmitted tragically from parent to child.

> "I am quite sensible of my weakness; I feel I have but" ([033-058])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] confession acknowledges her weakness without excusing it — the novel's final, honest word on the subject.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Embodies the tragedy of weakness — her physical frailty evokes sympathy, but her moral weakness (failure to resist temptation) is the novel's central [[prudence|caution]]ary lesson ([033-058])
- [[mr-temple]]: His paternal "weakness" — excessive indulgence — is implicitly critiqued as contributing to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s vulnerability ([016-046])
- [[mrs-temple]]: Praised for strength "without the weakness of the father" — the novel's model of firm, [[virtue|virtuous]] parenting ([016-046])
- [[montraville]]: His weakness is moral — he knows what is right but lacks the strength to do it, making him more culpable than the woman he [[seduction|seduce]]d
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Exploits others' weakness — she recognizes and preys upon [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s vulnerability, embo[[death|dying]] the predator that weakness invites
- [[captain-eldridge]]: His physical weakness evokes the reader's [[charity|compassion]] — the novel's model of frailty that deserves sympathy rather than [[reason|judgment]] ([003-092])

## Narrative Function

Weakness is the hinge on which Rowson's sympathy and [[reason|judgment]] turn. The novel [[poverty|want]]s readers to [[compassion|pity]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s weakness while also learning from it — to feel [[charity|compassion]] for the [[ruin|fall]]en without condoning the [[ruin|fall]]. This tension drives the novel's emotional complexity: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is simultaneously victim (of her own weakness, of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s manipulation, of [[montraville|Montraville]]'s seduction) and agent (of her own poor choices). Rowson resolves this tension through the [[death|deathbed]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s physical weakness becomes total, her moral accountability is acknowledged, and [[mercy|forgiveness]] — the novel's ultimate resolution — becomes possible precisely because weakness has given way to honesty.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: The opposite of moral weakness; virtue is the strength of [[reputation|character]] that resists temptation
- [[seduction]]: The system that exploits female weakness; Rowson's anatomy of how predators identify and exploit vulnerability
- [[prudence]]: The cultivated habit that guards against weakness; prudent women are less vulnerable to seduction
- [[folly]]: Weakness in action — the [[folly|foolish]] choices that proceed from weak [[reason|judgment]]
- [[guilt]]: The moral consequence of weakness; guilt is what weakness becomes when examined by conscience
- [[innocence]]: [[innocence|Innocence]] and weakness are related but distinct — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is innocent but weak; [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is neither
- [[courage]]: The virtue that overcomes weakness; moral courage is what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] lacks and what the novel urges readers to cultivate
