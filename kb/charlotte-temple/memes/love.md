# Love

In Rowson's moral vocabulary, [[affection|love]] is not a single concept but a field of contestation between two opposed models: the rational, parentally-sanctioned mutual affection that produces stable families, and the impulsive, unsanctioned passion that leads [[youth|young]] women to [[ruin|destruction]]. The novel does not condemn [[affection|love]] itself — indeed the narrator treats the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' marriage with warm approval — but insists that [[affection|love]] must be governed by "paternal approbation" (006-088) and reason to be legitimate. The word appears with remarkable frequency in the text, making it one of the novel's most insistent thematic concerns.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, [[affection|love]] is subjected to sustained moral scrutiny. The novel provides a positive model in the courtship and marriage of [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] and Lucy [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]], whose union is founded on mutual esteem, parental consent, and [[virtue|virtuous]] restraint. The narrator describes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as "the only pledge of their mutual [[affection|love]]" (005-086), framing [[affection|love]] within marriage as a generative, stable force.

Against this model, Rowson contrasts the counterfeit [[affection|love]] of seduction. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s letter to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is "made up of encomiums on her beauty, and vows of everlasting [[affection|love]] and constancy" (006-049) — professions that prove hollow once his [[passion|desire]] is gratified. The narrator directly warns her readers: "listen not to the voice of [[affection|love]], unless sanctioned by paternal approbation" (006-088). This is the novel's governing axiom on the subject: [[affection|love]] without parental and rational governance is not [[affection|love]] at all but a dangerous imposture.

The novel also anatomizes the tragic persistence of [[affection|love]] after [[deceit|betrayal]]. The narrator imagines the [[ruin|fall]]en woman's "bosom torn between [[regret|remorse]] for her crime and [[affection|love]] for her vile betrayer" (006-082), suggesting that [[affection|love]], once given, cannot be recalled even when the beloved proves unworthy — a psychological insight that deepens the novel's tragic dimension and aligns it with the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al tradition's interest in the irrational endurance of [[sensibility|feeling]].

## Typology: Love's Forms in the Novel

Rowson distinguishes four distinct manifestations of [[affection|love]], each carrying different moral weight:

| Form | Definition | Exemplar | Moral Status |
|------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Conjugal Love** | Mutual affection within a sanctioned marriage, producing [[family]] stability and offspring | [[mr-temple|Mr. and Mrs. Temple]] (005-086) | Virtuous — the novel's normative ideal |
| **Filial Love** | The [[nature|natural]] bond between parent and child, carrying reciprocal obligations of obedience and protection | [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[affection|love]] for her parents; [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s enduring [[affection|love]] for his [[ruin|ruined]] daughter (032-045) | Virtuous — the bond that seduction severs |
| **Romantic [[passion|Passion]]** | Unsanctioned erotic attraction operating outside parental governance and rational restraint | [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[sensibility|feeling]] for [[montraville|Montraville]], described as "a [[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-025) | Dangerous but redeemable — genuinely felt, catastrophically misdirected |
| **Counterfeit Love** | Strategic performance of romantic language to achieve predatory ends | [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "vows of everlasting [[affection|love]] and constancy" (006-049); [[belcour|Belcour]]'s designs which "were not [[honour|honourable]]" (016-078) | Vicious — the primary weapon of the [[seduction|seducer]] |

This four-part typology is the novel's most sustained contribution to [[sensibility|sentiment]]al discourse on [[affection|love]]. Rowson's innovation is not in condemning passion — earlier conduct literature did that — but in insisting on the *sincerity* of the victim's [[sensibility|feeling]] even as she traces its catastrophic consequences.

## Key Passages

> "Such were the parents of Charlotte Temple, who was the only pledge of their mutual love" (005-086)

This passage establishes the normative model of [[affection|love]] in the novel: mutual, generative, and situated within the sanctioned institution of marriage. The [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' [[affection|love]] produces [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], the novel's heroine, framing domestic [[affection|love]] as life-giving and socially constructive.

> "the letter was made up of encomiums on her beauty, and vows of everlasting love and constancy" (006-049)

The description of [[montraville|Montraville]]'s letter exposes the [[seduction|seducer]]'s counterfeit [[affection|love]]. The narrator's ironic tone — "Any reader who has the least knowledge of the world, will easily imagine" — positions false professions of [[affection|love]] as a predictable instrument of seduction against which [[youth|young]] women must be on guard.

> "her bosom torn between remorse for her crime and love for her vile betrayer" (006-082)

This passage captures the tragic after-life of seduction. Even after abandonment, the victim continues to feel [[affection|love]] for the man who destroyed her — a psychological truth that complicates any simple moral calculus and deepens the reader's sympathy.

> "listen not to the voice of love, unless sanctioned by paternal approbation" (006-088)

The novel's central moral directive on romantic [[affection|love]]. Rowson positions parental authority as the necessary safeguard against counterfeit passion, and sanctions only that [[affection|love]] which passes the test of [[family|familial]] and rational scrutiny.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central il[[passion|lust]]ration of [[affection|love]]'s dangers. Her [[sensibility|feeling]] for [[montraville|Montraville]], described as "a [[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-025), overrides [[duty|filial duty]] and rational [[prudence|caution]]. She [[experience]]s both the intoxicating power of unsanctioned [[affection|love]] and its catastrophic aftermath, ultimately recognizing that her passion "never would have conquered my affection to you, my beloved parents, had I not been encouraged... by one of my own sex" (022-026).

Her tragedy is that she [[affection|love]]s genuinely while being [[affection|love]]d only instrumentally.
- [[montraville]]: Embodies counterfeit [[affection|love]] — the [[seduction|seducer]] who uses the language of "everlasting [[affection|love]] and constancy" (006-049) to achieve his ends. His [[affection|love]] is revealed as appetite and vanity rather than genuine attachment; he abandons [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] when a wealthier match appears. His belated [[regret|remorse]] demonstrates the inadequacy of [[affection|love]] professed too late.
- [[mr-temple]]: With his wife Lucy [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]], models the novel's ideal of [[affection|love]]: founded on genuine esteem, sanctioned by [[family]], and enduring through [[misfortune|adversity]]. His steadfast [[affection|love]] for his daughter even after her ruin — the narrator describes him as grieving "all for the loss of a [[ruin|ruined]], ungrateful child" (032-045) — il[[passion|lust]]rates a parental [[affection|love]] that transcends moral failure.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Embodies [[affection|love]] as active [[charity|compassion]] — the neighbourly [[affection|love]] that the New Testament commands. Her benevolent [[friendship|friends]]hip toward the abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] models a form of [[affection|love]] that is neither romantic nor [[family|familial]] but no less powerful for that.

## Narrative Function

Love operates as the novel's primary engine of plot and its central moral testing ground. The seduction plot is driven by [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[affection|love]] for [[montraville|Montraville]] — or more precisely, by her belief that what she feels is [[affection|love]]. Rowson uses this to advance her stated didactic purpose: by tracing the consequences of unsanctioned [[affection|love]] step by step, she aims to teach [[youth|young]] readers to distinguish genuine affection from seductive counterfeit before it is too late.

The concept also creates the novel's deepest narrative tension: the gap between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s genuine [[affection|love]] and [[montraville|Montraville]]'s strategic performance of it. This asymmetry produces the tragic irony that sustains reader sympathy — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is destroyed not by loving wrongly but by being [[affection|love]]d falsely.
