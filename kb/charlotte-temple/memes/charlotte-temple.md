# Charlotte Temple

The protagonist and title [[reputation|character]] of Rowson's novel. Charlotte is a [[youth|young]] [[england|English]]woman of gentle birth whose seduction by the soldier [[montraville|Montraville]] sets in motion a narrative of ruin, repentance, and death that serves as the novel's central [[prudence|caution]]ary exemplum.

## Role in the Novel

Charlotte Temple is the protagonist whose story drives the novel's didactic purpose. She begins as a sheltered fifteen-year-old boarding-school pupil at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school, where her beauty and sensibility attract the attention of [[montraville|Montraville]] (001-021). Her elopement with him, facilitated by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]], leads to her journey to [[america|America]], her pregnancy, abandonment, and eventual death in poverty (034-068). Rowson presents Charlotte as a fundamentally [[virtue|virtuous]] but inadequately educated [[youth|young]] woman whose "too great sensibility" (005-085) makes her vulnerable to manipulation.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Charlotte is described as beautiful (001-050), [[nature|natural]]ly [[modesty|modest]] (007-044), and possessing [[nature|innate]] [[kindness|goodness]]. Her parents raised her with [[affection|love]] and moral [[education|instruction]], and "such were the parents of Charlotte Temple, who was the only pledge of their mutual [[affection|love]]" (005-085). Her final [[regret|penitence]] demonstrates her fundamentally [[virtue|virtuous]] nature.
- **Flaws**: Her "too great sensibility" — an excess of [[sensibility|feeling]] that overrides [[reason|judgment]] — is her fatal weakness. The narrator notes she has "taken one wrong step" not from wickedness but from in[[experience]] (009-004). She is easily flattered, unable to resist [[montraville|Montraville]]'s attentions (007-037), and lacks the worldly wisdom to recognize [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s manipulations.

## Key Actions

- Attends [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s boarding school, where she is a favourite pupil (006-024).
- Accepts [[montraville|Montraville]]'s letter and money, despite knowing she should refuse (007-075, 007-083).
- Elopes with [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] on the night of what was to be her birthday celebration (009-028).
- Journeys to [[america|America]] with [[montraville|Montraville]], believing his promise of marriage (011-040).
- Is abandoned by [[montraville|Montraville]] in [[new-york|New York]] when he transfers his [[love|affection]]s to [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]] (019-064).
- Be[[friendship|friends]] [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], who provides temporary comfort (017-054).
- Gives birth alone and destitute, then dies in the presence of her father, who arrives too late to save her (034-009, 034-068).

## Relationships

- [[montraville]]: Her [[seduction|seducer]]. Charlotte believes him "a man of honour" and [[trust]]s his promise of marriage (011-040). His abandonment of her causes her ruin.
- [[mr-temple]]: Her father, a man of genuine virtue who forgives Charlotte unconditionally. His arrival at her [[death|deathbed]] provides the novel's emotional climax (034-045).
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Her teacher turned corrupter. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] encourages Charlotte's elopement, preying on her sensibility and in[[experience]] (007-029, 007-090).
- [[belcour]]: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s false [[friendship|friend]], who intercepts the money [[montraville|Montraville]] sends to Charlotte and attempts to [[seduction|seduce]] the destitute [[youth|young]] woman (023-061).
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: A [[charity|compassion]]ate neighbour who be[[friendship|friends]] Charlotte in [[new-york|New York]], offering the only genuine kindness Charlotte receives after her abandonment.

## Moral Significance

Charlotte's story is the novel's central didactic vehicle. Rowson prefaces the novel by addressing "the [[youth|young]] and thoughtless of the fair sex" and offering Charlotte as a warning (P01-018). Charlotte's ruin is presented not as the consequence of [[nature|innate]] depravity but of inadequate education: her parents taught her virtue but not suspicion, leaving her "totally unprepared" for the world's dangers (005-085). Her [[death|deathbed]] repentance and her father's [[mercy|forgiveness]] complete the novel's moral argument — that while social ruin may be irreversible, spiritual redemption through sincere [[regret|penitence]] remains possible.

> "Oh my father! I am not fit for heaven, yet I cannot bear the thoughts of that dreadful place, where there is weeping and wailing and gnashing of teeth" (034-009)

## Fate / Resolution

Charlotte dies in poverty, attended by her father who arrives too late to save her life but in time to receive her final confession. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], having discovered her whereabouts through [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]] (formerly [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]), reaches her in a "miserable apartment" where she "breathed her last" shortly after his arrival. Her death is presented as both tragedy and moral lesson: "the fate of the [[affection|love]]ly Charlotte Temple" (034-068) serves as Rowson's final admonition to her readers.
