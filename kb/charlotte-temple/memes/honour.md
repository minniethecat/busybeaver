# Honour

The quality of principled integrity and uprightness that, in the moral vocabulary of late-eighteenth-century [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction, governs a person's conduct toward others — especially, in Rowson's treatment, a man's conduct toward women. The novel repeatedly exposes the gap between the word's rhetorical use and its genuine practice, making honour one of its most thoroughly deconstructed moral terms.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, honour is presented as the public and social dimension of private virtue. While virtue governs a woman's interior moral state, honour governs the external obligations that others — particularly men — owe to her. Rowson draws a sharp distinction between genuine honour and its counterfeit: the novel is populated with men who invoke the word while acting [[shame|dishonour]]ably, and the narrator consistently foregrounds this irony.

The narrator states that once a woman has "lost sight of the basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the female heart, rests, she grows hardened in guilt" (007-095). Here honour, reputation, and feminine self-respect are bound together as a single moral foundation. When the [[seduction|seducer]] Lewis offers to make a financial settlement on Lucy rather than marry her — what he "impiously termed... a life of honour" (003-074) — Rowson signals through the adverb "impiously" that the misuse of the word is itself a form of blasphemy against the moral order.

The novel's most devastating treatment of the concept comes in its warning about men who invoke honour without practising it. The narrator [[prudence|caution]]s [[youth|young]] women against those who would "[[trust]] entirely to the honour of a man, who perhaps hardly knows the meaning of the word" (006-073). This passage, appearing just before [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement, functions as the novel's central thesis on the concept: honour, when wielded by a [[seduction|seducer]], is a rhetorical weapon rather than a moral commitment.

## Taxonomy of Honour in the Novel

Rowson's [[reputation|character]]s can be mapped onto a three-part taxonomy of honour that structures the novel's moral argument:

| Category | Definition | Characters | Key Passage |
|----------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **Genuine Honour** | Principled action that protects the vulnerable; honour as male obligation performed publicly | [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] declaring "her honour henceforth is mine" (017-013) | (017-011) |
| **Rhetorical Honour** | The language of honour invoked without the intent or capacity to act upon it; honour as seduction tool | [[montraville]] calling himself "a man of honour" (011-040) while persuading [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to elope | (011-040) |
| **Absent Honour** | The outright negation of honour; predation without even the pretence of integrity | [[belcour]], whose "designs were not honourable" (016-078) | (016-078) |

This taxonomy is not merely descriptive but diagnostic: it allows the reader to classify male [[reputation|character]]s by their relationship to the word, distinguishing the genuinely principled from the cynically performative and the outright predatory.

## Key Passages

> "when once a woman... has lost sight of the basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the female heart, rests, she grows hardened in guilt, and will spare no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" (007-092)

This extended moral commentary from Chapter 7 establishes honour as foundational to female identity and social standing. The narrator links honour with reputation and self-respect, arguing that a woman who loses this foundation becomes not merely [[ruin|fall]]en but actively destructive — a predator of the innocence she once possessed. This passage connects [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]'s corruption to her loss of honour, explaining her eagerness to facilitate [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction.

> "abandon their friends, and trust entirely to the honour of a man, who perhaps hardly knows the meaning of the word, and if he does, will be too much the modern man of refinement, to practice it in their favour" (006-073)

The narrator's most explicit warning about the rhetorical abuse of honour. The phrase "modern man of refinement" is deeply ironic: refinement, which should imply moral cultivation, here signifies a fashionable disregard for the obligations honour imposes. This passage immediately precedes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s decision to elope, framing it as a fatal misplacement of [[trust]] in a man's honour.

> "live, as he impiously termed it, a life of honour" (003-074)

The [[seduction|seducer]] Lewis's offer to support Lucy [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] financially without marrying her is called "a life of honour." The narrator's interjection — "as he impiously termed it" — exposes the cynical misappropriation of the term. By pairing "impiously" with "honour," Rowson frames such linguistic deceit as a sin against both God and social order.

> "was with a man of honour, and that it was to insure your felicity by an union with him" (011-040)

[[montraville|Montraville]] assures [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] that her parents will forgive her elopement when they learn she is "with a man of honour" who intends marriage. The irony is structural: [[montraville|Montraville]] uses the language of honour to persuade [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to act in a way that will destroy her own. His self-designation as "a man of honour" is belied by the very act he is committing — persuading a [[youth|young]] woman to abandon her [[family]] and [[trust]] his word alone.

> "an undoubted title to my favour and protection by honourably uniting my fate to hers... that her honour henceforth is mine" (017-011, 017-013)

[[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s declaration before the ship's company represents the novel's positive exemplar of honour. Unlike [[montraville]] and [[belcour]], who use the word to deceive, [[mr-temple|Temple]] acts: he will marry [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], granting her the legal and social protection that restores her honour. His phrase "her honour henceforth is mine" redefines honour as a male responsibility to protect, not exploit, female [[weakness|vulnerability]].

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central case study in the consequences of misplaced [[trust]] in male honour. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] stakes everything on [[montraville|Montraville]]'s word, believing him "a man of honour" (011-040), and discovers too late that his understanding of the term carries no obligation to act upon it. Her [[death|deathbed]] [[regret|penitence]] represents the novel's argument that honour lost through seduction can be spiritually restored, if not socially (032-005).
- [[montraville]]: Embodies the gap between the rhetoric and practice of honour. He repeatedly invokes the language of honour — promising marriage, calling himself "a man of honour" (011-040) — yet abandons [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] when convenience and military duty provide a pretext. His belated [[regret|remorse]] demonstrates that he understands the meaning of honour but lacked the moral resolve to honour his word.
- [[belcour]]: Represents the outright negation of honour. The narrator explicitly warns: "Let not the reader imagine [[belcour|Belcour]]'s designs were honourable" (016-078). Where [[montraville|Montraville]] at least invokes honour rhetorically, [[belcour|Belcour]] abandons even the pretence, scheming to [[seduction|seduce]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] himself once [[montraville|Montraville]] discards her.

- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Having lost her own honour, she becomes an agent of [[shame|dishonour]] for others. The narrator's commentary on the woman who has "lost sight of the basis on which reputation, honour... rests" (007-092) describes [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s trajectory: she "will spare no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" (007-092).
- [[mr-temple]]: The novel's positive exemplar of honour as male obligation. He declares his intention to "honourably unite his fate to hers" (017-011) and warns that "should any man presume to speak in the least disrespectfully of her, I shall not hesitate to pronounce him a scoundrel" (017-014). [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s honour is performative — publicly declared and enforced — not merely asserted.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Demonstrates that honour is not exclusively a male concern. Her [[charity|compassion]]ate protection of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s dignity models a female honour expressed through active [[charity|benevolence]] rather than passive chastity.

## Narrative Function

Honour serves as the novel's diagnostic tool for testing male [[reputation|character]]. Every significant male figure is measured by his relationship to honour: [[mr-temple|Temple]] exemplifies it, [[montraville|Montraville]] invokes but betrays it, [[belcour|Belcour]] disregards it entirely, and Lewis (the [[seduction|seducer]] in the embedded [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] narrative) cynically appropriates its language. This tripartite taxonomy — genuine honour, rhetorical honour, and the absence of honour — structures the novel's moral argument about male conduct.

For female [[reputation|character]]s, honour functions as a warning about misplaced [[trust]]. The narrator repeatedly exhorts [[youth|young]] women not to "[[trust]] entirely to the honour of a man" (006-073), framing female ruin as the consequence not of [[love|passion]] alone but of a fatal epistemological error: believing that men who speak the language of honour will act upon it. Rowson's didactic purpose is clearest here: the novel is designed to teach [[youth|young]] women to distinguish the word from the deed.

The concept also articulates the novel's theology of redemption. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s decision to marry [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — to make "her honour... mine" (017-013) — enacts the possibility that honour can be restored through the principled action of a [[virtue|virtuous]] individual, even after it has been socially destroyed. [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s honour is not merely personal integrity but an active force that repairs what others have broken.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Honour is the social and reputational dimension of virtue; a woman's honour is the public face of her private virtue, and a man's honour is measured by his conduct toward female virtue
- [[love]]: The emotion that honour ought to govern; [[affection|love]] without honour becomes seduction, and honour without [[affection|love]] is mere legalism ([[mr-temple|Temple]] combines both)
- [[seduction]]: The primary threat to female honour; seduction operates through the rhetorical appropriation of honour's language to achieve [[shame|dishonour]]able ends
- [[duty]]: The active obligation that honour imposes; where honour names the principle, duty names the specific obligations it entails
- [[innocence]]: The state of honour intact; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence is what [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], having lost her own honour, seeks to destroy
- [[reputation]]: The social measurement of honour; "reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the female heart" are bound together as a single foundation (007-095)
- [[sensibility]]: The capacity for refined [[sensibility|feeling]] that, when unregulated by honour and reason, makes women vulnerable to the [[seduction|seducer]]'s manipulation of honourable language
