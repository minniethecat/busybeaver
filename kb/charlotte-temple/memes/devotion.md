Devotion in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) appears in two sharply contrasting contexts: the genuine religious observance of the schoolgirls returning from church, and the ironic "devotions" of male suitors to the coquettish [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]. This juxtaposition — sacred devotion versus profane — encapsulates Rowson's moral method: the same term reveals the distance between true [[virtue]] and its counterfeit.

## Key Passages

> "they returned from their devotions" — describing the schoolgirls leaving church, the occasion on which Montraville and Belcour first see Charlotte ([001-013])

> "Among the number who paid their devotions at her shrine, she singled one, a young Ensign of mean birth, indifferent education, and weak intellects" — on La Rue's suitors ([028-056])

## Narrative Function

Rowson's ironic deployment of "devotion" establishes the novel's moral hierarchy. The schoolgirls' return "from their devotions" frames [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] in an aura of religious propriety at the moment of her first encounter with [[montraville|Montraville]] — the [[innocence|innocent]] emerging from church into the path of the [[temptation|tempter]]. By contrast, the "devotions" paid at [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s "shrine" in the novel's final chapter reveal how completely she has inverted moral order: she receives the worship due to [[religion]] itself. The term thus marks the boundary between the world of genuine piety and the world of seductive performance.
