# Julia Franklin

The [[virtue|virtuous]] [[youth|young]] [[america|American]] woman whom [[montraville|Montraville]] marries after abandoning [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. Julia functions as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s structural opposite — her foil in almost every respect — and her presence in the narrative allows Rowson to il[[passion|lust]]rate the conditions under which female virtue can flourish.

## Role in the Novel

Julia is introduced late in the novel as [[montraville|Montraville]]'s new romantic interest. She is described as "the very reverse of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]: she was tall, elegantly formed, and possessed much ease and vivacity" (019-064). While [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story il[[passion|lust]]rates the tragedy of seduction, Julia's story demonstrates the possibility of [[virtue|virtuous]] courtship conducted under proper paternal supervision. Her marriage to [[montraville|Montraville]] is presented as a form of moral rehabilitation for him, though his [[regret|remorse]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death permanently shadows their union.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Julia is prudent, cheerful, and properly educated. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], who was left vulnerable by her parents' excessive [[trust]], Julia is protected by an attentive father and a secure domestic environment. She is capable of genuine [[love|affection]] without the "too great sensibility" that made [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] susceptible to manipulation.
- **Flaws**: Rowson gives Julia no significant moral flaws. As a narrative device, her primary limitation is that she functions more as a symbol of [[virtue|virtuous]] [[america|American]] womanhood than as a psychologically complex [[reputation|character]].

## Key Actions

- Meets [[montraville|Montraville]] at her father's house, where he is introduced as a respectable military officer (019-053).
- Accepts [[montraville|Montraville]]'s courtship and eventual proposal of marriage, conducted under her father's supervision.
- Marries [[montraville|Montraville]], becoming his lawful wife in contrast to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s informal union.
- Lives with [[montraville|Montraville]] after their marriage, her happiness shadowed by his unspoken guilt over [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].

## Relationships

- [[montraville]]: Her husband. Julia's relationship with [[montraville|Montraville]] represents the novel's ideal of legitimate courtship and marriage. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], who [[trust]]ed [[montraville|Montraville]]'s word without any external validation, Julia's union is sanctioned by [[family]] and society.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her unwitting rival and structural foil. Julia never meets [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], but her very existence in the narrative serves to highlight everything [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] lacked: [[family]] protection, worldly [[reason|prudence]], and a secure social position.
- [[mr-franklin]]: Her father, who provides the paternal protection that [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], living in [[england|England]], could not extend to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] in [[america|America]].

## Moral Significance

Julia Franklin embodies the novel's argument that female virtue requires social and [[family|familial]] infrastructure. She is not [[nature|innate]]ly more [[virtue|virtuous]] than [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — she is better protected. Her father's presence, her secure home, and her position in [[america|American]] society create the conditions under which her [[nature|natural]] [[kindness|goodness]] can flourish without being tested by the predatory male conduct that destroys [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. Rowson uses this contrast to argue that the culpability for a [[youth|young]] woman's [[ruin|fall]] belongs not to her alone but to the social structures that fail to protect her.

## Fate / Resolution

Julia marries [[montraville|Montraville]] and presumably lives a respectable life, though the novel hints that [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[regret|remorse]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death casts a permanent shadow over their happiness. She survives the novel as a testament to the protective power of [[family]] and the possibility of [[virtue|virtuous]] womanhood in a supportive environment.
