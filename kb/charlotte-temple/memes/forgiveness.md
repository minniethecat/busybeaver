Forgiveness, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is the moral act by which ruptured [[family]] bonds are restored. It is a Christian virtue that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents ultimately extend to their [[ruin|fall]]en daughter, though its healing arrives only in the novel's closing pages, when [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is already [[death|dying]]. Rowson positions [[mercy|forgiveness]] as the one force capable of [[ruin|undoing]] the damage wrought by seduction and [[deceit|betrayal]] — but she pointedly delays it to underscore the irrevocable social consequences of female ruin.

## Definition

Rowson presents [[mercy|forgiveness]] in explicitly Christian terms. It is the prerogative of parents who have been wronged by a child's disobedience, and it carries the power to restore moral order to a disordered world. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s repeated pleas for "their blessing and [[mercy|forgiveness]]" ([015-036]) frame the act as a two-part ritual: blessing restores the bond of [[affection|love]], [[mercy|forgiveness]] absolves the guilt. The novel's emotional climax hinges not on whether [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] will be forgiven — her parents' [[benevolence|generosity]] is never in doubt — but on whether [[mercy|forgiveness]] can arrive in time to matter.

## Key Passages

> "I will write to my dear parents, entreat their blessing and forgiveness; surely they will not refuse it." ([015-036])

> "How frequently have I pleaded for forgiveness, and entreated to be received again as a child, but my mother would not hear my prayers." ([022-074])

> "Oh! let me but receive your blessing and forgiveness before I die, and I shall be happy." ([022-089])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The supplicant. Her final letters to her parents are extended pleas for [[mercy|forgiveness]], and the granting of that [[mercy|forgiveness]] is her last earthly [[comfort|consolation]]. Her [[distress|suffering]] is framed not as punishment but as the expiation that makes [[mercy|forgiveness]] meaningful ([022-089]).
- [[mr-temple]]: The model of paternal [[mercy|forgiveness]]. He journeys to [[new-york|New York]] to recover his daughter and extends [[mercy|forgiveness]] without hesitation, embo[[death|dying]] the Christian ideal of the merciful father ([024-076]).
- [[mrs-temple]]: Her [[mercy|forgiveness]] is more [[distress|anguish]]ed and delayed — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] reports that "my mother would not hear my prayers" ([022-074]), representing the psychological realism with which Rowson treats the difficulty of forgiving a devastating [[deceit|betrayal]].

## Narrative Function

Forgiveness is the emotional terminus of *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*'s plot. All the novel's action — the seduction, the flight, the [[distress|suffering]], the abandonment — drives toward the question of whether [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] can be forgiven. Rowson uses [[mercy|forgiveness]] to complete her moral argument: the [[seduction|seduce]]d woman is not beyond redemption, but her redemption costs her life. The novel's closing tableau — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [[death|dying]] in her father's arms, forgiven — is the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's definitive gesture, converting the reader's tears into moral [[education|instruction]].

## Related Concepts

- [[repentance]]: Forgiveness and repentance are paired in the novel's moral economy. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] must repent before she can be forgiven, and her [[distress|suffering]] serves as proof of genuine [[regret|contrition]] ([018-073]).
- [[suffering]]: [[suffering|Suffering]] is the medium through which [[mercy|forgiveness]] becomes meaningful. "Sure thy [[suffering|sufferings]] have expiated them," [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is told of her faults ([018-073]).
- [[family]]: The [[family]] is the theatre of [[mercy|forgiveness]] in the novel. It is parents, not society, whose pardon matters.
- [[trust]]: Forgiveness restores the [[trust]] that seduction shattered. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents re-extend [[trust]] by forgiving her, though the restoration is cut short by death.
