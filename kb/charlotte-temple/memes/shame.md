Shame in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the social face of moral ruin — the public emotion that marks the [[ruin|fall]]en woman as [[ruin|fall]]en. Unlike guilt, which is an internal recognition of wrongdoing, shame is the visible, embodied consequence of transgression: it "bows her to the earth" ([018-065]), it reddens the cheek with a "burning blush" ([022-048]), it makes the [[suffering|suffer]]er [[poverty|want]] to "hide my shame and [[grief|sorrow]]" from the world ([027-058]). Rowson deploys shame as both warning and punishment, making it the affective bridge between private sin and public consequence.

## Definition

Rowson's shame is consistently embodied — it manifests as a blush, a bowed posture, a [[passion|desire]] to hide. When [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] feels "a glow of conscious shame" vermilion her face ([021-011]), the physiological response marks her moral state with inescapable visibility. The novel's rhetoric of shame oscillates between the narrator's sympathetic description of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] and the didactic function of shame as deterrent: the reader is meant to see [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s shame and recoil from the conduct that produced it. Crucially, Rowson distinguishes between the "blush of shame" that signals conscience ([002-043]) and the hardened shamelessness — "triumph in her shame" ([028-018]) — that marks [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s complete moral degradation.

## Key Passages

> "I can only behold the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and remorse!" — Charlotte on the verge of elopement ([012-044])

> "Shame bows her to the earth, remorse tears her distracted mind, and guilt, poverty, and disease close the dreadful scene" ([018-065])

> "A glow of conscious shame vermillioned Charlotte's face" ([021-011])

> "The burning blush of shame die my cheeks while I write it" — Charlotte's letter to her mother ([022-048])

> "Indignation and shame tinges my cheek while I write" — the narrator's own confession of shame ([027-121])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Shame is her constant [[adversity|affliction]]. From the first "glow of conscious shame" in [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s presence to her [[death|dying]] wish to "hide my shame and [[grief|sorrow]]," [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] embodies shame as the [[ruin|fall]]en woman's lived [[experience]] ([021-011], [027-058]).
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The counter-example who has overcome shame. Her ability to "triumph in her shame" ([028-018]) represents, for Rowson, the deepest moral corruption — the point at which even the blush of conscience has been extinguished.
- [[montraville]]: Shame as belated self-recognition. His shame comes too late but serves the novel's warning to male readers about the consequences of seduction.

## Narrative Function

Shame is the novel's primary mechanism for converting sexual transgression into readable moral drama. By making [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s shame visible — through blushes, tears, bowed posture, and [[distress|anguish]]ed letters — Rowson ensures that the consequences of seduction are legible even to readers who have never [[experience]]d them. Shame also structures the novel's gender politics: the [[ruin|fall]]en woman is permanently marked by shame, while her [[seduction|seducer]] can postpone his reckoning. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s shame is immediate and inescapable; [[montraville|Montraville]]'s is deferred and incomplete.

## Related Concepts

- [[guilt]]: Shame is guilt's public face. [[guilt|Guilt]] is the internal knowledge of wrongdoing; shame is its social manifestation.
- [[remorse]]: The emotional twin of shame — "shame bows her to the earth, [[regret|remorse]] tears her distracted mind" ([018-065]) — they operate in tandem to punish the [[ruin|fall]]en.
- [[honour]]: Shame is honour's opposite. The loss of honour produces shame, and shame is the evidence that honour has been forfeited.
- [[reputation]]: Shame's social dimension. [[reputation|Reputation]] is what is lost; shame is what is felt as a result of that loss.
