# America

The "new world" to which [[montraville|Montraville]] transports [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and where her tragedy reaches its conclusion. America is the novel's most symbolically complex space: it is at once a place of [[virtue|virtuous]] domesticity (the [[mr-franklin|Franklin]]s), sexual danger (the seduction plot's conclusion), and redemptive possibility ([[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s journey to forgive his daughter).

## Location

America, specifically the [[new-york|New-York]] region, is the setting for the novel's second half. As a British colony — the novel was published in 1791, shortly after American independence — it occupies an ambiguous position in Rowson's symbolic geography. It is [[england|English]] in language and culture but lacks the established social institutions, the dense networks of [[family]] and reputation, that structure [[england|English]] society.

## Narrative Role

America serves as the novel's space of consequences. Key narrative functions:

- [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s transatlantic journey brings her to America, where she is removed from all sources of protection (013-050).
- [[montraville|Montraville]]'s meeting with [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]] in America introduces the novel's American ideal of womanhood.
- [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s abandonment, pregnancy, and death all occur in America.
- [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s journey to America represents the novel's ultimate act of paternal devotion — crossing the ocean to forgive and reclaim his daughter.
- [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s daughter is born in America but taken to [[england|England]], completing a transatlantic circuit.

## Characters Associated

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Brought to America by [[montraville|Montraville]] and dies there, a victim of the isolation the new world imposes.
- [[julia-franklin]]: An American woman whose virtue flourishes under the protection of her American father — demonstrating that America can sustain virtue when [[family]] is present.
- [[mr-franklin]]: An American patriarch whose protective presence enables his daughter's virtue.
- [[montraville]]: Operates in America as a military officer, pursuing new romantic interests while his obligations to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] go unfulfilled.

## Symbolic Significance

America is the novel's most symbolically layered space. On one hand, it is the place of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|destruction]] — the isolation that enables [[montraville|Montraville]]'s abandonment and [[belcour|Belcour]]'s predation. On the other, it is the home of the [[mr-franklin|Franklin]]s, whose domestic virtue suggests that the new world can sustain the moral order when proper [[family]] structures are present. Rowson, who herself lived in America, avoids a simple old-world/new-world binary: America's danger lies not in any intrinsic quality but in its distance from the social networks that protect female virtue. Remove the [[family]], and any place becomes dangerous.

> "as Charlotte Temple, or to be suffered to take her from her friends" (013-050)
