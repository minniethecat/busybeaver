# Virtue

The governing moral concept of Rowson's novel, operating as both an ideal to be cultivated and a vulnerable possession that can be lost through seduction and folly. In the moral vocabulary of late-eighteenth-century [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction, virtue encompasses chastity, [[duty|filial duty]], religious observance, and rational self-governance.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, virtue functions as the novel's central moral currency. It is presented as a [[youth|young]] woman's most precious possession — one that, once lost, cannot be fully recovered. Rowson defines virtue not merely as sexual chastity but as a comprehensive moral quality rooted in religious principle, rational self-discipline, and obedience to parental authority. The narrative repeatedly contrasts "the precepts of religion and virtue" (006-093) with the seductive appeals of [[love|passion]] and flattery. [[education|Education]] in virtue is presented as a [[youth|young]] woman's primary defence: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s mother "possessed an elevated understanding" and sought to instill virtue in her daughter (016-046), while [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragic [[ruin|fall]] is attributed in part to her removal from this virtuous guidance.

The novel's moral framework aligns [[regret|penitence]] with virtue: the narrator explicitly states "how nearly [[regret|penitence]] is allied to virtue" (025-045), suggesting that sincere [[regret|remorse]] can restore a [[ruin|fall]]en woman to moral standing, even if social reputation cannot be recovered. This theological dimension — the possibility of redemption through [[regret|penitence]] — distinguishes Rowson's treatment from a purely punitive morality.

## Key Passages

> "when it runs counter to the precepts of religion and virtue." (006-093)

This passage, from the narrator's early moral commentary, establishes virtue and religion as the twin foundations of proper conduct. The coupling of "religion and virtue" recurs throughout the novel, grounding moral action in both revealed and [[nature|natural]] law.

> "how nearly penitence is allied to virtue." (025-045)

Spoken in the context of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s post-seduction [[distress|suffering]], this line articulates the novel's theology of redemption. It positions sincere [[regret|penitence]] not as the opposite of virtue but as its close relation — a crucial softening of what might otherwise be a rigidly punitive moral scheme.

> "when so justly impressed with the value of virtue, how could you, my dear Charlotte, suffer yourself to be led away by a passion?" (026-057)

This reproach, delivered by the virtuous [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], captures the novel's central puzzle: if [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] *knows* the value of virtue, why does she fail to uphold it? The question points to the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's [[fear|anxiety]] about the gap between moral knowledge and moral action.

> "dragged you from the calm repose which innocence and virtue ever enjoy" (026-062)

The association of virtue with "calm repose" and innocence reflects the novel's ideal of moral tranquility — a state [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has forfeited through her [[ruin|fall]]. The verb "dragged" emphasizes the violence done to her moral and psychological state.

> "he, having triumphed over the virtue of the artless cottager" (027-126)

This passage, referring to [[belcour|Belcour]]'s seduction of a rural woman, reveals how the novel frames seduction as a predatory contest in which male cunning "triumphs over" female virtue. The phrase "artless cottager" emphasizes the victim's [[nature|natural]] innocence and lack of worldly defence.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- **[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]**: The novel's central case study in the loss and partial recovery of virtue. Educated in virtuous principles by her mother, she [[ruin|fall]]s through a combination of naivety, [[love|passion]], and the manipulations of others. Her [[death|deathbed]] [[regret|penitence]] enacts the novel's argument that virtue can be spiritually restored even when social and physical consequences are irreversible (032-005).
- **Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]] ([[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s mother)**: Embodies mature, stable virtue grounded in religion and reason. Her example establishes the standard [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] fails to meet, and her [[mercy|forgiveness]] at the novel's close extends the possibility of redemption (016-046).
- **[[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]**: The novel's most thoroughgoing inversion of virtue. As [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s French teacher and later [[montraville|Montraville]]'s accomplice, she actively subverts the virtuous principles she was employed to teach, choosing pleasure and social advancement over moral integrity (014-089).
- **[[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]**: Functions as the voice of virtuous [[friendship|friends]]hip, reproaching [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] for her [[ruin|fall]] while extending [[charity|compassion]] and practical aid. Her speeches contain some of the novel's most explicit moral [[education|instruction]] (026-038).
- **[[montraville|Montraville]]**: Represents the male threat to female virtue. Though the novel grants him moments of conscience, his actions — seduction, abandonment, and ultimate [[regret|remorse]] too late — il[[passion|lust]]rate how male [[love|passion]] and irresolution destroy the virtue Rowson urges [[youth|young]] women to protect.

## Narrative Function

Virtue serves as the novel's primary didactic instrument. Rowson states her moral purpose in the Preface: the tale is designed "for the perusal of the [[youth|young]] and thoughtless of the fair sex" as a warning. The plot is structured as a demonstration: a virtuous [[youth|young]] woman, inadequately guarded, is [[seduction|seduce]]d, abandoned, and dies in [[regret|penitence]] — a sequence intended to "awaken that [[affection|love]] of virtue" (026-038) in the reader through the mechanism of [[sensibility|sentiment]]al identification and vicarious [[distress|suffering]].

The concept operates on multiple levels: as a **warning** (the consequences of lost virtue are catastrophic and irreversible in worldly terms), an **ideal** (the calm repose of innocence is presented as the [[nature|natural]] and desirable state for women), and a **source of narrative tension** (the gap between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s virtuous education and her actual choices drives the plot). Rowson's innovation within the seduction novel tradition is her emphasis on [[regret|penitence]] as a form of recovered virtue, offering readers both a [[prudence|caution]]ary tale and a model of Christian redemption.

## Related Concepts

- [[honour]]: Closely allied to virtue but carrying stronger social and reputational connotations; a woman's honour is the public face of her private virtue
- [[innocence]]: The state that precedes the loss of virtue; associated with the "calm repose" [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] forfeits
- [[seduction]]: The primary threat to virtue; the process by which male cunning "triumphs over" female moral defences
- [[duty]]: The active component of virtue — the obligations to parents, religion, and social norms that virtue requires one to fulfill
- [[repentance]]: The mechanism by which virtue can be spiritually recovered after a [[ruin|fall]]; "nearly allied" to virtue itself
- [[sensibility]]: The capacity for refined [[sensibility|feeling]] that, when unregulated by virtue and reason, becomes a [[weakness|vulnerability]] to seduction
- [[folly]]: The failure of [[reason|prudence]] that makes virtue vulnerable; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "[[folly|foolish]]" choices enable her seduction
