Independence, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is a middle-class ideal framed in explicitly economic terms. To be independent is to owe nothing to anyone — to be free from the compulsion that financial dependency creates. Rowson presents this independence as the necessary foundation of [[virtue|virtuous]] conduct: those who are dependent on others for their maintenance cannot freely choose the right. The novel's villains exploit this truth, using promises of financial support to ensnare the vulnerable.

## Definition

Rowson defines independence through its opposite: dependency. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] "resolved to p[[modesty|reserve]] independence, to marry where the heart was engaged, without the intervention of interested views" ([002-012]). Independence here is not wealth but the [[liberty|freedom]] from obligation that allows moral choice. The "genteel independence" ([010-053]) that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s grandfather enjoys is a [[modesty|modest]] sufficiency that protects him from the necessity of selling his principles. Rowson carefully distinguishes this [[virtue|virtuous]] independence from both poverty (which creates dependency) and excessive wealth (which corrupts). The novel's moral economy places independence at the centre: the dependent are always vulnerable, and [[weakness|vulnerability]] is the [[seduction|seducer]]'s opportunity.

## Key Passages

> "He resolved to preserve independence, to marry where the heart was engaged, without the intervention of interested views." ([002-012])

> "I would preserve you in that state of independence I have so long laboured to place you." ([010-027])

> "Having a genteel independence, it was by no means probable that he would marry at all." ([010-053])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mr-temple]]: The novel's exemplar of [[virtue|virtuous]] independence. His resolution to marry for [[affection|love]] rather than [[fate|fortune]] ([002-012]) is presented as the act of a man whose economic independence frees him to follow his heart and his principles.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy is in part a tragedy of dependency. Removed from her father's protection and economic support, she becomes dependent on [[montraville|Montraville]] — a dependency that strips her of the power to refuse his advances or leave him when he abandons her.
- [[montraville]]: Exploits the promise of independence to [[seduction|seduce]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], offering her a life free from her parents' authority. The irony is that his offer of "independence" from parents creates a far more dangerous dependency on him.
- [[captain-eldridge]]: His poverty strips him of independence, forcing him to accept [[benevolence|charity]] and placing his [[family]] in circumstances that make his daughter vulnerable. His story is the novel's object lesson in what happens when independence is lost.

## Narrative Function

Independence serves as the economic dimension of Rowson's moral argument. The novel insists that virtue requires a material foundation: one cannot be good while dependent on the wicked. This linking of moral and economic independence is [[reputation|character]]istic of the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's middle-class ideology. Rowson's readers, many of them [[youth|young]] women without independent means, are warned that losing their moral independence follows almost inevitably from losing their economic independence — and that the [[seduction|seducer]]'s first move is always to sever the victim from her economic protectors.

## Related Concepts

- [[liberty]]: Economic independence is the foundation of true liberty in Rowson's thought. Without independence, liberty is merely nominal.
- [[wealth]]: Independence occupies the [[virtue|virtuous]] middle ground between poverty and excessive wealth. It is sufficiency, not [[wealth|riches]], that Rowson celebrates.
- [[prudence]]: The virtue that p[[modesty|reserve]]s independence. Prudent management of resources maintains the independence that imprudent choices destroy.
- [[family]]: The [[family]] is the primary guarantor of a [[youth|young]] woman's independence. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s separation from her [[family]] is also her separation from the economic protection that independence requires.
