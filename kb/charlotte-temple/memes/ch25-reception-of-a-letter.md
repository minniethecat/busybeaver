[025-001] “AND where now is our poor [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]?” said [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] one evening, as

[025-002] the cold blasts of autumn whistled rudely over the heath, and the yellow

[025-003] appearance of the distant wood, spoke the near approach of winter. In

[025-004] vain the cheerful fire blazed on the hearth, in vain was he surrounded

[025-005] by all the comforts of life; the parent was still alive in his heart,

[025-006] and when he thought that perhaps his once darling child was ere this

[025-007] exposed to all the miseries of [[poverty|want]] in a distant land, without a [[friendship|friend]]

[025-008] to sooth and comfort her, without the benignant look of [[charity|compassion]] to

[025-009] cheer, or the angelic voice of [[compassion|pity]] to pour the balm of [[comfort|consolation]] on

[025-010] her wounded heart; when he thought of this, his whole soul dissolved in

[025-011] tenderness; and while he wiped the tear of [[distress|anguish]] from the eye of his

[025-012] patient, uncomplaining [[lucy|Lucy]], he struggled to suppress the sympathizing

[025-013] drop that started in his own.

## Section 14

[025-014] “Oh, my poor girl,” said [[mrs-temple|Mrs. Temple]], “how must she be altered, else

[025-015] surely she would have relieved our agonizing minds by one line to

[025-016] say she lived--to say she had not quite forgot the parents who almost

[025-017] idolized her.”

[025-018] “Gracious heaven,” said Mr. Temple, starting from his seat, “I, who would

[025-019] wish to be a father, to [[experience]] the agonizing pangs inflicted on a

[025-020] parent's heart by the [[gratitude|ingratitude]] of a child?” Mrs. Temple wept: her

[025-021] father took her hand; he would have said, “be comforted my child,”

[025-022] but the words died on his tongue. The sad silence that ensued was

[025-023] interrupted by a loud rap at the door. In a moment a servant entered

[025-024] with a letter in his hand.

[025-025] Mrs. Temple took it from him: she cast her eyes upon the superscription;

[025-026] she knew the writing. “'Tis Charlotte,” said she, eagerly breaking

[025-027] the seal, “she has not quite forgot us.” But before she had half gone

[025-028] through the contents, a sudden sickness seized her; she grew cold and

[025-029] giddy, and puffing it into her husband's hand, she cried--“Read it: I

[025-030] cannot.” Mr. Temple attempted to read it aloud, but frequently paused

[025-031] to give vent to his tears. “My poor deluded child,” said he, when he had

[025-032] finished.

[025-033] “Oh, shall we not forgive the dear [[repentance|penitent]]?” said Mrs. Temple. “We

[025-034] must, we will, my [[affection|love]]; she is willing to return, and 'tis our duty to

[025-035] receive her.”

[025-036] “Father of [[compassion|mercy]],” said [[captain-eldridge|Mr. Eldridge]], raising his clasped hands, “let

[025-037] me but live once more to see the dear wanderer restored to her afflicted

[025-038] parents, and take me from this world of [[melancholy|sorrow]] whenever it seemeth best

[025-039] to thy wisdom.”

[025-040] “Yes, we will receive her,” said Mr. Temple; “we will endeavour to heal

[025-041] her wounded spirit, and speak peace and comfort to her agitated soul. I

[025-042] will write to her to return immediately.'

[025-043] “Oh!” said Mrs. Temple, “I would if possible fly to her, support and

[025-044] cheer the dear sufferer in the approaching hour of distress, and tell

[025-045] her how nearly [[regret|penitence]] is allied to virtue. Cannot we go and conduct

[025-046] her home, my love?” continued she, laying her hand on his arm. “My

[025-047] father will surely forgive our absence if we go to bring home his

[025-048] darling.”

[025-049] “You cannot go, my Lucy,” said Mr. Temple: “the delicacy of your frame

[025-050] would but poorly sustain the fatigue of a long voyage; but I will go and

[025-051] bring the gentle [[repentance|penitent]] to your arms: we may still see many years of

[025-052] happiness.”

[025-053] The struggle in the bosom of Mrs. Temple between maternal and conjugal

[025-054] tenderness was long and painful. At length the former triumphed, and she

[025-055] consented that her husband should set forward to [[new-york|New-York]] by the first

[025-056] opportunity: she wrote to her Charlotte in the tenderest, most consoling

[025-057] manner, and looked forward to the [[happiness|happy]] hour, when she should again

[025-058] embrace her, with the most animated hope.
