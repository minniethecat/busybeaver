# Friendship

The bond that should protect the vulnerable but can also destroy them: in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, friendship is presented in its dual nature — as a moral ideal of disinterested [[charity|benevolence]] and as a mask that conceals the most dangerous forms of predation.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, friendship operates along a spectrum from genuine moral solidarity to calculated [[deceit|deception]]. At its best, friendship is presented as a bond of mutual [[love|affection]] grounded in shared virtue and expressed through acts of practical [[charity|benevolence]]. The novel's exemplary friendships — between [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] and [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]], between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] — are [[reputation|character]]ised by constancy, selflessness, and a willingness to act on behalf of the friend even at personal cost.

Yet friendship in the novel is also profoundly dangerous, because its forms can be counterfeited. The narrator warns explicitly of those who act "under the mask of friendship" (022-029), using the language and gestures of sincere attachment to draw the innocent toward ruin. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]'s relationship with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is the novel's most sustained study in false friendship: she presents herself as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s confidante and advisor while systematically dismantling the [[youth|young]] woman's moral defences. The novel thus insists that true friendship is known not by its professions but by its fruits — by whether it leads toward virtue or away from it.

The "unalterable friendship" (020-013) that [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] extends to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], after the latter's [[ruin|fall]], represents the novel's ideal: a friendship that survives [[shame|disgrace]], that sees past social condemnation to the [[distress|suffering]] person beneath, and that acts to restore rather than to judge. This friendship is the practical expression of the novel's core moral principle that "the heart that is truly [[virtue|virtuous]] is ever inclined to [[compassion|pity]] and forgive" (020-124).

## Key Passages

> "of friendship appear so open and disinterested, I will relate every" (002-092)

The narrator's [[reputation|character]]isation of [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s friendship toward [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] establishes the novel's ideal: friendship is "open" — transparent, without hidden motive — and "disinterested" — seeking the good of the other rather than personal advantage. These two qualities become the standard against which all other professions of friendship are measured.

> "friendship, and they became almost inseparable companions." (003-011)

The friendship between Lewis ([[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s son) and his schoolfellow is presented as [[nature|natural]] and unforced — a bond that arises from shared virtue and mutual regard. Its tragic outcome — Lewis's death defending his friend — establishes that genuine friendship may demand the ultimate sacrifice.

> "unalterable friendship of" (020-013)

The word "unalterable" is crucial to the novel's conception of true friendship. Unlike the shifting allegiances of the fashionable world, genuine friendship is constant — it does not waver when social circumstances change or when the friend [[ruin|fall]]s into [[shame|disgrace]]. [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s friendship for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] models this constancy.

> "under the mask of friendship, drew me on to ruin. Yet think not your" (022-029)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s retrospective diagnosis of her seduction identifies false friendship as its mechanism. The phrase "mask of friendship" captures the novel's darker insight: that the forms and language of sincere attachment can be deployed as instruments of predation. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] did not announce herself as an enemy; she presented herself as a friend, and it was precisely this presentation that made her influence so destructive.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- **[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]**: The victim of false friendship. Her [[trust]]ing nature — itself a virtue — makes her incapable of recognising that [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s professions of friendship are counterfeit. Her tragedy is in part a tragedy of misplaced [[trust]]: she gave the loyalty due to a true friend to one who meant her harm (022-029).
- **[[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]**: The novel's embodiment of genuine friendship. She extends "her friendship" (020-113) to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] after the latter's [[ruin|fall]], offering not [[reason|judgment]] but [[charity|compassion]] and practical aid. Her friendship is the novel's answer to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s [[deceit|betrayal]] — proof that true friendship exists and can be a force for restoration.
- **[[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]**: The novel's anatomy of false friendship. She cultivates [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[trust]], presents herself as a confidante, and uses her influence to guide [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] toward the choices that will destroy her. Her friendship is wholly instrumental — a tool for advancing her own interests at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s expense.
- **[[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]**: Demonstrates friendship as active [[charity|benevolence]]. His "open and disinterested" (002-092) regard for [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] translates immediately into practical action — financial assistance, emotional support, and ultimately the restoration of [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]]'s circumstances. His friendship is the standard by which all others are measured.

## Narrative Function

Friendship serves a structural role in the novel's moral economy. The contrast between true and false friendship — between [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s constancy and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s predation, between [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s [[charity|benevolence]] and [[montraville|Montraville]]'s self-interest — maps onto the novel's larger moral geography. Friendship is the social form that virtue takes in relation to others, and its corruption is the social form of vice.

The concept also functions didactically. Rowson's [[youth|young]] female readers are being trained to discriminate: to recognise that not everyone who calls themselves a friend is one, and that true friendship is known by its tendency to strengthen virtue rather than to weaken it. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s failure to make this discrimination is presented as one of the causes of her [[ruin|fall]] — a warning to the reader to be more discerning.

The novel's insistence on the possibility of post-lapsarian friendship — [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s willingness to befriend a [[ruin|fall]]en woman — is also theologically significant. It enacts in social terms the novel's argument that [[regret|penitence]] restores one to the possibility of moral relationship, even if social reputation cannot be recovered.
