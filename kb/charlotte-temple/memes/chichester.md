# Chichester

The [[england|English]] city whose boarding school, presided over by [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]], provides the setting for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s education and seduction. Chichester represents the protective institutional space that, in Rowson's analysis, proves disastrously insufficient to guard female virtue.

## Location

Chichester is a cathedral city in West Sussex, near the south coast of [[england|England]] and close to [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]]. Its proximity to the [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] barracks is narratively significant: the short distance between the military world and the school enables the predatory encounters that drive the plot. The city's respectability — its cathedral, its established institutions — provides a veneer of safety that the novel systematically exposes as illusory.

## Narrative Role

Chichester is the setting for the novel's first act — the seduction and elopement. Key events:

- [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] attends [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school in Chichester as a boarding pupil.
- [[montraville|Montraville]] first sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] when walking near the school (001-021).
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] facilitates clandestine meetings between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and [[montraville|Montraville]] in gardens near the school (006-032).
- [[montraville|Montraville]] plans a birthday celebration for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] at the school (008-003).
- [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] elope from the school on the night of the planned celebration (009-028).

## Characters Associated

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Educated at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school in Chichester, where she is a favourite pupil (006-024).
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Employed as a teacher at the Chichester school, where she corrupts rather than educates (006-007).
- [[madame-du-pont]]: Headmistress of the school in Chichester, whose authority proves insufficient to protect her pupils (001-016).
- [[montraville]]: Visits Chichester to pursue [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], exploiting the school's proximity to his barracks.

## Symbolic Significance

Chichester embodies the novel's critique of institutional education. The boarding school — a respected establishment in a respectable cathedral city — appears to offer protection but in fact exposes [[youth|young]] women to moral danger. Rowson's point is structural rather than personal: no institution, however well-intentioned, can substitute for vigilant parental protection. Chichester's school, with its French headmistress and its corrupt French teacher, also carries a subtle anti-Gallic subtext common in [[england|English]] [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction of the period.
