Gratitude in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is not merely a pleasant emotion but a binding [[duty|moral obligation]]. The novel treats ingratitude — the failure to honour and reciprocate kindness — as perhaps the most damning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s transgressions. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement is repeatedly framed not just as a sexual [[ruin|fall]] but as an act of filial ingratitude, a refusal to honour the [[affection|love]] and sacrifice of her parents. Rowson positions gratitude as the affective glue of [[family]] and society; its violation is the breach through which all other disasters enter.

## Definition

Rowson presents gratitude as an active [[virtue|moral virtue]] that governs the relationship between benefactor and recipient. [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s "gratitude for her deliverer" ([004-076]) models the proper response to kindness received. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s failure is described as "returning their kindness with ingratitude" ([011-031]), casting her elopement not as a crime of [[love|passion]] but as a failure of moral accounting. By the novel's end, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] herself names "her ingratitude to her parents as what lay most heavily upon her mind" ([033-075]), reinforcing Rowson's argument that ingratitude, more than sexual transgression, is the deepest wound.

## Key Passages

> "To see the expressive eyes of Lucy beaming at once with pleasure for her father's deliverance, and gratitude for her deliverer" ([004-076])

> "Was it not for the fear of giving pain to the best of parents, and returning their kindness with ingratitude, I would follow you through every danger" ([011-031])

> "Or has she, justly incensed at my ingratitude, driven the unhappy Charlotte from her remembrance?" ([022-015])

> "Can a parent's heart be torn by the ingratitude of a child?" ([025-020])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central example of ingratitude. Her flight from her parents is repeatedly [[reputation|character]]ized as a failure of gratitude, and her [[death|dying]] letters dwell on "her ingratitude to her parents as what lay most heavily upon her mind" ([033-075]).
- [[mr-temple]]: Models the proper response to ingratitude — [[mercy|forgiveness]] rather than [[revenge|retaliation]]. His journey to recover [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] enacts the parental [[affection|love]] that ingratitude could not extinguish.
- [[captain-eldridge]]: Embodies proper gratitude. His thankfulness toward [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] for delivering him from debtor's prison establishes the novel's positive model of the virtue ([004-076]).
- [[lucy]]: [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s daughter, whose "expressive eyes" beam with gratitude, serving as the novel's visual icon of the virtue in its purest form ([004-076]).

## Narrative Function

Gratitude and its violation structure the novel's moral argument. Rowson uses ingratitude as the master term for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s offence, subordinating sexual transgression to a broader failure of social and [[family|familial]] obligation. This rhetorical strategy allows Rowson to frame the seduction plot within the universal language of duty and reciprocity, making [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] a [[prudence|caution]]ary tale about the consequences of forgetting what one owes. The final chapters' emphasis on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s self-accusation of ingratitude ([033-075]) confirms the concept's centrality to the novel's moral design.

## Related Concepts

- [[filial-piety]]: The specific form of gratitude owed by children to parents. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s violation of filial [[religion|piety]] is the novel's originating sin.
- [[duty]]: Gratitude is a species of duty — the obligation to honour and reciprocate kindness received.
- [[guilt]]: Ingratitude produces guilt, and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[guilt|guilty]] conscience is largely a conscience of ingratitude.
- [[forgiveness]]: The remedy for ingratitude. Parental [[mercy|forgiveness]] restores the bond that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ingratitude severed.
