# Seduction

The central villainous act in Rowson's moral universe, encompassing not merely sexual transgression but a calculated process of emotional manipulation, false promise, and [[deceit|betrayal]] that destroys female innocence and devastates the [[family|familial]] bonds the novel holds sacred.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, seduction is defined less by a single act than by a sequence: flattery and attention awaken vanity and [[love|affection]] in a [[youth|young]] woman; specious promises of marriage and honour disarm her moral defences; isolation from [[family]] and [[friendship|friends]] removes external restraint; and once the seducer's [[passion|desire]] is gratified, the victim is abandoned to shame, poverty, and death. Rowson presents seduction not as a mutual [[ruin|fall]] but as an asymmetrical crime — the seducer is a "monster" (006-086), while the seduced is a victim whose chief fault is insufficient vigilance and excessive sensibility.

The narrator's voice rises to its most [[love|passion]]ate when denouncing seducers: she imagines "the good old man stooping to raise the weeping [[repentance|penitent]], while every tear from her eye is numbered by drops from his bleeding heart," and her "bosom glows with honest [[anger|indignation]]" as she wishes for "power to extirpate those monsters of seduction from the earth" (006-080, 006-086). This passage, one of the most rhetorically heightened in the novel, establishes seduction not as a private failing but as a social evil warranting righteous fury.

The novel's anatomy of seduction also emphasizes its gendered asymmetry. A seduced woman "knows there is no tie but honour, and that, in a man who has been [[guilt|guilty]] of seduction, is but very feeble: he may leave her in a moment to shame and [[poverty|want]]" (018-058). The seducer's bond, based on appetite rather than obligation, is inherently fragile — the very man who lacks the honour to resist seducing a [[virtue|virtuous]] girl will lack the honour to remain with her afterward.

## Key Passages

> "my bosom glows with honest indignation, and I wish for power to extirpate those monsters of seduction from the earth." (006-086)

The novel's most rhetorically intense moral pronouncement. Rowson's choice of "monsters" elevates seduction from a personal vice to a species of [[cruelty|inhumanity]], while "extirpate" connotes rooting out an infestation — seducers are not merely misguided men but a plague upon society.

> "she knows there is no tie but honour, and that, in a man who has been guilty of seduction, is but very feeble" (018-058)

This passage, from the narrator's extended reflection on the condition of the [[ruin|fall]]en woman, anatomizes the contractual absurdity of seduction. The only guarantee the victim holds — the seducer's honour — is by definition absent, since a man of honour would not have seduced her in the first place.

> "I never once reflected that the man who could stoop to seduction, would not hesitate to forsake the wretched object of his passion, whenever his capricious heart grew weary of her tenderness." (022-034)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s retrospective clarity, voiced in her letter to her mother, captures the tragic pattern of the novel: seduction is enabled by the victim's failure to "reflect," but the logic that makes seduction possible also makes abandonment inevitable. The word "stoop" frames seduction as a degradation of the seducer as well.

> "of Charlotte's seduction. 'Poor girl,' said he, 'I will at least write and bid her adieu'" (026-037)

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s moment of conscience on the eve of his marriage to [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]]. His [[compassion|pity]] — "Poor girl" — is genuine but impotent; the [[regret|remorse]] of the seducer arrives too late to undo the harm. This passage illuminates Rowson's view that seduction destroys not only the victim but also the moral integrity of the perpetrator.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[montraville]]: The novel's principal seducer. Though presented as "generous in his disposition" and "good-natured almost to a fault" (009-047), his pursuit of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is driven by vanity and impulse rather than [[affection|love]]. He uses flattery, feigned distress, and false promises of marriage to overcome her resistance (011-037), then abandons her when a wealthier match appears. His eventual [[regret|remorse]] (026-037) is sincere but ineffectual — a pattern Rowson uses to demonstrate that seduction's consequences cannot be undone by belated conscience.

- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The female seducer whose role the novel treats as uniquely pernicious. As [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s teacher and confidante, she abuses a position of [[trust]] to encourage the clandestine relationship, dismiss [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s scruples, and ultimately persuade her to elope. [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] is "shocked to the soul to find how large a share [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] had in the seduction of this amiable girl" (021-055). [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] embodies the novel's warning that women can be "more dangerous" seducers than men (007-096).
- [[belcour]]: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s companion and enabler, whose "dissipated, thoughtless, and capricious" [[reputation|character]] (009-037) facilitates the seduction plot. He entertains [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] while [[montraville|Montraville]] pursues [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], and later plays a decisive role in ensuring [[montraville|Montraville]]'s abandonment of her. His "triumph over the virtue of the artless cottager" (027-126) confirms him as an unrepentant serial seducer.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: The object and victim of seduction. Her tragedy il[[passion|lust]]rates the seduction sequence from the victim's perspective: vanity aroused by attention (001-025), moral qu[[charity|alms]] overcome by persuasion (007-090), [[duty|filial duty]] abandoned for [[love|passion]] (011-050), and the inevitable aftermath of abandonment, poverty, and death. Her belated recognition — that "the man who could stoop to seduction, would not hesitate to forsake" (022-034) — arrives only when the damage is irreparable.

## Narrative Function

Seduction is the engine of the novel's plot and the primary vehicle for its didactic purpose. The entire narrative arc — from [[montraville|Montraville]]'s first glimpse of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] through her death in childbirth — constitutes a seduction plot in the tradition of the eighteenth-century [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel. Rowson adapts this convention to serve an explicitly [[prudence|caution]]ary function: by tracing seduction step by step, she aims to teach [[youth|young]] readers to recognize its early stages and resist before it is too late.

The narrator's Preface instructs readers to regard the tale "not merely the effusion of Fancy, but as a reality," and her direct addresses to "my dear girls" (006-087) frame seduction as an ever-present danger requiring constant vigilance. Seduction operates in the novel on three levels: as a **plot mechanism** ([[montraville|Montraville]]'s pursuit and abandonment of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] drives the action), as a **moral warning** (the catastrophic consequences il[[passion|lust]]rate the stakes of [[prudence|imprudence]]), and as a **social critique** (the double standard that punishes the seduced woman while the seducer may "marry and forsake her for ever" (018-059) is exposed, if not remedied).

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: The quality seduction destroys; the novel's central moral standard against which the seducer's crime is measured
- [[honour]]: The tie that seduction exploits and dissolves — a seducer's honour is by definition absent, leaving the victim without recourse
- [[innocence]]: The state seduction preys upon; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence makes her vulnerable to manipulation and unable to foresee the consequences of her actions
- [[folly]]: The cognitive failure that enables seduction; [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s inability to "reflect" on the likely consequences of her choices
- [[sensibility]]: The capacity for refined [[sensibility|feeling]] that, improperly governed, becomes an entry point for seduction's appeal to emotion over reason
- [[repentance]]: The only available remedy after seduction — partial and spiritual rather than social — through which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] regains moral standing in death
