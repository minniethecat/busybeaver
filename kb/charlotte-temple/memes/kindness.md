Kindness in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the practical expression of a good heart. More [[modesty|modest]] than [[benevolence|charity]] — which addresses material need — and more active than [[charity|compassion]] — which can remain merely emotional — kindness is the quality that converts moral goodness into concrete [[comfort|relief]]. Rowson's novel draws its moral lines through kindness: those who extend it ([[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], the servant who tends [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]) are the novel's moral exemplars; those who withhold it ([[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], the landlady, [[belcour|Belcour]]) are condemned. Kindness is the novel's simplest and most reliable moral index.

## Definition

Rowson treats kindness as a virtue of action, not [[sensibility|sentiment]]. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "kindness and attention" to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] during her illness ([016-061]) demonstrates that even morally compromised [[reputation|character]]s can perform kindness — though [[montraville|Montraville]]'s kindness proves temporary. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s framing of her parents' [[affection|love]] as "kindness" that her [[gratitude|ingratitude]] has betrayed ([011-031]) reveals kindness's deeper meaning: it is the bond of reciprocal care that holds families and communities together. The novel's closing moral is that "vice in general" may be resisted by "acts of repeated kindness" ([032-020]), making kindness not merely a virtue but a moral strategy.

## Key Passages

> "Was it not for the fear of giving pain to the best of parents, and returning their kindness with ingratitude" — Charlotte's recognition of what she owes ([011-031])

> "The kindness and attention of Montraville soon restored her to health" — kindness from a morally compromised source ([016-061])

> "He had insured it by acts of repeated kindness; but vice in general" — on the limits of kindness as moral safeguard ([032-020])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mr-temple]]: Kindness as paternal devotion. His kindness to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is unconditional, surviving her transgression and extending to her [[death|dying]] moments.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Kindness as practical intervention. Her visits, her gifts, her presence model kindness as doing rather than merely [[sensibility|feeling]].
- [[charlotte-temple]]: Kindness's recipient and its debtor. Her inability to repay the kindness she has received — particularly from her parents — is one of the [[grief|sorrow]]s that weighs on her [[death|dying]] conscience.

## Narrative Function

Kindness is the novel's quiet moral counterpoint to the violence of seduction and [[deceit|betrayal]]. Each act of kindness — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s [[mercy|forgiveness]], [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s visits, the servant's care — is a small restoration of the moral order that seduction has disrupted. Rowson uses kindness to ensure that the novel's darkness is not total: the presence of kind [[reputation|character]]s prevents *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* from becoming a narrative of pure despair, offering instead a vision of moral community that survives individual tragedy.

## Related Concepts

- [[charity]]: Kindness's more materially focused cousin. [[charity|Charity]] addresses physical need; kindness addresses emotional need.
- [[compassion]]: The emotional state that motivates kindness. [[compassion|Compassion]] feels; kindness acts.
- [[gratitude]]: Kindness's proper response. The [[virtue|virtuous]] reciprocate kindness with gratitude; [[gratitude|ingratitude]] is kindness betrayed.
- [[friendship]]: The relationship structured by mutual kindness. True [[friendship|friends]]hip is sustained kindness.
