# Fate

Fate in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* operates as a pervasive moral and narrative force that governs the destinies of its [[reputation|character]]s. Rowson deploys the concept of fate not as blind chance but as a providential mechanism through which virtue is ultimately vindicated and vice punished. The novel's opening address establishes this framework immediately: the narrator's eye trembles for "the [[fate]] of" [[charlotte-temple]], framing the entire narrative as a [[prudence|caution]]ary tale shaped by inexorable destiny [P01-017].

Throughout the novel, [[reputation|character]]s invoke fate to make [[reason|sense]] of [[distress|suffering]], to deflect personal responsibility, and to locate meaning within catastrophe. The narrator weaves these invocations into a consistent moral architecture: fate is the handmaiden of providence, rewarding the [[repentance|penitent]] and destroying the unrepentant.

## Fate as Narrative Frame

Rowson uses fate as a structural device that bookends the novel. The prefatory address establishes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fate as the story's central concern, while the closing chapters return to the theme with redoubled moral force. In the final pages, the narrator reflects on "the [[fate]] of the [[affection|love]]ly [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]" as a completed moral exemplum [034-068].

The novel's chapter epigraphs and direct addresses to the reader consistently reinforce the [[reason|sense]] of an unavoidable destiny unfolding. This narrative strategy aligns with the didactic purposes of [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction: by presenting [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin as fated yet contingent on her choices, Rowson creates a powerful tension between free will and determinism that heightens the reader's moral [[fear|anxiety]].

## Characters' Relationship to Fate

### Charlotte Temple

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] perceives her destiny as shaped by forces beyond her control. When she foresees her own ruin, she declares: "I see my [[fate]] in those tearful eyes" [014-062]. This moment of tragic self-awareness exemplifies the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al heroine's relationship to fate — she recognizes its approach yet feels powerless to evade it. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s invocation of fate serves simultaneously as genuine despair and as a partial evasion of agency, complicating the novel's moral calculus.

### Montraville and Male Agency

[[montraville|Montraville]], by contrast, rarely invokes fate for himself but becomes its instrument for others. His letters and seduction set in motion the chain of events that becomes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fate. The narrator notes that he cared "nothing for the [[fate]] of the man" whose letters facilitated his designs [007-080], establishing a pattern in which male [[reputation|character]]s manipulate fate-like forces without acknowledging their own accountability.

### The Penitent's Fate

The novel's moral economy [[modesty|reserve]]s its most complex treatment of fate for those who repent. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], in her final hours, accepts her [[suffering|sufferings]] as just: "from this heaviest stroke of [[fate]]; I have deserved my" punishment [027-098]. This acceptance transforms fate from an external [[adversity|affliction]] into a vehicle of spiritual purification. The narrator later imagines that divine "[[compassion]] shall [[ruin|fall]] for the [[fate]] of" the [[repentance|penitent]] [028-034], suggesting that fate ultimately bends toward [[compassion|mercy]] for those who truly repent.

## Fate and Providence

Rowson carefully distinguishes fate from mere chance by aligning it with providence. In the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's theological framework, fate is the visible manifestation of divine will operating within human history. The narrator's reflection that offences are blotted "from the book of [[fate]], and they rise from the heavy" weight of sin through [[repentance]] [028-031] draws on the biblical imagery of a heavenly book in which human deeds are recorded and potentially erased.

## The Wayward Fate

In one of the novel's most self-conscious passages, the narrator describes "the path through which my wayward [[fate]] forced me to pass" [029-050]. This rare autobiographical intrusion suggests that the narrator, too, has been shaped by providential forces, lending authority to the moral lessons drawn from [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story. The adjective "wayward" is significant: it acknowledges that fate's path may seem erratic and cruel, yet the narrator's survival to tell the tale implies a larger benevolent design.

## Fate as Warning

The novel explicitly presents [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fate as a [[prudence|caution]]ary example. The narrator wishes that every [[youth|young]] woman might learn from "the [[fate]] of her mother, and teach her" own children to avoid similar ruin [022-082]. This didactic deployment of fate transforms personal tragedy into collective moral [[education|instruction]] — the core project of the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel. Even [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s hypothetical reflection on military service invokes fate as a comparative measure: "had it been my [[fate]]" to [[ruin|fall]] in battle, his [[family]]'s circumstance would differ [011-023].
