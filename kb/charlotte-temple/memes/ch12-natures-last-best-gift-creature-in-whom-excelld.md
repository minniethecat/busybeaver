[012-001] [[nature|Nature]]'s last, best gift:

[012-002] Creature in whom excell'd, whatever could

[012-003] To sight or thought be nam'd!

[012-004] Holy, divine! good, amiable, and sweet!

[012-005] How thou art [[ruin|fall]]'n!--

[012-006] WHEN [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] left her restless bed, her languid eye and pale cheek

[012-007] discovered to [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]] the little repose she had tasted.

[012-008] “My dear child,” said the affectionate governess, “what is the cause of

[012-009] the languor so apparent in your frame? Are you not well?”

[012-010] “Yes, my dear Madam, very well,” replied Charlotte, attempting to smile,

[012-011] “but I know not how it was; I could not sleep last night, and my spirits

[012-012] are depressed this morning.”

[012-013] “Come cheer up, my love,” said the governess; “I believe I have brought

[012-014] a cordial to revive them. I have just received a letter from your good

[012-015] mama, and here is one for yourself.”

[012-016] Charlotte hastily took the letter: it contained these words--

[012-017] “As to-morrow is the anniversary of the [[happiness|happy]] day that gave my [[love|beloved]]

[012-018] girl to the anxious wishes of a maternal heart, I have requested your

[012-019] governess to let you come home and spend it with us; and as I know you

[012-020] to be a good affectionate child, and make it your study to improve in

[012-021] those branches of education which you know will give most pleasure to

[012-022] your delighted parents, as a reward for your diligence and attention

[012-023] I have prepared an agreeable surprise for your reception. Your

[012-024] grand-father, eager to embrace the darling of his aged heart, will come

[012-025] in the chaise for you; so hold yourself in readiness to attend him

[012-026] by nine o'clock. Your dear father joins in every tender wish for your

[012-027] health and future felicity, which warms the heart of my dear Charlotte's

[012-028] affectionate mother, L. TEMPLE.”

[012-029] “Gracious heaven!” cried Charlotte, forgetting where she was, and

[012-030] raising her streaming eyes as in earnest supplication.

[012-031] Madame Du Pont was surprised. “Why these tears, my love?” said she.

[012-032] “Why this seeming agitation? I thought the letter would have rejoiced,

[012-033] instead of distressing you.”

[012-034] “It does rejoice me,” replied Charlotte, endeavouring at composure, “but

[012-035] I was praying for merit to deserve the unremitted attentions of the best

[012-036] of parents.”

[012-037] “You do right,” said Madame Du Pont, “to ask the assistance of

[012-038] heaven that you may continue to deserve their love. Continue, my dear

[012-039] Charlotte, in the course you have ever pursued, and you will insure at

[012-040] once their happiness and your own.”

[012-041] “Oh!” cried Charlotte, as her governess left her, “I have forfeited both

[012-042] for ever! Yet let me reflect:--the irrevocable step is not yet taken:

[012-043] it is not too late to recede from the brink of a precipice, from which I

[012-044] can only behold the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and [[repentance|remorse]]!”

[012-045] She arose from her seat, and flew to the apartment of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]. “Oh

[012-046] Mademoiselle!” said she, “I am snatched by a miracle from destruction!

[012-047] This letter has saved me: it has opened my eyes to the folly I was

[012-048] so near committing. I will not go, Mademoiselle; I will not wound the

[012-049] hearts of those dear parents who make my happiness the whole study of

[012-050] their lives.”

[012-051] “Well,” said Mademoiselle, “do as you please, Miss; but pray understand

[012-052] that my resolution is taken, and it is not in your power to alter it.

[012-053] I shall meet the gentlemen at the appointed hour, and shall not be

[012-054] surprized at any outrage which [[montraville|Montraville]] may commit, when he finds

[012-055] himself disappointed. Indeed I should not be astonished, was he to come

[012-056] immediately here, and reproach you for your instability in the hearing

[012-057] of the whole school: and what will be the consequence? you will bear

[012-058] the odium of having formed the resolution of eloping, and every girl

[012-059] of spirit will laugh at your [[poverty|want]] of [[courage|fortitude]] to put it in execution,

[012-060] while prudes and fools will load you with reproach and contempt. You

[012-061] will have lost the confidence of your parents, incurred their anger, and

[012-062] the scoffs of the world; and what fruit do you expect to reap from this

[012-063] piece of heroism, (for such no doubt you think it is?) you will have the

[012-064] pleasure to reflect, that you have deceived the man who adores you,

[012-065] and whom in your heart you prefer to all other men, and that you are

[012-066] separated from him for ever.”

[012-067] This eloquent harangue was given with such volubility, that Charlotte

[012-068] could not find an opportunity to interrupt her, or to offer a single

[012-069] word till the whole was finished, and then found her ideas so confused,

[012-070] that she knew not what to say.

[012-071] At length she determined that she would go with Mademoiselle to the

[012-072] place of assignation, convince Montraville of the necessity of adhering

[012-073] to the resolution of remaining behind; assure him of her [[love|affection]], and

[012-074] bid him adieu.

[012-075] Charlotte formed this plan in her mind, and exulted in the certainty of

[012-076] its success. “How shall I rejoice,” said she, “in this triumph of reason

[012-077] over inclination, and, when in the arms of my affectionate parents, lift

[012-078] up my soul in gratitude to heaven as I look back on the dangers I have

[012-079] escaped!”

[012-080] The hour of assignation arrived: Mademoiselle put what money and

[012-081] valuables she possessed in her pocket, and advised Charlotte to do

[012-082] the same; but she refused; “my resolution is fixed,” said she; “I will

[012-083] sacrifice love to duty.”

[012-084] Mademoiselle smiled internally; and they proceeded softly down the back

[012-085] stairs and out of the garden gate. Montraville and [[belcour|Belcour]] were ready to

[012-086] receive them.

[012-087] “Now,” said Montraville, taking Charlotte in his arms, “you are mine for

[012-088] ever.”

[012-089] “No,” said she, withdrawing from his embrace, “I am come to take an

[012-090] everlasting farewel.”

[012-091] It would be useless to repeat the conversation that here ensued, suffice

[012-092] it to say, that Montraville used every argument that had formerly been

[012-093] successful, Charlotte's resolution began to waver, and he drew her

[012-094] almost imperceptibly towards the chaise.

[012-095] “I cannot go,” said she: “cease, dear Montraville, to persuade. I must

[012-096] not: religion, duty, forbid.”

[012-097] “Cruel Charlotte,” said he, “if you disappoint my ardent [[hope|hopes]], by

[012-098] all that is sacred, this hand shall put a period to my existence. I

[012-099] cannot--will not live without you.”

[012-100] “Alas! my torn heart!” said Charlotte, “how shall I act?”

[012-101] “Let me direct you,” said Montraville, lifting her into the chaise.

[012-102] “Oh! my dear forsaken parents!” cried Charlotte.

[012-103] The chaise drove off. She shrieked, and fainted into the arms of her

[012-104] betrayer.
