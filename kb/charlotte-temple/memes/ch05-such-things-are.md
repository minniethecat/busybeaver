[005-001] MISS Weatherby was the only child of a wealthy man, almost idolized by

[005-002] her parents, flattered by her dependants, and never contradicted even

[005-003] by those who called themselves her [[friendship|friends]]: I cannot give a better

[005-004] description than by the following lines.

[005-005] The lovely maid whose form and face

[005-006] [[nature|Nature]] has deck'd with ev'ry grace,

[005-007] But in whose breast no virtues glow,

[005-008] Whose heart ne'er felt another's [[sorrow|woe]],

[005-009] Whose hand ne'er smooth'd the bed of pain,

[005-010] Or eas'd the captive's galling chain;

[005-011] But like the tulip caught the eye,

[005-012] Born just to be admir'd and die;

[005-013] When gone, no one regrets its loss,

[005-014] Or scarce remembers that it was.

[005-015] Such was Miss Weatherby: her form lovely as nature could make it, but

[005-016] her mind uncultivated, her heart unfeeling, her [[passion|passions]] impetuous, and

[005-017] her brain almost turned with flattery, dissipation, and pleasure; and

[005-018] such was the girl, whom a partial grandfather left independent mistress

[005-019] of the [[fate|fortune]] before mentioned.

[005-020] She had seen [[mr-temple|Temple]] frequently; and fancying she could never be [[happiness|happy]]

[005-021] without him, nor once imagining he could refuse a girl of her beauty and

[005-022] [[wealth|fortune]], she prevailed on her fond father to offer the alliance to the

[005-023] old Earl of D----, Mr. Temple's father.

[005-024] The Earl had received the offer courteously: he thought it a great match

[005-025] for Henry; and was too fashionable a man to suppose a wife could be any

[005-026] impediment to the friendship he professed for [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] and his daughter.

[005-027] Unfortunately for Temple, he thought quite otherwise: the conversation

[005-028] he had just had with his father, discovered to him the situation of

[005-029] his heart; and he found that the most affluent fortune would bring no

[005-030] increase of happiness unless [[lucy|Lucy]] Eldridge shared it with him; and the

[005-031] knowledge of the [[innocence|purity]] of her sentiments, and the integrity of his own

[005-032] heart, made him shudder at the idea his father had started, of marrying

[005-033] a woman for no other reason than because the affluence of her fortune

[005-034] would enable him to injure her by maintaining in splendor the woman

[005-035] to whom his heart was devoted: he therefore resolved to refuse Miss

[005-036] Weatherby, and be the event what it might, offer his heart and hand to

[005-037] Lucy Eldridge.

[005-038] Full of this determination, he fought his father, declared his

[005-039] resolution, and was commanded never more to appear in his presence.

[005-040] Temple bowed; his heart was too full to permit him to speak; he left the

[005-041] house precipitately, and hastened to relate the cause of his sorrows to

[005-042] his good old friend and his amiable daughter.

[005-043] In the mean time, the Earl, vexed to the soul that such a fortune should

[005-044] be lost, determined to offer himself a candidate for Miss Weatherby's

[005-045] favour.

[005-046] What wonderful changes are wrought by that reigning power, ambition! the

[005-047] love-sick girl, when first she heard of Temple's refusal, wept, raved,

[005-048] tore her hair, and vowed to found a protestant nunnery with her fortune;

[005-049] and by commencing abbess, shut herself up from the sight of cruel

[005-050] ungrateful man for ever.

## Section 4

[005-051] Her father was a man of the world: he suffered this first transport to

[005-052] subside, and then very deliberately unfolded to her the offers of the

[005-053] old Earl, expatiated on the many benefits arising from an elevated

[005-054] title, painted in glowing colours the surprise and vexation of Temple

[005-055] when he should see her figuring as a Countess and his mother-in-law, and

[005-056] begged her to consider well before she made any rash vows.

[005-057] The DISTRESSED fair one dried her tears, listened patiently, and at

[005-058] length declared she believed the surest method to revenge the slight put

[005-059] on her by the son, would be to accept the father: so said so done, and

[005-060] in a few days she became the Countess D----.

[005-061] Temple heard the news with emotion: he had lost his father's favour

[005-062] by avowing his [[love|passion]] for Lucy, and he saw now there was no hope of

[005-063] regaining it: “but he shall not make me miserable,” said he. “Lucy and I

[005-064] have no ambitious notions: we can live on three hundred a year for

[005-065] some little time, till the mortgage is paid off, and then we shall have

[005-066] sufficient not only for the comforts but many of the little elegancies

[005-067] of life. We will purchase a little cottage, my Lucy,” said he, “and

[005-068] thither with your reverend father we will retire; we will forget there

[005-069] are such things as splendor, profusion, and dissipation: we will have

[005-070] some cows, and you shall be queen of the dairy; in a morning, while I

[005-071] look after my garden, you shall take a basket on your arm, and sally

[005-072] forth to feed your poultry; and as they flutter round you in token of

[005-073] humble gratitude, your father shall smoke his pipe in a woodbine alcove,

[005-074] and viewing the serenity of your countenance, feel such real pleasure

[005-075] dilate his own heart, as shall make him forget he had ever been

[005-076] unhappy.”

[005-077] Lucy smiled; and Temple saw it was a smile of approbation. He sought

[005-078] and found a cottage suited to his taste; thither, attended by Love and

[005-079] Hymen, the happy trio retired; where, during many years of uninterrupted

[005-080] felicity, they cast not a wish beyond the little boundaries of their own

[005-081] tenement. Plenty, and her handmaid, [[prudence|Prudence]], presided at their board,

[005-082] Hospitality stood at their gate, Peace smiled on each face, Content

[005-083] reigned in each heart, and Love and Health strewed roses on their

[005-084] pillows.

[005-085] Such were the parents of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]], who was the only pledge of

[005-086] their mutual love, and who, at the earnest entreaty of a particular

[005-087] friend, was permitted to finish the education her mother had begun,

[005-088] at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school, where we first introduced her to the

[005-089] acquaintance of the reader.
