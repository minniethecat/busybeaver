# Belcour

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s companion who first accompanies him past [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s boarding school and later becomes the agent of her final [[ruin|destruction]]. Where [[montraville]] is morally weak, Belcour is morally void — a predator who feels no [[regret|remorse]] and whose "designs were never [[honour|honourable]]" (016-078). Among the major [[reputation|character]]s of *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, Belcour is the only one granted no interiority: we never see him reflect, hesitate, or regret. Rowson presents him as a force of pure malevolence, the walking negation of the [[honour]] and [[love]] the novel's positive [[reputation|character]]s embody.

## Role in the Novel

Belcour is introduced in the novel's opening scene as [[montraville|Montraville]]'s walking companion near the [[chichester|Chichester]] barracks (001-003). His role expands as the narrative progresses, and by the novel's second half he has become the architect of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s final ruin. When [[montraville|Montraville]], having abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], attempts to provide for her financially, Belcour intercepts the money and letters, leaving the pregnant [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] destitute (023-061). His subsequent attempt to [[seduction|seduce]] the now-helpless [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] reveals the full extent of his predatory nature.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Rowson attributes no virtue to Belcour. He is the novel's only major [[reputation|character]] presented without any redeeming quality — a deliberate artistic choice that makes him the moral baseline against which even [[montraville|Montraville]]'s weakness appears relatively sympathetic.
- **Flaws**: Belcour is deceitful, predatory, and utterly without conscience. The narrator's explicit warning — "Let not the reader imagine Belcour's designs were [[honour|honourable]]" (016-078) — signals his function as the novel's pure villain. He pretends [[friendship|friends]]hip to [[montraville|Montraville]] while secretly scheming to [[seduction|seduce]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] himself.

## Key Actions

- Walks with [[montraville|Montraville]] past [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school, where [[montraville|Montraville]] first sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (001-003).
- Encourages [[montraville|Montraville]]'s pursuit of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], dismissing concerns about honour and consequences.
- Intercepts the money and letters [[montraville|Montraville]] sends to support the abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], leaving her destitute.
- Appears at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s bedside after she has been abandoned by [[montraville|Montraville]], attempting to [[seduction|seduce]] her in her [[weakness|vulnerability]] (023-061).

## Relationships

- [[montraville]]: His nominal [[friendship|friend]] and primary dupe. Belcour exploits [[montraville|Montraville]]'s moral weakness, encouraging his worst impulses while secretly pursuing his own predatory agenda. Their relationship demonstrates how the morally weak become instruments of the morally void.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: His ultimate target. Belcour's conduct toward [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] moves from indifferent complicity in [[montraville|Montraville]]'s seduction to active predation once she is vulnerable and alone. The narrator's warning — "Belcour's designs were not [[honour|honourable]]" (016-078) — makes his intentions toward a helpless, pregnant woman chillingly explicit.

## Moral Significance

Belcour serves as the novel's moral baseline — the point beyond which human conduct cannot sink. While [[montraville]] represents the tragedy of moral weakness, Belcour represents the horror of moral absence. Rowson uses him to draw a crucial distinction between the [[seduction|seducer]] who feels [[regret|remorse]] ([[montraville|Montraville]]) and the predator who feels nothing.

> "Let not the reader imagine Belcour's designs were honourable" (016-078)

This direct authorial intervention is one of the novel's most striking. Rowson breaks the narrative frame to warn her readers about Belcour, as if she [[fear|fears]] they might otherwise mistake his villainy for mere rakishness. The intervention reveals the didactic urgency behind the novel: Rowson is not merely telling a story but teaching [[youth|young]] women to recognize predators who lack even the decency of hypocrisy.

Belcour also functions as a foil to [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]. Where [[mr-temple|Temple]] uses his social power to protect and restore (marrying the [[ruin|fall]]en [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], adopting her child), Belcour uses his to exploit and destroy. The novel's moral geography is defined by these two poles: [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s [[england|England]], where [[honour]] binds men to protect women, and Belcour's [[new-york|New-York]], where honour's absence leaves the vulnerable with no defence.

## Fate / Resolution

Rowson provides Belcour with no explicit resolution, an artistic choice that reinforces his function: unlike [[montraville|Montraville]], whose [[regret|remorse]] humanizes him, Belcour is not granted even the dignity of a meaningful ending. He simply disappears from the narrative once his function — the completion of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin — is accomplished. This narrative erasure is itself a moral [[reason|judgment]]: Belcour is not worth the author's attention once his predatory work is done.
