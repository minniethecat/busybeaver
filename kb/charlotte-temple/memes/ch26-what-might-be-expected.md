[026-001] IN the mean time the [[love|passion]] [[montraville|Montraville]] had conceived for Julia

[026-002] [[mr-franklin|Franklin]] daily encreased, and he saw evidently how much he was beloved

[026-003] by that amiable girl: he was likewise strongly prepossessed with an idea

[026-004] of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s perfidy. What wonder then if he gave himself up to the

[026-005] delightful sensation which pervaded his bosom; and finding no obstacle

[026-006] arise to oppose his happiness, he solicited and obtained the hand of

[026-007] Julia. A few days before his marriage he thus addressed [[belcour|Belcour]]:

[026-008] “Though Charlotte, by her abandoned conduct, has thrown herself from my

[026-009] protection, I still hold myself bound to support her till relieved

[026-010] from her present condition, and also to provide for the child. I do not

[026-011] intend to see her again, but I will place a sum of money in your hands,

[026-012] which will amply supply her with every convenience; but should she

[026-013] require more, let her have it, and I will see it repaid. I wish I could

[026-014] prevail on the poor deluded girl to return to her [[friendship|friends]]: she was an

[026-015] only child, and I make no doubt but that they would joyfully receive

[026-016] her; it would shock me greatly to see her henceforth leading a life of

[026-017] infamy, as I should always accuse myself of being the primary cause of

[026-018] all her errors. If she should chuse to remain under your protection, be

[026-019] kind to her, Belcour, I conjure you. Let not satiety prompt you to treat

[026-020] her in such a manner, as may drive her to actions which necessity might

[026-021] urge her to, while her better reason disapproved them: she shall never

[026-022] [[poverty|want]] a friend while I live, but I never more [[passion|desire]] to behold her; her

[026-023] presence would be always painful to me, and a glance from her eye would

[026-024] call the blush of [[guilt|conscious guilt]] into my cheek.

[026-025] “I will write a letter to her, which you may deliver when I am gone, as

[026-026] I shall go to St. Eustatia the day after my union with Julia, who will

[026-027] accompany me.”

[026-028] Belcour promised to fulfil the request of his friend, though nothing

[026-029] was farther from his intentions, than the least design of delivering the

[026-030] letter, or making Charlotte acquainted with the provision Montraville

[026-031] had made for her; he was bent on the complete ruin of the unhappy girl,

[026-032] and supposed, by reducing her to an entire dependance on him, to bring

[026-033] her by degrees to consent to gratify his ungenerous passion.

[026-034] The evening before the day appointed for the nuptials of Montraville and

[026-035] Julia, the former refired early to his apartment; and ruminating on the

[026-036] past scenes of his life, suffered the keenest [[repentance|remorse]] in the remembrance

[026-037] of Charlotte's seduction. “Poor girl,” said he, “I will at least write

[026-038] and bid her adieu; I will too endeavour to awaken that love of virtue in

[026-039] her bosom which her unfortunate attachment to me has extinguished.” He

[026-040] took up the pen and began to write, but words were denied him. How could

[026-041] he address the woman whom he had seduced, and whom, though he thought

[026-042] unworthy his tenderness, he was about to bid adieu for ever? How should

[026-043] he tell her that he was going to abjure her, to enter into the most

[026-044] indissoluble ties with another, and that he could not even own the

[026-045] infant which she bore as his child? Several letters were begun and

[026-046] destroyed: at length he completed the following:

[026-047] TO CHARLOTTE.

[026-048] “Though I have taken up my pen to address you, my poor injured girl, I

[026-049] feel I am inadequate to the task; yet, however painful the endeavour, I

[026-050] could not resolve upon leaving you for ever without one kind line to bid

[026-051] you adieu, to tell you how my heart bleeds at the remembrance of what

[026-052] you was, before you saw the hated Montraville. Even now imagination

[026-053] paints the scene, when, torn by contending [[passion|passions]], when, struggling

[026-054] between love and duty, you fainted in my arms, and I lifted you into

[026-055] the chaise: I see the [[suffering|agony]] of your mind, when, recovering, you found

[026-056] yourself on the road to [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]]: but how, my gentle girl, how could

[026-057] you, when so justly impressed with the value of virtue, how could you,

[026-058] when loving as I thought you loved me, yield to the solicitations of

[026-059] Belcour?

[026-060] “Oh Charlotte, conscience tells me it was I, villain that I am, who

[026-061] first taught you the allurements of guilty pleasure; it was I who

[026-062] dragged you from the calm repose which innocence and virtue ever enjoy;

[026-063] and can I, dare I tell you, it was not love prompted to the horrid deed?

[026-064] No, thou dear, fallen angel, believe your repentant Montraville, when

[026-065] he tells you the man who truly loves will never betray the object of his

[026-066] affection. Adieu, Charlotte: could you still find charms in a life of

[026-067] unoffend-ing innocence, return to your parents; you shall never want the

[026-068] means of support both for yourself and child. Oh! gracious heaven!

[026-069] may that child be entirely free from the vices of its father and the

[026-070] weakness of its mother.

[026-071] “To-morrow--but no, I cannot tell you what to-morrow will produce;

[026-072] Belcour will inform you: he also has cash for you, which I beg you will

[026-073] ask for whenever you may want it. Once more adieu: believe me could I

[026-074] hear you was returned to your friends, and enjoying that tranquillity of

[026-075] which I have robbed you, I should be as completely [[happiness|happy]] as even you,

[026-076] in your fondest hours, could wish me, but till then a [[melancholy|gloom]] will obscure

[026-077] the brightest prospects of MONTRAVILLE.”

[026-078] After he had sealed this letter he threw himself on the bed, and enjoyed

[026-079] a few hours repose. Early in the morning Belcour tapped at his door: he

[026-080] arose hastily, and prepared to meet his Julia at the altar.

[026-081] “This is the letter to Charlotte,” said he, giving it to Belcour: “take

[026-082] it to her when we are gone to Eustatia; and I conjure you, my dear

[026-083] friend, not to use any sophistical arguments to prevent her return to

[026-084] virtue; but should she incline that way, encourage her in the thought,

[026-085] and assist her to put her design in execution.”
