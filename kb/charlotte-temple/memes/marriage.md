Marriage, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is the social institution that divides the [[virtue|virtuous]] woman from the [[ruin|fall]]en one. It is the prize that [[montraville|Montraville]] dangles before [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to secure her compliance, the protection that her seduction denies her, and the fate she spends the novel waiting for — in vain. Rowson uses marriage as the novel's sharpest diagnostic instrument, exposing the [[weakness|vulnerability]] of women for whom marriage is the only legitimate social destination.

## Definition

Rowson presents marriage as both a moral safeguard and a site of [[deceit|betrayal]]. When [[montraville|Montraville]] persuades [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to elope, he does so "under promise of marriage" ([016-028]), a promise he repeats "by the most solemn promises of marriage" ([022-032]) even as he prepares to abandon her for [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]]. The novel makes clear that a promise of marriage from a libertine is worthless, yet it is also the only currency available to a woman in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s position. Marriage, in this world, is what transforms a seduction into a courtship — and its absence is what turns a [[love|romance]] into a ruin.

## Key Passages

> "He had indeed prevailed on her to leave her friends under promise of marriage, and she had trusted to his honour." ([016-028])

> "Montraville had bound himself to her by the most solemn promises of marriage, yet now he seemed inclined to desert her." ([022-032])

> "Perhaps, had she known his situation, she would have shrunk from engaging his affections, and on the very eve of marriage, have prevented his deserting the unfortunate Charlotte." ([027-012])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central victim of broken marriage promises. Her entire tragedy is structured around the gap between [[montraville|Montraville]]'s promise of marriage and his failure to honour it. Without marriage, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is not a wife but a [[ruin|fall]]en woman — a distinction that costs her everything ([016-028], [022-032]).
- [[montraville]]: Uses the promise of marriage as an instrument of seduction. His engagement to [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]], announced "a few days before his marriage" ([026-007]), is the final confirmation that his promises to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] were never sincere.
- [[julia-franklin]]: The woman who receives what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] was promised — marriage to [[montraville|Montraville]]. Rowson is careful not to villainize Julia; instead, the juxtaposition of the two women's fates underlines the arbitrary cruelty of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation ([026-007], [027-012]).
- [[belcour]]: Dismisses marriage as irrelevant to his pleasure-seeking, representing the male privilege of treating marriage as optional while women depend on it for survival.

## Narrative Function

Marriage is the novel's structural pivot. Every major plot event turns on whether [[montraville|Montraville]] will or will not marry [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]: the elopement, the journey to [[new-york|New York]], the pregnancy, the abandonment, and ultimately the death. Rowson uses marriage to dramatize the economic and social precarity of women: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has no independent means of support, no social standing outside of marriage, and no future once marriage is denied her. The novel is, in this [[reason|sense]], an extended argument about what happens to women when men are not held to their marital promises.

## Related Concepts

- [[seduction]]: [[seduction|Seduction]] and marriage are opposites in the novel's moral vocabulary. [[seduction|Seduction]] promises marriage and delivers ruin; marriage is what seduction is not.
- [[honour]]: A man's honour is measured by whether he keeps his promise of marriage. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s failure to marry [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is the novel's definitive breach of honour.
- [[ruin]]: The absence of marriage means ruin for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. The two concepts are inversely correlated: marriage prevents ruin; its absence causes it.
- [[family]]: Marriage is the foundation of [[family]], and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s exclusion from marriage means her exclusion from the domestic sanctuary that [[family]] represents.
