# Innocence

The [[nature|natural]], prelapsarian state of youth that Rowson presents as a [[youth|young]] woman's birthright — and the thing that seduction destroys. In the novel's moral economy, innocence is both a quality to be cherished and a [[weakness|vulnerability]] to be exploited, requiring the protections of vigilant guardianship and the fortifications of virtue and duty.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, innocence has a dual [[reputation|character]]. On one hand, it is a positive quality — the guilelessness, [[trust]], and purity that make a [[youth|young]] woman [[affection|love]]ly and that attract the right kind of [[love|affection]]. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] first appears to [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]] as part of "such an assemblage of youth and innocence" (001-017), and this quality is what first draws his genuine admiration before it becomes the target of his predatory [[passion|desire]].

On the other hand, innocence is a condition of defencelessness. The innocent girl, [[trust]]ing by nature and untutored in the world's deceits, is uniquely vulnerable to the arts of the [[seduction|seducer]]. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is described as taking "no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level" of her own depravity (007-097), a formulation that makes innocence precisely what the corrupt seek to destroy. [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]], reflecting on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s disappearance, dwells on "the innocence and [[kindness|gentleness]] of her disposition" (013-074) as proof that she must have been the victim of manipulation rather than a willing participant in her own ruin.

The novel's tragedy lies in the irreversibility of innocence lost. Once [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has been [[seduction|seduce]]d and abandoned, she may protest her "innocence" (023-078) against particular accusations — as she does when [[montraville|Montraville]] wrongly suspects her of infidelity with [[belcour|Belcour]] — but the deeper, constitutional innocence of her youth is gone forever. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[death|dying]] recognition that he "dragged you from the calm repose which innocence and virtue ever enjoy" (026-062) confirms that innocence, once taken, cannot be returned.

## Key Passages

> "Such an assemblage of youth and innocence naturally attracted the young soldiers" (001-017)

This is the novel's introduction of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — not as an individual but as part of a collective of "youth and innocence." The pairing is significant: innocence is presented as the [[nature|natural]] companion of youth, a quality so visible it "[[nature|natural]]ly attracted" attention. The adverb "[[nature|natural]]ly" does double work, suggesting both the inevitability of the attraction and its innocence in this first, prelapsarian moment.

> "no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" (007-097)

The narrator's description of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s designs on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] reveals the predatory logic of seduction. "Bring down" spatialises the moral universe: innocence is elevated, and seduction is a deliberate act of lowering. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s malice is not merely to corrupt but to *level* — to reduce [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to her own degraded condition.

> "when she reflected on the innocence and gentleness of her disposition, she concluded that it must have been the advice and machinations of La Rue, which led her to this imprudent step" (013-074)

[[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s post-elopement reflection il[[passion|lust]]rates how the concept of innocence functions within the novel's moral epistemology. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence is understood as a fixed quality of her [[reputation|character]] that makes certain actions — wilful rebellion — unthinkable, forcing those who know her to seek external explanations for her [[ruin|fall]].

> "she threw herself on her knees before him, protesting her innocence and entreating him not to leave her" (023-078)

This passage captures the tragic irony of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation. She protests her "innocence" of [[belcour|Belcour]]'s advances — and she is factually innocent of that specific charge — but her posture of desperate supplication makes visible the degradation that has already occurred. She can be innocent of this accusation while having already lost the deeper innocence of her former state.

> "dragged you from the calm repose which innocence and virtue ever enjoy" (026-062)

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[regret|remorse]]ful self-indictment crystallises the novel's association of innocence with tranquillity. "Calm repose" is what the innocent possess; "dragged" is what the [[seduction|seducer]] does. The violence of the verb underscores that innocence is not given away but taken — a framing that complicates the novel's ostensible message about female responsibility.

> "could you still find charms in a life of unoffending innocence, return to your parents" (026-067)

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[death|dying]] counsel offers a vision of a restored life — but the conditional "could you" acknowledges its impossibility. A life of "unoffending innocence" is what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] once had and can never have again. The passage marks the conceptual distance between innocence as a recoverable moral state (through [[regret|penitence]]) and innocence as an irreversible condition of being.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central figure of innocence lost. She enters the narrative as the embodiment of youthful innocence — a quality that attracts both protectors and predators — and exits it as a [[prudence|caution]]ary exemplar of innocence destroyed by seduction and [[deceit|betrayal]].
- [[montraville]]: The agent of innocence's [[ruin|destruction]]. His tragedy is that he recognises the value of what he has destroyed: his [[death|deathbed]] letter is a catalogue of the innocence he "dragged" [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] from, and his [[regret|remorse]] is the novel's most powerful testimony to the irreversibility of the loss he caused.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]: The enemy of innocence — a woman who, having lost her own, actively works to destroy it in others. Her campaign against [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence (007-097) is the novel's most brutal il[[passion|lust]]ration of how the corrupted become corrupters.
- [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]: Represents the protective function that innocence requires. Her [[charity|compassion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — extending [[friendship|friends]]hip and practical aid despite [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]]en state — models the response to [[ruin|ruined]] innocence that the novel's moral theology endorses.

## Narrative Function

Innocence serves as the novel's emotional and moral baseline — the condition whose loss generates the plot's affective power. Rowson opens by establishing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] within a community of innocence (001-017), then systematically strips away every protection — guardians, distance, reputation — until innocence is exposed, assaulted, and finally destroyed.

The concept drives the novel's [[sensibility|sentiment]]al machinery: the reader's [[compassion|pity]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] depends on the recognition that she was innocent — [[trust]]ing, unguarded, unequipped for the world — and that her [[ruin|fall]] was therefore more tragedy than crime. At the same time, innocence functions didactically as a warning: it is not self-protecting. The innocent girl needs the defences of religion, duty, and vigilant parents, and the novel's horror is that, in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s case, every one of those defences failed.

The final movement of the plot — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] [[regret|penitence]] and the narrator's call for the reader to learn from her example — attempts to salvage from destroyed innocence a moral lesson that might protect the innocence of others.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Innocence is the state that virtue protects; the loss of innocence is the consequence of virtue's failure
- [[seduction]]: The process by which innocence is destroyed; in Rowson's framing, seduction is inherently predatory — an assault on the innocent by the knowing
- [[sensibility]]: The refined capacity for [[sensibility|feeling]] that, in an innocent girl, is a charm but, unguarded by [[reason|prudence]], becomes an avenue for seduction
- [[ruin]]: The social and moral state that follows the loss of innocence; the novel's trajectory from innocence to ruin constitutes its plot
- [[repentance]]: The sole mechanism by which something of innocence's moral value can be recovered — not the state itself, but the spiritual standing it conferred
