# Fear

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, fear operates as the emotional register of moral [[weakness|vulnerability]]. Rowson presents fear not merely as a passive [[sensibility|feeling]] but as a narrative force: parental fears anticipate disaster, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fears signal her progressive descent, and the fear of social exposure governs the actions of nearly every [[reputation|character]]. Fear is both the prudent person's safeguard and the [[ruin|fall]]en woman's torment.

## Definition

In Rowson's moral vocabulary, fear has a dual [[reputation|character]]. Righteous fear — a parent's fear for a child's safety, a [[youth|young]] woman's fear of [[shame|disgrace]] — is aligned with [[reason|prudence]] and virtue. But fear born of guilt — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fear of discovery, [[montraville|Montraville]]'s fear of scandal — is corrosive and isolating. The narrator consistently frames the latter as a consequence of moral failure: "I much fear you have cast me from you" ([022-076]) is [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s recognition that fear has replaced the [[trust]] that once defined her relationships.

## Key Passages

> "…distracted with her fears for us both, sunk on the floor" ([003-044])

Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s maternal fear is the novel's archetype of [[virtue|virtuous]] anxiety — fear born of [[affection|love]] rather than guilt.

> "…stay, and to calm your fears, I will here protest" ([011-029])

[[montraville|Montraville]]'s promise to calm [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fears is the [[seduction|seducer]]'s classic strategy — and its eventual failure reveals the hollowness of his reassurances.

> "…speechless with surprize and fear" ([014-020])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s reaction upon realizing her situation captures fear as the body's recognition of moral danger before the mind fully comprehends it.

> "…much feared, infamous woman." ([020-059])

[[belcour|Belcour]]'s dismissal of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] reduces her to an object of fear and contempt — the [[ruin|fall]]en woman whom society fears to acknowledge.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Lives in a state of fear from her elopement onward — fear of discovery, of abandonment, and finally of death ([014-020], [022-076])
- [[mr-temple]]: His fear for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moral safety is prescient — a father's anxiety that the novel tragically vindicates ([014-068])
- [[mrs-temple]]: Her maternal fear for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] exemplifies [[virtue|virtuous]], selfless anxiety ([003-044])
- [[montraville]]: Fears scandal and social consequence more than moral wrong — his fear is self-regarding rather than protective ([019-020])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Affects fear and distress as performance, manipulating others through counterfeit emotion ([016-049])

## Narrative Function

Fear drives the novel's second half. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s initial transgression is enabled by fearlessness — her failure to fear the consequences of elopement. Once [[ruin|fall]]en, fear becomes her constant companion: fear of her parents' anger, fear of [[montraville|Montraville]]'s abandonment, fear of poverty, and fear of death. Rowson uses this trajectory to teach that prudent fear should precede action, not follow it. The novel's didactic purpose is encapsulated in this reversal: "I fear I have not only entailed lasting [[distress|misery]] on myself, but on your parents" ([019-073]).

- [[prudence]]: [[prudence|Prudence]] is partly the capacity for appropriate fear — fearing the right things at the right time

- [[despair]]: Fear's terminal point — when fear exhausts hope, despair follows
