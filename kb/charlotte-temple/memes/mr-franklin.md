# Mr. Franklin

[[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]]'s father, an [[america|American]] gentleman whose attentive guardianship of his daughter provides the novel's counter-example to the failed paternal protection that left [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] vulnerable.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Franklin appears primarily in his capacity as Julia's father, hosting [[montraville|Montraville]] at his home and supervising his daughter's courtship (019-053). His role is structurally significant rather than dramatically developed: he represents the protective paternal presence that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], separated from her father by the Atlantic Ocean, cannot access. [[belcour|Belcour]] deliberately distracts Mr. Franklin to give [[montraville|Montraville]] time alone with Julia (024-078), a detail that underscores the constant contest between paternal protection and male predation that structures the novel.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Mr. Franklin is a responsible and attentive father who maintains a respectable [[family|household]]. His supervision of Julia's courtship ensures that [[montraville|Montraville]]'s intentions are [[honour|honourable]] and publicly declared.
- **Flaws**: Like other [[virtue|virtuous]] [[reputation|character]]s in the novel, Mr. Franklin is given no significant moral flaws — he functions as a structural element rather than a psychologically complex [[reputation|character]].

## Key Actions

- Maintains a respectable [[family|household]] in [[america|America]] where Julia is raised under his protection.
- Receives [[montraville|Montraville]] as a guest and supervises his developing relationship with Julia (019-053).
- Is briefly distracted by [[belcour|Belcour]], allowing [[montraville|Montraville]] private conversation with Julia (024-078) — a moment that demonstrates the constant [[weakness|vulnerability]] of even well-protected [[youth|young]] women.

## Relationships

- [[julia-franklin]]: His daughter, whom he protects with attentive paternal care. His protective presence ensures that Julia's virtue is never seriously threatened.
- [[montraville]]: His eventual son-in-law. Mr. Franklin's acceptance of [[montraville|Montraville]] as a suitor, conducted under proper social supervision, contrasts with [[montraville|Montraville]]'s clandestine seduction of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].

## Moral Significance

Mr. Franklin represents the structural argument of Rowson's novel: female virtue requires active paternal protection. His presence in Julia's life — contrasted with [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s absence from [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s — makes the novel's point without requiring elaborate [[reputation|character]]ization. He is not [[virtue|virtuous]] in any extraordinary way; he is simply present, attentive, and in control of his [[family|household]]. These ordinary qualities, Rowson suggests, are sufficient to protect a [[youth|young]] woman from predation — and their absence is sufficient to explain [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]].

## Fate / Resolution

Mr. Franklin presumably continues as the head of his [[family|household]], having successfully guided his daughter into a respectable marriage. He survives the novel as a functional element whose narrative purpose — demonstrating the protective power of paternal presence — is fulfilled once Julia is safely married.
