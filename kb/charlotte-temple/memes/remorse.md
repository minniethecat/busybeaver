Remorse, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is guilt made visceral — the felt torment of a conscience that cannot be silenced. Rowson distinguishes [[regret|remorse]] from its gentler cousin repentance: [[regret|remorse]] is the raw [[distress|anguish]] of self-accusation, while repentance channels that [[distress|anguish]] toward moral reformation. The novel deploys [[regret|remorse]] as its primary emotional register for [[ruin|fall]]en [[reputation|character]]s, staging the [[seduction|seduce]]d woman's interior life as a drama of "never-ceasing [[regret|remorse]]" ([009-056]) from which there is no earthly escape.

## Definition

Rowson's [[regret|remorse]] is both a psychological [[adversity|affliction]] and a narrative device. It is the mechanism by which the novel's moral warnings are internalized: readers are meant to feel the "keenest remorse" ([026-036]) vicariously and thus avoid the actions that produce it. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], standing at "the brink of a precipice, from which I can only behold the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and [[regret|remorse]]" ([012-044]), articulates [[regret|remorse]] as the destination of the [[ruin|fall]]en, not merely a passing [[sensibility|sentiment]]. Crucially, Rowson separates [[regret|remorse]] from repentance: the [[ruin|fall]]en woman who feels [[regret|remorse]] but does not repent — [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] — is more damned than [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], whose [[regret|remorse]] leads to genuine [[regret|contrition]].

## Key Passages

> "I can only behold the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and remorse!" — Charlotte, on the verge of elopement ([012-044])

> "From the abyss of shame and remorse, is it not our duty to raise the fallen?" ([015-040])

> "She would then renew her complaints, and, with a heart bursting with disappointed love and wounded sensibility, retire to a bed which remorse had strewed with thorns" ([018-035])

> "Shame bows her to the earth, remorse tears her distracted mind" ([018-065])

> "Ruminating on the past scenes of his life, [Montraville] suffered the keenest remorse in the remembrance of Charlotte's seduction" ([026-036])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Remorse is her constant companion from the moment of her [[ruin|fall]]. Her "bed which [[regret|remorse]] had strewed with thorns" ([018-035]) is the novel's most vivid image of the concept. Her [[regret|remorse]], unlike [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s absent conscience, proves her moral worth even as it tortures her.
- [[montraville]]: Experiences [[regret|remorse]] belatedly, on the eve of his wedding to [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]]. His "keenest remorse in the remembrance of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction" ([026-036]) serves Rowson's warning to male [[seduction|seducer]]s.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The counter-example. She feels no [[regret|remorse]] for her role in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin, and Rowson uses her im[[regret|penitence]] to il[[passion|lust]]rate the ultimate stage of moral corruption.

## Narrative Function

Remorse is Rowson's instrument for converting plot into moral [[education|instruction]]. By immersing readers in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[regret|remorse]]-soaked interiority — her sleepless nights, her [[distress|anguish]]ed letters, her "bed of thorns" — Rowson ensures that the novel's didactic purpose is achieved through emotional identification rather than abstract precept. Remorse also serves a structural function: it marks the transition from [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s active transgression (Chapters 7–12) to her passive [[distress|suffering]] (Chapters 13–35), signalling that the novel's moral argument has shifted from warning to exemplum.

## Related Concepts

- [[guilt]]: Remorse is guilt's emotional manifestation — the felt pain of moral self-knowledge.
- [[repentance]]: Remorse that leads to amendment. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[regret|remorse]] culminates in genuine repentance; [[montraville|Montraville]]'s does not, making his [[regret|remorse]] a dead-end [[distress|suffering]].
- [[suffering]]: The broader category under which [[regret|remorse]] [[ruin|fall]]s. Remorse is moral [[distress|suffering]], distinguished from physical [[distress|suffering]] (illness, poverty) but no less real.
- [[shame]]: Remorse's social counterpart. [[shame|Shame]] is the blush of public exposure; [[regret|remorse]] is the private [[distress|anguish]] of conscience.
