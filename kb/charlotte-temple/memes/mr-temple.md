# Mr. Temple

Charlotte's father, whose [[modesty|modest]] estate and profound virtue establish the novel's moral standard. Mr. Temple is Rowson's portrait of patriarchal honour properly understood — not as reputation or social standing, but as principled [[charity|benevolence]] that actively works to repair the damage others have caused.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Temple is introduced early in the novel through the embedded story of his courtship of [[mrs-temple|Charlotte's mother]]. He "possessed a small estate of about four hundred pounds a year" (002-011), and his marriage to [[lucy|Lucy]] [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] — the daughter of the impoverished [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]], whom Temple be[[friendship|friends]] and supports — establishes his [[reputation|character]] as a man whose honour consists in action rather than words. His role becomes central in the novel's final chapters, when he searches for and finds the [[death|dying]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], offering her unconditional [[mercy|forgiveness]] and embo[[death|dying]] the novel's argument that true virtue responds to ruin with [[charity|compassion]] rather than condemnation.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Mr. Temple is generous, principled, forgiving, and steadfast. His treatment of [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] (002-033), his marriage to [[lucy|Lucy]] despite her [[family]]'s poverty, and his [[mercy|forgiveness]] of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] all demonstrate that his virtue is active and practical. He "declares his intention to [[honour|honourably]] unite his fate to hers" (017-011), publicly taking responsibility for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s honour.
- **Flaws**: Rowson gives Mr. Temple no significant flaws — he functions as a moral exemplar rather than a psychologically complex [[reputation|character]]. The only implicit criticism in the novel is that his education of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], while morally sound, failed to prepare her for the world's dangers.

## Key Actions

- Be[[friendship|friends]] the imprisoned [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]], paying his debts and securing his [[liberty|release]] (002-033).
- Marries [[lucy|Lucy]] [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]], [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s daughter, for [[affection|love]] rather than [[fate|fortune]] — a demonstration of his honour as active [[charity|benevolence]].
- Raises [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] as "the only pledge of their mutual [[affection|love]]" with moral [[education|instruction]] but insufficient worldly preparation (005-085).
- Upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin, searches for her rather than disowning her.
- Finds [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [[death|dying]] in poverty and offers her unconditional [[mercy|forgiveness]] in the novel's most moving scene (034-045).
- Publicly declares his intention to protect [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s honour by marrying her himself, a radical act of patriarchal restoration (017-011, 017-013).

## Relationships

- [[charlotte-temple]]: His [[love|beloved]] daughter, whose ruin he responds to with [[mercy|forgiveness]] rather than rejection. His arrival at her [[death|deathbed]] represents the novel's emotional and moral climax: "If thou art the father of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]], said he, gazing at the venerable stranger" (034-045).
- [[mrs-temple]]: His wife, [[lucy|Lucy]] [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]], whom he marries after be[[friendship|friend]]ing her father — a marriage founded on [[charity|compassion]] rather than convenience.
- [[captain-eldridge]]: His father-in-law, whom Temple rescues from debtor's prison, establishing his [[reputation|character]] as a man whose honour is expressed through benevolent action.

## Moral Significance

Mr. Temple is the novel's most important moral counterweight to its villains. Where [[montraville|Montraville]] uses the language of honour to deceive, Temple enacts honour as protection. Where [[belcour|Belcour]]'s designs "were never [[honour|honourable]]" (016-078), Temple's every action is directed toward the restoration of honour — his own, his [[family]]'s, and ultimately [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s. His declaration that "her honour henceforth is mine" (017-013) redefines honour as a male obligation to protect and restore, rather than exploit, female [[weakness|vulnerability]].

## Fate / Resolution

Mr. Temple survives the novel, having done everything a father could do. He finds [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], forgives her, and publicly assumes responsibility for her honour. His [[sorrow|grief]] at her death is profound but not destructive — he remains the moral anchor of the novel's world, demonstrating that genuine virtue endures even through tragedy.
