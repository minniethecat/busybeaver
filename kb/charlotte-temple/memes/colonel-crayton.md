# Colonel Crayton

A respectable older gentleman who becomes the unwitting husband of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]. His story il[[passion|lust]]rates the novel's uncomfortable acknowledgment that worldly justice does not always align with moral desert — the designing woman prospers through marriage to a worthy man.

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Crayton appears during the Atlantic crossing to [[america|America]], where [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], having been abandoned by [[belcour|Belcour]], "determined to change her battery, and attack the heart of Colonel Crayton" (016-025). He is deceived by her "feigned tale of distress" (016-023) and marries her, providing her with the social elevation that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — far less [[guilt|guilty]] than [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] — is denied. His later discovery of his wife's true [[reputation|character]] comes too late to undo the marriage.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Colonel Crayton is described as a worthy and respectable man. His willingness to marry a woman he believes to be wronged demonstrates his [[honour|honourable]] [[reputation|character]]. He is also the father of [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], whose virtues reflect well on his parenting.
- **Flaws**: He is susceptible to [[deceit|deception]], unable to distinguish genuine distress from calculated performance. His [[reason|judgment]] fails him precisely where it matters most — in choosing a wife.

## Key Actions

- Travels on the same ship as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[montraville|Montraville]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], and [[belcour|Belcour]] during the Atlantic crossing.
- Is targeted by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] as a replacement for the [[religion|faith]]less [[belcour|Belcour]] (016-025).
- Marries [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], believing her fabricated story of seduction and abandonment, and "[[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] became in due form [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]]" (017-077).
- Eventually discovers his wife's true [[reputation|character]] but, "after a little ineffectual struggle, he gave up the attempt" to reform her (028-053).

## Relationships

- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: His wife. Crayton is the unwitting instrument of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s social advancement. His decency makes him vulnerable to her manipulation, and his eventual recognition of her true nature cannot undo the marriage.
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: His daughter from a previous marriage. [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s virtue stands in stark contrast to her stepmother's corruption, creating painful domestic tensions.

## Moral Significance

Colonel Crayton's marriage to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is one of the novel's most troubling episodes. It demonstrates that the social institution of marriage — which should reward virtue — can instead be exploited by the designing and unprincipled. His story complicates the novel's moral framework by showing that worthy men can be deceived and that worldly [[wealth|prosperity]] does not always correlate with moral desert. Rowson offers no tidy resolution to this problem, acknowledging instead that "the endeavours of the wicked are often [[suffering|suffer]]ed to prosper" (028-026).

## Fate / Resolution

Colonel Crayton continues in his marriage to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], having "given up the attempt" to reform her. He survives the novel as a figure of diminished dignity — a worthy man trapped in a marriage to an unworthy woman, his domestic peace permanently compromised by his initial failure of [[reason|judgment]].
