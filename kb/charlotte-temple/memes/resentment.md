Resentment in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is a weapon of manipulation and a symptom of guilt. [[belcour|Belcour]] repeatedly seeks to "awaken her resentment against [[montraville|Montraville]]" as part of his scheme to isolate and seduce the abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. But resentment also appears as an involuntary reflex in [[montraville|Montraville]] himself — the "momentary flush" that crosses his cheek before giving way to the "death-like paleness" of remembered guilt.

## Key Passages

> "he took every opportunity of visiting her, and striving to awaken her resentment against Montraville" — Belcour's manipulation of Charlotte ([024-009])

> "Montraville started; a momentary flush of resentment crossed his cheek, but instantly gave place to a death-like paleness" — Montraville's reaction to being mocked ([024-043])

## Narrative Function

Rowson distinguishes between resentment as manipulation and resentment as involuntary response. [[belcour|Belcour]]'s calculated effort to "awaken" [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s resentment — to turn her [[grief]] into [[anger]] and thereby make her more pliable — exemplifies resentment as a tool of male predation. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "momentary flush of resentment" is more complex: it is the automatic response of wounded [[pride]], but it is immediately extinguished by conscience — "that monitor, whom, though we may in vain endeavour, we can never entirely silence" ([024-046]). Resentment thus marks the difference between the hardened villain who uses it and the guilty man who suppresses it.

## Related Concepts

- [[anger]]: The broader passion of which resentment is a specific, lingering form.
- [[revenge]]: Resentment nursed into action.
- [[pride]]: The wounded self-regard that feeds resentment.
