[014-001] SLOW and heavy passed the time while the carriage was conveying Mr.

[014-002] [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] home; and yet when he came in sight of the house, he wished a

[014-003] longer reprieve from the dreadful task of informing Mr. and Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]

[014-004] of their daughter's elopement.

[014-005] It is easy to judge the [[fear|anxiety]] of these affectionate parents, when they

[014-006] found the return of their father delayed so much beyond the expected

[014-007] time. They were now met in the dining parlour, and several of the [[youth|young]]

[014-008] people who had been invited were already arrived. Each different part of

[014-009] the company was employed in the same manner, looking out at the windows

[014-010] which faced the road. At length the long-expected chaise appeared. Mrs.

[014-011] Temple ran out to receive and welcome her darling: her young companions

[014-012] flocked round the door, each one eager to give her joy on the return

[014-013] of her birth-day. The door of the chaise was opened: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] was not

[014-014] there. “Where is my child?” cried [[mrs-temple|Mrs. Temple]], in breathless agitation.

[014-015] Mr. Eldridge could not answer: he took hold of his daughter's hand and

[014-016] led her into the house; and sinking on the first chair he came to, burst

[014-017] into tears, and sobbed aloud.

[014-018] “She is dead,” cried Mrs. Temple. “Oh my dear Charlotte!” and clasping

[014-019] her hands in an [[suffering|agony]] of distress, fell into strong hysterics.

[014-020] Mr. Temple, who had stood speechless with surprize and fear, now

[014-021] ventured to enquire if indeed his Charlotte was no more. Mr. Eldridge

[014-022] led him into another apartment; and putting the fatal note into

[014-023] his hand, cried--“Bear it like a Christian,” and turned from him,

[014-024] endeavouring to suppress his own too visible emotions.

[014-025] It would be vain to attempt describing what Mr. Temple felt whilst he

[014-026] hastily ran over the dreadful lines: when he had finished, the paper

[014-027] dropt from his unnerved hand. “Gracious heaven!” said he, “could

[014-028] Charlotte act thus?” Neither tear nor sigh escaped him; and he sat

[014-029] the image of mute [[melancholy|sorrow]], till roused from his stupor by the repeated

[014-030] shrieks of Mrs. Temple. He rose hastily, and rushing into the apartment

[014-031] where she was, folded his arms about her, and saying--“Let us be

[014-032] patient, my dear [[lucy|Lucy]],” nature relieved his almost bursting heart by a

[014-033] [[friendship|friendly]] gush of tears.

[014-034] Should any one, presuming on his own philosophic temper, look with an

[014-035] eye of contempt on the man who could indulge a woman's weakness, let him

[014-036] remember that man was a father, and he will then [[compassion|pity]] the [[distress|misery]] which

[014-037] wrung those drops from a noble, generous heart.

[014-038] Mrs. Temple beginning to be a little more composed, but still imagining

[014-039] her child was dead, her husband, gently taking her hand, cried--“You are

[014-040] mistaken, my [[affection|love]]. Charlotte is not dead.”

[014-041] “Then she is very ill, else why did she not come? But I will go to her:

[014-042] the chaise is still at the door: let me go instantly to the dear girl.

[014-043] If I was ill, she would fly to attend me, to alleviate my sufferings,

[014-044] and cheer me with her love.”

[014-045] “Be calm, my dearest Lucy, and I will tell you all,” said Mr. Temple.

[014-046] “You must not go, indeed you must not; it will be of no use.”

[014-047] “Temple,” said she, assuming a look of firmness and composure, “tell

[014-048] me the truth I beseech you. I cannot bear this dreadful suspense. What

[014-049] misfortune has befallen my child? Let me know the worst, and I will

[014-050] endeavour to bear it as I ought.”

[014-051] “Lucy,” replied Mr. Temple, “imagine your daughter alive, and in no

[014-052] danger of death: what misfortune would you then dread?”

[014-053] “There is one misfortune which is worse than death. But I know my child

[014-054] too well to suspect--”

[014-055] “Be not too confident, Lucy.”

[014-056] “Oh heavens!” said she, “what horrid images do you start: is it possible

[014-057] she should forget--”

[014-058] “She has forgot us all, my love; she has preferred the love of a

[014-059] stranger to the affectionate protection of her friends.

[014-060] “Not eloped?” cried she eagerly.

[014-061] Mr. Temple was silent.

[014-062] “You cannot contradict it,” said she. “I see my fate in those tearful

[014-063] eyes. Oh Charlotte! Charlotte! how ill have you requited our tenderness!

[014-064] But, Father of Mercies,” continued she, sinking on her knees, and

[014-065] raising her streaming eyes and clasped hands to heaven, “this once

[014-066] vouchsafe to hear a fond, a distracted mother's prayer. Oh let thy

[014-067] bounteous Providence watch over and protect the dear thoughtless girl,

[014-068] save her from the miseries which I fear will be her portion, and oh!

[014-069] of thine infinite [[compassion|mercy]], make her not a mother, lest she should one day

[014-070] feel what I now suffer.”

[014-071] The last words faultered on her tongue, and she fell fainting into the

[014-072] arms of her husband, who had involuntarily dropped on his knees beside

[014-073] her.

[014-074] A mother's [[grief|anguish]], when disappointed in her tenderest [[hope|hopes]], none but

[014-075] a mother can conceive. Yet, my dear young readers, I would have you read

[014-076] this scene with attention, and reflect that you may yourselves one day

[014-077] be mothers. Oh my friends, as you value your eternal happiness, wound

[014-078] not, by thoughtless [[gratitude|ingratitude]], the peace of the mother who bore you:

[014-079] remember the tenderness, the care, the unremitting anxiety with which

[014-080] she has attended to all your wants and wishes from earliest infancy to

[014-081] the present day; behold the mild ray of affectionate applause that beams

[014-082] from her eye on the performance of your duty: listen to her reproofs

[014-083] with silent attention; they proceed from a heart anxious for your future

[014-084] [[happiness|felicity]]: you must love her; nature, all-powerful nature, has planted

[014-085] the seeds of [[filial-piety|filial affection]] in your bosoms.

[014-086] Then once more read over the sorrows of poor Mrs. Temple, and remember,

[014-087] the mother whom you so dearly love and venerate will feel the same, when

[014-088] you, forgetful of the respect due to your maker and yourself, forsake

[014-089] the paths of virtue for those of vice and folly.
