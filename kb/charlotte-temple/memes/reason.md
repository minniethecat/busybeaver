# Reason

The rational faculty that, in Rowson's moral scheme, ought to govern conduct — its failure defines [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy, its restoration marks the beginning of repentance, and its total loss signals the final extremity of [[distress|suffering]].

## Definition

Reason in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* is less a philosophical concept than a practical moral faculty: the capacity to distinguish right from wrong and to act on that distinction despite the pressure of [[love|passion]]. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] is precipitated not by ignorance — she knows she should not leave school with [[montraville|Montraville]] — but by the suspension of reason under the influence of flattery, emotional manipulation, and her own untrained sensibility. The narrator notes that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "better reason disapproved" of the actions urged upon her, yet she complied nonetheless ([026-021]). In the novel's final extremity, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s reason is entirely "deprived" by [[distress|suffering]] ([031-075]), making her a spectacle of what happens when rational self-governance collapses. The novel thus functions as a conduct-book argument for cultivating reason as a bulwark against seduction.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The central case study — her reason is intact but overborne by [[love|passion]] and manipulation; its progressive failure traces her tragic arc ([026-021])
- [[montraville]]: Appeals to [[love|passion]] over reason, offering [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] a vision of romantic happiness that her rational faculty, if consulted, would reject ([012-076])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The active agent of unreason — she systematically undermines [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s judgment, encouraging the [[passion|passions]] that reason would restrain
- [[mr-temple]]: Represents reason embodied in benevolent patriarchy — his counsel and example model the rational self-governance [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] lacks

## Narrative Function

Reason functions as the absent guardian in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story. Its presence would have prevented the elopement; its absence enables every subsequent disaster. Rowson's narrative strategy is to make reason's importance visible through its conspicuous failure — the reader, witnessing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin, is meant to conclude that [[love|passion]] unchecked by reason leads inevitably to [[ruin|destruction]]. The restoration of reason at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] (her lucid confession and repentance) completes the novel's moral arc: reason returns too late to save her life but in time to save her soul.

## Related Concepts

- [[passion]]: Reason's antagonist — the force that overcomes rational judgment and drives the seduction plot forward
- [[prudence]]: Reason in its practical, decision-making form — the virtue [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] most conspicuously lacks
- [[folly]]: The consequence of reason's abdication — actions taken without rational de[[liberty|liberation]]
- [[repentance]]: The return of reason in its moral dimension — clear-eyed recognition of wrongdoing
