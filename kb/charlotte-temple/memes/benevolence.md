# Benevolence

Benevolence in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* represents the active, outward-facing dimension of [[virtue]] — not merely [[sensibility|feeling]] for others but doing for them. Rowson draws on the eighteenth-century language of "sensibility" and "beneficence" to construct an ideal of social morality in which individual [[compassion]] is expressed through concrete acts of aid and protection.

The novel's moral architecture places [[charity|benevolence]] in direct opposition to [[cruelty]]. Where cruelty isolates, neglects, and destroys, [[charity|benevolence]] connects, sustains, and restores. The benevolent [[reputation|character]]s in the novel — [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], the stranger who aids [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] in her final hours — serve as foils to the selfish and cruel, demonstrating that [[virtue]] is not merely the absence of vice but the active presence of goodwill.

## Benevolence Embodied

The novel's exemplary benevolent figure is described as known for his "[[charity|benevolence]] by all who knew him" [016-039]. This public dimension of [[charity|benevolence]] is significant: true [[charity|benevolence]], Rowson suggests, is visible, recognized, and woven into the fabric of social relations. It is not a private [[sensibility|sentiment]] but a public identity.

Yet the passage also introduces a crucial qualification: "but he was" — the sentence breaks, suggesting limits to even this exemplary [[charity|benevolence]]. Rowson's moral realism acknowledges that human [[charity|benevolence]] is imperfect, partial, and sometimes ineffectual. The most benevolent [[reputation|character]] cannot save everyone; the novel's tragedy unfolds despite the presence of benevolent actors.

## The Spirits of Benevolence

In one of the novel's most elevated passages, the narrator invokes the "dear spirits of [[charity|benevolence]], whose benign" influence guides moral action [029-048]. This personification elevates [[charity|benevolence]] from a human quality to a cosmic principle — a force that transcends individual actors and participates in divine [[fate|providence]].

The language of "spirits" and "benign" influence draws on the eighteenth-century discourse of "moral [[reason|sense]]" philosophy, particularly the work of Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, who argued that [[charity|benevolence]] is an [[nature|innate]] human disposition rather than a calculated self-interest. Rowson's narrator invokes this tradition to authorize the novel's moral claims.

## Benevolence in Action

The novel's final reference to [[charity|benevolence]] describes a [[reputation|character]] who practiced "[[charity|benevolence]], and returned home with a" heart full of the satisfaction that comes from doing good [033-042]. This passage emphasizes the reciprocity of benevolent action: it benefits not only the recipient but the giver. The benevolent person "returned home" enriched rather than depleted by generosity.

## Benevolence and the Sentimental Reader

Rowson's treatment of [[charity|benevolence]] serves a pedagogical purpose aimed directly at her readers. By presenting benevolent action as both morally obligatory and personally fulfilling, the novel encourages readers to emulate its exemplary figures. The narrator's direct addresses — "dear spirits of [[charity|benevolence]]" — invite readers to count themselves among those spirits, to become agents of the goodwill the novel celebrates.

This strategy aligns with the broader aims of [[sensibility|sentiment]]al fiction: to cultivate moral sensibility through narrative identification. Readers who weep for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] are being trained in the emotional foundations of [[charity|benevolence]]; readers who admire [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] are being shown [[charity|benevolence]]'s active expression. The novel thus functions as both mirror and model of benevolent sensibility.
