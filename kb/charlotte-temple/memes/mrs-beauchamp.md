# Mrs. Beauchamp

A [[youth|young]] married woman living near [[new-york|New-York]] who be[[friendship|friends]] the abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] during her pregnancy. Mrs. Beauchamp is Rowson's portrait of female [[virtue]] in action: she does not merely [[compassion|pity]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] from a distance but actively seeks to comfort and protect her. In the novel's moral economy, she serves as the counterweight to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] — where [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s corrupted sensibility makes her a predator of innocence, Mrs. Beauchamp's intact sensibility makes her a protector of the [[ruin|fall]]en. Her [[compassion]] is the novel's clearest argument that true [[love]] is not romantic [[love|passion]] but active [[charity|benevolence]] toward the [[distress|suffering]].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Beauchamp enters the narrative after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has been abandoned by [[montraville]] and is living in increasing isolation. She is introduced as a neighbour whose "benevolent heart was interested" in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s situation (017-054). Her role is to provide a counter-example to [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]: where [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] corrupted [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence, Mrs. Beauchamp attempts to restore her hope. She later serves as a crucial link in the narrative chain, helping [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] locate his [[death|dying]] daughter.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Mrs. Beauchamp is [[reputation|character]]ized by genuine [[charity|compassion]], moral courage, and active [[charity|benevolence]]. The narrator presents her as a woman whose [[sensibility]] is properly directed toward relieving the [[distress|suffering]] of others. She does not share the social prejudice that would condemn a [[ruin|fall]]en woman to isolation — an act of moral independence that distinguishes her from the novel's other female [[reputation|character]]s.
- **Flaws**: Rowson gives Mrs. Beauchamp no significant flaws — she serves as a moral exemplar of sympathetic womanhood, much as [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] serves as the exemplar of benevolent patriarchy.

## Key Actions

- Visits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] after learning of her situation from the landlady, offering [[friendship|friends]]hip rather than [[reason|judgment]] (017-054).
- Provides emotional support and practical assistance to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] during her pregnancy.
- Attempts to mediate between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and her father by writing to [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] about [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s whereabouts.
- Attends [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] during her final illness and prepares [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] for the shock of seeing his daughter in such reduced circumstances (033-084).

> "Mrs. Beauchamp, whose benevolent heart was interested in the fate of this unhappy girl" (017-054)

This introduction frames Mrs. Beauchamp's motivation as [[charity|benevolence]] — a form of [[love]] that expects nothing in return. Unlike [[montraville|Montraville]]'s self-interested [[love|passion]], Mrs. Beauchamp's [[affection|love]] is pure gift, modeling the [[charity|Christian charity]] that the novel's moral framework assumes but rarely depicts so directly.

## Relationships

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her [[friendship|friend]] and protegée. Mrs. Beauchamp's [[charity|compassion]] provides the only genuine comfort [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] receives after her abandonment. She treats [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] not as a [[ruin|fall]]en woman to be shunned but as a [[distress|suffering]] human being deserving of kindness.
- [[colonel-crayton]]: Her father. This relationship becomes significant when [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]] marries [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], making [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s corrupter into Mrs. Beauchamp's stepmother — a painful irony that Mrs. Beauchamp confronts with [[reputation|character]]istic dignity.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Her eventual stepmother. Mrs. Beauchamp is "shocked to the soul to find how large a share [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] had in the seduction of this amiable girl" (021-055). This discovery creates one of the novel's most complex relational knots: the [[virtue|virtuous]] daughter confronting her father's marriage to a woman she knows to be morally corrupt.

## Moral Significance

Mrs. Beauchamp represents the novel's argument that female virtue is not merely a passive state (the preservation of chastity) but an active force (the exercise of [[charity|compassion]]). She is the anti-[[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]: where [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s lost [[honour|honour]] made her a predator of innocence, Mrs. Beauchamp's intact honour makes her a protector of the [[ruin|fall]]en. Her conduct toward [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] embodies the novel's central moral claim — that true [[sensibility]] manifests as [[charity|benevolence]] toward the [[distress|suffering]], regardless of their social standing.

In the novel's broader symbolic geography, Mrs. Beauchamp's presence in [[new-york|New-York]] is significant. [[new-york|New-York]] is the space of isolation and [[weakness|vulnerability]]; Mrs. Beauchamp's [[charity|compassion]] proves that benevolent community is possible even in the place of maximum exposure. If [[england|England]] is the novel's symbolic home of institutional protection, Mrs. Beauchamp demonstrates that individual [[charity|compassion]] can create islands of protection even in the unprotected New World.

## Fate / Resolution

Mrs. Beauchamp survives the novel and continues in her role as wife and mother. She is present at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]], having fulfilled her function as the novel's exemplar of [[charity|compassion]]ate womanhood. Her final acts include facilitating [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s reunion with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and witnessing the redemptive power of parental [[mercy|forgiveness]].
