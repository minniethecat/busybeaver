# Ruin

The terminal state in the seduction novel's moral trajectory: ruin encompasses the loss of virginity, reputation, [[family]], and worldly prospects that follows a [[youth|young]] woman's fall. In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, ruin is presented as irreversible in social terms yet not final in spiritual ones — the novel's insistence on the possibility of repentance ensures that "ruined" is never the last word.

## Definition

In Rowson's moral vocabulary, ruin is the comprehensive destruction that seduction effects upon a [[youth|young]] woman. It encompasses several dimensions: **moral ruin** — the loss of virtue and innocence; **social ruin** — the forfeiture of reputation and respectable place in society; **[[family|familial]] ruin** — the severance from parents and the destruction of their peace; and **material ruin** — the poverty, homelessness, and physical deterioration that follow abandonment.

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [[experience]]s ruin as a progressive condition rather than a single event. She perceives it first as a future threat: "inclination urges me on to ruin" (011-069). Standing at the brink, she beholds "the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and [[regret|remorse]]" (012-044). After her fall, she acknowledges that [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] "under the mask of [[friendship|friends]]hip, drew me on to ruin" (022-029). In the novel's final third, ruin becomes an identity: she is "the ruined" (031-031), unrecognizable to former acquaintances. Yet the novel's closing formulation — [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] grieving for his "ruined, but repentant child" (033-082) — subordinates ruin to repentance, insisting that spiritual restoration can coexist with worldly destruction.

The novel also emphasizes that ruin is inflicted, not chosen. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is "drawn on" to ruin (022-029), not self-propelled; [[belcour|Belcour]] is "bent on the complete ruin of the un[[happiness|happy]] girl" (026-031); and the Preface frames the novel as the story of what "ruined poor [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]" (P01-035). This consistent use of the passive voice distributes moral agency across [[seduction|seducer]], accomplice, and circumstance, complicating any reading that would hold [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] solely responsible for her fate.

## Key Passages

> "indeed I do repent," replied Charlotte, "from my soul: but while discretion points out the impropriety of my conduct, inclination urges me on to ruin." (011-069)

This passage is remarkable for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moral self-awareness at the very moment of her fall. She perceives ruin clearly, repents sincerely, and yet proceeds — a psychological portrait of the divided will that deepens the novel's tragic dimension. The word "urges" emphasizes the compulsive, almost involuntary quality of her trajectory.

> "it is not too late to recede from the brink of a precipice, from which I can only behold the dark abyss of ruin, shame, and remorse!" (012-044)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moment of clarity upon receiving a letter from her parents. The metaphor of the "precipice" and "dark abyss" conveys ruin as a spatial fall — irreversible once taken, bottomless in its consequences. The triple formula of "ruin, shame, and [[regret|remorse]]" captures ruin's three dimensions: the objective condition (ruin), the social [[reason|judgment]] (shame), and the internal [[distress|suffering]] ([[regret|remorse]]).

> "under the mask of friendship, drew me on to ruin. Yet think not your Charlotte was so lost as to voluntarily rush into a life of infamy" (022-029)

From [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s letter to her mother, this passage distributes responsibility for her ruin. She acknowledges [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s agency ("drew me on") while insisting on her own lack of full volition ("not voluntarily rush"). The "mask of [[friendship|friends]]hip" trope positions female [[deceit|betrayal]] as a distinct and particularly pernicious species of ruin's causation.

> "followed the man up stairs) 'not know me, not remember the ruined'" (031-031)

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], now destitute and physically altered by [[distress|suffering]], confronts [[belcour|Belcour]] unrecognized. The definite article — "the ruined" — marks ruin as having become her identity, a condition so total that it has effaced her former self. The paradox is poignant: [[belcour|Belcour]] helped ruin her, yet cannot even recognize the ruin he has wrought.

> "all for the loss of a ruined, ungrateful child." (032-045) / "ruined, but repentant child." (033-082)

The novel's framing of ruin's meaning evolves across two chapters. [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] first grieves the loss of "a ruined, ungrateful child" (032-045), where ruin and [[gratitude|ingratitude]] are coupled as aggravating circumstances. But the novel's final word on the subject — "ruined, but repentant child" (033-082) — inserts the redemptive "but" that transforms ruin from a terminal condition into a state that repentance can transcend.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central figure of ruin, tracing the complete arc from [[virtue|virtuous]] innocence through seduction to destitute death. Her trajectory il[[passion|lust]]rates ruin's progressive nature — it is not a single moment but a sequence of losses (virtue, [[family]], reputation, health, life) — and its final subordination to repentance.
- [[montraville]]: The primary agent of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin. His professions of "everlasting [[affection|love]] and constancy" initiate the seduction that leads to her destruction, and his abandonment seals her fate. His [[regret|remorse]], while genuine, cannot reverse the ruin he has caused, il[[passion|lust]]rating the irreversibility of worldly consequences.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The female agent of ruin whose role the novel treats as uniquely pernicious. She "under the mask of [[friendship|friends]]hip" (022-029) dismisses [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moral scruples, mocks her fear of ruin ("Ruin! fiddlestick!" (011-070)), and actively facilitates the elopement. Unlike [[montraville|Montraville]], she shows no [[regret|remorse]], and unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], she prospers in worldly terms — a bitter irony that complicates the novel's moral scheme.

## Narrative Function

Ruin is the destination toward which the novel's plot drives, and the condition from which its theological resolution must redeem. Rowson uses ruin as a warning — the Preface states that the tale is designed to prevent [[youth|young]] women from sharing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fate — but also as a site of moral complexity. The novel consistently distributes responsibility for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin across multiple agents ([[montraville|Montraville]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], [[belcour|Belcour]]) while holding [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] accountable for her failure of [[reason|prudence]]. This complexity serves the didactic purpose: readers are warned not only against [[seduction|seducer]]s but against the [[friendship|friends]], circumstances, and internal weaknesses that facilitate ruin.

The narrative also uses ruin to structure the novel's emotional arc. The early chapters build toward ruin as threat; the middle chapters inhabit ruin as [[experience]]; and the final chapters transcend ruin through repentance and [[mercy|forgiveness]]. This movement from warning through [[distress|suffering]] to redemption gives the novel its [[reputation|character]]istic [[sensibility|sentiment]]al shape and ensures that its moral message is neither purely punitive nor naively optimistic.
