[002-001] MR. [[mr-temple|Temple]] was the youngest son of a nobleman whose [[fate|fortune]] was by no

[002-002] means adequate to the antiquity, grandeur, and I may add, pride of the

[002-003] [[family]]. He saw his elder brother made completely wretched by marrying a

[002-004] disagreeable woman, whose [[wealth|fortune]] helped to prop the sinking dignity

[002-005] of the house; and he beheld his sisters legally prostituted to old,

[002-006] decrepid men, whose titles gave them consequence in the eyes of the

[002-007] world, and whose affluence rendered them splendidly miserable. “I will

[002-008] not sacrifice internal happiness for outward shew,” said he: “I will

[002-009] seek Content; and, if I find her in a cottage, will embrace her with as

[002-010] much cordiality as I should if seated on a throne.”

[002-011] Mr. Temple possessed a small estate of about five hundred pounds a year;

[002-012] and with that he resolved to preserve independence, to marry where the

[002-013] feelings of his heart should direct him, and to confine his expenses

[002-014] within the limits of his income. He had a heart open to every generous

[002-015] [[sensibility|feeling]] of humanity, and a hand ready to dispense to those who wanted

[002-016] part of the blessings he enjoyed himself.

[002-017] As he was universally known to be the [[friendship|friend]] of the unfortunate, his

[002-018] advice and bounty was frequently solicited; nor was it seldom that he

[002-019] sought out indigent merit, and raised it from obscurity, confining his

[002-020] own expenses within a very narrow compass.

[002-021] “You are a benevolent fellow,” said a [[youth|young]] officer to him one day; “and

[002-022] I have a great mind to give you a fine subject to exercise the [[kindness|goodness]]

[002-023] of your heart upon.”

[002-024] “You cannot oblige me more,” said Temple, “than to point out any way by

[002-025] which I can be serviceable to my fellow creatures.”

[002-026] “Come along then,” said the young man, “we will go and visit a man who

[002-027] is not in so good a lodging as he deserves; and, were it not that he

[002-028] has an angel with him, who comforts and supports him, he must long since

[002-029] have sunk under his misfortunes.” The young man's heart was too full

[002-030] to proceed; and Temple, unwilling to irritate his feelings by making

[002-031] further enquiries, followed him in silence, til they arrived at the

[002-032] Fleet prison.

## Section 2

[002-033] The officer enquired for [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]: a person led them up several

[002-034] pair of dirty stairs, and pointing to a door which led to a miserable,

[002-035] small apartment, said that was the Captain's room, and retired.

[002-036] The officer, whose name was Blakeney, tapped at the door, and was bid to

[002-037] enter by a voice melodiously soft. He opened the door, and discovered to

[002-038] Temple a scene which rivetted him to the spot with astonishment.

[002-039] The apartment, though small, and bearing strong marks of poverty, was

[002-040] neat in the extreme. In an arm-chair, his head reclined upon his hand,

[002-041] his eyes fixed on a book which lay open before him, sat an aged man in

[002-042] a Lieutenant's uniform, which, though threadbare, would sooner call a

[002-043] blush of shame into the face of those who could neglect real merit, than

[002-044] cause the hectic of confusion to glow on the cheeks of him who wore it.

[002-045] Beside him sat a lovely creature busied in painting a fan mount. She was

[002-046] fair as the lily, but [[melancholy|sorrow]] had nipped the rose in her cheek before it

[002-047] was half blown. Her eyes were blue; and her hair, which was light brown,

[002-048] was slightly confined under a plain muslin cap, tied round with a black

[002-049] ribbon; a white linen gown and plain lawn handkerchief composed

[002-050] the remainder of her dress; and in this simple attire, she was more

[002-051] irresistibly charming to such a heart as Temple's, than she would have

[002-052] been, if adorned with all the splendor of a courtly belle.

[002-053] When they entered, the old man arose from his seat, and shaking Blakeney

[002-054] by the hand with great cordiality, offered Temple his chair; and there

[002-055] being but three in the room, seated himself on the side of his little

[002-056] bed with evident composure.

[002-057] “This is a strange place,” said he to Temple, “to receive visitors of

[002-058] distinction in; but we must fit our feelings to our station. While I am

[002-059] not ashamed to own the cause which brought me here, why should I blush

[002-060] at my situation? Our misfortunes are not our faults; and were it not for

[002-061] that poor girl--”

[002-062] Here the philosopher was lost in the father. He rose hastily from his

[002-063] seat, and walking toward the window, wiped off a tear which he was

[002-064] afraid would tarnish the cheek of a sailor.

[002-065] Temple cast his eye on Miss Eldridge: a pellucid drop had stolen from

[002-066] her eyes, and fallen upon a rose she was painting. It blotted and

[002-067] discoloured the flower. “'Tis emblematic,” said he mentally: “the rose

[002-068] of youth and health soon fades when watered by the tear of [[adversity|affliction]].”

[002-069] “My friend Blakeney,” said he, addressing the old man, “told me I could

[002-070] be of service to you: be so kind then, dear Sir, as to point out some

[002-071] way in which I can relieve the [[fear|anxiety]] of your heart and increase the

[002-072] pleasures of my own.”

[002-073] “My good young man,” said Eldridge, “you know not what you offer. While

[002-074] deprived of my [[independence|liberty]] I cannot be free from anxiety on my own account;

[002-075] but that is a trifling concern; my anxious thoughts extend to one more

[002-076] dear a thousand times than life: I am a poor weak old man, and must

[002-077] expect in a few years to sink into silence and oblivion; but when I am

[002-078] gone, who will protect that fair bud of innocence from the blasts of

[002-079] [[misfortune|adversity]], or from the cruel hand of insult and [[shame|dishonour]].”

[002-080] “Oh, my father!” cried Miss Eldridge, tenderly taking his hand, “be not

[002-081] anxious on that account; for daily are my prayers offered to heaven that

[002-082] our lives may terminate at the same instant, and one grave receive us

[002-083] both; for why should I live when deprived of my only friend.”

[002-084] Temple was moved even to tears. “You will both live many years,” said

[002-085] he, “and I hope see much happiness. Cheerly, my friend, cheerly; these

[002-086] passing clouds of adversity will serve only to make the sunshine of

[002-087] prosperity more pleasing. But we are losing time: you might ere this

[002-088] have told me who were your creditors, what were their demands, and other

[002-089] particulars necessary to your [[liberty|liberation]].”

[002-090] “My story is short,” said Mr. Eldridge, “but there are some particulars

[002-091] which will wring my heart barely to remember; yet to one whose offers

[002-092] of friendship appear so open and disinterested, I will relate every

[002-093] circumstance that led to my present, painful situation. But my child,”

[002-094] continued he, addressing his daughter, “let me prevail on you to take

[002-095] this opportunity, while my friends are with me, to enjoy the benefit of

[002-096] air and exercise.”

[002-097] “Go, my [[affection|love]]; leave me now; to-morrow at your usual hour I will expect

[002-098] you.”

[002-099] Miss Eldridge impressed on his cheek the kiss of [[filial-piety|filial affection]], and

[002-100] obeyed.
