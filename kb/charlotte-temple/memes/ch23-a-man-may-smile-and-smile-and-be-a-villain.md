[023-001] WHILE [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] was enjoying some small degree of comfort in the

[023-002] consoling friendship of [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], [[montraville|Montraville]] was advancing

[023-003] rapidly in his [[love|affection]] towards [[julia-franklin|Miss Franklin]]. Julia was an amiable

[023-004] girl; she saw only the fair side of his [[reputation|character]]; she possessed an

[023-005] independent [[fate|fortune]], and resolved to be [[happiness|happy]] with the man of her heart,

[023-006] though his rank and [[wealth|fortune]] were by no means so exalted as she had a

[023-007] right to expect; she saw the passion which Montraville struggled to

[023-008] conceal; she wondered at his timidity, but imagined the distance fortune

[023-009] had placed between them occasioned his backwardness, and made every

[023-010] advance which strict [[reason|prudence]] and a becoming modesty would permit.

[023-011] Montraville saw with pleasure he was not indifferent to her, but a

[023-012] spark of honour which animated his bosom would not [[suffering|suffer]] him to take

[023-013] advantage of her partiality. He was well acquainted with Charlotte's

[023-014] situation, and he thought there would be a double cruelty in forsaking

[023-015] her at such a time; and to marry Miss [[mr-franklin|Franklin]], while honour, humanity,

[023-016] every sacred law, obliged him still to protect and support Charlotte,

[023-017] was a baseness which his soul shuddered at.

[023-018] He communicated his uneasiness to [[belcour|Belcour]]: it was the very thing this

[023-019] pretended [[friendship|friend]] had wished. “And do you really,” said he, laughing,

[023-020] “hesitate at marrying the lovely Julia, and becoming master of her

[023-021] fortune, because a little [[folly|foolish]], fond girl chose to leave her friends,

[023-022] and run away with you to [[america|America]]. Dear Montraville, act more like a

[023-023] man of sense; this whining, pining Charlotte, who occasions you so much

[023-024] uneasiness, would have eloped with somebody else if she had not with

[023-025] you.”

[023-026] “Would to heaven,” said Montraville, “I had never seen her; my regard

[023-027] for her was but the momentary passion of [[passion|desire]], but I feel I shall love

[023-028] and revere Julia Franklin as long as I live; yet to leave poor Charlotte

[023-029] in her present situation would be cruel beyond description.”

[023-030] “Oh my good sentimental friend,” said Belcour, “do you imagine no body

[023-031] has a right to provide for the brat but yourself.”

[023-032] Montraville started. “Sure,” said he, “you cannot mean to insinuate that

[023-033] Charlotte is false.”

[023-034] “I don't insinuate it,” said Belcour, “I know it.”

[023-035] Montraville turned pale as ashes. “Then there is no [[religion|faith]] in woman,”

[023-036] said he.

[023-037] “While I thought you attached to her,” said Belcour with an air of

[023-038] indifference, “I never wished to make you uneasy by mentioning her

[023-039] perfidy, but as I know you love and are beloved by Miss Franklin, I was

[023-040] determined not to let these foolish scruples of honour step between you

[023-041] and happiness, or your tenderness for the peace of a perfidious girl

[023-042] prevent your uniting yourself to a woman of honour.”

[023-043] “Good heavens!” said Montraville, “what poignant reflections does a man

[023-044] endure who sees a lovely woman plunged in infamy, and is conscious he

[023-045] was her first [[seduction|seducer]]; but are you certain of what you say, Belcour?”

[023-046] “So far,” replied he, “that I myself have received advances from her

[023-047] which I would not take advantage of out of regard to you: but hang it,

[023-048] think no more about her. I dined at Franklin's to-day, and Julia bid

[023-049] me seek and bring you to tea: so come along, my lad, make good use of

[023-050] opportunity, and seize the gifts of fortune while they are within your

[023-051] reach.” Montraville was too much agitated to pass a happy evening even

[023-052] in the company of Julia Franklin: he determined to visit Charlotte early

[023-053] the next morning, tax her with her falsehood, and take an everlasting

[023-054] leave of her; but when the morning came, he was commanded on duty, and

[023-055] for six weeks was prevented from putting his design in execution.

[023-056] At length he found an hour to spare, and walked out to spend it with

[023-057] Charlotte: it was near four o'clock in the afternoon when he arrived at

[023-058] her cottage; she was not in the parlour, and without calling the servant

[023-059] he walked up stairs, thinking to find her in her bed room. He opened the

[023-060] door, and the first object that met his eyes was Charlotte asleep on the

[023-061] bed, and Belcour by her side.

[023-062] “[[death|Death]] and distraction,” said he, stamping, “this is too much. Rise,

[023-063] villain, and defend yourself.” Belcour sprang from the bed. The noise

[023-064] awoke Charlotte; terrified at the furious appearance of Montraville, and

[023-065] seeing Belcour with him in the chamber, she caught hold of his arm as he

[023-066] stood by the bed-side, and eagerly asked what was the matter.

[023-067] “Treacherous, infamous girl,” said he, “can you ask? How came he here?”

[023-068] pointing to Belcour.

[023-069] “As heaven is my witness,” replied she weeping, “I do not know. I have

[023-070] not seen him for these three weeks.”

[023-071] “Then you confess he sometimes visits you?”

[023-072] “He came sometimes by your [[passion|desire]].”

[023-073] “'Tis false; I never desired him to come, and you know I did not: but

[023-074] mark me, Charlotte, from this instant our connexion is at an end. Let

[023-075] Belcour, or any other of your favoured lovers, take you and provide for

[023-076] you; I have done with you for ever.”

[023-077] He was then going to leave her; but starting wildly from the bed, she

[023-078] threw herself on her knees before him, protesting her innocence and

[023-079] entreating him not to leave her. “Oh Montraville,” said she, “kill me,

[023-080] for [[compassion|pity]]'s sake kill me, but do not doubt my fidelity. Do not leave me

[023-081] in this horrid situation; for the sake of your unborn child, oh! spurn

[023-082] not the wretched mother from you.”

[023-083] “Charlotte,” said he, with a firm voice, “I shall take care that neither

[023-084] you nor your child [[poverty|want]] any thing in the approaching painful hour; but

[023-085] we meet no more.” He then endeavoured to raise her from the ground;

[023-086] but in vain; she clung about his knees, entreating him to believe her

[023-087] innocent, and conjuring Belcour to clear up the dreadful mystery.

[023-088] Belcour cast on Montraville a smile of contempt: it irritated him almost

[023-089] to madness; he broke from the feeble arms of the distressed girl; she

[023-090] shrieked and fell prostrate on the floor.

[023-091] Montraville instantly left the house and returned hastily to [[new-york|the city]].
