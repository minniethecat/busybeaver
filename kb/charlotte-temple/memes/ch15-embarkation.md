[015-001] IT was with the utmost difficulty that the united efforts of

[015-002] Mademoiselle and [[montraville|Montraville]] could support [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s spirits during

[015-003] their short ride from [[chichester|Chichester]] to [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]], where a boat waited to

[015-004] take them immediately on board the ship in which they were to embark for

[015-005] [[america|America]].

[015-006] As soon as she became tolerably composed, she entreated pen and ink

[015-007] to write to her parents. This she did in the most affecting, artless

[015-008] manner, entreating their [[forgiveness|pardon]] and blessing, and describing

[015-009] the dreadful situation of her mind, the conflict she suffered in

[015-010] endeavouring to conquer this unfortunate attachment, and concluded

[015-011] with saying, her only hope of future comfort consisted in the (perhaps

[015-012] delusive) idea she indulged, of being once more folded in their

[015-013] protecting arms, and hearing the words of peace and pardon from their

[015-014] lips.

[015-015] The tears streamed incessantly while she was writing, and she was

[015-016] frequently obliged to lay down her pen: but when the task was completed,

[015-017] and she had committed the letter to the care of Montraville to be sent

[015-018] to the post office, she became more calm, and indulging the delightful

[015-019] hope of soon receiving an answer that would seal her pardon, she in some

[015-020] measure assumed her usual cheerfulness.

[015-021] But Montraville knew too well the consequences that must unavoidably

[015-022] ensue, should this letter reach [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]: he therefore wisely resolved

[015-023] to walk on the deck, tear it in pieces, and commit the fragments to the

[015-024] care of Neptune, who might or might not, as it suited his convenience,

[015-025] convey them on shore.

[015-026] All Charlotte's [[hope|hopes]] and wishes were now concentred in one, namely that

[015-027] the fleet might be detained at Spithead till she could receive a letter

[015-028] from her [[friendship|friends]]: but in this she was disappointed, for the second

[015-029] morning after she went on board, the signal was made, the fleet weighed

[015-030] anchor, and in a few hours (the wind being favourable) they bid adieu to

[015-031] the white cliffs of Al-bion.

[015-032] In the mean time every enquiry that could be thought of was made by Mr.

[015-033] and [[mrs-temple|Mrs. Temple]]; for many days did they indulge the fond hope that she

[015-034] was merely gone off to be married, and that when the indissoluble knot

[015-035] was once tied, she would return with the partner she had chosen, and

[015-036] entreat their blessing and [[mercy|forgiveness]].

[015-037] “And shall we not forgive her?” said Mr. Temple.

[015-038] “Forgive her!” exclaimed the mother. “Oh yes, whatever be our errors,

[015-039] is she not our child? and though bowed to the earth even with shame

[015-040] and [[repentance|remorse]], is it not our duty to raise the poor [[repentance|penitent]], and whisper

[015-041] peace and comfort to her desponding soul? would she but return, with

[015-042] rapture would I fold her to my heart, and bury every remembrance of her

[015-043] faults in the dear embrace.”

[015-044] But still day after day passed on, and Charlotte did not appear,

[015-045] nor were any tidings to be heard of her: yet each rising morning was

[015-046] welcomed by some new hope--the evening brought with it disappointment.

[015-047] At length hope was no more; despair usurped her place; and the mansion

[015-048] which was once the mansion of peace, became the habitation of pale,

[015-049] dejected melancholy.

[015-050] The cheerful smile that was wont to adorn the face of Mrs. Temple was

[015-051] fled, and had it not been for the support of unaffected [[devotion|piety]], and a

[015-052] consciousness of having ever set before her child the fairest example,

[015-053] she must have sunk under this heavy [[adversity|affliction]].

[015-054] “Since,” said she, “the severest scrutiny cannot charge me with any

[015-055] breach of duty to have deserved this severe chastisement, I will bow

[015-056] before the power who inflicts it with humble resignation to his will;

[015-057] nor shall the duty of a wife be totally absorbed in the feelings of the

[015-058] mother; I will endeavour to appear more cheerful, and by appearing in

[015-059] some measure to have conquered my own [[melancholy|sorrow]], alleviate the [[suffering|sufferings]]

[015-060] of my husband, and rouse him from that torpor into which this misfortune

[015-061] has plunged him. My father too demands my care and attention: I must

[015-062] not, by a selfish indulgence of my own [[sorrow|grief]], forget the interest those

[015-063] two dear objects take in my happiness or [[distress|misery]]: I will wear a smile on

[015-064] my face, though the thorn rankles in my heart; and if by so doing, I in

[015-065] the smallest degree contribute to restore their peace of mind, I shall

[015-066] be amply rewarded for the pain the concealment of my own feelings may

[015-067] occasion.”

## Section 9

[015-068] Thus argued this excellent woman: and in the execution of so laudable

[015-069] a resolution we shall leave her, to follow the fortunes of the hapless

[015-070] victim of [[prudence|imprudence]] and evil counsellors.
