Courage, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is reconfigured from its masculine associations with martial bravery into a private, domestic virtue. The courage Rowson values is not displayed on battlefields but in sickrooms, in moral resistance, and in the endurance of [[distress|suffering]]. The novel's single direct use of the word "courage" — when [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] recounts how he "assumed courage" to ask his [[death|dying]] wife about their lost children ([004-003]) — frames the virtue as the moral strength required to face devastating emotional truths.

## Definition

Rowson defines courage implicitly through the novel's moral exemplars: it is the fortitude to act rightly in the face of fear, loss, or social pressure. Unlike the martial courage celebrated in masculine adventure narratives, Rowson's courage is exercised in domestic space — in the sickroom, in the confrontation with a [[seduction|seducer]], in the endurance of poverty. The novel distinguishes between true courage and its counterfeits: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s boldness in pursuit is not courage but reckless [[love|passion]], while [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s quiet endurance of his [[family]]'s [[ruin|destruction]] exemplifies the virtue in its purest form.

## Key Passages

> "At length I assumed courage to ask my dear girl how long her mother and brother had been dead." ([004-003])

> "She encouraged her hopes, calmed her fears, and before they parted for the night, it was determined that she should meet Montraville the ensuing evening." ([007-103])

> "He rather encouraged the growing passion of Montraville; and being pleased with the vivacity of Mademoiselle, resolved to leave no argument untried." ([009-059])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mr-temple]]: The novel's exemplar of moral courage. He faces the [[ruin|destruction]] of his [[family]] — his wife's death, his son's death, his daughter's ruin — with a fortitude that the narrator presents as heroic. His "courage" in asking about his lost children ([004-003]) is the novel's definitive image of the virtue.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] possesses a nascent courage that is tragically misdirected. She summons the courage to defy her parents and elope with [[montraville|Montraville]] — an act the novel frames as the misapplication of a virtue that, properly directed, would have fortified her resistance to seduction.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Represents the corruption of "encouragement." Where true courage strengthens moral resistance, [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s encouragements ([007-103]) weaken [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s defences, demonstrating how the language of fortitude can be weaponized by the predatory.

## Narrative Function

Courage serves as a diagnostic virtue in the novel's moral system: how a [[reputation|character]] exercises (or fails to exercise) courage reveals their moral standing. The novel's tragedy is partly one of misplaced courage — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s boldness in defying convention is the wrong kind of courage, exercised in service of [[love|passion]] rather than principle. Rowson's didactic purpose is served by showing that true courage is quiet, domestic, and aligned with duty, while its counterfeits lead to ruin.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Courage is a subordinate virtue in Rowson's moral system, one component of the comprehensive "virtue" that the novel exhorts readers to p[[modesty|reserve]].
- [[prudence]]: The companion virtue to courage. Without [[reason|prudence]], courage becomes recklessness — as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement demonstrates.
- [[suffering]]: Courage finds its deepest expression in the endurance of [[distress|suffering]]. The novel's most courageous figures are those who [[suffering|suffer]] without complaint.
- [[innocence]]: [[innocence|Innocence]] without courage is vulnerable; courage without innocence is dangerous. The novel insists on their pairing.
