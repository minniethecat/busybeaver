Beauty in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is a deeply ambivalent attribute — a gift of nature that makes its possessor "[[affection|love]]ly as nature could make it" ([005-015]) yet simultaneously renders her a target. Rowson's treatment of female beauty is radically pragmatic: beauty attracts male [[passion|desire]], male [[passion|desire]] leads to seduction, and seduction leads to ruin. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s beauty is repeatedly invoked not as a cause for celebration but as a source of danger, making the novel's treatment of the concept a sustained warning about the [[weakness|vulnerability]] that physical attractiveness entails in a world of predatory men.

## Definition

Rowson presents beauty as a [[nature|natural]] endowment that carries social risk disproportionate to its virtue. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is described as possessing "beauty of person, elegance of manner" ([006-057]), and [[montraville|Montraville]]'s initial interest is explicitly aesthetic — "encomiums on her beauty" ([006-048]). The novel's logic is clear: beauty attracts, and attraction, in the absence of vigilance, leads to ruin. The narrator's lament that [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] "labours to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" ([007-097]) crystallizes beauty's status as a [[weakness|vulnerability]] — something that must be protected rather than merely admired. Yet beauty is not condemned; when [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] lies [[death|dying]], "nature... heightened every beauty" ([024-086]), suggesting that beauty transcends its dangerous social function.

## Key Passages

> "She could refuse a girl of her beauty and fashion nothing" — on Charlotte's indulgent upbringing ([005-021])

> "His conversation was made up of encomiums on her beauty, and vows of everlasting love" ([006-048])

> "But when beauty of person, elegance of manner, and every human accomplishment, are united in a female form, she must expect to be beloved" — the narrator's warning ([006-057])

> "She labours to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" — on La Rue's predatory designs ([007-097])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Beauty's paradigmatic bearer and victim. Her physical attractiveness is the occasion of her down[[ruin|fall]] — it attracts [[montraville|Montraville]], who would otherwise never have noticed a schoolgirl — yet the novel insists that beauty itself is not the fault.
- [[montraville]]: Beauty's consumer. His attraction to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is aesthetic before it is emotional, and his abandonment of her for [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]] repeats the pattern: beauty captivates, but captivation does not guarantee fidelity.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Beauty's predator. She exploits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s beauty as a tool for her own advancement, "labouring to bring down innocence and beauty" ([007-097]) to serve her schemes.

## Narrative Function

Beauty is the novel's catalyst — the quality that sets the plot in motion. Without [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s beauty, [[montraville|Montraville]] would not have noticed her; without [[montraville|Montraville]]'s notice, the seduction would not have occurred. Rowson's treatment of beauty thus carries an implicit social critique: a world in which female beauty is systematically commodified by male [[passion|desire]] is a world in which female virtue is perpetually at risk. The novel's answer is not to condemn beauty but to warn that it requires protection — by parents, by education, and by the [[youth|young]] woman's own vigilance.

## Related Concepts

- [[innocence]]: Beauty's companion and its victim. "[[innocence|Innocence]] and beauty" are repeatedly paired as the novel's most vulnerable combination ([007-097]).
- [[seduction]]: Beauty's consequence in a predatory world. Beauty attracts the [[seduction|seducer]]; seduction destroys beauty's bearer.
- [[youth]]: Beauty's temporal frame. [[youth|Youth]]ful beauty is the novel's specific concern — the beauty of a girl on the threshold of adult society.
- [[modesty]]: Beauty's proper guardian. [[modesty|Modesty]] protects beauty from the predatory gaze that would otherwise consume it.
