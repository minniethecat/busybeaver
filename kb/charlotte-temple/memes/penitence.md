Penitence in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the redemptive emotion that bridges the fallen state and the return to [[virtue]]. Rowson presents penitence as a central sentimental virtue — the sincere tear of remorse that distinguishes the genuinely repentant from the merely hypocritical, and that carries the power to erase moral offence. The novel's didactic project depends on penitence: if wrongdoing can be followed by genuine remorse, then the fallen can be reclaimed as objects of [[compassion]] rather than condemnation.

## Key Passages

> "finding her reduced to the most abject want, and believing the penitence which she professed to be sincere, she took her into her own family" — on Mademoiselle La Rue's counterfeit penitence ([006-014])

> "tell her how nearly penitence is allied to virtue. Cannot we go and conduct her home, my love?" — Mrs. Temple, pleading to rescue Charlotte ([025-045])

> "the tear of penitence blots their offences from the book of fate, and they rise from the heavy, painful trial, purified and fit for a mansion in the kingdom of eternity" — the narrator's moral reflection ([028-030])

## Narrative Function

Penitence serves two contrasting functions in the novel. First, it appears as a counterfeit — [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] "professed" penitence to gain admission into the Temple household, but her subsequent conduct reveals it as a performance. This false penitence is a warning: appearances of remorse may be instrumental rather than genuine. Second, and more importantly, penitence is the mechanism of Charlotte's moral recovery. Mrs. Temple's plea that "penitence is allied to virtue" ([025-045]) articulates the novel's most generous moral principle — that the sincerely penitent are not forever separated from the virtuous. The narrator's concluding reflection that "the tear of penitence blots their offences from the book of fate" ([028-030]) provides the cosmic framework: penitence is not merely a social performance but a transaction with divine justice, one that purifies the sufferer and prepares them for "eternity."
