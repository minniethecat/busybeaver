# Prudence

The cardinal virtue of caution, forethought, and self-governing moral [[reason|judgment]] — in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, the protective quality whose absence enables seduction, and whose exercise p[[modesty|reserve]]s a woman's reputation and happiness in a world of predatory men and indifferent social institutions.

## Definition

In Rowson's moral vocabulary, [[reason|prudence]] is not merely caution but a compound of sound [[reason|judgment]], [[modesty|modest]] restraint, and vigilant self-governance. It is the practical expression of virtue in everyday conduct. For [[youth|young]] women especially, [[reason|prudence]] acts as a protective barrier between innocence and the dangers of the world: without it, even the well-intentioned may [[ruin|fall]] prey to seduction. The narrator presents [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s tragedy as fundamentally a failure of [[reason|prudence]] — not of [[nature|innate]] virtue — describing her as "the hapless victim of imprudence and evil counsellors" (015-070).

The novel contrasts a woman of "too high an opinion of her [[reason|prudence]]" (019-005) — someone [[trust]]ed but insufficiently scrutinized — with the watchful guardianship that true [[reason|prudence]] demands. Prudence also manifests in practical domestic economy: when [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] sits by a dwindling fire, "[[reason|prudence]] teaching her to be careful of what she had" (029-022), the virtue appears even in her reduced state, showing that [[reason|prudence]] survives moral down[[ruin|fall]] as a [[nature|natural]] instinct, however belated.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central cautionary example. Her imprudent decision to elope without her governess's knowledge (006-033) initiates her tragedy, and she herself later acknowledges the "accumulated miseries my own imprudence has brought upon me" (024-027).
- [[mr-temple]]: Embodies prudent guardianship through his vigilance. His failure is not in his own [[reason|prudence]] but in [[trust]]ing too readily in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s — the novel warns parents that placing "too high an opinion of her [[reason|prudence]]" (019-005) in a daughter is itself a form of imprudent neglect.
- [[montraville]]: Exploits the gap between [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s innocence and her imprudence. His seduction strategy depends on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] acting without prudent reflection, exploiting her "lively disposition" (019-003) in the absence of proper oversight.

## Narrative Function

Prudence — or more precisely, the absence of it — is the engine of the novel's plot. Each major turning point turns on a failure of [[reason|prudence]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s clandestine visit to the ship (006-033), her decision to leave school, her continued reliance on un[[trust]]worthy [[reputation|character]]s. Rowson deploys the [[reason|prudence]]/imprudence binary to fulfill her stated moral purpose: teaching [[youth|young]] women that "one false step" (009-005) in imprudence leads inexorably to ruin. The concept works simultaneously as a warning to [[youth|young]] readers and as a structural device that makes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] feel both preventable and, given her social circumstances, tragically probable.
