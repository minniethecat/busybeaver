Regret, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is the moral emotion par excellence — the pain that follows wrongdoing and signals the conscience's return to life. Unlike mere [[grief|sorrow]], which can be passive, regret is inherently moral: it implies a [[reason|judgment]] on one's own actions and a wish that they had been otherwise. Rowson uses regret to measure moral [[reputation|character]]: those who feel it are redeemable; those who do not are damned.

## Definition

Rowson defines regret as the retrospective [[distress|suffering]] that follows moral error. It is distinguished from repentance — which adds the determination to amend — and from remorse — which carries the sharper sting of self-condemnation. The novel's moral economy treats regret as the necessary first step toward redemption: without regret, there can be no repentance; without repentance, no [[mercy|forgiveness]]. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s insistence that he should not "[[suffering|suffer]] regret or unavailing [[grief|sorrow]]" ([003-081]) is presented as a self-[[deceit|deception]] — the attempt to evade regret is the attempt to evade moral responsibility itself. By the novel's end, "[[grief|sorrow]] and deep regret have taken their place" ([030-026]) in [[montraville|Montraville]]'s heart, and this emotional transformation is the novel's proof that he is not beyond redemption.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[montraville]]: The novel's most complex study in regret. His initial refusal to "[[suffering|suffer]] regret" ([003-081]) marks him as morally evasive; his eventual confession that "[[grief|sorrow]] and deep regret have taken their place" ([030-026]) signals his moral awakening. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s trajectory from evasion to acceptance of regret is the novel's il[[passion|lust]]ration of how conscience operates — slowly, painfully, but inescapably.
- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her "deep regret and [[distress|anguish]] of heart" ([018-010]) in the [[new-york|New York]] apartment represents regret compounded by isolation. She regrets her actions but has no sympathetic auditor — a condition Rowson presents as among the most pitiable.
- [[belcour]]: The novel's definitive portrait of the unrepentant. [[belcour|Belcour]] never expresses regret, and his absence of the emotion is the clearest sign of his irredeemable villainy. In Rowson's moral universe, the inability to feel regret is damnation itself.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Her later life as a "repentant [[repentance|penitent]]" — if genuine — would represent regret's ultimate redemptive possibility, though the novel leaves her fate ambiguous.

## Narrative Function

Regret is the novel's mechanism for distinguishing the redeemable from the irredeemable. Rowson's moral taxonomy sorts [[reputation|character]]s by their capacity for regret: those who feel it ([[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[montraville|Montraville]], [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]) are human, flawed but salvageable; those who do not ([[belcour|Belcour]]) are monsters. The novel's didactic project depends on producing regret in its readers — not for their own actions, but as a prophylactic emotion: read [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story, feel her regret vicariously, and be thereby p[[modesty|reserve]]d from ever having cause to feel your own.

## Related Concepts

- [[remorse]]: The sharper, more self-condemning form of regret. [[remorse|Remorse]] "tears her distracted soul" ([018-040]) while regret aches but does not destroy.
- [[repentance]]: Regret plus the determination to amend. Regret alone is passive; repentance is active.
- [[guilt]]: The objective condition of having done wrong. Regret is guilt's subjective echo — what the [[guilt|guilty]] feel when conscience awakens.
- [[forgiveness]]: The resolution of regret. To be forgiven is to have one's regret accepted and absolved.
