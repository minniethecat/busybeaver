[019-001] JULIA [[mr-franklin|Franklin]] was the only child of a man of large property, who, at

[019-002] the age of eighteen, left her independent mistress of an unincumbered

[019-003] income of seven hundred a year; she was a girl of a lively disposition,

[019-004] and humane, susceptible heart: she resided in [[new-york|New-York]] with an uncle,

[019-005] who loved her too well, and had too high an opinion of her prudence, to

[019-006] scrutinize her actions so much as would have been necessary with many

[019-007] [[youth|young]] ladies, who were not blest with her [[prudence|discretion]]: she was, at the

[019-008] time [[montraville|Montraville]] arrived at New-York, the life of society, and the

[019-009] universal toast. Montraville was introduced to her by the following

[019-010] accident.

[019-011] One night when he was upon guard, a dreadful fire broke out near Mr.

[019-012] Franklin's house, which, in a few hours, reduced that and several others

[019-013] to ashes; fortunately no lives were lost, and, by the assiduity of the

[019-014] soldiers, much valuable property was saved from the flames. In the midst

[019-015] of the confusion an old gentleman came up to Montraville, and, putting

[019-016] a small box into his hands, cried--“Keep it, my good Sir, till I come

[019-017] to you again;” and then rushing again into the thickest of the

[019-018] crowd, Montraville saw him no more. He waited till the fire was quite

[019-019] extinguished and the mob dispersed; but in vain: the old gentleman did

[019-020] not appear to claim his property; and Montraville, fearing to make any

[019-021] enquiry, lest he should meet with impostors who might lay claim, without

[019-022] any legal right, to the box, carried it to his lodgings, and locked it

[019-023] up: he naturally imagined, that the person who committed it to his care

[019-024] knew him, and would, in a day or two, reclaim it; but several weeks

[019-025] passed on, and no enquiry being made, he began to be uneasy, and

[019-026] resolved to examine the contents of the box, and if they were, as he

[019-027] supposed, valuable, to spare no pains to discover, and restore them

[019-028] to the owner. Upon opening it, he found it contained jewels to a large

[019-029] amount, about two hundred pounds in money, and a miniature picture set

[019-030] for a bracelet. On examining the picture, he thought he had somewhere

[019-031] seen features very like it, but could not recollect where. A few

[019-032] days after, being at a public assembly, he saw [[julia-franklin|Miss Franklin]], and the

[019-033] likeness was too evident to be mistaken: he enquired among his brother

[019-034] officers if any of them knew her, and found one who was upon terms of

[019-035] intimacy in the [[family]]: “then introduce me to her immediately,” said

[019-036] he, “for I am certain I can inform her of something which will give her

[019-037] peculiar pleasure.”

[019-038] He was immediately introduced, found she was the owner of the jewels,

[019-039] and was invited to breakfast the next morning in order to their

[019-040] restoration. This whole evening Montraville was honoured with Julia's

[019-041] hand; the lively sallies of her wit, the elegance of her manner,

[019-042] powerfully charmed him: he forgot [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], and indulged himself in

[019-043] saying every thing that was polite and tender to Julia. But on retiring,

[019-044] recollection returned. “What am I about?” said he: “though I cannot

[019-045] marry Charlotte, I cannot be villain enough to forsake her, nor must

[019-046] I dare to trifle with the heart of Julia Franklin. I will return this

[019-047] box,” said he, “which has been the source of so much uneasiness already,

[019-048] and in the evening pay a visit to my poor melancholy Charlotte, and

[019-049] endeavour to forget this fascinating Julia.”

[019-050] He arose, dressed himself, and taking the picture out, “I will [[modesty|reserve]]

[019-051] this from the rest,” said he, “and by presenting it to her when she

[019-052] thinks it is lost, enhance the value of the obligation.” He repaired to

[019-053] Mr. Franklin's, and found Julia in the breakfast parlour alone.

[019-054] “How [[happiness|happy]] am I, Madam,” said he, “that being the fortunate instrument

[019-055] of saving these jewels has been the means of procuring me the

[019-056] acquaintance of so amiable a lady. There are the jewels and money all

[019-057] safe.”

[019-058] “But where is the picture, Sir?” said Julia.

[019-059] “Here, Madam. I would not willingly part with it.”

[019-060] “It is the portrait of my mother,” said she, taking it from him: “'tis

[019-061] all that remains.” She pressed it to her lips, and a tear trembled in

[019-062] her eyes. Montraville glanced his eye on her grey night gown and black

[019-063] ribbon, and his own feelings prevented a reply.

[019-064] Julia Franklin was the very reverse of Charlotte [[mr-temple|Temple]]: she was tall,

[019-065] elegantly shaped, and possessed much of the air and manner of a woman

[019-066] of fashion; her complexion was a clear brown, enlivened with the glow of

[019-067] health, her eyes, full, black, and sparkling, darted their intelligent

[019-068] glances through long silken lashes; her hair was shining brown, and her

[019-069] features regular and striking; there was an air of innocent gaiety that

[019-070] played about her countenance, where good humour sat triumphant.

[019-071] “I have been mistaken,” said Montraville. “I imagined I loved Charlotte:

[019-072] but alas! I am now too late convinced my attachment to her was merely

[019-073] the impulse of the moment. I fear I have not only entailed lasting

[019-074] [[distress|misery]] on that poor girl, but also thrown a barrier in the way of my own

[019-075] happiness, which it will be impossible to surmount. I feel I [[affection|love]] Julia

[019-076] Franklin with ardour and sincerity; yet, when in her presence, I am

[019-077] sensible of my own inability to offer a heart worthy her acceptance, and

[019-078] remain silent.” Full of these painful thoughts, Montraville walked out

[019-079] to see Charlotte: she saw him approach, and ran out to meet him: she

[019-080] banished from her countenance the air of discontent which ever appeared

[019-081] when he was absent, and met him with a smile of joy.

[019-082] “I thought you had forgot me, Montraville,” said she, “and was very

[019-083] unhappy.”

[019-084] “I shall never forget you, Charlotte,” he replied, pressing her hand.

## Section 11

[019-085] The uncommon gravity of his countenance, and the brevity of his reply,

[019-086] alarmed her.

[019-087] “You are not well,” said she; “your hand is hot; your eyes are heavy;

[019-088] you are very ill.”

[019-089] “I am a villain,” said he mentally, as he turned from her to hide his

[019-090] emotions.

[019-091] “But come,” continued she tenderly, “you shall go to bed, and I will sit

[019-092] by, and watch you; you will be better when you have slept.”

[019-093] Montraville was glad to retire, and by pretending sleep, hide the

[019-094] agitation of his mind from her penetrating eye. Charlotte watched by him

[019-095] till a late hour, and then, lying softly down by his side, sunk into a

[019-096] profound sleep, from whence she awoke not till late the next morning.
