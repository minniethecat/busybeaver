Education, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is both a diagnosis and a prescription. Rowson opens the novel by identifying "a mistaken education" as one of the primary causes of female ruin, and throughout the narrative she contrasts proper moral instruction with the superficial accomplishments offered by fashionable boarding schools. The novel itself is an educational project — a warning to [[youth|young]] women that aims to succeed where formal schooling has failed.

## Definition

Rowson's critique of education is specific and structural. Young women, she argues, are either "spoilt by a mistaken education" ([P01-021]) or left defenceless by an education that teaches accomplishments — music, dancing, French — but not moral [[reason|judgment]]. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s education at [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s school has given her "a liberal education" ([006-008]) in the academic [[reason|sense]], but it has not taught her to recognize a [[seduction|seducer]] or to value her own virtue above flattery. Rowson draws a sharp contrast between the education of the mind and the education of the moral faculty: the former without the latter is a liability.

## Key Passages

> "Too many, whose minds are uncultivated, or spoilt by a mistaken education, are thrown on an unfriendly world." ([P01-021])

> "He had a liberal education, an insinuating address, and a handsome person." ([009-036])

> "Of mean birth, indifferent education, and weak intellects. How such a one could ever gain the affections of a man of Belcour's taste I cannot conceive." ([028-057])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's primary example of a [[youth|young]] woman whose "liberal education" ([005-087]) did not include the moral training necessary to resist seduction. Her accomplishments make her attractive; her lack of moral education makes her vulnerable.
- [[montraville]]: His "liberal education" ([003-009]) and polished manners are instruments of seduction, not virtue. Rowson warns that a well-educated libertine is more dangerous than an uncouth one.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: A professional educator who betrays the [[trust]] of her calling. She is the novel's darkest commentary on education: the person [[trust|entrusted]] with [[youth|young]] women's moral formation is herself morally bankrupt.
- [[belcour]]: Rowson's dismissive reference to his "indifferent education" ([028-057]) is a class marker — his lack of breeding explains his callousness.

## Narrative Function

Education is the novel's framing concern, announced in the very first paragraph of the preface. Rowson's narrator presents herself as a superior educator: where boarding schools have failed, the novel will succeed. Every episode of the story serves a pedagogical purpose — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] teaches [[reason|prudence]], [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] teaches the cost of disobedience, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death teaches the urgency of moral vigilance. The novel is, in its deepest structure, a textbook of female conduct disguised as a [[sensibility|sentiment]]al narrative.

## Related Concepts

- [[folly]]: The direct consequence of inadequate education — folly is what education is meant to prevent. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s folly is an educational failure before it is a moral one.
- [[innocence]]: The novel distinguishes between useful innocence and dangerous naivety. Education, properly understood, should protect the former while correcting the latter.
- [[prudence]]: The virtue that a proper education cultivates — the capacity to foresee consequences and act accordingly.
- [[virtue]]: The ultimate goal of education in Rowson's framework. All learning that does not tend toward virtue is, by implication, wasted.
