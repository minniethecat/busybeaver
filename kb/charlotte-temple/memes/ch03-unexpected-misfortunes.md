[003-001] “MY life,” said [[captain-eldridge|Mr. Eldridge]], “till within these few years was marked by

[003-002] no particular circumstance deserving notice. I early embraced the life

[003-003] of a sailor, and have served my King with unremitted ardour for many

[003-004] years. At the age of twenty-five I married an amiable woman; one son,

[003-005] and the girl who just now left us, were the fruits of our union. My

[003-006] boy had genius and spirit. I straitened my little income to give him a

[003-007] liberal education, but the rapid progress he made in his studies amply

[003-008] compensated for the inconvenience. At the academy where he received his

[003-009] education he commenced an acquaintance with a Mr. Lewis, a [[youth|young]] man

[003-010] of affluent [[fate|fortune]]: as they grew up their intimacy ripened into

[003-011] friendship, and they became almost inseparable companions.

[003-012] “George chose the profession of a soldier. I had neither [[friendship|friends]] or

[003-013] money to procure him a commission, and had wished him to embrace a

[003-014] nautical life: but this was repugnant to his wishes, and I ceased to

[003-015] urge him on the subject.

[003-016] “The friendship subsisting between Lewis and my son was of such a nature

[003-017] as gave him free access to our [[family]]; and so specious was his manner

[003-018] that we hesitated not to state to him all our little difficulties in

[003-019] regard to George's future views. He listened to us with attention, and

[003-020] offered to advance any sum necessary for his first setting out.

[003-021] “I embraced the offer, and gave him my note for the payment of it, but

[003-022] he would not [[suffering|suffer]] me to mention any stipulated time, as he said I

[003-023] might do it whenever most convenient to myself. [[About]] this time my dear

[003-024] [[lucy|Lucy]] returned from school, and I soon began to imagine Lewis looked at

[003-025] her with eyes of [[love|affection]]. I gave my child a [[prudence|caution]] to beware of him,

[003-026] and to look on her mother as her friend. She was unaffectedly artless;

[003-027] and when, as I suspected, Lewis made professions of love, she confided

[003-028] in her parents, and assured us her heart was perfectly unbiassed in his

[003-029] favour, and she would cheerfully submit to our direction.

[003-030] “I took an early opportunity of questioning him concerning his

[003-031] intentions towards my child: he gave an equivocal answer, and I forbade

[003-032] him the house.

[003-033] “The next day he sent and demanded payment of his money. It was not in

[003-034] my power to comply with the demand. I requested three days to endeavour

[003-035] to raise it, determining in that time to mortgage my half pay, and live

[003-036] on a small annuity which my wife possessed, rather than be under an

[003-037] obligation to so worthless a man: but this short time was not allowed

[003-038] me; for that evening, as I was sitting down to supper, unsuspicious of

[003-039] danger, an officer entered, and tore me from the embraces of my family.

[003-040] “My wife had been for some time in a declining state of health: ruin at

[003-041] once so unexpected and inevitable was a stroke she was not prepared to

[003-042] bear, and I saw her faint into the arms of our servant, as I left my

[003-043] own habitation for the comfortless walls of a prison. My poor Lucy,

[003-044] distracted with her [[fear|fears]] for us both, sunk on the floor and endeavoured

[003-045] to detain me by her feeble efforts, but in vain; they forced open her

[003-046] arms; she shrieked, and fell prostrate. But [[forgiveness|pardon]] me. The horrors of

[003-047] that night unman me. I cannot proceed.”

[003-048] He rose from his seat, and walked several times across the room: at

[003-049] length, attaining more composure, he cried--“What a mere infant I am!

[003-050] Why, Sir, I never felt thus in the day of battle.” “No,” said [[mr-temple|Temple]];

[003-051] “but the truly brave soul is tremblingly alive to the feelings of

[003-052] humanity.”

[003-053] “True,” replied the old man, (something like satisfaction darting across

[003-054] his features) “and painful as these feelings are, I would not exchange

[003-055] them for that torpor which the stoic mistakes for philosophy. How many

[003-056] exquisite delights should I have passed by unnoticed, but for these keen

[003-057] sensations, this quick sense of happiness or [[distress|misery]]? Then let us, my

[003-058] friend, take the cup of life as it is presented to us, tempered by the

[003-059] hand of a wise Providence; be thankful for the good, be patient under

[003-060] the evil, and presume not to enquire why the latter predominates.”

[003-061] “This is true philosophy,” said Temple.

[003-062] “'Tis the only way to reconcile ourselves to the cross events of life,”

[003-063] replied he. “But I forget myself. I will not longer intrude on your

[003-064] patience, but proceed in my melancholy tale.

[003-065] “The very evening that I was taken to prison, my son arrived from

[003-066] Ireland, where he had been some time with his regiment. From the

[003-067] distracted expressions of his mother and sister, he learnt by whom I

[003-068] had been arrested; and, late as it was, flew on the wings of wounded

[003-069] [[love|affection]], to the house of his false friend, and earnestly enquired the

[003-070] cause of this cruel conduct. With all the calmness of a cool deliberate

[003-071] villain, he avowed his passion for Lucy; declared her situation in

[003-072] life would not permit him to marry her; but offered to [[liberty|release]] me

[003-073] immediately, and make any settlement on her, if George would persuade

[003-074] her to live, as he impiously termed it, a life of honour.

[003-075] “Fired at the insult offered to a man and a soldier, my boy struck the

[003-076] villain, and a challenge ensued. He then went to a coffee-house in

[003-077] the neighbourhood and wrote a long affectionate letter to me, blaming

[003-078] himself severely for having introduced Lewis into the family, or

[003-079] permitted him to confer an obligation, which had brought inevitable

[003-080] ruin on us all. He begged me, whatever might be the event of the ensuing

[003-081] morning, not to suffer regret or unavailing [[melancholy|sorrow]] for his fate, to

[003-082] increase the [[grief|anguish]] of my heart, which he greatly feared was already

[003-083] insupportable.

[003-084] “This letter was delivered to me early in the morning. It would be vain

[003-085] to attempt describing my feelings on the perusal of it; suffice it to

[003-086] say, that a merciful Providence interposed, and I was for three weeks

[003-087] insensible to miseries almost beyond the strength of [[nature|human nature]] to

[003-088] support.

[003-089] “A fever and strong delirium seized me, and my life was despaired of. At

[003-090] length, nature, overpowered with fatigue, gave way to the salutary power

[003-091] of rest, and a quiet slumber of some hours restored me to reason, though

[003-092] the extreme weakness of my frame prevented my [[sensibility|feeling]] my distress so

[003-093] acutely as I otherways should.

[003-094] “The first object that struck me on awaking, was Lucy sitting by my

[003-095] bedside; her pale countenance and sable dress prevented my enquiries for

[003-096] poor George: for the letter I had received from him, was the first thing

[003-097] that occurred to my memory. By degrees the rest returned: I recollected

[003-098] being arrested, but could no ways account for being in this apartment,

[003-099] whither they had conveyed me during my illness.

[003-100] “I was so weak as to be almost unable to speak. I pressed Lucy's hand,

[003-101] and looked earnestly round the apartment in search of another dear

[003-102] object.

[003-103] “Where is your mother?” said I, faintly.

[003-104] “The poor girl could not answer: she shook her head in expressive

[003-105] silence; and throwing herself on the bed, folded her arms about me, and

[003-106] burst into tears.

[003-107] “What! both gone?” said I.

[003-108] “Both,” she replied, endeavouring to restrain her emotions: “but they

[003-109] are [[happiness|happy]], no doubt.”

[003-110] Here Mr. Eldridge paused: the recollection of the scene was too painful

[003-111] to permit him to proceed.
