Guilt in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the moral shadow that follows transgression. Unlike [[regret|remorse]], which is a felt [[distress|anguish]], guilt in Rowson's vocabulary is a state of being — the objective condition of having violated moral law. The novel traces guilt's corrosive trajectory: from the first "guilty joys" of seduction ([016-083]), through the social shame it engenders, to the self-accusation of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|dying]] letters. Rowson's treatment of guilt is relentlessly didactic: guilt is not merely felt but known, by oneself and by others, and its consequences are inescapable.

## Definition

Rowson distinguishes between guilt as a legal-moral fact ("a man who has been guilty of seduction" [018-058]) and guilt as an internalized identity. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s letters to her mother repeatedly use the self-designation "guilty, but repentant child" ([022-014]), framing guilt as a status that only parental [[mercy|forgiveness]] can resolve. For Rowson, guilt is progressive: the female heart, once [[ruin|fall]]en, "grows hardened in guilt" ([007-096]) unless checked by conscience and community. The novel's moral economy demands that guilt be acknowledged — [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "conscious guilt" ([026-024]) and [[belcour|Belcour]]'s defiance both demonstrate that guilt ignored becomes guilt compounded.

## Key Passages

> "The heedless Fair, who stoops to guilty joys, / Soon from her yielding heart each virtue flies" ([016-083])

> "Shame bows her to the earth, remorse tears her distracted mind, and guilt, poverty, and disease close the dreadful scene" ([018-066])

> "Will my once kind, my ever beloved mother, deign to receive a letter from her guilty, but repentant child?" ([022-014])

> "Yet by heavens I swear, Belcour, I thought I loved the lost, deluded Charlotte almost to madness" — Montraville's guilt-driven self-justification ([024-065])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central case study in guilt. Her trajectory moves from "guilty joys" to guilty conscience to guilty self-presentation. Her repeated epistolary confessions enact guilt as both psychological torment and social plea ([022-014]).
- [[montraville]]: Guilt as belated recognition. His "[[remorse|keenest remorse]] in the remembrance of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction" ([026-036]) comes too late to alter the plot's tragic course, but functions as a warning to male readers about the consequences of seduction.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: The counter-example who evades guilt. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], "she grows hardened in guilt" ([007-096]), demonstrating that guilt acknowledged is redemptive; guilt suppressed is corrosive.

## Narrative Function

Guilt is the engine of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moral awakening and the novel's primary didactic instrument. Rowson deploys guilt to stage the [[seduction|seduce]]d woman's progressive self-knowledge: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] moves from the heedless pursuit of guilty pleasure to the [[distress|anguish]]ed consciousness of her guilt. Structurally, guilt bridges the novel's two phases — the intoxication of seduction (Chapters 1–12) and the wages of sin (Chapters 13–35). The guilt-laden letters of Chapters 22–23 constitute the novel's emotional and moral climax, converting private shame into public warning.

## Related Concepts

- [[remorse]]: Guilt's emotional twin. While guilt is the state, [[regret|remorse]] is its felt torment. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "bed which [[regret|remorse]] had strewed with thorns" ([018-035]) literalizes the relationship.
- [[repentance]]: Guilt properly acknowledged leads to repentance. Rowson's moral economy requires that guilt be followed by genuine [[grief|sorrow]], not merely fear of consequences.
- [[seduction]]: The act that produces guilt. Rowson's novel is structured around the causal chain: seduction → guilt → [[distress|suffering]] → (potential) redemption.
- [[shame]]: Guilt's social face. Guilt is internal; shame is its public manifestation, "bowing her to the earth" ([018-065]).
