[016-001] ON board of the ship in which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and Mademoiselle were embarked,

[016-002] was an officer of large unincumbered [[fate|fortune]] and elevated rank, and whom

[016-003] I shall call [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]].

[016-004] He was one of those men, who, having travelled in their youth, pretend

[016-005] to have contracted a peculiar fondness for every thing foreign, and to

[016-006] hold in contempt the productions of their own country; and this affected

[016-007] partiality extended even to the women.

[016-008] With him therefore the blushing modesty and unaffected simplicity of

[016-009] Charlotte passed unnoticed; but the forward pertness of [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], the

[016-010] [[liberty|freedom]] of her conversation, the elegance of her person, mixed with a

[016-011] certain engaging JE NE SAIS QUOI, perfectly enchanted him.

[016-012] The reader no doubt has already developed the [[reputation|character]] of La Rue:

[016-013] designing, artful, and selfish, she had accepted the devoirs of [[belcour|Belcour]]

[016-014] because she was heartily weary of the retired life she led at the

[016-015] school, wished to be released from what she deemed a slavery, and to

[016-016] return to that vortex of folly and dissipation which had once plunged

[016-017] her into the deepest [[distress|misery]]; but her plan she flattered herself was now

[016-018] better formed: she resolved to put herself under the protection of no

[016-019] man till she had first secured a settlement; but the clandestine manner

[016-020] in which she left [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s prevented her putting this plan

[016-021] in execution, though Belcour solemnly protested he would make her a

[016-022] handsome settlement the moment they arrived at [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]]. This he

[016-023] afterwards contrived to evade by a pretended hurry of business; La Rue

[016-024] readily conceiving he never meant to fulfil his promise, determined to

[016-025] change her battery, and attack the heart of Colonel Crayton. She soon

[016-026] discovered the partiality he entertained for her nation; and having

[016-027] imposed on him a feigned tale of distress, representing Belcour as a

[016-028] villain who had seduced her from her [[friendship|friends]] under promise of marriage,

[016-029] and afterwards betrayed her, pretending great [[repentance|remorse]] for the errors she

[016-030] had committed, and declaring whatever her [[love|affection]] for Belcour might

[016-031] have been, it was now entirely extinguished, and she wished for nothing

[016-032] more than an opportunity to leave a course of life which her soul

[016-033] abhorred; but she had no friends to apply to, they had all renounced

[016-034] her, and guilt and misery would undoubtedly be her future portion

[016-035] through life.

[016-036] Crayton was possessed of many amiable qualities, though the peculiar

[016-037] trait in his character, which we have already mentioned, in a great

[016-038] measure threw a shade over them. He was [[love|beloved]] for his humanity and

[016-039] [[charity|benevolence]] by all who knew him, but he was easy and unsuspicious

[016-040] himself, and became a dupe to the artifice of others.

[016-041] He was, when very [[youth|young]], united to an amiable Parisian lady, and perhaps

[016-042] it was his affection for her that laid the foundation for the partiality

[016-043] he ever retained for the whole nation. He had by her one daughter, who

[016-044] entered into the world but a few hours before her mother left it. This

[016-045] lady was universally beloved and admired, being endowed with all the

[016-046] virtues of her mother, without the weakness of the father: she was

[016-047] married to Major [[mrs-beauchamp|Beauchamp]], and was at this time in the same fleet with

[016-048] her father, attending her husband to [[new-york|New-York]].

[016-049] Crayton was melted by the affected [[regret|contrition]] and distress of La Rue:

[016-050] he would converse with her for hours, read to her, play cards with her,

[016-051] listen to all her complaints, and promise to protect her to the utmost

[016-052] of his power. La Rue easily saw his character; her sole aim was to

[016-053] awaken a passion in his bosom that might turn out to her advantage,

[016-054] and in this aim she was but too successful, for before the voyage was

[016-055] finished, the infatuated Colonel gave her from under his hand a promise

[016-056] of marriage on their arrival at New-York, under forfeiture of five

[016-057] thousand pounds.

[016-058] And how did our poor Charlotte pass her time during a tedious and

[016-059] tempestuous passage? naturally delicate, the fatigue and sickness which

[016-060] she endured rendered her so weak as to be almost entirely confined to

[016-061] her bed: yet the kindness and attention of [[montraville|Montraville]] in some measure

[016-062] contributed to alleviate her [[suffering|sufferings]], and the hope of hearing from

[016-063] her friends soon after her arrival, kept up her spirits, and cheered

[016-064] many a gloomy hour.

[016-065] But during the voyage a great revolution took place not only in the

[016-066] [[wealth|fortune]] of La Rue but in the bosom of Belcour: whilst in pursuit of

[016-067] his amour with Mademoiselle, he had attended little to the interesting,

[016-068] inobtrusive charms of Charlotte, but when, cloyed by possession,

[016-069] and disgusted with the art and dissimulation of one, he beheld the

[016-070] simplicity and [[kindness|gentleness]] of the other, the contrast became too striking

[016-071] not to fill him at once with surprise and admiration. He frequently

[016-072] conversed with Charlotte; he found her sensible, well informed, but

[016-073] diffident and unassuming. The languor which the fatigue of her body and

[016-074] perturbation of her mind spread over her delicate features, served only

[016-075] in his opinion to render her more lovely: he knew that Montraville did

[016-076] not design to marry her, and he formed a resolution to endeavour to gain

[016-077] her himself whenever Montraville should leave her.

[016-078] Let not the reader imagine Belcour's designs were [[honour|honourable]]. Alas! when

[016-079] once a woman has forgot the respect due to herself, by yielding to the

[016-080] solicitations of illicit love, they lose all their consequence, even in

[016-081] the eyes of the man whose art has betrayed them, and for whose sake they

[016-082] have sacrificed every valuable consideration.

[016-083] The heedless Fair, who stoops to [[guilt|guilty]] joys,

[016-084] A man may [[compassion|pity]]--but he must despise.

[016-085] Nay, every libertine will think he has a right to insult her with his

[016-086] licentious passion; and should the unhappy creature shrink from the

[016-087] insolent overture, he will sneeringly taunt her with pretence of

[016-088] modesty.
