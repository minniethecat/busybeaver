Temptation in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the moral hazard that defines the novel's plot — the force that separates the [[ruin|fall]]en from the preserved. Rowson presents temptation through two complementary frames: as a spiritual trial that prayer may guard against, and as a social snare laid by predatory men for unprotected women. The tension between these frames — divine test versus social trap — is central to the novel's moral economy.

## Key Passages

> "kneel down each morning, and request kind heaven to keep you free from temptation, or, should it please to suffer you to be tried, pray for fortitude to resist the impulse of inclination when it runs counter to the precepts of religion and virtue" — Mr. Temple's parting advice to Charlotte ([006-091])

> "some provision made, to place them above the snares and temptations which vice ever holds out to the elegant, accomplished female, when oppressed by the frowns of poverty and the sting of dependance" — Mr. Franklin on his daughters ([010-007])

## Narrative Function

Rowson's treatment of temptation reveals the novel's double address. To parents, temptation is a practical danger that must be forestalled by adequate provision — poverty and dependence expose women to "the snares" of vice. To the young female reader, temptation is a spiritual trial demanding prayer and fortitude. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] falls because both protections fail: her father's advice is forgotten, and her financial vulnerability is exploited by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] and [[montraville|Montraville]]. Temptation thus links the novel's religious didacticism to its social critique.
