# Duty

The active and binding principle that translates moral knowledge into moral conduct. In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, duty operates as the practical expression of virtue — encompassing a [[youth|young]] woman's obligations to her parents, her Creator, and the social and religious norms that protect her from ruin.

## Definition

In Rowson's moral vocabulary, duty is the operative link between knowing what is right and doing it. It is framed as a [[youth|young]] woman's primary defence against seduction: when [[love|passion]] and flattery exert their pressure, duty is the countervailing force grounded in religion, [[filial-piety|filial obligation]], and rational self-governance. The novel presents duty as an inheritance of proper education and parental example — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is described as one who has been taught the "duty she owes her parents" (008-024) — yet it also dramatises how easily duty collapses under the sustained assault of seduction and the abdication of prudent guardianship.

The term appears across three registers in the novel. First, as **filial duty** — the obligation a daughter owes her parents, rooted in gratitude for their care and obedience to their authority. Second, as **religious duty** — the obligation owed to God, invoked by [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]] when she warns that "pleasure should never make us forget our duty to our Creator" (013-017). Third, as **conjugal duty** — the [[modesty|modest]], [[love|affection]]ate submission of a wife to her husband, exemplified in Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]], whose tenderness is "tempered so sweetly with the meek [[love|affection]] and submissive duty of the wife" (008-019). These three dimensions together form the moral architecture that the seduction plot systematically dismantles.

## Key Passages

> "tempered so sweetly with the meek affection and submissive duty of the wife" (008-019)

Here Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]] embodies the ideal of conjugal duty — a [[affection|love]] that expresses itself through [[modesty|modest]] submission rather than rebellious [[love|passion]]. The passage establishes duty as compatible with [[love|affection]], not opposed to it, and models the harmonious domestic order that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement will destroy.

> "I will sacrifice love to duty." (012-083)

This is [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s moment of highest moral clarity — her declaration on the night of the planned elopement. It is the point at which duty almost prevails over [[love|passion]]. The verb "sacrifice" is telling: Rowson frames the choice between [[affection|love]] and duty as one requiring genuine renunciation, not easy compliance.

> "I must not: religion, duty, forbid." (012-096)

When [[montraville|Montraville]] renews his persuasive assault at the garden gate, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s resistance invokes the twin authorities of religion and duty. The phrasing — "I must not" rather than "I will not" — reveals duty as an external restraint that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] [[experience]]s more as prohibition than as internalised conviction, foreshadowing its eventual failure.

> "pleasure should never make us forget our duty to our Creator" (013-017)

[[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s admonition, delivered just before [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s disappearance is discovered, articulates the religious dimension of duty. It arrives with tragic irony: the reader already knows [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has abandoned duty for pleasure, and the governess's words underscore the moral gravity of what has already occurred.

> "I know not where she is, or who has seduced her from her duty." (013-085)

[[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s confession to [[captain-eldridge|Mr. Eldridge]] frames [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement explicitly as a seduction *from duty*. The phrasing makes duty the thing lost — more fundamental even than physical chastity or social reputation — and seduction the means by which it is taken.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central case study in the conflict between duty and [[love|passion]]. Her education has given her the language of duty, and she can articulate its claims (012-083, 012-096), but her resolution proves too weak to withstand [[montraville|Montraville]]'s persuasions. Her tragedy is that she knows her duty and fails to perform it.
- [[mr-temple]]: Embodies benevolent paternal authority — the legitimate object of filial duty. His [[benevolence|generosity]] in forgiving [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] at the novel's close demonstrates that the breach of duty, while catastrophic, need not be final when met with [[regret|penitence]].
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Through her [[charity|compassion]]ate yet morally firm [[friendship|friends]]hip with the abandoned [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], she demonstrates that duty to one's fellow creatures — the obligation to "raise the poor [[repentance|penitent]]" — is itself a form of [[virtue|virtuous]] conduct.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]: The novel's most complete repudiation of duty. As [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s teacher, she was [[trust|entrusted]] with a duty of care and moral guidance; instead she facilitates the elopement and models the pursuit of pleasure over obligation.
- [[montraville|Montraville]]: The [[seduction|seducer]] who exploits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s weakness of resolution. His own belated recognition of his duty toward her — expressed in his [[death|deathbed]] letter — comes too late to avert tragedy, il[[passion|lust]]rating the irreversible consequences of duty abandoned.

## Narrative Function

Duty is the moral mechanism through which Rowson attempts to achieve her stated didactic purpose. The plot is organised around a series of points at which duty is invoked, tested, and ultimately broken: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s education in duty, her declaration that she will "sacrifice [[affection|love]] to duty," her final capitulation at the garden gate, and the retrospective [[reason|judgment]] that she was "[[seduction|seduce]]d from her duty" (013-085).

This structure makes duty the novel's primary locus of narrative tension. Every scene in which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] wavers — between the school and the chaise, between [[montraville|Montraville]]'s entreaties and the memory of her parents — is a drama of duty under siege. By showing duty failing despite [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sincere attachment to it, Rowson warns her readers that moral [[sensibility|sentiment]] alone is insufficient without vigilant guardianship and resolute self-command.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Duty is the active, behavioural component of virtue — the obligations that virtue requires one to fulfill
- [[honour]]: Closely allied to duty but carrying stronger connotations of social reputation; honour is the public face of private duty
- [[filial-piety]]: The specific duty owed by children to parents, foundational to the novel's moral scheme
- [[religion]]: Provides the ultimate sanction for duty; the two are repeatedly coupled as twin authorities
- [[repentance]]: The mechanism by which the breach of duty can be spiritually repaired, even when social consequences are irreversible
