Religion, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), provides the moral scaffolding upon which the novel's entire ethical argument rests. Though Rowson rarely sermonizes directly, Christian principles govern the behaviour expected of her [[reputation|character]]s: parents are owed obedience, seduction violates divine law, suicide is forbidden, and [[distress|suffering]] carries redemptive meaning. Religion is not merely one virtue among others in the novel — it is the framework that makes all other virtues intelligible.

## Definition

Rowson's Christianity is practical and moral rather than doctrinal. She invokes it at the novel's most charged moments to draw bright lines between right and wrong. When [[montraville|Montraville]] contemplates his designs on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], Rowson notes that they run "counter to the precepts of religion and virtue" ([006-093]), pairing religion and virtue as twin guardians of female conduct. When [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], in her deepest despair, considers suicide, Rowson quickly reminds the reader that "the Christian religion" forbids "to attempt to put a period to our own existence" ([029-016]) — a doctrinal intervention that saves [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] from compounding one sin with another.

## Key Passages

> "His designs were such as ran counter to the precepts of religion and virtue." ([006-093])

> "I dare not: religion, duty, forbid." ([012-096])

> "But, Charlotte, had it not been for the Christian religion, you would have attempted to put a period to your own existence." ([029-016])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her moral struggles are framed in religious terms. She knows seduction is wrong because religion says so, yet she [[ruin|fall]]s anyway — making her story a [[prudence|caution]]ary tale about the insufficiency of religious knowledge without the strength to act on it ([012-096]).
- [[montraville]]: He violates the precepts of religion knowingly. Rowson marks him as culpable precisely because he acts "counter to the precepts of religion and virtue" with full awareness ([006-093]).
- [[mr-temple]]: Represents the Christian ideal of the merciful father — his [[mercy|forgiveness]] of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] models divine grace in human form. His religious [[reputation|character]] grounds his capacity for unconditional [[affection|love]].

## Narrative Function

Religion serves three functions in the novel. First, it is the moral boundary-marker: [[reputation|character]]s who act against religion are unmistakably in the wrong. Second, it is the source of [[comfort|consolation]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] is made bearable by her religious faith, and her final scenes invoke Christian redemption. Third, it is the novel's rhetorical authority: Rowson, as narrator, can invoke religion to pass [[reason|judgment]] without appearing merely personal. The novel's famous closing moral — a warning to [[youth|young]] women — derives its force from the religious framework Rowson has established throughout.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]]: Religion and virtue are paired throughout the novel as mutually reinforcing moral forces ([006-093]).
- [[repentance]]: Christian repentance is the mechanism by which [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] restores her moral standing before God, even as her social standing is beyond repair.
- [[forgiveness]]: The Christian duty to forgive is what makes the novel's ending redemptive rather than merely tragic.
- [[duty]]: Religious duty — obedience to parents, fidelity to moral law — is the standard against which [[reputation|character]]s' actions are measured.
