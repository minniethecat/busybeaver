# Affection

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, [[love|affection]] is the [[nature|natural]] bond of [[virtue|virtuous]] attachment that Rowson presents as the emotional foundation of moral life. It encompasses filial devotion between parents and children, conjugal love between spouses, and genuine romantic [[sensibility|feeling]] — all contrasted with the counterfeit "[[love|passion]]" that leads to seduction and ruin.

## Definition

Rowson uses "[[love|affection]]" to describe a steady, benevolent emotional bond rooted in duty and virtue, distinct from the transient heat of [[love|passion]]. Affection is what [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s parents feel for her — a protective, guiding love — and what she in turn owes them as a dutiful daughter. When [[montraville|Montraville]]'s "[[love|affection]] soon began to decline" ([030-055]), the narrator signals not merely cooling [[love|romance]] but a moral failure: genuine [[love|affection]], in Rowson's vocabulary, is constant and protective. The loss of [[love|affection]] — whether parental, conjugal, or filial — is consistently presented as both cause and consequence of moral transgression.

## Key Passages

> "…his cheek the kiss of filial affection" ([002-099])

This early image of dutiful children kissing their father establishes [[love|affection]] as the novel's emotional ideal — a visible, physical expression of [[virtue|virtuous]] [[family]] bonds.

> "…behold the mild ray of affectionate applause that beams from the eye of the fond parent" ([014-081])

The narrator's direct appeal to readers links parental affection to moral education: [[love|affection]]ate parents raise [[virtue|virtuous]] children.

> "…the seeds of filial affection in your bosoms." ([014-085])

Rowson explicitly frames [[love|affection]] as something cultivated — planted by parents and nurtured through moral [[education|instruction]].

> "…you now see the child of your affections, the daughter whom you so fondly loved" ([018-008])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s letter to her parents invokes their [[love|affection]] as the standard against which her own [[ruin|fall]] is measured. The word "[[love|affection]]s" here carries the full weight of what she has betrayed.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's central figure of betrayed [[love|affection]] — she loses her parents' protective [[love|affection]] and [[suffering|suffer]]s the decline of [[montraville|Montraville]]'s ([030-055])
- [[mr-temple]]: The model of paternal [[love|affection]] — his love for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is steady, forgiving, and morally instructive ([014-081])
- [[mrs-temple]]: Embodies maternal [[love|affection]], writing "[[love|affection]]ate" letters that sustain [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s memory of virtue ([012-028])
- [[lucy]]: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[youth|young]]er sister, whose "filial affection" soothes their parents after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s loss ([013-006])
- [[montraville]]: His "[[love|affection]] soon began to decline" — the novel's primary example of false, transient romantic attachment ([030-055])
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: Offers [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] genuine, disinterested [[love|affection]] when she is abandoned — a rare example of non-[[family|familial]] [[virtue|virtuous]] love ([024-069])

## Narrative Function

Affection serves as Rowson's moral thermometer. When [[reputation|character]]s act from genuine [[love|affection]] — the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s' care for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], Lucy's devotion, [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s kindness — they advance virtue and healing. When [[love|affection]] is feigned or withdrawn — [[montraville|Montraville]]'s seduction, [[belcour|Belcour]]'s manipulation, [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s false [[friendship|friends]]hip — it drives the plot toward catastrophe. The novel's central tragedy is [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s progressive loss of the [[love|affection]] that once protected her: first her parents', then [[montraville|Montraville]]'s, and finally society's. Her [[death|deathbed]] scene, surrounded by her "[[love|affection]]ate parents" ([020-116]), restores [[love|affection]] as the novel's final redemptive force.
