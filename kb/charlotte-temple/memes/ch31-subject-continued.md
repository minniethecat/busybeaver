[031-001] WHEN the door was opened, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], in a voice rendered scarcely

[031-002] articulate, through cold and the extreme agitation of her mind, demanded

[031-003] whether [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]] was at home. The servant hesitated: he knew that

[031-004] his lady was engaged at a game of picquet with her dear Corydon,

[031-005] nor could he think she would like to be disturbed by a person whose

[031-006] appearance spoke her of so little consequence as Charlotte; yet there

[031-007] was something in her countenance that rather interested him in her

[031-008] favour, and he said his lady was engaged, but if she had any particular

[031-009] message he would deliver it.

[031-010] “Take up this letter,” said Charlotte: “tell her the unhappy writer of

[031-011] it waits in her hall for an answer.” The tremulous accent, the tearful

[031-012] eye, must have moved any heart not composed of adamant. The man took the

[031-013] letter from the poor suppliant, and hastily ascended the stair case.

## Section 17

[031-014] “A letter, Madam,” said he, presenting it to his lady: “an immediate

[031-015] answer is required.”

[031-016] Mrs. [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]] glanced her eye carelessly over the contents. “What stuff

[031-017] is this;” cried she haughtily; “have not I told you a thousand times

[031-018] that I will not be plagued with beggars, and petitions from people one

[031-019] knows nothing about? Go tell the woman I can't do any thing in it. I'm

[031-020] sorry, but one can't relieve every body.”

[031-021] The servant bowed, and heavily returned with this chilling message to

[031-022] Charlotte.

[031-023] “Surely,” said she, “Mrs. Crayton has not read my letter. Go, my

[031-024] good [[friendship|friend]], pray go back to her; tell her it is Charlotte [[mr-temple|Temple]] who

[031-025] requests beneath her hospitable roof to find shelter from the inclemency

[031-026] of the season.”

[031-027] “Prithee, don't plague me, man,” cried Mrs. Crayton impatiently, as the

[031-028] servant advanced something in behalf of the unhappy girl. “I tell you I

[031-029] don't know her.”

[031-030] “Not know me,” cried Charlotte, rushing into the room, (for she had

[031-031] followed the man up stairs) “not know me, not remember the [[ruin|ruined]]

[031-032] Charlotte Temple, who, but for you, perhaps might still have been

[031-033] innocent, still have been [[happiness|happy]]. Oh! La Rue, this is beyond every thing

[031-034] I could have believed possible.”

[031-035] “Upon my honour, Miss,” replied the unfeeling woman with the utmost

[031-036] effrontery, “this is a most unaccountable address: it is beyond my

[031-037] comprehension. John,” continued she, turning to the servant, “the

[031-038] [[youth|young]] woman is certainly out of her senses: do pray take her away, she

[031-039] terrifies me to death.”

[031-040] “Oh God,” cried Charlotte, clasping her hands in an [[suffering|agony]], “this is too

[031-041] much; what will become of me? but I will not leave you; they shall

[031-042] not tear me from you; here on my knees I conjure you to save me from

[031-043] perishing in the streets; if you really have forgot me, oh for [[benevolence|charity]]'s

[031-044] sweet sake this night let me be sheltered from the winter's piercing

[031-045] cold.” The kneeling figure of Charlotte in her affecting situation might

[031-046] have moved the heart of a stoic to [[charity|compassion]]; but Mrs. Crayton remained

[031-047] inflexible. In vain did Charlotte recount the time they had known each

[031-048] other at [[chichester|Chichester]], in vain mention their being in the same ship, in

[031-049] vain were the names of [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]] mentioned. Mrs. Crayton

[031-050] could only say she was sorry for her [[prudence|imprudence]], but could not think of

[031-051] having her own reputation endangered by encouraging a woman of that kind

[031-052] in her own house, besides she did not know what trouble and expense

[031-053] she might bring upon her husband by giving shelter to a woman in her

[031-054] situation.

[031-055] “I can at least die here,” said Charlotte, “I feel I cannot long

[031-056] survive this dreadful conflict. Father of [[compassion|mercy]], here let me finish

[031-057] my existence.” Her agonizing sensations overpowered her, and she fell

[031-058] senseless on the floor.

[031-059] “Take her away,” said Mrs. Crayton, “she will really frighten me into

[031-060] hysterics; take her away I say this instant.”

[031-061] “And where must I take the poor creature?” said the servant with a voice

[031-062] and look of compassion.

[031-063] “Any where,” cried she hastily, “only don't let me ever see her again. I

[031-064] declare she has flurried me so I shan't be myself again this fortnight.”

[031-065] John, assisted by his fellow-servant, raised and carried her down

[031-066] stairs. “Poor soul,” said he, “you shall not lay in the street this

[031-067] night. I have a bed and a poor little hovel, where my wife and her

[031-068] little ones rest them, but they shall watch to night, and you shall be

[031-069] sheltered from danger.” They placed her in a chair; and the benevolent

[031-070] man, assisted by one of his comrades, carried her to the place where his

[031-071] wife and children lived. A surgeon was sent for: he bled her, she gave

[031-072] signs of returning life, and before the dawn gave birth to a female

[031-073] infant. After this event she lay for some hours in a kind of stupor; and

[031-074] if at any time she spoke, it was with a quickness and incoherence that

[031-075] plainly evinced the total deprivation of her reason.
