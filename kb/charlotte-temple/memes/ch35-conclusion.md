[035-001] SHORTLY after the interment of his daughter, [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], with his

[035-002] dear little charge and her nurse, set forward for [[england|England]]. It would be

[035-003] impossible to do justice to the meeting scene between him, his [[lucy|Lucy]], and

[035-004] her aged father. Every heart of sensibility can easily conceive their

[035-005] feelings. After the first tumult of [[sorrow|grief]] was subsided, [[mrs-temple|Mrs. Temple]]

[035-006] gave up the chief of her time to her grand-child, and as she grew up and

[035-007] improved, began to almost fancy she again possessed her [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].

[035-008] It was about ten years after these painful events, that Mr. and Mrs.

[035-009] Temple, having buried their father, were obliged to come to London on

[035-010] particular business, and brought the little Lucy with them. They had

[035-011] been walking one evening, when on their return they found a poor

[035-012] wretch sitting on the steps of the door. She attempted to rise as they

[035-013] approached, but from extreme weakness was unable, and after several

[035-014] fruitless efforts fell back in a fit. Mr. Temple was not one of those

[035-015] men who stand to consider whether by assisting an object in distress

[035-016] they shall not inconvenience themselves, but instigated by the impulse

[035-017] of a noble [[sensibility|feeling]] heart, immediately ordered her to be carried into the

[035-018] house, and proper restoratives applied.

[035-019] She soon recovered; and fixing her eyes on Mrs. Temple, cried--“You know

[035-020] not, Madam, what you do; you know not whom you are relieving, or you

[035-021] would curse me in the [[resentment|bitterness]] of your heart. Come not near me, Madam,

[035-022] I shall contaminate you. I am the viper that stung your peace. I am the

[035-023] woman who turned the poor Charlotte out to perish in the street. Heaven

[035-024] have [[compassion|mercy]]! I see her now,” continued she looking at Lucy; “such, such

[035-025] was the fair bud of innocence that my vile arts blasted ere it was half

[035-026] blown.”

[035-027] It was in vain that Mr. and Mrs. Temple intreated her to be composed and

[035-028] to take some refreshment. She only drank half a glass of wine; and then

[035-029] told them that she had been separated from her husband seven years,

[035-030] the chief of which she had passed in riot, dissipation, and vice, till,

[035-031] overtaken by poverty and sickness, she had been reduced to part with

[035-032] every valuable, and thought only of ending her life in a prison; when a

[035-033] benevolent [[friendship|friend]] paid her debts and released her; but that her illness

[035-034] increasing, she had no possible means of supporting herself, and her

[035-035] friends were weary of relieving her. “I have fasted,” said she, “two

[035-036] days, and last night lay my aching head on the cold pavement: indeed it

[035-037] was but just that I should [[experience]] those miseries myself which I had

[035-038] unfeelingly inflicted on others.”

## Section 19

[035-039] Greatly as Mr. Temple had reason to detest [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mrs. Crayton]], he could not

[035-040] behold her in this distress without some emotions of [[compassion|pity]]. He gave her

[035-041] shelter that night beneath his hospitable roof, and the next day got her

[035-042] admission into an hospital; where having lingered a few weeks, she died,

[035-043] a striking example that vice, however prosperous in the beginning, in

[035-044] the end leads only to [[distress|misery]] and shame.
