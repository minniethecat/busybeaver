[004-001] “IT was some days,” continued [[captain-eldridge|Mr. Eldridge]], recovering himself, “before

[004-002] I could venture to enquire the particulars of what had happened during

[004-003] my illness: at length I assumed courage to ask my dear girl how long her

[004-004] mother and brother had been dead: she told me, that the morning after

[004-005] my arrest, George came home early to enquire after his mother's health,

[004-006] staid with them but a few minutes, seemed greatly agitated at parting,

[004-007] but gave them strict charge to keep up their spirits, and hope every

[004-008] thing would turn out for the best. In about two hours after, as they

[004-009] were sitting at breakfast, and endeavouring to strike out some plan to

[004-010] attain my [[independence|liberty]], they heard a loud rap at the door, which [[lucy|Lucy]] running

[004-011] to open, she met the bleeding body of her brother, borne in by two men

[004-012] who had lifted him from a litter, on which they had brought him from

[004-013] the place where he fought. Her poor mother, weakened by illness and the

[004-014] struggles of the preceding night, was not able to support this shock;

[004-015] gasping for breath, her looks wild and haggard, she reached the

[004-016] apartment where they had carried her [[death|dying]] son. She knelt by the bed

[004-017] side; and taking his cold hand, 'my poor boy,' said she, 'I will not be

[004-018] parted from thee: husband! son! both at once lost. Father of mercies,

[004-019] spare me!' She fell into a strong convulsion, and expired in about two

[004-020] hours. In the mean time, a surgeon had dressed George's wounds; but they

[004-021] were in such a situation as to bar the smallest [[hope|hopes]] of recovery. He

[004-022] never was sensible from the time he was brought home, and died that

[004-023] evening in the arms of his sister.

[004-024] “Late as it was when this event took place, my affectionate Lucy

[004-025] insisted on coming to me. 'What must he feel,' said she, 'at our

[004-026] apparent neglect, and how shall I inform him of the afflictions with

[004-027] which it has pleased heaven to visit us?'

[004-028] “She left the care of the dear departed ones to some neighbours who

[004-029] had kindly come in to comfort and assist her; and on entering the house

[004-030] where I was confined, found me in the situation I have mentioned.

[004-031] “How she supported herself in these trying moments, I know not: heaven,

[004-032] no doubt, was with her; and her [[fear|anxiety]] to preserve the life of one

[004-033] parent in some measure abated her [[adversity|affliction]] for the loss of the other.

[004-034] “My circumstances were greatly embarrassed, my acquaintance few,

[004-035] and those few utterly unable to assist me. When my wife and son were

[004-036] committed to their kindred earth, my creditors seized my house and

[004-037] furniture, which not being sufficient to discharge all their demands,

[004-038] detainers were lodged against me. No [[friendship|friend]] stepped forward to my

[004-039] [[comfort|relief]]; from the grave of her mother, my [[love|beloved]] Lucy followed an almost

[004-040] dying father to this melancholy place.

[004-041] “Here we have been nearly a year and a half. My half-pay I have given

[004-042] up to satisfy my creditors, and my child supports me by her industry:

[004-043] sometimes by fine needlework, sometimes by painting. She leaves me

[004-044] every night, and goes to a lodging near the bridge; but returns in

[004-045] the morning, to cheer me with her smiles, and bless me by her duteous

[004-046] [[love|affection]]. A lady once offered her an asylum in her [[family]]; but she

[004-047] would not leave me. 'We are all the world to each other,' said she. 'I

[004-048] thank God, I have health and spirits to improve the talents with which

[004-049] nature has endowed me; and I [[trust]] if I employ them in the support of a

[004-050] beloved parent, I shall not be thought an unprofitable servant. While he

[004-051] lives, I pray for strength to pursue my employment; and when it pleases

[004-052] heaven to take one of us, may it give the survivor resignation to bear

[004-053] the separation as we ought: till then I will never leave him.'”

[004-054] “But where is this inhuman persecutor?” said [[mr-temple|Temple]].

[004-055] “He has been abroad ever since,” replied the old man; “but he has

[004-056] left orders with his lawyer never to give up the note till the utmost

[004-057] farthing is paid.”

[004-058] “And how much is the amount of your debts in all?” said Temple.

[004-059] “Five hundred pounds,” he replied.

[004-060] Temple started: it was more than he expected. “But something must be

[004-061] done,” said he: “that sweet maid must not wear out her life in a prison.

[004-062] I will see you again to-morrow, my friend,” said he, shaking Eldridge's

[004-063] hand: “keep up your spirits: light and shade are not more happily

[004-064] blended than are the pleasures and pains of life; and the horrors of the

[004-065] one serve only to increase the splendor of the other.”

[004-066] “You never lost a wife and son,” said Eldridge.

[004-067] “No,” replied he, “but I can feel for those that have.” Eldridge pressed

[004-068] his hand as they went toward the door, and they parted in silence.

[004-069] When they got without the walls of the prison, Temple thanked his friend

[004-070] Blakeney for introducing him to so worthy a [[reputation|character]]; and telling him

[004-071] he had a particular engagement in [[new-york|the city]], wished him a good evening.

[004-072] “And what is to be done for this distressed man,” said Temple, as he

[004-073] walked up Ludgate Hill. “Would to heaven I had a [[fate|fortune]] that would

[004-074] enable me instantly to discharge his debt: what exquisite transport, to

[004-075] see the expressive eyes of Lucy beaming at once with pleasure for her

[004-076] father's [[liberty|deliverance]], and gratitude for her deliverer: but is not my

[004-077] [[wealth|fortune]] affluence,” continued he, “nay superfluous wealth, when compared

[004-078] to the extreme [[poverty|indigence]] of Eldridge; and what have I done to deserve

[004-079] ease and plenty, while a brave worthy officer starves in a prison? Three

[004-080] hundred a year is surely sufficient for all my wants and wishes: at any

[004-081] rate Eldridge must be relieved.”

[004-082] When the heart has will, the hands can soon find means to execute a good

[004-083] action.

[004-084] Temple was a [[youth|young]] man, his feelings warm and impetuous; unacquainted

[004-085] with the world, his heart had not been rendered callous by being

[004-086] convinced of its fraud and hypocrisy. He pitied their [[suffering|sufferings]],

[004-087] overlooked their faults, thought every bosom as generous as his own, and

[004-088] would cheerfully have divided his last guinea with an unfortunate fellow

[004-089] creature.

[004-090] No wonder, then, that such a man (without waiting a moment for the

[004-091] interference of Madam [[prudence|Prudence]]) should resolve to raise money sufficient

[004-092] for the relief of Eldridge, by mortgaging part of his fortune.

[004-093] We will not enquire too minutely into the cause which might actuate

[004-094] him in this instance: suffice it to say, he immediately put the plan in

[004-095] execution; and in three days from the time he first saw the unfortunate

[004-096] Lieutenant, he had the superlative [[happiness|felicity]] of seeing him at liberty,

[004-097] and receiving an ample reward in the tearful eye and half articulated

[004-098] thanks of the grateful Lucy.

[004-099] “And pray, young man,” said his father to him one morning, “what are

[004-100] your designs in visiting thus constantly that old man and his daughter?”

[004-101] Temple was at a loss for a reply: he had never asked himself the

[004-102] question: he hesitated; and his father continued--

[004-103] “It was not till within these few days that I heard in what manner

[004-104] your acquaintance first commenced, and cannot suppose any thing but

[004-105] attachment to the daughter could carry you such imprudent lengths for

[004-106] the father: it certainly must be her art that drew you in to mortgage

[004-107] part of your fortune.”

[004-108] “Art, Sir!” cried Temple eagerly. “Lucy Eldridge is as free from art as

[004-109] she is from every other error: she is--”

[004-110] “Everything that is amiable and lovely,” said his father, interrupting

[004-111] him ironically: “no doubt in your opinion she is a pattern of excellence

[004-112] for all her sex to follow; but come, Sir, pray tell me what are your

[004-113] designs towards this paragon. I hope you do not intend to complete your

[004-114] folly by marrying her.”

[004-115] “Were my fortune such as would support her according to her merit,

[004-116] I don't know a woman more formed to insure happiness in the married

[004-117] state.”

[004-118] “Then prithee, my dear lad,” said his father, “since your rank and

[004-119] fortune are so much beneath what your PRINCESS might expect, be so kind

[004-120] as to turn your eyes on Miss Weatherby; who, having only an estate of

[004-121] three thousand a year, is more upon a level with you, and whose father

[004-122] yesterday solicited the mighty honour of your alliance. I shall leave

[004-123] you to consider on this offer; and pray remember, that your union with

[004-124] Miss Weatherby will put it in your power to be more liberally the friend

[004-125] of Lucy Eldridge.”

[004-126] The old gentleman walked in a stately manner out of the room; and Temple

[004-127] stood almost petrified with astonishment, contempt, and rage.
