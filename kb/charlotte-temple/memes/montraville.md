# Montraville

The British army officer who [[seduction|seduce]]s [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]] and whose abandonment of her precipitates her tragic death. Rowson draws Montraville as a morally complex figure: neither purely villainous nor genuinely [[honour|honourable]], he is a man who understands virtue but repeatedly fails to practice it.

## Role in the Novel

Montraville is the primary agent of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s ruin, but Rowson complicates him beyond the stock [[seduction|seducer]]. He first appears walking with [[belcour|Belcour]] near the [[chichester|Chichester]] barracks when he sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] at her boarding-school window (001-001, 001-021). His initial attraction quickly transforms into a systematic seduction, aided by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]] (006-032). He persuades [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to elope and takes her to [[america|America]], where he eventually transfers his [[love|affection]]s to [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]] and abandons the pregnant [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]. His belated discovery of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death produces genuine [[distress|anguish]], and Rowson presents his [[regret|remorse]] as sincere but futile.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Montraville is capable of genuine [[sensibility|feeling]] and ultimately feels [[regret|remorse]] for his actions (035-039). He is not portrayed as [[nature|innate]]ly cruel but as morally weak. The narrator at times presents him as a victim of his own [[passion|passions]] and of [[belcour|Belcour]]'s influence.
- **Flaws**: His primary fault is moral cowardice — he knows what honour requires but lacks the strength to act on that knowledge. He is susceptible to flattery, easily influenced by [[belcour|Belcour]]'s cynical counsel, and allows convenience and military duty to provide a pretext for abandoning his obligations. The narrator notes how "well did Montraville improve each opportunity" for seduction (011-007).

## Key Actions

- First sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school and determines to pursue her (001-021, 001-050).
- Enlists [[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]'s assistance to gain access to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (006-032).
- Persuades [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to elope, promising marriage and invoking the language of honour (011-040).
- Takes [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] to [[america|America]], where they live as if married.
- Meets [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]] and becomes infatuated with her, describing her as "the very reverse of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]" (019-064).
- Abandons the pregnant [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] after [[belcour|Belcour]] convinces him she has been un[[religion|faith]]ful.
- Discovers [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death and is overcome with [[regret|remorse]], acknowledging his responsibility for her ruin (035-039).

## Relationships

- [[charlotte-temple]]: His victim. Montraville's conduct toward [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] follows the [[seduction|seducer]]'s arc: pursuit, conquest, possessive attachment, growing indifference, and final abandonment. His [[regret|remorse]] at her death is real but powerless to undo the harm.
- [[belcour]]: His false [[friendship|friend]], who encourages his worst impulses and secretly schemes to [[seduction|seduce]] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] himself once Montraville abandons her (023-061). [[belcour|Belcour]] intercepts the money Montraville sends to support [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].
- [[julia-franklin]]: The [[virtue|virtuous]] [[youth|young]] woman who becomes the object of Montraville's redirected [[love|affection]]s. Her virtue serves as a foil to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]]en state (019-064).

## Moral Significance

Montraville embodies Rowson's warning about the gap between the rhetoric and practice of honour. He repeatedly invokes the language of honour — promising marriage, calling himself "a man of honour" (011-040) — yet abandons [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] when his word becomes inconvenient. His genuine [[regret|remorse]] at the novel's end serves Rowson's didactic purpose: the [[seduction|seducer]] himself is [[ruin|ruined]] by his actions, condemned to live with the knowledge of the [[ruin|destruction]] he has caused. The narrator's final assessment suggests that "greatly as [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] had reason to detest" Montraville, even he could not withhold [[compassion|pity]] for a man destroyed by his own moral failure (035-039).

## Fate / Resolution

Montraville survives the novel but is shattered by [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death. His [[regret|remorse]] is presented as a living punishment: he must continue to exist with the knowledge that his weakness and selfishness caused the death of an innocent [[youth|young]] woman. Rowson denies him the clean ending of either death or redemption, leaving him suspended in eternal regret.
