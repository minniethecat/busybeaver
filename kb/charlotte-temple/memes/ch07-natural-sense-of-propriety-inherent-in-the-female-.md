[007-001] “I CANNOT think we have done exactly right in going out this evening,

[007-002] Mademoiselle,” said [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], seating herself when she entered her

[007-003] apartment: “nay, I am sure it was not right; for I expected to be very

[007-004] [[happiness|happy]], but was sadly disappointed.”

[007-005] “It was your own fault, then,” replied Mademoiselle: “for I am sure

[007-006] my cousin omitted nothing that could serve to render the evening

[007-007] agreeable.”

[007-008] “True,” said Charlotte: “but I thought the gentlemen were very free in

[007-009] their manner: I wonder you would [[suffering|suffer]] them to behave as they did.”

[007-010] “Prithee, don't be such a [[folly|foolish]] little prude,” said the artful woman,

[007-011] affecting anger: “I invited you to go in [[hope|hopes]] it would divert you, and

[007-012] be an agreeable change of scene; however, if your delicacy was hurt by

[007-013] the behaviour of the gentlemen, you need not go again; so there let it

[007-014] rest.”

[007-015] “I do not intend to go again,” said Charlotte, gravely taking off her

[007-016] bonnet, and beginning to prepare for bed: “I am sure, if [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]

[007-017] knew we had been out to-night, she would be very angry; and it is ten to

[007-018] one but she hears of it by some means or other.”

[007-019] “Nay, Miss,” said [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], “perhaps your mighty [[reason|sense]] of [[modesty|propriety]] may

[007-020] lead you to tell her yourself: and in order to avoid the censure you

[007-021] would incur, should she hear of it by accident, throw the blame on

[007-022] me: but I confess I deserve it: it will be a very kind return for that

[007-023] partiality which led me to prefer you before any of the rest of the

[007-024] ladies; but perhaps it will give you pleasure,” continued she, letting

[007-025] [[ruin|fall]] some hypocritical tears, “to see me deprived of bread, and for an

[007-026] action which by the most rigid could only be esteemed an inadvertency,

[007-027] lose my place and [[reputation|character]], and be driven again into the world, where I

[007-028] have already suffered all the evils attendant on poverty.”

[007-029] This was touching Charlotte in the most vulnerable part: she rose from

[007-030] her seat, and taking Mademoiselle's hand--“You know, my dear La Rue,”

[007-031] said she, “I [[affection|love]] you too well, to do anything that would injure you in

[007-032] my governess's opinion: I am only sorry we went out this evening.”

[007-033] “I don't believe it, Charlotte,” said she, assuming a little vivacity;

[007-034] “for if you had not gone out, you would not have seen the gentleman who

[007-035] met us crossing the field; and I rather think you were pleased with his

[007-036] conversation.”

[007-037] “I had seen him once before,” replied Charlotte, “and thought him an

[007-038] agreeable man; and you know one is always pleased to see a person with

[007-039] whom one has passed several cheerful hours. But,” said she pausing,

[007-040] and drawing the letter from her pocket, while a gentle suffusion of

[007-041] vermillion tinged her neck and face, “he gave me this letter; what shall

[007-042] I do with it?”

## Section 5

[007-043] “Read it, to be sure,” returned Mademoiselle.

[007-044] “I am afraid I ought not,” said Charlotte: “my mother has often told

[007-045] me, I should never read a letter given me by a [[youth|young]] man, without first

[007-046] giving it to her.”

[007-047] “Lord bless you, my dear girl,” cried the teacher smiling, “have you

[007-048] a mind to be in leading strings all your life time. Prithee open the

[007-049] letter, read it, and judge for yourself; if you show it your mother, the

[007-050] consequence will be, you will be taken from school, and a strict guard

[007-051] kept over you; so you will stand no chance of ever seeing the smart

[007-052] young officer again.”

[007-053] “I should not like to leave school yet,” replied Charlotte, “till I have

[007-054] attained a greater proficiency in my Italian and music. But you can, if

[007-055] you please, Mademoiselle, take the letter back to [[montraville|Montraville]], and

[007-056] tell him I wish him well, but cannot, with any propriety, enter into a

[007-057] clandestine correspondence with him.” She laid the letter on the table,

[007-058] and began to undress herself.

[007-059] “Well,” said La Rue, “I vow you are an unaccountable girl: have you

[007-060] no curiosity to see the inside now? for my part I could no more let a

[007-061] letter addressed to me lie unopened so long, than I could work miracles:

[007-062] he writes a good hand,” continued she, turning the letter, to look at

[007-063] the superscription.

[007-064] “'Tis well enough,” said Charlotte, drawing it towards her.

[007-065] “He is a genteel young fellow,” said La Rue carelessly, folding up her

[007-066] apron at the same time; “but I think he is marked with the small pox.”

[007-067] “Oh you are greatly mistaken,” said Charlotte eagerly; “he has a

[007-068] remarkable clear skin and fine complexion.”

[007-069] “His eyes, if I could judge by what I saw,” said La Rue, “are grey and

[007-070] [[poverty|want]] expression.”

[007-071] “By no means,” replied Charlotte; “they are the most expressive eyes

[007-072] I ever saw.” “Well, child, whether they are grey or black is of no

[007-073] consequence: you have determined not to read his letter; so it is likely

[007-074] you will never either see or hear from him again.”

[007-075] Charlotte took up the letter, and Mademoiselle continued--

[007-076] “He is most probably going to [[america|America]]; and if ever you should hear any

[007-077] account of him, it may possibly be that he is killed; and though he

[007-078] loved you ever so fervently, though his last breath should be spent in

[007-079] a prayer for your happiness, it can be nothing to you: you can feel

[007-080] nothing for the fate of the man, whose letters you will not open, and

[007-081] whose sufferings you will not alleviate, by permitting him to think you

[007-082] would remember him when absent, and pray for his safety.”

[007-083] Charlotte still held the letter in her hand: her heart swelled at the

[007-084] conclusion of Mademoiselle's speech, and a tear dropped upon the wafer

[007-085] that closed it.

[007-086] “The wafer is not dry yet,” said she, “and sure there can be no great

[007-087] harm--” She hesitated. La Rue was silent. “I may read it, Mademoiselle,

[007-088] and return it afterwards.”

[007-089] “Certainly,” replied Mademoiselle.

[007-090] “At any rate I am determined not to answer it,” continued Charlotte, as

[007-091] she opened the letter.

[007-092] Here let me stop to make one remark, and [[trust]] me my very heart aches

[007-093] while I write it; but certain I am, that when once a woman has stifled

[007-094] the sense of shame in her own bosom, when once she has lost sight of the

[007-095] basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to

[007-096] the female heart, rests, she grows hardened in guilt, and will spare

[007-097] no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with

[007-098] herself: and this proceeds from that diabolical spirit of envy, which

[007-099] repines at seeing another in the full possession of that respect and

[007-100] esteem which she can no longer hope to enjoy.

[007-101] Mademoiselle eyed the unsuspecting Charlotte, as she perused the letter,

[007-102] with a malignant pleasure. She saw, that the contents had awakened new

[007-103] emotions in her youthful bosom: she encouraged her hopes, calmed her

[007-104] [[fear|fears]], and before they parted for the night, it was determined that she

[007-105] should meet Montraville the ensuing evening.
