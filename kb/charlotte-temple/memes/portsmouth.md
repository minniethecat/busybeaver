# Portsmouth

The [[england|English]] port city from whose barracks [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]] set out on the walk that brings them past [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school. Portsmouth is the novel's starting point — the place where the seduction plot originates and the site of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s first, irreversible exposure to male predation.

## Location

Portsmouth is a historic port city on the south coast of [[england|England]], home to a major naval and military presence. In Rowson's novel, it serves as the garrison for [[montraville|Montraville]]'s regiment. The city's function as a departure point for transatlantic voyages is significant: it is from Portsmouth that ships sail for [[america|America]], and it is [[new-york|the city]] whose military population provides the novel's predatory male [[reputation|character]]s.

The novel opens with [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]] debating whether "to proceed to Portsmouth" or walk elsewhere (001-003), immediately establishing [[new-york|the city]] as the setting of military masculinity and its associated dangers.

## Narrative Role

Portsmouth serves as the novel's initiating location. Key events that occur here or originate here include:

- [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]] walk from the Portsmouth barracks past [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school, where [[montraville|Montraville]] first sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (001-021).
- The clandestine meetings between [[montraville|Montraville]], [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] are planned from Portsmouth.
- The elopement party departs from the Portsmouth area, heading for the ship that will take them to [[america|America]] (009-028).

## Characters Associated

- [[montraville]]: Stationed at Portsmouth as an army officer; his military identity is tied to [[new-york|the city]] (001-001).
- [[belcour]]: [[montraville|Montraville]]'s brother officer, also stationed at Portsmouth (001-003).
- [[charlotte-temple]]: First seen by [[montraville|Montraville]] from the Portsmouth road near her school.

## Symbolic Significance

Portsmouth represents the world of male mobility and military power — a place where [[youth|young]] officers, freed from [[family]] supervision, pursue pleasure without accountability. Its function as a port city — a site of departure and transit — also marks it as a threshold space: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s passage from Portsmouth to [[america|America]] is a passage from innocence to ruin. The city's association with the navy and army reinforces the novel's critique of military masculinity, in which honour is a matter of professional reputation rather than moral conduct.

> "proceed to Portsmouth?" Belcour preferred the former; and they sauntered about the streets of the town till the evening. (001-003)
