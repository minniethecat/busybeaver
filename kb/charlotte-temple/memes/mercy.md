# Mercy

The disposition to relieve [[distress|suffering]] and forgive wrongdoing — in Rowson's novel, [[compassion|mercy]] takes both its human form ([[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s [[charity|compassion]]ate intervention) and its divine form (God's forgiveness of the repentant sinner), fusing the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al and the Christian into a single moral vision.

## Definition

Mercy in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* operates on two planes. Human [[compassion|mercy]] is embodied by [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], who appears as "an angel of [[compassion|mercy]]" when she listens [[charity|compassion]]ately to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story ([021-053]) and arranges for her care. Divine [[compassion|mercy]] is invoked repeatedly in the novel's final chapters as [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[death|dying]] and repentant, turns toward God: "Father of [[compassion|mercy]]," she cries, "I acknowledge my transgression" ([029-081]). Rowson's deployment of [[compassion|mercy]] is strategic — by placing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death within a Christian framework of repentance and divine forgiveness, the novel transforms what would otherwise be a bleak [[prudence|caution]]ary tale into a redemptive one. The reader is left not with despair but with the hope that [[compassion|mercy]] extends even to the [[ruin|fall]]en.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: The novel's primary human agent of [[compassion|mercy]] — she listens without [[reason|judgment]], provides material aid, and brings [[mr-temple|Charlotte's father]] to her [[death|deathbed]] ([021-053])
- [[charlotte-temple]]: The recipient of [[compassion|mercy]], both human (from [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]) and divine (in her final repentance); her [[death|deathbed]] scene is structured as a petition for [[compassion|mercy]] ([029-081])
- [[mr-temple]]: The father whose [[compassion|mercy]] is tested to its limits — he arrives too late to save [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] but extends forgiveness at her [[death|deathbed]] ([033-016])

## Narrative Function

Mercy serves as the novel's theological and emotional resolution. Without it, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story would end in unrelieved despair — a [[prudence|caution]]ary tale without [[comfort|consolation]]. By introducing [[compassion|mercy]] as both a human virtue ([[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]'s intervention) and a divine attribute (the "Father of [[compassion|mercy]]" to whom [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] prays), Rowson achieves her stated goal: to instruct without destroying hope. The reader is warned against seduction's dangers but also assured that repentance and [[compassion|mercy]] remain possible. This dual function — warning and [[comfort|consolation]] — is central to the novel's didactic strategy.

## Related Concepts

- [[forgiveness]]: The interpersonal form of [[compassion|mercy]] — related but distinct, as [[compassion|mercy]] emphasizes the active [[comfort|relief]] of [[distress|suffering]] while forgiveness emphasizes the remission of fault
- [[repentance]]: The necessary precondition for divine mercy — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[death|deathbed]] [[regret|contrition]] opens the door to forgiveness
- [[religion]]: The institutional framework within which divine mercy operates
- [[suffering]]: The condition that [[compassion|mercy]] relieves — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[distress|suffering]] makes [[compassion|mercy]] both necessary and meaningful
