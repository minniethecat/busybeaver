[028-001] “BLESS my heart,” cries my [[youth|young]], volatile reader, “I shall never have

[028-002] patience to get through these volumes, there are so many ahs! and

[028-003] ohs! so much fainting, tears, and distress, I am sick to death of the

[028-004] subject.” My dear, cheerful, innocent girl, for innocent I will

[028-005] suppose you to be, or you would acutely feel the woes of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]],

[028-006] did conscience say, thus might it have been with me, had not Providence

[028-007] interposed to snatch me from [[ruin|destruction]]: therefore, my lively, innocent

[028-008] girl, I must request your patience: I am writing a tale of truth: I

[028-009] mean to write it to the heart: but if perchance the heart is rendered

[028-010] impenetrable by unbounded [[wealth|prosperity]], or a continuance in vice, I expect

[028-011] not my tale to please, nay, I even expect it will be thrown by with

[028-012] disgust. But softly, gentle fair one; I pray you throw it not aside till

[028-013] you have perused the whole; mayhap you may find something therein to

[028-014] repay you for the trouble. Methinks I see a sarcastic smile sit on your

[028-015] countenance.--“And what,” cry you, “does the conceited author suppose

[028-016] we can glean from these pages, if Charlotte is held up as an object of

[028-017] terror, to prevent us from falling into [[guilt|guilty errors]]? does not [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]

[028-018] triumph in her shame, and by adding art to guilt, obtain the [[love|affection]]

[028-019] of a worthy man, and rise to a station where she is beheld with respect,

[028-020] and cheerfully received into all companies. What then is the moral

[028-021] you would inculcate? Would you wish us to think that a deviation

[028-022] from virtue, if covered by art and hypocrisy, is not an object of

[028-023] detestation, but on the contrary shall raise us to fame and honour?

[028-024] while the hapless girl who falls a victim to her too great sensibility,

[028-025] shall be loaded with [[shame|ignominy]] and shame?” No, my fair querist, I mean no

[028-026] such thing. Remember the endeavours of the wicked are often suffered to

[028-027] prosper, that in the end their fall may be attended with more [[resentment|bitterness]]

[028-028] of heart; while the cup of [[adversity|affliction]] is poured out for wise and

[028-029] salutary ends, and they who are compelled to drain it even to the bitter

[028-030] dregs, often find comfort at the bottom; the tear of [[repentance|penitence]] blots

[028-031] their offences from the book of fate, and they rise from the heavy,

[028-032] painful trial, purified and fit for a mansion in the kingdom of

[028-033] eternity.

[028-034] Yes, my young [[friendship|friends]], the tear of [[charity|compassion]] shall fall for the fate of

[028-035] Charlotte, while the name of La Rue shall be detested and despised. For

[028-036] Charlotte, the soul melts with sympathy; for La Rue, it feels nothing

[028-037] but horror and contempt. But perhaps your gay hearts would rather

[028-038] follow the fortunate Mrs. [[colonel-crayton|Crayton]] through the scenes of pleasure and

[028-039] dissipation in which she was engaged, than listen to the complaints

[028-040] and miseries of Charlotte. I will for once oblige you; I will for once

[028-041] follow her to midnight revels, balls, and scenes of gaiety, for in such

[028-042] was she constantly engaged.

[028-043] I have said her person was lovely; let us add that she was surrounded by

[028-044] splendor and affluence, and he must know but little of the world who can

[028-045] wonder, (however faulty such a woman's conduct,) at her being followed

[028-046] by the men, and her company courted by the women: in short Mrs. Crayton

[028-047] was the universal favourite: she set the fashions, she was toasted by

[028-048] all the gentlemen, and copied by all the ladies.

[028-049] Colonel Crayton was a domestic man. Could he be [[happiness|happy]] with such a woman?

[028-050] impossible! Remonstrance was vain: he might as well have preached to the

[028-051] winds, as endeavour to persuade her from any action, however ridiculous,

[028-052] on which she had set her mind: in short, after a little ineffectual

[028-053] struggle, he gave up the attempt, and left her to follow the bent of

[028-054] her own inclinations: what those were, I think the reader must have seen

[028-055] enough of her [[reputation|character]] to form a just idea. Among the number who paid

[028-056] their devotions at her shrine, she singled one, a young Ensign of mean

[028-057] birth, indifferent education, and weak intellects. How such a man came

[028-058] into the army, we hardly know to account for, and how he afterwards rose

[028-059] to posts of honour is likewise strange and wonderful. But [[fate|fortune]] is

[028-060] blind, and so are those too frequently who have the power of dispensing

[028-061] her favours: else why do we see fools and knaves at the very top of the

[028-062] wheel, while patient merit sinks to the extreme of the opposite abyss.

[028-063] But we may form a thousand conjectures on this subject, and yet never

[028-064] hit on the right. Let us therefore endeavour to deserve her smiles, and

[028-065] whether we succeed or not, we shall feel more [[nature|innate]] satisfaction, than

[028-066] thousands of those who bask in the sunshine of her favour unworthily.

[028-067] But to return to Mrs. Crayton: this young man, whom I shall distinguish

[028-068] by the name of Corydon, was the reigning favourite of her heart. He

[028-069] escorted her to the play, danced with her at every ball, and when

[028-070] indisposition prevented her going out, it was he alone who was permitted

[028-071] to cheer the gloomy solitude to which she was obliged to confine

[028-072] herself. Did she ever think of poor Charlotte?--if she did, my dear

[028-073] Miss, it was only to laugh at the poor girl's [[poverty|want]] of spirit in

[028-074] consenting to be moped up in the country, while [[montraville|Montraville]] was enjoying

[028-075] all the pleasures of a gay, dissipated city. When she heard of his

[028-076] marriage, she smiling said, so there's an end of Madam Charlotte's

[028-077] [[hope|hopes]]. I wonder who will take her now, or what will become of the little

[028-078] affected prude?

[028-079] But as you have lead to the subject, I think we may as well return to

[028-080] the distressed Charlotte, and not, like the unfeeling Mrs. Crayton, shut

[028-081] our hearts to the call of humanity.
