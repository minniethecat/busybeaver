# Madame Du Pont

The French headmistress of the boarding school in [[chichester|Chichester]] where [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is educated. She is a figure of institutional respectability whose authority proves insufficient to protect her pupils from the predators who operate within her establishment.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Du Pont appears primarily at the novel's beginning, where she presides over the school "at the head of her school" (001-016) with apparent dignity. Her establishment provides the setting for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s first encounter with [[montraville|Montraville]] and for the seduction plot that unfolds under her roof. Though she is not portrayed as corrupt or negligent, her inability to detect or prevent [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s machinations and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement il[[passion|lust]]rates a key theme: institutional guardianship cannot substitute for parental vigilance.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: Madame Du Pont maintains a respectable establishment and is presumably a competent educator. She appears to genuinely care for her pupils' welfare, and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is described as her "favourite" (006-024).
- **Flaws**: She is largely absent from the narrative's moral action, [[trust]]ing her teachers to act appropriately and failing to detect the corruption within her school. Her ineffectiveness as a guardian is a structural critique rather than a personal failing.

## Key Actions

- Presides over her school, where [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is a pupil (001-016).
- Unwittingly hosts the birthday celebration that becomes the occasion for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement (008-011).
- Fails to prevent [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] from leaving the school grounds for their clandestine meeting with [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]].
- Remains unaware of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement until it is too late.

## Relationships

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her favourite pupil, whose virtue she esteems but whose [[weakness|vulnerability]] she fails to recognize or protect.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Her employee, whose corrupt [[reputation|character]] she entirely fails to perceive. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s presence at the school as a [[trust]]ed teacher is the novel's most damning indictment of institutional education.

## Moral Significance

Madame Du Pont is a minor [[reputation|character]] with a significant thematic function. Her school represents the novel's [[prudence|caution]] about institutional education: a respectable establishment with a respectable headmistress can nevertheless harbour moral danger. Rowson's point is not that Madame Du Pont is wicked but that she is insufficient — no institution can substitute for the attentive care of [[virtue|virtuous]] parents. The novel thus reinforces its central argument that the protection of female virtue is a parental, not institutional, responsibility.

## Fate / Resolution

Madame Du Pont disappears from the narrative after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement. Her fate is never revealed, and she functions purely as a setting-element whose thematic purpose is fulfilled once [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] leaves her school.
