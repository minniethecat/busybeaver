# Mademoiselle La Rue

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s French teacher and the most dangerous influence upon the novel's [[youth|young]] protagonist. Rowson presents La Rue as a case study in the corrupting power of lost virtue: having [[ruin|fall]]en herself, she becomes an active agent of corruption, "sparing no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" (007-092).

## Role in the Novel

La Rue serves as the facilitator of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction. As a teacher at [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school, she is "added to a very pleasing person" (006-007), making her an effective instrument for [[montraville|Montraville]]'s designs. Rowson reveals that La Rue "had eloped from a convent with a young officer" (006-011) before arriving at the school — a backstory that establishes her as already morally compromised. She "possessed too much of the spirit of intrigue to remain long without adventures" (006-017), a disposition that makes her a natural accomplice to seduction. She accompanies [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] during the elopement (009-028) and continues to influence events in [[america|America]]. Her eventual marriage to [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]] — a man of some standing — represents one of the novel's most troubling ironies: the unrepentant corrupter prospers while her innocent victim perishes.

## Character Traits

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]s**: La Rue possesses intelligence, charm, and social skill — qualities that make her effective as a corrupter. Her "pleasing person" (006-007) and sophisticated manner give her influence over the naive [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].
- **Flaws**: She is fundamentally selfish, manipulative, and morally bankrupt. Having lost her own virtue, she has no interest in protecting the innocence of others. The narrator explicitly describes her as a woman who "will spare no pains to bring down innocence and beauty to the shocking level with herself" (007-092). She is driven by self-interest and displays no genuine [[regret|remorse]] for her role in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|destruction]]. Her history of convent elopement (006-011) and her "spirit of intrigue" (006-017) reveal a pattern of moral recklessness that predates her involvement with Charlotte.

## Key Actions

- Becomes acquainted with [[montraville|Montraville]] and agrees to facilitate his access to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (006-007, 006-032).
- Manipulates [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[sensibility|feeling]]s, alternately flattering, cajoling, and shaming her to overcome her resistance — beginning the moment Charlotte enters her apartment: "'Mademoiselle,' said Charlotte, seating herself" (007-002) initiates the first of many manipulative conversations (007-029, 007-033).
- On the night of the elopement, "Charlotte and the teacher stole out at the back gate" (006-037) to meet [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[belcour|Belcour]].
- Accompanies [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] in the elopement from [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]]'s school (009-028).
- Later, in [[america|America]], marries [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]], achieving the social elevation that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is denied.
- When [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] seeks her help in her final distress, La Rue — now Mrs. Crayton — refuses to acknowledge her (031-033), demonstrating the completeness of her moral corruption.

## Relationships

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Her pupil and victim. La Rue preys on [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sensibility and in[[experience]], using her position of [[trust]] to facilitate seduction.
- [[montraville]]: Her co-conspirator. La Rue serves [[montraville|Montraville]]'s purposes while pursuing her own self-interest, seeing in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s seduction an opportunity for her own advancement.
- [[colonel-crayton]]: Her husband. La Rue's successful marriage to a respectable man is deeply ironic, as Rowson uses it to il[[passion|lust]]rate that worldly justice does not always align with moral desert.
- [[madame-du-pont]]: Her employer, whom she betrays by secretly facilitating [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement from the school — "stole out at the back gate" (006-037) under cover of darkness.

## Moral Significance

La Rue is Rowson's most unsettling creation. She embodies the novel's argument that the woman who has lost her honour becomes not merely [[ruin|fall]]en but actively dangerous. The narrator's extended commentary — "when once a woman has lost sight of the basis on which reputation, honour, every thing that should be dear to the female heart, rests, she grows hardened in guilt" (007-092) — is occasioned directly by La Rue's behaviour. She is the living refutation of the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al convention that [[ruin|fall]]en women are merely passive [[suffering|suffer]]ers. Her backstory — "eloped from a convent with a young officer" (006-011) — suggests a pattern: La Rue has been corrupting and being corrupted long before she meets Charlotte.

Yet La Rue's worldly success — her marriage to [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]] and her comfortable life — presents a troubling counter-narrative to the novel's ostensible moral framework. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], the [[virtue|virtuous]] innocent, dies in poverty; La Rue, the unrepentant corrupter, prospers. Rowson acknowledges this injustice but offers no resolution beyond the suggestion that divine [[reason|judgment]] will ultimately set things right.

## Fate / Resolution

Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], La Rue survives and prospers. Her marriage to [[colonel-crayton|Colonel Crayton]] gives her social standing and material comfort — a deeply ironic ending that Rowson uses to underscore the novel's message that worldly justice is imperfect and that true [[reason|judgment]] belongs to God alone. Her final appearance (031-033) shows her hardened beyond [[regret|repentance]], refusing even to acknowledge her former pupil's [[distress|suffering]].
