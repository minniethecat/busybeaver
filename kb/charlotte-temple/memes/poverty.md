Poverty in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is not a [[nature|natural]] condition but a punishment — the material consequence of moral transgression in a society that offers [[ruin|fall]]en women no economic safety net. Rowson's trilogy of "guilt, poverty, and disease" ([018-066]) positions poverty as the middle term in seduction's causal chain: guilt produces social exclusion, social exclusion produces poverty, and poverty accelerates the disease that kills. The novel's depiction of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s descent from gentlewoman to beggar is among the earliest and most influential representations of sexual transgression as economic catastrophe in [[america|American]] fiction.

## Definition

Rowson treats poverty as both a material condition and a moral spectacle. When [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]] visits [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s sickroom and "had never before beheld such a scene of poverty" ([033-011]), the shudder that follows is both sympathetic and didactic — the reader is meant to shudder too, and to absorb the lesson that poverty is what seduction leads to. The novel's economics are brutally clear: the [[ruin|fall]]en woman has no means of support, no right to assistance, and no social standing that would compel [[benevolence|charity]]. Poverty is thus the visible proof of ruin, the material form of social death.

## Key Passages

> "Shame bows her to the earth, remorse tears her distracted mind, and guilt, poverty, and disease close the dreadful scene" ([018-066])

> "Mrs. Beauchamp had never before beheld such a scene of poverty; she shuddered involuntarily" ([033-011])

> "The misery Charlotte had endured from the complicated evils of illness, poverty, and a broken heart" ([034-016])

> "All the evils attendant on poverty" — Captain Eldridge's description of his earlier suffering ([007-028])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Poverty's trajectory from gentlewoman to beggar. Her poverty is the direct consequence of her [[ruin|fall]], and its details — the bare room, the thin gruel, the wooden spoon — are rendered with documentary precision.
- [[captain-eldridge]]: Poverty as moral test survived. His earlier descent into debtor's prison il[[passion|lust]]rates poverty's threat to the [[virtue|virtuous]] as well as the [[ruin|fall]]en, though his rescue by [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]] offers the hope that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s story denies.
- [[mr-temple]]: Poverty's antidote. His wealth enables him to rescue [[captain-eldridge|Eldridge]] and to attempt to rescue [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], making him the novel's agent of economic as well as moral redemption.

## Narrative Function

Poverty is the novel's most potent instrument of realism. Rowson's depiction of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s material circumstances — the gruel, the bare room, the winter cold — grounds the novel's moral argument in physical detail. Poverty makes seduction's consequences tangible: the reader who might abstractly [[compassion|pity]] the [[ruin|fall]]en woman is confronted with the material reality of her [[distress|suffering]]. The concept also serves a class function: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s poverty is all the more shocking because she was raised in comfort, making her [[ruin|fall]] a warning to middle-class readers that virtue is their only reliable economic safeguard.

## Related Concepts

- [[suffering]]: The broader category encompassing poverty. Poverty is material [[distress|suffering]], distinct from emotional [[distress|suffering]] but often compounding it.
- [[guilt]]: Poverty's moral precondition. In the novel's logic, guilt produces poverty, and poverty intensifies guilt's torment.
- [[charity]]: The virtue that should relieve poverty but too often fails. The novel's critique of failed [[benevolence|charity]] is also a critique of a society that allows poverty to punish the [[ruin|fall]]en.
- [[wealth]]: Poverty's opposite. The novel contrasts [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s destitution with the comfort she once knew and the wealth of those who could have helped her.
