Pride in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is a destructive social force that operates at two levels: the institutional pride of aristocratic families that subordinates individual [[happiness]] to dynastic dignity, and the personal pride that converts wounded [[honour]] into a desire for [[revenge]]. Rowson's treatment of pride is notably ambivalent — it is condemned as a source of [[distress|misery]] yet recognised as a powerful motive force in the world her characters inhabit.

## Key Passages

> "means adequate to the antiquity, grandeur, and I may add, pride of the family" — on the Temple family's aristocratic pretensions ([002-002])

> "wish to revenge the affront which pride whispers him he has received" — on the selfish man's impulse to vengeance ([008-082])

## Narrative Function

In the novel's opening, pride is identified with the aristocratic family system that "legally prostitute[s]" daughters to "old, decrepid men" for the sake of titles ([002-002]–[002-006]). [[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]]'s rejection of this system — marrying for [[love]] rather than fortune — sets the novel's moral baseline. But pride also appears as a personal vice: the "selfish churl" who nurses grievances and "wish[es] to revenge the affront" acts from the same impulse that motivates [[montraville|Montraville]]'s seduction of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — pride wounded by [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s manipulations. Pride thus functions as both a systemic critique of aristocratic marriage and a psychological explanation for individual cruelty.

## Related Concepts

- [[revenge]]: Pride's natural consequence — wounded pride seeks vengeance.
- [[honour]]: The aristocratic code that feeds pride and produces [[distress|misery]].
- [[forgiveness]]: The virtue that pride prevents.
