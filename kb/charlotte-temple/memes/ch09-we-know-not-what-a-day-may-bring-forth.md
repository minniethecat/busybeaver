[009-001] VARIOUS were the sensations which agitated the mind of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], during

[009-002] the day preceding the evening in which she was to meet [[montraville|Montraville]].

[009-003] Several times did she almost resolve to go to her governess, show her

[009-004] the letter, and be guided by her advice: but Charlotte had taken one

[009-005] step in the ways of [[prudence|imprudence]]; and when that is once done, there are

[009-006] always innumerable obstacles to prevent the erring person returning to

[009-007] the path of [[virtue|rectitude]]: yet these obstacles, however forcible they may

[009-008] appear in general, exist chiefly in imagination.

[009-009] Charlotte feared the anger of her governess: she loved her mother,

[009-010] and the very idea of incurring her displeasure, gave her the greatest

[009-011] uneasiness: but there was a more forcible reason still remaining: should

[009-012] she show the letter to [[madame-du-pont|Madame Du Pont]], she must confess the means by

[009-013] which it came into her possession; and what would be the consequence?

[009-014] Mademoiselle would be turned out of doors.

[009-015] “I must not be ungrateful,” said she. “[[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is very kind to me;

[009-016] besides I can, when I see Montraville, inform him of the impropriety of

[009-017] our continuing to see or correspond with each other, and request him to

[009-018] come no more to [[chichester|Chichester]].”

[009-019] However prudent Charlotte might be in these resolutions, she certainly

[009-020] did not take a proper method to confirm herself in them. Several times

[009-021] in the course of the day, she indulged herself in reading over the

[009-022] letter, and each time she read it, the contents sunk deeper in her

[009-023] heart. As evening drew near, she caught herself frequently consulting

[009-024] her watch. “I wish this [[folly|foolish]] meeting was over,” said she, by way of

[009-025] apology to her own heart, “I wish it was over; for when I have seen him,

[009-026] and convinced him my resolution is not to be shaken, I shall feel my

[009-027] mind much easier.”

## Section 6

[009-028] The appointed hour arrived. Charlotte and Mademoiselle eluded the eye of

[009-029] vigilance; and Montraville, who had waited their coming with impatience,

[009-030] received them with rapturous and unbounded acknowledgments for their

[009-031] condescension: he had wisely brought [[belcour|Belcour]] with him to entertain

[009-032] Mademoiselle, while he enjoyed an uninterrupted conversation with

[009-033] Charlotte.

[009-034] Belcour was a man whose [[reputation|character]] might be comprised in a few words; and

[009-035] as he will make some figure in the ensuing pages, I shall here describe

[009-036] him. He possessed a genteel [[fate|fortune]], and had a liberal education;

[009-037] dissipated, thoughtless, and capricious, he paid little regard to

[009-038] the moral [[duty|duties]], and less to religious ones: eager in the pursuit of

[009-039] pleasure, he minded not the miseries he inflicted on others, provided

[009-040] his own wishes, however extravagant, were gratified. Self, darling self,

[009-041] was the idol he worshipped, and to that he would have sacrificed

[009-042] the interest and happiness of all mankind. Such was the [[friendship|friend]] of

[009-043] Montraville: will not the reader be ready to imagine, that the man who

[009-044] could regard such a character, must be actuated by the same feelings,

[009-045] follow the same pursuits, and be equally unworthy with the person to

[009-046] whom he thus gave his confidence?

[009-047] But Montraville was a different character: generous in his disposition,

[009-048] liberal in his opinions, and good-natured almost to a fault; yet eager

[009-049] and impetuous in the pursuit of a favorite object, he staid not to

[009-050] reflect on the consequence which might follow the attainment of his

[009-051] wishes; with a mind ever open to conviction, had he been so fortunate

[009-052] as to possess a friend who would have pointed out the cruelty of

[009-053] endeavouring to gain the heart of an innocent artless girl, when he

[009-054] knew it was utterly impossible for him to marry her, and when the

[009-055] gratification of his [[love|passion]] would be unavoidable infamy and [[distress|misery]] to

[009-056] her, and a cause of never-ceasing [[repentance|remorse]] to himself: had these dreadful

[009-057] consequences been placed before him in a proper light, the humanity of

[009-058] his nature would have urged him to give up the pursuit: but Belcour

[009-059] was not this friend; he rather encouraged the growing [[love|passion]] of

[009-060] Montraville; and being pleased with the vivacity of Mademoiselle,

[009-061] resolved to leave no argument untried, which he thought might prevail on

[009-062] her to be the companion of their intended voyage; and he made no doubt

[009-063] but her example, added to the rhetoric of Montraville, would persuade

[009-064] Charlotte to go with them.

[009-065] Charlotte had, when she went out to meet Montraville, flattered herself

[009-066] that her resolution was not to be shaken, and that, conscious of the

[009-067] impropriety of her conduct in having a clandestine intercourse with a

[009-068] stranger, she would never repeat the indiscretion.

[009-069] But alas! poor Charlotte, she knew not the [[deceit|deceitfulness]] of her own

[009-070] heart, or she would have avoided the [[adversity|trial]] of her stability.

[009-071] Montraville was tender, eloquent, ardent, and yet respectful. “Shall I

[009-072] not see you once more,” said he, “before I leave [[england|England]]? will you not

[009-073] bless me by an assurance, that when we are divided by a vast expanse of

[009-074] sea I shall not be forgotten?”

[009-075] Charlotte sighed.

[009-076] “Why that sigh, my dear Charlotte? could I flatter myself that a fear

[009-077] for my safety, or a wish for my welfare occasioned it, how [[happiness|happy]] would

[009-078] it make me.”

[009-079] “I shall ever wish you well, Montraville,” said she; “but we must meet

[009-080] no more.” “Oh say not so, my lovely girl: reflect, that when I leave my

[009-081] native land, perhaps a few short weeks may terminate my existence; the

[009-082] perils of the ocean--the dangers of war--”

[009-083] “I can hear no more,” said Charlotte in a tremulous voice. “I must leave

[009-084] you.”

[009-085] “Say you will see me once again.”

[009-086] “I dare not,” said she.

[009-087] “Only for one half hour to-morrow evening: 'tis my last request. I shall

[009-088] never trouble you again, Charlotte.”

[009-089] “I know not what to say,” cried Charlotte, struggling to draw her hands

[009-090] from him: “let me leave you now.”

[009-091] “And you will come to-morrow,” said Montraville.

[009-092] “Perhaps I may,” said she.

[009-093] “Adieu then. I will live upon that hope till we meet again.”

[009-094] He kissed her hand. She sighed an adieu, and catching hold of

[009-095] Mademoiselle's arm, hastily entered the garden gate.
