# Revenge

The [[passion|desire]] to inflict harm in return for a perceived injury — in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* a vice associated with the novel's libertine male [[reputation|character]]s, whose readiness to seek revenge transforms personal slights into catastrophic consequences for the innocent.

## Definition

Rowson presents revenge as a [[reputation|character]]istic vice of the libertine — a [[love|passion]] that, unchecked by moral scruple, drives men like [[belcour|Belcour]] to destroy others in response to wounded pride. When [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] refuses [[belcour|Belcour]]'s advances, "pride whispered" revenge and he resolved to punish her for the "affront" ([008-082]). This framing is significant: revenge in the novel is not about justice but about pride. It is the response of a man whose ego has been wounded, and it operates through the same machinery as seduction — [[deceit|deception]], manipulation, and the exploitation of [[weakness|vulnerability]]. The novel's treatment of revenge is thus gendered: revenge is a male vice that weaponizes the very [[trust]] and dependence that the culture of sensibility fostered in women.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[belcour]]: The novel's primary agent of revenge — his wounded pride at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s refusal drives him to deceive [[montraville|Montraville]] into believing [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] un[[religion|faith]]ful, setting the final tragedy in motion ([008-082], [020-038])
- [[montraville]]: His [[reason|sense]] of having been wronged by [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] (a false belief manufactured by [[belcour|Belcour]]) leads him to abandon her — his revenge, though misinformed, is no less destructive ([020-045])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Seeks revenge against [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] for perceived slights and jealousy — her cruelty in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s final days is motivated by vindictiveness

## Narrative Function

Revenge is the accelerant that turns seduction's slow-burning tragedy into catastrophe. [[belcour|Belcour]]'s revenge plot — convincing [[montraville|Montraville]] that [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has been un[[religion|faith]]ful — transforms what might have been a story of gradual estrangement into one of sudden, violent abandonment. Rowson uses revenge to show that the libertine code of [[honour|honor]] is not only morally bankrupt but actively destructive: the "slight" that [[belcour|Belcour]] revenges was nothing more than [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s refusal to betray [[montraville|Montraville]]. The novel thus exposes revenge as a [[love|passion]] that punishes virtue while claiming to defend [[honour|honor]].

## Related Concepts

- [[passion]]: The category of [[sensibility|feeling]] to which revenge belongs — an overpowering emotion that suspends moral [[reason|judgment]]
- [[pride]]: The psychological root of revenge in the novel's male [[reputation|character]]s — revenge is pride's response to injury
- [[ruin]]: The consequence toward which revenge drives — [[belcour|Belcour]]'s revenge is the proximate cause of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s final ruin
- [[deceit]]: The instrument of revenge — [[belcour|Belcour]] achieves his revenge through a lie about [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s fidelity
