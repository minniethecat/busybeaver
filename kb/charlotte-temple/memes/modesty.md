Modesty in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) is the behavioural expression of female virtue — the visible sign that a [[youth|young]] woman possesses the inner discipline to resist seduction. Rowson treats modesty as both [[nature|innate]] and cultivated: it is "that [[nature|innate]] modesty, which nature has implanted in the female breast" ([024-095]), yet it requires the reinforcement of education and social expectation. The novel charts modesty's erosion as a proxy for moral decline: [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s "blushing modesty and unaffected simplicity" ([016-008]) mark her initial virtue, while her eventual loss of these qualities signals her [[ruin|fall]].

## Definition

Rowson presents modesty as a compound of physical bearing and moral disposition. The "modesty of her gait" and the blush that accompanies self-consciousness are bodily signs of an inner moral state ([008-049]). Modesty is distinguished from mere timidity: it is an active virtue, a self-regulation that governs "what a strict [[reason|prudence]] and a becoming modesty would permit" ([023-010]). The novel's treatment of modesty is gendered — it is specifically a female virtue, the protective counterpart to male honour. When modesty is lost, Rowson suggests, the [[youth|young]] woman has lost her most essential defence against the predatory world.

## Key Passages

> "The modesty of her gait; her handmaids are sweetness, simplicity, and truth" — the narrator's idealized portrait of female virtue ([008-049])

> "The blushing modesty and unaffected simplicity of Charlotte" — Charlotte as she first appears to Montraville and his companions ([016-008])

> "What a strict prudence and a becoming modesty would permit" — the standard against which female conduct is measured ([023-010])

> "That innate modesty, which nature has implanted in the female breast" — the narrator on modesty's natural foundation ([024-095])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Modesty's trajectory from possession to loss. Her early "blushing modesty" ([016-008]) signals her [[nature|natural]] virtue; her gradual loss of it tracks her moral descent. Her recovery of shame in the novel's final chapters represents a partial restoration of the modesty she forfeited.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Modesty's antithesis. [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] has not merely lost modesty — she actively despises it, and her campaign to corrupt [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is a campaign against modesty itself.
- [[julia-franklin]]: Modesty preserved. Julia's possession of "a becoming modesty" serves as the novel's positive example — the woman whose modesty protects her from the fate that consumed [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]].

## Narrative Function

Modesty functions as the novel's behavioural barometer. When [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] blushes, hesitates, and defers, the reader is assured of her virtue; when she ceases to do so, her [[ruin|fall]] is confirmed. Rowson thus deploys modesty as a readable moral index, allowing the novel's didactic project to operate through description rather than overt moralizing. The concept also structures the novel's gender pedagogy: female readers are taught that modesty is their best protection, and that its loss — even through naivety rather than vice — is the first step toward ruin.

## Related Concepts

- [[prudence]]: Modesty's active partner. "Strict [[reason|prudence]] and a becoming modesty" ([023-010]) together constitute the ideal of feminine conduct.
- [[shame]]: The emotion that modesty, when violated, produces. [[shame|Shame]] is the evidence that modesty once existed and was lost.
