Deceit in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791) operates on two levels: the deliberate schemes of [[seduction|seducer]]s and the unconscious self-deceptions of the vulnerable. Rowson's most striking contribution to the theme is her insistence that "the heart is deceitful" ([024-067]) — that deceit is not only a weapon of villains but a universal human liability. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] is deceived by [[montraville|Montraville]] and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], but she is also deceived by her own heart, which tells her that clandestine meetings with a stranger are harmless. Rowson's treatment of deceit thus moves beyond simple villainy to a more sophisticated moral psychology.

## Definition

Rowson distinguishes between external deceit — the manipulative lies of [[seduction|seducer]]s — and internal deceit, or "the deceitfulness of her own heart" ([009-069]). The narrator's parenthetical observation that "poor [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], she knew not the deceitfulness of her own heart" ([009-069]) is the novel's key theoretical statement: innocence is vulnerable not only to others' wickedness but to its own capacity for self-deception. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s confession that "the heart is deceitful" ([024-067]) extends this diagnosis to the [[seduction|seducer]] himself, whose self-justifying rationalizations are a form of deceit no less damaging than [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s overt machinations.

## Key Passages

> "Poor Charlotte, she knew not the deceitfulness of her own heart, or she would have avoided the trial of her stability" ([009-069])

> "Would not the poor sailor, tost on a tempestuous ocean... gladly return to the shore he had left to trust to its deceitful calm?" — Charlotte's metaphor for her own situation ([021-069])

> "But the heart is deceitful, and I now can plainly discriminate between the impulse of a youthful passion, and the pure flame of disinterested affection" — Montraville's self-excusing confession ([024-067])

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: Deceit's primary victim and unwitting practitioner. She is deceived by [[montraville|Montraville]]'s tender eloquence and [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s counsel, but Rowson insists she is also deceived by "the deceitfulness of her own heart" ([009-069]), making her [[ruin|fall]] a compound of external manipulation and internal weakness.
- [[montraville]]: Deceit as self-justification. His belated recognition that "the heart is deceitful" ([024-067]) comes in the act of abandoning [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] for [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]], making his moral insight indistinguishable from his moral failure.
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Deceit as deliberate instrument. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s self-deception or [[montraville|Montraville]]'s rationalization, [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s deceit is calculated — she knowingly manipulates [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] toward ruin while presenting herself as a [[friendship|friend]].

## Narrative Function

Deceit is the mechanism by which the novel's tragedy unfolds. Rowson uses the dual nature of deceit — external manipulation and internal self-deception — to distribute responsibility across her cast: [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] is culpable for her schemes, [[montraville|Montraville]] for his rationalizations, and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] for her failure to know her own heart. The concept also serves Rowson's didactic purpose: by showing how "the heart is deceitful," she warns her readers — particularly [[youth|young]] female readers — to dis[[trust]] their own [[passion|desire]]s as much as they dis[[trust]] the blandishments of men.
