Sorrow, in Susanna Rowson's *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]* (1791), is not merely an emotion but a narrative engine and a moral signal. It is at once the proof of virtue — the [[virtue|virtuous]] feel [[grief|sorrow]] for the [[distress|suffering]] of others — and the instrument of moral education — [[grief|sorrow]] teaches what pleasure cannot. Rowson's novel, like the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al tradition it belongs to, is organized around the production and management of [[grief|sorrow]]: the reader is meant to weep, and through weeping, to learn.

## Definition

Rowson defines [[grief|sorrow]] as the [[nature|natural]] and proper response to loss, injustice, and the [[distress|suffering]] of the innocent. It is distinguished from mere unhappiness by its moral dimension: [[grief|sorrow]] is what the [[virtue|virtuous]] feel when confronted with the world's pain. "Sorrow had nipped the rose in her cheek before it was half blown" ([002-046]) — the image of premature blight captures [[grief|sorrow]]'s transformative power, its capacity to mark the body as well as the soul. The novel distinguishes between productive [[grief|sorrow]] (which leads to repentance, [[charity|compassion]], and moral growth) and destructive [[grief|sorrow]] (which overwhelms and paralyzes). The narrator's repeated addresses to the reader — "Then once more read over the [[grief|sorrow]]s of poor Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]], and remember, the Father of the universe is just" ([014-086]) — frame [[grief|sorrow]] as the medium of both moral and theological [[education|instruction]].

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's principal object of sympathetic [[grief|sorrow]]. Her [[distress|suffering]] is rendered in exquisite detail to produce the reader's emotional engagement. "Who can form an adequate idea of the [[grief|sorrow]] that preyed upon the mind of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]?" ([018-038]) — the question is rhetorical, inviting the reader to attempt precisely that imaginative act.
- [[mr-temple]]: His [[grief|sorrow]] at his [[family]]'s [[ruin|destruction]] is the novel's most powerful image of paternal grief. "The large drops of [[grief|sorrow]] rolled silently down his cheeks" ([013-100]) — his silent weeping exemplifies the masculine [[grief|sorrow]] that Rowson presents as more dignified and profound than theatrical [[grief|lamentation]].
- [[mrs-temple]]: Her [[grief|sorrow]]s receive extended treatment in the narrator's direct address to the reader ([014-086]). She represents maternal [[grief|sorrow]] in its most acute form — the grief of a mother who has lost her children to death and [[shame|disgrace]].
- [[montraville]]: His [[grief|sorrow]] after [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s death — "[[grief|sorrow]] and deep regret have taken their place" ([030-026]) — represents the redemptive potential of grief. His genuine [[grief|sorrow]] atones, in part, for his wrongs.

## Narrative Function

Sorrow is the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's primary affective tool. Rowson uses it to create the sympathetic identification between reader and [[reputation|character]] that is the mechanism of her moral [[education|instruction]]. By making the reader feel [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[grief|sorrow]], Rowson makes the reader absorb her warning: this could be you. The novel's famous closing address — "and may you, my dear [[youth|young]] readers, never [[experience]] the [[grief|sorrow]]s you have wept for in the history of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]" — makes explicit what the entire narrative has implied: [[grief|sorrow]], [[experience]]d vicariously, is the novel's gift to its audience, a vaccination against the real [[distress|suffering]] that follows a single false step.

## Related Concepts

- [[suffering]]: The physical and material dimension of [[grief|sorrow]]. Sorrow is [[distress|suffering]] [[experience]]d and expressed as emotion.
- [[repentance]]: Sorrow for one's own sins, distinguished from [[grief|sorrow]] for another's [[distress|suffering]]. The novel insists on the importance of both.
- [[compassion]]: The [[grief|sorrow]] one feels for another's pain — the virtue that Rowson's novel is designed to cultivate in its readers.
- [[melancholy]]: A more diffuse, less morally defined form of [[grief|sorrow]]. [[melancholy|Melancholy]] is a temperament; [[grief|sorrow]] is a response.
