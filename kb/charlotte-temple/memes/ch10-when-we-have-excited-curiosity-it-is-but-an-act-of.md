[010-001] MONTRAVILLE was the youngest son of a gentleman of [[fate|fortune]], whose

[010-002] [[family]] being numerous, he was obliged to bring up his sons to genteel

[010-003] professions, by the exercise of which they might hope to raise

[010-004] themselves into notice.

[010-005] “My daughters,” said he, “have been educated like gentlewomen; and

[010-006] should I die before they are settled, they must have some provision

[010-007] made, to place them above the snares and temptations which vice ever

[010-008] holds out to the elegant, accomplished female, when oppressed by the

[010-009] frowns of poverty and the sting of dependance: my boys, with only

[010-010] moderate incomes, when placed in the church, at the bar, or in the

[010-011] field, may exert their talents, make themselves [[friendship|friends]], and raise their

[010-012] fortunes on the basis of merit.”

[010-013] When [[montraville|Montraville]] chose the profession of arms, his father presented him

[010-014] with a commission, and made him a handsome provision for his private

[010-015] purse. “Now, my boy,” said he, “go! seek glory in the field of battle.

[010-016] You have received from me all I shall ever have it in my power to

[010-017] bestow: it is certain I have interest to gain you promotion; but be

[010-018] assured that interest shall never be exerted, unless by your future

[010-019] conduct you deserve it. Remember, therefore, your success in life

[010-020] depends entirely on yourself. There is one thing I think it my duty to

[010-021] [[prudence|caution]] you against; the precipitancy with which [[youth|young]] men frequently

[010-022] rush into matrimonial engagements, and by their thoughtlessness draw

[010-023] many a deserving woman into scenes of poverty and distress. A soldier

[010-024] has no business to think of a wife till his rank is such as to place him

[010-025] above the fear of bringing into the world a train of helpless innocents,

[010-026] heirs only to [[poverty|penury]] and [[adversity|affliction]]. If, indeed, a woman, whose [[wealth|fortune]]

[010-027] is sufficient to preserve you in that state of independence I would

[010-028] teach you to prize, should generously bestow herself on a young soldier,

[010-029] whose chief hope of future prosperity depended on his success in the

[010-030] field--if such a woman should offer--every barrier is removed, and I

[010-031] should rejoice in an union which would promise so much [[happiness|felicity]]. But

[010-032] mark me, boy, if, on the contrary, you rush into a precipitate union

[010-033] with a girl of little or no fortune, take the poor creature from a

[010-034] comfortable home and kind friends, and plunge her into all the evils

[010-035] a narrow income and increasing family can inflict, I will leave you to

[010-036] enjoy the blessed fruits of your rashness; for by all that is sacred,

[010-037] neither my interest or fortune shall ever be exerted in your favour. I

[010-038] am serious,” continued he, “therefore imprint this conversation on your

[010-039] memory, and let it influence your future conduct. Your happiness will

[010-040] always be dear to me; and I wish to warn you of a rock on which the

[010-041] peace of many an honest fellow has been wrecked; for believe me, the

[010-042] difficulties and dangers of the longest winter campaign are much easier

[010-043] to be borne, than the pangs that would seize your heart, when you beheld

[010-044] the woman of your choice, the children of your [[love|affection]], involved

[010-045] in penury and distress, and reflected that it was your own folly and

[010-046] precipitancy had been the prime cause of their [[suffering|sufferings]].”

[010-047] As this conversation passed but a few hours before Montraville took

[010-048] leave of his father, it was deeply impressed on his mind: when,

[010-049] therefore, [[belcour|Belcour]] came with him to the place of assignation with

[010-050] [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], he directed him to enquire of the French woman what were Miss

[010-051] [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s [[hope|expectations]] in regard to fortune.

[010-052] Mademoiselle informed him, that though Charlotte's father possessed a

[010-053] [[independence|genteel independence]], it was by no means probable that he could give his

[010-054] daughter more than a thousand pounds; and in case she did not marry to

[010-055] his liking, it was possible he might not give her a single SOUS; nor

[010-056] did it appear the least likely, that Mr. Temple would agree to her union

[010-057] with a young man on the point of embarking for the feat of war.

[010-058] Montraville therefore concluded it was impossible he should ever marry

[010-059] Charlotte Temple; and what end he proposed to himself by continuing the

[010-060] acquaintance he had commenced with her, he did not at that moment give

[010-061] himself time to enquire.
