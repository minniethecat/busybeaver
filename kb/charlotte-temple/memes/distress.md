# Distress

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, distress is the outward manifestation of inward suffering — the tears, fainting, and visible anguish that mark the novel's pivotal scenes. Rowson deploys distress as a moral signal: it summons the [[charity|compassion]] of [[virtue|virtuous]] [[reputation|character]]s ([[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], the clergyman), exposes the cruelty of vicious ones ([[belcour|Belcour]], [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]), and serves as the emotional currency of the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's appeal to readerly sympathy.

## Definition

Distress in Rowson's usage is both an emotional state and a social condition. A person "in distress" is one whose suffering is visible and acute — [[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]] in prison, [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] abandoned and [[death|dying]]. The novel repeatedly commands its readers to "relieve the distressed" ([008-064]), making distress a call to moral action. Crucially, Rowson distinguishes between distress that evokes genuine [[charity|compassion]] ([[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s real suffering) and "affected" distress that manipulates ([[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]]'s performances). The ability to distinguish between them is a mark of moral discernment.

## Key Passages

> "…prevented my feeling my distress so acutely as I should otherwise have done" ([003-092])

[[captain-eldridge|Captain Eldridge]]'s narrative reveals how [[religion|faith]] and duty can moderate even extreme distress — a model for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s later failure to find similar [[comfort|consolation]].

> "To relieve the distressed, redress the injured" ([008-064])

The narrator's direct statement of the novel's moral purpose: distress is not merely to be observed but actively relieved.

> "…her hands in an agony of distress, fell into strong hysterics." ([014-019])

Mrs. [[mr-temple|Temple]]'s physical collapse upon learning of [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s elopement — distress as the body's involuntary testimony to moral injury.

> "…the distressed Charlotte" ([028-080])

[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] has become defined by her distress — it is now her primary identity, the adjective that precedes her name.

> "…performed her duty towards a distressed fellow-creature." ([033-044])

The novel's moral ideal: responding to another's distress is not optional kindness but duty.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- [[charlotte-temple]]: The novel's archetypal figure of distress — her suffering escalates from emotional anguish to physical [[poverty|destitution]], culminating in death ([028-080])
- [[mrs-beauchamp]]: The model responder to distress — she sees [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s suffering and acts, without regard to reputation or inconvenience ([033-044])
- [[mademoiselle-la-rue]]: Uses "affected" (feigned) distress as manipulation — her counterfeit suffering contrasts with [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s genuine anguish ([016-049])
- [[mr-temple]]: His distress at [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s [[ruin|fall]] is paternal and redemptive — it leads not to rejection but to the desperate search for his daughter
- [[belcour]]: Refuses to respond to distress — his cruelty is defined by his indifference to [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s visible suffering
- [[captain-eldridge]]: His earlier distress in prison establishes the novel's template: [[virtue|virtuous]] suffering that evokes [[charity|compassion]]ate response ([003-092])

## Narrative Function

Distress is the [[sensibility|sentiment]]al novel's primary emotional technology. Rowson uses it to train readers in sympathy: each scene of distress invites the reader to practice the [[charity|compassion]] that the novel preaches. Within the plot, distress functions as both consequence (of seduction) and test (of other [[reputation|character]]s' virtue). [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s progressive distress — from [[fear|anxiety]] to tears to fainting to death — maps her moral descent and creates the emotional arc that gives the novel its didactic power. The [[comfort|relief]] of distress, performed by [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]], the clergyman, and ultimately the [[mr-temple|Temple]]s, enacts the novel's vision of a moral community bound by mutual [[charity|compassion]].
