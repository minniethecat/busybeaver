# Passion

The force that overthrows every defence: in *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, [[love|passion]] names the ungoverned emotional and erotic energies that drive the novel's catastrophe — operating in [[seduction|seducer]] and [[seduction|seduce]]d alike, [[love|passion]] is what reason cannot contain and what virtue cannot survive.

## Definition

In *[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]*, [[love|passion]] denotes powerful, unregulated emotion — particularly sexual desire and romantic attachment — that operates in opposition to reason, duty, and religious principle. The novel inherits the eighteenth-century opposition between [[love|passion]] and reason, but Rowson's treatment is distinctively gendered: male [[love|passion]] drives the action of seduction, while female [[love|passion]] (or the susceptibility to it) is the [[weakness|vulnerability]] that seduction exploits.

The novel's villains are [[reputation|character]]ised by [[love|passion]]'s dominance over their other faculties. Lewis's French schoolfellow is introduced as a man of "passions impetuous" (005-016), and it is precisely this lack of emotional self-governance that enables his villainy. [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[love|passion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] — at first presented as [[affection|love]] — reveals itself as "licentious [[love|passion]]" (016-086) when stripped of its romantic pretext. The language is explicit: [[love|passion]] in its unregulated form is not [[affection|love]] but appetite, and its gratification brings not happiness but "unavoidable infamy and [[distress|misery]]" (009-055).

Yet the novel does not simply condemn [[love|passion]]. [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s capacity for [[love|passion]]ate [[sensibility|feeling]] is inseparable from her sensibility — the same emotional depth that makes her morally admirable also makes her vulnerable. The narrator acknowledges "the growing [[love|passion]]" (009-059) that [[montraville|Montraville]] cultivates in [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], and [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s own retrospective analysis recognises that "powerful as that [[love|passion]] is when operating in a [[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-026), it would not have conquered her [[duty|filial duty]] without the corrupting influence of others. Passion is dangerous but not inherently evil; it becomes destructive when it operates without the governance of reason, [[reason|prudence]], and religious principle.

The novel's treatment of [[love|passion]] also extends to [[reputation|character]]s who are not directly involved in the central seduction plot. The "[[charity|compassion]]ate" response of one [[reputation|character]] (017-069) demonstrates that [[love|passion]] can take morally admirable forms — the word "[[charity|compassion]]" itself contains "[[love|passion]]," suggesting that [[sensibility|feeling]] *with* another is a species of [[love|passion]] that virtue requires.

## Key Passages

> "villain, he avowed his passion for Lucy; declared her situation" (003-071)

The framing of [[love|passion]]'s declaration as an act of villainy is significant: the avowal of [[love|passion]] is here not romantic but predatory. The speaker is identified as a "villain" before his [[love|passion]] is named, so that the reader understands from the outset that this [[love|passion]] is not [[affection|love]] but an instrument of harm.

> "her mind uncultivated, her heart unfeeling, her passions impetuous, and" (005-016)

The catalogue structure links impetuous [[love|passion]] with an uncultivated mind and an un[[sensibility|feeling]] heart. Passion without cultivation — without the discipline of education and moral formation — is dangerous. The passage implies a connection between intellectual cultivation and emotional self-governance that is central to the novel's educational project.

> "gratification of his passion would be unavoidable infamy and misery to" (009-055)

The word "gratification" reduces [[love|passion]] to appetite, and the consequence — "unavoidable infamy and [[distress|misery]]" — strips away any romantic justification. The novel insists that [[love|passion]], when gratified at the expense of virtue, produces only [[ruin|destruction]] for its object. This is the novel's central warning to its female readers.

> "awaken a passion in his bosom that might turn out to her advantage" (016-053)

This passage reveals the calculated use of [[love|passion]] as a strategy. The [[reputation|character]] considers awakening [[love|passion]] not as an end in itself but as a means to advantage — a transactional view of emotional manipulation that the novel condemns. Passion here is not spontaneous [[sensibility|feeling]] but a tool to be deployed.

> "licentious passion; and should the unhappy creature shrink from the" (016-086)

The adjective "licentious" strips [[love|passion]] of any romantic covering and names it for what it is: unregulated sexual desire that respects no moral boundary. The phrase "un[[happiness|happy]] creature" for its object emphasises the asymmetry of power and the [[ruin|destruction]] that [[love|passion]]'s gratification leaves in its wake.

## Characters Who Embody / Challenge This Concept

- **[[charlotte-temple|Charlotte Temple]]**: The novel's study in the [[weakness|vulnerability]] to [[love|passion]]. Her "[[youth|young]] heart glowing with sensibility" (022-026) makes her susceptible to the [[love|passion]] [[montraville|Montraville]] cultivates in her. She is undone not by her own [[love|passion]]ate nature alone but by the combination of her emotional openness and the predatory [[love|passion]] of others.
- **[[montraville|Montraville]]**: Embodies male [[love|passion]] in its most destructive form — a [[love|passion]] that is genuine enough to produce [[regret|remorse]] but too weak to govern itself. His [[love|passion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] moves from romantic pursuit to "licentious [[love|passion]]" (016-086) to [[guilt|guilty]] abandonment, tracing the arc of [[love|passion]]'s moral failure.
- **[[mademoiselle-la-rue|Mademoiselle La Rue]]**: Represents [[love|passion]]'s corruption when it becomes wholly instrumental. Unlike [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], who feels deeply, [[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]] uses the language of [[love|passion]] while remaining emotionally detached, exploiting the passions of others — [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s romantic susceptibility, [[montraville|Montraville]]'s desire — for her own advancement.
- **Lewis's schoolfellow ([[belcour|Belcour]])**: The novel's most thoroughgoing portrait of [[love|passion]] as vice. His "passions impetuous" (005-016) know no check, and his actions demonstrate the devastation that [[love|passion]] without conscience produces.

## Narrative Function

Passion is the engine of the novel's plot. Without [[montraville|Montraville]]'s [[love|passion]] for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], there is no seduction; without [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]]'s susceptibility to [[love|passion]], there is no [[ruin|fall]]; without the "turbulence of his passions" (024-090), there is no abandonment. Passion drives every major turn of the narrative, and the novel's moral project is fundamentally a project of [[love|passion]]-management: teaching [[youth|young]] women to recognise, resist, and govern the passions that would destroy them.

The concept also structures the novel's moral typology. Characters are distinguished not by whether they feel [[love|passion]] — nearly everyone does — but by how they relate to it. The [[virtue|virtuous]] ([[mr-temple|Mr. Temple]], [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]) govern their passions through reason and principle. The vicious ([[mademoiselle-la-rue|La Rue]], [[belcour|Belcour]]) are governed *by* their passions, using them as instruments of predation. The tragic ([[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], [[montraville|Montraville]]) are those in whom [[love|passion]] and conscience contend, with [[love|passion]] ultimately prevailing.

This typology serves Rowson's didactic purpose. By showing [[love|passion]]'s power over even the well-intentioned, she warns that good intentions are insufficient protection; only the active cultivation of reason, religion, and [[reason|prudence]] can guard against [[love|passion]]'s force.

## Related Concepts

- [[love]]: Passion's morally ambivalent counterpart; [[affection|love]] may be a species of [[love|passion]], but [[love|passion]] in its destructive form is [[affection|love]] stripped of duty, constancy, and [[duty|moral obligation]]
- [[virtue]]: The force that [[love|passion]] overthrows; virtue's central task is the governance of [[love|passion]] through reason and religious principle
- [[seduction]]: The social process through which [[love|passion]] operates; seduction is [[love|passion]] given method and strategy
- [[duty]]: The counterforce to [[love|passion]]; filial, religious, and social [[duty|duties]] are the restraints that [[love|passion]] strains against and, when strong enough, breaks
- [[sensibility]]: The emotional capacity that magnifies [[love|passion]]; a heart "glowing with sensibility" (022-026) feels [[love|passion]] more intensely and is therefore more vulnerable to its force
- [[folly]]: The failure of [[reason|judgment]] that allows [[love|passion]] to prevail; folly is what happens when [[love|passion]] overcomes [[reason|prudence]]
- [[ruin]]: The destination toward which unregulated [[love|passion]] drives; ruin is [[love|passion]]'s end point
