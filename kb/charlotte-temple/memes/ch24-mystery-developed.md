[024-001] UNFORTUNATELY for [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]], about three weeks before this unhappy

[024-002] rencontre, Captain [[mrs-beauchamp|Beauchamp]], being ordered to Rhode-Island, his lady

[024-003] had accompanied him, so that Charlotte was deprived of her [[friendship|friendly]]

[024-004] advice and consoling society. The afternoon on which [[montraville|Montraville]] had

[024-005] visited her she had found herself languid and fatigued, and after making

[024-006] a very slight dinner had lain down to endeavour to recruit her exhausted

[024-007] spirits, and, contrary to her [[hope|expectations]], had fallen asleep. She

[024-008] had not long been lain down, when [[belcour|Belcour]] arrived, for he took every

[024-009] opportunity of visiting her, and striving to awaken her [[anger|resentment]]

[024-010] against Montraville. He enquired of the servant where her mistress was,

[024-011] and being told she was asleep, took up a book to amuse himself: having

[024-012] sat a few minutes, he by chance cast his eyes towards the road, and saw

[024-013] Montraville approaching; he instantly conceived the diabolical scheme

[024-014] of ruining the unhappy Charlotte in his opinion for ever; he therefore

[024-015] stole softly up stairs, and laying himself by her side with the greatest

[024-016] precaution, for fear she should awake, was in that situation discovered

[024-017] by his credulous friend.

[024-018] When Montraville spurned the weeping Charlotte from him, and left her

[024-019] almost distracted with terror and despair, Belcour raised her from

[024-020] the floor, and leading her down stairs, assumed the part of a tender,

[024-021] consoling friend; she listened to the arguments he advanced with

[024-022] apparent composure; but this was only the calm of a moment: the

[024-023] remembrance of Montraville's recent cruelty again rushed upon her mind:

[024-024] she pushed him from her with some violence, and crying--“Leave me, Sir,

[024-025] I beseech you leave me, for much I fear you have been the cause of my

[024-026] fidelity being suspected; go, leave me to the accumulated miseries my

[024-027] own [[prudence|imprudence]] has brought upon me.”

[024-028] She then left him with precipitation, and retiring to her own apartment,

[024-029] threw herself on the bed, and gave vent to an [[suffering|agony]] of [[sorrow|grief]] which it is

[024-030] impossible to describe.

[024-031] It now occurred to Belcour that she might possibly write to Montraville,

[024-032] and endeavour to convince him of her innocence: he was well aware of her

[024-033] pathetic remonstrances, and, sensible of the tenderness of Montraville's

[024-034] heart, resolved to prevent any letters ever reaching him: he therefore

[024-035] called the servant, and, by the powerful persuasion of a bribe,

[024-036] prevailed with her to promise whatever letters her mistress might write

[024-037] should be sent to him. He then left a polite, tender note for Charlotte,

[024-038] and returned to [[new-york|New-York]]. His first business was to seek Montraville,

[024-039] and endeavour to convince him that what had happened would ultimately

[024-040] tend to his happiness: he found him in his apartment, solitary, pensive,

[024-041] and wrapped in disagreeable reflexions.

[024-042] “Why how now, whining, pining lover?” said he, clapping him on the

[024-043] shoulder. Montraville started; a momentary flush of resentment crossed

[024-044] his cheek, but instantly gave place to a death-like paleness, occasioned

[024-045] by painful remembrance remembrance awakened by that monitor, whom,

[024-046] though we may in vain endeavour, we can never entirely silence.

[024-047] “Belcour,” said he, “you have injured me in a tender point.” “Prithee,

[024-048] Jack,” replied Belcour, “do not make a serious matter of it: how could I

[024-049] refuse the girl's advances? and thank heaven she is not your wife.”

[024-050] “True,” said Montraville; “but she was innocent when I first knew her.

[024-051] It was I seduced her, Belcour. Had it not been for me, she had still

[024-052] been [[virtue|virtuous]] and [[happiness|happy]] in the [[love|affection]] and protection of her [[family]].”

[024-053] “Pshaw,” replied Belcour, laughing, “if you had not taken advantage of

[024-054] her easy nature, some other would, and where is the difference, pray?”

[024-055] “I wish I had never seen her,” cried he passionately, and starting from

[024-056] his seat. “Oh that cursed French woman,” added he with vehemence, “had

[024-057] it not been for her, I might have been happy--” He paused.

[024-058] “With [[julia-franklin|Julia Franklin]],” said Belcour. The name, like a sudden spark

[024-059] of electric fire, seemed for a moment to suspend his faculties--for a

[024-060] moment he was transfixed; but recovering, he caught Belcour's hand, and

[024-061] cried--“Stop! stop! I beseech you, name not the lovely Julia and

[024-062] the wretched Montraville in the same breath. I am a [[seduction|seducer]], a mean,

[024-063] ungenerous seducer of unsuspecting innocence. I dare not hope that

[024-064] [[innocence|purity]] like her's would stoop to unite itself with black, premeditated

[024-065] guilt: yet by heavens I swear, Belcour, I thought I loved the lost,

[024-066] abandoned Charlotte till I saw Julia--I thought I never could forsake

[024-067] her; but the heart is deceitful, and I now can plainly discriminate

[024-068] between the impulse of a youthful passion, and the pure flame of

[024-069] disinterested affection.”

[024-070] At that instant Julia [[mr-franklin|Franklin]] passed the window, leaning on her uncle's

[024-071] arm. She curtseyed as she passed, and, with the bewitching smile of

[024-072] [[modesty|modest]] cheerfulness, cried--“Do you bury yourselves in the house this

[024-073] fine evening, gents?” There was something in the voice! the manner! the

[024-074] look! that was altogether irresistible. “Perhaps she wishes my company,”

[024-075] said Montraville mentally, as he snatched up his hat: “if I thought she

[024-076] loved me, I would confess my errors, and [[trust]] to her [[benevolence|generosity]] to [[compassion|pity]]

[024-077] and [[forgiveness|pardon]] me.” He soon overtook her, and offering her his arm, they

[024-078] sauntered to pleasant but unfrequented walks. Belcour drew Mr. Franklin

[024-079] on one side and entered into a political discourse: they walked faster

[024-080] than the [[youth|young]] people, and Belcour by some means contrived entirely to

[024-081] lose sight of them. It was a fine evening in the beginning of autumn;

[024-082] the last remains of day-light faintly streaked the western sky, while

[024-083] the moon, with pale and virgin lustre in the room of gorgeous gold and

[024-084] purple, ornamented the canopy of heaven with silver, fleecy clouds,

[024-085] which now and then half hid her lovely face, and, by partly concealing,

[024-086] heightened every beauty; the zephyrs whispered softly through the trees,

[024-087] which now began to shed their leafy honours; a solemn silence reigned:

[024-088] and to a happy mind an evening such as this would give serenity, and

[024-089] calm, unruffled pleasure; but to Montraville, while it soothed

[024-090] the turbulence of his [[passion|passions]], it brought increase of melancholy

[024-091] reflections. Julia was leaning on his arm: he took her hand in his, and

[024-092] pressing it tenderly, sighed deeply, but continued silent. Julia was

[024-093] embarrassed; she wished to break a silence so unaccountable, but was

[024-094] unable; she loved Montraville, she saw he was unhappy, and wished to

[024-095] know the cause of his uneasiness, but that [[nature|innate]] modesty, which nature

[024-096] has implanted in the female breast, prevented her enquiring. “I am bad

[024-097] company, Miss Franklin,” said he, at last recollecting himself; “but

[024-098] I have met with something to-day that has greatly distressed me, and I

[024-099] cannot shake off the disagreeable impression it has made on my mind.”

[024-100] “I am sorry,” she replied, “that you have any cause of inquietude. I am

[024-101] sure if you were as happy as you deserve, and as all your friends wish

[024-102] you--” She hesitated. “And might I,” replied he with some animation,

[024-103] “presume to rank the amiable Julia in that number?”

[024-104] “Certainly,” said she, “the service you have rendered me, the knowledge

[024-105] of your worth, all combine to make me esteem you.”

[024-106] “Esteem, my lovely Julia,” said he passionately, “is but a poor cold

[024-107] word. I would if I dared, if I thought I merited your attention--but

[024-108] no, I must not--honour forbids. I am beneath your notice, Julia, I am

[024-109] miserable and cannot hope to be otherwise.” “Alas!” said Julia, “I [[compassion|pity]]

[024-110] you.”

[024-111] “Oh thou condescending charmer,” said he, “how that sweet word cheers my

[024-112] sad heart. Indeed if you knew all, you would pity; but at the same time

[024-113] I fear you would despise me.”

[024-114] Just then they were again joined by Mr. Franklin and Belcour. It had

[024-115] interrupted an interesting discourse. They found it impossible to

[024-116] converse on indifferent subjects, and proceeded home in silence. At

[024-117] Mr. Franklin's door Montraville again pressed Julia's hand, and faintly

[024-118] articulating “good night,” retired to his lodgings dispirited and

[024-119] wretched, from a consciousness that he deserved not the affection, with

[024-120] which he plainly saw he was honoured.
