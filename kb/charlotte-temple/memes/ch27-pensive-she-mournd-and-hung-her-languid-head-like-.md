[027-001] Pensive she mourn'd, and hung her languid head,

[027-002] Like a fair lily overcharg'd with dew.

[027-003] CHARLOTTE had now been left almost three months a prey to her own

[027-004] melancholy reflexions--sad companions indeed; nor did any one break in

[027-005] upon her solitude but [[belcour|Belcour]], who once or twice called to enquire after

[027-006] her health, and tell her he had in vain endeavoured to bring [[montraville|Montraville]]

[027-007] to hear reason; and once, but only once, was her mind cheered by the

[027-008] receipt of an affectionate letter from [[mrs-beauchamp|Mrs. Beauchamp]]. Often had she

[027-009] wrote to her perfidious [[seduction|seducer]], and with the most persuasive eloquence

[027-010] endeavoured to convince him of her innocence; but these letters were

[027-011] never suffered to reach the hands of Montraville, or they must, though

[027-012] on the very eve of marriage, have prevented his deserting the wretched

[027-013] girl. Real [[distress|anguish]] of heart had in a great measure faded her charms, her

[027-014] cheeks were pale from [[poverty|want]] of rest, and her eyes, by frequent, indeed

[027-015] almost continued weeping, were sunk and heavy. Sometimes a gleam of hope

[027-016] would play about her heart when she thought of her parents--“They cannot

[027-017] surely,” she would say, “refuse to forgive me; or should they deny their

[027-018] [[forgiveness|pardon]] to me, they win not hate my innocent infant on account of its

[027-019] mother's errors.” How often did the poor mourner wish for the consoling

[027-020] presence of the benevolent Mrs. Beauchamp.

[027-021] “If she were here,” she would cry, “she would certainly comfort me, and

[027-022] sooth the distraction of my soul.”

[027-023] She was sitting one afternoon, wrapped in these melancholy reflexions,

[027-024] when she was interrupted by the entrance of Belcour. Great as the

[027-025] alteration was which incessant [[melancholy|sorrow]] had made on her person, she was

[027-026] still interesting, still charming; and the unhallowed flame, which had

[027-027] urged Belcour to plant dissension between her and Montraville, still

[027-028] raged in his bosom: he was determined, if possible, to make her his

[027-029] mistress; nay, he had even conceived the diabolical scheme of taking her

[027-030] to [[new-york|New-York]], and making her appear in every public place where it was

[027-031] likely she should meet Montraville, that he might be a witness to his

[027-032] unmanly triumph.

[027-033] When he entered the room where [[charlotte-temple|Charlotte]] was sitting, he assumed

[027-034] the look of tender, consolatory friendship. “And how does my lovely

[027-035] Charlotte?” said he, taking her hand: “I fear you are not so well as I

[027-036] could wish.”

[027-037] “I am not well, Mr. Belcour,” said she, “very far from it; but the pains

[027-038] and infirmities of the body I could easily bear, nay, submit to them

[027-039] with patience, were they not aggravated by the most insupportable

[027-040] [[grief|anguish]] of my mind.”

[027-041] “You are not [[happiness|happy]], Charlotte,” said he, with a look of well-dissembled

[027-042] [[suffering|sorrow]].

[027-043] “Alas!” replied she mournfully, shaking her head, “how can I be happy,

[027-044] deserted and forsaken as I am, without a [[friendship|friend]] of my own sex to whom I

[027-045] can unburthen my full heart, nay, my fidelity suspected by the very man

[027-046] for whom I have sacrificed every thing valuable in life, for whom I have

[027-047] made myself a poor despised creature, an outcast from society, an object

[027-048] only of contempt and [[compassion|pity]].”

## Section 15

[027-049] “You think too meanly of yourself, Miss [[mr-temple|Temple]]: there is no one who

[027-050] would dare to treat you with contempt: all who have the pleasure of

[027-051] knowing you must admire and esteem. You are lonely here, my dear girl;

[027-052] give me leave to conduct you to New-York, where the agreeable society

[027-053] of some ladies, to whom I will introduce you, will dispel these sad

[027-054] thoughts, and I shall again see returning cheerfulness animate those

[027-055] lovely features.”

[027-056] “Oh never! never!” cried Charlotte, emphatically: “the [[virtue|virtuous]] part

[027-057] of my sex will scorn me, and I will never associate with infamy. No,

[027-058] Belcour, here let me hide my shame and sorrow, here let me spend my

[027-059] few remaining days in obscurity, unknown and unpitied, here let me die

[027-060] unlamented, and my name sink to oblivion.” Here her tears stopped her

[027-061] utterance. Belcour was awed to silence: he dared not interrupt her; and

[027-062] after a moment's pause she proceeded--“I once had conceived the

[027-063] thought of going to New-York to seek out the still dear, though cruel,

[027-064] ungenerous Montraville, to throw myself at his feet, and entreat his

[027-065] [[charity|compassion]]; heaven knows, not for myself; if I am no longer [[love|beloved]],

[027-066] I will not be indebted to his [[compassion|pity]] to redress my injuries, but I would

[027-067] have knelt and entreated him not to forsake my poor unborn--” She could

[027-068] say no more; a crimson glow rushed over her cheeks, and covering her

[027-069] face with her hands, she sobbed aloud.

[027-070] Something like humanity was awakened in Belcour's breast by this

[027-071] pathetic speech: he arose and walked towards the window; but the selfish

[027-072] passion which had taken possession of his heart, soon stifled these

[027-073] finer emotions; and he thought if Charlotte was once convinced she had

[027-074] no longer any dependance on Montraville, she would more readily throw

[027-075] herself on his protection. Determined, therefore, to inform her of all

[027-076] that had happened, he again resumed his seat; and finding she began to

[027-077] be more composed, enquired if she had ever heard from Montraville since

[027-078] the unfortunate recontre in her bed chamber.

[027-079] “Ah no,” said she. “I fear I shall never hear from him again.”

[027-080] “I am greatly of your opinion,” said Belcour, “for he has been for some

[027-081] time past greatly attached--”

[027-082] At the word “attached” a death-like paleness overspread the countenance

[027-083] of Charlotte, but she applied to some hartshorn which stood beside her,

[027-084] and Belcour proceeded.

[027-085] “He has been for some time past greatly attached to one [[julia-franklin|Miss Franklin]], a

[027-086] pleasing lively girl, with a large [[fate|fortune]].”

[027-087] “She may be richer, may be handsomer,” cried Charlotte, “but cannot love

[027-088] him so well. Oh may she beware of his art, and not [[trust]] him too far as

[027-089] I have done.”

[027-090] “He addresses her publicly,” said he, “and it was rumoured they were

[027-091] to be married before he sailed for Eustatia, whither his company is

[027-092] ordered.”

[027-093] “Belcour,” said Charlotte, seizing his hand, and gazing at him

[027-094] earnestly, while her pale lips trembled with convulsive agony, “tell me,

[027-095] and tell me truly, I beseech you, do you think he can be such a villain

[027-096] as to marry another woman, and leave me to die with want and misery in

[027-097] a strange land: tell me what you think; I can bear it very well; I

[027-098] will not shrink from this heaviest stroke of fate; I have deserved my

[027-099] afflictions, and I will endeavour to bear them as I ought.”

[027-100] “I fear,” said Belcour, “he can be that villain.”

[027-101] “Perhaps,” cried she, eagerly interrupting him, “perhaps he is married

[027-102] already: come, let me know the worst,” continued she with an affected

[027-103] look of composure: “you need not be afraid, I shall not send the

[027-104] fortunate lady a bowl of poison.”

[027-105] “Well then, my dear girl,” said he, deceived by her appearance,

[027-106] “they were married on Thursday, and yesterday morning they sailed for

[027-107] Eustatia.”

[027-108] “Married--gone--say you?” cried she in a distracted accent, “what

[027-109] without a last farewell, without one thought on my unhappy situation!

[027-110] Oh Montraville, may God forgive your perfidy.” She shrieked, and Belcour

[027-111] sprang forward just in time to prevent her falling to the floor.

[027-112] Alarming faintings now succeeded each other, and she was conveyed to

[027-113] her bed, from whence she earnestly prayed she might never more arise.

[027-114] Belcour staid with her that night, and in the morning found her in a

[027-115] high fever. The fits she had been seized with had greatly terrified him;

[027-116] and confined as she now was to a bed of sickness, she was no longer an

[027-117] object of [[passion|desire]]: it is true for several days he went constantly to see

[027-118] her, but her pale, emaciated appearance disgusted him: his visits became

[027-119] less frequent; he forgot the solemn charge given him by Montraville; he

[027-120] even forgot the money entrusted to his care; and, the burning blush of

[027-121] [[anger|indignation]] and shame tinges my cheek while I write it, this [[shame|disgrace]] to

[027-122] humanity and manhood at length forgot even the injured Charlotte; and,

[027-123] attracted by the blooming health of a farmer's daughter, whom he had

[027-124] seen in his frequent excursions to the country, he left the unhappy girl

[027-125] to sink unnoticed to the grave, a prey to sickness, [[sorrow|grief]], and penury;

[027-126] while he, having triumphed over the virtue of the artless cottager,

[027-127] rioted in all the intemperance of luxury and lawless pleasure.
