# Prejudice

Prejudice in *Gallions Reach* operates through the quiet mechanisms of social exclusion — the cold glance, the sense of being a stranger, the invisible barriers that mark who belongs and who does not.

## In the Novel

The experience of being an outsider runs through the novel. Jim Colet, walking through the City, feels "he was a stranger there. They might think he had no business" [003-029] — prejudice as the internalized awareness of being judged, of not fitting the expected type. Later, at a gathering, he "felt himself other than an outsider, watching the show" [005-006] — the double remove of being outside even outsider status.

Social judgment is rendered through physical coldness. Mrs. Perriam's figure suggests she was "contemptuous of the world" [004-055], her posture an act of social exclusion made visible. The novel's characters move through spaces where they are perpetually assessed, found wanting, or simply ignored.

## Key Passages

> He was an outsider. Better be off. Most of these people had forgotten him. [004-047]

The flatness of the judgment is itself the point — prejudice does not announce itself with drama but with forgetting, with the quiet withdrawal of attention.

> He was a stranger there. They might think he had no business in that quarter. [003-029]

Prejudice here is anticipated rather than experienced — the fear of judgment shapes behavior before any word is spoken.
