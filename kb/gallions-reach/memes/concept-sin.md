## Overview

Sin in *Gallions Reach* is not primarily a matter of doctrine. Tomlinson uses the word to evoke worldly experience, the accumulation of human frailty, and a kind of knowing darkness that characters carry with them. It is less about guilt than about having seen and known too much.

> Gillespie shook his paw in appreciative warning over sin. (014-004)

## Sin as Experience

When Helen Denny wonders whether Jimmy is "as simple as a child, or was as experienced as sin" (004-004), the phrase captures the novel's peculiar treatment of the concept. Sin here is not transgression but *knowingness* — a deep familiarity with the world's darker corners that makes one "not particularly interested" in surface pleasures. Gillespie's "long familiarity with sin" (014-003) is presented with "composure and fond irrelevance," suggesting a comfortable, almost affectionate relationship with human failing.

> He indulged in illustrative cases with composure and fond irrelevance. (014-003)

## The Film of the Past

The novel's most haunting image of sin is geological: "they had crawled under the film of sin and night which the past had left on the earth" (013-010). Sin becomes a residue, a layer deposited by history itself, under which humanity hides from an impending judgment. It is not individual but collective — the accumulated darkness of all that has come before.

> They had crawled under the film of sin and night which the past had left on the earth. (013-010)
