# Rangoon

Rangoon (present-day Yangon) is the port city that serves as the *Altair*'s eastern destination and the threshold through which Jim Colet first encounters Asia directly. It is mentioned as early as Chapter 2 — "as familiar a word as Colombo, Rangoon, Penang, Borneo, or China" (002-004) — placing it within the mental geography of imperial commerce that structures the novel's world. When the characters finally arrive there in Chapters 21–22, the city is not what anyone expected.

## Location & Context

Rangoon was the capital of British Burma in the 1920s, a major port on the Irrawaddy Delta and a node in the colonial shipping network that linked Calcutta, Singapore, Penang, and points east. For the crew and passengers of the *Altair*, it represents the farthest reach of their voyage — the place where the Indian Ocean gives way to the archipelagic East and where British colonial order begins to fray at the edges.

## Role in the Narrative

Rangoon haunts the novel long before it appears. In Chapter 21, the question "Do you know Rangoon?" is asked repeatedly (021-027, 021-047, 021-051), each repetition sharpening the anxiety that Colet — who does not know Rangoon, who has no connections there — is heading toward a place for which he is utterly unprepared. "They ought not to land you at Rangoon and just leave it at that" (021-022), someone says, and the remark captures the city's dual nature: it is both a destination and an abandonment.

The arrival itself is anticlimactic and unsettling in equal measure. "They arrived at Rangoon before the day had begun" (022-001) — the dawn arrival lending the scene a ghostly quality. "Rangoon was not the expected vision of the East" (022-006), the narrator observes. The "brief glimpse of the East at Rangoon had not been disturbing only" (022-016) — a careful double negative that captures the city's effect on Colet: it disturbs, but it also fascinates, and the two responses are inseparable.

After Rangoon, the coaster *Nibong* puts out for Penang (022-014), and Colet is aboard — the city having served its function as the hinge between the deep-sea voyage and the coastal sequel.

## Atmosphere & Description

Tomlinson's Rangoon is defined by what it is not. "Not the expected vision of the East" (022-006) — the negation itself is the description. The city arrives at dawn, before the day has properly begun, its outlines uncertain. Colet's "brief glimpse" (022-016) is both too short and too much; the city refuses to resolve into a single impression. This is consistent with the novel's broader treatment of travel as disorientation: places do not reveal themselves to the newcomer, they only unsettle him.

## Associated Characters

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — Arrives at Rangoon as a stranger, with no connections and no clear plan; his glimpse of the city is brief but disorienting.
- **[[norrie]]** — Accompanies Colet into Rangoon; the two of them "went off" into the city together (022-006).
- **[[penang]]** — The next destination after Rangoon; the coaster Nibong connects the two ports.
- **[[the-altair|The Altair]]** — The ship whose voyage terminates (or transforms) at Rangoon.

## Symbolic Meaning

Rangoon is the novel's threshold between the known and the unknown. London was familiar, the ship was a contained world, but Rangoon is the East made actual — not the East of imperial romance or boyhood fantasy, but a real city that does not match expectation, that disturbs even as it fascinates. The repeated question "Do you know Rangoon?" is not merely practical; it is existential. To know Rangoon is to have crossed a line that Colet has only just reached.
