# Mr. Collins

Mr. Collins is the second officer of the *Altair*, a junior figure in [[the-altair|the ship]]'s hierarchy who emerges from the margins during the novel's crisis chapters. Unlike the theatrical [[sinclair|Sinclair]] or the gruff [[gillespie|Gillespie]], Collins is defined by [[silence|quiet]] competence and an almost anonymous professionalism. He is first introduced with precise temporal notation: "just struck eight bells when Collins, the second officer, put his head in at the chart-room door" (018-028).

## Role in the Novel

Collins's function is that of the reliable subordinate. In [[the-altair|the ship]]'s hierarchy, he stands below [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] and Chief Officer [[sinclair|Sinclair]], yet above the crew. His rank is specific — "the second officer" — and his actions are correspondingly precise. He "put his head in at the chart-room door" (018-028), the gesture itself a deferential acknowledgment of hierarchy: he does not enter without permission.

In [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|the shipwreck]], Collins is given command of a boat: "Your boat, Collins, keep her away" (018-063). The order is terse, Collins's response is not recorded — the novel's treatment of him mirrors his professional reticence. He does his duty without commentary, and his silence is not absence but a kind of presence that requires no amplification.

In the lifeboat, "Collins sat mute and observant" (019-010). He does not speak, does not offer comfort or complaint. His muteness is not stoic posturing but the natural expression of a man whose relationship to crisis is mediated by professional habit.

## Character Traits

Collins is defined by observation rather than action, silence rather than speech. He "sat mute and observant" (019-010) through the long hours in the lifeboat, his attention fixed on the practical realities of survival rather than the emotional demands of the moment. When [[captain-hale|the captain]]'s boat calls across to them, "Collins held up a hand in understanding" (019-003) — a gesture that communicates competence without requiring words.

His introduction — putting his head in at the chart-room door at eight bells — establishes him as a man of routine, punctuality, and professional decorum. He is the kind of officer who knows his place and fills it without complaint, whose reliability is so consistent as to be almost invisible.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 18** — Collins puts his head in at the chart-room door at eight bells (018-028); is given command of a boat during the crisis: "Your boat, Collins, keep her away" (018-063).
- **Chapter 19** — In the lifeboat, Collins sits "mute and observant" (019-010), holding up a hand in acknowledgment of orders called across the water (019-003).

## Relationships

- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The master to whom Collins reports; the relationship is characterized by unspoken professional understanding.
- **[[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]]** — The chief officer above Collins in [[the-altair|the ship]]'s hierarchy.
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The passenger who shares the lifeboat with Collins and observes his [[silence|quiet]] competence.

## Significance

Collins represents a kind of minor-key heroism that the novel values without dramatizing. He is not [[captain-hale|the captain]], not the chief officer, not the engineer — yet his competence is no less real for being subordinate. His "mute and observant" presence in the lifeboat is a form of professional integrity: he does not panic, does not philosophize, does not even speak. He simply attends.

This attentiveness connects Collins to the novel's broader investment in observation as a mode of being. [[jim-colet|Colet]] is an observer by temperament; Collins is an observer by professional training. Their shared silence in the lifeboat creates an unexpected kinship between the introspective landsman and the professional seaman.
