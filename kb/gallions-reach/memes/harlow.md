# The Harlow

The *Harlow* is a ship mentioned in passing by the master of the [[the-altair|*Altair*]], but her brief appearance is freighted with the unspoken codes of the maritime world. She is "anchored astern of mine, the Harlow" (007-006) — a point of reference, a professional comparison, a name dropped into conversation with the weight of shared knowledge.

## Specifications

The *Harlow* is a steamer — "if that horse is like the Harlow, he'll want some stoking to raise a sweat" (007-008) — the comparison to a horse suggesting both power and recalcitrance. She is a known quantity among mariners, her reputation preceding her: a ship that requires effort to raise steam, a vessel that demands to be worked.

## Role in the Narrative

The *Harlow* appears only in Chapter 7, in the conversation between the *Altair*'s master and his interlocutor. Her name is invoked as a shared reference point: "heard of a gin and bitters on the Harlow. But you knew all right what to expect" (007-023). The ellipsis of this mention — the shared knowledge that needs no elaboration — reveals the density of the maritime world Tomlinson sketches in a few lines.

She is not a setting, not a stage for events — she is a name, and in the novel's economy, a name is enough. The *Harlow* exists as a node in the network of ships, ports, and men that constitutes the novel's maritime unconscious.

## Key Crew

The *Harlow*'s crew are unnamed. She exists in the novel only as a vessel, not as a society — the inverse of the *Altair*, whose human complement defines her.

## Voyages

No specific voyages are recorded for the *Harlow*. She is mentioned as being anchored astern of the *Altair* (007-006), suggesting she shares the same port or anchorage. Her presence is static, a point of comparison rather than a vessel in motion.

## Significance

The *Harlow* is what might be called a threshold entity — a ship with just enough presence in the text to merit a page, but not enough to sustain extended analysis. Her significance lies precisely in this marginality: she represents the hundreds of ships that populate the novel's background, the names dropped in wardrooms and on quaysides, the shared reference points of a world that exists mostly off the page.

She also serves a structural function: the comparison between *Harlow* and *Altair* — one recalcitrant, one "a sound old dear" (014-014) — establishes the spectrum of maritime character along which all the novel's vessels are placed.
