# The Doctor

An elderly doctor travelling as a passenger aboard the *Altair*, the steamer that carries Jim Colet from London towards the East. He is a quiet but perceptive figure whose philosophical remarks unsettle the more pragmatic members of the ship's company.

## Role in the Novel

The doctor appears during the breakfast conversations aboard the *Altair* in the early chapters. He serves as a counterpoint to the ship's master — where the master is bluff, proud, and dismissive of abstract thought, the doctor is reflective, "offensively quiet and kind" (007-024), and willing to entertain unsettling ideas. His suggestion that the voyage is "a ghost story" (007-032) becomes a recurring note in the novel's meditation on reality and illusion.

## Character Traits

The doctor is elderly, diffident, and given to "smiling sideways" when provoked (007-007). He contemplates the ship's master "furtively" when he wishes to speak (007-013), suggesting a cautious, observant nature. When told that his job is easy because he simply buries his mistakes, he is quietly amused rather than defensive (007-027). The master's reflection — "more in this chap than I supposed" (007-029) — confirms that the doctor's unassuming manner conceals unusual depth of thought. He admits freely when he is uncertain, saying "I dare say you are right" when challenged (007-034), but his calm conviction remains unshaken.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 7** — Introduced at breakfast aboard the *Altair*; engages in a philosophical debate with the master about reality, soundings, and whether the voyage is "a ghost story" (007-006 through 007-042)
- **Chapter 9** — Colet later recalls that "the doctor who talked at breakfast was right" (009-002), suggesting the doctor's observations prove prescient
- **Chapter 21** — Briefly referenced as the person who conveyed Colet's name to another passenger (021-058)

## Relationships

- **[[The Master]]** — The doctor and the ship's master are intellectual foils; the master shakes a "rebuking fork" at the doctor, proud of his practical knowledge, while the doctor quietly undermines the master's certainties with philosophical questions
- **[[Jim Colet]]** — Colet observes the breakfast conversations and later reflects that the doctor's perspective was correct; the doctor's words resonate with Colet's own sense of doubt and dislocation

## Significance

The doctor is one of the novel's several figures who gesture towards a deeper, less certain reality beneath the surface of things. His description of the voyage as "a ghost story" prefigures the novel's recurring concern with illusion, perception, and the thinness of the boundary between the real and the unreal. As a medical man who deals in life and death, he is positioned to speak with authority about what is and is not solid, and his quiet doubt serves as a subtle critique of the master's blustering certainty.
