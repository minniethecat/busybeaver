# Sinclair's Farewell to Circe

In the midst of the China Sea, surrounded by the building storm, [[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]] kisses his hand toward a mirage: "Farewell, Circe, my love!" (013-006). The gesture is theatrical, allusive, unexpectedly tender — and it is one of the novel's most memorable moments. [[sinclair|Sinclair]], the brisk chief officer, reveals in this gesture a capacity for sentiment that his professional demeanor conceals.

## The Event

The moment occurs in Chapter 13, as the *Altair* navigates [[china-sea|the Gulf]]. A mirage appears — a shore that is not there, an island that is only light — and [[sinclair|Sinclair]] responds with the instinct of a man who has spent his life at sea. "Farewell, Circe, my love!" The mythological reference transforms the optical illusion into something richer: an encounter with the enchanting and deceptive powers of [[china-sea|the sea]], acknowledged with a kiss and a farewell.

## Significance

[[sinclair|Sinclair]]'s farewell to Circe is the novel's most perfect fusion of the professional and the poetic. He is not a poet — he is a chief officer — but his gesture contains more poetry than [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s elaborate introspections. It is a reminder that feeling does not require articulation, that sentiment can be expressed in a kiss blown toward a shore that is not there.
