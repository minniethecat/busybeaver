# The River Thames

The River Thames is [[london|London]]'s great waterway — the river that carries the city's commerce to [[china-sea|the sea]] and, in the novel, carries [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] from [[colets-father|his father]]'s pointing finger toward [[indian-ocean|the ocean]] that will transform him. It is first invoked implicitly, in the opening scene at [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]], where the river widens toward its estuary. The [[the-sea|Thames]] is the novel's liquid spine: the water that connects [[london|London]] to the world beyond.

## In the Novel

The [[the-sea|Thames]] appears most vividly in the novel's opening — [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]] is a stretch of the Thames — and in [[colets-departure|the departure]] scenes at the docks. It is the river of [[london|London]]'s commerce, the waterway that made [[london|the City]] possible, and it is also the river of departure. To move down the Thames is to move from the known to the unknown, from the measured to the unmeasured.

## Significance

The [[the-sea|Thames]] is the novel's literal and symbolic connection between [[london|London]] and [[china-sea|the sea]]. It carries [[london|the City]]'s filth and its grandeur, its commerce and its [[dream|dreams]]. For [[jim-colet|Colet]], the Thames is the path of departure — the water that will carry him beyond [[colets-father|his father]]'s definition, beyond [[perriam|Perriam]]'s judgment, toward a world that [[london|London]] cannot measure.
