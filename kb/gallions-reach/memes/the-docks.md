# The Docks

The [[london|London]] docks appear in the novel's transitional chapters — the moment when [[jim-colet|Colet]] moves from the commercial City to the maritime world. It is here that he first encounters the *Harlow*'s captain and the "elderly doctor," here that the language shifts from ledgers to tides, from wages to weather.

## In the Novel

The docks are introduced through a tavern scene in Chapter 7, where [[jim-colet|Colet]] meets the maritime types who will become his companions and models. The *Harlow*'s captain complains about the doctor's "radical rag"; the doctor "smiled sideways" (007-007); the talk is of ships and seas rather than wages and trials. It is a world [[jim-colet|Colet]] observes with the same attentive opacity he brings to [[london|London]] — but a world whose values are beginning to claim him.

## Significance

The docks are the novel's literal threshold: the place where land meets water, where the commercial meets the maritime, where [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s old life ends and his new life begins. They are not romanticized — there is gin and bitters, rough talk, professional complaint — but they are rendered with an attention that [[london|London]]'s grey limestone never receives. The docks are the first intimation that there might be a world outside [[perriam|Perriam]]'s jurisdiction.
