# The Malay Amok

During the coastal steamer's passage from Penang, a Malay passenger suddenly rises and decapitates a Chinese coolie on deck in an act of *amok* — a sudden, unprovoked killing that exposes the violent undercurrent beneath the placid surface of colonial Southeast Asia and shatters Jim Colet's romantic preconceptions.

## Summary

The incident occurs on the foredeck of the small coaster carrying Colet eastward from Penang. A commercial traveller — a young Englishman with "judicious monocle and accent" — has been lecturing Colet on the simplicity and docility of the native population: "They admired the English. They would do anything for us." He points to a squatting Malay passenger as a picturesque example. Moments later, "a Chinese cooly was passing the crouching Malay, who rose, and was seized with a sudden convulsion. The Chinaman fell. Colet noticed in disbelief that the man's head was off. Colet did not move. He had not decided yet that the head was dead, for a little cigarette smoke still moved from its nostrils" [026-067].

## Participants

- **the Malay passenger** — The anonymous Malay who runs amok; no motive is established
- **[[jim-colet]]** — Witnesses the killing; the event marks the end of his romanticised view of the East
- **the commercial traveller** — The English hardware salesman whose complacent lecture on native docility is violently refuted

## Narrative Context

The amok occurs immediately after the commercial traveller's disquisition on the ease of understanding "these people." The timing is devastating: "The penalty had come so suddenly, just when he had allowed the motorcar, in spite of its size and appearance, to displace some old conceptions, to occupy with the certainty of a late solid engine the ancient mystery of the East" [026-067]. The other native passengers react with indifference — "The Chinamen stared indifferently at the place where the water had swirled." Only Colet is shocked, and his shock is the shock of recognition: the East he has been told about is not the East that exists.

## Key Passages

> "A Chinese cooly was passing the crouching Malay, who rose, and was seized with a sudden convulsion. The Chinaman fell. Colet noticed in disbelief that the man's head was off. Colet did not move. He had not decided yet that the head was dead, for a little cigarette smoke still moved from its nostrils." [026-067]

The amok is later recalled by Norrie, who refers to it with grim humour: "You'll be able to tell them all about the amok. It's such a juicy story" [027-007]. This casualness underscores the novel's quiet thesis — that violence in the colonial periphery is at once unimaginable to London and banal to those who live at its edges.
