# Character

Throughout *Gallions Reach*, the concept of "character" operates on multiple levels — moral reputation, performed identity, and the inscrutable essence of a person. Tomlinson's characters are repeatedly assessed, misjudged, and revealed through their actions rather than their words.

## Moral Character and Reputation

The novel is acutely concerned with how character is perceived versus what it actually is. Norrie remarks wryly on "flattering your sound moral character" when discussing a tiger in the jungle (PN 027-024), the irony being that a tiger neither knows nor cares about human virtue. The doctor, a recurring background figure, "had long ago decided" certain things about civilisation — a judgment of character applied to an entire way of life (PN 007-013).

## The Performed Self

Several characters are described as self-consciously inhabiting roles. Norrie himself is "only a self-conscious character in an unusual theatrical setting" (PN 027-003), acutely aware of his displacement. One figure is described as "a character of that place" (PN 027-002) — identity fused with environment, as though the jungle itself had shaped him. The novel suggests that character is partly innate and partly a performance shaped by circumstances.

## The Mystery of Others

Tomlinson repeatedly returns to the opacity of human character. Figures move through the London streets "with no character, in no hurry" (PN 006-026) — anonymous, illegible. In the East, people move "as would the characters of a ballet with an incomprehensible theme" (PN 023-003), their inner lives inaccessible to the Western observer.

## Colet's Character

The protagonist Colet is presented as "different" — "an odd character; his simplicity had an important weight in that community" (PN 034-001). His character is revealed not through introspection but through how others respond to him: with trust, suspicion, or incomprehension. This is Tomlinson's method throughout — character is what others make of you.
