# Trade

Trade is the invisible engine of *Gallions Reach*. The novel is set in motion by commerce — Colet works for Perriams, a City trading firm — and every ship that crosses the novel's pages carries cargo. Yet Tomlinson treats trade with characteristic ambivalence: it is the source of London's prosperity and the mechanism of colonial extraction, a force that connects the world and corrupts it.

## The City and Commerce

The novel opens in the heart of London's commercial district, where trade is both a livelihood and a way of seeing the world. The Perriams office is a monument to trade — its walls lined with the trophies of global commerce, including "Samian ware, and some hints of Etruscan commerce" (PN 003-033) that embed modern trade in an ancient lineage. The mercantile impulse, Tomlinson suggests, is as old as civilisation.

## Empire as Trade

The novel's geography is a map of imperial trade routes. Ships sail from London to the East carrying manufactured goods and return with raw materials — rubber, tin, spices. Colet's journey follows these routes, and at every port the machinery of commerce is visible. The Hudson's Bay Company is invoked as a precursor: "to go out to Rupert's Land and trade with Indians from a fort of logs" (PN 003-007). Trade and empire are inseparable; the flag follows the ledger.

## The Cost of Commerce

Tomlinson is attentive to what trade corrupts. "Trade poison" (PN 026-009) — the phrase refers to cheap spirits sold to colonial populations but resonates more broadly. The novel suggests that commerce, by reducing all value to price, poisons human relations. The trade in labour ("native labour out of tropical prodigality," PN 002-004) is the darkest expression of this reduction.

## Beyond Trade

Against the world of commerce, the novel sets moments of disinterested human connection — friendship, love, the contemplation of beauty. These are presented not as escapes from trade but as correctives to it, reminders that not everything has a price.
