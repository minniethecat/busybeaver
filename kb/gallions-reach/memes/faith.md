# Faith

In *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927), H. M. Tomlinson explores faith not as doctrinal religion but as the existential trust that holds human beings steady in the face of overwhelming forces. Faith attaches to ships, to fellow men, to the idea of human [[fate|destiny]] itself — and the novel tests each of these attachments against the indifferent power of [[china-sea|the sea]] and the jungle.

## In the Novel

Faith appears first in a secular, almost childlike register: the men gathered in [[london|the City]] express an "expression of childlike faith and dreamy beatitude" as they stand about their high stools (003-026). This early faith is unexamined, a posture absorbed from the commercial world's routine.

At sea, faith becomes a matter of survival. [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] "reposed in the faith that [[the-altair|the Altair]] was a sound old dear" (014-014), trusting his ship as one trusts a living creature. This faith is immediately tested: "As if to try his faith, his own ship then treated him with indignity" (015-015), plunging him into the violent motion of a storm. Faith, Tomlinson suggests, exists precisely to be tested — its value emerges only under duress.

The novel's most concentrated meditation on faith comes through the figure of [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]], standing watch in a storm: "That slight figure of the master, standing in profile, so watchful of vague and immense powers, and so undisturbed by their onrush, was like a token of [[silence|quiet]] faith untouched by calamity and overthrow" (017-023). Here faith is embodied, not preached — it is the steady posture of a man who has learned to time [[indian-ocean|the ocean]].

In the later chapters, faith shifts from the maritime to the human. [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s faith is sustained by the certitude that "its good men" are "holding fast" (018-021). The anonymous courage and faith of simple souls becomes a bulwark against [[indian-ocean|the ocean]]'s meaninglessness (022-015). Even in [[penang|Penang]], [[jim-colet|Colet]] sustains his "faith that [[penang|Penang]] somewhere must have a different roof to offer" (023-008).

## Related Characters

- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The embodiment of [[silence|quiet]] faith, standing undisturbed against [[indian-ocean|the ocean]]'s onslaught (017-023).
- **[[parsell|Mr. Parsell]]** — A character whose "faith in human [[fate|destiny]]" is ultimately derided by the indifferent universe (036-018).
- **[[sinclair|Sinclair]]** — His brisk departure adds to [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s "store of riches" — an imponderable credit that no bank manager would recognize but which sustains faith in human connection (022-015).

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's treatment of faith is deliberately non-theological. Faith in *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* is a practical virtue — the quality that keeps a captain at his post and a man walking toward an uncertain shore. It is "nebulous and shifting" (024-037), attaching to whatever object can bear its weight: a ship, a companion, a city waiting somewhere beyond the horizon.

The novel's final verdict on faith is characteristically ambiguous. [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s faith in human [[fate|destiny]] is sustained through shipwreck and jungle, but the last word belongs to a mocking call from the untraversed [[the-jungle|forest]] (036-018). Faith may be necessary, but nature does not confirm it.

## Related Concepts

- **[[fate|Fate]]** — Faith is the human response to fate; where fate is external and implacable, faith is the internal resource that meets it.
- **[[dream|Dream]]** — Paired with faith in the novel's opening: "childlike faith and dreamy beatitude" (003-026).
