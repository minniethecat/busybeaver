# Mr. Perriam

Mr. Perriam is the principal antagonist of the novel's [[london|London]] chapters — not a villain in any melodramatic sense, but the embodiment of commercial authority against which [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s sensibility chafes. He is the master of Perriams, Limited, a shipping firm in [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]], and his presence dominates the novel's early sections with the gravity of a force of nature.

## Role in the Novel

Perriam functions as the novel's representative of worldly power. His firm occupies the first floor of a building in [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]], "a region of grey limestone and glass" (002-001). He is both a specific man — "clean-shaven," with a "massive face" (002-007) — and a symbolic figure, "just the same as a priest of Baal" whose worshippers go "crazy before whatever they worshipped" (002-020).

## Character Traits

Perriam's defining characteristic is his capacity to dominate without haste. He waits — "Mr. Perriam did not look up. He waited, without moving" (002-009) — and his waiting is a form of pressure more effective than speech. His logic is "certainly right" (002-010) even when it is oppressive, and his judgment of [[jim-colet|Colet]] carries the weight of institutional necessity: "You know, [[jim-colet|Colet]], you are being tried? Very well" (002-018).

He is not cruel for its own sake. His cruelty is systemic — the cruelty of a man who has identified himself entirely with his commercial function. When he strikes the papers before him, it is with the conviction that "my judgment is what pays you and me" (002-018).

[[mrs-perriam|Mrs. Perriam]]'s silent withdrawal at the sound of his car (005-002) completes [[penang|the port]]rait: Perriam's authority extends from [[billiter-avenue|the office]] to the home, and those closest to him have learned to absent themselves at his approach.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 2** — Perriam's office confrontations with [[jim-colet|Colet]] establish the power dynamic (002-007 through 002-021).
- **Chapter 5** — His approach to his [[brixton|Brixton]] home, "coming up the stairs in heavy deliberation, like [[fate|destiny]]" (005-009).

## Relationships

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — Perriam's employee, whose trial and judgment form the dramatic core of the [[london|London]] chapters.
- **[[mrs-perriam|Mrs. Perriam]]** — His wife, who withdraws "at the first wave of disturbance sent before Mr. Perriam's car" (005-002).

## Significance

Perriam is more than a boss. He is [[london|the City]] made flesh — the force that reduces men to functionaries, that measures their worth in commercial terms, that "never read" (003-012). His approach is described as "like [[fate|destiny]]" (005-009), linking him to the novel's central thematic concern with fate. To leave Perriams is to leave [[london|London]]; to leave [[london|London]] is to escape the [[fate|destiny]] he represents.
