# Commerce

The mercantile world that Jim Colet inhabits — and ultimately flees — is the engine of imperial London, where profit is a religion and firms like Perriams, Limited connect the City to Rangoon, Penang, Borneo, and China.

## In the Novel

Commerce is the novel's baseline reality, the "chronicle" against which Colet's spiritual journey unfolds. Perriams, Limited occupies the first floor of a building in Billiter Avenue, its brass plate "as familiar a word as Colombo, Rangoon, Penang, Borneo, or China" [002-004]. The firm's reach across the eastern trade routes establishes the geographic frame of Colet's eventual journey.

For Colet, commerce is a form of worship he cannot sustain. The office becomes a temple to "Baal" — the mercantile god demanding sacrifice: "Serve the god, or be offered up" [002-021]. The Chinese porcelain figure of Kuan-yin on his desk offers the only counterpoint: "a luminous symbol of benignity amid alien things" [002-021], her presence suggesting an alternative to the commercial imperative.

The novel's critique sharpens through Perriam himself, described as a predatory force: "He could not help succeeding, that watchful and predatory monster. Saw his advantage and took it before the next chap knew there was anything to be got" [003-004]. Colet, by contrast, feels only half-present in this world, suspecting that commerce "asked for only half of a man, and that the worst and cleverest half" [002-021].

The tension culminates in a blunt assessment: "the art of commerce is the art of doing without more feeling than you need for luck" [004-062]. This reduction of human relations to transactional calculus is precisely what Colet rejects when he walks away from Perriams after his employer's death.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson portrays commerce as a diminished form of human engagement — necessary, powerful, but spiritually hollow. The novel is not anti-commerce in a political sense; rather, it treats the commercial mindset as a kind of sensory deprivation. Colet's escape from London is an escape from a world where everything has a price and nothing has intrinsic worth.

The juxtaposition of Perriam (the commercial predator) with Kuan-yin (the porcelain goddess of mercy) structures the novel's central conflict: transaction versus contemplation, profit versus beauty, the ledger versus the work of art. When Colet finally walks through the British Museum before fleeing London, he encounters objects "better than all the ledgers of British commerce" [003-033] — a quiet declaration of values.
