# Colet's Father

Jim Colet's father is never named in the novel. He appears in only a handful of [[time|passage]]s, and always in [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s memory rather than in the narrative present. Yet his presence looms over the novel's opening — and over [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s entire trajectory — with a gravity that belies his brief appearances. He is the man who pointed toward [[london|London]] and said, "See it? [[london|London]], my boy" (001-005), and in that gesture defined the world his son would have to leave.

## Role in the Novel

The father's role is originary. The novel opens with him: "There you are, [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]. See it? [[london|London]], my boy" (001-005). This is the novel's inaugural speech act — a naming, a pointing, a definition of the world. The father stands on [[the-altair|the steamer]] approaching [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]], and what he points to is [[london|London]]: [[london|the City]], the commercial world, the "region of grey limestone and glass" (002-001) that will claim his son.

Yet the father's authority is uncertain. "Sometimes his father did not mean what he said" (001-008) — the child's perception of adult opacity is the novel's first intimation that the world does not mean what it says. The father's words define [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s world, but their meaning is unstable from the start.

[[jim-colet|Colet]] reflects, in adulthood, that "his father had told him he was made, when he left school" (002-021) — the father's promise of a secure future revealed as the assurance that binds rather than liberates. "[[fate|Fate]] and duty to a father whose influence intrigued a living" (003-007) — the father's influence is both [[fate|destiny]] and obligation, the force that pushed [[jim-colet|Colet]] into "that chair" (005-006) in [[perriam|Perriams]]' office.

## Character Traits

The father is characterized exclusively through [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s memory — we never see him directly. He is a pointer, a namer, a definer of worlds. His speech is declarative: "See it? [[london|London]], my boy." His meaning, however, is elusive: "Sometimes his father did not mean what he said."

This combination of authority and opacity makes him a figure of almost mythic resonance. He is the Father in the novel's symbolic economy — the principle of origin, the voice that names the world, the force whose "influence intrigued a living" for his son. To leave [[london|London]] is to leave the father's definition; to seek the "younger [[light|sunlight]]" of the jungle is to seek a world his father never named.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 1** — The novel's opening: the father points to [[london|London]] from the darkness of [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]] (001-005). [[jim-colet|Colet]] looks at his father, whose meaning is uncertain (001-006, 001-008).
- **Chapter 2** — [[jim-colet|Colet]] recalls his father's assurance: "his father had told him he was made" (002-021).
- **Chapter 3** — "[[fate|Fate]] and duty to a father whose influence intrigued a living" (003-007).
- **Chapter 5** — [[jim-colet|Colet]] reflects that he sits in [[perriam|Perriams]]' office "because his father pushed him that way" (005-006).

## Relationships

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The son whose [[fate|destiny]] the father defines, and from whose definition the son must ultimately escape.
- [[london|London]] — The father points to [[london|London]]; his identity is indistinguishable from [[london|the City]] he names.

## Significance

[[jim-colet|Colet]]'s father is the novel's absent center. He never speaks in the narrative present; all his words are remembered. Yet he is the figure against whom the entire novel unfolds. To leave [[perriam|Perriams]], to leave [[london|London]], to leave England — all of these departures are, at the deepest level, a departure from the father's definition.

The father's anonymity is significant. He is never named because he does not need to be: he is the Father, the principle of origin, the voice that says "See it?" and in saying, defines what is seen. [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s journey is the long work of learning to see without that voice.
