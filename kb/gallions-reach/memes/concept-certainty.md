# Certainty

Certainty in *Gallions Reach* arrives in two forms: the intuitive and the engineered. The novel treats both with suspicion. Intuitive certainty — the "instant waking" kind — is unreasoned and therefore suspect. Engineered certainty — the "late solid engine" kind — is artificial and therefore unstable. Between them lies doubt, which the novel treats as the more honest condition.

## Instant Waking Certainty

The most vivid certainty in the novel is precognitive:

> Sleep with the instant waking certainty that something was going to happen. [013-008]

This is certainty that bypasses reason — a knowledge that arrives complete and unquestionable. The novel does not endorse it but registers its phenomenological force.

## The Engineered Certainty

Certainty can also be constructed, like a machine:

> Conceptions, to occupy with the certainty of a late solid engine the ancient vacancy. [026-066]

Here certainty is opposed to "ancient vacancy" — to the void that existed before conviction filled it. The phrase "late solid engine" suggests something recently built, reliable but also heavy, mechanical. Certainty fills space but does not necessarily illuminate it.

## The Artless and Profitless

One passage ties certainty to something "artless and profitless" kept "in the heart" [022-015]. Without this certainty, something is lost — though what exactly it secures remains unnamed. The pairing of "artless and profitless" suggests that genuine certainty has no agenda and serves no ulterior purpose.

## The Conviction of Good Cheer

Conviction appears in social contexts. A character's scrutiny is "met with a candid conviction of good cheer" [021-042] — conviction as a performance of confidence rather than an inner state. When conviction becomes "deep religious" it makes its "worst muck of things" [028-022], suggesting that the more fervent the conviction, the more damage it can do.

## The Counter-Current of Doubt

The novel consistently pairs certainty with its opposite. Characters see "though not without doubt" [013-009], and there is "a doubt whether it was possible to live alone" [008-007]. The refrain "no doubt about that" [009-001, 003-035, 004-062] appears so frequently — and the assertions it prefaces are so uncertain — that it functions as dramatic irony. The more the novel insists on the absence of doubt, the more doubt it registers.
