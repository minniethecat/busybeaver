# Destiny

Throughout *Gallions Reach*, characters grapple with whether their lives are shaped by destiny, accident, or choice. Colet's journey from City clerk to jungle wanderer is framed as a series of fatal coincidences that may — or may not — add up to a design.

## In the Novel

The language of destiny first appears with Perriam's approach up the office stairs: "He was coming up the stairs in heavy deliberation, like destiny. No escape now" [005-009]. Perriam is cast as an agent of fate — implacable, heavy, inescapable. The simile invests a commercial interview with mythological weight, and Colet's subsequent violence seems, in retrospect, almost destined.

Yet the novel refuses to settle the question. When Colet reflects on his situation, he wavers: "The boy was destiny right enough, though destiny had obviously made a mistake" [007-011]. The paradox — destiny that errs — suggests that what feels fated may simply be the accumulated weight of contingent choices.

In the jungle, the question sharpens. Norrie tells Colet: "You could let old destiny take its course" [034-012], and Colet responds: "I don't know destiny when I see it. What is it like?" [034-013]. The exchange captures the novel's ambivalence: destiny is invoked but never defined, felt but never proven.

The novel's final word on destiny is ironic but not dismissive. Colet declares "I'll call this destiny; and there you are" [034-037] — naming something as destiny is itself an act of interpretation, not a recognition of fact. The last reflection records that someone "had derided his faith in human destiny" [036-018], leaving the question open.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson treats destiny not as a theological doctrine but as a psychological experience. Characters feel themselves to be in the grip of forces beyond their control, but the novel never confirms whether those forces are real or imagined. This ambiguity is deliberate: *Gallions Reach* is a novel about a man who cannot decide whether he is acting or being acted upon.

The philosophical texture owes something to Conrad, for whom fate was always entangled with character. Colet's "destiny" is, in one reading, simply the consequence of his own nature — his mildness, his half-heartedness, his inability to fully inhabit the commercial role assigned to him. If that is destiny, then destiny is character, and character is fate.
