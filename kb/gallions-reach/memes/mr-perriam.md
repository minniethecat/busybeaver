# Mr. Perriam

The principal of **Perriams, Limited**, a London shipping firm where Jim Colet works as a clerk. He embodies the commercial, pragmatic values of the City — a world Colet increasingly finds stifling.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Perriam is Colet's employer and represents the established commercial order from which the protagonist must break free. When Colet challenges the firm's treatment of its labourers, Perriam responds with cold business logic: "It is what pays you and me," striking the papers before him in emphasis (002-018). His function in the narrative is to personify the world of trade and fixed rules that Colet ultimately leaves behind when he departs for the East.

## Character Traits

Perriam is authoritarian, exacting, and governed entirely by commercial logic. He does not look up when Colet enters his office, waiting wordlessly to assert his dominance (002-009). He has a "neat little brown beard" (002-010) and his reasoning, though harsh, is described as "certainly right" within its own terms (002-010). At his Brixton home, he is shown to be restless and filled with resentment, unable to settle comfortably even in domestic life (005-001, 005-010). He admires men who refuse to concede to ill-luck, seeing such stoicism as a mark of character (005-023, 005-025).

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 2** — Colet's pivotal confrontation with Perriam at the office, where Perriam dismisses concerns about labourers' hours and asserts the primacy of profit (002-005 through 002-023)
- **Chapter 3** — Colet reflects on Perriams as "the wrong pitch for him" after losing his position (003-003)
- **Chapter 5** — Perriam at his Brixton home, shown in domestic context, still filled with his characteristic resentment (005-001, 005-010, 005-012, 005-019, 005-023, 005-025)

## Relationships

- **[[Jim Colet]]** — Perriam is Colet's employer and antagonist in the early chapters; their confrontation over labour conditions precipitates Colet's departure from the firm
- **[[Mrs. Perriam]]** — Referenced indirectly as "she" who is "different" from Perriam, refusing to worship what he worships (002-019)

## Significance

Perriam functions as the embodiment of the commercial world that Tomlinson critiques throughout the novel. He is "just the same as a priest" in his devotion to business, with "some kind of secret religion inside him" (002-020, 002-021). The firm's name — Perriams, Limited — suggests limitation and constraint, contrasting with the boundless sea and jungle that Colet later encounters.
