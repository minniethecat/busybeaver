# Aldgate

Aldgate is one of the ancient eastern gates of the City of London. In *Gallions Reach*, it appears through its most distinctive landmark — Aldgate Pump — which serves as both a literal place and a symbolic threshold. The narrator describes how a character "came to Aldgate Pump, which is the starting-place" (006-002), the phrase investing the landmark with the gravity of departure: every journey east begins here.

## Location & Context

Aldgate Pump stands at the junction of Fenchurch Street and Leadenhall Street, historically marking the eastern boundary of the City of London. In the 1920s, the area was a commercial district of shipping offices, warehouses, and establishments that serviced the maritime trade — the London that faces the sea rather than the land. The pump itself had a folkloric reputation: its water was said to be unlucky, contaminated by the corpses of plague victims buried upstream, and "an aldgate pump" became slang for a worthless draft. Tomlinson uses this resonance subtly; the pump is a place where things begin, but what begins there may carry a hidden taint.

## Role in the Narrative

Aldgate appears at two crucial points in the novel, framing the London sections from departure to spectral return. In Chapter 6, [[mr-perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s clerk finds himself "by the stalls of Aldgate" (006-008), surrounded by the "distraction" of street commerce — hawkers, barrows, the jostle of East End life just beyond the City's formal boundary. The scene captures the vitality of a London that the shipping offices of [[perriams-limited|Perriams, Limited]] cannot contain.

In Chapter 25, the narrator images a ghost "clanking his chains west of Aldgate Pump, dutiful as an old soldier" (025-014). This second appearance transforms the landmark: from starting-place to haunting-ground, from the gate through which one departs to the gate beyond which one is trapped. The ghost is "dutiful," its captivity chosen — a figure of the [[character|self]] that cannot leave the familiar even when the familiar has become a cage.

## Atmosphere & Description

Aldgate is presented as a threshold — not a grand one, but a workaday boundary. The stalls and the "distraction" (006-008) suggest transit rather than settlement. The pump itself is unheroic: a water source, a landmark, a meeting-point. Tomlinson invests this ordinariness with quiet significance. Aldgate is where the novel's geography begins, the zero-point from which all [[distance|eastward movement]] is measured.

## Associated Characters

- **[[mr-perriam|Mr. Perriam]]** — His offices are near Aldgate; his clerk walks through it on the evening of crisis, the journey from Perriam's world to the street marking the first step away from commercial [[london|London]]'s certainties.
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — Departs from the London that Aldgate marks; his journey east begins at this boundary and will carry him far beyond anything the pump's water could foretell.

## Symbolic Meaning

Aldgate functions as the novel's eastern threshold within London itself — a secular marker of departure. Before [[plymouth|Plymouth]], before the open sea, before [[the-altair|the *Altair*]] and its wreck, there is Aldgate: the gate in the name itself a reminder that every journey begins with a crossing. The pump's reputed unluckiness adds an undertow of [[fate|foreboding]]: the water that starts the journey may also poison it.

The twice-occurring image — once as starting-place, once as the boundary beyond which a ghost is confined — makes Aldgate a quiet structural rhyme. What begins at the pump in Chapter 6 returns to it in Chapter 25, transformed: the journey that started in hope has become, in memory, a chain.
