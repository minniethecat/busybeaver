# Colet's Voyage East

The voyage from England to the East occupies the novel's middle chapters — the long sea [[time|passage]] during which [[jim-colet|Colet]] is neither the [[london|London]] clerk nor the jungle survivor but something in between. The voyage is a period of suspension, a parenthesis between worlds, and its duration is essential to the novel's effect. Transformation, Tomlinson suggests, takes time — the time of long watches, slow barometers, the gradual dissolution of certainties.

## The Event

The voyage is less a sequence of events than a condition. Days pass; weather changes; [[the-altair|the ship]]'s routine — bells, meals, watches — provides a structure more reliable than anything [[london|London]] offered. [[jim-colet|Colet]] observes [[billiter-avenue|the office]]rs, learns the rhythms of [[china-sea|the sea]], begins to shed the habits of [[london|the City]]. "He did not know the time, as he mounted, his mind at ease, the steps to the temple" (003-032) — this freedom from measured time, first experienced outside the British Museum, becomes the voyage's defining condition.

## Significance

The voyage is the novel's most sustained argument for attention as a form of transformation. [[jim-colet|Colet]] does not change through dramatic action but through prolonged observation — of [[captain-hale|Hale]]'s competence, [[sinclair|Sinclair]]'s theatricality, [[gillespie|Gillespie]]'s tactile intelligence, [[china-sea|the sea]]'s indifference. The voyage is the education of a sensibility.
