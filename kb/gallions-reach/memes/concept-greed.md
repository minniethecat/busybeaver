# Greed

Greed in *Gallions Reach* is the engine of its commercial world — the force that drives Perriam's trading house, lures men to tropical prospecting, and stands in tension with the novel's quieter values of fate and inner knowledge.

## In the Novel

The novel's London is built on commerce. Perriam's house on Billiter Street represents organized greed — a trading empire whose "ledgers of British commerce" [003-033] record the systematized pursuit of wealth. The firm's very structure embodies the principle that "if it won't kowtow to money!" then it makes no sense in the world [002-020].

Beyond London, greed takes the form of gold prospecting. The tropical venture that draws characters outward is fueled by the hope of striking fortune — the belief that the earth might yield "the wealth of the cave of Sinbad" [002-001] to those bold enough to seek it. Yet the novel is skeptical: the surgeon observes that "treasure is not the same as money" [007-037], drawing a line between true value and mere accumulation.

## Key Passages

> People always went crazy before money, couldn't believe it, and actually burnt it in the streets when it was worthless [002-020]

The money madness described here is not personal failing but a collective mania. The novel treats the worship of money as a kind of insanity that periodically overtakes societies.

> Good fortune had changed it. Perhaps the fate that is our common destiny had been suddenly deflected by his own act [032-003]

This passage links fortune and fate — wealth is a sudden deflection, not a steady state. It comes by chance and can depart the same way.
