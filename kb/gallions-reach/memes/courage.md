# Courage

Courage in *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927) is not the stuff of epic poetry. It is [[silence|quiet]]er — the summoning of resolve to mount stone steps, to stay in a room with a woman who unsettles you, to not look away from a fine porcelain bowl. Tomlinson's treatment of courage is characteristically understated, locating it in the small moments of not-retreating.

## In the Novel

The word "courage" first appears in an architectural register. [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]], contemplating the "high, massive, and grim colonnade" of a [[london|London]] building, reflects that watching doves is "easier than summoning up the courage to mount the spacious terrace of temple steps to [[malay-peninsula|the interior]]" (003-031). Courage here is a physical act of ascent — the decision to enter rather than observe from outside.

A woman provides the novel's most direct statement on courage. When [[jim-colet|Colet]] hesitates, she says [[silence|quiet]]ly, "You've only just come" (004-071). The narrator observes: "The courage of this woman! He dared not look at her." Courage is displaced from the expected protagonist to a female character whose [[silence|quiet]] persistence shames [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s hesitation.

At sea, courage takes on a professional character. The first real seas of [[colets-voyage|the voyage]] are received with something like relish: "It was like the real thing to see the decks getting wet, to be caught at a corner by a dollop of rollicking brine" (014-011). Courage here is not solemn endurance but active engagement — the pleasure of meeting genuine challenge.

In [[penang|Penang]], courage finds its most self-aware expression. [[jim-colet|Colet]] confesses to [[ah-loi|Mr. Ah Loi]]: "I only looked at these books because I had not the courage to go closer to your fine bowl there" (024-012). This is courage as aesthetic honesty — the admission of being intimidated by beauty, and the small act of naming that intimidation.

## Key Passages

> "They have the play of the doves to watch, which is easier than summoning up the courage to mount the spacious terrace of temple steps to the interior." (003-031)

> "The courage of this woman! He dared not look at her." (004-071)

> "I only looked at these books because I had not the courage to go closer to your fine bowl there." (024-012)

## Related Characters

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The protagonist whose courage is tested in small moments: entering a building, staying in a room, approaching beauty.
- **The unnamed woman** — Her [[silence|quiet]] courage in insisting [[jim-colet|Colet]] stay contrasts with his hesitation (004-071).
- **[[ah-loi|Mr. Ah Loi]]** — The [[penang|Penang]] host whose fine porcelain bowl becomes an unlikely test of courage (024-012).

## Interpretation

Tomlinson domesticates courage, relocating it from the battlefield to the drawing room, [[the-altair|the ship]]'s deck, and the collector's study. The novel's courage is defined by what one dares to approach — a building, a woman, a work of art — rather than what one dares to fight.

This redefinition aligns with the novel's broader project of attending to the unheroic. In a world where "the interminable and horrific caterpillar" of humanity flows over [[london|London]] Bridge (003-015), the individual act of summoning courage to approach beauty may be the only heroism available.

## Related Concepts

- **[[fear|Fear]]** — Courage and fear are paired throughout the novel; courage is not the absence of fear but the decision to act despite it.
- **[[faith|Faith]]** — Like courage, faith is a [[silence|quiet]] virtue sustained against overwhelming forces.
- **[[beauty|Beauty]]** — The object that most often calls forth courage in the novel; "[[beauty|Beauty]] may cause a little fear" (024-048).
