# Authority

Authority in *Gallions Reach* operates across multiple registers: the bureaucratic power of empire, the commercial authority of the firm, the social authority of class and gender, and the more diffuse authority of experience over innocence. Tomlinson traces how authority is claimed, contested, and undermined.

## In the Novel

Mr. Perriam is the novel's most concentrated figure of institutional authority. He sits "at his table, magisterial, but at his ease" (005-002), embodying the seamless fusion of personal composure and structural power. His authority derives from knowledge of commercial law and shipping practice, making him indispensable to the firm's operations. Yet this authority is revealed to be contingent—dependent on the colonial system that sustains it.

The novel's colonial setting foregrounds authority as an exercise in distance. British officials project authority across vast geographical and cultural gaps. Colet's encounter with officials "whose knowledge of ships... would have been infernal" (022-014, see [[knowledge]]) reveals authority as coercive potential held in reserve. The threat of force underwrites bureaucratic procedure.

Tomlinson also attends to the sensory dimension of authority. When "the authority of day was overturned" (014-019), the metaphor collapses natural and social orders: darkness dissolves the visible hierarchies that daylight sustains. This nocturnal destabilization recurs throughout the novel, particularly in jungle and sea passages where British authority frays at the edges.

## Key Passages

> He was put down. The authority of day was overturned. The ship became a phantom swaying in a void. (014-019)

> His intonation was that of one in authority. Colet twisted the point of his beard and looked at him over his glasses. (021-061)

> A blue had been withdrawn, by an authority who was able to declare, if pressed, that it had never been there. (004-039)

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's treatment of authority is fundamentally anti-metaphysical: authority is shown to be constructed, performed, and contingent rather than inherent or natural. The novel anticipates later postcolonial critiques by exposing the mechanisms through which imperial authority maintains itself—not through overt violence alone but through knowledge systems, social ritual, and the management of perception.

The withdrawal metaphor in "a blue had been withdrawn, by an authority who was able to declare, if pressed, that it had never been there" (004-039) captures the gaslighting quality of institutional power. Authority can retract what it once presented as fact, leaving those who noticed the retraction isolated and uncertain. This insight applies across the novel's domains: commercial, colonial, and interpersonal.
