# Escape

Escape is the novel's primary narrative engine in H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927). [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s flight from his life in [[london|London]] — from [[perriam|Perriams]], from the office, from the identity shaped by commercial obligation — provides the story's momentum. But escape in this novel is never simple: each flight carries its own entrapment, and the question of whether anyone truly escapes anything runs beneath the surface of the action.

## In the Novel

The desire for escape crystallizes early: [[jim-colet|Colet]] "would escape into another existence which, whatever it might prove to be, would be free from the worst consequences of the past" (005-008). The formulation is careful — he does not imagine a perfect future, only one "free from the worst consequences." Escape is not utopian but pragmatic: any existence not defined by the accumulating weight of the past is preferable.

The novel's most dramatic rendering of escape-as-impossibility comes with the approach of [[perriam|Perriam]] himself: "No escape now; might kill the beggar, though" (005-009). The thought that follows — "might kill the beggar" — reveals the violence latent in the desire to escape. When flight is impossible, the mind turns toward destruction.

At sea, escape shifts from social to physical terms. The ship "laboured, it beat down without ceasing glassy upheavals into fields of hissing white, but it could never escape to that dark and distant line where the wall of heaven stood about them" (012-008). The vessel's struggle against the sea mirrors [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s struggle against his circumstances — both are caught in systems larger than themselves, unable to reach the horizon.

The novel's geography offers alternative modes of flight. The Suez Canal is "a lucky way of escape" (013-007), a man-made passage that short-circuits the long way around. Meanwhile, the occupants of a sweltering port town "were enduring the orderly and stagnant heat till they had the means of escape" (022-006) — escape as patience, as waiting, as a condition deferred rather than achieved.

The jungle provides the novel's starkest image of failed escape: "one palm leaned out from it, its head over the river, as in an attempt at escape, which was checked, from the silent confusion" (030-012). The palm tree, frozen in mid-lean, becomes an emblem of the escape that is attempted but never completed.

In the novel's final crisis, [[jim-colet|Colet]] confronts the ultimate entrapment: "and he was trapped — he couldn't escape" (038-026). The simplicity of the statement — six words, no qualification — suggests that the novel's long meditation on escape arrives at a terminus where all avenues close.

## Related Concepts

- **[[fate|Fate]]** — The force from which escape is attempted; fate and escape define each other as antagonist and protagonist.
- **[[freedom|Freedom]]** — What escape is supposed to deliver, though the novel questions whether the two are the same.
- **[[london|London]]** — The primary object of Colet's escape; the city is the trap that must be fled.
- **[[the-sea|The Sea]]** — The medium of escape, and also its limit; the ship carries Colet away from [[london|London]] but itself cannot escape the horizon.
- **[[voyage|The Voyage]]** — The form that Colet's escape takes; a journey outward that is also a journey inward.
