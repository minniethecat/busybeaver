## Overview

The ghost in *Gallions Reach* is both a narrative device and a psychological reality. Characters tell ghost stories, speak of laying ghosts, and are themselves haunted — not always by the dead, but by the past that will not stay buried. Tomlinson treats the ghost as a figure for memory's persistence: "the implication of a word could haunt a man like a ghost" (003-008).

## The Ghost Story

Midway through the novel, a conversation between Captain Bennett and the doctor turns explicitly to the supernatural. "More like a ghost story than anything else," Bennett grumbles (007-031), and the doctor replies with unexpected seriousness: "It is a ghost story" (007-032). The exchange marks a tonal shift — the novel is, at some level, a story about what refuses to be laid to rest.

> "It is a ghost story," answered the doctor. (007-032)

## Laying the Ghost

In the later chapters, the language of ghost-laying becomes explicit. "I'm going back to lay his miserable ghost" (040-015) — the phrase suggests that the dead make claims on the living that must be discharged through action. Another voice answers: "I'd see his ghost to hell first" (040-016), and is corrected: "Well, it's not his ghost, really" (040-017). The ghost, it seems, is less the dead man than the unsettled business he represents.

> I'm going back to lay his miserable ghost. (040-015)

## Haunting as Memory

Tomlinson's most characteristic move is to treat haunting as a function of language and memory rather than the supernatural. "The implication of a word could haunt a man like a ghost" (003-008) — what haunts is not a spectre but significance itself, the way the past clings to what is said and thought. The ghostly mountains on the horizon are "as remote as the ghostly mountains and steeps of a land no man would ever reach" (014-008), figures for an unreachable elsewhere that is also the past.

> The implication of a word could haunt a man like a ghost. (003-008)
