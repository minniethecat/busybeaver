# Lycett

Lycett is a junior officer of the *Altair*, introduced as "Young Lycett" (018-001) — the epithet itself marking him as someone still early in his career, not yet weathered into the hardened competence of men like [[gillespie|Gillespie]] or [[sinclair|Sinclair]]. His arc in the novel is the arc of a young man who does his duty as [[the-altair|the ship]] founders, endures the lifeboat, and becomes one of the crisis's [[silence|quiet]] casualties.

## Role in the Novel

Lycett is [[billiter-avenue|the office]]r of the watch when the crisis begins. [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] asks him, "Can you make out her last signal, Lycett?" (017-043) — the question placing Lycett at the center of [[the-altair|the ship]]'s final communications with another vessel. When [[captain-hale|the captain]] leaves the bridge, he says simply, "Take over," (018-005) and Lycett does. He is left alone with the chart, the responsibilities, and the spreading [[silence|quiet]] of a ship that has begun to fail.

His youth makes him a sympathetic figure: he "faintly hesitated" (018-001), and when he "glanced at [[jim-colet|Colet]]" (018-021), the glance itself conveys what cannot be said — the awareness that something is wrong, the need to share that awareness with another human being. In the lifeboat, Lycett is "beside him, crying" (019-002), the tears not a failure of courage but a measure of what the young man has endured.

By Chapter 20, Lycett has become one of "the worst cases" (020-020). Someone says, "Somebody ought to cover up Lycett's face. The sun was too bright" (020-032). Whether he is dead or merely insensible, the novel does not say — only that [[wilson|Wilson]] is told to cover his face, and that others ask, "How's Lycett?" (021-004) with a concern that suggests he may still be alive.

## Character Traits

Lycett is defined by youth and vulnerability — qualities the novel treats not as weaknesses but as truths. He is not stoic like [[collins|Collins]] or gruff like [[gillespie|Gillespie]]; he cries, he hesitates, he glances at others for reassurance. His professionalism is real — he strikes eight bells, he takes the watch, he looks at the departing liner — but it is a professionalism still thin enough to let feeling through.

The detail that "only Lycett" looked at the departing liner (018-004) is significant. While the master had his back turned and others attended to their duties, Lycett watched the other ship go — a gesture that is both professional (tracking another vessel) and human (watching the last chance of rescue disappear).

## Relationships

- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The master who en[[faith|trust]]s Lycett with the watch: "Take over" (018-005).
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The passenger who shares the bridge with Lycett during the crisis and the lifeboat afterward.
- **[[wilson]]** — The crewman told to cover Lycett's face against the sun (020-033).
- **[[doctor|The Doctor]]** — The man asked about Lycett's condition in Chapter 21.

## Significance

Lycett is the novel's portrait of youth under extremity. He does nothing heroic; he hesitates, he cries, he becomes a casualty. And yet the novel treats him with a tenderness that amounts to an argument: that courage is not the absence of fear or tears but the persistence of duty through them.

He is also a structural device — the character through whom the cost of [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|the shipwreck]] is made personal. When his face is covered, when others ask "How's Lycett?", the abstraction of "casualties" becomes a specific young man whose name is known and whose fate matters.
