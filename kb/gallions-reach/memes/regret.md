# Regret

Regret in *Gallions Reach* is not a dramatic emotion but a quiet, persistent undertone — the "side glance" [003-008] that accompanies every forward step. It is the recognition that every choice forecloses alternatives, that experience is purchased at the cost of innocence, and that the past cannot be revisited except in memory.

## In the Novel

The novel introduces regret early, through a retrospective glance: "with a side glance, and a regret that he had gone to the city" [003-008]. The "side glance" is important — regret is not a direct confrontation with the past but a peripheral awareness, something seen out of the corner of the eye. It is not paralyzing; the character continues forward even as he looks back.

The classical register appears in the reflection that "there would be no reason to regret Ithaca and the young days" [025-014]. The Homeric allusion (Ithaca as home, as origin, as the place to which one cannot return unchanged) elevates regret from personal feeling to epic theme. The "young days" are irrecoverable not because they were better but because the self that inhabited them no longer exists.

The moral dimension surfaces when "Colet ventured to regret that aspect of our presence" [026-046] — regret for colonialism itself, for what Europeans have done and become in the East. This is regret not for personal loss but for collective guilt, and Colet's "ventured" suggests the delicacy of expressing such regret among those who do not share it.

The novel's final word on regret is pragmatic: "if you do you'll regret it. Let him be" [034-032]. Regret is here invoked as a warning — a future state to be avoided by present restraint. The progression is complete: from regret as backward glance, through regret as epic theme and moral emotion, to regret as practical wisdom. What begins as feeling ends as knowledge.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson treats regret as a cognitive emotion — it is felt, but it is also thought. Regret involves comparison (what is against what might have been), and comparison requires imagination. The capacity for regret is therefore, paradoxically, a sign of imaginative richness. Norrie's inability to regret is not a strength but a limitation; Colet's susceptibility to regret is the price of his sensitivity.

The novel distinguishes regret from nostalgia. Nostalgia falsifies the past by making it sweeter than it was; regret acknowledges loss without falsification. "No reason to regret Ithaca" [025-014] means not that Ithaca was not beautiful but that the present has its own claims. Regret, properly understood, does not devalue the present — it measures the cost at which the present was purchased.
