# Jim Colet

James "Jimmy" Colet is the central consciousness of *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927), the character through whose eyes the novel's world is perceived. He begins as a shipping clerk in the firm of [[perriam|Perriams, Limited]], in [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]], [[london|London]], and ends as a man who has faced [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|shipwreck]] and [[the-jungle|jungle]]. His journey is the novel's spine — a movement from the measured time of [[london|the City]] to the "younger [[light|sunlight]]" of the primeval [[the-jungle|forest]].

## Role in the Novel

Colet is the novel's protagonist and primary point-of-view character. The narrative opens with him as a child on [[the-altair|the steamer]] approaching [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]], [[colets-father|his father]] pointing toward the darkness: "There you are, Jim Colet. See it? [[london|London]]" (001-005). This inaugural scene establishes Colet as a character defined by thresholds — between childhood and adulthood, land and sea, known and unknown.

As an adult, Colet is "one of the multitude which entered this region every morning at nine o'clock and deserted it about six in the evening" (002-003). He is a clerk indistinguishable from thousands of others — except that he is "being tried" (002-018), tested by his employer [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]] and by circumstances he cannot control.

## Character Traits

Colet is introspective, literary, and self-doubting. He lingers at bookshop windows "searching its windows for insinuations and conjectural words" (003-012), finding in books what the commercial world denies him. He is acutely conscious of his own uncertainty: he pauses on landings, contemplates his walking stick as though "divining [[penang|the port]]ent of reflections in the heart of a crystal" (003-001), and does not consult his watch because he is "not thinking of time" (003-032).

His courage is [[silence|quiet]] and retrospective. He retreats from confrontation — "Colet retreated to his own room" (002-019) — but persists through [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|shipwreck]] and jungle. His transformation is not from weakness to strength but from diffidence to a kind of attentive endurance.

## Relationships

- **[[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]** — Colet's employer and antagonist, whose judgment tests Colet's resilience: "You know, Colet, you are being tried? Very well" (002-018).
- **[[helen-denny|Helen Denny]]** — A young woman whose attention Colet seeks and whose wandering attention pains him (004-010).
- **[[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]]** — The chief officer of the *Altair*, who becomes a mentor figure at sea.
- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The master of the *Altair*, whose [[silence|quiet]] faith in the face of storms inspires Colet.
- **[[norrie|Norrie]]** — A fellow traveler in the East whose pragmatic wisdom guides Colet through the jungle. "I like [[norrie|Norrie]]. He would have been the same" in any age (024-028).

## Significance

Colet is the vessel through which Tomlinson explores the novel's central questions: what happens to a man when he leaves the measured world of commerce for the unmeasured world of the elements? Can the sensibility formed in [[london|London]] survive the "earlier spell of the earth" (030-010)?

Colet's arc is not heroic in the conventional sense. He does not triumph — he endures. His transformation is registered in perception rather than action: by the novel's end, he has learned to see the world without [[london|London]]'s filters, to hear sounds "clearer and without fear" (030-010).
