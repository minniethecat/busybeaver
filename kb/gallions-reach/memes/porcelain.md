# Porcelain

The Kuan-yin figurine on Jim Colet's desk is the novel's central symbol of enduring beauty — a fragile, luminous presence that stands against the crushing logic of commerce, violence, and mortality.

## In the Novel

Porcelain, and specifically the ivory-coloured figure of Kuan-yin, appears at critical moments as a talisman of spiritual value. Colet keeps the figure on his desk at Perriams: "the Chinese madonna, in porcelain the colour of ivory" [002-019]. It is the only object in the office that does not belong to the world of samples, ledgers, and profit — a silent witness to Colet's growing alienation.

When Colet flees after Perriam's death, he takes the figure with him. Boarding the Altair, he reasons that "it was better to help to save a good piece of porcelain than to hurry to give the police a job of work" [008-005]. The porcelain becomes his moral compass — its preservation a higher priority than legal procedure.

In Penang, Ah Loi's collection elevates porcelain to a philosophy. His gateway is "flanked by a pair of porcelain beasts that were not dragons and ought not to have been dogs" [024-002], and he invites Colet: "Come and see my porcelain" [024-036]. For Ah Loi, a "glaze for porcelain could persist, like the thought of an anonymous benevolence" [024-037] — the craft of ceramics becomes a metaphor for enduring human care across generations.

The novel's thematic argument crystallises in a single sharp line: "You want to treat a crude earth as if it were porcelain. You waste feeling on what will never know it" [004-062]. The accusation, aimed at Colet's sentimentality, actually affirms the novel's own position — that treating the crude earth as porcelain is precisely what gives life meaning.

## Interpretation

Porcelain in *Gallions Reach* functions as what might be called a "sacramental object" — a material thing that conveys spiritual meaning. The Kuan-yin figure is not worshipped; it is simply present, "her eyes lowered," making no demands. Yet its very fragility and beauty make a claim on Colet that the robust machinery of commerce cannot.

The contrast between porcelain and the "crude earth" of commerce [004-062] is not merely aesthetic. It is ethical. Porcelain requires patience, skill, and care to produce; commerce, in Perriam's formulation, requires the suppression of feeling. The novel sides unequivocally with the porcelain — with the fragile, the beautiful, and the carefully made.
