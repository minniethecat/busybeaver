# Power

In *Gallions Reach*, power is everywhere and always indifferent to human concerns. Tomlinson maps three registers of power — commercial, natural, and colonial — and shows how each dwarfs the individual. The novel's deepest insight is that these powers are not malicious but simply heedless: they operate at scales that render human intention irrelevant.

## In the Novel

Commercial power is embodied in Perriam's firm, "a house whose establishment and power was unquestioned" [002-006]. The voice that answers the telephone carries "the harsh and imperious warning of absolutism" [002-006]. Perriam himself is "a figure of lasting power, able to continue" [005-024], a man who has aligned himself with institutional force and become indistinguishable from it. Within this system, Colet is invisible — a clerk whose name does not appear on any brass plate.

The sea introduces a different order of power. The ship moves through "the welter of huge and heedless powers" [015-017], and the deckhand's laconic observation — "Man, yon's a sad waste o' power" [014-001] — captures the disproportion between human effort and natural force. The engine room is described in terms of "suppressed energy" like "a powerful engine" [002-011] that "kept the mind alert" — power as both physical and psychological pressure.

In the jungle, power becomes explicitly dark. The narrative speaks of being "enthralled by the powers of darkness" [014-019], of Colet being "watchful of vague and immense powers" [017-023], and of "the task of beguiling the greater powers" [022-013]. The final image is Colet "in the midst of dark powers; for the Malays were waiting" [027-028] — power that is human, racial, and political, no less indifferent than the sea.

The novel traces a progression: from the abstract power of commerce (brass plates, ledgers, hierarchies), through the physical power of nature (storms, seas, jungle), to the social power of colonialism (the Malay presence, the implicit threat). At each stage, Colet is acted upon rather than acting. He never acquires power; he learns to recognize its shape.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's treatment of power is shaped by his experience as a war correspondent and his familiarity with the shipping trade. Power in his rendering is not the heroic power of individual will but the systemic power of institutions and the elemental power of nature. The novel's political dimension emerges most clearly in the jungle sections, where "dark powers" [027-028] refers simultaneously to the Malay presence, the colonial apparatus, and something older and less nameable.

The novel's critique is not that power corrupts but that power is structurally indifferent. Perriam is not evil; he is "a figure of lasting power" — power that persists because it is embedded in institutions, not because it is exercised by malign individuals. The sea and the jungle are not hostile; they are "aloof from the desires and anxieties of man" [012-008]. This triple register — commercial, natural, colonial — gives the novel's treatment of power a complexity that moves beyond simple anti-capitalist or anti-imperialist readings.
