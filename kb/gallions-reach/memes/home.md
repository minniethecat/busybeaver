# Home

For Jim Colet, home is not a place but a question. London is where he lives but never belongs; the East is where he is a stranger but begins to feel at ease. The novel traces the slow dissolution of one home and the uncertain discovery of another.

## In the Novel

The word "home" echoes through *Gallions Reach* with shifting meanings. It begins as an ironic marker: "Nearly home, Jim" [001-004], spoken on the train from Manchester, but the London Colet returns to is not a place of comfort. Perriam's own relationship to domestic space is revealing: "It was his address, or his house; he never called it his home" [005-001]. The distinction between house and home is a fault line running through the novel's treatment of belonging.

Colet's sense of displacement in London is rooted in the commercial world's indifference to human connection. Walking past East India Avenue, he regrets arriving "too late to see the old home of John Company" [003-008], sensing that names once carried weight and words once haunted "like a ghost." The city's history of trade — its ships, routes, and "cabalistic port-marks" [002-022] — offers a romance that the present-day office cannot.

The crowd of "home-goers" streaming from Ludgate Circus [003-026] represents a belonging Colet cannot access. He is among them but not of them, preoccupied with private guilt while they hurry toward their families. His true departure from home comes when he leaves England entirely, standing on the deck of the Altair and feeling, paradoxically, that he "could not have left it so securely far behind" [007-009].

In the East, home becomes something provisional — a Chinese hotel in Penang, a jungle camp, the deck of a ship. The doctor's observation that Colet "felt more at home" [007-029] in unfamiliar surroundings suggests that home, for him, is not about place but about a state of mind: the absence of pretense.

## Interpretation

Home in *Gallions Reach* is treated as an existential condition rather than a geographic location. The novel suggests that the Victorian ideal of home — stable, domestic, rooted — is an illusion sustained by those who never question it. Colet's journey strips away that illusion, forcing him to find belonging in movement rather than settlement.

The novel's treatment of home anticipates modernist themes of displacement and exile. Colet is a precursor to the rootless protagonists of later twentieth-century fiction — not a cosmopolitan by choice but a man who has simply discovered that "home" is a word, not a fact.
