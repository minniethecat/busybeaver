# Norrie

Norrie is a fellow passenger aboard the *Altair* who becomes [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s companion through the latter half of the novel, from the [[the-sea|Indian Ocean]] crossing into the [[the-jungle|Malayan jungle]]. He is first introduced when [[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]] tells [[jim-colet|Colet]], "Gentleman I was talking to yesterday, Mr. Norrie. He's coming here" (021-056). Norrie's presence in the novel offers an alternative mode of being to [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s introspective intensity — he is a man of leisurely, humane pragmatism.

## Role in the Novel

Norrie functions as [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s foil and steadying companion. Where [[jim-colet|Colet]] broods and interiorizes, Norrie engages the world with genial practicality. He appears in the second half of [[colets-voyage|the voyage]], after the *Altair* has weathered its first storms, and becomes [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s closest confidant through the [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|shipwreck]] and jungle episodes.

[[jim-colet|Colet]] observes Norrie with analytical detachment: "[[jim-colet|Colet]], alone now in his bunk, considered Norrie, but not his suggestion" (021-061). Yet the relationship deepens through shared experience. By the time they reach [[penang|Penang]], Norrie's presence has become a "warrant of literal meaning" for [[jim-colet|Colet]] (026-002) — a guarantee that the world remains intelligible.

## Character Traits

Norrie is characterized by his leisurely, understated manner. He reclines on settees, "began to form leisurely clouds" of pipe smoke (021-067), and surveys the world with a kind of plaintive bemusement: "Norrie surveyed him plaintively" (022-011). His speech is colloquial and direct — "Not a bit. It shows your sound sense" (022-005) — in contrast to the elaborate consciousness of [[jim-colet|Colet]].

Despite his apparent ease, Norrie is capable of emphatic moral seriousness: "Norrie was sadly emphatic. 'Rather'" (021-076). This combination of lightness and gravity makes him one of the novel's most balanced characters.

## Significance

Norrie represents the novel's investment in ordinary human decency as a counterweight to metaphysical [[fear|terror]]. In a world of shipwrecks, jungle fevers, and the collapse of commercial certainties, Norrie's pipe smoke and leisurely presence are not trivialities — they are affirmations. His sadness ("sadly emphatic") and his geniality coexist without contradiction, making him one of the novel's most fully realized secondary characters.

[[jim-colet|Colet]]'s final judgment on Norrie — "I like Norrie. He would have been the same" in any era (024-028) — elevates him from a mere traveling companion to a symbol of durable humanity. He is what survives when [[london|the City]], [[billiter-avenue|the office]], and the measured world have fallen away.
