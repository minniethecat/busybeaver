# Dream

The dream motif runs through H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927) as a thread connecting the novel's opening on [[river-thames|the Thames]] to its climactic scenes in the [[the-jungle|Eastern jungle]]. It operates on multiple levels: as the literal dreams of sleeping characters, as the metaphorical "dream" of [[london|London]] itself, and as the existential force that drives men to leave home for the unknown.

## In the Novel

The dream is introduced in the very first chapter through the voice of an old sailor on [[the-altair|the steamer]] moving up[[the-sea|river]]: "The same old dream. I suppose we can't live without dreaming" (001-017). The [[the-sea|river]] [[the-sea|Thames]] itself is declared "a [[the-sea|river]] of dreams" — a liquid repository of human longing and departure (001-017). A fellow passenger affirms with conviction: "The dream is true, if you have it. There is nothing else" (001-020), establishing the dream as a source of authentic truth that rivals — and even surpasses — material reality.

In [[london|London]], young [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] perceives the city as "like the dream in which you stood by yourself at [[darkness|night]] and saw all the stars break loose and stream down the sky" (003-019). This cosmic dream-vision of [[london|London]] persists alongside the oldest stone in the city, suggesting the dream is as ancient and enduring as civilization itself.

At sea, the dream takes on a more dangerous character. [[jim-colet|Colet]] confronts "the monstrous adversary of a dream" (010-001), and the tropical [[the-sea|ocean]] becomes "but an apparition of [[beauty|splendour]]" (014-008), a bright dream that threatens to dissolve the boundaries of self. [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] standing watch in a storm becomes "a token of [[silence|quiet]] faith untouched by calamity" (017-023), his steady presence a living refutation of the dream's destructive potential.

## Related Characters

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The protagonist navigates between dreams and waking, his "boyish dream" persisting through the novel's trials.
- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The shipmaster whose watchful, undisturbed presence during a storm stands in contrast to the dissolving dream-world of [[china-sea|the sea]] (017-023).
- **The old sailor** — The unnamed voice in Chapter 1 who introduces the dream motif and calls [[river-thames|the Thames]] "a [[the-sea|river]] of dreams" (001-017).

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's dream is never merely escapist. It is the substance of human striving — "true if you have it" — yet also the source of profound disorientation. The novel charts a geography of dreams: [[river-thames|the river]] of dreams at [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]], the dream-city of [[london|London]], the apparitional splendor of [[the-sea|the sea]], and finally [[the-jungle|the jungle]] where [[jim-colet|Colet]] must distinguish dream from reality.

The dream's ambiguity is its power. It can be the "bright dream" that draws one out of one's body (014-008) or the [[darkness|night]]mare confusion where a man watches violence unfold "as though it were not real" (026-068). Tomlinson refuses to resolve whether dreaming is salvation or delusion — in the world of *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]*, it is both, and it is inescapable.

## Related Concepts

- **[[fate|Fate]]** — Closely paired with dream in the novel's opening meditation: "his [[fate|luck]], or fate, I forget. The same old dream" (001-017).
- **[[the-jungle|The Jungle]]** — The terminal landscape where the dream's ambiguity must be confronted directly.

- **[[darkness|Darkness]]** — The medium through which the dream is first perceived at [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]].
