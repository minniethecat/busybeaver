# Opium

The house of Perriam, for all its brass plates and commercial respectability, rests on a foundation of opium. The novel does not belabour the point — it mentions it once, in passing — but the ghost of the opium clipper haunts every ledger in Billiter Avenue.

## In the Novel

The origin story is delivered in a single paragraph, almost as an aside: "There was a Perriam who had been master and part-owner of an opium clipper. There is no need to say any more about him. He was the master of an opium smuggler, and he was the origin of the firm" [002-005]. The sentence "There is no need to say any more about him" is a masterpiece of narrative restraint — it implies that the facts speak for themselves, that the moral judgment is already made, that the opium trade is simply what it is.

But the novel does say more, indirectly. The portrait of the opium clipper's master hangs in the office, its "rigid mouth and aggressive chin" [002-010] exerting a posthumous influence on the firm's culture. Young Colet, "as a boy in that office," had admired the portrait, and its suggestions shaped his own appearance — he grew his beard "through the firm suggestion of the rigid mouth" [002-010]. The opium trader's face becomes the template for commercial masculinity: hard, unyielding, predatory.

In the jungle, the opium theme resurfaces in a different register. Norrie observes the Chinese cook's habits: "If he wasn't so careful with the stuff I'd be afraid he'd drop some of his opium into the grub" [028-028]. The cook's opium use is entangled with his religious practices — the "little saucers of rice under one of the trees" for the devils he knows about. Opium here is not commerce but ritual, not profit but propitiation. The arc from the opium clipper to the cook's saucers traces the commodity's transformation from imperial trade good to personal sacrament.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's treatment of opium is characteristically indirect. He does not write an anti-opium polemic; he simply notes the fact and lets it resonate. The opium clipper is the repressed origin of Perriams, Limited — the violence and illegality that the brass plate and the ledgers are designed to conceal. That Colet modelled his own face on the opium trader's portrait is an irony the novel savours: the man who will eventually reject commerce altogether began by internalising its most predatory features.

The juxtaposition of the opium clipper (imperial commerce, profit, violence) with the Chinese cook's opium (ritual, personal, domestic) complicates any simple moral judgment. Opium is both the substance of empire and the solace of the colonised; its meaning depends on whose hands it is in and whose gods it is offered to.
