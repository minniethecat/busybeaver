# Fear

Fear permeates H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927) as a constant companion to its characters, from [[the-altair|the ship]]ping office in [[london|London]] to [[storm-in-the-china-sea|the storm]]-tossed deck of the *Altair* and the untraversed jungle. Tomlinson treats fear not as cowardice but as a rational response to genuinely overwhelming circumstances — a register of human vulnerability.

## In the Novel

Fear enters the novel through the domestic sphere. [[mrs-perriam|Mrs. Perriam]] withdraws at "the first wave of disturbance sent before [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s car" (005-002), her fear a silent, practiced ritual of self-preservation. Her husband, [[the-altair|the ship]]ping magnate, embodies the fear he inspires in others, ascending the stairs "in heavy deliberation, like [[fate|destiny]]" (005-009). [[jim-colet|Jimmy]] [[jim-colet|Colet]], facing [[perriam|Perriam]], knows "his fear was both natural and scrupulous" (005-002) — it is not weakness but accurate perception.

At sea, fear acquires cosmic dimensions. [[jim-colet|Colet]] "turned his head in measuring fear from the door aft to the head of [[the-altair|the Altair]]" as [[the-altair|the ship]] founders (018-067). The physical spacing of fear — the distance between the wheelhouse door and the forecastle deck — becomes a topography of peril. The seas pour "solidly across her middle," and fear is simply the correct reading of the situation.

Tomlinson offers a counterpoint through a cynical self-awareness: "Reason could always justify fears and [[love|desire]]s" (008-006). Fear, like [[love|desire]], can be rationalized after the fact — but this rationalization does not diminish its reality.

The novel's later movement reverses the relationship between fear and the world. In the primeval jungle, "sounds were clearer and without fear" (030-010) — as if fear were a product of civilization rather than nature, shed as the characters move deeper into [[the-jungle|the wild]].

## Key Passages

> "He was coming up the stairs in heavy deliberation, like destiny. No escape now; might kill the beggar, though." (005-009)

> "Colet turned his head in measuring fear from the door aft to the head of the Altair. Her forecastle deck was isolated, a raft of wreckage flush with the swirls and foam." (018-067)

> "Reason could always justify fears and desires." (008-006)

## Related Characters

- **[[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]** — The embodiment of fear in the commercial world, his approach described "like [[fate|destiny]]" (005-009).
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The protagonist whose fear is portrayed as natural and scrupulous rather than shameful (005-002).
- **[[mrs-perriam|Mrs. Perriam]]** — Her silent withdrawal at her husband's approach illustrates the domestic dimension of fear (005-002).

## Interpretation

Fear in *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* is not a flaw to be overcome but a faculty to be read. It registers real threats — the tyrant employer, [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|the foundering]] ship, the untraversed jungle. Tomlinson's innovation is to portray fear without moral judgment. [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s fear is "natural and scrupulous" (005-002); it coexists with courage rather than negating it.

The novel's geography maps a progression of fears: domestic fear (the [[perriam|Perriam]] household), social fear ([[billiter-avenue|the office]]), natural fear ([[storm-in-the-china-sea|the storm]] at sea), and finally the absence of fear in the primeval jungle where "sounds were clearer and without fear" (030-010). This trajectory suggests that fear is bound up with civilization itself — shed only when one enters a world too elemental for human categories.

## Related Concepts

- **[[fate|Fate]]** — Fear and fate are intertwined: [[perriam|Perriam]] approaches "like [[fate|destiny]]" (005-009), and fear is often the anticipation of an inescapable outcome.
- **[[courage|Courage]]** — The novel's counterpoint to fear, embodied in [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]'s steady watchfulness.
- **[[dream|Dream]]** — Like dream, fear blurs the boundary between internal and external reality.
