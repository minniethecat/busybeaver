# Silence

Silence in *Gallions Reach* is more than the absence of sound — it is an atmospheric presence that shapes how characters perceive their world, an absence that speaks, and a narrative device Tomlinson uses to create tension and introspection.

## As Narrative Atmosphere

Silence functions as a backdrop against which key moments unfold. In the novel's opening, "the lights went by in silence" [001-024], establishing a mood of quiet observation that persists throughout. The heat itself becomes a form of silence: "The silence was the heat. The heat was music" [025-007], blurring sensory categories.

## The Unsaid and the Unspoken

Silence often marks moments where speech fails or is withheld. A character waits "patiently for silence to fall" [005-024], suggesting that silence is the natural state and speech the interruption. When Colet "thought it better for the silence if he did not move" [008-004], the silence has become an active presence worthy of consideration.

## Silence as Foreboding

The novel's silences frequently carry an ominous weight. "That silence and brooding obscurity would never vouchsafe a new light" [013-010] — here silence is paired with darkness, becoming a hostile force that withholds revelation. After Perriam's death, "there was a long silence; nothing was to be heard" [019-010], and the silence becomes the vessel for unspoken guilt.

## Silence and Power Dynamics

Silence is also a tool of assertion and resistance. Men listen "in sceptical silence" [008-002], and a figure confronts another "with lowering insolence in silence" [006-014]. In these moments, silence is not passive — it is a deliberate withdrawal that carries its own force.
