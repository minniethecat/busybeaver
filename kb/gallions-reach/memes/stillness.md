# Stillness

Stillness in *Gallions Reach* is not merely the absence of sound or movement. It is a condition of perception — the state in which Colet, momentarily freed from the press of events, sees his situation clearly. The novel's stillnesses are its moments of revelation.

## In the Novel

The novel's first stillness is the office at Perriams, Limited: "an interior of imperturbable calm... It would have been impious to fracture its lucid stillness with a rude note" [002-006]. The hush is the acoustic signature of commercial power — the silence of clerks bent over ledgers, the quiet in which "pens could be heard adding to the treasure of numerals" [002-006]. This stillness is not peace but suppression: the enforced quiet of men who dare not raise their voices.

At sea, stillness becomes meditative. Alone on deck at night, Colet sits until "the monody of the waters, the far points of light, and a thin drizzle which began, all blurred into a stillness within which his waking mind became like one of the stars sunk deeply in the void" [006-052]. The self dissolves into the stillness; the boundary between observer and observed blurs. This is the novel's most sustained account of what stillness can do: it can absorb the self.

In the jungle, stillness takes on an oppressive character. The forest closes in: "their way was now at the bottom of a chasm. The dark forest was its walls" [036-011]. Here stillness is not meditative but stagnant — the heat concentrates, the air does not move, and Colet lapses into "limp but enduring desire, a desire for shade, for a break in the journey" [036-011]. Stillness becomes the physical correlative of exhaustion.

The novel's final register of stillness is almost submarine: "those rocks and tree-trunks were inhuman, as if, in that glassy stillness, they were shapes at the bottom of the sea" [038-021]. The jungle is revealed as a drowned world, and stillness is the medium of its alienness. In this glassy immobility, Colet confronts what is most foreign in the landscape — and in himself.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson uses stillness as a narrative punctuation — a pause in the action during which the novel's deeper concerns surface. The three stillnesses (office, sea, jungle) correspond to three modes of being: suppression, meditation, and confrontation. In the office, stillness is imposed; at sea, it is discovered; in the jungle, it is endured.

The progression is not toward peace but toward clarity. The "glassy stillness" of the jungle [038-021] is not comforting — the rocks and trees are "inhuman" — but it is truthful. What Colet finds in stillness is not solace but the world as it is, without the consolations of commerce, society, or even human companionship. This is the novel's most austere wisdom: stillness reveals, and what it reveals may not be what we wished to see.
