# Captain Bennett

Captain Bennett appears in a single scene — Chapter 7 — where he interviews [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] before the *Altair*'s departure. His skepticism about [[the-altair|the ship]] provides an early note of foreboding that the reader, like [[jim-colet|Colet]], may not yet fully appreciate.

## Role in the Novel

Bennett functions as a harbinger. When another character addresses him — "Why, yes, Captain Bennett, plenty in it. You are unjust" (007-007) — the exchange immediately establishes Bennett as someone whose judgment is being challenged on the subject of the *Altair*'s fitness. His response is laughter: "Captain Bennett laughed rudely" (007-018), the rudeness itself a sign that he knows more than he is willing to say. He "grumbled" (007-031) rather than spoke, and was "entangled for a moment" (007-022) by the conversation — the verb suggesting a man caught between professional courtesy and the impulse to warn.

## Character Traits

Bennett is gruff, skeptical, and unwilling to flatter. His rudeness is not malice but a kind of honesty — the honesty of a man who has spent enough years at sea to dis[[faith|trust]] easy [[hope|optimism]]. He does not tell [[jim-colet|Colet]] what to do; he merely conveys, through his manner, that the *Altair* is not a ship he would choose for himself.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 7** — Bennett interviews [[jim-colet|Colet]] before [[colets-voyage|the voyage]]; smiles sideways at [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s enthusiasm (007-007); laughs rudely (007-018); is "entangled for a moment" by the conversation (007-022); grumbles a final warning (007-031); and speaks before "all who were looking on" (007-035).

## Relationships

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The young purser Bennett interviews and implicitly warns about the *Altair*.

## Significance

Bennett is a minor character whose narrative function is to create dramatic irony. The reader, having heard Bennett's skepticism and witnessed his sideways smile, boards the *Altair* with a sense of unease that [[jim-colet|Colet]] himself lacks. In this, Bennett belongs to a long literary tradition: the character at the threshold who knows what lies ahead but cannot — or will not — say it plainly.

## Notable Quotes
