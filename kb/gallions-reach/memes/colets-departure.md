# Colet's Departure from London

[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s departure from [[london|London]] is the novel's inciting event — the moment when the protagonist stops being "one of the multitude which entered this region every morning at nine o'clock and deserted it about six in the evening" (002-003) and becomes something else. It is a departure without ceremony, without farewell speeches, without even a clear sense of destination. [[jim-colet|Colet]] leaves because he must — because [[perriam|Perriam]]'s judgment, [[helen-denny|Helen]]'s wandering attention, and [[colets-father|his father]]'s definition have made [[london|London]] uninhabitable.

## The Event

The departure is prepared through the novel's first six chapters — [[billiter-avenue|the office]], the social gatherings, the bookshop windows — and executed in the transitional space of the docks. [[jim-colet|Colet]] moves from [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]] to [[the-docks|the waterfront]], from the measured time of [[london|the City]] to the tidal time of [[china-sea|the sea]]. The *Altair*'s departure is not dramatized as a single moment but as a gradual shift: [[london|the City]] recedes, [[river-thames|the river]] widens, [[china-sea|the sea]] begins.

## Significance

[[jim-colet|Colet]]'s departure is the novel's founding gesture of freedom. It is not heroic — [[jim-colet|Colet]] is no adventurer — but it is irreversible. To leave [[london|London]] is to leave the father's definition, [[perriam|Perriam]]'s judgment, the privet hedge, the grey limestone and glass. The departure makes everything that follows possible, and everything that follows will test whether the departure was worth making.
