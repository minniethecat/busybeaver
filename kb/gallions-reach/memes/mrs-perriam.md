# Mrs. Perriam

Mrs. Perriam is one of the novel's most spectral presences — a woman defined almost entirely by her absence. She appears in only two [[time|passage]]s, and in both she is withdrawing: from the hall, from the telephone, from the domestic space that her husband's approach has rendered uninhabitable. Her silence is the novel's most economical indictment of the world [[perriam|Perriam]] represents.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Perriam functions as the domestic complement to her husband's commercial authority. Where [[perriam|Perriam]] dominates through presence — his "heavy deliberation" on the stairs, his massive face, his waiting without movement — Mrs. Perriam registers the cost of that domination through withdrawal.

"Mrs. Perriam was represented in the hall by" her absence (005-002) — the passive construction is precise. She is not present; she is "represented," her existence acknowledged only through the signs of her departure. She retreats "at the first wave of disturbance sent before [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s car" (005-002), the vehicle's approach sending a "wave" before it — the language of force, of physics, of inevitability. [[perriam|Perriam]]'s authority precedes him, and his wife has learned to read its approach and absent herself.

When [[jim-colet|Colet]] considers telephoning — "Should he telephone to Mrs. Perriam? 'Is that Mrs. Perriam? I have'" (006-004) — the fragmentary dialogue suggests a conversation that never quite happens, a connection never fully made.

## Character Traits

Mrs. Perriam is characterized exclusively through negative space. We never hear her speak; we never see her face. She is the shape left by a withdrawal, the silence after a door has closed. Her defining action is retreat — not flight, which would imply agency, but withdrawal, which implies accommodation to an inevitable force.

Her relationship to her husband is not described but demonstrated through spatial dynamics: she leaves when he arrives. The [[brixton|Brixton]] house is large enough for two people but not large enough for both of them to occupy it simultaneously.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 5** — Mrs. Perriam's withdrawal from the hall as her husband's car approaches (005-002).
- **Chapter 6** — [[jim-colet|Colet]] considers telephoning Mrs. Perriam; the fragmentary dialogue suggests an abortive connection (006-004).

## Relationships

- **[[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]** — Her husband, whose approach she has learned to anticipate and avoid (005-002).
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — [[perriam|Perriam]]'s employee, who considers telephoning her (006-004).
- [[brixton|Brixton]] — The domestic space from which she withdraws, and which her husband's authority has rendered uninhabitable.

## Significance

Mrs. Perriam is the novel's most radical exercise in characterization through absence. She matters precisely because she is not there — because her withdrawal testifies to what [[perriam|Perriam]]'s authority costs. The novel never condemns her husband directly; it does not need to. Mrs. Perriam's silent retreat says everything.

Her near-invisibility also reflects the novel's limitations in representing female experience. [[helen-denny|Helen Denny]] gets wandering attention and a hand on [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s arm; [[doris-oliver|Doris Oliver]] gets arched eyebrows and swaying beads; Mrs. Perriam gets only withdrawal. The novel's women are defined by their relationship to men — and Mrs. Perriam, by the fact that she cannot bear to be in the same house as hers.
