# Ignorance

In H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927), ignorance is not a simple absence of knowledge but a condition that characters inhabit and, in moments of crisis, recognize in themselves. [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s dawning awareness of his own ignorance — of the wider world beyond [[london|London]], of the consequences of his actions, of the forces shaping his life — becomes one of the novel's engines of transformation.

## In the Novel

[[jim-colet|Colet]]'s recognition of his ignorance is framed as both a limitation and a spur. His "fear was both natural and scrupulous, that ignorance and folly, while he was away, had deflected the orderly directions of his authority" (005-002). Here ignorance is something that acts in his absence — a force that warps the order he has tried to establish. The irony is that his own ignorance of the world beyond the office is precisely what enables that "orderly" authority to function; his knowledge is local and his ignorance is vast.

When confronted with the unfamiliar, [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s response is a kind of humble admission: "it was only his ignorance" (014-014). This is a moment of self-diagnosis rather than self-condemnation. To name one's ignorance is already to begin moving beyond it.

The novel's most explicit statement on ignorance comes as a declaration of temperament: "I'm so uncomfortable when in ignorance that even an unseen novelty anywhere near will make my nose twitch till I find it" (028-026). This restlessness in the face of the unknown — the refusal to remain comfortable in ignorance — drives the novel's outward movement from [[london|London]] to [[the-sea|the sea]] and the jungle. Ignorance, in this formulation, is not a moral failing but an intolerable state, and the cure is exploration.

## Key Passages

> "He knew his fear was both natural and scrupulous, that ignorance and folly, while he was away, had deflected the orderly directions of his authority." (005-002)

> "It was only his ignorance." (014-014)

## Related Concepts

- **[[knowledge|Knowledge]]** — The state ignorance aspires toward; [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s journey is from ignorance of the wider world toward a hard-won knowledge.
- **[[fear|Fear]]** — Linked with ignorance in Colet's self-assessment (005-002); fear is both caused by ignorance and the spur to overcome it.
- **[[wonder|Wonder]]** — The emotional response that accompanies the transition from ignorance to discovery.
- **[[escape|Escape]]** — Ignorance as a condition to escape from, parallel to Colet's physical escape from [[london|London]].
