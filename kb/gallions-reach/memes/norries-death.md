# Norrie's Death

Norrie's death from malaria in the jungle interior is the emotional centre of the novel's final third — a quiet, almost wordless extinction that leaves Colet alone with strangers in a strange land and forces him to confront the indifference of the world to human attachment.

## Summary

Norrie falls ill during the jungle expedition. The fever takes him rapidly: "Norrie sank into his hammock, and remained, still and yellow, with his eyes shut, as though dead" [030-015]. Colet doses him with quinine but the drug cannot keep pace with the disease. Norrie calls out once — "but when Colet went to him the sick man was moribund, with his eyes closed. The light died. The uproar in the woods instantly ceased. Night put out both day and the paean" [030-017]. Colet sits on a packing box beside the hammock through the night, waiting: "He touched Norrie's face; it was indifferent; it was hot and dry. What happened to men with malaria?" [030-017].

## Participants

- **[[norrie]]** — The pragmatic, worldly-wise drifter who becomes Colet's truest companion; his death leaves Colet alone in a way no other loss in the novel achieves
- **[[jim-colet]]** — Keeps vigil through the night; the death marks his final detachment from the world of London and Billiter Avenue

## Narrative Context

Norrie's death follows the failed expedition with Parsell and precedes Colet's feverish return to civilisation. It is the novel's pivot from adventure to aftermath. Norrie had been Colet's protector in the East — the man who knew the shops, the languages, the shortcuts — and without him Colet must navigate the return alone. When Colet later walks into the Penang hotel, he sees "over in that corner was where Norrie had talked to him, the night before they left to go round the coast" [039-001], and the memory is both comfort and wound.

## Key Passages

> "Norrie sank into his hammock, and remained, still and yellow, with his eyes shut, as though dead. The camp that evening suggested a depth in solitude which was more remote than Colet had ever known." [030-015]

> "The light died. The uproar in the woods instantly ceased. Night put out both day and the paean. The darkness and the silence were the same. Colet sat down on a packing box by the hammock, to wait." [030-017]

The death is notable for what it lacks — no last words, no dramatic farewell, no meaning extracted. The jungle offers no consolation. The darkness and silence that follow are absolute, and Colet's vigil is the novel's purest expression of companionship: simply staying, because there is nothing else to do.
