# The Father's Pointing

The novel opens with a gesture: a father pointing toward [[london|London]] from the darkness of [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]. "There you are, [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]. See it? [[london|London]], my boy" (001-005). This pointing is the novel's inaugural speech act — a naming, a definition, a world summoned into being by a father's voice. Everything that follows is, in some sense, a response to this gesture.

## The Event

The scene is brief but foundational. [[jim-colet|Colet]], a child, stands on [[the-altair|the steamer]] with [[colets-father|his father]]. The father points toward the darkness where [[london|London]]'s smoke is visible. "There's the smoke of it. [[london|London]]" (001-002). The child looks — "He wanted to see what [[london|London]] meant" (001-003) — but sees only "the solid" darkness. The father's words promise meaning that the child cannot yet perceive.

[[jim-colet|Colet]] looks at [[colets-father|his father]], but "his elders had forgotten him" (001-006). The forgetting is as significant as the pointing. The father defines the world and then turns away, leaving the child alone with a definition he cannot fully grasp.

## Significance

The father's pointing is the novel's primal scene — the moment when the world is named and the name is found wanting. [[jim-colet|Colet]] will spend the rest of the novel trying to see what [[london|London]] means, and his journey will teach him that [[london|London]]'s meaning is not what [[colets-father|his father]] thought it was. The pointing finger defines a world; the journey dissolves that definition and builds another in its place.
