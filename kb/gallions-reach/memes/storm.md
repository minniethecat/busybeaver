# Storm

Storms in *Gallions Reach* are never merely weather. They are the jungle's voice — the periodic reminder that human plans are contingent on forces that do not consult human wishes. The novel's storms structure its final movement, gathering, breaking, and passing with the rhythm of a second narrative running beneath the human one.

## In the Novel

The first storm warning comes as the party enters the jungle interior: the sky holds "the lurking masses of a thunderstorm in reserve for the evening" [026-042]. The phrase "in reserve" is characteristic — the storm is not an accident but a resource, held back for the moment of maximum narrative effect. Tomlinson treats the tropical storm as a dramatist treats a revelation: it must be prepared, delayed, and then released.

The jungle's humidity makes the storm feel inevitable: "neither perspiration nor another storm could saturate them more" [028-033]. The men are already as wet as the storm could make them; the distinction between inside and outside, between the body and the weather, has collapsed. The storm is not an external event but a condition they already inhabit.

In the deepest jungle, the storm becomes a daily ritual: Colet watches "the pitchy masses" gather "with the threat of the daily storm" [036-013]. The regularity of the storm — its daily return — makes it both more bearable and more oppressive. It is no longer a crisis but a clock.

The novel's final approach to storm is its most elemental. As the party struggles through the jungle, "the evening storm was near. That was what it was" [038-030]. The sentence strips the storm of metaphor — it is simply what it is, a fact of the equatorial afternoon. But the next passage restores the metaphorical dimension: "The storm was coming. What about repeating..." [038-033]. The storm breaks "like the sea breaking" — the jungle's own sea, a vertical ocean descending from the sky.

## Interpretation

The storms of *Gallions Reach* serve a double function. As realism, they anchor the jungle chapters in the actual climate of the Malay Peninsula, where tropical thunderstorms are a daily occurrence. As metaphor, they externalise the turbulence of Colet's inner journey — the gathering darkness, the waiting, the release, the drenched aftermath.

Tomlinson's storms are Conradian in their indifference to human purposes. They do not punish or reward; they simply are. The characters who fare best in the storms are those who, like Mat, accept them as facts rather than interpreting them as omens. The storm is the novel's purest expression of the natural world's autonomy — its refusal to mean anything beyond itself.
