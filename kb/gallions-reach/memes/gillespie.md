# Gillespie

Gillespie is the chief engineer of the *Altair*, the steamship that carries [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] to the East. He is "old Gillespie" (013-014), a veteran whose relationship to [[china-sea|the sea]] is mediated through engines rather than sails, through [[the-altair|the ship]]'s mechanical heart rather than its navigational brain. His domain is the engine-room, and his authority is that of the man who keeps [[the-altair|the ship]] moving.

## Role in the Novel

Gillespie represents the industrial working class in the novel's social spectrum. He is first mentioned when the pilot is still aboard — "the pilot is still with Mr. Gillespie" (009-011) — positioning him in the engine-room while [[billiter-avenue|the office]]rs occupy the bridge. This spatial separation marks a social distinction that the novel honors without romanticizing.

His function is both practical and symbolic. Practically, he keeps the engines turning. Symbolically, he embodies a form of knowledge — mechanical, physical, experiential — that complements [[captain-hale|Hale]]'s navigational authority and [[sinclair|Sinclair]]'s theatrical competence. When he speaks, it is with the authority of a man who knows machines: "Man, ye can do n'more" (018-043), he tells a crewman, the Scottish cadence of his speech marking his origins.

## Character Traits

Gillespie is characterized by physical expressiveness and a gruff warmth. He "shook his paw in appreciative" laughter (014-004), his hand likened to an animal's paw — a detail that links him to the physical, pre-civilized world that the novel increasingly valorizes. He glares "malignantly at old Gillespie" (013-014) — the reflexive hostility of the chief officer toward his engineer, which the novel presents as a kind of professional theater.

His gentleness emerges in crisis. "Presently Gillespie spoke to him" (019-012), breaking a tense silence with words of reassurance. When he "gently pressed his knee" (019-033) in the darkness, the gesture speaks more than words could. Gillespie's physicality — his paw-like hand, his gentle pressure — makes him the novel's most tactile character, a man whose relationship to the world is mediated through touch rather than abstraction.

His retort to [[sinclair|Sinclair]] is one of the novel's comic highlights: "And macadam, Gillespie. Don't forget that" / "I say, Gillespie. Didn't I ever tell you?" (012-003, 012-006) — the banter between engineer and officer establishing a workplace intimacy that cuts across rank.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 9** — Gillespie is first mentioned, the pilot still with him in the engine-room (009-011).
- **Chapter 12** — Gillespie's banter with [[sinclair|Sinclair]] reveals the social texture of shipboard life (012-003, 012-006).
- **Chapter 13** — "Engine-room!" The chief officer summons Gillespie during a crisis (013-014).
- **Chapter 14** — Gillespie "shook his paw in appreciative" laughter, the novel's most vivid physical characterization (014-004).
- **Chapters 18–19** — Gillespie's presence during [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|the shipwreck]] and his [[silence|quiet]] words of comfort to [[jim-colet|Colet]] (018-043, 019-012, 019-033).

## Relationships

- **[[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]]** — The chief officer with whom Gillespie maintains a working-class banter that is both [[love|affection]]ate and combative (012-003, 013-014).
- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The master under whom Gillespie serves; their relationship represents the division between bridge and engine-room.
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The landsman passenger whom Gillespie comforts with wordless physical presence during the crisis (019-033).

## Significance

Gillespie is the novel's most sustained portrait of working-class life at sea. He is neither idealized nor patronized: his competence is genuine, his gruffness is authentic, and his moments of tenderness are earned rather than sentimental. The "paw" that shakes in laughter is the same hand that gently presses [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s knee in the darkness — a continuity of physical expression that the novel presents as a form of integrity.

In a novel preoccupied with the inadequacy of language — the failure of [[london|London]]'s commercial speech, the silence of the jungle — Gillespie's tactile communication represents an alternative mode of human connection. He does not need to speak to comfort; his hand on [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s knee says everything.
