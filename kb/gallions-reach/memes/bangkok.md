# Bangkok

Bangkok appears only fleetingly in H. M. Tomlinson's *Gallions Reach*, invoked as one of the far-eastern ports that mark the limits of a maritime life. It is a name freighted with nostalgia and regret, not a place described in any detail.

## Location & Context

Bangkok, the capital of Siam, was a regular port of call on the Southeast Asian shipping routes in the early twentieth century. In the novel, it belongs to the geography of the *Altair*'s ship's surgeon — a man who "coasted in the China Sea" in his youth and now, older and disillusioned, finds himself "sent on uninteresting routes" [007-013].

## Role in the Narrative

Bangkok enters the novel through a single scene aboard the *Altair*. The ship's doctor, contemplating the captain with quiet disdain, thinks back to his younger days: "It was years since he himself coasted in the China Sea. Out there were the coasts of youth." He longs for "another chance to visit the ruins of Angkor" and inwardly scoffs that "a lot of interest Bangkok, or the ruins of a forgotten civilisation, would be to him" — judging the captain, with his "thin hair" and "insignificant mouth," as a man of no imagination [007-013].

Later, the doctor asks the passenger Colet directly: "You know Bangkok, Cambodia, those places, sir?" [007-014]. Colet's reply — "Pretty well. All round from that purgatory to Bangkok" [021-028] — suggests a man who has travelled these waters extensively, though the novel never shows him there.

## Atmosphere & Description

There is no direct description of Bangkok in the novel. It exists only as a name in a remembered geography, grouped with "Cambodia, those places" [007-014] — a shorthand for the exotic East that the doctor has lost and Colet knows. Its narrative function is not descriptive but emotional: Bangkok is a coordinate on the map of lost youth.
