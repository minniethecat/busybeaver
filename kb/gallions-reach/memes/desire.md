# Desire

*Gallions Reach* maps desire across its full register: from the hunger for immortality inscribed in brass on City walls, through commercial ambition, to the "limp but enduring desire" for nothing more than shade in the jungle. The novel asks what happens to desire when its objects are stripped away one by one.

## In the Novel

The novel opens with an image of desire made visible: the acres of brass nameplates on the walls of Billiter Avenue, "the marks of anxious men who desired a lasting impress of their insignificance" [002-002]. These inscriptions are a monument to the most fundamental human desire — the wish not to be forgotten. Yet the novel immediately undercuts this: the names are "no more important, famous, or delectable than those cut into trees or on tombstones" [002-002]. Desire for permanence, Tomlinson suggests, is universal but futile.

Colet himself is invisible behind Perriam's brass plate: an explorer "questing those acres of brass plates for a clue to a man he desired to find, could never happen on Colet at all" [002-004]. He is a man whose desires have not yet found expression — buried under the commercial identity that will soon be shed.

At sea, desire meets its cosmic limit. The sea and sky "were aloof from the desires and anxieties of man" [012-008]. This is the novel's recurring gesture: placing human longing against an indifferent universe, not to mock it but to measure it. In Port Said, by contrast, desire is reduced to its basest form: "purveyors of cosmopolitan lesions enticed with the smirking confidence that the desires of their own species were well known to them" [013-007]. The city is a marketplace of appetites, and the Canal offers "a lucky way of escape" [013-007].

The jungle compresses desire to its essence. Having found the tin — "the security which most men desire but usually fail to reach" [032-001] — Colet discovers that the attainment of a long-sought object brings no satisfaction. The campfire "burned with less of its old companionable light" [032-001]. His response is unexpected: "Almost impious to say damn the tin, but Colet had that desire" [032-007]. What he desires is not wealth but release from the obligation to want it.

In the deepest jungle, desire is stripped to the physical: "He lapsed gradually into limp but enduring desire, a desire for shade, for a break in the journey" [036-011]. The progression is complete — from brass-plate immortality to the simple wish for rest. Mr. Parsell, the ethnologist who "desired nothing better than an opportunity to meditate" [036-010], models an alternative: desire channeled into contemplation rather than acquisition.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson treats desire as a shape-shifter: it attaches to objects (brass plates, tin, a woman), then to states (security, escape), and finally to simplicities (shade, rest). This trajectory mirrors Colet's spiritual journey from the City to the jungle, and it aligns with the novel's implicit critique of commerce. Commerce, in Tomlinson's rendering, is desire institutionalized — a system that channels human longing into ledgers and brass plates.

The novel's wisdom about desire is practical rather than ascetic. It does not preach the extinction of desire but its clarification. When Colet says "damn the tin" [032-007], he is not renouncing desire but redirecting it — from what others want for him to what he wants for himself. The final figure of desire in the novel is the "limp but enduring" wish for shade [036-011]: irreducible, physical, honest. What survives the journey is not the grand ambition but the simple need.
