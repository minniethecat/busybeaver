# Solitude

Colet is alone from the moment he leaves Perriam's office for the last time. But solitude in *Gallions Reach* is not mere loneliness — it is the condition of possibility for his transformation.

## In the Novel

Solitude appears first in the urban context of London, where "charwomen with their pails who haunt the temporary solitude" [002-003] of empty offices are the only witnesses to the spaces Colet is about to abandon. The solitude of the office after hours — its stillness, its indifference — previews the larger solitudes to come.

At sea, solitude becomes cosmic. The dawn watch reveals "an immensity of light. It was solitude" [014-005]. The sea's emptiness mirrors Colet's internal state — a clearing out of the social self, leaving only the raw fact of existence.

The jungle deepens solitude into something almost metaphysical. Colet "foresaw that solitude in the jungle might plumb to depths where no light had ever been" [027-015]. The huts in the clearing seem "to note this invasion of its solitude" [027-018] — solitude here is not a personal condition but a property of the landscape itself, a watchful emptiness.

The old Malay who descends "the ladder of his home to them as coolly as though such visits were ordinary" [027-018] represents the other side of solitude: not isolation but self-possession. He is alone without being lonely, inhabiting his solitude without fear.

## Interpretation

Solitude in *Gallions Reach* is the necessary medium of Colet's transformation. London offers no solitude — only loneliness within a crowd. The sea offers the first taste of true solitude: the self alone with elements that do not care. The jungle offers the deepest solitude: the self alone with the knowledge that the world was here long before and will be here long after.

Tomlinson treats solitude not as a problem to be solved but as a truth to be accepted. The novel's movement is from the false community of commerce (where everyone is connected by transaction but no one is truly present) to the true solitude of the wild (where no one is connected but everything is real).
