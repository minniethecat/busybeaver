# Noise

Noise functions as a sensory motif in H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927), tracing [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]'s movement from the commercial din of [[london|London]] toward the elemental sounds of the sea and jungle. The novel uses noise to mark thresholds between worlds — the clamour of the city, the silence of the office, the wind on deck, and the alien sounds of the forest.

## In the Novel

The novel's treatment of noise begins in [[london|London]]'s commercial heart, where "that sudden noise in the plaster heaven of the office was the harsh and imperious warning of absolutism" (002-006). The office noise is not merely acoustic but political: it signifies the authority of [[perriam|Perriams, Limited]] and the commercial order that [[jim-colet|Colet]] must obey.

At the restaurant where [[jim-colet|Colet]] dines with [[helen-denny|Helen Denny]], noise becomes ambiance: "the long room, with its vistas deepening into a sort of maroon haze, was warm and chromatic, and sparkling with eager noises at the level of the table lights" (004-020). This urban noise — social, warm, chromatic — contrasts with the mechanical imperative of the office.

As [[jim-colet|Colet]] moves toward escape, noise transforms. On the river at night, "there was but a continuous and strenuous noise" (005-024) — an anonymous, elemental sound that differs from both office and restaurant. At sea, noise becomes the wind's voice: "I only made the noise of a thought" (021-051), as if noise itself could be rendered into something like contemplation.

In the jungle, noise takes on a threatening quality. The slashing of parangs becomes "as though the sanctity of an inviolable concealment were being riven" (030-012). A noise "up above, like the sea breaking" (038-033) signals danger that cannot be seen. The novel's trajectory — from manufactured noise to natural noise, from the known to the unknown — charts [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s own transformation from office functionary to man confronting the elemental.

## Key Passages

> "That sudden noise in the plaster heaven of the office was the harsh and imperious warning of absolutism." (002-006)

> "The long room, with its vistas deepening into a sort of maroon haze, was warm and chromatic, and sparkling with eager noises at the level of the table lights." (004-020)

> "There was but a continuous and strenuous noise." (005-024)

> "I only made the noise of a thought." (021-051)

> "And it was the only sound, except the occasional slashing of the parangs of the Malays, and that noise was as though the sanctity of an inviolable concealment were being riven." (030-012)

## Related Concepts

- **[[silence|Silence]]** — The absence against which noise is measured; the novel's progression is toward increasingly meaningful silences.
- **[[fear|Fear]]** — Noise often signals or amplifies fear in the novel, as when Colet "made less noise" to avoid detection (006-046).
- **[[london|London]]** — The source of the novel's urban noise, the world Colet must leave behind.
- **[[the-sea|The Sea]]** — The realm where noise becomes elemental — wind, wave, storm.
