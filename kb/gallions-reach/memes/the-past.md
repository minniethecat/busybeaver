# The Past

The past in *Gallions Reach* is not a settled record but an active, shaping presence — a weight that bears down on the present and a ghost that haunts every new beginning. Colet's journey eastward is, in part, an attempt to escape "the worst consequences of the past" (PN 005-008), to be "born again."

## The Weight of History

Tomlinson uses the material environment of London to make the past tangible. The City's institutional buildings look down "from their height and from the past upon a visitor, with fixity" (PN 002-005). History is architectural — stone walls that have witnessed generations of clerks and merchants. A book by Ballantyne serves as "a reminder of the past" (PN 003-007), a childhood memory that conjures the lost romance of adventure before it was replaced by commerce.

## Memory and Escape

Colet's desire to escape his past is one of the novel's driving impulses. "He would be born again" (PN 005-008) — the phrase is deliberately biblical, framing his departure as a kind of resurrection. But the past cannot be so easily shed. Throughout his journey, memories of London, of his failed marriage, of his former self, intrude upon the present.

## The East as Refuge

The East is imagined as a place outside history, a realm where one's past does not follow. This proves illusory. The colonial world has its own past — older, deeper, and equally inescapable. The Chinese characters on a temple (PN 024-011), the ancient Siamese temples described in Colet's geological book (PN 021-059), all testify to histories that predate and dwarf the British presence.

## The Past in the Present

Tomlinson's method is to show the past not as flashback but as a quality of present experience. A chance encounter, a familiar street, a sudden memory — these are the vectors through which the past resurfaces. The novel's structure, moving between London and the East, between memory and action, enacts this interpenetration of times.
