# Peace

Peace in *Gallions Reach* is not the absence of conflict but a positive quality — a stillness that invites and then withdraws. It appears as a "shrine" lost in the jungle [003-033], as "peaceful brightness" aboard ship [019-001], as a sleeper "so virtuous" that "peace itself would have thought" him its own [022-018], and finally as something broken — "the first flaws in the original peace" [036-011]. The novel traces peace as a condition that is always retrospective, always already lost.

## In the Novel

The first mention is elegiac: "a shrine of peace lost somewhere now in the jungle" [003-033]. Peace is not merely absent; it is sacred, a place of worship now inaccessible. This establishes the novel's governing attitude toward peace — it is something once possessed and now remembered, not something to be achieved.

At sea, peace takes on a visual quality: "The peaceful brightness of the vacant ocean" [019-001]. The vacancy is key — peace requires emptiness, the absence of human claim and commercial purpose. When Colet is left alone with "his thoughts in peace" [019-017], the sun "continued its fire from a cloudless sky" — peace as a pause within ongoing intensity, not its cancellation.

The most striking image comes through a sleeping figure, "in so virtuous a sleep that peace itself would have thought that it had him safe" [022-018]. Peace is personified as a guardian, but the subjunctive "would have thought" introduces doubt — peace may believe it has secured the sleeper, but the novel knows otherwise.

The final movement is loss: "the first flaws in the original peace. They broke the solitude at last" [036-011]. Peace is originary, primal — it precedes the flaws that will destroy it. Solitude and peace are coupled; when one breaks, the other goes with it. This late passage recasts the entire novel as a narrative of peace's erosion.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's peace is fundamentally elegiac. It belongs to the past — to a time before commerce, before colonialism, before the "flaws" that break the "original" condition. This aligns with the novel's broader primitivism: the jungle represents not savagery but an earlier, more integrated mode of existence that modernity has fractured.

Yet the novel is not merely nostalgic. Peace appears in the narrative's present tense as well — in the "vacant ocean," in the sleeping figure, in Colet's moments of solitary reflection. These are not permanent attainments but temporary reprieves, and the novel's honesty consists in acknowledging their brevity. Peace is not a goal to be reached but a quality to be briefly inhabited — like the "shrine" in the jungle, it is always "lost somewhere," always elsewhere, always then.
