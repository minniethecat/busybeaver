# Freedom

Colet's flight from London is a flight toward freedom — but freedom, in *Gallions Reach*, proves to be a more complicated destination than the word suggests. The novel traces the difference between escape and liberation.

## In the Novel

Freedom announces itself on the very first Saturday afternoon: "No need to change them in freedom's hour" [003-001]. Colet, released from Perriams for the weekend, feels the city open around him. The greengrocer's window outside the office "gave him the freedom to admire a favourite shop" [003-021] — a small liberty that hints at larger ones. The fruiterer's display becomes, improbably, "better than any Bond Street jeweller's," its raw produce the "raw material of the poet."

At sea, freedom acquires a darker edge. The ship is both liberation and prison. The lamp swinging in its gimbals suggests that "the tether would allow it no more freedom" [010-001] — a metaphor for the human condition aboard a vessel that is itself free only within the constraints of wind and current.

The most telling use of the word comes in Penang, where a woman's laughter was "freedom to Colet for a candid glance at beauty that was unusual and debateable" [024-048]. Here freedom is not escape but permission — the permission to look, to desire, to be human in a way that London never allowed.

## Interpretation

Freedom in *Gallions Reach* is not a political concept but an existential one. It is the capacity to look at beauty without shame, to admire a greengrocer's display without irony, to move through the world without the burden of a role. London denies this freedom; the East offers it.

But the novel is too honest to suggest that freedom is simply achieved by changing geography. The ship's swinging lamp reminds us that freedom always operates within constraints. Colet's achievement is not absolute liberty — which the novel treats as an illusion — but the discovery that some constraints are more habitable than others.
