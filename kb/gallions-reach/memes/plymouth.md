# Plymouth

Plymouth is the Devonshire port where the *Altair* makes its final English call before the long voyage east. It is at Plymouth that Captain Hale joins the ship — a detail mentioned in passing: "Captain Hale joins us at Plymouth" (008-011) — and the embarkation transforms the *Altair* from a ship awaiting its master into a ship under command.

## Location & Context

Plymouth, on the Devon coast, has been a major British naval and commercial port for centuries. In the 1920s, it remained a key point of departure for vessels bound for the Atlantic and beyond. For the *Altair*, it marks the boundary between the familiar waters of the English Channel and the open sea.

## Role in the Narrative

Plymouth appears briefly but significantly. Sinclair informs Colet that "Captain Hale joins us at Plymouth" (008-011), and the news carries an implication: the ship will be complete, its hierarchy fully assembled, its departure beyond recall. The suggestion that Colet might "run round to Plymouth with us" (009-020) frames the port as a point of no return — once past Plymouth, Colet is committed to the voyage.

In Chapter 11, Colet stands at the rail watching the hills "change into the hills of a Devon morning" (011-001) — Plymouth already receding into the distance, the English coast dissolving into a memory of green.

## Atmosphere & Description

Tomlinson gives Plymouth no direct description. It exists in the novel only as a name, a point on the chart, a place where the captain comes aboard. This minimalism is itself significant: Plymouth is less a place than a threshold, the last English word before the voyage begins in earnest.

## Associated Characters

- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — Joins the *Altair* at Plymouth, assuming command.
- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — Watches Plymouth recede from the rail; the last sight of England.
- **[[sinclair|Mr. Sinclair]]** — The chief officer who informs Colet of Captain Hale's impending arrival at Plymouth.

## Symbolic Meaning

Plymouth is the novel's gate of departure. After Plymouth, there is no England — only the sea, the ship, and the East. The captain's embarkation there makes the port a symbolic transfer point: authority is assumed, the voyage becomes official, and the last tether to home is cast off.
