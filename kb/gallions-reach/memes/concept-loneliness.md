# Loneliness

Loneliness in *Gallions Reach* is not a passive condition but an active, almost animate presence. It announces itself, takes shape, and becomes a defining feature of the novel's emotional geography. From the shrieking lamp to the "central loneliness" of being at sea, isolation functions as both setting and psychological state.

## The Shrieking Lamp

One of the most striking images of loneliness in the novel comes early:

> The lamp had shrieked in its loneliness. A figure, which reminded Jimmy... [006-027]

The verb "shrieked" transforms a household object into a sentient being, suggesting that loneliness permeates even the inanimate world. The figure that follows — a reminder of something Jimmy cannot quite place — ties loneliness to memory and the uncanny.

## Central Loneliness

At sea, loneliness becomes structural rather than incidental. A character is "brought back to a central loneliness" [022-016] when the ship departs — not loneliness as an emotion to be felt but as a center of gravity to which one returns:

> They were off. He began to hear the loneliness. [022-016]

Loneliness here is something you *hear*, not just feel. It has a sensory dimension that makes it inescapable.

## The Suggestion of Isolation

A single line captures the power of isolation as a threatening concept:

> The strike of the suggestion of that isolation. [016-012]

The phrase "strike of the suggestion" implies that isolation does not need to be fully realized to wound — the mere intimation of it is enough. This is consistent with the novel's broader interest in how ideas shape experience before they become facts.

## Solitude as Safety

The novel offers a counterpoint: solitude can also mean safety. One character reflects that "he could trust himself, when alone. There was safety in the night" [004-052]. Being alone means freedom from having to "listen to anyone" [004-047]. The city at night becomes a private space where intimacy is possible: "they might have been alone in the city" [004-068].

## Mr. Parsell's Elective Loneliness

In a late passage, Mr. Parsell is described as one who "would elect for loneliness" [035-019]. This framing of loneliness as a choice — an election — reframes the earlier depictions of imposed solitude and suggests that some characters come to prefer it.
