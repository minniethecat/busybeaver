# Wealth

Wealth in *Gallions Reach* is presented as a paradox: the goal of all commercial striving, yet spiritually worthless. From the cave of Sinbad to the British Museum, the novel measures the distance between having and being.

## In the Novel

The novel's treatment of wealth begins with a comparison between the commercial offices of Billiter Avenue and "the wealth of the cave of Sinbad" [002-001]. The simile is ironic — Sinbad's cave held jewels; the City holds ledgers. The real wealth, Tomlinson suggests, is not in the brass plates and mahogany desks but in the romance of distant places they represent.

Colet's escape from London takes him past the British Museum, "frowning darkly in its retirement with its wealth of the mind, unsolicitous of attention" [003-030]. The museum's wealth — sculpture, manuscripts, the accumulated thought of civilisations — offers a silent rebuke to the commercial wealth of Billiter Avenue. "The doves are alive," Colet notes; "they make love unashamed under the shadow of wisdom."

In the jungle, the question of wealth becomes existential. Colet reviews his situation and finds that "of all the frauds of the senses" [032-005], the desire for material accumulation is the most transparent. Norrie, who actually seeks gold, treats it with casual irony. The real wealth turns out to be Mr. Parsell's ethnological knowledge: "he wished to share this wealth. It was for everybody" [036-016].

## Interpretation

Wealth in the novel is treated as a category error — the mistake of valuing things over experiences, accumulation over attention. The British Museum passage is crucial: the museum's wealth is freely available to anyone who will look, yet most pass it by. Commerce, by contrast, demands constant vigilance and yields only temporary possession.

Tomlinson, who spent his career as a shipping clerk and journalist, writes about wealth with the authority of someone who has seen it from both sides. His critique is not Marxist but humanist: wealth is not evil, but it is insufficient. A life organised around its pursuit is a life half-lived.
