# Mr. Sinclair

Mr. Sinclair is the chief officer of the *Altair*, [[the-altair|the ship]] that carries [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]] from England to the East. He is the novel's most vivid secondary character — seasoned, theatrical, and possessed of a professional competence that makes [[china-sea|the sea]] navigable. In a novel about a landsman learning to read the elements, Sinclair is the man who already knows how.

## Role in the Novel

Sinclair appears first as a voice — "He could hear only Mr. Sinclair's voice. No doubt about that" (009-001) — and this auditory introduction captures something essential about his character. He is a man who fills space with speech, whose commands and observations organize [[the-altair|the ship]]'s life. He is the intermediary between [[captain-hale|the captain]]'s authority and the crew's labor, translating the requirements of [[china-sea|the sea]] into the language of men.

Sinclair serves as [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s informal mentor at sea. He is the first ship's officer to take an interest in the landsman, and his casual competence provides a model of how to inhabit the maritime world. When he kisses his hand to a mirage — "Farewell, Circe, my love!" (013-006) — he demonstrates [[china-sea|the sea]]man's characteristic blend of sentiment and irony.

## Character Traits

Sinclair is brisk, competent, and theatrically self-aware. He "marched stiffly away in that brisk manner, and he did not look back" (022-015). His movements are decisive; he does not hesitate or look behind him. This forward momentum is professional rather than personal — the habit of a man who has learned that [[china-sea|the sea]] rewards decisiveness.

His theatrical quality emerges in his playful relationship to sentiment. The kiss blown to Circe's shore is performance as much as feeling — a man who knows he is playing a role and enjoys the performance. This self-awareness distinguishes him from the dourer figures of [[the-altair|the ship]] ([[captain-hale|Captain Hale]], [[gillespie|Gillespie]]) and makes him a more accessible model for [[jim-colet|Colet]].

## Key Appearances

- **Chapters 7–9** — Sinclair is introduced aboard the *Altair*, his voice organizing [[the-altair|the ship]]'s departure (008-001, 009-001 through 009-007).
- **Chapter 13** — Sinclair's theatrical kiss toward the shore — "Farewell, Circe, my love!" (013-006).
- **Chapter 22** — Sinclair departs briskly, adding himself to [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s "store of riches" (022-015).

## Relationships

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The landsman whom Sinclair takes under informal tutelage, bridging office and sea.
- **[[captain-hale|Captain Hale]]** — The master under whom Sinclair serves; their relationship represents the hierarchy of shipboard command.

## Significance

Sinclair represents the possibility of adapting to [[china-sea|the sea]] without losing one's humanity. He is neither the grim professional ([[captain-hale|Hale]]) nor the comic relief ([[gillespie|Gillespie]]) but something in between — a man who does his job well and enjoys the theater of his own competence. For [[jim-colet|Colet]], he is proof that the transition from landsman to seaman need not be a loss of self.

## Notable Quotes

> "Farewell, Circe, my love!" Sinclair kissed his hand to the mirage. (013-006)

> "Sinclair had gone; but chance had added Sinclair to his store of riches, anyhow, though no bank manager would look at that credit." (022-015)
