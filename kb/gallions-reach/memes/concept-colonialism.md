# Colonialism

Colonialism in *Gallions Reach* is the unspoken premise of its global geography. The novel never mounts an explicit critique, but its portrayal of trade, native labour, and the commerce that connects London to the tropics quietly registers the inequalities of imperial order.

## In the Novel

The colonial world appears first as commerce. Perriam's house deals in "tropical prodigality" extracted by "native labour" [002-004] — the euphemisms of colonial economics barely concealing the extractive relationship. The City of London is thick with the material evidence of empire: "Chinese bowls of jade, lacquered boxes" [003-033], a "Chinese silk tapestry" [005-002], and a "porcelain" Chinese madonna [002-019] — all objects that have traveled from colony to metropole as luxury goods stripped of context.

Further east, the colonial encounter becomes more direct. Passengers observe "a crowd of noisy native passengers" [022-016] from the deck above, maintaining the physical separation that mirrors social hierarchy. The narrator notes dryly that the British are "a part of the East now as the natives. We're here now, you know" [026-045] — a claim of belonging that carries the weight of occupation.

## Key Passages

> The tropical prodigality which can be induced by native labour [002-004]

The passive voice — "can be induced" — encodes the colonial violence of extraction in the language of natural process. Labour is "induced" rather than demanded, as if tropical wealth simply required gentle encouragement.

> We're a part of the East now as the natives. We're here now, you know. [026-045]

The assertion of belonging, the appeal to presence as legitimacy — this is colonialism's self-justification in miniature, and the novel leaves it hanging, neither endorsed nor refuted.
