# Time

Time in *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927) is measured and then unmade. The novel begins with clocks and bells, the regular chimes of St. Mary Axe marking the hours of the commercial day. But as the narrative moves outward from [[london|London]], human timekeeping dissolves into the longer rhythms of [[china-sea|the sea]] and the timelessness of the jungle.

## In the Novel

Time enters the novel through its instruments. The church clock in St. Mary Axe "chimed a quarter-past, half past" (005-003), its sound echoing through the empty offices of [[perriam|Perriams]]. Time here is an imposition — the regular pulse of commerce that structures the working day and measures out [[the-silent-lifeboat|the waiting]] that fills it.

[[jim-colet|Colet]]'s relationship to time is characteristically uncertain. Standing before the British Museum, he does "not consult his watch. He did not know the time" (003-032). This temporal disorientation is willed — a suspension of the commercial clock in favor of something less measurable. He is "not thinking of time" because time, in its ordinary sense, has ceased to matter.

At sea, time fragments. The crew watches "two landscapes unfold, one in an evening without a date, and another that their pilot was watching" (009-002). The "evening without a date" represents time stripped of its calendar — pure diurnal rhythm, the alternation of light and dark without the grid of numbered days.

The novel's most startling temporal observation occurs on [[london|London]] Bridge, where [[jim-colet|Colet]] watches humanity pour "through time like a senseless fluid" (003-015). Time here is not a measure but a medium — something humans flow through rather than something that flows past them. The image collapses the distinction between time and substance, suggesting that humanity is itself a temporal phenomenon.

In the jungle, time approaches [[silence|stillness]]. The [[the-jungle|forest]] belongs to "an earlier spell of the earth" (030-010) — a time before human time, more ancient than the clocks of [[london|London]] and the bells of ships. [[jim-colet|Colet]] enters this earlier time and finds it "mute, except for its magnitude" (030-010).

## Key Passages

> "The church clock in St. Mary Axe chimed a quarter-past, half past; and its echo in the empty office where the shadows were deepening was like the memory of things gone." (005-003)

> "Humanity poured through time like a senseless fluid." (003-015)

> "He did not know the time, as he mounted, his mind at ease, the steps to the temple." (003-032)

> "The sunlight was younger, and sounds were clearer and without fear." (030-010)

## Related Characters

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The character whose temporal disorientation — not knowing the time, not thinking of time — marks his movement away from [[london|London]]'s commercial rhythms.
- **The pilot** — Watching "an evening without a date" (009-002), he navigates by a time that predates calendars.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson's treatment of time maps a journey away from measurement. [[london|London]]'s time is counted — quarters and halves chimed by church clocks, working days structured by bells. The sea's time is rhythmic — the alternation of watches, the diurnal round, evenings "without a date." The jungle's time is geological — "an earlier spell of the earth" that human clocks cannot register.

This trajectory is not a rejection of time but an expansion of it. The novel does not seek timelessness but a deeper time — a temporality that encompasses the church clock and the primeval [[the-jungle|forest]], the senseless fluid of humanity and the "younger [[light|sunlight]]" of an earth before measurement.

## Related Concepts

- **[[memory|Memory]]** — Time and memory are inseparable; the echo in the empty office is both a temporal and a mnemonic phenomenon.
- **[[light|Light]]** — Time is measured by light's passage; the "younger [[light|sunlight]]" of the jungle marks a time before human time.
- **[[darkness|Darkness]]** — The "insurrection of darkness" (014-019) is a temporal event — the abrupt abolition of day by [[darkness|night]].
