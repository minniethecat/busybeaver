# Ignorance

Ignorance in *Gallions Reach* is rarely simple. It is sometimes a shield, sometimes a weapon, and occasionally a condition so uncomfortable that any novelty — even an unseen one — is preferable to remaining in it. The novel explores ignorance across social, personal, and institutional registers.

## Ignorance as Incompetence

The most direct use of the word appears in relation to social behavior. A character's indiscretion is explained away:

> It was only his ignorance. It was born of his trust in life. [014-014]

Ignorance here is not condemned but contextualized — it emerges from trust, from an assumption that the world operates in good faith. The novel treats this form of ignorance with a certain gentleness.

## The Discomfort of Not Knowing

One of the novel's most revealing lines on the subject comes late:

> I'm so uncomfortable when in ignorance that even an unseen novelty alarms me. [028-026]

This is ignorance not as bliss but as acute distress. The speaker would rather confront an unknown threat than remain in a state of not-knowing — a position that inverts the cliché of ignorance as comfort.

## Ignorance as a Tool of Power

The novel also shows ignorance being deployed strategically. A character reflects that "ignorance and folly" might flourish in his absence [005-002], suggesting that ignorance is not always passive — it can be cultivated and left to grow. The "ignorant" are those kept shivering by the wind of authority, "humble and ignorant mortals" who are "intimidated" by institutions [003-031].

## Strategic Ignorance

In a crucial scene, characters "might have been ignorant, or they might have known" [018-033] — the ambiguity is itself significant. The novel suggests that claimed ignorance can be a form of plausible deniability, a chosen posture rather than a genuine condition.

## The Presumption of Ignorance

A layman is noted to be "not so ignorant as in fairness could be assumed" [032-029], while another character "did not look safe" and thus invited "the presumption of the ignorant" [036-006]. These passages suggest that ignorance is often projected onto others rather than accurately diagnosed — a social attribution rather than a fact.
