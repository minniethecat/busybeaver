# Chaos

The formless, primordial substrate beneath the thin veneer of orderly existence — both the literal wildness of sea and jungle, and the metaphysical darkness that haunts Tomlinson's modern characters.

## In the Novel

Chaos appears at the novel's philosophical foundation. Colet, contemplating the city from his office window, perceives "something inherent in this chaos which informed it" [003-020] — the suggestion that order is not the absence of chaos but something that emerges *within* it. The London fog becomes "the palpable residue of old chaos, something which had never settled" [006-024], rendering the imperial capital as built on primordial disorder. At sea, the ocean itself "was at once superior to the chaos" [016-019] — the ambiguous syntax leaving it unclear whether the sea masters chaos or *is* chaos, mastered by nothing.

## Key Passages

> "Something inherent in this chaos which informed it. Perhaps in..." [003-020]

Colet's early intuition — that chaos is not mere absence of order but a productive force with its own internal logic — sets up the novel's central philosophical tension.

> "The palpable residue of old chaos, something which had never settled." [006-024]

London's fog as chaos-made-visible: Tomlinson's metaphor for the fragility of civilization, the ancient disorder still present beneath the modern city's stone and routine.

> "He was at once superior to the chaos." [016-019]

The ship in the vastness of the ocean — a precarious order floating on the formless deep, the defining image of Tomlinson's existential vision.

## Interpretation

Chaos in *Gallions Reach* is not merely danger amplified but an ontological condition. Tomlinson, writing in 1927 — the year after the General Strike, in the shadow of the Great War's collapse of old orders — uses the sea as a figure for the formlessness that underlies civilization. The novel's structure mirrors this: Colet moves from London (apparent order) to the sea (manifest chaos) to the jungle (chaos as generative, teeming life), tracing a descent into the primordial that is also an ascent into truth. The "residue of old chaos" in London's fog [006-024] is the novel's final warning: chaos is not somewhere else. It is here, under the pavement, in the fog — the disorder that order is built on and that will outlast it.
