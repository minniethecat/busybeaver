# Labour

Labour in *Gallions Reach* appears in two registers: the intellectual and commercial work of London's City, and the invisible "native labour" (PN 002-004) that underwrites imperial prosperity. Tomlinson keeps the latter at the margins, but its presence exerts a quiet pressure on the novel's moral atmosphere.

## The City and the Office

The novel opens in the world of Perriams, a City firm where Colet works. The office is described with a sense of dread — "Monday's apprehension of laboured stone ascent" (PN 003-005), the commute imagined as a climb. Labour here is mental and spiritual rather than physical, but no less grinding. The office walls and their "fixity" look down upon visitors "from their height and from the past" (PN 002-005), suggesting institutions that have outlasted the individuals who serve them.

## Colonial Labour

The novel acknowledges the labour that produces colonial wealth without dwelling on it. The phrase "native labour out of tropical prodigality" (PN 002-004) is telling — labour is subordinated to the natural abundance it extracts, as though the tropics simply yielded their riches. A more explicit statement comes later: the firm's purpose "was to serve Perriams, not our labourers" (PN 002-016). This hierarchy — capital over labour, employer over worker — is stated as fact, not argument.

## Release from Labour

The novel's most lyrical passage on labour concerns its end. Those who survive shipwreck are "released at last from the labour and the wrecks where time is" (PN 019-027). This image links labour to temporality itself — to be free of work is to step outside time's demands. It is one of the novel's few gestures toward transcendence.

## Work as Identity

For Colet, work is identity but also a cage. His departure from Perriams is both liberation and loss of self. The novel suggests that labour, for all its burdens, provides the structures within which character is formed and tested.
