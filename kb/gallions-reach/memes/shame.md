# Shame

Shame haunts Colet from the moment of Perriam's death, yet the novel's journey eastward gradually dissolves its grip. *Gallions Reach* suggests that shame, like duty, is culturally constructed — and that what feels like moral weight in London may be irrelevant in the jungle.

## In the Novel

Colet's shame is mostly unspoken but omnipresent. After Perriam's death, he moves through London acutely aware of his guilt, seeing the world through the lens of his secret. The office workers could "make a heart feel ashamed" [004-053] with their clever talk, their apparent ease in the world Colet has just shattered. His shame isolates him.

In the East, shame begins to lose its definition. The jungle bedroom in Penang is a place where "dreams were there before you slept" [025-006], the uncanny environment dissolving the clear moral categories of London. The "shameless and noisy kissing" of the night — possibly geckos, possibly something more human — mocks the propriety Colet has left behind.

The novel's most radical treatment of shame comes through Mat, the Malay guide, who speaks of the Sakai tribespeople: "They were infidels, and they knew no shame. They were as the beasts" [037-013]. Mat's judgment is ironic — the novel has already suggested that shame is not a universal virtue but a local custom. The Sakai, living outside the moral economy of shame and guilt, represent a freedom that Colet glimpses but cannot fully inhabit.

## Interpretation

Shame in *Gallions Reach* is presented as a civilisational artifact — a mechanism of social control that loses its power when you leave the society that enforces it. Colet's journey is, among other things, a slow unlearning of shame. The East does not absolve him; rather, it teaches him that absolution is not required because the crime was never as significant as London made it seem.

This is not moral relativism but moral realism. The novel suggests that shame is proportional to the community that judges you. In the jungle, where Perriam's death means nothing, Colet's shame is revealed as a kind of vanity — the assumption that his actions matter as much to the universe as they do to him.
