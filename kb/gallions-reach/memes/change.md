# Change

*Gallions Reach* is a novel whose subject is change itself. Colet's journey from City clerk to jungle wanderer is not a single metamorphosis but a cascade of smaller transformations, each one making the next possible. The novel asks whether change is something that happens to us or something we choose — and whether the distinction matters.

## In the Novel

Change enters the novel as threat. In Perriams, Limited, the clerks eye Colet "furtively. They wanted a clue to any change that was imminent. Changes there were frequent and unexpected" [002-019]. In the commercial world, change means instability — a sudden promotion, a sudden dismissal, the unpredictable movements of a "watchful and predatory" employer. Colet's own presence in Perriam's private room is itself "a change" — portentous and unreadable.

But change soon reveals its other face: liberation. Aboard the *Altair*, "Jimmy felt a change of thought. It went over him with a glow of pleasure" [006-016]. The shift is internal and instantaneous — the thought that had weighed on him lifts, and the tough he had feared becomes, in his eyes, "a weight" — manageable, even admirable. This is the first intimation that change need not be inflicted from without; it can arise from within.

The sea passage compresses temporal change into visual metaphor: Colet at the rail, "watching a thin mist change into the hills of a Devon morning" [011-001]. The transformation of mist into land, of formlessness into form, prefigures Colet's own gradual coalescence into a new self. The captain's wisdom reinforces the point: "We might as well argue with the seasons. They change, when the turn comes" [013-025]. Change is natural, rhythmic, inevitable — resistance is futile, but understanding is possible.

In the jungle, change slows to a geological pace, and resistance to change becomes visible. After the loss of the *Altair*, Colet "crouched uncomfortably, without changing his position" [019-003] — the paralysis of a man who has not yet accepted what has happened. Change here is not transformation but shock, and the stillness is the body's refusal to move into a new reality.

The novel's deepest reflection on change comes from Ah Loi: "You have time to change your mind" [024-015]. The observation arises from the Chinaman's meditation on the Western neglect of touch — the idea that handling an object, weighing it, forces a pause in which reconsideration is possible. Change, in this rendering, is not the enemy of deliberation but its fruit.

In the archipelago, change becomes fluid and benign: "The thoughts changed. The next was better than the last" [022-023]. The islands drift past like "unspoken thoughts," and the mind, freed from the City's fixed categories, discovers that change can be a form of play.

## Interpretation

Tomlinson treats change not as a theme to be argued but as the narrative's structural principle. The novel moves from London to the Channel to the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean to the Malayan jungle — each transition a change of scale and register that forces Colet to shed another layer of his former self. The formal restlessness of the book — its refusal to settle in any one mode for long — mirrors its protagonist's journey.

The novel distinguishes between two kinds of change. One is passive: the seasons, the mist becoming hills, the thoughts that change by themselves. The other is active: "You have time to change your mind" [024-015]. The space between these two modes is where Colet's moral growth occurs. He cannot prevent the seasons from turning, but he can choose, in Ah Loi's pause, what to make of the turn.
