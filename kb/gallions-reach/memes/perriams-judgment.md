# Perriam's Judgment

The scene in [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s office in Chapter 2 is the novel's most concentrated dramatization of commercial power. [[jim-colet|Colet]] stands before his employer, and [[perriam|Perriam]] — "clean-shaven," with a "massive face" (002-007) — pronounces the judgment that will define [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s [[london|London]] life: "You know, [[jim-colet|Colet]], you are being tried? Very well" (002-018).

## The Event

[[perriam|Perriam]] does not look up. "He waited, without moving" (002-009) — [[the-silent-lifeboat|the waiting]] itself a form of pressure. His logic is "certainly right" (002-010) even when it is oppressive, and his conclusion carries the weight of institutional necessity: "my judgment is what pays you and me" (002-018). The judgment is not personal — it is commercial, which makes it more, not less, devastating. [[perriam|Perriam]] is not cruel; he is simply the instrument of a system that measures human worth in wages.

The scene is structured as a series of small physical details that accumulate into a portrait of power: [[perriam|Perriam]]'s "watch-guard" that [[jim-colet|Colet]] notices (002-011), the "massive face" that remains "clean-shaven" (002-007), the bell that "whirred" (002-006). Each detail is insignificant in isolation; together they constitute the apparatus of commercial judgment.

## Aftermath

The judgment hangs over [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s [[london|London]] life. It is the reason he searches bookshop windows for alternatives, the reason [[helen-denny|Helen]]'s wandering attention wounds him, the reason departure becomes necessary. [[perriam|Perriam]] has pronounced the verdict of the commercial world, and [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s only response — departure, [[shipwreck-of-the-altair|shipwreck]], [[the-jungle|jungle]] — is to seek a world where that verdict does not apply.

The judgment reverberates far beyond Chapter 2. When [[jim-colet|Colet]] later reflects on his [[london|London]] life, it is [[perriam|Perriam]]'s words — "being tried" — that echo. The commercial world has spoken, and its judgment is final. To escape it requires not argument but departure.

## Significance

[[perriam|Perriam]]'s judgment is the novel's most explicit statement of its central theme: the reduction of human beings to commercial functions. "Being tried" is [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s condition in [[london|London]], and to leave [[london|London]] is to leave the dock. The scene's power lies in its restraint — [[perriam|Perriam]] is not a villain but a functionary, and his judgment is not cruelty but logic. That makes it harder, not easier, to refute.
