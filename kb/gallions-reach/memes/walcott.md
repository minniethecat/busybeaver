# Walcott

A young biologist in London, known to Jim Colet before his departure for the East. His youth and scientific disposition provide a contrast to the commercial world of Perriams and to Colet's own restless uncertainty.

## Role in the Novel

Walcott appears in a London scene where he engages in conversation about the nature of things. When asked "What would you expect, Walcott? blood, these days?" (004-023), his response reveals a disillusionment that belies his youth. He later "evoked this disillusion" (004-040), suggesting that his scientific worldview has a deflating effect on romantic or idealistic assumptions. His presence in the London chapters helps establish the intellectual atmosphere Colet inhabits before his departure.

## Character Traits

Walcott is described as "so young that he was lively" (004-025), marking him as one of the younger members of Colet's London circle. Despite his youth, he displays a capacity for disillusionment that surprises his older companions. A moment of warmth is noted when Colet turns his head to young Walcott and they seem "very friendly" (004-046), suggesting a bond of genuine affection between them.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 4** — Participates in a London social gathering; his remarks about blood and disillusionment provoke reactions from older companions; shares a moment of friendliness with Colet (004-023, 004-025, 004-040, 004-046)

## Relationships

- **[[Jim Colet]]** — Walcott and Colet share a friendly rapport; Colet turns to him in a moment of warmth during a social gathering, and they appear "very friendly" together (004-046)
- **Unnamed companions** — The older figures in the London scene engage Walcott in conversation, drawing out his youthful perspective on disillusionment and science

## Significance

Walcott represents the younger generation of scientifically-minded Londoners — lively, idealistic, but already touched by disillusionment. His presence in the early chapters helps establish the range of intellectual types in Colet's London world before the protagonist's departure for the East marks a decisive break with that milieu.
