# Brixton

Brixton is a district of South [[london|London]], and in the novel it is the site of [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s home — the domestic extension of his commercial domain. The [[perriam|Perriams]]' house stands behind "their Brixton privet hedge" (005-002), the hedge marking a boundary that is simultaneously physical and psychological: the line between public and private, commercial and domestic, presence and absence.

## In the Novel

Brixton appears in the novel's [[london|London]] chapters as the setting for [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s visit to [[perriam|Perriam]]'s home. It is here that the full extent of [[perriam|Perriam]]'s authority becomes visible — an authority that does not stop at [[billiter-avenue|the office]] door but extends to the suburban villa, where [[mrs-perriam|Mrs. Perriam]] has learned to withdraw "at the first wave of disturbance sent before [[perriam|Mr. Perriam]]'s car" (005-002).

The privet hedge, the car's approach, the wife's withdrawal — these details accumulate into a portrait of domestic life deformed by commercial power. Brixton is not a refuge from [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]]; it is [[billiter-avenue|Billiter Avenue]]'s residential colony.

## Significance

Brixton matters because it demonstrates that [[perriam|Perriam]]'s world has no outside. The same logic that governs [[billiter-avenue|the office]] — judgment, trial, the authority of the master — governs the home. To escape [[perriam|Perriam]] is not to change neighborhoods; it is to leave [[london|London]] entirely.
