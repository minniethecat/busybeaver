# Duty

Duty runs through *Gallions Reach* as the invisible architecture of lives. Characters perform their duties — to firms, to fathers, to ships, to themselves — often without understanding why. The novel asks whether duty is a virtue or merely a habit that prevents genuine choice.

## In the Novel

Colet's relationship to duty is established early as a form of inheritance. He remains at Perriams because of "fate and duty to a father whose influence" [003-007] placed him there. The office is not a choice but a legacy, and duty is the name given to the absence of alternatives.

Perriam's version of duty is more aggressive. He operates through "a sense of duty no more admirable than the reflex of a hungry tiger" [005-006], reducing moral obligation to predatory instinct. The juxtaposition — Colet's passive duty versus Perriam's active predation — defines the ethical spectrum of the novel's London.

At sea, duty takes on a purer form. Sinclair at dinner is "merely performing a duty in eating" [011-009], his mechanical consumption reflecting the discipline of the ship. When young Lycett "went out to his duty" [018-001] to signal the passing liner, duty becomes a matter of survival — the flag, the signal, the protocol — rather than moral weight.

The novel's treatment of duty suggests that its value depends entirely on what it serves. Duty to commerce is hollow; duty to the ship is necessary; duty to oneself — the hardest kind — is what Colet must learn.

## Interpretation

Duty in the novel functions as a secular religion — a framework of obligation that gives structure to lives without providing meaning. Tomlinson is not anti-duty; he is anti-unexamined-duty. The novel traces Colet's progress from inherited duty (to his father's world) through professional duty (to Perriams) to chosen duty (to his own survival and, ultimately, to Kuan-yin).

This progression mirrors the novel's larger movement from London to the East, from commerce to contemplation, from the given to the chosen. Duty, properly understood, is not obedience but attention — a theme Ah Loi embodies in his care for porcelain and tradition.
