# Fate

A central thematic concept in H. M. Tomlinson's *[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]* (1927), fate (also rendered as "destiny," "fortune," or "luck") is the inscrutable force that governs human lives, channeling choices and rendering escape impossible. The novel treats fate not as mere superstition but as a palpable presence threading through the lives of its characters from the [[london|London]] offices to the jungles of the East.

## In the Novel

The concept of fate opens the novel through the voice of an old sailor on a [[the-sea|river]] of [[dream|dreams]], who reflects that men are driven by forces they cannot name — "his luck, or fate, I forget" (001-017). The protagonist, [[jim-colet|Jim Colet]], repeatedly confronts fate as an external agent that shapes his course. In [[the-altair|the ship]]ping offices of [[london|London]], [[jim-colet|Colet]] has "accepted his fate" even as his outward composure betrays him (003-001). The theme crystallizes when a boy selling newspapers becomes an instrument of destiny — "the boy was destiny right enough, though destiny ought to know better" (007-011) — showing how ordinary encounters take on fatalistic weight.

[[perriam|Perriam]], [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s superior, embodies the oppressive face of fate in the business world, ascending the stairs "in heavy deliberation, like destiny" (005-009). The novel juxtaposes this oppressive, urban fate with a more cosmic version encountered in the later chapters, where "old destiny" becomes something characters debate and even mock (034-012, 034-013).

## Key Passages

> "Called it fortune. His luck, or fate, I forget. The same old dream. I suppose we can't live without dreaming. He didn't want to go, and yet he never came back. This river has seen a lot of men of that sort. It is a river of dreams." (001-017)

> "He was coming up the stairs in heavy deliberation, like destiny. No escape now; might kill the beggar, though." (005-009)

> "I'll call this destiny; and there you are. That's what it means for you. Just look at the fellow. There's his view of the job in front of him. He's reciting the mantras to the men, the runes for a safe journey. They think he's a pawang, a sorcerer … and confound him, so he is. He's conjured something out of me." (034-037)

> "Colet heard that interruption as though a listener had mocked Mr. Parsell's happy auguries, had derided his faith in human destiny." (036-018)

## Related Characters

- **[[jim-colet|Jim Colet]]** — The protagonist, whose journey from [[london|London]] clerk to man confronting his fate forms the novel's spine. He "accepted his fate" in [[billiter-avenue|the office]] (003-001) yet repeatedly tests its boundaries.
- **[[perriam|Perriam]]** — [[jim-colet|Colet]]'s employer, described as approaching "like destiny" (005-009), embodying the oppressive, inescapable quality of fate in the commercial world.
- **[[parsell|Mr. Parsell]]** — A character whose "faith in human destiny" is ultimately derided by the indifferent universe (036-018).

## Interpretation

Tomlinson uses fate as a bridge between the mundane and the cosmic. In the early chapters, fate wears the face of office routine and social obligation — the "pigeonholes to cue any fate that might come" (018-001). As the novel moves outward from [[london|London]] to [[china-sea|the sea]] and the jungle, fate transforms into something more elemental. The [[the-sea|river]] [[the-sea|Thames]] at [[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]] becomes "a [[the-sea|river]] of [[dream|dreams]]" (001-017), and fate merges with the natural and supernatural forces that govern human existence.

The novel refuses to resolve the tension between acceptance and rebellion. Characters oscillate between embracing "the sad victims of fate" (028-020) and defying it. The shipboard scene where [[jim-colet|Colet]] watches a "pawang" conjure destiny (034-037) suggests that fate-making may itself be a form of human artifice — something we call into being by naming it.

## Related Concepts

- **[[gallions-reach|Gallions Reach]]** — The geographical and symbolic threshold where [[river-thames|the river]] meets [[china-sea|the sea]], and where fate becomes tangible.
- **[[the-sea|The Sea]]** — The novel's central metaphor for the unknown, the realm where fate reveals itself beyond the certainties of [[london|London]].
- **[[dream|Dream]]** — Closely paired with fate throughout the novel; the "same old dream" (001-017) that drives men to sea is inseparable from their fate.
