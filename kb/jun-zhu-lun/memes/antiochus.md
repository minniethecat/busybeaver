# Antiochus III

Antiochus III, known as Antiochus the Great, was a Hellenistic king of the Seleucid Empire who reigned from 223 to 187 BCE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli references him as an example of the dangers of failing to respect the balance of power in a region and provoking a stronger adversary — in his case, the Roman Republic.

## Reign and Ambition

Antiochus restored Seleucid power after decades of decline, earning the title "the Great" through campaigns that recovered lost territories from Asia Minor to Bactria. His ambition eventually brought him into conflict with Rome when he crossed into Greece in 192 BCE. The Romans defeated him decisively, stripping him of most of his territorial gains.

## Role in *The Prince*

### A Warning for Princes

Machiavelli cites Antiochus in the context of how princes lose their states through imprudent expansion. His reference is part of a broader discussion of the mistakes that bring down even capable rulers:

> "Antiochus" appears among examples of rulers whose ambition exceeded their strategic judgment [Chapter 3 reference].

Machiavelli's implicit lesson is that a prince must accurately assess his own strength relative to potential adversaries before committing to expansion. Antiochus, despite his earlier successes, miscalculated Rome's willingness and ability to intervene.

### Relevance to Italian Politics

For Machiavelli's Renaissance audience, Antiochus's fate would have been a relevant cautionary tale. The Italian states of Machiavelli's time were similarly caught between great powers (France and Spain), and a miscalculation by a minor prince could — and did — prove fatal. The lesson of Antiochus is to know when to expand and when to consolidate.

## Historical Assessment

Antiochus III was in many respects a successful ruler who revived a declining empire. For Machiavelli, however, the final outcome is what matters: a prince who ends in defeat and humiliation has, by definition, failed. His story reinforces the principle that political success requires not just ambition and military skill, but also sound strategic judgment about which wars to fight.
