# David

David is the biblical king of Israel, celebrated in the Old Testament as a warrior, poet, and founder of a royal dynasty. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli invokes David's confrontation with Goliath as an allegory for the necessity of a prince relying on his own forces rather than the arms of others.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Lesson of Own Arms

In Chapter 13, "Concerning Auxiliaries, Mixed Soldiery, and One's Own," Machiavelli retells the familiar biblical story to make a political point:

> "David offered himself to Saul to fight with Goliath, the Philistine champion. And Saul, to encourage him, armed him with his own weapons; which David rejected as soon as he had them on his back, saying that with them he could not give a good account of himself, and therefore he wished to meet the enemy with his sling and his knife" [013-064].

Machiavelli draws the conclusion directly: borrowed armor is useless. Saul's weapons — representing the arms of another — were a burden rather than a help. David's own sling and knife — representing a prince's native forces — were the instruments of victory (013-066).

### A Political Allegory

Machiavelli's use of David is not theological but political. The story illustrates a principle central to *The Prince*: a ruler who depends on the arms of others — whether auxiliaries, mercenaries, or foreign allies — is never secure. David's rejection of Saul's armor becomes a metaphor for the prince who casts off dependence and arms his own subjects.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli's David is stripped of religious significance and recast as a model of self-reliance. In the context of Renaissance Italy, where princes routinely hired mercenary captains and sought foreign protection, the biblical story provided a memorable argument for military self-sufficiency. David joins Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus in Machiavelli's pantheon of founders and armed prophets who succeeded because they relied on their own virtù.
