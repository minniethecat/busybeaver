## CHAPTER V. CONCERNING THE WAY TO GOVERN CITIES OR PRINCIPALITIES WHICH LIVED UNDER THEIR
OWN LAWS BEFORE THEY WERE ANNEXED

[005-001]Whenever those states which have been acquired as stated have been

[005-002]accustomed to live under their own laws and in freedom, there are three

[005-003]courses for those who wish to hold them: the first is to ruin them, the

[005-004]next is to reside there in person, the third is to permit them to live

[005-005]under their own laws, drawing a tribute, and establishing within it an

[005-006]oligarchy which will keep it friendly to you. Because such a government,

[005-007]being created by the prince, knows that it cannot stand without his

[005-008]friendship and interest, and does its utmost to support him; and therefore

[005-009]he who would keep a city accustomed to freedom will hold it more easily by

[005-010]the means of its own citizens than in any other way.

[005-011]There are, for example, the Spartans and the Romans. The Spartans held

[005-012]Athens and Thebes, establishing there an oligarchy: nevertheless they lost

[005-013]them. The Romans, in order to hold Capua, Carthage, and Numantia,

[005-014]dismantled them, and did not lose them. They wished to hold Greece as the

[005-015]Spartans held it, making it free and permitting its laws, and did not

[005-016]succeed. So to hold it they were compelled to dismantle many cities in the

[005-017]country, for in truth there is no safe way to retain them otherwise than

[005-018]by ruining them. And he who becomes master of a city accustomed to freedom

[005-019]and does not destroy it, may expect to be destroyed by it, for in

[005-020]rebellion it has always the watchword of liberty and its ancient

[005-021]privileges as a rallying point, which neither time nor benefits will ever

[005-022]cause it to forget. And whatever you may do or provide against, they never

[005-023]forget that name or their privileges unless they are disunited or

[005-024]dispersed, but at every chance they immediately rally to them, as Pisa

[005-025]after the hundred years she had been held in bondage by the Florentines.

[005-026]But when cities or countries are accustomed to live under a prince, and

[005-027]his family is exterminated, they, being on the one hand accustomed to obey

[005-028]and on the other hand not having the old prince, cannot agree in making

[005-029]one from amongst themselves, and they do not know how to govern

[005-030]themselves. For this reason they are very slow to take up arms, and a

[005-031]prince can gain them to himself and secure them much more easily. But in

[005-032]republics there is more vitality, greater hatred, and more desire for

[005-033]vengeance, which will never permit them to allow the memory of their

[005-034]former liberty to rest; so that the safest way is to destroy them or to

[005-035]reside there.
