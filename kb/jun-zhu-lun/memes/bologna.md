# Bologna

**Bologna** was a prosperous city of the Papal States, ruled for generations by the Bentivogli family — a living example in *The Prince* of how hereditary rule could survive, and how it could suddenly collapse when fortune turned.

## Overview

Bologna in Machiavelli's time was nominally part of the Papal States but effectively ruled by the Bentivogli as hereditary princes. Giovanni Bentivogli II governed from 1463 to 1506, presiding over a cultural golden age that made Bologna one of Italy's leading cities. But the city's apparent stability masked a fatal dependence on external protection — a vulnerability Machiavelli analyzed with precision.

Machiavelli himself "intended to gain Bologna" (011-057) in his analysis of ecclesiastical principalities, recognizing the city as a test case for how papal power could — or could not — extend into the territories it nominally controlled.

## Political Context

### Bentivogli Rule

The Bentivogli represented the model of a family that had made itself princely through time and habit rather than formal title. "Giovanni Bentivogli, born in Bologna 1438, died at Milan 1508" (019-076) — Machiavelli's biographical note situates the ruler precisely in time, treating his reign as a case of hereditary rule maintained through popular support. The Bentivogli "enjoyed in those days in Bologna" (019-068) such authority that the city's institutions bent to the family's will.

But this authority proved fragile. When Pope Julius II marched against Bologna in 1506, Giovanni Bentivogli fled, and the city returned to direct papal control. Machiavelli uses this episode to illustrate how a prince dependent on fortune — rather than his own arms — can lose everything in a single campaign.

### Papal Authority

Bologna's relationship with the papacy was permanently ambiguous. Julius II "intended to gain Bologna, to ruin the Venetians, and to drive the Spaniards out of Italy" (011-057) — an ambitious program that made Bologna a stepping stone rather than an end in itself. The city's return to papal control demonstrated Julius II's aggressive temporal policy, which Machiavelli admired even as he lamented its consequences for Italian liberty.

## Role in The Prince

### The Bentivogli as Exemplar

The Bentivogli appear in Chapter 19 as a key example of the hereditary prince who maintains power through popular support. Machiavelli presents Giovanni Bentivogli's long rule as evidence that a prince who avoids the hatred of his people can survive even the most adverse fortune. The contrast with other Italian princes who provoked popular revolt underscores the principle.

> "Bentivogli, who was prince in Bologna (grandfather of the present Annibale Bentivogli), having been killed by the Canneschi, who conspired against him" (019-063)

### Popular Support and Its Limits

The Bentivogli case demonstrates both the power and the limits of popular support. When Giovanni Bentivogli was driven from Bologna by Julius II, the Bolognese did not rise to defend their prince — suggesting that the Bentivogli's popular foundation had eroded over time. Machiavelli notes that "the Bentivogli returning to Bologna came to a similar decision" (020-096) — a reference to failed restoration attempts that proved the city's attachment to its former rulers had waned.

## Related Events

- The expulsion of the Bentivogli (1506): Pope Julius II personally led the campaign that ended Bentivogli rule
- Cesare Borgia's design on Bologna: Borgia intended to seize Bologna as part of his Romagna consolidation, seeing the city as a threat to his new state (027-006)
- The brief restoration (1511): French forces briefly restored the Bentivogli before the final papal takeover
