# Maximilian I

Maximilian I (1459–1519) was Holy Roman Emperor from 1493 until his death. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents him as a cautionary example of how a ruler's excessive secrecy and pliability undermine his own designs. Machiavelli served as an envoy to Maximilian's court in 1507–08, giving him firsthand knowledge of the Emperor's character.

## 生平概要

Maximilian was "born in 1459, died 1519, Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire" (023-034). He married Mary, daughter of Charles the Bold, and after her death, Bianca Sforza — thus becoming entangled in Italian politics. His reign coincided with the [[League of Cambrai]] (1508), the great European coalition against [[Republic of Venice|Venice]].

Despite presiding over the Empire at a pivotal moment, Maximilian's effectiveness was limited by personal traits that Machiavelli observed directly. The Emperor's court was one of Machiavelli's diplomatic postings, and the author "reveals the secret of his many failures" (P01-154) by analyzing his decision-making habits.

## 与《君主论》的关联

The translator's Introduction describes Maximilian as "one of the most interesting men of the age" (P01-154). But Machiavelli's portrait is clinical: "a secretive man, without force of character — ignoring the human agencies necessary to carry his schemes into effect, and never insisting on the fulfilment of his wishes" (P01-154).

Maximilian's primary appearance comes in [[Chapter 23]], on the subject of counselors and advisors. Machiavelli uses him to illustrate the danger of a prince who neither communicates his plans nor solicits opinions:

> "Fra Luca, the man of affairs to Maximilian, the present emperor, speaking of his majesty, said: He consulted with no one, yet never got his own way in anything." (023-023)

### 作为范例的角色

Maximilian is the **negative example of counsel mismanaged**. His failure pattern is precisely diagnosed: because he keeps his designs secret, no one can offer informed advice; when his plans inevitably become known during execution, "they are at once obstructed by those men whom he has around him, and he, being pliant, is diverted from them" (023-023).

The practical result is paralysis: "those things he does one day he undoes the next, and no one ever understands what he wishes or intends to do, and no one can rely on his resolutions" (023-023).

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's Maximilian is not a wicked ruler but a structurally ineffective one. His secrecy, combined with his pliability, produces a ruler who is simultaneously closed and weak — the exact opposite of what Machiavelli recommends. The episode grounds Machiavelli's advice in [[Chapter 23]]: a prince should "take counsel, but only when he wishes and not when others wish" (023-023), and should be "a constant inquirer" rather than either a recluse or a puppet.
