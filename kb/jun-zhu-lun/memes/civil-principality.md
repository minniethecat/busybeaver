A **civil principality** is one of the forms of principality identified by Machiavelli in *The Prince*. Unlike principalities acquired through arms, fortune, or hereditary succession, the civil principality arises when a private citizen ascends to power through the support of his fellow citizens — either the nobles or the people.

## Definition in Chapter 9

Machiavelli defines the civil principality in Chapter 9 (009-003):

> [009-003] by the favour of his fellow citizens—this may be called a civil principality.

He distinguishes it from principalities acquired through criminal means (Chapter 8), those acquired by one's own arms and virtù (Chapter 6), and those acquired through fortune or the arms of others (Chapter 7).

## Two Paths to a Civil Principality

A civil principality can be established through two opposing social forces:
- **The nobles (grandi)** — who wish to dominate and oppress the people
- **The people (popolo)** — who wish not to be dominated or oppressed

The aspiring prince must align with one of these forces. Machiavelli advises that aligning with the people is generally safer, because the people's goals are more moderate — they simply wish not to be oppressed — while the nobles' ambitions are harder to satisfy.

## Maintaining Power

Machiavelli offers specific advice for the civil prince. Once in power, he must keep the people's goodwill (009-103):

> [009-103] adopt such a course that his citizens always have need of the state and of him.

The civil prince must ensure that in times of crisis, citizens depend on the state. If they become self-sufficient during peaceful times, they will abandon the prince when trouble comes (009-098):

> [009-098] observes in quiet times, when citizens have need of the state, because then every one runs at his need and promises much; and when death is distant every one is willing to die for him; but when need approaches, when the state has need of its citizens, then he finds but few.

## Relation to the Absolute Principality

Machiavelli notes that a civil principality can transition toward absolute rule. When the prince governs through magistrates, the state is more vulnerable because magistrates may not act decisively in a crisis (009-088):

> [009-088] civil to the absolute order of government.

The civil prince must be ready to assume direct, personal authority when circumstances demand it, moving from civil governance toward a more absolute form.

## Differences from Other Principalities

| Type | Path to Power | Key Risk |
|------|--------------|----------|
| Civil Principality | Favour of fellow-citizens | Losing popular support |
| Criminal Principality | Violence and wickedness | Hatred of the people |
| Hereditary Principality | Inheritance | Complacency |
| New Principality | Arms or fortune | Instability |
