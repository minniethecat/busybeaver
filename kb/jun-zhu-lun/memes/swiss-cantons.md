# Swiss Cantons

The **Swiss Cantons** (the Old Swiss Confederacy) represented, for Machiavelli, a paradox: the most formidable infantry in Europe, organized by a republic of free communities — and simultaneously the model for what native arms could achieve and a warning about the dangers of relying on foreign troops.

## Overview

The Swiss Confederacy in the early 16th century was a loose alliance of rural cantons (Uri, Schwyz, Unterwalden, and their allies) that had won de facto independence from the Holy Roman Empire through a series of spectacular military victories over Burgundian and Imperial armies. Swiss pikemen, fighting in dense squares with extraordinary discipline, had shattered the armored cavalry that had dominated medieval warfare.

Their military reputation made them the most sought-after mercenaries in Europe. French kings, Milanese dukes, and popes all contracted Swiss infantry — and all discovered, as Machiavelli observed, that mercenaries who fight for pay fight differently than citizens who fight for survival.

## Role in The Prince

### The Swiss as Free and Armed

Machiavelli's admiration for the Swiss is rooted in his broader argument about the relationship between freedom and arms. In Chapter 12, he contrasts the Swiss favorably with Italian mercenary companies:

The Swiss are "armed and free" — their military prowess is a product of their political liberty, not a substitute for it. This causal connection — free institutions produce effective soldiers; subjected populations produce none — is fundamental to Machiavelli's republicanism and recurs throughout his writings.

> [P01-169] …nd with the assistance of the Swiss drove the French out…

The Swiss intervention that drove the French from Lombardy in 1512 demonstrated their decisive military weight. But the episode also illustrated the danger of relying on Swiss power: the Swiss installed Massimiliano Sforza as Duke of Milan — not as an act of liberation but as the installation of a client. Swiss "assistance" came with Swiss domination.

### Mercenaries vs. Native Arms

The Swiss occupy an ambiguous position in Machiavelli's military typology. As mercenaries hired by foreign princes, they represent the system he condemns — foreign arms that fight for pay and whose loyalty is contingent on payment. But as a free people defending their own liberty, the Swiss exemplify exactly what Machiavelli wants Italian princes to create: native militias of citizen-soldiers.

This ambiguity is deliberate. The Swiss demonstrate that military excellence is achievable without noble cavalry or professional standing armies — that disciplined infantry, when motivated by political liberty, can defeat any adversary. The tragedy, from Machiavelli's perspective, is that Italian states use Swiss mercenaries *instead of* creating their own equivalent, rather than learning from the Swiss model *and replicating it domestically*.

| Aspect | Swiss (as model) | Italian Use of Swiss (as error) |
|--------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| Organization | Citizen militia | Hired mercenaries |
| Motivation | Defense of liberty | Payment |
| Loyalty | To the confederacy | To the highest bidder |
| Machiavelli's Verdict | Admirable | Self-defeating |

### The Battle of Novara and the Limits of Swiss Power

The Swiss victory at Novara (1513), which confirmed their dominance over French arms in Italy, was followed within two years by their catastrophic defeat at Marignano (1515), where French artillery and cavalry — properly coordinated — broke the Swiss squares. Machiavelli, writing in 1513, could not have known of Marignano, but his analysis already contained the seeds of Swiss vulnerability: infantry without cavalry support, however formidable, is tactically brittle.
