# Philopoemen

Philopoemen was a Greek general and statesman, strategos of the Achaean League, who lived from 253 to 183 BCE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents him as the supreme exemplar of a military leader who devoted himself entirely to the study and practice of war — a model for every prince.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Prince at War

In Chapter 14, "That Which Concerns a Prince on the Subject of the Art of War," Machiavelli singles out Philopoemen for special praise:

> "Philopoemen, Prince of the Achaeans, among other praises which writers have bestowed on him, is commended because in time of peace he never had anything in his mind but the ways of war" [014-042].

Machiavelli elaborates: when Philopoemen rode through the countryside with his companions, he would pause and ask them to analyze the terrain — where to place the army, how to array the battle line, what advantage the ground offered for siege or defense (014-055). This habit of constant mental preparation is exactly what Machiavelli prescribes: a prince must "never take his mind from the study of war, and in peace he must exercise it more than in war" [014-041].

### The Lesson for Princes

Philopoemen exemplifies the principle that military competence is not a crisis skill but a permanent discipline. Machiavelli holds him up as the ideal: a ruler who treats peace as preparation for war, who studies terrain and tactics obsessively, and who understands that the art of war is the foundation of all other arts of government.

## Historical Assessment

Known to antiquity as "the last of the Greeks," Philopoemen was a historical figure whose career — defending the Achaean League against Sparta and later Rome — embodied the martial virtue Machiavelli admired. In *The Prince*, he is stripped of historical context and elevated to a universal type: the warrior-prince whose excellence in arms guarantees everything else. For Machiavelli, Philopoemen matters not for what he achieved but for how he prepared — his habits of mind are the real lesson.
