# Louis XII of France

Louis XII (1462–1515), known as "The Father of the People," was King of France from 1498 until his death. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses Louis as the preeminent cautionary example of how a ruler may lose a conquered territory through a cascade of self-inflicted errors. Louis's Italian campaign and its collapse occupy a substantial portion of [[Chapter 03]].

## 生平概要

Louis XII succeeded Charles VIII to the French throne. His reign was marked by extensive involvement in Italian affairs. He was "brought into Italy by the ambition of the Venetians, who desired to obtain half the state of Lombardy by his intervention" (003-160).

Early in his Italian enterprise, Louis secured significant gains: Genoa yielded, Florence became his friend, and numerous Italian lords — the Marquess of Mantua, the Duke of Ferrara, the Bentivogli, the lords of Faenza, Pesaro, Rimini, Camerino, Piombino, and the cities of Lucca, Pisa, and Siena — all made advances toward him. At this moment, Machiavelli observes, Louis could have easily maintained his position by keeping these "weak and timid" friends secure.

To retain the Duchy of Brittany for the French crown, Louis divorced his wife Jeanne (daughter of Louis XI) and married Anne of Brittany, the widow of Charles VIII — a dynastic maneuver accomplished with [[Pope Alexander VI]]'s assistance in dissolving the prior marriage (003-235).

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli devotes the latter half of [[Chapter 03]] to analyzing Louis's failures in Italy. He enumerates **five capital errors**:

1. He destroyed the minor powers
2. He increased the strength of one of the greater powers in Italy (the Church)
3. He brought in a foreign power (Spain)
4. He did not settle in the country
5. He did not send colonies

To these he adds a **sixth error**: taking away the Venetians' dominions — a move that would have been sound had Louis not already aggrandized the Church and brought Spain into Italy, but which, after those missteps, removed the only power capable of checking others' designs on Lombardy.

### 作为范例的角色

Louis XII is the definitive **negative example** in Machiavelli's statecraft analysis. Every error he made illustrates a general rule that Machiavelli extracts:

> "He who is the cause of another becoming powerful is ruined; because that predominancy has been brought about either by astuteness or else by force, and both are distrusted by him who has been raised to power." (003-160)

Machiavelli personally met Louis and observed his character. When Cardinal Rouen remarked that "the Italians did not understand war," Machiavelli famously replied that "the French did not understand statecraft" — meaning they had allowed the Church and Spain to grow great at France's expense.

## 历史评价

> "Thus King Louis lost Lombardy by not having followed any of the conditions observed by those who have taken possession of countries and wished to retain them. Nor is there any miracle in this, but much that is reasonable and quite natural." (003-160)

Machiavelli's verdict on Louis is not that he was incompetent, but that he violated discoverable laws of statecraft. Each of Louis's errors was avoidable. The episode serves as Machiavelli's most sustained demonstration that political success and failure follow rational principles, not fortune or providence.

Louis's deal with Alexander VI — dissolving his marriage in exchange for assistance in the Romagna — also becomes Machiavelli's entry point for discussing [[Faith of Princes]]: the question of whether and how rulers should keep their promises.
