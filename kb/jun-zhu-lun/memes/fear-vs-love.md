Machiavelli addresses one of the most famous questions in political philosophy: whether a ruler should seek to be **loved** or **feared**. His answer — that it is safer to be feared — has become one of the defining maxims of Machiavellian thought.

## The Central Argument (Chapter 17)

Machiavelli sets up the dilemma directly (017-028):

> [017-028] whether it be better to be loved than feared.

He acknowledges that ideally a prince would be both, but since reconciling the two is difficult, fear is the more reliable foundation of rule (017-029):

> [017-029] or feared than loved? It may be answered that one should wish to be both, but, because it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer to be feared than loved, when, of the two, either must be dispensed with.

## Why Fear Is More Reliable

Machiavelli's reasoning rests on a pessimistic view of human nature. People are inherently self-interested and unreliable — they will break bonds of love when it benefits them, but fear of punishment restrains them (017-041):

> [017-041] who is beloved than one who is feared, for love is preserved by the link of obligation which, owing to the baseness of men, is broken at every opportunity for their advantage; but fear preserves you by a dread of punishment which never fails.

## The Crucial Qualification: Avoid Hatred

Machiavelli is careful to distinguish fear from hatred. A prince must be feared but **not hated** (017-047):

> [017-047] feared whilst he is not hated, which will always be as long as he abstains from the property of his citizens and subjects and from their women.

This qualification is essential to the doctrine. Fear without hatred requires restraint — especially avoiding confiscation of property and violation of women, which Machiavelli identifies as the two fastest routes to popular hatred.

## Cesare Borgia as Exemplar

Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino) embodies the successful application of this principle. He cultivated both fear and popular support (007-203):

> [007-203] to make himself beloved and feared by the people, to be followed and revered by the soldiers.

## Historical Applications

The principle extends throughout Machiavelli's historical analysis. The Roman Emperor Severus succeeded by being feared without being hated (019-202):

> [019-202] will find him feared and respected by every one, and by the armies particularly.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Fear vs. Love is closely linked to several other Machiavellian concepts:

- **[[Cruelty]]** — Fear often requires acts of cruelty, but Machiavelli distinguishes "well-used" cruelty (swift, decisive, not repeated) from abuse
- **[[Fortuna]]** — Fortune is fickle like the people's love; fear is a more stable foundation against fortune's changes
- **[[Virtù]]** — The ability to decide when to use fear and when to show clemency is a mark of princely virtù
- **[[Faith / Faithlessness]]** — A prince who is feared can more easily break faith when necessary
