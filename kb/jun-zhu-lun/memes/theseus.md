# Theseus

Theseus is the legendary founder and king of Athens in Greek mythology. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli places Theseus among the four supreme exemplars of rulers who acquired their states through their own ability — Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus — treating the mythological figure as a political archetype rather than a historical personage [006-024].

## The Founder of Athens

According to myth, Theseus unified the scattered communities of Attica into a single political entity centered on Athens. He was also renowned for slaying the Minotaur and freeing Athens from tribute to Crete. Machiavelli draws on the political dimension of the myth: the founder who creates a new order out of fragmented elements.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Armed Prophet

In Chapter 6, Machiavelli argues that founders like Theseus succeeded because they were "armed" — they possessed the military force to impose their new institutions:

> "Theseus could not have manifested his ability had he not found the Athenians dispersed" [006-044].

The argument parallels Machiavelli's treatment of Moses: the founder's genius requires a material foundation — a people in need of liberation — and the willingness to use force.

### Self-Reliance

Machiavelli's key point about Theseus (and the other founders) is that they relied on their own arms and ability, not on fortune:

> "If Moses, Cyrus, Theseus, and Romulus had been unarmed they could not have enforced their constitutions for long" [006-076].

This is the definition of *virtù* in its highest form: the capacity to impose order through one's own resources.

### The Exhortation

In Chapter 26, the final exhortation to liberate Italy, Machiavelli returns to Theseus as a model. He argues that Italy's present condition — dispersed, oppressed, leaderless — presents the same opportunity that Theseus found in ancient Attica. A prince of *virtù*, recognizing the moment, could do for Italy what Theseus did for Athens.

## Historical Assessment

For Machiavelli, the mythological status of Theseus is irrelevant. What matters is the political truth the story contains: that new orders are created by armed founders who recognize the right moment to act. Theseus, stripped of myth, is a case study in successful revolution.
