# Mercenaries

Mercenaries (mercenary arms) are hired soldiers who fight for pay rather than loyalty. Machiavelli devotes Chapter XII to arguing that they are "useless and dangerous" and that a prince who relies on them will never be secure (012-015).

## Machiavelli's Case Against Mercenaries

The core argument is that mercenaries have no motivation beyond their stipend: "they have neither the fear of God nor fidelity to men, and destruction is deferred only so long as the attack is" (012-019). In peacetime they rob the prince; in wartime they flee the enemy. "They are ready enough to be your soldiers whilst you do not make war, but if war comes they take themselves off or run from the foe" (012-023).

Machiavelli traces Italy's military weakness directly to its reliance on mercenary captains (condottieri). These captains, "men without a state," built their fortunes by keeping wars going rather than winning them decisively. "They used every artifice to secure themselves against the hardships and dangers of war, never killing one another in battle but rather taking prisoners without ransom" (012-043).

## Historical Evidence

Francesco Sforza, the greatest of the condottieri, used his mercenary command to seize Milan and become its duke (012-047). His successors, lacking his ability, were ruined by the same system. Machiavelli contrasts the mercenary-dependent Italian states with the Swiss and German free cities, which maintain their own arms and their liberty.

## The Solution: One's Own Arms

The remedy, argued throughout Chapters XII–XIII, is for the prince to command his own subjects in arms. "No principality is secure without having its own forces" (013-038). A prince must personally understand the art of war and lead his own troops.
