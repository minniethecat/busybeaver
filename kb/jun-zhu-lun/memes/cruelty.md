# Cruelty

Cruelty (*crudeltà*) is a central concept in Machiavelli's political ethics. *The Prince* is notorious for its apparent endorsement of cruelty as a tool of governance, but Machiavelli draws a crucial distinction: cruelty can be "well-used" or "abused," and the difference determines whether a prince survives or falls.

## Well-Used Cruelty

Machiavelli defines well-used cruelty as that which is "done at a stroke, out of the necessity to secure oneself, and then not persisted in, but turned as much as possible to the advantage of one's subjects" (008-015). The paradigmatic case is Cesare Borgia's treatment of the Romagna. Borgia appointed Remirro de Orco, "a swift and cruel man," to pacify the lawless region (007-120). Once order was restored, Borgia had de Orco publicly executed — satisfying the people's desire for justice while deflecting their hatred from himself. Machiavelli notes that "if any cruelty had been perpetrated, it had not originated with him, but with the harsh nature of his minister" (007-127).

## Abused Cruelty

Cruelty is abused when it "increases rather than diminishes with time" (008-040). Agathocles of Syracuse exemplifies this pattern: "his barbarous cruelty and inhumanity, together with his infinite crimes, do not permit him to be celebrated among the most excellent men" (008-044). His cruelties were so "grievous and so brutal" that they won him power but not glory.

## Cruelty vs. Clemency

In Chapter 17, Machiavelli confronts the question directly: whether it is better to be loved than feared, or feared than loved. He argues that "one should wish to be both, but because it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer to be feared than loved" (017-028). Cesare Borgia was "considered cruel; nevertheless his cruelty reformed the Romagna, united it, and restored it to peace and loyalty" (017-003). Machiavelli warns that "a prince must take care not to misuse this clemency" — excessive mercy that permits disorder is itself a form of cruelty to the state.
