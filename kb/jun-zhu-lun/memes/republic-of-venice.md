# Republic of Venice

The Republic of Venice (*La Serenissima*) was a maritime republic and a major power in Renaissance Italy and the Mediterranean. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli treats Venice as both a model of institutional stability and a cautionary example of the dangers of mercenary reliance.

## Political Character

Venice was an aristocratic republic governed by an elected doge and a complex system of councils. Unlike the hereditary principalities that Machiavelli primarily analyzes, Venice represented the republican alternative — a state founded on laws and institutions rather than on a single prince. Machiavelli acknowledged Venice's longevity but was more interested in its military failures than its constitutional virtues.

## In The Prince

Machiavelli discusses Venice primarily in connection to its military disasters. The Republic had long relied on mercenary *condottieri* to fight its wars. This dependence proved catastrophic at the Battle of Vaila (Agnadello) in 1509, "when Venice lost in one day all that she had acquired over eight hundred years" (P01-164). The catastrophe was the direct result of entrusting the defense of the state to hired captains who had no loyalty beyond their pay.

The League of Cambrai (1508) — an alliance of France, Spain, the Holy Roman Empire, and the Papacy — was formed "with the object of crushing the Venetian Republic" (P01-163). This coalition nearly destroyed Venice, exposing how a rich but militarily weak state becomes prey to its neighbors.

## Machiavelli's Analysis

Venice serves Machiavelli's argument that native arms are indispensable to political survival. A republic that cannot defend itself with its own citizens will eventually fall to others, regardless of its wealth or diplomatic skill. The Venetian example underpins one of the central doctrines of *The Prince*: that a prince must build his state on "his own arms" and never depend on mercenaries or auxiliaries.
