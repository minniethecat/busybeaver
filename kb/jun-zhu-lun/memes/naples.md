# Naples

The **Kingdom of Naples** was the largest of Italy's five major states, a hereditary monarchy in the south of the peninsula that became the primary object of contention between [[france|France]] and [[spain|Spain]] during the Italian Wars.

## Overview

Naples was the southern anchor of Italian politics. Ruled by the Aragonese dynasty (a cadet branch of the Spanish royal house) until the French invasion of 1494, the kingdom was then contested between France and Spain for over a decade. Machiavelli uses Naples repeatedly as an example of a hereditary principality — discussed in Chapter 2 — and as a case study in the errors of [[louis-xii-of-france|Louis XII]].

## Political Context

### The Aragonese Kingdom

The Kingdom of Naples had been under Aragonese rule since 1442, when Alfonso the Magnanimous conquered it from the French House of Anjou. By Machiavelli's time, it was "a kingdom accustomed to the rule of one prince and his blood" — the classic hereditary principality that is "far easier to maintain than a new principality" because the prince need only maintain the established order (001-007).

### The French-Spanish Contest

The French crown claimed Naples through the Angevin line. Charles VIII invaded in 1494, marching through Italy and seizing Naples virtually unopposed. But France could not hold the kingdom: a Spanish counter-offensive under [[ferdinand-of-aragon|Ferdinand of Aragon]] drove the French out. Louis XII then agreed with Spain to partition Naples in 1500, but the partition broke down, and by 1504 Spain held the entire kingdom.

## Role in The Prince

### Louis XII's Fatal Error

Naples is central to Machiavelli's analysis of Louis XII's mistakes in Chapter 3. Louis "wished to have the kingdom of Naples" and "divided it with the King of Spain" (003-196). This was the fourth of Louis's five fatal errors: having driven out the lesser powers that could have been his allies, Louis then introduced a more powerful rival into Italy. Spain took all of Naples, and "the greatness of the Church and of Spain in Italy" (003-250) was the result.

### The Conquest Question

Naples illustrates a recurring Machiavellian theme: the difficulty of holding a newly conquered kingdom. Charles VIII conquered Naples easily but lost it just as quickly. Louis XII took Naples only to lose it to Spain. Machiavelli explains that "if France could have attacked Naples with her own forces, she would have kept it" (003-205) — the lesson being that conquests made with borrowed arms are insecure.

### The Hereditary Benchmark

In Chapter 2, Machiavelli establishes Naples as the benchmark for a hereditary principality: the kingdom passed within the Aragonese line, and "it was enough merely not to transgress the ancestral usages." The ease of governing Naples, once established, contrasts with the difficulty of conquering and holding it against rival claimants.

## Related Events

- The French invasion (1494): Charles VIII seized Naples without resistance
- The Treaty of Granada (1500): France and Spain agreed to partition the kingdom
- The Spanish conquest (1504): Spain drove France out and held Naples thereafter
