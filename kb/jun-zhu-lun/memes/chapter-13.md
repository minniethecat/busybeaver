## CHAPTER XIII. CONCERNING AUXILIARIES, MIXED SOLDIERY, AND ONE’S OWN

[013-001]Auxiliaries, which are the other useless arm, are employed when a prince is

[013-002]called in with his forces to aid and defend, as was done by Pope Julius in the

[013-003]most recent times; for he, having, in the enterprise against Ferrara, had poor

[013-004]proof of his mercenaries, turned to auxiliaries, and stipulated with Ferdinand,

[013-005]King of Spain,[1]

[013-006]for his assistance with men and arms. These arms may be useful and good in

[013-007]themselves, but for him who calls them in they are always disadvantageous; for

[013-008]losing, one is undone, and winning, one is their captive.

[013-009][1]

[013-010]Ferdinand V (F. II of Aragon and Sicily, F. III of Naples), surnamed “The

[013-011]Catholic,” born 1452, died 1516.

[013-012]And although ancient histories may be full of examples, I do not wish to

[013-013]leave this recent one of Pope Julius the Second, the peril of which cannot

[013-014]fail to be perceived; for he, wishing to get Ferrara, threw himself

[013-015]entirely into the hands of the foreigner. But his good fortune brought

[013-016]about a third event, so that he did not reap the fruit of his rash choice;

[013-017]because, having his auxiliaries routed at Ravenna, and the Switzers having

[013-018]risen and driven out the conquerors (against all expectation, both his and

[013-019]others), it so came to pass that he did not become prisoner to his

[013-020]enemies, they having fled, nor to his auxiliaries, he having conquered by

[013-021]other arms than theirs.

[013-022]The Florentines, being entirely without arms, sent ten thousand Frenchmen

[013-023]to take Pisa, whereby they ran more danger than at any other time of their

[013-024]troubles.

[013-025]The Emperor of Constantinople,[2]

[013-026]to oppose his neighbours, sent ten thousand Turks into Greece, who, on the war

[013-027]being finished, were not willing to quit; this was the beginning of the

[013-028]servitude of Greece to the infidels.

[013-029][2]

[013-030]Joannes Cantacuzenus, born 1300, died 1383.

[013-031]Therefore, let him who has no desire to conquer make use of these arms,

[013-032]for they are much more hazardous than mercenaries, because with them the

[013-033]ruin is ready made; they are all united, all yield obedience to others;

[013-034]but with mercenaries, when they have conquered, more time and better

[013-035]opportunities are needed to injure you; they are not all of one community,

[013-036]they are found and paid by you, and a third party, which you have made

[013-037]their head, is not able all at once to assume enough authority to injure

[013-038]you. In conclusion, in mercenaries dastardy is most dangerous; in

[013-039]auxiliaries, valour. The wise prince, therefore, has always avoided these

[013-040]arms and turned to his own; and has been willing rather to lose with them

[013-041]than to conquer with the others, not deeming that a real victory which is

[013-042]gained with the arms of others.

[013-043]I shall never hesitate to cite Cesare Borgia and his actions. This duke

[013-044]entered the Romagna with auxiliaries, taking there only French soldiers,

[013-045]and with them he captured Imola and Forli; but afterwards, such forces not

[013-046]appearing to him reliable, he turned to mercenaries, discerning less

[013-047]danger in them, and enlisted the Orsini and Vitelli; whom presently, on

[013-048]handling and finding them doubtful, unfaithful, and dangerous, he

[013-049]destroyed and turned to his own men. And the difference between one and

[013-050]the other of these forces can easily be seen when one considers the

[013-051]difference there was in the reputation of the duke, when he had the

[013-052]French, when he had the Orsini and Vitelli, and when he relied on his own

[013-053]soldiers, on whose fidelity he could always count and found it ever

[013-054]increasing; he was never esteemed more highly than when every one saw that

[013-055]he was complete master of his own forces.

[013-056]I was not intending to go beyond Italian and recent examples, but I am

[013-057]unwilling to leave out Hiero, the Syracusan, he being one of those I have

[013-058]named above. This man, as I have said, made head of the army by the

[013-059]Syracusans, soon found out that a mercenary soldiery, constituted like our

[013-060]Italian condottieri, was of no use; and it appearing to him that he could

[013-061]neither keep them nor let them go, he had them all cut to pieces, and

[013-062]afterwards made war with his own forces and not with aliens.

[013-063]I wish also to recall to memory an instance from the Old Testament

[013-064]applicable to this subject. David offered himself to Saul to fight with

[013-065]Goliath, the Philistine champion, and, to give him courage, Saul armed him

[013-066]with his own weapons; which David rejected as soon as he had them on his

[013-067]back, saying he could make no use of them, and that he wished to meet the

[013-068]enemy with his sling and his knife. In conclusion, the arms of others

[013-069]either fall from your back, or they weigh you down, or they bind you fast.

[013-070]Charles the Seventh,[3]

[013-071]the father of King Louis the Eleventh,[4]

[013-072]having by good fortune and valour liberated France from the English, recognized

[013-073]the necessity of being armed with forces of his own, and he established in his

[013-074]kingdom ordinances concerning men-at-arms and infantry. Afterwards his son,

[013-075]King Louis, abolished the infantry and began to enlist the Switzers, which

[013-076]mistake, followed by others, is, as is now seen, a source of peril to that

[013-077]kingdom; because, having raised the reputation of the Switzers, he has entirely

[013-078]diminished the value of his own arms, for he has destroyed the infantry

[013-079]altogether; and his men-at-arms he has subordinated to others, for, being as

[013-080]they are so accustomed to fight along with Switzers, it does not appear that

[013-081]they can now conquer without them. Hence it arises that the French cannot stand

[013-082]against the Switzers, and without the Switzers they do not come off well

[013-083]against others. The armies of the French have thus become mixed, partly

[013-084]mercenary and partly national, both of which arms together are much better than

[013-085]mercenaries alone or auxiliaries alone, but much inferior to one’s own

[013-086]forces. And this example proves it, for the kingdom of France would be

[013-087]unconquerable if the ordinance of Charles had been enlarged or maintained.

[013-088][3]

[013-089]Charles VII of France, surnamed “The Victorious,” born 1403, died

[013-090]1461.

[013-091][4]

[013-092]Louis XI, son of the above, born 1423, died 1483.

[013-093]But the scanty wisdom of man, on entering into an affair which looks well at

[013-094]first, cannot discern the poison that is hidden in it, as I have said above of

[013-095]hectic fevers. Therefore, if he who rules a principality cannot recognize evils

[013-096]until they are upon him, he is not truly wise; and this insight is given to

[013-097]few. And if the first disaster to the Roman Empire[5]

[013-098]should be examined, it will be found to have commenced only with the enlisting

[013-099]of the Goths; because from that time the vigour of the Roman Empire began to

[013-100]decline, and all that valour which had raised it passed away to others.

[013-101][5]

[013-102]“Many speakers to the House the other night in the debate on the

[013-103]reduction of armaments seemed to show a most lamentable ignorance of the

[013-104]conditions under which the British Empire maintains its existence. When Mr

[013-105]Balfour replied to the allegations that the Roman Empire sank under the weight

[013-106]of its military obligations, he said that this was ‘wholly

[013-107]unhistorical.’ He might well have added that the Roman power was at its

[013-108]zenith when every citizen acknowledged his liability to fight for the State,

[013-109]but that it began to decline as soon as this obligation was no longer

[013-110]recognised.”—*Pall Mall Gazette*, 15th May 1906.

[013-111]I conclude, therefore, that no principality is secure without having its

[013-112]own forces; on the contrary, it is entirely dependent on good fortune, not

[013-113]having the valour which in adversity would defend it. And it has always

[013-114]been the opinion and judgment of wise men that nothing can be so uncertain

[013-115]or unstable as fame or power not founded on its own strength. And one’s

[013-116]own forces are those which are composed either of subjects, citizens, or

[013-117]dependents; all others are mercenaries or auxiliaries. And the way to make

[013-118]ready one’s own forces will be easily found if the rules suggested by me

[013-119]shall be reflected upon, and if one will consider how Philip, the father

[013-120]of Alexander the Great, and many republics and princes have armed and

[013-121]organized themselves, to which rules I entirely commit myself.
