# Milan

Milan is a city in Lombardy, northern Italy, that was the seat of the Duchy of Milan in the 15th and 16th centuries. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses Milan as his primary example of a principality acquired through a combination of *virtù* and *fortuna*.

## Political History

Milan was ruled as a hereditary signoria by the Visconti family until the death of Filippo Maria Visconti in 1447 without a male heir. Francesco Sforza, a *condottiere* (mercenary captain) who had married Visconti's illegitimate daughter Bianca Maria, seized the city and declared himself duke in 1450. Sforza "rose from being a private person to be Duke of Milan, and that which he had acquired with a thousand anxieties he kept with little trouble" (007-033).

## In The Prince

Machiavelli invokes Milan repeatedly as a case study in the fragility of new conquests. Francesco Sforza's rise exemplifies the prince who acquires through ability — he "became Duke of Milan by the proper means and with great ability" (007-042). His son Ludovico Sforza (il Moro), by contrast, lost the state by inviting the French into Italy.

Louis XII of France occupied Milan twice — first in 1499, then losing it to Ludovico Sforza, and retaking it in 1500. Machiavelli uses Louis's errors in Milan to illustrate how a prince should handle newly acquired territories: he "took Milan at once, and as quickly lost it" (003-027), and "Milan was taken from France both the first and second time" (003-036) because the French king violated every principle of colonial governance.

## Lessons Drawn

The Milan case illustrates three Machiavellian principles: (1) that a principality acquired through ability is easier to keep than one acquired through fortune alone; (2) that a prince should never make another power stronger within his own sphere of influence; and (3) that the errors of Louis XII — displacing lesser lords, increasing the power of the Pope, and bringing Spain into Italy — each contributed to losing Milan and ultimately French power in Italy.
