# Arms (Arme)

Arms (arme) represent the material foundation of political power in Machiavelli's thought. The dictum that "there cannot be good laws where there are not good arms" (012-012) encapsulates the priority of military force over legal order. A prince who relies on the arms of others — mercenaries, auxiliaries, or allies — has already lost control of his fate.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli devotes Chapters 12–14 to military matters, and the theme of arms runs through the entire work. His discussion is both practical (what kinds of troops to use) and philosophical (what self-reliance means for a prince).

### 核心论点

- A prince must have no other object or thought than war and its organization, for this is the art of command (Ch. 14)
- Own arms are the foundation of all security; mercenaries are useless and dangerous, auxiliaries bring victory at the cost of dependence (012-010)
- A wise prince prefers to lose with his own arms than to win with those of others, because a victory gained with foreign arms is not a true victory (007-088)
- New principalities are acquired either by the arms of others or by one's own arms; those acquired by fortune and foreign arms are the least secure (001-010, 007-197)
- The prince must study the terrain of his own country, know how to organize infantry and cavalry, and personally command in war. Arms are not delegated — they are the prince's first and most essential competence

### 历史例证

- **Cesare Borgia** — initially dependent on French arms, he recognized the danger and resolved to rely only on his own forces. His decision to "depend no more upon the arms and the luck of others" is presented as the model of princely prudence (007-065, 007-088, 007-038)
- **Francesco Sforza** — rose from private citizen to Duke of Milan through his own arms and virtù, the exemplar of self-made military power
- **Hierarch of Syracuse** — raised his own army when he found the existing one unreliable, demonstrating that own arms trump inherited forces
- **The Swiss Cantons** — the exemplar of citizen-soldiers whose own arms made them "most armed and most free"

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Arms vs. Law**: They are co-foundational, but arms are logically prior — law without force cannot be enforced. "Where there are good arms, there are necessarily good laws" (012-012).
- **Arms vs. Fortune**: Fortune favors the armed. A prince who possesses his own arms can resist fortune; one who relies on foreign arms is at fortune's mercy.
- **Arms vs. Mercenaries / Auxiliaries**: These are the false alternatives to own arms. Mercenaries fight for pay and flee when danger comes; auxiliaries fight for another prince and leave you in their debt.
- **Arms vs. Virtù**: Virtù without arms is sterile; arms without virtù are ineffective. The two are mutually reinforcing.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

Arms are the material substrate of Machiavelli's entire political theory. The autonomy of the state, the security of the prince, the enforcement of law, and the pursuit of glory all depend on military self-sufficiency. The final chapter's call for Italian liberation is a call to arms — the only force capable of expelling the barbarians.

## 后世影响

Machiavelli's insistence on native militias over mercenaries influenced the development of the modern nation-state and citizen army. His argument that military power precedes legal order became a cornerstone of realist international relations theory. The concept of "self-help" in an anarchic international system traces directly to Machiavelli's doctrine of own arms.
