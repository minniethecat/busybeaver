## Overview

Fermo was a city in the Marche region of central Italy during the Renaissance. Located near the Adriatic coast, it was notable in Machiavelli's political analysis as the hometown of Oliverotto da Fermo, whose notorious coup against his uncle Giovanni Fogliani provides one of the most vivid case studies in The Prince.

## Political Context

Fermo was governed by leading citizens, with the Fogliani family holding significant authority. Giovanni Fogliani, Oliverotto's maternal uncle, was among the city's chief men. The city's political structure—governed by a loose oligarchy of leading families rather than a single prince—made it vulnerable to the kind of internal power grab that Oliverotto executed, as there was no unified military force to oppose a determined conspirator.

> "Oliverotto da Fermo... was brought up in his early childhood by his maternal uncle, Giovanni Fogliani. In his youth he was sent to serve under Paolo Vitelli, so that being thoroughly trained in that discipline, he might attain some high position in the army." (008-048)

## Role in The Prince

Fermo features in Chapter 8 of The Prince, where Machiavelli recounts the story of Oliverotto da Fermo as a primary example of "those who have attained a principality through crime." Oliverotto, having gained military experience under Paolo Vitelli, returned to Fermo and conspired with "some citizens of Fermo, to whom the enslavement of their country was dearer than its liberty" (008-056). He invited his uncle Giovanni Fogliani and the chief men of Fermo to a banquet, where he had them all murdered, then rode through the city to seize control.

> "He wrote to Giovanni Fogliani that, having been away from home many years, he wished to come to see him and his city... he arranged a formal banquet to which he invited Giovanni Fogliani and the chiefs of Fermo." (008-056)

Machiavelli uses the Fermo episode to distinguish between "cruelty well used"—applied once for necessity and then converted to the benefit of subjects—and "cruelty abused," which grows over time. Oliverotto's decisive but limited violence in seizing Fermo initially succeeded, but his broader pattern of treachery eventually led to his own death at Sinigaglia.

## Related Events

- **The Banquet of Fermo**: Oliverotto invited his uncle and the city's leaders to a banquet, where soldiers hidden in an adjacent room emerged and killed them all (008-071).
- **Consolidation of Power**: After the massacre, Oliverotto "rode through the town and besieged the chief magistrate in the palace" until "he was secure in the city of Fermo" (008-086).
- **The Sinigaglia Massacre**: Oliverotto da Fermo was among the condottieri executed by Cesare Borgia at Sinigaglia (027-014).
