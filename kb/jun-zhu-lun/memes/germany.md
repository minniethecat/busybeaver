## Overview

In The Prince, "Germany" refers to the German-speaking territories of the Holy Roman Empire, a complex political landscape of free imperial cities, ecclesiastical principalities, and secular lordships under the nominal authority of the Emperor. Machiavelli analyzes the German political constitution as a model of how institutional strength can make a territory resistant to conquest.

## Political Context

The Germany Machiavelli describes was politically decentralized, with substantial autonomy vested in its constituent cities and principalities. The Holy Roman Emperor held titular supremacy, but effective power was widely dispersed. This structure fascinated Machiavelli as an example of a political system that, despite its fragmentation, proved remarkably resilient against external threats.

> "The cities of Germany are absolutely free, they own but little territory around them, and they obey the Emperor when they choose, and they do not fear either him or any other power about them." (010-020)

## Role in The Prince

Germany features most prominently in Chapter 10 of The Prince, where Machiavelli analyzes how the strength of a principality should be measured. The German cities serve as his prime example of well-fortified states: they "are absolutely free, they own but little territory around them, and they obey the Emperor when they choose" (010-020). Despite their small size, these cities are "so fortified that everyone considers their capture a tedious and difficult task," because they maintain "proper ditches and walls," sufficient artillery, and year-long stocks of provisions.

This analysis supports Machiavelli's argument that a prince who has "a strong city and has not made himself hated" cannot be easily attacked. The German cities demonstrate that political and military preparedness, rather than territorial extent, is the true measure of a state's security.

In Chapter 25, Machiavelli invokes Germany alongside Spain and France as examples of countries that have succeeded "either through the force of their proper valour" (025-032). The Appendix also mentions German military affairs in the context of Castruccio Castracani's campaigns, where "all the Tuscan and Lombard Ghibellines... returned to Germany" after military setbacks (028-272).

> "They are so fortified that everyone considers their capture a tedious and difficult task, because all have proper ditches and walls, sufficient artillery, and always keep in their public storehouses enough to drink and eat and burn for a year." (010-020)
