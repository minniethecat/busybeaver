## CHAPTER XII. HOW MANY KINDS OF SOLDIERY THERE ARE, AND CONCERNING MERCENARIES

[012-001]Having discoursed particularly on the characteristics of such

[012-002]principalities as in the beginning I proposed to discuss, and having

[012-003]considered in some degree the causes of there being good or bad, and

[012-004]having shown the methods by which many have sought to acquire them and to

[012-005]hold them, it now remains for me to discuss generally the means of offence

[012-006]and defence which belong to each of them.

[012-007]We have seen above how necessary it is for a prince to have his

[012-008]foundations well laid, otherwise it follows of necessity he will go to

[012-009]ruin. The chief foundations of all states, new as well as old or

[012-010]composite, are good laws and good arms; and as there cannot be good laws

[012-011]where the state is not well armed, it follows that where they are well

[012-012]armed they have good laws. I shall leave the laws out of the discussion

[012-013]and shall speak of the arms.

[012-014]I say, therefore, that the arms with which a prince defends his state are

[012-015]either his own, or they are mercenaries, auxiliaries, or mixed. Mercenaries and

[012-016]auxiliaries are useless and dangerous; and if one holds his state based on

[012-017]these arms, he will stand neither firm nor safe; for they are disunited,

[012-018]ambitious, and without discipline, unfaithful, valiant before friends, cowardly

[012-019]before enemies; they have neither the fear of God nor fidelity to men, and

[012-020]destruction is deferred only so long as the attack is; for in peace one is

[012-021]robbed by them, and in war by the enemy. The fact is, they have no other

[012-022]attraction or reason for keeping the field than a trifle of stipend, which is

[012-023]not sufficient to make them willing to die for you. They are ready enough to be

[012-024]your soldiers whilst you do not make war, but if war comes they take themselves

[012-025]off or run from the foe; which I should have little trouble to prove, for the

[012-026]ruin of Italy has been caused by nothing else than by resting all her hopes for

[012-027]many years on mercenaries, and although they formerly made some display and

[012-028]appeared valiant amongst themselves, yet when the foreigners came they showed

[012-029]what they were. Thus it was that Charles, King of France, was allowed to seize

[012-030]Italy with chalk in hand;[1]

[012-031]and he who told us that our sins were the cause of it told the truth, but they

[012-032]were not the sins he imagined, but those which I have related. And as they were

[012-033]the sins of princes, it is the princes who have also suffered the penalty.

[012-034][1]

[012-035]“With chalk in hand,” “col gesso.” This is one of the

[012-036]*bons mots* of Alexander VI, and refers to the ease with which Charles

[012-037]VIII seized Italy, implying that it was only necessary for him to send his

[012-038]quartermasters to chalk up the billets for his soldiers to conquer the country.

[012-039]*Cf*. “The History of Henry VII,” by Lord Bacon: “King

[012-040]Charles had conquered the realm of Naples, and lost it again, in a kind of a

[012-041]felicity of a dream. He passed the whole length of Italy without resistance: so

[012-042]that it was true what Pope Alexander was wont to say: That the Frenchmen came

[012-043]into Italy with chalk in their hands, to mark up their lodgings, rather than

[012-044]with swords to fight.”

[012-045]I wish to demonstrate further the infelicity of these arms. The mercenary

[012-046]captains are either capable men or they are not; if they are, you cannot

[012-047]trust them, because they always aspire to their own greatness, either by

[012-048]oppressing you, who are their master, or others contrary to your

[012-049]intentions; but if the captain is not skilful, you are ruined in the usual

[012-050]way.

[012-051]And if it be urged that whoever is armed will act in the same way, whether

[012-052]mercenary or not, I reply that when arms have to be resorted to, either by

[012-053]a prince or a republic, then the prince ought to go in person and perform

[012-054]the duty of a captain; the republic has to send its citizens, and when one

[012-055]is sent who does not turn out satisfactorily, it ought to recall him, and

[012-056]when one is worthy, to hold him by the laws so that he does not leave the

[012-057]command. And experience has shown princes and republics, single-handed,

[012-058]making the greatest progress, and mercenaries doing nothing except damage;

[012-059]and it is more difficult to bring a republic, armed with its own arms,

[012-060]under the sway of one of its citizens than it is to bring one armed with

[012-061]foreign arms. Rome and Sparta stood for many ages armed and free. The

[012-062]Switzers are completely armed and quite free.

[012-063]Of ancient mercenaries, for example, there are the Carthaginians, who were

[012-064]oppressed by their mercenary soldiers after the first war with the Romans,

[012-065]although the Carthaginians had their own citizens for captains. After the

[012-066]death of Epaminondas, Philip of Macedon was made captain of their soldiers

[012-067]by the Thebans, and after victory he took away their liberty.

[012-068]Duke Filippo being dead, the Milanese enlisted Francesco Sforza against the

[012-069]Venetians, and he, having overcome the enemy at Caravaggio,[2]

[012-070]allied himself with them to crush the Milanese, his masters. His father,

[012-071]Sforza, having been engaged by Queen Johanna[3]

[012-072]of Naples, left her unprotected, so that she was forced to throw herself into

[012-073]the arms of the King of Aragon, in order to save her kingdom. And if the

[012-074]Venetians and Florentines formerly extended their dominions by these arms, and

[012-075]yet their captains did not make themselves princes, but have defended them, I

[012-076]reply that the Florentines in this case have been favoured by chance, for of

[012-077]the able captains, of whom they might have stood in fear, some have not

[012-078]conquered, some have been opposed, and others have turned their ambitions

[012-079]elsewhere. One who did not conquer was Giovanni Acuto,[4]

[012-080]and since he did not conquer his fidelity cannot be proved; but every one will

[012-081]acknowledge that, had he conquered, the Florentines would have stood at his

[012-082]discretion. Sforza had the Bracceschi always against him, so they watched each

[012-083]other. Francesco turned his ambition to Lombardy; Braccio against the Church

[012-084]and the kingdom of Naples. But let us come to that which happened a short while

[012-085]ago. The Florentines appointed as their captain Pagolo Vitelli, a most prudent

[012-086]man, who from a private position had risen to the greatest renown. If this man

[012-087]had taken Pisa, nobody can deny that it would have been proper for the

[012-088]Florentines to keep in with him, for if he became the soldier of their enemies

[012-089]they had no means of resisting, and if they held to him they must obey him. The

[012-090]Venetians, if their achievements are considered, will be seen to have acted

[012-091]safely and gloriously so long as they sent to war their own men, when with

[012-092]armed gentlemen and plebians they did valiantly. This was before they turned to

[012-093]enterprises on land, but when they began to fight on land they forsook this

[012-094]virtue and followed the custom of Italy. And in the beginning of their

[012-095]expansion on land, through not having much territory, and because of their

[012-096]great reputation, they had not much to fear from their captains; but when they

[012-097]expanded, as under Carmignuola,[5]

[012-098]they had a taste of this mistake; for, having found him a most valiant man

[012-099](they beat the Duke of Milan under his leadership), and, on the other hand,

[012-100]knowing how lukewarm he was in the war, they feared they would no longer

[012-101]conquer under him, and for this reason they were not willing, nor were they

[012-102]able, to let him go; and so, not to lose again that which they had acquired,

[012-103]they were compelled, in order to secure themselves, to murder him. They had

[012-104]afterwards for their captains Bartolomeo da Bergamo, Roberto da San Severino,

[012-105]the count of Pitigliano,[6]

[012-106]and the like, under whom they had to dread loss and not gain, as happened

[012-107]afterwards at Vaila,[7]

[012-108]where in one battle they lost that which in eight hundred years they had

[012-109]acquired with so much trouble. Because from such arms conquests come but

[012-110]slowly, long delayed and inconsiderable, but the losses sudden and portentous.

[012-115][4]

[012-117]in the English wars in France, and was knighted by Edward III; afterwards he

[012-118]collected a body of troops and went into Italy. These became the famous

[012-119]“White Company.” He took part in many wars, and died in Florence in

[012-120]1394. He was born about 1320 at Sible Hedingham, a village in Essex. He married

[012-121]Domnia, a daughter of Bernabo Visconti.

[012-122][5]

[012-123]Carmignuola. Francesco Bussone, born at Carmagnola about 1390, executed at

[012-124]Venice, 5th May 1432.

[012-127]fighting for Venice against Sigismund, Duke of Austria, in 1487. “Primo

[012-128]capitano in Italia.”—Machiavelli. Count of Pitigliano; Nicolo

[012-129]Orsini, born 1442, died 1510.

[012-132]And as with these examples I have reached Italy, which has been ruled for

[012-133]many years by mercenaries, I wish to discuss them more seriously, in order

[012-134]that, having seen their rise and progress, one may be better prepared to

[012-135]counteract them. You must understand that the empire has recently come to

[012-136]be repudiated in Italy, that the Pope has acquired more temporal power,

[012-137]and that Italy has been divided up into more states, for the reason that

[012-138]many of the great cities took up arms against their nobles, who, formerly

[012-139]favoured by the emperor, were oppressing them, whilst the Church was

[012-140]favouring them so as to gain authority in temporal power: in many others

[012-141]their citizens became princes. From this it came to pass that Italy fell

[012-142]partly into the hands of the Church and of republics, and, the Church

[012-143]consisting of priests and the republic of citizens unaccustomed to arms,

[012-144]both commenced to enlist foreigners.

[012-145]The first who gave renown to this soldiery was Alberigo da Conio,[8]

[012-146]the Romagnian. From the school of this man sprang, among others, Braccio and

[012-147]Sforza, who in their time were the arbiters of Italy. After these came all the

[012-148]other captains who till now have directed the arms of Italy; and the end of all

[012-149]their valour has been, that she has been overrun by Charles, robbed by Louis,

[012-150]ravaged by Ferdinand, and insulted by the Switzers. The principle that has

[012-151]guided them has been, first, to lower the credit of infantry so that they might

[012-152]increase their own. They did this because, subsisting on their pay and without

[012-153]territory, they were unable to support many soldiers, and a few infantry did

[012-154]not give them any authority; so they were led to employ cavalry, with a

[012-155]moderate force of which they were maintained and honoured; and affairs were

[012-156]brought to such a pass that, in an army of twenty thousand soldiers, there were

[012-157]not to be found two thousand foot soldiers. They had, besides this, used every

[012-158]art to lessen fatigue and danger to themselves and their soldiers, not killing

[012-159]in the fray, but taking prisoners and liberating without ransom. They did not

[012-160]attack towns at night, nor did the garrisons of the towns attack encampments at

[012-161]night; they did not surround the camp either with stockade or ditch, nor did

[012-162]they campaign in the winter. All these things were permitted by their military

[012-163]rules, and devised by them to avoid, as I have said, both fatigue and dangers;

[012-164]thus they have brought Italy to slavery and contempt.

[012-165][8]

[012-166]Alberigo da Conio. Alberico da Barbiano, Count of Cunio in Romagna. He was the

[012-167]leader of the famous “Company of St George,” composed entirely of

[012-168]Italian soldiers. He died in 1409.
