## CHAPTER IX. CONCERNING A CIVIL PRINCIPALITY

[009-001]But coming to the other point—where a leading citizen becomes the

[009-002]prince of his country, not by wickedness or any intolerable violence, but

[009-003]by the favour of his fellow citizens—this may be called a civil

[009-004]principality: nor is genius or fortune altogether necessary to attain to

[009-005]it, but rather a happy shrewdness. I say then that such a principality is

[009-006]obtained either by the favour of the people or by the favour of the

[009-007]nobles. Because in all cities these two distinct parties are found, and

[009-008]from this it arises that the people do not wish to be ruled nor oppressed

[009-009]by the nobles, and the nobles wish to rule and oppress the people; and

[009-010]from these two opposite desires there arises in cities one of three

[009-011]results, either a principality, self-government, or anarchy.

[009-012]A principality is created either by the people or by the nobles,

[009-013]accordingly as one or other of them has the opportunity; for the nobles,

[009-014]seeing they cannot withstand the people, begin to cry up the reputation of

[009-015]one of themselves, and they make him a prince, so that under his shadow

[009-016]they can give vent to their ambitions. The people, finding they cannot

[009-017]resist the nobles, also cry up the reputation of one of themselves, and

[009-018]make him a prince so as to be defended by his authority. He who obtains

[009-019]sovereignty by the assistance of the nobles maintains himself with more

[009-020]difficulty than he who comes to it by the aid of the people, because the

[009-021]former finds himself with many around him who consider themselves his

[009-022]equals, and because of this he can neither rule nor manage them to his

[009-023]liking. But he who reaches sovereignty by popular favour finds himself

[009-024]alone, and has none around him, or few, who are not prepared to obey him.

[009-025]Besides this, one cannot by fair dealing, and without injury to others,

[009-026]satisfy the nobles, but you can satisfy the people, for their object is

[009-027]more righteous than that of the nobles, the latter wishing to oppress,

[009-028]while the former only desire not to be oppressed. It is to be added also

[009-029]that a prince can never secure himself against a hostile people, because

[009-030]of there being too many, whilst from the nobles he can secure himself, as

[009-031]they are few in number. The worst that a prince may expect from a hostile

[009-032]people is to be abandoned by them; but from hostile nobles he has not only

[009-033]to fear abandonment, but also that they will rise against him; for they,

[009-034]being in these affairs more far-seeing and astute, always come forward in

[009-035]time to save themselves, and to obtain favours from him whom they expect

[009-036]to prevail. Further, the prince is compelled to live always with the same

[009-037]people, but he can do well without the same nobles, being able to make and

[009-038]unmake them daily, and to give or take away authority when it pleases him.

[009-039]Therefore, to make this point clearer, I say that the nobles ought to be

[009-040]looked at mainly in two ways: that is to say, they either shape their

[009-041]course in such a way as binds them entirely to your fortune, or they do

[009-042]not. Those who so bind themselves, and are not rapacious, ought to be

[009-043]honoured and loved; those who do not bind themselves may be dealt with in

[009-044]two ways; they may fail to do this through pusillanimity and a natural

[009-045]want of courage, in which case you ought to make use of them, especially

[009-046]of those who are of good counsel; and thus, whilst in prosperity you

[009-047]honour them, in adversity you do not have to fear them. But when for their

[009-048]own ambitious ends they shun binding themselves, it is a token that they

[009-049]are giving more thought to themselves than to you, and a prince ought to

[009-050]guard against such, and to fear them as if they were open enemies, because

[009-051]in adversity they always help to ruin him.

[009-052]Therefore, one who becomes a prince through the favour of the people ought

[009-053]to keep them friendly, and this he can easily do seeing they only ask not

[009-054]to be oppressed by him. But one who, in opposition to the people, becomes

[009-055]a prince by the favour of the nobles, ought, above everything, to seek to

[009-056]win the people over to himself, and this he may easily do if he takes them

[009-057]under his protection. Because men, when they receive good from him of whom

[009-058]they were expecting evil, are bound more closely to their benefactor; thus

[009-059]the people quickly become more devoted to him than if he had been raised

[009-060]to the principality by their favours; and the prince can win their

[009-061]affections in many ways, but as these vary according to the circumstances

[009-062]one cannot give fixed rules, so I omit them; but, I repeat, it is

[009-063]necessary for a prince to have the people friendly, otherwise he has no

[009-064]security in adversity.

[009-065]Nabis,[1]

[009-066]Prince of the Spartans, sustained the attack of all Greece, and of a victorious

[009-067]Roman army, and against them he defended his country and his government; and

[009-068]for the overcoming of this peril it was only necessary for him to make himself

[009-069]secure against a few, but this would not have been sufficient had the people

[009-070]been hostile. And do not let any one impugn this statement with the trite

[009-071]proverb that “He who builds on the people, builds on the mud,” for

[009-072]this is true when a private citizen makes a foundation there, and persuades

[009-073]himself that the people will free him when he is oppressed by his enemies or by

[009-074]the magistrates; wherein he would find himself very often deceived, as happened

[009-075]to the Gracchi in Rome and to Messer Giorgio Scali[2]

[009-076]in Florence. But granted a prince who has established himself as above, who can

[009-077]command, and is a man of courage, undismayed in adversity, who does not fail in

[009-078]other qualifications, and who, by his resolution and energy, keeps the whole

[009-079]people encouraged—such a one will never find himself deceived in them,

[009-080]and it will be shown that he has laid his foundations well.

[009-081][1]

[009-082]Nabis, tyrant of Sparta, conquered by the Romans under Flamininus in 195 B.C.;

[009-083]killed 192 B.C.

[009-084][2]

[009-085]Messer Giorgio Scali. This event is to be found in Machiavelli’s

[009-086]“Florentine History,” Book III.

[009-087]These principalities are liable to danger when they are passing from the

[009-088]civil to the absolute order of government, for such princes either rule

[009-089]personally or through magistrates. In the latter case their government is

[009-090]weaker and more insecure, because it rests entirely on the goodwill of

[009-091]those citizens who are raised to the magistracy, and who, especially in

[009-092]troubled times, can destroy the government with great ease, either by

[009-093]intrigue or open defiance; and the prince has not the chance amid tumults

[009-094]to exercise absolute authority, because the citizens and subjects,

[009-095]accustomed to receive orders from magistrates, are not of a mind to obey

[009-096]him amid these confusions, and there will always be in doubtful times a

[009-097]scarcity of men whom he can trust. For such a prince cannot rely upon what

[009-098]he observes in quiet times, when citizens have need of the state, because

[009-099]then every one agrees with him; they all promise, and when death is far

[009-100]distant they all wish to die for him; but in troubled times, when the

[009-101]state has need of its citizens, then he finds but few. And so much the

[009-102]more is this experiment dangerous, inasmuch as it can only be tried once.

[009-103]Therefore a wise prince ought to adopt such a course that his citizens

[009-104]will always in every sort and kind of circumstance have need of the state

[009-105]and of him, and then he will always find them faithful.
