# Lion and Fox

The metaphor of the lion and the fox is one of the most famous images in *The Prince*. Drawing on classical sources (Cicero, Plutarch), Machiavelli argues that the successful prince must combine two natures: the strength of the lion to frighten wolves (enemies in open combat) and the cunning of the fox to recognize snares (deceptions and traps). Force without cunning is blind; cunning without force is weak.

## Machiavelli 的论述

The lion and fox appear in Chapter 18 — "In What Way Princes Should Keep Faith" — where Machiavelli discusses how a prince should use both law and force, both human and animal natures.

### 核心论点

- A prince must know how to use both the man and the beast; ancient writers taught this allegorically by showing Achilles and other princes being tutored by Chiron the centaur — half man, half beast (018 — centaur passage)
- The prince is compelled to know well how to use the beast, and ought to choose the fox and the lion — the lion cannot defend against snares, and the fox cannot defend against wolves (018-019, 018-020)
- Therefore it is necessary to be a fox to discover the snares and a lion to frighten the wolves (018-021)
- Those who know best how to employ the fox have succeeded best in the course of history — cunning, when properly disguised, is more effective than brute force alone (018-031)
- A prudent lord cannot and should not keep faith when such observance would harm him and the reasons that made him promise have vanished (018 — context for the metaphor)

### 历史例证

- **Pope Alexander VI** — Machiavelli's prime example of the fox: he never did anything but deceive men and always found victims for his fraud. His knowledge of the fox's art made him terrifically successful (018 reference)
- **Septimius Severus** — combined the lion and the fox so effectively that he ruled the Roman Empire undisturbed; he was both a very fierce lion and a very astute fox (019-177)
- **Cesare Borgia** — used the fox in his dealings with the Orsini (luring them to Senigallia) and the lion in his subjugation of the Romagna

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Lion and Fox vs. Force and Fraud**: The lion represents force (open violence); the fox represents fraud (cunning and deception). Machiavelli sees both as legitimate modes of princely action.
- **Lion and Fox vs. Law and Arms**: The man rules through law, the beast through force. The lion and fox are the beast's two aspects — the lion corresponds to arms, the fox to strategic deception.
- **Lion and Fox vs. Faith/Faithlessness**: The fox's mode is to break faith when necessary. The prince who is all lion cannot make promises credibly; the prince who is all fox cannot enforce them.
