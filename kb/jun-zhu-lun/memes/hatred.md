# Hatred (Odio)

In Machiavelli's political thought, hatred (odio) represents the most dangerous sentiment a ruler can provoke in his subjects. While a prince may be loved or feared, he must at all costs avoid being hated, for hatred leads to conspiracies, rebellions, and ultimately the loss of the state.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli treats hatred as a structural threat to princely power, distinct from both fear and love. His analysis of hatred spans multiple chapters, with the most concentrated treatment in Chapters 17 and 19.

### 核心论点

- A prince should accept being feared if he cannot be loved, but must never be hated (017-046)
- Hatred is provoked above all by confiscation of property and violation of women — these the prince must avoid (017-088)
- Hatred from the people is more dangerous than hatred from the nobility, because the people are more numerous and can abandon the prince in crisis (019-083)
- Conspiracies against a prince succeed only when the conspirator believes the people are already disposed to hatred against the ruler (003-108)
- A prince who is hated by the people will find no refuge; every foreign enemy becomes an existential threat

### 历史例证

- **Cesare Borgia** — though feared for his cruelty in the Romagna, he avoided widespread hatred by executing Remirro de Orco publicly to satisfy the people (007-125, 007-165)
- **Roman Emperors** — Machiavelli analyzes how emperors who provoked hatred (through confiscation, cruelty to subjects) were invariably overthrown, while those who maintained popular favor survived
- **The French in Italy** — their occupation failed partly because they provoked hatred through oppressive taxation and disregard for local customs (003-108)

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

Hatred stands at the negative pole of Machiavelli's emotional calculus of rule:

- **Fear vs. Hatred**: Fear is compatible with stable rule; hatred is not. A feared prince can maintain power; a hated prince will fall. The boundary is property and women — violating these turns fear into hatred.
- **Love vs. Hatred**: Love is ideal but unreliable; hatred is fatal. Machiavelli's famous dilemma is not "loved vs. feared" but rather "how to be feared without being hated."
- **Cruelty**: Well-used cruelty (as by Cesare Borgia) may inspire fear but not necessarily hatred; poorly-used cruelty (as by Agathocles) breeds both fear and hatred.
- **Conspiracy**: Hatred is the precondition for successful conspiracy — conspirators act when they believe the people share their hatred of the prince.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

Hatred is the limiting condition on princely action. It defines the boundary beyond which virtù cannot save a ruler. The entire apparatus of princely statecraft — fortresses, arms, alliances, reputation — is designed to enable effective rule while avoiding the hatred that would undermine it.

## 后世影响

The concept of hatred as the fatal political emotion influenced subsequent republican thought (Montesquieu's "principle of monarchy is honor, not fear") and modern political science. The distinction between fear and hatred remains central to theories of political legitimacy and authoritarian stability.
