# Auxiliaries

Auxiliary arms are troops lent by an allied power to assist a prince. Machiavelli considers them even more dangerous than mercenaries, "because they are all united, and trained to obey someone else" (013-002). If they win, the prince becomes their prisoner; if they lose, he is ruined anyway.

## Why Auxiliaries Are Fatal

Machiavelli's argument is simple: auxiliaries are "useful to him who sends them, but fatal to him who calls them in" (013-014). Pope Julius II made the fatal error of relying on foreign auxiliaries in his campaigns, and "had he not been respected by his own, he would have been ruined by his allies before he could have been aided by them" (013-018).

The biblical story of David and Goliath illustrates the point: David refused Saul's armor because it was not his own; he went with his sling and his knife. "In conclusion, the arms of another either fall from your back, or weigh you down, or bind you fast" (013-020).

## Contrast with Mercenaries

While mercenaries are disunited and cowardly, auxiliaries are unified and disciplined — but loyal to another. A wise prince would rather lose with his own troops than win with someone else's, because the victory is not truly his.
