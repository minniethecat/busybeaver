## CHAPTER XVI. CONCERNING LIBERALITY AND MEANNESS

[016-001]Commencing then with the first of the above-named characteristics, I say

[016-002]that it would be well to be reputed liberal. Nevertheless, liberality

[016-003]exercised in a way that does not bring you the reputation for it, injures

[016-004]you; for if one exercises it honestly and as it should be exercised, it

[016-005]may not become known, and you will not avoid the reproach of its opposite.

[016-006]Therefore, any one wishing to maintain among men the name of liberal is

[016-007]obliged to avoid no attribute of magnificence; so that a prince thus

[016-008]inclined will consume in such acts all his property, and will be compelled

[016-009]in the end, if he wish to maintain the name of liberal, to unduly weigh

[016-010]down his people, and tax them, and do everything he can to get money. This

[016-011]will soon make him odious to his subjects, and becoming poor he will be

[016-012]little valued by any one; thus, with his liberality, having offended many

[016-013]and rewarded few, he is affected by the very first trouble and imperilled

[016-014]by whatever may be the first danger; recognizing this himself, and wishing

[016-015]to draw back from it, he runs at once into the reproach of being miserly.

[016-016]Therefore, a prince, not being able to exercise this virtue of liberality

[016-017]in such a way that it is recognized, except to his cost, if he is wise he

[016-018]ought not to fear the reputation of being mean, for in time he will come

[016-019]to be more considered than if liberal, seeing that with his economy his

[016-020]revenues are enough, that he can defend himself against all attacks, and

[016-021]is able to engage in enterprises without burdening his people; thus it

[016-022]comes to pass that he exercises liberality towards all from whom he does

[016-023]not take, who are numberless, and meanness towards those to whom he does

[016-024]not give, who are few.

[016-025]We have not seen great things done in our time except by those who have

[016-026]been considered mean; the rest have failed. Pope Julius the Second was

[016-027]assisted in reaching the papacy by a reputation for liberality, yet he did

[016-028]not strive afterwards to keep it up, when he made war on the King of

[016-029]France; and he made many wars without imposing any extraordinary tax on

[016-030]his subjects, for he supplied his additional expenses out of his long

[016-031]thriftiness. The present King of Spain would not have undertaken or

[016-032]conquered in so many enterprises if he had been reputed liberal. A prince,

[016-033]therefore, provided that he has not to rob his subjects, that he can

[016-034]defend himself, that he does not become poor and abject, that he is not

[016-035]forced to become rapacious, ought to hold of little account a reputation

[016-036]for being mean, for it is one of those vices which will enable him to

[016-037]govern.

[016-038]And if any one should say: Caesar obtained empire by liberality, and many

[016-039]others have reached the highest positions by having been liberal, and by

[016-040]being considered so, I answer: Either you are a prince in fact, or in a

[016-041]way to become one. In the first case this liberality is dangerous, in the

[016-042]second it is very necessary to be considered liberal; and Caesar was one

[016-043]of those who wished to become pre-eminent in Rome; but if he had survived

[016-044]after becoming so, and had not moderated his expenses, he would have

[016-045]destroyed his government. And if any one should reply: Many have been

[016-046]princes, and have done great things with armies, who have been considered

[016-047]very liberal, I reply: Either a prince spends that which is his own or his

[016-048]subjects’ or else that of others. In the first case he ought to be

[016-049]sparing, in the second he ought not to neglect any opportunity for

[016-050]liberality. And to the prince who goes forth with his army, supporting it

[016-051]by pillage, sack, and extortion, handling that which belongs to others,

[016-052]this liberality is necessary, otherwise he would not be followed by

[016-053]soldiers. And of that which is neither yours nor your subjects’ you can be

[016-054]a ready giver, as were Cyrus, Caesar, and Alexander; because it does not

[016-055]take away your reputation if you squander that of others, but adds to it;

[016-056]it is only squandering your own that injures you.

[016-057]And there is nothing wastes so rapidly as liberality, for even whilst you

[016-058]exercise it you lose the power to do so, and so become either poor or

[016-059]despised, or else, in avoiding poverty, rapacious and hated. And a prince

[016-060]should guard himself, above all things, against being despised and hated;

[016-061]and liberality leads you to both. Therefore it is wiser to have a

[016-062]reputation for meanness which brings reproach without hatred, than to be

[016-063]compelled through seeking a reputation for liberality to incur a name for

[016-064]rapacity which begets reproach with hatred.
