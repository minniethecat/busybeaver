# Pope Alexander VI

**Pope Alexander VI** (born Rodrigo Borgia, 1431–1503) was the pontiff whose papacy (1492–1503) transformed the political landscape of Renaissance Italy. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli treats him as the supreme example of ecclesiastical power deployed for secular state-building — and as the figure whose ambitions created the conditions for Cesare Borgia's extraordinary career.

## Life and Pontificate

Rodrigo Borgia was born in Xàtiva, Valencia, and rose through the Church hierarchy under the patronage of his uncle, Pope Calixtus III. His election to the papacy in 1492 was secured through simony on an unprecedented scale — a fact Machiavelli treats not as scandal but as political datum.

As pope, Alexander pursued two intertwined objectives: enriching his children (particularly Cesare and Lucrezia) and consolidating papal temporal power in the Romagna. His methods combined diplomatic manipulation, strategic marriage alliances, and the ruthless use of military force through Cesare.

> [P01-117] the ambitions of Pope Alexander VI and his son, Cesare Borgia, t…

The father-son partnership between Alexander and Cesare is one of the most striking features of Renaissance papal politics. Alexander provided legitimacy, funding, and ecclesiastical cover; Cesare provided military execution. Together, they came closer than any previous pope to converting the Papal States from a nominal suzerainty into a real territorial state.

## Role in The Prince

### The Ecclesiastical Principality

Alexander VI is Machiavelli's primary example in Chapter 11 ("Concerning Ecclesiastical Principalities"). The analysis is layered:

> [003-240] Alexander VI. Born 1460, died 1510.

Machiavelli notes that ecclesiastical principalities are acquired either by fortune or by virtue — but maintained without either, "sustained by the ancient ordinances of religion." Alexander VI's achievement was to demonstrate that even within these constraints, a pope could use the instruments of temporal power to build something approaching a secular state.

### The Source of Cesare's Fortune

Alexander is central to Machiavelli's analysis of Cesare Borgia in Chapter 7. Cesare acquired his state "by the fortune of his father" — and lost it when that fortune failed:

> [007-191] Alexander VI died of fever, 18th August 15…

Alexander's death in August 1503 is the pivotal event of Chapter 7. Cesare, who had planned for every contingency except his own illness coinciding with his father's death, lost everything within months. The lesson is characteristically Machiavellian: fortune, even when apparently secure, cannot be relied upon; only one's own arms and virtue provide lasting security.

### The Secular Pope

> [P01-299] Alexander VI. The cloak of religion still …

Machiavelli observes that Alexander "never did anything, nor thought of anything, but the aggrandisement of his children and the deception of men." The "cloak of religion" — which Alexander wielded with cynical mastery — was instrumental, not spiritual. Yet Machiavelli's tone is not condemnatory but analytical: Alexander understood that ecclesiastical power, combined with secular ambition, could achieve what neither could achieve alone.

> [012-036] bons mots of Alexander VI, and refers to the ease with …

Alexander's famous quips — such as his remark that it was easier to deceive a thousand men than to satisfy one — are cited by Machiavelli as evidence of the pope's clear-eyed understanding of political psychology. Alexander knew that appearances mattered more than realities, a principle his son Cesare would apply with devastating effectiveness.

## The Borgia Strategy

| Element | Alexander's Role | Cesare's Role |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| Legitimacy | Papal authority | Military command |
| Funding | Church revenues | Booty and conquest |
| Diplomacy | Papal alliances | Local negotiations |
| Elimination of Rivals | Excommunication, interdict | Assassination, war |
| Long-term Goal | Borgia dynasty in Romagna | Independent kingdom |

The partnership was complementary but fundamentally unstable. Alexander could provide Cesare with everything except what mattered most: independent virtù. When Alexander died, Cesare's dependence on paternal fortune was exposed — and the Romagna project collapsed.

## Historical Evaluation

Alexander VI remains one of the most controversial figures in papal history. Catholic historians have traditionally portrayed him as the nadir of the Renaissance papacy — a simoniac, a nepotist, and a cynical manipulator of sacred office for secular gain. Machiavelli's treatment is more nuanced: Alexander was effective, and effectiveness, in *The Prince*, carries its own form of justification.

Modern historiography has partially rehabilitated Alexander, emphasizing his administrative reforms of the Papal States and his diplomatic skill in navigating the French-Spanish rivalry that dominated Italian politics after 1494. The Borgia papacy, seen in this light, was less a moral collapse than a rational — if extreme — response to the structural weakness of papal temporal power in an age of rising nation-states.
