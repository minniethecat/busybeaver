# League of Cambrai

The **League of Cambrai**, concluded on 10 December 1508, was an alliance of France, the Papacy, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire against the Republic of Venice. For Machiavelli, it exemplified both the irresistible force of united great powers and the self-defeating logic of coalitions whose members are also rivals.

## Background

By 1508, Venice had become the most territorially ambitious state in Italy. The republic's expansion into the Romagna — traditionally papal territory — had particularly antagonized Pope Julius II. Its control of Adriatic trade routes clashed with Imperial interests. Its occupation of Cremona and other former Milanese possessions angered Louis XII, who considered himself the rightful Duke of Milan.

The genius of Julius II lay in persuading Venice's various enemies to act simultaneously rather than sequentially. Each power had its own grievances; each saw advantage in dismembering Venetian territory; each — as Machiavelli would later observe — was strengthening a rival by participating.

## The Treaty and Its Consequences

> [P01-161] events arising out of the League of Cambrai, made in 1508 between the…

The treaty partitioned Venice's mainland possessions: France would receive Cremona, Bergamo, and Brescia; the Empire would receive Verona, Vicenza, and Padua; Spain would receive the Apulian ports; the Pope would receive the Romagna cities. Venice would be reduced to its lagoon.

The military outcome was swift. The Battle of Vaila (Agnadello) on 14 May 1509 destroyed the Venetian field army and opened the Terraferma to occupation. Yet within eighteen months, the League had collapsed — not because Venice recovered, but because the victors turned on each other.

## Role in The Prince

### The Double-Edged Alliance

The League of Cambrai appears in *The Prince* primarily through Machiavelli's analysis of Louis XII's five errors in Chapter 3. Louis's decision to join the League was, in Machiavelli's framework, an error not because it was unjust but because it strengthened the Pope — who would later organize Louis's expulsion.

> [P01-167] …ench, because friendship with France had dictated the entire polic…

The League illustrates Machiavelli's general rule: "He who is the cause of another's becoming powerful is ruined." By helping Julius II humble Venice, Louis empowered the one Italian ruler capable of organizing resistance to French domination. The pope used his enhanced prestige to form the Holy League (1511), which drove France from Italy within two years.

### Fortune and Coalition Politics

> [P01-169] against France, and with the assistance of t…

The Swiss, who had fought alongside France at Vaila, were subsequently recruited by Julius II into the anti-French Holy League. This reversal — allies becoming enemies within a single campaigning season — exemplifies the volatility of fortune in coalition warfare. A prince who relies on allies he does not control exposes himself to fortune's caprice.

### The Limits of Power Politics

The League also demonstrates a subtler Machiavellian point: victory itself can be destabilizing. The very completeness of Venice's defeat removed the common enemy that had held the coalition together. Once Venice was no longer a threat, the structural rivalries among the League's members reasserted themselves. The pattern — coalition forms against a common enemy, defeats it, then collapses into internal conflict — is one Machiavelli recognized as nearly inevitable in multi-polar power systems.
