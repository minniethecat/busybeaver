## CHAPTER VII. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES WHICH ARE ACQUIRED EITHER BY THE ARMS OF OTHERS
OR BY GOOD FORTUNE

[007-001]Those who solely by good fortune become princes from being private

[007-002]citizens have little trouble in rising, but much in keeping atop; they

[007-003]have not any difficulties on the way up, because they fly, but they have

[007-004]many when they reach the summit. Such are those to whom some state is

[007-005]given either for money or by the favour of him who bestows it; as happened

[007-006]to many in Greece, in the cities of Ionia and of the Hellespont, where

[007-007]princes were made by Darius, in order that they might hold the cities both

[007-008]for his security and his glory; as also were those emperors who, by the

[007-009]corruption of the soldiers, from being citizens came to empire. Such stand

[007-010]simply elevated upon the goodwill and the fortune of him who has elevated

[007-011]them—two most inconstant and unstable things. Neither have they the

[007-012]knowledge requisite for the position; because, unless they are men of

[007-013]great worth and ability, it is not reasonable to expect that they should

[007-014]know how to command, having always lived in a private condition; besides,

[007-015]they cannot hold it because they have not forces which they can keep

[007-016]friendly and faithful.

[007-017]States that rise unexpectedly, then, like all other things in nature which are

[007-018]born and grow rapidly, cannot leave their foundations and correspondencies[1]

[007-019]fixed in such a way that the first storm will not overthrow them; unless, as is

[007-020]said, those who unexpectedly become princes are men of so much ability that

[007-021]they know they have to be prepared at once to hold that which fortune has

[007-022]thrown into their laps, and that those foundations, which others have laid

[007-023]*before* they became princes, they must lay *afterwards*.

[007-024][1]

[007-025]“Le radici e corrispondenze,” their roots (i.e. foundations) and

[007-026]correspondencies or relations with other states—a common meaning of

[007-027]“correspondence” and “correspondency” in the sixteenth

[007-028]and seventeenth centuries.

[007-029]Concerning these two methods of rising to be a prince by ability or fortune, I

[007-030]wish to adduce two examples within our own recollection, and these are

[007-031]Francesco Sforza[2]

[007-032]and Cesare Borgia. Francesco, by proper means and with great ability, from

[007-033]being a private person rose to be Duke of Milan, and that which he had acquired

[007-034]with a thousand anxieties he kept with little trouble. On the other hand,

[007-035]Cesare Borgia, called by the people Duke Valentino, acquired his state during

[007-036]the ascendancy of his father, and on its decline he lost it, notwithstanding

[007-037]that he had taken every measure and done all that ought to be done by a wise

[007-038]and able man to fix firmly his roots in the states which the arms and fortunes

[007-039]of others had bestowed on him.

[007-040][2]

[007-041]Francesco Sforza, born 1401, died 1466. He married Bianca Maria Visconti, a

[007-042]natural daughter of Filippo Visconti, the Duke of Milan, on whose death he

[007-043]procured his own elevation to the duchy. Machiavelli was the accredited agent

[007-044]of the Florentine Republic to Cesare Borgia (1478-1507) during the

[007-045]transactions which led up to the assassinations of the Orsini and Vitelli at

[007-046]Sinigalia, and along with his letters to his chiefs in Florence he has left an

[007-047]account, written ten years before *The Prince*, of the proceedings of the

[007-048]duke in his “Descritione del modo tenuto dal duca Valentino nello

[007-049]ammazzare Vitellozzo Vitelli,” etc., a translation of which is appended

[007-050]to the present work.

[007-051]Because, as is stated above, he who has not first laid his foundations may

[007-052]be able with great ability to lay them afterwards, but they will be laid

[007-053]with trouble to the architect and danger to the building. If, therefore,

[007-054]all the steps taken by the duke be considered, it will be seen that he

[007-055]laid solid foundations for his future power, and I do not consider it

[007-056]superfluous to discuss them, because I do not know what better precepts to

[007-057]give a new prince than the example of his actions; and if his dispositions

[007-058]were of no avail, that was not his fault, but the extraordinary and

[007-059]extreme malignity of fortune.

[007-060]Alexander the Sixth, in wishing to aggrandize the duke, his son, had many

[007-061]immediate and prospective difficulties. Firstly, he did not see his way to

[007-062]make him master of any state that was not a state of the Church; and if he

[007-063]was willing to rob the Church he knew that the Duke of Milan and the

[007-064]Venetians would not consent, because Faenza and Rimini were already under

[007-065]the protection of the Venetians. Besides this, he saw the arms of Italy,

[007-066]especially those by which he might have been assisted, in hands that would

[007-067]fear the aggrandizement of the Pope, namely, the Orsini and the Colonnesi

[007-068]and their following. It behoved him, therefore, to upset this state of

[007-069]affairs and embroil the powers, so as to make himself securely master of

[007-070]part of their states. This was easy for him to do, because he found the

[007-071]Venetians, moved by other reasons, inclined to bring back the French into

[007-072]Italy; he would not only not oppose this, but he would render it more easy

[007-073]by dissolving the former marriage of King Louis. Therefore the king came

[007-074]into Italy with the assistance of the Venetians and the consent of

[007-075]Alexander. He was no sooner in Milan than the Pope had soldiers from him

[007-076]for the attempt on the Romagna, which yielded to him on the reputation of

[007-077]the king. The duke, therefore, having acquired the Romagna and beaten the

[007-078]Colonnesi, while wishing to hold that and to advance further, was hindered

[007-079]by two things: the one, his forces did not appear loyal to him, the other,

[007-080]the goodwill of France: that is to say, he feared that the forces of the

[007-081]Orsini, which he was using, would not stand to him, that not only might

[007-082]they hinder him from winning more, but might themselves seize what he had

[007-083]won, and that the king might also do the same. Of the Orsini he had a

[007-084]warning when, after taking Faenza and attacking Bologna, he saw them go

[007-085]very unwillingly to that attack. And as to the king, he learned his mind

[007-086]when he himself, after taking the Duchy of Urbino, attacked Tuscany, and

[007-087]the king made him desist from that undertaking; hence the duke decided to

[007-088]depend no more upon the arms and the luck of others.

[007-089]For the first thing he weakened the Orsini and Colonnesi parties in Rome, by

[007-090]gaining to himself all their adherents who were gentlemen, making them his

[007-091]gentlemen, giving them good pay, and, according to their rank, honouring them

[007-092]with office and command in such a way that in a few months all attachment to

[007-093]the factions was destroyed and turned entirely to the duke. After this he

[007-094]awaited an opportunity to crush the Orsini, having scattered the adherents of

[007-095]the Colonna house. This came to him soon and he used it well; for the Orsini,

[007-096]perceiving at length that the aggrandizement of the duke and the Church was

[007-097]ruin to them, called a meeting of the Magione in Perugia. From this sprung the

[007-098]rebellion at Urbino and the tumults in the Romagna, with endless dangers to the

[007-099]duke, all of which he overcame with the help of the French. Having restored his

[007-100]authority, not to leave it at risk by trusting either to the French or other

[007-101]outside forces, he had recourse to his wiles, and he knew so well how to

[007-102]conceal his mind that, by the mediation of Signor Pagolo—whom the duke

[007-103]did not fail to secure with all kinds of attention, giving him money, apparel,

[007-104]and horses—the Orsini were reconciled, so that their simplicity brought

[007-105]them into his power at Sinigalia.[3]

[007-106]Having exterminated the leaders, and turned their partisans into his friends,

[007-107]the duke laid sufficiently good foundations to his power, having all the

[007-108]Romagna and the Duchy of Urbino; and the people now beginning to appreciate

[007-109]their prosperity, he gained them all over to himself. And as this point is

[007-110]worthy of notice, and to be imitated by others, I am not willing to leave it

[007-111]out.

[007-112][3]

[007-113]Sinigalia, 31st December 1502.

[007-114]When the duke occupied the Romagna he found it under the rule of weak masters,

[007-115]who rather plundered their subjects than ruled them, and gave them more cause

[007-116]for disunion than for union, so that the country was full of robbery, quarrels,

[007-117]and every kind of violence; and so, wishing to bring back peace and obedience

[007-118]to authority, he considered it necessary to give it a good governor. Thereupon

[007-119]he promoted Messer Ramiro d’Orco,[4]

[007-120]a swift and cruel man, to whom he gave the fullest power. This man in a short

[007-121]time restored peace and unity with the greatest success. Afterwards the duke

[007-122]considered that it was not advisable to confer such excessive authority, for he

[007-123]had no doubt but that he would become odious, so he set up a court of judgment

[007-124]in the country, under a most excellent president, wherein all cities had their

[007-125]advocates. And because he knew that the past severity had caused some hatred

[007-126]against himself, so, to clear himself in the minds of the people, and gain them

[007-127]entirely to himself, he desired to show that, if any cruelty had been

[007-128]practised, it had not originated with him, but in the natural sternness of the

[007-129]minister. Under this pretence he took Ramiro, and one morning caused him to be

[007-130]executed and left on the piazza at Cesena with the block and a bloody knife at

[007-131]his side. The barbarity of this spectacle caused the people to be at once

[007-132]satisfied and dismayed.

[007-133][4]

[007-134]Ramiro d’Orco. Ramiro de Lorqua.

[007-135]But let us return whence we started. I say that the duke, finding himself

[007-136]now sufficiently powerful and partly secured from immediate dangers by

[007-137]having armed himself in his own way, and having in a great measure crushed

[007-138]those forces in his vicinity that could injure him if he wished to proceed

[007-139]with his conquest, had next to consider France, for he knew that the king,

[007-140]who too late was aware of his mistake, would not support him. And from

[007-141]this time he began to seek new alliances and to temporize with France in

[007-142]the expedition which she was making towards the kingdom of Naples against

[007-143]the Spaniards who were besieging Gaeta. It was his intention to secure

[007-144]himself against them, and this he would have quickly accomplished had

[007-145]Alexander lived.

[007-146]Such was his line of action as to present affairs. But as to the future he

[007-147]had to fear, in the first place, that a new successor to the Church might

[007-148]not be friendly to him and might seek to take from him that which

[007-149]Alexander had given him, so he decided to act in four ways. Firstly, by

[007-150]exterminating the families of those lords whom he had despoiled, so as to

[007-151]take away that pretext from the Pope. Secondly, by winning to himself all

[007-152]the gentlemen of Rome, so as to be able to curb the Pope with their aid,

[007-153]as has been observed. Thirdly, by converting the college more to himself.

[007-154]Fourthly, by acquiring so much power before the Pope should die that he

[007-155]could by his own measures resist the first shock. Of these four things, at

[007-156]the death of Alexander, he had accomplished three. For he had killed as

[007-157]many of the dispossessed lords as he could lay hands on, and few had

[007-158]escaped; he had won over the Roman gentlemen, and he had the most numerous

[007-159]party in the college. And as to any fresh acquisition, he intended to

[007-160]become master of Tuscany, for he already possessed Perugia and Piombino,

[007-161]and Pisa was under his protection. And as he had no longer to study France

[007-162](for the French were already driven out of the kingdom of Naples by the

[007-163]Spaniards, and in this way both were compelled to buy his goodwill), he

[007-164]pounced down upon Pisa. After this, Lucca and Siena yielded at once,

[007-165]partly through hatred and partly through fear of the Florentines; and the

[007-166]Florentines would have had no remedy had he continued to prosper, as he

[007-167]was prospering the year that Alexander died, for he had acquired so much

[007-168]power and reputation that he would have stood by himself, and no longer

[007-169]have depended on the luck and the forces of others, but solely on his own

[007-170]power and ability.

[007-171]But Alexander died five years after he had first drawn the sword. He left the

[007-172]duke with the state of Romagna alone consolidated, with the rest in the air,

[007-173]between two most powerful hostile armies, and sick unto death. Yet there were

[007-174]in the duke such boldness and ability, and he knew so well how men are to be

[007-175]won or lost, and so firm were the foundations which in so short a time he had

[007-176]laid, that if he had not had those armies on his back, or if he had been in

[007-177]good health, he would have overcome all difficulties. And it is seen that his

[007-178]foundations were good, for the Romagna awaited him for more than a month. In

[007-179]Rome, although but half alive, he remained secure; and whilst the Baglioni, the

[007-180]Vitelli, and the Orsini might come to Rome, they could not effect anything

[007-181]against him. If he could not have made Pope him whom he wished, at least the

[007-182]one whom he did not wish would not have been elected. But if he had been in

[007-183]sound health at the death of Alexander,[5]

[007-184]everything would have been different to him. On the day that Julius the

[007-185]Second[6] was

[007-186]elected, he told me that he had thought of everything that might occur at the

[007-187]death of his father, and had provided a remedy for all, except that he had

[007-188]never anticipated that, when the death did happen, he himself would be on the

[007-189]point to die.

[007-190][5]

[007-191]Alexander VI died of fever, 18th August 1503.

[007-192][6]

[007-193]Julius II was Giuliano della Rovere, Cardinal of San Pietro ad Vincula, born

[007-194]1443, died 1513.

[007-195]When all the actions of the duke are recalled, I do not know how to blame

[007-196]him, but rather it appears to be, as I have said, that I ought to offer

[007-197]him for imitation to all those who, by the fortune or the arms of others,

[007-198]are raised to government. Because he, having a lofty spirit and

[007-199]far-reaching aims, could not have regulated his conduct otherwise, and

[007-200]only the shortness of the life of Alexander and his own sickness

[007-201]frustrated his designs. Therefore, he who considers it necessary to secure

[007-202]himself in his new principality, to win friends, to overcome either by

[007-203]force or fraud, to make himself beloved and feared by the people, to be

[007-204]followed and revered by the soldiers, to exterminate those who have power

[007-205]or reason to hurt him, to change the old order of things for new, to be

[007-206]severe and gracious, magnanimous and liberal, to destroy a disloyal

[007-207]soldiery and to create new, to maintain friendship with kings and princes

[007-208]in such a way that they must help him with zeal and offend with caution,

[007-209]cannot find a more lively example than the actions of this man.

[007-210]Only can he be blamed for the election of Julius the Second, in whom he made a

[007-211]bad choice, because, as is said, not being able to elect a Pope to his own

[007-212]mind, he could have hindered any other from being elected Pope; and he ought

[007-213]never to have consented to the election of any cardinal whom he had injured or

[007-214]who had cause to fear him if they became pontiffs. For men injure either from

[007-215]fear or hatred. Those whom he had injured, amongst others, were San Pietro ad

[007-216]Vincula, Colonna, San Giorgio, and Ascanio.[7]

[007-217]The rest, in becoming Pope, had to fear him, Rouen and the Spaniards excepted;

[007-218]the latter from their relationship and obligations, the former from his

[007-219]influence, the kingdom of France having relations with him. Therefore, above

[007-220]everything, the duke ought to have created a Spaniard Pope, and, failing him,

[007-221]he ought to have consented to Rouen and not San Pietro ad Vincula. He who

[007-222]believes that new benefits will cause great personages to forget old injuries

[007-223]is deceived. Therefore, the duke erred in his choice, and it was the cause of

[007-224]his ultimate ruin.

[007-225][7]

[007-226]San Giorgio is Raffaello Riario. Ascanio is Ascanio Sforza.
