# Necessity (Necessità)

In *The Prince*, **necessity** (*necessità*) is the fundamental force that both constrains and enables political action — a prince who acts out of necessity is justified; one who ignores necessity is doomed.

## Machiavelli's Argument

### Necessity as Political Justification

Machiavelli repeatedly invokes necessity to explain and justify actions that would otherwise be condemned. For a prince, necessity transforms the morally questionable into the politically mandatory.

> [002-016] …rince has less cause and less necessity to offend; hence it

In hereditary principalities, the absence of necessity to offend makes rulers more secure. Conversely, new princes face greater necessity to act harshly because their position is inherently precarious. The degree of necessity directly correlates with the legitimacy of extreme measures.

### Necessity in State Acquisition

When acquiring new territories, necessity drives both the conqueror and the conquered:

> [003-008] …on another natural and common necessity,

The "natural and common necessity" that drives a prince to expand is the same force that compels subjects to accept new rule. Machiavelli treats this not as moral failing but as political physics — an inescapable dynamic that wise rulers harness rather than resist.

> [003-210] that necessity.

In the context of mixed principalities, necessity compels actions that might otherwise seem excessive. The prince who hesitates when necessity demands action loses his state; the one who acts decisively, even ruthlessly, preserves it.

### Necessity and Military Power

The connection between necessity and arms is one of Machiavelli's most direct arguments:

> [013-073] the necessity of being armed with forces of…

A prince must maintain his own arms — the necessity is absolute. Without native troops, a ruler "of necessity" falls into ruin:

> [012-008] …laid, otherwise it follows of necessity he will go to

This military necessity undergirds Machiavelli's famous rejection of mercenaries and auxiliaries. The dependence on foreign arms is not merely imprudent; it violates the fundamental necessity of self-sufficiency.

### Necessity and Princely Virtues

The exercise of virtue itself is governed by necessity:

> [015-015] …use of it or not according to necessity. Therefore,

A prince need not possess every virtue, but must appear to possess them — and must know when necessity compels their actual exercise. This pragmatic calculus distinguishes the successful prince from the failed idealist.

> [021-075] necessity compels him, as is said above…

When necessity compels, even normally undesirable actions (breaking faith, cruelty) become legitimate instruments of rule.

### Necessity in Extreme Circumstances

The most dramatic invocation of necessity appears in cases of existential threat:

> [008-028] …haginians, reduced to extreme necessity, were

Agathocles, the Sicilian tyrant, exemplifies how extreme necessity drives extreme measures. His rise through crime is contextualized by the necessity of his circumstances — though Machiavelli still distinguishes necessity-driven action from gratuitous cruelty.

> [008-124] him change; because if the necessity for this comes in troubled ti…

When necessity arrives during troubled times, only the prince who has prepared through prior harshness can survive. Waiting until necessity strikes to act is fatal.

## Relation to Other Concepts

### Necessity and [[Fortuna]]

Where Fortuna represents the chaotic, uncontrollable element of political life, Necessity represents the predictable constraints that a prudent prince can anticipate and master. Fortuna may be a river that floods unpredictably, but necessity is the riverbank — fixed, knowable, and navigable by the wise.

### Necessity and [[Virtù]]

Virtù is the quality that allows a prince to recognize necessity and act upon it decisively. A prince with virtù does not merely submit to necessity; he channels it, using its force to propel his own designs. Necessity without virtù produces paralysis; virtù without awareness of necessity produces recklessness.

### Necessity and Cruelty

Cruelty, when well-used, is cruelty compelled by necessity. The distinction between "cruelty well-used" and "cruelty abused" in Chapter 8 hinges precisely on whether necessity drove the action. Necessity transforms cruelty from vice into instrument.

## Historical Exemplars

### Agathocles of Syracuse

Agathocles rose from low origins to become king of Syracuse through "extreme necessity" (008-028). His case illustrates the Machiavellian paradox: necessity can drive a man to crime, yet the same necessity earns him a place among rulers rather than criminals — provided he acts decisively and once, rather than repeatedly and gratuitously.

### Cesare Borgia

The Duke Valentino embodied necessity-driven action. His swift cruelty through Remirro de Orco, his elimination of the Orsini conspirators at Sinigaglia, his reliance on his own arms rather than auxiliaries — each decision was framed by Machiavelli as compelled by political necessity rather than personal vice.

### Scipio Africanus

Scipio's excessive clemency toward his mutinous troops (014-066) represents a failure to act when necessity demanded severity. His "liberality" and "mercy" — normally virtues — became vices because they defied political necessity. The contrast with Agathocles is instructive: both men faced necessity, but only one recognized it.

## In Machiavelli's System

Necessity occupies a foundational position in Machiavelli's political philosophy. It serves three interconnected functions:

1. **Explanatory**: Why do princes act as they do? Necessity.
2. **Justificatory**: Why may princes break conventional moral rules? Necessity.
3. **Predictive**: What determines whether a prince will succeed? His capacity to recognize and respond to necessity.

The concept bridges Machiavelli's descriptive realism (this is how politics works) and his prescriptive counsel (this is how you must act to survive). In this sense, necessity is the engine of *The Prince*: every chapter's advice traces back to the necessities imposed by the political world as it is, not as it ought to be.

## Later Influence

The Machiavellian concept of necessity influenced early modern statecraft through the doctrine of *ragion di stato* (reason of state), which held that the state's survival constituted a necessity overriding ordinary moral constraints. Thinkers from Botero to Hobbes absorbed and transformed the concept, stripping it of Machiavelli's rhetorical particularity while preserving its structural function: necessity as the ultimate political justification.

In the 20th century, theorists of "political realism" from Max Weber to Hans Morgenthau echoed Machiavelli's necessity logic in their critiques of moralistic foreign policy. The claim that political actors face necessities that ordinary morality cannot adjudicate remains one of Machiavelli's most enduring — and controversial — legacies.
