## CHAPTER XXVI. AN EXHORTATION TO LIBERATE ITALY FROM THE BARBARIANS

[026-001]Having carefully considered the subject of the above discourses, and

[026-002]wondering within myself whether the present times were propitious to a new

[026-003]prince, and whether there were elements that would give an opportunity to

[026-004]a wise and virtuous one to introduce a new order of things which would do

[026-005]honour to him and good to the people of this country, it appears to me

[026-006]that so many things concur to favour a new prince that I never knew a time

[026-007]more fit than the present.

[026-008]And if, as I said, it was necessary that the people of Israel should be

[026-009]captive so as to make manifest the ability of Moses; that the Persians

[026-010]should be oppressed by the Medes so as to discover the greatness of the

[026-011]soul of Cyrus; and that the Athenians should be dispersed to illustrate

[026-012]the capabilities of Theseus: then at the present time, in order to

[026-013]discover the virtue of an Italian spirit, it was necessary that Italy

[026-014]should be reduced to the extremity that she is now in, that she should be

[026-015]more enslaved than the Hebrews, more oppressed than the Persians, more

[026-016]scattered than the Athenians; without head, without order, beaten,

[026-017]despoiled, torn, overrun; and to have endured every kind of desolation.

[026-018]Although lately some spark may have been shown by one, which made us think

[026-019]he was ordained by God for our redemption, nevertheless it was afterwards

[026-020]seen, in the height of his career, that fortune rejected him; so that

[026-021]Italy, left as without life, waits for him who shall yet heal her wounds

[026-022]and put an end to the ravaging and plundering of Lombardy, to the

[026-023]swindling and taxing of the kingdom and of Tuscany, and cleanse those

[026-024]sores that for long have festered. It is seen how she entreats God to send

[026-025]someone who shall deliver her from these wrongs and barbarous insolencies.

[026-026]It is seen also that she is ready and willing to follow a banner if only

[026-027]someone will raise it.

[026-028]Nor is there to be seen at present one in whom she can place more hope than in

[026-029]your illustrious house,[1]

[026-030]with its valour and fortune, favoured by God and by the Church of which it is

[026-031]now the chief, and which could be made the head of this redemption. This will

[026-032]not be difficult if you will recall to yourself the actions and lives of the

[026-033]men I have named. And although they were great and wonderful men, yet they were

[026-034]men, and each one of them had no more opportunity than the present offers, for

[026-035]their enterprises were neither more just nor easier than this, nor was God more

[026-036]their friend than He is yours.

[026-037][1]

[026-038]Giuliano de Medici. He had just been created a cardinal by Leo X. In 1523

[026-039]Giuliano was elected Pope, and took the title of Clement VII.

[026-040]With us there is great justice, because that war is just which is

[026-041]necessary, and arms are hallowed when there is no other hope but in them.

[026-042]Here there is the greatest willingness, and where the willingness is great

[026-043]the difficulties cannot be great if you will only follow those men to whom

[026-044]I have directed your attention. Further than this, how extraordinarily the

[026-045]ways of God have been manifested beyond example: the sea is divided, a

[026-046]cloud has led the way, the rock has poured forth water, it has rained

[026-047]manna, everything has contributed to your greatness; you ought to do the

[026-048]rest. God is not willing to do everything, and thus take away our free

[026-049]will and that share of glory which belongs to us.

[026-050]And it is not to be wondered at if none of the above-named Italians have

[026-051]been able to accomplish all that is expected from your illustrious house;

[026-052]and if in so many revolutions in Italy, and in so many campaigns, it has

[026-053]always appeared as if military virtue were exhausted, this has happened

[026-054]because the old order of things was not good, and none of us have known

[026-055]how to find a new one. And nothing honours a man more than to establish

[026-056]new laws and new ordinances when he himself was newly risen. Such things

[026-057]when they are well founded and dignified will make him revered and

[026-058]admired, and in Italy there are not wanting opportunities to bring such

[026-059]into use in every form.

[026-060]Here there is great valour in the limbs whilst it fails in the head. Look

[026-061]attentively at the duels and the hand-to-hand combats, how superior the

[026-062]Italians are in strength, dexterity, and subtlety. But when it comes to armies

[026-063]they do not bear comparison, and this springs entirely from the insufficiency

[026-064]of the leaders, since those who are capable are not obedient, and each one

[026-065]seems to himself to know, there having never been any one so distinguished

[026-066]above the rest, either by valour or fortune, that others would yield to him.

[026-067]Hence it is that for so long a time, and during so much fighting in the past

[026-068]twenty years, whenever there has been an army wholly Italian, it has always

[026-069]given a poor account of itself; the first witness to this is Il Taro,

[026-070]afterwards Allesandria, Capua, Genoa, Vaila, Bologna, Mestri.[2]

[026-071][2]

[026-072]The battles of Il Taro, 1495; Alessandria, 1499; Capua, 1501; Genoa, 1507;

[026-073]Vaila, 1509; Bologna, 1511; Mestri, 1513.

[026-074]If, therefore, your illustrious house wishes to follow these remarkable

[026-075]men who have redeemed their country, it is necessary before all things, as

[026-076]a true foundation for every enterprise, to be provided with your own

[026-077]forces, because there can be no more faithful, truer, or better soldiers.

[026-078]And although singly they are good, altogether they will be much better

[026-079]when they find themselves commanded by their prince, honoured by him, and

[026-080]maintained at his expense. Therefore it is necessary to be prepared with

[026-081]such arms, so that you can be defended against foreigners by Italian

[026-082]valour.

[026-083]And although Swiss and Spanish infantry may be considered very formidable,

[026-084]nevertheless there is a defect in both, by reason of which a third order

[026-085]would not only be able to oppose them, but might be relied upon to

[026-086]overthrow them. For the Spaniards cannot resist cavalry, and the Switzers

[026-087]are afraid of infantry whenever they encounter them in close combat. Owing

[026-088]to this, as has been and may again be seen, the Spaniards are unable to

[026-089]resist French cavalry, and the Switzers are overthrown by Spanish

[026-090]infantry. And although a complete proof of this latter cannot be shown,

[026-091]nevertheless there was some evidence of it at the battle of Ravenna, when

[026-092]the Spanish infantry were confronted by German battalions, who follow the

[026-093]same tactics as the Swiss; when the Spaniards, by agility of body and with

[026-094]the aid of their shields, got in under the pikes of the Germans and stood

[026-095]out of danger, able to attack, while the Germans stood helpless, and, if

[026-096]the cavalry had not dashed up, all would have been over with them. It is

[026-097]possible, therefore, knowing the defects of both these infantries, to

[026-098]invent a new one, which will resist cavalry and not be afraid of infantry;

[026-099]this need not create a new order of arms, but a variation upon the old.

[026-100]And these are the kind of improvements which confer reputation and power

[026-101]upon a new prince.

[026-102]This opportunity, therefore, ought not to be allowed to pass for letting

[026-103]Italy at last see her liberator appear. Nor can one express the love with

[026-104]which he would be received in all those provinces which have suffered so

[026-105]much from these foreign scourings, with what thirst for revenge, with what

[026-106]stubborn faith, with what devotion, with what tears. What door would be

[026-107]closed to him? Who would refuse obedience to him? What envy would hinder

[026-108]him? What Italian would refuse him homage? To all of us this barbarous

[026-109]dominion stinks. Let, therefore, your illustrious house take up this

[026-110]charge with that courage and hope with which all just enterprises are

[026-111]undertaken, so that under its standard our native country may be ennobled,

[026-112]and under its auspices may be verified that saying of Petrarch:

[026-113]Virtu contro al Furore Prendera l’arme, e fia il combatter corto: Che l’antico valore Negli italici cuor non e ancor morto.  Virtue against fury shall advance the fight, And it i’ th’ combat soon shall put to flight: For the old Roman valour is not dead, Nor in th’ Italians’ brests extinguished.  Edward Dacre, 1640.
