# Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius was a Roman emperor from 161 to 180 CE and one of the most celebrated Stoic philosophers. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses him as a pivotal case study — the virtuous ruler who alone among the "good" emperors died a natural death and left a stable succession, precisely because his authority rested on hereditary legitimacy, not new conquest.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Exception Among Good Emperors

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli surveys the Roman emperors from Marcus to Maximinus, identifying a pattern: most "good" emperors met violent ends. Marcus is the exception:

> "From these causes it arose that Marcus, Pertinax, and Alexander, being all men of modest life, lovers of justice, enemies to cruelty, humane and benignant, came to a sad end except Marcus; he alone lived and died most honourably, because he had succeeded to the empire by hereditary right" [019-142].

Machiavelli's analysis is characteristically unsentimental: Marcus survived not because of his virtue but because he inherited power rather than seizing it (019-144). A new prince who imitates Marcus's methods without his hereditary advantage will fail.

### The Limits of Virtue

The key lesson Machiavelli draws is that political survival depends on circumstances. Marcus's virtue — his justice, humanity, and philosophical temperament — was sustainable only because "he had inherited [the empire], and he had no need to take it from the soldiers" (019-226). For a new prince, Machiavelli argues, the methods of Severus — force and cunning — are more reliable than the methods of Marcus.

> "It is not necessary to follow those of Marcus, nor, again, is it necessary to follow those of Severus, but he ought to take from Severus those parts which are necessary to found his state, and from Marcus those which are proper and glorious to keep a state that is already established and firm" [019-291].

This synthesis — Severus for founding, Marcus for maintaining — is one of the most famous passages in *The Prince*, encapsulating Machiavelli's distinction between the ethics of creation and the ethics of preservation (019-289).

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli accords Marcus Aurelius a respect unusual in *The Prince* — he is the "philosopher" who begins the imperial succession analyzed in Chapter 19 (019-116). Yet Machiavelli's admiration is purely pragmatic: Marcus succeeded because his circumstances allowed virtue to work. For a prince who must seize power, the philosopher's example is as dangerous as it is admirable. This duality makes Marcus Aurelius the perfect counterpoint to Severus in Machiavelli's political calculus.
