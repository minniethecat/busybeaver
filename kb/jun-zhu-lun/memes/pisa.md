## Overview

Pisa was an independent maritime republic and city-state in Tuscany during the Renaissance period. Located on the Arno River near the Tyrrhenian Sea, it was a significant commercial and military power in medieval Italy. By Machiavelli's time, Pisa had been conquered by Florence after a prolonged struggle, making it a recurring example in The Prince of how acquired territories resist or submit to new rulers.

## Political Context

Pisa maintained its independence through the medieval period but came under increasing Florentine pressure in the 15th century. The Pisans attempted to leverage external powers—particularly France—to maintain their autonomy. Machiavelli himself was involved in negotiations concerning Pisa during his diplomatic career (P01-109). The city's resistance to Florentine rule made it a central case study in Machiavelli's analysis of how a prince should deal with newly acquired territories.

> "As soon as Florence had acquired sufficient strength, whoever pounced down upon Pisa... nobody can deny that it would have been a very great advantage to the Florentines to have taken Pisa." (012-087)

## Role in The Prince

Pisa serves multiple analytical purposes in The Prince. In Chapter 3, Machiavelli notes that "the Pisans" were among the many parties who would "immediately rally" to a French king invading Italy (003-175), demonstrating how minor states naturally align against dominant regional powers. Chapter 5 discusses whether conquered states should be destroyed or allowed to live under their own laws—Pisa, having been a free city, serves as an implicit example of the difficulties of holding formerly republican territories (005-024).

In Chapter 12, Machiavelli argues that Florence's reliance on mercenary forces prolonged the Pisan war unnecessarily: "nobody can deny that it would have been a very great advantage to the Florentines to have taken Pisa" earlier, had they used their own arms (012-087). This feeds into his broader argument against mercenary troops. Chapter 20 presents Pisa as a contrasting case to Pistoia: "it was necessary to hold Pistoia by factions and Pisa by fortresses" (020-035), illustrating that different cities require different methods of control.

## Related Events

- **The Pisan War (1494–1509)**: Florence's protracted campaign to reconquer Pisa after the city revolted during Charles VIII's invasion. Machiavelli was personally involved in diplomatic efforts related to this conflict (P01-109).
- **Cesare Borgia's Campaign**: When Borgia moved against Pisa, Lucca and Siena "yielded at once"—but Pisa's strategic position meant its fate was tied to broader Italian power dynamics (007-164).
