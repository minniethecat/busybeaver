## CHAPTER VIII. CONCERNING THOSE WHO HAVE OBTAINED A PRINCIPALITY BY WICKEDNESS

[008-001]Although a prince may rise from a private station in two ways, neither of

[008-002]which can be entirely attributed to fortune or genius, yet it is manifest

[008-003]to me that I must not be silent on them, although one could be more

[008-004]copiously treated when I discuss republics. These methods are when, either

[008-005]by some wicked or nefarious ways, one ascends to the principality, or when

[008-006]by the favour of his fellow-citizens a private person becomes the prince

[008-007]of his country. And speaking of the first method, it will be illustrated

[008-008]by two examples—one ancient, the other modern—and without

[008-009]entering further into the subject, I consider these two examples will

[008-010]suffice those who may be compelled to follow them.

[008-011]Agathocles, the Sicilian,[1]

[008-012]became King of Syracuse not only from a private but from a low and abject

[008-013]position. This man, the son of a potter, through all the changes in his

[008-014]fortunes always led an infamous life. Nevertheless, he accompanied his infamies

[008-015]with so much ability of mind and body that, having devoted himself to the

[008-016]military profession, he rose through its ranks to be Praetor of Syracuse. Being

[008-017]established in that position, and having deliberately resolved to make himself

[008-018]prince and to seize by violence, without obligation to others, that which had

[008-019]been conceded to him by assent, he came to an understanding for this purpose

[008-020]with Amilcar, the Carthaginian, who, with his army, was fighting in Sicily. One

[008-021]morning he assembled the people and the senate of Syracuse, as if he had to

[008-022]discuss with them things relating to the Republic, and at a given signal the

[008-023]soldiers killed all the senators and the richest of the people; these dead, he

[008-024]seized and held the princedom of that city without any civil commotion. And

[008-025]although he was twice routed by the Carthaginians, and ultimately besieged, yet

[008-026]not only was he able to defend his city, but leaving part of his men for its

[008-027]defence, with the others he attacked Africa, and in a short time raised the

[008-028]siege of Syracuse. The Carthaginians, reduced to extreme necessity, were

[008-029]compelled to come to terms with Agathocles, and, leaving Sicily to him, had to

[008-030]be content with the possession of Africa.

[008-031][1]

[008-032]Agathocles the Sicilian, born 361 B.C., died 289 B.C.

[008-033]Therefore, he who considers the actions and the genius of this man will

[008-034]see nothing, or little, which can be attributed to fortune, inasmuch as he

[008-035]attained pre-eminence, as is shown above, not by the favour of any one,

[008-036]but step by step in the military profession, which steps were gained with

[008-037]a thousand troubles and perils, and were afterwards boldly held by him

[008-038]with many hazardous dangers. Yet it cannot be called talent to slay

[008-039]fellow-citizens, to deceive friends, to be without faith, without mercy,

[008-040]without religion; such methods may gain empire, but not glory. Still, if

[008-041]the courage of Agathocles in entering into and extricating himself from

[008-042]dangers be considered, together with his greatness of mind in enduring and

[008-043]overcoming hardships, it cannot be seen why he should be esteemed less

[008-044]than the most notable captain. Nevertheless, his barbarous cruelty and

[008-045]inhumanity with infinite wickedness do not permit him to be celebrated

[008-046]among the most excellent men. What he achieved cannot be attributed either

[008-047]to fortune or genius.

[008-048]In our times, during the rule of Alexander the Sixth, Oliverotto da Fermo,

[008-049]having been left an orphan many years before, was brought up by his

[008-050]maternal uncle, Giovanni Fogliani, and in the early days of his youth sent

[008-051]to fight under Pagolo Vitelli, that, being trained under his discipline,

[008-052]he might attain some high position in the military profession. After

[008-053]Pagolo died, he fought under his brother Vitellozzo, and in a very short

[008-054]time, being endowed with wit and a vigorous body and mind, he became the

[008-055]first man in his profession. But it appearing a paltry thing to serve

[008-056]under others, he resolved, with the aid of some citizens of Fermo, to whom

[008-057]the slavery of their country was dearer than its liberty, and with the

[008-058]help of the Vitelleschi, to seize Fermo. So he wrote to Giovanni Fogliani

[008-059]that, having been away from home for many years, he wished to visit him

[008-060]and his city, and in some measure to look upon his patrimony; and although

[008-061]he had not laboured to acquire anything except honour, yet, in order that

[008-062]the citizens should see he had not spent his time in vain, he desired to

[008-063]come honourably, so would be accompanied by one hundred horsemen, his

[008-064]friends and retainers; and he entreated Giovanni to arrange that he should

[008-065]be received honourably by the Fermians, all of which would be not only to

[008-066]his honour, but also to that of Giovanni himself, who had brought him up.

[008-067]Giovanni, therefore, did not fail in any attentions due to his nephew, and

[008-068]he caused him to be honourably received by the Fermians, and he lodged him

[008-069]in his own house, where, having passed some days, and having arranged what

[008-070]was necessary for his wicked designs, Oliverotto gave a solemn banquet to

[008-071]which he invited Giovanni Fogliani and the chiefs of Fermo. When the

[008-072]viands and all the other entertainments that are usual in such banquets

[008-073]were finished, Oliverotto artfully began certain grave discourses,

[008-074]speaking of the greatness of Pope Alexander and his son Cesare, and of

[008-075]their enterprises, to which discourse Giovanni and others answered; but he

[008-076]rose at once, saying that such matters ought to be discussed in a more

[008-077]private place, and he betook himself to a chamber, whither Giovanni and

[008-078]the rest of the citizens went in after him. No sooner were they seated

[008-079]than soldiers issued from secret places and slaughtered Giovanni and the

[008-080]rest. After these murders Oliverotto, mounted on horseback, rode up and

[008-081]down the town and besieged the chief magistrate in the palace, so that in

[008-082]fear the people were forced to obey him, and to form a government, of

[008-083]which he made himself the prince. He killed all the malcontents who were

[008-084]able to injure him, and strengthened himself with new civil and military

[008-085]ordinances, in such a way that, in the year during which he held the

[008-086]principality, not only was he secure in the city of Fermo, but he had

[008-087]become formidable to all his neighbours. And his destruction would have

[008-088]been as difficult as that of Agathocles if he had not allowed himself to

[008-089]be overreached by Cesare Borgia, who took him with the Orsini and Vitelli

[008-090]at Sinigalia, as was stated above. Thus one year after he had committed

[008-091]this parricide, he was strangled, together with Vitellozzo, whom he had

[008-092]made his leader in valour and wickedness.

[008-093]Some may wonder how it can happen that Agathocles, and his like, after infinite

[008-094]treacheries and cruelties, should live for long secure in his country, and

[008-095]defend himself from external enemies, and never be conspired against by his own

[008-096]citizens; seeing that many others, by means of cruelty, have never been able

[008-097]even in peaceful times to hold the state, still less in the doubtful times of

[008-098]war. I believe that this follows from severities[2]

[008-099]being badly or properly used. Those may be called properly used, if of evil it

[008-100]is possible to speak well, that are applied at one blow and are necessary to

[008-101]one’s security, and that are not persisted in afterwards unless they can

[008-102]be turned to the advantage of the subjects. The badly employed are those which,

[008-103]notwithstanding they may be few in the commencement, multiply with time rather

[008-104]than decrease. Those who practise the first system are able, by aid of God or

[008-105]man, to mitigate in some degree their rule, as Agathocles did. It is impossible

[008-106]for those who follow the other to maintain themselves.

[008-107][2]

[008-108]Mr Burd suggests that this word probably comes near the modern equivalent of

[008-109]Machiavelli’s thought when he speaks of “crudelta” than the

[008-110]more obvious “cruelties.”

[008-111]Hence it is to be remarked that, in seizing a state, the usurper ought to

[008-112]examine closely into all those injuries which it is necessary for him to

[008-113]inflict, and to do them all at one stroke so as not to have to repeat them

[008-114]daily; and thus by not unsettling men he will be able to reassure them,

[008-115]and win them to himself by benefits. He who does otherwise, either from

[008-116]timidity or evil advice, is always compelled to keep the knife in his

[008-117]hand; neither can he rely on his subjects, nor can they attach themselves

[008-118]to him, owing to their continued and repeated wrongs. For injuries ought

[008-119]to be done all at one time, so that, being tasted less, they offend less;

[008-120]benefits ought to be given little by little, so that the flavour of them

[008-121]may last longer.

[008-122]And above all things, a prince ought to live amongst his people in such a

[008-123]way that no unexpected circumstances, whether of good or evil, shall make

[008-124]him change; because if the necessity for this comes in troubled times, you

[008-125]are too late for harsh measures; and mild ones will not help you, for they

[008-126]will be considered as forced from you, and no one will be under any

[008-127]obligation to you for them.
