# Ferdinand of Aragon

Ferdinand II of Aragon (1452–1516), known as Ferdinand the Catholic, was King of Aragon and, through his marriage to Isabella I, joint ruler of a unified Spain. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents Ferdinand as the contemporary exemplar of a ruler who uses religion as political theater — achieving extraordinary results while privately possessing none of the virtues he publicly displays.

## 生平概要

Ferdinand, in Machiavelli's telling, "can almost be called a new prince, because he has risen, by fame and glory, from being an insignificant king to be the foremost king in Christendom" (021-002). His reign was marked by a series of bold enterprises: the conquest of Granada, the expulsion of the Moors and Jews, the acquisition of Naples (divided with [[Louis XII of France]]), and campaigns in Africa and Italy.

Machiavelli notes the strategic cunning behind these campaigns. Ferdinand began with Granada "quietly at first and without any fear of hindrance, for he held the minds of the barons of Castile occupied in thinking of the war" (021-002). He used "the money of the Church and of the people to sustain his armies," building a military force that would earn him lasting prestige.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Ferdinand appears most prominently in [[Chapter 21]], where Machiavelli uses him as the prime example of how a prince should conduct himself to gain renown. His method is described in detail: always undertaking "great enterprises and setting a fine example" (021-002), keeping his subjects in a state of "suspense and admiration" (021-002), and moving from one campaign to the next without giving anyone time to oppose him.

### 作为范例的角色

Ferdinand is the definitive **example of religious hypocrisy as statecraft**. Machiavelli's verdict, given in the Introduction, is unsparing:

> "Machiavelli has painted Ferdinand of Aragon as the man who accomplished great things under the cloak of religion, but who in reality had no mercy, faith, humanity, or integrity; and who, had he allowed himself to be influenced by such motives, would have been ruined." (P01-150)

This assessment is so pointed that when writing *The Prince*, Machiavelli could not mention Ferdinand by name in the relevant passage — it "would have been clearly impossible to mention Ferdinand's name here without giving offence" (018-103).

## 历史评价

Ferdinand represents, for Machiavelli, the highest form of political success — and the deepest challenge to conventional morality. The translator observes that "the cloak of religion still conceals the vices which Machiavelli laid bare in the character of Ferdinand of Aragon" (P01-300), noting the enduring relevance of this analysis.

Ferdinand's significance in *The Prince* extends beyond his own example. His partition of Naples with [[Louis XII of France]] is one of the five errors Machiavelli attributes to Louis — though for Ferdinand, who was on the gaining side, the same action was a triumph. This asymmetry illustrates a core Machiavellian insight: the same action may be wise or foolish depending on the power and position of the actor.
