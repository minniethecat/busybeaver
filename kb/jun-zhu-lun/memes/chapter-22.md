## CHAPTER XXII. CONCERNING THE SECRETARIES OF PRINCES

[022-001]The choice of servants is of no little importance to a prince, and they

[022-002]are good or not according to the discrimination of the prince. And the

[022-003]first opinion which one forms of a prince, and of his understanding, is by

[022-004]observing the men he has around him; and when they are capable and

[022-005]faithful he may always be considered wise, because he has known how to

[022-006]recognize the capable and to keep them faithful. But when they are

[022-007]otherwise one cannot form a good opinion of him, for the prime error which

[022-008]he made was in choosing them.

[022-009]There were none who knew Messer Antonio da Venafro as the servant of

[022-010]Pandolfo Petrucci, Prince of Siena, who would not consider Pandolfo to be

[022-011]a very clever man in having Venafro for his servant. Because there are

[022-012]three classes of intellects: one which comprehends by itself; another

[022-013]which appreciates what others comprehended; and a third which neither

[022-014]comprehends by itself nor by the showing of others; the first is the most

[022-015]excellent, the second is good, the third is useless. Therefore, it follows

[022-016]necessarily that, if Pandolfo was not in the first rank, he was in the

[022-017]second, for whenever one has judgment to know good and bad when it is said

[022-018]and done, although he himself may not have the initiative, yet he can

[022-019]recognize the good and the bad in his servant, and the one he can praise

[022-020]and the other correct; thus the servant cannot hope to deceive him, and is

[022-021]kept honest.

[022-022]But to enable a prince to form an opinion of his servant there is one test

[022-023]which never fails; when you see the servant thinking more of his own

[022-024]interests than of yours, and seeking inwardly his own profit in

[022-025]everything, such a man will never make a good servant, nor will you ever

[022-026]be able to trust him; because he who has the state of another in his hands

[022-027]ought never to think of himself, but always of his prince, and never pay

[022-028]any attention to matters in which the prince is not concerned.

[022-029]On the other hand, to keep his servant honest the prince ought to study

[022-030]him, honouring him, enriching him, doing him kindnesses, sharing with him

[022-031]the honours and cares; and at the same time let him see that he cannot

[022-032]stand alone, so that many honours may not make him desire more, many

[022-033]riches make him wish for more, and that many cares may make him dread

[022-034]chances. When, therefore, servants, and princes towards servants, are thus

[022-035]disposed, they can trust each other, but when it is otherwise, the end

[022-036]will always be disastrous for either one or the other.
