## CHAPTER XXIV. WHY THE PRINCES OF ITALY HAVE LOST THEIR STATES

[024-001]The previous suggestions, carefully observed, will enable a new prince to

[024-002]appear well established, and render him at once more secure and fixed in

[024-003]the state than if he had been long seated there. For the actions of a new

[024-004]prince are more narrowly observed than those of an hereditary one, and

[024-005]when they are seen to be able they gain more men and bind far tighter than

[024-006]ancient blood; because men are attracted more by the present than by the

[024-007]past, and when they find the present good they enjoy it and seek no

[024-008]further; they will also make the utmost defence of a prince if he fails

[024-009]them not in other things. Thus it will be a double glory for him to have

[024-010]established a new principality, and adorned and strengthened it with good

[024-011]laws, good arms, good allies, and with a good example; so will it be a

[024-012]double disgrace to him who, born a prince, shall lose his state by want of

[024-013]wisdom.

[024-014]And if those seigniors are considered who have lost their states in Italy

[024-015]in our times, such as the King of Naples, the Duke of Milan, and others,

[024-016]there will be found in them, firstly, one common defect in regard to arms

[024-017]from the causes which have been discussed at length; in the next place,

[024-018]some one of them will be seen, either to have had the people hostile, or

[024-019]if he has had the people friendly, he has not known how to secure the

[024-020]nobles. In the absence of these defects states that have power enough to

[024-021]keep an army in the field cannot be lost.

[024-022]Philip of Macedon, not the father of Alexander the Great, but he who was

[024-023]conquered by Titus Quintius, had not much territory compared to the

[024-024]greatness of the Romans and of Greece who attacked him, yet being a

[024-025]warlike man who knew how to attract the people and secure the nobles, he

[024-026]sustained the war against his enemies for many years, and if in the end he

[024-027]lost the dominion of some cities, nevertheless he retained the kingdom.

[024-028]Therefore, do not let our princes accuse fortune for the loss of their

[024-029]principalities after so many years’ possession, but rather their own

[024-030]sloth, because in quiet times they never thought there could be a change

[024-031](it is a common defect in man not to make any provision in the calm

[024-032]against the tempest), and when afterwards the bad times came they thought

[024-033]of flight and not of defending themselves, and they hoped that the people,

[024-034]disgusted with the insolence of the conquerors, would recall them. This

[024-035]course, when others fail, may be good, but it is very bad to have

[024-036]neglected all other expedients for that, since you would never wish to

[024-037]fall because you trusted to be able to find someone later on to restore

[024-038]you. This again either does not happen, or, if it does, it will not be for

[024-039]your security, because that deliverance is of no avail which does not

[024-040]depend upon yourself; those only are reliable, certain, and durable that

[024-041]depend on yourself and your valour.
