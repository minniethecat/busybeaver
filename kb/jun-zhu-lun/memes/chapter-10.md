## CHAPTER X. CONCERNING THE WAY IN WHICH THE STRENGTH OF ALL PRINCIPALITIES OUGHT TO BE
MEASURED

[010-001]It is necessary to consider another point in examining the character of

[010-002]these principalities: that is, whether a prince has such power that, in

[010-003]case of need, he can support himself with his own resources, or whether he

[010-004]has always need of the assistance of others. And to make this quite clear

[010-005]I say that I consider those who are able to support themselves by their

[010-006]own resources who can, either by abundance of men or money, raise a

[010-007]sufficient army to join battle against any one who comes to attack them;

[010-008]and I consider those always to have need of others who cannot show

[010-009]themselves against the enemy in the field, but are forced to defend

[010-010]themselves by sheltering behind walls. The first case has been discussed,

[010-011]but we will speak of it again should it recur. In the second case one can

[010-012]say nothing except to encourage such princes to provision and fortify

[010-013]their towns, and not on any account to defend the country. And whoever

[010-014]shall fortify his town well, and shall have managed the other concerns of

[010-015]his subjects in the way stated above, and to be often repeated, will never

[010-016]be attacked without great caution, for men are always adverse to

[010-017]enterprises where difficulties can be seen, and it will be seen not to be

[010-018]an easy thing to attack one who has his town well fortified, and is not

[010-019]hated by his people.

[010-020]The cities of Germany are absolutely free, they own but little country

[010-021]around them, and they yield obedience to the emperor when it suits them,

[010-022]nor do they fear this or any other power they may have near them, because

[010-023]they are fortified in such a way that every one thinks the taking of them

[010-024]by assault would be tedious and difficult, seeing they have proper ditches

[010-025]and walls, they have sufficient artillery, and they always keep in public

[010-026]depots enough for one year’s eating, drinking, and firing. And beyond

[010-027]this, to keep the people quiet and without loss to the state, they always

[010-028]have the means of giving work to the community in those labours that are

[010-029]the life and strength of the city, and on the pursuit of which the people

[010-030]are supported; they also hold military exercises in repute, and moreover

[010-031]have many ordinances to uphold them.

[010-032]Therefore, a prince who has a strong city, and had not made himself

[010-033]odious, will not be attacked, or if any one should attack he will only be

[010-034]driven off with disgrace; again, because that the affairs of this world

[010-035]are so changeable, it is almost impossible to keep an army a whole year in

[010-036]the field without being interfered with. And whoever should reply: If the

[010-037]people have property outside the city, and see it burnt, they will not

[010-038]remain patient, and the long siege and self-interest will make them forget

[010-039]their prince; to this I answer that a powerful and courageous prince will

[010-040]overcome all such difficulties by giving at one time hope to his subjects

[010-041]that the evil will not be for long, at another time fear of the cruelty of

[010-042]the enemy, then preserving himself adroitly from those subjects who seem

[010-043]to him to be too bold.

[010-044]Further, the enemy would naturally on his arrival at once burn and ruin

[010-045]the country at the time when the spirits of the people are still hot and

[010-046]ready for the defence; and, therefore, so much the less ought the prince

[010-047]to hesitate; because after a time, when spirits have cooled, the damage is

[010-048]already done, the ills are incurred, and there is no longer any remedy;

[010-049]and therefore they are so much the more ready to unite with their prince,

[010-050]he appearing to be under obligations to them now that their houses have

[010-051]been burnt and their possessions ruined in his defence. For it is the

[010-052]nature of men to be bound by the benefits they confer as much as by those

[010-053]they receive. Therefore, if everything is well considered, it will not be

[010-054]difficult for a wise prince to keep the minds of his citizens steadfast

[010-055]from first to last, when he does not fail to support and defend them.
