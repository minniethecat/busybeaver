# Niccolò Machiavelli

Niccolò Machiavelli (3 May 1469 – 21 June 1527) was a Florentine diplomat, historian, and political philosopher. He served as secretary to the Second Chancery of the [[Republic of Florence]] and is the author of *The Prince*, from which this wiki is constructed. His name appears throughout the introductory and dedicatory materials as both narrator and actor in the events he describes.

## 生平概要

Machiavelli was "born at Florence on 3rd May 1469" (P01-037), the son of Bernardo di Niccolò Machiavelli, "a lawyer of some repute" (P01-043). He entered the public service of the Florentine Republic and undertook numerous diplomatic missions across Italy and France.

His public career was divided into two periods. The first was spent in the service of the Florentine Republic, during which he traveled extensively as an envoy. He was sent to France in 1500 "to obtain terms from Louis XII for continuing the war against Pisa" (P01-108). In 1503, he was dispatched to Rome to observe the papal conclave after the death of Pius III (P01-130).

When the [[Medici Family]] returned to power in 1512, "Machiavelli lost his office" (P01-053). This marked the beginning of the second period of his life — "the period of Machiavelli's literary activity" (P01-055), during which he composed *The Prince*, the *Discourses on Livy*, and the *History of Florence*.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli is simultaneously the **author** and a **character** in *The Prince*. The work was "finally dedicated to Lorenzo de' Medici" (P01-210), the grandson of [[Lorenzo de' Medici]], though "there is no evidence that Lorenzo ever" read it (P01-212).

### 作为范例的角色

Machiavelli appears in the text not as a political exemplar but as a **first-hand observer**. His diplomatic missions gave him direct access to the major figures of his age. He "had several meetings with Louis XII of France" (P01-148) and "never hesitates to cite the actions of" the rulers he encountered (P01-119).

His personal experiences shape the book's most famous passages. The episode at Nantes, where Cardinal Rouen told him "the Italians did not understand war" and he replied "the French did not understand statecraft," crystallizes the book's central argument: that political success obeys discoverable laws.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's reputation is complex. His contemporaries viewed him as "a type of that rare combination, the successful statesman and author" (P01-134), though his own diplomatic missions were often "quite barren of results" (P01-276). In the conduct of his personal affairs "he was timid and time-serving" (P01-276), yet his writings display audacious clarity.

The name "Machiavelli" has since become synonymous with cunning and amoral statecraft — a simplification that obscures the analytical rigor of his work. His true contribution was to separate political analysis from moral prescription, asking not how rulers *should* behave but how effective rulers *do* behave.

> "Machiavelli never hesitates to cite the actions of" rulers as they actually occurred, not as moralists wish them to be. (P01-119)
