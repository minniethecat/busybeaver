## CHAPTER XV. CONCERNING THINGS FOR WHICH MEN, AND ESPECIALLY PRINCES, ARE PRAISED OR BLAMED

[015-001]It remains now to see what ought to be the rules of conduct for a prince

[015-002]towards subject and friends. And as I know that many have written on this

[015-003]point, I expect I shall be considered presumptuous in mentioning it again,

[015-004]especially as in discussing it I shall depart from the methods of other

[015-005]people. But, it being my intention to write a thing which shall be useful

[015-006]to him who apprehends it, it appears to me more appropriate to follow up

[015-007]the real truth of the matter than the imagination of it; for many have

[015-008]pictured republics and principalities which in fact have never been known

[015-009]or seen, because how one lives is so far distant from how one ought to

[015-010]live, that he who neglects what is done for what ought to be done, sooner

[015-011]effects his ruin than his preservation; for a man who wishes to act

[015-012]entirely up to his professions of virtue soon meets with what destroys him

[015-013]among so much that is evil.

[015-014]Hence it is necessary for a prince wishing to hold his own to know how to

[015-015]do wrong, and to make use of it or not according to necessity. Therefore,

[015-016]putting on one side imaginary things concerning a prince, and discussing

[015-017]those which are real, I say that all men when they are spoken of, and

[015-018]chiefly princes for being more highly placed, are remarkable for some of

[015-019]those qualities which bring them either blame or praise; and thus it is

[015-020]that one is reputed liberal, another miserly, using a Tuscan term (because

[015-021]an avaricious person in our language is still he who desires to possess by

[015-022]robbery, whilst we call one miserly who deprives himself too much of the

[015-023]use of his own); one is reputed generous, one rapacious; one cruel, one

[015-024]compassionate; one faithless, another faithful; one effeminate and

[015-025]cowardly, another bold and brave; one affable, another haughty; one

[015-026]lascivious, another chaste; one sincere, another cunning; one hard,

[015-027]another easy; one grave, another frivolous; one religious, another

[015-028]unbelieving, and the like. And I know that every one will confess that it

[015-029]would be most praiseworthy in a prince to exhibit all the above qualities

[015-030]that are considered good; but because they can neither be entirely

[015-031]possessed nor observed, for human conditions do not permit it, it is

[015-032]necessary for him to be sufficiently prudent that he may know how to avoid

[015-033]the reproach of those vices which would lose him his state; and also to

[015-034]keep himself, if it be possible, from those which would not lose him it;

[015-035]but this not being possible, he may with less hesitation abandon himself

[015-036]to them. And again, he need not make himself uneasy at incurring a

[015-037]reproach for those vices without which the state can only be saved with

[015-038]difficulty, for if everything is considered carefully, it will be found

[015-039]that something which looks like virtue, if followed, would be his ruin;

[015-040]whilst something else, which looks like vice, yet followed brings him

[015-041]security and prosperity.
