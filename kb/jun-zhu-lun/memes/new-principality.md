# New Principality

The new principality is the central analytical focus of *The Prince*. Machiavelli defines it as a state that is "either entirely new, as was Milan to Francesco Sforza," or annexed as a "member" to an existing hereditary state (001-005). In either case, "the difficulties occur in a new principality" (003-001).

## The Challenge of Innovation

Machiavelli identifies the fundamental difficulty: "men change their rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and this hope induces them to take up arms against him who rules: wherein they are deceived" (003-004). The new prince must inevitably offend those whose support he needed to seize power, yet cannot fully satisfy them.

## Modes of Acquisition

New principalities are acquired by four means, each analyzed in a dedicated chapter:
- By one's own arms and ability (virtù) — Ch. VI, exemplified by Francesco Sforza (007-031)
- By the arms of others or by fortune — Ch. VII, exemplified by Cesare Borgia
- By wickedness (crime) — Ch. VIII, exemplified by Agathocles and Oliverotto da Fermo
- By the favor of fellow citizens (civil principality) — Ch. IX

## Holding the New State

The new prince must establish firm foundations immediately upon acquisition. Cesare Borgia exemplifies this imperative: recognizing that he owed his state to his father's fortune, he "determined to make himself secure by making the soldiers his own" and eliminating rival captains (007-055). Those "who solely by good fortune become princes from being private citizens have little trouble in rising, but much in keeping atop" (007-001).
