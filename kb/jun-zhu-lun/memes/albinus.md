# Clodius Albinus

Clodius Albinus was a Roman general and usurper who governed Britain before challenging Septimius Severus for the imperial throne. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses Albinus as a case study in political deception: Severus lured him with the title of Caesar, then destroyed him once the eastern rival Niger was eliminated.

## The Usurpation

After the murder of Pertinax in 193 CE, Severus seized power in Rome. To neutralize Albinus's rival claim, Severus offered him the title of Caesar (junior emperor) and a share of power. Albinus accepted, believing the promise. But once Severus had defeated Pescennius Niger in the east, he turned on Albinus, declaring him a public enemy and defeating him at the Battle of Lugdunum in 197 CE.

## Role in *The Prince*

### A Victim of Fraud

Machiavelli's Chapter 19 analysis of Severus includes Albinus as the primary example of a rival undone by cunning. Severus "had conquered and killed Niger" first, then turned his full force against Albinus, who had been lulled into a false sense of security [019-195].

The episode illustrates a core Machiavellian principle: a prince must never trust the promises of a rival who has not yet secured his own position. Severus used Albinus as a temporary ally and discarded him when he was no longer useful.

### The Lion and the Fox

Albinus's fate serves as the negative counterpart to Severus's success. Where Severus embodied both the lion (military force) and the fox (cunning deception), Albinus allowed himself to be the victim of both. His failure to recognize Severus's true intentions and his failure to strike first when he had the chance made him an example of what happens to princes who cannot see through the fraud of others.

## Historical Assessment

Clodius Albinus was a capable governor and general, but in Machiavelli's framework he lacked the crucial political intelligence that distinguished Severus. His story reinforces the warning that in politics, trust is a weapon others will use against you.
