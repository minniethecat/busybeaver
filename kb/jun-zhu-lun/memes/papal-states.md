# Papal States

The Papal States were the temporal domain of the Pope in central Italy. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli devotes Chapter XI to "Ecclesiastical Principalities," arguing that these are the most secure form of state because they are held "by ancient ordinances of religion" rather than by arms or ability (011-001).

## Machiavelli's Analysis

Machiavelli's treatment is notably skeptical: ecclesiastical principalities "are acquired either by ability or by fortune, but they are maintained without either" (011-002). The prince (the Pope) need not govern well, defend his borders, or satisfy his subjects — the power of religion does all this for him. "These princedoms alone are secure and happy" (011-008).

## The Borgia Papacy

Pope Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia, reigned 1492–1503) transformed the Papal States from a collection of semi-independent feudal fiefs into a centralized territorial state. Machiavelli credits Alexander not with personal ability but with using his son Cesare Borgia and the wealth of the Church to strengthen papal temporal power (011-018). Pope Julius II continued the consolidation through military campaigns.
