# Philip II of Macedon

Philip II was the king of Macedon from 359 to 336 BCE and the father of Alexander the Great. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli cites him as a model of military organization — a ruler who understood that a prince must rely on his own soldiers, not mercenaries or auxiliaries.

## Historical Context

Philip transformed Macedon from a weak, peripheral kingdom into the dominant power of Greece. Through diplomatic cunning and military innovation — including the creation of the Macedonian phalanx — he subdued the Greek city-states and laid the foundation for his son's conquest of the Persian Empire. Machiavelli notes a key episode from his early career:

> "After the death of Epaminondas, Philip of Macedon was made captain of their soldiers by the Thebans, and after victory he took away their liberty" [012-066].

This example appears in Chapter 12, where Machiavelli warns against relying on auxiliary troops — foreign soldiers who will ultimately betray you.

## Role in *The Prince*

### Arming One's Own Forces

In Chapter 13, "Concerning Auxiliaries, Mixed Soldiery, and One's Own," Machiavelli cites Philip as positive proof that a wise prince arms his own subjects:

> "If one will consider how Philip, the father of Alexander the Great, and many republics and princes have armed and organized their own forces, it will be seen that they did so with great advantage to themselves" [013-119].

Philip's success stands in contrast to the Italian princes of Machiavelli's own day, who relied on mercenaries and consequently became dependent on foreign powers. For Machiavelli, Philip embodies the principle that a prince's foundation is his own army.

### Warnings About Auxiliaries

Machiavelli also notes the darker lesson of Philip's career with the Thebans (012-066): auxiliary troops serve their own interests, not yours. Philip, as captain of the Theban army, used his position to conquer his former allies. This demonstrates why a prince should never place his security in the hands of foreign soldiers.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli distinguishes Philip II from the later Philip V of Macedon — "not the father of Alexander the Great, but he who was conquered by Titus Quintius" [024-022] — emphasizing that the father was the successful military organizer who armed his own people. Philip II serves in *The Prince* as an ancient exemplar of the self-reliant ruler, a model Machiavelli urges his contemporaries to imitate.
