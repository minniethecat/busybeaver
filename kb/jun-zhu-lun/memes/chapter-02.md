## CHAPTER II. CONCERNING HEREDITARY PRINCIPALITIES

[002-001]I will leave out all discussion on republics, inasmuch as in another place

[002-002]I have written of them at length, and will address myself only to

[002-003]principalities. In doing so I will keep to the order indicated above, and

[002-004]discuss how such principalities are to be ruled and preserved.

[002-005]I say at once there are fewer difficulties in holding hereditary states,

[002-006]and those long accustomed to the family of their prince, than new ones;

[002-007]for it is sufficient only not to transgress the customs of his ancestors,

[002-008]and to deal prudently with circumstances as they arise, for a prince of

[002-009]average powers to maintain himself in his state, unless he be deprived of

[002-010]it by some extraordinary and excessive force; and if he should be so

[002-011]deprived of it, whenever anything sinister happens to the usurper, he will

[002-012]regain it.

[002-013]We have in Italy, for example, the Duke of Ferrara, who could not have

[002-014]withstood the attacks of the Venetians in ’84, nor those of Pope Julius in

[002-015]’10, unless he had been long established in his dominions. For the

[002-016]hereditary prince has less cause and less necessity to offend; hence it

[002-017]happens that he will be more loved; and unless extraordinary vices cause

[002-018]him to be hated, it is reasonable to expect that his subjects will be

[002-019]naturally well disposed towards him; and in the antiquity and duration of

[002-020]his rule the memories and motives that make for change are lost, for one

[002-021]change always leaves the toothing for another.
