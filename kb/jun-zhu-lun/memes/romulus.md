# Romulus

Romulus is the legendary founder and first king of Rome. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli places Romulus among the four supreme exemplars of rulers who acquired their states through their own *virtù* — Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus — treating the mythological figure as a political archetype [006-024].

## The Founding of Rome

According to Roman tradition, Romulus and his twin brother Remus were abandoned as infants and suckled by a she-wolf. Romulus killed Remus in a dispute over the founding of the city and became Rome's first king, establishing its fundamental institutions: the Senate, the legions, and the patron-client system.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Founder as Political Model

In Chapter 6, "Concerning New Principalities Which Are Acquired by One's Own Arms and Ability," Machiavelli presents Romulus as the model of a self-made founder:

> "It was necessary that Romulus should not remain in Alba, and that he should be exposed at his birth, in order that he should become king of Rome and founder of that fatherland" [006-040].

Machiavelli's point is that Romulus's success depended not on fortune but on specific conditions that he recognized and exploited. His abandonment and exile, rather than being misfortunes, were the necessary preconditions for his achievement — they created the circumstances in which his *virtù* could manifest.

### Armed and Self-Reliant

Like the other founders, Romulus succeeded because he was armed:

> "If Moses, Cyrus, Theseus, and Romulus had been unarmed they could not have enforced their constitutions for long" [006-076].

The implicit parallel is with Machiavelli's own time: Italy, like Romulus's Alba, is in need of a founder who will impose new institutions by force.

### The Killing of Remus

Machiavelli does not explicitly mention Romulus's murder of his brother, but the act is consistent with his political philosophy. In the *Discourses*, Machiavelli would later argue that Romulus's killing of Remus was justified because it served the founding of a great state. A founder, in Machiavelli's view, cannot be bound by conventional morality.

## Historical Assessment

For Machiavelli, Romulus represents the archetype of the founder-legislator who creates order out of chaos through personal force. The Rome that Romulus founded would become the greatest empire in history, validating — in Machiavelli's consequentialist framework — whatever acts were necessary to establish it.
