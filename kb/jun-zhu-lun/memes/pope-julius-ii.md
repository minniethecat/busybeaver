# Pope Julius II

Pope Julius II (born Giuliano della Rovere, 1443–1513) was one of the most consequential Renaissance popes and a recurring figure in Machiavelli's political analysis. Known as the "Warrior Pope" for his military campaigns to restore and expand the Papal States, Julius II appears in *The Prince* as both a case study in ecclesiastical power and an exemplar of impetuous action succeeding where caution would have failed.

## 生平概要

Giuliano della Rovere was born into a noble but modest family in Albisola, near Savona. His uncle, Pope Sixtus IV, elevated him to the cardinalate, and he spent decades as a powerful figure in the Curia before being elected pope in 1503 (007-193, 007-210). His pontificate was defined by military campaigns to recover papal territories lost during the Borgia papacy, earning him the title of "Warrior Pope." He personally led armies into battle, commissioned Michelangelo to paint the Sistine Chapel ceiling, and laid the foundation for St. Peter's Basilica. He died in 1513, having transformed the papacy into a major temporal power.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli references Julius II in multiple chapters, treating him as a key example of ecclesiastical principality and of how impetuous action can succeed through fortune's favor.

### 作为范例的角色

- **Impetuosity rewarded**: In Chapter 25, Machiavelli presents Julius II as the archetype of the impetuous temperament — a ruler who acted boldly and swiftly, and whose success demonstrates that fortune favors the bold (025-066). Julius never gave fortune time to work against him.
- **Ecclesiastical principality**: Julius II appears in Chapter 11 as the pope who consolidated the temporal power of the Church after Alexander VI, using the foundations laid by his predecessor to expand papal territory (011-052, 011-056). His success illustrates how ecclesiastical principalities, sustained by religious institutions, are uniquely secure.
- **Papal election and miscalculation**: Machiavelli notes that Alexander VI miscalculated in supporting della Rovere's election to the papacy, believing he could control him. Julius II turned against the Borgia interests immediately upon assuming power (007-210), demonstrating the risks of creating powerful rivals.
- **The Holy League**: In 1511, Julius II formed the Holy League against France, driving the French out of Italy — a dramatic example of papal diplomatic and military power reshaping the peninsula's balance (P01-168).

## 历史评价

> Julius II was Giuliano della Rovere, elected pope in 1503 after the brief pontificate of Pius III. His pontificate was devoted to the recovery of the temporal power of the papacy, which had been largely appropriated by Cesare Borgia during the reign of Alexander VI. (007-193)

> Pope Julius the Second went to work impetuously in all his affairs, and found the times and circumstances so favourable to this mode of proceeding that he always obtained a happy result. (025-066)

Machiavelli uses Julius II to advance a nuanced argument: while impetuous action is generally inferior to cautious prudence, fortune is a woman who responds to boldness. Julius II succeeded not because impetuosity is always superior, but because the times demanded it and fortune favored his temperament. In different circumstances, Machiavelli suggests, Julius's rashness would have ruined him.
