# Conspiracy (Congiura)

Conspiracy (congiura) occupies a central place in Machiavelli's analysis of political danger. Chapter 19 of *The Prince* is devoted almost entirely to the subject, and the Appendices on Castruccio Castracani and Duke Valentino both feature conspiracies prominently. For Machiavelli, the conspiracy is the most immediate threat to princely rule — and its success or failure reveals whether the prince governs well.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli's treatment of conspiracy is remarkably systematic. He analyzes the conditions under which conspiracies form, the factors that determine their success, and the measures a prince can take to prevent them.

### 核心论点

- A prince who avoids hatred has little to fear from conspiracies, because conspirators act only when they believe the people share their animosity toward the ruler (019-028, 019-080)
- The most dangerous moment for a conspiracy is between its formation and its execution — fear of punishment, hope of reward, and uncertainty paralyze conspirators
- Conspiracies rarely succeed without popular support; a prince who retains the goodwill of the people is "established upon good foundations"
- The punishment of conspirators must be swift, public, and exemplary — it serves both justice and deterrence

### 历史例证

- **Conspiracy against Castruccio Castracani** — the Lanfranchi family of Lucca plotted against him; but the conspiracy was discovered because one conspirator sought additional adherents and encountered a hostile party (028-488, 028-500, 028-502, 028-504, 028-506, 028-521)
- **The Pazzi Conspiracy** — alluded to in Machiavelli's own biography; he himself was suspected of complicity in an abortive conspiracy against the Medici (P01-179)
- **Roman Imperial Conspiracies** — Chapter 19 surveys how emperors who alienated the people and the army fell to conspiracies, while those who maintained their support survived

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Conspiracy vs. Hatred**: Hatred is the soil in which conspiracies grow. A prince who is not hated has little reason to fear conspiracy.
- **Conspiracy vs. Fortresses**: Fortresses may protect a prince from external enemies but not from the hatred of his own subjects — a conspiracy from within renders fortifications useless.
- **Conspiracy vs. Fortune**: Fortune may expose a prince to conspiracies, but virtù in maintaining popular support neutralizes them.
- **Conspiracy vs. Cruelty**: Well-measured cruelty against conspirators deters future plots; excessive cruelty against innocents provokes the hatred that breeds conspiracy.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

Conspiracy is the negative test of princely rule. A prince who governs well — avoiding hatred, respecting property, maintaining order — renders conspiracy impossible because he denies conspirators their essential resource: a discontented populace.

## 后世影响

Machiavelli's analysis of conspiracy influenced early modern political theory and security studies. His insight that the success of conspiracies depends less on the skill of conspirators than on the condition of popular sentiment remains a core principle of political stability analysis.
