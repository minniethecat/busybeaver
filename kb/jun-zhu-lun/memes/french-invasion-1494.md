# French Invasion of 1494

The French invasion of Italy in 1494, led by King Charles VIII of France, was the pivotal event that shattered the Italian balance of power and inaugurated more than half a century of foreign intervention in the peninsula. Machiavelli treats this invasion as the watershed moment after which Italian politics became a theater of great-power rivalry.

## Background

Before 1494, the Italian states — Milan, Venice, Florence, the Papal States, and Naples — maintained a precarious equilibrium through diplomacy and shifting alliances. Ludovico Sforza, the de facto ruler of Milan, invited Charles VIII to press his dynastic claim to the Kingdom of Naples, hoping to weaken his rivals.

Charles VIII crossed the Alps with a large army in September 1494. As Machiavelli notes, "Before Charles, King of France, passed into Italy, this country was under the control of the Pope, the Venetians, the King of Naples, the Duke of Milan, and the Florentines" (011-023). The invasion overturned this order.

## The Invasion

Charles's army marched through Italy virtually unopposed. Florence, under Piero de' Medici, capitulated without resistance. The Medici were expelled and the Florentine Republic was restored. Charles reached Naples in February 1495 and claimed the kingdom, but his rapid success provoked a counter-league — the League of Venice — formed by the Pope, Venice, Milan, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire.

Charles retreated north, fought the League at Fornovo (July 1495), and escaped to France, losing most of his Italian gains. But the door had been opened: Louis XII would invade again in 1499, and the Italian Wars continued until 1559.

## In The Prince

Machiavelli returns repeatedly to the consequences of 1494. The invasion exposed the weakness of the Italian states — their reliance on mercenary arms, their internal divisions, and their failure to anticipate the danger. He writes that "Charles VIII invaded Italy in 1494" (011-043) and that his easy march proved mercenaries were worthless. The entire final chapter of *The Prince* is a plea for a leader to drive the "barbarians" from Italy.
