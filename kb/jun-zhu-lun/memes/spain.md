# Spain

**Spain** in Machiavelli's era was a newly unified kingdom under [[ferdinand-of-aragon|Ferdinand of Aragon]] that emerged as the dominant foreign power in Italy, rivaling and eventually surpassing [[france|France]] in influence over the peninsula.

## Overview

Spain's rise to power in Italy is one of the central geopolitical developments chronicled in *The Prince*. Under Ferdinand of Aragon, Spain acquired the Kingdom of [[naples|Naples]], established a presence in northern Italy, and became a decisive force in Italian affairs. Machiavelli invokes Spanish power frequently — "the greatness of the Church and of Spain in Italy" (003-250) — as a fact that any Italian prince must reckon with.

## Political Context

### The Spanish Crown

The marriage of Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile in 1469 united Spain's two largest kingdoms. Ferdinand, whom Machiavelli praises as a model prince in Chapter 21, expanded Spanish power through marriage alliances, military conquest, and — as Machiavelli emphasizes — the use of religion as a political instrument. Spain under Ferdinand controlled not only the Iberian Peninsula but also Sicily, Sardinia, and after 1503, the Kingdom of Naples.

### Spanish Power in Italy

Spain's Italian strategy operated on multiple levels. Militarily, Spanish infantry — the *tercios* — were the most feared soldiers in Europe. Diplomatically, Spain cultivated allies among the Italian states, particularly the Papal States and Florence. "The present King of Spain would not have undertaken or carried out" (016-031) his conquests without the reputation for cunning that Machiavelli advises princes to cultivate.

## Role in The Prince

### Ferdinand's Exemplary Kingdom

Machiavelli holds up Spain under Ferdinand as an example of a principality acquired by fortune and maintained by virtue. Ferdinand's conquest of Naples, his use of religion to justify wars ("he always did great things under the pretext of religion"), and his ability to keep his subjects "in suspense and admiration" make him a model prince.

### The Spanish Factor in Italian Politics

When [[louis-xii-of-france|Louis XII]] made his errors in Italy — dividing Naples with Spain, then fighting Spain for the whole kingdom — Spain emerged victorious. Machiavelli notes that Louis "brought Spain into Italy" (003-216) by his own actions, strengthening a rival at his own expense. For Machiavelli, this illustrates the cardinal rule: a prince should never introduce a powerful foreigner into his neighborhood.

### Spanish Arms

Machiavelli's military analysis in Chapter 4 references how "the Romans in Spain, France, and Greece" (004-064) had difficulty holding provinces where local powers remained strong — a lesson relevant to Spain's own Italian adventures.

## Related Events

- The Treaty of Granada (1500): France and Spain agreed to partition Naples
- The Spanish-French war over Naples (1502–1504): Spain defeated France and took all of Naples
- The Holy League (1511): Spain joined the papacy and Venice against France
