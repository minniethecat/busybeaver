# Girolamo Savonarola

Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498) was a Dominican friar who dominated Florentine politics in the 1490s, establishing a theocratic republic after the expulsion of the [[Medici Family]]. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli cites him as the definitive example of the unarmed prophet who is inevitably ruined — a lesson in the necessity of force behind reform.

## 生平概要

Savonarola arrived in Florence in 1490 and rapidly gained influence through fiery sermons denouncing corruption in the Church and the Medici regime. When [[Charles VIII of France]] invaded Italy in 1494, the Medici were driven out, and Savonarola became the de facto ruler of Florence, instituting a regime of moral austerity and democratic reform.

His power, however, rested solely on popular belief. When the political winds shifted and the Pope excommunicated him, the populace turned. In 1498, Savonarola was hanged and burned in the Piazza della Signoria.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli mentions Savonarola twice. In the Introduction, the translator notes that Florence was split between "the fervent and austere Savonarola, the other by the splendour-loving [[Lorenzo de' Medici|Lorenzo]]" (P01-063) — two poles of Florentine life. The translator adds that Savonarola "only furnished Machiavelli with a subject of a gibe in *The Prince*, where he is cited as an example of an unarmed prophet who came to a bad end" (P01-064).

The crucial passage is in [[Chapter 06]], where Machiavelli draws a direct parallel between Savonarola and the great founders of antiquity:

> "If Moses, Cyrus, Theseus, and Romulus had been unarmed they could not have enforced their constitutions for long — as happened in our time to Fra Girolamo Savonarola, who was ruined with his new order of things immediately the multitude believed in him no longer, and he had no means of keeping steadfast those who believed or of making the unbelievers to believe." (006-078)

### 作为范例的角色

Savonarola is the **negative archetype of the unarmed prophet**. His failure illustrates Machiavelli's central argument about the relationship between persuasion and force: moral or religious authority, without the backing of arms, collapses the moment belief wavers. The prophet who cannot compel belief when it fades is doomed.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's treatment of Savonarola is not unsympathetic. He places him in the same analytical category as Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus — the greatest founders in history. The failure is structural, not personal: Savonarola lacked the arms that even a Moses required. Machiavelli's "gibe" is not mockery but a clinical observation about the machinery of power.
