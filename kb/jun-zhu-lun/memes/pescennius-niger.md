# Pescennius Niger

Pescennius Niger was a Roman general and governor of Syria who claimed the imperial throne in 193 CE during the Year of the Five Emperors. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli briefly references him as the first rival whom Septimius Severus defeated in his methodical campaign to secure sole power.

## The Year of the Five Emperors

Following the assassination of Pertinax in 193 CE, three rival claimants emerged: Septimius Severus in Pannonia, Pescennius Niger in Syria, and Clodius Albinus in Britain. Niger was proclaimed emperor by his legions and controlled the wealthy eastern provinces, making him a formidable rival.

## Role in *The Prince*

### Severus's First Target

Machiavelli's discussion of Niger is embedded in his analysis of Severus's strategic genius. Severus understood that he could not fight both rivals simultaneously, so he neutralized Albinus with a false promise while marching east against Niger:

> "Severus had conquered and killed Niger" before turning his attention to Albinus [019-195].

Niger was defeated in a series of engagements culminating at the Battle of Issus (194 CE). He was captured and executed, and his head was sent to Rome as a warning.

### The Strategic Lesson

Niger's defeat illustrates several Machiavellian principles. First, Severus's strategy of defeating enemies one at a time — rather than fighting on two fronts — is presented as a model of prudence. Second, Niger's failure to form an effective alliance with Albinus (each acted independently against Severus) demonstrates the fatal consequence of disunity among rivals.

### The Severan Consolidation

For Machiavelli, Niger's defeat was the first step in Severus's systematic consolidation of power — a process that demonstrated the combination of force and fraud that makes Severus the exemplar of Chapter 19. Niger, despite controlling the east's substantial resources, was outmaneuvered strategically and defeated in detail.

## Historical Assessment

Pescennius Niger was a competent governor and a serious contender for the throne. For Machiavelli, however, his role is defined entirely by his defeat: he serves as the negative example that makes Severus's success intelligible. His story reinforces the warning that military resources alone, without strategic cunning, cannot secure political power.
