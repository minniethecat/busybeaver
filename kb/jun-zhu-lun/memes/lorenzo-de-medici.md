# Lorenzo de' Medici

Lorenzo de' Medici (1449–1492), known as **Il Magnifico** (the Magnificent), was the de facto ruler of [[Republic of Florence|Florence]] during the height of the Italian Renaissance. His governance coincided with the youth of Machiavelli and left an enduring impression on the future author of *The Prince*, which was ultimately dedicated to the grandson of Lorenzo.

## 生平概要

Under the guidance of Lorenzo, Florence flourished as "an Italian power" (P01-049). His rule was characterized by cultural patronage and political astuteness. The intellectual life of the city, however, was split between two opposing currents: "one directed by the fervent and austere Savonarola, the other by the splendour-loving Lorenzo" (P01-063).

Lorenzo died in 1492. Two years later, in 1494, the Medici were driven from Florence — the same year Machiavelli entered public service. The Republic that followed lasted until 1512, when the Medici returned to power.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Although Lorenzo himself died before Machiavelli wrote *The Prince*, his influence pervades the work in three ways:

First, "the magnificence of the Medicean rule during the life of Lorenzo appeared to have impressed Machiavelli strongly, for he frequently recurs to it in his writings" (P01-069). The Florence of Lorenzo was the political world that formed the sensibilities of Machiavelli.

Second, *The Prince* was "finally dedicated to Lorenzo de' Medici" (P01-210) — not Lorenzo il Magnifico himself, but his grandson, [[Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici]]. The dedication opens with the salutation: "To the Magnificent Lorenzo Di Piero De' Medici" (P02-001).

Third, "there is no evidence that Lorenzo ever received or even read it: he certainly never gave Machiavelli any employment" (P01-212). The dedication, in other words, failed to achieve its practical purpose — a poignant irony given the teachings of the book on political effectiveness.

### 作为范例的角色

Lorenzo il Magnifico serves as a **background presence** rather than an explicit exemplar in *The Prince*. He represents the Medicean golden age that Machiavelli hoped to see restored under a wise prince. The contrast between the Florence of Lorenzo and the chaotic Italy of the later years of Machiavelli frames the urgent plea of the book for unified native rule.

## 历史评价

Lorenzo was simultaneously a patron of arts and letters, a skillful diplomat who maintained the balance of power among Italian states, and a ruler who suppressed republican institutions in Florence. The admiration of Machiavelli for his magnificence reflects the preference of the author for effective governance over procedural liberty — a tension that runs throughout *The Prince*.
