## CHAPTER XVII. CONCERNING CRUELTY AND CLEMENCY, AND WHETHER IT IS BETTER TO BE LOVED THAN
FEARED

[017-001]Coming now to the other qualities mentioned above, I say that every prince

[017-002]ought to desire to be considered clement and not cruel. Nevertheless he ought

[017-003]to take care not to misuse this clemency. Cesare Borgia was considered cruel;

[017-004]notwithstanding, his cruelty reconciled the Romagna, unified it, and restored

[017-005]it to peace and loyalty. And if this be rightly considered, he will be seen to

[017-006]have been much more merciful than the Florentine people, who, to avoid a

[017-007]reputation for cruelty, permitted Pistoia to be destroyed.[1]

[017-008]Therefore a prince, so long as he keeps his subjects united and loyal, ought

[017-009]not to mind the reproach of cruelty; because with a few examples he will be

[017-010]more merciful than those who, through too much mercy, allow disorders to arise,

[017-011]from which follow murders or robberies; for these are wont to injure the whole

[017-012]people, whilst those executions which originate with a prince offend the

[017-013]individual only.

[017-014][1]

[017-015]During the rioting between the Cancellieri and Panciatichi factions in 1502 and

[017-016]1503.

[017-017]And of all princes, it is impossible for the new prince to avoid the

[017-018]imputation of cruelty, owing to new states being full of dangers. Hence

[017-019]Virgil, through the mouth of Dido, excuses the inhumanity of her reign

[017-020]owing to its being new, saying:

[017-021]“Res dura, et regni novitas me talia cogunt Moliri, et late fines custode tueri.”[2]

[017-022]Nevertheless he ought to be slow to believe and to act, nor should he

[017-023]himself show fear, but proceed in a temperate manner with prudence and

[017-024]humanity, so that too much confidence may not make him incautious and too

[017-025]much distrust render him intolerable.

[017-026][2]

[017-027]. . . against my will, my fate A throne unsettled, and an infant state, Bid me defend my realms with all my pow’rs, And guard with these severities my shores.  Christopher Pitt.

[017-028]Upon this a question arises: whether it be better to be loved than feared

[017-029]or feared than loved? It may be answered that one should wish to be both,

[017-030]but, because it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer

[017-031]to be feared than loved, when, of the two, either must be dispensed with.

[017-032]Because this is to be asserted in general of men, that they are

[017-033]ungrateful, fickle, false, cowardly, covetous, and as long as you succeed

[017-034]they are yours entirely; they will offer you their blood, property, life,

[017-035]and children, as is said above, when the need is far distant; but when it

[017-036]approaches they turn against you. And that prince who, relying entirely on

[017-037]their promises, has neglected other precautions, is ruined; because

[017-038]friendships that are obtained by payments, and not by greatness or

[017-039]nobility of mind, may indeed be earned, but they are not secured, and in

[017-040]time of need cannot be relied upon; and men have less scruple in offending

[017-041]one who is beloved than one who is feared, for love is preserved by the

[017-042]link of obligation which, owing to the baseness of men, is broken at every

[017-043]opportunity for their advantage; but fear preserves you by a dread of

[017-044]punishment which never fails.

[017-045]Nevertheless a prince ought to inspire fear in such a way that, if he does

[017-046]not win love, he avoids hatred; because he can endure very well being

[017-047]feared whilst he is not hated, which will always be as long as he abstains

[017-048]from the property of his citizens and subjects and from their women. But

[017-049]when it is necessary for him to proceed against the life of someone, he

[017-050]must do it on proper justification and for manifest cause, but above all

[017-051]things he must keep his hands off the property of others, because men more

[017-052]quickly forget the death of their father than the loss of their patrimony.

[017-053]Besides, pretexts for taking away the property are never wanting; for he

[017-054]who has once begun to live by robbery will always find pretexts for

[017-055]seizing what belongs to others; but reasons for taking life, on the

[017-056]contrary, are more difficult to find and sooner lapse. But when a prince

[017-057]is with his army, and has under control a multitude of soldiers, then it

[017-058]is quite necessary for him to disregard the reputation of cruelty, for

[017-059]without it he would never hold his army united or disposed to its duties.

[017-060]Among the wonderful deeds of Hannibal this one is enumerated: that having

[017-061]led an enormous army, composed of many various races of men, to fight in

[017-062]foreign lands, no dissensions arose either among them or against the

[017-063]prince, whether in his bad or in his good fortune. This arose from nothing

[017-064]else than his inhuman cruelty, which, with his boundless valour, made him

[017-065]revered and terrible in the sight of his soldiers, but without that

[017-066]cruelty, his other virtues were not sufficient to produce this effect. And

[017-067]short-sighted writers admire his deeds from one point of view and from

[017-068]another condemn the principal cause of them. That it is true his other

[017-069]virtues would not have been sufficient for him may be proved by the case

[017-070]of Scipio, that most excellent man, not only of his own times but within

[017-071]the memory of man, against whom, nevertheless, his army rebelled in Spain;

[017-072]this arose from nothing but his too great forbearance, which gave his

[017-073]soldiers more license than is consistent with military discipline. For

[017-074]this he was upbraided in the Senate by Fabius Maximus, and called the

[017-075]corrupter of the Roman soldiery. The Locrians were laid waste by a legate

[017-076]of Scipio, yet they were not avenged by him, nor was the insolence of the

[017-077]legate punished, owing entirely to his easy nature. Insomuch that someone

[017-078]in the Senate, wishing to excuse him, said there were many men who knew

[017-079]much better how not to err than to correct the errors of others. This

[017-080]disposition, if he had been continued in the command, would have destroyed

[017-081]in time the fame and glory of Scipio; but, he being under the control of

[017-082]the Senate, this injurious characteristic not only concealed itself, but

[017-083]contributed to his glory.

[017-084]Returning to the question of being feared or loved, I come to the

[017-085]conclusion that, men loving according to their own will and fearing

[017-086]according to that of the prince, a wise prince should establish himself on

[017-087]that which is in his own control and not in that of others; he must

[017-088]endeavour only to avoid hatred, as is noted.
