[P01-037]Nicolo Machiavelli, born at Florence on 3rd May 1469. From 1494 to 1512

[P01-038]held an official post at Florence which included diplomatic missions to

[P01-039]various European courts. Imprisoned in Florence, 1512; later exiled and

## INTRODUCTION

[P01-042]Nicolo Machiavelli was born at Florence on 3rd May 1469. He was the second

[P01-043]son of Bernardo di Nicolo Machiavelli, a lawyer of some repute, and of

[P01-044]Bartolommea di Stefano Nelli, his wife. Both parents were members of the

[P01-045]old Florentine nobility.

[P01-046]His life falls naturally into three periods, each of which singularly

[P01-047]enough constitutes a distinct and important era in the history of

[P01-048]Florence. His youth was concurrent with the greatness of Florence as an

[P01-049]Italian power under the guidance of Lorenzo de’ Medici, Il Magnifico. The

[P01-050]downfall of the Medici in Florence occurred in 1494, in which year

[P01-051]Machiavelli entered the public service. During his official career

[P01-052]Florence was free under the government of a Republic, which lasted until

[P01-053]1512, when the Medici returned to power, and Machiavelli lost his office.

[P01-054]The Medici again ruled Florence from 1512 until 1527, when they were once

[P01-055]more driven out. This was the period of Machiavelli’s literary activity

[P01-056]and increasing influence; but he died, within a few weeks of the expulsion

[P01-057]of the Medici, on 22nd June 1527, in his fifty-eighth year, without having

[P01-058]regained office.

## YOUTH — Æt. 1-25—1469-94

[P01-059]Although there is little recorded of the youth of Machiavelli, the

[P01-060]Florence of those days is so well known that the early environment of this

[P01-061]representative citizen may be easily imagined. Florence has been described

[P01-062]as a city with two opposite currents of life, one directed by the fervent

[P01-063]and austere Savonarola, the other by the splendour-loving Lorenzo.

[P01-064]Savonarola’s influence upon the young Machiavelli must have been slight,

[P01-065]for although at one time he wielded immense power over the fortunes of

[P01-066]Florence, he only furnished Machiavelli with a subject of a gibe in *The

[P01-067]Prince*, where he is cited as an example of an unarmed prophet who came to

[P01-068]a bad end. Whereas the magnificence of the Medicean rule during the life

[P01-069]of Lorenzo appeared to have impressed Machiavelli strongly, for he

[P01-070]frequently recurs to it in his writings, and it is to Lorenzo’s grandson

[P01-071]that he dedicates *The Prince*.

[P01-072]Machiavelli, in his “History of Florence,” gives us a picture of the young

[P01-073]men among whom his youth was passed. He writes: “They were freer than

[P01-074]their forefathers in dress and living, and spent more in other kinds of

[P01-075]excesses, consuming their time and money in idleness, gaming, and women;

[P01-076]their chief aim was to appear well dressed and to speak with wit and

[P01-077]acuteness, whilst he who could wound others the most cleverly was thought

[P01-078]the wisest.” In a letter to his son Guido, Machiavelli shows why youth

[P01-079]should avail itself of its opportunities for study, and leads us to infer

[P01-080]that his own youth had been so occupied. He writes: “I have received your

[P01-081]letter, which has given me the greatest pleasure, especially because you

[P01-082]tell me you are quite restored in health, than which I could have no

[P01-083]better news; for if God grant life to you, and to me, I hope to make a

[P01-084]good man of you if you are willing to do your share.” Then, writing of a

[P01-085]new patron, he continues: “This will turn out well for you, but it is

[P01-086]necessary for you to study; since, then, you have no longer the excuse of

[P01-087]illness, take pains to study letters and music, for you see what honour is

[P01-088]done to me for the little skill I have. Therefore, my son, if you wish to

[P01-089]please me, and to bring success and honour to yourself, do right and

[P01-090]study, because others will help you if you help yourself.”

## OFFICE — Æt. 25-43—1494-1512

[P01-091]The second period of Machiavelli’s life was spent in the service of the

[P01-092]free Republic of Florence, which flourished, as stated above, from the

[P01-093]expulsion of the Medici in 1494 until their return in 1512. After serving

[P01-094]four years in one of the public offices he was appointed Chancellor and

[P01-095]Secretary to the Second Chancery, the Ten of Liberty and Peace. Here we

[P01-096]are on firm ground when dealing with the events of Machiavelli’s life, for

[P01-097]during this time he took a leading part in the affairs of the Republic,

[P01-098]and we have its decrees, records, and dispatches to guide us, as well as

[P01-099]his own writings. A mere recapitulation of a few of his transactions with

[P01-100]the statesmen and soldiers of his time gives a fair indication of his

[P01-101]activities, and supplies the sources from which he drew the experiences

[P01-102]and characters which illustrate *The Prince*.

[P01-103]His first mission was in 1499 to Catherina Sforza, “my lady of Forli” of

[P01-104]*The Prince*, from whose conduct and fate he drew the moral that it is far

[P01-105]better to earn the confidence of the people than to rely on fortresses.

[P01-106]This is a very noticeable principle in Machiavelli, and is urged by him in

[P01-107]many ways as a matter of vital importance to princes.

[P01-108]In 1500 he was sent to France to obtain terms from Louis XII for

[P01-109]continuing the war against Pisa: this king it was who, in his conduct of

[P01-110]affairs in Italy, committed the five capital errors in statecraft

[P01-111]summarized in *The Prince*, and was consequently driven out. He, also, it

[P01-112]was who made the dissolution of his marriage a condition of support to

[P01-113]Pope Alexander VI; which leads Machiavelli to refer those who urge that

[P01-114]such promises should be kept to what he has written concerning the faith

[P01-115]of princes.

[P01-116]Machiavelli’s public life was largely occupied with events arising out of

[P01-117]the ambitions of Pope Alexander VI and his son, Cesare Borgia, the Duke

[P01-118]Valentino, and these characters fill a large space of *The Prince*.

[P01-119]Machiavelli never hesitates to cite the actions of the duke for the

[P01-120]benefit of usurpers who wish to keep the states they have seized; he can,

[P01-121]indeed, find no precepts to offer so good as the pattern of Cesare

[P01-122]Borgia’s conduct, insomuch that Cesare is acclaimed by some critics as the

[P01-123]“hero” of *The Prince*. Yet in *The Prince* the duke is in point of fact

[P01-124]cited as a type of the man who rises on the fortune of others, and falls

[P01-125]with them; who takes every course that might be expected from a prudent

[P01-126]man but the course which will save him; who is prepared for all

[P01-127]eventualities but the one which happens; and who, when all his abilities

[P01-128]fail to carry him through, exclaims that it was not his fault, but an

[P01-129]extraordinary and unforeseen fatality.

[P01-130]On the death of Pius III, in 1503, Machiavelli was sent to Rome to watch

[P01-131]the election of his successor, and there he saw Cesare Borgia cheated into

[P01-132]allowing the choice of the College to fall on Giuliano delle Rovere

[P01-133](Julius II), who was one of the cardinals that had most reason to fear the

[P01-134]duke. Machiavelli, when commenting on this election, says that he who

[P01-135]thinks new favours will cause great personages to forget old injuries

[P01-136]deceives himself. Julius did not rest until he had ruined Cesare.

[P01-137]It was to Julius II that Machiavelli was sent in 1506, when that pontiff

[P01-138]was commencing his enterprise against Bologna; which he brought to a

[P01-139]successful issue, as he did many of his other adventures, owing chiefly to

[P01-140]his impetuous character. It is in reference to Pope Julius that

[P01-141]Machiavelli moralizes on the resemblance between Fortune and women, and

[P01-142]concludes that it is the bold rather than the cautious man that will win

[P01-143]and hold them both.

[P01-144]It is impossible to follow here the varying fortunes of the Italian

[P01-145]states, which in 1507 were controlled by France, Spain, and Germany, with

[P01-146]results that have lasted to our day; we are concerned with those events,

[P01-147]and with the three great actors in them, so far only as they impinge on

[P01-148]the personality of Machiavelli. He had several meetings with Louis XII of

[P01-149]France, and his estimate of that monarch’s character has already been

[P01-150]alluded to. Machiavelli has painted Ferdinand of Aragon as the man who

[P01-151]accomplished great things under the cloak of religion, but who in reality

[P01-152]had no mercy, faith, humanity, or integrity; and who, had he allowed

[P01-153]himself to be influenced by such motives, would have been ruined. The

[P01-154]Emperor Maximilian was one of the most interesting men of the age, and his

[P01-155]character has been drawn by many hands; but Machiavelli, who was an envoy

[P01-156]at his court in 1507-8, reveals the secret of his many failures when he

[P01-157]describes him as a secretive man, without force of character—ignoring

[P01-158]the human agencies necessary to carry his schemes into effect, and never

[P01-159]insisting on the fulfilment of his wishes.

[P01-160]The remaining years of Machiavelli’s official career were filled with

[P01-161]events arising out of the League of Cambrai, made in 1508 between the

[P01-162]three great European powers already mentioned and the pope, with the

[P01-163]object of crushing the Venetian Republic. This result was attained in the

[P01-164]battle of Vaila, when Venice lost in one day all that she had won in eight

[P01-165]hundred years. Florence had a difficult part to play during these events,

[P01-166]complicated as they were by the feud which broke out between the pope and

[P01-167]the French, because friendship with France had dictated the entire policy

[P01-168]of the Republic. When, in 1511, Julius II finally formed the Holy League

[P01-169]against France, and with the assistance of the Swiss drove the French out

[P01-170]of Italy, Florence lay at the mercy of the Pope, and had to submit to his

[P01-171]terms, one of which was that the Medici should be restored. The return of

[P01-172]the Medici to Florence on 1st September 1512, and the consequent fall of

[P01-173]the Republic, was the signal for the dismissal of Machiavelli and his

[P01-174]friends, and thus put an end to his public career, for, as we have seen,

[P01-175]he died without regaining office.

## LITERATURE AND DEATH — Æt. 43-58—1512-27

[P01-176]On the return of the Medici, Machiavelli, who for a few weeks had vainly

[P01-177]hoped to retain his office under the new masters of Florence, was

[P01-178]dismissed by decree dated 7th November 1512. Shortly after this he was

[P01-179]accused of complicity in an abortive conspiracy against the Medici,

[P01-180]imprisoned, and put to the question by torture. The new Medicean pope, Leo

[P01-181]X, procured his release, and he retired to his small property at San

[P01-182]Casciano, near Florence, where he devoted himself to literature. In a

[P01-183]letter to Francesco Vettori, dated 13th December 1513, he has left a very

[P01-184]interesting description of his life at this period, which elucidates his

[P01-185]methods and his motives in writing *The Prince*. After describing his

[P01-186]daily occupations with his family and neighbours, he writes: “The evening

[P01-187]being come, I return home and go to my study; at the entrance I pull off

[P01-188]my peasant-clothes, covered with dust and dirt, and put on my noble court

[P01-189]dress, and thus becomingly re-clothed I pass into the ancient courts of

[P01-190]the men of old, where, being lovingly received by them, I am fed with that

[P01-191]food which is mine alone; where I do not hesitate to speak with them, and

[P01-192]to ask for the reason of their actions, and they in their benignity answer

[P01-193]me; and for four hours I feel no weariness, I forget every trouble,

[P01-194]poverty does not dismay, death does not terrify me; I am possessed

[P01-195]entirely by those great men. And because Dante says:

[P01-196]Knowledge doth come of learning well retained, Unfruitful else,

[P01-197]I have noted down what I have gained from their conversation, and have

[P01-198]composed a small work on ‘Principalities,’ where I pour myself out as

[P01-199]fully as I can in meditation on the subject, discussing what a

[P01-200]principality is, what kinds there are, how they can be acquired, how they

[P01-201]can be kept, why they are lost: and if any of my fancies ever pleased you,

[P01-202]this ought not to displease you: and to a prince, especially to a new one,

[P01-203]it should be welcome: therefore I dedicate it to his Magnificence

[P01-204]Giuliano. Filippo Casavecchio has seen it; he will be able to tell you

[P01-205]what is in it, and of the discourses I have had with him; nevertheless, I

[P01-206]am still enriching and polishing it.”

[P01-207]The “little book” suffered many vicissitudes before attaining the form in

[P01-208]which it has reached us. Various mental influences were at work during its

[P01-209]composition; its title and patron were changed; and for some unknown

[P01-210]reason it was finally dedicated to Lorenzo de’ Medici. Although

[P01-211]Machiavelli discussed with Casavecchio whether it should be sent or

[P01-212]presented in person to the patron, there is no evidence that Lorenzo ever

[P01-213]received or even read it: he certainly never gave Machiavelli any

[P01-214]employment. Although it was plagiarized during Machiavelli’s lifetime,

[P01-215]*The Prince* was never published by him, and its text is still disputable.

[P01-216]Machiavelli concludes his letter to Vettori thus: “And as to this little

[P01-217]thing [his book], when it has been read it will be seen that during the

[P01-218]fifteen years I have given to the study of statecraft I have neither slept

[P01-219]nor idled; and men ought ever to desire to be served by one who has reaped

[P01-220]experience at the expense of others. And of my loyalty none could doubt,

[P01-221]because having always kept faith I could not now learn how to break it;

[P01-222]for he who has been faithful and honest, as I have, cannot change his

[P01-223]nature; and my poverty is a witness to my honesty.”

[P01-224]Before Machiavelli had got *The Prince* off his hands he commenced his

[P01-225]“Discourse on the First Decade of Titus Livius,” which should be read

[P01-226]concurrently with *The Prince*. These and several minor works occupied him

[P01-227]until the year 1518, when he accepted a small commission to look after the

[P01-228]affairs of some Florentine merchants at Genoa. In 1519 the Medicean rulers

[P01-229]of Florence granted a few political concessions to her citizens, and

[P01-230]Machiavelli with others was consulted upon a new constitution under which

[P01-231]the Great Council was to be restored; but on one pretext or another it was

[P01-232]not promulgated.

[P01-233]In 1520 the Florentine merchants again had recourse to Machiavelli to

[P01-234]settle their difficulties with Lucca, but this year was chiefly remarkable

[P01-235]for his re-entry into Florentine literary society, where he was much

[P01-236]sought after, and also for the production of his “Art of War.” It was in

[P01-237]the same year that he received a commission at the instance of Cardinal

[P01-238]de’ Medici to write the “History of Florence,” a task which occupied him

[P01-239]until 1525. His return to popular favour may have determined the Medici to

[P01-240]give him this employment, for an old writer observes that “an able

[P01-241]statesman out of work, like a huge whale, will endeavour to overturn the

[P01-242]ship unless he has an empty cask to play with.”

[P01-243]When the “History of Florence” was finished, Machiavelli took it to Rome

[P01-244]for presentation to his patron, Giuliano de’ Medici, who had in the

[P01-245]meanwhile become pope under the title of Clement VII. It is somewhat

[P01-246]remarkable that, as, in 1513, Machiavelli had written *The Prince* for the

[P01-247]instruction of the Medici after they had just regained power in Florence,

[P01-248]so, in 1525, he dedicated the “History of Florence” to the head of the

[P01-249]family when its ruin was now at hand. In that year the battle of Pavia

[P01-250]destroyed the French rule in Italy, and left Francis I a prisoner in the

[P01-251]hands of his great rival, Charles V. This was followed by the sack of

[P01-252]Rome, upon the news of which the popular party at Florence threw off the

[P01-253]yoke of the Medici, who were once more banished.

[P01-254]Machiavelli was absent from Florence at this time, but hastened his

[P01-255]return, hoping to secure his former office of secretary to the “Ten of

[P01-256]Liberty and Peace.” Unhappily he was taken ill soon after he reached

[P01-257]Florence, where he died on 22nd June 1527.

## THE MAN AND HIS WORKS

[P01-258]No one can say where the bones of Machiavelli rest, but modern Florence

[P01-259]has decreed him a stately cenotaph in Santa Croce, by the side of her most

[P01-260]famous sons; recognizing that, whatever other nations may have found in

[P01-261]his works, Italy found in them the idea of her unity and the germs of her

[P01-262]renaissance among the nations of Europe. Whilst it is idle to protest

[P01-263]against the world-wide and evil signification of his name, it may be

[P01-264]pointed out that the harsh construction of his doctrine which this

[P01-265]sinister reputation implies was unknown to his own day, and that the

[P01-266]researches of recent times have enabled us to interpret him more

[P01-267]reasonably. It is due to these inquiries that the shape of an “unholy

[P01-268]necromancer,” which so long haunted men’s vision, has begun to fade.

[P01-269]Machiavelli was undoubtedly a man of great observation, acuteness, and

[P01-270]industry; noting with appreciative eye whatever passed before him, and

[P01-271]with his supreme literary gift turning it to account in his enforced

[P01-272]retirement from affairs. He does not present himself, nor is he depicted

[P01-273]by his contemporaries, as a type of that rare combination, the successful

[P01-274]statesman and author, for he appears to have been only moderately

[P01-275]prosperous in his several embassies and political employments. He was

[P01-276]misled by Catherina Sforza, ignored by Louis XII, overawed by Cesare

[P01-277]Borgia; several of his embassies were quite barren of results; his

[P01-278]attempts to fortify Florence failed, and the soldiery that he raised

[P01-279]astonished everybody by their cowardice. In the conduct of his own affairs

[P01-280]he was timid and time-serving; he dared not appear by the side of

[P01-281]Soderini, to whom he owed so much, for fear of compromising himself; his

[P01-282]connection with the Medici was open to suspicion, and Giuliano appears to

[P01-283]have recognized his real forte when he set him to write the “History of

[P01-284]Florence,” rather than employ him in the state. And it is on the literary

[P01-285]side of his character, and there alone, that we find no weakness and no

[P01-286]failure.

[P01-287]Although the light of almost four centuries has been focused on *The

[P01-288]Prince*, its problems are still debatable and interesting, because they

[P01-289]are the eternal problems between the ruled and their rulers. Such as they

[P01-290]are, its ethics are those of Machiavelli’s contemporaries; yet they cannot

[P01-291]be said to be out of date so long as the governments of Europe rely on

[P01-292]material rather than on moral forces. Its historical incidents and

[P01-293]personages become interesting by reason of the uses which Machiavelli

[P01-294]makes of them to illustrate his theories of government and conduct.

[P01-295]Leaving out of consideration those maxims of state which still furnish

[P01-296]some European and eastern statesmen with principles of action, *The

[P01-297]Prince* is bestrewn with truths that can be proved at every turn. Men are

[P01-298]still the dupes of their simplicity and greed, as they were in the days of

[P01-299]Alexander VI. The cloak of religion still conceals the vices which

[P01-300]Machiavelli laid bare in the character of Ferdinand of Aragon. Men will

[P01-301]not look at things as they really are, but as they wish them to be—and

[P01-302]are ruined. In politics there are no perfectly safe courses; prudence

[P01-303]consists in choosing the least dangerous ones. Then—to pass to a

[P01-304]higher plane—Machiavelli reiterates that, although crimes may win an

[P01-305]empire, they do not win glory. Necessary wars are just wars, and the arms

[P01-306]of a nation are hallowed when it has no other resource but to fight.

[P01-307]It is the cry of a far later day than Machiavelli’s that government should

[P01-308]be elevated into a living moral force, capable of inspiring the people

[P01-309]with a just recognition of the fundamental principles of society; to this

[P01-310]“high argument” *The Prince* contributes but little. Machiavelli always

[P01-311]refused to write either of men or of governments otherwise than as he

[P01-312]found them, and he writes with such skill and insight that his work is of

[P01-313]abiding value. But what invests *The Prince* with more than a merely

[P01-314]artistic or historical interest is the incontrovertible truth that it

[P01-315]deals with the great principles which still guide nations and rulers in

[P01-316]their relationship with each other and their neighbours.

[P01-317]In translating *The Prince* my aim has been to achieve at all costs an

[P01-318]exact literal rendering of the original, rather than a fluent paraphrase

[P01-319]adapted to the modern notions of style and expression. Machiavelli was no

[P01-320]facile phrasemonger; the conditions under which he wrote obliged him to weigh

[P01-321]every word; his themes were lofty, his substance grave, his manner nobly plain

[P01-322]and serious. *Quis eo fuit unquam in partiundis rebus, in definiendis, in

[P01-323]explanandis pressior?* In *The Prince*, it may be truly said, there is

[P01-324]reason assignable, not only for every word, but for the position of every word.

[P01-325]To an Englishman of Shakespeare’s time the translation of such a treatise

[P01-326]was in some ways a comparatively easy task, for in those times the genius of

[P01-327]the English more nearly resembled that of the Italian language; to the

[P01-328]Englishman of to-day it is not so simple. To take a single example: the word

[P01-329]*intrattenere*, employed by Machiavelli to indicate the policy adopted by

[P01-330]the Roman Senate towards the weaker states of Greece, would by an Elizabethan

[P01-331]be correctly rendered “entertain,” and every contemporary reader

[P01-332]would understand what was meant by saying that “Rome *entertained*

[P01-333]the Ætolians and the Achaeans without augmenting their power.” But to-day

[P01-334]such a phrase would seem obsolete and ambiguous, if not unmeaning: we are

[P01-335]compelled to say that “*Rome maintained friendly relations with the

[P01-336]Ætolians*,” etc., using four words to do the work of one. I have tried

[P01-337]to preserve the pithy brevity of the Italian so far as was consistent with an

[P01-338]absolute fidelity to the sense. If the result be an occasional asperity I can

[P01-339]only hope that the reader, in his eagerness to reach the author’s

[P01-340]meaning, may overlook the roughness of the road that leads him to it.
