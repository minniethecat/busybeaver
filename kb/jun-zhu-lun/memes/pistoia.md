## Overview

Pistoia was a city-state in Tuscany, located approximately 30 kilometers northwest of Florence. During the Renaissance, it lay within Florence's sphere of influence and was notorious for its deep factional divisions. Machiavelli uses Pistoia as a primary example of how internal factionalism can be both a liability and a tool for the ruling power.

## Political Context

Pistoia was perennially divided between opposing factions—a characteristic that defined its political life throughout the medieval and Renaissance periods. The city's internal conflicts made it dependent on external powers for stability, and Florence frequently intervened in Pistoian affairs. The city oscillated between submission to Florence and periods of autonomy, with its factional structure making it simultaneously difficult to govern and relatively easy to manipulate.

> "Pistoia was divided, as it always had been, into the Bianchi and Neri factions." (028-347)

## Role in The Prince

Pistoia appears in two significant passages that illustrate complementary political principles. In Chapter 17, Machiavelli presents a striking contrast: Cesare Borgia "was considered cruel; notwithstanding, his cruelty reconciled the Romagna, unified it, and restored it to peace and loyalty"—while the Florentines, "to avoid being called cruel, permitted Pistoia to be destroyed" (017-007). This juxtaposition serves Machiavelli's argument that measured cruelty can be more merciful than excessive leniency.

In Chapter 20, Machiavelli makes a more tactical observation: "it was necessary to hold Pistoia by factions and Pisa by fortresses" (020-035). This pair of cases demonstrates that different subject cities require different governing strategies—factional divisions that would be dangerous in one context can be exploited as a tool of control in another.

> "Our forefathers, and those who were reckoned wise, were accustomed to say that it was necessary to hold Pistoia by factions and Pisa by fortresses." (020-035)

## Related Events

- **Castruccio's Annexation**: Castruccio Castracani of Lucca exploited Pistoia's factional divisions to bring the city under his control. He cultivated relationships with leaders of both the Bianchi and Neri factions, ultimately orchestrating a nighttime coup that delivered Pistoia into his hands (028-344).
- **The Pistoian Exiles**: Baldo Cecchi and other exiled Pistoians in Florence conspired with their allies inside the city to regain power, a scheme that Castruccio intercepted and turned to his advantage (028-394).
