## DESCRIPTION OF THE METHODS ADOPTED BY THE DUKE
VALENTINO WHEN MURDERING VITELLOZZO VITELLI, OLIVEROTTO DA FERMO, THE SIGNOR

PAGOLO, AND THE DUKE DI GRAVINA ORSINI

BY NICOLO MACHIAVELLI

[027-001]The Duke Valentino had returned from Lombardy, where he had been to clear

[027-002]himself with the King of France from the calumnies which had been raised

[027-003]against him by the Florentines concerning the rebellion of Arezzo and

[027-004]other towns in the Val di Chiana, and had arrived at Imola, whence he

[027-005]intended with his army to enter upon the campaign against Giovanni

[027-006]Bentivogli, the tyrant of Bologna: for he intended to bring that city

[027-007]under his domination, and to make it the head of his Romagnian duchy.

[027-008]These matters coming to the knowledge of the Vitelli and Orsini and their

[027-009]following, it appeared to them that the duke would become too powerful,

[027-010]and it was feared that, having seized Bologna, he would seek to destroy

[027-011]them in order that he might become supreme in Italy. Upon this a meeting

[027-012]was called at Magione in the district of Perugia, to which came the

[027-013]cardinal, Pagolo, and the Duke di Gravina Orsini, Vitellozzo Vitelli,

[027-014]Oliverotto da Fermo, Gianpagolo Baglioni, the tyrant of Perugia, and

[027-015]Messer Antonio da Venafro, sent by Pandolfo Petrucci, the Prince of Siena.

[027-016]Here were discussed the power and courage of the duke and the necessity of

[027-017]curbing his ambitions, which might otherwise bring danger to the rest of

[027-018]being ruined. And they decided not to abandon the Bentivogli, but to

[027-019]strive to win over the Florentines; and they sent their men to one place

[027-020]and another, promising to one party assistance and to another

[027-021]encouragement to unite with them against the common enemy. This meeting

[027-022]was at once reported throughout all Italy, and those who were discontented

[027-023]under the duke, among whom were the people of Urbino, took hope of

[027-024]effecting a revolution.

[027-025]Thus it arose that, men’s minds being thus unsettled, it was decided by

[027-026]certain men of Urbino to seize the fortress of San Leo, which was held for

[027-027]the duke, and which they captured by the following means. The castellan

[027-028]was fortifying the rock and causing timber to be taken there; so the

[027-029]conspirators watched, and when certain beams which were being carried to

[027-030]the rock were upon the bridge, so that it was prevented from being drawn

[027-031]up by those inside, they took the opportunity of leaping upon the bridge

[027-032]and thence into the fortress. Upon this capture being effected, the whole

[027-033]state rebelled and recalled the old duke, being encouraged in this, not so

[027-034]much by the capture of the fort, as by the Diet at Magione, from whom they

[027-035]expected to get assistance.

[027-036]Those who heard of the rebellion at Urbino thought they would not lose the

[027-037]opportunity, and at once assembled their men so as to take any town,

[027-038]should any remain in the hands of the duke in that state; and they sent

[027-039]again to Florence to beg that republic to join with them in destroying the

[027-040]common firebrand, showing that the risk was lessened and that they ought

[027-041]not to wait for another opportunity.

[027-042]But the Florentines, from hatred, for sundry reasons, of the Vitelli and

[027-043]Orsini, not only would not ally themselves, but sent Nicolo Machiavelli,

[027-044]their secretary, to offer shelter and assistance to the duke against his

[027-045]enemies. The duke was found full of fear at Imola, because, against

[027-046]everybody’s expectation, his soldiers had at once gone over to the enemy

[027-047]and he found himself disarmed and war at his door. But recovering courage

[027-048]from the offers of the Florentines, he decided to temporize before

[027-049]fighting with the few soldiers that remained to him, and to negotiate for

[027-050]a reconciliation, and also to get assistance. This latter he obtained in

[027-051]two ways, by sending to the King of France for men and by enlisting

[027-052]men-at-arms and others whom he turned into cavalry of a sort: to all he

[027-053]gave money.

[027-054]Notwithstanding this, his enemies drew near to him, and approached

[027-055]Fossombrone, where they encountered some men of the duke and, with the aid

[027-056]of the Orsini and Vitelli, routed them. When this happened, the duke

[027-057]resolved at once to see if he could not close the trouble with offers of

[027-058]reconciliation, and being a most perfect dissembler he did not fail in any

[027-059]practices to make the insurgents understand that he wished every man who

[027-060]had acquired anything to keep it, as it was enough for him to have the

[027-061]title of prince, whilst others might have the principality.

[027-062]And the duke succeeded so well in this that they sent Signor Pagolo to him

[027-063]to negotiate for a reconciliation, and they brought their army to a

[027-064]standstill. But the duke did not stop his preparations, and took every

[027-065]care to provide himself with cavalry and infantry, and that such

[027-066]preparations might not be apparent to the others, he sent his troops in

[027-067]separate parties to every part of the Romagna. In the meanwhile there came

[027-068]also to him five hundred French lancers, and although he found himself

[027-069]sufficiently strong to take vengeance on his enemies in open war, he

[027-070]considered that it would be safer and more advantageous to outwit them,

[027-071]and for this reason he did not stop the work of reconciliation.

[027-072]And that this might be effected the duke concluded a peace with them in

[027-073]which he confirmed their former covenants; he gave them four thousand

[027-074]ducats at once; he promised not to injure the Bentivogli; and he formed an

[027-075]alliance with Giovanni; and moreover he would not force them to come

[027-076]personally into his presence unless it pleased them to do so. On the other

[027-077]hand, they promised to restore to him the duchy of Urbino and other places

[027-078]seized by them, to serve him in all his expeditions, and not to make war

[027-079]against or ally themselves with any one without his permission.

[027-080]This reconciliation being completed, Guido Ubaldo, the Duke of Urbino,

[027-081]again fled to Venice, having first destroyed all the fortresses in his

[027-082]state; because, trusting in the people, he did not wish that the

[027-083]fortresses, which he did not think he could defend, should be held by the

[027-084]enemy, since by these means a check would be kept upon his friends. But

[027-085]the Duke Valentino, having completed this convention, and dispersed his

[027-086]men throughout the Romagna, set out for Imola at the end of November

[027-087]together with his French men-at-arms: thence he went to Cesena, where he

[027-088]stayed some time to negotiate with the envoys of the Vitelli and Orsini,

[027-089]who had assembled with their men in the duchy of Urbino, as to the

[027-090]enterprise in which they should now take part; but nothing being

[027-091]concluded, Oliverotto da Fermo was sent to propose that if the duke wished

[027-092]to undertake an expedition against Tuscany they were ready; if he did not

[027-093]wish it, then they would besiege Sinigalia. To this the duke replied that

[027-094]he did not wish to enter into war with Tuscany, and thus become hostile to

[027-095]the Florentines, but that he was very willing to proceed against

[027-096]Sinigalia.

[027-097]It happened that not long afterwards the town surrendered, but the

[027-098]fortress would not yield to them because the castellan would not give it

[027-099]up to any one but the duke in person; therefore they exhorted him to come

[027-100]there. This appeared a good opportunity to the duke, as, being invited by

[027-101]them, and not going of his own will, he would awaken no suspicions. And

[027-102]the more to reassure them, he allowed all the French men-at-arms who were

[027-103]with him in Lombardy to depart, except the hundred lancers under Mons. di

[027-104]Candales, his brother-in-law. He left Cesena about the middle of December,

[027-105]and went to Fano, and with the utmost cunning and cleverness he persuaded

[027-106]the Vitelli and Orsini to wait for him at Sinigalia, pointing out to them

[027-107]that any lack of compliance would cast a doubt upon the sincerity and

[027-108]permanency of the reconciliation, and that he was a man who wished to make

[027-109]use of the arms and councils of his friends. But Vitellozzo remained very

[027-110]stubborn, for the death of his brother warned him that he should not

[027-111]offend a prince and afterwards trust him; nevertheless, persuaded by

[027-112]Pagolo Orsini, whom the duke had corrupted with gifts and promises, he

[027-113]agreed to wait.

[027-114]Upon this the duke, before his departure from Fano, which was to be on

[027-115]30th December 1502, communicated his designs to eight of his most trusted

[027-116]followers, among whom were Don Michele and the Monsignor d’Euna, who was

[027-117]afterwards cardinal; and he ordered that, as soon as Vitellozzo, Pagolo

[027-118]Orsini, the Duke di Gravina, and Oliverotto should arrive, his followers

[027-119]in pairs should take them one by one, entrusting certain men to certain

[027-120]pairs, who should entertain them until they reached Sinigalia; nor should

[027-121]they be permitted to leave until they came to the duke’s quarters, where

[027-122]they should be seized.

[027-123]The duke afterwards ordered all his horsemen and infantry, of which there

[027-124]were more than two thousand cavalry and ten thousand footmen, to assemble

[027-125]by daybreak at the Metauro, a river five miles distant from Fano, and

[027-126]await him there. He found himself, therefore, on the last day of December

[027-127]at the Metauro with his men, and having sent a cavalcade of about two

[027-128]hundred horsemen before him, he then moved forward the infantry, whom he

[027-129]accompanied with the rest of the men-at-arms.

[027-130]Fano and Sinigalia are two cities of La Marca situated on the shore of the

[027-131]Adriatic Sea, fifteen miles distant from each other, so that he who goes

[027-132]towards Sinigalia has the mountains on his right hand, the bases of which

[027-133]are touched by the sea in some places. The city of Sinigalia is distant

[027-134]from the foot of the mountains a little more than a bow-shot and from the

[027-135]shore about a mile. On the side opposite to the city runs a little river

[027-136]which bathes that part of the walls looking towards Fano, facing the high

[027-137]road. Thus he who draws near to Sinigalia comes for a good space by road

[027-138]along the mountains, and reaches the river which passes by Sinigalia. If

[027-139]he turns to his left hand along the bank of it, and goes for the distance

[027-140]of a bow-shot, he arrives at a bridge which crosses the river; he is then

[027-141]almost abreast of the gate that leads into Sinigalia, not by a straight

[027-142]line, but transversely. Before this gate there stands a collection of

[027-143]houses with a square to which the bank of the river forms one side.

[027-144]The Vitelli and Orsini having received orders to wait for the duke, and to

[027-145]honour him in person, sent away their men to several castles distant from

[027-146]Sinigalia about six miles, so that room could be made for the men of the

[027-147]duke; and they left in Sinigalia only Oliverotto and his band, which

[027-148]consisted of one thousand infantry and one hundred and fifty horsemen, who

[027-149]were quartered in the suburb mentioned above. Matters having been thus

[027-150]arranged, the Duke Valentino left for Sinigalia, and when the leaders of

[027-151]the cavalry reached the bridge they did not pass over, but having opened

[027-152]it, one portion wheeled towards the river and the other towards the

[027-153]country, and a way was left in the middle through which the infantry

[027-154]passed, without stopping, into the town.

[027-155]Vitellozzo, Pagolo, and the Duke di Gravina on mules, accompanied by a few

[027-156]horsemen, went towards the duke; Vitellozo, unarmed and wearing a cape

[027-157]lined with green, appeared very dejected, as if conscious of his

[027-158]approaching death—a circumstance which, in view of the ability of

[027-159]the man and his former fortune, caused some amazement. And it is said that

[027-160]when he parted from his men before setting out for Sinigalia to meet the

[027-161]duke he acted as if it were his last parting from them. He recommended his

[027-162]house and its fortunes to his captains, and advised his nephews that it

[027-163]was not the fortune of their house, but the virtues of their fathers that

[027-164]should be kept in mind. These three, therefore, came before the duke and

[027-165]saluted him respectfully, and were received by him with goodwill; they

[027-166]were at once placed between those who were commissioned to look after

[027-167]them.

[027-168]But the duke noticing that Oliverotto, who had remained with his band in

[027-169]Sinigalia, was missing—for Oliverotto was waiting in the square

[027-170]before his quarters near the river, keeping his men in order and drilling

[027-171]them—signalled with his eye to Don Michelle, to whom the care of

[027-172]Oliverotto had been committed, that he should take measures that

[027-173]Oliverotto should not escape. Therefore Don Michele rode off and joined

[027-174]Oliverotto, telling him that it was not right to keep his men out of their

[027-175]quarters, because these might be taken up by the men of the duke; and he

[027-176]advised him to send them at once to their quarters and to come himself to

[027-177]meet the duke. And Oliverotto, having taken this advice, came before the

[027-178]duke, who, when he saw him, called to him; and Oliverotto, having made his

[027-179]obeisance, joined the others.

[027-180]So the whole party entered Sinigalia, dismounted at the duke’s quarters,

[027-181]and went with him into a secret chamber, where the duke made them

[027-182]prisoners; he then mounted on horseback, and issued orders that the men of

[027-183]Oliverotto and the Orsini should be stripped of their arms. Those of

[027-184]Oliverotto, being at hand, were quickly settled, but those of the Orsini

[027-185]and Vitelli, being at a distance, and having a presentiment of the

[027-186]destruction of their masters, had time to prepare themselves, and bearing

[027-187]in mind the valour and discipline of the Orsinian and Vitellian houses,

[027-188]they stood together against the hostile forces of the country and saved

[027-189]themselves.

[027-190]But the duke’s soldiers, not being content with having pillaged the men of

[027-191]Oliverotto, began to sack Sinigalia, and if the duke had not repressed

[027-192]this outrage by killing some of them they would have completely sacked it.

[027-193]Night having come and the tumult being silenced, the duke prepared to kill

[027-194]Vitellozzo and Oliverotto; he led them into a room and caused them to be

[027-195]strangled. Neither of them used words in keeping with their past lives:

[027-196]Vitellozzo prayed that he might ask of the pope full pardon for his sins;

[027-197]Oliverotto cringed and laid the blame for all injuries against the duke on

[027-198]Vitellozzo. Pagolo and the Duke di Gravina Orsini were kept alive until

[027-199]the duke heard from Rome that the pope had taken the Cardinal Orsino, the

[027-200]Archbishop of Florence, and Messer Jacopo da Santa Croce. After which

[027-201]news, on 18th January 1502, in the castle of Pieve, they also were

[027-202]strangled in the same way.
