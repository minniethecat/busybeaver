# Caracalla

Caracalla was a Roman emperor who reigned from 198 to 217 CE, initially as co-emperor with his father Septimius Severus and later as sole ruler. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli references him as an example of a prince whose cruelty toward his own people proved self-destructive — a case study in the limits of fear as an instrument of rule.

## The Severan Succession

Caracalla inherited the empire from his father Septimius Severus, one of the most effective emperors in Machiavelli's analysis. He was initially co-emperor with his younger brother Geta, whom he had murdered within a year of their father's death. This act set the tone for a reign marked by paranoia and violence.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Failure of Excessive Cruelty

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli notes that while Severus flourished, his dynasty quickly unraveled. His son Caracalla "came to a bad end" — assassinated by his own Praetorian prefect while on campaign [019-167]. Machiavelli attributes this to Caracalla's inability to maintain the balance his father achieved:

> "The opposite characters of Commodus, Severus, Antoninus" produced different results, but all except Severus "came to a bad end" [019-170].

Caracalla, identified here as Antoninus (his official imperial name), serves as evidence that even the son of the most successful emperor could not sustain power if he failed to manage the critical relationship with his soldiers and subjects.

### The Father-Son Contrast

The implicit argument in Chapter 19 is that Caracalla inherited his father's ferocity — the "lion" — but not his cunning — the "fox." Where Severus used cruelty selectively and strategically, Caracalla used it indiscriminately, killing his brother, executing his father's advisers, and ordering massacres of civilians. This indiscriminate cruelty generated the hatred that Machiavelli consistently identifies as fatal for a prince.

### The Severan Pattern

For Machiavelli, the Severan dynasty illustrates the fragility of political inheritance. Severus founded the dynasty through *virtù*, but his son lacked the same quality. The lesson is that the personal qualities of a prince cannot be bequeathed — each ruler must possess *virtù* in his own right.

## Historical Assessment

Caracalla is best remembered historically for the Constitutio Antoniniana (212 CE), which granted Roman citizenship to all free inhabitants of the empire. But for Machiavelli, his reign teaches a simpler lesson: inherited power is worthless without the personal capacity to manage it. His assassination at the age of twenty-nine confirmed Machiavelli's thesis that a ruler who makes himself hated cannot survive.
