# Fortuna

Fortuna (Fortune) is the Machiavellian concept of the unpredictable, uncontrollable force that governs human affairs. Machiavelli personifies Fortune as a woman and a raging river — powerful but potentially resistible through foresight and bold action.

## Machiavelli's Treatment

### Core Argument

Machiavelli opens Chapter XXV by acknowledging the widespread belief that "the affairs of the world are in such wise governed by fortune and by God that men with their wisdom cannot direct them" (025-001). Against this fatalism, he stakes out a middle ground: Fortune controls only half of human actions, leaving the other half to free will and human ability (025-009).

The central metaphor of the chapter compares Fortune to "one of those raging rivers, which when in flood overflows the plains, sweeping away trees and buildings" (025-018). The prince who prepares dikes and barriers during calm weather can channel the next flood harmlessly. The prince who does nothing is swept away.

### Historical Cases

Machiavelli uses Pope Julius II as a case study in fortune and temperament. Julius "proceeded impetuously in all his affairs" and because "the times and circumstances conformed to his methods," he always succeeded (025-055). Had he lived longer and encountered times requiring caution, he would have been ruined. The lesson: success depends on matching one's conduct to the times, and the prince who cannot adapt falls when fortune changes (025-065).

### Gender and Agency

In a famous and controversial passage, Machiavelli concludes that "fortune is a woman, and if you wish to keep her under it is necessary to beat and ill-use her" (025-092). He argues that Fortune favors the young and bold — those who act with impetuosity rather than cold caution. The metaphor underscores his view that passive virtue cannot master fortune; only active, aggressive virtù can.

## Relation to Virtù

Fortuna and [[Virtù]] form the fundamental opposition of Machiavellian political theory. In Chapter VI, Machiavelli shows that the greatest founders — Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus — "owed nothing to fortune beyond opportunity" (006-032). Fortune provides the occasion (occasione); virtù seizes it. Without virtù, fortune's opportunities are wasted. Without fortune's opportunities, virtù has no material to work upon.

## Legacy

The virtù-fortuna dyad became one of the most influential conceptual frameworks in Western political thought. The idea that human agency can resist and channel impersonal forces — rather than simply submitting to divine providence — marks a decisive step toward secular political analysis.
