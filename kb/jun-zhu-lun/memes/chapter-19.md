## CHAPTER XIX. THAT ONE SHOULD AVOID BEING DESPISED AND HATED

[019-001]Now, concerning the characteristics of which mention is made above, I have

[019-002]spoken of the more important ones, the others I wish to discuss briefly

[019-003]under this generality, that the prince must consider, as has been in part

[019-004]said before, how to avoid those things which will make him hated or

[019-005]contemptible; and as often as he shall have succeeded he will have

[019-006]fulfilled his part, and he need not fear any danger in other reproaches.

[019-007]It makes him hated above all things, as I have said, to be rapacious, and

[019-008]to be a violator of the property and women of his subjects, from both of

[019-009]which he must abstain. And when neither their property nor their honor is

[019-010]touched, the majority of men live content, and he has only to contend with

[019-011]the ambition of a few, whom he can curb with ease in many ways.

[019-012]It makes him contemptible to be considered fickle, frivolous, effeminate,

[019-013]mean-spirited, irresolute, from all of which a prince should guard himself

[019-014]as from a rock; and he should endeavour to show in his actions greatness,

[019-015]courage, gravity, and fortitude; and in his private dealings with his

[019-016]subjects let him show that his judgments are irrevocable, and maintain

[019-017]himself in such reputation that no one can hope either to deceive him or

[019-018]to get round him.

[019-019]That prince is highly esteemed who conveys this impression of himself, and

[019-020]he who is highly esteemed is not easily conspired against; for, provided

[019-021]it is well known that he is an excellent man and revered by his people, he

[019-022]can only be attacked with difficulty. For this reason a prince ought to

[019-023]have two fears, one from within, on account of his subjects, the other

[019-024]from without, on account of external powers. From the latter he is

[019-025]defended by being well armed and having good allies, and if he is well

[019-026]armed he will have good friends, and affairs will always remain quiet

[019-027]within when they are quiet without, unless they should have been already

[019-028]disturbed by conspiracy; and even should affairs outside be disturbed, if

[019-029]he has carried out his preparations and has lived as I have said, as long

[019-030]as he does not despair, he will resist every attack, as I said Nabis the

[019-031]Spartan did.

[019-032]But concerning his subjects, when affairs outside are disturbed he has

[019-033]only to fear that they will conspire secretly, from which a prince can

[019-034]easily secure himself by avoiding being hated and despised, and by keeping

[019-035]the people satisfied with him, which it is most necessary for him to

[019-036]accomplish, as I said above at length. And one of the most efficacious

[019-037]remedies that a prince can have against conspiracies is not to be hated

[019-038]and despised by the people, for he who conspires against a prince always

[019-039]expects to please them by his removal; but when the conspirator can only

[019-040]look forward to offending them, he will not have the courage to take such

[019-041]a course, for the difficulties that confront a conspirator are infinite.

[019-042]And as experience shows, many have been the conspiracies, but few have

[019-043]been successful; because he who conspires cannot act alone, nor can he

[019-044]take a companion except from those whom he believes to be malcontents, and

[019-045]as soon as you have opened your mind to a malcontent you have given him

[019-046]the material with which to content himself, for by denouncing you he can

[019-047]look for every advantage; so that, seeing the gain from this course to be

[019-048]assured, and seeing the other to be doubtful and full of dangers, he must

[019-049]be a very rare friend, or a thoroughly obstinate enemy of the prince, to

[019-050]keep faith with you.

[019-051]And, to reduce the matter into a small compass, I say that, on the side of

[019-052]the conspirator, there is nothing but fear, jealousy, prospect of

[019-053]punishment to terrify him; but on the side of the prince there is the

[019-054]majesty of the principality, the laws, the protection of friends and the

[019-055]state to defend him; so that, adding to all these things the popular

[019-056]goodwill, it is impossible that any one should be so rash as to conspire.

[019-057]For whereas in general the conspirator has to fear before the execution of

[019-058]his plot, in this case he has also to fear the sequel to the crime;

[019-059]because on account of it he has the people for an enemy, and thus cannot

[019-060]hope for any escape.

[019-061]Endless examples could be given on this subject, but I will be content with

[019-062]one, brought to pass within the memory of our fathers. Messer Annibale

[019-063]Bentivogli, who was prince in Bologna (grandfather of the present Annibale),

[019-064]having been murdered by the Canneschi, who had conspired against him, not one

[019-065]of his family survived but Messer Giovanni,[1]

[019-066]who was in childhood: immediately after his assassination the people rose and

[019-067]murdered all the Canneschi. This sprung from the popular goodwill which the

[019-068]house of Bentivogli enjoyed in those days in Bologna; which was so great that,

[019-069]although none remained there after the death of Annibale who was able to rule

[019-070]the state, the Bolognese, having information that there was one of the

[019-071]Bentivogli family in Florence, who up to that time had been considered the son

[019-072]of a blacksmith, sent to Florence for him and gave him the government of their

[019-073]city, and it was ruled by him until Messer Giovanni came in due course to the

[019-074]government.

[019-075][1]

[019-076]Giovanni Bentivogli, born in Bologna 1438, died at Milan 1508. He ruled Bologna

[019-077]from 1462 to 1506. Machiavelli’s strong condemnation of conspiracies may

[019-078]get its edge from his own very recent experience (February 1513), when he had

[019-079]been arrested and tortured for his alleged complicity in the Boscoli

[019-080]conspiracy.

[019-081]For this reason I consider that a prince ought to reckon conspiracies of

[019-082]little account when his people hold him in esteem; but when it is hostile

[019-083]to him, and bears hatred towards him, he ought to fear everything and

[019-084]everybody. And well-ordered states and wise princes have taken every care

[019-085]not to drive the nobles to desperation, and to keep the people satisfied

[019-086]and contented, for this is one of the most important objects a prince can

[019-087]have.

[019-088]Among the best ordered and governed kingdoms of our times is France, and

[019-089]in it are found many good institutions on which depend the liberty and

[019-090]security of the king; of these the first is the parliament and its

[019-091]authority, because he who founded the kingdom, knowing the ambition of the

[019-092]nobility and their boldness, considered that a bit to their mouths would

[019-093]be necessary to hold them in; and, on the other side, knowing the hatred

[019-094]of the people, founded in fear, against the nobles, he wished to protect

[019-095]them, yet he was not anxious for this to be the particular care of the

[019-096]king; therefore, to take away the reproach which he would be liable to

[019-097]from the nobles for favouring the people, and from the people for

[019-098]favouring the nobles, he set up an arbiter, who should be one who could

[019-099]beat down the great and favour the lesser without reproach to the king.

[019-100]Neither could you have a better or a more prudent arrangement, or a

[019-101]greater source of security to the king and kingdom. From this one can draw

[019-102]another important conclusion, that princes ought to leave affairs of

[019-103]reproach to the management of others, and keep those of grace in their own

[019-104]hands. And further, I consider that a prince ought to cherish the nobles,

[019-105]but not so as to make himself hated by the people.

[019-106]It may appear, perhaps, to some who have examined the lives and deaths of

[019-107]the Roman emperors that many of them would be an example contrary to my

[019-108]opinion, seeing that some of them lived nobly and showed great qualities

[019-109]of soul, nevertheless they have lost their empire or have been killed by

[019-110]subjects who have conspired against them. Wishing, therefore, to answer

[019-111]these objections, I will recall the characters of some of the emperors,

[019-112]and will show that the causes of their ruin were not different to those

[019-113]alleged by me; at the same time I will only submit for consideration those

[019-114]things that are noteworthy to him who studies the affairs of those times.

[019-115]It seems to me sufficient to take all those emperors who succeeded to the

[019-116]empire from Marcus the philosopher down to Maximinus; they were Marcus and

[019-117]his son Commodus, Pertinax, Julian, Severus and his son Antoninus

[019-118]Caracalla, Macrinus, Heliogabalus, Alexander, and Maximinus.

[019-119]There is first to note that, whereas in other principalities the ambition

[019-120]of the nobles and the insolence of the people only have to be contended

[019-121]with, the Roman emperors had a third difficulty in having to put up with

[019-122]the cruelty and avarice of their soldiers, a matter so beset with

[019-123]difficulties that it was the ruin of many; for it was a hard thing to give

[019-124]satisfaction both to soldiers and people; because the people loved peace,

[019-125]and for this reason they loved the unaspiring prince, whilst the soldiers

[019-126]loved the warlike prince who was bold, cruel, and rapacious, which

[019-127]qualities they were quite willing he should exercise upon the people, so

[019-128]that they could get double pay and give vent to their own greed and

[019-129]cruelty. Hence it arose that those emperors were always overthrown who,

[019-130]either by birth or training, had no great authority, and most of them,

[019-131]especially those who came new to the principality, recognizing the

[019-132]difficulty of these two opposing humours, were inclined to give

[019-133]satisfaction to the soldiers, caring little about injuring the people.

[019-134]Which course was necessary, because, as princes cannot help being hated by

[019-135]someone, they ought, in the first place, to avoid being hated by every

[019-136]one, and when they cannot compass this, they ought to endeavour with the

[019-137]utmost diligence to avoid the hatred of the most powerful. Therefore,

[019-138]those emperors who through inexperience had need of special favour adhered

[019-139]more readily to the soldiers than to the people; a course which turned out

[019-140]advantageous to them or not, accordingly as the prince knew how to

[019-141]maintain authority over them.

[019-142]From these causes it arose that Marcus, Pertinax, and Alexander, being all

[019-143]men of modest life, lovers of justice, enemies to cruelty, humane, and

[019-144]benignant, came to a sad end except Marcus; he alone lived and died

[019-145]honoured, because he had succeeded to the throne by hereditary title, and

[019-146]owed nothing either to the soldiers or the people; and afterwards, being

[019-147]possessed of many virtues which made him respected, he always kept both

[019-148]orders in their places whilst he lived, and was neither hated nor

[019-149]despised.

[019-150]But Pertinax was created emperor against the wishes of the soldiers, who,

[019-151]being accustomed to live licentiously under Commodus, could not endure the

[019-152]honest life to which Pertinax wished to reduce them; thus, having given

[019-153]cause for hatred, to which hatred there was added contempt for his old

[019-154]age, he was overthrown at the very beginning of his administration. And

[019-155]here it should be noted that hatred is acquired as much by good works as

[019-156]by bad ones, therefore, as I said before, a prince wishing to keep his

[019-157]state is very often forced to do evil; for when that body is corrupt whom

[019-158]you think you have need of to maintain yourself—it may be either the

[019-159]people or the soldiers or the nobles—you have to submit to its

[019-160]humours and to gratify them, and then good works will do you harm.

[019-161]But let us come to Alexander, who was a man of such great goodness, that

[019-162]among the other praises which are accorded him is this, that in the

[019-163]fourteen years he held the empire no one was ever put to death by him

[019-164]unjudged; nevertheless, being considered effeminate and a man who allowed

[019-165]himself to be governed by his mother, he became despised, the army

[019-166]conspired against him, and murdered him.

[019-167]Turning now to the opposite characters of Commodus, Severus, Antoninus

[019-168]Caracalla, and Maximinus, you will find them all cruel and rapacious-men

[019-169]who, to satisfy their soldiers, did not hesitate to commit every kind of

[019-170]iniquity against the people; and all, except Severus, came to a bad end;

[019-171]but in Severus there was so much valour that, keeping the soldiers

[019-172]friendly, although the people were oppressed by him, he reigned

[019-173]successfully; for his valour made him so much admired in the sight of the

[019-174]soldiers and people that the latter were kept in a way astonished and awed

[019-175]and the former respectful and satisfied. And because the actions of this

[019-176]man, as a new prince, were great, I wish to show briefly that he knew well

[019-177]how to counterfeit the fox and the lion, which natures, as I said above,

[019-178]it is necessary for a prince to imitate.

[019-179]Knowing the sloth of the Emperor Julian, he persuaded the army in

[019-180]Sclavonia, of which he was captain, that it would be right to go to Rome

[019-181]and avenge the death of Pertinax, who had been killed by the praetorian

[019-182]soldiers; and under this pretext, without appearing to aspire to the

[019-183]throne, he moved the army on Rome, and reached Italy before it was known

[019-184]that he had started. On his arrival at Rome, the Senate, through fear,

[019-185]elected him emperor and killed Julian. After this there remained for

[019-186]Severus, who wished to make himself master of the whole empire, two

[019-187]difficulties; one in Asia, where Niger, head of the Asiatic army, had

[019-188]caused himself to be proclaimed emperor; the other in the west where

[019-189]Albinus was, who also aspired to the throne. And as he considered it

[019-190]dangerous to declare himself hostile to both, he decided to attack Niger

[019-191]and to deceive Albinus. To the latter he wrote that, being elected emperor

[019-192]by the Senate, he was willing to share that dignity with him and sent him

[019-193]the title of Caesar; and, moreover, that the Senate had made Albinus his

[019-194]colleague; which things were accepted by Albinus as true. But after

[019-195]Severus had conquered and killed Niger, and settled oriental affairs, he

[019-196]returned to Rome and complained to the Senate that Albinus, little

[019-197]recognizing the benefits that he had received from him, had by treachery

[019-198]sought to murder him, and for this ingratitude he was compelled to punish

[019-199]him. Afterwards he sought him out in France, and took from him his

[019-200]government and life. He who will, therefore, carefully examine the actions

[019-201]of this man will find him a most valiant lion and a most cunning fox; he

[019-202]will find him feared and respected by every one, and not hated by the

[019-203]army; and it need not be wondered at that he, a new man, was able to hold

[019-204]the empire so well, because his supreme renown always protected him from

[019-205]that hatred which the people might have conceived against him for his

[019-206]violence.

[019-207]But his son Antoninus was a most eminent man, and had very excellent

[019-208]qualities, which made him admirable in the sight of the people and

[019-209]acceptable to the soldiers, for he was a warlike man, most enduring of

[019-210]fatigue, a despiser of all delicate food and other luxuries, which caused

[019-211]him to be beloved by the armies. Nevertheless, his ferocity and cruelties

[019-212]were so great and so unheard of that, after endless single murders, he

[019-213]killed a large number of the people of Rome and all those of Alexandria.

[019-214]He became hated by the whole world, and also feared by those he had around

[019-215]him, to such an extent that he was murdered in the midst of his army by a

[019-216]centurion. And here it must be noted that such-like deaths, which are

[019-217]deliberately inflicted with a resolved and desperate courage, cannot be

[019-218]avoided by princes, because any one who does not fear to die can inflict

[019-219]them; but a prince may fear them the less because they are very rare; he

[019-220]has only to be careful not to do any grave injury to those whom he employs

[019-221]or has around him in the service of the state. Antoninus had not taken

[019-222]this care, but had contumeliously killed a brother of that centurion, whom

[019-223]also he daily threatened, yet retained in his bodyguard; which, as it

[019-224]turned out, was a rash thing to do, and proved the emperor’s ruin.

[019-225]But let us come to Commodus, to whom it should have been very easy to hold

[019-226]the empire, for, being the son of Marcus, he had inherited it, and he had

[019-227]only to follow in the footsteps of his father to please his people and

[019-228]soldiers; but, being by nature cruel and brutal, he gave himself up to

[019-229]amusing the soldiers and corrupting them, so that he might indulge his

[019-230]rapacity upon the people; on the other hand, not maintaining his dignity,

[019-231]often descending to the theatre to compete with gladiators, and doing

[019-232]other vile things, little worthy of the imperial majesty, he fell into

[019-233]contempt with the soldiers, and being hated by one party and despised by

[019-234]the other, he was conspired against and was killed.

[019-235]It remains to discuss the character of Maximinus. He was a very warlike

[019-236]man, and the armies, being disgusted with the effeminacy of Alexander, of

[019-237]whom I have already spoken, killed him and elected Maximinus to the

[019-238]throne. This he did not possess for long, for two things made him hated

[019-239]and despised; the one, his having kept sheep in Thrace, which brought him

[019-240]into contempt (it being well known to all, and considered a great

[019-241]indignity by every one), and the other, his having at the accession to his

[019-242]dominions deferred going to Rome and taking possession of the imperial

[019-243]seat; he had also gained a reputation for the utmost ferocity by having,

[019-244]through his prefects in Rome and elsewhere in the empire, practised many

[019-245]cruelties, so that the whole world was moved to anger at the meanness of

[019-246]his birth and to fear at his barbarity. First Africa rebelled, then the

[019-247]Senate with all the people of Rome, and all Italy conspired against him,

[019-248]to which may be added his own army; this latter, besieging Aquileia and

[019-249]meeting with difficulties in taking it, were disgusted with his cruelties,

[019-250]and fearing him less when they found so many against him, murdered him.

[019-251]I do not wish to discuss Heliogabalus, Macrinus, or Julian, who, being

[019-252]thoroughly contemptible, were quickly wiped out; but I will bring this

[019-253]discourse to a conclusion by saying that princes in our times have this

[019-254]difficulty of giving inordinate satisfaction to their soldiers in a far

[019-255]less degree, because, notwithstanding one has to give them some

[019-256]indulgence, that is soon done; none of these princes have armies that are

[019-257]veterans in the governance and administration of provinces, as were the

[019-258]armies of the Roman Empire; and whereas it was then more necessary to give

[019-259]satisfaction to the soldiers than to the people, it is now more necessary

[019-260]to all princes, except the Turk and the Soldan, to satisfy the people

[019-261]rather the soldiers, because the people are the more powerful.

[019-262]From the above I have excepted the Turk, who always keeps round him twelve

[019-263]thousand infantry and fifteen thousand cavalry on which depend the

[019-264]security and strength of the kingdom, and it is necessary that, putting

[019-265]aside every consideration for the people, he should keep them his friends.

[019-266]The kingdom of the Soldan is similar; being entirely in the hands of

[019-267]soldiers, it follows again that, without regard to the people, he must

[019-268]keep them his friends. But you must note that the state of the Soldan is

[019-269]unlike all other principalities, for the reason that it is like the

[019-270]Christian pontificate, which cannot be called either an hereditary or a

[019-271]newly formed principality; because the sons of the old prince are not the

[019-272]heirs, but he who is elected to that position by those who have authority,

[019-273]and the sons remain only noblemen. And this being an ancient custom, it

[019-274]cannot be called a new principality, because there are none of those

[019-275]difficulties in it that are met with in new ones; for although the prince

[019-276]is new, the constitution of the state is old, and it is framed so as to

[019-277]receive him as if he were its hereditary lord.

[019-278]But returning to the subject of our discourse, I say that whoever will

[019-279]consider it will acknowledge that either hatred or contempt has been fatal

[019-280]to the above-named emperors, and it will be recognized also how it

[019-281]happened that, a number of them acting in one way and a number in another,

[019-282]only one in each way came to a happy end and the rest to unhappy ones.

[019-283]Because it would have been useless and dangerous for Pertinax and

[019-284]Alexander, being new princes, to imitate Marcus, who was heir to the

[019-285]principality; and likewise it would have been utterly destructive to

[019-286]Caracalla, Commodus, and Maximinus to have imitated Severus, they not

[019-287]having sufficient valour to enable them to tread in his footsteps.

[019-288]Therefore a prince, new to the principality, cannot imitate the actions of

[019-289]Marcus, nor, again, is it necessary to follow those of Severus, but he

[019-290]ought to take from Severus those parts which are necessary to found his

[019-291]state, and from Marcus those which are proper and glorious to keep a state

[019-292]that may already be stable and firm.
