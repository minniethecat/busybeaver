## THE LIFE OF CASTRUCCIO CASTRACANI OF LUCCA

WRITTEN BY NICOLO MACHIAVELLI

[028-001]And sent to his friends ZANOBI BUONDELMONTI And LUIGI ALAMANNI

###
CASTRUCCIO CASTRACANI 1284-1328

[028-002]It appears, dearest Zanobi and Luigi, a wonderful thing to those who have

[028-003]considered the matter, that all men, or the larger number of them, who

[028-004]have performed great deeds in the world, and excelled all others in their

[028-005]day, have had their birth and beginning in baseness and obscurity; or have

[028-006]been aggrieved by Fortune in some outrageous way. They have either been

[028-007]exposed to the mercy of wild beasts, or they have had so mean a parentage

[028-008]that in shame they have given themselves out to be sons of Jove or of some

[028-009]other deity. It would be wearisome to relate who these persons may have

[028-010]been because they are well known to everybody, and, as such tales would

[028-011]not be particularly edifying to those who read them, they are omitted. I

[028-012]believe that these lowly beginnings of great men occur because Fortune is

[028-013]desirous of showing to the world that such men owe much to her and little

[028-014]to wisdom, because she begins to show her hand when wisdom can really take

[028-015]no part in their career: thus all success must be attributed to her.

[028-016]Castruccio Castracani of Lucca was one of those men who did great deeds,

[028-017]if he is measured by the times in which he lived and the city in which he

[028-018]was born; but, like many others, he was neither fortunate nor

[028-019]distinguished in his birth, as the course of this history will show. It

[028-020]appeared to be desirable to recall his memory, because I have discerned in

[028-021]him such indications of valour and fortune as should make him a great

[028-022]exemplar to men. I think also that I ought to call your attention to his

[028-023]actions, because you of all men I know delight most in noble deeds.

[028-024]The family of Castracani was formerly numbered among the noble families of

[028-025]Lucca, but in the days of which I speak it had somewhat fallen in estate,

[028-026]as so often happens in this world. To this family was born a son Antonio,

[028-027]who became a priest of the order of San Michele of Lucca, and for this

[028-028]reason was honoured with the title of Messer Antonio. He had an only

[028-029]sister, who had been married to Buonaccorso Cenami, but Buonaccorso dying

[028-030]she became a widow, and not wishing to marry again went to live with her

[028-031]brother. Messer Antonio had a vineyard behind the house where he resided,

[028-032]and as it was bounded on all sides by gardens, any person could have

[028-033]access to it without difficulty. One morning, shortly after sunrise,

[028-034]Madonna Dianora, as the sister of Messer Antonio was called, had occasion

[028-035]to go into the vineyard as usual to gather herbs for seasoning the dinner,

[028-036]and hearing a slight rustling among the leaves of a vine she turned her

[028-037]eyes in that direction, and heard something resembling the cry of an

[028-038]infant. Whereupon she went towards it, and saw the hands and face of a

[028-039]baby who was lying enveloped in the leaves and who seemed to be crying for

[028-040]its mother. Partly wondering and partly fearing, yet full of compassion,

[028-041]she lifted it up and carried it to the house, where she washed it and

[028-042]clothed it with clean linen as is customary, and showed it to Messer

[028-043]Antonio when he returned home. When he heard what had happened and saw the

[028-044]child he was not less surprised or compassionate than his sister. They

[028-045]discussed between themselves what should be done, and seeing that he was

[028-046]priest and that she had no children, they finally determined to bring it

[028-047]up. They had a nurse for it, and it was reared and loved as if it were

[028-048]their own child. They baptized it, and gave it the name of Castruccio

[028-049]after their father. As the years passed Castruccio grew very handsome, and

[028-050]gave evidence of wit and discretion, and learnt with a quickness beyond

[028-051]his years those lessons which Messer Antonio imparted to him. Messer

[028-052]Antonio intended to make a priest of him, and in time would have inducted

[028-053]him into his canonry and other benefices, and all his instruction was

[028-054]given with this object; but Antonio discovered that the character of

[028-055]Castruccio was quite unfitted for the priesthood. As soon as Castruccio

[028-056]reached the age of fourteen he began to take less notice of the chiding of

[028-057]Messer Antonio and Madonna Dianora and no longer to fear them; he left off

[028-058]reading ecclesiastical books, and turned to playing with arms, delighting

[028-059]in nothing so much as in learning their uses, and in running, leaping, and

[028-060]wrestling with other boys. In all exercises he far excelled his companions

[028-061]in courage and bodily strength, and if at any time he did turn to books,

[028-062]only those pleased him which told of wars and the mighty deeds of men.

[028-063]Messer Antonio beheld all this with vexation and sorrow.

[028-064]There lived in the city of Lucca a gentleman of the Guinigi family, named

[028-065]Messer Francesco, whose profession was arms and who in riches, bodily

[028-066]strength, and valour excelled all other men in Lucca. He had often fought

[028-067]under the command of the Visconti of Milan, and as a Ghibelline was the

[028-068]valued leader of that party in Lucca. This gentleman resided in Lucca and

[028-069]was accustomed to assemble with others most mornings and evenings under

[028-070]the balcony of the Podesta, which is at the top of the square of San

[028-071]Michele, the finest square in Lucca, and he had often seen Castruccio

[028-072]taking part with other children of the street in those games of which I

[028-073]have spoken. Noticing that Castruccio far excelled the other boys, and

[028-074]that he appeared to exercise a royal authority over them, and that they

[028-075]loved and obeyed him, Messer Francesco became greatly desirous of learning

[028-076]who he was. Being informed of the circumstances of the bringing up of

[028-077]Castruccio he felt a greater desire to have him near to him. Therefore he

[028-078]called him one day and asked him whether he would more willingly live in

[028-079]the house of a gentleman, where he would learn to ride horses and use

[028-080]arms, or in the house of a priest, where he would learn nothing but masses

[028-081]and the services of the Church. Messer Francesco could see that it pleased

[028-082]Castruccio greatly to hear horses and arms spoken of, even though he stood

[028-083]silent, blushing modestly; but being encouraged by Messer Francesco to

[028-084]speak, he answered that, if his master were agreeable, nothing would

[028-085]please him more than to give up his priestly studies and take up those of

[028-086]a soldier. This reply delighted Messer Francesco, and in a very short time

[028-087]he obtained the consent of Messer Antonio, who was driven to yield by his

[028-088]knowledge of the nature of the lad, and the fear that he would not be able

[028-089]to hold him much longer.

[028-090]Thus Castruccio passed from the house of Messer Antonio the priest to the

[028-091]house of Messer Francesco Guinigi the soldier, and it was astonishing to

[028-092]find that in a very short time he manifested all that virtue and bearing

[028-093]which we are accustomed to associate with a true gentleman. In the first

[028-094]place he became an accomplished horseman, and could manage with ease the

[028-095]most fiery charger, and in all jousts and tournaments, although still a

[028-096]youth, he was observed beyond all others, and he excelled in all exercises

[028-097]of strength and dexterity. But what enhanced so much the charm of these

[028-098]accomplishments, was the delightful modesty which enabled him to avoid

[028-099]offence in either act or word to others, for he was deferential to the

[028-100]great men, modest with his equals, and courteous to his inferiors. These

[028-101]gifts made him beloved, not only by all the Guinigi family, but by all

[028-102]Lucca. When Castruccio had reached his eighteenth year, the Ghibellines

[028-103]were driven from Pavia by the Guelphs, and Messer Francesco was sent by

[028-104]the Visconti to assist the Ghibellines, and with him went Castruccio, in

[028-105]charge of his forces. Castruccio gave ample proof of his prudence and

[028-106]courage in this expedition, acquiring greater reputation than any other

[028-107]captain, and his name and fame were known, not only in Pavia, but

[028-108]throughout all Lombardy.

[028-109]Castruccio, having returned to Lucca in far higher estimation than he left

[028-110]it, did not omit to use all the means in his power to gain as many friends

[028-111]as he could, neglecting none of those arts which are necessary for that

[028-112]purpose. About this time Messer Francesco died, leaving a son thirteen

[028-113]years of age named Pagolo, and having appointed Castruccio to be his son’s

[028-114]tutor and administrator of his estate. Before he died Francesco called

[028-115]Castruccio to him, and prayed him to show Pagolo that goodwill which he

[028-116](Francesco) had always shown to HIM, and to render to the son the

[028-117]gratitude which he had not been able to repay to the father. Upon the

[028-118]death of Francesco, Castruccio became the governor and tutor of Pagolo,

[028-119]which increased enormously his power and position, and created a certain

[028-120]amount of envy against him in Lucca in place of the former universal

[028-121]goodwill, for many men suspected him of harbouring tyrannical intentions.

[028-122]Among these the leading man was Giorgio degli Opizi, the head of the

[028-123]Guelph party. This man hoped after the death of Messer Francesco to become

[028-124]the chief man in Lucca, but it seemed to him that Castruccio, with the

[028-125]great abilities which he already showed, and holding the position of

[028-126]governor, deprived him of his opportunity; therefore he began to sow those

[028-127]seeds which should rob Castruccio of his eminence. Castruccio at first

[028-128]treated this with scorn, but afterwards he grew alarmed, thinking that

[028-129]Messer Giorgio might be able to bring him into disgrace with the deputy of

[028-130]King Ruberto of Naples and have him driven out of Lucca.

[028-131]The Lord of Pisa at that time was Uguccione of the Faggiuola of Arezzo,

[028-132]who being in the first place elected their captain afterwards became their

[028-133]lord. There resided in Paris some exiled Ghibellines from Lucca, with whom

[028-134]Castruccio held communications with the object of effecting their

[028-135]restoration by the help of Uguccione. Castruccio also brought into his

[028-136]plans friends from Lucca who would not endure the authority of the Opizi.

[028-137]Having fixed upon a plan to be followed, Castruccio cautiously fortified

[028-138]the tower of the Onesti, filling it with supplies and munitions of war, in

[028-139]order that it might stand a siege for a few days in case of need. When the

[028-140]night came which had been agreed upon with Uguccione, who had occupied the

[028-141]plain between the mountains and Pisa with many men, the signal was given,

[028-142]and without being observed Uguccione approached the gate of San Piero and

[028-143]set fire to the portcullis. Castruccio raised a great uproar within the

[028-144]city, calling the people to arms and forcing open the gate from his side.

[028-145]Uguccione entered with his men, poured through the town, and killed Messer

[028-146]Giorgio with all his family and many of his friends and supporters. The

[028-147]governor was driven out, and the government reformed according to the

[028-148]wishes of Uguccione, to the detriment of the city, because it was found

[028-149]that more than one hundred families were exiled at that time. Of those who

[028-150]fled, part went to Florence and part to Pistoia, which city was the

[028-151]headquarters of the Guelph party, and for this reason it became most

[028-152]hostile to Uguccione and the Lucchese.

[028-153]As it now appeared to the Florentines and others of the Guelph party that

[028-154]the Ghibellines absorbed too much power in Tuscany, they determined to

[028-155]restore the exiled Guelphs to Lucca. They assembled a large army in the

[028-156]Val di Nievole, and seized Montecatini; from thence they marched to

[028-157]Montecarlo, in order to secure the free passage into Lucca. Upon this

[028-158]Uguccione assembled his Pisan and Lucchese forces, and with a number of

[028-159]German cavalry which he drew out of Lombardy, he moved against the

[028-160]quarters of the Florentines, who upon the appearance of the enemy withdrew

[028-161]from Montecarlo, and posted themselves between Montecatini and Pescia.

[028-162]Uguccione now took up a position near to Montecarlo, and within about two

[028-163]miles of the enemy, and slight skirmishes between the horse of both

[028-164]parties were of daily occurrence. Owing to the illness of Uguccione, the

[028-165]Pisans and Lucchese delayed coming to battle with the enemy. Uguccione,

[028-166]finding himself growing worse, went to Montecarlo to be cured, and left

[028-167]the command of the army in the hands of Castruccio. This change brought

[028-168]about the ruin of the Guelphs, who, thinking that the hostile army having

[028-169]lost its captain had lost its head, grew over-confident. Castruccio

[028-170]observed this, and allowed some days to pass in order to encourage this

[028-171]belief; he also showed signs of fear, and did not allow any of the

[028-172]munitions of the camp to be used. On the other side, the Guelphs grew more

[028-173]insolent the more they saw these evidences of fear, and every day they

[028-174]drew out in the order of battle in front of the army of Castruccio.

[028-175]Presently, deeming that the enemy was sufficiently emboldened, and having

[028-176]mastered their tactics, he decided to join battle with them. First he

[028-177]spoke a few words of encouragement to his soldiers, and pointed out to

[028-178]them the certainty of victory if they would but obey his commands.

[028-179]Castruccio had noticed how the enemy had placed all his best troops in the

[028-180]centre of the line of battle, and his less reliable men on the wings of

[028-181]the army; whereupon he did exactly the opposite, putting his most valiant

[028-182]men on the flanks, while those on whom he could not so strongly rely he

[028-183]moved to the centre. Observing this order of battle, he drew out of his

[028-184]lines and quickly came in sight of the hostile army, who, as usual, had

[028-185]come in their insolence to defy him. He then commanded his centre

[028-186]squadrons to march slowly, whilst he moved rapidly forward those on the

[028-187]wings. Thus, when they came into contact with the enemy, only the wings of

[028-188]the two armies became engaged, whilst the center battalions remained out

[028-189]of action, for these two portions of the line of battle were separated

[028-190]from each other by a long interval and thus unable to reach each other. By

[028-191]this expedient the more valiant part of Castruccio’s men were opposed to

[028-192]the weaker part of the enemy’s troops, and the most efficient men of the

[028-193]enemy were disengaged; and thus the Florentines were unable to fight with

[028-194]those who were arrayed opposite to them, or to give any assistance to

[028-195]their own flanks. So, without much difficulty, Castruccio put the enemy to

[028-196]flight on both flanks, and the centre battalions took to flight when they

[028-197]found themselves exposed to attack, without having a chance of displaying

[028-198]their valour. The defeat was complete, and the loss in men very heavy,

[028-199]there being more than ten thousand men killed with many officers and

[028-200]knights of the Guelph party in Tuscany, and also many princes who had come

[028-201]to help them, among whom were Piero, the brother of King Ruberto, and

[028-202]Carlo, his nephew, and Filippo, the lord of Taranto. On the part of

[028-203]Castruccio the loss did not amount to more than three hundred men, among

[028-204]whom was Francesco, the son of Uguccione, who, being young and rash, was

[028-205]killed in the first onset.

[028-206]This victory so greatly increased the reputation of Castruccio that

[028-207]Uguccione conceived some jealousy and suspicion of him, because it

[028-208]appeared to Uguccione that this victory had given him no increase of

[028-209]power, but rather than diminished it. Being of this mind, he only waited

[028-210]for an opportunity to give effect to it. This occurred on the death of

[028-211]Pier Agnolo Micheli, a man of great repute and abilities in Lucca, the

[028-212]murderer of whom fled to the house of Castruccio for refuge. On the

[028-213]sergeants of the captain going to arrest the murderer, they were driven

[028-214]off by Castruccio, and the murderer escaped. This affair coming to the

[028-215]knowledge of Uguccione, who was then at Pisa, it appeared to him a proper

[028-216]opportunity to punish Castruccio. He therefore sent for his son Neri, who

[028-217]was the governor of Lucca, and commissioned him to take Castruccio

[028-218]prisoner at a banquet and put him to death. Castruccio, fearing no evil,

[028-219]went to the governor in a friendly way, was entertained at supper, and

[028-220]then thrown into prison. But Neri, fearing to put him to death lest the

[028-221]people should be incensed, kept him alive, in order to hear further from

[028-222]his father concerning his intentions. Ugucionne cursed the hesitation and

[028-223]cowardice of his son, and at once set out from Pisa to Lucca with four

[028-224]hundred horsemen to finish the business in his own way; but he had not yet

[028-225]reached the baths when the Pisans rebelled and put his deputy to death and

[028-226]created Count Gaddo della Gherardesca their lord. Before Uguccione reached

[028-227]Lucca he heard of the occurrences at Pisa, but it did not appear wise to

[028-228]him to turn back, lest the Lucchese with the example of Pisa before them

[028-229]should close their gates against him. But the Lucchese, having heard of

[028-230]what had happened at Pisa, availed themselves of this opportunity to

[028-231]demand the liberation of Castruccio, notwithstanding that Uguccione had

[028-232]arrived in their city. They first began to speak of it in private circles,

[028-233]afterwards openly in the squares and streets; then they raised a tumult,

[028-234]and with arms in their hands went to Uguccione and demanded that

[028-235]Castruccio should be set at liberty. Uguccione, fearing that worse might

[028-236]happen, released him from prison. Whereupon Castruccio gathered his

[028-237]friends around him, and with the help of the people attacked Uguccione;

[028-238]who, finding he had no resource but in flight, rode away with his friends

[028-239]to Lombardy, to the lords of Scale, where he died in poverty.

[028-240]But Castruccio from being a prisoner became almost a prince in Lucca, and

[028-241]he carried himself so discreetly with his friends and the people that they

[028-242]appointed him captain of their army for one year. Having obtained this,

[028-243]and wishing to gain renown in war, he planned the recovery of the many

[028-244]towns which had rebelled after the departure of Uguccione, and with the

[028-245]help of the Pisans, with whom he had concluded a treaty, he marched to

[028-246]Serezzana. To capture this place he constructed a fort against it, which

[028-247]is called to-day Zerezzanello; in the course of two months Castruccio

[028-248]captured the town. With the reputation gained at that siege, he rapidly

[028-249]seized Massa, Carrara, and Lavenza, and in a short time had overrun the

[028-250]whole of Lunigiana. In order to close the pass which leads from Lombardy

[028-251]to Lunigiana, he besieged Pontremoli and wrested it from the hands of

[028-252]Messer Anastagio Palavicini, who was the lord of it. After this victory he

[028-253]returned to Lucca, and was welcomed by the whole people. And now

[028-254]Castruccio, deeming it imprudent any longer to defer making himself a

[028-255]prince, got himself created the lord of Lucca by the help of Pazzino del

[028-256]Poggio, Puccinello dal Portico, Francesco Boccansacchi, and Cecco Guinigi,

[028-257]all of whom he had corrupted; and he was afterwards solemnly and

[028-258]deliberately elected prince by the people. At this time Frederick of

[028-259]Bavaria, the King of the Romans, came into Italy to assume the Imperial

[028-260]crown, and Castruccio, in order that he might make friends with him, met

[028-261]him at the head of five hundred horsemen. Castruccio had left as his

[028-262]deputy in Lucca, Pagolo Guinigi, who was held in high estimation, because

[028-263]of the people’s love for the memory of his father. Castruccio was received

[028-264]in great honour by Frederick, and many privileges were conferred upon him,

[028-265]and he was appointed the emperor’s lieutenant in Tuscany. At this time the

[028-266]Pisans were in great fear of Gaddo della Gherardesca, whom they had driven

[028-267]out of Pisa, and they had recourse for assistance to Frederick. Frederick

[028-268]created Castruccio the lord of Pisa, and the Pisans, in dread of the

[028-269]Guelph party, and particularly of the Florentines, were constrained to

[028-270]accept him as their lord.

[028-271]Frederick, having appointed a governor in Rome to watch his Italian

[028-272]affairs, returned to Germany. All the Tuscan and Lombardian Ghibellines,

[028-273]who followed the imperial lead, had recourse to Castruccio for help and

[028-274]counsel, and all promised him the governorship of his country, if enabled

[028-275]to recover it with his assistance. Among these exiles were Matteo Guidi,

[028-276]Nardo Scolari, Lapo Uberti, Gerozzo Nardi, and Piero Buonaccorsi, all

[028-277]exiled Florentines and Ghibellines. Castruccio had the secret intention of

[028-278]becoming the master of all Tuscany by the aid of these men and of his own

[028-279]forces; and in order to gain greater weight in affairs, he entered into a

[028-280]league with Messer Matteo Visconti, the Prince of Milan, and organized for

[028-281]him the forces of his city and the country districts. As Lucca had five

[028-282]gates, he divided his own country districts into five parts, which he

[028-283]supplied with arms, and enrolled the men under captains and ensigns, so

[028-284]that he could quickly bring into the field twenty thousand soldiers,

[028-285]without those whom he could summon to his assistance from Pisa. While he

[028-286]surrounded himself with these forces and allies, it happened at Messer

[028-287]Matteo Visconti was attacked by the Guelphs of Piacenza, who had driven

[028-288]out the Ghibellines with the assistance of a Florentine army and the King

[028-289]Ruberto. Messer Matteo called upon Castruccio to invade the Florentines in

[028-290]their own territories, so that, being attacked at home, they should be

[028-291]compelled to draw their army out of Lombardy in order to defend

[028-292]themselves. Castruccio invaded the Valdarno, and seized Fucecchio and San

[028-293]Miniato, inflicting immense damage upon the country. Whereupon the

[028-294]Florentines recalled their army, which had scarcely reached Tuscany, when

[028-295]Castruccio was forced by other necessities to return to Lucca.

[028-296]There resided in the city of Lucca the Poggio family, who were so powerful

[028-297]that they could not only elevate Castruccio, but even advance him to the

[028-298]dignity of prince; and it appearing to them they had not received such

[028-299]rewards for their services as they deserved, they incited other families

[028-300]to rebel and to drive Castruccio out of Lucca. They found their

[028-301]opportunity one morning, and arming themselves, they set upon the

[028-302]lieutenant whom Castruccio had left to maintain order and killed him. They

[028-303]endeavoured to raise the people in revolt, but Stefano di Poggio, a

[028-304]peaceable old man who had taken no hand in the rebellion, intervened and

[028-305]compelled them by his authority to lay down their arms; and he offered to

[028-306]be their mediator with Castruccio to obtain from him what they desired.

[028-307]Therefore they laid down their arms with no greater intelligence than they

[028-308]had taken them up. Castruccio, having heard the news of what had happened

[028-309]at Lucca, at once put Pagolo Guinigi in command of the army, and with a

[028-310]troop of cavalry set out for home. Contrary to his expectations, he found

[028-311]the rebellion at an end, yet he posted his men in the most advantageous

[028-312]places throughout the city. As it appeared to Stefano that Castruccio

[028-313]ought to be very much obliged to him, he sought him out, and without

[028-314]saying anything on his own behalf, for he did not recognize any need for

[028-315]doing so, he begged Castruccio to pardon the other members of his family

[028-316]by reason of their youth, their former friendships, and the obligations

[028-317]which Castruccio was under to their house. To this Castruccio graciously

[028-318]responded, and begged Stefano to reassure himself, declaring that it gave

[028-319]him more pleasure to find the tumult at an end than it had ever caused him

[028-320]anxiety to hear of its inception. He encouraged Stefano to bring his

[028-321]family to him, saying that he thanked God for having given him the

[028-322]opportunity of showing his clemency and liberality. Upon the word of

[028-323]Stefano and Castruccio they surrendered, and with Stefano were immediately

[028-324]thrown into prison and put to death. Meanwhile the Florentines had

[028-325]recovered San Miniato, whereupon it seemed advisable to Castruccio to make

[028-326]peace, as it did not appear to him that he was sufficiently secure at

[028-327]Lucca to leave him. He approached the Florentines with the proposal of a

[028-328]truce, which they readily entertained, for they were weary of the war, and

[028-329]desirous of getting rid of the expenses of it. A treaty was concluded with

[028-330]them for two years, by which both parties agreed to keep the conquests

[028-331]they had made. Castruccio thus released from this trouble, turned his

[028-332]attention to affairs in Lucca, and in order that he should not again be

[028-333]subject to the perils from which he had just escaped, he, under various

[028-334]pretences and reasons, first wiped out all those who by their ambition

[028-335]might aspire to the principality; not sparing one of them, but depriving

[028-336]them of country and property, and those whom he had in his hands of life

[028-337]also, stating that he had found by experience that none of them were to be

[028-338]trusted. Then for his further security he raised a fortress in Lucca with

[028-339]the stones of the towers of those whom he had killed or hunted out of the

[028-340]state.

[028-341]Whilst Castruccio made peace with the Florentines, and strengthened his

[028-696]From what has been related here it will be seen that Castruccio was a man

[028-697]of exceptional abilities, not only measured by men of his own time, but

[028-698]also by those of an earlier date. In stature he was above the ordinary

[028-699]height, and perfectly proportioned. He was of a gracious presence, and he

[028-700]welcomed men with such urbanity that those who spoke with him rarely left

[028-701]him displeased. His hair was inclined to be red, and he wore it cut short

[028-702]above the ears, and, whether it rained or snowed, he always went without a

[028-703]hat. He was delightful among friends, but terrible to his enemies; just to

[028-704]his subjects; ready to play false with the unfaithful, and willing to

[028-705]overcome by fraud those whom he desired to subdue, because he was wont to

[028-706]say that it was the victory that brought the glory, not the methods of

[028-707]achieving it. No one was bolder in facing danger, none more prudent in

[028-708]extricating himself. He was accustomed to say that men ought to attempt

[028-709]everything and fear nothing; that God is a lover of strong men, because

[028-710]one always sees that the weak are chastised by the strong. He was also

[028-711]wonderfully sharp or biting though courteous in his answers; and as he did

[028-712]not look for any indulgence in this way of speaking from others, so he was

[028-713]not angered with others did not show it to him. It has often happened that

[028-714]he has listened quietly when others have spoken sharply to him, as on the

[028-715]following occasions. He had caused a ducat to be given for a partridge,
