# Native Troops

Native troops — a prince's "own arms" — are the militia drawn from his own subjects. Machiavelli elevates this concept to the very foundation of princely power: without native arms, no principality can be secure, regardless of the prince's other abilities.

## The Central Doctrine

Machiavelli states the principle with characteristic force: "the principal foundations of all states... are good laws and good arms; and as there cannot be good laws where the state is not well armed, it follows that where they are well armed they have good laws" (012-001). Arms are not merely a tool of the prince but the precondition of all other political goods.

He identifies four kinds of arms: "one's own, mercenaries, auxiliaries, or mixed" (012-015). Of these, only native troops are reliable. Mercenaries are "useless and dangerous" (012-015); auxiliaries are "more dangerous than mercenaries" because they are united under foreign command (013-016). Mixed arms — a combination of native and foreign troops — are better than purely mercenary forces but still inferior to a fully native army.

## Historical Evidence

The entire political history Machiavelli recounts is a demonstration of this thesis. Francesco Sforza became Duke of Milan through his own arms; his sons, relying on others, lost the state. Cesare Borgia initially depended on French auxiliaries, found them unreliable, and turned to "deceits and frauds" to secure himself — but Machiavelli implies that even Borgia would ultimately have needed his own arms had he lived longer.

The catastrophic defeat of Venice at Vaila — "when Venice lost in one day all that she had acquired over eight hundred years" (P01-164) — occurred because the Republic relied entirely on mercenary *condottieri*. A republic "armed with its own arms" would have been secure; without them, it was prey.

## The Arming of Subjects

Machiavelli's final chapter calls for the arming of Italy against the "barbarians." The advice is practical: a prince who arms his subjects binds them to him, for "when you arm them, those arms become yours" (020-001). The unarmed prince "diminishes the value of his own arms" and renders himself contemptible.
