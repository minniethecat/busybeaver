## CHAPTER XIV. THAT WHICH CONCERNS A PRINCE ON THE SUBJECT OF THE ART OF WAR

[014-001]A prince ought to have no other aim or thought, nor select anything else

[014-002]for his study, than war and its rules and discipline; for this is the sole

[014-003]art that belongs to him who rules, and it is of such force that it not

[014-004]only upholds those who are born princes, but it often enables men to rise

[014-005]from a private station to that rank. And, on the contrary, it is seen that

[014-006]when princes have thought more of ease than of arms they have lost their

[014-007]states. And the first cause of your losing it is to neglect this art; and

[014-008]what enables you to acquire a state is to be master of the art. Francesco

[014-009]Sforza, through being martial, from a private person became Duke of Milan;

[014-010]and the sons, through avoiding the hardships and troubles of arms, from

[014-011]dukes became private persons. For among other evils which being unarmed

[014-012]brings you, it causes you to be despised, and this is one of those

[014-013]ignominies against which a prince ought to guard himself, as is shown

[014-014]later on. Because there is nothing proportionate between the armed and the

[014-015]unarmed; and it is not reasonable that he who is armed should yield

[014-016]obedience willingly to him who is unarmed, or that the unarmed man should

[014-017]be secure among armed servants. Because, there being in the one disdain

[014-018]and in the other suspicion, it is not possible for them to work well

[014-019]together. And therefore a prince who does not understand the art of war,

[014-020]over and above the other misfortunes already mentioned, cannot be

[014-021]respected by his soldiers, nor can he rely on them. He ought never,

[014-022]therefore, to have out of his thoughts this subject of war, and in peace

[014-023]he should addict himself more to its exercise than in war; this he can do

[014-024]in two ways, the one by action, the other by study.

[014-025]As regards action, he ought above all things to keep his men well

[014-026]organized and drilled, to follow incessantly the chase, by which he

[014-027]accustoms his body to hardships, and learns something of the nature of

[014-028]localities, and gets to find out how the mountains rise, how the valleys

[014-029]open out, how the plains lie, and to understand the nature of rivers and

[014-030]marshes, and in all this to take the greatest care. Which knowledge is

[014-031]useful in two ways. Firstly, he learns to know his country, and is better

[014-032]able to undertake its defence; afterwards, by means of the knowledge and

[014-033]observation of that locality, he understands with ease any other which it

[014-034]may be necessary for him to study hereafter; because the hills, valleys,

[014-035]and plains, and rivers and marshes that are, for instance, in Tuscany,

[014-036]have a certain resemblance to those of other countries, so that with a

[014-037]knowledge of the aspect of one country one can easily arrive at a

[014-038]knowledge of others. And the prince that lacks this skill lacks the

[014-039]essential which it is desirable that a captain should possess, for it

[014-040]teaches him to surprise his enemy, to select quarters, to lead armies, to

[014-041]array the battle, to besiege towns to advantage.

[014-042]Philopoemen,[1]

[014-043]Prince of the Achaeans, among other praises which writers have bestowed on him,

[014-044]is commended because in time of peace he never had anything in his mind but the

[014-045]rules of war; and when he was in the country with friends, he often stopped and

[014-046]reasoned with them: “If the enemy should be upon that hill, and we should

[014-047]find ourselves here with our army, with whom would be the advantage? How should

[014-048]one best advance to meet him, keeping the ranks? If we should wish to retreat,

[014-049]how ought we to pursue?” And he would set forth to them, as he went, all

[014-050]the chances that could befall an army; he would listen to their opinion and

[014-051]state his, confirming it with reasons, so that by these continual discussions

[014-052]there could never arise, in time of war, any unexpected circumstances that he

[014-053]could not deal with.

[014-054][1]

[014-055]Philopoemen, “the last of the Greeks,” born 252 B.C., died 183 B.C.

[014-056]But to exercise the intellect the prince should read histories, and study

[014-057]there the actions of illustrious men, to see how they have borne

[014-058]themselves in war, to examine the causes of their victories and defeat, so

[014-059]as to avoid the latter and imitate the former; and above all do as an

[014-060]illustrious man did, who took as an exemplar one who had been praised and

[014-061]famous before him, and whose achievements and deeds he always kept in his

[014-062]mind, as it is said Alexander the Great imitated Achilles, Caesar

[014-063]Alexander, Scipio Cyrus. And whoever reads the life of Cyrus, written by

[014-064]Xenophon, will recognize afterwards in the life of Scipio how that

[014-065]imitation was his glory, and how in chastity, affability, humanity, and

[014-066]liberality Scipio conformed to those things which have been written of

[014-067]Cyrus by Xenophon. A wise prince ought to observe some such rules, and

[014-068]never in peaceful times stand idle, but increase his resources with

[014-069]industry in such a way that they may be available to him in adversity, so

[014-070]that if fortune chances it may find him prepared to resist her blows.
