# Achilles

Achilles is the legendary Greek hero of the Trojan War, the central figure of Homer's *Iliad*. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli invokes Achilles twice: as a model of martial virtue worthy of emulation, and as a symbol of the prince's need to combine the nature of man and beast.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Exemplar of Imitation

In Chapter 14, "That Which Concerns a Prince on the Subject of the Art of War," Machiavelli recommends that a prince study the deeds of great leaders:

> "Alexander the Great imitated Achilles, Caesar imitated Alexander, Scipio imitated Cyrus" [014-062].

Achilles stands at the head of this chain of emulation — he is the archetypal warrior whose example inspires conquerors across the ages. For Machiavelli, this demonstrates that excellence in statecraft is cultivated through the deliberate study and imitation of historical and legendary exemplars.

### The Centaur's Pupil

In Chapter 18, "Concerning the Way in Which Princes Should Keep Faith," Machiavelli makes his most famous argument — that a prince must know how to use both law (the way of man) and force (the way of the beast). He invokes Achilles to sanction this claim:

> "Achilles and many other princes of old were given to the Centaur Chiron to be brought up and educated" [018-014].

The centaur, half-man and half-beast, is the perfect tutor for a prince. Machiavelli interprets Achilles's mythical education as allegorical proof that a ruler must master both natures. To be only human — only lawful, only merciful — is insufficient; the prince must also know when to be the lion and the fox.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli's use of Achilles is characteristically secular. The hero's religious or literary significance is irrelevant; what matters is his practical political meaning. As a model for emulation and a symbol of the necessary union of force and fraud, Achilles serves *The Prince* as both inspiration and argument — proof that even the ancients understood what Renaissance princes have forgotten.
