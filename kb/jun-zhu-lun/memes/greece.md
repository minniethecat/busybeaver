## Overview

In The Prince, Greece appears not as a contemporary Renaissance power but as the classical world of city-states and kingdoms that Machiavelli draws upon for historical parallels. References span from the Hellenistic period after Alexander the Great through the Roman conquest, providing a rich source of political examples for the author's analysis of principalities.

## Political Context

Machiavelli's Greece is primarily the Greece of the Hellenistic and Roman Republican periods—a landscape of competing leagues, kingdoms, and city-states. The region was characterized by political fragmentation that made it vulnerable to intervention by stronger external powers. This structural weakness is precisely what Machiavelli analyzes: the inability of divided Greek states to resist either Macedonian or Roman hegemony.

> "The Romans were brought into Greece by the Ætolians; and in every other country where they obtained a footing, they were brought in by the inhabitants." (003-104)

## Role in The Prince

Greece serves as Machiavelli's primary classical laboratory for analyzing how foreign powers enter and dominate a region. In Chapter 3, he uses the Roman intervention in Greece to demonstrate his rule that outside powers are always "brought in" by local factions seeking advantage against their neighbors (003-104). The Ætolians invited Roman intervention against Macedon, and the result was the subjugation of all Greece—a warning to princes who invite foreign aid.

Chapter 4 examines why Alexander's successors maintained control of Greece while other conquerors failed, arguing that the centralized nature of the Persian-style kingdom made it harder to hold than the federated structure of Greece after Roman conquest (004-064). In Chapter 5, Machiavelli contrasts the Spartan and Roman methods of holding Greece: the Spartans tried to impose oligarchies and failed, while the Romans dismantled the existing political structures and succeeded (005-014).

> "They wished to hold Greece as the Spartans and the Romans held it. The Spartans tried to do so by establishing an oligarchy there, and failed." (005-014)

## Related Events

- **The Roman Conquest of Greece**: Rome's gradual absorption of Greek states through the mechanism of invitations by local factions (003-104).
- **Antiochus and the Ætolian War**: King Antiochus III of the Seleucid Empire intervened in Greece at the invitation of the Ætolian League, only to be defeated by Rome—an example Machiavelli uses of failed foreign intervention (003-144, 021-045).
- **The Servitude of Greece**: Machiavelli references the Ottoman conquest of Byzantine Greece as a contemporary warning: "they sent ten thousand Turks into Greece, who, on the war continuing, were the cause of the servitude of Greece to the infidels" (013-028).
