# Giovanni Fogliani

Giovanni Fogliani was an Italian nobleman and the maternal uncle of Oliverotto da Fermo. In Chapter 8 of *The Prince*, Machiavelli recounts how Fogliani was treacherously murdered by his nephew in the infamous Banquet of Fermo — a cautionary tale about misplaced trust and criminal ambition.

## Historical Context

Fogliani was a respected citizen of Fermo, a city in the Marche region of Italy. After Oliverotto's father died, Fogliani took in his young nephew and raised him "in the early days of his youth" (008-050). He later arranged for Oliverotto to serve as a soldier under Paolo Vitelli, facilitating his nephew's military career.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Banquet of Fermo

Machiavelli tells the story in Chapter 8, "Concerning Those Who Have Obtained a Principality by Wickedness," as a vivid example of criminal usurpation. Oliverotto, having established himself as a capable condottiero, decided to seize Fermo for himself. He wrote to his uncle:

> "So he wrote to Giovanni Fogliani that, having been away from home for many years, he wished to visit him and his city" [008-058].

Fogliani, suspecting nothing, received his nephew warmly and allowed Oliverotto's soldiers into the city. Then came the betrayal:

> "He invited Giovanni Fogliani and the chiefs of Fermo to a banquet. When the viands and all the entertainments were ended, Oliverotto artfully began certain grave discourses..." [008-071].

At the signal, Oliverotto's soldiers emerged and killed Fogliani and all the other guests, after which Oliverotto seized control of Fermo.

### Machiavelli's Judgment

Machiavelli uses Fogliani's murder to illustrate how trust — especially toward family — can be weaponized by an ambitious criminal. Fogliani's fate is not an example of Machiavellian virtù but rather of the vulnerability of those who fail to recognize the danger posed by those closest to them.

## Historical Assessment

Giovanni Fogliani remains a minor figure in Renaissance history, remembered almost entirely through Machiavelli's account. His murder serves in *The Prince* as a stark warning: even the bonds of blood offer no protection against a prince who has resolved to be wicked. Fogliani's tragedy is that he trusted his nephew, and that trust cost him his life and his city.
