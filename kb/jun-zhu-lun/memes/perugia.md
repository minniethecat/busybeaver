# Perugia

**Perugia** was an Umbrian city-state ruled by the Baglioni family — known to Machiavelli's readers as the site where conspirators gathered against Cesare Borgia, and where a prince's failure to act decisively proved fatal.

## Overview

Perugia occupied a strategic position in central Italy, controlling the routes between Tuscany and the Papal States. Under the Baglioni, the city maintained a precarious independence, but its rulers never consolidated the kind of stable hereditary authority that the Este enjoyed in Ferrara. The Baglioni were tyrants rather than princes — rulers whose power rested on force rather than legitimacy.

Machiavelli knew Perugia well. The city was "already possessed" (007-160) by Cesare Borgia during his Romagna campaign, and it was at nearby Magione — "in the district of Perugia" (027-012) — that Borgia's enemies gathered to plot his destruction.

## Political Context

### The Baglioni Tyranny

The Baglioni family ruled Perugia through the classic methods of Renaissance tyranny: control of the city's fortresses, command of armed retainers, and elimination of rivals. But they lacked the ancient legitimacy of the Este or the broad popular support that sustained the Bentivogli in Bologna. Their rule was, in Machiavelli's terms, sustained by fortune rather than virtù.

### The Magione Conspiracy

In October 1502, the enemies of Cesare Borgia gathered at Magione — "in the district of Perugia, to which came the Baglioni, the tyrant of Perugia" (027-014) — to plot the Duke's overthrow. The conspirators included Giampaolo Baglioni, lord of Perugia, along with representatives of the Orsini, the Vitelli, and other displaced lords. The gathering at Perugia made the city a pivot point in Borgia's political drama.

## Role in The Prince

### The Magione Meeting

Machiavelli devotes sustained attention to the Magione conspiracy in Chapter 7 and the Valentino Appendix. "A meeting of the Magione in Perugia" (007-097) brought together all of Borgia's enemies. From this conspiracy "sprung the rebellion at Urbino and the tumults in the Romagna" — a cascade of troubles that tested Borgia's political genius and ultimately demonstrated his mastery of the arts of deception and revenge.

### The Missed Opportunity

Machiavelli's most striking reflection on Perugia concerns what Cesare Borgia did NOT do. Borgia had captured Giampaolo Baglioni during the Sinigaglia operation, and Machiavelli observes that the Duke should have eliminated Baglioni as a threat — just as he eliminated the Orsini leaders. The failure to destroy Perugia's ruler left a potential enemy alive, a violation of the principle that a prince must, when striking, strike so hard that there is no fear of revenge.

> "From this sprung the rebellion at Urbino and the tumults in the Romagna, with endless dangers to the duke, who overcame them all with the assistance of the French." (007-097)

### Fortune and the Baglioni

The Baglioni's continued rule of Perugia — despite their manifest vulnerabilities — illustrates Machiavelli's larger point about fortune. Some rulers survive not through skill but through the accidents of timing and the indulgence of stronger powers. Perugia, like other minor Italian states, endured only so long as the great powers found it convenient to tolerate its existence.

## Related Events

- The Magione Conspiracy (1502): Borgia's enemies gathered near Perugia to organize his overthrow
- The Sinigaglia Massacre (1502): Borgia retaliated by executing the conspiracy's leaders, though Baglioni escaped
- The Salt War (1540): after Machiavelli's death, Perugia lost its independence to the Papal States
