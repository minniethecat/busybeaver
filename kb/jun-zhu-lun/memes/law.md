# Law (Legge)

Law (legge) in Machiavelli's political vocabulary is inseparable from arms. The famous formula — "good laws and good arms" — appears early in The Prince and structures the entire work. Yet Machiavelli subordinates law to force: without good arms, good laws are powerless. The prince must know how to use both the man and the beast.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli's discussion of law spans Chapters 3, 5, 6, 12, and 18. His treatment is characteristically unsentimental — law is an instrument of rule, not an expression of justice.

### 核心论点

- Good laws and good arms are the twin foundations of every state; where there are good arms, there are necessarily good laws (012-010, 012-012)
- Conquered states accustomed to living under their own laws present a particular challenge: the prince must either destroy them, reside there, or permit their laws while drawing tribute (005-002, 005-005, 005-015)
- New principalities face difficulties precisely because men willingly change rulers hoping for better conditions, but find that new laws and taxes are rarely improvements (003-054, 003-058)
- The prince must be both man and beast — the man rules through law, the beast through force. The centaur Chiron, tutor to Achilles and other princes, symbolizes this duality (018 — centaur metaphor)
- Law alone is insufficient; the fox knows how to circumvent snares (law), but the lion frightens wolves (force). A prudent prince uses both natures.

### 历史例证

- **Moses** — received the laws on Mount Sinai and enforced them through divine authority, combining law with the appearance of divine sanction (006-059)
- **The Romans** — their success in governing conquered territories rested on their practice of sending colonies and permitting local laws where appropriate (003-054)
- **Republic of Venice** — an example of a state accustomed to its own laws and liberty; such states are harder to hold than principalities (005-002)
- **The Kingdom of France** — an example of a state where laws and institutions were so well established that the barons could check the king when necessary

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Law vs. Arms**: They are complementary but arms are primary — good laws cannot exist without good arms, because without force, laws cannot be enforced.
- **Law vs. Force**: The centaur duality (Ch. 18). Law is the human mode; force is the animal mode. The prince needs both.
- **Law vs. Liberty**: States accustomed to liberty and their own laws resist conquest more fiercely than those used to princely rule (005-002).
- **Law vs. Foundations**: Good laws are part of good foundations, but only when backed by good arms. Laws without enforcement are parchment barriers.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

Law occupies a paradoxical position in The Prince: it is indispensable but insufficient. Machiavelli's realism refuses to treat law as a self-executing moral order. Law requires armed force to be effective, and even then, it must be supplemented by the prince's personal virtù and strategic use of force.

## 后世影响

Machiavelli's instrumental view of law — as a tool of state-building rather than an expression of natural justice — influenced the development of legal positivism and the modern concept of sovereignty. His pairing of "good laws and good arms" anticipates the Weberian definition of the state as the monopoly of legitimate violence.
