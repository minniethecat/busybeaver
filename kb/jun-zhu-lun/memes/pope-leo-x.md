# Pope Leo X

Giovanni de' Medici, who reigned as **Pope Leo X** (1513–1521), was the son of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] and a key figure in the political landscape surrounding [[niccolo-machiavelli|Machiavelli]]'s *The Prince*.

## Overview

Pope Leo X appears in *The Prince* primarily through his relationship to the Medici family and the ecclesiastical principality. Machiavelli mentions him in Chapter 11, "Concerning Ecclesiastical Principalities," noting that he was the Cardinal de' Medici before ascending to the papacy (011-074). The Medici pope exemplifies the unique position of ecclesiastical rulers — principalities acquired without either fortune or virtue, sustained by ancient religious institutions.

## Historical Context

Leo X was born Giovanni di Lorenzo de' Medici in 1475, the second son of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo il Magnifico]]. He was made a cardinal at age 13 and became pope in 1513. His pontificate was marked by the continuing Italian Wars, the aftermath of the [[league-of-cambrai|League of Cambrai]], and the growing power of the Medici family in Florence.

As pope, Leo X exercised significant influence over Italian politics. In 1523, he created Giuliano de' Medici as a cardinal (026-038), extending Medici ecclesiastical power.

## Role in The Prince

### The Ecclesiastical Principality

Machiavelli's discussion of ecclesiastical principalities in Chapter 11 frames the papacy as a unique form of rule. Such states are "acquired either by fortune or by virtue, and are maintained without either" — sustained instead by "ancient religious institutions" that are so powerful they support the prince regardless of how he lives.

Machiavelli notes the temporal power of the Church had grown under Pope Alexander VI and was consolidated under Pope Julius II. Leo X inherited this strengthened position:

> Pope Leo X was the Cardinal de' Medici. (011-074)

### The Medici Context

As a Medici, Leo X represented the family that Machiavelli sought to impress with *The Prince*. The work was originally dedicated to [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici]], Leo X's nephew, and the concluding Chapter 26 exhorts the Medici to liberate Italy.

### Dedicatee Connection

Machiavelli's dedication of *The Prince* to the younger Lorenzo de' Medici reflects the Medici family's political ascendance under Leo X's patronage. The pope's ability to grant ecclesiastical positions — such as creating Giuliano de' Medici as cardinal (026-038) — demonstrated the fusion of religious and temporal power that the Medici wielded.

## Machiavelli's Assessment

Machiavelli does not directly assess Leo X's character or rule. Instead, the pope appears as a structural element in the political landscape — the embodiment of ecclesiastical power that frames Machiavelli's argument about Italy's condition. The fact that *The Prince* was addressed to the Medici, with Leo X at the apex of the family's power, shapes the work's strategic orientation.
