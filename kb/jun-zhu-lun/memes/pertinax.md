# Pertinax

Pertinax was a Roman emperor who reigned for only three months in 193 CE before being killed by the Praetorian Guard. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents his fate as a cautionary example: a ruler who is too moderate and tries to reform corrupt institutions will be destroyed by those who benefit from the old order.

## Rise and Fall

Pertinax rose from humble origins through military service to become a respected senator. When Commodus was assassinated, the Senate proclaimed Pertinax emperor. Machiavelli notes:

> "Pertinax was created emperor against the wishes of the soldiers" [019-150].

His attempt to restore discipline and reduce the privileges of the Praetorian Guard — "the honest life to which Pertinax wished to reduce them" [019-152] — provoked his murder after just eighty-seven days in power.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Limits of Moderation

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli groups Pertinax with Marcus Aurelius and Alexander Severus as "good" emperors who nonetheless met tragic ends. The lesson is stark:

> "It would have been useless and dangerous for Pertinax to imitate Severus" [019-283].

Machiavelli's analysis suggests that Pertinax's virtue — his moderation and refusal to be cruel — was precisely what destroyed him. In a corrupt environment, a prince who attempts radical reform without first securing armed support invites his own destruction [019-167].

### Contrast with Severus

The juxtaposition of Pertinax with Septimius Severus is central to Machiavelli's argument. Where Severus satisfied the army and used cunning to defeat his rivals, Pertinax attempted moral reform without the necessary force. Machiavelli's conclusion is pragmatic: a prince must adapt to circumstances, and in a corrupt state, the methods that worked for a philosopher-emperor like Marcus Aurelius will not suffice [019-142].

## Historical Assessment

Pertinax was respected as a capable administrator and an honest man. For Machiavelli, however, his fate illustrates a recurring theme: political success depends not on personal virtue but on the effective management of power relationships, particularly with the military. The "useless and dangerous" quality of his moderation makes him the counter-example to Severus's successful combination of force and fraud.
