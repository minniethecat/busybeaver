# Virtù

Virtù is the central Machiavellian concept denoting the proactive ability, ingenuity, and forceful skill that a prince must possess to acquire and maintain political power. Unlike the Christian virtue of moral goodness, Machiavellian virtù is amoral — it encompasses both admirable qualities (courage, intelligence, decisiveness) and, when necessary, ruthless action.

## Machiavelli's Treatment

### Core Argument

Machiavelli introduces virtù in opposition to **Fortuna** (fortune/luck). While fortune controls roughly half of human affairs, virtù is the human capacity to anticipate, resist, and channel fortune's force (025-009). A prince who relies entirely on fortune will fall when fortune changes; a prince armed with virtù can weather the storm.

The foundational passage appears in Chapter VI, where Machiavelli distinguishes between those who rise to power through their own ability (virtù) and those who rise through fortune:

> Without that opportunity their powers of mind would have been extinguished, and without those powers the opportunity would have come in vain. (006-035)

This passage establishes the dual requirement: virtù alone is not enough without the opportunity (occasione) provided by fortune, but fortune's opportunity is wasted without virtù.

### Historical Examples

Machiavelli illustrates virtù through exemplars who rose from private station to princely power by their own arms and ability:

- **Moses** — found the Israelites enslaved and led them to freedom (006-037)
- **Cyrus** — found the Persians discontented and built an empire (006-042)
- **Romulus** — abandoned at birth, rose to found Rome (006-040)
- **Theseus** — united the scattered Athenians (006-044)

These founders owed nothing to fortune beyond opportunity; everything else was virtù (006-032). Machiavelli contrasts them with figures like **Cesare Borgia**, who acquired his state through the fortune of his father Pope Alexander VI, yet displayed virtù in his subsequent efforts to consolidate power (007-030).

Chapter XXV develops the metaphor further: fortune is a raging river that overflows and destroys everything before it, but virtù is the foresight to build dikes and barriers during calm weather so that the next flood is channeled harmlessly (025-018). The prince who adapts his conduct to the times prospers; the prince who persists in a single mode when circumstances change is ruined (025-065).

### Relation to Other Concepts

Virtù stands at the center of Machiavelli's conceptual web:

| Concept | Relationship to Virtù |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Fortuna** | The adversary — virtù is what resists and channels fortune |
| **Occasione** | The opportunity that fortune provides, which virtù must seize |
| **Necessità** | The constraint that forces virtù into action |
| **Prudence** | The foresight component of virtù — reading the times and adapting |

Virtù is the quality that enables a prince to be both the lion (strong against wolves) and the fox (cunning against traps), as Machiavelli argues in Chapter XVIII.

## Legacy

The concept of virtù fundamentally reshaped Western political thought by divorcing political effectiveness from Christian morality. Later thinkers from Hobbes to Nietzsche grappled with its implications. The term "Machiavellian" itself derives from this revaluation of virtue as amoral political skill.
