# Oliverotto da Fermo

Oliverotto da Fermo (Oliverotto Euffreducci, 1475–1503) is the central example in Chapter 8 of *The Prince* — "Concerning Those Who Have Obtained a Principality by Wickedness." His career illustrates the mechanics and moral limits of criminal principality: a man of military skill who seized power through organized treachery, only to be undone by a more skilled practitioner of the same arts.

## 生平概要

Oliverotto was raised by his maternal uncle, Giovanni Fogliani, after being orphaned as a child. He trained as a soldier under Paolo Vitelli and later his brother Vitellozzo, rising to command through discipline and skill (008-048). Having acquired military reputation, Oliverotto conceived a plan to seize his native city of Fermo. He wrote to his uncle Giovanni, asking to be received honorably into the city with his followers. Giovanni, suspecting nothing, welcomed his nephew with ceremony. Oliverotto then arranged a formal banquet, inviting Giovanni and the leading citizens of Fermo. At the banquet, after the meal, Oliverotto artfully turned the conversation to serious matters (008-070, 008-073). At his signal, armed men emerged and slaughtered Giovanni and all the principal citizens. Oliverotto then mounted his horse, rode through the town, and besieged the magistrates in the palace. Within hours, he was master of Fermo (008-080). His rule lasted barely a year. In 1503, Cesare Borgia lured him to Senigallia, where Oliverotto was seized and strangled alongside Vitellozzo Vitelli and other conspirators (027-118, 027-147, 027-168).

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli devotes Chapter 8 primarily to Oliverotto's case, using it to distinguish between cruelty well-used and cruelty abused.

### 作为范例的角色

- **Criminal principality**: Oliverotto is the paradigmatic case of a private citizen who rises to power through wickedness rather than fortune or virtue. Machiavelli contrasts his method with that of Agathocles of Syracuse — both seized power through violence, but Agathocles used cruelty with prudence while Oliverotto's violence was less artfully employed.
- **The banquet ambush**: The method of Oliverotto's coup — inviting his victims to a banquet and murdering them there — is a classic example of the "conspiracy" model Machiavelli analyzes. Its effectiveness depended on his uncle's trust, which Oliverotto systematically betrayed.
- **Cruelty abused**: Unlike Agathocles, Oliverotto did not consolidate his rule by eliminating all threats at once. His cruelty was episodic rather than systematic, leaving him vulnerable. Machiavelli implies this is why his principality did not endure.
- **Victim of superior virtù**: Oliverotto's death at Senigallia demonstrates the hierarchy of political skill in Machiavelli's world. He was competent enough to seize a city, but Cesare Borgia was competent enough to destroy him. The lesson is that criminal principality can win a state but cannot secure it against a more artful opponent.

## 历史评价

> Oliverotto da Fermo, being left an orphan, was brought up by Giovanni Fogliani, his maternal uncle... After the murder of his uncle and the leading citizens, Oliverotto mounted on horseback and rode through the town, besieging the supreme magistrate in the palace, so that through fear they were forced to obey him and form a government. (008-048, 008-080)

> Oliverotto da Fermo was sent to propose that if the duke wished to meet them, they would come to Sinigaglia. (027-091)

Machiavelli's treatment of Oliverotto is clinically dispassionate. He does not moralize about the murders but analyzes them as a technique of power acquisition. The critical judgment is practical, not ethical: Oliverotto's method succeeded in the short term but failed to establish durable foundations, making him vulnerable to a more capable rival.
