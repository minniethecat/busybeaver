# Vitellozzo Vitelli

Vitellozzo Vitelli (died 31 December 1502) was an Italian *condottiero* and lord of Città di Castello. He is remembered in *The Prince* as one of the principal victims of [[Cesare Borgia]]'s most famous act of strategic deception: the massacre at Sinigaglia. Machiavelli considered this episode significant enough to write a separate account of it before composing *The Prince*.

## 生平概要

Vitellozzo Vitelli was the brother of Pagolo Vitelli, another prominent condottiero. After Pagolo's death, Vitellozzo inherited his brother's military following and became one of the leading mercenary captains in central Italy. The young [[Oliverotto da Fermo]], after being orphaned, "fought under his brother Vitellozzo, and in a very short time, being endowed with wit and a vigorous body and mind, he became the first man in his profession" (007-049).

Vitellozzo was part of the coalition of condottieri captains — including the [[Orsini Family]] — who initially served Cesare Borgia but later conspired against him, fearing the Duke's growing power would consume them all.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Vitellozzo's fate is recounted in [[Chapter 07]], where Machiavelli describes how Cesare Borgia lured the conspiring captains to Sinigaglia under a pretense of reconciliation, then had them seized and strangled. Machiavelli's earlier work, *"Del modo tenuto dal duca Valentino nell' ammazzare Vitellozzo Vitelli, Oliverotto da Fermo, etc."* (1502), provides a detailed account of this event (007-049).

In [[Chapter 08]], Vitellozzo appears again as the commander under whom Oliverotto da Fermo rose to prominence. When Oliverotto seized Fermo through treachery, he "had become formidable to all his neighbours" — until he, like Vitellozzo, "allowed himself to be overreached by Cesare Borgia" (007-140). Machiavelli records that Oliverotto "was strangled, together with Vitellozzo, whom he had made his leader in valour and wickedness" at Sinigaglia.

### 作为范例的角色

Vitellozzo Vitelli serves as a **cautionary figure** in two respects:

First, he exemplifies the vulnerability of mercenary captains who mistake their own military prowess for political security. Despite being "the first man in his profession," he was outmaneuvered by a prince who understood that force must be directed by statecraft.

Second, his death at Sinigaglia is the climactic demonstration of Cesare Borgia's method: patiently tolerating disloyalty, gathering the disloyal in one place, and eliminating them all at once. The episode is Machiavelli's prime illustration of cruelty "well used" — decisive, swift, and strategically complete.

> "He was strangled, together with Vitellozzo, whom he had made his leader in valour and wickedness." (007-140)

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's treatment of Vitellozzo is notably impersonal — he is presented as a military instrument rather than a political actor. Unlike [[Agathocles of Syracuse]] or Oliverotto, Vitellozzo is not analyzed for his own ambitions or methods. He appears only in relation to Borgia, as a problem the Duke successfully solved.

This narrative choice reflects Machiavelli's analytical priorities: the condottieri captains matter not as individual agents but as a class of obstacle that a new prince must learn to overcome.
