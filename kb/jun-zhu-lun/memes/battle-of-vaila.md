# Battle of Vaila

The **Battle of Vaila** (also known as **Agnadello**), fought on 14 May 1509, was the crushing defeat of the Venetian army by the forces of the League of Cambrai — a moment Machiavelli invokes as the archetypal demonstration of how fortune can destroy in hours what centuries have built.

## Background

Venice had spent the 15th century expanding from a maritime republic into a territorial power on the Italian mainland, acquiring Brescia, Bergamo, Cremona, and parts of the Romagna. By 1500, the Republic of Venice was arguably the wealthiest and most stable Italian state — but its expansion had accumulated powerful enemies.

> [P01-161] events arising out of the League of Cambrai, made in 1508 between the…

The **League of Cambrai** (1508) united former rivals against Venice: Pope Julius II, Louis XII of France, Emperor Maximilian, and Ferdinand of Aragon all agreed to partition Venice's mainland possessions. The alliance was unprecedented — the major European powers setting aside their rivalries to dismember an Italian state they all feared.

## The Battle

On 14 May 1509, the Venetian army under Bartolomeo d'Alviano — against orders to avoid engagement — confronted the French vanguard near Agnadello. The French army, commanded by Louis XII in person and led by the formidable Gian Giacomo Trivulzio, routed the Venetians.

> [P01-164] battle of Vaila, when Venice lost in one day …

The consequence was catastrophic: "Venice lost in one day all that she had accumulated over eight hundred years." Venice's mainland empire — the *Terraferma* — collapsed within weeks. Cities that had been Venetian for generations opened their gates to French and Imperial forces without resistance.

Machiavelli's formulation — "in one day" — is both literal and symbolic. The battle lasted a single day; its consequences, a single season. Yet Venice itself survived. The republic's maritime core, protected by its lagoon and fleet, proved impregnable — and the League of Cambrai, having achieved its immediate objective, promptly collapsed as its members turned on each other.

## Role in The Prince

### Fortune's Caprice

> [025-033] invasion would not have made the great…

Machiavelli classifies Venice's defeat as a case study in fortune — specifically, the kind of overwhelming fortune that no amount of virtù could have resisted. The League of Cambrai was, in Machiavelli's terms, an "extraordinary and extreme" manifestation of fortune against which human preparation was useless.

Yet Venice's survival — the republic's ability to absorb catastrophic territorial loss and persist — also serves as evidence of institutional resilience. The Venetian constitution, which Machiavelli admired, proved more durable than Venetian territory.

### The Danger of Dependence

Venice's defeat at Vaila also illustrates Machiavelli's argument about military dependence. The republic had relied heavily on mercenary *condottieri* to defend its mainland territories — the very system Machiavelli condemns in Chapters 12–13. When the mercenaries faced the professional French army at Agnadello, the structural weakness of dependent arms was exposed.

> [P01-110] affairs in Italy, committed the five capital errors…

The broader context — Louis XII's errors in Italy — is the analytical framework through which Machiavelli views the League of Cambrai. Louis's decision to join the League appeared to bring short-term advantage (Venetian territory) but ultimately empowered his rivals (the Pope, Spain) and contributed to his own expulsion from Italy. The battle is thus a two-sided lesson: Venice learned the cost of military dependence; France learned the cost of strengthening rivals.

## Historical Significance

Vaila marked the effective end of Venice as a major territorial power in Italy. The republic recovered some of its mainland possessions after the League's collapse, but never its former extent or ambition. The battle also demonstrated a structural reality of Renaissance warfare: the new French artillery and professional infantry could overwhelm the mercenary armies on which Italian states had relied for generations.

For Machiavelli, Vaila was the empirical confirmation of his military thesis. A state that does not possess its own arms — whether Venice with its condottieri or Florence with its French alliance — lives at the mercy of fortune. And fortune, as Vaila demonstrated, can be devastatingly swift.
