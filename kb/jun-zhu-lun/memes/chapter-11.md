## CHAPTER XI. CONCERNING ECCLESIASTICAL PRINCIPALITIES

[011-001]It only remains now to speak of ecclesiastical principalities, touching

[011-002]which all difficulties are prior to getting possession, because they are

[011-003]acquired either by capacity or good fortune, and they can be held without

[011-004]either; for they are sustained by the ancient ordinances of religion,

[011-005]which are so all-powerful, and of such a character that the principalities

[011-006]may be held no matter how their princes behave and live. These princes

[011-007]alone have states and do not defend them; and they have subjects and do

[011-008]not rule them; and the states, although unguarded, are not taken from

[011-009]them, and the subjects, although not ruled, do not care, and they have

[011-010]neither the desire nor the ability to alienate themselves. Such

[011-011]principalities only are secure and happy. But being upheld by powers, to

[011-012]which the human mind cannot reach, I shall speak no more of them, because,

[011-013]being exalted and maintained by God, it would be the act of a presumptuous

[011-014]and rash man to discuss them.

[011-015]Nevertheless, if any one should ask of me how comes it that the Church has

[011-016]attained such greatness in temporal power, seeing that from Alexander

[011-017]backwards the Italian potentates (not only those who have been called

[011-018]potentates, but every baron and lord, though the smallest) have valued the

[011-019]temporal power very slightly—yet now a king of France trembles

[011-020]before it, and it has been able to drive him from Italy, and to ruin the

[011-021]Venetians—although this may be very manifest, it does not appear to

[011-022]me superfluous to recall it in some measure to memory.

[011-023]Before Charles, King of France, passed into Italy,[1]

[011-024]this country was under the dominion of the Pope, the Venetians, the King of

[011-025]Naples, the Duke of Milan, and the Florentines. These potentates had two

[011-026]principal anxieties: the one, that no foreigner should enter Italy under arms;

[011-027]the other, that none of themselves should seize more territory. Those about

[011-028]whom there was the most anxiety were the Pope and the Venetians. To restrain

[011-029]the Venetians the union of all the others was necessary, as it was for the

[011-030]defence of Ferrara; and to keep down the Pope they made use of the barons of

[011-031]Rome, who, being divided into two factions, Orsini and Colonnesi, had always a

[011-032]pretext for disorder, and, standing with arms in their hands under the eyes of

[011-033]the Pontiff, kept the pontificate weak and powerless. And although there might

[011-034]arise sometimes a courageous pope, such as Sixtus, yet neither fortune nor

[011-035]wisdom could rid him of these annoyances. And the short life of a pope is also

[011-036]a cause of weakness; for in the ten years, which is the average life of a pope,

[011-037]he can with difficulty lower one of the factions; and if, so to speak, one

[011-038]people should almost destroy the Colonnesi, another would arise hostile to the

[011-039]Orsini, who would support their opponents, and yet would not have time to ruin

[011-040]the Orsini. This was the reason why the temporal powers of the pope were little

[011-041]esteemed in Italy.

[011-042][1]

[011-043]Charles VIII invaded Italy in 1494.

[011-044]Alexander the Sixth arose afterwards, who of all the pontiffs that have

[011-045]ever been showed how a pope with both money and arms was able to prevail;

[011-046]and through the instrumentality of the Duke Valentino, and by reason of

[011-047]the entry of the French, he brought about all those things which I have

[011-048]discussed above in the actions of the duke. And although his intention was

[011-049]not to aggrandize the Church, but the duke, nevertheless, what he did

[011-050]contributed to the greatness of the Church, which, after his death and the

[011-051]ruin of the duke, became the heir to all his labours.

[011-052]Pope Julius came afterwards and found the Church strong, possessing all the

[011-053]Romagna, the barons of Rome reduced to impotence, and, through the

[011-054]chastisements of Alexander, the factions wiped out; he also found the way open

[011-055]to accumulate money in a manner such as had never been practised before

[011-056]Alexander’s time. Such things Julius not only followed, but improved

[011-057]upon, and he intended to gain Bologna, to ruin the Venetians, and to drive the

[011-058]French out of Italy. All of these enterprises prospered with him, and so much

[011-059]the more to his credit, inasmuch as he did everything to strengthen the Church

[011-060]and not any private person. He kept also the Orsini and Colonnesi factions

[011-061]within the bounds in which he found them; and although there was among them

[011-062]some mind to make disturbance, nevertheless he held two things firm: the one,

[011-063]the greatness of the Church, with which he terrified them; and the other, not

[011-064]allowing them to have their own cardinals, who caused the disorders among them.

[011-065]For whenever these factions have their cardinals they do not remain quiet for

[011-066]long, because cardinals foster the factions in Rome and out of it, and the

[011-067]barons are compelled to support them, and thus from the ambitions of prelates

[011-068]arise disorders and tumults among the barons. For these reasons his Holiness

[011-069]Pope Leo[2]

[011-070]found the pontificate most powerful, and it is to be hoped that, if others made

[011-071]it great in arms, he will make it still greater and more venerated by his

[011-072]goodness and infinite other virtues.

[011-073][2]

[011-074]Pope Leo X was the Cardinal de’ Medici.
