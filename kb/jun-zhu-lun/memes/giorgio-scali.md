# Giorgio Scali

**Messer Giorgio Scali** was a 14th-century Florentine citizen whose rise to power through popular favor is cited by Machiavelli in Chapter 9 of *The Prince* as an example of a civil principality acquired with the people's support.

## Overview

Scali appears in Chapter 9, "Concerning a Civil Principality," where Machiavelli discusses how a private citizen becomes prince through the favor of his fellow citizens — either the people or the nobles. Scali is compared to the Gracchi in Rome as an example of one who rose through popular support rather than aristocratic backing:

> ...to the Gracchi in Rome and to Messer Giorgio Scali (009-075)

Machiavelli references Scali again later in the chapter, noting that his story is told in the author's own *Florentine Histories*:

> Messer Giorgio Scali. This event is to be found in Machiavelli's (009-085)

## Historical Context

Giorgio Scali was a prominent figure in Florence during the Ciompi revolt of 1378, when the lower guilds and wool workers seized control of the city government. Scali aligned himself with the popular faction, using the revolt to advance his own political standing. Along with Tommaso Strozzi and others, he became one of the leaders of the *popolo minuto* (the "little people") government.

His career exemplified the pattern Machiavelli describes in Chapter 9: a private citizen who reaches power not through fortune or force, but through the favor of his fellow citizens. Scali rose as a champion of the people against the nobility — a classic civil principality ascent.

However, his power proved unstable. Like many popular leaders, his position depended on maintaining the people's goodwill. When the political winds shifted, he was executed in 1382 after the restoration of the oligarchic government.

## Role in The Prince

### Civil Principality Exemplar

In Chapter 9, Machiavelli distinguishes between two paths to civil principality: through the favor of the people or through the favor of the nobles. The one who comes to power through the people "maintains himself more easily" than the one elevated by the nobles, because the people ask only not to be oppressed, while the nobles wish to oppress.

Scali, alongside the Gracchi, serves as a historical example of the popular path. Machiavelli's brief reference assumes his reader's familiarity with the Florentine historical figure, which is why he directs readers to his own *Florentine Histories* for the full account.

## Machiavelli's Assessment

By grouping Scali with the Gracchi, Machiavelli places him in the tradition of leaders who challenged the established order through popular mobilization. The comparison is not necessarily flattering: both the Gracchi and Scali met violent ends, illustrating the precariousness of power built solely on popular favor without military foundation — a theme central to *The Prince*.

The briefness of Machiavelli's treatment here — two passing references — reflects the fact that the *Florentine Histories* had already covered Scali's story in detail. For the purposes of *The Prince*, Scali is simply a recognizable name that illustrates a theoretical point about civil principalities.
