# Alexander the Great

Alexander the Great was the king of Macedon who conquered the Persian Empire and created one of the largest empires of the ancient world by the age of thirty. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents Alexander as a model of military virtue, strategic imitation, and the proper use of generosity — a figure whose life every prince should study.

## Conquests and Legacy

Alexander succeeded his father Philip II at the age of twenty and within a decade had conquered an empire stretching from Greece to India. His military genius, personal courage, and ability to inspire loyalty made him the archetype of the conquering prince in Western political thought.

## Role in *The Prince*

### Exemplar of Military Virtue

In Chapter 14, "That Which Concerns a Prince on the Subject of the Art of War," Machiavelli recommends that a prince study the campaigns of great commanders. He cites Alexander alongside Cyrus, Scipio, and Caesar as generals whose methods "a wise prince ought to observe" [014-062]. Alexander's imitation of Achilles — carrying the *Iliad* on his campaigns — illustrates the principle that a prince should consciously model himself on historical exemplars [014-063].

### The Generous Prince

In Chapter 16, "Concerning Liberality and Meanness," Machiavelli presents Alexander as an example of strategic generosity:

> "Cyrus, Caesar, and Alexander were ready givers" [016-054].

But Machiavelli's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: Alexander's liberality was made possible only by the wealth of conquered territories. A prince who gives away his own resources, rather than the spoils of conquest, will soon impoverish himself and lose the respect of his subjects.

### The Problem of Succession

Implicit in Machiavelli's references to Alexander is the question of succession. Alexander's empire fragmented immediately after his death because he failed to establish stable institutions. For Machiavelli, this is the ultimate test of a founder: whether what he builds survives him. Alexander, for all his genius, failed this test.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli treats Alexander as a model to be studied rather than simply admired. His conquests demonstrated what personal *virtù* could achieve; his failure to secure succession demonstrated its limits. For the Renaissance prince, Alexander was both inspiration and warning.
