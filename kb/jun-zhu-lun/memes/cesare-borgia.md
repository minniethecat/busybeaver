# Cesare Borgia

Cesare Borgia (1475–1507), known as Duke Valentino (Il Valentino), is the most extensively analyzed figure in *The Prince*. Machiavelli presents him as the paradigmatic case of a prince who acquires his state through the fortune of his father Pope Alexander VI, yet demonstrates virtù in his efforts to consolidate and maintain power.

## Life and Rise

Cesare was the illegitimate son of Rodrigo Borgia (later Pope Alexander VI). When his father became pope in 1492, Cesare was made a cardinal, but he renounced the clergy to become a secular prince. Through his father's diplomatic maneuvering and the military support of French King Louis XII, Cesare acquired the Romagna region as his duchy.

## Role in The Prince

### The Exemplar of Virtù-Through-Fortune

Machiavelli devotes the entirety of Chapter VII to Cesare's career. He acknowledges that Cesare "acquired his state during the ascendancy of his father, and lost it on its decline" — a classic case of fortune-made prince (007-031). Yet Machiavelli insists that Cesare did everything a prudent prince should do to secure his position.

### Consolidation of Power

Cesare's key actions demonstrate Machiavellian virtù in practice. He eliminated the rival Orsini faction through the famous trap at Sinigaglia, where he had Vitellozzo Vitelli, Oliverotto da Fermo, and others strangled. He appointed the ruthless Remirro de Orco to pacify the Romagna, then had him executed and displayed in the public square to deflect popular hatred from himself (007-077). He began building his own military forces rather than relying on auxiliaries.

### The One Failure

Machiavelli's only criticism of Cesare is that he failed to prevent the election of Pope Julius II, a longtime Borgia enemy. Having been gravely ill at the time of his father's death, Cesare could not control the papal conclave — but Machiavelli considers this a failure of foresight, not fortune. "The only course he ought to have pursued was to have prevented any one of the cardinals he had injured from becoming pope" (007-096).

## Machiavelli's Judgment

Despite Cesare's ultimate downfall, Machiavelli concludes: "having summed up all that the duke did, I cannot possibly blame him; on the contrary, it seems to me that I ought to offer him for imitation to all those who by fortune or by the arms of others are raised to government" (007-105). Cesare is the imperfect but instructive model of the new prince.
