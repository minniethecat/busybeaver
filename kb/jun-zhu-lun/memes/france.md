# France

The **Kingdom of France**, under **Louis XII** (r. 1498–1515), was the premier military power intervening in Italian affairs during Machiavelli's diplomatic career. In Chapter 3 of *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses Louis XII's Italian campaign as a comprehensive case study of how a prince can lose what he has gained through systematic political errors.

## Political and Military Status

France in the early 16th century was the most centralized and militarily capable monarchy in Europe. The Valois kings had consolidated feudal territories, built a professional army, and accumulated sufficient resources to project power across the Alps into Italy — a capability that no Italian state could match individually.

> [P01-108] In 1500 he was sent to France to obtain terms from Louis XI…

Machiavelli visited the French court twice (1500, 1504) as a Florentine envoy. His direct observations of French power — and French arrogance — inform the sharply critical analysis of Chapter 3.

## In The Prince: Louis XII's Five Errors

Chapter 3 contains Machiavelli's most sustained analysis of a single ruler's strategic failures. Louis XII, having occupied Milan in 1499, committed five errors that cost him all his Italian possessions:

| Error | Description | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1. Destroyed the lesser powers | Crushed minor Italian states that could have balanced Venice | Left no buffer against stronger rivals |
| 2. Increased the power of the Pope | Strengthened Alexander VI's temporal authority | Created a rival he could not control |
| 3. Brought Spain into Italy | Invited Ferdinand of Aragon as an ally | Spain became the dominant power |
| 4. Did not reside in Italy | Governed through deputies rather than personal presence | Lost direct control over Milan |
| 5. Did not establish colonies | Failed to plant French settlements in conquered territories | No durable presence |

> [003-018] …ns Louis the Twelfth, King of France, quickly occupied

The rapidity of Louis's initial conquest concealed its fragility. Milan was taken quickly; it was lost equally quickly:

> [003-036] …rtheless Milan was taken from France both the first and the second

France lost Milan twice — first to Ludovico Sforza's return, then to the Swiss-backed Holy League. Each loss stemmed from the structural errors Machiavelli identified, not from battlefield defeat alone.

### The Logic of the Errors

> [003-027] cause France to lose Milan the first time …

Machiavelli's diagnosis is systematic: Louis's errors were not random misjudgments but the predictable consequence of a deeper failure — the failure to understand that acquiring new territory requires fundamentally different strategies than governing old territory. A hereditary king can afford to be guided by habit; a conqueror cannot.

> [003-040] …uisition than did the King of France.

The King of France, secure in his hereditary realm, approached Italy with the mindset of a hereditary prince — seeking short-term advantage (tribute, prestige, papal favor) rather than building durable structures of control. This category error explains all five mistakes.

### The General Rule

> [003-049] which have been bound to France for so long a time: and, alth…

From Louis's failure, Machiavelli extracts a general principle: "He who is the cause of another's becoming powerful is ruined." By empowering the Pope and inviting Spain into Italy, Louis created the forces that would expel him. The rule applies universally: a prince must never strengthen a rival, even temporarily, when that rival could eventually become a threat.

> [001-005] …entirely new, as was Milan to Francesco Sforza, or they

France's acquisition of Milan illustrates the category of "mixed principality" — territory annexed to an existing state but culturally distinct. The structural challenges of mixed principalities, analyzed at length in Chapter 3, are exemplified by the French experience in Lombardy.

## Strategic Implications

France's Italian failures had consequences beyond Louis XII's reign. The French invasion of 1494 had shattered the Italian balance of power; France's subsequent failures left a vacuum that Spain filled. By the time Machiavelli wrote *The Prince* (1513), Spanish dominance in Italy was essentially complete — and France, under Francis I, would attempt and fail to reverse it.

> [P01-145] …ch in 1507 were controlled by France, Spain, and Germany, with

The tripartite division of Italian influence among France, Spain, and the Empire was the geopolitical reality Machiavelli confronted. His urgent call for a native Italian prince to unite the peninsula (Chapter 26) was a response to the demonstrated inability of any Italian state — or any single foreign power — to establish stable hegemony.
