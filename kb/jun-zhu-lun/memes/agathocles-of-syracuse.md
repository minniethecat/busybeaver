# Agathocles of Syracuse

**Agathocles** (361–289 BC), son of a potter, rose to become king of Syracuse — and in Machiavelli's *The Prince*, he stands as the definitive example of the prince who acquires power through crime rather than fortune or virtue.

## Life and Rise to Power

Agathocles was born in poverty in Sicily (008-032). Through military talent and relentless ambition, he rose through the ranks of the Syracusan army. His defining act of cruelty occurred after he had gathered the Senate and the wealthiest citizens of Syracuse for what they believed was a political consultation:

> [008-011] Agathocles, the Sicilian,[1]

At a prearranged signal, his soldiers slaughtered the entire assembly. With Syracuse's ruling class eliminated in a single stroke, Agathocles seized absolute power without opposition. His subsequent military campaigns against Carthage demonstrated genuine strategic ability:

> [008-029] …mpelled to come to terms with Agathocles, and, leaving Sicily to him, …

The Carthaginians, unable to defeat him, negotiated a settlement that left Sicily in his control. His rule lasted until his death in 289 BC — a reign sustained not by fortune or inheritance but by sheer capacity for calculated violence.

## Role in The Prince

### The Criminal Prince Paradigm

Machiavelli devotes the second half of Chapter 8 to Agathocles as the primary example of acquiring princedom "by wickedness" (*per sceleratezza*). The analysis is subtle: Agathocles is neither simply condemned nor simply praised.

> [008-041] the courage of Agathocles in entering into and extricat…

Machiavelli acknowledges his courage (*animo*) — the ability to enter danger and extricate himself from it. This is the language of virtù, applied to a man whose methods were undeniably criminal. The tension between Agathocles's virtù and his wickedness is the analytical heart of Chapter 8.

### The Distinction: Cruelty Well-Used vs. Abused

Machiavelli's crucial distinction — cruelty "well-used" versus cruelty "abused" — is formulated through the Agathocles case:

| Dimension | Well-Used Cruelty | Abused Cruelty |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| Timing | Done at once, for necessity | Repeated, escalating over time |
| Purpose | Self-preservation, then converted to subjects' benefit | Personal gratification |
| Outcome | Subjects eventually accept rule | Subjects remain hostile forever |

> [008-088] been as difficult as that of Agathocles if he had not allowed himself…

Agathocles belongs in an intermediate category. His initial crime — the Senate massacre — was "well-used" by Machiavelli's criteria: done once, out of necessity, and not repeated. But the sheer scale of the atrocity places him beyond simple admiration.

### The Puzzle of Enduring Rule

> [008-093] …wonder how it can happen that Agathocles, and his like, after infinite

Machiavelli poses an explicit puzzle: how can a man who has committed "infinite treacheries, cruelties, and impieties" live securely in his city for years, defend against enemies, and never face conspiracy from his citizens?

> [008-105] …in some degree their rule, as Agathocles did. It is impossible

The answer lies in the *pattern* of cruelty. Those who inflict injuries all at once, then transition to beneficence, can reconcile their subjects. Those who continue injuring cannot. Agathocles, despite the magnitude of his initial crime, understood this calculus and governed thereafter with sufficient restraint to maintain power.

## Comparison with Other Exemplars

### Agathocles and [[Cesare Borgia]]

Both men exemplify cruelty well-used, but through different methods. Borgia's cruelty was delegated (Remirro de Orco) and geographically targeted (Romagna); Agathocles's was personal and concentrated on a single day. Borgia's reliance on fortune (his father Pope Alexander VI) ultimately undid him; Agathocles owed nothing to fortune and everything to his own *animo*.

### Agathocles and [[Oliverotto da Fermo]]

Oliverotto's rise — the murder of his uncle and the leading citizens of Fermo at a banquet — directly echoes Agathocles's method. Machiavelli treats Oliverotto as the Renaissance reincarnation of the Agathoclean pattern, though Oliverotto's downfall at Sinigaglia demonstrates that the same method, imperfectly executed, can fail.

## Historical Evaluation

Machiavelli's treatment of Agathocles reflects a broader historiographical tension. Ancient sources — Diodorus Siculus, Polybius — portrayed Agathocles alternately as tyrant and capable ruler. Machiavelli's innovation is to make this ambivalence intellectually productive: Agathocles proves that political success and moral wickedness are independent variables, not opposites. A man can be both a criminal and an effective prince.

This claim scandalized Machiavelli's contemporaries and has remained central to interpretations of *The Prince* ever since. Whether one sees Agathocles as proof of Machiavelli's cynicism or his realism depends on whether one believes the political world *should* be judged by the moral standards Machiavelli shows it does not follow.
