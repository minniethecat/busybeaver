# Francesco Sforza

Francesco Sforza (1401–1466) was the greatest of the Italian condottieri (mercenary captains) who became Duke of Milan. Machiavelli presents him as the textbook example of a prince who acquired his state "by proper means and with great ability" (007-031).

## Life

Born the illegitimate son of the condottiero Muzio Attendolo Sforza, Francesco inherited his father's mercenary company and became the most sought-after captain in Italy. He fought for and against Milan, Venice, and Florence. In 1450, after the death of the last Visconti duke, he seized Milan for himself.

## Role in The Prince

Machiavelli uses Sforza as the counterpoint to Cesare Borgia. Sforza "from being a private person rose to be Duke of Milan, and that which he had acquired with a thousand anxieties he kept with little trouble" (007-032). He exemplifies the prince who rises through his own arms and virtù — the highest category in Machiavelli's typology.

## Significance

In Chapter I, Machiavelli names Milan under Sforza as the paradigmatic case of an "entirely new" principality (001-005). The Sforza dynasty illustrates both the power of virtù (Francesco's rise) and the vulnerability of hereditary succession (his descendants' decline). The French under Louis XII repeatedly took and lost Milan, demonstrating the difficulty of holding a state where the old ruling family's memory persists (003-018).
