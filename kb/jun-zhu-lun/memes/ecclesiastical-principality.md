# Ecclesiastical Principality

The ecclesiastical principality is the final category in Machiavelli's typology of states. Unlike hereditary, new, or mixed principalities, ecclesiastical principalities are "acquired by fortune or ability, but maintained without either" — a paradox that Machiavelli explains through the unique power of religion.

## Unique Character

Machiavelli opens his discussion by stating that "it only remains now to speak of ecclesiastical principalities" (011-001), treating them last because they defy his usual categories. "These alone are secure and happy," he writes, because "they are sustained by the ancient institutions of religion, which are so powerful and of such quality that they keep their princes in power however they act and live" (011-001).

## Why They Endure

The ecclesiastical prince rules without needing to defend his state. "These princes alone have states and do not defend them; and they have subjects and do not govern them." The subjects, "though not governed, do not care, and they neither can nor do they think of alienating themselves from such princes" (011-002). The religious foundations of the state make rebellion unthinkable.

## In Machiavelli's Analysis

Machiavelli's treatment of ecclesiastical principalities is notably brief — barely a full chapter — because, by his own admission, they operate outside the logic of virtù and fortuna. A secular prince must fight, scheme, and build; a pope inherits a system so deeply embedded that it maintains itself. This makes the category theoretically empty for Machiavelli's purposes (he is interested in what princes *do*, not what they inherit) but politically crucial, since the Papal States were the dominant power in central Italy during his lifetime.
