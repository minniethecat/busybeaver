# Giampaolo Baglioni

**Giampaolo Baglioni** (also Gianpagolo or Giovanpagolo Baglioni, c. 1470–1520) was the tyrant of Perugia and a member of the powerful Baglioni family who ruled the city. He appears in *The Prince* as a participant in the conspiracy against Cesare Borgia at Magione.

## Overview

Baglioni is mentioned in two contexts in *The Prince*. In Chapter 7, the Baglioni family appears among the Roman baronial families who could not harm Cesare Borgia even when he was "but half alive" (007-179). In the appendix on Duke Valentino (Chapter 27), Giampaolo Baglioni is explicitly named among the conspirators who gathered at Magione to plot against Borgia's expanding power (027-014).

## Historical Context

The Baglioni family dominated Perugia during the Renaissance, ruling the city through a combination of military force and political manipulation. Giampaolo succeeded his father Rodolfo as the leading figure of the family. Like many Italian lords of the period, he was both a tyrant (in Machiavellian terms) and a *condottiero* — a mercenary captain who sold his military services to larger powers.

Perugia was one of the papal cities, and the Baglioni's relationship with the papacy was complex: sometimes allies, sometimes rebels. Their power derived from control of the city's fortifications and the loyalty of their faction.

## Role in The Prince

### Against Cesare Borgia

Baglioni's most significant appearance comes in the Magione conspiracy of 1502. After Cesare Borgia seized Bologna, the smaller lords of central Italy feared he would destroy them all. They held a meeting at Magione, near Perugia:

> Upon this a meeting was called at Magione in the district of Perugia, to which came the cardinal, Pagolo, and the Duke di Gravina Orsini, Vitellozzo Vitelli, Oliverotto da Fermo, Gianpagolo Baglioni, the tyrant of Perugia, and Messer Antonio da Venafro, sent by Pandolfo Petrucci, the Prince of Siena. (027-014)

The conspirators discussed Borgia's power and ambition, deciding "not to abandon the Bentivogli, but to strive to win over the Florentines" (027-018). This conspiracy ultimately failed — Borgia lured the conspirators to Senigallia, where he had [[vitellozzo-vitelli|Vitellozzo Vitelli]] and [[oliverotto-da-fermo|Oliverotto da Fermo]] strangled.

### The Baglioni and Cesare Borgia

Machiavelli mentions the Baglioni in Chapter 7 to illustrate the strength of Borgia's foundations. Even when Borgia was gravely ill in Rome — "but half alive" — the Baglioni, Vitelli, and Orsini could not harm him (007-179). This testimony to Borgia's power is significant: the Baglioni were among the most warlike families in central Italy, yet they could not move against the weakened duke.

## Machiavelli's Assessment

Machiavelli does not offer an explicit judgment of Baglioni's character. Implicitly, Baglioni represents the type of Italian lord whose power was local and precarious — strong enough to tyrannize a single city, but not strong enough to resist a determined conqueror like Cesare Borgia. His participation in the Magione conspiracy illustrates the reactive, defensive posture of Italy's lesser princes, who could only band together in desperation when threatened.
