# Catherina Sforza

Catherina Sforza (1463–1509) was the Countess of Forlì and a member of the powerful [[Sforza Family]]. She is significant in *The Prince* as the subject of Machiavelli's first diplomatic mission in 1499, and from her fate he extracted a lasting political lesson about the limits of military defense.

## 生平概要

Catherina was the illegitimate daughter of Galeazzo Maria Sforza, Duke of Milan. Through marriage she became the ruler of Forlì and Imola, territories in the Romagna. She was known for her boldness — famously, when her children were taken hostage, she reportedly lifted her skirts and declared she could make more.

Her career as a ruler ended when [[Cesare Borgia]], in his campaign to consolidate the Romagna, besieged and captured Forlì in 1499–1500. Catherina was taken prisoner to Rome.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli's first diplomatic mission was "in 1499 to Catherina Sforza, 'my lady of Forli' of *The Prince*, from whose conduct and fate he drew the moral that it is far better to earn the confidence of the people than to rely on fortresses" (P01-103).

Later, the translator notes that Machiavelli's diplomatic missions were often unsuccessful: he was "misled by Catherina Sforza, ignored by [[Louis XII of France|Louis XII]], overawed by [[Cesare Borgia]]" (P01-276). The personal humiliation of being outmaneuvered by a countess he had been sent to negotiate with may have sharpened his analysis.

### 作为范例的角色

Catherina Sforza is the **exemplar of fortress-dependence** — the ruler who trusts in walls rather than popular support. When Cesare Borgia attacked, her fortresses, however strong, could not save her because the people were not with her. Machiavelli's moral — "it is far better to earn the confidence of the people than to rely on fortresses" (P01-103) — is one of the most consistently repeated teachings in *The Prince*.

## 历史评价

Catherina's portrayal in *The Prince* is notable for what it omits. Machiavelli does not dwell on her personal courage or remarkable life story. He mentions her only to extract the strategic lesson. This selective treatment is characteristic of his method: persons matter for the principles their careers demonstrate, not for their biographies as such.
