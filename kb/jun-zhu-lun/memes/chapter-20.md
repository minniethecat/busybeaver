## CHAPTER XX. ARE FORTRESSES, AND MANY OTHER THINGS TO WHICH PRINCES OFTEN RESORT,
ADVANTAGEOUS OR HURTFUL?

[020-001]1. Some princes, so as to hold securely the state, have disarmed their

[020-002]subjects; others have kept their subject towns distracted by factions;

[020-003]others have fostered enmities against themselves; others have laid

[020-004]themselves out to gain over those whom they distrusted in the beginning of

[020-005]their governments; some have built fortresses; some have overthrown and

[020-006]destroyed them. And although one cannot give a final judgment on all of

[020-007]these things unless one possesses the particulars of those states in which

[020-008]a decision has to be made, nevertheless I will speak as comprehensively as

[020-009]the matter of itself will admit.

[020-010]2. There never was a new prince who has disarmed his subjects; rather when

[020-011]he has found them disarmed he has always armed them, because, by arming

[020-012]them, those arms become yours, those men who were distrusted become

[020-013]faithful, and those who were faithful are kept so, and your subjects

[020-014]become your adherents. And whereas all subjects cannot be armed, yet when

[020-015]those whom you do arm are benefited, the others can be handled more

[020-016]freely, and this difference in their treatment, which they quite

[020-017]understand, makes the former your dependents, and the latter, considering

[020-018]it to be necessary that those who have the most danger and service should

[020-019]have the most reward, excuse you. But when you disarm them, you at once

[020-020]offend them by showing that you distrust them, either for cowardice or for

[020-021]want of loyalty, and either of these opinions breeds hatred against you.

[020-022]And because you cannot remain unarmed, it follows that you turn to

[020-023]mercenaries, which are of the character already shown; even if they should

[020-024]be good they would not be sufficient to defend you against powerful

[020-025]enemies and distrusted subjects. Therefore, as I have said, a new prince

[020-026]in a new principality has always distributed arms. Histories are full of

[020-027]examples. But when a prince acquires a new state, which he adds as a

[020-028]province to his old one, then it is necessary to disarm the men of that

[020-029]state, except those who have been his adherents in acquiring it; and these

[020-030]again, with time and opportunity, should be rendered soft and effeminate;

[020-031]and matters should be managed in such a way that all the armed men in the

[020-032]state shall be your own soldiers who in your old state were living near

[020-033]you.

[020-034]3. Our forefathers, and those who were reckoned wise, were accustomed to

[020-035]say that it was necessary to hold Pistoia by factions and Pisa by

[020-036]fortresses; and with this idea they fostered quarrels in some of their

[020-037]tributary towns so as to keep possession of them the more easily. This may

[020-038]have been well enough in those times when Italy was in a way balanced, but

[020-039]I do not believe that it can be accepted as a precept for to-day, because

[020-040]I do not believe that factions can ever be of use; rather it is certain

[020-041]that when the enemy comes upon you in divided cities you are quickly lost,

[020-042]because the weakest party will always assist the outside forces and the

[020-043]other will not be able to resist. The Venetians, moved, as I believe, by

[020-044]the above reasons, fostered the Guelph and Ghibelline factions in their

[020-045]tributary cities; and although they never allowed them to come to

[020-046]bloodshed, yet they nursed these disputes amongst them, so that the

[020-047]citizens, distracted by their differences, should not unite against them.

[020-048]Which, as we saw, did not afterwards turn out as expected, because, after

[020-049]the rout at Vaila, one party at once took courage and seized the state.

[020-050]Such methods argue, therefore, weakness in the prince, because these

[020-051]factions will never be permitted in a vigorous principality; such methods

[020-052]for enabling one the more easily to manage subjects are only useful in

[020-053]times of peace, but if war comes this policy proves fallacious.

[020-054]4. Without doubt princes become great when they overcome the difficulties

[020-055]and obstacles by which they are confronted, and therefore fortune,

[020-056]especially when she desires to make a new prince great, who has a greater

[020-057]necessity to earn renown than an hereditary one, causes enemies to arise

[020-058]and form designs against him, in order that he may have the opportunity of

[020-059]overcoming them, and by them to mount higher, as by a ladder which his

[020-060]enemies have raised. For this reason many consider that a wise prince,

[020-061]when he has the opportunity, ought with craft to foster some animosity

[020-062]against himself, so that, having crushed it, his renown may rise higher.

[020-063]5. Princes, especially new ones, have found more fidelity and assistance

[020-064]in those men who in the beginning of their rule were distrusted than among

[020-065]those who in the beginning were trusted. Pandolfo Petrucci, Prince of

[020-066]Siena, ruled his state more by those who had been distrusted than by

[020-067]others. But on this question one cannot speak generally, for it varies so

[020-068]much with the individual; I will only say this, that those men who at the

[020-069]commencement of a princedom have been hostile, if they are of a

[020-070]description to need assistance to support themselves, can always be gained

[020-071]over with the greatest ease, and they will be tightly held to serve the

[020-072]prince with fidelity, inasmuch as they know it to be very necessary for

[020-073]them to cancel by deeds the bad impression which he had formed of them;

[020-074]and thus the prince always extracts more profit from them than from those

[020-075]who, serving him in too much security, may neglect his affairs. And since

[020-076]the matter demands it, I must not fail to warn a prince, who by means of

[020-077]secret favours has acquired a new state, that he must well consider the

[020-078]reasons which induced those to favour him who did so; and if it be not a

[020-079]natural affection towards him, but only discontent with their government,

[020-080]then he will only keep them friendly with great trouble and difficulty,

[020-081]for it will be impossible to satisfy them. And weighing well the reasons

[020-082]for this in those examples which can be taken from ancient and modern

[020-083]affairs, we shall find that it is easier for the prince to make friends of

[020-084]those men who were contented under the former government, and are

[020-085]therefore his enemies, than of those who, being discontented with it, were

[020-086]favourable to him and encouraged him to seize it.

[020-087]6. It has been a custom with princes, in order to hold their states more

[020-088]securely, to build fortresses that may serve as a bridle and bit to those who

[020-089]might design to work against them, and as a place of refuge from a first

[020-090]attack. I praise this system because it has been made use of formerly.

[020-091]Notwithstanding that, Messer Nicolo Vitelli in our times has been seen to

[020-092]demolish two fortresses in Citta di Castello so that he might keep that state;

[020-093]Guido Ubaldo, Duke of Urbino, on returning to his dominion, whence he had been

[020-094]driven by Cesare Borgia, razed to the foundations all the fortresses in that

[020-095]province, and considered that without them it would be more difficult to lose

[020-096]it; the Bentivogli returning to Bologna came to a similar decision. Fortresses,

[020-097]therefore, are useful or not according to circumstances; if they do you good in

[020-098]one way they injure you in another. And this question can be reasoned thus: the

[020-099]prince who has more to fear from the people than from foreigners ought to build

[020-100]fortresses, but he who has more to fear from foreigners than from the people

[020-101]ought to leave them alone. The castle of Milan, built by Francesco Sforza, has

[020-102]made, and will make, more trouble for the house of Sforza than any other

[020-103]disorder in the state. For this reason the best possible fortress is—not

[020-104]to be hated by the people, because, although you may hold the fortresses, yet

[020-105]they will not save you if the people hate you, for there will never be wanting

[020-106]foreigners to assist a people who have taken arms against you. It has not been

[020-107]seen in our times that such fortresses have been of use to any prince, unless

[020-108]to the Countess of Forli,[1]

[020-109]when the Count Girolamo, her consort, was killed; for by that means she was

[020-110]able to withstand the popular attack and wait for assistance from Milan, and

[020-111]thus recover her state; and the posture of affairs was such at that time that

[020-112]the foreigners could not assist the people. But fortresses were of little value

[020-113]to her afterwards when Cesare Borgia attacked her, and when the people, her

[020-114]enemy, were allied with foreigners. Therefore, it would have been safer for

[020-115]her, both then and before, not to have been hated by the people than to have

[020-116]had the fortresses. All these things considered then, I shall praise him who

[020-117]builds fortresses as well as him who does not, and I shall blame whoever,

[020-118]trusting in them, cares little about being hated by the people.

[020-119][1]

[020-120]Catherine Sforza, a daughter of Galeazzo Sforza and Lucrezia Landriani, born

[020-121]1463, died 1509. It was to the Countess of Forli that Machiavelli was sent as

[020-122]envoy on 1499. A letter from Fortunati to the countess announces the

[020-123]appointment: “I have been with the signori,” wrote Fortunati,

[020-124]“to learn whom they would send and when. They tell me that Nicolo

[020-125]Machiavelli, a learned young Florentine noble, secretary to my Lords of the

[020-126]Ten, is to leave with me at once.” *Cf*. “Catherine

[020-127]Sforza,” by Count Pasolini, translated by P. Sylvester, 1898.
