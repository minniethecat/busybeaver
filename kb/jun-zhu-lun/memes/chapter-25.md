## CHAPTER XXV. WHAT FORTUNE CAN EFFECT IN HUMAN AFFAIRS AND HOW TO WITHSTAND HER

[025-001]It is not unknown to me how many men have had, and still have, the opinion that

[025-002]the affairs of the world are in such wise governed by fortune and by God that

[025-003]men with their wisdom cannot direct them and that no one can even help them;

[025-004]and because of this they would have us believe that it is not necessary to

[025-005]labour much in affairs, but to let chance govern them. This opinion has been

[025-006]more credited in our times because of the great changes in affairs which have

[025-007]been seen, and may still be seen, every day, beyond all human conjecture.

[025-008]Sometimes pondering over this, I am in some degree inclined to their opinion.

[025-009]Nevertheless, not to extinguish our free will, I hold it to be true that

[025-010]Fortune is the arbiter of one-half of our actions,[1]

[025-011]but that she still leaves us to direct the other half, or perhaps a little

[025-012]less.

[025-013][1]

[025-014]Frederick the Great was accustomed to say: “The older one gets the more

[025-015]convinced one becomes that his Majesty King Chance does three-quarters of the

[025-016]business of this miserable universe.” Sorel’s “Eastern

[025-017]Question.”

[025-018]I compare her to one of those raging rivers, which when in flood overflows

[025-019]the plains, sweeping away trees and buildings, bearing away the soil from

[025-020]place to place; everything flies before it, all yield to its violence,

[025-021]without being able in any way to withstand it; and yet, though its nature

[025-022]be such, it does not follow therefore that men, when the weather becomes

[025-023]fair, shall not make provision, both with defences and barriers, in such a

[025-024]manner that, rising again, the waters may pass away by canal, and their

[025-025]force be neither so unrestrained nor so dangerous. So it happens with

[025-026]fortune, who shows her power where valour has not prepared to resist her,

[025-027]and thither she turns her forces where she knows that barriers and

[025-028]defences have not been raised to constrain her.

[025-029]And if you will consider Italy, which is the seat of these changes, and

[025-030]which has given to them their impulse, you will see it to be an open

[025-031]country without barriers and without any defence. For if it had been

[025-032]defended by proper valour, as are Germany, Spain, and France, either this

[025-033]invasion would not have made the great changes it has made or it would not

[025-034]have come at all. And this I consider enough to say concerning resistance

[025-035]to fortune in general.

[025-036]But confining myself more to the particular, I say that a prince may be

[025-037]seen happy to-day and ruined to-morrow without having shown any change of

[025-038]disposition or character. This, I believe, arises firstly from causes that

[025-039]have already been discussed at length, namely, that the prince who relies

[025-040]entirely on fortune is lost when it changes. I believe also that he will

[025-041]be successful who directs his actions according to the spirit of the

[025-042]times, and that he whose actions do not accord with the times will not be

[025-043]successful. Because men are seen, in affairs that lead to the end which

[025-044]every man has before him, namely, glory and riches, to get there by

[025-045]various methods; one with caution, another with haste; one by force,

[025-046]another by skill; one by patience, another by its opposite; and each one

[025-047]succeeds in reaching the goal by a different method. One can also see of

[025-048]two cautious men the one attain his end, the other fail; and similarly,

[025-049]two men by different observances are equally successful, the one being

[025-050]cautious, the other impetuous; all this arises from nothing else than

[025-051]whether or not they conform in their methods to the spirit of the times.

[025-052]This follows from what I have said, that two men working differently bring

[025-053]about the same effect, and of two working similarly, one attains his

[025-054]object and the other does not.

[025-055]Changes in estate also issue from this, for if, to one who governs himself

[025-056]with caution and patience, times and affairs converge in such a way that

[025-057]his administration is successful, his fortune is made; but if times and

[025-058]affairs change, he is ruined if he does not change his course of action.

[025-059]But a man is not often found sufficiently circumspect to know how to

[025-060]accommodate himself to the change, both because he cannot deviate from

[025-061]what nature inclines him to do, and also because, having always prospered

[025-062]by acting in one way, he cannot be persuaded that it is well to leave it;

[025-063]and, therefore, the cautious man, when it is time to turn adventurous,

[025-064]does not know how to do it, hence he is ruined; but had he changed his

[025-065]conduct with the times fortune would not have changed.

[025-066]Pope Julius the Second went to work impetuously in all his affairs, and

[025-067]found the times and circumstances conform so well to that line of action

[025-068]that he always met with success. Consider his first enterprise against

[025-069]Bologna, Messer Giovanni Bentivogli being still alive. The Venetians were

[025-070]not agreeable to it, nor was the King of Spain, and he had the enterprise

[025-071]still under discussion with the King of France; nevertheless he personally

[025-072]entered upon the expedition with his accustomed boldness and energy, a

[025-073]move which made Spain and the Venetians stand irresolute and passive, the

[025-074]latter from fear, the former from desire to recover the kingdom of Naples;

[025-075]on the other hand, he drew after him the King of France, because that

[025-076]king, having observed the movement, and desiring to make the Pope his

[025-077]friend so as to humble the Venetians, found it impossible to refuse him.

[025-078]Therefore Julius with his impetuous action accomplished what no other

[025-079]pontiff with simple human wisdom could have done; for if he had waited in

[025-080]Rome until he could get away, with his plans arranged and everything

[025-081]fixed, as any other pontiff would have done, he would never have

[025-082]succeeded. Because the King of France would have made a thousand excuses,

[025-083]and the others would have raised a thousand fears.

[025-084]I will leave his other actions alone, as they were all alike, and they all

[025-085]succeeded, for the shortness of his life did not let him experience the

[025-086]contrary; but if circumstances had arisen which required him to go

[025-087]cautiously, his ruin would have followed, because he would never have

[025-088]deviated from those ways to which nature inclined him.

[025-089]I conclude, therefore that, fortune being changeful and mankind steadfast

[025-090]in their ways, so long as the two are in agreement men are successful, but

[025-091]unsuccessful when they fall out. For my part I consider that it is better

[025-092]to be adventurous than cautious, because fortune is a woman, and if you

[025-093]wish to keep her under it is necessary to beat and ill-use her; and it is

[025-094]seen that she allows herself to be mastered by the adventurous rather than

[025-095]by those who go to work more coldly. She is, therefore, always,

[025-096]woman-like, a lover of young men, because they are less cautious, more

[025-097]violent, and with more audacity command her.
