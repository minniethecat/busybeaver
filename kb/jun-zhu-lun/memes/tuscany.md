# Tuscany

**Tuscany** was the heart of Renaissance Italy — home to Florence, Machiavelli's native city, and the region where Cesare Borgia nearly established a unified principality through the combination of papal authority and his own arms.

## Overview

Tuscany in the early 16th century was dominated by the [[republic-of-florence|Republic of Florence]], but the region also included the Republics of Siena and Lucca, the lordship of Piombino, and smaller territories. Its strategic position between the Papal States to the south and Lombardy to the north made Tuscany a natural target for any ambitious prince seeking to consolidate central Italy.

Machiavelli's perspective on Tuscany is intimate and urgent: the region was his political world. When he warns that a prince who relies on auxiliaries risks "becoming the master of Tuscany" (003-194) only to be undone by his own foreign troops, he speaks from direct observation of Florence's vulnerabilities.

## Political Context

### The Florentine Core

Florence exerted gravitational pull over Tuscany, absorbing Pisa in 1406 and extending its control through a network of dependent territories. But Florentine dominance was never secure. Other Tuscan cities — Siena, Lucca, Pistoia — maintained their independence through a combination of fortification, diplomacy, and the region's characteristically fragmented geography.

Machiavelli notes that "for instance, in Tuscany" (014-035) there are cities accustomed to living under their own laws, which makes them harder for a new prince to hold — a practical observation drawn from Florence's own difficult experience subjugating Pisa.

### Borgia's Tuscan Campaign

Cesare Borgia's ambitions turned toward Tuscany after consolidating the Romagna. Machiavelli records how Borgia "attacked Tuscany" (007-086) and sought to "become master of Tuscany, for he already possessed Perugia" (007-160). Borgia's campaign alarmed Florence, which sent Machiavelli himself as an envoy to observe the Duke's methods at close quarters — an experience that deeply shaped *The Prince*.

## Role in The Prince

### The Borgia Case Study

Tuscany features prominently in Chapter 7, the celebrated portrait of Cesare Borgia. Machiavelli describes how Borgia, having secured the Romagna, "undertook an expedition against Tuscany" (027-092) — a move that tested Florence's defenses and the Duke's own diplomatic position with France. The episode illustrates the principle that a prince must secure his flanks before advancing: Borgia needed French acquiescence for any Tuscan campaign.

### The Guelph Party and Factional Politics

In the Castruccio Castracani appendix, Machiavelli provides a detailed history of Tuscan factional politics. "The knights of the Guelph party in Tuscany" (028-200) and their Ghibelline rivals shaped the region's political landscape for centuries. The narrative shows how factionalism — the very disease Machiavelli diagnoses in Italian politics — repeatedly prevented Tuscan unity.

> "He was already becoming the master of Tuscany, for he already possessed Perugia and Piombino, and Pisa was under his protection." (007-160)

### The Emperor's Lieutenant

Machiavelli also traces how imperial authority once structured Tuscan politics. The "emperor's lieutenant in Tuscany" (028-265) represented a form of legitimate external authority that, in Machiavelli's analysis, could have provided a framework for order — had it been sustained. The narrative of Castruccio's rise shows how a capable leader could, through his own virtù, assemble "the empire of Tuscany into his hands" (028-529).

## Related Events

- The Pisan War (1494–1509): Florence's long struggle to reconquer Pisa, which Machiavelli helped direct as a Florentine official
- Borgia's Tuscan campaign (1501–1502): Cesare Borgia advanced on Florence, threatening the republic
- The fall of the Florentine Republic (1512): Spanish troops restored the Medici, ending Machiavelli's political career
