# Giovanni Bentivogli

Giovanni II Bentivogli (1443–1508) was the de facto ruler of Bologna during the Italian Wars. He appears in *The Prince* in two distinct roles: as one of the minor Italian lords who rushed to make advances to [[Louis XII of France]] (Chapter 3), and as the beneficiary of a remarkable demonstration of popular loyalty that saved his family's rule (Chapter 19).

## 生平概要

Giovanni Bentivogli governed Bologna from 1463, initially as the city's *gonfaloniere* and later as its undisputed signore. His rule was characterized by cultural patronage — he employed numerous artists and architects — and by careful navigation of the great-power conflicts that convulsed Italy after the French invasion of 1494.

When Louis XII entered Italy, Giovanni was among "the Bentivogli" who, along with the Marquess of Mantua, the Duke of Ferrara, and numerous other lords, "made advances to him to become his friend" (003-160). This rush of Italian potentates to the French king illustrated how quickly a conqueror could gain allies — and how little those allies were worth.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Giovanni's most significant appearance in *The Prince* is not as himself but as the child-survivor of a political murder. In [[Chapter 19]], Machiavelli recounts how Messer Annibale Bentivogli, "prince in Bologna (grandfather of the present Annibale), having been murdered by the Canneschi" (019-008), left only one survivor: Giovanni, then an infant.

The people of Bologna, enraged by the assassination, rose up and "murdered all the Canneschi" (019-008). So great was the Bentivogli family's popularity that the Bolognese, learning a supposed Bentivogli was living in Florence — a man "who up to that time had been considered the son of a blacksmith" — sent for him and gave him the government of their city "until Messer Giovanni came in due course to the government" (019-008).

> "This sprung from the popular goodwill which the house of Bentivogli enjoyed in those days in Bologna." (019-008)

### 作为范例的角色

The Bentivogli episode serves as Machiavelli's illustration that **popular goodwill is a prince's surest defense**. The family survived assassination not through fortresses or armies, but through the affection of the Bolognese people — a loyalty so deep that the citizens would accept even the nominal rule of a supposed blacksmith's son rather than see the Bentivogli house extinguished.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's use of the Bentivogli story is characteristic: a local episode, drawn from recent memory, is elevated to a general principle. The Bentivogli's survival through popular love is contrasted throughout *The Prince* with rulers who relied on fortresses and were destroyed — among them [[Catherina Sforza]], whose fortress at Forlì could not save her.
