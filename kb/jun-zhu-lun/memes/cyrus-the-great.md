# Cyrus the Great

Cyrus the Great was the founder of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, reigning from approximately 559 to 530 BCE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli places Cyrus among the four supreme exemplars of rulers who acquired their states through their own *virtù* — alongside Moses, Romulus, and Theseus [006-024].

## Rise to Power

Cyrus overthrew the Median Empire and expanded Persian rule across much of the ancient Near East. Machiavelli groups him with other "armed prophets" who succeeded because they relied on their own forces rather than fortune:

> "In considering Cyrus and others who have acquired or founded kingdoms, all will be found admirable" [006-028].

Machiavelli argues that Cyrus's success depended on specific conditions — in his case, the discontent of the Persians under Median rule [006-042].

## Role in *The Prince*

### As an Armed Prophet

Cyrus appears in Chapter 6, "Concerning New Principalities Which Are Acquired by One's Own Arms and Ability," as the quintessential example of a prince who rose through merit. Machiavelli's key point is that such princes succeed because they are armed and self-reliant:

> "If Moses, Cyrus, Theseus, and Romulus had been unarmed they could not have enforced their constitutions for long" [006-076].

### Military Virtue

In Chapter 14, Machiavelli recommends Xenophon's biography of Cyrus as essential reading for any prince. He praises Cyrus's generosity and military prowess, grouping him with Caesar and Alexander as "ready givers" who won loyalty through liberality [016-054]. In Chapter 26, he invokes Cyrus as a model of the redeeming prince who could liberate Italy [026-011].

## Historical Assessment

Cyrus was renowned in antiquity for his tolerant governance and military genius. For Machiavelli, Cyrus represents the ideal of a founder-legislator who not only conquered but created enduring institutions. The reference to Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* suggests Machiavelli saw Cyrus as the classical model of the philosopher-king adapted to political reality.
