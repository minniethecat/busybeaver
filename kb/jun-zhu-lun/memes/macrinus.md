# Macrinus

**Macrinus** (Marcus Opellius Macrinus) was a Roman emperor who reigned briefly in 217–218, succeeding [[caracalla|Caracalla]] after his assassination. He appears in Chapter 19 of *The Prince* among the emperors Machiavelli declines to discuss in detail.

## Overview

Macrinus is mentioned twice in *The Prince*, both times in Chapter 19. Machiavelli lists him among the succession of emperors following [[septimius-severus|Septimius Severus]] — "Caracalla, Macrinus, Heliogabalus, Alexander, and Maximinus" (019-118) — and then explicitly sets him aside as one of those not worth detailed analysis:

> I do not wish to discuss Heliogabalus, Macrinus, or Julian, who, being altogether contemptible, were quickly eliminated. (019-251)

## Historical Context

Macrinus was a praetorian prefect under Caracalla who orchestrated the emperor's assassination during a campaign against Parthia in 217. He became the first Roman emperor of equestrian (non-senatorial) origin. His reign lasted only fourteen months: his attempts to reduce military pay alienated the army, and the Severan women — [[julia-maesa|Julia Maesa]] and her faction — successfully promoted the young [[heliogabalus|Heliogabalus]] as a rival claimant. Macrinus was defeated in battle and executed in 218.

## Role in The Prince

### Brief Mention

Macrinus appears in Machiavelli's catalogue of Roman emperors who ruled after Severus. Chapter 19 analyzes why some emperors prospered while others perished, using the Severan succession as a case study in the dynamics of hatred and contempt.

Machiavelli places Macrinus in the category of those who were "altogether contemptible" and "quickly eliminated" (019-251). The term "contemptible" (in Machiavelli's framework) suggests a ruler who failed to command respect — either through perceived weakness, indecisiveness, or inability to maintain the dignity of the office.

### The Transitional Emperor

Macrinus serves as a transitional figure between Caracalla's cruelty and Heliogabalus's excess. His brief reign illustrates a recurring Machiavellian theme: a new prince who comes to power through conspiracy (Caracalla's assassination) must immediately secure the army's loyalty. Macrinus's failure to do so led directly to his downfall.

## Machiavelli's Assessment

Machiavelli's dismissal of Macrinus as "contemptible" suggests a ruler whose brief tenure offered no lasting lessons beyond the obvious: a prince who cannot command the respect of his soldiers will not remain a prince. Unlike [[septimius-severus|Severus]], who combined the lion and the fox, Macrinus possessed neither quality in sufficient measure.
