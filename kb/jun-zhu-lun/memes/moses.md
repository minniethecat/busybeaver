# Moses

Moses is the biblical prophet who led the Israelites out of Egyptian slavery and delivered the divine law at Mount Sinai. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli places Moses first among the four great founders — Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus — who acquired their states through their own *virtù* rather than through fortune [006-024].

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Armed Prophet

Machiavelli's treatment of Moses in Chapter 6 is remarkable for its secular analysis of a religious figure. He brackets the question of divine inspiration and focuses on Moses's political achievement:

> "Although one should not discuss Moses, he having been a mere executor of the things that God commanded, yet he ought to be admired if only for that grace which made him worthy to speak with God" [006-026].

This passage is often read as Machiavelli's ironic nod to religious orthodoxy while his real analysis treats Moses as a political founder no different from Cyrus or Romulus.

### The Necessity of Force

Machiavelli's key insight about Moses is that even a divinely commissioned leader needed armed force to succeed:

> "It was necessary, therefore, to Moses that he should find the people of Israel in Egypt enslaved and oppressed by the Egyptians, in order that they should follow him to escape from servitude" [006-037].

The unspoken conclusion is that Moses's authority rested not on miracles but on the material condition of his followers' desperation — and on his willingness to use force when necessary [006-076].

### The Exhortation to Liberate Italy

In the final chapter of *The Prince* (Chapter 26), Machiavelli returns to Moses as a model for the prince who would liberate Italy from foreign domination. He argues that God made manifest the ability of Moses through the circumstances of his time, and that Italy's present condition offers a similar opportunity for a redeeming prince [026-009].

## Historical Assessment

For Machiavelli, Moses represents the archetype of the founder-legislator. The political reading of Moses — stripped of theology and analyzed as a case study in successful state-founding — exemplifies the core method of *The Prince*: reducing seemingly miraculous events to the observable laws of political success.
