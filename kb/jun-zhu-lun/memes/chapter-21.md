## CHAPTER XXI. HOW A PRINCE SHOULD CONDUCT HIMSELF SO AS TO GAIN RENOWN

[021-001]Nothing makes a prince so much esteemed as great enterprises and setting a

[021-002]fine example. We have in our time Ferdinand of Aragon, the present King of

[021-003]Spain. He can almost be called a new prince, because he has risen, by fame

[021-004]and glory, from being an insignificant king to be the foremost king in

[021-005]Christendom; and if you will consider his deeds you will find them all

[021-006]great and some of them extraordinary. In the beginning of his reign he

[021-007]attacked Granada, and this enterprise was the foundation of his dominions.

[021-008]He did this quietly at first and without any fear of hindrance, for he

[021-009]held the minds of the barons of Castile occupied in thinking of the war

[021-010]and not anticipating any innovations; thus they did not perceive that by

[021-011]these means he was acquiring power and authority over them. He was able

[021-012]with the money of the Church and of the people to sustain his armies, and

[021-013]by that long war to lay the foundation for the military skill which has

[021-014]since distinguished him. Further, always using religion as a plea, so as

[021-015]to undertake greater schemes, he devoted himself with pious cruelty to

[021-016]driving out and clearing his kingdom of the Moors; nor could there be a

[021-017]more admirable example, nor one more rare. Under this same cloak he

[021-018]assailed Africa, he came down on Italy, he has finally attacked France;

[021-019]and thus his achievements and designs have always been great, and have

[021-020]kept the minds of his people in suspense and admiration and occupied with

[021-021]the issue of them. And his actions have arisen in such a way, one out of

[021-022]the other, that men have never been given time to work steadily against

[021-023]him.

[021-024]Again, it much assists a prince to set unusual examples in internal

[021-025]affairs, similar to those which are related of Messer Bernabo da Milano,

[021-026]who, when he had the opportunity, by any one in civil life doing some

[021-027]extraordinary thing, either good or bad, would take some method of

[021-028]rewarding or punishing him, which would be much spoken about. And a prince

[021-029]ought, above all things, always endeavour in every action to gain for

[021-030]himself the reputation of being a great and remarkable man.

[021-031]A prince is also respected when he is either a true friend or a downright

[021-032]enemy, that is to say, when, without any reservation, he declares himself

[021-033]in favour of one party against the other; which course will always be more

[021-034]advantageous than standing neutral; because if two of your powerful

[021-035]neighbours come to blows, they are of such a character that, if one of

[021-036]them conquers, you have either to fear him or not. In either case it will

[021-037]always be more advantageous for you to declare yourself and to make war

[021-038]strenuously; because, in the first case, if you do not declare yourself,

[021-039]you will invariably fall a prey to the conqueror, to the pleasure and

[021-040]satisfaction of him who has been conquered, and you will have no reasons

[021-041]to offer, nor anything to protect or to shelter you. Because he who

[021-042]conquers does not want doubtful friends who will not aid him in the time

[021-043]of trial; and he who loses will not harbour you because you did not

[021-044]willingly, sword in hand, court his fate.

[021-045]Antiochus went into Greece, being sent for by the Ætolians to drive out

[021-046]the Romans. He sent envoys to the Achaeans, who were friends of the

[021-047]Romans, exhorting them to remain neutral; and on the other hand the Romans

[021-048]urged them to take up arms. This question came to be discussed in the

[021-049]council of the Achaeans, where the legate of Antiochus urged them to stand

[021-050]neutral. To this the Roman legate answered: “As for that which has been

[021-051]said, that it is better and more advantageous for your state not to

[021-052]interfere in our war, nothing can be more erroneous; because by not

[021-053]interfering you will be left, without favour or consideration, the guerdon

[021-054]of the conqueror.” Thus it will always happen that he who is not your

[021-055]friend will demand your neutrality, whilst he who is your friend will

[021-056]entreat you to declare yourself with arms. And irresolute princes, to

[021-057]avoid present dangers, generally follow the neutral path, and are

[021-058]generally ruined. But when a prince declares himself gallantly in favour

[021-059]of one side, if the party with whom he allies himself conquers, although

[021-060]the victor may be powerful and may have him at his mercy, yet he is

[021-061]indebted to him, and there is established a bond of amity; and men are

[021-062]never so shameless as to become a monument of ingratitude by oppressing

[021-063]you. Victories after all are never so complete that the victor must not

[021-064]show some regard, especially to justice. But if he with whom you ally

[021-065]yourself loses, you may be sheltered by him, and whilst he is able he may

[021-066]aid you, and you become companions on a fortune that may rise again.

[021-067]In the second case, when those who fight are of such a character that you

[021-068]have no anxiety as to who may conquer, so much the more is it greater

[021-069]prudence to be allied, because you assist at the destruction of one by the

[021-070]aid of another who, if he had been wise, would have saved him; and

[021-071]conquering, as it is impossible that he should not do with your

[021-072]assistance, he remains at your discretion. And here it is to be noted that

[021-073]a prince ought to take care never to make an alliance with one more

[021-074]powerful than himself for the purposes of attacking others, unless

[021-075]necessity compels him, as is said above; because if he conquers you are at

[021-076]his discretion, and princes ought to avoid as much as possible being at

[021-077]the discretion of any one. The Venetians joined with France against the

[021-078]Duke of Milan, and this alliance, which caused their ruin, could have been

[021-079]avoided. But when it cannot be avoided, as happened to the Florentines

[021-080]when the Pope and Spain sent armies to attack Lombardy, then in such a

[021-081]case, for the above reasons, the prince ought to favour one of the

[021-082]parties.

[021-083]Never let any Government imagine that it can choose perfectly safe

[021-084]courses; rather let it expect to have to take very doubtful ones, because

[021-085]it is found in ordinary affairs that one never seeks to avoid one trouble

[021-086]without running into another; but prudence consists in knowing how to

[021-087]distinguish the character of troubles, and for choice to take the lesser

[021-088]evil.

[021-089]A prince ought also to show himself a patron of ability, and to honour the

[021-090]proficient in every art. At the same time he should encourage his citizens

[021-091]to practise their callings peaceably, both in commerce and agriculture,

[021-092]and in every other following, so that the one should not be deterred from

[021-093]improving his possessions for fear lest they be taken away from him or

[021-094]another from opening up trade for fear of taxes; but the prince ought to

[021-095]offer rewards to whoever wishes to do these things and designs in any way

[021-096]to honour his city or state.

[021-097]Further, he ought to entertain the people with festivals and spectacles at

[021-098]convenient seasons of the year; and as every city is divided into guilds or

[021-099]into societies,[1]

[021-100]he ought to hold such bodies in esteem, and associate with them sometimes, and

[021-101]show himself an example of courtesy and liberality; nevertheless, always

[021-102]maintaining the majesty of his rank, for this he must never consent to abate in

[021-103]anything.

[021-104][1]

[021-105]“Guilds or societies,” “in arti o in tribu.”

[021-106]“Arti” were craft or trade guilds, *cf*. Florio:

[021-107]“Arte . . . a whole company of any trade in any city or corporation

[021-108]town.” The guilds of Florence are most admirably described by Mr Edgcumbe

[021-109]Staley in his work on the subject (Methuen, 1906). Institutions of a somewhat

[021-110]similar character, called “artel,” exist in Russia to-day,

[021-111]*cf*. Sir Mackenzie Wallace’s “Russia,” ed. 1905:

[021-112]“The sons . . . were always during the working season members of an

[021-113]artel. In some of the larger towns there are artels of a much more complex

[021-114]kind— permanent associations, possessing large capital, and pecuniarily

[021-115]responsible for the acts of the individual members.” The word

[021-116]“artel,” despite its apparent similarity, has, Mr Aylmer Maude

[021-117]assures me, no connection with “ars” or “arte.” Its

[021-118]root is that of the verb “rotisya,” to bind oneself by an oath; and

[021-119]it is generally admitted to be only another form of “rota,” which

[021-120]now signifies a “regimental company.” In both words the underlying

[021-121]idea is that of a body of men united by an oath. “Tribu” were

[021-122]possibly gentile groups, united by common descent, and included individuals

[021-123]connected by marriage. Perhaps our words “sects” or

[021-124]“clans” would be most appropriate.
