# Liberality

In Chapter 16 of *The Prince*, Machiavelli subverts the traditional virtue of **liberality** (generosity), arguing that a prince who practices it will inevitably impoverish his state and earn the people's hatred — while the prince who practices parsimony (meanness) will ultimately be loved more.

## Machiavelli's Argument

### The Paradox of Liberality

Machiavelli opens Chapter 16 with a characteristic inversion:

> [016-002] …t would be well to be reputed liberal. Nevertheless, liberality

Being *reputed* liberal is valuable; actually *being* liberal is destructive. This distinction between appearance and reality — central to Machiavelli's entire ethical project — receives its clearest economic articulation in the analysis of liberality.

> [016-006] …aintain among men the name of liberal is

The phrase "name of liberal" signals Machiavelli's core insight: liberality is a reputational asset, not a behavioral prescription. The prince who seeks the reputation without the practice succeeds; the prince who confuses the two destroys himself.

### The Economic Trap

The mechanism of liberality's failure is brutally straightforward: generosity consumes its own means.

> [016-057] … nothing wastes so rapidly as liberality, for even whilst you

Liberality is self-extinguishing. The more a prince gives, the more is expected; the more he taxes to fund giving, the more he is hated. The trajectory is inescapable: generosity → depletion → taxation → hatred → ruin.

> [016-009] … wish to maintain the name of liberal, to unduly weigh

A prince who persists in liberality "unduly weighs" upon his subjects — the very people whose love he sought to purchase. The irony is complete: the virtue meant to secure popularity destroys it.

> [016-012] …ed by any one; thus, with his liberality, having offended many

Liberality offends more people than it pleases because the many who are taxed to fund it hate the prince, while the few who receive benefits are never satisfied. The arithmetic of generosity is politically fatal.

### The Superior Alternative: Parsimony

Against liberality, Machiavelli proposes parsimony — carefully managed economy:

> [016-019] to be more considered than if liberal, seeing that with his economy…

A prince who is "mean" (parsimonious) in the short term can fund wars, public works, and emergencies without burdening his subjects. His reputation may suffer initially, but over time the people recognize the benefits of fiscal discipline and come to consider him "more liberal" than the spendthrift prince who ultimately ruins them.

> [016-022] …mes to pass that he exercises liberality towards all from whom he d…

The prince who exercises liberality toward "all from whom he does not take" — that is, who gives only what is not extracted from his own subjects — avoids the trap entirely. This is the liberality of the conqueror who distributes captured wealth, not of the ruler who taxes his people.

### Historical Evidence

Machiavelli marshals specific examples to prove his case:

> [016-027] …he papacy by a reputation for liberality, yet he did

Pope Julius II used a reputation for liberality to attain the papacy, but once in power, "he did not" continue the practice — recognizing that papal revenues would not sustain it. The strategic use of liberality *to acquire* power followed by its abandonment *to preserve* power exemplifies Machiavelli's instrumental approach to virtue.

> [016-032] …prises if he had been reputed liberal. A prince,

The counterfactual is telling: had this prince been reputed liberal, he would not have succeeded in his enterprises. Liberality would have drained the resources needed for military and political action.

## Relation to Other Concepts

### Liberality and [[Virtù]]

In Machiavelli's framework, true virtù does not consist in practicing conventional virtues like liberality, but in knowing when to *appear* to practice them and when to violate them for the state's preservation. The virtuoso prince understands the liberality paradox and chooses parsimony without hesitation.

### Liberality and [[Cruelty]]

Liberality and cruelty form a complementary pair in Machiavelli's system. Excessive liberality leads to the cruelty of taxation; well-measured cruelty (such as Cesare Borgia's execution of Remirro de Orco) can prevent the need for greater cruelty later. The prince who refuses to be "cruel" (parsimonious) in small matters will be forced into genuine cruelty in large ones.

### Liberality and Necessity

Necessity dictates the limits of liberality. A prince whose state faces existential threat cannot afford generosity; necessity compels parsimony. Only in conditions of surplus — conquest, inherited wealth — can liberality be safely practiced, and even then only instrumentally.

## In Machiavelli's System

The analysis of liberality in Chapter 16 is not an isolated critique of one virtue but a template for Machiavelli's broader method: take a conventional virtue, trace its real-world consequences, and demonstrate that the conventional understanding leads to political failure. The same method is applied to:

- **Cruelty vs. Clemency** (Ch. 17): Clemency leads to disorder; measured cruelty preserves order.
- **Faith/Faithlessness** (Ch. 18): Keeping faith is praiseworthy; breaking it is often necessary.
- **Fear vs. Love** (Ch. 17): Being loved is desirable; being feared is reliable.

In each case, Machiavelli's target is not the virtue itself but the *naive belief* that virtues always produce good outcomes. The liberality analysis is the economic instantiation of this broader epistemological challenge.

## Later Influence

Machiavelli's critique of liberality influenced early modern fiscal thought, particularly the development of the "fiscal-military state" concept. The idea that parsimony in peacetime enables military expenditure in wartime became a cornerstone of state-building theory, echoed in the writings of Justus Lipsius and the *raison d'état* tradition.

The paradox of liberality also prefigures what economists now call the "resource curse" — the observation that abundant resources (whether from conquest, as in Caesar's case, or natural wealth) can distort incentives and lead to worse governance outcomes than scarcity. Machiavelli's insight that *how* wealth is obtained matters more than *how much* wealth a prince possesses remains remarkably contemporary.
