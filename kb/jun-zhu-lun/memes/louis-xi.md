# Louis XI

Louis XI (1423–1483) was King of France from 1461 until his death. In *The Prince*, he appears indirectly through the consequences of his dynastic arrangements: his daughter Jeanne's marriage to [[Louis XII of France]] became the lever by which [[Pope Alexander VI]] extracted French support for his son [[Cesare Borgia]]'s campaign in the Romagna.

## 生平概要

Louis XI, known as "the Prudent" (le Prudent) and "the Universal Spider" (l'universelle araigne) for his diplomatic machinations, was the son of Charles VII. He spent much of his reign consolidating royal authority against the great feudal lords, particularly the Duke of Burgundy, and laying the foundations of French absolutism.

His daughter Jeanne, born with physical disabilities, was married to the future Louis XII — a union that Louis XII later sought to dissolve in order to marry [[Anne of Brittany]], the widow of [[Charles VIII of France]].

## 与《君主论》的关联

Louis XI's relevance to *The Prince* is genealogical and structural rather than analytical. When Louis XII "divorced his wife, Jeanne, daughter of Louis XI, and married in 1499 Anne of Brittany" (003-235), the dissolution required papal approval. Alexander VI granted it — in exchange for Louis XII's military support in the Romagna.

This transaction, in which the dissolution of an unwanted marriage became the entry point for papal aggrandizement in Italy, is one of the "five errors" Machiavelli attributes to Louis XII. The marriage that Louis XI had arranged became, under his successor, a link in the chain of events that drew France into Italian entanglements.

### 作为范例的角色

Louis XI is a **background structural figure** — not analyzed for his own statecraft, but significant as the arranger of a dynastic union whose dissolution had consequences that Machiavelli traces through multiple chapters.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli does not directly evaluate Louis XI. The king appears only as a patronymic — "daughter of Louis XI" — in a footnote. Yet the footnote records a moment of genuine political consequence: the dissolution of his daughter's marriage was the price of papal support, and papal support was the mechanism by which the Church acquired temporal power in the Romagna. Louis XI's dynastic planning thus had effects that extended far beyond his own reign and kingdom.
