# Commodus

Commodus was a Roman emperor who reigned from 177 to 192 CE. He was the son of Marcus Aurelius, one of the most respected emperors in Roman history. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents Commodus as the primary example of how a ruler's personal cruelty and corruption can destroy an inherited legacy.

## Rise and Fall

Commodus inherited the empire from his father Marcus Aurelius, one of Rome's greatest philosopher-emperors. Unlike his father, who was loved and admired, Commodus adopted a tyrannical style of rule that alienated the Senate, the army, and the people. His reign ended in assassination, orchestrated by his own court after years of increasingly erratic behavior.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Danger of Being Hated

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli uses Commodus to demonstrate the fatal consequence of being hated. He groups Commodus with other failed emperors, noting the pattern:

> "Commodus, Pertinax, Julian, Severus and his son Antoninus" all faced the problem of maintaining power, but Commodus stands out as the one who inherited a secure throne and lost it through his own vices [019-117].

### Contrast with Marcus Aurelius

Machiavelli's analysis of Commodus gains its force from the comparison with his father. Marcus Aurelius was "a man of most holy life" who was loved and revered; Commodus, despite inheriting the same position, "came to a bad end" because of his fundamentally different character [019-167]. The lesson is clear: inheritance alone cannot protect a prince who makes himself hated.

### The Severus Contrast

Machiavelli contrasts the "opposite characters of Commodus, Severus, Antoninus" to demonstrate that success depends not on any single quality but on adapting to circumstances [019-167]. Where Severus combined force with cunning and succeeded, Commodus combined force with contemptible behavior and failed:

> "All, except Severus, came to a bad end" [019-170].

## Historical Assessment

For Machiavelli, Commodus illustrates the fragility of inherited power. The son of the most admired emperor could not sustain what his father built, because he lacked the essential quality of *virtù* — the ability to manage appearances, maintain the respect of the army, and avoid the contempt of the people. His fate serves as a warning that a prince must study not only how to acquire power but how to keep it.
