# Rome

**Rome** appears in *The Prince* in two guises: as the ancient republic whose political wisdom Machiavelli constantly invokes, and as the contemporary seat of the papacy whose temporal power shaped Italian politics in the early 16th century.

## Overview

Rome was the capital of the Papal States and the symbolic center of Western Christendom. In Machiavelli's lifetime, the city was contested by the great Roman baronial families — the [[orsini-family|Orsini]] and the Colonnesi — whose private armies and factional violence made Rome "but half alive" as a political entity. Machiavelli himself was sent to Rome on diplomatic missions in 1503 to observe the papal conclave (P01-130).

Beyond the contemporary city, Rome's ancient past provides Machiavelli with his deepest reservoir of political examples. The Roman Republic and Empire supply more exemplars in *The Prince* than any other source.

## Political Context

### The Baronial City

Under the Renaissance papacy, Rome was a city where secular and ecclesiastical power collided. The Orsini and Colonnesi families controlled fortified compounds within the city, each commanding private armies. Machiavelli notes that Cesare Borgia won over "the gentlemen of Rome, so as to be able to curb the Orsini" (007-152), using Rome's factional divisions to his advantage.

### Ancient Rome as Political Model

Machiavelli's political thought is saturated with Roman examples. Chapter 6 invokes "Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus, and such like" — the founders of states — with Romulus as "founder of the fatherland" (006-042). The Roman Republic's mixed constitution, its military discipline, and its ability to adapt to fortune are held up as the standard against which modern states must measure themselves.

## Role in The Prince

### The Roman Emperors

The Roman emperors of Chapter 19 — from [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]] to [[maximinus|Maximinus]] — form the longest sustained case study in *The Prince*. Machiavelli uses their reigns to demonstrate the fatal consequences of being hated or despised. The emperors' successes and failures illustrate the central theme: a prince's security depends on the people's goodwill and the army's loyalty.

### Ancient Roman Virtue

Machiavelli's concept of virtù is essentially Roman: the capacity for bold, decisive action that shapes fortune rather than yielding to it. The Romans' success in conquering and holding provinces — discussed in Chapter 4 — demonstrates the importance of extinguishing the memory of former rulers and establishing colonies.

### The Republican Legacy

The Roman Republic's balancing of patrician and plebeian interests provides the analytical framework for Chapter 9 on civil principalities. Machiavelli contrasts the Gracchi — popular reformers whose story parallels that of [[giorgio-scali|Giorgio Scali]] in Florence (009-075) — with the stabilizing role of the Senate.

## Related Events

- The papal consolidation under Alexander VI and Julius II: the popes suppressed baronial power in Rome
- The sack of Rome (1527): occurred after Machiavelli's death, but the city's vulnerability was evident earlier
