# Liberty (Libertà)

Liberty (libertà) in Machiavelli's political thought refers to the condition of self-governing republics and free peoples — a state that is both militarily resilient and politically fragile. Unlike later liberal conceptions of individual rights, Machiavelli's liberty is collective and institutional: a people are free when they govern themselves under their own laws rather than under a prince.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli addresses liberty primarily in the context of conquest and regime change. A people accustomed to liberty are the most difficult to subdue, and once conquered, the only secure options are to destroy their city or to rule through their own institutions.

### 核心论点

- A city or province accustomed to living under its own laws and in liberty is far harder to hold than one used to princely rule. The memory of liberty never fades, and former freedoms fuel perpetual rebellion (005-020, 005-034)
- When conquering a formerly free republic, the prince has three options: destroy it, reside there personally, or allow it to live under its own laws while extracting tribute — the first is safest (005-020)
- Liberty is so deeply valued that neither the passage of time nor the conferral of benefits can extinguish the memory of it in a free people (005-034)
- The institutions on which liberty depends — laws, customs, and civic bodies — must be preserved if a conquered republic is to be governed indirectly (019-089)

### 历史例证

- **The Roman Republic** — Machiavelli's archetype of a liberty-loving people; Rome's conquest of free cities like Capua and Carthage required either destruction or the establishment of colonies to extinguish the memory of freedom (005-020)
- **The Pisans** — after a century of Florentine rule, Pisa still remembered its former liberty and continually sought to recover it, illustrating that the desire for freedom cannot be extinguished by benefits or time (005-034)
- **The Spartans** — conquered free cities but allowed them to retain their own laws, making the Spartan empire durable yet still vulnerable to eventual revolt

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Liberty vs. Virtù**: Virtù is the active capacity to acquire and maintain; liberty is the institutional condition that virtù may either destroy (in a conqueror) or defend (in a citizen). The prince's virtù extinguishes liberty; the citizen's virtù sustains it.
- **Liberty vs. Fortune**: Fortune threatens liberty by introducing external shocks that destabilize free institutions. A free people with strong laws can better resist fortune's blows than a principality dependent on a single ruler's qualities.
- **Liberty vs. Principality**: These are opposing political forms. A principality is rule by one; liberty is rule by law and shared governance. Machiavelli treats the transition from liberty to principality as irreversible without destruction.
- **Liberty vs. Necessity**: Necessity can compel a free people to accept princely rule temporarily, but the desire for liberty persists beneath. When necessity lifts, liberty reasserts itself.
