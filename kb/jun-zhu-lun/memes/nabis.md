# Nabis

Nabis was a ruler of Sparta who reigned as tyrant from approximately 207 to 192 BCE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli cites him as an example of a prince who came to power through the favor of his fellow citizens — what Machiavelli calls a "civil principality" — and who maintained his position through prudent management of internal politics.

## Rise to Power

Nabis came to power in Sparta during a period of decline, establishing himself as a reformist tyrant. He redistributed land, freed helots, and strengthened Sparta's military. Machiavelli groups him with other rulers who acquired power through a combination of popular support and political cunning.

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Civil Prince

In Chapter 9, "Concerning a Civil Principality," Machiavelli presents Nabis as a case study in how a prince who rises through the support of the people can maintain power:

> "Nabis, tyrant of Sparta, sustained the attack of all Greece and of a victorious Roman army, and defended his country and his state against them" [009-065].

The passage emphasizes that a prince who has the support of his own people can withstand even overwhelming external threats.

### Resistance to External Threats

Nabis's achievement — defending Sparta against the combined might of Rome and the Greek states — illustrates a recurring Machiavellian theme:

> "Nabis, tyrant of Sparta, conquered a great part of Greece" through internal consolidation and strategic alliances [009-082].

Machiavelli returns to Nabis in Chapter 19, reiterating that a prince who avoids being hated by his own people "can resist every attack, as I said Nabis the Spartan did" [019-030].

### The Lesson

The lesson Machiavelli draws from Nabis is twofold. First, the foundation of a prince's security is the goodwill of his subjects, not the strength of his fortresses or armies. Second, even a tyrant — a term Machiavelli uses without the modern pejorative connotation — can succeed if he governs with the people's interests in mind. Nabis's redistribution of land and freeing of slaves were not acts of virtue but acts of political prudence that secured his popular base.

## Historical Assessment

For Machiavelli, Nabis represents the successful "new prince" of antiquity: a ruler who seized power through ability rather than inheritance, governed through the support of the common people rather than the nobles, and survived challenges that destroyed other states. His example is particularly relevant to Machiavelli's argument that a prince's first priority must be to avoid the hatred of his own people.
