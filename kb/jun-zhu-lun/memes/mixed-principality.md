# Mixed Principality

A mixed principality is the second category in Machiavelli's typology of states: a domain formed when a prince annexes a new territory to his existing realm. It is "not entirely new, but like a member added to the hereditary state of the prince who acquires it" (003-001). Machiavelli identifies the mixed principality as the most politically unstable form of rule.

## Inherent Difficulties

Machiavelli warns that "the difficulties occur in a new principality" (003-001) — and the mixed principality inherits all these difficulties while adding its own. The new subjects expect improvement from their change of ruler but "find themselves deceived, because they inevitably find they have made their condition worse" (003-013). The prince must station troops, impose taxes, and displace local elites — all of which create enemies among those he needs as allies.

Machiavelli's famous maxim applies here: "men change their rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and this hope induces them to take up arms against their ruler; but they only deceive themselves" (003-013).

## The French Failure in Milan

Louis XII of France provides Machiavelli's case study in mismanaging a mixed principality. Louis annexed Milan to the French crown but committed five cardinal errors: he destroyed the lesser powers that could have been his allies, increased the power of the Pope, brought Spain into Italy, failed to settle there personally, and did not send colonies. "It was enough to take the state from the Duke of Milan the first time" (003-027), but Louis's errors made the acquisition unsustainable. Milan was lost twice, and ultimately French power in Italy collapsed.

## Remedies

Machiavelli prescribes two remedies for holding a mixed principality: (1) the prince should go and live there personally, as the Ottoman Turk did in Greece; or (2) he should establish colonies, which cost little and offend only the displaced minority. Garrisons, by contrast, are expensive and "offend everyone" (003-056).
