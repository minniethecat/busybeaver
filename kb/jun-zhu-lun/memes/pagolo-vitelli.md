# Pagolo Vitelli

Pagolo Vitelli was an Italian condottiero who served as captain of the Florentine army in the late fifteenth century. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli mentions him twice: once as the commander under whom Oliverotto da Fermo learned the military arts, and once as a model of prudent leadership whose fate illustrates the danger of mercenary captains.

## Historical Context

Vitelli belonged to the prominent Vitelli family of Città di Castello, a dynasty of condottieri who sold their military services to the highest bidder among the Italian city-states. The Vitelli were among the most respected mercenary captains of their era, alongside the Orsini. Machiavelli notes that Oliverotto da Fermo was sent "to fight under Pagolo Vitelli, that, being trained under his discipline, he might attain some high position in the military profession" [008-051].

## Role in *The Prince*

### The Mercenary Captain

In Chapter 12, Machiavelli presents Vitelli as a case study in the unreliability of mercenary leadership:

> "The Florentines appointed as their captain Pagolo Vitelli, a most prudent man, who with a high reputation had risen from a private station" [012-085].

This passage occurs in Machiavelli's extended critique of mercenary armies. Vitelli was hired by Florence to prosecute the war against Pisa — but his prudence and reputation did not prevent the campaign from failing. For Machiavelli, Vitelli exemplifies the structural problem: no matter how capable the individual captain, a mercenary's interests are never fully aligned with those of his employer.

### Oliverotto's Mentor

Vitelli's second appearance in *The Prince* is as a facilitator of Oliverotto's rise. By training under Vitelli, Oliverotto gained the military skills and reputation that enabled him to later seize Fermo through treachery (008-051). Vitelli thus plays an unwitting role in one of Machiavelli's most vivid cautionary tales about criminal usurpation.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli's treatment of Pagolo Vitelli is ambivalent. He is acknowledged as "a most prudent man" yet serves primarily as evidence for the thesis that mercenary captains are unreliable. His connection to Oliverotto adds a darker dimension — the military profession he represents is morally neutral, serving both the republic of Florence and the criminal ambitions of his protégé equally.
