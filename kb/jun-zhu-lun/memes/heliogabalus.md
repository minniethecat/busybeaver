# Heliogabalus

**Heliogabalus** (also known as Elagabalus) was a Roman emperor of the Severan dynasty whose brief and notorious reign (218–222) earned him a place among the contemptible rulers catalogued in Chapter 19 of *The Prince*.

## Overview

Heliogabalus appears in *The Prince* as one of the Roman emperors whom Machiavelli lists but declines to discuss in detail. He is mentioned in Chapter 19, "That One Should Avoid Being Despised and Hated," alongside other late Severan rulers whose reigns were marked by cruelty, effeminacy, and contempt from the army. Machiavelli names him explicitly — "Caracalla, Macrinus, Heliogabalus, Alexander, and Maximinus" (019-118) — but then sets him aside:

> I do not wish to discuss Heliogabalus, Macrinus, or Julian, who, being altogether contemptible, were quickly eliminated. (019-251)

## Historical Context

Heliogabalus became emperor at age 14, installed by the army after the defeat of [[macrinus|Macrinus]]. His reign was infamous for religious innovation — he attempted to replace the traditional Roman pantheon with the Syrian sun god Elagabal — and for personal excess that alienated the Praetorian Guard and the Senate. He was assassinated by the Praetorians in 222 at age 18, his body dragged through the streets and thrown into the Tiber.

## Role in The Prince

### A Contemptible Ruler

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli surveys the Roman emperors from Marcus Aurelius to Maximinus to illustrate his central argument: a prince must avoid being hated and despised. The emperors who succeeded — and those who failed — demonstrate the fatal consequences of contempt.

Heliogabalus belongs to the category of those who were "altogether contemptible" and "quickly eliminated" (019-251). Machiavelli does not elaborate on his specific failings, grouping him with Macrinus and Julian as rulers so obviously inadequate that they require no detailed analysis.

### The Severan Pattern

Heliogabalus represents the terminal phase of the Severan dynasty's descent. [[caracalla|Caracalla]] began the slide toward cruelty; [[macrinus|Macrinus]] briefly succeeded him; Heliogabalus accelerated the dynasty's collapse with his contemptible behavior; [[severus-alexander|Alexander Severus]] would be the last Severan emperor before the Crisis of the Third Century began.

## Machiavelli's Assessment

Machiavelli's judgment is implicit but clear: Heliogabalus was contemptible — that is, he failed to maintain the dignity and severity necessary for a prince to command respect. His elimination was swift and inevitable. For Machiavelli, the lesson is that a prince who makes himself contemptible cannot survive, regardless of his hereditary claim or military support.
