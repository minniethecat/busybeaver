# Flatterers

Flatterers represent one of the most insidious dangers a prince faces. Unlike armed enemies or conspirators, flatterers do not attack the prince's body but his judgment. Machiavelli devotes the entirety of Chapter 23 to this subject, arguing that the choice of counselors and the ability to avoid flattery are among the most important tests of princely wisdom.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Chapter 23 of *The Prince* — "How Flatterers Should Be Avoided" — provides Machiavelli's definitive analysis. His advice is practical and psychologically acute.

### 核心论点

- Courts are full of flatterers, because men are naturally pleased by praise and self-love makes them susceptible to it (023-003)
- The only way to guard against flatterers is to let men understand that telling the truth will not offend the prince — but this creates a new danger: if everyone can speak freely, the prince loses respect (023-008)
- The prudent solution is to choose wise counselors and give only them the liberty to speak truthfully — and only on matters the prince asks about, not on everything (023-020)
- A prince who is not himself wise cannot receive good counsel, because flatterers will inevitably dominate his court. Good counsel depends on the prince's own prudence, not the other way around
- The prince must be a constant inquirer and a patient listener; when he detects that a counselor is withholding truth out of fear, he must show displeasure

### 历史例证

- **Emperor Maximilian I** — the negative exemplar who never took counsel from anyone yet was constantly swayed by those around him, a contradiction that Machiavelli finds instructive
- **The Prudent Prince** — Machiavelli's ideal: a ruler who selects a small number of wise men, consults them on specific matters, but ultimately decides for himself

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Flatterers vs. Counselors**: Flatterers tell the prince what he wants to hear; counselors tell him what he needs to hear. The prince's ability to distinguish them is a function of his own wisdom.
- **Flatterers vs. Prudence**: Prudence is the prince's defense against flattery. A prudent prince recognizes flattery and values truth-telling.
- **Flatterers vs. Reputation**: A prince surrounded by flatterers loses touch with reality and makes catastrophic errors. His reputation for wisdom depends on avoiding this trap.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

The chapter on flatterers is one of the most psychologically subtle in The Prince. It reveals Machiavelli's understanding that political failure often stems not from external threats but from the cognitive distortions that power itself creates. The prince who cannot hear truth is already lost.

## 后世影响

Machiavelli's analysis of flattery influenced early modern advice literature for princes (the "mirror for princes" tradition) and the broader study of court politics. His insight that good counsel requires a wise prince — rather than wise counsel making a good prince — remains a foundational principle of leadership studies.
