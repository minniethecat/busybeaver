# Neutrality (Neutralità)

Neutrality (neutralità) in Machiavelli's strategic analysis is the worst possible course in a conflict between powerful neighbors. In Chapter 21, he argues that a prince who remains neutral earns the contempt of the victor and the ingratitude of the loser, while a decisive declaration wins respect and allies regardless of the outcome.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli's discussion of neutrality is concentrated in Chapter 21 — "How a Prince Should Conduct Himself in Order to Gain Reputation" — where it serves as a key piece of strategic advice.

### 核心论点

- When two powerful neighbors come to blows, a prince must declare himself decisively for one side — neutrality is the worst option because the victor will not respect a neutral and the loser will not thank one (021-055)
- The friend who wins will demand your neutrality despite your having declared for him — but at least you share his fortune; the friend who loses will receive you but you share his ruin
- A prince who declares himself openly for one side earns respect even in defeat, because he has shown himself a reliable ally who shares both fortune and misfortune
- Castruccio Castracani kept his promise of neutrality only so long as it served his interests, then acted when the moment favored decisive action (028-425)

### 历史例证

- **The Romans** — never remained neutral in the conflicts of their neighbors. They always intervened decisively, and this policy built their empire
- **Castruccio Castracani** — his neutrality toward the Lucchese was tactical and temporary; when the opportunity arose, he abandoned neutrality and seized control (028-425)
- **Florence** — Machiavelli's own city often pursued neutrality disastrously, finding itself at the mercy of victors who despised its fence-sitting

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Neutrality vs. Prudence**: Prudence might seem to counsel neutrality — avoiding costly commitments. But Machiavellian prudence recognizes that apparent safety is the greatest danger.
- **Neutrality vs. Virtù**: The prince of virtù acts decisively; the neutral prince merely reacts. Neutrality is the absence of virtù.
- **Neutrality vs. Fortune**: Fortune favors the bold. The neutral prince waits passively while fortune — which is a woman, and favors the young and impetuous — passes him by.
