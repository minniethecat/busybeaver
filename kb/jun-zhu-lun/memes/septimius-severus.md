# Septimius Severus

Septimius Severus was a Roman emperor who reigned from 193 to 211 CE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents him as a model of political prudence — a ruler who knew when to be fierce and when to be cunning, embodying the combined qualities of the lion and the fox.

## Rise to Power

Severus came to power during the Year of the Five Emperors (193 CE), following the assassination of Commodus and the brief reigns of Pertinax and Didius Julianus. Machiavelli notes that Severus, wishing to make himself master of the empire, had to contend with two rival claimants: Pescennius Niger in the east and Clodius Albinus in the west [019-186]. His strategy of defeating them one at a time — first Niger, then Albinus — demonstrated a calculated ruthlessness that Machiavelli admired.

## Role in *The Prince*

Machiavelli discusses Severus in Chapter 19, "That One Should Avoid Being Despised and Hated," as part of a broader analysis of Roman emperors. He contrasts Severus with his predecessors and successors:

> "In Severus there was so much valour that, keeping the soldiers friendly, he was able to reign happily" [019-171].

Machiavelli observes that Severus possessed the dual nature essential for a prince — the ferocity of the lion and the cunning of the fox [019-195]. Unlike Commodus, Pertinax, and other emperors who came to bad ends, Severus alone among them died naturally and left a stable succession.

### As an Exemplar of Virtù

Machiavelli presents Severus as an example of a "new prince" who seized power through his own ability. His key insight is that Severus understood the importance of managing both the army and the people simultaneously — satisfying the soldiers with rewards while keeping the populace from hating him [019-289].

## Historical Assessment

Severus successfully founded the Severan dynasty and expanded the empire's borders. Machiavelli notes that subsequent emperors tried to imitate him but failed because they lacked either his valour or his prudence [019-286]. For Machiavelli, Severus provides the template: a prince must "take from Severus those parts which are necessary to found his state" [019-290].
