**Occasione** (opportunity) is a key concept in Machiavelli's political philosophy, particularly developed in Chapter 6 of *The Prince*. It describes the favorable circumstances that allow a prince's **[[Virtù]]** to manifest and achieve great things. Without occasione, even the most able leader cannot succeed; but without virtù, opportunity passes unused.

## The Virtù–Occasione Pair

Machiavelli presents virtù and occasione as a complementary pair. Both are necessary for political success (006-034):

> [006-034] Without that opportunity their powers of mind would have been extinguished, and without those powers the opportunity would have come in vain.

He elaborates that opportunity provides the raw material — the situation that calls for action — while virtù provides the capacity to recognize and exploit it (006-033):

> [006-033] beyond opportunity, which brought them the material and gave them the opportunity to mould it into whatever form seemed best to them.

## The Great Founders

Machiavelli illustrates the virtù–occasione dynamic through the great founders of states and religions:

- **Moses** found the Israelites enslaved in Egypt — a people ready for liberation, which was his occasione
- **Cyrus** found the Persians discontented under Median rule
- **Romulus** was exposed at birth and driven from Alba — adversity that became his occasione to found Rome
- **Theseus** found the Athenians dispersed and vulnerable

Each of these founders recognized the opportunity that their historical moment offered (006-047):

> [006-047] enabled them to recognize the opportunity whereby their country was made glorious and most happy.

## Fortune vs. Opportunity

Machiavelli distinguishes between fortune and opportunity. Fortune is blind chance — the unpredictable currents of fate that no one controls. Opportunity is the specific, recognizable opening that fortune provides. Hiero of Syracuse illustrates a figure who owed almost nothing to fortune beyond the bare opportunity (006-091):

> [006-091] owe anything to fortune but opportunity; for the Syracusans, being oppressed, elected him captain, and from that it was but a short step to become their prince.

Hiero's virtù turned a single opportunity into enduring power, demonstrating that the capable prince needs only the smallest opening from fortune.

## Recognizing the Moment

A central challenge for the prince is recognizing when occasione has arrived. Machiavelli repeatedly emphasizes that opportunities are fleeting — they must be seized immediately or lost forever (004-053):

> [004-053] lost whenever time brings the opportunity.

The ability to perceive and act upon opportunity is itself a dimension of virtù. Princes fail not only through lack of ability but through failure to recognize the moment for action.

## The Final Exhortation (Chapter 26)

Machiavelli concludes *The Prince* by arguing that Italy in his own time presents the ultimate occasione — a nation oppressed, divided, and desperate for a liberator. He implores Lorenzo de' Medici to seize this opportunity as Moses, Cyrus, and Theseus seized theirs (026-034, 026-102):

> [026-034] each one of them had no more opportunity than the present offers, for the deliverance of Italy was more necessary than that of the Hebrews, more just than that of the Persians, and more admirable than that of the Athenians.

> [026-102] This opportunity, therefore, ought not to be allowed to pass, so that Italy may at length find her liberator.

## Relation to Other Concepts

- **[[Virtù]]** — the active capacity that seizes occasione
- **[[Fortuna]]** — fortune provides the raw material; occasione is the specific opening fortune offers
- **[[Necessity (Necessità)]]** — necessity creates the conditions that make occasione urgent
- **[[Prudence]]** — the practical judgment to recognize when occasione has arrived
