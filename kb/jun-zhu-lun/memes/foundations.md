# Foundations (Fondamenti)

Foundations (fondamenti) are the structural preconditions of stable rule in Machiavelli's political theory. A prince who builds on weak foundations will inevitably fall, regardless of his personal qualities. The metaphor of construction runs throughout The Prince: principalities are built on foundations, and the depth of those foundations determines whether the edifice withstands the storms of fortune.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Machiavelli's most concentrated discussion of foundations appears in Chapter 7, where he analyzes Cesare Borgia's career. The concept also appears in Chapter 6 and underpins the entire analysis of why some principalities endure while others collapse.

### 核心论点

- He who has not first laid his foundations may be able to lay them afterwards with great effort, but only at the cost of much trouble to the architect and danger to the building (007-051)
- Foundations consist of two elements: good laws and good arms. Without these, no state — whether principality or republic — can be secure
- A prince who rises rapidly through fortune (like Cesare Borgia) cannot lay his foundations quickly enough; those who rise through virtù (like Francesco Sforza) can build from the ground up (007-018, 007-022)
- The duke laid sufficiently good foundations for his future power, having controlled the Romagna, won the friendship of the Roman barons, and secured influence in the College of Cardinals (007-107)
- Even the firmest foundations may be tested by extreme fortune — Borgia's foundations were solid enough that only his illness at a critical moment prevented their completion (007-025, 007-175)

### 历史例证

- **Cesare Borgia** — the central case study. Machiavelli details how Borgia laid foundations step by step: subduing the Romagna, eliminating the Orsini, securing Roman support, and building his own army. These foundations were so firm that only extraordinary misfortune (his father's death coinciding with his own grave illness) could undo him (007-051, 007-055, 007-107, 007-175)
- **Hierarch of Syracuse** — rose from private station to king by laying his own foundations: disbanding the old militia, appointing new commanders, and building a loyal army (006-096)
- **Francesco Sforza** — the exemplar of foundations built through virtù rather than fortune; having become duke through his own arms and ability, he maintained his state with little difficulty

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Foundations vs. Arms**: Arms are one of the two pillars of foundations. Without good arms, no foundation is solid.
- **Foundations vs. Law**: Law is the second pillar. Good laws regularize rule and create predictable order.
- **Foundations vs. Fortune**: Fortune tests foundations. A prince on good foundations can withstand fortune's blows; one on weak foundations collapses at the first shock.
- **Foundations vs. Virtù**: Virtù is the architect that builds foundations. But even the greatest virtù cannot sustain a state indefinitely without structural supports.

### 在 Machiavelli 思想体系中的位置

Foundations represent the structural dimension of Machiavelli's political thought — the recognition that personal qualities alone are insufficient for stable rule. Institutions, laws, and arms are the permanent architecture of power. The prince who neglects foundations for personal glory builds a monument that will not outlast him.

## 后世影响

Machiavelli's emphasis on foundations — the institutional preconditions of stable rule — influenced the development of constitutional thought and the modern understanding of state-building. The concept anticipates later theories of institutional resilience and the distinction between personal and structural power.
