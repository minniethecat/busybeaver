# Saul

Saul was the first king of Israel in the biblical narrative. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli uses Saul's arming of David as a negative exemplum — a ruler who offered borrowed weapons that were rejected in favor of native arms, illustrating the folly of depending on forces that are not fully one's own.

## Role in *The Prince*

### Borrowed Weapons

In Chapter 13, "Concerning Auxiliaries, Mixed Soldiery, and One's Own," Machiavelli retells the familiar biblical episode to make a political argument:

> "David offered himself to Saul to fight with Goliath, the Philistine champion. And Saul, to give him courage, armed him with his own weapons; which David rejected as soon as he had them on his back" [013-064].

Saul's armor — heavy, unfamiliar, constraining — represents the arms of another. David's rejection of it is the key moment: Saul meant well, but his weapons were useless to David precisely because they were not David's own (013-065). For Machiavelli, Saul embodies the well-intentioned prince who nonetheless makes the fatal error of thinking borrowed strength can substitute for native capability.

### A Political Reading

Machiavelli's reading strips the biblical account of its theological dimension and recasts it as statecraft. Saul is not condemned — he is pitied as a ruler who failed to understand that no prince can be secure who relies on the arms of others. The lesson is directed at Machiavelli's Italian contemporaries, who habitually hired mercenary captains and sought foreign protection rather than arming their own citizens.

## Historical Assessment

Saul appears in *The Prince* only in relation to David, and Machiavelli's treatment is brief. The king's tragedy — providing arms that were rejected — serves as the negative backdrop against which David's wisdom shines. For Machiavelli, Saul is not a villain but a warning: a prince who, however powerful, cannot transfer his strength to another.
