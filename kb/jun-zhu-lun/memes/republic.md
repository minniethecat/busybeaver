# Republic

A republic (*repubblica*) is a form of government in which the people, or their representatives, exercise sovereign power. Though *The Prince* is a manual for princely rule, the republic is ever-present as the alternative political form — the one Machiavelli himself served and the one he analyzed at length in the *Discourses on Livy*.

## Republic vs. Principality

Machiavelli's state typology turns on the distinction between republics and principalities. The opening lines of *The Prince* declare: "All states, all powers that have held and hold rule over men, have been and are either republics or principalities" (001-001). A principality is ruled by one; a republic, by many. This simple division structures Machiavelli's entire political science.

The republic possesses virtues that the principality lacks. A free republic "flourishes" (P01-092) because its citizens have a stake in its survival. Republics grow more steadily than principalities because they harness the ambition of many rather than depending on the virtue of one. Yet republics also have characteristic weaknesses: they are slow to act, vulnerable to faction, and can be "crushed" by coalitions of princes, as Venice nearly was in the League of Cambrai.

## In Machiavelli's Career

Machiavelli's own political life was spent in service to the Florentine Republic (1498–1512). He "held an official post at Florence which included diplomatic missions to various European courts" (P01-038). When the Medici returned and the Republic fell, Machiavelli was "imprisoned in Florence, 1512; later exiled" (P01-039). *The Prince* itself was written in exile, partly as an attempt to win Medici favor — but it is impossible to read the urgent final chapter, exhorting a prince to liberate Italy, without hearing the voice of the republican patriot.

## The Republic in The Prince

Though *The Prince* focuses on princely rule, Machiavelli draws on republican examples throughout. The Roman Republic's military discipline, the Venetian Republic's institutional stability, and the Florentine Republic's vulnerability all inform his analysis. The republic is the unspoken counterpoint — the form of government that can survive without a single exceptional individual, provided it keeps its arms and its laws.
