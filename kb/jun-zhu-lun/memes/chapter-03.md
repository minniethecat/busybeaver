## CHAPTER III. CONCERNING MIXED PRINCIPALITIES

[003-001]But the difficulties occur in a new principality. And firstly, if it be

[003-002]not entirely new, but is, as it were, a member of a state which, taken

[003-003]collectively, may be called composite, the changes arise chiefly from an

[003-004]inherent difficulty which there is in all new principalities; for men

[003-005]change their rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and this hope

[003-006]induces them to take up arms against him who rules: wherein they are

[003-007]deceived, because they afterwards find by experience they have gone from

[003-008]bad to worse. This follows also on another natural and common necessity,

[003-009]which always causes a new prince to burden those who have submitted to him

[003-010]with his soldiery and with infinite other hardships which he must put upon

[003-011]his new acquisition.

[003-012]In this way you have enemies in all those whom you have injured in seizing

[003-013]that principality, and you are not able to keep those friends who put you

[003-014]there because of your not being able to satisfy them in the way they

[003-015]expected, and you cannot take strong measures against them, feeling bound

[003-016]to them. For, although one may be very strong in armed forces, yet in

[003-017]entering a province one has always need of the goodwill of the natives.

[003-018]For these reasons Louis the Twelfth, King of France, quickly occupied

[003-019]Milan, and as quickly lost it; and to turn him out the first time it only

[003-020]needed Lodovico’s own forces; because those who had opened the gates to

[003-021]him, finding themselves deceived in their hopes of future benefit, would

[003-022]not endure the ill-treatment of the new prince. It is very true that,

[003-023]after acquiring rebellious provinces a second time, they are not so

[003-024]lightly lost afterwards, because the prince, with little reluctance, takes

[003-025]the opportunity of the rebellion to punish the delinquents, to clear out

[003-026]the suspects, and to strengthen himself in the weakest places. Thus to

[003-027]cause France to lose Milan the first time it was enough for the Duke

[003-028]Lodovico[1]

[003-029]to raise insurrections on the borders; but to cause him to lose it a second

[003-030]time it was necessary to bring the whole world against him, and that his armies

[003-031]should be defeated and driven out of Italy; which followed from the causes

[003-032]above mentioned.

[003-033][1]

[003-034]Duke Lodovico was Lodovico Moro, a son of Francesco Sforza, who married

[003-035]Beatrice d’Este. He ruled over Milan from 1494 to 1500, and died in 1510.

[003-036]Nevertheless Milan was taken from France both the first and the second

[003-037]time. The general reasons for the first have been discussed; it remains to

[003-038]name those for the second, and to see what resources he had, and what any

[003-039]one in his situation would have had for maintaining himself more securely

[003-040]in his acquisition than did the King of France.

[003-041]Now I say that those dominions which, when acquired, are added to an

[003-042]ancient state by him who acquires them, are either of the same country and

[003-043]language, or they are not. When they are, it is easier to hold them,

[003-044]especially when they have not been accustomed to self-government; and to

[003-045]hold them securely it is enough to have destroyed the family of the prince

[003-046]who was ruling them; because the two peoples, preserving in other things

[003-047]the old conditions, and not being unlike in customs, will live quietly

[003-048]together, as one has seen in Brittany, Burgundy, Gascony, and Normandy,

[003-049]which have been bound to France for so long a time: and, although there

[003-050]may be some difference in language, nevertheless the customs are alike,

[003-051]and the people will easily be able to get on amongst themselves. He who

[003-052]has annexed them, if he wishes to hold them, has only to bear in mind two

[003-053]considerations: the one, that the family of their former lord is

[003-054]extinguished; the other, that neither their laws nor their taxes are

[003-055]altered, so that in a very short time they will become entirely one body

[003-056]with the old principality.

[003-057]But when states are acquired in a country differing in language, customs,

[003-058]or laws, there are difficulties, and good fortune and great energy are

[003-059]needed to hold them, and one of the greatest and most real helps would be

[003-060]that he who has acquired them should go and reside there. This would make

[003-061]his position more secure and durable, as it has made that of the Turk in

[003-062]Greece, who, notwithstanding all the other measures taken by him for

[003-063]holding that state, if he had not settled there, would not have been able

[003-064]to keep it. Because, if one is on the spot, disorders are seen as they

[003-065]spring up, and one can quickly remedy them; but if one is not at hand,

[003-066]they are heard of only when they are great, and then one can no longer

[003-067]remedy them. Besides this, the country is not pillaged by your officials;

[003-068]the subjects are satisfied by prompt recourse to the prince; thus, wishing

[003-069]to be good, they have more cause to love him, and wishing to be otherwise,

[003-070]to fear him. He who would attack that state from the outside must have the

[003-071]utmost caution; as long as the prince resides there it can only be wrested

[003-072]from him with the greatest difficulty.

[003-073]The other and better course is to send colonies to one or two places,

[003-074]which may be as keys to that state, for it is necessary either to do this

[003-075]or else to keep there a great number of cavalry and infantry. A prince

[003-076]does not spend much on colonies, for with little or no expense he can send

[003-077]them out and keep them there, and he offends a minority only of the

[003-078]citizens from whom he takes lands and houses to give them to the new

[003-079]inhabitants; and those whom he offends, remaining poor and scattered, are

[003-080]never able to injure him; whilst the rest being uninjured are easily kept

[003-081]quiet, and at the same time are anxious not to err for fear it should

[003-082]happen to them as it has to those who have been despoiled. In conclusion,

[003-083]I say that these colonies are not costly, they are more faithful, they

[003-084]injure less, and the injured, as has been said, being poor and scattered,

[003-085]cannot hurt. Upon this, one has to remark that men ought either to be well

[003-086]treated or crushed, because they can avenge themselves of lighter

[003-087]injuries, of more serious ones they cannot; therefore the injury that is

[003-088]to be done to a man ought to be of such a kind that one does not stand in

[003-089]fear of revenge.

[003-090]But in maintaining armed men there in place of colonies one spends much

[003-091]more, having to consume on the garrison all the income from the state, so

[003-092]that the acquisition turns into a loss, and many more are exasperated,

[003-093]because the whole state is injured; through the shifting of the garrison

[003-094]up and down all become acquainted with hardship, and all become hostile,

[003-095]and they are enemies who, whilst beaten on their own ground, are yet able

[003-096]to do hurt. For every reason, therefore, such guards are as useless as a

[003-097]colony is useful.

[003-098]Again, the prince who holds a country differing in the above respects

[003-099]ought to make himself the head and defender of his less powerful

[003-100]neighbours, and to weaken the more powerful amongst them, taking care that

[003-101]no foreigner as powerful as himself shall, by any accident, get a footing

[003-102]there; for it will always happen that such a one will be introduced by

[003-103]those who are discontented, either through excess of ambition or through

[003-104]fear, as one has seen already. The Romans were brought into Greece by the

[003-105]Ætolians; and in every other country where they obtained a footing they

[003-106]were brought in by the inhabitants. And the usual course of affairs is

[003-107]that, as soon as a powerful foreigner enters a country, all the subject

[003-108]states are drawn to him, moved by the hatred which they feel against the

[003-109]ruling power. So that in respect to those subject states he has not to

[003-110]take any trouble to gain them over to himself, for the whole of them

[003-111]quickly rally to the state which he has acquired there. He has only to

[003-112]take care that they do not get hold of too much power and too much

[003-113]authority, and then with his own forces, and with their goodwill, he can

[003-114]easily keep down the more powerful of them, so as to remain entirely

[003-115]master in the country. And he who does not properly manage this business

[003-116]will soon lose what he has acquired, and whilst he does hold it he will

[003-117]have endless difficulties and troubles.

[003-118]The Romans, in the countries which they annexed, observed closely these

[003-119]measures; they sent colonies and maintained friendly relations with[2]

[003-120]the minor powers, without increasing their strength; they kept down the

[003-121]greater, and did not allow any strong foreign powers to gain authority. Greece

[003-122]appears to me sufficient for an example. The Achaeans and Ætolians were kept

[003-123]friendly by them, the kingdom of Macedonia was humbled, Antiochus was driven

[003-124]out; yet the merits of the Achaeans and Ætolians never secured for them

[003-125]permission to increase their power, nor did the persuasions of Philip ever

[003-126]induce the Romans to be his friends without first humbling him, nor did the

[003-127]influence of Antiochus make them agree that he should retain any lordship over

[003-128]the country. Because the Romans did in these instances what all prudent princes

[003-129]ought to do, who have to regard not only present troubles, but also future

[003-130]ones, for which they must prepare with every energy, because, when foreseen, it

[003-131]is easy to remedy them; but if you wait until they approach, the medicine is no

[003-132]longer in time because the malady has become incurable; for it happens in this,

[003-133]as the physicians say it happens in hectic fever, that in the beginning of the

[003-134]malady it is easy to cure but difficult to detect, but in the course of time,

[003-135]not having been either detected or treated in the beginning, it becomes easy to

[003-136]detect but difficult to cure. Thus it happens in affairs of state, for when the

[003-137]evils that arise have been foreseen (which it is only given to a wise man to

[003-138]see), they can be quickly redressed, but when, through not having been

[003-139]foreseen, they have been permitted to grow in a way that every one can see

[003-140]them, there is no longer a remedy. Therefore, the Romans, foreseeing troubles,

[003-141]dealt with them at once, and, even to avoid a war, would not let them come to a

[003-142]head, for they knew that war is not to be avoided, but is only to be put off to

[003-143]the advantage of others; moreover they wished to fight with Philip and

[003-144]Antiochus in Greece so as not to have to do it in Italy; they could have

[003-145]avoided both, but this they did not wish; nor did that ever please them which

[003-146]is forever in the mouths of the wise ones of our time:—Let us enjoy the

[003-147]benefits of the time—but rather the benefits of their own valour and

[003-148]prudence, for time drives everything before it, and is able to bring with it

[003-149]good as well as evil, and evil as well as good.

[003-151]See remark in the introduction on the word “intrattenere.”

[003-152]But let us turn to France and inquire whether she has done any of the

[003-153]things mentioned. I will speak of Louis[3]

[003-154](and not of Charles)[4]

[003-155]as the one whose conduct is the better to be observed, he having held

[003-156]possession of Italy for the longest period; and you will see that he has done

[003-157]the opposite to those things which ought to be done to retain a state composed

[003-158]of divers elements.

[003-159][3]

[003-160]Louis XII, King of France, “The Father of the People,” born 1462,

[003-161]died 1515.

[003-162][4]

[003-163]Charles VIII, King of France, born 1470, died 1498.

[003-164]King Louis was brought into Italy by the ambition of the Venetians, who

[003-165]desired to obtain half the state of Lombardy by his intervention. I will

[003-166]not blame the course taken by the king, because, wishing to get a foothold

[003-167]in Italy, and having no friends there—seeing rather that every door

[003-168]was shut to him owing to the conduct of Charles—he was forced to

[003-169]accept those friendships which he could get, and he would have succeeded

[003-170]very quickly in his design if in other matters he had not made some

[003-171]mistakes. The king, however, having acquired Lombardy, regained at once

[003-172]the authority which Charles had lost: Genoa yielded; the Florentines

[003-173]became his friends; the Marquess of Mantua, the Duke of Ferrara, the

[003-174]Bentivogli, my lady of Forli, the Lords of Faenza, of Pesaro, of Rimini,

[003-175]of Camerino, of Piombino, the Lucchese, the Pisans, the Sienese—everybody

[003-176]made advances to him to become his friend. Then could the Venetians

[003-177]realize the rashness of the course taken by them, which, in order that

[003-178]they might secure two towns in Lombardy, had made the king master of

[003-179]two-thirds of Italy.

[003-180]Let any one now consider with what little difficulty the king could have

[003-181]maintained his position in Italy had he observed the rules above laid

[003-182]down, and kept all his friends secure and protected; for although they

[003-183]were numerous they were both weak and timid, some afraid of the Church,

[003-184]some of the Venetians, and thus they would always have been forced to

[003-185]stand in with him, and by their means he could easily have made himself

[003-186]secure against those who remained powerful. But he was no sooner in Milan

[003-187]than he did the contrary by assisting Pope Alexander to occupy the

[003-188]Romagna. It never occurred to him that by this action he was weakening

[003-189]himself, depriving himself of friends and of those who had thrown

[003-190]themselves into his lap, whilst he aggrandized the Church by adding much

[003-191]temporal power to the spiritual, thus giving it greater authority. And

[003-192]having committed this prime error, he was obliged to follow it up, so much

[003-193]so that, to put an end to the ambition of Alexander, and to prevent his

[003-194]becoming the master of Tuscany, he was himself forced to come into Italy.

[003-195]And as if it were not enough to have aggrandized the Church, and deprived

[003-196]himself of friends, he, wishing to have the kingdom of Naples, divided it

[003-197]with the King of Spain, and where he was the prime arbiter in Italy he

[003-198]takes an associate, so that the ambitious of that country and the

[003-199]malcontents of his own should have somewhere to shelter; and whereas he

[003-200]could have left in the kingdom his own pensioner as king, he drove him

[003-201]out, to put one there who was able to drive him, Louis, out in turn.

[003-202]The wish to acquire is in truth very natural and common, and men always do

[003-203]so when they can, and for this they will be praised not blamed; but when

[003-204]they cannot do so, yet wish to do so by any means, then there is folly and

[003-205]blame. Therefore, if France could have attacked Naples with her own forces

[003-206]she ought to have done so; if she could not, then she ought not to have

[003-207]divided it. And if the partition which she made with the Venetians in

[003-208]Lombardy was justified by the excuse that by it she got a foothold in

[003-209]Italy, this other partition merited blame, for it had not the excuse of

[003-210]that necessity.

[003-211]Therefore Louis made these five errors: he destroyed the minor powers, he

[003-212]increased the strength of one of the greater powers in Italy, he brought

[003-213]in a foreign power, he did not settle in the country, he did not send

[003-214]colonies. Which errors, had he lived, were not enough to injure him had he

[003-215]not made a sixth by taking away their dominions from the Venetians;

[003-216]because, had he not aggrandized the Church, nor brought Spain into Italy,

[003-217]it would have been very reasonable and necessary to humble them; but

[003-218]having first taken these steps, he ought never to have consented to their

[003-219]ruin, for they, being powerful, would always have kept off others from

[003-220]designs on Lombardy, to which the Venetians would never have consented

[003-221]except to become masters themselves there; also because the others would

[003-222]not wish to take Lombardy from France in order to give it to the

[003-223]Venetians, and to run counter to both they would not have had the courage.

[003-224]And if any one should say: “King Louis yielded the Romagna to Alexander

[003-225]and the kingdom to Spain to avoid war,” I answer for the reasons given

[003-226]above that a blunder ought never to be perpetrated to avoid war, because it is

[003-227]not to be avoided, but is only deferred to your disadvantage. And if another

[003-228]should allege the pledge which the king had given to the Pope that he would

[003-229]assist him in the enterprise, in exchange for the dissolution of his

[003-230]marriage[5]

[003-231]and for the cap to Rouen,[6]

[003-232]to that I reply what I shall write later on concerning the faith of princes,

[003-233]and how it ought to be kept.

[003-234][5]

[003-235]Louis XII divorced his wife, Jeanne, daughter of Louis XI, and married in 1499

[003-236]Anne of Brittany, widow of Charles VIII, in order to retain the Duchy of

[003-237]Brittany for the crown.

[003-238][6]

[003-239]The Archbishop of Rouen. He was Georges d’Amboise, created a cardinal by

[003-240]Alexander VI. Born 1460, died 1510.

[003-241]Thus King Louis lost Lombardy by not having followed any of the conditions

[003-242]observed by those who have taken possession of countries and wished to

[003-243]retain them. Nor is there any miracle in this, but much that is reasonable

[003-244]and quite natural. And on these matters I spoke at Nantes with Rouen, when

[003-245]Valentino, as Cesare Borgia, the son of Pope Alexander, was usually

[003-246]called, occupied the Romagna, and on Cardinal Rouen observing to me that

[003-247]the Italians did not understand war, I replied to him that the French did

[003-248]not understand statecraft, meaning that otherwise they would not have

[003-249]allowed the Church to reach such greatness. And in fact it has been seen

[003-250]that the greatness of the Church and of Spain in Italy has been caused by

[003-251]France, and her ruin may be attributed to them. From this a general rule

[003-252]is drawn which never or rarely fails: that he who is the cause of another

[003-253]becoming powerful is ruined; because that predominancy has been brought

[003-254]about either by astuteness or else by force, and both are distrusted by

[003-255]him who has been raised to power.
