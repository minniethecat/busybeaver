## CHAPTER IV. WHY THE KINGDOM OF DARIUS, CONQUERED BY ALEXANDER, DID NOT REBEL AGAINST THE
SUCCESSORS OF ALEXANDER AT HIS DEATH

[004-001]Considering the difficulties which men have had to hold to a newly

[004-002]acquired state, some might wonder how, seeing that Alexander the Great

[004-003]became the master of Asia in a few years, and died whilst it was scarcely

[004-004]settled (whence it might appear reasonable that the whole empire would

[004-005]have rebelled), nevertheless his successors maintained themselves, and had

[004-006]to meet no other difficulty than that which arose among themselves from

[004-007]their own ambitions.

[004-008]I answer that the principalities of which one has record are found to be

[004-009]governed in two different ways; either by a prince, with a body of

[004-010]servants, who assist him to govern the kingdom as ministers by his favour

[004-011]and permission; or by a prince and barons, who hold that dignity by

[004-012]antiquity of blood and not by the grace of the prince. Such barons have

[004-013]states and their own subjects, who recognize them as lords and hold them

[004-014]in natural affection. Those states that are governed by a prince and his

[004-015]servants hold their prince in more consideration, because in all the

[004-016]country there is no one who is recognized as superior to him, and if they

[004-017]yield obedience to another they do it as to a minister and official, and

[004-018]they do not bear him any particular affection.

[004-019]The examples of these two governments in our time are the Turk and the

[004-020]King of France. The entire monarchy of the Turk is governed by one lord,

[004-021]the others are his servants; and, dividing his kingdom into sanjaks, he

[004-022]sends there different administrators, and shifts and changes them as he

[004-023]chooses. But the King of France is placed in the midst of an ancient body

[004-024]of lords, acknowledged by their own subjects, and beloved by them; they

[004-025]have their own prerogatives, nor can the king take these away except at

[004-026]his peril. Therefore, he who considers both of these states will recognize

[004-027]great difficulties in seizing the state of the Turk, but, once it is

[004-028]conquered, great ease in holding it. The causes of the difficulties in

[004-029]seizing the kingdom of the Turk are that the usurper cannot be called in

[004-030]by the princes of the kingdom, nor can he hope to be assisted in his

[004-031]designs by the revolt of those whom the lord has around him. This arises

[004-032]from the reasons given above; for his ministers, being all slaves and

[004-033]bondmen, can only be corrupted with great difficulty, and one can expect

[004-034]little advantage from them when they have been corrupted, as they cannot

[004-035]carry the people with them, for the reasons assigned. Hence, he who

[004-036]attacks the Turk must bear in mind that he will find him united, and he

[004-037]will have to rely more on his own strength than on the revolt of others;

[004-038]but, if once the Turk has been conquered, and routed in the field in such

[004-039]a way that he cannot replace his armies, there is nothing to fear but the

[004-040]family of this prince, and, this being exterminated, there remains no one

[004-041]to fear, the others having no credit with the people; and as the conqueror

[004-042]did not rely on them before his victory, so he ought not to fear them

[004-043]after it.

[004-044]The contrary happens in kingdoms governed like that of France, because one

[004-045]can easily enter there by gaining over some baron of the kingdom, for one

[004-046]always finds malcontents and such as desire a change. Such men, for the

[004-047]reasons given, can open the way into the state and render the victory

[004-048]easy; but if you wish to hold it afterwards, you meet with infinite

[004-049]difficulties, both from those who have assisted you and from those you

[004-050]have crushed. Nor is it enough for you to have exterminated the family of

[004-051]the prince, because the lords that remain make themselves the heads of

[004-052]fresh movements against you, and as you are unable either to satisfy or

[004-053]exterminate them, that state is lost whenever time brings the opportunity.

[004-054]Now if you will consider what was the nature of the government of Darius,

[004-055]you will find it similar to the kingdom of the Turk, and therefore it was

[004-056]only necessary for Alexander, first to overthrow him in the field, and

[004-057]then to take the country from him. After which victory, Darius being

[004-058]killed, the state remained secure to Alexander, for the above reasons. And

[004-059]if his successors had been united they would have enjoyed it securely and

[004-060]at their ease, for there were no tumults raised in the kingdom except

[004-061]those they provoked themselves.

[004-062]But it is impossible to hold with such tranquillity states constituted

[004-063]like that of France. Hence arose those frequent rebellions against the

[004-064]Romans in Spain, France, and Greece, owing to the many principalities

[004-065]there were in these states, of which, as long as the memory of them

[004-066]endured, the Romans always held an insecure possession; but with the power

[004-067]and long continuance of the empire the memory of them passed away, and the

[004-068]Romans then became secure possessors. And when fighting afterwards amongst

[004-069]themselves, each one was able to attach to himself his own parts of the

[004-070]country, according to the authority he had assumed there; and the family

[004-071]of the former lord being exterminated, none other than the Romans were

[004-072]acknowledged.

[004-073]When these things are remembered no one will marvel at the ease with which

[004-074]Alexander held the Empire of Asia, or at the difficulties which others

[004-075]have had to keep an acquisition, such as Pyrrhus and many more; this is

[004-076]not occasioned by the little or abundance of ability in the conqueror, but

[004-077]by the want of uniformity in the subject state.
