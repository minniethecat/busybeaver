# Ramiro d'Orco

Ramiro d'Orco (also known as Ramiro de Lorqua; executed 26 December 1502) was a governor appointed by [[Cesare Borgia]] in the Romagna region. He appears in *The Prince* as an example of how a ruler may use a subordinate to impose cruel but necessary order, then sacrifice that subordinate to deflect public hatred.

## 生平概要

Ramiro d'Orco was known as "a swift and cruel man" (007-119). Cesare Borgia, having recently conquered the Romagna, found the region plagued by lawlessness and violence. To restore order, Borgia gave Ramiro "the fullest power" to govern the territory.

Under Ramiro's administration, peace and unity were restored with "the greatest success" (007-119). However, Borgia recognized that conferring such excessive authority risked making both himself and his governor odious to the populace.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli presents Ramiro d'Orco in [[Chapter 07]] as a central figure in Cesare Borgia's statecraft. The episode illustrates how a prudent ruler delegates harsh measures to a minister, then distances himself from the cruelty.

After Ramiro had restored order, Borgia established a civil court of judgment under a respected president, giving each city its own advocate. This administrative reform was designed to show that the previous severity had been Ramiro's doing, not the Duke's.

### 作为范例的角色

Ramiro d'Orco serves as a **negative instrument** in Machiavelli's political analysis. He is not portrayed as villainous in his own right, but rather as a tool whose fate demonstrates the cold calculus of princely rule:

> "Under this pretence he took Ramiro, and one morning caused him to be executed and left on the piazza at Cesena with the block and a bloody knife at his side." (007-129)

## 历史评价

> "The barbarity of this spectacle caused the people to be at once satisfied and dismayed." (007-129)

Machiavelli does not condemn Borgia for this act. Instead, he presents it as an example of effective political theater: the Duke simultaneously purged himself of an unpopular instrument, demonstrated that cruelty did not originate with him, and shocked the populace into a mixture of satisfaction and fear.

The Ramiro episode became a textbook case of [[Fear and Cruelty]] in Renaissance political thought — illustrating how a ruler may use cruelty well (to restore order) and then contain its backlash (by sacrificing the agent who applied it).
