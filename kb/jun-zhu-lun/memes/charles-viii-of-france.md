# Charles VIII of France

Charles VIII (1470–1498) was King of France from 1483 until his death. In *The Prince*, he appears as the predecessor whose poor record in Italy set the stage for [[Louis XII of France]]'s difficulties. It was Charles who "invaded Italy in 1494" (011-043), initiating the period of foreign interference that would shape Machiavelli's political world.

## 生平概要

Charles VIII's Italian expedition of 1494 was a watershed event. His army marched through the peninsula with unprecedented speed, toppling the Medici in [[Republic of Florence|Florence]] and briefly occupying Naples. However, his inability to consolidate his gains meant that his conquests evaporated almost as quickly as they were won.

Machiavelli's judgment is delivered through comparison with Louis XII. When Louis later entered Italy, "every door was shut to him owing to the conduct of Charles" (003-163). The Italian powers, having experienced Charles's invasion, were wary of French intentions. Louis had to "accept those friendships which he could get" (003-163) — a disadvantageous position inherited from his predecessor.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Charles appears primarily in [[Chapter 03]], where Machiavelli explains why Louis XII had such difficulty establishing himself in Italy. Louis "regained at once the authority which Charles had lost" (003-163) after acquiring Lombardy — a recovery that makes Louis's subsequent self-destruction all the more striking.

Charles is also a figure in the dynastic politics of the period. His widow, [[Anne of Brittany]], married Louis XII in 1499, a union that "retain[ed] the Duchy of Brittany for the crown" (003-236) but required the dissolution of Louis's prior marriage — a transaction with [[Pope Alexander VI]] that became one of Louis's fatal entanglements.

### 作为范例的角色

Charles VIII is a **shadow exemplar** in *The Prince* — a ruler whose negative example is felt indirectly, through the consequences his failures impose on his successor. He demonstrates, by absence, the qualities Machiavelli demands: the ability to maintain what has been acquired, rather than merely to seize it.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's treatment of Charles is brief but pointed. The king's legacy was to open Italy to centuries of foreign domination while leaving France weaker than before. His name serves as a benchmark for failure: Louis XII's initial recovery of "the authority which Charles had lost" makes the subsequent catalog of Louis's errors all the more educational, since it proves the position was salvageable.
