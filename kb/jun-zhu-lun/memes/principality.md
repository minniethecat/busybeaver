# Principality

The principality (principato) is the fundamental political unit of Machiavelli's analysis in *The Prince*. He opens the work by declaring that "all states, all powers, that have held and hold rule over men have been and are either republics or principalities" (001-001). The entire treatise is devoted to the latter.

## Taxonomy of Principalities

Machiavelli constructs a systematic classification that structures the book's first eleven chapters:

- **Hereditary principalities** (Ch. II): Ruled by a long-established dynasty, easiest to maintain
- **New principalities** (Ch. I–IX): Acquired by ability, fortune, crime, or popular consent
- **Mixed principalities** (Ch. III): Territories annexed to an existing hereditary state
- **Civil principalities** (Ch. IX): Where a private citizen becomes prince through the favor of fellow citizens
- **Ecclesiastical principalities** (Ch. XI): Ruled by the Church, held by religious authority rather than arms

## Principles of Rule

The core challenge of the principality is maintaining power after acquisition. Machiavelli argues that new principalities present the greatest difficulties: "men change their rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and this hope induces them to take up arms against him who rules: wherein they are deceived, because they afterwards find by experience they have gone from bad to worse" (003-004).

For mixed principalities, the prince must eliminate the former ruling line, avoid increasing taxes, and establish colonies rather than military garrisons (003-025). Colonies are cheaper and offend only the displaced few, whereas soldiers offend everyone they are quartered upon.
