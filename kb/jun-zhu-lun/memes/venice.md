# Venice

The **Republic of Venice** — La Serenissima — was Italy's most stable and prosperous state in the Renaissance, a maritime republic whose aristocratic constitution and imperial ambitions feature prominently in *The Prince*.

## Overview

Venice was unique among Italian states: an ancient republic governed by a hereditary merchant aristocracy, with a vast maritime empire stretching down the Adriatic coast. Machiavelli discusses Venice as both a constitutional model and a cautionary example. The republic's catastrophic defeat at the Battle of Vaila (Agnadello) in 1509 — "when Venice lost in one day all that she had taken eight hundred years to conquer" (P01-164) — illustrated the fatal weakness of a state that relied on mercenary arms.

## Political Context

### The Serene Republic

Venice's constitution was the most stable in Italy. Power was held by the Great Council of noble families, with the Doge as a ceremonial and executive figurehead. Machiavelli admired the republic's longevity but criticized its military dependence on mercenary captains (*condottieri*). In Chapter 12, he notes that Venice's hiring of the condottiero Carmagnola — "fighting for Venice against Sigismund, Duke of Austria" (012-127) — led to disaster when Carmagnola proved treacherous and had to be executed.

### The Italian Wars

Venice's ambition to expand onto the Italian mainland (*terraferma*) drew it into conflict with all the major powers. The League of Cambrai (1508), formed by [[pope-julius-ii|Pope Julius II]], France, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire, was designed to dismember the Venetian empire. The Battle of Vaila (1509) nearly destroyed the republic, though Venice survived through skilled diplomacy and the divisions among its enemies.

## Role in The Prince

### The Aristocratic Republic

In Chapter 5, Machiavelli discusses how to govern conquered republics. Venice — like Sparta and, implicitly, Rome — represents the type of state whose citizens are "accustomed to living under their own laws and in liberty," making conquest and retention particularly difficult. The Venetian nobility's deep-rooted attachment to their republican institutions meant that only destruction or residence by the conqueror could secure the state.

### The Condottiero Warning

Venice serves as Machiavelli's prime example of the dangers of mercenary arms. The republic's reliance on hired captains is contrasted with Rome's citizen-soldiers. Her reliance on mercenaries left her vulnerable to betrayal and defeat.

### The Balance of Power

Venice's diplomatic maneuvering appears repeatedly in Machiavelli's analysis. The Venetians brought [[louis-xii-of-france|Louis XII]] into Italy to further their own ambitions (003-164) — only to find themselves facing the combined power of the League of Cambrai. This illustrates Machiavelli's warning against introducing a powerful foreigner into one's neighborhood.
