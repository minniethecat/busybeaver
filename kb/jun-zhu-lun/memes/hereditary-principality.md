# Hereditary Principality

The hereditary principality is, in Machiavelli's taxonomy, a state where "the family has been long established" as the ruling line (001-003). It is the simplest form of principality to govern and maintain.

## Why Hereditary States Are Easier to Keep

Machiavelli devotes Chapter II to this argument. The key advantage is inertia: subjects are accustomed to the ruling family, and the prince need only avoid provoking them. "The hereditary prince has less cause and less necessity to offend" his subjects, so he is "more loved" (002-008). Even if an external power seizes the state temporarily, the usurper will find it nearly impossible to hold against the old dynasty's residual loyalty.

Louis XII of France learned this lesson in Milan: he "quickly occupied Milan, and as quickly lost it" because the people who had opened the gates found themselves deceived in their hopes (003-018). The hereditary loyalties to the Sforza family reasserted themselves at the first opportunity.

## Contrast with New Principalities

The hereditary principality serves as the baseline against which Machiavelli measures the greater difficulties of new principalities. Every other type — new, mixed, civil, ecclesiastical — requires active virtù to maintain; the hereditary prince coasts on custom and established affection.
