# Duke of Gravina

Francesco Orsini, the Duke of Gravina, was an Italian nobleman of the powerful Orsini family and a condottiero active during the Italian Wars. He is remembered in *The Prince* as one of the chief victims of Cesare Borgia's most famous act of political cunning: the massacre at Sinigaglia.

## The Orsini Conspiracy

The Orsini family, along with other noble houses, had initially supported Cesare Borgia in his campaign to conquer the Romagna. However, as Borgia's power grew, the conspirators grew alarmed and met at La Magione to plot against him. Machiavelli does not name Gravina individually in Chapter 7, but the Appendix on Duke Valentino identifies him among the leaders:

> "The cardinal, Pagolo, and the Duke di Gravina Orsini, Vitellozzo Vitelli, [and others]" met at Sinigaglia [027-013].

Borgia, feigning reconciliation, lured the conspirators to Sinigaglia under the pretense of a friendly meeting. Once they arrived:

> "The Duke di Gravina and Oliverotto should arrive" at the appointed place [027-118].

## The Massacre

When the conspirators entered the city, Borgia's soldiers seized them. Machiavelli's account describes the aftermath with chilling precision:

> "Vitellozzo, Pagolo, and the Duke di Gravina on mules, accompanied by a few" were led away [027-155].

Unlike Oliverotto and Vitellozzo, who were executed immediately, Gravina and Pagolo Orsini were kept alive temporarily:

> "Pagolo and the Duke di Gravina Orsini were kept alive until" Borgia received confirmation that Rome had been secured [027-198]. Once word arrived, they too were strangled.

## Role in *The Prince*

The Duke of Gravina serves as a minor but significant figure in Machiavelli's narrative. His death, along with the others at Sinigaglia, demonstrates the ruthlessness with which Cesare Borgia eliminated his rivals and consolidated power. For Machiavelli, the episode illustrates both the effectiveness of Borgia's methods and the fatal error of the conspirators: trusting a prince they had previously betrayed.

Borgia's ability to lure the Duke of Gravina and the other conspirators into a trap, despite their awareness of his ruthlessness, is presented as a masterclass in political deception.
