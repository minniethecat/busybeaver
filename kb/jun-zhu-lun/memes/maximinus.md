# Maximinus

Maximinus, also known as Maximinus Thrax, was a Roman emperor who reigned from 235 to 238 CE. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli presents him as an example of a ruler whose excessive cruelty and relentless warfare alienated his army, leading to his assassination.

## Rise to Power

Maximinus rose from humble origins as a Thracian shepherd to become a soldier of extraordinary physical strength. Machiavelli notes that after the army killed Alexander Severus, "they elected Maximinus to the empire" (019-237). His accession marked a dramatic break from the Severan dynasty and began the Crisis of the Third Century.

## Role in *The Prince*

### Cruelty Without Prudence

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli discusses Maximinus as part of his analysis of Roman emperors from Marcus Aurelius onward (019-116). He groups Maximinus with Commodus and Caracalla as rulers defined by cruelty:

> "Caracalla, and Maximinus, you will find them all cruel and rapacious" [019-168].

Machiavelli's judgment is clear: Maximinus was "a very warlike man" (019-235) whose constant campaigns exhausted his soldiers. Unlike Septimius Severus, who combined force with cunning, Maximinus relied solely on brute strength without cultivating loyalty.

### The Limits of Terror

The lesson Machiavelli draws is that cruelty must be tempered with prudence. Maximinus's relentless warfare — "he was a very warlike man" [019-235] — and his rapacious taxation turned both the army and the people against him. His fate demonstrates that a prince who is feared without being loved, and who gives his soldiers no rest, will eventually be overthrown.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli places Maximinus alongside Heliogabalus, Alexander, and other emperors of the third century (019-118). Later rulers who "imitated Severus" — among whom Machiavelli counts Caracalla, Commodus, and Maximinus — failed because they lacked Severus's prudence despite matching his severity (019-286). For Machiavelli, Maximinus exemplifies the danger of excessive military ambition unaccompanied by political skill.
