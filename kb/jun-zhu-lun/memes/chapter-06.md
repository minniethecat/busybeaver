## CHAPTER VI. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES WHICH ARE ACQUIRED BY ONE’S OWN ARMS AND
ABILITY

[006-001]Let no one be surprised if, in speaking of entirely new principalities as

[006-002]I shall do, I adduce the highest examples both of prince and of state;

[006-003]because men, walking almost always in paths beaten by others, and

[006-004]following by imitation their deeds, are yet unable to keep entirely to the

[006-005]ways of others or attain to the power of those they imitate. A wise man

[006-006]ought always to follow the paths beaten by great men, and to imitate those

[006-007]who have been supreme, so that if his ability does not equal theirs, at

[006-008]least it will savour of it. Let him act like the clever archers who,

[006-009]designing to hit the mark which yet appears too far distant, and knowing

[006-010]the limits to which the strength of their bow attains, take aim much

[006-011]higher than the mark, not to reach by their strength or arrow to so great

[006-012]a height, but to be able with the aid of so high an aim to hit the mark

[006-013]they wish to reach.

[006-014]I say, therefore, that in entirely new principalities, where there is a

[006-015]new prince, more or less difficulty is found in keeping them, accordingly

[006-016]as there is more or less ability in him who has acquired the state. Now,

[006-017]as the fact of becoming a prince from a private station presupposes either

[006-018]ability or fortune, it is clear that one or other of these things will

[006-019]mitigate in some degree many difficulties. Nevertheless, he who has relied

[006-020]least on fortune is established the strongest. Further, it facilitates

[006-021]matters when the prince, having no other state, is compelled to reside

[006-022]there in person.

[006-023]But to come to those who, by their own ability and not through fortune,

[006-024]have risen to be princes, I say that Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus, and

[006-025]such like are the most excellent examples. And although one may not

[006-026]discuss Moses, he having been a mere executor of the will of God, yet he

[006-027]ought to be admired, if only for that favour which made him worthy to

[006-028]speak with God. But in considering Cyrus and others who have acquired or

[006-029]founded kingdoms, all will be found admirable; and if their particular

[006-030]deeds and conduct shall be considered, they will not be found inferior to

[006-031]those of Moses, although he had so great a preceptor. And in examining

[006-032]their actions and lives one cannot see that they owed anything to fortune

[006-033]beyond opportunity, which brought them the material to mould into the form

[006-034]which seemed best to them. Without that opportunity their powers of mind

[006-035]would have been extinguished, and without those powers the opportunity

[006-036]would have come in vain.

[006-037]It was necessary, therefore, to Moses that he should find the people of

[006-038]Israel in Egypt enslaved and oppressed by the Egyptians, in order that

[006-039]they should be disposed to follow him so as to be delivered out of

[006-040]bondage. It was necessary that Romulus should not remain in Alba, and that

[006-041]he should be abandoned at his birth, in order that he should become King

[006-042]of Rome and founder of the fatherland. It was necessary that Cyrus should

[006-043]find the Persians discontented with the government of the Medes, and the

[006-044]Medes soft and effeminate through their long peace. Theseus could not have

[006-045]shown his ability had he not found the Athenians dispersed. These

[006-046]opportunities, therefore, made those men fortunate, and their high ability

[006-047]enabled them to recognize the opportunity whereby their country was

[006-048]ennobled and made famous.

[006-049]Those who by valorous ways become princes, like these men, acquire a

[006-050]principality with difficulty, but they keep it with ease. The difficulties

[006-051]they have in acquiring it rise in part from the new rules and methods

[006-052]which they are forced to introduce to establish their government and its

[006-053]security. And it ought to be remembered that there is nothing more

[006-054]difficult to take in hand, more perilous to conduct, or more uncertain in

[006-055]its success, than to take the lead in the introduction of a new order of

[006-056]things, because the innovator has for enemies all those who have done well

[006-057]under the old conditions, and lukewarm defenders in those who may do well

[006-058]under the new. This coolness arises partly from fear of the opponents, who

[006-059]have the laws on their side, and partly from the incredulity of men, who

[006-060]do not readily believe in new things until they have had a long experience

[006-061]of them. Thus it happens that whenever those who are hostile have the

[006-062]opportunity to attack they do it like partisans, whilst the others defend

[006-063]lukewarmly, in such wise that the prince is endangered along with them.

[006-064]It is necessary, therefore, if we desire to discuss this matter

[006-065]thoroughly, to inquire whether these innovators can rely on themselves or

[006-066]have to depend on others: that is to say, whether, to consummate their

[006-067]enterprise, have they to use prayers or can they use force? In the first

[006-068]instance they always succeed badly, and never compass anything; but when

[006-069]they can rely on themselves and use force, then they are rarely

[006-070]endangered. Hence it is that all armed prophets have conquered, and the

[006-071]unarmed ones have been destroyed. Besides the reasons mentioned, the

[006-072]nature of the people is variable, and whilst it is easy to persuade them,

[006-073]it is difficult to fix them in that persuasion. And thus it is necessary

[006-074]to take such measures that, when they believe no longer, it may be

[006-075]possible to make them believe by force.

[006-076]If Moses, Cyrus, Theseus, and Romulus had been unarmed they could not have

[006-077]enforced their constitutions for long—as happened in our time to Fra

[006-078]Girolamo Savonarola, who was ruined with his new order of things

[006-079]immediately the multitude believed in him no longer, and he had no means

[006-080]of keeping steadfast those who believed or of making the unbelievers to

[006-081]believe. Therefore such as these have great difficulties in consummating

[006-082]their enterprise, for all their dangers are in the ascent, yet with

[006-083]ability they will overcome them; but when these are overcome, and those

[006-084]who envied them their success are exterminated, they will begin to be

[006-085]respected, and they will continue afterwards powerful, secure, honoured,

[006-086]and happy.

[006-087]To these great examples I wish to add a lesser one; still it bears some

[006-088]resemblance to them, and I wish it to suffice me for all of a like kind: it is

[006-089]Hiero the Syracusan.[1]

[006-090]This man rose from a private station to be Prince of Syracuse, nor did he,

[006-091]either, owe anything to fortune but opportunity; for the Syracusans, being

[006-092]oppressed, chose him for their captain, afterwards he was rewarded by being

[006-093]made their prince. He was of so great ability, even as a private citizen, that

[006-094]one who writes of him says he wanted nothing but a kingdom to be a king. This

[006-095]man abolished the old soldiery, organized the new, gave up old alliances, made

[006-096]new ones; and as he had his own soldiers and allies, on such foundations he was

[006-097]able to build any edifice: thus, whilst he had endured much trouble in

[006-098]acquiring, he had but little in keeping.

[006-099][1]

[006-100]Hiero II, born about 307 B.C., died 216 B.C.
