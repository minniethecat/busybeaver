## DEDICATION

[P02-001]To the Magnificent Lorenzo Di Piero De’ Medici

[P02-002]Those who strive to obtain the good graces of a prince are accustomed to come

[P02-003]before him with such things as they hold most precious, or in which they see

[P02-004]him take most delight; whence one often sees horses, arms, cloth of gold,

[P02-005]precious stones, and similar ornaments presented to princes, worthy of their

[P02-006]greatness.

[P02-007]Desiring therefore to present myself to your Magnificence with some testimony

[P02-008]of my devotion towards you, I have not found among my possessions anything

[P02-009]which I hold more dear than, or value so much as, the knowledge of the actions

[P02-010]of great men, acquired by long experience in contemporary affairs, and a

[P02-011]continual study of antiquity; which, having reflected upon it with great and

[P02-012]prolonged diligence, I now send, digested into a little volume, to your

[P02-013]Magnificence.

[P02-014]And although I may consider this work unworthy of your countenance,

[P02-015]nevertheless I trust much to your benignity that it may be acceptable, seeing

[P02-016]that it is not possible for me to make a better gift than to offer you the

[P02-017]opportunity of understanding in the shortest time all that I have learnt in so

[P02-018]many years, and with so many troubles and dangers; which work I have not

[P02-019]embellished with swelling or magnificent words, nor stuffed with rounded

[P02-020]periods, nor with any extrinsic allurements or adornments whatever, with which

[P02-021]so many are accustomed to embellish their works; for I have wished either that

[P02-022]no honour should be given it, or else that the truth of the matter and the

[P02-023]weightiness of the theme shall make it acceptable.

[P02-024]Nor do I hold with those who regard it as a presumption if a man of low and

[P02-025]humble condition dare to discuss and settle the concerns of princes; because,

[P02-026]just as those who draw landscapes place themselves below in the plain to

[P02-027]contemplate the nature of the mountains and of lofty places, and in order to

[P02-028]contemplate the plains place themselves upon high mountains, even so to

[P02-029]understand the nature of the people it needs to be a prince, and to understand

[P02-030]that of princes it needs to be of the people.

[P02-031]Take then, your Magnificence, this little gift in the spirit in which I send

[P02-032]it; wherein, if it be diligently read and considered by you, you will learn my

[P02-033]extreme desire that you should attain that greatness which fortune and your

[P02-034]other attributes promise. And if your Magnificence from the summit of your

[P02-035]greatness will sometimes turn your eyes to these lower regions, you will see

[P02-036]how unmeritedly I suffer a great and continued malignity of fortune.
