# Didius Julianus

Didius Julianus was a Roman emperor who reigned for only nine weeks in 193 CE during the Year of the Five Emperors. In *The Prince*, Machiavelli mentions him as an example of weakness — a ruler whose sloth allowed a more determined rival to seize power.

## Rise and Fall

After the Praetorian Guard assassinated Pertinax, they infamously auctioned the imperial throne to the highest bidder. Didius Julianus won the bidding and was proclaimed emperor. However, his authority was hollow. Machiavelli describes the decisive moment:

> "Knowing the sloth of the Emperor Julian, he persuaded the army in Illyria to elect him emperor and killed Julian" [019-179].

The "he" is Septimius Severus, who marched on Rome, deposed Julianus, and had him executed after a reign of just sixty-six days (019-185).

## Role in *The Prince*

### Weakness as Fatal Flaw

In Chapter 19, Machiavelli lists Julianus among the succession of emperors from Marcus Aurelius to Maximinus (019-117). He groups Julianus with Heliogabalus and Macrinus as rulers "who, being altogether contemptible, were quickly destroyed" (019-251). For Machiavelli, Julianus's fate illustrates a cardinal principle: a prince who is despised — whether for sloth, ineptitude, or lack of martial virtue — will not survive.

### Contrast with Severus

The juxtaposition of Julianus with Severus is instructive. Where Julianus attempted to rule by purchase and lacked military authority, Severus acted with decisive force. Machiavelli uses this contrast to reinforce his argument that a prince must possess sufficient virtù to command respect from the army and the people.

## Historical Assessment

Machiavelli accords Julianus little attention — he is merely one name in the roll call of failed emperors. The speed of his fall (019-179, 019-185) demonstrates that in politics, money without arms is worthless. His example supports Machiavelli's broader thesis: a prince who cannot defend himself with his own forces will inevitably fall to someone who can.
