# Italy

**Italy** in Machiavelli's time was not a unified nation but a fragmented peninsula of city-states, kingdoms, republics, and papal territories — the primary theater of the political struggles analyzed in *The Prince*.

## Overview

Italy in the early 16th century was the battleground of Europe. The peninsula was divided among five major powers — the [[republic-of-venice|Republic of Venice]], the Duchy of [[milan|Milan]], the [[republic-of-florence|Republic of Florence]], the [[papal-states|Papal States]], and the Kingdom of [[naples|Naples]] — along with numerous smaller principalities. Foreign powers, particularly [[france|France]] and [[spain|Spain]], repeatedly invaded, exploiting the divisions among Italian states.

Machiavelli's entire political project revolves around Italy's condition. *The Prince* opens by cataloguing the types of principalities using Italian examples — "We have in Italy, for example, the Duke of Ferrara" (002-013) — and closes with the famous exhortation to liberate Italy from the barbarians.

## Political Context

### The Five Powers

Italy's political landscape was dominated by five major states. The Kingdom of Naples controlled the south; the Papal States stretched across the center; the Republic of Florence and Duchy of Milan contested the north-center; and the Republic of Venice held the northeast with its maritime empire.

The smaller states — Ferrara, Mantua, Siena, Lucca, Perugia — survived by balancing among the larger powers. This fragmentation made Italy "the prey of whoever has attacked her" — a condition Machiavelli diagnoses as stemming from the lack of a unified native military.

### Foreign Invasions

The French invasion of 1494 under [[charles-viii-of-france|Charles VIII]] shattered the Italian balance. Machiavelli traces how "King Louis was brought into Italy by the ambition of the Venetians" (003-164), and how each foreign intervention deepened Italy's dependence on outside powers. Spain, under [[ferdinand-of-aragon|Ferdinand of Aragon]], established its dominance in Naples and Sicily.

## Role in The Prince

### The Divided Peninsula

Machiavelli uses Italy's divisions as a cautionary example throughout *The Prince*. In Chapter 3, he analyzes Louis XII's errors in Italy: the French king "was driven out of Italy" (003-031) because he failed to respect the balance of powers and alienated his allies. The "greatness of the Church and of Spain in Italy" (003-250) grew precisely because Italian states invited foreign intervention to settle their quarrels.

### The Liberation Exhortation

Chapter 26, "An Exhortation to Liberate Italy from the Barbarians," is *The Prince*'s climactic call to arms. Machiavelli argues that Italy is "more enslaved than the Hebrews, more oppressed than the Persians, more scattered than the Athenians" — a land "without a head, without order, beaten, despoiled, torn, overrun." The chapter envisions a native prince, armed with his own forces, who would unite the peninsula and expel the foreigners.

## Related Events

- The French invasion of 1494: Charles VIII marched through Italy and seized Naples without resistance
- The League of Cambrai (1508): Venice lost most of its mainland possessions in a single day at the Battle of Vaila (Agnadello)
- The Spanish consolidation: Ferdinand of Aragon secured Naples, making Spain the dominant power in Italy
