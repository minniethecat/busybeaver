## CHAPTER XVIII. CONCERNING THE WAY IN WHICH PRINCES SHOULD KEEP FAITH

[1]

[018-001]“The present chapter has given greater offence than any other portion of

[018-002]Machiavelli’s writings.” Burd, “Il Principe,” p. 297.

[018-003]Every one admits how praiseworthy it is in a prince to keep faith, and to live

[018-004]with integrity and not with craft. Nevertheless our experience has been that

[018-005]those princes who have done great things have held good faith of little

[018-006]account, and have known how to circumvent the intellect of men by craft, and in

[018-007]the end have overcome those who have relied on their word. You must know there

[018-008]are two ways of contesting,[2]

[018-009]the one by the law, the other by force; the first method is proper to men, the

[018-010]second to beasts; but because the first is frequently not sufficient, it is

[018-011]necessary to have recourse to the second. Therefore it is necessary for a

[018-012]prince to understand how to avail himself of the beast and the man. This has

[018-013]been figuratively taught to princes by ancient writers, who describe how

[018-014]Achilles and many other princes of old were given to the Centaur Chiron to

[018-015]nurse, who brought them up in his discipline; which means solely that, as they

[018-016]had for a teacher one who was half beast and half man, so it is necessary for a

[018-017]prince to know how to make use of both natures, and that one without the other

[018-018]is not durable. A prince, therefore, being compelled knowingly to adopt the

[018-019]beast, ought to choose the fox and the lion; because the lion cannot defend

[018-020]himself against snares and the fox cannot defend himself against wolves.

[018-021]Therefore, it is necessary to be a fox to discover the snares and a lion to

[018-022]terrify the wolves. Those who rely simply on the lion do not understand what

[018-023]they are about. Therefore a wise lord cannot, nor ought he to, keep faith when

[018-024]such observance may be turned against him, and when the reasons that caused him

[018-025]to pledge it exist no longer. If men were entirely good this precept would not

[018-026]hold, but because they are bad, and will not keep faith with you, you too are

[018-027]not bound to observe it with them. Nor will there ever be wanting to a prince

[018-028]legitimate reasons to excuse this non-observance. Of this endless modern

[018-029]examples could be given, showing how many treaties and engagements have been

[018-030]made void and of no effect through the faithlessness of princes; and he who has

[018-031]known best how to employ the fox has succeeded best.

[018-032][2]

[018-033]“Contesting,” *i.e*. “striving for mastery.” Mr

[018-034]Burd points out that this passage is imitated directly from Cicero’s

[018-035]“De Officiis”: “Nam cum sint duo genera decertandi, unum per

[018-036]disceptationem, alterum per vim; cumque illud proprium sit hominis, hoc

[018-037]beluarum; confugiendum est ad posterius, si uti non licet superiore.”

[018-038]But it is necessary to know well how to disguise this characteristic, and to be

[018-039]a great pretender and dissembler; and men are so simple, and so subject to

[018-040]present necessities, that he who seeks to deceive will always find someone who

[018-041]will allow himself to be deceived. One recent example I cannot pass over in

[018-042]silence. Alexander the Sixth did nothing else but deceive men, nor ever thought

[018-043]of doing otherwise, and he always found victims; for there never was a man who

[018-044]had greater power in asserting, or who with greater oaths would affirm a thing,

[018-045]yet would observe it less; nevertheless his deceits always succeeded according

[018-046]to his wishes,[3] because he well understood this side of mankind.

[018-047][3]

[018-048]“Nondimanco sempre gli succederono gli inganni (ad votum).” The

[018-049]words “ad votum” are omitted in the Testina addition, 1550.  Alexander never did what he said, Cesare never said what he did.  Italian Proverb.

[018-050]Therefore it is unnecessary for a prince to have all the good qualities I

[018-051]have enumerated, but it is very necessary to appear to have them. And I

[018-052]shall dare to say this also, that to have them and always to observe them

[018-053]is injurious, and that to appear to have them is useful; to appear

[018-054]merciful, faithful, humane, religious, upright, and to be so, but with a

[018-055]mind so framed that should you require not to be so, you may be able and

[018-056]know how to change to the opposite.

[018-057]And you have to understand this, that a prince, especially a new one, cannot

[018-058]observe all those things for which men are esteemed, being often forced, in

[018-059]order to maintain the state, to act contrary to fidelity,[4]

[018-060]friendship, humanity, and religion. Therefore it is necessary for him to have a

[018-061]mind ready to turn itself accordingly as the winds and variations of fortune

[018-062]force it, yet, as I have said above, not to diverge from the good if he can

[018-063]avoid doing so, but, if compelled, then to know how to set about it.

[018-064][4]

[018-065]“Contrary to fidelity” or “faith,” “contro alla

[018-066]fede,” and “tutto fede,” “altogether faithful,”

[018-067]in the next paragraph. It is noteworthy that these two phrases, “contro

[018-068]alla fede” and “tutto fede,” were omitted in the Testina

[018-069]edition, which was published with the sanction of the papal authorities. It may

[018-070]be that the meaning attached to the word “fede” was “the

[018-071]faith,” *i.e*. the Catholic creed, and not as rendered here

[018-072]“fidelity” and “faithful.” Observe that the word

[018-073]“religione” was suffered to stand in the text of the Testina, being

[018-074]used to signify indifferently every shade of belief, as witness “the

[018-075]religion,” a phrase inevitably employed to designate the Huguenot heresy.

[018-076]South in his Sermon IX, p. 69, ed. 1843, comments on this passage as follows:

[018-077]“That great patron and Coryphaeus of this tribe, Nicolo Machiavel, laid

[018-078]down this for a master rule in his political scheme: ‘That the show of

[018-079]religion was helpful to the politician, but the reality of it hurtful and

[018-080]pernicious.’”

[018-081]For this reason a prince ought to take care that he never lets anything

[018-082]slip from his lips that is not replete with the above-named five

[018-083]qualities, that he may appear to him who sees and hears him altogether

[018-084]merciful, faithful, humane, upright, and religious. There is nothing more

[018-085]necessary to appear to have than this last quality, inasmuch as men judge

[018-086]generally more by the eye than by the hand, because it belongs to

[018-087]everybody to see you, to few to come in touch with you. Every one sees

[018-088]what you appear to be, few really know what you are, and those few dare

[018-089]not oppose themselves to the opinion of the many, who have the majesty of

[018-090]the state to defend them; and in the actions of all men, and especially of

[018-091]princes, which it is not prudent to challenge, one judges by the result.

[018-092]For that reason, let a prince have the credit of conquering and holding

[018-093]his state, the means will always be considered honest, and he will be

[018-094]praised by everybody; because the vulgar are always taken by what a thing

[018-095]seems to be and by what comes of it; and in the world there are only the

[018-096]vulgar, for the few find a place there only when the many have no ground

[018-097]to rest on.

[018-098]One prince[5]

[018-099]of the present time, whom it is not well to name, never preaches anything else

[018-100]but peace and good faith, and to both he is most hostile, and either, if he had

[018-101]kept it, would have deprived him of reputation and kingdom many a time.

[018-102][5]

[018-103]Ferdinand of Aragon. “When Machiavelli was writing *The Prince* it

[018-104]would have been clearly impossible to mention Ferdinand’s name here

[018-105]without giving offence.” Burd’s “Il Principe,” p. 308.
