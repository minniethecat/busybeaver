In *The Prince*, Machiavelli devotes a substantial portion of Chapter 20 to the question of **fortresses**: whether a prince should build them for security, or whether they offer a false sense of protection. His conclusion — that the best fortress is the goodwill of the people — is one of the most frequently cited passages in the work.

## Chapter 20: The Fortress Question

Machiavelli frames the question historically. Different princes have adopted different strategies (020-005):

> [020-005] some have built fortresses; some have overthrown and destroyed them.

He notes that the value of fortresses depends on circumstances, particularly whether a prince fears his own people more than external enemies (020-036):

> [020-036] fortresses; and with this idea they formed their opinion that they were injurious rather than serviceable.

## The Best Fortress

Machiavelli delivers his definitive judgment near the chapter's end. The most secure fortification is not made of stone, but of popular support (020-103):

> [020-103] this reason the best possible fortress is—not to be hated by the people.

He elaborates that even physical fortresses cannot protect a prince from a hostile population (020-104):

> [020-104] although you may hold the fortresses, yet they will not save you if the people hate you.

## When Fortresses Are Useful

Machiavelli does not dismiss fortresses entirely. They can serve specific purposes when a prince faces rivals or seeks to consolidate newly acquired territory. He describes the case of the Countess of Forlì, who relied on a fortress when popular sentiment turned against her after the death of her husband. However, even in such cases, the fortress only delays the inevitable without popular backing.

## Fortresses as a Bridle

Some rulers build fortresses as a bridle on their own subjects, using them as bases of intimidation (020-088):

> [020-088] securely, to build fortresses that may serve as a bridle on those who might seek to act against you.

But Machiavelli views this strategy as ultimately self-defeating — it signals mistrust and tends to generate the very opposition it seeks to suppress.

## Destroying Fortresses

Machiavelli records cases where rulers deliberately demolished fortresses to signal their confidence in popular support. Cesare Borgia's lieutenant Ramiro d'Orco destroyed fortresses after pacifying the Romagna. Francesco Sforza similarly demolished fortresses in territories he had won (020-092):

> [020-092] demolish two fortresses in Citta di Castello so that the town might be held by the affection of the people.

## Historical Judgment

Looking across the historical record, Machiavelli notes the limited utility of fortresses in his own era (020-107):

> [020-107] seen in our times that such fortresses have been of use to any prince.

He concludes that a prince who relies on fortresses over popular support has chosen the weaker foundation (020-116):

> [020-116] had the fortresses. All these things considered then, I shall praise him who builds fortresses as well as him who does not, but I blame whoever, trusting in them, cares little about being hated by the people.
