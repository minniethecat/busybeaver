One of Machiavelli's most controversial teachings concerns the obligation of a prince to keep his word. In Chapter 18 of *The Prince*, he argues that while faithfulness is praiseworthy, experience shows that princes who have achieved great things have often known how to manipulate trust and break faith when circumstances demanded it.

## The Central Doctrine (Chapter 18)

Machiavelli begins by acknowledging the conventional view (018-001):

> How laudable it is for a prince to keep good faith and to live with integrity and not with craft, every one knows.

But he immediately pivots to the realities of political life:

> Nevertheless our experience has been that those princes who have done great things have held good faith of little account, and have known how to circumvent the intellect of men by craft, and in the end have overcome those who have relied on their word.

## The Fox and the Lion

A prince, Machiavelli argues, must be both **fox and lion**. The lion is strong enough to frighten wolves, but the fox is clever enough to recognize snares. A prince who keeps faith blindly is like a lion without a fox's cunning — vulnerable to deception by others.

This metaphor has two dimensions:
- **The lion** — strength, directness, the capacity for force
- **The fox** — cunning, deception, the capacity to recognize and avoid traps

Machiavelli insists that a prince must be capable of both: force when straightforward action is possible, cunning when the situation requires deception.

## Appearing Faithful

A crucial element of the doctrine is that a prince must **appear** faithful even when acting faithlessly. Men judge by appearances, and as long as the prince successfully projects an image of integrity, he will escape condemnation.

The pope whom Machiavelli singles out for special mention in this context is Alexander VI, who "never did anything else but deceive men" and yet found continual success precisely because men were so willing to believe his professions of faith.

## The Limits of Faithlessness

Machiavelli does not counsel unlimited deception. A prince must calculate when to break faith. The breach must be:
1. **Strategic** — serving a clear political purpose
2. **Plausibly deniable** — the prince must be able to justify or conceal the action
3. **Proportionate** — excessive deception destroys the prince's reputation and makes future dealings impossible

A prince who is known as faithless loses all diplomatic credibility, and his promises become worthless even to potential allies.

## Connection to the Fear vs. Love Calculus

The doctrine of faith and faithlessness is deeply connected to the question of fear and love. A prince who is feared can more easily break faith when necessary, because the fear he inspires deters retaliation. A prince who is merely loved is more constrained, because his support depends on voluntary goodwill that deception erodes.
