# Pandolfo Petrucci

Pandolfo Petrucci (1452–1512) was the de facto ruler of Siena from 1487 until his death. He appears in *The Prince* in two distinct contexts: as an illustration of how a prince can profitably employ former enemies, and as proof that a ruler who knows how to choose good servants is wise — even if he is not himself a genius.

## 生平概要

Pandolfo rose to power in Siena through a combination of political maneuvering and alliance-building. Though Siena was nominally a republic, Pandolfo governed as a signore (lord), maintaining the forms of republican government while exercising personal authority.

His rule was marked by caution and pragmatism. He survived the turbulent period of Cesare Borgia's Romagna campaign by avoiding direct confrontation with the Duke — a feat many of his contemporaries failed to achieve.

## 与《君主论》的关联

Machiavelli cites Pandolfo twice, each time as a positive example.

In [[Chapter 20]], on the question of whether fortresses and other defenses are useful, Machiavelli notes: "Pandolfo Petrucci, Prince of Siena, ruled his state more by those who had been distrusted than by others" (020-035). This observation supports Machiavelli's argument that former enemies, once won over, often serve more faithfully than long-time allies — because they must work harder to prove their loyalty.

In [[Chapter 22]], on the selection of ministers, Machiavelli offers Pandolfo as proof that a prince need not be brilliant if he knows how to recognize brilliance in others. Antonio da Venafro, Pandolfo's minister, was so capable that "who would not consider Pandolfo to be a very clever man in having Venafro for his servant" (022-005).

> "Therefore, it follows necessarily that, if Pandolfo was not in the first rank, he was in the second, for whenever one has judgment to know good and bad when it is said and done, although he himself may not have the initiative, yet he can recognize the good and the bad in his servant." (022-005)

### 作为范例的角色

Pandolfo Petrucci is a **dual exemplar**: of pragmatic personnel management (employing former enemies) and of the second-order intelligence that suffices for a prince — the ability to judge, if not to originate.

## 历史评价

Machiavelli's treatment of Pandolfo is notably warm. He is not a grand figure like Cesare Borgia or Ferdinand of Aragon, but a provincial ruler who governed competently through a combination of shrewd judgment and wise delegation. In a book that often dwells on spectacular failures, Pandolfo represents the quieter possibility of competent rule.
