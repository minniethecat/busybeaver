# Fraud (Frode)

Fraud (frode) in Machiavelli's political vocabulary is not a vice but a tool — one of the two modes of combat available to the prince (alongside force). The prince who knows how to use fraud skillfully prevails where force alone would fail. This instrumental view of deception is among Machiavelli's most controversial contributions to political thought.

## Machiavelli 的论述

Fraud appears primarily in Chapters 7 and 18, and in the Appendix on Castruccio Castracani, always within the context of successful political action.

### 核心论点

- A prince must make himself beloved and feared through force or fraud — both are legitimate means of securing rule (007-203)
- The fox represents fraud: the ability to recognize snares and escape them requires cunning, not strength (018 — lion and fox passage)
- Those who have known best how to employ the fox — that is, fraud — have succeeded best in the course of history
- Fraud must be disguised: the prince who appears merciful, faithful, humane, and religious while acting by fraud achieves the greatest success
- Castruccio Castracani overcame by fraud those whom he desired to subdue, showing that fraud is not only a defensive tool but an offensive one (028-705)

### 历史例证

- **Pope Alexander VI** — the supreme practitioner of fraud, who "never did anything but deceive men" and always found fresh victims. His fraud succeeded because he understood human credulity (Ch. 18)
- **Cesare Borgia** — used fraud at Senigallia to lure the Orsini leaders to their deaths, eliminating his enemies in a single stroke (Ch. 7)
- **Castruccio Castracani** — overcame his enemies by fraud when they could not be defeated in open combat (028-705)

## 概念辨析

### 与其他概念的关系

- **Fraud vs. Force**: The two modes of animal combat. Force (the lion) is open and direct; fraud (the fox) is covert and indirect. A complete prince uses both.
- **Fraud vs. Faith**: Fraud necessarily involves breaking faith — the prince who uses fraud must appear faithful while acting faithlessly. This tension defines Chapter 18.
- **Fraud vs. Virtù**: Fraud is not opposed to virtù but is an expression of it. The virtù-ous prince knows when and how to deceive.
