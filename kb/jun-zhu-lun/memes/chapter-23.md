## CHAPTER XXIII. HOW FLATTERERS SHOULD BE AVOIDED

[023-001]I do not wish to leave out an important branch of this subject, for it is

[023-002]a danger from which princes are with difficulty preserved, unless they are

[023-003]very careful and discriminating. It is that of flatterers, of whom courts

[023-004]are full, because men are so self-complacent in their own affairs, and in

[023-005]a way so deceived in them, that they are preserved with difficulty from

[023-006]this pest, and if they wish to defend themselves they run the danger of

[023-007]falling into contempt. Because there is no other way of guarding oneself

[023-008]from flatterers except letting men understand that to tell you the truth

[023-009]does not offend you; but when every one may tell you the truth, respect

[023-010]for you abates.

[023-011]Therefore a wise prince ought to hold a third course by choosing the wise

[023-012]men in his state, and giving to them only the liberty of speaking the

[023-013]truth to him, and then only of those things of which he inquires, and of

[023-014]none others; but he ought to question them upon everything, and listen to

[023-015]their opinions, and afterwards form his own conclusions. With these

[023-016]councillors, separately and collectively, he ought to carry himself in

[023-017]such a way that each of them should know that, the more freely he shall

[023-018]speak, the more he shall be preferred; outside of these, he should listen

[023-019]to no one, pursue the thing resolved on, and be steadfast in his

[023-020]resolutions. He who does otherwise is either overthrown by flatterers, or

[023-021]is so often changed by varying opinions that he falls into contempt.

[023-022]I wish on this subject to adduce a modern example. Fra Luca, the man of affairs

[023-023]to Maximilian,[1]

[023-024]the present emperor, speaking of his majesty, said: He consulted with no one,

[023-025]yet never got his own way in anything. This arose because of his following a

[023-026]practice the opposite to the above; for the emperor is a secretive man—he

[023-027]does not communicate his designs to any one, nor does he receive opinions on

[023-028]them. But as in carrying them into effect they become revealed and known, they

[023-029]are at once obstructed by those men whom he has around him, and he, being

[023-030]pliant, is diverted from them. Hence it follows that those things he does one

[023-031]day he undoes the next, and no one ever understands what he wishes or intends

[023-032]to do, and no one can rely on his resolutions.

[023-033][1]

[023-034]Maximilian I, born in 1459, died 1519, Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire. He

[023-035]married, first, Mary, daughter of Charles the Bold; after her death, Bianca

[023-036]Sforza; and thus became involved in Italian politics.

[023-037]A prince, therefore, ought always to take counsel, but only when he wishes

[023-038]and not when others wish; he ought rather to discourage every one from

[023-039]offering advice unless he asks it; but, however, he ought to be a constant

[023-040]inquirer, and afterwards a patient listener concerning the things of which

[023-041]he inquired; also, on learning that any one, on any consideration, has not

[023-042]told him the truth, he should let his anger be felt.

[023-043]And if there are some who think that a prince who conveys an impression of

[023-044]his wisdom is not so through his own ability, but through the good

[023-045]advisers that he has around him, beyond doubt they are deceived, because

[023-046]this is an axiom which never fails: that a prince who is not wise himself

[023-047]will never take good advice, unless by chance he has yielded his affairs

[023-048]entirely to one person who happens to be a very prudent man. In this case

[023-049]indeed he may be well governed, but it would not be for long, because such

[023-050]a governor would in a short time take away his state from him.

[023-051]But if a prince who is not inexperienced should take counsel from more

[023-052]than one he will never get united counsels, nor will he know how to unite

[023-053]them. Each of the counsellors will think of his own interests, and the

[023-054]prince will not know how to control them or to see through them. And they

[023-055]are not to be found otherwise, because men will always prove untrue to you

[023-056]unless they are kept honest by constraint. Therefore it must be inferred

[023-057]that good counsels, whencesoever they come, are born of the wisdom of the

[023-058]prince, and not the wisdom of the prince from good counsels.
