# Jerusalem

Jerusalem — the City of David and the location of Solomon's Temple — is the geographical centre of Hobbes' biblical-political argument. It is the capital of the Kingdom of God by covenant, the model for every Christian commonwealth.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references Jerusalem throughout Parts III and IV. It was the seat of God's kingdom in Israel: David established his throne there, and Solomon built the Temple there. Hobbes insists that Jerusalem was a civil capital, not merely a cultic centre — "the Kingdome of God is a Civill Kingdome" with Jerusalem as its "Regall City" (Chapter XXXV). The New Jerusalem of the Book of Revelation is interpreted by Hobbes as the kingdom of God to be established on earth after the resurrection — a real city in a real commonwealth.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Jerusalem in Chapters XXXV, XXXVIII, XL, and XLIV. The city's dual identity — political capital and religious centre — makes it the perfect illustration of Hobbes' thesis that civil and religious authority are inseparable.
