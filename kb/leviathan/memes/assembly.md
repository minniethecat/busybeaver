# Assembly

Hobbes' analysis of assemblies covers both sovereign assemblies (aristocracy and democracy) and subordinate assemblies (councils, synods, parliaments). His treatment is pragmatic but always governed by the principle that no assembly may claim authority independent of the sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses assemblies in Chapters XIX, XXII, and XLII. A sovereign assembly is the representative of the commonwealth in an aristocracy or democracy (Chapter XIX). Subordinate assemblies are "Systemes" — "any numbers of men joyned in one Interest, or one Businesse" — which may be political (authorized by the sovereign) or private (unauthorized, and therefore unlawful). In Chapter XLII, Hobbes argues that ecclesiastical assemblies (councils, synods) have no legislative authority — they may deliberate and advise but cannot bind the conscience or override the civil law.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses assemblies in Chapter XIX (forms of commonwealth), Chapter XXII ("Of Systemes Subject, Politicall, and Private"), and Chapter XLII (ecclesiastical assemblies). Chapter XXII provides a detailed taxonomy of corporate bodies and their relationship to sovereign authority.
