# Representation

Hobbes' theory of representation is one of his most original contributions to political philosophy. By treating the sovereign as a representative (actor) and subjects as authors, he transforms political authority from a relation of command into a relation of authorization.

## Definition

The theory is laid out in Chapter XVI ("Of Persons, Authors, and things Personated"): "A Person, is he, whose words or actions are considered, either as his own, or as representing the words or actions of an other man... When they are considered as his owne, then is he called a Naturall Person; And when they are considered as representing the words and actions of another, then he is a Feigned or Artificiall person" (Chapter XVI (017-001)). The person represented is the Author; the one who represents is the Actor: "Of Persons Artificiall, some have their words and actions Owned by those whom they represent. And then the Person is the Actor; and he that owneth his words and actions, is the AUTHOR: In which case the Actor acteth by Authority" (Chapter XVI (017-002)).

## Context

Chapter XVI is the crucial hinge between Part I (Of Man) and Part II (Of Common-wealth). The theory of representation provides the metaphysical basis for the social contract: when the multitude covenants to authorize one representative, they become a single artificial person — the commonwealth. "A Multitude of men, are made One Person, when they are by one man, or one Person, Represented; so that it be done with the consent of every one of that Multitude in particular" (Chapter XVI (017-014)).

## Key Arguments

**The Actor Bears the Person.** Representation is not delegation — the actor does not merely carry messages. The actor bears the person of the author, meaning that the author owns every action of the actor. "Every man giving their common Representer, Authority from himselfe in particular; and owning all the actions the Representer doth" (Chapter XVI (017-015)).

**From Many to One.** Representation solves the problem of how a multitude — which is naturally many, not one — can act as a unity. "It is the Unity of the Representer, not the Unity of the Represented, that maketh the Person One" (Chapter XVI (017-014)). The sovereign is the principle of unity in the commonwealth.

**Authorization Without Limit.** When authors give their representative authority "without stint," they own everything the representative does. When they limit the authority, they own only what is within the commission. The social contract gives the sovereign unlimited authorization for peace and defence.
