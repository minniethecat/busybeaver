# St. John

St. John the Evangelist occupies a distinctive role in Hobbes' *Leviathan* — he is the apostolic authority most frequently cited on matters of Christology, pneumatology, and eschatology. Hobbes draws on John's Gospel, epistles, and the Apocalypse to argue against Trinitarian orthodoxy and to support his interpretation of spirit, prophecy, and the kingdom of God.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes appeals to John primarily when contesting what he considers the corruptions of primitive Christianity by later ecclesiastical tradition. John's writings provide Hobbes with what he takes to be the earliest and least Hellenised account of Christian doctrine:

> St. John the Evangelist, and, I think, St. John onely, useth the word *Logos* for the Son of God (039-016)

Hobbes reads John's Logos not as the second person of a Triune Godhead but as God's Word or command — the divine speech-act by which God creates and governs. This anti-Trinitarian reading is central to Hobbes' project of demystifying Christian theology and rendering it compatible with civil sovereignty.

## Key References

**The Incarnation**: Hobbes cites John 1:14 ("the Word was made flesh") to argue that Christ's divine sonship consists in his anointing as Messiah, not in a metaphysical generation: "St. John saith (chap. 11.51.) that 'He spake not this of himselfe: but being High Priest that year, he prophecyed that Jesus should die for the nation'" (039-019).

**The Testing of Spirits**: Hobbes returns repeatedly to 1 John 4:1-2 as the criterion for distinguishing true prophets from false: "St. John advised us, 1 Epistle, Chap. 4. verse 1. 'Beleeve not every Spirit, but try the Spirits whether they are of God'" (046-025). For Hobbes, the article that "Jesus is the Christ" is the sole necessary article of faith — and John is its chief apostolic guarantor.

**The Apocalypse**: Hobbes reads Revelation as a prophecy of ecclesiastical corruption, not a literal prediction of end-times events. John's visions of the Beast and Antichrist are, in Hobbes' reading, figures for the papacy: "whom the Apostle there properly calleth Antichrist" (039-032).

## John and Hobbes' Anti-Trinitarianism

Hobbes' treatment of John's writings constitutes one of the most radical elements of *Leviathan*. He systematically reads every Trinitarian proof-text in John's writings through a unitarian lens:

> St. John indeed (1 Epist. 5.7.) saith, "There are three that beare witnesse in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit; and these Three are One." But this is not in the ancient copies. (045-003)

Hobbes disputes the authenticity of the Johannine Comma — the strongest biblical proof-text for the Trinity — on textual-critical grounds, anticipating the conclusions of later biblical scholarship by over a century.
