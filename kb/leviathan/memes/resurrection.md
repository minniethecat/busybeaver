# Resurrection

The Resurrection is one of the most extensively discussed theological events in *Leviathan*. Hobbes' interpretation of the resurrection as bodily and earthly — rather than spiritual and heavenly — is essential to his argument that Christ's kingdom will be a civil kingdom on earth, established only at the Second Coming.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the resurrection primarily in Chapter XXXVIII ("Of the Signification in Scripture of Eternall Life, Hel, Salvation, and the World to Come") and Chapter XLIV ("Of Spirituall Darknesse"). He argues that "Eternall Life" in scripture refers to life in the resurrected body on a renewed earth, not to the immortality of a disembodied soul in heaven. This materialist eschatology strips the clergy of their claim to govern souls in the afterlife and concentrates all temporal authority in the civil sovereign.

## Key References

Hobbes argues in Chapter XXXVIII that "the Kingdome of God is a Civill Common-wealth" and that after the resurrection, "the Subjects of that Kingdome are the bodies of the faithfull, raised from the dead" (Chapter XXXVIII). In Chapter XLIV, he attacks the doctrine of the immortality of the soul as a Greek philosophical corruption of biblical teaching.
