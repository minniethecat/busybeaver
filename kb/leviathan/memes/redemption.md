# Redemption

Hobbes interprets redemption — Christ's saving work — through the lens of his contractual and legal theory. Redemption is the payment of a ransom that frees mankind from the obligation of sin, understood juridically as the satisfaction of a debt.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses redemption primarily in Chapter XLI, where he analyses Christ's three offices. As Redeemer, Christ "paid the price of our Redemption" — this is the first office, accomplished at his first coming. Hobbes' account of redemption is notably legalistic: "The Kingdome of God is a Civill Kingdome; which consisted, first in the Obligation of the people of Israel to those Laws, which Moses should bring unto them from Mount Sinai" (Chapter XXXV). Redemption restores men to a condition in which they can again become subjects of God's kingdom. Hobbes' treatment of redemption strips it of mystical elements and presents it as a transaction — a reading consistent with his broader contractual theory.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses redemption in Chapter XLI and references it in the context of God's kingdom in Chapter XXXV. The concept is less extensively treated than resurrection or judgement, reflecting Hobbes' relative lack of interest in soteriology compared to ecclesiology.
