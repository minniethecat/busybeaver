# Episcopacy

Hobbes' treatment of episcopacy reflects his Erastian principles — bishops have authority only insofar as the civil sovereign grants it. Episcopacy is not, for Hobbes, a divinely instituted form of church government but a prudential arrangement that varies by commonwealth.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses episcopacy in Chapter XLII, alongside Presbyterianism and Independency, as one of the forms of church government. He acknowledges that "Bishops" were appointed in the early church, but insists that their authority was "to Teach, and to Baptize" — not to govern. In England, the monarch is "the Supreme Pastor," and bishops exercise their functions by the sovereign's authority. Hobbes' treatment of episcopacy is notably more moderate than his attack on Presbyterianism and Independency — reflecting his own preference for episcopal church government under royal supremacy.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses episcopacy in Chapter XLII, in his survey of forms of church government. The discussion reflects the debates of the 1640s about whether episcopacy was of divine right (the Laudian position) or merely a human institution.
