# Covenant

In Hobbes' political philosophy, a covenant is a contract in which one or both parties perform in the future, distinguished from a simple contract (where both transfer rights simultaneously). The covenant is the mechanism through which individuals escape the state of nature and create the sovereign.

## Definition

Hobbes distinguishes covenant from contract: "one of the Contractors, may deliver the Thing contracted for on his part, and leave the other to perform his part at some determinate time after, and in the mean time be trusted; and then the Contract on his part, is called PACT, or COVENANT" (Chapter XIV (015-040)). A covenant of mutual trust becomes void if either party fears the other will not perform, for "he that performeth first, has no assurance the other will performe after; because the bonds of words are too weak to bridle mens ambition, avarice, anger, and other Passions, without the feare of some coercive Power" (Chapter XIV (019-012)).

## Context

The covenant is central to Hobbes' argument across the entire *Leviathan*. In Part I, Chapter XIV, he defines covenants as the foundation of natural law. In Part I, Chapter XV, he establishes that justice consists in keeping covenants — "the definition of INJUSTICE, is no other than the not performance of Covenant" (Chapter XV (016-032)). The covenant reaches its full political expression in Part II, Chapter XVII, where "the Generation of that great LEVIATHAN" occurs through the mutual covenant in which every man says to every man: "I Authorise and give up my Right of Governing my selfe, to this Man, or to this Assembly of men, on this condition, that thou give up thy Right to him, and Authorise all his Actions in like manner" (Chapter XVII (019-023)).

## Key Arguments

**Covenants Without the Sword Are But Words.** Hobbes argues that covenants without a coercive power to enforce them are merely verbal, incapable of securing peace. "Covenants, without the Sword, are but Words, and of no strength to secure a man at all" (Chapter XVII (019-012)). The sovereign power provides the terror that makes covenant-keeping rational.

**Justice as Covenant-Keeping.** Hobbes defines justice derivatively from covenant: "When a Covenant is made, then to break it is Unjust: And the definition of INJUSTICE, is no other than the not performance of Covenant. And whatsoever is not Unjust, is Just" (Chapter XV (016-032)). This definition makes justice entirely dependent on a prior covenant — there is no justice in the state of nature.

**Covenants Made from Fear Are Valid.** Against the common view that promises made under duress are void, Hobbes insists that "Covenants entred into by fear, in the condition of meer Nature, are obligatory" (Chapter XIV (016-009)). This principle is essential to his argument that both commonwealth by institution and commonwealth by acquisition produce equally binding obligations (Chapter XX (022-002)).
