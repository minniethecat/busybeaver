# Paradise

Hobbes' treatment of Paradise reflects his materialist hermeneutics. The Garden of Eden was a real place on earth; the Paradise of the afterlife is the renewed earth after the resurrection. Both are earthly, not spiritual.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Paradise primarily in Chapter XXXVIII. He argues that "the Paradise wherein Adam was placed, was a terrestriall Paradise" — a real garden on earth. The "Paradise" promised to the thief on the cross ("Today shalt thou be with me in Paradise") is interpreted by Hobbes not as an immediate translation to heaven but as a promise of the future kingdom. "Paradise is a place of joy on earth... after the Resurrection" (Chapter XXXVIII). This interpretation, like Hobbes' treatment of heaven and hell, eliminates the intermediate state of disembodied souls and concentrates eschatological hope on the future earthly kingdom.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Paradise in Chapter XXXVIII, alongside his treatments of heaven, hell, and eternal life. The discussion is part of his systematic reinterpretation of Christian eschatology in materialist and political terms.
