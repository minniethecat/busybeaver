# Old Testament

The Old Testament serves Hobbes as the constitutional archive of God's first kingdom on earth — the commonwealth of Israel. It is not, in Hobbes' treatment, a collection of spiritual allegories but a body of legal and historical documents recording the institution, operation, and interruption of divine sovereignty over a particular people. "The whole Scripture of the Old Testament, was set forth in the form we have it" (036-038) by the authority of the Jewish commonwealth itself.

## Hobbes' Use of This Work

Hobbes reads the Old Testament as a political document. Its narratives — the covenant with Abraham, the Exodus, the giving of the Law at Sinai, the establishment of the monarchy — are treated as constitutional precedents. Its laws — the Decalogue, the judicial laws of Moses, the ceremonial regulations — are treated as the positive legislation of God's kingdom.

Hobbes' primary interest is in the Old Testament's testimony that God's kingdom was a civil kingdom: "In the Old Testament, the sign of Admission was Circumcision" (038-026), and its sacraments, priesthood, and prophets all operated under the authority of the civil sovereign — Moses, then the kings. The Old Testament, in Hobbes' reading, demonstrates that even God himself governed through civil institutions.

## Key Citations

**The Canon**: "the whole Scripture of the Old Testament, was set forth in the form we have it" (036-038) after the return from Babylon. Hobbes argues that the Old Testament canon was determined by the Jewish commonwealth — a civil body, not a priestly council — and that this establishes the precedent for civil determination of the canon.

**Prophecy**: "In the Old Testament, he spake by Dreams, or Visions" (039-022). Hobbes distinguishes between the manner of prophecy in the Old Testament (direct revelation to sovereign prophets like Moses) and in the New Testament (preaching and teaching under civil authority).

**Law**: "Moses gave to the people of Israel at the renewing of the Covenant" (028-042) the body of laws that constituted the Israelite commonwealth. The Old Testament is, fundamentally, a law book.

## Context

In Hobbes' time, the Old Testament was invoked by Puritans and radicals who sought to model English government on the Israeli theocracy — often to the detriment of monarchy. Hobbes' counter-reading treats Israel not as a theocracy separate from civil government but as a civil government whose sovereign happened to be God. The lesson for England is not that priests should rule but that even divine rule operates through civil institutions.
