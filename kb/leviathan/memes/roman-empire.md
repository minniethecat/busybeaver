# Roman Empire

The Roman Empire serves Hobbes as both a positive example of effective sovereignty and a negative example of how its institutional legacy was exploited by the Papacy. The Empire's combination of military power, legal order, and territorial administration represents, for Hobbes, the model of civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references the Roman Empire in several contexts. In Chapter XXI, he discusses the liberty of subjects under the Empire as an example of how liberty is always defined by law. In Chapter XLVII, he famously describes the Papacy as "the Ghost of the deceased Romane Empire, sitting crowned upon the grave thereof" — the Papacy appropriated the Empire's universal jurisdiction while lacking its civil foundation. Hobbes also references Roman law and Roman institutions (the Dictator, the Tribunes) to illustrate points about sovereignty and its limits.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Roman Empire in Chapters XXI, XLVI, and XLVII. The Empire's legal tradition — particularly Roman civil law — is an important source for Hobbes' own juridical theory, though he does not treat it as authoritative in the way he treats biblical sources.
