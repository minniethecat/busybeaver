# Joseph

Joseph, the son of Jacob who rose to become vizier of Egypt, appears in *Leviathan* as part of Hobbes' argument about the Kingdom of God. Joseph's exercise of civil authority over Egypt on Pharaoh's behalf illustrates Hobbes' claim that God's representatives exercise temporal power.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references Joseph in the context of the Kingdom of God in Chapter XXXV and Chapter XL. Joseph's story — sold into slavery by his brothers, rising through his interpretation of dreams to become Egypt's chief administrator — demonstrates that God's providence operates through civil institutions. Joseph exercised sovereign authority in Egypt as Pharaoh's lieutenant, distributing grain during famine and administering the land. This model of delegated sovereignty — where the deputy exercises full authority on behalf of the sovereign — prefigures Hobbes' theory of the sovereign as God's lieutenant in every commonwealth.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Joseph briefly in Chapter XXXV and Chapter XL as part of the narrative of God's dealings with the patriarchs. Joseph's role is less extensively treated than that of Abraham, Moses, or David, but his position as Pharaoh's lieutenant serves Hobbes' larger argument that God's authority on earth is always exercised through civil representatives.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Joseph is a central figure in Genesis 37–50. He is Jacob's favourite son, sold into slavery by his jealous brothers, and taken to Egypt. Through his ability to interpret dreams, he rises to become Pharaoh's chief minister, saving Egypt and his own family from famine. His story is one of the great narratives of the Hebrew Bible — a tale of betrayal, endurance, and reconciliation. Joseph is traditionally seen as a type of Christ, whose suffering leads to the salvation of many.
