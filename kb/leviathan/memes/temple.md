# Temple

The Temple of Jerusalem — built by Solomon and rebuilt after the Babylonian exile — is the religious centre of Hobbes' political reading of the Old Testament. Hobbes emphasizes that the Temple was a royal project, built and controlled by the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Temple in Chapters XXXV, XL, and XLV. Solomon, the civil sovereign, "built a Temple for the Lord" (1 Kings 6) — the Temple was a work of the sovereign, not of the priests. The priests served in the Temple "by the Authority of the King" — they were "the Kings Subjects" as much as any other Israelite. Hobbes draws the lesson for Christian commonwealths: churches (the equivalents of the Temple) are "built, and consecrated, and maintained by the Authority of the Civill Soveraign" (Chapter XL). The Temple's destruction by Nebuchadnezzar, permitted by God, demonstrates that even the holiest place is subject to divine — and by analogy, civil — sovereignty.
