# St. Paul

St. Paul is the most frequently cited New Testament figure in Hobbes' *Leviathan*, appearing across the full span of the work — from the definition of obedience in Part II to the ecclesiology of Parts III and IV, and the eschatology of the conclusion. Hobbes treats Paul's epistles as an authoritative source for Christian doctrine, but reads them through the lens of his own civil philosophy: every Pauline text is marshalled to support the indivisibility of sovereign power.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses St. Paul as a proof-text for his central political claims. When arguing for the obedience of servants to masters, he quotes "St. Paul saith, (Coll. 3. 20) 'Servants obey in all things your Masters'" (022-016). When discussing excommunication in the early church, he cites Paul's practice at Ephesus (024-052) and his instructions to the Corinthians (045-019). When treating the resurrection, he relies on "St. Paul (1 Cor. 15.42, 43.)" for the distinction between the natural and spiritual body (041-017).

> For obedience of servants St. Paul saith, (Coll. 3. 20) "Servants obey in all things your Masters according to the flesh." (022-016)

Paul functions in Leviathan as Hobbes' principal witness that Christianity, properly understood, requires obedience to civil authority — not the divided allegiance taught by the Church of Rome.

## Key References

Hobbes invokes Paul across four major doctrinal domains:

**Obedience**: "Children obey your Parents in all things" (Col. 3:20, cited at 045-010). Hobbes extends Paul's household codes to the political realm: what applies to the family applies equally to the commonwealth.

**Resurrection and the Afterlife**: "St. Paul (1 Cor. 15.42, 43.) to the question of what kind of body the dead shall rise with, answereth" (041-017). Hobbes uses Paul to argue that the resurrected body is spiritual, not natural — a point crucial to his mortalist theology.

**Church Authority**: Paul's excommunication of the incestuous man at Corinth (1 Cor. 5:3-5) is Hobbes' model for church discipline under civil oversight: "the practise of St. Paul (1 Cor. 5.3, 4, & 5.) where he saith... deliver such a one to Satan" (045-019).

**Christ's Kingdom**: Paul's statement that Christ shall deliver the kingdom to God the Father (1 Cor. 15:24, cited at 044-017) is Hobbes' proof that Christ's kingdom is not of this world — and therefore that no ecclesiastical authority can claim temporal power.

## Hobbes' Hermeneutics

Hobbes' use of Paul is consistently selective and political. He never cites Paul's statements about Christian liberty (Gal. 5:1) or the transcendence of the law. Instead, every citation reinforces the duty of submission. This is not carelessness — it is method. Hobbes reads scripture through the lens of his own conclusion: that the sovereign must be absolute in all matters, spiritual as well as temporal.

> St. Paul sayes further to the Galatians, that their doctrin for Gods Word (035-009)

Even when discussing false teachers, Hobbes frames Paul's warnings as support for the sovereign's role in judging doctrine. The test of a spirit, per 1 John (cited at 046-025), becomes in Hobbes' hands a civil test: the sovereign determines which doctrines are publicly admissible.
