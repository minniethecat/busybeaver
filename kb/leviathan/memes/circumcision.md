# Circumcision

Circumcision is treated in *Leviathan* not as a mere ritual but as the constitutive sign of the Abrahamic covenant — the "memoriall, and a token of this Covenant" (Gen. 17:11, cited in Part III) by which Abraham and his posterity were separated as God's "peculiar people" and bound to obedience to God's positive law. Hobbes' analysis of circumcision is thoroughly political: it establishes God's sovereignty over Israel by pact, making circumcision the visible mark of allegiance to a divine king.

## Hobbes' Reference

Hobbes locates circumcision within his larger argument about the nature of God's kingdom. The covenant with Abraham is, in Hobbes' reading, "an Institution by pact, of Gods peculiar Soveraignty over the seed of Abraham" — not a merely spiritual relationship but a political one. Circumcision is its sign, just as baptism is the sign of admission to the New Covenant.

> And for a memoriall, and a token of this Covenant, he ordaineth (verse 11.) the Sacrament of Circumcision.

Crucially, Hobbes notes that "it is of Abraham (not of Moses) St. Paul saith (Rom. 4.11.) that he is the Father of the Faithfull, that is, of those that are loyall, and doe not violate their Allegiance sworn to God, then by Circumcision, and afterwards in the New Covenant by Baptisme." The language of allegiance is Hobbes' gloss — Paul's "faith" becomes Hobbes' "loyalty," and circumcision becomes an oath of fealty.

## Key Passages

**The Institution**: God's covenant with Abraham (Gen. 17:7-8) promises land and posterity in exchange for exclusive allegiance. Circumcision is the physical sign of this contract — a permanent mark that identifies the bearer as a subject of God's peculiar kingdom.

**Circumcision and the Old Covenant**: Hobbes is explicit that this covenant "containeth a Contract between God and Abraham; by which Abraham obligeth himself, and his posterity, in a peculiar manner to be subject to Gods positive Law." Circumcision is thus not merely a religious ceremony but a legal act — the ratification of a contract establishing divine sovereignty.

**Baptism as Successor**: Hobbes draws a direct line from circumcision to baptism: "In the Old Testament, the sign of Admission was Circumcision; in the New Testament, Baptisme." Both are "Sacraments of Admission" used once, because "there needs but one Admission" into the kingdom of God.

## Circumcision and the Sacraments

Hobbes classifies circumcision as one of two Old Testament "Sacraments of Admission" (the other being the Passover as a Sacrament of Commemoration). His definition of a sacrament is strikingly juridical:

> A SACRAMENT, is a separation of some visible thing from common use; and a consecration of it to Gods service, for a sign, either of our admission into the Kingdome of God, to be of the number of his peculiar people, or for a Commemoration of the same.

This definition — "consecration," "sign," "admission," "kingdom" — reframes sacramental theology in the language of political incorporation. Circumcision is how one becomes a subject of God's kingdom under the Old Covenant; baptism is how one becomes a subject under the New.
