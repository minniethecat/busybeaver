# Mount Sinai

Mount Sinai is the geographical location of the founding of God's kingdom by covenant. Hobbes interprets the giving of the Law at Sinai not as a purely religious event but as the constitutional moment of a civil commonwealth in which God was the sovereign and Moses his lieutenant.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Mount Sinai in Chapter XXXV, where the covenant at Sinai establishes "the Kingdome of God by the Old Covenant." At Sinai, God "propounded to the people of Israel a Covenant; wherein, upon condition that they would obey his Lawes, hee would be their King" (Chapter XXXV). The Israelites consented, making God their civil sovereign. The giving of the Law at Sinai is thus the paradigmatic act of sovereignty — the sovereign declares the law, and the people consent to be bound by it. This is the model Hobbes applies to all commonwealths.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Mount Sinai in Chapter XXXV ("Of the Signification in Scripture of the Kingdome of God"). The covenant at Sinai is the foundation of Hobbes' argument that God's kingdom is a civil kingdom, and that all subsequent legitimate sovereignty is modelled on it.
