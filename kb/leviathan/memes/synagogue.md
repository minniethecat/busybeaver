# Synagogue

The Synagogue — the local assembly of Jews for worship and teaching — serves Hobbes as a historical model for the proper relationship between religious assemblies and civil authority. In the Israelite commonwealth, synagogues were always subordinate to the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references the Synagogue in Chapter XL and Chapter XLII. In the Kingdom of God by covenant, "the Synagogues were Schools, wherein the Law of Moses was taught every Sabbath day" — they were institutions of teaching, not government. The authority to establish and regulate synagogues belonged to the civil sovereign (Moses, then the High Priests as God's lieutenants, then the Kings of Judah). Hobbes uses this model to argue that Christian churches, like Jewish synagogues, are "Schools" for teaching doctrine — not independent jurisdictions.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Synagogue in Chapter XL (on the Kingdom of God in Israel) and Chapter XLII (on ecclesiastical power). The distinction between the Synagogue as a teaching institution and the Sanhedrin as a governing body is important to Hobbes' argument.
