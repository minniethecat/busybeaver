# Judah

The Kingdom of Judah is referenced throughout Parts III and IV of *Leviathan* as the southern successor-state to the united kingdom of Israel. For Hobbes, Judah is significant primarily as the seat of the Davidic monarchy and the tribal lineage of the Messiah — both themes central to his argument that Christ's kingdom is not of this world.

## Hobbes' Reference

Judah appears in Leviathan almost always in conjunction with Israel — the two kingdoms that emerged from the division after Solomon. Hobbes invokes "the Chronicles of the Kings of Israel, to the Books of the Prophet Samuel" (036-028) and notes the parallel annals kept by the two kingdoms: "the High Priests, and Kings of Judah, in the Old Testament" (036-040). The historical records of Judah serve Hobbes as evidence for the nature of prophecy, the scope of canon, and the proper relationship between priesthood and monarchy.

> The Kings of Israel and Judah, the event of the battle (037-019)

Prophets in Judah, as in Israel, spoke to kings — not over them. Hobbes uses this fact to establish that prophecy in the Old Testament was subject to royal authority, not independent of it.

## Key Passages

**The Divided Kingdom**: After Solomon's death, the ten northern tribes seceded to form the Kingdom of Israel, leaving Judah (with Benjamin) as the southern kingdom under the house of David. Hobbes does not narrate this event in detail, but he presupposes it in his discussions of later biblical history. The division matters to Hobbes because it demonstrates that political unity depends on covenant, not on common ancestry or religion.

**The Line of David**: "he shall be one of the tribe of Judah, a Jew, not a Samaritan" (041-026) — Hobbes cites the Davidic descent of the Messiah to establish that Christ's kingship is hereditary and tribal, not universal in this age. The Messiah comes from Judah, and his kingdom, when it comes, will be the restoration of David's throne — a literal, political kingdom, not a spiritual abstraction.

**Prophets of Judah**: Hobbes notes that prophets operated "in the dayes of the Kings of Israel, or Judah" (039-014) as royal counsellors. Prophets like Isaiah and Jeremiah, who ministered in Judah, spoke God's word to kings — but the kings remained sovereign. The prophet's authority was declarative, not legislative; he announced God's will but did not command the kingdom.

## Judah in Hobbes' Political Theology

Judah serves Hobbes as the historical link between the Davidic covenant and the messianic expectation. God's promise to David of an everlasting throne (2 Sam. 7) was conditional on obedience — and when Judah's kings proved disobedient, the kingdom fell. But the promise was not revoked; it was deferred. Christ, as the son of David, will fulfil it at his second coming.

Until then, Judah — like all earthly kingdoms — is governed by human sovereigns who exercise God's authority by delegation. The lesson Hobbes draws from Judah's history is that no nation, however specially favoured by God, is exempt from the logic of sovereignty: divided authority produces destruction.
