# Moses

Moses occupies a unique position in *Leviathan* as both a biblical authority and a political exemplar. Hobbes treats Moses' rule over Israel not as a theocracy in the conventional sense but as a civil sovereignty in which God was the sovereign and Moses his lieutenant — a model that supports Hobbes' argument for undivided sovereign power.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses Moses extensively in Parts III and IV to argue that ecclesiastical authority is subordinate to civil sovereignty. In the commonwealth of Israel, God was the sovereign and Moses was "God's Lieutenant" — the civil representative through whom God spoke to the people (Chapter XL (044-001)). When Aaron made the golden calf, he usurped Moses' civil authority under pretence of religious authority — Hobbes' paradigm for the dangers of priestly power. This reading of Moses' role is central to Hobbes' Erastian argument that the sovereign, not the church, holds ultimate religious authority.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Moses' authority at length in Chapter XL ("Of the Rights of the Kingdome of God, in Abraham, Moses, the High Priests, and the Kings of Judah"). He argues that "Moses was alone King" — all authority, including religious, was in his person (Chapter XL (044-012)). In Chapter XXXIII, Hobbes discusses the authorship of the Pentateuch, noting that though tradition attributes it to Moses, "it is manifest, the Booke was written after Moses his time" — a cautious nod to early biblical criticism (Chapter XXXIII (037-011)).

## Biblical/Historical Background

Moses is the central figure of the Torah (Pentateuch) — liberator of Israel from Egypt, recipient of the Law at Sinai, and leader of the Israelites through the wilderness. In Christian and Jewish tradition, Moses is the paradigmatic prophet and lawgiver. Hobbes' treatment of Moses reflects Reformation debates about the relationship between civil and ecclesiastical power, particularly the question of whether the Israelite commonwealth was a theocracy (rule by priests) or a civil kingdom with God as sovereign and Moses as his lieutenant.
