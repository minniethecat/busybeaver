# Sacrifice

Sacrifice in Hobbes' *Leviathan* operates at two levels: the ritual sacrifices of the Old Testament priesthood, which Hobbes treats as acts of civil obedience within the Mosaic commonwealth; and the unique sacrifice of Christ on the cross, which Hobbes interprets as the foundational act of the New Covenant — a vicarious satisfaction that both fulfills and abolishes the sacrificial system.

## Hobbes' Reference

Hobbes' discussion of sacrifice is consistently political. Old Testament sacrifices are not private acts of piety but public acts of allegiance, administered by a priesthood that is subordinate to the civil sovereign. The Levitical priests "offered in sacrifice, and the Gifts they offered, and the Sacraments they administred, were not instituted by their own, but by Gods command" (033-052). Sacrifice, like all worship, is a sign of honour — and public honour is a matter of sovereign jurisdiction.

> God having determined his sacrifice, for the reduction of his elect (044-014)

Christ's sacrifice is the pivot on which Hobbes' covenant theology turns. It is not a mystical transaction but a legal one: Christ "offered up himself for our sinnes upon the Crosse" (044-010), satisfying the demands of divine justice and establishing the New Covenant. This is the model for Hobbes' entire theory of representation — Christ acts as the representative of the elect, just as the sovereign represents the commonwealth.

## Key Passages

**The Old Testament Priestly System**: Hobbes describes Aaron's role in detail: "Aaron alone was to sacrifice for himself and the Priests and the people" (044-011). The exclusivity of priestly sacrifice is not, for Hobbes, a grant of independent spiritual power. Aaron's priesthood is created by Moses' civil authority, and its function is to maintain the covenant between God and the Israelite commonwealth.

**Christ's Self-Sacrifice**: "he offered himselfe in sacrifice for the sinnes of them that should by faith accept him" (044-013). Hobbes reads the crucifixion as a legal satisfaction — Christ pays the penalty owed by sinners, fulfilling the law's demands. This forensic interpretation strips sacrifice of any magical or sacerdotal character and renders it compatible with Hobbes' juridical framework.

**Sacrifice and Obedience**: Hobbes approvingly cites the prophetic principle that "to obey, is better than sacrifice" (033-054), a refrain from 1 Samuel that Hobbes uses to subordinate ritual to moral and civil duty. The point is characteristically Hobbesian: what God demands is not blood but obedience — and obedience means submission to lawful authority.

## Significance in Leviathan

Sacrifice serves Hobbes as a bridge between the Old and New Covenants, and between ecclesiastical and civil authority. By treating sacrifice as a matter of law (divine command) rather than magic (priestly power), Hobbes demystifies the central act of religious worship and brings it within the scope of sovereign regulation. Christ's sacrifice, the supreme act of obedience, becomes the model for Christian citizenship: the Christian's duty is not to offer sacrifices but to obey — and the object of obedience is the sovereign.

|-----------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Old | Animal offerings | Aaronic priesthood | Covenant maintenance under Mosaic law |
| New | Christ's self-offering | Christ alone | Covenant inauguration — once for all |
| New (ongoing) | Prayer, blessing, obedience | All believers | Signs of allegiance under civil sovereign |
