# Ascension

The Ascension of Christ into heaven marks for Hobbes the definitive end of Christ's earthly presence and the beginning of the age in which no one exercises Christ's kingly authority on earth — a key step in his argument against papal and ecclesiastical claims to temporal power.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Ascension in Chapter XLI as the transition between Christ's offices. After his resurrection, Christ "conversed with his Disciples, and instructed them" for forty days, then ascended into heaven. This marks the end of his earthly presence and the beginning of the apostolic ministry — a ministry of teaching only. Christ "left no Government... neither of State, nor Religion, but Temporall" (Chapter XLII), and will not exercise kingly authority until his return.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Ascension in Chapters XLI and XLII. In Chapter XLI, the Ascension marks the end of Christ's teaching ministry on earth. In Chapter XLII, Hobbes argues that "after our Saviour's Ascension, the Apostles had no Power given them, but to preach, and teach."
