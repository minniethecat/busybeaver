# Apostles

The Apostles are central to Hobbes' argument against ecclesiastical independence. Their commission from Christ was "to Teach, and to Baptize" — not to govern — and they exercised no temporal authority over any commonwealth.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Apostles extensively in Chapter XLII. Their authority was purely ministerial: they preached the Gospel, baptized converts, and "ordained Elders in every Church" — but these elders "had no Authority to governe, but onely to teach." The Apostles "had no Power to make Lawes, but onely to declare the Lawes of Christ" (Chapter XLII). Moreover, their office was personal and temporary: "the Apostles had no Successors in their Office of Apostleship" — there are no apostles today, only teachers and pastors authorized by the civil sovereign.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Apostles in Chapter XLII, in his systematic argument that the New Testament establishes no form of church government. The Apostles are the paradigmatic "Ministers of the Gospel" who "have no Commission to Command, but onely to Preach" (Chapter XLI).
