# Obligation

Obligation in Hobbes is a consequence of voluntary acts — specifically, the laying down of right through covenant. There is no natural obligation; all obligation is self-incurred, making it one of the most radical features of Hobbes' moral and political philosophy.

## Definition

Obligation arises when a man voluntarily divests himself of liberty: "when a man hath... abandoned, or granted away his Right; then is he said to be OBLIGED, or BOUND, not to hinder those, to whom such Right is granted, or abandoned, from the benefit of it" (Chapter XIV (015-038)). This obligation is self-imposed — it is "a bond, that tyeth him not to performe, on his part, but to suffer the consequence of breaking it" (Chapter XIV (015-007)). The crucial point is that "there being nothing to which a man may not be compelled, by the danger of death; it followeth, that no man is obliged by Covenant not to resist violence offered to his own body" (Chapter XXI (023-026)).

## Context

Obligation is central to the transition from the state of nature to civil society. In the state of nature, the Laws of Nature oblige "in foro interno" (in conscience) but "not alwayes in foro externo" (in action) — because without a common power, keeping one's obligations is self-destructive (Chapter XV (016-030)). The creation of the sovereign transforms this: the sovereign's sword provides the assurance that others will also keep their covenants, making it rational for each individual to fulfil their obligations.

## Key Arguments

**All Obligation Is Voluntary.** Hobbes sharply distinguishes between counsel and command. A command "is directed to a mans own benefit; and Counsell, to the benefit of another man" — but obligation arises only from covenant, which is a voluntary act (Chapter XXV (029-003)). The sovereign commands; the subject is obliged only because he covenanted to obey.

**The Limits of Obligation.** No covenant can oblige a man not to defend his own life: "A Covenant not to defend my selfe from force, by force, is alwayes voyd" (Chapter XIV (015-026)). This sets an absolute limit on political obligation — the subject's duty to the sovereign ends where self-preservation begins.

**Obligation Without the Sword.** Without coercive power, the Laws of Nature "are contrary to our naturall Passions" and cannot produce actual compliance (Chapter XVII (019-012)). Obligation becomes effective only when the sovereign provides the terror that makes covenant-keeping rational.
