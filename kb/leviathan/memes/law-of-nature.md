# Law of Nature

In Hobbes' moral and political philosophy, the Law of Nature (*Lex Naturalis*) is a rational precept discoverable by reason, distinct from the Right of Nature. While the Right of Nature is the liberty to do whatever preserves one's life, the Law of Nature imposes obligation: it forbids self-destructive action and commands the seeking of peace.

## Definition

Hobbes distinguishes Right from Law with precision: "For though they that speak of this subject, use to confound *Jus*, and *Lex*, Right and Law; yet they ought to be distinguished; because RIGHT, consisteth in liberty to do, or to forbeare; Whereas LAW, determineth, and bindeth to one of them: so that Law, and Right, differ as much, as Obligation, and Liberty; which in one and the same matter are inconsistent" (Chapter XIV (015-015)). He then defines the first and fundamental Law of Nature: "That every man, ought to endeavour Peace, as farre as he has hope of obtaining it; and when he cannot obtain it, that he may seek, and use, all helps, and advantages of Warre" (Chapter XIV (015-016)). From this first law, Hobbes derives a second: "That a man be willing, when others are so too, as farre-forth, as for Peace, and defence of himselfe he shall think it necessary, to lay down this right to all things; and be contented with so much liberty against other men, as he would allow other men against himselfe" (Chapter XIV (015-037)).

## Context

The Laws of Nature are introduced in Chapters XIV and XV of *Leviathan*, immediately after Hobbes' description of the natural condition of mankind as a state of war (Chapter XIII). They represent the rational way out of that condition — the principles that reason dictates for self-preservation. However, Hobbes insists that the Laws of Nature, by themselves, are not sufficient: they "bind *in foro interno*" (to a desire they should take place) but "not always *in foro externo*" (to putting them in act), because without a common power, "he that should be modest, and tractable, and performe all he promises... should but make himselfe a prey to others" (Chapter XV (017-005)). This creates the logical necessity for the sovereign to enforce them.

## Key Arguments

**Reason Discovers These Laws.** The Laws of Nature are not commands of a sovereign but dictates of reason. "A LAW OF NATURE (*Lex Naturalis*) is a Precept, or generall Rule, found out by Reason" (Chapter XIV (015-033)). They are eternal and unchanging, unlike civil laws which are the commands of the sovereign.

**Summary in the Golden Rule.** Hobbes condenses all the Laws of Nature into a single summary rule: "*Do not that to another, which thou wouldest not have done to thy selfe*" (Chapter XV (019-012)). This negative formulation (unlike the positive Golden Rule) reflects Hobbes' emphasis on restraint and non-harm rather than positive benevolence.

**The Science of the Laws of Nature Is Moral Philosophy.** Hobbes calls the study of the Laws of Nature "the true and onely Moral Philosophy" (Chapter XV (016-070)). Moral philosophy is thus not about ultimate goods but about the conditions of peaceful coexistence — a radical departure from Aristotelian ethics.
