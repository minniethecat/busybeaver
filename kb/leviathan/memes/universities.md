# Universities

Hobbes' critique of the universities is one of the most striking institutional analyses in *Leviathan*. He argues that universities, by teaching "Aristotelity" and scholastic philosophy, have been sources of seditious doctrines that weaken the authority of civil sovereigns.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses universities in Chapter XLVI ("Of Darknesse from Vain Philosophy") and in the Review and Conclusion. He argues that "the Universities have been to this Nation, as the Wooden Horse was to the Trojans" — appearing as instruments of learning while in fact serving to introduce doctrines subversive of government (Review and Conclusion). The teaching of Aristotle's *Politics* and *Ethics* in the universities "hath so disposed men to believe, that the power of a King, is not absolute" (Chapter XLVI). Hobbes' solution is not to abolish universities but to reform them: the sovereign should "order the Universities, and all the Bookes that are taught in them."

## Key References

Hobbes discusses universities in Chapter XLVI and the Review and Conclusion. His criticism reflects his broader attack on scholastic philosophy and his conviction that political stability requires control over the institutions that shape public opinion.
