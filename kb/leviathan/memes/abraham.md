# Abraham

Abraham is the foundational figure in Hobbes' biblical politics. His covenant with God is treated not as a purely religious event but as the founding of the first commonwealth — the Kingdom of God by covenant — establishing principles that Hobbes applies to all civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes devotes extensive attention to Abraham in Chapter XXXV and Chapter XL. For Hobbes, God's covenant with Abraham is the original model of sovereignty: God was sovereign, and Abraham was both subject and God's representative on earth. Crucially, "in the Kingdome of God after the Flood, the Soveraign Power was first in Abraham; and that Abraham had the sole right of commanding his own children and servants in all matters whatsoever, both Religious, and Civill" (Chapter XL (044-003)). This makes Abraham the paradigmatic sovereign — his authority over his family was undivided, covering both civil and religious matters. Hobbes draws the lesson: "in every Christian Common-wealth, the Civill Soveraign is the Supreme Pastor" (Chapter XLII).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses God's covenant with Abraham in Chapter XXXV, where the kingdom of God "by the Old Covenant" is defined (Chapter XXXV (039-002)). In Chapter XL, he traces the succession of God's sovereignty through Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Abraham's covenant establishes that "there was no other Word of God, by which they could come to the knowledge of their duty, but that which Abraham himself spake unto them" — the sovereign is the sole interpreter of divine will for his subjects (Chapter XL (044-005)).

## Biblical/Historical Background

Abraham (originally Abram) is the first patriarch of Israel in the Book of Genesis. God's covenant with Abraham (Genesis 12, 15, 17) promises land, descendants, and blessing. Abraham is revered in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam as the father of monotheistic faith. Hobbes' reading of Abraham as a civil sovereign reflects post-Reformation debates about whether Abraham's authority derived from his status as a father (patriarchal theory) or from covenant (contractual theory) — Hobbes insists on the latter.
