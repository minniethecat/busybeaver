# Reason

Hobbes offers a radically deflationary account of reason as nothing more than computation: "Reckoning (that is, Adding and Substracting) of the Consequences of generall names agreed upon" (Chapter V (006-001)). This mechanical conception strips reason of its scholastic and Cartesian pretensions while making it the essential tool for escaping the state of nature.

## Definition

"When a man Reasoneth, hee does nothing else but conceive a summe totall, from Addition of parcels; or conceive a Remainder, from Substraction of one summe from another" (Chapter V (006-001)). Reason is not an innate faculty distinguishing humans from beasts — it is acquired through industry and method, and "Children therefore are not endued with Reason at all, till they have attained the use of Speech" (Chapter V (006-010)). Reckoning without words is prone to error, which is why definitions — "settling the signification of... words" (Chapter IV (005-020)) — are the foundation of all genuine knowledge.

## Context

Reason appears early in *Leviathan* (Chapter V, "Of Reason, and Science") and underpins everything that follows. The Laws of Nature are "conclusions, or Theoremes concerning what conduceth to the conservation and defence of themselves" discovered by reason (Chapter XV (016-069)). The very construction of the commonwealth is a work of reason — an artificial creation achieved through reckoning the consequences of remaining in the state of nature versus submitting to a sovereign.

## Key Arguments

**Reason as Computation.** Hobbes' computational theory of reason is among the earliest in Western philosophy. All reasoning reduces to addition and subtraction: lawyers add facts to find laws, logicians add propositions to form syllogisms, and "in summe, in what matter soever there is place for Addition and Substraction, there also is place for Reason" (Chapter V (006-002)).

**Reason Discovers the Laws of Nature.** The Laws of Nature are dictates of reason — they are not innate ideas or divine commands but rational conclusions about what conductes to self-preservation. "A LAW OF NATURE... is a Precept, or generall Rule, found out by Reason" (Chapter XIV (015-033)).

**Science as the Fruit of Reason.** Science is "the Knowledge of Consequences, and dependence of one fact upon another" (Chapter V (006-017)). It depends entirely on having fixed definitions — without which reasoning is mere verbal manipulation.
