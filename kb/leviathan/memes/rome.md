# Rome

Rome functions in *Leviathan* as a dual symbol: the model of effective civil sovereignty (Imperial Rome) and the source of the greatest threat to civil sovereignty (Papal Rome). Hobbes' political theology is structured around the distinction between what Rome was and what the Papacy has made of it.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references Rome in two distinct contexts. Imperial Rome represents the power of civil sovereignty properly exercised — a commonwealth that achieved peace and order through undivided authority. Papal Rome, by contrast, is "the Ghost of the deceased Romane Empire, sitting crowned upon the grave thereof" (Chapter XLVII). The Pope — the "Bishop of Rome" — claims the universal jurisdiction of the Caesars but without their civil foundation. Hobbes uses this contrast to delegitimize papal authority while affirming the Roman model of sovereignty.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Rome in Chapters XXI, XLVI, and XLVII. The Papacy's Roman location is not incidental — it is the institutional inheritance of the Empire's universal claims, repurposed for ecclesiastical domination.
