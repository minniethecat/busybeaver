# Power

Hobbes provides one of the most influential analyses of power in political philosophy, defining it in terms of means to future ends and tracing how the restless human desire for power drives both conflict and the creation of the commonwealth.

## Definition

Hobbes defines power in Chapter X: "The POWER of a Man, (to take it Universally,) is his present means, to obtain some future apparent Good" (Chapter X (012-001)). He distinguishes two kinds: "Naturall Power, is the eminence of the Faculties of Body, or Mind" — strength, eloquence, prudence, nobility — while "Instrumentall are those Powers, which acquired by these, or by fortune, are means and Instruments to acquire more" — riches, reputation, friends, and good success (Chapter X (012-002)). The greatest of human powers is "that which is compounded of the Powers of most men, united by consent, in one person... that has the use of all their Powers depending on his will; such as is the Power of a Common-wealth" (Chapter X (012-003)).

## Context

The analysis of power in Chapter X is foundational to Hobbes' political theory. It establishes that human beings are driven by "a perpetuall and restlesse desire of Power after power, that ceaseth onely in Death" (Chapter XI (013-002)) — a psychological fact that, combined with equality of ability (Chapter XIII), explains why the state of nature is a state of war. The sovereign's absolute power is the solution: by concentrating all individual powers into one artificial person, the commonwealth ends the competition for power that defines natural human existence.

## Key Arguments

**The Relational Nature of Power.** Power is not a fixed property but relational — "to have servants, is Power; to have friends, is Power: for they are strengths united" (Chapter X (012-004)). Reputation of power is itself power, because it attracts the adherence of those who seek protection.

**Sovereign Power as the Greatest Power.** The sovereign's power is created when individuals transfer their natural powers to a representative. This artificial power surpasses any natural power precisely because it coordinates the strength of all subjects toward common ends — peace and defence.

**Honour as Power's Acknowledgment.** Hobbes ties honour directly to power: "To pray to another, for ayde of any kind, is to HONOUR; because a signe we have an opinion he has power to help" (Chapter X (012-016)). The entire system of honour, dignity, and worth is an expression of power relations.
