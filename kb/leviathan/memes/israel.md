# Israel

Israel is the central geographical and political entity in Hobbes' biblical argument. It is not merely a land but a commonwealth — the only historical instance, Hobbes argues, of a true Kingdom of God by covenant. "The Children of Israel, were a Common-wealth in the Wildernesse" (026-008), and their constitution under Moses provides Hobbes with the model for understanding the relationship between divine and civil authority.

## Hobbes' Reference

Israel appears throughout Parts II, III, and IV of *Leviathan* as the paradigm case of a commonwealth instituted directly by God. Unlike the gentile nations, which had only the law of nature, Israel had a positive law — the Mosaic code — delivered by God's lieutenant:

> Moses, who governed the Israelites, (that were not his, but Gods people,) not in his owne name, with Hoc Dicit Moses; but in Gods Name, with Hoc Dicit Dominus. (017-013)

This makes Moses a viceroy, not a sovereign — the sovereign is God. The relationship between Moses and Aaron models the proper relationship between civil and ecclesiastical authority: Moses (the civil magistrate) appoints Aaron (the priest), not vice versa.

## Key Passages

**The Rejection of God's Kingdom**: When the elders of Israel demand a king "like all the nations" (1 Sam. 8:7), they commit what Hobbes regards as the original sin of politics — rejecting divine sovereignty for human: "when the Elders of Israel (grieved with the corruption of the sons of Samuel) demanded a King" (038-015), God consented but warned them of the burdens of human monarchy. This passage is fundamental to Hobbes' argument that all merely human sovereignty is a consequence of the fall from divine government.

**The Renewal of the Covenant**: "Moses gave to the people of Israel at the renewing of the Covenant" (028-042) — the Sinai covenant is Hobbes' model for how a multitude becomes a people: by covenant with a sovereign. The Israelites' consent to the Mosaic law is, in Hobbes' reading, the archetype of the social contract.

**Israel as a Holy Nation**: "The whole Nation of the Israelites were a people Holy to God" (038-025). Hobbes interprets holiness not as moral purity but as separation — being set apart as God's peculiar possession. This is a political status, not a spiritual one: Israel is holy because God is its sovereign, not because its people are virtuous.

## Israel and the Kingdom of God

Hobbes' entire eschatology turns on the distinction between the kingdom of God by covenant (Israel under the Old Covenant) and the kingdom of God by promise (the messianic kingdom to come). Israel under Moses and the judges was God's literal kingdom on earth — a civil commonwealth with God as its sovereign. This kingdom was "interrupted" by the Israelites' rejection of God in favour of Saul, and will only be restored when Christ returns to reign.

> After the Israelites had rejected God, the Prophets did foretell his restitution (038-017)

The interval between Israel's rejection of God and Christ's return is the age of merely human sovereignty — the age of Leviathan. In this age, civil sovereigns exercise the powers that belonged to God in the Israelite commonwealth.
