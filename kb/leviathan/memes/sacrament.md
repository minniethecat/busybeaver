# Sacrament

Hobbes' discussion of the sacraments is part of his broader argument that all ecclesiastical functions are subordinate to civil sovereignty. The sacraments are "signes" and "seales" — not instruments of governance.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes defines a sacrament as "a visible signe of invisible grace" — following Augustine — but immediately subordinates this definition to his political theology. The two sacraments recognized by the Church of England (Baptism and the Lord's Supper) are acts of teaching and profession, not acts of ruling. "The Sacraments are the admission into the Kingdome of God, and the confirmation of the same" — they relate to membership in a spiritual kingdom, not to temporal jurisdiction (Chapter XLII). The administration of sacraments, like all religious functions, is ultimately subject to the sovereign's authority: "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor."

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the sacraments in Chapter XLII and Chapter XLIV. He addresses the Catholic doctrine of transubstantiation as an example of "Spirituall Darknesse" — a philosophical corruption that turns a simple memorial into a claim of miraculous power wielded by priests (Chapter XLIV).
