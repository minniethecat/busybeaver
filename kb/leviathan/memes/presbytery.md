# Presbytery

Hobbes' treatment of Presbyterianism reflects the immediate political context of the English Civil War, in which Presbyterian claims to independent ecclesiastical authority played a significant role. Hobbes sees Presbyterianism as replicating the papal error — claiming a spiritual jurisdiction separate from and potentially opposed to civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Presbyterianism in Chapter XLII and Chapter XLVII. He acknowledges that Presbyterian government — rule by assemblies of elders — has some biblical warrant (the "Elders" of the early church), but he insists that these elders "had no Authority to governe, but onely to teach." In a Christian commonwealth, all assemblies — including presbyteries and synods — must be authorized by the civil sovereign. Without such authorization, they are "not a lawfull Assembly, but an Unlawfull Conventicle" (Chapter XLII). Hobbes' criticism of Presbyterianism is less intense than his attack on Catholicism, but the principle is the same: no ecclesiastical body may claim independent authority.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Presbyterian church government in Chapter XLII, alongside his treatment of episcopacy and independency. The discussion reflects the debates of the Westminster Assembly (1643–1653) and the Solemn League and Covenant (1643).
