# Last Judgement

The Last Judgement is crucial to Hobbes' eschatological argument. By locating Christ's kingdom after — not before — the final judgement, Hobbes denies that any ecclesiastical authority can claim to rule on Christ's behalf in the present age.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Last Judgement in the context of Christ's three offices: Redeemer (past, at his first coming), Teacher (present, through the scriptures and ministers), and King (future, after the resurrection and judgement). "The time of Christ's coming to judge the world, is called in Scripture, the Day of Judgement" (Chapter XLI). Because Christ is not yet reigning as king, "there is no other Government in this life, neither of State, nor Religion, but Temporall" (Chapter XLII).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Last Judgement in Chapters XLI and XLII. In Chapter XLI, he argues that Christ's kingly office "is not yet begun" — it awaits the resurrection and judgement. In Chapter XLIV, he attacks the doctrine of purgatory and private judgement as inventions serving ecclesiastical power.
