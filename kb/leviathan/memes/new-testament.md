# New Testament

The New Testament is Hobbes' authority for the transition from the Old Covenant to the New — from the kingdom of God by covenant with Israel to the kingdom of God by promise to all believers. Hobbes reads the New Testament not as establishing an independent ecclesiastical authority but as confirming that "the Scripture of the New Testament is there only Law, where the lawfull Civill Power hath made it so" (045-043).

## Hobbes' Use of This Work

Hobbes treats the New Testament as a legal instrument. Its central proposition is that Jesus is the Christ — the Messiah promised in the Old Testament — and that his kingdom will be established at his second coming, not in the present age. Until then, Christians live under the authority of their civil sovereigns, to whom the New Testament commands obedience.

The New Testament's teaching on the kingdom is Hobbes' most important theological resource: "the Books of the New Testament, though most perfect Rules of Christian doctrine, could not be made Law" (045-046) except by the civil sovereign. The apostles preached; they did not legislate. Their writings become law only when adopted by the state.

## Key Citations

**The Canon**: "As for the Books of the New Testament, they are equally acknowledged for Canon" (036-021) by all Christian churches. But Hobbes insists that acknowledgment is not authority — only the sovereign can make them law.

**The Kingdom**: Christ's kingdom "is not of this world," and Hobbes cites this Johannine text repeatedly to establish that no ecclesiastical officer — pope, bishop, or presbyter — may exercise temporal jurisdiction. The New Testament establishes a kingdom that is future, not present.

**Apostolic Authority**: "The Writers of the New Testament lived all in lesse then an age after Christs Ascension" (036-039) and their writings carry the authority of eyewitness testimony. But this is testimonial, not legislative, authority. The apostles could bear witness to Christ; they could not command princes.

## Context

In the religious conflicts of Hobbes' England, the New Testament was the battlefield. Catholics cited Matthew 16:18 ("Thou art Peter") for papal supremacy; Presbyterians cited the apostolic council of Acts 15 for conciliar governance; Independents cited the Spirit's freedom for congregational autonomy. Hobbes' response to all parties was the same: the New Testament contains no grant of coercive jurisdiction. All coercive power belongs to the civil sovereign, and the New Testament confirms rather than challenges this arrangement.
