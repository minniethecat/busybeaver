# Dominion

Hobbes distinguishes two paths to sovereignty: institution (voluntary covenant) and acquisition (force). Dominion is sovereign power acquired by force, yet Hobbes insists that its rights are identical to those of instituted sovereignty — a claim essential to his theory of political obligation.

## Definition

"A Common-wealth by Acquisition, is that, where the Soveraign Power is acquired by Force; And it is acquired by force, when men singly, or many together by plurality of voyces, for fear of death, or bonds, do authorise all the actions of that Man, or Assembly, that hath their lives and liberty in his Power" (Chapter XX (022-001)). Dominion takes two forms: "Dominion Paternall" (over children by generation) and "Dominion Despoticall" (over vanquished enemies by conquest). In both cases, "the Rights, and Consequences of Soveraignty, are the same in both" — institution and acquisition produce identical obligations (Chapter XX (022-003)).

## Context

The concept of dominion in Chapter XX ("Of Dominion Paternall, and Despoticall") addresses a potential weakness in Hobbes' theory: if sovereignty derives from covenant, how can conquerors who never covenanted with the conquered claim legitimate authority? Hobbes' answer — that the conquered covenant by submitting in exchange for their lives — makes dominion as legitimate as institution. This argument scandalized contemporaries who distinguished between legitimate (consensual) and illegitimate (coerced) government.

## Key Arguments

**Fear Justifies Dominion.** The key to Hobbes' argument is that consent given from fear is still consent: "Covenants entred into by fear, in the condition of meer Nature, are obligatory" (Chapter XIV (016-009)). Therefore, a conquered people who submit to a conqueror to save their lives are genuinely obliged — their covenant is as binding as any other.

**Paternal Dominion Is Not by Generation.** Against patriarchal theorists like Sir Robert Filmer, Hobbes denies that dominion over children derives from generation: "Dominion is acquired two wayes; By Generation, and by Conquest. The right of Dominion by Generation, is that, which the Parent hath over his Children" — but "it is not by Generation, as if the Parent had Dominion over the Child because he begat him; but by the Childs Consent, either expresse, or by other sufficient arguments declared" (Chapter XX (022-004)).

**Dominion Is Absolute.** Whether by institution or acquisition, sovereignty is always absolute: "His Power cannot, without his consent, be Transferred to another: He cannot Forfeit it: He cannot be Accused by any of his Subjects, of Injury" (Chapter XX (022-003)).
