# Land of Promise

The Land of Promise — Canaan, promised by God to Abraham in Genesis — is the territorial expression of God's covenant and the model for all grants of territory and jurisdiction in Hobbes' political theory.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Land of Promise in Chapter XXXV, in the context of God's covenant with Abraham. The promise of land was a constitutive element of the covenant: "I will give unto thee, and to thy seed after thee, the land wherein thou art a stranger, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession" (Genesis 17:8). Hobbes emphasizes that this was a grant of real, territorial jurisdiction — not a metaphor. "The Kingdome of God is a Civill Kingdome" (Chapter XXXV), and the Land of Promise is its territory. The promise is fulfilled through the conquest under Joshua and the establishment of the monarchy under David — all understood by Hobbes as civil-political events.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Land of Promise in Chapter XXXV. The territorial promise to Abraham is the foundation of Hobbes' reading of biblical history as the history of a civil commonwealth.
