# David

King David serves Hobbes as the exemplary sovereign of the Israelite commonwealth — a king whose authority over religious matters was absolute despite not being a priest. Hobbes uses David's actions to prove that civil sovereignty includes supreme ecclesiastical authority.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses David in two key argumentative contexts. First, David establishes the pattern of sovereign authority in Israel: though he was of the tribe of Judah (not Levi), he exercised authority over the Ark of the Covenant and the priests, demonstrating that religious authority derives from civil sovereignty, not priestly lineage. Second, David's sin with Bathsheba and Uriah provides Hobbes with a complex case study in sovereign accountability — "David, when he killed Uriah, sinned against God only; because Uriah was his Subject, and consequently, David had the Right of Punishment over him" (on the principle that the sovereign cannot commit injustice against a subject, Chapter XVIII and XXI).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses David's authority in Chapter XL in the context of the succession of God's kingdom: "After the death of Moses, the Kingdome of God, in his Lieutenants... continued in the High Priest... till the time of Saul, and after Saul, in David, and his line for ever" (Chapter XL). In Chapter XXXIII, Hobbes discusses the authorship of the Psalms, traditionally attributed to David. David's taking of the shewbread (1 Samuel 21) is discussed as a case of sovereign prerogative overriding ceremonial law.

## Biblical/Historical Background

David (c. 1040–970 BC) was the second king of Israel, successor to Saul, and founder of the Davidic dynasty. He is presented in the books of Samuel, Kings, and Chronicles as Israel's greatest king — a warrior, poet, and administrator who united the tribes and established Jerusalem as the capital. In Christian theology, David is the ancestor of Jesus and a type of Christ. Hobbes' use of David as a model of undivided sovereignty reflects the Erastian argument that the kings of Israel, not the priests, held ultimate authority in the commonwealth.
