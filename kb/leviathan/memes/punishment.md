# Punishment

Hobbes provides a rigorous definition of punishment that excludes private vengeance, retroactive penalties, and punishments not authorized by law. His theory makes punishment a rational instrument of state power rather than an expression of moral desert.

## Definition

"A Punishment, is an Evill inflicted by publique Authority, on him that hath done, or omitted that which is Judged by the same Authority to be a Transgression of the Law; to the end that the will of men may thereby the better be disposed to obedience" (Chapter XXVIII (033-001)). Hobbes excludes several things from the category of true punishment: "All Evill which is inflicted without intention, or possibility of disposing the Delinquent... to obey the Lawes, is not Punishment; but an act of Hostility" (Chapter XXVIII (033-004)). He further specifies that "Evill inflicted upon a Subject, before a publique Condemnation, and Judiciall hearing, is not to be called Punishment; but an act of Hostility" (Chapter XXVIII (033-010)).

## Context

Punishment is treated in Chapter XXVIII ("Of Punishments, and Rewards") in Part II, as part of Hobbes' theory of the commonwealth. The sovereign's right to punish is derived from the original covenant — each subject authorized the sovereign to do whatever is necessary for peace and defence, including the infliction of punishment. However, Hobbes insists that punishment must be proportionate and publicly declared: law must precede punishment, because where there is no law, there is no transgression.

## Key Arguments

**Punishment Is Not Revenge.** Hobbes distinguishes punishment from "acts of Hostility" — the former serves the end of future obedience, while the latter serves no public purpose. Punishment must be "to the end that the will of men may thereby the better be disposed to obedience" (Chapter XXVIII (033-001)).

**No Punishment Without Prior Law.** "A Punishment can never cease to be due, as long as the Law remaines in force; but the Law may be Abolished, or a Pardon granted" (Chapter XXVIII (033-011)). The requirement of prior public law distinguishes Hobbes' theory from arbitrary tyranny — the sovereign punishes, but only according to laws the subject could have known.

**Punishment of the Innocent.** Hobbes acknowledges that the sovereign may sometimes punish the innocent — but this is "against the Law of Nature" because it violates the principle of gratitude and equity (Chapter XXVIII (033-017)). Yet the subject has no right of resistance, because the sovereign's authority is absolute.
