# School (Philosophical)

The philosophical schools, and particularly the Scholastic tradition of the medieval universities, function in *Leviathan* as Hobbes' paradigmatic example of institutionalised error. The Schools are not merely mistaken — they are politically dangerous. Their teachings provide the ideological foundation for divided sovereignty, papal pretension, and civil war. Hobbes' critique of the Schools runs from epistemology (their meaningless terminology) to politics (their seditious doctrines).

## Hobbes' Analysis

Hobbes accuses the Schools of substituting empty words for genuine knowledge. Terms like "Hypostatical, Transubstantiate, Consubstantiate, Eternal-Now" are "learned by rote from the Schooles" (006-025) and are not merely useless — they are instruments of deception. The Schoolmen's vocabulary is designed to intimidate the laity and to make ecclesiastical authority seem intellectually necessary.

> From hence it is, that the Schooles say, Heavy bodies fall downwards, out of an appetite to rest (003-001)

This apparently innocent example illustrates Hobbes' deeper charge: the Schools explain natural phenomena by attributing purposes to inanimate objects — a mode of explanation that is scientifically vacuous and, when applied to politics, disastrous. If bodies have "appetites," then political bodies can have "wills," and the church can claim to speak for the body politic's true will against the sovereign.

## Key Passages

**The Schools and Vain Philosophy**: Hobbes devotes Chapter XLVI to "Darknesse from Vain Philosophy." The Schools are the institutional carriers of this darkness: "the Universities, and from the Schooles of Law, or from the Books, which the eminent men in those Schooles have written" (032-037) propagate doctrines that undermine civil obedience.

**Meaningless Speech**: "For the Schooles find in meere Appetite to go, or move, no actuall Motion at all: but because some Motion they must acknowledge, they call it Metaphoricall Motion" (007-016). Hobbes' nominalism — his doctrine that names are arbitrary signs conventionally attached to things — makes the Schools' metaphysical vocabulary not false but meaningless. Words without referents are not propositions but noise.

**The Definition of Justice**: Hobbes singles out "that definition of Justice in the Schooles: For they say, that 'Justice is the constant will of giving to every man his own'" (016-033). This definition, he argues, presupposes what it claims to establish — what is a man's "own" is precisely what the law determines. The Schools' definition invites every man to judge his own case, the very condition of war.

## Political Danger of the Schools

The Schools matter to Hobbes because they matter to politics. Their doctrines — separated essences, eternal justice, the primacy of conscience — provide the intellectual framework for ecclesiastical resistance to civil authority. A population taught by the Schools to believe in transcendent standards of justice will not obey the sovereign when his commands conflict with priestly interpretation.

The remedy, for Hobbes, is institutional: "it is the duty of the Sovereign to be Judge of what Opinions and Doctrines are averse, and what conducing to Peace" (025-006). The Schools must be brought under civil control, or their teachings will continue to produce "Ghosts, and Fayries" — invisible authorities that compete with the visible sovereign.
