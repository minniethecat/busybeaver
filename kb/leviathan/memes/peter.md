# Peter (Apostle)

Simon Peter, the chief of Christ's apostles, is central to Hobbes' argument against papal supremacy. Hobbes interprets Christ's commission to Peter ("Feed my sheep") as a grant of pastoral (teaching) authority only — not temporal jurisdiction — thereby undermining the Catholic claim that Peter (and his successors, the Popes) received supreme governmental power.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Peter extensively in Chapter XLII ("Of Power Ecclesiasticall"), where he argues against Cardinal Bellarmine's defence of papal authority. Hobbes insists that "our Saviour gave to Peter no power but to teach, and to baptize" (Chapter XLII). The famous passage "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my Church" (Matthew 16:18) refers, in Hobbes' reading, not to Peter personally but to "the Confession of Peter" — Peter's declaration that Jesus is the Christ. The keys of the kingdom given to Peter are "not the keys of Heaven, but of the Kingdome of Heaven" — the power to open the way to salvation through teaching, not the power to govern.

## Key References

Hobbes addresses Peter's authority in Chapter XLII in his detailed refutation of Bellarmine's *De Summo Pontifice*. The discussion occupies a substantial portion of the chapter and represents one of Hobbes' most sustained pieces of biblical-legal argumentation.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Simon Peter (died c. AD 64–68) was one of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus and, according to Catholic tradition, the first Bishop of Rome (Pope). He is a prominent figure in the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles. The Catholic Church claims that Peter's primacy, and the succession of the Popes as his successors, confers universal jurisdiction over the Church — a claim that was one of the principal targets of Protestant polemic in the 16th and 17th centuries. Hobbes' argument against this claim is a sophisticated contribution to this debate.
