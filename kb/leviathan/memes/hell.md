# Hell

Hobbes' interpretation of hell is one of the most controversial elements of *Leviathan*. By reducing hell to the grave (in some passages) or to a future punishment after the resurrection (in others), he eliminates one of the principal instruments of ecclesiastical power — the threat of eternal damnation wielded by the clergy.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses hell in Chapters XXXVIII and XLIV. He argues that the Hebrew word *Sheol* and the Greek *Hades* properly refer to "the Grave" — the common destination of all the dead, not a place of eternal torment. The "Hell fire" and "everlasting torments" of the New Testament refer to punishments after the resurrection and Last Judgement — in a future earthly kingdom, not in a disembodied spiritual state. Hobbes' interpretation strips the clergy of the power to bind and loose in the afterlife: "there is no Government in the world to come, but that which is to be under Christ, after the Resurrection" (Chapter XXXVIII).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses hell in Chapters XXXVIII and XLIV. His materialist eschatology is one of the principal reasons *Leviathan* was denounced as heretical — the doctrine of eternal conscious torment was central to Christian orthodoxy.
