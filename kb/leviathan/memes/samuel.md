# Samuel

The prophet Samuel occupies a pivotal role in Hobbes' political reading of the Old Testament. Samuel's reluctant anointing of Saul as Israel's first king, and his subsequent rejection of Saul in favour of David, provides Hobbes with essential material for his argument about the nature of sovereign authority in Israel.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses Samuel most extensively in his analysis of 1 Samuel 8 — the passage where the Israelites demand a king and Samuel warns them what a king will do. Traditional Christian readings saw this as God's condemnation of monarchy, but Hobbes reads it as a description of the rights of sovereignty: "This is the Right of Kings" (Chapter XX (022-010)). Samuel's description of the king's powers — taking sons for war, daughters for service, fields and vineyards for his officers — is not a warning against tyranny but a catalogue of what any sovereign may rightfully do. The point is that "the power of a King... is not to be limited, because it is not to be resisted" (Chapter XX).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Samuel in Chapter XX, where he famously reinterprets 1 Samuel 8 as a statement of sovereign rights. In Chapter XL, Hobbes traces the transition of God's kingdom from the period of the Judges (including Samuel) to the monarchy of Saul and David.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Samuel is a major figure in the books of 1 and 2 Samuel. He was the last of the Judges of Israel and a prophet who anointed both Saul and David as kings. His mother Hannah's song (1 Samuel 2) and his call narrative (1 Samuel 3) are among the most famous passages in the Old Testament. Hobbes' reading of 1 Samuel 8 is one of the most notorious pieces of biblical exegesis in the history of political thought — turning an apparent anti-monarchical text into a defence of absolute sovereignty.
