# Baptism

Hobbes treats baptism as the sacrament of admission into Christ's kingdom. His interpretation emphasizes that baptism confers membership in a spiritual kingdom that is not yet of this world — and therefore does not exempt the baptized from obedience to the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses baptism primarily in Chapter XLII ("Of Power Ecclesiasticall"). He argues that baptism is a covenant — "a Contract of every man with every man" to profess faith in Christ. The power to baptize was given by Christ to his apostles, but "to Teach, and to Baptize, is all that our Saviour gave commission of to his Apostles" (Chapter XLII) — not to govern. The administration of baptism is therefore a ministerial, not a magisterial, function.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses baptism at length in Chapter XLII, where he refutes Bellarmine's argument that baptism and the other sacraments confer ecclesiastical jurisdiction. "Baptisme is a Sacrament of Admission into the Kingdome of God... But to have Authority to admit into the Kingdome of God, is not to have Authority to governe in it" (Chapter XLII).
