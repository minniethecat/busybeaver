# Creation

The Creation of the world is fundamental to Hobbes' argument for God's natural kingdom. Because God created everything, his power is irresistible and his right to rule is absolute — a model for all civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses the Creation in Chapter XXXI ("Of the Kingdome of God by Nature") to establish that God's sovereignty over all mankind is based on his irresistible power as Creator. "The Right of Nature, whereby God reigneth over men... is not to be derived from his Creating them, as if he desired obedience as a Gratitude for his benefits; but from his Irresistible Power" (Chapter XXXI). Creation is thus not a gift that creates obligation through gratitude but an assertion of power that creates obligation through irresistibility.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Creation in Chapter XXXI in the context of God's natural kingdom. He also references the Genesis creation account in Chapter XXXIV when discussing the meaning of "Spirit" in scripture — "The Spirit of God moved upon the face of the Waters" (Genesis 1:2) is interpreted materialistically.
