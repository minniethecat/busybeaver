# Honour

Hobbes redefines honour not as a moral quality but as the social expression of power. In his system, honouring someone is simply acknowledging their power, and all social relations can be understood through the lens of honour and dishonour.

## Definition

Hobbes defines honour by reducing it to power: "To pray to another, for ayde of any kind, is to HONOUR; because a signe we have an opinion he has power to help" (Chapter X (012-016)). Conversely, "To neglect, or to contemn, is to DISHONOUR" (Chapter X (012-017)). The sovereign determines what signs of honour are publicly recognized: "the value set upon them by the Soveraigne Power of the Common-wealth" determines which titles, offices, and ceremonies confer honour (Chapter X (011-056)). Honour is thus not an intrinsic quality but a publicly conferred status — "The manifestation of the Value we set on one another, is that which is called Honouring, and Dishonouring" (Chapter X (012-018)).

## Context

The analysis of honour in Chapter X ("Of Power, Worth, Dignity, Honour, and Worthinesse") is a remarkable piece of sociological analysis. Hobbes treats honour as a system of signs — external behaviours (praying, obeying, giving gifts) that communicate internal estimations of power. This semiotics of honour connects directly to his political theory: the sovereign must be the ultimate source of honour, because if private persons could confer public honour independently, they would compete with the sovereign's authority.

## Key Arguments

**Honour as a Sign of Power.** All honouring is fundamentally about power: "To obey, is to Honour; because no man obeyes them, whom they think have no power to help, or hurt them" (Chapter X (012-021)). This makes honour both a consequence of power and a means of acquiring it — reputation of power attracts followers, which increases actual power.

**The Sovereign as the Fountain of Honour.** In a commonwealth, "the Fountain of Honour" is the sovereign, who alone can create new titles, dignities, and marks of public distinction. Private judgments of honour that contradict the sovereign's determinations are a form of sedition, because they claim an authority the sovereign alone possesses.

**Honour Varies by Common-wealth.** What counts as honourable varies between commonwealths, because "the value of all things... is measured by the appetite of the Contractors" — and different sovereigns authorize different standards of honour (Chapter X (012-019)).
