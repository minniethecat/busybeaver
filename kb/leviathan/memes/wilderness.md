# Wilderness

The Wilderness of the Exodus is the transitional space in which Israel journeyed from bondage in Egypt to the possession of Canaan, and in which the covenant at Mount Sinai was made. For Hobbes, the wilderness is the setting of the foundational events of God's kingdom by covenant.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references the Wilderness in Chapter XXXV and Chapter XL. It is in the wilderness that God gave the Law to Moses at Mount Sinai, establishing his kingdom by covenant. The wilderness period is also when Moses exercised undivided civil and religious authority as God's lieutenant — a model Hobbes uses to argue that all legitimate sovereignty is originally undivided. The Israelites' "murmuring" in the wilderness (demanding food, water, and a return to Egypt) illustrates the dangers of popular discontent and the necessity of strong sovereign authority.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Wilderness in Chapters XXXV and XL. The Exodus-wilderness narrative is the biblical foundation of his argument that God's kingdom is a civil kingdom established by covenant.
