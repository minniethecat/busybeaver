# Solomon

King Solomon, David's son and successor, is cited by Hobbes both as an authority (the Book of Proverbs) and as a cautionary example (his descent into idolatry). Solomon's dual role reflects Hobbes' complex use of biblical materials — scripture provides political wisdom, but no biblical figure is exempt from Hobbes' critical reading.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses Solomon in two distinct ways. Positively, he cites Proverbs as a source of political wisdom — particularly Proverbs 24:21 ("My son, fear God and the King") which Hobbes reads as establishing that religious and civil obedience are inseparable (Chapter XXXI). Negatively, Solomon's idolatry — his building of high places for the gods of his foreign wives (1 Kings 11) — illustrates that "the wisest King of Israel... was seduced" by false worship (Chapter XLV). This proves that "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor" — because even the wisest man can err in religion, religious authority cannot be left to private judgment but must reside in the sovereign.

## Key References

Hobbes cites Solomon's Proverbs in Chapter XXXI to support the duty of obedience to the sovereign. In Chapter XLV, he discusses Solomon's idolatry as part of his argument against the veneration of images and the independence of ecclesiastical authority.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Solomon (c. 990–931 BC) was the son of David and Bathsheba, known in biblical tradition for his wisdom, wealth, and the building of the First Temple in Jerusalem. The books of Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Songs are traditionally attributed to him. His reign marked the zenith of Israel's power and prosperity, but his later years were marked by idolatry influenced by his many foreign wives. After his death, the kingdom split into Israel (north) and Judah (south). Hobbes' ambivalent treatment of Solomon — wise author of Proverbs, foolish idolater — serves his argument that no human wisdom, however great, can substitute for the institutional authority of the sovereign.
