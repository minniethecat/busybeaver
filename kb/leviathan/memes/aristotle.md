# Aristotle

Aristotle is less a historical figure in *Leviathan* than a philosophical antagonist — the representative of a tradition Hobbes aims to overthrow. Hobbes' rejection of Aristotle's doctrine of natural sociability, his teleological metaphysics, and his theory of mixed government structures the entire argument of *Leviathan*.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes treats Aristotle as the primary source of error in political philosophy. He rejects Aristotle's claim that man is by nature a political animal (Chapter XVII), attacks the doctrine of mixed government derived from Aristotle's *Politics* (Chapter XIX), and dismisses Aristotelian metaphysics of "separated essences" as meaningless (Chapter IV–V). Hobbes' own method — starting from definitions, reasoning by computation — is a direct alternative to Aristotelian syllogistic logic.

## Key References

Hobbes criticizes Aristotle most directly in Chapter XLVI, where he attacks "the Philosophy of Aristotle" as not only false but "repugnant to Government" (Chapter XLVI (049-001)). In Chapter XXI, he rejects the Aristotelian distinction between monarchy and tyranny as mere emotional labeling (Chapter XXI (023-023)). In Chapter IV, Hobbes criticizes Aristotle's doctrine of categories as serving "to no other end, but to make men think they have some knowledge" when they have only learned a vocabulary (Chapter IV (005-014)).

## Biblical/Historical Background

Aristotle (384–322 BC) was a Greek philosopher, student of Plato, and tutor to Alexander the Great. His works — especially the *Politics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*, and *Metaphysics* — dominated medieval scholastic philosophy. Hobbes studied Aristotle at Oxford and his rejection of Aristotelianism was a defining feature of his intellectual project. By the 17th century, Aristotle's authority was waning under the assault of the new mechanical philosophy, of which Hobbes was a leading exponent.
