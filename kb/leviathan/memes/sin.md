# Sin

In Hobbes' *Leviathan*, sin is the transgression of a law, understood always in relation to an obligating command. Hobbes collapses the traditional distinction between sin and crime at the level of definition — "A Crime, is a sinne, consisting in the Committing (by Deed, or Word) of that which the Law forbiddeth, or the Omission of what it hath commanded" (029-003) — while preserving it at the level of the tribunal: sin is judged by God, crime by the civil sovereign.

## Definition

Hobbes defines sin strictly in juridical terms. There is no sin where there is no law: "where no Civill Law is, there is no Crime" (029-003). This extends to divine law: the sovereign, though above civil law, "is subject to no Civill Law, sinneth in all he does against his Conscience" (031-007), meaning the sovereign remains accountable to God even while unaccountable to men. Sin therefore presupposes a known command and a capable agent — Hobbes rejects the idea that ignorance of the law excuses if the law has been sufficiently published.

The definitional link between sin and crime serves Hobbes' broader project of subordinating ecclesiastical jurisdiction to civil authority. By making every crime a sin (a violation of the law of nature, which is God's law) but not every sin a crime (only those prohibited by civil law), he creates a hierarchy in which the civil sovereign, not the church, determines punishable conduct.

## Sin and the Law of Nature

Sin in the state of nature is violation of the laws of nature, which "oblige *in foro interno*; that is to say, they bind to a desire they should take place: but *in foro externo*; that is, to the putting them in act, not alwayes" (017-002). Because there is no common power to enforce compliance, men in the state of nature are not accountable to any human tribunal. Sin, in this context, is a matter of conscience alone.

> So that the nature of Justice, consisteth in keeping of valid Covenants: but the Validity of Covenants begins not but with the Constitution of a Civill Power, sufficient to compell men to keep them. (017-003)

The transition from the state of nature to civil society transforms sin from a purely internal matter into a legally cognizable category. The sovereign's law becomes the public measure of right and wrong.

## Original Sin and Adam's Transgression

Hobbes addresses the doctrine of original sin primarily through its consequences — mortality and the loss of Edenic immortality:

> Adam if he had not sinned, had had an Eternall Life on Earth (041-002)

> Adam and Eve, if they had not sinned, had lived on Earth Eternally (041-003)

Hobbes does not dwell on inherited guilt. His concern is with the civil and theological consequences: Adam's sin introduced death into the world, which in turn made necessary the institution of a Redeemer and the establishment of God's kingdom by covenant. The chain is: sin → death → redemption → covenant → kingdom. This is Hobbes' distinctive reading of salvation history, in which each stage has institutional, not merely spiritual, implications.

## Sin, Repentance, and Salvation

Hobbes treats repentance as the mechanism by which sin is remitted. In his reading of the New Testament, "Repentance implyeth a turning away from sinne, which is the same, with the returne of the will to obedience" (046-023). The key word is *obedience* — Hobbes consistently frames salvation as a matter of obeying rightful authority. To repent is not to feel remorse but to resume compliance with law.

Remission of sins is tied to two conditions: faith in Christ and obedience to God's laws. Hobbes emphasizes that these are not separable: "faith to obtaine remission of sinnes to the Penitent, and consequently that justification, before God, is by Faith" (046-037). But this faith is demonstrated through works of obedience, not mere profession.

| Stage | Mechanism | Authority |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Sin | Transgression of law | Law of nature / civil law / divine law |
| Repentance | Return to obedience | Conscience + sovereign |
| Remission | Faith + obedience | God alone |
| Punishment | Sovereign's judgment | Civil sovereign |

## Sin in the Kingdom of Darkness

In Part IV, Hobbes addresses what he calls "Spirituall Darknesse from Misinterpretation of Scripture." One component of this darkness is the doctrine "That Subjects sinne in obeying the Commands of their Soveraigns" (032-037). Hobbes regards this teaching — which he associates with the Church of Rome — as politically seditious and theologically erroneous. If subjects may judge their sovereign's commands to be sinful, civil order dissolves.

> God spared not the Angels that sinned, but cast them down into Hell (037-034)

Hobbes invokes the fallen angels to reinforce the principle that even the most exalted beings are subject to judgment when they rebel against lawful authority. The lesson is pointed: if angels cannot plead privilege against divine law, subjects cannot plead conscience against civil law.
