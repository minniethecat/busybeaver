# Joshua

Joshua, the military leader who succeeded Moses and led the conquest of Canaan, appears in *Leviathan* as an example of sovereign authority exercised through warfare. Hobbes uses Joshua to argue that the sovereign's right to make war is absolute and divinely sanctioned.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references Joshua primarily in Part III, in his discussion of the Kingdom of God in Israel. Joshua succeeded Moses not through inheritance but through divine appointment — confirming Hobbes' view that sovereignty is constituted by authority (covenant or commission), not by blood. In Chapter XL, Hobbes notes that after Moses' death, "the Kingdome of God, in his Lieutenants... continued in the High Priest," but Joshua exercised civil-military authority as God's chosen commander. Joshua's conquest — including the utter destruction of Jericho and other Canaanite cities — illustrates Hobbes' claim that in war, the sovereign may do whatever is necessary for victory and security.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Joshua in Chapter XL in the context of the succession of God's authority after Moses. Joshua's role in leading the conquest of Canaan is also referenced in discussions of God's sovereign right over all nations, which is the basis of Hobbes' argument that God's kingdom is a civil kingdom.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Joshua, son of Nun, was Moses' assistant and successor as leader of the Israelites. The Book of Joshua describes the conquest and settlement of Canaan under his leadership, including the famous battle of Jericho (Joshua 6). He is presented as a faithful and obedient servant of God who completes the mission Moses began. In Christian typology, Joshua (whose name in Hebrew is the same as "Jesus") prefigures Christ as the one who leads God's people into the promised land.
