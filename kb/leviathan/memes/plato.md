# Plato

Plato appears in *Leviathan* primarily as part of Hobbes' critique of classical philosophy. While Hobbes treats Plato more respectfully than Aristotle, he ultimately rejects Platonic metaphysics as meaningless and Platonic politics as impractical.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes engages Plato in two main contexts. In Chapter XLVI, he groups Plato with Aristotle in his critique of "the Philosophy of the Grecians" — the doctrine of "separated Essences" is dismissed as "Vain Philosophy" (Chapter XLVI). However, in Chapter XXIX, Hobbes cites Plato approvingly: " Plato... saith, that Law is the Publique Conscience" — a formulation that aligns with Hobbes' own view that law is the public reason of the commonwealth.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Plato in Chapter XLVI ("Of Darknesse from Vain Philosophy") as part of the tradition of Greek metaphysics that he considers a source of error and sedition. In Chapter XXIX, Hobbes references Plato's definition of law in the context of the things that weaken a commonwealth.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Plato (c. 428–348 BC) was an Athenian philosopher, student of Socrates, and teacher of Aristotle. His dialogues — especially the *Republic*, *Laws*, and *Statesman* — are foundational texts of Western political philosophy. Hobbes' relationship to Plato is more complex than his outright rejection of Aristotle: he shares Plato's hostility to democracy, his emphasis on unity and order, and his belief that philosophy should serve political ends. But Hobbes rejects Plato's idealism and his belief in a transcendent Form of the Good accessible only to philosophers.
