# Warre

Hobbes' concept of war extends far beyond actual combat. In his famous formulation, war consists not in battle but in the known disposition to fight during a time when there is no assurance to the contrary. This definition transforms war from an event into a condition — the natural state of mankind.

## Definition

Hobbes gives his most famous definition in Chapter XIII: "Hereby it is manifest, that during the time men live without a common Power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called Warre; and such a warre, as is of every man, against every man. For WARRE, consisteth not in Battell onely, or in the act of fighting; but in a tract of time, wherein the Will to contend by Battell is sufficiently known" (Chapter XIII (014-008)). This definition is crucial: war is not an act but a disposition — a state of radical insecurity in which "there is no place for Industry... no Culture of the Earth... no Arts; no Letters; no Society; and which is worst of all, continuall feare, and danger of violent death; And the life of man, solitary, poore, nasty, brutish, and short" (Chapter XIII (014-009)).

## Context

The concept of war appears most forcefully in Chapter XIII ("Of the Naturall Condition of Mankind, as concerning their Felicity, and Misery"), where Hobbes deploys it to demolish the Aristotelian view that man is naturally political. The natural condition is not society but war — and war persists wherever there is no common power. This includes not only the hypothetical state of nature but also relations between sovereign states, which "are in continuall jealousies, and in the state and posture of Gladiators" (Chapter XIII (014-012)). The escape from this condition is the sole justification for the absolute sovereign.

## Key Arguments

**Three Causes of War.** Hobbes identifies three principal causes of conflict in human nature: "First, Competition; Secondly, Diffidence; Thirdly, Glory. The first, maketh men invade for Gain; the second, for Safety; and the third, for Reputation" (Chapter XIII (014-006)). These correspond to the three forms of power-seeking described in Chapters X–XI.

**Equality Produces War.** Paradoxically, it is the natural equality of mankind — the fact that "the weakest has strength enough to kill the strongest" — that makes war universal (Chapter XIII (014-001)). If men were radically unequal, the strong could simply dominate and peace would prevail. Equality creates the condition in which everyone has reason to fear everyone else.

**The Right to War.** In the state of nature, "every man has a Right to every thing; even to one anothers body" (Chapter XIV (015-004)). This unlimited right of nature — the liberty to do whatever one judges necessary for self-preservation — is the legal corollary of the fact of war.
