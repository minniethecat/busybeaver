# Cicero

Cicero serves Hobbes not as a philosophical authority but as a source of linguistic evidence for his theory of persons. Hobbes cites Cicero's use of the term "persona" to establish the theatrical and legal origins of the concept of representation.

## Role in Leviathan

Cicero appears in Chapter XVI ("Of Persons, Authors, and things Personated"), where Hobbes traces the etymology of "person." Hobbes notes that "Persona in latine signifies the Disguise, or Outward Appearance of a man, counterfeited on the Stage" and draws the connection to representation: "he that acteth another, is said to beare his Person." He then quotes Cicero: "Unus Sustineo Tres Personas; Mei, Adversarii, & Judicis — I beare three Persons; my own, my Adversaries, and the Judges" (Chapter XVI (017-004)). This brief citation is the philological foundation of Hobbes' entire theory of representation — the sovereign as actor, subjects as authors.

## Key References

The primary reference to Cicero is in Chapter XVI (017-004). Hobbes also mentions Cicero in the context of Roman political institutions in Chapter XXI, when discussing the liberty of subjects under different forms of commonwealth.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC) was a Roman statesman, orator, and philosopher whose works were central to Renaissance humanist education. His philosophical writings — particularly *De Officiis* (On Duties) and *De Re Publica* (On the Commonwealth) — influenced early modern political thought. Hobbes' use of Cicero is characteristic of his method: he mines classical sources not for their substantive doctrines (which he often rejects) but for linguistic and conceptual resources he can repurpose for his own system.
