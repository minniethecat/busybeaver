# Jesus Christ

The treatment of Jesus Christ in *Leviathan* is one of the most controversial aspects of Hobbes' political theology. Hobbes argues that Christ's mission was to teach and to establish a spiritual kingdom — not to exercise temporal authority — and that this principle requires the complete subordination of church to state in all earthly matters.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes' interpretation of Christ in Parts III and IV serves a single, relentless purpose: to demonstrate that no ecclesiastical authority can legitimately challenge civil sovereignty. Christ's kingdom "is not of this world" (John 18:36), meaning that "his office was to declare the way to the Kingdome... but not to reign in this world himself" (Chapter XLI (045-004)). Christ left no temporal government — he gave the apostles only the power to teach and to forgive sins, not to rule. Therefore, "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor, to whose charge the whole flock of his Subjects is committed" (Chapter XLII (046-024)). The Pope's claim to temporal authority over kings is, in Hobbes' view, a usurpation of Christ's teaching.

## Key References

Hobbes' discussion of Christ is concentrated in Chapters XLI ("Of the Office of our Blessed Saviour") and XLII ("Of Power Ecclesiasticall"). In Chapter XLI, Hobbes argues that Christ's three offices — Redeemer, Teacher, and King — are exercised at different times: he was Redeemer in his first coming, Teacher during his ministry, and will be King only at his second coming (Chapter XLI). In Chapter XLII, Hobbes argues that "there is no other Government in this life, neither of State, nor Religion, but Temporall" (Chapter XLII (046-040)).

## Biblical/Historical Background

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BC–c. AD 30/33) is the central figure of Christianity, believed by Christians to be the incarnate Son of God, whose life, death, and resurrection constitute the basis of Christian faith. Hobbes was writing in a context of intense debate about the relationship between church and state — the Reformation had shattered the unity of Western Christendom, and the English Civil War was fought in significant part over the question of whether the king or Parliament (increasingly allied with Puritan religious claims) held ultimate authority. Hobbes' radical solution was to give the sovereign all ecclesiastical power.
