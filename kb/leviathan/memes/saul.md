# Saul

King Saul is the first monarch of Israel and a key figure in Hobbes' biblical politics. His reign and rejection by God serve Hobbes as evidence that while God may judge sovereigns, subjects never may.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Saul primarily in the context of God's kingdom in Israel. Saul was anointed by Samuel at God's command, making him God's lieutenant — the civil sovereign whose authority derived from divine appointment through the prophet. When Saul disobeyed God's command regarding the Amalekites (1 Samuel 15), God rejected him and instructed Samuel to anoint David. Hobbes uses this to argue two points: first, that God alone (not the people) has the right to depose a sovereign; second, that the prophet Samuel acted as God's messenger, not as an independent ecclesiastical authority with the right to judge kings. In Chapter XL, Hobbes traces how "after Saul, in David, and his line for ever," God's kingdom was established by covenant (Chapter XL).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Saul's reign and rejection in Chapter XL, in the context of the succession of God's lieutenants in Israel. In Chapter XXXVI, Saul's consultation of the witch of Endor (1 Samuel 28) is discussed as an example of the biblical teaching on spirits and demons — Hobbes uses it to argue that Samuel's "ghost" was not actually Samuel but a phantasm.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Saul (fl. 11th century BC) was the first king of the united Kingdom of Israel, anointed by the prophet Samuel. His reign, described in 1 Samuel 9–31, was marked by military successes followed by progressive estrangement from God and Samuel. His jealousy of David and his eventual death in battle against the Philistines are among the most dramatic narratives in the Old Testament. Hobbes' treatment of Saul reflects his broader project of extracting political lessons from biblical narrative — lessons that consistently support absolute, undivided sovereignty.
