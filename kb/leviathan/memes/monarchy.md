# Monarchy

Monarchy — "when the Representative is One man" — is the form of government Hobbes most consistently favours. In Chapter XIX of *Leviathan*, he systematically compares monarchy with aristocracy and democracy, finding that "the difference between these three kindes of Government, consisteth not in the difference of Power; but in the difference of Convenience, or Aptitude to produce the Peace, and Security of the people" (021-003). Monarchy, he argues, is the most apt.

## Hobbes' Analysis

Hobbes' case for monarchy is pragmatic rather than principled. All sovereigns — whether one, few, or many — possess the same absolute power by the logic of the social contract. The question is only which form produces the fewest inconveniences. Hobbes identifies six advantages of monarchy:

> First, that the Publique and the Private interest are most closely united in Monarchy. (021-014)

> Secondly, that a Monarch receiveth counsell of whom, when, and where he pleaseth. (021-015)

> Thirdly, that the Resolutions of a Monarch are subject to no other Inconstancy, than that of Humane Nature. (021-016)

> Fourthly, that a Monarch cannot disagree with himselfe, out of envy, or interest. (021-017)

> Fifthly, that in Monarchy there is this inconvenience; that any Subject, by the power of one man, for the enriching of a favourite or flatterer, may be deprived of all he possesseth. (021-018)

The fifth point Hobbes concedes as a real disadvantage, but argues it is no worse in monarchy than in aristocracy (where favourites multiply) or democracy (where orators are the flatterers).

## Key Passages

**The Tyranny Objection**: "they that are discontented under Monarchy, call it Tyranny; and they that are displeased with Aristocracy, call it Oligarchy" (021-012). Hobbes dismisses the distinction between king and tyrant as merely verbal — a matter of the speaker's attitude, not the government's substance. The same power, loved, is monarchy; hated, is tyranny.

**Succession**: Hobbes devotes considerable attention to "the right of Succession, is in Monarchy: And the difficulty ariseth from this, that he that is to name a Successor, is alwayes the Soveraign" (021-028). This is monarchy's distinctive weakness: the sovereign's death creates an interval of uncertainty that can be exploited. Hobbes' solution is that the sovereign should designate his successor while alive — but he acknowledges this can fail.

**The Comparison with Assembly Government**: "they that live under the government of Democracy, or other Soveraign Assembly, attribute the incommodities of it to the Forme, but the commodity to the People" (020-039). This is a psychological asymmetry Hobbes exploits: men blame the monarch but credit the people, making assembly government seem better than it is.

## Monarchy in Hobbes' Common-Wealth

Hobbes wrote *Leviathan* during the English Civil War and the Interregnum. His defence of monarchy is not merely theoretical — it is an intervention in the most urgent political question of his time. Yet his argument is carefully structured to be acceptable to any sovereign, monarchical or otherwise. The logic of absolute sovereignty holds regardless of the number of rulers. Hobbes' preference for monarchy is a prudential judgment, not a deduction from first principles.

> The reason to be alike in a Monarchy, and in a Popular Government (020-023)

This is the core of Hobbes' political science: all sovereigns are equal in power, and their differences are matters of administrative convenience, not legitimacy. Monarchy wins on convenience, but any form of undivided sovereignty is acceptable.
