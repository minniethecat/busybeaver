# Consecration

Hobbes' treatment of consecration — the act of dedicating persons or things to God's service — reflects his consistent Erastianism. The power to consecrate is not an independent ecclesiastical power but a function of civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses consecration in the context of the Israelite commonwealth and its implications for Christian politics. In the Old Testament, "the Consecration of the High Priest, and of the Tabernacle" was performed by Moses — the civil sovereign (Chapter XL). This establishes the pattern: consecration is an act of the sovereign, not of a separate priestly caste. In a Christian commonwealth, "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor," and therefore has the authority to consecrate churches, appoint ministers, and determine the forms of public worship.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses consecration in Chapter XL in the context of Moses' authority, and in Chapter XLII in the context of the sovereign's ecclesiastical powers. The argument is consistent with his broader position that all acts of public religious significance fall under civil jurisdiction.
