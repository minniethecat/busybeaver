# Excommunication

Hobbes' treatment of excommunication is a crucial test case for his theory of the relationship between ecclesiastical and civil power. He insists that excommunication has no temporal consequences — it cannot deprive a person of civil rights, property, or liberty — and that the threat of excommunication cannot be used to challenge the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses excommunication in Chapter XLII. He defines it as "a sentence, whereby a man is separated from the company of the faithfull" — purely a matter of spiritual fellowship. Crucially, "Excommunication, where there is no Civill Power to Judge of it, hath no effect at all" (Chapter XLII). The power to excommunicate was given by Christ to the Apostles as part of their teaching ministry, not as a tool of government. In a Christian commonwealth, "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor" and therefore has ultimate authority over excommunication — the sovereign cannot be excommunicated, and excommunication cannot be used to release subjects from their political obligations.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses excommunication in Chapter XLII, in the context of his refutation of Bellarmine's arguments for papal power. The issue of excommunication was central to the conflicts between popes and kings in medieval Europe, and Hobbes' treatment is designed to eliminate any basis for ecclesiastical independence.
