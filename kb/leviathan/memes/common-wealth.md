# Common-wealth

The Common-wealth (Latin: *Civitas*) is Hobbes' term for the body politic — the artificial person brought into existence through the mutual covenant of every man with every man, authorizing a sovereign representative to act on their behalf for peace and common defence.

## Definition

Hobbes provides the definitive formulation of the Common-wealth in Chapter XVII: "This is more than Consent, or Concord; it is a reall Unitie of them all, in one and the same Person, made by Covenant of every man with every man." The multitude so united becomes "a COMMON-WEALTH, in latine CIVITAS. This is the Generation of that great LEVIATHAN, or rather (to speake more reverently) of that Mortall God, to which wee owe under the Immortall God, our peace and defence" (Chapter XVII (019-023)). Hobbes further condenses this into a formal definition: "One Person, of whose Acts a great Multitude, by mutuall Covenants one with another, have made themselves every one the Author, to the end he may use the strength and means of them all, as he shall think expedient, for their Peace and Common Defence" (Chapter XVII (019-024)).

## Context

The Common-wealth is the solution to the problem laid out in Part I of *Leviathan*. After establishing that the natural condition is war (Chapter XIII) and that the laws of nature cannot bind without coercive power (Chapters XIV–XV), Hobbes introduces the Common-wealth in Chapter XVII as the artificial remedy. It forms the conceptual foundation for all of Part II ("Of Common-wealth"), where Hobbes discusses the rights of sovereigns (Chapter XVIII), the forms of commonwealth (Chapter XIX), dominion (Chapter XX), and the liberty of subjects (Chapter XXI).

## Key Arguments

**Artificial Personhood.** The Common-wealth is not a natural entity but an artificial person, created when the multitude authorizes a representative. The unity of the Common-wealth derives from "the Unity of the Representer, not the Unity of the Represented" (Chapter XVI (017-014)). Without representation, the multitude remains many, not one.

**The End Is Peace and Defence.** The "finall Cause, End, or Designe of men" in instituting a Common-wealth is "the foresight of their own preservation, and of a more contented life" — escape from "that miserable condition of Warre" (Chapter XVII (019-011)). All the rights of the sovereign are justified by this end.

**Three Forms Only.** Hobbes identifies exactly three kinds of Common-wealth, distinguished by who holds sovereign power: Monarchy (one man), Aristocracy (an assembly of a part), and Democracy (an assembly of all). "Other kind of Common-wealth there can be none: for either One, or More, or All must have the Soveraign Power (which I have shewn to be indivisible) entire" (Chapter XIX (021-011)).
