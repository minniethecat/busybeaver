# Conventicle

The concept of the conventicle — an unauthorized religious assembly — illustrates Hobbes' insistence that all assemblies within a commonwealth must be authorized by the sovereign. The term had immediate political resonance in 17th-century England, where "conventicles" of Protestant dissenters were a source of ongoing political conflict.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses conventicles in Chapter XXII and Chapter XLII. In Chapter XXII ("Of Systemes Subject"), he distinguishes between "Politicall Systemes" (authorized by the sovereign) and "Private Systemes" (unauthorized assemblies, which are unlawful). A religious assembly not authorized by the sovereign "is not a lawfull Assembly, but an Unlawfull Conventicle" (Chapter XLII). The concept of the conventicle was central to the legal regime of the Clarendon Code (1661–1665), which aimed to suppress Nonconformist worship — Hobbes' theory provides the philosophical justification for this policy.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses conventicles in Chapters XXII and XLII. His treatment of unauthorized assemblies reflects his conviction that any independent gathering for religious purposes is potentially seditious because it creates an alternative source of authority and loyalty within the commonwealth.
