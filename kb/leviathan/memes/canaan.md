# Canaan

Canaan, the land promised to Abraham and conquered by Joshua, is the physical territory of the Kingdom of God. Hobbes reads the biblical narrative of the conquest not as a purely religious event but as the territorial foundation of a civil commonwealth.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Canaan in Chapters XXXV and XL. God's promise of the land to Abraham was part of the covenant establishing his kingdom: "I will give unto thee, and to thy seed after thee, the land wherein thou art a stranger, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession" (Genesis 17:8, cited in Chapter XXXV). The conquest of Canaan under Joshua was an act of sovereign authority — God, as sovereign, commanded the conquest; Joshua, as his lieutenant, executed it. The territory of Canaan is thus the geographical expression of God's covenant kingdom — a real land with real borders, not a metaphor for heaven.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Canaan in Chapters XXXV and XL. The Promised Land is the territorial dimension of his argument that God's kingdom is a civil, not merely spiritual, entity.
