# Adam

Adam is a recurring reference point in *Leviathan*, used by Hobbes to illustrate arguments about human nature, paternal authority, and the relationship between sin and law.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses Adam in several distinct contexts. In Chapter XIII, he refutes the idea that Adam's sin caused the natural world to become hostile — for Hobbes, the state of nature is a condition of human relations, not a cosmological condition. In Chapter XX, Hobbes discusses Adam's dominion over his children: "Dominion is acquired two wayes; By Generation, and by Conquest" — and Adam's authority over his offspring is the model of paternal dominion (Chapter XX (022-004)). In Chapter XXXI, Hobbes notes that Adam knew God through natural reason — establishing that natural religion is possible without scripture.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Adam's condition before the Fall in Chapter XXXI ("Of the Kingdome of God by Nature"). He argues that Adam's knowledge of God was natural, not revealed: "God declared his Power by the Creation, and his Goodnesse in that he gave being to Adam before he had sinned" (Chapter XXXI). Adam's dominion over Eve and his children is discussed in Chapter XX as the prototype of paternal sovereignty.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Adam is the first human being in Genesis 1–3, created by God and placed in the Garden of Eden. His disobedience — eating the forbidden fruit — results in the Fall and the introduction of sin and death into the world. In Christian theology, Adam is the typological counterpart to Christ (the "second Adam"). Hobbes' treatment of Adam is notably naturalistic — he downplays the Fall's cosmic significance and reads Adam primarily as the first father and sovereign.
