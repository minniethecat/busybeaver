# Kingdom of God

The Kingdom of God is the organizing concept of Hobbes' political theology. His argument that God's kingdom is "a Civill Common-wealth" — not a spiritual or merely metaphorical kingdom — is the foundation of his claim that ecclesiastical authority is subordinate to civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes develops the concept of the Kingdom of God across Parts III and IV. He distinguishes between God's "Naturall Kingdome" (over all mankind, by virtue of his irresistible power as Creator) and his "Propheticall Kingdome" (over Israel, by virtue of covenant). The latter was a real civil commonwealth in which "God himselfe was the Soveraigne" and Moses, the High Priests, and the Kings of Judah were his lieutenants (Chapters XXXV, XL). In the Christian era, God's kingdom is "not of this world" — it will be established on earth only after Christ's second coming and the resurrection. This means that "there is no other Government in this life, neither of State, nor Religion, but Temporall" (Chapter XLII).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Kingdom of God extensively in Chapters XXXI, XXXV, XXXVIII, XL, XLI, and XLII — nearly the entirety of Parts III and IV is structured around this concept. It is arguably the single most important theological concept in *Leviathan*.
