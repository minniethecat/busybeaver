# Justice

In Hobbes' moral philosophy, justice is not an independent moral quality but is derived entirely from the keeping of covenants. This definition makes justice an artificial virtue that cannot exist in the state of nature and depends entirely on the existence of a sovereign power to enforce covenants.

## Definition

Hobbes provides one of the most striking definitions in the history of philosophy: "When a Covenant is made, then to break it is Unjust: And the definition of INJUSTICE, is no other than the not performance of Covenant. And whatsoever is not Unjust, is Just" (Chapter XV (016-032)). This definition is purely formal — justice has no content beyond covenant-keeping. Before any covenant exists, "nothing can be Unjust. The notions of Right and Wrong, Justice and Injustice have there no place. Where there is no common Power, there is no Law: where no Law, no Injustice" (Chapter XIII (014-013)). He elaborates on types of justice in Chapter XV: "Commutative Justice" is the justice of a contractor — "the performance of Covenant, in buying, and selling" — while "Distributive Justice" is "the Justice of an Arbitrator; that is to say, the act of defining what is Just" (Chapter XV (016-044)).

## Context

Justice is established in Chapter XV ("Of other Lawes of Nature") as the third Law of Nature: "That men performe their Covenants made." Hobbes' definition of justice marks a radical break with classical and scholastic traditions that saw justice as an objective moral order. For Hobbes, justice does not precede political society — it is created by it. In the state of nature, where no covenants are binding because no power enforces them, "Force, and Fraud, are in warre the two Cardinall vertues" (Chapter XIII (014-013)). Justice becomes possible only when the sovereign provides the "terror of some Power" to make covenant-keeping rational.

## Key Arguments

**No Justice Without a Sovereign.** Justice requires not just covenants but an enforcing power. Without the sovereign's sword, there is no assurance that others will keep their covenants, and therefore no obligation to keep one's own. Hobbes rejects the "Fool" who "hath sayd in his heart, there is no such thing as Justice" — the Fool argues it is sometimes reasonable to break covenants, but Hobbes replies that in a commonwealth, "he that breaketh his Covenant... cannot be received into any Society" and "consequently is cast out of Society, and perisheth" (Chapter XV (016-034)).

**The Foole's Objection.** Hobbes raises and answers the most serious challenge to his theory of justice: the argument that covenant-breaking can be rational when it serves one's interest. He responds that reason dictates keeping covenants because the long-term consequences of being excluded from society outweigh any short-term gain — a proto-game-theoretic argument.

**Justice Toward Men, Not God.** Hobbes distinguishes justice of actions from justice of men: "a Just man... is he that taketh all the care he can, that his Actions may be all Just" (Chapter XV (017-009)). Justice concerns human relations, not divine judgment — another departure from scholastic natural law theory.
