# Ordination

Hobbes discusses ordination as part of his broader argument about ecclesiastical authority. The laying on of hands by the Apostles conferred the authority to teach and baptize — not the authority to govern — and in a Christian commonwealth, the civil sovereign determines who may exercise pastoral functions.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses ordination in Chapter XLII. The Apostles "ordained Elders in every Church" (Acts 14:23), but this ordination was "by the holding up of hands" — a democratic procedure that conferred teaching authority, not ruling authority. In a Christian commonwealth, "the Pastors are chosen by the Church, but their Authority to teach, is from the Civill Soveraign" (Chapter XLII). The sovereign may "ordain Pastors, and Teachers" — the power of ordination, like all ecclesiastical powers, is ultimately civil.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses ordination in Chapter XLII as part of his argument that "the Civill Soveraign is Supreme Pastor." The discussion connects ordination to the broader themes of authorization and representation developed in Chapter XVI.
