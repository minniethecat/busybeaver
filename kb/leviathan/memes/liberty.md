# Liberty

In Hobbes' philosophy, liberty is defined in purely physical terms — the absence of opposition or external impediments to motion — and is then extended to human beings and political subjects. This mechanistic definition underpins Hobbes' controversial argument that subjects retain liberty only where the law is silent.

## Definition

Hobbes defines liberty in unmistakably physical terms: "LIBERTY, or FREEDOME, signifieth (properly) the absence of Opposition; (by Opposition, I mean externall Impediments of motion;)" (Chapter XXI (023-001)). This definition applies equally to inanimate bodies, animals, and humans: "a Free-Man, is he, that in those things, which by his strength and wit he is able to do, is not hindred to doe what he has a will to" (Chapter XXI (023-002)). Crucially, "Feare, and Liberty are consistent; as when a man throweth his goods into the Sea for Feare the ship should sink, he doth it neverthelesse very willingly, and may refuse to do it if he will: It is therefore the action, of one that was free" (Chapter XXI (023-003)). This compatibility of fear and liberty is essential to Hobbes' argument that covenants made under fear of the sovereign are freely made and therefore binding.

## Context

The concept of liberty appears most prominently in Chapter XXI ("Of the Liberty of Subjects"), where Hobbes confronts the apparent tension between absolute sovereignty and individual freedom. The definition also draws on the mechanistic psychology of Part I: as all human action is caused by appetites and aversions (Chapter VI), liberty cannot mean freedom from causation — only freedom from external restraints. This places Hobbes in opposition to republican conceptions of liberty as participation in self-government.

## Key Arguments

**Liberty and Fear Are Compatible.** Hobbes' most distinctive claim is that actions done from fear are free: "Feare, and Liberty are consistent" (Chapter XXI (023-003)). This applies directly to the social contract — subjects freely covenant to obey the sovereign, even though they do so from fear of the state of nature.

**The Liberty of Subjects.** In a commonwealth, "the Liberty of a Subject, lyeth therefore onely in those things, which in regulating their actions, the Soveraign hath praetermitted" — that is, where the law is silent (Chapter XXI (023-018)). The subject is not free to disobey the sovereign's commands, but retains liberty in matters the sovereign has not regulated.

**The Right of Self-Preservation.** Hobbes acknowledges one inalienable liberty: a subject retains "the Liberty to defend his owne body" even against the sovereign's command (Chapter XXI (023-026)). No covenant can oblige a man not to resist violence against his own life.
