# Sovereign

The central political concept in Hobbes' *Leviathan*: the artificial person (one man or an assembly) who represents the unified will of the multitude and holds absolute, indivisible sovereign power, instituted by covenant for the end of peace and common defence.

## Definition

Hobbes defines the Sovereign in explicit terms through the mechanism of the social covenant. When individuals confer all their power and strength upon one man or assembly, that representative becomes the bearer of their collective person:

> "I Authorise and give up my Right of Governing my selfe, to this Man, or to this Assembly of men, on this condition, that thou give up thy Right to him, and Authorise all his Actions in like manner." (019-023)

From this covenant arises the Commonwealth, and within it:

> "he that carryeth this Person, is called SOVERAIGNE, and said to have Soveraigne Power; and every one besides, his SUBJECT." (019-025)

The essence of the Commonwealth itself is defined as "One Person, of whose Acts a great Multitude, by mutuall Covenants one with another, have made themselves every one the Author, to the end he may use the strength and means of them all, as he shall think expedient, for their Peace and Common Defence." (019-024)

## Context

The Sovereign appears at the pivotal moment of Hobbes' argument in Part II, "Of Common-wealth." Having established in Part I that the natural condition of mankind is war (Chapter XIII (015-009)), and that the laws of nature alone cannot secure peace because "Covenants, without the Sword, are but Words" (Chapter XVII (019-012)), Hobbes makes the Sovereign the solution to the problem of human conflict. The concept bridges Hobbes' anthropology (Part I) and his political theory (Part II), forming the foundation for his arguments about the rights of sovereignty (Chapter XVIII), the forms of commonwealth (Chapter XIX), and the relationship between civil and ecclesiastical power (Parts III and IV).

## Key Arguments

**Institution by Covenant.** The Sovereign is created not by nature but by artifice — through a mutual covenant in which each individual authorizes the sovereign to act on their behalf. This makes every subject the "Author" of whatever the Sovereign does (Chapter XVI (017-014)). No man can justly protest the actions of the institution he himself authorized (Chapter XVIII (020-005)).

**Absolute and Indivisible Power.** The rights of the Sovereign are absolute and cannot be forfeited, transferred, or divided (Chapter XVIII (020-004)). Sovereign power is indivisible: "to erect two Soveraigns" is to "divide that Power, which (if men will live in Peace) is indivisible, and thereby reduce the Multitude into the condition of Warre" (Chapter XIX (021-013)).

**Two Paths to Sovereignty.** Hobbes distinguishes between two ways of attaining sovereign power: a Commonwealth by Institution (voluntary covenant among men) and a Commonwealth by Acquisition (conquest or paternal dominion). Crucially, "the Rights, and Consequences of Soveraignty, are the same in both" (Chapter XX (022-003)) — the sovereign's authority does not depend on how it was acquired.

**Judgment Without Appeal.** The Sovereign is the ultimate judge of what is necessary for peace and defence, of doctrines, and of all controversies. The Sovereign's actions cannot be justly accused by any subject (Chapter XVIII (020-006)), and "What Soever the Soveraigne Doth, Is Unpunishable By The Subject" (Chapter XVIII (020-007)).
