# Fear

Fear is the most important passion in Hobbes' political philosophy — at once the cause of the state of war (mutual fear) and the solution to it (fear of the sovereign's sword).

## Definition

Hobbes classifies fear among the aversions: "Aversion, with opinion of Hurt from the object, FEARE" (Chapter VI (007-003)). In the state of nature, this fear is universal — every person fears every other, because all are equal in their capacity to kill: "From this equality of ability, ariseth equality of hope in the attaining of our Ends. And therefore if any two men desire the same thing, which neverthelesse they cannot both enjoy, they become enemies" (Chapter XIII (014-003)).

## Context

Fear operates at every level of Hobbes' system. In Part I, it is a fundamental passion (Chapter VI). In Chapter XIII, mutual fear drives men to war. In Chapter XIV, fear of death motivates the search for peace. In Chapter XVII, fear of the sovereign's coercive power makes covenants binding: "Covenants, without the Sword, are but Words" (Chapter XVII (019-012)). Fear is thus both the disease and the cure.

## Key Arguments

**Fear as the Origin of Society.** Hobbes rejects the Aristotelian view that humans are naturally political. Society originates from mutual fear and the fear of violent death, which drives men to create the commonwealth.

**Fear and Liberty Are Consistent.** Actions done from fear are voluntary — a man who throws goods overboard to save his ship "doth it neverthelesse very willingly" (Chapter XXI (023-003)). Covenants made from fear are fully binding, which makes the social contract valid.

**The Terror of the Sovereign.** The sovereign's power rests on fear: without "the terror of some Power," the laws of nature go unobserved (Chapter XVII (019-012)). Fear of punishment is what makes covenant-keeping rational.
