# Sabbath

Hobbes treats the Sabbath as an example of a ceremonial law — binding under the Old Covenant but, like all ceremonial laws, subject to the sovereign's determination in a Christian commonwealth. His discussion of the Sabbath illustrates his broader argument about the relationship between divine and civil law.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Sabbath primarily in Chapter XXXV and Chapter XLII. In the Kingdom of God by the Old Covenant, observance of the Sabbath was commanded by God through Moses. But in the Christian era, ceremonial laws — including the Sabbath — are binding only insofar as the civil sovereign commands their observance. Hobbes distinguishes between the moral law (which is eternal, being identical with the law of nature) and the ceremonial law (which is positive law, changeable by the sovereign). The Sabbath belongs to the latter category.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Sabbath in Chapter XXXV in the context of God's kingdom by covenant. In Chapter XLII, he argues that "the Civill Lawes are the Rules of Good and Evill, Just and Unjust" — including determinations about which days are to be observed as holy.
