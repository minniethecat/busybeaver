# Independency

Independency — the Congregationalist claim that each local church is autonomous and governed by its own members — is treated by Hobbes as one of the errors that weaken a commonwealth. Like Presbyterianism and Catholicism, Independency separates spiritual authority from civil sovereignty, but it goes further in dispersing that authority to every individual congregation.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses Independency in Chapter XLII and Chapter XLVII. He groups it with Presbyterianism as Protestant variants of the papal error — the claim that there is a spiritual jurisdiction independent of civil sovereignty. Independency is particularly dangerous because it allows "every man to be Judge of his own Religion" — which is "to reduce the Common-wealth to the condition of Nature, where every man is Judge of what is Good, and Evill" (Chapter XLII). The Independents were a significant political force during the English Civil War, closely associated with Oliver Cromwell and the New Model Army.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Independency in Chapter XLII, in the context of the different forms of church government. His criticism is less extensive than his attack on Catholicism but reflects his concern that religious fragmentation undermines political unity.
