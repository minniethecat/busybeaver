# Knowledge

Hobbes opens Chapter IX with a foundational distinction: "There are of KNOWLEDGE two kinds; whereof one is *Knowledge Of Fact*: the other *Knowledge Of The Consequence Of One Affirmation To Another*" (010-001). The former is absolute, derived from sense and memory; the latter is conditional, called Science, and constitutes the domain of philosophy. This taxonomy is not merely descriptive — it is the organising principle of Hobbes' entire intellectual system, from natural philosophy to civil philosophy.

## Two Kinds of Knowledge

Hobbes defines Knowledge of Fact as "nothing else, but Sense and Memory, and is Absolute Knowledge; as when we see a Fact doing, or remember it done: And this is the Knowledge required in a Witnesse" (010-001). Its register is History — either natural history (facts of nature independent of human will) or civil history (the voluntary actions of men in commonwealths).

Science, by contrast, is "Conditionall; as when we know, that, *If The Figure Showne Be A Circle, Then Any Straight Line Through The Centre Shall Divide It Into Two Equall Parts*" (010-001). Its register is books of philosophy — demonstrations of consequences from one affirmation to another. The philosopher, not the witness, is the paradigm knower of science.

> Science is the knowledge of Consequences, and dependance of one fact upon another: by which, out of that we can presently do, we know how to do something else when we will, or the like, another time. (006-027)

This definition makes science fundamentally practical. Knowledge of consequences enables action; the purpose of knowing causes is to produce effects.

## The Chain from Sense to Science

Hobbes traces knowledge through a developmental sequence rooted in his materialist psychology. Sense produces memory; accumulated memory of many similar things constitutes experience; experience yields prudence — "a Presumption of the Future, contracted from the Experience of time Past" (005-008). But prudence is not science.

> As, much Experience, is Prudence; so, is much Science, *Sapience*. For though wee usually have one name of Wisedome for them both; yet the Latines did alwayes distinguish between *Prudentia* and *Sapientia*. (006-031)

The transition from prudence to science requires reason — "the Pace; Encrease of *Science*, the *Way*; and the Benefit of man-kind, the *End*" (006-030). Reason is computation with names, and when names are properly defined and consequences correctly deduced, the result is science.

## The Classification of Sciences

In Chapter IX, Hobbes presents a systematic table of the sciences, organised hierarchically:

| Level | Science | Subject Matter |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Philosophia Prima | First Philosophy | Quantity and Motion indeterminate |
| Mathematiques | Geometry, Arithmetic | Quantity and Motion determined by figure and number |
| Cosmography | Astronomy, Geography | Great parts of the world |
| Physiques | Meteorology, etc. | Qualities of bodies |
| Morall Philosophy | Ethics | Consequences of passions |
| Civill Philosophy | Politics | Consequences of speech and contracts |

> Geometry, (which is the onely Science that it hath pleased God hitherto to bestow on mankind,) (005-023)

Hobbes' admiration for geometry is central to his method. He believes that if moral and civil philosophy could be built on equally clear definitions, their conclusions would be as certain as those of geometry. The problem is not the subject matter but the abuse of speech — "metaphors, and senselesse and ambiguous words" (005-024) that lead men into absurdity.

## Knowledge and Power

For Hobbes, knowledge is not contemplative but instrumental. Understanding causes gives men the capacity to produce effects: "by which, out of that we can presently do, we know how to do something else when we will" (006-027). This instrumental conception of knowledge underlies Hobbes' entire civil philosophy: the sovereign needs knowledge of human nature to govern effectively, and subjects need knowledge of the law to obey correctly.

> But yet they that have no Science, are in better, and nobler condition, with their naturall Prudence; than men, that by mis-reasoning, or by trusting them that reason wrong, fall upon false and absurd generall rules. (006-029)

This is a characteristic Hobbesian caution: false science is worse than no science. A man acting on prudence alone may err, but a man acting on false doctrine will err systematically.
