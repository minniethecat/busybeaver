# Levitical Priesthood

Hobbes' analysis of the Levitical Priesthood is central to his argument that all ecclesiastical authority derives from and is subordinate to civil sovereignty. The priests of Israel exercised their functions not by inherent spiritual authority but as lieutenants of the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Levitical Priesthood primarily in Chapter XL, tracing the succession of authority after Moses. Aaron and his descendants were set apart for priestly service, but "the High Priest was the Lieutenant of God" — his authority came from God through Moses, the civil sovereign. After the establishment of the monarchy, "the Kings of Judah were the Lieutenants of God," and the High Priest exercised authority only under the king. Hobbes draws the political lesson: "the Civill Soveraign in every Common-wealth, is the Supreme Pastor" — the Christian sovereign occupies the same position relative to Christian ministers that the Kings of Judah occupied relative to the Levitical priests (Chapter XL).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Levitical Priesthood in Chapter XL ("Of the Rights of the Kingdome of God, in Abraham, Moses, the High Priests, and the Kings of Judah"). This chapter is the biblical foundation for his Erastian argument in Chapter XLII.
