# Scripture (Holy Bible)

The Holy Scripture is the most frequently cited source in *Leviathan* and the foundation of Hobbes' political theology. Hobbes does not treat scripture as a repository of mystical truths but as a body of law — the record of God's commands, covenants, and acts of sovereignty. "The Scripture was written, that the Kingdome of God might be administred by our Saviour" (035-009), and its authority derives not from the church that transmits it but from the sovereign who enforces it.

## Hobbes' Use of This Work

Hobbes cites scripture strategically, not devotionally. In Part I (Of Man), scripture is largely absent — the argument proceeds from definitions and deductions. In Part II (Of Common-Wealth), scripture appears to confirm conclusions already reached by reason: "Let us now consider what the Scripture teacheth in the same point. To Moses, the children of Israel say thus" (022-016). In Parts III and IV, scripture becomes the primary field of argument, as Hobbes undertakes to demonstrate that "the Principles from Authority of Scripture" (032-027) support his Erastian conclusions.

Hobbes' hermeneutical principle is clear: scripture means what the sovereign says it means. "For, whosoever hath a lawfull Power over any Writing, to make it Law, hath the power also to approve, or disapprove the interpretation of the same" (036-045). This is not a theological claim but a political one — it follows from the logic of sovereignty itself.

## Key Citations

**The Canon Question**: Hobbes devotes Chapter XXXIII to "Of the Number, Antiquity, Scope, Authority, and Interpreters of the Books of Holy Scripture." He argues that the canon was determined not by ecclesiastical councils of their own authority but by civil sovereigns: "the question of the Scripture, is the question of what is Law" (036-020). The books of scripture are laws, and only the sovereign can make or authenticate laws.

**Scripture as Law**: "the Scripture of the New Testament is there only Law, where the lawfull Civill Power hath made it so" (045-043). Hobbes' most radical claim: scripture has no binding force independent of the civil sovereign who adopts it. The Bible is not self-authenticating; its authority is mediated through the state.

**The Scope of Scripture**: Hobbes enumerates the books of the Old Testament (036-020) and New Testament (036-021), arguing that their canonisation was a political act. The church received, but did not create, the canon — and the sovereign may determine which books are received.

## Context

In 17th-century England, the question of scriptural authority was at the centre of political conflict. Protestants insisted on *sola scriptura* — scripture alone as the rule of faith — but disagreed violently about who could interpret it. Catholics claimed the church as the infallible interpreter; Puritans claimed the individual conscience. Hobbes' solution was characteristically political: the civil sovereign is the authoritative interpreter, for the same reason the sovereign is the authoritative interpreter of all law.
