# Job

The figure of Job provides Hobbes with his most powerful biblical argument for absolute sovereignty. God's response to Job from the whirlwind — "Where wast thou when I layd the foundations of the earth?" — is the ultimate expression of power not answerable to human reason, which Hobbes takes as the paradigm for sovereign authority.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes uses Job most extensively in Chapter XXXI ("Of the Kingdome of God by Nature") and in his discussion of the problem of evil in Chapter XXXI. When Job demands that God justify his suffering, God does not provide a rational explanation but asserts his absolute power: "God himselfe... having planted in Job an opinion of his power, asked him these questions; Where wast thou when I layd the foundations of the earth? Job 38.4" (Chapter XXXI). Hobbes draws the political lesson: "Power irresistible justifies all actions, really and properly, in whomsoever it be found; lesse power does not" — because God's power is irresistible, whatever God does to Job is just by definition.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Job in Chapter XXXI, where the Book of Job is the key text for his argument that God's sovereignty is based on power, not on covenant or creation. The lesson Hobbes draws from Job is that subjects have no right to question the sovereign's actions, because "Power irresistible" makes all actions just — just as Job had no right to question God.

## Biblical/Historical Background

Job is the protagonist of the Book of Job, a wisdom text of the Hebrew Bible. Job is a righteous man who suffers catastrophic losses — his children, his wealth, his health — as part of a test permitted by God. Job demands an explanation, and God answers from a whirlwind with a series of rhetorical questions asserting his creative power and wisdom. Job repents "in dust and ashes," and God restores his fortunes. The book is one of the most profound treatments of the problem of suffering in world literature. Hobbes' reading — that might makes right — is deliberately provocative and reduces the theological complexity of the book to a single political principle.
