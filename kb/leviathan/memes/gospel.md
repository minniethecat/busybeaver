# Gospel

Hobbes interprets the Gospel not as a new law but as the proclamation that Christ is the Messiah and that his kingdom will be established at the resurrection. This interpretation is central to his argument that the Gospel does not create an ecclesiastical government independent of civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Gospel in Chapter XLI and Chapter XLII. The word "Gospel" means "good news" — specifically, the good news "that Jesus is the Christ" (Chapter XLI). This proclamation was the sole content of the Apostles' preaching. The Gospel "is not a Law, but a Proclamation of a Kingdome to come" — it announces a future condition, not a present jurisdiction. Therefore, "the Ministers of the Gospel... have no Commission to Command, but onely to Preach" (Chapter XLI).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Gospel in Chapters XLI–XLII, where he systematically argues that the New Testament does not establish any form of church government with temporal authority. The Gospel "was not given to the Apostles, as a new Law, to governe the world by; but as a Doctrine, to be preached."
