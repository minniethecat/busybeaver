# Levites

The tribe of Levi, set apart for priestly and Temple service in the Old Testament, provides Hobbes with a key biblical precedent for the subordination of ecclesiastical officers to the civil sovereign.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses the Levites in Chapter XL. Though they were "separated to the service of the Tabernacle," the Levites were not independent of civil authority: "Moses and Aaron were of the Tribe of Levi; but the High Priesthood was not hereditary to the Tribe of Levi" (Chapter XL). The Levites received tithes and cities, but these were granted by the civil sovereign — they had no inherent right to them. Hobbes draws the political lesson: "the maintenance of the Ministers of the Church, is by the Civill Soveraign to be determined."

## Key References

Hobbes discusses the Levites in Chapter XL, in the context of the Kingdom of God in Israel. The subordination of the Levitical order to the civil sovereign is part of his broader argument against any independent ecclesiastical jurisdiction.
