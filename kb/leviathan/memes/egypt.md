# Egypt

Egypt is the biblical land of Israel's bondage and the paradigmatic foreign kingdom in *Leviathan*. Hobbes uses Egypt to illustrate the difference between a kingdom under God's covenant (Israel) and a kingdom that is not — and to emphasize that even Pharaoh's authority was legitimate civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes references Egypt in several contexts. In Chapter XXXV, Egypt is the land from which God delivered Israel, establishing his kingdom by covenant at Sinai. In Chapter XL, Joseph's rule over Egypt as Pharaoh's lieutenant illustrates the model of delegated sovereignty. In Chapter XX, Hobbes discusses how the Israelites' subjection to Pharaoh was a form of dominion by acquisition. Hobbes does not treat Pharaoh as an illegitimate ruler — he had sovereign authority over Egypt by conquest, and the Israelites were legitimately his subjects until God delivered them.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses Egypt in Chapters XX, XXXV, and XL. The Exodus from Egypt is the foundational event of God's kingdom by covenant, and Egypt serves as the negative counterpart to Israel throughout Hobbes' biblical politics.
