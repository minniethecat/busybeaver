# Marriage

Hobbes' treatment of marriage is characteristically juridical. Marriage is a contract, like all other contracts, and its regulation falls under the authority of the civil sovereign — not the church.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses marriage in Chapter XX (on paternal dominion) and Chapter XLVII (on the benefits of the Leviathan state). Marriage establishes the family, which Hobbes treats as a "little Common-wealth" — the smallest unit of political organization. The rights of parents over children derive from the marriage contract and the consent of the children (expressed or implied). Hobbes insists that "Matrimony is no Sacrament" — the Catholic Church's claim to jurisdiction over marriage is an unwarranted extension of ecclesiastical power into the civil sphere (Chapter XLVII).

## Key References

Hobbes discusses marriage in Chapter XX in the context of dominion by generation, and in Chapter XLVII in the context of his critique of Papal usurpations of civil authority.
