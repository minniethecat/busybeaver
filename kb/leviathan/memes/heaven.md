# Heaven

Hobbes' treatment of heaven is characteristically materialist and political. He systematically distinguishes between different biblical uses of the term, always with the aim of redirecting expectation from a spiritual afterlife to a future earthly kingdom under God's civil sovereignty.

## Role in Leviathan

Hobbes discusses heaven in Chapters XXXIV and XXXVIII. He distinguishes three senses: (1) the physical heavens — "the spacious ayr" (Chapter XXXIV); (2) the Kingdom of Heaven — which is God's civil kingdom on earth, preached by Christ as a future reality; and (3) the eternal dwelling of the blessed — which Hobbes identifies not as a disembodied spiritual state but as life in the resurrected body on a renewed earth after the Last Judgement. "The Kingdome of Heaven is a Kingdome of the living, not of the dead" (Chapter XXXVIII). This interpretation eliminates the clergy's claim to hold the keys to a spiritual heaven — the heaven of Christian hope is a future civil commonwealth.

## Key References

Hobbes discusses heaven in Chapters XXXIV and XXXVIII. His materialist interpretation of heaven is one of the most radical features of his biblical hermeneutics.
