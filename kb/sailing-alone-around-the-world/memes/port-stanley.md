## Navigational Reference

Slocum noted the course and distance to Port Stanley, in the Falkland Islands, during his passage through the waters south of the Strait of Magellan (008-006).

## Narrative Significance

Port Stanley served as a navigational reference point after Slocum's successful transit of the Strait of Magellan (008-006). For the solo mariner emerging from the treacherous channels of [[patagonia|Patagonia]] into the South Atlantic, fixing the course and distance to a known harbor provided both a practical bearing and a psychological marker — a sign that he had reached the open ocean and the next phase of his voyage.
> On the fourth day of the gale, rapidly nearing the pitch of Cape Horn, I inspected my chart and pricked off the course and distance to Port Stanley, in the Falkland Islands, where I might find my way and refit, when I saw through a rift in the clouds a high mountain, about seven leagues away on the port beam. The fierce edge of the gale by this time had blown off, and I had already bent a square sail on the boom in place of the mainsail, which was torn to rags.
>
> (008-006)
