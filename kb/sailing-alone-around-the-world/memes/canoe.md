## Definition and Construction

A narrow, lightweight boat propelled by one or more paddlers using single-bladed paddles. Canoes range from simple dugouts hollowed from a single log to sophisticated craft of bark, skin, or planks. In the late 19th century, canoes remained the primary watercraft of indigenous peoples across the Americas and the Pacific Islands — a technology largely unchanged for millennia, yet perfectly adapted to coastal and riverine environments.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The canoe appears in two sharply contrasting roles in Slocum's narrative: as the vessel of hostile Fuegians in the Strait of Magellan, and as the vessel of his own return from Brazil — the *Liberdade*.

Slocum's first experience with canoes came not as an observer but as a builder and sailor: "my return home from Brazil in the canoe Liberdade" (001-001). After the loss of the *Aquidneck*, Slocum and his family built the *Liberdade*, a sailing canoe in which they voyaged from Brazil to the United States — "with my family was made in the canoe Liberdade, without accident" (001-004). This extraordinary journey, covering thousands of miles in a home-built canoe, demonstrated Slocum's resourcefulness and remains one of the most remarkable small-boat voyages in maritime history.

The second and more dangerous encounter with canoes occurred in the Strait of Magellan, where Fuegian canoes surrounded the *Spray*: "they tried to surround me with their canoes, to shoot straight, and began sending arrows" (007-014). Slocum observed "four at the paddles in the canoe nearest to me, there were others behind" (007-019), making a rapid calculation of the threat. The Fuegian canoes, though primitive in construction, were highly maneuverable — "faster than a rowboat or a canoe" (007-029) — making them formidable harassment platforms in the confined waters of the strait.
> Besides cleverly climbing the trees, the men of Keeling build exquisitely modeled canoes. By far the best workmanship in boat-building I saw on the voyage was here. Many finished mechanics dwelt under the palms at Keeling, and the hum of the bandsaw and the ring of the anvil were heard from morning till night.
>
> (016-022)


The contrast between Slocum's own use of a canoe for peaceful passage and the Fuegians' use of canoes for ambush illustrates the dual nature of indigenous watercraft in the age of exploration: vessels of profound cultural knowledge that could serve as either instruments of connection or weapons of resistance.
