The **boom** is the horizontal spar to which the foot (bottom edge) of a fore-and-aft sail is attached. On a gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the main-boom pivots at the mast and extends aft, controlled by the mainsheet. It is both a sail-shaping tool—tensioning the foot of the sail—and a potential hazard: an uncontrolled boom swinging across the vessel (a jibe) can cause injury, dismasting, or capsize.

## Definition

In [[rigging|rig]]ging terminology, the boom is the spar at the bottom of any fore-and-aft sail. The main-boom carries the mainsail's foot; a jib-boom extends forward from the bowsprit to carry a [[jib|flying-jib]] or outer jib. The boom attaches to the mast via a gooseneck—a hinged fitting that allows the boom to swing side to side and pivot up and down. The mainsheet attaches to the boom's aft end and controls its angle relative to the wind.

The boom is under enormous strain. In a fresh breeze, the entire force of the mainsail—[[tonnage|tons]] of wind pressure—is transmitted through the boom to the mast and the sheet. A broken boom disables the mainsail entirely, and a jury-[[rigging|rig]]ged repair at sea is one of the most demanding feats of seamanship.

## Nautical Context

For the single-handed sailor, the boom is both friend and enemy. A well-controlled boom, trimmed to the wind, makes the vessel fast and balanced. An accidental jibe—when the wind catches the sail from behind and throws the boom across with tremendous force—can sweep gear overboard, tear out [[rigging|rig]]ging, or knock the sailor unconscious. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s constant attention to trim and his careful [[helm|steering]] reflect a professional's respect for the boom's destructive potential.

"Fishing the boom" is the seaman's term for repairing a broken spar by lashing splints (often spare wood or oars) alongside the break—a temporary measure to restore some function until a permanent repair can be made in port. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] performed this operation at least twice during the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The boom appears in the narrative's opening moments. On departure from [[boston|Boston]]:

> "She stood seaward, with her boom well off to port, and swung past the ferries with lively heels." (002-002)

The boom's position—"well off to port"—indicates a broad reach, the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s fastest point of sail. This image of the little sloop striding out to sea, boom squared away, [[current|set]]s the [[tonnage|ton]]e for the entire voyage.

The boom proved vulnerable. Off the coast of [[brazil|Brazil]], disaster struck:

> "The main-sheet parted, and away went the main-boom, broken short at the rigging." (005-003)

A parted sheet—the line controlling the boom—allowed it to swing uncontrolled until it struck the [[rigging|rig]]ging and snapped. This was one of the voyage's more serious equipment failures, requiring immediate repair:

> "I fitted a new sheet, and in the meantime I fished the boom." (005-004)

"Fishing the boom"—lashing splints to the break—kept the vessel moving. Later, at [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a permanent repair: "I shortened the boom, which had been broken when off the coast of [[brazil|Brazil]]" (005-029). The shortened boom changed the sail's shape slightly but proved serviceable for the remainder of the voyage.

The boom's vulnerability to sudden movement appears again in the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]:

> "The sheet parted and the boom unshipped, and it was then clear that the safest plan was to lock the wheel" (010-002)

An unshipped boom—one that has jumped off its gooseneck—disables the mainsail entirely. Only [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s quick reactions prevented more serious damage.

The boom also served non-[[sail|sailing]] purposes. At [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found an inventive use for his steelyards: "I hooked my steelyards to the boom at once, ready to weigh out the correct amount" (011-001)—the boom as a convenient overhead support for weighing supplies, a reminder that every part of a small vessel serves multiple functions.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] improvised a jib-boom for the homeward passage: "a serviceable jib-boom on the homeward voyage" (013-007), made from a bamboo pole acquired in the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]]. The bamboo jib-boom was a characteristically resourceful solution—light, strong, and free.
