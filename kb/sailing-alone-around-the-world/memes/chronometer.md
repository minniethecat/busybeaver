# Chronometer

The marine chronometer is a highly accurate timepiece that maintains Greenwich (or another reference [[longitude|meridian]]) time despite the motion, temperature changes, and humidity of the sea environment. Combined with a sextant sight of the sun or stars, the chronometer enables a navigator to calculate longitude — the east–west position that cannot be determined by celestial observation alone.

## Definition

[[longitude|Longitude]] is time: the Earth rotates 15 degrees every hour, so the difference between local noon (observed by sextant) and the chronometer's reference time yields the ship's longitude. A chronometer must keep time to within a few seconds per day — an error of four seconds translates to roughly one [[knot|nautical mile]] of position error at the equator. The instrument is typically housed in a gimbaled wooden box to remain level despite the ship's rolling, and is treated with extreme care — it is never wound carelessly, never moved unnecessarily, and never allowed to run down.

## Nautical Context

Before the marine chronometer, longitude was estimated by [[navigation|dead reckoning]] — a method that accumulated error with every passing day and led to countless shipwrecks. John Harrison's 18th-century chronometers revolutionized navigation, and by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era (the late 19th century), a reliable chronometer was standard equipment on any well-found ocean-going vessel. Lunarian methods (calculating longitude from lunar distances) existed as a backup, but required complex tables and clear skies. For the practical navigator, the chronometer was indispensable.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s relationship with the chronometer was complicated. He began his voyage without one — a circumstance he acknowledged candidly: "The want of a chronometer for the voyage was all that needed to be said about my method of navigation" (002-009). On his small budget, a proper chronometer was an unaffordable luxury.

The absence of a chronometer did not prevent [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] from obtaining longitude fixes when the opportunity arose. At [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, he met a naval officer who "without being solicited gave the [[longitude|longitude by chronometer]] as 48° W., 'as near as I can make it'" (006-002) — a kindness from one navigator to another, filling the gap in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s equipment with shared knowledge.

Later in the voyage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reflected philosophically on his chronometer-free navigation: "A good longitude is found independent of chronometers. The work of the lunarian, though complex, is sure" (011-027). Whether this was genuine confidence or a solo sailor making peace with necessity, the chronometer remained the one instrument [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed without — and yet he completed the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

> The want of a chronometer for the voyage was all that now worried me. In our newfangled notions of navigation it is supposed that a mariner cannot find his way without one; and I had myself drifted into this way of thinking. My old chronometer, a good one, had been long in disuse. It would cost fifteen dollars to clean and rate it. Fifteen dollars! For sufficient reasons I left that timepiece at home, where the Dutchman left his anchor.
>
> (002-009)
