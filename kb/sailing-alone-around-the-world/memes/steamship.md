A powered vessel propelled by one or more steam engines, typically driving paddle wheels or screw propellers. By the 1890s, steamships had become the dominant mode of long-distance maritime transport, increasingly displacing sail-powered vessels on the world's major shipping routes.

## Definition

A steamship is a watercraft whose primary means of propulsion is steam power, generated by coal-fired boilers driving reciprocating engines. Unlike sailing vessels, steamships operate independently of wind conditions and can maintain schedule and course regardless of weather — a decisive advantage that reshaped global maritime commerce in the 19th century. By the time of Slocum's voyage (1895–1898), steamships fell into several categories: ocean liners carrying passengers and mail on fixed schedules, tramp steamers picking up cargo wherever available, mail-steamers serving colonial routes, and specialized vessels like bullock-drogers carrying livestock.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Steamships appear at critical junctures throughout Slocum's circumnavigation, often marking transitions between phases of the voyage or providing moments of human contact in otherwise isolated waters.

At the very outset of the voyage in April 1895, the contrast between Slocum's small sloop and industrial shipping is drawn at the Boston docks, where "a great steamship, fully manned, officered" lay near the *Spray* (002-002) — a juxtaposition foreshadowing the recurring theme of the lone sailor amid industrial-scale maritime traffic.

In the North Atlantic, Slocum spoke the transatlantic steamship *Olympia*, whose captain provided the first independent verification of his position after leaving the American coast (003-001). This encounter and the subsequent meeting with the *Java* steamship are detailed in [[steamship-olympia]] and [[java-steamship]].

Off the River Plate, "about noon a steamship, a bullock-droger, from the river Plate hove in sight, steering northeast, and making bad weather of it" (005-010). Slocum signaled her but received no answer — the steamer was laboring too heavily in heavy seas to notice the small sloop.

After departing [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]] on November 28, 1895, the steamship *South Wales* spoke the *Spray* and provided Slocum with news and position verification (006-002), an encounter recounted in [[ss-south-wales]].

The most dramatic steamship episode occurred in the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]]. Near the entrance, Slocum observed "the wreck of a great steamship smashed on the beach abreast" (007-007) — a grim reminder that these waters imperiled even large powered vessels. Shortly after, the American steamship *Colombia* appeared: "On the following day the loneliness of the place was broken by the appearance of a great steamship, making for the anchorage with a lofty bearing. She was no Diego craft. I knew the sheer, the model, and the poise" (009-010). Captain Henderson generously resupplied the *Spray* in an act of American hospitality detailed in [[visit-to-ss-colombia]].

In the Coral Sea, a French mail-steamer from New Caledonia was "blown considerably out of her course" by a severe gale that Slocum himself had weathered without realizing its full intensity (013-009). Later, near Australia, Slocum glimpsed "the stern of a large, unmanageable steamship passing the range of my window" from a hotel where he was nursing neuralgia (014-016).

Throughout the voyage, steamships served as floating proofs of Slocum's position — their captains confirming his longitude when celestial navigation was uncertain — and as reminders that the age of [[sail]] was giving way to steam.

> That the best of sailors might do worse than even I alone was borne in upon me not a league from Boston docks, where a great steamship, fully manned, officered, and piloted, lay stranded and broken. This was the *Venetian*. She was broken completely in two over a ledge. So in the first hour of my lone voyage I had proof that the *Spray* could at least do better than this full-handed steamship, for I was already farther on my voyage than she. "Take warning, *Spray*, and have a care," I uttered aloud to my bark, passing fairylike silently down the bay.
>
> (002-002)
