**Grenada** is a volcanic island in the southeastern [[west-indies|Caribbean Sea]], the southernmost of the Windward Islands. Known as the "Isle of Spice" for its nutmeg and mace production, Grenada was a British colony at the time of [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s visit in 1898. The island sits approximately 160 kilometers north of [[trinidad|Trinidad]] and south of St. Vincent.

## Arrival at St. George's

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] approached Grenada from the south, carrying letters of introduction from contacts in [[mauritius|Mauritius]]. He arrived at midnight on May 22, 1898, [[anchor|anchoring]] in the roads off St. George's before entering the inner harbor at daylight:

> About midnight of the 22nd of May I arrived at the island, and cast anchor in the roads off the town of St. George, entering the inner harbor at daylight on the morning of the 23rd, which made forty-two days' sailing from the Cape of Good Hope. It was a good run, and I doffed my cap again to the pilot of the *Pinta*. (020-010)

The passage from [[cape-town|Cape Town]] — forty-two days across the South Atlantic — was swift by the standards of the day, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] attributed the good fortune to the same celestial guidance he had relied upon throughout the voyage.

## A Charming Introduction

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reception at Grenada was unusually warm. His chapter [[course|heading]] described "a charming introduction to Grenada" and "talks to friendly auditors" (020-001). He had been expected:

> Lady Bruce, in a note to the *Spray* at Port Louis, said Grenada was a lovely island, and she wished the sloop might call there on the voyage home. When the *Spray* arrived, I found that she had been fully expected. "How so?" I asked. "Oh, we heard that you were at Mauritius," they said, "and from Mauritius, after meeting Sir Charles Bruce, our old governor, we knew you would come to Grenada." This was a charming introduction, and it brought me in contact with people worth knowing. (020-011)

The islanders had followed news of his progress across the oceans. The courthouse at St. George's was tendered to him free of charge, and "a highly intelligent audience filled the hall to listen to a talk about the seas the *[[spray|Spray]]* had crossed, and the countries she had visited" (020-013).

## Departure and the Caribbean Passage

The *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed from Grenada on May 28, coasting under the lee of the Antilles northward (020-012). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was still without charts of the Caribbean islands — he had been unable to obtain one even at Grenada — and navigated with great caution in mid-channel.

His next stop was Dominica, where a comic encounter with a deputy harbormaster ensued over anchorage regulations. The official demanded [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] move his vessel; [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] replied he wanted to be quarantined anyway. The stalemate ended when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] requested charts and was told with stiff dignity: "Cha'ts do'sn't grow on dis island" (020-012). He immediately sailed for [[antigua|Antigua]].

## Maritime Context

Grenada's position at the southern gateway to the Windward Islands made it a natural stopping point for vessels [[sail|sailing]] north from [[south-america|South America]] toward the [[united-states|United States]]. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], it was one of the final Caribbean ports before the homeward stretch. The forty-two-day passage from [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope to Grenada demonstrated the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s remarkable speed; only the final Atlantic leg to [[newport|Newport]] remained.
