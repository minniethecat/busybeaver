# Forestaysail

The **forestaysail** (or simply *staysail*) is a triangular fore-and-aft sail [[current|set]] on the forestay — the wire or rope stay that runs forward from the mast to the bowsprit or stemhead. Unlike the jib, which is [[current|set]] flying from the [[mast|masthead]] to the bowsprit end, the forestaysail is hanked to the stay itself, making it more secure in [[gale|heavy weather]]. It is a compact, powerful sail essential for balancing a vessel and maintaining steerageway in gale conditions.

## Definition

The forestaysail is cut as an isosceles triangle with its luff attached to the forestay by hanks (metal clips). It is smaller in area than a full jib, and its position closer to the mast — rather than at the end of the bowsprit — moves the center of effort aft, which helps balance the [[rudder|helm]]. On a cutter or yawl, the forestaysail is a standard part of the sail plan; on a sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, it was [[current|set]] in addition to or in place of the jib when conditions demanded a more manageable headsail.

## Nautical Context

In [[gale|heavy weather]], reducing sail area is essential for survival. The forestaysail is often the last headsail standing — small enough to handle [[gale|gale-force wind]]s, yet powerful enough to keep the vessel moving and prevent her from being overwhelmed by following seas. Its low center of effort and secure attachment to the stay make it less likely to blow out than a larger jib. When a vessel is running before a gale under bare poles or a deeply [[reefing|reefed]] mainsail, [[current|set]]ting a [[reefing|reefed]] forestaysail can provide just enough drive to maintain steerageway and prevent broaching.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The forestaysail appears at [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s most perilous moments, always as the sail of last resort. After a pirate felucca was [[mast|dismasted]] by the same squall that broke his boom off the [[morocco|Moroccan coast]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "sailed comfortably on under the jib and forestaysail, which I now [[current|set]]" (005-004) — the combination of head[[sail|sails]] providing steady drive while he fished the broken boom.

In [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], facing a [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] gale, "[[sloop|the sloop]] could carry no more than a [[mainsail|three-reefed mainsail]] and forestaysail; with these she held on stoutly and was not blown out of the strait" (007-008). The forestaysail's reliability in extreme conditions is confirmed again off [[cape-pillar|Cape Pillar]], where the *[[spray|Spray]]* drove before a [[gale|storm]] of terrifying force: "She was running now with a [[reefing|reefed]] forestaysail, the sheets flat amidship" (008-002).

When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sighted land through a rift in the [[gale|storm]] clouds after four days driving toward [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]], he "hoisted this awkward sail [[reefing|reefed]], the forestaysail being already [[current|set]], and under this sail brought her at once on the wind [[course|heading]] for the land" (008-006). The forestaysail had been carrying the vessel through the worst of the gale.

At the [[gale|storm]]'s fiercest peak, "when [[sloop|the sloop]] was in the fiercest squalls, with only the [[reefing|reefed]] forestaysail [[current|set]], even that small sail shook her from [[keel|keelson]] to truck when it shivered by the leech" (008-007). The image of the *[[spray|Spray]]* under a single small forestaysail in the worst seas off [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] is the ultimate testament to this sail's role in heavy-weather seamanship.
