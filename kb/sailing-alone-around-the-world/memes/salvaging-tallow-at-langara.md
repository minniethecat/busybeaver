# Salvaging Tallow at Langara

In March 1896, while making his way through the western reaches of the [[Strait-of-Magellan|Strait of Magellan]], [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] discovered large quantities of wreckage washed ashore near Langara Cove — the remains of a ship that had been destroyed in a great gale. Among the wreckage were casks and lumps of tallow, a barrel of wine, and other goods. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] spent days salvaging the cargo, loading the *[[spray|Spray]]* until her [[deck|decks]] were greased and her hold was full. The tallow, which he later traded for supplies and sold to a German soap-boiler at [[Samoa]], provided the financial resources that carried him through the remainder of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Context

Shipwrecks were common in [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] in the age of sail. The combination of fierce williwaws, strong currents, narrow channels, and poor visibility made [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] one of the most dangerous waterways in the world. When a ship went down, her cargo often washed ashore on the rocky beaches of the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]] — a windfall for anyone who happened to pass that way.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a former merchant captain, had an eye for commercial opportunity. His previous commands — the *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]*, the *[[aquidneck|Aquidneck]]*, the *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]* — had all involved cargo. The *[[spray|Spray]]* herself, though a pleasure craft by design, was stout enough to carry a substantial load. The tallow salvage was thus not an act of desperation but a reversion to familiar habit: "the habits of an old trader would come to the surface" (009-023).

The timing of the discovery was fortunate. The *[[spray|Spray]]* was low on funds after the expenses of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] passage, and the tallow — a commodity used in soap-making, candle manufacture, and cooking — was a readily marketable product throughout [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] islands.

## Narrative

### Discovery at Langara

After departing [[borgia-bay|Borgia Bay]] on a fine morning, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] worked his way along the coast to Langara Cove. There he found the beach strewn with wreckage:

> "That night I anchored in Langara Cove, a few miles farther along, where on the following day I discovered wreckage and goods washed up from the sea. I worked all day now, salving and boating off a cargo to the sloop. The bulk of the goods was tallow in casks and in lumps from which the casks had broken away; and embedded in the seaweed was a barrel of wine, which I also towed alongside." — (009-021)

The discovery raised the question of the wreck's origin. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] speculated that the same gale that had driven the *[[spray|Spray]]* off [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] — the great [[gale|storm]] of March 3 to 8 — had destroyed the unknown vessel:

> "There were no Indians about Langara; evidently there had not been any since the great gale which had washed the wreckage on shore. Probably it was the same gale that drove the Spray off Cape Horn, from March 3 to 8. Hundreds of tons of kelp had been torn from beds in deep water and rolled up into ridges on the beach. A specimen stalk which I found entire, roots, leaves, and all, measured one hundred and thirty-one feet in length." — (009-022)

The detail of the 131-foot kelp stalk is characteristic of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative style: a moment of natural wonder amid the practical business of salvage.

### The Work of Salvage

The labor was hard and the weather hostile:

> "I had not sailed far, however, when I came abreast of more tallow in a small cove, where I anchored, and boated off as before. It rained and snowed hard all that day, and it was no light work carrying tallow in my arms over the boulders on the beach. But I worked on till the Spray was loaded with a full cargo." — (009-023)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], then in his early fifties, carried the heavy tallow casks alone over slippery rocks in rain and snow — a feat of physical endurance that the matter-of-fact prose barely emphasizes. The result was a sloop laden to the gunwales with a cargo that transformed her appearance:

> "I was happy then in the prospect of doing a good business farther along on the voyage, for the habits of an old trader would come to the surface. I sailed from the cove about noon, greased from top to toe, while my vessel was tallowed from keelson to truck. My cabin, as well as the hold and deck, was full of tallow." — (009-023)

### Stowage and Use

At [[port-angosto|Port Angosto]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reorganized [[sloop|the sloop]] to accommodate the cargo:

> "I remained at Port Angosto some days, busily employed about the sloop. I stowed the tallow from the deck to the hold, arranged my cabin in better order, and took in a good supply of wood and water. I also mended the sloop's sails and rigging, and fitted a jigger, which changed the rig to a yawl." — (010-004)

The tallow also found culinary use. At [[Juan-Fernandez-Island|Juan Fernandez]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s buns — fried in tallow — were served alongside the islanders' doughnuts, prompting the observation that "between buns and doughnuts there was little difference except in name. Both had been fried in tallow, which was the strong point in both" (011-001).

### The Final Sale

The tallow accompanied the *[[spray|Spray]]* across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. At [[samoa|Samoa]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] finally converted the cargo into cash:

> "Returning from the bank, where I had been to deposit money early in the day, I found Howard in the midst of a very excited crowd, telling imaginary habits of the fish. It was a good show; the people wished to see it, and it was my wish that they should; but owing to his over-stimulated enthusiasm, I was obliged to let Howard resign. The income from the show and the proceeds of the tallow I had gathered in the Strait of Magellan, the last of which I had disposed of to a German soap-boiler at Samoa, put me in ample funds." — (013-023)

The tallow that had been salvaged from a wreck on a desolate [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n beach had crossed [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] and been sold to a German soap-maker in the South Seas — a transaction that perfectly encapsulates the global maritime economy of the nineteenth century and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s resourcefulness as both sailor and trader.

## Aftermath

The financial contribution of the tallow to the voyage was substantial. Combined with the proceeds from [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s lecture-show at [[samoa|Samoa]], it "put me in ample funds" for the remainder of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. The salvage also demonstrated that the *[[spray|Spray]]* — despite being a small recreational sloop — was capable of carrying a commercial cargo when the opportunity arose.

The tallow salvage is also notable for what it reveals about [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s character. Most sailors in his position, having survived [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Horn gale and the dangers of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]], would have pressed on without delay. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the former merchant captain, saw the commercial opportunity and seized it — working alone in rain and snow until his vessel was fully laden.

## Significance

The salvaging of tallow at Langara is one of the most distinctive episodes in *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. It [[current|set]]s [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] apart from other single-handed voyagers: he was not merely an adventurer but a practical mariner who understood that a voyage of this length required resources. The tallow — salvaged from a wreck, carried across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], and sold at a profit — represents the intersection of seamanship, commerce, and self-reliance that defined the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]].
