# Encounter with Black Pedro

During his passage through the [[Strait-of-Magellan|Strait of Magellan]] in early 1896, [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] twice encountered a notorious [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegian]] outlaw known as "[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]" — a bearded savage who had murdered a kinsman of Captain [[samblich|Pedro Samblich]] of [[Sandy-Point]].

The encounters unfolded over several days near [[charles-island|Charles Island]] and [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]], testing [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s resourcefulness and his ability to manage hostile contact without resorting to violence.

[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]'s appearances bookended a critical chapter of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] passage, during which the *[[spray|Spray]]* was also visited by the steamship *Colombia* and pursued by a fleet of canoes.

## Summary

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] first learned of [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] from Captain [[samblich|Samblich]] at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]], who warned him that the outlaw was in the vicinity of [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] and had "sworn to murder the first white man he met" (007-019). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later said his first shot at a canoe near [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] — which missed — may have been aimed at [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] himself (007-001). The second and more dramatic encounter occurred at [[charles-island|Charles Island]] during [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s return passage through [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]]. [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] came alongside in a canoe with two squaws, begging with the [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegian]] cry of "yammerschooner." Through a combination of quick thinking and psychological maneuvering, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] established a connection by mentioning [[samblich|Samblich]] and [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] — places the outlaw knew — and managed to keep him at a safe distance. When [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] later returned after visiting the steamship *Colombia* and asked to borrow [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s rifle "to shoot a guanaco," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] deflected the request and departed the following morning (009-011).

## Context

The [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegian]] peoples of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] and [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] had a fearsome reputation among nineteenth-century mariners. Charles Darwin, who visited the region on HMS *[[hms-beagle|Beagle]]*, described them as "the most abject and miserable creatures I anywhere beheld." By the 1890s, contact with sealers, whalers, and the missionary [[current|set]]tlement at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] had produced a complex dynamic: some natives traded peacefully, others begged or stole, and a few — like [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] — were known criminals who had committed violent crimes against [[current|set]]tlers and shipwrecked sailors.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had already been briefed on the dangers of the region. Captain [[samblich|Samblich]], the Austrian trader at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]], had given him a bag of carpet-tacks to scatter [[deck|on deck]] at night as a deterrent against barefoot boarders — a gift that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later called "worth more than all the fighting men and dogs of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]" (007-012).

## Narrative

### First Encounter: The Missed Shot

As the *[[spray|Spray]]* approached [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] on her first passage through [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed canoes manned by savages. The chapter [[course|heading]] for that day records "a miss-shot for '[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]'" (007-001), suggesting the outlaw was among the pursuing party. The savages came within hail, crying their perpetual begging word:

> "Canoes manned by savages from Fortescue now came in pursuit. The wind falling light, they gained on me rapidly till coming within hail, when they ceased paddling, and a bowlegged savage stood up and called to me, 'Yammerschooner! yammerschooner!' which is their begging term." — (007-019)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used a trick that would serve him well throughout [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] passage: he went below, changed clothes, and came out at a different hatch to create the impression of a larger crew. He also arranged a piece of bowsprit forward to resemble a gun:

> "I was not for letting on that I was alone, and so I stepped into the cabin, and, passing through the hold, came out at the fore-scuttle, changing my clothes as I went along. That made two men." — (007-019)

The ruse worked. The savages, uncertain about the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s crew, kept their distance. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anchored that night at [[three-island-cove|Three Island Cove]], noting that the absence of seals on the rocks was a reliable sign of the presence of natives — and conversely, where birds and seals were found, the coast was safe (007-020).

### Second Encounter: Charles Island

On the return passage through [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* encountered another gale and took shelter in the lee of [[charles-island|Charles Island]]. Signal fires on the bluffs indicated the presence of natives, and soon a canoe put off for [[sloop|the sloop]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] allowed only one canoe to approach, laying his Martini-Henry rifle conspicuously on the cabin top (009-007).

In the canoe were two squaws and one Indian — "the hardest specimens of humanity I had ever seen in any of my travels" (009-007). The man had a full beard, which marked him as exceptional among [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegians]], who are generally beardless. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recognized him:

> "A full beard identified him as the Black Pedro whom, it was true, I had met before, and I had the good fortune to shoot him on the spot — but not with a leaden bullet." — (009-007)

The "bullet" [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used was psychological. He engaged [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] in conversation, asking about [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] and the mission, establishing a connection that disarmed the outlaw's immediate hostility. When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] mentioned Captain [[samblich|Samblich]], [[black-pedro|Pedro]]'s reaction was charged: "When you sailed through here before," he said, "you fired a shot at me," adding with some warmth that it was "muy malo" (009-008). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] affected not to understand and kept the exchange light, offering small gifts — a cooper's drawknife and some tobacco — while firmly refusing to allow [[black-pedro|Pedro]] aboard.

> "My own brother could not have been more delighted to see me, and he begged me to lend him my rifle to shoot a guanaco for me in the morning. I assured the fellow that if I remained there another day I would lend him the gun, but I had no mind to remain." — (009-011)

The [[chile|Chile]]ans on the gunboat *[[chilean-gunboat-condor|Condor]]*, who later passed through [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]], saw [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] and learned that he had visited the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s deck twice. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wryly noted: "The officers of the *[[chilean-gunboat-condor|Condor]]* told me that when their vessel passed through they saw [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] and learned that he had visited my vessel's deck — but I knew better than that" (010-006). [[black-pedro|Pedro]] may have boasted, but [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s precautions had kept him off [[sloop|the sloop]].

## Aftermath

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounters with [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] reinforced the lessons of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] passage: constant vigilance, psychological management of hostile contacts, and the importance of local intelligence. Captain [[samblich|Samblich]]'s carpet-tacks — scattered [[deck|on deck]] each night — and the visible presence of the Martini-Henry rifle were effective deterrents.

The encounter also illustrated a recurring theme of the voyage: [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s preference for non-violent resolution. He could have shot [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] — he had the rifle ready and the outlaw was within range — but chose instead to manage the situation through conversation, misdirection, and the implied threat of force without its actual use. "I had the good fortune to shoot him on the spot," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote, "but not with a leaden bullet" (009-007).

After the second encounter, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed from [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] under hard wind, confident the savages could not follow, and sprinkled tacks [[deck|on deck]] before turning in — "not to neglect a precaution" (009-009). The following day brought the arrival of the steamship *Colombia*, and the dangers of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] receded, at least for a time.

## Significance

The Black Pedro encounter is one of the most vivid episodes of human contact in *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. Unlike the [[pirate-chase-off-gibraltar|pirate chase off Gibraltar]], which was a pursuit by unknown assailants, the [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] episodes involved a named, identifiable antagonist with a known criminal history. The encounters tested [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s diplomatic skills as much as his seamanship, and his handling of them — using wit rather than gunfire — became a point of pride in the narrative.

The episode also illuminates the complex frontier society of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] in the 1890s. [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] himself was a product of contact between Europeans and [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegians]]: he had lived at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]], knew Captain [[samblich|Samblich]], and spoke some Spanish mixed with the native jargon. He was a murderer and a beggar, a product of two worlds who belonged fully to neither — and in that sense, he was as much a figure of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] as the williwaws and the kelp beds that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] navigated alongside him.
