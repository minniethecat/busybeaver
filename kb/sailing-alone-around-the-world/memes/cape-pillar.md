# Cape Pillar

Cape Pillar was the gateway to the [[pacific-ocean|Pacific]]. After weeks of threading through the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rounded this "grim sentinel" — (008-004) — and felt the ocean swell beneath the *[[spray|Spray]]* for the first time since leaving [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]]. It was a milestone in navigation, a psychological threshold, and a place where the sea tested his seamanship one final time before opening the way to [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez]].

## Geography

Cape Pillar marks the western entrance of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], standing at the tip of Desolacion Island. To the east lies the labyrinth of channels and fjords that form the strait; to the west, the vast and open [[pacific-ocean|Pacific Ocean]]. The [[tide|tide-race]] off the cape was notorious: [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted that in the "[[tide|tide-race]] off Cape Pillar, however, where the sea was" — (008-003) turbulent, he could barely spare attention from the [[rudder|helm]] even to eat. The cape's cliffs and the deep water at their base concentrated the tidal flow into a violent churn, testing the seamanship of every vessel that sought passage.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s approach to Cape Pillar began from [[port-tamar|Port Tamar]], where he had anchored for the night. With the cape "in sight to the west" — (007-028), he made his preparations. The chapter [[course|heading]] that follows captures the moment: "From Cape Pillar into [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]" — (008-001). The *[[spray|Spray]]* "sailed from [[port-tamar|Port Tamar]] direct for Cape Pillar, with the wind from the north" — (008-002) — favorable for rounding.

The passage was not easy. In the "[[tide|tide-race]] off Cape Pillar" — (008-003), the sea tossed the *[[spray|Spray]]* violently, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] skipped meals rather than leave the [[rudder|helm]]. What made it bearable was the knowledge that this was the last obstacle: east of Cape Pillar was the confined water of the strait; west lay the boundless Pacific.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] called Cape Pillar "the grim sentinel of [[cape-horn|the Horn]]" — (008-004) — grouping it with the legendary [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] as one of the great southern headlands. But unlike [[cape-horn|the Horn]], which he had intentionally avoided, Cape Pillar was a cape he had to round. When he did, the sea changed character: "plunged into [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] Ocean at once" — (008-002), he wrote, with a sense of relief that would carry him across the world's greatest ocean.

> The first day of the storm gave the *Spray* her actual test in the worst sea that Cape Horn or its wild regions could afford, and in no part of the world could a rougher sea be found than at this particular point, namely, off Cape Pillar, the grim sentinel of the Horn.
>
> (008-004)

## Historical Context

Cape Pillar has been a significant navigation landmark since [[ferdinand-magellan|Ferdinand Magellan]]'s expedition first transited the strait in 1520. For centuries of sail, it marked the transition between two oceans — a point where captains could finally [[current|set]] course west-northwest for the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]] islands or south toward [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]. By the 1890s, the strait was used less by commercial sail and more by small vessels, survey ships, and the occasional lone navigator like [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]. The [[chile|Chile]]an government maintained aids to navigation along the strait, but Cape Pillar itself remained an unlit headland, its passage made solely by seamanship.
