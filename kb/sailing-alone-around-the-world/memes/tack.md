# Tack

In [[sail|sailing]], a **tack** is both the direction a vessel [[sail|sails]] relative to the wind ([[port|port tack]] when the wind comes over the [[port|port side]], [[starboard|starboard tack]] when over the starboard side) and the maneuver of turning the bow through the wind to change direction — also called *coming about*.

## Definition

When a [[sail|sailing]] vessel is on a **[[port|port tack]]**, the wind blows across the port (left) side and the [[sail|sails]] are [[current|set]] to the starboard ([[rigging|rig]]ht) side. On a **[[starboard|starboard tack]]**, the reverse is true. The verb *to tack* or the noun *tacking* refers to the zigzag progression a vessel makes when [[sail|sailing]] upwind, turning the bow through the wind at each change of direction. This is distinct from *jibing*, where the stern passes through the wind when [[sail|sailing]] downwind.

## Nautical Context

Tacking is fundamental to windward [[sail|sailing]]. A square-[[rigging|rig]]ged ship or fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]ged vessel cannot sail directly into the wind; it must progress at an angle, tacking back and forth across the wind. The maneuver requires coordinated handling of [[sail|sails]] and [[rudder|helm]]: the [[rudder|helm]] is put over (typically to leeward), the bow swings through the eye of the wind, the [[sail|sails]] are trimmed to the new tack, and the vessel [[current|set]]tles on her new course. On a sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, this is a single-handed operation demanding timing and attention to sail trim.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s departure from [[boston|Boston]] opens with a tacking maneuver: "A short board was made up the harbor on the [[port|port tack]], then coming about she stood seaward, with her boom well off to port" (002-002). This moment marks the beginning of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — the *[[spray|Spray]]* working her way out of a confined harbor under sail alone.

During the long [[australia|Australia]]n coastal passage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] describes the routine in light headwinds: he would leave "this pleasant occupation merely to trim [[sail|sails]] or tack, or to lie down and rest, while [[spray|the Spray]] nibbled at the miles" (014-019). Here, tacking is presented as a matter-of-fact, almost casual action — a reflection of his seamanship and the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s easy handling.

Near the end of the voyage, off Tobago, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was forced into rapid tacking to avoid a perceived [[reefing|reef]]: "I luffed instantly offshore, and then tacked, [[course|heading]] in for the island. Finding myself, shortly after, close in with the land, I tacked again offshore" (020-009). The danger proved to be the revolving light of Trinidad Island, not a [[reefing|reef]] at all, but the episode shows how tacking was both a routine maneuver and an emergency response. On the final approach to New York, he "hove [[sloop|the sloop]] round from starboard to [[port|port tack]], to make for a quiet harbor to think the matter over" (021-008), using a tack as a deliberate, reflective pause before the voyage's end.
