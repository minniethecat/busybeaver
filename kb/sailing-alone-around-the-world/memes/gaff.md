A spar that supports the head (upper edge) of a fore-and-aft four-cornered sail, suspended from the mast by a pair of halyards and controlled by a downhaul. The gaff rig was the predominant mainsail configuration for working sailing vessels in the 19th century, offering more sail area per unit of mast height than a triangular Bermudan rig.

## Nautical Function

The gaff is raised and lowered by two lines: the peak halyard, which lifts the outer end, and the throat halyard, which lifts the inner end nearest the mast. A downhaul pulls the gaff downward when the sail is being doused. The gaff must be free to pivot at the mast — a failure of the halyard block or downhaul can cause the gaff to swing dangerously across the deck. For a single-handed sailor, managing the gaff in heavy weather was one of the more demanding physical tasks aboard, requiring strength, timing, and reliable gear.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The gaff appears in Slocum's narrative at moments of high tension — gear failures that tested his seamanship. Off the coast of Australia, "the halyard-block broke from the gaff. It was time to reef and refit" (021-006). A broken halyard block meant the mainsail could not be properly controlled, forcing an immediate response.

Slocum's modifications to the *Spray's* rig reflected hard-won experience with the gaff. "A stout downhaul on the gaff was a necessity, because with the jigger the wind could more easily catch the mainsail aback" (022-008). The yawl conversion had changed the airflow over the mainsail, making positive control of the gaff even more critical. He also noted the importance of knowing "the length of her mast, boom, or gaff" (022-015) — precise measurements needed to calculate the vessel's center of effort and balance.

Earlier in the voyage, before the yawl conversion, Slocum had outfitted the *Spray* with basic gear including "a gaff, a pugh, and a dip-net, all of which I had made myself" (002-007) — a reminder that aboard a small vessel, the skipper was also the rigger, carpenter, and net-maker.
