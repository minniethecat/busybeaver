The **Drake Passage** is the body of water between [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] at the southern tip of South America and the South Shetland Islands of Antarctica. It connects the southwestern [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic Ocean]] with the southeastern [[pacific-ocean|Pacific Ocean]], and is the shortest crossing from Antarctica to any other landmass. Named after the English explorer Sir [[francis-drake|Francis Drake]], it is one of the most treacherous sea passages in the world.

## Geography and Conditions

The passage spans approximately 500 miles at its narrowest point. The prevailing westerly winds, compressed between the Andes and the Antarctic Peninsula, accelerate through the Drake Passage with exceptional ferocity. Mariners speak of the "Furious Fifties" — the latitude band between 50° and 60° south where [[gale|gales]] are the norm rather than the exception. Williwaws — sudden, violent katabatic winds descending from coastal mountains — pose an additional hazard.

## Slocum and the Drake Passage

[[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] did not sail directly through the Drake Passage. His chosen route took him through the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], the narrow and winding channel north of the passage, which offered some shelter from the open [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]]. However, the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s struggle to clear the strait's western reaches — it took seven attempts — demonstrated why the Drake Passage route, though more direct, was avoided by small vessels. The same weather systems that made the strait so difficult to exit were born in the unimpeded westerlies sweeping through the Drake Passage.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] held the earlier circumnavigators in high regard, mentioning Sir [[francis-drake|Francis Drake]] as "one of the old circumnavigators" when describing the grand scenery of the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]] (018-014). [[francis-drake|Drake]]'s passage through these southern waters in 1578 — blown south of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] by a [[gale|storm]] after transiting the strait — had first revealed the open ocean passage that would later bear his name.

> One of the old circumnavigators (Sir Francis Drake, I think), when he first saw this magnificent pile, sang, "'Tis the fairest thing and the grandest cape I've seen in the whole circumference of the earth."
>
> (018-014)
