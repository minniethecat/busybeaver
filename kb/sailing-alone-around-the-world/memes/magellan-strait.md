## Geography

The Strait of Magellan is a 570-kilometer navigable sea route separating mainland South America from Tierra del Fuego. The strait connects the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans and is characterized by narrow channels, unpredictable winds, and strong currents. For Slocum, approaching from the Atlantic side, the entrance was marked by Cape Virgins, which he sighted before entering (007-007).

## Role in the Voyage

The transit of the Strait of Magellan was one of the most demanding phases of Slocum's voyage. After passing Cape Virgins, the Spray entered the strait and Slocum described the scene as "again real and" vivid after the open ocean approach (007-007). He navigated the Spray through the strait's full length under sail, noting that "the greatest vigilance was necessary" in these confined and dangerous waters (007-023). The strait represented the gateway from the Atlantic to the Pacific, and Slocum later considered but rejected the possibility of "once more entering the Strait of Magellan and beating through again" into the Atlantic (008-007). The Cockburn Channel offered an alternative route into the strait opposite Cape Froward (008-010).

## Historical Context

The strait was discovered by Ferdinand Magellan in 1520 during the first circumnavigation of the globe. For centuries it served as a critical passage for sailing ships transiting between the Atlantic and Pacific before the opening of the Panama Canal, though its narrow confines and violent weather made it as feared as it was essential. By the time of Slocum's voyage in 1896, steamships increasingly favored the Strait of Magellan over the more dangerous open-water passage around Cape Horn.

> In the Strait of Magellan the greatest vigilance was necessary. In this instance I reasoned that I had all about me the greatest danger of the whole voyage—the treachery of cunning savages, for which I must be particularly on the alert.
>
> (007-023)
