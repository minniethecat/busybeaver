# Compass

The mariner's compass is a navigational instrument that uses a magnetized needle to indicate direction relative to magnetic north, enabling a vessel to hold a steady course at sea even when landmarks and celestial bodies are obscured.

## Definition

A compass consists of a magnetized needle or "card" freely suspended on a pivot, housed in a binnacle (a protective stand) near the ship's [[rudder|helm]]. The needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field, allowing the [[helm|helmsman]] to read the ship's [[course|heading]] against a graduated circular card marked with the cardinal and intercardinal points. On a [[sail|sailing]] vessel, the compass is the most fundamental [[helm|steering]] reference — every course [[current|set]], every tack made, is measured against the compass card.

## Nautical Context

In the age of sail, the compass was the primary instrument for maintaining course over long ocean passages. While [[navigation|celestial navigation]] (sun sights, star sights) fixed a vessel's position, the compass governed every hour spent at the [[rudder|helm]] between those fixes. A reliable compass meant the difference between a safe landfall and disaster on a lee shore. Mariners learned to account for two forms of error: *variation* (the difference between magnetic and true north, which varies by location) and *deviation* (error induced by iron aboard the ship itself). A well-adjusted compass, properly compensated, was one of a captain's most prized possessions.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] relied on his compass throughout the three-year [[voyage|circumnavigation]], and his narrative reveals the instrument's varied [[persons-by-role|roles]] — from routine course-[[current|set]]ting to survival in extreme conditions.

Departing [[gloucester|Gloucester]] on his first day at sea, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[current|set]] his initial offshore course by compass: "Thence shaping her course east, by compass, to go north of Cashes Ledge and the Amen Rocks" (002-011). The compass was his first act of departure — the instrument that translated intent into direction.

During a fierce [[gale|storm]] near the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]], the compass became a barometer of danger: "Every point of the compass threatened a wild [[gale|storm]]" (005-008). The phrase captures how, in a rotating gale, the wind can shift through every direction, and the compass card tells the story of the [[gale|storm]]'s passage.

At [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], [[captain-howard|Captain Howard]] witnessed the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s remarkable self-[[helm|steering]] ability: he "sat near the binnacle and watched the compass while [[sloop|the sloop]] held her course so steadily that one would have declared that the card was nailed fast" (006-014). The compass here served as silent witness to the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s exceptional balance — the card motionless, proving what every sailor knows: a well-built boat steers herself.

Earlier, at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] ([[sandy-point|Punta Arenas]]), the [[samblich|Austrian sailor Samblich]] gave [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] a replacement compass "which was certainly better than mine" (007-013) — a simple but vital gift, reflecting the brotherhood of the sea where one sailor's surplus compass could save another's voyage.

Most remarkably, during [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s month-long Pacific passage, he navigated by celestial bodies alone, proudly declaring: "I wished for no other compass to guide me, for these were true" (011-019) — the Southern Cross, the rising and [[current|set]]ting sun forming a clock aloft "made by the Great Architect." Even here, the compass was present by its absence; [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s confidence in [[navigation|celestial navigation]] was measured against his trust in the magnetic compass.

> Thence shaping her course east, by compass, to go north of Cashes Ledge and the Amen Rocks, I sat and considered the matter all over again, and asked myself once more whether it were best to sail beyond the ledge and rocks at all. I had only said that I would sail round the world in the *Spray*, "dangers of the sea excepted," but I must have said it very much in earnest. The "charter-party" with myself seemed to bind me, and so I sailed on.
>
> (002-011)
