# Storm

A [[gale|storm]], in maritime usage, is a wind of force 10 (48–55 [[knot|knots]], a "whole gale") or force 11 (56–63 [[knot|knots]], a "violent storm") on the Beaufort scale, with correspondingly high seas, driving spray, and severely reduced visibility. Beyond the gale — beyond the squall — lies the [[gale|storm]], where waves reach 30 feet or more, the sea surface is white with foam, and the mariner's options narrow to survival. For the solo sailor, a [[gale|storm]] is the extreme edge of human and vessel capability.

## Definition

On the Beaufort scale, force 10 ("[[gale|storm]]" or "whole gale") brings seas of 20–30 feet with long, overhanging crests and dense foam streaks; force 11 ("violent storm") raises seas to 45 feet, the surface white with foam and spray, visibility near zero. Force 12 is the hurricane. In a [[gale|storm]], a [[sail|sailing]] vessel's tactics depend on sea-room: with ample room, the vessel can run before the wind under bare poles or with a [[gale|storm]] trysail; with a lee shore threatening, the vessel must claw offshore or face destruction. The [[gale|storm]] is the ultimate democrat of the sea — affecting great ships and small sloops alike.

## Nautical Context

Storm tactics for the single-hander are an exercise in minimalism. There is no watch to share, no off-watch to rest. Every action — [[reef|reefing]], lashing the [[rudder|helm]], streaming a [[sea-anchor|drogue]], pumping the bilge — must be done alone. Preparation is everything: the [[gale|storm]] is survived in the hours before it strikes, when [[sail|sails]] are shortened, gear is secured, and the vessel is made ready. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s method — lashing the [[rudder|helm]], going below, and trusting the *[[spray|Spray]]* to look after herself — required both a vessel of exceptional balance and a sailor of exceptional faith in his ship.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Storms punctuate [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative at every major transition — [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]], the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. Each [[gale|storm]] is a chapter in his education as a solo sailor.

The Atlantic [[gale|storm]] that struck near the [[azores|Azores]] was the first great test. The gale "sprang up suddenly" (004-014) and built. "Through the night [[sloop|the sloop]] was reaching, under [[mainsail|close-reefed mainsail]] and [[jib|bobbed jib]]" (004-014) — the [[gale|storm]] [[sail|canvas]], the minimum sail. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was ill, the deck was swept clean, but the *[[spray|Spray]]* held her course. When "the gale broke, and I saw many signs of land" (004-015), it was vindication.

Off the African coast, "a furious gale" (005-002) struck — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s language escalating from "rude gale" to "furious gale" to "[[gale|storm]]" as conditions worsened. In [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[gale|storm]]s combined with fierce currents to create some of the most dangerous conditions of the voyage. And in the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] experienced the full force of the high-latitude [[gale|storm]]s that have tested sailors since [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]].

Each [[gale|storm]] left its mark — damage to repair, lessons to absorb — but also deepened [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s confidence. By the voyage's end, the [[gale|storm]] was not a terror but a known quantity, met with a practiced routine.

> July 31 a gale sprang up suddenly from the north, with heavy seas, and I shortened sail. The Spray made only fifty-one miles on her course that day.
>
> (004-014)
