A **sextant** is a navigational instrument that measures the angular distance between two visible objects—typically the sun, moon, or a star and the horizon. By measuring the altitude of a celestial body above the horizon, a navigator can determine latitude and, with accurate time and calculations, longitude. For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], the sextant was his primary means of knowing where he was on the featureless ocean—the one instrument without which the voyage would have been impossible.

## Definition

The sextant operates on the principle of double reflection: a system of mirrors brings the image of a celestial body down to the horizon, allowing the navigator to read the precise angle on a graduated arc. The instrument's name comes from its arc, which spans 60 degrees—one-sixth of a circle—though most sextants measure up to 120 degrees or more through the mirror arrangement.

To "take the sun" at noon—measuring its maximum altitude—yields the vessel's latitude directly. To determine longitude requires precise knowledge of Greenwich time (from a chronometer) and the sun's position relative to the [[longitude|meridian]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], lacking a reliable chronometer for much of the voyage, relied heavily on latitude [[sail|sailing]] and occasional lunar observations—measuring the moon's position relative to stars, a technique that requires exceptional skill.

## Nautical Context

The sextant was the pinnacle of marine navigation technology in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era. Developed from the octant and refined through the 18th and 19th centuries, it remained the primary ocean navigation instrument until the advent of GPS in the late 20th century. Using a sextant on a moving deck—balancing on a pitching vessel while [[course|track]]ing a celestial body through clouds and spray—is one of the most demanding skills in seamanship.

For the solo sailor, the sextant presented a particular challenge. On a crewed vessel, someone could steady the navigator; alone, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had to brace himself, hold the instrument, adjust the micrometer, and read the scale—all while the *[[spray|Spray]]* rolled under him. His sextant observations were acts of athletic concentration as much as mathematical precision.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s relationship with his navigation instruments was famously casual. He lost his chronometer early and navigated much of the voyage with what he called "a tin clock"—a cheap alarm clock purchased for a dollar that lost or gained time unpredictably but, remarkably, kept rate well enough to be usable.

His sextant, however, he treated with reverence. The instrument was one of the few pieces of equipment he mentions by make or quality, and it appears in critical moments throughout the voyage.

[[navigation|Navigation]] was a daily ritual:

> "At the meridian altitude of the sun I called aloud, 'Eight bells,' after the custom on a ship at sea." (003-008)

The noon sight—measuring the sun's highest point—was the navigator's most important observation of the day. Even when alone and speaking to an imaginary crew, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] never missed it. The ritual provided structure and purpose to the otherwise formless days at sea.

The sextant also features in one of the book's most charming passages. When visited by the pilot of the *[[pinta|Pinta]]* during his fever off the [[azores|Azores]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was in no condition to navigate. The apparition promised to "guide your ship [[tonnage|ton]]ight"—but in the morning, the real navigator was back at his sextant, verifying position.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] took particular pride in his landfalls—arriving at islands and ports after weeks at sea with no landmarks. His ability to "raise" a distant island exactly where his sextant told him it should be was a repeated source of quiet satisfaction. That satisfaction was the navigator's reward: the proof that the mathematics, the instrument, and the sailor's skill had all converged on a dot in the ocean.
