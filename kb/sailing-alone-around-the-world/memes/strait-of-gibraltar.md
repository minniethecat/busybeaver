# Strait of Gibraltar

The [[strait-of-gibraltar|Strait of Gibraltar]] was a pivotal waypoint in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage — the gateway between the [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic]] and the [[mediterranean-sea|Mediterranean]], and the place where the plan for the homeward route was first reconsidered. The *[[spray|Spray]]* passed through the strait, anchored under the shadow of the great Rock, and was received with honors by the British garrison. But it was also here — while being chased by a Moorish pirate — that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] began to question whether the [[suez|Suez Canal]] was the [[rigging|rig]]ht way home.

## Geography

The Strait of Gibraltar is the narrow channel separating the southern tip of Spain from the northern coast of [[morocco|Morocco]]. At its narrowest point, it is just under eight [[knot|nautical mile]]s wide — close enough that the lights of both continents are visible simultaneously. For millennia, it has been the only natural passage between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean and [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] Sea, making it one of the most strategically significant waterways on Earth. The [[gibraltar|Rock of Gibraltar]], a British possession since 1713, dominates the eastern entrance on the European side.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s passage through the strait was the triumphant conclusion of his preliminary Atlantic crossing. The chapter [[course|heading]] records the moment succinctly: "At [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]" — (004-001). The *[[spray|Spray]]* "passed through the Strait of [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], where she cast anchor at 3 p.m., less than twenty-nine days from [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]]" — (004-016). It was a remarkable feat — a solo crossing in under a month, in a small sloop, arriving in better health than when he left.

The reception at [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] exceeded anything he could have expected. "[[sir-f-carrington|Sir F. Carrington]], at the time governor of [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], with other high officers" — (004-022) came to see the little sloop that had crossed the ocean alone. "That one should like [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] would go without saying" — (004-023), [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote, and he spent days exploring the fortifications, noting that "none of the great sights of the world are at all approaching these of [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] in conception or execution" — (004-024).

But the strait was also the scene of a near-disaster. On departing for [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* was chased by a Moroccan pirate vessel — an encounter that shook [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s confidence in the Suez route. He abandoned the [[mediterranean-sea|Mediterranean]] plan and instead [[current|set]] course south, toward the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]] — a decision that would define the second half of his voyage.

> She passed through the Strait of Gibraltar, where she cast anchor at 3 p.m. of the same day, less than twenty-nine days from Cape Sable.
>
> (004-016)

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] was a vital British naval base — the key to controlling access to [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]]. The [[suez|Suez Canal]] had increased its strategic value enormously, making it the gateway to India and the Far East. The garrison was substantial, and the fortifications — the product of British military engineering over two centuries — were among the most formidable in the world. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s warm reception by the governor reflected both the curiosity of an isolated garrison and the genuine admiration that naval men felt for an exceptional feat of seamanship.
