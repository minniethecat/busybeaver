# Joshua Slocum

Captain Joshua Slocum (1844–1909) was a [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]n-born American merchant sailor, master mariner, and author who became the first person to complete a solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] of the globe. His account of the voyage, *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World* (1900), is a classic of maritime literature — plainspoken, humorous, and filled with the hard-won wisdom of a lifetime at sea.

## Biography

Slocum was born on February 20, 1844, in Annapolis County, [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], into a family of farmers and mariners. He went to sea as a boy and rose through the ranks to command some of the finest American [[sail|sailing]] vessels of his era, including the clipper ship *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]*. His career spanned the golden age of sail and its decline in the face of steam competition. By the 1890s, with [[sail|sailing]] [[cargo|freight]]er work scarce, Slocum found himself — in his own words — an old sailor trying to quit the sea, but "born in the breezes" (001-005) and unable to stay ashore.

> "I was born in the breezes, and I had studied the sea as perhaps few men have studied it, neglecting all else." — (001-005)

## Role in the Voyage

Slocum's [[voyage|circumnavigation]] began in April 1895 from [[boston|Boston]], Massachusetts, aboard the 37-foot sloop *[[spray|Spray]]*, a derelict oyster boat he had rebuilt with his own hands. Starting from "a stout oak-tree nearby for a keel" (001-008), he transformed a rotting hulk into a vessel capable of [[sail|sailing]] [[voyage|around the world]]. Over three years, he covered approximately 46,000 miles, crossing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], navigating [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], traversing [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], rounding [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, and returning to [[newport|Newport]], [[newport|Rhode Island]] in June 1898. He sailed without a chronometer, without an engine, and without a crew — the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s uncanny ability to hold her course with a lashed [[rudder|helm]] making solo ocean [[sail|sailing]] possible. Slocum described his method with characteristic modesty: "The want of a chronometer for the voyage was all that needed to be said about my method of navigation" (002-009).

## Significance

Slocum's achievement was unprecedented and has never been equaled in its spirit. He was not a wealthy yachtsman but a working captain who built his own vessel, provisioned it simply, and [[current|set]] out alone across oceans that had always been sailed by crews. His book, written in the voice of a practical seaman, became an immediate classic and remains in print. Slocum disappeared at sea in 1909 while [[sail|sailing]] alone toward the Orinoco River — a fitting end for a man who, by his own accounting, had "studied the sea as perhaps few men have studied it" (001-005).
