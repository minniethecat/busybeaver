# Alexander Selkirk

Alexander Selkirk (1676–1721) was a Scottish sailor who, after a dispute with his captain, was voluntarily marooned on an uninhabited island in the [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] archipelago — and survived alone for four years and four months. His story inspired Daniel Defoe's *[[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]*, and for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], visiting Selkirk's island was a meeting with the patron saint of solitary mariners.

## Biography

Born Alexander Selcraig in Lower Largo, Fife, he went to sea as a young man and joined a privateering expedition under William Dampier. In 1704, after quarreling with the captain of the *Cinque Ports*, he asked to be put ashore on [[juan-fernandez|Más a Tierra]] — the largest of the [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez Islands]], 400 miles off the coast of [[chile|Chile]]. He took a musket, a knife, a Bible, and little else. When he was rescued in 1709, he had become a legend.

## In Slocum's Visit

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] on April 26, 1896, fifteen days out from [[cape-pillar|Cape Pillar]]. The island was not merely a geographical waypoint — it was hallowed ground for anyone who knew the story of the castaway:

> "The blue hills of Juan Fernandez, high among the clouds, could be seen about thirty miles off. A thousand emotions thrilled me when I saw the island, and I bowed my head to the deck. We may mock the Oriental salaam, but for my part I could find no other way of expressing myself." (010-019)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a pilgrimage to Selkirk's lookout — the high point from which the castaway had scanned the horizon for a sail. He found there a bronze tablet, erected by the officers of H.M.S. *Topaze* in 1868, and transcribed its inscription into his book. The tablet commemorated Selkirk's four years and four months of solitude, his rescue by the privateer *Duke* in 1709, and his death as a lieutenant in the Royal Navy at age forty-seven (011-004) (011-010).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own solitude was voluntary and temporary; Selkirk's was enforced and indefinite. Yet the skipper of the *[[spray|Spray]]* felt a kinship with the castaway — both men alone on an island in the vast Pacific, each making his peace with solitude. His verdict on the island and its most famous resident was characteristically plainspoken:

> "Juan Fernandez, as a place of call, is a lovely spot. The hills are well wooded, the valleys fertile, and pouring down through many ravines are streams of pure water. There are no serpents on the island, and no wild beasts other than pigs and goats. ... There was not a police officer or a lawyer among them. The domestic economy of the island was simplicity itself. The fashions of Paris did not affect the inhabitants; each dressed according to his own taste. ... Blessed island of Juan Fernandez! Why Alexander Selkirk ever left you was more than I could make out." (011-002)

The line is partly jest — but only partly. After the terrors of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] and the long Pacific passage, the green hills, pure streams, and simple society of [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] must have seemed like paradise. In the castaway's island, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found a vision of the life he might have chosen — if the sea had not called him back.

## Significance

Selkirk, like [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] and [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]], belongs to the historical substratum of the voyage — the earlier mariners whose experiences give depth and context to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own. The castaway and the circumnavigator: two extremes of the sailor's relationship with solitude.
