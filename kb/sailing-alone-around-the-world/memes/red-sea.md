# Red Sea

The Red Sea is a narrow seawater inlet of the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] lying between Africa and Asia, connected to [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] Sea via the [[suez|Suez Canal]]. In the 1890s, the Red Sea was known among sailors for its hazards, including piracy and difficult navigation.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s original intention was to sail eastward from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] through [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] and the [[suez|Suez Canal]] into the Red Sea, and then continue east about the world (005-002). He abandoned this plan at [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], choosing instead to sail westward across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]].

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later reflected on this decision with relief: "My determination to sail westward from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] not only enabled me to escape the pirates of the Red Sea, but, in bringing me to [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], landed me on familiar shores" (005-024). The mention of "pirates of the Red Sea" refers to the ongoing piracy problems that plagued the region during the late 19th century.

## Piracy in the Late 19th Century

The Red Sea piracy [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] referred to was a genuine maritime security concern of the era. Somali and Arabian coastal raiders targeted slow-moving merchant vessels, particularly [[sail|sailing]] ships that could not outrun their dhows.

British naval patrols from Aden provided some protection, but the vast coastline and numerous inlets made comprehensive policing impossible. For a single-handed sailor in a small sloop, the risk was acute — a vessel like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, with only one man on watch, would have been an easy target.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s decision to avoid the Red Sea was both prudent and, in retrospect, fortunate — it forced him onto the far more demanding but ultimately legendary westward route.

## Navigation and Climate

The Red Sea is notorious among mariners for its challenging conditions: strong and unpredictable winds channeled between high desert coasts, extreme heat, and coral [[reefing|reef]]s that extend far offshore. In the age of sail, the passage from Suez to the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] required careful timing to coincide with favorable monsoon winds. The combination of navigational difficulty and piracy made the Red Sea one of the least attractive waterways for a lone sailor in the 1890s.

> Now, my course to Gibraltar had been taken with a view to proceed up the Mediterranean Sea, through the Suez Canal, down the Red Sea, and east about, instead of a western route, which I finally adopted.
>
> (005-002)
