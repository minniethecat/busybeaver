# New Guinea

New Guinea appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative primarily through his encounters with other vessels, his observations of colonial politics, and his awareness of the human cost of the region's gold rushes. Though he did not land there, the island figured in the routes, stories, and tragedies of the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]] that surrounded his voyage through the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]] and [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]].

## Geography

New Guinea is the world's second-largest island, located north of [[australia|Australia]] in the western Pacific. Its mountainous interior and dense tropical forests made much of the island unexplored in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time. The island straddles the equator, with its western half under Dutch control and the eastern portion divided between German (Kaiser-Wilhelmsland in the northeast) and British (southeastern quarter) spheres of influence in the 1890s.

## Role in the Voyage

### The Akbar Incident

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s most detailed connection to New Guinea comes through his encounter with the cutter-yacht *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* on May 13, 1897, off [[port-macquarie|Port Macquarie]] on the [[newcastle|New South Wales]] coast. The *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*'s owner "wanted to race the poor old [[spray|Spray]] to [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] en route" — which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] declined "on the ground of the unfairness of three young yachtsmen in a clipper against an old sailor all alone in a craft of coarse build" (014-020)(014-021).

The encounter had a sobering outcome:

> "They were bound for New Guinea, so they said; perhaps it was." (014-020)

The *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* was already in distress when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found her — her mainsail blown to ribbons, her jigger blown away, "her [[rigging|rig]]ging flew at loose ends. The [[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]], in a word, was a wreck" (014-024). The crew refused [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s suggestion to sail for [[sydney|Sydney]] and insisted on making for [[newcastle|Newcastle]] under jury-[[sail|sails]] (014-026).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s concern was justified. The final entry for the *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* in his narrative reads: "May 31, the yacht *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*, from [[sydney|Sydney]] for New Guinea, three hands on board, lost at Crescent Head; the crew saved" (014-035). The crew survived, but the yacht was lost — a cautionary tale of Pacific navigation.

### Colonial Annexation

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded a significant political observation about New Guinea during his passage through the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]]:

> "It was —— who annexed the island of New Guinea to Great Britain." (015-013)

This refers to the British protectorate proclaimed over southeastern New Guinea in 1884 by Commodore James Elphinstone Erskine, which became the colony of British New Guinea (later the Territory of Papua). The 1890s saw continued European colonial expansion in [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], with New Guinea divided among the Dutch, Germans, and British — a process [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed while [[sail|sailing]] through the very waters that connected these colonial territories.

### Human Cost of the Gold Rushes

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s most affecting observation about New Guinea concerns the human toll of its mineral wealth. At [[cooktown|Cooktown, Queensland]] — a staging point for the New Guinea goldfields — he witnessed:

> "Physical wrecks of miners from New Guinea, destitute and dying. Many had left their bones there, and the destitute survivors who had drifted back to the mainland were getting their scurvy-grass and recovering from the effects of their hardships." (015-012)

This passage, with its harrowing description of "physical wrecks" and miners who "had left their bones there," reveals the grim underside of the colonial gold rushes. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] connected this to his own experience: "I think it was in 1866 that the old steamship *Soushay*, from [[batavia|Batavia]] for [[sydney|Sydney]], put in at [[cooktown|Cooktown]] for scurvy-grass... On her sick-list was my fevered self" (015-012). Having himself been a sick sailor put ashore in a distant port, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s sympathy for the New Guinea miners was personal and profound.

### Thursday Island and Torres Strait

While not New Guinea itself, [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] in [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] — the gateway between the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]] and the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — served as the final [[australia|Australia]]n outpost before [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] departed for the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] crossing. Here he witnessed a Jubilee celebration with "some four hundred native warriors and their wives and children brought across from the mainland," a ceremony that highlighted the proximity and cultural connections between the [[australia|Australia]]n mainland, the [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] islands, and New Guinea to the north (015-025).

## Historical Context

New Guinea in the 1890s was a frontier of European colonial expansion. The southeast (British New Guinea) was administered from Port Moresby, while the northeast (German New Guinea, or Kaiser-Wilhelmsland) was a German colonial enterprise. Gold discoveries on the Mambare and Gira Rivers in the 1890s drew prospectors from across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] — many ill-prepared and ill-fated, as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s observations at [[cooktown|Cooktown]] attest.

The island's position north of the [[great-barrier-reef|Barrier Reef]] and [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] made it a natural reference point for vessels navigating the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]]. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], passing through these waters in mid-1897, New Guinea was simultaneously a destination for the doomed *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*, a colonial possession being carved up by European powers, and a source of human tragedy witnessed in the broken miners of [[cooktown|Cooktown]].
