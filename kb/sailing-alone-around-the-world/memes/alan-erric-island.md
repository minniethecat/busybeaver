**Alan Erric Island** is a small island near [[port-angosto|Port Angosto]] in the western reaches of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], discovered and named by [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] during his struggles to exit the strait into [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. On nautical charts of the period it had been marked merely as a point of land — a peninsula rather than an island (010-010).

## Geography

The island lies in the complex archipelago of the western [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], a region of drowned mountain valleys, deep fjords, and scattered islets carved by Pleistocene glaciation. The terrain is rugged and mountainous, with steep slopes descending directly to the sea. Vegetation is sparse due to the extreme winds and the rocky substrate; what grows is mostly low scrub and hardy grasses.

The waters surrounding Alan Erric Island are subject to powerful [[current|tidal current]]s that sweep through the narrow channels of the western strait. These currents, combined with the unpredictable williwaws that descend from the surrounding peaks, make navigation in this area extremely challenging for small [[sail|sailing]] vessels. It was precisely these tidal forces that caused the *[[spray|Spray]]* to [[current|drift]] repeatedly around the island — an involuntary [[voyage|circumnavigation]] that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found both exasperating and comical.

## Discovery

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s discovery of the island occurred during his seventh and final attempt to [[anchor|weigh anchor]] from [[port-angosto|Port Angosto]] on April 13. He chose this date despite superstitious misgivings: "Difficulties, however, multiplied all about in so strange a manner that had I been given to superstitious fears I should not have persisted in [[sail|sailing]] on a thirteenth day, notwithstanding that a fair wind blew in the offing" (010-010).

One of these difficulties was both frustrating and comical: the *[[spray|Spray]]* [[current|drift]]ed three times around a small island against [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s will. In exasperation, he apostrophized his vessel:

> "Didn't you know that you couldn't climb a tree!" — (010-010)

Yet his frustration soon softened: "But the poor old [[spray|Spray]] had essayed, and successfully too, nearly everything else in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], and my heart softened toward her when I thought of what she had gone through. Moreover, she had discovered an island" (010-010).

## Naming

Noting that the feature was charted as a peninsula rather than an island, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] exercised the discoverer's privilege with characteristic humor:

> "I named it Alan Erric Island, after a worthy literary friend whom I had met in strange by-places, and I put up a sign, 'Keep off the grass,' which, as discoverer, was within my rights." — (010-010)

The identity of "Alan Erric" remains uncertain. Some scholars suggest it may be a pseudonym or anagram for a friend or acquaintance [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered during his travels. The whimsical sign — "Keep off the grass" — reflects [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s playful spirit even amid the hardships of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]].

## Historical Context

The charting and naming of geographical features in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] has a long history dating to [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]'s expedition of 1520. For centuries, the strait's complex coastline resisted accurate mapping; even by the late nineteenth century, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed through, charts of the western reaches remained imperfect. Features that appeared as peninsulas on one chart could prove to be islands on closer inspection — as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] discovered.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s naming of Alan Erric Island belongs to a tradition of navigator-discoverers who left their mark on the charts of [[patagonia|Patagonia]]. His discovery illustrates how even in the late 1890s, a small vessel under sail could still contribute to geographical knowledge in this remote and poorly charted region. The island stands as one of the few geographical features named by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] himself during his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].
