A sailing vessel rigged with fore-and-aft sails on two or more masts, with the forward mast (foremast) being shorter than or equal in height to the mainmast. By the 1890s, the schooner was the workhorse of the North Atlantic fisheries and coastal trade, valued for its maneuverability, modest crew requirements, and ability to sail close to the wind.

## Definition and Rig

The defining feature of a schooner is its fore-and-aft rig: the sails are set parallel to the keel rather than square across the ship, allowing the vessel to point higher into the wind than a square-rigger. The simplest schooner has two masts (main and fore), but larger schooners could carry three, four, or even more masts. The schooner's principal advantage for coastal work is that it requires fewer hands than a square-rigged vessel of comparable size — a crucial economic consideration. Fishing schooners, in particular, were renowned for their speed and weatherliness, qualities that made them the platform of choice for the Grand Banks cod fishery.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Schooners appear throughout Slocum's narrative, often as informal benchmarks against which he measured the *Spray's* performance, and as reminders of his own early seafaring life.

Slocum's first taste of the sea had been aboard a schooner: "I was informed that the vessel was on a fishing voyage, and that I had been shipped as cook on a fishing-schooner" (001-003) — a formative (if short-lived) experience that introduced him to life afloat. Off the coast of Nova Scotia, Slocum noted "the reef where the schooner Hesperus struck I passed close aboard" (002-005), referring to the wreck that inspired Longfellow's famous poem. These waters also reminded him of a story he had "read somewhere of a fishing-schooner hooking her anchor into a whale" (002-012).

In the approaches to the Azores, "a large schooner came out of Liverpool, Nova Scotia" (003-004), a welcome sight of a familiar rig in mid-ocean. Off the South American coast came a more poignant encounter: "On September 23 the fine schooner Nantasket of Boston, from Buenos Aires, passed close aboard" (005-019) — a reminder of New England maritime commerce reaching across hemispheres.

Each schooner encounter reinforced Slocum's connection to the North Atlantic sailing tradition he had grown up in, and underscored the *Spray's* own heritage as a working vessel adapted for a singular voyage.

> A large schooner came out of Liverpool, Nova Scotia, this morning, steering eastward. The Spray put her hull down astern in five hours.

> (003-004)
