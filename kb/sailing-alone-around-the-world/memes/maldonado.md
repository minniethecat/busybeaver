# Maldonado

Maldonado was [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s refuge after the near-disaster on the sands of [[uruguay|Uruguay]]. The British consul — and his daughter — extended a warm welcome, and the bay provided a peaceful anchorage where the *[[spray|Spray]]* could rest and her captain could recover his spirits. It was also a place of good fishing, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], ever the practical sailor, did not fail to note.

## Geography

Maldonado lies on the northern shore of the [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio]] de la Plata estuary, east of [[montevideo|Montevideo]] and not far from the popular resort of Punta del Este. The bay is well sheltered, and in the 1890s it was a modest port serving the agricultural hinterland. For a sailor arriving from the open Atlantic, it offered the first secure anchorage after the rough waters off the [[uruguay|Uruguayan coast]].

## In the Voyage

The arrival at Maldonado was a dramatic conclusion to a harrowing chapter. After the *[[spray|Spray]]* was pulled from the beach, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made his way along the coast. "The following day I anchored at Maldonado. The British consul, his daughter" — (006-008) came to greet him, a reception that must have felt like deliverance after the ordeal on the beach.

The consul had already been [[course|track]]ing his progress. Earlier reports had come "from the British consul at Maldonado — A warm greeting at [[montevideo|Montevideo]]" — (006-001). The diplomatic corps of this small port took a keen interest in the lone American.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted the natural abundance of the bay: "In the waters of Maldonado Bay a variety of fishes abound" — (006-009). After weeks of ship's provisions, the prospect of fresh fish was welcome. The bay was rich in marine life, and the sailor who had lived off the sea for months appreciated a place where the sea gave freely.

The visit to Maldonado marked the end of the [[uruguay|Uruguay]]an interlude. From there, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would sail to [[montevideo|Montevideo]] — and soon afterward to [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], where the *[[spray|Spray]]* would be modified for the southern passage. But Maldonado remained in his memory as the place where danger had turned to kindness, and where the voyage had been saved.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, Maldonado was a small departmental capital with a British consulate — a reflection of the significant British commercial presence in [[uruguay|Uruguay]]. British investment in railways, utilities, and agriculture had made [[uruguay|Uruguay]] something of an informal British economic colony. The consul who greeted [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was part of this network, and his warm reception of the American sailor reflected both personal curiosity and the broader culture of maritime hospitality that British diplomats maintained in ports [[voyage|around the world]].

> On the following day I anchored at Maldonado. The British consul, his daughter, and another young lady came on board, bringing with them a basket of fresh eggs, strawberries, bottles of milk, and a great loaf of sweet bread. This was a good landfall, and better cheer than I had found at Maldonado once upon a time when I entered the port with a stricken crew in my bark, the *Aquidneck*.
>
> (006-008)
