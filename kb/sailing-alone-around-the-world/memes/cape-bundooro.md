# Cape Bundooro

Cape Bundooro is a coastal cape on the southern shore of [[australia|Australia]], near the entrance to [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]]. It occupies a warm place in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative — not for danger or drama, but for the human connection of a Christmas greeting exchanged across the water.

## First Passage (December 1896)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* approached Cape Bundooro on December 12, 1896, during her southward run toward [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]]. It was a rare fine day on a coast known for rough weather: "The [[spray|Spray]] early in the morning passed [[twofold-bay|Twofold Bay]] and later Cape Bundooro in a smooth sea with land close aboard" — (013-015). The cape's [[beacon|lighthouse]] keepers and the few people ashore made the passage memorable.

"The [[beacon|lighthouse]] on the cape dipped a flag to [[spray|the Spray]]'s flag, and children on the balconies of a cottage near the shore waved handkerchiefs as she passed by" — (013-015). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted the Christmas decorations ashore: "I saw festoons of evergreen in token of Christmas, near at hand. I saluted the merrymakers, wishing them a 'Merry Christmas' and could hear them say, 'I wish you the same'" — (013-015).

From Cape Bundooro, the *[[spray|Spray]]* continued toward the treacherous waters of [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]]: "From Cape Bundooro I passed by Cliff Island in [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]], and exchanged signals with the light-keepers while [[spray|the Spray]] worked up under the island. The wind howled that day while the sea broke over their rocky home" — (013-016).

## Return Passage (April 1897)

Months later, retracing her course northward after rounding [[australia|Australia]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* passed Bundooro again. "A foretaste of winter wind sent [[spray|the Spray]] flying round [[cape-howe|Cape Howe]] and as far as Cape Bundooro farther along, which she passed on the following day, retracing her course northward" — (014-014).

The reception was as warm as before: "My old Christmas friends on Bundooro seemed to be up and moving when I came the second time by their cape, and we exchanged signals again, while [[sloop|the sloop]] sailed along as before in a smooth sea and close to the shore" — (014-014). The repetition of this small ritual — a dipped flag, a waved handkerchief — gave the lonely voyager a sense of continuity and human connection on an otherwise empty coast.

## Geography

Cape Bundooro lies east of [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] on [[victoria|the Victoria]] coast, near [[eden|Twofold Bay]]. It is a modest headland, but its [[beacon|lighthouse]] served as a vital navigation mark for coasting vessels making the run between [[sydney|Sydney]] and [[melbourne|Melbourne]] in the days before GPS and radar.

> There was little to report on this part of the voyage, except changeable winds, “busters,” and rough seas. The 12th of December, however, was an exceptional day, with a fine coast wind, northeast. The *Spray* early in the morning passed Twofold Bay and later Cape Bundooro in a smooth sea with land close aboard. The lighthouse on the cape dipped a flag to the *Spray*’s flag, and children on the balconies of a cottage near the shore waved handkerchiefs as she passed by. There were only a few people all told on the shore, but the scene was a happy one. I saw festoons of evergreen in token of Christmas, near at hand. I saluted the merrymakers, wishing them a “Merry Christmas” and could hear them say, “I wish you the same.”
>
> (013-015)
