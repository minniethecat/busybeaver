# Cape of Good Hope

The Cape of Good Hope is the great headland near the southern tip of Africa — a name bestowed by King John II of Portugal in hope of a sea route to India, replacing its older name: the Cape of [[storm|Storm]]s.

For every sailor who has rounded it, the Cape is a milestone. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rounded it alone, on Christmas Day 1897, and spent three months in its shadow.

## Geography

The Cape is not the southernmost point of Africa — that is Cape Agulhas, about 90 miles to the east — but it is the point where the coastline turns decisively eastward, making it the psychological southernmost point for mariners. The meeting of cold and warm currents creates some of the most dangerous sea conditions in the world.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted that gales swept round the cape "on an average, every thirty-six hours," though "one gale was much the same as another, with no more serious result than to blow [[spray|the Spray]] along on her course when it was fair, or to blow her back somewhat when it was ahead." (018-003)

## In the Voyage

### Approaching the Cape

The Cape of Good Hope was "the most prominent point to pass." (018-002) [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] knew that "from [[cape-town|Table Bay]] I could count on the aid of brisk [[trade-wind|trades]], and then [[spray|the Spray]] would soon be at home." (018-002) The distance to [[cape-town|Table Bay]] was about eight hundred miles "over what might prove a rough sea." (018-002)

He mused on the early Portuguese navigators, who "were more than sixty-nine years struggling to round this cape before they got as far as [[port-elizabeth|Algoa Bay]], and there the crew mutinied." (018-002) It was Vasco da Gama who sailed successfully round "the Cape of [[storm|Storm]]s, as the Cape of Good Hope was then called." (018-002)

On Christmas Day 1897, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "came to the pitch of the cape." (018-003) Two days later, [[spray|the Spray]] passed Cape Agulhas in company with a steamship. (018-002) The [[beacon|lighthouse]] keeper on Agulhas "exchanged signals with [[spray|the Spray]] as she passed, and afterward wrote me at New York congratulations on the completion of the voyage." (018-002)

### The Flying Dutchman and Arrival

One more gale forced [[spray|the Spray]] into Simons Bay. "When it moderated she beat around the Cape of Good Hope, where they say the Flying Dutchman is still [[sail|sailing]]." (018-005)

> One more gale of wind came down upon the *Spray* from the west after she passed Cape Agulhas, but that one she dodged by getting into Simons Bay. When it moderated she beat around the Cape of Good Hope, where they say the *Flying Dutchman* is still sailing. The voyage then seemed as good as finished; from this time on I knew that all, or nearly all, would be plain sailing.
>
> (018-005)

After the hard weather, [[spray|the Spray]] "ran into a calm under Table Mountain, where she lay quietly till the generous sun rose over the land and drew a breeze in from the sea." (018-006) She anchored off [[cape-town|Cape Town]] "simply to rest clear of the bustle of commerce." (018-006)

### Cape Town Sojourn

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] remained at [[cape-town|Cape Town]] for about three months, [[spray|the Spray]] berthed in the Alfred Dry Docks "in the care of the port authorities, while I traveled the country over from Simons Town to [[pretoria|Pretoria]], being accorded by the colonial government a free railroad pass over all the land." (018-007)

He was invited to Newlands Vineyard, visited the famous Cape Observatory as a guest of Dr. David Gill, the astronomer royal, and received a visit from Admiral Sir Harry Rawson of the Royal Navy. (018-009) At a dinner party, the song of a cricket reminded him of home; his host, Mr. Branscombe, captured a pair and sent them aboard [[spray|the Spray]]. (019-015)

### Departure

The [[spray|Spray]] sailed from South Africa on March 26, 1898, "riding over a heavy swell, in full view of Table Mountain and the high peaks of the Cape of Good Hope." (018-014) [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recalled that [[francis-drake|Sir Francis Drake]], when he first saw this cape, had sung: "'Tis the fairest thing and the grandest cape I've seen in the whole circumference of the earth." (018-014)

## Significance

The Cape of Good Hope is one of the three great capes — with [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] and [[cape-leeuwin|Cape Leeuwin]] — that define a [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], it was not just a navigational milestone but a place of extended rest, social warmth, and reflection. The three months in [[cape-town|Cape Town]] represented the longest shore interval of the entire voyage.
