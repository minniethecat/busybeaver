# Pinta

The *Pinta* was one of the three vessels of [[christopher-columbus|Christopher Columbus]]'s first voyage across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] in 1492 — a caravel, fast and maneuverable, captained by Martín Alonso Pinzón. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the *Pinta* was more than a historical vessel: her name became a private joke stretched across the entire Atlantic, bestowed on the pilot-fish that followed the [[Spray]] for days, and her pilot — in a fever dream during a [[gale]] — came to steer [[sloop|the sloop]] when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could no longer hold the [[helm]].

## Specifications

The *Pinta* was a caravel — a small, highly maneuverable [[sail|sailing]] vessel developed by the Portuguese in the 15th century and adopted by the Spanish for exploration. Caravels typically carried lateen (triangular) [[sail|sails]] on two or three [[mast|masts]], allowing them to sail closer to the wind than square-[[rigging|rig]]ged vessels — an essential capability for navigating uncharted waters. The *Pinta* was the fastest of [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]]'s three ships and was the first to sight land in the New World (by the lookout Rodrigo de Triana on October 12, 1492).

## History

The *Pinta* was built around 1490, likely in Palos de la Frontera, Spain. She participated in [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]]'s first voyage (1492–1493) and possibly in later expeditions, but her fate after 1500 is unrecorded — a common end for the working vessels of the Age of Discovery. Unlike the *[[santa-maria|Santa Maria]]*, which was wrecked on [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]]'s first voyage, or the *[[victoria|Victoria]]*, which completed [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]], the *Pinta* simply vanished from history.

## Role in the Voyage

The *Pinta* enters [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative not as a historical reference but as a living presence — first in a dream, then in the form of a fish. During a severe Atlantic [[gale]], ill and exhausted after days of fighting [[gale|heavy weather]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had a fever dream in which the pilot of the *Pinta* appeared at the [[rudder|helm]]:

> "I am the pilot of the *Pinta* come to aid you. Lie quiet, señor captain, and I will guide your ship." — (004-003)

When he awoke, the [[Spray]] was still holding her [[course]] perfectly — "going like a racehorse" (004-011). The dream-pilot had done his work. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a practical man not given to mysticism, recorded the vision with characteristic humor rather than awe; but the note of gratitude is unmistakable.

The name "Pinta" then transferred to the pilot-fish that followed the *[[spray|Spray]]* across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] adopted the creature as a companion — "calling him, I suppose, to the phantom *Pinta*" (004-011) — and wrote of it with genuine affection. The fish stayed with [[sloop|the sloop]] for days, a living talisman, and its departure was noted with regret.

## Significance

In [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative, the *Pinta* bridges the historical and the personal. [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]]'s caravel and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s pilot-fish are linked by a name and a function — both guided a vessel across an unknown sea. The dream sequence, unusual in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s matter-of-fact narrative, reveals the psychological strain of single-handed [[sail|sailing]]: when the body is exhausted and the mind is alone, the boundary between waking and dreaming, between history and the present, thins.

The *Pinta* also connects [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to the larger tradition of Atlantic exploration. [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]], [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] are linked not only by their voyages but by the vessels that carried them — and by the names, jokes, and dreams that accompanied them across the ocean.
