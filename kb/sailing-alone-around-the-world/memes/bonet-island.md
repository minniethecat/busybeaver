**Bonet Island** is a small island in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], "one hundred and thirty-six feet high" (009-017), where [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] narrowly escaped an ambush by [[fuegians|Fuegian natives]] during his passage through the strait's western reaches.

## Geography

Bonet Island lies in the western portion of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], in the archipelago-studded waters between the Brunswick Peninsula and the islands of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]. The island rises to 136 feet (approximately 41 meters) above sea level, with grass growing on its summit — vegetation that provided cover for the [[fuegians|Fuegians]] who hid there (009-017).

The waters around Bonet Island are swept by strong [[current|tidal current]]s characteristic of the narrow channels of the western strait. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted that the *[[spray|Spray]]* was "[[current|drift]]ing helplessly with the tide, and with every prospect of going on the rocks, for there was no anchorage, at least, none that my cables would reach" (009-017). The combination of treacherous currents, rocky shores, and limited anchorage made this stretch of the strait particularly hazardous for a small [[sail|sailing]] vessel.

## The Fuegian Encounter

While navigating the western reaches of the strait, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed a group of six [[fuegians|Fuegians]] in a ship's boat "stolen probably from a murdered crew" (009-017). Two of the savages stood erect wearing sea-boots, which "sustained the suspicion that they had fallen upon some luckless ship's crew, and also added a hint that they had already visited [[spray|the Spray]]'s deck" (009-017).

The [[fuegians|Fuegians]] paddled past [[sloop|the sloop]] at a distance of a hundred yards in what [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] judged to be a calculated maneuver. He kept a sharp lookout over a small island "which soon came in range between them and [[sloop|the sloop]], completely hiding them from view" (009-017). The *[[spray|Spray]]* was now "[[current|drift]]ing helplessly with the tide, and with every prospect of going on the rocks" (009-017).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] soon spotted movement in the grass on top of the island:

> "And, sure enough, I soon saw a movement in the grass just on top of the island, which is called Bonet Island and is one hundred and thirty-six feet high. I fired several shots over the place, but saw no other sign of the savages." — (009-017)

As [[sloop|the sloop]] swept past, "the rebound of the tide carrying her clear, there on the other side was the boat, surely enough exposing their cunning and treachery" (009-017). The ambush had been real — the [[fuegians|Fuegians]] had used the island to conceal their true position while awaiting the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s [[current|drift]] toward the rocks.

## Aftermath

A stiff breeze came up suddenly, scattering the canoes and extricating the *[[spray|Spray]]* from her dangerous position. After clearing the savages and Bonet Island, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was treated to a spectacular moonlit scene:

> "A heavy cloud-bank that had swept across the sky then cleared away, and the night became suddenly as light as day, or nearly so. A high mountain was mirrored in the channel ahead, and the Spray sailing along with her shadow was as two sloops on the sea." — (009-018)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* made [[borgia-bay|Borgia Bay]] the following afternoon, where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anchored for the second time.

## Historical Context

Encounters between European mariners and the indigenous peoples of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] were a recurring feature of voyages through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] from the sixteenth century onward. The [[fuegians|Fuegians]] — comprising several distinct groups including the Yámana (Yahgan) and Kawésqar (Alacaluf) — were canoe-going people who had inhabited the channels and islands of the region for thousands of years.

By the late nineteenth century, contact with European sealers, whalers, and passing ships had often been violent. The [[fuegians|Fuegians]]' possession of a ship's boat "stolen probably from a murdered crew" (009-017) reflects the tragic cycle of violence that characterized these encounters. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experience at Bonet Island — a tense standoff resolved by gunfire and a fortunate breeze — exemplifies the mutual suspicion and danger that defined interactions between lone sailors and indigenous peoples in this remote corner of the world. Despite the peril, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s account, while employing the prejudicial language of his era, also conveys a certain respect for the [[fuegians|Fuegians]]' cunning as navigators and tacticians.
