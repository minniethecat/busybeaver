The **Narrow Escape from Shipwreck** occurred on December 11, 1895, when [[sloop|the sloop]] *[[spray|Spray]]* ran "hard and fast on the beach" at Castillo Chicos, on the coast of [[uruguay|Uruguay]]. [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] nearly drowned while attempting to carry out an anchor through the surf in his leaking dory, and the stranding became one of the most dramatic and personally perilous episodes of the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]] (006-001).

## Summary

After departing [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]] on November 28, 1895, the *[[spray|Spray]]* coasted southward along the Brazilian and [[uruguay|Uruguay]]an shores. On the night of December 10–11, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — deceived by the false appearance of sand-hills under a bright moon — hugged the shore too closely. At daybreak, the *[[spray|Spray]]* struck the beach and grounded. The effort to refloat her nearly cost [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] his life when his overloaded dory repeatedly swamped in the surf. With help from local residents, he eventually floated [[sloop|the sloop]], though she sustained damage to her keel and false shoe (006-001) (006-003).

## Context

The *[[spray|Spray]]* had sailed from [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]] after a stay that included fitting a yawl [[rigging|rig]] for the rough waters of [[patagonia|Patagonia]] and [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. Local shipmasters had inspected [[sloop|the sloop]] and contributed gear — [[captain-jones|Captain Jones]] gave an anchor, and a steamship donated a cable. Well-provisioned and in good spirits, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed south on November 28, immediately encountering a gale that "tore up things generally along the coast" (006-001).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was navigating by [[navigation|dead reckoning]] with a "tin clock," and had been gratified when a passing steamship confirmed his longitude exactly. But this confidence proved costly: he allowed the *[[spray|Spray]]* to [[current|drift]] too close to an unfamiliar shore.

## Narrative

### The Stranding

On the night of December 10, what appeared to be safe distance from shore was, in reality, a treacherous approach. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later admitted his mistake:

> "I confess a weakness: I hugged the shore entirely too close. In a word, at daybreak on the morning of December 11 the Spray ran hard and fast on the beach. This was annoying; but I soon found that the sloop was in no great danger. The false appearance of the sand-hills under a bright moon had deceived me, and I lamented now that I had trusted to appearances at all." — (006-002)

### The Perilous Anchor Work

With the tide falling and the *[[spray|Spray]]* already "sewed a foot" into the sand, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] launched his small dory to carry out a kedge anchor. But the loaded dory "was swamped at once in the surf, the load being too great for her." He cut the cable into two loads and tried again. On the second attempt, the leaking dory filled completely and began to sink:

> "I sprang from the oars to my feet, and lifting the anchor above my head, threw it clear just as she was turning over. I grasped her gunwale and held on as she turned bottom up, for I suddenly remembered that I could not swim." — (006-003)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], unable to swim, clung to the overturned dory as the current carried him seaward. "Three times I had been under water, in trying to [[rigging|rig]]ht the dory." Yet in the midst of mortal danger, he experienced an extraordinary calm:

> "Whatever the danger may have been, much or little, I can truly say that the moment was the most serene of my life." — (006-003)

On his fourth attempt, he [[rigging|rig]]hted the dory, climbed in, and paddled to shore — "somewhat the worse for wear and pretty full of salt water." The anchor, dropped at just the [[rigging|rig]]ht distance, allowed him to heave the *[[spray|Spray]]* off when the tide returned (006-004).

### The Boy and the Sloop

While [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rested on the sand, a local boy on horseback discovered the stranded vessel. Believing he had found an abandoned prize, the boy hitched his horse to [[sloop|the sloop]]'s bobstay and tried to drag it home. Finding it too heavy, he hid [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s skiff behind a dune — evidently planning to return with more horses. When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] revealed himself, he told the boy his ship was "from the moon" and "here for a cargo of boys." The startled lad coiled his lariat, perhaps contemplating towing [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] instead, but was placated by the offer of ship's biscuits. He soon returned with butter, milk, and eggs, and the two became friends (006-004) (006-005).

## Aftermath

With the help of a German [[current|set]]tler, a [[uruguay|Uruguay]]an soldier, and an Italian called "Angel of Milan," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] floated the *[[spray|Spray]]* and sailed for [[montevideo|Montevideo]] before the port captain's promised steam-tug even arrived. The stranding cost the *[[spray|Spray]]* "no small amount of pounding on the hard sand" — she lost her shoe and part of her [[keel|false keel]] — but the damage was "readily mended afterward in dock" (006-007).

At [[montevideo|Montevideo]], the Royal Mail Steamship Company repaired the *[[spray|Spray]]* free of charge and gave [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] twenty pounds sterling. The calkers made [[sloop|the sloop]] tight, carpenters mended the keel, and the dory was repainted "till I hardly knew it from a butterfly" (006-008). After a holiday excursion to [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* was ready for the most dangerous leg of the voyage: [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] and [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]].

## Significance

The stranding at Castillo Chicos is one of the most vivid and personally revealing episodes in *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. It exposes [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s humanity — his fallibility in navigation, his physical vulnerability (he could not swim), and his extraordinary composure under mortal threat. His remark that the moment was "the most serene of my life" has become one of the most quoted lines from the book, capturing the peculiar equanimity that made [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] so well-suited to solo voyaging. The incident also demonstrates the kindness of strangers that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered repeatedly: the German [[current|set]]tler, the [[uruguay|Uruguay]]an soldier, the Italian "Angel of Milan," and even the would-be horse thief who brought breakfast.
