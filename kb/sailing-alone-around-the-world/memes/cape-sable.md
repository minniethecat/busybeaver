**Cape Sable** is a headland and island at the southern extremity of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], marking the boundary between the [[bay-of-fundy|Bay of Fundy]] and the open [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic Ocean]]. For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], it was the point of departure from North American waters and the reference mark from which he measured the first ocean leg of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Slocum's Departure

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s approach to Cape Sable was unhurried. After departing [[yarmouth|Yarmouth]], following a stay at [[westport|Westport]] where he had reunited with old schoolmates, he was "in no hurry to round Cape Sable," making "a short excursion with some friends to [[st-marys-bay|St. Mary's Bay]]" before [[course|heading]] to sea (002-016).

On July 1, 1895, at last the weather turned favorable. The log entry for the following day is one of the voyage's most memorable:

> On the following day, the head sea having gone down, I sailed from Yarmouth, and let go my last hold on America. The log of my first day on the Atlantic in the *Spray* reads briefly: "9:30 a.m. sailed from Yarmouth. 4:30 p.m. passed Cape Sable; distance, three cables from the land. The sloop making eight knots. Fresh breeze N. W." (003-003)

Before sun[[current|set]] he was taking his supper of strawberries and tea "in smooth water under the lee of the east-coast land" (003-003), the *[[spray|Spray]]* skirting the [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] shore for the last time.

## The Atlantic Crossing

The cape became [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s benchmark for [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing. By July 10, "eight days at sea, the *[[spray|Spray]]* was twelve hundred miles east of Cape Sable" (003-009) — a pace of 150 miles a day, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted was "the greatest run the *[[spray|Spray]]* ever made before or since in so few days" (003-009).

The speed of his passage became a point of pride. On July 16, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered the bark *[[java-steamship|Java]]* of Glasgow, her captain was incredulous at the little [[sloop|sloop's]] progress:

> "How long has it been calm about here?" roared the captain of the *Java*, as I came within hail of him. "Dunno, cap'n," I shouted back as loud as I could bawl. "I haven't been here long." At this the mate on the forecastle wore a broad grin. "I left Cape Sable fourteen days ago," I added. (I was now well across toward the Azores.) "Mate," he roared to his chief officer — "mate, come here and listen to the Yankee's yarn. Haul down the flag, mate, haul down the flag!" In the best of humor, after all, the *Java* surrendered to the *Spray*. (003-010)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* reached [[flores-island|Flores]] in the [[azores|Azores]] "exactly eighteen days from Cape Sable" (003-017) — a remarkably fast passage for a 36-foot sloop. The complete crossing to [[fayal|Faial]] took "less than twenty-nine days from Cape Sable" (004-016).

## Sable Island Fog

After passing Cape Sable, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed east of [[sable-island|Sable Island]] — a place he called "the [[sable-island|Island of Tragedies]]" (003-004). Dense fog descended: "I was in a world of fog, shut off from the universe" (003-004). He navigated by the lead line through the night, passing the dangerous shoals. Within a few years, he noted, [[sable-island|Sable Island]] had been "reduced from forty miles in length to twenty, and that of three [[beacon|lighthouse]]s built on it since 1880, two have been washed away" (003-004).

## Geographic Significance

Cape Sable sits at approximately 43.4°N, where the cold waters of [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]] — home to the world's highest tides — meet the Gulf of Maine. The cape's low, sandy profile belies the dangerous shoals and tide races that extend offshore, making it a notorious navigation hazard. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]n by birth, passing Cape Sable marked the transition from familiar home waters to the open Atlantic and the true beginning of his solitary voyage.
