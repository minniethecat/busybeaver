# Robinson Crusoe

Robinson Crusoe, the fictional hero of Daniel Defoe's 1719 novel, is the most famous castaway in literature. Shipwrecked alone on a tropical island, he survives by ingenuity and faith — a story that has captivated readers (and sailors) for three centuries. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], [[sail|sailing]] alone across the world's oceans, Crusoe was more than a literary character: he was a mirror.

## Biography (Literary)

In Defoe's novel, Crusoe is a young Englishman who defies his father's wishes and goes to sea. After a series of adventures, he is shipwrecked on an uninhabited island — based on [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]]'s [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] — where he spends 28 years. Through resourcefulness and self-discipline, he builds a life: shelter, agriculture, animal husbandry, and finally the companionship of Friday, a native he rescues from cannibals. The novel is, among other things, a meditation on solitude, self-reliance, and civilization.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] visited [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] — Crusoe's island (010-001) — as a kind of literary pilgrim. He knew the novel well, and his own situation — a man alone on the sea, depending entirely on his own resources — mirrored Crusoe's in ways both obvious and subtle. Crusoe was shipwrecked against his will; [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had chosen his solitude. Crusoe built a kingdom from [[current|drift]]wood; [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had built his vessel from a derelict hulk. Both men measured their days by the sun and their achievements by survival.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reflections on Crusoe are characteristically understated — he was not a man for literary criticism — but the presence of the fictional castaway in the narrative testifies to the power of a story that had shaped the imagination of every English-speaking sailor since 1719.

## Significance

Crusoe and [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]] — the fictional and the real — stand together at [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], reminding the reader that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage moves through a landscape layered with stories. The sea has its literature as well as its geography, and the solo circumnavigator [[sail|sails]] through both.
