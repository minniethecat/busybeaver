After weathering a severe gale in the [[mozambique-channel|Mozambique Channel]] that tested the *[[spray|Spray]]* as severely as [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at [[durban|Durban]] on November 17, 1897. The city — "the continuation of a garden" — received him with keen interest, local press coverage, and the hospitality of its two yacht clubs. His near month-long stay provided a welcome respite before the final leg of his [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] crossing.

## Summary

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at [[durban|Durban]] after a passage from [[mauritius|Mauritius]] that included a hard gale in the [[mozambique-channel|Mozambique Channel]] from November 6–9, which "the *[[spray|Spray]]* suffered as much as she did anywhere, except off [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]" (017-014). The signalman on the bluff reported [[sloop|the sloop]]'s approach, and the local paper carried news of her arrival within three minutes of her being sighted (017-016). Moored at [[durban|Durban]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was warmly received by the city's two yacht clubs, sailed with the premier of the colony, visited public schools, and entertained an extraordinary visit from three Boers sent by [[president-kruger|President Krüger]] to prove the world was flat. He departed on December 14, 1897, for [[port-elizabeth|Port Elizabeth]]. (017-025) (017-026)

## Context

[[durban|Durban]], the commercial center of what [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] called the "Garden Colony" of [[durban|Natal]], was a bustling port city with an active yachting community. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had just endured some of the roughest weather of the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — the [[mozambique-channel|Mozambique Channel]] gale had torn his [[sail|sails]] and demanded constant vigilance (017-014). He arrived in need of repairs and rest. The port was well-equipped for such, with a pilot tug that came out to meet the *[[spray|Spray]]* and led her across the bar in rough conditions (017-020).

[[south-africa|South Africa]] at the time was a British colony, though the Boer republics to the north maintained their independence — a tension that would soon erupt into the Second Boer War (1899–1902). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit coincided with a period of relative calm.

## Narrative

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s approach to [[durban|Durban]] was visible from land long before she arrived. The signalman at the bluff station reported her fifteen miles off, and when she came within eight miles, he observed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s seamanship:

> "The *Spray* is short-sailing offshore; the skipper has been obliged to reef" — (017-016)

This observation, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted with amusement, "was printed three minutes later in a [[durban|Durban]] morning journal, which was handed to me when I arrived in port" (017-016). The local press coverage of his arrival was extensive.

A [[durban|Durban]] newspaper captured the scene at the quay:

> "As briefly noted yesterday, the *Spray*, with a crew of one man, arrived at this port yesterday afternoon on her cruise round the world. The *Spray* made quite an auspicious entrance to Natal. Her commander sailed his craft right up the channel past the main wharf, and dropped his anchor near the *Florence* at the yacht-step." — (017-019)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found a warm welcome among fellow sailors: "I found that [[durban|Durban]] supported two yacht-clubs, both of them full of enterprise" (017-021). He was invited to sail in the crack yacht *Florence* of the Royal [[durban|Natal]] Yacht Club with Captain Spradbrow and the Right Honorable Harry Escombe, premier of the colony (017-021).

During his stay, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] visited many public schools and "had the pleasure of meeting many b[[rigging|rig]]ht children" (017-025). But perhaps the most peculiar encounter came from [[pretoria|Pretoria]]:

> "While I was at Durban they came from Pretoria to obtain data from me, and they seemed annoyed when I told them that the world was round. They had come to prove that it was flat." — (017-023)

Three Boers, acting on the opinion of [[president-kruger|President Krüger]], had prepared a work to support the flat-earth contention. "It sounds odd," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reflected, "to hear scholars and statesmen say the world is flat" (017-023).

## Aftermath

On December 14, 1897, after nearly a month at [[durban|Durban]], "the 'crew' of the *[[spray|Spray]]*, after having a fine time in [[durban|Natal]], swung [[sloop|the sloop]]'s dinghy in [[deck|on deck]], and sailed with a morning land-wind" (017-026). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] departed with fond memories of the city's hospitality, his vessel refitted and himself refreshed for the passage around [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope.

The next leg would take him past the southern tip of Africa — a miles[[tonnage|ton]]e that brought him into [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and, symbolically, onto the homeward stretch of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Significance

The [[durban|Durban]] visit was significant for several reasons. First, it demonstrated the growing fame of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage — the newspaper coverage, the yacht club hospitality, and the visit from [[pretoria|Pretoria]] all showed that news of the single-handed [[voyage|circumnavigation]] had preceded him. Second, the flat-earth episode provided one of the voyage's most memorable anecdotes, illustrating the intellectual climate of the Boer republics on the eve of the [[south-africa|South Africa]]n War. Finally, [[durban|Durban]] marked the last major port of call before [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rounded [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope and entered Atlantic waters — the psychological threshold of the homeward leg.
