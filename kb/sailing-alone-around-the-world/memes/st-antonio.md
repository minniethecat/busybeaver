## Geography

St. Antonio (Santo Antão) is the northwesternmost island of [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde archipelago, located in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean approximately 600 kilometers off the coast of West Africa. The island is of volcanic origin and is characterized by high, rugged terrain that creates blustery, squally conditions in the surrounding waters. It lies near the major [[sail|sailing]] routes between Europe, Africa, and South America.

## Slocum's Navigation Without Longitude

One of the most remarkable aspects of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage was his reliance on [[navigation|dead reckoning]] rather than chronometer-based longitude observations. Early in his preparations, he had questioned the modern dependence on instruments: "In our newfangled notions of navigation it is supposed that a mariner cannot find his way without one; and I had myself [[current|drift]]ed into this way of thinking." (002-009) He dispensed with a chronometer, choosing instead to navigate by the ancient arts of latitude observation and [[navigation|dead reckoning]].

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later explained his method: "I think I have already stated that I kept my longitude, at least, mostly by intuition." (011-024) He used [[sextant|lunar observation]]s — the "lunarian" method — to verify his position when the moon was visible, finding profound satisfaction in this traditional art: "The work of the lunarian, though seldom practised in these days of chronometers, is beautifully edifying, and there is nothing in the realm of navigation that lifts one's heart up more in adoration." (011-027)

## In Slocum's Voyage

After passing through the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed southward toward [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands. On September 10, 1895, [[spray|the Spray]] made an accurate landfall at St. Antonio:

> On September 10 the Spray passed the island of St. Antonio, the northwesternmost of the Cape Verdes, close aboard. The landfall was wonderfully true, considering that no observations for longitude had been made. (005-014)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s pride in the accuracy of his navigation is evident. Without a chronometer, he had steered across hundreds of miles of open ocean and made landfall precisely where expected — a validation of his self-reliant approach to seamanship. As [[spray|the Spray]] drew past the island, the conditions were challenging:

> The wind, northeast, as the sloop drew by the island, was very squally, but I reefed her sails snug, and steered broad from the highland of blustering St. Antonio. (005-014)

Having cleared [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] entered the open South Atlantic, alone once more:

> Then leaving the Cape Verde Islands out of sight astern, I found myself once more sailing a lonely sea and in a solitude supreme all around. When I slept I dreamed that I was alone. This feeling never left me; but, sleeping or waking, I seemed always to know the position of the sloop, and I saw my vessel moving across the chart, which became a picture before me. (005-014)

This passage is one of the most evocative in the entire narrative — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s sense of solitude had become so profound that it penetrated even his dreams, yet his navigational awareness never left him.

## The South Atlantic Passage

After St. Antonio, [[spray|the Spray]] ran southward through the [[trade-wind|trade winds]]. The sloop was "rapidly drawing toward the region of doldrums, and the force of the trade-winds was lessening." (005-017) On September 23, the schooner Nantasket of [[boston|Boston]] came up with [[spray|the Spray]], and the passing hail between vessels was a brief human connection in the vastness. (005-019)

By September 30, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] crossed the equator at longitude 29°30' W, just over two weeks after passing St. Antonio. (005-023) The crossing was a milestone in more senses than one — the solitary circumnavigator was now in the Southern Hemisphere, [[sail|sailing]] toward the coast of Brazil and, beyond it, the dreaded [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]].

## Historical Context

The [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]] were a Portuguese colony and an important waypoint for [[sail|sailing]] vessels making the passage from Europe to South America or around [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed the islands without landing, intent on making southing toward [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]. His route choice — passing close to St. Antonio rather than the more southern islands of the archipelago — was a deliberate navigational decision to verify his position before committing to the long South Atlantic crossing.

St. Antonio lies on the same route followed by the old circumnavigators. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was conscious of this lineage: "I tried to compare my state with that of old circumnavigators, who sailed exactly over the route which I took from [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]] or farther back to this point and beyond, but there was no comparison." (014-019) His solitude, his small vessel, and his reliance on traditional navigation all [[current|set]] him apart from his predecessors.

## Significance

The passage past St. Antonio marks [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s last sight of land before the long South Atlantic crossing to the coast of South America. His pride in the "wonderfully true" landfall without longitude observations reflects the self-reliant seamanship that characterized the entire voyage. (005-014) The passage from St. Antonio into the "solitude supreme" of the South Atlantic [[current|set]]s the stage for one of the loneliest stretches of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — a solitude so complete it followed him even into his dreams.
