**Reefing** is the seamanship practice of reducing a sail's exposed area by partially lowering it and securing the unused portion. Reefing allows a vessel to carry the appropriate amount of sail for the wind strength—enough to maintain control, not so much that the vessel is over-[[sail|canvas]]sed and dangerous. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[reef|reefing]] was a constant, often exhausting task that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] performed alone, sometimes in appalling conditions.

## Definition

To reef a sail, the halyard is eased, lowering the sail partially. The excess cloth is then gathered along the foot or luff and tied down using **reef-points**—short lines sewn into the sail at intervals. Multiple rows of reef-points allow progressive reduction: first reef (small reduction), second reef (moderate), third reef or "close-reefed" (deep reduction for survival conditions). A **double-reefed mainsail** means two rows of reef-points are tied, leaving roughly half the full sail area exposed.

Reefing the jib is more awkward because it requires going forward to the bowsprit—the crew must work at the extreme end of the vessel, often while green water comes over the bow. The practice of "[[reef|reefing]] and sweating it up" means tightening the [[reef|reefed sail]] using body weight on the halyard ("sweating") to get it properly [[current|set]].

## Nautical Context

The old sailor's adage—"If you're thinking about [[reef|reefing]], it's time to reef"—reflects hard-won experience. It is always easier to shake out a reef (remove it) than to put one in once the wind has built. Delaying a reef risks being caught with too much sail in a sudden squall, which can dismast or capsize a vessel.

For the solo sailor, [[reef|reefing]] is particularly hazardous because no one remains at the [[rudder|helm]] while the sailor goes forward. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] relied on the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s exceptional balance—she would hold her course for extended periods with the [[rudder|helm]] lashed, giving him time to go forward and reef the jib.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Reefing is one of the most frequent actions in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative, a ritual of survival performed in every ocean.

Early in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] confronted the fundamental choice:

> "I was not long in reefing the mainsail and sweating it up; perhaps it was done more than an hour before it was necessary" (005-003)

This was the mark of seamanship: reef early, before the squall. His prompt action saved the jib [[halyard|halyards]] from strain.

In the rough waters off [[patagonia|Patagonia]], [[reef|reefing]] became constant:

> "My attention was turned to reefing sails, and no time was to be lost about it, for the wind was puffy and the sky looked ugly" (005-008)

The most dangerous [[reef|reefing]] moment came at the bowsprit in rough seas. In the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]:

> "While I was at the end of the bowsprit reefing the jib, she ducked me under three times" (018-003)

Each time the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s bow plunged into a wave, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was submerged while clinging to the bowsprit—[[reef|reefing]] the jib in conditions that would have kept most crews below [[deck|decks]].

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s preferred heavy-weather configuration was specific: "double-reefed mainsail and [[jib|whole jib]]" (004-003). This combination balanced the *[[spray|Spray]]*—a shortened mainsail reduced weather [[rudder|helm]] while the full jib kept her from falling off. He also reflected, with characteristic understatement: "I regretted that I had not stepped the mast instead" (006-021) while hanging from the bowsprit.

Reefing was not always enough. When conditions overwhelmed even a deeply [[reef|reefed sail]], the only option was to heave-to under bare poles or deploy the [[anchor|sea-anchor]].
