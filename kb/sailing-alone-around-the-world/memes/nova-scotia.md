**Nova Scotia** is a maritime province on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast of Canada, comprising a peninsula connected to New Brunswick and Cape Breton Island. It was the birthplace of [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] and the formative landscape of his early life as a sailor. The province's deep seafaring tradition — fishing, shipbuilding, and transatlantic trade — produced generations of mariners, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was among its most celebrated.

## Slocum's Homeland

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] opens his narrative with an affectionate description of his native province:

"In the fair land of Nova Scotia, a maritime province, there is a ridge called North Mountain, overlooking the [[bay-of-fundy|Bay of Fundy]] on one side and the fertile Annapolis valley on the other. On the northern slope of the range grows the hardy spruce-tree, well adapted for ship-timbers, of which many vessels of all classes have been built" (001-002).

He was born on North Mountain on February 20, 1844, into a family of sailors: "On both sides my family were sailors; and if any [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] should be found not seafaring, he will show at least an inclination to whittle models of boats and contemplate voyages" (001-002). Though he later became a naturalized citizen of the United States, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] acknowledged Nova Scotia as fundamental to his identity.

## The Return to Home Waters

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s shakedown cruise in 1895 brought [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] back to the Nova Scotia coast he had known since boyhood. After departing [[gloucester|Gloucester]], he made "the run from [[gloucester|Gloucester]] to Nova Scotia — a shaking up in home waters" (002-001). The vessel put into [[westport|Westport]] harbor, where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "had spent eight years of my life as a lad" (002-013). This return to the scenes of his youth, aboard a vessel he had rebuilt with his own hands, was [[cargo|freight]]ed with personal meaning.

> The *Spray* sailed directly over the southwest ledge through the worst tide-race in the Bay of Fundy, and got into Westport harbor in Nova Scotia, where I had spent eight years of my life as a lad.
>
> (002-013)

As the *[[spray|Spray]]* headed to sea for [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing, a schooner "came out of Liverpool, Nova Scotia" (003-004) — the harbor town on the province's south shore — one of the last familiar sights before the open ocean.

## Maritime Culture

Nova Scotia in the nineteenth century was a cradle of Atlantic seafaring. Its shipyards built vessels for the global carrying trade, its fishermen worked the Grand Banks, and its merchant captains sailed routes spanning the world's oceans. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s description of Nova Scotians as "hardy, robust, and strong" and "disposed to compete in the world's commerce" (001-002) reflects both pride and accuracy. The province produced a disproportionate share of the era's master mariners, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — the first man to sail alone [[voyage|around the world]] — stands as the ultimate expression of that culture.
