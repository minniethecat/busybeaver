# Boston

Boston was both the beginning and the end of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s great voyage. He had been "cast up from ocean in the chill of winter" onto the streets of this city, and it was here, in the early 1890s, that he refitted the old oyster sloop that would become the *[[spray|Spray]]*. The city that was the cradle of American maritime enterprise became the cradle of the first solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Geography

Boston lies on a natural harbor where the Charles River meets [[massachusetts-bay|Massachusetts Bay]]. In the 1890s, it was one of the busiest ports on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] seaboard — a center of shipbuilding, trade, and fishing. Its wharves had seen clipper ships, whalers, and fishing schooners come and go. The waterfront was rough-hewn, practical, and steeped in salt.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s connection to Boston runs through the entire narrative. "One midwinter day of 1892, in Boston, where I had been cast up from ocean in the chill of winter" — (001-006), his journey from shipwrecked sailor to circumnavigator began. He found the *[[spray|Spray]]* in nearby [[fairhaven|Fairhaven]], but it was in Boston's harbor that she was moored and from which she departed.

The preparations for the voyage drew on Boston's maritime community. "A great lantern, and a lady in Boston sent me the price of a large and much needed stove" — (002-009). There was even an unexpected encounter at sea: a passing captain "knew John Wilson of Boston" — (003-020) and, incredulous, asked [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] if he knew him too.

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s Atlantic route was repeatedly described as spanning "Boston to [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]" — (003-021) — a phrase that became a refrain in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s telling. And when the voyage ended, she returned "more than when I sailed from Boston" — (021-016), having circumnavigated the globe and arrived back "in better condition than when she sailed from Boston on her long voyage" — (021-017).

> One midwinter day of 1892, in Boston, where I had been cast up from old ocean, so to speak, a year or two before, I was cogitating whether I should apply for a command, and again eat my bread and butter on the sea, or go to work at the shipyard, when I met an old acquaintance, a whaling-captain, who said: "Come to Fairhaven and I'll give you a ship. But," he added, "she wants some repairs." The captain's terms, when fully explained, were more than satisfactory to me. They included all the assistance I would require to fit the craft for sea. I was only too glad to accept, for I had already found that I could not obtain work in the shipyard without first paying fifty dollars to a society, and as for a ship to command—there were not enough ships to go round. Nearly all our tall vessels had been cut down for coal-barges, and were being ignominiously towed by the nose from port to port, while many worthy captains addressed themselves to Sailors' Snug Harbor.
>
> (001-006)

## Historical Context

Boston in the 1890s was a city in transition between its golden age of sail and the emerging age of steam. Its maritime traditions were still alive — the Boston pilot, the Boston-built schooner, the Boston lady who took interest in a lone sailor's voyage — but the clipper ships were fading. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s *[[spray|Spray]]*, a rebuilt oyster sloop, was a throwback to an earlier era, and his departure from Boston was the opening chapter of a voyage that would prove sail still had stories to tell.
