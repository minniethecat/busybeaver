# Captain James Cook

Captain James Cook (1728–1779) was a British explorer, navigator, and cartographer who made three voyages to [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] Ocean, charting many areas and recording islands and coastlines on European maps for the first time. [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] reflected on Cook's legacy when he visited [[cooktown|Cooktown, Queensland]] — named after the great navigator — during his own Pacific passage.

## Biography

James Cook joined the Royal Navy and rose through the ranks to become one of history's greatest maritime explorers. His three Pacific voyages (1768–1779) mapped vast stretches of [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], including the eastern coastline of [[australia|Australia]], New Zealand, and the Hawaiian Islands. His ship HMS *Endeavour* famously ran aground on the [[great-barrier-reef|Great Barrier Reef]] in 1770 and was repaired at the site of what would become [[cooktown|Cooktown]] (015-011).

Cook was killed in Hawaii on February 14, 1779, during a confrontation with native Hawaiians.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounter with Cook's legacy came at [[cooktown|Cooktown]], where he docked the *[[spray|Spray]]* "nearly abreast the Captain Cook monument" and "went ashore to feast my eyes on the very stones the great navigator had seen, for I was now on a seaman's consecrated ground" (015-011).

The visit prompted a classic [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anecdote:

> "But there seemed a question in Cooktown's mind as to the exact spot where his ship, the *Endeavor*, hove down for repairs on her memorable voyage around the world. Some said it was not at all at the place where the monument now stood." — (015-011)

When a young lady asked [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s opinion as "one of some authority in nautical matters," he responded with characteristic whimsy: "I could see no reason why Captain Cook, if he made up his mind to repair his ship inland, couldn't have dredged out a channel to the place where the monument now stood, if he had a dredging-machine with him" (015-011).

The lady then asked if [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had visited the spot further down the harbor where Cook was murdered. A schoolboy intervened to correct her:

> "'Captain Cook wasn't murdered 'ere at all, ma'am; 'e was killed in Hafrica: a lion et 'im.'" — (015-011)

## Significance

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit to [[cooktown|Cooktown]] places his own [[voyage|circumnavigation]] within the broader tradition of Pacific exploration. Though [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed alone in a small sloop while Cook commanded Royal Navy expeditions, both men navigated the same waters and faced the same [[reefing|reef]]s. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reverence for Cook — calling [[cooktown|Cooktown]] "a seaman's consecrated ground" — acknowledges his place in this lineage.

The humorous schoolboy anecdote also reveals [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s wry appreciation for how history becomes garbled in popular memory, a theme that runs throughout his narrative.
