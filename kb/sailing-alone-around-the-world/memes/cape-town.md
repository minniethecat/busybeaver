# Cape Town

Cape Town was [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s longest rest stop of the voyage — three months ashore, a railway journey to the Transvaal, dinners in town, and a thorough preparation of the *[[spray|Spray]]* for [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing. It was also a place where he sensed the gathering clouds of war, and where his little sloop, moored in Table Bay, attracted the curiosity of Cape society.

## Geography

Cape Town lies at the foot of Table Mountain, on the shore of Table Bay, near the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]]. It was the principal port of South Africa in the 1890s — a way station on the route to India and the East, and a city of growing colonial confidence. The harbor was busy with steamers and [[sail|sailing]] ships, and the town itself was a mix of Dutch, British, and African influences.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived intending to rest the *[[spray|Spray]]* — and himself. He "shipped for a three months' rest at Cape Town" — (018-001), the longest pause since the voyage began. The sloop lay "in the bay off the city of Cape Town, where she remained a day" — (018-007) before being brought to a secure mooring.

During the stay, he made a railway journey inland. "A railway trip to the Transvaal" — (018-001) took him into the Boer republic, where tensions with Britain were already rising. He observed the political climate with a sailor's detachment, but he noticed the signs: "Many had told me at Cape Town that, in their opinion, war was" — (020-004) imminent. The Boer War would break out within two years.

Social life ashore was pleasant. "Again at Cape Town, while dining out one day, I" — (019-015) records another convivial evening. A Scots lady he had met earlier in the voyage reappeared: a gift from "a Scots lady at Cape Town long after named 'Bruce'" — (009-006) was a reminder that the voyage had created connections across the hemispheres.

Even General Roberts, the British military hero, made an appearance in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s thoughts: "as General Roberts had done at Cape Town, a short commentary on the voyage" — (021-002).

> The steam-tug *Alert*, then out looking for ships, came to the *Spray* off the Lion's Rump, and in lieu of a larger ship towed her into port. The sea being smooth, she came to anchor in the bay off the city of Cape Town, where she remained a day, simply to rest clear of the bustle of commerce.
>
> (018-007)

## Historical Context

Cape Town in the mid-1890s was the capital of [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Colony and the economic hub of southern Africa. The discovery of gold in the Transvaal had transformed the region, and the political tensions between Boers and British were intensifying. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s three-month stay coincided with a period of relative calm before the [[gale|storm]] — the Jameson Raid of 1895 had already occurred, and the Second Boer War was on the horizon. For a visiting American sailor, Cape Town offered rest, society, and a front-row seat to history in the making.
