**Port** is the left-hand side of a vessel when facing forward (toward the bow). Its counterpart is [[starboard]] ([[rigging|rig]]ht). The port side is also the traditional docking side—the reason the term "port" was adopted in the first place.

## Definition

The term "port" replaced the older "larboard" in the nineteenth century, because "larboard" and "starboard" sounded too similar and caused confusion in shouted commands at sea. The new term "port" was chosen because the left side of the vessel was the side that faced the dock (port) when the [[helm|steering]] board on the [[rigging|rig]]ht side had to be kept clear.

In modern nautical usage, port is one half of the fundamental left-[[rigging|rig]]ht directional pair (port/starboard), with the companion terms **port bow** (left-forward quadrant), **port beam** (directly left), and **port quarter** (left-aft quadrant). A vessel on **port tack** has the wind coming over its port (left) side; under COLREGs, a port-tack vessel must give way to a starboard-tack vessel.

## Nautical Context

Port tack is the "give-way" tack under the rules of the road: "port tack gives way to [[starboard|starboard tack]]." Every sailor learns this as a fundamental safety rule. The split-second decision about which vessel is on which tack can prevent collisions at sea.

The preference for port-side docking persists in modern practice. Most vessels are designed with boarding gates, gangways, and shell doors on the port side. Marina berths are typically approached port-side-to. The historical reason—protecting the [[helm|steering]] board—is long obsolete, but the custom has become embedded in vessel design and port infrastructure.

Port and starboard are also critical in describing [[sail|sailing]] maneuvers. "Coming about on the port tack" means the vessel turns through the wind and [[current|set]]tles on a course with the wind coming from the left. "[[jib|Jib]]ing to port" means the mainsail crosses to the port side, a maneuver that requires careful control to avoid an uncontrolled crash-jibe.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used port references throughout the book with a professional mariner's consistency, though "port" in his text serves double duty—sometimes referring to the side of the vessel, sometimes to a harbor (as in "port of call"). This dual meaning is intrinsic to nautical English.

The voyage began on port tack. Departing from [[boston|Boston]] Harbor, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded: "was made up the harbor on the port tack, then coming about she stood out to sea" (002-002). The port tack took him across the harbor, then a tack change (coming about) brought him onto [[starboard|starboard tack]] to clear the harbor entrance.

Near the end of the voyage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered a French bark on the final Atlantic crossing: "a French bark on the port tack, making for Cayenne, hove in sight" (020-007). The French vessel was headed south toward the South American coast, while [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was northbound for home.

In the final chapters, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the approach to a harbor entrance near the end of his voyage:

> "I hove the sloop round from starboard to port tack, to make for a quiet harbor" (021-008)

This shift from starboard to port tack—swinging the vessel's head across the wind—was the maneuver that brought the *[[spray|Spray]]* onto her final approach to [[newport|Newport]], completing the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

The term "port" has additional significance in the book's narrative structure. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] uses "port" in its harbor sense dozens of times—each arrival at a new port marks the completion of a passage. From [[westport|Westport]], [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], where he took shelter after the first Atlantic gale, to [[fayal|Fayal]] in the [[azores|Azores]], [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]], [[montevideo|Montevideo]], [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], [[samoa|Samoa]], and dozens of other ports—each one is a milestone in the voyage. The port-as-harbor and port-as-direction share the same word because they share the same origin: the side of the ship that faced the dock.
