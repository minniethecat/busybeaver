# Hesperus

The *Hesperus* is a literary vessel — the doomed schooner of Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's 1842 ballad "The Wreck of the Hesperus." Her [[reefing|reef]], Norman's Woe, lies off the coast of [[gloucester|Gloucester, Massachusetts]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], departing on his [[voyage|circumnavigation]], passed close aboard and noted it: "the [[reefing|reef]] where the schooner *Hesperus* struck I passed close aboard" (002-005). The line is brief but resonant — a nod to the maritime literature that accompanied [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s practical seamanship.

## The Poem

Longfellow's poem tells the story of a skipper who, ignoring the warnings of an old sailor, takes his schooner to sea in a winter [[gale|storm]] with his young daughter aboard. The vessel is wrecked on Norman's Woe, and at dawn, a fisherman finds the daughter's body lashed to the mast — an image that became one of the most famous in American poetry.

## In Slocum's Narrative

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reference to the *Hesperus* is casual but meaningful. He was departing [[gloucester|Gloucester]], the port that sent fishing schooners to the Grand Banks in all weathers. The *Hesperus* was a local legend — every [[gloucester|Gloucester]] sailor knew the poem and the [[reefing|reef]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s "I passed close aboard" is a sailor's acknowledgment: *I know the story, I know the [[reefing|reef]], and I am [[sail|sailing]] past it into the open sea*.

## Legacy

The *Hesperus* represents the literary dimension of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage — the books and poems that sailed with him as surely as his provisions. The *[[spray|Spray]]* carried a library, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] read at sea constantly. Longfellow's schooner was part of that library, and its [[reefing|reef]] was the last literary landmark before [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]].

> I heard the clanking of the dismal bell on Norman's Woe as we went by; and the reef where the schooner Hesperus struck I passed close aboard.
>
> (002-005)
