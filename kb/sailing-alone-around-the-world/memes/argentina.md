# Argentina

Argentina appears in Slocum's narrative not as a destination but as a presence — naval, military, and human — encountered during his arduous passage through the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]] and his subsequent Pacific crossing.

## In the Voyage

Slocum's first encounter with Argentina came at Port Angosto, during his repeated attempts to clear the strait. The Chilean gunboat *Condor* and the **Argentine cruiser *Azopardo*** arrived in port. Captain Mascarella of the *Azopardo* sent a boat to the *Spray* offering to tow Slocum to Sandy Point — if he would abandon his voyage and return. Slocum, for whom giving up was "the thing farthest from my mind," politely declined. (010-006)

The officers of the *Azopardo* told Slocum they had seen the outlaw Black Pedro in the strait and learned he had visited the *Spray*. Though they blamed Slocum for not shooting the man, the *Azopardo* — as a foreign man-of-war — had no authority to arrest the Fuegian. (010-006)

> As the days went by, the season became more favorable for a chance to clear the strait with a fair wind, and so I made up my mind after six attempts, being driven back each time, to be in no further haste to sail. The bad weather on my last return to Port Angosto for shelter brought the Chilean gunboat *Condor* and the Argentine cruiser *Azopardo* into port. As soon as the latter came to anchor, Captain Mascarella, the commander, sent a boat to the *Spray* with the message that he would take me in tow to Sandy Point, if I gave up the voyage and returned to duty, meaning to a navy ship. The thing farthest from my mind was to give up the voyage, and I did not feel that I was returning to duty, but to the dearest, most delightful of all duties, my wife and children. And I directed the good friend who brought the message to thank Captain Mascarella for his kind offer and to say that I would not give up the voyage.
>
> (010-006)

## Encounters

At Port Angosto, Slocum met Professor Dusen of a Swedish scientific expedition to South America and the Pacific Islands. The professor's **Argentine cook** pronounced the water at a particular brook "*muy rico*" (very delicious), and Dusen's camp included **three well-armed Argentines** to defend against possible attacks from savages. Slocum noted wryly that they seemed disgusted when he filled his water at a smaller stream rather than following their advice. (010-009)

## Geopolitical Context

Later, at [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez]], Slocum learned from the islanders of **a war between Chile and the Argentine** — news that had not reached him during his time in either country. In a characteristic touch of humor, he responded by telling the islanders that according to the latest reports, their own island had sunk — a rumor that, remarkably, had also reached Australia when he arrived there months later. (010-022)

## Significance

Argentina represents the geopolitical undercurrent of Slocum's voyage. Though he sailed alone, he moved through waters shaped by national rivalries — Chile and Argentina, naval powers and outlaws, scientific expeditions and military patrols. The offer of a tow from the *Azopardo* was both genuine assistance and a reminder that the solo sailor was never entirely free of the world's claims.
