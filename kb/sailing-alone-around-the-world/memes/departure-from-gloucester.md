The **Departure from Gloucester** on May 7, 1895, was the moment the *[[spray|Spray]]* left the American coast to begin her transatlantic crossing. After fitting out at [[gloucester|Gloucester]] with fishermen's stores — dry cod, a barrel of oil, a "fisherman's own" lantern — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed out of the cove to a send-off from factory workers waving handkerchiefs and caps. It was the first departure into the open ocean of a voyage that would last over three years (002-011).

## Summary

The *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived at [[gloucester|Gloucester]] on April 24, 1895, just hours after departing [[boston|Boston]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] spent about two weeks there, fitting out with provisions donated by local fishermen and the wharf owners who took "a great interest in the voyage." On May 7, with the weather mild and a light southwest wind, the *[[spray|Spray]]* stood out of the cove past Eastern Point, receiving a "hearty salute — the last of many kindnesses to her at [[gloucester|Gloucester]]" (002-011).

## Context

After the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s first trial run from [[boston|Boston]] to [[gloucester|Gloucester]] — a day of exhilarating [[sail|sailing]] during which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] felt "a thrilling pulse beat high in me" — [[sloop|the sloop]] lay at [[gloucester|Gloucester]] for refitting. The local fishermen contributed dry cod, a barrel of oil "to calm the waves," a gaff, a pugh, a dip-net, and a lantern that "would throw a light a great distance round" (002-003). The wharf owners, "old skippers themselves," also provided a case of copper paint.

## Narrative

The day of departure was mild, and the departure itself was unexpectedly emotional:

> "The weather was mild on the day of my departure from Gloucester. On the point ahead, as the Spray stood out of the cove, was a lively picture, for the front of a tall factory was a flutter of handkerchiefs and caps. Pretty faces peered out of the windows from the top to the bottom of the building, all smiling bon voyage." — (002-011)

Some hailed him to ask "where away and why alone." [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made as if to stand in, and "a hundred pairs of arms reached out, and said come, but the shore was dangerous!" (002-011).

The *[[spray|Spray]]* worked out of the bay against the light wind and by noon had squared away off Eastern Point. The wind freshened, and [[sloop|the sloop]] was soon past Thatcher's Island lights, [[course|heading]] east. That night, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] lay down for "the first time at sea alone, not to sleep, but to doze and to dream" — dreaming of a whale towing the *[[spray|Spray]]*, until a brewing [[gale|storm]] woke him (002-012).

## Aftermath

The departure from [[gloucester|Gloucester]] marked the true beginning of the [[voyage|ocean voyage]]. The *[[spray|Spray]]* coasted along [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] visited old schoolmates at [[westport|Westport]], before rounding [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]] and [[course|heading]] into the open Atlantic. Eighteen days from [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* made landfall at [[fayal|Fayal]] in the [[azores|Azores]] — the first ocean crossing of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] (003-019).

## Significance

The [[gloucester|Gloucester]] departure is the moment the *[[spray|Spray]]* ceased to be a coastal cruiser and became an ocean-going vessel. The send-off from the factory workers — the last sight of home — and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first night alone at sea mark the emotional threshold of the voyage. [[gloucester|Gloucester]]'s fishermen, who equipped the *[[spray|Spray]]* with practical gifts, represented the maritime community's quiet investment in the venture.
