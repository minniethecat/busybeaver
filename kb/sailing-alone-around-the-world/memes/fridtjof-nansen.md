# Fridtjof Nansen

Fridtjof Nansen (1861–1930) was a Norwegian explorer, scientist, and humanitarian whose Arctic expeditions — particularly his attempt to reach the North Pole — made him one of the most admired figures of his time. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], navigating the southern oceans while Nansen pushed into the polar ice, was aware of the parallel: two solitary enterprises at opposite ends of the Earth. (002-015)

## Biography

Born near Oslo, Nansen first made his name by crossing Greenland on skis in 1888. His most famous expedition (1893–1896) involved deliberately freezing his ship *[[fram|Fram]]* into the Arctic ice and [[current|drift]]ing toward the North Pole — a bold plan that succeeded in reaching farther north than any previous expedition, though not the pole itself. He later became a Nobel Peace Prize laureate for his work with refugees.

## Role in the Voyage

Nansen appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative as a contemporary explorer whose achievements provided context for his own. While Nansen battled ice and polar bears in the Arctic, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] battled [[gale|storm]]s and solitude in the southern oceans. Both men were pushing the boundaries of what one person could accomplish against nature. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], who read newspapers and books at every port, was certainly aware of Nansen's exploits.

> It was the 13th of the month, and 13 is my lucky number—a fact registered long before Dr. Nansen sailed in search of the north pole with his crew of thirteen.
>
> (002-015)

## Significance

Nansen and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] represent two faces of the same [[victoria|Victoria]]n impulse: the drive to go farther, alone, into the unknown. The Arctic explorer and the solo circumnavigator, each in his own element, each proving that individual human effort could still achieve what had once required great expeditions.
