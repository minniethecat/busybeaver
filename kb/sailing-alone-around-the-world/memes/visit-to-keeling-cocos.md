After a careful twenty-three-day passage from [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at the [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos atoll on July 17, 1897, and spent over three weeks among the thousand inhabitants of this remote coral paradise. His stay included cleaning and painting [[spray|the Spray]], observing the unique society established by the Ross family, and a near-disastrous boating accident that nearly cost him his life.

## Summary

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed [[spray|the Spray]] from [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] ([[torres-strait|Torres Strait]]) to the [[keel|Keel]]ing [[keeling-islands|Cocos Islands]], covering 2,700 miles in twenty-three days with only three hours of actual [[helm|steering]] thanks to [[sloop|the sloop]]'s self-[[helm|steering]] capabilities. He arrived on July 17, 1897, and anchored off the harbor landing. The atoll — home to about a thousand souls under the benevolent governance of the Ross family — welcomed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] with open arms. He hauled [[spray|the Spray]] ashore for cleaning and painting, attended a dance aboard H.M.S. *Iphegenia*, and nearly drowned in a small-boat accident before departing on August 22 for [[rodriguez-island|Rodriguez]]. (016-001) (016-002) (016-025)

## Context

[[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos, a coral atoll lying between 11°50′ and 12°12′ S and 96°51′ and 96°58′ E, was discovered in 1608–9 by Captain William [[keel|Keel]]ing of the East India Company and later [[current|set]]tled by Captain John Clunis-Ross in the early nineteenth century (016-005) (016-006). By the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit, the atoll was a dependency of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]]s Settlements, governed from Singapore, with the Ross family acting as resident administrators. The islands had famously been contested between Ross and one Alexander Hare, who had established a rival [[current|set]]tlement of Malay women on a neighboring islet before being driven out (016-008) (016-009).

The voyage from [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] had been [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s longest open-ocean passage since leaving South America, and he approached the small atoll with particular care, knowing that missing it would mean [[current|drift]]ing aimlessly in the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] (016-002).

## Narrative

The approach to [[keel|Keel]]ing required extraordinary precision. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had to navigate 550 miles from [[christmas-island|Christmas Island]] with extreme care, "keeping a [[course|true course]] else I would miss the atoll" (016-002). The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s legendary self-[[helm|steering]] carried her the bulk of the distance:

> "I didn't touch the helm, for with the current and heave of the sea the sloop found herself at the end of the run absolutely in the fairway of the channel. You couldn't have beaten it in the navy!" — (016-004)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] trimmed the [[sail|sails]] and brought the *[[spray|Spray]]* up the couple of miles to the harbor landing, casting anchor at 3:30 p.m. on July 17 (016-004).

His first impression upon landing was the extraordinary welcome:

> "The children have all come to welcome you," explained Mr. Ross, as they mustered at the jetty by hundreds, of all ages and sizes. — (016-010)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found the islanders hospitable, noting their musical greeting of "Are you walking?" ("Jalan, jalan?") — a custom he found charming (016-010). He described the atmosphere as idyllic:

> "If there is a paradise on this earth it is Keeling." — (016-020)

During his stay, H.M.S. *Iphegenia* arrived on July 22, carrying Justice Andrew J. Leech and court officers on a circuit of inspection (016-020). Finding the *[[spray|Spray]]* hauled ashore and tied to a coconut tree, and no grievances to try, the occasion was celebrated with a dance in the governor's great hall (016-020).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] hauled his vessel ashore, cleaned and painted her bottom, and observed the community's skilled boatbuilding — "by far the best workmanship in boat-building I saw on the voyage was here" (016-022).

## Aftermath

Just before departure, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] nearly lost his life in a small-boat accident within sight of the village, when he carelessly loaded heavy tridacna shells into a boat and went a[[current|drift]] (016-023). He reflected ruefully that he had "sailed over oceans" and "sailed round the whole world without so nearly meeting a watery end" as in that small boat in the calm lagoon (016-023).

On August 22, the *[[spray|Spray]]* departed [[keel|Keel]]ing, clearing the "flashing [[reefing|reef]]s" and [[course|heading]] for [[rodriguez-island|Rodriguez]], 1,900 miles distant:

> "Long before dark Keeling Cocos, with its thousand souls, as sinless in their lives as perhaps it is possible for frail mortals to be, was left out of sight, astern." — (016-025)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would later reflect on his [[keel|Keel]]ing visit as one of the most memorable and pleasant interludes of the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Significance

The [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos visit marked [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s entry into the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] phase of his voyage and demonstrated his growing confidence as a single-handed navigator — covering 2,700 miles with virtually no [[rudder|helm]]-tending. The stay also provided one of the voyage's most vivid ethnographic portraits, with [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s description of the island society — particularly its children, its justice system (or lack of need for one), and its boatbuilding craft — offering a unique window into a remote colonial outpost at the close of the nineteenth century.
