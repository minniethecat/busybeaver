# Pirate Chase off Gibraltar

On August 25, 1895, the day the *[[Spray]]* sailed from [[Gibraltar]], a Moorish pirate felucca pursued the little sloop along the African coast. The chase — which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] escaped through quick seamanship and a rising [[gale]] — was a turning point in the voyage: it convinced him to abandon the [[suez|Suez Canal]] route through [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] and instead sail westward across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] to round [[Cape Horn]]. The decision, made in the immediate aftermath of the chase, transformed the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] from a relatively sheltered passage into one of the great sea adventures of the era.

## Summary

The *[[spray|Spray]]* departed [[Gibraltar]] on August 25, 1895, towed clear of the harbor by a British tug. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s plan was to sail east through [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] to the [[suez|Suez Canal]] and then across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. Almost immediately after clearing the harbor, a felucca — a fast, lateen-[[rigging|rig]]ged vessel common on the North African coast — gave chase. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] identified the crew as "the sons of generations of pirates" (005-003). The felucca closed rapidly, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could see "the tuft of hair on the heads of the crew — by which, it is said, Mohammed will pull the villains up into heaven" (005-003). Through a combination of quick sail-handling, a rising gale, and the cover of darkness, the *[[spray|Spray]]* escaped. The chase lasted only a few hours but its consequences reshaped the entire voyage.

## Context

[[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] in the 1890s was a British fortress and naval base, but the waters just beyond — the [[morocco|Moroccan coast]] and the approaches to [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] — were notorious for piracy. Moorish pirates operated from the North African coast, preying on small vessels that strayed too close to shore. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a seasoned merchant captain, knew the danger but had not anticipated being chased on his first day out of port.

His original plan — "a direct course to reach the [[suez|Suez Canal]]" (005-001) — would have taken him through the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], another region infamous for piracy. The chase crystallized a realization: a single-handed sailor in a small, slow sloop was a vulnerable target in pirate waters. The alternative — the western route across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] — was physically far more dangerous but involved no human predators. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] chose the sea over the pirates.

## Narrative

The chase began within hours of the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s departure from [[Gibraltar]]. A tug belonging to her Majesty towed [[sloop|the sloop]] into open water, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[current|set]] sail. Almost immediately, a felucca bore down on him:

> "I was not long in reefing the mainsail and sweating it up — probably not more than fifteen minutes; but the felucca had in the meantime so shortened the distance between us that I now saw the tuft of hair on the heads of the crew — by which, it is said, Mohammed will pull the villains up into heaven — and they were coming on like the wind." — (005-003)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s response was professional and rapid. He [[reefing|reefed]] the mainsail, [[current|set]] the jib and fore[[forestaysail|staysail]], and pointed the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s head offshore. As darkness fell, he made a critical tactical decision: "I hauled [[sloop|the sloop]]'s head two points offshore to allow for the [[current|set]] of current and heavy rollers toward the land. This gave me the wind three points on the [[starboard|starboard quarter]] and a steady pull in the head[[sail|sails]]" (005-004). The felucca, unable to match the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s offshore [[course|heading]] in the building sea, fell away in the darkness.

> "By the time I had things in this order it was dark, and a heavy sea was running, and I was glad that by the sloop's good sailing she was rapidly leaving the coast behind." — (005-004)

## Aftermath

The morning after the chase, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a decision that defined the voyage: he would not sail through [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] and the [[red-sea|Red Sea]]. Instead, he would cross [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], round [[Cape Horn]], and complete the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] the long way. He framed this as a positive choice — "my determination to sail westward from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] not only enabled me to escape the pirates of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], but, in bringing me to [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], landed me on familiar shores" (005-024).

The aftermath of the chase, however, was not purely triumphant. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s boom was damaged during the escape — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would later shorten it in [[Pernambuco]] (005-029) — and the psychological impact lingered. The solo sailor had been reminded, on his very first day of the main voyage, that the sea was not the only danger.

> "The wind, for a few days following my escape from the pirates, blew a steady but moderate gale, and the sea, though agitated into long rollers, was not uncomfortably rough or dangerous, and while sitting in my cabin I could hardly realize that any sea was running at all." — (005-005)

The relief in these words is palpable. The pirate chase was brief — measured in hours, not days — but it was the closest [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] came to a human threat during the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. All the other dangers — gales, breakers, williwaws, [[reef|reefs]] — were natural ones, which a skilled seaman could face on his own terms.

## Significance

The pirate chase off [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] is one of the crucial turning points of *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. If [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had not been chased — if the felucca had not appeared, or if he had sailed a day earlier — he would have gone east, through [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] and the [[suez|Suez Canal]], and the voyage would have been shorter, safer, and far less memorable. The chase forced the western route, which led to the great Atlantic gales, the [[Strait-of-Magellan|Strait of Magellan]], [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Horn [[storm|tempest]], and the long Pacific passages that define the narrative.

The chase also reveals [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s seamanship in a moment of genuine peril. He did not panic; he did not fire a gun (he may not have had one); he simply sailed his vessel better than the pirates sailed theirs. The combination of quick [[reef|reefing]], an offshore course change, and the exploitation of darkness and [[gale|heavy weather]] was the work of a master mariner — and it [[current|set]] the [[tonnage|ton]]e for the entire voyage.
