## Geography

Fuerteventura is the easternmost island of the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]] archipelago, situated in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean approximately 100 kilometers off the coast of Africa. The island is of volcanic origin and rises to an elevation of 2,700 feet, making it visible from many leagues away in clear weather. (005-008) A navigable channel separates Fuerteventura from the African mainland, providing a passage for vessels [[sail|sailing]] southward along the African coast.

## The Route Decision

Before reaching the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had made one of the most consequential decisions of his voyage. His original plan was to transit [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]], pass through the [[suez|Suez Canal]], and proceed eastward [[voyage|around the world]]. (005-002) At [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], however, he reconsidered: intelligence about the challenges of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] route, combined with the practical difficulties of navigating [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] in a small sloop, led him to change course for [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] instead. (005-001)

This decision transformed the voyage from a relatively conventional eastward passage into the great test of seamanship for which the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] would become famous — a solo rounding of [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] against the prevailing westerlies.

## In Slocum's Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] approached the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]] after departing [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]. On the morning of September 1, 1895, after several days of unsettled weather, the islands came into view:

> September 1, in the early morning, land-clouds rising ahead told of the Canary Islands not far away. (005-008)

The weather then deteriorated rapidly, forcing [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to take immediate measures:

> A change in the weather came next day: storm-clouds stretched their arms across the sky; from the east, to all appearances, might come a fierce harmattan, or from the south might come the fierce hurricane. Every point of the compass threatened a wild storm. My attention was turned to reefing sails, and no time was to be lost over it, either, for the sea in a moment was confusion itself, and I was glad to head the sloop three points or more away from her true course that she might ride safely over the waves. (005-008)

In the midst of this [[gale|storm]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] steered for the passage between Africa and Fuerteventura:

> I was now scudding her for the channel between Africa and the island of Fuerteventura, the easternmost of the Canary Islands, for which I was on the lookout. At 2 p.m., the weather becoming suddenly fine, the island stood in view, already abeam to starboard, and not more than seven miles off. Fuerteventura is twenty-seven hundred feet high, and in fine weather is visible many leagues away. (005-008)

The sudden clearing of the weather after the violent squall is characteristic of the region. The brief window of calm allowed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to fix his position and confirm his passage through the channel. By the following day, [[spray|the Spray]] was clear of the islands, but a new gale brought reddish-brown dust from the African shore:

> It howled dismally while it lasted, and though it was not the season of the harmattan, the sea in the course of an hour was discolored with a reddish-brown dust. (005-009)

This African dust [[gale|storm]], though out of season, vividly reminded [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] of the proximity of the Sahara and the volatile weather systems it generates.

## Historical Context

The [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]] have been an important waypoint for Atlantic navigation since the age of exploration, serving as the last European outpost before the long passage to the Americas or around Africa. In [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time, they were a common provisioning stop for [[sail|sailing]] vessels. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], however, did not land — he was intent on making southing toward [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] and passed through the channel without [[anchor|anchoring]].

The channel between Fuerteventura and Africa had been known to mariners for centuries. It offered a leeward passage that could provide shelter from the prevailing [[trade-wind|northeast trades]], though the proximity of the African coast also brought the risk of harmattan winds and the sudden squalls characteristic of the region. (005-008)

## Significance

The passage past Fuerteventura represents a critical decision point in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage. He had initially planned to enter [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] and transit the [[suez|Suez Canal]], but after encountering unfavorable conditions and learning of the challenges ahead, he changed course for [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]. (005-001) (005-002) The appearance of Fuerteventura through the [[gale|storm]] affirmed that he had committed to the southern route — a decision that would lead to the most dramatic chapters of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

The dramatic weather — sudden squalls, a brief clearing, and the African dust [[gale|storm]] — illustrates the volatile conditions that made this coastal passage both challenging and memorable. The image of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] scudding through the narrow channel between a volcanic island and the African mainland, alone in a [[gale|storm]] and committed to the world's most dangerous cape, captures the spirit of the entire voyage. (005-008) (005-009)
