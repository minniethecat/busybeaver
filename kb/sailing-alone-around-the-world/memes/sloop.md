A **sloop** is a [[sail|sailing]] vessel with a single mast, a fore-and-aft mainsail, and a single headsail (jib). It is the simplest complete sail plan—one mast, two working [[sail|sails]]—and was [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s deliberate choice for the *[[spray|Spray]]* because it required the fewest hands to manage. The term appears throughout the narrative, used interchangeably with "[[spray|the Spray]]" and "the sloop."

## Definition

In [[rigging|rig]] classification, a sloop carries one mast stepped forward of amidships, with a mainsail [[current|set]] aft (either gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged or Bermuda-[[rigging|rig]]ged) and a single jib forward. This distinguishes it from a cutter (one mast, two head[[sail|sails]]), a ketch (two [[mast|masts]], mizzen forward of the rudder post), or a yawl (two [[mast|masts]], mizzen aft of the rudder post). The sloop's simplicity means all sail controls—[[halyard|halyards]], sheets, [[reef|reefing]] lines—lead to one point, making it the ideal [[rigging|rig]] for solo [[sail|sailing]]. The *[[spray|Spray]]* was gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged, carrying a four-sided mainsail with a gaff at the head and a boom at the foot.

## Nautical Context

The sloop [[rigging|rig]] has been the workhorse of small-boat [[sail|sailing]] for centuries—fishing sloops, pilot sloops, and pleasure sloops share the same basic configuration. Its advantage for the single-hander is that all lines are within reach. Its weakness is that the mast is unstayed laterally by any second mast, so the shrouds must be [[current|set]] up carefully—a failure of the [[rigging|standing rigging]] on a sloop is catastrophic.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s contemporaries generally considered a sloop too small for a [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. The prevailing wisdom favored larger vessels with crew. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s successful voyage—"a very antiquated sloop called [[spray|the Spray]]" (001-007)—challenged that assumption and inspired generations of small-boat sailors.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The word "sloop" appears hundreds of times in the text, often in place of the vessel's name—a convention that personalizes the boat without sentimentality.

The *[[spray|Spray]]* was introduced with disarming modesty:

> "The next day I discovered that it was a very antiquated sloop called the Spray, which the neighbors had made up their minds I would not accept" (001-007)

The "antiquated sloop" became one of history's most famous vessels.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s pride in his sloop's performance is evident. After a fast Atlantic run: "The sloop had made ninety miles in the twenty-four hours" (004-011). In [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]: "The sloop making [[knot|eight knots]]" (003-003)—a remarkable speed for a nine-[[tonnage|ton]] vessel.

The sloop's handling characteristics saved [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] repeatedly. When a squall struck: "The sloop naturally rounded in the wind" (002-006)—hove-to automatically when the [[rudder|helm]] was released. This self-[[helm|steering]] quality, partly the sloop's design and partly [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s trim, was essential for a solo sailor who needed to sleep.

At [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]], visiting shipmasters "inspected the sloop's [[rigging|rig]]ging" (005-030), their professional curiosity piqued by the tiny vessel that had crossed [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] alone. In the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], "the sloop was reaching, under [[mainsail|close-reefed mainsail]]" (004-014) through conditions that would test any vessel.

The sloop became a character in its own [[rigging|rig]]ht. In the encounter with the great ship *Olympus* off the [[azores|Azores]]: "My little sloop passing his great ship had so astonished him that he rubbed his siesta" (003-010)—the tiny sloop outrunning imagination.
