**Port Elizabeth** is a port city on Algoa Bay on the southeastern coast of South Africa. For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], it was a waypoint on his coastal passage from [[durban|Durban]] to [[cape-town|Cape Town]] during the final stages of his solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Geographic Context

Port Elizabeth lies on the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] coast of South Africa's Eastern Cape province. During the late nineteenth century, it was a growing colonial port serving the interior's agricultural and mineral wealth. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], nearing the end of his three-year [[voyage|circumnavigation]], the South African coast represented the final major cruising ground before [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing toward home.

## Slocum's Visit

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] departed [[durban|Durban]] on December 14, 1897, after what he described as "a fine time in [[durban|Natal]]" (017-026). His destination was [[cape-town|Table Bay]] ([[cape-town|Cape Town]]), approximately eight hundred miles away.

He anticipated the passage "might prove a rough sea" (018-002). Port Elizabeth, situated on Algoa Bay roughly midway along this coast, was a natural port of call for vessels making the passage.

On the first day out from [[durban|Durban]], the wind fell calm, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sat reflecting on the voyage ahead:

> On the first day out from Durban it fell calm, and I sat thinking about these things and the end of the voyage. The distance to Table Bay, where I intended to call, was about eight hundred miles over what might prove a rough sea. (018-002)

## Algoa Bay and Maritime History

Algoa Bay held deep significance in the history of navigation. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reflected on the early Portuguese navigators who struggled for decades to round the southern cape:

> The early Portuguese navigators, endowed with patience, were more than sixty-nine years struggling to round this cape before they got as far as Algoa Bay, and there the crew mutinied. They landed on a small island, now called Santa Cruz, where they devoutly set up the cross, and swore they would cut the captain's throat if he attempted to sail farther. Beyond this they thought was the edge of the world, which they too believed was flat; and fearing that their ship would sail over the brink of it, they compelled Captain Diaz, their commander, to retrace his course, all being only too glad to get home. (018-002)

Bartolomeu Dias, compelled by his crew to turn back at this point, planted a cross on what became Santa Cruz Island — the farthest point reached by Europeans along the African coast in 1488. It would be another year before Vasco da Gama successfully rounded [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] and opened the sea route to India. A year after Dias's retreat, as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted:

> A year later, we are told, Vasco da Gama sailed successfully round the "Cape of Storms," as the Cape of Good Hope was then called, and discovered Natal on Christmas or Natal day; hence the name. From this point the way to India was easy. (018-002)

## The Cape Passage

After passing Port Elizabeth and the Algoa Bay vicinity, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] continued southwest toward [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope. [[gale|Gale]]s were frequent in these waters — "one occurring, on an average, every thirty-six hours" (018-003).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] remarked that "one gale was much the same as another, with no more serious result than to blow the *[[spray|Spray]]* along on her course when it was fair, or to blow her back somewhat when it was ahead" (018-003).

On Christmas Day, 1897, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reached the pitch of the cape. The *[[spray|Spray]]* "was trying to stand on her head" (018-003), and while [[reef|reefing]] the jib at the end of the bowsprit, "she ducked me under water three times for a Christmas box" (018-003).

A passing English steamer signaled "Wishing you a Merry Christmas," which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] took with good humor — "I think the captain was a humorist; his own ship was throwing her propeller out of water" (018-003).
