## Geography

South America is the fourth-largest continent, bounded by the Caribbean Sea to the north, the Atlantic Ocean to the east, and the Pacific Ocean to the west. Slocum's voyage traced significant portions of both coastlines — the Atlantic side from Brazil southward through Uruguay and Argentina to the Strait of Magellan, and the Pacific side along the Chilean coast after his passage through the strait.

## Role in the Voyage

South America figured prominently in Slocum's route. He encountered a Swedish scientific expedition bound for "South America and the Pacific Islands" during his own voyage (010-009). Later, while in port at an island in the Pacific, he observed that most of the residents "were mostly from the mainland of South America," including a lady from Chile (011-002). The continent's waters proved challenging: in "South American waters the bowsprit and boom" of the Spray sustained damage that required repair (022-011).

## Historical Context

By the 1890s, South America's coastline was well charted, but its southern reaches remained sparsely populated and primitive in terms of maritime infrastructure. Ports like Montevideo, Buenos Aires, and Pernambuco offered Slocum opportunities to reprovision, while the Patagonian channels and the Strait of Magellan tested his seamanship in ways that the open ocean could not.
