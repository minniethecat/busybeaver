**Port Stevens** (modern Port Stephens) is a large natural harbor on the coast of [[newcastle|New South Wales]], [[australia|Australia]], about 160 kilometers north-northeast of [[sydney|Sydney]]. [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] passed this prominent coastal landmark during his southward voyage along the [[australia|Australia]]n east coast after departing [[newcastle|Newcastle]].

## Geography

Port Stephens is a drowned river valley estuary formed by the confluence of the Karuah and Myall rivers, opening into the Tasman Sea through a narrow entrance between prominent headlands. Its entrance is flanked by Yacaaba Head to the north and Tomaree Head to the south.

The harbor covers approximately 134 square kilometers, making it one of the largest natural harbors on the [[newcastle|New South Wales]] coast. The surrounding region has sandy beaches, coastal dune systems, and a temperate maritime climate with warm summers and mild winters.

In [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time (the 1890s), the area was sparsely [[current|set]]tled, with fishing and small-scale agriculture as the primary activities. The harbor's deep water and sheltered anchorages had made it a refuge for coastal shipping since the early colonial period.

## Slocum's Passage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed from [[newcastle|Newcastle]] on May 9 with a strong southwest wind that "sent [[spray|the Spray]] gallantly on as far as Port Stevens, where it fell calm and then came up ahead" (014-017). The weather was fine and remained so for many days, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted was "a great change from the state of the weather experienced here some months before" (014-017). He was referring to his earlier passage along this same coast, when he had been bound northward and had encountered much more severe conditions.

After passing Port Stevens, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] continued south past Seal Rocks and Cape Hawk:

> "The wind, for a few days after passing Port Stevens, Seal Rocks, and Cape Hawk, was light and dead ahead; but these points are photographed on my memory from the trial of beating round them some months before when bound the other way." — (014-019)

## A Pleasant Cruise

The passage past Port Stevens marked the beginning of a more comfortable leg of the voyage. With a good stock of books on board, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "fell to reading day and night, leaving this pleasant occupation merely to trim [[sail|sails]] or tack, or to lie down and rest, while [[spray|the Spray]] nibbled at the miles" (014-019). He reflected on how his experience compared favorably with that of earlier circumnavigators who had followed the same route:

> "Their hardships and romantic escapes — those of them who escaped death and worse sufferings — did not enter into my experience, sailing all alone around the world." — (014-019)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] concluded with characteristic modesty: "For me is left to tell only of pleasant experiences, till finally my adventures are prosy and tame" (014-019).

## Historical Context

Port Stephens was first sighted by Europeans in 1770 when [[captain-cook|Captain James Cook]] passed the entrance and named it after Sir Philip Stephens, then Secretary to the Admiralty. Cook did not enter the harbor, and the first detailed survey was conducted by acting surveyor-general Charles Grimes in 1795. During the early colonial period of [[newcastle|New South Wales]], the harbor served as a refuge for coastal vessels plying the route between [[sydney|Sydney]] and the [[current|set]]tlements to the north.

By the 1890s, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed, the port was a modest coastal [[current|set]]tlement. The [[australia|Australia]]n coast in this region had become familiar to maritime traffic, with regular steamer services connecting the coastal towns. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s observation that the weather was "a great change from the state of the weather experienced here some months before" (014-017) reflects the seasonal variability that coastal sailors in these waters had long contended with — the region's southerly busters and sudden calms were well known to navigators of the [[newcastle|New South Wales]] coast.
