## Geography

Halifax is the capital city of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], situated on one of the world's deepest natural harbors on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] coast of Canada. In the 1890s, Halifax was a major British imperial naval station and a key port for transatlantic shipping.

The approaches to the harbor are marked by prominent navigational features including Chebucto Head, George's Island, and the [[sambro-light|Sambro]] Island [[beacon|lighthouse]]. These landmarks formed a critical reference system for vessels departing North America for the open Atlantic.

## In Slocum's Voyage

Although [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] did not enter Halifax harbor, the city served as an important departure reference point at the start of his Atlantic crossing. The day began with a fast run along the [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] coast. At noon on July 3, Ironbound Island was abeam, and the *[[spray|Spray]]* was "again at her best" (003-004).

Earlier that morning, a large schooner came out of Liverpool, [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], [[helm|steering]] eastward. The *[[spray|Spray]]* overtook her decisively — "put her [[hull|hull down]] astern in five hours" (003-004). This was a satisfying demonstration of the little [[sloop|sloop's]] speed against a conventional merchant vessel.

By evening, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had reached the approaches to Halifax. He noted: "At 6:45 p.m. I was in close under Chebucto Head light, near Halifax harbor. I [[current|set]] my flag and squared away, taking my departure from George's Island before dark to sail east of [[sable-island|Sable Island]]" (003-004).

## The Departure Point

Taking one's departure was a formal navigational ritual in the age of sail — the precise moment and position from which a vessel began its ocean passage. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], George's Island at the mouth of Halifax harbor served as this ceremonial and practical starting point.

From this position, he watched the coastal lights of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] recede into the night as the *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed into the open Atlantic. Among these lights, he gave special attention to [[sambro-light|Sambro]], which he called "[[gibraltar|the Rock]] of Lamentations" — a [[beacon|lighthouse]] famous among mariners for the many vessels wrecked on its surrounding ledges (003-004).

The weather on departure was "propitious for a good run," with the wind coming out nor'west and clear after a rude gale the day before (003-003). The head sea had gone down, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had sailed from [[yarmouth|Yarmouth]] that morning, letting go his "last hold on America" (003-003).

## Strategic Significance

Halifax's position at the gateway to [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] made it the natural departure point for eastbound vessels from the northeastern seaboard. By squaring away from George's Island, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was following a well-established route taken by countless ships before him.

Yet his circumstances were unique: a single-handed sailor in a 36-foot sloop, embarking on what would become the first solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] of the globe. The lights of Halifax marked the last familiar shore he would see before facing the full force of the North Atlantic alone.

> I watched light after light sink astern as I sailed into the unbounded sea, till Sambro, the last of them all, was below the horizon. The *Spray* was then alone, and sailing on, she held her course.
>
> (003-004)
