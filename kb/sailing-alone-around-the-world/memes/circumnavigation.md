The **First Solo Circumnavigation** was [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s three-year voyage [[voyage|around the world]] aboard [[sloop|the sloop]] *[[spray|Spray]]*, from April 24, 1895 to July 3, 1898. Covering over 46,000 miles, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] became the first person to sail alone around the globe. The voyage began in [[boston|Boston]], Massachusetts, and concluded at [[newport|Newport]], [[newport|Rhode Island]], after traversing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], and the South Atlantic (002-002) (003-011).

## Summary

On the morning of April 24, 1895, with a fair wind and the twelve-o'clock whistles blowing in [[boston|Boston]] harbor, [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] — a retired sea captain then in his early fifties — [[current|set]] sail alone in the *[[spray|Spray]]*, a 36-foot sloop he had rebuilt with his own hands. Three years, two months, and two days later, he arrived at [[newport|Newport]], [[newport|Rhode Island]], having accomplished what no one had done before: a solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] of the Earth (002-002).

## Context

The [[voyage|circumnavigation]] was the natural culmination of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s lifelong relationship with the sea. Born in [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] in 1844, he had gone to sea as a boy and risen to command of the *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]*, one of the finest American [[sail|sailing]] vessels of the 1880s. After the wreck of his bark *[[aquidneck|Aquidneck]]* on the coast of Brazil, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found himself ashore, a shipmaster without a ship in a declining age of sail (001-004).

> "I was born in the breezes, and I had studied the sea as perhaps few men have studied it, neglecting all else." — (001-005)

In the winter of 1892, [[eben-pierce|Captain Eben Pierce]] gave [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] the derelict *[[spray|Spray]]*. Over thirteen months, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rebuilt her from keel to mast, spending $553.62 on materials and investing his own labor (001-015). After a season of fishing (at which he admitted he was inept), he resolved on the voyage: "I had resolved on a voyage [[voyage|around the world]]" (002-002).

## Narrative

### Departure and Atlantic Crossing (1895)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* departed [[boston|Boston]] on April 24, 1895. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the moment with palpable emotion:

> "A thrilling pulse beat high in me. My step was light on deck in the crisp air. I felt that there could be no turning back, and that I was engaging in an adventure the meaning of which I thoroughly understood." — (002-002)

After fitting out at [[gloucester|Gloucester]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed to [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], then across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] to the [[azores|Azores]] and [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]. At [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], British naval officers warned [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] that [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] was infested with pirates, prompting him to turn westward and take the longer but safer route around South America (004-025).

### Cape Horn and the Strait of Magellan (1896)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* reached [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] in February 1896. This proved the most treacherous part of the voyage. Williwaws — violent katabatic winds — repeatedly drove [[sloop|the sloop]] from her anchorages, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was forced to navigate the strait's narrow channels alone. After reaching [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] and being driven back by a [[gale|storm]], he recrossed the strait, "circumnavigating the wildest part of desolate [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]" (007-018).

### Across the Pacific (1896)

From the strait, the *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed to [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] ([[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]'s island), then across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] to [[samoa|Samoa]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was hosted by [[robert-louis-stevenson|Robert Louis Stevenson]]'s widow at Vailima. The 72-day Pacific passage included a narrow escape from collision with a great whale (012-003). From [[samoa|Samoa]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* continued to [[australia|Australia]], arriving at [[newcastle|Newcastle]] and [[sydney|Sydney]], where Commodore Foy presented her with a new suit of [[sail|sails]] (013-012).

### Indian Ocean and the Cape of Good Hope (1897)

From [[australia|Australia]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], stopping at the [[keel|Keel]]ing [[keeling-islands|Cocos Islands]], [[rodriguez-island|Rodrigues]], and [[mauritius|Mauritius]], before rounding [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope — the traditional halfway point of any [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

### Atlantic Return and Homecoming (1898)

The final Atlantic crossing brought the *[[spray|Spray]]* to the Caribbean, then up the eastern seaboard of the United States. On July 3, 1898, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at [[newport|Newport]], [[newport|Rhode Island]], "End of a cruise of over forty-six thousand miles" (021-001). He had been gone three years, two months, and two days.

## Aftermath

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s achievement brought him international fame. *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*, published in 1900, became a classic of maritime literature. The *[[spray|Spray]]* became an icon of small-boat seamanship, inspiring generations of solo sailors. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] himself, however, never fully [[current|set]]tled ashore. In 1909, at the age of 65, he [[current|set]] sail alone again — this time bound for the Orinoco River — and was never seen again.

> "If the Spray discovered no continents on her voyage, it may be that there were no more continents to be discovered... The sea has been much maligned. To find one's way to lands already discovered is a good thing, and the Spray made the discovery that even the worst sea is not so terrible to a well-appointed ship." — (021-015)

## Significance

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]] was a landmark in maritime history — the first proof that a single person could sail [[voyage|around the world]] alone. More broadly, it was a testament to self-reliance, craftsmanship, and the idea that extraordinary achievements could come from humble means: a $553 boat, rebuilt by hand, carrying one man safely around the planet.
