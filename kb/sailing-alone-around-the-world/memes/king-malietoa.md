# King Malietoa

Malietoa Laupepa (c. 1841–1898) was the King of [[samoa|Samoa]] during [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s visit to [[apia|Apia]] in 1896. A significant figure in [[samoa|Samoa]]n history, Malietoa navigated the complex colonial politics of the late nineteenth century while maintaining his royal authority. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounter with the king provides a vivid glimpse of [[samoa|Samoa]]n court life and colonial pageantry.

## Biography

Malietoa was one of the paramount chiefs of [[samoa|Samoa]], holding the royal title Malietoa, one of the highest in [[samoa|Samoa]]n tradition. During his reign, [[samoa|Samoa]] was caught between German, British, and American colonial interests—a political tension that would eventually lead to the Tripartite Convention of 1899 and the partition of the islands (014-001).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] records that Malietoa "never got less than forty-five dollars a month for the job, as he told me myself, and this amount had lately been raised, so that he could live on the fat of the land" (014-001). Beachcombers, with their characteristic irreverence, had previously nicknamed him "Tin-of-salmon Malietoa," suggesting a less prosperous era (014-001).

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] visited King Malietoa while in [[apia|Apia, Samoa]], during his Pacific crossing. The visit was prompted by a message from "Mr. W—— of New York, a gentleman interested in missionary work," who had "charged me, when I sailed, to give his remembrance to the king of the Cannibal Islands" (014-001).

The encounter had moments of both ceremony and comedy:

> "As my interpreter and I entered the front door of the palace, the king's brother, who was viceroy, sneaked in through a taro-patch by the back way, and sat cowering by the door while I told my story to the king." — (014-001)

Malietoa, upon learning that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had come without sending credentials ahead so the king might appear in full regalia, excused himself and returned in spectacular fashion:

> "His Majesty then excused himself, while I talked with his daughter, the beautiful Faamu-Sami (a name signifying 'To make the sea burn'), and soon reappeared in the full-dress uniform of the German commander-in-chief, Emperor William himself." — (014-001)

The king's daughter, Faamu-Sami ("To Make the Sea Burn"), and her cousin Mrs. Jardine, who was Malietoa's niece, both appear in the narrative. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later met the Jardine family at Somer[[current|set]] on the [[australia|Australia]]n coast, where [[captain-jones|Captain Jones]] brought pearl shells aboard the *[[spray|Spray]]* (016-005).

## Significance

King Malietoa represents the intersection of indigenous Pacific royalty and European colonial influence that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered throughout his voyage. The image of the [[samoa|Samoa]]n king donning a German emperor's uniform to receive a solitary American sailor captures the era's cultural collisions with gentle humor.

Malietoa's warm reception of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], despite the lack of diplomatic protocol, exemplifies the hospitality that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] received throughout [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] islands. The encounter also connects multiple threads of the narrative: Malietoa's niece Mrs. Jardine appears later in [[australia|Australia]], and his daughter Faamu-Sami is remembered by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] with evident fondness (016-005).
