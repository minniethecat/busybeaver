**St. Nicholas Bay** is a bay in the western reaches of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], where [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] anchored twice during his passage through the strait and experienced — and narrowly survived — the violent williwaw squalls characteristic of the region.

## Geography

St. Nicholas Bay lies on the western side of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], in [[chile|Chile]]an [[patagonia|Patagonia]], between the mainland and the islands of the [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] archipelago. The bay is dominated by a high mountain on its southern shore, under which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anchored in eight fathoms of water (007-015). The surrounding terrain is steep and mountainous, typical of the western [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] region, with sparse vegetation due to the extreme winds that sweep down from the peaks.

The bay's location in the western strait places it in one of the most meteorologically violent zones on earth. The williwaws — katabatic gusts that plunge from the mountain slopes with hurricane force — are the defining climatic feature of this region. These winds are generated when cold, dense air from the Andean ice fields descends rapidly through the mountain passes, accelerating to destructive velocities. The bay's anchorage, while offering some shelter under the mountain, remained exposed to these sudden and violent downdrafts.

## First Anchorage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] first reached St. Nicholas Bay after a fair wind from [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] brought him there in a single day. He had been warned he might encounter savages, but "seeing no signs of life, I came to anchor in eight fathoms of water, where I lay all night under a high mountain" (007-015). It was here that he had his first experience with the williwaws:

> "Here I had my first experience with the terrific squalls, called williwaws, which extended from this point on through the strait to the Pacific. They were compressed gales of wind that Boreas handed down over the hills in chunks. A full-blown williwaw will throw a ship, even without sail on, over on her beam ends." — (007-015)

This vivid description — gales "handed down over the hills in chunks" — has become one of the most quoted passages in the book, capturing both the violence and the peculiar character of these [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n winds.

## Second Arrival and Near-Disaster

After being driven seaward by repeated williwaws, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] struggled back toward St. Nicholas Bay. He "kept on [[sail|sailing]] in hope, since I had no choice but to go on, [[course|heading]] across for St. Nicholas Bay, where I had cast anchor February 19. It was now the 10th of March!" (008-016). Upon reaching the bay the second time, he had "circumnavigated the wildest part of desolate [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]" (008-016).

But the *[[spray|Spray]]* had not yet safely arrived. As she approached St. Nicholas, "the parting of a [[forestaysail|staysail]] sheet in a williwaw, when the sea was turbulent and she was plunging into the [[gale|storm]], brought me forward to see instantly a dark cliff ahead and breakers so close under the bows" (008-016). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] thought himself lost, crying inwardly: "Is the hand of fate against me, after all, leading me in the end to this dark spot?" (008-016). He sprang aft and threw the [[helm|wheel]] over, and "at the touch of her [[rudder|helm]] she swung clear of the danger, and in the next moment she was in the lee of the land" (008-016).

## Historical Context

St. Nicholas Bay, like most anchorages in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], entered maritime history through the age of exploration. The strait itself was discovered by [[ferdinand-magellan|Ferdinand Magellan]] in 1520 and for centuries remained the primary route between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and Pacific oceans — a passage notorious for its unpredictable weather, strong currents, and treacherous lee shores.

By the late nineteenth century, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made his passage, the strait was regularly used by steamers and [[sail|sailing]] vessels. The [[chile|Chile]]an government had established the [[current|set]]tlement of [[sandy-point|Punta Arenas]] ([[sandy-point|Sandy Point]]) as a coaling station and port of refuge. Nevertheless, for a small [[sail|sailing]] vessel like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the western reaches of the strait remained as dangerous as they had been in [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]'s time. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experiences at St. Nicholas Bay — the williwaws, the near shipwreck — were not unusual; they were the common hazards of navigating the strait without an engine, relying solely on sail and seamanship.
