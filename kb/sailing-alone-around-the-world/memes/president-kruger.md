# Paul Kruger

Stephanus Johannes Paulus "Paul" Kruger (1825–1904), known as Oom Paul ("Uncle Paul"), was the president of the South African Republic (Transvaal). [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] met him in [[pretoria|Pretoria]] during the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s stopover in South Africa in 1897, in an encounter that produced one of the voyage's most memorable anecdotes: Kruger's insistence that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could not have sailed *around* the world because the world is flat.

## Biography

Paul Kruger was born on October 10, 1825, in [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Colony. He became a leader of the Boer resistance against British rule and served as president of the South African Republic from 1883 to 1900. A deeply religious man with little formal education, Kruger was known for his terse, memorable sayings and his conservative worldview (018-019).

Kruger led the Transvaal during the discovery of gold on the Witwatersrand, which brought immense wealth and foreign pressure to his republic. His resistance to British demands for voting [[rigging|rig]]hts for the *Uitlanders* (foreign miners) eventually led to the Second Boer War (1899–1902). He died in exile in Switzerland in 1904 (018-019).

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] met Kruger in [[pretoria|Pretoria]] during a railway trip from [[cape-town|Cape Town]] in 1897. The encounter was arranged by Judge Beyers, who presented [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to the president:

> "His Excellency received me cordially enough; but my friend Judge Beyers, the gentleman who presented me, by mentioning that I was on a voyage around the world, unwittingly gave great offense to the venerable statesman, which we both regretted deeply." — (018-019)

Kruger's response became legendary:

> "Mr. Krüger corrected the judge rather sharply, reminding him that the world is flat. 'You don't mean *round* the world,' said the president; 'it is impossible! You mean *in* the world. Impossible!' he said, 'impossible!'" — (018-019)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], far from being offended, was delighted by the incident. He wrote: "The incident pleased me more than anything else that could have happened. It was a nugget of information quarried out of Oom Paul, some of whose sayings are famous" (018-019).

Kruger's flat-earth conviction was not isolated. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] records that three Boers, "favored by the opinion of President Krüger," prepared a pamphlet to support the flat-earth theory, and later approached [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] in [[durban|Durban]] for navigational data (018-020).

## Significance

Kruger's encounter with [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] serves as one of the most colorful episodes in *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. The story illustrates the cultural gap between [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s empirical experience (he had just completed most of a [[voyage|circumnavigation]]) and Kruger's unshakeable religious-literalism. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] treats the episode with characteristic wry humor rather than condescension.

The anecdote has persisted in the lore of the book, frequently quoted by reviewers and readers as emblematic of the unexpected encounters that defined [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage. Kruger also famously said of the English, "They took first my coat and then my trousers," and declared that "Dynamite is the cornerstone of the South African Republic" (018-019).
