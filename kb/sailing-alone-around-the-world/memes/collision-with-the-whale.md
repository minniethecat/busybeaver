The **Collision with the Whale** was a narrow escape that occurred during [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s 72-day Pacific passage from [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] to [[samoa|Samoa]]. While [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was below deck at night, the *[[spray|Spray]]* came close to striking a great whale that was "absentmindedly plowing the ocean." The incident was one of the few dramatic moments in an otherwise uneventful crossing of the world's largest ocean (012-003).

## Summary

During the long Pacific passage — 43 days to the vicinity of [[nukahiva|Nukahiva]], plus 29 more to [[samoa|Samoa]] — the *[[spray|Spray]]* narrowly avoided colliding with a great whale at night. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], who was below deck at the time, was roused by the noise of the whale's startled snort and the commotion as it turned to clear the vessel. He reached the deck in time to be drenched by water thrown up from the whale's flukes (012-003).

## Context

The passage from [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] to [[samoa|Samoa]] covered thousands of miles of open Pacific, the longest unbroken stretch of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had departed [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] on May 5, 1896, after a pleasant stay at [[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]'s island. For 72 days he saw no other ships, though "there was no end of companionship; the very coral [[reefing|reef]]s kept me company" (012-001).

> "To be alone forty-three days would seem a long time, but in reality, even here, winged moments flew lightly by." — (012-001)

Whales were not unknown to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — his benefactor [[eben-pierce|Captain Eben Pierce]] had been a whaling captain, and [[fairhaven|Fairhaven]] was a whaling port. But meeting one unexpectedly at night in the open ocean was an altogether different experience.

## Narrative

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] tells the story with characteristic understatement:

> "First among the incidents of the voyage from Juan Fernandez to Samoa (which were not many) was a narrow escape from collision with a great whale that was absentmindedly plowing the ocean at night while I was below. The noise from his startled snort and the commotion he made in the sea, as he turned to clear my vessel, brought me on deck in time to catch a wetting from the water he threw up with his flukes." — (012-003)

The whale was "apparently frightened" by the encounter, [[course|heading]] quickly to the east while the *[[spray|Spray]]* continued west. Soon after, a second whale passed — "evidently a companion, following in its wake" (012-003). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] saw no more whales on that leg of the voyage, "nor did I wish to" — a rare admission of relief from a seasoned mariner (012-003).

The incident highlights the vulnerability of a small vessel on the open ocean. The *[[spray|Spray]]*, at just 36 feet 9 inches and 9 [[tonnage|tons]], would have been no match for a full-grown whale. A collision could have stove in her hull and ended the voyage — and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s life — in minutes.

## Aftermath

The encounter passed without damage. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] continued westward toward [[samoa|Samoa]], where he arrived at [[apia|Apia]] and was warmly welcomed. The incident became, in retrospect, merely a colorful anecdote in a voyage filled with them. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s matter-of-fact telling — treating a near-death experience with the same [[tonnage|ton]]e as any other day's event — is characteristic of his narrative style throughout the book.

## Significance

The whale encounter is representative of the unpredictable dangers of solo [[sail|sailing]]. Unlike [[gale|storm]]s or navigation errors, which a skilled mariner could anticipate and prepare for, a collision with a sleeping whale was purely a matter of chance. That the *[[spray|Spray]]* survived the near-miss — like the many [[gale|storm]]s, groundings, and other hazards she weathered — testifies both to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s luck and to the sturdy construction of the little sloop.
