Panama is the narrow isthmus connecting North and South America, mentioned twice in [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] narrative — once in connection with the fate of a steamer he had encountered in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], and once during his visit to [[samoa|Samoa]].

## Geography

Panama forms the land bridge between Central and South America, bounded by the Caribbean Sea to the north and [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] Ocean to the south. At the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage (1895–1898), the region was part of Colombia — the Panama Canal would not open until 1914, nearly two decades later. The isthmus had long been a vital transit route between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and Pacific oceans.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] never visited Panama during his [[voyage|circumnavigation]], but it appears in his narrative through two references. The first concerns the steamer *Colombia*, which had assisted [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. He later learned of her fate:

> "Had the captain given me his steamer, his company would have been no worse off than they were two or three months later. I read afterward, in a late California paper, 'The *Colombia* will be a total loss.' On her second trip to Panama she was wrecked on the rocks of the California coast." — (009-014)

The second reference comes from [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit to Vailima, the [[samoa|Samoa]] estate of [[robert-louis-stevenson|Robert Louis Stevenson]]'s widow:

> "Calling to say goodbye to my friends at Vailima, I met Mrs. Stevenson in her Panama hat, and went over the estate with her." — (013-007)

Mrs. [[robert-louis-stevenson|Stevenson]] ordered two bamboo trees cut for the *[[spray|Spray]]* during this visit, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used for spare spars on the homeward voyage.

## The Panama Canal and Maritime Context

By the 1890s, Panama was a province of Colombia and the site of one of the great engineering sagas of the age. The French had recently abandoned their attempt to build a canal across the isthmus (1881–1889), and the United States would take up the project after [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage.

For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the absence of a canal meant that transiting between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and Pacific required either the long passage around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] or the dangerous [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]]. His choice of the strait — and the [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n encounters that followed — was a consequence of this geographical reality. A Panama Canal would have completely changed the route of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

The "Panama hat" mentioned in connection with Mrs. [[robert-louis-stevenson|Stevenson]] was, despite its name, traditionally made in Ecuador from the plaited leaves of the toquilla palm. By the late nineteenth century, it had become an internationally fashionable accessory, making Mrs. [[robert-louis-stevenson|Stevenson]]'s headwear a recognizable cultural reference for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s readers.
