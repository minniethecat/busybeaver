Cape Virgins (Cabo Vírgenes) is the southeastern promontory marking [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] entrance to [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], at the southern extremity of continental [[patagonia|Patagonia]].

## Geography

Cape Virgins stands at the southeastern entrance of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], forming [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] gateway to the passage between continental South America and [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]. The cape is characterized by desolate, windswept terrain typical of southern [[patagonia|Patagonia]]. The waters around the cape are treacherous, with powerful [[tide|tide-race]]s and fierce currents sweeping around the headland (007-007). The shoreline is marked by sunken rocks draped in trailing kelp, presenting navigational hazards to vessels entering the strait (007-007).

The wreck of a great steamship, smashed on the beach abreast of the cape, "gave a gloomy aspect to the scene" as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] entered (007-007) — a stark reminder of the price exacted by these waters.

## The Patagonian Approach

The approach to Cape Virgins along the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]] was marked by an unnatural stillness after weeks of violent weather. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], respecting the region's reputation, "gave all the capes a berth of about fifty miles, for these dangers extend many miles from the land" (007-005). Even at this distance, the notorious [[tide|tide-race]]s — "the dread of big craft or little along this coast" — remained a constant peril (007-005).

During this passage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] witnessed an atmospheric phenomenon unique to the region: "The phenomenon of mirage frequently occurred" (007-006). The distorted air above the cold [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n waters played tricks on perception, bending the horizon and making distant objects loom strangely. This eerie prelude — mirages, stillness, and the unseen threat of [[tide|tide-race]]s — gave the approach to Cape Virgins a dreamlike quality that belied the ordeal to come.

## The Great Wave Before the Cape

Well before reaching Cape Virgins, the *[[spray|Spray]]* survived one of the most harrowing moments of the entire voyage. While reaching under short sail off the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered "a tremendous wave, the culmination, it seemed, of many waves" that "rolled down upon her in a [[gale|storm]], roaring as it came" (007-005).

With only a moment's warning, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] got all sail down and climbed the peak halliards out of danger as "the mighty crest towering [[mast|masthead]]-high above me" bore down upon [[sloop|the sloop]] (007-005). The mountain of water submerged the vessel entirely: "She shook in every timber and reeled under the weight of the sea, but rose quickly out of it, and rode grandly over the rollers that followed" (007-005). For what may have been a minute, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could "see no part of [[spray|the Spray]]'s hull" from his perch in the [[rigging|rig]]ging (007-005).

The incident, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described as filling him with fear, was "only one more test of [[spray|the Spray]]'s seaworthiness" and "reassured me against rude [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]" (007-005). From that moment until reaching Cape Virgins, by contrast, "nothing occurred to move a pulse and [[current|set]] blood in motion" (007-006).

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] approached Cape Virgins after recovering from a near-sinking by a tremendous wave off the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]]. The weather between the wave incident and the cape was unusually tranquil, marked by mirages that distorted his perception of the sea (007-006).

On February 11, 1896, [[spray|the Spray]] rounded Cape Virgins and entered [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. The approach was difficult — a northeast gale sent "feather-white spume along the coast" and the sea was rough enough to "swamp an ill-appointed ship" (007-007). As [[sloop|the sloop]] neared the entrance, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed two great [[tide|tide-race]]s, one close to the point and another farther offshore. He guided [[spray|the Spray]] between them through churning combers with close-[[reef|reefed sail]]s (007-007).

The cape did not give easy passage. Shortly after entering, fitful rain-squalls gave way to a violent southwest gale that struck like "a shot from a cannon" and blew hard for thirty hours (007-008). A fierce current swept around the cape, but [[spray|the Spray]] stemmed it and found smoother water under the lee of Cape Virgins, though the wreck of a great steamship smashed on the beach "gave a gloomy aspect to the scene" (007-007).

> On February 11 the *Spray* rounded Cape Virgins and entered the Strait of Magellan. The scene was again real and gloomy; the wind, northeast, and blowing a gale, sent feather-white spume along the coast; such a sea ran as would swamp an ill-appointed ship. As the sloop neared the entrance to the strait I observed that two great tide-races made ahead, one very close to the point of the land and one farther offshore. Between the two, in a sort of channel, through combers, went the *Spray* with close-reefed sails. But a rolling sea followed her a long way in, and a fierce current swept around the cape against her; but this she stemmed, and was soon chirruping under the lee of Cape Virgins and running every minute into smoother water. However, long trailing kelp from sunken rocks waved forebodingly under her keel, and the wreck of a great steamship smashed on the beach abreast gave a gloomy aspect to the scene.
>
> (007-007)

## The Virgins Exact Their Tribute

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was philosophical about the rough reception at the cape. "I was not to be let off easy," he wrote. "The Virgins would collect tribute even from [[spray|the Spray]] passing their promontory" (007-008). After the northeast gale had blown itself out, "fitful rain-squalls from the northwest" took its place (007-008).

Exhausted, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[reefing|reefed]] [[sloop|the sloop]]'s [[sail|sails]] and sat in the cabin to rest. But he could not relax: "I was so strongly impressed with what in all nature I might expect that as I dozed the very air I breathed seemed to warn me of danger" (007-008). The instinct proved correct — within hours the southwest gale struck, testing both vessel and captain to their limits before the *[[spray|Spray]]* finally found shelter farther into the strait.

## Into the Strait

After the southwest gale subsided, the weather eased. "After this gale followed only a smart breeze," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded, and the *[[spray|Spray]]*, "passing through the narrows without mishap, cast anchor at [[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] on February 14, 1896" (007-009). The ordeal at Cape Virgins had ended, and the vessel had successfully entered [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] — but the challenges of the passage through the strait, with its williwaws, savage inhabitants, and treacherous anchorages, were only beginning.

[[sandy-point|Sandy Point]] ([[sandy-point|Punta Arenas]]) was a [[chile|Chile]]an coaling-station of about two thousand inhabitants, "of mixed nationality, but mostly [[chile|Chile]]ans" (007-011). Here [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered [[pedro-samblich|Captain Pedro Samblich]], "a good Austrian of large experience," who gave the *[[spray|Spray]]* a bag of carpet-tacks to spread [[deck|on deck]] as a defense against [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n boarders (007-012). When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] protested he had no use for carpet-tacks, "[[samblich|Samblich]] smiled at my want of experience, and maintained stoutly that I would have use for them" (007-012). The captain's advice proved prescient: the tacks, spread [[deck|on deck]] each night, became one of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s most effective defenses in the dangerous waters ahead, worth — as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later wrote — "more than all the fighting men and dogs of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]]" (007-012).
